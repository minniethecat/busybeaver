## Overview

The Reverend Arthur Bennett was the Church of England (Anglican) Chaplain attached to the Mavericks regiment. He first appears when Kim is discovered lurking in the regimental camp at night, and he plays a key role — alongside Father Victor, the Catholic chaplain — in identifying Kim's parentage and setting him on the path to St. Xavier's School. (005-052)

> It was Bennett, the Church of England Chaplain of the regiment, limping in dusty black. One of his flock had made some rude remarks about the Chaplain's mettle; and to abash him Bennett had marched step by step with the men that day. The black dress, gold cross on the watch-chain, the hairless face, and the soft, black wideawake hat would have marked him as a holy man anywhere in all India.
>
> (005-052)

## Discovery of Kim

Bennett found Kim in the darkness of the camp after the boy had been caught by the regimental guards. Initially suspicious, Bennett summoned Father Victor for advice, and together the two chaplains examined the boy. When Father Victor asked Kim about his origins, Bennett stood by, and it was to Bennett that Father Victor passed Kim's identity papers — the leather amulet-case containing his father's clearance certificate, Masonic documents, and birth certificate. (005-073)(005-075)(005-078)

## Relationship with Father Victor

The relationship between Bennett (Anglican) and Father Victor (Catholic) reflected the religious divisions within the regiment. Between Bennett and "the Irish contingent" — the Catholic soldiers — lay what Bennett believed to be "an unbridgeable gulf." Yet the two chaplains worked together professionally when the situation demanded it, as in the case of Kim's identification. Kipling noted that "whenever the Church of England dealt with a human problem she was likely to call in the Church of Rome," and Father Victor's practical experience often complemented Bennett's institutional authority. (005-074)(005-080)(005-082)

When Bennett expressed outrage at what he considered blasphemy, Father Victor responded with pragmatic counsel. Bennett was described as always leaving the officers' Mess after a particular hour, and Father Victor once teased him about his predictable habits, noting that Bennett believed in Providence — a gentle theological ribbing between the two chaplains. (005-064)(005-065)(005-134)
