# Saharunpore

Saharunpore (modern Saharanpur) is a small town among the fruit gardens of the upper Doab, serving as a quiet waypoint on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]]. In the novel, it is the place to which the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] retreats to recover from the rigours of the road, and the home of the [[the-kulu-woman|Kulu Woman]] whose story entangles with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s own.

## In *Kim*

Saharunpore first enters the narrative as a safe harbour. After [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] falls ill on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]], [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] stop "a little behind Saharunpore, among the fruit gardens" (004-130), where the [[kulu|Kulu]] Woman tends to the old man. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] recuperates in a "little fruit-village behind Saharunpore" (004-160), a peaceful interlude before the journey resumes.

The town reappears as a landmark on the railway line. When [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] travel by train, "the leisurely train halted at Saharunpore" (012-044), and the station — described as "clean and well scented" in the early morning (012-046) — provides a moment of calm before the journey northward toward the hills.

Saharunpore also serves as a character anchor. [[the-kulu-woman|The Kulu Woman]] is identified by her connection to the town: "[[the-kulu-woman|the woman from Kulu]]. That is by Saharunpore" (011-121). A clergyman at Saharunpore — "her puro, her clergyman, at Saharunpore" (006-077) — is mentioned as a figure who might vouch for her. In the novel's closing chapter, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] returns to "the long white rambling house behind Saharunpore" (015-038) where the [[kulu|Kulu]] Woman resides, bringing his [[pilgrimage]] full circle.

> "Oho! the woman from Kulu. That is by Saharunpore." Kim laughed.
>
> (011-121)

## Description

Saharunpore is characterised not by grand monuments but by its orchards and gardens. It is a town of fruit-trees — "among the fruit-trees behind Saharunpore" [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] is "honoured... as the woman honoured the prophet" (009-153). The surrounding countryside is fertile and peaceful, a marked contrast to the crowded bazaars of [[lahore|Lahore]] and the tense railway stations where spycraft unfolds.

The long white house behind the town — the [[kulu|Kulu]] Woman's residence — is a "rambling" structure that serves as a place of rest and recovery for [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s and wanderers on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]].

## Historical Context

Saharanpur, in the Saharanpur district of what is now Uttar Pradesh, was a minor administrative town under British rule, known for its botanical gardens (established 1817) and its position on the railway line between [[delhi|Delhi]] and [[punjab|the Punjab]]. Its fruit gardens were, and remain, a distinctive feature of the region's agriculture.

In Kipling's time, the town would have been a typical wayside station on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] — large enough to offer rest and provisions to travellers, small enough to feel like a retreat from the pressures of the city.

## Travel Routes

Saharunpore lies on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] and the main railway line:
- **To the southeast**: [[delhi|Delhi]]
- **To the northwest**: [[umballa|Umballa]] and [[lahore|Lahore]]
- **To the north**: the hill stations of the Himalayas
