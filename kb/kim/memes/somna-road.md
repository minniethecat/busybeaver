# Somna Road

Somna Road is a railway station on the North-Western line across [[punjab|the Punjab]] that appears in Chapter 11 of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. It is not a destination or a described place but a point of encounter — the station where the wounded spy E.23 tumbles into [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s compartment, setting in motion one of the novel's most tightly plotted espionage episodes.

## In *Kim*

Somna Road arrives without fanfare in the middle of Chapter 11. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], travelling with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and the Kamboh farmer from [[jullundur|Jullundur]], is enjoying the rhythm of the railway journey when "at Somna Road the Fates sent him a matter to think upon" (011-126). Into their compartment tumbles "a mean, lean little person — a Mahratta, so far as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] could judge by the cock of the tight turban. His face was cut, his muslin upper-garment was badly torn, and one leg was bandaged" (011-126).

This is E.23, a fellow agent in [[the-great-game|the Great Game]], though [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] does not yet know it. The man claims a country-cart upset and nearly killed him, but [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s trained eye catches the inconsistencies: "all his injuries seemed clean cuts, and a mere fall from a cart could not cast a man into such extremity of terror" (011-126). [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s suspicion sharpens when he notices the man's amulet — strung on square-plaited copper wire with black enamel on silver, the kind used by agents to identify one another.

Later, when [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and the Mahratta speak privately, the agent reveals his route: "I ran into the Fleshers' Ward and came out by the House of the Jew, who feared a riot and pushed me forth. I came afoot to Somna Road — I had only money for my *tikkut* to [[delhi|Delhi]] — and there, while I lay in a ditch with a fever, one sprang out of the bushes and beat me and cut me and searched me from head to foot. Within earshot of the *te-rain* it was!" (011-154). The agent's pursuers attacked him within hearing distance of the trains at Somna Road, making the station a waypoint in the clandestine pursuit that runs through the chapter.

[[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]] later enters at the next station down the line, and the three agents — [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], [[the-babu|the Babu]], and E.23 — orchestrate the spy's escape from his trackers. Somna Road is the hinge on which this entire sequence turns.

> But at Somna Road the Fates sent him a matter to think upon. There tumbled into the compartment, as the train was moving off, a mean, lean little person — a Mahratta, so far as Kim could judge by the cock of the tight turban. His face was cut, his muslin upper-garment was badly torn, and one leg was bandaged.
>
> (011-126)

## Description

Somna Road is a minor railway station on the North-Western Railway line running through [[punjab|the Punjab]]. In the novel it is barely described — it is simply a stop on the line, a platform where passengers board and alight. Its narrative significance comes not from any physical feature but from its role as the entry point of the spy E.23 into [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s compartment, transforming a routine train journey into an episode of espionage.

## Historical Context

The North-Western Railway was one of the principal rail lines of British India, connecting [[lahore|Lahore]] with [[delhi|Delhi]] and the United Provinces. By the 1890s, the railway network had transformed travel across [[punjab|the Punjab]], making journeys that once took weeks possible in days. The proliferation of wayside stations like Somna Road reflected the density of the railway network and its penetration into rural India. For Kipling, the railway was both a symbol of imperial modernity and a stage for the chance encounters that drive his plot.
