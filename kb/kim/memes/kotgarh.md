## The Mission Station

Kotgarh is distinguished among Himalayan villages by the presence of a Christian mission house, where European missionaries once operated. The Woman of Shamlegh recounts that she lived at the Mission-house in Kotgarh in her youth, where she wore European clothes, spoke English, and learned to play the piano. She was converted to Christianity there—she uses the English word "Ker-lis-ti-an"—before a Sahib whom she nursed through illness promised to marry her and then abandoned her, leading her to renounce the faith and return to her hill people. (014-069) (014-131) (014-132)

## Communication Hub

Kotgarh serves as a node in the hill-country information network. In Chapter 14, news of the confrontation with the Russian spies at Shamlegh reaches Kotgarh by the following day, demonstrating the speed at which information travels through the mountain communities despite the difficult terrain. The village is known to the hillfolk as a destination for longer journeys—they speak of trips "as far as Kotgarh" in the same breath as journeys to "marvellous Simla." (014-061) (014-069)

## Later Reference

In Chapter 15, Kotgarh is mentioned as having a "European colony" and a "mission-station," marking it as one of the few outposts of Western presence in the remote Himalayan region. (015-004)

## In the Text

The Woman of Shamlegh recounts her youthful experience at the Christian mission in Kotgarh:

> Once, long ago, I wore European clothes at the Mission-house yonder. Once, long ago, I was Ker-lis-ti-an and spoke English---as the Sahibs speak it. Yes. My Sahib said he would return and wed me---yes, wed me. He went away---I had nursed him when he was sick---but he never returned.
>
> (014-131)
