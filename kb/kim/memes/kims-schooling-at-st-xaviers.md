# Kim's Schooling at St. Xavier's

After the Mavericks regiment claimed Kim as the son of Kimball O'Hara, the question of his future divided his new guardians. Father Victor and Colonel Creighton saw in him a potential agent for the Great Game; Mahbub Ali had already proven the boy's value as a message-carrier; the lama, for his part, offered to pay for an education that would make Kim a Sahib. The decision was made: Kim would attend St. Xavier's in Partibus at Lucknow, the finest school available to the sons of British India — and the place where a street boy from Lahore would be transformed into a young man capable of moving between two worlds.

## Context

When Father Victor unstrung Kim's amulet and read the papers within, the boy's identity was established: he was white, the son of an Irish colour-sergeant, and therefore entitled — even obligated — to a European education. But what kind? The Military Orphanage offered a spartan, regimented existence. St. Xavier's at Lucknow offered something more.

Father Victor explained the options to Kim: "The best schooling a boy can get in India is, of course, at St Xavier's in Partibus at Lucknow" (005-187). The cost was three hundred rupees a year — a sum the lama, against all expectation, agreed to pay. His letter, laboriously composed with a bazar scribe, arrived at the regiment: the money would be forwarded per hundi from Benares, where the lama had found refuge at the Temple of the Tirthankars (006-076).

Colonel Creighton, with characteristic pragmatism, endorsed the plan: "I strongly recommend sending the boy to St Xavier's. He can go down on pass as a soldier's orphan, so the railway fare will be saved" (006-157). The Lodge would be spared the expense; the regiment would contribute an outfit; and Kim would receive the education that would make him valuable to the Great Game.

## The Event

### The journey to Lucknow

Kim's departure was abrupt. Father Victor gave him parting instructions: "They'll make a man o' you, O'Hara, at St Xavier's — a white man, an', I hope, a good man" (007-031). Colonel Creighton, travelling to Lucknow on his own business, accompanied the boy. At the crowded Lucknow station, there was no sign of the lama — Kim swallowed his disappointment. The Colonel bundled him into a ticca-gharri and "despatched him alone to St Xavier's" (007-049).

On the journey, Kim confronted his own fractured identity for the first time:

> "No; I am Kim. This is the great world, and I am only Kim. Who is Kim?" He considered his own identity, a thing he had never done before, till his head swam. He was one insignificant person in all this roaring whirl of India, going southward to he knew not what fate. (007-031)

### Life at St. Xavier's

The great old school of St. Xavier's in Partibus stood in vast grounds over against the Gumti River — "block on block of low white buildings" (007-056). Within its walls, Kim entered a world utterly unlike the Lahore bazaars. His schoolmates were the sons of railway officials, telegraph supervisors, warrant-officers, and planters — "two or three hundred precocious youths, most of whom had never seen the sea" (007-084).

Kim adapted with the same quick cunning he had always shown. He learned to write fair English. He was indicted for smoking and for using abuse "more full-flavoured than even St Xavier's had ever heard" (007-084). He learned to wash with the scrupulosity of the native-born. And quietly, persistently, "he measured himself against his self-reliant mates" (007-084).

### Two educations

Kim's formal education at St. Xavier's was only half the story. During the long holidays, when other boys returned to their families, Kim went north — to the bazaars, to Mahbub Ali's stable, to Lurgan Sahib's shop in Simla. There he received a second education: the crafts of the Great Game.

Hindu and Mohammedan costume, the memory game, the recognition of jewels and facial types, the ability to pass unnoticed through any crowd — these were not taught at St. Xavier's. They were taught by Lurgan Sahib's patience and Mahbub Ali's rough wisdom. The boy who could recite passages of Caesar was also the boy who could recite, from memory, the contents of a tray after a single glance.

This dual curriculum — Sahib by day, operative by holiday — made Kim something rare: a young man who could sit at a British officer's table and, an hour later, vanish into the bazaars as a low-caste Hindu boy. He was becoming, in Hurree Babu's later phrase, "a Friend of all the World" in a deeper sense than the Lahore street children had ever meant.

## Consequences

- **Kim becomes literate and credentialed.** His three years at St. Xavier's give him the formal education necessary to operate in British official circles — the language, the manners, the paperwork of empire.

- **The lama's investment is fulfilled.** The three hundred rupees a year, scraped together from the Temple at Benares, transforms Kim from a street boy into a Sahib. The lama's faith in his chela is validated.

- **Kim's dual identity deepens.** St. Xavier's sharpens the tension between Kim's white identity (reinforced by school discipline) and his native identity (maintained during holidays with Mahbub and Lurgan). This tension becomes the central psychological drama of the novel.

- **The Great Game gains a uniquely trained agent.** No other operative in Creighton's network can move as fluently between worlds as Kim. His schooling, combined with his street upbringing, makes him irreplaceable.
