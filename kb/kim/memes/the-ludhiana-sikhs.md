## Overview

The Ludhiana Sikhs were a regiment of the British Indian Army, composed primarily of Sikh soldiers from the Ludhiana district of the Punjab. Kim and the Teshoo Lama encountered soldiers of this regiment during their train journey from Lahore to Umballa. (002-042)

## The Soldiers on the Train

A Sikh artisan traveling in the third-class carriage spoke proudly of the regiment, rolling out its name sonorously. A young Dogra soldier, also aboard, recounted a recent border action at the Pirzai Kotal where the Dogra companies of the Ludhiana Sikhs had acquitted themselves well, facing eight Afridi standards on the ridge. The soldier described how the regiment had "schooled" their opponents after the enemy had marked their dead, and spoke of a Mohammedan regiment that had served alongside them at the same battle. (002-042)(002-049)(002-090)

## Military Character

The Sikh soldiers of the regiment maintained their distinctive identity, captured in the soldier's blunt declaration:

> "We of the Ludhiana Sikhs" — he rolled it out sonorously — "do not trouble our heads with doctrine. We fight."
>
> (002-042)

The proverb "let thy hair grow long and talk Punjabi, that is all that makes a Sikh" was quoted in their presence. The regiment represented the British Indian Army's practice of recruiting specific ethnic and religious communities into distinct units, each with their own traditions and martial reputation. (002-101)(002-190)
