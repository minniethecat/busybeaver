## The Song of Nikal Seyn

As the old soldier rides with Kim and the lama along the Grand Trunk Road, he sings "the song of Nikal Seyn before Delhi — the old song." (003-184) Kipling transcribes the refrain: "*Ahi! Nikal Seyn is dead — he died before Delhi! Lances of the North, take vengeance for Nikal Seyn.*" (003-185) The old soldier marks the trills "with the flat of his sword on the pony's rump," transforming the dusty road into a memorial for a general dead three decades. (003-185)

> And they fared out from the gloom of the mango-tope, the old man's high, shrill voice ringing across the field, as wail by long-drawn wail he unfolded the story of Nikal Seyn [Nicholson] — the song that men sing in the Punjab to this day. Kim was delighted, and the lama listened with deep interest.

> (003-185)

## A Folk Hero of the Punjab

Nikal Seyn — John Nicholson — was a British officer whose death leading the assault on Delhi during the Mutiny made him a legend in the Punjab. The "song that men sing in the Punjab to this day" (003-185) preserves his memory in the vernacular tradition, not in official histories. The old soldier's choice to sing it aloud, sword in hand, shows how Nicholson became a figure of Punjabi martial culture, transcending his official role.
