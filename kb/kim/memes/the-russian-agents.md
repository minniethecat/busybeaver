**The Russian Agents** are the principal antagonists of the final section of Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. Two foreign intelligence officers — one Russian, one French — they enter the Himalayan borderlands under the cover of a hunting expedition, carrying rifles and survey equipment. (012-149)

Their arrogance and cultural ignorance bring them into direct conflict with Kim and the lama, triggering the novel's climactic sequence.

## Two Strangers in the Hills

Word of the foreign agents first reaches Kim through [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]], who reports their movements to Colonel Creighton. "Over the Passes this year after snow-melting come two strangers under cover of shooting wild goats.

They bear guns, but they bear also chains and levels and compasses." (012-149) The survey equipment betrayed their true purpose: reconnaissance and mapping of the sensitive frontier region, part of the long rivalry between the British and Russian empires that defined the Great Game.

Hurree Babu, who had been tracking the pair's route, confided his unease to Kim: "They are Russians, and highly unscrupulous people.

I — I do not want to consort with them without a witness." (012-157) He had deduced their itinerary through process of elimination: from Leh down the Indus to Hanlé, then into the Chini valley — the only route by which they could approach Simla from the north.

(013-024) "You see, one Russian is a Frenchman," the Babu noted, "and I know my French pretty well." (012-168) With this information, the Babu set out ahead — umbrella bobbing, patent-leather shoes gleaming — to intercept the two men before they reached Simla.

## The Confrontation at Ziglaur

After days of tracking, the Babu finally presented himself to the two sodden and rheumatic foreigners at their camp at Ziglaur, introducing himself as "agent for His Royal Highness, the Rajah of Rampur." (013-033)

The men — one "visibly French, the other Russian" (013-034) — were stranded: their native servants had fallen sick, their coolies had deserted, and they were thoroughly miserable in the Himalayan rain.

The Babu, with his umbrella and patent-leather shoes, struck them as absurd: "Decidedly this fellow is an original. He is like the nightmare of a Viennese courier." (013-049) The Russian responded with cultural arrogance: "He represents *in petto* India in transition — the monstrous hybridism of East and West. It is *we* who can deal with Orientals." (013-050)

The critical moment came when the foreigners encountered Kim and the lama on a hillside. The lama sat, monumental and calm, displaying the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]] — his painted chart of Buddhist cosmology.

The Frenchman was struck by the tableau: "It is like a picture for the birth of a religion — the first teacher and the first disciple." (013-059) But the Russian, driven by a deeper insecurity, grew agitated: "We have nowhere left our mark yet. Nowhere! *That*, do you understand, is what disquiets me." (013-060)

When the lama refused to sell the Wheel of Life, the Russian snatched at the chart — and tore it. "The lama rose at the insult; his hand went to the heavy iron pencase." (013-083) Before he could strike, the Russian hit the old man full in the face. Kim launched himself at the Russian, and the two rolled downhill locked together.

"The blow had waked every unknown Irish devil in the boy's blood." (013-084) The coolies, who had witnessed sacrilege, fled in terror. The confrontation had shattered the fragile peace of the hills.

## Aftermath and Humiliation

In the chaos that followed, the hill people stripped the foreigners of their baggage and guns. The Babu, playing his double role to perfection, pretended to rescue the Russian while actually ensuring that Kim's assault succeeded.

Later, back in Simla, the Babu recounted the operation with relish: "The bottoms of their trousers were *quite* torn, and old Nahan Rajah, he thought they were European soldiers deserting." (015-098) He had fed the hill people such tales that "all the hill-people believe all Russians are all beggars." (015-098)

The lama, reflecting on the encounter, saw it as a spiritual sign: "Ignorance and Lust met Ignorance and Lust upon the road, and they begat Anger.

The blow was a sign to me, who am no better than a strayed yak, that my place is not here." (014-102) The torn Wheel of Life — reduced to "no more than the breadth of my fingernail" (014-103) — became, in the lama's interpretation, evidence that his remaining attachment to the world of things had been stripped away.

## Role in the Novel

The Russian and French agents serve as the antagonists who crystallize the novel's final movement. They represent European ignorance of India — armed with chains and compasses, confident in their racial superiority, yet utterly outmaneuvered by the native intelligence network.

Their incompetence and brutality (striking a holy man, tearing a sacred object) drive the action toward its climax while demonstrating the superiority of Creighton's approach: indirect control through local knowledge, rather than the blunt instruments of imperial arrogance.

The Russian's outburst — "We have nowhere left our mark yet. Nowhere!" (013-060) — captures the existential anxiety that drives imperial competition. It is not territory he craves but significance, a mark upon the world.

In striking the lama, he inadvertently sets in motion the spiritual conclusion of the novel: for the lama, the blow becomes proof that he must renounce the Hills and return to the plains, completing his Search.
