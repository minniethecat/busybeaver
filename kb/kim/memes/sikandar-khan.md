## Identity

Sikandar Khan is a horse trader and the head of a *kafila* (caravan) operating in northern India. He belongs to the network of traders, horse-dealers, and spies that [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] moves within. He is described as "old Sikandar Khan" by the courtesan known as the Flower of Delight.

## Appearances

### Chapter 1

Sikandar Khan is first mentioned in a night-time conversation between Mahbub Ali and the Flower of Delight at the Kashmir Serai in [[lahore|Lahore]]. The Flower, listing the horse-dealers who pass through her quarters, names Sikandar Khan alongside Nur Ali Beg and Farrukh Shah. Her dismissive description of each dealer provides a glimpse into the rough-and-ready world of the northern horse trade:

> "Farrukh Shah is a bear, Ali Beg a swashbuckler, and old Sikandar Khan---yaie!"
>
> (001-220)

### Chapter 8

Sikandar Khan reappears on the Kalka–Simla road. Mahbub Ali and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], travelling upward from Umballa, encounter him descending with "a few unsaleable screws — remnants of his string." Mahbub, who "has more of horse-coping in his little fingernail than Sikandar Khan in all his tents," engages in "eight hours' laborious diplomacy and untold tobacco" to buy two of the worst horses (008-160). The encounter is part of the leisurely, richly detailed journey that Kim remembers with delight as one of the happiest periods of his wandering life.

## Significance

Sikandar Khan is a minor but colourfully drawn figure in Kipling's portrait of the northern Indian horse trade — a world of hard bargaining, caravan routes, and rough camaraderie that forms part of the economic and cultural backdrop of Kim's India. His appearance in two widely separated chapters (the first and the eighth) suggests the continuity of this commercial network across the geography of the novel.
