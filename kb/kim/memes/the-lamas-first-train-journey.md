# The Lama's First Train Journey

At Lahore station, the Teshoo Lama boarded a railway carriage for the first time. He had pretended to be "well used to trains" (002-009), but the rattle of the iron horse terrified him. This moment of technological encounter — a Tibetan holy man confronting Raj machinery — marked the true beginning of his wanderings across India.

## Context

After the Wonder House encounter, Kim declared the lama's quest required travel: "By road and the trains" (001-084). Kim secured a timetable — "the written paper of the hours of the trains that go south" (001-088) — and planned their route.

The lama had crossed the Himalayas on foot. He understood mountains and the slow pace of pilgrimage. But the train — the iron serpent of the Raj — was entirely outside his experience.

## The Event

### Boarding

At Lahore station, the lama maintained his dignity. He had "pretended" familiarity with trains (002-009), but when the engine shuddered into motion, his composure cracked:

> "It is the train — only the te-rain. It will not come here" (002-010)

Kim reassured him. The noise, speed, and enclosed carriages disoriented a man who had spent his life in Tibet's high, quiet places.

A Sikh artisan's wife observed: "He has never entered a train. Oh, see!" (002-027). Other passengers viewed the lama as a curiosity — a holy man out of his element.

### Cultural collision

The carriage held a cross-section of India: a Sikh and his wife, a Hindu money-lender, a Mohammedan horse-dealer — the polyglot humanity of the Grand Trunk Road in miniature. For Kim, familiar; for the lama, bewildering.

Despite his fear, the lama endured without complaint. He trusted his young chela, who handled tickets, compartments, and passengers with practised ease.

### A new chapter

The train carried them from Lahore toward Umballa. On foot thereafter, they would travel the Grand Trunk Road. "And truly the Grand Trunk Road is a wonderful spectacle" (003-188).

## Consequences

The train launched the journey that anchors the novel — from Lahore to the Grand Trunk Road. Kim proved his worth by navigating tickets, seats, and the lama's fears. The journey captures the novel's central collision of ancient pilgrimage and imperial infrastructure. And though frightened, the lama endured.
