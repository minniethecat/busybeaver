# Taksali Gate

The Taksali Gate is one of the historic gates of the walled city of [[lahore|Lahore]]. In *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, it serves not as a described landmark but as a recurring reference point for the street culture of faquirs and the rough-and-ready education that shaped [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] during his years as a [[lahore|Lahore]] street boy.

## In *Kim*

The Taksali Gate enters the novel as a marker of [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s intimate knowledge of [[lahore|Lahore]]'s underclass. When [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] decides not to mock [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] but to help him, the narrator explains: "Had [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] been at all an ordinary boy, he would have carried on the play; but one does not know [[lahore|Lahore city]], and least of all the *faquirs* by the Taksali Gate, for thirteen years without also knowing human nature" (003-096). The gate, and the faquirs who gathered there, are presented as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s tutors in reading people.

The faquirs of the Taksali Gate become a touchstone for [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s mimicry and streetcraft. In Chapter 3, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] watches [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] negotiate with a train conductor and "copied the very inflection of their lewd disciples" — the faquirs by the Taksali Gate (003-103). In Chapter 4, when [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] needs a confidential tone to extract information, he drops into "the one, he well knew, that few could resist" — a manner described as "a direct plagiarism from a *faquir* by the Taksali Gate" (004-096).

The Gate's faquirs also represent the underside of [[lahore|Lahore]]'s spiritual economy. In Chapter 9, when [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]] demonstrates his ability to dilate and contract his pupils at will, the narrator notes: "There was a *faquir* by the Taksali Gate who had just this gift and made money by it, especially when cursing silly women" (009-011). [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] watches with professional interest, recognising the trick as one he has seen before among the gate's holy men.

In Chapter 11, during the exorcism scene in [[benares|Benares]], [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]] threatens the Kamboh with a curse: "This was part of an old curse picked up from a *faquir* by the Taksali Gate in the days of [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s innocence. It lost nothing by repetition" (011-205). The curse, drawn from [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s [[lahore|Lahore]] childhood, shows how the Gate's influence travels with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] across India.

> Had Kim been at all an ordinary boy, he would have carried on the play; but one does not know Lahore city, and least of all the faquirs by the Taksali Gate, for thirteen years without also knowing human nature.

> (003-096)

## Description

The Taksali Gate is one of the thirteen gates of the walled city of [[lahore|Lahore]], located on the eastern side of the old city. In Kipling's time it gave onto the area of bazaars and streets where faquirs, holy men, and beggars congregated — a natural gathering place for those living on the margins of the city's religious and commercial life. The name "Taksali" derives from the mint (*taksal*) that once operated near the gate.

## Historical Context

The Taksali Gate (also spelled Taxali Gate) was one of the original gates of [[lahore|Lahore]]'s walled city, built during the Mughal period. By the late nineteenth century, the area around the gate was known for its dense population of musicians, dancing-girls, and religious mendicants. Kipling, who lived in [[lahore|Lahore]] from 1882 to 1887 as a journalist for the *Civil and Military Gazette*, knew the old city intimately, and the Taksali Gate's faquirs would have been a familiar sight. The gate still stands today in modern [[lahore|Lahore]], Pakistan.
