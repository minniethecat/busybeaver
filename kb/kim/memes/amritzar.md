# Amritzar

**Amritzar** (modern Amritsar) is the holy city of the Sikh faith in the Punjab region. In Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, it is mentioned as the birthplace of Kim's father and features in the lively train journey scene at the beginning of the novel.

## In *Kim*

Amritzar is first mentioned when Kim, travelling with the lama on the train from Lahore, calls out "Now another to Amritzar" as he works the compartment for alms (002-014). The city is embodied in the character of the "Amritzar girl" — a courtesan who shares the train compartment and engages in sharp-tongued banter with the other passengers (002-022). She and the other travellers debate the nature of the lama's spiritual quest, providing comic relief and local colour. Later in the novel, Kim reveals that his father "came from Amritzar — by Jandiala," establishing a personal connection to the city (011-020).

> "Now another to Amritzar," said Kim, who had no notion of spending Mahbub Ali's money on anything so crude as a paid ride to Umballa. "The price is so much. The small money in return is just so much. I know the ways of the te-rain ... Never did yogi need chela as thou dost," he went on merrily to the bewildered lama.
>
> (002-014)

## Description

The novel does not describe Amritzar itself in detail — the city appears only through its inhabitants on the train. The Amritzar courtesan is portrayed as worldly, witty, and protective of the lama once she understands his holiness: she steps out at her destination with her bundle, having participated in the collective effort to guide the lama and Kim on their journey (002-059). Her presence evokes the cosmopolitan character of the Punjab, where Sikhs, Hindus, Muslims, and Europeans intersect on the arteries of the Grand Trunk Road and the railway.

## Historical Context

Amritsar, founded in 1577 by Guru Ram Das, the fourth Sikh Guru, is the spiritual centre of Sikhism and home to the Harmandir Sahib (the Golden Temple). In the late 19th century, when *Kim* is set, Amritsar was a major commercial and railway junction in the Punjab province of British India. The city's railway station, opened in 1862, connected Amritsar to Lahore, Delhi, and the broader Indian rail network — the same network that Kim and the lama use to begin their pilgrimage.
