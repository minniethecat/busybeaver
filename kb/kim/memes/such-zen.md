## Location and Description

Such-zen is a Buddhist monastery (lamassery) situated in the high mountains of Tibet, opposite the Painted Rocks. It serves as the spiritual home and administrative seat of Teshoo Lama, who was its Abbot before embarking on his pilgrimage to find the River of the Arrow. The lama describes the monastery as his place of origin and authority, repeatedly invoking his title as "Abbot of Such-zen" when exercising spiritual authority. (001-053) (013-106)

> The lama, haltingly at first, spoke to the Curator of his own lamassery, the Such-zen, opposite the Painted Rocks, four months' march away. The Curator brought out a huge book of photos and showed him that very place, perched on its crag, overlooking the gigantic valley of many-hued strata.
>
> (001-053)

## The Lama's Connection

The lama spent much of his life at Such-zen before, as he recounts, he "rose to the Great Renunciation." It was there that he acquired his spiritual knowledge and authority, and the memory of its high hills and painted rocks remains vivid throughout his wanderings in India. When traveling through the Himalayas in Chapter 13, the lama compares the grand mountain landscapes to those of Such-zen, remarking that beside his home monastery, these great peaks are "flatter than a rice-field." (001-053) (002-173) (013-008)

## Abbot's Authority

The lama's former position as Abbot of Such-zen carries significant weight both in Tibet and among the hill Buddhists of the Himalayas. When the Russian spy strikes him in Chapter 13, the lama's moral authority—grounded in his abbotship—prevents the hillmen from taking violent revenge. Later, when physically and spiritually wounded, he reflects on his identity as "I who was Abbot of Such-zen," drawing strength from his former office. (013-106) (014-100) (015-052)

## The Painted Rocks

The Painted Rocks, situated opposite Such-zen, are a notable landmark associated with the monastery. In his moment of enlightenment in Chapter 15, the lama sees in his vision "my own Painted Rocks at Such-zen" alongside every camp and village of his journey, suggesting the deep spiritual significance of this place in his consciousness. (015-184)
