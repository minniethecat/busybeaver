# Kashmir

Kashmir is a Himalayan region whose presence in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* is woven through its products, its people, and one notable natural event. While the novel never takes the reader into the Vale itself, Kashmir surfaces repeatedly — in the bustling [[the-serai|Kashmir Serai]] of [[lahore|Lahore]], in the earthquake that marked [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s birth, and in the valuable saffron traded across the subcontinent.

> An earthquake in Srinagar which is in Kashmir.
>
> (002-181)

## In *Kim*

Kashmir enters the novel obliquely but meaningfully. The most personal connection comes through [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s birth. [[kimball-ohara|Kimball O'Hara]], [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s father, tied the infant [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s life to a natural event: "upon the hour that I cried first fell the great earthquake in Srinagar which is in Kashmir" (002-181). The woman who cared for [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] repeated this story, and "the earthquake had been felt in India, and for long stood a leading date in [[punjab|the Punjab]]" (002-181). Kashmir thus provides the chronology of [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s earliest [[identity-and-disguise|identity]].

The region's people appear in the margins of [[lahore|Lahore]]'s intelligence world. In Chapter 1, [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]]'s downfall at the Gate of the Harpies involves "a smooth-faced Kashmiri pundit" (001-211), who aided in searching the drunken [[mahbub-ali|horse-trader]] for the coded message. The pundit's presence hints at the web of informants and intermediaries that spans from Kashmir to [[punjab|the Punjab]].

Kashmir's material wealth surfaces in Chapter 12, when [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]] lists his medicinal wares: "saffron from Kashmir, and the best salep of Kabul" (012-102). The saffron, prized across Asia, places Kashmir within the trade networks that the novel's characters traverse.

## Description

Kashmir is not described firsthand in the novel, but its associations are consistent: a northern, mountainous region famed for its saffron, its pundits, and its connection to [[the-serai|the Kashmir Serai]] — the great caravanserai in [[lahore|Lahore]] where Central Asian traders and travelers converged. The region sits beyond the plains, accessible through mountain passes like those [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] cross in Chapter 13, where "the snow-bred, shag-haired sort that came into [[the-serai|the Kashmir Serai]]" (013-027) Bactrian camels appear as a reminder of the northern trade routes.

## Historical Context

In the late nineteenth century, Kashmir was a princely state under the suzerainty of British India, ruled by Maharaja Pratap Singh (1885–1925). The Vale of Kashmir was renowned for its natural beauty, its high-altitude saffron cultivation, and its strategic position on trade routes between India, [[tibet|Tibet]], and Central Asia. Srinagar, the summer capital, was a centre of Kashmiri Brahmin scholarship — the "Kashmiri pundit" class that appears in Kipling's [[lahore|Lahore]] scenes.

The earthquake referenced in Chapter 2 may correspond to the 1885 Srinagar earthquake or an earlier seismic event used by Kipling as a narrative device to anchor [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s birthdate.

## Associated Characters

- [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] — his birth date is tied to the Srinagar earthquake
- [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] — frequented [[the-serai|the Kashmir Serai]] and was robbed by a Kashmiri pundit's associate

## Travel Routes

Kashmir lies north of [[punjab|the Punjab]], accessible through mountain passes. The Kashmir Serai in [[lahore|Lahore]] served as the southern terminus for caravans arriving from Kashmir and Central Asia, connecting the Vale to the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] and the railway network of British India.
