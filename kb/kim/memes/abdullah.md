## Identity

Abdullah is the son of a sweetmeat-seller in the Lahore bazar. He is a Mohammedan boy who forms part of the group of street children that includes [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[chota-lal|Chota Lal]]. Together they play king-of-the-castle on the great gun [[the-zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]] outside the [[lahore|Lahore]] Museum.

## Role in the Opening Scene

Abdullah appears in the opening pages of the novel, where the street children jostle for possession of Zam-Zammah. He clambers up the gun's wheel and is one of the first to notice the approach of [[the-teshoo-lama|the Teshoo Lama]]. His reactions help establish the street-level perspective through which Kipling introduces the lama's strange appearance.

Kipling gives Abdullah a distinct voice in the children's dialogue, and the banter between the boys reveals the religious diversity of Lahore's street life:

> "That is true," said Abdullah. "It is full of heathen buts."
> "Būt-parast (idolater)," said Abdullah, the Mohammedan.
>
> (001-040)

## Religious Identity

Abdullah is explicitly identified as a Mohammedan. His comments about "heathen būts" (idols) and his use of the term "būt-parast" (idolater) mark him as a Muslim child who distinguishes himself from the Hindu and Buddhist figures around him. This religious self-identification mirrors the broader religious landscape of Kipling's Lahore, where communities coexist in close quarters.

## Significance

Though a minor character limited to the novel's opening scene, Abdullah serves as part of the ensemble that establishes Kim's world — a Lahore street culture of mixed religions, sharp humour, and childhood camaraderie. His inclusion alongside Chota Lal (who also has a wiki page) completes the named members of Kim's Zam-Zammah play-group.
