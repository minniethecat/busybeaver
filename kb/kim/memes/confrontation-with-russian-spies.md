# Confrontation with the Russian Spies

In the shadow of the Himalayan passes, two foreign agents — one Russian, one French — had crossed into British Indian territory under the cover of a shooting expedition. They carried chains, levels, compasses, and maps: the tools of military survey, not sport (012-149). Their mission was to chart the northern approaches, and in doing so they collided with Kim, the lama, and the Babu Hurree Chunder Mookerjee — agents of the Great Game. What began as a polite encounter on a hillside ended in violence: the Russian tore the lama's sacred diagram, struck the holy man across the face, and found himself rolling downhill with Kim at his throat.

## Context

The Great Game — the shadow war of intelligence between Britain and Russia in Central Asia — had been gathering momentum throughout Kim's adolescence. Mahbub Ali's coded messages, Creighton's Ethnological Survey, and Hurree Babu's patient intelligence-gathering were all directed against the threat of Russian infiltration across the northern passes.

The Babu had been tracking the two strangers since they crossed into the hills. He had met them, ingratiated himself, and sent word south: "Come over the Passes this year after snow-melting come two strangers under cover of shooting wild goats. They bear guns, but they bear also chains and levels and compasses" (012-149). He understood his peril — "They are Russians, and highly unscrupulous people. I do not want to consort with them without a witness" (012-157) — but he also knew that Kim and the lama were travelling north. A plan formed: the Babu would walk ahead as a stranger, Kim and the lama would follow, and between them they would have the foreigners covered.

Kim, for his part, was exhilarated. Lying in the dark, he reflected: "Truly, it runs like a shuttle throughout all Hind. And my share and my joy I owe to the lama here. Also to Mahbub Ali — also to Creighton Sahib, but chiefly to the Holy One" (012-168).

## The Event

### The meeting on the hillside

The gentlemen were delighted when the Babu presented himself. "One was visibly French, the other Russian, but they spoke English not much inferior to the Babu's" (013-034). They were anxious to push on to Simla and dismissive of the Babu's elaborate deference. In French, they spoke freely: "When we get into India proper thou wilt see" (013-040). They did not know that the Babu understood every word.

The Babu kept his eyes on "a kilta full of maps and documents — an extra-large one with a double red oil-skin cover. He did not wish to steal anything. He only desired to know what to steal, and, incidentally, how to get away when he had stolen it" (013-052).

### The lama's sacred diagram

When Kim and the lama arrived at the same camp, the foreigners' attention shifted. The lama sat drawing — a great Wheel of Life, intricate with symbols. The Frenchman was struck by the scene: "Look! It is like a picture for the birth of a religion — the first teacher and the first disciple" (013-059). But the Russian saw things differently: "We have nowhere left our mark yet. Nowhere! That, do you understand, is what disquiets me" (013-060). The lama's monumental calm reproached him, and his resentment kindled.

The Russian offered rupees for the diagram. The lama, uncomprehending, began to fold up the Wheel. Then the Russian "drew out a handful of rupees, and snatched half-jestingly at the chart, which tore in the lama's grip" (013-082). A murmur of horror rose from the hillmen coolies — the Written Word had been defiled.

### The blow

> "It was too late. Before Kim could ward him off, the Russian struck the old man full on the face. Next instant he was rolling over and over downhill with Kim at his throat. The blow had waked every unknown Irish devil in the boy's blood" (013-084).

The hillside dissolved into chaos. The Frenchman ran toward the lama, fumbling at his revolver, but a shower of cutting stones from the coolies drove him back. The hillmen fled, dragging the lama with them. The Babu, in a moment of inspired absurdity, cast himself bodily upon Kim — ostensibly to separate the combatants, in reality to ensure that Kim did not kill the Russian outright: "All right, sar! All right! Don't shoot. I go to rescue" (013-087).

The coolies, terrified at the sacrilege, wailed: "He struck the Holy One — we saw it! Our cattle will be barren — our wives will cease to bear! The snows will slide upon us as we go home" (013-098).

### Aftermath in the hills

The lama, half-stunned, whispered to Kim under the pines: "I have come near to great evil, chela. I was tempted to loose the bullet; and truly, in Tibet there would have been a heavy and a slow death for them... He struck me across the face... upon the flesh" (013-110). The blow became a spiritual sign: the lama concluded it meant the Hills were not for him, that his place was in the Plains.

## Consequences

- **The Russian/French expedition is disrupted.** Their maps and documents fall into the hands of the Great Game network; their survey of the northern passes is compromised.

- **The lama turns back toward the Plains.** He concludes: "The Arrow fell in the Plains — not in the Hills. Therefore, what make we here?" (014-107). This redirection leads directly to his final discovery of the River.

- **Kim proves himself in physical combat.** His furious defence of the lama demonstrates a depth of loyalty beyond his spy-training — the Irish devils of his blood, long dormant, have been awakened.

- **The Babu recovers the documents.** In the confusion, Hurree Babu secures the foreigners' maps and papers, completing the intelligence mission that brought him into the hills.
