## Geographic Significance

Kedarnath is one of the great Himalayan peaks that dominate the skyline on the northern mountain route traversed by the lama and Kim in Chapter 13. Together with its twin peak Badrinath, it is described as one of the "kings of that wilderness"—a massive, snow-capped presence that takes the first sunlight at dawn and glows "like molten silver" through the day before putting on "jewels" at sunset. (013-009)

> In the dawns they flared windy-red above stark blue, as Kedarnath and Badrinath — kings of that wilderness — took the first sunlight. All day long they lay like molten silver under the sun, and at evening put on their jewels again.
>
> (013-009)

## Narrative Role

The peaks of Kedarnath and Badrinath serve as silent sentinels throughout the mountain journey. Despite the travelers' extensive marchings through the hills, the two peaks remain seemingly unchanged—Kim observes after days of travel that only from a ten-thousand-foot hummock can he detect that "a shoulder-knot or horn of the two great lords had—ever so slightly—changed outline." This immensity underscores the scale of the Himalayan landscape and the insignificance of human movement within it. (013-027)

## Later Reference

In Chapter 15, the roaring gusts of wind "off Kedarnath" are mentioned as a force of nature in the high mountains, contributing to the harsh conditions of the Himalayan environment through which the characters journey. (015-008)
