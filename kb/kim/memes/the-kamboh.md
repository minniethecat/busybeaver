## Identity and Background

The Kamboh is a chance-met Punjabi farmer "from Jullundur-way" who joins [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and the [[teshoo-lama|lama]] on their journey in Chapter 11. (011-018) "Kamboh" refers to his agricultural caste rather than a personal name—he is always identified by this communal designation. He wears a blue robe and carries himself with the sturdy independence of a prosperous peasant farmer. (011-109)

> Kim was guided to the Temple of the Tirthankars, about a mile outside the city, near Sarnath, by a chance-met Punjabi farmer — a Kamboh from Jullundur-way who had appealed in vain to every God of his homestead to cure his small son, and was trying Benares as a last resort.
>
> (011-018)

## Traveling Companion

The Kamboh becomes an integral member of the traveling party, his practical nature complementing the lama's spirituality and Kim's cunning. He "plied Kim with ten thousand questions concerning the walk and the works" of the lama, demonstrating the mixture of curiosity and skepticism characteristic of rural Punjab. (011-125) Kim performs tricks and tells tales "before the lama and the admiring Kamboh," using his street-learned skills to entertain and impress. (011-126)

## His Child's Sickness

The Kamboh's deepest concern is for his young child, who falls gravely ill during the journey. The child is struck by fever and appears near death, which terrifies the superstitious farmer. (011-160) When the Kamboh jealously questions whether Kim's chanting has any real power, it reveals his desperate hope for supernatural intervention. (011-161)

Kim, drawing on the medical knowledge he learned from [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]], prepares a remedy, mixing "the Kamboh's flour with the mingled drugs." (011-175) The child recovers, and the grateful Kamboh emerges "like a buffalo from his wallow," his faith in his companions restored. (011-195) Kim later reflects with satisfaction: "I saved the Kamboh's child." (012-037)

## Character and Wisdom

Though initially skeptical of the lama's spiritual quest, the Kamboh proves himself a man of practical intelligence and genuine warmth. He is a skilled cook, able to prepare "tarkeean" (vegetable curry), declaring confidently: "It is a good curry, as I know." (011-131) When rebuked, he accepts correction meekly: "I am thy debtor for one word." (011-143)

His relationship with the supernatural is revealing—he is "grossly superstitious" like many of his class (011-164), yet also pragmatic enough to recognize genuine help when it comes. The Kamboh represents the ordinary people of rural India whom Kim learns to understand and move among: neither spy nor holy man, but the fabric of the land itself.

## Disappearance

The Kamboh vanishes from the narrative during the tense events at the railway station in Chapter 12. When Kim asks "Where is the Kamboh gone, Holy One?" the lama replies simply that "a fear gripped him." (012-034) (012-035) The farmer, overwhelmed by the dangerous spy drama unfolding around him, quietly departs—his role as a traveling companion complete, his child saved, his brief intersection with the Great Game ended.
