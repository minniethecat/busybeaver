The **Ethnological Survey** (also referred to as the Ethnological Survey of India) is Colonel Creighton's official department, which serves as a cover for British intelligence operations in the Great Game. While ostensibly dedicated to ethnographic and anthropological research, the Survey functions as the institutional front for gathering military and political intelligence across northern India and the Himalayan frontier. (006-121)(006-152)

## In *Kim*

The Ethnological Survey first appears when Father Victor identifies it as the organisation employing the enigmatic figure who turns out to be Colonel Creighton. The Survey is presented as a legitimate scholarly institution, but those familiar with the Great Game recognise its deeper purpose. Creighton himself is described as heading the Survey, and Kim later becomes affiliated with it as a chain-man — a junior surveyor whose duties conveniently require travel through strategically sensitive regions. (006-121)(006-152)(009-142)

> "Of the Ethnological Survey?" said Father Victor. The Englishman's brows were bent.
>
> (006-121)

## R.17 and the Locked Books

Section R.17 of the Ethnological Survey contained the locked books that catalogued intelligence data. The Survey's official scientific mission — collecting ethnological notes and mapping terrain — provided perfect cover for observing troop movements, political allegiances, and the activities of Russian agents along the northern frontier. (009-142) The locked ledgers of R.17 represent the institutional memory of the Great Game, accessible only to those within Creighton's trusted circle.

## Historical Basis

The real Ethnological Survey of India was established in the late 19th century as a genuine anthropological institution. Kipling transforms this historical organisation into a narrative device, blending the actual scholarly work of colonial ethnography with the clandestine world of political espionage. This dual nature reflects a broader theme in *Kim*: the ambiguous boundary between knowledge-gathering and intelligence-gathering in the colonial context.
