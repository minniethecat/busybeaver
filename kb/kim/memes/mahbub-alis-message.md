# Mahbub Ali's Message

In Lahore, the horse-trader Mahbub Ali entrusts the young Kim with a secret message to be delivered to an Englishman at Umballa. Kim accepts the task without knowing its contents, setting in motion his first act of espionage and his entry into the Great Game — the shadowy contest of intelligence between Britain and Russia in Central Asia.

## Context

Kim had long been a familiar figure in the back alleys of Lahore, known as the "Little Friend of all the World" (001-007), running errands for men of fashion who conducted "intrigue" by night. Among them, Mahbub Ali the Pashtun horse-dealer was the most formidable — a man Kim knew to be more than a mere trader.

When Kim met the Tibetan lama at the Lahore Museum and resolved to accompany him on his spiritual quest, he first sought funds. He went to Mahbub Ali's stable near the railway station, where the big red-bearded Afghan received him with characteristic gruffness (001-163). Mahbub fed the lama and, as the holy man slept, drew Kim aside.

> "And if thou wilt carry a message for me as far as Umballa, I will give thee money. It concerns a horse — a white stallion which I have sold to an officer of the Creighton Sahib's" (001-197).

Kim accepted, though he understood this was no ordinary horse-trade. He had seen Mahbub compose coded telegrams: "Creighton, Laurel Bank, Umballa. Horse is Arabian as already advised" (001-204). The message was intelligence, and Kim — though he did not yet know the phrase — was being recruited into the Great Game.

## The Event

### Kim carries the message

The lama obtained a railway ticket for Kim to Umballa (002-005). On the train, Kim encountered diverse travellers, but he kept the folded paper hidden. That night, when the train halted and the passengers slept, Kim sat apart and examined the message:

> "His face, by the full ray of the moon, was altered. He looked on the papers, not as a little vagabond, but as a man" (002-146).

It was then that a shadow moved nearby. Kim froze — the message was open, its coded contents exposed. Two agents of a rival power had been following the paper, and one now crept close to read it (002-154). Kim had stumbled into a world where messages were watched, and boys who carried them could vanish.

At Umballa the train emptied, and Kim moved swiftly through the unfamiliar streets. He located the bungalow on the pretext of asking for water, delivered the message, and slipped away. Colonel Creighton took the paper without betraying recognition — a transaction so brief that Kim, for all his street cunning, half-wondered whether he had imagined the weight of the thing he carried.

### The message confirmed

Years later, in the shamiana of the Mavericks regiment, Mahbub Ali himself confirmed what Kim had done:

> "I sent him to deliver a message once from Lahore. A message concerning the pedigree of a white stallion" (006-112).

And Kim, recalling the event, remembered the fear and the thrill of that moonlit carriage: "Even when I was wounded, I remembered the pedigree" (008-071). The message had reached its destination. Kim had proven himself — a boy who could be trusted with secrets.

## Consequences

The delivery of Mahbub Ali's message had far-reaching consequences:

- **Kim entered the Great Game.** The success of this first mission marked Kim in Mahbub's estimation as a potential agent. Colonel Creighton took notice. Within a year, Kim would be sent to St. Xavier's for formal education, then to Lurgan Sahib for advanced training in disguise and memory.

- **The lama's journey acquired a hidden dimension.** The lama believed Kim was simply a chela helping him find the River of the Arrow. But Kim now carried a second, secret purpose — one that would shadow the pilgrimage through every stage (002-069).

- **Mahbub's network tightened.** The message contained intelligence about Russian movements. Its safe delivery allowed Creighton's network to act, furthering the Great Game in the passes north of Leh (008-105).
