# The Wheel of Life

**The Wheel of Life** (also called the Wheel of Things) is the central Buddhist concept that drives [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s [[pilgrimage|spiritual journey]] in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. It represents the endless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara) from which [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] seeks liberation through [[the-search-for-the-river|finding the River]] of the Arrow.

## In the Novel

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] of [[tibet|Tibet]] dedicates his life to freeing himself from the Wheel of Life. As a Buddhist monk, he believes that attachment to worldly things keeps souls bound to the cycle of reincarnation. His [[pilgrimage]] across India with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is not merely a physical journey but a spiritual quest to transcend this wheel.

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] explains his mission early in the novel: he seeks [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]], a miraculous river that will wash away his remaining attachments and free him from the Wheel. His search is described as one made "by these books, following the Blessed Feet in all their wanderings" (001-099), though he acknowledges there are "things which they have not sought out."

Throughout the novel, the Wheel of Life serves as the spiritual counterpoint to the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. While [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] navigates the world of espionage and politics, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] pursues liberation from the very world that [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is learning to manipulate. This tension creates the novel's central philosophical dialectic — the temporal versus the eternal.

## Key Passages

> "I will bring thee a written picture of the Padma Samthora — such as I used to make on silk at the lama's house — showing the Wheel of Life." (001-099)
> — The lama to the curator, describing the traditional Buddhist diagram

> "To those who follow the Way there is neither black nor white, Hind nor Bhotiyal. We be all souls seeking escape from the Wheel of Things." (012-052)
> — The lama expressing the universality of the Buddhist path

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s Wheel of Life diagram is described as a traditional Buddhist painting showing the six realms of existence, the twelve links of dependent origination, and the path to liberation. The novel portrays [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] as a skilled artist of such diagrams, which he creates as acts of devotion.

## Related Characters

- **[[Kimball O'Hara]]** — The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s chela (disciple), who is gradually influenced by [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual teachings

## Symbolism

The Wheel of Life symbolizes the totality of conditioned existence in Buddhist philosophy. In the novel, it represents:
- **Spiritual aspiration**: The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s quest to transcend worldly suffering
- **Contrast to the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]**: While the [[the-great-game|Great Game]] represents worldly entanglement, the Wheel represents the desire to escape it
- **Universal condition**: The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] teaches that all beings — Indian, [[tibet|Tibet]]an, English — are equally bound to the Wheel
