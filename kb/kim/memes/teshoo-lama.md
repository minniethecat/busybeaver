# Teshoo Lama

The **Teshoo Lama** is the [[tibet|Tibet]]an Buddhist monk whose [[pilgrimage]] to find [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]] provides the spiritual framework of Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. As [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s guru, he represents the contemplative, otherworldly dimension of the novel — a counterweight to the political machinations of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]].

## Background

The lama comes from the Such-zen lamassery in [[tibet|Tibet]], where he served as abbot before embarking on his [[pilgrimage]]. He describes himself as having lived "in peace in our lamasseries" before setting out to "see the Four Holy Places" (001-036). His [[pilgrimage]] is motivated by a dream that directed him to find [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]], a sacred stream whose waters can wash away sin and free the soul from the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]].

He is skilled in creating traditional Buddhist diagrams, promising to make "a written picture of the Padma Samthora... showing the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]]" (001-099) for [[the-curator|the Lahore curator]]. Before his [[pilgrimage]], he had not traveled outside [[tibet|Tibet]], making India an entirely new world to him.

## Role in the Narrative

The lama first appears outside the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]], where [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], intrigued by his distinctive appearance and spiritual mission, volunteers to become his chela (disciple). Their relationship defines the novel. Upon seeing Buddhist artifacts at the museum, the lama reacts with profound emotion: "It is Sakya Muni himself," the lama half sobbed (001-046).

Together they travel the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]], the lama seeking his River while [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] navigates the parallel world of espionage. The lama's teachings about the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]] and the illusory nature of [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] (012-052) offer a philosophical framework that challenges the worldly values of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. His absolute sincerity and unworldliness are portrayed as a form of strength rather than weakness.

The lama insists that spiritual truth transcends all human categories: "There is neither black nor white, Hind nor [[tibet|Bhotiyal]]. We be all souls seeking escape from the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Things]]" (012-052). This universalism forms the novel's deepest ethical statement.

In the novel's climax, the lama achieves his spiritual breakthrough — a vision of [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]] — which he describes as the supreme moment of his life (015-058). This achievement, coming at the same time as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s espionage triumph, suggests that spiritual and worldly success can coexist.

## Relationships

- **[[Kimball O'Hara]]** — The lama's beloved chela (disciple), whom he calls "[[kimball-ohara|Friend of All the World]]"
- **[[The Curator]]** — The [[lahore|Lahore]] museum scholar who recognizes the lama's Buddhist learning
- **[[Mahbub Ali]]** — The lama tolerates him as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s friend but remains wary of his worldly influence

## Key Passages

> "It is Sakya Muni himself," the lama half sobbed; and under his breath began the wonderful Buddhist invocation. (001-046)

> "We be all souls seeking escape from the Wheel of Things. There is neither black nor white, Hind nor Bhotiyal." (012-052)

> "I have found the River — the River of the Arrow!" (015-058)
