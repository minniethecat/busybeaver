# Nilang Pass

The Nilang Pass is a high Himalayan crossing that appears in the final chapter of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* as one of the formidable obstacles on [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s journey through the mountains. It is mentioned in a retrospective passage that catalogues the hardships of the mountain travel, placing the pass among the most dramatic landscapes of the novel.

## In *Kim*

The Nilang Pass appears in Chapter 15, when the narrator looks back over the journey that [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] have made through the hills. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]], seeking [[the-search-for-the-river|the River of the Arrow]], has been carried in a *dooli* (palanquin) through the high Himalaya.

The narrative describes the devotion of the hill-folk who have borne [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]: a worn litter rests "on the edge of the Doon, [[mussoorie|Mussoorie]] well behind them and the Plains spread out in golden dust before" — bearing "a sick [[teshoo-lama|lama]] who seeks a River for his healing" (015-007).

The passage that names the Nilang Pass is a sweeping catalogue of the terrain the party has crossed with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]:

"Over the Nilang Pass in storm when the driven snow-dust filled every fold of the impassive [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s drapery; between the black horns of Raieng where they heard the whistle of the wild goats through the clouds; pitching and strained on the shale below..." (015-008).

The catalogue continues: "...hard-held between shoulder and clenched jaw when they rounded the hideous curves of the Cut Road under Bhagirati; swinging and creaking to the steady jog-trot of the descent into the Valley of the Waters..." (015-008).

The Nilang Pass is listed first among the ordeals — its storm of driven snow-dust establishing the severity of the mountain crossing. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s drapery filling with snow yet his demeanour remaining "impassive" captures the spiritual endurance that defines his character throughout the novel, even as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and the hill-folk porters strain physically against the terrain.

At the journey's end, the cost is visible on everyone. "[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] — his face is drawn and tired — pays very small silver from his belt, heaves out the food-bag... and helps [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] to his feet. The peace has come again into the old man's eyes" (015-010).

The Nilang Pass is the gateway to the most arduous section of this journey — the high Himalayan crossing that tests body and spirit alike before the descent into the kinder climate of the lower hills.

> Over the Nilang Pass in storm when the driven snow-dust filled every fold of the impassive lama's drapery; between the black horns of Raieng where they heard the whistle of the wild goats through the clouds; pitching and strained on the shale below; hard-held between shoulder and clenched jaw when they rounded the hideous curves of the Cut Road under Bhagirati; swinging and creaking to the steady jog-trot of the descent into the Valley of the Waters...
>
> (015-008)

## Description

The Nilang Pass is situated in the Garhwal Himalaya of modern Uttarakhand, India, at an elevation of approximately 5,000 metres (16,400 feet). It lies on the route between the Bhagirathi Valley and the Jad Ganga (Nilang) Valley, near the border with [[tibet|Tibet]].

The pass is snowbound for much of the year and subject to sudden storms even in summer. The terrain includes shale slopes, steep descents, and the "hideous curves" of the mountain roads that Kipling describes.

## Historical Context

In Kipling's time, the Garhwal Himalaya was part of the princely state of Tehri Garhwal, under British suzerainty. The high passes of the region were used by [[tibet|Tibet]]an traders and [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s bound for the holy sites of Kailash.

The Nilang Pass lay on a traditional route between the Indian plains and the [[tibet|Tibet]]an plateau. Its mention in the novel anchors [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual quest in the real geography of the Himalaya.
