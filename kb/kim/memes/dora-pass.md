# Dora Pass

The Dora Pass is a mountain pass on the North-West Frontier that appears in the opening chapter of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* as the route by which [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] carries a vital piece of intelligence into British India. Though mentioned only once, the pass symbolises the porous frontier across which the clandestine traffic of [[the-great-game|the Great Game]] flows.

## In *Kim*

The Dora Pass enters the novel in a single but loaded sentence. As [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] rides toward [[lahore|Lahore]] with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], the narrator reveals what the [[mahbub-ali|horse-trader]] carries.

It is an unaddressed statement with five pin-holes that "most scandalously betrayed the five confederated Kings, the sympathetic Northern Power, a Hindu banker in Peshawur, a firm of gun-makers in Belgium, and an important, semi-independent Mohammedan ruler to the south" (001-203).

This was R17's work, which Mahbub had "picked up beyond the Dora Pass" and was carrying to [[umballa|Umballa]] (001-203). The pass sits at the threshold between the arena of espionage and the network of railways and serais through which agents moved information.

[[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] was "registered in one of the locked books of the Indian Survey Department as C25 IB" (001-201), part of the apparatus that received and acted on frontier intelligence.

The report Mahbub carries is described as more dangerous than dynamite: "Dynamite was milky and innocuous beside that report of C25" (001-203). The pass is the conduit of a payload capable of triggering military mobilisation.

When the report reaches [[umballa|Umballa]], the General responds: "Warn the Pined and Peshawar brigades. It will disorganize all the summer reliefs, but we can't help that... Eight thousand should be enough" (002-157).

> Mahbub had picked up beyond the Dora Pass and was carrying in for R17, who, owing to circumstances over which he had no control, could not leave his post of observation. Dynamite was milky and innocuous beside that report of C25; and even an Oriental, with an Oriental's views of the value of time, could see that the sooner it was in the proper hands the better.
>
> (001-203)

## Description

The Dora Pass (also known as the Dorah Pass) is a high mountain pass in the Hindu Kush, situated on the border between modern Pakistan and Afghanistan. It connects the Chitral district of Pakistan with the Wakhan Corridor of Afghanistan.

At an elevation of approximately 4,300 metres (14,100 feet), it is a formidable crossing, passable only in summer and even then demanding endurance from travellers and their animals.

## Historical Context

In Kipling's time, the Dora Pass lay in one of the most sensitive frontier zones of the British Empire. Chitral, to the south, had been the site of the Chitral Expedition of 1895, in which a small British garrison was besieged and relieved.

The passes of the Hindu Kush — the Dora, the Baroghil, and others — were the routes by which Russian influence could seep south toward India. Their control was central to British frontier policy. Kipling's mention of the Dora Pass would have resonated with contemporary readers familiar with the Chitral campaign.
