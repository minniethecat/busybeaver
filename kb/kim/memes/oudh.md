## A Region of Landholders

Oudh (Awadh) is a historical region in what is now Uttar Pradesh, North India. In *Kim*, it appears primarily as a marker of social class and geography. The Kulu woman, denying the bazaar rumour that Hill Rajahs sell their womenfolk, scoffs that the practice belongs to the plains: "Down south they sell them — to zemindars and such — all of Oudh." (004-087)

> Down south they sell them---to zemindars and such---all of Oudh.
>
> (004-087)

## The Oudh Landholder Class

The region's landholding class features in the novel's social landscape. Father Victor, describing to Kim the composition of the Mavericks regiment, notes that the "officers were large landholders in Oudh or the Deccan." (007-085) This detail places Oudh within the network of agrarian wealth that supplied the British Indian Army's officer corps — a world into which Kim is being inducted.

## Kim's Assumed Identity

In an extended passage of disguise and role-play, Kim passes himself off as "the son of an Oudh landholder in the fullest of Native dress," a performance that allows him to move through Indian society with a borrowed respectability. (009-114) The Oudh landholder persona becomes one of Kim's many masks in the Great Game.

## Historical Oudh

The historical region of Oudh (Awadh) was a princely state annexed by the British East India Company in 1856, just a year before the Indian Rebellion of 1857. This annexation was among the grievances that fueled the Mutiny. The region's talukdars (large landholders) were a powerful agrarian elite whose loyalty the British sought to secure, and whose sons, as Father Victor notes, went on to serve as officers in regiments like the Mavericks. (007-085)
