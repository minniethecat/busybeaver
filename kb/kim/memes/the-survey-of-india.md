# The Survey of India

**The Survey of India** is the British colonial institution responsible for mapping the Indian subcontinent. In *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, it serves a dual purpose — legitimate scientific mapping and cover for [[The Great Game|Great Game]] intelligence operations.

[[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]] arranges for [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s eventual entry "into the Survey of India as a chain-man" (007-048), a position providing official cover for intelligence work. The Survey represents the public, institutional face of the network whose covert side operates through the [[the-chain-men|Chain]].

## In *Kim*

The Survey of India appears primarily as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s career destination. [[Colonel Creighton]]'s plan for [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] involves education at [[St Xavier's School]] followed by Survey training, which requires competence in mathematics, drawing, and [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]].

The skills of a surveyor — measuring distances, recording terrain, carrying detailed mental maps — are identical to the skills of a spy. As one officer explains: "Thou must learn how to make pictures of roads and mountains and rivers, to carry these pictures in thine eye till a suitable time comes to set them upon paper" (007-035).

Mapping expeditions into the northern frontier regions provided ideal cover for observing Russian activities and gathering strategic information. The Survey's scientific legitimacy made it the perfect institutional host for [[the-chain-men|the Chain]]'s covert operations.

The Survey operates throughout the novel as the respectable counterpart to the hidden [[the-chain-men|intelligence network]]. Where [[the-chain-men|the Chain]] Men pass messages in secret, the Survey produces maps in public — yet their information frequently serves the same masters.

## Historical Background

The Great Trigonometrical Survey of India, begun in 1802 under William Lambton and continued by George Everest, was one of the most ambitious mapping projects of the 19th century. Survey teams measured the subcontinent from Cape Comorin to the Himalayas, establishing the baseline for all subsequent Indian cartography.

Under the cover of scientific surveying, officers and their Indian assistants mapped the Himalayan approaches to [[tibet|Tibet]] and Central Asia — intelligence of immense strategic value during the rivalry with Russia. Kipling's portrayal of the Survey as a dual-use institution reflects this historical [[illusion-and-reality|reality]] with remarkable [[loyalty-and-betrayal|fidelity]].

The Survey's methods — triangulation, baseline measurement, astronomical [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]] — required both mathematical sophistication and the ability to operate in remote, politically sensitive terrain. These were precisely the conditions under which intelligence gathering could be conducted without arousing suspicion.
