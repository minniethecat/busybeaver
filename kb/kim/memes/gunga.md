## The River Everyone Knows

When the lama, travelling by train through the Punjab, asks his fellow passengers "What rivers have ye by Benares?", the answer is immediate and unanimous: "We have Gunga." (002-075) The banker in the carriage has "thrice made pilgrimage to Gunga" and declares proudly that "Who bathes in her is made clean and goes to the Gods." (002-079-080) For ordinary Indians, Gunga is the supreme sacred river — so fundamental that the carriage looks at the lama "in amazement" that anyone could be ignorant of it. (002-085)

## The Lama's Distinction

But the lama does not accept Gunga as his goal. He presses further: "Who, then, made Gunga in the beginning?" (002-082) When told "The Gods," he probes deeper: "What like of Gods were they?" (002-084) His search is not satisfied by the universally known Gunga — he seeks "a certain River of healing," the River of the Arrow, which he distinguishes from the Ganges entirely. "There is Gunga — and Gunga alone — who washes away sin" runs the murmur through the carriage, but the lama knows his river is different. (002-095)

> "It is not Gunga. The River that I know washes from all taint of sin."
>
> (003-164)

## In Kim's Experience

Kim himself has "drank Gunga-water to the edge of dropsy" (003-163), suggesting intimate familiarity with the river. Yet he too separates it from the lama's quest: "It is not Gunga. The River that I know washes from all taint of sin." (003-164) Even a "seller of Ganges-water" appears among the pilgrims at Benares. (004-040) Gunga thus serves as the foil against which the lama's transcendent River of the Arrow is measured — the known versus the sought, the popular versus the esoteric.
