# The Kulu Woman

**The Kulu Woman** (also called the Sahiba, the Maharanee) is a wealthy widow from the [[kulu|Kulu hills]]. She attaches herself to [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] during their travels, providing food, money, and protection.

She is one of the few significant female characters in *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* and represents the tradition of pious lay devotees who supported wandering holy men.

## Background

The woman comes from a wealthy family in [[kulu|Kulu]], a Himalayan region. Widowed and independent, she travels in a curtained palanquin with a retinue of servants. Her devotion to [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] is immediate and absolute — she recognizes his holiness and determines to support his [[pilgrimage]].

## Role in the Narrative

The [[kulu|Kulu]] woman becomes [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s most devoted lay supporter. She feeds [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]], gives them money, and uses her social standing to smooth their way. Her relationship with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] is one of pure devotion: she sees him as a living saint and treats him with reverence bordering on worship.

Her practicality complements [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s unworldliness. Where [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] forgets to eat, she ensures he is fed. Where [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s begging fails, her wealth intervenes. She represents the material foundation of spiritual life — the lay support without which monks and [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s cannot survive.

## Relationships

- **[[Teshoo Lama]]** — Object of her devoted patronage and reverence
- **[[Kimball O'Hara]]** — She treats [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] with maternal protectiveness

Additional context: (004-045).

Her relationship with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] illustrates the traditional Buddhist economy of mutual support between monks and laity. (003-188)
