# Punjab

The Punjab is the beating heart of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*'s geography. From the opening image of [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] astride [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]] — "Who hold [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]]... hold the Punjab" (001-002) — to the novel's final scenes, the province provides the soil, the people, and the political context for the entire narrative. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s English blood entitles him to the Punjab — "the English held the Punjab and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] was English" (001-003) — yet his [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] is profoundly Punjabi: he speaks the vernacular, moves through its bazars, and calls its people his own.

## In *Kim*

The Punjab is established in the novel's first paragraph as the prize of empire and the ground of [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s [[identity-and-disguise|identity]]. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s father, [[kimball-ohara|Kimball O'Hara]], "took a post on the Sind, Punjab, and [[delhi|Delhi]] Railway" (001-003), and the orphaned [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] grows up in [[lahore|Lahore]], the provincial capital. The great green-bronze gun [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]] symbolizes conquest and belonging.

The province's landscape is evoked in luminous passages. Traveling by train, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] witness "all the rich Punjab... out in the splendour of the keen sun" (002-071), the morning mists smoking "golden, rose, saffron, and pink" across the flat green levels. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s quest unfolds across the Punjab's rivers and fields: "To search every river in the Punjab is no small matter" (002-097).

Punjabi [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] pervades the novel's social texture. A constable at the Museum is described as "a big Punjabi" (001-008); [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] meets "a chance-met Punjabi farmer" (011-018) at [[benares|Benares]], a Kamboh from [[jullundur|Jullundur]] who speaks "in Punjabi" (011-035) and declares fervently: "No, the Punjab for me, and the soil of the [[jullundur|Jullundur]]-*doab* for the best soil in it" (011-117). Even [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s chameleon skills include the ability to "talk Punjabi" (002-101) — a mark of belonging.

The province's institutions also anchor the plot: the Punjab High Court, the Punjab University (001-137), the Punjab Masonic Orphanage (005-187), and the "yellow-trousered Punjab policemen" (012-009) who appear on the railway platform. [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] is known as "one of the best horse-dealers in the Punjab" (001-201), his cover [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] rooted in the province's horse trade. The earthquake in [[kashmir|Srinagar]], tied to [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s birth, "had been felt in India, and for long stood a leading date in the Punjab" (002-181). Even the old soldier's song of Nikal Seyn is "the song that men sing in the Punjab to this day" (003-185).

## Description

The Punjab — "the Land of the Five Rivers" — is depicted as a vast agricultural plain, crisscrossed by railways, irrigation canals, and the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]]. Its fields stretch "flat green" (002-071) under the Indian sun; its villages cluster in mango topes; its cities — [[lahore|Lahore]], Umritsar, [[umballa|Umballa]] — hum with the commerce of empire. It is a province of contrasts: ancient and modern, Hindu and Muslim and Sikh, native and British.

## Historical Context

The Punjab was annexed by the British East India Company in 1849 after the Second Anglo-Sikh War, making it one of the last major territories brought under British rule in India. By the 1890s, it was a strategically vital province, bordering Afghanistan and serving as the staging ground for the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. Its irrigation systems, railways, and military cantonments were key infrastructure of empire. [[lahore|Lahore]], the provincial capital, was a major cultural and administrative centre.

## Travel Routes

[[the-grand-trunk-road|The Grand Trunk Road]], the novel's great artery, traverses the Punjab from [[mahbub-ali|the Afghan]] frontier to [[delhi|Delhi]]. The Sind, Punjab, and [[delhi|Delhi]] Railway connects the province's cities. From [[lahore|Lahore]], roads and rail lines radiate to [[umballa|Umballa]], [[simla|Simla]], and the Himalayan foothills; to Peshawar and the Khyber Pass; to [[delhi|Delhi]] and the Gangetic plain beyond.
