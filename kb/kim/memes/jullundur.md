# Jullundur

Jullundur is a district and city in [[punjab|the Punjab]] that recurs through the early and middle chapters of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. It enters the novel not as a described setting but as a point of origin — the home of several characters who cross paths with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], and a reference point for the prosperous agricultural life of [[punjab|the Punjab]].

## In *Kim*

Jullundur first appears in Chapter 2 on the night train to [[umballa|Umballa]]. The wife of a well-to-do Jat cultivator, squeezed into the crowded third-class carriage, declares: "There is no room even for a mouse" (002-020). She is identified as "a Hindu Jat from the rich Jullundur district" — the epithet "rich" establishing the region's agricultural reputation from the first mention.

As the train passes through [[punjab|the Punjab]] countryside, the same woman points to the flourishing crops and says: "Though past question we have good Gods Jullundur-way. See how they have blessed the crops" (002-096). Her remark links the fertility of the land to divine favour, a theme that runs through [[punjab|the Punjab]] chapters.

Jullundur even enters the legendary history of the region. A Sikh fellow-passenger declares of Alexander the Great: "He paved the streets of Jullundur and built a great tank near [[umballa|Umballa]]. That pavement holds to this day" (002-100). And later: "We have a Red Bull in our village behind Jullundur city, and he grazes by choice in the very greenest of our fields!" (002-119).

In Chapter 11, Jullundur reappears through another traveller — a [[punjab|Punjab]]i farmer, "a Kamboh from Jullundur-way who had appealed in vain to every God of his homestead to cure his small son" (011-018). This Kamboh becomes a significant figure in the [[benares|Benares]] episode, describing himself as a neighbour of sorts: "Jandiala — Jullundur? Oho! Then we be neighbours in some sort" (011-021). He later praises the agricultural quality of his homeland: "[[punjab|the Punjab]] for me, and the soil of the Jullundur-*doab* for the best soil in it" (011-117).

The Kamboh's association with Jullundur serves as a running motif — [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]] both address him as "man from Jullundur" (011-139, 011-205), making the place-name part of his [[identity-and-disguise|identity]] in the narrative.

> "There is no room even for a mouse," shrilled the wife of a well-to-do cultivator---a Hindu Jat from the rich Jullundur district.
>
> (002-020)

## Description

Jullundur is located in the Doaba region of [[punjab|the Punjab]] — the fertile tract of land between the Beas and Sutlej rivers, known as the Jullundur Doab. In *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*, the district is consistently characterised through its agricultural richness: the "rich Jullundur district," the "greenest of our fields," the "best soil" of the doab. The region is depicted as a heartland of Jat and Kamboh farming communities, whose prosperity and pride in their land are defining traits.

## Historical Context

Jullundur (modern Jalandhar) was an important district headquarters in [[punjab|the Punjab]] under British rule. The city had ancient roots — as the Sikh passenger's reference to Alexander the Great suggests, it was associated with Hellenistic settlements. By Kipling's time, it was a major railway junction on the line between [[lahore|Lahore]] and [[umballa|Umballa]], and the district was known for its productive agriculture, particularly wheat and sugarcane. The Jullundur Doab's fertility made it one of the most prosperous agricultural regions in British India.

- The Kamboh farmer from Jullundur-way — sought a cure for his son in [[benares|Benares]], Chapter 11

## Travel Routes

Jullundur lies on the main railway line between [[lahore|Lahore]] and [[umballa|Umballa]], making it a natural reference point for [[punjab|the Punjab]] chapters. Travellers from Jullundur appear on both the train (Chapter 2) and in [[benares|Benares]] (Chapter 11), illustrating the mobility of [[punjab|Punjab]]is along the railway network that Kipling depicts.
