# The Babu's Escape from the Russian Spies

In the high valleys of the Himalayas, Hurree Chunder Mookerjee — the rotund, umbrella-wielding Bengali Babu — executed one of the most brilliant deceptions in the Great Game. Presenting himself as a harmless agent of the Rajah of Rampur, he ingratiated himself with two foreign spies — one Russian, one French — feigned drunkenness, catalogued their equipment, upset their document-case, and ultimately delivered their maps and papers into British hands. All of this he accomplished while maintaining the persona of a bumbling, overly effusive native clerk, "an unfortunate product of English rule" (013-039).

## Context

The Great Game had entered a critical phase. Russian agents had been crossing the northern passes, mapping the approaches to British India under cover of a shooting expedition. The Babu had been tracking them since Leh, following their trail across the Karakorum and down through the Himalayan valleys. His assignment: to determine what intelligence they had gathered and, if possible, to prevent it from reaching Russian territory.

Kim and the lama, travelling north through the same hills, were also on a converging course with the spies. The Babu, knowing that Kim would be following his blue-and-white umbrella as a rendezvous point, arranged for the boy and the lama to serve as backup witnesses. "If you will kindly keep within sight of my umbrella, which is fine fixed point for cadastral survey, I shall feel much better" (013-022), he had told Kim earlier.

## The Event

### The Approach

The Babu arranged his approach with theatrical precision. After a thunderstorm scattered the spies' baggage coolies, the Babu appeared at their camp — oily, wet, smiling — having wrung out his clothes, donned his patent-leather shoes, and opened his blue-and-white umbrella. With "mincing gait and a heart beating against his tonsils" (013-033), he introduced himself as "agent for His Royal Highness, the Rajah of Rampur. What can I do for you, please?" (013-033).

The Russian and Frenchman were delighted. "One was visibly French, the other Russian, but they spoke English not much inferior to the Babu's" (013-034). Seeing only a toadying native clerk, they underestimated him completely.

### The Performance

The Babu's genius lay in constructing a character his targets wanted to believe in. He fawned, he babbled, he flattered. When they offered him drink — "a glass of whitish fluid like to gin" (013-038) — he accepted and asked for more. Soon he "became thickly treasonous" (013-038), denouncing the British Government that had educated him but refused to pay him adequately. "He babbled tales of oppression and wrong till the tears ran down his cheeks for the miseries of his land" (013-039).

The Russian and Frenchman were entirely taken in. "They are all just of that pattern," one remarked to the other in French. "When we get into India proper thou wilt see" (013-040). They saw exactly what they expected to see: a weak, corrupt, disloyal product of European meddling in Asia. They did not see the intelligence agent cataloguing everything in their camp.

### The Critical Moment

Between his theatrical sobs and protestations, the Babu studied the spies' equipment. His attention fixed on "a kilta full of maps and documents — an extra-large one with a double red oil-skin cover. He did not wish to steal anything. He only desired to know what to steal, and, incidentally, how to get away when he had stolen it" (013-052).

With calculated clumsiness, the Babu stumbled against the kilta, upsetting it just enough to glimpse its contents while appearing merely drunk. The document case — the red oilskin kilta — was everything: survey notes, route maps, compass readings of the northern passes. This was the intelligence the Russians had gathered over months of travel, and the Babu now knew exactly what it contained and where it was kept.

### The Aftermath

The Babu's role was not to steal the documents himself — that task would fall to Kim, later, in the confrontation on the hillside. But the Babu had done the essential preparatory work: he had identified the target, assessed the security, and established a relationship of trust with the spies that would enable the final operation.

When Kim and the lama arrived at the same camp, the stage was set for the violent confrontation that would follow. The Russian, resentful of the lama's spiritual authority, tore the sacred Wheel of Life diagram and struck the old man. In the chaos, Kim fought the Russian, the hill coolies fled, and the Babu — in a moment of inspired absurdity — cast himself upon Kim bodily, ostensibly to separate the combatants, in reality to ensure Kim did not kill the Russian and thus compromise the greater mission.

## Consequences

- **The intelligence is secured.** The Babu's patient reconnaissance made possible the recovery of the Russian maps and documents, a major coup for the Great Game.

- **The Russian expedition is neutralized.** Without their maps and with their cover blown, the foreign spies are rendered ineffective. The Babu later escorts them to Simla, still playing the buffoon.

- **The Babu is revealed as a master operator.** His self-deprecating manner — "I am a fearful man" (013-033) — masks a courage and cunning that rivals any European agent in the Game.

- **Kim's respect for the Babu deepens.** Having initially seen the Babu as a figure of comedy, Kim comes to understand that the Bengali's absurdity is itself a weapon.
