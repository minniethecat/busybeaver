# The Woman of Shamlegh's Rescue

After the violent confrontation on the Himalayan hillside — the Russian's blow to the lama, Kim's furious counter-attack, and the chaotic flight of the coolies — it was the Woman of Shamlegh who took command. She provided food, shelter, and crucial intelligence to the wounded party at Shamlegh-under-the-Snow, shielding them from the pursuing foreign agents and enabling their eventual recovery and escape.

## Context

The party — Kim, the lama, Hurree Babu, and a scattering of hill coolies — had crossed into the high country above Shamlegh when the Russians and the Frenchman intercepted them. The confrontation turned violent when the Russian struck the lama, and Kim, in a fury, threw himself at the foreigner (013-086). The coolies fled downhill, dragging the lama with them. They found refuge in the summer settlement of Shamlegh.

Shamlegh itself was a seasonal outpost: "The place was only three stone huts, perched on a ledge above the valley" (013-137). Its summer population consisted of "the Woman of Shamlegh and two or three coolies" (013-137). The Woman was no ordinary villager — she held land from the Rajah and answered to no man (014-063).

## The Event

### Arrival and shelter

The coolies brought the wounded lama to Shamlegh, where the Woman received them. Kim, battered from the fight and struggling with his own injuries, made contact with her. The Woman assessed the situation with practical authority:

> "Tell me what to do. I am at Shamlegh-under-the-Snow. The old man is sick, and the Babu has gone to bring help." (014-048)

Unlike the terrified coolies, the Woman showed no fear of the Sahibs. She had "no love for Sahibs" (013-138), yet she did not turn Kim and the lama away. She provided food and arranged shelter for the wounded party.

### Intelligence and protection

The Woman's value extended beyond simple hospitality. She knew the movements of the Russians and shared this intelligence with Kim. When the Russian and Frenchman fled downhill, abandoning their baggage, the Woman ensured the party's position was not betrayed.

Her authority in the region was absolute during the summer season. She declared: "I am the Woman of Shamlegh, and I hold from the Rajah. I do not love Sahibs, but I love the Rajah" (014-063). This political alignment — loyal to the local ruler, suspicious of foreign interlopers — made her a natural ally.

The Woman also oversaw the distribution of the foreigners' abandoned baggage among the coolies, managing the delicate politics of reward and silence: "We will make fair division at Shamlegh" (013-115).

### Kim's recovery

Under the Woman's protection, Kim began to recover from the physical and psychological shock of the confrontation. The simple care of food, rest, and the Woman's practical competence provided the stability Kim needed to regain his strength. The lama, too, received attention — though his injuries were not physical, his spiritual crisis deepened in the thin mountain air.

## Key Passages

> "I have already looked into their baskets---but we will make fair division at Shamlegh. Give him a little more. It is good medicine. Feel! His heart goes better now."
>
> (013-115)

## Consequences

- **The party finds sanctuary.** Without the Woman's intervention, Kim and the lama would have been exposed to further danger from the fleeing foreigners or the frightened coolies.
- **Intelligence is preserved.** The Woman's knowledge of local movements and her authority over the coolies ensured that the party's position was not betrayed to the Russians, allowing the document recovery operation to succeed.
- **Kim's transition from agent to human.** The Woman's care marks a turn in Kim's narrative arc: from the spy-game's frenetic action back toward the lama's spiritual quest.
- **A memorable minor character.** The Woman of Shamlegh appears only briefly, but her independence, authority, and practical wisdom make her one of the novel's most striking Himalayan characters.
