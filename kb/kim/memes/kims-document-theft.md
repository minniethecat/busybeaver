# Kim's Document Theft

In the turmoil that followed the Russian's assault on the lama, the foreign spies' baggage — containing maps, survey records, and diplomatic papers — was abandoned on the hillside. Kim and Hurree Chunder Mookerjee seized the opportunity. The documents that fell into their hands that day would prove to be one of the most significant intelligence coups of the Great Game — a complete portrait of Russian designs on the northern passes, secured by a Bengali schoolmaster and an Irish street boy in the thin Himalayan air.

## Context

For weeks, Hurree Babu had been tracking the two foreign agents — one Russian, one French — who had crossed into British Indian territory under cover of a hunting expedition. He had identified their equipment: "chains and levels and compasses" (012-149), the unmistakable tools of military survey. He had ingratiated himself with them, accepted their condescension, and waited.

His patience was strategic. The Babu's eyes were fixed on "a kilta full of maps and documents — an extra-large one with a double red oil-skin cover" (013-052). His instructions from Creighton were clear: discover what intelligence the Russians were gathering and, if possible, secure it. "He did not wish to steal anything. He only desired to know what to steal, and, incidentally, how to get away when he had stolen it" (013-052).

The Babu knew the Russians carried a document of special importance: "a sealed, gilded, and illuminated document such as one King sends to another" (014-029). This was not mere survey work. It was political.

## The Event

### Opportunity in chaos

When the Russian struck the lama and Kim leaped to his defence, the hillside erupted. The coolies fled, dragging the wounded lama with them. The Frenchman fired his revolver blindly into the twilight (013-086). The baggage — the precious kilta of documents — was abandoned in the stampede.

The Babu, who had cast himself upon Kim in an apparent attempt to separate the combatants, used the confusion to assess the situation. The Russian was battered and half-conscious. The Frenchman was scrambling for higher ground. And the kilta lay unguarded.

### Recovery

In the aftermath, as Kim and the lama recovered under the pines, the Babu gathered what he could. The foreigners had fled downhill, leaving behind not only their camp equipment but also the bulk of their surveying instruments and papers. The kilta's contents — maps annotated with Russian notations, compass readings, elevation measurements — fell into the hands of the Great Game network.

Kim, for his part, understood the significance. He had carried Mahbub Ali's messages, had trained under Lurgan Sahib in the arts of memory and disguise. Now he had participated in a direct counter-intelligence operation — not as a messenger, but as an agent in the field.

### The Babu's triumph

The Babu's method was characteristically oblique. He did not seize the documents by force; he simply ensured they were not returned to their owners. When the camp reassembled after the chaos, the kilta had already been moved — by which hand, no one could say precisely. The hillmen, terrified of the sacrilege they had witnessed, wanted no part of the foreigners' belongings.

The Babu later confirmed the success of the operation. Among the recovered materials were detailed surveys of the Leh-Yarkand route, notes on local political allegiances, and the sealed diplomatic document — evidence of a coordinated Russian effort to extend influence across the northern frontier.

## Consequences

- **Russian intelligence is compromised.** The maps and documents recovered provide Creighton's network with a complete picture of Russian survey activities, enabling counter-measures along the northern passes.

- **The Great Game advances.** The operation marks a significant victory for British intelligence: two foreign agents are exposed and driven from the field, their work undone by a Bengali clerk and an Irish boy.

- **Kim's status as a field agent is confirmed.** His role in the confrontation and document recovery proves he can operate independently in the field — not merely as a messenger but as an active participant in intelligence operations.

- **The Babu's reputation is enhanced.** Hurree Chunder Mookerjee, dismissed by the Russians as a comic Oriental, has proven himself a formidable intelligence operative — patient, perceptive, and effective.
