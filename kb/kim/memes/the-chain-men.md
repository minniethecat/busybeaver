# The Chain Men

**The Chain Men** are the hidden operatives of the British intelligence apparatus in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. They form a network — referred to as "the Chain" — running across British India and beyond, linking field agents, informants, couriers, and spymasters in a continuous line of information gathering and transmission.

## In the Novel

The Chain Men are never fully revealed in the novel, operating instead as a shadow presence whose existence is hinted at rather than displayed. [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]], [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]], [[hurree-babu|Hurree Babu]], and eventually [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] himself are all links in this chain, each serving a different function within the network. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is formally told he will "enter [[the-survey-of-india|the Survey]] of India as a chain-man" (007-033), making explicit his intended role in the intelligence apparatus.

The chain is described as spanning the length of India, with each link responsible for [[identity-and-disguise|passing]] intelligence to the next. The system relies on the compartmentalization of information — each agent knows only their immediate contacts, protecting the network if any single link is compromised.

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s training under [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]] is designed to make him a functional link in this chain — able to observe, remember, and report without detection. His value lies in his ability to move through Indian society unnoticed, serving as a link where European agents cannot operate. As [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]]'s men explain, there are situations where "bad people living in those hills... will slay the chain-man if he be seen to look like a Sahib" (007-035), underscoring the need for agents who can pass as natives.

## Key Passages

> "We be all on one lead-rope, then," said Kim at last, "the Colonel, Mahbub Ali, and I — when I become a chain-man." (007-048)
> — Kim acknowledging his place within the intelligence chain

> "The chain is complete from end to end of India." (007-126)
> — Describing the reach of British intelligence operations

The chain men concept reflects the actual intelligence practices of the British Raj, which relied heavily on native informants and intermediaries rather than European officers. The novel portrays this as an efficient if morally ambiguous system.

## Historical Background

The Chain Men of *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* are based on the historical network of "political officers" and native agents operated by the British Indian government during the 19th century. The British maintained a system of informants, surveyors ([[identity-and-disguise|disguise]]d as traders or [[pilgrimage|pilgrim]]s), and local intermediaries who mapped the northern frontier regions and reported on Russian activities. This network was particularly active in [[punjab|the Punjab]], the Northwest Frontier, and the approaches to [[tibet|Tibet]].
