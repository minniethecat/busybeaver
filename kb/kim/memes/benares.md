# Benares

**Benares** (modern Varanasi, also called Kashi) is the ancient holy city on the banks of the Ganges. In the novel, it serves as the [[Teshoo Lama]]'s declared [[pilgrimage]] destination and functions throughout the narrative as a symbol of spiritual aspiration, standing in quiet counterpoint to the worldly machinations of the [[The Great Game|Great Game]].

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s first words to [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] include his destination: "First to Kashi [Benares]: where else?" (001-082). This refrain — "we go to Benares" — echoes through the early chapters, anchoring the narrative's spiritual dimension even as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] is drawn into the world of espionage.

> First to Kashi \[Benares\]: where else? There I shall meet one of the pure faith in a Jain temple of that city. He also is a Seeker in secret, and from him haply I may learn.
>
> (001-082)

## In *Kim*

Benares enters the story on the very first page of dialogue between [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] and [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] announces his [[pilgrimage]] to "the Four Holy Places," beginning with Benares, where he will seek the "[[the-river-of-the-arrow|River of the Arrow]]" (001-143).

The city is spoken of more than it is visited during the main narrative. It is the destination [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] never quite reaches during his journey with [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], deferred by the events that draw them northward toward the Himalayas. Yet Benares remains a constant presence in [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s thoughts — a promised land that represents the fulfillment of his spiritual quest.

The Temple of the Tirthankars at Benares serves as a practical anchor for the characters. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] uses it as a mailing address (006-093), receiving letters and money there through a native banker. It is "the place I have chosen" (007-081), he tells [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], a fixed point in his otherwise wandering existence.

When [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] are separated, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] returns to Benares, where [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] later finds him "a little thinner and a little yellower" (009-148) but still patiently awaiting the boy's return.

## Description

Benares is described primarily through [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s reverent anticipation rather than direct narration. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] speaks of its temples, its sacred river, and the holy men who gather on its ghats. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], the pragmatic child of [[lahore|Lahore]]'s bazaars, initially views Benares with the skepticism of one who has seen too many holy men to be impressed by holy cities.

The Temple of the Tirthankars — a Jain temple within the predominantly Hindu city — provides a specific, grounded location within the sacred geography. It represents [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s Buddhist path intersecting with India's diverse religious landscape.

## Historical Context

Benares (Varanasi) is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world and the holiest city in Hinduism. For Buddhists, nearby Sarnath is where the Buddha gave his first sermon — a connection that would resonate deeply with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]], a [[tibet|Tibet]]an Buddhist making [[pilgrimage]] to the sites associated with the Buddha's life.

In Kipling's time, Benares was governed as part of the United Provinces of British India. The city's sacred status largely insulated it from direct colonial interference, preserving its character as a center of Hindu learning, [[pilgrimage]], and ritual.

## Travel Routes

Benares lies on the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]] and the main railway line:
- **To the northwest**: [[Umballa]] → [[delhi|Delhi]]
- **To the east**: Patna → Calcutta
- **By river**: The Ganges connects Benares to Allahabad, Patna, and the Bengal delta
