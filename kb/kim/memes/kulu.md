# Kulu

Kulu is a Himalayan region that appears throughout *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]* as a point of origin, a place of memory, and the northern threshold between India and [[tibet|Tibet]]. It is most strongly associated with the [[the-kulu-woman|Kulu Woman]] — the Raiput widow who becomes the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]]'s travelling companion and protector.

## In *Kim*

Kulu enters the narrative early: in Chapter 1, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] tells [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], "I came by Kulu — from beyond the Kailas" (001-031), establishing the region as [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s entry point into India. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s previous disciple died in Kulu: "halting in Kulu awhile a fever took him" (001-112), a loss that sets the stage for [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] becoming the new chela.

> "I came by Kulu---from beyond the Kailas---but what know you? From the Hills where"---he sighed---"the air and water are fresh and cool."
>
> (001-031)

[[the-kulu-woman|The Kulu Woman]] first appears in Chapter 4, identified as being "of Kulu or Kangra folk" (004-053). She becomes a recurring presence, caring for [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] in his frailty and providing [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] with maternal concern. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s bond with the Kulu Woman spans much of the journey: "keep with that Kulu woman — on no account stray from her" (005-139).

Kulu appears in later chapters as a reference point in conversations. "The woman from Kulu. That is by [[saharunpore|Saharunpore]]" (011-121) marks her origin. In Chapter 12, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] suggests "Let us go to [[the-kulu-woman|the Kulu woman]]'s house" (012-046) as a place of safety. The region also features in folk stories: "spirits and fiends of Kulu" (009-153) that the Kulu Woman recounts.

## Description

The novel depicts Kulu as a highland region of pines and mountain air — "the pines of the Kangra and Kulu hills with the dust and the fumes of the Plains" (004-161). It is a place of remote beauty, home to Raiput communities and folk traditions distinct from the plains. The region's isolation makes it both a refuge and a waypoint between [[tibet|Tibet]] and India.

## Historical Context

The Kulu Valley in Himachal Pradesh was an independent kingdom until annexed by the British in 1846. By the late 19th century, it was part of [[punjab|the Punjab]] Hills district, known for its temperate climate, apple orchards, and as a gateway to the [[tibet|Tibet]]an plateau via the Rohtang Pass.

## Associated Characters

- [[the-kulu-woman|The Kulu Woman]] — the Raiput widow from Kulu
- [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]] — entered India through Kulu
- [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] — travels with the Kulu Woman

## Travel Routes

Kulu lies north of [[punjab|the Punjab]] plains, accessible via the Kangra Valley and paths leading toward the high passes into [[tibet|Tibet]]. From Kulu one can travel south to [[saharunpore|Saharunpore]] and the [[the-grand-trunk-road|Grand Trunk Road]], or north toward the [[tibet|Tibet]]an plateau.
