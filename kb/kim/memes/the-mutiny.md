## The Black Year

The old soldier who accompanies Kim and the lama on the Grand Trunk Road is a veteran of "the Mutiny" — the Indian Rebellion of 1857 — having "served the Government in the days of the Mutiny as a native officer in a newly raised cavalry regiment." (003-064) The lama recalls it as "the Black Year" which he had heard of even in distant Tibet. (003-140) For the old soldier, it is the defining event of his life: "All earth knew, and trembled!" (003-141)

## The Soldier's Account

The old Rissaldar describes the Mutiny as a madness:

> "The Gods, who sent it for a plague, alone know. A madness ate into all the Army, and they turned against their officers. That was the first evil, but not past remedy if they had then held their hands. But they chose to kill the Sahibs' wives and children. Then came the Sahibs from over the sea and called them to most strict account."
>
> (003-139)

He was one of only three men from his regiment of "six hundred and eighty sabres" who "stood fast to their salt." (003-145) He rode "seventy miles with an English Memsahib and her babe on my saddle-bow" to safety. (003-147) His account emphasizes loyalty over ideology: he stayed true not from politics but because he had "talked with the men of Sobraon, of Chilianwallah, of Moodkee and Ferozeshah" — veterans of earlier Sikh Wars who understood the cost of betrayal.

## Memory and Identity

Decades later, the Mutiny still structures the old soldier's identity. He wears the Order of British India and receives visits from Commissioners who "come riding to me through the crops" to "talk out the old skirmishes, one dead man's name leading to another." (003-149) Kim, lying by the fire, hears "tales of the Mutiny and young captains thirty years in their graves" (003-113) — the rebellion living on as oral history. Even the lama's guide would later have "talked of the Mutiny" (007-055), showing how deeply the event permeates the collective memory of British India.
