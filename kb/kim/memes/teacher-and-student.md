# Teacher and Student

**The Teacher and Student relationship** — the guru-chela bond — is the central human relationship in Rudyard Kipling's *[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]*. The novel is structured around [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s dual apprenticeship: to the [[teshoo-lama|Teshoo Lama]], who teaches him spiritual wisdom, and to the spymasters of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]], who teach him worldly craft. Between these two kinds of teaching, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] must forge his own understanding.

## In the Novel

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s relationship with [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] is defined as that of chela (disciple) to guru (teacher). The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] provides [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] with spiritual teaching — lessons about the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Life]], the nature of [[illusion-and-reality|illusion]], and the path to liberation. But [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] also teaches [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]: he guides him physically along the road, begs for their food, and protects him from the dangers of the world. Their relationship is reciprocal, each serving as the other's teacher in different domains, a bond [[teshoo-lama|the lama]] invokes when he speaks of souls "seeking escape from the [[the-wheel-of-life|Wheel of Things]]" (012-052).

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s other teachers are more conventional master-apprentice relationships. [[mahbub-ali|Mahbub Ali]] teaches him the practical skills of intelligence work — how to carry messages, how to read men, how to move unnoticed. [[lurgan-sahib|Lurgan Sahib]] teaches him memory, [[secrecy-and-surveillance|observation]], and [[identity-and-disguise|disguise]]. [[colonel-creighton|Colonel Creighton]] teaches him the strategic context in which all these skills will be deployed (007-033).

The novel presents these two kinds of teaching — spiritual and practical — not as contradictory but as complementary. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] needs both to become complete. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] gives him a moral center; the spymasters give him the skills to survive. Together, they produce the novel's ideal: a man who can act in the world without being corrupted by it.

## Key Passages

> "My teaching I owe to thee. I have eaten thy bread three years." (011-055)
> — Kim acknowledging the lama as his primary teacher

The guru-chela relationship is presented as one of the deepest bonds in Indian culture, transcending even blood relationships. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] are not related by birth but by spiritual adoption, a bond the novel treats as sacred.

## Symbolism

The teacher-student theme connects to broader symbolic patterns:
- **The chain of transmission**: Wisdom passed from teacher to student across generations
- **Two paths**: Spiritual teaching ([[teshoo-lama|the lama]]) and worldly teaching (the spymasters)
- **Integration**: [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s task is to synthesize both kinds of teaching into a unified self
