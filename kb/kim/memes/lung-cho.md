## The Abbot of Lung-Cho

The Lung-Cho Monastery holds a crucial place in the Teshoo Lama's backstory. The lama tells the Curator at the Wonder House that his drawing of the Wheel of Life — the intricate diagram that sits at the heart of the novel's spiritual symbolism — came from Lung-Cho: "he is now Abbot of the Lung-Cho Monastery — gave it me." (001-050) The lama himself expresses a certain disbelief at his own ascension: "He who is now Abbot of Lung-Cho told me, but I did not believe." (001-054)

## The Wheel of Life Origin

The drawing of the Wheel of Life that the lama carries is the physical artifact that sets the entire pilgrimage in motion. It was at Lung-Cho that the Abbot entrusted this sacred diagram to the Teshoo Lama, charging him to verify its accuracy against the living Buddhist traditions of India. (001-050) This commission transforms a personal spiritual quest into a mission with institutional sanction. The lama's journey is thus anchored, from its very beginning, in the authority of the Lung-Cho Monastery.

> He who is now Abbot of the Lung-Cho Monastery gave it me.
>
> (001-050)

## Authority Recognized by the Kulu Woman

The monastery's standing extends beyond the lama's personal connection. When the Kulu woman (the Hill Rajah's widow) encounters the lama on the Grand Trunk Road, she recognizes Lung-Cho's authority. She explains to Kim that she "has heard of the Abbot of the Lung-Cho lamassery. It was to him I put the matter, and behold in the due time all came about as I desired." (004-101) The Abbot's blessing, she believes, was instrumental in the birth of her grandson.

## The Limits of Lung-Cho's Knowledge

Despite the Abbot's spiritual stature, the lama notes an important limitation: "She knew the Abbot of Lung-Cho, but she did not know of my River — nor the tale of the Arrow." (004-124) This distinction between worldly religious authority (represented by Lung-Cho) and the lama's personal, transcendent quest (the River of the Arrow) is a central tension in the novel.
