## A Sacred Buddhist Site

Buddh Gaya (also spelled Bodh Gaya) is the site of the Mahabodhi Temple, regarded as the place where the Buddha attained enlightenment. In *Kim*, it appears primarily as a destination of pilgrimage and spiritual significance.

The lama, when describing his travels to the Curator of the Wonder House, mentions Buddh Gaya as part of his route: "then to Mahabodhi, which is Buddh Gaya — to the Monastery — to the Dam." (001-062) He later tells the Curator that perhaps Kim "will go with me to Buddh Gaya. Thence north and west to Kapilavastu." (001-082)

> …then to Mahabodhi, which is Buddh Gaya---to the Monastery---to the Dam.
>
> (001-062)

## The Kulu Woman's Pilgrimage

The Hill Rajah's widow, the Kulu woman whom Kim and the lama meet on the Grand Trunk Road, reveals that she travels to Buddh Gaya to perform shraddha — the Hindu ritual of offering to ancestors — for the father of her children. (004-101) She is delighted to learn that the lama's road runs the same way, exclaiming that she "desires greatly that we should go with her to Buddh Gaya; her road being ours." (004-122)

## A Meeting Place in the Great Game

Later in the novel, Hurree Babu mentions Buddh Gaya as one of his accustomed rendezvous points, alongside the temple of the Tirthankars, "when he is in from the Road." (010-046) It also appears as a place where the Babu planned to "interrogate him on releegious matters" in connection with the Russian spies. (010-124)

## Distinction from the Lama's Goal

Despite its sanctity, Buddh Gaya is not the lama's ultimate destination. When Kim imagines the lama might settle there, the text pointedly notes that "this was not Ceylon, nor Buddh Gaya, nor Bombay, nor some grass-tufted place by the river" — the lama's River of the Arrow lies elsewhere. (012-064)

## In the Lama's Journey

For the Teshoo Lama, Buddh Gaya holds deep significance as a Buddhist holy site. He names it among the sacred places he intends to visit, placing it alongside Kapilavastu and the monasteries of Tibet. (001-062)(001-082) Yet Buddh Gaya is a waypoint, not the destination — the lama's true goal remains the River of the Arrow, a personal and transcendent quest that no established shrine can fulfill.
