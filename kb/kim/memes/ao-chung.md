## The Men of Ao-chung

The men of Ao-chung are renowned in the hills as skilled *shikarris*—professional trackers and hunting guides employed by British sahibs. The Ao-chung man who serves as leader of the coolies in Chapter 13 is a prominent figure: he has served both Fostum Sahib and Yankling Sahib over many hunting seasons, knows the medicinal use of whisky from his service, and carries himself with the authority of experience. (013-085) (013-113) (013-127)

## Leadership in Crisis

When the confrontation with the Russian spies erupts in Chapter 13, the man from Ao-chung takes command of the situation. He snatches up the Sahibs' guns, organizes the retreat to Shamlegh, and presides over the division of the baggage. Initially inclined to violent revenge against the Russians for striking the lama, he is restrained by the lama's moral authority and threat of spiritual consequences. (013-085) (013-099) (013-106)

Despite the whisky-fueled negotiations over the spoils, the Ao-chung man shows a practical intelligence: he recognizes that the Russian agents' hunting trophies were bought in the Leh bazaar rather than earned, and shrewdly calculates that no blame will attach to the hillfolk. His loyalty remains to the British sahibs he knows personally, particularly Yankling Sahib. (013-130) (013-141) (013-142)

## In the Text

The Teshoo Lama's moral authority over the hillmen is demonstrated when the man from Ao-chung is rebuked for contemplating violence against the Russian agents:

> "Hast thou heard? I say there shall be no killing---I who was Abbot of Such-zen. Is it any lust of thine to be re-born as a rat, or a snake under the eaves---a worm in the belly of the most mean beast? Is it thy wish to---" The man from Ao-chung fell to his knees, for the voice boomed like a Tibetan devil-gong.
>
> (013-106)
