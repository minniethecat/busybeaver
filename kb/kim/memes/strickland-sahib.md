Strickland Sahib is a British police officer and intelligence operative in [[the-great-game|the Great Game]]. He appears at Delhi railway station where he assists the spy [[e23|E23]] in evading capture via the intelligence network.

E23 confirms the rescue: "Strickland Sahib has pulled me to land." (012-032) He is later seen "explaining himself to a Mr Strickland on Delhi platform, while E23 was paddling through byways in the old city." (012-044)

## At Delhi Station

Strickland's role at Delhi station is brief but decisive. As a tall, sallow District Superintendent of Police — "belt, helmet, polished spurs and all — strutting and twirling his dark moustache" (012-017) — he cuts an imposing figure on the platform. When E.23, disguised as a Saddhu, walks almost into the Englishman's arms, Strickland berates him in clumsy Urdu: "*Tum mut?* You drunk?

You mustn't bang about as though Delhi station belonged to you, my friend." (012-020)

Yet this apparent antagonism is cover. E.23 later tells Kim: "Strickland Sahib has pulled me to land." (012-032) The police officer, operating as part of the intelligence network, has provided the critical extraction that allows E.23 to disappear into Delhi's old city.

The scene demonstrates the integration of official police authority with the covert Great Game — Strickland's uniform and position mask his role as a handler for field agents.

The confusion that follows is also part of the cover: "an over-zealous policeman had arrested, on charge of murder done in a far southern State, a horribly indignant Ajmir cotton-broker, who was explaining himself to a Mr Strickland on Delhi platform, while E23 was paddling through byways into the locked heart of Delhi city." (012-044)

Strickland's presence on the platform, ostensibly dealing with the bureaucratic aftermath of a wrongful arrest, provides the screen behind which E.23 vanishes.

## In the Great Game

As a District Superintendent of Police, Strickland occupies the intersection of formal law enforcement and the informal intelligence network that Colonel Creighton directs. Unlike field operatives such as Mahbub Ali or Hurree Babu, Strickland operates in the open — his authority and uniform are themselves tools of the Game.

His willingness to assist E.23 suggests that the Great Game's reach extends into every level of British Indian administration.

The police network that Strickland represents serves as the defensive infrastructure of the Game — tracking suspicious persons, managing informants, and providing safe passage for agents in transit.

When Kim reflects on the breadth of the intelligence apparatus — "Truly, it runs like a shuttle throughout all Hind" (012-168) — officers like Strickland are among the threads that make the fabric whole.
