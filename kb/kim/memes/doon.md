## Geographic Position

The Doon (also known as the Dehradun valley) lies between the outermost Siwalik Hills and the higher Himalayan ranges. It is described as "half-tropical," representing the last zone of relative warmth and accessibility before travelers commit to the narrow hill-roads leading into the true mountains. The lama and Kim cross the Doon after leaving Mussoorie behind, heading north into the huddled peaks. (013-007)

## Narrative Role

The Doon marks a significant threshold in the journey. On its terraces, the lama still leans on Kim's shoulder, weak from the plains. The valley represents civilization's last outpost before the wilderness of the high mountains. In Chapter 12, characters speak of going "straight into the Doon," describing it as "verree verdant and picturesque"—a landscape of white siris trees providing thin shade, a contrast to both the barren highlands and the crowded plains. (012-050) (012-168)

Later in the narrative, Kim recalls that they were "weak and fainting down below there in the Doon," emphasizing how the valley represents the low point from which the mountain journey ascended—both physically and spiritually. (014-100)

Hurree Babu describes the Doon's beauty as he plans his route:

> "I go from here straight into the Doon. It is verree verdant and painted meads. I shall go to Mussoorie to good old Munsoorie Pahar, as the gentlemen and ladies say."
>
> (012-168)
