## Identity and Role

Yankling Sahib is a British sportsman and hunter operating in the Himalayan regions. He is well known among the hillfolk of Spiti and Ao-chung as an employer of local *shikarris* (trackers and hunting guides). The hillmen who work for him speak of him with familiarity and respect, contrasting his merry-minded nature with the stern and suspicious foreigners they encounter. (013-130)

> "Not for five years was I Yankling Sahib's *shikarri* without knowing that medicine. I too have tasted it. Behold!"
>
> (013-113)

## Relationship with Hillmen

Several of the coolies in Chapter 13 have served Yankling Sahib as *shikarris* over multiple hunting seasons. The man from Ao-chung proudly declares that he served Yankling Sahib for five years and would have been with him still but for the *beegar* (corvée labor). He describes learning about medicine—whisky used to revive injured men—from this experience. (013-113) (013-115) (013-127)

Yankling Sahib is remembered for an incident where he twisted his foot beyond Astor, and the Ao-chung man used whisky to revive him. The hillmen view him as a "man of a merry mind" who would laugh at the troubles that befall those who antagonize the hillfolk. (013-142)

## Reputation Among Hillfolk

The hillmen draw a clear distinction between "true Sahibs" like Yankling Sahib and Fostum Sahib—who employ cooks, bearers, and retinues of servants—and the foreign spies who travel without such entourage. Yankling Sahib represents the established British presence in the hills: a figure who understands local customs, employs hillmen fairly, and whose good opinion matters to the mountain communities. (013-145) (013-119)

## Later Appearance

Yankling Sahib appears in Chapter 15, described as "the merry-minded man, spy-glass at eye," watching the landscape of Ladakh from the blue shale hills. (015-003)
