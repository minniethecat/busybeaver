# Kim Meets the Lama

**[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] meets [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]** is the inciting incident of Kipling's novel — the chance encounter outside the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] that sets every subsequent thread of the narrative in motion.

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]], sitting astride the great cannon [[the-zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]], sees a strange figure round the corner from the Motee Bazar. He is "such a man as [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], who thought he knew all castes, had never seen" (001-015) — an old [[tibet|Tibet]]an [[teshoo-lama|lama]] in fold upon fold of dingy wool, carrying an iron pencase and a wooden rosary.

From this moment of mutual curiosity, the novel's twin journeys are born: [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual [[pilgrimage]] and [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s entry into the [[The Great Game|Great Game]].

## Context

Before this meeting, [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s life is defined by the [[lahore|Lahore]] streets. He is "Little Friend of all the World," a white boy burned brown by the Indian sun, running errands for sleek young men and living by his wits. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]], meanwhile, has traveled from [[tibet|Tibet]] "by [[kulu|Kulu]] — from beyond the Kailas" (001-015), seeking the Four Holy Places of Buddhism.

Neither knows the other exists until this moment. The meeting is entirely accidental — or, in [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s worldview, karmically determined.

## The Meeting

The encounter begins with curiosity from both sides. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] drops from [[zam-zammah|Zam-Zammah]] to interrogate the stranger: "What is your caste? Where is your house? Have you come far?" (001-030).

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]], for his part, wants only to find the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] — the [[the-wonder-house|Lahore Museum]] — and see its collection of Buddhist artifacts. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], amused and intrigued, offers to guide him: "Come with me and I will show" (001-041).

Inside the Museum, [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s encounter with the Greco-Buddhist sculptures transforms the scene. He half-sobs before a relief of the Buddha, reciting the Buddhist invocation: "To Him the Way, — the Law, — Apart — Whom Maya held beneath her heart" (001-045).

[[kimball-ohara|Kim]] watches, uncomprehending but fascinated. The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s genuine spiritual passion is unlike anything in [[kimball-ohara|Kim]]'s experience of the world's holy men, most of whom he has learned to view with street-wise skepticism.

## Significance

This meeting establishes the central structural device of the novel: the counterpoint between [[teshoo-lama|the lama]]'s spiritual quest and the worldly intelligence operations of the [[the-great-game|Great Game]]. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] will spend the rest of the novel navigating between these two worlds, serving both masters — the holy man seeking enlightenment and the spies seeking geopolitical advantage.

The meeting also introduces the theme of the unexpected mentor. [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], the orphan who has learned to [[loyalty-and-betrayal|trust]] no one, finds himself drawn to this strange old man from beyond the mountains. "I go with thee to [[benares|Benares]]" (001-148), he says, and the words commit him to a journey that will transform his life.

The [[teshoo-lama|lama]]'s first words to [[kimball-ohara|Kim]] — asking about the [[the-wonder-house|Wonder House]] — contain the novel's epistemological question in miniature. What is worth wondering at? What is worth seeking? The [[teshoo-lama|lama]] seeks enlightenment; [[kimball-ohara|Kim]], though he does not yet know it, seeks belonging. Their meeting begins both quests.

## Key Passages

> "Such a man as Kim, who thought he knew all castes, had never seen." (001-015)
> — The novel's introduction of the lama, seen through Kim's experienced but suddenly inadequate eyes

> "What is your caste? Where is your house? Have you come far?" (001-030)
> — Kim's opening interrogation, mapping the stranger onto the social grid he knows

> "The Lord! The Lord! It is Sakya Muni himself." (001-045)
> — The lama's ecstatic recognition of the Buddha image in the Museum

> "I go with thee to Benares." (001-148)
> — The commitment that binds Kim's fate to the lama's for the remainder of the novel
