# Mrs. Logan

Fanny Logan is the widowed mother of Polly Logan and a regular presence at Lady Edward Tantamount's social gatherings. She is characterized by emotional openness — she weeps openly at the Bach concert, abandons herself to feelings without shame — and by a tender, occasionally exasperated love for her daughter. In a novel populated by cynics, sensualists, and intellectuals armored against feeling, Mrs. Logan's unguarded sentimentality offers a rare note of genuine warmth.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Logan appears in the novel's first major set piece, the musical evening at Lady Edward's in Chapter 2, where her tearful response to Bach's music provides one of the chapter's few moments of unironic emotion. She reappears in Chapter 16 as the recipient of Polly's late-night confession of a social gaffe. Though a minor character — she speaks few lines and acts even less — her emotional presence and her relationship with Polly humanize the rarefied Tantamount social circle.

## Appearance and Personality

Mrs. Logan is physically slight — John Bidlake calls her "that little woman in black" (002). Her black dress signals her widowhood: her husband Eric died nearly two years before the novel's action, and she is still in mourning. Her most defining trait is emotional vulnerability: "She was easily moved, especially by music; and when she felt an emotion, she did not try to repress it, but abandoned herself wholeheartedly to it" (002). Her body "shook and swayed in time with the pulse and undulation of the melody" (002), a physical responsiveness to beauty that contrasts with the calculated sophistication of characters like Lucy Tantamount.

During the Bach, her grief for her husband rises on the current of the music. The memory of "darling, darling Eric, dead now almost two years; dead, and still so young" brings tears that she wipes away (002). Yet these are "somehow happy tears, in spite of her sadness" — the music admits the reality of loss while affirming something vaster and more comprehensive (002). This is one of the novel's most direct statements of its musical-philosophical theme: the possibility of including sadness within a larger happiness.

## Relationships

- **[[polly-logan]]** (daughter): Mrs. Logan's most important relationship is with her twenty-year-old daughter. At the concert, she watches Polly from two rows back — "her slim little neck, with the pearls that darling Eric had given her on her eighteenth birthday" (002). When Polly turns and gives her a quick smile, "Mrs. Logan's sad and musical happiness was complete" (002). In their later scene (Chapter 16), Polly bursts into her mother's darkened bedroom to confess a social blunder. Mrs. Logan is "almost angry that her anxiety had been for nothing," but "could not help laughing" (016). Their relationship is affectionate, exasperated, and deeply forgiving — a model of maternal love in a novel where parent-child relationships are often strained or absent.

- **[[lady-edward-tantamount]]** (hostess): Mrs. Logan is a guest at Lady Edward's events and moves comfortably in her social circle, though she lacks the sophistication of the inner set.

- **[[john-bidlake]]** (fellow guest): Bidlake notices Mrs. Logan at the concert and mockingly describes her as "rolling her eyes and swaying her body like St. Teresa in an ecstasy" (002). His cynicism and her sincerity are positioned as rival responses to the same aesthetic experience.

## Plot Arc

Mrs. Logan's two scenes — the concert (Chapter 2) and Polly's midnight confession (Chapter 16) — form a small but coherent arc. In the first, she mourns her husband publicly through the medium of music; in the second, she is roused from sleep to comfort her daughter. Both scenes establish her as a character defined by emotional availability — she is there to feel, and to be felt with.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Logan embodies a rare quality in *Point Counter Point*: sincerity without naivety. She is not immune to grief — her tears for Eric are real — but she does not armor herself against feeling as the novel's intellectuals do. Her response to Bach — the sense that sadness is "included within some vaster, more comprehensive happiness" (002) — articulates a possibility that the novel's more sophisticated characters cannot reach. Philip Quarles intellectualizes; Lucy Tantamount sensualizes; John Bidlake aestheticizes. Only Mrs. Logan simply feels, and in feeling, finds consolation.
