# Cuthbert Arkwright

Cuthbert Arkwright is a comic-satiric minor character, a publisher of pornographic literature masquerading as artistic patronage, who appears at the social gatherings around Lucy Tantamount and Maurice Spandrell. He provides comic relief through his noisy, principled drunkenness, but also serves as an object of Huxley's satire on the conflation of libertinism with artistic freedom.

## Character Overview

Arkwright makes his living — "and in the process convinced himself that he was serving the arts" (010) — by "printing limited and expensive editions of the more scabrous specimens of the native and foreign literatures." His rationalization is that he is fighting the good fight against censorship and philistinism: "if he boasted of his fornications, it was because respectable people had thought Blake a madman, because Bowdler had edited Shakespeare, and the author of *Madame Bovary* had been prosecuted" (010). The ironic gap between his high-minded justification and his thoroughly self-interested behaviour is the source of his comic effect.

## Appearance and Personality

Huxley describes Arkwright as "blond, beef-red, with green and bulging eyes, his large face shining" (010). He is physically excessive in a way that mirrors his behavioural excess. His noisiness and drunkenness are presented as deliberate: "He had an idea that by bawling and behaving offensively he was defending art against the philistines. Tipsy, he felt himself arrayed on the side of the angels, of Baudelaire, of Edgar Allan Poe, of De Quincey, against the dull unspiritual mob" (010).

Spandrell's assessment of Arkwright — and of his companions Willie Weaver and Peter Slipe — is memorably cruel: "They look like the advertisement of a patent medicine... Slipe's the patient before, Weaver's the same after one bottle, and Cuthbert Arkwright illustrates the appalling results of taking the complete cure" (010).

## Relationships

- **[[lucy-tantamount]]** (social connection): Arkwright is one of Lucy's circle, and his query about why she always writes in pencil — "I simply cannot read what you write" (010) — becomes a recurring motif. Walter Bidlake later recalls this question with contempt, thinking "The lout!" (025).

- **[[maurice-spandrell]]** (fellow decadent): Spandrell treats Arkwright with characteristic contempt, but they move in the same circles. Spandrell's famous put-down — "The trouble with Cuthbert is that he's never quite learnt to distinguish art from pornography" (010) — is both a diagnosis of Arkwright and an ironic comment on Spandrell's own condition, since the line between art and obscenity is one Spandrell himself deliberately blurs.

- **[[mark-rampion]]** (antagonist): Rampion takes Arkwright's approbation as "a personal insult" (010). When Arkwright shouts "Hear, hear!" to one of Rampion's points, Rampion frowns — the endorsement of a pornographer devalues the argument, and Rampion's philosophy of "phallism" must be distinguished from Arkwright's mere libertinism. The difference between Rampion's healthy animality and Arkwright's decadent excess is essential to the novel's moral scheme.

## Plot Arc

Arkwright appears primarily in the restaurant scene of Chapter 10, where he invades the Rampions' quiet corner with a "loud and alcoholic party" (010) including Willie Weaver and Peter Slipe. He attempts to persuade Rampion to let him publish his work, is put down by Spandrell, and engages in the wider debate about sex, art, and civilization before departing for another party. He resurfaces briefly later, asking "Why the devil doesn't Quarles come back to London?" in hopes of securing a preface for his new illustrated edition (026).

## Thematic Significance

Cuthbert Arkwright represents the degradation of genuine artistic rebellion into mere commercialized obscenity. He borrows the language of artistic freedom — Baudelaire, Poe, Blake — to justify behaviour that is simply self-indulgent. His character is a counterpoint to Rampion: where Rampion preaches an integrated life of body *and* spirit, Arkwright represents body without spirit, appetite without purpose.

He also plays a structural role as one of the "noisy" elements from which Lucy Tantamount seeks distraction. Her social world is populated by Arkwrights — loud, drunk, fundamentally unserious people whose presence prevents any genuine conversation or connection.
