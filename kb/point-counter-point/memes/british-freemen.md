## Organization Overview

The British Freemen (formally the Brotherhood of British Freemen) is a paramilitary political organization founded and led by Everard Webley. Its members wear distinctive green uniforms — described by Lady Edward Tantamount as resembling "the male chorus at a musical comedy" — and operate under "military discipline" that Webley enforces with menacing intensity. The organization's stated mission, in Webley's own words, is "to keep the world safe for intelligence," though this claim is immediately undercut by the novel's satirical framing: Illidge recalls a Daily Herald cartoon depicting uniformed Freemen "kicking and bludgeoning a workman to death" while a capitalist looks on approvingly.

## Key Members

- **Everard Webley** (founder and leader): The charismatic and authoritarian head of the Freemen, who leads rallies on horseback in Hyde Park. His assassination by Maurice Spandrell becomes a major plot event, and the posthumous martyrdom turns him into an even more potent political symbol — as Spandrell notes on a postcard, "the dead lion seems likely to do much more damage than the live dog."

- **Hugo Brockle** (member): A young Freemen member who chafes under Webley's military discipline. When he reports late after spending the afternoon with Polly Logan, Webley's quiet fury reveals the latent violence beneath the organization's surface respectability.

## Narrative Function

The British Freemen serve as the novel's political counterpoint — representing the authoritarian, nationalist response to social disorder that Huxley saw rising across Europe in the 1920s. They appear at several key moments:

At Lady Edward's party, Webley attempts to recruit Lord Edward Tantamount, only to encounter the Old Man's mulish refusal: "I'm not interested in politics" — a rejection that exposes the limits of political ideology against scientific absorption. The Freemen's Hyde Park rally draws an enormous crowd, with Webley addressing his "green-coated ranks" on horseback, an image Philip Quarles records in his notebook as a "gargoylish symbol of demagogy." The Freemen's paramilitary violence — members beating a heckler — is immediately repressed by Webley himself, who insists on keeping ranks, revealing the tension between the movement's public discipline and its thuggish reality.

The organization's most dramatic appearance comes after Webley's assassination, when Spandrell sends an anonymous letter to the "Secretary General, Brotherhood of British Freemen" revealing the murderer's location. Three uniformed Freemen arrive at Spandrell's flat and shoot him dead — an extrajudicial execution that completes the cycle of political violence.

## Real-World Counterpart

Huxley modeled the British Freemen on the British Union of Fascists, founded by Oswald Mosley in 1932 — though Point Counter Point was published in 1928, predating Mosley's organization. The green uniforms, paramilitary structure, and nationalist rhetoric more directly evoke the Freikorps movements of post-WWI Germany and the early fascist street movements of the 1920s. The "B.B.F." abbreviation and Hyde Park rallies suggest Huxley was extrapolating from observable political trends rather than depicting a specific existing organization.
