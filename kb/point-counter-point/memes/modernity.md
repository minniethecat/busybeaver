# Modernity

*Point Counter Point* captures a specific historical moment — London in the late 1920s — when the Victorian world had definitively ended but its replacement had not yet taken stable form. The novel's contrapuntal structure itself embodies modernity's characteristic simultaneity: multiple consciousnesses, multiple value systems, multiple tempos of life all sounding together.

## Conceptual Definition

Modernity in the novel is less a doctrine than a condition: the experience of living after tradition, when inherited certainties about class, gender, religion, and art have dissolved. Characters navigate this condition differently — some embrace the dissolution (Lucy Tantamount), others resist it (Marjorie Carling), and still others attempt to theorize their way through it (Philip Quarles).

## Textual Manifestations

### Character Vessels

**Lucy Tantamount** is modernity incarnate: self-possessed, sexually autonomous, emotionally detached. She treats love as a series of discrete episodes rather than a narrative with a telos. Her lifestyle — fast cars, fashionable parties, serial affairs — would have been unthinkable for a woman of her class a generation earlier.

**Everard Webley** represents modernity's dark political face: the fascist response to liberal drift. His British Freemen movement channels the hunger for order and meaning that modernity simultaneously creates and frustrates.

**Lord Edward Tantamount** embodies the benign version of modern specialization: absorbed in his laboratory, indifferent to politics, a man for whom the scientific method has replaced all other forms of knowing.

### Narrative Carriers

The novel's many vehicular journeys — Walter's bus rides, Lucy's motor car — map the modern city as a space of dislocation. Characters are perpetually in transit between emotional states as they are between postal districts. The speed of modern transport parallels the speed of modern emotional life.

## Intellectual Lineage

Huxley was writing in the shadow of T. S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* (1922), which had diagnosed modernity as spiritual sterility. But Huxley's approach is more novelistic and less mythopoeic: he shows modernity through the texture of daily life rather than through symbolic landscapes. Oswald Spengler's *Decline of the West* (1918) and the broader discourse of cultural pessimism inform the novel's ambivalent relationship to progress.

## Contrapuntal Relationships

Modernity forms counterpoint with:
- **Love**: Traditional romantic commitment versus modern serial desire
- **Religion**: Inherited faith versus secular modernity
- **Science and art**: Modernity's twin engines, each claiming to succeed where religion failed
- **Death**: The modern capacity for mass death (World War I, political violence) versus individual mortality
