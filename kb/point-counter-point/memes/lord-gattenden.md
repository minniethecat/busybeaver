# Lord Gattenden

Lord Gattenden, the fifth Marquess, is the elder brother of Lord Edward Tantamount and the head of the Tantamount family. Crippled and confined to a bath chair drawn by a grey donkey, he has spent his life in intellectual pursuits — a philosophical counterpoint to his brother's scientific ones — and appears in the novel as a figure of both comic absurdity and unexpected pathos.

## Character Overview

The fifth Marquess lives permanently at Gattenden, the family's colossal Strawberry Hill Gothic castle, not because he likes the house or scenery — he "was hardly aware of them" (027) — but because only at Gattenden can he safely go for drives in his bath chair. "Pall Mall is no place for grey donkeys and paralytic old gentlemen who read and meditate as they drive" (027). He has ceded Tantamount House in London to his brother and retreated into a world of pure abstraction.

## Appearance and Personality

Lord Gattenden's defining characteristic is the complete detachment of his mind from his body. He is a cripple, but "which luckily doesn't prevent the mind from running," he is fond of saying (027). His mind has been "running, mazily, hither and thither, all his life," while the grey donkey only walks very slowly. The contrast between his furious intellectual velocity and his literal immobility is both comic and philosophically suggestive.

The narrator notes that his lack of interest in the visible world is "his revenge on the universe for having made him a cripple" (027). Unlike his brother Lord Edward, who is also physically odd but deeply engaged with the material world through his scientific work, the fifth Marquess has retreated into Platonism — the belief that the world of appearances is less real than the world of ideas. "The world of appearances, as he liked, platonically, to call visible and tangible reality, did not interest him" (027).

## Relationships

- **[[lord-edward-tantamount]]** (brother): The Marquess's only meaningful relationship in the novel is with his younger brother. When he believes he has discovered a mathematical proof of God's existence — reducing "m over nought equals infinity" to the startling conclusion that "a positive number is the product of zero and infinity" (015) — he telephones Lord Edward excitedly from Gattenden, forty miles away. Illidge, answering the phone, reports to the dinner party with deadpan gravity: "It's Lord Gattenden. He's just discovered a new proof of the existence of God" (015). The juxtaposition of this earnest metaphysical quest with the sophisticated cynicism of Lady Edward's party is one of the novel's most effective comic moments.

## Plot Arc

Lord Gattenden appears only briefly — a telephone call in Chapter 15, and glimpses seen through car windows in later chapters — but his presence permeates the novel as a symbol of the aristocratic world from which the Tantamounts descend. The family's monuments — Trajan's column with the bronze statue of the first Marquess, James Wyatt's Gothic castle — surround him, reminders of a grandeur that has dwindled to a crippled old man in a donkey-drawn chair reading a thick red morocco quarto.

His most significant appearance is visual rather than narrative: as Elinor drives up to Gattenden anxious about her sick child, she glimpses through the park gates "Lord Gattenden's bath chair standing just inside the gate. The ass had stopped and was eating grass at the side of the road, the reins hung loose and the marquess was too deeply absorbed in a thick red morocco quarto to be able to think of driving" (028). This image — of life continuing "regularly, unvaryingly, in the same old familiar way" — is "as reassuring" to Elinor as the landscape itself.

## Thematic Significance

Lord Gattenden represents an extreme of one of the novel's central concerns: the relationship between mind and body. He is a mind without a functioning body — a living experiment in what happens when consciousness is freed from physical capability. His "discovery" of a mathematical proof of God is absurd precisely because it attempts to reach the absolute through pure abstraction, bypassing entirely the embodied, experiential world that the novel consistently values over pure intellect.

He also serves a structural function as a counterpoint to his brother. Lord Edward's scientific work is also abstract, but it engages with the material world — salamanders' tails, newt embryos, the slow processes of biological development. The fifth Marquess's mathematics, by contrast, is disconnected from any material reality. The two brothers embody two versions of the intellectual life: one grounded in physical process, the other floating in pure abstraction.

Finally, Lord Gattenden is part of the novel's depiction of the English aristocracy in decline. The first Marquess was Viceroy of Ireland and Father of Scientific Agriculture; the fifth is a cripple in a donkey chair reading quartos in the park. The trajectory from empire-building to abstract speculation is, Huxley implies, the trajectory of a class that has lost its function.
