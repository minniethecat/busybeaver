# Ethel Cobbett

Ethel Cobbett is the secretary and former admirer of Denis Burlap at the *Literary World*, and one of the novel's most tragic minor characters. Introduced as a sarcastic, resentful presence in Burlap's office, her backstory of romantic disappointment, her principled devotion to the memory of Burlap's dead wife Susan, and her gradual disillusionment with Burlap's hypocrisy culminate in her dismissal and suicide — a death that Burlap registers only as a relief from inconvenience.

## Character Overview

Miss Cobbett functions as a foil to Denis Burlap, exposing the gap between his spiritual pretensions and his actual selfishness. She is a minor character but one whose arc carries disproportionate narrative weight: her fate provides the novel's most damning indictment of Burlap's character. She appears across several chapters, primarily in the *Literary World* office scenes, and her story is told partly through narrative summary and partly through dramatic scenes.

## Appearance and Personality

Huxley provides a detailed physical description of Miss Cobbett: "a small woman, black-haired, darkly downy at the corners of her upper lip, with brown eyes disproportionately large for her thin, rather sickly little face. Sombre and passionate eyes in which there was, almost permanently, an expression of reproach that could flash up into sudden anger or, as at this moment, derision" (020). Her physical frailty — the thin, sickly face — contrasts with the intensity of her emotions, creating a character whose appearance mirrors her position in the world: diminished but burning with unexpressed feeling.

Her permanent expression of reproach is justified by her history. Born into "reasonable prosperity," her father's death plunged her into poverty (020). She became engaged to Harry Markham, who was killed in the war, condemning her "to shorthand and typing for the rest of her natural existence" (020). Harry was "the only man who had ever loved her, who had been prepared to take the risk of loving her" (020). The narrator notes that other men "found her too disquietingly violent and impassioned and serious. She took things terribly seriously" (020). This seriousness — which Huxley presents as both her defining virtue and her social liability — is the key to her tragedy.

## Relationships

- **[[denis-burlap]]** (employer, former object of affection): The central relationship of Ethel's adult life. Burlap recruited her from a Birmingham insurance office after she wrote to him about his late wife Susan. She initially worshipped him as "a real writer, a man with a mind and a soul" (020). But she also fell in love with him, and her discovery of his spiritual hypocrisy — the gulf between his talk of Susan's memory and his "disguised and platonic and slimily spiritual promiscuities" (020) — turned her devotion into bitter resentment. She remained his secretary for eighteen months of open warfare before he fired her.

- **[[beatrice-gilray]]** (rival): The arrival of Beatrice Gilray at the *Literary World* was "the last straw" for Miss Cobbett (020). Beatrice, with her thousand-pound investment and her usurpation of editorial work, became the focus of Ethel's jealousy and resentment. Their office warfare — snubbing, snapping, sarcasm — provided the novel's most vivid depiction of workplace hostility.

## Plot Arc

Ethel Cobbett's story spans five phases, told through flashback and present-tense scenes. First, her discovery of Burlap through his writing about the dead Susan, and her letter to him — a letter so emotionally charged that Burlap incorporated its substance into an article titled "Of Such is the Kingdom of Heaven" (020). Second, her recruitment from Birmingham to London, where Burlap guaranteed her permanent employment in "an explosive burst of generous feeling" (020). Third, her gradual disillusionment as she realized that Burlap's spiritual grief for Susan was a performance, and her own love for him was being manipulated for cheap labour. Fourth, the office war with Beatrice Gilray, in which "Miss Cobbett did as little as she could" while Burlap hovered "remotely, but not quite impartially, like a god with a prejudice in favour of virtue" (020). Fifth, Burlap's cowardly dismissal of her by post while she was on holiday, followed by her suicide: "she lay down with her head in an oven and turned on the gas" (020). The narrator delivers this information with devastating restraint, noting only that Burlap could not have foreseen it and that his mood on the evening of her dismissal was "one of unmixed contentment" (020).

## Thematic Significance

Ethel Cobbett embodies the novel's critique of a particular kind of modern cruelty: the exploitation of emotional sincerity by those who perform emotion for profit. Burlap uses the language of grief, spirituality, and platonic love as instruments of seduction and labour extraction. Ethel, who takes words seriously — who *means* what she says — is destroyed by a man for whom words are merely tools. Her tragedy is that she cannot separate language from truth, performance from reality.

Her story also serves as a counterpoint to the novel's other secretary, Miss Fulkes, Lord Edward's devoted assistant. Where Miss Fulkes is content with her subordinate role, Ethel Cobbett's passionate nature refuses to accept the degradation of being treated "as though she were just a machine for taking down letters and copying articles" (020). Her suicide is the logical endpoint of a character who has been systematically dehumanized by a man who "worships" while exploiting, who speaks of "the freemasonry of suffering" (020) while inflicting it.

The contrapuntal relationship between Ethel Cobbett and Beatrice Gilray is also significant: both women are involved with Burlap, both are deceived by his spiritual pose, but Beatrice has money and therefore power, while Ethel has only her principles — and principles, Huxley suggests, are no protection against a world run by Burlaps.
