# Sidney Quarles

Sidney Quarles is one of Huxley's most elaborate comic creations — Philip Quarles's father, a failed politician turned perpetual author of an unwritten magnum opus on democracy, whose entire existence is an exercise in sustained self-deception. Through Sidney, Huxley satirizes the type of the Great Man manqué: impressive in appearance and manner, hollow in substance.

## Character Overview

Sidney Quarles is, as John Bidlake acidly observes, "only a façade, an impressive appearance, a voice, a superficial cleverness, and nothing more" (020-003). Behind the handsome front lives "the genuine Sidney, feeble, lacking all tenacity of purpose in important matters, though obstinate where trifles were concerned, easily fired with enthusiasm, and still more easily bored" (020-003). His life story — traced in a magnificent extended passage in Chapter XX — is a catalogue of promising beginnings that dwindle into nothing, of grand projects sustained only by the apparatus that surrounds them.

## Appearance and Personality

In his youth, Sidney was "broad-shouldered and proportionately tall" with golden-brown hair, athletic bearing, and an intellectual forehead. Now, in late middle age, he is "portly now to the verge of stoutness," his hair "surrounded a pink and polished tonsure," his face "large, high-coloured, fleshy… less moon-like" (020-001). He remains "an imposing figure" — and that imposition is the key to his character: he looks like a great man.

His voice is one of Huxley's cruelest comic inventions: "resonant and full of those baa-ings with which the very Oxonian are accustomed to enrich the English language. 'Really' in Sidney's mouth was always 'ryahly,' 'mere' was 'myah.' It was as though a flock of sheep had broken loose in his vocabulary" (020-002).

## Relationships

- **[[rachel-quarles]]** (wife): Sidney's marriage is defined by his obscure but consuming resentment of Rachel's quiet superiority. "Sidney did not, of course, acknowledge his wife's superiority. But the obscure realization and resentment of it, the desire to prove that, in spite of everything, he was really as good as she, or indeed much better, conditioned his whole life" (020-005). Every grand project Sidney undertakes — politics, the book on government, the massive work on democracy — is, at root, an attempt to match Rachel's effortless competence.

- **[[philip-quarles]]** (son): Sidney's relationship with Philip is characterized by self-absorption. When Philip returns from abroad, Sidney "had only perfunctorily enquired if the voyage had been good, and had gone on, almost before Philip could answer, to speak about his own affairs. For the first moment Philip had been surprised and even a little nettled. But the moon, he reflected, seems larger than Sirius, because it is nearer" (020).

- **[[gladys-helmsley]]** (mistress and secretary): Sidney's London mistress, chosen from the working class so that he can feel "unequivocally superior and genuinely admired" (020).

- **[[john-bidlake]]** (contemporary): Old Bidlake sees through Sidney with characteristic bluntness: "Why is it that women always needs must love the lowest when they see it — or rather him? Curious. Particularly in this case. One would have given Rachel Quarles too much sense to be taken in by such a vacuum" (020).

## Plot Arc

Sidney's background is narrated in a single tour-de-force passage in Chapter XX during Philip and Elinor's visit to Chamford. His trajectory follows a pattern of decline disguised as redirection: from promising university man to failed businessman (he half-ruins the sugar business), from aspiring politician to Parliamentary nonentity, from published author of an ignored book on government to perpetual "worker" on a book on democracy that, after seven years, has not progressed beyond the accumulation of card indices.

In the novel's present, Sidney has just acquired a dictaphone for capturing his "idyahs" at night and is using the "ink cloud" of research on "the history of democracy among the Ancient Indians" to cover his trips to London — where he pursues his affair with Gladys Helmsley, a working-class typist.

The comic highlight is Sidney's study, furnished with three typewriters (including an electric calculating machine), card indices, filing cabinets, loose-leaf notebooks, and a dictaphone — an arsenal of productivity apparatus that produces nothing. As the narrator notes of the electric typewriter: "the motor did everything for you — everything, that is to say, except actually compose your book" (020-004).

## Thematic Significance

Sidney Quarles is the novel's most fully realized study in masculine inadequacy. His entire life is a performance staged for an audience that includes himself — each new gadget, each new intellectual enthusiasm, a prop in the theater of his self-importance. He is the type of the man who substitutes the appearance of work for work itself, who mistakes the tools of production for production, who measures his worth by the scale of his ambitions rather than the substance of his achievements.

His relationship with Rachel inverts the novel's usual gender dynamics: here it is the wife who is competent, practical, and genuinely admired, while the husband fumbles through a series of increasingly desperate attempts to prove himself her equal. This inversion serves as a contrapuntal variation on the novel's other marital portraits — Philip and Elinor (where detachment is the problem), Walter and Marjorie (where weakness is), and Mark and Mary Rampion (where wholeness, uniquely, prevails).

Sidney also embodies the novel's critique of intellectual pretension. His book on democracy — perpetually researched, never written — is a monument to the gap between aspiration and execution that characterizes modern intellectual life.
