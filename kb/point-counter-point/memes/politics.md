# Politics

Political ideology in *Point Counter Point* appears primarily through its most extreme manifestation — Everard Webley's British Freemen, a paramilitary fascist movement that channels the era's hunger for order and masculine certainty. Huxley presents politics as a symptom rather than a solution: the more intensely characters commit to ideological programs, the more they reveal their psychological needs.

## Conceptual Definition

The novel treats politics as a secular religion — a substitute for faith that promises meaning but delivers violence. Webley's movement mirrors the actual rise of British fascism in the 1920s (the British Union of Fascists was founded in 1932, making Huxley's portrait prescient). Parliamentary democracy appears only as background noise, too feeble to address the spiritual void that extremism fills.

## Textual Manifestations

### Character Vessels

**Everard Webley** embodies the fascist temptation: order, hierarchy, uniforms, the aesthetics of power. His assassination by Spandrell is the novel's central political act — and Spandrell's motives are entirely apolitical, suggesting that political violence is never truly about politics.

**Illidge**, the scientist from a working-class background, represents the resentment-driven politics of class. His bitterness at the class system makes him vulnerable to ideological capture.

**Mark Rampion** rejects all organized politics in favor of individual vitality — a Lawrentian position that the novel treats with sympathy but also irony.

### Narrative Carriers

The British Freemen rallies and meetings dramatize the theatrical nature of political commitment. Webley's uniforms and rituals borrow from religion and theater, confirming that politics in modernity is a performance addressed to emotional needs.

## Intellectual Lineage

The novel draws on contemporary anxieties about fascism (Mussolini's Italy, the rising Nazi movement), the legacy of the Great War's political disillusionment, and D. H. Lawrence's critique of industrial democracy. Huxley's treatment anticipates the "mass man" theories of Ortega y Gasset.

## Contrapuntal Relationships

Politics forms counterpoint with:
- **Religion**: Political ideology as secular faith
- **Science and art**: The apolitical alternatives to political commitment
- **Death**: Political violence as the terminus of ideological conviction
- **Class**: The material basis of political consciousness
