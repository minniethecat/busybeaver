# Miss Fulkes

Miss Fulkes is little Phil's governess, a member of the Bidlake household staff whose richly detailed inner life of romantic fantasy illuminates one of the novel's recurring themes: the distance between imagined and actual existence. She appears primarily in Chapter XIV, teaching geography and art to the young boy, while her mind wanders to Ciro's, the Mediterranean, and Lord Wonersh.

## Character Overview

Miss Fulkes is a minor character who exemplifies Huxley's gift for the revealing detail. She is one of the novel's "ordinary" people — not an intellectual, not an artist, not wealthy or powerful — whose consciousness is rendered with the same care as the novel's major figures. Her inner life, glimpsed in a single sustained passage, is a miniature of the novel's larger concerns: the gap between desire and reality, the consolations of fantasy, the class constraints that define a life.

## Appearance and Personality

Miss Fulkes is described primarily through her fantasies. She sees herself "in that lovely Lanvin frock that had been illustrated last month in *Vogue*, with pearls, dancing at Ciro's, which looked (for she had never been at Ciro's) curiously like the Hammersmith Palais de Danse, where she had been" (014-002). The parenthetical correction — Ciro's, which she has never visited, resembles the Hammersmith Palais where she has — is vintage Huxley: the fantasy punctured by reality, yet the fantasy persists.

Her romantic imagination fixes on Lord Wonersh, a guest at the Bidlake house, who "looked like Shelley and lived like Byron and owned half Oxford Street and had come to the house last February with old Mr. Bidlake and had perhaps spoken to her twice" (014-002). Again, the qualifier — "perhaps" — undermines the fantasy it describes. She imagines him kissing her hand, then transforms the scene into a ride in the park, then a yacht in the Mediterranean, then a motor car — the images succeeding one another with cinema-like speed.

## Relationships

- **Phil** (charge): Her relationship with little Phil, Walter Bidlake and Marjorie Carling's illegitimate son, is the professional context of her life. She teaches him geography (explaining why India is red on the map), corrects his pronunciation ("lawn-mower"), and supervises his drawing lessons, which she dignifies with the name "Art" and "Imagination Training" (014-002). Her pedagogical manner is prim and slightly condescending — the tone of a woman asserting the small authority her position affords.

- **[[john-bidlake]]** (employer): Old John Bidlake brought Lord Wonersh to the house, inadvertently providing the raw material for Miss Fulkes's fantasies.

- **[[walter-bidlake]]** and **[[marjorie-carling]]** (Phil's parents): As Phil's governess, Miss Fulkes occupies a liminal position in relation to the novel's central romantic drama. She is a spectator to the Bidlake household's dysfunction, her own fantasy life a private compensation for the emotional chaos around her.

- **[[lord-edward-tantamount]]**: The passages describing Miss Fulkes with Phil are intercut with scenes of Lord Edward Tantamount in his laboratory, creating a contrapuntal contrast between the governess's trivial pedagogy and the scientist's investigation of newt development. Both are engaged in "education" of a sort, but at vastly different intellectual levels — one of Huxley's many implicit commentaries on class and knowledge.

## Plot Arc

Miss Fulkes appears in a single sustained episode in Chapter XIV, intercut with Mrs. Bidlake's garden meditation and Lord Edward Tantamount's laboratory work. The chapter is a masterpiece of contrapuntal structure, with three consciousnesses operating simultaneously at different registers: Mrs. Bidlake's aesthetic-religious reverie among the tulips, Lord Edward's scientific absorption in his newts, and Miss Fulkes's romantic daydreams at the schoolroom window.

The chapter ends with Miss Fulkes feeling guilty about her neglect of Phil, then briskly returning to her duties — a pattern, the narrative implies, that defines her days.

## Thematic Significance

Miss Fulkes represents the novel's engagement with ordinary consciousness. Unlike the intellectuals who dominate the narrative — Philip, Rampion, Spandrell — Miss Fulkes does not analyze her experience; she escapes it. Her fantasies are the consolations of a life circumscribed by class and gender, the imaginative compensations of someone who will never wear a Lanvin frock or dance at Ciro's or kiss Lord Wonersh's hand.

Huxley's treatment of her is characteristic: neither sentimental nor cruel. The precision with which her fantasies are rendered — the confusion of Ciro's with the Hammersmith Palais, the "perhaps" that qualifies Lord Wonersh's attention — makes her simultaneously comic and poignant. She is, in miniature, what all the novel's characters are in their different ways: a consciousness trapped in circumstances it did not choose, constructing a world of meaning from whatever materials are available.

The contrapuntal placement of her daydreams against Lord Edward's scientific work and Mrs. Bidlake's aesthetic meditation creates one of the novel's most effective structural ironies. The governess dreaming of Lanvin frocks, the scientist dissecting newts, the aging woman remembering Pinturicchio — three minds, three worlds, held in momentary suspension by the chapter's form.
