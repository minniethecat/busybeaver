# Mr. Sita Ram

Mr. Sita Ram is an Indian nationalist politician who appears in a single extended scene — a dinner party in Bombay that Philip and Elinor Quarles attend during their travels. He is a figure of comic excess: sweating, oratorical, full of scrapbooked quotations, and devoted to the cause of Indian independence. Though a minor character, he enriches the novel's political landscape by providing an ironic perspective on British liberalism from the colonial periphery.

## Character Overview

Mr. Sita Ram appears only in Chapter 4 of the novel, during the Quarleses' brief stay in Bombay. The scene is a dinner party at the house of a local acquaintance, and Sita Ram dominates the conversation with passionate speeches about British oppression, Indian rights, and the glories of English literature. His function is both comic and analytical: through Sita Ram, Huxley satirizes the contradictions of colonial nationalism — a movement that simultaneously resents and admires the culture of the colonizer.

## Appearance and Personality

Sita Ram is physically unprepossessing. He is described with "brown and pouchy cheeks," and sweat that visibly pursues its course down his face — "he seemed to be weeping for Mother India" (004). A particularly vivid detail captures a drop of sweat hanging "an iridescent jewel in the lamplight, at the end of his nose" that trembles during his oratory and finally falls "among the broken morsels of fish on Mr. Sita Ram's plate" (004). This image is quintessential Huxley: the grandiose political sentiment collapsed into a gross physical detail.

His personality is characterized by florid self-importance and a habit of quoting from his own speeches. He points "an accusing finger at Philip Quarles" and intones, "De Indians drank deir liberalism at your fountains" (004). He follows this with a roll-call of English luminaries: "Burke and Bacon, Milton and Macaulay..." — a performance interrupted only by Elinor's alarm at a toad on the veranda. His speech patterns, with their distinctive pronunciation ("de" for "the," "tinker" for "thinker"), mark him as a colonial subject performing Englishness with imperfect fluency.

## Relationships

- **[[philip-quarles]]** (dinner companion): Philip is Sita Ram's primary audience at the Bombay dinner. Philip responds to his oratory with deliberately misplaced schoolboy slang — "Golly!" — which Sita Ram, "could hardly be expected to catch the full significance of" (004). The cultural gap between the ironizing English intellectual and the earnest Indian nationalist is absolute.

- **[[elinor-quarles]]** (hostess, briefly): Elinor is so unsettled by a toad on the veranda that she interrupts Sita Ram's peroration. He is annoyed at the interruption — "It is vexatious to be interrupted in the middle of a peroration" (004) — revealing his priorities: oratory before all else.

## Plot Arc

The dinner party in Chapter 4 provides Sita Ram his sole appearance. The scene proceeds through several courses of dubious food (including rissoles and sweetmeats that taste "hauntingly through their sweetness of mutton fat") and several rounds of Sita Ram's oratory (004). Under the influence of warm champagne, his eloquence revives after the toad interruption. His refrain — "Dere is one law for de English, and anoder for de Indians, one for de oppressors and anoder for de oppressed" (004) — is the political heart of the scene, but the comic framing makes it impossible to take entirely seriously.

## Thematic Significance

Sita Ram represents the novel's engagement with colonial politics, a theme that runs parallel to its domestic political concerns. Like Everard Webley's fascist program and Illidge's class resentment, Sita Ram's nationalism is a species of political conviction that Huxley subjects to ironic scrutiny. The comedy of his characterization does not entirely undermine his political point — the inequality he protests is real — but Huxley refuses to let political passion escape the deflating pressure of physical reality.

Sita Ram also serves as a mirror for Philip Quarles. Both are intellectuals who quote liberally from their reading; both are somewhat detached from the human realities around them (Philip's response to the toad is cold disapproval of Elinor's fear; Sita Ram's response is annoyance at the interruption of his speech). The Indian nationalist and the English novelist-intellectual are, in their different ways, prisoners of their own rhetoric.
