# Lucy Tantamount

Lucy Tantamount is the novel's most modern character — sexually autonomous, emotionally detached, moving through London society at the speed of her motor car. She is less a person than a force, defined by the effects she produces in others rather than by any interior life the narrator reveals. For Walter Bidlake, she is obsession made flesh; for the novel's thematic structure, she is the counterpoint to Marjorie Carling's dependent love.

## Character Overview

Daughter of Lady Edward Tantamount, Lucy belongs to the aristocracy by birth and to the future by temperament. She treats love as a series of discrete experiences rather than a continuous narrative — the "off with the old love and on with the new" philosophy that so torments Walter. Her freedom is genuine but chilling: the novel asks whether autonomy purchased at the price of emotional connection is worth having.

## Appearance and Personality

Lucy is consistently described in terms of her physical impact on others — beautiful, elegant, desirable. Her personality is characterized by a kind of serene indifference. She does not need Walter; she does not need anyone. This self-sufficiency, which Walter experiences as cruelty, is simply her condition.

## Relationships

- **Walter Bidlake**: Lucy is the object of Walter's most intense desire and the agent of his most complete humiliation. She accepts his devotion without returning it, treating his passion as a natural phenomenon that requires no reciprocal response.

- **Lady Edward Tantamount**: Lucy's mother, whose musical parties provide the novel's social stage. Their relationship is characterized by mutual independence — the aristocratic family as federation rather than unit.

- **Marjorie Carling**: Though they barely interact, Lucy and Marjorie are structurally opposed — the woman who cannot be held versus the woman who cannot let go.

## Plot Arc

Lucy does not change. She remains from first appearance to last exactly what she is: self-contained, desirable, indifferent. Her narrative function is not to develop but to reveal — to expose, through her effect on Walter, the gap between romantic fantasy and emotional reality.

## Thematic Significance

Lucy embodies modernity in its most personal aspect: the transformation of love from a binding commitment to a voluntary exchange. She is what happens to the idea of romance when women achieve the autonomy that was previously reserved for men. The novel neither celebrates nor condemns her — it simply registers her existence as a fact that the other characters must navigate.
