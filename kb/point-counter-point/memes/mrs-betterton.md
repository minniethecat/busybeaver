# Mrs. Betterton

Mary Betterton is a literary hostess who frequents Lady Edward Tantamount's social gatherings. Decades before the novel's present, she was the romantic object of John Bidlake's youthful passion. Now stout and middle-aged, she has become a figure whose intellectual earnestness and moral sentimentality Huxley portrays with gentle satirical precision. Her function in the novel is both comic and thematic: she embodies a kind of well-meaning but hollow Victorian earnestness that has lost its cultural footing in the sophisticated, cynical world of the 1920s.

## Character Overview

Mrs. Betterton is one of the novel's recurring minor characters, seen chiefly at Lady Edward's musical evening and party. She serves as a foil to younger characters like Lucy Tantamount and as a reminder — distressing to John Bidlake — of the passage of time and the transformation of youthful beauty into middle-aged stoutness. Her literary and philosophical pretensions are treated with affectionate mockery, but Huxley never makes her merely ridiculous.

## Appearance and Personality

Physically, Mrs. Betterton is described as "massive in dove grey, with arms, old Bidlake reflected, like thighs and hair that was, in relation to the fleshy cheeks and chins, ridiculously short, curly, and auburn" (002). Her nose, "which had tilted up so charmingly in the days when he had ridden the black horse and she the bay, was now preposterous, an absurd irrelevance in the middle-aged face" (002). This description is filtered through Bidlake's appalled gaze, but the physical detail is specific and vivid.

Her personality is defined by an explosive, emphatic mode of speech: her words "came out in gushes, explosively, as though they were being forced through a narrow aperture under emotional pressure" (002). She is earnest to the point of humorlessness. She had once had "a passion for Burne-Jones" which Bidlake cured her of, "but never, alas, of her prejudice in favour of virtue" (002). Her enthusiasms are always "on the boil" (003), and she clasps her hands together when excited by an idea.

## Relationships

- **[[john-bidlake]]** (former lover): Two decades before the novel's action, John Bidlake and Mary Betterton were romantically involved. He painted his third and best group of *Bathers* during their affair. Now, seeing her transformed by age, he "hated her for being so repulsive, for having once been so charming" (002). When he sees her, he reflexively touches wood — a superstitious gesture of warding-off. His aversion is the novel's most vivid expression of the horror of aging.

- **[[denis-burlap]]** (intellectual admirer): Mrs. Betterton finds Burlap's spiritual and literary manner deeply appealing. She thinks his averted face looks "so spiritual, so beautiful in its way" (003). She prefers, in Burlap's company, to believe that John Bidlake was a cynic — Burlap's uplifting conversation about cynicism, greatness, and art aligns perfectly with her need to take ideas seriously.

- **[[lucy-tantamount]]** (interlocutor): At Lady Edward's party, Mrs. Betterton engages Lucy in a debate about whether cynicism is a product of overindulgent upbringing. Lucy's cool, ironic manner contrasts sharply with Mrs. Betterton's earnest didacticism. Their exchange dramatizes a generational shift in sensibility.

## Plot Arc

Mrs. Betterton appears at two of the novel's key social gatherings. At the Chapter 2 concert, John Bidlake catches sight of her and is overwhelmed by memories and revulsion — she has become, in his eyes, a "memento mori" (002). At the larger party (Chapters 3-5), she engages in earnest intellectual conversation with Burlap and with Lucy Tantamount, discoursing on the cynicism of the younger generation and the importance of making pleasures rare. She quotes Shakespeare — "Therefore are feasts so solemn and so rare" — to support her thesis that children are satiated with amusements (003).

Later in the novel, Polly Logan imitates Mrs. Betterton's emphatic voice — "My dear, how sweet you look in white!" — in her private mirror soliloquy (016), confirming that Mrs. Betterton's mannerisms are distinctive enough to be recognizable in parody.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Betterton represents the residual Victorian moral earnestness that Huxley's novel subjects to ironic scrutiny. Her belief in the soul, her conviction that pleasures should be rare to be genuine, her faith in the uplifting power of art — all are treated as relics of a simpler age, out of place in the sophisticated, sexually frank, and morally relativistic world of Lady Edward's parties.

But Huxley's satire is tempered. Mrs. Betterton is not a villain — she is kind, if overbearing, and her affection for the memory of what she once was (and whom she once loved) is genuinely moving. Bidlake's visceral revulsion at her aging body tells us as much about his own fear of death as it does about her. The memento mori reminds — and the reminder is painful precisely because it is true.
