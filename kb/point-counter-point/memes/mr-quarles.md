# Mr. Quarles (Sidney Quarles)

Sidney Quarles is a pompous but hollow former MP who poses as a great intellectual while filling his study with unused card indices, dictaphones, and calculating typewriters — the props of a book on democracy that will never be written.

## Character Overview

Sidney Quarles is Philip Quarles's father and one of the novel's most sustained satirical portraits of intellectual pretension. He enters politics as a young man of promising appearance and superficial cleverness, but proves to be a "façade, an impressive appearance, a voice, a superficial cleverness, and nothing more" (020-005). After failing in both business and politics, he retreats into the role of philosopher-statesman, supposedly writing a definitive work on democracy which he has been composing — or claiming to compose — for more than seven years (020-006). He appears primarily in Chapters XIX, XX, XXX, and XXXII.

## Appearance and Personality

Mr. Quarles is physically imposing — broad-shouldered and good-looking in his youth (020-003). His characteristic gesture is a "sudden lifting of the chin," a sign of superiority which Elinor recognizes as absurd when she sees it unconsciously reproduced by his infant grandson (019-044). His personality is defined by a combination of grandiosity and weakness: he is "easily fired with enthusiasm, and still more easily bored," lacking "all tenacity of purpose in important matters, though obstinate where trifles were concerned" (020-005). He craves admiration and arranges his life to receive it, particularly from women of a lower social class whom he can impress (020-076).

## Relationships

- **[[rachel-quarles]]**: His wife of over thirty years manages him with consummate tact, steering him away from the business and estate management he would ruin, and preserving his self-esteem while effectively running his life (020-009, 020-010).
- **[[philip-quarles]]**: His son views him with "neither love nor dislike" but with "a touch of amused resignation," until the discovery of his affair with Gladys provokes "sudden anger and contempt" (032-062).
- **[[elinor-quarles]]**: His daughter-in-law finds him "absurd, deplorable" and sees through his pretensions immediately (019-044).
- **[[gladys-helmsley]]**: His young mistress from a lower class; he conducts a cheap affair with her, attempting to impress her with his importance while spending as little money as possible (024-003, 024-005).
- **[[john-bidlake]]**: The painter who originally assessed him as "only a façade" (020-005).

## Plot Arc

Sidney Quarles begins his adult life with inherited wealth — a sugar fortune, an Essex estate, a town house, and a country house (020-004). His political ambitions lead him to Parliament, where he fails to distinguish himself and eventually resigns, blaming the "injustice of his party chiefs" (020-013). After his wife Rachel saves him from ruining both the family business and the estate, he retreats into the persona of a great thinker laboring on a book about democracy. The enormous apparatus of card indices, filing cabinets, calculating typewriters, and eventually a dictaphone serve as "visible proofs of his labour" and "symbolized the terrific difficulty of his task" (020-008).

His affair with Gladys Helmsley provides "holidays not only from chastity, but also from that sense of inferiority which his wife's merits and his own shortcomings had forced upon him" (020-076). When Gladys appears at his home while Philip is visiting, the resulting confrontation exposes the full extent of his hypocrisy to his son (031-016, 031-018).

## Thematic Significance

Sidney Quarles embodies Huxley's critique of intellectual pretension and the English establishment. His elaborate apparatus of research materials — all empty — satirizes the substitution of appearance for substance in public life. His relationship with Rachel, a woman of genuine moral seriousness, highlights the contrapuntal contrast between authentic and performative virtue. The dictaphone scene, in which Philip mistakes his father's dictation for the ravings of a madman, crystallizes the novel's argument that the line between self-importance and insanity is thinner than it appears (032-064).
