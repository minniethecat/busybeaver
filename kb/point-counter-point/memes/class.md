# Class

Class consciousness permeates *Point Counter Point* as both sociological fact and psychological burden. Characters are acutely aware of their place in the social hierarchy, and this awareness shapes their romantic choices, political commitments, and self-understanding. Huxley presents class not as a Victorian relic but as a living force that survives the dissolution of other certainties.

## Conceptual Definition

In the novel's world, class is simultaneously rigid (birth determines accent, manners, expectations) and fluid (the 1920s offer new possibilities for mobility through politics, art, and marriage). The tension between class as destiny and class as performance generates much of the novel's social comedy and its underlying pathos.

## Textual Manifestations

### Character Vessels

**Illidge** embodies class resentment — the brilliant working-class scientist whose intellectual achievements cannot erase his sense of exclusion from the upper-class world he serves.

**Lucy Tantamount** represents aristocratic privilege rendered effortless and amoral. Her class position enables her sexual freedom; she is protected by rank from the consequences that would destroy a woman of lower status.

**Walter Bidlake** occupies the anxious middle — professional, educated, but not wealthy enough to be secure. His relationship with Marjorie is shadowed by the knowledge that he cannot afford the life she deserves.

**Everard Webley** channels class anxiety into political mobilization, offering the middle classes an identity that transcends their economic insecurity.

### Narrative Carriers

The novel's party scenes function as class tableaux, with each gathering defined by its social register — from Lady Edward's aristocratic soirées to Burlap's bohemian literary salon.

## Intellectual Lineage

The novel reflects the post-World War I erosion of the British class system. The war had mixed officers and enlisted men, women had entered the workforce, and the General Strike of 1926 had dramatized class conflict. Huxley treats these developments with the anthropologist's detachment and the satirist's wit.

## Contrapuntal Relationships

Class forms counterpoint with:
- **Love**: Romantic choice as class aspiration or transgression
- **Politics**: Class interest as political motivation
- **Modernity**: The acceleration of class mobility in the 1920s
- **Science and art**: Education and taste as class markers
