# Molly d'Exergillod

Molly d'Exergillod, the Comtesse d'Exergillod, is a French aristocrat and celebrated conversationalist who moves through the novel's social circles as an embodiment of intelligence divorced from life. Her compulsive transformation of every experience into witty talk — her "professional athletics of the tongue" — makes her one of Huxley's most incisive satirical portraits, and her encounter with Philip Quarles provides the novel with one of its sharpest moments of self-critique.

## Character Overview

Molly appears primarily in two contexts: the dinner party at Lady Edward's, where she holds forth to Denis Burlap, and a later scene in which Philip Quarles attempts to seduce her and is defeated by her superior civilization. She is the daughter of Mr. Justice Brabant, "so well known for his table talk and his witticisms from the bench," and the wife of d'Exergillod, "a disciple of Robert de Montesquiou" who "had won the distinction of being mentioned in *Sodome et Gomorrhe* by Marcel Proust" (007). Conversation is her inheritance and her vocation.

## Appearance and Personality

Huxley describes Molly's beauty as "rather creamy and florid" (028), and notes that Philip Quarles is attracted to her despite — or because of — her physical presence, which contrasts with his own cerebral nature. Her manner is characterized by the practised intimacy of the professional talker: "leaning intimately, she would press the rhetorical question: 'Don't you think so?'" (028).

Her conversation is not spontaneous but industriously prepared. "Malicious friends said that she could be heard practising her paradoxes in bed, before she got up in the morning. She herself admitted that she kept diaries in which she recorded, as well as the complicated history of her own feelings and sensations, every trope and anecdote and witticism that caught her fancy" (007). These same friends had "found her, like an examinee the night before his ordeal, laboriously mugging up Jean Cocteau's epigrams about art and Mr. Birrell's after-dinner stories" (007). She is, in effect, a walking anthology of other people's wit.

For Molly, "the raw facts of living, the sensations, the feelings, the thoughts and recollections, were as uninteresting in themselves as so many lumps of rock. They were of value only when they had been transformed by conversational art and industry into elegant words and well-shaped phrases" (028). A sunset exists to be described as "like a mixture of Bengal lights, Mendelssohn, soot, and strawberries and cream"; spring flowers "make you feel as you feel when you're convalescent after influenza" (028). Experience is never direct — it must always be mediated through talk.

## Relationships

- **[[philip-quarles]]** (intellectual flirtation): The most revealing of Molly's relationships. Philip is attracted to her physical presence but ultimately recoils from her because she is too much like himself — a consciousness so hypertrophied that it cannot experience anything directly. When he kisses her, she refuses the role of the palaeolithic lover, asking "what's the point of being natural unless you have enough art to do it well and enough consciousness to know how natural you're being?" (028). Philip's response — "If you were really and consistently civilized, you'd take steps to make yourself less desirable. Desirability's barbarous. You ought to look like George Eliot. Goodbye" (028) — is both a defeat and a recognition. He later reflects that "in Molly he perceived a kind of Max Beerbohm version of himself. The spectacle was alarming" (028).

- **[[denis-burlap]]** (fellow performer): At Lady Edward's dinner party, Molly "serves up" to Burlap the same conversational material she had already presented to several previous audiences. Burlap, himself a performer of spirituality and grief, makes an appreciative audience for her performance of wit.

- **[[lady-edward-tantamount]]** (hostess): Molly tells Burlap she considers Lady Edward "a bit of a fairy" — light and detached — and Lady Edward deflects the characterization back onto Molly.

## Plot Arc

Molly's narrative function is to embody a particular pathology of consciousness — the point at which self-awareness becomes a barrier to experience. Her scene with Philip Quarles is pivotal: Philip's failed seduction and his subsequent recognition that Molly is a parody of himself provide one of the novel's most important moments of self-knowledge. The episode also catalyzes Philip's resolution to speak honestly with Elinor about their marriage — a resolution he fails to execute when Elinor receives him coldly.

## Thematic Significance

Molly d'Exergillod is the novel's most extreme example of what Huxley calls "the disease of consciousness." She cannot experience a sunset without thinking of how to describe it; she cannot feel an emotion without preparing a phrase to express it. Her "professional athletics of the tongue" represent the endpoint of a civilization that has substituted talk for life, performance for sincerity, wit for wisdom.

Her encounter with Philip dramatizes the novel's central contrapuntal theme: the mind divorced from the body, intelligence divorced from instinct, produces sterility. Philip, who has spent the novel observing and analyzing rather than living, sees in Molly what he might become — and the sight is alarming enough to provoke the closest thing to a resolution he achieves in the novel. That the resolution fails (Elinor's cold reception silences him) does not diminish the importance of the recognition.

Molly also serves as a female counterpart to Denis Burlap: both are performers who have lost the ability to distinguish between performance and self, though Burlap's performance is spiritual and Molly's is intellectual.
