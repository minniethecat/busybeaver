# Pall Mall

Pall Mall, with its gentlemen's clubs and political associations, represents the establishment England that the novel's modern characters both belong to and critique. It is the spatial embodiment of tradition — masculine, institutional, resistant to change.

## Location and Character

The district's clubs encode a specific model of Englishness: male, upper-class, predicated on exclusion. Characters who move through Pall Mall are defined by their relationship to this tradition — whether they belong to it, aspire to it, or reject it.

## Narrative Significance

Pall Mall scenes encode political and social power, providing the counterpoint to Chelsea's bohemianism and Soho's cosmopolitanism.

## Associated Characters

- Everard Webley (political organizing)
- General Knoyle (military tradition)
- Various establishment figures
