# Chelsea

Chelsea, home to John Bidlake's studio and the artistic-bohemian world he represents, is London's aesthetic quarter — a space where art, sensuality, and unconventional living find their physical home.

## Location and Character

Chelsea in the 1920s retained its nineteenth-century character as a haven for artists and writers. The district's associations with beauty and freedom provide the spatial correlative to the novel's aesthetic themes.

## Narrative Significance

Chelsea represents art as a way of life rather than a mere profession. John Bidlake's studio is both workplace and temple — the site where paint becomes flesh and flesh becomes paint.

## Associated Characters

- John Bidlake (studio and home)
- Walter Bidlake (occasional visitor, son of the house)
- Various artists and models
