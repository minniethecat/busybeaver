# Weaving

## Definition

Weaving is the process of interlacing threads of spun yarn on a loom to produce cloth. Before the industrial revolution, weaving was the most skilled and best-remunerated of the textile trades, and the weaver was an independent craftsman who worked at home, owned his loom, and sold his cloth in a competitive market. Engels traces the destruction of this class — the hand-weavers — by the power-loom, a machine that could produce cloth faster, cheaper, and more uniformly than any hand-worker. The history of weaving in the early nineteenth century is, for Engels, the history of mechanised capital destroying a skilled craft and reducing its practitioners from independence to [[poverty|pauperism]]. (P04-003, 006-003)

## Engels's Analysis

> The weaver had his little bit of land, which he cultivated in his spare hours. He was thus able to live cheaply and to lay by a little money. But when the spinning-jenny made yarn cheap, the demand for woven goods rose sharply, and the weaver could earn more than ever. This induced him to give up his land and devote himself wholly to weaving. (003-001)

Engels narrates a tragic sequence. The [[machinery|mechanisation]] of spinning, paradoxically, initially benefited the hand-weaver. Cheaper yarn meant more cloth to be woven, and the weaver — whose craft had not yet been mechanised — could command high wages. Peasants left their farms for the loom; agricultural [[parish|parishes]] emptied into the weaving districts. But this prosperity was a trap. When the power-loom arrived, perfected between 1820 and 1830, it produced cloth at a fraction of the hand-weaver's cost. The weaver's skill, which had sustained him through the first phase of industrialisation, became worthless overnight. (003-001, 006-003)

> The power-loom has reduced hand-weaving to the condition of hand-spinning, and is driving the hand-weaver, step by step, out of the field. Hand-weaving is the last refuge of workers who cannot find other employment. (006-003, 006-005)

The displacement of hand-weavers by the power-loom created a [[surplus-population|surplus labour]] force of unprecedented size. Engels reports that by the 1830s, the hand-weavers — once the best-paid workers in England — had become the worst-paid, earning less than [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers. They could not find [[factory-system|factory]] work because the power-loom required fewer hands; they could not return to [[agriculture|farming]] because they had sold their land; they could not learn a new trade because their entire working lives had been spent at the loom. They were, in Engels's phrase, "the last refuge" — the bottom of the labour market, to which workers displaced from other trades descended before falling into [[poverty|pauperism]]. (006-003, 006-005)

> It is a fact that steam-weaving has become so general that the hand-weaver is driven out of almost every branch of the trade. (007-003)

The spread of steam-weaving through the 1830s accelerated the hand-weaver's decline. Steam-power freed the [[factory-system|factory]] from the need for a water-course, allowing weaving mills to be built in existing [[urban|manufacturing town]]s where coal was cheap and labour was abundant. The combination of [[steam-engine|steam-power]] and the power-loom created the integrated cotton [[factory-system|factory]] — spinning and weaving under one roof — that made the hand-weaver economically irrelevant. By the time Engels wrote, hand-weaving survived only in a few specialised branches where the power-loom had not yet been adapted to the fineness of the cloth. (007-003, 007-006)

> The introduction of machinery to the spinning and weaving of cotton gave rise to the factory system and to the modern proletariat. (007-006)

Engels closes his discussion of weaving by returning to the larger arc of his argument. The [[machinery|mechanisation]] of spinning and weaving was the originating event of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism — the transformation that created the [[factory-system|factory]], the proletariat, and the antagonism between capital and labour that structures the whole of modern society. The hand-weaver, reduced from independent craftsman to [[pauperism|pauper]], was the human face of this transformation. His fate was not an accident or a temporary dislocation but the necessary consequence of a system in which [[machinery|machines]] served capital rather than labour. (007-006)

## The Domestic Weavers

Before the power-loom, the weaving family occupied a distinctive position in English society. The weaver worked at home, often in a cottage with a garden or a small field attached; his wife and daughters wound bobbins and prepared the warp, while the weaver himself threw the shuttle. He sold his cloth to a factor at the weekly market and bought his yarn from the spinner. He was a small independent producer, not a [[wages|wage-labour]]er — a man who owned his [[capital|means of production]] and controlled his own working day. Engels describes this [[cottage-system|domestic system]] with something approaching nostalgia, contrasting it with the degradation of the [[factory-system|factory]] hand who owned nothing, controlled nothing, and worked a sixteen-hour day at the pleasure of the mill-owner. (P04-003, P04-006)

## Historical Context

The decline of the hand-weavers was one of the great social catastrophes of early industrial England. Parliamentary committees investigated their condition repeatedly — in 1834, 1835, and 1838 — and each report documented a further deterioration in wages, diet, and housing. The hand-weavers' [[poverty|destitution]] generated a substantial pamphlet literature, and their plight was invoked by Tories, Radicals, and Chartists alike as evidence that [[capital|industrial capital]]ism was destroying the English [[proletariat|working class]]. The power-loom weavers who replaced them worked in factories that were among the most dangerous and unhealthy in the textile industry, inhaling cotton dust that caused "weaver's cough" and working in heat and humidity that weakened the constitution and shortened life.
