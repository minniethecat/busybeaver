# Hand-Loom Weaver

## Definition

The hand-loom weaver was the skilled artisan who produced woven cloth on a manually operated loom, working in his own cottage or a small workshop. Before the introduction of the [[power-loom|power loom]] in the 1780s, the hand-loom weaver was one of the largest and best-paid groups of [[proletariat|industrial workers]] in Britain — a craftsman who could earn enough to maintain a decent standard of living and who enjoyed a measure of independence in his work. By the 1840s, the hand-loom weaver had become a byword for immiseration: wages had collapsed from twenty shillings a week in 1800 to five or six shillings, families had been reduced to a potato diet, children had been sent to work in factories at the age of six, and the weaver's cottage had become a sweatshop. This transformation, which Engels documents in detail, was one of the most dramatic cases of technological displacement in industrial history. (P01-004)

## Engels's Analysis

> In India millions of hand-weavers were finally crushed out by the Lancashire power-loom. (P01-004)

The destruction of the hand-loom weavers is one of Engels's most powerful exhibits in his case against the capitalist mode of production. The hand-loom weaver's fate was not the result of laziness, incompetence, or moral failing — it was the direct and predictable consequence of competition from machine production. A [[power-loom|power loom]] operated by a single worker could produce as much cloth in a day as several hand-loom weavers working the same hours. The capitalist who adopted the [[power-loom|power loom]] could sell cloth at a price that no hand weaver could match. The hand weaver's only response was to work longer hours for lower wages — but this was a race to the bottom, and the bottom was starvation. (P01-004)

> Spinning and weaving was carried on in the workingman's home. (P04-003)

Engels traces the history of the hand-loom weaver from the pre-industrial period, when weaving was a domestic industry carried on in rural cottages as a supplement to agricultural income. The weaver of the late eighteenth century owned his loom, grew some of his own [[diet|food]], and was integrated into a rural community that provided a measure of social security. The introduction of the spinning jenny and the [[power-loom|power loom]] destroyed this world. The weaver who could not afford to mechanise was reduced to dependence on the merchant-capitalist who supplied his yarn and marketed his cloth — a dependence that, over time, reduced him to the condition of a wage-worker employed at home, without the protections that the [[factory-system|factory]] operative eventually won through collective action. (P04-003)

The condition of the surviving hand-loom weavers in the 1840s was, Engels reports, among the worst he encountered. In the weaving districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], and western Ireland, he found families working sixteen hours a day for wages that did not cover the cost of [[diet|food]]. The weaver's children were sent to the [[factory-system|factory]] at the earliest possible age, not because the [[factory-system|factory]] offered a better life, but because their wages were necessary for the family's survival. The weaver's cottage, once a site of independent production, had become a prison of poverty from which there was no escape — because there was nowhere to go and no other trade to learn. The hand-loom weaver's tragedy was that he was the last of his kind. (007-007)

## Historical Context

The hand-loom weavers were the largest group of [[proletariat|industrial workers]] in Britain at the turn of the nineteenth century, numbering an estimated 250,000 in England and Scotland alone. Their decline was one of the most extensively documented social phenomena of the early industrial period. Parliamentary select committees investigated their condition in 1834–35, and their reports — which Engels draws on — are among the most harrowing documents of nineteenth-century social investigation. The committees found entire communities of weavers reduced to [[poverty|destitution]], living on oatmeal and potatoes, their children stunted and diseased, their cottages unfurnished and unheated.

The hand-loom weavers also became a political cause. The campaign for a minimum wage for weavers was one of the earliest demands for state intervention in the labour market, and the weavers themselves were among the most active participants in the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. Engels treats the hand-loom weavers as a case study in the limits of individual resistance to structural economic forces: no amount of thrift, industry, or skill could save a worker whose product had been rendered obsolete by machinery. The weaver's only hope, Engels implies, lay in collective action — in the trade union, the cooperative, the political movement — that could challenge the system that had destroyed him.
