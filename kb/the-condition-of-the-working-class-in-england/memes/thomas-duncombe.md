# Thomas Duncombe

## Life

Thomas Slingsby Duncombe (1796–1861) was a British Radical politician who served as MP for Hertford (1826–1832) and Finsbury (1834–1861). He was one of the most prominent radical voices in the unreformed and reformed Parliament alike — a wealthy aristocrat who used his position to champion the causes of the poor, the imprisoned, and the disenfranchised. He was a supporter of [[chartism|Chartism]], an advocate for the rights of trade unions, and one of the few MPs who could speak to working-class audiences with credibility. (P04-024)

## Role in Engels's Account

> Thomas Duncombe, the representative of the workers in the House of Commons. (P04-024)

Duncombe appears in Engels's Introduction as an example of the political representation that the [[proletariat|working class]] had achieved — however imperfectly — through the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Act of 1832]] and through the agitation that continued after it. Engels describes him as "the representative of the workers in the House of Commons," a designation that captures both Duncombe's genuine commitment to working-class causes and the structural limitation of that commitment: he was a member of the bourgeoisie who represented workers, not a worker who represented himself.

The central episode in Engels's treatment of Duncombe is his presentation of the second Chartist petition to Parliament in 1842. The petition, bearing over three million signatures, demanded universal male suffrage, annual parliaments, the secret ballot, equal electoral districts, the abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and [[wages|payment]] of members. Duncombe's speech presenting the petition was, Engels suggests, one of the moments when the [[proletariat|working class]] came closest to achieving a hearing within the constitutional system — and the Commons' overwhelming rejection of the petition (by 287 votes to 49) demonstrated the limits of that system.

Duncombe's career illustrates a broader theme in Engels's analysis: the relationship between the [[proletariat|working class]] and its middle-class allies. Engels acknowledges the existence of individual members of the bourgeoisie — Duncombe, Lord [[lord-ashley|Ashley]], [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]] — who genuinely sought to improve the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]]. But he insists that such individuals were exceptional, that they could achieve only limited results within the existing political system, and that the [[proletariat|working class]] could not rely on bourgeois philanthropy or radicalism for its emancipation. The [[proletariat|working class]], Engels argues, must be its own liberator.

## Context

Duncombe's political career spanned the period of the [[reform-bill-1832|Great Reform Act]] (1832) through the Chartist period and into the mid-Victorian era. He was a transitional figure — a Radical of the pre-Reform type who survived into the post-Reform era by adapting his politics to the new conditions of mass agitation and working-class organisation. His relationship with [[chartism|Chartism]] was complex: he was not a Chartist himself, but he was trusted by Chartist leaders and was one of the few MPs willing to present their petitions and raise their grievances in Parliament.

Duncombe also played a role in the campaign for the rights of trade unions, particularly during the prosecution of the Tolpuddle Martyrs in 1834 and the broader legal assault on union organisation that followed. His parliamentary interventions on behalf of imprisoned trade unionists were among the few instances in which the [[proletariat|working class]] found a voice within the unreformed political system. Engels treats Duncombe's career as evidence that even the most sympathetic bourgeois politician could not substitute for the independent political organisation of the [[proletariat|working class]].
