# Stockport

## Overview

Stockport lies on the Cheshire side of the River Mersey but belonged functionally to the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] district of [[manchester|Manchester]]. It was one of the substantial [[urban|factory town]]s surrounding the great cotton city, with a population of tens of thousands by the 1840s, almost entirely working-class. Engels includes Stockport among the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] towns that "are almost wholly working-people's districts, interspersed only with factories, a few thoroughfares lined with shops, and a few lanes along which the gardens and houses of the manufacturers are scattered like villas." He devotes particular attention to Stockport's physical setting, its extraordinary concentration of cellar [[housing|dwellings]], and the crisis conditions that afflicted it during the depression of 1842. (002-031, 002-033)

## Engels's Account

### Topography and Appearance

Engels's description of Stockport is among the most visually vivid in the book:

> "There is Stockport, too, which lies on the Cheshire side of the Mersey, but belongs nevertheless to the manufacturing district of Manchester. It lies in a narrow valley along the Mersey, so that the streets slope down a steep hill on one side and up an equally steep one on the other, while the railway from Manchester to Birmingham passes over a high viaduct above the city and the whole valley. Stockport is renowned throughout the entire district as one of the duskiest, smokiest holes, and looks, indeed, especially when viewed from the viaduct, excessively repellent." (002-033)

### Cellar Dwellings

Engels singles out Stockport for having the highest concentration of cellar [[housing|dwellings]] he observed in the [[manchester|Manchester]] district:

> "But far more repulsive are the cottages and cellar dwellings of the working-class, which stretch in long rows through all parts of the town from the valley bottom to the crest of the hill. I do not remember to have seen so many cellars used as dwellings in any other town of this district." (002-033)

The general description of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] towns applies fully to Stockport: "Cellar [[housing|dwellings]] are general here; wherever it is in any way possible, these subterranean dens are constructed, and a very considerable portion of the population dwells in them." The towns themselves were "badly and irregularly built with foul courts, lanes, and back alleys, reeking of coal smoke." (002-031)

### Economic Crisis and Poor Rates

During the crisis of 1842, Stockport experienced some of the most severe economic distress in the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts. Engels records that "for every pound paid in house-rent, eight shillings of poor-rate had to be paid, so that the rate alone formed forty percent of the house-rent." Whole streets stood vacant, the population diminished by at least twenty thousand, and on the doors of the empty houses "might be read: 'Stockport to let.'" The crisis of 1842 was the most violent Engels had witnessed, and Stockport's experience illustrated how completely the [[urban|factory town]]s depended on the [[cotton-industry|cotton trade]]. (003-013)

### Child Labour and Adulteration

Leach, a source cited repeatedly by Engels, told a story about Stockport regarding children's wages. The surrounding towns, including Stockport, were noted as places where operatives could comparatively easily attend the [[manchester|Manchester]] meeting of the workingmen, suggesting their integration into the labour movement. Engels also records a note from the Guardian newspaper about the Stockport Sessions, where two dealers were fined one shilling each -- one of them having "seven light weights and a false scale, and both having been warned" -- evidence of the systematic adulteration of provisions sold to the [[proletariat|working class]]. (006-003, 006-050, 002-061)
