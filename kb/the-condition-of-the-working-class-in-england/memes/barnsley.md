## Overview

Barnsley was an industrial town in the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] during the 1840s, situated on the South [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] coalfield. Its economy combined linen weaving — the traditional textile trade of the district — with [[coal|coal mining]], which expanded rapidly with the growth of [[railway|railways]] and [[steam-engine|steam power]]. Engels mentions Barnsley in his survey of the West Riding's industrial geography, grouping it with the larger and better-known towns of [[halifax|Halifax]] and [[huddersfield|Huddersfield]].

## Engels's Account

### The West Riding Industrial Landscape

Engels includes Barnsley in his enumeration of West Riding towns marked by industrial growth:

> "The West Riding, especially of Barnsley, Halifax and Huddersfield." [002-029]

The passage appears in the context of Engels's description of the physical transformation of the [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] and [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[rural|countryside]] — a once-pastoral landscape now "thickly peopled" with [[urban|factory town]]s. By grouping Barnsley with [[halifax|Halifax]] and [[huddersfield|Huddersfield]], Engels implicitly acknowledges it as part of the West Riding's industrial core, even though Barnsley was smaller and less intensively industrialised than those towns.

The West Riding linen industry, centred on Barnsley, was a domestic (household-based) manufacture that had not yet been fully mechanised in the 1840s. Hand-loom linen weavers in the Barnsley district experienced the same pressures — declining wages, competition from [[factory-system|factory]]-produced cloth, and the precariousness of a trade in transition — that Engels documents in detail for the cotton and woollen industries.
