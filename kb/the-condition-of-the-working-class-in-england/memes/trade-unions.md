## Definition

Trade Unions (also referred to by Engels as "Trades Unions" or "combinations") are voluntary associations of workingmen formed to protect individual workers against the collective power of employers. In Engels's analysis, they represent far more than simple wage-bargaining organisations: they are the first systematic attempt by the [[proletariat]] to overcome the [[competition]] among themselves that forms the foundation of bourgeois economic power.

## Engels's Analysis

Engels treats trade unions as the organisational starting point of the English labour movement. His analysis traces their development from illegal secret societies to legally recognised associations, and evaluates their strategic significance — both their practical achievements and their inherent limitations.

### Legal Origins: The Repeal of the Combination Acts

The decisive moment in trade union history, for Engels, came in 1824, when an "old, unreformed, oligarchic-Tory parliament" enacted a law that "never could have passed the House of Commons later, when the Reform Bill had legally sanctioned the distinction between bourgeoisie and proletariat":

> This was enacted in 1824, and repealed all laws by which coalitions between workingmen for labour purposes had hitherto been forbidden. The workingmen obtained a right previously restricted to the aristocracy and bourgeoisie, the right of free association. [008-008]

Before 1824, secret coalitions had existed — Engels cites a general strike of [[glasgow|Glasgow]] weavers in 1812 and the Scottish miners' association of 1818 — but "the secrecy with which everything was conducted crippled their growth." Once the right of free association was granted, "these combinations were very soon spread over all England and attained great power" [008-008].

### Objects and Methods

Engels enumerates the aims of the trade unions with characteristic precision. They sought to deal with employers "en masse, as a power"; to regulate wages according to employers' profits and "keep it uniform in each trade throughout the country"; to limit the number of apprentices so as to maintain the demand for labour; to counteract wage reductions achieved through new machinery; and to provide financial assistance to unemployed members through the "tramping" system [008-008].

Organisationally, each union employed a salaried President and Secretary — Engels notes dryly that "it is to be expected that no manufacturer will employ such persons" — and collected weekly contributions. In favourable circumstances, "the various trades of single districts united in a federation and held delegate conventions at set times." Several attempts were made "to unite the workers of one branch over all England in one great Union," and in 1830, for the first time, an effort was made "to form one universal trades association for the whole United Kingdom" [008-008].

### Strikes and Their Significance

For Engels, the strike is the union's primary weapon, but its importance exceeds the immediate economic outcome. He confronts the objection that strikes are futile when dictated by market conditions:

> It will be asked, "Why, then, do the workers strike in such cases, when the uselessness of such measures is so evident?" Simply because they *must* protest against every reduction, even if dictated by necessity; because they feel bound to proclaim that they, as human beings, shall not be made to bow to social circumstances, but social conditions ought to yield to them as human beings. [008-012]

The strike, then, is not merely an economic instrument but an assertion of human dignity — a refusal to accept the logic of the labour market. Engels further observes that "the active resistance of the English workingmen has its effect in holding the money greed of the bourgeoisie within certain limits" and "keeping alive the opposition of the workers to the social and political omnipotence of the bourgeoisie" [008-012].

### Trade Unions and the Abolition of Competition

The most profound dimension of Engels's analysis is his argument that trade unions strike at the "vital nerve" of capitalism: [[competition]] among workers. He writes:

> They imply the recognition of the fact that the supremacy of the bourgeoisie is based wholly upon the competition of the workers among themselves; i.e., upon their want of cohesion. And precisely because the Unions direct themselves against the vital nerve of the present social order, however one-sidedly, in however narrow a way, are they so dangerous to this social order. [008-012]

This passage crystallises Engels's dialectical reading of trade unionism: the unions are at once limited — "one-sided" and "narrow" — and revolutionary in their implications. The moment workers "resolve to be bought and sold no longer, when in the determination of the value of labour, they take the part of men possessed of a will as well as of working-power, at that moment the whole Political Economy of today is at an end" [008-012].

### The Limits of Trade Unionism

Despite their importance, Engels is clear that "something more is needed than Trades Unions and strikes to break the power of the ruling class" [008-012]. The unions, alongside [[chartism|Chartism]] and [[socialism|Socialism]], form only one section of the broader labour movement. Engels notes that "the incredible frequency of these strikes proves best of all to what extent the social war has broken out in England" [008-020], yet the unions alone cannot resolve the structural antagonism between capital and labour.

## Historical Context

Engels wrote at a pivotal moment in British trade union history. The repeal of the [[combination-laws|Combination Acts]] in 1824–1825 had opened a legal space for union organisation, but the 1834 case of the Tolpuddle Martyrs — prosecuted under an obscure law against administering secret oaths — demonstrated the continued vulnerability of unions to legal repression. [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]]'s Grand National Consolidated Trades Union, founded in 1834, represented the first attempt at a national union federation, but collapsed within a year. Engels's analysis, written in 1844–1845, captures trade unionism at the threshold between its illegal, clandestine phase and its emergence as a permanent feature of British industrial society.

## Related Concepts

Trade unions are intimately connected to [[strikes]] (their primary tactical instrument), [[competition]] (the economic mechanism they seek to overcome among workers), [[chartism]] (the political movement representing the same constituency), and [[socialism]] (which Engels presents as the theoretical complement to practical union organisation).
