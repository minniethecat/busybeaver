# 1844 Miners' Strike

## Overview

The 1844 strike of the Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]] coal-miners was one of the largest industrial disputes of the early Victorian period. The mine-owners, faced with falling coal prices, imposed a wage reduction on the miners, who responded with a general strike that shut down the entire coalfield. The dispute lasted several months and ended in defeat for the miners, who were starved back to work. Engels, who was living in England at the time, used the strike to illustrate the structural advantages that capital enjoys over labour in industrial conflict. (008-015)

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels presents the miners' strike as a demonstration of the asymmetry of power in capitalist society. The miner, with no savings and no alternative employment, could not sustain a prolonged stoppage. The mine-owner, cushioned by capital, could wait. The owners escalated by evicting striking miners from company-owned cottages and importing strike-breakers from Ireland and Wales. The strike collapsed not because the miners' demands were unreasonable but because the economic structure made victory impossible — the worker must eat; the capitalist need not compromise. (008-015)

Engels notes that even a lost strike has political value. The 1844 strike taught the miners the necessity of organisation beyond a single coalfield, the importance of strike funds, and the identity of their interests with workers in other industries. The defeat of 1844 laid the groundwork for stronger miners' unions in subsequent decades. Each failed strike, Engels argues, pushes the proletariat one step closer to the recognition that economic struggle is insufficient without political transformation. (009-001)
