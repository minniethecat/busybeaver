# Poverty

## Definition

Poverty, in Engels's analysis, is not an accident but a necessary product of the capitalist mode of production. He distinguishes between the poverty of pre-industrial peasants — harsh but stable, rooted in the limits of agricultural technique — and the poverty of the industrial proletariat, which is dynamic: it intensifies during commercial crises when workers are thrown out of employment, and it persists even in periods of prosperity because wages are systematically driven to [[diet|subsistence]] level. Engels's concept of poverty is broad — encompassing insufficient [[diet|food]], inadequate clothing, unsanitary housing, and the constant insecurity that tomorrow's wage may not arrive. (002-072)

> Since capital, the direct or indirect control of the means of subsistence and production, is the weapon with which this social warfare is carried on, it is clear that all the disadvantages of such a state must fall upon the poor. For him no man has the slightest concern. Cast into the whirlpool, he must struggle through as well as he can. If he is so happy as to find work, i.e., if the bourgeoisie does him the favour to enrich itself by means of him, wages await him which scarcely suffice to keep body and soul together; if he can get no work he may steal, if he is not afraid of the police, or starve, in which case the police will take care that he does so in a quiet and inoffensive manner. (002-005)

Engels opens his investigation with this stark diagnosis: under capitalism, capital itself is a weapon, and the poor are its intended casualties. The worker, stripped of all property and dependent entirely on wages, is caught in a system that offers him precisely two outcomes — exploitation through work or death by starvation — and the police exist to ensure that the latter is carried out discreetly. This is not hyperbole but, in Engels's view, the logical consequence of a system in which the means of subsistence are privately controlled and allocated according to profit rather than need. (002-005)

## Engels's Analysis

> True, poverty often dwells in hidden alleys close to the palaces of the rich; but, in general, a separate territory has been assigned to it. (002-008)

Engels documents poverty as a spatially concentrated phenomenon. In every industrial city he visits — [[manchester|Manchester]], [[london|London]], [[dublin|Dublin]], [[glasgow|Glasgow]] — the [[proletariat|working class]] is segregated into distinct districts where poverty is the uniform condition. This spatial concentration serves a dual function: it conceals poverty from the bourgeoisie, who can navigate the city without ever entering a working-class quarter, and it intensifies poverty's effects by packing the destitute together in unventilated courts and cellars. The architectural layout of the industrial city is, for Engels, a material expression of class domination. (002-008)

In [[dublin|Dublin]], Engels found poverty in its most extreme form — a city where "the poor are not housed but packed" and where the dissolution of the old linen industry had left tens of thousands without any means of [[diet|subsistence]]. The [[dublin|Dublin]] poor, he notes, wore rags that could not be dignified with the name of clothing, ate potatoes and nothing else, and lived in [[housing|dwellings]] whose filth exceeded anything found in England. [[dublin|Dublin]] was a preview, Engels suggests, of what the English industrial cities would become if the commercial system continued its course unchecked. (002-020)

> The cause of this poverty lies in the existing social conditions, above all, in competition. (010-010)

The structural analysis of poverty occupies the book's concluding chapters. Engels argues that competition — between workers for employment, between capitalists for markets — drives wages toward the minimum necessary for [[diet|subsistence]] and, during crises, below it. The worker's poverty is not a personal failing but the rational outcome of a system in which labour power is a commodity whose price fluctuates with supply and demand. When the supply of workers exceeds the demand — as it always does, thanks to the [[surplus-population|industrial reserve army]] created by machinery — wages fall and poverty deepens. This is not a malfunction of capitalism but its normal operation. (010-010)

The moral dimension of poverty is a recurring theme. Engels documents how the bourgeoisie, having created the conditions of working-class destitution, then blames the worker for the consequences: "poverty is a crime, and, as such, to be visited with all the penalties and all the terrors of the law" (011-010). The poor are imprisoned for vagrancy, punished for theft committed under the pressure of hunger, and lectured on thrift by the same class that pays them starvation wages. This hypocrisy — creating poverty and then criminalising its victims — is, for Engels, one of the bourgeoisie's defining moral characteristics. (011-010)

### The Mechanism of Impoverishment

Engels traces the concrete mechanisms through which capitalism generates poverty. During periods of prosperity, wages rise enough to enable bare subsistence, but the worker's position remains fundamentally insecure: he "consumes today what he earned yesterday" and is "subject to every possible chance" (005-026). A single illness, accident, or commercial fluctuation can plunge him into destitution. The security that the peasant derived from his land — however meagre — is entirely absent for the proletarian, who owns nothing but his labour power and must sell it daily or starve. (005-026)

This insecurity is, in Engels's analysis, more demoralising than poverty itself. The worker lives suspended between subsistence and starvation, never knowing whether tomorrow will bring employment or the workhouse. Engels observes that the smaller peasants of Germany are "usually poor, and often suffer want, but they are less at the mercy of accident, they have at least something secure" (005-026). The proletarian, by contrast, has no cushion whatsoever against the fluctuations of the market — a structural vulnerability that ensures even the employed worker lives in a state of permanent precarity.

### Poverty and Crime

> The workingman lived in poverty and want, and saw that others were better off than he. He was at a loss to understand why he who did more for the community than the rich idler should be the one to suffer under these conditions. Want conquered his inherited respect for the sacredness of property, and he stole. (008-005)

Engels presents crime as the rational response of a human being placed in an impossible situation. The worker, seeing the immense wealth produced by his own labour concentrated in the hands of idle owners, draws the natural conclusion that the social order is rigged against him. When hunger becomes unbearable, the ethical prohibition against theft — instilled by the very society that starves him — gives way to the imperatives of survival. Engels does not condone crime, but he insists that it is the predictable and inevitable consequence of poverty deliberately maintained by a class that monopolises both property and political power. (008-005)

During commercial crises, this logic intensifies. When factories close and wages disappear, the army of the unemployed swells dramatically, and "want reaches its highest pitch" (003-013). The alternative to crime is begging or starvation. Engels describes the streets of the industrial towns filled with able-bodied workers reduced to mendicancy, hounded by a [[police|police force]] that treated poverty itself as a public-order problem. "When these people find no work and will not rebel against society," he writes, "what remains for them but to beg? And surely no one can wonder at the great army of beggars, most of them able-bodied men, with whom the police carries on perpetual war" (003-012). The "perpetual war" between police and beggars is, for Engels, the distilled image of class society: a state apparatus that protects property by criminalising the human consequences of the property system itself.

### The Bourgeoisie's Moral Defence

> The failings of the workers in general may be traced to an unbridled thirst for pleasure, to want of providence, and of flexibility in fitting into the social order... But is that to be wondered at? When a class can purchase few of the enjoyments of the civilised state, can scarcely procure the mere necessaries of subsistence, why should it not seek its pleasure in drink? (005-040)

Engels systematically rebuts the standard bourgeois explanation of poverty — that the poor are improvident, intemperate, and morally deficient. He acknowledges that working-class life is marked by drunkenness and apparent lack of foresight, but he reframes these as consequences rather than causes of poverty. A worker who can afford nothing but the barest necessities of life has no incentive to save; a worker who lives in a damp, overcrowded cellar has every reason to seek the temporary warmth and oblivion that [[gin-palaces|gin]] provides. The bourgeoisie, having deprived the worker of every material condition for a decent existence, then condemns him for the moral degradation that material deprivation inevitably produces. (005-040)

### Death as the Final Indignity

> As in life, so in death. The poor are dumped into the earth like infected cattle. The pauper burial-ground of St. Brides, London, is a bare morass, in use as a cemetery since the time of Charles II, and filled with heaps of bones; every Wednesday the paupers are thrown into a ditch fourteen feet deep; a curate rattles through the Litany at the top of his speed; the ditch is loosely covered in, to be reopened the next Wednesday, and filled with corpses as long as one more can be forced in. (011-016)

Engels traces the condition of poverty to its ultimate terminus: the grave. The treatment of the dead mirrors, and amplifies, the treatment of the living. Just as the worker in life was packed into cellars and unventilated courts, so in death he is crammed into mass graves, his body disposed of with a haste and contempt that would be unthinkable for a member of the propertied classes. The [[manchester|Manchester]] pauper burial-ground, Engels notes, was traversed by a railway line — an act of desecration that provoked no protest because the dead were "the outcast and superfluous" (011-016). The fate of the pauper corpse is the final, irrefutable proof of the bourgeois conviction that the poor are not fully human.

## Historical Context

Engels's treatment of poverty draws on two intellectual traditions. From the classical political economists — particularly [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and David Ricardo — he takes the concept of the [[diet|subsistence]] wage and the iron law of wages. From the social investigators — James [[james-kay-shuttleworth|Kay-Shuttleworth]], Edwin Chadwick, and the parliamentary commissions — he takes the empirical method of documenting poverty through first-hand observation and statistical aggregation. Engels's innovation is to fuse these traditions: he uses the data of the bourgeoisie's own investigators to demonstrate the structural thesis that poverty is inherent to capitalism, not a remediable defect.

The concept also reflects Engels's engagement with contemporary debates about the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]. The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|1834 Poor Law]] Amendment Act had abolished outdoor relief and made the workhouse the sole form of assistance — a reform justified by the [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian argument that poverty resulted from overpopulation and individual improvidence. Engels's book is, in part, a sustained rebuttal of this [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian framework. Poverty, he insists, is not caused by the poor having too many children but by the [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] appropriating the product of their labour.

The historical context of Engels's investigation was one of unprecedented economic fluctuation. The crisis of 1842 provides his most detailed case study. In [[stockport|Stockport]], the poor-rate consumed forty percent of house-rent. In [[bolton|Bolton]], rental values sank from 86,000 pounds to 36,000 pounds, while 14,000 people — more than twenty percent of the population — required relief. In [[leeds|Leeds]], the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] guardians exhausted a reserve fund before the crisis even reached its peak. Thousands who had been self-supporting were thrown into pauperism within weeks by a commercial collapse they had no hand in causing. (003-013)
