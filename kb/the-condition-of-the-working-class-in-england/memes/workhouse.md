# Workhouse

## Definition

The workhouse was the institution established under the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Amendment Act of 1834 as the sole form of relief for the able-bodied poor. Its defining principle — "less eligibility" — required that conditions inside the workhouse be worse than those of the worst-paid independent labourer outside, so that only the genuinely desperate would apply. Inmates were separated by sex (breaking up families), subjected to a penal discipline of hard labour and silence, and fed a diet calculated to be just sufficient to sustain life. Engels calls the workhouse a "[[poor-law|Poor Law]] Bastille" — a comparison that captures both its carceral character and the popular hatred it inspired. (011-012)

## Engels's Analysis

> Even children from the workhouses were employed in multitudes. (006-014)

Engels documents the workhouse as a site of multiple forms of exploitation. Workhouse children were contracted out to [[manufacturer|factory owner]]s as a cheap, docile labour force — a practice that combined the state's interest in minimising poor rates with the manufacturer's interest in minimising wages. The children were housed in overcrowded apprentices' dormitories adjoining the factories, worked the same long hours as adult operatives, and were fed just enough to keep them alive. The system was, in Engels's description, a form of state-sponsored child slavery that persisted well into the 1830s, despite repeated parliamentary investigations. (006-014)

> They went so far as to storm the workhouse of Carmarthen. (010-008)

The workhouse was not merely an institution but a flashpoint of class conflict. Engels records multiple instances in which rural labourers, driven to desperation by the refusal of outdoor relief, attacked workhouses — at Carmarthen, at Narbeth, and elsewhere. These attacks were not random violence but targeted collective action: the workhouse was the most visible symbol of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] regime that had replaced traditional forms of [[parish|parish relief]] with a system designed, in Engels's words, "to make poverty a crime." The attacks on workhouses were part of the broader pattern of working-class resistance that Engels sees as the harbinger of revolutionary consciousness. (010-008)

The internal regime of the workhouse is depicted with a horror that Engels reserves for few other institutions. In the Greenwich workhouse, he reports, an inmate died of starvation in the summer of 1843 — a case that came before a coroner's jury and was ruled to be "aggravated by the gross neglect of the workhouse authorities." In the Coventry workhouse, a similar death occurred two days after the victim's admission. These were not anomalies, Engels argues, but the logical consequence of a system whose operating principle was to make relief as unattractive as possible. When the law required that the workhouse be worse than starvation wages, death from neglect was not a failure of the system but its fulfilment. (011-014, 011-017)

## Historical Context

The workhouse system Engels describes was the product of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Amendment Act of 1834, which was itself shaped by the [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian conviction that poverty resulted from overpopulation and that any form of relief that made the poor more comfortable would only encourage them to breed. The Act's authors — particularly Edwin Chadwick and [[nassau-william-senior|Nassau William Senior]] — designed the workhouse as a behavioural instrument: it would deter the able-bodied from seeking relief and would reform the character of those who did enter through labour, discipline, and religious instruction.

The workhouse was also a site of resistance — a fact Engels highlights. The anti-[[poor-law|Poor Law]] movement of the late 1830s, which in the north of England merged with the [[chartism|Chartist movement]], drew much of its energy from popular opposition to the workhouse system. The slogan "No Bastilles!" captured the association between the workhouse and political imprisonment, and the movement's tactics — mass meetings, petitions, and in some cases direct action against workhouse construction — prefigured the forms of working-class organisation that Engels celebrates in his closing chapters.
