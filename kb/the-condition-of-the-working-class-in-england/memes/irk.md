## Overview

The River Irk is a small tributary of the River Irwell in [[manchester|Manchester]], England. Along with the Medlock, it flows into the Irwell within [[manchester|Manchester]]'s boundaries. In Engels's *The Condition of the Working Class in England*, the Irk valley serves as the central exhibit of the extreme squalor, [[crowding|overcrowding]], and environ[[ignorance|mental degradation]] produced by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. Engels's description of the Irk district — its courts, cellars, privies, and tanneries — constitutes one of the most visceral passages in nineteenth-century social reportage.

## Engels's Account

### Geography

Engels locates the Irk within [[manchester|Manchester]]'s topography:

> "Manchester lies at the foot of the southern slope of a range of hills ... Manchester proper lies on the left bank of the Irwell, between that stream and the two smaller ones, the Irk and the Medlock, which here empty into the Irwell." [002-037]

North of the Irk lay Cheetham Hill, where the upper bourgeoisie resided "on the breezy heights" in "free, wholesome country air" — a stark contrast to the working-class quarters gathered along the river's polluted banks [002-037].

### The Old Town

The Old Town of [[manchester|Manchester]], situated between the commercial district and the Irk, contained:

> "the streets, even the better ones, are narrow and winding, as Todd Street, Long Millgate, Withy Grove, and Shude Hill, the houses dirty, old, and tumbledown, and the construction of the side streets utterly horrible." [002-038]

These were "remnants of the old pre-[[manufacture|manufacturing]] [[manchester|Manchester]]," abandoned by their former middle-class inhabitants and left to "a population strongly mixed with Irish blood" [002-038].

### The River

Engels's most famous passage describes the Irk itself:

> "At the bottom flows, or rather stagnates, the Irk, a narrow, coal-black, foul-smelling stream, full of debris and refuse, which it deposits on the shallower right bank. In dry weather, a long string of the most disgusting, blackish-green, slime pools are left standing on this bank, from the depths of which bubbles of miasmatic gas constantly arise and give forth a stench unendurable even on the bridge forty or fifty feet above the surface of the stream." [002-039]

He catalogues the industrial effluents: "tanneries, bonemills, and gasworks, from which all drains and refuse find their way into the Irk, which receives further the contents of all the neighbouring sewers and privies" [002-039].

### The Courts

The courts on the Irk's steep south bank represented, in Engels's judgment, the worst housing he had ever seen:

> "Right and left a multitude of covered passages lead from the main street into numerous courts, and he who turns in thither gets into a filth and disgusting grime, the equal of which is not to be found — especially in the courts which lead down to the Irk, and which contain unqualifiedly the most horrible dwellings which I have yet beheld." [002-039]

Specific details reinforce the degradation:

> "In one of these courts there stands directly at the entrance, at the end of the covered passage, a privy without a door, so dirty that the inhabitants can pass into and out of the court only by passing through foul pools of stagnant urine and excrement." [002-039]

Allen's Court, below Ducie Bridge, "was in such a state at the time of the cholera that the [[police|sanitary police]] ordered it evacuated, swept, and disinfected with chloride of lime" [002-039].

### Cellar Dwellings and Sanitation

Beyond Ducie Bridge, Engels describes the railway cutting that exposed the most hidden [[housing|slums]]:

> "Immediately under the railway bridge there stands a court, the filth and horrors of which surpass all the others by far, just because it was hitherto so shut off, so secluded that the way to it could not be found without a good deal of trouble." [002-040]

He found [[housing|dwellings]] where "kitchen, living and sleeping-room all in one," a room "scarcely five feet long by six broad" containing two beds, stairs, and a chimney [002-040].

Sanitary provision was virtually non-existent:

> "Privies are so rare here that they are either filled up every day, or are too remote for most of the inhabitants to use. How can people wash when they have only the dirty Irk water at hand, while pumps and water pipes can be found in decent parts of the city alone?" [002-041]

### Irish Town

North of the Old Town, between the Irk and St. George's Road, lay the district known as "Irish Town" or "New Town":

> "Single rows of houses or groups of streets stand, here and there, like little villages on the naked, not even grass-grown clay soil; the houses, or rather cottages, are in bad order, never repaired, filthy, with damp, unclean, cellar dwellings; the lanes are neither paved nor supplied with sewers, but harbour numerous colonies of swine penned in small sties or yards, or wandering unrestrained through the neighbourhood." [002-045]

### Pauper Burial-Ground

Finally, Engels records the desecration of the Irk valley [[pauperism|pauper]] cemetery:

> "In Manchester, the pauper burial-ground lies opposite to the Old Town, along the Irk: this, too, is a rough, desolate place. About two years ago a railroad was carried through it. If it had been a respectable cemetery, how the bourgeoisie and the clergy would have shrieked over the desecration! But it was a pauper burial-ground, the resting-place of the outcast and superfluous, so no one concerned himself about the matter." [011-016]
