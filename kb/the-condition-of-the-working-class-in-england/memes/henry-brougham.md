# Henry Brougham, 1st Baron Brougham and Vaux

## Life

Henry Brougham (1778–1868) was one of the most prominent British statesmen of the early nineteenth century. Born and educated in [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], he moved to [[london|London]] and established himself as a brilliant lawyer, parliamentary orator, and political reformer. He served as Lord Chancellor from 1830 to 1834 in Lord Grey's Whig ministry, playing a central role in the passage of the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Act of 1832]], which extended the franchise and redistributed parliamentary seats.

Brougham was a founder of the Society for the Diffusion of Useful Knowledge (1826), an advocate of popular education, and a prolific writer on law, science, and politics. He was also a leading figure in the anti-slavery movement and a champion of legal reform. In the 1840s, he supported the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] and the programme of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] that Engels would subject to sustained critique.

## Role in Engels's Account

Brougham appears in a single sentence of Engels's text, but that sentence carries the weight of an entire political argument. After documenting the systematic immiseration of the [[proletariat|working class]], Engels turns to the Liberal claim that [[free-trade|Free Trade]] would relieve workers' suffering:

> "And that is Free Trade. Lord Brougham said we ought to put by something for a rainy day. But the workingman has no rainy day; his whole life is one long rainy day, and the bourgeoisie tells him to put by something!" (006-057)

The irony is savage. Brougham's bourgeois maxim — "put by something for a rainy day" — assumes a regularity of income and a margin of surplus that the [[proletariat|working class]] simply does not possess. For the worker, Engels insists, every day is a rainy day: wages are at or near [[diet|subsistence]], employment is insecure, and the cycle of prosperity and crisis makes saving structurally impossible. The ad[[prostitution|vice]] to save, offered by a Lord Chancellor whose own wealth was secure, is not merely useless but cruel — a way of blaming the poor for their poverty.

Engels's citation of Brougham is part of a broader critique of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] ideology. The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] was presented by its advocates, including Brougham, as a measure that would lower [[diet|food]] prices and benefit working-class consumers. Engels, looking ahead to his 1885 preface, would argue that [[free-trade|Free Trade]] served the interests of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] capitalists, not the workers, by cheapening the cost of labour-power while exposing British industry to the competitive pressures that would eventually produce chronic unemployment.

## Context

Brougham was among the most famous public figures of his day, and Engels's readers would have recognised the name. By invoking Brougham, Engels was not attacking an obscure ideologue but a leading statesman of the ruling order — a man who had held the highest judicial office in the land and was revered by Liberals as a champion of reform. The choice of target is deliberate: if even the most enlightened of the bourgeoisie offer nothing but hollow ad[[prostitution|vice]], what can be expected of the class as a whole?

## Legacy

Brougham's reputation today rests on his legal reforms, his role in the 1832 Reform Act, and the carriage that bears his name (the Brougham). His [[free-trade|Free Trade]] advocacy, like that of [[john-bright|John Bright]] and [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]], is viewed more ambiguously in light of the socialist critique. Engels's single sentence — "the workingman has no rainy day" — distils that critique into an aphorism that has outlasted nearly everything else Brougham said.
