# Industrial Revolution

## Definition

The industrial revolution is Engels's term for the transformation of English society — beginning around 1760 and accelerating through the Napoleonic Wars — by which machine production replaced handicraft, the [[factory-system|factory]] system superseded domestic industry, and a wage-dependent proletariat displaced independent artisans and yeomen. Engels describes it not merely as a technological shift but as "a revolution which altered the whole civil society": it reorganised [[property|property relations]], concentrated population in [[urban|manufacturing town]]s, and created the two great antagonistic classes — bourgeoisie and proletariat — whose relation dominates the rest of his book. (P04-001, P04-022)

The pre-industrial system of domestic manufacture, Engels explains, was rural, dispersed, and relatively humane. The weaver owned his loom, worked at his own pace, cultivated a small plot of land, and retained a measure of independence. He was not rich, but he was not a proletarian — he owned his [[capital|means of production]] and had some control over his working life. Machinery destroyed this world. (P04-003, P04-006)

## Engels's Analysis

> The history of the proletariat in England begins with the second half of the last century, with the invention of the steam-engine and of machinery for working cotton. These inventions gave rise, as is well known, to an industrial revolution, a revolution which altered the whole civil society. (P04-001)

Engels locates the industrial revolution's proximate cause in the mechanical inventions of the late eighteenth century — most decisively James Hargreaves's [[machinery|spinning-jenny]] (1764), [[richard-arkwright|Richard Arkwright]]'s water-frame (1769), and [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s [[machinery|steam-engine]] (1769). But he argues that these inventions were themselves responses to a prior market expansion: the growth of cotton imports from the colonies had created a demand that [[spinning|hand-spinning]] could not satisfy. The invention of machinery was thus "occasioned by the demand for goods — not, as some have represented it, by the need to replace labour." The machine answered a bottleneck, and in answering it, transformed the entire mode of production. (P04-001, P04-022)

> The industrial revolution is of the same importance for England as the political revolution for France, and the philosophical revolution for Germany. (P04-022)

By the 1830s, Engels writes, the industrial revolution had remade the face of England. The old towns of the weavers and spinners — [[manchester|Manchester]], [[leeds|Leeds]], [[birmingham|Birmingham]] — had swollen into great cities. Their populations were no longer composed of independent domestic workers but of [[factory-system|factory]] hands, carriers, builders, and the ser[[prostitution|vice]] trades that attended a dense urban population. Engels describes this process as a violent uprooting: the agricultural peasantry was driven off the land by the enclosure of common fields and the consolidation of large farms, and the displaced populations flowed into the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts, where they formed a reserve of labour for the factories. (P04-022)

The steam-engine was the final, decisive innovation. While the earliest mills depended on water-power and were therefore confined to river valleys, the steam-engine freed industry from geography. Factories could now be built wherever coal was cheap and labour was plentiful — which is to say, in the towns. The concentration of industry in urban centres accelerated the growth of Manchester, Leeds, Bradford, and Birmingham, creating the "great towns" whose conditions Engels documents in harrowing detail. (P04-008, P04-015)

> The hands, eldest children of the industrial revolution, have from the beginning to the present day formed the nucleus of the Labour Movement. (001-001)

The industrial revolution created not just a [[proletariat|working class]] but a class with a distinct political character. Factory workers — the "eldest children" of the transformation — were the first to organise, to form trade unions, and to develop a political consciousness. Engels argues that the concentration of workers in factories and towns was the material precondition for the emergence of [[chartism|Chartism]] and the labour movement. Dispersed cottage workers could not organise; concentrated [[factory-system|factory]] hands could. In this sense, the industrial revolution created the conditions not only for [[exploitation|capitalist exploitation]] but also for working-class resistance. (001-001)

> The industrial revolution has simply carried this out to its logical end by making the workers machines pure and simple, taking from them the last trace of independent activity, and so forcing them to think and demand a position worthy of men. As in France politics, so in England manufacture, and the movement of civil society in general drew into the whirl of history the last classes which had remained sunk in apathetic indifference to the universal interests of mankind. (P04-005)

### The Pre-Industrial World

Engels's analysis of the industrial revolution begins with a detailed portrait of the world it destroyed. Before the rise of machinery, the typical English textile worker was not a proletarian in the modern sense but a small-scale producer who combined weaving with subsistence agriculture. The weaver owned his loom, worked in his own cottage, and retained the dignity of an independent producer even while earning a modest living. Engels describes this world with remarkable precision:

> Shut off from the towns, which they never entered, their yarn and woven stuff being delivered to travelling agents for payment of wages — so shut off that old people who lived quite in the neighbourhood of the town never went thither until they were robbed of their trade by the introduction of machinery — the weavers stood upon the moral and intellectual plane of the yeomen with whom they were usually immediately connected through their little holdings. They were "respectable" people, good husbands and fathers, led moral lives because they had no temptation to be immoral, there being no groggeries or low houses in their vicinity. (P04-005)

Yet Engels is careful not to romanticise this pre-industrial world. The weavers "could rarely read and far more rarely write; went regularly to church, never talked politics, never conspired, never thought." Intellectually, he argues, "they were dead; lived only for their petty, private interest, for their looms and gardens, and knew nothing of the mighty movement which, beyond their horizon, was sweeping through mankind." The industrial revolution, by destroying this cosy but intellectually stagnant existence, created the material conditions for the working class to become conscious of its interests and to organise politically. The misery of the present was not a reason to return to the past but to press forward to a future in which the productive power of manufacture was directed to human ends rather than private profit. (P04-005)

### The Concentration of Property and Population

The industrial revolution was not merely a technological event; it was simultaneously a process of social centralisation. As Engels explains, the new machinery required capital investment on a scale that excluded the individual worker from ownership. Where a hand-loom cost a few pounds and could be operated in a cottage, a steam-powered mill with hundreds of power-looms represented an investment of thousands of pounds. The ownership of productive property concentrated in fewer and fewer hands, and the former independent producers were reduced to wage-labourers who owned nothing but their capacity to work. (P04-007, P04-009)

This centralisation of property was mirrored by a centralisation of population. The [[canal|canals]] and [[railway|railways]] that Engels describes as the infrastructure of the revolution made it possible to concentrate industry in towns where coal was cheap and labour plentiful. Lancashire, which a century earlier had been "chiefly swamp land, thinly populated," became "sown with towns and villages" and "the most densely populated strip of country in England." The canal system was a product of private capital, not state initiative: "These improvements, too, like the railroads and roadways, are nearly all the work of private individuals and companies." (P04-019, 001-004)

The result was a dramatic dislocation of population. Agricultural labourers and displaced handicraft workers migrated to the manufacturing districts in search of employment, swelling the populations of Manchester, Leeds, Bradford, Birmingham, and dozens of smaller industrial towns. A process that Engels captures in one of his most memorable formulations:

> The rapid extension of manufacture demands hands, wages rose, and troops of workmen migrated from the agricultural districts to the towns. The centralisation of property in the hands of a few thus kept pace with the centralisation of population. (P04-009)

### The Scale of the Transformation

In his survey of the cotton industry, Engels offers a statistical portrait of the revolution's magnitude. Between 1771–1775, England imported fewer than five million pounds of raw cotton annually. By 1841, cotton imports had reached 528 million pounds, and Engels predicted they would reach at least 600 million by 1844. In 1834, England exported 556 million yards of woven cotton goods, and over eight million mule spindles were at work in the cotton industry alone, alongside 110,000 power-looms and 250,000 hand-looms. The power consumed by these mills was equivalent to 33,000 horsepower from steam and 11,000 from water — and Engels judged that by 1845, the "power and number of the machines and the number of the workers is greater by one-half than it was in 1834." (P04-010)

The effect on the landscape was equally dramatic. Lancashire was transformed "from an obscure, ill-cultivated swamp into a busy, lively region, multiplying its population tenfold in eighty years, and causing giant cities such as Liverpool and Manchester, containing together 700,000 inhabitants, and their neighbouring towns, Bolton with 60,000, Rochdale with 75,000, Oldham with 50,000, Preston with 60,000, Ashton and Stalybridge with 40,000, and a whole list of other manufacturing towns to spring up as if by a magic touch." The same transformation occurred in the wool districts of Yorkshire, in the Scottish cotton belt around Glasgow, and in the hosiery and lace districts of the Midlands. (P04-010, P04-011)

The transformation of agriculture was no less thoroughgoing. Landed property passed "into the hands of new owners and cultivators"; great holders "applied capital to the improvement of the soil, tore down needless fences, drained, manured, employed better tools, and applied a rotation of crops." Sir Humphrey Davy applied chemistry to agriculture, and between 1760 and 1834 nearly seven million acres of waste land were reclaimed. And yet, despite this, England was "transformed from a grain exporting to a grain importing country" — a change that Engels uses to underscore the revolution's paradoxical character: agricultural productivity increased, but the growth of the industrial population outstripped it. (P04-017)

> The English working-class will not hear of the beneficent effects of free competition and the industrial revolution, because they are the first to suffer from these beneficent arrangements. (P04-005)

Engels closes his discussion of the industrial revolution by contrasting the bourgeoisie's celebration of progress with the proletariat's experience of it. For the mill-owner, the revolution meant wealth, political power, and the triumph of British industry over its rivals. For the worker, it meant the loss of independence, a twelve- or fourteen-hour working day, a cellar dwelling in a [[manchester|Manchester]] court, and early death from consumption or [[cholera|typhus]]. The duality of the industrial revolution — creative for capital, destructive for the worker — is the organising tension of Engels's entire work. (P04-005, P04-022)

## Historical Context

The phrase "industrial revolution" was not yet standard in Engels's time — his usage in the 1844 original German (*industrielle Revolution*) was among the earliest in print, drawing on French revolutionary language to underline the epochal character of the changes he described. Later historians would debate the pace, causes, and dating of the revolution, but Engels's core insight — that the [[machinery|mechanisation]] of production transformed social relations as fundamentally as political revolutions transform institutions — remains a foundational contribution to the historiography of industrialisation.

The industrial revolution also transformed the material infrastructure of the nation. The [[canal|canal]] network, the [[railway|railways]], the roads and bridges — all were built by private capital to serve the new industrial geography. These infrastructural improvements, Engels notes, were "nearly all the work of private individuals and companies," not of the state, reflecting the bourgeois character of the revolution itself. The Duke of Bridgewater's canal, connecting the Worsley coal mines to Manchester and the Mersey, demonstrated the economic logic: cheap coal transport lowered the cost of steam power, which powered more factories, which demanded more coal, which justified more canals. (P04-019, 001-004)

Engels's treatment of the industrial revolution was part of a broader argument about the relationship between technological change and class formation. Unlike liberal economists who treated machinery as an unambiguous benefit, or [[thomas-carlyle|Thomas Carlyle]] who denounced it from a conservative standpoint, Engels insisted on the revolution's dialectical character. The same machines that immiserated the hand-weaver and drove the agricultural labourer off the land also concentrated workers in factories and towns where they could develop class consciousness and organise. The factory system created the modern proletariat — and, in doing so, created the social force that would ultimately abolish it. (P04-023, 003-001)

The destruction of the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] was a necessary precondition for the creation of the modern [[proletariat|working class]]. Only when workers were concentrated in factories and towns could they develop class consciousness. Scattered, isolated cottage workers could not organise; the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, working side by side and living in the same [[housing|slums]], could. The cottage system had to die for the proletariat to be born. (P04-005, P04-001)
