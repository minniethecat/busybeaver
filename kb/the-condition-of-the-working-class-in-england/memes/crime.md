# Crime

## Definition

Crime, in Engels's analysis, is not a deviation from the normal functioning of industrial society but one of its normal products. He documents the steep increase in criminal prosecutions in England between 1805 and 1842 — from 4,605 to 31,309 annually — and argues that this sevenfold increase cannot be explained by improved policing, more rigorous prosecution, or any supposed moral deterioration of the population. The increase in crime, he contends, is the direct statistical expression of the immiseration produced by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism: when workers are denied the means of [[diet|subsistence]], some will steal rather than starve. Crime is thus a form of primitive, individual resistance to a system that has made lawful survival impossible for a significant fraction of the population. (005-042, 008-005)

## Engels's Analysis

> I did not believe that so much crime, misery, and disease could exist in any civilised country. (002-026)

Engels places crime within a triad of urban social pathologies: crime, misery, and disease. These three, he argues, are not independent phenomena but three expressions of a single underlying cause — the competitive degradation of the [[proletariat|working class]]. The [[urban|great town]]s, with their crowded courts, their unemployed Irish, their neglected children, and their total absence of social provision, were incubators of crime for the same reason they were incubators of [[cholera|typhus]]: the conditions of life made lawful survival impossible. (002-026, 002-056)

> The workingman lived in poverty and want, and saw that others were better off than he. He was at a loss to understand why he who did more for the community than the rich idler should be the one to suffer under these conditions. Want conquered his inherited respect for the sacredness of property, and he stole. (008-005)

Engels narrates the career of the working-class criminal not as a fall from grace but as a rational economic calculation. The worker who cannot find employment, whose family is starving, and who observes the wealth of the bourgeoisie on every street, follows a logical sequence: [[poverty|want]] defeats the inherited inhibition against theft; theft succeeds in providing immediate relief; but once caught and imprisoned, the thief is marked for life and excluded from honest employment, making recidivism almost certain. The criminal law, in this account, does not prevent crime but manufactures criminals. (008-005, 008-006)

> The workers soon realised that crime did not help matters. The criminal could at best protest against the existing order of society only as an individual, in single acts, while the whole power of society bore down upon him and crushed him with its overwhelming superiority. (008-006)

The transition from individual crime to collective resistance — from theft to trade unionism and [[chartism|Chartism]] — is, for Engels, the education of the proletariat. The worker learns, through bitter experience, that individual acts of protest are futile against the organised force of the state. The thief is caught, tried, and imprisoned; the burglar is transported; the arsonist is hanged. Against these fates, the collective action of the strike, the union, and the political movement offers a more rational — and more threatening — form of resistance. Engels treats the decline in certain forms of working-class crime and the simultaneous rise of organised labour as two aspects of the same historical development. (008-006, 010-006)

> As usual, these greater crimes were the end of the movement; they separated the more timid from the more resolute, and brought on the catastrophe more quickly. (010-008)

The rural incendiarism of the 1830s — the systematic burning of hayricks and farm buildings by desperate [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers — provides Engels with his most dramatic example of crime as collective protest. The "Swing riots" of 1830–31, in which threatening letters signed by the mythical "Captain Swing" accompanied a wave of machine-breaking and arson across the southern counties, represented the [[farming|agricultural labour]]er's equivalent of the [[factory-system|factory]] worker's trade union: a collective action, however primitive, against the conditions that made life unendurable. Engels notes that these rural crimes had a political character that simple theft lacked — they were directed not against individual [[bourgeoisie|property-holder]]s but against the system of property itself. (010-008, 010-006)

> If demoralisation and crime multiply twenty years longer, who can say what the result will be? (005-043)

Engels closes his discussion of crime with a prophetic warning. The sevenfold increase in prosecutions over thirty-seven years, if it continued at the same pace, would produce a society in which a substantial fraction of the [[proletariat|working class]] passed through the prisons. Such a society, he warns, is unsustainable — not because crime is immoral but because the concentration of an ever-larger criminalised population, in prison and out, creates the conditions for a collective explosion that no amount of policing can contain. (005-043)

## Historical Context

Engels's analysis of crime as a social product rather than an individual moral failing was a significant departure from the dominant discourse of his time, which treated crime as a consequence of individual depravity, idleness, or insufficient religious instruction. His approach anticipated the sociological criminology of the later nineteenth century — particularly the work of Adolphe Quetelet in Belgium and Henry Mayhew in [[london|London]] — while grounding the analysis more explicitly in class relations than either of them would. The criminal statistics Engels cites were drawn from parliamentary returns and the annual reports of the Home Office.
