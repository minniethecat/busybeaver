# Edward Baines

## Life

Sir Edward Baines (1800–1890) was a British newspaper editor, historian, and Liberal politician. He was the son of Edward Baines (1774–1848), the founder and editor of the *[[leeds|Leeds]] Mercury*, one of the most influential provincial newspapers in England. The younger Baines succeeded his father as editor of the *Mercury* and later served as Member of Parliament for [[leeds|Leeds]] from 1859 to 1874.

Baines was a prolific author whose works included a *History of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]* and, most importantly for Engels, the *History of the Cotton [[manufacture|Manufacture]] of Great Britain* (1835). This work, a detailed chronicle of the technological and commercial development of the cotton industry, was one of the standard reference texts of the period, cited by Engels alongside the writings of [[andrew-ure|Dr. Andrew Ure]].

## Role in Engels's Account

Baines appears in Engels's text as a factual authority on the development of [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]], but without the sharp political critique that Engels directs at figures like [[andrew-ure|Ure]] or [[john-ramsay-mcculloch|McCulloch]]. In the opening of Chapter 6, "The Several Branches of Industry," Engels writes:

> "The history of cotton manufacture as related by Ure, Baines, and others is the story of improvements in every direction, most of which have become domesticated in the other branches of industry as well. Handwork is superseded by machine-work almost universally, nearly all operations are conducted by the aid of steam or water, and every year is bringing further improvements." (006-002)

The neutral citation — "as related by [[andrew-ure|Ure]], Baines, and others" — is notable. Engels does not attack Baines's reliability or accuse him of apologetics. Baines is simply listed among the sources whose factual account of technological change Engels accepts as accurate. The political meaning Engels draws from that history — that [[machinery|mechanisation]] immiserates the [[proletariat|worker]] while enriching the capitalist — is Engels's own, but the technical narrative he relies on is Baines's.

In the endnotes, Engels records Baines's work in his bibliography:

> "History of the Cotton Manufacture of Great Britain, by E. Baines, Esq." (013-002)

## Context

Baines's political affiliations placed him firmly within the Liberal [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest. As a [[leeds|Leeds]] politician, he was close to the world of woollen and flax manufacture that Engels describes alongside cotton. Yet Engels's citation of Baines, unlike his treatment of the Liberal economist [[nassau-william-senior|Nassau Senior]] or the apologist [[andrew-ure|Ure]], carries no explicit political critique. Baines's *History* was, by the standards of its time, a relatively dispassionate work of economic history, and Engels treats it accordingly — as a source of facts to be appropriated for a political argument the author himself would not have endorsed.

Baines also co-edited the *[[leeds|Leeds]] Mercury* with his father, a newspaper that supported [[free-trade|Free Trade]], parliamentary reform, and the interests of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]. These were precisely the political causes that Engels viewed as the programme of the industrial bourgeoisie against both the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] and the [[proletariat|working class]].

## Legacy

Baines's *History of the Cotton [[manufacture|Manufacture]]* remains a valuable primary source for historians of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. His civic contributions to [[leeds|Leeds]] earned him a knighthood in 1880. Engels's citation of Baines illustrates a broader feature of *The Condition of the Working Class in England*: the work's empirical foundation rests largely on bourgeois sources — parliamentary reports, economic histories, statistical compilations — whose authors would have rejected Engels's conclusions. Engels turned the bourgeoisie's own documentation against it.
