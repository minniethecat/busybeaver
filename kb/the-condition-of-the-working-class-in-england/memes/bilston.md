## Overview

Bilston is an industrial town in Staffordshire, part of the Black Country iron and coal district. During the 1840s, it was a centre of ironworking, nail-making, and [[coal|coal mining]], with a [[proletariat|working class]] that Engels cites as suffering particularly high mortality rates. Like its neighbours [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]], [[dudley|Dudley]], and [[willenhall|Willenhall]], Bilston's [[forge|iron forge]]s and [[mining|mines]] produced coarse metal goods under conditions of extreme physical hardship.

## Engels's Account

### Mortality

Bilston appears in Engels's text primarily through a mortality comparison with [[birmingham|Birmingham]]:

> "Birmingham shows even during the reign of an epidemic a far smaller mortality than, for instance, Wolverhampton, Dudley, and Bilston, only a few miles distant." [002-024]

The phrase "only a few miles distant" emphasises how sharply mortality could vary between neighbouring towns — [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s relative prosperity and newer building (with no cellar [[housing|dwellings]]) produced measurably lower [[mortality|death rate]]s than the smaller Black Country towns where ironworking conditions were harsher and housing worse.

### Iron District Conditions

Bilston is part of the Staffordshire iron district that Engels describes in detail:

> "In Wolverhampton, Willenhall, Bilston, Sedgeley, Wednesfield, Darlaston, Dudley, Walsall, Wednesbury, etc., there are, therefore, fewer factories, but chiefly single forges, where the small masters work alone, or with one or more apprentices, who serve them until reaching the twenty-first year." [007-010]

This passage describes the characteristic organisation of the Black Country metal trades — small forges rather than large factories, with apprentices bound to masters until age 21. Engels documents that the apprentices were fed "tainted meat" and "meat from diseased animals," that their [[housing|dwellings]] were "bad and filthy," and that physical deformities were widespread.

### Nail-Making

The nail-making trade, concentrated in Bilston and surrounding villages, was among the worst-paid industrial occupations Engels documented. Nailers (including children from age 10–12) earned 5¾ pence for 1,200 nails, each nail requiring 12 hammer blows — meaning the worker lifted 18,000 pounds of hammer weight for less than sixpence [007-010].
