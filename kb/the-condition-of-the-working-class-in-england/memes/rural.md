# Rural

## Definition

The rural, in Engels's usage, encompasses the agricultural districts, villages, and small towns that had not yet been absorbed into the industrial urban system but were being transformed by the same capitalist dynamics. Engels refuses the pastoral nostalgia that characterised much nineteenth-century writing about the countryside. For him, the rural was not a space of pre-capitalist harmony but a zone of exploitation whose specific forms — the gang system, the tied cottage, the game laws — were as brutal in their own way as the [[factory-system|factory]]. The rural labourer was, in Engels's analysis, the industrial worker's counterpart: both were proletarians, both were exploited, and both were being shaped by the same historical forces. (010-003)

## Engels's Analysis

> The cottage-system, in rural districts, made use of by the manufacturers. (P01-006)

Engels's treatment of the rural is concentrated in two sections: the historical account of the transition from [[domestic-industry|cottage industry]] to [[factory-system|factory production]] in the Introduction, and the chapter on "The [[agricultural-proletariat|Agricultural Proletariat]]" near the book's conclusion. In the Introduction, he describes how rural domestic industry — spinning and weaving carried on in farm cottages as a supplement to agricultural income — was destroyed by machine competition, driving the rural population into the [[urban|factory town]]s and creating the industrial proletariat. This was not a one-time event but a continuous process that was still underway in the 1840s, as hand-loom weaving in the villages of Ireland and western England succumbed to the [[power-loom|power loom]]. (P04-003)

In "The [[agricultural-proletariat|Agricultural Proletariat]]," Engels turns to those who remained in the countryside. The condition of the rural labourer, he argues, was in some respects worse than that of the [[factory-system|factory]] operative. The agricultural worker was atomised — scattered across the countryside in isolated cottages, lacking the concentration that made trade union organisation possible in the towns. He was subject to the gang system, in which contractors employed mixed gangs of men, women, and children for seasonal work at starvation wages, housing them in barns and sheds. He was policed by the game laws, which made it a criminal offence to supplement his diet with a hare or pheasant from the landlord's estate, even as the landlord's game destroyed his crops. And he was trapped in the tied [[domestic-industry|cottage system]], which made his home dependent on his employment and turned every assertion of his rights into a risk of eviction. (010-003)

> Rural populations live in a free, healthy atmosphere. (005-003)

The contrast between rural health and urban disease is one of Engels's recurring analytical de[[prostitution|vice]]s, but he complicates it significantly. The rural labourer might breathe cleaner air than the [[manchester|Manchester]] operative, but his diet was often worse, his medical care was nonexistent, his housing was as bad as any urban cellar dwelling, and his isolation made him more vulnerable to the arbitrary power of the landlord and the farmer. The apparent health advantage of the countryside was, Engels suggests, more a statistical artefact of the smaller, more dispersed population than a genuine indication of better living conditions. The rural was not a refuge from capitalism but its frontier. (005-003)

## Historical Context

Engels wrote at a moment when the English countryside was undergoing its most dramatic transformation since the enclosures of the eighteenth century. The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 (the year after his book was published) would intensify the commercialisation of English [[farming|agriculture]], accelerating the proletarianisation of the rural labour force. The railway network, which Engels discusses elsewhere in the book, was penetrating rural districts and integrating them into national markets, destroying local economies and traditional ways of life. The countryside Engels describes — of tied cottages, gang labour, and game preserves — was not a relic of feudalism but the product of a specifically capitalist transformation of [[agriculture|agricultural production]].

The rural also figures in Engels's analysis as a political problem. The isolation of the rural labourer, his dependence on the farmer and the landlord, and his lack of the organisational resources that the [[urban|factory town]]s provided made the countryside a weak link in the chain of working-class organisation. Engels notes that the [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers were the last to join the trade union and [[chartism|Chartist movement]]s, and that their participation, when it came, was often more episodic and less disciplined than that of the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives. The politicisation of the countryside was, in Engels's framework, one of the essential tasks of the workers' movement — and one of the most difficult.
