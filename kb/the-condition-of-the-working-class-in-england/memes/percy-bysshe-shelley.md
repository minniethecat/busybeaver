## Overview

Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792–1822) was an English Romantic poet. Engels observes that Shelley's works were read almost exclusively by English workingmen, noting that the bourgeoisie owned only "castrated editions, family editions, cut down in accordance with the hypocritical morality of today" (008-039).

## Readership Among the Working Class

In his discussion of working-class self-education and intellectual life, Engels identifies Shelley alongside Byron as an author whose real audience was the proletariat:

> Shelley, the genius, the prophet, Shelley, and Byron, with his glowing sensuality and his bitter satire upon our existing society, find most of their readers in the proletariat; the bourgeoisie owns only castrated editions, family editions, cut down in accordance with the hypocritical morality of today.
>
> (008-039)

Engels presents this as evidence of the independent intellectual development of the English working class. He notes that workingmen attended lectures on scientific, aesthetic, and economic subjects, and that "the epoch-making products of modern philosophical, political, and poetical literature are read by workingmen almost exclusively" (008-039).

## Context: Proletarian Education

The discussion of Shelley appears within Engels' broader argument that the proletariat had surpassed the bourgeoisie in genuine intellectual culture. The Socialists had translated French materialists such as Helvétius, Holbach, and Diderot into cheap English editions. Works by Strauss and Proudhon also circulated primarily among workingmen. Alongside Shelley and Byron, the philosophers Jeremy Bentham and William Godwin found their audience "almost exclusively the property of the proletariat" (008-039).

This self-education of the working class took place in proletarian reading-rooms and Socialist institutes, separate from the Mechanics' Institutes controlled by the bourgeoisie.
