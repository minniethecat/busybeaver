## Overview

The Poor Law Commission was the central administrative authority established by the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|Poor Law Amendment Act of 1834]] to implement and supervise the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] throughout England and Wales. Headed by three Commissioners based at Somerset House in [[london|London]], the body represented a radical centralisation of [[pauperism|poor relief]] administration, replacing the parish-based system of the Old [[poor-law|Poor Law]] with a uniform, deterrent regime centred on the [[workhouse]] system. The Commission operated from 1834 until its replacement by the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Board in 1847.

The Commission was the institutional expression of the [[malthus|Malthusian]] and [[utilitarianism|Utilitarian]] doctrines that Engels identifies as the ideological foundation of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]]. Its reports and policies treated poverty not as a social product of industrial [[capitalism]] but as an individual moral failing to be disciplined through the threat of the workhouse.

## Engels's Account

Engels's treatment of the Poor Law Commission is inseparable from his broader indictment of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] as "the most open declaration of war of the [[bourgeoisie]] upon the [[proletariat]]" [011-008]. He presents the Commissioners as ideologues who translated [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian population theory into administrative practice:

> "Words of the Report of the Poor Law Commissioners. This description of the action of the Old Poor Law is certainly correct; relief fosters laziness and increase of 'surplus population.' Under present social conditions it is perfectly clear that the poor man is compelled to be an egotist... But what follows therefrom? That our present social conditions are good for nothing, and not as the Malthusian Commissioners conclude, that poverty is a crime, and, as such, to be visited with heinous penalties which may serve as a warning to others." [011-013]

Engels notes that the Commissioners, despite their severity, stopped short of the logical extreme of their own doctrine. He quotes the original [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian recommendation that poverty be treated "as a crime under the title 'superfluity,'" punished by starvation, then observes:

> "The commissioners were not quite so barbarous; death outright by starvation was something too terrible even for a Poor Law Commissioner." [011-011]

Instead, the Commission pursued a policy of deterrence: relief would be granted only within the workhouse, and workhouse conditions would be made deliberately less eligible than the worst independent labourer's existence. Engels characterises their reasoning with bitter sarcasm: "Live you shall, but live as an awful warning to all those who might have inducements to become 'superfluous'" [011-011].

## Statistical Function

Beyond administration, the Commission served an important documentary function that Engels exploits throughout his work. The Commissioners' periodic reports — particularly their statistical surveys of [[poverty|pauperism]] — provide Engels with quantitative evidence of working-class immiseration:

> "Of this surplus population there are, according to the reports of the Poor Law commissioners, on an average, a million and a half in England and Wales." [003-013]

Engels uses these official figures strategically: data produced by the bourgeoisie's own administrative apparatus becomes evidence against the system that produced it.

## Structure and Operation

The Commission consisted of three Commissioners appointed by the Crown, assisted by a Secretary and a corps of Assistant Commissioners who travelled the country establishing [[poor-law]] unions and supervising local Boards of Guardians. This structure — a central policy-making body with regional agents — represented an early model of centralised bureaucratic administration that prefigured later Victorian regulatory bodies.

The Commission's principal instrument was the workhouse. Local [[parish|parishes]] were grouped into unions, each required to build a workhouse where able-bodied [[pauperism|pauper]]s would receive relief under a disciplinary regime designed, in Engels's account, to be punitive rather than rehabilitative.

## Legacy and Abolition

Growing opposition to the Commission — from [[chartism|Chartists]], Tory radicals, and working-class organisations — led to its replacement by the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Board in 1847, a reform that brought the poor law administration under more direct parliamentary and ministerial oversight. The Andover workhouse scandal of 1845–46, in which inmates were reduced to gnawing bones meant for the institution's dogs, crystallised public revulsion against the Commission's system.
