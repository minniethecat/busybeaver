## Definition

Ignorance, in Engels's usage, denotes the systematic intellectual deprivation of the [[proletariat|working class]] — the denial of literacy, secular knowledge, and the capacity for critical thought. It is not an innate characteristic of workers but a condition imposed by the bourgeoisie, who understood that an educated proletariat would be a politically dangerous one. Engels documents this imposed ignorance through parliamentary reports, educational statistics, and workers' own testimony, treating it as a central mechanism of class domination.

## Engels's Analysis

### Ignorance as Bourgeois Strategy

Engels attributes working-class ignorance to deliberate policy. The bourgeoisie's fear of an educated [[proletariat|working class]] is explicit: "hence the utter ignorance on the part of the whole middle-class as to the real condition of the proletariat" (P04-025) — a willed ignorance that is the mirror image of the ignorance the bourgeoisie imposes on workers. The [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]'s ignorance of working-class life is the moral complement to working-class ignorance of letters: both are products of a class system that depends on mutual incomprehension.

### The Statistical Evidence

Engels marshals the statistical reports of parliamentary commissions to document the scale of working-class ignorance. "This report gives evidence of ignorance in the working-class of England such as can hardly be paralleled elsewhere" (005-020). He cites specific data: in [[manchester|Manchester]], of children attending school, "scarcely 6,500 can read" (007-015); in the mining districts, adult workers who could not write their own names; in the [[urban|factory town]]s, a generation raised without any formal instruction whatsoever. The statistics are not presented as curiosities but as an indictment — a society that produces mass illiteracy after decades of self-proclaimed progress stands condemned by its own numbers.

### The Mechanism of Ignorance Production

Engels identifies the concrete mechanisms through which ignorance is produced. Factory children work twelve or fourteen hours and are physically incapable of benefiting from even the inadequate [[sunday-school|Sunday schools]] available. "As a matter of fact, their ignorance of religious and of secular knowledge is complete" (009-010). The exhaustion of labour makes education impossible; the lack of education makes escape from labour impossible. It is a closed loop designed to reproduce a docile, unthinking workforce generation after generation.

Engels also notes the spatial dimension: workers live in districts without schools, in [[housing|dwellings]] without books, in communities where all adults are illiterate. The condition is self-reproducing, because illiterate parents cannot teach their children to read. Breaking the cycle requires an intervention — state-funded secular education — that the bourgeoisie, in Engels's time, consistently refused to provide.

### Ignorance, Immorality, and Crime

Engels links ignorance to the broader pattern of working-class degradation: "great [[poverty|want]], immorality, and ignorance prevail among the workers" (007-014). The three are presented as mutually reinforcing: poverty makes education impossible, ignorance fosters the "immorality" (drunkenness, sexual licence) that the bourgeoisie condemns, and the resulting disorder justifies further repression. This is Engels's answer to the bourgeois moralists who blamed working-class [[prostitution|vice]] on working-class character: [[prostitution|vice]] is a product of conditions, and the primary condition is imposed ignorance.

### The Political Dimension

Engels's interest in ignorance is ultimately political. An ignorant class cannot organise effectively, cannot articulate its interests, and cannot challenge the ideology that justifies its exploitation. The [[chartism|Chartist movement]]'s demand for universal education was therefore not a philanthropic afterthought but a central strategic objective: only an educated [[proletariat|working class]] could achieve political power. Engels's detailed documentation of ignorance is thus an argument for education as a condition of revolution.
