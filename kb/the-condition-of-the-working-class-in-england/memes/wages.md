# Wages

## Definition

Wages are the price paid by the capitalist to the worker for the use of his labour-power, typically calculated by the day or by the piece. Engels treats wages not as a fair exchange but as a structurally determined minimum: competition among workers for employment drives wages down to [[diet|subsistence]] level, while the capitalist's control of capital enables him to resist wage demands. The worker who refuses the wage on offer is replaced by another who will accept it. (003-001)

Engels further conceptualises the wage relation in structurally subordinating terms. In the capitalist's calculus, the worker is not a human being but a factor of production whose cost must be minimised. As Engels writes:

> The human being, the worker, is regarded in manufacture simply as a piece of capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of wages. (001-004)

This formulation strips the wage relation of any pretense to voluntary exchange between equals. The worker does not sell a product — he sells himself, or rather his labour-power, for a price denominated in wages. The interest metaphor is deliberate: just as the capitalist expects a return on machinery, so too he expects a return on the human capital he rents.

## Engels's Analysis

Engels observes that the level of wages is determined not by the value of what the worker produces but by the balance of power between capital and labour. In periods of industrial expansion, when demand for labour outstrips supply, wages rise. The worker enjoys a brief period of relative comfort — and the bourgeoisie panics, warning that high wages will make English goods uncompetitive. But the boom always ends, and with the bust wages collapse. The worker who had briefly tasted prosperity is thrown back into [[poverty|destitution]], and the cycle repeats. This cyclical instability, Engels argues, is inherent to [[capital|industrial capital]]ism and makes the worker's condition permanently precarious. (003-001, P01-010)

The piece-wage system, Engels notes, is particularly insidious because it disguises exploitation as incentive. The worker is paid by the unit produced rather than by the hour, and the faster he works, the more he earns. But the piece-rate is set so that even the fastest worker barely earns [[diet|subsistence]], and as machinery improves, the piece-rate is reduced — the worker must run faster to stay in the same place. The piece-wage system also atomises the workforce: each worker competes against his fellows for the highest output, making collective organisation more difficult. (005-040)

### The Cyclical Determination of Wages

The industrial cycle is the central mechanism governing wage levels under capitalism. Engels describes the cycle with forensic precision: prosperity, overproduction, crisis, and stagnation follow one another in a rhythm that is as predictable as it is devastating for the worker. (003-010, 008-028)

When trade is good and demand for goods is rising, manufacturers compete for labour. Wages rise, workers spend more, and the whole system appears healthy. But this prosperity carries within it the seeds of its own destruction. New mills are built, new machinery is installed, and more goods are produced than the market can absorb. Engels catches the moment of reversal in a passage that reads like a weather report from the economy:

> Then the collapse comes. Speculators abroad, desperate for cash, begin selling below market price. Prices fluctuate wildly, credit is shaken, bankruptcies cascade — first abroad, then in England. The discovery is made that three times more goods are on hand than can be consumed. Panic seizes the market, firms fail, reserves are thrown onto the market in a moment of anxiety, and the alarm feeds on itself. Mills close, wages fall, and the working class is plunged into destitution. (003-010)

The fall in wages during the crisis phase is not a gradual adjustment but a catastrophic collapse. Workers who had saved nothing during the boom — because Engels shows they could not save from wages that barely covered subsistence (005-040) — are thrown onto the street with nothing. The workhouse and starvation are their only alternatives.

Engels concedes that Malthus was descriptively accurate within the framework of capitalism: wages are indeed determined by the supply of and demand for labour. But he rejects the normative conclusion that this arrangement is natural or inevitable. For Engels, the fact that the market sets wages at subsistence is not a law of nature but a law of capitalism, and one that can and must be overthrown. (003-008)

## Wages in Historical Perspective

Engels traces the trajectory of wages from the pre-industrial to the industrial era. Before the factory system, the hand-loom weaver was an independent producer who owned his tools and supplemented his income with a small holding of land. The introduction of Hargreaves' spinning jenny in 1764 paradoxically raised weavers' wages in the short term, as the abundance of cheap yarn increased demand for woven goods. Engels notes that in this early transitional period:

> A family of four adults and two children could earn four pounds sterling per week with eight hours of daily work — and often more if trade was good and work pressed. (P04-006)

But this prosperity was fleeting. The power-loom soon replaced the hand-loom, and the class of independent weavers was destroyed. Hand-weaver wages collapsed from twenty shillings a week in 1800 to five shillings in 1830. The weaver who had been a prosperous artisan became a pauperised proletarian, and the brief era of high wages served only to accelerate the migration from countryside to town, swelling the industrial labour pool and ultimately depressing wages for all. (P04-006)

## Wages and the Irish Labour Reserve

Irish immigration provided the industrial bourgeoisie with a powerful instrument for depressing English wages. Engels quotes a contemporary observer who describes the Irish worker as a labour reserve willing to work for the barest minimum:

> The wild Milesian features, looking false ingenuity, restlessness, unreason, misery, and mockery, salute you on all highways and byways... He is the sorest evil this country has to strive with. In his rags and laughing savagery, he is there to undertake all work that can be done by mere strength of hand and back — for wages that will purchase him potatoes. (004-001)

The presence of this vast reserve army — Engels estimates over a million Irish had immigrated to England by the 1840s — meant that whenever English workers demanded higher wages, the capitalist could threaten to replace them with Irish labourers who would accept less. The Irish worker thus functioned as a wage-depressing mechanism that kept the entire English working class at subsistence level.

## Wages and the Agricultural Proletariat

The collapse of agricultural wages followed a parallel logic to the industrial case. Engels charts the dissolution of the patriarchal relation between farmer and farm-hand, which was replaced by a system of casual day labour that left rural workers without security or subsistence:

> The farm hands have become day labourers almost everywhere, are employed only when needed by the farmers, and, therefore, often have no work for weeks together, especially in winter. (010-002)

In the agricultural districts of southern England, wages fell to as low as six shillings a week — a sum on which a family could not survive without parish relief. The old Poor Law had subsidised wages by making up the difference between the farmer's payment and the labourer's subsistence; the new Poor Law of 1834 abolished this arrangement, leaving the labourer to the mercy of the market and the workhouse. Engels treats the agricultural case as evidence that the wage system, in all its forms, produces the same result: the immiseration of the worker and the enrichment of the employer. (010-002)
