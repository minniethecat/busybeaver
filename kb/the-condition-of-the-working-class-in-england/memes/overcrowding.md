# Overcrowding

## Definition

Overcrowding, in Engels's usage, refers to the extreme [[crowding|population density]] in working-class districts — a condition in which multiple families occupied single rooms, entire households lived in cellars below ground, and every available space was converted to human habitation. It was not merely a description of density but a structural consequence of rapid industrial urbanisation: the influx of workers into the towns far outstripped the construction of housing, and the housing that was built was constructed for profit, not for habitability. (002-001)

## Engels's Analysis

> The crowding of human beings upon a limited space is one of the most powerful agencies in producing epidemic disease. (002-022)

Engels documents [[crowding|overcrowding]] with statistical precision. In [[manchester|Manchester]]'s Irish Town, he reports finding up to fifteen people living in a single room — men, women, and children of all ages sleeping on straw, the windows boarded up, the floor damp with sewage. In the cellar [[housing|dwellings]] of [[liverpool|Liverpool]], entire families inhabited underground rooms where the ceiling was so low that an adult could not stand upright. The arithmetic of [[crowding|overcrowding]] is simple: wages too low to afford decent housing + housing stock built for speculation, not need = the worker is forced into whatever shelter is cheapest, regardless of its fitness for human habitation. (002-001)

The connection between [[crowding|overcrowding]] and disease is a central motif of Chapter 2 ("The Great Towns"). Engels cites official mortality statistics showing that [[mortality|death rate]]s in overcrowded districts were two to three times higher than in middle-class neighbourhoods. Typhus, [[Cholera]], and [[consumption|tuberculosis]] were endemic in these quarters, and the epidemics that periodically swept through the industrial towns invariably began in the most densely populated streets. When cholera struck [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1832 and 1842, it was the overcrowded courts and cellars — not the suburban villas — that became its breeding ground. The [[Sanitation|sanitary police]] made temporary raids, closing condemned [[housing|dwellings]], but the underlying economic logic reasserted itself within weeks and the [[crowding|overcrowding]] resumed. Overcrowding, in Engels's analysis, is not an incidental feature of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism but its predictable product: the same economic logic that maximises profit from labour maximises profit from the housing that labourers inhabit. (002-022)

## Moral Dimension

Engels is attentive to the moral as well as the physical consequences of [[crowding|overcrowding]]. When parents, children, and lodgers of both sexes share a single room — and often a single bed — the conditions for what Engels calls "immorality" are created. He reports testimony from [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors and physicians documenting the sexual abuse and precocious sexual knowledge that resulted from overcrowded living conditions. These observations, shocking to Engels's Victorian readership, were intended to demonstrate that the moral degradation the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] attributed to working-class character was in fact a product of working-class conditions. (002-011)

> In this overcrowding it is nothing unusual to find a man and his wife, his mother and sisters, grown-up sons and daughters, and perhaps a grandchild or two, all living in one room. (002-011)

The role of [[Irish Immigration]] in intensifying [[crowding|overcrowding]] is a recurrent theme. Engels observes that the Irish custom of crowding many persons into a single room — "at home in his mud-cabin there was only one room for all domestic purposes" — was transplanted wholesale into English industrial cities, where it merged with the profit-driven housing market to produce conditions of density without precedent. The Irish immigrant, Engels notes, "needs only salt for condiment, he lodges to his mind in any pig-hutch or dog-hutch," and his willingness to accept the minimum of shelter exerted a downward pressure on housing standards that degraded the entire [[proletariat|working class]]. (004-002)
