# Sir Robert Peel (1750–1830)

## Life

Sir Robert Peel (1750–1830) was a wealthy [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r from [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and a Tory Member of Parliament. He was the father of Sir Robert Peel (1788–1850), the future Prime Minister. The elder Peel was one of the early industrialists who made a fortune in the textile trade, yet he distinguished himself from many of his class by supporting legislative restraints on the worst abuses of the [[factory-system|factory]] system, particularly regarding child labour. (006-014)

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels mentions Peel at two key moments in his narrative — both concerned with the early history of [[factory-act|factory legislation]]. The first reference appears in Engels's discussion of the [[prostitution|sexual exploitation]] of female [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, where he notes that the "jus primae noctis" of the master over female employees was a recognised feature of early industrial life. It was public objection to this "revolting system" that provoked the first reform efforts:

> "As early as 1796, the public objection to this revolting system found such vigorous expression through Dr. Percival and Sir Robert Peel (father of the Cabinet Minister, and himself a cotton manufacturer), that in 1802 Parliament passed an Apprentices' Bill, by which the most crying evils were removed." (006-014)

Engels's parenthetical identification — "father of the Cabinet Minister, and himself a [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r" — is significant. It underscores Engels's point that even members of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class could be stirred to humanitarian action, while simultaneously implying that such philanthropy was exceptional. Peel was a [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r who agitated against the abuses of the system from which he drew his wealth — a contradiction Engels notes without fully resolving. (006-014)

The second reference places Peel in the lineage of reform that led from [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] to subsequent [[factory-act|Factory Acts]]:

> "The late Sir Robert Peel and other philanthropists united with him, and gradually secured the Factory Acts of 1818, 1825, and 1831, of which the first two were never enforced, and the last only here and there." (006-045)

Here Engels frames Peel as part of a coalition of philanthropic forces aligned with [[robert-owen|Owen]]'s early agitation. However, the cynical conclusion — that the first two Acts were never enforced — is typical of Engels's treatment of reform legislation: the laws existed on paper but the bourgeoisie ensured they remained dead letters. Peel is thus presented as an honourable individual whose efforts were ultimately frustrated by the class interests of his fellow manufacturers. (006-045, 006-014)

## Context

Peel's career illustrates a broader pattern that Engels identifies throughout the text: the most far-sighted sections of the bourgeoisie occasionally break ranks and support reform, either from genuine humanitarian conviction or from an understanding that unregulated exploitation is unsustainable. Peel stands alongside [[lord-ashley]], [[richard-oastler]], and [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] in the gallery of early reformers whom Engels treats with a measure of respect — while never losing sight of the fact that they operated within, and helped preserve, a fundamentally exploitative system. His son, the Prime Minister Sir Robert Peel, also appears in Engels's narrative as a defender of the [[new-poor-law]] and later an architect of the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]. (006-045, 011-019)
