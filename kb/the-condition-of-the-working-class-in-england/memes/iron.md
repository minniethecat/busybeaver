## Definition

Iron, in Engels's analysis of the [[industrial-revolution]], is the material infrastructure of [[machinery|mechanisation]] itself. The transformation of iron production — from charcoal smelting in small forges to coke-fired blast furnaces and the puddling process — enabled the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] of machinery, bridges, [[railway|railways]], and the built environment of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. Engels treats iron as an index of industrial development: its production figures measure the scale of the revolution.

## Engels's Analysis

### Iron and the Industrial Take-Off

Engels places iron at the centre of the cascading innovations that constituted the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. The steam engine created demand for coal; coal made cheap iron possible; cheap iron enabled more and better machinery. The relationship was self-reinforcing:

> The steam-engine first gave importance to the broad coalfields of England; the production of machinery began now for the first time, and with it arose a new interest in the iron mines which supplied raw material for it. [P04-015]

### The Puddling Revolution

The decisive technological breakthrough was the puddling process, patented by Henry Cort in 1784, which converted coke-smelted pig iron into wrought iron by removing carbon impurities:

> The beginning of the use of coke in iron smelting had been made in the last century, and in 1780 a new method was invented of converting into available wrought-iron coke-smelted iron, which up to that time had been convertible into cast-iron only. This process, known as "puddling" ... opened a wholly new field for the production of English iron. [P04-015]

The consequences were dramatic: "Smelting furnaces were built fifty times larger than before, the process of smelting was simplified by the introduction of hot blasts, and iron could thus be produced so cheaply that a multitude of objects which had before been made of stone or wood were now made of iron" [P04-015].

### The Iron Bridge and the Built Environment

Engels uses the iron bridge as a symbol of the new industrial landscape. Thomas Paine's 1788 iron bridge in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] inaugurated an era in which "nearly all bridges, especially for railroad traffic, are built of cast-iron," while [[london|London]]'s Southwark Bridge across the Thames demonstrated the material's architectural ambition [P04-016]. "Iron pillars, supports for machinery, etc., are universally used," and the introduction of gas lighting and [[railway|railways]] "opened new outlets for English iron products" [P04-016].

### The Growth of Iron Production

Engels provides quantitative evidence of iron's extraordinary expansion. In 1740, total British iron production was 17,000 tons; by 1834 it had reached "nearly 700,000 tons" [P04-016]. Exports rose from 4,300 tons of iron products and 4,600 tons of pig iron in 1805 to 16,200 and 107,000 tons respectively in 1834 [P04-016]. [[coal|Coal]] consumption for pig iron smelting alone exceeded 3,000,000 tons annually [P04-016].

### Huntsman's Cast Steel

A parallel innovation was Benjamin Huntsman's crucible steel process (developed in [[sheffield|Sheffield]] in the 1740s, though Engels dates it to 1790):

> Huntsman, a Sheffielder, discovered in 1790 a method for casting steel, by which much labour was saved, and the production of wholly new cheap goods rendered practicable; and through the greater purity of the material placed at its disposal, and the more perfect tools, new machinery and minute division of labour, the metal trade of England now first attained importance. [P04-016]

### The Iron Districts: Birmingham and Staffordshire

Engels devotes detailed attention to the social conditions of iron workers. In [[birmingham|Birmingham]], the persistence of small-scale handicraft production alongside large capitalist factories produces a mixed social formation — "neither genuine proletarians ... nor genuine bourgeois" — that Engels identifies as the reason for [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s political moderation [007-009].

In Staffordshire, conditions are "still worse." The iron district of [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]], [[willenhall|Willenhall]], [[bilston|Bilston]], and surrounding towns is characterised by small forges where masters and apprentices work in conditions of extreme deprivation:

> They get almost exclusively meat from diseased animals or such as have died a natural death, or tainted meat, or fish to eat, with veal from calves killed too young, and pork from swine smothered during transportation. [007-010]

The physical consequences are severe: "countless persons who have, from perpetually filing at the lathe, crooked backs and one leg crooked, 'hind-leg' as they call it, so that the two legs have the form of a K; while it is said that more than one-third of the workingmen there are ruptured" [007-010].

### Nail-Making: The Extreme of Exploitation

In Sedgeley and the surrounding nail-making district, Engels finds perhaps the most extreme exploitation in the entire book. Children begin work at ten or twelve years old and are "accounted fully skilled only when they make a thousand nails a day. For twelve hundred nails the pay is 5 3/4d. Every nail receives twelve blows, and since the hammer weighs 1 1/4 pounds, the nailer must lift 18,000 pounds to earn this miserable pay" [007-010].

The educational and moral results are catastrophic. Commissioner Horne "asserts, and supplies ample proofs of his assertion, that there exists absolutely no moral sense among the workers" [007-010]. Children "were so little capable of thinking of what they said, so stolid, so hopelessly stupid, that they often asserted that they were well treated... when they were forced to work twelve to fourteen hours, were clad in rags, did not get enough to eat, and were beaten" [007-010].

### Coal and Iron: The Energy-Industry Nexus

Engels documents the interdependence of coal and iron. The Northumberland and [[durham|Durham coalfield]]s alone "yield annually more than 5,000,000 tons for shipping, and employ from 40 to 50,000 men," expanding from 14 [[mining|mines]] in 1753 to 130 [[mining|mines]] in 1843 [P04-016]. The growth of iron smelting drove [[coal|coal mining]], which in turn enabled further industrial expansion — a feedback loop that for Engels defined the material basis of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism.

## Historical Context

The British iron industry underwent its most rapid expansion between 1780 and 1840, transforming South Wales, Staffordshire, and the Clyde Valley into industrial landscapes. The industry was closely tied to military demand (naval cannon, armaments) and railway construction. The transition from charcoal to coke smelting also shifted the geography of production from wooded areas (Sussex Weald) to [[coal|coalfields]]. Engels's figures, drawn from parliamentary reports and trade statistics, document an industry in the midst of explosive growth.
