# John Ramsay McCulloch

## Life

John Ramsay McCulloch (1789–1864) was a Scottish economist and a prominent figure in the classical school of political economy that included [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], [[david-ricardo|David Ricardo]], and [[thomas-malthus|Thomas Malthus]]. He held the chair of Political Economy at University College [[london|London]] from 1828 and published widely on taxation, commerce, and political economy. His edition of [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]]'s *Wealth of Nations*, which Engels cites as "McCulloch's edition in one volume" (013-002), was a standard reference work of the period. He also edited the *Scotsman* newspaper and contributed regularly to the *[[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] Review*.

McCulloch was a committed advocate of [[free-trade|Free Trade]] and laissez-faire economic policy. His economic writings, which treated the laws of political economy as immutable natural laws, made him one of the most influential public intellectuals of the 1830s and 1840s.

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels deploys McCulloch as Exhibit A in his critique of bourgeois economic science — a discipline he portrays as fundamentally apologetic, designed to rationalise the existing distribution of property rather than to understand the social world objectively.

The passage in which McCulloch appears is Engels's concluding assessment of the English bourgeoisie's prospects. Engels warns that American manufacture, with its "inexhaustible resources" and "energetic, active population," is rapidly overtaking British industry and may soon vanquish English competition altogether:

> "Should English manufacture be thus vanquished — and in the course of the next twenty years, if the present conditions remain unchanged, this is inevitable — the majority of the proletariat must become forever superfluous, and has no other choice than to starve or to rebel." (011-021)

Against this looming threat, Engels places McCulloch's complacent orthodoxy:

> "Does the English bourgeoisie reflect upon this contingency? On the contrary; its favourite economist, McCulloch, teaches from his student's desk, that a country so young as America, which is not even properly populated, cannot carry on manufacture successfully or dream of competing with an old manufacturing country like England. It were madness in the Americans to make the attempt, for they could only lose by it; better far for them to stick to their agriculture, and when they have brought their whole territory under the plough, a time may perhaps come for carrying on manufacture with a profit." (011-021)

The irony Engels constructs is devastating: "So says the wise economist, and the whole bourgeoisie worships him, while the Americans take possession of one market after another." (011-021) The academic economist, secure in his theoretical deductions, is oblivious to the material processes unfolding across the Atlantic — and the bourgeoisie, which funds his chair and purchases his books, prefers his soothing falsehoods to the alarming truth.

## Context

Engels's treatment of McCulloch belongs to a broader pattern: the use of mainstream economists as hostile witnesses whose own doctrines, properly understood, reveal the contradictions of the capitalist system. [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] is cited for his candid comparison of wage labour to slavery (003-008); [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]] is invoked for his theory of population, which Engels simultaneously uses and subverts. McCulloch represents a different variant: the economist as purveyor of ideology rather than knowledge — not candid like [[adam-smith|Smith]], not paradoxical like [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], but simply wrong, his errors serving the material interests of the class that celebrates him.

## Legacy

McCulloch's prediction about American industrial incapacity was refuted by history even more decisively than Engels could have anticipated in 1845. By the end of the nineteenth century, the United States had surpassed Britain as the world's leading industrial power. Engels's citation of McCulloch has outlived McCulloch's own reputation: the passage is now read principally as a testament to Engels's foresight about the trajectory of global capitalism and as a case study in the ideological character of establishment economic doctrine.
