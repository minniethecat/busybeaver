# Bradford

## Overview

Bradford, in the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], lay at the junction of several valleys, seven miles from [[leeds|Leeds]]. It was the principal centre of the worsted trade, where long English wool was converted into yarn, and its population had risen from 29,000 in 1801 to 77,000 by 1831 -- a nearly threefold increase in thirty years. Engels visited Bradford personally and left a vivid description of its topography, [[sanitary|sanitation]], and the physical toll exacted by its factories. The testimony of Bradford's surgeons, Beaumont and Sharp, supplied Engels with some of his most damning evidence about the [[factory-system|factory]] system's effects on children's bodies. (P04-011, P04-012, 002-029)

## Engels's Account

### The Town and Its Sanitation

Engels provides a memorable description of Bradford's setting and atmosphere. The town sat "upon the banks of a small, coal-black, foul-smelling stream." On weekdays, "the town is enveloped in a grey cloud of coal smoke," but on a fine Sunday, viewed from the surrounding heights, it offered "a superb picture." Within the town, however, "reigns the same filth and discomfort as in [[leeds|Leeds]]." The older portions, built upon steep hillsides, were "narrow and irregular." In the lanes, alleys, and courts, "lie filth and debris in heaps; the houses are ruinous, dirty, and miserable." Near the river and the valley bottom, Engels found many houses whose ground floors, half-buried in the hillside, were "totally abandoned." The workingmen's cottages crowded between the tall factories in the valley bottoms were "among the worst built and dirtiest districts of the whole town." In the newer portions, cottages were more regular, built in rows, but shared "all the evils incident to the customary method of providing workingmen's [[housing|dwellings]]." (002-029)

### Medical Testimony: The Multitude of Cripples

Bradford provides Engels with some of his most powerful medical evidence. Commissioner Power testified:

> "I can bear witness that the factory system in Bradford has engendered a multitude of cripples, and that the effect of long continued labour upon the physique is apparent, not alone in actual deformity, but also, and much more generally, in stunted growth, relaxation of the muscles, and delicacy of the whole frame." (006-022)

Surgeon Beaumont of Bradford gave further testimony about the systemic damage caused by [[factory-system|factory]] labour:

> "To my thinking, the system, according to which work is done in the mills here, produces a peculiar relaxation of the whole organism, and thereby makes children in the highest degree susceptible to epidemic, as well as to incidental illness. I regard the absence of all appropriate regulations for ventilation and cleanliness in the mills very decidedly as the chief cause of that peculiar tendency or susceptibility to morbid affections which I have so frequently met in my practice." (006-024)

Even under the most favourable conditions -- Wood's mill in Bradford, described as "the best arranged of the district" -- Dr. Ray found that three-fifths of all children employed there required medical treatment from him in 1842, and that the worst effect was "not the predominance of deformities, but of enfeebled and morbid constitutions." Ray acknowledged that conditions had improved after children's [[working-day|working hours]] were reduced to ten per day. (006-025)

### Labour Unrest

In 1844, Bradford was one of two towns, alongside [[sheffield|Sheffield]], where Chartist spy outbreaks occurred, generating considerable excitement in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]]. Like other West Riding towns, Bradford was deeply involved in the labour movements of the period. (008-028)
