# Socialism

## Definition

Socialism, in the context of Engels's 1845 book, refers to the body of ideas and movements that proposed collective or cooperative alternatives to capitalist [[property|private property]] and competition. Engels distinguishes carefully between different currents: the Owenite socialism of [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]] and his followers, which had been the dominant form of English socialism since the 1820s; the "socialistic and communistic elements" that were beginning to penetrate the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]] from continental Europe; and the emerging Chartist socialism that Engels himself was helping to shape. The book is not itself a socialist manifesto — it is subtitled "From Personal Observation and Authentic Sources" and presents itself as a factual report — but its analysis is unmistakably socialist in its conclusions. (P01-008)

## Engels's Analysis

> Modern international Socialism, since fully developed as a science, scarcely existed in 1844. (P01-008)

In the Preface to the English edition (written in 1892, nearly fifty years after the original publication), Engels reflects on the development of socialism since 1844. The book, he notes, was written at a time when socialism was still in its infancy — when Owenism was declining, when [[chartism|Chartism]] was at its height but had not yet developed a distinctly socialist programme, and when [[karl-marx|Marx]] and Engels themselves were only beginning to formulate the theoretical framework that would become [[karl-marx|Marx]]ism. The book's importance lies partly in its status as a document of this transitional moment: it captures the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]] at the point when the proletarian movement was beginning to move beyond trade unionism and [[chartism|Chartism]] toward a more thoroughgoing critique of capitalism. (P01-010)

> After the breakdown of Owenism, there has been no Socialism in England. (P01-010)

The [[robert-owen|Owen]]ite movement receives sustained attention in Engels's historical analysis. [[robert-owen|Robert Owen]], a wealthy [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r who had made his fortune at New Lanark, broke with his class to become the leading advocate of cooperative socialism in Britain. His proposals — cooperative villages, labour exchanges, the replacement of money with labour notes, and the gradual supersession of competition by cooperation — attracted a mass following among workers in the 1820s and 1830s. But Engels's assessment of Owenism is ultimately critical: [[robert-owen|Owen]] believed that socialism could be achieved through rational persuasion and benevolent example, without class struggle. The collapse of [[robert-owen|Owen]]'s Grand National Consolidated Trades Union in 1834 and the failure of his cooperative communities demonstrated the inadequacy of this approach. Real socialism, Engels implies, would have to be built by the [[proletariat|working class]] itself, through its own organisations and struggles. (P01-010)

> Meanwhile the socialist agitation also goes forward. (008-035)

In the book's later chapters, Engels describes the socialist movement of the 1840s as a diffuse but growing force. Socialist ideas were spreading among the [[proletariat|working class]] through lectures, pamphlets, and the radical press. The "socialistic and communistic elements" that Engels mentions in his chapter on "The Attitude of the [[bourgeoisie|Bourgeoisie]]" were entering the English labour movement through contact with continental exiles — German, French, and Polish radicals who had fled to England after the failure of the 1830 revolutions. Engels's own role in this process — as a German socialist living in [[manchester|Manchester]], participating in the [[chartism|Chartist movement]], and writing for both English and German audiences — was a concrete instance of the internationalisation of socialism that the book's preface celebrates. (008-035, 011-023)

## Historical Context

The socialism Engels describes was one of three major currents in early nineteenth-century English radicalism, alongside [[chartism|Chartism]] (the political movement for democratic reform centred on the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]]) and trade unionism (the industrial movement for better wages and conditions). The relationship between these currents was complex and often tense. The Chartist leaders were suspicious of [[robert-owen|Owen]]ite utopianism, which they saw as a distraction from the immediate political struggle for universal suffrage. The trade unionists were focused on bread-and-butter issues and were often hostile to the "foreign" ideas of continental socialism. Engels's book can be read — in part — as an intervention in these debates, an attempt to demonstrate that the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]] made socialism not a utopian dream but a practical necessity.

The year the book was published, 1845, was also the year that [[karl-marx|Marx]] and Engels began their theoretical collaboration in earnest. *The German Ideology*, written in 1845–46 but not published in their lifetimes, developed the materialist conception of history that would provide the theoretical foundation for the socialism Engels describes in his later preface as "fully developed as a science." *The Condition of the Working Class in England* occupies an intermediate position in Engels's intellectual development: it is not yet "scientific socialism," but its method — grounding political conclusions in empirical investigation of material conditions — points directly toward it.
