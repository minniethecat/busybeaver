# New Poor Law 1834

## Overview

The [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Amendment Act of 1834 — universally known among the [[proletariat|working class]] as the "[[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]]" — was a fundamental restructuring of English [[pauperism|poor relief]]. It abolished the system of outdoor relief (cash or provisions given to the poor in their own homes) that had operated since the Elizabethan [[poor-law|Poor Law]] of 1601, and replaced it with a centralised administration under three [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Commissioners. Relief for the able-bodied poor was henceforth available only inside a [[workhouse]], where conditions were deliberately made harsher than those of the lowest-paid independent labourer.

For Engels, the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] is the most damning exhibit in his indictment of the English bourgeoisie. He calls it "the most open declaration of war of the bourgeoisie upon the proletariat" and treats it as the legislative expression of [[Malthus|Malthusian]] population theory — the doctrine that poverty is a natural check on population, that the poor are "superfluous," and that charity only encourages reproduction among the unfit. (011-012)

## Engels's Account

Engels opens his treatment of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] in Chapter 11, "Attitude of the [[bourgeoisie|Bourgeoisie]] Towards the [[proletariat|Proletariat]]," by linking it explicitly to [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian ideology. He summarises the [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian argument: "the earth is perennially overpopulated, whence poverty, misery, distress, and immorality must prevail." Under this theory, [[pauperism|poor relief]] is "nonsense, since they serve only to maintain, and stimulate the increase of, the surplus population." [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], Engels states, "declares in plain English that the right to live, a right previously asserted in favour of every man in the world, is nonsense." Engels quotes [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]'s poetic citation — that the poor man comes to the feast of Nature and finds no cover laid for him — and adds that "she bids him begone." (011-012)

The Old [[poor-law|Poor Law]], Engels explains, rested on the Elizabethan principle that the parish was duty-bound to keep the poor from starvation. The poor man "demanded his weekly relief as his right, not as a favour." But in 1833, with the Whig bourgeoisie newly empowered by the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill]], a Royal Commission on the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]s was appointed. Its report condemned outdoor relief as "a check upon industry, a reward for improvident marriage, a stimulus to increased population." Engels concedes the accuracy of some of this critique — relief under existing conditions did foster dependency — but insists the Commissioners drew the wrong conclusion: that poverty was a crime to be punished rather than a product of the economic system. (011-012, 011-013)

The practical mechanism of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] was the workhouse — what Engels, using the working-class term, calls the "[[poor-law|Poor Law]] [[workhouse|Bastille]]." The commissioners, he writes, reasoned as follows:

> "'Good,' said they, 'we grant you poor a right to exist, but only to exist; the right to multiply you have not, nor the right to exist as befits human beings. You are a pest, and if we cannot get rid of you as we do of other pests, you shall feel, at least, that you are a pest.'" (011-012)

The workhouse regime was designed to be "the most repulsive residence which the refined ingenuity of a [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian can invent":

> "The food is worse than that of the most ill-paid workingman while employed, and the work harder... Meat, especially fresh meat, is rarely furnished, chiefly potatoes, the worst possible bread and oatmeal porridge, little or no beer. The food of criminal prisoners is better, as a rule, so that the paupers frequently commit some offence for the purpose of getting into jail." (011-012)

Families were systematically broken apart — husbands, wives, and children confined to separate wings and permitted to see each other only at stated intervals — "to prevent the 'superfluous' from multiplying, and 'demoralised' parents from influencing their children." The workhouse was "a jail too": tobacco was forbidden, visitors strictly controlled, inmates wore uniforms, and those who failed to complete their assigned tasks went without [[diet|food]]. (011-012)

Engels reinforces his argument with a catalogue of documented atrocities drawn from the workhouse system: a five-year-old boy in Greenwich shut into the dead-room to sleep on coffin lids; a man at Coventry dying of a neglected wound after being discharged from the workhouse where his wife was reprimanded for bringing him beer; a [[birmingham|Birmingham]] investigation that found tramps "shut up naked under a staircase in a black hole" for eight to ten days; a woman in the sixth month of pregnancy shut in a reception-room without a bed for weeks while her husband was imprisoned for protesting. These are not isolated cases, Engels insists, but the logical application of the law's spirit. (011-012)

> "The spirit and not the letter of the law is followed in the treatment of the poor." (011-013)

The [[pauperism|pauper]] burial-grounds completed the picture. In [[manchester|Manchester]], the cemetery opposite the Old Town along the [[Irk]] was "a rough, desolate place"; a railway was driven through it without objection because the dead were only [[pauperism|pauper]]s — "the resting-place of the outcast and superfluous." Engels describes corpses "heaped up just as it happened, and piles were driven into newly-made graves, so that the water oozed out of the swampy ground, pregnant with putrefying matter." (011-012)

Engels concludes that the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] has "intensified, more than any other measure of the party in power, the hatred of the working-class against the [[bourgeoisie|property-holder]]s." It "has contributed so greatly to accelerate the labour movement, and especially to spread [[chartism|Chartism]]" because the bourgeoisie had "formulated so clearly in this law its conception of its duties towards the proletariat" — namely that "the non-possessing class exists solely for the purpose of being exploited, and of starving when the [[bourgeoisie|property-holder]]s can no longer make use of it." (011-012)

The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] also figures in Engels's account of earlier chapters. In Chapter 10, "The [[agricultural-proletariat|Agricultural Proletariat]]," he shows how the abolition of outdoor relief created a class of "total [[pauperism|pauper]]s" in the [[rural|countryside]], where wages had been driven to the minimum and the old system of [[parish|parish relief]] had cushioned the worst depredations of agricultural capitalism. Before the new law, "three to four million half [[pauperism|pauper]]s had existed"; afterward, "a million of total [[pauperism|pauper]]s now appeared, and the rest, still half [[pauperism|pauper]]s, merely went without relief." (010-002)

The [[farming|agricultural labour]]er's situation was made yet more desperate. Refusal to work for starvation wages meant refusal of all relief except the workhouse — "the hated workhouse is refused by the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] guardians as to a lazy vagabond." The guardians were "the very farmers from whom or from whose neighbours and acquaintances alone he can get work," creating a closed circuit of power and exploitation. (010-004)

In the [[rural|countryside]], the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] became a direct incitement to violence. Engels reports that changes in the [[poor-law|Poor Law]], along with low wages and the introduction of machinery, were among the immediate causes of the "Swing" incendiarism riots of 1830–31, when farmers' hayricks, barns, and stables were burnt nightly throughout southern England. The [[rural|countryside]], Engels argues, had entered "social war." (010-006)

The [[chartism|Chartist movement]] made repeal of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] one of its central demands. In Chapter 8, Engels describes how "the movements against the new [[poor-law|Poor Law]] and for the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] were already in the closest relation to [[chartism|Chartism]]." At the great meetings of 1838, [[Richard Oastler]] circulated petitions for [[poor-law|Poor Law]] repeal alongside the national petition for the [[chartism|People's Charter]]. Engel reports that when striking workers marched on [[stockport|Stockport]] during the general strike of August 1842, their first violent clash with the authorities occurred "as they were storming the workhouse, the favourite child of the bourgeoisie." (008-028, 008-029)

## Context

The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] was designed by a commission dominated by two utilitarian reformers, [[Edwin Chadwick]] and [[Nassau William Senior]], both disciples of [[Jeremy Bentham]]. Chadwick, a former secretary to [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]], applied the principle of "less eligibility" — the rule that the condition of the [[pauperism|pauper]] inside the workhouse must be less desirable than that of the lowest independent labourer outside. Senior, the first professor of political economy at Oxford, contributed the [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian theoretical framework. The 1834 Act centralised [[pauperism|poor relief]] administration, grouping [[parish|parishes]] into [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Unions, each with its own workhouse, overseen by elected Boards of Guardians but ultimately answerable to the three Commissioners in [[london|London]].

The workhouse system had deep roots in English social policy. [[parish|Parish]] workhouses had existed under the Old [[poor-law|Poor Law]], but they served primarily the aged, infirm, and orphaned. The innovation of 1834 was to make the workhouse the exclusive channel of relief for the able-bodied and to design it as an instrument of deterrence rather than of care. "Less eligibility" was operationalised through the separation of families, monotonous diet, hard and useless labour (stone-breaking for men, oakum-picking for women), and the stigma of the workhouse uniform. (011-012)

## Aftermath

The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] provoked sustained resistance. In the industrial North, where cyclical unemployment made outdoor relief essential to survival, [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Unions were often prevented from building workhouses until the late 1830s or later. The anti-[[poor-law|Poor Law]] movement merged with the Ten Hours agitation and [[chartism|Chartism]], creating a unified working-class political platform. The demand for repeal of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] remained central to Chartist petitions through the 1840s.

In practice, the law was never enforced with the full severity the Commissioners intended. Outdoor relief continued in many districts, especially in [[lancashire|Lancashire]] during the Cotton Famine of the 1860s, when the scale of distress overwhelmed the workhouse system. The [[poor-law|Poor Law]] was not formally abolished until the twentieth century, but its [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian rigour was progressively softened. For Engels, however, the significance of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] lay less in its implementation than in its declaration of intent: it was the moment when "the expulsion of the proletariat from State and society" was publicly proclaimed. (011-013)
