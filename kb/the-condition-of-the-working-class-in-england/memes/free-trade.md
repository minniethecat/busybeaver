# Free Trade

## Definition

Free Trade, in the context of Engels's England, was the political-economic programme of the industrial bourgeoisie for abolishing protective tariffs — particularly the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], which imposed duties on imported grain. Its advocates argued that free trade would lower [[diet|food]] prices, benefit consumers, and increase national prosperity. Engels does not dispute the economic logic but insists on identifying whose interests it served: cheap bread meant lower wages, because the worker's wage was determined by the cost of [[diet|subsistence]]. Free Trade was, in Engels's analysis, the manufacturers' method of reducing labour costs without appearing to attack workers directly. (P01-010)

## Engels's Analysis

> The capitalist class clamoured for Free Trade in corn, and threatened to enforce it by sending the starving masses into the field against the landlords. (P01-010)

Engels's treatment of Free Trade is shaped by his first-hand observation of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] in [[manchester|Manchester]]. He watched the League's operatives — paid agents of the manufacturers — canvass working-class districts with the argument that repealing the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] would make bread cheaper. He also watched the same manufacturers oppose every measure that would genuinely improve workers' lives: the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]], trade union rights, [[factory-system|factory]] inspection with teeth. The contradiction was not hypocrisy of individual manufacturers but a structural feature of bourgeois reform: every concession to labour must be compatible with profit. (P01-010)

Engels's key insight is that Free Trade is not a neutral policy for maximising welfare but a class strategy for redistributing income. The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] transferred income from consumers to landowners through higher grain prices. Their repeal would transfer income from landowners to manufacturers through lower wages. In neither case would workers benefit — the worker's wage would fall to match the lower price of bread, leaving real income unchanged. This argument, which Engels makes with statistical rigour, anticipates the later [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist analysis of wages under capitalism. (P01-004)

Engels is also attentive to the political dimension. The battle over Free Trade was, he argues, the mechanism by which the industrial bourgeoisie displaced the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] as the dominant fraction of the ruling class. The 1846 repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] was the bourgeoisie's political coming-of-age — the moment when economic power translated into legislative power. The workers, who had been promised cheaper bread, received instead a lesson in political economy: the state serves the class that controls it. (008-032)
