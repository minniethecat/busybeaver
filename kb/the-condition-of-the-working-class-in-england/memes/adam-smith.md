# Adam Smith

## Life

Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and political economist, best known for *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776), which established the framework of classical political economy. Smith argued that [[competition|free competition]], the division of labour, and the pursuit of individual self-interest produced — through the mechanism of the "invisible hand" — outcomes that benefited society as a whole. His analysis of the division of labour, the determination of wages, and the accumulation of capital provided the intellectual foundation for the economic system that Engels documents in its most advanced form: the [[capital|industrial capital]]ism of 1840s England. (003-008)

## Role in Engels's Account

> As Adam Smith comfortingly pointed out, free labour was cheaper than slave labour. (003-008)

Engels's treatment of Adam Smith is dialectical. He acknowledges Smith's descriptive insights — particularly the analysis of how wages are determined by the supply of and demand for labour, and the observation that the worker, unlike the slave, is paid only when he is employed and bears the cost of his own reproduction — as accurate descriptions of the capitalist labour market. Smith was right, Engels concedes, to observe that the worker "is sold like a piece of goods, rises and falls in value like a commodity," and that the employer's interest is to buy labour as cheaply as possible.

But Engels distinguishes sharply between Smith's descriptive accuracy and his normative framework. Smith described the working of competition without questioning whether competition itself was a desirable basis for social organisation. He analysed the determination of wages without asking whether wages determined by the market could ever be sufficient for a decent human existence. And he provided intellectual legitimacy to a system whose human costs — the overcrowded cellars, the adulterated bread, the twelve-hour days for eight-year-olds — he could not have foreseen and would not have endorsed. Smith's "invisible hand" was, in the world Engels documented, a hand that strangled workers.

The specific context of Engels's citation of Smith is the discussion of competition in "The Industrial [[proletariat|Proletariat]]." Engels is explaining the mechanism by which wages are driven to [[diet|subsistence]] level: the worker competes with other workers for employment, the employer competes with other employers for markets, and both forms of competition converge to depress wages. Smith provided the theoretical framework for understanding this process, and Engels — despite his opposition to Smith's normative conclusions — acknowledges the theoretical contribution. But he immediately politicises it: the "freedom" that Smith celebrated was, for the worker, the freedom to starve. The worker was "free" in theory; in practice, he was compelled to accept whatever wages the market offered, because the alternative was the workhouse or death. (003-008)

## Context

Smith's *Wealth of Nations* was published in 1776, on the eve of the industrial revolution that would transform Britain. Smith could not have anticipated the [[factory-system|factory]] system, the industrial city, or the mass proletariat that Engels documents. But his theoretical framework — free trade, competition, the division of labour, the determination of wages by supply and demand — provided the intellectual justification for the policies that made the industrial revolution possible and that blocked reforms that might have mitigated its human costs.

Engels's critique of Smith is part of a broader critique of classical political economy that runs throughout the book. Smith, Ricardo, [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]], and their followers treated the laws of political economy as natural laws, like the laws of physics. Engels treats them as historical laws — laws that operated under specific social conditions ([[property|private property]], competition, wage labour) and that could be changed by changing those conditions. The worker who understood that the "iron law of wages" was not a law of nature but a law of capitalism had taken the first step toward abolishing it.
