# Cholera

## Definition

In Engels's *Condition of the Working Class in England*, cholera is not merely a medical phenomenon but a social indictment. The cholera epidemics that repeatedly visited English industrial cities — most notably in 1832 and 1842 — are, in Engels's framework, the physical embodiment of "social murder": the conditions of filth, [[crowding|overcrowding]], and [[poverty|destitution]] in working-class quarters were the necessary and known causes of epidemic disease, and the bourgeoisie's refusal to remedy those conditions constituted, in Engels's famous formulation, "disguised, malicious murder." Cholera, which struck the [[housing|slums]] first and hardest, occasionally threatened to breach the spatial barriers separating worker and bourgeois quarters — and only then, Engels notes, did the "otherwise sleepy conscience of the [[police|sanitary police]]" briefly stir. (P01-005, 002-050)

## Cholera as Social Murder

> When one individual inflicts bodily injury upon another, such injury that death results, we call the deed manslaughter; when the assailant knew in advance that the injury would be fatal, we call his deed murder. But when society places hundreds of proletarians in such a position that they inevitably meet a too early and an unnatural death... its deed is murder just as surely as the deed of the single individual. (002-053, introduction to Chapter 5)

The cholera epidemic serves as Engels's most vivid illustration of this thesis. The disease was known to be associated with filth, stagnant water, and [[crowding|overcrowding]] — precisely the conditions Engels had documented in exhaustive detail in the working-class quarters of [[manchester|Manchester]], [[london|London]], [[glasgow|Glasgow]], and every other industrial city. The bourgeoisie, Engels argues, knew of these conditions — the parliamentary reports, the sanitary inquiries, the mortality statistics were all matters of public record — and yet "does nothing to improve these conditions." The knowledge converts negligence into culpability, omission into commission: "That it *knows* the consequences of its deeds; that its act is, therefore, not mere manslaughter, but murder, I shall have proved, when I cite official documents, reports of Parliament and of the Government, in substantiation of my charge." (002-053)

## The Geography of Epidemic Disease

> In general, the streets of this parish were not kept in such a state at the time of the cholera that the sanitary police ordered the evacuation of many houses, and the closing of many streets. (002-039)

Engels documents the spatial logic of epidemic disease in [[manchester|Manchester]]: it was concentrated in the working-class districts — the Old Town, Little Ireland, [[ancoats|Ancoats]] — where "the streets are unpaved, without sewers, filled with stagnant pools and rotting refuse," where privies without doors stood at court entrances, where cellar [[housing|dwellings]] filled with water from adjacent rivers. The disease struck these quarters relentlessly. In one street Engels describes, no house "escaped the cholera." Yet the physical geography of the city, deliberately arranged to conceal working-class misery behind commercial facades and to separate bourgeois suburbs from proletarian quarters, meant that the bourgeoisie experienced epidemic disease as a distant threat rather than an immediate reality — until it was not. (002-039, 002-050)

## The Sanitary Police's Hypocritical Awakening

> Occasionally when an epidemic threatens, the otherwise sleepy conscience of the sanitary police is a little stirred, raids are made into the workingmen's districts, whole rows of cellars and cottages are closed... but this does not last long: the condemned cottages soon find occupants again, the owners are much better off by letting them, and the sanitary police won't come again so soon. (002-050)

The pattern Engels describes is one of crisis-driven, performative intervention. When cholera threatened to spread from working-class to middle-class districts — as it periodically did, for the "conditions of the [[urban|great town]]s make its spread into the better quarters unavoidable" — the authorities acted. Houses were condemned, streets closed, cellars evacuated. But the interventions were temporary and ineffective: the condemned [[housing|dwellings]] were soon reoccupied, the owners profited from renewed letting, and the structural conditions that bred disease — the profit-driven system of jerry-building, the absence of drainage and ventilation, the [[crowding|overcrowding]] caused by inadequate wages — remained unchanged. "[[police|Police]] regulations have been plentiful as blackberries," Engels would later write, "but they can only hedge in the distress of the workers, they cannot remove it." (002-050, P01-005)

## Cholera and the 1885 Retrospect

In the 1885 preface, Engels acknowledges that some of the worst conditions he described had been ameliorated: "Little Ireland has disappeared," drainage had been "introduced or improved," "wide avenues have been opened out athwart many of the worst '[[housing|slums]]'." But he immediately qualifies this: the bourgeoisie had "made further progress in the art of hiding the distress of the working-class," and "in regard to their [[housing|dwellings]], no substantial improvement has taken place." The cholera epidemics had prompted [[sanitary|sanitation]] reforms, but these reforms operated at the surface — removing the visible evidence of misery rather than its structural causes. The "repeated visitations of cholera, typhus, smallpox, and other epidemics" had frightened the bourgeoisie into minimal concessions, but the system that produced epidemic conditions remained intact. (P01-005)
