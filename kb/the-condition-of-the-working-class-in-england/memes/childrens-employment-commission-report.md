# Children's Employment Commission Report (1842)

## Overview

The Children's Employment Commission was established by Parliament in 1840 to investigate the employment of children in [[mining|mines]], [[coal|collieries]], and other trades not covered by the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]]. Its first report, published in 1842, focused on [[coal|coal mines]] and produced testimony so horrifying that even a Parliament dominated by mine-owners was forced to legislate. The Mines and Collieries Act of 1842 prohibited the underground employment of women and of children under ten -- the first legislative recognition that mining labour required regulation distinct from [[factory-system|factory]] labour. (005-020)

> Let us turn now to the most important branch of British mining, the iron and coal mines, which the Children's Employment Commission treats in common, and with all the detail which the importance of the subject demands. Nearly the whole of the first part of this report is devoted to the condition of the workers employed in these mines. (009-004)

The Commission exposed practices that the mine-owners had long insisted did not exist. In the Cornish mines, about 19,000 men and 11,000 women and children were employed, partly above and partly below ground. Dr. Barham's report for the Commission documented how the inhalation of an atmosphere containing little oxygen, mixed with dust and the smoke of blasting powder, seriously affected the lungs, disturbed the action of the heart, and diminished the activity of the digestive organs. Men who began this work in early youth never reached normal stature, and most miners died of slow consumption in middle age. (009-002)

## Key Findings

The Commission's investigators travelled to every mining district in Britain and interviewed workers, owners, overseers, surgeons, and clergymen. Their findings documented a system of child labour more brutal than anything found in textile factories. Children as young as four were employed as "trappers" -- sitting alone in darkness for twelve hours, opening and closing a ventilation door when coal carts passed. Older children (six to eight) worked as "hurriers" or "thrusters," pushing or pulling loaded coal tubs along passages where the roof was less than three feet high. They crawled on hands and knees, harnessed to the tub by a chain that passed between their legs and attached to a waist-belt, their foreheads often protected by a leather cap. (005-020)

> In the coal and iron mines which are worked in pretty much the same way, children of four, five, and seven years are employed. They are set to transporting the ore or coal loosened by the miner from its place to the horse-path or the main shaft, and to opening and shutting the doors (which separate the divisions of the mine and regulate its ventilation) for the passage of workers and material. For watching the doors the smallest children are usually employed, who thus pass twelve hours daily, in the dark, alone, sitting usually in damp passages without even having work enough to save them from the stupefying, brutalising tedium of doing nothing. (009-005)

The Commission's testimony on the physical effects was graphic. Children developed deformities of the spine, pelvis, and legs from prolonged stooping and crawling. Girls employed as hurriers reported menstrual disorders and miscarriages. Boys and young men who began work in the [[mining|mines]] at six or seven were, by thirty, often crippled with rheumatism, asthma, and the miners' characteristic "black spit" -- the expectoration of coal dust that had accumulated in their lungs. The Commission's medical witnesses estimated that a collier at thirty had the body of a man of sixty. (005-022)

> The children and young people who are employed in transporting coal and iron-stone all complain of being overtired. Even in the most recklessly conducted industrial establishments there is no such universal and exaggerated overwork. The whole report proves this, with a number of examples on every page. It is constantly happening that children throw themselves down on the stone hearth or the floor as soon as they reach home, fall asleep at once without being able to take a bite of food, and have to be washed and put to bed while asleep; it even happens that they lie down on the way home, and are found by their parents late at night asleep on the road. It seems to be a universal practice among these children to spend Sunday in bed to recover in some degree from the overexertion of the week. Church and school are visited by but few, and even of these the teachers complain of their great sleepiness and the want of all eagerness to learn. (009-007)

In the thin-seam coal mines, conditions were even worse. Where the coal seams were too narrow to justify removing adjacent rock, the passages were kept so low that standing upright was impossible. The workingman lay on his side to loosen the coal with his pick, resting on his elbow as a pivot, which produced inflammations of the joint. Women and children who transported the coal crawled on their hands and knees, fastened to the tub by a harness and chain that frequently passed between their legs, while a man behind pushed with hands and head -- the latter producing painful swellings and ulcers. In many cases the shafts were wet, so that workers crawled through several inches of dirty or salt water, exposed to special irritation of the skin. (009-008)

> All this deals only with the average of the English coal mines. But there are many in which the state of things is much worse, those, namely, in which thin seams of coal are worked. The coal would be too expensive if a part of the adjacent sand and clay were removed; so the mine owners permit only the seams to be worked; whereby the passages which elsewhere are four or five feet high and more are here kept so low that to stand upright in them is not to be thought of. The workingman lies on his side and loosens the coal with his pick; resting upon his elbow as a pivot, whence follow inflammations of the joint, and in cases where he is forced to kneel, of the knee also. The women and children who have to transport the coal crawl upon their hands and knees, fastened to the tub by a harness and chain (which frequently passes between the legs), while a man behind pushes with hands and head. (009-008)

### The Report's Findings on Deformity and Disease

The Commission's medical evidence established a comprehensive catalogue of occupational diseases specific to mining. Distortions of the legs, knees bent inwards and feet bent outwards, and deformities of the spinal column were so frequent that physicians testified a miner could be recognised by his shape among a hundred other persons. Women employed in the mines "seem to suffer especially from this work, and are seldom, if ever, as straight as other women," with testimony documenting pelvic deformities leading to difficult, even fatal, childbearing. Beyond skeletal damage, miners suffered from diseases of the digestive organs, hypertrophy and inflammation of the heart, hernias, asthma, and rheumatism -- nearly all of which Engels traces to overwork and the damp, dust-filled atmosphere of the pits. (009-007)

The peculiar disease of coal miners was "black spittle," which arose from the saturation of the entire lung with coal particles. It manifested in general debility, headache, oppression of the chest, and thick black mucous expectoration. Every case of this disease ended fatally. Yet Dr. Mackellar of Pencaitland, East Lothian, testified for the Commission that in all properly ventilated coal mines the disease was unknown, while miners who transferred from well-ventilated to ill-ventilated pits were regularly seized by it. The Commission thus established that black spittle was not a natural hazard but a direct consequence of the mine owners' refusal to install ventilators -- a finding Engels seized upon as emblematic of the "profit-greed" of capital. (009-007)

> The peculiar disease of workers of this sort is "black spittle," which arises from the saturation of the whole lung with coal particles, and manifests itself in general debility, headache, oppression of the chest, and thick, black mucous expectoration. In some districts this disease appears in a mild form; in others, on the contrary, it is wholly incurable, especially in Scotland. Here, besides the symptoms just mentioned, which appear in an intensified form, short, wheezing, breathing, rapid pulse (exceeding 100 per minute), and abrupt coughing, with increasing leanness and debility, speedily make the patient unfit for work. Every case of this disease ends fatally. (009-007)

The Commission also documented the structural consequences of child labour on the life-course. Commissioner Symonds examined a nineteen-year-old boy who, beyond the evidence of his teeth, showed no indication that he was more than eleven or twelve years old. The prolongation of childhood was, in the Commission's analysis, not a protection but a sign of arrested development. Among boys as well as girls, puberty was retarded -- in boys often until the eighteenth year. Engels summarised the Commission's conclusion through Dr. Southwood Smith, one of the commissioners: "through prolonged childhood on the one hand and premature age on the other, that period of life in which the human being is in full possession of his powers, the period of manhood, is greatly shortened, while the length of life in general is below the average." (009-007)

The Commission's evidence on moral and educational deprivation was equally damning. Commissioner Grainger, reporting on children in [[birmingham|Birmingham]], stated that they were "utterly wanting in all that could be in the remotest degree called a useful education." Commissioner Horne found a girl of eleven in [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]] who had attended both day and Sunday school but "had never heard of another world, of Heaven, or another life." These findings supported Engels's broader argument that the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] deliberately restricted working-class education to the merest literacy, sufficient only to make workers functional but not questioning. (005-020, 005-022)

## Engels's Use

Engels draws on the 1842 Report most extensively in Chapter 9 ("The [[mining|Mining]] [[proletariat|Proletariat]]"), where it provides the empirical backbone for his portrait of mining as the most physically destructive form of industrial labour. He reproduces the Commission's testimony verbatim -- the children's own words, the surgeons' clinical descriptions, the overseers' matter-of-fact admissions -- and structures the chapter as an extended gloss on the Report's findings. (009-001)

Engels is scrupulous about acknowledging his debt to the Commission. He notes that the Report's evidence was gathered under oath, that it was cross-checked by multiple investigators, and that the mine-owners' attempts to discredit it had failed. This care about sources is characteristic: Engels [[poverty|want]]s his readers to understand that what he reports is not socialist propaganda but documented fact from a parliamentary commission dominated by the very class whose conduct it exposed. (005-020)

The Commission's broader significance, for Engels, lies in what it reveals about the limits of market-based reform. The mine-owners had insisted for decades that they treated their workers well, that child labour in mining was "light work" (opening a door, after all, was not physically strenuous), and that regulation was unnecessary. The Commission's report demolished these claims, but only because a public agitation -- led by Lord [[lord-ashley|Ashley]] and supported by the Ten Hours' movement -- had forced Parliament to investigate. The lesson Engels draws is that reform requires both documented evidence and organised political pressure: neither alone is sufficient. (005-022)

The schools, according to the Commission, contributed almost nothing to the morality of the working class. Engels quotes the Report's findings explicitly: "The schools contribute, according to the confession of all authorities, and especially of the Children's Employment Commission, almost nothing to the morality of the working-class." He uses this finding to indict the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] for its calculated limitation of working-class instruction to precisely the level that would make workers functional without making them critical. (005-022) In Chapter 7, Engels similarly draws on the Commission's findings on the [[education|educational]] and moral condition of working children, noting Commissioner Horne's report on Willenhall that "there exists absolutely no moral sense among the workers" -- a finding Engels does not endorse as a moral judgment but presents as evidence of the social destruction wrought by industrial capitalism. (007-010)

> If we sum up briefly the results of the work in coal mines, we find, as Dr. Southwood Smith, one of the commissioners, does, that through prolonged childhood on the one hand and premature age on the other, that period of life in which the human being is in full possession of his powers, the period of manhood, is greatly shortened, while the length of life in general is below the average. This, too, on the debit side of the bourgeoisie's reckoning! (009-007)

## Historical Significance

The 1842 Report became, along with the 1833 Factory Commission Report, the foundational document of mid-nineteenth-century social reform. Its influence extended beyond mining: it established the principle that child labour was a legitimate object of state regulation, that parliamentary commissions could serve as instruments of exposure, and that the testimony of working people was admissible evidence in legislative deliberation. Engels's own book stands in this tradition -- a private citizen's investigation, published outside official channels, but drawing on the same documentary method that the parliamentary commissions had pioneered. (005-022)

The Mines and Collieries Act of 1842, which prohibited the underground employment of women and of children under ten, was the Commission's direct legislative fruit. It was the first act of Parliament to regulate mining labour, and it established the precedent that the testimony of working people -- including children -- could serve as the basis for legislation. The Commission thus not only documented suffering but created the political conditions under which that suffering could be addressed. (009-010)

The Commission's method set a precedent for subsequent parliamentary inquiries into the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]]. Its practice of sending investigators into the field, interviewing workers directly, and publishing testimony verbatim was a departure from earlier commissions that had relied on the testimony of employers and local notables. This methodological innovation was crucial to the Report's impact: the words of working children, reported without editorial softening, carried a force that no amount of statistical abstraction could match. Engels recognised this, and his own book adopted the same strategy -- letting the voices of workers speak for themselves, framed by analysis but not displaced by it. (009-001, 005-022)
