# Sir Archibald Alison

## Life

Sir Archibald Alison (1792–1867) was a Scottish lawyer, historian, and political economist. He served as Sheriff of Lanarkshire — the chief judicial officer for the county encompassing [[glasgow|Glasgow]] — from 1834 until his death. In this capacity he compiled official crime statistics that Engels would later cite extensively. His major literary work was a multi-volume *History of Europe from the Commencement of the French Revolution* (1833–1842), a conservative historiography that established his reputation as a leading Tory intellectual. He also published *Principles of Population and Their Connection with Human Happiness* (1840), a [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian analysis that Engels engaged critically. His brother was Dr. W. P. Alison, a physician and social reformer whose *Observations on the Management of the Poor in Scotland* (1840) Engels also quotes. (005-032, 013-002)

## Role in Engels's Account

Alison appears in Engels's text more frequently than almost any other named authority — thirteen times across multiple chapters. Engels uses him as an indispensable statistical source on crime, poverty, and public health in [[glasgow]] and the industrial districts, yet consistently qualifies his reliance with sharp criticism of Alison's political limitations.

On the factual level, Engels accepts Alison's data without reservation. He cites Alison's figures for [[cholera|fever]] epidemics in Ireland: 60,000 cases "in a more recent year" in Cork (005-006). He draws on Alison's [[glasgow|Glasgow]] statistics showing "some thirty thousand workingmen get drunk every Saturday night" and that "in 1830, one house in twelve, and in 1840, one house in ten, was a public-house" (005-037). He quotes Alison's crime figures for Lanarkshire, where "population has doubled once in thirty years, and crime once in five and a half, or six times more rapidly than the population" (005-042). For Ireland, he uses Alison's estimate that 2,300,000 persons — 27 percent of the population — could not survive without assistance (010-009, 010-011).

Yet Engels's deployment of Alison is always dialectical. He makes the Tory sheriff a witness against the bourgeoisie by showing that even a conservative acknowledges the depth of social crisis. Engels writes that Alison "attributes almost everything" to the centralisation of population in great cities, and notes that this is "natural, for the propertied class has too direct an interest in the other conditions which tend to destroy the worker body and soul." (005-030)

The key passage comes when Engels delivers his verdict on Alison as a thinker:

> "Alison is humane enough to admit this; he is no thoroughbred Liberal manufacturer, but only a half developed Tory bourgeois, and he has, therefore, an open eye, now and then, where the full-fledged bourgeois is still stone blind." (005-032)

The phrase "half-bourgeois Alison" recurs. It captures Engels's ambivalent assessment: Alison sees more than the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] Liberals because his Tory paternalism retains a residual sense of social obligation, but he cannot see far enough because his class position prevents him from recognising that the system itself — not merely its urban symptoms — is the source of [[exploitation]]. He blames the great cities rather than the mode of production. Engels deploys Alison's data while drawing conclusions Alison himself refused to draw: that the [[proletariat]]'s misery, drunkenness, and [[crime|criminality]] were not the consequences of urban [[crowding]] but of the [[competition]] logic inherent in [[capital]]. (005-032)

Alison's analysis of Irish poverty receives similar treatment. Engels quotes Alison's figure of 120 million pounds sterling needed to bring Irish soil to English fertility levels (010-011), using it to demonstrate the depth of Irish immiseration while implicitly rejecting Alison's [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian framework — the idea that population pressure rather than colonial [[exploitation]] and land monopoly explains Irish misery.

## Context

Alison's brother, Dr. W. P. Alison, appears separately in Engels's text as a medical authority on [[cholera|fever]] epidemics and the [[sanitary|sanitary conditions]] of the poor (002-021, 005-007, 013-001). The two Alisons represent the Tory philanthropic tradition — genuinely concerned with working-class suffering, opposed to the naked [[competition]] of Liberal political economy, but ultimately committed to preserving the existing social order. Their presence in Engels's text complements that of parliamentary reformers like [[lord-ashley]] and [[richard-oastler]]: Tory aristocrats who documented the degradation of the [[proletariat]] while refusing to challenge the [[capital]] that produced it. Engels noted that both brothers were "religious Tories" (013-001, 013-002).
