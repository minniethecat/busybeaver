# Ten Hours' Bill Movement

## Overview

The [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] movement was a sustained working-class campaign to secure a legal maximum ten-hour working day in textile factories. Originating in the [[factory-system|factory]] districts of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] and [[lancashire|Lancashire]] in the early 1830s, the movement gained momentum through the advocacy of [[Michael Sadler]] and [[Richard Oastler]] — Tories who forged an unlikely alliance with the industrial proletariat — and became the defining legislative struggle between labour and capital in early Victorian England. Engels treats the movement as the central case study of how the [[proletariat|working class]] learned to wage political war against the bourgeoisie. (006-045)

The campaign proceeded in phases. [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] secured a parliamentary committee in 1832 whose report exposed [[factory-system|factory]] conditions but was attacked as partisan. After [[michael-sadler|Sadler]]'s death, [[Lord Ashley]] (later the 7th Earl of [[lord-ashley|Shaftesbury]]) became the parliamentary champion. The agitation intensified through the late 1830s and reached a dramatic climax in 1844 when [[lord-ashley|Ashley]] carried a House of Commons resolution in favour of a ten-hour day (179 to 170), only for the ministry under [[Robert Peel|Sir Robert Peel]] and [[Sir James Graham]] to manoeuvre the House into reversing itself. The movement finally achieved legislative success in the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1847. (006-007, 006-050)

## Engels's Account

Engels opens his discussion of the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] in Chapter 6 with Lord [[lord-ashley|Ashley]]'s speech of 15 March 1844, which used government statistics to expose the demographics of [[factory-system|factory]] labour. [[lord-ashley|Ashley]]'s data showed that of 419,560 [[factory-system|factory]] operatives in the British Empire in 1839, 192,887 — nearly half — were under eighteen, and only 96,569, less than one full quarter, were adult males. Engels deploys these figures to argue that the [[factory-system|factory]] system systematically displaced adult men with cheaper women and children, dissolving the family and inverting "the existing social order." (006-007)

Engels's account of the [[parliamentary-commission-1833|Factory Commission of 1833]] and its report provides the backstory. The Commission, "composed of Liberal bourgeois," was established in response to [[michael-sadler|Sadler]]'s committee. Engels describes its report as written "on the side of the bourgeoisie" — "it reproaches the operatives that in sustaining the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] they thought, not of the children only, but of themselves as well." Yet even this hostile report, Engels insists, proved the operatives' case: "it cannot whitewash the manufacturers, and still it leaves such a mass of infamies upon the shoulders of the employers, that even after this report, the agitation for the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]], the hatred against the manufacturers, and the committee's severest epithets applied to them are all fully justified." (006-045)

The dramatic parliamentary events of 1844 receive detailed treatment. [[lord-ashley|Ashley]]'s successful resolution of 19 March 1844 was reversed after Graham threatened resignation; the House then "swallowed whole" what it had just rejected. Engels comments:

> "The House of Commons by its votes upon this subject, each vote reversing the last, has brought itself into the greatest contempt among all the workers, and proved most brilliantly the Chartists' assertion of the necessity of its reform." (006-050)

In Chapter 8, Engels shows how the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] fit within the broader political realignment of the [[proletariat|working class]]. The movement was "already in the closest relation to [[chartism|Chartism]]." At the meetings of 1838, Oastler was active circulating petitions for [[factory-system|factory]] reform "along with the national petition for the [[chartism|People's Charter]] adopted in [[birmingham|Birmingham]]." The question of [[factory-system|factory]] hours thus became inseparable from the working-class political programme. (008-028)

When the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] emerged, the Chartists' refusal to subordinate ten-hours agitation to [[free-trade|Free Trade]] completed the split between proletarian and bourgeois radicalism. The Radical bourgeoisie "attacked the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] and all exclusively workingmen's demands," while the Chartist workingmen "espoused with redoubled zeal all the struggles of the proletariat against the bourgeoisie." The [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]], "protection of the workers against the capitalist, good wages, a guaranteed position, repeal of the new [[poor-law|Poor Law]]" — all these demands belonged to [[chartism|Chartism]] "quite as essentially as the 'Six Points'" and were "directly opposed to [[competition|free competition]] and [[free-trade|Free Trade]]." (008-032, 008-033)

Writing in 1844–45, Engels was confident of ultimate victory: "There remains, however, no doubt that, in a very short time, the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] will really be adopted." The manufacturers' economic arguments — that a ten-hour day would raise costs and destroy competitiveness — were, he conceded, "all half true," but they proved only that "the industrial greatness of England can be maintained only through the barbarous treatment of the operatives, the destruction of their health, the social, physical, and mental decay of whole generations." (006-050)

The bill was also the occasion for the most dangerous legislative attack on working-class organising. In the 1844 session, a Mr. Miles proposed a Master and Servant Bill that would have empowered employers to have any worker who refused work imprisoned for two months with hard labour, on the employer's oath alone. Engels notes that this bill "aroused the workingmen to the utmost fury, the more so as the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] was before Parliament at the same time, and had called forth a considerable agitation." Mass meetings and petitions led by [[Thomas Duncombe]], the Chartist MP, defeated the bill. (011-007)

## Context

The Ten Hours movement must be understood against the backdrop of the prevailing [[factory-system|factory]] working day of fourteen to sixteen hours. Engels documents that even under the [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act of 1833]], which nominally capped young persons at twelve hours, the law was widely evaded, and adults remained entirely unprotected. The demand for a ten-hour maximum was not about comfort but survival — [[factory-system|factory]] labour, Engels argues, produced a distinctive pattern of exhaustion, deformity, and premature ageing.

The movement's cross-class alliance — Tory philanthropists leading proletarian agitation — was a distinctive feature of early Victorian politics. [[Richard Oastler]], called "[[richard-oastler|the king of the factory children]]" by his working-class admirers, combined opposition to the [[factory-system|factory]] system with opposition to the [[new-poor-law-1834|New Poor Law]], for which he was imprisoned for debt. The Whigs offered to pay his debts if he would cease agitation; he refused and conducted his campaigns from prison through his "Fleet Papers." (006-049)

## Aftermath

The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1847, passed shortly after Engels completed his manuscript, limited the working day for women and young persons to ten hours. In the 1892 Preface, Engels noted that the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] was eventually "enacted," but characterised it — along with other reforms — as a measure that accelerated "the concentration of capital in the hands of the few" by raising costs for smaller manufacturers who depended on longer hours, while large capitalists with more efficient machinery could absorb the change. The Ten Hours Act nonetheless established the principle that the state could regulate adult [[working-day|working hours]], a precedent that shaped labour law throughout the nineteenth century. (P01-004)

The movement also had lasting political consequences. Engels argues that the Ten Hours agitation taught the [[proletariat|working class]] — and the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] in particular — the skills of mass organisation, petitioning, and parliamentary pressure that would later be deployed by the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. The experience of watching the House of Commons reverse its own vote under ministerial pressure reinforced working-class conviction that parliamentary reform through the [[chartism|People's Charter]] was a prerequisite for all other reforms. (006-050)
