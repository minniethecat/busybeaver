## Definition

Morality, in Engels's analysis, is not a universal code but a class-specific ideology. The [[bourgeoisie]] promulgates a system of morals centred on the "sacredness of property" that serves its own interests, while the material conditions of proletarian life make adherence to that system structurally impossible. Engels treats the moral degradation of the [[proletariat|working class]] not as a character failing but as a predictable consequence of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism.

## Engels's Analysis

### Commercial Morality as Expediency

Engels opens his treatment of morality with a characteristically materialist observation: even bourgeois commercial honesty is not a matter of principle but of market expediency. In the preface to the 1892 English edition, he notes:

> A certain standard of commercial morality is unavoidably developed, purely as a means of saving time and trouble. [P01-003]

The large-scale capitalist, operating in competitive markets, discovers that "those tricks do not pay any longer in a large market, where time is money" [P01-003]. Morality, from the outset, is revealed as epiphenomenal to economic relations — a convenience rather than a conviction.

### The Failure of Moral Education

The [[bourgeoisie]]'s effort to inculcate its morality through [[education|schooling]] is, Engels argues, a comprehensive failure. Religious and moral instruction are intertwined in English schools, producing confusion rather than ethical formation:

> The simple principles which, for plain human beings, regulate the relations of man to man, brought into the direst confusion by our social state, our war of each against all, necessarily remain confused and foreign to the workingman when mixed with incomprehensible dogmas, and preached in the religious form of an arbitrary and dogmatic commandment. [005-022]

Engels notes with grim irony that the bourgeoisie "does not even take the trouble to impress upon the workers the morality of the day, which the bourgeoisie has patched together in its own interest for its own protection" [005-022]. Even this minimal ideological investment is too great an effort for a class Engels portrays as "enfeebled and sluggish."

### The Structural Impossibility of Bourgeois Morality

Engels's most incisive argument is that the proletarian cannot respect bourgeois morality because the material basis for that morality — property ownership — is denied to him:

> It is all very pretty and very agreeable to the ear of the bourgeois to hear the "sacredness of property" asserted; but for him who has none, the sacredness of property dies out of itself. Money is the god of this world; the bourgeois takes the proletarian's money from him and so makes a practical atheist of him. [005-024]

When poverty intensifies to starvation, "the temptation to disregard all social order does but gain power" [005-024]. The worker faces a choice "between starving slowly, killing himself speedily, or taking what he needs where he finds it — in plain English, stealing" [005-024]. Engels cites Dr. Alison's testimony that "[[poverty|want]] leaves the workingman" with precisely this trilemma, and notes without surprise "that most of them prefer stealing to starvation and suicide" [005-024].

### Moral Consequences of Women's Factory Labour

The employment of women in factories produces what Engels terms "even worse" moral consequences than the physical ones. The concentration of both sexes in confined workspaces, without moral or mental education, creates conditions he describes through official testimony:

> The collecting of persons of both sexes and all ages in a single workroom, the inevitable contact, the crowding into a small space of people, to whom neither mental nor moral education has been given, is not calculated for the favourable development of the female character. [006-012]

A witness in [[leicester|Leicester]] "would rather let his daughter beg than go into a [[factory-system|factory]]," describing them as "perfect gates of hell" [006-012]. Commissioner Cowell judged that "the morality of the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives is somewhat below the average of that of the working-class in general" [006-012].

### Morality in the Lace and Metal Districts

The theme recurs across industries. In [[nottingham|Nottingham]]'s lace district, Engels reports unanimity among "the police, the clergy, the manufacturers, the working-people, and the parents" that "the present system of labour is a most fruitful source of immorality" [007-003]. The irregular hours — winders and threaders "called upon whenever the spools on a frame are empty" — "engender a multitude of physical and moral ills, especially early and unbridled sexual licence" [007-003].

### The Irish Immigration Question

Engels addresses the widespread bourgeois attribution of working-class immorality to Irish immigration. While acknowledging that Irish immigrants brought habits formed under extreme deprivation, he frames this as a competitive economic pressure rather than a racial-moral failing:

> These people having grown up almost without civilisation, accustomed from youth to every sort of privation, rough, intemperate, and improvident, bring all their brutal habits with them among a class of the English population which has, in truth, little inducement to cultivate education and morality. [004-001]

## Historical Context

Engels's treatment of morality draws on the utilitarian ethics of [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] and the political economy of [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]], while anticipating [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s later critique of bourgeois morality as ideological superstructure. The 1840s saw intense public debate about working-class "[[prostitution|vice]]," fuelled by statistical societies and the [[public-health|sanitary reform]] movement. Engels's innovation was to reverse the causal arrow: immorality was not the cause of poverty but its product.

## Related Concepts

Morality is tightly linked to [[religion]] (the institutional vehicle for moral instruction), [[education]] (the site of its failed transmission), [[competition]] (the economic logic that shapes all social relations), [[crime]] (the legal consequence of moral dissolution), and [[drunkenness]] (which Engels treats as a rational response to intolerable conditions).
