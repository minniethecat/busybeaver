# Richard Arkwright

## Life

Sir Richard Arkwright (1732–1792) was born in [[preston|Preston]], [[lancashire|Lancashire]], the youngest of thirteen children. He began his working life as a barber and wigmaker before turning his entrepreneurial energies to the [[machinery|mechanisation]] of [[spinning|cotton spinning]]. In 1767 he developed the spinning throstle — later known as the water frame — a machine "calculated from the beginning for mechanical motive power" and "based upon wholly new principles." (P04-008) Unlike [[james-hargreaves|Hargreaves's]] spinning jenny, which could be operated by hand in a cottage, Arkwright's water frame required waterpower and [[factory-system|factory]]-scale organisation from the outset.

Arkwright established his first water-powered mill at Cromford, [[derby|Derby]]shire, in 1771, and rapidly built additional mills across [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and the Midlands. He was knighted in 1786 and died a wealthy man in 1792, having transformed the structure of the British cotton industry.

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels accords Arkwright a pivotal place in the history of the [[industrial-revolution]]. In the preface to the English edition, Engels narrates the sequence of inventions that created the modern proletariat:

> "And the factory system, the beginning of which was thus made, received a fresh extension in 1767, through the spinning throstle invented by Richard Arkwright, a barber, in Preston, in North Lancashire. After the steam-engine, this is the most important mechanical invention of the 18th century." (P04-008)

The water frame's dependence on mechanical power meant that it could not be adopted by individual hand-spinners. It required capital, purpose-built premises, and a disciplined workforce — the essential elements of the [[factory-system|factory]] system:

> "It was calculated from the beginning for mechanical motive power, and was based upon wholly new principles." (P04-008)

Engels traces how Arkwright's water frame, combined with [[samuel-crompton|Crompton's]] mule (1785) and Arkwright's own carding engine and preparatory frames, made "the [[factory-system|factory]] system... the prevailing one for the spinning of cotton." (P04-008) The [[machinery|steam-engine]] of [[james-watt|James Watt]], applied to spinning from 1785, "made doubly important" these mechanical innovations, setting off the chain reaction of technological change that Engels presents as the material foundation of the class structure he analyses.

Engels also notes that Arkwright was among the inventors whose [[machinery|machines]] were destroyed by workers resisting the introduction of machinery:

> "The first inventors, Arkwright and others, were persecuted in this way and their machines destroyed." (008-006)

This early machine-breaking — the Luddite response — Engels characterises as "the most primitive form of protest," one that "never became the universal expression of the public opinion of the workingmen." (008-006) The more mature expression of class opposition would be [[trade-unions|trade unionism]] and [[chartist-movement|Chartism]].

## Context

Arkwright's career exemplifies the social mobility that [[capital|industrial capital]]ism made possible — the barber who became a knight and a magnate — but Engels's interest is not in Arkwright as an individual success story. Rather, Arkwright appears as an agent of the structural transformation that created the [[proletariat]] as a class. The water frame did not simply increase productivity; it fundamentally altered the relations of production, concentrating workers in factories, separating them from the [[capital|means of production]], and rendering their labour subordinate to the rhythm of the machine.

The destruction of Arkwright's [[machinery|machines]] places him at the origin of a conflict that would define the century: the struggle between labour and capital over the control and consequences of technology.

## Legacy

Arkwright's system of [[factory-system|factory]] organisation — centralised production, division of labour, mechanical power, and disciplined timekeeping — spread from cotton to the whole of British [[manufacture|manufacturing]]. The [[cotton-industry|cotton industry]] he helped create became the engine of British industrial dominance, and the [[urban|factory town]]s of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] became the terrain on which Engels conducted his investigation. Arkwright's legacy, in Engels's account, is thus profoundly ambivalent: he was both a genius of invention and an unwitting architect of the proletarian condition.
