## Overview

Bethnal Green was a densely populated working-class district in East [[london|London]] during the 1840s, known for its concentration of hand-loom silk weavers and its extreme poverty. Engels included Bethnal Green in his catalogue of [[london|London]] [[housing|slums]], citing it alongside [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]] and [[st-giles|St. Giles]] as evidence of the degradation produced by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism in the imperial capital.

## Engels's Account

### London's East End Slums

Engels situates Bethnal Green within the geography of [[london|London]] poverty, describing the district east of the Tower of [[london|London]]:

> "The district lies east of the Tower in Whitechapel and Bethnal Green, where the greatest mass of London's working people are crowded together." [002-011]

The area housed tens of thousands of silk weavers, whose trade had been devastated by [[machinery|mechanisation]] and foreign competition, reducing once-independent artisans to desperate poverty.

### The Workhouse

Bethnal Green's workhouse became emblematic of the cruelty of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] system. Engels records a particularly harrowing case:

> "In Bethnal Green workhouse, London, a woman in labour was forced to share a bed teeming with vermin." [011-015]

This case, drawn from contemporary [[poor-law|Poor Law]] inquiries, illustrated the degrading conditions to which the destitute were subjected under the workhouse system.

### Religious Commentary

The district's poverty attracted the attention of religious reformers. Engels cites the testimony of a local clergyman:

> "Preacher of St. Philip's, Bethnal Green." [012-002]

The reference appears in Engels's discussion of mortality statistics compiled by D. W. P. Alison, documenting the dramatically shorter life expectancy of working-class residents compared to affluent [[london|London]]ers.
