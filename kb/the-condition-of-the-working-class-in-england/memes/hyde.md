## Overview

Hyde is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in Cheshire, situated in the Tame valley approximately seven miles east of [[manchester|Manchester]] and adjacent to [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]]. During the 1840s, it was a cotton-spinning centre. Hyde appears in Engels's account primarily through its connection to the manufacturer Thomas [[ashton|Ashton]] and as one of the destinations of the mass workers' march during the general strike of August 1842.

## Engels's Account

### Thomas Ashton

Engels identifies the manufacturer Thomas [[ashton|Ashton]] of Hyde as a figure in the labour movement conflicts:

> "violent labour movement, young Ashton, a manufacturer in Hyde, near Ashton-under-Lyne" [008-015]

Thomas [[ashton|Ashton]] is noted in Engels's list of model employers — manufacturers who, unlike the majority, maintained some paternalistic concern for their workers. Engels mentions him alongside other "enlightened" manufacturers such as the Ashworths of Turton and [[john-fielden|Fielden]] of Todmorden, contrasting them with the mass of "liberal manufacturers" who opposed all [[factory-act|factory regulation]] [013-008].

### The 1842 General Strike

Hyde was one of the towns targeted by the mass mobilisation of August 1842:

> "August 8th they proceeded to Ashton and Hyde five thousand strong" [008-029]

The arrival of 5,000 workers in this relatively small town underscores the scale of the general strike and the interconnected geography of working-class action across the [[manchester|Manchester]] district. Hyde's proximity to [[ashton|Ashton]] made the two towns natural twin targets for the marching workers, who moved from [[factory-system|factory]] to [[factory-system|factory]] demanding that workers "turn out" in solidarity.

### Industrial Character

Hyde belonged to the ring of satellite towns supplying cotton yarn to [[manchester|Manchester]]'s finishing and commercial operations. Like its neighbours, it grew rapidly in the early decades of the nineteenth century as water-powered spinning mills gave way to steam-driven factories.
