## Overview

Preston is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in North [[lancashire|Lancashire]], situated on the River Ribble. With a population of about 60,000 in the 1840s [P04-010], it was one of the larger cotton-spinning centres in [[lancashire|Lancashire]]. It holds particular significance in Engels's history of industrial technology as the place where [[richard-arkwright|Richard Arkwright]], a barber by trade, developed the key innovation that launched the [[factory-system|factory]] system.

## Engels's Account

### Technological Origins

Engels records Preston's role in the invention of the spinning frame:

> "James Hargreaves, a weaver, in Standhill, near Blackburn, in North Lancashire, in 1764, invented the spinning-jenny... Richard Arkwright, a barber, in Preston, in North Lancashire, in 1767, invented the spinning-frame, the first machine to be driven by water-power, and specially adapted for the production of cotton twist." [P04-008]

Arkwright's water-frame, developed in Preston, was "the first machine to be driven by water-power" — the technological breakthrough that made large-scale [[factory-system|factory production]] possible, replacing the domestic spinning wheel with mechanised, water-driven spinning.

### Urban Conditions

Engels ranks Preston among the worst of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[urban|factory town]]s:

> "Among the worst of these towns after Preston and Oldham is Bolton" [002-032]

By placing Preston *first* in the category of "worst" towns, Engels signals that its conditions were among the most degraded even by the low standards of industrial [[lancashire|Lancashire]]. However, unlike [[bolton|Bolton]], he does not provide a separate detailed physical description of Preston's streets and housing — Preston appears primarily as a benchmark against which other towns are measured.

### Economic Role

Preston is listed among the [[manchester|Manchester]] satellite towns with their commercial infrastructure:

> "Hence Bolton, Preston, Wigan, Bury, Rochdale, Middleton, Heywood, Oldham, Ashton, Stalybridge, Stockport, etc., with their innumerable 'respectable' retail dealers" [002-031]
