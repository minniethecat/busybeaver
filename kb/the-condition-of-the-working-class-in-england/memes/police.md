# Police

## Definition

The police in Engels's England were the public force charged with maintaining order, enforcing the law, and — nominally — protecting the health and safety of the population. Engels treats the police as an institution with a dual character. On one side, it is the sanitary police, responsible for inspecting the condition of working-class [[housing|dwellings]] and enforcing minimal standards of cleanliness. On the other side, it is the constabulary, whose truncheon represents the coercive power of the bourgeois state over the [[proletariat|working class]]. In both roles, Engels argues, the police serve the interests of property rather than persons. (008-022, 002-047)

## Engels's Analysis

### The Police as an Instrument of Class Rule

> The English bourgeois finds himself reproduced in his law, as he does in his God, the policeman's truncheon which, in a certain measure, is his own club, has for him a wonderfully soothing power. But for the workingman quite otherwise! The workingman knows too well, has learned from too oft-repeated experience, that the law is a rod which the bourgeois has prepared for him. (008-022)

This passage is Engels's most concentrated statement on the class character of the police. The law, he argues, is sacred to the bourgeois because it is his own composition, enacted with his consent and for his benefit. The policeman's truncheon is a metonym for the entire legal apparatus — it is the bourgeois's own club, brandished on his behalf. For the workingman, the same truncheon represents an alien and hostile force. Engels insists that the English worker does not fear the police in the sense of internalising their authority; he merely avoids them when he can, and resists them when organised resistance is possible. He cites weekly beatings of policemen in [[manchester|Manchester]] and an attempted storming of a police station as evidence that working-class deference to the constabulary was thin. (008-022)

> It is ridiculous to assert that the English workingman fears the police, when every week in Manchester policemen are beaten, and last year an attempt was made to storm a station-house secured by iron doors and shutters. (008-022)

### The Police and Starvation

> If he can get no work he may steal, if he is not afraid of the police, or starve, in which case the police will take care that he does so in a quiet and inoffensive manner. (002-005)

Engels presents the police as the enforcer of a stark binary: the unemployed worker may steal and face arrest, or starve — and even in starvation, the police intervene, not to prevent death from hunger, but to ensure that it occurs discreetly, without disturbing public order. The phrase "in a quiet and inoffensive manner" is bitterly sarcastic: the police manage the visibility of starvation rather than its causes. (002-005)

### The Sanitary Police: Conscience Stirred but Asleep

Engels devotes considerable attention to the sanitary police, the branch of the constabulary charged with inspecting working-class [[housing|dwellings]]. His verdict is damning: the sanitary police were active only during epidemics, and even then their interventions were superficial and impermanent.

> As the police charged with care of the streets, does not trouble itself about the condition of these courts, as everything quietly lies where it is thrown, there is no cause for wonder at the filth and heaps of ashes and offal to be found here. (002-047)

In the working-class courts of [[manchester|Manchester]], the police were simply absent. They did not enforce street cleaning; they did not compel landlords to provide drainage. The result was the accumulation of filth that Engels documents in detail — stagnant pools, heaps of refuse, privies shared by hundreds of people. This neglect was not accidental; it reflected the police's understanding that their duty was to protect the propertied districts, not the working-class quarters. (002-047)

> Occasionally when an epidemic threatens, the otherwise sleepy conscience of the sanitary police is a little stirred, raids are made into the workingmen's districts, whole rows of cellars and cottages are closed, as happened in the case of several lanes near Oldham Road; but this does not last long: the condemned cottages soon find occupants again, the owners are much better off by letting them, and the sanitary police won't come again so soon. (002-049)

The pattern was consistent: epidemic threat aroused the sanitary police; a few [[housing|dwellings]] were condemned; the panic subsided; the condemned [[housing|dwellings]] were re-let; and the police did not return. The "sleepy conscience" of the sanitary police was, in Engels's account, the conscience of the bourgeoisie itself — capable of short-lived alarm when cholera threatened to cross the boundary between the [[housing|slums]] and the respectable quarters, but incapable of sustained attention to the conditions that bred [[cholera|epidemic disease]]. (002-049)

> In spite of all the preachings of the physicians, in spite of the excitement into which the cholera epidemic plunged the sanitary police by reason of the condition of Little Ireland, in spite of everything, in this year of grace 1844, it is in almost the same state as in 1831! (002-050)

In Little Ireland, the most notorious slum in [[manchester|Manchester]], the sanitary police had inspected and reported in 1831 during the [[cholera|cholera epidemic]]. Thirteen years later, Engels found the district "in almost the same state." The police had not followed up; the landlords had not been compelled to improve their properties; and the inhabitants continued to live in conditions that Engels describes as below "the lowest stage of humanity." (002-050, 002-051)

### The Health Commission: Documentation Without Action

During the cholera visitation, a Health Commission was formed and the sanitary police made a systematic survey of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s working-class districts. The results were damning.

> There were inspected, in all, 6,951 houses — naturally in Manchester proper alone, Salford and the other suburbs being excluded. Of these, 6,565 urgently needed whitewashing within; 960 were out of repair; 939 had insufficient drains; 1,435 were damp; 452 were badly ventilated; 2,221 were without privies. Of the 687 streets inspected, 248 were unpaved, 53 but partially paved, 112 ill-ventilated, 352 containing standing pools, heaps of debris, refuse, etc. (002-054)

Engels notes that even this unprecedented inspection produced almost no remedial action: "To cleanse such an Augean stable before the arrival of the cholera was, of course, out of the question. A few of the worst nooks were therefore cleansed, and everything else left as before." The sanitary police could document the filth; they could not, or would not, compel its removal. The inspection was a performance of concern that changed nothing. (002-054)

### The Police and the Beggars

> When these people find no work and will not rebel against society, what remains for them but to beg? And surely no one can wonder at the great army of beggars, most of them able-bodied men, with whom the police carries on perpetual war. (003-012)

Engels describes a "perpetual war" between the police and the beggars who filled the streets of [[urban|factory town]]s. The beggars were not criminals in any ordinary sense — they were unemployed workers who had exhausted every other means of survival. Yet the police treated them as enemies of public order, harassing and dispersing them. For Engels, this "war" was a microcosm of the larger class war: the police, as agents of the bourgeoisie, made it impossible for the surplus population even to beg in peace. (003-012)

### The Police and Commercial Fraud

The police also appear in Engels's account in connection with commercial fraud — specifically, the systematic cheating of working-class consumers by small retailers using false weights and measures.

> An incredible number of convictions for such offences may be read in the police reports. (002-060)

Yet Engels notes that the police response to commercial fraud was wholly inadequate. Legal penalties attached to very few forms of adulteration unless they involved revenue frauds against the state. The petty retailer who defrauded workers could simply move to another district if detected. The police reports documented the fraud but rarely suppressed it. (002-060)

### The Police in Industrial Conflict

> The firm, meanwhile, succeeded with great difficulty in securing hands from the neighbourhood, and among the knobsticks, against whom in the beginning intimidation was used, the proprietors set twelve men to guard the yard, all ex-soldiers and policemen, armed with guns. (008-021)

During the brickmakers' strike of May 1843, the police appeared not as neutral keepers of the peace but as armed guards protecting strike-breakers and the employer's property. Ex-soldiers and policemen, armed with guns, were hired by the firm to defend the brickyard against the striking workers. When the strikers attacked, the police-guards fired on them from behind a hedge. The military was called in, and the police rounded up the retreating workers. In this episode, Engels shows the police functioning as the direct, armed instrument of the employer in an industrial dispute. (008-021)

### Uneven Enforcement: Manchester and Salford

> If, in Manchester, the police, from time to time, every six or ten years, makes a raid upon the working-people's districts, closes the worst dwellings, and causes the filthiest spots in these Augean stables to be cleansed, in Salford it seems to have done absolutely nothing. (002-052)

Even within the metropolitan area, police enforcement was uneven. [[manchester|Manchester]] received occasional — if ineffectual — attention; [[salford|Salford]] received none. Engels attributes this disparity not to any difference in the character of the police force but to the political leverage of the different bourgeoisies. [[manchester|Manchester]]'s bourgeoisie, more exposed to public scrutiny and international opinion, made occasional gestures towards sanitary improvement. [[salford|Salford]]'s bourgeoisie did not bother. The police simply reflected the priorities of their masters. (002-052)
