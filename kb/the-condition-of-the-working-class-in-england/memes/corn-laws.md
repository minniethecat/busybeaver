# Corn Laws

## Overview

The Corn Laws were a series of protectionist statutes that imposed heavy tariffs on imported grain to maintain high prices for British-grown wheat. Originally passed in 1815 after the Napoleonic Wars to protect landowners' wartime profits, the laws were modified in 1822 and 1828 but retained their essential character: a sliding scale of duties that made imported grain prohibitively expensive whenever domestic prices fell below a threshold. The effect was to transfer income from every bread-buying household to the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]. For the [[proletariat|working class]], whose [[diet|food]] budget was dominated by bread, the Corn Laws functioned as a regressive tax that made [[diet|subsistence]] more expensive precisely when wages were falling. (P01-010)

## Engels's Account

Engels views the battle over the Corn Laws as the defining political confrontation between two fractions of the propertied class: the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] (who profited from protection) and the industrial bourgeoisie (who [[poverty|want]]ed cheap bread to reduce wage costs). The workers, Engels insists, were not the intended beneficiaries of repeal — they were the rhetorical justification used by [[free-trade|Free Trade]] advocates who cared far more about labour costs than about hungry families. (008-028)

> In order to keep up prices, the Corn Laws were passed, prohibiting the importation of foreign grain. (010-001)

The Corn Laws appear throughout the text as a structural determinant of working-class misery. High bread prices meant that a larger share of the worker's wage went to [[diet|food]], leaving less for housing, clothing, and other necessities. Engels documents how, in the [[rural|agricultural districts]], the Corn Laws paradoxically harmed the very labourers they were supposedly protecting: by keeping grain prices high, they enriched landlords while [[agricultural-proletariat|farm labourers]] remained among the worst-paid workers in England. (010-001)

## Impact

The [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], founded in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1838, became the most powerful middle-class reform organisation in Britain. Engels watched it operate at close quarters and chronicles its cynical relationship with the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]]. The League parroted humanitarian arguments about cheap bread while opposing every measure that would genuinely improve workers' conditions: it opposed the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]], opposed trade union rights, and supported the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]]. When Chartist leaders pointed out this hypocrisy, the League denounced them as enemies of progress. (008-029)

The repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846, under Sir [[robert-peel|Robert Peel]]'s Conservative government, marked a watershed. Engels treats it as the moment when the industrial bourgeoisie definitively triumphed over the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] — not through revolution but through the inexorable logic of capital accumulation. The victory was incomplete: the 1846 Act provided for a three-year phase-out of duties rather than immediate abolition. But the principle was established, and the political balance of power had shifted irreversibly. (P01-004)
