## Overview

Walsall was a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in the Black Country district of South Staffordshire during the 1840s, known for its diverse metal trades including lock-making, bit and spur [[manufacture|manufacturing]], and saddlers' ironmongery. Engels mentions Walsall in the context of the wave of working-class unrest that spread across the industrial districts of the Midlands during the early 1840s, when hunger, unemployment, and Chartist agitation drove workers to direct action.

## Engels's Account

### Black Country Unrest

Engels lists Walsall among the South Staffordshire towns where working people rose in protest against their conditions:

> "In Wolverhampton, Willenhall, Bilston, Sedgeley, Wednesfield, Darlaston, Dudley, Walsall, Wednesbury, etc., there are..." [007-010]

The passage appears in Engels's discussion of the 1842 general strike and disturbances — the closest Britain came to a nationwide working-class insurrection. The Black Country towns, with their concentration of ironworkers, miners, and metal-trades artisans, were centres of Chartist organisation and trade union militancy.

The enumeration of so many small and medium-sized towns in this passage serves Engels's argument that industrial unrest was not confined to major cities like [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[birmingham|Birmingham]] but permeated the entire [[manufacture|manufacturing]] landscape. The Black Country, with its interlocking network of specialised metal-working towns, was particularly susceptible to the spread of strike action, as workers in one town could quickly learn of and join actions in neighbouring communities.
