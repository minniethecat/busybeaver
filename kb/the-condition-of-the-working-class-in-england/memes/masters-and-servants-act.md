## Overview

The Masters and Servants Acts were a series of statutes dating from the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries that governed employment relations in England. Unlike modern labour law, which treats employer and employee as equal parties to a civil contract, the Masters and Servants Acts embedded a fundamental asymmetry: a worker who left employment before the end of the agreed term, or who performed work inadequately, could be prosecuted criminally and imprisoned; an employer who breached the contract faced only civil damages.

The 1823 Act (4 Geo. IV c. 34) consolidated earlier legislation and extended its reach across a widening range of trades. It empowered magistrates — often manufacturers themselves — to enforce labour discipline through the threat of incarceration. For Engels, this legal architecture was not an aberration but the logical expression of class rule.

## Engels's Account

Engels identifies the Masters and Servants Act as a defining issue of working-class politics in the 1840s. In his account of the Parliamentary session of 1844, he lists it alongside the Poor Relief Bill and the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] as one of three legislative debates that dominated the year:

> "With every session of Parliament the working-class gains ground, the interests of the middle-class diminish in importance; and, in spite of the fact that the middle-class is the chief, in fact, the only power in Parliament, the last session of 1844 was a continuous debate upon subjects affecting the working-class, the Poor Relief Bill, the Factory Act, the Masters' and Servants' Act; and [[thomas-duncombe|Thomas Duncombe]], the representative of the workingmen in the House of Commons, was the great man of the session." [P04-024]

The passage is significant because Engels presents the prominence of these working-class issues in Parliament as evidence that the proletariat's political influence was growing, even without formal representation. The Masters and Servants Act exemplified, in Engels's view, how the law functioned as an instrument of class domination — criminalising the worker's breach of contract while treating the master's breach as a civil matter.

## Class Inequality in Law

The asymmetry Engels identifies in the Masters and Servants Act is part of his broader argument about the legal framework of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. He regards legislation of this kind as exposing the pretensions of bourgeois justice:

- Workers could be imprisoned for leaving a job; employers could not be imprisoned for withholding wages
- Magistrates who enforced the Act were drawn from the same [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class as the employers
- The threat of prosecution served as a disciplinary mechanism that suppressed wage demands and union organisation

This critique is consistent with Engels's treatment of the [[combination-laws|Combination Laws]] and other anti-worker legislation. In each case, he argues that the legal system is not neutral between classes — it is designed and operated by the bourgeoisie in their own interest.

## Thomas Duncombe and Parliamentary Opposition

Engels's account highlights the role of [[thomas-duncombe|Thomas Duncombe]], the radical MP for Finsbury, who functioned as an unofficial representative of the [[proletariat|working class]] in the Commons. [[thomas-duncombe|Duncombe]]'s prominence during the 1844 session — in which the Masters and Servants Act was debated — demonstrated to Engels that the workers' political weight was increasing even within an unreformed Parliament.
