# Canal

## Definition

In Engels's account of English industrial development, canals constitute one of the foundational infrastructural achievements that made the [[factory-system|factory]] system possible. Before 1755, as Engels notes, "England possessed almost no canals." The canal-building era, inaugurated by James Brindley's Duke of Bridgewater's Canal in 1759, created an inland transport network of 2,200 miles of canals and 1,800 miles of navigable river that linked the [[coal|coalfields]] to the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts, the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts to the ports, and the ports to world markets. For Engels, canals are not neutral engineering works but instruments of capital centralisation — they are among "the three great levers with which manufacture, since the middle of the last century, has been busy putting the world out of joint." (P04-019, 001-003)

## Canals and the Infrastructure of Industrialisation

> Before 1755 England possessed almost no canals. In that year a canal was built in Lancashire from Sankey Brook to St. Helen's; and in 1759, James Brindley built the first important one, the Duke of Bridgewater's canal from Manchester, and the coal mines of the district to the mouth of the Mersey passing, near Barton, by aqueduct, over the river Irwell. (P04-019)

The canal system Engels describes was a product of private capital, not state initiative: "These improvements, too, like the [[railway|railroads]] and roadways, are nearly all the work of private individuals and companies." The Duke of Bridgewater's canal, connecting the [[coal|coal mines]] of Worsley to [[manchester|Manchester]] and the Mersey, demonstrated the economic logic: cheap coal transport lowered the cost of [[steam-engine|steam power]], which powered more factories, which demanded more coal, which justified more canals. This self-reinforcing cycle — Engels's centralising tendency made physical — transformed the geography of production. [[lancashire|Lancashire]], which a century earlier had been "chiefly swamp land, thinly populated," became "sown with towns and villages" and "the most densely populated strip of country in England." (P04-019, 001-004)

## Canals as a Force of Urban Centralisation

> The greater the town, the greater its advantages. It offers roads, railroads, canals; the choice of skilled labour increases constantly, new establishments can be built more cheaply because of the competition among builders and machinists who are at hand, than in remote country districts. (001-004)

Engels situates canals within the broader mechanism of industrial urbanisation. A town with canal access attracts manufacturers seeking cheap transport; manufacturers bring workers; workers create demand for trades and ser[[prostitution|vice]]s; the settlement grows; its growing population and infrastructure attract still more capital. Canals thus function as a ratchet of centralisation, each new waterway reinforcing the advantage of the towns it serves at the expense of the [[rural|countryside]]. This dynamic, Engels argues, operates with a "mad rush" that, if sustained, would eventually merge the [[urban|great town]]s into a single conurbation — "[[manchester|Manchester]] and [[liverpool|Liverpool]] would meet at [[warrington|Warrington]] or Newton." (001-004)

## Canals and the Geography of Working-Class Life

The canal system also shaped the spatial organisation of working-class existence in direct and often grim ways. In Engels's description of [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]], the town sits "on the slope of a hill at the foot of which are the canal and the river Tame." The factories are "thickly crowded" along the canal in the valley, "belching forth black smoke from their chimneys," while the workers' cottages stretch up the hillside in regimented rows. The canal functions as both an industrial artery and a barrier, separating the [[factory-system|factory]] zone from the residential streets. In [[manchester|Manchester]] itself, Engels observes that the "largest mills of [[manchester|Manchester]] lining the canals, colossal six and seven-storied buildings towering with their slender chimneys far above the low cottages of the workers." The physical relationship — monumental mill, waterway, huddled workers' housing — maps the social hierarchy onto the urban landscape. The canal brought the [[factory-system|factory]], and the [[factory-system|factory]] brought the slum. (002-034, 002-049)

## Canals, Coal, and Steam

The canal system's most consequential economic function was the movement of coal. Before canals, the cost of overland coal transport made [[steam-engine|steam power]] economically viable only at the pithead. Canals reduced the cost of coal transport by as much as eighty percent, making [[steam-engine|steam power]] accessible in towns distant from [[coal|coalfields]]. This, in turn, freed [[factory-system|factory]] location from dependence on water-power sites and enabled the concentration of industry in urban centres. Engels notes this connection implicitly: the canal age and the steam age were coeval, and each reinforced the other. The result was the explosive growth of towns like [[manchester|Manchester]], where the convergence of canal-borne coal, Irish Sea cotton imports, and steam-driven machinery created the "classic type of a modern [[urban|manufacturing town]]." (P04-019)
