## Overview

Stalybridge is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] on the River Tame at the [[lancashire|Lancashire]]–Cheshire border, directly adjacent to [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]]. During the 1840s, it was one of the newer and rapidly growing cotton-spinning centres in the [[manchester|Manchester]] industrial district. Engels groups it with [[ashton|Ashton]] in his population estimates, reflecting the practical continuity of the two towns as a single industrial conurbation along the Tame valley.

## Engels's Account

### Population and Growth

Engels records Stalybridge's population alongside [[ashton|Ashton]]:

> "Ashton and Stalybridge with 40,000" [P04-010]

The pairing of [[ashton|Ashton]] and Stalybridge in this estimate reflects their geographic contiguity — the two towns formed an essentially continuous built-up area along the Tame valley, their mills and workers' housing merging at the boundary. Stalybridge's rapid growth was typical of the newer [[urban|factory town]]s that Engels describes springing up wherever water power and later [[steam-engine|steam power]] could be harnessed.

### Commercial Infrastructure

The town appears in Engels's litany of [[manchester|Manchester]] satellite towns:

> "Middleton, Heywood, Oldham, Ashton, Stalybridge, Stockport, etc., with their innumerable 'respectable' retail dealers" [002-031]

### Industrial Character

Stalybridge was predominantly a cotton-spinning town, its mills clustered along the River Tame and the [[huddersfield|Huddersfield]] Narrow [[canal|Canal]]. Like its neighbours, it experienced the boom-and-bust cycle of the [[cotton-industry|cotton trade]], with periods of full employment alternating with crises that threw thousands out of work. The town's [[proletariat|working class]] shared the housing conditions, dietary privation, and political radicalism Engels documents across the [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] district.
