# The Philosophy of Manufactures

## Overview

Andrew [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s *The Philosophy of Manufactures* (1835) was the most influential contemporary defence of the [[factory-system|factory]] system. [[andrew-ure|Ure]], a [[glasgow|Glasgow]]-trained physician and chemist, presented [[machinery|mechanisation]] as a triumph of human ingenuity that liberated workers from drudgery and promised a new era of abundance. The book was widely cited by manufacturers as scientific justification for opposing [[factory-act|factory legislation]], and Engels treats it accordingly — not as a work of disinterested scholarship but as the intellectual arm of the bourgeoisie's economic power. (005-032)

## Key Findings

[[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s central argument was that [[machinery|mechanisation]] transformed the nature of labour. The hand-weaver was a skilled artisan who controlled his own pace of work; the [[factory-system|factory]] operative merely tended a machine that did the real work. [[andrew-ure|Ure]] presented this as emancipation: the machine freed the worker from physical toil and elevated him to the role of supervisor. The worker's only remaining duty was to "watch with attentive eye the operations of the mechanism." Engels observes that this is an accurate description of [[factory-system|factory]] labour — it is the normative gloss that makes it ideology rather than science. (006-002)

[[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s second major claim concerned the health effects of [[factory-system|factory]] work. He argued that [[child-labour|factory children]] were healthier than children employed in other trades, that the [[factory-system|factory]]'s regulated temperature and ventilation were superior to the home, and that complaints about overwork were exaggerated by sentimental reformers who had never set foot in a mill. Engels responds with a point-by-point refutation using the evidence of parliamentary commissions — the very reports [[andrew-ure|Ure]] dismissed as biased. (006-044)

## Engels's Use

Engels's treatment of [[andrew-ure|Ure]] is the most sustained polemic in the book against a single author. He quotes [[andrew-ure|Ure]] at length, often without explicit rebuttal, trusting the reader to recognise the gulf between [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s idealised [[factory-system|factory]] and the conditions Engels has been documenting for five chapters. The technique is effective: [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s lyricism about "the benignant power of steam" reads as grotesque when juxtaposed with the mortality statistics, the testimony of mutilated child workers, and the squalor of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s [[housing|slums]]. (005-032)

> Ure proves further that the inventions of machinery have rendered the workman's labour lighter. (008-019)

Engels is particularly attentive to [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s argument that [[machinery|mechanisation]] reduces the need for skill — which, per [[andrew-ure|Ure]], is a benefit because it allows women and children to replace adult men. [[andrew-ure|Ure]] frames the displacement of skilled male workers as a humanitarian advance: the family no longer depends on a single breadwinner; now the wife and children can contribute too. Engels does not dispute the fact — [[factory-system|factory]] employment of women and children increased dramatically — but presents the same fact as evidence of the destruction of the family and the degradation of labour to its lowest market price. (008-017)

> This is the law against which Dr. Ure inveighs in such unmeasured terms. (006-047)

[[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s hostility to [[factory-act|factory legislation]] is, for Engels, the clearest evidence of his bad faith. [[andrew-ure|Ure]] argued that the [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act of 1834]] was unnecessary because [[competition|market competition]] already protected workers — a claim Engels demolishes by pointing out that competition drove wages down, not up. [[andrew-ure|Ure]] argued that the Act's educational requirements were pointless because [[child-labour|factory children]] were already better educated than rural children — a claim contradicted by every parliamentary report Engels cites. The pattern is consistent: [[andrew-ure|Ure]] inverts every documented harm of the [[factory-system|factory]] system into an alleged benefit, and Engels documents each inversion. (006-047)

## Historical Significance

*The Philosophy of Manufactures* was more than a single author's opinion: it represented the consolidated ideology of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest at the moment of its political ascendance. [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s arguments — that [[machinery|mechanisation]] benefits workers, that [[factory-act|factory legislation]] is unnecessary, that competition protects the weak — became the boilerplate of bourgeois political economy for the next generation. Engels recognised that to defeat this ideology, it was not enough to document conditions in the [[factory-system|factory]] districts; one had to engage with the ideologues on their own ground, exposing their logic as apologetics dressed as science. (006-044)
