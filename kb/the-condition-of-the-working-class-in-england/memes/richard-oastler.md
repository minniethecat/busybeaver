# Richard Oastler

## Life

Richard Oastler (1789–1861) was a Tory land steward and the most prominent popular leader of the [[factory-system|factory]] reform movement in England. He served as agent on the estate of a Mr. Thornley, to whom he owed money, and was imprisoned for debt when he refused to give up his agitation against the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]]. From prison, he published his *Fleet Papers* against the [[factory-system|factory]] system and the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]. The working people paid his debt through a collection, securing his release. (006-049, 006-050)

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels presents Oastler as a figure who commanded extraordinary loyalty among the [[proletariat|working class]] despite his Tory politics. He was, together with Lord [[lord-ashley|Ashley]], one of the two Tory parliamentarians who took up the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] after [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]]'s death. Engels describes Oastler as "the particular favourite of the workingmen," noting that they called him their "good old king" and "the king of the factory children":

> "Oastler especially, who carried on a constant agitation in the factory districts, and had been active in the same way during Sadler's life, was the particular favourite of the workingmen. They called him their 'good old king,' 'the king of the factory children.'" (006-049)

His imprisonment was a political act: "The Whigs offered repeatedly to pay his debt and confer other favours upon him if he would only give up his agitation against the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]. But in vain; he remained in prison." (006-050)

Upon his release, Oastler "threw himself into the movement with all his might" for the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]]. Engels also notes that Oastler was active in the early Chartist period: "In all the meetings of that time the Tory Oastler was active, and hundreds of petitions for improvements of the social condition of the workers were circulated along with the national petition for the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]]." (008-028)

## Context

Oastler belonged to the philanthropic section of the Tory party that Engels calls "Young England" — Tories who sought a restoration of a romanticised feudal past. Engels is careful to distinguish Oastler's individual merit from his class position: his reforms, like [[lord-ashley|Ashley]]'s, were concessions extracted by working-class agitation. A workingman, Robert Pounder of [[leeds|Leeds]], wrote a letter to Oastler that Engels reproduces in full, demonstrating the personal trust the operatives placed in him. (006-008)
