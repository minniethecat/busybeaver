## Overview

Ancoats was a densely populated working-class district in east-central [[manchester|Manchester]] during the 1840s. Bounded by St. George's Road, Great Ancoats Street, and the River Medlock, it contained the largest cotton mills in [[manchester|Manchester]] and housed a substantial Irish immigrant community. Engels devoted detailed attention to Ancoats in his survey of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s working-class quarters, describing it as a district where industrial production and residential squalor stood side by side.

## Engels's Account

### Location and Layout

Engels situates Ancoats within [[manchester|Manchester]]'s industrial geography, identifying it as the workingmen's quarter east of St. George's Road:

> "The whole workingmen's district east of St. George's Road and Ancoats Street, and is the one most thickly built-up and the worst." [002-048]

The area's density was extreme by the standards of the time, with tenements packed between [[factory-system|factory]] walls and the polluted River Medlock:

> "Southward from Great Ancoats Street, lies a great, straggling, workingmen's quarter, which stretches from St. Michael's Church to Withy Grove..." [002-050]

### Industrial Character

The district was dominated by [[manchester|Manchester]]'s largest textile mills, as Engels notes:

> "In this district included under the name Ancoats, stand the largest mills of Manchester..." [002-049]

The proximity of mills to housing meant residents lived in constant noise, smoke, and industrial pollution.

### Irish Immigration and Living Conditions

Ancoats was a major centre of Irish immigration to [[manchester|Manchester]]. Engels grouped Ancoats with Irish Town and Little Ireland as the districts where Irish mill hands concentrated:

> "The mill hands in Irish Town, Ancoats, and Little Ireland, I was only able to observe..." [002-053]

The living conditions were among the worst in [[manchester|Manchester]]. Engels reports that Irish immigrants formed the lowest stratum of the [[proletariat|working class]], occupying the most dilapidated housing:

> "In the worst quarters of the Old Town, Ancoats, Little Ireland, etc., it is in the Irish neighbourhoods that one finds the most wretched conditions." [005-005]

### Social Unreliability

Engels provides a revealing detail about the social practices of non-paying debtors in Ancoats:

> "If no one trusts him in Ancoats, he moves to Chorlton or Hulme..." [002-060]

This passage illustrates the anonymity afforded by [[manchester|Manchester]]'s large and impersonal working-class districts, where individuals could evade debts simply by relocating to another quarter.
