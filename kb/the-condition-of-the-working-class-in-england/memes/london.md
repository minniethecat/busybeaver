# London

## Overview

London, the metropolis of the British Empire and commercial capital of the world, contained some two and a half million inhabitants at the time of Engels's investigation. It was a city of unprecedented scale — Engels notes that a man "may wander for hours together without reaching the beginning of the end." The colossal centralisation of population and commerce brought with it the extremes of both wealth and poverty, concentrated within a few square miles. (002-001)

As the seat of government, finance, and overseas trade, London was not primarily a [[manufacture|manufacturing]] city like [[manchester]] or [[birmingham]], yet its working-class districts were among the most extensive and miserable in the kingdom. Engels uses London as the archetype of the modern [[urban|great city]], in which the principle of social atomisation — the dissolution of mankind into isolated monads, each pursuing private interest — "is here carried out to its utmost extreme." (002-002)

## Engels's Account

Engels begins his survey of the [[urban|great town]]s with London. He describes the imposing view of the Thames from the sea, the "masses of buildings, the wharves on both sides... the countless ships along both shores," as so vast and impressive that "a man cannot collect himself, but is lost in the marvel of England's greatness." Yet he immediately adds that the sacrifices behind this grandeur become apparent only after one visits the [[housing|slums]]. (002-001)

What struck Engels most forcibly about London was not merely its poverty but the psychological condition of its inhabitants — the "brutal indifference, the unfeeling isolation of each in his private interest." He describes the crowds on the streets:

> "The hundreds of thousands of all classes and ranks crowding past each other, are they not all human beings with the same qualities and powers, and with the same interest in being happy? ... And still they crowd by one another as though they had nothing in common, nothing to do with one another, and their only agreement is the tacit one, that each keep to his own side of the pavement." (002-002)

This social atomisation, Engels argues, is the expression of the economic system of [[competition]] and the [[social-war|war of each against all]]. He concludes his opening portrait with this famous passage:

> "What is true of London, is true of Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, is true of all great towns. Everywhere barbarous indifference, hard egotism on one hand, and nameless misery on the other, everywhere social warfare, every man's house in a state of siege, everywhere reciprocal plundering under the protection of the law." (002-004)

The [[capital]]-holders, the [[bourgeoisie]], seize everything for themselves, while the poor are left with "scarcely a bare existence." (002-004)

### The Slums of London

When Engels turns to the [[housing|dwellings]] of the working-class, "London comes first." His principal example is [[st-giles|St. Giles]], "the famous rookery," situated in the midst of the most populous part of the city, surrounded by broad, splendid avenues — Oxford Street, Regent Street, Trafalgar Square, and the Strand. Of the houses in the narrow courts and alleys, he writes:

> "Scarcely a whole windowpane can be found, the walls are crumbling, doorposts and window-frames loose and broken, doors of old boards nailed together, or altogether wanting in this thieves' quarter, where no doors are needed, there being nothing to steal. Heaps of garbage and ashes lie in all directions, and the foul liquids emptied before the doors gather in stinking pools." (002-009)

He notes that the inhabitants of [[st-giles|St. Giles]] were "the poorest of the poor, the worst paid workers with thieves and the victims of prostitution indiscriminately huddled together, the majority Irish, or of Irish extraction." (002-009)

The economic exploitation embedded in these [[housing|slums]] is made explicit by Engels:

> "The abominable dwellings in Drury Lane, just mentioned, bring in the following rents: two cellar dwellings, 3s.; one room, ground-floor, 4s.; second-storey, 4s. 6d.; third-floor, 4s.; garret-room, 3s. weekly, so that the starving occupants of Charles Street alone, pay the house-owners a yearly tribute of 2,000 pounds, and the 5,336 families above mentioned in Westminster, a yearly rent of 40,000 pounds." (002-010)

[[st-giles|St. Giles]] was not the only London slum. Engels catalogues the "hundreds and thousands of alleys and courts" throughout the metropolis, noting that "close to the splendid houses of the rich such a lurking-place of the bitterest poverty may often be found." He cites statistics from the *Journal of the Statistical Society*: in the [[parish|parishes]] of St. John and St. Margaret in Westminster, 5,366 workingmen's families — 26,830 persons — lived in 5,294 [[housing|dwellings]] in 1840, of which three-fourths possessed but one room. In the aristocratic parish of St. George, Hanover Square, 1,465 workingmen's families, nearly 6,000 persons, lived under similar conditions, with more than two-thirds crowded at one family per room. (002-010)

### Bethnal Green and the East End

The most extensive working-people's district, Engels notes, lay east of the Tower in [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]] and [[bethnal-green|Bethnal Green]], where "the greatest masses of London working-people live." He quotes at length the testimony of G. Alston, preacher of St. Philip's, [[bethnal-green|Bethnal Green]]:

> "It contains 1,400 houses, inhabited by 2,795 families, or about 12,000 persons. The space upon which this large population dwells, is less than 400 yards (1,200 feet) square, and in this overcrowding it is nothing unusual to find a man, his wife, four or five children, and, sometimes, both grandparents, all in one single room, where they eat, sleep, and work. I believe that before the Bishop of London called attention to this most poverty-stricken parish, people at the West End knew as little of it as of the savages of Australia or the South Sea Isles." (002-011)

Engels adds further evidence from coroner's inquests and police reports, including the case of Ann Galway in Bermondsey Street, who died on a heap of feathers without bedstead, sheet, or coverlet, and the case of a widow in Spitalfields found with six children huddled in a back room with "no furniture but two old rush-bottomed chairs with the seats gone, a small table with two legs broken, a broken cup, and a small dish." These cases, Engels argues, illustrate the "social murder" committed daily by the capitalist system. (002-009, 002-011)

## Statistics

Engels provides several statistical observations on London:

- **Population**: approximately 2,500,000 (the largest city in the world at the time). (002-001)
- **Irish immigrant population**: 120,000 poor Irish, the largest concentration in any English city. (004-001)
- **[[parish|Parish]] [[crowding|overcrowding]]** (1840, Westminster): 5,366 workingmen's families in 5,294 [[housing|dwellings]]; three-fourths of families in one room. (002-010)
- **St. George's, Hanover Square**: 1,465 workingmen's families, nearly 6,000 persons, under similar conditions. (002-010)
- **Annual rent extracted from the poor** in Westminster: 40,000 pounds from the 5,336 families. (002-010)
