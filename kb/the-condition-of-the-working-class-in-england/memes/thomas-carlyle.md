# Thomas Carlyle

## Life

Thomas Carlyle (1795–1881) was a Scottish essayist, historian, and social critic, and one of the most influential voices of Victorian Britain. His works *The French Revolution* (1837), *[[chartism|Chartism]]* (1840), and *Past and Present* (1843) earned him a reputation as the "sage of Chelsea" — a prophet denouncing the spiritual emptiness of industrial civilisation. A Tory in origin, Carlyle called for a return to strong leadership and the "organisation of labour," a phrase Engels would appropriate and redirect. Engels describes him as "the half-German Englishman," acknowledging Carlyle's deep intellectual debts to German Romanticism and Idealism. (013-011)

## Role in Engels's Account

Carlyle is by far the most frequently cited contemporary author in Engels's text. Engels uses Carlyle for three distinct purposes: as a source of vivid descriptive detail about working-class misery, as a diagnostic voice on the moral consequences of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, and as a representative of the limits of bourgeois critique.

First, Carlyle supplies the images. His phrase "a suit of tatters" becomes Engels's shorthand for the [[poverty|destitution]] of the Irish and English working poor:

> "They wear, as Thomas Carlyle says — 'A suit of tatters, the getting on or off which is said to be a difficult operation, transacted only in festivals and the high tides of the calendar.'" (002-057)

Second, Carlyle gives Engels a language for the spiritual degradation of industrial life. In the chapter on Results, Engels quotes Carlyle at length on the cotton spinners:

> "'Their trade, now in plethoric prosperity, anon extenuated into inanition and "short time," is of the nature of gambling; they live by it like gamblers, now in luxurious superfluity, now in starvation. Black, mutinous discontent devours them; simply the miserablest feeling that can inhabit the heart of man.'" (005-026)

The passage continues with Carlyle's image of the industrial world as a "murky, simmering Tophet, of copperas fumes, cotton fuz, gin riot, wrath and toil, created by a Demon, governed by a Demon" — a rhetoric that Engels deploys to underscore his own argument that the [[factory-system|factory]] system is not merely economically inefficient but morally and spiritually destructive. (005-026)

Carlyle also supplies Engels with a diagnosis of bourgeois morality: the reduction of all human relations to "Cash Payment." Engels quotes this formulation in his chapter on the attitude of the bourgeoisie, where he argues that the [[bourgeoisie]] treats the [[proletariat]] as a commodity, governed by the laws of supply and demand. (011-002)

Third, Engels uses Carlyle as a diagnostic case. He praises Carlyle for having "sounded the social disorder more deeply than any other English bourgeois" and notes that Carlyle "demands the organisation of labour" — a phrase Engels and the [[labour-movement]] would later fill with revolutionary content. Yet Engels also observes that Carlyle's frame of reference remains firmly bourgeois: he laments the injustice but cannot see that it is inherent in the system of [[competition]] and [[property|private property]]. (013-011, 011-020)

On the Irish question, Carlyle provides Engels with a vivid but problematic description of Irish immigrants. Engels quotes Carlyle's extended passage about "the wild Milesian features" driving out the Saxon native, then adds his own qualification: "If we except his exaggerated and one-sided condemnation of the Irish national character, Carlyle is perfectly right." This moment — Engels simultaneously relying on and correcting Carlyle — captures their relationship: Carlyle is a witness whose testimony Engels trusts but whose interpretations he rejects. (004-001, 004-002)

Carlyle's *[[chartism|Chartism]]* also provides Engels with a key analytical insight: that the movement was rooted in working-class misery, not merely political agitation:

> "In 1838, Carlyle explained Chartism and the revolutionary activity of the workingmen as arising out of the misery in which they live, and only wondered that they have sat so quietly eight long years at the Barmecide feast, at which they have been regaled by the Liberal bourgeoisie with empty promises." (011-020)

## Context

Carlyle's presence throughout Engels's text reveals the intellectual context of the 1840s in which socialist and Tory critiques of industrialism often shared a common language — "the condition of England question," "the organisation of labour," the attack on "Mammonism" — before diverging sharply on solutions. Carlyle called for a return to feudal authority and hero-worship; Engels called for proletarian revolution. Engels's extensive use of Carlyle, including the *[[chartism|Chartism]]* quotations that close the book's main argument and the reference to *Past and Present* in the endnotes, demonstrates a strategy of appropriating bourgeois testimony and turning it against its source. (013-011, 011-020)
