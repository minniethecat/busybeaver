# Andrew Ure

## Life

Andrew Ure (1778–1857) was a Scottish physician and chemist who became one of the most prominent apologists for the [[factory-system|factory]] system in the 1830s. He was professor of chemistry and natural philosophy at the Andersonian Institution in [[glasgow|Glasgow]] and later worked as an analytical chemist in [[london|London]]. His book *[[philosophy-of-manufactures|The Philosophy of Manufactures]]* (1835) was a comprehensive defence of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism that argued — against the [[factory-system|factory]] reformers — that [[factory-system|factory]] labour was light, healthy, and morally improving, and that legislative interference with the labour market was both economically destructive and philosophically misguided. (005-032)

## Role in Engels's Account

> Dr. Andrew Ure betrays the other side. (005-032)

Ure appears in Engels's text as the definitive voice of bourgeois ideology in its most candid form. Where other defenders of the [[factory-system|factory]] system wrapped their arguments in humanitarian language, Ure was refreshingly — or appallingly — direct. He described the [[factory-system|factory]] as "a vast automaton, composed of various mechanical and intellectual organs, acting in uninterrupted concert for the production of a common object, all of them being subordinated to a self-regulated moving force." The worker, in this vision, was not a human being but a "mechanical organ" — a component of the machine whose value was determined entirely by his productivity.

Engels seizes on Ure's language as a confession. The bourgeoisie, he argues, regards the worker not as a person but as a factor of production. Ure's philosophy of manufactures is the theoretical expression of this practical reality: the worker is reduced to an appendage of the machine, and his claims to health, education, leisure, or dignity are irrelevant to the calculus of profit. That Ure states this openly — rather than concealing it behind religious or humanitarian rhetoric — makes him, in Engels's ironic treatment, a more honest spokesman for capitalism than the parliamentary reformers who voted against the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] while professing concern for the [[proletariat|working class]].

The specific context of Engels's citation of Ure is the discussion of the intellectual and moral condition of the [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels is arguing that the [[factory-system|factory]] system not only destroys workers' bodies but degrades their minds — by reducing them to machine-tenders, by denying them education, and by exhausting them to the point where intellectual development is impossible. Ure's description of the worker as a "mechanical organ" perfectly captures, in Engels's view, the bourgeoisie's actual attitude toward the [[proletariat|working class]], stripped of sentimental disguise. (005-032)

## Context

Ure's *Philosophy of [[manufacture|Manufacture]]s* was published in 1835, at the height of the debate over the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833. The Act had limited the [[working-day|working hours]] of children and established a [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate — reforms that the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest had fiercely resisted. Ure's book was a contribution to this debate, arguing that [[factory-system|factory]] conditions were already adequate and that further regulation would cripple British industry. His celebration of the "gigantic automaton" was intended as a defence of the [[factory-system|factory]] system; Engels repurposes it as an indictment.

Ure also represents a broader phenomenon that Engels documents throughout the book: the role of intellectuals — scientists, economists, clergymen — in providing ideological cover for [[exploitation|capitalist exploitation]]. Just as [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]] provided the pseudo-scientific justification for the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]], Ure provided the pseudo-scientific justification for the [[factory-system|factory]] system. The bourgeoisie, Engels argues, could not maintain its rule by force alone; it required a class of intellectual retainers who could make exploitation appear natural, necessary, and even benevolent.
