# James Watt

## Life

James Watt (1736–1819) was a Scottish engineer, instrument maker, and inventor. While employed as a mathematical instrument maker at the University of [[glasgow|Glasgow]], Watt was asked to repair a model of Thomas Newcomen's atmospheric [[machinery|steam-engine]]. Recognising its inefficiency — the cylinder had to be alternately heated and cooled with every stroke — Watt devised the separate condenser in 1765, a breakthrough that transformed the [[machinery|steam-engine]] from a crude pump into a universal source of industrial motive power. He patented the invention in 1769 and, in partnership with the manufacturer Matthew Boulton, produced [[machinery|steam-engine]]s that powered the factories, [[mining|mines]], and mills of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

Watt's engine was not the first [[machinery|steam-engine]], but it was the first that could be applied economically across the full range of [[manufacture|manufacturing]] processes. By the time of his death in 1819, [[steam-engine|steam power]] had become the defining technology of British industry, and Watt's name had become synonymous with the age of machinery.

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels accords Watt's [[machinery|steam-engine]] primacy in the sequence of inventions that created the modern [[proletariat]]. The opening sentence of his historical preface declares:

> "The history of the proletariat in England begins with the second half of the last century, with the invention of the steam-engine and of machinery for working cotton." (P04-001)

Later in the same preface, Engels narrates the convergence of mechanical inventions that established the [[factory-system|factory]] system. After describing [[richard-arkwright|Arkwright's]] water frame and [[samuel-crompton|Crompton's]] mule, he adds:

> "And all this machinery was made doubly important by James Watt's steam-engine, invented in 1764, and used for supplying motive power for spinning since 1785." (P04-008)

The phrase "made doubly important" captures Engels's understanding of the [[machinery|steam-engine]]'s role. The spinning [[machinery|machines]] created the technical possibility of [[factory-system|factory production]]; Watt's engine provided the power that made that possibility economically dominant. Without steam, the water frame and mule would have remained limited to sites with suitable waterpower. With steam, factories could be built anywhere — and were, concentrating workers in the new industrial towns that became the terrain of Engels's investigation.

The [[machinery|steam-engine]] also drove the expansion of [[coal|coal mining]], iron production, and machinery manufacture — the whole interconnected complex of heavy industry that multiplied the proletariat and intensified its exploitation:

> "The steam-engine first gave importance to the broad coalfields of England; the production of machinery began now for the first time, and with it arose a new interest in the iron mines which supplied raw material for it." (P04-015)

## Context

Watt's invention was, in Engels's analysis, a world-historical event — but not in the sense of heroic individual genius. Engels's treatment is characteristically structural: Watt appears not as a Promethean creator but as an agent of the technological transformation that reconfigured class relations. The [[machinery|steam-engine]] created the conditions for the [[factory-system|factory]] system; the [[factory-system|factory]] system created the proletariat as a mass class; the proletariat, in Engels's dialectical scheme, would become the gravedigger of the capitalist order that created it.

Engels's dating of the proletariat's origin to the [[machinery|steam-engine]]'s invention is a deliberate theoretical claim, rejecting both longer genealogies (ancient slaves, medieval serfs) and shorter ones ([[chartism|Chartism]], trade unions). The proletariat is a specifically modern class, brought into being by a specific technology at a specific historical moment. Understanding that technology is therefore essential to understanding the class itself.

## Legacy

James Watt's [[machinery|steam-engine]] is universally recognised as one of the pivotal inventions of the modern era. The unit of power, the watt, is named in his honour. For the [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist tradition, Watt holds a more specific significance: his engine, applied to production, created the material basis for the modern [[proletariat|working class]] and, with it, the possibility of its self-emancipation.
