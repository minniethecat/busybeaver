# Steam Engine

## Definition

The steam engine, in Engels's historical narrative, is the technological pivot around which the entire industrial revolution turns. "The history of the proletariat in England," he declares, "begins with the second half of the last century, with the invention of the [[machinery|steam-engine]] and of machinery for working cotton." James Watt's engine, patented in 1769 and first applied to [[spinning|cotton spinning]] in 1785, supplied what Engels calls the third of the "three great levers with which manufacture, since the middle of the last century, has been busy putting the world out of joint" — the first two being the division of labour and the application of water-power. The steam engine was qualitatively different from its predecessors: it liberated industry from geographical dependence on rivers, made possible the concentration of factories in urban centres, and generated a cascade of derived demand — for coal, for iron, for machinery, for transport — that transformed every sector of the economy. (P04-001, 001-003)

## Steam as the Decisive Breakthrough

> The history of the proletariat in England begins with the second half of the last century, with the invention of the steam-engine and of machinery for working cotton. These inventions gave rise, as is well known, to an industrial revolution, a revolution which altered the whole civil society. (P04-001)

Engels's account of steam technology emphasises its compound effect. The steam engine did not merely replace water-power; it transformed the economics of industrial location, scale, and organisation. Before steam, factories were tied to rivers with sufficient fall to drive water-wheels; the [[factory-system|factory]] village was necessarily a rural settlement, and the mill-owner exercised a quasi-feudal authority over his hands. After steam, factories could concentrate in towns, where labour was abundant, transport cheap, and markets close. The scale of production increased dramatically: steam engines of thirty, forty, a hundred horsepower became common, driving hundreds of [[machinery|machines]] in a single building. The social relations of production were transformed in parallel: the rural mill-owner became the urban manufacturer, the handicraftsman became the [[factory-system|factory]] operative, and the "patriarchal relation between workingmen and employers" was replaced by the "bond of pecuniary profit." (P04-001, P04-015)

## Steam and the Self-Reinforcing Cycle of Industrial Expansion

> The steam-engine first gave importance to the broad coalfields of England; the production of machinery began now for the first time, and with it arose a new interest in the iron mines which supplied raw material for it. (P04-015)

Engels traces a cascading sequence of industrial transformations set in motion by steam. The engine created demand for coal, which drove the expansion of mining, which generated demand for steam-powered pumping engines, which consumed more coal. The engine required iron for its construction, which stimulated iron-smelting, which was transformed by the application of coke and the puddling process, which made iron cheap enough to use for machinery, bridges, rails, and buildings — all of which required more steam power for their production. The steam engine also transformed transport: "as it had transformed the means of communication by land, so did the introduction of steam revolutionise travel by sea," with the first steamboat launched in 1807 and over five hundred steam vessels plying to and from British ports by 1836. Engels presents this as a single, interconnected system: "the impulse, once given, was communicated to all branches of industrial activity." (P04-015)

## The Spinning Throstle and the Factory System

> After the steam-engine, this is the most important mechanical invention of the 18th century. It was calculated from the beginning for mechanical motive power, and was based upon wholly new principles. (P04-008)

Arkwright's spinning throstle (1767) occupies a special place in Engels's technological genealogy. Unlike the jenny, which had been designed for hand operation, the throstle was "calculated from the beginning for mechanical motive power" — it presupposed the steam engine and made the [[factory-system|factory]] system structurally necessary. The combination of the steam engine, the throstle, Crompton's mule (1785), and Cartwright's power-loom (1785–1804) completed the [[machinery|mechanisation]] of cotton textile production. Engels's point is that no single invention was sufficient; it was the *system* of mutually reinforcing technologies — steam providing power, the spinning [[machinery|machines]] providing speed, the power-loom providing demand for yarn — that constituted the industrial revolution as a qualitative break. This is why Engels insists that "England is the classic soil of this transformation": only in England did all the elements converge simultaneously. (P04-008, P04-001)

## Steam, Gender, and the Degradation of Labour

The application of steam power to textile production had a direct and transformative effect on the composition of the labour force. Engels observes that "the more the use of the arms, the expenditure of strength, can be transferred to steam or waterpower, the fewer men need be employed; and as women and children work more cheaply, and in these branches better than men, they take their places." The steam-driven machine eliminated the need for muscular strength, and the capitalist, paying wages rather than piece-rates, selected the cheapest hands available. The result was the wholesale displacement of adult male workers by women and children, the destruction of the family economy, and the creation of what Engels calls "a physically degenerate race, robbed of all humanity." The steam engine, in freeing production from the limits of human strength, degraded the human being who attended it. (006-006)

## Steam and Urban Concentration

The steam engine was also the proximate cause of the urban concentration Engels documents throughout the book. Water-powered mills were dispersed along rivers; steam-powered mills congregated in towns where coal was cheap and labour abundant. This concentration produced the "[[urban|great town]]s" — [[manchester|Manchester]], [[leeds|Leeds]], [[birmingham|Birmingham]], [[glasgow|Glasgow]] — whose working-class quarters Engels describes in such relentless detail. The physical form of these towns — massive mills lining [[canal|canals]] and [[railway|railways]], surrounded by dense belts of workers' housing — was a direct expression of steam's spatial logic. Engels captures this in his description of [[ancoats|Ancoats]], where "the largest mills of [[manchester|Manchester]] lining the [[canal|canals]], colossal six and seven-storied buildings towering with their slender chimneys far above the low cottages of the workers." The steam engine, by concentrating production, concentrated poverty, and by concentrating poverty, made it visible — and politically combustible. (002-049, 001-004)

## Historical Context: Watt and the Scottish Enlightenment

Engels's treatment of the steam engine's history reflects the broader intellectual context of nineteenth-century industrial historiography. [[james-watt|James Watt]] (1736–1819), a [[glasgow|Glasgow]] instrument-maker, did not invent the steam engine — Newcomen's atmospheric engine had been pumping water from [[mining|mines]] since 1712 — but his separate condenser (patented 1769) and his invention of the sun-and-planet gear for converting reciprocating to rotary motion made the steam engine a universal prime mover. Engels, writing for a German audience in 1845, takes the basic narrative as established knowledge and focuses on its social consequences. The technical details interest him only insofar as they explain the "gigantic advance achieved in [[manufacture|English manufacture]] since 1760" — an advance whose "mightiest result" was "the English proletariat." (P04-001, P04-015)
