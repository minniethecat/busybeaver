# Machinery

## Definition

Machinery, in Engels's usage, refers to the [[steam-engine|steam-power]]ed and water-powered machines that transformed English [[manufacture|manufacturing]] between 1760 and 1840: the spinning-jenny, the water-frame, the mule, the power-loom, and the steam-engine itself. Machinery is not merely a technical phenomenon in Engels's analysis but a social force — it is the agent that dissolves the old order of independent craftsmen, creates the modern proletariat, and concentrates both capital and population in the [[urban|factory town]]s. (P04-001)

## Engels's Analysis

> Before the introduction of machinery, the spinning and weaving of raw materials was carried on in the home of the workman. (P04-003)

The pre-industrial system of domestic manufacture, Engels explains, was rural, dispersed, and relatively humane. The weaver owned his loom, worked at his own pace, cultivated a small plot of land, and retained a measure of independence. He was not rich, but he was not a proletarian — he owned his [[capital|means of production]] and had some control over his working life. Machinery destroyed this world. (P04-003)

The first machine to disrupt the old order was the spinning-jenny, invented by James Hargreaves in 1764. It allowed a single worker to spin multiple threads simultaneously, dramatically increasing yarn output. The jenny was followed by Arkwright's water-frame (1769), which produced stronger thread suitable for warp, and then by Crompton's mule (1785), which combined the advantages of both. Each invention created a crisis for the hand-spinners who could not compete with machine production. (P04-005, P04-014)

> These inventions gave rise, as is well known, to an industrial revolution, a revolution which altered the whole civil society. (P04-001)

Engels emphasises that machinery did not simply increase productivity — it reorganised society. The introduction of the power-loom drove hand-weavers to starvation while creating enormous wealth for the mill-owners who could afford the new technology. Machinery simultaneously created the industrial proletariat and the industrial bourgeoisie: the worker who tended the machine and the capitalist who owned it. This was not a gradual evolution but a revolution, as Engels's choice of language makes clear. (P04-001, P04-009)

The steam-engine was the final, decisive innovation. While the earliest mills depended on water-power and were therefore confined to river valleys, the steam-engine freed industry from geography. Factories could now be built wherever coal was cheap and labour was plentiful — which is to say, in the towns. The concentration of industry in urban centres accelerated the growth of [[manchester|Manchester]], [[leeds|Leeds]], [[bradford|Bradford]], and [[birmingham|Birmingham]], creating the "[[urban|great town]]s" whose conditions Engels documents in harrowing detail. (P04-008, P04-015)

> Hand-workers have been driven by machinery from one position after another. (P04-009)

The displacement of hand-workers was not a one-time event but an ongoing process. Each new machine rendered a new category of skilled workers redundant. The hand-spinner was eliminated first, then the hand-weaver, then the cloth-dresser and the dyer. Those who could not adapt to [[factory-system|factory]] labour were reduced to [[poverty|pauperism]] or forced into the army of the unemployed — the "surplus population" that Engels identifies as a permanent feature of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. (P04-009)

## The Deskilling of Labour

> The industrial revolution has simply carried this out to its logical end by making the workers machines pure and simple, taking from them the last trace of independent activity, and so forcing them to think and demand a position worthy of men. (P04-004)

### The Machine as Master

Where the pre-industrial craftsman controlled both the pace and the product of his work, the [[factory-system|factory]] operative became a mere attendant upon machinery. Engels charts a steady degradation: the hand-spinner who once understood every stage of production was reduced to a machine-minder who watched for broken threads. The power-loom weaver who had been a skilled artisan became a [[child-labour|child]]-tender, overseeing looms that did all the meaningful work. Machinery, Engels argues, inverted the natural relationship between tool and user — the machine was no longer the worker's instrument but his master, dictating the rhythm of the working day and reducing human labour to the monotonous repetition of a few simple motions. (P04-004, P04-009)

> Every improvement in machinery throws workers out of employment, and the greater the advance, the more numerous the unemployed; each great improvement produces, therefore, upon a number of workers the effect of a commercial crisis, creates want, wretchedness, and crime. (P04-019)

Engels was particularly struck by the paradox of mechanical progress: inventions that ought to have lightened human toil instead intensified misery. The self-acting mule, introduced in the 1830s, could be tended by a child where formerly a skilled adult spinner had been required. Wages collapsed, adult men were driven from the mills, and the family itself was turned upside down — the wife and children went to the factory while the unemployed husband stayed home. Machinery, far from liberating humanity from drudgery, had become an instrument of immiseration because it was owned by one class and worked by another. The gains of productivity flowed to the capitalist, while the costs of displacement fell wholly upon the worker. (P04-019)
