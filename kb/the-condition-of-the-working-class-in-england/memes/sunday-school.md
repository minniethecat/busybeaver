## Definition

Sunday schools were the primary form of educational provision for working-class children in industrial England, operating on Sundays — the only day when [[child-labour|factory children]] were not at work. Founded by religious charities and churches, they offered basic instruction in reading and in Christian doctrine. Engels's assessment is devastating: the Sunday school system was not an educational institution in any meaningful sense but a mechanism for inculcating bourgeois morality and religious submission while leaving the [[proletariat|working class]] functionally illiterate.

## Engels's Analysis

### The Failure to Educate

Engels presents statistical evidence that Sunday schools failed even their minimal objective of teaching children to read. He cites figures showing "of those who are in a position to attend school, scarcely 6,500 can read" (007-013), a devastating indictment of an institution that had been operating for decades. The children's own testimony, which Engels reproduces, is more vivid: working children tell investigators they "don't go to school, haven't got no clothes" and cannot read (007-015). The Sunday school's reach was exaggerated in bourgeois propaganda and minimal in practice.

### The Factory Connection

The Sunday school was inextricably linked to the [[factory-system|factory]] system. Engels notes one survey found "of 17 years of age, from one Sunday school, of whom 26 employed in the [[factory-system|factory]]" (006-029), showing that Sunday school attendance and [[factory-system|factory]] employment were co-extensive — the children worked six days and were sent to Sunday school for a few hours on the seventh. The [[factory-act|Factory Act]]'s education clauses mandated that child [[factory-system|factory]] workers receive some [[education|schooling]], and the Sunday school was the manufacturers' solution: a form of education that cost them nothing, occupied the children's single day of rest, and taught docility rather than critical thought.

### Religious Indoctrination as Social Control

Engels identifies the ideological function of Sunday schools: "Under the mask of philanthropy, the manufacturers introduced the Sunday school system" (006-044). The schools taught that poverty was ordained by God, that obedience to masters was a Christian duty, and that the sufferings of this world would be rewarded in the next. This was, in Engels's view, a deliberate strategy to produce compliant workers who would not combine in trade unions or embrace [[chartism|Chartism]]. The religious content was not incidental to the Sunday school's purpose but essential to it — the bourgeoisie understood that literacy without ideological control was politically dangerous.

### The Physical Exhaustion Barrier

The irony Engels exposes is that even if Sunday schools had been effective, their pupils were too physically exhausted to learn. Children who had worked twelve or fourteen hours in the [[factory-system|factory]] spent "Sunday in bed to recover in some degree from the exhaustion of the week" (009-007). Those who did attend were "half-asleep, stupid from exhaustion, and incapable of following the simplest instruction" (009-010). The Sunday school's failure was thus structural: the [[factory-system|factory]] system that made Sunday the only available day for education simultaneously made children physically incapable of benefiting from it.

### Comparison with Day Schools

Engels contrasts Sunday schools unfavourably with even the inadequate day schools: "The few day schools at the command of the working-class are wholly insufficient" (005-020). Day schools were "not within their reach, the Sunday schools are useless" (009-010). The point is not that Sunday schools were worse than day schools but that both were systematically inaccessible to working-class children. The education clauses of [[factory-act|factory legislation]] that Engels discusses were, in practice, a dead letter: "the [[housing|dwellings]] are bad and the children have no clothes to attend Sunday school" (007-010).
