# Aristocracy

## Definition

The term "aristocracy" carries two distinct meanings in Engels's text. Primarily, it denotes the landed aristocracy — the hereditary nobility whose wealth and political power rest on land ownership rather than [[capital|industrial capital]]. Engels also extends the term to designate a privileged stratum within the [[proletariat|working class]] itself: the "working-class aristocracy" of skilled, unionised labourers who, during the period of England's industrial monopoly after 1848, secured a relatively comfortable position at the expense of the unorganised mass of workers. (P01-010, P01-012)

## The Landed Aristocracy

### Political Position

The landed aristocracy appears in Engels's analysis as a declining class whose interests are increasingly subordinated to those of the industrial bourgeoisie. In the preface to the 1887 American edition, Engels summarises the political trajectory: the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill of 1832]] was "the victory of the whole [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] over the landed aristocracy," and the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 was the conclusive victory of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] capitalist over the landed interest. (P01-010)

> The Reform Bill of 1831 had been the victory of the whole capitalist class over the landed aristocracy. The repeal of the Corn Laws was the victory of the manufacturing capitalist not only over the landed aristocracy, but over those sections of capitalists, too, whose interests were more or less bound up with the landed interest. (P01-010)

Despite this political decline, the landed aristocracy retained formal privileges well into the nineteenth century. The House of Lords and the monarchy — "the so-called monarchical and aristocratic elements of the Constitution" — survived, Engels argues, only because the bourgeoisie found it convenient to maintain their sham existence. The aristocracy's remaining political power was a hollow form, sustained by bourgeois self-interest rather than genuine authority. (008-025)

> The so-called monarchical and aristocratic elements of the Constitution can maintain themselves only because the bourgeoisie has an interest in the continuance of their sham existence; and more than a sham existence neither possesses today. (008-025)

### The Game Laws

One of the landed aristocracy's most conspicuous legal privileges was the [[game-laws|Game Laws]], which Engels treats as emblematic of aristocratic indifference to working-class life. These laws protected game birds and hares on aristocratic estates with extraordinary severity: a poacher caught taking game to feed his starving family could be imprisoned, and a second offence carried a minimum sentence of seven years' transportation. Engels notes that these laws produced frequent bloody conflicts between poachers and gamekeepers, and that two gamekeepers had shot themselves the previous year rather than continue the work. (010-005)

> Such is the moderate price at which the landed aristocracy purchases the noble sport of shooting; but what does it matter to the lords of the soil? Whether one or two more or less of the "surplus" live or die matters nothing, and even if in consequence of the Game Laws half the surplus population could be put out of the way, it would be all the better for the other half — according to the philanthropy of the English landlords. (010-005)

### The Corn Laws as Aristocratic Mechanism

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were the economic instrument through which the landed aristocracy extracted rent from the entire population, and especially from the [[proletariat|working class]]. By imposing heavy tariffs on imported grain, the laws kept bread prices artificially high, transferring income from every bread-buying household to the landowning class. Engels frames the 1846 repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] as the decisive moment when the industrial bourgeoisie defeated the landed aristocracy for control of state policy, aligning the nation's commercial and financial regime with the interests of [[manufacture|manufacturing]] capital. (P01-010, 008-029)

The [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], the bourgeois organisation that agitated for repeal, was prepared to use the [[proletariat|working class]] as a battering ram against the aristocracy. During the crisis of 1842, the League's leaders adopted revolutionary language and attempted to instrumentalise working-class desperation:

> The workingmen were to take the chestnuts from the fire to save the bourgeoisie from burning their own fingers. The old idea of a "holy month," a general strike, broached in 1839 by the Chartists, was revived. This time, however, it was not the workingmen who wished to quit work, but the manufacturers who wished to close their mills and send the operatives into the country parishes upon the property of the aristocracy, thus forcing the Tory Parliament and the Tory Ministry to repeal the Corn Laws. (008-029)

The Tory party and the old unreformed parliament functioned as the political arm of the landed aristocracy. Even the right of free association — the legal basis for trade unionism — was granted not by the reformed, bourgeois parliament but by "the old, unreformed, oligarchic-Tory parliament" in 1824. Engels observes that this law "never could have passed the House of Commons later, when the Reform Bill had legally sanctioned the distinction between bourgeoisie and proletariat." The right had previously been "restricted to the aristocracy and bourgeoisie." (008-008)

> The workingmen obtained a right previously restricted to the aristocracy and bourgeoisie, the right of free association. (008-008)

### Aristocracy as Bourgeoisie

In his definitive theoretical statement at the start of Chapter 11, Engels collapses the distinction between aristocracy and bourgeoisie from the proletariat's standpoint. For the [[proletariat|working class]], property ownership is the only meaningful social distinction:

> In speaking of the bourgeoisie I include the so-called aristocracy, for this is a privileged class, an aristocracy, only in contrast with the bourgeoisie, not in contrast with the proletariat. The proletarian sees in both only the property-holder — i.e., the bourgeois. Before the privilege of property all other privileges vanish. (011-001)

This passage encapsulates Engels's materialist reduction of all pre-capitalist social distinctions to the single cleavage of property. The aristocracy's titles, lineage, and formal precedence are mere decoration over the hard fact of ownership. The only practical difference Engels concedes is that the bourgeois proper stands in direct relation to the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] proletariat, while the aristocrat encounters chiefly the [[farming|agricultural labour]]er. (011-001)

## The Working-Class Aristocracy

In his 1885 retrospective published in the *Commonweal* and appended to the 1887 edition, Engels introduces a second concept of aristocracy: the "aristocracy among the working-class." During the period of England's industrial monopoly (roughly 1848–1870), a minority of skilled workers — [[factory-system|factory]] hands protected by the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]], and members of the great trade unions of engineers, carpenters, and [[brick|bricklayers]] — secured permanently improved conditions. These workers "succeeded in enforcing for themselves a relatively comfortable position, and they accept it as final." Engels describes them as "the model workingmen of Messrs. Leone Levi & Giffen, and they are very nice people indeed nowadays to deal with, for any sensible capitalist in particular and for the whole [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] in general." (P01-010)

This labour aristocracy had shared in the benefits of England's monopoly position in world markets. But Engels warns that with the breakdown of that monopoly — brought about by the industrialisation of Germany, America, and other competitors — the privileged position of even this protected minority would vanish, and "there will be [[socialism|Socialism]] again in England." (P01-010)

In the 1892 preface to the English edition, Engels notes the contrast between the old unionism of this labour aristocracy and the "New Unionism" of the unskilled masses:

> The old Unions preserve the traditions of the time when they were founded, and look upon the wages system as a once for all established, final fact... The new Unions were founded at a time when the faith in the eternity of the wages system was severely shaken; their founders and promoters were Socialists either consciously or by feeling; the masses, whose adhesion gave them strength, were rough, neglected, looked down upon by the working-class aristocracy. (P01-012)

This internal stratification of the [[proletariat|working class]] — into a privileged labour aristocracy and an impoverished mass — became a central theme of later [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist analysis, most notably in Lenin's theory of imperialism, which traced the roots of reformism to the bribery of a labour aristocracy through superprofits extracted from colonies.
