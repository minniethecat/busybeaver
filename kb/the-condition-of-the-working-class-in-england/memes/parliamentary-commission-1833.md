# Parliamentary Factory Commission of 1833

## Overview

The Parliamentary Factory Commission of 1833 — formally the Royal Commission of Inquiry into the Employment of Children in Factories — was appointed by the Whig government to investigate the conditions of labour in textile mills and to make recommendations for legislation. Its report, published in multiple volumes during 1833 and 1834, collected an unprecedented body of testimony from manufacturers, overseers, workers, medical practitioners, and children themselves. Engels relies heavily on this report throughout *The Condition of the Working Class in England*, treating it as the most authoritative documentary source on the horrors of the [[factory-system|factory]] system. (006-015, 006-016)

The Commission was appointed against the background of the Ten Hours Movement and the agitation led by [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]] and [[Richard Oastler]]. Its creation was, in part, a political manoeuvre by the Whig government to preempt more radical legislation. Yet the evidence it gathered — on the employment of children as young as five, on working days of fourteen to sixteen hours, on the systematic physical abuse of child workers, and on the [[prostitution|sexual exploitation]] of female operatives — proved so damning that Engels presents it as the bourgeoisie's self-incrimination. The report of the Central Commission, Engels writes, "shall convict them" — the manufacturers — "in their own admitted testimony." (006-016)

## Engels's Account

Engels structures much of his chapter on the [[factory-system|factory]] system around the Commission's findings. He describes the commission's evidence on the age of child workers: manufacturers "began to employ children rarely of five years, often of six, very often of seven, usually of eight to nine years." (006-016) The working day "often lasted fourteen to sixteen hours, exclusive of meals and intervals." (006-016) The manufacturers "permitted overlookers to flog and maltreat children, and often took an active part in so doing themselves." (006-016) Engels cites the case of a Scottish manufacturer who "rode after a sixteen years old runaway, forced him to return running after the employer as fast as the master's horse trotted, and beat him the whole way with a long whip." (006-016)

Beyond the physical brutality, Engels uses the Commission's evidence to document what he calls the moral degradation of the [[factory-system|factory]] system — a degradation that he treats not as a failure of individual morality but as a structural consequence of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. The Commission's reports on the [[prostitution|sexual exploitation]] of female operatives are presented without mitigation. "It is not unusual for manufacturers to take liberties with the female operatives, especially when the latter are dependent upon them for employment," and the "Factories' Inquiry Commission's Report gives dozens of examples of this." (006-011) Commissioner Cowell "expresses it as his opinion, that the morality of the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives is somewhat below the average of that of the working-class in general." (006-012) Engels cites Commissioner Hawkins, who attributed the early and reckless marriages of [[factory-system|factory]] girls to their complete lack of preparation for domestic life — "they have neither means, time, nor opportunity to learn the ordinary duties of household life." (006-011)

The physical consequences of [[factory-system|factory]] labour constituted another central theme of the Commission's report, and Engels quotes the evidence extensively. "The reports of the Commission touching this barbarism surpass all that is known to me of the most repulsive kind," he writes. "The Commissioners mention a crowd of cripples who appeared before them, and whose injuries clearly proceeded from long working-hours." (006-017) The deformities included "distortion of the spine, legs, and arms; relaxed and contracted muscles; injuries to the joints, especially the hip and knee joints; varicose veins, ulcers of the legs," and chronic diseases of the digestive system. (006-017) The [[lancashire|Lancashire]] Commissioners — Cowell, Tufnell, and Hawkins — added extensive testimony on the premature ageing of [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, the prevalence of ruptures, and the high rates of infant mortality among the children of [[factory-system|factory]] women.

Engels draws particular attention to the evidence of Commissioner Mackintosh, who reported in 1833 that he "found it difficult to believe the statements of the older hands as to their ages; they age so very early." (006-031) The manufacturer Ashworth admitted "that, towards the fortieth year, the spinners can no longer prepare the required quantity of yarn, and are therefore 'sometimes' discharged," referring to forty-year-old operatives as "old people." (006-031)

The Commission's findings on the educational and moral condition of working children provided Engels with further ammunition. Commissioner Grainger, reporting on children in [[Birmingham]], stated that they were "utterly [[poverty|want]]ing in all that could be in the remotest degree called a useful education." Commissioner Horne found a girl of eleven in [[Wolverhampton]] who had attended both day and Sunday school but "had never heard of another world, of Heaven, or another life." (005-020) Commissioner Horne's report on [[willenhall|Willenhall]] was even more damning: "there exists absolutely no moral sense among the workers." (007-010)

The Commission was not impartial. Engels is aware that its commissioners included figures sympathetic to the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest — "a man after the heart of the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], [[andrew-ure|Dr. Andrew Ure]]." (005-032) Yet the evidence they collected, Engels argues, spoke for itself. The very fact that a parliamentary inquiry — conducted by men who were not hostile to capitalism — could produce such findings was proof of the irredeemable character of the [[factory-system|factory]] system. Engels's rhetorical strategy throughout is to let the bourgeoisie's own evidence speak: "Their own admitted testimony shall convict them."

## Context

The Commission was appointed against the background of a decade of agitation. The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1819, which had prohibited the employment of children under nine in cotton mills, was widely evaded. The 1831 [[factory-act|Factory Act]], promoted by Sir John Hobhouse, had extended some protections but was limited to cotton mills and weakly enforced. The Ten Hours Movement, led by [[Richard Oastler]] and supported by the philanthropic wing of the Tory Party under [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]], demanded a ten-hour maximum working day for all persons under eighteen. The Whig government, reluctant to legislate, appointed the Commission in April 1833 as a delaying tactic. The Commission was chaired by Thomas Tooke, the economist and free-trader, and included Edwin Chadwick, the future architect of the [[New Poor Law 1834]], and [[southwood-smith|Dr. Thomas Southwood Smith]], a [[public-health|sanitary reform]]er. Its district commissioners — who conducted the actual investigations — included figures who would become associated with the statistical and [[public-health|sanitary reform]] movements.

The Commission's report was published in the summer of 1833 and led directly to the [[Factory Act 1833]], which prohibited the employment of children under nine in textile mills (except silk), limited children aged nine to thirteen to nine hours of work per day, and limited young persons aged thirteen to eighteen to twelve hours. The Act also established the first [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate. Engels analyses the [[factory-act-1833|1833 Factory Act]] as inadequate — a compromise that left the fundamental exploitation of the [[factory-system|factory]] system intact — but he acknowledges that the Commission's report provided an evidentiary foundation that could not be ignored.

## Aftermath

The Commission's report became, in Engels's hands, a foundational document of the socialist critique of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. Its evidence was cited by generations of labour reformers and socialists. The Commission also established the model of the parliamentary inquiry as a tool of social investigation — a model that would be replicated in the [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] of 1842 (whose report Engels also cites on the conditions in [[mining|mines]]) and the Health of Towns Commission of 1844. Engels's method of relying on official, bourgeois sources to indict the bourgeoisie became a characteristic feature of [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist social criticism.
