# Karl Marx

## Life

Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, political economist, historian, and revolutionary socialist. Born in Trier in the Prussian Rhineland, he studied law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin, where he became associated with the Young Hegelians. After a brief career in radical journalism — editing the *Rheinische Zeitung* until its suppression in 1843 — he moved to Paris, where in 1844 he began his lifelong intellectual and political partnership with Friedrich Engels. Together they wrote *The Holy Family* (1845) and *The German Ideology* (1845–46), works in which they developed the materialist conception of history. Their most famous collaborative text, *The Communist Manifesto*, appeared in 1848. Marx spent the bulk of his mature life in [[london|London]], where he produced the *Critique of Political Economy* (1859) and the first volume of *Das Kapital* (1867), the latter being the systematic exposition of his critique of [[capital]] and the theory of [[surplus-value]]. He died in [[london|London]] in 1883.

Modern scientific socialism, Engels asserts in his 1892 preface, was "chiefly and almost exclusively" Marx's achievement. The depth and influence of Marx's theoretical work dwarfed Engels's own early contributions — and Engels, with characteristic personal modesty, was the first to insist on this point. (P01-007, P01-008)

## Role in Engels's Account

Unlike the other figures in Engels's book — surgeons, [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors, MPs, manufacturers — Marx does not appear as a character within the 1845 text of *The Condition of the Working Class in England*. He belonged to the book's future, not its subject matter. His presence is felt solely in the prefaces and postscripts Engels added to later editions.

In the 1892 English edition preface, Engels references Marx three times, each reference marking a stage in the relationship between his own early work and the mature theory Marx developed.

First, Engels explains why he did not update the book to cover the period from 1844 to 1892:

> "the first volume of Das Kapital, by Karl Marx, an English translation of which is before the public, contains a very ample description of the state of the British working-class, as it was about 1865, that is to say, at the time when British industrial prosperity reached its culminating point. I should, then, have been obliged again to go over the ground already covered by Marx's celebrated work." (P01-007)

This is a remarkable acknowledgment. Engels, now the elder statesman of European socialism, publicly defers to Marx's work as having rendered his own youthful investigation obsolete — at least for the period after 1844. Das Kapital is the canonical text; Engels's book is its precursor. (P01-007)

Second, Engels describes the theoretical gulf between his 1845 self and the present:

> "Modern international Socialism, since fully developed as a science, chiefly and almost exclusively through the efforts of Marx, did not as yet exist in 1844. My book represents one of the phases of its embryonic development..." (P01-008)

The biological metaphor — embryonic development, fish-ancestors, gill-arches — is Engels's way of acknowledging that his book belonged to the pre-history of scientific socialism. He could see, in 1892, the philosophical limitations of his 24-year-old self: the residual Hegelian idealism, the talk of universal human emancipation that Marx's class analysis rendered obsolete. (P01-008)

Third, and most memorably, Engels quotes one of Marx's characteristic aphorisms to describe the ironic outcomes of the 1848 revolutions:

> "The very people who put it down have become, as Karl Marx used to say, its testamentary executors. Louis Napoleon had to create an independent and united Italy, Bismarck had to revolutionise Germany and to restore Hungarian independence, and the English manufacturers had to enact the People's Charter." (P01-010)

This is Marx as Engels [[poverty|want]]ed to remember him: the master of ironic historical paradox, the thinker who saw that the bourgeoisie would be forced, by the logic of its own development, to implement the political programme of its gravediggers. The aphorism captures in miniature the dialectical method that Marx and Engels developed together. (P01-010)

## Context

Engels's references to Marx in the 1892 preface serve a dual purpose. They honour his departed collaborator — Marx had died nine years earlier — and they position Engels's early work within the genealogy of scientific socialism. The preface is, among other things, an exercise in intellectual self-historicisation: Engels, writing as the acknowledged co-founder of the movement, explains to a new generation of English readers how the flawed but vital book they are about to read relates to the mature theory of [[surplus-value]] and class struggle elaborated in *Das Kapital*. The relationship between Engels's 1845 empirical study of the [[proletariat]] and Marx's later theoretical critique of [[capital]] is one of mutual reinforcement: Engels provided the concrete documentation of working-class conditions that Marx's [[exploitation]] theory systematised. By 1892, Engels could observe that the [[labour-movement]] and the [[bourgeoisie]]'s own reforms had vindicated much of what his youthful book had prophesied, and that Marx's analysis had given these prophecies their scientific foundation.
