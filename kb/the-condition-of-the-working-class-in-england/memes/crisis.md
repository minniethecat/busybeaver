# Crisis

## Definition

A commercial crisis, in Engels's analysis, is the point in the industrial cycle at which production overtakes demand, markets become glutted, credit collapses, and the entire productive apparatus grinds to a halt. It is not an accident or the result of external shocks — it is inherent to the capitalist mode of production, a necessary consequence of unregulated competition, production for profit rather than need, and the blindness of individual capitalists to the total market conditions. Each crisis throws masses of workers into unemployment, concentrates capital into fewer hands, and leaves the system more vulnerable to the next, more severe crisis. (003-010)

## The Cyclical Pattern

Engels describes the industrial cycle with precision. The movement is perpetually: prosperity, crisis, prosperity, crisis — a "perennial round" that English industry completes roughly once every five or six years, though later evidence from 1842 to 1868 showed the real period was closer to ten years, with secondary revulsions between the major crashes. (003-010, P01-010)

> So it goes on perpetually — prosperity, crisis, prosperity, crisis, and this perennial round in which English industry moves is, as has been before observed, usually completed once in five or six years. (003-010)

The cycle begins with a period of expansion. Rising prices and favourable reports from distant markets spur manufacturers to produce at full capacity. New mills are built, every available hand is employed, and speculation drives prices and production to "madness." Engels paints a vivid picture of the frenzy:

> It is a frantic struggle, which carries away even the most experienced and phlegmatic; goods are spun, woven, hammered, as if all mankind were to be newly equipped, as though two thousand million new consumers had been discovered in the moon. (003-010)

Then the collapse comes. Speculators abroad, desperate for cash, begin selling below market price. Prices fluctuate wildly, credit is shaken, bankruptcies cascade — first abroad, then in England. The discovery is made that three times more goods are on hand than can be consumed. Panic seizes the market, firms fail, reserves are thrown onto the market in a moment of anxiety, and the alarm feeds on itself. Mills close, wages fall, and the [[proletariat|working class]] is plunged into [[poverty|destitution]]. (003-010)

## The Structural Mechanism

> Whence comes this incongruity? It lies in the nature of industrial competition and the commercial crises which arise from them. In the present unregulated production and distribution of the means of subsistence, which is carried on not directly for the sake of supplying needs, but for profit, in the system under which everyone works for himself to enrich himself, disturbances inevitably arise at every moment. (003-010)

Engels identifies the root cause of crises in the fundamental irrationality of capitalist production. The manufacturer knows approximately how much of each article is consumed in each country annually, but he cannot know how much is on hand at any given moment, let alone how much his competitors are exporting. "Everything is done blindly, as guesswork, more or less at the mercy of accident." On the slightest favourable report, each manufacturer exports what he can, and before long the market is glutted. (003-010)

The mechanism escalates through the centralising tendency of competition. Initially, crises were limited to single branches and single markets. But competition drives displaced workers from one branch into others, transfers unsold goods from one saturated market to others, and gradually unites the minor crises into one "periodically recurring crisis" affecting all branches and all markets simultaneously. Each crisis is more intense than the last, because the scale of production has grown and the number of competitors — both domestic and foreign — has multiplied. (003-010)

## The Reserve Army

Crises reveal the function of the [[surplus-population]] as an [[surplus-population|industrial reserve army]]. It is "clear that [[manufacture|English manufacture]] must have, at all times save the brief periods of highest prosperity, an unemployed reserve army of workers, in order to be able to produce the masses of goods required by the market in the liveliest months." (003-011)

During the crisis, this reserve army swells enormously. Workers discharged from closed mills join the permanently surplus population on the streets, and the desperation of the unemployed — willing to work at any wage — becomes a weapon in the hands of employers against those still employed. (003-011)

> During a crisis this number naturally increases markedly, and want reaches its highest pitch. (003-013)

The crisis of 1842 — the most violent of its era — provides Engels's most detailed case study. In [[stockport|Stockport]], the poor-rate consumed forty percent of house-rent. In [[bolton|Bolton]], rental values sank from 86,000 pounds to 36,000 pounds, while 14,000 people — more than twenty percent of the population — required relief. In [[leeds|Leeds]], the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] guardians exhausted a reserve fund of 10,000 pounds plus a contribution of 7,000 pounds before the crisis even reached its height. The starving workmen "stood in the streets in all directions, begged singly or in crowds, besieged the sidewalks in armies, and appealed to the passersby for help; they begged, not cringing like ordinary beggars, but threatening by their numbers, their gestures, and their words." (003-013)

## Human Consequences

> To what extent want and suffering prevail among these unemployed during such a crisis, I need not describe. The poor-rates are insufficient, vastly insufficient; the philanthropy of the rich is a raindrop in the ocean, lost in the moment of falling, beggary can support but few among the crowds. (003-014)

The crisis kills. If not for the credit extended by small shopkeepers and the mutual aid of workers themselves, Engels writes, "every crisis would remove a multitude of the surplus through death by starvation." Even as it is, many perish indirectly — through [[disease]], through the [[fever]] epidemics that follow every commercial crash, through the physical and mental deterioration that prolonged privation inflicts. (003-014, 005-007)

> A period of privation, a commercial crisis or a bad harvest, has each time produced the typhus epidemic in Ireland as in Scotland, and the fury of the plague has fallen almost exclusively on the working-class. (005-007)

The connection between crisis and epidemic is amply documented. In [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] and [[glasgow|Glasgow]], [[cholera|typhus]] broke out "with especial violence, after the commercial crisis, subsiding somewhat each time after having raged about three years." The epidemic of 1817 attacked 6,000 persons in [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]]; that of 1837 attacked 10,000. Each repetition was more violent than the last. (005-005)

> But the fury of the epidemic in all former periods seems to have been child's play in comparison with its ravages after the crisis of 1842. One-sixth of the whole indigent population of Scotland was seized by the fever. (005-006)

The crisis also deepens the degradation of the worker's daily life. "Temporary [[poverty|want]] of sufficient [[diet|food]], to which almost every workingman is exposed at least once in the course of his life, only contributes to intensify the effects of his usual sufficient but bad diet. Children who are half-starved, just when they most need ample and nutritious [[diet|food]] — and how many such there are during every crisis and even when trade is at its best — must inevitably become weak, scrofulous and rachitic in a high degree." (005-008)

## The Psychological Toll

The [[proletariat]] lives in a state of permanent insecurity, and the crisis is the ultimate expression of this precariousness. The worker "who has nothing but his two hands, who consumes today what he earned yesterday, who is subject to every possible chance, and has not the slightest guarantee for being able to earn the barest necessities of life, whom every crisis, every whim of his employer may deprive of bread" is placed in "the most revolting, inhuman position conceivable for a human being." (005-026)

This insecurity shapes working-class character and behaviour. The worker who saves nothing when wages are high is not improvident but rational: "To accumulate lasting property for himself is impossible; and if it were not, he would only cease to be a workingman and another would take his place." The worker who lives extravagantly during the brief periods of prosperity is simply seizing the only joy available to him before the inevitable crash. The system that alternately inflates and crushes his fortunes makes "all paths uncertain for them, all life a bewilderment; society, steadfastness, peaceable continuance, the first blessings of man are not theirs." (005-026)

## Machinery and Crisis

[[Machinery]] intensifies the crisis dynamic by compounding the cycle of overproduction with the displacement of labour. "Every improvement in machinery throws workers out of employment, and the greater the advance, the more numerous the unemployed; each great improvement produces, therefore, upon a number of workers the effect of a commercial crisis, creates [[poverty|want]], wretchedness, and crime." (006-003)

Certain categories of workers are especially vulnerable. The hand-loom cotton weavers are "first to suffer from every crisis, and last to be relieved from it." Their trade is permanently overcrowded because it is the "last refuge of workers thrown out of employment in other branches." Even in average seasons, they earn no more than six or seven shillings a week for fourteen to eighteen hours of daily labour. (006-005)

## Trade Unions and Crisis

The [[trade-unions]] are powerless against the crisis. "In a commercial crisis the Union itself must reduce wages or dissolve wholly." The strike fund is exhausted by the mass of unemployed members requiring relief, the credit extended by sympathetic shopkeepers is withdrawn, and "[[poverty|want]] compels the workingman to place himself once more under the yoke of the bourgeoisie." (008-010)

Yet the unions are not wholly useless. They can, in the recovery phase, "bring about a more rapid increase of wages after a crisis than would otherwise follow" — because the manufacturer's interest is to delay raising wages until forced by competition, but the union can demand an increase as soon as the market improves, when the supply of available workers is still relatively small. (008-011)

## The Inevitable Crisis

Engels's analysis of the crisis is not merely descriptive but prophetic. He sees in the cyclical pattern of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism the seeds of its own destruction. The crisis mechanism — overproduction, market saturation, collapse — cannot be reformed within the system, because it is the system. [[free-trade|Free Trade]], far from preventing crises, universalises them by extending the scale of production and the reach of competition. The concentration of capital that each crisis accelerates brings the system nearer to its breaking point. (P01-010)

> The centralisation of capital strides forward without interruption, the division of society into great capitalists and non-possessing workers is sharper every day, the industrial development of the nation advances with giant strides towards the inevitable crisis. (007-017)

Forty years later, in his 1885 preface, Engels could reflect on the accuracy of this analysis. The crisis of 1847 had indeed arrived on schedule, triggered by the Irish famine and followed by the prospect of revolution — a revolution that was only averted by the French Revolution of 1848, which frightened the English [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] into political retrenchment. The crisis of 1857 and the crash of 1866 followed the same pattern, though each was treated as "natural, inevitable events, which must be fatalistically submitted to, and which always set themselves right in the end." The chronic depression that set in after 1876 — without the expected full crisis or the anticipated recovery — demonstrated that the system was entering a new phase, one in which the old cyclical pattern might give way to a permanent stagnation. (P01-010)
