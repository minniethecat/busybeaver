## Overview

The *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* was founded in 1821 by John Edward Taylor, a [[manchester|Manchester]] cotton merchant and reformer, in the aftermath of the Peterloo Massacre. From its inception, the paper represented the voice of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and commercial [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]: Liberal in politics, free-trade in economics, Nonconformist in religion, and reformist — but not radical — on social questions. Its editorial line consistently supported the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], and the extension of the franchise along lines that favoured the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest.

By the time Engels arrived in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1842, the *Guardian* was the dominant newspaper of industrial [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and an indispensable source for anyone seeking to understand the economic and social life of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts. Engels read it, evidently, with close attention.

## Engels's Use of the Guardian

Engels employs the *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* in three distinct ways, corresponding to his broader method of treating bourgeois sources as hostile witnesses:

**As a factual record of local incidents**: Engels cites the *Guardian* for specific events — adulterated [[diet|food]] seized by market inspectors, a girl killed in a mine explosion near [[wigan]], prosecutions for fraud against working-class consumers:

> "In the six weeks, from July 1st to August 14th, the same sheet reported three similar cases. According to the *Guardian* for August 3rd, a pig, weighing 200 pounds, which had been found dead and decayed, was cut up and exposed for sale by a butcher at Heywood, and was then seized." [002-059]

**As evidence of bourgeois consciousness**: The *Guardian*'s reporting of the mine explosion is cited not only for the fact of the death but for the paper's own indifference — "no one troubled himself further about the fact that an infringement of the law was thus revealed" [009-011]. The newspaper's report becomes evidence of the systematic disregard for working-class life that characterises bourgeois public discourse.

**As a market and commercial source**: The *Guardian*'s commercial intelligence — corn prices, cotton market reports, trade statistics — provides the economic backdrop against which Engels's social descriptions unfold.

## The Guardian in Engels's Source Hierarchy

The *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* occupies a distinctive position among Engels's sources. Unlike the parliamentary Blue Books and official reports, which Engels treats as documents extracted from the state by political pressure, the newspaper represents the bourgeoisie's own voluntary self-presentation — what the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class says about itself when it is not compelled to testify. That this self-presentation inadvertently reveals as much as it conceals is, for Engels, further confirmation of his thesis: the bourgeoisie cannot narrate its own activity without exposing what it would prefer to hide.
