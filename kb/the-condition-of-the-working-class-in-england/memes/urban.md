# Urban

## Definition

In Engels's analysis, the "great town" or urban centre is not merely a geographical setting but a causal force in the creation and degradation of the [[proletariat|working class]]. The concentration of population in industrial cities is driven by the logic of capital centralisation — the [[factory-system|factory]] requires workers massed together, and this massing creates markets, infrastructure, and labour pools that attract still more capital. The resulting city is a spatial expression of class relations: the working-class quarters are systematically separated from bourgeois districts, concealed behind commercial facades, and built to the minimum standard that can extract maximum rent. The urban condition, as Engels presents it, is not an accident of industrialisation but its necessary product. (001-004)

## Industrial Centralisation and Urban Growth

> The greater the town, the greater its advantages. It offers roads, railroads, canals; the choice of skilled labour increases constantly, new establishments can be built more cheaply because of the competition among builders and machinists who are at hand, than in remote country districts. (001-004)

Engels describes a self-reinforcing cycle of urban growth. The [[factory-system|factory]] concentrates workers; workers create demand for ser[[prostitution|vice]]s and goods; craftsmen and tradespeople settle nearby; the settlement grows into a village, then a town, then a city. Each stage increases the advantages of the location for further industrial investment. The logic is merciless: "every new [[factory-system|factory]] built in the country bears in it the germ of a manufacturing town." Engels even projects that if the trend continued unchecked, [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[liverpool|Liverpool]] would physically merge. This centralising dynamic does not merely reshape geography — it reshapes society, concentrating property, dissolving the old [[bourgeoisie|middle class]], and reducing the social structure to two classes: rich and poor. (001-004)

## The Great Town as Social War

> What is true of London, is true of Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, is true of all great towns. Everywhere barbarous indifference, hard egotism on one hand, and nameless misery on the other, everywhere social warfare, every man's house in a state of siege, everywhere reciprocal plundering under the protection of the law. (002-004)

The urban environment is the stage on which the "social war" is most openly enacted. Engels describes the streets of [[london|London]] as a spectacle of atomised humanity — hundreds of thousands of people [[overcrowding|crowding]] past each other with "brutal indifference," each absorbed in private interest. The spatial density of the city does not produce solidarity but isolation: "The dissolution of mankind into monads, of which each one has a separate principle, the world of atoms, is here carried out to its utmost extreme." This theme of urban alienation, where physical proximity produces moral distance, runs through the entire work. (002-002, 002-004)

## The Segregated City

> The town itself is peculiarly built, so that a person may live in it for years, and go in and out daily without coming into contact with a working-people's quarter or even with workers. (002-037)

Engels's analysis of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s urban geography is one of the most detailed sections of the book. He reveals a city deliberately constructed to conceal the suffering of the [[proletariat|working class]] from the bourgeoisie. The commercial district at the centre is ringed by a mile-and-a-half-wide belt of working-class quarters, which is in turn surrounded by middle-class and upper-class suburbs. The main thoroughfares are lined with shops that serve as a screen — the wealthy businessman can travel from his suburban villa to the Exchange through an unbroken series of respectable shopfronts, never glimpsing the "grimy misery that lurks to the right and the left." Engels calls this arrangement "hypocritical" and suggests it is the product of "unconscious tacit agreement, as well as with outspoken conscious determination." The spatial organisation of the city is, in effect, a lie. (002-037)

## The Urban Housing Catastrophe

> Every great city has one or more slums, where the working-class is crowded together. True, poverty often dwells in hidden alleys close to the palaces of the rich; but, in general, a separate territory has been assigned to it, where, removed from the sight of the happier classes, it may struggle along as it can. (002-008)

The working-class districts Engels describes — the "Old Town" of [[manchester|Manchester]], Little Ireland, the wynds of [[glasgow|Glasgow]], the rookery of [[st-giles|St. Giles]] in [[london|London]] — share common features across all English cities. Streets are unpaved, without sewers, filled with stagnant pools and rotting refuse. Ventilation is blocked by the chaotic, profit-driven method of construction: back-to-back houses, courts accessible only through narrow covered passages, cellar [[housing|dwellings]] below the water level of adjacent rivers. Engels walks the reader through the [[irk|Irk]] valley in [[manchester|Manchester]], where privies without doors stand at court entrances, where the river is "coal black, foul-smelling, full of debris and refuse," where tanneries and gasworks dump their waste directly into the water, and where human beings live in one-room huts five feet by six. (002-008, 002-039, 002-040)

> Such is the Old Town of Manchester, and on rereading my description, I am forced to admit that instead of being exaggerated, it is far from black enough to convey a true impression of the filth, ruin, and uninhabitableness, the defiance of all considerations of cleanliness, ventilation, and health which characterise the construction of this single district, containing at least twenty to thirty thousand inhabitants. (002-044)

Engels stresses that this is not a legacy of pre-industrial poverty but a product of the "industrial epoch alone." The old houses of [[manchester|Manchester]] have been crammed with workers; every vacant space has been built over for maximum profit; the landowners and municipality have done nothing to improve conditions when rebuilding. The principle governing urban construction is that "no hole is so bad but that some poor creature must take it who can pay for nothing better." The value of land rises with manufacture, and the more it rises, "the more madly was the work of building up carried on, without reference to the health or comfort of the inhabitants, with sole reference to the highest possible profit." (002-044)

## The Consequences of Urbanisation

> In a word, we must confess that in the workingmen's dwellings of Manchester, no cleanliness, no convenience, and consequently no comfortable family life is possible; that in such dwellings only a physically degenerate race, robbed of all humanity, degraded, reduced morally and physically to bestiality, could feel comfortable and at home. (002-053)

The physical conditions of the urban [[housing|slums]] produce what Engels sees as the degradation of the [[proletariat|working class]]. [[overcrowding|Overcrowding]] — whole families in single rooms, lodgers sharing beds with married couples, fifteen to twenty persons in a single cellar — destroys decency and family life. The lack of [[sanitary|sanitation]] breeds [[cholera|epidemic disease]], which periodically threatens to spread to bourgeois districts, provoking temporary bursts of sanitary policing. The Irish immigrants, crowded into the worst quarters, are particularly vulnerable: Engels quotes [[james-kay-shuttleworth|Dr. Kay]]'s account of cellars where water constantly wells up through clay-plugged holes, and of [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]] who must bale out their [[housing|dwellings]] every morning. (002-050, 002-053, 002-055)
