# Proletariat

## Definition

The proletariat is the class of wage-workers who, owning no [[capital|means of production]], must sell their labour-force to the capitalist for a daily wage. In Engels's usage, the term encompasses all who subsist by working for wages, including [[factory-system|factory]] hands, miners, [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers, and the [[surplus-population|industrial reserve army]]. The proletariat is distinguished from the bourgeoisie — the class that owns the [[capital|means of production]] and appropriates the surplus value created by workers — and from the petty bourgeoisie, a transitional class of small traders and independent craftsmen being progressively crushed by [[competition|industrial competition]]. (P04-023, 003-001)

## Engels's Analysis

> The history of the proletariat in England begins with the second half of the last century, with the invention of the steam-engine and of machinery for working cotton. These inventions gave rise, as is well known, to an industrial revolution, a revolution which altered the whole civil society. (P04-001)

Engels traces the origin of the English proletariat to the industrial revolution of the late eighteenth century. Before this transformation, England was a country of small towns, simple industries, and a thin but proportionally large agricultural population. Workers were typically independent craftsmen or yeomen who owned their tools and worked at home. The introduction of machinery — first in [[spinning|cotton spinning]], then across all branches of manufacture — dissolved this order. Machines concentrated production in factories, concentrated capital in the hands of a few owners, and concentrated population in the great [[urban|manufacturing town]]s. (P04-022)

> We have already seen how the proletariat was called into existence by the introduction of machinery. The rapid extension of manufacture demanded hands, wages rose, and troops of workmen migrated from the agricultural districts to the towns. (P04-023)

The creation of the proletariat was not a single event but an ongoing process driven by the mechanism of competition. Engels identifies three distinct pathways by which competition generated the industrial working class: first, by raising weavers' wages during boom periods and thereby inducing agricultural peasants to abandon their farms for the loom; second, by driving small farmers off the land through the large farm system; and third, by ruining the small bourgeoisie — independent artisans and shopkeepers — whose workshops could not compete with [[factory-system|factory production]]. All three streams fed the growing reservoir of labour in the industrial towns. (003-001)

[[competition|Competition]] also served as the bourgeoisie's sharpest weapon against the proletariat once the class was formed. Engels describes how competition among workers for employment drove down wages and working conditions, while competition among manufacturers drove the demand for ever-cheaper labour. The worker, possessing nothing but labour-power, faced the capitalist who owned both the [[capital|means of production]] and the product of labour. This asymmetry is the structural foundation of Engels's entire analysis. (003-002)

> The order of our investigation of the different sections of the proletariat follows naturally from the foregoing history of its rise. The first proletarians were connected with manufacture, were engendered by it, and accordingly, those employed in manufacture, in the working up of raw materials, will first claim our attention. (001-001)

Engels structures his investigation of the proletariat hierarchically. Factory workers — the "eldest children of the industrial revolution" — receive the most detailed examination because they are the most concentrated, the most politically conscious, and the most directly shaped by machinery. Miners come next, followed by [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers, and finally the Irish immigrant workers who formed the lowest stratum of the English working class. Engels argues that the degree of intelligence and political awareness among workers is in direct proportion to their connection with manufacture. Factory hands formed the nucleus of the [[labour-movement|Labour Movement]] from the beginning, and other sections joined in proportion as their handicraft was invaded by machinery. (001-001, 001-002, 001-005)

> The failings of the workers in general may be traced to an unbridled thirst for pleasure, to want of providence, and of flexibility in fitting into the social order... But is that to be wondered at? When a class can purchase few of the enjoyments of the civilised state, can scarcely procure the mere necessaries of subsistence, why should it not seek its pleasure in drink? (005-040)

Engels does not moralise about the [[prostitution|vice]]s of the working class — [[drunkenness|intemperance]], sexual licence, lack of providence — but rather explains them as rational responses to impossible conditions. A worker who has no prospect of improving his situation, whose children are likely to die before their fifth birthday, and whose body is worn out by the age of forty, has no incentive to defer gratification. The [[prostitution|vice]]s that the bourgeoisie deplores are, in Engels's analysis, the predictable and human consequences of [[exploitation|capitalist exploitation]]. (005-040)

## The Proletariat as a Distinct Nation

### Separation from the Bourgeoisie

> In view of all this, it is not surprising that the working-class has gradually become a race wholly apart from the English bourgeoisie. The bourgeoisie has more in common with every other nation of the earth than with the workers in whose midst it lives. (005-034)

In the closing chapters of his study, Engels draws out the social and moral consequences of proletarian existence. The working class, he argues, has been transformed into a distinct nation within the nation — speaking different dialects, holding different ideas and values, thinking different thoughts, and confronting the bourgeoisie as an antagonistic class. This separation is not accidental or temporary but is the necessary product of the economic system: the bourgeoisie benefits from the proletariat's labour while sharing none of its living conditions, eating at different tables, breathing different air, and dying at different ages. (005-034)

The gulf between the two classes was complete. The bourgeoisie inhabited the well-paved, well-lit, policed quarters of the town, while the proletariat was crowded into the unpaved, unlit, unpoliced districts — often hidden behind rows of shops that served as façades concealing the wretchedness behind them. A bourgeois could spend a lifetime in [[manchester|Manchester]] without ever seeing a working-class quarter.
