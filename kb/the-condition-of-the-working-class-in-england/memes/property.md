# Property

## Definition

In Engels's usage, "property" refers to the exclusive ownership of the [[capital|means of production]] — land, factories, machinery, raw materials, and capital — by the bourgeoisie. The proletariat, by contrast, owns "no property whatever, not even the pretended property of his labour-power," since that labour-power can only be realised by selling it to the capitalist who owns the instruments of work. Engels argues that this concentration of property is not a natural distribution of resources but a historical result of the industrial revolution, the enclosure of common lands, and the competitive destruction of small independent producers. (P04-006, P04-007)

## Engels's Analysis

> These workers had some property in the little tools of their trade, their own weaver's loom, or their spinning-wheel, which they worked themselves. But the introduction of machinery changed this. Now a single machine could do more work than a hundred hand-workers. There was, therefore, no longer any room for the hand-worker's property in his own tools. (P04-007)

The pivotal historical change, as Engels narrates it, was the separation of the worker from the [[capital|means of production]]. Before the industrial revolution, the weaver owned his loom and the spinner her wheel; the [[farming|agricultural labour]]er might rent a small plot for supplementary cultivation. These forms of property, modest as they were, gave the worker a degree of independence and a fallback against [[poverty|destitution]]. The [[factory-system|factory]] system destroyed both. The machine, requiring capital investment beyond any individual worker's means, concentrated the ownership of productive property in the hands of the [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]]. The worker was left with nothing to sell but labour-power. (P04-007, P04-009)

> The rapid extension of manufacture demands hands, wages rose, and troops of workmen migrated from the agricultural districts to the towns. The centralisation of property in the hands of a few thus kept pace with the centralisation of population. (P04-009)

Engels draws a direct connection between the concentration of property and the growth of the [[urban|manufacturing town]]s. As machinery centralised production in factories, and factories clustered where water-power and then [[steam-engine|steam-power]] were available, both capital and labour were drawn from the [[rural|countryside]] into the urban centres. The same process that concentrated property in fewer hands concentrated the propertyless masses in fewer square miles — creating the conditions Engels describes in Chapter 2, where tens of thousands of workers are packed into the courts and cellars of [[manchester|Manchester]], [[leeds|Leeds]], and [[birmingham|Birmingham]]. (P04-009, 001-003)

> Here the centralisation of property has reached the highest point. The mills of Manchester, for instance, belong almost exclusively to a few large capitalists. (001-005)

The [[urban|factory town]]s exhibit property concentration in its most extreme form. Engels observes that in [[manchester|Manchester]], the great mills were owned by a handful of families, while the hundreds of thousands who worked in them owned nothing — not their [[housing|dwellings]], which they rented from the same class that employed them; not their tools, which belonged to the mill-owner; not even their wages, which were consumed in weekly [[diet|subsistence]] with no possibility of accumulation. This absolute propertylessness is, for Engels, the defining condition of the proletarian. (001-005, 002-073)

> To accumulate lasting property for himself is impossible; and if by the greatest self-denial he does succeed in saving something, it goes to the pawnbroker when he is out of work. (005-024)

The propertyless worker exists in a state of permanent economic precarity. Even the most frugal worker cannot hope to acquire enough savings to weather a trade downturn, an illness, or a period of unemployment. The pawnshop — which Engels documents as a ubiquitous institution in working-class neighbourhoods — is the symbol of this precarity: the worker's few possessions (clothing, furniture, bedding) circulate between the pawnbroker and the home as the family oscillates between employment and [[poverty|want]]. The sacredness of property, so vigorously defended by the bourgeoisie, is a doctrine that protects only those who already possess it. (005-024, 005-019)

## Historical Context

Engels's treatment of property draws on the classical political economy of [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and David Ricardo, who recognised the distinction between the three factors of production — land, labour, and capital — and the corresponding classes of landlords, workers, and capitalists. Engels radicalises this framework by arguing that the separation of labour from property is not a technical feature of markets but the historical foundation of class domination. His analysis anticipates the argument [[karl-marx|Marx]] would later develop systematically in *[[capital|Capital]]*, where the "so-called primitive accumulation" is shown to be the violent process by which the mass of the population was divorced from the [[capital|means of production]].
