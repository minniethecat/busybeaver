# Manchester

## Location and Significance

Manchester, in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], was the world's first industrial city — "Cottonopolis," the centre of the global [[cotton-industry|cotton trade]] and the symbol of the new industrial age. By 1844, its population had swelled to over 350,000, having grown tenfold in sixty years. Engels lived in Manchester from 1842 to 1844, working in the offices of Ermen & Engels, his father's cotton-spinning firm. It was here, walking the streets of the working-class districts and studying [[factory-system|factory]] conditions at first hand, that he gathered the material for *The Condition of the Working Class in England*. (002-030)

Engels characterises South Lancashire with Manchester as "the headquarters of the manufacturing district," noting that the entire region stretching across Lancashire and neighbouring [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] formed an interconnected industrial landscape where factory towns merged into one another. The city was the epicentre not only of industrial production but of working-class political organisation — "from which all labour movements emanate." (002-030)

> If we cross Blackstone Edge or penetrate it with the railroad, we enter upon that classic soil on which English manufacture has achieved its masterwork and from which all labour movements emanate, namely, South Lancashire with its central city Manchester. (002-030)

## Role in Engels's Account

Manchester is not merely the setting of Engels's investigation — it is its principal subject. His famous description of the city's spatial organisation, in which the commercial thoroughfares screen the working-class courts and cellars from bourgeois view, is among the most influential passages of urban sociology ever written. Engels maps the city's class geography: the Exchange and the warehouses on the main streets; the counting-houses and shops behind them; and "the working-people's quarters" — "stretching like a girdle, averaging a mile and a half in breadth, around the commercial district." The bourgeois may travel for years through Manchester without ever entering a workers' quarter, because the main roads form a cordon that hides the poverty behind. (002-037, 002-044)

The Old Town of Manchester, Engels writes, is a district where "the filth, ruin, and uninhabitableness... defy all description." Cellar [[housing|dwellings]], overcrowded courts accessible only through narrow covered passages, streets without drainage where "heaps of refuse, offal, and sickening filth lie among standing pools." It is in Manchester, Engels argues, that the capitalist system reveals its essential character most clearly — because nowhere else was the contrast between the wealth created by industry and the misery of those who created it so stark. (002-044)

> Such is the Old Town of Manchester, and on rereading my description, I am forced to admit that instead of being exaggerated, it is far from black enough to convey a true impression of the filth, ruin, and uninhabitableness, the defiance of all considerations of cleanliness, ventilation, and health which characterise the construction of this single district, containing at least twenty to thirty thousand inhabitants. (002-044)

## Sanitary Conditions and Housing

Engels's documentation of sanitary conditions in Manchester is forensic in its detail. He catalogues every district: the Old Town between the commercial centre and the [[irk|Irk]], the courts along the Irk's steep south bank where houses rise directly from the river, the great workingmen's quarters of Ancoats and Hulme, and the notorious enclave of Little Ireland. In all of them, the pattern was the same: unpaved streets, no drainage, privies shared by hundreds, and cellars inhabited by entire families. (002-038, 002-039, 002-053)

When [[cholera]] approached in 1831, the bourgeoisie panicked and appointed a Health Commission. The inspection of Manchester proper revealed a city of appalling neglect:

> There were inspected, in all, 6,951 houses — naturally in Manchester proper alone, Salford and the other suburbs being excluded. Of these, 6,565 urgently needed whitewashing within; 960 were out of repair; 939 had insufficient drains; 1,435 were damp; 452 were badly ventilated; 2,221 were without privies. Of the 687 streets inspected, 248 were unpaved, 53 but partially paved, 112 ill-ventilated, 352 containing standing pools, heaps of debris, refuse, etc. (002-054)

The cellar population was substantial. Engels cites Gaskell's count of 20,000 persons living in cellars in Manchester proper — roughly twelve percent of the working-class population, estimated at 175,000. Overcrowding extended beyond the cellars: in one case recorded by the sanitary report, a man, his wife, and his adult sister-in-law were found sleeping in a single bed — a circumstance recorded six separate times in Manchester alone. (002-056)

### Little Ireland

Of all Manchester's working-class districts, none achieved greater notoriety than Little Ireland. The sanitary police inspected it in 1831 during the cholera epidemic; thirteen years later, Engels found the district "in almost the same state." The police had not followed up; the landlords had not been compelled to improve their properties. Engels describes the inhabitants continuing to live in conditions below "the lowest stage of humanity." (002-050, 002-051)

## Engels's Verdict

After twenty months of personal observation, Engels reached a damning conclusion about the conditions he had witnessed. His verdict is among the most famous passages of the book — a judgment not only on Manchester but on the industrial system that produced it:

> In a word, we must confess that in the workingmen's dwellings of Manchester, no cleanliness, no convenience, and consequently no comfortable family life is possible; that in such dwellings only a physically degenerate race, robbed of all humanity, degraded, reduced morally and physically to bestiality, could feel comfortable and at home. (002-053)

The universality of Manchester's condition extended to every industrial city in Britain. Engels situated Manchester within a national landscape of urban misery, linking it explicitly to the other great towns:

> What is true of London, is true of Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, is true of all great towns. Everywhere barbarous indifference, hard-hearted egotism on the one hand, and nameless misery on the other, everywhere social warfare, every man's house in a state of siege, everywhere reciprocal plundering under the protection of the law, and all so shameless, so openly avowed that one shrinks from the consequences of our social state and their open expression. (002-022)

Even within the metropolitan area, enforcement was uneven. Manchester received occasional — if ineffectual — sanitary attention; [[salford|Salford]], across the Irwell, received none:

> If, in Manchester, the police, from time to time, every six or ten years, makes a raid upon the working-people's districts, closes the worst dwellings, and causes the filthiest spots in these Augean stables to be cleansed, in Salford it seems to have done absolutely nothing. (002-052)
