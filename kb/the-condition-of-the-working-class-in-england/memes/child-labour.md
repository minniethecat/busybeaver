# Child Labour

## Definition

Child labour was the systematic employment of children — some as young as four or five — in the factories, [[mining|mines]], and workshops of industrial England. Engels devotes a substantial portion of the book to documenting the conditions under which children worked: fourteen-hour days, night shifts, beatings with leather straps to keep them awake, and physical deformities caused by prolonged standing at machinery. Child labour was not, for Engels, an unfortunate by-product of industrialisation but integral to it: children were cheaper than adults, more docile, and their small hands could reach into machinery that adult hands could not. (006-001)

The demand for child labour was structurally embedded in the [[factory-system]] itself. As Engels explains in his analysis of machinery, the transition from skilled adult labour to machine-tending systematically favoured women and children over adult men. The machines required "no muscular strength, but only flexibility of finger," with the consequence that "men are, therefore, not only not needed for it, but actually, by reason of the greater muscular development of the hand, less fit for it than women and children." (006-006) This was no accident of design but the logical outcome of a system that sought to minimise wage costs. In the [[spinning]]-mills, women and girls held exclusive possession of the throstles; among the mules, piecers were "usually children or women." (006-006)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels's treatment of child labour is organised around the evidence of parliamentary commissions. He quotes the testimony of [[factory-system|factory]] surgeons who documented the physical damage — curvature of the spine, knock-knees, flattened feet, and injuries from unguarded machinery. He reproduces the statements of overseers who described beating children with leather straps ("strapping") to keep them awake during the final hours of a shift. He cites the testimony of the children themselves, who described falling asleep standing up, being doused with cold water to wake them, and returning home too exhausted to eat. (006-010)

> The ruinous influence of the factory system began at an early day to attract general attention. In 1802 Parliament passed an Apprentices' Bill, by which the most crying evils were removed. (006-014)

Engels traces the legislative history of child labour regulation as a history of evasion. The 1802 [[apprentice|Apprentice]]s' Act — the first [[factory-act|factory legislation]] — applied only to [[pauperism|pauper]] apprentices in cotton mills and had no enforcement mechanism. Its principal effect was to teach manufacturers that [[factory-system|factory]] acts could be ignored with impunity. Subsequent acts — 1819, 1825, 1831 — were each slightly stronger than the last, but the absence of [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors meant that they remained "dead letters." It was not until the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833, which created the first (grossly inadequate) inspectorate, that legislation had any measurable effect on children's working conditions. (006-014)

### Factory Children: The Evidence of the 1833 Commission

The Factories' Inquiry Commission of 1833 provides the most extensive evidentiary basis for Engels's indictment. The commissioners documented how children were rented out from [[workhouse]]s to manufacturers as [[apprentice]]s, treated "with the utmost recklessness and barbarity." (006-014) Even children from the workhouses were "rented out for a number of years to the manufacturers as apprentices," lodged, fed, and clothed in common, "completely the slaves of their masters." (006-014) This system — the [[apprentice|apprentice system]] — was eventually crowded out by competition from free workers, but the principle that children were disposable inputs of production persisted.

Engels reports the effects of factory labour on children's physical development with clinical precision. A nine-year-old child sent into the mill worked 6.5 hours daily (formerly 8, earlier still 12 to 14, even 16 hours) until the thirteenth year, then twelve hours until eighteen. (006-015) The result was a predictable catalogue of deformity and disease:

> The great mortality among children of the working-class, and especially among those of the factory operatives, is proof enough of the unwholesome conditions under which they pass their first year. These influences are at work, of course, among the children who survive, but not quite so powerfully as upon those who succumb. The result in the most favourable case is a tendency to disease, or some check in development, and consequent less than normal vigour of the constitution. (006-015)

The Commission also documented the effects on girls specifically. Female operatives who had worked in mills from their ninth year proved "wholly inexperienced and unfit as housekeepers," unable to knit, sew, cook, or wash. (006-011) Dr. Hawkins, Commissioner for Lancashire, testified that mill girls, when married, had "not the vaguest idea how to set about" caring for their children. (006-011)

### Mining Children: The 1842 Report

Engels extends his analysis to mining in Chapter 9, where the [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] Report of 1842 documented conditions that shocked even a Parliament accustomed to ignoring working-class suffering. Children as young as four worked as "trappers" — sitting alone in total darkness for twelve hours, opening and closing ventilation doors when coal carts passed. Girls of eight crawled on hands and knees through tunnels too low to stand, harnessed to coal tubs by chains that passed between their legs. Engels's decision to reproduce this testimony verbatim, without editorial softening, was a deliberate rhetorical strategy: the facts, stated plainly, were more powerful than any polemic. (009-001)

The mining children's testimony is among the most harrowing in the book:

> In the coal and iron mines which are worked in pretty much the same way, children of four, five, and seven years are employed. They are set to transporting the ore or coal loosened by the miner from its place to the horse-path or the main shaft, and to opening and shutting the doors (which separate the divisions of the mine and regulate its ventilation) for the passage of workers and material. For watching the doors the smallest children are usually employed, who thus pass twelve hours daily, in the dark, alone, sitting usually in damp passages without even having work enough to save them from the stupefying, brutalising tedium of doing nothing. (009-005)

The physical consequences were catastrophic. The children and young people employed in transporting coal "all complain of being overtired," with the report documenting cases where "children throw themselves down on the stone hearth or the floor as soon as they reach home, fall asleep at once without being able to take a bite of food, and have to be washed and put to bed while asleep." (009-007) For the girls and women working in thin coal seams, conditions were still worse:

> The women and children who have to transport the coal crawl upon their hands and knees, fastened to the tub by a harness and chain (which frequently passes between the legs), while a man behind pushes with hands and head. The pushing with the head engenders local irritations, painful swellings, and ulcers. (009-008)

Engels used this evidence to demonstrate that the exploitation of children was not a regrettable excess of particular manufacturers but a structural feature of the system. As he notes in his analysis of machinery and the division of labour, "the human being, the worker, is regarded in manufacture simply as a piece of capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of wages." (006-004) The child, no less than the adult, was capital.

## The Family

Engels treats child labour as destructive not only to the child but to the family. When children worked in factories, they were removed from parental supervision — and, often, from any supervision at all. Engels reports testimony of sexual abuse of [[factory-system|factory]] girls by male overseers, of children learning obscene language and habits from adult workers, and of mothers who worked in mills leaving infants at home dosed with opiates ("quietness syrups") to keep them from crying. The bourgeois family, which the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] sentimentalised as a haven from the market, was for the [[proletariat|working class]] impossible: the market invaded the family and dissolved it. (006-011)

The employment of children inverted the natural order of the household. Engels notes that when children "get on far enough to earn more than they cost their parents from week to week, they begin to pay the parents a fixed sum for board and lodging, and keep the rest for themselves." (006-007) This was a recurring pattern from the fourteenth or fifteenth year onward — the children "emancipate themselves, and regard the paternal dwelling as a lodging-house, which they often exchange for another, as suits them." (006-007) The economic logic that made children profitable dissolved the bonds of parental authority and affection alike.

Engels recounts, through the testimony of a workingman named Robert Pounder, the story of Jack — a man reduced to mending his wife's stockings while she worked in the factory. When asked about his situation, Jack replied: "There is work enough for women folks and childer hereabouts, but none for men; thou mayest sooner find a hundred pound on the road than work for men." (006-009) This testimony was not incidental: for Engels, the substitution of child and female labour for adult male labour was the most vivid proof that industrial capitalism had inverted the natural social order. The factory system had, in his phrase, "unsexed the man and taken from the woman all womanliness." (006-010)

> Can anyone imagine a more insane state of things than that described in this letter? And yet this condition, which unsexes the man and takes from the woman all womanliness without being able to bestow upon the man true womanliness, or the woman true manliness — this condition which degrades, in the most shameful way, both sexes, and, through them, Humanity, is the last result of our much-praised civilisation, the final achievement of all the efforts and struggles of hundreds of generations to improve their own situation and that of their posterity. (006-010)
