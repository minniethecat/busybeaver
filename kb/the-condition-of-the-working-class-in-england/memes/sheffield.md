# Sheffield

## Overview

Sheffield, in the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], was by the 1840s the undisputed centre of English cutlery and steel manufacture. Its population had risen from 46,000 in 1801 to 110,000 in 1844, fuelled by the expansion of iron and steel production across industrial Britain. Sheffield produced knives, razors, forks, files, and metal goods of all descriptions. The consumption of coal in the city alone reached 515,000 tons in 1836. Engels devoted substantial attention to Sheffield's occupational diseases -- particularly the fearsome "grinders' asthma" -- and to its social conditions. (P04-016, 007-009)

## Engels's Account

### The Grinding Trades and Occupational Disease

The most distinctive and horrifying feature of Sheffield industry described by Engels is the trade of grinding knife-blades and forks. Dry grinding, which involves sharpening blades on a dry stone, produced a fine, sharp-edged metal dust that filled the workroom atmosphere and was "necessarily inhaled." Dry grinders had an average life expectancy of barely thirty-five years; even wet grinders (who worked with water-cooled stones) rarely exceeded forty-five. The fork grinders -- about eighty men and seventy boys -- died between the ages of twenty-eight and thirty-two. (007-011)

Dr. Knight of Sheffield provided Engels with a detailed clinical description of "grinders' asthma." The disease typically began to manifest around the twentieth year: shortness of breath on the slightest exertion, habitual shoulder-raising to ease breathing, a crouching posture, a dirty yellow complexion, and a rough, hoarse voice. Sufferers expectorated "considerable quantities of dust, either mixed with phlegm or in balls or cylindrical masses." The final stages brought night sweats, diarrhoea, wasting, and death from consumption, "after they have lingered months, or even years, unfit to support themselves." Knight noted drily that "the hardest [[drunkenness|drink]]ers among the grinders are the longest lived among them, because they are longest and oftenest absent from their work." Attempts to introduce covered grindstones and dust-extraction systems were resisted or destroyed by the grinders themselves, who feared that safer conditions would attract more workers and depress wages -- they preferred "a short life and a merry one." Engels reports that "he who is once a grinder falls into despair, as though he had sold himself to the devil." (007-011, 007-015)

### Education and Social Conditions

[[education|Education]] in Sheffield was, according to Engels, "upon a very low plane." A clergyman who studied the statistics estimated that of 16,500 working-class children eligible for school, scarcely 6,500 could read. Children were taken from school between the seventh and twelfth year at the latest. The quality of teachers was abysmal: one was a convicted thief who "found no other way of supporting himself after being released from jail than teaching school." (007-013)

[[housing|Housing]] conditions in Sheffield, while briefly mentioned, were similarly poor to those found in other [[urban|factory town]]s. Engels notes that "in [[leicester|Leicester]], [[derby|Derby]], and Sheffield, it is no better" than in [[nottingham|Nottingham]], where the majority of houses were back-to-back without through-ventilation. (002-024)

### Immorality and Social Disorder

The moral conditions Engels describes in Sheffield are exceptionally bleak. Young people spent entire Sundays "lying in the street tossing coins or fighting dogs," frequenting gin palaces where they sat with sweethearts until late at night, then "taking walks in solitary couples." Commissioner Symonds visited an alehouse where forty to fifty young people of both sexes, nearly all under seventeen, sat each lad beside his lass, [[drunkenness|drink]]ing, playing cards, and dancing. Among them were "openly avowed professional prostitutes." Early, unbridled sexual intercourse and youthful prostitution beginning at fourteen or fifteen were described by all witnesses as "extraordinarily frequent." (007-013)

[[crime|Crime]]s of a "savage and desperate sort" were common. Engels records that one year before Commissioner Symonds's visit, a band composed chiefly of young persons was arrested while preparing to set fire to the town, equipped with lances and inflammable substances. The labour movement in Sheffield, Engels notes, shared this same "savage character." In 1844, Sheffield and [[bradford|Bradford]] saw Chartist spy outbreaks that generated great excitement. (007-013, 008-028)
