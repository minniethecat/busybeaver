# Chartist Movement

## Overview

[[chartism|Chartism]] was the organised expression of working-class political consciousness in early Victorian Britain. Taking its name from the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter of 1838]], the movement demanded six reforms: universal male suffrage, secret ballots, equal electoral districts, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, [[wages|payment]] of MPs, and annual parliaments. [[peoples-charter|The Charter]] itself was the product of the [[general-workingmens-association|London Working Men's Association]], but the movement rapidly spread to the industrial districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], where economic grievances gave political demands their explosive force. Engels, living in [[manchester|Manchester]] during the [[plug-plot-riots|1842 General Strike]] (the "[[plug-plot-riots|Plug Plot]]"), witnessed [[chartism|Chartism]]'s high-water mark and the state's violent response. (008-025)

## Engels's Account

> The English Chartist is politically a republican. (008-025)

Engels's characterisation of [[chartism|Chartism]] is striking for its warmth — unusual in a book otherwise severe in its analysis. He presents the Chartist not as a violent revolutionary but as a working man who has drawn the correct conclusion from his circumstances: that political power, not charity or paternalist reform, is the only path to emancipation. The Chartist understands that the bourgeoisie controls the state, that the state protects property, and that property is the source of his exploitation. Engels reports attending Chartist meetings in [[manchester|Manchester]] and describes the movement's leaders — [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]], James Leach, and others — with evident respect. (008-025)

> When Feargus O'Connor, the Chartist leader, came to Manchester during the insurrection of 1842, he appeared, in the eyes of the authorities, solely responsible for the rising. (002-056)

Engels's direct observation of Chartist agitation shapes the book's political analysis. He documents how the movement combined economic demands (repeal of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]], the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]]) with constitutional ones (the Charter), understanding — as the bourgeoisie did not — that economic and political power were inseparable. The 1842 strike, which began among [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] workers over a wage reduction and expanded into a general stoppage with explicit Chartist demands, is treated as a watershed: the moment when trade unionism and political radicalism fused. (002-056)

Engels is also frank about [[chartism|Chartism]]'s internal divisions. The movement split between "moral force" Chartists (led by [[william-lovett|William Lovett]], who favoured education and peaceful petitioning) and "physical force" Chartists (led by O'Connor, who threatened armed resistance). Engels does not take sides in this debate but observes that the bourgeoisie feared both factions equally: even the most moderate Chartist demand — universal suffrage — would destroy the property-based electoral system that kept manufacturers in power. (008-029)

## Activities

The Chartists pursued multiple strategies simultaneously. Mass petitioning — the three great Chartist petitions of 1839, 1842, and 1848, each bearing millions of signatures — was the movement's signature tactic. But Engels also documents the [[national-charter-association|National Charter Association]]'s extensive network of local branches, its newspapers (the *Northern Star* had the largest circulation of any provincial paper in Britain), and its attempts to build alliances with trade unions and middle-class reformers. When peaceful petitioning failed, the movement turned to the general strike — the "sacred month" — and, in some districts, to armed drilling. (008-026)

The movement's relationship with the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] is particularly revealing. Both organisations courted working-class support, but for incompatible purposes: the League [[poverty|want]]ed cheap bread to reduce wages; the Chartists [[poverty|want]]ed political power to raise them. When Chartist leaders pointed out this contradiction, the League denounced them as enemies of progress and allies of the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]. Engels treats the League's hostility to [[chartism|Chartism]] as proof that the bourgeoisie's commitment to "reform" was contingent on reform leaving [[property|property relations]] untouched. (008-029)
