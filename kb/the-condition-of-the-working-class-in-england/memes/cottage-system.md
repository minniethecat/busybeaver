# Cottage System

## Definition

The [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] (also known as the domestic system or putting-out system) was the dominant mode of textile production in England before the industrial revolution. Under this system, spinning and weaving were carried out in the worker's home, using hand-operated tools owned by the worker. A merchant-middleman supplied the raw material and collected the finished product, paying the worker by the piece. Engels treats the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] as the baseline against which the degradation effected by the [[factory-system|factory]] system must be measured — it was not an idyll, but the worker under the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] retained a measure of independence that the [[factory-system|factory]] operative lost entirely. (P04-003)

## Engels's Analysis

> Before the introduction of machinery, the spinning and weaving of raw materials was carried on in the home of the workman. (P04-003)

Engels is careful not to romanticise the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]]. The cottage worker was poor; his work was monotonous; his life was governed by the fluctuations of the market. But he was not, in the strict sense, a proletarian. He owned his spinning-wheel or his hand-loom. He controlled his [[working-day|working hours]] — if the weather was fine, he might work in his garden; if demand was slack, he might work fewer hours. He was typically also a small cultivator, renting an acre or two of land that provided a [[diet|subsistence]] cushion against the vicissitudes of the market. This combination of industrial and [[farming|agricultural labour]], Engels argues, gave the pre-industrial worker a degree of security and independence that the [[factory-system|factory]] operative could never attain. (P04-001, P04-003)

The introduction of machinery destroyed this world. The [[machinery|spinning-jenny]], the water-frame, and the power-loom concentrated production in factories where steam or water power could be harnessed. The cottage worker could not compete with machine production — a single [[factory-system|factory]] operative tending a power-loom could produce in a day what a hand-weaver took a week to make. The cottage worker had three choices: starve, migrate to the [[urban|factory town]], or sink into the agricultural proletariat. All three routes led to the same destination: the loss of independence and the transformation of the independent producer into a [[wages|wage-labour]]er. (P04-005)

The destruction of the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]], Engels observes, was a necessary precondition for the creation of the modern [[proletariat|working class]]. Only when workers were concentrated in factories and towns could they develop class consciousness. The scattered, isolated cottage workers could not organise; the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, working side by side and living in the same [[housing|slums]], could. The [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] had to die for the proletariat to be born. (P04-005, P04-001)
