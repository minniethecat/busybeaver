# Lord Ashley

## Life

Anthony Ashley-Cooper (1801–1885), styled Lord Ashley until he succeeded to the earldom of Shaftesbury in 1851, was a Tory MP and the leading parliamentary advocate for [[factory-system|factory]] reform in the 1830s and 1840s. He chaired the committee that investigated child labour, introduced the Mines and Collieries Act of 1842 prohibiting underground work by women and children, and was instrumental in the passage of the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]]. He was deeply religious, an evangelical Anglican who saw [[factory-system|factory]] reform as a Christian duty. (005-038)

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels treats Ashley with a characteristic mixture of respect for his evidence and scepticism about his motives. He quotes Ashley's parliamentary statements on working-class conditions extensively — particularly Ashley's evidence that the [[proletariat|working class]] was "shamefully neglected" by the state — because Ashley, as a Tory aristocrat, could not be dismissed as a radical agitator. His testimony carried weight precisely because he was of the ruling class. (005-038, 005-020)

Yet Engels is careful to distinguish Ashley's individual philanthropy from the class interests he ultimately served. The reforms Ashley championed — the [[factory-act|Factory Act]], the Mines Act, the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] — were not victories of humanitarianism over greed, in Engels's analysis, but concessions extracted by working-class agitation that the most far-sighted sections of the bourgeoisie accepted because they accelerated the concentration of capital. The [[manufacturer|factory owner]] who could afford to comply with the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] was the large capitalist who thereby gained an advantage over his smaller, less capitalised competitors. Ashley's reforms, however well-intentioned, strengthened the hand of big capital. (005-038)

### Evidence on Working-Class Expenditure

Engels drew on Ashley's statistical evidence about the economic condition of the working class across multiple domains. One of Ashley's most striking findings concerned the scale of working-class spending on alcohol — a fact Engels used not to moralise against the workers but to illustrate the depth of social misery that drove them to drink:

> When one has seen the extent of intemperance among the workers in England, one readily believes Lord Ashley's statement that this class annually expends something like twenty-five million pounds sterling upon intoxicating liquor: and the deterioration in external conditions, the frightful shattering of mental and physical health, the ruin of all domestic relations which follow may readily be imagined.
>
> (005-038)

This passage, drawn from Ashley's parliamentary testimony, serves Engels's argument that [[drunkenness]] was not a moral failing of the [[proletariat|working class]] but a symptom of the conditions imposed upon it by the [[industrial-revolution|industrial system]]. The figure of twenty-five million pounds — an enormous sum for the period — demonstrated both the scale of working-class suffering and, Engels implies, the hypocrisy of a [[middle-class|bourgeoisie]] that profited from the liquor trade while condemning its consequences.

## Leadership of the Ten Hours' Movement

After Michael Sadler's death, Ashley assumed leadership of the Ten Hours' Bill campaign in Parliament. Engels records this transition:

> Sadler's place, he having died, was filled in the House of Commons by Lord Ashley and Richard Oastler, both Tories.
>
> (006-049)

Ashley introduced the Ten Hours' Bill on March 15, 1844, delivering a speech that Engels documents in detail. In that speech, Ashley presented statistical evidence about the proportion of women and children in the factory workforce:

> Let us take some of the statements of a speech with which Lord Ashley introduced the Ten Hours' Bill, March 15th, 1844, into the House of Commons.
>
> (006-007)

Ashley repeated the testimony of workwomen describing the conditions of their labour, and presented evidence showing that more than half of all factory workers — 52 percent — were women and children (006-007).

On March 19, 1844, Ashley achieved a parliamentary breakthrough:

> Lord Ashley carried, with a majority of 179 to 170, a resolution that the word "Night" in the Factory Act should be defined as the period from 6 p.m. to 6 a.m.
>
> (006-050)

This resolution, while a procedural step rather than final legislation, demonstrated the growing political strength of the factory reform movement. The "masses of petitions supporting it which poured in from all sides brought them allies" in Parliament (006-050).

### Testimony of Workwomen and the Dissolution of the Family

In his March 1844 speech, Ashley read into the parliamentary record the direct testimony of factory workwomen. Engels reproduces Ashley's presentation of this evidence to illustrate the destruction of family life by the [[factory-system|factory system]]:

> Lord Ashley repeats the testimony of several workwomen: "M. H., twenty years old, has two children, the youngest a baby, that is tended by the other, a little older. The mother goes to the mill shortly after five o'clock in the morning, and comes home at eight at night; all day the milk pours from her breasts, so that her clothing drips with it."
>
> (006-007)

This graphic testimony — a mother whose breast milk soaks through her clothing during the thirteen-hour working day — was among the most powerful evidence Ashley deployed. Engels included it not merely to document suffering but to demonstrate how the [[factory-system|factory system]] dissolved the family, forcing mothers to abandon infants who were then put out to nurse for a shilling or eighteenpence a week.

Ashley continued with the testimony of another workwoman, whose case was still more extreme:

> "H. W. has three children, goes away Monday morning at five o'clock, and comes back Saturday evening; has so much to do for the children then that she cannot get to bed before three o'clock in the morning; often wet through to the skin, and obliged to work in that state."
>
> (006-007)

This woman, who worked and lived entirely at the mill from Monday to Saturday, saw her children only on Sunday, the sole day of rest. Engels deploys these accounts — drawn from Ashley's parliamentary speech — to substantiate his claim that the employment of women in factories "dissolves the family utterly and of necessity."

Ashley also presented evidence of how factory labour inverted the traditional relations of authority within the working-class household. When children became wage-earners, they often asserted their independence from their parents in brutal fashion:

> Lord Ashley gives an example of this in his speech: A man berated his two daughters for going to the public house, and they answered that they were tired of being ordered about, saying, "Damn you, we have to keep you!" Determined to keep the proceeds of their work for themselves, they left the family dwelling, and abandoned their parents to their fate.
>
> (006-010)

This episode illustrates Engels's argument that the [[factory-system|factory system]] not only exploited labour but destroyed the moral foundations of [[proletariat|working-class]] life, turning children against parents and dissolving the bonds of family affection into cash relations.

## The Mines Act

Ashley also acted swiftly on the issue of mine labour. When the Children's Employment Commission's Report documented the horrific conditions in mines — including women and children working underground — Engels records:

> Lord Ashley hastened to bring in a bill wholly forbidding the work of women in the mines, and greatly limiting that of children.
>
> (009-011)

This became the Mines and Collieries Act of 1842.

### Limitations of the Mines Act

Despite Ashley's swift action, the legislation proved difficult to enforce. Engels notes that the Act "has remained a dead letter in most districts, because no mine inspectors were appointed to watch over its being carried into effect" (009-011). The evasion of the law was particularly easy in the remote country districts where mines were situated:

> The evasion of the law is very easy in the country districts in which the mines are situated; and no one need be surprised that the Miners' Union laid before the Home Secretary an official notice, last year, that in the Duke of Hamilton's coal mines in Scotland, more than sixty women were at work; or that the *Manchester Guardian* reported that a girl perished in an explosion in a mine near Wigan, and no one troubled himself further about the fact that an infringement of the law was thus revealed.
>
> (009-011)

This passage underscores the limits of legislative reform without administrative enforcement — a theme that runs throughout Engels's work. Ashley's good intentions could not compensate for the structural resistance of the [[mine|mining]] interests and the absence of an effective [[factory-inspectorate|inspectorate]].

## Evidence from Manufacturers

Ashley's campaign gathered evidence from across the industrial districts. Engels notes that even some manufacturers corroborated the reformers' claims. A Manchester manufacturer admitted in a letter to Ashley "that, towards the fortieth year of their age, the operatives became prematurely old" from factory labour (006-031).

Engels provides the full context: of 1,600 operatives employed in several factories in Harpur and Lanark, only 10 were over 45 years of age; of 22,094 operatives in Stockport and Manchester, only 143 were over 45 (006-031). The manufacturer Mr. Ashworth, a large factory owner, acknowledged in his letter to Ashley that towards the fortieth year, spinners could no longer prepare the required quantity of yarn and were therefore discharged — calling operatives of forty "old people" (006-031).

## Political Affiliation

Engels places Ashley within the broader political landscape, associating him with "Young England," the group of philanthropic Tories that included D'Israeli, Borthwick, Ferrand, and Lord John Manners. Engels notes that "Lord Ashley, too, is in sympathy with them" (013-011). While Engels regarded Young England's desire to restore "Merry England" as "unattainable and ridiculous," he acknowledged their "good intention" and "courage to resist the existing state of things" (013-011).

The comparison between Ashley and his fellow reformers is instructive. Unlike [[richard-oastler|Richard Oastler]], who was imprisoned for debt arising from his agitation against the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]], Ashley operated from within the establishment. Unlike [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]], who led the initial parliamentary campaign for the [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] before his death in 1835, Ashley had the aristocratic standing and political connections to sustain the legislative struggle over nearly two decades. And unlike the radical Chartists such as [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]], Ashley rejected the [[proletariat|working class]]'s claim to political power — his reforms were acts of paternalistic charity, not democratic rights.

## Legacy

Ashley's parliamentary career illustrates the central tension in Engels's analysis of reform. The [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] to which Ashley devoted his political life were genuine improvements in the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]] — Engels never denies this — yet they functioned simultaneously as instruments of class stabilisation. By ameliorating the worst excesses of the [[factory-system|factory system]], they made the system itself more durable. The [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] was both a working-class victory and a concession that helped preserve capitalist [[property|property]] relations.

This duality makes Ashley a particularly revealing figure in Engels's account. He is neither villain nor hero but a symptom of a contradiction: the very decency that led him to champion reform was placed in the service of a system whose logic, Engels argued, was fundamentally indecent. Ashley's story, as Engels tells it, is the story of nineteenth-century British reformism itself — sincere, limited, and ultimately incorporated into the machinery of the social order it sought to moderate.
