# Birmingham

## Overview

Birmingham, located in Warwickshire in the English Midlands, was one of the great industrial cities of the early nineteenth century. Its population surged from 73,000 in 1801 to 200,000 in 1844, driven by the expansion of the metal trades, for which the city was the national headquarters. Unlike the [[urban|factory town]]s of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], Birmingham retained a distinctive structure of small workshops and small masters — what the French economist Leon Faucher called a "democratie industrielle." (007-009)

Engels discusses Birmingham in the context of his survey of the [[urban|great town]]s, comparing its [[housing|working-class housing]] conditions with those of [[manchester]], [[liverpool]], and [[glasgow]]. While Birmingham was not spared the filth and [[crowding|overcrowding]] common to all industrial cities, Engels notes that it fared somewhat better than the [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] towns in certain respects. (002-024)

## Engels's Account

### Housing Conditions

Birmingham enters Engels's survey of [[housing|working-class housing]] conditions in Chapter 2, "The Great Towns." He quotes from the *Artisan* journal, which presented a mixed picture. On the one hand, Birmingham's older quarters contained many bad districts, "filthy and neglected, full of stagnant pools and heaps of refuse." Courts were very numerous — "reaching two thousand" — and contained the greater number of the city's working-people: (002-024)

> "These courts are usually narrow, muddy, badly ventilated, ill-drained, and lined with eight to twenty houses, which, by reason of having their rear walls in common, can usually be ventilated from one side only. In the background, within the court, there is usually an ash heap or something of the kind, the filth of which cannot be described." (002-024)

On the other hand, the report observes that "the newer courts are more sensibly built and more decently kept," and that the cottages were "much less crowded than in [[manchester]] and [[liverpool]], wherefore Birmingham shows even during the reign of an epidemic a far smaller mortality than, for instance, [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]], [[dudley|Dudley]], and [[bilston|Bilston]]." Cellar [[housing|dwellings]], one of the most notorious features of [[housing|working-class housing]] in [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]], were "unknown" in Birmingham, though a few cellars were misused as workrooms. (002-024)

The lodging-houses for [[proletariat]] were "rather numerous," over four hundred, chiefly in courts in the heart of the town. Engels describes them as "nearly all disgustingly filthy and ill-smelling, the refuge of beggars, thieves, tramps, and prostitutes, who eat, [[drunkenness|drink]], smoke, and sleep here without the slightest regard to comfort or decency in an atmosphere endurable to these degraded beings only." (002-024)

### The Metal Trades

Birmingham's economic character is discussed in Chapter 7, "The Remaining Branches of Industry." Engels identifies Birmingham as the headquarters of the metal-wares industry, where "the finer metal goods of all sorts are produced." Unlike the [[factory-system|factory]] system of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], the metal trades in Birmingham retained something of the old handicraft character: (007-009)

> "The small employers are still to be found, who work with their apprentices in the shop at home, or when they need steam-power, in great factory buildings which are divided into little shops, each rented to a small employer."

Engels describes this system as a midway position between the genuine [[proletariat]] and the [[bourgeoisie]]:

> "These small employers are neither genuine proletarians, since they live in part upon the work of their apprentices, nor genuine bourgeois, since their principal means of support is their own work. This peculiar midway position of the Birmingham ironworkers is to blame for their having so rarely joined wholly and unreservedly in the English labour movements. Birmingham is a politically radical, but not a Chartist, town." (007-009)

However, Engels also notes that larger capitalist-owned factories existed in Birmingham, and in these the [[factory-system]] "reigns supreme," producing all the same ills — the [[division-of-labour]] carried to extreme detail, the employment of vast numbers of women and children, the [[crowding|crowding out]] of adult men from employment, and the physical deterioration of the [[proletariat]]. (007-009)

### The Working Population

Engels reports the testimony of a recruiting surgeon who stated:

> "The working men are small, delicate, and of very slight physical power; many of them deformed, too, in the chest or spinal column." (007-009)

According to the assertion of a recruiting sergeant, "the people of Birmingham are smaller than those anywhere else, being usually 5 feet 4 to 5 inches tall." Out of 613 recruits, only 238 were found fit for military ser[[prostitution|vice]]. (007-009)

Regarding education, Engels reports that more than half the children between five and fifteen years in Birmingham attended no school whatsoever, and that those who did attended so irregularly that it was "impossible to give them any training of an enduring kind." (007-009)

## Statistics

- **Population 1801**: 73,000
- **Population 1844**: 200,000
- **Courts**: approximately 2,000, containing the majority of the working population (002-024)
- **Lodging-houses for proletarians**: over 400 (002-024)
- **Children not attending school**: more than half of those aged 5–15 (007-009)
- **Military fitness**: 238 of 613 recruits fit for ser[[prostitution|vice]] (007-009)
