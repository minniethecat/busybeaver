# Francis Sharp

## Life

Francis Sharp was a Member of the Royal College of Surgeons practising in [[leeds|Leeds]], [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], during the 1820s and 1830s. He served as a surgeon at the [[leeds|Leeds]] hospital and was one of several medical practitioners who gave evidence before the Central Commission of the Factories' Inquiry Commission in 1833. Before moving to [[leeds|Leeds]], he had practised in Scarborough, and the contrast between the health of children in the seaside town and those in the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts formed one basis of his medical testimony. (006-017, 006-023)

Little is recorded of his personal life beyond his professional activities. He appears alongside a surgeon named Beaumont of [[bradford|Bradford]], with whom he jointly gave evidence on the effects of [[factory-system|factory]] labour on children's bodies. Sharp's testimony is distinguished by the clinical specificity with which he described the skeletal deformities he encountered in his [[leeds|Leeds]] practice — deformities he had never seen before coming to the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts. (006-017, 006-019)

## Role in Engels's Account

Engels cites Sharp's testimony as authoritative medical evidence of the physical damage inflicted by the [[factory-system]] on working-class children. In his chapter on "Single Branches of Industry," Engels introduces Sharp immediately after describing the brutal conditions in textile mills — conditions exacerbated by the intensification of [[machinery]] and [[competition]] among manufacturers — using Sharp's clinical observations to demonstrate the physiological consequences of "long working-hours":

> "This distortion usually consists of a curving of the spinal column and legs, and is described as follows by Francis Sharp, M.R.C.S., of Leeds: 'I never saw the peculiar bending of the lower ends of the thigh bones before I came to Leeds... I was soon led to change my opinion in consequence of the mass of patients who presented themselves at the hospital... Thus far I have seen about a hundred such cases, and can, most decidedly, express the opinion that they are the consequences of overwork.'" (006-017)

Sharp distinguished these deformities from rachitis (rickets), noting that they appeared in children aged fourteen to eighteen — an age group not typically susceptible to rickets — and that the condition "had first appeared after children began to work in the mills." He recorded approximately one hundred cases of bent thigh bones and "not less than three hundred" cases of spinal curvature, all in mill children who "themselves attributed the evil to this cause." (006-017)

Engels later returns to Sharp's testimony, citing his general observations on the overall health of mill children compared with those raised outside [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts:

> "When I moved from Scarborough to Leeds, I was at once struck by the fact that the general appearance of the children was much paler, and their fibre less vigorous here than in Scarborough and its environs... I have met with numberless cases of scrofula, lung trouble, mesenteric affections, and indigestion, concerning which I, as a medical man, have no doubt that they arose from mill work." (006-023)

Sharp concluded that "the nervous energy of the body is weakened by the long hours, and the foundation of many diseases laid," adding the stark observation: "If people from the country were not constantly coming in, the race of mill-hands would soon be wholly degenerate." (006-023)

Engels groups Sharp's evidence with that of other medical professionals — including Dr. Ray, Surgeon Beaumont of [[bradford|Bradford]], Sir David Barry, and Dr. Loudon — to build an overwhelming medical case against the [[factory-system|factory]] system. The cumulative weight of these surgeons' testimony, Engels argues, demonstrates that the physical deformities of [[child-labour|factory children]] were not incidental but systemic, the direct and predictable consequence of the [[bourgeoisie]]'s pursuit of profit. For Engels, Sharp's clinic in [[leeds]] was a laboratory of the [[proletariat]]'s physical degradation — proof that the [[factory-system]] did not merely exploit [[labour-movement|labour]], but deformed human bodies. (006-017, 006-019)
