# Jeremy Bentham

## Life

Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) was an English philosopher, jurist, and social reformer who founded the doctrine of utilitarianism — the principle that the moral worth of an action is determined by its contribution to the greatest happiness of the greatest number. His major works included *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789) and numerous writings on legal reform, prison design (the Panopticon), political economy, and constitutional theory.

Bentham's influence extended far beyond academic philosophy. His ideas inspired the "Philosophical Radicals" — a group of politicians and intellectuals, including [[james-mill|James Mill]] and [[john-stuart-mill|John Stuart Mill]], who advocated parliamentary reform, legal codification, and laissez-faire economic policy. By the 1840s, Bentham's utilitarianism had become a significant intellectual current within both the Radical bourgeoisie and the emerging [[labour-movement|working-class movement]].

## Role in Engels's Account

Bentham shares a single paragraph with [[william-godwin|William Godwin]] in Engels's text, in a passage discussing the intellectual and literary culture of the English [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels argues that the proletariat, far from being ignorant or uncultivated, had become the principal audience for the most advanced philosophical and literary works of the age:

> "The two great practical philosophers of latest date, Bentham and Godwin, are, especially the latter, almost exclusively the property of the proletariat; for though Bentham has a school within the Radical bourgeoisie, it is only the proletariat and the Socialists who have succeeded in developing his teachings a step forward." (008-039)

The distinction Engels draws is significant. Unlike [[william-godwin|Godwin]], whose radicalism was too extreme for the post-revolutionary bourgeoisie, Bentham retained a "school within the Radical bourgeoisie." The middle-class Radicals — the advocates of [[free-trade|Free Trade]], parliamentary reform, and utilitarian legislation — claimed Bentham as their intellectual forebear. But Engels insists that it was "only the proletariat and the Socialists" who had actually advanced Bentham's thought.

This is a dialectical claim: the bourgeoisie adopts Bentham's conclusions but not his critical method; the [[proletariat|working class]] takes up the method and extends it beyond the limits the bourgeoisie is willing to tolerate. Bentham's greatest-happiness principle, applied consistently, points toward the abolition of class society — a conclusion that the bourgeois Benthamites refused to draw.

## Context

The passage in which Bentham appears (008-039) is one of Engels's most optimistic assessments of working-class intellectual culture. He describes workers attending lectures "upon scientific, aesthetic, and economic subjects," studying [[percy-bysshe-shelley|Shelley]] and [[lord-byron|Byron]], and reading translations of the French materialist philosophers. This cultural vitality contrasts sharply with the moral and physical degradation Engels has documented in preceding chapters: the proletariat, crushed by the [[factory-system|factory]] system and the [[housing|slums]], has nonetheless produced an independent intellectual life that surpasses the bourgeoisie's "castrated editions" and "hypocritical morality."

Bentham's presence in this narrative serves a specific purpose. The fact that a major bourgeois philosopher is now read principally by workers demonstrates the historical trajectory Engels [[poverty|want]]s to establish: the bourgeoisie has abandoned its own Enlightenment heritage, and the [[proletariat|working class]] has become its true inheritor.

## Legacy

Bentham's utilitarianism has had an immense and lasting influence on legal theory, public policy, and moral philosophy. Engels's observation that the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]] adopted and radicalised Bentham's ideas points to a neglected dimension of Bentham's reception history: the role of utilitarian thought in the development of working-class politics. The "greatest happiness" principle, taken seriously, had implications that Bentham's middle-class followers resisted but that the Socialists and Chartists were willing to explore.
