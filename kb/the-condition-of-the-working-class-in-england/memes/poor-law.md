# Poor Law

## Definition

The Poor Law, in the England Engels describes, was the body of legislation governing public relief of [[poverty|destitution]]. The Old Poor Law, dating from the Elizabethan era, had provided a patchwork of parish-based relief — outdoor [[wages|payment]]s to the able-bodied poor, supplemented by workhouses for the incapacitated. The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 abolished outdoor relief for the able-bodied, centralised administration under elected Boards of Guardians, and made the workhouse the sole form of assistance — a reform explicitly designed, in the words of its architect Edwin Chadwick, to make the condition of the [[pauperism|pauper]] "less eligible" than that of the worst-paid independent labourer. Engels calls it "the most open declaration of war of the bourgeoisie upon the proletariat" (011-009).

## Engels's Analysis

> The Poor Law is the expression of the Malthusian theory of population. (010-004)

Engels devotes sustained attention to the Poor Law in the book's closing chapters, treating it as the condensation of bourgeois political economy into legal form. The [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian premise — that poverty resulted from overpopulation, that the poor bred irresponsibly, and that any relief that made them more comfortable would only encourage them to produce more children — provided the intellectual justification for abolishing outdoor relief. The workhouse, with its penal discipline, separation of sexes, and deliberately inadequate diet, was the institutional expression of this theory. The law did not aim to relieve poverty but to deter it — to make the condition of the [[pauperism|pauper]] so miserable that workers would accept any wage, however low, rather than seek relief. (010-004)

The implementation of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] provoked the most intense resistance of any nineteenth-century social legislation. In the industrial north, where cyclical unemployment made the old system of outdoor relief essential to working-class survival, the law was met with mass meetings, petitions, and in some cases direct action against the construction of workhouses. The anti-Poor Law movement merged with [[chartism|Chartism]], and the demand for repeal of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] became one of the central planks of the working-class political programme. Engels treats this resistance as evidence that the workers understood the class character of the law — that they recognised it not as a measure of social improvement but as an act of class war. (010-002)

> The intent of the law is too loudly outspoken for there to be any mistake about it. (011-013)

The [[poor-law-commission|Poor Law Commissioners]]' own reports provide Engels with some of his most damning evidence. He quotes extensively from their documentation of workhouse conditions — the inadequate diet, the separation of husbands from wives, the employment of workhouse children in factories — to demonstrate that the system was functioning exactly as intended. When inmates died of starvation in the Greenwich and Coventry workhouses, the coroners' juries ruled these deaths to be the result of "gross neglect" — but Engels insists that the neglect was systemic, not individual. A law whose operating principle was to make relief worse than starvation wages was, by design, a law that killed. (011-012, 011-013)

## Historical Context

The Poor Law Amendment Act of 1834 was the product of the Royal Commission into the Operation of the Poor Laws, chaired by [[nassau-william-senior|Nassau William Senior]] and shaped intellectually by Edwin Chadwick, a [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite reformer who had served as [[jeremy-bentham|Jeremy Bentham]]'s secretary. The Commission's report combined [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian population theory with [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]ite administrative rationalism: centralise administration, abolish outdoor relief, make the workhouse the sole form of assistance, and apply the principle of less eligibility. The Act was one of the most consequential pieces of social legislation in British history, remaining substantially in force until the early twentieth century.

Engels's critique of the Poor Law draws also on the radical tradition of William Cobbett and the anti-Poor Law movement. Cobbett had denounced the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] as a "robbery of the poor" and had called for the restoration of the Elizabethan system. Engels goes further, arguing that even the Old Poor Law — with its parish-based outdoor relief — was preferable to the New, but that neither addressed the structural cause of poverty, which was the capitalist mode of production itself. Reform of the Poor Law was necessary but insufficient; abolition of the system that made [[pauperism|pauper]]s of workers was the only genuine solution.
