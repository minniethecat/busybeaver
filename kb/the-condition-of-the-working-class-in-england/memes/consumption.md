## Definition

Consumption (phthisis, later tuberculosis) was the nineteenth century's most feared wasting disease. Engels documents it as the characteristic affliction of the industrial [[proletariat|working class]], particularly among textile operatives, metalworkers, and other trades involving dust inhalation, sedentary postures, and prolonged [[working-day|working hours]]. In Engels's analysis, consumption is not a misfortune of nature but a direct product of capitalist production: the [[factory-system|factory]] system literally consumes the bodies of the workers who sustain it.

## Engels's Analysis

### Occupational Causation

Engels meticulously traces consumption to specific industrial occupations. Among textile workers, "the dust of the cotton enters the lungs and causes bronchitis and consumption" (007-007). The disease was so prevalent in certain trades that Engels can state flatly that workers "usually die of consumption after suffering the severest pain" (007-004). The causation is specific and documented: spindle-grinders inhaling metallic and stone dust, lace-makers bent over pillows compressing their chests in positions that "engender consumption in many cases" (007-011), file-cutters stooping over work that produces "all the usual symptoms of consumption of the lungs" (007-012).

Engels's method is epidemiological before the term existed: he traces disease clusters to specific occupations, working postures, and workshop conditions, demonstrating that consumption was not capriciously distributed but followed the geography of industrial production.

### The Life Course of the Consumptive Worker

Engels traces the typical trajectory of the consumptive [[factory-system|factory]] operative. Mill-girls entering [[factory-system|factory]] work in childhood develop spinal deformities, scrofula, and "the health of the female lace-makers is ruined from the earliest age; they usually die of consumption" (007-003). The disease does not strike suddenly but progresses through a predictable sequence: chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats, haemoptysis, and death, typically before the age of thirty. Engels notes that "many of them die of consumption" (007-015) and that "consumption usually soon ends the sad life" (007-019) of those too weakened to continue working.

### Underconsumption and Undernourishment

The economic meaning of "consumption" and the disease meaning converge in Engels's analysis. Workers cannot consume adequate [[diet|food]] because wages are driven to [[diet|subsistence]] minimum: "the consumption of meat had been decidedly less in the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts than previously" (003-013). This [[diet|nutrition]]al deprivation weakens resistance to the disease. Engels connects the "hectic appearance of great cities" (005-005) — "hectic" being the clinical term for consumptive flushing — to the broader pattern of capitalist underconsumption.

### Housing and Contagion

Overcrowded housing provided the medium for consumption's spread. Engels documents how "especially unwholesome is the living in damp [[housing|dwellings]]" (006-038), noting that "the worst cases end in consumption." The damp, unventilated, overcrowded rooms of working-class districts — where entire families slept in single rooms with boarded-up windows to avoid the window-tax — created ideal conditions for the airborne transmission of tubercle bacilli, a mechanism Engels intuited though the microbiology was unknown in his time.

### Social Murder

Engels's cumulative portrait of consumption rises to the charge of "social murder." When society knows that certain occupations, wages, and housing conditions create fatal disease and does nothing to change them, it is complicit in each death. The manufacturer who exposes workers to dust, the landlord who rents damp cellars, the legislator who refuses [[factory-system|factory]] inspection — all are accessories to the consumption that kills thousands of workers annually. Engels's documentation of this single disease thus becomes an indictment of the entire social order.
