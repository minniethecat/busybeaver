# Surplus Population

## Definition

The surplus population, also termed the "industrial reserve army," is the permanently unemployed or underemployed portion of the [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels argues that this population is not an accident or a temporary disruption but a structural necessity of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism: machinery continually displaces workers, creating a pool of unemployed labour that can be drawn into production during booms and discarded during busts, while its very existence depresses wages for the employed. (P04-009, 003-001)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels traces the emergence of the surplus population to the introduction of machinery. Each new machine — the [[machinery|spinning-jenny]], the power-loom, the throstle — rendered a category of skilled hand-workers redundant. The hand-weaver who had spent years learning his craft found that a machine operated by an unskilled teenager could produce more cloth in a day than he could in a week. His skill, once his livelihood, became worthless. He had no choice but to join the mass of displaced workers seeking any employment at any wage. (P04-009)

The surplus population serves a double function in the capitalist system. During periods of expansion, it provides a ready supply of labour that can be absorbed into production without bidding up wages — manufacturers can expand output without competing for scarce workers. During periods of contraction, the surplus population expands, absorbing the workers discharged from factories and serving as a visible warning to the employed: resist a wage cut, and you join them. Engels observes that this "reserve army" is deliberately maintained by the bourgeoisie as a disciplinary mechanism. (003-001)

The Irish immigrant population forms a disproportionately large share of the surplus population in English industrial towns. Engels argues that Irish immigration served as a safety valve for English industry — when native workers became too expensive or too restive, employers simply imported Irish labourers who, having grown up in conditions of extreme privation, would accept lower wages and worse conditions. The Irish "reserve" depressed wages for the entire [[proletariat|working class]] and provided the bourgeoisie with a ready supply of strike-breakers. (002-005)
