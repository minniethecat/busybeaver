# Farming

## Definition

Farming, in the England Engels describes, was in the midst of a profound transformation. The old system — in which tenant farmers employed a permanent workforce of labourers who lived in tied cottages, supplemented their wages from small allotments, and maintained a measure of patriarchal relationship with their employer — was giving way to a new system of capitalist agriculture. The farmer was becoming a businessman who calculated profit and loss, employed labour only when needed, and used the threat of the workhouse to discipline his workforce. The labourer was becoming a proletarian — landless, casualised, and subject to the same competitive pressures that Engels documents in the [[urban|factory town]]s. (010-002)

## Engels's Analysis

> He gradually abandoned his farming and gave his whole time to weaving. (P04-006)

In the book's Introduction, Engels describes the historical process by which the expansion of domestic industry — spinning and weaving in rural cottages — initially supplemented and then gradually supplanted [[agriculture|farming]] as the primary occupation of the rural population. The yeoman farmer of the pre-industrial period had combined agriculture with domestic manufacture, growing his own [[diet|food]] and spinning his own yarn, selling his surplus at local markets. The expansion of the [[cottage-system|putting-out system]] transformed him first into a weaver who also farmed, and then into a weaver who abandoned [[agriculture|farming]] altogether — a process that made him entirely dependent on the merchant-capitalist for his [[diet|subsistence]]. This separation of the rural population from the land was the precondition for the creation of the industrial proletariat. (P04-006)

> So long as this lasted, the poverty of the farm hands was less conspicuous. (010-002)

The condition of the agricultural worker is the subject of one of the book's most detailed chapters. Engels describes a sector in which the old patriarchal relations had broken down without being replaced by anything better. The farmer, squeezed between falling prices and fixed rents, economised on labour — employing workers only for the harvest, paying the lowest possible wages, and replacing permanent labourers with the gang system, in which a contractor supplied gangs of women and children for seasonal work. The agricultural labourer, unlike the [[factory-system|factory]] operative, was isolated — scattered across the [[rural|countryside]] in isolated cottages and hamlets, lacking the concentration that made trade union organisation possible. (010-002)

The contrast between the agricultural labourer of the south and east of England and the [[factory-system|factory]] operative of the industrial north is one of the book's most striking comparative analyses. The [[factory-system|factory]] operative was worse housed and breathed worse air, but he was concentrated in towns where organisation was possible and where the collective experience of exploitation produced a collective response. The agricultural labourer was better housed and breathed cleaner air, but he was isolated, dependent on a single employer, and trapped in a system — the tied cottage, the gang system, the game laws — from which individual escape was impossible. Engels's conclusion is nuanced: neither condition was acceptable, but the [[factory-system|factory]] operative had at least the possibility of collective resistance, which the agricultural labourer lacked. (010-003)

## Historical Context

The transformation of English [[agriculture|farming]] that Engels describes was part of a longer process of agricultural capitalism that had begun with the enclosures of the eighteenth century. The enclosure of common lands had deprived the rural population of the resources — grazing for a cow, fuel from the common, a plot for potatoes — that had previously supplemented wages and provided a measure of independence. The agricultural labourer of the 1840s was the descendant of the cottager who had lost his common rights, the yeoman who had sold his land, and the domestic worker who had been displaced by machinery.

The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] in 1846 — the year after Engels published his book — would intensify the commercialisation of English [[agriculture|farming]] by exposing it to competition from imported grain. The subsequent decades would see the further proletarianisation of the agricultural labour force, the consolidation of farm holdings, and the depopulation of the [[rural|countryside]] — processes that Engels's analysis had anticipated.
