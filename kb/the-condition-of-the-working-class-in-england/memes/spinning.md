# Spinning

## Definition

Spinning, in the context of Engels's analysis, refers to the mechanical transformation of raw cotton into thread — the industrial process that inaugurated the [[factory-system|factory]] system in England and created the first concentrated industrial proletariat. Engels devotes significant attention to the spinning industry because it was the site of the decisive technological innovations — Hargreaves's [[machinery|spinning-jenny]], Arkwright's water-frame, and Crompton's mule — that broke the bottleneck of hand-spinning, concentrated production in factories, and established the social relations of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism that would spread to every other branch of manufacture. (P04-003, P04-006)

## Engels's Analysis

> Before the introduction of machinery, the spinning and weaving of raw materials was carried on in the workingman's house. He and his family spun the yarn — the wife and daughters at the spinning-wheel, the husband at the loom or at the jenny. (P04-003)

Engels opens his history of the industrial proletariat with a description of the [[cottage-system|domestic system]] that preceded the [[factory-system|factory]]. Before 1760, spinning was a household occupation. The weaver-farmer and his family cultivated a small plot of land and spun and wove in their cottage, producing for a local market. The work was hard but the family was independent: it owned its tools, set its own pace, and retained the product of its labour. The introduction of machinery shattered this world. (P04-003, P04-006)

> Hargreaves's spinning-jenny carried sixteen or eighteen spindles instead of one, and was worked by a single person. The product was therefore multiplied, and the price of yarn fell. (P04-006)

The [[machinery|spinning-jenny]] (1764) was the first machine to break the bottleneck of hand-spinning. Where the spinning-wheel turned one spindle at a time, the jenny turned sixteen or eighteen, multiplying output per worker by an order of magnitude. Arkwright's water-frame (1769) took the process further by harnessing water-power to drive the spindles, making the jenny obsolete and creating the first factories — great buildings erected on streams, where hundreds of workers tended [[machinery|machines]] they did not own. Crompton's mule (1779) combined the principles of the jenny and the water-frame to produce finer, stronger thread than either alone. These three inventions, Engels argues, did more to transform English society than any political revolution. (P04-006, P04-008)

> The capitalists began to set up spinning jennies in great buildings and to use water-power for driving them, so that they could do with fewer hands than before. (P04-008)

The consequence of mechanised spinning was the concentration of production in factories and the proletarianisation of the spinning workforce. The hand-spinner, who had worked at home with a spinning-wheel he owned, could not compete with the [[factory-system|factory]]'s water-driven mule. He was forced to abandon his cottage and follow the work into the mill, where he became a [[wages|wage-labour]]er tending a machine he would never own. The spinning industry thus produced, earlier and more completely than any other, the social type that would define the nineteenth century: the [[factory-system|factory]] hand, propertyless, concentrated in great numbers, and wholly dependent on the capitalist for the means of [[diet|subsistence]]. (P04-008, 006-003)

> The power-loom has reduced hand-weaving to the condition of hand-spinning, and is driving the hand-weaver, step by step, out of the field. (006-003)

By the 1830s, the [[machinery|mechanisation]] that had transformed spinning was arriving in weaving. The power-loom, perfected in the 1820s, did to the hand-weaver what the mule had done to the hand-spinner a generation earlier. Engels notes the grim symmetry: the same process that created the [[factory-system|factory]] proletariat in spinning was now creating a surplus population of displaced hand-weavers, who swelled the reserve army of labour and competed with the [[factory-system|factory]] hands for employment. The spinning industry thus established the pattern that would repeat across every branch of manufacture: [[machinery|mechanisation]] → concentration → proletarianisation → [[surplus-population|surplus labour]] → immiseration. (006-003, 006-004)

> The work is wholly uninteresting — watching the threads to see that none break, and piecing them when they do — and requires no thought whatever. (006-006)

The machine, Engels observes, did not merely concentrate workers in factories; it transformed the character of their labour. The hand-spinner was a craftsman who understood the entire process from raw cotton to finished thread. The [[factory-system|factory]] spinner was a machine-minder whose work consisted of watching for broken threads and tying them together — a repetitive, mindless task that could be performed as well by a child of nine as by an adult. This degradation of labour — from craft to machine-tending — is, for Engels, one of the most insidious consequences of industrialisation: the worker is not merely exploited but deskilled, reduced to an appendage of the machine. (006-006, 006-007)

## Historical Context

The [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] industry was the largest [[manufacture|manufacturing]] sector in the world by the 1830s, employing hundreds of thousands of workers and exporting thread and cloth to every continent. [[manchester|Manchester]], the centre of the [[cotton-industry|cotton trade]], was the city Engels knew best and the subject of his most detailed urban analysis in Chapter 2. The spinning industry served as Engels's central case study because it was the first to undergo full [[machinery|mechanisation]], the most thoroughly concentrated, and the best documented — and because it was the industry whose workers formed the vanguard of the labour movement.
