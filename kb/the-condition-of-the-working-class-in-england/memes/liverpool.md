# Liverpool

## Overview

Liverpool, on the Mersey estuary in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], was one of the great port cities created by the industrial revolution. Alongside [[manchester]], it was a "giant city" that had been called into existence by the cotton industry, and the two cities together contained some 700,000 inhabitants by the mid-1840s. The first passenger railway in the world opened between Liverpool and [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1830, cementing the connection between the port and the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] hinterland. (001-004)

Engels describes Liverpool as a city of striking contrasts — immense commercial wealth and grandeur on the one hand, and some of the most wretched [[housing|working-class housing]] conditions in England on the other. As a port, Liverpool also had one of the largest Irish immigrant populations of any English city, which Engels discusses in detail in his chapter on [[irish-immigration]]. (002-023, 004-001)

## Engels's Account

### Cellar Dwellings and Courts

In his survey of the [[urban|great town]]s, Engels gives a particularly damning account of Liverpool's [[housing|working-class housing]]. He writes:

> "Liverpool, with all its commerce, wealth, and grandeur yet treats its workers with the same barbarity. A full fifth of the population, more than 45,000 human beings, live in narrow, dark, damp, badly-ventilated cellar dwellings, of which there are 7,862 in the city." (002-023)

Beyond the cellar [[housing|dwellings]], Engels reports that Liverpool contained 2,270 courts — "small spaces built up on all four sides and having but one entrance, a narrow, covered passageway, the whole ordinarily very dirty and inhabited exclusively by [[proletariat]]." These courts were characteristic of the worst [[housing|working-class housing]] in industrial England and are described at greater length in Engels's treatment of [[manchester]]. (002-023)

The depth of Liverpool's housing crisis is captured in Engels's stark summation:

> "Liverpool, with all its commerce, wealth, and grandeur yet treats its workers with the same barbarity." (002-023)

### Irish Immigration

Liverpool was a principal entry point for Irish immigrants to England. Engels reports that out of the million-plus Irish who had emigrated to England by the 1840s, Liverpool contained 34,000 poor Irish people — the third-largest concentration after [[london|London]] (120,000) and [[manchester]] (40,000). These immigrants, Engels argues, "having grown up almost without civilisation, accustomed from youth to every sort of privation, rough, intemperate, and improvident," formed the lowest stratum of the urban working-class and exerted downward pressure on the [[wages]] and living standards of native English workers. (004-001)

The prevalence of cellar [[housing|dwellings]] in Liverpool was closely connected to Irish immigration. Engels observes that "the majority of the families who live in cellars are almost everywhere of Irish origin," and that the Irish, having "discovered the minimum of the necessities of life," competed with English workers on terms that forced the general level of working-class existence downward. (004-001)

### Mortality

Liverpool's death-rate was among the highest of any English city. According to mortality tables Engels cites from the *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* of July 31, 1844, the death-rate in Liverpool was one in 31.90 including the suburb of West [[derby|Derby]], and one in 29.90 excluding it — meaning that roughly one person in thirty died each year, compared to the national average of one in forty-five. (005-013)

Engels also provides striking data on class-differentiated life expectancy in Liverpool for 1840:

> "In Liverpool, in 1840, the average longevity of the upper-classes, gentry, professional men, etc., was thirty-five years; that of the business men and better-placed handicraftsmen, twenty-two years; and that of the operatives, day-labourers, and serviceable class in general, but fifteen years." (005-016)

This means that the average Liverpool labourer lived less than half as long as a member of the gentry — a statistic Engels presents as evidence of the "social murder" committed by the capitalist system against the working-class.

The epidemics that ravaged the working-class districts of the [[urban|great town]]s were especially severe in Liverpool. Engels reports:

> "In Manchester and Liverpool, epidemics are three times more fatal than in country districts; affections of the nervous system are quintupled, and stomach troubles trebled, while deaths from affections of the lungs in cities are to those in the country as 2.5 to 1. Fatal cases of smallpox, measles, scarlet fever, and whooping cough, among small children, are four times more frequent." (005-006)

Despite Liverpool having a similar population to [[manchester|Manchester]], the mortality from [[cholera|typhus]] could reach eight percent during outbreaks in those cities, while in [[glasgow]] the mortality during the 1843 epidemic reached thirty-two percent. (005-006)

## Statistics

- **Cellar [[housing|dwellings]]**: 7,862, housing more than 45,000 people (one-fifth of the population) (002-023)
- **Courts**: 2,270, exclusively inhabited by proletarians (002-023)
- **Irish population**: 34,000 (004-001)
- **[[mortality|Mortality]] rate**: one in 31.90 (including West [[derby|Derby]]); one in 29.90 (excluding West [[derby|Derby]]) (005-013)
- **Average longevity (1840)**: gentry and professionals: 35 years; business class: 22 years; operatives and labourers: 15 years (005-016)
