# Disease

## Definition

In *The Condition of the Working Class in England*, disease is not treated as a medical category but as a social indicator — the physical manifestation, measurable and inescapable, of the degradation produced by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. Engels catalogues the diseases endemic to the [[proletariat|working class]] — [[cholera|typhus]], typhoid, consumption ([[consumption|tuberculosis]]), scrofula, rickets, and intermittent [[cholera|fever]]s — and argues through the testimony of physicians, [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors, and poor-law commissioners that these diseases arise not from individual weakness or moral failing but from the "unnatural" conditions of industrial life: overcrowded [[housing|dwellings]], putrid air and water, adulterated [[diet|food]], and labour that exhausts the body before it is fully formed. (P01-005, 005-003)

## Engels's Analysis

> What is true of London, is true of Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, is true of all great towns. Everywhere barbarous indifference, hard-hearted egotism on the one hand, and nameless misery on the other, everywhere social warfare, every man's house in a state of siege, everywhere reciprocal plundering under the protection of the law, and all so shameless, so openly avowed that one shrinks from the consequences of our social state and their open expression. (002-022)

Engels opens his discussion of urban disease with a damning diagnosis of the [[urban|great town]]s themselves. The cities that the bourgeoisie celebrates as monuments to British industry are, in Engels's description, breeding-grounds of disease — not despite the wealth they produce but because of how that wealth is distributed. The same class that owns the mills owns the cottages in which the mill-hands live; the same economic logic that drives wages to [[diet|subsistence]] level drives housing to its most profitable — and therefore most crowded — form. [[cholera|Cholera]], [[cholera|typhus]], and consumption are the inevitable products of this arrangement. (002-022, 002-026)

> To these and other sources of disease must be added that pigs were kept in the houses, and that the dung-heaps and refuse were allowed to accumulate before the doors. (002-055)

The physical environment of the working-class quarters supplied the conditions for [[cholera|epidemic disease]]. Engels documents — street by street, court by court — the absence of drainage, the accumulation of refuse, the keeping of livestock in human [[housing|dwellings]], and the proximity of privies to living quarters. In the courts of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s Old Town, he reports, the streets were never cleaned; slops and offal were thrown into open gutters that might be cleared only when it rained. Under such conditions, the wonder was not that disease spread but that anyone survived at all. (002-055, 002-072)

> All these evils — want, dirt, and disease — combine to produce a general depression of the vital powers, and a premature old age among the working classes. (002-072)

Engels documents a predictable sequence: [[crowding|overcrowding]] and poor ventilation weaken the constitution; inadequate and adulterated [[diet|food]] fail to sustain it; disease attacks it; and if the worker survives the acute illness, the chronic effects — "general depression of the vital powers" — persist. The worker at forty, Engels reports, is an old man; the working-class child who survives infancy is stunted and scrofulous. The bourgeoisie might attribute this to the workers' [[prostitution|vice]]s, but Engels insists that the causal arrow runs the other way: [[drunkenness|intemperance]] and improvidence are the consequences of disease and despair, not their causes. (002-072, 003-014)

> Another category of diseases arises directly from the food of the working-class, which is often totally indigestible, or at least indigestible for weakly stomachs. (005-008)

The workers' diet was not merely insufficient in quantity but actively harmful in quality. Engels cites reports of flour adulterated with gypsum and chalk, butter stretched with suet, tea extended with used tea-leaves dried and recoloured, and meat from diseased cattle sold to the poor after being rejected by middle-class customers. The adulteration of [[diet|food]] was a recognised trade — a profitable one, since the working-class consumer had no means of testing what he bought and no alternative market to turn to. The diseases of mal[[diet|nutrition]] — scrofula, rickets, consumption — were thus intimately connected to the commercial fraud that Engels treats as a natural expression of competitive capitalism. (005-008, 005-009)

> The wonder is that a contagious disease like this fever does not spread farther. But when an epidemic comes, it finds the ground prepared, and its ravages are fearful. (005-007)

Engels notes that [[cholera|epidemic disease]]s such as [[cholera|typhus]] and cholera were periodic in the [[urban|manufacturing town]]s, flaring up whenever a trade depression threw workers out of employment and into still worse housing and diet. The medical authorities — Dr. James Kay, Dr. [[peter-gaskell|Peter Gaskell]], the reports of the [[poor-law-commission|Poor Law Commissioners]] — all confirmed what Engels observed: the working-class districts were permanently seeded with disease, and every economic downturn translated directly into excess mortality. The bourgeoisie, Engels notes with bitter irony, regarded [[cholera|epidemic disease]] as an act of God, when it was in fact the predictable consequence of the system by which they profited. (005-007, 003-009)

## Historical Context

Engels's documentation of working-class disease belongs to the great nineteenth-century tradition of [[public-health|sanitary reform]] and social medicine, alongside the work of Edwin Chadwick, Dr. William Farr, and Dr. John Snow. His contribution was to connect the epidemiological data to the economic structure — to show that disease was not a sanitary problem amenable to drainage and ventilation alone but a class problem rooted in [[property|property relations]]. The 1848 [[public-health|Public Health]] Act, passed three years after Engels's book appeared, implemented some of the sanitary measures his evidence supported, while leaving the economic structure that generated disease entirely untouched.

## Related Concepts

- [[Mortality]] — the statistical expression of disease, closely tracked by Engels through parish registers and parliamentary returns
- [[Housing]] — the overcrowded, unventilated [[housing|dwellings]] where disease incubated
- [[Overcrowding]] — the spatial condition that compacted infection and prevented recovery
- [[Child Labour]] — the employment of children in factories, which stunted growth and predisposed them to disease
- [[Factory System]] — the mode of production whose hours, heat, dust, and posture produced specific occupational illnesses
- [[Pauperism]] — the condition of the unemployed and unemployable, among whom disease was most prevalent
