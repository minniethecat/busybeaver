## Overview

The People's Charter was the foundational political document of the [[chartist-movement|Chartist movement]], drafted in 1838 by the [[general-workingmens-association|London Working Men's Association]] under the leadership of [[william-lovett|William Lovett]]. It set out six demands for parliamentary reform: universal male suffrage, vote by secret ballot, equal electoral districts, [[wages|payment]] of members of Parliament, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and annual general elections.

The Charter represented the political awakening of the English [[proletariat|working class]] after the disappointment of the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill of 1832]], which had extended the franchise only to the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]es. For the Chartists, democratic reform was not an end in itself but the means by which the [[proletariat|working class]] could secure economic and social justice.

## The Six Points

The Charter's six demands were:

1. **Universal suffrage** — a vote for every man over twenty-one years of age, of sound mind, and not convicted of crime
2. **Vote by ballot** — to protect voters from employer intimidation
3. **Equal electoral districts** — to eliminate the gross disparities between rural and urban constituencies
4. **Payment of Members of Parliament** — so that working men could afford to serve
5. **Abolition of property qualifications** for MPs
6. **Annual Parliaments** — the most radical demand, intended to prevent corruption through frequent accountability

These demands were not novel individually; the innovation lay in bundling them into a single programme and linking them to a mass [[labour-movement|working-class movement]].

## Engels's Account

Engels treats the People's Charter as the political culmination of working-class resistance to [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. In his telling, the Charter emerged organically from the workers' experience of exploitation, following the formation of [[trade-unions|Trades Unions]] and earlier forms of collective action.

> "The proposed law is the People's Charter, which in form is purely political, and demands a democratic basis for the House of Commons. Chartism is of an essentially social nature, a class movement." [008-023]

Engels emphasises that for the workers, the Charter was never merely a constitutional document — it was a "knife and fork question":

> "The Charter means a good house, good food and drink, good wages, short working hours, and certainty of employment." [008-027]

This materialist reading is central to Engels's argument: the political demands of [[chartism|Chartism]] were driven by economic desperation. He reports that the Charter was adopted with enthusiasm at mass meetings:

> "In 1838 the first national petition for the People's Charter [was] adopted in Birmingham. In 1839 half a million signatures were obtained; the petition was rejected by the House of Commons." [008-028]

Engels documents the radicalisation that followed the rejection of the first petition, describing how a general strike was planned for the "sacred month" and how the movement's leaders — particularly [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]] — urged the workers to prepare for insurrection if necessary.

## The Charter in Engels's Political Framework

Engels regards the Charter as a transitional form. While he admires its mobilising power, he ultimately sees it as a stage that the [[proletariat|working class]] must pass through on the way to socialism. In the 1892 Preface, he reflects on how the Charter's demands were gradually absorbed by the bourgeoisie themselves:

> "And, practically, that horrid 'People's Charter' actually became the political programme of the very manufacturers who had opposed it to the last. 'The Abolition of the Property Qualification' and 'Vote by Ballot' are now the law of the land." [P01-010]

This co-option, in Engels's view, proved that political reform alone was insufficient — the workers needed economic transformation as well.
