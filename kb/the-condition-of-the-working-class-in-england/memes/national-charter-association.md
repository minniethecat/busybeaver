# National Charter Association

## Overview

The National Charter Association (NCA), founded in [[manchester|Manchester]] in July 1840, was the organisational backbone of [[chartism|Chartism]] during its peak years. It emerged from the failure of the first Chartist petition in 1839 and the subsequent imprisonment of many Chartist leaders. Unlike the [[general-workingmens-association|London Working Men's Association]], which was a small circle of skilled artisans, the NCA was conceived as a mass organisation with a national structure: paid memberships, local branches, elected delegates, a central executive, and regular conventions. At its height, the NCA claimed over forty thousand members organised in four hundred local branches. (008-028)

## Engels's Account

Engels observed the NCA during the most intense period of Chartist agitation (1842–1844) and his account reflects first-hand experience. He attended NCA meetings in [[manchester|Manchester]], read the *Northern Star* (the NCA's newspaper, which [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]] edited), and reported the movement's internal debates with the familiarity of a participant-observer. The NCA, in Engels's telling, was the political form that working-class consciousness took when it broke free of middle-class tutelage. (008-029)

> From the rules of the Chartist Association, nominally because this name had been forbidden. (008-031)

Engels documents the state's attempts to suppress the NCA — banning its name, arresting its leaders, breaking up its meetings — and the movement's resilience in response. When the NCA's public gatherings were prohibited, it reconstituted itself under new names. When O'Connor was imprisoned, the *Northern Star* continued publication. The NCA's experience demonstrated, for Engels, that the [[proletariat|working class]] could not achieve political power through the existing constitutional order because that order was designed to exclude it. The question was whether the [[proletariat|working class]] would draw the revolutionary conclusion or retreat into economic trade unionism. (008-031)

Engels is attentive to the NCA's financial organisation as well as its politics. The membership card — costing two pence — was both a revenue source and a badge of identity. The NCA's ability to sustain itself through working-class subscriptions, without patronage from middle-class reformers, was, in Engels's view, crucial evidence of the proletariat's capacity for self-government. This stood in deliberate contrast to the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], which was lavishly funded by manufacturers and whose "grassroots" organisation was a facade maintained by paid agents. (008-029)

## Activities

The NCA was the organising force behind the second Chartist petition of 1842, which carried over three million signatures and was presented to Parliament on a thirty-foot roll. When the petition was rejected, the NCA's leadership supported the general strike that swept through the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] textile districts in August 1842 — the so-called "[[plug-plot-riots|Plug Plot]]." Engels reports the arrests, trials, and transportations that followed with barely suppressed fury: the state's response to a political petition was to imprison its organisers and deport its supporters. (008-028)

The NCA also functioned as a mutual-aid organisation, providing legal defence for arrested members, relief for strikers' families, and a network of Chartist schools and reading rooms. These activities were, for Engels, as significant as the petitions and strikes: they demonstrated that the [[proletariat|working class]] was building the institutions of an alternative society within the shell of the existing one. (008-029)
