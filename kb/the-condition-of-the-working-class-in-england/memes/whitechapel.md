## Overview

Whitechapel was a densely populated working-class parish in East [[london|London]] during the 1840s. Adjoining [[bethnal-green|Bethnal Green]] and lying east of the Tower of [[london|London]], it housed a large population of casual labourers, dock workers, and street traders. Engels regarded Whitechapel as one of the worst slum districts not only in [[london|London]] but in the entire British Isles, placing it in the same category as [[st-giles|St. Giles]] in [[london|London]], the Liberties of [[dublin|Dublin]], and the wynds of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]].

## Engels's Account

### The East End

Engels describes Whitechapel and neighbouring [[bethnal-green|Bethnal Green]] as the core of [[london|London]]'s working-class East End:

> "The district lies east of the Tower in Whitechapel and Bethnal Green, where the greatest mass of London's working people are crowded together." [002-011]

The concentration of poverty in this area was extreme, with families packed into dilapidated tenements, narrow courts, and unventilated alleys — conditions that bred disease, crime, and premature death.

### Comparison with Other British Slums

In a sweeping comparison of urban degradation across the British Isles, Engels ranks Whitechapel among the very worst:

> "The lowest nooks of St. Giles and Whitechapel, the Liberties of Dublin, the wynds of Edinburgh..." [002-025]

This passage places Whitechapel at the bottom of an international hierarchy of urban squalor. By grouping Whitechapel with districts in [[dublin|Dublin]] and [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], Engels emphasises that the degradation of the [[proletariat|working class]] was not a localised phenomenon but a structural feature of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism across Britain and Ireland.

The comparison also serves Engels's rhetorical purpose: if such conditions existed in [[london|London]] — the wealthiest city in the world and the seat of the British Empire — then they represented a systemic failure of the social order, not isolated instances of misfortune.
