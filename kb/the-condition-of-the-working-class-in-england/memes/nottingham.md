# Nottingham

## Overview

Nottingham, situated in the East Midlands, was by the 1840s one of the principal centres of the English hosiery and lace industries. Its population had grown substantially on the back of the [[machinery|mechanisation]] of frame-knitting, producing stockings and lace for domestic and foreign markets. The introduction of the bobbin-net machine and other improvements in lace manufacture made Nottingham the headquarters of this trade, which supported some 200,000 persons nationally. Engels includes Nottingham among the [[urban|factory town]]s whose living conditions are broadly comparable to [[manchester|Manchester]], but he also records distinctive features: the notorious back-to-back housing, the truck system of [[truck-system|payment in goods]], and the horrific abuse of opiate-laced patent medicines to pacify infants. (P04-010, 002-024, 005-011)

## Engels's Account

### Housing Conditions

Engels reports that Nottingham contained about 11,000 houses in total, of which between 7,000 and 8,000 were built back to back with a rear party wall -- meaning that through-ventilation was impossible. A single privy commonly served several houses, and an investigation shortly before Engels wrote found that many rows of houses had been erected over shallow drains covered only by the floorboards of the ground floor. This style of construction, driven by the pressure to pack workers as densely as possible near the workshops, made the [[housing|dwellings]] insanitary even by the standards of the [[urban|factory town]]s. (002-024)

### The Lace and Hosiery Trades

Nottingham, together with [[derby|Derby]] and [[leicester|Leicester]], formed the stocking-weaving district of England. Engels notes that the stocking weavers worked exceptionally long hours for very low wages -- six or seven shillings a week for sixteen to eighteen hours of daily labour. The sudden expansion of the industry following the invention of the lace-frame and bobbin-net machine created a large proletariat of frame-knitters, winders, threaders, and lace-runners. The [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] reported that the long working-hours, combined with a sedentary life and the constant strain on the eyes, "usually enfeeble the whole frame, and especially the eyes." By the age of forty, nearly all wore spectacles. Children employed at spooling and hemming, often beginning at six or seven years of age, worked ten to twelve hours daily in small, close rooms, and it was "not uncommon for them to faint at their work." (007-002, P04-010)

### Moral Conditions

The irregular [[working-day|working hours]] of the lace trade -- where winders and threaders were summoned to factories at all hours, including night, depending on when frames became empty -- produced what Engels describes as "a multitude of physical and moral ills, especially early and unbridled sexual licence." Commissioner Grainger reported that "all the inhabitants of Nottingham, the police, the clergy, the manufacturers, the working-people, and the parents of the children are all unanimously of opinion that the present system of labour is a most fruitful source of immorality." The fact that threaders (mainly boys) and winders (usually girls) were called in at the same hours gave them "the finest opportunity to form improper connections." (007-003)

### Physical Deformity and Workplace Innovation

Engels records one positive detail about Nottingham: it was in this town that chairs were introduced in factories, allowing operatives to sit rather than stand for the entire working day. The result was that the characteristic deformities caused by prolonged standing -- bent knees, spinal curvature, and swollen joints -- disappeared among those who used seats, and the operatives ceased to object to the [[working-day|length of the working day]]. Engels uses this as evidence that the physical damage of [[factory-system|factory]] labour was not inherent to the work itself but was a consequence of employers' refusal to provide even the simplest accommodations. (006-021)

### Laudanum and Infant Mortality

One of the most shocking details in Engels's account of Nottingham concerns the use of Godfrey's Cordial, a patent medicine prepared with opiates. Women who worked at home, caring for their own and others' children, gave this preparation to infants to keep them quiet, "and, as many believe, to strengthen them." The Coroner of Nottingham testified before a Parliamentary Commission that a single apothecary had used thirteen hundredweight of laudanum in one year in preparing this cordial. Children treated in this way were "pale, feeble, wilted, and usually die before completing the second year." The practice was widespread in all the [[urban|great town]]s, but Nottingham supplied some of the most damning evidence. (005-011)
