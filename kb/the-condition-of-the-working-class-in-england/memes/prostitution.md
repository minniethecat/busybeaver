## Definition

Prostitution, in Engels's analysis, is the sale of sexual access for money, understood not as an individual moral transgression but as a social institution integral to [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] relations. Engels treats prostitution as the logical extension of the commodification of labour: just as the worker sells his or her productive capacity, the prostitute sells sexual access. But Engels goes further, arguing that the bourgeoisie's purchase of working-class women's bodies is "the most tangible form of the exploitation of the proletariat by the bourgeoisie," making visible what [[wages|wage-labour]] disguises — that capitalism is a system in which the possessing class consumes the bodies of the non-possessing class.

## Engels's Analysis

### The Economic Determination

Engels insists that prostitution is economically determined. Women enter prostitution not from moral depravity but from poverty: "Theft and prostitution form the chief means of [[diet|subsistence]] of the poorest class in the large towns" (002-027). Factory women's wages were so low that survival was impossible without supplementary income, and for many the only available supplement was prostitution. Engels cites the case of women who "betook themselves to a life of prostitution rather than submit to this tyranny" (006-055) — the tyranny being the [[factory-system|factory]] system itself. Prostitution was not an alternative to [[wages|wage-labour]] but an extension of it, the commodification of the body taken to its logical extreme.

### The Scale of the Phenomenon

Engels estimates the scale of prostitution in industrial cities with characteristic attention to numbers: "how many of the 40,000 prostitutes in [[london|London]] maintain themselves by this means?" (005-039). The figure of 40,000 — drawn from contemporary police estimates — is deployed as evidence not of working-class depravity but of bourgeois demand. Prostitution on this scale is a market, and a market requires buyers. Engels's analysis consistently redirects moral condemnation from the sellers to the buyers: the married bourgeois man who maintains a mistress, the [[manufacturer|factory owner]] who exploits his female operatives, the gentleman who visits the working-class districts after dark.

### The Spatial Dimension

Engels documents prostitution as a spatial phenomenon, concentrated in the same districts where the poorest workers lived. In the worst streets of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[london|London]], "thieves and the victims of prostitution indiscriminately huddled together" (002-009). The geography of prostitution mapped exactly onto the geography of poverty, confirming Engels's thesis that prostitution was not a moral choice but an economic necessity. The same districts that produced the highest mortality rates, the worst [[crowding|overcrowding]], and the lowest wages produced the highest concentration of prostitution — a convergence that Engels treats as systematic rather than coincidental.

### Prostitution and the Factory System

Factory work itself blurred into prostitution. Engels describes female operatives subjected to sexual exploitation by overseers and employers, with the threat of dismissal enforcing compliance. The [[factory-system|factory]] system created "youthful prostitution, beginning with persons of fourteen or fifteen years of age" (007-013) because it congregated young women in establishments under male authority, paid them wages insufficient for [[diet|subsistence]], and offered no protection against sexual coercion. Engels notes that in certain trades, "prostitution is almost epidemic among them" (007-004), the "them" being female [[factory-system|factory]] operatives for whom [[wages|wage-labour]] and sex-work were continuous rather than distinct.

### The Bourgeoisie's Complicity

Engels's most stinging accusation is directed at the bourgeoisie's hypocrisy. The same middle-class moralists who condemned working-class "vice" were the buyers who sustained the prostitution market. Engels documents "his full share in maintaining prostitution" (002-051) — the typical bourgeois man who sermonised about morality on Sunday and visited prostitutes on Saturday. Beyond individual hypocrisy, Engels identifies a structural complicity: the bourgeoisie as a class benefited from prostitution because it functioned as a safety-valve, channelling working-class discontent into individual degradation rather than collective resistance.

### Prostitution as Class Exploitation

In Engels's most radical formulation, prostitution is not an exception to capitalist social relations but their purest expression. "The labour of women entails the degradation of the female sex to the level of prostitution" (009-010). Wage-labour itself is a form of prostitution — the sale of the body for another's use — and sexual prostitution merely makes explicit what the wage-form conceals. This is not a metaphor for Engels but an analysis: the same logic that commodifies labour-power commodifies the body, and the bourgeois who buys twelve hours of a worker's time and the bourgeois who buys an hour of a prostitute's body are engaged in transactions of the same fundamental kind.
