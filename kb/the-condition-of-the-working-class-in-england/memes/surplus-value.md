# Surplus Value

## Definition

Surplus value, though the term was more fully theorised by [[karl-marx|Marx]] in *Das Kapital*, is already implicit in Engels's analysis of 1844. It is the difference between the value the worker produces during his working day and the value of his wage — the unpaid labour that the capitalist appropriates as profit. Engels presents this concept in a nascent form when he observes that the worker who reproduces the value of his wage in a few hours must then work additional hours whose product goes entirely to the capitalist. (P01-004)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels was not yet using the technical vocabulary of *Das Kapital* when he wrote *The Condition of the Working Class in England*, but the concept of surplus value is everywhere present in his descriptions of the wage relation. He notes that the [[factory-system|factory]] operative produces far more value each day than he receives in wages, and that this excess flows to the capitalist as profit. He observes that machinery, which should reduce the worker's toil, instead intensifies it — because the capitalist, having invested in machinery, must run it continuously to recover his investment, and because machinery enables the capitalist to employ cheaper labour (women and children) and to drive down piece-rates. (P01-004)

In the 1885 preface to the English edition, Engels explicitly connects his earlier work to [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s mature theory. He acknowledges that his 1844 book predates the full development of the theory of surplus value but argues that the empirical evidence he gathered in [[manchester|Manchester]] provided the factual foundation on which [[karl-marx|Marx]]'s later theoretical work was built. The [[factory-system|factory]] inspector reports, the medical testimony, the statistical tables — all of this was evidence of surplus value extraction, even if Engels had not yet named it. (P01-004)
