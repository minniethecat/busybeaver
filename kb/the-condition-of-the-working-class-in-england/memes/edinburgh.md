## Overview

Edinburgh, the capital of Scotland, appears in Engels's analysis primarily as an example of how even cities renowned for their physical beauty harboured extreme working-class squalor. With a population of approximately 160,000 in the 1840s, Edinburgh's "superb situation" — praised for its dramatic setting between hills and the Firth of Forth — did nothing to protect its working-class inhabitants from conditions comparable to those of [[london|London]]'s worst [[housing|slums]].

## Engels's Account

### The Contrast of Beauty and Misery

Engels opens his Edinburgh discussion with the city's aesthetic reputation:

> "Edinburgh, whose superb situation, which has won for it the name of the Modern Athens, seems to offer exceptional advantages for a more decent style of building" [002-021]

But he immediately qualifies this impression — the reality of working-class Edinburgh contradicted its external appearance.

### The Wynds

The medieval wynds (narrow alleyways) of Edinburgh's Old Town were, Engels argued, as bad as anything in [[london|London]]:

> "The wynds of Edinburgh are in many respects similar to Edinburgh, possessing the same wynds, the same tall buildings, six to eight stories, the same narrowness; the same lack of all provision for cleanliness and comfort." [002-025]

Drawing on medical literature, Engels cites:

> "In the Edinburgh Medical and Surgical Journal, Dr. Hennen gives a description of these wynds which fully corresponds with the accounts we have of the London slums." [002-022]

### Irish Immigration

Edinburgh had one of the largest Irish immigrant populations among British cities:

> "Thus there are in London, 120,000; in Manchester, 40,000; in Liverpool, 34,000; Bristol, 24,000; Glasgow, 40,000; Edinburgh, 29,000, poor Irish people." [004-001]

With 29,000 Irish residents, Edinburgh's Irish population was third only to [[london|London]] and [[glasgow|Glasgow]] among the cities Engels surveyed.

### Mortality and Disease

Edinburgh's mortality statistics compared unfavourably with English industrial cities:

> "in Edinburgh, in 1838–39, one in 29" [005-013]

This rate (1 death per 29 inhabitants annually) placed Edinburgh above [[manchester|Manchester]]'s rate of 1 in 32.72. Typhus outbreaks in the Scottish cities were also notably severe:

> "In Edinburgh and Glasgow it broke out in 1817, after the famine; in 1826 and 1827, after the commercial crisis; in 1837, it raged with extraordinary violence" [005-005]
