## Overview

Bristol is a historic seaport city on the River Avon in Somerset, southwestern England. By the 1840s it was one of England's major maritime commercial centres, which Engels identifies alongside Hull, [[liverpool|Liverpool]], and [[london|London]] as one of the ports that "monopolise almost the whole maritime commerce of Great Britain" [001-004]. Its population of approximately 64,000 in 1841 placed it among the larger English cities of the period, though significantly smaller than [[london|London]], [[manchester|Manchester]], or [[liverpool|Liverpool]].

## Engels's Account

Engels places Bristol in the context of the centralising tendency of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, noting that maritime commerce concentrated in a handful of great harbours in the same way that [[manufacture|manufacturing]] concentrated population:

> "If it were possible for this mad rush of manufacture to go on at this rate for another century, every manufacturing district of England would be one great manufacturing town, and Manchester and Liverpool would meet at Warrington or Newton; for in commerce, too, this centralisation of the population works in precisely the same way, and hence it is that one or two great harbours, such as Hull and Liverpool, Bristol, and London, monopolise almost the whole maritime commerce of Great Britain." [001-004]

The most detailed social data Engels provides for Bristol concerns housing conditions. He reports findings from a survey of working-class families:

> "In Bristol, on one occasion, 2,800 families were visited, of whom 46 percent occupied but one room each." [002-023]

This figure — nearly half of the surveyed families living in single-room accommodation — is cited alongside similar conditions in [[liverpool|Liverpool]], where over 45,000 people lived in cellar [[housing|dwellings]]. Engels groups Bristol among the "great seaport towns" where workers are treated with "the same barbarity" regardless of the city's commercial wealth and grandeur.

## Irish Immigration

Bristol is also one of the English cities that Engels lists as having a significant Irish immigrant population. He recorded 24,000 Irish residents in Bristol during the early 1840s:

> "It has been calculated that more than a million have already immigrated, and not far from fifty thousand still come every year, nearly all of whom enter the industrial districts, especially the great cities, and there form the lowest class of the population. Thus there are in London, 120,000; in Manchester, 40,000; in Liverpool, 34,000; Bristol, 24,000; Glasgow, 40,000; Edinburgh, 29,000, poor Irish people." [004-001]

This Irish population, Engels argues, formed "the lowest class of the population" and competed with native English workers for the poorest-paid labour, driving down wages across the [[proletariat|working class]].

## Chartist Activity

Bristol appears in Engels's narrative of working-class political awakening through the figure of W. P. Roberts, a Chartist solicitor based in the city. Roberts served as the miners' "Attorney General" during the great coal miners' union movement of 1843–1844:

> "Such a movement seized the workers of Northumberland and Durham that they placed themselves at the forefront of a general Union of coal miners throughout the kingdom, and appointed W. P. Roberts, a Chartist solicitor, of Bristol, their 'Attorney General,' he having distinguished himself in earlier Chartist trials." [009-013]

Roberts was empowered to draft new employment contracts for the miners and represented 100,000 union members by 1844, making Bristol a node in the national Chartist network despite being geographically distant from the northern coal districts.
