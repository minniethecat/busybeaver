# Thomas Robert Malthus

## Life

Thomas Robert Malthus (1766–1834) was an English clergyman and political economist, best known for his *Essay on the Principle of Population* (1798), which argued that population, when unchecked, tends to increase geometrically while the means of [[diet|subsistence]] increase only arithmetically, making poverty, famine, and premature death inevitable features of the human condition. He was a founding figure of classical political economy, alongside [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] and David Ricardo, and his doctrines shaped British social policy for decades — most infamously, according to Engels, in the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834. (011-008, 003-009)

## Role in Engels's Account

Malthus is, for Engels, the supreme theoretical enemy. Where Engels treats most bourgeois economists as confused apologists, he treats Malthus as something worse: a systematic ideologist who provides pseudo-scientific justification for the starvation of the poor.

Engels first introduces Malthus in the chapter on [[competition]], where he concedes that under existing capitalist relations, the Malthusian mechanism has a certain descriptive truth. The worker "is sold like a piece of goods, rises and falls in value like a commodity," and if demand falls, surplus workers "die of starvation." Engels then adds:

> "And, so far, Malthus was perfectly right in his theory of population. The only difference as compared with the old, outspoken slavery is this, that the worker of today seems to be free because he is not sold once for all, but piecemeal." (003-008)

This is Engels's characteristic move: grant Malthus descriptive accuracy within capitalism, then use that accuracy to indict capitalism itself. If Malthus's theory is true, it is true only because the system makes it true. Engels goes further, arguing that [[surplus-population]] is not a natural phenomenon but a product of the [[competition]] among workers and the introduction of [[machinery]]:

> "And Malthus, who carried the foregoing proposition of Smith farther, was also right, in his way, in asserting that there are always more people on hand than can be maintained from the available means of subsistence. Surplus population is engendered rather by the competition of the workers among themselves, which forces each separate worker to labour as much each day as his strength can possibly admit." (003-009)

In the chapter on the attitude of the [[bourgeoisie]], Engels escalates. He declares that "the most open declaration of war of the bourgeoisie upon the proletariat is Malthus' Law of Population and the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] framed in accordance with it." The Malthusian logic, Engels explains, treats poverty as a crime of "superfluity" and recommends punishment by starvation. Malthus explicitly denied the right to live to the poor: (011-008)

> "Malthus declares in plain English that the right to live, a right previously asserted in favour of every man in the world, is nonsense. He quotes the words of a poet, that the poor man comes to the feast of Nature and finds no cover laid for him, and adds that 'she bids him begone,' for he did not before his birth ask of society whether or not he is welcome." (011-008)

This is Engels's moral core. Malthus is not merely an economist Engels disagrees with; he is the theorist who authorises social murder. The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 translated Malthusian doctrine into policy: the workhouse system, the separation of families, the deliberately punitive diet and labour regime — all were designed to make relief so repellent that only the truly desperate would seek it. The workhouse, Engels writes, "has been made the most repulsive residence which the refined ingenuity of a Malthusian can invent." (011-012, 011-011)

Engels also identifies the Malthusian doctrine as a weapon in the employers' arsenal. In his discussion of machinery displacing workers, he notes the hypocrisy of the manufacturers who "at other times swears by Malthus" but suddenly abandons population theory when confronted with the human cost of technological unemployment: "'Where could the millions by which the population of England has increased find work, without the improvements in machinery?' As though the bourgeois did not know well enough that without machinery... these 'millions' would never have been brought into the world and grown up!" (006-004)

## Context

Malthus's doctrine was the explicit ideological foundation of the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834, which Engels treats as the definitive statement of bourgeois intentions towards the [[proletariat]]. The [[poor-law-commission|Poor Law Commissioners]], "these wise Malthusians," were "so thoroughly convinced of the infallibility of their theory that they did not for one moment hesitate to cast the poor into the Procrustean bed of their economic notions and treat them with the most revolting cruelty." Engels's attack on Malthus is not merely a dispute among economists — it is an attack on the philosophy that held that some lives were surplus, a philosophy that found its institutional expression in the workhouse system and its political defence in the government of Sir [[robert-peel|Robert Peel]]. (011-011, 011-008, 011-019)
