# Reform Bill of 1832

## Overview

The Reform Bill of 1832 (formally the Representation of the People Act 1832) was a landmark piece of British legislation that restructured parliamentary representation by abolishing rotten boroughs and extending the franchise to property-owning sections of the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]. Engels treats the Reform Bill not as a democratic advance but as a class victory of the industrial bourgeoisie over the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] — a victory that excluded the [[proletariat|working class]] and drove the deepest wedge yet between the propertied and the propertyless. (P01-010)

Engels identifies the Reform Bill as the moment when the bourgeoisie became the ruling class in Parliament and when the distinction between bourgeoisie and proletariat received legal sanction. The [[proletariat|working class]], which had supported the agitation for reform in the belief that political rights would bring economic relief, was left with nothing. This betrayal, Engels argues, was the political education that made [[chartism|Chartism]] inevitable. (008-008, 008-023)

## Engels's Account

In the preface to the English edition, Engels describes the Reform Bill as "the victory of the whole [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] over the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]." The [[manufacture|manufacturing]] capitalists had accumulated vast wealth but held little direct political power. The old borough system, controlled by the landed oligarchy, was an obstacle to the legislative programme the industrial bourgeoisie required: free trade, the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], and the legal framework for unrestrained [[factory-system|factory production]]. (P01-010)

Engels argues that the Reform Bill legally sanctioned a division that already existed in social fact. Before 1832, the old unreformed Parliament could — almost by accident — pass legislation that benefited workers, because the Tory oligarchy did not represent the industrial bourgeoisie's immediate interests. Engels cites the 1824 repeal of the [[combination-laws|Combination Laws]], enacted by the old oligarchic-Tory Parliament, as a law "which never could have passed the House of Commons later, when the Reform Bill had legally sanctioned the distinction between bourgeoisie and proletariat, and made the bourgeoisie the ruling class." (008-008) After 1832, Parliament was firmly in the hands of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest, and legislation such as the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 — which Engels calls "the reform of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] according to [the bourgeoisie's] own point of view" — became typical. (011-009)

The [[proletariat|working class]] had participated in the reform agitation of 1830–1832, mobilised by Radical orators who promised political rights as the key to economic improvement. When the Bill passed and it became clear that the £10 householder franchise excluded the vast majority of workers, the sense of betrayal was profound. Engels describes how the disappointment over the Reform Bill, combined with the commercial depression of 1837–1839, radicalised the lower [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] and the [[proletariat|working class]] alike, and fuelled the explosive growth of the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. (008-023, 008-026)

Engels frames the Reform Bill as the closing of one political epoch and the opening of another. Before 1832, the conflict was between the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] and the rising bourgeoisie. After 1832, the question became what Engels calls "the national question" — the claim of the [[proletariat|working class]], "who live and die only by the toil of their hands," to a share in the advantages of society. (P04-024) This is the question that defines the entire text.

## Context

The Reform Bill was preceded by mass agitation, including the formation of Political Unions in [[Birmingham]] under Thomas Attwood and in other industrial centres. The rejection of the first Reform Bill by the House of Lords in October 1831 triggered widespread rioting in [[Bristol]], [[Nottingham]], and other towns. The final passage of the Bill in June 1832 was widely celebrated by the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] as a triumph of popular pressure. For the [[proletariat|working class]], it was the beginning of independent political organisation.

The £10 householder franchise extended the vote to approximately one in five adult males, largely the shopkeepers, merchants, and professional men who formed the lower ranks of the bourgeoisie. Agricultural labourers, [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, miners, and the urban poor remained disenfranchised.

## Aftermath

The exclusion of the [[proletariat|working class]] from the franchise became the central grievance of the [[Chartist Movement 1838]], whose first and most fundamental demand was universal male suffrage. Engels traces the lineage of [[chartism|Chartism]] directly to the disappointment of 1832. (008-026) The Reform Bill also set the stage for the legislative programme of the 1830s — the [[New Poor Law 1834]], the [[Factory Act 1833]], and the municipal reforms — all of which Engels analyses as expressions of bourgeois class rule.
