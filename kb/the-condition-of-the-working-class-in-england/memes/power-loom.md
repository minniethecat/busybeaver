## Definition

The power-loom was the steam-driven mechanical loom that automated the weaving of cloth. Developed in the late eighteenth century and widely adopted in the 1820s–1840s, it completed the [[machinery|mechanisation]] of textile production that the spinning [[machinery|machines]] had begun. In Engels's analysis, the power-loom was the technological agent of social catastrophe: it destroyed the independence of the [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]] — once "the most honourable, conscientious, and respected class of English workmen" — and reduced them to the lowest stratum of the industrial proletariat.

## Engels's Analysis

### The Technological Revolution in Weaving

Engels places the power-loom within the broader trajectory of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. By the 1830s, the textile districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] contained "110,000 power and 250,000 hand-looms" (P04-010), the two technologies coexisting in direct competition. The power-loom's advantage was not merely speed but concentration: "great buildings" were erected "to use waterpower for driving them" (P04-008), bringing weaving into the [[factory-system|factory]] alongside spinning. What had been a [[domestic-industry|cottage industry]] — the weaver working at home on a hand-loom, often supplementing [[farming|agricultural labour]] — became [[factory-system|factory production]], subject to the discipline of the clock and the overseer.

### The Destruction of the Hand-Loom Weavers

The competition between power and hand was not a fair one. "The power-loom weaver is in competition with the hand-loom weaver" (003-002), but the power-loom could produce far more cloth per worker, driving down piece-rates for [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]] to starvation levels. Engels describes this as a slow extermination: "the power-loom has taken possession of the weaving industry" (006-003), and the hand-loom weaver became "the most ill-used" (006-005) section of the [[proletariat|working class]]. The process Engels documents was not a sudden displacement but a protracted agony lasting decades, as [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]] accepted ever-lower wages in a losing battle with machinery.

### Concentration and the Factory System

The power-loom required [[factory-system|factory]] organisation: "especially by the power-loom and the self-acting mule, machinery has gained the victory over hand-labour" (002-030). This victory meant that weaving, like spinning before it, was concentrated in large establishments employing hundreds of operatives. The "great buildings" Engels describes were not merely workplaces but social institutions: they gathered workers under one roof, subjected them to uniform discipline, and made possible the collective resistance — trade unions and strikes — that the scattered cottage weavers could never sustain. The power-loom thus created the material conditions for the very class consciousness that would challenge the system.

### The Global Reach

Engels notes that the power-loom's effects extended far beyond [[lancashire|Lancashire]]: "China was more and more being crushed out by the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] power-loom" (P01-004). The [[machinery|mechanisation]] of weaving in England destroyed [[weaving|hand-weaving]] industries across the world, integrating distant markets into the British industrial system and creating a global division of labour in which England manufactured and the rest of the world supplied raw materials and consumed finished goods. This global dimension is characteristic of Engels's analysis: the local [[factory-system|factory]] and the world market are linked by the same technological logic.

### Mental and Physical Degradation

Engels cites medical testimony on the effects of power-loom work: the noise of hundreds of looms in a single room, the constant attention required to tend multiple [[machinery|machines]], the standing posture maintained for twelve or fourteen hours. The power-loom operative suffered "the deterioration of the mental and physical powers" (006-021) that Engels sees as the universal consequence of [[factory-system|factory]] labour under capitalism. The machine that was technically the most advanced product of human ingenuity became, in its social application, an instrument of human degradation.
