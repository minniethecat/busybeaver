# Middle-Class

The **middle-class** in Engels's *The Condition of the Working Class in England* (1845) denotes the property-owning, non-aristocratic social stratum that rose to economic and political dominance through the [[industrial-revolution]]. The term encompasses both the wealthy [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and commercial bourgeoisie and the precarious "lower middle-class" of small shopkeepers and handicraftsmen. Engels portrays the middle-class as the ruling class of nineteenth-century England — one whose interests are fundamentally opposed to those of the [[proletariat]], and whose political and economic ascendancy rests upon the systematic exploitation of [[proletariat|industrial workers]].

## The Middle-Class as Ruling Class

Engels identifies the middle-class as the dominant force in English society, wielding control over Parliament, the economy, and public discourse. In his 1892 preface, he observes how the middle-class used political crises to consolidate its power: "The French Revolution of 1848 saved the English middle-class"[^P01-010], allowing the [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] to triumph while the [[chartism|Chartist movement]] collapsed. He notes that the middle-class "can never obtain full social and political power over the nation except by the help of the working-class"[^P01-010], describing a tactical manipulation of workers' movements for bourgeois ends.

The middle-class, Engels contends, regards the condition of the proletariat not as a matter of justice but as a matter of political convenience. While the working-class gains ground with every session of Parliament, "the interests of the middle-class diminish in importance"[^P04-024]. The middle-class refuses to acknowledge the distress of workers because it "must bear the moral responsibility for this distress"[^P04-025]. Engels describes the "absurd freedom from anxiety, with which the middle-class dwells upon a soil that is honeycombed, and may any day collapse"[^P04-025].

## Distinction Between Upper and Lower Middle-Class

Engels draws a sharp distinction between the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] bourgeoisie and the lower middle-class. The former, enriched by industry, occupies the comfortable suburban villas of [[manchester|Manchester]]; the latter is progressively crushed by the same forces that empower the wealthy.

> "The lower middle-class consists only of small shopkeepers, and very very few handicraftsmen."[^P04-023]

The destruction of the lower middle-class is a central theme of Engels's analysis of the industrial revolution. He explains that "manufacture, on a small scale, created the middle-class; on a large scale, it created the working-class"[^001-003]. The introduction of machinery and the [[factory-system|factory]] system ruined petty producers: "the numerous, petty middle-class of the 'good old times' has been annihilated by manufacture, and resolved into rich capitalists on the one hand and poor workers on the other"[^001-003].

In the [[urban|great town]]s, this process is nearly complete: "there exist here only a rich and a poor class, for the lower middle-class vanishes more completely with every passing day"[^001-005]. The class that was once most stable has become "the most restless one ... of whom one may amass a fortune, while ninety-nine become insolvent"[^001-005].

## An Opponent to the Working Class

In his dedication "To the Working-Classes of Great-Britain," Engels states his conclusion plainly. He "forsook the company and the dinner-parties, the port-wine and champagne of the middle-classes"[^P03-002] to study workers' lives at first hand. Having had "ample opportunity to watch the middle-classes, your opponents," he concludes that workers "are right, perfectly right in expecting no support whatever from them"[^P03-003].

The middle-class professes concern for the workers but acts otherwise: "Their interest is diametrically opposed to yours, though they always will try to maintain the contrary and to make you believe in their most hearty sympathy with your fates. Their doings give them the lie."[^P03-003]

## Spatial Segregation and Hypocrisy

Engels devotes a detailed section of Chapter 2 to the spatial arrangements by which the middle-class insulates itself from the sight of working-class misery. In [[manchester|Manchester]], "the working-people's quarters are sharply separated from the sections of the city reserved for the middle-class; or, if this does not succeed, they are concealed with the cloak of charity"[^002-037]. The commercial thoroughfares are lined with shops that screen the [[housing|slums]] behind them, allowing the bourgeoisie to travel "through the middle of all the labouring districts to their places of business, without ever seeing that they are in the midst of the grimy misery that lurks to the right and the left"[^002-037].

This deliberate physical separation mirrors a moral blind spot. Engels remarks that "the eager assurances of the middle-class, that the working-class is doing famously"[^002-037] cannot be reconciled with the systematic concealment of working-class districts.

## Consumer Exploitation

The middle-class exploits workers not only as producers but also as consumers. Engels documents how the working-class receives inferior goods: "the buyers of the middle-class have had the first choice during the morning, when the market teems with the best of everything"[^002-059]. By the time workers can shop on Saturday evening, "the best has vanished." The middle-class also profits from the adulteration of [[diet|food]]: "Dealers and manufacturers adulterate all kinds of provisions in an atrocious manner, and without the slightest regard to the health of the consumers"[^002-059]. Engels cites the *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* and the *[[liverpool|Liverpool]] Mercury* — both middle-class organs — as witnesses against their own class.

## Middle-Class Privilege and Mortality

The advantages of middle-class status are measurable in life expectancy. Citing the Registrar-General's mortality statistics for [[manchester|Manchester]], Engels notes that "the general average is improved by the smaller mortality of the upper and middle-classes"[^005-013]. Dr. P. H. Holland's investigation of Chorlton-on-Medlock found that mortality in first-class streets with first-class houses was one in 51, compared to one in 25 in the worst streets — a gap Engels attributes directly to class.

Even in matters of health and clothing, the class divide is stark: "the damp air of England ... obliges almost the whole middle-class to wear flannel next the skin"[^002-057], whereas workers "are scarcely ever in a position to use a thread of woollen clothing"[^002-057]. The contrast between the "broadcloth" of the middle-class and the "fustian" of the workingman — the latter used "as characteristic for the middle-class"[^002-056] in distinguishing itself — becomes a visible marker of class division.

## The Blame for Urban Misery

Engels confronts the tendency of middle-class writers to attribute working-class degradation to the growth of cities rather than to the economic system. He argues that "the inference that manufacture and the middle-class which profits from it alone have created the cities is so remote, that it is extremely convenient for the ruling class to ascribe all the evil to this apparently unavoidable source"[^005-030]. In reality, "the great cities really only secure a more rapid and certain development for evils already existing in the germ"[^005-030].

## The Lower Middle-Class and Chartism

The political alliance between the lower middle-class and workers was unstable but significant. During the Chartist agitation of 1837--1839, "the lower middle-class was just then in a very bellicose and violent state of mind in consequence of the disappointment over the Reform Bill"[^008-026] and viewed the [[chartism|Chartist movement]] favourably. But this alliance was tactical and temporary: the bourgeoisie soon turned its attention to the [[corn-laws]], and "the consequence was a relaxation of the tie between the Radical bourgeoisie and the proletariat"[^008-026].

## Progressive Ruin and the Coming Revolution

In his concluding chapter, Engels returns to the fate of the lower middle-class as a driver of historical change. The "progressive ruin of the lower middle-class and the giant strides with which capital is concentrating itself in the hands of the few"[^011-022] would swell the ranks of the proletariat until it "would soon embrace the whole nation, with the exception of a few millionaires"[^011-022]. Engels predicts that "the commercial crises, the mightiest levers for all independent development of the proletariat, will probably shorten the process, acting in concert with foreign competition and the deepening ruin of the lower middle-class"[^011-023]. The middle-class, through its relentless pursuit of profit and its destruction of all intermediate social strata, is digging its own grave.
