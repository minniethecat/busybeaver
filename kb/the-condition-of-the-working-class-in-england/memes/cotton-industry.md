## Definition

The cotton industry was the first and most completely mechanised sector of British [[manufacture|manufacturing]], centred in [[lancashire|Lancashire]]. Its technological history — Kay's flying shuttle, Hargreaves's [[machinery|spinning-jenny]], Arkwright's water-frame, Crompton's mule, Cartwright's power-loom — was the history of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] itself. Engels treats the cotton industry as the paradigmatic case of capitalist production: its capital concentration, its exploitation of child and female labour, its cyclical crises of overproduction and unemployment, and its creation of a dense industrial proletariat concentrated in [[manchester|Manchester]] and the surrounding [[urban|factory town]]s.

## Engels's Analysis

### The Pace-Setter of Industrialisation

Engels begins his account of the [[factory-system|factory]] system "minutely, beginning with the cotton industry" (P04-010). He documents its growth in precise statistical terms: from 1771–1775, cotton imports averaged under 5 million pounds annually; by the 1840s, they exceeded 500 million. This hundredfold increase was not merely quantitative but qualitative — it represented a complete reorganisation of production, from the scattered [[domestic-industry|cottage industry]] of the eighteenth century to the concentrated [[factory-system|factory]] system of the nineteenth. The cotton industry was "the leading department of English industry" (P04-011), and Engels treats its history as the template for all subsequent industrial development.

### The Technological Sequence

The [[machinery|mechanisation]] of cotton followed a logic that Engels traces in detail. [[spinning|Spinning]] was mechanised first (the jenny, water-frame, and mule), creating a bottleneck in weaving that was resolved by the power-loom. Each new machine increased the productivity of labour and simultaneously devalued existing skills: the hand-spinner was displaced by the mule, the hand-loom weaver by the power-loom. "In the cotton industry of South [[lancashire|Lancashire]], the victory of machinery over hand-labour was complete" (002-030). The human consequences of this victory — mass unemployment among hand-workers, falling wages, and the degradation of the surviving [[factory-system|factory]] operatives — form the substance of Engels's indictment.

### The Factory Workforce

The cotton industry employed the largest and most concentrated [[factory-system|factory]] workforce in Britain. Engels quotes statistics showing that "of 419,560 [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, 272,000 were employed in the cotton industry" (006-007), far exceeding any other sector. This workforce was distinctive in composition: a high proportion of women and children, reflecting the industry's strategy of substituting cheap, unskilled labour for expensive skilled artisans. "From the beginning of [[manufacture|manufacturing industry]], children have been employed in the cotton mills" (006-014), Engels notes, documenting a system of child labour that began with parish apprentices and continued through the [[factory-system|factory]] era.

### Cyclical Crises

The cotton industry was subject to periodic crises of overproduction that Engels analyses in detail. "The history of cotton manufacture as related by [[andrew-ure|Ure]]" (006-002) showed a pattern of boom years followed by years of depression, with wages rising in the boom and workers being thrown onto the streets in the bust. Engels traces this to the anarchy of capitalist production: each manufacturer expands output to capture market share, but the combined result of all manufacturers doing so is overproduction, falling prices, and crisis. The cotton industry, as the most developed sector, exhibited this cycle in its purest form.

### The Subsidiary Trades

The cotton industry spawned a network of subsidiary trades: cotton-printing, cotton-velvet cutting, bleaching, and dyeing. Engels exa[[mining|mines]] the conditions of "operatives employed in the cotton-printing establishments of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]" (007-006), finding them as degraded as the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives proper. The "large consumption of cotton velvet" (007-007) created a specialised workforce of velvet-cutters, a trade Engels singles out for its particularly high rates of occupational disease. The cotton industry was thus not a single trade but an entire industrial ecosystem, each branch reproducing the characteristic patterns of exploitation.
