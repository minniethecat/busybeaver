## Overview

Samuel Crompton (1753–1827) was an English inventor from Firwood, Lancashire, who combined the principles of James Hargreaves' spinning jenny and Richard Arkwright's water frame (throstle) to create the **spinning mule** in 1785 (P04-008). The mule became a key machine in the mechanization of cotton spinning during the Industrial Revolution.

## Invention of the Spinning Mule

Crompton's mule was developed after the introduction of the spinning jenny (1764) by James Hargreaves and the water-powered throstle (1767) by Richard Arkwright. Engels describes the mule as a combination of the two earlier machines:

> By the combination of the peculiarities of the jenny and throstle, Samuel Crompton, of Firwood, Lancashire, contrived the mule in 1785.
>
> (P04-008)

The mule produced finer and stronger yarn than either of its predecessors and was adopted rapidly. Alongside Arkwright's carding engine and preparatory frames developed around the same period, the mule helped establish the factory system as the dominant mode of cotton spinning.

Crompton's invention, together with James Watt's steam engine (invented 1764, applied to spinning from 1785), and Dr. Edmund Cartwright's power-loom (invented in the late 18th century, perfected around 1804), completed the mechanization of the cotton textile industry (P04-008).

## Legacy

Crompton's spinning mule remained in widespread use throughout the 19th century. Engels notes that by the 1840s, over eight million mule spindles were in operation in England (P04-010). Unlike Arkwright, Crompton did not patent his invention and received little financial reward.
