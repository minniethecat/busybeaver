## Overview

Dr. Edmund Cartwright (1743–1823) was an English country parson who invented the **power-loom** in the closing years of the 18th century. Engels notes that by about 1804, Cartwright had perfected the machine to the point where it could successfully compete with hand-weavers (P04-008).

## The Power-Loom

Engels situates Cartwright's invention as one of the key mechanical breakthroughs that completed the factory system in cotton textiles:

> In the closing years of the last century, Dr. Cartwright, a country parson, had invented the power-loom, and about 1804 had so far perfected it, that it could successfully compete with the hand-weaver.
>
> (P04-008)

The power-loom mechanized weaving, the second major stage of textile production after spinning. Where Crompton's mule and Arkwright's throstle had revolutionized spinning, Cartwright's power-loom applied mechanical power to the loom itself, displacing the hand-weaver.

## Place in the Industrial Revolution

Engels places Cartwright's power-loom in a sequence of inventions that collectively established the factory system:

1. **1764**: James Hargreaves' spinning jenny (P04-006)
2. **1764**: James Watt's improved steam engine, applied to spinning from 1785 (P04-008)
3. **1767**: Richard Arkwright's water-powered throstle (P04-008)
4. **1785**: Samuel Crompton's spinning mule, combining the jenny and throstle (P04-008)
5. **Late 18th century**: Cartwright's power-loom, perfected by c. 1804 (P04-008)

All of these machines, Engels notes, "was made doubly important by James Watt's steam-engine" (P04-008), which supplied reliable motive power independent of water sources.

## Social Impact

The mechanization of weaving through Cartwright's power-loom — combined with the earlier mechanization of spinning — displaced vast numbers of hand-workers. The hand-weavers who survived the initial transition faced progressively declining wages and deteriorating conditions, a process Engels documents across multiple chapters of his study.
