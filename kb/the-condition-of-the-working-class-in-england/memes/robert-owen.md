# Robert Owen

## Life

Robert Owen (1771–1858) was a Welsh textile manufacturer who became the most influential British socialist of the early nineteenth century. He made his fortune as the manager and part-owner of the New Lanark cotton mills in Scotland, where he demonstrated that it was possible to run a profitable [[factory-system|factory]] while providing workers with decent housing, education for children, and reasonable [[working-day|working hours]]. New Lanark became an international showcase — visited by thousands of dignitaries and reformers — and Owen used its success to argue that character was formed by circumstances, not by individual moral choice, and that improving the material conditions of the [[proletariat|working class]] was therefore both a moral duty and a practical necessity. (006-045)

## Role in Engels's Account

> Later, towards 1817, Robert Owen, then a manufacturer in New Lanark, began to agitate for legislative limitation of the working day. (006-045)

Engels treats Owen with a mixture of respect and criticism. Owen's practical achievements at New Lanark are acknowledged: he was among the first manufacturers to recognise that the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] were not merely humanitarian measures but also economically rational, since they prevented the competitive degradation that drove wages and conditions downward for all. His agitation for legislative limitation of the working day — beginning with his campaign for a ten-hour bill in 1817 — was a concrete contribution to the [[factory-system|factory]] reform movement that Engels documents in detail. Owen, unlike most manufacturers, understood that unrestricted competition was destructive not only of workers' lives but of the long-term interests of capital itself.

But Engels's overall assessment of [[socialism|Owenism]] is ultimately negative. The collapse of Owen's cooperative communities — New Harmony in America, Orbiston in Scotland, Queenswood in Hampshire — demonstrated, in Engels's view, the futility of attempting to build socialism within capitalism through voluntary association and rational persuasion. Owen believed that the ruling class could be convinced to adopt socialism once its superiority was demonstrated; Engels believed that the ruling class would never voluntarily surrender its power and that socialism could only be achieved through the class struggle of the proletariat itself.

> After the breakdown of Owenism, there has been no Socialism in England. (P01-010)

In the 1892 Preface to the English edition, Engels reflects on the historical trajectory of [[socialism|English socialism]] since 1844. [[socialism|Owenism]], he notes, had collapsed by the mid-1830s, and the socialism that succeeded it — the socialism of the Chartist left, of the [[labour-movement|trade union movement]], of the Social Democratic Federation — was a very different phenomenon: working-class rather than philanthropic, political rather than utopian, international rather than insular. Owen's historical importance, in this retrospective assessment, was that he demonstrated the possibility of an alternative to capitalism — but the task of realising that alternative fell to a different class and a different movement. (P01-010)

## Context

Owen's career illuminates the political economy of [[factory-system|factory]] reform that Engels analyses throughout the book. As a manufacturer who supported the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]], Owen was an anomaly — most manufacturers opposed any limitation on [[working-day|working hours]] as an infringement of their property rights and a threat to British competitiveness. Engels uses Owen's example to demonstrate that the manufacturers' opposition to reform was not economically necessary but politically motivated: Owen proved that a humane [[factory-system|factory]] could be profitable, but the majority of manufacturers chose the alternative because it was more profitable and because they preferred unrestricted power over their workers.

The Owenite movement that developed from the 1820s onward — with its cooperative societies, labour exchanges, and trade unions — was one of the largest [[labour-movement|working-class movement]]s in Britain before [[chartism|Chartism]]. The Grand National Consolidated Trades Union, founded in 1834 with Owen's support, briefly united hundreds of thousands of workers before collapsing under the combined pressure of employer lockouts and internal divisions. Engels treats this episode as a crucial learning experience for the [[proletariat|working class]]: the failure of Owenite trade unionism demonstrated that economic organisation alone, without political power, was insufficient.
