# Domestic Industry

## Definition

Domestic industry, in Engels's usage, refers to the system of manufacture in which production was carried out in workers' homes using hand-operated implements — the spinning wheel, the hand loom, the frame for stocking-knitting — and organised through a network of merchant-capitalists who supplied raw materials and marketed finished goods. The [[cottage-system|domestic system]] was the dominant form of industrial production in England before the introduction of machinery, and it persisted in certain branches — notably lace-making, hosiery, and straw-plaiting — well into the [[factory-system|factory]] era. Engels distinguishes between the independent domestic producer (the yeoman-weaver who owned his tools and worked his own plot of land) and the dependent outworker (who owned nothing but his labour power and was essentially a wage-worker employed at home). (006-002)

## Engels's Analysis

> Before the introduction of machinery, the spinning and weaving of raw materials was carried on in the workingman's home. (P04-003)

The destruction of domestic industry by machine competition is one of Engels's central historical narratives. In the decades before the [[factory-system|factory]] system, the weaver who owned his loom and cultivated a small plot of land could earn enough to live decently — not in luxury, but in security. He determined his own [[working-day|working hours]], enjoyed a measure of independence, and was integrated into a rural community. The introduction of the spinning jenny, the [[power-loom|power loom]], and the steam engine destroyed this world. Machine-produced goods were cheaper and more uniform than hand-made products; the weaver who had once earned fifteen shillings a week saw his wages fall to five, then to [[diet|subsistence]] level, then below it. (P01-004)

> The chief branches of English industry were won for the factory system. (P04-009)

The transition was not instantaneous but proceeded branch by branch. Cotton was the first industry to be mechanised, in the 1770s and 1780s, with the spinning jenny and Arkwright's water frame. Woollen manufacture followed more slowly, with hand-loom weaving persisting into the 1820s and 1830s. Linen and silk mechanised later still. In each branch, the same pattern repeated: machinery displaced hand labour, the independent producer was reduced to a dependent outworker, the outworker was reduced to a [[factory-system|factory]] hand, and the [[factory-system|factory]] hand was reduced — during crises — to a [[pauperism|pauper]]. Engels documents this process not as technological progress but as a progressive immiseration of the producing class. (P04-009, P04-022)

The persistence of domestic industry in certain trades after the general victory of the [[factory-system|factory]] system created some of the worst conditions Engels documents. In the lace-making districts of [[nottingham|Nottingham]], in the straw-plaiting villages of Bedfordshire, and in the nail-making hamlets of Staffordshire, workers who had been driven out of mechanised industries turned to the surviving domestic trades — and competed each other into starvation. The domestic worker, lacking even the minimal protection of the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] (which applied only to mills and [[mining|mines]]), worked longer hours for lower wages than the [[factory-system|factory]] operative. The home that had once been a site of independent production became a sweatshop. (006-002)

## Historical Context

The [[cottage-system|domestic system]] Engels describes had its heyday in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, when merchant-capitalists in the woollen and worsted trades "put out" raw materials to rural households for spinning and weaving. This system — the *Verlagssystem* of German economic history — was a transitional form between feudal craft production and capitalist [[factory-system|factory production]]. The producer still owned his [[capital|means of production]] (the loom, the spinning wheel) but was dependent on the merchant for raw materials and market access. The system's internal contradiction — the producer's formal independence combined with his economic dependence — was resolved by the [[factory-system|factory]] system, which concentrated both [[capital|means of production]] and labour power under the capitalist's direct control.

Engels's analysis of domestic industry draws on the reports of the parliamentary commissions that investigated [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]]' distress in the 1830s. These reports documented the catastrophic decline in hand-weaver wages — from twenty shillings a week in 1800 to five shillings in 1830 — and the social consequences: the dissolution of families, the employment of children, the abandonment of [[farming|agriculture]], and the migration to [[urban|factory town]]s. Engels uses these official sources to demonstrate that the destruction of domestic industry was not a natural economic process but a form of class violence, sanctioned and facilitated by the state.
