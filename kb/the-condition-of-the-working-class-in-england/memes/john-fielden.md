# John Fielden

## Life

John Fielden (1784–1849) was a [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r at Todmorden, [[lancashire|Lancashire]], who became one of the most prominent radical voices in early nineteenth-century British politics. Born into a Quaker family, he entered the cotton-spinning business with his brothers, building what became one of the largest [[manufacture|manufacturing]] enterprises in the Todmorden valley. Unlike most [[manufacturer|mill owner]]s, Fielden consistently aligned himself with the interests of his workers. He was elected to Parliament in 1832 as a Radical MP for [[oldham|Oldham]], a seat he held until his death in 1849. In Parliament, he was the most steadfast advocate of the [[ten-hours-bill]], the proposal to limit the working day in textile factories to ten hours. He published *The Curse of the [[factory-system|Factory System]]* (1836), an indictment of unrestrained [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, and continued to champion [[factory-system|factory]] reform and opposition to the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 throughout his career. (013-011)

Fielden was notable among the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class precisely because he broke ranks. Where other [[manufacturer|mill owner]]s argued that [[factory-act|factory legislation]] would ruin British industry, Fielden insisted that humane conditions were compatible with profitable enterprise. His own mills at Todmorden operated on shorter hours than the industry norm, and he became living proof that the manufacturers' economic arguments against the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] were hollow.

## Role in Engels's Account

Fielden appears in Engels's text in an unusual place — not in the main body of the work, but in a footnote that Engels appended to his chapter on "The Attitude of the [[bourgeoisie|Bourgeoisie]] towards the [[proletariat|Proletariat]]." This footnote is one of the most revealing passages in the entire book, because it shows Engels acknowledging complexity within the class analysis he otherwise presents in stark, uncompromising terms.

> "To prevent misconstructions and consequent objections, I would observe that I have spoken of the bourgeoisie as a class, and that all such facts as refer to individuals serve merely as evidence of the way of thinking and acting of a class. Hence I have not entered upon the distinctions between the divers sections, subdivisions and parties of the bourgeoisie, which have a mere historical and theoretical significance. And I can, for the same reason, mention but casually the few members of the bourgeoisie who have shown themselves honourable exceptions. These are, on the one hand, the pronounced Radicals, who are almost Chartists, such as a few members of the House of Commons, the manufacturers Hindly of Ashton, and Fielden of Todmordon (Lancashire), and, on the other hand, the philanthropic Tories..." (013-011)

The passage is densely significant. First, Engels classifies Fielden not as a Tory philanthropist (like [[lord-ashley]] or [[richard-oastler]]) but as a "pronounced Radical" who was "almost Chartist." This is a much stronger endorsement. The Tories opposed [[factory-system|factory]] abuses from paternalist principle; Fielden opposed them from democratic conviction, aligning himself with the workers' own political movement — [[chartism]].

Second, Engels's placement of Fielden in a footnote reflects his methodological scruples. The book's polemical purpose requires him to analyse the [[bourgeoisie]] as a class with unified interests opposed to those of the [[proletariat]]. Individual exceptions like Fielden do not invalidate the class analysis — but Engels feels bound to register them, in fairness and to forestall the objection that he has caricatured the bourgeoisie without nuance. Fielden's existence proves that a manufacturer could, in fact, see the injustice of the [[factory-system]] and act against his own immediate economic interest. Unlike the [[truck-system]] manufacturers Engels condemns elsewhere, Fielden's Todmorden mills demonstrated that shorter hours and [[housing]] provision for workers were compatible with competitive [[machinery]]-driven production. (013-011)

Third, the phrase "honourable exceptions" is carefully chosen. Fielden's honour consists not merely in his philanthropic sentiment — Engels found plenty of that among the Tories — but in his willingness to break with his class politically. By being "almost Chartist," Fielden recognised that ameliorative legislation, while necessary, was insufficient, and that the [[proletariat|working class]] required political power to secure its own emancipation.

## Context

Fielden's 1836 book *The Curse of the [[factory-system|Factory System]]* directly influenced Engels's own thinking. Fielden argued that the [[factory-system|factory]] system was not a temporary excess that could be regulated into benevolence but a fundamentally destructive social arrangement that consumed the lives of workers — especially children — in the pursuit of profit. His Todmorden mills, operated on shorter hours, demonstrated that a humane alternative was practically feasible. Engels's brief reference to Fielden, though passing, represents an important concession: the [[bourgeoisie|capitalist class]] was not monolithic, and the political choices of individual capitalists mattered.
