## Overview

Warrington is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], situated on the River Mersey approximately midway between [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[liverpool|Liverpool]]. During the 1840s, it was notable for its needle factories, which Engels cites as an example of the extreme exploitation of women and children in metal [[manufacture|manufacturing]] — a sector often overshadowed by attention to the textile trades.

## Engels's Account

### Needle Factories

Engels's most detailed reference to Warrington comes in his section on the metal-working industries:

> "needle factories in Warrington, Lancashire, where great want, immorality, and overcrowding prevail among the workers, and especially among the women and children" [007-014]

This brief but damning characterisation places Warrington's needle factories among the worst workplaces Engels surveyed. The needle industry was notorious for employing young children to perform repetitive, dangerous tasks — grinding and polishing needles produced fine metal dust that caused respiratory disease, and the work required hours of close attention under poor lighting.

### Regional Connections

Warrington appears in Engels's broader discussion of the [[factory-system|factory]] system's expansion because of its position on the transport networks connecting the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] industrial district to [[liverpool|Liverpool]]'s port and the Cheshire salt fields. The town grew as a result of these geographic advantages, its population swelling with the expansion of metal-working, glass-making, and chemical industries alongside the older textile trades.

### Working-Class Conditions

The "great [[poverty|want]], immorality, and [[crowding|overcrowding]]" Engels ascribes to Warrington's needle workers reflect the broader patterns he documents across all industrial towns: wages insufficient for [[diet|subsistence]], housing crammed into courts and back-to-backs, and the moral degradation Engels saw as an inevitable consequence of a system that treated human beings as expendable instruments of production.
