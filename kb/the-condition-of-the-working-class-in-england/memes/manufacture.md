# Manufacture

## Definition

In Engels's usage, manufacture denotes not merely the technical act of making goods, but the entire system of large-scale industrial production that arose in England between 1760 and 1840. It encompasses the substitution of machine-work for hand-work, the concentration of workers in factories and of population in [[urban|manufacturing town]]s, the division of the population into capitalists and [[wages|wage-labour]]ers, and the social relations — above all, the relation between the manufacturer and his "hands" — that define the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]]. (P01-003, 001-001)

Engels treats manufacture as the central explanatory category of his investigation. The structure of his book is organised around it: the first proletarians were "connected with manufacture, were engendered by it," and the subsequent chapters trace its effects through every branch of industry, from textiles to metals to mining to [[farming|agriculture]]. The degree of intelligence and political consciousness among workers, Engels argues, is "in direct proportion to their relation to manufacture," with [[factory-system|factory]] hands being the most enlightened and [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers the least. (001-001)

## Historical Development

Engels traces the rise of English manufacture across the introduction to his work, beginning with the [[machinery|spinning-jenny]] of 1764 and ending with the [[factory-system|factory]] system of the 1840s. The crucial transition was from domestic production — in which the weaver worked at home, owned his tools, and controlled his own pace of labour — to [[factory-system|factory production]], in which the worker owned nothing but his labour-power and worked under the discipline of the machine and the clock. (P04-003, P04-008)

> Meanwhile, let us trace the development of English manufacture somewhat more minutely, beginning with the cotton industry. In the years 1771–1775, there were annually imported into England rather less than 5,000,000 pounds of raw cotton; in the year 1841 there were imported 528,000,000 pounds, and the import for 1844 will reach at least 600,000,000 pounds. (P04-010)

The development of [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]] prompted parallel revolutions in wool, linen, and silk. In wool, the quantity worked up rose from 101 million pounds in 1801 to 180 million pounds in 1835. In silk, the reduction of the import duty in 1824 prompted a surge in twisting mills from 780,000 spindles to 1,180,000 in a single year. Each branch exhibited the same pattern: machinery multiplied output, lowered prices, expanded markets, and drew ever-larger populations into [[factory-system|factory]] labour. (P04-011, P04-015)

The growth of manufacture was self-reinforcing. The demand for machinery stimulated iron production and [[coal|coal mining]]; the demand for raw materials expanded the carrying trade; the concentration of workers in [[urban|factory town]]s created markets for builders, bakers, tailors, and all the trades of urban life. Engels describes this as a "universal whirl of activity" into which "everything was drawn." (P04-016, P04-017)

## Centralisation of Property and Population

The most consequential feature of manufacture, in Engels's analysis, is its tendency to centralise — centralise capital in fewer hands, centralise population in larger towns, and centralise political power in the manufacturing interest.

> It has been already suggested that manufacture centralises property in the hands of the few. It requires large capital with which to erect the colossal establishments that ruin the petty trading bourgeoisie and with which to press into its service the forces of Nature, so driving the hand labour of the independent workman out of the market. The division of labour, the application of water and especially steam, and the application of machinery, are the three great levers with which manufacture, since the middle of the last century, has been busy putting the world out of joint. Manufacture, on a small scale, created the middle-class; on a large scale, it created the working-class, and raised the elect of the middle-class to the throne, but only to overthrow them the more surely when the time comes. (001-003)

This centralisation of property is inseparable from the centralisation of population. A [[factory-system|factory]] requires many workers in a single building; the workers require housing and ser[[prostitution|vice]]s; the concentration of demand attracts new investment; the village becomes a town, the town a city. Engels presents this as a law of industrial development, driven by the logic of capital itself: the worker is "regarded in manufacture simply as a piece of capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of wages." (001-004)

> The centralising tendency of manufacture does not, however, stop here. Population becomes centralised just as capital does; and, very naturally, since the human being, the worker, is regarded in manufacture simply as a piece of capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of wages. (001-004)

The results are visible in the landscape. [[manchester|Manchester]] and its surrounding towns, the "classic soil on which English manufacture has achieved its masterwork," had been transformed within eighty years from an "obscure, ill-cultivated swamp" into a region of giant cities. The population of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] multiplied tenfold; [[glasgow|Glasgow]] grew from 30,000 to 300,000; [[bradford|Bradford]] from 29,000 to 77,000. (002-030, P04-010, P04-012)

## Manufacture and the Working Class

For Engels, the most important consequence of manufacture is the creation of the modern proletariat. Before the industrial revolution, the weaver was an independent producer who owned his tools and could supplement his income with a small plot of land. He was "no proletarian, he had a stake in the country, he was permanently settled, and stood one step higher in society than the English workman of today." Manufacture destroyed this independence, transforming the weaver into a wage-worker who owned nothing but his labour-power and had "no other prospect than that of remaining a toiler all his life." (P04-003, P04-023)

> The rapid extension of manufacture demanded hands, wages rose, and troops of workmen migrated from the agricultural districts to the towns. Population multiplied enormously, and nearly all the increase took place in the proletariat. (P04-023)

The condition of manufacture is one of permanent instability. Engels argues that English manufacture must have, "at all times save the brief periods of highest prosperity, an unemployed reserve army of workers," in order to produce the masses of goods demanded by the market in the shortest possible time during periods of peak demand. This structural insecurity — the alternation of [[cholera|fever]]ish overwork with enforced idleness — is for Engels an inherent feature of the [[factory-system|manufacturing system]], not an accidental one. (003-011)

The political consequences of manufacture are direct. Because manufacture concentrated workers in cities and factories, it made possible forms of collective action — trade unions, strikes, political associations — that had been impossible for dispersed cottage workers. The [[factory-system|factory]] hands, Engels writes, are the "nucleus of the [[labour-movement|Labour Movement]]," and the other branches of the [[proletariat|working class]] join the movement "just in proportion as their handicraft has been invaded by the progress of machinery." (001-001)

## Manufacture and the Bourgeoisie

Manufacture did not only create the [[proletariat|working class]]; it also created its antagonist.

> The industrial revolution has simply carried this out to its logical end by making the workers machines pure and simple, taking from them the last trace of independent activity, and so forcing them to think and demand a position worthy of men. As in France politics, so in England manufacture, and the movement of civil society in general drew into the whirl of history the last classes which had remained sunk in apathetic indifference to the universal interests of mankind. (P04-005)

The manufacturing interest became the dominant political force in England. The repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], the [[factory-act-1833|Factory Acts]], and the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill]] were all episodes in the ascendance of the manufacturing capitalists over the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] and, later, over the [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels observes in the 1885 preface that the largest manufacturers, "formerly the leaders of the war against the working-class, were now the foremost to preach peace and harmony" — a shift Engels attributes not to moral improvement but to the logic of competition, which made petty extortions unprofitable for giant capitalists while enabling them to crush their smaller rivals. (P01-004, P01-010)

The immense and rapid development of manufactures had also, by the 1840s, outstripped the extension of foreign markets, producing the pattern of cyclical crisis — every ten years "a general commercial [[crisis|crash]], followed, after a long period of chronic depression, by a few short years of prosperity, and always ending in [[cholera|fever]]ish overproduction and consequent renewed collapse." This contradiction between the productive power of manufacture and the social relations within which it operated was, for Engels, the fundamental instability of the capitalist system. (P01-010)

## The Moral Condition of Manufacture

Engels's most polemical charge is that manufacture, for all its claims to progress and civilisation, produces moral degradation. The concentration of workers in factories — of "persons of both sexes and all ages in a single workroom" — subjects the most vulnerable to the influence of the most dissolute. The employment of women dissolves the family, inverts the traditional division of domestic labour, and exposes working women to the sexual predation of their employers. The [[philosophy-of-manufactures|bourgeois apologetics]] that present these conditions as inevitable or even beneficial are, for Engels, the clearest evidence of the hypocrisy of the manufacturing class. (006-001, 006-012, 005-032)

> It is self-evident that a girl who has worked in a mill from her ninth year is in no position to understand domestic work, whence it follows that female operatives prove wholly inexperienced and unfit as housekeepers. (006-011)

Yet Engels is careful not to moralise from a pre-industrial standpoint. He explicitly rejects nostalgia for the "good old times," describing the pre-industrial weavers as intellectually dead, living only "for their petty, private interest, for their looms and gardens, and knew nothing of the mighty movement which, beyond their horizon, was sweeping through mankind." Manufacture, by destroying this cosy but subhuman existence, created the material conditions for the [[proletariat|working class]] to become conscious of its interests and to organise politically. The misery of the present is not a reason to return to the past but to press forward to a future in which the productive power of manufacture is directed to human ends rather than private profit. (P04-005)
