# Mortality

## Definition

Mortality, in Engels's usage, refers to the systematically elevated death rate among the [[proletariat|working class]] — a phenomenon he documents with statistical rigour across all eleven chapters. Engels treats mortality not as a biological fact but as a social indicator: the difference between the death rate in a working-class district and a middle-class district was, for him, a direct measure of the violence that the capitalist system inflicted on workers' bodies. A worker who died at thirty of [[consumption|tuberculosis]] in an unventilated cellar dwelling was not a victim of natural causes but of social murder. (005-004)

## Engels's Analysis

> An excessive mortality, an unbroken series of epidemics, and a progressive deterioration of the physique of the working population must be expected. (005-004)

Engels's mortality data is the book's most powerful empirical weapon. He reproduces the statistics of Dr. James Kay, Dr. John Roberton, and the [[manchester|Manchester]] Statistical Society showing that the average age at death for a [[manchester|Manchester]] labourer was seventeen years, compared with thirty-eight for the gentry and professional classes. In [[liverpool|Liverpool]], the difference was even starker: the average life expectancy of the "[[proletariat|working class]]" was fifteen years, against thirty-five for the upper classes. These figures, Engels notes, were not drawn from socialist propaganda but from official reports — often from physicians who were themselves members of the bourgeoisie. (005-004)

The analysis is differentiated by social class, occupation, and district — a methodological sophistication unusual for 1845. Engels breaks down mortality by age cohort (infant mortality was especially high in [[factory-system|factory]] districts), by disease ([[consumption|tuberculosis]], [[cholera|typhus]], and cholera were the great killers), and by locality (rural [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers lived longer than urban [[factory-system|factory]] workers, but both died young by middle-class standards). The implication is clear: mortality was inversely proportional to income, and the gradient was steep. (002-024)

The [[Cholera]] epidemics of 1832 and 1842 provided the most spectacular illustration of the class gradient in mortality. Engels documents how the disease, while capable of crossing into bourgeois districts, struck the working-class quarters with disproportionate lethality. In one [[manchester|Manchester]] street, "no house escaped the cholera"; in middle-class streets, the toll was negligible. The epidemic functioned as a natural experiment, isolating the effect of housing conditions on death rates: "the cholera visited with particular intensity the crowded, ill-ventilated, and filthy parts of the town," Engels writes, quoting Dr. Alison, providing a statistical demonstration of what he had already argued on structural grounds — that the bourgeoisie's neglect of [[housing|working-class housing]] was a form of murder. (002-039, 002-053)

Engels also documents the class gradient in infant mortality — a statistic that he treats as particularly damning because infants are innocent of any "moral" failings that the bourgeoisie attributed to adult workers. In [[manchester|Manchester]], over half of all working-class children died before the age of five; in middle-class families, the figure was under twenty per cent. The difference could not be attributed to genetics or "habits" — it was a direct product of [[crowding|overcrowding]], mal[[diet|nutrition]], lack of ventilation, and the employment of mothers in factories, which left infants without care. (002-028)

## Methodological Significance

Engels's use of mortality statistics was methodologically innovative. He was not the first to collect such data — the statistical societies of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[london|London]] had been publishing mortality tables since the 1830s — but he was the first to use them systematically as evidence of a structural critique. Mortality, for Engels, was not an unfortunate by-product of industrialisation but its expected outcome. The [[factory-system|factory]] that employed a child for fourteen hours, the landlord who packed a family into a cellar, the municipality that failed to provide drainage — all were complicit in the worker's premature death. Engels's innovation was to treat mortality as a form of evidence in a political indictment. (005-007)
