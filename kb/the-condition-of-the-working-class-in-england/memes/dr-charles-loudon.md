## Overview

Dr. Charles Loudon was a British physician who served as a commissioner on the 1833 Factory Inquiry Commission. His report contained some of the most damning conclusions about child labour in English factories, and Engels draws extensively on his evidence.

## Evidence on Child Labour

Loudon's testimony was particularly forceful on the question of working hours for children. Engels quotes his concluding assessment:

> In conclusion, I think it has been clearly proved that children have been worked a most unreasonable and cruel length of time daily, and that even adults have been expected to do a certain quantity of labour which scarcely any human being is able to endure.
>
> (006-026)

This finding was central to Engels' argument that the factory system systematically exploited vulnerable workers beyond the limits of human endurance.

## Medical Evidence

Loudon's report documented specific medical conditions resulting from factory labour, including malformation of the pelvis and other affections. Although he noted that such cases did not directly come under his personal observation, he emphasised that "every physician must regard them as probable consequences of such working-hours, and as vouched for besides by men of the highest medical credibility" (006-033).

## Role in the Commission

Loudon was one of the central commissioners, alongside Drinkwater and Power, whose reports "contain a multitude of examples" of physical distortions caused by factory work (006-019). His evidence, along with Barry's, was cited by Engels in the endnotes to the first English edition of the book (013-002, 013-006).
