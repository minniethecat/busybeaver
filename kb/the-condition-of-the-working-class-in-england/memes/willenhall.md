## Overview

Willenhall was a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in the Black Country district of Staffordshire, situated near [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]]. In the 1840s it was known for lock-making, key-cutting, and small-scale hardware production carried out in domestic workshops and small factories. Engels cites Willenhall in his discussions of child labour and industrial unrest in the iron-[[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts.

## Engels's Account

### Child Labour and Ignorance

Engels uses Willenhall as an example of the extreme ignorance produced by the [[factory-system|factory]] system, where children worked from an early age and received no education:

> "They had never heard of London nor of Willenhall, though the latter was but an hour's walk from their homes." [005-020]

This passage (referring to children in a nearby [[manufacture|manufacturing]] district) illustrates the narrow, circumscribed world of the industrial working-class child — labouring from the age of six or seven, they knew nothing beyond their immediate surroundings. Even towns within walking distance were terra incognita to children who had never been taught to read or to understand a map.

### Hardware District Unrest

Willenhall was part of the South Staffordshire iron and hardware [[manufacture|manufacturing]] district, which experienced significant labour unrest during the 1840s:

> "In Wolverhampton, Willenhall, Bilston, Sedgeley, Wednesfield, Darlaston, and other places, the people rose..." [007-010]

This passage documents the spread of working-class militancy across the Black Country towns, where ironworkers, miners, and lock-makers joined the broader pattern of strikes, demonstrations, and Chartist agitation that characterised the "hungry forties."
