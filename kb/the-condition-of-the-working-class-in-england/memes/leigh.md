## Overview

Leigh is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], situated approximately ten miles west of [[manchester|Manchester]]. During the 1840s, it was a mixed textile centre combining silk and [[weaving|cotton weaving]]. Engels documents Leigh's role in the [[cottage-system|putting-out system]] of silk weaving, where middlemen transported woven goods between the domestic workshops of Leigh and the commercial warehouses of [[manchester|Manchester]].

## Engels's Account

### Silk Weaving and the Putting-Out System

Engels's sole mention of Leigh comes in a description of the silk-weaving trade's organisation:

> "comes twice a week to Leigh, in Lancashire, to gather up woven goods, bring new material, and pay wages" [007-007]

This passage describes the classic [[cottage-system|putting-out system]]: a [[manchester|Manchester]]-based middleman travelled to Leigh twice weekly to collect finished woven silk from domestic workshops, deliver fresh raw material, and pay wages. The system kept weavers formally independent — they worked in their own homes, not in factories — while rendering them economically dependent on the merchant-capitalist who controlled access to raw silk and markets.

### Domestic Industry

Leigh's silk weavers were part of a broader pattern of domestic [[manufacture|manufacturing]] that Engels analyses alongside [[factory-system|factory production]]. While not subjected to the [[factory-system|factory]] bell and overseer's discipline, domestic weavers faced their own forms of exploitation: piece-rate wages set by middlemen, the requirement to supply their own workshop space and lighting, and intense competition that drove [[wages|earnings]] down. The twice-weekly rhythm of collection and [[wages|payment]] meant families lived from hand to mouth, a single delayed [[wages|payment]] capable of precipitating hunger.

### Hybrid Economy

Leigh's economy illustrates the hybrid character of many [[lancashire|Lancashire]] towns — silk weaving coexisted with [[spinning|cotton spinning]] and the residual hand-loom weaving trade that was being steadily eliminated by the power-loom. Engels's broader analysis of the "ruin of the [[hand-loom-weaver|hand-loom weavers]]" applies to Leigh as it does to other towns where [[machinery|mechanisation]] in one sector destroyed livelihoods while creating (lower-paid) employment in another.
