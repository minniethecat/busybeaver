# General Workingmen's Association of London

## Overview

The General Workingmen's Association of London, more commonly known as the London Working Men's Association (LWMA), was founded in 1836 by the cabinet-maker and self-educated radical [[william-lovett|William Lovett]], with support from a small circle of skilled artisans and political reformers. It was the first durable working-class political organisation in Britain to articulate a comprehensive programme of democratic reform. In 1838, the LWMA published the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]] — drafted primarily by [[william-lovett|Lovett]] with assistance from the radical MP Francis Place — which became the founding document of the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. (008-023)

## Engels's Account

> The General Workingmen's Association of London, with William Lovett at its head, was the centre of the movement. (008-023)

Engels situates the LWMA in the lineage of working-class self-organisation, tracing its emergence from the disappointment that followed the 1832 Reform Bill. That Bill had enfranchised the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] while explicitly excluding the [[proletariat|working class]] from the franchise. The LWMA represented the [[proletariat|working class]]'s first organised response: if Parliament would not include workers, workers would demand inclusion on their own terms. The [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]], Engels notes, was not a radical break with British constitutional tradition but an attempt to reclaim it — universal suffrage, after all, had been the programme of the Levellers in the 1640s. (008-023)

Engels's treatment of the LWMA is notably more sympathetic than his treatment of middle-class reform organisations. The difference, for Engels, is authenticity: the LWMA was composed of working men advancing working-class demands, while the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] was composed of manufacturers advancing [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interests under the pretence of humanitarian concern. The LWMA's leaders — [[william-lovett|Lovett]], Henry Hetherington, James Watson — were self-educated artisans who had been blacklisted, imprisoned, and harassed for their publishing and organising activities. They had earned the right to speak for the [[proletariat|working class]]. (008-023)

## Activities

The LWMA's primary contribution was the articulation of the six points of the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]]. These demands were drafted through a process of deliberation within the Association's membership and published as a parliamentary bill — a deliberate framing choice that asserted the [[proletariat|working class]]'s right to legislate. The LWMA also functioned as a hub for radical publishing, correspondence with provincial working-class organisations, and the organisation of the first Chartist National Convention in 1839. (008-028)

After the first Chartist petition was rejected by Parliament in 1839, the LWMA's influence waned as the movement's centre of gravity shifted northward to the industrial districts, where [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]]'s more confrontational style resonated. But the LWMA's founding role was decisive: it transformed working-class grievance into a constitutional programme, giving [[chartism|Chartism]] a coherence that distinguished it from the episodic riots and machine-breaking of earlier decades. (008-028)
