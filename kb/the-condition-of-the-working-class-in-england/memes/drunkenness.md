# Drunkenness

## Definition

Drunkenness — the habitual or episodic heavy consumption of alcohol, especially cheap gin and adulterated beer — was one of the most widely remarked-upon characteristics of the English [[proletariat|working class]] in the 1830s and 1840s. Engels acknowledges the prevalence of drunkenness among [[factory-system|factory]] hands, miners, [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers, and the Irish poor, but he refuses to treat it as a cause of working-class degradation. Instead, he argues that drunkenness is the effect — the worker drinks because drink is the only relief available from a life of exhausting labour, physical pain, and hopeless privation. The temperance movement, which treated drink as the root of poverty, had the causal arrow backward: poverty caused drink, not the reverse. (004-002, 005-009)

## Engels's Analysis

> Liquor is almost their only source of pleasure, and all things conspire to make it attractive to them. The workingman comes home from his work tired and exhausted; he finds a comfortless, filthy, cheerless dwelling; he urgently needs some stimulant; he must have something to make his toil endurable, to make the prospect of the next day's suffering less dreadful. (005-009)

Engels's analysis of drunkenness is fundamentally environmental. The worker who spends twelve or fourteen hours in the heat, noise, and dust of a [[factory-system|factory]]; who returns to a damp cellar or an overcrowded cottage; who eats adulterated [[diet|food]] that fails to nourish; and who has no access to parks, libraries, or decent public spaces — this worker will drink because drinking is one of the few pleasures available to him. Engels does not romanticise the gin-shop; he describes it as a squalid institution whose patrons emerge sicker and more desperate than they entered. But he insists that the gin-shop would not exist without the [[factory-system|factory]], the cellar dwelling, and the competitive wage system that made life unbearable. (005-009, 005-024)

> The temperance societies have accomplished little or nothing, because the causes of intemperance remain. (005-009)

Engels is scathing about the temperance movement. The teetotal societies that sprang up in the 1830s, often with middle-class leadership and evangelical rhetoric, urged workers to sign the pledge and foreswear drink. Engels notes that some workers did so, and that their health and finances improved as a result. But the temperance societies, he argues, treated a symptom while leaving the disease untouched. A worker who stopped drinking remained poor, remained overworked, and remained housed in conditions that made sobriety a daily ordeal. The temperance movement was, in Engels's view, a characteristically bourgeois response to a social problem — an effort to reform the worker's morals rather than the system that made moral life impossible. (005-009)

> The inevitable consequence is drunkenness and all that is generally called dissipation. (006-051)

In the [[factory-system|factory]] districts, Engels notes, the connection between labour and drink was particularly direct. The heat of the spinning and weaving sheds — maintained at a tropical temperature to prevent the thread from breaking — created an intense thirst that workers slaked with whatever was available. Factory owners sometimes provided beer on the premises, or allowed publicans to sell it at the [[factory-system|factory]] gate. The "Saint Monday" tradition, by which workers took Monday off to recover from the weekend's drinking, was both a survival strategy — the body could not sustain a six-day week of fourteen-hour days — and an economic necessity for the publican and the distiller. Engels notes that every trade downturn produced a spike in drunkenness, as workers with nothing to do and no prospect of employment sought oblivion in the gin-shop. (006-051, 006-004, 006-016)

> The excessive labour of all miners must engender drunkenness is self-evident. (009-010)

Among miners, Engels presents drunkenness as nearly universal — and as the direct consequence of the most physically punishing labour in the industrial economy. The collier who spent his day underground in heat and darkness, carrying loads of coal up ladders on his back, emerged to a world in which the only available relief was the public house. Engels quotes the testimony of mine-owners and physicians confirming that the [[mining|colliers]] were the most drunken of all workers, and he notes — as he does throughout — that their drunkenness was not a moral failing but a rational response to conditions that a sober man could scarcely endure. (009-010)

> The Irish have brought with them filth and drunkenness. (004-002)

Engels's discussion of Irish immigration includes a treatment of Irish drinking habits that is notably harsh in its phrasing, reflecting the ethnic prejudices of his time. He argues that Irish immigrants — driven from their country by famine and land confiscation — brought with them a pattern of heavy drinking that merged with, and intensified, the drunkenness already endemic in the English working-class districts. The Irish worker, he notes, lived at a standard below even the English poor, subsisting on potatoes and spending what little surplus he had on whiskey. But even here, Engels treats the behaviour as environmentally determined: the Irish peasant, expropriated from his land and crowded into an English cellar, drank for the same reason the English [[factory-system|factory]] hand did — because the alternative was to face unendurable conditions with a clear head. (004-002)

## Historical Context

The gin craze of the eighteenth century — the mass consumption of cheap spirits documented by Hogarth in "Gin Lane" (1751) — had been partly checked by the Gin Acts, but heavy drinking remained endemic among the English poor through the early nineteenth century. The Beer Act of 1830, which liberalised the sale of beer and permitted any householder to open a beer-shop on [[wages|payment]] of a two-guinea licence, produced a dramatic increase in the number of drinking establishments. Engels notes that the beer-shops were often cleaner and more respectable than the gin-palaces, but that the overall effect of the Act was to make alcohol still more readily available to a population that already drank too much. The temperance movement, which began in the 1820s and gained momentum in the 1830s, was the first organised attempt to address working-class drinking on a national scale.
