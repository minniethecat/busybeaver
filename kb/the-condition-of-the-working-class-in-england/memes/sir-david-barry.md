## Overview

Sir David Barry was a British physician and surgeon who served as a commissioner on the 1833 Factory Inquiry Commission. His evidence, frequently cited by Engels, documented the severe physiological damage inflicted on children and young workers by prolonged factory labour (006-019, 006-020).

## Evidence to the Factory Commission

Barry's testimony focused on the physical effects of factory work on growing bodies. He documented specific medical conditions in detail:

> The unfavourable influences of millwork upon the hands are the following...
>
> (006-021)

Engels notes that Barry "frequently observed" flattening of the foot among child operatives, a condition also documented by physicians and surgeons in Leeds (006-020). This deformity resulted from the continuous standing posture required by mill work, often for fourteen hours or more per day.

Barry found that the physical damage was not limited to exceptional cases. Engels cites Barry's testimony that in stronger children with better nutrition, the effects manifested differently — as chronic pain in the back, hips, and legs rather than visible deformity. Even these relatively fortunate cases suffered lasting damage:

> In cases, in which a stronger constitution, better food, and other more favourable circumstances enabled the young operative to resist this effect of a barbarous exploitation, we find, at least, pain in the back, hips, and legs.
>
> (006-020)

## Case Studies

Barry's evidence included individual case histories that Engels reproduces. One such case was Thomas McDurt, a workman described as having "slight fever" and other symptoms of systemic physical breakdown from factory labour (006-035).

Barry's reports "contain hundreds of examples" of factory-related afflictions. Engels observes that Barry and the other medical witnesses "know almost no operative who did not suffer from some of these affections" (006-020).

## Respiratory Conditions in Flax Mills

Barry testified specifically about the health hazards of flax-spinning. Along with Stuart and Mackintosh, he "expressed himself in the most vigorous terms as to the unwholesomeness of this work, and the small consideration shown by most of the manufacturers for the health of the girls who do it" (006-038). The air in flax-spinning rooms was filled with fibre dust that caused respiratory disease among workers, particularly the young women and girls employed in this branch of the industry.

## Role in the Factory Commission

Barry served alongside fellow commissioners Tufnell, Cowell, Hawkins, Drinkwater, Power, and Loudon. While Engels criticises Cowell, Tufnell, and Hawkins for having "almost wholly neglected" the physiological effects of the factory system in Lancashire, he notes that Barry's evidence, along with Tufnell's, "are less directed to this point" but still provide important individual examples (006-019).

Engels cites Barry extensively in his endnotes to the 1845 English edition, drawing from Barry's evidence at pages 6, 8, 13, and 21 of the Factories Inquiry Commission's Report (013-005, 013-006).
