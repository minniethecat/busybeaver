## Definition

Social war (socialer Krieg) is the term Engels uses to characterise the fundamental condition of society under capitalism. Drawing on Hobbes's conception of the state of nature as a "war of all against all," Engels argues that capitalist competition reproduces this war within civil society itself. The social war is not a metaphor but a description: the same logic of mutual destruction that Hobbes ascribed to the pre-social state operates within the market, the [[factory-system|factory]], the street, and the home. "The social war, the war of each against all, is here openly declared" (002-003).

## Engels's Analysis

### The Hobbesian Framework

Engels explicitly invokes the Hobbesian formula: "everywhere social warfare, every man's house in a state of siege" (002-004). But where Hobbes saw the war of all against all as the condition that the social contract was designed to escape, Engels sees it as the condition that capitalist society actively produces. The bourgeoisie does not transcend the state of nature; it universalises it, making competition the sole principle of social organisation and denying workers the protection that property affords the propertied.

### Competition as the Weapon

"[[competition|Competition]] is the weapon with which this social warfare is carried on" (002-005). Engels is precise: competition is not the cause of social war but its instrument. The underlying cause is the division of society into property-owners and propertyless, and the treatment of labour-power as a commodity to be bought at the lowest possible price. [[competition|Competition]] is the surface form taken by a deeper antagonism: the bourgeoisie's collective interest in maximising surplus value versus the proletariat's collective interest in survival.

### The Position of the Non-Possessing Class

The social war places "the non-possessing class in a position which makes it impossible for them to escape the consequences" (002-007). This is the core of Engels's argument: the worker is not a free contractor in a voluntary exchange but a combatant in a war he cannot win and cannot quit. The worker must sell his labour-power or starve; the employer can wait. Every aspect of working-class life — the insanitary dwelling, the adulterated [[diet|food]], the twelve-hour working day, the [[ignorance|lack of education]] — is a wound inflicted in this one-sided war.

### The Destruction of Social Ties

Engels traces the consequences of social war beyond the economic sphere into morality and family life. "The social order makes family life almost impossible for the worker" (005-041). In a society organised around mutual hostility, "all social ties" give way (005-043), and the worker is isolated, thrown back upon his individual resources in a struggle he cannot win alone. This atomisation is functional for capitalism: isolated workers are cheaper and safer than organised workers. The social war is thus a political strategy as well as an economic condition.

### The Inability to Escape

Engels emphasises the worker's "inability to do anything towards gaining an assured position" (005-009). The social war is not a contest in which both sides have a chance; it is a siege in which one side holds all the fortifications. The worker's wages are driven to the minimum of [[diet|subsistence]], his dwelling is whatever the landlord will rent at that price, his [[diet|food]] is whatever the market will sell at that price. Every transaction is an act of war, and the worker enters each one already defeated.

### From Social War to Social Revolution

Engels's concept of social war contains an implicit political programme. If capitalist society is a state of war, then working-class resistance — trade unions, strikes, [[chartism|Chartism]] — is not aggression but self-defence. And the ultimate resolution of social war is not a truce but a transformation: "an imminent social revolution" (P01-010) that will end the war by abolishing the class division that produces it. The social war concept thus links Engels's descriptive analysis to his revolutionary politics.
