# Robert Hyde Greg

## Life

Robert Hyde Greg (1795–1875) was a leading [[manchester|Manchester]] [[cotton-industry|cotton manufacture]]r and a prominent figure in the Liberal politics of the industrial North. He inherited and expanded Quarry Bank Mill at Styal in Cheshire, one of the best-known [[factory-system|factory]] communities of the early industrial period. The Greg family was at the centre of the [[manchester|Manchester]] [[manufacture|manufacturing]] elite: Robert Hyde Greg's father, Samuel Greg, had founded Quarry Bank in 1784, and the family was closely connected to the networks of Liberal reform that included the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] and the campaign for [[free-trade|Free Trade]].

Greg was a serious-minded employer who built housing, a school, and a church for his workers — the model of the "country manufacturer" whose paternalism Engels would subject to devastating critique.

## Role in Engels's Account

Greg appears in Engels's text in two sharply contrasting guises: first as an honest witness who inadvertently confirms the physical deterioration of [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, and second as the calculated manager of appearances who conceals the realities of [[factory-system|factory]] life from visiting observers.

In the first instance, Engels quotes Greg on the stunting of [[factory-system|factory]] workers' bodies. Reporting on the weight of young operatives, Engels notes:

> "One of the largest manufacturers of Manchester, leader of the opposition against the workingmen, I think Robert Hyde Greg himself, said, on one occasion, that if things went on as at present, the operatives of Lancashire would soon be a race of pygmies." (006-029)

The admission is remarkable: a leading manufacturer, opposing [[factory-system|factory]] reform, concedes that the system he profits from is producing a physically degenerate population. The accompanying statistics — 46 youths from a Sunday school, with the 26 employed in mills averaging 104.5 pounds against 117.7 pounds for the 20 not employed in mills (006-029) — give quantitative precision to Greg's alarming prediction. Engels uses this concession to devastating effect: even the manufacturers themselves cannot deny the evidence before their eyes.

In the second instance, Greg serves a quite different rhetorical purpose. Engels constructs an extended satirical portrait of how a "respectable" [[manchester|Manchester]] manufacturer receives a visiting foreign inquirer:

> "You come to Manchester, you wish to make yourself acquainted with the state of affairs in England. You naturally have good introductions to respectable people. You drop a remark or two as to the condition of the workers. You are made acquainted with a couple of the first Liberal manufacturers, Robert Hyde Greg, perhaps, Edmund Ashworth, Thomas Ashton, or others." (013-008)

The manufacturer escorts the visitor through an "admirably arranged building," a [[factory-system|factory]] in the country with "lofty, airy rooms," "fine machinery," and "here and there a healthy-looking operative." He provides "an excellent lunch" and conducts the visitor to "new, clean and neat" cottages — "naturally only to overlookers, mechanics, etc." The employer's presence ensures no "indiscreet questions" are asked. (013-008)

Engels then lists what is concealed: the truck shop, the workers' hatred of the manufacturer, the use of the works school "to train children to subordination," the [[factory-system|factory]] reading-room that carries only pro-bourgeois newspapers, the dismissal of workers who read Chartist or Socialist literature. The entire performance is designed to present:

> "an easy, patriarchal relation... what the bourgeoisie promises the workers if they become its slaves, mentally and morally." (013-008)

## Context

The Greg family's Quarry Bank Mill was, in many respects, a model [[factory-system|factory]] by the standards of its time — healthier than the urban mills of [[manchester|Manchester]], with better housing and more amenities. Engels does not dispute this. His critique cuts deeper: the paternalism of the "country manufacturer" is not an alternative to exploitation but its more insidious form. The worker at Quarry Bank may breathe cleaner air than the operative in an [[ancoats]] cellar dwelling, but he remains a wage-slave whose family's [[diet|subsistence]] depends absolutely on the employer's will.

The phrase "patriarchal servitude" (013-008) captures Engels's central argument: capitalist relations of production can wear a benevolent face without changing their underlying character. Greg's model village is simply the "country manufacture" variant of the same system that produces the horrors of [[manchester|Manchester's]] [[housing|slums]].

Greg also appears as a figure in the political struggle over [[factory-system|factory]] reform. Engels describes him as "leader of the opposition against the workingmen" (006-029) — a prominent voice among those manufacturers who resisted the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] and the Ten Hours' movement. His Liberal politics aligned him with [[richard-cobden|Richard Cobden]] and [[john-bright|John Bright]] in the campaign for [[free-trade|Free Trade]], which Engels would later criticise as a strategy for reducing wages by cheapening the cost of workers' [[diet|subsistence]].

## Legacy

Quarry Bank Mill is now a National Trust property and museum, preserving both the physical fabric of early industrial production and the complex story of the Greg family's relationship with their workforce. Engels's portrait of Greg as the archetypal paternalist manufacturer remains one of the most quoted passages in discussions of early industrial labour relations, precisely because it refuses the simple dichotomy of good and bad employers in favour of a structural analysis of the wage relation itself.
