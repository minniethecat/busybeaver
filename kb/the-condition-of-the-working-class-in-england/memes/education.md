# Education

## Definition

Education, in Engels's treatment, refers to the formal and informal instruction — or more often its absence — available to working-class children and adults in industrial England. Unlike the middle-class reformers who debated curriculum and pedagogy, Engels treats education primarily as a question of access: who receives instruction, of what kind, and to what end. His conclusion, supported by parliamentary testimony and statistical returns, is that the English bourgeoisie systematically denied education to the proletariat because an educated [[proletariat|working class]] would be less docile, less exploitable, and more likely to organise against its oppressors. (005-020, 005-024)

## Engels's Analysis

> The bourgeoisie has but one interest in the education of the proletariat — that of keeping it within the bounds which the interest of the bourgeoisie prescribes. It bestows upon them only so much education as lies in the interest of the bourgeoisie; and that is not much. (005-020)

Engels argues that the educational provision for working-class children was deliberately minimal. Factory children who worked twelve or fourteen hours a day could not attend school in any meaningful sense; the few hours of schooling mandated by the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] were often provided in the [[factory-system|factory]] itself, by an overseer who could neither read nor write. The church schools — National Society and British and Foreign School Society — taught reading but not writing, on the principle that a working-class child who could read the Bible was adequately instructed, while one who could write might forge his master's signature. The result was a population in which a substantial minority could not sign their own names on the marriage register. (005-020, 007-004)

> A class about whose education no one troubles himself, which is a play-ball to a thousand chances, knows no security in life — what incentives has it to be provident? (005-040)

The absence of education was not merely an intellectual privation but a moral one. Engels argues that the worker who has never been taught to read, who has received no moral or religious instruction beyond the rote catechism of the Sunday school, and who has been treated from childhood as an instrument of production rather than as a human being, cannot reasonably be expected to display the bourgeois virtues of providence, temperance, and foresight. The [[prostitution|vice]]s Engels catalogues — drunkenness, sexual licence, reckless improvidence — are, in his analysis, the natural consequences of an educational void deliberately maintained by the employing class. (005-040, 007-006)

> 0.22 had enjoyed a higher education, while the degree of education of the rest was very limited. (005-042)

Engels deploys statistical evidence — from prison registers, marriage registers, and parliamentary returns — to demonstrate the scale of educational deprivation. In the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts, he reports, a significant fraction of the adult population was functionally illiterate. Among [[child-labour|factory children]], the situation was worse: the [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] found children of twelve and fourteen who could not name the monarch or the Apostle who betrayed Christ. Engels presents these statistics not as failures of individual ambition but as structural outcomes of a system in which the child's labour was worth more to the mill-owner than the child's mind. (005-042, 006-012)

> They are almost wholly without education, least of all do they receive a religious education. (007-004)

In the later chapters, Engels returns to the theme of education as a class marker. The [[factory-system|factory]] operatives of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], he notes, had begun to demand education for themselves and their children — a demand that the bourgeoisie resisted because an educated [[proletariat|working class]] would be a politically conscious one. The [[chartism|Chartist movement]], with its reading-rooms, its newspapers, and its lectures, was itself an educational project: the workers, denied instruction by the state and the church, set about educating themselves. This self-education, Engels suggests, was the bourgeoisie's worst nightmare — and the proletariat's best hope. (007-004, 007-006)

## Historical Context

The educational landscape Engels describes was shaped by the absence of a national system of elementary education — England would not introduce compulsory schooling until the Forster Act of 1870. In the 1840s, working-class education was provided by a patchwork of voluntary societies (the National Society, the British and Foreign School Society), [[factory-system|factory]] schools of negligible quality, and the [[sunday-school|Sunday schools]] that taught religious literacy but little else. The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833 required two hours of schooling per day for child workers but provided no mechanism for enforcement; mill-owners routinely flouted the requirement, and the [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors were too few to compel compliance.
