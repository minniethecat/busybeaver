## Overview

St. Helens was a rapidly growing industrial town in south [[lancashire|Lancashire]] during the 1840s, situated between [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]]. Its economy was based on [[coal|coal mining]], glass [[manufacture|manufacturing]], and chemical production. The town lay at the terminus of the Sankey [[canal|Canal]] (opened 1757), one of Britain's earliest industrial [[canal|waterway]]s, which connected the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] coalfield to the River Mersey and the port of [[liverpool|Liverpool]].

## Engels's Account

### Tramping Artisans

Engels mentions St. Helens in the context of "tramping" — the practice by which unemployed workers walked from town to town seeking work, often supported by trade union travelling funds:

> "A workingman, being on tramp, came to St. Helens, in Lancashire, and there sought employment." [006-009]

The tramping system was an important feature of early trade unionism, providing a safety net for skilled workers during periods of local unemployment and facilitating the spread of union organisation across industrial districts.

### The Sankey Canal

Engels references St. Helens as a landmark in the history of Britain's canal infrastructure:

> "In Lancashire from Sankey Brook to St. Helen's; and in 1759, James Brindley commenced the Duke of Bridgewater's Canal." [P04-019]

The Sankey [[canal|Canal]] (also known as the St. Helens [[canal|Canal]]) was opened in 1757, predating Brindley's more famous Bridgewater [[canal|Canal]] by two years. It was built to transport coal from the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] coalfield to the growing chemical and glass industries of St. Helens and onward to [[liverpool|Liverpool]]. This early transport infrastructure was a crucial enabler of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]'s industrialisation, providing cheap bulk transport decades before [[railway|railways]] reached the region.
