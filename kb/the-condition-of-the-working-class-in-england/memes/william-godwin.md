# William Godwin

## Life

William Godwin (1756–1836) was an English political philosopher, novelist, and radical intellectual. His major work, *An Enquiry Concerning Political Justice and Its Influence on General Virtue and Happiness* (1793), was one of the most influential works of radical political philosophy published in Britain during the revolutionary era of the 1790s. Godwin argued for the perfectibility of human beings through reason, the abolition of government and property, and the transformation of society through rational persuasion rather than violent revolution.

Godwin was married to the feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft, who died shortly after giving birth to their daughter Mary (later Mary Shelley, author of *Frankenstein*). His philosophical circle included many of the leading radical and literary figures of the period. By the 1840s, when Engels was writing, Godwin's direct political influence had waned, but his philosophical works continued to circulate among working-class readers.

## Role in Engels's Account

Godwin appears in Engels's text in a single rich paragraph that discusses the intellectual culture of the English [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels is arguing, against bourgeois assumptions, that the [[proletariat]] has attained a level of independent education and philosophical sophistication that surpasses that of the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]]:

> "The two great practical philosophers of latest date, Bentham and Godwin, are, especially the latter, almost exclusively the property of the proletariat; for though Bentham has a school within the Radical bourgeoisie, it is only the proletariat and the Socialists who have succeeded in developing his teachings a step forward." (008-039)

The claim is striking: Godwin's philosophy, rooted in Enlightenment rationalism and anarchist political theory, had been abandoned by the bourgeoisie in favour of more self-serving doctrines, while the [[proletariat|working class]] had taken it up and extended it. Engels presents this as evidence of the intellectual vitality of the proletarian movement — workers read not merely pamphlets and newspapers, but "the epoch-making products of modern philosophical, political, and poetical literature."

The context is Engels's discussion of working-class reading habits. He notes that [[percy-bysshe-shelley|Shelley]] and [[lord-byron|Byron]] "find most of their readers in the proletariat" and that the Socialist institutes have translated "the French materialists, Helvetius, Holbach, Diderot, etc., and disseminated them, with the best English works, in cheap editions." (008-039) Godwin and [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] represent the philosophical dimension of this working-class intellectual culture.

## Context

Godwin's *Political Justice* had argued that government and property were the sources of human misery and that rational enlightenment would eventually render both unnecessary. These ideas resonated with the [[robert-owen|Owen]]ite and early Socialist movements that Engels observed in England. Engels's claim that Godwin had become "almost exclusively the property of the proletariat" reflects the real process by which Godwin's radicalism, too extreme for the post-revolutionary bourgeoisie, was preserved and developed within the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]].

The pairing of Godwin with [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] as "practical philosophers" is revealing. Engels values both thinkers not as abstract theorists but as contributors to a practical programme of social transformation. Godwin's rationalist anarchism and [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s utilitarian calculus both provided intellectual resources for the critique of existing institutions.

## Legacy

Godwin's *Political Justice* is now recognised as a foundational text of philosophical anarchism and a major work of late Enlightenment political thought. Engels's testimony to Godwin's popularity among working-class readers in the 1840s provides valuable evidence for the intellectual history of the early labour movement — a history in which radical philosophy, [[socialism|Owenite socialism]], and Chartist politics overlapped and cross-fertilised. The claim that the proletariat, not the bourgeoisie, was the true inheritor of the Enlightenment's radical promise would become a recurring theme in [[karl-marx|Marx]]ist thought.
