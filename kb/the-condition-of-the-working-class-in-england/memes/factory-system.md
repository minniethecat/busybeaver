# Factory System

## Definition

The factory system is the mode of industrial production that concentrates workers, [[machinery|machines]], and production processes under one roof, driven by centralised power sources (steam or water). Regulated by the [[factory-act|Factory Act]], it encompasses mills for spinning and weaving wool, silk, cotton, and flax. Engels treats the factory system as the archetypal institution of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism — the site where the proletariat is created, exploited, and ultimately organised into a class. (P04-008, 006-001)

## Engels's Analysis

> The factory system, the beginning of which was thus made, received in 1764 a fresh impulse through the invention of the spinning-jenny. (P04-008)

Engels opens his analysis of the factory system by emphasising its novelty. It was "an entirely novel creation of the industrial period," with no precedent in earlier forms of production. The factory was not merely a larger workshop; it represented a qualitative transformation in the relation between worker and employer. The independent craftsman who owned his tools and set his own hours was replaced by the factory operative who owned nothing, worked by the clock, and submitted to the discipline of the machine. (P04-008, 007-001)

The physical effects of factory labour on workers — particularly children — are among Engels's most harrowing descriptions. He documents the testimony of factory surgeons and inspectors who observed the systematic deformation of young bodies: curved spines, flattened chests, deformed pelvises from prolonged standing, and the peculiar "factory leg" — knock-knees and bent bones caused by years of standing at [[machinery|machines]]. (006-019, 006-025)

> I can bear witness that the factory system in Bradford has engendered a multitude of cripples. (006-022)

This testimony from a factory surgeon carries the weight of direct observation. The crippling was not occasional or accidental but was the predictable, systematic result of children standing for twelve to fourteen hours a day at [[machinery|machines]], in overheated rooms filled with cotton dust. Engels uses this medical evidence to indict not individual mill-owners but the system itself: "the factory system" — not greedy Mr. X or cruel Mr. Y — "has engendered a multitude of cripples." The grammatical choice is deliberate: the subject is the system, not the person. (006-022, 006-025)

Despite its brutality, Engels acknowledges that the factory system concentrated workers in a way that enabled political organisation. The cotton workers of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], densely packed in [[manchester|Manchester]] and its satellite towns, became the most politically conscious and best-organised section of the English proletariat. They stood at the head of the labour movement, as their masters the [[manchester|Manchester]] manufacturers stood at the head of the bourgeois agitation. The factory, which destroyed the worker's body, also created the conditions for his political awakening. (006-041, 006-045)

## Effects on Workers' Health

### Child Labour and Mortality

The factory system began its consumption of human life at the earliest possible age. The Central Commission's report documented that manufacturers employed children rarely of five years, often of six, very often of seven, and usually of eight to nine years. The working-day for these children often lasted fourteen to sixteen hours, exclusive of meal-times (006-016). Engels presents this not as exceptional abuse but as the normal operating procedure of the factory system — a system that treated children's bodies as consumable inputs no different from cotton or coal.

> The report of the Central Commission relates that the manufacturers began to employ children rarely of five years, often of six, very often of seven, usually of eight to nine years; that the working-day often lasted fourteen to sixteen hours, exclusive of meal-times. (006-016)

### Premature Ageing and Mortality

The factory system consumed workers at an extraordinary rate. Engels marshals statistical evidence showing that factory operatives aged prematurely — men worn out by forty, women deformed by years of standing at machines. The mortality figures for factory districts far exceeded those for agricultural regions, and the death rate among operatives' children was especially shocking.

> The men wear out very early in consequence of the conditions under which they live and work. Most of them are unfit for hard work at forty years of age. (006-030)

The statistical evidence assembled by Engels is stark. In Harpur and Lanark, of 1,600 operatives employed in several factories, only 10 were over 45 years of age. In a survey of 22,094 operatives in Stockport and Manchester, the proportion of workers surviving past middle age was negligible — the factory used them up and discarded them (006-031). The surgeon Smellie of Glasgow, who treated operatives chiefly, stated bluntly that forty years was old age for a factory worker (006-032).

The effects on women were equally severe. Factory work produced distinctive deformities in the female body — contracted pelvis from prolonged standing, which led to difficult and dangerous childbirth. Midwives and accoucheurs testified that factory operatives underwent "more difficult confinement than other women" (006-033). The factory did not merely exploit women's labour; it damaged their capacity for reproduction, threatening the very continuity of the working class.

> The influence of factory-work upon the female physique also is marked and peculiar. The deformities entailed by long hours of standing are especially injurious to women in confinement, a circumstance which is testified to by several midwives. (006-033)
