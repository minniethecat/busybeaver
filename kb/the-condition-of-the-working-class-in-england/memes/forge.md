# Forge

## Definition

The forge, in the industrial landscape Engels describes, was the workplace where iron was heated in a charcoal or coal-fired hearth and shaped by hammering on an anvil. The forge worker — the blacksmith or the nailsmith — was one of the archetypal figures of pre-industrial craft production: a skilled artisan who controlled his tools, determined his own pace of work, and passed his trade from father to son. By the 1840s, the independent forge was being displaced by the iron foundry and the rolling mill — large-scale, capital-intensive enterprises that concentrated production and reduced the skilled smith to a wage labourer tending a machine. (005-009)

## Engels's Analysis

> He drank to forget the wretchedness of his condition. (005-009)

Engels discusses forge workers and other metalworkers in the context of his analysis of drunkenness and working-class demoralisation. The forge was one of the hottest, most physically demanding workplaces in the industrial economy. The smith worked in front of a furnace, often stripped to the waist, swinging a heavy hammer for twelve or fourteen hours a day. The combination of extreme heat, physical exhaustion, and the ready availability of [[drunkenness|drink]] (public houses often served as hiring halls for metalworkers) made the forge a site of particularly intense alcohol consumption. Engels treats this [[drunkenness|drink]]ing not as a moral failing but as a rational response to intolerable conditions — "the certainty of forgetting for an hour or two the wretchedness of his condition" (005-009).

The transformation of metalworking from a craft to an industry was, for Engels, another case study in the general process of proletarianisation. The village smith who had shod horses, repaired ploughs, and forged tools for his neighbours was replaced by the [[factory-system|factory]] producing standardised iron goods. The nailsmith who had worked at his own forge was reduced to a domestic outworker, competing with machine-made nails and earning a fraction of his former wage. And the skilled forge worker who found employment in the new ironworks found that his craft knowledge counted for little in a [[factory-system|factory]] where the pace of work was set by the machine and the division of labour reduced his contribution to a single repetitive operation. (005-009)

The physical toll of forge work was among the most severe Engels documented. The constant exposure to heat, the inhalation of coal smoke and metal particles, the strain of heavy hammering — all produced characteristic patterns of disease and premature death. Engels notes that forge workers, along with grinders and miners, had among the shortest life expectancies of any occupational group. The forge was, in the most literal sense, a workplace that consumed the worker's body. (005-009)

## Historical Context

The iron industry that Engels describes was in the midst of a technological revolution. The introduction of coke smelting in the eighteenth century had freed iron production from its dependence on charcoal, enabling the vast expansion of output that accompanied the railway boom of the 1830s and 1840s. The puddling furnace, invented by Henry Cort in 1784, had made it possible to produce wrought iron on an industrial scale. And the rolling mill had replaced the forge hammer for many operations, reducing the skilled smith's role to that of a machine tender. The forge survived in niche applications — tool-making, farriery, repair work — but as a site of large-scale production it was being superseded.

The forge worker's experience illustrates a broader pattern in Engels's analysis: the degradation of skilled labour by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. The craftsman who controlled his tools, determined his pace, and took pride in his product was being replaced by the operative who tended a machine, worked at the pace the machine dictated, and had no connection to the final product. This was not, in Engels's view, a temporary disruption but a permanent transformation — one that would only be reversed when the workers themselves took control of the [[capital|means of production]].
