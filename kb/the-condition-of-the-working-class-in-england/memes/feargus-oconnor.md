# Feargus O'Connor

## Life

Feargus O'Connor (1794–1855) was an Irish-born barrister, politician, and the most charismatic and controversial leader of the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. A nephew of the Irish republican Arthur O'Connor, Feargus was elected MP for Cork in 1832 but was unseated due to a property qualification dispute. He turned to the emerging [[labour-movement|working-class movement]] in England and became the dominant figure of radical politics in the late 1830s and 1840s.

O'Connor founded and edited the *Northern Star*, a weekly newspaper based in [[leeds|Leeds]] that became the most widely read working-class publication in Britain, with a circulation exceeding 50,000 at its peak. He was the leading advocate of "physical force" [[chartism|Chartism]] — the view that moral persuasion alone would not secure the Charter and that the threat, and if necessary the reality, of armed force must be part of the working-class strategy. His rivalry with [[william-lovett|William Lovett]] and the "moral force" faction shaped the internal politics of [[chartism|Chartism]] throughout its most active period.

## Role in Engels's Account

O'Connor appears in Engels's text in a single vivid image — not as a political analyst but as a symbol of working-class identity and defiance. In the midst of a long paragraph cataloguing the material deprivation of workers' clothing — fustian trousers, cotton shirts, paper caps — Engels suddenly shifts register:

> "When Feargus O'Connor, the Chartist leader, came to Manchester during the insurrection of 1842, he appeared, amidst the deafening applause of the workingmen, in a fustian suit of clothing." (002-056)

The detail is carefully chosen. Fustian — a heavy cotton fabric — was "the proverbial costume of the workingmen," who were "called 'fustian jackets,' and call themselves so in contrast to the gentlemen who wear broadcloth." O'Connor, a former MP and barrister who could certainly have afforded broadcloth, deliberately wore the uniform of the class whose cause he championed. The gesture was theatrical — "amidst the deafening applause" — but its political meaning was precise: the Chartist leader identified physically with the workers he led, refusing the symbolic markers that separated the middle-class politician from his constituents.

The 1842 insurrection to which Engels refers was the general strike that swept the industrial districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] in August of that year. O'Connor's appearance "in a fustian suit of clothing" was an act of solidarity at a moment of maximum class tension, when workers had withdrawn their labour en masse and the authorities feared revolution.

## Context

Engels's treatment of O'Connor is characteristically indirect: he does not analyse O'Connor's politics, quote his speeches, or assess his leadership. Instead, he uses a single potent image — the fustian suit — to evoke the entire relationship between the Chartist leader and the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]]. It is a literary technique that trusts the concrete detail to carry the political argument: the suit says what a paragraph of analysis might labour to explain about class identity, symbolic solidarity, and the theatre of popular politics.

O'Connor's "physical force" [[chartism|Chartism]] eventually led to his political marginalisation and, after 1848, his mental breakdown. He died in 1855, his reputation eclipsed by the movement's failure. Engels, writing in 1845, could not have known this outcome, and his text captures O'Connor at the height of his influence.

## Legacy

O'Connor remains a contested figure in the history of British radicalism — hailed by some as a genuine tribune of the [[proletariat|working class]], dismissed by others as a demagogue whose extremism doomed [[chartism|Chartism]] to defeat. Engels's image of the fustian suit captures the paradox: O'Connor's theatrical radicalism was both the source of his connection to the masses and, perhaps, the limit of his political effectiveness.
