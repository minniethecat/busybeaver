# Competition

## Definition

Competition, in Engels's political-economic framework, is the organising principle of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. It manifests as competition among workers for employment (driving wages down), competition among manufacturers for markets (driving innovation and exploitation), and competition among nations for industrial supremacy (driving imperial expansion). Engels does not treat competition as a natural law but as a historically specific feature of capitalist production — and one that is ultimately destructive to both the worker and the system itself. (003-001)

## Engels's Analysis

> We have seen in the introduction how competition created the proletariat at the very beginning of the industrial movement. (003-001)

Engels traces three mechanisms by which competition generates the [[proletariat|working class]]. First, boom-period demand raises weavers' wages, drawing agricultural peasants off the land and into [[manufacture|manufacturing]] — only for the subsequent bust to leave them stranded as proletarians with no land to return to. Second, competition among farmers drives the consolidation of land into large farms, displacing smallholders who become [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers or migrate to the towns. Third, competition among manufacturers ruins the petty bourgeoisie — independent artisans and small shopkeepers — who sink into the proletariat when their workshops cannot match [[factory-system|factory production]]. Competition thus does not merely regulate an existing labour market; it creates that market by destroying pre-capitalist forms of livelihood. (003-001)

> Competition is the sharpest weapon against the proletariat in the hands of the bourgeoisie. (003-002)

Once the proletariat exists, competition becomes the bourgeoisie's instrument of control. The worker who demands higher wages is replaced by another who will work for less. The worker who joins a union is blacklisted. The worker who strikes is starved into submission. Engels understands that competition among workers is not a free choice but a structural compulsion: the worker must sell his labour to survive, and must sell it at whatever price the market will bear. The [[manufacturer|factory owner]], by contrast, can wait — his capital gives him the power to hold out through a strike, to stockpile inventory, to relocate production. Competition is not a level playing field but a mechanism that systematically advantages capital over labour. (003-002)

> Competition is the completest expression of the battle of all against all which rules in modern civil society. This battle, a battle for life, for existence, for everything, in case of need a battle of life and death, is fought not between the different classes of society only, but also between the individual members of these classes. Each is in the way of the other, and each seeks to crowd out all who are in his way, and to put himself in their place. The workers are in constant competition among themselves as the members of the bourgeoisie among themselves. (003-002)

The competitive dynamic also operates among the bourgeoisie themselves. Engels observes that the petty swindling and wage-theft of early [[capital|industrial capital]]ism — the truck system, the falsification of weights and measures, the adulteration of goods — gave way, over time, to more "moralised" forms of exploitation. This was not because the bourgeoisie became more ethical but because competition among large manufacturers made petty theft uneconomical. The millionaire mill-owner could afford to pay slightly higher wages and observe the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] because his capital allowed him to undersell smaller competitors who depended on wage-theft to survive. Competition thus accelerated the concentration of capital, crushing the small capitalist as remorselessly as it crushed the hand-weaver. (P01-004)

## The Surplus Population

A central consequence of competition, in Engels's analysis, is the creation of a permanent surplus population — an "industrial reserve army" of unemployed and underemployed workers. This surplus is not an accidental by-product of industrial capitalism but a structural necessity. By maintaining a pool of workers desperate for any employment, the surplus population disciplines the employed: the worker who demands higher wages knows that a replacement waits at the factory gate. (003-003)

The surplus population expands and contracts with the industrial cycle, but never disappears. During boom periods, factories absorb surplus workers rapidly; during crises, they discharge them in masses. Engels notes that even in relatively prosperous times, the Poor Law commissioners reported a million and a half paupers in England and Wales alone — a figure that excluded the greater multitude who struggled on without recourse to parish relief. This permanent pool of destitution was not a failure of the competitive system but its intended product. (003-003, 003-013)

> The proletarian is helpless; left to himself, he cannot live a single day. The bourgeoisie has gained a monopoly of all means of existence in the broadest sense of the word. What the proletarian needs, he can obtain only from this bourgeoisie, which is protected in its monopoly by the power of the State. (003-003)

The surplus population also functions as a regulator of wages through demographic pressure. Engels, following Adam Smith, observes that the demand for men — like the demand for any other commodity — regulates the production of men. When wages rise and the worker prospers, marriages multiply and more children survive, increasing the labour supply until wages are driven down again. The worker, in Engels's framing, is treated as a commodity whose reproduction is governed by the same laws of supply and demand as any manufactured article. (003-009)

### Competition Among Workers

The competition among workers for employment is, Engels argues, the "worst side of the present state of things" in its effect upon the worker. Each worker is pitted against every other: the employed against the unemployed, the skilled against the unskilled, the English worker against the Irish immigrant. This intra-class competition prevents the formation of collective resistance and drives wages to the barest subsistence minimum. The worker who will not accept the bourgeoisie's terms — however degrading — is replaced by one who will. (003-002, 003-004)

> Fine freedom, where the proletarian has no other choice than that of either accepting the conditions which the bourgeoisie offers him, or of starving, of freezing to death, of sleeping naked among the beasts of the forests! A fine "equivalent" valued at pleasure by the bourgeoisie! (003-004)

Engels draws a sharp parallel between the position of the "free" wage labourer and the slave. The difference, he contends, is only in the form of the transaction: the slave is sold once and for all, while the worker sells himself piecemeal — by the day, by the hour — always at the mercy of the market. Both are property of the possessing class; the worker's formal freedom is merely the freedom to choose which master to serve, or to starve. The ultimate limit to the downward pressure of competition upon wages is physiological: no worker will work for less than he needs to survive, for if he must starve, he will prefer to starve in idleness. (003-005, 003-008)
