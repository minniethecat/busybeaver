## Definition

Fever — specifically epidemic [[cholera|typhus]] — occupies a central place in Engels's empirical argument about the lethal consequences of industrial urbanisation. More than any single disease, [[cholera|fever]] serves in *The Condition of the Working Class in England* as a medical index of social conditions: its prevalence, distribution, and mortality rate map directly onto class structure. Engels treats [[cholera|fever]] not as a natural misfortune but as a socially produced phenomenon, "directly" attributable to "the bad state of the [[housing|dwellings]] in the matters of ventilation, drainage, and cleanliness" [005-005].

## Engels's Analysis

### Typhus as a Class Disease

Engels opens his discussion of [[cholera|fever]] with the unequivocal assertion that it is class-specific. Drawing on the official report on the sanitary condition of the [[proletariat|working class]], he states:

> Typhus, that universally diffused affliction, is attributed by the official report on the sanitary condition of the working-class, directly to the bad state of the dwellings in the matters of ventilation, drainage, and cleanliness. This report, compiled, it must not be forgotten, by the leading physicians of England from the testimony of other physicians, asserts that a single ill-ventilated court, a single blind alley without drainage, is enough to engender fever, and usually does engender it, especially if the inhabitants are greatly crowded. [005-005]

The disease "has the same character almost everywhere, and develops in nearly every case into specific [[cholera|typhus]]" [005-005]. Its geography is the geography of poverty: "the working-people's quarters of all [[urban|great town]]s and cities, and in single ill-built, ill-kept streets of smaller places."

### London and Manchester: Fever in the Metropolis

In [[london|London]], Engels notes that the [[cholera|fever]] "has now prevailed for a considerable time," with its "extraordinary violence in the year 1837" prompting the official sanitary report [005-005]. The [[london|London]] Fever Hospital recorded 1,462 patients in 1843 — "418 more than in any previous year" [005-005]. Engels notes that many victims were "working-people from the country, who had endured the severest privation while migrating, and, after their arrival, had slept hungry and half-naked in the streets, and so fallen victims to the [[cholera|fever]]" [005-005]. The mortality rate reached 16.5 per cent.

In [[manchester|Manchester]], "in the worst quarters of the Old Town, [[ancoats|Ancoats]], Little Ireland, etc., it is rarely extinct" [005-005]. Engels observes that the [[cholera|fever]]'s persistence is taken for granted — an accepted feature of working-class existence.

### Scotland and Ireland: Fever at Scale

The Scottish and Irish epidemics provide Engels with evidence of [[cholera|fever]]'s catastrophic potential when concentrated poverty meets economic crisis:

> But the fury of the epidemic in all former periods seems to have been child's play in comparison with its ravages after the crisis of 1842. One-sixth of the whole indigent population of Scotland was seized by the fever. [005-006]

In [[glasgow|Glasgow]], "twelve percent of the population were seized in the year 1843; 32,000 persons, of whom thirty-two percent perished" — a mortality rate four times that of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[liverpool|Liverpool]] [005-006]. Engels notes that the illness "reached a crisis on the seventh and fifteenth days; on the latter, the patient usually became yellow, which our authority regards as an indication that the cause of the malady was to be sought in mental excitement and anxiety" [005-006].

Ireland's figures are even more staggering. In 1817–1818, "39,000 [[cholera|fever]] patients passed through the [[dublin|Dublin]] hospital," rising to 60,000 in a more recent year [005-006]. In Cork, the [[cholera|fever]] hospital received one-seventh of the population; in Limerick, one-fourth; and in the worst quarter of Waterford, "nineteen-twentieths of the whole population were ill of the [[cholera|fever]] at one time" [005-006].

### The Causal Mechanism: Privation and Immunity

Engels is careful to articulate the causal mechanism. Fever is not simply the result of bad air but of the interaction between physiological vulnerability and environmental conditions:

> Dr. Alison, who has made a careful study of this disease, attributes it directly to the want and the wretched condition of the poor... He asserts that privations and the insufficient satisfaction of vital needs are what prepare the frame for contagion and make the epidemic widespread and terrible. He proves that a period of privation, a commercial crisis or a bad harvest, has each time produced the typhus epidemic in Ireland as in Scotland, and that the fury of the plague has fallen almost exclusively on the working-class. [005-007]

A "noteworthy fact" in Alison's testimony: "the majority of persons who perish by [[cholera|typhus]] are fathers of families, precisely the persons who can least be spared by those dependent upon them" [005-007]. The disease strikes at the economic foundation of the working-class household.

### Childhood Fever Mortality

Engels devotes particular attention to childhood mortality from [[cholera|fever]] and related [[cholera|epidemic disease]]s. Comparing urban and rural [[mortality|death rate]]s, he marshals statistical evidence:

> Fatal cases of smallpox, measles, scarlet fever, and whooping cough, among small children, are four times more frequent; those of water on the brain are trebled, and convulsions ten times more frequent. [005-017]

In [[manchester|Manchester]], "more than fifty-seven percent of the children of the working-class perish before the fifth year, while but twenty percent of the children of the higher classes" die in the same period [005-017]. The comparison is devastating: working-class children are nearly three times as likely to die before age five as their bourgeois counterparts.

### Patent Medicines and Opiates

Engels documents a grim coping mechanism: the widespread use of opiate-based patent medicines, particularly among working mothers. Godfrey's Cordial, a laudanum preparation, was administered to infants to keep them quiet while mothers worked:

> They often begin to give this medicine to newly-born children, and continue, without knowing the effects of this "heartsease," until the children die. The less susceptible the child's system to the action of the opium, the greater the quantities administered. [005-011]

One [[nottingham|Nottingham]] apothecary "had, according to his own statement, used thirteen hundredweight of laudanum in one year in the preparation of Godfrey's Cordial" [005-011]. The children so treated "are pale, feeble, wilted, and usually die before completing the second year" [005-011]. Engels treats this not as parental negligence but as a structural consequence of women's industrial employment.

### The Moral Analogy

In a striking passage, Engels extends the [[cholera|fever]] metaphor to moral contagion. Quoting an authority who observes that "when the higher orders for their own profit have drawn the labouring-classes in great numbers into a small space, the contagion of guilt becomes rapid and unavoidable," Engels draws the parallel explicitly: "The lower orders... are frequently hardly more to be blamed for yielding to the temptations which surround them than for falling victims to the [[cholera|typhus]] [[cholera|fever]]" [005-031]. Moral and physical infection share the same aetiology — the crowded, un[[sanitary|sanitary conditions]] produced by [[capital|industrial capital]]ism.

## Historical Context

The 1830s–1840s saw the rise of the [[public-health|sanitary reform]] movement in Britain, prompted by Edwin Chadwick's *[[report-sanitary-condition|Report on the Sanitary Condition of the Labouring Population]]* (1842). The [[cholera|typhus]] epidemics of 1837–1843 generated official inquiries and the first systematic mapping of disease onto social class. Engels drew extensively on this literature, transforming its reformist conclusions into a revolutionary indictment: if disease was produced by social conditions, only social transformation could eliminate it.

## Related Concepts

Fever is connected to [[disease]] (the broader category of industrially-produced illness), [[housing]] (the built environment that concentrates contagion), [[mortality]] (the statistical measure of class differentials in death), [[pauperism]] (the economic condition that "prepares the frame for contagion"), [[overcrowding]] (the spatial condition of epidemic spread), and [[child-labour]] (the practice that drove mothers to administer opiate cordials).
