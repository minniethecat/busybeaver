# Working Day

## Definition

The working day, as Engels uses the term, is not merely the number of hours the worker labours but the contested boundary between the time the worker sells to the capitalist and the time he retains for himself — for rest, family, and human development. Engels distinguishes between the legal working day (as limited, haltingly, by the [[factory-act|Factory Acts]]) and the actual working day (which, in the absence of effective enforcement, routinely exceeded the legal limit). The working day is, in the book's analysis, the terrain on which the most immediate form of class conflict is fought: the capitalist's drive to extract maximum [[surplus-population|surplus labour]] versus the worker's struggle to preserve his physical and mental capacity. (003-009)

## Engels's Analysis

> The worker is compelled to labour as much each day as his strength can possibly permit. (003-009)

Engels documents working hours across multiple industries and categories of worker. In the cotton mills, a working day of fourteen to sixteen hours was common for adults; children of eight or nine worked twelve to fourteen hours. In the lace and hosiery trades, where domestic outwork predominated, hours were even longer because the worker, paid by the piece rather than the hour, had no fixed limit and was compelled by poverty to work as long as physical exhaustion permitted. In the [[mining|mines]], women and children dragged coal tubs through low passages for twelve-hour shifts, emerging into daylight only on Sundays. The working day, Engels shows, was not a fixed parameter of industrial production but a variable that capitalists pushed to its biological limit — and sometimes beyond. (003-009, 003-010)

> The operative must sacrifice health and strength. (006-021)

The physiological consequences of the long working day are a central theme of the chapters on "Single Branches of Industry" and "The [[factory-system|Factory System]] Generally." Engels documents the specific deformities and diseases produced by excessive hours in particular trades: the bent knees and relaxed ligaments of the lace-makers, the spinal curvatures of the potters, the lung diseases of the grinders. These were not accidental injuries but the systematic and predictable effects of a working day whose length was determined not by the worker's physical capacity but by the capitalist's competitive imperative. The worker who could not keep up was simply replaced — by another worker, by a child, or by a machine. (006-021)

The struggle to limit the working day is central to Engels's political narrative. The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833, which limited children's hours to nine per day (forty-eight per week) and required two hours of [[education|schooling]], is treated as a partial victory — achieved through decades of working-class agitation against fierce bourgeois resistance. But Engels emphasises its limitations: the Act did not apply to adults, its enforcement was inadequate (the four [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors were charged with covering the entire United Kingdom), and manufacturers devised elaborate systems of "relays" and false time-keeping to circumvent its provisions. The legal limitation of the working day was, in Engels's framework, a necessary but insufficient reform — a defensive victory that would have to be followed by more fundamental challenges to the capitalist ownership of the [[capital|means of production]]. (P01-004)

## Historical Context

The movement to limit the working day was the earliest and most sustained focus of English [[factory-system|factory]] reform. Beginning with the [[apprentices-act-1802|Health and Morals of Apprentices Act]] of 1802 (which limited [[pauperism|pauper]] apprentices to twelve hours), the campaign proceeded through the Cotton Mills Act of 1819 (which prohibited the employment of children under nine), the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833 (which established a limited inspectorate), and the [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours Act]] of 1847 (passed two years after Engels's book was published). Each Act was fiercely resisted by the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest, which argued — in terms Engels records and rebuts — that any limitation on hours would destroy British industrial competitiveness.

The philosophical basis for limiting the working day had been articulated by the [[factory-system|factory]] reform movement's intellectual leaders — particularly [[richard-oastler|Richard Oastler]], who coined the phrase "[[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] slavery" to describe child labour, and Lord [[lord-ashley|Ashley]] (later the Earl of [[lord-ashley|Shaftesbury]]), who led the parliamentary campaign. Engels treats these figures with a mixture of respect for their practical achievements and criticism of their paternalistic framework: they sought to regulate the exploitation of labour, not to abolish it. The [[proletariat|working class]]'s own organisations — the Short Time Committees, the trade unions — would be the agents of more fundamental change.
