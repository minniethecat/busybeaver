## Definition

The apprentice was a youth bound by legal indenture to serve a master craftsman for a fixed term — typically seven years — in exchange for training in a trade and eventual qualification as a journeyman. The system dated from the medieval guilds and presupposed a stable craft hierarchy: apprentice → journeyman → master. Engels documents how [[capital|industrial capital]]ism systematically destroyed every element of this structure, replacing the apprentice with the unskilled [[factory-system|factory]] child and the independent master with the capitalist manufacturer.

## Engels's Analysis

### From Apprentice to Factory Child

The pre-industrial apprentice lived in the master's household, received training in all aspects of a trade, and could reasonably expect to become a master himself. Engels describes how this was replaced: "in place of the former masters and apprentices, came great capitalists and working-men who had no hope of rising" (P04-023). The [[factory-system|factory]] system abolished the ladder from apprenticeship to independence. The "great capitalists" owned the [[capital|means of production]] absolutely; the "working-men" were permanently proletarianised, with no prospect of becoming masters.

### The Factory Apprentice as Cheap Labour

The apprentice system was perverted under industrialism. Engels documents how [[parish|parishes]] and poor-law authorities "bound children of seven to fourteen years to the manufacturers as apprentices" (006-014), treating them not as trainees but as a supply of cheap, captive labour. These parish apprentices were "lodged, fed, and clothed in the [[factory-system|factory]]" (006-014), working the same hours as adults but paid nothing beyond [[diet|subsistence]]. The indenture that had once protected the apprentice's right to training now bound the child to the manufacturer as a form of property.

### The Surviving Apprentice in Handicraft Trades

In the surviving handicraft trades, the old apprentice system persisted in attenuated form. Engels notes that in some trades, "small masters work with their apprentices in the shop at home" (007-009), and others "work alone, or with one or more apprentices, who serve them until reaching manhood" (007-010). But these remnant apprenticeships were not the benign institutions of medieval memory. The small master, pressed by competition from [[factory-system|factory production]], exploited his apprentices as intensively as any manufacturer, often housing them in the same wretched conditions as the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives.

### The Loss of Independence

The crucial transformation Engels identifies is the severing of the apprentice's path to independence. Under the old system, "the small employer to whom the apprentice still stood in a more or less patriarchal relation" (007-018) was a transitional figure — the apprentice could expect to become such a small employer himself. Under [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, this relation was broken. The apprentice who completed his term found no independent trade to enter; he became a [[wages|wage-labour]]er like any other, his skill devalued by machinery that could be operated by an unskilled child.

### The Trade Unions and Apprentice Limitation

Engels notes a paradoxical response from the skilled workers who survived into the industrial era: some trade unions sought "to limit the number of apprentices, and so to keep wages high" (008-008). This was a defensive strategy, an attempt to preserve craft scarcity in the face of machine competition. Engels neither condemns nor endorses this approach; he simply records it as a fact about the transition period, when skilled workers still had some bargaining power and sought to preserve it through apprenticeship restriction — a strategy that was ultimately doomed by technological change.

### The Broader Significance

The destruction of apprenticeship was not merely an economic change but a social and moral one. The apprentice system had provided, for all its flaws, a structure of intergenerational transmission: skills, standards, and a sense of craft identity passed from master to apprentice. The [[factory-system|factory]] system replaced this with a purely instrumental relation: the child as machine-minder, replaceable and disposable. The "patriarchal relation" Engels invokes was no idyll — it involved exploitation and often brutality — but it had the virtue of being a relation between persons, not between capital and labour-power.
