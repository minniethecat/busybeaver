# Sir James Graham

## Life

Sir James Graham (1792–1861) was a British statesman who served as Home Secretary in Sir [[robert-peel|Robert Peel]]'s government from 1841 to 1846. He entered Parliament as a Whig but moved steadily rightward, serving in Peel's Conservative cabinet and becoming one of the architects of the government's approach to [[factory-system|factory]] reform — an approach that Engels treats as a masterclass in the politics of concession: granting just enough reform to defuse agitation while preserving the essential interests of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class. (006-050)

## Role in Engels's Account

> The Home Secretary, Sir James Graham, proposed, in 1843, a bill limiting the working hours of children to six and a half hours per day. (006-050)

Graham appears in Engels's narrative as the author of the 1843 Factory Bill, which proposed to reduce children's [[working-day|working hours]] to six and a half per day while simultaneously placing [[factory-system|factory]] schools under the control of the Church of England. Engels analyses this bill as a classic example of bourgeois reform: it offered a genuine concession on hours — one that would limit the exploitation of child labour — but attached conditions that advanced the interests of the established church and extended its control over [[education|working-class education]].

The bill provoked fierce opposition from Dissenters (Nonconformist Protestants), who saw it as an attempt to establish Anglican hegemony over education. The [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest, already hostile to any limitation on hours, exploited this religious controversy to defeat the bill entirely. The result was that the children got neither shorter hours nor improved education — the only beneficiaries were the manufacturers, who continued to employ children for as long as they pleased. Engels treats this outcome as typical of parliamentary reform: the bourgeoisie uses sectarian divisions within the [[proletariat|working class]] and the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] to block reforms that would benefit workers while advancing measures that benefit capital.

Engels's analysis of the Graham bill also illuminates his broader critique of the state. The state, in Engels's framework, is not a neutral arbiter between classes but an instrument of class rule. When the Home Secretary proposes a Factory Bill, he does so not to protect workers but to manage social conflict — conceding enough to prevent unrest while ensuring that the essential structures of exploitation remain intact. Graham's bill is a case study in this logic of concession and containment.

## Context

The Graham bill of 1843 was one of the key legislative episodes in the [[factory-system|factory]] reform movement that Engels documents. The bill followed the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833, which had limited children's hours and established a [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate, but which — as Engels repeatedly notes — was inadequately enforced and left adult workers entirely unprotected. Graham's bill represented an attempt to extend protection while simultaneously advancing the church's educational agenda. The alliance between the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest and the Dissenters that defeated it was a temporary and cynical one — the manufacturers had no sympathy for Nonconformist religious scruples, and the Dissenters had no interest in defending child labour — but it was effective in blocking reform.

Graham's later career as Home Secretary included responsibility for the government's response to Chartist agitation. The same Home Secretary who proposed to limit children's [[working-day|working hours]] was also responsible for the police and military forces that suppressed Chartist meetings and imprisoned Chartist leaders. This duality — reform and repression as twin instruments of class rule — is central to Engels's analysis of the state.
