## Overview

Dundee was a major industrial city on the Firth of Tay in eastern Scotland, and one of Britain's principal centres of flax-spinning and linen manufacture in the 1840s. The introduction of power-driven flax-spinning machinery transformed Dundee from a modest trading port into a densely populated industrial city, drawing workers from rural Scotland and Ireland. Engels documented Dundee as part of his survey of the textile industries, noting both its early adoption of [[factory-system|factory]]-based flax production and the occupational diseases associated with the trade.

## Engels's Account

### Early Adoption of Flax Machinery

Engels notes Dundee as an early site of mechanised flax spinning, alongside [[leeds|Leeds]] and Belfast:

> "Universal application in Leeds, Dundee, and Belfast." [P04-014]

This passage, from Engels's survey of industrial development, places Dundee among the pioneering cities of the flax industry — a Scottish counterpart to the great linen centres of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] and Ulster. The [[machinery|mechanisation]] of flax spinning began around 1787, when John Kendrew and Thomas Porthouse of Darlington patented a flax-spinning machine; by the 1840s, Dundee's mills processed imported flax and hemp on an industrial scale.

### Occupational Disease

The health consequences of flax-spinning work were severe, and Engels documents them alongside other [[disease|industrial diseases]]:

> "In the flax-spinning mills of Dundee and Dunfermline, and in the cotton mills of Manchester..." [006-020]

Engels groups flax-spinning with other textile processes as a source of chronic [[disease|occupational illness]], including respiratory diseases from flax dust ("pouce"), spinal deformities from prolonged standing, and varicose veins from the humid, heated atmosphere of the spinning rooms.
