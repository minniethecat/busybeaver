# Peter Gaskell

## Life

Peter Gaskell was a British surgeon and social commentator active in the early 1830s. Little is recorded of his personal life, but his professional training as a medical practitioner gave him direct observation of the physical deterioration of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[proletariat|working class]] during the early phase of industrialisation. In 1833 he published *[[manufacturing-population-of-england|The Manufacturing Population of England]]: Its Moral, Social and Physical Condition, and the Changes Which Have Arisen from the Use of Steam [[machinery|Machinery]]; with an Examination of Infant Labour*, a work that Engels describes as "depicting chiefly the state of the working-class in [[lancashire|Lancashire]]." (013-002)

Engels characterises Gaskell as a Liberal who "wrote at a time when it was not a feature of Liberalism to chant the happiness of the workers" and who is "therefore unprejudiced, and can afford to have eyes for the evils of the present state of things, and especially for the [[factory-system|factory]] system." (013-002) At the same time, Engels notes that Gaskell wrote before the Factories Enquiry Commission and adopted from "untrustworthy sources many assertions afterwards refuted by the Report of the Commission." (013-002)

## Role in Engels's Account

Gaskell is one of Engels's most frequently cited statistical authorities. His data on cellar [[housing|dwellings]], crime rates, medical needs, and secret distilleries provides the empirical backbone for several chapters of *The Condition of the Working Class in England*.

On housing conditions, Engels reports:

> "Gaskell gives the number of persons living in cellars in Manchester proper as 20,000. The Weekly Dispatch gives the number, 'according to official reports,' as twelve percent of the working-class, which agrees with Gaskell's number; the workers being estimated at 175,000, 21,000 would form twelve percent of it." (002-056)

On the inadequacy of medical provision, Engels cites Gaskell to illustrate the scale of unmet need:

> "But what is that in a city in which, according to Gaskell's calculation, three-fourths of the population need medical aid every year?" (005-010)

Gaskell also documented the hidden economy of illicit alcohol production in [[manchester|Manchester]]:

> "Gaskell estimates these secret distilleries in Manchester alone at more than a hundred, and their product at 156,000 gallons at the least." (005-037)

Perhaps Gaskell's most striking contribution is his comparative crime statistics. Engels presents Gaskell's data on the ratio of property offences to population across European nations:

> "The proportion of offences against property to the population, which in the Netherlands is as 1 : 7,140, and in France, as 1 : 1,804, was in England, when Gaskell wrote, as 1 : 799." (005-042)

This devastating comparison — England with nearly nine times the property crime rate of the Netherlands — serves Engels's argument that [[capital|industrial capital]]ism produces crime as a direct consequence of immiseration.

## Context

Engels acknowledges a significant debt to Gaskell's work: "The history of the development of the proletariat contained in the introduction to the present work, is chiefly taken from this work of Gaskell's." (013-002) Yet he also identifies a critical limitation: Gaskell, like [[james-kay-shuttleworth|Dr. Kay]], "confuses the whole working-class with the mill hands." (013-002)

By 1833, Gaskell had reached a pessimistic conclusion about the prospects for peaceful resolution of class conflict. Engels quotes him approvingly:

> "As early as 1833, Gaskell declared that he despaired of a peaceful issue, and that a revolution can hardly fail to follow." (011-020)

This places Gaskell among the earliest observers — alongside [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] — to recognise the revolutionary potential inherent in the degradation of the English [[proletariat]].

Gaskell's work belongs to the tradition of middle-class social investigation that flourished in the 1830s and early 1840s, alongside the reports of [[james-kay-shuttleworth|James Kay-Shuttleworth]], [[archibald-alison|Archibald Alison]], and the parliamentary commissions. Unlike the later Liberal apologists such as [[andrew-ure|Dr. Ure]], Gaskell's pre-commission timing allowed him a measure of candour about working-class suffering that Engels valued.

## Legacy

Gaskell's *Manufacturing Population* did not achieve the lasting influence of Engels's own work, but its detailed statistical documentation of cellar dwelling, crime rates, and public health made it an important primary source for the social history of early industrial England. Engels's extensive reliance on Gaskell's data — even while criticising his analytical limitations — ensured that Gaskell's findings reached a far wider audience through *The Condition of the Working Class in England* than they ever would have through the original publication alone.
