## Overview

Leicester is a major [[urban|manufacturing town]] in the East Midlands of England and the historic centre of the hosiery (framework-knitting) industry. By the 1840s, it was one of the principal industrial centres outside [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]]. Engels includes Leicester in his survey of working-class conditions, citing its substandard housing, child labour in hosiery workshops, and the severe distress caused by the framework-knitters' declining wages.

## Engels's Account

### Housing Conditions

Engels groups Leicester with [[derby|Derby]] and [[sheffield|Sheffield]] as [[urban|factory town]]s where housing was uniformly poor:

> "In Leicester, Derby, and Sheffield, it is no better." [002-024]

This judgement follows his description of [[nottingham|Nottingham]], where between 7,000 and 8,000 of 11,000 houses "are built back to back with a rear parti-wall so that no through ventilation is possible" [002-024] — implying similar conditions prevailed in Leicester.

### The Hosiery Industry

Leicester's dominant trade was framework-knitting, a domestic industry organised through middlemen who supplied yarn and collected finished goods. Engels draws on the [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] to describe the workers:

> "The framework-knitters of Nottingham, Derby, and Leicester. Touching these workers, the report of the Children's Employment Commission asserts that their condition is in the highest degree deplorable." [007-002]

A Leicester witness in the Commission's evidence testified to the desperation of parents sending children into the trade:

> "A witness in Leicester said that he would rather let his child beg than send it into a factory." [006-012]

This testimony reveals both the severity of [[factory-system|factory]] conditions and the limited options available to working-class families.

### Child Labour and Moral Conditions

Engels reports that child labour in Leicester's hosiery trade was associated with moral corruption:

> "In the town of Leicester, too, it is very common. There are in Leicester many framework-knitters' shops in which the operatives, male and female, young girls and boys, and married women, work together in such a way that the moral consequences are most pernicious." [006-057]

### Labour Unrest

Leicester also experienced Chartist-related legal prosecutions during the industrial conflicts of the period:

> "four in Leicester" [009-015]

This refers to arrests during the widespread strikes and disturbances, indicating that Leicester's workers participated in the national movement despite being distant from the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] epicentres.

### Mortality

A case from 1843 illustrates the brutal treatment of the sick poor:

> "November, 1843, a man died at Leicester, who had been dismissed two days before from the workhouse, and whose body was covered with vermin" [011-015]
