# Agricultural Proletariat

## Definition

The agricultural proletariat is the class of rural [[wages|wage-labour]]ers — farm hands, day-labourers, and the families dependent on their wages — who work the land but own none of it. Engels argues that this class was created by the same process that produced the industrial proletariat: the enclosure of common fields, the consolidation of small farms into large ones worked by hired labour, and the introduction of threshing-[[machinery|machines]] and other agricultural machinery that made the independent smallholder economically unviable. The [[farming|agricultural labour]]er, once a yeoman with a stake in the land, became a propertyless wage-worker, distinguished from the [[factory-system|factory]] hand only by the rural setting of his exploitation. (P04-007)

## Engels's Analysis

> The same machine gave rise to the agricultural proletariat. There had, hitherto, been a large number of small landed proprietors, who tilled their own land in quiet contentment. The industrial revolution displaced them, and forced them to sell their land to the great farmers, whose land was tilled by hired labourers. (P04-007)

The formation of the agricultural proletariat paralleled the formation of the industrial [[proletariat|working class]]. Before the industrial revolution, England possessed a substantial class of yeomen — small freeholders who owned the land they cultivated and produced for their own [[diet|subsistence]] and local markets. The enclosure movement, which accelerated through the eighteenth century, extinguished common rights and concentrated land in the hands of large proprietors. The agricultural revolution that accompanied enclosure — new rotations, larger fields, and eventually machinery — made the small farm economically obsolete. The yeoman became first a tenant, then a labourer for wages, and finally — if the wages failed — a [[pauperism|pauper]] or a migrant to the [[urban|manufacturing town]]s. (P04-007, 010-002)

> For a time after the rise of the agricultural proletariat, the patriarchal relation between farmer and hand was maintained. The labourer lived in the farm-house, ate at the farmer's table, and was regarded as a member of the family. This relation has wholly disappeared. The farmer now regards his labourers as hands, as chattels, who are to be got as cheaply as possible and worked as hard as possible. (010-002)

Engels describes a decisive transition in the social relations of [[farming|agricultural labour]]. The old "patriarchal" arrangement — in which the [[farming|farm labour]]er lived with the farmer's family, shared their table, and was embedded in a network of mutual obligation — was dissolved by the same competitive logic that governed the [[factory-system|factory]]. The farmer, pressed by falling grain prices after the Napoleonic Wars and rising rents charged by the landlord, could no longer afford the paternalism of an earlier era. The labourer became a commodity, hired when needed and dismissed when not, paid the lowest wage the market would bear, and housed in the cheapest cottage that could be thrown up on the farmer's land. (010-002)

> The consequences which a numerous agricultural proletariat in connection with large farming brings with it — pauperism, dependence on the parish, the moral and intellectual degradation of the agricultural population — all these consequences are visible in the agricultural districts of England. (010-008)

The [[farming|agricultural labour]]er of the 1840s, as Engels documents, was among the most wretched of all English workers. His wages, supplemented by [[parish|parish relief]] under the old [[poor-law|Poor Law]] and by inadequate outdoor relief under the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834, could not support a family. His cottage — often a single room — was worse even than the cellar [[housing|dwellings]] of [[manchester|Manchester]], since the rural labourer had no access to the few amenities the town offered. His children, put to work as bird-scarers and stone-pickers from the age of six or seven, grew up illiterate and stunted. And unlike the [[factory-system|factory]] worker, who had the numerical concentration to organise and resist, the [[farming|agricultural labour]]er was scattered across the [[rural|countryside]], isolated in his parish, and dependent on the farmer for employment, housing, and relief. This isolation made the agricultural proletariat, in Engels's view, the section of the [[proletariat|working class]] most thoroughly crushed by the system — and the one least capable of resisting its degradation. (010-008)

## The Irish Dimension

Engels notes that the agricultural proletariat was swelled by Irish immigration, particularly at harvest time, when bands of Irish labourers crossed the Channel to take work at wages lower than English labourers could subsist on. This competition further depressed the condition of the English agricultural worker, driving wages down and forcing families onto [[parish|parish relief]]. The Irish [[farming|agricultural labour]]er, Engels observes, lived at a standard below even the English agricultural poor — sleeping in barns or ditches, subsisting on potatoes alone — and this imported poverty set the floor for rural wages across England. The Irish influx thus served the farmer as a permanent mechanism for disciplining the English labourer and keeping wages at starvation level. (010-008)

## Historical Context

The condition of the [[farming|agricultural labour]]er had been a subject of parliamentary and philanthropic concern since the 1790s. The Speenhamland system (1795) of wage supplementation from parish rates was an early attempt to address rural poverty but had the perverse effect of subsidising low wages and encouraging farmers to pay less. The [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 abolished outdoor relief and forced the able-bodied poor into the workhouse, intensifying the misery Engels describes. The "revolt of the field" — the [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers' union movement led by Joseph Arch in the 1870s — lay three decades in the future when Engels wrote.
