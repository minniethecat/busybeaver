# Anti-Corn Law League

## Overview

The Anti-Corn Law League was founded in 1838 in [[Manchester]] by Richard Cobden, [[john-bright|John Bright]], and other [[manchester|Manchester]] manufacturers. Its stated objective was the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] — the tariffs on imported grain that kept bread prices high and protected the interests of the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]. Engels devotes sustained analysis to the League, but his verdict is unambiguous: the League was a bourgeois organisation that pursued the interests of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] class while invoking the hunger of the [[proletariat|working class]] as a moral pretext. (008-028, 008-031)

Engels argues that the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were an obstacle to the industrial bourgeoisie's project of reducing wages. Cheap bread, in the League's calculus, would allow manufacturers to lower wages while maintaining the workers' [[diet|subsistence]], thus making British goods more competitive internationally. The [[proletariat|working class]], Engels insists, was not deceived. They understood that the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] served bourgeois interests and responded with indifference or hostility to the League's agitation. (008-033)

## Engels's Account

### Origins and Strategy

Engels traces the League's origins to the strategic calculation of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] bourgeoisie after the passage of the [[Reform Bill of 1832]]. With political power secured, the industrialists turned to "more practical projects, more profitable for itself, namely the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]." (008-028) The Anti-Corn Law Association was formed in [[manchester|Manchester]], the centre of the [[cotton-industry|cotton trade]], and the immediate consequence was "a relaxation of the tie between the Radical bourgeoisie and the proletariat." (008-028) The [[proletariat|working class]] had supported bourgeois reform in 1832 and been betrayed; they would not make the same mistake twice.

The Association soon transformed into the Anti-Corn Law League, which Engels says "assumed a decidedly revolutionary tone." (008-029) The League organised mass meetings, distributed millions of pamphlets, and deployed the full resources of [[manchester|Manchester]] capital — all in a campaign against the landed interest. But this "revolutionary" posture was directed only against the aristocracy, never against the capitalist system itself. The League's real objective, Engels argues, was to use the economic crisis and the distress of the [[proletariat|working class]] as a lever to force the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] through Parliament. (008-029)

In 1842, the League's strategy was exposed during the wave of strikes and [[factory-system|factory]] closures known as the [[plug-plot-riots|Plug Plot]] riots. Engels recounts how the manufacturers sought to instrumentalise the unrest, hoping that the workers would direct their anger against the aristocracy rather than against their employers. The workers refused to be used — at a mass meeting in [[ashton|Ashton]] and [[hyde|Hyde]], "the question discussed was not, as the bourgeoisie had hoped, the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]], but 'a fair day's wages for a fair day's work.'" (008-029)

### The Split with Chartism

The second point of dissension between [[chartism|Chartism]] and the bourgeoisie, and "the main one, which brought [[chartism|Chartism]] to light in its purity, was the repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]." (008-031) The bourgeois Radicals made the [[corn-laws|Corn Law]] question — which for the English is the [[free-trade|Free Trade]] question — "their main business" and "promised the people salvation and a paradise upon earth" from repeal. (008-032)

> The workingmen will not hear of Free Trade and the repeal of the Corn Laws (a fact incomprehensible to the whole English bourgeoisie), and while at least wholly indifferent to the Corn Law question, are most deeply embittered against its advocates. (008-033)

The workers were not convinced. Engels reports their position with evident satisfaction: the working class saw through the League's rhetoric and recognised that cheap bread was a means to cheap wages. The bourgeoisie's incomprehension at this refusal was, for Engels, proof of their blindness to their own class interests. (008-033)

> The Radical bourgeoisie made the Corn Law question, which for the Englishman is the Free Trade question, their main business, and promised the people salvation and a paradise upon earth from the repeal. (008-032)

### Bourgeois Hypocrisy

The League also engaged in what Engels describes as "the most despicable falsehoods and tricks to win the support of the workers." (011-005)

> The manufacturers poured hundreds and thousands of pounds into the treasury of the Anti-Corn Law League, while everyone knows that they are only sending the butter after the cheese, that they calculate upon earning it all back in the first ten years after the repeal of the Corn Laws. (011-005)

Engels does, however, acknowledge one unintentional service rendered by the League: by pushing [[competition|free competition]] to its extreme conclusion, it hastened the point at which "all further development within the present order comes to an end, and the only possible step farther is a radical transformation of the social order." (010-007)

> The repeal of the Corn Laws was the victory of the manufacturing capitalist not only over the landed aristocracy, but over those sections of capitalists, too, whose interests were more or less bound up with the landed interest. (P01-010)

## Context

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were enacted in 1815 to protect British [[farming|agriculture]] after the Napoleonic Wars, prohibiting the importation of wheat until the domestic price reached 80 shillings per quarter. They benefited the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] at the expense of the industrial population. The League's campaign combined economic argument (free trade would lower the cost of living) with political strategy (the aristocracy was the common enemy of manufacturers and workers). The League was exceptionally well-funded and well-organised, with its headquarters in [[Manchester]] and branches across the industrial districts. Richard Cobden and [[john-bright|John Bright]] were its most prominent parliamentary advocates. (008-028)

## Aftermath

The [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] were repealed in 1846 under the Conservative Prime Minister Sir [[robert-peel|Robert Peel]], a decision that split the Tory Party. The repeal marked the definitive political triumph of the industrial bourgeoisie over the landed interest. Engels, writing in the preface to the English edition, acknowledges this. The League's methods — mass organisation, press campaigns, and parliamentary lobbying — became the model for subsequent bourgeois political movements. For the [[proletariat|working class]], repeal brought cheaper bread but also confirmed Engels's thesis: bourgeois free trade served bourgeois interests. (P01-010)
