# Housing

## Definition

Housing, in Engels's analysis, is not merely a question of shelter but a direct measure of working-class immiseration and a visible manifestation of class structure. The spatial organisation of the industrial city — with the bourgeoisie living on the main thoroughfares and the workers hidden away in courts and cellars behind them — is a physical expression of the class relation. The worker's dwelling is both a product of and a contributor to his degradation. (002-037)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels's descriptions of working-class housing in [[manchester|Manchester]] are among the most powerful and detailed passages of the book. He documents the cellar dwellings where entire families lived in single rooms below ground level, damp and unventilated, subject to flooding from overflowing cesspools. He describes the courts — narrow alleyways lined with back-to-back cottages, accessible only through covered passages, invisible from the main streets. He catalogues the [[crowding|overcrowding]]: multiple families sharing a single room, lodgers sleeping on straw in corners, children sharing beds with adults. (002-037, 002-044)

The spatial arrangement of [[manchester|Manchester]], Engels argues, is deliberately designed to shield the bourgeoisie from the sight of working-class misery. The commercial streets that the bourgeois travels form a cordon around the working-class districts. A gentleman may go from his office to his club to his suburban villa without ever entering a workers' quarter. This physical separation enables the moral separation: the bourgeoisie can profess ignorance of conditions that are, literally, around the corner. (002-037)

Engels notes that by 1885, the worst abuses he described in 1844 had been addressed — "Little Ireland" had been demolished, drainage had been introduced, wide avenues had been cut through the worst slums. But he insists that this was not an improvement in working-class conditions but merely a relocation of misery. The destruction of one slum created [[crowding|overcrowding]] in another; the new avenues benefited the bourgeoisie who commuted along them, not the workers who had been displaced to build them. "The bourgeoisie have made further progress in the art of hiding the distress of the working-class." (P01-005)

## Cellar Dwellings and Courts

Engels assembled quantitative evidence alongside his vivid descriptions. A sanitary inspection of Manchester recorded 6,951 houses examined — of which 6,565 urgently needed whitewashing, 960 were out of repair, 939 had insufficient drains, 1,435 were damp, 452 were badly ventilated, and 2,221 were without privies. Of the 687 streets inspected, 248 were unpaved and 352 contained standing pools and heaps of refuse. (002-054)

> There were inspected, in all, 6,951 houses — naturally in Manchester proper alone, Salford and the other suburbs being excluded. Of these, 6,565 urgently needed whitewashing within; 960 were out of repair; 939 had insufficient drains; 1,435 were damp; 452 were badly ventilated; 2,221 were without privies. Of the 687 streets inspected, 248 were unpaved, 53 but partially paved, 112 ill-ventilated, 352 containing standing pools, heaps of debris, refuse, etc. (002-054)

The cellar dwelling was the lowest rung of working-class housing. In Manchester proper, approximately 20,000 people — twelve percent of the working population — lived in cellars below ground level. In Liverpool the proportion was even higher: a fifth of the population, more than 45,000 people, lived in cellar dwellings, of which there were 7,862 in the city. These cellars were typically dark, damp, and unventilated, receiving light only through the door. Engels cites Gaskell and the *Weekly Dispatch* to substantiate these figures, pressing the point that this was not speculation but documented fact. (002-023, 002-056)

> "Liverpool, with all its commerce, wealth, and grandeur yet treats its workers with the same barbarity. A full fifth of the population, more than 45,000 human beings, live in narrow, dark, damp, badly-ventilated cellar dwellings, of which there are 7,862 in the city." (002-023)

Climbing above ground did not guarantee better conditions. The courts of Manchester, Birmingham, and Leeds were narrow, muddy, ill-drained, and lined with back-to-back houses ventilated from one side only. In the background of every court there was usually an ash heap, and privies — when they existed — were often without doors, so filthy that inhabitants passed through pools of stagnant urine and excrement to enter or leave the court. (002-024, 002-047)

> "These courts are usually narrow, muddy, badly ventilated, ill-drained, and lined with eight to twenty houses, which, by reason of having their rear walls in common, can usually be ventilated from one side only. In the background, within the court, there is usually an ash heap or something of the kind, the filth of which cannot be described." (002-024)

### Overcrowding

The density of occupation was extreme. Engels reports that in one London parish, 1,400 houses contained 2,795 families — about 12,000 people — on a space less than 400 yards square. It was common to find a man, his wife, four or five children, and both grandparents sharing a single room where they ate, slept, and worked. In Leeds the standard cottage occupied no more than five square yards and consisted of a cellar, a living room, and one sleeping-room. Brothers and sisters and lodgers of both sexes shared the parents' sleeping-room. (002-010, 002-011, 002-028)

> In this overcrowding it is nothing unusual to find a man and his wife, his mother and sisters, grown-up sons and daughters, and perhaps a grandchild or two, all living in one room. (002-011)

This overcrowding was not merely a matter of discomfort. It was, as Engels repeatedly emphasises, one of the most powerful agencies in producing epidemic disease. Typhus, cholera, and [[tuberculosis|consumption]] were endemic in the overcrowded districts. The housing conditions — the damp walls, the absence of ventilation, the accumulation of filth and refuse, the pigs kept in houses — combined with the exhaustion of factory labour to produce what Engels called a physically degenerate race. (002-022, 002-055, 002-072)

> Such is the Old Town of Manchester, and on rereading my description, I am forced to admit that instead of being exaggerated, it is far from black enough to convey a true impression of the filth, ruin, and uninhabitableness, the defiance of all considerations of cleanliness, ventilation, and health which characterise the construction of this single district, containing at least twenty to thirty thousand inhabitants. (002-044)

The housing market itself was a mechanism of exploitation. Engels documents the rents extorted for the worst dwellings: two cellar rooms at three shillings weekly, a garret at the same price, so that the starving occupants of a single street paid the house-owners a yearly tribute of two thousand pounds. The construction of new working-class cottages was governed solely by the speculating builder's calculation of profit. A carpenter and builder would jointly lease a series of sites and cover them with dwellings thrown up with the utmost disregard for everything except immediate advantage. The cottages looked neat from the street — their massive brick walls deceived the eye — but behind them lay back alleys, unventilated rooms, and the absence of every sanitary provision. (002-010, 002-049)

> In a word, we must confess that in the workingmen's dwellings of Manchester, no cleanliness, no convenience, and consequently no comfortable family life is possible; that in such dwellings only a physically degenerate race, robbed of all humanity, degraded, reduced morally and physically to bestiality, could feel comfortable and at home. (002-053)

The workingman, returning exhausted from the factory, found a comfortless, filthy, cheerless dwelling. Engels links the housing question directly to the question of [[drunkenness]]: liquor was almost the worker's only source of pleasure, an urgent stimulant needed to make the prospect of the next day's suffering less dreadful. The wretched dwelling was thus not only a consequence of exploitation but a cause of further degradation. (005-009)
