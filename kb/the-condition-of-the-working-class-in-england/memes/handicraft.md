## Definition

Handicraft, in Engels's analysis, designates the pre-industrial mode of production in which skilled artisans worked with hand tools in small workshops, typically under a master-and-[[apprentice|apprentice system]]. The concept is central to his account of class formation: the destruction of handicraft by [[factory-system|machinery]] and the consequent proletarianisation of artisans marks the historical emergence of a permanent, self-conscious [[proletariat|working class]].

## Engels's Analysis

### The Destruction of Handicraft by Machinery

Engels situates the decline of handicraft at the heart of the [[industrial-revolution]]. The introduction of machinery transformed not only the technical process of production but the entire social structure:

> Though the rising manufacture first attained importance by transforming tools into machines, workrooms into factories, and consequently, the toiling lower middle-class into the toiling proletariat... this happened outside of the domain of manufacture proper, in the province of handicraft and retail trade as well. In the place of the former masters and apprentices, came great capitalists and workingmen who had no prospect of rising above their class. [P04-023]

The key transformation is the elimination of social mobility. Under the handicraft system, the apprentice could hope to become a master; under the [[factory-system]], "he who was born to toil had no other prospect than that of remaining a toiler all his life" [P04-023]. Engels identifies this foreclosure of aspiration as the condition that made the proletariat "for the first time, an integral, permanent class" capable of "an independent movement" [P04-023].

### Handicraftsmen as a Transitional Class

In Engels's taxonomy of the proletariat, handicraftsmen occupy an intermediate and diminishing position. He orders his investigation by proximity to manufacture: [[factory-system|factory]] hands first (most advanced in class consciousness), then miners, then [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers. Handicraftsmen appear as a category defined by their relationship to the industrial transformation:

> We shall find the same order again among the industrial workers, and shall see how the factory hands, eldest children of the industrial revolution, have from the beginning to the present day formed the nucleus of the Labour Movement, and how the others have joined this movement just in proportion as their handicraft has been invaded by the progress of machinery. [001-001]

### Centralisation and the Absorption of Handicraft

The centralising tendency of manufacture — what Engels calls the concentration of "population... just as capital does" — absorbs handicraft settlements into industrial towns. A [[factory-system|factory]] village attracts "handicraftsmen, shoemakers, tailors, bakers, carpenters, stonemasons" who provide ser[[prostitution|vice]]s for the growing workforce, leading to a spiral of urbanisation:

> So the village grows into a small town, and the small town into a large one. The greater the town, the greater its advantages. [001-004]

This process does not preserve handicraft independence but subsumes it. The small master is gradually crushed by the [[factory-system|factory]] system's economies of scale.

### The Birmingham Metal Trades: Persistence of Handicraft

[[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s metal industry provides Engels with a case study of handicraft's partial survival within [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. The city retains "something of the old handicraft character," with "small employers... who work with their apprentices in the shop at home" [007-009]. Léon Faucher characterised this as "Démocratie industrielle."

However, Engels is sceptical. The small master is trapped between capital and labour: "For one who grows rich ten are ruined, and a hundred placed at a greater disadvantage than ever, by the pressure of the one upstart who can afford to sell more cheaply than they" [007-009]. The apprentices are "quite as badly off under the small employers as under the manufacturers" [007-009].

This intermediate position explains, for Engels, [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s political ambiguity — "a politically radical, but not a Chartist, town" — since the small master is "neither genuine proletarian... nor genuine bourgeois" [007-009].

### Handicraftsmen in Crisis

During the [[crisis|commercial crisis]] of 1842, even handicraftsmen — "smiths, [[brick|bricklayers]], and others, who usually have full employment in the most depressed periods" — suffered "greatly from [[poverty|want]] of work and reduction of wages" [003-013]. This demonstrates that by the 1840s, even the remnants of the handicraft system were fully integrated into, and vulnerable to, the cyclical crises of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism.

### Handicraftsmen and the Labour Movement

By Engels's time, "the constant contact with the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives, the pressure of the great capitalists... the influences of life in towns, and the fall of wages, have made nearly all the handicraftsmen active participators in labour movements" [007-018]. The distinction between [[factory-system|factory]] hand and handicraftsman was eroding as both were proletarianised.

## Historical Context

The decline of handicraft was a central political-economic question of the 1830s–1840s. The Handloom Weavers' Commission (1838–1841) documented the immiseration of formerly independent artisans. Engels's account draws on this official literature while integrating it into a historical-materialist narrative of class formation that would later be systematised in *[[capital|Capital]]*.

## Related Concepts

Handicraft is linked to [[industrial-revolution]] (the transformative event), [[factory-system]] (the system that superseded it), [[domestic-industry]] (the transitional form), [[division-of-labour]] (applied with "strict" rigour even before [[machinery|mechanisation]]), [[cottage-system]] (the rural counterpart), and [[competition]] (the mechanism of the small master's ruin).
