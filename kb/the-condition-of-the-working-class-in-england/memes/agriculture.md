## Definition

Agriculture, in Engels's usage, refers to the cultivation of land and rearing of livestock as a sector of the English economy undergoing transformation under [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. Engels does not treat [[farming|agriculture]] as a timeless category but as a specific historical form: the enclosure-driven, landlord-dominated, increasingly capitalist mode of rural production that displaced smallholders and created a landless agricultural proletariat. The fate of [[farming|agriculture]] is central to Engels's narrative because the industrial [[proletariat|working class]] was largely recruited from the rural population displaced by agricultural "improvement."

## Engels's Analysis

### Enclosure and the End of the Yeomanry

The transformation of English [[farming|agriculture]] began long before the [[factory-system|factory]] system but accelerated with it. Engels traces how "[[proletariat|industrial workers]] withdrew from [[farming|agriculture]], a great number of small holdings were consolidated" (P04-007), describing the enclosure movement that drove peasants off the land. This was not a natural evolution but a deliberate process: "[[farming|agriculture]] was affected in still another way by the introduction of new owners and cultivators" (P04-017). The old yeoman class — independent small farmers who owned their land — was systematically eliminated by parliamentary enclosures that concentrated land in the hands of large proprietors.

### The Agricultural Proletariat

The displaced smallholders became the first generation of the industrial proletariat. Engels's analysis is integrated: "in the first place, manufacture influenced [[farming|agriculture]], and in the fourth, the condition of the [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers" (001-001). The structure of his book itself reflects this — the agricultural proletariat is treated as the first section of the [[proletariat|working class]], preceding [[factory-system|factory]] workers, because Engels understands rural dispossession as the historical precondition of urban exploitation. Those who remained on the land became [[wages|wage-labour]]ers on terms barely above starvation.

### The Corn Laws and Artificial Scarcity

Engels analyses the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]] — tariffs on imported grain — as a class instrument of the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]. The laws "had given English [[farming|agriculture]] an artificial prosperity" (010-001) by keeping grain prices high, benefiting landlords and large tenant farmers at the expense of both [[manufacturer|industrial capitalist]]s (who paid higher wages) and workers (who paid higher [[diet|food]] prices). But this artificial protection also created stagnation: "the longer [[farming|agriculture]] had remained stationary, the more inevitable became the reaction" (010-002). When the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] eventually triumphed in 1846, it represented the victory of [[capital|industrial capital]] over landed capital — but Engels insists that workers gained nothing from the change, as wages were simply reduced to match the lower [[diet|food]] prices.

### Agriculture and Industrial Capitalism

Engels sees [[farming|agriculture]] and industry as increasingly integrated under capitalism. The ideal, in his ironic formulation, would be "persons who shall carry on both [[farming|agriculture]] and manufacture and enjoy equal advantages" (008-035) — but this vision is presented as a bourgeois fantasy contradicted by the actual concentration of both land and capital. Smallholders who attempted to combine farming with domestic industry — the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] — were crushed between falling piece-rates and rising rents. Engels notes that [[agricultural-proletariat|agricultural workers]] "held fast to [[farming|agriculture]], and remained upon the lowest level of civilisation" (010-011), not from choice but because they had no alternative. The "stationary" condition of [[farming|agriculture]] (010-002) was not a sign of health but of arrested development under landlord monopoly.

### The Cost of Agricultural "Improvement"

Engels notes the environmental and social costs of intensified [[farming|agriculture]]: "as [[farming|agriculture]] improved and forests were cut down, fuel became gradually more expensive" (P04-015). The clearing of forests for farmland and the enclosure of commons deprived the rural poor of traditional rights — foraging, grazing, wood-gathering — that had supplemented their [[diet|subsistence]]. The "improvement" of [[farming|agriculture]], in Engels's telling, meant the impoverishment of the agricultural population.
