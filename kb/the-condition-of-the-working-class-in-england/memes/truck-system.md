# Truck System

## Definition

The truck system was a method of wage [[wages|payment]] common in early industrial England, especially in mining and [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts, whereby workers received their wages not in cash but in goods or in credits redeemable only at the employer's company store (the "truck shop"). Because the employer controlled both the wage and the price of goods, the truck system functioned as a secondary deduction from the worker's real income. Engels treats it as one of the central mechanisms by which the bourgeoisie extracted surplus from the proletariat outside the formal wage contract. (007-001)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels documents the truck system across multiple branches of industry, from the [[coal|coal mines]] of Northumberland to the textile mills of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]]. The system took various forms: in some cases, workers were paid entirely in goods; in others, they received part of their wages in cash and the remainder in credits at the truck shop. The common element was the employer's ability to set prices above market rates, effectively reducing real wages by 10 to 25 per cent without altering the nominal wage figure. The worker who was paid 12 shillings a week but forced to buy his flour, tea, and bacon at the truck shop at inflated prices was, in reality, earning 9 or 10 shillings. (007-001, 007-005)

The truck system was particularly entrenched in the mining districts, where colliery owners operated company stores in isolated villages where no alternative shops existed. The miner had no choice but to accept truck payment because there was literally nowhere else to spend his wages. Engels observes that this geographic monopoly made the truck system among the most oppressive of all forms of [[exploitation|wage exploitation]] — the miner was trapped not only by his economic dependence on the employer but by physical isolation. (009-010)

The Truck Act of 1831 nominally prohibited the practice, but Engels notes that evasion was widespread. Employers simply rebranded the truck shop as an independent establishment, staffed by a relative or former employee of the mill-owner, and continued the practice under a different name. The worker who refused to patronise the "independent" shop found that his employment was mysteriously terminated. Engels treats the persistence of the truck system after its legal prohibition as evidence that capital will always find ways to circumvent regulations that threaten its profits. (007-005)
