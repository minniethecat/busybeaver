# Strike

## Definition

A strike (or "turn-out," in the terminology of Engels's time) is the collective cessation of work by a body of workers, intended to compel the employer to concede to demands regarding wages, hours, or conditions. Engels treats the strike not merely as an economic tactic but as the elementary school of class consciousness — the moment when workers who had previously competed against each other for employment first learn to act collectively against their common employer. (008-001)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels describes strikes as the necessary consequence of the competitive system. When trade is good, manufacturers compete for labour, and wages rise. When trade slackens, competition among workers for scarce employment drives wages down. The worker who accepts a wage cut to keep his job under[[mining|mines]] his fellow workers; the only defence against this downward spiral is collective action. The strike, by temporarily removing labour from the market, forces the employer to treat with the workers as a body rather than as isolated individuals. This is why, Engels argues, the bourgeoisie hates strikes above all else — a strike is the practical refutation of the doctrine that capital and labour bargain as equal parties. (008-001, 008-010)

The 1844 strike of the Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]] coal-miners provides Engels with a case study in the dynamics of industrial conflict. The mine-owners, faced with falling coal prices, imposed a wage reduction. The miners responded with a general strike that shut down the entire coalfield for months. The owners retaliated with evictions — the miners lived in company-owned cottages — and with the importation of strike-breakers from Ireland and Wales. Engels uses this strike to illustrate the savage inequality of the contest: the miner, with no savings and no alternative employment, was starved back to work, while the coal-owner, cushioned by capital, could afford to wait. (008-015)

Yet Engels does not regard the defeated strike as a failure. Even lost strikes advance the worker's education: they teach the necessity of organisation, the importance of strike funds, and the identity of interest among workers in different trades and districts. The strike that fails today lays the groundwork for the union that will succeed tomorrow. Each defeated strike pushes the proletariat toward the recognition that economic struggle alone is insufficient and that political organisation — ultimately, [[chartism|Chartism]] and socialism — is necessary. (008-010)
