# Coal

## Definition

Coal, in Engels's analysis of the condition of England in 1844, is the mineral foundation of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. It fuelled the [[machinery|steam-engine]]s that drove the factories, fired the blast furnaces that smelted iron, heated the [[housing|dwellings]] of the [[urban|manufacturing town]]s, and powered the [[railway|railways]] and steamships that carried manufactured goods to foreign markets. Engels treats coal not merely as a commodity but as the energy substrate of the entire industrial system — the material precondition without which "the industrial revolution" could not have occurred in the form it took. (P04-015, P04-016)

## Engels's Analysis

### Coal as the Basis of the Industrial Revolution

Engels argues that coal gave England its decisive industrial advantage. The [[machinery|steam-engine]], which made mechanised [[factory-system|factory production]] possible on a large scale, required fuel; Britain's abundant coalfields supplied it at a cost no competitor could match. Engels writes:

> The steam-engine first gave importance to the broad coalfields of England; the production of machinery began now for the first time, and with it arose a new interest in the iron mines which supplied raw material for it. [P04-015]

This causal chain — coal enabling steam, steam enabling machinery, machinery enabling factories — is fundamental to Engels's account of how the industrial revolution unfolded. Coal was not an auxiliary resource but the originating condition of [[machinery|mechanisation]] itself. By 1844, the consumption of coal in a single city was staggering: [[sheffield|Sheffield]] alone consumed 515,000 tons of coal in 1836. The smelting of pig iron consumed more than three million tons of coal annually, and the [[mining|mines]] of Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]] yielded over five million tons for shipping, employing between forty and fifty thousand men. (P04-016)

### The Expansion of Coal Mining

The scale of coal extraction expanded dramatically over Engels's lifetime. In Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]], two of the richest coalfields in Britain, the number of working [[mining|mines]] grew from 14 in 1753 to 40 in 1800, 76 in 1836, and 130 in 1843. Engels presents these figures to demonstrate the accelerating pace of extraction:

> All the English and Scotch deposits are now worked, and the mines of Northumberland and Durham alone yield annually more than 5,000,000 tons for shipping, and employ from 40 to 50,000 men. [P04-016]

The coalfields extended across "almost the whole North and West of England, central Scotland, and certain districts of Ireland," producing what Engels calls "a superabundance of coal." Coal mining had, by the 1840s, become one of the largest single employments in the country, and the [[mining|colliers]] one of the most significant sections of the industrial proletariat. (009-001)

### The Coal Mining Proletariat

Engels situates the coal miners as a distinct section of the proletariat, created by the industrial revolution's demand for fuel:

> The production of raw materials and of fuel for manufacture attained importance only in consequence of the industrial change, and engendered a new proletariat, the coal and metal miners. [001-001]

He devotes Chapter 9 of the book to the [[mining|mining proletariat]], documenting their conditions in harrowing detail drawn from the [[childrens-employment-commission-report|Children's Employment Commission]] reports. The coal and iron [[mining|mines]] are treated together because they were "worked in pretty much the same way." Children of four, five, and seven years were employed in the pits, set to transporting loosened coal or iron ore, or to watching the ventilation doors. The door-watchers — the smallest children — spent twelve hours daily alone in the dark, sitting in damp passages "without even having work enough to save them from the stupefying, brutalising tedium of doing nothing." (009-004, 009-005, 001-001)

### The Labour of Transporting Coal

The work of transporting coal underground was brutally hard. Engels describes children and young people hauling large, wheelless tubs over uneven mine floors, through water, and up steep inclines through passages so low that workers were "forced to creep on hands and knees." One boy or man would push and another would pull, in heat and darkness, for eleven or twelve hours — and often longer, with double shifts of twenty-four or even thirty-six hours at a stretch. "Set times for meals," Engels notes, "are almost unknown." (009-005)

The physical consequences were devastating:

> The children and young people who are employed in transporting coal and iron-stone all complain of being overtired. Even in the most recklessly conducted industrial establishments there is no such universal and exaggerated overwork. [009-007]

Engels reports children falling asleep on the stone hearth as soon as they reached home, unable to eat; children found asleep on the road by their parents late at night; and the universal practice of spending Sunday in bed "to recover in some degree from the overexertion of the week." Church and school were sparsely attended, and teachers complained that those who did attend were too sleepy to learn. (009-007)

### Occupational Disease and Deformity

The toll on the miner's body was systematic. Stunted growth, retarded puberty, and characteristic deformities marked the collier:

> Distortions of the legs, knees bent inwards and feet bent outwards, deformities of the spinal column and other malformations, appear the more readily in constitutions thus weakened, in consequence of the almost universally constrained position during work; and they are so frequent that in Yorkshire and Lancashire, as in Northumberland and Durham, the assertion is made by many witnesses, not only by physicians, that a miner may be recognised by his shape among a hundred other persons. [009-007]

Women employed in the [[mining|mines]] "seem to suffer especially from this work, and are seldom, if ever, as straight as other women," with testimony of pelvic deformities leading to "difficult, even fatal, childbearing." Beyond skeletal deformities, miners suffered from diseases of the digestive organs, heart disease (hypertrophy, inflammation of the heart and pericardium), hernias, asthma, and rheumatism — nearly all of which Engels traces to overwork and the damp, dust-filled atmosphere of the pits. (009-007)

The signature disease of the coal miner was "black spittle":

> The peculiar disease of workers of this sort is "black spittle," which arises from the saturation of the whole lung with coal particles, and manifests itself in general debility, headache, oppression of the chest, and thick, black mucous expectoration. [009-007]

This condition, untreatable in severe cases, was a death sentence: "Every case of this disease ends fatally." Yet Engels notes that Dr. Mackellar of East Lothian testified that "in all the coal mines which are properly ventilated this disease is unknown, while it frequently happens that miners who go from well to ill-ventilated [[mining|mines]] are seized by it." The disease was thus not a natural hazard but a product of the mine owners' refusal to install ventilators — a direct consequence of what Engels calls the "profit-greed" of capital. (009-007)

The cumulative result of these afflictions was premature ageing and early death:

> A coal miner who can follow his calling after the 45th or 50th year is a very great rarity indeed. It is universally recognised that such workers enter upon old age at forty. [009-007]

The loaders, whose work required constant lifting of heavy coal blocks into tubs, fared even worse: "the loaders are old before they are young," as the proverb in the districts had it. Summarising the evidence of [[southwood-smith|Dr. Southwood Smith]], Engels concludes that "through prolonged childhood on the one hand and premature age on the other, that period of life in which the human being is in full possession of his powers, the period of manhood, is greatly shortened, while the length of life in general is below the average." (009-007)

### The Thin-Seam Mines

Conditions were still worse in [[mining|mines]] where thin seams of coal were worked. Mine owners declined to remove the surrounding sand and clay because it would make extraction too expensive; the result was passages so low that "to stand upright in them is not to be thought of." Workers lay on their sides to cut the coal, resting on an elbow as a pivot — a posture that produced severe inflammation of the joint. Women and children who transported the coal crawled on hands and knees, harnessed to the tub by a chain that frequently passed between their legs:

> It can be readily imagined how greatly the diseases already peculiar to the miners are fostered by this especially frightful, slavish toil. [009-008]

### Dangers, Accidents, and the Bourgeoisie's Indifference

The coal mine, Engels writes, was a uniquely lethal workplace:

> In the whole British Empire there is no occupation in which a man may meet his end in so many diverse ways as in this one. The coal mine is the scene of a multitude of the most terrifying calamities, and these come directly from the selfishness of the bourgeoisie. [009-009]

Explosions of hydrocarbon gas, accumulations of carbonic acid gas, roof collapses, and broken ropes on the winding gear all took a steady toll. Engels reports that on September 28, 1844, a single explosion at Haswell Colliery in [[durham|Durham]] killed 96 men. He cites the [[mining|Mining]] Journal's estimate of some fourteen hundred fatalities annually, and notes that the [[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian alone reported "at least two or three accidents every week for [[lancashire|Lancashire]]." Yet coroner's juries, composed of men dependent on the mine owners or bound by custom, routinely returned verdicts of "Accidental Death." Engels directly indicts the bourgeoisie: the dangers could have been "almost entirely removed" by proper ventilation, but "for this purpose the bourgeoisie has no money to spare." The Davy lamp, offered as a substitute safety measure, was "wholly useless because of its dull light" and was therefore usually abandoned in favour of a candle. (009-009)

### Education, Morals, and Isolation

In the coal districts, Engels found education and morality at "an excessively low plane," worse even than the degraded condition of the [[factory-system|factory]] operatives. The isolation of the mining villages was key: "The workers live in the country in neglected regions, and if they do their weary work, no human being outside the police force troubles himself about them." Few miners could read, still fewer could write. Church attendance was rare; the clergy complained of the miners' "irreligion as beyond comparison." Their knowledge of religion, Engels acidly notes, was restricted to "the terms of their oaths." (009-010)

The moral consequences of mine labour were direct and structural. Overwork itself "must engender drunkenness." The heat of the pits forced miners — men, women, and children — to work "wholly naked, and in most cases, nearly so," with consequences "in the dark, lonely [[mining|mines]]" that Engels leaves readers to imagine. The number of illegitimate children was "disproportionately large," though Engels draws a distinction between the sexual relations of the mining districts — brutal as they were — and the prostitution of the great cities. The labour of women "dissolves the family, and makes the mother totally incapable of household work," just as it did in the factories. (009-010)

### Economic Oppression

Beyond the physical dangers, Engels documents systemic economic exploitation. The truck system — [[truck-system|payment in goods]] rather than cash — was "the rule, not the exception" in the coal districts, "carried on in the most direct and undisguised manner." The [[domestic-industry|cottage system]] was "universal, and here almost a necessity," but was used "for the better plundering of the workers." Mine owners manipulated weights and measures; wages were reckoned by measure while coal was sold by weight; tubs not perfectly full earned no pay at all, while excess measure went uncompensated. Fines were imposed at the overseer's arbitrary discretion:

> The fine system in general is so highly perfected in the coal mines, that a poor devil who has worked the whole week and comes for his wages, sometimes learns from the overseer, who fine at discretion and without summoning the workers, that he not only has no wages but must pay so-and-so much in fines extra! [009-012]

The concentrations of power were extreme. Nearly all the Justices of the Peace in the coal districts were themselves mine owners or their relatives and friends, and possessed "almost unlimited power in these poor, uncivilised regions where there are few newspapers, these few in the ser[[prostitution|vice]] of the ruling class, and but little other agitation." Engels concludes: "It is almost beyond conception how these poor coal miners have been plundered and tyrannised over by Justices of the Peace acting as judges in their own cause." (009-012)

### The Great Strike of 1844

The accumulated oppression of the coal districts produced, after long quiescence, a spectacular act of resistance. Through contact with the more politically conscious [[factory-system|factory]] operatives and the efforts of Chartist organisers, the miners of Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]] began to organise:

> Such a movement seized the workers of Northumberland and Durham that they placed themselves at the forefront of a general Union of coal miners throughout the kingdom, and appointed W. P. Roberts, a Chartist solicitor, of Bristol, their "Attorney General." [009-013]

The union grew rapidly: 60,000 members were represented at the first conference in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1844, and six months later, at the second conference in [[glasgow|Glasgow]], the figure had risen to 100,000. The miners drew up a new agreement with demands that included [[wages|payment]] by weight, an end to the fines system, and recognition of the union. The mine owners refused even to negotiate, declaring that "for them the Union did not exist" and that they dealt only with individual workmen. (009-013, 009-015)

On March 31, 1844, 40,000 miners in Northumberland and [[durham|Durham]] laid down their picks. The strike lasted nearly five months, sustained by union funds that provided each family with a weekly contribution of 2s. 6d. Roberts, their attorney, conducted a simultaneous legal campaign, travelling across England to challenge the despotic powers of the Justices of the Peace, obtaining acquittal after acquittal for miners who had been imprisoned on flimsy charges. (009-015)

When the strike held firm after four months, the mine owners deployed their ultimate weapon: eviction. The cottages belonged to them, and in July they served notice on the entire strikebreaking workforce:

> In July, notice to quit was served the workers, and, in a week, the whole forty thousand were put out of doors. This measure was carried out with revolting cruelty. The sick, the feeble, old men and little children, even women in childbirth, were mercilessly turned from their beds and cast into the roadside ditches. [009-016]

Even this the miners endured. They set up their household goods on the moors and harvested fields, held out for eight more weeks under the open sky, through the wet autumn, with no shelter but the calico curtains of their beds. When the mine owners imported strikebreaking workers from Ireland and Wales, the union's position finally collapsed. The miners were forced to renounce the union, discharge Roberts, and accept the owners' terms. (009-016)

Engels's assessment of the strike is unambiguous in its admiration:

> Thus ended at the close of September the great five months' battle of the coal miners against the mine owners, a battle fought on the part of the oppressed with an endurance, courage, intelligence, and coolness which demands the highest admiration. What a degree of true human culture, of enthusiasm and strength of character, such a battle implies on the part of men who, as we have seen in the Children's Employment Commission's Report, were described as late as 1840, as being thoroughly brutal and wanting in moral sense! [009-016]

### The Aftermath and Significance

The strike was a defeat in the immediate sense, but Engels argues it was far from futile. It had "torn the miners of the North of England forever from the intellectual death in which they had hitherto lain," made at least two-thirds of them Chartists, and forced public attention onto their condition. Thomas [[thomas-duncombe|Duncombe]], the only Chartist member of the House of Commons, raised the miners' petition in Parliament during a debate on the export duty on coal, compelling the bourgeois press to report their case. After the Haswell explosion, Roberts persuaded Prime Minister Peel to commission Professors Lyell and Faraday to investigate. (009-017)

The strike's influence spread: "Scarcely had it become known that the coal miners of the North had been forced to renounce the Union and discharge Roberts, when the miners of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] formed a Union of some ten thousand men, and guaranteed their Attorney General a salary of 1,200 pounds a year." (009-018)

Engels draws from the miners' struggle a general political conclusion:

> Thus the workingmen are constantly coming to see more clearly that, united, they too are a respectable power, and can, in the last extremity, defy even the might of the bourgeoisie. And this insight, the gain of all labour movements, has been won for all the miners of England by the Union and the strike of 1844. [009-018]

### Coal in the Industrial Landscape

Coal determined not only the location of [[mining|mines]] but the atmosphere of the industrial towns. Engels's descriptions of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts are saturated with coal imagery. [[bradford|Bradford]] was "enveloped in a grey cloud of coal smoke" on weekdays. The towns around [[manchester|Manchester]] were "reeking of coal smoke" and "especially dingy" from the red brick "turned black with time." The [[irk|Irk]] in [[manchester|Manchester]] was a "coal-black, foul-smelling stream," as was the Medlock — "coal black, stagnant and foul." Coal was simultaneously the source of wealth and the visible sign of the degradation that wealth produced. (002-029, 002-031, 002-039, 002-050)

### Coal and England's Industrial Monopoly

In his 1885 postscript, Engels returned to coal as a factor in the shifting balance of global industrial power. The [[free-trade|Free Trade]] system of 1847 had assumed England would be the world's sole [[manufacture|manufacturing]] centre. But:

> The conditions of modern industry, steam-power and machinery, can be established wherever there is fuel, especially coals. And other countries beside England — France, Belgium, Germany, America, even Russia — have coals. [P01-010]

The diffusion of coal deposits across the industrialising world meant that England's [[manufacture|manufacturing]] monopoly was "irretrievably broken up." Coal, the foundation of England's industrial supremacy, had become the means by which its competitors would overtake it. Engels saw coal as the material basis of a coming crisis: "America, with its inexhaustible resources, with its unmeasured coal and iron fields... has in less than ten years created a manufacture which already competes with England." (P01-010, 011-021)
