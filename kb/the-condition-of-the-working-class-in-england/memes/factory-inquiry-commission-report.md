# Factories' Inquiry Commission Report (1833)

## Overview

The Factories' Inquiry Commission was appointed by Parliament in 1833 to investigate conditions in textile mills and recommend legislative reforms. Its report — a massive multi-volume document combining testimony from workers, overseers, [[factory-system|factory]] surgeons, and manufacturers — provided the most comprehensive account of [[factory-system|factory]] conditions yet assembled. The Commission's findings, particularly the evidence of systematic overwork of children as young as six or seven, forced Parliament to pass the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833, which established the first (though grossly inadequate) [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate. (006-010)

## Key Findings

The Commission documented [[working-day|working hours]] that routinely exceeded fourteen per day for children under thirteen, with some manufacturers operating "relay systems" that allowed adult machinery to run sixteen or eighteen hours while children were technically employed for shorter shifts. It recorded widespread physical deformities caused by prolonged standing — knock-knees, curvature of the spine, flattened feet — and a range of injuries from unguarded machinery: crushed fingers, scalped heads, amputated limbs. It documented the practice of "strapping" — beating children with leather straps to keep them awake during the final hours of a shift — as routine rather than exceptional. (006-011)

The Commission's medical witnesses provided some of the most damaging evidence. Factory surgeons testified that the children they examined were, on average, shorter, lighter, and more susceptible to disease than children not employed in factories. One surgeon reported that he could identify a [[factory-system|factory]] child by sight: the pallor, the stooped posture, the prematurely aged face. The Commission's report reproduced this testimony verbatim, and Engels quotes it at length. (006-012)

## Engels's Use

Engels treats the 1833 Commission Report as the single most important empirical source on [[factory-system|factory]] conditions, citing it throughout Chapter 6 ("Single Branches of Industry") and Chapter 7 ("The Remaining Branches"). His method is forensic: he quotes the Commission's findings, compares them with the subsequent [[factory-act|Factory Act]]'s provisions, and demonstrates that the Act's protections fell far short of what the Commission's own evidence demanded. (006-001)

The Commission's testimony also serves a rhetorical function. Engels lets the manufacturers' own statements damn them. When Commissioner Cowell reports that [[factory-system|factory]] girls "were unchaste" — that [[prostitution|sexual exploitation]] was endemic among the children and young women who worked under male overseers — Engels reproduces the finding without embellishment, trusting the reader to feel the appropriate horror. When a manufacturer testifies that he opposes the ten-hour day because it would "ruin the trade," Engels notes that this is the same manufacturer who insists his workers are well-treated and contented. (006-012)

Engels is attentive to the Commission's political context. The 1833 inquiry was not a disinterested academic exercise but a response to the massive public agitation led by [[richard-oastler|Richard Oastler]], [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]], and the Ten Hours' movement. [[manufacturer|Manufacturer]]s initially refused to cooperate with the Commission, then flooded it with self-serving testimony when they realised the report would shape legislation regardless. Engels notes this dynamic — the Commission's value, paradoxically, was that it forced the bourgeoisie to testify under oath about conditions they preferred to keep hidden. (006-010)

## Historical Significance

The 1833 Report set a precedent that Engels's own book followed: the documented testimony of working people, corroborated by medical and statistical evidence, was politically explosive. The Report showed that parliamentary investigation could expose what the market concealed — and that legislative reform, however inadequate, was possible when public outrage was mobilised. Engels's *Condition of the Working Class in England* was, in this sense, a continuation of the Commission's method by other means: documentary exposure of conditions that the ruling class preferred to ignore. (006-010)
