# Pauperism

## Definition

Pauperism, for Engels, is the condition of the worker who can no longer sell his labour — the final stage of proletarianisation. Unlike the employed worker, who is exploited but survives, the pauper is surplus to capital's requirements: too old, too sick, too broken by labour to work, and therefore dependent on the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] for [[diet|subsistence]]. Engels treats [[poverty|pauperism]] not as an anomaly of the capitalist system but as its necessary by-product. Every industrial cycle created a fresh wave of paupers — workers discarded when they were no longer profitable. (010-002)

## Engels's Analysis

> Pauperism in the country districts has increased more rapidly than population. (011-009)

Engels documented [[poverty|pauperism]] most extensively in the [[rural|agricultural districts]] (Chapter 10), where the introduction of machinery and the consolidation of small farms into large estates had created a class of landless labourers who could find work only during harvest. For the rest of the year, they depended on [[parish|parish relief]] — which the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] of 1834 had made as punitive as possible. The workhouse was [[poverty|pauperism]]'s institutional form: a prison for the poor, designed to make relief so degrading that only the truly desperate would seek it. (010-002)

The statistical evidence Engels marshals is damning. In the agricultural counties, he reports that paupers constituted up to twelve per cent of the total population — a figure that included not only the unemployed but the underemployed, whose wages were so low that they required [[parish|parish relief]] to survive even while working. The "labouring poor" were, in effect, subsidised by the parish: the farmer paid wages below [[diet|subsistence]], and the ratepayer made up the difference through the [[poor-law|Poor Law]]. Engels treats this as a direct subsidy from the public to the employer — a transfer of income from taxpayers to farmers that allowed agricultural wages to remain below [[diet|subsistence]]. (010-009)

In the industrial districts, [[poverty|pauperism]] took a different form. The [[factory-system|factory]] system's cycles of boom and bust threw workers into [[poverty|destitution]] during every downturn. Engels describes the [[manchester|Manchester]] cotton workers who, when trade was slack, "were to be seen in thousands on the streets, begging or picking rags." These were not the chronically poor but workers temporarily pauperised by an economic system over which they had no control. The difference between the industrial pauper and the agricultural pauper was that the former expected to work again when trade revived — an expectation that kept them in the towns rather than returning to the [[rural|countryside]]. (P01-010)

The role of [[Irish Immigration]] in swelling the pauper class was, in Engels's analysis, both quantitative and qualitative. The Irish arrived in England with nothing — "their [[diet|food]] consists of potatoes and potatoes only; whatever they earn beyond these needs they spend upon [[drunkenness|drink]]" — and formed the lowest stratum of the proletariat, the stratum from which paupers were most readily recruited. Engels estimates that more than a million Irish had immigrated, concentrated in the [[urban|great town]]s where the competition they exerted on wages drove English workers toward the pauper threshold. The Irish themselves, accustomed to "the minimum of the necessities of life," constituted a ready-made pauper population — people who, in [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s phrase, "lodged to their mind in any pig-hutch or dog-hutch" and who could survive on wages that would starve an English worker. (004-001, 004-002)

## Political Significance

Pauperism was a political problem as well as an economic one. The existence of a permanent class of paupers — people who could not work, or could not work enough — was a standing refutation of the bourgeois claim that the free market benefited everyone. Engels uses [[poverty|pauperism]] statistics to make a political point: the system that produced unprecedented wealth also produced unprecedented [[poverty|destitution]]. The two were not in contradiction but in causal relation: the wealth of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s manufacturers was built on the labour of workers who, when they could no longer work, became [[manchester|Manchester]]'s paupers. (011-009)
