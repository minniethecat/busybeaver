## Overview

Lancashire is a historic county in northwestern England that served as the epicentre of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]'s cotton textile industry. By the 1840s, it was the most intensively industrialised region in the world, centred on [[manchester|Manchester]], which Engels describes as "the classic type" of the modern [[urban|manufacturing town]]. Engels crossed into South Lancashire from the [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] border across Blackstone Edge, entering what he called "that classic soil on which English manufacture has achieved its masterwork and from which all labour movements emanate" (002-030). The county's [[urban|factory town]]s, canal networks, and rapidly urbanising population formed the empirical foundation for Engels's study of the condition of the English [[proletariat|working class]].

## Engels's Account

Engels identifies Lancashire — specifically South Lancashire — as the heartland of the industrial system whose effects on the [[proletariat|working class]] he documents throughout his book. He describes the region as "the most densely populated strip of country in England," noting that land which "a hundred years ago chiefly swamp land, thinly populated, is now sown with towns and villages" (002-030). In Lancashire, Engels asserts, English manufacture "finds at once its starting point and its centre," and the [[manchester|Manchester]] Exchange serves as "the thermometer for all the fluctuations of trade" (002-030).

Engels observes that "in the cotton industry of South Lancashire, the application of the forces of Nature, the superseding of hand labour by machinery (especially by the power-loom and the self-acting mule), and the division of labour, are seen at the highest point" (002-030). The cottage-building patterns Engels documents were found "all over Manchester and throughout Lancashire and Yorkshire, often mixed up together" (002-048).

> "The South-Scotch character of the architecture of Boness, the seaport of Linlithgow, and the environs of the cotton factories of South Lancashire, to which we shall come, present the same state of things." (002-030)

He characterises South Lancashire with [[manchester|Manchester]] as "the headquarters of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] district" (002-030), noting that the entire region stretching across Lancashire and neighbouring [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] formed an interconnected industrial landscape where [[urban|factory town]]s merged into one another.

Speaking of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts in which the workers' movement took shape, Engels emphasises the centrality of Lancashire:

> "Lancashire, and especially Manchester, is the seat of the most powerful Unions, the central point of Trades' organisation, the place from which the general labour movement of the country has again and again taken its origin." (008-040)

He notes that "the factory operatives, and especially those of the cotton district, form the nucleus of the labour movement" and that Lancashire was "the central point of Chartism, the place which numbers most Socialists" (008-040).

## Industrial Geography

The textile industry defined Lancashire's economic geography. Engels traces the origin of the [[factory-system|factory]] system to this county, where [[richard-arkwright|Richard Arkwright]]'s water-frame spinning machine was first deployed:

> "The first mill was erected in 1785 by Messrs. Livesey, Hargreaves, Hall, and Bridge, at Lowris, near Blackburn, in North Lancashire. This machine was the rough beginning of the so-called power-loom." (P04-006)

The spinning jenny, the machine that Engels identifies as the "first invention which gave rise to a radical change in the state of the English workers," was invented in 1764 by James Hargreaves "of Standhill, near Blackburn, in North Lancashire" (P04-006). Arkwright's spinning throstle followed shortly afterwards, emerging from his work as "a barber, in Preston, in Lancashire" (P04-008).

The county's river valleys and canal networks provided early transport infrastructure. The first English canal of the modern era was "built in Lancashire from Sankey Brook to St. Helen's" in 1755, followed by the Duke of Bridgewater's canal from [[manchester|Manchester]] to the Mersey in 1759 (P04-019).

By the 1840s, Lancashire was covered by a dense network of [[urban|factory town]]s: [[manchester|Manchester]], [[oldham|Oldham]], [[bolton|Bolton]], [[preston|Preston]], [[blackburn|Blackburn]], [[rochdale|Rochdale]], [[bury|Bury]], [[st-helens|St. Helens]], [[warrington|Warrington]], [[wigan|Wigan]], and [[leigh|Leigh]] among them.

### Textile and Metal Trades

Beyond cotton spinning and weaving, Lancashire hosted diverse industrial activity. Engels describes "a great number of operatives employed in the cotton-printing establishments of Lancashire, Derbyshire, and the West of Scotland," noting that mechanical ingenuity had produced "brilliant results" in this branch, though at devastating human cost: "one man, with the assistance of one child, now does with a machine the work done formerly by 200 block printers" (007-006).

The county was also a centre of metal-working. Engels records "needle factories in Warrington, Lancashire, where great want, immorality, and ignorance prevail among the workers, and especially among the children; and a number of nail forges in the neighbourhood of Wigan, in Lancashire" (007-014). He further notes that the production of machinery by machinery — "machinery for planing and boring, cutting screws, wheels, nuts, and bolts" — was concentrated "especially in Lancashire," where workers were "deprived of their last refuge, the creation of the very enemy which supersedes them" (007-014).

## Social Impact

Engels documents the devastating social consequences of Lancashire's rapid industrialisation. According to the medical commissioners he cites, the physical deterioration of [[factory-system|factory]] operatives was severe:

> "Dr. Hawkins, Commissioner for Lancashire, expresses his opinion as follows: 'I am of opinion that, at present, the operatives of Lancashire would soon be a race of pygmies.'" (006-029)

Engels corroborates this with testimony from a recruiting officer who "testified that operatives are little adapted for military service, looked thin and nervous, and were frequently rejected by the surgeons" (006-029). He adds that "one of the largest manufacturers of Manchester, leader of the opposition against the workingmen," had himself warned "that if things went on as at present, the operatives of Lancashire would soon be a race of pygmies" (006-029).

Accident rates in Lancashire's [[coal|coal mines]] were also staggering:

> "In nearly all mining districts of Lancashire, there occur three accidents every week." (009-009)

The bourgeoisie of Lancashire, Engels argues, led the national campaign against [[factory-act|factory regulation]]:

> "The manufacturers, especially those of Lancashire, take the lead of the bourgeoisie in the movement against the Factory Acts." (006-001)

The social dislocation Engels observed extended to the complete disruption of domestic life. He recounts the story of a workingman who travelled to [[st-helens|St. Helens]], Lancashire, and visited an old friend, only to find him "in a miserable, damp cellar, scarcely furnished," sitting by the fire mending his wife's stockings "with the bodkin" while she laboured in the [[factory-system|factory]] from half-past five until eight at night (006-009). Engels uses this vignette to illustrate the inversion of traditional family roles under industrial [[capital|capitalism]], observing that such conditions "unsexes the man and takes from the woman all womanliness" (006-009).

## Labour Movement

Despite its status as the heartland of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, Lancashire was also the birthplace of organised working-class resistance. The county hosted powerful trade unions, Chartist organisations, and miners' associations. In 1844, Lancashire miners formed a Union of "some ten thousand members" and guaranteed their attorney a salary of 1,200 pounds a year (009-018). Strikes occurred with such frequency that "in 1842, 4,497 were made in Lancashire alone, or more than 14 percent of the whole number of operatives" (005-042).

The intensity of class conflict in Lancashire occasionally erupted into violence. Engels records that in 1831, "during a violent labour movement, young Ashton, a manufacturer in Hyde, near Manchester, was shot one evening when crossing a field" and that "incendiarisms and attempts to blow up" were resorted to by desperate workers (008-015).
