## Overview

Carlisle was the county town of Cumberland, situated near the Scottish border in north-west England. In the 1840s it was a growing [[manufacture|manufacturing]] centre with cotton mills, calico printing works, and railway workshops. Engels cites Carlisle in his statistical analysis of [[mortality|working-class mortality]], using the town's [[mortality|death rate]]s to illustrate the differential impact of industrialisation on life expectancy between social classes.

## Engels's Account

### Mortality Statistics

Engels includes Carlisle in the mortality data he presents to demonstrate the health consequences of industrial working-class life:

> "Town of Carlisle, 1779–1787, before introduction of factories..." [005-018]

The passage appears in Engels's chapter on the "Results" of industrialisation, where he marshals statistical evidence from parliamentary reports and local surveys to prove that working-class life expectancy was dramatically lower than that of the upper classes. He contrasts the mortality rates of industrial towns with [[rural|agricultural districts]], arguing that the [[factory-system|factory]] system itself — not merely urban living — was responsible for the excess deaths.

Carlisle serves Engels's argument as a "before" data point: mortality rates recorded in the late eighteenth century, before the town's cotton mills were established, provided a baseline against which the health impact of subsequent industrialisation could be measured. This comparative method — using historical data from the same location to control for other variables — was characteristic of Engels's rigorously empirical approach.
