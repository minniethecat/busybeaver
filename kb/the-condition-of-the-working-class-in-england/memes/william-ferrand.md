## Overview

William Ferrand was a British Tory Member of Parliament and a member of the "Young England" group. Engels singles him out as one of the few MPs who actively defended working-class interests, describing him as a "vigorous opponent" of repressive anti-labour legislation (011-007).

## Opposition to the Masters and Servants Bill

In the parliamentary session of 1844, an obscure MP named Mr. Miles proposed a bill regulating the relation of master and servant that initially seemed unobjectionable. However, during the committee stage, despotic provisions were inserted, including one empowering employers to have workers imprisoned for two months with hard labour for refusal to work — on the employer's oath alone, without independent evidence (011-007).

The bill aroused "the workingmen to the utmost fury." Engels reports that working-class petitions flooded into Thomas Duncombe, "the representative of the interests of the proletariat" in Parliament. Ferrand stood alongside Duncombe:

> This man was, except Ferrand, the representative of "Young England," the only vigorous opponent of the bill...
>
> (011-007)

The bill was ultimately defeated when other Radicals belatedly joined the opposition.

## Young England

Ferrand was a member of the "Young England" group, a faction of philanthropic Tories who sought a romantic restoration of pre-industrial social relations. Engels lists its parliamentary members as:

> D'Israeli, Borthwick, Ferrand, Lord John Manners, etc. Lord Ashley, too, is in sympathy with them.
>
> (013-011)

Engels regarded Young England's goal of restoring "Merry England" with its "brilliant features and romantic feudalism" as "unattainable and ridiculous, a satire upon all historic development." Nevertheless, he acknowledged the group's "good intention" and "courage to resist the existing state of things and prevalent prejudices, and to recognise the vileness of our present condition" (013-011).
