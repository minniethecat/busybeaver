## Overview

George Gordon Byron, Lord Byron (1788–1824), was an English Romantic poet. Engels observes that Byron's works — like those of Shelley — were read primarily by English workingmen rather than by the bourgeoisie, who restricted themselves to expurgated editions (008-039).

## Readership Among the Working Class

Engels describes Byron's appeal to the proletariat in terms of his "glowing sensuality" and "bitter satire upon our existing society":

> Shelley, the genius, the prophet, Shelley, and Byron, with his glowing sensuality and his bitter satire upon our existing society, find most of their readers in the proletariat; the bourgeoisie owns only castrated editions, family editions, cut down in accordance with the hypocritical morality of today.
>
> (008-039)

The passage highlights a class division in literary culture: the bourgeoisie, constrained by its own social prejudices and "hypocritical morality," could not engage with Byron's radical content in its authentic form. The proletariat, by contrast, had "open eyes" for genuinely progressive literature (008-039).

## Social and Political Significance

Engels uses Byron's working-class readership as evidence of the intellectual independence and sophistication of the English proletariat. Despite their material deprivation — Engels mentions workingmen "whose fustian jackets scarcely held together" — the proletariat actively engaged with advanced literature, science, and philosophy (008-039).

The relationship between Byron's poetry and the working class also exemplifies a broader pattern Engels identifies: the most intellectually advanced literature of the age found its audience outside the bourgeoisie. The French materialists, Strauss, Proudhon, Bentham, and Godwin were all, according to Engels, read predominantly by the proletariat rather than by the middle class (008-039).
