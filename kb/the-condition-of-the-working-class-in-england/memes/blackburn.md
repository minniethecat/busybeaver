## Overview

Blackburn was a cotton-[[urban|manufacturing town]] in North [[lancashire|Lancashire]], situated in the heart of the textile district that Engels described as the epicentre of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. While Engels does not provide a detailed description of Blackburn's housing or working conditions in the same way he does for [[manchester|Manchester]] or [[bolton|Bolton]], the town appears in his account of the technological developments that transformed textile production — notably as the home region of James Hargreaves, inventor of the spinning jenny.

## Engels's Account

### The Spinning Jenny

Engels locates the origins of one of the key inventions of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] in the [[rural|countryside]] near Blackburn:

> "James Hargreaves, of Standhill, near Blackburn, in North Lancashire. This machine, the spinning jenny, was the first important improvement upon the old spinning-wheel." [P04-006]

Hargreaves invented the spinning jenny around 1764, a hand-powered multiple-spindle machine that allowed one worker to spin eight or more threads simultaneously. The invention dramatically increased yarn production, though it initially provoked hostility from hand-spinners who saw their livelihood threatened — a pattern of technological displacement that Engels traces throughout his account of industrialisation.

The location of Hargreaves's invention in the Blackburn area was not coincidental. North [[lancashire|Lancashire]] was already a centre of textile production, and the pressure to increase output in response to growing demand (driven by Kay's flying shuttle of 1733) created the conditions for mechanical innovation.

### Context in Lancashire's Textile District

Blackburn lay within the broader [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] district that Engels describes as the most intensively industrialised region in the world. The town's mills, like those of neighbouring [[preston|Preston]], [[bolton|Bolton]], and [[oldham|Oldham]], employed thousands of workers in spinning and weaving, and experienced the same cycles of boom and bust, the same degradation of living conditions, and the same patterns of working-class militancy that Engels documents throughout his book.
