## Overview

The Report of the Select Committee on Factory Children's Labour — known as the [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] Committee Report after its chairman, the Tory MP [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]] — was published in 1832 after an intensive parliamentary investigation into the conditions of child labour in British textile factories. The committee heard testimony from workers, overseers, and medical practitioners, compiling a record of physical abuse, excessive [[working-day|working hours]], and systematic exploitation that shocked public opinion and provided the documentary foundation for the [[factory-system|factory]] reform movement.

The report is a pivotal document in Engels's account of [[factory-act|factory legislation]]. He treats it neither as a neutral fact-finding exercise nor as a wholly reliable source, but as a politically significant intervention whose very biases are historically revealing.

## Engels's Assessment

Engels offers a characteristically dialectical judgement on the [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] Committee and its report. He identifies the committee as a product of the "philanthropic section of the Tory party," acting from motives that mixed genuine moral concern with political calculation — an alliance of paternalist Tories with working-class agitation against the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest represented by the Whigs:

> "The philanthropic section of the Tory party, then led by Michael Sadler, seized upon the plan, and brought it before Parliament. Sadler obtained a parliamentary committee for the investigation of the factory system, and this committee reported in 1832." [006-045]

But Engels immediately qualifies the report's authority:

> "Its report was emphatically partisan, composed by strong enemies of the factory system, for party ends. Sadler permitted himself to be betrayed by his noble enthusiasm into the most distorted and erroneous statements, drew from his witnesses by the very form of his questions, answers which contained the truth, but truth in a perverted form." [006-045]

This paragraph condenses Engels's mature historiographical method: he simultaneously affirms the report's factual basis (the witnesses "contained the truth") and identifies the political mediations that shaped its presentation ("truth in a perverted form"). The truth of working-class suffering exists; the question is who controls its representation.

## Political Consequences

The report's publication had two immediate consequences. First, it galvanised the [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] movement, providing Parliamentary legitimacy to an agitation that manufacturers had dismissed as demagogic. Second, it provoked the manufacturers to demand their own investigation — the [[parliamentary-commission-1833|Factory Commission of 1833]], whose report Engels cites more frequently and considers "somewhat nearer the truth" while noting its opposite partisan deviation: "on every page it betrays sympathy with the manufacturers" [006-045].

The sequence — [[factory-inquiry-commission-report|Sadler Report]] (1832) → manufacturers' counter-demand → Factory Commission (1833) → [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act (1833)]] — is for Engels a model of how bourgeois legislative reform operates: not through the victory of one party's truth claims, but through the contradictory pressure of competing class interests mediated by the state.

## Documentary Significance

The [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] Committee Report holds a distinctive place in the history of social investigation. It was one of the first parliamentary inquiries to take oral testimony directly from working-class witnesses — children, young workers, and adult operatives — and to publish that testimony as part of an official document. This innovation, however imperfect in execution, established a precedent that the later [[factory-system|factory]] and mining commissions would follow. For Engels, the publication of working-class voices in Parliamentary Blue Books is a telling paradox: the bourgeois state, compelled by political pressure, becomes the vehicle for documenting the [[proletariat|working class]]'s own experience of exploitation.
