# Leeds

## Overview

Leeds, situated on the River Aire in the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], was the principal centre of the English woollen cloth industry at the time of Engels's investigation. Its population had grown from 53,000 in 1801 to 123,000 by 1831, and Engels estimates it had increased a further 20 to 25 percent by the mid-1840s. Leeds was one of the four cities Engels names as representative of the condition of all great industrial towns in England — alongside [[london]], [[manchester]], and [[birmingham]]. (002-004, 001-004)

The city lay in what Engels describes as "a charming region, a beautiful green hill country," and he notes that the grey stone houses of the [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] woollen district "look so neat and clean in comparison with the blackened [[brick|brick buildings]] of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]." Yet he immediately qualifies this impression: "on coming into the towns themselves, one finds little to rejoice over." (002-028)

## Engels's Account

### The River Aire and the Working-Class Districts

Engels describes Leeds as lying "on a gentle slope that descends into the valley of the Aire," a river which "flows through the city for about a mile-and-a-half and is exposed to violent floods during thaws or heavy rain." He draws particular attention to the contrast between the higher western quarters, which were "clean, for such a large town," and the low-lying districts along the river and its tributary becks, which were "narrow, dirty, and enough in themselves to shorten the lives of the inhabitants, especially of little children." (002-028)

The worst working-class districts were clustered around Kirkgate, Marsh Lane, Cross Street, and Richmond Road:

> "Added to this, the disgusting state of the workingmen's districts about Kirkgate, Marsh Lane, Cross Street and Richmond Road, which is chiefly attributable to their unpaved, drainless streets, irregular architecture, numerous courts and alleys, and total lack of the most ordinary means of cleanliness, all this taken together is explanation enough of the excessive mortality in these unhappy abodes of filthy misery." (002-028)

### The Pollution of the Aire

Like all the industrial rivers Engels observed, the Aire was transformed by its passage through the city of [[leeds]]:

> "In consequence of the overflows of the Aire (which, it must be added, like all other rivers in the service of manufacture, flows into the city at one end clear and transparent, and flows out at the other end thick, black, and foul, smelling of all possible refuse), the houses and cellars are often so full of water that they have to be pumped out." (002-028)

The flooding of the sewers during the spring floods of 1839 had a measurable impact on mortality. Engels reports that, according to the Registrar of Births and Deaths for the affected part of the town, "there were three deaths to two births, whereas in the same three months, in every other part of the town, there were three births to two deaths." (002-028)

### Housing Conditions

Engels describes the typical Leeds cottage in grim detail:

> "An ordinary cottage in Leeds occupies not more than five yards square of land, and usually consists of a cellar, a living room, and one sleeping-room. These contracted dwellings, filled day and night with human beings, are another point dangerous alike to the morals and the health of the inhabitants." (002-028)

The Report on the Health of the Working-Classes, which Engels cites, found the [[crowding|overcrowding]] produced a moral as well as physical crisis:

> "In Leeds we found brothers and sisters, and lodgers of both sexes, sharing the parents' sleeping-room, whence arise consequences at the contemplation of which human feeling shudders." (002-028)

Beyond the city itself, Engels briefly discusses nearby [[bradford|Bradford]], "which, but seven miles from Leeds at the junction of several valleys, lies upon the banks of a small, coal-black, foul-smelling stream." [[bradford|Bradford]], too, exhibited "the same filth and discomfort as in Leeds," with the older portions of the town "narrow and irregular" and the valley bottom filled with workingmen's cottages "crowded between the tall factories." (002-029)

### Mortality and the Factory System

Engels places Leeds in his mortality tables for [[urban|factory town]]s. Out of every 10,000 deaths in Leeds, 5,286 were children under five years of age — a rate even higher than that of [[manchester]] and other [[urban|factory town]]s. This meant that more than half of all deaths in Leeds were of children who had not yet reached their fifth birthday, a statistic Engels presents as evidence of the systematic destruction of working-class life. (005-018)

## Statistics

- **Population 1801**: 53,000
- **Population 1831**: 123,000
- **Estimated population mid-1840s**: approximately 150,000 (20–25% increase from 1831)
- **Child mortality**: 5,286 out of 10,000 deaths were of children under 5 (005-018)
- **Spring flood mortality (1839)**: 3 deaths to 2 births in the affected district, versus 3 births to 2 deaths elsewhere (002-028)
- **Typical cottage size**: not more than 5 yards square, consisting of cellar + living room + 1 sleeping-room (002-028)
