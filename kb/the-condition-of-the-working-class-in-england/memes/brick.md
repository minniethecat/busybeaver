# Brick

## Definition

Brick appears throughout Engels's text not as a neutral building material but as a material sign of the condition of the [[proletariat|working class]]. Its colour — whether bright red or blackened — indexes the age and degradation of a district. Its thickness — whether a full brick or a half-brick laid on edge — reveals the speculative logic that governed [[housing|working-class housing]] construction. And the brickmaking trade itself, organised into a militant union, supplied Engels with one of his most dramatic illustrations of working-class revolutionary courage. In the industrial towns of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], brick was the universal medium in which the class relation was literally built. (002-031, 002-049)

## Brick as Index of Urban Degradation

Engels repeatedly uses the colour of brickwork as a diagnostic mark. New cottages in [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]] appear "bright red" and comfortable; the older streets, by contrast, show bricks "turned black with time" from coal smoke, their corners crumbling. The contrast between the "neat and clean" grey stone houses of the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] and the "blackened brick buildings of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]" is one of the first observations Engels makes upon entering the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] district. The blackening of brick is not merely aesthetic — it is the visible deposit of the same coal smoke that fills the lungs of the working-class inhabitants. (002-028, 002-031, 002-034)

> The towns themselves are badly and irregularly built with foul courts, lanes, and back alleys, reeking of coal smoke, and especially dingy from the originally bright red brick, turned black with time, which is here the universal building material. (002-031)

## Brick Construction and Speculative Housing

Engels devotes sustained attention to the construction of brick cottages for workingmen, revealing the economic logic that produced structurally unsound [[housing|dwellings]]. The outer walls of workingmen's cottages, he reports, are "one whole brick thick at most," and he had personally observed cottages under construction whose outer walls were "but one-half brick thick, the bricks lying not sidewise but lengthwise, their narrow ends touching." The massive appearance of newly-built brick walls was a deception: the walls were "as thin as it is possible to make them." (002-049)

> All such cottages look neat and substantial at first; their massive brick walls deceive the eye, and, on passing through a newly-built workingmen's street, without remembering the back alleys and the construction of the houses themselves, one is inclined to agree with the assertion of the Liberal manufacturers that the working population is nowhere so well housed as in England. (002-049)

The thinness of brick walls was not a matter of poor workmanship but of structural economic incentives. Contractors leased land for fixed terms — twenty, thirty, or ninety-nine years — after which the land and all improvements reverted to the original owner with no compensation. Builders therefore minimised expenditure: the thinner the wall, the fewer the bricks, the lower the cost. Moreover, since commercial crises periodically emptied whole streets, and since the final occupants of a decaying cottage were typically Irish families who stripped wooden fittings for firewood, Engels calculated that workingmen's cottages lasted only forty years on average. The brick that looked so substantial was, in practice, a temporary and degrading form of shelter. (002-049)

In [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]], Engels observed streets where "the bricks in the house-corners are no longer firm but shift about, in which the walls have cracks and will not hold the chalk whitewash inside." These were houses that, at most fifty years old, were already in a state of advanced decay — their brickwork a material record of the speculator's refusal to build for permanence. (002-034)

## Bricklayers and the Labour Market

Bricklayers appear in Engels's analysis in two distinct registers. In the labour market, they are among the trades pressured by [[irish-immigration|Irish competition]]. Because brickmaking and bricklaying required "more of strength than skill," they were among the "simple, less exact" occupations into which Irish immigrants flooded, exerting downward pressure on wages and degrading the conditions of English workers in the trade. (004-003)

> Such occupations are therefore especially overcrowded with Irishmen: hand-weavers, bricklayers, porters, jobbers, and such workers, count hordes of Irishmen among their number, and the pressure of this race has done much to depress wages and lower the working-class. (004-003)

Yet bricklayers also belonged, by 1885, to the "aristocracy among the working-class." Engels notes in his Preface to the 1885 English edition that the great Trades' Unions — "the engineers, the carpenters, and joiners, the bricklayers, are each of them a power" — had secured for themselves "a relatively comfortable position." More remarkably, the bricklayers and bricklayers' labourers had proven capable of "successfully resist[ing] the introduction of machinery," an achievement that set them apart from textile workers, whose labour was perpetually threatened by [[machinery|mechanisation]]. This dual status — vulnerable to [[irish-immigration|Irish competition]] in the 1840s, a protected labour aristocracy by the 1880s — traces the uneven trajectory of working-class improvement Engels acknowledged in his later reflections. (P01-010)

During the crisis of 1842, even bricklayers — "who usually have full employment in the most depressed periods" — suffered "greatly from [[poverty|want]] of work and reduction of wages." That a trade normally insulated from cyclical downturns should feel the crisis testified to its unprecedented severity. (003-013)

## The Brickmakers' Strike of 1843

The most dramatic episode involving brick in Engels's text is the strike and armed confrontation at Pauling & Henfrey's brickyard in [[manchester|Manchester]] in May 1843. The firm had "increased the size of the bricks without raising wages, and sold the bricks, of course, at a higher price." When the workers struck and the Brickmakers' Union declared war on the firm, the proprietors hired armed guards — "all ex-soldiers and policemen, armed with guns." (008-021)

> When intimidation proved unavailing, the brickyard, which lay scarcely a hundred paces from an infantry barracks, was stormed at ten o'clock one night by a crowd of brickmakers, who advanced in military order, the first ranks armed with guns. (008-021)

The brickmakers forced their way into the yard, fired on the watchmen, stamped out the wet bricks, tore down the dried rows, and demolished furniture in the overlooker's dwelling. The watchmen, positioned behind a hedge, fired on the attackers who were silhouetted against a burning brickkiln. The firing lasted half an hour. Engels reports that one arrested brickmaker had received three bullets — in the thigh, the calf, and the shoulder — and had nonetheless travelled more than four miles on foot before being captured. For Engels, the episode proved that the [[proletariat|working class]] "possess revolutionary courage, and do not shun a rain of bullets" when they had "a specific end in view." (008-021)

The strike over brick dimensions encapsulates the logic of [[exploitation|capitalist exploitation]] Engels documents throughout the book: the employer's attempt to extract more value from labour without increasing wages, the workers' collective resistance through their union, the escalation to armed confrontation, and the intervention of the state — the military barracks a hundred paces away — on behalf of property. The brick itself, enlarged in size to increase the firm's profit, became the material object around which class violence crystallised. (008-021)
