# Irish Immigration Wave

## Overview

The migration of [[irish-immigration|Irish workers]] to the industrial districts of England was one of the largest population movements within the British Isles during the early nineteenth century. Engels estimates that more than one million Irish had immigrated to England by the 1840s, with approximately fifty thousand arriving each year. The majority settled in the great [[manufacture|manufacturing]] cities: 120,000 in [[London]], 40,000 in [[Manchester]], 34,000 in [[Liverpool]], 40,000 in [[Glasgow]], and 24,000 in [[Bristol]]. (004-001)

Engels treats Irish immigration not as an accidental feature of the period but as a structural necessity of English [[capital|industrial capital]]ism. The rapid extension of manufacture required a constant supply of cheap, unskilled labour, and the impoverished population of Ireland provided it. Engels applies the concept of a reserve army of labour: the Irish had nothing to lose at home and much to gain in England, and their presence in the labour market exerted continuous downward pressure on wages and living standards for the English [[proletariat|working class]] as a whole. (P04-023, 004-001)

## Engels's Account

The fourth chapter of Engels's work is devoted entirely to Irish immigration, and its opening sentence frames the phenomenon in explicitly economic terms: "The rapid extension of English industry could not have taken place if England had not possessed in the numerous and impoverished population of Ireland a reserve at command." (004-001) This is the logic of the [[surplus-population|industrial reserve army]]: capitalism requires a pool of labourers who are desperate enough to work for [[diet|subsistence]] wages, and the Irish — dispossessed by centuries of colonial exploitation and dependent on a single crop, the potato — filled this role.

Engels describes the condition of the Irish immigrant in unflinching terms. Quoting Thomas [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]], he paints a picture of men "who migrate for fourpence to England, on the deck of a steamship on which they are often packed like cattle." Their [[diet|food]] "consists of potatoes and potatoes only"; "shoes they know not"; "whatever they earn beyond these needs they spend upon [[drunkenness|drink]]." (004-002) The Irishman, Engels writes, "needs only salt for condiment, he lodges to his mind in any pig-hutch or dog-hutch, roosts in outhouses, and wears a suit of tatters." (004-002)

The consequence of this desperate condition was that the Irish immigrant could underbid the English worker for every form of unskilled labour. [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]], as Engels quotes him, describes how "the uncivilised Irishman, not by his strength, but by the opposite of strength, drives the Saxon native out, takes possession in his room." (004-002) The English worker was forced to compete on terms set by the most destitute: "That the condition of the lower multitude of English labourers approximates more and more to that of the Irish, competing with them in all the markets." (004-002)

Engels identifies multiple channels through which Irish immigration degraded the condition of the English [[proletariat|working class]]. First, economic competition: the Irish worker's lower standard of living enabled him to accept wages that an English worker could not survive on, driving down wages for whole branches of industry. (004-002) Second, cultural importation: Engels argues that the Irish brought with them "the custom of [[overcrowding|crowding]] many persons into a single room," which "has been chiefly implanted by the Irish immigration." (004-002) The Irish pigsty against the house wall, the filth deposited before the door, and the indifference to [[sanitary|sanitation]] were, in Engels's account, habits imported from rural Ireland that became catastrophic in the concentrated environment of the industrial city.

Third, Engels identifies what he calls the passionate, mercurial Irish temperament as "another influence of great moment in forming the character of the English workers." (005-033) The mixture of "hot Irish blood" with the "native northern coldness" of the English produced, in Engels's view, a [[proletariat|working class]] more volatile, more passionate, and more capable of rebellion than would otherwise have emerged.

Engels's account of the Irish is unsparing and often prejudiced, reflecting the racial and cultural hierarchies of his time. He describes the Irish as "almost without civilisation," "accustomed from youth to every sort of privation, rough, intemperate, and improvident," whose "brutal habits" they bring among the English population. (004-001) Yet his economic analysis transcends these limitations: the Irish immigrant is not the cause of English working-class misery but the mechanism through which the structural pressures of capitalism are transmitted. The Irish are victims twice over — first of colonial dispossession at home, then of exploitation in England.

## Context

The roots of Irish mass migration lay in the colonial relationship between Britain and Ireland. The Irish peasantry, dependent on the potato as a [[diet|subsistence]] crop, was extremely vulnerable to crop failure. The famine of 1817 and the partial failures of the 1830s sent waves of migrants across the Irish Sea. Engels wrote before the Great Famine of 1845–1852, which would send hundreds of thousands more to England, but his analysis anticipated its logic: "Then came the commercial [[crisis|crash]] of 1847 and the Irish famine, and with both the prospect of revolution." (P01-010)

The Irish concentrated in the worst-paid branches of industry: [[weaving|hand-weaving]], bricklaying, portering, and unskilled [[factory-system|factory]] labour. (004-003) They inhabited the worst [[housing|dwellings]] — Engels reports that "the majority of the families who live in cellars are almost everywhere of Irish origin" — and formed the lowest stratum of the urban proletariat. (004-002)

## Aftermath

The [[irish-immigration|Irish immigration wave]] permanently altered the composition of the English [[proletariat|working class]]. By the time of the 1851 census, Irish-born residents constituted a significant minority in every major industrial city. The children of Irish immigrants — what Engels calls those "strongly mixed with Irish blood" — became a permanent feature of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] [[proletariat|working class]]. (004-002) The Irish presence in the labour movement was also significant: [[irish-immigration|Irish workers]] were prominent in the Chartist agitation and later in the [[labour-movement|trade union movement]], and the Irish revolutionary tradition contributed to the militancy of the English [[proletariat|working class]] in the later nineteenth century.
