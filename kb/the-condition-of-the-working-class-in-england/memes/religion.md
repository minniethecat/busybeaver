## Definition

Religion, in Engels's analysis of industrial England, functions primarily as an instrument of class domination. The [[bourgeoisie]] deploys sectarian religious instruction to pacify the [[proletariat|working class]], while simultaneously the harsh conditions of industrial labour drive workers toward practical irreligion — a development Engels views as a progressive emancipation from the "inherited stable tenets" that keep the proletariat submissive.

## Engels's Analysis

Engels devotes sustained attention to the role of religion throughout *The Condition of the Working Class in England*. He exa[[mining|mines]] it from two complementary angles: as a failed educational project imposed by the bourgeoisie, and as a sphere in which working-class consciousness has decisively broken from middle-class norms.

### Religion in Working-Class Education

The [[education]] provided to workers is overwhelmingly religious in content, but Engels argues this serves sectarian interests rather than genuine enlightenment. The State Church and dissenting sects operate schools "for the sole purpose of keeping the children of the brethren of the faith within the congregation." The result is a curriculum that prioritises dogma over useful knowledge:

> The consequence is that religion, and precisely the most unprofitable side of religion, polemical discussion, is made the principal subject of instruction, and the memory of the children overburdened with incomprehensible dogmas and theological distinctions; that sectarian hatred and bigotry are awakened as early as possible, and all rational mental and moral training shamefully neglected. [005-020]

The practical outcome of this religious education is, Engels demonstrates through Commissioner Horne's testimony, profound ignorance even on religious subjects. Children who attended Sunday school for five or six years could not identify Jesus Christ, the Apostles, or basic biblical figures [005-020]. Engels presents this as evidence that religious instruction is not merely ideologically objectionable but empirically ineffective.

### Working-Class Irreligion

Perhaps more significant for Engels's argument is the widespread indifference to religion among the [[proletariat|working class]]. He describes this as one of the defining characteristics separating workers from the bourgeoisie:

> The workers speak other dialects, have other thoughts and ideals, other customs and moral principles, a different religion and other politics than those of the bourgeoisie. Thus they are two radically dissimilar nations. [005-034]

Engels attributes working-class irreligion not to philosophical atheism but to the material conditions of proletarian life. The worker's "faulty education saves him from religious prepossessions, he does not understand religious questions, does not trouble himself about them, knows nothing of the fanaticism that holds the bourgeoisie bound; and if he chances to have any religion, he has it only in name, not even in theory" [005-036]. This practical secularism, Engels argues, makes the worker "more unconstrained, freer from inherited stable tenets and cut-and-dried opinions, than the bourgeois who is saturated with the class prejudices poured into him from his earliest youth" [005-036].

### Religion and the Mechanics' Institutes

The bourgeoisie, recognising the danger of independent [[education|working-class education]], attempts to recapture the educational sphere through Mechanics' Institutes. Engels describes how these institutions, originally founded by workers, were co-opted:

> Here all education is tame, flabby, subservient to the ruling politics and religion, so that for the workingman it is merely a constant sermon upon quiet obedience, passivity, and resignation to his fate. [008-038]

### Religion Among the Mining Population

The irreligion Engels observes among [[factory-system|factory]] operatives is even more pronounced in the mining districts. He reports that miners "go to church seldom or never; all the clergy complain of their irreligion as beyond comparison" [009-010]. The "categories of religion are known to them only from the terms of their oaths" — a detail Engels deploys to underline the complete failure of religious institutions to reach this segment of the [[proletariat|working class]] [009-010].

### English Socialism and the Rejection of Religion

Engels identifies English [[socialism|Socialism]] as providing "the most pronounced expression of the prevailing absence of religion among the workingmen, an expression so pronounced indeed that the mass of the workingmen, being unconsciously and merely practically irreligious, often draw back before it" [008-036]. This passage is significant for Engels's dialectical argument: the workers are already de facto irreligious, but the explicit theoretical rejection of religion represented by [[socialism|Socialism]] remains a step too far for many. Engels predicts, however, that "necessity will force the workingmen to abandon the remnants of a belief which, as they will more and more clearly perceive, serves only to make them weak and resigned to their fate, obedient and faithful to the vampire [[property|property-holding class]]" [008-036].

### The Bourgeoisie and Religion

Engels's portrait of bourgeois religiosity is equally critical. He argues that the bourgeoisie instrumentalises religion politically — supporting the Church establishment when it serves class interests, and extending the logic of [[competition|free competition]] to religion itself. In a characteristic passage, he predicts that [[competition|free competition]] "will suffer no limitation, no State supervision; the whole State is but a burden to it" and will soon extend to "religion, too, as the State Church collapses more and more" [011-002]. The [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] controversy of 1843–44 provides a case study: Graham's education bill foundered on sectarian rivalries when its proposal to place schools under Church of England supervision provoked dissenter opposition. The [[proletariat|working class]], "divided by the Church question," remained "inactive" [006-050] — Engels's implicit argument being that religious divisions within the bourgeoisie themselves become obstacles to even modest reforms, while simultaneously fragmenting working-class solidarity.

## Historical Context

Engels's treatment of religion must be understood against the backdrop of the 1840s: the established Church of England's close alliance with the Tory Party, the rising influence of dissenting sects (Methodists, Quakers, Unitarians) among the industrial [[bourgeoisie|middle class]], and the secularist traditions emerging within [[chartism|Chartism]] and [[robert-owen|Owen]]ite [[socialism|Socialism]]. The demand for "strictly secular public education, leaving religion to the ministers of the sects" [005-020] was a key working-class political demand that anticipated later nineteenth-century secularist movements. The extension of [[competition|free competition]] to the religious sphere — "soon to be in religion, too, as the State Church collapses" [011-002] — reflects the same [[free-trade]] logic Engels saw governing all social relations under [[capital|industrial capital]]ism.

## Related Concepts

Religion intersects with several key concepts in Engels's analysis: [[education]] (the primary institutional mechanism through which religion is imposed), [[morality]] (which Engels distinguishes from religious ethics), [[chartism]] and [[socialism]] (the [[labour-movement|working-class movement]]s that offered secular alternatives), and [[competition]] (the economic logic Engels sees extending into the religious sphere).
