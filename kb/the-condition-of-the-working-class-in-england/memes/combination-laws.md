# Combination Laws

## Overview

The Combination Laws (formally the Combination Acts of 1799 and 1800) were statutes that made it a criminal offence for workers to combine together for the purpose of raising wages or improving working conditions. They effectively outlawed trade unions and collective bargaining. The repeal of these laws in 1824, followed by a restrictive amendment in 1825, was a pivotal moment in the history of the English [[Labour Movement]]. Engels analyses the repeal as a legal revolution — albeit one enacted by the old unreformed Parliament — that transformed the workers' struggle from clandestine conspiracy into open organisation. (008-008)

Engels draws particular attention to the historical irony of the repeal: it was passed by "the old, unreformed, oligarchic-Tory parliament, a law which never could have passed the House of Commons later, when the [[Reform Bill of 1832]] had legally sanctioned the distinction between bourgeoisie and proletariat, and made the bourgeoisie the ruling class." (008-008) This is one of Engels's sharpest observations: the pre-democratic Parliament, dominated by the landed interest, could — on occasion — enact legislation that benefited workers, precisely because the landed oligarchy did not directly represent the industrial bourgeoisie's interests.

## Engels's Account

Before 1824, Engels explains, workers' combinations existed only in secret. "Secret coalitions had, it is true, previously existed, but could never achieve great results." (008-008) He provides examples: a general strike of weavers in [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1812, organised by a secret association and repeated in 1822, during which vitriol was thrown into the faces of two workers who refused to join, blinding both men. In 1818, the association of Scottish miners was powerful enough to carry out a general strike. (008-008) These secret associations maintained "regular lists, treasurers, bookkeepers, and local branches," and required members to take "an oath of fidelity and secrecy." The clandestine character of these organisations, however, "crippled their growth." (008-008)

The repeal of 1824 changed everything. "When, on the other hand, the workingman received in 1824 the right of free association, these combinations were very soon spread over all England and attained great power. In all branches of industry Trades Unions were formed with the outspoken intention of protecting the single workingman against the tyranny and neglect of the bourgeoisie." (008-008)

Engels provides a detailed account of the functions of the newly legalised unions. Their objectives were to "deal, en masse, as a power, with the employers; to regulate the rate of wages according to the profit of the latter, to raise it when opportunity offered, and to keep it uniform in each trade throughout the country." They sought to negotiate a universal scale of wages, to limit the number of apprentices so as to maintain the demand for labour, to counteract "the indirect wages reductions which the manufacturers brought about by means of new tools and machinery," and to provide financial assistance to unemployed members through a system of tramping — the wandering worker supported by fellow unionists from town to town. (008-009)

The unions developed an organisational structure Engels describes in detail: a salaried President and Secretary (who could not be employed by any manufacturer), a committee that collected weekly contributions and administered funds, and federations of trades within districts that held regular delegate conventions. The ambition was to unite "the workers of one branch over all England in one great union," and Engels notes that attempts had been made — and had occasionally succeeded — to form a single general union of all workers across all trades. (008-009)

The right of coalition, Engels argues, was of immense political significance beyond its immediate economic effects. "We shall see later how the rebellion of the working-class against the bourgeoisie in England is legalised by the right of coalition." (013-002) The combination — the strike — was not only an economic weapon but the form in which the [[proletariat|working class]] first experienced collective power and class identity. The right to combine was the legal recognition that workers were not isolated atoms in a competitive labour market but a class with common interests.

## Context

The Combination Acts were passed in the atmosphere of political repression that followed the French Revolution. The British governing class feared that workers' associations would become vehicles for Jacobin ideas. The Acts were part of a broader pattern of anti-democratic legislation that included the suspension of habeas corpus and the Seditious Meetings Act. Under the Combination Acts, any worker who joined a union or attempted to bargain collectively could be prosecuted. Magistrates, who were typically employers or their allies, enforced the law with vigour.

The repeal of 1824 was championed by Francis Place, a Radical tailor from [[London]], and Joseph Hume, a Radical MP. Their strategy was to present the issue as one of economic efficiency rather than workers' rights, arguing that the illegality of unions simply drove them underground and made them more dangerous. The 1824 Act abolished all statutory prohibitions on combination, but a wave of strikes in 1825 led Parliament to pass a subsequent Act that limited the right of combination to peaceful bargaining over wages and hours, prohibiting intimidation and violence. This compromise framework — legal but constrained — remained the basis of British trade union law for much of the nineteenth century.

## Aftermath

The immediate consequence of the 1824 repeal was the explosive growth of trade unions across all branches of industry. The unions that Engels describes in 1844 — the spinners' unions, the builders' unions, the miners' unions — were direct products of the legal framework established in 1824. The unions became the organisational backbone of the [[Chartist Movement 1838]] and the broader [[Labour Movement]]. Without the right of combination, the mass strikes of the 1830s and 1840s — including the [[1844 Miners' Strike]] — would have been impossible.

Yet Engels is also clear about the limits of trade unionism. In the closing sections of his chapter on the labour movement, he argues that "something more is needed than Trades Unions and strikes to break the power of the ruling class." (008-012) The unions could moderate the worst excesses of [[exploitation|capitalist exploitation]] but could not abolish it. The lesson that Engels draws from the history of the Combination Laws is that legal rights, however hard-won, are insufficient; the ultimate emancipation of the [[proletariat|working class]] requires political revolution.
