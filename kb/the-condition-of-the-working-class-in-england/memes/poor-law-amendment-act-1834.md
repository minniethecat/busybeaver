# Poor Law Amendment Act (1834)

## Overview

The [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Amendment Act of 1834 was a Whig reform that fundamentally restructured [[pauperism|poor relief]] in England and Wales. Its central principle was "less eligibility": the condition of the [[pauperism|pauper]] receiving relief must be less desirable than that of the lowest-paid independent labourer. To enforce this, the Act abolished outdoor relief (cash or [[diet|food]] given to the poor in their own homes) and required all able-bodied [[pauperism|pauper]]s to enter workhouses — grim, prison-like institutions where families were separated, [[diet|food]] was meagre, and labour was compulsory. The Act was administered by [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Unions under the oversight of three [[poor-law-commission|Poor Law Commissioners]] based in [[london|London]]. (003-010)

## Engels's Account

> Philanthropy and the Poor Law help many of them to a painful prolongation of their existence. (003-010)

> The New Poor Law was the open declaration of war of the bourgeoisie upon the proletariat. (011-008)

Engels does not treat the New Poor Law as a well-intentioned reform, even a flawed one. He presents it as a calculated act of class warfare. The [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian rationale — that the poor breed recklessly and must be deterred from reproducing — was, in Engels's reading, the ideological cover for a simpler objective: forcing the rural poor off the land and into the factories at whatever wages the manufacturers chose to pay. By making outdoor relief inaccessible, the law eliminated the last [[diet|subsistence]] alternative to [[factory-system|factory]] labour. (010-002)

> The movements against the new Poor Law and for the Ten Hours' Bill were already in the closest relation with each other. (008-028)

The New Poor Law became a galvanising issue for the early labour movement. Engels documents how opposition to the workhouse system brought together Chartists, trade unionists, and [[factory-system|factory]] reformers. The Act was resented not merely because it was cruel — though cruelty was amply documented — but because it was transparently ideological: a law designed to create a compliant labour force by making starvation the only alternative to the [[factory-system|factory]]. In the industrial districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], where Engels lived, resistance to the Act was particularly fierce, and [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Guardians themselves sometimes refused to enforce its harshest provisions. (008-028)

## Malthusian Foundation

Engels anchors his entire critique of the New Poor Law in [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian population theory, which he identifies as the ideological weapon of the [[bourgeoisie]] against the [[proletariat]]. He summarises the [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ian doctrine as holding "that the earth is perennially overpopulated, whence poverty, misery, distress, and immorality must prevail; that it is the lot, the eternal destiny of mankind, to exist in too great numbers, and therefore in diverse classes, of which some are rich, educated, and moral, and others more or less poor, distressed, ignorant, and immoral." Under this logic, [[pauperism|poor relief]] is "nonsense, since they serve only to maintain, and stimulate the increase of, the surplus population whose competition crushes down wages for the employed." (011-008)

> Malthus declares in plain English that the right to live, a right previously asserted in favour of every man in the world, is nonsense. He quotes the words of a poet, that the poor man comes to the feast of Nature and finds no cover laid for him, and adds that "she bids him begone," for he did not before his birth ask of society whether or not he is welcome. (011-008)

This theory, Engels contends, became "the pet theory of all genuine English bourgeois" because it provided the most convenient excuse for their treatment of the poor. The [[poor-law-commission|Poor Law Commissioners]] of 1833, dispatched to investigate the old system, were thoroughgoing [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]ians who brought back a report that characterised the Old [[poor-law|Poor Law]] as a national calamity: (011-009)

> A check upon industry, a reward for improvident marriage, a stimulus to increased population, and a means of counterbalancing the effect of an increased population upon wages; a national provision for discouraging the honest and industrious, and protecting the lazy, vicious, and improvident; calculated to destroy the bonds of family life, hinder systematically the accumulation of capital, scatter that which is already accumulated, and ruin the taxpayers. (011-009)

The commissioners' solution was not the outright abolition of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] — which they lacked the courage for — but a system that was "yet more barbarous than that of laissez-faire, because it interferes actively in cases in which the latter is passive." In Engels's rendering, the commissioners' message to the poor was: "We grant you poor a right to exist, but only to exist; the right to multiply you have not, nor the right to exist as befits human beings." (011-009)

### The Workhouse System

The [[workhouse]] system created what Engels calls a "bastille for the poor" — a deterrent institution that, by design, was worse than the worst [[factory-system|factory]]. The regulations for these workhouses, or "Poor Law Bastilles" as the people called them, were "such as to frighten away everyone who has the slightest prospect of life without this form of public charity." (011-012)

The food was "worse than that of the most ill-paid workingman while employed, and the work harder." Meat was rarely furnished; the diet consisted chiefly of potatoes, the worst possible bread, and oatmeal porridge, with little or no beer. Engels notes pointedly that "the food of criminal prisoners is better, as a rule, so that the paupers frequently commit some offence for the purpose of getting into jail." The workhouse was indeed a jail: he who did not finish his task received nothing to eat; he who wished to go out had to ask permission; tobacco was forbidden, along with gifts from relatives or friends outside. The paupers wore a workhouse uniform and were "handed over, helpless and without redress, to the caprice of the inspectors." Men broke stones; women, children, and aged men picked oakum. (011-012)

To prevent the "superfluous" from multiplying, families were broken up — husband in one wing, wife in another, children in a third — permitted to see one another only at stated times, and only when they had "behaved well." The psychological effect was as important as the material one: the existence of the workhouse as a threat was intended to discipline workers into accepting any conditions rather than seeking relief. (011-012)

Engels reinforces his indictment with documented atrocities: a five-year-old boy at Greenwich shut into the dead-room to sleep on coffin lids; a man at Coventry dying of a neglected wound after being discharged from the workhouse where his wife was reprimanded for bringing him beer; tramps at [[birmingham|Birmingham]] found "shut up naked under a staircase in a black hole" for eight to ten days. (011-013)

## Opposition and Resistance

The New Poor Law encountered fierce resistance across England. The movement against it was inseparable from the agitation for the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] and the broader [[chartism|Chartist]] movement. Engels documents how opposition to the workhouse system brought together [[labour-movement|Chartists]], trade unionists, and [[factory-system|factory]] reformers in a common front against what they perceived as class legislation. (008-028)

Some resistance was direct and violent. In the countryside, the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] became a direct incitement to arson and attack. Engels reports that changes in the [[poor-law|Poor Law]], along with low wages and the introduction of machinery, were among the immediate causes of the "Swing" incendiarism riots of 1830–31, when farmers' hayricks, barns, and stables were burnt nightly throughout southern England. The [[rural|countryside]], Engels argues, had entered "social war." (010-006)

The [[workhouse]] itself was a flashpoint of class conflict. Engels records multiple instances in which rural labourers, driven to desperation by the refusal of outdoor relief, attacked workhouses — at Carmarthen, at Narbeth, and elsewhere. These attacks were targeted collective action, directed at the most visible symbol of the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] regime that had replaced traditional forms of [[parish|parish relief]] with a system designed, in Engels's words, "to make poverty a crime." (010-008)

The Act was also resisted by local authorities who saw it as an intrusion by central government into local affairs. In the industrial districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], existing patterns of [[factory-system|factory]] employment made outdoor relief less necessary, and some [[poor-law|Poor Law]] Guardians refused to enforce the Act's harshest provisions. The [[bourgeoisie|manufacturers]], for their part, exploited the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] for their own benefit — Engels recounts the case of a manufacturer in Sheffield who reported workers who refused wage reductions to the [[poor-law|Poor Law]] authorities as persons who "could find work but would not take it," thereby compelling acceptance of lower wages under threat of the workhouse. (010-002)

## Aftermath

Engels concludes that the New Poor Law "intensified, more than any other measure of the party in power, the hatred of the working-class against the property-holders." It "contributed so greatly to accelerate the labour movement, and especially to spread Chartism" because the bourgeoisie had "formulated so clearly in this law its conception of its duties towards the proletariat" — namely that "the non-possessing class exists solely for the purpose of being exploited, and of starving when the property-holders can no longer make use of it." (011-012)

The Act's legacy extended even into death. Engels documents the treatment of the pauper dead as the ultimate expression of the law's logic:

> In Manchester, the pauper burial-ground lies opposite to the Old Town, along the Irk: this, too, is a rough, desolate place. About two years ago a railroad was carried through it. If it had been a respectable cemetery, how the bourgeoisie and the clergy would have shrieked over the desecration! But it was a pauper burial-ground, the resting-place of the outcast and superfluous, so no one concerned himself about the matter. (011-016)

> Can anyone wonder that the poor decline to accept public relief under these conditions? That they starve rather than enter these bastilles? (011-012)

Engels reports five cases of persons who, when the guardians refused them outdoor relief, went back to their miserable homes and died of starvation rather than enter the [[workhouse]]. He notes that the Act achieved its object: the poor were afraid to seek relief. But it also produced an unintended consequence — from [[newcastle|Newcastle]] to [[dover|Dover]], there was but one voice among the workers: the voice of hatred against the new law. The bourgeoisie had committed itself so openly, so frankly, to the principle that the non-possessing class existed only to be exploited, that no worker could fail to grasp its meaning. (011-012)
