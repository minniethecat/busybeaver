# Oldham

## Overview

Oldham, situated in South [[lancashire|Lancashire]] northeast of [[manchester|Manchester]], was by the 1840s one of the major cotton-spinning towns of the industrial district. Engels records its population at 50,000, making it one of the substantial [[urban|factory town]]s surrounding [[manchester|Manchester]] -- alongside [[bolton|Bolton]] (60,000), [[rochdale|Rochdale]] (75,000), and [[preston|Preston]] (60,000). Like its neighbours, Oldham was "almost wholly working-people's districts, interspersed only with factories, a few thoroughfares lined with shops, and a few lanes along which the gardens and houses of the manufacturers are scattered like villas." Engels singles out Oldham and [[preston|Preston]] as being "among the worst" of these towns. (P04-010, 002-031, 002-032)

## Engels's Account

### The Town and Housing

Engels describes the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[urban|factory town]]s in general terms that apply directly to Oldham: "badly and irregularly built with foul courts, lanes, and back alleys, reeking of coal smoke, and especially dingy from the originally bright red brick, turned black with time, which is here the universal building material." Cellar [[housing|dwellings]] were "general here; wherever it is in any way possible, these subterranean dens are constructed, and a very considerable portion of the population dwells in them." Engels states explicitly that these towns are "purely industrial," conducting all their business through [[manchester|Manchester]] upon which they were "in every respect dependent," and inhabited "only by workingmen and petty tradesmen." (002-031)

### Working Conditions and Accidents

[[manchester-guardian|The Manchester Guardian]] of July 24, 1843, reported a horrifying [[factory-system|factory]] accident in Oldham: a girl was caught by a strap, carried around fifty times by the machinery, and died with not a single bone left unbroken. This incident is cited by Engels among a series of gruesome [[factory-system|factory]] accidents reported in the [[manchester|Manchester]] area within a few weeks in the summer of 1843. The accident illustrates the dangers of unguarded machinery, particularly the strapping that conveyed motive power from shafts to individual [[machinery|machines]]. (006-038)

### Child Labour and the Operatives' Condition

Oldham, along with [[ashton|Ashton-under-Lyne]] and [[rochdale|Rochdale]], is mentioned by Engels as a town where operatives were "forced" into certain conditions of employment. The town shared the general characteristics of the [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] district: workers employed from early childhood, a twelve-hour or longer working day, and the full catalogue of occupational diseases -- deformities of the spine and legs from prolonged standing, chest affections from fibrous dust in the carding and combing rooms, and the general enfeeblement and premature ageing common to all [[factory-system|factory]] operatives. Engels notes that female operatives in particular displayed "lowness of stature, leanness and paleness," and that among married women "all the characteristic peculiarities of the English wife are conspicuously [[poverty|want]]ing." (006-058)

### Labour Unrest

Oldham, like the other [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[urban|factory town]]s, was a centre of labour militancy. In the crime reports from the [[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian of October 30, 1844, Oldham recorded "one strike for wages, one theft, one fight between Irish women, one non-union hatter assaulted by Union men, one mother beaten by her son, one attack upon the police, one robbery of a church" -- a snapshot of the social tensions and industrial conflict endemic to the cotton towns. Engels also notes a strike at Kennedy's mill in [[manchester|Manchester]], and the broader pattern of labour organisation across the [[cotton-industry|Lancashire cotton]] district of which Oldham was a part. (005-043)
