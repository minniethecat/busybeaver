## Overview

Salford is an industrial town in [[lancashire|Lancashire]] situated on a peninsula formed by a sharp bend of the River Irwell, directly opposite [[manchester|Manchester]]. Despite its larger historical importance — having given its name to the Salford Hundred, the administrative district of the region — by the 1840s it had become entirely a working-class district. Engels describes Salford's housing conditions as worse than [[manchester|Manchester]]'s, with its courts and narrow lanes rivaling the most degraded quarters he had seen anywhere.

## Engels's Account

### Geography and Demography

Engels locates Salford in his topographical survey of [[manchester|Manchester]]:

> "On the left bank of the Irwell, bounded by a sharp curve of the river, lies Salford, and farther westward Pendleton" [002-037]

He later characterises it in unequivocal terms:

> "If we cross the Irwell to Salford, we find on a peninsula formed by the river, a town of eighty thousand inhabitants, which, properly speaking, is one large workingmen's quarter, penetrated by a single wide avenue." [002-052]

The phrase "one large workingmen's quarter" is one of Engels's most compressed characterisations — an entire town of 80,000 people, with no significant middle-class residential districts, distinguished from [[manchester|Manchester]] only by a single broad street.

### Housing Conditions

Engels compares Salford unfavourably even to [[manchester|Manchester]]'s [[housing|slums]]:

> "The average construction of Salford is in this respect much worse than that of Manchester, and so, too, in respect to cleanliness." [002-052]

Salford's narrow lanes reminded him of Genoa's medieval alleyways:

> "All Salford is built in courts or narrow lanes, so narrow, that they remind me of the narrowest I have ever seen, the little lanes of Genoa." [002-052]

The older quarter, opposite [[manchester|Manchester]]'s Old Church, was "an old and therefore very unwholesome, dirty, and ruinous locality" [002-052] — comparable in condition to [[manchester|Manchester]]'s Old Town on the opposite bank.

### Administrative Boundaries and Worker Protection

Engels notes a significant consequence of the municipal boundary between [[manchester|Manchester]] and Salford: [[manchester|Manchester]]'s sanitary regulations did not apply across the river:

> "Manchester has no authority in Salford, the population of both places being, in all essentials, one industrial mass" [006-007]

This jurisdictional gap meant that [[factory-system|factory]] accidents and sanitary violations in Salford were excluded from [[manchester|Manchester]]'s official statistics, systematically undercounting the true scale of industrial injury and disease in the conurbation:

> "the accidents which happened in Salford are not included here, nor those which occurred in the neighbouring manufacturing towns" [006-038]

### Mortality

Despite being excluded from some [[manchester|Manchester]] statistics, Engels's comparative mortality data reveals Salford's elevated [[mortality|death rate]]:

> "Manchester, including Chorlton and Salford, one in 32.72; and excluding them, one in 30.75" [005-013]

This suggests that when [[manchester|Manchester]]'s mortality was calculated excluding the worst districts (including Salford), the rate appeared lower — precisely because the most afflicted population was excluded from the count.

### Pawnbroking

Salford's working-class poverty drove a thriving pawnbroking trade. Engels records:

> "twelve in a single street of Salford, Chapel Street — and pawn what little they possess" [005-038]

The concentration of a dozen pawnshops on one street alone testifies to the depth of working-class immiseration.
