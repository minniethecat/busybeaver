## Overview

Bolton is a [[urban|manufacturing town]] in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], approximately eleven miles northwest of [[manchester|Manchester]]. With a population of about 60,000 in the 1840s [P04-010], it was one of the larger cotton-spinning centres in the [[manchester|Manchester]] orbit. Engels describes it as one of the worst of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[urban|factory town]]s, characterised by a single dirty main street, a polluted watercourse, and an industrial [[proletariat|working class]] afflicted by unemployment during trade crises.

## Engels's Account

### Urban Conditions

Engels's assessment of Bolton's physical environment is damning:

> "Among the worst of these towns after Preston and Oldham is Bolton, eleven miles northwest of Manchester. It has, so far as I have been able to observe in my repeated visits, but one main street, a very dirty one, Deansgate, which serves as a market, and is even in the finest weather a dark, unattractive hole in spite of the fact that, except for the factories, its sides are formed by low one and two-storied houses. Here, as everywhere, the older part of the town is especially ruinous and miserable." [002-032]

A polluted watercourse running through the town contributed to the degradation:

> "A dark-coloured body of water, which leaves the beholder in doubt whether it is a brook or a long string of stagnant puddles, flows through the town and contributes its share to the total pollution of the air, by no means pure without it." [002-032]

### Economic Hardship

During the [[crisis|trade crisis]] of 1842, Bolton experienced severe unemployment. Engels records:

> "'Stockport to let.' In Bolton, where, in ordinary years, the average number of persons maintained by one operative was 2.26, the average number maintained during the crisis was 1.37 — a reduction of nearly 40 percent." [003-013]

This statistic — a 40% reduction in dependents supported per worker — illustrates the depth of distress in [[urban|manufacturing town]]s when demand collapsed.

### Food Adulteration

Engels documents systematic [[diet|food]] adulteration in Bolton, citing contemporary newspaper reports of prosecutions against dealers selling tainted meat:

> "hams seized at a dealer's in Bolton, were publicly burnt, and the dealer fined" [002-059]

Similar cases appeared repeatedly in the *[[manchester|Manchester]] Guardian* throughout summer 1844 [002-068][002-069][002-070], indicating a widespread and persistent problem with adulterated [[diet|food]] sold to the [[proletariat|working class]].

### Labour Movement

Bolton was also a site of working-class militancy. During the general strike and disturbances of 1842:

> "general strike and disturbance in Bolton, to which the authorities here turned their attention" [008-029]

Earlier, during the power-loom riots, the mill of Ainsworth and Crompton at Bolton was attacked, though "did no damage" [008-015].
