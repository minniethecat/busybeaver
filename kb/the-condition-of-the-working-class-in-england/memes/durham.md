## Overview

Durham, together with neighbouring Northumberland, formed one of Britain's most important coal-mining regions during the 1840s. The Durham coalfield supplied fuel for industry and shipping, with [[newcastle|Newcastle]] serving as the principal coal port. Engels documented the region extensively in his chapter on mining and industrial labour, highlighting both the scale of production and the severe hazards faced by miners.

## Engels's Account

### Scale of Production

The Durham and Northumberland [[mining|mines]] were among the most productive in Britain. Engels records:

> "The mines of Northumberland and Durham alone yield annually more than five million tons of coal." [P04-016]

This output was critical to powering Britain's industrial economy, feeding steam engines in factories, [[railway|railways]], and steamships.

### Mining Operations

In addition to coal, the region produced significant quantities of lead and other minerals:

> "On the borders of Northumberland and Durham, are the extensive lead mines of Allenheads..." [009-003]

The mining workforce was substantial, with entire communities dependent on colliery employment. Engels describes a pattern common to mining districts throughout the North of England:

> "In Yorkshire, as in Northumberland and Durham, the assertion is made by many that the miner is well paid." [009-007]

### The Haswell Colliery Disaster

One of the most significant events Engels documents is the Haswell Colliery explosion, which killed 95 workers:

> "On September 28, 1844, an explosion of carbonic acid gas killed 95 men and boys in Haswell Colliery, Durham. The carbonic acid gas, which had accumulated in a disused shaft, burst through into the workings." [009-009]

The disaster exemplified the lethal dangers of mid-nineteenth-century [[coal|coal mining]], where safety precautions were minimal and workers' lives were treated as expendable by mine owners.

### Labour Organisation

The miners of Northumberland and Durham were among the earliest to organise for better conditions:

> "The workers of Northumberland and Durham that they placed themselves at the head of the movement for a general union of miners." [009-013]

The Miners' Advocate, published in [[newcastle|Newcastle-upon-Tyne]], served as a voice for mining communities across the region.

### The Power of Mine Owners

Engels also illustrates the near-feudal power wielded by mine owners over local communities:

> "A coal-owner who owns considerable mines in Durham threatened the small tradesmen that he would refuse them custom if they continued to support the strike." [009-016]

During the 1844 miners' strike, this tactic was used to starve striking workers into submission by cutting off credit at local shops.
