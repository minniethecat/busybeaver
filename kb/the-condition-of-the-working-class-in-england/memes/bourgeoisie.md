# Bourgeoisie

## Definition

The bourgeoisie, in Engels's usage, encompasses all property-holders — both the industrial and commercial middle class proper and the [[aristocracy|so-called aristocracy]]. The proletarian, Engels argues, sees in both only the property-holder, because before the privilege of property all other privileges vanish. The sole difference is that the bourgeois proper stands in active relations with the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and mining proletarians, while the aristocrat comes into contact chiefly with [[farming|agricultural labour]]ers. What unites them is their shared interest in preserving the system of [[property|private property]] from which both derive their wealth. (001-003)

## Engels's Analysis

> Again, the repeated visitations of cholera, typhus, smallpox, and other epidemics have shown the British bourgeois the urgent necessity of sanitation in his towns and cities, if he wishes to save himself and family from falling victims to such diseases. (P01-005)

Engels's portrait of the bourgeoisie is relentlessly critical. He does not present the capitalist class as actively malicious but as structurally compelled to exploit. The bourgeois, by the logic of the system, must extract surplus value from the worker to accumulate capital and survive competition. Individual bourgeois may be personally benevolent — Engels acknowledges that some manufacturers built better housing or supported temperance — but the system overrides individual intentions. The bourgeoisie as a class cannot improve the condition of the proletariat without undermining its own position. (005-034)

The spatial organisation of [[manchester|Manchester]] becomes, in Engels's analysis, a physical manifestation of bourgeois hypocrisy. The city's commercial thoroughfares screen the wretched courts and cellars where workers live:

> Manchester lies at the foot of the southern slope of a range of hills... The working-class quarters are sharply separated from the parts of the city reserved for the middle class. (002-037)

The bourgeoisie, Engels observes, has designed the city so that a person may travel from one end to the other without ever seeing the misery of the working-class districts. This physical separation mirrors the moral separation Engels identifies: the bourgeoisie has more in common with the bourgeoisie of every other nation than with the proletarians living in the same city. The two classes are, in effect, two nations within one state. (002-037, 005-034)

The bourgeois response to working-class distress is, in Engels's view, a mixture of callousness and self-deception. When confronted with evidence of workers starving or dying of preventable diseases, the bourgeoisie comforts itself with the doctrine that the free market will eventually benefit all:

> The English bourgeoisie, charitable as it is, does not think it necessary to believe that the worker prefers honest work to starvation. (002-006)

This paternalism coexists with a studied indifference to the structural causes of poverty. The bourgeois, Engels argues, treats the worker as a commodity to be purchased at the lowest possible price and discarded when no longer profitable. The [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] and other [[factory-system|factory]] reforms are not victories of bourgeois conscience but rather concessions extracted by working-class agitation, conceded only when they no longer threatened profits and in fact served to accelerate the concentration of capital by crushing smaller competitors. (P01-005)

> The failings of the workers in general may be traced to an unbridled thirst for pleasure... But is that to be wondered at? (005-040)

The bourgeoisie, in Engels's telling, judges the proletariat by standards it does not apply to itself. The worker who [[drunkenness|drink]]s to forget his misery is condemned as intemperate; the bourgeois who [[drunkenness|drink]]s claret at his club is simply dining. The worker who has children outside marriage is immoral; the bourgeois who keeps a mistress is discrete. This double standard allows the capitalist class to attribute the wretched condition of the workers to their own failings rather than to the economic system that enriches the bourgeoisie at the proletariat's expense. (005-032, 005-040)

## Bourgeois Hypocrisy and Charity

> Mr. Editor — For some time past our main streets are haunted by swarms of beggars, who try to awaken the pity of the passersby in a most shameless and annoying manner, by exposing their tattered clothing, sickly aspect, and disgusting wounds and deformities. I should think that when one not only pays the poor-rate, but also contributes largely to the charitable institutions, one had done enough to earn a right to be spared such disagreeable and impertinent molestations. And why else do we pay such high rates for the maintenance of the municipal police, if they do not even protect us so far as to make it possible to go to or out of town in peace? (011-003)

The philanthropy of the English bourgeoisie is, in Engels's view, a species of hypocrisy. The letter from "A Lady" published in the *Manchester Guardian* reveals the bourgeois attitude in its unvarnished form: charity is a transaction, a payment to purchase immunity from the unpleasant sight of poverty. Engels observes that this letter was published without editorial comment as if its sentiments were perfectly natural and reasonable — a sign that it expressed the temper of the great majority of the English bourgeoisie. The bourgeois gives nothing outright but regards his gifts as a business matter, making a bargain with the poor, saying, in effect, that a subscription to the infirmary purchases the right not to be troubled by the sight of misery. (011-003, 011-004)

### The Philanthropic Transaction

The charity of the Christian bourgeois is infamous, Engels declares: she signs herself "A Lady" because she has lost the courage to call herself a woman. The poor, by contrast, are relieved much more by the poor than by the bourgeoisie, as Canon Parkinson himself attested. Such relief given by an honest [[proletariat|proletarian]] who knows what it is to be hungry, for whom sharing his scanty meal is really a sacrifice borne with pleasure, has a wholly different character from the carelessly-tossed alms of the luxurious bourgeois. (011-004)

This hypocrisy extends to the political sphere. The [[corn-laws|Corn Law]] repeal agitation is presented to the workingmen as a measure for their benefit — to lower the price of bread — but Engels argues the manufacturers have in view solely the immediate advantage the repeal would bring them. The workers, especially since the insurrection of 1842, are no longer to be misled. They demand of everyone who presents himself as interested in their welfare that he declare himself in favour of the [[chartism|People's Charter]] as proof of sincerity. Whoever declines is pronounced an enemy, whether a declared foe or a false friend. (011-005)

## The Bourgeoisie as Ruling Class: Law and State

> The working-class forms here some 78 percent of the whole population (about 300,000), and lives in parts of the city which exceed in wretchedness and squalor the lowest nooks of St. Giles and Whitechapel, the Liberties of Dublin, the Wynds of Edinburgh. (002-044)

The power of the bourgeoisie extends beyond the economic sphere to the state itself. Laws are necessary, Engels argues, only because there are persons who own nothing. Though this is directly expressed in but few laws — those against vagabonds and tramps in which the proletariat as such is outlawed — yet enmity to the proletariat is so emphatically the basis of the law that the judges, especially the Justices of the Peace who are bourgeois themselves, find this meaning without further consideration. (011-006)

If a rich man is brought before the court, the judge regrets the trouble, treats the matter favourably, and if forced to condemn, does so with extreme regret, ending in a miserable fine the bourgeois throws contemptuously upon the table. But if a poor devil appears — having almost always spent the night in the station-house — he is regarded as guilty from the beginning, his defence set aside with a contemptuous dismissal, and a fine imposed he cannot pay and must work out with months on the treadmill. (011-006)

The conduct of the police corresponds precisely to that of the magistrates. The bourgeois may do what he will and the police remain polite, adhering strictly to the law. But the proletarian is roughly and brutally treated; his poverty casts the suspicion of every sort of crime upon him and cuts him off from legal redress. For him the protecting forms of the law do not exist. The property-holding class in Parliament, meanwhile, struggles against the better feelings of those not yet fallen prey to egotism and seeks to subjugate the proletariat still further. One piece of common land after another is appropriated, depriving the poor of pasture and their children of playgrounds, driving the young people to the beer-shops. (011-006, 011-007)

## Bourgeois Economics: Malthus and the Poor Law

> A check upon industry, a reward for improvident marriage, a stimulus to increased population, and a means of counterbalancing the effect of an increased population upon wages; a national provision for discouraging the honest and industrious, and protecting the lazy, vicious, and improvident; calculated to destroy the bonds of family life, hinder systematically the accumulation of capital, scatter that which is already accumulated, and ruin the taxpayers. (011-009)

The most open declaration of war of the bourgeoisie upon the proletariat is, in Engels's assessment, [[thomas-malthus|Malthus]]'s Law of Population and the [[poor-law-amendment-act-1834|New Poor Law]] framed in accordance with it. Malthus's theory — that the earth is perennially overpopulated, that poverty and misery must prevail, that the poor man's right to live is nonsense — became the pet theory of all genuine English bourgeois. It is the most specious excuse for them, and has a good deal of truth in it under existing conditions, for the bourgeoisie has made those conditions. (011-008)

The New Poor Law, passed in 1834, abolished all outdoor relief in money and provisions. The only relief allowed was admission to the [[new-poor-law-1834|workhouse]], made the most repulsive residence the refined ingenuity of a Malthusian could invent. The food is worse than that of the most ill-paid workingman, the work harder; meat is rarely furnished; the food of criminal prisoners is better, as a rule. Families are broken up — the husband in one wing, the wife in another, the children in a third — and permitted to see one another only at stated times and when they have behaved well. The workhouse is, in effect, a prison. (011-012)

The bourgeoisie has formulated in the New Poor Law its conception of its duties towards the proletariat so clearly that it has been appreciated even by the dullest: the non-possessing class exists solely for the purpose of being exploited, and of starving when the property-holders can no longer make use of it. This law did not originate with any one section: the [[liberal-party|Liberal]] party enacted it, the Conservative party with Peel at its head defended it, and the "Noble Lords" gave their consent each time. Thus is the expulsion of the proletariat from state and society publicly proclaimed. (011-018, 011-019)

## Competition and the Class Struggle

The bourgeoisie, in Engels's analysis, has gained a monopoly of all means of existence in the broadest sense of the word. What the proletarian needs, he can obtain only from this bourgeoisie, which is protected in its monopoly by the power of the State. The proletarian is, in law and in fact, the slave of the bourgeoisie, which can decree his life or death. (003-003)

Competition is the completest expression of the battle of all against all which rules in modern civil society. This battle is fought not only between the different classes but also between the individual members of each class. The workers compete among themselves, and this competition of the workers among themselves is the sharpest weapon against the proletariat in the hands of the bourgeoisie. Hence the effort of the workers to nullify this competition by associations — the [[trade-unions|trade unions]] — and hence the hatred of the bourgeoisie towards these associations, and its triumph in every defeat which befalls them. (003-002)

The relation of the manufacturer to his operatives has nothing human in it; it is purely economic. The manufacturer is [[capital|Capital]], the operative Labour. If the operative insists that he is not Labour but a man who possesses the attribute of labour force, if he refuses to be sold and bought in the market as the commodity "Labour," the bourgeois reason comes to a standstill. He cannot comprehend that he holds any other relation to the operatives than that of purchase and sale; he sees in them not human beings, but hands. (011-002)
