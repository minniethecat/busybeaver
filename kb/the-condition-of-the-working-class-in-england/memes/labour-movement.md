# Labour Movement

## Definition

The labour movement, in Engels's analysis, encompasses all forms of collective working-class organisation: trade unions, strikes, political associations, and the [[chartism|Chartist movement]] itself. It is not a single organisation but a historical process — the awakening of the proletariat to its class interests and its development, over decades, from local trade societies to a national political force. The labour movement, Engels argues, keeps pace with industrial development: the most mechanised districts produce the most organised workers. (006-001, 008-040)

## Engels's Analysis

> The great cities are the birthplaces of labour movements; in them the workers first began to reflect upon their own condition. (005-032)

Engels locates the origins of the labour movement in the physical concentration of workers in the [[urban|factory town]]s. A hand-weaver in a rural cottage, isolated from other workers, could not develop class consciousness because he had no experience of a class. The [[factory-system|factory]], by bringing hundreds of workers under one roof, created the material conditions for collective identity. Workers who toiled together, lived in the same [[housing|slums]], and suffered the same privations began to recognise their common interests. From this recognition, trade unions were born. (005-032, 002-030)

The first trade unions were illegal — combinations of workers were prohibited under the [[combination-laws|Combination Acts]] of 1799–1800. Workers who organised faced imprisonment or transportation. Engels describes how unions operated secretly, with members taking oaths of loyalty and communicating through coded messages. The repeal of the [[combination-laws|Combination Acts]] in 1824 legalised trade unionism, but the struggle was far from over: employers responded with lockouts, blacklists, and the "document" — a declaration that the worker would not join a union, which the worker was required to sign as a condition of employment. (008-015)

> The factory-workers, and the cotton-workers pre-eminently stand, at the head of the labour movement. (006-001)

The cotton workers of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], Engels observes, were the vanguard of the labour movement. More concentrated, better paid in boom times (and therefore better able to sustain strike funds), and more directly exposed to the fluctuations of [[capital|industrial capital]]ism, they developed the strongest unions and the most militant traditions. [[manchester|Manchester]] became the centre of English trade unionism, and the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] [[factory-system|factory]] operatives provided the organisational backbone of [[chartism|Chartism]]. (006-001, 008-040)

The labour movement, in Engels's teleology, progresses through stages: from individual resistance (theft, machine-breaking) to collective economic organisation (trade unions, strikes) to political action ([[chartism|Chartism]]). Each stage represents a higher level of class consciousness. The worker who smashes a machine is still treating the symptom rather than the cause; the worker who joins a union has begun to identify the employer as the adversary; the worker who supports [[chartism|Chartism]] has grasped that the entire political order must be transformed. (008-020)

## Phases of Development

Engels traces the labour movement through distinct historical phases, each marking an advance in proletarian consciousness. The revolt of the workers began soon after the first industrial development, evolving from isolated individual acts to organised collective action. (008-004)

### Early Forms of Resistance

> The earliest, crudest, and least fruitful form of this rebellion was that of crime. The workingman lived in poverty and want, and saw that others were better off than he. It was not clear to his mind why he, who did more for society than the rich idler, should be the one to suffer under these conditions. Want conquered his inherited respect for the sacredness of property, and he stole. (008-005)

The workers soon discovered that crime offered no collective solution. The criminal protested alone, and the whole might of society crushed each individual offender. The next phase was machine-breaking — [[machinery|machine]]s were destroyed in the belief that they caused unemployment — but this too was isolated and futile. Both forms of resistance, Engels argues, treated symptoms rather than the capitalist system itself. What was needed was organisation. (008-005)

## The Unions as Schools of War

> But what gives these Unions and the strikes arising from them their real importance is this, that they are the first attempt of the workers to abolish competition. They imply the recognition of the fact that the supremacy of the bourgeoisie is based wholly upon the competition of the workers among themselves. (008-012)

The trade unions, Engels contends, are far more than instruments for wage bargaining. They constitute the military school of the working class — the institution in which workers learn discipline, solidarity, and the courage required for sustained struggle. Engels argues that the English workingman develops in strikes a peculiar courage unmatched elsewhere: to face months of gradual starvation with a starving family, standing firm against the overwhelming power of capital, requires a determination that surpasses the momentary bravery of insurrection. (008-012, 008-020)

## The Chartist-Socialist Divide

> Chartism is no political movement, where the main point is your getting the ballot. Chartism is a knife and fork question: the Charter means a good house, good food and drink, prosperity, and short working-hours. (008-027)

The mature labour movement in Engels's time was divided into two principal currents: [[chartism|Chartism]] and [[socialism|English Socialism]]. The Chartists, though theoretically less developed, were "genuine proletarians all over, the representatives of their class." The Socialists had more far-reaching proposals but, originating from the bourgeoisie, struggled to amalgamate with working-class life. Engels predicts that the union of these two currents — Socialism adopting the mass character of Chartism, Chartism absorbing the theoretical depth of Socialism — would create the true intellectual leadership of England. (008-037)

Meanwhile, proletarian self-education flourished outside bourgeois institutions. Workers founded their own schools and reading-rooms, translated French materialist philosophy, and eagerly consumed the works of [[percy-bysshe-shelley|Shelley]], [[lord-byron|Byron]], and [[william-godwin|Godwin]] — literature the bourgeoisie sanitised in "family editions." Engels himself witnessed workers in torn fustian jackets discoursing on geology and astronomy with more knowledge than most "cultivated" German bourgeois. (008-039)
