## Overview

*Letters on the Factory Act, as It Affects the Cotton [[manufacture|Manufacture]], Addressed to the Right Honourable the President of the Board of Trade* (1837) was the English political economist [[nassau-william-senior|Nassau W. Senior]]'s published intervention in the [[factory-system|factory]] reform controversy. Writing in the aftermath of the [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act of 1833]], Senior argued that the legal limitation of [[working-day|working hours]] threatened the profitability — and therefore the viability — of the cotton industry, on which British commercial prosperity depended.

Senior is best remembered in this context for his "last hour" theory: the claim, which Engels treats with particular scorn, that manufacturers' entire profit was earned in the final hour of the working day, making any reduction of hours economically ruinous.

## Engels's Critique

For Engels, Senior's *Letters* epitomise the ideological character of bourgeois political economy. The work is not engaged as a contribution to economic analysis worthy of serious refutation, but as evidence of the bad faith with which the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interest opposed all regulation:

> "Nassau W. Senior, *Letters on the Factory Act to the Rt. Hon. the President of the Board of Trade* (Chas. Poulett Thomson, Esq.), London, 1837." [013-002]

Engels cites Senior in the context of his broader attack on [[andrew-ure|Andrew Ure]] and other pro-manufacturer writers who depicted [[factory-act|factory legislation]] as economically destructive and morally pernicious. Senior, [[andrew-ure|Ure]], and the political economists form, in Engels's narrative, the intellectual wing of the capitalist defence: their function is to provide a respectable, theoretical justification for the brutal practices Engels has documented empirically.

The "last hour" argument is for Engels not an analytical error to be corrected but a confession: in claiming that all profit depends on the last hour of labour, Senior inadvertently reveals the extortionate character of the entire working day.

## Place in Engels's Source Hierarchy

Senior occupies a peculiar position in Engels's gallery of antagonists. He is cited not as a source of factual evidence — Engels draws his facts from the [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors, the medical commissioners, and the workers' own testimony — but as a specimen of the ideology Engels is attacking. The *Letters on the Factory Act* is treated as a primary document of bourgeois consciousness, as revealing in its blindnesses as the parliamentary reports are in their findings.

This method — treating hostile texts as evidence of the system they defend — is characteristic of Engels's dialectical approach throughout the work. The bourgeois economist who cannot see exploitation is himself the best evidence that exploitation is systematically concealed.
