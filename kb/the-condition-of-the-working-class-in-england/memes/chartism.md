# Chartism

## Definition

Chartism was the first mass working-class political movement, active in Britain from 1838 to 1848. It centred on the [[peoples-charter|People's Charter]], which demanded six reforms: universal male suffrage, secret ballot, equal electoral districts, [[wages|payment]] of MPs, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and annual parliaments. Engels treats Chartism not merely as a reform movement but as the political expression of the proletariat's class consciousness — "the compact form of their opposition to the bourgeoisie." (008-023)

> These six points, which are all limited to the reconstitution of the House of Commons, harmless as they seem, are sufficient to overthrow the whole English Constitution, Queen and Lords included. The so-called monarchical and aristocratic elements of the Constitution can maintain themselves only because the bourgeoisie has an interest in the continuance of their sham existence; and more than a sham existence neither possesses today. (008-025)

Engels argued that the Six Points, though formally limited to parliamentary reform, struck at the foundation of the British state. Universal suffrage would destroy the property-based electoral system; annual parliaments would make government responsive to popular will; the abolition of the property qualification would open public office to working men. The Charter was, in form, a modest programme of democratic reform; in substance, it was a revolutionary demand. (008-025)

## Origins and Formation

> Chartism was from the beginning in 1835 chiefly a movement among the workingmen, though not yet sharply separated from the bourgeoisie. (008-026)

The movement's origins lie in the disappointment that followed the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill of 1832]]. The working class had supported the agitation for reform in the belief that political rights would bring economic relief. When the Bill passed and the £10 householder franchise excluded the vast majority of workers, the sense of betrayal was profound. Engels traces the lineage of Chartism directly to this moment: the Reform Bill made the bourgeoisie the ruling class and gave legal sanction to the distinction between propertied and propertyless. (008-023, 008-026)

The [[general-workingmens-association|London Working Men's Association]] (LWMA), founded in 1836 by the cabinet-maker and self-educated radical [[william-lovett|William Lovett]], was the institutional birthplace of the People's Charter. The LWMA was the first durable working-class political organisation in Britain to articulate a comprehensive programme of democratic reform. In 1838 the association published the People's Charter — drafted primarily by Lovett with assistance from the radical MP Francis Place — whose six points became the founding document of the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. (008-023)

> Since the workingmen do not respect the law, but simply submit to its power when they cannot change it, it is most natural that they should at least propose alterations in it, that they should wish to put a proletarian law in the place of the legal fabric of the bourgeoisie. This proposed law is the People's Charter, which in form is purely political, and demands a democratic basis for the House of Commons. (008-023)

The LWMA represented the working class's first organised response to exclusion: if Parliament would not include workers, workers would demand inclusion on their own terms. The People's Charter, Engels notes, was not a radical break with British constitutional tradition but an attempt to reclaim it — universal suffrage had been the programme of the Levellers in the 1640s. (008-023)

## Engels's Analysis

> Chartism was from the beginning in 1835 chiefly a movement among the workingmen, though not yet sharply separated from the bourgeoisie. (008-026)

Engels traces Chartism's evolution from a broad Radical alliance to a purely [[labour-movement|working-class movement]]. In its early years, Chartist demands for political reform were shared by sections of the radical bourgeoisie, especially the small traders and shopkeepers being crushed by [[competition|industrial competition]]. This alliance was always uneasy: the bourgeois radicals [[poverty|want]]ed political reform to break the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]]'s monopoly on power; the workers [[poverty|want]]ed political power as a means to social and economic emancipation. (008-026, 008-031)

The decisive break came after the failed uprisings and mass arrests of 1839–1842. When the bourgeoisie recoiled from the Chartists' willingness to use physical force, the movement shed its bourgeois elements and became "purely a workingman's cause." The "Complete" newspapers that had once championed Chartism — the *Weekly Dispatch*, *Weekly Chronicle*, *Examiner* — fell silent or turned hostile. In their place arose a working-class press that articulated the specifically proletarian character of the movement. (008-031, 008-032)

> Chartism is of an essentially social nature, a class movement. The "Six Points" which for the Radical bourgeois are the beginning and end of the matter... are for the Chartist a mere means to further ends. (008-034)

This is Engels's central insight about Chartism: the Six Points were never the real goal. For the bourgeois Radical, parliamentary reform was an end in itself — a way to gain entry to the political class. For the Chartist worker, the Charter was a means to social transformation. "Chartism," Engels writes, "is essentially of a social nature." The demand for the vote was a demand for the power to change the conditions of life — to abolish the [[poor-law|Poor Law]], to regulate [[factory-system|factory]] hours, to protect wages. Political democracy was the instrument; social equality was the aim. (008-034, 008-033)

### The Social Character of Chartism

The social question was central to Chartism from its earliest public meetings. In a meeting of 200,000 men on Kersall Moor, the Mons Sacer of Manchester, the Reverend Joseph Rayner Stephens gave the movement its enduring slogan:

> Chartism is no political movement, where the main point is your getting the ballot. Chartism is a knife and fork question: the Charter means a good house, good food and drink, prosperity, and short working-hours. (008-027)

Stephens's formulation captured what Engels called the "peculiarly social character of the workingmen's Chartism." The demand for the vote was inseparable from the demand for bread. Chartism was never, in its proletarian heart, a narrowly constitutional movement — it was a movement for the transformation of the conditions of life. (008-027)

## The 1842 Crisis and Its Aftermath

The crisis of 1842 was Chartism's high-water mark and the moment of its definitive separation from bourgeois radicalism. A commercial depression threw thousands out of work; the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], suffering under the crisis, adopted a revolutionary tone and sought to instrumentalise working-class distress. Engels records how the manufacturers calculated that flooding rural parishes with unemployed factory workers would overwhelm the local poor-rates and force the landed interest to capitulate on the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]. (008-029)

> This time, however, it was not the workingmen who wished to quit work, but the manufacturers who wished to close their mills and send the operatives into the country parishes upon the property of the aristocracy, thus forcing the Tory Parliament and the Tory Ministry to repeal the Corn Laws. (008-029)

The workers refused to be used. At mass meetings in [[ashton|Ashton]] and [[hyde|Hyde]], "the question discussed was not, as the bourgeoisie had hoped, the repeal of the Corn Laws, but 'a fair day's wages for a fair day's work.'" The general strike that swept through Lancashire, Yorkshire, and the manufacturing districts — the [[plug-plot-riots|Plug Plot]] riots — demonstrated that Chartism had become a movement with its own independent objectives. (008-029)

The bourgeoisie's response to the insurrectionary wave was decisive. The propertied class closed ranks. Special constables were sworn in — Engels notes that the German merchants in Manchester "marched in an entirely superfluous manner through the city with their cigars in their mouths and thick truncheons in their hands." Troops were deployed. At [[preston|Preston]] the military was given the command to fire upon the crowd, "so that the unintentional revolt of the people stood all at once face to face, not only with the whole military power of the Government, but with the whole property-holding class as well." The insurrection subsided. (008-030)

The fruit of the uprising was the decisive separation of the proletariat from the bourgeoisie. "From this moment," Engels writes, "Chartism was purely a workingman's cause freed from all bourgeois elements." The Radical bourgeoisie joined hands with the Liberals against the workingmen and made the repeal of the Corn Laws their sole objective. The Chartists, isolated but independent, consolidated their organisation in the [[national-charter-association|National Charter Association]], founded in [[manchester|Manchester]] in July 1840, which at its height claimed over forty thousand members in four hundred local branches. (008-029, 008-031, 008-032)

## Chartism and Socialism

Engels's treatment of the relationship between Chartism and [[socialism]] is one of the most forward-looking passages in the book. The workingmen's movement, he observes, is divided into two sections:

> Hence it is evident that the workingmen's movement is divided into two sections, the Chartists and the Socialists. The Chartists are theoretically the more backward, the less developed, but they are genuine proletarians all over, the representatives of their class. The Socialists are more farseeing, propose practical remedies against distress, but, proceeding originally from the bourgeoisie, are for this reason unable to amalgamate completely with the working-class. The union of Socialism with Chartism, the reproduction of French Communism in an English manner, will be the next step, and has already begun. Then only, when this has been achieved, will the working-class be the true intellectual leader of England. (008-035, 008-036)

This passage contains, in embryo, the programme of Marx and Engels's later political work: the fusion of the English working-class movement's mass base with socialist theory. Chartism provided the proletarian authenticity and the organisational infrastructure; socialism provided the theoretical clarity about capitalism's systemic nature and the long-term goal of collective ownership. The synthesis Engels calls for here would take decades to achieve, but its outline was already visible in the Chartist-socialist alliances of the mid-1840s. (008-035, 008-036)

> At the very moment when Chartism was bound to assert itself in its full strength, it collapsed internally. (P01-010)

Engels identifies the French Revolution of 1848 as the event that saved the English bourgeoisie. The socialist proclamations of the victorious French workers frightened the English petty bourgeoisie, who abandoned the Chartist alliance and rallied to the side of order. The Chartist demonstration of April 10, 1848 — planned as a mass presentation of a third petition to Parliament — was contained by massive police and military mobilisation. The movement, already weakened by internal divisions, never recovered. (P01-010)

Yet Engels does not regard Chartism as a failure. It was the first independent political organisation of the [[proletariat|working class]] anywhere in the world. It demonstrated that the proletariat could articulate its own demands, build its own institutions, and sustain a national movement over a decade. The lessons of Chartism — about class solidarity, political organisation, and the limits of bourgeois reformism — directly shaped the emerging socialist movement. (005-032)
