## Overview

Paisley was a significant textile-[[urban|manufacturing town]] in west-central Scotland, situated near [[glasgow|Glasgow]]. In the 1840s it was renowned for thread production and for the distinctive "Paisley pattern" woven into shawls and textiles — a design adapted from Kashmiri imports. Engels mentions Paisley in his survey of the geographical distribution of the cotton industry, noting that thread [[manufacture|manufacturing]] and spinning had spread to the Scottish lowlands as part of the broader industrialisation of textile production.

## Engels's Account

### Thread Manufacturing

Engels identifies Paisley as one of the centres of cotton thread production:

> "The thread manufactured and spun is carried on in Paisley and elsewhere in Scotland." [P04-015]

Cotton thread — a specialised product used for sewing rather than weaving — was a significant branch of the textile industry. Paisley's thread mills, powered by water and later steam, produced sewing thread for both the domestic market and export. The industry employed large numbers of women and children, who were preferred for their dexterity in handling fine thread and whose wages were lower than those of adult men.

### The Scottish Cotton District

Engels notes that Lanarkshire and Renfrewshire — the counties containing [[glasgow|Glasgow]] and Paisley respectively — formed the centre of the Scottish cotton district:

> "The centre for the cotton district of Scotland, for Lanarkshire and Renfrewshire." [P04-010]

The Scottish cotton industry, while smaller than [[lancashire|Lancashire]]'s, was significant, employing tens of thousands of workers. Paisley's proximity to [[glasgow|Glasgow]], a major port with access to American cotton imports, positioned it within the transatlantic supply chain that linked slave-produced raw cotton from the American South to the textile mills of Britain.
