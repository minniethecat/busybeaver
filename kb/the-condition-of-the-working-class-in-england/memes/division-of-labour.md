# Division of Labour

## Definition

The division of labour is the fragmentation of the production process into specialised, repetitive operations, each performed by a different worker. In pre-industrial manufacture under the [[domestic-industry|cottage system]], a single worker might card, spin, and weave. Under the [[factory-system|factory]] system, each of these operations is performed by a different worker — or, increasingly, by a machine. The division of labour increases productivity enormously but reduces the worker from a craftsman who understands the entire production process to a mere "hand" who performs one mindless operation. (P04-006)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels traces the division of labour from its origins in spinning and weaving to its full development under the [[factory-system|factory]] system. In the early stages of manufacture, the division of labour increased the skill required of the worker: a specialist spinner could produce finer yarn than a generalist. But as machinery advanced, the division of labour was increasingly delegated to [[machinery|machines]] rather than workers. The specialist spinner was replaced by the [[machinery|spinning-jenny]]; the hand-weaver by the power-loom. The worker who remained was not a specialist but a machine-minder, whose task was to tend the machine rather than to perform a skilled operation. (P04-006)

The degradation of the worker through the division of labour is a central theme in Engels's critique. He writes of the [[factory-system|factory]] operative who spends twelve hours a day performing the same two or three movements — tying broken threads, feeding raw material, watching for jams — until his body is deformed and his mind is atrophied. This is not an accident of bad [[factory-system|factory]] management but the logical endpoint of the division of labour: the worker is reduced to an appendage of the machine. What [[adam-smith|Adam Smith]] celebrated as the source of the wealth of nations, Engels condemns as the source of the degradation of the worker. (006-010)
