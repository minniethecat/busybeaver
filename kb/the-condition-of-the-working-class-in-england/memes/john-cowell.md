## Overview

John Cowell was a British factory commissioner assigned to Lancashire during the 1833 Factory Inquiry Commission. His evidence on the physical and moral condition of factory operatives is extensively cited — and sometimes criticised — by Engels.

## Statistical Evidence on Stunted Growth

Cowell provided striking comparative weight data demonstrating the physical effects of factory labour on child development. Engels cites his findings:

> Cowell gives the weight of 46 youths of 17 years of age, from one Sunday school, of whom 26 employed in mills, averaged 104.5 pounds, and 20 not employed in mills, 117.7 pounds.
>
> (006-029)

The difference — over 13 pounds, approximately 11% less body weight — was presented as clear evidence that factory labour stunted the growth of young operatives.

## On Factory Morality

Cowell recorded the moral judgments of witnesses about factory conditions. Engels cites a witness from Leicester who "would rather let his daughter beg than go into a factory" and described mills as "perfect gates of hell." A Manchester witness asserted that "three-fourths of the young factory employees, from fourteen to twenty years of age, were unchaste" (006-012).

Cowell himself expressed the opinion "that the morality of the factory operatives is" degraded by their working conditions (006-012).

## Engels' Criticism

While Engels draws heavily on Cowell's evidence, he is also critical. Engels notes that the Lancashire commissioners — Cowell, Tufnell, and Hawkins — "have almost wholly neglected" the physiological results of the factory system in their reports, despite Lancashire equalling Yorkshire in the number of crippled workers (006-019). This criticism reflects Engels' view that the commissioners, while documenting real abuses, did not go far enough in their condemnation.
