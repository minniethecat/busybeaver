## Overview

Wolverhampton is an industrial town in Staffordshire, in the English West Midlands. During the 1840s, it formed part of the Black Country's metal-working district, specialising in the production of coarse metal goods including locks and nails. Engels uses Wolverhampton as a case study of the "incredibly low" educational standards among the [[proletariat|working class]] and the brutal conditions endured by metal-working apprentices.

## Engels's Account

### Mortality and Housing

Engels mentions Wolverhampton in comparison with [[birmingham|Birmingham]], noting that despite [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s poor conditions, its mortality rates were lower:

> "Birmingham shows even during the reign of an epidemic a far smaller mortality than, for instance, Wolverhampton, Dudley, and Bilston, only a few miles distant." [002-024]

This brief reference establishes Wolverhampton as one of the unhealthier industrial towns in the region, with higher [[mortality|death rate]]s than its larger neighbour.

### Educational Deprivation

The most detailed account Engels provides of Wolverhampton comes through Commissioner Horne's findings on [[education|working-class education]], which Engels reproduces at length:

> "'In Wolverhampton,' says Commissioner Horne, 'I found, among others, the following example: A girl of eleven years had attended both day and Sunday school, "had never heard of another world, of Heaven, or another life." A boy, seventeen years old, did not know that twice two are four, nor how many farthings in two pence even when the money was placed in his hand. Several boys had never heard of London nor of Willenhall, though the latter was but an hour's walk from their homes, and in the closest relations with Wolverhampton. Several had never heard the name of the Queen nor other names, such as Nelson, Wellington, Bonaparte; but it was noteworthy that those who had never heard even of St. Paul, Moses, or Solomon, were very well instructed as to the life, deeds, and character of Dick Turpin, and especially of Jack Sheppard.'" [005-020]

Commissioner Horne found that even children who had attended Sunday school for years knew nothing of basic religious doctrine, with one child stating after five years of Sunday school attendance that they "does not know who Jesus Christ is" [005-020].

### Metal Manufacturing

Wolverhampton occupies a specific economic niche in Engels's typology of industrial England. Unlike [[lancashire|Lancashire]]'s textile factories or [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s fine metal goods, Wolverhampton produced coarser items:

> "In Staffordshire, especially at Wolverhampton, where the coarser articles, locks, nails, etc., are manufactured." [007-009]

In contrast to [[birmingham|Birmingham]]'s "Démocratie industrielle" of small workshops, Wolverhampton's metal district was characterised by:

> "fewer factories, but chiefly single forges, where the small masters work alone, or with one or more apprentices, who serve them until reaching the twenty-first year." [007-010]

### Apprentice Conditions

The treatment of apprentices in Wolverhampton's forges was among the worst Engels documented. They were fed:

> "almost exclusively meat from diseased animals or such as have died a natural death, or tainted meat, or fish to eat, with veal from calves killed too young, and pork from swine smothered during transportation" [007-010]

Engels notes that this practice "seems to be universal in Wolverhampton" [007-010], with consequences including frequent bowel complaints. Children typically had "rarely other clothing than their working rags" [007-010], preventing even Sunday school attendance. The physical toll was severe — "the children are puny, weak, and, in many cases, severely crippled" [007-010], with cases of retarded puberty extending to age nineteen.
