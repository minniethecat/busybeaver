## Overview

The Chartist Petition of 1842 was the second of three great national petitions presented to the House of Commons by the [[chartism|Chartist movement]]. Containing over 3.3 million signatures — approximately one-third of the adult population of Great Britain — it demanded the six points of the [[chartism|People's Charter]]: universal male suffrage, secret ballot, equal electoral districts, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, [[wages|payment]] of MPs, and annual parliaments. Engels regarded [[chartism|Chartism]] as the most important working-class political movement of the age and treated the 1842 petition as evidence of the class consciousness developing among British workers.

## Engels's Account

### Chartism and Free Trade

Engels contrasts [[chartism|Chartism]] with the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]], the middle-class free trade movement:

> "The manufacturing capitalists, from the Chartist opposition, not to Free Trade..." [P01-010]

Engels argues that the Chartist opposition to the [[anti-corn-law-league|Anti-Corn Law League]] exposed the class division within the reform camp: the [[bourgeoisie|middle class]] [[poverty|want]]ed cheaper bread (through repeal of the [[corn-laws|Corn Laws]]) to enable lower wages, while the [[proletariat|working class]] [[poverty|want]]ed political power to change the entire system.

### Feargus O'Connor and Mass Mobilisation

The Chartist leader [[feargus-oconnor|Feargus O'Connor]] was central to the 1842 campaign:

> "When Feargus O'Connor, the Chartist leader, came to Manchester during the insurrection of 1842, he appeared, amidst the deafening cheers of the workingmen..." [002-056]

O'Connor's ability to draw mass crowds in [[manchester|Manchester]] — the heart of the industrial economy — demonstrated the depth of working-class support for [[chartism|Chartism]]. Engels presents this as evidence that the [[proletariat|working class]] had developed both political consciousness and organisational capacity.

### The Chartist Argument Validated

Engels believed the 1842 crisis vindicated the Chartist analysis:

> "Had proved most brilliantly the Chartists' assertion of the necessity of political power for the working class." [006-050]

The failure of Parliament to respond to the petition — it was rejected by 287 votes to 49 — proved, in Engels's view, that the ruling class would never voluntarily concede political power. The rejection radicalised sections of the [[chartism|Chartist movement]] and contributed to the outbreak of the 1842 general strike later that year.
