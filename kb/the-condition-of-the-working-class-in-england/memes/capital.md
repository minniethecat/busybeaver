# Capital

## Definition

Capital, in Engels's usage, is not simply money but accumulated wealth employed to purchase means of production (machinery, raw materials, [[factory-system|factory]] buildings) and labour-power. The possession of capital is what distinguishes the bourgeoisie from the proletariat: the capitalist owns the means of production; the worker owns only his labour-power. This asymmetry -- capital's ability to wait, the worker's inability to -- is the foundation of the class relation. (001-003, 003-002)

## Engels's Analysis

Engels observes that the industrial revolution involved not only a technological transformation but a massive concentration of capital. Before machinery, [[manufacture|manufacturing]] was dispersed and required relatively little fixed capital -- a hand-loom was an affordable tool. The [[factory-system|factory]] system changed this equation entirely. A [[steam-engine|steam-power]]ed mill with hundreds of power-looms represented an investment of thousands of pounds, accessible only to those who already possessed significant wealth or could attract investors. Capital became a barrier to entry that excluded the worker from independent production and forced him into [[wages|wage-labour]]. (001-003)

The competitive dynamic among capitalists themselves accelerates capital concentration. Smaller manufacturers, with less capital, cannot survive price wars or trade downturns. They are absorbed by larger competitors or driven into bankruptcy, their former owners joining the ranks of the proletariat. The result is a continuous transfer of capital from many hands to few -- "the centralisation of capital," as [[karl-marx|Marx]] would later theorise. Engels documents this process across multiple industries and notes that it was accelerated by apparently benevolent reforms: the [[factory-act|Factory Act]], by imposing costs that only large manufacturers could bear, drove small competitors out of business faster than competition alone could. (P01-004)

The capitalist's control of capital gives him a decisive advantage in industrial disputes. The worker must sell his labour daily to survive; the capitalist can wait. A strike that starves the worker merely reduces the capitalist's profits for a few months -- annoying, but not life-threatening. This imbalance, Engels argues, means that strikes, however well-organised, cannot ultimately change the structural relation between capital and labour. Only political transformation can do that. (003-002)

## Capital as Structural Power

Engels's deepest insight into capital concerns not its quantity but its function as a weapon of social warfare. In the competitive order of industrial society, capital is the instrument through which class domination is exercised -- not by force of arms but by control of the conditions of existence itself.

> Since capital, the direct or indirect control of the means of subsistence and production, is the weapon with which this social warfare is carried on, it is clear that all the disadvantages of such a state must fall upon the poor. For him no man has the slightest concern. Cast into the whirlpool, he must struggle through as well as he can. If he is so happy as to find work, i.e., if the bourgeoisie does him the favour to enrich itself by means of him, wages await him which scarcely suffice to keep body and soul together; if he can get no work he may steal, if he is not afraid of the police, or starve, in which case the police will take care that he does so in a quiet and inoffensive manner. (002-005)

This passage reveals the core of Engels's argument: capital is not a neutral factor of production but a relation of domination. The worker, Engels argues, is reduced in the eyes of the capitalist to a commodity whose value fluctuates with the market. He presents this dehumanisation with remarkable explicitness.

> The centralising tendency of manufacture does not, however, stop here. Population becomes centralised just as capital does; and, very naturally, since the human being, the worker, is regarded in manufacture simply as a piece of capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of wages. (001-004)

The reduction of the worker to a "piece of capital" is not merely rhetorical. It expresses the actual logic of industrial production, in which the labourer is an input cost to be minimised, no different in principle from coal or cotton.

> The bourgeois can increase his capital only in commerce and manufacture, and in both cases he needs workers. Even if he invests his capital at interest, he needs them indirectly; for without commerce and manufacture, no one would pay him interest upon his capital, no one could use it. So the bourgeois certainly needs workers, not indeed for his immediate living, for at need he could consume his capital, but as we need an article of trade or a beast of burden -- as a means of profit. (003-007)

### The Concentration Tendency and Its Consequences

The historical trajectory of capital under industrial capitalism is toward ever greater concentration. Engels identifies this tendency as irreversible under the existing system, driven by competition among capitalists themselves. The small capitalist is crushed by the large, the independent producer by the factory, the skilled artisan by the machine. Each crisis accelerates the process.

> And for a very good reason. The fact is, that all these concessions to justice and philanthropy were nothing else but means to accelerate the concentration of capital in the hands of the few, for whom the niggardly extra extortions of former years had lost all importance and had become actual nuisances; and to crush all the quicker and all the safer their smaller competitors, who could not make both ends meet without such perquisites. (P01-004)

This 1885 retrospective judgement is especially significant. Engels observes that reforms like the Factory Acts, far from checking capital's power, served the interests of the largest capitalists by eliminating smaller competitors who relied on petty exploitation to survive. What appeared as moral progress was in fact a mechanism of capital concentration.

In the concluding chapter, Engels forecasts that this concentration tendency will ultimately produce a revolutionary crisis. The intermediate classes -- the small proprietors, the independent artisans, the petty bourgeoisie -- are being ground down between large capital and the expanding proletariat.

> The proletariat would increase in geometrical proportion, in consequence of the progressive ruin of the lower middle-class and the giant strides with which capital is concentrating itself in the hands of the few; and the proletariat would soon embrace the whole nation, with the exception of a few millionaires. (011-022)

This polarisation thesis -- society dividing into an ever-smaller number of capitalists and an ever-growing proletariat -- is the foundation of Engels's revolutionary politics. Capital's concentration is, paradoxically, the instrument of its own destruction: by simplifying the class structure, it makes possible the collective consciousness and organised resistance that will overthrow it.
