# Glasgow

## Overview

Glasgow, on the River Clyde in Lanarkshire, was the second city of the United Kingdom and the centre of the Scottish cotton district. Its population had surged from 30,000 to approximately 300,000 since the introduction of the cotton industry — a tenfold increase driven by the same industrial revolution that transformed [[manchester]] and the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] towns. Engels describes Glasgow as the site of some of the most appalling working-class living conditions in the entire British Isles. (001-004, 002-025)

Engels's treatment of Glasgow draws heavily on two sources: the *Artisan* journal and the reports of J. C. Symonds, the Government Commissioner for investigating the condition of hand-weavers. Between them, they paint a picture that Engels himself, widely travelled and familiar with the worst [[housing|slums]] of [[london]] and [[manchester]], found unmatched in its degradation. (002-025, 002-026)

## Engels's Account

### The Wynds of Glasgow

The wynds — narrow lanes and alleyways characteristic of Scottish cities — were the focus of Engels's horror. He quotes the *Artisan*:

> "The working-class forms here some 78% of the whole population (about 300,000), and lives in parts of the city which exceed in wretchedness and squalor the lowest nooks of St. Giles and Whitechapel, the Liberties of Dublin, the Wynds of Edinburgh. There are numbers of such localities in the heart of the city, south of the Trongate, westward from the Saltmarket, in Calton and off the High Street, endless labyrinths of lanes or wynds into which open at almost every step, courts or blind alleys, formed by ill-ventilated, high-piled, waterless, and dilapidated houses." (002-025)

In these wynds, [[crowding|overcrowding]] reached extremes that surpassed even the worst of [[london]] and [[manchester]]:

> "They contain three or four families upon each floor, perhaps twenty persons. In some cases each storey is let out in sleeping places, so that fifteen to twenty persons are packed, one on top of the other, I cannot say accommodated, in a single room." (002-025)

These districts, Engels writes, "shelter the poorest, most depraved, and worthless members of the community, and may be regarded as the sources of those frightful epidemics which, beginning here, spread desolation over Glasgow." (002-025)

### Symonds's Testimony

The Government Commissioner J. C. Symonds provided Engels with some of the most vivid testimony in the entire book. Symonds stated that he had "seen wretchedness in some of its worse phases both here and upon the Continent," but that nothing prepared him for Glasgow:

> "Until I visited the wynds of Glasgow I did not believe that so much crime, misery, and disease could exist in any civilised country. In the lower lodging-houses ten, twelve, sometimes twenty persons of both sexes, all ages and various degrees of nakedness, sleep indiscriminately huddled together upon the floor. These dwellings are usually so damp, filthy, and ruinous, that no one could wish to keep his horse in one of them." (002-026)

Symonds estimated that the wynds of Glasgow contained "a fluctuating population of fifteen to thirty thousand human beings." He describes the courts as consisting "wholly of narrow alleys and square courts, in the middle of every one of which there lies a dung heap." Visiting the sleeping-places at night with the Superintendent of [[police|Police]], Symonds found "a complete layer of human beings stretched upon the floor, often fifteen to twenty, some clad, others naked, men and women indiscriminately. Their bed was a litter of mouldy straw, mixed with rags. There was little or no furniture, and the only thing which gave these dens any shimmer of habitableness was a fire upon the hearth." (002-027)

Symonds concluded that "theft and prostitution form the chief means of [[diet|subsistence]] of this population" and declared that "an extended examination of the lowest districts of other cities never revealed anything half so bad, either in intensity of moral and physical infection, nor in comparative density of population." Crucially, he noted that most of the houses in the quarter had been "declared by the Court of Guild ruinous and unfit for habitation, but precisely these are the most densely populated, because, according to the law, no rent can be demanded for them." (002-027)

### Alcoholism

Engels devotes particular attention to the extraordinary rates of [[drunkenness|intemperance]] in Glasgow. He reports the estimate of [[archibald-alison|Sheriff Alison]]:

> "Sheriff Alison asserts that in Glasgow some thirty thousand workingmen get drunk every Saturday night, and the estimate is certainly not exaggerated; and that in that city in 1830, one house in twelve, and in 1840, one house in ten, was a public-house." (005-037)

Engels contextualises this statistic within broader trends of rising alcohol consumption across Britain. In Scotland, excise was paid upon 2,300,000 gallons of spirits in 1823; by 1837, the figure had risen to 6,620,000 gallons. Engels argues that this [[drunkenness|intemperance]] was not a moral failing but a "necessary, inevitable effect of certain conditions" — the only available escape for a [[proletariat]] whose "enfeebled frame, weakened by bad air and bad [[diet|food]], violently demands some external stimulus" and whose "social need can be gratified only in the public-house." (005-037)

### Mortality and Disease

Glasgow's death-rate was among the worst in Britain. Engels reports that, according to Dr. Cowen, the average mortality in Glasgow since 1830 was one in 30, and in single years rose as high as one in 22 to 24 — more than double the national average of one in 45. (005-013)

During the [[cholera|typhus]] epidemic that followed the crisis of 1842, the devastation in Glasgow was unparalleled. Engels reports that "twelve percent of the population were seized in the year 1843; 32,000 persons, of whom thirty-two percent perished," whereas the mortality from [[cholera|typhus]] in [[manchester]] and [[liverpool]] during the same period "does not ordinarily exceed eight percent." The scale of the epidemic led Engels to describe the Glasgow wynds as "the sources of those frightful epidemics which, beginning here, spread desolation." (005-006, 002-025)

The physical toll of industrial labour is further attested by Surgeon Smellie of Glasgow, who treated operatives and reported that "forty years is old age for them" — a finding Engels uses to illustrate the premature ageing and enfeeblement produced by the [[factory-system]]. (006-032)

### Irish Population

Glasgow had a substantial Irish immigrant population. Engels reports 40,000 poor Irish in the city — the same number as [[manchester]] and more than [[liverpool]]'s 34,000, though far fewer than [[london]]'s 120,000. These immigrants, concentrated in the worst districts, contributed to the downward pressure on [[wages]] and living standards that Engels identifies as a consequence of [[irish-immigration]]. (004-001)

## Statistics

- **Population**: approximately 300,000 (up from 30,000 before industrialisation) (001-004, 002-025)
- **Working-class share of population**: 78% (002-025)
- **Irish population**: 40,000 (004-001)
- **Wynd population**: 15,000 to 30,000 fluctuating residents (002-027)
- **Public-houses**: 1 in 10 houses by 1840 (1 in 12 in 1830) (005-037)
- **Workers drunk every Saturday night**: approximately 30,000 ([[archibald-alison|Sheriff Alison]]'s estimate) (005-037)
- **[[mortality|Mortality]] rate**: average of 1 in 30; as high as 1 in 22 to 24 in single years (005-013)
- **1843 [[cholera|typhus]] epidemic**: 32,000 persons infected (12% of population), 32% mortality (005-006)
- **Average working-class longevity**: 40 years considered "old age" (Surgeon Smellie) (006-032)
