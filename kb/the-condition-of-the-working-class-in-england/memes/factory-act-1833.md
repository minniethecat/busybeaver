# Factory Act 1833

## Overview

The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833 — sometimes referred to as the Factory Act of 1834, the year it took full effect — was the first effective piece of British [[factory-act|factory legislation]]. It prohibited the employment of children under nine years of age in textile mills (except silk mills), limited the [[working-day|working hours]] of children aged nine to thirteen to forty-eight per week or nine hours per day, and capped young persons aged fourteen to eighteen at sixty-nine hours per week or twelve hours per day. It mandated two hours of compulsory [[education|schooling]] daily for children under fourteen and, most significantly, created the [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate — a corps of four inspectors empowered to enter mills at any time, take testimony under oath, and prosecute violators. (006-045)

The Act emerged from the Whig government's response to the growing public outcry over [[factory-system|factory]] conditions, led by [[Michael Sadler]] and the [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours movement]]. After [[michael-sadler|Sadler]]'s 1832 parliamentary committee report was attacked by manufacturers as partisan and distorted, the Whigs appointed a Royal Commission — the [[parliamentary-commission-1833|Factories' Inquiry Commission of 1833]] — composed of Liberal bourgeois members sympathetic to [[manufacture|manufacturing]] interests. (006-015)

## Engels's Account

### The Commission and Its Report

Engels devotes sustained attention to the [[factory-act|Factory Act]] and its origins in Chapter 6, "Single Branches of Industry." He characterises the 1833 Commission as a body "composed of Liberal bourgeois" whose report "on every page betrays sympathy with the manufacturers, distrust of the [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] report, repugnance to the workingmen agitating independently and the supporters of the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]]." Yet even this manufacturer-friendly commission "cannot whitewash the manufacturers" and "leaves such a mass of infamies upon the shoulders of the employers" that the agitation for reform was "fully justified." (006-045)

> The report of the Central Commission relates that the manufacturers began to employ children rarely of five years, often of six, very often of seven, usually of eight to nine years; that the working-day often lasted fourteen to sixteen hours, exclusive of meal-times. (006-015)

The evidence compiled by the Commission, though collected by men sympathetic to the manufacturers, was so damning that even they could not suppress it entirely. The report documented children of five years working fourteen-hour days, deformities caused by prolonged standing, and systematic evasion of even the minimal protections of earlier Factory Acts. (006-015, 006-045)

### The Bourgeois Response

Engels specifically indicts the bourgeois economist [[Andrew Ure]], who attacked even the modest 1833 provisions. Engels quotes [[andrew-ure|Ure]]'s complaint that the law was "an absurd and despotic measure directed against the manufacturers" and that children thrown out of [[factory-system|factory]] work — "cast out from the warm spinning-room into a cold world" — would subsist "only by begging and stealing." For Engels, such rhetoric is proof of the capitalist's absolute refusal to acknowledge any social obligation toward the [[proletariat|working class]]. (006-044)

> This was the state of things before 1833. Since that time, the Factory Act of 1834 came into operation... (006-045)

The immediate consequences of the Act were mixed. Engels acknowledges that "the reduction of working-hours to an average of twelve to thirteen, and the superseding of children as far as possible" led to the disappearance of "some of the most crying evils." Deformities, once widespread, "arose now only in cases of weak constitution." (006-045) But he immediately qualifies this:

> "Care has been taken to give the brutal profit-greed of the bourgeoisie a hypocritical, civilised form, to restrain the manufacturers through the arm of the law from too conspicuous villainies, and thus to give them a pretext for self-complacently parading their sham philanthropy. That is all." (006-045)

Compulsory education, he notes, "remained wholly a dead letter," with manufacturers employing "worn-out operatives" as teachers and children learning nothing. Inspectors Horner and Saunders reported in 1844 that in branches where children could be replaced by adults, "the working-day is still fourteen to sixteen hours, or even longer," and that many employers "disregard the law, shorten the meal times, work children longer than is permitted, and risk prosecution, knowing that the possible fines are trifling in comparison with the certain profits." (006-045)

In the 1892 Preface, Engels reflects on the longer arc: the 1833 Act was the beginning of a process by which "the truck system was suppressed, the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] was enacted, and a number of other secondary reforms introduced." But these reforms were enacted not from humanitarian motives but "to accelerate the concentration of capital in the hands of the few" — making regulation acceptable to the largest capitalists as a weapon against smaller competitors. (P01-004)

From Engels's perspective, the Act also serves the bourgeoisie politically. The legislation gave them something to point to as evidence of their benevolence, even as conditions in many factories remained brutal. He quotes the 1833 report as showing "the most shameful recklessness of the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] bourgeoisie towards its employees" and "the whole infamy of the industrial exploiting system in its full inhumanity." The Act mitigated the worst visible abuses without touching the system that produced them. (011-009)

## Context

### Pre-1833 Factory Legislation

The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833 was preceded by a series of largely ineffective statutes: the [[apprentice|Apprentice]]s' Act of 1802 ([[Robert Peel]] the elder), and the [[factory-acts-1818-1825-1831|Factory Acts of 1818, 1825, and 1831]] (Sir J.C. [[factory-acts-1818-1825-1831|Hobhouse's Act]]). Engels notes that "the first two were never enforced, and the last only here and there," because operatives who testified against their masters risked dismissal. [[factory-acts-1818-1825-1831|Hobhouse's Act]] capped young persons at twelve hours daily but lacked any enforcement mechanism. (006-045)

The [[factory-act|Factory Act]] of 1833 was the first to break with this pattern of unenforceable laws by creating a salaried inspectorate. This institutional innovation proved enduring — the [[factory-system|factory]] inspectorate survived and expanded, eventually becoming the model for labour regulation across industrialising Europe. The Act's limitations, however, ensured that agitation for a [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] would continue with renewed intensity through the late 1830s and 1840s, led by [[Lord Ashley]] and [[Richard Oastler]]. (006-049)

> The Act of 1833 made it necessary for all factory children under fourteen to go to school two hours daily, the manufacturer to provide the teacher. (006-047)

## Aftermath

The 1833 Act established the legal principle that the state could intervene in the labour contract to protect those deemed incapable of protecting themselves — children and young persons. The inspectorate model was gradually extended to [[mining|mines]] in 1842 and to other industries. The Act also hardened the political battle lines that would define [[factory-system|factory]] reform politics for the next two decades: Tory philanthropic paternalists aligned with working-class agitation against Whig-Liberal laissez-faire ideology, a fault line that shaped the campaigns for the [[ten-hours-bill|Ten Hours' Bill]] and contributed to the development of [[chartism|Chartism]]. (006-050, 008-028)
