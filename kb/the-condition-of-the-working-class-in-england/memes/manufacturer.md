## Definition

The manufacturer is the industrial capitalist who emerged from the transformation of commerce and handicraft during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. Unlike the merchant who merely distributed goods, the manufacturer owns the [[capital|fixed capital]] — factories, machinery, raw materials — and employs [[wages|wage-labour]]ers to produce commodities. Engels treats the manufacturer as the quintessential figure of the industrial bourgeoisie: the direct exploiter of the proletariat and the political leader of the middle-class party.

## Engels's Analysis

### The Rise of the Manufacturing Class

Engels traces the manufacturer's origins to the late eighteenth century, when the invention of the [[machinery|steam-engine]] and spinning machinery "called forth the former large merchants into manufacturers" (P04-023). The transition from merchant capitalism to [[capital|industrial capital]]ism was decisive: the manufacturer no longer merely circulated commodities but controlled production itself. The new industrial scale demanded massive [[capital|fixed capital]] investment — "capital for the use of which the manufacturer pays interest under the name of rent" (001-004) — which concentrated economic power in fewer hands.

### Competition and Exploitation

Engels emphasises that competition between manufacturers drives the relentless degradation of working-class conditions. "The competition of manufacturer against manufacturer by means of petty thefts upon the working-men" (P01-004) is the structural mechanism through which wages are depressed and hours extended. Each manufacturer, pressed by [[competition|market competition]], seeks to extract maximum [[surplus-population|surplus labour]]: "If a manufacturer can employ ten hands nine hours, he employs them twelve hours, and if he can employ one hundred hands twelve hours, he employs five hundred hands" (003-009). The competitive logic leaves no room for humanitarian restraint.

Engels specifically notes the manufacturers' response to the [[factory-act|Ten Hours' Bill]] agitation, using testimony from manufacturers themselves to show that "the poor-rate in the [[manufacture|manufacturing]] districts is far higher than in the [[rural|agricultural districts]]" (003-013), undermining claims that [[factory-system|factory]] labour improved workers' welfare.

### Political Leadership of the Middle-Class Party

After the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Act of 1832]], the manufacturers became "the party led by the manufacturers" (P01-010), the dominant faction of the bourgeoisie. Engels depicts them as a politically conscious class that understood its interests clearly. The Liberal manufacturers, led by figures like Cobden and [[john-bright|Bright]], advanced free-trade ideology as a weapon against both the [[aristocracy|landed aristocracy]] and the [[proletariat|working class]]. Engels singles out the assertion "of the Liberal manufacturers that the working population of England is in a highly prosperous condition" (002-049) as deliberate misrepresentation.

The manufacturer class also produced its own intellectual defenders. Engels notes that [[andrew-ure|Dr. Andrew Ure]]'s *Philosophy of [[manufacture|Manufacture]]s* was "valued as an authority by the manufacturers, and a fanatical opponent of the workers" (002-053), illustrating how the class sponsored ideological production alongside material production.

### Spatial and Moral Dimensions

The manufacturer's wealth created a new urban geography. "The gardens and houses of the manufacturers are scattered like villas" (002-031) on the outskirts of industrial towns, while workers were crammed into the central [[housing|slums]]. This spatial segregation was both a reflection and a reinforcement of class division: "anyone who knows [[manchester|Manchester]] knows that the wealthy manufacturers and merchants live in the outskirts" (002-037). The manufacturer's physical removal from the working-class districts enabled a moral removal — a denial of responsibility for the conditions their factories created.

Engels further indicts manufacturers for the adulteration of consumer goods: "Dealers and manufacturers adulterate all kinds of provisions" (002-059), including the notorious case of "Parr's Life Pills" sold by a manufacturer (005-011). This adulteration affected both workers and the lower [[bourgeoisie|middle class]], revealing the manufacturer's profit motive as indifferent to human welfare.

### The Manufacturer and Machinery

The manufacturer's demand for machinery was a self-reinforcing cycle: "the demand for machinery was stimulated by individual inventors and manufacturers" (P04-015). Each improvement in machinery allowed the manufacturer to displace skilled workers with unskilled labour, including women and children, further cheapening labour-power and increasing surplus value. The [[factory-system|factory]] system, built by the manufacturers, thus transformed not only production but the entire social order.
