# Irish Immigration

## Definition

Irish immigration, in Engels's analysis, denotes the large-scale movement of impoverished Irish labourers into England's industrial centres during the first half of the nineteenth century. Engels treats this migration not as an incidental demographic phenomenon but as a structural precondition of English industrial expansion: "The rapid extension of English industry could not have taken place if England had not possessed in the numerous and impoverished population of Ireland a reserve at command." The Irish immigrant, arriving with nothing but strength to sell, forms the lowest stratum of the urban proletariat — simultaneously enabling the [[factory-system|factory]] system's insatiable appetite for labour and exerting a downward pressure on wages, housing standards, and moral conditions that Engels argues has degraded the entire English [[proletariat|working class]]. (004-001)

## Engels's Analysis: The Reserve Army from Ireland

> The rapid extension of English industry could not have taken place if England had not possessed in the numerous and impoverished population of Ireland a reserve at command. The Irish had nothing to lose at home, and much to gain in England; and from the time when it became known in Ireland that the east side of St. George's Channel offered steady work and good pay for strong arms, every year has brought armies of the Irish hither. (004-001)

Engels estimates that over a million Irish had already immigrated, with nearly fifty thousand arriving annually by the 1840s. These immigrants concentrated overwhelmingly in the industrial districts and great cities: 120,000 in [[london|London]], 40,000 each in [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[glasgow|Glasgow]], 34,000 in [[liverpool|Liverpool]], 24,000 in [[bristol|Bristol]], 29,000 in [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]]. Engels emphasises that these people arrived "having grown up almost without civilisation, accustomed from youth to every sort of privation, rough, intemperate, and improvident," bringing what he characterises as "brutal habits" into a [[proletariat|working class]] already degraded by industrial conditions. The Irish competition operated on the lowest plane possible in a civilised country, and its effect was inexorable: where the Irishman could subsist on potatoes alone, the English worker's wage was dragged down toward the Irish minimum. (004-001)

## The Degradation of Living Standards

> The worst dwellings are good enough for them; their clothing causes them little trouble, so long as it holds together by a single thread; shoes they know not; their food consists of potatoes and potatoes only; whatever they earn beyond these needs they spend upon drink. What does such a race want with high wages? (004-002)

Engels's description of the Irish mode of living is unsparing. He observes that the majority of cellar-dwelling families are of Irish origin, that the custom of [[overcrowding|crowding]] many persons into a single room "has been chiefly implanted by the Irish immigration," and that the Irish habit of keeping pigs in living quarters — "this new and unnatural method of cattle-raising in cities is wholly of Irish origin" — exemplified the collision between rural Irish custom and urban density. The Irishman, unaccustomed to furniture, sleeping on straw with rags for bedding, using combustible parts of the dwelling for fuel, was, in Engels's account, living at a level of material civilisation that made him the perfect competitor to drive English wages downward. [[james-kay-shuttleworth|Dr. Kay]]'s phrase, which Engels endorses, captures the mechanism: the Irish "discovered the minimum of the necessities of life, and are now making the English workers acquainted with it." (004-002)

## Economic Competition and Wage Depression

The labour market mechanism Engels describes is straightforward. The Irish compete successfully in all branches "which demand little or no skill": hand-weavers, [[brick|bricklayers]], porters, jobbers, and similar occupations. For work requiring long training or steady application — the work of the mechanic or skilled mill-hand — Engels acknowledges the Irishman is "on too low a plane," and would have to "adopt the English civilisation." But for simple work dependent on strength rather than skill, the Irishman is as good as the Englishman, and will accept lower wages. The consequence, Engels argues, is that "the wages of English workingmen should be forced down further and further in every branch in which the Irish compete with him." This downward pressure is not confined to the Irish-occupied trades; it radiates outward, as the displaced English workers seek employment in adjacent branches. (004-002)

This competition also had a spatial dimension. Engels notes that wherever a district is "distinguished for especial filth and especial ruinousness, the explorer may safely count upon meeting chiefly those Celtic faces." The Irish concentrated in the worst quarters of every industrial city, and their presence — a fifth or a quarter of the working population in many [[urban|great town]]s — meant that "the life, habits, intelligence, moral status — in short, the whole character of the working-class assimilates a great part of the Irish characteristics." (004-002)

## Historical Context: Carlyle and the Irish Question

Engels engages directly with Thomas [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s celebrated polemic on the Irish, quoting at length from *[[chartism|Chartism]]* (1839). [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] had described the Irish immigrant as "the sorest evil this country has to strive with," a figure whose mere presence drove down the Saxon worker's condition: "The uncivilised Irishman, not by his strength, but by the opposite of strength, drives the Saxon native out, takes possession in his room." Engels, while noting his disagreement with [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]]'s "exaggerated and one-sided condemnation of the Irish national character," declares [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] "perfectly right" in his economic analysis. What [[thomas-carlyle|Carlyle]] saw as a racial tragedy, Engels reframes as a structural feature of capitalist labour markets: the Irish immigrant is not morally culpable but is a product of colonial conditions who becomes an instrument of wage depression. (004-001, 004-002)

## The Dialectical Reversal

> Another influence of great moment in forming the character of the English workers is the Irish immigration already referred to. On the one hand it has, as we have seen, degraded the English workers, removed them from civilisation, and aggravated the hardship of their lot; but, on the other hand, it has thereby deepened the chasm between workers and bourgeoisie, and hastened the approaching crisis. (005-033)

Engels's analysis culminates in a characteristic dialectical reversal. The very degradation inflicted by Irish competition sharpens the class antagonism and accelerates the revolutionary crisis. Moreover, the Irish temperament — "passionate, mercurial," "generous to a fault, and ruled primarily by sentiment" — when mixed with the "stable, reasoning, persevering English" character, produces, Engels argues, a more combustible and politically advanced [[proletariat|working class]]. "The rough egotism of the English bourgeoisie would have kept its hold upon the working-class much more firmly if the Irish nature... had not intervened." The Irish immigration, by making conditions more intolerable, hastens the moment when they become intolerable no longer. (005-033)
