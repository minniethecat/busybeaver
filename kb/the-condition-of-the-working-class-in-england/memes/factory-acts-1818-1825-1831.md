## Overview

The [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] of 1818, 1825, and 1831 formed the pre-1833 regulatory framework for [[factory-system|factory]] labour in England. They built on the [[apprentices-act-1802|Apprentices' Act of 1802]] and represented incremental attempts — largely unsuccessful — to impose legal limits on the exploitation of workers, particularly children and young persons, in the cotton textile industry.

Each successive Act expanded its scope slightly. The 1818 Act (58 Geo. III c. 87) applied to cotton mills only. The 1825 Act (6 Geo. IV c. 63) tightened some provisions. The 1831 Act (1 & 2 Will. IV c. 39), introduced by Sir J.C. Hobhouse, was the most ambitious of the three: it prohibited night work for anyone under twenty-one in cotton mills and limited young persons under eighteen to a twelve-hour day and nine hours on Saturday.

## Engels's Account

Engels's assessment of these early [[factory-act|Factory Acts]] is blunt: they were largely paper laws, honoured in the breach by manufacturers and unenforced by the state:

> "The late Sir Robert Peel and other philanthropists united with him, and gradually secured the Factory Acts of 1818, 1825, and 1831, of which the first two were never enforced, and the last only here and there." [006-045]

This pattern — legislation enacted but not enforced — is a recurring theme in Engels's narrative. The gap between the law on the books and the law in practice is for him evidence that the bourgeoisie's legislative concessions to the [[proletariat|working class]] were never sincerely intended.

## The 1831 Act (Hobhouse's Act)

The 1831 Act deserves particular attention as the immediate predecessor to the [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act of 1833]]. Engels acknowledges that it went further than earlier attempts:

> "This law of 1831, based upon the motion of Sir J.C. Hobhouse, provided that in cotton mills no one under twenty-one should be employed between half-past seven at night and half-past five in the morning; and that in all factories young persons under eighteen should work no longer than twelve hours daily, and nine hours on Saturday." [006-045]

However, the Act contained a fatal weakness: workers who testified against their employers faced dismissal. As Engels observes, "since operatives could not testify against their masters without being discharged, this law helped matters very little" [006-045]. In the larger towns, manufacturers reached informal agreements to comply; in the [[rural|countryside]], they simply ignored the law.

## The Road to 1833

The failure of the 1831 Act set the stage for the more comprehensive [[factory-act-1833|Factory Act of 1833]], which introduced the first system of government [[factory-system|factory]] inspectors. Engels documents how the Ten Hours' movement — driven by [[michael-sadler|Michael Sadler]] and the [[ten-hours-bill|Trades Unions]] — gathered momentum precisely because the earlier Acts had proven ineffectual. The [[michael-sadler|Sadler]] Committee of 1832 and the subsequent [[parliamentary-commission-1833|Factory Commission of 1833]] were direct responses to the enforcement vacuum Engels describes.
