# Henrik Ibsen

Henrik Ibsen appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* both as a named playwright and, more significantly, as the source of a governing metaphor for the entire argument. Shaw was a committed Ibsenite — his 1891 *The Quintessence of Ibsenism* was instrumental in introducing Ibsen's social problem plays to English audiences — and *Getting Married* continues this advocacy.

## Ibsen as Cultural Reference

Shaw invokes Ibsen in a passage of sustained cultural contempt, listing the great European artists whom the English middle class instinctively dismisses: "What they called patriotism was a conviction that because they were born in Tooting or Camberwell, they were the natural superiors of Beethoven, of Rodin, of Ibsen, of Tolstoy and all other benighted foreigners" [[006-001]].

The passage is characteristic Shaw: the English are not malign but ignorant, and their ignorance has real consequences. A population that dismisses Ibsen as a "benighted foreigner" is a population incapable of the self-examination that marriage reform requires.

## The Chain Stitch Metaphor

Shaw's most important use of Ibsen is the "chain stitch" metaphor that closes the section on the marriage reform problem. Marriage, Shaw argues, "threatens to come to pieces in all directions if a single thread of it be cut. Ibsen's similitude of the machine-made chain stitch, which unravels the whole seam at the first pull when a single stitch is ripped, is very applicable to the knot of marriage" [[015-001]].

The chain stitch metaphor explains why marriage reform is both necessary and terrifying. Every proposed change — easier divorce, economic independence for women, legal recognition of unmarried partnerships — threatens not just one aspect of marriage but the entire interlocking system. The chain stitch is Shaw's image for the institutional interconnectedness that makes piecemeal reform impossible and comprehensive reform politically unthinkable.

## Ibsen's Dramatic Method

The chain stitch metaphor also captures Ibsen's dramatic method — his preference for the "retrospective" play in which past secrets unravel the present. *A Doll's House* (1879) and *Ghosts* (1881) both operate on this principle: one revelation pulls the thread, and the entire domestic fabric comes apart.

Shaw inherits this method. *Getting Married*, though less naturalistic than Ibsen, similarly stages a domestic occasion (a wedding) that unravels into a comprehensive critique of the institution it is supposed to celebrate.
