# Christianity

The religion whose historical relationship with marriage Shaw analyzes as a series of inversions — from an initial "fierce attack on marriage" [005-003] through the excommunication of sex to the sanctification of matrimony as a holy sacrament. Shaw's critique is not anti-Christian but anti-clerical: he argues that Christianity's institutional compromises on marriage have produced a legacy of confusion that continues to deform English law and social practice.

## Shaw 的定义与用法

Shaw treats Christianity not as a unified theological system but as a historical movement whose positions on marriage shifted radically in response to political necessity. "Christianity began with a fierce attack on marriage; and to this day the celibacy of the Roman Catholic priesthood is a standing protest against its compatibility with the higher life" [005-003]. The early Christian preference for celibacy — embodied in "St. Paul's reluctant sanction of marriage" and "his contemptuous 'better to marry than to burn'" [005-003] — established a theological framework in which marriage was a concession to weakness rather than a positive good.

This initial suspicion of marriage was not sustainable as a social institution. Shaw traces the church's "complete right-about-face": "the Christian Church refused to have anything to do with marriage. The result was, not the abolition of sex, but its excommunication. And, of course, the consequences of persuading people that matrimony was an unholy state were so grossly carnal, that the Church had to execute a complete right-about-face, and try to make people understand that it was a holy state" [042-002]. The result, in Shaw's analysis, is theological incoherence — a sacrament that began as a sin.

## 在婚姻辩论中的位置

Christianity's importance to Shaw's argument is both historical and polemical. Historically, the Church of England's jurisdiction over marriage (through ecclesiastical courts until 1857, and through cultural authority thereafter) meant that Christian doctrine about marriage directly shaped the legal framework Shaw critiques. The Matrimonial Causes Act 1857 transferred jurisdiction to civil courts, but the moral assumptions embedded in the law remained Christian in origin.

Shaw's strategy is not to argue against Christianity but to show that Christianity itself — properly understood — is on his side. The early church's hostility to marriage, the Catholic priesthood's celibacy, and St. Paul's grudging tolerance all demonstrate that "the religious revolt against marriage is a very old one" [005-003]. Shaw positions himself not as an enemy of Christian morality but as its more consistent exponent: if Christianity truly values the "higher life" over carnal concerns, then the church should support, not oppose, reforms that make marriage less of a compulsory institution.

## 历史 / 社会语境

In 1908, the Church of England remained the established church with significant political influence, though its authority was under pressure from secularization, Nonconformist Protestant dissent, and the growing political power of Roman Catholicism (reinforced by Irish immigration). The ecclesiastical courts had lost their matrimonial jurisdiction only half a century earlier, and the cultural authority of the church on questions of sexual morality was still substantial.

Shaw's reference to "indissolubleoluble soluble Catholic marriage" (a characteristically Shavian play on words) and his discussion of the Deceased Wife's Sister Act (prohibiting a widower from marrying his deceased wife's sister) highlight the concrete legal consequences of Christian moral doctrine. By 1908, the Deceased Wife's Sister Marriage Act had passed (1907), but the controversy it generated exemplified the entanglement of theology and legislation that Shaw sought to untangle.

## Shaw 的批评与替代

Shaw's critique of Christianity's role in marriage law operates on three levels:

1. **Historical inconsistency**: The church's reversal from condemning marriage to sacralizing it reveals that doctrine follows social necessity, not divine revelation. "Christianity never got any grip of the world until it virtually reduced its standard" [010-002] — institutional survival required compromise.

2. **Theological confusion**: The doctrine that "sex, with all its operations, is in itself absolutely an obscene thing" [042-001] is, in Shaw's view, "a blasphemy against life" and "an accusation of indecency against God." A theology that treats procreation as sinful while simultaneously sanctifying the institution that enables it cannot produce coherent law.

3. **Practical harm**: The church's "excommunication" of sex from respectable discourse prevents rational discussion of marriage reform. Shaw lists "Christianity" alongside "respectability, morality, anti-socialism" as forces that inhibit clear thinking about marriage [003-001].

Shaw's alternative is not the abolition of religion but the separation of theological doctrine from civil law. The state should regulate marriage as a civil contract, leaving religious communities to impose whatever additional constraints they choose on their own members — a position consistent with his broader Fabian commitment to institutional rationalization.
