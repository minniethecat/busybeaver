# Church of England

The Church of England appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* not merely as a religious institution but as the doctrinal and legal custodian of marriage — and therefore as an obstacle to its reform. Shaw's treatment is characteristically irreverent: he traces the Church's marriage doctrine to political compromise rather than theological conviction.

## Historical Formation

Shaw locates the Church of England's marriage doctrine in the sequence of Tudor-Stuart religious settlements. The English people, he observes, "accepted the Reformation under Henry and Edward, the Restoration under Mary, and, after Mary's death, the shandygaff which Elizabeth compounded from both doctrines and called the Articles of the Church of England" [[004-003]].

The word "shandygaff" — a mixed drink of beer and ginger beer — is chosen deliberately. Shaw means that the Elizabethan settlement was not a coherent theological position but a political cocktail, blending Protestant and Catholic elements to keep the peace. The marriage doctrine inherited from this compromise is correspondingly muddled.

## As Obstacle to Reform

The Church, in Shaw's analysis, has been reduced to irrelevance by its own democratic instincts. "The result of trying to make the Church of England reflect the notions of the average churchgoer has reduced it to a cipher except for the purposes of a petulantly irreligious social and political club" [[014-001]].

This is a double indictment: the Church is ineffective because it follows rather than leads public opinion, and what leadership it does offer is confined to "petulantly irreligious" club politics — not moral or spiritual guidance on questions like marriage reform.

## In the Play

The Church appears in the play's dialogue through the character of the Bishop. Mrs George, discussing the Bishop's wife's religious sensibilities, notes that she "habitually spoke of the Church of England as The Scarlet Woman" [[049-1777]].

The Scarlet Woman is the Whore of Babylon from the Book of Revelation — a striking phrase for a bishop's wife to use about her own husband's Church. Shaw uses it to underscore the internal contradictions within the Church's own clerical households: even its most invested members cannot take its authority seriously.

## Role in the Marriage System

The Church of England's specific role in the marriage system is as officiant and moral guarantor of a legal institution Shaw considers unjust. The clergy perform marriages as agents of the state, lending religious sanction to a system that Shaw describes as "commercial sex slavery" [[043-002]]. Shaw notes that "some of our bishops imagine that in the phrase 'Whom God hath joined,' the word God means the district registrar or the Reverend John Smith" [[045-003]] — confusing divine authority with bureaucratic function.
