# Nature

In *Getting Married*, Bernard Shaw systematically attacks the rhetorical use of **Nature** and **the natural** to justify existing marriage arrangements. The play and its preface argue that what is called "natural" in marriage is in fact conventional — and that genuine natural impulses are often suppressed by the very institutions that claim Nature's authority.

## Nature vs. Convention

Shaw's preface draws a sharp distinction between Nature and social convention. Marriage "as we understand it is no more natural to us than a cage is natural to a bird" ([007-002]). This metaphor reframes the entire debate: if marriage is an artificial construct, then reforming it is not an attack on Nature but a question of rational design.

The Bishop echoes this theme in the play when he observes wryly that "Nature abhors a vacuum, Anthony" ([049-2799]) — a scientific truism deployed to puncture a social pretension. Throughout the drama, appeals to what is "natural" are exposed as defenses of custom rather than biological imperatives.

## Natural Appetites and Social Control

The play stages a recurring tension between natural impulse and social regulation. The General's outburst — "But, great Heavens, the natural appetites—" ([049-346]) — is cut short, the sentence unfinished, illustrating the Victorian inability to discuss sex openly even while claiming Nature's authority.

Shaw's preface argues that "the natural relation between husband and wife and parent and child" is in fact "one of property" ([032-001]), not one of affection. What the law calls natural — the husband's ownership of his wife's person and labor — is exposed as a legal fiction dressed in biological rhetoric.

## The Natural in the Play's Characters

The characters' relationship to Nature reveals their social positions. **Reginald Bridgenorth** declares that "marriage came natural to me" ([049-106]), presenting his conventionality as biological destiny. His brother's servant **Collins** offers the counterpoint: "it didnt come natural" to him — his wife "had to break me into it" ([049-108–109]). The contrast between Reginald's unreflective acceptance and Collins's candid admission exposes "the natural" as a matter of temperament and social conditioning, not universal law.

## Domestic "Natural" Affection

Shaw devotes a preface section to what he calls "Spurious 'Natural' Affection" ([349–349]), arguing that the forced proximity of family life produces not genuine love but its simulation. Children raised "in a hothouse atmosphere of unnatural affection" ([032-002]) may be emotionally crippled by the very intensity that the ideology of the natural family demands.

This critique extends to motherhood: the preface challenges the assumption that all women have "a natural turn for mothering and coddling" children ([021-001]). Shaw treats this as an empirical question, not a metaphysical certainty — and argues that the law should reflect actual diversity rather than idealized Nature.

> Shaw's method is to take the Victorian claim that marriage is "natural" and reverse it: if Nature is the standard, then the existing marriage system fails by its own criterion. Genuine natural impulses — sexual desire, maternal affection, the drive for autonomy — are precisely what marriage law, in Shaw's account, most effectively suppresses.

> "It's not natural: it's not right: it's—" ([049-265]). The character's equation of the unnatural with the wrong, and the unfinished sentence that follows, captures Shaw's diagnosis: the appeal to Nature conceals an inability to articulate a genuine moral argument.
