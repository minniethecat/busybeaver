# Othello

Shakespeare's *Othello* (c. 1603) receives sustained attention in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*. Shaw devotes an entire section — titled "Othello and Desdemona" — to analyzing the play as an illustration of the proprietary psychology that underlies conventional marriage.

## The Play in Brief

*Othello* is Shakespeare's tragedy of a Moorish general in the Venetian army who is driven by the machinations of his ensign Iago to murder his wife Desdemona, believing her unfaithful. The play is Shakespeare's most concentrated study of sexual jealousy and the destruction it wreaks.

## Shaw's Analysis

Shaw's reading of *Othello* is characteristically unsentimental. He treats the play not as a study of tragic love but as a document of the proprietary instinct in marriage. Othello, Shaw argues, does not suffer from wounded love but from wounded ownership.

Shaw groups Othello with Shakespeare's Antony as an example of how "his most infatuated and passionate lovers... both of them betray the commercial and proprietary instinct the moment they lose their tempers" [[004-004]]. Othello's famous agony — the thought of "keeping a corner in the thing he loves for others' uses" — is, Shaw insists, "not what a man feels about the thing he loves, but about the thing he owns" [[004-004]].

## The Toothbrush Analogy

Shaw sharpens his point with a memorable anecdote. He recounts hearing a lady, in a discussion of "group marriage," say "with cold disgust that she would as soon think of lending her toothbrush to another woman as her husband" [[004-004]].

The analogy transforms Othello's tragedy into domestic comedy — and in doing so, exposes the logic that underlies both. Othello's murderous jealousy and the lady's fastidious possessiveness operate on the same principle: the spouse as personal property, like a toothbrush, not to be shared. "The sense of outraged manhood with which I felt myself and all other husbands thus reduced to the rank of a toilet appliance gave me a very unpleasant taste of what Desdemona might have felt had she overheard Othello's outburst" [[004-004]].

## Critical Significance

Shaw's use of *Othello* is characteristic of his method throughout the preface: he takes a cultural monument — the most famous literary treatment of marital jealousy — and reads it against the grain to expose assumptions the culture prefers not to examine. Othello is not a cautionary tale about the dangers of jealousy, in Shaw's reading; it is a documentary record of what men feel when they treat women as property.

Shaw's argument implies that Othello's tragedy is not individual pathology but cultural logic taken to its extreme. If marriage law is founded on the wife-as-property principle, then Othello's violence is the principle's logical conclusion — not its aberration.

## Key Passages

> "His most infatuated and passionate lovers are Antony and Othello; yet both of them betray the commercial and proprietary instinct the moment they lose their tempers" [[004-004]].

> "Othello's worst agony is the thought of 'keeping a corner in the thing he loves for others' uses.' But this is not what a man feels about the thing he loves, but about the thing he owns" [[004-004]].

> "I never understood the full significance of Othello's outburst until I one day heard a lady... say with cold disgust that she would as soon think of lending her toothbrush to another woman as her husband" [[004-004]].
