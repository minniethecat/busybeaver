# The Protestant Reformation

The Protestant Reformation of the sixteenth century serves as a recurring historical reference point in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*. Shaw invokes the Reformation not to praise its theological achievements but to explain the peculiar legal and doctrinal inheritance that still shapes English marriage law in the early twentieth century.

## Shaw's Analysis

In the preface, Shaw traces how the Reformation transformed marriage without fully liberating it from ecclesiastical control. The Reformers' rebellion against sacerdotalism — the priestly mediation of divine grace — was so radical that "marriage was very nearly excommunicated again" [[043-002]]. The modern institution of civil marriage, which Shaw describes as the site of "fierce controversies and political conflicts," would have been "thoroughly approved of by Calvin, and hailed with relief by Luther" [[043-002]].

Yet the Reformation did not produce a clean break. Shaw argues that the "instinctive doctrine that there is something holy and mystic in sex" survived the doctrinal upheaval, requiring "a ritual affirmation" that could not simply be "thrown on the scrap-heap with the sale of Indulgences and the like" [[043-002]].

## Historical Context in the Preface

Shaw places the Reformation within a longer historical arc of English religious settlement. He describes how the English people passively accepted successive religious regimes — "the Reformation under Henry and Edward, the Restoration under Mary, and, after Mary's death, the shandygaff which Elizabeth compounded from both doctrines and called the Articles of the Church of England" [[004-003]].

This historical passivity, Shaw suggests, explains why marriage law remained unreformed: "If matters were left to these simple folk, there would never be any changes at all" [[004-003]]. The Reformation thus becomes an example of how institutional change can occur without corresponding changes in popular consciousness — and without addressing the fundamental injustice Shaw identifies in existing marriage law.

## The Mixed Legacy

Shaw's conclusion is unsparing: the Reformation "left marriage where it was: a curious mixture of commercial sex slavery, early Christian sex abhorrence, and later Christian sex sanctification" [[043-002]]. By this he means that post-Reformation marriage retained the economic subordination of women (the commercial element), the early Church's distrust of sexuality (abhorrence), and the later medieval elevation of marriage to a holy estate (sanctification) — three contradictory elements that together make honest reform impossible.

The Reformation thus stands in Shaw's argument as a missed opportunity: a moment when marriage might have been reconceived on rational, human grounds, but which instead preserved its worst features under new management.

## Key Passages

> "When the great protest of the XVI century came, and the Church was reformed in several countries, the Reformation was so largely a rebellion against sacerdotalism that marriage was very nearly excommunicated again" [[043-002]].
