# Deceased Wife's Sister Act 1907

The Deceased Wife's Sister Act of 1907 is one of the few specific pieces of legislation that Bernard Shaw names in his preface to *Getting Married*. He uses it to illustrate the political cowardice that he believes prevents genuine marriage law reform in Britain.

## The Act in Shaw's Argument

Shaw introduces the Act in a passage diagnosing the paralysis of British government on the marriage question. He argues that no government "will touch the marriage question if it can possibly help it, even when there is a demand for the extension of marriage, as in the case of the recent long-delayed Act legalizing marriage with a deceased wife's sister" [[012-001]].

The key word is "long-delayed." The Act had been debated in Parliament for decades — since the 1830s — before finally passing in 1907. For Shaw, this glacial pace of reform is not an anomaly but the norm: the British state will only act when public pressure becomes overwhelming, and even then only to extend marriage rather than to liberalize divorce or address the deeper injustices of the marriage law.

## The Play's Treatment

The Act is also referenced within the play itself. The General, a character who embodies conventional military and social attitudes, recalls being consulted by the Government about the proposal: "But when the Government sounded you as to whether you'd marry a man to his deceased wife's sister you very naturally and properly told them you'd see them damned first" [[049-1482]].

This line exposes the gap between the political class's public posture (opposing reform to appease conventional opinion) and the actual outcome (the Act passing anyway). The General's reflexive opposition, presented as natural and proper, is precisely the attitude Shaw wants his audience to question.

Hotchkiss mockingly invokes the Act later in the play: "Deceased Wife's Sister Act? That did not prevent its becoming law" [[049-1479]]. The sarcasm underscores Shaw's point: reforms happen despite — not because of — the conventional wisdom.

## Significance for Shaw's Argument

Shaw places the Act within a broader critique: that marriage law reform in Britain proceeds only when it extends marriage (allowing more people to marry) rather than when it liberalizes exit from marriage (expanding divorce). "When a reform in the other direction is needed (for example, an extension of divorce), not even the existence of the most unbearable hardships will induce our statesmen to move" [[012-001]].

The Deceased Wife's Sister Act thus serves as Shaw's Exhibit A for a pattern: Parliament will reluctantly permit more marriage, but it will not touch the conditions that make existing marriages unbearable.
