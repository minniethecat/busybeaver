# Polygamy

The general term for marriage systems in which an individual has more than one spouse — encompassing both polygyny (one man, multiple wives) and polyandry (one woman, multiple husbands). In *Getting Married*, Shaw uses polygamy as a conceptual wedge to challenge the moral foundations of state-regulated monogamy, arguing that spousal numbers are determined by demographics and economics, not ethics.

## Shaw 的定义与用法

Shaw approaches polygamy through the lens of practical necessity rather than moral judgment. In the preface, he argues that "the number of wives permitted to a single husband or of husbands to a single wife under a marriage system, is not an ethical problem: it depends solely on the proportion of the sexes in the population" [018-001]. This framing is deliberately provocative in an Edwardian context where polygamy was regarded as a mark of civilizational backwardness.

He distinguishes between the sentiment of monogamy — the genuine emotional attachment between two people — and its political enforcement. "Monogamy has a sentimental basis which is quite distinct from the political one of equal numbers of the sexes" [036-001]. The emotional case for exclusive partnership does not, in Shaw's argument, translate into a justification for legally prohibiting other arrangements.

## 在婚姻辩论中的位置

Polygamy occupies a pivotal place in Shaw's critique of marriage law. He deploys it to illustrate three structural flaws in the monogamy-as-moral-imperative position:

1. **Legal arbitrariness**: If polygyny is tolerated or practiced de facto (through serial marriages, mistresses, and double households), the categorical prohibition of polyandry reveals that the law polices female sexuality more strictly than male conduct.

2. **Economic dependence**: Shaw observes that polygamy in societies where "the women are the breadwinners" is economically rational for men, whereas in Western capitalism "women are not paid for their social work" and are "quartered on the wages of their husbands" [018-001]. The economic structure, not moral reasoning, determines which marriage form prevails.

3. **Population logic**: In the dramatic dialogue, characters discuss polygamy in terms of practical outcomes. The line "when it comes to flat polygamy and polyandry, we ought" [049-693] captures Shaw's refusal to treat the topic as inherently scandalous — it is, in his framework, a matter for rational assessment.

## 历史 / 社会语境

In 1908 Britain, polygamy was legally prohibited (bigamy was a criminal offence under the 1861 Act), but the imperial context complicated matters. The British Empire governed populations where polygyny was practiced under Islamic and customary law — Shaw's reference to "unlimited Kulin polygamy" and "Muslim polygamy limited to four" [003-001] acknowledges this imperial reality. The colonial administration's selective tolerance of polygamy for non-Christian subjects exposed the religious, rather than rational, basis of the prohibition.

Shaw also engages the anthropological literature accessible to his educated audience, referencing Kaffir (Xhosa) marriage practices as an example of polygyny functioning within a coherent economic system — in contrast to the economic incoherence of English marriage, where wives were financially dependent on husbands without compensation for domestic and reproductive labor.

## Shaw 的批评与替代

Shaw's critique operates on two fronts:

1. **Against moral condemnation of polygamy**: "No law, however stringent, can prevent polygamy among groups of people who choose to live loosely and be monogamous only in appearance" [036-003]. The law cannot enforce monogamy; it can only punish those whose arrangements are visible enough to prosecute.

2. **Against the economic structure that makes polygamy impossible for most**: Even if polygyny were legal, "at least four out of five of our men could not afford two wives unless their wages were nearly doubled" [018-001]. The economic argument works in both directions: the existing wage structure enforces monogamy for the poor while the wealthy maintain de facto polygamous arrangements.

Shaw's proposed alternative is characteristically institutional: rather than regulating the number of spouses through criminal law, the state should separate the contractual, economic, and reproductive functions of marriage into distinct institutions — a system in which polygamy (in either form) would be a matter of private contract, not public morality.
