# Slavery

Bernard Shaw deploys the language of slavery throughout *Getting Married* as his most provocative analogy for marriage. He argues that a marriage from which one cannot freely exit is not a partnership but a form of ownership — "a commercial contract of property and slavery" disguised by religious ceremony [[011-001]]. The comparison is intentional, polemical, and designed to shock readers out of complacent acceptance.

## Marriage as Slavery

Shaw's argument is structurally simple: if a person cannot leave a relationship without legal penalty, social ruin, or economic destitution, then they are not a free participant but a coerced one. "It is simple slavery to compel them to remain together" when one party wants to leave [[039-002]]. The absence of a practical right of exit transforms marriage from contract to bondage.

He extends the analysis to women's economic position: "the sex slavery involved has become complicated" by the pretense of free choice, but the underlying reality is unchanged [[027-001]]. A woman who marries because she has no other means of survival has not consented freely — she has been economically coerced.

## Historical and Literary Context

Shaw invokes Mary Wollstonecraft, noting that "women who loathe sex slavery more fiercely than Mary Wollstonecraft" continue to enter marriage because the alternatives are worse [[004-003]]. He also contrasts modern marriage with chattel slavery, arguing that the difference is one of degree, not kind: "maintain marriage as a slavery" by refusing to allow dissolution [[040-001]].

## The "Sex Slavery" Charge

The phrase "sex slavery" is Shaw at his most incendiary. He means it literally: a system in which one party (typically the woman) provides sexual access, domestic labor, and reproductive capacity in exchange for economic support, with no practical right of exit. That this arrangement is sanctified by religion and celebrated by sentiment does not, in Shaw's view, change its essential character.

## Shaw's Solution

The remedy is structural: "make divorce as easy, as cheap, and as private as marriage." Only when exit is genuinely available does the slavery analogy lose its force. A marriage entered freely and terminable freely is a contract; a marriage that cannot be left is bondage, regardless of how affectionately it began.

## Key Passages

> "It is simple slavery to compel them to remain together" [[039-002]].
