# Contract

Bernard Shaw's reform proposal centers on a single conceptual shift: marriage should be treated as a civil contract rather than a religious sacrament. "There is some sense in a contract to perform or abstain from actions that are reasonably within our control," he argues, "but a contract to love or to remain married is not such a contract" [[025-001]]. This reframing is the key to his entire reform program.

## The Logical Problem with Marriage Contracts

Shaw identifies a fundamental incoherence in the marriage contract. "A contract for better for worse is a contract that should not be tolerated" because it obliges the parties to endure conditions that were not contemplated at signing [[003-002]]. No rational person would sign a business contract with such terms; yet marriage demands exactly this.

The problem is that marriage contracts to an emotional state — love, fidelity, mutual affection — that cannot be contracted for. "There is no sense in a contract to feel or not to feel" [[025-001]]. A contract that cannot be enforced on its own terms is not a contract at all but a pretense.

## Civil Contract as Reform

Shaw's solution is to make marriage a genuine civil contract: "distinction between one civil contract and another" should be minimized [[049-1466]]. A marriage contract should specify the practical terms — property arrangements, parental obligations, duration — and provide for orderly dissolution when those terms cannot be met.

He envisions "contracts at a registry office" replacing religious ceremonies, with "governments refusing to make these contracts reasonable" being the only obstacle [[049-1471]].

## Private Ordering

Shaw also acknowledges that private ordering already circumvents the formal system: "settlements and private contracts of various kinds" effectively govern the economic terms of marriage among the wealthy [[022-001]]. The problem is that the poor have no access to these private arrangements and are governed by the default rules — which are designed for property protection, not human welfare.

## The Limits of Contract

Shaw is not naive about contract's limitations. Reforming the legal form of marriage will not, by itself, change the underlying economic and social conditions that coerce people into disadvantageous arrangements. But making the contract honest is a necessary first step toward making the institution just.

## Key Passages

> "A contract for better for worse is a contract that should not be tolerated" [[003-002]].
