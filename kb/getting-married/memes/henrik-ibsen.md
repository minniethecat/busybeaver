# Henrik Ibsen

Henrik Ibsen (1828–1906), the Norwegian playwright often regarded as the father of modern drama, appears twice in Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*: once as a name in a list of cultural superiors dismissed by English provincialism, and once as the source of the "chain stitch" metaphor that Shaw deploys to capture the institutional fragility of marriage. Shaw, himself an Ibsenite and author of *The Quintessence of Ibsenism* (1891), draws on Ibsen's dramatic method and imagery throughout his argument.

## Ibsen in the Preface

### The Provincialism of English Respectability

Shaw uses Ibsen's name as a synecdoche for European cultural achievement against which English middle-class complacency defines itself:

> What they called patriotism was a conviction that because they were born in Tooting or Camberwell, they were the natural superiors of Beethoven, of Rodin, of Ibsen, of Tolstoy and all other benighted foreigners [015-001].

The enumeration — Beethoven, Rodin, Ibsen, Tolstoy — spans music, visual art, drama, and literature. That Shaw places Ibsen in this company signals his esteem: Ibsen is not merely a playwright but a cultural force whose moral seriousness exposes the vacuity of English philistinism.

### The Chain Stitch Metaphor

In the section on the practical difficulties of marriage reform, Shaw confronts the statesman's reluctance to tamper with an institution that, however flawed, seems to hold the entire social fabric together:

> Meanwhile, note how much reason a statesman has to pause before meddling with an institution which, unendurable as its drawbacks are, threatens to come to pieces in all directions if a single thread of it be cut. Ibsen's similitude of the machine-made chain stitch, which unravels the whole seam at the first pull when a single stitch is ripped, is very applicable to the knot of marriage [027-001].

The chain stitch image — drawn from Ibsen's social dramas — captures the paradox of institutional reform: a system so brittle that pulling one loose end destroys the whole garment may be a system that *should* be replaced, yet the very fact of its brittleness deters reformers. Shaw uses Ibsen's metaphor not to counsel caution but to diagnose the institutional cowardice that prevents necessary change.

## Relationship to Shaw's Marriage Argument

Ibsen functions in Shaw's rhetoric as both a cultural authority and a methodological model. The chain stitch metaphor is quintessentially Ibsenite: it takes a domestic, material image and expands it into a structural analysis of social institutions. Shaw's entire preface operates in this Ibsenite mode — moving from the concrete particular (a bad marriage, a conference of respectable men, a household of three) to the structural universal.
