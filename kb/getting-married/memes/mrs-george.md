# Mrs George Collins

Mrs George Collins is the most socially mobile character in Shaw's *Getting Married* — a working-class woman who "gets round" everyone and whose practical wisdom about marriage exposes the limitations of upper-class moralizing. She is the wife of the greengrocer Collins (the Bishop's factotum) and functions as the play's democratic conscience.

## Character Background

Mrs George is married to Collins, who serves the Bishop's household. She works as a mayoress and has "come to know a lot about" the domestic lives of the community through her public role [[049-171]]. Her social position — neither servant nor gentlewoman — allows her to move between classes with unusual freedom. Collins describes her as someone who "gets round" everyone, remarking that "Mrs George can get round anybody if she wants to" [[049-185]].

## Role in the Play

Mrs George serves as intermediary, advisor, and truth-teller. She speaks plainly where the gentry speak euphemistically. When Lesbia asks pointedly "About Mrs George?" the question signals that Mrs George herself has become a subject of upper-class scrutiny [[049-216]]. Her famous declaration "I'm not your property, young man" encapsulates Shaw's feminist argument in everyday language [[049-2552]].

## Key Passages

> "Mrs George can get round anybody if she wants to" — Collins on his wife [[049-185]].

> "We always thought that Mrs George was too good to be true" — the Bishop [[049-1681]].

> "I'm not your property, young man" — Mrs George claims her autonomy [[049-2552]].
