# Charles Dickens

Bernard Shaw invokes **Charles Dickens** twice in the preface to *Getting Married*, each time as an authority on social reality. Dickens serves Shaw as the novelist who most convincingly captured the texture of English life — including the domestic misery that marriage law both creates and conceals.

## Dickens and Social Reality

Shaw calls Dickens "the most convincingly real of all the nineteenth-century novelists" ([007-001]), praising his ability to render the actual conditions of English life rather than idealized versions of it. This is high praise from Shaw, who was himself a social realist in the theater and who shared Dickens's conviction that art should illuminate social conditions.

The specific reference is to Dickens's portrayal of domestic unhappiness: the "idealizations of home-bred womanhood" that Shaw attacks in the preface find their antidote, he suggests, in Dickens's unflinching depictions of family life.

## The Boy Dickens

Shaw's second reference is to "the boy Dickens and the blacking factory" ([007-002]) — the traumatic childhood experience that shaped Dickens's understanding of social inequality. Shaw uses this biographical detail to argue that early experience of harsh reality produces sharper social insight than sheltered upbringing.

This reference serves Shaw's larger argument: that the "natural" affection of family life is often a construct, and that those who have been excluded from it — like the young Dickens — may see marriage and family more clearly than those comfortably inside them.

## Dickens and Shaw

The affinity between Dickens and Shaw runs deeper than these two references suggest. Both writers used comedy to advance social criticism; both were fascinated by institutions and their failures; both believed that law could and should be reformed by rational argument. Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* can be read as a Dickensian social pamphlet updated for the Edwardian stage.
