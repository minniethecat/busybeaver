# Equality

Bernard Shaw's egalitarianism runs through *Getting Married* like a watermark. He measures every institution — marriage law, divorce procedure, property rights, parental obligation — against the standard of equality between the sexes and between social classes. The existing system fails this test at every point.

## Equality Between the Sexes

Shaw's most sustained application of the equality principle is to the differential treatment of husbands and wives. The law allows a husband to divorce his wife for adultery but not vice versa on equal terms; it treats the wife's property as her husband's; it assumes, "as no decent person ever does, its fundamental assumption that there can be no companionship between men and women because the woman has a 'sphere' of her own."

All of this violates what Shaw calls "the quintessential equality of all" — a deeper equality that precedes and underlies social distinctions [[049-3240]].

## Equality and Class

Shaw is equally attentive to class inequality in the operation of marriage law. The wealthy can afford private settlements that effectively customize their marriage terms; the poor are governed by default rules designed for property protection. Divorce is "as easy, as cheap, and as private as marriage" only for those who can pay for it.

## Equality as Rhetorical Principle

Shaw's character General Bridgenorth invokes "the sacred equality" as something violated by the existing system [[049-1092]]. Another character retorts that "that sentiment is not your equality, General, but your dignity" — distinguishing genuine equality from male pride [[049-1096]]. This exchange captures Shaw's method: he uses dramatic dialogue to test and refine his concepts, exposing the self-interest that often masquerades as principle.

## Key Passages

> "The quintessential equality of all" — deeper than social distinction [[049-3240]].

> "That sentiment is not your equality, General, but your dignity" [[049-1096]].
