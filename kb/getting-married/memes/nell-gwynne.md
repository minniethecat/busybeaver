# Nell Gwynne

Nell Gwynne (1650–1687), the Restoration actress who became the long-term mistress of King Charles II, appears in Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* as a cultural touchstone — a figure whose popular reputation reveals, by inversion, the actual moral structure of English society. Shaw invokes her not for her biography but for what the collective imagination has done with it.

## Shaw's Analysis of Literary Tradition

In the chapter "Hearth and Home," Shaw examines how the excessive segregation and sentimentality of domestic life produces a "revolt, driven under ground and exacerbated" [007-002] that reshapes literature itself:

> Literary traditions spring up in which the libertine and profligate — Tom Jones and Charles Surface are the heroes, and decorous, law-abiding persons — Blifil and Joseph Surface — are the villains and butts. People like to believe that Nell Gwynne has every amiable quality and the Bishop's wife every odious one [007-002].

This is not a claim about the historical Nell Gwynne but about a cultural reflex: the public, suffocated by domestic respectability, inverts its own professed values in fantasy. The prostitute-mistress becomes virtuous; the Bishop's wife becomes villainous. Shaw is not endorsing this inversion — he diagnoses it as a symptom of a system in which "virtue" has become indistinguishable from "the mere morality that steals its name" [007-002].

## Nell Gwynne as Cultural Symptom

Shaw's use of Nell Gwynne is structurally similar to his use of Nelson: both figures serve to expose society's actual (as opposed to professed) moral priorities. Society claims to value marital fidelity and sexual continence; in practice, it idolizes Nelson (the adulterer) and romanticizes Nell Gwynne (the royal mistress). The gap between professed and actual morality is Shaw's central diagnostic target, and Nell Gwynne is one of the clearest exhibits in his case.

## Later Echo

Nell Gwynne reappears briefly in Shaw's discussion of divorce law, where he writes:

> What woman would not rather marry ten Pepyses? what man a dozen Nell Gwynnes? [049-2552]

This offhand comparison — Samuel Pepys (the famous diarist and serial philanderer) against Nell Gwynne — reinforces Shaw's point that the actual grounds on which people choose partners bear no relation to the legal grounds for divorce. Adultery, Shaw argues, is far from the worst marital offense; a "sentimental grievance" elevated to legal primacy by a system still rooted in property logic.
