# Morality

Bernard Shaw's treatment of morality in *Getting Married* is characteristically double-edged: he attacks "vulgar morality" as a system of hypocrisy that serves property interests, while simultaneously advancing his own moral vision — a utilitarian ethics grounded in human welfare rather than religious prohibition. The result is not moral nihilism but moral reconstruction.

## The Critique of Conventional Morality

Shaw argues that what passes for morality in marriage discourse is "the morality of the tenth commandment" — a prohibition on coveting what belongs to another man, including his wife [[004-001]]. This is not ethics but property law disguised as virtue. The "vulgar morality we mostly profess on the subject" serves to protect existing property arrangements, not to promote human flourishing [[012-001]].

He distinguishes genuine virtue from "the mere morality that steals its name" — the hollow rule-following that substitutes for ethical reflection [[007-002]]. When the character Lesbia declares "I loathe the whole marriage morality of the middle classes," she speaks for Shaw himself [[049-3261]].

## Morality as Social Control

Shaw's analysis extends to the social function of morality: "the enforcement by law of sexual morality" is a project of control, not protection [[035-001]]. The state has no business policing sexual conduct between consenting adults; its legitimate interest is in the welfare of children and the prevention of exploitation.

## Shaw's Alternative Morality

Shaw's positive moral vision emerges from his critique. The true measure of an institution is its effect on human welfare — a utilitarian calculus that asks not "Is this arrangement sinful?" but "Does this arrangement produce more happiness than misery?" By this standard, the existing marriage system is profoundly immoral, regardless of its conformity to conventional rules.

"The establishment of a conventional morality of marriage" must give way to "sensible people" making rational arrangements [[036-002]]. This is not libertinism — Shaw is notably puritanical about the importance of responsibility, honesty, and care for children — but a reorientation of morality from rules to consequences.

## Key Passages

> "The morality of the tenth commandment" — marriage morality as property protection [[004-001]].

> "I loathe the whole marriage morality of the middle classes" — Lesbia [[049-3261]].

> "The establishment of a conventional morality of marriage" must be replaced [[036-002]].
