# Queen Victoria

Queen Victoria (1819–1901) appears in Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* not as a historical actor but as a symbolic figure: the personification of conventional, respectable, and unreflective marriage morality. In the chapter "Shelley and Queen Victoria," Shaw stages an imagined debate between the two figures to model what genuine argument about marriage might look like — and to condemn the shallow moralism that substitutes for it in public discourse.

## The Imagined Debate

Shaw's rhetorical move is to imagine Shelley and Queen Victoria "arguing out their differences in another world" [025-001]. The choice of these two figures is deliberate: Shelley represents the radical critique of marriage as an institution; Victoria embodies its conventional defense, associated in the popular imagination with domestic virtue and marital propriety.

Shaw does not stack the deck in Shelley's favor. He grants that Victoria, in this imagined dialogue, "has long ago found that she cannot settle the question by classing Shelley with George IV. as a bad man" [025-001] — that is, name-calling ("libertine," "immoral") proves insufficient against serious argument. Symmetrically, "Shelley is not likely to have called her vile names on the general ground that as the economic dependence of women makes marriage a money bargain... there is no essential difference between a married woman and the woman of the streets" [025-001].

The point is not who wins but that genuine debate is possible — that intelligent people on opposite sides of the marriage question can argue without resorting to mud-slinging. This is precisely what Shaw believes public discourse about marriage *fails* to do.

## The Critique of Public Discourse

The imagined debate immediately gives way to Shaw's real target: the debased state of actual public controversy:

> Unfortunately, all the people whose methods of controversy are represented by our popular newspapers are not Queen Victorias and Shelleys. A great mass of them, when their prejudices are challenged, have no other impulse than to call the challenger names [025-001].

Queen Victoria, in Shaw's construction, is not the enemy of reform but a standard of intellectual honesty that contemporary debaters fail to meet. She is capable — in the afterlife, at least — of engaging with arguments rather than merely hurling epithets.

## Symbolic Function in Shaw's Argument

Victoria serves a triple function. First, she is a synecdoche for the respectable public that Shaw needs to persuade — the "thoughtlessly conventional people" whose eyes must be opened [025-001]. Second, her presence as a debating partner for Shelley dramatizes Shaw's conviction that the marriage question can be discussed rationally across ideological divides. Third, and most pointedly, the contrast between Victoria's imagined open-mindedness and the closed-mindedness of actual newspaper controversialists is an indictment of the public sphere that stands between Shaw's argument and its audience.
