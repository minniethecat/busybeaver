# Divorce Law Reform

The movement for **divorce law reform** forms the immediate legal and political context of Bernard Shaw's *Getting Married* (1908). The play and its preface intervene in a decades-long debate over the accessibility, grounds, and social meaning of divorce in English law.

## The Legal Landscape Before 1908

When Shaw wrote *Getting Married*, English divorce law was governed by the Matrimonial Causes Act 1857, which had transferred divorce jurisdiction from ecclesiastical courts to a secular Court for Divorce and Matrimonial Causes. However, divorce remained expensive, scandalous, and effectively inaccessible to most of the population. A husband could divorce his wife for adultery alone; a wife had to prove adultery aggravated by cruelty, desertion, or other offenses.

Shaw's preface directly attacks this asymmetry: "the injustice of forbidding a woman to divorce her husband for unfaithfulness" while permitting a husband to divorce his wife on the same grounds is, he argues, indefensible on any rational basis ([037-001]).

## Shaw's Critique of Divorce Reformers

Shaw is notably impatient with contemporary divorce reformers. He charges that "divorce reformers are so much preoccupied with the question of divorce" ([037-001]) that they fail to address the deeper structural problems of marriage itself. Making divorce easier, in Shaw's view, is necessary but insufficient — the entire institution needs redesign, not just a more accessible exit.

The preface argues that divorce should not be a judicial inquiry into marital misconduct but an administrative recognition that a marriage has ended. Shaw proposes that "the State acting on the petition of either party" should dissolve marriages without moral judgment ([046-005]).

## The Play's Treatment of Divorce

In the play proper, divorce appears primarily as a threat and a scandal. The **Divorce Court** is invoked as a place of social ruin ([049-415]) — a deterrent that keeps unhappy couples trapped in marriage. The characters' anxiety about divorce reflects the gap between the legal possibility of divorce (since 1857) and its social unacceptability in respectable Edwardian society.

## Subsequent Reforms

Shaw's arguments anticipated reforms that would unfold over the following century: the extension of divorce grounds in 1923 (equalizing adultery standards), 1937 (adding cruelty, desertion, and incurable insanity), and ultimately the Divorce Reform Act 1969, which introduced the principle of irretrievable breakdown.
