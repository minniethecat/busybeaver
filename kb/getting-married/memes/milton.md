# John Milton

John Milton appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* in a single, resonant phrase: "the great Miltonic tradition." Shaw invokes Milton not as a poet but as a reformer — the author of the divorce tracts of 1643–1645 who argued, against Puritan orthodoxy, that incompatibility should be grounds for dissolving a marriage.

## The Miltonic Tradition

Shaw's reference to the "Miltonic tradition" comes in a passage comparing national attitudes toward sex education. Lectures on sexual hygiene are given "in Germany, France, and even prudish America (where the great Miltonic tradition in this matter still lives)" [[024-001]].

The "Miltonic tradition" is the tradition of speaking openly about sex and marriage — of treating them as subjects for rational public discussion rather than shameful concealment. That Shaw locates this tradition in "prudish America" rather than England is another indictment of English culture: even America, for all its prudishness, has preserved a tradition of honest sexual discourse that England has lost.

## Milton's Divorce Tracts

Milton wrote four tracts on divorce between 1643 and 1645: *The Doctrine and Discipline of Divorce*, *The Judgment of Martin Bucer*, *Tetrachordon*, and *Colasterion*. All argued that marriage should be dissolvable on grounds of incompatibility — a position that in Puritan England was radical enough to bring Milton before Parliament and nearly cost him his liberty.

Shaw does not engage with these tracts in detail, but his invocation of "the great Miltonic tradition" signals his awareness that the argument he is making in *Getting Married* has a distinguished English precedent. Milton, like Shaw, argued that marriage law should serve human happiness rather than theological doctrine.

## Protestant Reformism

Milton's divorce tracts belong to the broader Protestant reformation of marriage that Shaw analyzes in the preface. The Reformation "was so largely a rebellion against sacerdotalism that marriage was very nearly excommunicated again" [[043-002]], and Milton represents the radical edge of that rebellion — the argument that if marriage is not a sacrament but a civil contract, then divorce must be a civil option.
