# Registry Office

The Registry Office appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* as a telling detail rather than a sustained argument. It is the physical location where civil marriage — marriage as a purely legal contract, without religious ceremony — takes place. Shaw uses it to expose a contradiction: the state claims marriage is a sacred union, yet it provides a secular mechanism for entering into one.

## Civil Marriage as Reform

Shaw notes that "our modern civil marriage, round which so many fierce controversies and political conflicts have raged" was one of the practical consequences of the Reformation — "thoroughly approved of by Calvin, and hailed with relief by Luther" [[043-002]]. The Registry Office is the institutional embodiment of this Reformation principle: marriage as a civil contract, not a sacrament.

Yet the existence of the Registry Office does not resolve the deeper problem. Civil marriage theoretically treats both parties as equal contracting agents, but the substantive law of marriage still "is not founded on either assumption" of equality — it is founded instead on "the morality of the tenth commandment" treating the wife as chattel [[004-001]].

## The Reluctant Couple

Shaw opens the preface by describing young couples who come to him seeking advice about whether to marry. "When they finally give up the idea of reforming our marriage institutions by private enterprise and personal righteousness, and consent to be led to the Registry or even to the altar, they insist on first arriving at an explicit understanding that both parties are to be perfectly free" [[001-001]].

"Led to the Registry or even to the altar" — the phrase positions the Registry Office as the less-committed option. The couple who go to the Registry rather than a church are signaling their rejection of the religious dimension of marriage, yet even they cannot escape the legal framework. The "explicit understanding" they negotiate — a private agreement to ignore the legal bond — reveals how little the institution of marriage commands moral assent even among those who comply with it.

## Symbolic Function

The Registry Office serves a symbolic function in Shaw's argument. It is the place where the state's contradictions are on display: a secular office administering a law that treats women as property, a civil contract that cannot be freely dissolved, a legal bond that even the parties entering into it have privately agreed to ignore. The Registry Office makes visible what the church ceremony obscures — that marriage is, in its legal essence, a state-administered contract, not a sacred mystery.
