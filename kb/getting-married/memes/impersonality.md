# Impersonality

In Shaw's argument, the condition of treating the sexual relation as a social transaction rather than a personal attachment — a conceptual move he deploys to disentangle marriage law from romantic sentiment. Impersonality in this sense is not coldness or indifference but a methodological stance: the recognition that the state's interest in marriage is institutional (regulating contracts, property, children) rather than sentimental (enforcing love, fidelity, or emotional commitment).

## Shaw 的定义与用法

Shaw introduces impersonality as a counterpoint to the assumption — pervasive among his contemporaries — that any relaxation of marriage law would produce "a wild outbreak of licentiousness." He identifies this as a "really appalling prevalence of the superstition that the sexual instinct in men is utterly promiscuous" [029-001]. The moralists' error, in Shaw's analysis, is to conflate two distinct concepts: promiscuity (indiscriminate sexual availability) and impersonality (treating the sexual relation as a matter of social contract rather than personal passion).

He clarifies the distinction sharply: "But promiscuity and impersonality are not the same thing. No man ever fell in love with the entire female sex, nor any woman with the entire male sex" [029-001]. Impersonality does not eliminate selectivity — it simply recognizes that the legal framework governing marriage should be designed around the institutional functions of the relationship (economic partnership, child-rearing, social status) rather than around the emotional dimension that is, in Shaw's view, inherently personal and unlegislatable.

## 在婚姻辩论中的位置

Impersonality functions as the logical engine of Shaw's reform argument. His proposal to separate the contractual, reproductive, and romantic dimensions of marriage depends on the prior claim that the state can and should treat marriage impersonally — that is, as one social institution among others rather than as a sacred mystery.

The rhetorical force of the concept lies in its contrast with what Shaw sees as the governing sentiment of English marriage law: the belief that the state must enforce fidelity and permanence because, without legal coercion, sexual chaos would ensue. Shaw counters that "selection, carried even to such fastidiousness as to induce people to say quite commonly that there is only one man or woman in the world for them, is the rule in nature" [029-001]. The impersonality of the legal framework is compatible with — and indeed protective of — the intensely personal nature of actual romantic attachment.

## 历史 / 社会语境

Shaw's deployment of impersonality draws on Fabian socialist thought, which treated social institutions as rationally designable systems rather than organic expressions of communal sentiment. The concept also resonates with the emerging professionalization of social work and public administration in Edwardian Britain — the idea that trained experts could manage social problems more effectively than inherited custom.

The moral panic Shaw addresses — that "nothing but the most severe coercion can save their wives and daughters from quite indiscriminate rapine" [029-001] — was a real feature of Edwardian discourse. The white slavery panic, campaigns against "vice," and anxieties about urban anonymity all fed the belief that sexual behavior required constant legal and social policing. Shaw's insistence on impersonality is thus a direct challenge to one of the period's most deeply held assumptions.

## Shaw 的批评与替代

Shaw's critique of the promiscuity-impersonality conflation operates at several levels:

1. **Psychological**: Human beings are naturally selective in their sexual and romantic attachments. "We often do not fall in love at all; and when we do we fall in love with one person and remain indifferent to thousands of others" [029-001]. Nature, not law, enforces monogamous sentiment.

2. **Commercial**: Shaw's analogy of the picture-postcard shop — no shopkeeper would substitute one actor's portrait for another on the ground that "the sexual instinct is promiscuous" [029-001] — demonstrates that even in commerce, everyone recognizes the highly personal nature of attraction. Yet "the moment a discussion arises on marriage," the same people argue that only "the most severe coercion" prevents chaos.

3. **Institutional**: The law cannot and should not attempt to regulate the personal dimension of marriage. An impersonal legal framework — one concerned with contracts, property, and children — would be both more just and more effective than the existing system, which attempts to legislate emotion.

Shaw's alternative is a marriage law that is "impersonal" in the sense of being institutionally rational: designed to address the actual social functions of marriage (economic partnership, child welfare, property transfer) without pretending to enforce fidelity, love, or permanence — matters that are, in his view, beyond the reach of any law.
