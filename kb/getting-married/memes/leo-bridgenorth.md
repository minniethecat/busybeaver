# Leo Bridgenorth

Leo Bridgenorth, the son of the Bishop and Edith, represents the younger generation's confrontation with the marriage question in Shaw's *Getting Married*. His romantic entanglement with Sinjon Hotchkiss — unconventional for Edwardian drama — challenges both family expectations and the rigid categories of marriage law.

## Character Background

Leo is "coming" to the wedding that anchors the play, and his presence is anticipated with anxiety by his mother [049-441]. "You know how fond I am of Leo" — the Bishop's affection for his son is genuine, but it coexists with institutional expectations that Leo's choices violate [049-493].

## Role in the Play

Leo's romantic arrangement — preferring Sinjon's companionship to conventional marriage — embodies Shaw's argument that human affection does not fit the legal categories provided by marriage law. The character's youth signals that the marriage question is not merely a problem for the older generation; it will intensify as the young reject institutions they did not design.
