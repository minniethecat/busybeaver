# Labor Exchanges

The Labor Exchanges occupy a unique position in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*: they are the one existing government institution he explicitly endorses. Where Shaw's analysis is overwhelmingly critical — of the Church, Parliament, the Cabinet, the marriage law — his treatment of the Labor Exchanges is constructive and forward-looking.

## Shaw's Endorsement

Shaw introduces the Labor Exchanges in the context of women's economic dependence on men — which he identifies as the root cause of the injustice in marriage. "Until the economic independence of women is achieved, we shall have to remain impaled on the other horn of the dilemma and maintain marriage as a slavery" [[027-001]].

The Labor Exchanges represent, for Shaw, the institutional mechanism that could deliver this independence. He addresses his remarks to "the Government of the day (1910)" and praises the Exchanges it "has very wisely established throughout the country" [[040-001]].

## The Wife and Mother Problem

Shaw uses the Labor Exchanges to pose a pointed question: "What do these Exchanges do when a woman enters and states that her occupation is that of a wife and mother; that she is out of a job; and that she wants an employer?" [[040-001]].

The question is not rhetorical — it exposes the legal and economic fiction at the heart of marriage. If being a wife is a form of employment (as the economic-dependence model implies), then unemployed wives should be able to register at the Labor Exchange. If the Exchange refuses them, "they are clearly excluding nearly the whole female sex from the benefit of the Act" [[040-001]]. If it accepts them, "they must become matrimonial agencies, unless, indeed, they are prepared to become something worse" [[040-001]].

## The White Slave Problem

Shaw extends the Labor Exchange logic to its most uncomfortable conclusion. "Suppose, again, a woman presents herself at the Labor Exchange, and states her trade as that of a White Slave" — the term for women forced into prostitution [[041-001]]. If the Exchange turns her away, it abandons the most vulnerable women to destitution. If it finds her honest employment, it demonstrates that economic independence is the alternative to both marriage-as-prostitution and literal prostitution.

This is Shaw's argument at its most provocative: the Labor Exchanges, by making women economically independent, would "cause a great number of childless unhappy marriages to break up spontaneously, whether the marriage laws are altered or not" [[041-001]]. Reform need not be legislated directly — it follows automatically from economic justice.

## Institutional Role in Reform

The Labor Exchanges thus represent, in Shaw's schema, an indirect path to marriage reform. Rather than attacking marriage law directly (which makes the Cabinet "shiver with apprehension"), the state should build institutions that give women economic alternatives. When women can support themselves, marriages maintained "by economic pressure alone will dissolve when that pressure is removed" [[041-001]].

## Key Passages

> "Let me ask the Government of the day (1910) a question with regard to the Labor Exchanges it has very wisely established throughout the country" [[040-001]].

> "What do these Exchanges do when a woman enters and states that her occupation is that of a wife and mother; that she is out of a job; and that she wants an employer?" [[040-001]].

> "The solution of the problem of finding independent employment for women may cause a great number of childless unhappy marriages to break up spontaneously" [[041-001]].
