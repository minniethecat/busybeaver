# Girolamo Savonarola

Girolamo Savonarola (1452–1498), the Dominican friar who briefly ruled Florence as a theocratic republic before being excommunicated and burned at the stake, appears in Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* as exhibit A in the argument that "an immoderately good man is very much more dangerous than an immoderately bad man" [019-001]. Shaw uses Savonarola to challenge the unexamined assumption that goodness, unlike badness, requires no moderation.

## The Argument from Dangerous Goodness

In the chapter "The Gospel of Laodicea," Shaw develops his critique of moral extremism. The argument opens with a deliberately shocking proposition:

> The truth is, an immoderately good man is very much more dangerous than an immoderately bad man: that is why Savonarola was burnt and John of Leyden torn to pieces with red-hot pincers whilst multitudes of unredeemed rascals were being let off with clipped ears, burnt palms, a flogging, or a few years in the galleys [019-001].

The historical asymmetry is the argument's force: ordinary criminals received milder punishments than saints. The reason, Shaw suggests, is that the fanatic's goodness threatens the social order in ways that ordinary vice does not. The criminal breaks rules; the fanatic rewrites them, and the rewriting may be more destructive than the breaking.

## The Ambiguity of the Prophet

Shaw complicates the Savonarola example by acknowledging that the line between dangerous fanatic and true prophet is impossible to draw in the moment:

> The fact that the insanity may be privileged, as Savonarola's was up to the point of wrecking the social life of Florence, does not alter the case. We always hesitate to treat a dangerously good man as a lunatic because he may turn out to be a prophet in the true sense: that is, a man of exceptional sanity who is in the right when we are in the wrong. However necessary it may have been to get rid of Savonarola, it was foolish to poison Socrates and burn St. Joan of Arc [019-001].

This passage performs the very ambiguity it describes. Savonarola is simultaneously "necessary to get rid of" and adjacent to Socrates and Joan of Arc — genuine prophets whose executions were mistakes. Shaw is not passing judgment on Savonarola's historical role but using him to illustrate a structural problem: society cannot reliably distinguish the dangerous good man from the prophetic one, yet it must act on that distinction anyway.

## Connection to the Marriage Argument

Savonarola's relevance to Shaw's marriage argument lies in the parallel between religious and domestic fanaticism. Just as Savonarola's immoderate goodness "wrecked the social life of Florence," the immoderate domestic sentiment that Shaw critiques throughout the preface wrecks the social life of the family. The cure for marital misery is not more goodness — more self-sacrifice, more devotion, more intensity — but a temperate, Laodicean moderation that recognizes limits.
