# Edith Bridgenorth

Edith Bridgenorth (née Hotchkiss) is a central figure in Shaw's *Getting Married*. As the Bishop's wife and the mother of the household, she presides over the domestic sphere that the play both celebrates and dismantles. Her wedding day crisis — the discovery that her marriage may be legally invalid — triggers the play's extended debate about what marriage actually is.

## Character Background

Edith is married to Alfred Bridgenorth, the Bishop of Chelsea. She is the mother of Leo and the sister-in-law of Lesbia Grantham. She occupies the role of the respectable Edwardian matron — efficient, socially adept, and deeply invested in the appearances that maintain social order. She is described as "the Bishop's lady" who occupies the kitchen — the Bishop's "favorite room" — managing the household with quiet competence [[049-004]], [[049-003]].

## Role in the Play

Edith's function is to embody the contradictions of conventional marriage. She has performed the role of wife and mother impeccably, yet on her own wedding day crisis she finds herself asking whether her marriage was ever legally valid. The question "Is Edith to be given away by this Respondent?" captures her sudden vulnerability [[049-490]].

## Key Passages

> "The Bishop's lady" — Edith's social position defined by her husband's role [[049-027]].

> "Is Edith to be given away by this Respondent?" — the question that destabilizes her marriage [[049-490]].

> "I can't stay away from Edith's wedding" — the gravity of the occasion [[049-462]].
