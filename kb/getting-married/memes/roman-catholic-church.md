# Roman Catholic Church

The Roman Catholic Church serves as the doctrinal opposite pole in Bernard Shaw's analysis of marriage systems. Where the Church of England is a muddled compromise, the Roman Church is a model of clarity — but its clarity produces, in Shaw's view, an institution so rigid that it provokes its own rejection.

## Indissoluble Marriage

Shaw identifies the defining feature of Catholic marriage as its indissolubility. He uses it as one term in a catalog of marriage regimes: "sacramental marriage, indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage, marriage of divorced persons, Scotch marriage, Irish marriage, English marriage" [[003-001]]. The word "indissoluble" is attached specifically to the Roman Catholic variant — it is what distinguishes Catholic marriage in the public imagination.

The consequences of this rigidity, Shaw argues, are counterproductive. The Catholic prohibition on divorce has not preserved marriage but has driven populations toward systems that permit it: "on the continent from indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage to marriage that can be dissolved by an hour's visit to Paris" [[023-001]].

## Celibacy

The second feature of the Roman Catholic system that Shaw discusses is priestly celibacy. "To this day the celibacy of the Roman Catholic priesthood is a standing protest against marriage" [[005-003]]. The celibate priesthood embodies the early Christian doctrine that virginity is a higher spiritual state than marriage — a doctrine that, even for those who reject it, "gave virginity a heavenly value" and thereby "poisons the well of marriage reform" [[042-002]].

## Legal Pluralism

Shaw notes that English law accommodates Catholic beliefs through a legal fiction. "A Roman Catholic may obey his Church by assenting to the doctrine of indissoluble marriage, but the State compels him to accept a marriage that can be dissolved if the marriage is celebrated in England" [[003-002]]. The English state imposes its own marriage law regardless of the parties' religious convictions — a legal pluralism that Shaw presents as fact without endorsing.

## Shaw's View

Shaw does not defend the Catholic position but uses it diagnostically. The Roman Catholic system demonstrates what happens when marriage law is built on theological absolutism rather than human need: it becomes something people flee from rather than something they inhabit. The Catholic model is wrong, but at least it is clear about what it believes — unlike the Church of England's "shandygaff." This clarity makes it a useful analytical foil.
