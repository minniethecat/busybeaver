# England

England is the jurisdictional and cultural frame of Bernard Shaw's entire argument about marriage. The preface to *Getting Married* is not a universal treatise on marriage but a specific critique of English marriage law, English politics, and English habits of thought. Shaw invokes other nations — France, Italy, America, Germany — only to illuminate England by contrast.

## English Marriage Law

Shaw's opening declaration establishes England as the object of his critique: "Because our marriage law is inhuman and unreasonable to the point of downright abomination" [[001-001]]. The possessive "our" identifies the law as specifically English, and the critique that follows is grounded in the peculiarities of English legal and religious history.

The English marriage system, Shaw argues, rests on a foundation of incoherence. The law is not based on either of the two principles that might justify it — the pursuit of happiness or the raising of children: "If we adopt the common romantic assumption that the object of marriage is bliss... If we accept the view that the object of marriage is to provide for the production and rearing of children" — "our marriage law is not founded on either assumption" [[004-001]].

## English Political Paralysis

The specifically English character of the problem extends to its politics. No government "will touch the marriage question if it can possibly help it" because English public opinion is "a mere mob of classes, each with its own habits and prejudices" [[012-001]].

This paralysis is presented as a national characteristic. "It is our cautious custom to lag behind the rest of the world to see how their experiments in reform turn out before venturing ourselves, and then take advantage of their experience to get ahead of them" [[047-001]]. The strategy of cautious imitation is offered without endorsement — Shaw clearly thinks England should lead rather than follow.

## Religious and Legal Pluralism

England's marriage law is also characterized by its accommodation of multiple religious regimes. Shaw lists the forms of marriage that coexist within English jurisdiction: "sacramental marriage, indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage, marriage of divorced persons, Scotch marriage, Irish marriage, English marriage" [[003-001]]. This pluralism is not presented as enlightened tolerance but as incoherence — each community operates under different rules, and the state's own law is merely one option among many.

## England as Comparison

England appears throughout the preface in comparative contexts:
- "Theoretically, our civilly married couples are making a Protestant protest against the Roman Catholic marriage" [[004-002]]
- "On the continent from indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage to marriage that can be dissolved by an hour's visit to Paris" [[023-001]]
- "Even prudish America (where the great Miltonic tradition in this matter still lives)" [[024-001]]

England, in Shaw's comparative framework, occupies a middle position — not as rigid as Catholic Europe, not as rational as Shaw would like, suspended between tradition and reform in a state of permanent indecision.
