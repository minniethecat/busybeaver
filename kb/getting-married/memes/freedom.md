# Freedom

Bernard Shaw's concept of freedom in *Getting Married* is primarily negative freedom — freedom *from* coercion, economic compulsion, and legal entrapment — rather than positive freedom to do as one pleases. "Such freedom is, of course, impossible in the present state of society," he concedes, but it remains the regulative ideal toward which reform must aim [[045-003]].

## Freedom from Economic Compulsion

Shaw's primary concern is with the economic constraints that make genuine marital consent impossible. When marriage is "the only alternative to celibacy and indigence," a woman's consent is not free but coerced [[027-001]]. True freedom requires that women have the economic means to choose marriage — or to choose not to marry — without material penalty.

He puts the point bluntly: the marriage system's faults "are not even the price of freedom" — they are the price of refusing to reform [[002-001]]. Freedom is not what we sacrifice for stable marriage; it is what we sacrifice by maintaining the current system.

## Freedom to Exit

Shaw's most insistent demand is for freedom of exit. A marriage that cannot be left is not a free association but a prison, regardless of how cheerfully it began. Freedom to divorce is not the enemy of marriage but its necessary condition: "nothing except divorce can ever raise" the standard of marital conduct [[045-001]].

## The Limits of Freedom

Shaw is not a libertarian. He recognizes that "such freedom is, of course, impossible" in pure form — social obligations, especially toward children, constrain individual choice. His position is that constraints should be explicit, rationally justified, and equally distributed, rather than hidden behind sentimental rhetoric about sacred unions.
