# Horatio Nelson

Horatio Nelson (1758–1805), British naval hero and Vice-Admiral, appears in Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* as a key exhibit in his argument about society's hypocritical enforcement of marriage norms. Shaw contrasts Nelson's public idolization — despite flagrant marital infidelity — with the savage condemnation of Percy Bysshe Shelley, whose real "crime" was not sexual misconduct but intellectual criticism of the institution of marriage itself.

## Historical Background

Nelson was Britain's foremost naval commander during the Napoleonic Wars, celebrated for victories at the Nile, Copenhagen, and Trafalgar. In his personal life, he separated from his wife Frances Nisbet and formed a well-known ménage à trois with Sir William Hamilton, the British envoy to Naples, and Hamilton's wife Emma. This arrangement was an open secret in London society [004-002].

## Shaw's Use of Nelson as Moral Evidence

Shaw deploys Nelson in two distinct argumentative contexts within the preface.

### The Double Standard of Moral Judgment

In the section on "Survivals of Sex Slavery," Shaw argues that marriage is enforced by public opinion with ferocious intolerance for intellectual dissent, even as sexual laxity is winked at:

> so that we find the austere Shelley denounced as a fiend in human form, whilst Nelson, who openly left his wife and formed a menage a trois with Sir William and Lady Hamilton, was idolized [004-002].

Shaw's point is not to condemn Nelson's conduct but to expose the incoherence of a social morality that punishes criticism of marriage more savagely than it punishes departure from marital norms. Shelley's "unpardonable offence was that he attacked marriage as an institution" [004-002] — Nelson, who merely violated the institution's rules while leaving its ideological authority intact, was celebrated.

### The "Household of Three" and Sentimental Monogamy

In a later passage on the sentimental basis of monogamy, Shaw invokes Nelson as the rare exception that proves the rule [036-002]. Shaw argues that in a genuine "marriage of sentiment," no room exists for a third party — with one striking exception:

> Take for example the famous case of Nelson and Sir William and Lady Hamilton. The secret of this household of three was not only that both the husband and Nelson were devoted to Lady Hamilton, but that they were also apparently devoted to one another [036-002].

Shaw treats this triadic affection as so exceptional as to be "practicably negligible in the establishment of a conventional morality of marriage" [036-002]. The Nelson-Hamilton household is not a model for reform but a limit case — proof that the sentimental logic of monogamy holds for virtually all human relationships.

## Relationship to Shaw's Marriage Argument

Nelson serves a dual rhetorical function: he exposes the hypocrisy of a society that venerates rule-breakers while persecuting rule-critics, and he demonstrates that even the most famous counterexample to sentimental monogamy actually reinforces the rule. In both uses, Nelson is not a hero or villain but a diagnostic tool — his life reveals the contradictions Shaw aims to resolve through rational marriage reform.
