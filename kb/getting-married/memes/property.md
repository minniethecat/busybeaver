# Property

Bernard Shaw identifies property as the invisible hand behind marriage law. In *Getting Married*, he argues that resistance to reform is fundamentally economic: "the attack on marriage is an attack on property; so that Shelley was something more than a charlatan when he exploited the rich men of his time by his revolutionary marriage doctrines" [[004-003]]. The marriage system exists to regulate the transmission of wealth, not to promote human happiness.

## Property as the Hidden Foundation

Shaw argues that the marriage contract is "a commercial contract of property and slavery" disguised by sentiment and religious ceremony [[011-001]]. The law's real concern is ensuring that children are legitimate heirs and that property passes through recognized channels. This explains why adultery by wives is treated more severely than adultery by husbands: a wife's infidelity threatens the paternity of heirs; a husband's does not.

## Economic Compulsion

Marriage is compulsory, Shaw argues, because women have no independent economic existence: "To a woman without property or marketable talent a husband is a necessity" [[027-001]]. This economic dependency distorts consent — a woman marries not from genuine choice but from survival necessity. "A woman without property" is "sexually attractive" precisely because her economic vulnerability makes her available [[026-002]].

## The Wife as Property

Shaw is blunt about the legal reality: "a husband or a wife is an article of property, greatly depreciated in value" by any deviation from market expectations [[044-001]]. The "theory that the wife is the property of the husband" persists even in a society that professes companionate marriage [[045-003]].

## Reform Implications

Shaw's analysis of property leads him to a radical conclusion: meaningful marriage reform requires economic reform. As long as women cannot support themselves independently, marriage will remain a form of economic servitude regardless of what the law says. His proposed solution — making marriage a purely civil contract with easy exit — depends on women having the economic means to exercise genuine choice.

In the play, Mrs George declares: "I'm not your property, young man" [[049-2552]] — a line that encapsulates Shaw's feminist-economic argument in seven words.
