# London

The play *Getting Married* is set at the country house of the Bridgenorth family, but **London** is the metropolitan gravitational center around which its characters' social lives orbit. The city represents the world of clubs, courts, commerce, and class — the Edwardian social fabric that Shaw's marriage critique both inhabits and dissects.

## The London Conference

Shaw's preface describes how "a rationalist divine once organized in London a conference of respectable married men" ([006-001]) to discuss marriage reform privately — an event that could not be held publicly without scandal. This London conference provides the dramatic inspiration for the play's central situation: a group of people gathered to discuss marriage honestly.

## Clubs and Class

The characters repeatedly reference London institutions. **Sinjon Hotchkiss** recalls being expelled from "two of the smartest clubs in London" ([049-1065]), and **Reginald Bridgenorth** is noted to be "in London, and wants to come to the wedding" ([049-409]). London clubs function in the play as markers of class membership and its fragility — the social infrastructure that enforces the norms Shaw critiques.

The General's observation that "no servant in London is strong enough to close a door" ([049-2602]) is a comic aside that reveals the class dynamics underpinning the marriage system: the domestic labor that makes middle-class marriage possible is performed by an invisible servant class.

## London as Escape and Trap

For the character of **Collins**, the coal merchant, London represents economic opportunity — "I ordered my first ton of coal by myself in London" ([049-2270]) — and a form of liberation. Yet the city is also the site of the **Divorce Court** ([049-415]), the legal institution that adjudicates the failures of the marriage system Shaw critiques.
