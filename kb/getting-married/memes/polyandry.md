# Polyandry

A marriage system in which one woman is married to multiple husbands simultaneously. In *Getting Married*, Bernard Shaw addresses polyandry not as an exotic practice but as the structurally symmetrical counterpart to polygyny — one that reveals the gendered asymmetry embedded in Western marriage law.

## Shaw 的定义与用法

Shaw treats polyandry as a matter of demographic arithmetic rather than morality. He argues that "the number of wives permitted to a single husband or of husbands to a single wife under a marriage system, is not an ethical problem: it depends solely on the proportion of the sexes in the population" [018-001]. His framing deliberately strips the concept of its sensational connotations and reframes it as a question of population management — the same rational calculus he applies to polygyny.

Shaw is careful to distinguish polyandry from promiscuity and "impersonality," noting that a marriage system permitting multiple husbands would still impose obligations and structure on sexual relations. The prohibition of polyandry in Western law is, in his view, not a natural moral intuition but a legal artifact born of the same demographic and economic logic that produces monogamy [018-001].

## 在婚姻辩论中的位置

Polyandry serves a specific argumentative function in Shaw's preface: it demonstrates that the state's regulation of marriage numbers is not grounded in universal morality but in population economics. If polygyny is permitted under some legal systems (notably Islamic law, which Shaw references), then the categorical prohibition of polyandry under English law exposes the state's interest in controlling female sexuality more than in upholding any consistent marital ideal.

The concept also appears in the dramatic portion of the text. In the play, characters reference polyandry as a rhetorical provocation — a way to test whether the assembled company truly believes in sexual equality when pushed to its logical conclusions. The fact that polyandry "is not allowed" [020-001] while de facto polygyny is tolerated becomes a motif illustrating Shaw's broader argument about the hypocrisy of marriage law administration.

## 历史 / 社会语境

At the time of Shaw's writing (1908), polyandry existed in anthropological literature primarily as a description of fraternal polyandry in Tibet and parts of India. For Shaw's Edwardian audience, the term carried an air of the exotic and morally suspect. Shaw exploits this discomfort by applying the same demographic logic to polyandry that he applies to the more familiar polygyny: if the survival of the nation required it — for instance, after a catastrophic war that killed "three-quarters of the men" [018-001] — polyandry would become a demographic necessity, not a moral choice.

The legal context is decisive: English marriage law at the time criminalized bigamy under the Offences Against the Person Act 1861, making no distinction between polygyny and polyandry. Both were equally illegal, yet Shaw's point is that social disapproval attaches far more strongly to the idea of a woman with multiple husbands than to the well-known reality of men maintaining multiple households.

## Shaw 的批评与替代

Shaw's critique operates at two levels:

1. **Demographic**: The number of spouses in a marriage system is not a moral question but an arithmetic one determined by sex ratios in the population [018-001]. This framing undercuts both religious objections to polygamy and the sentimental attachment to monogamy as a romantic ideal.

2. **Economic**: Shaw introduces the economic dimension by comparing Kaffir polygyny — where "the women are the breadwinners" — with Western arrangements where "women are not paid for their social work in the bearing and rearing of children" but are "quartered on the wages of their husbands" [018-001]. Under this economic analysis, polyandry would be economically infeasible under current wage structures, just as polygyny is for most men.

Shaw's alternative is not to advocate for legalizing polyandry but to expose the incoherence of state-regulated marriage numbers. The ultimate implication is that "polygyny and polyandry as a means of educating children" [045-001] are both inferior to a system that separates child-rearing from the contractual obligations of marriage — consistent with his broader argument for contract-based, rather than status-based, personal relations.

## 关键引文

It is a simple matter of necessity; for if a large proportion of women were killed or disabled, no possible readjustment of our marriage law could avert the depopulation and consequent political ruin of the country, because a woman with several husbands bears fewer children than a woman with one, whereas a man can produce as many families as he has wives.
