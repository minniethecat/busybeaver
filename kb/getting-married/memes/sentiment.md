# Sentiment

Bernard Shaw wages a relentless campaign against sentimentality in *Getting Married*. He argues that the romantic and emotional discourse surrounding marriage — its "sentimentality, its romance, its Amoris" — functions as a smokescreen that obscures the institution's cruelty and irrationality [[011-001]]. Sentiment, in Shaw's analysis, is not a harmless embellishment of marriage but a principal obstacle to reform.

## Sentiment as Obstacle

Shaw's critique is structural: sentiment prevents clear thinking about marriage by substituting feeling for analysis. "We cannot disentangle the institution from the sentiments, sympathetic or abhorrent, which it evokes" [[026-001]]. As long as marriage is discussed in the language of eternal love and sacred union, the practical questions — Who owns the property? Who supports the children? Who can leave? — remain unasked and unanswered.

## The Romance Trap

Shaw is especially hostile to the romantic idealization of marriage. "The revolt is also against its sentimentality, its romance" [[011-001]]. Romantic love, he argues, is a temporary condition that cannot bear the weight of a lifelong legal contract. To base marriage on romantic sentiment is to guarantee its failure, because sentiment is mutable while the legal bond is rigid.

## Sentiment vs. Reason

Shaw's alternative is not the elimination of sentiment but its subordination to reason. "Though a little sentiment may be a good thing, excessive sentimentality" distorts judgment [[009-003]]. The goal is not to make marriage emotionless but to ensure that emotion does not prevent rational institutional design. "Reducing home life and domestic sentiment to something like reasonable proportions" is the project [[007-002]].

## The Strategic Use of Sentiment

Shaw is not above using sentiment himself — his play is full of pathos and emotional confrontation. But he deploys sentiment strategically, to expose the cruelty hidden beneath sentimental rhetoric. When a character invokes "purity, home, motherhood, respectability," Shaw wants the audience to see the economic coercion and legal injustice behind those warm words.
