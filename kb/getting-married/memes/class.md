# Class

Class pervades Bernard Shaw's analysis of marriage in *Getting Married*, though he rarely names it directly. His Fabian socialism informs every argument: the marriage system serves property owners at the expense of the poor, and reform is resisted because "the attack on marriage is an attack on property" — which is to say, an attack on class privilege [[004-003]].

## Class and Marriage Law

Shaw's analysis reveals a two-tier marriage system. The wealthy can afford private settlements, legal advice, and de facto separation arrangements that function as private divorce. The poor, governed by default rules designed to protect property transmission, have no such options. A working-class woman trapped in a brutal marriage has no practical remedy; a wealthy man can buy his way out of any inconvenient union.

## Class and Morality

Shaw is especially attentive to the class dimensions of sexual morality. "The whole marriage morality of the middle classes" — with its obsessive concern for respectability, its horror of scandal, its enforcement of hypocritical standards — serves to maintain class boundaries [[049-3261]]. Working-class sexual conduct is policed differently than upper-class conduct, and the middle class polices itself most severely of all.

## The Gentry and Reform

In the play, Shaw dramatizes class tension through the Bridgenorth family — gentry whose comfortable assumptions about marriage are challenged by characters from different class positions. The Bishop's ecclesiastical authority, the General's military dignity, the young men's professional ambitions — all are shaped by class interest. Shaw's point is that no one approaches the marriage question from a position of pure reason; everyone has a class stake.

## Toward Class-Blind Institutions

Shaw's reform proposals aim at institutions that would function equally regardless of class: civil marriage contracts with standardized terms, easy divorce available to all, state support for children independent of parents' marital status. These proposals are socialist in their implication — they would reduce the class privilege embedded in existing marriage law.
