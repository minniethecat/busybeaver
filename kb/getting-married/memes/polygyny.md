# Polygyny

In *Getting Married*, Bernard Shaw deploys polygyny as a heuristic device — not to advocate for it, but to expose the demographic logic underlying all marriage systems, including the monogamy that his contemporaries treat as self-evidently moral. His analysis is characteristically unsentimental: polygyny is not inherently wicked; it is simply limited by the equal birth ratio of men and women.

## Shaw's Demographic Analysis

Shaw's starting point is pragmatic rather than moralistic. He observes that polygyny could theoretically solve the problem of identifying a child's father "by legalizing polygyny, because there are more adult women than men" [[017-001]]. The demographic imbalance, not abstract ethics, is what matters.

His key insight is that polygyny works only when limited: "women do not object to polygyny when it is customary: on the contrary, they are its staunchest defenders" [[019-001]]. The reason? In limited polygyny — "by the Mohammedan rule a man cannot marry more than four wives" — the system remains stable [[020-001]]. The constraint prevents the catastrophic imbalance that unlimited polygyny would produce.

## The Practical Limit

Shaw argues that "if we are to make polygyny a success, we must limit it" [[020-002]]. This is the same principle that governs monogamy: institutions must respect demographic reality. The sex ratio is approximately equal, so "polygyny and polyandry as a means of enabling everyone to marry are ruled out by the fact that the numbers of the sexes are about equal" [[045-001]].

## Polygyny as an Argumentative Tool

Shaw's real purpose in discussing polygyny is to undermine the moral absolutism of monogamy advocates. By showing that polygyny can be a stable and accepted institution when properly constrained, he demonstrates that marriage forms are not divinely ordained but are human arrangements shaped by material conditions. "What we want at present is not more sexual liberty but better regulation" [[036-002]].

This rhetorical move is typical of Shaw: he uses the exotic example to make the familiar one strange, forcing readers to examine their own assumptions.
