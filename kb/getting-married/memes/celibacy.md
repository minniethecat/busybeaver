# Celibacy

Bernard Shaw addresses celibacy in *Getting Married* from two angles: as a religious ideal whose historical influence shaped Western marriage law, and as an involuntary condition imposed on people who cannot afford or risk marriage under existing laws. In both senses, he treats celibacy not as a spiritual discipline but as a social phenomenon with material causes and consequences.

## The Religious Tradition

Shaw traces the "religious revolt against marriage" to early Christianity, noting that "to this day the celibacy of the Roman Catholic priesthood is a standing protest against marriage" [[005-003]]. He argues that the elevation of celibacy as a higher spiritual state created an enduring contradiction in Christian societies: marriage was permitted but disparaged, necessary but impure.

The "half-discarded doctrine of celibacy" — which "gave virginity a heavenly value" — continues to shape attitudes toward sexuality even among people who reject its theological basis [[042-002]]. This legacy, Shaw argues, poisons the well of marriage reform by making any honest discussion of sexual relations seem impious.

## Involuntary Celibacy

Shaw's more pressing concern is with the celibacy imposed by bad law. "The couples condemned to celibacy by separation orders which do not permit remarriage" are victims of a system that forbids both marriage and its dissolution [[012-001]]. Similarly, women without independent means are forced into "life-long celibacy and indigence" because they cannot afford the risks of marriage [[027-001]].

The marriage system, Shaw argues, "condemns a great many women to the celibacy of neglect" — not through active prohibition but through making marriage too dangerous or inaccessible [[019-001]].

## Celibacy as Critique

Shaw uses celibacy to expose the inconsistency of those who defend marriage as sacred while treating sexual relations as degrading. If celibacy is the higher state, "then marriage is legalized impurity" — a conclusion that the Church flinches from but cannot logically escape. The alternative, which Shaw prefers, is to treat sexual relations within marriage as normal and healthy, removing the theological stain that makes honest reform impossible.
