# France

France appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* as a comparative reference point — the continental nation whose approach to marriage, population, and public morality illuminates English failures by contrast. Shaw's references to France are brief but pointed, serving to demonstrate that other nations address questions England refuses to face.

## Zola and Population Policy

Shaw invokes France in the context of the falling birth rate — the one issue that "is now making some action in the matter imperative" on marriage reform [[012-001]]. He recalls that "when Zola tried to repopulate France by writing a novel in praise of parentage, the only comment made here was that the book could not possibly be translated into English, as its subject was too improper" [[012-001]].

The anecdote is a double indictment. France at least has public intellectuals addressing the population question directly. England's response is to declare the subject unfit for translation — a prurient refusal to engage that Shaw presents as characteristically English.

## Continental Comparison

France is also implicitly present in Shaw's references to "the continent" and its more flexible marriage regimes. The Roman Catholic model of indissoluble marriage has driven populations "from indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage to marriage that can be dissolved by an hour's visit to Paris" [[023-001]].

Paris here functions as the symbol of liberal divorce — a destination where marriages contracted under stricter regimes can be unwound. For English readers in 1908, the "visit to Paris" was a real practice: English couples seeking divorce would sometimes establish residence in France to take advantage of more permissive French law. But Shaw's point is the absurdity of such arrangements — marriage law that can be circumvented by a train ticket is marriage law that has lost its moral legitimacy.

## Key Passages

> "When Zola tried to repopulate France by writing a novel in praise of parentage, the only comment made here was that the book could not possibly be translated into English, as its subject was too improper" [[012-001]].

> "On the continent from indissoluble Roman Catholic marriage to marriage that can be dissolved by an hour's visit to Paris" [[023-001]].
