# Paris

Paris appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* in a single, memorable phrase: "marriage that can be dissolved by an hour's visit to Paris" [[023-001]]. The city functions not as a geographical location but as a symbol — the metonym for accessible divorce.

## Divorce Tourism

In the early twentieth century, "an hour's visit to Paris" referred to a real practice. English couples seeking divorce would travel to France, establish nominal residence, and obtain a divorce under French law — which, while not permissive by modern standards, was more accessible than English divorce law, which required an Act of Parliament for each divorce until 1857 and remained restrictive afterward.

Shaw's phrase captures the absurdity of a system where the cost and inconvenience of a Channel crossing determine whether a marriage can be dissolved. The law is not being obeyed; it is being evaded. And the evasion is open, known, and tolerated — everyone understands what "an hour's visit to Paris" means.

## Symbolic Function

Paris serves Shaw's argument as a symbol of liberalization by default rather than by design. English marriage law has not been reformed to permit divorce; it has simply been rendered irrelevant by the availability of continental alternatives. This is, for Shaw, the worst of both worlds: the law retains its harsh provisions on paper while losing its moral authority in practice.

The solution Shaw proposes — "grant divorce whenever it is desired, without asking why" [[047-001]] — would make the visit to Paris unnecessary. A reformed English law would achieve by honest legislation what is currently achieved by expensive evasion.
