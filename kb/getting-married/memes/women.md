# Women

**Women** occupy a central position in Bernard Shaw's *Getting Married* (1908) — not as passive subjects of marriage law, but as economic and political agents whose subordination is the primary flaw Shaw identifies in the institution of marriage. Shaw's analysis spans from the "economic slavery of women" in the preface to the varied female perspectives dramatized in the play proper.

## Economic Dependence

Shaw devotes an entire preface section to "The Economic Slavery of Women" ([309–309]), arguing that marriage functions as a labor market in which women's domestic work is uncompensated and legally invisible. The existing system, he contends, leaves a woman without property in a position where "to be sexual" is a matter of economic survival ([026-002]).

The preface proposes placing "the work of a wife and mother on the same footing as other work" — a radical proposal for 1908 that anticipates later campaigns for wages for housework ([046-008]). Shaw frames this not as charity but as a necessary correction to a system where "the man is the purchaser and the woman the purchased" ([034-001]).

## The Right to Motherhood

Shaw argues for what he calls "the right to motherhood" as distinct from the obligation to marry. He proposes state payments to mothers, "thereby making motherhood a real profession as it ought to be" ([016-002]). This separates reproduction from the marriage contract — a woman should be able to have children without entering into the legal and economic subordination that marriage entails.

The preface addresses the "superfluous woman" problem directly: the demographic reality that in early 20th-century England there were "something like 1 1/11 woman to 1 man" ([020-002]), making universal marriage mathematically impossible. Shaw uses this fact to argue that marriage cannot be the sole legitimate framework for women's lives.

## Women in the Play

The play's female characters embody the range of positions available to Edwardian women. **Mrs Bridgenorth (Edith)** represents the "born wife and mother" ([049-180]) — content within domesticity yet quietly managing the household's moral economy. **Lesbia Grantham** represents the unmarried woman who refuses marriage on principle, declaring she is "not a marrying woman" ([049-072]) and asserting a woman's right to refuse motherhood ([049-1543]).

**Edith Bridgenorth** (the younger) represents the new woman of the early 20th century — educated, conscious of her dependence on her mother's income ([049-1181]), and caught between traditional expectations and personal autonomy. The General's observation that "women are not all the same to me" ([049-2481]) and Mrs George's retort that "women are all different" ([049-2482]) encapsulate the play's refusal to treat women as a homogeneous category.

## Votes for Women

The suffrage movement was at its height when Shaw wrote *Getting Married*. The preface speculates that giving women the vote might transform politics not because women are inherently more virtuous, but because their structural position gives them different interests — particularly in marriage law reform. Shaw suggests that "by the operation of Votes for Women" a more rational approach to marriage law might emerge, "if there be any virtue in the franchise" ([035-001]).

> Shaw's argument in the preface is that the "woman question" cannot be solved within existing marriage law. The economic independence of women — through paid work, state support for motherhood, and political enfranchisement — is prerequisite to any genuine reform of the marriage institution.

> The "masterless woman" ([004-003]) — one who is neither wife nor daughter nor servant — represents in Shaw's analysis both a threat to the social order and its potential salvation: "her bondage which is the masterless woman's only alternative to celibacy is veiled" by the conventions of polite society.
