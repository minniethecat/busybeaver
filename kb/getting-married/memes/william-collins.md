# William Collins

William Collins, the greengrocer, is the working-class anchor of Shaw's *Getting Married*. As the Bishop's factotum and the husband of Mrs George, he moves through the household observing the gentry's marital crises with a combination of deference and shrewd judgment. His plain speech provides a democratic counterpoint to the upper-class characters' elaborate moralizing.

## Character Background

Collins is described as "an elderly man" in "evening dress" — a servant who has risen to the dignity of formal attire [049-005], [049-004]. He is married to Mrs George Collins, the mayoress, whose social mobility far exceeds his own. His role is to facilitate the gentry's lives while maintaining his own household's stability.

## Role in the Play

Collins opens the play in conversation with Mrs Bridgenorth, establishing the domestic routine that the marriage crisis will disrupt. He is a witness rather than a participant in the central conflict, but his presence reminds the audience that marriage law affects all classes — and that working-class marriages, lacking the cushion of property, face even greater pressures.

## Key Passages

> "Collins is an elderly man with a rather quizzical expression" [049-005].

> "He is in evening dress" — the dignity of the working man [049-004].

> "Mrs George can get round anybody if she wants to" [049-185].
