# Divorce Court

The **Divorce Court** — formally the Court for Divorce and Matrimonial Causes established by the Matrimonial Causes Act 1857 — appears in Bernard Shaw's *Getting Married* not as a solution to failed marriages but as a theater of public humiliation that deters the unhappily married from seeking legal remedy.

## The Court as Deterrent

Shaw's preface argues that the adversarial nature of divorce proceedings makes the Court an instrument of punishment rather than relief. The requirement to prove matrimonial offense — adultery, cruelty, desertion — transforms private misery into public spectacle. The result is that "the temporal Courts" ([003-002]) function as an obstacle to divorce rather than a facilitator of it.

The play's characters treat the Divorce Court as a place of last resort and social ruin. When one character mentions "the Divorce Court" ([049-415]), the reference carries the weight of scandal — a threat to reputation that keeps characters bound to marriages they would otherwise leave.

## Judicial vs. Administrative Dissolution

Shaw's alternative to the adversarial divorce court is administrative dissolution: marriage should be terminated by a civil registrar on the petition of either party, without moral inquiry into conduct. The State should record the end of a marriage as neutrally as it records a death — establishing facts, not assigning blame.

The preface contends that judges "have nothing to hope from our Courts" ([044-002]) when it comes to understanding the intimate dynamics of marriage. Legal training, in Shaw's view, is the wrong qualification for adjudicating emotional and sexual relationships.

## The Court in the Play

The Divorce Court is never seen on stage — the play is set at the Bridgenorth country house — but it haunts the proceedings as an offstage threat. Characters discuss "wicked lies in Court" ([049-1530]) and the social damage of being publicly exposed in divorce proceedings. The Court functions dramaturgically as the institutional embodiment of everything Shaw wants to reform.
