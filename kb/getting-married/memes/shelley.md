# Percy Bysshe Shelley

Percy Bysshe Shelley occupies a unique position in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*: he is the exemplary case of the writer punished not for sexual misconduct but for questioning the institution of marriage itself. Shaw uses Shelley to draw a crucial distinction between private transgression and public critique — and to argue that society reserves its fiercest condemnation for the latter.

## Public vs. Private Transgression

Shaw's analysis of Shelley turns on a revealing comparison: "We find the austere Shelley denounced as a fiend in human form, whilst Nelson, who openly left his wife and formed a menage a trois with Sir William and Lady Hamilton, was idolized" [[004-002]].

The contrast is structural, not incidental. Nelson committed adultery openly — a private sin — and was celebrated as a national hero. Shelley wrote against marriage — a public challenge — and was "denounced as a fiend." Shaw's explanation: "Shelley might have had an illegitimate child in every county in England if he had done so frankly as a sinner. His unpardonable offence was that he attacked marriage as an institution" [[004-002]].

## The Attack on Property

The reason the institution matters more than individual conduct is, in Shaw's analysis, economic. "The attack on marriage is an attack on property; so that Shelley was something more hateful to a husband than a horse thief: to wit, a wife thief" [[004-003]].

Shelley's radicalism thus threatens not merely sexual morality but the entire property order on which Victorian society rested. The wife-as-property is the foundation; attack that, and the whole structure trembles.

## Shelley in the Play

Shaw embeds Shelley's example into the play's argument through the question of child custody. He notes that children can be taken from parents "because they hold Shelley's opinions" [[045-001]] — a hypothetical that in 1908 was not entirely hypothetical, given the use of moral and religious criteria in custody decisions.

## Legacy in Shaw's Argument

Shelley serves Shaw's argument as proof that society's stated morality (sexual fidelity) is not its actual morality (defense of property). The man who commits adultery in private is forgiven; the man who questions marriage in public is destroyed. The implication for Shaw's reform project is clear: honest public discussion of marriage will face fiercer resistance than private misconduct ever does.
