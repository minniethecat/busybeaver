# Alfred Bridgenorth (the Bishop)

Alfred Bridgenorth, the Bishop of Chelsea, is the patriarchal figurehead of Shaw's *Getting Married*. As both a bishop and a father, he embodies the dual authority — ecclesiastical and domestic — that Shaw subjects to sustained critique. His presence anchors the play's debate in the institutional Church, whose teachings on marriage are tested against the lived experience of his own family.

## Character Background

The Bishop presides over a household in Chelsea that doubles as the play's setting. His "favorite room" is the kitchen — a detail that Shaw uses to signal his democratic inclinations [[049-003]]. He is married to Edith, father to Leo, and brother to Reginald (the General). His ecclesiastical authority makes him the natural arbiter of the marriage questions the play raises — but Shaw systematically undermines every basis on which that authority might rest.

## Role in the Play

The Bishop's position is paradoxical: he is the highest religious authority present, yet he is personally implicated in the marriage crisis and professionally committed to doctrines he cannot honestly defend. Shaw treats the Bishop with more sympathy than one might expect — he is a decent man trapped in an indecent institution [[007-002]].
