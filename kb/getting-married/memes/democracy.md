# Democracy

A system of governance based on popular sovereignty. In *Getting Married*, Shaw engages democracy not as a form of government to be celebrated but as a practical method that can produce disastrous outcomes when misapplied — particularly when "the ignorance of its majorities" is allowed to dictate policy on sexual and marital questions.

## Shaw 的定义与用法

Shaw defines democracy against its populist caricature. "The modern notion that democracy means governing a country according to the ignorance of its majorities is never more disastrous than when there is some question of sexual morals to be dealt with" [013-001]. For Shaw, democracy properly understood is not rule by popular opinion but a system for identifying and implementing the collective will through skilled statesmanship.

He draws an important distinction between the statesman and the official: "Political Science means nothing else than the devizing of the best ways of fulfilling the will of the world; and, I repeat, it is skilled work. Once the way is discovered, the methods laid down, and the machinery provided, the work of the statesman is done, and that of the official begins" [014-001]. The democratic statesman's job is to devise — a creative, expert activity — not to mirror popular prejudice. This framing is essential to Shaw's case that marriage law reform requires expertise, not referenda.

## 在婚姻辩论中的位置

Democracy is not a central topic in *Getting Married* but functions as a methodological premise underlying Shaw's entire argument. His preface repeatedly implies that marriage law, as it stood in 1908, reflected the "ignorance of majorities" rather than the kind of rational, expert-driven reform he advocates. The divorce court's practice of requiring petitioners to prove fault — "the one question that should never be put to a petitioner for divorce is 'Why?'" [039-001] — is an example of democratic sentiment (the belief that marriage should be permanent, that divorce requires moral justification) overriding practical governance.

Shaw's invocation of "a democracy of supermen" [014-001] — the idea that skilled work requires skilled workers — connects democracy to his broader Fabian socialist commitments. The "vital need for a democracy of supermen" refers not to eugenic hierarchy but to the principle that democratic governance succeeds only when citizens and statesmen alike are educated to think beyond inherited prejudice.

## 历史 / 社会语境

Shaw wrote in the aftermath of the Third Reform Act (1884) and on the eve of the Parliament Act (1911), during a period when British democracy was expanding but still excluded women from the franchise. The "modern democracy" Shaw critiques was a system in which public opinion — shaped by newspapers, churches, and party politics — functioned as a de facto legislature on moral questions, while actual legislation lagged behind social change.

The American reference — "The American Constitution, one of the few modern political documents drawn up by men who were forced by the sternest circumstances to think out what they really had to face" [039-002] — reveals Shaw's preference for constitutional design over majoritarian improvisation. The Founders, in his view, thought seriously about governance because circumstances forced them to; British marriage law, by contrast, accumulated through precedent and sentiment rather than deliberate design.

## Shaw 的批评与替代

Shaw's critique of democracy-as-popular-opinion has specific implications for marriage reform:

1. **Moral questions are governance questions**: When "the conditions to which modern democracy commits us" [039-002] are applied to marriage, the result is that private relationships are governed by public sentiment — a category error that Shaw regards as the root of the problem.

2. **Expertise matters**: Just as a traffic policeman need not be wiser than the drivers he directs but must know the system of signals [014-001], marriage law requires expert design, not moral ratification by the majority.

3. **Rights precede legislation**: Shaw traces a line from the natural right to life to "many others" [039-002] — implying that personal rights (including the right to contract and dissolve marriage) derive from fundamental principles that democratic majorities cannot legitimately override.

Shaw's alternative is a Fabian model of expert governance applied to personal relations: the state should design institutions based on rational analysis of human needs and social consequences, not on majoritarian moral sentiment.
