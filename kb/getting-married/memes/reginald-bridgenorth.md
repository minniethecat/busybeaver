# Reginald Bridgenorth

Reginald Bridgenorth is the Bishop's brother in Shaw's *Getting Married*. His romantic situation — middle-aged, unhappily married, pursuing the unavailable Lesbia — dramatizes the human cost of the marriage system's rigidity. He embodies the predicament of those trapped in marriages they cannot leave.

## Character Background

Reginald is described as "in London, and wants to come" to the wedding [049-409]. At "Reginald's age, with a young wife," he represents the mismatch between legal permanence and human change [049-418]. The General's threat to "cut Reginald dead if I met him in the street" signals family tensions rooted in marital complications [049-439].

## Role in the Play

Reginald does not appear on stage — his presence is reported, not embodied — but his situation shapes the play's action. He is the absent center of a marital crisis that pulls other characters into conflict. Shaw uses him to show that even those physically absent from the drama are entangled in the marriage question.
