# Lesbia Grantham

Lesbia Grantham is the moral center of Bernard Shaw's *Getting Married* (1908). She is a woman of independent means who consistently refuses marriage proposals — not from hostility to men but from a principled rejection of the legal subordination that marriage entails. Through Lesbia, Shaw dramatizes his argument that a rational woman, offered the choice, would refuse marriage under existing laws.

## Character Background

Lesbia is the sister of Reginald Bridgenorth and sister-in-law to Edith. She is financially independent, intelligent, and thoroughly unsentimental about the institution of marriage. She enters the play "through the window" — a characteristic entrance that signals her refusal to follow conventional paths [[049-194]].

General Bridgenorth (her brother-in-law) has proposed to her repeatedly: "For the tenth and last time" she must refuse him [[049-246]]. He insists "We are two years older, Lesbia. I'm fifty: you are over forty" — as if age should drive her to accept [[049-249]]. She remains unmoved.

## Role in the Play

Lesbia functions as Shaw's mouthpiece for the feminist argument against marriage-as-enslavement. She refuses to become any man's property regardless of his personal qualities. Her rejection of the General is not personal — "You have never given me a moment of weakness" — but structural: no individual man, however admirable, can compensate for the institution's fundamental injustice [[049-253]].

When the General presses "Lesbia: tell me" and pleads "Don't say that, Lesbia," she holds firm [[049-256]], [[049-264]]. Her refusal dramatizes Shaw's argument that the problem is not bad husbands but a bad system — even the best of men cannot offer a marriage worth accepting under present laws.

## Lesbia and the Marriage Question

Lesbia embodies Shaw's most radical proposition: that a free woman would choose *not* to marry. She is not celibate from religious conviction, nor does she despise men. She simply calculates — correctly, in Shaw's view — that the legal disabilities of marriage outweigh any benefits. Her presence in the play forces the other characters (and the audience) to confront the possibility that refusal is rational.

## Dramatic Function

Shaw uses Lesbia as a dramatic foil to the other female characters: Edith (already married), Mrs George (the working-class intermediary), and Alice (the young romantic). Where they are caught in the marriage system, Lesbia stands outside it — and her position is shown to be the most dignified and defensible.

## Key Passages

> "For the tenth and last time" — Lesbia's repeated refusal of the General [[049-246]].

> "You have never given me a moment of weakness" — Lesbia to the General [[049-253]].

> "Don't say that, Lesbia" — the General's plea, which she ignores [[049-264]].
