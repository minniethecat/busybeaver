# General Bridgenorth

The General (Reginald's brother) is Shaw's portrait of masculine persistence misdirected. In *Getting Married*, he has proposed to Lesbia Grantham repeatedly — "for the tenth and last time" — and been refused each time [049-246]. His presence dramatizes the asymmetry between male desire and female autonomy under existing marriage law.

## Character Background

"General Bridgenorth is a well set up man of fifty" — a retired military officer of dignified bearing whose romantic life has been a series of failures [049-044]. He contrasts with his brother the Bishop: where the Bishop represents ecclesiastical authority, the General represents secular masculine authority — and both are equally impotent before the marriage question.

## Role in the Play

The General's function is to embody the pathos of the marriage system from the male perspective. He is not a villain; he genuinely admires Lesbia. But his proposals — framed as rescue, motivated by loneliness — reveal the coercive assumptions embedded in romantic pursuit. "We are two years older, Lesbia. I'm fifty: you are over forty" [049-249]. His logic — that age should make Lesbia more willing to accept — is precisely the logic Shaw critiques.

## Key Passages
