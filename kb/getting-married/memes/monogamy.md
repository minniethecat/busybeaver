# Monogamy

Bernard Shaw's analysis of monogamy in *Getting Married* is characteristically deflationary: he argues that the institution rests not on moral superiority or divine ordinance but on a simple demographic fact — men and women are born in roughly equal numbers. This materialist framing allows him to bypass theological debate and assess monogamy as a practical arrangement with specific costs and benefits.

## The Demographic Foundation

Shaw's key insight is that "the foundation of the institution of monogamy is not any inherent viciousness in polygyny or polyandry, but the hard fact that men and women are born in about equal numbers" [[018-001]]. This is not a moral argument but a numerical constraint: any other system would leave a significant portion of one sex without partners, creating social instability.

He extends this reasoning to explain cross-cultural variation: "other civilizations have not gravitated to monogamy, like the latter-day saints of Salt Lake City, because their polygyny is limited" [[020-001]]. When polygyny is capped by practical constraints (the Mohammedan four-wife limit), the system can function without catastrophic imbalance — though Shaw does not endorse it.

## The Defense of Monogamy — and Its Hidden Interests

Shaw wryly observes that "men are unanimous in defence of monogamy, the men because it excludes polyandry, and the women because it excludes polygyny" [[019-001]]. Each sex, he suggests, defends monogamy not from abstract principle but from a rational calculation of self-interest: men fear sharing wives; women fear sharing husbands.

This is Shaw at his most characteristic: he strips away moral rhetoric to reveal the material interests beneath. Monogamy is defended as sacred, but the motives are practical.

## Monogamy and the Marriage Crisis

Shaw argues that "monogamy will take care of itself provided any reasonable reform of the marriage laws is carried out" [[036-001]]. His point is that monogamy is not fragile — it is the default equilibrium of equal sex ratios. The real threat to marriage is not alternative arrangements but the cruelty and irrationality of the existing legal apparatus, which drives people away from marriage altogether.

## Relationship to Shaw's Broader Argument

Monogamy serves Shaw's larger argument as the baseline from which all marriage debate must proceed. He does not advocate for polygyny or polyandry as reforms — he merely uses them to expose the hollowness of moralistic defenses of monogamy. If monogamy is truly the best arrangement, it should be defensible on practical grounds without resort to sacerdotal claims of sacred indissolubility.
