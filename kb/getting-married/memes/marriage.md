# Marriage

The central institution under critique in Bernard Shaw's *Getting Married* (1908). Shaw treats marriage not as a sacred bond but as a legal contract riddled with contradictions — simultaneously compulsory yet inadequate, sacred yet commercial, romantic yet coercive. His analysis ranges across law, religion, economics, and biology to argue that the marriage system of Edwardian England fails all parties: men, women, children, and society.

## Shaw's Definition and Critique

Shaw distinguishes marriage — the actual living arrangement between partners — from the legal and religious apparatus surrounding it. He insists that "no subject is more dangerous to touch" because "the marriage law is inhuman and unreasonable to the point of downright abomination" [[001-001]]. The law, he argues, compels people into marriage regardless of suitability:

> "Whenever a man and woman are to be united for life, or separated by divorce, or relieved of their property, is contemplated, marriage is in effect compulsory upon all normal people" [[002-002]].

Shaw's fundamental challenge is that the institution rests on incompatible foundations. If marriage is for bliss, "then the very strongest reason for dissolving it is that it shall be disagreeable" [[004-001]]. If it is sacred and indissoluble, then entering it lightly is a desecration [[004-002]]. If it is about property, then "the attack on marriage is an attack on property," which Shaw concedes is the real driver of resistance to reform [[004-003]].

## The Case Against the Existing System

### The Compulsion Problem

Shaw argues that marriage is de facto compulsory because society offers no dignified alternative. "Marriage is in effect compulsory upon all normal people" because economic survival, social respectability, and even basic housing are conditioned upon it [[002-002]]. This compulsion distorts consent and makes the ceremony a farce.

### The Depopulation Paradox

Shaw observes that marriage, far from encouraging procreation, has begun to "depopulate the country" by making parenthood economically ruinous [[005-001]]. The burdens of supporting a family under the marriage system drive the most conscientious people to remain childless, while the least scrupulous reproduce without restraint.

### Religious Contradictions

Shaw traces the "religious revolt against marriage" back to early Christianity, noting that "Christianity took a high view of marriage as a means of multiplying souls for the Church," yet simultaneously celebrated celibacy as the higher state [[005-003]]. He skewers the inconsistency: if celibacy is the ideal, "then marriage is legalized impurity, a conclusion which is frankly accepted by thoroughgoing ascetics" [[023-001]].

### Monogamy, Polygyny, and the Numbers Problem

Shaw examines the demographic foundations of monogamy with characteristic irreverence. "The foundation of the institution of monogamy is not any inherent viciousness in polygyny or polyandry, but the hard fact that men and women are born in about equal numbers" [[018-001]]. He notes that "men are unanimous in defence of monogamy, the men because it excludes polyandry, and the women because it excludes polygyny" [[019-001]]. Yet the real issue is practical: polygyny would require limiting the number of wives per man, and "if we are to make polygyny a success, we must limit it" — a constraint few advocates contemplate [[020-002]].

## Historical and Social Context

Shaw writes in the aftermath of late-Victorian marriage reform debates, including the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 and the Deceased Wife's Sister Act. The Edwardian era saw mounting pressure to reform divorce laws, grant married women property rights, and address the cruelty of indissoluble unions. Shaw positions himself as a radical: "If marriage cannot be made to produce something better than we are producing now, our society will decay" [[007-001]].

He is acutely aware of class dimensions: working-class couples could not afford divorce; middle-class couples were trapped by respectability; and the wealthy could circumvent the law through private arrangements. The system, in Shaw's view, inflicted its worst cruelties on those least able to escape it.

## Shaw's Proposed Reforms

Shaw's reform agenda is characteristically sweeping:

1. **Make marriage a purely civil contract**, stripping away religious ceremony and sacerdotal claims of indissolubility [[025-001]].

2. **Separate marriage from parenthood** — the real legal obligation should attach to the child, not to the conjugal relationship [[022-001]]. "The condition of marriage now attached to legitimate parenthood" is the source of endless injustice.

3. **Establish easy divorce by mutual consent**, removing the requirement to prove fault. Shaw argues that "the reform of marriage, then, will be a very splendid and hazardous adventure," but necessary [[015-001]].

4. **Remove economic compulsion** — women must have independent means so that "shepherds get sheaves of offers of marriage" only when genuinely desired, not out of economic desperation [[007-002]].

Shaw is clear-eyed about the difficulty: "anyone who undertakes to deal with marriage will have to face an amazingly complicated public question" [[012-001]]. The issue touches property, religion, sex, children, and national identity — every reform provokes a different constituency.

## Shaw's Rhetorical Strategy

Shaw deploys several characteristic techniques: ironic inversion (treating marriage as a "trade" in which women are bought, [[026-002]]), historical comparison (juxtaposing Christian monogamy with Mohammedan polygyny, [[020-001]]), and biological framing (the sex ratio argument for monogamy). His preface functions as a legal brief, while the play that follows dramatizes the human consequences of the abstract arguments.

## Key Passages

> "If the mischief stopped at the law and the Church, it would be bad enough; but it spreads into every corner of our lives" [[001-001]].

> "The marriage ceremony is quite useless as a means of inducing people to keep their vows" [[025-001]].

> "Not by any reform of the marriage laws can this be dealt with" — Shaw concedes that law alone cannot solve the deeper social problem [[027-002]].
