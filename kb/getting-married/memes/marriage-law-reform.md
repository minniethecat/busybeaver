# Marriage Law Reform Movement

The movement to reform British marriage law provides the immediate political context for Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married*. Shaw writes at a moment when the existing marriage law is widely acknowledged to be "inhuman and unreasonable to the point of downright abomination" [[001-001]], yet reform remains politically impossible because no government is willing to confront the public opinion that sustains the status quo.

## Shaw's Diagnosis

Shaw opens his preface with a blunt assessment: "Because our marriage law is inhuman and unreasonable to the point of downright abomination, the bolder and more rebellious spirits form illicit unions" [[001-001]]. The law, in his view, is not merely imperfect but actively harmful — it drives people into illegal arrangements and punishes those who try to live honestly.

The root of the problem, Shaw argues, lies in the law's foundation. Marriage law is not based on either of the two principles that might justify it: "If we adopt the common romantic assumption that the object of marriage is bliss, then the very strongest reason for dissolving a marriage is that it shall be disagreeable to one or other or both of the parties. If we accept the view that the object of marriage is to provide for the production and rearing of children, then childlessness should be a conclusive reason for dissolution" [[004-001]].

Neither principle governs the actual law. Instead, the law's foundation is "the morality of the tenth commandment" — treating the wife as a man's chattel, alongside "his house, his ox, and his ass" [[004-001]].

## The Political Impasse

The reform movement faces a structural obstacle: "The mere mention of the marriage question makes a British Cabinet shiver with apprehension and hastily pass on to safer business" [[015-001]]. Shaw identifies a paradox at the heart of democratic reform: the electorate's views on marriage are "narrow, personal, jealous, and corrupt," while any serious reform requires "public, far-sighted, and impersonal" reasoning [[015-001]].

Yet Shaw insists that reform is inevitable. Just as "the snake does change its skin in spite of" popular resistance, "our marriage-law skin is causing discomfort to thoughtful people and will presently be cast whether the others are satisfied with it or not" [[004-003]].

## Divorce Reform

The specific reform Shaw advocates most urgently is the liberalization of divorce. In the longest section of the preface devoted to reform, he argues that the existing system of requiring proof of misconduct (adultery, cruelty, desertion) produces manufactured cases, public scandal, and injustice: "the only effect of compelling people to plead and prove misconduct is that cases are manufactured and clean linen purposely smirched and washed in public" [[047-001]].

Shaw proposes replacing the fault-based system with divorce on demand: "the sensible thing to do then is to grant divorce whenever it is desired, without asking why" [[047-001]].

## Broader Social Reform

Shaw is clear that marriage law reform is only one piece of a larger project. The economic dependence of women on men — which he calls "the compulsory sale of their persons, in marriage or otherwise" — must be addressed through "the general movement for the prevention of destitution" [[027-002]]. The Minority Report of the Royal Commission on the Poor Law, with its proposals for labor exchanges and economic independence, receives Shaw's endorsement as the necessary complement to legal reform [[047-001]].

## Key Passages

> "Because our marriage law is inhuman and unreasonable to the point of downright abomination" [[001-001]].

> "No statesman worth the name can possibly act on these views" [[012-001]].

> "The reform of marriage, then, will be a very splendid and very hazardous adventure for the Prime Minister who takes it in hand" [[015-001]].

> "The sensible thing to do then is to grant divorce whenever it is desired, without asking why" [[047-001]].
