# America

Throughout the preface to *Getting Married*, Bernard Shaw uses **America** as a comparative case study — a laboratory of social experiments in marriage, divorce, and sexual relations that illuminates the English situation by contrast. America appears as both a cautionary example and a source of reform ideas.

## American Divorce Laws

Shaw opens the preface with a reference to "the pleas on which American wives obtain divorces" ([003-002]), immediately establishing America as the jurisdiction where marriage law has been most thoroughly tested. The American experiment with liberal divorce statutes provides empirical evidence that Shaw uses to challenge English assumptions about the permanence of marriage.

The "experiments made by some of the American States" ([023-001]) in varying marriage and divorce laws serve Shaw's argument that marriage is a legislative construct, not a natural or divine constant. If different American states can have different marriage laws, then English law is a choice, not a necessity.

## Mormon Polygyny

Shaw devotes sustained attention to Mormon polygyny in Utah, arguing that it was "the men" not the women who attacked the practice ([019-001]). He uses the Mormon example to challenge the English assumption that women are natural monogamists: in Salt Lake City, he claims, women could "see and meet the ablest men" and preferred shared access to a capable husband over exclusive possession of a mediocre one ([020-001]).

This argument is characteristically Shavian — provocative, counter-intuitive, and designed to force the reader to re-examine assumptions about what is "natural" in marriage.

## Constitutional Contrast

Shaw invokes the American Constitution as "one of the few modern political documents drawn up by men who were forced by the sternest circumstances to think out what they really had to face" ([039-002]). The contrast with English marriage law — which Shaw presents as inherited rather than thought through — is explicit.

## America and Europe

Shaw groups America with Europe in observing that together they "have left us a century behind" ([034-001]) on marriage law reform. Even "prudish America" ([044-002]) is ahead of England in Shaw's account — a rhetorical move that uses American reputation for puritanism to shame English complacency.
