# Motherhood

One of Bernard Shaw's most radical proposals in *Getting Married* is the separation of motherhood from marriage. He argues that the condition of parenthood should be detached from the condition of matrimony — that the legal and economic obligations should flow to the child, not between the spouses. This reframing treats motherhood as a public profession deserving of state support rather than a private consequence of marriage.

## Motherhood as a Profession

Shaw proposes "making motherhood a real profession as it ought to be" — with payments, professional recognition, and social status comparable to any other skilled occupation [[016-002]]. He rejects the Victorian idealization of motherhood ("purity, home, motherhood, respectability, honor, decency") as a sentimental cover for exploitation [[027-002]]. The reality, he argues, is that mothers are unpaid laborers whose work is appropriated by husbands and the state.

## The Right to Refuse

Shaw extends his argument to the question of consent. A woman should have "no right to refuse motherhood" only if society meets its reciprocal obligation to support mothers and children [[049-1543]]. But society has not met that obligation; therefore a woman's refusal of motherhood is legitimate self-defense against an exploitative system.

## Separation from Marriage

The core of Shaw's argument is structural: "the condition of marriage now attached to legitimate parenthood" is the root of injustice [[022-001]]. By linking a child's legal status to its parents' marital status, the law punishes children for their parents' choices. Shaw proposes that the state's interest should attach directly to the child at birth, establishing paternal responsibility through registration rather than through the marriage contract.

## Economic Independence

Shaw's analysis of motherhood is inseparable from his analysis of women's economic dependence. As long as women need husbands to support children, they cannot freely choose marriage or motherhood. His solution — "a real profession" of motherhood with state payments — is designed to give women the economic independence that makes genuine consent possible.

## Key Passages

> "Making motherhood a real profession as it ought to be" [[016-002]].

> "The condition of marriage now attached to legitimate parenthood" is the problem [[022-001]].

> "No right to refuse motherhood" — conditional on society meeting its obligations [[049-1543]].
