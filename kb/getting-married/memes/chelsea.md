# Chelsea

Chelsea is the physical setting of Bernard Shaw's play *Getting Married*. The entire dramatic action unfolds in a single location: the Norman kitchen of the Palace of the Bishop of Chelsea on a spring morning in 1908. The choice of setting is not incidental — Shaw uses the architectural and social context of Chelsea to establish the world of his characters.

## The Setting

The play's opening stage directions describe the location in detail: "On a fine morning in the spring of 1908 the Norman kitchen in the Palace of the Bishop of Chelsea looks very spacious and clean and handsome and healthy" [[049-001]].

The Bishop's palace is a XII-century Norman building that has survived various architectural fashions. Shaw's description of the building's history doubles as a miniature history of English taste: the palace "has been lucky enough to escape being carved up into XV century Gothic, or shaved into XVIII century ashlar, or 'restored' by a XIX century builder and a Victorian architect with a deep sense of the umbrella-like gentlemanliness of XIV century vaulting" [[049-001]].

## Social Geography

Chelsea in 1908 was a London district undergoing social transformation — from a relatively modest area to an increasingly fashionable artistic and upper-middle-class neighborhood. The Bishop's residence there places the action at the intersection of ecclesiastical authority, upper-class domesticity, and the artistic-bohemian Chelsea that was emerging in the Edwardian period.

The Bishop (Alfred Bridgenorth) has used the Ecclesiastical Commissioners' funds to strip away Victorian additions and restore the Norman architecture: "he has got rid of the wall papers, the paint, the partitions, the exquisitely planed and moulded casings with which the Victorian cabinetmakers enclosed and hid the huge black beams of hewn oak" [[049-001]]. The restoration is a physical metaphor for the reformist impulse — stripping away accumulated convention to reveal something older, more honest, and more durable.

## Significance in the Play

Chelsea serves several dramatic functions:

1. **Confinement**: The single-setting format creates pressure-cooker intensity. Characters cannot leave; conflicts cannot be deferred by changing location.
2. **Status marker**: The Bishop's Palace signals institutional authority and genteel respectability — the very respectability Shaw's arguments about marriage are designed to unsettle.
3. **Historical depth**: The Norman architecture connects the play's contemporary marriage debate to centuries of accumulated law, custom, and church authority.
