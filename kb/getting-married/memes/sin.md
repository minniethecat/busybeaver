# Sin

In *Getting Married*, Bernard Shaw confronts the religious category of sin as it applies to marriage and sexual relations. He argues that the Church's classification of certain marital arrangements as "sinful" — while ignoring real social evils — has poisoned honest discussion of marriage reform. His treatment is characteristically irreverent: he does not deny the concept of sin but radically relocates it from private sexual conduct to public social injustice.

## The Misapplication of Sin

Shaw observes that the Church treats civilly married couples who have not undergone religious ceremony as "living in open sin" [[004-002]]. This classification, he argues, is absurd: the civil contract is legally valid and often more honest than religious marriages entered into lightly. By treating the ceremony rather than the substance of the relationship as the determinant of sin, the Church elevates form over content.

## Real Sin vs. Manufactured Sin

Shaw's deeper argument is that the focus on sexual sin distracts from genuine moral failures. The marriage system itself produces injustice — economic coercion, the punishment of children for their parents' status, the condemnation of women to dependency — yet these structural evils are rarely called "sin." Shaw would redirect the moral energy spent on policing sexual conduct toward reforming the institutions that produce human misery.

## Religious Hypocrisy

Shaw notes the inconsistency of a society that professes Christian morality while operating a marriage system that the early Church would have condemned. "All of them abominations in the sight of some sect or other," he observes of the various marriage forms recognized by law [[003-001]]. The proliferation of sects, each with its own list of sins, makes the category of sin politically unusable as a basis for law.

## Toward a Social Definition

Shaw's alternative is implicit throughout the preface: sin should be defined by its social consequences, not by theological categories. The real sin is not unconventional marital arrangements but the cruelty, poverty, and hypocrisy that the existing system produces. This reframing is consistent with his broader Fabian socialist ethics: morality is measured by human welfare, not by compliance with religious rules.
