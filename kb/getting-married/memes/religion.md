# Religion

Bernard Shaw's treatment of religion in *Getting Married* is both a critique and a proposal. He attacks the Christian Church for its inconsistent and often cruel influence on marriage law, but he does not dismiss religion itself. Instead, he calls for "building up a religion of our own out of our western inspiration and western sentiment" — a reformed faith that would support rather than obstruct rational marriage reform [[037-001]].

## The Borrowed Religion

Shaw's central historical claim is that Western marriage law suffers from having "borrowed our religion from the East" [[037-001]]. Eastern Christianity, with its ascetic ideals and suspicion of the body, bequeathed to Europe a religious framework fundamentally at odds with Western social realities. "The result is that we all believe that our religion is on its last legs, whereas the truth is that it is not yet born" [[037-001]].

This is a characteristic Shaw move: the problem is not too much religion but the wrong religion — one imported from a different civilization and ill-suited to modern Western life.

## The Minister's Dilemma

Shaw sympathetically portrays "the thoughtful and observant minister of religion" who "is troubled by the determination of his congregation not to apply their religion to their marriage" [[005-003]]. The clergy are caught between official doctrine and pastoral reality: they bless marriages they know to be imprudent and refuse to dissolve marriages they know to be dead. This institutional hypocrisy corrupts both religion and marriage.

## Religion as Obstacle and Resource

Shaw identifies religion as a primary obstacle to marriage reform: "leave your law to judges and your religion to bishops, and you will presently find yourself without either law or religion" [[046-010]]. The Church's claim to jurisdiction over marriage prevents the state from rationalizing the law.

Yet Shaw also sees religion as a potential resource. The character who says "as her religion is love, her children shall be fully acknowledged" represents a reformed religious sensibility that could support rather than oppose humane marriage arrangements [[032-002]].

## The Unborn Religion

Shaw's most striking image is of a "religion not yet born, though the age walks visibly pregnant with it" [[037-001]]. This religion would be built from Western experience — democratic, rational, focused on human welfare rather than ascetic denial. It would support marriage as a human institution subject to human improvement, not as a divine mystery beyond criticism.
