# Parliament

Parliament appears in Bernard Shaw's preface to *Getting Married* primarily through its absence — as the institution that ought to reform marriage law but will not. Shaw's analysis of Parliament is a study in democratic paralysis: the body that has the legal power to act is the body most structurally prevented from using it.

## Political Paralysis

Shaw's most vivid image of parliamentary inaction comes early in the preface: "The mere mention of the marriage question makes a British Cabinet shiver with apprehension and hastily pass on to safer business" [[015-001]]. The Cabinet is not, strictly, Parliament — but it is the executive committee of the parliamentary majority, and its "shiver" reflects the electoral calculus that governs all parliamentary action on marriage.

The reason for the paralysis is structural. Parliamentarians are elected by constituencies whose views on marriage are "narrow, personal, jealous, and corrupt" [[015-001]], while any serious reform requires reasoning that is "public, far-sighted, and impersonal." The electoral incentive is to avoid the question entirely — and Parliament obliges.

## Reform by Extension Only

When Parliament does act on marriage, Shaw observes, it acts only to extend marriage — allowing more people to marry — never to liberalize exit from marriage. The "recent long-delayed Act legalizing marriage with a deceased wife's sister" exemplifies this pattern [[012-001]]. Parliament will eventually permit what public pressure demands, but only in the direction of more marriage, never less.

"When a reform in the other direction is needed (for example, an extension of divorce), not even the existence of the most unbearable hardships will induce our statesmen to move" [[012-001]].

## Democratic Irony

Shaw draws an ironic contrast between Parliament's formal democratic character and its actual behavior. "We may be enthusiastic Liberals or Conservatives without any hope of seats in Parliament, knighthoods, or posts in the Government, because party politics do not make the slightest difference in our daily lives" [[022-001]].

The observation cuts deep: people can hold passionate party loyalties precisely because party politics is irrelevant to their actual lives. But when it comes to marriage — something that intimately affects daily life — the same voters will punish any politician who touches it. Parliament thus reflects popular will accurately enough on the one subject where popular will is most harmful.

## The Reform Adventure

Shaw's challenge to Parliament is direct: "The reform of marriage, then, will be a very splendid and very hazardous adventure for the Prime Minister who takes it in hand" [[015-001]]. He predicts that any reforming premier "will be posted on every hoarding and denounced in every Opposition paper" [[015-001]]. Yet the reform "cannot be put off for ever" [[015-001]].

> "No Government will touch the marriage question if it can possibly help it, even when there is a demand for the extension of marriage" [[012-001]].
