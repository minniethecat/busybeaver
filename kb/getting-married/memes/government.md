# Government

In *Getting Married* (1908), Bernard Shaw treats **Government** and the **State** as indispensable but deeply flawed actors in the regulation of marriage. The preface and play together advance a paradoxical argument: the State has simultaneously failed to take marriage seriously enough — treating it as a religious sacrament rather than a civil contract — and overreached by enforcing permanent monogamous bonds that the State itself lacks the competence to adjudicate.

## The Abdication Thesis

Shaw's central charge is that Government has abdicated its responsibility for rational marriage law. The preface notes that existing marriage law is "one on which no sane Government could act for a moment" ([012-001]), yet Governments continue to enforce it because the alternative — open political debate about sex, property, and child-rearing — is considered too dangerous.

The play's characters echo this critique. **Sinjon Hotchkiss** observes that "Governments refuse to make these contracts" ([049-1471]) — referring to the individualized marriage contracts Shaw proposes — because doing so would require acknowledging that marriage is a civil matter, not a sacrament.

## State Marriage vs. Real Marriage

The General's remark that "State marriage is already divorced from religious marriage" ([049-1488]) captures a key Shaw theme: the legal fact of civil marriage exists alongside a cultural refusal to treat it as genuinely civil. The result is a hybrid institution that has the enforceability of law without the rationality of legislation.

Shaw proposes that the State should register marriages as it registers births and deaths — as matters of public record, not moral judgment. "Such registration is marriage," he writes in the preface ([003-002]), reducing marriage to its administrative essence.

## The Government's Dilemma

The play dramatizes the Government's structural incapacity to reform marriage law. When the General mentions that "the Government sounded you as to whether" reform was possible ([049-1481]), he touches on the political reality: no Government wants to be seen as undermining the family, so even modest reforms are deferred indefinitely.

Shaw identifies a class dimension to this paralysis: the propertied classes resist reform because marriage law protects inheritance, while "no Government would dare to interfere" with their domestic arrangements ([049-936]). The working classes, whose marriages are least protected by property, have the least political voice.

## Labor Exchanges for Wives

One of Shaw's most striking proposals is that the State should establish employment exchanges for domestic work — treating wives and mothers as workers whose labor has economic value. He asks "the Government of the day (1910)" whether it has considered the "unemployed wives and mothers" who "must find new places in the labor market" if marriage is reformed ([041-001]).

This proposal connects Government to the economic analysis that runs through the preface: if marriage is a labor relation, then the State's role is not to sanctify it but to regulate its terms — minimum standards, exit conditions, and recognition of the work performed.
