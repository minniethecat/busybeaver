# Jacqueline Jeannin

Jacqueline Jeannin, nee Langeais, is Olivier Jeannin's wife and one of the most complex secondary figures in Romain Rolland's *Jean-Christophe*. Daughter of a wealthy Parisian bourgeois family, her marriage to the idealistic poet-intellectual Olivier represents Rolland's anatomy of a union doomed by class incompatibility, the corrosive power of wealth, and the fatal gap between romantic idealism and sensual reality. She is not a villain: Rolland presents her as a victim of her own nature, her heredity, and her husband's inability to hold her -- "She was a better creature than the people she lived with: and for that reason she did worse" (029-766).

## Biography

### Background and Youth

Jacqueline Langeais is the only daughter of a wealthy and fashionable Parisian family. Her father, M. Langeais, is a successful man of the world -- charming, clever, and wholly absorbed in his own affairs. Her mother, Madame Langeais, is a society woman whose primary occupation is the care of her imaginary illnesses and her discreet social intrigues. Neither parent sacrifices personal convenience for the child; she is left largely alone to amuse herself with "her thousand and one fancies" (029-106).

Her childhood is marked by precocious sensuality. By six years old she is telling her dolls love-stories whose characters feature "husband, wife, and lover" (029-106). Rolland emphasizes that she "saw no harm in it" -- there was "a strain of innocent sensuality, which rang out in the distance like the sound of invisible bells" (029-106). She attended a fashionable school with her friend Simone Adam, where the pair wove romantic fantasies about actors, authors, and musicians, "always wanting to fall in love, to have their lives filled with a love-affair" (029-112).

### Aunt Marthe: The Lost Moral Anchor

The one stabilizing influence in Jacqueline's youth is her father's sister, Marthe Langeais. Tall, dressed always in black, with "a sort of stiff distinction of feature and movement," Marthe is a woman of penetrating intelligence and stoic self-denial who "might have formed interesting friendships with certain distinguished men and women" but preferred solitude (029-117, 029-118). She is the only adult who notices Jacqueline's adolescent turmoil: "the child, sinking and not daring to cry for help, found only her Aunt Marthe standing by her side and holding out her hand" (029-119).

Marthe is dying of cancer, which Jacqueline discovers only gradually. Her death, coinciding with Jacqueline's discovery of her mother's probable infidelity (029-175, 029-177), leaves the girl "horribly deserted and alone" -- "Marthe's death came at the same time as the very worst hours of her moral distress" (029-174). Jacqueline's religious faith, already nominal, is shattered by the contrast between her mother's hypocritical piety and her aunt's honest unbelief. She "needed the support of a religion" but found none (029-174).

### Courtship by Olivier

Jacqueline first meets Jean-Christophe Krafft at one of her mother's fashionable receptions. He is the musician of the hour, and she finds his bluntness and noisy gaiety offensive: "There was too much sunlight in Christophe" (029-180). But Christophe persistently talks about his friend Olivier, and through his accounts Jacqueline "felt a secret emotion in thus catching a glimpse of a soul so much in accordance with her ideas" (029-181).

When Olivier visits, he hardly speaks -- but "he did not need to speak. His intelligent eyes, his smile, his refined manners, the tranquillity that was in and inundated by his personality, could not but attract Jacqueline" (029-182). Both Christophe and Olivier fall in love with her, but when Jacqueline realizes Olivier returns her feelings, she becomes cold and hostile: "Love, with which till then she had only played, love, to which she had so often called, was there, before her eyes: she saw it gaping before her like an abyss, and she flung back in terror" (029-203).

The turning point comes in the garden of the Langeais villa near the forest of Isle-Adam. Walking in the "gray and cold" June weather, through gooseberry bushes and strawberry beds, Jacqueline weeps silently and Olivier asks if she hates him. She shakes her head no. "And suddenly they kissed. O boundless happiness! Religious happiness! So sweet and so profound that it is almost sorrow!" (029-216). Under the trees, "Olivier kissed Jacqueline's wet hair: she turned her face up to him, and, for the first time, he felt loving lips against his, a girl's lips, warm and parted a little. They were nigh swooning" (029-233).

### The Marriage Struggle

The engagement provokes fierce opposition from M. Langeais, whose paternal jealousy -- "that strange pure affection which many fathers feel for their daughters" -- makes him suspect and resist (029-250). In the resulting conflict, Jacqueline's violent will emerges: she threatens suicide if denied her marriage, declaring "Very well. We will kill ourselves" (029-255). Christophe, acting as intermediary, eventually persuades M. Langeais by reporting this threat (029-257, 029-259).

The wedding is a civil ceremony -- neither the "indifferent Olivier nor the rebellious Jacqueline" wished a religious one. Jacqueline furtively puts out her tongue at her friend Simone: "she had made a bet with her that being 'married' would not affect her in the least, and it looked as though she would win it" (029-271). The couple leave for Italy that evening, looking "like a boy" and "like a little girl" (029-272).

The marriage begins in provincial France, where Olivier takes a modest teaching post. For a time, love sustains them: "They could not bear to be out of each other's sight" (029-332). But soon an "indefinable feeling of emptiness, a vague weariness not without a certain charm, came over them" (029-335). Jacqueline "was often in tears without any cause for weeping" (029-335). Work bores her -- "She persuaded herself that it stood in the way of her love: no doubt because her love had already become less ardent" (029-332).

### The Destructive Power of Wealth

The marriage's delicate balance is shattered by an inheritance. A sister of Madame Langeais dies, doubling Jacqueline's fortune. When she "came in for her legacy, Olivier remembered what Christophe had said about money" (029-365). Jacqueline laughs at his concerns, but soon complains they are "not well enough off" (029-369). They move to a larger flat: "Instead of the cozy little rooms, all radiant with her spirit... they took an enormous, comfortable, well-arranged flat which they did not, could not, love, where they were bored to death" (029-370).

The transformation accelerates. Jacqueline patronizes great dressmakers, dismisses the family sempstress, travels, and "very quickly caught the color of these sterile and rotten men and women" (029-370). Her real character "broke through the veils of idealism in which in girlish bashful modesty she had enshrouded herself" -- she reaches her mother's state of mind, practicing religion "punctiliously and indifferently" (029-375). Rolland's judgment is devastating: "She was neither angel nor brute. She was just a poor bored woman" (029-375).

### The Child and the Collapse

Jacqueline and Olivier experience a brief reconciliation after M. Langeais's death. A pilgrimage to the places of their honeymoon "softened her and filled her with tenderness" (029-729). Jacqueline becomes pregnant, but the child brings no salvation. She is unable to nurse; a wet nurse is procured who "was like the real mother" (029-747). A "slight attack of phlebitis" leaves her bedridden, and she comes to resent the child: "she thought herself crippled for life: and there rose in her a dumb, harsh, and bitter rancor... against the innocent cause of her illness" (029-749).

When Jacqueline emerges from illness, "physically stronger, plumper, and rejuvenated -- but morally she was more ill than ever," the last ties of thought binding her to Olivier are broken (029-752). She finds Olivier's letters to Cecile Fleury and interprets them as romantic betrayal (029-759). "To her the betrayal was not so much in the act as in the thought and intention. She would have found it easier to forgive the man she loved for taking a mistress than for secretly giving his heart to another woman" (029-759).

### Desertion and Aftermath

Jacqueline falls in love with "a philanderer, a Parisian writer, who was neither young nor handsome, a man who was heavy, red-faced, dissipated, with bad teeth, absolutely and terribly heartless" (029-765). She "flung herself into it headlong with desperate frankness" (029-766), abandoning both husband and child. "After Jacqueline's flight, Christophe had thought that Olivier would come back" (032-006), but Olivier cannot resume their old life together.

The affair collapses rapidly: "She had wearied of her lover more quickly than her lover had done of her" (034-028). She returns "broken, disgusted, aged," with "the too flagrant scandal of her adventure" having "closed many doors to her" -- even her own mother is "so offensive and so contemptuous that Jacqueline had found it impossible to stay with her" (034-028). Olivier's death, in a street riot, is "the last blow" (034-028).

### Later Years

In the novel's final volume, Jacqueline has "lived a very worthy life," withdrawn from society and devoted to her son Georges (041-004). But her efforts at motherhood are erratic -- "alternate periods of exalted tenderness and weary indifference" (041-005). When Georges grows into a man and distances himself, "Jacqueline's whole existence crumbled away" again (041-014).

Unable to "live nor die without love," she falls under the sway of Sister Angele, a charismatic nun whose "strangely enticing mixture of lofty mysticism and lawyer's cunning" (041-016) exploits Jacqueline's desperate need for attachment. She gives up "her money, her will, her heart" (041-016) to the nun. Eventually, through shame and pride, she cannot bring herself to see Christophe again, and communicates only through letters -- "a short letter filled with restrained emotion" (040-193).

## Role in the Novel

Jacqueline's primary function in the novel is to embody the destructive collision between idealism and sensuality, between the life of the mind and the life of the body. She loved Olivier for "all the qualities which made him different from herself and the world she lived in: because he was poor, because he was uncompromising in his moral ideas, because he was awkward and shy in society" (029-199). That love was genuine but unsustainable: "she had only believed in that endeavor, that faith, while they were gilded by the sun of love: and as the sun died down she saw them as barren, gloomy mountains standing out against the empty sky" (029-380).

Rolland presents Jacqueline's tragedy as both personal and structural. She is the product of a particular class and a particular kind of family -- her mother's frivolity and her father's self-absorption are replicated in her own life. Olivier sees "a form of heredity which was stronger than her will, her mother's soul reappearing in her: he saw her falling like a stone down to the depths of the stock from which she came" (029-753). At the same time, Rolland insists she is not wicked: "Poor Jacqueline was quite innocent, as innocent in drifting apart from Olivier as Olivier was in not holding her. She was what Nature had made her" (029-379).

## Relationships

### With Olivier Jeannin

The marriage is the novel's most extended study of incompatibility between a sensitive intellectual and a sensual bourgeois woman. Olivier's idealism attracts her but cannot hold her; her sensuality attracts him but exhausts him. After the separation, Olivier acknowledges his share of responsibility: "She had trusted herself to him: how had he dealt with his trust? If he was not strong enough to make her happy, why had he bound her to himself?" (029-870).

### With Christophe

Their relationship is complex. Initially hostile, she comes to flirt with him during the period of her marriage's dissolution, and at one point kisses him in the dark during a walk in the garden: "Suddenly he felt her hands on his, her lips on his. He held her close... Their lips parted; they wrenched away from each other" (029-637). Christophe flees the house the next morning. In the final volume, shame prevents them from meeting.

### With Aunt Marthe

The only relationship Jacqueline can look back on without bitterness. Marthe's stoic kindness and intelligence stand as the moral counterweight to everything the Langeais household represents, and her death removes Jacqueline's last moral compass.

### With Georges Jeannin

Her son becomes the sole object of her love after the marriage's collapse. She "poured all her need of love" into him but cannot make him love her in return, repeating the pattern of her earlier relationships: needing love desperately but lacking the capacity to make it sustainable.

## Key Events

1. Childhood in the wealthy Langeais household -- described as "a little fair girl, charmingly slender" with "the Parmigianninesque smile of a pure faun" (029-103).
2. Adolescent crisis and the loss of Aunt Marthe to cancer, leaving her morally adrift (029-164, 029-174).
3. Discovery of her mother's infidelity, which shatters her last illusions about her family (029-175, 029-177).
4. First meeting with Christophe and, through him, Olivier (029-180, 029-182).
5. The declaration in the garden at Isle-Adam and first kiss (029-201, 029-216, 029-233).
6. Suicide threat to force her father's consent to the marriage (029-255, 029-259).
7. Civil marriage ceremony and departure for Italian honeymoon (029-271, 029-272).
8. Provincial life: the slow discovery that love alone cannot sustain a marriage (029-335, 029-336).
9. The inheritance that doubles her fortune and accelerates the marriage's collapse (029-365, 029-370).
10. Birth of her son and the resentment that follows physical illness (029-735, 029-749).
11. Discovery of Olivier's letters to Cecile, which she interprets as betrayal (029-759).
12. The fixed idea: falling in love with a worthless philanderer and deserting husband and child (029-765, 029-766, 032-006).
13. Return after the affair's collapse, "broken, disgusted, aged" (034-028).
14. Later life in Volume X: devotion to Georges, loss of his affection, and surrender to Sister Angele's influence (041-004, 041-005, 041-015, 041-016).

## Themes and Significance

Jacqueline is Rolland's most fully realized portrait of the tragic bourgeois woman: intelligent enough to recognize her emptiness, sensual enough to crave fulfillment, but formed by an environment that offers her no resources for genuine life. Her trajectory from romantic idealism to cynical materialism recapitulates her mother's life in compressed form.

Her story is also Rolland's meditation on the incompatibility of the intellectual and the sensual temperaments. Olivier cannot give Jacqueline what she needs because he does not understand what that is; she cannot receive what he offers because she has no vocabulary for it. Their tragedy is mutual -- "Jacqueline also was a victim: -- she was his victim" (029-870).

Finally, she embodies the destructive power of unanchored sexuality: "She had the dangerous merit of always being frank with herself and of never shirking the consequences of her own actions" (029-766). Her frankness destroys what her hypocrisy might have preserved, and her honesty makes her, in Rolland's paradoxical judgment, both "a better creature than the people she lived with" and a force for devastation.
