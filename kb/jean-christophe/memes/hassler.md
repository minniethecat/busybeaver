# Hassler

## Biography / 生平

Francois Marie Hassler is a young German composer of prodigious talent who first comes to Jean-Christophe's attention when the boy is six years old. Hassler's first opera leaves the child in a state of rapture, and from that moment the composer becomes the fixed star of Jean-Christophe's inner sky. Hassler is staying as the guest of the Grand Duke when Jean-Christophe meets him; he conducts a concert of his own compositions, and afterward the boy is presented to him by his grandfather Jean Michel (004-025, 004-030). Hassler takes to the child warmly, kissing him, teasing him, and promising that one day, when Jean-Christophe has become a good musician, he should come to Berlin: "I shall make something of you" (004-031).

Years later, when Jean-Christophe is a struggling young composer isolated in his provincial town, he remembers Hassler's promise and journeys across Germany to see him. By this time Hassler is famous throughout Germany and serves as Kapellmeister in a great northern city (017-004, 017-005). But the man Jean-Christophe finds is not the hero of his childhood memory. Hassler has grown bald, stout, yellowish, and sleepy-looking (017-014). He has drifted into a life of lazy indulgence, surrounded by a coterie of decadent writers and critics whose adulation has made him too pleased with himself (017-006). His art, though still touched by flashes of genius, has been compromised by his comfort and cynicism. The disillusionment of this encounter becomes one of the formative experiences of Jean-Christophe's young manhood (017-072).

## Role in the Novel / 小说角色

Hassler serves as a symbol of idealized artistic greatness and, later, of the corrupting power of fame. For the young Jean-Christophe, Hassler represents everything he wants to become: a creator of beautiful things, a great man who commands the adoration of audiences. The brilliant meteor of Hassler's visit has a decisive influence on Jean-Christophe's mind: "All his childhood Hassler was the model on which his eyes were fixed, and to follow his example the little man of six decided that he also would write music" (004-048).

When Jean-Christophe is later drowning in the mediocrity of provincial life, he instinctively turns to the memory of Hassler as a lifeline, believing that the great man will understand him as one free spirit to another (017-003). The subsequent meeting and disillusionment thus serves a crucial narrative function: it teaches Jean-Christophe that artistic reputation and artistic integrity are not the same thing, and that even the most gifted artists can be destroyed by comfort and flattery.

Hassler appears or is referenced in multiple volumes: first in the childhood section (Volume 1, Chapter 4), then in Jean-Christophe's young adulthood as a critic defending him (Volume 7, Chapter 15), in a bitter comparison with the old musician Schulz (Volume 8, Chapter 17), and finally as the man who might have saved Jean-Christophe from his provincial prison but refused to help (017-309).

## Relationships / 人际关系

**Jean-Christophe Krafft**: Hassler is the boy's idol and later the target of his greatest disillusionment. Jean-Christophe's love for the composer is total and naive: "Ah, I would die for him---die for him!" he thinks after their first meeting (004-047). Decades later, when Jean-Christophe lays his hand on Hassler's and says with love, "There is myself!" --- meaning he is someone who understands --- Hassler cannot reciprocate; his eyes remain dull, his response an ironic little bow (017-064, 017-066).

**Jean Michel Krafft**: Jean-Christophe's grandfather had been one of the first to perform Hassler's works, and Hassler acknowledges him with flattering words at the serenade reception (004-030).

**The Grand Duke**: Hassler's patron during his visit, hosting him at the Palace and appearing with him at the window during the torchlit serenade (004-029).

**His Coterie**: Hassler is surrounded by writers, painters, and decadent critics who represent the party of revolt against reaction (017-006). Their adulation has been fatal to him, making him too pleased with himself and contributing to his artistic decline.

## Key Events / 关键事件

**First Hearing (004-025)**: The young Jean-Christophe learns that Hassler, the author of the opera that so moved him, will visit his town and conduct a concert. He lives only in the hope of seeing him.

**The Concert (004-028)**: Hassler appears on stage to loud applause. His music is a quivering, jerky reflection of his nervous temperament, and Jean-Christophe, overwhelmed, cannot keep still in his seat.

**The Serenade and Meeting (004-029--004-043)**: After the concert, Jean Michel takes the boy to serenade Hassler at the Palace. Inside, Jean-Christophe is presented to the master, who kisses him, teases him, fills his pockets with cake, and invites him to Berlin when he becomes a musician.

**Jean-Christophe's Defense of Hassler (015-230)**: As a young critic, Jean-Christophe bombards a colleague who has attacked "the most gifted of living composers, the most advanced representative of the new school, Hassler, the writer of programme symphonies, extravagant in truth, but full of genius."

**The Disillusioning Visit (017-003--017-072)**: Jean-Christophe travels across Germany to see Hassler. The man he meets is physically changed --- bald, stout, yellowish --- and spiritually diminished. Hassler does not remember him, eats breakfast during the visit, mocks the young man's hopes, dismisses his own works as "hog-waste," and offers no encouragement. Christophe leaves absolutely crushed.

**The Missed Letter (017-077)**: Unknown to Christophe, Hassler had a change of heart after the visit and sent a letter with an opera ticket to his hotel, regretting his coldness. Christophe had already departed.

**Schulz's Contrast (017-192)**: The old musician Schulz, shut up in a provincial town almost all his life, is contrasted with Hassler, "young, famous, in the very thick of the artistic movement," yet interested in nothing and unwilling to know anything --- a devastating judgment on the corruption of fame.

**Final Abandonment (017-309)**: "Hassler, the only man who could have saved him, had refused to help him." This sentence captures the moral weight of Hassler's failure.

## Themes & Significance / 主题与意义

Hassler embodies the novel's meditation on the relationship between artistic genius and moral character. He is truly gifted --- even the disillusioned Jean-Christophe can see flashes of genius in his music (017-053). But genius without character is fragile. Hassler's Southern German nature, "soft and indolent, not made to resist excess of fortune or misfortune," drifts into lazy enjoyment: "He loved good food, heavy drinking, idle lounging, and sensuous thoughts" (017-006).

The encounter with Hassler is one of the great lessons of Jean-Christophe's education: that hero-worship is dangerous, that fame does not equal integrity, and that the true artist must wage an "ungrateful, inevitable battle" against the world without laying down his arms (017-007). Hassler has surrendered; Jean-Christophe must not.

Hassler also serves a structural function in the novel, appearing first as a distant ideal, then as a living disappointment, and finally as a warning. The contrast drawn between Hassler and old Schulz (017-192) is particularly telling: Schulz, confined to a provincial town, keeps his spirit alive through curiosity and love; Hassler, with all the freedom of fame and travel, is "interested in nothing and unwilling to know anything."

The tragedy of Hassler is that he is not unaware of his decay: "No one was more conscious than himself of his decay. In truth, he was the only one to be conscious of it --- at rare moments which, naturally, he avoided" (017-006). This self-awareness, and his choice to avoid it, makes him more than a simple villain. He is a cautionary figure: the artist who stops fighting.

> "It was Hassler, and yet it was not he. He still had his great smooth brow, his face as unwrinkled as that of a babe; but he was bald, stout, yellowish, sleepy-looking; his lower lip drooped a little, his mouth looked bored and sulky." (017-014)

> "He loved good food, heavy drinking, idle lounging, and sensuous thoughts. His whole art smacked of these things, although he was too gifted for the flashes of his genius not still to shine forth from his lax music which drifted with the fashion." (017-006)

> "\"Hog-waste!\" he said. \"That is what these swine want. Do you think there are ten people in the world who love music? Is there a single one?\"" (017-058)
