# Grand Journal

The *Grand Journal* is the Paris newspaper that transforms Jean-Christophe Krafft from an obscure German composer into a public celebrity -- and just as quickly discards him. Edited by Arsene Gamache, the paper represents the industrialization of reputation in modern Paris: a machine that makes and unmakes artists according to its own commercial and political interests, without any genuine engagement with their work.

## Role in the Novel

The *Grand Journal* enters the narrative suddenly, when a journalist invades Christophe's bedroom one morning to interview him about an article that has just appeared. "It was a journalist, who, finding the door open, had entered without ceremony. Christophe was furious, and jumped out of bed: 'What the devil are you doing here?' he shouted. He grabbed his pillow to hurl it at the intruder" (026-xxx). The article, which Christophe has not even read, declares him "the greatest musical genius of the age" -- based on almost no knowledge of his actual work.

The article was orchestrated by Christophe's friend Olivier Jeannin, who, "who could do nothing to advance his own interests, and had a horror of notoriety, and avoided journalists like the plague, took quite another view of these things where his friend was in question" (026-xxx). Olivier had quietly fed information to the paper, setting in motion a machine that "once it got going, it was impossible to direct or control" (026-xxx).

The *Grand Journal* constructs a fabricated version of Christophe: "a Republican genius -- the great musician of democracy" (026-xxx). The article knew nothing of his actual compositions but invented his political views, using him as a cudgel against other French musicians. In Paris, when reading such praise, "it is always as well to ask yourself: 'Whom is he decrying?'" (026-xxx).

## The Gamache Regime

Arsene Gamache, the editor, is a self-made press baron who believes he has proprietary rights over the artists he promotes. "He took it as a matter of course that their fame should be associated with his own, much as Louis XIV grouped Moliere, Le Brun, and Lulli about his throne" (029-xxx). A coarse businessman with no knowledge of art, Gamache "could not tolerate the existence of anything he did not like: he decreed that it was bad and pernicious: and he would ruin it in the public interest" (029-xxx).

Rolland's indictment is devastating: "It is both comic and terrible to see such coarse-grained uncultivated men of affairs presuming to control not only politics and money, but also the mind, and offering it a kennel with a collar and a dish of food, or, if it refuses, having the power to let loose against it thousands of idiots whom they have trained into a docile pack of hounds" (029-xxx).

Christophe, incapable of gratitude for patronage he never sought, quickly alienates Gamache by refusing to write music for a commercial libretto promoted by the paper. "Christophe had contrived to make enemies of his patrons of the *Grand Journal*, as was only likely. Christophe had been endowed by Heaven with the virtue extolled by Goethe: *non-gratitude*" (029-xxx).

## The Fate of Manufactured Fame

The *Grand Journal* episode follows the arc of all manufactured celebrity. For a fortnight, Christophe is the subject of intense press attention; his indiscretions at a champagne lunch with Gamache are published, his correcting letter is suppressed. Journalists fabricate a description of his room and a conversation that never happened (026-xxx). Then, as suddenly as it began, "it was all over in a fortnight. The newspapers gave up talking about him" (026-xxx).

When the campaign ends, Christophe learns what his fame actually means: "When his name was mentioned, people said, not: 'The author of *David* or *Gargantua*,' but: 'Oh yes! The *Grand Journal* man!'" (026-xxx). His identity has been reduced to his association with a newspaper that neither understood nor cared about his art.

After Christophe's break with Gamache, the *Grand Journal* turns on him with a vengeance, accusing him of plagiarism and mobilizing nationalist resentment. "They used every possible weapon. They dragged out once more the old pettifogging engine of war which has always served the impotent against creative men" (029-xxx). The paper that made him now works to destroy him.

## Significance

The *Grand Journal* represents the commercialization of art criticism in the modern metropolis. It is the antithesis of everything Christophe stands for: integrity, artistic truth, the slow and difficult labor of genuine creation. Through it, Rolland anatomizes the relationship between money, power, and culture in Paris -- a city where a newspaper editor can make or break an artist "much as Louis XIV grouped Moliere, Le Brun, and Lulli about his throne" (029-xxx), and where an artist's actual work matters less than the label the press attaches to his name.
