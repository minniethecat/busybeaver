# Schulz

**Peter Schulz** is the elderly *Musikdirektor* of a small Rhenish town, a seventy-five-year-old organist and music teacher who becomes one of [[jean-christophe|Jean-Christophe Krafft's]] earliest and most touching admirers. In a novel populated by critics, rivals, and indifferent audiences, Schulz stands out as the embodiment of pure, selfless musical love — an old man whose lifelong devotion to art finds its vindication in the discovery of Christophe's compositions.

## A Life of Quiet Devotion

Schulz is introduced in Volume 4 (*La Révolte*) as the "Musikdirektor Professor Dr. Peter Schulz" [016-402], a title whose formality belies the warmth of the man. At seventy-five, he has spent his life in obscurity, serving the musical needs of a small community:

> Peter Schulz was seventy-five. He had always lived in the little Rhenish town, a modest, hard-working musician, organist of the principal church, music teacher at the Gymnasium, and conductor of the local choral society. [017-081]

His career has been one of uncomplaining service. He taught generations of children, conducted innumerable Sunday services, and organized town concerts that "nobody attended" [017-082]. Yet Schulz never grew bitter — he retained a childlike capacity for wonder that the novel treats with deep tenderness.

## The Discovery of Christophe's Music

Schulz's encounter with Christophe's compositions is rendered as a moment of revelation. Having received a copy of Christophe's *Lieder* from a publisher's catalog, the old man sits alone in his house on a snowy evening and experiences something approaching ecstasy:

> And so old Schulz listened, in the silence of his little house buried in the snow. He read the *Lieder*, singing them softly under his breath, stopping after each one to exclaim with wonder and joy. He forgot the cold, he forgot his solitude. [017-083]

> To old Schulz they were a whole world suddenly opening. He was alone in his house, the snow was falling outside, yet he was no longer alone: the music filled every corner with warm presence. [017-084]

This passage is significant. It establishes the novel's recurring theme of [[music|music]] as a force that transcends isolation, age, and the indifference of the world. For Schulz, who "had not seen anybody all day" [017-085], the encounter with Christophe's *[[lied|Lieder]]* is an annunciation — proof that the art he has served all his life has not betrayed him.

## The Letter and the Visit

Schulz's response to this discovery is characteristically generous: he writes to Christophe, a young composer he has never met, a letter of unrestrained praise:

> The enthusiasm of Peter Schulz was not shaken. He sent Christophe a long letter full of gratitude and admiration. [016-416]

Christophe, starved for recognition in his provincial German years, is deeply moved by the letter. When he happens to pass through the region, he resolves to visit the old man — a decision that leads to one of the novel's most warmly comic and affecting episodes.

The visit becomes an event of seismic importance in Schulz's small world. He enlists his friends Kunz and Pottpetschmidt, and the three elderly musicians prepare to receive Christophe with a solemnity normally reserved for royalty:

> Schulz went along the road to the town, his heart beating. When old Schulz came back with a face an ell long, Kunz knew that something had happened — something tremendous. [017-138]

When Christophe finally arrives, the scene between the young firebrand and his elderly worshipper is rendered with Rolland's characteristic blend of comedy and pathos:

> "Krafft!" cried Schulz.
>
> They had never seen each other. But they knew each other at once. [017-116]

> Schulz beamed and devoured him with his eyes, like a schoolboy on holiday. He could not take his gaze from Christophe's face. [017-147]

## The Evening of Music

The centerpiece of the Schulz episode is an impromptu concert in the old man's house. Christophe plays his compositions while the three old friends — Schulz at the piano, Kunz listening rapt, Pottpetschmidt singing — are transported:

> Pottpetschmidt sang, Schulz accompanied, and Kunz listened in a state of religious ecstasy. [017-106]

> Schulz laughed to himself in the street. He was beside himself with joy. "What a man! What a man!" [017-108]

The evening is one of the rare moments of unalloyed happiness in Christophe's early career. For Schulz, it represents the culmination of a lifetime's service to music:

> Old Schulz had sometimes found gratitude in the hearts of the children he taught; but it had been like water on a dry field, too little even to receive rain. And now, at the end of his life, this young man had brought him the ocean. [017-082]

## Significance as a Character Type

### The Pure Listener

Schulz represents a vital figure in Rolland's musical universe: the *pure listener*. Unlike the professional critics who dismiss Christophe, or the fashionable audiences who consume music as social currency, Schulz responds to art with undefended sincerity. His musical judgment is not intellectual but instinctive — and for Rolland, this instinct is a surer guide than any critical methodology.

### The Generational Bridge

Schulz also serves as a bridge between musical generations. He is old enough to have known "the great masters" — [[beethoven-music|Beethoven]], Schubert — and young enough in spirit to recognize their heir in Christophe. His enthusiasm validates Christophe's sense of artistic lineage:

| Function | How Schulz Fulfills It |
|----------|----------------------|
| Validator | First established musician to recognize Christophe's genius |
| Ancestor | Links Christophe to the Beethovenian tradition |
| Pure listener | Represents music's ideal audience — sincere, receptive, grateful |
| Foil | Contrasts with the professional critics and careerists |

### Comedy and Pathos

Rolland handles Schulz with a lightness unusual in the novel. The comedy of the old man's preparations for Christophe's visit — the frantic tidying, the enlistment of friends, the ceremonial gravity — never descends into mockery. Instead, the humor deepens our affection for a character whose devotion to art has kept his soul young:

> Schulz was out of breath, but he called out all the same, in a voice trembling with emotion: "Krafft is here!" [017-111]
