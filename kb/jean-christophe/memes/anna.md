# Anna Braun

Anna Braun (born Anna Maria Senfl) is the most psychologically complex female figure in the second half of *Jean-Christophe*. She appears in Volume IX (*The Burning Bush*) as the silent, severe wife of the doctor who takes the shattered Jean-Christophe into his home after Olivier's death. Beneath her frozen exterior lies a volcanic passion, and the collision of her repressed desire with Christophe's own grief-wracked vulnerability produces the novel's darkest moral crisis — an adultery followed by a double suicide attempt, from which both barely escape with their lives.

## Biography

Anna was born to tragedy. Her father, Martin Senfl, was the scion of a wealthy commercial family, an adventurer who had traveled through Asia and South America, shedding his old prejudices along the way. Against his family's furious protests, he married the daughter of a farmer — a woman of "doubtful reputation" who had been his mistress (033-158). The town closed its ranks against the couple. Unable to find work, broken by the implacable hostility of his community, Martin Senfl died of illness five months after the marriage. Four months later his wife died in childbirth, "casting the infant Anna upon the shores which she was leaving" (033-158).

The child was taken in by Martin's mother, the formidable Madame Senfl — a woman of "the narrowest piety," rich and mean, who kept a draper's shop in a gloomy street of the old town (033-159). She treated Anna less as a granddaughter than as an unpaid servant, regarding her as guilty of her parents' original sin. She "punished everything that was natural in her gestures, words, thoughts, as a crime" and "killed all joy in her young life" (033-159). Anna grew up in the shadow of the old *Munster*, whose grotesque statuary of hellfire — "immodest and distorted statues with a fire burning between their legs, while round their loins crawled toads and snakes" — gave permanent shape to her religious terrors (033-159).

Her piety was not mystical but materialistic and punitive. She fasted, wore a corset lined with pins that stabbed her with every movement, and once drove a nail into her own hand as a crude imitation of crucifixion (033-208). "When she tormented herself, it was not in any hope of advantage to be gained in the next world, but came only from a cruel boredom which rebounded against herself" (033-159).

Braun met her at a wedding feast when she was twenty-two. She was stiff, badly dressed, and almost silent — yet he was captivated by what he saw as her simplicity and solid domestic qualities (033-160). He married her without a dowry, and for seven years they lived as "a model couple" — side by side, never understanding each other, and never worrying about it (033-161).

## Role in the Novel

Anna's function in the novel is both structural and thematic. Structurally, she represents the Swiss interlude — the period of convalescence and catastrophe that follows the novel's great central tragedy, Olivier's death. After Christophe arrives at the Braun household shattered, half-mad with grief, it is Anna who first appears to him — a silent, shadowy figure in a dark doorway — and it is Anna who becomes the object of the passion that rushes into the void left by his friend's death.

Thematically, Anna embodies Rolland's most searching exploration of repressed female desire and the destructive power of religious discipline. She is not a villain but a victim — of her grandmother's cruelty, of the town's sanctimonious surveillance, of her own inability to acknowledge or express the life that seethes beneath her carapace. Rolland presents her as a case study in what happens when a passionate nature is systematically denied every outlet.

The music that she and Christophe share becomes the only channel through which her real self can emerge. When she sings, "the ordinary little woman" becomes "an imperious Venus, the incarnation of all the furies of the soul" (033-156). But outside music, the self must be suppressed again — and the suppression becomes murderous. Her brief affair with Christophe is less a love story than a mutual catastrophe, two wounded people whose collision produces a fire that neither can control.

## Relationships

### Braun

Anna feels no romantic love for her husband. "At no point in her life had the young wife had any love for her husband; the idea of such a thing never seemed to her to play any part in the life of an honest woman" (033-161). Yet she respects him, is grateful for his kindness in marrying her despite her stigmatized origins, and holds a strong sense of conjugal honor. When the adultery begins, her remorse expresses itself not in tenderness toward Braun but in harshness — "the greater her remorse grew for what she had done to him, the more harshly she spoke to him" (033-241). When Christophe, tormented by guilt, wants to confess everything to Braun, Anna furiously refuses: she is "quite determined that Braun should never know" (033-297). Her protection of him is flawed, prideful, but real.

### Jean-Christophe Krafft

Their relationship follows a terrible logic. Christophe arrives broken by Olivier's death; Anna has been broken since childhood by her grandmother and her faith. Each sees in the other something that mirrors their own hidden self. The turning point comes when Anna sings: Christophe sees for the first time her "dark eyes in which there was kindled a light of wildness" and her "wide, passionate mouth with its clear-cut lips, the voluptuous, rather heavy and cruel smile" (033-117). After the fateful country excursion — where Anna, freed from the town and its expectations, climbs hillsides like a goat, dances like a Bacchante, and reveals her "boyish" childhood exploits — the slide toward the abyss becomes irreversible (033-170, 033-219).

The consummation of the affair is preceded by one of the novel's most chilling scenes: Anna standing in the dark passage outside Christophe's room, listening to him play, "a figure standing motionless just inside" (033-113). That frozen, listening figure is the emblem of everything in her that waits, watches, and cannot speak. When she finally comes to his room in the night — a "white form" appearing silently in the darkness — it is with the inevitability of a force of nature (033-227).

Yet even in their intimacy there is no lasting tenderness. She tells him she hates him, she calls him a coward, she despises him for his weakness — and then flings her arms around his neck and says, "Poor Christophe! I have made you suffer" (033-254). The oscillation between hatred and pity defines the relationship: they are bound not by love but by a shared inability to live or die alone.

## Key Events

**The First Glimpse (033-064).** Anna brings food to the prostrate Christophe. He barely registers her, yet "the image of the young woman was graven with photographic clarity" — a prophetic first impression.

**The Mirror Scene (033-115).** Christophe, writing at the table, catches Anna's reflection in a mirror across the room. She is staring at him with "burning and hard" eyes, "fastened upon him, searching through him with dumb insistent ardor." This is the first crack in her facade.

**The Song (033-117).** When Anna finally agrees to sing, the transformation is absolute. The "rather raucous and husky voice" becomes a "deep" instrument of "grandeur that was both moving and pure." Christophe shivers: "it seemed to him to be the very voice of his own heart."

**The Country Excursion (033-168-220).** On a day trip when Braun is called away, Anna misses church for the first time in her life. In the countryside she sheds her carapace entirely — climbing mountains on all fours, wrestling with Christophe, dancing wildly at a village inn, becoming "a Bacchante, drunk with life" (033-219). But as the train returns to town, "life die[s] down in her, and all her beautiful body with its savage grace drop[s] back into its casing of stone" (033-220).

**The First Night (033-225-228).** Music dissolves the last barriers. After a shared song, "in a flash — she bent down to him, he turned to her: their lips met: he drank her breath" (033-225). Later that night she comes to his room in silence, a ghostly figure. The chapter heading warns: "O music, that openest the abysses of the soul!" (033-224).

**The Suicide Pact (033-315-393).** On the Wednesday before Carnival, with scandal closing in and the town's malicious surveillance tightening, Anna turns on the gas in her room. Christophe breaks in and saves her. They load Braun's old revolver. It misfires twice. Anna screams: "Oh! It is horrible, horrible! *He* will not let me die!" (033-392). The failed suicide is the novel's most harrowing scene, and it breaks the fever: Christophe flees to Italy that same evening.

## Themes and Significance

Anna is Rolland's indictment of religious culture that denies the body and punishes natural instinct. "The atmosphere of piety had withered her" (033-126), Christophe thinks early on — and the novel traces that withering back to its roots in Madame Senfl's shop, the hellfire sermons, the corset of pins. Yet Anna is not merely a victim. Her capacity for self-torture, her refusal of tenderness, her statement that "when a woman loves, she wants to destroy the man she loves so that no one else may have him" (033-131) — these are active, terrifying forces within her.

She also embodies the oppressive power of the community. The town's invisible surveillance — the mirrors at window corners, the servants who spy, the minister who makes "dark allusion" — is the social equivalent of the God who watches and judges. Anna cannot escape either. "She despised the town: but she could not have borne the town to despise herself" (033-304).

Her fate after Christophe's flight is left ambiguous. She recovers physically but remains spiritually shattered. The novel does not show us her death, but the arc of her story — from the silent wife who "hardly existed" to the woman who attempted suicide, who saw the truth of her own desire and could not bear it — is one of the book's most fully realized tragedies.
