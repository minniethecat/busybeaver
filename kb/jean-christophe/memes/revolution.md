# Revolution

The **Revolution** — both as historical memory (1848) and as contemporary force (syndicalism, socialism, anarchism) — runs through the later volumes of *Jean-Christophe* as the political shadow of [[jean-christophe|Christophe's]] artistic journey. The hero, temperamentally apolitical, is repeatedly drawn into revolutionary currents by friendship, circumstance, and the logic of his own uncompromising nature. In Rolland's treatment, revolution is at once **necessary** (the old order is unjust) and **dangerous** (collective action threatens individual creativity).

## The Legacy of 1848

The Revolution of 1848 — crushed but never forgotten — haunts the novel's political imagination. Christophe's grandfather [[jean-michel|Jean Michel]] is a child of that revolutionary generation, and his idealism bears its stamp:

> Like some old revolutionary, he hewed away at social conventions with a sort of innocent fury. [007-039]

The 1848 generation believed in the **redemptive power of political action** — a faith that the novel treats with sympathy but skepticism. Christophe inherits his grandfather's revolutionary temper without his political convictions: he rebels instinctively, not programmatically.

## Christophe as Accidental Revolutionary

Christophe's relationship to revolution is paradoxical. His temperament — passionate, uncompromising, indifferent to social convention — makes him a natural ally of revolutionary movements. Yet his artistic vocation makes him suspicious of all collective action:

> …he who had scandalized the German Philistines, thought himself a revolutionary in art, discovered that in France he was a conservative… [019-233]

This paradox — the radical artist who becomes a conservative in a more radical environment — is one of the novel's sharpest observations about the **relativity of political identity**. Christophe's "conservatism" is not doctrinal but instinctive: he refuses to subordinate individual vision to collective programs, whether those programs come from the right or the left.

## The Syndicalist Movement

Volume IX (*Le Buisson Ardent*) introduces the world of French syndicalism through Christophe's friendship with workers and activists. The novel's treatment of this material is extensive and ambivalent:

> …it was the very soul of the revolutionary movement. There it assumed its most theoretical and most practical form. [025-189]

Rolland portrays the syndicalist movement with genuine respect for its idealism and honesty about its limitations:

| Strength of Syndicalism | Weakness of Syndicalism |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| Moral seriousness | Utopian abstraction |
| Working-class authenticity | Anti-intellectualism |
| International solidarity | Revolutionary violence |
| Critique of bourgeois art | Hostility to individual genius |

The key figure is **Emmanuel**, a syndicalist worker who becomes Christophe's friend and who represents the novel's most sympathetic portrait of revolutionary commitment:

> …nor the burning flame of our revolutionaries. You have not seen a single one of our real men… [025-156]

## Revolution and Art

The novel's central political question is: **can revolutionary art exist?** Christophe's answer is complex. He believes art must be **true** to life, and life under capitalism is deformed — therefore authentic art necessarily has a critical, even revolutionary dimension. But he also believes art must be **free**, and revolutionary movements demand art that serves political ends:

> …the most revolutionary and the most reactionary forces came together against him… [017-321]

Christophe is caught between the reactionaries who hate him for his honesty and the revolutionaries who distrust him for his independence. This double rejection is the novel's emblem of the artist's political **loneliness**.

## The Riot Episode

The [[paris-riots|Paris Riots (May Day)]] represent revolution's eruption into direct violence — and the novel's most politically charged set piece. Christophe and [[olivier-jeannin|Olivier Jeannin]] are caught in a street battle between workers and police:

> A revolution was taking place in him. Ernest's words had set his mind on fire. [007-119]

The riot's outcome — Olivier's death — is the novel's tragic judgment on revolutionary violence: it devours the innocent, even those who sympathize with its aims.

## Revolution and the Individual

Ultimately, Rolland's treatment of revolution reflects his **heroic individualism**. Collective movements are necessary — the old order *must* be overthrown — but the individual genius must remain sovereign. Christophe's political arc is one of deepening sympathy for revolutionary ideals combined with stubborn refusal to be absorbed by any movement:

> "After the social revolution?" Christophe asked.
> "The revolution?" said she. "Oh, there'll be more than one revolution before that happens." [021-157]

This prophecy — that revolution is not a single event but a **permanent condition** of modern life — is among the novel's most prescient political insights.
