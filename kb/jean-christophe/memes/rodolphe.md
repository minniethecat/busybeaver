# Rodolphe

Rodolphe is Jean-Christophe's younger brother — a minor but consistently drawn character who serves as a foil to the novel's hero. Where Jean-Christophe is guided by passion, art, and an unguarded heart, Rodolphe is governed by convention, commerce, and self-interest. He is the brother who chose the respectable path, and his presence in the novel is a standing rebuke to the Krafft family's artistic ambitions. He appears in Volumes I through VII, and is mentioned again in Volume IX, his arc tracing a flat line of cold self-satisfaction from childhood to middle age.

## Biography

Rodolphe is one of Melchior and Louisa Krafft's three surviving sons, born into the family's declining fortunes. From early childhood he is singled out by his uncle Theodore, a wealthy businessman, as the one grandson suitable for a commercial career (006-022). The family's plan for him — that he "follow in his uncle's footsteps" — shapes his entire identity. He becomes Theodore's favorite and, after Melchior's death, moves into his uncle's office and household (011-004).

As a child, Rodolphe is described as "malicious as a monkey" (003-011), always taking advantage of Jean-Christophe's moments of distraction to create chaos. He breaks toys, spills water, dirties his frock, and knocks plates over while rummaging through cupboards. Already, his behavior is marked by a slyness that will characterize his adult self: he knows when Jean-Christophe is vulnerable and exploits it.

As a young man, Rodolphe is "steady, quiet, but sly" (006-161). He affects the values of his uncle Theodore, despising music and artists, and parroting the family gossip against Jean-Christophe. He refuses to acknowledge Jean-Christophe's authority in the house, though he accepts without question the food his elder brother provides. Together with Ernest, he steals from Jean-Christophe — including a gold watch, a gift from the Prince — and mocks his credulity behind his back.

After Melchior's death, Rodolphe flees the house of death as quickly as possible, moving into Uncle Theodore's office permanently (011-004). He appears briefly during the family's subsequent move to a smaller lodging: he "watched them load the furniture, gave some advice, and went away again looking mightily busy" (011-047). The gesture is emblematic — he participates in family life only on his own terms, when it costs him nothing and allows him to display his superiority.

In adulthood, Rodolphe becomes a businessman in Hamburg. He is "orderly and correct, assiduous in his business, strictly moral" (013-271). He never asks for money, and he never gives any. He visits his mother regularly every Sunday, stays exactly one hour, and talks exclusively about himself — "boasting about himself, his firm, and everything that concerned him, never asking about the others, and taking no interest in them, and going away when the hour was up, quite satisfied with having done his duty" (013-271). His filial piety is a performance, calculated to the minute.

Rodolphe's defining characteristic as an adult is his jealousy of Jean-Christophe. He is "cut to the quick by the least praise of Christophe" and "secretly afraid of his future success" (017-344). He pretends to ignore Christophe's achievements while privately exploiting his small fame in commercial circles. When Christophe decides to leave Germany, Rodolphe seizes the opportunity to lecture him on filial duty — despite having "never worried much about his mother" and leaving all financial support to Christophe (017-344). Christophe sees through the hypocrisy and kicks him out, calling him "a rascal and a hypocrite" (017-344).

At Louisa's deathbed, Rodolphe is absent — settled in Hamburg, as distant in geography as he has always been in affection (026-406). He arrives for the funeral "by one train and left by the next" (027-007), following the ceremony with perfect correctness but offering no word of thanks to Olivier, who had arranged everything. He is, in the novel's final portrait of him, incapable of genuine human warmth.

## Role in the Novel

Rodolphe functions as the novel's embodiment of respectable mediocrity — the life Jean-Christophe might have led had he suppressed his artistic nature and conformed to family expectations. His existence is a mirror that reflects, by inversion, everything the novel values in its hero.

His relationship with Uncle Theodore is particularly instructive. Where Jean-Christophe rebels against Theodore's contempt for art and spits in his face, Rodolphe absorbs Theodore's values wholesale. He becomes a "Theodore" in miniature — a man who measures everything by commercial success and treats art with deliberate contempt. The novel never suggests that Rodolphe is unhappy; rather, his contentment is the most damning thing about him. He is "quite satisfied with having done his duty" (013-271), and that satisfaction is a form of spiritual death.

Structurally, Rodolphe appears in only a handful of crucial scenes, but each appearance reinforces the same judgment. His role in the novel's moral economy is to show what it costs to refuse to feel — and what it costs those around such a person.

## Relationships

Rodolphe's relationship with Jean-Christophe defines him. From childhood, he resents his elder brother's authority and gifts; in adulthood, he channels this resentment into cold contempt, refusing to acknowledge Christophe's success while secretly fearing it. The gold watch theft in childhood (006-161) prefigures all subsequent betrayals: Rodolphe takes what he can from Christophe and offers nothing in return.

With Ernest, Rodolphe shares a bond of mutual exploitation. Ernest plays both brothers against each other, "flattering Rodolphe's vanity and jealousy" while accepting his rebukes deferentially (013-271). Rodolphe, despite his avarice — or perhaps because of it — allows Ernest to "despoil him just as Christophe did" (013-271).

His relationship with Louisa is the novel's starkest illustration of duty without love. The one-hour Sunday visits, filled with self-regarding monologue, are a ritual of self-congratulation rather than care.

## Key Events

Rodolphe's malicious childhood pranks establish his character early, as he takes advantage of Jean-Christophe minding baby Ernest to wreak havoc (003-011). His appointment as Theodore's protege cements his path away from art and into commerce (006-022). After Melchior's death, he flees to Theodore's house while Jean-Christophe remains to bear the burden (011-004). His perfunctory appearance during the family's move — watching and advising without helping — epitomizes his approach to family obligations (011-047). When Christophe plans to leave Germany, Rodolphe's self-righteous intervention nearly destroys the last fragile peace between mother and son (017-344). His final appearance, at Louisa's funeral, is his most damning: he arrives, performs, and departs without a word of genuine feeling (027-007).

## Themes and Significance

Rodolphe represents the novel's critique of bourgeois respectability — a life that is outwardly correct but inwardly empty. He is "strictly moral" in the narrow sense of never violating social convention, but his morality is a structure built to house his self-regard. The contrast with Jean-Christophe is absolute: where one brother gives everything and is perpetually wounded, the other gives nothing and feels nothing.

The novel does not present Rodolphe as evil — he is too small for that. He is, rather, the product of a family and a society that value appearance over substance. His tragedy, if it can be called that, is that he never suspects he might be missing anything.
