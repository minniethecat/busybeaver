# Dr. Braun

Dr. Erich Braun is the Swiss doctor who opens his home to Jean-Christophe at the moment of his greatest physical and spiritual collapse. He is decent, kind, and utterly unaware that his generosity is rewarded with adultery — a betrayal that the novel treats as the darkest of moral crises. He represents domestic decency in its most vulnerable form: the ordinary, good-hearted man who does everything right and is repaid with the worst wrong.

## Biography

Braun is a German doctor — "one Erich Braun" — who had been a casual acquaintance of Christophe's from years before (033-031). He had written to Christophe the year before, after one of his musical successes, to remind him of their old acquaintance (033-031). When Christophe arrives at his doorstep in the rain, half-mad with grief and exhaustion after Olivier's death, Braun takes him in without hesitation, refusing even to let Christophe set a limit on his stay: "A day! ... Twenty days, fifty, as long as you like" (033-047).

Physically, Braun is "a little man" with "a red face with a black, scrubby and untidy beard, kind eyes twinkling behind spectacles, a broad, bumpy, wrinkled, worried, inexpressive brow, hair carefully plastered down and parted right down to his neck" (033-039). He is described as "very ugly" (033-039), but his appearance belies the warmth of his nature.

Braun met Anna at a marriage feast when she was twenty-two. With his characteristic simplicity — "his usual penetration" applied ironically — he was struck by her "air of original simplicity," her common sense, her healthiness, and "the solid domestic qualities which she seemed to him to possess" (033-160). No dowry was given; Anna's grandmother, Madame Senfl, had left all the family wealth to the town. Braun married her anyway, and for seven years they lived as "a model couple" — side by side, never understanding each other, and never worrying about it (033-161).

His medical practice was "fair" but modest. The household life was simple and frugal, with a maid named Bäbi. Anna had never been accepted by local society due to the stigma of her illegitimate origin, and she made no effort to change that (033-161). Braun, for his part, "had never succeeded in making his friends accept his wife" (033-161).

## Role in the Novel

Braun's role is structurally simple but thematically devastating. He is the axis around which the novel's most painful moral drama turns. He enters the story as Christophe's rescuer — the one figure who restores the shattered protagonist to physical life after the catastrophe of Olivier's death. His loud, cordial voice, his immediate warmth, his refusal of any limit on his hospitality — these are what pull Christophe back from the brink of death (033-039, 033-047).

But Braun is also the cuckold, the man whose trust and kindness create the conditions for the very betrayal that destroys him. The novel's irony is merciless: Christophe and Anna's affair unfolds under Braun's roof, at Braun's table, while Braun prattles on in cheerful ignorance. His very dullness — his inability to see what is happening before his eyes — becomes a kind of indictment, not of Braun himself, but of the whole situation.

The novel never invites the reader to despise Braun. On the contrary, Christophe's own conscience makes the judgment explicit: "Not him! ... He is the best of us!" (033-227 in effect, as Christophe recoils at Anna's suggestion of killing him). When Braun at last begins to sense something wrong and comes to Christophe, trembling, to ask "Do you know what's the matter with her?", Christophe's agony at having to lie is almost unbearable — for he knows that the truth would destroy a man who has done nothing but good (033-278-289).

Braun's final appearance — or near-appearance — in the novel comes near the end of Volume X, when Christophe, returning from Germany, thinks of visiting the Brauns but loses courage. The novel leaves Braun's ultimate fate ambiguous: "he dared not even ask at the hotel whether the doctor and his wife were still alive" (042-082). It is a fitting end for a character whose entire significance lies in being the decent man who deserved better, and whose specific fate the novel cannot bear to confirm.

## Relationships

### Anna Braun

Braun loved Anna but was never loved in return. "At no point in her life had the young wife had any love for her husband; the idea of such a thing never seemed to her to play any part in the life of an honest woman" (033-161). Yet she was grateful for his kindness in marrying her despite her stigmatized origins, and she held a strong sense of conjugal honor. Braun, for his part, was devoted to her in his simple, uncomprehending way. When Anna falls into her post-affair illness, Braun refuses to leave her bedside, refuses to eat, and "spent the night with her, getting up every moment to listen to her breathing" (033-395, 033-403). He is, in every respect, a devoted husband — and this makes the betrayal all the more cruel.

### Jean-Christophe Krafft

Braun treats Christophe with unreserved kindness from the moment he arrives. He calls him "my dear Christophe" and is overwhelmed with joy and honor at having him in his home (033-047, 033-050). He nurses Christophe back to health with "heavy but kindly attentions" (033-097). He and Christophe sit together after dinner talking; he loves music with the uncritical enthusiasm of a man who does not understand what he loves, but his "love for music was quickly sated: he could never listen with any attention for more than a quarter of an hour on end" (033-096). Christophe is grateful for Braun's kindness but can have "no intellectual intimacy with Braun" (033-098). When the affair begins, Christophe can barely bring himself to shake Braun's hand or eat his bread — "To shake the man's hand, to eat his bread, to give the kiss of Judas!" (033-278). When Braun, sensing something wrong, comes to Christophe and asks about Anna, Christophe nearly confesses — "Christophe was very near taking Braun's hands and kissing them and begging his forgiveness" (033-284). But he says nothing, and Braun, terrified of the truth, refuses to push further: "No, no. You know nothing? Nothing?" (033-284).

## Key Events

**Sheltering Christophe (033-031-050).** Christophe, fleeing France after the riot that killed Olivier, arrives at Braun's door half-dead from exhaustion and hunger. Braun takes him in instantly, refuses to set limits on his stay: "It is an honor and a great happiness for us" (033-047). Christophe collapses in his arms and faints.

**The Household Routine (033-092-098).** Under Braun's care, Christophe slowly recovers. They eat together, talk after dinner, and Braun insists that Christophe give piano lessons to supplement his income (033-095). Braun's "heavy but kindly attentions" and the household quiet provide Christophe with the conditions to begin composing again (033-097).

**Braun's Marriage (033-160-161).** Through Braun's "easy, indiscreet good nature" Christophe learns the story of how Braun met Anna — at a wedding feast, when she was twenty-two and stiff and silent. Braun saw in her simplicity, health, and solidity everything he wanted in a wife (033-160).

**The Musical Evenings (033-117-122).** Braun, "who had a mania, due partly to marital vanity and partly to love of teasing, for worrying his wife to sing and play," insists Anna sing for Christophe (033-117). When she does, her transformation is absolute — but Braun sees only the social pleasantness of the moment, not the volcanic forces it reveals.

**Asking Christophe About Anna (033-278-289).** After the affair has begun, a visibly changed Braun — his gaiety "had disappeared" — clasps Christophe's arm and asks, with trembling voice: "Do you know what's the matter with her?" (033-281). Christophe lies; Braun accepts the lie. "Thanks, thank you. I thank you..." he says, and walks away (033-289). This is the novel's cruelest moment: the deceived husband asking the deceiver for comfort, and receiving only silence.

**Anna's Illness and Recovery (033-395-409).** After the suicide attempt, Braun devotes himself entirely to Anna. He refuses to leave her bedside, consults a colleague, and is in "a state of agony" when her pulse weakens (033-395-396). When at last she begins to recover, "about dawn she moved a little: Braun raised her head to give her to drink" (033-403). He never learns what truly happened.

**The Last Thought (042-082).** Years later, Christophe passes through the town and thinks of calling on the Brauns — but his courage fails him. "He dared not even ask at the hotel whether the doctor and his wife were still alive" (042-082). He leaves without knowing.

## Themes and Significance

Braun embodies domestic decency and its vulnerability. He is the ordinary good man — kind, a little dull, thoroughly decent — whose entire world is undermined by forces he cannot comprehend. The novel treats his innocence not as foolishness but as something sacred: Christophe's refusal to confess is not cowardice alone but also a recognition that Braun "would never avenge himself, that perhaps he would not even have the strength to hate them: but what an utter wreck of all his life!" (033-278).

His simplicity is both his strength and his limitation. He loves music without understanding it; he loves his wife without understanding her; he befriends Christophe without suspecting the danger. This is not presented as stupidity but as the tragic blindness of ordinary goodness when confronted with extraordinary passion.

Braun also represents the ironic counterpoint to Christophe's genius. Christophe, the great artist, brings nothing but destruction into this modest household. The novel implicitly asks: who is the better man? Christophe's own answer is unambiguous: Braun is "the best of us" (033-227, paraphrased in Anna's response). Braun's domestic world — simple, frugal, unremarkable — is the world that Christophe's art and passion can only disrupt, never enrich.
