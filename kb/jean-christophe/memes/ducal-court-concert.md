# Court Concert for the Grand Duke

The court concert and its aftermath represent Jean-Christophe's final, irreparable break with the world of his birth. It is not a single event but a cascade: the disastrous concert, the singer's rebellion, the hostile press, the publication in a socialist newspaper, the summons to the Palace, the screaming confrontation with the Grand Duke, and the final betrayal by a journalist who turns Christophe's confidences into scandal.

## The Concert

The concert program includes an overture to Hebbel's *Judith*, a symphony titled "The Dream of Life," and a song-cycle. But the omens are bad from the start. The Grand Duke, offended by Christophe's candid remarks — he called Mendelssohn's *Elijah* "a clerical humbug's paternoster" and Schumann's lieder "*Backfisch Musik*" (015-094) — does not attend. "The royal box was only occupied by Court people, a few ladies-in-waiting" (015-108).

The hall is a third empty (015-109). The audience is cold: "Every phrase seemed to drop into depths of indifference" (015-108). When the sparse applause ends, "four pairs of hands went on clapping when the others had finished: but they awoke no echo, and stopped ashamed: that made the emptiness seem more empty" (015-109).

The singer — the "blue lady" from Dresden and Bayreuth, a celebrated performer of Brunnhilde and Kundry — rebels against Christophe's conducting. She sings the lieder her own way, defying his instructions. Christophe stops mid-bar and shouts "Stop!" (015-110). When she continues, he says icily: "Begin again!" (015-111). She submits, but her fury is audible through the closed door of her box.

The audience retaliates by ostentatiously applauding Siegmund Ochs's *Festmarsch* — a vapid piece by Christophe's crafty colleague who has been spreading his confidences mockingly through the town. The press delivers a unified verdict: "Knowledge of counterpoint. Complicated writing. Lack of inspiration. No melody. Written with the head, not with the heart. Want of sincerity. Trying to be original" (015-128).

## The Confrontation

Blocked from all respectable outlets, Christophe gives an article to a socialist newspaper. The Palace summons him. He enters to find the Grand Duke, Princess Adelaide, and officers finishing dinner in high spirits:

> When Christophe entered he heard the Grand Duke's thick laugh. But he stopped dead when he saw Christophe. He growled and pounced on him.
> — (016-258)

The Grand Duke waves the crumpled newspaper: "You're a blackguard, sir!" (016-260). He screams that Christophe has insulted everything "dear to all true Germans." Christophe, trembling but defiant, refuses to apologize: "I am not your slave. I shall say what I like and write what I like" (016-265).

The confrontation becomes physical. Christophe upsets an ornament, then picks up an ashtray "mechanically." The princess watches "the scene and exchanging ironically commiserating remarks with her neighbors" (016-276). The Grand Duke, apoplectic, roars: "Go! Go! I'll have you thrown out!" (016-279).

Christophe stumbles home, shutters himself in his room. At 2 a.m. "he wanted to go and kill the Grand Duke. He was devoured by hate and shame" (016-282).

## The Journalist's Betrayal

The socialist editor visits that evening, feigning sympathy. Christophe pours everything out. The next morning his story appears on the front page, "immeasurably enlarged, having suffered that peculiar deformation which everything has to suffer in its passage through the mind of a journalist" (016-287). The article attacks the Grand Duke and Court with low invective so personal that only Christophe could have provided the details.

Christophe writes a disclaimer. The paper refuses to publish it. They threaten to publish his original letter. A follow-up paragraph calls him a "servant of the Court, who even when they are dismissed remain servants and are incapable of being free" (016-290).

## Narrative Significance

The court concert and its aftermath is the event that makes Christophe's flight from Germany inevitable. It demonstrates the full machinery of provincial society — the court, the press, the colleagues, the hangers-on — united against the individual who refuses to compromise. Christophe is not important enough to destroy deliberately; he is simply crushed by a system that cannot tolerate honesty.

## Themes

- **The artist vs. the court**: Patronage is ownership; the Grand Duke treats Christophe as property, not as an artist
- **The press as weapon**: The journalists — both the respectable critics and the socialist editor — use Christophe's words against him
- **The cost of integrity**: Christophe says what he thinks because he cannot do otherwise; the consequences are catastrophic and also liberating
