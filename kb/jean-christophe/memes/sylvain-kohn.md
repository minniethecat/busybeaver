# Sylvain Kohn

Sylvain Kohn is the first Parisian contact Jean-Christophe seeks out upon arriving in the capital, and his character embodies everything the novel critiques about the commodification of art. A German Jew from Mainz who has reinvented himself in Paris — even changing his name to "Hamilton" — Kohn is a figure of consummate adaptability, cynical worldliness, and a paradoxical relationship with music that is at once genuine and profoundly shallow.

## Biography

Sylvain Kohn was born in Mainz, Germany, and attended the same school as Jean-Christophe, where he played "many pranks" on the young musician until Christophe's threats of violence curbed his malice (019-014). He eventually relocated to Paris, securing a position in a major publishing house in the neighborhood of the Madeleine. There he renamed himself "Hamilton" — a transformation that signals his wholesale adoption of Parisian identity and his shedding of German provincialism (019-082).

By the time Christophe arrives in Paris, Kohn has become a fixture of the city's cultural scene: "the little German Jew, clod as he was, had made himself the chronicler and arbiter of Parisian fashion and smartness" (019-077). He writes "insipid society paragraphs and articles in a delicately involved manner" and positions himself as "the champion of French style, French smartness, French gallantry, French wit — Regency, red heels, Lauzun" (019-077). The narrator notes that "in Paris ridicule leads to everything, even to fame and fortune," and Kohn has prospered precisely by embracing the ridiculous (019-077).

## Physical Description

Physically, Kohn is described as "short, thickset, clean-shaven, like an American; his complexion was too red, his hair too black; he had a heavy, massive face, coarse-featured; little darting, wrinkled eyes, a rather crooked mouth, a heavy, cunning smile" (019-077). He dresses fashionably, "trying to cover up the defects of his figure, high shoulders, and wide hips," and his vanity centers on his appearance — "the only thing that touched his vanity: he would gladly have put up with any insult if only he could have been a few inches taller and of a better figure" (019-077).

## Role in the Novel

Kohn functions as Jean-Christophe's guide — and misguide — through the Parisian world of arts and letters. His role is structurally crucial: through Kohn's introductions, Christophe encounters the publishing world (Daniel Hecht), the literary coteries (Théophile Goujart, Lucien Lévy-Coeur), the theater (Achille Roussin), and the salons of Jewish high society. But Kohn's guidance consistently leads Christophe into the novel's most damning illustrations of artistic corruption.

The narrator makes this function explicit: "He felt that such a guide as Sylvain Kohn must lead him hopelessly astray" (019-329), and "Sylvain Kohn was no safer as a guide in that world than in the world of books, and, thanks to him, Christophe's first impression was almost as repulsive as that of his first essay in literature" (019-330).

Kohn also represents the novel's critique of the pseudo-cosmopolitanism that substitutes for genuine culture. His borrowed French identity is revealed as hollow when Christophe realizes "that Kohn was a thousand times more of a foreigner in France than himself" (019-401). When Christophe asks for "France," Kohn's notorious reply is: "We are France," followed by gurgling laughter (019-405). He is "so cocksure and so satisfied with the general flabbiness of the French" that he cannot see his own alienation (019-401).

## Relationships

Kohn's relationship with Christophe is the primary focus of his appearances. They were schoolfellows, but the bond was always unequal — the young Kohn a mocking trickster, Christophe his violent corrector (019-014). When they meet again in Paris, Kohn greets Christophe with "extraordinary delight" and no apparent rancor (019-076). "There was no rancor about Kohn: he was too clever for that. He had long ago forgotten the rough treatment he had suffered at Christophe's hands" (019-076). His welcome is calculated — he is delighted to display his importance and Parisian sophistication to his old schoolfellow, and he recognizes Christophe's visit as a "tribute to his power" (019-076).

But his helpfulness is superficial. He promises introductions, then dodges Christophe for a week with sham excuses about illness (019-140). When cornered, he arranges the disastrous introduction to Daniel Hecht where he introduces Christophe "in a bantering, pretentious voice, with exaggerated praises" (019-150) — a performance calculated more to amuse himself than to assist his protege.

He introduces Christophe to literary dinners and the theater, acting as a cultural cicerone who shows off his German discovery. At these gatherings, "Sylvain Kohn saw to it that his friends were kept amused" at Christophe's expense (019-233). He uses Christophe as entertainment — inviting him to play his excellent piano (which "he never used himself" (019-242)), then secretly inviting guests to listen, violating Christophe's explicit prohibition (019-245).

In the *David* performance episode, Kohn is instrumental in the scheme to use Christophe's biblical oratorio as a vehicle for Roussin's mistress, the singer Sainte-Ygraine. Kohn watches the revelation "delightedly" and takes Christophe's arm laughing as they descend the theater stairs (021-060).

## Key Events

**First meeting in Paris** [019-012 to 019-082]: Christophe seeks out Kohn at his publishing house. Kohn greets him with theatrical warmth, reveals his adopted name Hamilton, and promises introductions to "anyone you like" (019-104). He is, however, "horribly shocked by his voracity and his peasant manners" at lunch (019-106).

**The broken promises** [019-108 to 019-147]: Kohn dodges Christophe for days, claiming illness and business. When Christophe finally catches him, Kohn reluctantly arranges an introduction to Daniel Hecht, introducing him in a manner designed to humiliate rather than help (019-150).

**The restaurant dinner** [019-223 to 019-235]: Kohn carries Christophe off to a dinner of young artists, where he introduces him as "a man who had just escaped from a German prison" and "saw to it that his friends were kept amused" at Christophe's expense (019-233).

**Musical evenings** [019-242 to 019-245]: Kohn invites Christophe to use his piano. Despite having no real understanding of music, he is "enslaved sensually by Christophe's music" — a genuine but superficial attraction (019-243). He cannot refrain from making absurd comments ("Don't come in! I've someone here" after the opening chords of the Fifth Symphony). The evenings invariably end in quarrels, and when Kohn secretly invites guests to listen, Christophe permanently severs the arrangement (019-245).

**Guide to French culture** [019-292 to 019-408]: Kohn takes Christophe to the theater and introduces him to French literature — but the culture he presents is the degraded culture of the boulevards. Christophe's repeated horror at what he sees leads to their philosophical break, crystallized when Kohn claims "We are France" (019-405).

**The David affair** [021-029 to 021-063]: Kohn is complicit in Roussin's scheme to produce Christophe's *David* as a vehicle for Sainte-Ygraine. He dismisses Christophe's objections with ironic one-liners: "That won't hurt the part" (021-036) and "That won't bother him" (021-046). When Christophe finally understands the deception, Kohn "delightedly" tells him the full story (021-060).

## Themes and Significance

Kohn embodies the commercialization and trivialization of art that the novel relentlessly critiques. He is a "passionate lover of music — God knows why! for he knew nothing at all about it, and he liked bad music just as well as good" (019-238). His love for music is real but entirely sensual and undiscriminating — he weeps at Wagner then strums Offenbach, rendering musical judgment impossible.

He represents the Parisian culture industry as a closed system of mutual back-scratching. His introductions are not acts of generosity but displays of power; his friendships are strategies for advancement; his art criticism (such as it is) is indistinguishable from social performance. When he declares "We are artists. We follow art for art's sake" (019-377), the novel has already demonstrated that neither "art" nor "artists" mean anything in his vocabulary.

His adopted identity as "Hamilton" symbolizes the cultural inauthenticity of the world he inhabits. He is a German who has become more Parisian than the Parisians, championing "French style" and "French gallantry" while remaining fundamentally alienated from any genuine national tradition. His claim that "We are France" is the novel's most devastating irony — Kohn is precisely what France is not, or rather, what France has become in its decadent phase.

For Jean-Christophe, Kohn is a necessary antagonist — the embodiment of everything the genuine artist must resist. Every encounter with Kohn clarifies Christophe's own values by showing him their opposite. The tension between Christophe's authenticity and Kohn's performance is one of the novel's central moral dramas.
