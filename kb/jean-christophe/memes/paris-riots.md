# Paris Riots (May Day)

The May Day demonstration and its escalation into street violence is the novel's most devastating dramatic event — a moment when the forces Jean-Christophe has navigated all his life suddenly crystallize into lethal form. Olivier Jeannin, the gentle intellectual who "no man desired less than he" to unleash violence, becomes the catalyst of an insurrection, and Jean-Christophe is transformed from observer to combatant to fugitive in a single afternoon.

## Context

The events unfold in late April and early May, during a period of rising revolutionary tension in Paris. The C.G.T. (Confédération Générale du Travail) has been threatening a general strike for May Day. "A sinister rumor ran through Paris. The blustering leaders of the C.G.T. were doing their best to spread it" (033-200). Parisians flee to the country or hoard provisions; Pierre Canet is seen "in his motor, carrying two hams and a sack of potatoes" (033-200).

Olivier, recovering from influenza, is initially reluctant to go out. He has premonitions: "I wonder what your life will be... But I shan't see anything of it all. I shall be left stuck stupidly by the wayside" (033-245). Christophe, in his characteristic blend of enthusiasm and contempt, insists: "I want to see their First of May. If I don't come back tonight, you will know that I have been locked up" (033-248). Olivier, unable to let his friend go alone, accompanies him.

## The Demonstration

The streets on May Day morning are deceptively calm. "Working-people in their Sunday clothes were walking about rather listlessly. At the street corners, and near the Metro stations were groups of policemen in plain clothes" (033-252). Christophe mocks the predicted upheaval. But as they approach the Place de la République, the crowd thickens and changes character:

> The multitude was not hostile. The people did not know what they wanted. Until they did know they were content to amuse themselves — after their own nervous, brutal fashion, still without malice — to amuse themselves with pushing and being pushed, insulting the police and each other.
> — (033-258)

Christophe is immediately swept up: "Hardly had he set foot inside the stream than he was swept along: though he was a foreigner in this crowd of Frenchmen and a stranger to their demands, yet he was suddenly engulfed by them: little he cared what they wanted: he wanted it too" (033-260). Olivier, who "did not want it," follows with a sad smile.

## Olivier's Death

The violence escalates when cuirassiers charge to clear the square. In the stampede, Emmanuel — the thirteen-year-old hunchbacked apprentice who has become Olivier's protégé — is knocked from his perch on a newspaper kiosk and trampled. Olivier sees him fall: "Olivier did not stop to think: he rushed down the steps and ran to his aid... Olivier holding out his hand to the boy to help him up, the savage rush of the police knocked them both over" (033-278).

The irony is devastating: "So Olivier, the aristocrat, the essentially middle-class nature, released the spring of the battle, which no man desired less than he" (033-278). He receives "a jab from a bayonet, meant for someone else, in his left breast: he fell: the crowd trampled him underfoot" (033-280).

Olivier is carried unconscious to Amelie's back-shop. Manousse Heimann, the Russian-Jewish doctor with ambiguous police connections, examines him and "at once he saw that it was all over" (033-295). Olivier briefly regains consciousness — "He felt Emmanuel's tears and kisses on his hand. He smiled faintly, and painfully laid his hand on the boy's head. Such a heavy hand it was!" (033-310) — then dies.

## Christophe's Transformation

Unaware of Olivier's fate, Christophe undergoes his own transformation on the barricade. Confronted by a policeman with murder in his eyes, "murderous desire awoke in him... He plunged the sword into his breast, felt that he was killing him, and killed him. And suddenly the whole thing was changed: he was mad, intoxicated, and he roared aloud" (033-282).

His roar transforms the riot into an insurrection: "The crowd had smelt blood. In a moment it became a savage pack. On all sides swords were drawn. The red flag appeared in the windows of the houses" (033-284). Christophe, "unrecognizable," stands on the barricade singing his revolutionary song.

Manousse arranges Christophe's escape — Canet's motor to Laroche, then a train to Switzerland. Christophe goes, dazed, believing Olivier will follow. Days later, in a Swiss hotel, he receives the letter:

> He opened it, saw that Olivier was dead, and fainted.
> — (033-330)

## Narrative Significance

The May Day riot is the novel's great hinge — the event that separates the Paris years from the Swiss exile, friendship from solitude, engagement from withdrawal. Olivier's death is the most consequential loss in Jean-Christophe's life after his grandfather's death in childhood. It forces him into Switzerland, where he will face his darkest moral crisis (with Anna), and where he will eventually find his way back to art through the mountains and silence.
