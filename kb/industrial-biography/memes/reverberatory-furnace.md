The **reverberatory furnace** (also called the air furnace) is a type of furnace in which the flame and hot gases from the burning fuel pass over a low partition wall and are reflected (reverberated) down onto the charge, so that the metal is heated without direct contact with the fuel. This principle was fundamental to the [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] process]] and to coal-fired [[iron-founding]], making it one of the key metallurgical inventions of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## 定义

Dr. John Percy, the nineteenth-century metallurgist, described the reverberatory furnace as follows:

> "It consists essentially of three parts — a fireplace at one end, a stack or chimney at the other, and a bed between both on which the matter is heated. The fireplace is separated from the bed by a low partition wall called the fire-bridge, and both are covered by an arched roof which rises from the end wall." (018-148)

The term "reverberatory" refers to the fact that "the flame or current of heated gases from the fuel is caused to be reverberated or reflected down upon the substance under operation before passing into the chimney." (018-141) This separation of fuel and charge was the furnace's key advantage: it allowed pit-coal (which contained sulphur and other impurities) to be used without contaminating the iron — a problem that had frustrated ironmasters for decades.

## 历史发展

### 起源

The reverberatory furnace has a long prehistory. Rovenson, in his *Treatise of Metallica* of 1613, "describes a reverberatory furnace in which iron was to be smelted by pit-coal, though it does not appear that he succeeded in perfecting his invention." (018-145) The idea of using reflected heat to avoid fuel contamination was understood long before it could be made to work reliably at industrial scale.

### 在搅炼中的应用

The reverberatory furnace found its first major industrial application in the work of the [[Cranege brothers]] (George and Thomas), who in 1766 patented a process for converting pig iron into [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] using a coal-fired reverberatory furnace. Their patent specification stated:

> "A reverberatory furnace being built of a proper construction, the pig or [[pig-iron|cast iron]] is put into it, and without the addition of anything else than common raw pit coal, is converted into good [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]], and, being taken red-hot from the reverberatory furnace to the [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]], is drawn out into bars of various shapes and sizes, according to the will of the workmen." (016-157)

This was the foundation upon which [[Henry Cort]] built his revolutionary [[puddling-process|puddling]] process in 1783–1784. Cort improved the furnace design by making the bottom hollow, "so as to contain the fluid metal," and by incorporating the stirring ([[puddling-process|puddling]]) action that exposed the molten iron more thoroughly to the oxidising atmosphere. (022-020) Cort's design combined the reverberatory principle of [[cranege-brothers|the Craneges]] with the mechanical working method of [[peter-onions|Peter Onions]], producing a practical system that could convert any grade of pig iron into malleable wrought iron at scale.

## 工艺原理

The furnace's arched roof acted as a reflector, directing radiant heat downward onto the charge on the bed. The fire-bridge separated the fuel grate from the metal bath, preventing physical contact while allowing the hot flame and gases to sweep across the bed. The oxidising atmosphere burned off carbon and silicon from the iron, progressively converting brittle pig iron into forgeable wrought iron.

The key advantages of the reverberatory design were:

| Feature | Benefit |
|---------|---------|
| Fuel-charge separation | Allowed use of raw pit-coal without sulphur contamination |
| Reflected heat | Uniform heating of the charge from above |
| Oxidising atmosphere | Burned off carbon — the essential [[puddling-process|puddling]] reaction |
| No blast required | Simpler, cheaper operation than blast furnaces |
| Continuous operation | Could process charge after charge without cooling |

## 经济与工业影响

Without the reverberatory furnace, the [[iron-industry|British iron industry]] would have remained dependent on charcoal for refining — a bottleneck that had constrained production for centuries. By enabling coal-fired [[puddling-process|puddling]], the furnace unlocked the vast coal reserves of Britain and allowed the production of wrought iron to increase from roughly 30,000 tons per year in 1780 to over 3,000,000 tons by 1860. The reverberatory furnace thus stands as one of the indispensable technologies of the Industrial Revolution, the silent partner to the more famous [[puddling-process|puddling]] process it enabled.
