# Forge Hammer

The water-powered trip hammer used for forging wrought iron blooms into [[wrought-iron|bar iron]], the primary forging tool of the iron industry before the steam hammer

## Background
Before powered hammers, all forging was done by hand with sledgehammers. The water-powered trip hammer, developed in the Middle Ages, mechanised the heaviest forging operations.

## Inventor
- **Inventor**: Unknown (medieval development)
- **Key improvers**: The Cranege brothers, [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]], [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]

## How It Works
A water wheel lifted a heavy iron head by means of cams on the wheel shaft. As the cam rotated past the lift point, the hammer head dropped by gravity onto the anvil. The rate of blows was determined by the water wheel speed. The hammer could deliver heavy blows but offered no control over blow force. (016-160)

## Significance
The trip hammer was the mainstay of iron forging for centuries. Its limitations, particularly the lack of blow control and the small size of forgings possible, drove the development of Nasmyth's steam hammer. The trip hammer was gradually superseded from the 1840s onward.
