# Cementation Process

The process of converting wrought iron into blister steel by prolonged heating packed in charcoal, the essential first stage of pre-Bessemer steel production

## Background
Before the cementation process, steel could only be produced in very small quantities by the direct reduction of iron ore. The cementation process made steel production possible on a commercial scale for the first time.

## Inventor
- **Inventor**: Unknown (developed in Germany, 16th century; introduced to [[england|England]] in the early 17th century)
- **Notable improver**: [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]] (who used blister steel as the raw material for cast steel)

## How It Works
Wrought iron bars were packed in charcoal powder inside sealed sandstone or iron chests. The chests were heated to approximately 1100 degrees C for 7 to 10 days. Carbon from the charcoal diffused into the iron, transforming the outer layers into high-carbon steel. The resulting blister steel, named for the blistered appearance of its surface, contained approximately 1.5 percent carbon. (019-182)

## Significance
The cementation process was the foundation of [[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s steel industry. Blister steel was used directly for tools and springs, and it provided the raw material for Huntsman's [[cast-steel|crucible steel]] process. The process remained in use until the Bessemer process rendered it obsolete in the late 19th century.
