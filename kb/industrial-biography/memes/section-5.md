[005-001]Although we hear of the smith mostly in connexion with the fabrication

[005-002]of instruments of war in the Middle Ages, his importance was no less

[005-003]recognized in the ordinary affairs of rural and industrial life.  He

[005-004]was, as it were, the rivet that held society together.  Nothing could

[005-005]be done without him.  Wherever tools or implements were wanted for

[005-006]building, for trade, or for husbandry, his skill was called into

[005-007]requisition.  In remote places he was often the sole mechanic of his

[005-008]district; and, besides being a tool-maker, a farrier, and agricultural

[005-009]implement maker, he doctored cattle, drew teeth, practised phlebotomy,

[005-010]and sometimes officiated as parish clerk and general newsmonger; for

[005-011]the smithy was the very eye and tongue of the village.  Hence

[005-012]Shakespeare's picture of the smith in King John:

[005-013]"I saw a smith stand with his hammer, thus,

[005-014]The whilst his iron did on the anvil cool,

[005-015]With open mouth swallowing a tailor's news."

[005-016]The smith's tools were of many sorts; but the chief were his hammer,

[005-017]pincers, chisel, tongs, and anvil.  It is astonishing what a variety of

[005-018]articles he turned out of his smithy by the help of these rude

[005-019]implements.  In the tooling, chasing, and consummate knowledge of the

[005-020]capabilities of iron, he greatly surpassed the modern workman; for the

[005-021]mediaeval blacksmith was an artist as well as a workman.  The numerous

[005-022]exquisite specimens of his handicraft which exist in our old gateways,

[005-023]church doors, altar railings, and ornamented dogs and andirons, still

[005-024]serve as types for continual reproduction.  He was, indeed, the most

[005-025]"cunninge workman" of his time.  But besides all this, he was an

[005-026]engineer.  If a road had to be made, or a stream embanked, or a trench

[005-027]dug, he was invariably called upon to provide the tools, and often to

[005-028]direct the work.  He was also the military engineer of his day, and as

[005-029]late as the reign of Edward III. we find the king repeatedly sending

[005-030]for smiths from the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] to act as engineers for the royal

[005-031]army at the siege of Berwick.

[005-032]The smith being thus the earliest and most important of mechanics, it

[005-033]will readily be understood how, at the time when surnames were adopted,

[005-034]his name should have been so common in all European countries.

[005-035]"From whence came Smith, all be he knight or squire,

[005-036]But from the smith that forgeth in the fire?" [21]

[005-037]Hence the multitudinous family of Smiths in [[england|England]], in some cases

[005-038]vainly disguised under the "Smythe" or "De Smijthe;" in Germany, the

[005-039]Schmidts; in Italy, the Fabri, Fabricii, or Fabbroni; in France, the Le

[005-040]Febres or Lefevres; in [[scotland|Scotland]], the Gows, Gowans, or Cowans.  We have

[005-041]also among us the Brownsmiths, or makers of brown bills; the Nasmyths,

[005-042]or nailsmiths; the Arrowsmiths, or makers of arrowheads; the

[005-043]Spearsmiths, or spear makers; the Shoosmiths, or horse shoers; the

[005-044]Goldsmiths, or workers in gold; and many more.  The Smith proper was,

[005-045]however, the worker in iron--the maker of iron tools, implements, and

[005-046]arms--and hence this name exceeds in number that of all the others

[005-047]combined.

[005-048]In course of time the smiths of particular districts began to

[005-049]distinguish themselves for their excellence in particular branches of

[005-050]iron-work.  From being merely the retainer of some lordly or religious

[005-051]establishment, the smith worked to supply the general demand, and

[005-052]gradually became a manufacturer.  Thus the makers of swords, tools,

[005-053]bits, and nails, congregated at [[birmingham|Birmingham]]; and the makers of knives

[005-054]and arrowheads at [[sheffield|Sheffield]].  Chaucer speaks of the Miller of

[005-055]Trompington as provided with a [[sheffield|Sheffield]] whittle:--

[005-056]"A Shefeld thwytel bare he in his hose." [22]

[005-057]The common English arrowheads manufactured at Sheffield were long

[005-058]celebrated for their excellent temper, as Sheffield iron and steel

[005-059]plates are now.  The battle of Hamildon, fought in [[scotland|Scotland]] in 1402,

[005-060]was won mainly through their excellence.  The historian records that

[005-061]they penetrated the armour of the Earl of Douglas, which had been three

[005-062]years in making; and they were "so sharp and strong that no armour

[005-063]could repel them."  The same arrowheads were found equally efficient

[005-064]against French armour on the fields of Crecy and Agincourt.

[005-065]Although Scotland is now one of the principal sources from which our

[005-066]supplies of iron are drawn, it was in ancient times greatly distressed

[005-067]for want of the metal.  The people were as yet too little skilled to be

[005-068]able to turn their great mineral wealth to account.  Even in the time

[005-069]of Wallace, they had scarcely emerged from the Stone period, and were

[005-070]under the necessity of resisting their iron-armed English adversaries

[005-071]by means of rude weapons of that material.  To supply themselves with

[005-072]swords and spearheads, they imported steel from Flanders, and the rest

[005-073]they obtained by marauding incursions into [[england|England]].  The district of

[005-074][[furness|Furness]] in [[lancashire|Lancashire]]--then as now an iron-producing district--was

[005-075]frequently ravaged with that object; and on such occasions the Scotch

[005-076]seized and carried off all the manufactured iron they could find,

[005-077]preferring it, though so heavy, to every other kind of plunder.[23]

[005-078][[About]] the same period, however, iron must have been regarded as almost

[005-079]a precious metal even in England itself; for we find that in Edward the

[005-080]Third's reign, the pots, spits, and frying-pans of the royal kitchen

[005-081]were classed among his Majesty's jewels.[24]

[005-082]The same famine of iron prevailed to a still greater extent in the

[005-083]Highlands, where it was even more valued, as the clans lived chiefly by

[005-084]hunting, and were in an almost constant state of feud.  Hence the smith

[005-085]was a man of indispensable importance among the Highlanders, and the

[005-086]possession of a skilful armourer was greatly valued by the chiefs.  The

[005-087]story is told of some delinquency having been committed by a Highland

[005-088]smith, on whom justice must be done; but as the chief could not

[005-089]dispense with the smith, he generously offered to hang two weavers in

[005-090]his stead!

[005-091]At length a great armourer arose in the Highlands, who was able to

[005-092]forge armour that would resist the best Sheffield arrow-heads, and to

[005-093]make swords that would vie with the best weapons of Toledo and Milan.

[005-094]This was the famous Andrea de Ferrara, whose swords still maintain

[005-095]their ancient reputation.  This workman is supposed to have learnt his

[005-096]art in the Italian city after which he was called, and returned to

[005-097]practise it in secrecy among the Highland hills.  Before him, no man in

[005-098]Great Britain is said to have known how to temper a sword in such a way

[005-099]as to bend so that the point should touch the hilt and spring back

[005-100]uninjured.  The swords of Andrea de Ferrara did this, and were

[005-101]accordingly in great request; for it was of every importance to the

[005-102]warrior that his weapon should be strong and sharp without being

[005-103]unwieldy, and that it should not be liable to snap in the act of

[005-104]combat.  This celebrated smith, whose personal identity[25] has become

[005-105]merged in the Andrea de Ferrara swords of his manufacture, pursued his

[005-106]craft in the Highlands, where he employed a number of skilled workmen

[005-107]in forging weapons, devoting his own time principally to giving them

[005-108]their required temper.  He is said to have worked in a dark cellar, the

[005-109]better to enable him to perceive the effect of the heat upon the metal,

[005-110]and to watch the nicety of the operation of tempering, as well as

[005-111]possibly to serve as a screen to his secret method of working.[26]

[005-112]Long after Andrea de Ferrara's time, the Scotch swords were famous for

[005-113]their temper; Judge Marshal Fatten, who accompanied the Protector's

[005-114]expedition into Scotland in 1547, observing that "the Scots came with

[005-115]swords all broad and thin, of exceeding good temper, and universally so

[005-116]made to slice that I never saw none so good, so I think it hard to

[005-117]devise a better."  The quality of the steel used for weapons of war was

[005-118]indeed of no less importance for the effectual defence of a country

[005-119]then than it is now.  The courage of the attacking and defending forces

[005-120]being equal, the victory would necessarily rest with the party in

[005-121]possession of the best weapons.

[005-122]England herself has on more than one occasion been supposed to be in

[005-123]serious peril because of the decay of her [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]s.  Before

[005-124]the Spanish Armada, the production of iron had been greatly discouraged

[005-125]because of the destruction of timber in the smelting of the ore--the

[005-126]art of reducing it with pit coal not having yet been invented; and we

[005-127]were consequently mainly dependent upon foreign countries for our

[005-128]supplies of the material out of which arms were made.  The best iron

[005-129]came from Spain itself, then the most powerful nation in Europe, and as

[005-130]celebrated for the excellence of its weapons as for the discipline and

[005-131]valour of its troops.  The Spaniards prided themselves upon the

[005-132]superiority of their iron, and regarded its scarcity in England as an

[005-133]important element in their calculations of the conquest of the country

[005-134]by their famous Armada.  "I have heard," says Harrison, "that when one

[005-135]of the greatest peers of Spain espied our nakedness in this behalf, and

[005-136]did solemnly utter in no obscure place, that it would be an easy matter

[005-137]in short time to conquer England because it wanted armour, his words

[005-138]were not so rashly uttered as politely noted."  The vigour of Queen

[005-139]Elizabeth promptly supplied a remedy by the large importations of iron

[005-140]which she caused to be made, principally from Sweden, as well as by the

[005-141]increased activity of the forges in [[sussex|Sussex]] and the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]];

[005-142]"whereby," adds Harrison, "England obtained rest, that otherwise might

[005-143]have been sure of sharp and cruel wars.  Thus a Spanish word uttered by

[005-144]one man at one time, overthrew, or at the leastwise hindered sundry

[005-145]privy practices of many at another." [27]  Nor has the subject which

[005-146]occupied the earnest attention of politicians in Queen Elizabeth's time

[005-147]ceased to be of interest; for, after the lapse of nearly three hundred

[005-148]years, we find the smith and the iron manufacturer still uppermost in

[005-149]public discussions.  It has of late years been felt that our

[005-150]much-prized "hearts of oak" are no more able to stand against the prows

[005-151]of mail which were supposed to threaten them, than the sticks and

[005-152]stones of the ancient tribes were able to resist the men armed with

[005-153]weapons of bronze or steel.  What Solon said to Croesus, when the

[005-154]latter was displaying his great treasures of gold, still holds

[005-155]true:--"If another comes that hath better iron than you, he will be

[005-156]master of all that gold."  So, when an alchemist waited upon the Duke

[005-157]of Brunswick during the Seven Years' War, and offered to communicate

[005-158]the secret of converting iron into gold, the Duke replied:--"By no

[005-159]means:  I want all the iron I can find to resist my enemies:  as for

[005-160]gold, I get it from England." Thus the strength and wealth of nations

[005-161]depend upon coal and iron, not forgetting Men, far more than upon gold.

[005-162]Thanks to our Armstrongs and Whitworths, our Browns and our Smiths, the

[005-163]iron defences of England, manned by our soldiers and our sailors,

[005-164]furnish the assurance of continued security for our gold and our

[005-165]wealth, and, what is infinitely more precious, for our industry and our

[005-166]liberty.
