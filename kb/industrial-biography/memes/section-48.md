[048-001]It is remarkable that the inventor of the steam-hammer should have so

[048-002]effectually contradicted the name he bears and reversed the motto of

[048-003]his family; for so far from being "Nae Smyth," he may not

[048-004]inappropriately be designated the very Vulcan of the nineteenth

[048-005]century.  His hammer is a tool of immense power and pliancy, but for

[048-006]which we must have stopped short in many of those gigantic engineering

[048-007]works which are among the marvels of the age we live in.  It possesses

[048-008]so much precision and delicacy that it will chip the end of an egg

[048-009]resting in a glass on the anvil without breaking it, while it delivers

[048-010]a blow of ten tons with such a force as to be felt shaking the parish.

[048-011]It is therefore with a high degree of appropriateness that Mr. Nasmyth

[048-012]has discarded the feckless hammer with the broken shaft, and assumed

[048-013]for his emblem his own magnificent steam-hammer, at the same time

[048-014]reversing the family motto, which he has converted into "Non Marte sed

[048-015]Arte."

[048-016][[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] belongs to a family whose genius in art has long been

[048-017]recognised.  His father, Alexander Nasmyth of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], was a

[048-018]landscape-painter of great eminence, whose works are sometimes

[048-019]confounded with those of his son Patrick, called the English Hobbema,

[048-020]though his own merits are peculiar and distinctive.  The elder Nasmyth

[048-021]was also an admirable portrait painter, as his head of Burns--the best

[048-022]ever painted of the poet--bears ample witness.  His daughters, the

[048-023]Misses Nasmyth, were highly skilled painters of landscape, and their

[048-024]works are well known and much prized.  James, the youngest of the

[048-025]family, inherits the same love of art, though his name is more

[048-026]extensively known as a worker and inventor in iron.  He was born at

[048-027][[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], on the 19th of August, 1808; and his attention was early

[048-028]directed to mechanics by the circumstance of this being one of his

[048-029]father's hobbies.  Besides being an excellent painter, Mr. Nasmyth had

[048-030]a good general knowledge of architecture and civil engineering, and

[048-031]could work at the lathe and handle tools with the dexterity of a

[048-032]mechanic.  He employed nearly the whole of his spare time in a little

[048-033]workshop which adjoined his studio, where he encouraged his youngest

[048-034]son to work with him in all sorts of materials.  Among his visitors at

[048-035]the studio were Professor Leslie, Patrick Miller of Dalswinton, and

[048-036]other men of distinction.  He assisted Mr. Miller in his early

[048-037]experiments with paddle-boats, which eventually led to the invention of

[048-038]the steamboat.  It was a great advantage for the boy to be trained by a

[048-039]father who so loved excellence in all its forms, and could minister to

[048-040]his love of mechanics by his own instruction and practice.  James used

[048-041]to drink in with pleasure and profit the conversation which passed

[048-042]between his father and his visitors on scientific and mechanical

[048-043]subjects; and as he became older, the resolve grew stronger in him

[048-044]every day that he would be a mechanical engineer, and nothing else.  At

[048-045]a proper age, he was sent to the High School, then as now celebrated

[048-046]for the excellence of its instruction, and there he laid the

[048-047]foundations of a sound and liberal education.  But he has himself told

[048-048]the simple story of his early life in such graphic terms that we feel

[048-049]we cannot do better than quote his own words:--[1]

[048-050]"I had the good luck," he says, "to have for a school companion the son

[048-051]of an iron founder.  Every spare hour that I could command was devoted

[048-052]to visits to his father's [[foundry|iron foundry]], where I delighted to watch the

[048-053]various processes of moulding, iron-melting, casting, forging,

[048-054]pattern-making, and other smith and metal work; and although I was only

[048-055]about twelve years old at the time, I used to lend a hand, in which

[048-056]hearty zeal did a good deal to make up for want of strength.  I look

[048-057]back to the Saturday afternoons spent in the workshops of that small

[048-058]foundry, as an important part of my education.  I did not trust to

[048-059]reading about such and such things; I saw and handled them; and all the

[048-060]ideas in connection with them became permanent in my mind.  I also

[048-061]obtained there--what was of much value to me in after life--a

[048-062]considerable acquaintance with the nature and characters of workmen.

[048-063]By the time I was fifteen, I could work and turn out really respectable

[048-064]jobs in wood, brass, iron, and steel:  indeed, in the working of the

[048-065]latter inestimable material, I had at a very early age (eleven or

[048-066]twelve) acquired considerable proficiency.  As that was the pre-lucifer

[048-067]match period, the possession of a steel and tinder box was quite a

[048-068]patent of nobility among boys.  So I used to forge old files into

[048-069]'steels' in my father's little workshop, and harden them and produce

[048-070]such first-rate, neat little articles in that line, that I became quite

[048-071]famous amongst my school companions; and many a task have I had excused

[048-072]me by bribing the monitor, whose grim sense of duty never could

[048-073]withstand the glimpse of a steel.

[048-074]"My first essay at making a steam engine was when I was fifteen.  I

[048-075]then made a real working; [[steam-engine]], 1 3/4 diameter cylinder, and 8

[048-076]in. stroke, which not only could act, but really did some useful work;

[048-077]for I made it grind the oil colours which my father required for his

[048-078]painting.  Steam engine models, now so common, were exceedingly scarce

[048-079]in those days, and very difficult to be had; and as the demand for them

[048-080]arose, I found it both delightful and profitable to make them; as well

[048-081]as sectional models of steam engines, which I introduced for the

[048-082]purpose of exhibiting the movements of all the parts, both exterior and

[048-083]interior.  With the results of the sale of such models I was enabled to

[048-084]pay the price of tickets of admission to the lectures on natural

[048-085]philosophy and chemistry delivered in the University of Edinburgh.

[048-086][[About]] the same time (1826) I was so happy as to be employed by

[048-087]Professor Leslie in making models and portions of apparatus required by

[048-088]him for his lectures and philosophical investigations, and I had also

[048-089]the inestimable good fortune to secure his friendship.  His admirably

[048-090]clear manner of communicating a knowledge of the fundamental principles

[048-091]of mechanical science rendered my intercourse with him of the utmost

[048-092]importance to myself.  A hearty, cheerful, earnest desire to toil in

[048-093]his service, caused him to take pleasure in instructing me by

[048-094]occasional explanations of what might otherwise have remained obscure.

[048-095]"About the years 1827 and 1828, the subject of steam-carriages for

[048-096]common roads occupied much of the attention of the public.  Many tried

[048-097]to solve the problem.  I made a working model of an engine which

[048-098]performed so well that some friends determined to give me the means of

[048-099]making one on a larger scale.  This I did; and I shall never forget the

[048-100]pleasure and the downright hard work I had in producing, in the autumn

[048-101]of 1828, at an outlay of 60L., a complete steam-carriage, that ran many

[048-102]a mile with eight persons on it.  After keeping it in action two

[048-103]months, to the satisfaction of all who were interested in it, my

[048-104]friends allowed me to dispose of it, and I sold it a great bargain,

[048-105]after which the engine was used in driving a small factory.  I may

[048-106]mention that in that engine I employed the waste steam to cause an

[048-107]increased draught by its discharge up the chimney.  This important use

[048-108]of the waste steam had been introduced by [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] some years

[048-109]before, though entirely unknown to me.

[048-110]"The earnest desire which I cherished of getting forward in the real

[048-111]business of life induced me to turn my attention to obtaining

[048-112]employment in some of the great engineering establishments of the day,

[048-113]at the head of which, in my fancy as well as in reality, stood that of

[048-114][[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], of [[london|London]].  It was the summit of my ambition to get

[048-115]work in that establishment; but as my father had not the means of

[048-116]paying a premium, I determined to try what I could do towards attaining

[048-117]my object by submitting to Mr. Maudslay actual specimens of my

[048-118]capability as a young workman and draughtsman.  To this end I set to

[048-119]work and made a small steam-engine, every part of which was the result

[048-120]of my own handiwork, including the casting and the forging of the

[048-121]several parts.  This I turned out in such a style as I should even now

[048-122]be proud of.  My sample drawings were, I may say, highly respectable.

[048-123]Armed with such means of obtaining the good opinion of the great Henry

[048-124]Maudslay, on the 19th of May, 1829, I sailed for [[london|London]] in a Leith

[048-125]smack, and after an eight days' voyage saw the metropolis for the first

[048-126]time.  I made bold to call on Mr. Maudslay, and told him my simple

[048-127]tale.  He desired me to bring my models for him to look at.  I did so,

[048-128]and when he came to me I could see by the expression of his cheerful,

[048-129]well-remembered countenance, that I had attained my object.  He then

[048-130]and there appointed me to be his own private workman, to assist him in

[048-131]his little paradise of a workshop, furnished with the models of

[048-132]improved machinery and engineering tools of which he has been the great

[048-133]originator.  He left me to arrange as to wages with his chief cashier,

[048-134]Mr. Robert Young, and on the first Saturday evening I accordingly went

[048-135]to the counting-house to enquire of him about my pay.  He asked me what

[048-136]would satisfy me.  Knowing the value of the situation I had obtained,

[048-137]and having a very modest notion of my worthiness to occupy it, I said,

[048-138]that if he would not consider 10s. a week too much, I thought I could

[048-139]do very well with that.  I suppose he concluded that I had some means

[048-140]of my own to live on besides the 10s. a week which I asked.  He little

[048-141]knew that I had determined not to cost my father another farthing when

[048-142]I left-home to begin the world on my own account.  My proposal was at

[048-143]once acceded to.  And well do I remember the pride and delight I felt

[048-144]when I carried to my three shillings a week lodging that night my first

[048-145]wages.  Ample they were in my idea; for I knew how little I could live

[048-146]on, and was persuaded that by strict economy I could easily contrive to

[048-147]make the money support me.  To help me in this object, I contrived a

[048-148]small cooking apparatus, which I forthwith got made by a tinsmith in

[048-149][[lambeth|Lambeth]], at a cost of 6s., and by its aid I managed to keep the eating

[048-150]and drinking part of my private account within 3s. 6d. per week, or 4s.

[048-151]at the outside.  I had three meat dinners a week, and generally four

[048-152]rice and milk dinners, all of which were cooked by my little apparatus,

[048-153]which I set in action after breakfast.  The oil cost not quite a

[048-154]halfpenny per day.  The meat dinners consisted of a stew of from a half

[048-155]to three quarters of a lb.  of leg of beef, the meat costing 3 1/2d.

[048-156]per lb., which, with sliced potatoes and a little onion, and as much

[048-157]water as just covered all, with a sprinkle of salt and black pepper, by

[048-158]the time I returned to dinner at half-past six furnished a repast in

[048-159]every respect as good as my appetite.  For breakfast I had coffee and a

[048-160]due proportion of quartern loaf.  After the first year of my employment

[048-161]under Mr. Maudslay, my wages were raised to 15s. a week, and I then,

[048-162]but not till then, indulged in the luxury of butter to my bread.  I am

[048-163]the more particular in all this, to show you that I was a thrifty

[048-164]housekeeper, although only a lodger in a 3s. room.  I have the old

[048-165]apparatus by me yet, and I shall have another dinner out of it ere I am

[048-166]a year older, out of regard to days that were full of the real romance

[048-167]of life.
