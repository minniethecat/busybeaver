A towering furnace used to smelt iron ore into pig iron by blasting heated air through layers of ore, coke, and limestone — the foundation of industrial-scale iron production from the 18th century onward.

## Definition

The blast furnace is a vertical shaft furnace in which iron ore is reduced to metallic iron through the counterflow of hot reducing gases generated by the combustion of coke. Unlike earlier bloomery furnaces which produced wrought iron directly in small batches, the blast furnace produces liquid pig iron in continuous operation on a much larger scale. (001-263) The product, pig iron, can be cast directly into moulds or refined into [[wrought-iron]] or [[steel]].

## Historical Development

### Origins

The blast furnace evolved from the earlier bloomery and the German *Stückofen* (high bloomery furnace). By the 15th century, larger furnaces with water-powered bellows were producing [[pig-iron|cast iron]] in continental Europe. The first English blast furnace is recorded at Newbridge, [[sussex|Sussex]], in 1496.

> The cast-iron tombstones and slabs in many English churches date from the period of Henry VI. The first cast-iron cannons of English manufacture were made at Buxted in [[sussex|Sussex]] in 1543. (007-110, 007-137)

### The Hot Blast Revolution

The single most important improvement to the blast furnace was the introduction of the hot blast by [[James Beaumont Neilson]] in 1828. Rather than blowing cold air into the furnace, Neilson heated the air before injection.

> Trials with heated air in the blast-furnace proved remarkably successful, producing a given quantity of iron with less than half the fuel previously required. (001-263)

Neilson's innovation dramatically reduced fuel consumption and allowed the use of raw coal instead of coke in some furnaces, fundamentally changing the economics of the Scottish iron industry.

### Scale and Efficiency

Through the late 18th and early 19th centuries, blast furnaces grew in size and efficiency:

> A blast-furnace in 1794 would now be regarded as a mere toy. The weekly production of a large modern furnace equals what was then the annual output of a small ironworks. (026-161)

> The production of pig-iron from the blast-furnace increased with the expansion of the railway system, which created an immense new demand for iron of all kinds. (026-146)

By the mid-19th century, a single blast furnace could produce over 100 tons of pig iron per week, compared to 10–15 tons at the end of the 18th century.

## Process

### Materials

Three principal materials are charged into the top of the blast furnace:

| Material | Role |
|----------|------|
| Iron ore | Source of iron (hematite, magnetite, or clay ironstone) |
| Coke (or coal) | Fuel and reducing agent |
| Limestone | Flux to remove impurities as slag |

### Operation

The blast furnace operates continuously. Hot air at 600–800°C is blown into the lower part of the furnace through water-cooled nozzles called tuyeres. The coke combusts, generating carbon monoxide which rises through the descending ore, reducing iron oxides to metallic iron. The molten iron collects at the bottom (the hearth) and is periodically tapped out. (021-005)

> It comes from the blast-furnace. This is effected by driving a current of air through the molten metal, by which the carbon is burned out. (021-005)

### From Pig Iron to Bar Iron

The pig iron produced by the blast furnace contains 3–4% carbon, making it hard but brittle. To produce malleable [[wrought-iron|bar iron]], the carbon must be removed:

> All methods of manufacturing bar from cast-iron, and of [[puddling-process|puddling]], involve the decarbonization of the pig metal by exposure to an oxidizing flame or current of air. (022-042)

## Economic Impact

The blast furnace transformed iron from a scarce, expensive metal into a cheap, abundant material. Before the blast furnace era, iron production in Britain was measured in thousands of tons; by the 1860s, annual production exceeded four million tons. The availability of cheap pig iron enabled the construction of [[iron-bridge]]s, railways, steam engines, and [[iron-shipbuilding|iron ships]].

> Thirty blast-furnaces were at work at the Carron Ironworks at its height. The office of accountant was a busy one. (025-161)

The concentration of blast furnaces in districts like [[south-wales|South Wales]], [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], and the [[clyde|Clyde]] Valley created industrial landscapes entirely shaped by iron-making.
