[006-008]A knowledge of the mixture of copper, tin, and zinc, seems to have been

[006-009]among the first discoveries of the metallurgist.  Instruments

[006-010]fabricated from these alloys, recommended by the use of ages, the

[006-011]perfection of the art, the splendour and polish of their surfaces, not

[006-012]easily injured by time and weather, would not soon be superseded by the

[006-013]invention of simple iron, inferior in edge and polish, at all times

[006-016]elegance."--(Papers on Iron and Steel, 365-6.) By some secret method

[006-018]invention of steel, the ancients manufactured bronze tools capable of

[006-020]reverence for classic metallurgy, had a bronze razor made with which he

[006-146]CHAPTER II.

[006-147]EARLY ENGLISH IRON MANUFACTURE.

[006-148]"He that well observes it, and hath known the welds of [[sussex|Sussex]], Surry,

[006-149]and [[kent|Kent]]', the grand nursery especially of oake and beech, shal find

[006-150]such an alteration, within lesse than 30 yeeres, as may well strike a

[006-151]feare, lest few yeeres more, as pestilent as the former, will leave

[006-152]fewe good trees standing in those welds.  Such a heate issueth out of

[006-153]the many forges and furnaces for the making of iron, and out of the

[006-154]glasse kilnes, as hath devoured many famous woods within the

[006-155]welds,"--JOHN NORDEN, Surveyors' Dialogue (1607).

[006-156]Few records exist of the manufacture of iron in England in early times.

[006-157]After the Romans left the island, the British, or more probably the

[006-158]Teutonic tribes settled along the south coast, continued the smelting

[006-159]and manufacture of the metal after the methods taught them by the

[006-160]colonists.  In the midst of the insecurity, however, engendered by

[006-161]civil war and social changes, the pursuits of industry must necessarily

[006-162]have been considerably interfered with, and the art of iron-forging

[006-163]became neglected.  No notice of iron being made in [[sussex|Sussex]] occurs in

[006-164]Domesday Book, from which it would appear that the manufacture had in a

[006-165]great measure ceased in that county at the time of the Conquest, though

[006-166]it was continued in the iron-producing districts bordering on Wales.

[006-167]In many of the Anglo-Saxon graves which have been opened, long iron

[006-168]swords have been found, showing that weapons of that metal were in

[006-169]common use.  But it is probable that iron was still scarce, as ploughs

[006-170]and other agricultural implements continued to be made of wood,--one of

[006-171]the Anglo-Saxon laws enacting that no man should undertake to guide a

[006-172]plough who could not make one; and that the cords with which it was

[006-173]bound should be of twisted willows.  The metal was held in esteem

[006-174]principally as the material of war.  All male adults were required to

[006-175]be provided with weapons, and honour was awarded to such artificers as

[006-176]excelled in the fabrication of swords, arms, and defensive armour.[1]

[006-177]Camden incidentally states that the manufacture of iron was continued

[006-178]in the western counties during the Saxon era, more particularly in the

[006-179]Forest of Dean, and that in the time of Edward the Confessor the

[006-180]tribute paid by the city of [[gloucester|Gloucester]] consisted almost entirely of

[006-181]iron rods wrought to a size fit for making nails for the king's ships.

[006-182]An old religious writer speaks of the ironworkers of that day as

[006-183]heathenish in their manners, puffed up with pride, and inflated with

[006-184]worldly prosperity.  On the occasion of St.  Egwin's visit to the

[006-185]smiths of Alcester, as we are told in the legend, he found then given

[006-186]up to every kind of luxury; and when he proceeded to preach unto them,

[006-187]they beat upon their anvils in contempt of his doctrine so as

[006-188]completely to deafen him; upon which he addressed his prayers to

[006-189]heaven, and the town was immediately destroyed.[2]
