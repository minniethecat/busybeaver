[028-001]Mr. Neilson throughout watched carefully over its working, and exerted

[028-002]himself in all ways to promote its usefulness, in which he had the

[028-003]zealous co-operation of the leading workmen themselves, and the

[028-004]gratitude of all.  On the opening of the new and enlarged rooms in

[028-005]1825, we find him delivering an admirable address, which was thought

[028-006]worthy of republication, together with the reply of George Sutherland,

[028-007]one of the workmen, in which Mr. Neilson's exertions as its founder and

[028-008]chief supporter were gratefully and forcibly expressed.[2]

[028-009]It was during the period of his connection with the [[glasgow|Glasgow]] Gas-works

[028-010]that Mr. Neilson directed his attention to the smelting of iron.  His

[028-011]views in regard to the subject were at first somewhat crude, as appears

[028-012]from a paper read by him before the [[glasgow|Glasgow]] Philosophical Society early

[028-013]in 1825.  It appears that in the course of the preceding year his

[028-014]attention had been called to the subject by an iron-maker, who asked

[028-015]him if he thought it possible to purify the air blown into the blast

[028-016]furnaces, in like manner as carburetted hydrogen gas was purified.  The

[028-017]ironmaster supposed that it was the presence of sulphur in the air that

[028-018]caused blast-furnaces to work irregularly, and to make bad iron in the

[028-019]summer months.  Mr. Neilson was of opinion that this was not the true

[028-020]cause, and he was rather disposed to think it attributable to the want

[028-021]of a due proportion of oxygen in summer, when the air was more

[028-022]rarefied, besides containing more aqueous vapour than in winter.  He

[028-023]therefore thought the true remedy was in some way or other to throw in

[028-024]a greater proportion of oxygen; and he suggested that, in order to dry

[028-025]the air, it should be passed, on its way to the furnace, through two

[028-026]long tunnels containing calcined lime.  But further inquiry served to

[028-027]correct his views, and eventually led him to the true theory of

[028-028]blasting.

[028-029]Shortly after, his attention was directed by Mr. James Ewing to a

[028-030]defect in one of the Muirkirk blast-furnaces, situated about half a

[028-031]mile distant from the blowing-engine, which was found not to work so

[028-032]well as others which were situated close to it.  The circumstances of

[028-033]the case led Mr. Neilson to form the opinion that, as air increases in

[028-034]volume according to temperature, if he were to heat it by passing it

[028-035]through a red-hot vessel, its volume would be increased, according to

[028-036]the well-known law, and the blast might thus be enabled to do more duty

[028-037]in the distant furnace.  He proceeded to make a series of experiments

[028-038]at the Gas-works, trying the effect of heated air on the illuminating

[028-039]power of gas, by bringing up a stream of it in a tube so as to surround

[028-040]the gas-burner.  He found that by this means the combustion of the gas

[028-041]was rendered more intense, and its illuminating power greatly

[028-042]increased.  He proceeded to try a similar experiment on a common

[028-043]smith's fire, by blowing the fire with heated air, and the effect was

[028-044]the same; the fire was much more brilliant, and accompanied by an

[028-045]unusually intense degree of heat.

[028-046]Having obtained such marked results by these small experiments, it

[028-047]naturally occurred to him that a similar increase in intensity of

[028-048]combustion and temperature would attend the application of the process

[028-049]to the blast-furnace on a large scale; but being only a gas-maker, he

[028-050]had the greatest difficulty in persuading any ironmaster to permit him

[028-051]to make the necessary experiment's with blast-furnaces actually at

[028-052]work.  Besides, his theory was altogether at variance with the

[028-053]established practice, which was to supply air as cold as possible, the

[028-054]prevailing idea being that the coldness of the air in winter was the

[028-055]cause of the best iron being then produced.  Acting on these views, the

[028-056]efforts of the ironmasters had always been directed to the cooling of

[028-057]the blast, and various expedients were devised for the purpose.  Thus

[028-058]the regulator was painted white, as being the coolest colour; the air

[028-059]was passed over cold water, and in some cases the air pipes were even

[028-060]surrounded by ice, all with the object of keeping the blast cold.

[028-061]When, therefore, Mr. Neilson proposed entirely to reverse the process,

[028-062]and to employ hot instead of cold blast, the incredulity of the

[028-063]ironmasters may well be imagined.  What!  Neilson, a mere maker of gas,

[028-064]undertake to instruct practical men in the manufacture of iron!  And to

[028-065]suppose that heated air can be used for the purpose!  It was

[028-066]presumption in the extreme, or at best the mere visionary idea of a

[028-067]person altogether unacquainted with the subject!

[028-068]At length, however, Mr. Neilson succeeded in inducing Mr. Charles

[028-069]Macintosh of Crossbasket, and Mr. Colin Dunlop of the [[clyde-iron-works|[[clyde|Clyde]] Iron Works]],

[028-070]to allow him to make a trial of the hot air process.  In the first

[028-071]imperfect attempts the air was heated to little more than 80 degrees

[028-072]Fahrenheit, yet the results were satisfactory, and the scoriae from the

[028-073]furnace evidently contained less iron.  He was therefore desirous of

[028-074]trying his plan upon a more extensive scale, with the object, if

[028-075]possible, of thoroughly establishing the soundness of his principle.

[028-076]In this he was a good deal hampered even by those ironmasters who were

[028-077]his friends, and had promised him the requisite opportunities for

[028-078]making a fair trial of the new process.  They strongly objected to his

[028-079]making the necessary alterations in the furnaces, and he seemed to be

[028-080]as far from a satisfactory experiment as ever.  In one instance, where

[028-081]he had so far succeeded as to be allowed to heat the blast-main, he

[028-082]asked permission to introduce deflecting plates in the main or to put a

[028-083]bend in the pipe, so as to bring the blast more closely against the

[028-084]heated sides of the pipe, and also increase the area of heating

[028-085]surface, in order to raise the temperature to a higher point; but this

[028-086]was refused, and it was said that if even a bend were put in the pipe

[028-087]the furnace would stop working.  These prejudices proved a serious

[028-088]difficulty in the way of our inventor, and several more years passed

[028-089]before he was allowed to put a bend in the blast-main.  After many

[028-090]years of perseverance, he was, however, at length enabled to work out

[028-091]his plan into a definite shape at the [[clyde|Clyde]] Iron Works, and its

[028-092]practical value was at once admitted.  At the meeting of the Mechanical

[028-093]Engineers' Society held in May, 1859, Mr. Neilson explained that his

[028-094]invention consisted solely in the principle of heating the blast

[028-095]between the engine and the furnace, and was not associated with any

[028-096]particular construction of the intermediate heating apparatus.  This,

[028-097]he said, was the cause of its success; and in some respects it

[028-098]resembled the invention of his countryman, [[james-watt|James Watt]], who, in

[028-099]connection with the [[steam-engine]], invented the plan of condensing the

[028-100]steam in a separate vessel, and was successful in maintaining his

[028-101]invention by not limiting it to any particular construction of the

[028-102]condenser.  On the same occasion he took the opportunity of

[028-103]acknowledging the firmness with which the English ironmasters had stood

[028-104]by him when attempts were made to deprive him of the benefits of his

[028-105]invention; and to them he acknowledged he was mainly indebted for the

[028-106]successful issue of the severe contests he had to undergo.  For there

[028-107]were, of course, certain of the ironmasters, both English and Scotch,

[028-108]supporters of the cause of free trade in others' inventions, who sought

[028-109]to resist the patent, after it had come into general use, and had been

[028-110]recognised as one of the most valuable improvements of modern times.[3]

[028-111]The patent was secured in 1828 for a term of fourteen years; but, as

[028-112]Mr. Neilson did not himself possess the requisite capital to enable him

[028-113]to perfect the invention, or to defend it if attacked, he found it

[028-114]necessary to invite other gentlemen, able to support him in these

[028-115]respects, to share its profits; retaining for himself only three-tenths

[028-116]of the whole.  His partners were Mr. Charles Macintosh, Mr. Colin

[028-117]Dunlop, and Mr. John Wilson of Dundyvan.  The charge made by them was

[028-118]only a shilling a ton for all iron produced by the new process; this

[028-119]low rate being fixed in order to ensure the introduction of the patent

[028-120]into general use, as well as to reduce to a minimum the temptations of

[028-121]the ironmasters to infringe it.

[028-122]The first trials of the process were made at the blast-furnaces of

[028-123]Clyde and Calder; from whence the use of the hot blast gradually

[028-124]extended to the other iron-mining districts.  In the course of a few

[028-125]years every furnace in [[scotland|Scotland]], with one exception (that at Carron),

[028-126]had adopted the improvement; while it was also employed in half the

[028-127]furnaces of [[england|England]] and Wales, and in many of the furnaces on the

[028-128]Continent and in America.  In course of time, and with increasing

[028-129]experience, various improvements were introduced in the process, more

[028-130]particularly in the shape of the air-heating vessels; the last form

[028-131]adopted being that of a congeries of tubes, similar to the tubular

[028-132]arrangement in the boiler of the locomotive, by which the greatest

[028-133]extent of heating surface was provided for the thorough heating of the

[028-134]air.  By these modifications the temperature of the air introduced into

[028-135]the furnace has been raised from 240 degrees to 600 degrees, or the

[028-136]temperature of melting lead.  To protect the nozzle of the air-pipe as

[028-137]it entered the furnace against the action of the intense heat to which

[028-138]it was subjected, a spiral pipe for a stream of cold water constantly

[028-139]to play in has been introduced within the sides of the iron tuyere

[028-140]through which the nozzle passes; by which means the tuyere is kept

[028-141]comparatively cool, while the nozzle of the air-pipe is effectually

[028-142]protected.[4]

[028-143]This valuable invention did not escape the usual fate of successful

[028-144]patents, and it was on several occasions the subject of protracted

[028-145]litigation.  The first action occurred in 1832; but the objectors

[028-146]shortly gave in, and renewed their licence.  In 1839, when the process

[028-147]had become generally adopted throughout [[scotland|Scotland]], and, indeed, was

[028-148]found absolutely essential for smelting the peculiar ores of that

[028-149]country--more especially Mushet's Black Band--a powerful combination

[028-150]was formed amongst the ironmasters to resist the patent.  The

[028-151]litigation which ensued extended over five years, during which period

[028-152]some twenty actions were proceeding in Scotland, and several in

[028-153][[england|England]].  Three juries sat upon the subject at different times, and on

[028-154]three occasions appeals were carried to the House of Lords.  One jury

[028-155]trial occupied ten days, during which a hundred and two witnesses were

[028-156]examined; the law costs on both sides amounting, it is supposed, to at

[028-157]least 40,000L.  The result was, that the novelty and merit of Mr.

[028-158]Neilson's invention were finally established, and he was secured in the

[028-159]enjoyment of the patent right.

[028-160]We are gratified to add, that, though Mr. Neilson had to part with

[028-161]two-thirds of the profits of the invention to secure the capital and

[028-162]influence necessary to bring it into general use, he realized

[028-163]sufficient to enable him to enjoy the evening of his life in peace and

[028-164]comfort.  He retired from active business to an estate which he

[028-165]purchased in 1851 in the Stewartry of Kirkcudbright, where he is found

[028-166]ready to lend a hand in every good work--whether in agricultural

[028-167]improvement, railway extension, or the moral and social good of those

[028-168]about him.  Mindful of the success of his Workmen's Institution at the

[028-169]Glasgow Gas-Works, he has, almost at his own door, erected a similar

[028-170]Institution for the use of the parish in which his property is

[028-171]situated, the beneficial effects of which have been very marked in the

[028-172]district.  We may add that Mr. Neilson's merits have been recognised by

[028-173]many eminent bodies--by the [[institution-of-civil-engineers|Institution of Civil Engineers]], the

[028-174][[chemical-society|Chemical Society]], and others--the last honour conferred on him being

[028-175]his election as a Member of the [[royal-society|Royal Society]] in 1846.
