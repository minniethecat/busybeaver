[057-001][3] Useful Information for Engineers, 2nd series, 1860, p. 211.

[057-002][4] Lecture at [[derby|Derby]]--Useful Information for Engineers, 2nd series, p.

[057-003]212.

[057-004][5] One of the reasons which induced Kennedy thus early to begin the

[057-005]business of mule-spinning has been related as follows.  While employed

[057-006]as apprentice at Chowbent, he happened to sleep over the master's

[057-007]apartment; and late one evening, on the latter returning from market,

[057-008]his wife asked his success.  "I've sold the eightys," said he, "at a

[057-009]guinea a pound."  "What," exclaimed the mistress, in a loud voice,

[057-010]"sold the eightys for ONLY a guinea a pound!  I never heard of such a

[057-011]thing."  The apprentice could not help overhearing the remark, and it

[057-012]set him a-thinking.  He knew the price of cotton and the price of

[057-013]labour, and concluded there must be a very large margin of profit.  So

[057-014]soon as he was out of his time, therefore, he determined that he should

[057-015]become a cotton spinner.

[057-016][6] The subject will be found fully treated in Mr. Fairbairn's own

[057-017]work, A Treatise on Mills and Mill-Work, embodying the results of his

[057-018]large experience.

[057-019][7] Lives of the Engineers, vol. iii. 416-40.  See also An Account of

[057-020]the Construction of the Britannia and [[tubular-bridge|Conway Tubular Bridge]]s.  By

[057-021][[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]], C.E. 1849.

[057-022][8] Useful Information for Engineers, 2nd series, 225.  The mere list

[057-023]of Mr. Fairbairn's writings would occupy considerable space; for,

[057-024]notwithstanding his great labours as an engineer, he has also been an

[057-025]industrious writer.  His papers on Iron, read at different times before

[057-026]the [[british-association|British Association]], the [[royal-society|Royal Society]], and the Literary and

[057-027]Philosophical Institution of [[manchester|Manchester]], are of great value.  The

[057-028]treatise on "Iron" in the Encyclopaedia Britannica is from his pen, and

[057-029]he has contributed a highly interesting paper to Dr. Scoffern's Useful

[057-030]Metals and their Alloys on the Application of Iron to the purposes of

[057-031]Ordnance, Machinery, Bridges, and House and Ship Building.  Another

[057-032]valuable but less-known contribution to Iron literature is his Report

[057-033]on Machinery in General, published in the Reports on the Paris

[057-034]Universal Exhibition of 1855.  The experiments conducted by Mr.

[057-035]Fairbairn for the purpose of proving the excellent properties of iron

[057-036]for shipbuilding--the account of which was published in the Trans

[057-037]actions of the [[royal-society|Royal Society]] eventually led to his further experiments

[057-038]to determine the strength and form of the Britannia and Conway Tubular

[057-039]Bridges, plate-girders, and other constructions, the result of which

[057-040]was to establish quite a new era in the history of bridge as well as

[057-041]ship building.

[057-042][9] [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] Debate, 7th July, 1862.
