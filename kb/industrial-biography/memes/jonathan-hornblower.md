Jonathan Hornblower was a British engineer and steam engine improver of the late eighteenth century, best known for his involvement in one of the most significant patent law cases of the early [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]: the infringement suit brought by [[Matthew Boulton|Boulton]] and [[James Watt]] against Hornblower and Maberly. (034-014)

## The Patent Case

The case of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] versus Hornblower and Maberly was a landmark patent dispute concerning [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s patent for the separate condenser, the key innovation that made the [[steam-power|Watt steam]] engine dramatically more efficient than earlier [[atmospheric-engine]] designs. (035-003)

[[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] aggressively defended their patent rights, and the suit against Hornblower and Maberly became one of the defining legal battles that shaped early patent law in Britain.

Hornblower had developed improvements to the steam engine that, in the view of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]], infringed upon Watt's protected designs. [[james-watt|James Watt]] himself gave evidence in the case, defending the originality and scope of his invention. (034-014)

## [[steam-engine|Steam Engine]] Development

Though the specific details of Hornblower's engine improvements are thinly documented in [[Samuel Smiles]]'s narrative, his work represented part of the broader wave of steam engine innovation that followed Watt's breakthrough. The legal entanglements with [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] illustrate the tensions between [[patents|patent protection]] and technological progress that characterized the early industrial era.
