New Lanark Mills, situated on the [[clyde|River Clyde]] in Lanarkshire, [[scotland|Scotland]], was one of the largest cotton-manufacturing establishments in Britain during the early 19th century. The mills became internationally famous under the management of Robert Owen, who made them a model of social and industrial reform.

## The Mills

The New Lanark Mills were founded by David Dale in 1785, taking advantage of the water power of the Falls of [[clyde|Clyde]]. The works grew into a substantial complex of [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]] mills, employing hundreds of workers. The mills used water-driven mule-spinning frames and water frames to produce cotton yarn on a large scale.

## [[self-acting-mule|[[self-acting|Self-Acting]] Mule]] Invention

The effort to mechanise mule-spinning engaged many inventors, and New Lanark Mills played a part in this history. William Belly, of the New Lanark Mills, invented one of the early [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] machines. His device was one of several attempts—alongside those of [[william-strutt|William Strutt]] of [[derby|Derby]] and others—to make the [[mule-spinning|spinning mule]] fully automatic, dispensing with the skilled operative who guided the carriage. (055-020)

> "Like so many other inventions, the idea of the self-acting mule was not new. Thus [[william-strutt|Mr. William Strutt]] of Derby, the father of Lord Belper, invented a machine of this sort at an early period; Mr. William Belly, of the New Lanark Mills, invented a second; and various other projectors tried their skill in the same direction." (055-020)

## Robert Owen and Social Reform

Under Robert Owen's management from 1800, New Lanark became famous as a model industrial community. Owen provided housing, education, and improved working conditions for the mill workers and their families, demonstrating that industrial enterprise could be combined with social welfare. The mills drew visitors from across Europe and America.
