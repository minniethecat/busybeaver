# Boring Mill

[[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]]'s precision boring mill (1774) — the first machine capable of boring an iron cylinder true enough for [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s steam engine, solving the fundamental problem that had blocked the development of efficient steam power for a decade.

## Background

[[james-watt|James Watt]] invented the separate condenser in 1765, but for nearly a decade he could not build a working engine because no one could bore a cylinder accurately. The cylinders produced by existing boring methods were out-of-round by as much as three-eighths of an inch — enough to leak steam around any piston. "The engine could act with precision" only if the piston fit within a small tolerance. Yet "better work could not be had" from the ironfounders of the day. (031-204)

[[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]], an ironmaster with a reputation for precision cannon-boring, took on the challenge. He had developed methods for boring cannon barrels true, and he adapted this technology to the much larger cylinders Watt required.

## Inventor

- **Inventor**: [[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]] (1728–1808)
- **Patent**: 1774
- **Ironworks**: Bersham and Bradley, in [[shropshire|Shropshire]] and [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]]
- **Nickname**: "Iron-mad Wilkinson"

## How It Works

Wilkinson's boring mill used a rigid boring bar supported at both ends, with the cutting head mounted in the middle. The cylinder casting was clamped to a sliding carriage that moved past the rotating cutter. The key innovations were:

1. **Supported boring bar**: Both ends of the bar were held in bearings, eliminating the deflection that caused earlier borings to be bell-mouthed
2. **Rigid tool mounting**: The cutter was fixed firmly to the bar, rather than being hand-held
3. **Mechanical feed**: The cylinder carriage was advanced by a leadscrew, ensuring a uniform cut

The result was cylinders bored true to within "the thickness of a thin sixpence" — approximately 0.05 inches over diameters of up to 72 inches.

## Significance

Wilkinson's boring mill was the critical enabling technology for Watt's steam engine. Without it, the steam engine would have remained an inefficient pumping device rather than the universal prime mover of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. The mill also:

- Established the principle of supported-tool precision boring
- Enabled the production of accurate cannon barrels
- Demonstrated that iron could be machined to geometric precision
- Created the demand for ever-more-accurate machine tools
