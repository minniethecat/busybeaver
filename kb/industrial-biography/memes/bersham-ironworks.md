# Bersham Ironworks

Bersham Ironworks near Wrexham was [[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]]'s centre for precision boring in North Wales. The cylinders for [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s early steam engines were bored here using Wilkinson's precision boring mill.

## Historical Background

[[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]] established his ironworks at Bersham after taking over his father's existing operation. The works specialised in cannon-boring, which gave Wilkinson the technical experience that led to his most famous innovation — the precision boring mill.

The ability to bore cylinders to within "the thickness of a thin shilling" of true was the breakthrough that made Watt's steam engine commercially viable. Before this, Watt had struggled for years to find cylinders that were sufficiently steam-tight.

## Precision Boring Innovation

Wilkinson's boring mill at Bersham in 1775 was the first to produce cylinders accurate enough for [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s separate condenser engine. Wilkinson's method used a rigid boring bar supported at both ends, with the cylinder casting rotating around it — a fundamentally different approach from the cantilevered boring bars then in use.

The Bersham works also cast and bored cylinders for [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton & Watt]] engines exported to mines in [[cornwall|Cornwall]]. Wilkinson's monopoly on accurate cylinder boring gave him a strategic position in the early steam engine industry.

## Legacy

Bersham's role in advancing precision engineering was foundational. The techniques developed there spread to other engineering centres, influencing later precision toolmakers like [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] and [[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]]. The works represent one of the key sites where the accuracy required for the machine age was first achieved.
