# Pig Iron

The primary product of the blast furnace and the foundational raw material of the iron industry — a high-carbon (2–4%) iron cast into ingots, destined either for remelting into cast-iron goods or for conversion into wrought iron through [[puddling-process|puddling]] or finery processes.

## Definition

Pig iron is the crude iron obtained directly from smelting iron ore in a blast furnace. It is named for the traditional method of casting the molten iron: the main channel (the "sow") branched into shorter side channels (the "pigs"), resembling a sow suckling piglets. The resulting ingots — typically 20–40 kg each — were the standard trading unit of the iron industry. (012-017)

Chemically, pig iron is an alloy of iron with 2–4% carbon, plus silicon, manganese, phosphorus, and sulphur from the ore and fuel. The high carbon content makes it hard but brittle — it cannot be forged or rolled without first being converted to wrought iron or steel. "From its most imperfect state as pig-iron," the metal was progressively refined through successive operations into [[wrought-iron|bar iron]], steel, and finished products. (017-139)

Pig iron differs from wrought iron primarily in carbon content: pig iron contains 2–4% carbon, while wrought iron contains less than 0.1%. This small chemical difference produces radically different mechanical properties. "Crude or pig iron consists of iron combined with a large portion of carbon," rendering it fusible (easily melted) but brittle, whereas wrought iron is tough and malleable. (019-095)

## Historical Development

### The [[charcoal|Charcoal]] Era

Before the 18th century, pig iron was produced almost exclusively using charcoal as fuel. This limited production: a single furnace consumed the charcoal from several square miles of woodland annually. Britain's dwindling forests and rising demand for iron created an acute fuel crisis in the 17th century.

### The Coke Revolution

The successful smelting of iron with coke (pit-coal) — first attempted by [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]], commercialised by [[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1709 — broke the charcoal constraint. After Darby "succeeded at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in smelting iron ore with pit-coal," pig iron production began its long rise. (021-122) However, for decades after Darby's innovation, coke-smelted pig iron was viewed as inferior and its producers were "constrained to sell the pig-iron to the charcoal-ironmasters," who controlled the market for quality [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. (010-120)

The breakthrough for coke pig iron came with the development of processes for "melting the cast or pig iron in a hearth heated with pit-coal" — the early reverberatory furnace — which allowed the conversion of coke pig iron into acceptable [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. (021-125)

### The Mass Production Era

The combination of coke smelting, [[puddling-process|puddling]], and the hot blast drove pig iron production to extraordinary levels. By 1860, Britain produced over 3.8 million tons of pig iron annually — more than the rest of the world combined. A single large furnace could produce over 100 tons per week. "Making pig-iron with pit coal" had been transformed from an experimental curiosity into the foundation of industrial civilisation. (012-017)

## Production Process

Pig iron is produced in a blast furnace — a tall, refractory-lined shaft charged from the top with iron ore, coke, and limestone flux. A powerful blast of preheated air is blown in at the bottom. The coke burns, generating temperatures over 1,500°C and producing carbon monoxide, which chemically reduces the iron ore to metallic iron. The limestone flux combines with impurities (silica, alumina) to form molten slag.

Periodically — typically every 4–6 hours — the furnace is tapped. The molten iron runs into channels moulded in sand on the cast-house floor. The main runner is the "sow"; the lateral ingot moulds are the "pigs." Once cooled, the pigs are broken from the sow, stacked, and shipped to customers.

## Uses and Markets

Pig iron has three principal destinations:

1. **Foundries**: Pig iron is remelted in a cupola furnace and cast directly into moulds to produce cast-iron goods — pots, stoves, pipes, engine cylinders, machinery frames, and architectural elements. This was the dominant use before the [[puddling-process|puddling]] era.
2. **Forge/Conversion**: Pig iron is refined into wrought iron or steel through [[puddling-process|puddling]], the Bessemer process, or basic oxygen steelmaking. The majority of pig iron production follows this route.
3. **Specialty grades**: Certain pig irons, particularly those smelted from pure ores with charcoal, were prized for producing high-quality castings or for conversion into special steels.

By the late 19th century, the global trade in pig iron was enormous. British pig iron was exported to the United States, continental Europe, and the colonies. The price of pig iron became a benchmark for industrial economic health — a fall in the pig iron price signalled overproduction, while a rise indicated booming demand for railways, ships, and machinery.
