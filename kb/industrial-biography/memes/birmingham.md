Birmingham was the centre of Britain's metal trades in the eighteenth century — the town where "the makers of nails, knives, and all sorts of ironmongery congregated, along with the workers in brass and steel." (005-053)

It was at Birmingham that [[Matthew Boulton]] established the [[Soho Manufactory]], where [[James Watt]]'s steam engine was perfected, and where the partnership of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] transformed the mechanical power available to industry throughout the world.

## The Metal Trades Centre

Birmingham's rise as a manufacturing centre rested on its metal trades. Nail-making, button-turning, buckle-forging, gun-smithing, and the fabrication of brass and steel wares employed thousands of artisans in the town and its surrounding villages.

The town's metalworkers developed a reputation for skill that drew custom from across Britain and abroad. When an inventor or improver needed a prototype tested, it was to "a skilled Birmingham blacksmith" that they sent their design. (006-036)

By the mid-eighteenth century, Birmingham had become "a principal seat of the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]," and men of scientific and practical ability were drawn to settle there. (024-143) Among them was Dr. [[john-roebuck|John Roebuck]], who established himself at Birmingham in 1745 and went on to found the [[Carron Iron Works]] in [[scotland|Scotland]], applying chemical knowledge to the improvement of iron-making. (024-143)

## Boulton, Watt, and Soho

Birmingham's greatest contribution to the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] came through the partnership of [[Matthew Boulton]] and [[James Watt]].

Boulton had inherited a modest metal-working business and transformed it into the [[Soho Manufactory]] — one of the first and most celebrated examples of large-scale factory organisation in Britain. When Watt's separate condenser made the steam engine a practical source of rotary power, Boulton provided the capital, the works, and the manufacturing skill to bring it to market.

The partnership of [[Boulton and Watt]] operated from Soho for a quarter of a century, supplying steam engines to mines, mills, and manufactures across Britain and Europe. The firm became a nursery of engineering talent: men trained at Soho went on to found works in [[Manchester]], [[Glasgow]], [[leeds|Leeds]], and [[london|London]].

## Birmingham and the Industrial Economy

The Birmingham district's industrial character was shaped by the nature of its trades. Unlike [[Manchester]], which was dominated by a single fibre (cotton) and a single process (spinning), Birmingham's economy was diversified across scores of metal trades — from the making of steel pens to the casting of cannon.

The town's manufacturers were known for their "apparent ardour" in pursuing new branches of trade, a restless energy that observers compared to the enterprise of [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]] men in the same period. (007-179)

Birmingham's practical chemists and metalworkers also contributed to the ancillary industries upon which iron-making depended. The refining works at Birmingham supplied chemicals used in the pickling and finishing of iron; the vitriol works provided sulphuric acid for the galvanising trades. (025-124)

## Legacy

By the time the Industrial Biography was written in 1863, Birmingham had grown from a town of metal-working artisans into one of the great manufacturing cities of the world. Its pattern of diversified, skill-intensive manufacture — as opposed to the mill-based mass production of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] — gave it a distinctive character and resilience that persisted through the nineteenth century and beyond.
