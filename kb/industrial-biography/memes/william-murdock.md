# William Murdock

William Murdock (sometimes spelled Murdoch) was a Scottish engineer and inventor associated with the firm of [[Matthew Boulton|Boulton]] and [[James Watt]], who contributed to the early development of the [[locomotive|steam locomotive]].

## Early Career

Murdock was employed by the firm of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] at their Soho works in [[birmingham|Birmingham]], where he distinguished himself as a talented engineer.

## The Steam [[locomotive|Locomotive]]

Murdock built one of the earliest working model steam locomotives, demonstrating the possibility of steam-powered land transport. He is placed by [[Samuel Smiles]] in the lineage of locomotive pioneers, alongside [[Nicolas-Joseph Cugnot|Cugnot]], [[Richard Trevithick]], and the Stephensons. (031-159)

Although Murdock did not develop his locomotive to full commercial scale, his experiments proved influential. Trevithick, who knew of Murdock's work, went on to build the first full-scale steam locomotive in 1801.

## Gas Lighting

Beyond locomotives, Murdock is most famous for his invention of coal gas lighting. He pioneered the practical use of coal gas for illumination, first at his own house in Redruth and later at the Soho works, an innovation that transformed urban life in the 19th century.

## Legacy

William Murdock's contributions spanned both steam locomotion and gas lighting — two of the transformative technologies of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. His name stands in the continuum of locomotive development from the earliest experiments to the age of railways.
