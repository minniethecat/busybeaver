A powered mechanical device that cuts, shapes, or finishes metal with precision and repeatability — the technology that made modern [[Engineering]] possible, enabling the production of steam engines, locomotives, textile machinery, and every other machine of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Definition

A machine tool is a powered machine — typically driven by steam, water, or later electricity — that uses a cutting tool to remove material from a metal workpiece, producing a component of specified dimensions and finish. Unlike hand tools wielded by a craftsman, the machine tool constrains the relative motion of the tool and workpiece, achieving accuracy unattainable by human hand alone. (002-018) The key machine tools of the Industrial Revolution were the [[Lathe]], the [[planing-machine]], the drilling machine, and the slotting machine.

## Historical Development

### Origins in the 18th Century

Before machine tools, all metal parts were shaped by hand with files, chisels, and hammers. A skilled fitter could spend weeks fitting a single [[steam-engine]] cylinder to its piston. The bottleneck was precision: no two handmade parts were identical, so every assembly required individual fitting.

> The early steam engines were constructed by common smiths and millwrights with hammers and chisels. The builders had no means of producing accurate work beyond what their eye and hand afforded. (001-304)

The first self-acting machine tools emerged in the late 18th century. [[John Wilkinson]]'s boring mill (1774) made accurate [[steam-engine]] cylinders possible. [[Henry Maudslay]]'s slide-rest lathe (c. 1800) was the true breakthrough:

> His improvements in machine-tools, particularly his invention of the slide-rest, gave the mechanical engineer a command of accuracy such as the workman's hand could never achieve. (002-018)

### The Maudslay School

Maudslay trained a generation of machine-tool builders who spread the technology throughout British industry. [[Joseph Clement]], [[James Nasmyth]], [[Joseph Whitworth]], and [[Richard Roberts]] all passed through Maudslay's workshop, each contributing innovations:

> The great inventors in the machine-tool world — Bramah, Maudslay, Clement, Fox, Roberts, Nasmyth, and Whitworth. (002-152)

[[Richard Roberts]] developed the self-acting planing machine, the self-acting slotting machine, and improvements to the lathe:

> Automatic machine-tools by the mechanical engineers of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[leeds|Leeds]] soon produced parts with a uniformity that enabled the replacement of broken components by spare parts kept in stock. (032-014)

### Joseph Whitworth and Standardization

[[Joseph Whitworth]] took precision to a new level. His true-plane method and standard screw threads (Whitworth thread, 1841) made interchangeable parts a commercial reality:

> The importance of modern machine-tools rests not only on their productive power but on their ability to produce with uniformity. Without uniformity, the principle of interchangeable manufacture — one of the greatest modern economies — would be impossible. (002-187)

## Industrial Impact

Machine tools were the "mother machines" — they made all other machines possible. Without them, there could be no precision steam engines, no metal-working textile machinery, no locomotives, no [[iron-shipbuilding|iron ships]]. Every subsequent advance in mechanical engineering depended on the prior advance of machine tools.

> Summary of progress in machine-tools: from the rude appliances of the early mechanics to the perfectly self-acting and self-regulating mechanisms that have enabled the engineer to accomplish results which to the older race of handicraftsmen appeared impossible. (002-152)

The social impact was equally profound. Machine tools displaced the old system of apprenticeship that required years to train a craftsman, replacing it with a system in which semi-skilled operatives could tend machines that did the skilled work automatically.
