# Punching Machine

The punching machine — a power-driven tool for cutting precise holes in wrought-iron plates — was first devised by [[Henry Maudslay]] for boiler-plate manufacture and later transformed by [[Richard Roberts]] into the self-acting Jacquard punching machine. Between them, these two inventions mechanised one of the most labour-intensive operations in iron shipbuilding and bridge construction, replacing inaccurate hand-punching with "mathematical accuracy" at industrial speed.

## Background

Before the power-driven punching machine, all holes in iron plates were punched by hand. The work was slow, inaccurate, and physically punishing.

> "The operations of shearing and punching had before been very imperfectly done by hand, with great expenditure of labour." (039-067)

As [[iron-shipbuilding|iron steamships]] grew larger and railway bridges spanned wider rivers, the volume of plate-work soared. A single iron ship required thousands of rivet holes, each punched through thick wrought-iron plate. Hand methods could not keep pace.

## Inventors

- **Original inventor**: [[Henry Maudslay]] — devised the first power punching machine for boiler-plates while fulfilling a [[admiralty|Royal Navy]] contract for ships' iron tanks
- **Major improver**: [[Richard Roberts]] — invented the self-acting Jacquard punching machine (patented 1832), capable of punching any pattern of holes with mathematical precision
- **Later development**: Roberts also created a combined machine for simultaneously shearing iron and punching both webs of angle- or T-iron (047-055)

## How It Works

Maudslay's original machine used a steam-driven punch that descended vertically through the iron plate, shearing out a clean hole against a matching die below. The plate was moved manually between strokes to position each hole.

Roberts's Jacquard punching machine was a far more sophisticated device. It was "a self-acting tool of great power, used for punching any required number of holes, of any pitch and to any pattern, with mathematical accuracy, in bridge or boiler plates." (047-035)

The term "Jacquard" referred to its use of a patterned control mechanism — analogous to the Jacquard loom — that automatically positioned the plate for each successive hole. The operator set the hole pattern once, and the machine executed it repeatedly without further adjustment. This eliminated the cumulative positioning errors inherent in manual plate-handling.

## Development and Impact

The immediate trigger for Roberts's invention came during the construction of [[Robert Stephenson]]'s tubular bridge at Conway in 1848. Workmen combinations (strikes) hampered progress on the iron girders, and "the punching of the iron plates by hand was a tedious and expensive as well as an inaccurate process." (047-043) The contractors found it "absolutely necessary to adopt some new method of punching" to finish on time.

They appealed to Roberts, who "evolved the machine in question during his evening's leisure — for the most part while quietly sipping his tea." (047-046) Once installed, "the contractors were enabled to proceed with the punching of the plates independent of the refractory men, and the work was executed with a despatch, accuracy, and excellence that would not otherwise have been possible." (047-046)

The Jacquard punching machine became a standard tool in bridge and shipyard workshops. [[William Fairbairn]] used it extensively in his shipbuilding operations: "Aided by the Jacquard punching-machine of Roberts, the riveting of plates of the largest size has thus become one of the simplest operations in iron-manufacturing." (056-011)

The combined shearing-and-punching machine that Roberts later developed could shear iron and punch both webs of angle- or T-iron simultaneously to any required pitch, though it was described as "ahead even of this fast-manufacturing age." (047-056)
