[015-049]CHAPTER V.

[015-050]COALBROOKDALE IRON WORKS--THE DARBYS AND REYNOLDSES.

[015-051]"The triumph of the industrial arts will advance the cause of

[015-052]civilization more rapidly than its warmest advocates could have hoped,

[015-053]and contribute to the permanent prosperity and strength of the country

[015-054]far move than the most splendid victories of successful war."--C.

[015-055]BABBAGE, The Exposition of 1851.

[015-056][[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s invention of smelting iron with coke made of pit-coal was,

[015-057]like many others, born before its time.  It was neither appreciated by

[015-058]the iron-masters nor by the workmen.  All schemes for smelting ore with

[015-059]any other fuel than charcoal made from wood were regarded with

[015-060]incredulity.  As for [[dudley|Dudley]]'s Metallum Martis, as it contained no

[015-061]specification, it revealed no secret; and when its author died, his

[015-062]secret, whatever it might be, died with him.  Other improvements were

[015-063]doubtless necessary before the invention could be turned to useful

[015-064]account.  Thus, until a more powerful blowing-furnace had been

[015-065]contrived, the production of pit-coal iron must necessarily have been

[015-066]limited.  Dudley himself does not seem to have been able to make more

[015-067]on an average than five tons a-week, and seven tons at the outside.

[015-068]Nor was the iron so good as that made by charcoal; for it is admitted

[015-069]to have been especially liable to deterioration by the sulphureous

[015-070]fumes of the coal in the process of manufacture.

[015-071]Dr. Plot, in his 'History of [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]],' speaks of an experiment

[015-072]made by one Dr. Blewstone, a High German, as "the last effort" made in

[015-073]that county to smelt iron-ore with pit-coal.  He is said to have "built

[015-074]his furnace at Wednesbury, so ingeniously contrived (that only the

[015-075]flame of the coal should come to the ore, with several other

[015-076]conveniences), that many were of opinion he would succeed in it.  But

[015-077]experience, that great baffler of speculation, showed it would not be;

[015-078]the sulphureous vitriolic steams that issue from the pyrites, which

[015-079]frequently, if not always, accompanies pit-coal, ascending with the

[015-080]flame, and poisoning the ore sufficiently to make it render much worse

[015-081]iron than that made with charcoal, though not perhaps so much worse as

[015-082]the body of the coal itself would possibly do." [1]  Dr. Plot does not

[015-083]give the year in which this "last effort" was made; but as we find that

[015-084]one Dr. Frederic de Blewston obtained a patent from Charles II. on the

[015-085]25th October, 1677, for "a new and effectual way of melting down,

[015-086]forging, extracting, and reducing of iron and all metals and minerals

[015-087]with pit-coal and sea-coal, as well and effectually as ever hath yet

[015-088]been done by charcoal, and with much less charge;" and as Dr. Plot's

[015-089]History, in which he makes mention of the experiment and its failure,

[015-090]was published in 1686, it is obvious that the trial must have been made

[015-091]between those years.

[015-092]As the demand for iron steadily increased with the increasing

[015-093]population of the country, and as the supply of timber for smelting

[015-094]purposes was diminishing from year to year, [[england|England]] was compelled to

[015-095]rely more and more upon foreign countries for its supply of

[015-096]manufactured iron.  The number of English forges rapidly dwindled, and

[015-097]the amount of the home production became insignificant in comparison

[015-098]with what was imported from abroad.  Yarranton, writing in 1676, speaks

[015-099]of "the many iron-works laid down in [[kent|Kent]], [[sussex|Sussex]], [[surrey|Surrey]], and in the

[015-100]north of England, because the iron of Sweadland, Flanders, and Spain,

[015-101]coming in so cheap, it cannot be made to profit here."  There were many

[015-102]persons, indeed, who held that it was better we should be supplied with

[015-103]iron from Spain than make it at home, in consequence of the great waste

[015-104]of wood involved by the manufacture; but against this view Yarranton

[015-105]strongly contended, and held, what is as true now as it was then, that

[015-106]the manufacture of iron was the keystone of England's industrial

[015-107]prosperity.  He also apprehended great danger to the country from want

[015-108]of iron in event of the contingency of a foreign war.  "When the

[015-109]greatest part of the iron-works are asleep," said he, "if there should

[015-110]be occasion for great quantities of guns and bullets, and other sorts

[015-111]of iron commodities, for a present unexpected war, and the Sound happen

[015-112]to be locked up, and so prevent iron coming to us, truly we should then

[015-113]be in a fine case!"

[015-114]Notwithstanding these apprehended national perils arising from the want

[015-115]of iron, no steps seem to have been taken to supply the deficiency,

[015-116]either by planting woods on a large scale, as recommended by Yarranton,

[015-117]or by other methods; and the produce of English iron continued steadily

[015-118]to decline.  In 1720-30 there were found only ten furnaces remaining in

[015-119]blast in the whole [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], where the iron-smelters were

[015-120]satisfied with working up merely the cinders left by the Romans.  A

[015-121]writer of the time states that we then bought between two and three

[015-122]hundred thousand pounds' worth of foreign iron yearly, and that England

[015-123]was the best customer in Europe for Swedish and Russian iron.[2]  By

[015-124]the middle of the eighteenth century the home manufacture had so much

[015-125]fallen off, that the total production of Great Britain is supposed to

[015-126]have amounted to not more than 18,000 tons a year; four-fifths of the

[015-127]iron used in the country being imported from Sweden.[3]

[015-128]The more that the remaining ironmasters became straitened for want of

[015-129]wood, the more they were compelled to resort to cinders and coke made

[015-130]from coal as a substitute.  And it was found that under certain

[015-131]circumstances this fuel answered the purpose almost as well as charcoal

[015-132]of wood.  The coke was made by burning the coal in heaps in the open

[015-133]air, and it was usually mixed with coal and peat in the process of

[015-134]smelting the ore.  Coal by itself was used by the country smiths for

[015-135]forging whenever they could procure it for their smithy fires; and in

[015-136]the midland counties they had it brought to them, sometimes from great

[015-137]distances, slung in bags across horses' backs,--for the state of the

[015-138]roads was then so execrable as not to admit of its being led for any

[015-139]considerable distance in carts.  At length we arrive at a period when

[015-140]coal seems to have come into general use, and when necessity led to its

[015-141]regular employment both in smelting the ore and in manufacturing the

[015-142]metal.  And this brings us to the establishment of the [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]

[015-143]works, where the smelting of iron by means of coke and coal was first

[015-144]adopted on a large scale as the regular method of manufacture.

[015-145]Abraham Darby, the first of a succession of [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]rs who bore

[015-146]the same name, was the son of a farmer residing at Wrensnest, near

[015-147]Dudley.  He served an apprenticeship to a maker of malt-kilns near

[015-148][[birmingham|Birmingham]], after which he married and removed to [[bristol|Bristol]] in 1700, to

[015-149]begin business on his own account.  Industry is of all politics and

[015-150]religions:  thus Dudley was a Royalist and a Churchman, Yarranton was a

[015-151][[house-of-commons|Parliament]]arian and a Presbyterian, and Abraham Darby was a Quaker.  At

[015-152][[bristol|Bristol]] he was joined by three partners of the same persuasion, who

[015-153]provided the necessary capital to enable him to set up works at Baptist

[015-154]Mills, near that city, where he carried on the business of malt-mill

[015-155]making, to which he afterwards added brass and iron founding.

[015-156]At that period cast-iron pots were in very general use, forming the

[015-157]principal cooking utensils of the working class.  The art of casting

[015-158]had, however, made such small progress in England that the pots were

[015-159]for the most part imported from abroad.  Darby resolved, if possible,

[015-160]to enter upon this lucrative branch of manufacture; and he proceeded to

[015-161]make a number of experiments in pot-making.  Like others who had

[015-162]preceded him, he made his first moulds of clay; but they cracked and

[015-163]burst, and one trial failed after another.  He then determined to find

[015-164]out the true method of manufacturing the pots, by travelling into the

[015-165]country from whence the best were imported, in order to master the

[015-166]grand secret of the trade.  With this object he went over to Holland in

[015-167]the year 1706, and after diligent inquiry he ascertained that the only

[015-168]sure method of casting "Hilton ware," as such castings were then

[015-169]called, was in moulds of fine dry sand.  This was the whole secret.

[015-170]Returning to Bristol, accompanied by some skilled Dutch workmen, Darby

[015-171]began the new manufacture, and succeeded to his satisfaction.  The work

[015-172]was at first carried on with great secrecy, lest other makers should

[015-173]copy the art; and the precaution was taken of stopping the keyhole of

[015-174]the workshop-door while the casting was in progress.  To secure himself

[015-175]against piracy, he proceeded to take out a patent for the process in

[015-176]the year 1708, and it was granted for the term of fourteen years.  The

[015-177]recital of the patent is curious, as showing the backward state of

[015-178]English iron-founding at that time.  It sets forth that "whereas our

[015-179]trusty and well-beloved Abraham Darby, of our city of Bristol, smith,

[015-180]hath by his petition humbly represented to us, that by his study,

[015-181]industry, and expense, he hath found out and brought to perfection a

[015-182]new way of casting iron bellied pots and other iron bellied ware in

[015-183]sand only, without loam or clay, by which such iron pots and other ware

[015-184]may be cast fine and with more ease and expedition, and may be afforded

[015-185]cheaper than they can be by the way commonly used; and in regard to

[015-186]their cheapness may be of great advantage to the poor of this our

[015-187]kingdom, who for the most part use such ware, and in all probability

[015-188]will prevent the merchants of England going to foreign markets for such

[015-189]ware, from whence great quantities are imported, and likewise may in

[015-190]time supply other markets with that manufacture of our dominions,

[015-191]&c..... grants the said Abraham Darby the full power and sole privilege

[015-192]to make and sell such pots and ware for and during the term of fourteen

[015-193]years thence ensuing."
