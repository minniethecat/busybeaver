The Rainhill Trials of October 1829 were the competitive tests held by the directors of the [[Liverpool and Manchester Railway]] to determine the most suitable form of motive power for the world's first inter-city passenger railway.

The trials marked the definitive transition from stationary engines and horse-drawn carriages to steam locomotion on railways, and they established [[George Stephenson]]'s *Rocket* as the prototype for locomotive design for a generation.

## Background

The [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway, authorised by [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] in 1826, was the most ambitious railway project yet undertaken in Britain. Its promoters — merchants and manufacturers determined to break the monopoly of the canal companies on freight traffic between the port of [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and the mills of [[manchester|Manchester]] — faced a critical decision: what form of power should draw the trains?

Three options were considered: stationary steam engines pulling ropes, horse-drawn carriages, and self-propelled [[locomotive|steam locomotive]]s. The directors, unable to agree, resolved to settle the question by public competition.

[[John Kennedy]], the [[manchester|Manchester]] cotton-spinner and machine-maker, was appointed one of the three judges "selected because of his sound judgment and proved impartiality, as well as his knowledge of mechanical engineering." (055-026) The other judges were John Rastrick, a [[locomotive|locomotive engine]]er, and Nicholas Wood, a [[coal-mining|colliery]] viewer and railway engineer.

## The Competition

The trials were held on a level stretch of track at Rainhill, some nine miles from [[liverpool|Liverpool]]. The conditions were demanding: each locomotive was required to haul a load of three times its own weight at a speed of at least ten miles an hour, over a distance equivalent to the full journey between [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]].

Five locomotives entered the competition:

- **The Rocket** — designed by George and [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]]
- **The Novelty** — designed by John Braithwaite and John Ericsson
- **The Sans Pareil** — designed by Timothy Hackworth
- **The Perseverance** — designed by Timothy Burstall
- **The Cycloped** — a horse-powered machine, withdrawn before the trials began

The Rocket was the clear winner, achieving speeds of up to 29 miles an hour and demonstrating reliability that none of its rivals could match. Its success rested on two technical innovations: the multi-tubular boiler (increasing the heating surface) and the blast-pipe (using exhaust steam to increase the draught through the fire).

## Significance

The Rainhill Trials were a watershed in the history of transport. They proved conclusively that steam locomotion was not merely a novelty for [[coal-mining|colliery]] tramways but a practical and powerful means of long-distance travel, capable of speeds and loads that far exceeded anything possible by horse or stationary engine.

For [[John Kennedy]], the trials represented the convergence of the two great industries of South [[lancashire|Lancashire]]: the cotton manufacture that had made his fortune, and the railway system that would make [[Manchester]] the hub of a national transport network.

For [[George Stephenson]], the victory at Rainhill confirmed his position as the pre-eminent locomotive builder of the age and launched a career that would make railways the defining infrastructure of the nineteenth century.

> "By these successive steps did this poor Scotch boy become one of the leading men of Manchester." — Smiles, on Kennedy's career, which included his role as judge at Rainhill. (055-031)

The [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway opened in September 1830 and was an immediate commercial success, carrying passengers and freight at speeds and costs that transformed the economic geography of industrial Britain.
