# John Wilkinson

John Wilkinson (1728–1808), known as "Iron-mad Wilkinson," was an industrialist who pioneered the use of [[pig-iron|cast iron]] in construction and engineering. His precision boring mill of 1774 produced cylinders accurate enough for [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s steam engines — a critical advance without which the steam revolution could not have proceeded. (018-178)

## Early Life and Career

Wilkinson was born in 1728 and grew up in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]]. He established works at [[bersham-ironworks|Bersham]] and later at Bradley near Bilston in [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]]. (046-029) Under his management, these works became among the most innovative in Britain, producing cannon, cylinders, pipes, and structural castings.

## Major Contributions

Wilkinson's boring mill was the first machine capable of boring a tolerably true cylinder, solving the problem that had frustrated Watt's early attempts to build a working steam engine. (018-178) Before Wilkinson's invention, cylinders were so imperfect that steam leaked past the piston.

He also built the first iron ship — a barge of cast-iron plates launched on the [[severn|Severn]] in 1787, demonstrating that iron could float despite widespread scepticism. (018-177) He was one of the promoters of the first iron bridge at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1779, alongside [[abraham-darby-iii|Abraham Darby III]]. (018-169)

Wilkinson established iron works in France and introduced English iron-making techniques to the continent. (018-180)

## Legacy

So devoted was Wilkinson to iron that he was buried in 1808 in a cast-iron coffin of his own design, and a cast-iron obelisk was erected over his grave. His boring technology laid the foundation for precision engineering, without which neither [[james-watt|Watt]]'s engines nor the machine tools of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] could have been realised.
