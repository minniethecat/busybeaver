## Overview

Greenwich, situated on the south bank of the [[thames|River Thames]] in south-east [[london|London]], holds a distinguished place in British industrial and scientific history. As the home of the Royal Observatory (founded 1675), Greenwich was the global reference point for timekeeping and navigation, two disciplines essential to the maritime trade that carried British iron, coal, and manufactured goods around the world.

## Maritime and Scientific Significance

The Royal Observatory at Greenwich was established by King Charles II to solve the problem of determining longitude at sea. The accurate timekeeping and astronomical observations conducted at Greenwich underpinned the navigational systems that enabled Britain's global maritime trade. Iron ships, steam engines, and [[locomotive|railway locomotive]]s — the products of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] — were exported worldwide along routes made safe by the science practised at Greenwich.

## Engineering and Industrial Connections

The [[thames|Thames]]-side location of Greenwich placed it at the heart of [[london|London]]'s maritime industrial district. Shipyards, rope walks, and engineering works lined the river, supplying and repairing the merchant fleet and the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]]. The engineers of the age visited Greenwich for its scientific and practical value; [[James Nasmyth]] visited the district, gaining much valuable insight into the manufacturing practices of the capital. (053-147)

Greenwich's connection to the engineering profession was further cemented by its proximity to [[Woolwich]], where the [[woolwich-arsenal|Royal Arsenal]] and the Royal Military Academy served as centres of ordnance manufacturing and engineering education. Together, Greenwich and [[woolwich|Woolwich]] formed a [[thames|Thames]]-side corridor of technical expertise that fed the Industrial Revolution's demand for skilled mechanics, instrument-makers, and engineers.

## The Royal Hospital and Architecture

Greenwich is also notable for the Royal Hospital for Seamen (now the Old Royal Naval College), designed by Sir Christopher Wren and Nicholas [[john-hawks|Hawks]]moor, which stands as one of the finest ensembles of Baroque architecture in Britain. The hospital provided care for retired seamen of the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]], the service that protected the trade routes essential to Britain's industrial prosperity.
