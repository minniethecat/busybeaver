**Forging** is the process of shaping heated iron or [[wrought-iron|Wrought Iron]] by hammering, either by hand or under a power-driven [[tilt-hammer|Tilt Hammer]]. From antiquity until the rise of machine tools, forging was the universal method of converting iron into usable articles — weapons, tools, horseshoes, and machinery components. "It was the knowledge of the art of iron-forging which laid the foundation of many of our great industries." (003-174)

## Definition

Forging involves heating iron to incandescence and then beating it on an anvil with hammers to give it the desired shape, simultaneously refining the metal's grain structure through compressive working. The smith's tools were "of many sorts; but the chief were his hammer, pincers, chisel, tongs, and anvil." (005-016) Unlike [[casting|Cast Iron]], which is poured molten into moulds, [[wrought-iron|forged iron]] retains its fibrous structure and is tougher and more ductile.

## History

### Ancient and Medieval Smithing

The smith occupied a central place in ancient societies. In the Bible, "there was no smith found throughout all the land of Israel" during the Philistine wars, forcing the Israelites to go to their enemies to sharpen their tools. (003-165) In Norse mythology, Thor wielded a hammer, and in Celtic tradition the smith was a semi-divine figure whose craft commanded reverence. (006-092)

The medieval smith was the universal artisan: he made and repaired all iron implements of daily life — "axes, chisels, saws, and hammers for the carpenter, nails for the builder, shoes for the horse, and spears for the man-at-arms; but, above all, he forged swords." (004-147) The quality of a smith's swords was the measure of his craft, and the bards celebrated the greatest sword-makers in verse. (004-167)

### The Catalan Forge and Early Furnace Forging

In the early direct process, iron was produced and forged at the same hearth. The [[catalan-forge|Catalan Forge]], employed in Spain and along the Mediterranean coast, produced small blooms of nearly pure iron that were immediately hammered into bars. The process used [[charcoal|Charcoal]] exclusively and produced iron "having many of the qualities of steel, capable of being used for weapons or tools after a comparatively small amount of forging." (004-043)

### Mechanisation

The [[tilt-hammer|Tilt Hammer]], driven by water power, mechanised the heaviest forging work from the medieval period onward. By the 19th century, [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]'s [[steam-hammer|Steam Hammer]] (1839) and the development of massive forge presses extended forging to pieces of unprecedented size — engine shafts, cannon, and ship components.

## Process and Significance

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Heat source** | [[charcoal|Charcoal]] forge, later coke and coal |
| **Primary tool** | Hammer and anvil, later [[tilt-hammer]], steam hammer |
| **Products** | Weapons, tools, nails, horseshoes, machinery components, structural iron |
| **Distinction** | Forged iron = tough, fibrous; [[cast-iron|Cast Iron]] = hard, brittle |

The art of forging was fundamental to civilisation. As Smiles observed, without the smith's hammer there could be no agriculture, no building, no machinery, and no defence. The industrial age did not eliminate forging but transformed it — from the village smithy to the great forge-shops of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[birmingham|Birmingham]], where steam hammers shaped iron on a scale the ancient smiths could never have imagined.
