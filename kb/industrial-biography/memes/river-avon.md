The River Avon was an early focus of English inland navigation improvement in the seventeenth century. [[Andrew Yarranton]], the pioneering ironmaster and engineer, was engaged to survey the river and plan the opening up of its navigation for commercial transport.

## Navigation Improvements

Andrew Yarranton was commissioned to survey the River Avon to find out how the navigation might be improved for industrial transport. (013-153) His work opening up the navigation of the Avon was part of a broader seventeenth-century effort to connect [[england|England]]'s rivers and improve the movement of raw materials — particularly iron, coal, and timber — between production centres and markets. (013-006)

## Industrial Context

The Avon navigation project represented an early phase of English canal and river engineering that predated the great canal-building era of the later eighteenth century. Yarranton's survey work on the Avon sat alongside his other navigation proposals, including schemes for the [[River Severn]] and other waterways, forming part of his vision for an interconnected inland transport network.
