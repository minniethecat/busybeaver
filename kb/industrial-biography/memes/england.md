# England

England was the birthplace of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] and the centre of the world's [[iron-industry|iron trade]] in the 18th and 19th centuries. From the iron forges of the West of England and the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], through the great iron districts of the [[midlands|Midlands]] and the North, to the engineering workshops of [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[birmingham|Birmingham]], England's industrial landscape was shaped by iron. (004-131) (005-073)

## Historical Context

### Early Iron Scarcity

England was not always rich in iron. At the time of the Spanish Armada in 1588, there was a notable scarcity of iron in England. (001-107) The production of English iron was greatly reduced in subsequent centuries as the ancient woods that fuelled the charcoal furnaces were depleted. (001-125) England herself was on more than one occasion supposed to be in danger of running out of workable iron ore. (005-122)

> "England herself has on more than one occasion been supposed to be in danger of exhaustion of her mineral resources." (005-122)

### The Smith Tradition

The importance of iron-working to English society was reflected in the multitudinous family of Smiths in England, in some cases the name originating from the occupation itself. (005-037) Iron was so valuable that it was regarded as a precious metal even in England itself; for in Edward the Confessor's time, iron was listed among the precious commodities. (005-079) The strength and wealth of England were built on iron, and as the saying went, the iron defences of England, manned by soldiers and sailors, protected the nation. (005-160) (005-163)

## Industrial Districts

### The West of England

The West of England was an early centre of iron-making, with forges operating both in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] and in [[south-wales|South Wales]]. (004-131) Traces of ancient iron-smelting were found in many parts of England, sometimes in heaps as if they had been thrown away by early bloomery workers. (004-092)

### The Midlands and North

The expansion of the English [[iron-industry|iron trade]] in the 18th century centred on the coal and iron districts of the Midlands — [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], [[shropshire|Shropshire]], and [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]] — and the North, including [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], [[lancashire|Lancashire]], and the [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]] coalfields. English ironmasters competed with their Scotch counterparts, and the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of both nations expanded together through the application of new technologies. (028-107)

## Key Regions

- [[forest-of-dean]] — ancient iron-mining district in [[gloucester|Gloucester]]shire
- [[birmingham]] — the hardware and engineering capital of the Midlands
- [[manchester]] — the cotton and engineering hub of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]
- [[liverpool]] — the great port of England's Atlantic trade
- [[london]] — the imperial capital and centre of scientific societies
