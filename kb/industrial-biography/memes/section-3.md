[003-001]The injunction given to man to "replenish the earth and subdue it"

[003-002]could not possibly be fulfilled with implements of stone.  To fell a

[003-003]tree with a flint hatchet would occupy the labour of a month, and to

[003-004]clear a small patch of ground for purposes of culture would require the

[003-005]combined efforts of a tribe.  For the same reason, dwellings could not

[003-006]be erected; and without dwellings domestic tranquillity, security,

[003-007]culture, and refinement, especially in a rude climate, were all but

[003-008]impossible.  Mr. Emerson well observes, that "the effect of a house is

[003-009]immense on human tranquillity, power, and refinement.  A man in a cave

[003-010]or a camp--a nomad--dies with no more estate than the wolf or the horse

[003-011]leaves.  But so simple a labour as a house being achieved, his  chief

[003-012]enemies are kept at bay.  He is safe from the teeth of wild animals,

[003-013]from frost, sunstroke, and weather; and fine faculties begin to yield

[003-014]their fine harvest.  Inventions and arts are born, manners, and social

[003-015]beauty and delight."  But to build a house which should serve for

[003-016]shelter, for safety, and for comfort--in a word, as a home for the

[003-017]family, which is the nucleus of society--better tools than those of

[003-018]stone were absolutely indispensable.

[003-019]Hence most of the early European tribes were nomadic:  first hunters,

[003-020]wandering about from place to place like the American Indians, after

[003-021]the game; then shepherds, following the herds of animals which they had

[003-022]learnt to tame, from one grazing-ground to another, living upon their

[003-023]milk and flesh, and clothing themselves in their skins held together by

[003-024]leathern thongs.  It was only when implements of metal had been

[003-025]invented that it was possible to practise the art of agriculture with

[003-026]any considerable success.  Then tribes would cease from their

[003-027]wanderings, and begin to form settlements, homesteads, villages, and

[003-028]towns.  An old Scandinavian legend thus curiously illustrates this last

[003-029]period:--There was a giantess whose daughter one day saw a husbandman

[003-030]ploughing in the field.  She ran and picked him up with her finger and

[003-031]thumb, put him and his plough and oxen into her apron, and carried them

[003-032]to her mother, saying, "Mother, what sort of beetle is this that I have

[003-033]found wriggling in the sand?" But the mother said, "Put it away, my

[003-034]child; we must begone out of this land, for these people will dwell in

[003-035]it."

[003-036]M. Worsaae of Copenhagen, who has been followed by other antiquaries,

[003-037]has even gone so far as to divide the natural history of civilization

[003-038]into three epochs, according to the character of the tools used in

[003-039]each.  The first was the Stone period, in which the implements chiefly

[003-040]used were sticks, bones, stones, and flints.  The next was the Bronze

[003-041]period, distinguished by the introduction and general use of a metal

[003-042]composed of copper and tin, requiring a comparatively low degree of

[003-043]temperature to smelt it, and render it capable of being fashioned into

[003-044]weapons, tools, and implements; to make which, however, indicated a

[003-045]great advance in experience, sagacity, and skill in the manipulation of

[003-046]metals.  With tools of bronze, to which considerable hardness could be

[003-047]given, trees were felled, stones hewn, houses and ships built, and

[003-048]agriculture practised with comparative facility.  Last of all came the

[003-049]Iron period, when the art of smelting and working that most difficult

[003-050]but widely diffused of the minerals was discovered; from which point

[003-051]the progress made in all the arts of life has been of the most

[003-052]remarkable character.

[003-053]Although Mr. Wright rejects this classification as empirical, because

[003-054]the periods are not capable of being clearly defined, and all the three

[003-055]kinds of implements are found to have been in use at or about the same

[003-056]time,[2] there is, nevertheless, reason to believe that it is, on the

[003-057]whole, well founded.  It is doubtless true that implements of stone

[003-058]continued in use long after those of bronze and iron had been invented,

[003-059]arising most probably from the dearness and scarcity of articles of

[003-060]metal; but when the art of smelting and working in iron and steel had

[003-061]sufficiently advanced, the use of stone, and afterwards of bronze tools

[003-062]and weapons, altogether ceased.

[003-063]The views of M. Worsaae, and the other Continental antiquarians who

[003-064]follow his classification, have indeed received remarkable confirmation

[003-065]of late years, by the discoveries which have been made in the beds of

[003-066]most of the Swiss lakes.[3]  It appears that a subsidence took place in

[003-067]the waters of the Lake of Zurich in the year 1854, laying bare

[003-068]considerable portions of its bed.  The adjoining proprietors proceeded

[003-069]to enclose the new land, and began by erecting permanent dykes to

[003-070]prevent the return of the waters.  While carrying on the works, several

[003-071]rows of stakes were exposed; and on digging down, the labourers turned

[003-072]up a number of pieces of charred wood, stones blackened by fire,

[003-073]utensils, bones, and other articles, showing that at some remote

[003-074]period, a number of human beings had lived over the spot, in dwellings

[003-075]supported by stakes driven into the bed of the lake.

[003-076]The discovery having attracted attention, explorations were made at

[003-077]other places, and it was shortly found that there was scarcely a lake

[003-078]in Switzerland which did not yield similar evidence of the existence of

[003-079]an ancient Lacustrine or Lake-dwelling population.  Numbers of their

[003-080]tools and implements were brought to light--stone axes and saws, flint

[003-081]arrowheads, bone needles, and such like--mixed with the bones of wild

[003-082]animals slain in the chase; pieces of old boats, portions of twisted

[003-083]branches, bark, and rough planking, of which their dwellings had been

[003-084]formed, the latter still bearing the marks of the rude tools by which

[003-085]they had been laboriously cut.  In the most ancient, or lowest series

[003-086]of deposits, no traces of metal, either of bronze or iron, were

[003-087]discovered; and it is most probable that these lake-dwellers lived in

[003-088]as primitive a state as the South Sea islanders discovered by Captain

[003-089]Cook, and that the huts over the water in which they lived resembled

[003-090]those found in Papua and Borneo, and the islands of the Salomon group,

[003-091]to this day.

[003-092]These aboriginal Swiss lake-dwellers seem to have been succeeded by a

[003-093]race of men using tools, implements, and ornaments of bronze.  In some

[003-094]places the remains of this bronze period directly overlay those of the

[003-095]stone period, showing the latter to have been the most ancient; but in

[003-096]others, the village sites are altogether distinct.  The articles with

[003-097]which the metal implements are intermixed, show that considerable

[003-098]progress had been made in the useful arts.  The potter's wheel had been

[003-099]introduced.  Agriculture had begun, and wild animals had given place to

[003-100]tame ones.  The abundance of bronze also shows that commerce must have

[003-101]existed to a certain extent; for tin, which enters into its

[003-102]composition, is a comparatively rare metal, and must necessarily have

[003-103]been imported from other European countries.

[003-104]The Swiss antiquarians are of opinion that the men of bronze suddenly

[003-105]invaded and extirpated the men of flint; and that at some still later

[003-106]period, another stronger and more skilful race, supposed to have been

[003-107]Celts from Gaul, came armed with iron weapons, to whom the men of

[003-108]bronze succumbed, or with whom, more probably, they gradually

[003-109]intermingled.  When iron, or rather steel, came into use, its

[003-110]superiority in affording a cutting edge was so decisive that it seems

[003-111]to have supplanted bronze almost at once;[4] the latter metal

[003-112]continuing to be employed only for the purpose of making scabbards or

[003-113]sword-handles.  Shortly after the commencement of the iron age, the

[003-114]lake-habitations were abandoned, the only settlement of this later

[003-115]epoch yet discovered being that at Tene, on Lake Neufchatel:  and it is

[003-116]a remarkable circumstance, showing the great antiquity of the

[003-117]lake-dwellings, that they are not mentioned by any of the Roman

[003-118]historians.

[003-119]That iron should have been one of the last of the metals to come into

[003-120]general use, is partly accounted for by the circumstance that iron,

[003-121]though one of the most generally diffused of minerals, never presents

[003-122]itself in a natural state, except in meteorites; and that to recognise

[003-123]its ores, and then to separate the metal from its matrix, demands the

[003-124]exercise of no small amount of observation and invention.  Persons

[003-125]unacquainted with minerals would be unable to discover the slightest

[003-126]affinity between the rough ironstone as brought up from the mine, and

[003-127]the iron or steel of commerce.  To unpractised eyes they would seem to

[003-128]possess no properties in common, and it is only after subjecting the

[003-129]stone to severe processes of manufacture that usable metal can be

[003-130]obtained from it.  The effectual reduction of the ore requires an

[003-131]intense heat, maintained by artificial methods, such as furnaces and

[003-132]blowing apparatus.[5]  But it is principally in combination with other

[003-133]elements that iron is so valuable when compared with other metals.

[003-134]Thus, when combined with carbon, in varying proportions, substances are

[003-135]produced, so different, but each so valuable, that they might almost be

[003-136]regarded in the light of distinct metals,--such, for example, as

[003-137]cast-iron, and cast and bar steel; the various qualities of iron

[003-138]enabling it to be used for purposes so opposite as a steel pen and a

[003-139]railroad, the needle of a mariner's compass and an Armstrong gun, a

[003-140]surgeon's lancet and a steam engine, the mainspring of a watch and an

[003-141]iron ship, a pair of scissors and a Nasmyth hammer, a lady's earrings

[003-142]and a tubular bridge.

[003-143]The variety of purposes to which iron is thus capable of being applied,

[003-144]renders it of more use to mankind than all the other metals combined.

[003-145]Unlike iron, gold is found pure, and in an almost workable state; and

[003-146]at an early period in history, it seems to have been much more

[003-147]plentiful than iron or steel.  But gold was unsuited for the purposes

[003-148]of tools, and would serve for neither a saw, a chisel, an axe, nor a

[003-149]sword; whilst tempered steel could answer all these purposes.  Hence we

[003-150]find the early warlike nations making the backs of their swords of gold

[003-151]or copper, and economizing their steel to form the cutting edge.  This

[003-152]is illustrated by many ancient Scandinavian weapons in the museum at

[003-153]Copenhagen, which indicate the greatest parsimony in the use of steel

[003-154]at a period when both gold and copper appear to have been comparatively

[003-155]abundant.

[003-156]The knowledge of smelting and working in iron, like most other arts,

[003-157]came from the East.  Iron was especially valued for purposes of war, of

[003-158]which indeed it was regarded as the symbol, being called "Mars" by the

[003-159]Romans.[6]  We find frequent mention of it in the Bible.  One of the

[003-160]earliest notices of the metal is in connexion with the conquest of

[003-161]Judea by the Philistines.  To complete the subjection of the

[003-162]Israelites, their conquerors made captive all the smiths of the land,

[003-163]and carried them away.  The Philistines felt that their hold of the

[003-164]country was insecure so long as the inhabitants possessed the means of

[003-165]forging weapons.  Hence "there was no smith found throughout all the

[003-166]land of Israel; for the Philistines said, Lest the Hebrews make them

[003-167]swords or spears.  But the Israelites went down to the Philistines, to

[003-168]sharpen every man his share, and his coulter, and his axe, and his

[003-169]mattock." [7]

[003-170]At a later period, when Jerusalem was taken by the Babylonians, one of

[003-171]their first acts was to carry the smiths and other craftsmen captives

[003-172]to Babylon.[8]  Deprived of their armourers, the Jews were rendered

[003-173]comparatively powerless.

[003-174]It was the knowledge of the art of iron-forging which laid the

[003-175]foundation of the once great empire of the Turks.  Gibbon relates that

[003-176]these people were originally the despised slaves of the powerful Khan

[003-177]of the Geougen.  They occupied certain districts of the mountain-ridge

[003-178]in the centre of Asia, called Imaus, Caf, and Altai, which yielded iron

[003-179]in large quantities.  This metal the Turks were employed by the Khan to

[003-180]forge for his use in war.  A bold leader arose among them, who

[003-181]persuaded the ironworkers that the arms which they forged for their

[003-182]masters might in their own hands become the instruments of freedom.

[003-183]Sallying forth from their mountains, they set up their standard, and

[003-184]their weapons soon freed them.  For centuries after, the Turkish nation

[003-185]continued to celebrate the event of their liberation by an annual

[003-186]ceremony, in which a piece of iron was heated in the fire, and a

[003-187]smith's hammer was successively handled by the prince and his nobles.
