[021-001]Among the improved processes invented of late years for the manufacture

[021-002]of steel are those of Heath, Mushet, and Bessemer.  The last promises

[021-003]to effect before long an entire revolution in the iron and steel trade.

[021-004]By it the crude metal is converted by one simple process, directly as

[021-005]it comes from the blast-furnace.  This is effected by driving through

[021-006]it, while still in a molten state, several streams of atmospheric air,

[021-007]on which the carbon of the [[pig-iron|crude iron]] unites with the oxygen of the

[021-008]atmosphere, the temperature is greatly raised, and a violent ebullition

[021-009]takes place, during which, if the process be continued, that part of

[021-010]the carbon which appears to be mechanically mixed and diffused through

[021-011]the [[pig-iron|crude iron]] is entirely consumed.  The metal becomes thoroughly

[021-012]cleansed, the slag is ejected and removed, while the sulphur and other

[021-013]volatile matters are driven off; the result being an ingot of malleable

[021-014]iron of the quality of charcoal iron.  An important feature in the

[021-015]process is, that by stopping it at a particular stage, immediately

[021-016]following the boil, before the whole of the carbon has been abstracted

[021-017]by the oxygen, the crude iron will be found to have passed into the

[021-018]condition of cast-steel of ordinary quality.  By continuing the

[021-019]process, the metal losing its carbon, it passes from hard to soft

[021-020]steel, thence to steely iron, and last of all to very soft iron; so

[021-021]that by interrupting the process at any stage, or continuing it to the

[021-022]end, almost any quality of iron and steel may be obtained.  One of the

[021-023]most valuable forms of the metal is described by Mr. Bessemer as

[021-024]"semi-steel," being in hardness about midway between ordinary

[021-025]cast-steel and soft [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]].  The Bessemer processes are now in

[021-026]full operation in [[england|England]] as well as abroad, both for converting crude

[021-027]into [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]], and for producing steel; and the results are

[021-028]expected to prove of the greatest practical utility in all cases where

[021-029]iron and steel are extensively employed.

[021-030]Yet, like every other invention, this of Mr. Bessemer had long been

[021-031]dreamt of, if not really made.  We are informed in Warner's Tour

[021-032]through the Northern.  Counties of [[england|England]], published at Bath in 1801,

[021-033]that a Mr. Reed of Whitehaven had succeeded at that early period in

[021-034]making steel direct from the ore; and [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] clearly alludes to the

[021-035]process in his "Papers on Iron and Steel."  Nevertheless, Mr. Bessemer

[021-036]is entitled to the merit of working out the idea, and bringing the

[021-037]process to perfection, by his great skill and indomitable perseverance.

[021-038]In the Heath process, carburet of manganese is employed to aid the

[021-039]conversion of iron into steel, while it also confers on the metal the

[021-040]property of welding and working more soundly under the hammer--a fact

[021-041]discovered by [[josiah-heath|Mr. Heath]] while residing in India.  [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]]'s process

[021-042]is of a similar character.  Another inventor, Major Uchatius, an

[021-043]Austrian engineer, granulates crude iron while in a molten state by

[021-044]pouring it into water, and then subjecting it to the process of

[021-045]conversion.  Some of the manufacturers still affect secrecy in their

[021-046]operations; but as one of the Sanderson firm--famous for the excellence

[021-047]of their steel--remarked to a visitor when showing him over their

[021-048]works, "the great secret is to have the courage to be honest--a spirit

[021-049]to purchase the best material, and the means and disposition to do

[021-050]justice to it in the manufacture."

[021-051]It remains to be added, that much of the success of the [[sheffield|Sheffield]]

[021-052]manufactures is attributable to the practical skill of the workmen, who

[021-053]have profited by the accumulated experience treasured up by their class

[021-054]through many generations.  The results of the innumerable experiments

[021-055]conducted before their eyes have issued in a most valuable though

[021-056]unwritten code of practice, the details of which are known only to

[021-057]themselves.  They are also a most laborious class; and Le Play says of

[021-058]them, when alluding to the fact of a single workman superintending the

[021-059]operations of three steel-casting furnaces--"I have found nowhere in

[021-060]Europe, except in England, workmen able for an entire day, without any

[021-061]interval of rest, to undergo such toilsome and exhausting labour as

[021-062]that performed by these [[sheffield|Sheffield]] workmen."

[021-085]CHAPTER VII.

[021-086]THE INVENTIONS OF HENRY CORT.

[021-087]"I have always found it in mine own experience an easier matter to

[021-088]devise manie and profitable inventions, than to dispose of one of them

[021-089]to the good of the author himself."--Sir Hugh Platt, 1589.

[021-090][[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] was born in 1740 at Lancaster, where his father carried on

[021-091]the trade of a builder and brickmaker.  Nothing is known as to Henry's

[021-092]early history; but he seems to have raised himself by his own efforts

[021-093]to a respectable position.  In 1765 we find him established in [[surrey|Surrey]]

[021-094]Street, Strand, carrying on the business of a navy agent, in which he

[021-095]is said to have realized considerable profits.  It was while conducting

[021-096]this business that he became aware of the inferiority of British iron

[021-097]compared with that obtained from foreign countries.  The English

[021-098]wrought iron was considered so bad that it was prohibited from all

[021-099]government supplies, while the [[pig-iron|cast iron]] was considered of too brittle

[021-100]a nature to be suited for general use.[1]  Indeed the Russian

[021-101]government became so persuaded that the English nation could not carry

[021-102]on their manufactures without Russian iron, that in 1770 they ordered

[021-103]the price to be raised from 70 and 80 copecs per pood to 200 and 220

[021-104]copecs per pood.[2]

[021-105]Such being the case, Cort's attention became directed to the subject in

[021-106]connection with the supply of iron to the Navy, and he entered on a

[021-107]series of experiments with the object of improving the manufacture of

[021-108]English iron.  What the particular experiments were, and by what steps

[021-109]he arrived at results of so much importance to the British [[iron-industry|iron trade]],

[021-110]no one can now tell.  All that is known is, that about the year 1775 he

[021-111]relinquished his business as a navy agent, and took a lease of certain

[021-112]premises at Fontley, near Fareham, at the north-western corner of

[021-113][[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] Harbour, where he erected a forge and an iron mill.  He was

[021-114]afterwards joined in partnership by Samuel Jellicoe (son of Adam

[021-115]Jellicoe, then Deputy-Paymaster of Seamen's Wages), which turned out,

[021-116]as will shortly appear, a most unfortunate connection for Cort.

[021-117]As in the case of other inventions, Cort took up the manufacture of

[021-118]iron at the point to which his predecessors had brought it, carrying it

[021-119]still further, and improving upon their processes.  We may here briefly

[021-120]recite the steps by which the manufacture of bar-iron by means of

[021-121]pit-coal had up to this time been advanced.  In 1747, Mr. Ford

[021-122]succeeded at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in smelting iron ore with pit-coal, after

[021-123]which it was refined in the usual way by means of coke and charcoal.

[021-124]In 1762, [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] (hereafter to be referred to) took out a patent

[021-125]for melting the cast or pig iron in a hearth heated with pit-coal by

[021-126]the blast of bellows, and then working the iron until it was reduced to

[021-127]nature, or metallized, as it was termed; after which it was exposed to

[021-128]the action of a hollow pit-coal fire urged by a blast, until it was

[021-129]reduced to a loop and drawn out into bar-iron under a common

[021-130]forge-hammer.  Then the brothers Cranege, in 1766, adopted the

[021-131]reverberatory or [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]], in which they placed the pig or cast

[021-132]iron, and without blast or the addition of anything more than common

[021-133]raw pit-coal, converted the same into good malleable iron, which being

[021-134]taken red hot from the reverberatory furnace to the [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]], was

[021-135]drawn into bars according to the will of the workman.  [[peter-onions|Peter Onions]] of

[021-136]Merthyr Tydvil, in 1783, carried the manufacture a stage further, as

[021-137]described by him in his patent of that year.  Having charged his

[021-138]furnace ("bound with iron work and well annealed") with pig or fused

[021-139]cast iron from the smelting furnace, it was closed up and the doors

[021-140]were luted with sand.  The fire was urged by a blast admitted

[021-141]underneath, apparently for the purpose of keeping up the combustion of

[021-142]the fuel on the grate.  Thus Onions' furnace was of the nature of a

[021-143][[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] furnace]], the fire of which was urged by a blast.  The fire was

[021-144]to be kept up until the metal became less fluid, and "thickened into a

[021-145]kind of froth, which the workman, by opening the door, must turn and

[021-146]stir with a bar or other iron instrument, and then close the aperture

[021-147]again, applying the blast and fire until there was a ferment in the

[021-148]metal."  The patent further describes that "as the workman stirs the

[021-149]metal," the scoriae will separate, "and the particles of iron will

[021-150]adhere, which particles the workman must collect or gather into a mass

[021-151]or lump."  This mass or lump was then to be raised to a white heat, and

[021-152]forged into malleable iron at the forge-hammer.

[021-153]Such was the stage of advance reached in the manufacture of bar-iron,

[021-154]when [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] published his patents in 1783 and 1784.  In dispensing

[021-155]with a blast, he had been anticipated by [[cranege-brothers|the Craneges]], and in the

[021-156]process of puddling by Onions; but he introduced so many improvements

[021-157]of an original character, with which he combined the inventions of his

[021-158]predecessors, as to establish quite a new era in the history of the

[021-159][[iron-industry|iron manufacture]], and, in the course of a few years, to raise it to the

[021-160]highest state of prosperity.  As early as 1786, Lord Sheffield

[021-161]recognised the great national importance of Cort's improvements in the

[021-162]following words:--"If Mr. Cort's very ingenious and meritorious

[021-163]improvements in the art of making and working iron, the [[steam-engine]] of

[021-164][[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]], and [[lord-dundonald|Lord Dundonald]]'s discovery of making coke at half

[021-165]the present price, should all succeed, it is not asserting too much to

[021-166]say that the result will be more advantageous to Great Britain than the

[021-167]possession of the thirteen colonies (of America); for it will give the

[021-168]complete command of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] to this country, with its vast

[021-169]advantages to navigation."  It is scarcely necessary here to point out

[021-170]how completely the anticipations of Lord Sheffield have been fulfilled,

[021-171]sanguine though they might appear to be when uttered some seventy-six

[021-172]years ago.[3]

[021-173]We will endeavour as briefly as possible to point out the important

[021-174]character of Mr. Cort's improvements, as embodied in his two patents of

[021-175]1783 and 1784.  In the first he states that, after "great study,

[021-176]labour, and expense, in trying a variety of experiments, and making

[021-177]many discoveries, he had invented and brought to perfection a peculiar

[021-178]method and process of preparing, welding, and working various sorts of

[021-179]iron, and of reducing the same into uses by machinery:  a furnace, and

[021-180]other apparatus, adapted and applied to the said process."  He first

[021-181]describes his method of making iron for "large uses," such as shanks,

[021-182]arms, rings, and palms of anchors, by the method of piling and

[021-183]faggoting, since become generally practised, by laying bars of iron of

[021-184]suitable lengths, forged on purpose, and tapering so as to be thinner

[021-185]at one end than the other, laid over one another in the manner of

[021-186]bricks in buildings, so that the ends should everywhere overlay each

[021-187]other.  The faggots so prepared, to the amount of half a ton more or

[021-188]less, were then to be put into a common air or balling furnace, and

[021-189]brought to a welding heat, which was accomplished by his method in a

[021-190]much shorter time than in any hollow fire; and when the heat was

[021-191]perfect, the faggots were then brought under a forge-hammer of great

[021-192]size and weight, and welded into a solid mass.  Mr. Cort alleges in the

[021-193]specification that iron for "larger uses" thus finished, is in all

[021-194]respect's possessed of the highest degree of perfection; and that the

[021-195]fire in the balling furnace is better suited, from its regularity and

[021-196]penetrating quality, to give the iron a perfect welding heat throughout

[021-197]its whole mass, without fusing in any part, than any fire blown by a

[021-198]blast.  Another process employed by Mr. Cort for the purpose of

[021-199]cleansing the iron and producing a metal of purer grain, was that of

[021-200]working the faggots by passing them through rollers.  "By this simple

[021-201]process," said he, "all the earthy particles are pressed out and the

[021-202]iron becomes at once free from dross, and what is usually called

[021-203]cinder, and is compressed into a fibrous and tough state."  The

[021-204]objection has indeed been taken to the process of passing the iron

[021-205]through rollers, that the cinder is not so effectually got rid of as by

[021-206]passing it under a tilt hammer, and that much of it is squeezed into

[021-207]the bar and remains there, interrupting its fibre and impairing its

[021-208]strength.
