The Navy Board (also known as the Navy Office) was the British government body responsible for the civil administration of the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]], including the procurement of ships, stores, and armaments. Through its oversight of iron quality and its testing of new manufacturing processes, the Board played a significant part in the development of the [[iron-industry|British iron industry]] during the 18th century.

## [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] and the Navy

The most significant connection between the Navy Board and industrial history was the career of [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]. Before becoming an ironmaster, Cort established himself in [[surrey|Surrey]] Street, Strand, [[london|London]], carrying on the business of a navy agent, in which he was said to have realized considerable profits.

> "In 1765 we find him established in Surrey Street, Strand, carrying on the business of a navy agent, in which he is said to have realized considerable profits. It was while conducting this business that he became aware of the inferiority of British iron." (021-005)

It was through his naval agency work that Cort recognized the poor quality of British [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] compared to imported Swedish and Russian iron. This insight directly led him to abandon his naval agency around 1775 and take a lease at Fontley, near Fareham, where he began the experiments that culminated in the [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] process]] and the [[grooved-rollers|grooved rolling mill]]. (021-007)

Cort took a foundry at Fontley, near Fareham, at the north-western corner of [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] Harbour. (001-210) (021-112) The location was strategic — close to the naval dockyard at [[portsmouth-dockyard|Portsmouth]], where the Navy's demand for quality iron was constant and growing.

## [[admiralty|Admiralty]] Iron Testing

The Navy Board and Admiralty played a crucial role in validating new iron manufacturing techniques. In 1787, the Admiralty appointed persons to test [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]d by Cort's new puddling and rolling processes. The tests were satisfactory — the Admiralty having, by the persons appointed for that purpose, tested the iron and pronounced it "superior to the best Oregrounds iron." (022-103)

The Admiralty accordingly adopted British-manufactured iron for naval use. (021-075) Cort's iron, approved by the Admiralty, marked a turning point in the British [[iron-industry|iron trade]] — for the first time, British-made bar iron was judged equal or superior to the imported Swedish and Russian iron that had dominated the naval market. (001-216)

## Wartime Procurement and Impact

The great naval war with France drove an immense expansion in the Navy Board's procurement needs. During the Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars (1793–1815), the demand for iron — for ships' fittings, anchors, chains, and armaments — increased dramatically. The Navy Board's adoption of British iron encouraged the expansion of ironworks across [[south-wales|South Wales]], the Midlands, and [[scotland|Scotland]].

The deficiencies in the naval accounts after the war, however, led to scrutiny of procurement practices. Cort himself, despite his technical achievements, found his affairs entangled with Adam Jellicoe, a deputy-paymaster of the Navy, whose financial irregularities led to the seizure of Cort's properties at Fontley and Gosport. (023-007) (023-015)

## Later Developments

In the early 19th century, [[samuel-bentham|Samuel Bentham]] and the [[admiralty|Lords of the Admiralty]] collaborated on dockyard mechanization in 1801. They introduced steam-powered pumps, sawmills, and block-making machinery to Portsmouth and other naval yards. (038-163)

The Navy Board was dissolved in 1832 and its functions absorbed into the Board of Admiralty. By this time, British iron had become the standard for naval construction worldwide.
