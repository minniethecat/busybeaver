The **Self-Acting** principle — that machines should operate automatically, regulating their own motion without continuous human guidance — was a defining idea of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. Championed by [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], it transformed manufacturing from a craft dependent on individual dexterity into a system of precise, repeatable mechanical production.

## Definition

A self-acting machine is one that, once set in motion and supplied with material, executes its operation automatically without the workman needing to guide the tool. "With this object he aimed at the contrivance of improved [[machine-tool|machine-tools]], which should be as much self-acting and self-regulating as possible." (036-164) The self-acting principle was Maudslay's guiding design philosophy — to make machines "in a measure independent of the carelessness or want of dexterity of the workman." (036-162)

## History

### Before Self-Acting Tools

As late as the 1790s, manufacturing relied entirely on hand skill. [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] recalled that "about sixty years ago, there were no self-acting tools; everything was executed by hand. There were neither planing, slotting, nor [[shaping-machine|shaping machine]]s; and the whole stock of an engineering or machine establishment might be summed up in a few ill-constructed lathes, and a few drills and boring machines of rude construction." (037-093) Every turned cylinder, every planed surface, every cut thread depended on the eye and hand of an individual workman.

### Maudslay's Breakthrough

Maudslay made the self-acting principle his special pursuit while working at [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]]'s workshop. His first great embodiment of the idea was the [[slide-rest|Slide Rest]] (1794), which held and guided the cutting tool mechanically rather than by hand. From there, the self-acting [[lathe|Lathe]] evolved, where the tool carriage "advances with the revolution of the work in the lathe, and the turning done by this beautiful yet simple arrangement is as accurate as it is regular." (037-153)

### Proliferation

The self-acting principle spread across manufacturing:

| Machine | Innovation |
|---------|-----------|
| **Self-acting lathe** | Automatic tool advance, precise duplication (Maudslay, Clement) |
| **Self-acting mule** | Automatic spinning, replacing the spinner's skill (002-120) |
| **Planing machine** | Self-acting sliding bed for flat surfaces (Clement) |
| **Slide rest** | The enabling invention for all self-acting tools |

By the mid-19th century, "self-acting lathes, spinning-mules, and [[steam-engine]]s" (037-068) were the backbone of British industry. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] credited Maudslay's self-acting [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] as the foundation "to which we unquestionably owe much of the power and accuracy of our present self-acting machines." (036-091)

## Industrial Significance

The self-acting principle had profound consequences:

1. **Precision**: Machines could hold tolerances impossible by hand, producing truly interchangeable parts.
2. **Productivity**: One machine, tended by a semi-skilled operator, could outproduce dozens of skilled artisans.
3. **Democratisation of manufacture**: Complex articles no longer required master craftsmen — the machine embodied the skill.
4. **Scale**: [[steam-power|Steam Power]] driving self-acting machines enabled the [[industrial-revolution|factory system]] at industrial scale.
