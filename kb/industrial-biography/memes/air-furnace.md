The [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] (or reverberatory [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]]) was a type of refining furnace used in iron-making where the fuel and the metal charge were kept separate. Heat was transferred by flame reflection from the arched roof onto the pig iron below, avoiding the contamination that occurred when iron was in direct contact with coal or coke fuel.

## Invention Background

Before the [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]], iron was refined in hearths where fuel and metal were mixed. This introduced sulfur and other impurities from the coal, degrading the quality of the refined iron. The need for cleaner refining led to furnace designs where flame and hot gases passed over the metal without direct fuel contact. (021-131)

## Working Principle

In the reverberatory or [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]], pig iron was placed on the hearth bed. The fuel burned in a separate firebox, and the flame was drawn across the arched roof, which radiated heat downward onto the metal. The furnace operated without blast—air was drawn in naturally through the fire grate, and the draft carried the flame through the furnace chamber. (022-018)

The design was similar to that described by Onions and later improved by Cort. The hollow bridge of the reverberatory furnace allowed air circulation that sustained combustion efficiency. (022-023)

## Technical Significance

The [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] was critical to the development of [[puddling-process|puddling]]. Without the separation of fuel and metal, the [[puddling-process|puddling]] process—where a workman stirred molten pig iron in the furnace hearth to decarburize it—would not have been possible. (021-131)

The furnace was used alongside forges and mills in early ironworks, including the Cranston works which in 1637 operated "four furnaces and nine forges, besides the [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]]s and mills." (017-120) By the mid-18th century it was standard equipment: in 1742 Tilly erected a small air-furnace at the Forge for experiments, and by 1760 the design had been refined for production use. (016-145)

## Later Improvements

The [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] evolved into the [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] furnace]] after [[Henry Cort]] added a separate firebox and working door through which the puddler could stir the molten metal. This combination—[[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] separation plus mechanical stirring—enabled the mass production of wrought iron. (022-018)
