The **British Association for the Advancement of Science** (BAAS, commonly called the **British Association**) was founded in 1831 to promote scientific inquiry across Britain. Its annual meetings became important venues where engineers and ironworkers presented their research alongside academic scientists.

## Engineering Presentations

[[[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]](/william-fairbairn) delivered a notable address before the British Association at [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1861 (041-091), covering his pioneering work on iron shipbuilding and the strength of materials. [[[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]](/james-nasmyth) also presented to the British Association at the [[manchester|Manchester]] meeting of 1861, describing how, in the early days of his career, "the whole of the machinery was executed by hand" — there were neither planing, slotting, nor [[shaping-machine|shaping machine]]s (051-180). This firsthand account documented the transition from hand-crafted engineering to machine-tool production.

Fairbairn continued to present various papers before the British Association throughout his career, alongside contributions to the [[Royal Society]] and the [[Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society|Manchester Lit and Phil]] (056-028, 057-026).
