# William Strutt

William Strutt of Derby was a cotton manufacturer who, together with [[Richard Arkwright]], was one of the founders of the modern cotton manufacture in Britain. He was the father of Lord Belper.

## The Arkwright and Strutt Partnership

Strutt entered into partnership with Richard Arkwright during the early years of the cotton industry. The firm of Arkwright and Strutt became one of the great names in textile manufacturing, pioneering the [[industrial-revolution|factory system]] and the application of water-powered machinery to cotton spinning. (045-069)

## Derby Cotton Industry

Based in Derby, Strutt's operations were part of the Midlands cotton industry that, alongside [[manchester|Manchester]], drove the transformation of textile production. The firm's demand for machinery provided substantial employment to engineers and machine makers, including the celebrated lathe-maker [[James Fox]] of Derby, who supplied machinery to Arkwright and Strutt. (045-067, 045-068)

## Innovation and Engineering

Strutt was noted for his interest in technical innovation. He was described as "the father of Lord Belper," reflecting the social elevation that successful industrialists could achieve. (046-151) The Strutt family's mills at Belper and Milford became models of factory organization.

## Legacy

William Strutt exemplified the generation of cotton manufacturers who transformed a cottage industry into a mechanized factory system. His partnership with Arkwright and the demand his firm generated for precision machinery helped spur the development of the engineering industry in the Midlands. His son, Edward Strutt, was raised to the peerage as Lord Belper, marking the Strutt family's transition from industrial pioneers to the aristocracy.
