Dr. John Roebuck (1718–1794) was a physician, chemist, and one of the most enterprising improvers in iron-making during the eighteenth century. Described as "a forgotten public benefactor," he founded the Carron Ironworks and played a crucial role in the early development of [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s steam engine. (001-212) (001-230)

## Early Life

John Roebuck's father carried on a manufacturing business at [[sheffield|Sheffield]] at the beginning of the eighteenth century. John was born there in 1718. After studying medicine at [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], where he contracted many intimate friendships among the literary and scientific circles, he proceeded to Leyden to complete his medical training. (024-127) (024-136)

## Career

### Early Chemical and Industrial Research

Returning to [[england|England]] after qualifying as a physician at Leyden, Roebuck settled in [[birmingham|Birmingham]], where his attention was early drawn to chemical and industrial pursuits. He soon became one of the most skilled practical chemists in Britain. (024-142) (024-146) His inquiries had an almost exclusively practical character, directed toward improvements in manufacturing processes. (024-158)

### Carron Ironworks

Roebuck proceeded to strike out new branches of industry, and his most notable venture was the establishment of the Carron Ironworks in [[scotland|Scotland]] — the first large-scale ironworks in the country. Dr. Roebuck was indefatigable in his efforts to expand iron production, and the works at Carron grew steadily under his direction. (024-175) (025-013)

### Partnership with James Watt

Roebuck's most consequential partnership came when he became involved with [[james-watt|James Watt]]. Roebuck became a partner to Watt, providing financial support and encouragement during the critical early development of the steam engine. It was hoped that with the aid of Dr. Roebuck, Watt might yet succeed — and though Roebuck's own fortunes waned, his patronage proved essential to the engine's eventual success. (024-186) (025-092) (001-243)

## Influence and Legacy

> Had Dr. Roebuck wholly succeeded in his projects, he would have deserved the title of a great public benefactor. His hopeful nature was his strength and his weakness — he was accustomed to great enterprise, and his failures were those of ambition, not of idleness. (024-122) (025-084) (025-109)

Roebuck called to his aid the celebrated engineer [[john-smeaton|John Smeaton]], and his home became a meeting-place for the leading scientific and industrial figures of the age. Professor Black of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] was among his intimate friends, and Dugald Stewart later resided in the house that had been Roebuck's. (025-029) (025-051) (025-057)
