# Forest of Dean

The Forest of Dean in [[gloucester|Gloucester]]shire was one of the most ancient iron-producing regions of Britain. Iron was made there in Anglo-Saxon times, and the forest supplied smiths and engineers for centuries. The great cinder heaps found throughout the forest bear witness to its long industrial history. (001-111) (004-126)

## Industrial History

### Early Iron Production

The iron industry in the Forest of Dean dates back to before the Roman occupation. The Romans worked iron in the district using foot-blast furnaces, and the cinders from their operations remained visible for centuries afterward. The forest's combination of iron ore, timber for charcoal, and streams for water power made it a natural centre for iron smelting. (006-051) (006-056)

> "In the Forest of Dean and thereabouts the iron is made at this day of cinders, being the rough and offal thrown by in the Romans' time." (006-051)

### Medieval and Early Modern Period

By the time of Edward the Confessor, the Forest of Dean had become a significant source of iron for the kingdom. The forest supplied smiths to act as engineers for the royal armies, and its forges were worked continuously through the medieval period. (005-030) (006-179) (007-002)

### Later Development

The Forest of Dean continued as an iron-producing district alongside the rise of the great ironworks of [[south-wales|South Wales]] and the West of [[england|England]]. The forest's iron-makers adapted to changing technology, and the region maintained its reputation as a centre of skilled ironworking into the modern era. (004-131)
