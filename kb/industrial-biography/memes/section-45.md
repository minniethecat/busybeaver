[045-001][2] See more particularly The Transactions of the Society for the

[045-002]Encouragement of Arts, vol. xxxiii. (1817), at pp. 74, 157, 160, 175,

[045-003]208 (an admirable drawing; of Mr. James Allen's Theodolite); vol.

[045-004]xxxvi. (1818), pp. 28, 176 (a series of remarkable illustrations of Mr.

[045-005]Clement's own invention of an Instrument for Drawing Ellipses); vol.

[045-006]xliii. (1825), containing an illustration of the Drawing Table invented

[045-007]by him for large drawings; vol. xlvi. (1828), containing a series of

[045-008]elaborate illustrations of his Prize Turning [[lathe|Lathe]]; and xlviii. 1829,

[045-009]containing illustrations of his Self-adjusting Double Driver Centre

[045-010]Chuck.

[045-011][3] Transactions of the Society for the Encouragement of Arts, vol.

[045-012]xlix.  p.157.

[045-013][4] A complete account of the calculating machine, as well as of an

[045-014]analytical engine afterwards contrived by Mr. Babbage, of still greater

[045-015]power than the other, will be found in the Bibliotheque Universelle de

[045-016]Geneve, of which a translation into English, with copious original

[045-017]notes, by the late Lady Lovelace, daughter of Lord Byron, was published

[045-018]in the 3rd vol. of Taylor's Scientific Memoirs ([[london|London]], 1843).  A

[045-019]history of the machine, and of the circumstances connected with its

[045-020]construction, will also be found in Weld's History of the Royal

[045-021]Society, vol. ii. 369-391.  It remains to be added, that the perusal by

[045-022]Messrs. Scheutz of Stockholm of Dr. Lardner's account of Mr. Babbage's

[045-023]engine in the [[edinburgh-review|[[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] Review]], led those clever mechanics to enter

[045-024]upon the scheme of constructing and completing it, and the result is,

[045-025]that their machine not only calculates the tables, but prints the

[045-026]results.  It took them nearly twenty years to perfect it, but when

[045-027]completed the machine seemed to be almost capable of thinking.  The

[045-028]original was exhibited at the Paris Exhibition of 1855.  A copy of it

[045-029]has since been secured by the English government at a cost of 1200L.,

[045-030]and it is now busily employed at Somerset House in working out annuity

[045-031]and other tables for the Registrar-General.  The copy was constructed,

[045-032]with several admirable improvements, by the Messrs. Donkin, the

[045-033]well-known mechanical engineers, after the working drawings of the

[045-034]Messrs. Scheutz.

[045-036]CHAPTER XIV.

[045-037]FOX OF DERBY--MURRAY OF LEEDS--ROBERTS AND WHITWORTH OF MANCHESTER.

[045-038]"Founders and senators of states and cities, lawgivers, extirpers of

[045-039]tyrants, fathers of the people, and other eminent persons in civil

[045-040]government, were honoured but with titles of Worthies or demi-gods;

[045-041]whereas, such as were inventors and authors of new arts, endowments,

[045-042]and commodities towards man's life, were ever consecrated amongst the

[045-043]gods themselves."--BACON, Advancement of Learning.

[045-044]While such were the advances made in the arts of tool-making and

[045-045]engine-construction through the labours of Bramah, Maudslay, and

[045-046]Clement, there were other mechanics of almost equal eminence who

[045-047]flourished about the same time and subsequently in several of the

[045-048]northern manufacturing towns.  Among these may be mentioned [[james-fox|James Fox]]

[045-049]of [[derby|Derby]]; [[matthew-murray|Matthew Murray]] and [[peter-fairbairn|Peter Fairbairn]] of [[leeds|Leeds]]; [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]],

[045-050][[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]], [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], and [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] of [[manchester|Manchester]];

[045-051]to all of whom the manufacturing industry of Great Britain stands in

[045-052]the highest degree indebted.

[045-053][[james-fox|James Fox]], the founder of the [[derby|Derby]] firm of mechanical engineers, was

[045-054]originally a butler in the service of the Rev. Thomas Gisborne, of

[045-055]Foxhall Lodge, [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]].  Though a situation of this kind might

[045-056]not seem by any means favourable for the display of mechanical ability,

[045-057]yet the butler's instinct for handicraft was so strong that it could

[045-058]not be repressed; and his master not only encouraged him in the

[045-059]handling of tools in his leisure hours, but had so genuine an

[045-060]admiration of his skill as well as his excellent qualities of

[045-061]character, that he eventually furnished him with the means of beginning

[045-062]business on his own account.

[045-063]The growth and extension of the cotton, silk, and lace trades, in the

[045-064]neighbourhood of Derby, furnished Fox with sufficient opportunities for

[045-065]the exercise of his mechanical skill; and he soon found ample scope for

[045-066]its employment.  His lace machinery became celebrated, and he supplied

[045-067]it largely to the neighbouring town of [[nottingham|Nottingham]]; he also obtained

[045-068]considerable employment from the great firms of Arkwright and

[045-069][[william-strutt|Strutt]]--the founders of the modern cotton manufacture.  Mr. Fox also

[045-070]became celebrated for his lathes, which were of excellent quality,

[045-071]still maintaining their high reputation; and besides making largely for

[045-072]the supply of the home demand, he exported much machinery abroad, to

[045-073]France, Russia, and the Mauritius.

[045-074]The present Messrs. Fox of Derby, who continue to carry on the business

[045-075]of the firm, claim for their grandfather, its founder, that he made the

[045-076]first planing machine in 1814,[1] and they add that the original

[045-077]article continued in use until quite recently.  We have been furnished

[045-078]by Samuel Hall, formerly a workman at the Messrs. Fox's, with the

[045-079]following description of the machine:--"It was essentially the same in

[045-080]principle as the planing machine now in general use, although differing

[045-081]in detail.  It had a self-acting ratchet motion for moving the slides

[045-082]of a compound slide rest, and a self-acting reversing tackle,

[045-083]consisting of three bevel wheels, one a stud, one loose on the driving

[045-084]shaft, and another on a socket, with a pinion on the opposite end of

[045-085]the driving shaft running on the socket.  The other end was the place

[045-086]for the driving pulley.  A clutch box was placed between the two

[045-087]opposite wheels, which was made to slide on a feather, so that by means

[045-088]of another shaft containing levers and a tumbling ball, the box on

[045-089]reversing was carried from one bevel wheel to the opposite one."  The

[045-090]same James Fox is also said at a very early period to have invented a

[045-091]screw-cutting machine, an engine for accurately dividing and cutting

[045-092]the teeth of wheels, and a self-acting lathe.  But the evidence as to

[045-093]the dates at which these several inventions are said to have been made

[045-094]is so conflicting that it is impossible to decide with whom the merit

[045-095]of making them really rests.  The same idea is found floating at the

[045-096]same time in many minds, the like necessity pressing upon all, and the

[045-097]process of invention takes place in like manner:  hence the

[045-098]contemporaneousness of so many inventions, and the disputes that arise

[045-099]respecting them, as described in a previous chapter.

[045-100]There are still other claimants for the merit of having invented the

[045-101]planing machine; among whom may be mentioned more particularly Matthew

[045-102]Murray of [[leeds|Leeds]], and [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]] of [[manchester|Manchester]].  We are informed by

[045-103]Mr. March, the present mayor of Leeds, head of the celebrated

[045-104]tool-manufacturing firm of that town, that when he first went to work

[045-105]at Matthew Murray's, in 1814, a planing machine of his invention was

[045-106]used to plane the circular part or back of the D valve, which he had by

[045-107]that time introduced in the [[steam-engine]].  Mr. March says, "I recollect

[045-108]it very distinctly, and even the sort of framing on which it stood.

[045-109]The machine was not patented, and like many inventions in those days,

[045-110]it was kept as much a secret as possible, being locked up in a small

[045-111]room by itself, to which the ordinary workmen could not obtain access.

[045-112]The year in which I remember it being in use was, so far as I am aware,

[045-113]long before any planing-machine of a similar kind had been invented."

[045-114]Matthew Murray was born at [[stockton-on-tees|[[stockton-on-tees|Stockton]]-on-Tees]] in the year 1763.  His

[045-115]parents were of the working class, and Matthew, like the other members

[045-116]of the family, was brought up with the ordinary career of labour before

[045-117]him.  When of due age his father apprenticed him to the trade of a

[045-118]blacksmith, in which he very soon acquired considerable expertness.  He

[045-119]married before his term had expired; after which, trade being slack at

[045-120]Stockton, he found it necessary to look for work elsewhere.  Leaving

[045-121]his wife behind him, he set out for Leeds with his bundle on his back,

[045-122]and after a long journey on foot, he reached that town with not enough

[045-123]money left in his pocket to pay for a bed at the Bay Horse inn, where

[045-124]he put up.  But telling the landlord that he expected work at

[045-125]Marshall's, and seeming to be a respectable young man, the landlord

[045-126]trusted him; and he was so fortunate as to obtain the job which he

[045-127]sought at Mr. Marshall's, who was then beginning the manufacture of

[045-128]flax, for which the firm has since become so famous.

[045-129]Mr. Marshall was at that time engaged in improving the method of

[045-130]manufacture,[2] and the young blacksmith was so fortunate or rather so

[045-131]dexterous as to be able to suggest several improvements in the

[045-132]machinery which secured the approval of his employer, who made him a

[045-133]present of 20L., and very shortly promoted him to be the first mechanic

[045-134]in the workshop.  On this stroke of good fortune Murray took a house at

[045-135]the neighbouring village of Beeston, sent to Stockton for his wife, who

[045-136]speedily joined him, and he now felt himself fairly started in the

[045-137]world.  He remained with Mr. Marshall for about twelve years, during

[045-138]which he introduced numerous improvements in the machinery for spinning

[045-139]flax, and obtained the reputation of being a first-rate mechanic.  This

[045-140]induced Mr. James Fenton and Mr. David Wood to offer to join him in the

[045-141]establishment of an engineering and machine-making factory at Leeds;

[045-142]which he agreed to, and operations were commenced at Holbeck in the

[045-143]year 1795.

[045-144]As Mr. Murray had obtained considerable practical knowledge of the

[045-145]steam-engine while working at Mr. Marshall's, he took principal charge

[045-146]of the engine-building department, while his partner Wood directed the

[045-147]machine-making.  In the branch of engine-building Mr. Murray very

[045-148]shortly established a high reputation, treading close upon the heels of

[045-149][[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]--so close, indeed, that that firm became very jealous

[045-150]of him, and purchased a large piece of ground close to his works with

[045-151]the object of preventing their extension.[3]  His additions to the

[045-152]steam-engine were of great practical value, one of which, the

[045-153]self-acting apparatus attached to the boiler for the purpose of

[045-154]regulating the intensity of fire under it, and consequently the

[045-155]production of steam, is still in general use.  This was invented by him

[045-156]as early as 1799.  He also subsequently invented the D slide valve, or

[045-157]at least greatly improved it, while he added to the power of the

[045-158]air-pump, and gave a new arrangement to the other parts, with a view to

[045-159]the simplification of the powers of the engine.  To make the D valve

[045-160]work efficiently, it was found necessary to form two perfectly plane

[045-161]surfaces, to produce which he invented his planing machine.  He was

[045-162]also the first to adopt the practice of placing the piston in a

[045-163]horizontal position in the common condensing engine.  Among his other

[045-164]modifications in the steam-engine, was his improvement of the

[045-165]locomotive as invented by Trevithick; and it ought to be remembered to

[045-166]his honour that he made the first locomotive that regularly worked upon

[045-167]any railway.

[045-168]This was the engine erected by him for Blenkinsop, to work the

[045-169]Middleton [[coal-mining|colliery]] railway near Leeds, on which it began to run in

[045-170]1812, and continued in regular use for many years.  In this engine he

[045-171]introduced the double cylinder--Trevithick's engine being provided with

[045-172]only one cylinder, the defects of which were supplemented by the

[045-173]addition of a fly-wheel to carry the crank over the dead points.

[045-174]But Matthew Murray's most important inventions, considered in their

[045-175]effects on manufacturing industry, were those connected with the

[045-176]machinery for heckling and spinning flax, which he very greatly

[045-177]improved.  His heckling machine obtained for him the prize of the gold

[045-178]medal of the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]]; and this as well as his machine for wet

[045-179]flax-spinning by means of sponge weights proved of the greatest

[045-180]practical value.  At the time when these inventions were made the flax

[045-181]trade was on the point of expiring, the spinners being unable to

[045-182]produce yarn to a profit; and their almost immediate effect was to

[045-183]reduce the cost of production, to improve immensely the quality of the

[045-184]manufacture, and to establish the British linen trade on a solid

[045-185]foundation.  The production of flax-machinery became an important

[045-186]branch of manufacture at Leeds, large quantities being made for use at

[045-187]home as well as for exportation, giving employment to an increasing

[045-188]number of highly skilled mechanics.[4]  Mr. Murray's faculty for

[045-189]organising work, perfected by experience, enabled him also to introduce

[045-190]many valuable improvements in the mechanics of manufacturing.  His

[045-191]pre-eminent skill in mill-gearing became generally acknowledged, and

[045-192]the effects of his labours are felt to this day in the extensive and

[045-193]still thriving branches of industry which his ingenuity and ability

[045-194]mainly contributed to establish.  All the machine tools used in his

[045-195]establishment were designed by himself, and he was most careful in the

[045-196]personal superintendence of all the details of their construction.  Mr.

[045-197]Murray died at Leeds in 1826, in his sixty-third year.
