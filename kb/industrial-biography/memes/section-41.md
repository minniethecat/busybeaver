[041-001]But while he had much toleration for modest and meritorious inventors,

[041-002]he had a great dislike for secret-mongers,--schemers of the close,

[041-003]cunning sort,--and usually made short work of them.  He had an almost

[041-004]equal aversion for what he called the "fiddle-faddle inventors," with

[041-005]their omnibus patents, into which they packed every possible thing that

[041-006]their noddles could imagine.  "Only once or twice in a century," said

[041-007]he, "does a great inventor appear, and yet here we have a set of

[041-008]fellows each taking out as many patents as would fill a cart,--some of

[041-009]them embodying not a single original idea, but including in their

[041-010]specifications all manner of modifications of well-known processes, as

[041-011]well as anticipating the arrangements which may become practicable in

[041-012]the progress of mechanical improvement." Many of these "patents" he

[041-013]regarded as mere pit-falls to catch the unwary; and he spoke of such

[041-014]"inventors" as the pests of the profession.

[041-015]The personal appearance of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] was in correspondence with

[041-016]his character.  He was of a commanding presence, for he stood full six

[041-017]feet two inches in height, a massive and portly man.  His face was

[041-018]round, full, and lit up with good humour.  A fine, large, and square

[041-019]forehead, of the grand constructive order, dominated over all, and his

[041-020]bright keen eye gave energy and life to his countenance.  He was

[041-021]thoroughly "jolly" and good-natured, yet full of force and character.

[041-022]It was a positive delight to hear his cheerful, ringing laugh.  He was

[041-023]cordial in manner, and his frankness set everybody at their ease who

[041-024]had occasion to meet him, even for the first time.  No one could be

[041-025]more faithful and consistent in his friendships, nor more firm in the

[041-026]hour of adversity.  In fine, [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] was, as described by his

[041-027]friend Mr. Nasmyth, the very beau ideal of an honest, upright,

[041-028]straight-forward, hard-working, intelligent Englishman.

[041-029]A severe cold which he caught on his way home from one of his visits to

[041-030]France, was the cause of his death, which occurred on the 14th of

[041-031]February, 1831.  The void which his decease caused was long and deeply

[041-032]felt, not only by his family and his large circle of friends, but by

[041-033]his workmen, who admired him for his industrial skill, and loved him

[041-034]because of his invariably manly, generous, and upright conduct towards

[041-035]them.  He directed that he should be buried in [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]]

[041-036]parish-churchyard, where a cast-iron tomb, made to his own design, was

[041-037]erected over his remains.  He had ever a warm heart for [[woolwich|Woolwich]], where

[041-038]he had been born and brought up.  He often returned to it, sometimes to

[041-039]carry his mother a share of his week's wages while she lived, and

[041-040]afterwards to refresh himself with a sight of the neighbourhood with

[041-041]which he had been so familiar when a boy.  He liked its green common,

[041-042]with the soldiers about it; Shooter's Hill, with its out-look over [[kent|Kent]]

[041-043]and down the valley of the [[thames|Thames]]; the river busy with shipping, and

[041-044]the royal craft loading and unloading their armaments at the dockyard

[041-045]wharves.  He liked the clangour of the Arsenal smithy where he had

[041-046]first learned his art, and all the busy industry of the place.  It was

[041-047]natural, therefore, that, being proud of his early connection with

[041-048][[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]], he should wish to lie there; and Woolwich, on its part, let

[041-049]us add, has equal reason to be proud of Henry Maudslay.

[041-127]CHAPTER XIII.

[041-128]JOSEPH CLEMENT.

[041-129]"It is almost impossible to over-estimate the importance of these

[041-130]inventions.  The Greeks would have elevated their authors among the

[041-131]gods; nor will the enlightened judgment of modern times deny them the

[041-132]place among their fellow-men which is so undeniably their

[041-133]due."--[[edinburgh-review|[[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] Review]].

[041-134]That Skill in mechanical contrivance is a matter of education and

[041-135]training as well as of inborn faculty, is clear from the fact of so

[041-136]many of our distinguished mechanics undergoing the same kind of

[041-137]practical discipline, and perhaps still more so from the circumstance

[041-138]of so many of them passing through the same workshops.  Thus Maudslay

[041-139]and Clement were trained in the workshops of Bramah; and Roberts,

[041-140]Whitworth, Nasmyth, and others, were trained in those of Maudslay.

[041-141][[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]] was born at Great Ashby in Westmoreland, in the year

[041-142]1779.  His father was a hand-loom weaver, and a man of remarkable

[041-143]culture considering his humble station in life.  He was an ardent

[041-144]student of natural history, and possessed a much more complete

[041-145]knowledge of several sub-branches of that science than was to have been

[041-146]looked for in a common working-man.  One of the departments which he

[041-147]specially studied was Entomology.  In his leisure hours he was

[041-148]accustomed to traverse the country searching the hedge-bottoms for

[041-149]beetles and other insects, of which he formed a remarkably complete

[041-150]collection; and the capture of a rare specimen was quite an event in

[041-151]his life.  In order more deliberately to study the habits of the bee

[041-152]tribe, he had a number of hives constructed for the purpose of enabling

[041-153]him to watch their proceedings without leaving his work; and the

[041-154]pursuit was a source of the greatest pleasure to him.  He was a lover

[041-155]of all dumb creatures; his cottage was haunted by birds which flew in

[041-156]and out at his door, and some of them became so tame as to hop up to

[041-157]him and feed out of his hand.  "Old Clement" was also a bit of a

[041-158]mechanic, and such of his leisure moments as he did not devote to

[041-159]insect-hunting, were employed in working a lathe of his own

[041-160]construction, which he used to turn his bobbing on, and also in various

[041-161]kinds of amateur mechanics.

[041-162]His boy Joseph, like other poor men's sons, was early set to work.  He

[041-163]received very little education, and learnt only the merest rudiments of

[041-164]reading and writing at the village school.  The rest of his education

[041-165]he gave to himself as he grew older.  His father needed his help at the

[041-166]loom, where he worked with him for some years; but, as handloom weaving

[041-167]was gradually being driven out by improved mechanism, the father

[041-168]prudently resolved to put his son to a better trade.  They have a

[041-169]saying in [[cumberland|Cumberland]] that when the bairns reach a certain age, they are

[041-170]thrown on to the house-rigg, and that those who stick on are made

[041-171]thatchers of, while those who fall off are sent to St.  Bees to be made

[041-172]parsons of.  Joseph must have been one of those that stuck on--at all

[041-173]events his father decided to make him a thatcher, afterwards a slater,

[041-174]and he worked at that trade for five years, between eighteen and

[041-175]twenty-three.

[041-176]The son, like the father, had a strong liking for mechanics, and as the

[041-177]slating trade did not keep him in regular employment, especially in

[041-178]winter time, he had plenty of opportunity for following the bent of his

[041-179]inclinations.  He made a friend of the village blacksmith, whose smithy

[041-180]he was accustomed to frequent, and there he learned to work at the

[041-181]forge, to handle the hammer and file, and in a short time to shoe

[041-182]horses with considerable expertness.  A cousin of his named Farer, a

[041-183]clock and watchmaker by trade, having returned to the village from

[041-184][[london|London]], brought with him some books on mechanics, which he lent to

[041-185]Joseph to read; and they kindled in him an ardent desire to be a

[041-186]mechanic instead of a slater.  He nevertheless continued to maintain

[041-187]himself by the latter trade for some time longer, until his skill had

[041-188]grown; and, by way of cultivating it, he determined, with the aid of

[041-189]his friend the village blacksmith, to make a turning-lathe.  The two

[041-190]set to work, and the result was the production of an article in every

[041-191]way superior to that made by Clement's father, which was accordingly

[041-192]displaced to make room for the new machine.  It was found to work very

[041-193]satisfactorily, and by its means Joseph proceeded to turn fifes,

[041-194]flutes, clarinets, and hautboys; for to his other accomplishments he

[041-195]joined that of music, and could play upon the instruments that he made.

[041-196]One of his most ambitious efforts was the making of a pair of

[041-197][[northumberland|Northumberland]] bagpipes, which he finished to his satisfaction, and

[041-198]performed upon to the great delight of the villagers.  To assist his

[041-199]father in his entomological studies, he even contrived, with the aid of

[041-200]the descriptions given in the books borrowed from his cousin the

[041-201]watchmaker, to make for him a microscope, from which he proceeded to

[041-202]make a reflecting telescope, which proved a very good instrument.  At

[041-203]this early period (1804) he also seems to have directed his attention

[041-204]to screw-making--a branch of mechanics in which he afterwards became

[041-205]famous; and he proceeded to make a pair of very satisfactory

[041-206]die-stocks, though it is said that he had not before seen or even heard

[041-207]of such a contrivance for making screws.
