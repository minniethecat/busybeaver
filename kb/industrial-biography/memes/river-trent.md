# River Trent

The River Trent is one of the principal rivers of the English Midlands and was a vital artery for the transport of coal, iron, and manufactured goods during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. Its navigation and canal connections were the subject of pioneering civil engineering schemes in the 17th and 18th centuries. (012-158) (012-170)

## Industrial History

### Early Navigation Schemes

In the 1660s, [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]] proposed making the River Stour navigable and uniting it with other rivers to open communication with the Trent. His plan, described in '[[england|England]]'s Improvement by Sea and Land', was a bold vision for an integrated inland waterway network. (012-158) (012-170)

### Brindley's Canal

More than a century after Yarranton's proposal, [[james-brindley|James Brindley]] the great canal maker took up the challenge of connecting the Trent with other river systems. Brindley's approach differed from Yarranton's — rather than making the river itself navigable, he planned to cut a canal parallel with the river and supply it with water from it. (012-170)

### The Trent and Mersey Canal

The navigable communication that now connects Trent and [[severn|Severn]], running in the course of Yarranton's original project, became one of the key transport corridors of the Industrial Revolution. The [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]] Canal]], the [[bridgewater-canal|Bridgewater Canal]], and the Trent-[[severn|Severn]] link together formed a waterway network that enabled the cheap movement of raw materials and finished goods. (014-163)
