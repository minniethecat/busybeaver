A machine that converts the expansive force of steam into rotary mechanical power — the invention that freed industry from dependence on water wheels and wind, and powered the factories, mines, railways, and ships of the [[Industrial Revolution]].

## Definition

A steam engine is a heat engine that uses steam as its working fluid. Water is heated in a boiler to produce high-pressure steam, which is admitted into a cylinder where it pushes a piston. The reciprocating motion of the piston is converted to rotary motion by a crank and flywheel. (001-241) The steam engine is distinct from the earlier atmospheric engine (which used steam only to create a vacuum) and from the [[Steam Hammer]] (which is a specific application, not a prime mover).

## Historical Development

### The [[atmospheric-engine|Newcomen Engine]]

The first practical steam engine was the atmospheric engine invented by [[Thomas Newcomen]] in 1712. It used steam at atmospheric pressure to drive a piston, then condensed the steam with a jet of cold water to create a vacuum, allowing atmospheric pressure to push the piston back. Newcomen engines were used almost exclusively for pumping water from mines:

> The coal could not be worked because of the water flowing into the mines; necessitating the employment of the atmospheric pumping-engine invented by Newcomen. (016-101)

### James Watt's Improvements

[[James Watt]] transformed the steam engine from a crude pump into a universal prime mover. His key innovations were:

1. **Separate condenser** (1765): By condensing steam in a separate vessel instead of the cylinder itself, Watt eliminated the wasteful cooling and reheating of the cylinder on every stroke.

2. **Double-acting engine** (1782): Steam was admitted alternately to both sides of the piston, producing power on both the up and down strokes.

3. **Sun-and-planet gear** (1781): Converted reciprocating motion to rotary motion, enabling the engine to drive machinery directly.

> The progress of Watt in inventing the steam-engine marks the greatest single advance in mechanical power since the water-wheel. (001-241)

> Roebuck becomes a partner in the steam-engine patent, providing the capital that allowed Watt to continue his experiments at Kinneil. (001-243)

[[Matthew Boulton]]'s partnership with Watt from 1775 provided the manufacturing capability and business acumen to bring the engine to market:

> Boulton disposed of his entire interest in Watt's steam-engine, the value of which, by the extension of the patent, had become enormous. (025-126)

### Subsequent Improvements

After Watt's patent expired in 1800, a wave of improvements followed:

> Improvements in the steam-engine by Trevithick, Woolf, and others introduced high-pressure steam and compound expansion, greatly increasing efficiency and reducing the size and cost of engines. (002-017, 002-053)

[[Richard Trevithick]] pioneered the high-pressure engine (c. 1800), which eliminated the condenser and used steam at 30–50 psi to produce more power from a smaller, lighter engine — making steam locomotion possible. Woolf's compound engine used steam twice, first in a high-pressure cylinder and then in a low-pressure one, improving fuel economy.

> Improvements in steam-engines continued through the work of numerous engineers. The Cornish pumping engine, developed by Trevithick and Arthur Woolf, achieved thermal efficiencies that would not be surpassed until the late 19th century. (002-108)

## Industrial Impact

### Mining

The steam engine's first and most urgent application was mine drainage. Deep mines in [[cornwall|Cornwall]] and the North of [[england|England]] were limited by the ability to pump out groundwater. The Newcomen engine, and later Watt's and the Cornish engines, allowed mines to go far deeper:

> The steam-engine of Watt had an extraordinary influence on the [[iron-industry|iron trade]], by enabling the blast-furnace proprietors to command more powerful and regular supplies of blast. (022-046)

### Iron Production

The steam engine revolutionized iron-making by:
- Powering blast furnace blowing engines (replacing water wheels)
- Driving rolling mills and [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]]s
- Pumping water for blast furnace cooling
- Operating cranes and charging machinery

> The making and working of iron advanced in parallel with the steam-engine of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]. The demand for iron to build engines, boilers, and machinery created a self-reinforcing cycle of improvement. (021-163)

### Factories and Transport

By the 1830s, steam engines powered textile mills, engineering works, ironworks, breweries, and every other kind of factory:

> Machinery, for steam-engines, and for railways, at one-third the former cost. The steam engine had become the universal prime mover of British industry. (024-007)

The application of the steam engine to locomotion produced the railway age:

> The steam-engine, in the form of the [[locomotive|Locomotive]], opened up districts hitherto inaccessible, and gave a new stimulus to industry in every direction. (030-090)

## Economic Significance

The steam engine created the modern [[industrial-revolution|factory system]] by liberating manufacturing from riverside locations. Factories could now be built wherever coal was cheap, which meant wherever there was access to canal or rail transport. The geography of British industry — the concentration of cotton spinning in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], iron-making in [[south-wales|South Wales]] and the [[clyde|Clyde]], engineering in [[manchester|Manchester]] and [[leeds|Leeds]] — was shaped fundamentally by the steam engine's fuel requirements and power capabilities.

> To witness the success of the steam-engine, the opening up of the coal-fields, the extension of the iron trade, and the general advance of manufacturing industry was to live through the greatest transformation in human history. (025-136)
