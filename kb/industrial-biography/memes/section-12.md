[012-004]attending on the House, for giving interruption to the execution of the

[012-005]ordinance of the militia in the county of [[leicester|Leicester]]."

[012-014][12] [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]], in his 'Papers on Iron,' says, that "although he had

[012-015]carefully examined every spot and relic in Dean Forest likely to denote

[012-016]the site of [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s enterprising but unfortunate experiment of

[012-017]making pig-iron with pit coal," it had been without success; neither

[012-018]could he find any traces of the like operations of Cromwell and his

[012-019]partners.

[012-020][13] Dudley says, "Major Wildman, more barbarous to me than a wild man,

[012-021]although a minister, bought the author's estate, near 200L. per annum,

[012-022]intending to compell from the author his inventions of making iron with

[012-023]pitcole, but afterwards passed my estate unto two barbarous brokers of

[012-024][[london|London]], that pulled down the author's two mantion houses, sold 500

[012-025]timber trees off his land, and to this day are his houses unrepaired."

[012-026]Wildman himself fell under the grip of Cromwell.  Being one of the

[012-027]chiefs of the Republican party, he was seized at Exton, near

[012-028]Marlborough, in 1654, and imprisoned in [[chepstow|[[chepstow|Chepstow]] Castle]].

[012-061]CHAPTER IV.

[012-062]ANDREW YARRANTON.

[012-063]"There never have been wanting men to whom [[england|England]]'s improvement by sea

[012-064]and land was one of the dearest thoughts of their lives, and to whom

[012-065][[england|England]]'s good was the foremost of their worldly considerations.  And

[012-066]such, emphatically, was [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]], a true patriot in the best

[012-067]sense of the word."--DOVE, Elements of Political Science.

[012-068]That industry had a sore time of it during the civil wars will further

[012-069]appear from the following brief account of [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]], which may

[012-070]be taken as a companion memoir to that of Dud Dudley.  For Yarranton

[012-071]also was a [[worcestershire|Worcester]] ironmaster and a soldier--though on the opposite

[012-072]side,--but more even than Dudley was he a man of public spirit and

[012-073]enterprise, an enlightened political economist (long before political

[012-074]economy had been recognised as a science), and in many respects a true

[012-075]national benefactor.  Bishop Watson said that he ought to have had a

[012-076]statue erected to his memory because of his eminent public services;

[012-077]and an able modern writer has gone so far as to say of him that he was

[012-078]"the founder of English political economy, the first man in England who

[012-079]saw and said that peace was better than war, that trade was better than

[012-080]plunder, that honest industry was better than martial greatness, and

[012-081]that the best occupation of a government was to secure prosperity at

[012-082]home, and let other nations alone." [1]

[012-083]Yet the name of Andrew Yarranton is scarcely remembered, or is at most

[012-084]known to only a few readers of half-forgotten books.  The following

[012-085]brief outline of his history is gathered from his own narrative and

[012-086]from documents in the State Paper Office.

[012-087]Andrew Yarranton was born at the farmstead of Larford, in the parish of

[012-088]Astley, in [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]], in the year 1616.[2]  In his sixteenth year

[012-089]he was put apprentice to a Worcester linendraper, and remained at that

[012-090]trade for some years; but not liking it, he left it, and was leading a

[012-091]country life when the civil wars broke out.  Unlike Dudley, he took the

[012-092]side of the [[house-of-commons|Parliament]], and joined their army, in which he served for

[012-093]some time as a soldier.  His zeal and abilities commended him to his

[012-094]officers, and he was raised from one position to another, until in the

[012-095]course of a few years we find him holding the rank of captain.  "While

[012-096]a soldier," says he, "I had sometimes the honour and misfortune to

[012-097]lodge and dislodge an army;" but this is all the information he gives

[012-098]us of his military career.  In the year 1648 he was instrumental in

[012-099]discovering and frustrating a design on the part of the Royalists to

[012-100]seize Doyley House in the county of Hereford, and other strongholds,

[012-101]for which he received the thanks of Parliament "for his ingenuity,

[012-102]discretion, and valour," and a substantial reward of 500L.[3]  He was

[012-103]also recommended to the Committee of Worcester for further employment.

[012-104]But from that time we hear no more of him in connection with the civil

[012-105]wars.  When Cromwell assumed the supreme control of affairs, Yarranton

[012-106]retired from the army with most of the Presbyterians, and devoted

[012-107]himself to industrial pursuits.

[012-108]We then find him engaged in carrying on the manufacture of iron at

[012-109]Ashley, near Bewdley, in Worcestershire.  "In the year 1652", says he,

[012-110]"I entered upon iron-works, and plied them for several years." [4]  He

[012-111]made it a subject of his diligent study how to provide employment for

[012-112]the poor, then much distressed by the late wars.  With the help of his

[012-113]wife, he established a manufacture of linen, which was attended with

[012-114]good results.  Observing how the difficulties of communication, by

[012-115]reason of the badness of the roads, hindered the development of the

[012-116]rich natural resources of the western counties,[5] he applied himself

[012-117]to the improvement of the navigation of the larger rivers, making

[012-118]surveys of them at his own cost, and endeavouring to stimulate local

[012-119]enterprise so as to enable him to carry his plans into effect.

[012-120]While thus occupied, the restoration of Charles II. took place, and

[012-121]whether through envy or enmity Yarranton's activity excited the

[012-122]suspicion of the authorities.  His journeys from place to place seemed

[012-123]to them to point to some Presbyterian plot on foot.  On the 13th of

[012-124]November, 1660, Lord Windsor, Lord-Lieutenant of the county, wrote to

[012-125]the Secretary of State--"There is a quaker in prison for speaking

[012-126]treason against his Majesty, and a countryman also, and Captain

[012-127]Yarrington for refusing to obey my authority." [6]  It would appear

[012-128]from subsequent letters that Yarranton must have lain in prison for

[012-129]nearly two years, charged with conspiring against the king's authority,

[012-130]the only evidence against him consisting of some anonymous letter's.

[012-131]At the end of May, 1662, he succeeded in making his escape from the

[012-132]custody of the Provost Marshal.  The High Sheriff scoured the country

[012-133]after him at the head of a party of horse, and then he communicated to

[012-134]the Secretary of State, Sir Edward Nicholas, that the suspected

[012-135]conspirator could not be found, and was supposed to have made his way

[012-136]to [[london|London]].  Before the end of a month Yarranton was again in custody,

[012-137]as appears from the communication of certain justices of [[surrey|Surrey]] to Sir

[012-138]Edward Nicholas.[7]  As no further notice of Yarranton occurs in the

[012-139]State Papers, and as we shortly after find him publicly occupied in

[012-140]carrying out his plans for improving the navigation of the western

[012-141]rivers, it is probable that his innocence of any plot was established

[012-142]after a legal investigation.  A few years later he published in London

[012-143]a 4to. tract entitled 'A Full Discovery of the First Presbyterian Sham

[012-144]Plot,' which most probably contained a vindication of his conduct.[8]

[012-145]Yarranton was no sooner at liberty than we find him again occupied with

[012-146]his plans of improved inland navigation.  His first scheme was to

[012-147]deepen the small river Salwarp, so as to connect Droitwich with the

[012-148][[severn|Severn]] by a water communication, and thus facilitate the transport of

[012-149]the salt so abundantly yielded by the brine springs near that town.  In

[012-150]1665, the burgesses of Droitwich agreed to give him 750L. and eight

[012-151]salt vats in Upwich, valued at 80L. per annum, with three-quarters of a

[012-152]vat in Northwich, for twenty-one years, in payment for the work.  But

[012-153]the times were still unsettled, and Yarranton and his partner Wall not

[012-154]being rich, the scheme was not then carried into effect.[9]  In the

[012-155]following year we find him occupied with a similar scheme to open up

[012-156]the navigation of the river Stour, passing by Stourport and

[012-157][[kidderminster|Kidderminster]], and connect it by an artificial cut with the river

[012-158][[river-trent|Trent]].  Some progress was made with this undertaking, so far in advance

[012-159]of the age, but, like the other, it came to a stand still for want of

[012-160]money, and more than a hundred years passed before it was carried out

[012-161]by a kindred genius--[[james-brindley|James Brindley]], the great canal maker.  Mr.

[012-162]Chambers says that when Yarranton's scheme was first brought forward,

[012-163]it met with violent opposition and ridicule.  The undertaking was

[012-164]thought wonderfully bold, and, joined to its great extent, the sandy,

[012-165]spongy nature of the ground, the high banks necessary to prevent the

[012-166]inundation of the Stour on the canal, furnished its opponents, if not

[012-167]with sound argument, at least with very specious topics for opposition

[012-168]and laughter.[10]  Yarranton's plan was to make the river itself

[012-169]navigable, and by uniting it with other rivers, open up a communication

[012-170]with the [[river-trent|Trent]]; while Brindley's was to cut a canal parallel with the

[012-171]river, and supply it with water from thence.  Yarranton himself thus

[012-172]accounts for the failure of his scheme in 'England's Improvement by Sea

[012-173]and Land':--"It was my projection," he says, "and I will tell you the

[012-174]reason why it was not finished.  The river Stour and some other rivers

[012-175]were granted by an Act of Parliament to certain persons of honor, and

[012-176]some progress was made in the work, but within a small while after the

[012-177]Act passed[11] it was let fall again; but it being a brat of my own, I

[012-178]was not willing it should be abortive, wherefore I made offers to

[012-179]perfect it, having a third part of the inheritance to me and my heirs

[012-180]for ever, and we came to an agreement, upon which I fell on, and made

[012-181]it completely navigable from [[stourbridge|Stourbridge]] to [[kidderminster|Kidderminster]], and carried

[012-182]down many hundred tons of coal, and laid out near 1000L., and there it

[012-183]was obstructed for want of money." [12]
