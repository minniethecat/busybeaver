[022-001]It does not appear that there was any novelty in the use of rollers by

[022-002]Cort; for in his first specification he speaks of them as already well

[022-003]known.[4]  His great merit consisted in apprehending the value of

[022-004]certain processes, as tested by his own and others' experience, and

[022-005]combining and applying them in a more effective practical form than had

[022-006]ever been done before.  This power of apprehending the best methods,

[022-007]and embodying the details in one complete whole, marks the practical,

[022-008]clear-sighted man, and in certain cases amounts almost to a genius.

[022-009]The merit of combining the inventions of others in such forms as that

[022-010]they shall work to advantage, is as great in its way as that of the man

[022-011]who strikes out the inventions themselves, but who, for want of tact

[022-012]and experience, cannot carry them into practical effect.

[022-013]It was the same with Cort's second patent, in which he described his

[022-014]method of manufacturing bar-iron from the ore or from cast-iron.  All

[022-015]the several processes therein described had been practised before his

[022-016]time; his merit chiefly consisting in the skilful manner in which he

[022-017]combined and applied them.  Thus, like [[cranege-brothers|the Craneges]], he employed the

[022-018]reverberatory or [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]], without blast, and, like Onions, he

[022-019]worked the fused metal with iron bars until it was brought into lumps,

[022-020]when it was removed and forged into [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]].  Cort, however,

[022-021]carried the process further, and made it more effectual in all

[022-022]respects.  His method may be thus briefly described:  the bottom of the

[022-023]reverberatory furnace was hollow, so as to contain the fluid metal,

[022-024]introduced into it by ladles; the heat being kept up by pit-coal or

[022-025]other fuel.  When the furnace was charged, the doors were closed until

[022-026]the metal was sufficiently fused, when the workman opened an aperture

[022-027]and worked or stirred about the metal with iron bars, when an

[022-028]ebullition took place, during the continuance of which a bluish flame

[022-029]was emitted, the carbon of the cast-iron was burned off, the metal

[022-030]separated from the slag, and the iron, becoming reduced to nature, was

[022-031]then collected into lumps or loops of sizes suited to their intended

[022-032]uses, when they were drawn out of the doors of the furnace.  They were

[022-033]then stamped into plates, and piled or worked in an [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]], heated

[022-034]to a white or welding heat, shingled under a [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]], and passed

[022-035]through the grooved rollers after the method described in the first

[022-036]patent.

[022-037]The processes described by Cort in his two patents have been followed

[022-038]by [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]rs, with various modifications, the results of

[022-039]enlarged experience, down to the present time.  After the lapse of

[022-040]seventy-eight years, the language employed by Cort continues on the

[022-041]whole a faithful description of the processes still practised:  the

[022-042]same methods of manufacturing bar from cast-iron, and of [[puddling-process|puddling]],

[022-043]piling, welding, and working the bar-iron through grooved rollers--all

[022-044]are nearly identical with the methods of manufacture perfected by Henry

[022-045]Cort in 1784.  It may be mentioned that the development of the powers

[022-046]of the [[steam-engine]] by Watt had an extraordinary effect upon the

[022-047]production of iron.  It created a largely increased demand for the

[022-048]article for the purposes of the shafting and machinery which it was

[022-049]employed to drive; while at the same time it cleared pits of water

[022-050]which before were unworkable, and by being extensively applied to the

[022-051]blowing of iron-furnaces and the working of the rolling-mills, it thus

[022-052]gave a still further impetus to the manufacture of the metal.  It would

[022-053]be beside our purpose to enter into any statistical detail on the

[022-054]subject; but it will be sufficient to state that the production of

[022-055]iron, which in the early part of last century amounted to little more

[022-056]than 12,000 tons, about the middle of the century to about 18,000 tons,

[022-057]and at the time of Cort's inventions to about 90,000 tons, was found,

[022-058]in 1820, to have increased to 400,000 tons; and now the total quantity

[022-059]produced is upwards of four millions of tons of pig-iron every year, or

[022-060]more than the entire production of all other European countries.  There

[022-061]is little reason to doubt that this extraordinary development of the

[022-062][[iron-industry|iron manufacture]] has been in a great measure due to the inventions of

[022-063][[henry-cort|Henry Cort]].  It is said that at the present time there are not fewer

[022-064]than 8200 of Cort's furnaces in operation in Great Britain alone.[5]

[022-065]Practical men have regarded Cort's improvement of the process of

[022-066]rolling the iron as the most valuable of his inventions.  A competent

[022-067]authority has spoken of Cort's grooved rollers as of "high

[022-068]philosophical interest, being scarcely less than the discovery of a new

[022-069]mechanical Power, in reversing the action of the wedge, by the

[022-070]application of force to four surfaces, so as to elongate a mass,

[022-071]instead of applying force to a mass to divide the four surfaces."  One

[022-072]of the best authorities in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of last century, Mr.

[022-073]Alexander Raby of Llanelly, like many others, was at first entirely

[022-074]sceptical as to the value of Cort's invention; but he had no sooner

[022-075]witnessed the process than with manly candour he avowed his entire

[022-076]conversion to his views.

[022-077]We now return to the history of the chief author of this great branch

[022-078]of national industry.  As might naturally be expected, the principal

[022-079]ironmasters, when they heard of Cort's success, and the rapidity and

[022-080]economy with which he manufactured and forged bar-iron, visited his

[022-081]foundry for the purpose of examining his process, and, if found

[022-082]expedient, of employing it at their own works.  Among the first to try

[022-083]it were [[richard-crawshay|Richard Crawshay]] of Cyfartha, [[samuel-homfray|Samuel Homfray]] of [[penydarran|Penydarran]]

[022-084](both in [[south-wales|South Wales]]), and [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]].  Richard

[022-085]Crawshay was then (in 1787) forging only ten tons of bar-iron weekly

[022-086]under the hammer; and when he saw the superior processes invented by

[022-087]Cort he readily entered into a contract with him to work under his

[022-088]patents at ten shillings a ton royalty, In 1812 a letter from Mr.

[022-089]Crawshay to the Secretary of Lord [[sheffield|Sheffield]] was read to the House of

[022-090]Commons, descriptive of his method of working iron, in which he said,

[022-091]"I took it from a Mr. Cort, who had a little mill at Fontley in

[022-092]Hampshire:  I have thus acquainted you with my method, by which I am

[022-093]now making more than ten thousand tons of bar-iron per annum."  Samuel

[022-094]Homfray was equally prompt in adopting the new process.  He not only

[022-095]obtained from Cort plans of the [[puddling-process|puddling]]-furnaces and patterns of the

[022-096]rolls, but borrowed Cort's workmen to instruct his own in the necessary

[022-097]operations; and he soon found the method so superior to that invented

[022-098]by Onions that he entirely confined himself to manufacturing after

[022-099]Cort's patent.  We also find [[richard-reynolds|Mr. Reynolds]] inviting Cort to conduct a

[022-100]trial of his process at [[ketley|Ketley]], though it does not appear that it was

[022-101]adopted by the firm at that time.[6]

[022-102]The quality of the iron manufactured by the new process was found

[022-103]satisfactory; and the [[admiralty|Admiralty]] having, by the persons appointed by

[022-104]them to test it in 1787, pronounced it to be superior to the best

[022-105]Oregrounds iron, the use of the latter was thenceforward discontinued,

[022-106]and Cort's iron only was directed to be used for the anchors and other

[022-107]ironwork in the ships of the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]].  The merits of the invention

[022-108]seem to have been generally conceded, and numerous contracts for

[022-109]licences were entered into with Cort and his partner by the

[022-110]manufacturers of bar-iron throughout the country.[7]  Cort himself made

[022-111]arrangements for carrying on the manufacture on a large scale, and with

[022-112]that object entered upon the possession of a wharf at Gosport,

[022-113]belonging to Adam Jellicoe, his partner's father, where he succeeded in

[022-114]obtaining considerable Government orders for iron made after his

[022-115]patents.  To all ordinary eyes the inventor now appeared to be on the

[022-116]high road to fortune; but there was a fatal canker at the root of this

[022-117]seeming prosperity, and in a few years the fabric which he had so

[022-118]laboriously raised crumbled into ruins.  On the death of Adam Jellicoe,

[022-119]the father of Cort's partner, in August, 1789,[8] defalcations were

[022-120]discovered in his public accounts to the extent of 39,676L, and his

[022-121]books and papers were immediately taken possession of by the

[022-122]Government.  On examination it was found that the debts due to Jellicoe

[022-123]amounted to 89,657L, included in which was a sum of not less than

[022-124]54,853L. owing to him by the Cort partnership.  In the public

[022-125]investigation which afterwards took place, it appeared that the capital

[022-126]possessed by Cort being insufficient to enable him to pursue his

[022-127]experiments, which were of a very expensive character, Adam Jellicoe

[022-128]had advanced money from time to time for the purpose, securing himself

[022-129]by a deed of agreement entitling him to one-half the stock and profits

[022-130]of all his contracts; and in further consideration of the capital

[022-131]advanced by Jellicoe beyond his equal share, Cort subsequently assigned

[022-132]to him all his patent rights as collateral security.  As Jellicoe had

[022-133]the reputation of being a rich man, Cort had not the slightest

[022-134]suspicion of the source from which he obtained the advances made by him

[022-135]to the firm, nor has any connivance whatever on the part of Cort been

[022-136]suggested.  At the same time it must be admitted that the connexion was

[022-137]not free from suspicion, and, to say the least, it was a singularly

[022-138]unfortunate one.  It was found that among the moneys advanced by

[022-139]Jellicoe to Cort there was a sum of 27,500L. entrusted to him for the

[022-140]payment of seamen's and officers' wages.  How his embarrassments had

[022-141]tempted him to make use of the public funds for the purpose of carrying

[022-142]on his speculations, appears from his own admissions.  In a memorandum

[022-143]dated the 11th November, 1782, found in his strong box after his death,

[022-144]he set forth that he had always had much more than his proper balance

[022-145]in hand, until his engagement, about two years before, with Mr. Cort,

[022-146]"which by degrees has so reduced me, and employed so much more of my

[022-147]money than I expected, that I have been obliged to turn most of my Navy

[022-148]bills into cash, and at the same time, to my great concern, am very

[022-149]deficient in my balance.  This gives me great uneasiness, nor shall I

[022-150]live or die in peace till the whole is restored."  He had, however,

[022-151]made the first false step, after which the downhill career of

[022-152]dishonesty is rapid.  His desperate attempts to set himself right only

[022-153]involved him the deeper; his conscious breach of trust caused him a

[022-154]degree of daily torment which he could not bear; and the discovery of

[022-155]his defalcations, which was made only a few days before his death,

[022-156]doubtless hastened his end.

[022-157]The Government acted with promptitude, as they were bound to do in such

[022-158]a case.  The body of Jellicoe was worth nothing to them, but they could

[022-159]secure the property in which he had fraudulently invested the public

[022-160]moneys intrusted to him.  With this object the them Paymaster of the

[022-161]Navy proceeded to make an affidavit in the Exchequer that [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]

[022-162]was indebted to His Majesty in the sum of 27,500L. and upwards, in

[022-163]respect of moneys belonging to the public treasury, which "Adam

[022-164]Jellicoe had at different times lent and advanced to the said Henry

[022-165]Cort, from whom the same now remains justly due and owing; and the

[022-166]deponent saith he verily believes that the said Henry Cort is much

[022-167]decayed in his credit and in very embarrassed circumstances; and

[022-168]therefore the deponent verily believes that the aforesaid debt so due

[022-169]and owing to His Majesty is in great danger of being lost if some more

[022-170]speedy means be not taken for the recovery than by the ordinary process

[022-171]of the Court."  Extraordinary measures were therefore adopted.  The

[022-172]assignments of Cort's patents, which had been made to Jellicoe in

[022-173]consideration of his advances, were taken possession of; but Samuel

[022-174]Jellicoe, the son of the defaulter, singular to say, was put in

[022-175]possession of the properties at Fontley and Gosport, and continued to

[022-176]enjoy them, to Cort's exclusion, for a period of fourteen years.  It

[022-177]does not however appear that any patent right was ever levied by the

[022-178]assignees, and the result of the proceeding was that the whole benefit

[022-179]of Cort's inventions was thus made over to the ironmasters and to the

[022-180]public.  Had the estate been properly handled, and the patent rights

[022-181]due under the contracts made by the ironmasters with Cort been duly

[022-182]levied, there is little reason to doubt that the whole of the debt

[022-183]owing to the Government would have been paid in the course of a few

[022-184]years.  "When we consider," says Mr. Webster, "how very simple was the

[022-185]process of demanding of the contracting ironmasters the patent due

[022-186](which for the year 1789 amounted to 15,000L., in 1790 to 15,000L., and

[022-187]in 1791 to 25,000L.), and which demand might have been enforced by the

[022-188]same legal process used to ruin the inventor, it is not difficult to

[022-189]surmise the motive for abstaining."  The case, however, was not so

[022-190]simple as Mr. Webster puts it; for there was such a contingency as that

[022-191]of the ironmasters combining to dispute the patent right, and there is

[022-192]every reason to believe that they were prepared to adopt that course.[9]
