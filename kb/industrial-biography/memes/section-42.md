[042-001]So clever a workman was not likely to remain long a village slater.

[042-002]Although the ingenious pieces of work which he turned out by his lathe

[042-003]did not bring him in much money, he liked the occupation so much better

[042-004]than slating that he was gradually giving up that trade.  His father

[042-005]urged him to stick to slating as "a safe thing;" but his own mind was

[042-006]in favour of following his instinct to be a mechanic; and at length he

[042-007]determined to leave his village and seek work in a new line.  He

[042-008]succeeded in finding employment in a small factory at Kirby Stephen, a

[042-009]town some thirteen miles from Great Ashby, where he worked at making

[042-010][[power-loom|power-looms]].  From an old statement of account against his employer

[042-011]which we have seen, in his own handwriting, dated the 6th September,

[042-012]1805, it appears that his earnings at such work as "fitting the first

[042-013]set of iron loames," "fitting up shittles," and "making moddles," were

[042-014]3s. 6d. a day; and he must, during the same time, have lived with his

[042-015]employer, who charged him as a set-off "14 weaks bord at 8s. per weak."

[042-016]He afterwards seems to have worked at piece-work in partnership with

[042-017]one Andrew Gamble supplying the materials as well as the workmanship

[042-018]for the looms and shuttles.  His employer, Mr. George Dickinson, also

[042-019]seems to have bought his reflecting telescope from him for the sum of

[042-020]12L.

[042-021]From Kirby Stephen Clement removed to [[carlisle|Carlisle]], where he was employed

[042-022]by Forster and Sons during the next two years at the same description

[042-023]of work; and he conducted himself, according; to their certificate on

[042-024]his leaving their employment to proceed to [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1807, "with great

[042-025]sobriety and industry, entirely to their satisfaction."  While working

[042-026]at [[glasgow|Glasgow]] as a turner, he took lessons in drawing from Peter

[042-027]Nicholson, the well-known writer on carpentry--a highly ingenious man.

[042-028]Nicholson happened to call at the shop at which Clement worked in order

[042-029]to make a drawing of a power-loom; and Clement's expressions of

[042-030]admiration at his expertness were so enthusiastic, that Nicholson,

[042-031]pleased with the youth's praise, asked if he could be of service to him

[042-032]in any way.  Emboldened by the offer, Clement requested, as the

[042-033]greatest favour he could confer upon him, to have the loan of the

[042-034]drawing he had just made, in order that he might copy it.  The request

[042-035]was at once complied with; and Clement, though very poor at the time,

[042-036]and scarcely able to buy candle for the long winter evenings, sat up

[042-037]late every night until he had finished it.  Though the first drawing he

[042-038]had ever made, he handed it back to Nicholson instead of the original,

[042-039]and at first the draughtsman did not recognise that the drawing was not

[042-040]his own.  When Clement told him that it was only the copy, Nicholson's

[042-041]brief but emphatic praise was--"Young man, YOU'LL DO!"  Proud to have

[042-042]such a pupil, Nicholson generously offered to give him gratuitous

[042-043]lessons in drawing, which were thankfully accepted; and Clement,

[042-044]working at nights with great ardour, soon made rapid progress, and

[042-045]became an expert draughtsman.

[042-046]Trade being very slack in Glasgow at the time, Clement, after about a

[042-047]year's stay in the place, accepted a situation with Messrs. Leys,

[042-048]Masson, and Co., of [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]], with whom he began at a guinea and a half

[042-049]a week, from which he gradually rose to two guineas, and ultimately to

[042-050]three guineas.  His principal work consisted in designing and making

[042-051][[power-loom|power-looms]] for his employers, and fitting them up in different parts

[042-052]of the country.  He continued to devote himself to the study of

[042-053]practical mechanics, and made many improvements in the tools with which

[042-054]he worked.  While at Glasgow he had made an improved pair of die-stocks

[042-055]for screws; and, at [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]], he made a turning-lathe with a sliding

[042-056]mandrill and guide-screws, for cutting screws, furnished also with the

[042-057]means for correcting guide-screws.  In the same machine he introduced a

[042-058]small slide rest, into which he fixed the tool for cutting the

[042-059]screws,--having never before seen a slide rest, though it is very

[042-060]probable he may have heard of what Maudslay had already done in the

[042-061]same direction.  Clement continued during this period of his life an

[042-062]industrious self-cultivator, occupying most of his spare hours in

[042-063]mechanical and landscape drawing, and in various studies.  Among the

[042-064]papers left behind him we find a ticket to a course of instruction on

[042-065]Natural Philosophy given by Professor Copland in the Marischal College

[042-066]at Aberdeen, which Clement attended in the session of 1812-13; and we

[042-067]do not doubt that our mechanic was among the most diligent of his

[042-068]pupils.  Towards the end of 1813, after saving about 100L. out of his

[042-069]wages, Clement resolved to proceed to [[london|London]] for the purpose of

[042-070]improving himself in his trade and pushing his way in the world.  The

[042-071]coach by which he travelled set him down in Snow Hill, Holborn; and his

[042-072]first thought was of finding work.  He had no friend in town to consult

[042-073]on the matter, so he made inquiry of the coach-guard whether he knew of

[042-074]any person in the mechanical line in that neighbourhood.  The guard

[042-075]said, "Yes; there was Alexander Galloway's show shop, just round the

[042-076]corner, and he employed a large number of hands."  Running round the

[042-077]corner, Clement looked in at Galloway's window, through which he saw

[042-078]some lathes and other articles used in machine shops.  Next morning he

[042-079]called upon the owner of the shop to ask employment.  "What can you

[042-080]do?" asked Galloway.  "I can work at the forge," said Clement.

[042-081]"Anything else?"  "I can turn."  "What else?"  "I can draw."  "What!"

[042-082]said Galloway, "can you draw? Then I will engage you."  A man who could

[042-083]draw or work to a drawing in those days was regarded as a superior sort

[042-084]of mechanic.  Though Galloway was one of the leading tradesmen of his

[042-085]time, and had excellent opportunities for advancement, he missed them

[042-086]all.  As Clement afterwards said of him, "He was only a mouthing

[042-087]common-council man, the height of whose ambition was to be an

[042-088]alderman;" and, like most corporation celebrities, he held a low rank

[042-089]in his own business.  He very rarely went into his workshops to

[042-090]superintend or direct his workmen, leaving this to his foremen--a

[042-091]sufficient indication of the causes of his failure as a mechanic.[1]

[042-092]On entering Galloway's shop, Clement was first employed in working at

[042-093]the lathe; but finding the tools so bad that it was impossible to

[042-094]execute satisfactory work with them, he at once went to the forge, and

[042-095]began making a new set of tools for himself.  The other men, to whom

[042-096]such a proceeding was entirely new, came round him to observe his

[042-097]operations, and they were much struck with his manual dexterity.  The

[042-098]tools made, he proceeded to use them, displaying what seemed to the

[042-099]other workmen an unusual degree of energy and intelligence; and some of

[042-100]the old hands did not hesitate already to pronounce Clement to be the

[042-101]best mechanic in the shop.  When Saturday night came round, the other

[042-102]men were curious to know what wages Galloway would allow the new hand;

[042-103]and when he had been paid, they asked him.  "A guinea," was the reply.

[042-104]"A guinea!  Why, you are worth two if you are worth a shilling," said

[042-105]an old man who came out of the rank--an excellent mechanic, who, though

[042-106]comparatively worthless through his devotion to drink, knew Clement's

[042-107]money value to his employer better than any man there; and he added,

[042-108]"Wait for a week or two, and if you are not better paid than this, I

[042-109]can tell you of a master who will give you a fairer wage."  Several

[042-110]Saturdays came round, but no advance was made on the guinea a week; and

[042-111]then the old workman recommended Clement to offer himself to Bramah at

[042-112]Pimlico, who was always on the look out for first-rate mechanics.

[042-113]Clement acted on the advice, and took with him some of his drawings, at

[042-114]sight of which Bramah immediately engaged him for a month; and at the

[042-115]end of that time he had given so much satisfaction, that it was agreed

[042-116]he should continue for three months longer at two guineas a week.

[042-117]Clement was placed in charge of the tools of the shop, and he showed

[042-118]himself so apt at introducing improvements in them, as well as in

[042-119]organizing the work with a view to despatch and economy, that at the

[042-120]end of the term Bramah made him a handsome present, adding, "if I had

[042-121]secured your services five years since, I would now have been a richer

[042-122]man by many thousands of pounds."  A formal agreement for a term of

[042-123]five years was then entered into between Bramah and Clement, dated the

[042-124]1st of April, 1814, by which the latter undertook to fill the office of

[042-125]chief-draughtsman and superintendent of the Pimlico Works, in

[042-126]consideration of a salary of three guineas a week, with an advance of

[042-127]four shillings a week in each succeeding year of the engagement.  This

[042-128]arrangement proved of mutual advantage to both.  Clement devoted

[042-129]himself with increased zeal to the improvement of the mechanical

[042-130]arrangements of the concern, exhibiting his ingenuity in many ways, and

[042-131]taking; a genuine pride in upholding the character of his master for

[042-132]turning out first-class work.

[042-133]On the death of Bramah, his sons returned from college and entered into

[042-134]possession of the business.  They found Clement the ruling mind there

[042-135]and grew jealous of him to such an extent that his situation became

[042-136]uncomfortable; and by mutual consent he was allowed to leave before the

[042-137]expiry of his term of agreement.  He had no difficulty in finding

[042-138]employment; and was at once taken on as chief draughtsman at Maudslay

[042-139]and Field's where he was of much assistance in proportioning the early

[042-140]marine engines, for the manufacture of which that firm were becoming

[042-141]celebrated.  After a short time, he became desirous of beginning

[042-142]business on his own account as a mechanical engineer.  He was

[042-143]encouraged to do this by the Duke of [[northumberland|Northumberland]], who, being a great

[042-144]lover of mechanics and himself a capital turner, used often to visit

[042-145]Maudslay's, and thus became acquainted with Clement, whose expertness

[042-146]as a draughtsman and mechanic he greatly admired.  Being a man of

[042-147]frugal and sober habits, always keeping his expenditure very

[042-148]considerably within his income, Clement had been enabled to accumulate

[042-149]about 500L., which he thought would be enough for his purpose; and he

[042-150]accordingly proceeded, in 1817, to take a small workshop in Prospect

[042-151]Place, Newington Butts, where he began business as a mechanical

[042-152]draughtsman and manufacturer of small machinery requiring first-class

[042-153]workmanship.

[042-154]From the time when he took his first gratuitous lessons in drawing from

[042-155]Peter Nicholson, at Glasgow, in 1807, he had been steadily improving in

[042-156]this art, the knowledge of which is indispensable to whoever aspires to

[042-157]eminence as a mechanical engineer,--until by general consent Clement

[042-158]was confessed to stand unrivalled as a draughtsman.  Some of the very

[042-159]best drawings contained in the Transactions of the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]],

[042-160]from the year 1817 downwards,--especially those requiring the

[042-161]delineation of any unusually elaborate piece of machinery,--proceeded

[042-162]from the hand of Clement.  In some of these, he reached a degree of

[042-163]truth in mechanical perspective which has never been surpassed.[2]  To

[042-164]facilitate his labours, he invented an extremely ingenious instrument,

[042-165]by means of which ellipses of all proportions, as well as circles and

[042-166]right lines, might be geometrically drawn on paper or on copper.  He

[042-167]took his idea of this instrument from the trammel used by carpenters

[042-168]for drawing imperfect ellipses; and when he had succeeded in avoiding

[042-169]the crossing of the points, he proceeded to invent the straight-line

[042-170]motion.  For this invention the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]] awarded him their gold

[042-171]medal in 1818.  Some years later, he submitted to the same Society his

[042-172]invention of a stand for drawings of large size.  He had experienced

[042-173]considerable difficulty in making such drawings, and with his

[042-174]accustomed readiness to overcome obstacles, he forthwith set to work

[042-175]and brought out his new drawing-table.
