[038-001]One of the first uses to which Mr. Maudslay applied the improved slide

[038-002]rest, which he perfected shortly after beginning business in Margaret

[038-003]Street, Cavendish Square, was in executing the requisite tools and

[038-004]machinery required by Mr. (afterwards Sir Marc Isambard) Brunel for

[038-005]manufacturing ships' blocks.  The career of Brunel was of a more

[038-006]romantic character than falls to the ordinary lot of mechanical

[038-007]engineers.  His father was a small farmer and postmaster, at the

[038-008]village of Hacqueville, in Normandy, where Marc Isambard was born in

[038-009]1769.  He was early intended for a priest, and educated accordingly.

[038-010]But he was much fonder of the carpenter's shop than of the school; and

[038-011]coaxing, entreaty, and punishment alike failed in making a hopeful

[038-012]scholar of him.  He drew faces and plans until his father was almost in

[038-013]despair.  Sent to school at Rouen, his chief pleasure was in watching

[038-014]the ships along the quays; and one day his curiosity was excited by the

[038-015]sight of some large [[cast-iron|iron casting]]s just landed.  What were they? How had

[038-016]they been made? Where did they come from? His eager inquiries were soon

[038-017]answered.  They were parts of an engine intended for the great Paris

[038-018]water-works; the engine was to pump water by the power of steam; and

[038-019]the castings had been made in [[england|England]], and had just been landed from an

[038-020]English ship.  "[[england|England]]!" exclaimed the boy, "ah! when I am a man I

[038-021]will go see the country where such grand machines are made!" On one

[038-022]occasion, seeing a new tool in a cutler's window, he coveted it so much

[038-023]that he pawned his hat to possess it.  This was not the right road to

[038-024]the priesthood; and his father soon saw that it was of no use urging

[038-025]him further:  but the boy's instinct proved truer than the father's

[038-026]judgment.

[038-027]It was eventually determined that he should qualify himself to enter

[038-028]the royal navy, and at seventeen he was nominated to serve in a

[038-029]corvette as "volontaire d'honneur."  His ship was paid off in 1792, and

[038-030]he was at Paris during the trial of the King.  With the incautiousness

[038-031]of youth he openly avowed his royalist opinions in the cafe which he

[038-032]frequented.  On the very day that Louis was condemned to death, Brunel

[038-033]had an angry altercation with some ultra-republicans, after which he

[038-034]called to his dog, "Viens, citoyen!"  Scowling looks were turned upon

[038-035]him, and he deemed it expedient to take the first opportunity of

[038-036]escaping from the house, which he did by a back-door, and made the best

[038-037]of his way to Hacqueville.  From thence he went to Rouen, and succeeded

[038-038]in finding a passage on board an American ship, in which he sailed for

[038-039]New York, having first pledged his affections to an English girl,

[038-040]Sophia Kingdom, whom he had accidentally met at the house of Mr.

[038-041]Carpentier, the American consul at Rouen.

[038-042]Arrived in America, he succeeded in finding employment as assistant

[038-043]surveyor of a tract of land along the Black River, near Lake Ontario.

[038-044]In the intervals of his labours he made occasional visits to New York,

[038-045]and it was there that the first idea of his block-machinery occurred to

[038-046]him.  He carried his idea back with him into the woods, where it often

[038-047]mingled with his thoughts of Sophia Kingdom, by this time safe in

[038-048]England after passing through the horrors of a French prison.  "My

[038-049]first thought of the block-machinery," he once said, "was at a dinner

[038-050]party at Major-General Hamilton's, in New York; my second under an

[038-051]American tree, when, one day that I was carving letters on its bark,

[038-052]the turn of one of them reminded me of it, and I thought, 'Ah! my

[038-053]block! so it must be.' And what do you think were the letters I was

[038-054]cutting? Of course none other than S.  K."  Brunel subsequently

[038-055]obtained some employment as an architect in New York, and promulgated

[038-056]various plans for improving the navigation of the principal rivers.

[038-057]Among the designs of his which were carried out, was that of the Park

[038-058]Theatre at New York, and a cannon foundry, in which he introduced

[038-059]improvements in casting and boring big guns.  But being badly paid for

[038-060]his work, and a powerful attraction drawing him constantly towards

[038-061]England, he determined to take final leave of America, which he did in

[038-062]1799, and landed at Falmouth in the following March.  There he again

[038-063]met Miss Kingdom, who had remained faithful to him during his six long

[038-064]years of exile, and the pair were shortly after united for life.

[038-065]Brunel was a prolific inventor.  During his residence in America, he

[038-066]had planned many contrivances in his mind, which he now proceeded to

[038-067]work out.  The first was a duplicate writing and drawing machine, which

[038-068]he patented.  The next was a machine for twisting cotton thread and

[038-069]forming it into balls; but omitting to protect it by a patent, he

[038-070]derived no benefit from the invention, though it shortly came into very

[038-071]general use.  He then invented a machine for trimmings and borders for

[038-072]muslins, lawns, and cambrics,--of the nature of a sewing machine.  His

[038-073]famous block-machinery formed the subject of his next patent.

[038-074]It may be explained that the making of the blocks employed in the

[038-075]rigging of ships for raising and lowering the sails, masts, and yards,

[038-076]was then a highly important branch of manufacture.  Some idea may be

[038-077]formed of the number used in the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]] alone, from the fact that a

[038-078]74-gun ship required to be provided with no fewer than 1400 blocks of

[038-079]various sizes.  The sheaved blocks used for the running rigging

[038-080]consisted of the shell, the sheaves, which revolved within the shell,

[038-081]and the pins which fastened them together.  The fabrication of these

[038-082]articles, though apparently simple, was in reality attended with much

[038-083]difficulty.  Every part had to be fashioned with great accuracy and

[038-084]precision to ensure the easy working of the block when put together, as

[038-085]any hitch in the raising or lowering of the sails might, on certain

[038-086]emergencies, occasion a serious disaster.  Indeed, it became clear that

[038-087]mere hand-work was not to be relied on in the manufacture of these

[038-088]articles, and efforts were early made to produce them by means of

[038-089]machinery of the most perfect kind that could be devised.  In 1781, Mr.

[038-090]Taylor, of Southampton, set up a large establishment on the river

[038-091]Itchen for their manufacture; and on the expiry of his contract, the

[038-092]Government determined to establish works of their own in [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]]

[038-093]Dockyard, for the purpose at the same time of securing greater economy,

[038-094]and of being independent of individual makers in the supply of an

[038-095]article of such importance in the equipment of ships.

[038-096][[samuel-bentham|Sir Samuel Bentham]], who then filled the office of Inspector-General of

[038-097]Naval Works, was a highly ingenious person, and had for some years been

[038-098]applying his mind to the invention of improved machinery for working in

[038-099]wood.  He had succeeded in introducing into the royal dockyards

[038-100]sawing-machines and planing-machines of a superior kind, as well as

[038-101]block-making machines.  Thus the specification of one of his patents,

[038-102]taken out in 1793, clearly describes a machine for shaping the shells

[038-103]of the blocks, in a manner similar to that afterwards specified by

[038-104]Brunel.  Bentham had even proceeded with the erection of a building in

[038-105][[portsmouth-dockyard|[[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] Dockyard]] for the manufacture of the blocks after his method,

[038-106]the necessary [[steam-engine]] being already provided; but with a singular

[038-107]degree of candour and generosity, on Brunel's method being submitted to

[038-108]him, Sir Samuel at once acknowledged its superiority to his own, and

[038-109]promised to recommend its adoption by the authorities in his department.

[038-110]The circumstance of Mrs. Brunel's brother being Under-Secretary to the

[038-111][[navy-board|Navy Board]] at the time, probably led Brunel in the first instance to

[038-112]offer his invention to the [[admiralty|Admiralty]].  A great deal, however, remained

[038-113]to be done before he could bring his ideas of the block-machinery into

[038-114]a definite shape; for there is usually a wide interval between the

[038-115]first conception of an intricate machine and its practical realization.

[038-116]Though Brunel had a good knowledge of mechanics, and was able to master

[038-117]the intricacies of any machine, he laboured under the disadvantage of

[038-118]not being a practical mechanic and it is probable that but for the help

[038-119]of someone possessed of this important qualification, his invention,

[038-120]ingenious and important though it was, would have borne no practical

[038-121]fruits.  It was at this juncture that he was so fortunate as to be

[038-122]introduced to [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], the inventor of the sliderest.

[038-123]It happened that a M. de Bacquancourt, one of the French emigres, of

[038-124]whom there were then so many in [[london|London]], was accustomed almost daily to

[038-125]pass Maudslay's little shop in Wells-street, and being himself an

[038-126]amateur turner, he curiously inspected the articles from time to time

[038-127]exhibited in the window of the young mechanic.  One day a more than

[038-128]ordinarily nice piece of screw-cutting made its appearance, on which he

[038-129]entered the shop to make inquiries as to the method by which it had

[038-130]been executed.  He had a long conversation with Maudslay, with whom he

[038-131]was greatly pleased; and he was afterwards accustomed to look in upon

[038-132]him occasionally to see what new work was going on.  Bacquancourt was

[038-133]also on intimate terms with Brunel, who communicated to him the

[038-134]difficulty he had experienced in finding a mechanic of sufficient

[038-135]dexterity to execute his design of the block-making machinery.  It

[038-136]immediately occurred to the former that [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] was the very man

[038-137]to execute work of the elaborate character proposed, and he described

[038-138]to Brunel the new and beautiful tools which Maudslay had contrived for

[038-139]the purpose of ensuring accuracy and finish.  Brunel at once determined

[038-140]to call upon Maudslay, and it was arranged that Bacquancourt should

[038-141]introduce him, which he did, and after the interview which took place

[038-142]Brunel promised to call again with the drawings of his proposed model.

[038-143]A few days passed, and Brunel called with the first drawing, done by

[038-144]himself; for he was a capital draughtsman, and used to speak of drawing

[038-145]as the "alphabet of the engineer."  The drawing only showed a little

[038-146]bit of the intended machine, and Brunel did not yet think it advisable

[038-147]to communicate to Maudslay the precise object he had in view; for

[038-148]inventors are usually very chary of explaining their schemes to others,

[038-149]for fear of being anticipated.  Again Brunel appeared at Maudslay's

[038-150]shop with a further drawing, still not explaining his design; but at

[038-151]the third visit, immediately on looking at the fresh drawings he had

[038-152]brought, Maudslay exclaimed, "Ah! now I see what you are thinking of;

[038-153]you want machinery for making blocks." At this Brunel became more

[038-154]communicative, and explained his designs to the mechanic, who fully

[038-155]entered into his views, and went on from that time forward striving to

[038-156]his utmost to work out the inventor's conceptions and embody them in a

[038-157]practical machine.

[038-158]While still occupied on the models, which were begun in 1800, Maudslay

[038-159]removed his shop from Wells-street, where he was assisted by a single

[038-160]journeyman, to Margaret-street, Cavendish-square, where he had greater

[038-161]room for carrying on his trade, and was also enabled to increase the

[038-162]number of his hands.  The working models were ready for inspection by

[038-163][[samuel-bentham|Sir Samuel Bentham]] and the [[admiralty|Lords of the Admiralty]] in 1801, and having

[038-164]been fully approved by them, Brunel was authorized to proceed with the

[038-165]execution of the requisite machinery for the manufacture of the ship's

[038-166]blocks required for the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]].  The whole of this machinery was

[038-167]executed by Henry Maudslay; it occupied him very fully for nearly six

[038-168]years, so that the manufacture of blocks by the new process was not

[038-169]begun until September, 1808.

[038-170]We despair of being able to give any adequate description in words of

[038-171]the intricate arrangements and mode of action of the block-making

[038-172]machinery.  Let any one attempt to describe the much more simple and

[038-173]familiar process by which a shoemaker makes a pair of shoes, and he

[038-174]will find how inadequate mere words are to describe any mechanical

[038-175]operation.[13]  Suffice it to say, that the machinery was of the most

[038-176]beautiful manufacture and finish, and even at this day will bear

[038-177]comparison with the most perfect machines which can be turned out with

[038-178]all the improved appliances of modern tools.  The framing was of

[038-179]cast-iron, while the parts exposed to violent and rapid action were all

[038-180]of the best hardened steel.  In turning out the various parts, Maudslay

[038-181]found his slide rest of indispensable value.  Indeed, without this

[038-182]contrivance, it is doubtful whether machinery of so delicate and

[038-183]intricate a character could possibly have been executed.  There was not

[038-184]one, but many machines in the series, each devoted to a special

[038-185]operation in the formation of a block.  Thus there were various

[038-186]sawing-machines,--the Straight Cross-Cutting Saw, the Circular

[038-187]Cross-Cutting Saw, the Reciprocating Ripping-saw, and the Circular

[038-188]Ripping-Saw.  Then there were the Boring Machines, and the Mortising

[038-189]Machine, of beautiful construction, for cutting the sheave-holes,

[038-190]furnished with numerous chisels, each making from 110 to 150 strokes a

[038-191]minute, and cutting at every stroke a chip as thick as pasteboard with

[038-192]the utmost precision.  In addition to these were the Corner-Saw for

[038-193]cutting off the corners of the block, the [[shaping-machine|Shaping Machine]] for

[038-194]accurately forming the outside surfaces, the Scoring Engine for cutting

[038-195]the groove round the longest diameter of the block for the reception of

[038-196]the rope, and various other machines for drilling, riveting, and

[038-197]finishing the blocks, besides those for making the sheaves.
