[031-001]Steam-locomotion by sea and land, had long been dreamt of and

[031-002]attempted.  Blasco de Garay made his experiment in the harbour of

[031-003]Barcelona as early as 1543; Denis Papin made a similar attempt at

[031-004]Cassel in 1707; but it was not until Watt had solved the problem of the

[031-005][[steam-engine]] that the idea of the steam-boat could be developed in

[031-006]practice, which was done by Miller of Dalswinton in 1788.  Sages and

[031-007]poets have frequently foreshadowed inventions of great social moment.

[031-008]Thus Dr. Darwin's anticipation of the locomotive, in his Botanic

[031-009]Garden, published in 1791, before any locomotive had been invented,

[031-010]might almost be regarded as prophetic:

[031-011]Soon shall thy arm, unconquered Steam! afar

[031-012]Drag the slow barge, and drive the rapid car.

[031-013]Denis Papin first threw out the idea of atmospheric locomotion; and

[031-014]Gauthey, another Frenchman, in 1782 projected a method of conveying

[031-015]parcels and merchandise by subterraneous tubes,[6] after the method

[031-016]recently patented and brought into operation by the [[london|London]] Pneumatic

[031-017]Despatch Company.  The balloon was an ancient Italian invention,

[031-018]revived by Mongolfier long after the original had been forgotten.  Even

[031-019]the reaping machine is an old invention revived.  Thus Barnabe Googe,

[031-020]the translator of a book from the German entitled 'The whole Arte and

[031-021]Trade of Husbandrie,' published in 1577, in the reign of Elizabeth,

[031-022]speaks of the reaping-machine as a worn-out invention--a thing "which

[031-023]was woont to be used in France.  The device was a lowe kinde of carre

[031-024]with a couple of wheeles, and the frunt armed with sharpe syckles,

[031-025]whiche, forced by the beaste through the corne, did cut down al before

[031-026]it.  This tricke," says Googe, "might be used in levell and champion

[031-027]countreys; but with us it wolde make but ill-favoured woorke." [7]  The

[031-028][[thames-tunnel|[[thames|Thames]] Tunnel]] was thought an entirely new manifestation of engineering

[031-029]genius; but the tunnel under the Euphrates at ancient Babylon, and that

[031-030]under the wide mouth of the harbour at Marseilles (a much more

[031-031]difficult work), show that the ancients were beforehand with us in the

[031-032]art of tunnelling.  Macadamized roads are as old as the Roman empire;

[031-033]and suspension bridges, though comparatively new in Europe, have been

[031-034]known in China for centuries.

[031-035]There is every reason to believe--indeed it seems clear that the Romans

[031-036]knew of gunpowder, though they only used it for purposes of fireworks;

[031-037]while the secret of the destructive Greek fire has been lost

[031-038]altogether.  When gunpowder came to be used for purposes of war,

[031-039]invention busied itself upon instruments of destruction.  When recently

[031-040]examining the Museum of the Arsenal at Venice, we were surprised to

[031-041]find numerous weapons of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries

[031-042]embodying the most recent English improvements in arms, such as

[031-043]revolving pistols, rifled muskets, and breech-loading cannon.  The

[031-044]latter, embodying [[william-armstrong|Sir William Armstrong]]'s modern idea, though in a rude

[031-045]form, had been fished up from the bottom of the Adriatic, where the

[031-046]ship armed with them had been sunk hundreds of years ago.  Even

[031-047]Perkins's steam-gun was an old invention revived by Leonardo da Vinci

[031-048]and by him attributed to Archimedes.[8]  The Congreve rocket is said to

[031-049]have an Eastern origin, Sir William Congreve having observed its

[031-050]destructive effects when employed by the forces under Tippoo Saib in

[031-051]the Mahratta war, on which he adopted and improved the missile, and

[031-052]brought out the invention as his own.

[031-053]Coal-gas was regularly used by the Chinese for lighting purposes long

[031-054]before it was known amongst us.  Hydropathy was generally practised by

[031-055]the Romans, who established baths wherever they went.  Even chloroform

[031-056]is no new thing.  The use of ether as an anaesthetic was known to

[031-057]Albertus Magnus, who flourished in the thirteenth century; and in his

[031-058]works he gives a recipe for its preparation.  In 1681 Denis Papin

[031-059]published his Traite des Operations sans Douleur, showing that he had

[031-060]discovered methods of deadening pain.  But the use of anaesthetics is

[031-061]much older than Albertus Magnus or Papin; for the ancients had their

[031-062]nepenthe and mandragora; the Chinese their mayo, and the Egyptians

[031-063]their hachisch (both preparations of Cannabis Indica), the effects of

[031-064]which in a great measure resemble those of chloroform.  What is perhaps

[031-065]still more surprising is the circumstance that one of the most elegant

[031-066]of recent inventions, that of sun-painting by the daguerreotype, was in

[031-067]the fifteenth century known to Leonardo da Vinci,[9] whose skill as an

[031-068]architect and engraver, and whose accomplishments as a chemist and

[031-069]natural philosopher, have been almost entirely overshadowed by his

[031-070]genius as a painter.[10]  The idea, thus early born, lay in oblivion

[031-071]until 1760, when the daguerreotype was again clearly indicated in a

[031-072]book published in Paris, written by a certain Tiphanie de la Roche,

[031-073]under the anagrammatic title of Giphantie.  Still later, at the

[031-074]beginning of the present century, we find Thomas Wedgwood, Sir Humphry

[031-075]Davy, and [[james-watt|James Watt]], making experiments on the action of light upon

[031-076]nitrate of silver; and only within the last few months a silvered

[031-077]copper-plate has been found amongst the old household lumber of Matthew

[031-078]Boulton (Watt's partner), having on it a representation of the old

[031-079]premises at Soho, apparently taken by some such process.[11]

[031-080]In like manner the invention of the electric telegraph, supposed to be

[031-081]exclusively modern, was clearly indicated by Schwenter in his

[031-082]Delasements Physico-Mathematiques, published in 1636; and he there

[031-083]pointed out how two individuals could communicate with each other by

[031-084]means of the magnetic needle.  A century later, in 1746, Le Monnier

[031-085]exhibited a series of experiments in the Royal Gardens at Paris,

[031-086]showing how electricity could be transmitted through iron wire 950

[031-087]fathoms in length; and in 1753 we find one Charles Marshall publishing

[031-088]a remarkable description of the electric telegraph in the Scots

[031-089]Magazine, under the title of 'An expeditions Method of conveying

[031-090]Intelligence.' Again, in 1760, we find George Louis Lesage, professor

[031-091]of mathematics at Geneva, promulgating his invention of an electric

[031-092]telegraph, which he eventually completed and set to work in 1774.  This

[031-093]instrument was composed of twenty-four metallic wires, separate from

[031-094]each other and enclosed in a non-conducting substance.  Each wire ended

[031-095]in a stalk mounted with a little ball of elder-wood suspended by a silk

[031-096]thread.  When a stream of electricity, no matter how slight., was sent

[031-097]through the wire, the elder-ball at the opposite end was repelled, such

[031-098]movement designating some letter of the alphabet.  A few years later we

[031-099]find Arthur Young, in his Travels in France, describing a similar

[031-100]machine invented by a M. Lomond of Paris, the action of which he also

[031-101]describes.[12]  In these and similar cases, though the idea was born

[031-102]and the model of the invention was actually made, it still waited the

[031-103]advent of the scientific mechanical inventor who should bring it to

[031-104]perfection, and embody it in a practical working form.

[031-105]Some of the most valuable inventions have descended to us without the

[031-106]names of their authors having been preserved.  We are the inheritors of

[031-107]an immense legacy of the results of labour and ingenuity, but we know

[031-108]not the names of our benefactors.  Who invented the watch as a measurer

[031-109]of time? Who invented the fast and loose pulley? Who invented the

[031-110]eccentric? Who, asks a mechanical inquirer,[13] "invented the method of

[031-111]cutting screws with stocks and dies? Whoever he might be, he was

[031-112]certainly a great benefactor of his species.  Yet (adds the writer) his

[031-113]name is not known, though the invention has been so recent."  This is

[031-114]not, however, the case with most modern inventions, the greater number

[031-115]of which are more or less disputed.  Who was entitled to the merit of

[031-116]inventing printing has never yet been determined.  Weber and Senefelder

[031-117]both laid claim to the invention of lithography, though it was merely

[031-118]an old German art revived.  Even the invention of the penny-postage

[031-119]system by Sir Rowland Hill is disputed; Dr. Gray of the [[british-museum|British Museum]]

[031-120]claiming to be its inventor, and a French writer alleging it to be an

[031-121]old French invention.[14]  The invention of the steamboat has been

[031-122]claimed on behalf of Blasco de Garay, a Spaniard, Papin, a Frenchman,

[031-123]Jonathan Hulls, an Englishman, and Patrick Miller of Dalswinton, a

[031-124]Scotchman.  The invention of the spinning machine has been variously

[031-125]attributed to Paul, Wyatt, Hargreaves, Higley, and Arkwright.  The

[031-126]invention of the balance-spring was claimed by Huyghens, a Dutchman,

[031-127]Hautefeuille, a Frenchman, and Hooke, an Englishman.  There is scarcely

[031-128]a point of detail in the locomotive but is the subject of dispute.

[031-129]Thus the invention of the blast-pipe is claimed for Trevithick, George

[031-130]Stephenson, Goldsworthy Gurney, and Timothy Hackworth; that of the

[031-131]tubular boiler by Seguin, Stevens, Booth, and W.  H.  James; that of

[031-132]the link-motion by John Gray, Hugh Williams, and [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]].

[031-133]Indeed many inventions appear to be coincident.  A number of minds are

[031-134]working at the same time in the same track, with the object of

[031-135]supplying some want generally felt; and, guided by the same experience,

[031-136]they not unfrequently arrive at like results.  It has sometimes

[031-137]happened that the inventors have been separated by great distances, so

[031-138]that piracy on the part of either was impossible.  Thus Hadley and

[031-139]Godfrey almost simultaneously invented the quadrant, the one in [[london|London]],

[031-140]the other in Philadelphia; and the process of electrotyping was

[031-141]invented at the same time by Mr. Spencer, a working chemist at

[031-142][[liverpool|Liverpool]], and by Professor Jacobi at St.  Petersburg.  The safety-lamp

[031-143]was a coincident invention, made about the same time by Sir Humphry

[031-144]Davy and [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]]; and perhaps a still more remarkable

[031-145]instance of a coincident discovery was that of the planet Neptune by

[031-146]Leverrier at Paris, and by Adams at Cambridge.

[031-147]It is always difficult to apportion the due share of merit which

[031-148]belongs to mechanical inventors, who are accustomed to work upon each

[031-149]other's hints and suggestions, as well as by their own experience.

[031-150]Some idea of this difficulty may be formed from the fact that, in the

[031-151]course of our investigations as to the origin of the planing

[031-152]machine--one of the most useful of modern tools--we have found that it

[031-153]has been claimed on behalf of six inventors--Fox of [[derby|Derby]], Roberts of

[031-154][[manchester|Manchester]], [[matthew-murray|Matthew Murray]] of [[leeds|Leeds]], Spring of [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]], Clement and

[031-155][[george-rennie|George Rennie]] of London; and there may be other claimants of whom we

[031-156]have not yet heard.  But most mechanical inventions are of a very

[031-157]composite character, and are led up to by the labour and the study of a

[031-158]long succession of workers.  Thus Savary and Newcomen led up to Watt;

[031-159]Cugnot, [[william-murdock|Murdock]], and Trevithick to the Stephensons; and Maudslay to

[031-160]Clement, Roberts, Nasmyth, Whitworth, and many more mechanical

[031-161]inventors.  There is scarcely a process in the arts but has in like

[031-162]manner engaged mind after mind in bringing it to perfection.  "There is

[031-163]nothing," says Mr. [[john-hawks|Hawks]]haw, "really worth having that man has

[031-164]obtained, that has not been the result of a combined and gradual

[031-165]process of investigation.  A gifted individual comes across some old

[031-166]footmark, stumbles on a chain of previous research and inquiry.  He

[031-167]meets, for instance, with a machine, the result of much previous

[031-168]labour; he modifies it, pulls it to pieces, constructs and reconstructs

[031-169]it, and by further trial and experiment he arrives at the long

[031-170]sought-for result." [15]

[031-171]But the making of the invention is not the sole difficulty.  It is one

[031-172]thing to invent, said [[marc-isambard-brunel|Sir Marc Brunel]], and another thing to make the

[031-173]invention work.  Thus when Watt, after long labour and study, had

[031-174]brought his invention to completion, he encountered an obstacle which

[031-175]has stood in the way of other inventors, and for a time prevented the

[031-176]introduction of their improvements, if not led to their being laid

[031-177]aside and abandoned.  This was the circumstance that the machine

[031-178]projected was so much in advance of the mechanical capability of the

[031-179]age that it was with the greatest difficulty it could be executed.

[031-180]When labouring upon his invention at [[glasgow|Glasgow]], Watt was baffled and

[031-181]thrown into despair by the clumsiness and incompetency of his workmen.

[031-182]Writing to [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] on one occasion, he said, "You ask what is the

[031-183]principal hindrance in erecting engines? It is always the smith-work."

[031-184]His first cylinder was made by a whitesmith, of hammered iron soldered

[031-185]together, but having used quicksilver to keep the cylinder air-tight,

[031-186]it dropped through the inequalities into the interior, and "played the

[031-187]devil with the solder."  Yet, inefficient though the whitesmith was,

[031-188]Watt could ill spare him, and we find him writing to [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] almost

[031-189]in despair, saying, "My old white-iron man is dead!" feeling his loss

[031-190]to be almost irreparable.  His next cylinder was cast and bored at

[031-191]Carron, but it was so untrue that it proved next to useless.  The

[031-192]piston could not be kept steam tight, notwithstanding the various

[031-193]expedients which were adopted of stuffing it with paper, cork, putty,

[031-194]pasteboard, and old hat.  Even after Watt had removed to [[birmingham|Birmingham]],

[031-195]and he had the assistance of Boulton's best workmen, Smeaton expressed

[031-196]the opinion, when he saw the engine at work, that notwithstanding the

[031-197]excellence of the invention, it could never be brought into general use

[031-198]because of the difficulty of getting its various parts manufactured

[031-199]with sufficient precision.  For a long time we find Watt, in his

[031-200]letters, complaining to his partner of the failure of his engines

[031-201]through "villainous bad workmanship." Sometimes the cylinders, when

[031-202]cast, were found to be more than an eighth of an inch wider at one end

[031-203]than the other; and under such circumstances it was impossible the

[031-204]engine could act with precision.  Yet better work could not be had.

[031-205]First-rate workmen in machinery did not as yet exist; they were only in

[031-206]process of education.  Nearly everything had to be done by hand.  The

[031-207]tools used were of a very imperfect kind.  A few ill-constructed

[031-208]lathes, with some drills and boring-machines of a rude sort,

[031-209]constituted the principal furniture of the workshop.  Years after, when

[031-210]Brunel invented his block-machines, considerable time elapsed before he

[031-211]could find competent mechanics to construct them, and even after they

[031-212]had been constructed he had equal difficulty in finding competent hands

[031-213]to work them.[16]
