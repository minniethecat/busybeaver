Thomas Sharp was the capitalist partner in the firm of Sharp, Roberts, and Co., established in [[manchester|Manchester]] in the early nineteenth century. The firm was formed following [[Richard Roberts]]'s improvements in the machine for making weavers' reeds, and Roberts was the acting mechanical partner for many years. Sharp provided the financial backing that enabled the firm to undertake ambitious manufacturing projects. (046-103)

## Early Life

Little is recorded of Sharp's early life beyond his role as a Manchester capitalist. He appears in the historical record principally through his partnership with [[Richard Roberts]] and the firm they built together at the Atlas Works.

## Career

### The Firm of Sharp, Roberts, and Co.

The firm of Sharp, Roberts, and Co. was one of the leading engineering establishments in [[manchester|Manchester]] during the early nineteenth century. Sharp provided the capital and business direction, while [[Richard Roberts]] contributed mechanical genius — a partnership that proved remarkably successful in both textile machinery and locomotive manufacture.

### The [[self-acting-mule|[[self-acting|Self-Acting]] Mule]]

Sharp played a decisive role in one of the most important textile inventions of the nineteenth century. During the long-continued strike of cotton-spinners in 1824, the manufacturers of [[manchester|Manchester]] sought a mechanic who could devise a self-acting [[mule-spinning|spinning mule]] that would render them less dependent on the refractory spinners. After repeated refusals from other mechanics, Mr. Ashton of Staleybridge and two other leading spinners called on Sharp, Roberts, and Co. (046-126)

Roberts was reluctant to take on the problem, protesting that he knew nothing whatever about [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]]. After the manufacturers' third visit, they appealed to Mr. Sharp, the capitalist of the firm, who promised to use his best endeavours to induce his mechanical partner to take the matter in hand.

Sharp's intervention proved crucial — Roberts, who had in fact been carefully pondering the subject all the while, was now ready. When Sharp introduced the subject, Roberts said he had turned the matter over and thought he could construct the required self-acting machinery. (046-135) The result was the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]], patented in 1825, one of the most elaborate and beautiful pieces of machinery ever contrived. (046-143)

### [[locomotive|Locomotive]] Manufacture

A considerable branch of business carried on by the firm was the manufacture of iron billiard-tables, constructed with almost perfect truth by means of Roberts's planing-machine. But a much more important and remunerative department was the manufacture of locomotives, begun by the firm shortly after the opening of the [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway. (047-001)

In 1834, Sharp, Roberts, and Co. began the manufacture of locomotives on a large scale. The compactness of their engines, the excellence of their workmanship, and the numerous original improvements introduced in them, speedily secured for the engines of the Atlas firm a high reputation and a very large demand. (047-021)

## Legacy

Sharp's role as capitalist and partner to [[Richard Roberts]] placed him at the intersection of two transformative industries — textile manufacture and railway locomotion. His financial backing and business judgment enabled Roberts's mechanical genius to flourish, and the firm's products — from the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] to the Atlas locomotives — became landmarks of early Victorian engineering.
