The **Edinburgh Review**, founded in 1802, was one of the most influential quarterly periodicals of nineteenth-century Britain. Alongside its literary and political coverage, the Review published detailed technical assessments of mechanical engineering that shaped public and professional opinion.

## Engineering Criticism

The Edinburgh Review covered major engineering developments with analytical rigor. It published an extensive description of [[[james-watt|James Watt]]](/james-watt)'s steam engine, devoting many pages "in endeavouring to describe its action" (044-066), though some features of the engine were given up as indescribable. The Review's account of the [[steam-engine|Watt engine]] led "those clever mechanics to enter upon the scheme of constructing and completing it" (045-023), demonstrating the journal's catalytic role in disseminating engineering knowledge.

A writer in the Edinburgh Review praised [[[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]](/james-nasmyth) as possessing "an intellect as profound as it is versatile" (051-103), cementing Nasmyth's reputation in the broader intellectual culture. The Review also articulated a key insight about the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]: that "Skill in mechanical contrivance is a matter of education" (041-133), recognizing that engineering expertise was cultivated, not innate.
