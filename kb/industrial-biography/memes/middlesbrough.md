Middlesbrough, situated on the south bank of the River Tees in the North Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], grew from a small hamlet into one of the most important centres of iron production in Victorian Britain.

## The Cleveland [[ironstone|Ironstone]]

The foundation of Middlesbrough's prosperity was the discovery and exploitation of the Cleveland ironstone deposits in the nearby hills. The ironstone of Cleveland had been known since medieval times—the monks of nearby abbeys were acquainted with the art of forging and early turned to account the riches of the Cleveland ironstone. (001-040) However, it was not until the mid-19th century that the deposits were worked on a large scale.

## Rise as an Iron Town

The development of Middlesbrough as an iron-producing town was led by the partnership of Bolckow and Vaughan, who established ironworks on the Tees in the 1840s. The town rapidly attracted blast furnaces, rolling mills, and associated industries, drawing in workers from across Britain. By the 1860s, Middlesbrough was producing a substantial portion of Britain's pig iron, earning it the nickname "Ironopolis."

The growth of the iron industry transformed Middlesbrough from a village of fewer than thirty inhabitants at the start of the 19th century to a major industrial port, shipping coal, ironstone, and finished iron products along the coast and to [[london|London]].
