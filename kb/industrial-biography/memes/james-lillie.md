## Early Life

James Lillie was a shopmate of [[William Fairbairn]] in [[manchester|Manchester]]. In 1816–17, when Fairbairn began to aim at taking a higher position and entertained the ambition of beginning business on his own account, he induced Lillie to join him in the undertaking. This proved the beginning of a business connection which lasted for a period of fifteen years. (054-043)

## The Founding of Fairbairn and Lillie

The first job Fairbairn executed on his own account was the erection of an iron conservatory and hothouse for Mr. J. Hulme of Clayton, near [[manchester|Manchester]], and Lillie joined him in this initial venture. Although the patterns for the conservatory were all made and the castings were begun, the work was not proceeded with, as a [[birmingham|Birmingham]] firm claimed the plan was an infringement of their patent. (054-044)

The young firm were consequently under the necessity of looking about them for other employment. In 1817 they hired a small shed at a rent of 12 shillings a week, in which they set up a lathe of their own making, capable of turning shafts of from 3 to 6 inches diameter, and they hired a strong Irishman to drive the wheel and assist at the heavy work. Their first job was the erection of a cullender, and their next a calico-polishing machine. (054-058)

## Crisis and Breakthrough

Orders came in slowly, and James Lillie began to despair of success. His more hopeful partner, Fairbairn, strenuously urged him to perseverance, and so buoyed him up with hopes of orders that he determined to go on a little longer. They then issued cards among the manufacturers and made a tour of the principal firms, offering their services and soliciting work. (054-060)

Amongst others, Fairbairn called upon Adam and George Murray, the large cotton-spinners. Mr. [[adam-murray|Adam Murray]] received him kindly, heard his explanations, and invited him to call on the following day with his partner. The next day, when Fairbairn and Lillie called together, Murray took them over his mill and asked whether they felt themselves competent to renew with horizontal cross-shafts the whole of the work by which the mule-spinning machinery was turned. (054-071)

This was a formidable enterprise for a young firm without capital and almost without plant to undertake, but they had confidence in themselves and boldly replied that they were willing and able to execute the work. Murray prudently visited their workshop; despite finding only a single lathe, he was more favourably impressed by the individual merits of the partners than by the excellence of their [[machine-tool|machine-tools]], and he gave them the order. (054-077)

## Rising Reputation

Working "late and early — from 5 in the morning until 9 at night for a considerable period," Fairbairn and Lillie succeeded in completing the alterations within the time specified, and to Murray's entire satisfaction. The practical skill of the young men being thus proved, Murray took the opportunity of recommending them to his friends in the trade. (054-090)

The firm's most important early work was the new system of mill gearing they designed for the large new mill erected in 1818 for the firm of MacConnel and Kennedy. Their gearing system proved a great improvement on all that had preceded it, and Mr. [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]] added an original invention of his own in a system of double speeds. (055-077)

The satisfactory execution of this important work at once placed the firm of Fairbairn and Lillie in the very front rank of engineering millwrights. Kennedy's good word was of itself a passport to fame and business, and orders poured in so rapidly that they had difficulty in keeping pace with the demands of the trade. (055-082)

## Partnership and Legacy

The partnership of Fairbairn and Lillie lasted for fifteen years, during which time the firm grew from a single shed with one lathe into a substantial engineering establishment with a factory of its own, fitted with the most improved machinery for turning out millwork. (054-045)
