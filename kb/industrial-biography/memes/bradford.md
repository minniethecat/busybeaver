## Overview

Bradford, in the West Riding of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], emerged as one of the major industrial centres of nineteenth-century Britain. While primarily known for its dominance in worsted wool textile manufacture, Bradford's growth was supported by the iron and engineering industries of the surrounding [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] coalfield. The town's textile mills, steam engines, and iron-framed buildings were products of the same industrial forces chronicled throughout the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Industrial Growth

Bradford's transformation accelerated with the mechanisation of the woollen and worsted trades during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The town's position at the edge of the [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] coalfield gave it access to cheap fuel for steam power, while the soft water of the Pennine streams was ideal for washing and processing wool. (007-076)

The introduction of the [[power-loom|power loom]] and spinning machinery transformed Bradford's mills into large-scale industrial enterprises. By the 1830s, the town had surpassed Norwich as the centre of the worsted trade, and its population grew from around 6,000 in 1801 to over 100,000 by 1851.

## Engineering and Iron Connections

Bradford's textile industry depended on the products of the iron and engineering trades. Steam engines built in [[Leeds]], [[Manchester]], and [[Birmingham]] powered the mills, while iron frames, columns, and beams from the [[south-wales|South Wales]] and Midlands ironworks enabled the construction of the large multi-storey mills that dominated the town's skyline.

The engineer [[James Nasmyth]] visited Bradford during his walking tour of English industrial centres, travelling on foot between Bradford and Trowbridge to [[Bristol]], gaining valuable insight into the manufacturing practices of the West Country and the North. (053-173) This journey reflected the interconnected nature of Britain's industrial geography, where engineers moved freely between textile districts, ironworks, and port cities.

## Transport Links

Bradford's industrial growth was facilitated by improved transport connections. The [[leeds|Leeds]] and [[liverpool|Liverpool]] Canal, completed in 1816, provided a water route for heavy goods. The arrival of the railway in the 1840s connected Bradford directly to the national network, enabling the efficient import of wool, coal, and iron and the export of finished textiles.
