**Iron founding** is the process of melting pig or [[pig-iron|cast iron]] and pouring it into moulds to produce shaped iron products — from domestic pots and kettles to massive engineering castings. It was one of the earliest branches of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] to be industrialised, centred upon foundries that often operated alongside blast furnaces.

## 定义

Iron founding differs from smelting (producing iron from ore) and refining (converting pig iron to wrought iron). It is a manufacturing process: pig iron is remelted in a cupola or [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] and cast into shapes determined by sand or loam moulds. The term "founding" derives from the same root as "foundry" — the workshop where the casting takes place. The principal products ranged from hollow-ware (pots, kettles) in the early period to structural castings, engine parts, and railway components in the nineteenth century.

## 历史发展

### 起源：Coalbrookdale

The earliest documented English [[foundry|iron foundry]] of significance was at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in [[shropshire|Shropshire]], established by [[Abraham Darby I|Abraham Darby]] around 1709. The Darby Memorandum Book records that:

> The make of iron at the Coalbrookdale foundry, in 1713, varied from five to ten tons a week. The principal articles cast were pots, kettles, and other "hollow ware," direct from the smelting-furnace; the rest of the metal was run into pigs. In course of time we find that other castings were turned out: a few grates, smoothing-irons, door-frames, weights, baking-plates, cart-bushes, iron pestles and mortars, and occasionally a tailor's goose. (016-019)

The trade gradually expanded until as many as 150 pots and kettles were cast in a single week. The castings of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] "gradually acquired a reputation" and the works became a principal seat of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]]. (016-040) After Abraham Darby's death, his son and grandson "largely extended the operations of the firm, until [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], or, as it was popularly called, 'Bedlam,' became the principal seat of one of the most important branches of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]]." (016-046)

### 技术演进

Early founding relied on charcoal-fired furnaces. The transition to coke, pioneered at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], was gradual. The Darby Memorandum Book records the fuel mixture used: "five baskets of coke, two of brays, and one of peat" — coke gradually replacing charcoal as wood became scarce. (016-027)

By the later eighteenth century, the [[reverberatory-furnace]] and [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]] were adopted in foundries, allowing the metal to be melted without direct contact with the fuel. This improved both quality and scale. The [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] process]] and rolling mill (1783–1784) further integrated the foundry into a continuous production chain: blast furnace → pig iron → foundry casting.

## 工艺范围

Iron founding encompassed a wide range of products and scales:

| Era | Typical Products | Notable Foundries |
|-----|-----------------|-------------------|
| Early 1700s | Pots, kettles, hollow-ware, firebacks | [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] |
| Mid 1700s | Grates, weights, machine frames, pipes | Carron, Bersham |
| Late 1700s | Cannon, cylinders, structural beams, bridges | [[ketley|Ketley]], [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] |
| Early 1800s | Engine parts, railway chairs, columns, decorative ironwork | Various |

The transition from domestic castings to engineering and structural castings marked the foundry's evolution from a craft shop to an industrial enterprise. (017-065) By the early nineteenth century, foundries were integral to every major ironworks, producing everything from small machine parts to massive [[steam-engine]] cylinders.

## 经济与工业影响

Iron founding was a crucial link in the [[iron-industry|iron industry]] chain. Without foundries capable of producing precision castings, the steam engine, the railway, and the iron bridge would have been impossible. The foundry created the demand that drove blast furnace production, and its products — from the humble cooking pot to the [[Iron Bridge|iron bridge]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] — transformed daily life and the built environment across Britain. (016-048)
