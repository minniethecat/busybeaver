[011-001]In 1643, according to the statement contained in his petition above

[011-002]referred to, [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] acted as military engineer in setting out the

[011-003]fortifications of [[worcestershire|Worcester]] and Stafford, and furnishing them with

[011-004]ordnance.  After the taking of Lichfield, in which he had a share, he

[011-005]was made Colonel of Dragoons, and accompanied the Queen with his

[011-006]regiment to the royal head-quarters at Oxford.  The year after we find

[011-007]him at the siege of [[gloucester|Gloucester]], then at the first battle of Newbury

[011-008]leading the forlorn hope with Sir George Lisle, afterwards marching

[011-009]with Sir Charles Lucas into the associate counties, and present at the

[011-010]royalist rout at [[newport|Newport]].  That he was esteemed a valiant and skilful

[011-011]officer is apparent from the circumstance, that in 1645 he was

[011-012]appointed general of Prince Maurice's train of artillery, and

[011-013]afterwards held the same rank under Lord Ashley.  The iron districts

[011-014]being still for the most part occupied by the royal armies, our

[011-015]military engineer turned his practical experience to account by

[011-016]directing the forging of drakes[9] of bar-iron, which were found of

[011-017]great use, giving up his own dwelling-house in the city of [[worcestershire|Worcester]]

[011-018]for the purpose of carrying on the manufacture of these and other arms.

[011-019]But Worcester and the western towns fell before the [[house-of-commons|Parliament]]arian

[011-020]armies in 1646, and all the iron-works belonging to royalists, from

[011-021]which the principal supplies of arms had been drawn by the King's army,

[011-022]were forthwith destroyed.

[011-023][[dudley|Dudley]] fully shared in the dangers and vicissitudes of that trying

[011-024]period, and bore his part throughout like a valiant soldier.  For two

[011-025]years nothing was heard of him, until in 1648, when the king's party

[011-026]drew together again, and made head in different parts of the country,

[011-027]north and south.  Goring raised his standard in Essex, but was driven

[011-028]by Fairfax into Colchester, where he defended himself for two months.

[011-029]While the siege was in progress, the royalists determined to make an

[011-030]attempt to raise it.  On this [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] again made his appearance in

[011-031]the field, and, joining sundry other counties, he proceeded to raise

[011-032]200 men, mostly at his own charge.  They were, however, no sooner

[011-033]mustered in Bosco Bello woods near Madeley, than they were attacked by

[011-034]the Parliamentarians, and dispersed or taken prisoners.  Dud was among

[011-035]those so taken, and he was first carried to Hartlebury Castle and

[011-036]thence to Worcester, where he was imprisoned.  Recounting the

[011-037]sufferings of himself and his followers on this occasion, in the

[011-038]petition presented to Charles II. in 1660,[10] he says, "200 men were

[011-039]dispersed, killed, and some taken, namely, Major Harcourt, Major

[011-040]Elliotts, Capt.  Long, and Cornet Hodgetts, of whom Major Harcourt was

[011-041]miserably burned with matches.  The petitioner and the rest were

[011-042]stripped almost naked, and in triumph and scorn carried up to the city

[011-043]of Worcester (which place Dud had fortified for the king), and kept

[011-044]close prisoners, with double guards set upon the prison and the city."

[011-045]Notwithstanding this close watch and durance, [[dudley|Dudley]] and Major Elliotts

[011-046]contrived to break out of gaol, making their way over the tops of the

[011-047]houses, afterwards passing the guards at the city gates, and escaping

[011-048]into the open country.  Being hotly pursued, they travelled during the

[011-049]night, and took to the trees during the daytime.  They succeeded in

[011-050]reaching [[london|London]], but only to drop again into the lion's mouth; for

[011-051]first Major Elliotts was captured, then Dudley, and both were taken

[011-052]before Sir John Warner, the Lord Mayor, who forthwith sent them before

[011-053]the "cursed committee of insurrection," as Dudley calls them.  The

[011-054]prisoners were summarily sentenced to be shot to death, and were

[011-055]meanwhile closely imprisoned in the Gatehouse at Westminster, with

[011-056]other Royalists.

[011-057]The day before their intended execution, the prisoners formed a plan of

[011-058]escape.  It was Sunday morning, the 20th August, 1648, when they seized

[011-059]their opportunity, "at ten of the cloeke in sermon time;" and,

[011-060]overpowering the gaolers, Dudley, with Sir Henry Bates, Major Elliotts,

[011-061]Captain South, Captain Paris, and six others, succeeded in getting

[011-062]away, and making again for the open country.  Dudley had received a

[011-063]wound in the leg, and could only get along with great difficulty.  He

[011-064]records that he proceeded on crutches, through Worcester, Tewkesbury,

[011-065]and [[gloucester|Gloucester]], to [[bristol|Bristol]], having been "fed three weeks in private in

[011-066]an enemy's hay mow."  Even the most lynx-eyed Parliamentarian must have

[011-067]failed to recognise the quondam royalist general of artillery in the

[011-068]helpless creature dragging himself along upon crutches; and he reached

[011-069][[bristol|Bristol]] in safety.

[011-070]His military career now over, he found himself absolutely penniless.

[011-071]His estate of about 200L. per annum had been sequestrated and sold by

[011-072]the government;[11] his house in Worcester had been seized and his

[011-073]sickly wife turned out of doors; and his goods, stock, great shop, and

[011-074]ironworks, which he himself valued at 2000L., were destroyed.  He had

[011-075]also lost the offices of Serjeant-at-arms, Lieutenant of Ordnance, and

[011-076]Surveyor of the Mews, which he had held under the king; in a word, he

[011-077]found himself reduced to a state of utter destitution.

[011-078]Dudley was for some time under the necessity of living in great privacy

[011-079]at Bristol; but when the king had been executed, and the royalists were

[011-080]finally crushed at Worcester, Dud gradually emerged from his

[011-081]concealment.  He was still the sole possessor of the grand secret of

[011-082]smelting iron with pit-coal, and he resolved upon one more commercial

[011-083]adventure, in the hope of yet turning it to good account.  He succeeded

[011-084]in inducing Walter Stevens, linendraper, and John Stone, merchant, both

[011-085]of Bristol, to join him as partners in an ironwork, which they

[011-086]proceeded to erect near that city.  The buildings were well advanced,

[011-087]and nearly 700L. had been expended, when a quarrel occurred between

[011-088]Dudley and his partners, which ended in the stoppage of the works, and

[011-089]the concern being thrown into Chancery.  Dudley alleges that the other

[011-090]partners "cunningly drew him into a bond," and "did unjustly enter

[011-091]staple actions in Bristol of great value against him, because he was of

[011-092]the king's party;" but it would appear as if there had been some twist

[011-093]or infirmity of temper in Dudley himself, which prevented him from

[011-094]working harmoniously with such persons as he became associated with in

[011-095]affairs of business.

[011-096]In the mean time other attempts were made to smelt iron with pit-coal.

[011-097]Dudley says that Cromwell and the then [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] granted a patent to

[011-098]Captain Buck for the purpose; and that Cromwell himself, Major Wildman,

[011-099]and various others were partners in the patent.  They erected furnaces

[011-100]and works in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]];[12] but, though Cromwell and his

[011-101]officers could fight and win battles, they could not smelt and forge

[011-102]iron with pit-coal.  They brought one Dagney, an Italian glass-maker,

[011-103]from Bristol, to erect a new furnace for them, provided with sundry

[011-104]pots of glass-house clay; but no success attended their efforts.  The

[011-105]partners knowing of Dudley's possession of the grand secret, invited

[011-106]him to visit their works; but all they could draw from him was that

[011-107]they would never succeed in making iron to profit by the methods they

[011-108]were pursuing.  They next proceeded to erect other works at Bristol,

[011-109]but still they failed.  Major Wildman[13] bought Dudley's sequestrated

[011-110]estate, in the hope of being able to extort his secret of making iron

[011-111]with pit-coal; but all their attempts proving abortive, they at length

[011-112]abandoned the enterprise in despair.  In 1656, one Captain Copley

[011-113]obtained from Cromwell a further patent with a similar object; and

[011-114]erected works near Bristol, and also in the Forest of Kingswood.  The

[011-115]mechanical engineers employed by Copley failed in making his bellows

[011-116]blow; on which he sent for Dudley, who forthwith "made his bellows to

[011-117]be blown feisibly;" but Copley failed, like his predecessors, in making

[011-118]iron, and at length he too desisted from further experiments.

[011-119]Such continued to be the state of things until the Restoration, when we

[011-120]find Dud Dudley a petitioner to the king for the renewal of his patent.

[011-121]He was also a petitioner for compensation in respect of the heavy

[011-122]losses he had sustained during the civil wars.  The king was besieged

[011-123]by crowds of applicants of a similar sort, but Dudley was no more

[011-124]successful than the others.  He failed in obtaining the renewal of his

[011-125]patent.  Another applicant for the like privilege, probably having

[011-126]greater interest at court, proved more successful.  Colonel Proger and

[011-127]three others[14] were granted a patent to make iron with coal; but

[011-128]Dudley knew the secret, which the new patentees did not; and their

[011-129]patent came to nothing.

[011-130]Dudley continued to address the king in importunate petitions, asking

[011-131]to be restored to his former offices of Serjeant-at-arms, Lieutenant of

[011-132]Ordnance, and Surveyor of the Mews or Armoury.  He also petitioned to

[011-133]be appointed Master of the Charter House in Smithfield, professing

[011-134]himself willing to take anything, or hold any living.[15]  We find him

[011-135]sending in two petitions to a similar effect in June, 1660; and a third

[011-136]shortly after.  The result was, that he was reappointed to the office

[011-137]of Serjeant-at-Arms; but the Mastership of the Charter-House was not

[011-138]disposed of until 1662, when it fell to the lot of one Thomas

[011-139]Watson.[16]  In 1661, we find a patent granted to [[william-chamberlaine|Wm. Chamberlaine]]

[011-140]and--Dudley, Esq., for the sole use of their new invention of plating

[011-141]steel, &c., and tinning the said plates; but whether Dud Dudley was the

[011-142]person referred to, we are unable precisely to determine.  A few years

[011-143]later, he seems to have succeeded in obtaining the means of prosecuting

[011-144]his original invention; for in his Metallum Martis, published in 1665,

[011-145]he describes himself as living at Green's Lodge, in [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]]; and

[011-146]he says that near it are four forges, Green's Forge, Swin Forge, Heath

[011-147]Forge, and Cradley Forge, where he practises his "perfect invention."

[011-148]These forges, he adds, "have barred all or most part of their iron with

[011-149]pit-coal since the authors first invention In 1618, which hath

[011-150]preserved much wood.  In these four, besides many other forges, do the

[011-151]like [sic ]; yet the author hath had no benefit thereby to this

[011-152]present."  From that time forward, Dud becomes lost to sight.  He seems

[011-153]eventually to have retired to St.  Helen's in [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]], where he

[011-154]died in 1684, in the 85th year of his age.  He was buried in the parish

[011-155]church there, and a monument, now destroyed, was erected to his memory,

[011-156]bearing the inscription partly set forth underneath.[17]
