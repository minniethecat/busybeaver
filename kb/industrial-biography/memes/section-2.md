[002-189]INDUSTRIAL BIOGRAPHY.

[002-190]CHAPTER I.

[002-191]IRON AND CIVILIZATION.

[002-192]"Iron is not only the soul of every other manufacture, but the main

[002-193]spring perhaps of civilized society."--FRANCIS HORNER.

[002-194]"Were the use of iron lost among us, we should in a few ages be

[002-195]unavoidably reduced to the wants and ignorance of the ancient savage

[002-196]Americans; so that he who first made known the use of that contemptible

[002-197]mineral may be truly styled the father of Arts and the author of

[002-198]Plenty."--JOHN LOCKE.

[002-199]When Captain Cook and the early navigators first sailed into the South

[002-200]Seas on their voyages of discovery, one of the things that struck them

[002-201]with most surprise was the avidity which the natives displayed for

[002-202]iron.  "Nothing would go down with our visitors," says Cook, "but

[002-203]metal; and iron was their beloved article."  A nail would buy a

[002-204]good-sized pig; and on one occasion the navigator bought some four

[002-205]hundred pounds weight of fish for a few wretched knives improvised out

[002-206]of an old hoop.

[002-207]"For iron tools," says Captain Carteret, "we might have purchased

[002-208]everything upon the Freewill Islands that we could have brought away.

[002-209]A few pieces of old iron hoop presented to one of the natives threw him

[002-210]into an ecstasy little short of distraction."  At Otaheite the people

[002-211]were found generally well-behaved and honest; but they were not proof

[002-212]against the fascinations of iron.  Captain Cook says that one of them,

[002-213]after resisting all other temptations, "was at length ensnared by the

[002-214]charms of basket of nails."  Another lurked about for several days,

[002-215]watching the opportunity to steal a coal-rake.

[002-216]The navigators found they could pay their way from island to island

[002-217]merely with scraps of iron, which were as useful for the purpose as

[002-218]gold coins would have been in Europe.  The drain, however, being

[002-219]continuous, Captain Cook became alarmed at finding his currency almost

[002-220]exhausted; and he relates his joy on recovering an old anchor which the

[002-221]French Captain Bougainville had lost at Bolabola, on which he felt as

[002-222]an English banker would do after a severe run upon him for gold, when

[002-223]suddenly placed in possession of a fresh store of bullion.

[002-224]The avidity for iron displayed by these poor islanders will not be

[002-225]wondered at when we consider that whoever among them was so fortunate

[002-226]as to obtain possession of an old nail, immediately became a man of

[002-227]greater power than his fellows, and assumed the rank of a capitalist.

[002-228]"An Otaheitan chief," says Cook, "who had got two nails in his

[002-229]possession, received no small emolument by letting out the use of them

[002-230]to his neighbours for the purpose of boring holes when their own

[002-231]methods failed, or were thought too tedious."

[002-232]The native methods referred to by Cook were of a very clumsy sort; the

[002-233]principal tools of the Otaheitans being of wood, stone, and flint.

[002-234]Their adzes and axes were of stone.  The gouge most commonly used by

[002-235]them was made out of the bone of the human forearm.  Their substitute

[002-236]for a knife was a shell, or a bit of flint or jasper.  A shark's tooth,

[002-237]fixed to a piece of wood, served for an auger; a piece of coral for a

[002-238]file; and the skin of a sting-ray for a polisher.  Their saw was made

[002-239]of jagged fishes' teeth fixed on the convex edge of a piece of hard

[002-240]wood.  Their weapons were of a similarly rude description; their clubs

[002-241]and axes were headed with stone, and their lances and arrows were

[002-242]tipped with flint.  Fire was another agency employed by them, usually

[002-243]in boat-building.  Thus, the New Zealanders, whose tools were also of

[002-244]stone, wood, or bone, made their boats of the trunks of trees hollowed

[002-245]out by fire.

[002-246]The stone implements were fashioned, Captain Cook says, by rubbing one

[002-247]stone upon another until brought to the required shape; but, after all,

[002-248]they were found very inefficient for their purpose.  They soon became

[002-249]blunted and useless; and the laborious process of making new tools had

[002-250]to be begun again.  The delight of the islanders at being put in

[002-251]possession of a material which was capable of taking a comparatively

[002-252]sharp edge and keeping it, may therefore readily be imagined; and hence

[002-253]the remarkable incidents to which we have referred in the experience of

[002-254]the early voyagers.  In the minds of the natives, iron became the

[002-255]representative of power, efficiency, and wealth; and they were ready

[002-256]almost to fall down and worship their new tools, esteeming the axe as a

[002-257]deity, offering sacrifices to the saw, and holding the knife in

[002-258]especial veneration.

[002-259]In the infancy of all nations the same difficulties must have been

[002-260]experienced for want of tools, before the arts of smelting and working

[002-261]in metals had become known; and it is not improbable that the

[002-262]Phoenician navigators who first frequented our coasts found the same

[002-263]avidity for bronze and iron existing among the poor woad-stained

[002-264]Britons who flocked down to the shore to see their ships and exchange

[002-265]food and skins with them, that Captain Cook discovered more than two

[002-266]thousand years later among the natives of Otaheite and New Zealand.

[002-267]For, the tools and weapons found in ancient burying-places in all parts

[002-268]of Britain clearly show that these islands also have passed through the

[002-269]epoch of stone and flint.

[002-270]There was recently exhibited at the Crystal Palace a collection of

[002-271]ancient European weapons and implements placed alongside a similar

[002-272]collection of articles brought from the South Seas; and they were in

[002-273]most respects so much alike that it was difficult to believe that they

[002-274]did not belong to the same race and period, instead of being the

[002-275]implements of races sundered by half the globe, and living at periods

[002-276]more than two thousand years apart.  Nearly every weapon in the one

[002-277]collection had its counterpart in the other,--the mauls or celts of

[002-278]stone, the spearheads of flint or jasper, the arrowheads of flint or

[002-279]bone, and the saws of jagged stone, showing how human ingenuity, under

[002-280]like circumstances, had resorted to like expedients.  It would also

[002-281]appear that the ancient tribes in these islands, like the New

[002-282]Zealanders, used fire to hollow out their larger boats; several

[002-283]specimens of this kind of vessel having recently been dug up in the

[002-284]valleys of the Witham and the [[clyde|Clyde]], some of the latter from under the

[002-285]very streets of modern [[glasgow|Glasgow]].[1]  Their smaller boats, or coracles,

[002-286]were made of osiers interwoven, covered with hides, and rigged with

[002-287]leathern sails and thong tackle.

[002-288]It will readily be imagined that anything like civilization, as at

[002-289]present understood, must have been next to impossible under such

[002-290]circumstances.  "Miserable indeed," says Carlyle, "was the condition of

[002-291]the aboriginal savage, glaring fiercely from under his fleece of hair,

[002-292]which with the beard reached down to his loins, and hung round them

[002-293]like a matted cloak; the rest of his body sheeted in its thick natural

[002-294]fell.  He loitered in the sunny glades of the forest, living on wild

[002-295]fruits; or, as the ancient Caledonians, squatted himself in morasses,

[002-296]lurking for his bestial or human prey; without implements, without

[002-297]arms, save the ball of heavy flint, to which, that his sole possession

[002-298]and defence might not be lost, he had attached a long cord of plaited

[002-299]thongs; thereby recovering as well as hurling it with deadly, unerring

[002-300]skill."
