David Mushet (1772–1847) was a Scottish metallurgist, ironmaster, and one of the most skilled assayers of his era, best known for his discovery of the Black Band ironstone and his lifelong contributions to the science of iron and steel manufacture. (025-155)

## Early Life

David Mushet was born at Dalkeith, near [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], in 1772. The Mushets were an old Kincardine family. (025-155) (027-023) His early career led him into the study of iron ores and metals, where his aptitude for chemical analysis and assaying soon became evident. (001-247)

## Career

### Black Band Discovery

The great expansion of the Scottish [[iron-industry|iron trade]] was mainly due to the discovery by David Mushet of the Black Band ironstone in 1801. This discovery, made while he was on the staff of the [[calder-ironworks|Calder Ironworks]] Company, proved to be of immense value to the iron industry, though Mushet himself was left without adequate recognition or reward. (025-155) (025-171)

### Assayer and Metallurgical Research

David Mushet became the most skilled assayer of his time, and his labours bore their due fruits. He published the results of his investigations, establishing himself as a leading authority on all matters relating to iron and steel manufacture. (026-035) (026-121) (026-170) While residing in India for a period, Mushet also developed a process for producing steel direct from the ore. (021-041)

### Titanium Discovery

Among the important results of Mr. Mushet's lifelong labours was the discovery of Titanium in the hearth of a blast furnace — the first time this element had been identified in such a setting. (026-160)

## Publications

David Mushet was the author of *Papers on Iron and Steel*, published in [[london|London]] in 1840, a work widely referenced by contemporaries and later writers on metallurgical subjects. (027-028) (006-004) (012-014)

## Influence and Legacy

> Mushet became the most skilled assayer of his day, and his labours bore their due fruits — though he himself never received any compensation commensurate with the value of his discoveries. (026-035) (026-159)

Mushet's discoveries were adopted by other ironmasters who profited greatly from them, while Mushet himself remained comparatively unrewarded. His name was brought into consideration for public recognition, but he died in 1847 without having received the honours his work merited. (026-134) (026-178)
