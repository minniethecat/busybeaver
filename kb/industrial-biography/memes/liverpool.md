# Liverpool

Liverpool was one of the principal ports of 19th-century Britain, serving as a gateway for industrial goods and railway machinery. The city's commercial importance grew dramatically with the opening of the [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|Liverpool and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway]], the world's first inter-city railway. (016-094)

## Industrial Significance

Liverpool became a centre for the distribution of iron goods and machinery produced in the manufacturing districts of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and beyond. The city's docks handled shipments of locomotives, steam engines, and structural ironwork destined for markets across the British Empire. (050-175)

> "The rapid growth of Liverpool was one of the marvels of the age, driven by the energy of its merchants and the genius of its engineers." (053-186)

## Railway Hub

The [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|Liverpool and Manchester Railway]], opened in 1830, transformed Liverpool into the world's first railway-connected port city. [[locomotive|Locomotive]]s built at the [[stephenson-works|Stephensons' [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] works]] were shipped through Liverpool, and the railway itself stimulated demand for iron rails, bridges, and rolling stock. (047-007)

## Key Figures

- [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] — his [[locomotive|locomotives]] for the Liverpool and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway were shipped via Liverpool
- [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]] — [[manchester|Manchester]] cotton spinner and early director of the Liverpool and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway (055-027)
- [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] — built [[iron-shipbuilding|iron ships]] at his Millwall yard that docked at Liverpool
