# Cranege Brothers

[[thomas-cranege|Thomas Cranege]] and George Cranege were two foremen at the Coalbrookdale ironworks who, in 1766, invented a revolutionary process for refining pig iron into [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] using a reverberatory furnace fired by raw coal, eliminating the need for wood charcoal.

## Early Life

Little is recorded of the early lives of the Cranege brothers. Thomas Cranege worked at the Bridgenorth Forge, while his brother George worked at "the Dale" — the Coalbrookdale works in [[shropshire|Shropshire]]. They rose to positions as foremen under the management of [[Richard Reynolds]]. (016-127)

## The [[reverberatory-furnace|Reverberatory Furnace]] Invention

### Background

By the mid-18th century, ironmakers had succeeded in using coal to smelt ironstone in the first stage of production. However, the second stage — refining pig iron into malleable [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] — still relied on wood charcoal in a finery. The question arose whether coal might also be used in this refining stage. (016-113, 016-117)

### The Cranege Process

The Cranege brothers proposed to [[Richard Reynolds]] that the refining could be performed in a reverberatory furnace, in which the iron would not mix with the coal but be heated solely by the flame. Reynolds was initially skeptical, but authorized them to conduct an experiment. (016-118, 016-122)

On 25 April 1766, Reynolds wrote to Thomas Goldney of [[bristol|Bristol]] describing the brothers' success:

> "It is some time since Thos. Cranege, who works at Bridgenorth Forge, and his brother George, of the Dale, spoke to me about a notion they had conceived of making bar iron without wood charcoal." (016-127, 016-128)

### The Patent

A patent was secured in the name of the brothers Cranege on 17 June 1766, with the specification adopting the very words of Reynolds's letter as the description of the process. By this method, which became known as [[puddling-process|puddling]], manufacturers could produce iron in increased quantity at a large reduction in price. (016-163, 016-166)

## Impact and Legacy

The wrought iron produced at Coalbrookdale by the Cranege process was described as "very good, quite tough, and broke with a long, bright, fibrous fracture." (016-172, 016-173)

Their invention was later improved upon by [[Peter Onions]] and subsequently by [[Henry Cort]], who combined the Cranege process with his own innovations to establish a new era in iron manufacturing. Cort was anticipated by the Craneges in dispensing with the blast, and by Onions in the puddling process itself. (016-169, 021-155, 021-157)

Despite the importance of their invention, the Cranege brothers did not rise above the rank of foremen, and the patent does not appear to have proved of much advantage to them financially. Richard Reynolds's generosity in securing the patent in their own names was noted by contemporaries. (016-176, 016-178)

## Influences and Assessment

> "There can be no doubt as to the originality and the importance of their invention." (016-170)

The Cranege brothers' innovation was a crucial stepping stone in the development of commercial iron puddling, bridging the gap between charcoal refining and the Cort process that would transform the iron industry.
