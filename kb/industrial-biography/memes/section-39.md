[039-001]The total number of machines employed in the various operations of

[039-002]making a ship's block by the new method was forty-four; and after being

[039-003]regularly employed in [[portsmouth-dockyard|[[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] Dockyard]] for upwards of fifty years,

[039-004]they are still as perfect in their action as on the day they were

[039-005]erected.  They constitute one of the most ingenious and complete

[039-006]collections of tools ever invented for making articles in wood, being

[039-007]capable of performing most of the practical operations of carpentry

[039-008]with the utmost accuracy and finish.  The machines are worked by a

[039-009][[steam-engine]] of 32-horse power, which is also used for various other

[039-010]dockyard purposes.  Under the new system of block-making it was found

[039-011]that the articles were better made, supplied with much greater

[039-012]rapidity, and executed at a greatly reduced cost.  Only ten men, with

[039-013]the new machinery, could perform the work which before had required a

[039-014]hundred and ten men to execute, and not fewer than 160,000 blocks of

[039-015]various kinds and sizes could be turned out in a year, worth not less

[039-016]than 541,000L.[14]

[039-017]The satisfactory execution of the block-machinery brought Maudslay a

[039-018]large accession of fame and business; and the premises in Margaret

[039-019]Street proving much too limited for his requirements, he again resolved

[039-020]to shift his quarters.  He found a piece of ground suitable for his

[039-021]purpose in Westminster Road, [[lambeth|Lambeth]].  Little more than a century since

[039-022]it formed part of a Marsh, the name of which is still retained in the

[039-023]adjoining street; its principal productions being bulrushes and

[039-024]willows, which were haunted in certain seasons by snipe and waterfowl.

[039-025]An enterprising riding-master had erected some premises on a part of

[039-026]the marsh, which he used for a riding-school; but the speculation not

[039-027]answering, they were sold, and [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] became the proprietor.

[039-028]Hither he removed his machinery from Margaret Street in 1810, adding

[039-029]fresh plant from time to time as it was required; and with the aid of

[039-030]his late excellent partner he built up the far-famed establishment of

[039-031]Maudslay, Field, and Co.  There he went on improving his old tools and

[039-032]inventing new ones, as the necessity for them arose, until the original

[039-033]slide-lathes used for making the block-machinery became thrown into the

[039-034]shade by the comparatively gigantic [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] of the modern school.

[039-035]Yet the original lathes are still to be found in the collection of the

[039-036]firm in Westminster Road, and continue to do their daily quota of work

[039-037]with the same precision as they did when turned out of the hands of

[039-038]their inventor and maker some sixty years ago.

[039-039]It is unnecessary that we should describe in any great detail the

[039-040]further career of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]].  The rest of his life was full of

[039-041]useful and profitable work to others as well as to himself.  His

[039-042]business embraced the making of flour and saw mills, mint machinery,

[039-043]and steam-engines of all kinds.  Before he left Margaret Street, in

[039-044]1807, he took out a patent for improvements in the steam-engine, by

[039-045]which he much simplified its parts, and secured greater directness of

[039-046]action.  His new engine was called the Pyramidal, because of its form,

[039-047]and was the first move towards what are now called Direct-acting

[039-048]Engines, in which the lateral movement of the piston is communicated by

[039-049]connecting-rods to the rotatory movement of the crank-shaft.  Mr.

[039-050]Nasmyth says of it, that "on account of its great simplicity and

[039-051]GET-AT-ABILITY of parts, its compactness and self-contained steadiness,

[039-052]this engine has been the parent of a vast progeny, all more or less

[039-053]marked by the distinguishing features of the original design, which is

[039-054]still in as high favour as ever."  Mr. Maudslay also directed his

[039-055]attention in like manner to the improvement of the marine engine, which

[039-056]he made so simple and effective as to become in a great measure the

[039-057]type of its class; and it has held its ground almost unchanged for

[039-058]nearly thirty years.  The 'Regent,' which was the first steamboat that

[039-059]plied between [[london|London]] and Margate, was fitted with engines by Maudslay

[039-060]in 1816; and it proved the forerunner of a vast number of marine

[039-061]engines, the manufacture of which soon became one of the most important

[039-062]branches of mechanical engineering.

[039-063]Another of Mr. Maudslay's inventions was his machine for punching

[039-064]boiler-plates, by which the production of ironwork of many kinds was

[039-065]greatly facilitated.  This improvement originated in the contract which

[039-066]he held for some years for supplying the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]] with iron plates

[039-067]for ships' tanks.  The operations of shearing and punching had before

[039-068]been very imperfectly done by hand, with great expenditure of labour.

[039-069]To improve the style of the work and lessen the labour, Maudslay

[039-070]invented the machine now in general use, by which the holes punched in

[039-071]the iron plate are exactly equidistant, and the subsequent operation of

[039-072]riveting is greatly facilitated.  One of the results of the improved

[039-073]method was the great saving which was at once effected in the cost of

[039-074]preparing the plates to receive the rivets, the price of which was

[039-075]reduced from seven shillings per tank to ninepence.  He continued to

[039-076]devote himself to the last to the improvement of the lathe,--in his

[039-077]opinion the master-machine, the life and soul of engine-turning, of

[039-078]which the planing, screw-cutting, and other machines in common use, are

[039-079]but modifications.  In one of the early lathes which he contrived and

[039-080]made, the mandrill was nine inches in diameter; it was driven by

[039-081]wheel-gearing like a crane motion, and adapted to different speeds.

[039-082]Some of his friends, on first looking at it, said he was going "too

[039-083]fast;" but he lived to see work projected on so large a scale as to

[039-084]prove that his conceptions were just, and that he had merely

[039-085]anticipated by a few years the mechanical progress of his time.  His

[039-086]large removable bar-lathe was a highly important tool of the same kind.

[039-087]It was used to turn surfaces many feet in diameter.  While it could be

[039-088]used for boring wheels, or the side-rods of marine engines, it could

[039-089]turn a roller or cylinder twice or three times the diameter of its own

[039-090]centres from the ground-level, and indeed could drive round work of any

[039-091]diameter that would clear the roof of the shop.  This was therefore an

[039-092]almost universal tool, capable of very extensive uses.  Indeed much of

[039-093]the work now executed by means of special tools, such as the planing or

[039-094]slotting machine, was then done in the lathe, which was used as a

[039-095][[shaping-machine|cutter-shaping machine]], fitted with various appliances according to the

[039-096]work.

[039-097]Maudslay's love of accuracy also led him from an early period to study

[039-098]the subject of improved screw-cutting.  The importance of this

[039-099]department of mechanism can scarcely be overrated, the solidity and

[039-100]permanency of most mechanical structures mainly depending on the

[039-101]employment of the screw, at the same time that the parts can be readily

[039-102]separated for renewal or repair.  Any one can form an idea of the

[039-103]importance of the screw as an element in mechanical construction by

[039-104]examining say a steam-engine, and counting the number of screws

[039-105]employed in holding it together.  Previous to the time at which the

[039-106]subject occupied the attention of our mechanic, the tools used for

[039-107]making screws were of the most rude and inexact kind.  The screws were

[039-108]for the most part cut by hand:  the small by filing, the larger by

[039-109]chipping and filing.  In consequence of the great difficulty of making

[039-110]them, as few were used as possible; and cotters, cotterils, or

[039-111]forelocks, were employed instead.  Screws, however, were to a certain

[039-112]extent indispensable; and each manufacturing establishment made them

[039-113]after their own fashion.  There was an utter want of uniformity.  No

[039-114]system was observed as to "pitch," i.e.  the number of threads to the

[039-115]inch, nor was any rule followed as to the form of those threads.  Every

[039-116]bolt and nut was sort of specialty in itself, and neither owed nor

[039-117]admitted of any community with its neighbours.  To such an extent was

[039-118]this irregularity carried, that all bolts and their corresponding nuts

[039-119]had to be marked as belonging to each other; and any mixing of them

[039-120]together led to endless trouble, hopeless confusion, and enormous

[039-121]expense.  Indeed none but those who lived in the comparatively early

[039-122]days of machine-manufacture can form an adequate idea of the annoyance

[039-123]occasioned by the want of system in this branch of detail, or duly

[039-124]appreciate the services rendered by Maudslay to mechanical engineering

[039-125]by the practical measures which he was among the first to introduce for

[039-126]its remedy.  In his system of screw-cutting machinery, his taps and

[039-127]dies, and screw-tackle generally, he laid the foundations of all that

[039-128]has since been done in this essential branch of machine-construction,

[039-129]in which he was so ably followed up by several of the eminent mechanics

[039-130]brought up in his school, and more especially by [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]] and

[039-131][[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]].  One of his earliest self-acting screw lathes, moved

[039-132]by a guide-screw and wheels after the plan followed by the latter

[039-133]engineer, cut screws of large diameter and of any required pitch.  As

[039-134]an illustration of its completeness and accuracy, we may mention that

[039-135]by its means a screw five feet in length, and two inches in diameter,

[039-136]was cut with fifty threads to the inch; the nut to fit on to it being

[039-137]twelve inches long, and containing six hundred threads.  This screw was

[039-138]principally used for dividing scales for astronomical purposes; and by

[039-139]its means divisions were produced so minute that they could not be

[039-140]detected without the aid of a magnifier.  The screw, which was sent for

[039-141]exhibition to the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]], is still carefully preserved amongst

[039-142]the specimens of Maudslay's handicraft at the [[lambeth|[[lambeth|Lambeth]] Works]], and is a

[039-143]piece of delicate work which every skilled mechanic will thoroughly

[039-144]appreciate.  Yet the tool by which this fine piece of turning was

[039-145]produced was not an exceptional tool, but was daily employed in the

[039-146]ordinary work of the manufactory.

[039-147]Like every good workman who takes pride in his craft, he kept his tools

[039-148]in first-rate order, clean, and tidily arranged, so that he could lay

[039-149]his hand upon the thing he wanted at once, without loss of time.  They

[039-150]are still preserved in the state in which he left them, and strikingly

[039-151]illustrate his love of order, "nattiness," and dexterity.  Mr. Nasmyth

[039-152]says of him that you could see the man's character in whatever work he

[039-153]turned out; and as the connoisseur in art will exclaim at sight of a

[039-154]picture, "That is Turner," or "That is Stansfield," detecting the hand

[039-155]of the master in it, so the experienced mechanician, at sight of one of

[039-156]his machines or engines, will be equally ready to exclaim, "That is

[039-157]Maudslay;" for the characteristic style of the master-mind is as clear

[039-158]to the experienced eye in the case of the finished machine as the

[039-159]touches of the artist's pencil are in the case of the finished picture.

[039-160]Every mechanical contrivance that became the subject of his study came

[039-161]forth from his hand and mind rearranged, simplified, and made new, with

[039-162]the impress of his individuality stamped upon it.  He at once stripped

[039-163]the subject of all unnecessary complications; for he possessed a

[039-164]wonderful faculty of KNOWING WHAT TO DO WITHOUT--the result of his

[039-165]clearness of insight into mechanical adaptations, and the accurate and

[039-166]well-defined notions he had formed of the precise object to be

[039-167]accomplished.  "Every member or separate machine in the system of

[039-168]block-machinery," says Mr. Nasmyth, "is full of Maudslay's presence;

[039-169]and in that machinery, as constructed by him, is to be found the parent

[039-170]of every engineering tool by the aid of which we are now achieving such

[039-171]great things in mechanical construction.  To the tools of which

[039-172]Maudslay furnished the prototypes are we mainly indebted for the

[039-173]perfection of our textile machinery, our locomotives, our marine

[039-174]engines, and the various implements of art, of agriculture, and of war.

[039-175]If any one who can enter into the details of this subject will be at

[039-176]the pains to analyse, if I may so term it, the machinery of our modern

[039-177]engineering workshops, he will find in all of them the strongly-marked

[039-178]features of Maudslay's parent machine, the slide rest and slide

[039-179]system--whether it be a planing machine, a slotting machine, a

[039-180]slide-lathe, or any other of the wonderful tools which are now enabling

[039-181]us to accomplish so much in mechanism."
