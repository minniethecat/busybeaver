[056-001]To facilitate the manufacture of his iron-sided ships, Mr. Fairbairn,

[056-002]about the year 1839, invented a machine for riveting boiler plates by

[056-003]steam-power.  The usual method by which this process had before been

[056-004]executed was by hand-hammers, worked by men placed at each side of the

[056-005]plate to be riveted, acting simultaneously on both sides of the bolt.

[056-006]But this process was tedious and expensive, as well as clumsy and

[056-007]imperfect; and some more rapid and precise method of fixing the plates

[056-008]firmly together was urgently wanted.  Mr. Fairbairn's machine

[056-009]completely supplied the want.  By its means the rivet was driven into

[056-010]its place, and firmly fastened there by a couple of strokes of a hammer

[056-011]impelled by steam.  Aided by the Jacquard punching-machine of Roberts,

[056-012]the riveting of plates of the largest size has thus become one of the

[056-013]simplest operations in iron-manufacturing.

[056-014]The thorough knowledge which Mr. Fairbairn possessed of the strength of

[056-015]wrought-iron in the form of the hollow beam (which a wrought-iron ship

[056-016]really is) naturally led to his being consulted by the late Robert

[056-017]Stephenson as to the structures by means of which it was proposed to

[056-018]span the estuary of the Conway and the Straits of Menai; and the result

[056-019]was the Conway and Britannia [[tubular-bridge|Tubular Bridge]]s, the history of which we

[056-020]have fully described elsewhere.[7]  There is no reason to doubt that by

[056-021]far the largest share of the merit of working out the practical details

[056-022]of those structures, and thus realizing [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]]'s magnificent

[056-023]idea of the tubular bridge, belongs to Mr. Fairbairn.

[056-024]In all matters connected with the qualities and strength of iron, he

[056-025]came to be regarded as a first-rate authority, and his advice was often

[056-026]sought and highly valued.  The elaborate experiments instituted by him

[056-027]as to the strength of iron of all kinds have formed the subject of

[056-028]various papers which he has read before the [[british-association|British Association]], the

[056-029][[royal-society|Royal Society]], and the Literary and Philosophical Society of

[056-030][[manchester|Manchester]].  His practical inquiries as to the strength of boilers have

[056-031]led to his being frequently called upon to investigate the causes of

[056-032]boiler explosions, on which subject he has published many elaborate

[056-033]reports.  The study of this subject led him to elucidate the law

[056-034]according to which the density of steam varies throughout an extensive

[056-035]range of pressures and atmospheres,--in singular confirmation of what

[056-036]had before been provisionally calculated from the mechanical theory of

[056-037]heat.  His discovery of the true method of preventing the tendency of

[056-038]tubes to collapse, by dividing the flues of long boilers into short

[056-039]lengths by means of stiffening rings, arising out of the same

[056-040]investigation, was one of the valuable results of his minute study of

[056-041]the subject; and is calculated to be of essential value in the

[056-042]manufacturing districts by diminishing the chances of boiler

[056-043]explosions, and saving the lamentable loss of life which has during the

[056-044]last twenty years been occasioned by the malconstruction of boilers.

[056-045]Among Mr. Fairbairn's most recent, inquiries are those conducted by him

[056-046]at the instance of the British Government relative to the construction

[056-047]of iron-plated ships, his report of which has not yet been made public,

[056-048]most probably for weighty political reasons.

[056-049]We might also refer to the practical improvements which Mr. Fairbairn

[056-050]has been instrumental in introducing in the construction of buildings

[056-051]of various kinds by the use of iron.  He has himself erected numerous

[056-052]iron structures, and pointed out the road which other manufacturers

[056-053]have readily followed.  "I am one of those," said he, in his 'Lecture

[056-054]on the Progress of Engineering,' "who have great faith in iron walls

[056-055]and iron beams; and although I have both spoken and written much on the

[056-056]subject, I cannot too forcibly recommend it to public attention.  It is

[056-057]now twenty years since I constructed an iron house, with the machinery

[056-058]of a corn-mill, for Halil Pasha, then Seraskier of the Turkish army at

[056-059]Constantinople.  I believe it was the first iron house built in this

[056-060]country; and it was constructed at the works at Millwall, [[london|London]], in

[056-061]1839." [8]

[056-062]Since then iron structures of all kinds have been erected:  iron

[056-063]lighthouses, iron-and-crystal palaces, iron churches, and iron bridges.

[056-064]Iron roads have long been worked by iron locomotives; and before many

[056-065]years have passed a telegraph of iron wire will probably be found

[056-066]circling the globe.  We now use iron roofs, iron bedsteads, iron ropes,

[056-067]and iron pavement; and even the famous "wooden walls of [[england|England]]" are

[056-068]rapidly becoming reconstructed of iron.  In short, we are in the midst

[056-069]of what Mr. Worsaae has characterized as the [[industrial-revolution|Age of Iron]].

[056-070]At the celebration of the opening of the North Wales Railway at Bangor,

[056-071]almost within sight of his iron bridge across the Straits of Menai,

[056-072][[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] said, "We are daily producing from the bowels of the

[056-073]earth a raw material, in its crude state apparently of no worth, but

[056-074]which, when converted into a [[locomotive|locomotive engine]], flies over bridges of

[056-075]the same material, with a speed exceeding that of the bird, advancing

[056-076]wealth and comfort throughout the country.  Such are the powers of that

[056-077]all-civilizing instrument, Iron."

[056-078]Iron indeed plays a highly important part in modern civilization.  Out

[056-079]of it are formed alike the sword and the ploughshare, the cannon and

[056-080]the printing-press; and while civilization continues partial and

[056-081]half-developed, as it still is, our liberties and our industry must

[056-082]necessarily in a great measure depend for their protection upon the

[056-083]excellence of our weapons of war as well as on the superiority of our

[056-084]instruments of peace.  Hence the skill and ingenuity displayed in the

[056-085]invention of rifled guns and artillery, and iron-sided ships and

[056-086]batteries, the fabrication of which would be impossible but for the

[056-087]extraordinary development of the iron-manufacture, and the marvellous

[056-088]power and precision of our tool-making machines, as described in

[056-089]preceding chapters.

[056-090]"Our strength, wealth, and commerce," said Mr. Cobden in the course of

[056-091]a recent debate in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]], "grow out of the skilled

[056-092]labour of the men working in metals.  They are at the foundation of our

[056-093]manufacturing greatness; and in case you were attacked, they would at

[056-094]once be available, with their hard hands and skilled brains, to

[056-095]manufacture your muskets and your cannon, your shot and your shell.

[056-096]What has given us our Armstrongs, Whitworths, and Fairbairns, but the

[056-097]free industry of this country? If you can build three times more

[056-098]steam-engines than any other country, and have threefold the force of

[056-099]mechanics, to whom and to what do you owe that, but to the men who have

[056-100]trained them, and to those principles of commerce out of which the

[056-101]wealth of the country has grown? We who have some hand in doing that,

[056-102]are not ignorant that we have been and are increasing the strength of

[056-103]the country in proportion as we are raising up skilled artisans." [9]

[056-104]The reader who has followed us up to this point will have observed that

[056-105]handicraft labour was the first stage of the development of human

[056-106]power, and that machinery has been its last and highest.  The

[056-107]uncivilized man began with a stone for a hammer, and a splinter of

[056-108]flint for a chisel, each stage of his progress being marked by an

[056-109]improvement in his tools.  Every machine calculated to save labour or

[056-110]increase production was a substantial addition to his power over the

[056-111]material resources of nature, enabling him to subjugate them more

[056-112]effectually to his wants and uses; and every extension of machinery has

[056-113]served to introduce new classes of the population to the enjoyment of

[056-114]its benefits.  In early times the products of skilled industry were for

[056-115]the most part luxuries intended for the few, whereas now the most

[056-116]exquisite tools and engines are employed in producing articles of

[056-117]ordinary consumption for the great mass of the community.  Machines

[056-118]with millions of fingers work for millions of purchasers--for the poor

[056-119]as well as the rich; and while the machinery thus used enriches its

[056-120]owners, it no less enriches the public with its products.

[056-121]Much of the progress to which we have adverted has been the result of

[056-122]the skill and industry of our own time.  "Indeed," says Mr. Fairbairn,

[056-123]"the mechanical operations of the present day could not have been

[056-124]accomplished at any cost thirty years ago; and what was then considered

[056-125]impossible is now performed with an exactitude that never fails to

[056-126]accomplish the end in view."  For this we are mainly indebted to the

[056-127]almost creative power of modern [[machine-tool|machine-tools]], and the facilities which

[056-128]they present for the production and reproduction of other machines.  We

[056-129]also owe much to the mechanical agencies employed to drive them.  Early

[056-130]inventors yoked wind and water to sails and wheels, and made them work

[056-131]machinery of various kinds; but modern inventors have availed

[056-132]themselves of the far more swift and powerful, yet docile force of

[056-133]steam, which has now laid upon it the heaviest share of the burden of

[056-134]toil, and indeed become the universal drudge.  Coal, water, and a

[056-135]little oil, are all that the [[steam-engine]], with its bowels of iron and

[056-136]heart of fire, needs to enable it to go on working night and day,

[056-137]without rest or sleep.  Yoked to machinery of almost infinite variety,

[056-138]the results of vast ingenuity and labour, the Steam-engine pumps water,

[056-139]drives spindles, thrashes corn, prints books, hammers iron, ploughs

[056-140]land, saws timber, drives piles, impels ships, works railways,

[056-141]excavates docks; and, in a word, asserts an almost unbounded supremacy

[056-142]over the materials which enter into the daily use of mankind, for

[056-143]clothing, for labour, for defence, for household purposes, for

[056-144]locomotion, for food, or for instruction.

[056-145][1] Long after, when married and settled at [[manchester|Manchester]], the fiddle,

[056-146]which had been carefully preserved, was taken down from the shelf for

[056-147]the amusement of the children; but though they were well enough pleased

[056-148]with it, the instrument was never brought from its place without

[056-149]creating alarm in the mind of their mother lest anybody should hear it.

[056-150]At length a dancing-master, who was giving lessons in the

[056-151]neighbourhood, borrowed the fiddle, and, to the great relief of the

[056-152]family, it was never returned.  Many years later Mr. Fairbairn was

[056-153]present at the starting of a cotton mill at Wesserling in Alsace

[056-154]belonging to Messrs. Gros, Deval, and Co., for which his Manchester

[056-155]firm had provided the mill-work and water-wheel (the first erected in

[056-156]France on the suspension principle, when the event was followed by an

[056-157]entertainment).  During dinner Mr. Fairbairn had been explaining to M.

[056-158]Gros, who spoke a little English, the nature of home-brewed beer, which

[056-159]he much admired, having tasted it when in [[england|England]].  The dinner was

[056-160]followed by music, in the performance of which the host himself took

[056-161]part; and on Mr. Fairbairn's admiring his execution on the violin, M.

[056-162]Gros asked him if he played.  "A little," was the almost unconscious

[056-163]reply.  "Then you must have the goodness to play some," and the

[056-164]instrument was in a moment placed in his hands, amidst urgent requests

[056-165]from all sides that he should play.  There was no alternative; so he

[056-166]proceeded to perform one of his best tunes--"The Keel Row."  The

[056-167]company listened with amazement, until the performer's career was

[056-168]suddenly cut short by the host exclaiming at the top of his voice,

[056-169]"Stop, stop, Monsieur, by gar that be HOME-BREWED MUSIC!"

[056-170][2] "Although not a native of [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]]," he then said, "he owed almost

[056-171]everything to [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]].  He got the rudiments of his education there,

[056-172]such as it was; and that was (something like that of his revered

[056-173]predecessor [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]]) at a [[coal-mining|colliery]].  He was brought up as an

[056-174]engineer at the Percy Main Colliery.  He was there seven years; and if

[056-175]it had not been for the opportunities he then enjoyed, together with

[056-176]the use of the library at North Shields, he believed he would not have

[056-177]been there to address them.  Being self-taught, but with some little

[056-178]ambition, and a determination to improve himself, he was now enabled to

[056-179]stand before them with some pretensions to mechanical knowledge, and

[056-180]the persuasion that he had been a useful contributor to practical

[056-181]science and objects connected with mechanical engineering."--Meeting of

[056-182]the Institute of Mechanical Engineers at Newcastle-on-[[river-tyne|Tyne]], 1858.
