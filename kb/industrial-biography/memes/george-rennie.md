George Rennie was a celebrated mechanical engineer and millwright based in [[london|London]] during the early nineteenth century. His engineering works were situated at the south end of [[Blackfriars Bridge]] in [[london|London]], and he was among the prominent mechanical engineers of his era alongside figures such as [[matthew-murray|Matthew Murray]] of [[leeds|Leeds]] and [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]] of [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]]. (031-155)

## Early Career and Engineering Works

Rennie established himself as a leading millwright and engineer in [[london|London]]. He was associated with the design of the bridge at Kelso, built after his plans. (052-141) His firm became a notable training ground for young engineers: he employed both [[William Fairbairn]] and an associate who came to [[london|London]] seeking work as millwrights.

When Fairbairn and his companion arrived at Rennie's works at [[blackfriars-bridge|Blackfriars Bridge]], the celebrated engineer sent them to his foreman with instructions to set them to work. (053-076) The shop, however, was filled with trade union men who sought to exclude outside workers, regardless of their skill. (053-078)

## Association with William Fairbairn

Before engaging with Rennie's [[london|London]] works, Fairbairn had worked on the construction of the Kelso bridge, which had been designed by Rennie. While helping to carry a handbarrow-load of stone at Kelso, Fairbairn's strength proved insufficient and a stone fell upon him, inflicting a serious wound that left him crippled for months. (052-141)

## Scholarly Contributions

Rennie contributed to the technical literature of his field. He wrote the preface to Buchanan's *Practical Essays on Millwork and Other Machinery*, a significant engineering text of the period. (041-071) This places Rennie among the engineers who sought to codify and disseminate mechanical knowledge during the early [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Mechanical Invention Claims

Rennie was one of several engineers cited as claimants in the development of important mechanical inventions of the era. [[Samuel Smiles]] notes that most mechanical inventions were "of a very composite character" and were advanced by a long succession of contributors, listing George Rennie of [[london|London]] alongside Matthew Murray of [[leeds|Leeds]], Mr. Spring of [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]], and [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]]. (031-155)
