# James Watt

James Watt was an instrument-maker at [[glasgow|Glasgow]] University when he conceived the separate condenser in 1765. His partnership with [[matthew-boulton|Matthew Boulton]] at the [[soho-manufactory|Soho Manufactory]] created the first commercial steam engine works. Watt's engines powered the factories, mines, and mills of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. (022-046)

## Early Life and Career

James Watt was born in 1736 at [[greenock|Greenock]] on the [[clyde|Clyde]], son of a shipwright and instrument maker. He trained in [[london|London]] as a mathematical instrument maker, then returned to [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1757 as instrument maker to the university. The post gave him workshop space and contact with Scottish Enlightenment figures including [[joseph-black|Joseph Black]].

In 1763, the university sent Watt a model [[newcomen-engine|Newcomen engine]] for repair. Studying it, Watt found that most heat was wasted reheating the cylinder after each injection of cold water. This led him to the separate condenser: condense steam in a separate cold vessel so the main cylinder stays hot. He confirmed the principle in 1765. (022-048)

The path from concept to working engine took more than a decade. Watt lacked capital for large experiments and spent years as a canal surveyor. In 1775 he formed his partnership with [[matthew-boulton|Matthew Boulton]], whose [[soho-manufactory|Soho Manufactory]] had the skilled workmen and resources needed. The first practical [[steam-engine|Watt engine]]s appeared in 1776.

## Major Contributions

The separate condenser was Watt's master invention, reducing fuel consumption by 75%. A [[newcomen-engine|Newcomen engine]] burning 15 pounds of coal per horsepower-hour could be replaced with a Watt engine burning 4 pounds. This saving made steam power economical in locations distant from coal mines.

His double-acting engine applied steam pressure to both sides of the piston, doubling power without increasing size. The parallel motion linkage converted vertical piston motion to rotary beam motion while keeping the piston rod perfectly straight — a problem that had defeated earlier designs.

The sun-and-planet gear converted reciprocating motion to rotary motion, enabling steam engines to drive mill machinery directly. The centrifugal governor, adapted from mill machinery, automatically regulated engine speed via a steam throttle. This was the first practical feedback control mechanism in mechanical engineering.

## Legacy

Watt's partnership with Boulton at [[soho-manufactory|Soho]] was the model of industrial collaboration: Boulton provided business acumen; Watt provided engineering genius. By 1800, when Watt's patent expired, over 500 Boulton & Watt engines were at work across Britain.

Watt retired in 1800, handing the firm to his sons. He died in 1819 at [[heathfield-hall|Heathfield Hall]] near [[birmingham|Birmingham]]. The unit of power — the watt — was named in his honour. His place in industrial history is unique: the steam engine was the prime mover of the Industrial Revolution, and Watt's improvements made it universal.
