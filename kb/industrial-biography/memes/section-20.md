[020-001]The process of making cast-steel, as invented by [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]], may

[020-002]be thus summarily described.  The melting is conducted in clay pots or

[020-003]crucibles manufactured for the purpose, capable of holding about 34

[020-004]lbs. each.  Ten or twelve of such crucibles are placed in a

[020-005]melting-furnace similar to that used by brass founders; and when the

[020-006]furnace and pots are at a white heat, to which they are raised by a

[020-007]coke fire, they are charged with bar steel reduced to a certain degree

[020-008]of hardness, and broken into pieces of about a pound each.  When the

[020-009]pots are all thus charged with steel, lids are placed over them, the

[020-010]furnace is filled with coke, and the cover put down.  Under the intense

[020-011]heat to which the metal is exposed, it undergoes an apparent

[020-012]ebullition.  When the furnace requires feeding, the workmen take the

[020-013]opportunity of lifting the lid of each crucible and judging how far the

[020-014]process has advanced.  After about three hours' exposure to the heat,

[020-015]the metal is ready for "teeming."  The completion of the melting

[020-016]process is known by the subsidence of all ebullition, and by the clear

[020-017]surface of the melted metal, which is of a dazzling brilliancy like the

[020-018]sun when looked at with the naked eye on a clear day.  The pots are

[020-019]then lifted out of their place, and the liquid steel is poured into

[020-020]ingots of the shape and size required.  The pots are replaced, filled

[020-021]again, and the process is repeated; the red-hot pots thus serving for

[020-022]three successive charges, after which they are rejected as useless.

[020-023]When Huntsman had perfected his invention, it would naturally occur to

[020-024]him that the new metal might be employed for other purposes besides

[020-025]clock-springs and pendulums.  The business of clock-making was then of

[020-026]a very limited character, and it could scarcely have been worth his

[020-027]while to pursue so extensive and costly a series of experiments merely

[020-028]to supply the requirements of that trade.  It is more probable that at

[020-029]an early stage of his investigations he shrewdly foresaw the extensive

[020-030]uses to which cast-steel might be applied in the manufacture of tools

[020-031]and cutlery of a superior kind; and we accordingly find him early

[020-032]endeavouring to persuade the manufacturers of [[sheffield|Sheffield]] to employ it in

[020-033]the manufacture of knives and razors.  But the cutlers obstinately

[020-034]refused to work a material so much harder than that which they had been

[020-035]accustomed to use; and for a time he gave up all hopes of creating a

[020-036]demand in that quarter.  Foiled in his endeavours to sell his steel at

[020-037]home, Huntsman turned his attention to foreign markets; and he soon

[020-038]found he could readily sell abroad all that he could make.  The merit

[020-039]of employing cast-steel for general purposes belongs to the French,

[020-040]always so quick to appreciate the advantages of any new discovery, and

[020-041]for a time the whole of the cast-steel that Huntsman could manufacture

[020-042]was exported to France.  When he had fairly established his business

[020-043]with that country, the [[sheffield|Sheffield]] cutlers became alarmed at the

[020-044]reputation which cast-steel was acquiring abroad; and when they heard

[020-045]of the preference displayed by English as well as French consumers for

[020-046]the cutlery manufactured of that metal, they readily apprehended the

[020-047]serious consequences that must necessarily result to their own trade if

[020-048]cast-steel came into general use.  They then appointed a deputation to

[020-049]wait upon Sir George Savile, one of the members for the county of York,

[020-050]and requested him to use his influence with the government to obtain an

[020-051]order to prohibit the exportation of cast-steel.  But on learning from

[020-052]the deputation that the Sheffield manufacturers themselves would not

[020-053]make use of the new steel, he positively declined to comply with their

[020-054]request.  It was indeed fortunate for the interests of the town that

[020-055]the object of the deputation was defeated, for at that time Mr.

[020-056]Huntsman had very pressing and favourable offers from some spirited

[020-057]manufacturers in [[birmingham|Birmingham]] to remove his furnaces to that place; and

[020-058]it is extremely probable that had the business of cast-steel making

[020-059]become established there, one of the most important and lucrative

[020-060]branches of its trade would have been lost to the town of Sheffield.

[020-061]The Sheffield makers were therefore under the necessity of using the

[020-062]cast-steel, if they would retain their trade in cutlery against France;

[020-063]and Huntsman's home trade rapidly increased.  And then began the

[020-064]efforts of the Sheffield men to wrest his secret from him.  For

[020-065]Huntsman had not taken out any patent for his invention, his only

[020-066]protection being in preserving his process as much a mystery as

[020-067]possible.  All the workmen employed by him were pledged to inviolable

[020-068]secrecy; strangers were carefully excluded from the works; and the

[020-069]whole of the steel made was melted during the night.  There were many

[020-070]speculations abroad as to Huntsman's process.  It was generally

[020-071]believed that his secret consisted in the flux which he employed to

[020-072]make the metal melt more readily; and it leaked out amongst the workmen

[020-073]that he used broken bottles for the purpose.  Some of the

[020-074]manufacturers, who by prying and bribing got an inkling of the process,

[020-075]followed Huntsman implicitly in this respect; and they would not allow

[020-076]their own workmen to flux the pots lest they also should obtain

[020-077]possession of the secret.  But it turned out eventually that no such

[020-078]flux was necessary, and the practice has long since been discontinued.

[020-079]A Frenchman named Jars, frequently quoted by Le Play in his account of

[020-080]the manufacture of steel in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]],[6] paid a visit to Sheffield

[020-081]towards the end of last century, and described the process so far as he

[020-082]was permitted to examine it.  According to his statement all kinds of

[020-083]fragments of broken steel were used; but this is corrected by Le Play,

[020-084]who states that only the best bar steel manufactured of Dannemora iron

[020-085]was employed.  Jars adds that "the steel is put into the crucible with

[020-086]A FLUX, the composition of which is kept secret;" and he states that

[020-087]the time then occupied in the conversion was five hours.

[020-088]It is said that the person who first succeeded in copying Huntsman's

[020-089]process was an ironfounder named Walker, who carried on his business at

[020-090]Greenside near Sheffield, and it was certainly there that the making of

[020-091]cast-steel was next begun.  Walker adopted the "ruse" of disguising

[020-092]himself as a tramp, and, feigning great distress and abject poverty, he

[020-093]appeared shivering at the door of Huntsman's foundry late one night

[020-094]when the workmen were about to begin their labours at steel-casting,

[020-095]and asked for admission to warm himself by the furnace fire.  The

[020-096]workmen's hearts were moved, and they permitted him to enter.  We have

[020-097]the above facts from the descendants of the Huntsman family; but we add

[020-098]the traditional story preserved in the neighbourhood, as given in a

[020-099]well-known book on metallurgy:--

[020-100]"One cold winter's night, while the snow was falling in heavy flakes,

[020-101]and the manufactory threw its red glared light over the neighbourhood,

[020-102]a person of the most abject appearance presented himself at the

[020-103]entrance, praying for permission to share the warmth and shelter which

[020-104]it afforded.  The humane workmen found the appeal irresistible, and the

[020-105]apparent beggar was permitted to take up his quarters in a warm corner

[020-106]of the building.  A careful scrutiny would have discovered little real

[020-107]sleep in the drowsiness which seemed to overtake the stranger; for he

[020-108]eagerly watched every movement of the workmen while they went through

[020-109]the operations of the newly discovered process.  He observed, first of

[020-110]all, that bars of blistered steel were broken into small pieces, two or

[020-111]three inches in length, and placed in crucibles of fire clay.  When

[020-112]nearly full, a little green glass broken into small fragments was

[020-113]spread over the top, and the whole covered over with a closely-fitting

[020-114]cover.  The crucibles were then placed in a furnace previously prepared

[020-115]for them, and after a lapse of from three to four hours, during which

[020-116]the crucibles were examined from time to time to see that the metal was

[020-117]thoroughly melted and incorporated, the workmen proceeded to lift the

[020-118]crucible from its place on the furnace by means of tongs, and its

[020-119]molten contents, blazing, sparkling, and spurting, were poured into a

[020-120]mould of cast-iron previously prepared:  here it was suffered to cool,

[020-121]while the crucibles were again filled, and the process repeated.  When

[020-122]cool, the mould was unscrewed, and a bar of cast-steel presented

[020-123]itself, which only required the aid of the hammerman to form a finished

[020-124]bar of cast-steel.  How the unauthorized spectator of these operations

[020-125]effected his escape without detection tradition does not say; but it

[020-126]tells us that, before many months had passed, the Huntsman manufactory

[020-127]was not the only one where cast-steel was produced." [7]

[020-128]However the facts may be, the discovery of the elder Huntsman proved of

[020-129]the greatest advantage to Sheffield; for there is scarcely a civilized

[020-130]country where Sheffield steel is not largely used, either in its most

[020-131]highly finished forms of cutlery, or as the raw material for some home

[020-132]manufacture.  In the mean time the demand for Huntsman's steel steadily

[020-133]increased, and in 1770, for the purpose of obtaining greater scope for

[020-134]his operations, he removed to a large new manufactory which he erected

[020-135]at [[attercliffe|Attercliffe]], a little to the north of Sheffield, more conveniently

[020-136]situated for business purposes.  There he continued to flourish for six

[020-137]years more, making steel and practising benevolence; for, like the

[020-138]Darbys and Reynoldses of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], he was a worthy and highly

[020-139]respected member of the Society of Friends.  He was well versed in the

[020-140]science of his day, and skilled in chemistry, which doubtless proved of

[020-141]great advantage to him in pursuing his experiments in metallurgy.[8]

[020-142]That he was possessed of great perseverance will be obvious from the

[020-143]difficulties he encountered and overcame in perfecting his valuable

[020-144]invention.  He was, however, like many persons of strong original

[020-145]character, eccentric in his habits and reserved in his manner.  The

[020-146][[royal-society|Royal Society]] wished to enrol him as a member in acknowledgment of the

[020-147]high merit of his discovery of cast-steel, as well as because of his

[020-148]skill in practical chemistry; but as this would have drawn him in some

[020-149]measure from his seclusion, and was also, as he imagined, opposed to

[020-150]the principles of the Society to which he belonged, he declined the

[020-151]honour.  Mr. Huntsman died in 1776, in his seventy-second year, and was

[020-152]buried in the churchyard at [[attercliffe|Attercliffe]], where a gravestone with an

[020-153]inscription marks his resting-place.

[020-154]His son continued to carry on the business, and largely extended its

[020-155]operations.  The Huntsman mark became known throughout the civilised

[020-156]world.  Le Play the French Professor of Metallurgy, in his Memoire of

[020-157]1846, still speaks of the cast-steel bearing the mark of "Huntsman and

[020-158]Marshall" as the best that is made, and he adds, "the buyer of this

[020-159]article, who pays a higher price for it than for other sorts, is not

[020-160]acting merely in the blind spirit of routine, but pays a logical and

[020-161]well-deserved homage to all the material and moral qualities of which

[020-162]the true Huntsman mark has been the guarantee for a century." [9]

[020-163]Many other large firms now compete for their share of the trade; and

[020-164]the extent to which it has grown, the number of furnaces constantly at

[020-165]work, and the quantity of steel cast into ingots, to be tilted or

[020-166]rolled for the various purposes to which it is applied, have rendered

[020-167]Sheffield the greatest laboratory in the world of this valuable

[020-168]material.  Of the total quantity of cast-steel manufactured in [[england|England]],

[020-169]not less than five-sixths are produced there; and the facilities for

[020-170]experiment and adaptation on the spot have enabled the Sheffield

[020-171]steel-makers to keep the lead in the manufacture, and surpass all

[020-172]others in the perfection to which they have carried this important

[020-173]branch of our national industry.  It is indeed a remarkable fact that

[020-174]this very town, which was formerly indebted to Styria for the steel

[020-175]used in its manufactures, now exports a material of its own conversion

[020-176]to the Austrian forges and other places on the Continent from which it

[020-177]was before accustomed to draw its own supplies.
