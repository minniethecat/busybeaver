[049-001]"On the death of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] in 1831, I passed over to the service

[049-002]of his worthy partner, Mr. [[joshua-field|Joshua Field]], and acted as his draughtsman,

[049-003]much to my advantage, until the end of that year, when I returned to

[049-004][[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], to construct a small stock of engineering tools for the

[049-005]purpose of enabling me to start in business on my own account.  This

[049-006]occupied me until the spring of 1833, and during the interval I was

[049-007]accustomed to take in jobs to execute in my little workshop in

[049-008][[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], so as to obtain the means of completing my stock of

[049-009]tools.[2]  In June, 1834, I went to [[manchester|Manchester]], and took a flat of an

[049-010]old mill in Dale Street, where I began business.  In two years my stock

[049-011]had so increased as to overload the floor of the old building to such

[049-012]an extent that the land lord, Mr. Wrenn, became alarmed, especially as

[049-013]the tenant below me--a glass-cutter--had a visit from the end of a

[049-014]20-horse engine beam one morning among his cut tumblers.  To set their

[049-015]anxiety at rest, I went out that evening to Patricroft and took a look

[049-016]at a rather choice bit of land bounded on one side by the canal, and on

[049-017]the other by the [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|[[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway]].  By the end of the

[049-018]week I had secured a lease of the site for 999 years; by the end of the

[049-019]month my wood sheds were erected; the ring of the hammer on the smith's

[049-020]anvil was soon heard all over the place; and the [[bridgewater-foundry|Bridgewater Foundry]]

[049-021]was fairly under way.  There I toiled right heartily until December

[049-022]31st, 1856, when I retired to enjoy in active leisure the reward of a

[049-023]laborious life, during which, with the blessing of God, I enjoyed much

[049-024]true happiness through the hearty love which I always had for my

[049-025]profession; and I trust I may be allowed to say, without undue vanity,

[049-026]that I have left behind me some useful results of my labours in those

[049-027]inventions with which my name is identified, which have had no small

[049-028]share in the accomplishment of some of the greatest mechanical works of

[049-029]our age."  If Mr. Nasmyth had accomplished nothing more than the

[049-030]invention of his steam-hammer, it would have been enough to found a

[049-031]reputation.  Professor Tomlinson describes it as "one of the most

[049-032]perfect of artificial machines and noblest triumphs of mind over matter

[049-033]that modern English engineers have yet developed." [3]

[049-034]The hand-hammer has always been an important tool, and, in the form of

[049-035]the stone celt, it was perhaps the first invented.  When the hammer of

[049-036]iron superseded that of stone, it was found practicable in the hands of

[049-037]a "cunning" workman to execute by its means metal work of great beauty

[049-038]and even delicacy.  But since the invention of cast-iron, and the

[049-039]manufacture of wrought-iron in large masses, the art of hammer-working

[049-040]has almost become lost; and great artists, such as Matsys of Antwerp

[049-041]and Rukers of Nuremberg were,[4] no longer think it worth their while

[049-042]to expend time and skill in working on so humble a material as

[049-043]wrought-iron.  It is evident from the marks of care and elaborate

[049-044]design which many of these early works exhibit, that the workman's

[049-045]heart was in his work, and that his object was not merely to get it out

[049-046]of hand, but to execute it in first-rate artistic style.

[049-047]When the use of iron extended and larger ironwork came to be forged,

[049-048]for cannon, tools, and machinery, the ordinary hand-hammer was found

[049-049]insufficient, and the helve or forge-hammer was invented.  This was

[049-050]usually driven by a water-wheel, or by oxen or horses.  The [[tilt-hammer]]

[049-051]was another form in which it was used, the smaller kinds being worked

[049-052]by the foot.  Among Watt's various inventions, was a tilt-hammer of

[049-053]considerable power, which he at first worked by means of a water-wheel,

[049-054]and afterwards by a steam engine regulated by a fly-wheel.  His first

[049-055]hammer of this kind was 120 lbs. in weight; it was raised eight inches

[049-056]before making each blow.  Watt afterwards made a tilt-hammer for Mr.

[049-057]Wilkinson of Bradley Forge, of 7 1/2 cwt., and it made 300 blows a

[049-058]minute.  Other improvements were made in the hammer from time to time,

[049-059]but no material alteration was made in the power by which it was worked

[049-060]until Mr. Nasmyth took it in hand, and applying to it the force of

[049-061]steam, at once provided the worker in iron with the most formidable of

[049-062][[machine-tool|machine-tools]].  This important invention originated as follows:

[049-063]In the early part of 1837, the directors of the Great Western

[049-064]Steam-Ship Company sent Mr. Francis Humphries, their engineer, to

[049-065]consult Mr. Nasmyth as to some engineering tools of unusual size and

[049-066]power, which were required for the construction of the engines of the

[049-067]"Great Britain" steamship.  They had determined to construct those

[049-068]engines on the vertical trunk-engine principle, in accordance with Mr.

[049-069]Humphries' designs; and very complete works were erected by them at

[049-070]their [[bristol|Bristol]] dockyard for the execution of the requisite machinery,

[049-071]the most important of the tools being supplied by Nasmyth and Gaskell.

[049-072]The engines were in hand, when a difficulty arose with respect to the

[049-073]enormous paddle-shaft of the vessel, which was of such a size of

[049-074]forging as had never before been executed.  Mr. Humphries applied to

[049-075]the largest engineering firms throughout the country for tenders of the

[049-076]price at which they would execute this part of the work, but to his

[049-077]surprise and dismay he found that not one of the firms he applied to

[049-078]would undertake so large a forging.  In this dilemma he wrote to Mr.

[049-079]Nasmyth on the 24th November,1838, informing him of this unlooked-for

[049-080]difficulty.  "I find," said he, "there is not a forge-hammer in [[england|England]]

[049-081]or [[scotland|Scotland]] powerful enough to forge the paddle-shaft of the engines

[049-082]for the 'Great Britain!' What am I to do?  Do you think I might dare to

[049-083]use cast-iron?"

[049-084]This letter immediately set Mr. Nasmyth a-thinking.  How was it that

[049-085]existing hammers were incapable of forging a wrought-iron shaft of

[049-086]thirty inches diameter? Simply because of their want of compass, or

[049-087]range and fall, as well as power of blow.  A few moments' rapid thought

[049-088]satisfied him that it was by rigidly adhering to the old traditional

[049-089]form of hand-hammer--of which the tilt, though driven by steam, was but

[049-090]a modification--that the difficulty had arisen.  When even the largest

[049-091]hammer was tilted up to its full height, its range was so small, that

[049-092]when a piece of work of considerable size was placed on the anvil, the

[049-093]hammer became "gagged," and, on such an occasion, where the forging

[049-094]required the most powerful blow, it received next to no blow at

[049-095]all,--the clear space for fall being almost entirely occupied by the

[049-096]work on the anvil.

[049-097]The obvious remedy was to invent some method, by which a block of iron

[049-098]should be lifted to a sufficient height above the object on which it

[049-099]was desired to strike a blow, and let the block fall down upon the

[049-100]work,--guiding it in its descent by such simple means as should give

[049-101]the required precision in the percussive action of the falling mass.

[049-102]Following out this idea, Mr. Nasmyth at once sketched on paper his

[049-103]steam-hammer, having it clearly before him in his mind's eye a few

[049-104]minutes after receiving Mr. Humphries' letter narrating his

[049-105]unlooked-for difficulty.  The hammer, as thus sketched, consisted of,

[049-106]first an anvil on which to rest the work; second, a block of iron

[049-107]constituting the hammer or blow-giving part; third, an inverted

[049-108]steam-cylinder to whose piston-rod the block was attached.  All that

[049-109]was then required to produce by such means a most effective hammer, was

[049-110]simply to admit steam in the cylinder so as to act on the under side of

[049-111]the piston, and so raise the block attached to the piston-rod, and by a

[049-112]simple contrivance to let the steam escape and so permit the block

[049-113]rapidly to descend by its own gravity upon the work then on the anvil.

[049-114]Such, in a few words, is the rationale of the steam-hammer.

[049-115]By the same day's post, Mr. Nasmyth wrote to Mr. Humphries, inclosing a

[049-116]sketch of the invention by which he proposed to forge the "Great

[049-117]Britain" paddle-shaft.  Mr. Humphries showed it to Mr. Brunel, the

[049-118]engineer-inchief of the company, to Mr. Guppy, the managing director,

[049-119]and to others interested in the undertaking, by all of whom it was

[049-120]heartily approved.  Mr. Nasmyth gave permission to communicate his

[049-121]plans to such forge proprietors as might feel disposed to erect such a

[049-122]hammer to execute the proposed work,--the only condition which he made

[049-123]being, that in the event of his hammer being adopted, he was to be

[049-124]allowed to supply it according to his own design.

[049-125]The paddle-shaft of the "Great Britain" was, however, never forged.

[049-126][[About]] that time, the substitution of the Screw for the Paddle-wheel as

[049-127]a means of propulsion of steam-vessels was attracting much attention;

[049-128]and the performances of the "Archimedes" were so successful as to

[049-129]induce Mr. Brunel to recommend his Directors to adopt the new power.

[049-130]They yielded to his entreaty.  The great engines which Mr. Humphries

[049-131]had designed were accordingly set aside; and he was required to produce

[049-132]fresh designs of engines suited for screw propulsion.  The result was

[049-133]fatal to Mr. Humphries.  The labour, the anxiety, and perhaps the

[049-134]disappointment, proved too much for him, and a brain-fever carried him

[049-135]off; so that neither his great paddle-shaft nor Mr. Nasmyth's

[049-136]steam-hammer to forge it was any longer needed.

[049-137]The hammer was left to bide its time.  No forge-master would take it

[049-138]up.  The inventor wrote to all the great firms, urging its superiority

[049-139]to every other tool for working [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] into all kinds of forge

[049-140]work.  Thus he wrote and sent illustrative sketches of his hammer to

[049-141]Accramans and Morgan of [[bristol|Bristol]], to the late Benjamin Hick and Rushton

[049-142]and Eckersley of [[bolton|Bolton]], to Howard and Ravenhill of Rotherhithe, and

[049-143]other firms; but unhappily bad times for the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] had set in; and

[049-144]although all to whom he communicated his design were much struck with

[049-145]its simplicity and obvious advantages, the answer usually given

[049-146]was--"We have not orders enough to keep in work the forge-hammers we

[049-147]already have, and we do not desire at present to add any new ones,

[049-148]however improved."  At that time no patent had been taken out for the

[049-149]invention.  Mr. Nasmyth had not yet saved money enough to enable him to

[049-150]do so on his own account; and his partner declined to spend money upon

[049-151]a tool that no engineer would give the firm an order for.  No secret

[049-152]was made of the invention, and, excepting to its owner, it did not seem

[049-153]to be worth one farthing.

[049-154]Such was the unpromising state of affairs, when M. Schneider, of the

[049-155]Creusot Iron Works in France, called at the Patricroft works together

[049-156]with his practical mechanic M. Bourdon, for the purpose of ordering

[049-157]some tools of the firm.  Mr. Nasmyth was absent on a journey at the

[049-158]time, but his partner, Mr. Gaskell, as an act of courtesy to the

[049-159]strangers, took the opportunity of showing them all that was new and

[049-160]interesting in regard to mechanism about the works.  And among other

[049-161]things, Mr. Gaskell brought out his partner's sketch or "Scheme book,"

[049-162]which lay in a drawer in the office, and showed them the design of the

[049-163][[steam-hammer|Steam Hammer]], which no English firm would adopt.  They were much struck

[049-164]with its simplicity and practical utility; and M.  Bourdon took careful

[049-165]note of its arrangements.  Mr. Nasmyth on his return was informed of

[049-166]the visit of MM. Schneider and Bourdon, but the circumstance of their

[049-167]having inspected the design of his steam-hammer seems to have been

[049-168]regarded by his partner as too trivial a matter to be repeated to him;

[049-169]and he knew nothing of the circumstance until his visit to France in

[049-170]April, 1840.  When passing through the works at Creusot with M.

[049-171]Bourdon, Mr. Nasmyth saw a crank shaft of unusual size, not only forged

[049-172]in the piece, but punched.  He immediately asked, "How did you forge

[049-173]that shaft?"  M. Bourdon's answer was, "Why, with your hammer, to be

[049-174]sure!"  Great indeed was Nasmyth's surprise; for he had never yet seen

[049-175]the hammer, except in his own drawing!  A little explanation soon

[049-176]cleared all up.  M. Bourdon said he had been so much struck with the

[049-177]ingenuity and simplicity of the arrangement, that he had no sooner

[049-178]returned than he set to work, and had a hammer made in general

[049-179]accordance with the design Mr. Gaskell had shown him; and that its

[049-180]performances had answered his every expectation.  He then took Mr.

[049-181]Nasmyth to see the steam-hammer; and great was his delight at seeing

[049-182]the child of his brain in full and active work.  It was not, according

[049-183]to Mr. Nasmyth's ideas, quite perfect, and he readily suggested several

[049-184]improvements, conformable with the original design, which M. Bourdon

[049-185]forthwith adopted.
