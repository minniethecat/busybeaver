# Board of Longitude

The Board of Longitude was a British government body established by the Longitude Act of 1714, charged with administering the Longitude Prize — a reward of up to £20,000 for a practical method of determining a ship's longitude at sea. The Board was composed of scientific, naval, and political figures and operated until its dissolution in 1828.

## Establishment

The problem of determining longitude at sea was one of the great scientific challenges of the 17th and 18th centuries. Following the loss of several naval vessels due to navigational errors, [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] passed the Longitude Act, creating both the prize and the Board to judge competing proposals. The Board included the Astronomer Royal, the President of the Royal Society, and senior figures from the [[admiralty|Admiralty]] and the universities of Oxford and Cambridge.

## Activities

### The Longitude Prize

The Board evaluated a wide range of proposals, including astronomical methods (lunar distances) and mechanical timekeeping (marine chronometers). The two most successful approaches were:

- **Lunar Distance Method**: championed by Nevil Maskelyne, the Astronomer Royal, relying on precise astronomical tables and mathematical calculation.
- **Marine Chronometer**: developed by John Harrison, whose H4 timekeeper successfully maintained accurate time on long sea voyages.

The Board eventually awarded Harrison a series of payments totalling much of the prize money, though with significant bureaucratic resistance. Maskelyne's *Nautical Almanac* (first published 1766) provided the lunar distance tables needed by navigators.

### Scientific Tables

The Board also commissioned mathematical and astronomical tables essential to navigation. Even with expert preparation, these tables were prone to error. [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]] notes that "in a multiplication-table prepared by a man so eminent as Dr. Hutton for the Board of Longitude, no fewer than forty errors were discovered in a single page taken at random," and the "Tables of the Board of Longitude were found equally incorrect." (044-015–019) Such errors highlighted the need for mechanical calculation, which later spurred the development of Charles Babbage's Difference Engine.

## Dissolution

The Board was dissolved in 1828, its functions absorbed by other Admiralty and scientific bodies. The longitude problem was effectively solved through the convergence of improved chronometers, better astronomical tables, and eventually, radio time signals.
