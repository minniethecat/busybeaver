# River Thames

The River Thames, flowing through [[london|London]] to the North Sea, was the principal commercial artery of Britain during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. Its banks in [[london|London]] were lined with engineering workshops, iron-foundries, and shipbuilding yards that powered the nation's industrial growth. (023-089) (023-108)

## Industrial Corridor

### Thames Street Industries

Upper Thames Street in the City of [[london|London]] was a notable centre of engineering enterprise. Here merchants and manufacturers established workshops that produced precision instruments and machinery. (009-100) Among them was the celebrated engineer [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], who maintained a smith's shop in York Yard, Upper Thames Street, where he executed work of the highest quality. (023-089)

The Steelyard on the Thames, in the immediate neighbourhood of Upper Thames Street, had been a Hanseatic trading post for centuries and remained a symbol of the river's commercial importance. (023-108)

### The [[thames-tunnel|Thames Tunnel]]

One of the most ambitious engineering projects on the Thames was the [[thames-tunnel|Thames Tunnel]], driven beneath the river by [[marc-isambard-brunel|Marc Isambard Brunel]]. The tunnel was thought an entirely impractical undertaking, but Brunel persisted through floods, financial crises, and technical obstacles. (031-028) (041-114)

While occupied with his [[thames-tunnel|Thames Tunnel]] works, Brunel received vital support from [[henry-maudslay|Maudslay]], who supplied the tunnelling shield that made excavation possible. (040-099)

### Millwall and Shipbuilding

Downstream at Millwall on the Thames, large engineering works grew up in the nineteenth century, including shipbuilding yards that produced iron vessels for the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]] and merchant service. These works were among the largest industrial establishments on the lower Thames. (055-182) (041-043)

## Canal Connections

The Thames was connected to the [[severn|Severn]] by ambitious canal projects, designed to link the two great river systems and create a navigable waterway across southern [[england|England]]. (013-002) The tideway of the Thames extended navigable access far inland, though iron-smelting furnaces along its upper reaches were restricted by legislation that forbade new works within fourteen miles of the river. (013-022) (008-135)
