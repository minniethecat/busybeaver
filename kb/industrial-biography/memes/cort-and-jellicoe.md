# Cort and Jellicoe

[[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]'s partnership with Samuel Jellicoe financed the development of the [[puddling-process|puddling]] process and grooved rollers at [[fontley-forge|Fontley Forge]].

## The Partnership

Cort took a foundry at Fontley in Hampshire and entered into partnership with Adam Jellicoe to finance his experiments in iron-making. (001-211) The partnership enabled Cort to develop and patent his improved processes for manufacturing [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. Licences were taken at royalties estimated to yield £27,500 to the owners of the patents — a substantial sum for the time. (024-060)

Adam Jellicoe was then Deputy-Paymaster of Seamen's Wages, handling substantial government funds. (021-115) He was afterwards joined in partnership by Samuel Jellicoe, his son. (021-114) The experiments at Fontley were of a very expensive character, and Adam Jellicoe advanced to Cort progressively large sums of money to carry them forward. (022-127)

## The Jellicoe Defalcation

The partnership ended in catastrophe. Adam Jellicoe was discovered to have committed public defalcations — embezzlement of naval funds entrusted to him in his capacity as a government paymaster. (001-217) When the defalcations were uncovered, Cort's patents and property were seized by the Crown to satisfy Jellicoe's debts, even though Cort himself was entirely innocent of any wrongdoing. (001-218)

As Jellicoe had advanced to Cort beyond his equal share, Cort subsequently assigned to him all his patent rights as collateral security. (022-131) There was a sum of £27,500 entrusted to Jellicoe for the use of Cort — money which he had at different times lent and advanced to Cort for the purpose of enabling him to perfect his inventions. (022-139) (022-164)

On examination, it was found that the debts due to Jellicoe from the Crown were far larger than the sums he had embezzled, so the Crown seized Cort's patent rights. (022-122) As one contemporary observer noted: the body of Jellicoe was worth nothing to them, but they could seize his interest in the patents. (022-158)

The ruin of [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] followed swiftly. (001-220) Jellicoe died at his private dwelling-house, No. 14, Highbury Place, Islington, on the 30th August, 1789, after a fortnight's illness. While some accounts suggested he committed suicide under the pressure of dread of exposure, this does not appear to be confirmed by contemporary newspaper accounts. (024-064)

## The Aftermath

After the death of Adam Jellicoe, the assignments of Cort's patents that had been made to Jellicoe in security for his advances were seized by the Crown. (022-118) (022-172)

Samuel Jellicoe, the son of the defaulter — singular to say — was put in possession of the properties at Fontley and Gosport, and continued to carry on the business. (022-174) (022-175)

## Impact and Legacy

Cort has been called the very Tubal Cain of [[england|England]] — one of the principal founders of the iron age. (024-004) To him we mainly owe the abundance of wrought-iron for machinery, for [[steam-engine|steam-engine]]s, and for railways, at one-third the previous price.

In the opinion of [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] of [[manchester|Manchester]], the inventions of Henry Cort had already added six hundred millions sterling to the wealth of the kingdom, while giving employment to some six hundred thousand working people during three generations. (024-013)

Yet while the great ironmasters, by freely availing themselves of his inventions, were adding estate to estate, the only estate secured by Henry Cort was the little domain of six feet by two in which he lies interred in Hampstead Churchyard. (024-018)
