Samuel Smiles (1812–1904) was a Scottish author, social reformer, and advocate of self-improvement. He is best known as the author of *Self-Help* (1859), a foundational text of Victorian self-improvement literature.

He was also the biographer of the engineers and inventors who drove the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. His *Industrial Biography: Iron Workers and Tool Makers* (1863), by Samuel Smiles, is the primary source for this wiki. (001-007)

## Industrial Biography

*Industrial Biography: Iron Workers and Tool Makers* was published in 1863, presenting the lives and achievements of the men who developed Britain's iron industry and machine-tool trades.

Smiles observed that the field of Industrial Biography is a wide one, and acknowledged that his work was only a beginning. (001-089)

He traced the history of iron-making from the early efforts of [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] in the 1620s through the achievements of [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]], [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]], and [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]].

Smiles's approach to biography emphasized character, perseverance, and practical skill. His subjects were not statesmen or generals but artisans, mechanics, and inventors — men who "made their own way in the world" through ingenuity and hard work.

The work combined biographical narrative with technical description, making the history of engineering accessible to a general readership.

## Other Works

Smiles introduced many of his subjects in his earlier *Lives of the Engineers*, a multi-volume work covering [[james-brindley|James Brindley]], [[john-smeaton|John Smeaton]], [[john-rennie|John Rennie]], and [[thomas-telford|Thomas Telford]]. While preparing that work he collected materials that later formed the basis of *Industrial Biography*. (001-012)

His *Lives of the Engineers* also covered figures such as [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] and [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]] in detail. (018-172) The series remains a standard reference for early industrial history, alongside his later volume on [[matthew-boulton|Boulton]] and [[james-watt|James Watt]].

*Self-Help*, published in 1859, became one of the most influential books of the Victorian era. It argued that individual character and perseverance, rather than birth or privilege, were the keys to success.

The thesis was illustrated with biographical sketches of self-made men including many of the inventors and engineers Smiles later profiled in *Industrial Biography*. The book referenced industrial achievements such as tin-plate manufacture as examples of practical ingenuity. (014-175)

## Legacy

Smiles's biographical method — combining technical accuracy with narrative accessibility — created the template for popular engineering biography.

His works preserved detailed accounts of inventions and working methods that would otherwise have been lost. His insistence on naming the often-anonymous mechanics and artisans who built the Industrial Revolution gave them a permanent place in the historical record.
