# Separate Condenser

[[james-watt|James Watt]]'s separate condenser for the steam engine, patented in 1769, which dramatically improved engine efficiency by condensing steam in a separate vessel rather than in the cylinder

## Background
In Newcomen's [[steam-engine|atmospheric engine]], steam was condensed inside the cylinder by injecting cold water. This cooled the cylinder walls, so that when fresh steam entered on the next stroke, much of its energy was wasted in reheating the cylinder. Watt realised that if condensation could occur in a separate vessel, the cylinder could remain hot and efficiency would dramatically improve.

## Inventor
- **Inventor**: [[james-watt|James Watt]] (1736-1819)
- **Patent**: 1769 (Enlarged and extended by Act of [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] to 1800)
- **Partner**: [[matthew-boulton|Matthew Boulton]]

## How It Works
Watt's separate condenser was a vessel connected to the cylinder by a pipe with a valve. At the appropriate point in the stroke, the valve opened and steam rushed into the condenser, which was kept cold by water spray. The steam condensed, creating a partial vacuum without cooling the cylinder. The result was a dramatic reduction in fuel consumption, from 30 to 75 percent depending on the application.

## Significance
The separate condenser was the critical invention that made the steam engine economically viable for widespread use. Without it, steam power would have been limited to coal mines where fuel was essentially free. It enabled steam power to spread to manufacturing, transport, and every sector of the economy.
