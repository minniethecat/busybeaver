# Steamboat

The steamboat — a vessel driven by steam engine rather than wind or muscle — was among the most transformative applications of steam power in the early nineteenth century. First developed experimentally by Patrick Miller of Dalswinton in 1788, steamboats were refined on the rivers of Britain and America and soon extended to coastal and ocean navigation, reshaping maritime commerce and travel.

## Background

The idea of steam-driven water transport had been contemplated since the early days of the steam engine. The challenge was twofold: a marine steam engine had to be compact and reliable enough to operate at sea, and the paddle-wheel or screw propeller had to transmit power efficiently to the water.

Before the steam engine reached sufficient reliability, "the idea of the steam-boat could be developed in practice, which was done by Miller of Dalswinton in 1788." (031-008) Earlier proposals by Denis Papin and others remained experimental.

## Inventor

- **Pioneer**: Patrick Miller of Dalswinton, [[scotland|Scotland]] — demonstrated the first practical steamboat in 1788
- **Key improvers**: Henry Bell (built the *Comet*, 1812), William Symington
- **Early commercial operators**: The *Regent*, the first steamboat on the [[thames|Thames]], ran between [[london|London]] and Margate

## Development

The earliest steamboats operated on sheltered rivers and estuaries. The *Regent*, "which was the first steamboat that plied on the [[thames|Thames]]," ran between [[london|London]] and Margate. (039-058)

Steamboats soon extended to coastal and ocean routes. The adoption of iron hull construction — pioneered by [[William Fairbairn]] and others — dramatically expanded the possibilities for steam navigation. Iron hulls were lighter, stronger, and more durable than wood, and could be built to sizes impossible in timber.

As the century progressed, steamboats increasingly replaced sailing vessels on scheduled routes. "Iron roads have long been worked by iron locomotives; and before many years have passed, the navigable rivers will be navigated by iron steamboats." (039-173)

## Significance

The steamboat transformed maritime transport in several ways:

- **Reliability**: Unlike sailing vessels, steamboats could maintain schedules regardless of wind conditions
- **Speed**: Steam power enabled faster passage, particularly on rivers where tacking was difficult
- **Industrial demand**: Steamboat construction drove the growth of marine engineering and iron shipbuilding
- **Global connectivity**: Steam navigation opened regular passenger and freight routes across oceans

The steamboat, together with the [[Locomotive]], created the steam-powered transport network that underpinned nineteenth-century industrial expansion.
