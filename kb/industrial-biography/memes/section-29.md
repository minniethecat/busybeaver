[029-001]The invention of the hot blast, in conjunction with the discovery of

[029-002]the Black Band ironstone, has had an extra ordinary effect upon the

[029-003]development of the iron-manufacture of [[scotland|Scotland]].  The coals of that

[029-004]country are generally unfit for coking, and lose as much as 55 per

[029-005]cent. in the process.  But by using the hot blast, the coal could be

[029-006]sent to the blast-furnace in its raw state, by which a large saving of

[029-007]fuel was effected.[5]  Even coals of an inferior quality were by its

[029-008]means made available for the manufacture of iron.  But one of the

[029-009]peculiar qualities of the Black Band ironstone is that in many cases it

[029-010]contains sufficient coaly matter for purposes of calcination, without

[029-011]any admixture of coal whatever.  Before its discovery, all the iron

[029-012]manufactured in [[scotland|Scotland]] was made from clay-band; but the use of the

[029-013]latter has in a great measure been discontinued wherever a sufficient

[029-014]supply of Black Band can be obtained.  And it is found to exist very

[029-015]extensively in most of the midland Scotch counties,--the coal and iron

[029-016]measures stretching in a broad belt from the Firth of Forth to the

[029-017]Irish Channel at the Firth of [[clyde|Clyde]].  At the time when the hot blast

[029-018]was invented, the fortunes of many of the older works were at a low

[029-019]ebb, and several of them had been discontinued; but they were speedily

[029-020]brought to life again wherever Black Band could be found.  In 1829, the

[029-021]year after Neilson's patent was taken out, the total make of Scotland

[029-022]was 29,000 tons.  As fresh discoveries of the mineral were made, in

[029-023]Ayrshire and Lanarkshire, new works were erected, until, in 1845, we

[029-024]find the production of Scotch pig-iron had increased to 475,000 tons.

[029-025]It has since increased to upwards of a million of tons,

[029-026]nineteen-twentieths of which are made from Black Band ironstone.[6]

[029-027]Employment has thus been given to vast numbers of our industrial

[029-028]population, and the wealth and resources of the Scotch iron districts

[029-029]have been increased to an extraordinary extent.  During the last year

[029-030]there were 125 furnaces in blast throughout Scotland, each employing

[029-031]about 400 men in making an average of 200 tons a week; and the money

[029-032]distributed amongst the workmen may readily be computed from the fact

[029-033]that, under the most favourable circumstances, the cost of making iron

[029-034]in wages alone amounts to 36s. a-ton.[7]

[029-035]An immense additional value was given to all land in which the Black

[029-036]Band was found.  [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] mentions that in 1839 the proprietor of the

[029-037]Airdrie estate derived a royalty of 16,500L. from the mineral, which

[029-038]had not before its discovery yielded him one farthing.  At the same

[029-039]time, many fortunes have been made by pushing and energetic men who

[029-040]have of late years entered upon this new branch of industry.  Amongst

[029-041]these may be mentioned the Bairds of Gartsherrie, who vie with the

[029-042]Guests and Crawshays of [[south-wales|South Wales]], and have advanced themselves in

[029-043]the course of a very few years from the station of small farmers to

[029-044]that of great capitalists owning estates in many counties, holding the

[029-045]highest character commercial men, and ranking among the largest

[029-046]employers of labour in the kingdom.

[029-066]to the use of raw pit-coal, and the other twenty tons to the use of hot

[029-067]blast."  [Papers on Iron and Steel, 127.] The produce per furnace is

[029-068]now 200 tons a-week and upwards.  The hot blast process was afterwards

[029-069]applied to the making of iron with the anthracite or stone coal of

[029-070]Wales; for which a patent was taken out by George Crane in 1836.

[029-071]Before the hot blast was introduced, anthracite coal would not act as

[029-072]fuel in the blast-furnace.  When put in, it merely had the effect of

[029-073]putting the fire out.  With the aid of the hot blast, however, it now

[029-074]proves to be a most valuable fuel in smelting.

[029-077]the hot blast the saving on our present produce of 400,000 tons of

[029-078]pig-iron is 2,000,000 tons of coals, 200,000 tons of limestone, and

[029-079]#650,000 sterling per annum."  But as the Scotch produce is now above a

[029-080]million tons of pig-iron a year, the above figures will have to be

[029-081]multiplied by 2 1/2 to give the present annual savings.

[029-084]CHAPTER X.

[029-085]MECHANICAL INVENTIONS AND INVENTORS.

[029-086]"L'invention nest-elle pas la poesie de la science? . . .  Toutes les

[029-087]grandes decouvertes portent avec elles la trace ineffacable d'une

[029-088]pensee poetique.  Il faut etre poete pour creer.  Aussi, sommes-nous

[029-089]convaincus que si les puissantes machines, veritable source de la

[029-090]production et de l'industrie de nos jours, doivent recevoir des

[029-091]modifications radicales, ce sera a des hommes d'imagination, et non

[029-092]point a dea hommes purement speciaux, que l'on devra cette

[029-093]transformation."--E. M. BATAILLE, Traite des Machines a Vapeur.

[029-094]Tools have played a highly important part in the history of

[029-095]civilization.  Without tools and the ability to use them, man were

[029-096]indeed but a "poor, bare, forked animal,"--worse clothed than the

[029-097]birds, worse housed than the beaver, worse fed than the jackal.  "Weak

[029-098]in himself," says Carlyle, "and of small stature, he stands on a basis,

[029-099]at most for the flattest-soled, of some half square foot, insecurely

[029-100]enough; has to straddle out his legs, Jest the very wind supplant him.

[029-101]Feeblest of bipeds!  Three quintals are a crushing load for him; the

[029-102]steer of the meadow tosses him aloft like a waste rag.  Nevertheless he

[029-103]can use tools, can devise tools:  with these the granite mountain melts

[029-104]into light dust before him; he kneads glowing iron as if it were soft

[029-105]paste; seas are his smooth highway, winds and fire his unvarying

[029-106]steeds.  Nowhere do you find him without tools: without tools he is

[029-107]nothing; with tools he is all."  His very first contrivances to support

[029-108]life were tools of the simplest and rudest construction; and his latest

[029-109]achievements in the substitution of machinery for the relief of the

[029-110]human hand and intellect are founded on the use of tools of a still

[029-111]higher order.  Hence it is not without good reason that man has by some

[029-112]philosophers been defined as A TOOL-MAKING ANIMAL.

[029-113]Tools, like everything else, had small beginnings.  With the primitive

[029-114]stone-hammer and chisel very little could be done.  The felling of a

[029-115]tree would occupy a workman a month, unless helped by the destructive

[029-116]action of fire.  Dwellings could not be built, the soil could not be

[029-117]tilled, clothes could not be fashioned and made, and the hewing out of

[029-118]a boat was so tedious a process that the wood must have been far gone

[029-119]in decay before it could be launched.  It was a great step in advance

[029-120]to discover the art of working in metals, more especially in steel, one

[029-121]of the few metals capable of taking a sharp edge and keeping it.  From

[029-122]the date of this discovery, working in wood and stone would be found

[029-123]comparatively easy; and the results must speedily have been felt not

[029-124]only in the improvement of man's daily food, but in his domestic and

[029-125]social condition.  Clothing could then be made, the primitive forest

[029-126]could be cleared and tillage carried on; abundant fuel could be

[029-127]obtained, dwellings erected, ships built, temples reared; every

[029-128]improvement in tools marking a new step in the development of the human

[029-129]intellect, and a further stage in the progress of human civilization.

[029-130]The earliest tools were of the simplest possible character, consisting

[029-131]principally of modifications of the wedge; such as the knife, the

[029-132]shears (formed of two knives working on a joint), the chisel, and the

[029-133]axe.  These, with the primitive hammer, formed the principal

[029-134]stock-in-trade of the early mechanics, who were handicraftsmen in the

[029-135]literal sense of the word.  But the work which the early craftsmen in

[029-136]wood, stone, brass, and iron, contrived to execute, sufficed to show

[029-137]how much expertness in the handling of tools will serve to compensate

[029-138]for their mechanical imperfections.  Workmen then sought rather to aid

[029-139]muscular strength than to supersede it, and mainly to facilitate the

[029-140]efforts of manual skill.  Another tool became added to those mentioned

[029-141]above, which proved an additional source of power to the workman.  We

[029-142]mean the Saw, which was considered of so much importance that its

[029-143]inventor was honoured with a place among the gods in the mythology of

[029-144]the Greeks.  This invention is said to have been suggested by the

[029-145]arrangement of the teeth in the jaw of a serpent, used by Talus the

[029-146]nephew of Daedalus in dividing a piece of wood.  From the

[029-147]representations of ancient tools found in the paintings at Herculaneum

[029-148]it appears that the frame-saw used by the ancients very nearly

[029-149]resembled that still in use; and we are informed that the tools

[029-150]employed in the carpenters' shops at Nazareth at this day are in most

[029-151]respects the same as those represented in the buried Roman city.

[029-152]Another very ancient tool referred to in the Bible and in Homer was the

[029-153]File, which was used to sharpen weapons and implements.  Thus the

[029-154]Hebrews "had a file for the mattocks, and for the coulters, and for the

[029-155]forks, and for the axes, and to sharpen the goads." [1]  When to these

[029-156]we add the adze, plane-irons, the anger, and the chisel, we sum up the

[029-157]tools principally relied on by the early mechanics for working in wood

[029-158]and iron.

[029-159]Such continued to be the chief tools in use down almost to our own day.

[029-160]The smith was at first the principal tool-maker; but special branches

[029-161]of trade were gradually established, devoted to tool-making.  So long,

[029-162]however, as the workman relied mainly on his dexterity of hand, the

[029-163]amount of production was comparatively limited; for the number of

[029-164]skilled workmen was but small.  The articles turned out by them, being

[029-165]the product of tedious manual labour, were too dear to come into common

[029-166]use, and were made almost exclusively for the richer classes of the

[029-167]community.  It was not until machinery had been invented and become

[029-168]generally adopted that many of the ordinary articles of necessity and

[029-169]of comfort were produced in sufficient abundance and at such prices as

[029-170]enabled them to enter into the consumption of the great body of the

[029-171]people.

[029-172]But every improver of tools had a long and difficult battle to fight;

[029-173]for any improvement in their effective power was sure to touch the

[029-174]interests of some established craft.  Especially was this the case with

[029-175]machines, which are but tools of a more complete though complicated

[029-176]kind than those above described.

[029-177]Take, for instance, the case of the Saw.  The tedious drudgery of

[029-178]dividing timber by the old fashioned hand-saw is well known.  To avoid

[029-179]it, some ingenious person suggested that a number of saws should be

[029-180]fixed to a frame in a mill, so contrived as to work with a

[029-181]reciprocating motion, upwards and downwards, or backwards and forwards,

[029-182]and that this frame so mounted should be yoked to the [[water-wheel|mill wheel]], and

[029-183]the saws driven by the power of wind or water.  The plan was tried,

[029-184]and, as may readily be imagined, the amount of effective work done by

[029-185]this machine-saw was immense, compared with the tedious process of

[029-186]sawing by hand.
