# Foundry

An industrial establishment where metal — primarily [[Cast Iron]] — is melted in a cupola or reverberatory furnace and poured into moulds to produce everything from engine cylinders and bridge arches to domestic pots and pans.

## Definition

A foundry is a workshop or factory equipped with furnaces for melting metal, moulding floors or pits for shaping castings, and cranes and tackle for handling heavy moulds and castings. (001-210) It is distinguished from a smelting works (which reduces ore to metal in a [[Blast Furnace]]) by its focus on remelting pig iron or scrap and casting it into finished shapes. The related process of [[Iron Founding]] describes the craft; the foundry is the physical plant where that craft is practiced.

## Historical Development

### Early Foundries

The earliest English foundries grew up alongside blast furnaces in the Weald of [[sussex|Sussex]] in the 16th century, producing cannon and shot. As the iron industry shifted north and west in the 18th century, foundries multiplied in [[south-wales|South Wales]], the Midlands, and the [[clyde|Clyde]] Valley.

> At the foundry in 1713, the weekly output varied from five to ten tons — a small quantity by later standards, but significant for the time. (016-019)

### The Great Foundries of the Industrial Revolution

The [[Carron Iron Works]] (founded 1759) on the River Carron near [[falkirk|Falkirk]] was one of the first truly integrated ironworks, combining blast furnaces, a foundry, and finishing shops:

> The foundry, and the ironwork was successfully carried on. Carron became celebrated for its cannon, its shot, and its ornamental castings. (017-065)

> The foundry, which, with the levels, roads, and wharfs, constituted one of the largest iron-making establishments in Britain. (017-121)

[[John Wilkinson]] established foundries at Bersham and later at Bradley, specializing in the casting of accurate [[steam-engine]] cylinders. His boring mill — itself a [[Machine Tool]] — gave his foundry a decisive advantage in quality.

### The Specialized Foundry

By the early 19th century, foundries had become specialized. Some focused on heavy engineering castings (mill beams, engine cylinders, bridge components), others on light work (stoves, railings, domestic ware), and still others on artistic castings (statues, architectural ornament, cemetery monuments).

> The [[ketley|Ketley]] foundry produced a range of castings from heavy [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]]s to delicate fire-grates. (017-176)

## Foundry Process

### The [[cupola-furnace|Cupola Furnace]]

The heart of a foundry is the cupola — a small, cylindrical shaft furnace in which pig iron and scrap are melted with coke and limestone. The cupola is a miniature [[Blast Furnace]] optimized for remelting rather than smelting:

1. Pig iron ingots and scrap are charged into the top with layers of coke
2. A blast of air is forced through tuyeres near the bottom
3. Molten iron collects in the well and is tapped into ladles
4. The ladle is carried or crane-lifted to the moulds

### Moulding

The mould is typically made of sand mixed with clay and moisture, rammed around a wooden pattern in a two-part box (the cope and drag). For hollow castings, a core of baked sand is suspended inside the mould cavity. The skill of the moulder — packing sand to the right consistency, positioning the pattern, cutting runners and risers — determined the quality of the finished casting.

### Pouring and Finishing

Molten iron at ~1,300°C is poured into the mould through a system of channels (runners). After cooling — which can take hours for small castings and days for large ones — the sand is knocked off and the casting is fettled: runners and risers are cut away, and the surface is cleaned with chisels, files, or grinding wheels.

## Economic Role

Foundries were the manufacturing backbone of the [[Industrial Revolution]]. Every steam engine required cylinders, beams, and bearings from a foundry. Every [[Iron Bridge]] required arch ribs cast in a foundry. Every railway required chairs, rails, and signal posts from foundries. The geographic spread of foundries — from the colossal works of [[south-wales|South Wales]] to small jobbing foundries in every market town — meant that cast-iron goods were available throughout Britain at declining real cost.
