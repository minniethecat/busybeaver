Josiah Heath was a British metallurgist and inventor whose process for manufacturing steel, employing carburet of manganese, was one of the three great steel-making innovations of the nineteenth century, ranked alongside the processes of [[David Mushet]] and [[Henry Bessemer]]. (021-002)

## The Heath Process

Heath made his key discovery while residing in India. Observing the effects of manganese on steel, he founded upon this observation his celebrated process for producing cast-steel. (026-152) The innovation was significant enough to be noted in the preface to [[Samuel Smiles]]'s *Industrial Biography*, which listed it among the major inventions in iron and steel manufacture. (001-202)

In the Heath process, carburet of manganese is employed to aid the conversion of iron into steel. The manganese compound acts as a purifying agent, removing impurities from the molten metal and producing a more uniform and workable steel product. (021-038)

This placed Heath's method alongside the two other dominant processes of the era: Mushet's process, which also utilized manganese-based compounds, and Bessemer's pneumatic converter process. (021-041)

## Historical Significance

The Heath process occupied an important place in the evolution of steel-making technology. While the Bessemer process ultimately became the dominant method for mass-producing steel, Heath's work with manganese-carburet compounds contributed essential chemical knowledge to the field.

The recognition of manganese as a critical alloying and purifying element in steel production, a principle established independently by both Heath and Mushet, has remained fundamental to modern steel-making practice.
