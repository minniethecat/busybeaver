# Tilt Hammer

A mechanical forging hammer driven by a [[Water Wheel]], consisting of a heavy iron head mounted on a wooden beam that pivots near its midpoint — cams on a rotating shaft lift one end, tilting the hammer head up, then release it to fall under gravity onto the workpiece.

## Definition

The tilt hammer (also called a trip hammer or helve hammer) is a water-powered forging machine used for shaping heavy iron workpieces. A rotating shaft fitted with cams (projecting pegs or wipers) engages the tail end of a pivoted beam, depressing it and thereby raising the hammer head at the opposite end. As the shaft continues to rotate, the cam releases the tail, and the hammer head falls by gravity onto the anvil and workpiece. The cycle repeats with each rotation of the shaft. (049-050)

## Historical Development

### Water-Powered Forging

The tilt hammer was one of the earliest machine tools, dating from at least the 12th century in Europe. By transferring the repetitive lifting and dropping of a heavy hammer from a human arm to a water wheel, it increased the force and speed of forging by orders of magnitude.

> The tilt-hammer was usually driven by a water-wheel, or by oxen or horses. It was one of the earliest applications of non-human power to the working of iron. (049-050)

### Industrial Applications

In the 18th and early 19th centuries, tilt hammers were the principal means of forging heavy iron workpieces — anchor shanks, large shafts, forge-welded assemblies, and the massive components of early steam engines and water wheels.

> Among [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]'s various inventions for the [[soho-manufactory|Soho Manufactory]] was a tilt-hammer of considerable size and power, used for forging the heavier parts of [[steam-engine|steam-engine]]s. (049-052)

[[james-watt|James Watt]] himself recognized the limitations of the tilt hammer for the finest work:

> Watt afterwards made a tilt-hammer for Mr. Roebuck at the Carron Ironworks. It proved serviceable for rough forging, though the blow was not sufficiently rapid or controllable for the finer sorts of work. (049-056)

### Legacy

The tilt hammer remained in use well into the 19th century, particularly in districts with reliable water power. It was eventually superseded by [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]'s [[Steam Hammer]] (1839), which offered far greater control, power, and versatility:

> The tilt-hammer continued in use for forging heavy masses of iron long after the introduction of the steam hammer, particularly in remote districts where water power was abundant and cheap. (049-050)

## How It Works

The tilt hammer consists of:

| Component | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Hammer head | Iron block weighing 50–500 kg, the striking face |
| Helve (beam) | Wooden or iron beam, pivoted near its midpoint |
| Cams (wipers) | Projections on a rotating shaft that engage the tail of the beam |
| Anvil | Heavy iron block on which the workpiece rests |
| Water wheel | Provides rotary power to the camshaft |
| Tup (optional) | In later designs, the hammer head is the tup |

The number of cams on the shaft determines the blow rate — typically 60–200 blows per minute, depending on the wheel speed and the number of cams.

## Applications

Tilt hammers were used for:

- Drawing out (lengthening) iron bars and shafts
- Forge-welding large assemblies
- Shaping anchor arms and shanks
- [[forging|Forging]] steam engine beams and connecting rods
- Producing heavy [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] from piled scrap or puddled iron blooms

Lighter, faster tilt hammers were used in the [[Cutlery]] trade for forging blades, and in [[Nail-Making]] for producing nail rods.
