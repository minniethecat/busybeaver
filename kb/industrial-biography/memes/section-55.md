[055-001]His apprenticeship over, [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]] commenced business[5] in a small

[055-002]way in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1791, in conjunction with two other workmen,

[055-003]Sandford and MacConnel.  Their business was machine-making and

[055-004]mule-spinning, Kennedy taking the direction of the machine department.

[055-005]The firm at first put up their mules for spinning in any convenient

[055-006]garrets they could hire at a low rental.  After some time, they took

[055-007]part of a small factory in Canal Street, and carried on their business

[055-008]on a larger scale.  Kennedy and MacConnel afterwards occupied a little

[055-009]factory in the same street,--since removed to give place to Fairbairn's

[055-010]large machine works.  The progress of the firm was steady and even

[055-011]rapid, and they went on building mills and extending their

[055-012]business--Mr. Kennedy, as he advanced in life, gathering honour,

[055-013]wealth, and troops of friends.  Notwithstanding the defects of his

[055-014]early education, he was one of the few men of his class who became

[055-015]distinguished for his literary labours in connexion principally with

[055-016]the cotton trade.  Towards the close of his life, he prepared several

[055-017]papers of great interest for the Literary and Philosophical Society of

[055-018][[manchester|Manchester]], which are to be found printed in their Proceedings; one of

[055-019]these, on the Invention of the Mule by [[samuel-crompton|Samuel Crompton]], was for a long

[055-020]time the only record which the public possessed of the merits and

[055-021]claims of that distinguished inventor.  His knowledge of the history of

[055-022]the cotton manufacture in its various stages, and of mechanical

[055-023]inventions generally, was most extensive and accurate.  Among his

[055-024]friends he numbered [[james-watt|James Watt]], who placed his son in his establishment

[055-025]for the purpose of acquiring knowledge and experience of his

[055-026]profession.  At a much later period he numbered [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] among

[055-027]his friends, having been one of the first directors of the [[liverpool|Liverpool]]

[055-028]and Manchester Railway, and one of the three judges (selected because

[055-029]of his sound judgment and proved impartiality, as well as his knowledge

[055-030]of mechanical engineering) to adjudicate on the celebrated competition

[055-031]of [[locomotive|Locomotive]]s at Rainhill.  By these successive steps did this poor

[055-032]Scotch boy become one of the leading men of Manchester, closing his

[055-033]long and useful life in 1855 at an advanced age, his mental faculties

[055-034]remaining clear and unclouded to the last.  His departure from life was

[055-035]happy and tranquil--so easy that it was for a time doubtful whether he

[055-036]was dead or asleep.

[055-037]To return to Mr. Fairbairn's career, and his progress as a millwright

[055-038]and engineer in Manchester.  When he and his partner undertook the

[055-039]extensive alterations in Mr. Murray's factory, both were in a great

[055-040]measure unacquainted with the working of cotton-mills, having until

[055-041]then been occupied principally with corn-mills, and printing and

[055-042]bleaching works; so that an entirely new field was now opened to their

[055-043]united exertions.  Sedulously improving their opportunities, the young

[055-044]partners not only thoroughly mastered the practical details of

[055-045]cotton-mill work, but they were very shortly enabled to introduce a

[055-046]series of improvements of the greatest importance in this branch of our

[055-047]national manufactures.  Bringing their vigorous practical minds to bear

[055-048]on the subject, they at once saw that the gearing of even the best

[055-049]mills was of a very clumsy and imperfect character.  They found the

[055-050]machinery driven by large square cast-iron shafts, on which huge wooden

[055-051]drums, some of them as much as four feet in diameter, revolved at the

[055-052]rate of about forty revolutions a minute; and the couplings were so

[055-053]badly fitted that they might be heard creaking and groaning a long way

[055-054]off.  The speeds of the driving-shafts were mostly got up by a series

[055-055]of straps and counter drums, which not only crowded the rooms, but

[055-056]seriously obstructed the light where most required for conducting the

[055-057]delicate operations of the different machines.  Another serious defect

[055-058]lay in the construction of the shafts, and in the mode of fixing the

[055-059]couplings, which were constantly giving way, so that a week seldom

[055-060]passed without one or more breaks-down.  The repairs were usually made

[055-061]on Sundays, which were the millwrights' hardest working days, to their

[055-062]own serious moral detriment; but when trade was good, every

[055-063]consideration was made to give way to the uninterrupted running of the

[055-064]mills during the rest of the week.

[055-065]It occurred to Mr. Fairbairn that the defective arrangements thus

[055-066]briefly described, might be remedied by the introduction of lighter

[055-067]shafts driven at double or treble the velocity, smaller drums to drive

[055-068]the machinery, and the use of wrought-iron wherever practicable,

[055-069]because of its greater lightness and strength compared with wood.  He

[055-070]also provided for the simplification of the hangers and fixings by

[055-071]which the shafting was supported, and introduced the "half-lap

[055-072]coupling" so well known to millwrights and engineers.  His partner

[055-073]entered fully into his views; and the opportunity shortly presented

[055-074]itself of carrying them into effect in the large new mill erected in

[055-075]1818, for the firm of MacConnel and Kennedy.  The machinery of that

[055-076]concern proved a great improvement on all that had preceded it; and, to

[055-077]Messrs. Fairbairn and Lillie's new system of gearing Mr. Kennedy added

[055-078]an original invention of his own in a system of double speeds, with the

[055-079]object of giving an increased quantity of twist in the finer

[055-080]descriptions of mule yarn.

[055-081]The satisfactory execution of this important work at once placed the

[055-082]firm of Fairbairn and Lillie in the very front rank of engineering

[055-083]millwrights.  Mr. Kennedy's good word was of itself a passport to fame

[055-084]and business, and as he was more than satisfied with the manner in

[055-085]which his mill machinery had been planned and executed, he sounded

[055-086]their praises in all quarters.  Orders poured in upon them so rapidly,

[055-087]that they had difficulty in keeping pace with the demands of the trade.

[055-088]They then removed from their original shed to larger premises in

[055-089]Matherstreet, where they erected additional lathes and other

[055-090]tool-machines, and eventually a [[steam-engine]].  They afterwards added a

[055-091]large cellar under an adjoining factory to their premises; and from

[055-092]time to time provided new means of turning out work with increased

[055-093]efficiency and despatch.  In due course of time the firm erected a

[055-094]factory of their own, fitted with the most improved machinery for

[055-095]turning out millwork; and they went on from one contract to another,

[055-096]until their reputation as engineers became widely celebrated.  In

[055-097]1826-7, they supplied the water-wheels for the extensive cotton-mills

[055-098]belonging to Kirkman Finlay and Company, at Catrine Bank in Ayrshire.

[055-099]These wheels are even at this day regarded as among the most perfect

[055-100]hydraulic machines in Europe.  [[About]] the same time they supplied the

[055-101]mill gearing and water-machinery for Messrs. Escher and Company's large

[055-102]works at Zurich, among the largest cotton manufactories on the

[055-103]continent.

[055-104]In the mean while the industry of Manchester and the neighbourhood,

[055-105]through which the firm had risen and prospered, was not neglected, but

[055-106]had the full benefit of the various improvements which they were

[055-107]introducing in mill machinery.  In the course of a few years an entire

[055-108]revolution was effected in the gearing.  Ponderous masses of timber and

[055-109]cast-iron, with their enormous bearings and couplings, gave place to

[055-110]slender rods of wrought-iron and light frames or hooks by which they

[055-111]were suspended.  In like manner, lighter yet stronger wheels and

[055-112]pulleys were introduced, the whole arrangements were improved, and, the

[055-113]workmanship being greatly more accurate, friction was avoided, while

[055-114]the speed was increased from about 40 to upwards of 300 revolutions a

[055-115]minute.  The fly-wheel of the engine was also converted into a first

[055-116]motion by the formation of teeth on its periphery, by which a

[055-117]considerable saving was effected both in cost and power.

[055-118]These great improvements formed quite an era in the history of mill

[055-119]machinery; and exercised the most important influence on the

[055-120]development of the cotton, flax, silk, and other branches of

[055-121]manufacture.  Mr. Fairbairn says the system introduced by his firm was

[055-122]at first strongly condemned by leading engineers, and it was with

[055-123]difficulty that he could overcome the force of their opposition; nor

[055-124]was it until a wheel of thirty tons weight for a pair of engines of

[055-125]100-horse power each was erected and set to work, that their

[055-126]prognostications of failure entirely ceased.  From that time the

[055-127]principles introduced by Mr. Fairbairn have been adopted wherever steam

[055-128]is employed as a motive power in mills.

[055-129]Mr. Fairbairn and his partner had a hard uphill battle to fight while

[055-130]these improvements were being introduced; but energy and perseverance,

[055-131]guided by sound judgment, secured their usual reward, and the firm

[055-132]became known as one of the most thriving and enterprising in

[055-133]Manchester.  Long years after, when addressing an assembly of working

[055-134]men, Mr. Fairbairn, while urging the necessity of labour and

[055-135]application as the only sure means of self-improvement, said, "I can

[055-136]tell you from experience, that there is no labour so sweet, none so

[055-137]consolatory, as that which is founded upon an honest, straightforward,

[055-138]and honourable ambition."  The history of any prosperous business,

[055-139]however, so closely resembles every other, and its details are usually

[055-140]of so monotonous a character, that it is unnecessary for us to pursue

[055-141]this part of the subject; and we will content ourselves with briefly

[055-142]indicating the several further improvements introduced by Mr. Fairbairn

[055-143]in the mechanics of construction in the course of his long and useful

[055-144]career.

[055-145]His improvements in water-wheels were of great value, especially as

[055-146]regarded the new form of bucket which he introduced with the object of

[055-147]facilitating the escape of the air as the water entered the bucket

[055-148]above, and its readmission as the water emptied itself out below.  This

[055-149]arrangement enabled the water to act upon the wheel with the maximum of

[055-150]effect in all states of the river; and it so generally recommended

[055-151]itself, that it very soon became adopted in most water-mills both at

[055-152]home and abroad.[6]  His labours were not, however, confined to his own

[055-153]particular calling as a mill engineer, but were shortly directed to

[055-154]other equally important branches of the constructive art.  Thus he was

[055-155]among the first to direct his attention to iron ship building as a

[055-156]special branch of business.  In 1829, Mr. Houston, of Johnstown, near

[055-157]Paisley, launched a light boat on the Ardrossan Canal for the purpose

[055-158]of ascertaining the speed at which it could be towed by horses with two

[055-159]or three persons on board.  To the surprise of Mr. Houston and the

[055-160]other gentlemen present, it was found that the labour the horses had to

[055-161]perform in towing the boat was mach greater at six or seven, than at

[055-162]nine miles an hour.  This anomaly was very puzzling to the

[055-163]experimenters, and at the request of the Council of the Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]]

[055-164]Canal, Mr. Fairbairn, who had already become extensively known as a

[055-165]scientific mechanic, was requested to visit [[scotland|Scotland]] and institute a

[055-166]series of experiments with light boats to determine the law of

[055-167]traction, and clear up, if possible, the apparent anomalies in Mr.

[055-168]Houston's experiments.  This he did accordingly, and the results of his

[055-169]experiments were afterwards published, The trials extended over a

[055-170]series of years, and were conducted at a cost of several thousand

[055-171]pounds.  The first experiments were made with vessels of wood, but they

[055-172]eventually led to the construction of iron vessels upon a large scale

[055-173]and on an entirely new principle of construction, with angle iron ribs

[055-174]and wrought-iron sheathing plates.  The results proved most valuable,

[055-175]and had the effect of specially directing the attention of naval

[055-176]engineers to the employment of iron in ship building.

[055-177]Mr. Fairbairn himself fully recognised the value of the experiments,

[055-178]and proceeded to construct an iron vessel at his works at Manchester,

[055-179]in 1831, which went to sea the same year.  Its success was such as to

[055-180]induce him to begin iron shipbuilding on a large scale, at the same

[055-181]time as the Messrs. Laird did at Birkenhead; and in 1835, Mr. Fairbairn

[055-182]established extensive works at Millwall, on the [[thames|Thames]],--afterwards

[055-183]occupied by Mr. Scott Russell, in whose yard the "Great Eastern"

[055-184]steamship was erected,--where in the course of some fourteen years he

[055-185]built upwards of a hundred and twenty [[iron-shipbuilding|iron ships]], some of them above

[055-186]2000 tons burden.  It was in fact the first great iron shipbuilding

[055-187]yard in Britain, and led the way in a branch of business which has

[055-188]since become of first-rate magnitude and importance.  Mr. Fairbairn was

[055-189]a most laborious experimenter in iron, and investigated in great detail

[055-190]the subject of its strength, the value of different kinds of riveted

[055-191]joints compared with the solid plate, and the distribution of the

[055-192]material throughout the structure, as well as the form of the vessel

[055-193]itself.  It would indeed be difficult to over-estimate the value of his

[055-194]investigations on these points in the earlier stages of this now highly

[055-195]important branch of the national industry.
