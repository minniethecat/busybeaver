**Cutlery** — the manufacture of knives, razors, scissors, and other fine-edged hand tools — was the principal trade of [[Sheffield]] from the seventeenth century onward. Its global reputation was secured by the introduction of [[Benjamin Huntsman]]'s cast steel in the 1740s, which replaced the traditional cemented ("blister") steel and enabled the production of cutlery of a far superior quality.

## 定义

Cutlery in the industrial context refers specifically to the manufacture of edged tools and implements — knives, razors, scissors, shears, surgical instruments — as distinct from heavier edge-tool making (axes, scythes) or general ironmongery. The trade was distinguished by its reliance on high-quality steel, precision grinding, and skilled hand-finishing. [[sheffield|Sheffield]] dominated the English cutlery trade, with its name becoming synonymous with quality cutlery worldwide.

## 历史发展

### 起源：Sheffield 的地利

[[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s cutlery trade grew from the confluence of local iron ore, abundant water power for grinding wheels, and sandstone suitable for grinding stones. By the early eighteenth century, [[sheffield|Sheffield]] was already a recognised centre for knife-making, though the quality of its products was limited by the available steel — cemented [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] of uneven carbon distribution.

### Huntsman 铸钢的革命

The pivotal moment came when [[Benjamin Huntsman]], a Doncaster clockmaker, invented his process for making cast steel — a homogeneous, high-carbon steel produced by melting cemented [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] in closed clay crucibles. Huntsman foresaw "the extensive uses to which cast-steel might be applied in the manufacture of tools and cutlery of a superior kind," and began endeavouring to persuade [[sheffield|Sheffield]] manufacturers to employ it. (020-030)

The [[sheffield|Sheffield]] cutlers, however, obstinately refused to work a material "so much harder than that which they had been accustomed to use." (020-034) Rebuffed at home, Huntsman exported his entire output to France. The French quickly recognised the value of cast-steel cutlery, and for a time "the whole of the cast-steel that Huntsman could manufacture was exported to France." (020-041)

Alarmed by the growing reputation of French cast-steel cutlery — and by reports that English consumers now preferred imported French knives — the [[sheffield|Sheffield]] cutlers realised their error. They appointed a deputation to petition [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] to prohibit the export of cast steel. The MP they approached, Sir George Savile, "positively declined to comply with their request" upon learning that they themselves refused to use the material. (020-050) This defeat forced the [[sheffield|Sheffield]] makers to adopt cast steel, and Huntsman's home trade rapidly increased. By 1770, his operations had grown so large that he built a new manufactory at [[Attercliffe]]. (020-134)

### 全球声誉

The adoption of cast steel transformed [[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s cutlery trade from a regional craft into a global industry. As Smiles observed:

> There is scarcely a civilized country where Sheffield steel is not largely used, either in its most highly finished forms of cutlery, or as the raw material for some home manufacture. (020-131)

The discovery proved "of the greatest advantage to [[sheffield|Sheffield]]" — the town's name became a mark of quality, and the cutlery trade grew into one of Britain's most lucrative export industries. (020-128)

## 工艺特点

The manufacture of cutlery involved several distinct stages:

1. **[[forging|Forging]]**: The steel was heated and hammered to rough shape at the forge
2. **Grinding**: Blades were ground to edge on sandstone wheels powered by water or steam — a skilled and hazardous trade
3. **Hardening and [[tempering|Tempering]]**: Blades were heated, quenched, and tempered to achieve the required hardness and flexibility
4. **Finishing**: Handles were fitted (in horn, bone, wood, or silver), and blades polished

The hardness of cast steel, which initially deterred the [[sheffield|Sheffield]] cutlers, was precisely the quality that gave the finished product its superior cutting edge and durability. (020-034)

## 经济与工业影响

The transformation of the cutlery trade through cast steel illustrates a broader pattern of the British [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]: a technical innovation (Huntsman's crucible process) was resisted by incumbent manufacturers, adopted first abroad, and only accepted at home under threat of foreign competition. The episode also demonstrates how a single material innovation could define a town's economy for generations — [[sheffield|Sheffield]] remained the world's cutlery capital well into the twentieth century. (020-060)

The cutlery trade also spurred related industries: grinding-wheel quarrying, handle-making, silver-plating (the [[sheffield|Sheffield]] plate technique), and the production of special-purpose steels — all of which contributed to [[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s emergence as a major industrial centre. (024-128)
