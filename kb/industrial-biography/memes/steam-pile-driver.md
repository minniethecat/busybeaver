The steam pile driver was invented by [[James Nasmyth]] in 1845. It applied steam power to the ancient task of driving foundation piles—heavy timber or iron posts driven into the ground to support structures built on soft soil. Nasmyth's design used a steam-operated hammer to deliver repeated powerful blows, replacing the slow and labor-intensive manual pile-driving methods. (002-140)

## Invention Background

The important uses of the hammer in modern engineering extended beyond the forge. Nasmyth, who had revolutionized forging with his [[steam-hammer]], recognized that steam-powered impact could also be applied to pile-driving—hammering foundation piles into riverbeds and soft ground for bridges, docks, and large buildings. (002-140)

## Technical Significance

Nasmyth's steam pile driver brought the power and speed of steam machinery to civil engineering foundations. It was a natural extension of his steam hammer concept, adapting the controlled vertical blow from forging metal to driving piles. The invention was part of Nasmyth's broader contribution to heavy engineering machinery, alongside his work with machine tools. (002-058)
