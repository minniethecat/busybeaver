# Institution of Mechanical Engineers

The Institution of Mechanical Engineers (IMechE) was founded in 1847 with [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] as its first president. It became the primary professional body for mechanical engineers in Britain.

## History

The Institution was established at a time when mechanical engineering was emerging as a distinct profession. The locomotive itself, as [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] observed, had not been "the invention of any one man, but of a race of mechanical engineers." (030-092) The Institution provided a forum for this growing community of practitioners to share knowledge and advance their field.

## Role in the Profession

By the mid-19th century, mechanical engineering had become one of the most important branches of industry. The perfection of automatic [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] had made the manufacture of the [[steam-engine|steam-engine]] a matter of comparative ease and certainty. (032-014) These tools were themselves among the most striking results of mechanical invention of the day. (032-047)

The Institution's meetings served as a platform for presenting significant technical papers. At the meeting of the Institute of Mechanical Engineers at [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1856, [[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]] read a paper on the essential importance of possessing a true plane as a standard of reference in mechanical constructions. (047-107)

## [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]'s Presidency

[[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]], who had begun his career as an apprentice engine-man, later had the opportunity, in his capacity as President of the Institute of Mechanical Engineers at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]], of making public acknowledgment of the opportunities for education which he had enjoyed in his early years. (053-026) Fairbairn had made the acquaintance of [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] while the latter was employed in working the ballast-engine at Willington Quay, and greatly admired him as a workman. (053-014)

## Legacy

The Institution of Mechanical Engineers continued the tradition of the great mechanical engineering workshops that came before it. The shops of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] had been regarded as a first-class school for mechanical engineers, where every workman felt that the quality of his workmanship was fully understood and appreciated. (040-127) The Institution carried this commitment to excellence forward as the professional body for the discipline.
