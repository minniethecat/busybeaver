The steam locomotive — a self-propelled engine running on iron rails — was pioneered by [[Richard Trevithick]] in 1803 at the [[penydarran|[[penydarran|Penydarran]] Ironworks]] in [[south-wales|South Wales]].

It was [[Matthew Murray]] of [[leeds|Leeds]] who in 1812 built the first locomotive to work regularly on a railway. Together with subsequent refinements by [[George Stephenson]] and [[Richard Roberts]], the locomotive became the prime mover of the railway age, reshaping land transport, industry, and society across the nineteenth century.

## Background

The need for mechanical traction on land was driven by the growing scale of coal and iron production. Horses could not economically move the tonnage of coal, iron ore, and finished metal that furnaces and forges produced. Stationary steam engines had been used for pumping and winding at mines since the early 1700s, but the idea of making a steam engine propel itself along a road or rail was more challenging.

Dr. Erasmus Darwin poetically anticipated the locomotive in his *Botanic Garden* (1791), published before any locomotive had been invented. (031-008) The idea of atmospheric locomotion was first suggested by Denis Papin, and the Frenchman Gauthey projected a method of conveying goods by steam in 1782.

[[richard-trevithick|Richard Trevithick]], working in [[cornwall|Cornwall]] and later [[south-wales|South Wales]], was the first to build a high-pressure steam engine compact enough to move itself. His experiments culminated in the [[penydarran|Penydarran]] locomotive of 1803.

## Inventor

- **Pioneer**: [[Richard Trevithick]] (1771–1833), who built the [[first-locomotive|first railway locomotive]] in 1803
- **First working railway locomotive**: [[Matthew Murray]] (1765–1826), for the Middleton Colliery Railway near [[leeds|Leeds]], 1812
- **Key improvers**: [[George Stephenson]], [[Richard Roberts]], [[Timothy Hackworth]]
- **Patent**: Trevithick's high-pressure engine patent covered the locomotive concept

## How It Works

The locomotive is a high-pressure steam engine mounted on a wheeled frame, running on iron rails. Key elements include:

1. **Boiler**: Generates steam under high pressure, with a fire-tube design that maximises heating surface. The multi-tubular boiler — an arrangement "by which the greatest extent of heating surface was provided" — was a critical advance. (028-132)
2. **Cylinders and pistons**: Steam admitted to the cylinder drives the piston, which connects via rods to the driving wheels
3. **Blast-pipe**: Exhaust steam is directed up the chimney through a constricted nozzle, creating a forced draught that intensifies the fire. This was "the one thing needful that has made it effective in practice." (036-121)
4. **Driving wheels**: Power is transmitted to the wheels through cranks and coupling rods. Roberts patented "an arrangement for communicating power to both driving-wheels of the locomotive, at all times in the exact proportions required when turning." (047-011)

The locomotive hauls a train of wagons or carriages coupled behind it, the iron rails providing a smooth, low-friction running surface.

## Development and Key Innovations

| Date | Inventor | Innovation |
|------|----------|-----------|
| 1803 | [[richard-trevithick|Richard Trevithick]] | First railway locomotive, [[penydarran|Penydarran Ironworks]] (024-107) |
| 1812 | Matthew Murray | First locomotive in regular railway service, Middleton Colliery (045-165) |
| 1814 | [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] | Improved locomotive for Killingworth Colliery |
| 1829 | [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] | *Rocket* wins [[rainhill-trials|Rainhill Trials]], establishing the modern locomotive form |
| 1830s | [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]] | Steam-brake, dual driving-wheel power, large-scale manufacture (047-006) |

Trevithick's [[penydarran|Penydarran]] engine "was employed to bring down metal from the furnaces to the Old Forge" but was taken off the road "because the tram-plates were found too weak to bear its weight without breaking." (024-107) This highlighted that locomotive and railway had to develop together — stronger rails were needed for heavier engines.

Matthew Murray's twin-cylinder engine for the Middleton Colliery Railway began running in 1812 "and continued in regular use for many years." (045-165) Murray was the first to place the cylinders horizontally in the common condensing engine, an arrangement that became standard.

After the opening of the [[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway in 1830, locomotive manufacture became "one of the chief branches of future mechanical engineering." (047-006) The firm of Sharp, Roberts and Co. began building locomotives on a large scale in 1834.

## Significance

The locomotive revolutionised transport: for the first time in history, goods and people could move faster than a horse could carry them — and do so on a predictable schedule. Its impact extended far beyond railways:

- **Coal and iron**: Cheaper transport of raw materials reduced costs throughout the industrial economy
- **Urbanisation**: Cities grew along railway lines; commuting became possible
- **Engineering**: Locomotive works drove advances in precision machining, metallurgy, and standardised manufacture
- **Global trade**: Railways opened continental interiors to world markets

> "Iron roads have long been worked by iron locomotives; and before many years have passed, the navigable rivers will be navigated by iron steamboats." (039-173)
