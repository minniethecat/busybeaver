# Cornwall

Cornwall was, from ancient times, the principal source of tin in the British Isles. The county's mineral wealth fuelled the bronze age, supported Roman trade, and continued to supply the metal trades through the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], though by the 17th century the tin trade had fallen into a deep depression. (006-008) (014-055)

## Industrial Significance

### Ancient Tin Mining

The knowledge of mixing copper and tin to produce bronze was among the earliest metallurgical arts. Cornwall's tin, together with copper from other regions, provided the essential ingredients for this revolutionary alloy. The Romans gave the metal the name Jupiter and the moon Luna — a mark of its high standing in the ancient world. (006-008) (006-029)

Cornwall's tin continued to be traded throughout the Roman period and into the medieval era, with the county retaining its pre-eminence as Britain's principal tin-producing region through centuries of continuous extraction. (006-158)

### The Tin Trade in the 17th Century

By the late 1600s, the Cornish tin trade had fallen into a severe depression. [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]], the indefatigable promoter of English industry, noted that Cornish tin was selling as low as 70 shillings the hundredweight. (014-055)

Yarranton proposed combining Cornish tin with "Roman cinders" (iron-smelting slag) to revive the trade. He wrote an amusing dialogue between a tin-miner of Cornwall, an iron-miner of [[forest-of-dean|Dean Forest]], and a traveller, from which we gather that Yarranton's business continued to be that of an active promoter of the nation's mineral industries. (014-056) (014-066) (014-068)
