# Cast Steel

A revolutionary steel-making process invented by [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]] in the 1740s, in which blister steel was melted in sealed clay crucibles at extremely high temperatures to produce a homogeneous, high-quality steel — the foundation of [[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s global reputation for fine steel products.

## Definition

Cast steel is steel produced by melting blister steel (itself made by cementation — heating wrought iron bars packed in charcoal) in a sealed clay crucible at temperatures exceeding 1,600°C. The intense heat fuses the steel into a completely homogeneous liquid, which is then poured into moulds to form ingots. The resulting product possesses "great cohesion and closeness of grain," making it far superior to blister steel for tools, cutlery, springs, and precision instruments. (019-097)

Cast steel is technically the first form of molten steel ever produced. Unlike modern Bessemer or open-hearth steel, it is made in small batches (each crucible holding 15–20 kg) and its quality depends on the skill of the melters and the purity of the raw materials. Cast steel "is presented in perhaps its very highest state of perfection" among iron products. (019-093)

## Historical Development

### [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]] and the Invention

The invention of cast steel "is due to [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]], of [[attercliffe|Attercliffe]], near [[sheffield|Sheffield]]." Born in Lincolnshire in 1704, Huntsman was a clockmaker by trade who became dissatisfied with the quality of imported German steel for his clock springs. This led him to experiment with steel-making methods. (019-107) (019-130)

Huntsman's experiments "extended over many years before the desired result was obtained." He discovered that by heating blister steel to a temperature high enough to melt it completely — far higher than had previously been achieved — he could produce a perfectly homogeneous material. To reach such temperatures, he used a specially designed coke-fired furnace and sealed clay crucibles capable of withstanding the intense heat. (019-169)

Huntsman established his works at [[attercliffe|Attercliffe]], near [[sheffield|Sheffield]], around 1740. Initially he kept his method a secret, but [[sheffield|Sheffield]] cutlers, suspicious of the superior foreign steel appearing in their markets, eventually discovered his process through industrial espionage. "How they wrested Huntsman's secret from him" is a famous story — a rival disguised as a tramp obtained access to Huntsman's works on a bitterly cold night and observed the process. (001-199)

### [[sheffield|Sheffield]]'s Rise

Once the secret was out, [[sheffield|Sheffield]] became the world centre of cast-steel production. The city's "manufactures of [[sheffield|Sheffield]] is that of cast-steel" grew rapidly, and by the 19th century [[sheffield|Sheffield]] steel was prized globally for cutlery, edge tools, files, springs, and later for armour plate and railway components. (019-092) Huntsman's discovery was recognised internationally: the French professor who wrote on steel-making declared that "the memorable discovery of [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]] is completely accomplished." (019-010)

## Process

The Huntsman process for making cast steel:

1. **Cementation**: Wrought iron bars are packed in charcoal powder inside sealed sandstone chests and heated to about 1,100°C for 7–10 days. Carbon diffuses into the iron, producing blister steel (named for the blistered surface appearance).
2. **Crucible Preparation**: Small fireclay crucibles (each holding about 15–20 kg) are prepared and pre-heated.
3. **Melting**: Broken pieces of blister steel are placed in the crucibles along with a flux. The crucibles are sealed and placed in a coke-fired furnace, where they are heated to over 1,600°C for several hours until the steel is fully molten.
4. **Teeming**: The crucible is lifted from the furnace using long tongs and the molten steel is poured (teemed) into cast-iron moulds to form ingots.
5. **Working**: The ingots are reheated and forged or rolled into bars, sheets, or specific shapes.

The fuel used by Huntsman was coke, the same fuel described by later writers as "those in use at the present day" — another testament to how far ahead of his time Huntsman was. (019-182)

## Economic and Industrial Impact

Cast steel established [[sheffield|Sheffield]] as the global capital of fine steel production. The superior homogeneity and consistency of crucible steel made it essential for:
- **[[cutlery|Cutlery]] and edge tools**: [[sheffield|Sheffield]] knives, razors, and scissors dominated world markets
- **Clock and watch springs**: The application that had first motivated Huntsman
- **Files and precision tools**: Machine tool cutters, files, and dies that enabled the engineering revolution
- **Surgical instruments**: High-quality, corrosion-resistant steel for medical tools

Cast steel remained the premier quality steel-making method until the development of electric arc furnaces in the early 20th century. Even today, "crucible steel" is a byword for the highest quality tool steel.
