[054-001]Arrived at [[liverpool|Liverpool]], after a voyage of two days--which was then

[054-002]considered a fair passage--our engineer proceeded to [[manchester|Manchester]], which

[054-003]had already become the principal centre of manufacturing operations in

[054-004]the North of [[england|England]].  As we have already seen in the memoirs of

[054-005]Nasmyth, Roberts, and Whitworth, [[manchester|Manchester]] offered great attractions

[054-006]for highly-skilled mechanics; and it was as fortunate for Manchester as

[054-007]for [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] himself that he settled down there as a working

[054-008]millwright in the year 1814, bringing with him no capital, but an

[054-009]abundance of energy, skill, and practical experience in his trade.

[054-010]Afterwards describing the characteristics of the millwright of that

[054-011]time, Mr. Fairbairn said--"In those days a good millwright was a man of

[054-012]large resources; he was generally well educated, and could draw out his

[054-013]own designs and work at the lathe; he had a knowledge of mill

[054-014]machinery, pumps, and cranes, and could turn his hand to the bench or

[054-015]the forge with equal adroitness and facility.  If hard pressed, as was

[054-016]frequently the case in country places far from towns, he could devise

[054-017]for himself expedients which enabled him to meet special requirements,

[054-018]and to complete his work without assistance.  This was the class of men

[054-019]with whom I associated in early life--proud of their calling, fertile

[054-020]in resources, and aware of their value in a country where the

[054-021]industrial arts were rapidly developing." [4]

[054-022]When [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] entered Manchester he was twenty-four years of

[054-023]age; and his hat still "covered his family."  But, being now pretty

[054-024]well satiated with his "wandetschaft,"--as German tradesmen term their

[054-025]stage of travelling in search of trade experience,--he desired to

[054-026]settle, and, if fortune favoured him, to marry the object of his

[054-027]affections, to whom his heart still faithfully turned during all his

[054-028]wanderings.  He succeeded in finding employment with Mr. Adam

[054-029]Parkinson, remaining with him for two years, working as a millwright,

[054-030]at good wages.  Out of his earnings he saved sufficient to furnish a

[054-031]two-roomed cottage comfortably; and there we find him fairly installed

[054-032]with his wife by the end of 1816.  As in the case of most men of a

[054-033]thoughtful turn, marriage served not only to settle our engineer, but

[054-034]to stimulate him to more energetic action.  He now began to aim at

[054-035]taking a higher position, and entertained the ambition of beginning

[054-036]business on his own account.  One of his first efforts in this

[054-037]direction was the preparation of the design of a [[iron-bridge|cast-iron bridge]] over

[054-038]the Irwell, at Blackfriars, for which a prize was offered.  The attempt

[054-039]was unsuccessful, and a stone bridge was eventually decided on; but the

[054-040]effort made was creditable, and proved the beginning of many designs.

[054-041]The first job he executed on his own account was the erection of an

[054-042]iron conservatory and hothouse for Mr. J. Hulme, of Clayton, near

[054-043]Manchester; and he induced one of his shopmates, [[james-lillie|James Lillie]], to join

[054-044]him in the undertaking.  This proved the beginning of a business

[054-045]connection which lasted for a period of fifteen years, and laid the

[054-046]foundation of a partnership, the reputation of which, in connection

[054-047]with mill-work and the construction of iron machinery generally,

[054-048]eventually became known all over the civilized world.

[054-049]Although the patterns for the conservatory were all made, and the

[054-050]castings were begun, the work was not proceeded with, in consequence of

[054-051]the notice given by a [[birmingham|Birmingham]] firm that the plan after which it was

[054-052]proposed to construct it was an infringement of their patent.  The

[054-053]young firm were consequently under the necessity of looking about them

[054-054]for other employment.  And to be prepared for executing orders, they

[054-055]proceeded in the year 1817 to hire a small shed at a rent of 12s. a

[054-056]week, in which they set up a lathe of their own making, capable of

[054-057]turning shafts of from 3 to 6 inches diameter; and they hired a strong

[054-058]Irishman to drive the wheel and assist at the heavy work.  Their first

[054-059]job was the erection of a cullender, and their next a calico-polishing

[054-060]machine; but orders came in slowly, and [[james-lillie|James Lillie]] began to despair

[054-061]of success.  His more hopeful partner strenuously urged him to

[054-062]perseverance, and so buoyed him up with hopes of orders, that he

[054-063]determined to go on a little longer.  They then issued cards among the

[054-064]manufacturers, and made a tour of the principal firms, offering their

[054-065]services and soliciting work.

[054-066]Amongst others, Mr. Fairbairn called upon the Messrs. Adam and George

[054-067]Murray, the large cotton-spinners, taking with him the designs of his

[054-068]iron bridge.  Mr. [[adam-murray|Adam Murray]] received him kindly, heard his

[054-069]explanations, and invited him to call on the following day with his

[054-070]partner.  The manufacturer must have been favourably impressed by this

[054-071]interview, for next day, when Fairbairn and Lillie called, he took them

[054-072]over his mill, and asked whether they felt themselves competent to

[054-073]renew with horizontal cross-shafts the whole of the work by which the

[054-074]mule-spinning machinery was turned.  This was a formidable enterprise

[054-075]for a young firm without capital and almost without plant to undertake;

[054-076]but they had confidence in themselves, and boldly replied that they

[054-077]were willing and able to execute the work.  On this, Mr. Murray said he

[054-078]would call and see them at their own workshop, to satisfy himself that

[054-079]they possessed the means of undertaking such an order.  This proposal

[054-080]was by no means encouraging to the partners, who feared that when Mr.

[054-081]Murray spied "the nakedness of the land" in that quarter, he might

[054-082]repent him of his generous intentions.  He paid his promised visit, and

[054-083]it is probable that he was more favourably impressed by the individual

[054-084]merits of the partners than by the excellence of their

[054-085][[machine-tool|machine-tools]]--of which they had only one, the lathe which they had

[054-086]just made and set up; nevertheless he gave them the order, and they

[054-087]began with glad hearts and willing hands and minds to execute this

[054-088]their first contract.  It may be sufficient to state that by working

[054-089]late and early--from 5 in the morning until 9 at night for a

[054-090]considerable period--they succeeded in completing the alterations

[054-091]within the time specified, and to Mr. Murray's entire satisfaction.

[054-092]The practical skill of the young men being thus proved, and their

[054-093]anxiety to execute the work entrusted to them to the best of their

[054-094]ability having excited the admiration of their employer, he took the

[054-095]opportunity of recommending them to his friends in the trade, and

[054-096]amongst others to Mr. [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]], of the firm of MacConnel and

[054-097]Kennedy, then the largest spinners in the kingdom.

[054-098]The Cotton Trade had by this time sprung into great importance, and was

[054-099]increasing with extraordinary rapidity.  Population and wealth were

[054-100]pouring into South [[lancashire|Lancashire]], and industry and enterprise were

[054-101]everywhere on foot.  The foundations were being laid of a system of

[054-102]manufacturing in iron, machinery, and textile fabrics of nearly all

[054-103]kinds, the like of which has perhaps never been surpassed in any

[054-104]country.  It was a race of industry, in which the prizes were won by

[054-105]the swift, the strong, and the skilled.  For the most part, the early

[054-106][[lancashire|Lancashire]] manufacturers started very nearly equal in point of worldly

[054-107]circumstances, men originally of the smallest means often coming to the

[054-108]front--work men, weavers, mechanics, pedlers, farmers, or labourers--in

[054-109]course of time rearing immense manufacturing concerns by sheer force of

[054-110]industry, energy, and personal ability.  The description given by one

[054-111]of the largest employers in Lancashire, of the capital with which he

[054-112]started, might apply to many of them: "When I married," said he, "my

[054-113]wife had a spinning-wheel, and I had a loom--that was the beginning of

[054-114]our fortune."  As an illustration of the rapid rise of Manchester men

[054-115]from small beginnings, the following outline of [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]]'s career,

[054-116]intimately connected as he was with the subject of our memoir--may not

[054-117]be without interest in this place.

[054-118]John Kennedy was one of five young men of nearly the same age, who came

[054-119]from the same neighbourhood in [[scotland|Scotland]], and eventually settled in

[054-120]Manchester as cottons-pinners about the end of last century.  The

[054-121]others were his brother James, his partner [[james-macconel|James MacConnel]], and the

[054-122]brothers Murray, above referred to--Mr. Fairbairn's first extensive

[054-123]employers.  John Kennedy's parents were respectable peasants, possessed

[054-124]of a little bit of ground at Knocknalling, in the stewartry of

[054-125]Kirkcudbright, on which they contrived to live, and that was all.  John

[054-126]was one of a family of five sons and two daughters, and the father

[054-127]dying early, the responsibility and the toil of bringing up these

[054-128]children devolved upon the mother.  She was a strict disciplinarian,

[054-129]and early impressed upon the minds of her boys that they had their own

[054-130]way to make in the world.  One of the first things she made them think

[054-131]about was, the learning of some useful trade for the purpose of

[054-132]securing an independent living; "for," said she, "if you have gotten

[054-133]mechanical skill and intelligence, and are honest and trustworthy, you

[054-134]will always find employment and be ready to avail yourselves of

[054-135]opportunities for advancing yourselves in life."  Though the mother

[054-136]desired to give her sons the benefits of school education, there was

[054-137]but little of that commodity to be had in the remote district of

[054-138]Knocknalling.  The parish-school was six miles distant, and the

[054-139]teaching given in it was of a very inferior sort--usually administered

[054-140]by students, probationers for the ministry, or by half-fledged

[054-141]dominies, themselves more needing instruction than able to impart it.

[054-142]The Kennedys could only attend the school during a few months in

[054-143]summer-time, so that what they had acquired by the end of one season

[054-144]was often forgotten by the beginning of the next.  They learnt,

[054-145]however, to read the Testament, say their catechism, and write their

[054-146]own names.

[054-147]As the children grew up, they each longed for the time to come when

[054-148]they could be put to a trade.  The family were poorly clad; stockings

[054-149]and shoes were luxuries rarely indulged in; and Mr. Kennedy used in

[054-150]after-life to tell his grandchildren of a certain Sunday which he

[054-151]remembered shortly after his father died, when he was setting out for

[054-152]Dalry church, and had borrowed his brother Alexander's stockings, his

[054-153]brother ran after him and cried, "See that you keep out of the dirt,

[054-154]for mind you have got my stockings on!"  John indulged in many

[054-155]day-dreams about the world that lay beyond the valley and the mountains

[054-156]which surrounded the place of his birth.  Though a mere boy, the

[054-157]natural objects, eternally unchangeable, which daily met his eyes--the

[054-158]profound silence of the scene, broken only by the bleating of a

[054-159]solitary sheep, or the crowing of a distant cock, or the thrasher

[054-160]beating out with his flail the scanty grain of the black oats spread

[054-161]upon a skin in the open air, or the streamlets leaping from the rocky

[054-162]clefts, or the distant church-bell sounding up the valley on

[054-163]Sundays--all bred in his mind a profound melancholy and feeling of

[054-164]loneliness, and he used to think to himself, "What can I do to see and

[054-165]know something of the world beyond this?"  The greatest pleasure he

[054-166]experienced during that period was when packmen came round with their

[054-167]stores of clothing and hardware, and displayed them for sale; he

[054-168]eagerly listened to all that such visitors had to tell of the ongoings

[054-169]of the world beyond the valley.

[054-170]The people of the Knocknalling district were very poor.  The greater

[054-171]part of them were unable to support the younger members, whose custom

[054-172]it was to move off elsewhere in search of a living when they arrived at

[054-173]working years,--some to America, some to the West Indies, and some to

[054-174]the manufacturing districts of the south.  Whole families took their

[054-175]departure in this way, and the few friendships which Kennedy formed

[054-176]amongst those of his own age were thus suddenly snapped, and only a

[054-177]great blank remained.  But he too could follow their example, and enter

[054-178]upon that wider world in which so many others had ventured and

[054-179]succeeded.  As early as eight years of age, his mother still impressing

[054-180]upon her boys the necessity of learning to work, John gathered courage

[054-181]to say to her that he wished to leave home and apprentice himself to

[054-182]some handicraft business.  Having seen some carpenters working in the

[054-183]neighbourhood, with good clothes on their backs, and hearing the men's

[054-184]characters well spoken of, he thought it would be a fine thing to be a

[054-185]carpenter too, particularly as the occupation would enable him to move

[054-186]from place to place and see the world.  He was as yet, however, of too

[054-187]tender an age to set out on the journey of life; but when he was about

[054-188]eleven years old, [[adam-murray|Adam Murray]], one of his most intimate acquaintances,

[054-189]having gone off to serve an apprenticeship in Lancashire with Mr.

[054-190]Cannan of Chowbent, himself a native of the district, the event again

[054-191]awakened in him a strong desire to migrate from Knocknalling.  Others

[054-192]had gone after Murray, [[james-macconel|James MacConnel]] and two or three more; and at

[054-193]length, at about fourteen years of age, Kennedy himself left his native

[054-194]home for Lancashire.  [[About]] the time that he set out, Paul Jones was

[054-195]ravaging the coasts of Galloway, and producing general consternation

[054-196]throughout the district.  Great excitement also prevailed through the

[054-197]occurrence of the Gordon riots in [[london|London]], which extended into remote

[054-198]country places; and Kennedy remembered being nearly frightened out of

[054-199]his wits on one occasion by a poor dominie whose school he attended,

[054-200]who preached to his boys about the horrors that were coming upon the

[054-201]land through the introduction of Popery.  The boy set out for [[england|England]]

[054-202]on the 2nd of February, 1784, mounted upon a Galloway, his little

[054-203]package of clothes and necessaries strapped behind him.  As he passed

[054-204]along the glen, recognising each familiar spot, his heart was in his

[054-205]mouth, and he dared scarcely trust himself to look back.  The ground

[054-206]was covered with snow, and nature quite frozen up.  He had the company

[054-207]of his brother Alexander as far as the town of New Galloway, where he

[054-208]slept the first night.  The next day, accompanied by one of his future

[054-209]masters, Mr. James Smith, a partner of Mr. Cannan's, who had originally

[054-210]entered his service as a workman, they started on ponyback for

[054-211]Dumfries.  After a long day's ride, they entered the town in the

[054-212]evening, and amongst the things which excited the boy's surprise were

[054-213]the few street-lamps of the town, and a waggon with four horses and

[054-214]four wheels.  In his remote valley carts were as yet unknown, and even

[054-215]in Dumfries itself they were comparative rarities; the common means of

[054-216]transport in the district being what were called "tumbling cars." The

[054-217]day after, they reached Longtown, and slept there; the boy noting

[054-218]ANOTHER lamp.  The next stage was to [[carlisle|Carlisle]], where Mr. Smith, whose

[054-219]firm had supplied a carding engine and spinning-jenny to a small

[054-220]manufacturer in the town, went to "gate" and trim them.  One was put up

[054-221]in a small house, the other in a small room; and the sight of these

[054-222]machines was John Kennedy's first introduction to [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]].

[054-223]While going up the inn-stairs he was amazed and not a little alarmed at

[054-224]seeing two men in armour--he had heard of the battles between the Scots

[054-225]and English--and believed these to be some of the fighting men; though

[054-226]they proved to be but effigies.  Five more days were occupied in

[054-227]travelling southward, the resting places being at Penrith, Kendal,

[054-228]Preston, and Chorley, the two travellers arriving at Chowbent on Sunday

[054-229]the 8th of February, 1784.  Mr. Cannan seems to have collected about

[054-230]him a little colony of Scotsmen, mostly from the same neighbourhood,

[054-231]and in the evening there was quite an assembly of them at the "Bear's

[054-232]Paw," where Kennedy put up, to hear the tidings from their native

[054-233]county brought by the last new comer.  On the following morning the boy

[054-234]began his apprenticeship as a carpenter with the firm of Cannan and

[054-235]Smith, serving seven years for his meat and clothing.  He applied

[054-236]himself to his trade, and became a good, steady workman.  He was

[054-237]thoughtful and self-improving, always endeavouring to acquire knowledge

[054-238]of new arts and to obtain insight into new machines.  "Even in early

[054-239]life," said he, in the account of his career addressed to his children,

[054-240]"I felt a strong desire to know what others knew, and was always ready

[054-241]to communicate what little I knew myself; and by admitting at once my

[054-242]want of education, I found that I often made friends of those on whom I

[054-243]had no claims beyond what an ardent desire for knowledge could give me."
