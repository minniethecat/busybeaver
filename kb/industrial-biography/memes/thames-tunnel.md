## Overview

The [[thames|Thames]] Tunnel, running beneath the [[thames|River Thames]] between Rotherhithe and Wapping in [[london|London]], was a landmark achievement in civil engineering. Constructed between 1825 and 1843 under the direction of [[Marc Isambard Brunel]], it was the first tunnel ever successfully driven beneath a navigable river. The project introduced the tunnelling shield — an invention that transformed underground engineering and remains the basis of modern tunnel-boring technology.

## The Tunnelling Shield

The central innovation of the Thames Tunnel was Brunel's tunnelling shield, a massive cast-iron framework divided into cells. Within each cell, workmen excavated the face of the tunnel while the shield protected them from collapse. As excavation progressed, hydraulic jacks pushed the shield forward, and bricklayers lined the newly exposed section behind it. The shield was patented by Brunel in 1818 and represented a fundamental advance in underground construction. (031-028)

## Construction and Challenges

Work on the Thames Tunnel began in 1825 from the Rotherhithe side. The project faced repeated setbacks: flooding of the workings, financial crises that halted construction for years at a time, and the immense difficulty of working beneath the riverbed. The tunnel flooded catastrophically in 1828, leading to a seven-year suspension. Brunel's son, [[Isambard Kingdom Brunel]], then a young engineer, was nearly killed in one such inundation and spent months recovering.

Despite these challenges, Brunel persisted. While occupied with the Thames Tunnel works, his reputation as an engineer of extraordinary determination and ingenuity attracted the attention of other project promoters. (040-099)

## Completion and Legacy

The excavation of the Thames Tunnel was eventually completed, aided by improved pumping equipment and the accumulated experience of the workforce. The tunnel opened to the public in 1843, initially as a pedestrian thoroughfare with shops and entertainments. It was later converted to railway use and now forms part of the [[london|London]] Overground network.

The tunnelling shield proved to be Brunel's most lasting contribution to engineering. It established the principle that underground works beneath water could be conducted safely through mechanical support at the face — a concept that underpins all modern tunnel construction. (041-114)
