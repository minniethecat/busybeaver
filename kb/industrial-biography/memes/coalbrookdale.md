# Coalbrookdale

Coalbrookdale in [[shropshire|Shropshire]] was the epicentre of the iron revolution. [[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]] established his coke-smelting works here in 1709. The valley possessed all the resources needed: iron ore, coal, limestone, and water power. In 1779 the world's first iron bridge was cast at Coalbrookdale and erected across the [[severn|Severn]]. (001-180)

## Industrial Significance

The Coalbrookdale valley held a remarkable concentration of natural resources. Iron ore lay near the surface in the hills; coal seams outcropped along the valley sides; limestone was quarried locally; and the [[severn|River Severn]] provided water power and transport. This made Coalbrookdale the natural birthplace of coke-based iron smelting.

[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]] chose the site in 1709 for its water power from the [[severn|Severn]]. His original furnace was a charcoal furnace, but proximity to coal seams enabled experiments with coke. After successful coke smelting, the [[coalbrookdale-company|Coalbrookdale Company]] expanded rapidly under successive generations of the Darby family.

By the mid-18th century, under [[abraham-darby-ii|Abraham Darby II]], the works produced cylinders for [[newcomen-engine|Newcomen engines]] and structural castings. These iron cylinders, bored at Coalbrookdale, were shipped to mines across Britain — making the works the first large-scale producer of engineering castings.

The [[first-iron-bridge-1779|Iron Bridge]] (1779) showed Coalbrookdale's mastery of casting at architectural scale. [[abraham-darby-iii|Abraham Darby III]] oversaw casting of the ribs, each 100 feet long in open sand moulds. The bridge used nearly 400 tons of iron and proved [[pig-iron|cast iron]] could replace stone. It survives as a UNESCO World Heritage site.

By the early 19th century, Coalbrookdale declined relative to newer iron districts. [[south-wales|South Wales]] and the Black Country had deeper coal seams and better rail connections. The company continued in decorative castings but could not compete in rails or structural sections.

The techniques developed at Coalbrookdale — coke smelting, engineering castings, structural iron — became the foundation of the entire iron industry. Though the valley declined as a production centre, its legacy shaped every subsequent iron district in Britain and abroad.

## Key Figures

- **[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]]** (1678–1717): Established the works in 1709 and smelted iron with coke.

- **[[abraham-darby-ii|Abraham Darby II]]** (1711–1763): Expanded into engine cylinders and engineering castings.

- **[[abraham-darby-iii|Abraham Darby III]]** (1750–1789): Oversaw casting of the [[first-iron-bridge-1779|Iron Bridge]] in 1779.

- **[[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]]** (1735–1816): Quaker ironmaster who introduced iron rails for wagonways.
