[017-001]Among the important improvements introduced by [[richard-reynolds|Mr. Reynolds]] while

[017-002]managing the [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] Works, was the adoption by him for the first

[017-003]time of iron instead of wooden rails in the tram-roads along which coal

[017-004]and iron were conveyed from one part of the works to another, as well

[017-005]as to the loading-places along the river [[severn|Severn]].  He observed that the

[017-006]wooden rails soon became decayed, besides being liable to be broken by

[017-007]the heavy loads passing over them, occasioning much loss of time,

[017-008]interruption to business, and heavy expenses in repairs.  It occurred

[017-009]to him that these inconveniences would be obviated by the use of rails

[017-010]of cast-iron; and, having tried an experiment with them, it answered so

[017-011]well, that in 1767 the whole of the wooden rails were taken up and

[017-012]replaced by rails of iron.  Thus was the era of iron railroads fairly

[017-013]initiated at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], and the example of [[richard-reynolds|Mr. Reynolds]] was shortly

[017-014]after followed on all the tramroads throughout the Country.

[017-015]It is also worthy of note that the first iron bridge ever erected was

[017-016]cast and made at the Coalbrookdale Works--its projection as well as its

[017-017]erection being mainly due to the skill and enterprise of Abraham Darby

[017-018]the third.  When but a young man, he showed indications of that

[017-019]sagacity and energy in business which seemed to be hereditary in his

[017-020]family.  One of the first things he did on arriving at man's estate was

[017-021]to set on foot a scheme for throwing a bridge across the [[severn|Severn]] at

[017-022]Coalbrookdale, at a point where the banks were steep and slippery, to

[017-023]accommodate the large population which had sprung up along both banks

[017-024]of the river.  There were now thriving iron, brick, and pottery works

[017-025]established in the parishes of Madeley and Broseley; and the old ferry

[017-026]on the Severn was found altogether inadequate for ready communication

[017-027]between one bank and the other.  The want of a bridge had long been

[017-028]felt, and a plan of one had been prepared during the life time of

[017-029]Abraham Darby the second; but the project was suspended at his death.

[017-030]When his son came of age, he resolved to take up his father's dropped

[017-031]scheme, and prosecute it to completion, which he did.  Young Mr. Darby

[017-032]became lord of the manor of Madeley in 1776, and was the owner of

[017-033]one-half of the ferry in right of his lordship.  He was so fortunate as

[017-034]to find the owner of the other or Broseley half of the ferry equally

[017-035]anxious with himself to connect the two banks of the river by means of

[017-036]a bridge.  The necessary powers were accordingly obtained from

[017-037][[house-of-commons|Parliament]], and a bridge was authorized to be built "of cast-iron,

[017-038]stone, brick, or timber."  A company was formed for the purpose of

[017-039]carrying out the project, and the shares were taken by the adjoining

[017-040]owners, Abraham Darby being the principal subscriber.[9]

[017-041]The construction of a bridge of iron was an entirely new idea.  An

[017-042]attempt had indeed been made at Lyons, in France, to construct such a

[017-043]bridge more than twenty years before; but it had entirely failed, and a

[017-044]bridge of timber was erected instead.  It is not known whether the

[017-045]Coalbrookdale masters had heard of that attempt; but, even if they had,

[017-046]it could have been of no practical use to them.

[017-047]Mr. Pritchard, an architect of Shrewsbury, was first employed to

[017-048]prepare a design of the intended structure, which is still preserved.

[017-049]Although Mr. Pritchard proposed to introduce cast-iron in the arch of

[017-050]the bridge, which was to be of 120 feet span, it was only as a sort of

[017-051]key, occupying but a few feet at the crown of the arch.  This sparing

[017-052]use of [[pig-iron|cast iron]] indicates the timidity of the architect in dealing

[017-053]with the new material--his plan exhibiting a desire to effect a

[017-054]compromise between the tried and the untried in bridge-construction.

[017-055]But the use of iron to so limited an extent, and in such a part of the

[017-056]structure, was of more than questionable utility; and if Mr.

[017-057]Pritchard's plan had been adopted, the problem of the iron bridge would

[017-058]still have remained unsolved.

[017-059]The plan, however, after having been duly considered, was eventually

[017-060]set aside, and another, with the entire arch of cast-iron, was prepared

[017-061]under the superintendence of Abraham Darby, by Mr. Thomas Gregory, his

[017-062]foreman of pattern-makers.  This plan was adopted, and arrangements

[017-063]were forthwith made for carrying it into effect.  The abutments of the

[017-064]bridge were built in 1777-8, during which the castings were made at the

[017-065]foundry, and the ironwork was successfully erected in the course of

[017-066]three months.  The bridge was opened for traffic in 1779, and proved a

[017-067]most serviceable structure.  In 1788 the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]] recognised Mr.

[017-068]Darby's merit as its designer and erector by presenting him with their

[017-069]gold medal; and the model of the bridge is still to be seen in the

[017-070]collection of the Society.  Mr. [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] has said of the

[017-071]structure:  "If we consider that the manipulation of cast-iron was then

[017-072]completely in its infancy, a bridge of such dimensions was doubtless a

[017-073]bold as well as an original undertaking, and the efficiency of the

[017-074]details is worthy of the boldness of the conception." [10]  Mr.

[017-075]Stephenson adds that from a defect in the construction the abutments

[017-076]were thrust inwards at the approaches and the ribs partially fractured.

[017-077]We are, however, informed that this is a mistake, though it does appear

[017-078]that the apprehension at one time existed that such an accident might

[017-079]possibly occur.

[017-080]To remedy the supposed defect, two small land arches were, in the year

[017-081]1800, substituted for the stone approach on the Broseley side of the

[017-082]bridge.  While the work was in progress, Mr. Telford, the well-known

[017-083]engineer, carefully examined the bridge, and thus spoke of its

[017-084]condition at the time:--"The great improvement of erecting upon a

[017-085]navigable river a bridge of cast-iron of one arch only was first put in

[017-086]practice near Coalbrookdale.  The bridge was executed in 1777 by Mr.

[017-087]Abraham Darby, and the ironwork is now quite as perfect as when it was

[017-088]first put up.  Drawings of this bridge have long been before the

[017-089]public, and have been much and justly admired." [11]  A Coalbrookdale

[017-090]correspondent, writing in May, 1862, informs us that "at the present

[017-091]time the bridge is undergoing repair; and, special examination having

[017-092]been made, there is no appearance either that the abutments have moved,

[017-093]or that the ribs have been broken in the centre or are out of their

[017-094]proper right line.  There has, it is true, been a strain on the land

[017-095]arches, and on the roadway plates, which, however, the main arch has

[017-096]been able effectually to resist."

[017-097]The bridge has now been in profitable daily use for upwards of eighty

[017-098]years, and has during that time proved of the greatest convenience to

[017-099]the population of the district.  So judicious was the selection of its

[017-100]site, and so great its utility, that a thriving town of the name of

[017-101]Ironbridge has grown up around it upon what, at the time of its

[017-102]erection, was a nameless part of "the waste of the manor of Madeley."

[017-103]And it is probable that the bridge will last for centuries to come.

[017-104]Thus, also, was the use of iron as an important material in

[017-105]bridge-building fairly initiated at Coalbrookdale by Abraham Darby, as

[017-106]the use of iron rails was by [[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]].  We need scarcely add

[017-107]that since the invention and extensive adoption of railway locomotion,

[017-108]the employment of iron in various forms in railway and bridge

[017-109]structures has rapidly increased, until iron has come to be regarded as

[017-110]the very sheet-anchor of the railway engineer.

[017-111]In the mean time the works at Coalbrookdale had become largely

[017-112]extended.  In 1784, when the government of the day proposed to levy a

[017-113]tax on pit-coal, Richard Reynolds strongly urged upon Mr. Pitt, then

[017-114]Chancellor of the Exchequer, as well as on Lord Gower, afterwards

[017-115]Marquis of Stafford, the impolicy of such a tax.  To the latter he

[017-116]represented that large capitals had been invested in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]],

[017-117]which was with difficulty carried on in the face of the competition

[017-118]with Swedish and Russian iron.  At Coalbrookdale, sixteen "fire

[017-119]engines," as steam engines were first called, were then at work, eight

[017-120]blast-furnaces and nine forges, besides the [[reverberatory-furnace|air furnace]]s and mills at

[017-121]the foundry, which, with the levels, roads, and more than twenty miles

[017-122]of iron railways, gave employment to a very large number of people.

[017-123]"The advancement of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] within these few years," said he,

[017-124]"has been prodigious.  It was thought, and justly, that the making of

[017-125]pig-iron with pit coal was a great acquisition to the country by saving

[017-126]the wood and supplying a material to manufactures, the production of

[017-127]which, by the consumption of all the wood the country produced, was

[017-128]formerly unequal to the demand, and the nail trade, perhaps the most

[017-129]considerable of any one article of manufactured iron, would have been

[017-130]lost to this country had it not been found practicable to make nails of

[017-131]iron made with pit coal.  We have now another process to attempt, and

[017-132]that is to make BAR IRON with pit coal; and it is for that purpose we

[017-133]have made, or rather are making, alterations at Donnington Wood,

[017-134][[ketley|Ketley]], and elsewhere, which we expect to complete in the present year,

[017-135]but not at a less expense than twenty thousand pounds, which will be

[017-136]lost to us, and gained by nobody, if this tax is laid upon our coals."

[017-137]He would not, however, have it understood that he sought for any

[017-138]PROTECTION for the homemade iron, notwithstanding the lower prices of

[017-139]the foreign article.  "From its most imperfect state as pig-iron," he

[017-140]observed to Lord [[sheffield|Sheffield]], "to its highest finish in the regulating

[017-141]springs of a watch, we have nothing to fear if the importation into

[017-142]each country should be permitted without duty."  We need scarcely add

[017-143]that the subsequent history of the iron trade abundantly justified

[017-144]these sagacious anticipations of Richard Reynolds.

[017-145]He was now far advanced in years.  His business had prospered, his

[017-146]means were ample, and he sought retirement.  He did not desire to

[017-147]possess great wealth, which in his opinion entailed such serious

[017-148]responsibilities upon its possessor; and he held that the accumulation

[017-149]of large property was more to be deprecated than desired.  He therefore

[017-150]determined to give up his shares in the ironworks at [[ketley|Ketley]] to his sons

[017-151]William and Joseph, who continued to carry them on.  William was a man

[017-152]of eminent ability, well versed in science, and an excellent mechanic.

[017-153]He introduced great improvements in the working of the coal and iron

[017-154]mines, employing new machinery for the purpose, and availing himself

[017-155]with much ingenuity of the discoveries then being made in the science

[017-156]of chemistry.  He was also an inventor, having been the first to employ

[017-157](in 1788) inclined planes, consisting of parallel railways, to connect

[017-158]and work canals of different levels,--an invention erroneously

[017-159]attributed to Fulton, but which the latter himself acknowledged to

[017-160]belong to [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]].  In the first chapter of his 'Treatise on

[017-161]Canal Navigation,' published in 1796, Fulton says:--"As local

[017-162]prejudices opposed the Duke of Bridgewater's canal in the first

[017-163]instance, prejudices equally strong as firmly adhered to the principle

[017-164]on which it was constructed; and it was thought impossible to lead one

[017-165]through a country, or to work it to any advantage, unless by locks and

[017-166]boats of at least twenty-five tons, till the genius of Mr. William

[017-167]Reynolds, of Ketley, in [[shropshire|Shropshire]], stepped from the accustomed path,

[017-168]constructed the first inclined plane, and introduced boats of five

[017-169]tons.  This, like the Duke's canal, was deemed a visionary project, and

[017-170]particularly by his Grace, who was partial to locks; yet this is also

[017-171]introduced into practice, and will in many instances supersede lock

[017-172]canals."  Telford, the engineer, also gracefully acknowledged the

[017-173]valuable assistance he received from [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] in planning the

[017-174]iron aqueduct by means of which the [[ellesmere-canal|Ellesmere Canal]] was carried over

[017-175]the Pont Cysylltau, and in executing the necessary castings for the

[017-176]purpose at the Ketley foundry.
