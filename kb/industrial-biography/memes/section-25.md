[025-001]In order to carry them on with the best chances of success, he brought

[025-002]a large number of skilled workmen from [[england|England]], who formed a nucleus of

[025-003]industry at Carron, where their example and improved methods of working

[025-004]served to train the native labourers in their art.  At a subsequent

[025-005]period, Mr. Cadell, of Carronpark, also brought a number of skilled

[025-006]English nail-makers into [[scotland|Scotland]], and settled them in the village of

[025-007]Camelon, where, by teaching others, the business has become handed down

[025-008]to the present day.

[025-009]The first furnace was blown at Carron on the first day of January,

[025-010]1760; and in the course of the same year the [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]] turned

[025-011]out 1500 tons of iron, then the whole annual produce of [[scotland|Scotland]].

[025-012]Other furnaces were shortly after erected on improved plans, and the

[025-013]production steadily increased.  [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] was indefatigable in his

[025-014]endeavours to improve the manufacture, and he was one of the first, as

[025-015]we have said, to revive the use of pit-coal in refining the ore, as

[025-016]appears from his patent of 1762.  He there describes his new process as

[025-017]follows:--"I melt pig or any kind of cast-iron in a hearth heated with

[025-018]pit-coal by the blast of bellows, and work the metal until it is

[025-019]reduced to nature, which I take out of the fire and separate to pieces;

[025-020]then I take the metal thus reduced to nature and expose it to the

[025-021]action of a hollow pit-coal fire, heated by the blast of bellows, until

[025-022]it is reduced to a loop, which I draw out under a common [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]]

[025-023]into bar-iron."  This method of manufacture was followed with success,

[025-024]though for some time, as indeed to this day, the principal production

[025-025]of the Carron Works was castings, for which the peculiar quality of the

[025-026]Scotch iron admirably adapts it.  The well-known Carronades,[2] or

[025-027]"Smashers," as they were named, were cast in large numbers at the

[025-028]Carron Works.  To increase the power of his blowing apparatus, Dr.

[025-029]Roebuck called to his aid the celebrated Mr. Smeaton, the engineer, who

[025-030]contrived and erected for him at Carron the most perfect apparatus of

[025-031]the kind then in existence.  It may also be added, that out of the

[025-032]Carron enterprise, in a great measure, sprang the Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]]

[025-033]Canal, the first artificial navigation in Scotland.  The Carron

[025-034]Company, with a view to securing an improved communication with

[025-035][[glasgow|Glasgow]], themselves surveyed a line, which was only given up in

[025-036]consequence of the determined opposition of the landowners; but the

[025-037]project was again revived through their means, and was eventually

[025-038]carried out after the designs of Smeaton and Brindley.

[025-039]While the Carron foundry was pursuing a career of safe prosperity, Dr.

[025-040]Roebuck's enterprise led him to embark in coal-mining, with the object

[025-041]of securing an improved supply of fuel for the iron works.  He became

[025-042]the lessee of the Duke of Hamilton's extensive coal-mines at

[025-043]Boroughstoness, as well as of the salt-pans which were connected with

[025-044]them.  The mansion of Kinneil went with the lease, and there Dr.

[025-045]Roebuck and his family took up their abode.  Kinneil House was formerly

[025-046]a country seat of the Dukes of Hamilton, and is to this day a stately

[025-047]old mansion, reminding one of a French chateau.  Its situation is of

[025-048]remarkable beauty, its windows overlooking the broad expanse of the

[025-049]Firth of Forth, and commanding an extensive view of the country along

[025-050]its northern shores.  The place has become in a measure classical,

[025-051]Kinneil House having been inhabited, since [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]]'s time, by

[025-052]Dugald Stewart, who there wrote his Philosophical Essays.[3]  When Dr.

[025-053]Roebuck began to sink for coal at the new mines, he found it necessary

[025-054]to erect pumping-machinery of the most powerful kind that could be

[025-055]contrived, in order to keep the mines clear of water.  For this purpose

[025-056]the Newcomen engine, in its then state, was found insufficient; and

[025-057]when Dr. Roebuck's friend, Professor Black, of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], informed him

[025-058]of a young man of his acquaintance, a mathematical instrument maker at

[025-059][[glasgow|Glasgow]], having invented a [[steam-engine]] calculated to work with

[025-060]increased power, speed, and economy, compared with Newcomen's; Dr.

[025-061]Roebuck was much interested, and shortly after entered into a

[025-062]correspondence with [[james-watt|James Watt]], the mathematical instrument maker

[025-063]aforesaid on the subject.  The Doctor urged that Watt, who, up to that

[025-064]time, had confined himself to models, should come over to Kinneil

[025-065]House, and proceed to erect a working; engine in one of the

[025-066]outbuildings.  The English workmen whom he had brought; to the Carron

[025-067]works would, he justly thought, give Watt a better chance of success

[025-068]with his engine than if made by the clumsy whitesmiths and blacksmiths

[025-069]of Glasgow, quite unaccustomed as they were to first-class work; and he

[025-070]proposed himself to cast the cylinders at Carron previous to Watt's

[025-071]intended visit to him at Kinneil.

[025-072]Watt paid his promised visit in May, 1768, and Roebuck was by this time

[025-073]so much interested in the invention, that the subject of his becoming a

[025-074]partner with Watt, with the object of introducing the engine into

[025-075]general use, was seriously discussed.  Watt had been labouring at his

[025-076]invention for several years, contending with many difficulties, but

[025-077]especially with the main difficulty of limited means.  He had borrowed

[025-078]considerable sums of money from Dr. Black to enable him to prosecute

[025-079]his experiments, and he felt the debt to hang like a millstone round

[025-080]his neck.  Watt was a sickly, fragile man, and a constant sufferer from

[025-081]violent headaches; besides he was by nature timid, desponding,

[025-082]painfully anxious, and easily cast down by failure.  Indeed, he was

[025-083]more than once on the point of abandoning his invention in despair.  On

[025-084]the other hand, Dr. Roebuck was accustomed to great enterprises, a bold

[025-085]and undaunted man, and disregardful of expense where he saw before him

[025-086]a reasonable prospect of success.  His reputation as a practical

[025-087]chemist and philosopher, and his success as the founder of the

[025-088]Prestonpans Chemical Works and of the [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]], justified the

[025-089]friends of Watt in thinking that he was of all men the best calculated

[025-090]to help him at this juncture, and hence they sought to bring about a

[025-091]more intimate connection between the two.  The result was that Dr.

[025-092]Roebuck eventually became a partner to the extent of two-thirds of the

[025-093]invention, took upon him the debt owing by Watt to Dr. Black amounting

[025-094]to about 1200L., and undertook to find the requisite money to protect

[025-095]the invention by means of a patent.  The necessary steps were taken

[025-096]accordingly and the patent right was secured by the beginning of 1769,

[025-097]though the perfecting of his model cost Watt much further anxiety and

[025-098]study.

[025-099]It was necessary for Watt occasionally to reside with Dr. Roebuck at

[025-100]Kinneil House while erecting his first engine there.  It had been

[025-101]originally intended to erect it in the neighbouring town of

[025-102]Boroughstoness, but as there might be prying eyes there, and Watt

[025-103]wished to do his work in privacy, determined "not to puff," he at

[025-104]length fixed upon an outhouse still standing, close behind the mansion,

[025-105]by the burnside in the glen, where there was abundance of water and

[025-106]secure privacy.  Watt's extreme diffidence was often the subject of

[025-107]remark at Dr. Roebuck's fireside.  To the Doctor his anxiety seemed

[025-108]quite painful, and he was very much disposed to despond under

[025-109]apparently trivial difficulties.  Roebuck's hopeful nature was his

[025-110]mainstay throughout.  Watt himself was ready enough to admit this; for,

[025-111]writing to his friend Dr. Small, he once said, "I have met with many

[025-112]disappointments; and I must have sunk under the burthen of them if I

[025-113]had not been supported by the friendship of Dr. Roebuck."

[025-114]But more serious troubles were rapidly accumulating upon Dr. Roebuck

[025-115]himself; and it was he, and not Watt, that sank under the burthen.  The

[025-116]progress of Watt's engine was but slow, and long before it could be

[025-117]applied to the pumping of Roebuck's mines, the difficulties of the

[025-118]undertaking on which he had entered overwhelmed him.  The opening out

[025-119]of the principal coal involved a very heavy outlay, extending over many

[025-120]years, during which he sank not only his own but his wife's fortune,

[025-121]and--what distressed him most of all--large sums borrowed from his

[025-122]relatives and friends, which he was unable to repay.  The consequence

[025-123]was, that he was eventually under the necessity of withdrawing his

[025-124]capital from the refining works at [[birmingham|Birmingham]], and the vitriol works at

[025-125]Prestonpans.  At the same time, he transferred to Mr. Boulton of Soho

[025-126]his entire interest in Watt's steam-engine, the value of which, by the

[025-127]way, was thought so small that it was not even included among the

[025-128]assets; Roebuck's creditors not estimating it as worth one farthing.

[025-129]Watt sincerely deplored his partner's misfortunes, but could not help

[025-130]him.  "He has been a most sincere and generous friend," said Watt, "and

[025-131]is a truly worthy man."  And again, "My heart bleeds for him, but I can

[025-132]do nothing to help him:  I have stuck by him till I have much hurt

[025-133]myself; I can do so no longer; my family calls for my care to provide

[025-134]for them."  The later years of Dr. Roebuck's life were spent in

[025-135]comparative obscurity; and he died in 1794, in his 76th year.

[025-136]He lived to witness the success of the steam-engine, the opening up of

[025-137]the Boroughstoness coal,[4] and the rapid extension of the Scotch iron

[025-138]trade, though he shared in the prosperity of neither of those branches

[025-139]of industry.  He had been working ahead of his age, and he suffered for

[025-140]it.  He fell in the breach at the critical moment, and more fortunate

[025-141]men marched over his body into the fortress which his enterprise and

[025-142]valour had mainly contributed to win.  Before his great undertaking of

[025-143]the Carron Works, Scotland was entirely dependent upon other countries

[025-144]for its supply of iron.  In 1760, the first year of its operations, the

[025-145]whole produce was 1500 tons.  In course of time other iron works were

[025-146]erected, at [[clyde|Clyde]] Cleugh, Muirkirk, and [[devon|Devon]]--the managers and

[025-147]overseers of which, as well as the workmen, had mostly received their

[025-148]training and experience at Carron--until at length the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of

[025-149]Scotland has assumed such a magnitude that its manufacturers are

[025-150]enabled to export to [[england|England]] and other countries upwards of 500,000

[025-151]tons a-year.  How different this state of things from the time when

[025-152]raids were made across the Border for the purpose of obtaining a store

[025-153]of iron plunder to be carried back into Scotland!

[025-154]The extraordinary expansion of the Scotch [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of late years has

[025-155]been mainly due to the discovery by [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] of the Black Band

[025-156]ironstone in 1801, and the invention of the [[hot-blast|Hot Blast]] by James Beaumont

[025-157]Neilson in 1828.  [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] was born at Dalkeith, near [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], in

[025-158]1772.[5]  Like other members of his family he was brought up to

[025-159]metal-founding.  At the age of nineteen he joined the staff of the

[025-160][[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]], near Glasgow, at a time when the Company had only two

[025-161]blast-furnaces at work.  The office of accountant, which he held,

[025-162]precluded him from taking any part in the manufacturing operations of

[025-163]the concern.  But being of a speculative and ingenious turn of mind,

[025-164]the remarkable conversions which iron underwent in the process of

[025-165]manufacture very shortly began to occupy his attention.  The subject

[025-166]was much discussed by the young men about the works, and they

[025-167]frequently had occasion to refer to Foureroy's well-known book for the

[025-168]purpose of determining various questions of difference which arose

[025-169]among them in the course of their inquiries.  The book was, however, in

[025-170]many respects indecisive and unsatisfactory; and, in 1793, when a

[025-171]reduction took place in the Company's staff, and David Mushet was left

[025-172]nearly the sole occupant of the office, he determined to study the

[025-173]subject for himself experimentally, and in the first place to acquire a

[025-174]thorough knowledge of assaying, as the true key to the whole art of

[025-175]iron-making.
