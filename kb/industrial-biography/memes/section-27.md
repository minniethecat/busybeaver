[027-001][3] Wilkie the painter once paid him a visit there while in [[scotland|Scotland]]

[027-002]studying the subject of his "Penny Wedding;" and Dugald Stewart found

[027-003]for him the old farm-house with the cradle-chimney, which he introduced

[027-004]in that picture.  But Kinneil House has had its imaginary inhabitants

[027-005]as well as its real ones, the ghost of a Lady Lilburn, once an occupant

[027-006]of the place, still "haunting" some of the unoccupied chambers.  Dugald

[027-007]Stewart told Wilkie one night, as he was going to bed, of the unearthly

[027-008]wailings which he himself had heard proceeding from the ancient

[027-009]apartments; but to him at least they had been explained by the door

[027-010]opening out upon the roof being blown in on gusty nights, when a

[027-011]jarring and creaking noise was heard all over the house.  One advantage

[027-012]derived from the house being "haunted" was, that the garden was never

[027-013]broken into, and the winter apples and stores were at all times kept

[027-014]safe from depredation in the apartments of the Lady Lilburn.

[027-015][4] [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] had been on the brink of great good fortune, but he did

[027-016]not know it.  Mr. Ralph Moore, in his "Papers on the Blackband

[027-017][[ironstone|Ironstone]]s" ([[glasgow|Glasgow]], 1861), observes:--"Strange to say, he was leaving

[027-018]behind him, almost as the roof of one of the seams of coal which he

[027-019]worked, a valuable blackband ironstone, upon which Kinneil Iron Works

[027-020]are now founded.  The coal-field continued to be worked until the

[027-021]accidental discovery of the blackband about 1845.  The old coal-pits

[027-022]are now used for working the ironstone."

[027-023][5] The Mushets are an old Kincardine family; but they were almost

[027-024]extinguished by the plague in the reign of Charles the Second.  Their

[027-025]numbers were then reduced to two; one of whom remained at Kincardine,

[027-026]and the other, a clergyman, the Rev. George Mushet, accompanied

[027-027]Montrose as chaplain.  He is buried in Kincardine churchyard.

[027-028][6] Papers on Iron and Steel.  By [[david-mushet|David Mushet]].  [[london|London]], 1840.

[027-029][7] This valuable description of iron ore was discovered by [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]],

[027-030]as he afterwards informs us (Papers on Iron and Steel, 121), in the

[027-031]year 1801, when crossing the river Calder, in the parish of Old

[027-032]Monkland.  Having subjected a specimen which he found in the river-bed

[027-033]to the test of his crucible, he satisfied himself as to its properties,

[027-034]and proceeded to ascertain its geological position and relations.  He

[027-035]shortly found that it belonged to the upper part of the coal-formation,

[027-036]and hence he designated it carboniferous ironstone.  He prosecuted his

[027-037]researches, and found various rich beds of the mineral distributed

[027-038]throughout the western counties of [[scotland|Scotland]].  On analysis, it was found

[027-039]to contain a little over 50 per cent.  of protoxide of iron.  The coaly

[027-040]matter it contained was not its least valuable ingredient; for by the

[027-041]aid of the hot blast it was afterwards found practicable to smelt it

[027-042]almost without any addition of coal.  Seams of black band have since

[027-043]been discovered and successfully worked in [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]]shire,

[027-044][[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], and North Wales.

[027-045]CHAPTER IX.

[027-046]INVENTION OF THE HOT BLAST--JAMES BEAUMONT NEILSON.

[027-047]"Whilst the exploits of the conqueror and the intrigues of the

[027-048]demagogue are faithfully preserved through a succession of ages, the

[027-049]persevering and unobtrusive efforts of genius, developing the best

[027-050]blessings of the Deity to man, are often consigned to oblivion."--David

[027-051]Mushet.

[027-052]The extraordinary value of the Black Band ironstone was not at first

[027-053]duly recognised, perhaps not even by Mr. Mushet himself.  For several

[027-054]years after its discovery by him, its use was confined to the Calder

[027-055]Iron Works, where it was employed in mixture with other ironstones of

[027-056]the argillaceous class.  It was afterwards partially used at the [[clyde|Clyde]]

[027-057]Iron Works, but nowhere else, a strong feeling of prejudice being

[027-058]entertained against it on the part of the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] generally.  It was

[027-059]not until the year 1825 that the Monkland Company used it alone,

[027-060]without any other mixture than the necessary quantity of limestone for

[027-061]a flux.  "The success of this Company," says Mr. Mushet, "soon gave

[027-062]rise to the Gartsherrie and Dundyvan furnaces, in the midst of which

[027-063]progress came the use of raw pit-coal and the [[hot-blast|Hot Blast]]--the latter one

[027-064]of the greatest discoveries in metallurgy of the present age, and,

[027-065]above every other process, admirably adapted for smelting the Blackband

[027-066]ironstone."  From the introduction of this process the extraordinary

[027-067]development of the iron-manufacture of Scotland may be said to date;

[027-068]and we accordingly propose to devote the present chapter to an account

[027-069]of its meritorious inventor.

[027-070][[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]] was born at Shettleston, a roadside village

[027-071]about three miles eastward of [[glasgow|Glasgow]], on the 22nd of June, 1792.  His

[027-072]parents belonged to the working class.  His father's earnings during

[027-073]many laborious years of his life did not exceed sixteen shillings a

[027-074]week.  He had been bred to the trade of a mill-wright, and was for some

[027-075]time in the employment of [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] as an engine-wright at his

[027-076][[coal-mining|colliery]] near Boroughstoness.  He was next employed in a like capacity

[027-077]by Mr. Beaumont, the mineral-manager of the collieries of Mrs.

[027-078]Cunningham of Lainshaw, near Irvine in Ayrshire; after which he was

[027-079]appointed engine-wright at Ayr, and subsequently at the Govan Coal

[027-080]Works near Glasgow, where he remained until his death.  It was while

[027-081]working at the Irvine Works that he first became acquainted with his

[027-082]future wife, Marion Smith, the daughter of a Renfrewshire bleacher, a

[027-083]woman remarkable through life for her clever, managing, and industrious

[027-084]habits.  She had the charge of Mrs. Cunningham's children for some time

[027-085]after the marriage of that lady to Mr. Beaumont, and it was in

[027-086]compliment to her former mistress and her husband that she named her

[027-087]youngest son James Beaumont after the latter.

[027-088]The boy's education was confined to the common elements of reading,

[027-089]writing, and arithmetic, which he partly acquired at the parish school

[027-090]of Strathbungo near Glasgow, and partly at the Chapel School, as it was

[027-091]called, in the Gorbals at Glasgow.  He had finally left school before

[027-092]he was fourteen.  Some time before he left, he had been partially set

[027-093]to work, and earned four shillings a week by employing a part of each

[027-094]day in driving a small condensing engine which his father had put up in

[027-095]a neighbouring quarry.  After leaving school, he was employed for two

[027-096]years as a gig boy on one of the winding engines at the Govan [[coal-mining|colliery]].

[027-097]His parents now considered him of fit age to be apprenticed to some

[027-098]special trade, and as Beaumont had much of his father's tastes for

[027-099]mechanical pursuits, it was determined to put him apprentice to a

[027-100]working engineer.  His elder brother John was then acting as engineman

[027-101]at Oakbank near Glasgow, and Beaumont was apprenticed under him to

[027-102]learn the trade.  John was a person of a studious and serious turn of

[027-103]mind, and had been strongly attracted to follow the example of the

[027-104]brothers Haldane, who were then exciting great interest by their

[027-105]preaching throughout the North; but his father set his face against his

[027-106]son's "preaching at the back o' dikes," as he called it; and so John

[027-107]quietly settled down to his work.  The engine which the two brothers

[027-108]managed was a very small one, and the master and apprentice served for

[027-109]engineman and fireman.  Here the youth worked for three years,

[027-110]employing his leisure hours in the evenings in remedying the defects of

[027-111]his early education, and endeavouring to acquire a knowledge of English

[027-112]grammar, drawing, and mathematics.

[027-113]On the expiry of his apprenticeship, Beaumont continued for a time to

[027-114]work under his brother as journeyman at a guinea a week; after which,

[027-115]in 1814, he entered the employment of William Taylor, coal-master at

[027-116]Irvine, and he was appointed engine-wright of the colliery at a salary

[027-117]of from 70L. to 80L. a year.  One of the improvements which he

[027-118]introduced in the working of the colliery, while he held that office,

[027-119]was the laying down of an edge railway of cast-iron, in lengths of

[027-120]three feet, from the pit to the harbour of Irvine, a distance of three

[027-121]miles.  At the age of 23 he married his first wife, Barbara

[027-122]Montgomerie, an Irvine lass, with a "tocher" of 250L.  This little

[027-123]provision was all the more serviceable to him, as his master, Taylor,

[027-124]becoming unfortunate in business, he was suddenly thrown out of

[027-125]employment, and the little fortune enabled the newly-married pair to

[027-126]hold their heads above water till better days came round.  They took a

[027-127]humble tenement, consisting of a room and a kitchen, in the Cowcaddens,

[027-128]Glasgow, where their first child was born.

[027-129][[About]] this time a gas-work, the first in Glasgow, was projected, and

[027-130]the company having been formed, the directors advertised for a

[027-131]superintendent and foreman, to whom they offered a "liberal salary."

[027-132]Though Beaumont had never seen gaslight before, except at the

[027-133]illumination of his father's colliery office after the Peace of Amiens,

[027-134]which was accomplished in a very simple and original manner, without

[027-135]either condenser, purifier, or gas-holder, and though he knew nothing

[027-136]of the art of gas-making, he had the courage to apply for the

[027-137]situation.  He was one of twenty candidates, and the fortunate one; and

[027-138]in August, 1817, we find him appointed foreman of the Glasgow Gasworks,

[027-139]for five years, at the salary of 90L. a year.  Before the expiry of his

[027-140]term he was reappointed for six years more, at the advanced salary of

[027-141]200L., with the status of manager and engineer of the works.  His

[027-142]salary was gradually increased to 400L. a year, with a free

[027-143]dwelling-house, until 1847, when, after a faithful service of thirty

[027-144]years, during which he had largely extended the central works, and

[027-145]erected branch works in Tradeston and Partick, he finally resigned the

[027-146]management.

[027-147]The situation of manager of the Glasgow Gas-works was in many respects

[027-148]well suited for the development of Mr. Neilson's peculiar abilities.

[027-149]In the first place it afforded him facilities for obtaining theoretical

[027-150]as well as practical knowledge in Chemical Science, of which he was a

[027-151]diligent student at the Andersonian University, as well as of Natural

[027-152]Philosophy and Mathematics in their higher branches.  In the next place

[027-153]it gave free scope for his ingenuity in introducing improvements in the

[027-154]manufacture of gas, then in its infancy.  He was the first to employ

[027-155]clay retorts; and he introduced sulphate of iron as a self-acting

[027-156]purifier, passing the gas through beds of charcoal to remove its oily

[027-157]and tarry elements.  The swallow-tail or union jet was also his

[027-158]invention, and it has since come into general use.

[027-159]While managing the Gas-works, one of Mr. Neilson's labours of love was

[027-160]the establishment and direction by him of a Workmen's Institution for

[027-161]mutual improvement.  Having been a workman himself, and experienced the

[027-162]disadvantages of an imperfect education in early life, as well as the

[027-163]benefits arising from improved culture in later years, he desired to

[027-164]impart some of these advantages to the workmen in his employment, who

[027-165]consisted chiefly of persons from remote parts of the Highlands or from

[027-166]Ireland.  Most of them could not even read, and his principal

[027-167]difficulty consisted in persuading them that it was of any use to

[027-168]learn.  For some time they resisted his persuasions to form a Workmen's

[027-169]Institution, with a view to the establishment of a library, classes,

[027-170]and lectures, urging as a sufficient plea for not joining it, that they

[027-171]could not read, and that books would be of no use to them.  At last Mr.

[027-172]Neilson succeeded, though with considerable difficulty, in inducing

[027-173]fourteen of the workmen to adopt his plan.  Each member was to

[027-174]contribute a small sum monthly, to be laid out in books, the Gas

[027-175]Company providing the members with a comfortable room in which they

[027-176]might meet to read and converse in the evenings instead of going to the

[027-177]alehouse.  The members were afterwards allowed to take the books home

[027-178]to read, and the room was used for the purpose of conversation on the

[027-179]subjects of the books read by them, and occasionally for lectures

[027-180]delivered by the members themselves on geography, arithmetic,

[027-181]chemistry, and mechanics.  Their numbers increased so that the room in

[027-182]which they met became insufficient for their accommodation, when the

[027-183]Gas Company provided them with a new and larger place of meeting,

[027-184]together with a laboratory and workshop.  In the former they studied

[027-185]practical chemistry, and in the latter they studied practical

[027-186]mechanics, making for themselves an air pump and an electrifying

[027-187]machine, as well as preparing the various models used in the course of

[027-188]the lectures.  The effects on the workmen were eminently beneficial,

[027-189]and the institution came to be cited as among the most valuable of its

[027-190]kind in the kingdom.[1]
