[024-001]Illustrations of a like kind might be multiplied to any extent, showing

[024-002]the growth in our own time of an iron aristocracy of great wealth and

[024-003]influence, the result mainly of the successful working of the

[024-004]inventions of the unfortunate and unrequited [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]].  He has been

[024-005]the very Tubal Cain of [[england|England]]--one of the principal founders of our

[024-006]iron age.  To him we mainly owe the abundance of wrought-iron for

[024-007]machinery, for [[steam-engine|steam-engine]]s, and for railways, at one-third the price

[024-008]we were before accustomed to pay to the foreigner.  We have by his

[024-009]invention, not only ceased to be dependent upon other nations for our

[024-010]supply of iron for tools, implements, and arms, but we have become the

[024-011]greatest exporters of iron, producing more than all other European

[024-012]countries combined.  In the opinion of Mr. Fairbairn of [[manchester|Manchester]], the

[024-013]inventions of [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] have already added six hundred millions

[024-014]sterling to the wealth of the kingdom, while they have given employment

[024-015]to some six hundred thousand working people during three generations.

[024-016]And while the great ironmasters, by freely availing themselves of his

[024-017]inventions, have been adding estate to estate, the only estate secured

[024-018]by Henry Cort was the little domain of six feet by two in which he lies

[024-019]interred in Hampstead Churchyard.

[024-099][11] [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] says of the early manufacture of iron at Merthyr Tydvil

[024-100]that "A modification of the charcoal refinery, a hollow fire, was

[024-101]worked with coke as a substitute for charcoal, but the bar-iron

[024-102]hammered from the produce was very inferior."  The pit-coal cast-iron

[024-107][12] It may be worthy of note that the first locomotive run upon a

[024-112]CHAPTER VIII.

[024-113]THE SCOTCH IRON MANUFACTURE--DR. ROEBUCK DAVID MUSHET.

[024-114]"Were public benefactors to be allowed to pass away, like hewers of

[024-115]wood and drawers of water, without commemoration, genius and enterprise

[024-116]would be deprived of their most coveted distinction."--Sir Henry

[024-117]Englefield.

[024-118]The account given of [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] in a Cyclopedia of Biography, recently

[024-119]published in [[glasgow|Glasgow]], runs as follows:--"Roebuck, John, a physician and

[024-120]experimental chemist, born at [[sheffield|Sheffield]], 1718; died, after ruining

[024-121]himself by his projects, 1794."  Such is the short shrift which the man

[024-122]receives who fails.  Had [[john-roebuck|Dr. Roebuck]] wholly succeeded in his projects,

[024-123]he would probably have been esteemed as among the greatest of

[024-124]Scotland's benefactors.  Yet his life was not altogether a failure, as

[024-125]we think will sufficiently appear from the following brief account of

[024-126]his labours:--

[024-127]At the beginning of last century, [[john-roebuck|John Roebuck]]'s father carried on the

[024-128]manufacture of cutlery at [[sheffield|Sheffield]],[1] in the course of which he

[024-129]realized a competency.  He intended his son to follow his own business,

[024-130]but the youth was irresistibly attracted to scientific pursuits, in

[024-131]which his father liberally encouraged him; and he was placed first

[024-132]under the care of Dr. Doddridge, at Northampton, and afterwards at the

[024-133]University of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], where he applied himself to the study of

[024-134]medicine, and especially of chemistry, which was then attracting

[024-135]considerable attention at the principal seats of learning in Scotland.

[024-136]While residing at [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] young Roebuck contracted many intimate

[024-137]friendships with men who afterwards became eminent in literature, such

[024-138]as Hume and Robertson the historians, and the circumstance is supposed

[024-139]to have contributed not a little to his partiality in favour of

[024-140]Scotland, and his afterwards selecting it as the field for his

[024-141]industrial operations.

[024-142]After graduating as a physician at Leyden, Roebuck returned to England,

[024-143]and settled at [[birmingham|Birmingham]] in the year 1745 for the purpose of

[024-144]practising his profession.  [[birmingham|Birmingham]] was then a principal seat of the

[024-145]metal manufacture, and its mechanics were reputed to be among the most

[024-146]skilled in Britain.  Dr. Roebuck's attention was early drawn to the

[024-147]scarcity and dearness of the material in which the mechanics worked,

[024-148]and he sought by experiment to devise some method of smelting iron

[024-149]otherwise than by means of charcoal.  He had a laboratory fitted up in

[024-150]his house for the purpose of prosecuting his inquiries, and there he

[024-151]spent every minute that he could spare from his professional labours.

[024-152]It was thus that he invented the process of smelting iron by means of

[024-153]pit-coal which he afterwards embodied in the patent hereafter to be

[024-154]referred to.  At the same time he invented new methods of refining gold

[024-155]and silver, and of employing them in the arts, which proved of great

[024-156]practical value to the Birmingham trades-men, who made extensive use of

[024-157]them in their various processes of manufacture.

[024-158]Dr. Roebuck's inquiries had an almost exclusively practical direction,

[024-159]and in pursuing them his main object was to render them subservient to

[024-160]the improvement of the industrial arts.  Thus he sought to devise more

[024-161]economical methods of producing the various chemicals used in the

[024-162]Birmingham trade, such as ammonia, sublimate, and several of the acids;

[024-163]and his success was such as to induce him to erect a large laboratory

[024-164]for their manufacture, which was conducted with complete success by his

[024-165]friend Mr. Garbett.  Among his inventions of this character, was the

[024-166]modern process of manufacturing vitriolic acid in leaden vessels in

[024-167]large quantities, instead of in glass vessels in small quantities as

[024-168]formerly practised.  His success led him to consider the project of

[024-169]establishing a manufactory for the purpose of producing oil of vitriol

[024-170]on a large scale; and, having given up his practice as a physician, he

[024-171]resolved, with his partner Mr. Garbett, to establish the proposed works

[024-172]in the neighbourhood of Edinburgh.  He removed to Scotland with that

[024-173]object, and began the manufacture of vitriol at Prestonpans in the year

[024-174]1749.  The enterprise proved eminently lucrative, and, encouraged by

[024-175]his success, Roebuck proceeded to strike out new branches of

[024-176]manufacture.  He started a pottery for making white and brown ware,

[024-177]which eventually became established, and the manufacture exists in the

[024-178]same neighbourhood to this day.

[024-179]The next enterprise in which he became engaged was one of still greater

[024-180]importance, though it proved eminently unfortunate in its results as

[024-181]concerned himself.  While living at Prestonpans, he made the friendship

[024-182]of Mr. [[william-cadell|William Cadell]], of Cockenzie, a gentleman who had for some time

[024-183]been earnestly intent on developing the industry of Scotland, then in a

[024-184]very backward condition.  Mr. Cadell had tried, without success, to

[024-185]establish a manufactory of iron; and, though he had heretofore failed,

[024-186]he hoped that with the aid of Dr. Roebuck he might yet succeed.  The

[024-187]Doctor listened to his suggestions with interest, and embraced the

[024-188]proposed enterprise with zeal.  He immediately proceeded to organize a

[024-189]company, in which he was joined by a number of his friends and

[024-190]relatives.  His next step was to select a site for the intended works,

[024-191]and make the necessary arrangements for beginning the manufacture of

[024-192]iron.  After carefully examining the country on both sides of the

[024-193]Forth, he at length made choice of a site on the banks of the river

[024-194]Carron, in Stirlingshire, where there was an abundant supply of wafer,

[024-195]and an inexhaustible supply of iron, coal, and limestone in the

[024-196]immediate neighbourhood, and there Dr. Roebuck planted the first

[024-197]ironworks in Scotland.
