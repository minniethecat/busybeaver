Andrew Yarranton (1616–1684) was an English ironmaster, soldier, and pioneering writer on political economy. Smiles describes him as "emphatically" a true patriot and "a man of public spirit and enterprise, an enlightened political economist long before political economy had been recognised as a science." (012-066) (012-073) His memoir forms a companion piece to that of [[Dud Dudley]] — both were [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]] ironmasters and soldiers, though Yarranton fought on the [[house-of-commons|Parliament]]ary side. (012-070) (012-071)

Bishop Watson declared that Yarranton "ought to have had a statue erected to his memory because of his eminent public services," and a modern writer has gone so far as to call him "the founder of English political economy, the first man in [[england|England]] who saw and said that peace was better than war, that trade was better than plunder, that honest industry was better than martial greatness." (012-075) (012-078) (012-079)

## Early Life

Andrew Yarranton was born at the farmstead of Larford, in the parish of Astley, [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]], in the year 1616. In his sixteenth year he was apprenticed to a [[worcestershire|Worcester]] linendraper, but the trade did not suit him. When the civil wars broke out, Yarranton took the side of [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] and joined their army, serving as a soldier. His zeal and abilities commended him to his officers, and within a few years he had risen to the rank of captain. "While a soldier," he wrote, "I had sometimes the honour and misfortune to lodge and dislodge an army." (012-087) (012-088) (012-091) (012-094) (012-096)

After the wars, Yarranton returned to [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]] and engaged in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]]. But his restless mind ranged far beyond manufacture. He became convinced that [[england|England]]'s economic prosperity depended on improved inland navigation — making rivers navigable so that coal, iron, and agricultural produce could move cheaply between districts. (014-104) (014-105)

## Career

### River Navigation Schemes

Yarranton's great project was the improvement of [[england|England]]'s rivers for navigation. He personally surveyed the principal rivers of [[england|England]] — the [[river-avon|Avon]], the [[severn|Severn]], the Wye, the Dee, and the [[thames|Thames]] — and published his findings in his book *[[england|England]]'s Improvement by Sea and Land* (1677). In this work he proposed making rivers navigable to connect the manufacturing districts with the sea, anticipating by more than a century the canal-building mania of the late eighteenth century.

His greatest practical achievement was making the river Stour navigable from [[stourbridge|Stourbridge]] to the [[severn|Severn]] at [[kidderminster|Kidderminster]], which opened the [[worcestershire|Worcestershire]] iron and coal districts to wider trade. He also made the river Avon navigable from [[bristol|Bristol]] to Stratford-upon-Avon.

### The Ironmaster and Economist

Yarranton was himself an ironmaster, familiar with the [[charcoal]] furnaces and forges of the West Midlands. He was among the first to recognise that the exhaustion of [[england|England]]'s forests posed a fundamental threat to the iron industry, and he advocated for the use of pit-coal in ironmaking decades before [[Abraham Darby I]]'s successful experiments at [[Coalbrookdale]].

His writings on political economy were remarkable for their time. Writing in an age dominated by mercantilist thinking, Yarranton argued that national wealth consisted not in the accumulation of gold but in the productive industry of the people. "Peace," he wrote, "is better than war; trade is better than plunder; honest industry is better than martial greatness." (012-079) (012-080)

> "Yet the name of Andrew Yarranton is scarcely remembered, or is at most known to only a few readers of half-forgotten books." (012-083)

## Influence and Legacy

Yarranton's vision of a prosperous industrial [[england|England]] connected by navigable waterways was in many respects a century ahead of its time. The canal age that began with the [[Bridgewater Canal]] in 1761 was the fulfilment of ideas Yarranton had first propounded in the 1670s. His philosophy that government's best occupation was "to secure prosperity at home, and let other nations alone" anticipated the free-trade arguments of [[Adam Smith]] by a century. (012-081)

Smiles presents Yarranton alongside [[Dud Dudley]] as one of the two great seventeenth-century ironmaster-memoirists: both men of action who understood that [[england|England]]'s industrial greatness would be built on iron, coal, and unfettered trade. (001-075)
