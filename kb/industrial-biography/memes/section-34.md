[034-001]Bramah continued his useful labours as an inventor for many years.  His

[034-002]study of the principles of [[hydraulic-power|hydraulics]], in the course of his invention

[034-003]of the press, enabled him to introduce many valuable improvements in

[034-004]pumping-machinery.  By varying the form of the piston and cylinder he

[034-005]was enabled to obtain a rotary motion,[3] which he advantageously

[034-006]applied to many purposes.  Thus he adopted it in the well known

[034-007]fire-engine, the use of which has almost become universal.  Another

[034-008]popular machine of his is the beer-pump, patented in 1797, by which the

[034-009]publican is enabled to raise from the casks in the cellar beneath, the

[034-010]various liquors sold by him over the counter.  He also took out several

[034-011]patents for the improvement of the [[steam-engine]], in which, however,

[034-012]Watt left little room for other inventors; and hence Bramah seems to

[034-013]have entertained a grudge against Watt, which broke out fiercely in the

[034-014]evidence given by him in the case of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] versus [[jonathan-hornblower|Hornblower]]

[034-015]and Maberly, tried in December 1796.  On that occasion his temper seems

[034-016]to have got the better of his judgment, and he was cut short by the

[034-017]judge in the attempt which he then made to submit the contents of the

[034-018]pamphlet subsequently published by him in the form of a letter to the

[034-019]judge before whom the case was tried.[4]  In that pamphlet he argued

[034-020]that Watt's specification had no definite meaning; that it was

[034-021]inconsistent and absurd, and could not possibly be understood; that the

[034-022]proposal to work steam-engines on the principle of condensation was

[034-023]entirely fallacious; that Watt's method of packing the piston was

[034-024]"monstrous stupidity;" that the engines of Newcomen (since entirely

[034-025]superseded) were infinitely superior, in all respects, to those of

[034-026]Watt;--conclusions which, we need scarcely say, have been refuted by

[034-027]the experience of nearly a century.

[034-028]On the expiry of [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]]'s patent, Bramah introduced several

[034-029]valuable improvements in the details of the condensing engine, which

[034-030]had by that time become an established power,--the most important of

[034-031]which was his "four-way cock," which he so arranged as to revolve

[034-032]continuously instead of alternately, thus insuring greater precision

[034-033]with considerably less wear of parts.  In the same patent by which he

[034-034]secured this invention in 1801, he also proposed sundry improvements in

[034-035]the boilers, as well as modifications in various parts of the engine,

[034-036]with the object of effecting greater simplicity and directness of

[034-037]action.

[034-038]In his patent of 1802, we find Bramah making another great stride in

[034-039]mechanical invention, in his tools "for producing straight, smooth, and

[034-040]parallel surfaces on wood and other materials requiring truth, in a

[034-041]manner much more expeditious and perfect than can be performed by the

[034-042]use of axes, saws, planes, and other cutting instruments used by hand

[034-043]in the ordinary way."  The specification describes the object of the

[034-044]invention to be the saving of manual labour, the reduction in the cost

[034-045]of production, and the superior character of the work executed.  The

[034-046]tools were fixed on frames driven by machinery, some moving in a rotary

[034-047]direction round an upright shaft, some with the shaft horizontal like

[034-048]an ordinary wood-turning lathe, while in others the tools were fixed on

[034-049]frames sliding in stationary grooves.  A wood-planing machine[5] was

[034-050]constructed on the principle of this invention at [[woolwich-arsenal|[[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]] Arsenal]],

[034-051]where it still continues in efficient use.  The axis of the principal

[034-052]shaft was supported on a piston in a vessel of oil, which considerably

[034-053]diminished the friction, and it was so contrived as to be accurately

[034-054]regulated by means of a small forcing-pump.  Although the machinery

[034-055]described in the patent was first applied to working on wood, it was

[034-056]equally applicable to working on metals; and in his own shops at

[034-057]Pimlico Bramah employed a machine with revolving cutters to plane

[034-058]metallic surfaces for his patent locks and other articles.  He also

[034-059]introduced a method of turning spherical surfaces, either convex or

[034-060]concave, by a tool moveable on an axis perpendicular to that of the

[034-061]lathe; and of cutting out concentric shells by fixing in a similar

[034-062]manner a curved tool of nearly the same form as that employed by common

[034-063]turners for making bowls.  "In fact," says Mr. Mallet, "Bramah not only

[034-064]anticipated, but carried out upon a tolerably large scale in his own

[034-065]works--for the construction of the patent hydraulic press, the

[034-066][[water-closet]], and his locks--a surprisingly large proportion of our

[034-067]modern tools." [6]  His remarkable predilection in favour of the use of

[034-068]hydraulic arrangements is displayed in his specification of the

[034-069]surface-planing machinery, which includes a method of running pivots

[034-070]entirely on a fluid, and raising and depressing them at pleasure by

[034-071]means of a small forcing-pump and stop-cock,--though we are not aware

[034-072]that any practical use has ever been made of this part of the invention.

[034-073]Bramah's inventive genius displayed itself alike in small things as in

[034-074]great--in a tap wherewith to draw a glass of beer, and in a hydraulic

[034-075]machine capable of tearing up a tree by the roots.  His powers of

[034-076]contrivance seemed inexhaustible, and were exercised on the most

[034-077]various subjects.  When any difficulty occurred which mechanical

[034-078]ingenuity was calculated to remove, recourse was usually had to Bramah,

[034-079]and he was rarely found at a loss for a contrivance to overcome it.

[034-080]Thus, when applied to by the [[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] in 1806, to construct a

[034-081]machine for more accurately and expeditiously printing the numbers and

[034-082]date lines on Bank notes, he at once proceeded to invent the requisite

[034-083]model, which he completed in the course of a month.  He subsequently

[034-084]brought it to great perfection the figures in numerical succession

[034-085]being changed by the action of the machine itself,--and it still

[034-086]continues in regular use.  Its employment in the Bank of [[england|England]] alone

[034-087]saved the labour of a hundred clerks; but its chief value consisted in

[034-088]its greater accuracy, the perfect legibility of the figures printed by

[034-089]it, and the greatly improved check which it afforded.

[034-090]We next find him occupying himself with inventions connected with the

[034-091]manufacture of pens and paper.  His little pen-making machine for

[034-092]readily making quill pens long continued in use, until driven out by

[034-093]the invention of the steel pen; but his patent for making paper by

[034-094]machinery, though ingenious, like everything he did, does not seem to

[034-095]have been adopted, the inventions of Fourdrinier and Donkin in this

[034-096]direction having shortly superseded all others.  Among his other minor

[034-097]inventions may be mentioned his improved method of constructing and

[034-098]sledging carriage-wheels, and his improved method of laying

[034-099]water-pipes.  In his specification of the last-mentioned invention, he

[034-100]included the application of water-power to the driving of machinery of

[034-101]every description, and for hoisting and lowering goods in docks and

[034-102]warehouses,--since carried out in practice, though in a different

[034-103]manner, by [[william-armstrong|Sir William Armstrong]].[7]  In this, as in many other

[034-104]matters, Bramah shot ahead of the mechanical necessities of his time;

[034-105]and hence many of his patents (of which he held at one time more than

[034-106]twenty) proved altogether profitless.  His last patent, taken out in

[034-107]1814, was for the application of Roman cement to timber for the purpose

[034-108]of preventing dry rot.

[034-109]Besides his various mechanical pursuits, Bramah also followed to a

[034-110]certain extent the profession of a civil engineer, though his more

[034-111]urgent engagements rendered it necessary for him to refuse many

[034-112]advantageous offers of employment in this line.  He was, however, led

[034-113]to carry out the new water-works at Norwich, between the years 1790 and

[034-114]1793, in consequence of his having been called upon to give evidence in

[034-115]a dispute between the corporation of that city and the lessees, in the

[034-116]course of which he propounded plans which, it was alleged, could not be

[034-117]carried out.  To prove that they could be carried out, and that his

[034-118]evidence was correct, he undertook the new works, and executed them

[034-119]with complete success; besides demonstrating in a spirited publication

[034-120]elicited by the controversy, the insufficiency and incongruity of the

[034-121]plans which had been submitted by the rival engineer.

[034-122]For some time prior to his death Bramah had been employed in the

[034-123]erection of several large machines in his works at Pimlico for sawing

[034-124]stone and timber, to which he applied his hydraulic power with great

[034-125]success.  New methods of building bridges and canal-locks, with a

[034-126]variety of other matters, were in an embryo state in his mind, but he

[034-127]did not live to complete them.  He was occupied in superintending the

[034-128]action of his [[hydraulic-press|hydrostatic press]] at Holt Forest, in Hants--where upwards

[034-129]of 300 trees of the largest dimensions were in a very short time torn

[034-130]up by the roots,--when he caught a severe cold, which settled upon his

[034-131]lungs, and his life was suddenly brought to a close on the 9th of

[034-132]December, 1814, in his 66th year.

[034-133]His friend, Dr. Cullen Brown,[8] has said of him, that Bramah was a man

[034-134]of excellent moral character, temperate in his habits, of a pious turn

[034-135]of mind,[9] and so cheerful in temperament, that he was the life of

[034-136]every company into which he entered.  To much facility of expression he

[034-137]added the most perfect independence of opinion; he was a benevolent and

[034-138]affectionate man; neat and methodical in his habits, and knew well how

[034-139]to temper liberality with economy.  Greatly to his honour, he often

[034-140]kept his workmen employed, solely for their sake, when stagnation of

[034-141]trade prevented him disposing of the products of their labour.  As a

[034-142]manufacturer he was distinguished for his promptitude and probity, and

[034-143]he was celebrated for the exquisite finish which he gave to all his

[034-144]productions.  In this excellence of workmanship, which he was the first

[034-145]to introduce, he continued while he lived to be unrivalled.

[034-146]Bramah was deservedly honoured and admired as the first mechanical

[034-147]genius of his time, and as the founder of the art of tool-making in its

[034-148]highest branches.  From his shops at Pimlico came [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]],

[034-149][[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]], and many more first-class mechanics, who carried the

[034-150]mechanical arts to still higher perfection, and gave an impulse to

[034-151]mechanical engineering, the effects of which are still felt in every

[034-152]branch of industry.

[034-153]The parish to which Bramah belonged was naturally proud of the

[034-154]distinction he had achieved in the world, and commemorated his life and

[034-155]career by a marble tablet erected by subscription to his memory, in the

[034-156]parish church of Silkstone.  In the churchyard are found the tombstones

[034-157]of Joseph's father, brother, and other members of the family; and we

[034-158]are informed that their descendants still occupy the farm at

[034-159]Stainborough on which the great mechanician was born.

[034-160][1] The lock invented by Bramah was patented in 1784.  Mr. Bramah

[034-161]himself fully set forth the specific merits of the invention in his

[034-162]Dissertation on the Construction of Locks.  In a second patent, taken

[034-163]out by him in 1798, he amended his first with the object of preventing

[034-164]the counterfeiting of keys, and suspending the office of the lock until

[034-165]the key was again in the possession of the owner.  This he effected by

[034-166]enabling the owner so to alter the sliders as to render the lock

[034-167]inaccessible to such key if applied by any other person but himself, or

[034-168]until the sliders had been rearranged so as to admit of its proper

[034-169]action.  We may mention in passing that the security of Bramah's locks

[034-170]depends on the doctrine of combinations, or multiplication of numbers

[034-171]into each other, which is known to increase in the most rapid

[034-172]proportion.  Thus, a lock of five slides admits of 3,000 variations,

[034-173]while one of eight will have no less than 1,935,360 changes; in other

[034-174]words, that number of attempts at making a key, or at picking it, may

[034-175]be made before it can be opened.

[034-177]1144 tons.

[034-178][3] Dr. Thomas Young, in his article on Bramah in the Encyclopaedia

[034-179]Britannica, describes the "rotative principle" as consisting in making

[034-180]the part which acts immediately on the water in the form of a slider,

[034-181]"sweeping round a cylindrical cavity, and kept in its place by means of

[034-182]an eccentric groove; a contrivance which was probably Bramah's own

[034-183]invention, but which had been before described, in a form nearly

[034-184]similar, by Ramelli, Canalleri, Amontons, Prince Rupert, and Dr. Hooke.
