# Grooved Rollers

[[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]'s grooved rollers (1784) — the innovation that turned puddled iron into finished [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] with unprecedented speed and consistency, completing the integrated coke-based iron production system that powered the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Background

Before Cort's rollers, [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] was shaped exclusively under [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]]s. A workman manipulated the hot iron bloom under repeated hammer blows, gradually working it into a bar of roughly square or flat section. This was slow — a skilled forge crew could produce a few hundredweight of [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] per day — and the resulting bars varied in dimension and straightness. The manual hammer method could not meet the growing demand for [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] from nail-makers, wire-drawers, and structural ironworkers.

[[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] recognised that rolling — passing hot iron between driven cylinders — could form bars faster and more uniformly than forging. But plain cylindrical rolls could only produce flat sheets. To make bars of specific cross-section (round, square, flat, angle), the rolls needed profiled grooves.

## Inventor

- **Inventor**: [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] (1740–1800)
- **Patent**: 1784 (British patent, same year as his [[puddling-process|puddling]] patent)
- **Works**: [[fontley-forge|Fontley Forge]], near Fareham, Hampshire
- **Partners**: Samuel Jellicoe (financial backer), Adam Jellicoe

## How It Works

Cort's rolling mill consisted of two heavy iron rolls mounted one above the other in a sturdy cast-iron frame. The rolls were powered by a water wheel (later a steam engine) and rotated in opposite directions. The key innovation was the **grooved periphery**: each roll had a series of gradually reducing grooves cut around its circumference.

The process operated as follows:

1. The hot puddled iron bloom was fed into the largest groove, where the rolls gripped it and reduced its cross-section
2. The bar was then passed through successively smaller grooves, each pass reducing the section further and bringing it closer to final dimensions
3. The final pass through the finishing groove produced a bar of precise cross-section — round, square, flat, or angle — with a smooth surface and uniform dimensions

A single rolling mill with a crew of three or four men could produce as much [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] in an hour as a [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]] crew could produce in a day. The "same methods of manufacturing bar from cast-iron, and of [[puddling-process|puddling]], piling, welding, and working the bar-iron through grooved rollers" remained standard practice for over a century. (022-042)

## Significance

Cort's grooved rollers, combined with his [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] furnace]], formed a complete system: blast furnace → pig iron → [[puddling-process|puddling furnace]] → wrought iron bloom → grooved rollers → finished bar. This integrated system:

- Reduced the cost of [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] by more than 60%
- Produced bars of consistent cross-section for the first time
- Enabled production of specialised sections (rails, angles, tees) for railways and construction
- Made Britain the world's dominant iron producer

Cort's work was so significant that a parliamentary committee declared the two inventions — [[puddling-process|puddling]] and grooved rollers — "so novel in their principle or their application as fairly to entitle the petitioners to a parliamentary reward." (024-074)

## Later Improvements

- **John Birkinshaw** (1820): Invented the first rail rolling method, producing wrought-iron rails directly
- Grooved roll design evolved into the complex roll pass sequences used in modern rolling mills for rails, beams, and structural sections
