[[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]]'s steam hammer (1839) was the most versatile forging tool of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

It could crack the shell of an egg placed in a wineglass without breaking the glass, yet deliver a blow of five tons with crushing force.

The hammer solved the central problem of heavy engineering: how to forge the massive iron shafts, paddle-wheels, and armour plates demanded by the age of [[Iron Shipbuilding|iron steamships]] and railways.

Along with the [[Hydraulic Press]] and the [[Slide Rest]], the steam hammer stands as one of the defining machine-tool inventions of the nineteenth century.

## Background: The [[forging|Forging]] Problem

Before the steam hammer, large iron forgings presented an intractable difficulty.

Water-powered [[tilt-hammer|helve hammer]]s could deliver heavy blows but offered almost no control: a 5-ton shaft required the same force to shape as a small connecting rod.

Hand forging gave control but no power. Neither method could produce the 30-inch-diameter paddle shaft required for Brunel's SS *Great Britain*, then the largest steamship planned.

[[Isambard Kingdom Brunel|Brunel]] could find no forge in Britain capable of the task.

> "The critical need came from marine engineering: the paddle shafts for the Great Britain were too large for any existing hammer to forge." (002-120)

[[James Nasmyth]], trained in [[Henry Maudslay]]'s [[london|London]] workshop and established at [[Patricroft Foundry]] near [[Manchester]], saw the problem and designed a solution of elegant simplicity.

## How It Works

Nasmyth's steam hammer operates on a principle as straightforward as it is effective.

A heavy iron block (the *tup* or hammer-head) is attached to a piston rod extending into a vertical steam cylinder mounted directly above the anvil.

Steam admitted above the piston drives the hammer down with combined steam pressure and gravity; steam admitted below the piston lifts it back for the next stroke.

The operator controls a valve that proportions the steam, determining the force of each blow — from a tap light enough to crack an egg to a five-ton strike.

Nasmyth's key innovation was the **self-acting valve gear**, which allowed the hammer to strike repeated blows automatically while the forge crew manoeuvred the hot workpiece into position.

This freed smiths from having to trip each blow manually, a vast improvement over earlier trip-hammers.

The hammer frame was a massive cast-iron arch, with an anvil block set deep into the foundry floor on timber piles to absorb repeated shocks.

## Impact on Industry

The steam hammer transformed heavy forging across multiple industries:

| Industry | Application |
|----------|------------|
| Marine Engineering | Paddle shafts, screw shafts, stern frames for [[iron-shipbuilding|iron steamships]] |
| Railways | [[locomotive|Locomotive]] axles, crankshafts, coupling rods |
| Armaments | Wrought-iron armour plate for warships, gun barrels |
| Construction | Bridge components, structural ironwork |

Brunel's SS *Great Britain* was completed using Nasmyth hammers.

The invention enabled the forging of wrought-iron components on a scale previously impossible, directly supporting the growth of [[Iron Shipbuilding]] that [[William Fairbairn]] and others pioneered.

Like the [[self-acting mule]] in [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]], the steam hammer was adopted by manufacturers "only when compelled to do so by strikes" of skilled forge workers. (051-008)

## Later Improvements

Nasmyth's original single-acting design was improved and extended by several engineers.

[[james-watt|James Watt]] & Co. of [[Birmingham]] developed the double-acting hammer, using steam both above and below the piston for greater control.

Francis Humphreys produced large steam hammers for the [[woolwich-arsenal|Royal Arsenal]] at [[woolwich|Woolwich]].
Schneider & Co. of Le Creusot, France, built the largest hammer of the era — a 100-ton monster — under licence from Nasmyth.

Nasmyth himself manufactured steam hammers at Patricroft for over twenty years, exporting them to workshops and shipyards across Europe and North America.

The largest examples delivered blows exceeding 100 tons and remained in productive use well into the twentieth century.
