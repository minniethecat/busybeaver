[008-001]Many new men also became enriched, and founded county families; the

[008-002]Fuller family frankly avowing their origin in the singular motto of

[008-003]Carbone et forcipibus--literally, by charcoal and tongs.[10]

[008-004]Men then went into [[sussex|Sussex]] to push their fortunes at the forges, as they

[008-005]now do in Wales or [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]]; and they succeeded then, as they do

[008-006]now, by dint of application, industry, and energy.  The [[sussex|Sussex]]

[008-007]Archaeological Papers for 1860 contain a curious record of such an

[008-008]adventurer, in the history of the founder of the Gale family.  Leonard

[008-009]Gale was born in 1620 at Riverhead, near Sevenoaks, where his father

[008-010]pursued the trade of a blacksmith.  When the youth had reached his

[008-011]seventeenth year, his father and mother, with five of their sons and

[008-012]daughters, died of the plague, Leonard and his brother being the only

[008-013]members of the family that survived.  The patrimony of 200L. left them

[008-014]was soon spent; after which Leonard paid off his servants, and took to

[008-015]work diligently at his father's trade.  Saving a little money, he

[008-016]determined to go down into Sussex, where we shortly find him working

[008-017]the St.  Leonard's Forge, and afterwards the Tensley Forge near

[008-018]Crawley, and the Cowden Iron-works, which then bore a high reputation.

[008-019]After forty years' labour, he accumulated a good fortune, which he left

[008-020]to his son of the same name, who went on iron-forging, and eventually

[008-021]became a county gentleman, owner of the house and estate of Crabbett

[008-022]near Worth, and Member of [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] for East Grinstead.

[008-023]Several of the new families, however, after occupying a high position

[008-024]in the county, again subsided into the labouring class, illustrating

[008-025]the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] proverb of "Twice clogs, once boots," the sons

[008-026]squandering what the father's had gathered, and falling back into the

[008-027]ranks again.  Thus the great Fowles family of Riverhall disappeared

[008-028]altogether from Sussex.  One of them built the fine mansion of

[008-029]Riverhall, noble even in decay.  Another had a grant of free warren

[008-030]from King James over his estates in Wadhurst, Frant, Rotherfield, and

[008-031]Mayfield.  Mr. Lower says the fourth in descent from this person kept

[008-032]the turnpike-gate at Wadhurst, and that the last of the family, a

[008-033]day-labourer, emigrated to America in 1839, carrying with him, as the

[008-034]sole relic of his family greatness, the royal grant of free warren

[008-035]given to his ancestor.  The Barhams and Mansers were also great

[008-036]iron-men, officiating as high sheriffs of the county at different

[008-037]times, and occupying spacious mansions.  One branch of these families

[008-038]terminated, Mr. Lower says, with Nicholas Barham, who died in the

[008-039]workhouse at Wadhurst in 1788; and another continues to be represented

[008-040]by a wheelwright at Wadhurst of the same name.

[008-041]The [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]] of Sussex reached its height towards the close of

[008-042]the reign of Elizabeth, when the trade became so prosperous that,

[008-043]instead of importing iron, [[england|England]] began to export it in considerable

[008-044]quantities, in the shape of iron ordnance.  Sir Thomas Leighton and Sir

[008-045]Henry Neville had obtained patents from the queen, which enabled them

[008-046]to send their ordnance abroad, the consequence of which was that the

[008-047]Spaniards were found arming their ships and fighting us with guns of

[008-048]our own manufacture.  Sir Walter Raleigh, calling attention to the

[008-049]subject in the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]], said, "I am sure heretofore one ship

[008-050]of Her Majesty's was able to beat ten Spaniards, but now, by reason of

[008-051]our own ordnance, we are hardly matcht one to one."  Proclamations were

[008-052]issued forbidding the export of iron and brass ordnance, and a bill was

[008-053]brought into [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] to put a stop to the trade; but, not

[008-054]withstanding these prohibitions, the Sussex guns long continued to be

[008-055]smuggled out of the country in considerable numbers.  "It is almost

[008-056]incredible," says Camden, "how many guns are made of the iron in this

[008-057]county.  Count Gondomar (the Spanish ambassador) well knew their

[008-058]goodness when he so often begged of King James the boon to export

[008-059]them."  Though the king refused his sanction, it appears that Sir

[008-060]Anthony Shirley of Weston, an extensive iron-master, succeeded in

[008-061]forwarding to the King of Spain a hundred pieces of cannon.

[008-062]So active were the Sussex manufacturers, and so brisk was the trade

[008-063]they carried on, that during the reign of James I.  it is supposed

[008-064]one-half of the whole quantity of iron produced in [[england|England]] was made

[008-065]there.  Simon Sturtevant, in his 'Treatise of Metallica,' published in

[008-066]1612, estimates the whole number of iron-mills in England and Wales at

[008-067]800, of which, he says, "there are foure hundred milnes in Surry, [[kent|Kent]],

[008-068]and Sussex, as the townsmen of Haslemere have testified and numbered

[008-069]unto me."  But the townsmen of Haslemere must certainly have been

[008-070]exaggerating, unless they counted smiths' and farriers' shops in the

[008-071]number of iron-mills.  [[About]] the same time that Sturtevant's treatise

[008-072]was published, there appeared a treatise entitled the 'Surveyor's

[008-073]Dialogue,' by one John Norden, the object of which was to make out a

[008-074]case against the iron-works and their being allowed to burn up the

[008-075]timber of the country for fuel.  Yet Norden does not make the number of

[008-076]iron-works much more than a third of Sturtevant's estimate.  He says,

[008-077]"I have heard that there are or lately were in Sussex neere 140 hammers

[008-078]and furnaces for iron, and in it and [[surrey|Surrey]] adjoining three or four

[008-079]glasse-houses."  Even the smaller number stated by Norden, however,

[008-080]shows that Sussex was then regarded as the principal seat of the

[008-081]iron-trade.  Camden vividly describes the noise and bustle of the

[008-082]manufacture--the working of the heavy hammers, which, "beating upon the

[008-083]iron, fill the neighbourhood round about, day and night, with continual

[008-084]noise."  These hammers were for the most part worked by the power of

[008-085]water, carefully stored in the artificial "Hammer-ponds" above

[008-086]described.  The hammer-shaft was usually of ash, about 9 feet long,

[008-087]clamped at intervals with iron hoops.  It was worked by the revolutions

[008-088]of the water-wheel, furnished with projecting arms or knobs to raise

[008-089]the hammer, which fell as each knob passed, the rapidity of its action

[008-090]of course depending on the velocity with which the water-wheel

[008-091]revolved.  The forge-blast was also worked for the most part by

[008-092]water-power.  Where the furnaces were small, the blast was produced by

[008-093]leather bellows worked by hand, or by a horse walking in a gin.  The

[008-094]foot-blasts of the earlier iron-smelters were so imperfect that but a

[008-095]small proportion of the ore was reduced, so that the iron-makers of

[008-096]later times, more particularly in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], instead of

[008-097]digging for ironstone, resorted to the beds of ancient scoriae for

[008-098]their principal supply of the mineral.

[008-099]Notwithstanding the large number of furnaces in blast throughout the

[008-100]county of Sussex at the period we refer to, their produce was

[008-101]comparatively small, and must not be measured by the enormous produce

[008-102]of modern iron-works; for while an iron-furnace of the present day will

[008-103]easily turn out 150 tons of pig per week, the best of the older

[008-104]furnaces did not produce more than from three to four tons.  One of the

[008-105]last extensive contracts executed in Sussex was the casting of the iron

[008-106]rails which enclose St.  Paul's Cathedral.  The contract was thought

[008-107]too large for one iron-master to undertake, and it was consequently

[008-108]distributed amongst several contractors, though the principal part of

[008-109]the work was executed at Lamberhurst, near Tunbridge Wells.  But to

[008-110]produce the comparatively small quantity of iron turned out by the old

[008-111]works, the consumption of timber was enormous; for the making of every

[008-112]ton of pig-iron required four loads of timber converted into charcoal

[008-113]fuel, and the making of every ton of bar-iron required three additional

[008-114]loads.  Thus, notwithstanding the indispensable need of iron, the

[008-115]extension of the manufacture, by threatening the destruction of the

[008-116]timber of the southern counties, came to be regarded in the light of a

[008-117]national calamity.  Up to a certain point, the clearing of the Weald of

[008-118]its dense growth of underwood had been of advantage, by affording

[008-119]better opportunities for the operations of agriculture.  But the

[008-120]"voragious iron-mills" were proceeding to swallow up everything that

[008-121]would burn, and the old forest growths were rapidly disappearing.  An

[008-122]entire wood was soon exhausted, and long time was needed before it grew

[008-123]again.  At Lamberhurst alone, though the produce was only about five

[008-124]tons of iron a-week, the annual consumption of wood was about 200,000

[008-125]cords!  Wood continued to be the only material used for fuel

[008-126]generally--a strong prejudice existing against the use of sea-coal for

[008-127]domestic purposes.[11]  It therefore began to be feared that there

[008-128]would be no available fuel left within practicable reach of the

[008-129]metropolis; and the contingency of having to face the rigorous cold of

[008-130]an English winter without fuel naturally occasioning much alarm, the

[008-131]action of the Government was deemed necessary to remedy the apprehended

[008-132]evil.

[008-133]To check the destruction of wood near [[london|London]], an Act was passed in 1581

[008-134]prohibiting its conversion into fuel for the making of iron within

[008-135]fourteen miles of the [[thames|Thames]], forbidding the erection of new ironworks

[008-136]within twenty-two miles of [[london|London]], and restricting the number of works

[008-137]in [[kent|Kent]], [[surrey|Surrey]], and Sussex, beyond the above limits.  Similar

[008-138]enactments were made in future Parliaments with the same object, which

[008-139]had the effect of checking the trade, and several of the Sussex

[008-140]ironmasters were under the necessity of removing their works elsewhere.

[008-141]Some of them migrated to [[glamorgan|Glamorganshire]], in [[south-wales|South Wales]], because of the

[008-142]abundance of timber as well as ironstone in that quarter, and there set

[008-143]up their forges, more particularly at [[aberdare|Aberdare]] and Merthyr Tydvil.  Mr.

[008-144]Llewellin has recently published an interesting account of their

[008-145]proceedings, with descriptions of their works,[12] remains of which

[008-146]still exist at Llwydcoed, Pontyryns, and other places in the [[aberdare|Aberdare]]

[008-147]valley.  Among the Sussex masters who settled in [[glamorgan|Glamorganshire]] for the

[008-148]purpose of carrying on the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]], were Walter Burrell, the

[008-149]friend of John Ray, the naturalist, one of the Morleys of Glynde in

[008-150]Sussex, the Relfes from Mayfield, and the Cheneys from Crawley.

[008-151]Notwithstanding these migrations of enterprising manufacturers, the

[008-152][[iron-industry|iron trade]] of Sussex continued to exist until the middle of the

[008-153]seventeenth century, when the waste of timber was again urged upon the

[008-154]attention of Parliament, and the penalties for infringing the statutes

[008-155]seem to have been more rigorously enforced.  The trade then suffered a

[008-156]more serious check; and during the civil wars, a heavy blow was given

[008-157]to it by the destruction of the works belonging to all royalists, which

[008-158]was accomplished by a division of the army under Sir William Waller.

[008-159]Most of the Welsh ironworks were razed to the ground about the same

[008-160]time, and were not again rebuilt.  And after the Restoration, in 1674,

[008-161]all the royal ironworks in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] were demolished, leaving

[008-162]only such to be supplied with ore as were beyond the forest limits; the

[008-163]reason alleged for this measure being lest the iron manufacture should

[008-164]endanger the supply of timber required for shipbuilding and other

[008-165]necessary purposes.

[008-166]From this time the iron manufacture of Sussex, as of England generally,

[008-167]rapidly declined.  In 1740 there were only fifty-nine furnaces in all

[008-168]England, of which ten were in Sussex; and in 1788 there were only two.

[008-169]A few years later, and the Sussex iron furnaces were blown out

[008-170]altogether.  Farnhurst, in western, and Ashburnham, in eastern Sussex,

[008-171]witnessed the total extinction of the manufacture.  The din of the iron

[008-172]hammer was hushed, the glare of the furnace faded, the last blast of

[008-173]the bellows was blown, and the district returned to its original rural

[008-174]solitude.  Some of the furnace-ponds were drained and planted with hops

[008-175]or willows; others formed beautiful lakes in retired pleasure-grounds;

[008-176]while the remainder were used to drive flour-mills, as the streams in

[008-177]North Kent, instead of driving fulling-mills, were employed to work

[008-178]paper-mills.  All that now remains of the old iron-works are the

[008-179]extensive beds of cinders from which material is occasionally taken to

[008-180]mend the Sussex roads, and the numerous furnace-ponds, hammer-posts,

[008-181]forges, and cinder places, which mark the seats of the ancient

[008-182]manufacture.
