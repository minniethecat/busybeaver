Archibald Cochrane, 9th Earl of Dundonald (1748–1831), was a Scottish nobleman and inventor whose discovery of an economical method for producing [[coke]] from coal contributed to the development of the iron-smelting industry. (021-164)

## Coke-Making Discovery

Lord Dundonald's principal contribution to industrial technology was his discovery of a process for making coke from coal at significantly reduced cost, approximately half the prevailing price. (021-164)

This innovation was noted by [[Samuel Smiles]] alongside the work of [[Matthew Boulton]] and [[James Watt]] as one of the key technical advances that supported the growth of iron-making in Britain.

The use of coke as a substitute for charcoal in iron-smelting had been a critical breakthrough pioneered by [[Abraham Darby I]] at [[Coalbrookdale]] early in the eighteenth century. (015-129) The availability of inexpensive coke was essential to the expansion of blast furnace operations, freeing ironmasters from dependence on dwindling supplies of charcoal derived from wood. (015-132)

## Legacy

Dundonald's process for cheaper coke production addressed one of the key cost constraints facing the iron industry in the late eighteenth century. By reducing the fuel cost barrier, his discovery contributed to the economic viability of [[coke-smelting|coke-fired smelting]] at a time when the industry was rapidly expanding to meet growing demand from railways, shipbuilding, and construction.
