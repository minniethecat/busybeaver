Matthew Murray (1765–1826) was a [[leeds|Leeds]] engineer and machine-tool builder who contributed to the development of the planing machine and early [[locomotive|steam locomotive]]s.

He was one of the leading mechanical engineers of the northern manufacturing towns during the early 19th century, alongside figures such as [[james-fox|James Fox]] of [[derby|Derby]], [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]], and [[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]] of [[manchester|Manchester]].

## Early Career

Matthew Murray was born at [[stockton-on-tees|[[stockton-on-tees|Stockton]]-on-Tees]] in 1765. (045-114) He began his working life as a blacksmith, employed by [[john-marshall|Marshall]] of Leeds, the prominent flax-spinner. His mechanical aptitude soon became evident, and he progressed from smith's work to the design and construction of machine tools for the textile industry. (046-005)

Murray was employed by Messrs. [[adam-murray|Adam Murray]] and Co. before establishing his own engineering works. (002-170) His early experience in the textile machinery trade of [[leeds|Leeds]] gave him a practical grounding in the precision mechanics that would later define his machine-tool inventions.

## The [[planing-machine|Planing Machine]]

Murray is credited as one of several contenders for the invention of the planing machine — one of the most useful of modern machine tools. The invention has been claimed on behalf of six inventors: Fox of Derby, Roberts and [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]] of Manchester, Matthew Murray of Leeds, Spring of [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]], and [[george-rennie|George Rennie]] of [[london|London]]. (031-154)

The evidence for Murray's priority is strong. At Matthew Murray's works in 1814, a planing machine was in regular use — predating the machines built by other claimants. (045-105) Mr. March, the mayor of Leeds, confirmed that Murray constructed a working planing machine used in his workshop. (046-055)

Murray's most important inventions were connected with the construction of machine tools and the improvement of the steam engine. His faculty for mechanical design attracted highly skilled mechanics to his works. (045-174) (045-188)

## [[locomotive|Locomotive]] Building

Murray's firm, Fenton, Murray and Wood, became one of the earliest builders of steam locomotives in [[england|England]]. Having obtained considerable practical experience in engine-building, Murray's firm branched into locomotive construction. (045-144) (045-147)

The Leeds engineers competed with [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] in the nascent locomotive market, and Murray's designs influenced the development of railway engineering in the 1810s and 1820s. It is possible that Matthew Murray may have anticipated some of the features later associated with Stephenson's locomotives. (047-146)

## Legacy

Murray died at Leeds in 1826, in his sixty-first year. (045-197)

Along with [[peter-fairbairn|Peter Fairbairn]] of Leeds and [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]] of Manchester, Murray represented the emergence of a distinct northern school of mechanical engineering — practical, self-taught, and oriented toward the machine-tool and locomotive trades. (045-049) (045-102)
