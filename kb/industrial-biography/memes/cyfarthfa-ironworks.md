# Cyfarthfa Ironworks

The Cyfarthfa Ironworks at [[merthyr-tydfil|Merthyr Tydfil]] became under [[richard-crawshay|Richard Crawshay]] one of the largest iron-producing establishments in the world by the early 19th century. (023-072)

## [[richard-crawshay|Richard Crawshay]]

Richard Crawshay was the first of the great ironmasters who had the sense to appreciate and adopt the methods of manufacturing iron invented by [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]. The story of his rise shows how, as respects mere money-making, shrewdness is more potent than invention, and business faculty than manufacturing skill. (023-072)

Crawshay was born at Normanton near [[leeds|Leeds]], the son of a small [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] farmer. When a youth he worked on his father's farm, but at the age of fifteen a difference with his father unsettled his mind, and he determined to leave his home and seek his fortune elsewhere. (023-077)

Arriving in [[london|London]], young Crawshay found employment at an ironmonger's shop in York Yard, Upper [[thames|Thames]] Street, where his first duties were to clean out the office, run errands, and act as porter when occasion required. He was very attentive, industrious, and shrewd, and became known in the office as "The Yorkshire Boy." (023-092)

His master gave him an interest in the flat-iron business, which prospered under his charge. After a few more years the master retired, leaving Crawshay the cast-iron business in York Yard. (023-100)

## Adoption of the [[puddling-process|Puddling Process]]

When the principal ironmasters heard of Cort's success in manufacturing and forging bar-iron, they visited his foundry to examine his process. Among the first to try it were Richard Crawshay of Cyfartha, [[samuel-homfray|Samuel Homfray]] of [[penydarran|Penydarran]], and [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]. (022-083)

Crawshay was then, in 1787, forging only ten tons of bar-iron weekly under the hammer. When he saw the superior processes invented by Cort, he readily entered into a contract with him to work under his patents at ten shillings a ton royalty. (022-085)

The results were transformative. By 1812, Crawshay wrote to the Secretary of Lord [[sheffield|Sheffield]] describing his method: "I took it from a Mr. Cort, who had a little mill at Fontley in Hampshire: I have thus acquainted you with my method, by which I am now making more than ten thousand tons of bar-iron per annum." (022-089)

This thousand-fold increase — from ten tons weekly to over ten thousand tons annually — illustrates the revolutionary impact of Cort's [[puddling-process|puddling]] process when adopted at scale.

## Legacy

The Crawshay family, along with the Guests of [[dowlais-ironworks|Dowlais]], became one of the great iron-making dynasties of [[south-wales|South Wales]]. They advanced themselves from the station of small farmers to that of great capitalists, owning estates in many counties and ranking among the largest employers of labour in the kingdom. (029-042)

The growth of [[merthyr-tydfil|Merthyr Tydfil]] and its industry was in large part driven by the Cyfarthfa works. The Crawshay fortune, built on the foundations of Cort's inventions, exemplifies how manufacturing skill combined with business acumen could transform a region.
