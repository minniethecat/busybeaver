# Bridgewater Canal

The Bridgewater Canal, built by the [[duke-of-bridgewater|Duke of Bridgewater]] in the 1760s, was one of the most influential engineering projects of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. (017-162)

Despite fierce local opposition, the canal demonstrated that artificial waterways could dramatically reduce the cost of transporting coal and other heavy goods, sparking a canal-building mania across Britain. (017-162)

## Industrial Significance

### A Visionary Project

Despite strong local prejudices that opposed the Duke of Bridgewater's canal in the first instance, the project proved transformative. The canal connected the Duke's coal mines at Worsley to [[manchester|Manchester]], reducing the cost of coal in the city by half. (017-162)

As [[robert-fulton|Robert Fulton]] noted in his 1796 treatise on canal navigation, public opinion initially treated the canal as a visionary project — much like the inclines of [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] at [[ketley|Ketley]] — but its success firmly established the principle of inland navigation. (017-161) (017-169)

### Influence on Canal Engineering

The Bridgewater Canal's success inspired a generation of canal engineers, including [[thomas-telford|Thomas Telford]], who acknowledged the canal as a precedent for his own works. The [[ellesmere-canal|Ellesmere Canal]], with its celebrated iron aqueduct powered by the [[pontycysyllte-aqueduct|Pontycysyllte]] design, was among the projects that followed in the Duke's pioneering footsteps. (017-172) (017-174)

### The [[bridgewater-foundry|Bridgewater Foundry]]

The name Bridgewater was later adopted by [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] for his celebrated engineering works, the [[bridgewater-foundry|Bridgewater Foundry]], near [[manchester|Manchester]]. There the ring of the hammer on the smith's anvil was soon heard all over the place. (049-016) (049-020)
