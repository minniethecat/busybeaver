[052-001][[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] was born at Kelso on the 19th of February, 1787.  His

[052-002]parents occupied a humble but respectable position in life.  His

[052-003]father, Andrew Fairbairn, was the son of a gardener in the employment

[052-004]of Mr. Baillie of Mellerston, and lived at Smailholm, a village lying a

[052-005]few miles west of Kelso.  Tracing the Fairbairns still further back, we

[052-006]find several of them occupying the station of "portioners," or small

[052-007]lairds, at Earlston on the Tweed, where the family had been settled

[052-008]since the days of the Solemn League and Covenant.  By his mother's

[052-009]side, the subject of our memoir is supposed to be descended from the

[052-010]ancient Border family of Douglas.

[052-011]While Andrew Fairbairn (William's father) lived at Smailholm, Walter

[052-012]Scott was living with his grandmother in Smailholm or Sandyknowe Tower,

[052-013]whither he had been sent from [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] in the hope that change of air

[052-014]would help the cure of his diseased hip-joint; and Andrew, being nine

[052-015]years his senior, and a strong youth for his age, was accustomed to

[052-016]carry the little patient about in his arms, until he was able to walk

[052-017]by himself.  At a later period, when Miss Scott, Walter's aunt, removed

[052-018]from Smailholm to Kelso, the intercourse between the families was

[052-019]renewed.  Scott was then an [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] advocate, engaged in collecting

[052-020]materials for his Minstrelsy of the Scottish Border, or, as his aunt

[052-021]described his pursuit, "running after the auld wives of the country

[052-022]gatherin' havers."  He used frequently to read over by the fireside in

[052-023]the evening the results of his curious industry, which, however, were

[052-024]not very greatly appreciated by his nearest relatives; and they did not

[052-025]scruple to declare that for the "Advocate" to go about collecting

[052-026]"ballants" was mere waste of time as well as money.

[052-027][[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]'s first schoolmaster was a decrepit old man who went

[052-028]by the name of "Bowed Johnnie Ker,"--a Cameronian, with a nasal twang,

[052-029]which his pupils learnt much more readily than they did his lessons in

[052-030]reading and arithmetic, notwithstanding a liberal use of "the tawse."

[052-031]Yet Johnnie had a taste for music, and taught his pupils to SING their

[052-032]reading lessons, which was reckoned quite a novelty in education.

[052-033]After a short time our scholar was transferred to the parish-school of

[052-034]the town, kept by a Mr. White, where he was placed under the charge of

[052-035]a rather severe helper, who, instead of the tawse, administered

[052-036]discipline by means of his knuckles, hard as horn, which he applied

[052-037]with a peculiar jerk to the crania of his pupils.  At this school

[052-038]Willie Fairbairn lost the greater part of the singing accomplishments

[052-039]which he had acquired under "Bowed Johnnie," but he learnt in lieu of

[052-040]them to read from Scott and Barrow's collections of prose and poetry,

[052-041]while he obtained some knowledge of arithmetic, in which he proceeded

[052-042]as far as practice and the rule of three.  This constituted his whole

[052-043]stock of school-learning up to his tenth year.  Out of school-hours he

[052-044]learnt to climb the ruined walls of the old abbey of the town, and

[052-045]there was scarcely an arch, or tower, or cranny of it with which he did

[052-046]not become familiar.

[052-047]When in his twelfth year, his father, who had been brought up to

[052-048]farm-work, and possessed considerable practical knowledge of

[052-049]agriculture, was offered the charge of a farm at Moy in Ross-shire,

[052-050]belonging to Lord Seaforth of Brahan Castle.  The farm was of about 300

[052-051]acres, situated on the banks of the river Conan, some five miles from

[052-052]the town of Dingwall.  The family travelled thither in a covered cart,

[052-053]a distance of 200 miles, through a very wild and hilly country,

[052-054]arriving at their destination at the end of October, 1799.  The farm,

[052-055]when reached, was found overgrown with whins and brushwood, and covered

[052-056]in many places with great stones and rocks; it was, in short, as nearly

[052-057]in a state of nature as it was possible to be.  The house intended for

[052-058]the farmer's reception was not finished, and Andrew Fairbairn, with his

[052-059]wife and five children, had to take temporary refuge in a miserable

[052-060]hovel, very unlike the comfortable house which they had quitted at

[052-061]Kelso.  By next spring, however, the new house was ready; and Andrew

[052-062]Fairbairn set vigorously to work at the reclamation of the land.  After

[052-063]about two years' labours it exhibited an altogether different

[052-064]appearance, and in place of whins and stones there were to be seen

[052-065]heavy crops of barley and turnips.  The barren years of 1800 and 1801,

[052-066]however, pressed very hardly on Andrew Fairbairn as on every other

[052-067]farmer of arable land.  [[About]] that time, Andrew's brother Peter, who

[052-068]acted as secretary to Lord Seaforth, and through whose influence the

[052-069]former had obtained the farm, left Brahan Castle for the West Indies

[052-070]with his Lordship, who--notwithstanding his being both deaf and

[052-071]dumb--had been appointed to the Governorship of Barbadoes; and in

[052-072]consequence of various difficulties which occurred shortly after his

[052-073]leaving, Andrew Fairbairn found it necessary to give up his holding,

[052-074]whereupon he engaged as steward to Mackenzie of Allengrange, with whom

[052-075]he remained for two years.

[052-076]While the family lived at Moy, none of the boys were put to school.

[052-077]They could not be spared from the farm and the household.  Those of

[052-078]them that could not work afield were wanted to help to nurse the

[052-079]younger children at home.  But Andrew Fairbairn possessed a great

[052-080]treasure in his wife, who was a woman of much energy of character,

[052-081]setting before her children an example of patient industry, thrift,

[052-082]discreetness, and piety, which could not fail to exercise a powerful

[052-083]influence upon them in after-life; and this, of itself, was an

[052-084]education which probably far more than compensated for the boys' loss

[052-085]of school-culture during their life at Moy.  Mrs. Fairbairn span and

[052-086]made all the children's clothes, as well as the blankets and sheeting;

[052-087]and, while in the Highlands, she not only made her own and her

[052-088]daughters' dresses, and her sons' jackets and trowsers, but her

[052-089]husband's coats and waistcoats; besides helping her neighbours to cut

[052-090]out their clothing for family wear.

[052-091]One of William's duties at home was to nurse his younger brother Peter,

[052-092]then a delicate child under two years old; and to relieve himself of

[052-093]the labour of carrying him about, he began the construction of a little

[052-094]waggon in which to wheel him.  This was, however, a work of some

[052-095]difficulty, as all the tools he possessed were only a knife, a gimlet,

[052-096]and an old saw.  With these implements, a piece of thin board, and a

[052-097]few nails, he nevertheless contrived to make a tolerably serviceable

[052-098]waggon-body.  His chief difficulty consisted in making the wheels,

[052-099]which he contrived to surmount by cutting sections from the stem of a

[052-100]small alder-tree, and with a red-hot poker he bored the requisite holes

[052-101]in their centres to receive the axle.  The waggon was then mounted on

[052-102]its four wheels, and to the great joy of its maker was found to answer

[052-103]its purpose admirably.  In it he wheeled his little brother--afterwards

[052-104]well known as [[peter-fairbairn|Sir Peter Fairbairn]], mayor of [[leeds|Leeds]]--in various

[052-105]directions about the farm, and sometimes to a considerable distance

[052-106]from it; and the vehicle was regarded on the whole as a decided

[052-107]success.  His father encouraged him in his little feats of construction

[052-108]of a similar kind, and he proceeded to make and rig miniature boats and

[052-109]ships, and then miniature wind and water mills, in which last art he

[052-110]acquired such expertness that he had sometimes five or six mills going

[052-111]at a time.  The machinery was all made with a knife, the water-spouts

[052-112]being formed by the bark of a tree, and the millstones represented by

[052-113]round discs of the same material.  Such were the first constructive

[052-114]efforts of the future millwright and engineer.

[052-115]When the family removed to Allengrange in 1801, the boys were sent to

[052-116]school at Munlachy, about a mile and a half distant from the farm.  The

[052-117]school was attended by about forty barefooted boys in tartan kilt's,

[052-118]and about twenty girls, all of the poorer class.  The schoolmaster was

[052-119]one Donald Frazer, a good teacher, but a severe disciplinarian.  Under

[052-120]him, William made some progress in reading, writing, and arithmetic;

[052-121]and though he himself has often lamented the meagreness of his school

[052-122]instruction, it is clear, from what he has since been enabled to

[052-123]accomplish, that these early lessons were enough at all events to set

[052-124]him fairly on the road of self-culture, and proved the fruitful seed of

[052-125]much valuable intellectual labour, as well as of many excellent

[052-126]practical books.

[052-127]After two years' trial of his new situation, which was by no means

[052-128]satisfactory, Andrew Fairbairn determined again to remove southward

[052-129]with his family; and, selling off everything, they set sail from

[052-130]Cromarty for Leith in June, 1803.  Having seen his wife and children

[052-131]temporarily settled at Kelso, he looked out for a situation, and

[052-132]shortly after proceeded to undertake the management of Sir William

[052-133]Ingleby's farm at Ripley in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]].  Meanwhile William was placed

[052-134]for three months under the charge of his uncle William, the parish

[052-135]schoolmaster of Galashiels, for the purpose of receiving instruction in

[052-136]book-keeping and land-surveying, from which he derived considerable

[052-137]benefit.  He could not, however, remain longer at school; for being of

[052-138]the age of fourteen, it was thought necessary that he should be set to

[052-139]work without further delay.  His first employment was on the fine new

[052-140]bridge at Kelso, then in course of construction after the designs of

[052-141][[george-rennie|Mr. Rennie]]; but in helping one day to carry a handbarrow-load of stone,

[052-142]his strength proving insufficient, he gave way under it, and the stones

[052-143]fell upon him, one of them inflicting a serious wound on his leg, which

[052-144]kept him a cripple for months.  In the mean time his father, being

[052-145]dissatisfied with his prospects at Ripley, accepted the appointment of

[052-146]manager of the Percy Main Colliery Company's farm in the neighbourhood

[052-147]of [[newcastle-upon-tyne|[[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]]-on-Tyne]], whither he proceeded with his family towards the

[052-148]end of 1803, William joining them in the following February, when the

[052-149]wound in his leg had sufficiently healed to enable him to travel.

[052-150]Percy Main is situated within two miles of North Shields, and is one of

[052-151]the largest collieries in that district.  William was immediately set

[052-152]to work at the [[coal-mining|colliery]], his first employment being to lead coals from

[052-153]behind the screen to the pitmen's houses.  His Scotch accent, and

[052-154]perhaps his awkwardness, exposed him to much annoyance from the "pit

[052-155]lads," who were a very rough and profligate set; and as boxing was a

[052-156]favourite pastime among them, our youth had to fight his way to their

[052-157]respect, passing through a campaign of no less than seventeen pitched

[052-158]battles.  He was several times on the point of abandoning the work

[052-159]altogether, rather than undergo the buffetings and insults to which he

[052-160]was almost a daily martyr, when a protracted contest with one of the

[052-161]noted boxers of the [[coal-mining|colliery]], in which he proved the victor, at length

[052-162]relieved him from further persecution.

[052-163]In the following year, at the age of sixteen, he was articled as an

[052-164]engineer for five years to the owners of Percy Main, and was placed

[052-165]under the charge of Mr. Robinson, the engine-wright of the colliery.

[052-166]His wages as apprentice were 8s. a week; but by working over-hours,

[052-167]making wooden wedges used in pit-work, and blocking out segments of

[052-168]solid oak required for walling the sides of the mine, he considerably

[052-169]increased his earnings, which enabled him to add to the gross income of

[052-170]the family, who were still struggling with the difficulties of small

[052-171]means and increasing expenses.  When not engaged upon over-work in the

[052-172]evenings, he occupied himself in self-education.  He drew up a scheme

[052-173]of daily study with this object, to which he endeavoured to adhere as

[052-174]closely as possible,--devoting the evenings of Mondays to mensuration

[052-175]and arithmetic; Tuesdays to history and poetry; Wednesdays to

[052-176]recreation, novels, and romances; Thursdays to algebra and mathematics;

[052-177]Fridays to Euclid and trigonometry; Saturdays to recreation; and

[052-178]Sundays to church, Milton, and recreation.  He was enabled to extend

[052-179]the range of his reading by the help of the North Shields Subscription

[052-180]Library, to which his father entered him a subscriber.  Portions of his

[052-181]spare time were also occasionally devoted to mechanical construction,

[052-182]in which he cultivated the useful art of handling tools.  One of his

[052-183]first attempts was the contrivance of a piece of machinery worked by a

[052-184]weight and a pendulum, that should at the same time serve for a

[052-185]timepiece and an orrery; but his want of means, as well as of time,

[052-186]prevented him prosecuting this contrivance to completion.  He was more

[052-187]successful with the construction of a fiddle, on which he was ambitious

[052-188]to become a performer.  It must have been a tolerable instrument, for a

[052-189]professional player offered him 20s. for it.  But though he succeeded

[052-190]in making a fiddle, and for some time persevered in the attempt to play

[052-191]upon it, he did not succeed in producing any satisfactory melody, and

[052-192]at length gave up the attempt, convinced that nature had not intended

[052-193]him for a musician.[1]
