[014-001]He pointed out very graphically the straits to which a man is put who

[014-002]is possessed of real property enough, but in a time of pressure is

[014-003]unable to turn himself round for want of ready cash.  "Then," says he,

[014-004]"all his creditors crowd to him as pigs do through a hole to a bean and

[014-005]pease rick."  "Is it not a sad thing," he asks, "that a goldsmith's boy

[014-006]in Lombard Street, who gives notes for the monies handed him by the

[014-007]merchants, should take up more monies upon his notes in one day than

[014-008]two lords, four knights, and eight esquires in twelve months upon all

[014-009]their personal securities? We are, as it were, cutting off our legs and

[014-010]arms to see who will feed the trunk.  But we cannot expect this from

[014-011]any of our neighbours abroad, whose interest depends upon our loss."

[014-012]He therefore proposed his registry of property as a ready means of

[014-013]raising a credit for purposes of trade.  Thus, he says, "I can both in

[014-014][[england|England]] and Wales register my wedding, my burial, and my christening,

[014-015]and a poor parish clerk is entrusted with the keeping of the book; and

[014-016]that which is registered there is held good by our law.  But I cannot

[014-017]register my lands, to be honest, to pay every man his own, to prevent

[014-018]those sad things that attend families for want thereof, and to have the

[014-019]great benefit and advantage that would come thereby.  A register will

[014-020]quicken trade, and the land registered will be equal as cash in a man's

[014-021]hands, and the credit thereof will go and do in trade what ready money

[014-022]now doth."  His idea was to raise money, when necessary, on the land

[014-023]registered, by giving security thereon after a form which he suggested.

[014-024]He would, in fact, have made land, as gold now is, the basis of an

[014-025]extended currency; and he rightly held that the value of land as a

[014-026]security must always be unexceptionable, and superior to any metallic

[014-027]basis that could possibly be devised.

[014-028]This indefatigable man continued to urge his various designs upon the

[014-029]attention of the public until he was far advanced in years.  He

[014-030]professed that he was moved to do so (and we believe him) solely by an

[014-031]ardent love for his country, "whose future flourishing," said he, "is

[014-032]the only reward I ever hope to see of all my labours."  Yarranton,

[014-033]however, received but little thanks for his persistency, while he

[014-034]encountered many rebuffs.  The public for the most part turned a deaf

[014-035]ear to his entreaties; and his writings proved of comparatively small

[014-036]avail, at least during his own lifetime.  He experienced the lot of

[014-037]many patriots, even the purest--the suspicion and detraction of his

[014-038]contemporaries.  His old political enemies do not seem to have

[014-039]forgotten him, of which we have the evidence in certain rare

[014-040]"broadsides" still extant, twitting him with the failure of his

[014-041]schemes, and even trumping up false charges of disloyalty against

[014-042]him.[19]

[014-043]In 1681 he published the second part of '[[england|England]]'s Improvement,'[20] in

[014-044]which he gave a summary account of its then limited growths and

[014-045]manufactures, pointing out that England and Ireland were the only

[014-046]northern kingdoms remaining unimproved; he re-urged the benefits and

[014-047]necessity of a voluntary register of real property; pointed out a

[014-048]method of improving the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]], lessening the growing power of

[014-049]France, and establishing home fisheries; proposed the securing and

[014-050]fortifying of Tangier; described a plan for preventing fires in [[london|London]],

[014-051]and reducing the charge for maintaining the Trained Bands; urged the

[014-052]formation of a harbour at Newhaven in [[sussex|Sussex]]; and, finally, discoursed

[014-053]at considerable length upon the tin, iron, linen, and woollen trades,

[014-054]setting forth various methods for their improvement.  In this last

[014-055]section, after referring to the depression in the domestic tin trade

[014-056](Cornish tin selling so low as 70s. the cwt.), he suggested a way of

[014-057]reviving it.  With the Cornish tin he would combine "the Roman cinders

[014-058]and iron-stone in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], which makes the best iron for

[014-059]most uses in the world, and works up to the best advantage, with

[014-060]delight and pleasure to the workmen."  He then described the history of

[014-061]his own efforts to import the manufacture of tin-plates into England

[014-062]some sixteen years before, in which he had been thwarted by

[014-063]Chamberlaine's patent, as above described,--and offered sundry queries

[014-064]as to the utility of patents generally, which, says he, "have the

[014-065]tendency to drive trade out of the kingdom." Appended to the chapter on

[014-066]Tin is an exceedingly amusing dialogue between a tin-miner of [[cornwall|Cornwall]],

[014-067]an iron-miner of Dean Forest, and a traveller (himself).  From this we

[014-068]gather that Yarranton's business continued to be that of an

[014-069]iron-manufacturer at his works at Ashley near Bewdley.  Thus the

[014-070]iron-miner says, "[[About]] 28 years since Mr. Yarranton found out a vast

[014-071]quantity of Roman cinders, near the walls of the city of [[worcestershire|Worcester]],

[014-072]from whence he and others carried away many thousand tons or loads up

[014-073]the river [[severn|Severn]], unto their iron-furnaces, to be melted down into

[014-074]iron, with a mixture of the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] iron-stone; and within 100

[014-075]yards of the walls of the city of [[worcestershire|Worcester]] there was dug up one of the

[014-076]hearths of the Roman foot-blasts, it being then firm and in order, and

[014-077]was 7 foot deep in the earth; and by the side of the work there was

[014-078]found a pot of Roman coin to the quantity of a peck, some of which was

[014-079]presented to Sir [Wm.] Dugdale, and part thereof is now in the King's

[014-080]Closet." [21]

[014-081]In the same year (1681) in which the second part of 'England's

[014-082]Improvement' appeared, Yarranton proceeded to Dunkirk for the purpose

[014-083]of making a personal survey of that port, then belonging to England;

[014-084]and on his return he published a map of the town, harbour, and castle

[014-085]on the sea, with accompanying letterpress, in which he recommended, for

[014-086]the safety of British trade, the demolition of the fortifications of

[014-087]Dunkirk before they were completed, which he held would only be for the

[014-088]purpose of their being garrisoned by the French king.  His 'Full

[014-089]Discovery of the First Presbyterian Sham Plot' was published in the

[014-090]same year; and from that time nothing further is known of Andrew

[014-091]Yarranton.  His name and his writings have been alike nearly forgotten;

[014-092]and, though Bishop Watson declared of him that he deserved to have a

[014-093]statue erected to his memory as a great public benefactor, we do not

[014-094]know that he was so much as honoured with a tombstone; for we have been

[014-095]unable, after careful inquiry, to discover when and where he died.

[014-096]Yarranton was a man whose views were far in advance of his age.  The

[014-097]generation for whom he laboured and wrote were not ripe for their

[014-098]reception and realization; and his voice sounded among the people like

[014-099]that of one crying in the wilderness.  But though his exhortations to

[014-100]industry and his large plans of national improvement failed to work

[014-101]themselves into realities in his own time, he broke the ground, he

[014-102]sowed the seed, and it may be that even at this day we are in some

[014-103]degree reaping the results of his labours.  At all events, his books

[014-104]still live to show how wise and sagacious [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]] was beyond

[014-105]his contemporaries as to the true methods of establishing upon solid

[014-106]foundations the industrial prosperity of England.

[014-109][2] A copy of the entries in the parish register relating to the

[014-110]various members of the Yarranton family, kindly forwarded to us by the

[014-111]Rev. H. W. Cookes, rector of Astley, shows them to have resided in that

[014-112]parish for many generations.  There were the Yarrantons of Yarranton,

[014-113]of Redstone, of Larford, of Brockenton, and of Longmore.  With that

[014-114]disregard for orthography in proper names which prevailed some three

[014-115]hundred years since, they are indifferently designated as Yarran,

[014-116]Yarranton, and Yarrington.  The name was most probably derived from two

[014-117]farms named Great and Little Yarranton, or Yarran (originally

[014-118]Yarhampton), situated in the parish of Astley.  The Yarrantons

[014-119]frequently filled local offices in that parish, and we find several of

[014-120]them officiating at different periods as bailiffs of Bewdley.

[014-154]subsequently renewed the attempt to make the Salwarp navigable.  He

[014-155]constructed five out of the six locks, and then abandoned the scheme.

[014-156]Gough, in his edition of Camden's Brit. ii. 357, Lond. 1789, says, "It

[014-157]is not long since some of the boats made use of in Yarranton's

[014-158]navigation were found.  Neither tradition nor our projector's account

[014-159]of the matter perfectly satisfy us why this navigation was

[014-160]neglected.....  We must therefore conclude that the numerous works and

[014-161]glass-houses upon the Stour, and in the neighbourhood of [[stourbridge|Stourbridge]],

[014-162]did not then exist, A.D. 1666.  ....The navigable communication which

[014-163]now connects [[river-trent|Trent]] and [[severn|Severn]], and which runs in the course of

[014-164]Yarranton's project, is already of general use....  The canal since

[014-165]executed under the inspection of Mr. Brindley, running parallel with

[014-166]the river....  cost the proprietors 105,000L."

[014-167][13] In the dedication of his book, entitled Englands Improvement by

[014-168]Sea and Land, Part I., Yarranton gives the names of the "noble

[014-169]patriots" who sent him on his journey of inquiry.  They were Sir Waiter

[014-170]Kirtham Blount, Bart., Sir Samuel Baldwin and Sir Timothy Baldwin,

[014-171]Knights, Thomas Foley and Philip Foley, Esquires, and six other

[014-172]gentlemen.  The father of the Foleys was himself supposed to have

[014-173]introduced the art of iron-splitting into England by an expedient

[014-174]similar to that adopted by Yarranton in obtaining a knowledge of the

[014-175]tin-plate manufacture (Self-Help, p.145).  The secret of the

[014-176]silk-throwing machinery of Piedmont was in like manner introduced into

[014-177]England by Mr. Lombe of [[derby|Derby]], who shortly succeeded in founding a

[014-178]flourishing branch of manufacture.  These were indeed the days of

[014-179]romance and adventure in manufactures.
