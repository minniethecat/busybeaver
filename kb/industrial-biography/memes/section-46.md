[046-001]We have not yet exhausted the list of claimants to the invention of the

[046-002][[planing-machine|Planing Machine]], for we find still another in the person of Richard

[046-003]Roberts of [[manchester|Manchester]], one of the most prolific of modern inventors.

[046-004]Mr. Roberts has indeed achieved so many undisputed inventions, that he

[046-005]can readily afford to divide the honour in this case with others.  He

[046-006]has contrived things so various as the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] and the best

[046-007]electro-magnet, wet gas-meters and dry planing machines, iron

[046-008]billard-tables and turret-clocks, the centrifugal railway and the drill

[046-009]slotting-machine, an apparatus for making cigars and machinery for the

[046-010]propulsion and equipment of steamships; so that he may almost be

[046-011]regarded as the Admirable Crichton of modern mechanics.

[046-012][[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]] was born in 1789, at Carreghova in the parish of

[046-013]Llanymynech.  His father was by trade a shoemaker, to which he

[046-014]occasionally added the occupation of toll-keeper.  The house in which

[046-015]Richard was born stood upon the border line which then divided the

[046-016]counties of Salop and Montgomery; the front door opening in the one

[046-017]county, and the back door in the other.  Richard, when a boy, received

[046-018]next to no education, and as soon as he was of fitting age was put to

[046-019]common labouring work.  For some time he worked in a quarry near his

[046-020]father's dwelling; but being of an ingenious turn, he occupied his

[046-021]leisure in making various articles of mechanism, partly for amusement

[046-022]and partly for profit.  One of his first achievements, while working as

[046-023]a quarryman, was a spinning-wheel, of which he was very proud, for it

[046-024]was considered "a good job."  Thus he gradually acquired dexterity in

[046-025]handling tools, and he shortly came to entertain the ambition of

[046-026]becoming a mechanic.

[046-027]There were several ironworks in the neighbour hood, and thither he went

[046-028]in search of employment.  He succeeded in finding work as a

[046-029]pattern-maker at Bradley, near Bilston; under [[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]], the

[046-030]famous ironmaster--a man of great enterprise as well as mechanical

[046-031]skill; for he was the first man, as already stated, that Watt could

[046-032]find capable of boring a cylinder with any approach to truth, for the

[046-033]purposes of his [[steam-engine|steam-engine]]s.  After acquiring some practical

[046-034]knowledge of the art of working in wood as well as iron, Roberts

[046-035]proceeded to [[birmingham|Birmingham]], where he passed through different shops,

[046-036]gaining further experience in mechanical practice.  He tried his hand

[046-037]at many kinds of work, and acquired considerable dexterity in each.  He

[046-038]was regarded as a sort of jack-of-all-trades; for he was a good turner,

[046-039]a tolerable wheel-wright, and could repair mill-work at a pinch.

[046-040]He next moved northward to the Horsley ironworks, Tipton, where he was

[046-041]working as a pattern-maker when he had the misfortune to be drawn in

[046-042]his own county for the militia.  He immediately left his work and made

[046-043]his way homeward to Llanymynech, determined not to be a soldier or even

[046-044]a militiaman.  But home was not the place for him to rest in, and after

[046-045]bidding a hasty adieu to his father, he crossed the country northward

[046-046]on foot and reached [[liverpool|Liverpool]], in the hope of finding work there.

[046-047]Failing in that, he set out for [[manchester|Manchester]] and reached it at dusk, very

[046-048]weary and very miry in consequence of the road being in such a wretched

[046-049]state of mud and ruts.  He relates that, not knowing a person in the

[046-050]town, he went up to an apple-stall ostensibly to buy a pennyworth of

[046-051]apples, but really to ask the stall-keeper if he knew of any person in

[046-052]want of a hand.  Was there any turner in the neighbourhood? Yes, round

[046-053]the corner.  Thither he went at once, found the wood-turner in, and was

[046-054]promised a job on the following morning.  He remained with the turner

[046-055]for only a short time, after which he found a job in Salford at lathe

[046-056]and tool-making.  But hearing that the militia warrant-officers were

[046-057]still searching for him, he became uneasy and determined to take refuge

[046-058]in [[london|London]].

[046-059]He trudged all the way on foot to that great hiding-place, and first

[046-060]tried Holtzapffel's, the famous tool-maker's, but failing in his

[046-061]application he next went to Maudslay's and succeeded in getting

[046-062]employment.  He worked there for some time, acquiring much valuable

[046-063]practical knowledge in the use of tools, cultivating his skill by

[046-064]contact with first-class workmen, and benefiting by the spirit of

[046-065]active contrivance which pervaded the Maudslay shops.  His manual

[046-066]dexterity greatly increased, and his inventive ingenuity fully

[046-067]stimulated, he determined on making his way back to Manchester, which,

[046-068]even more than [[london|London]] itself, at that time presented abundant openings

[046-069]for men of mechanical skill.  Hence we find so many of the best

[046-070]mechanics trained at Maudslay's and Clement's--Nasmyth, Lewis, Muir,

[046-071]Roberts, Whitworth, and others--shortly rising into distinction there

[046-072]as leading mechanicians and tool-makers.

[046-073]The mere enumeration of the various results of Mr. Roberts's inventive

[046-074]skill during the period of his settlement at Manchester as a mechanical

[046-075]engineer, would occupy more space than we can well spare.  But we may

[046-076]briefly mention a few of the more important.  In 1816, while carrying

[046-077]on business on his own account in Deansgate, he invented his improved

[046-078]sector for correctly sizing wheels in blank previously to their being

[046-079]cut, which is still extensively used.  In the same year he invented his

[046-080]improved screw-lathe; and in the following year, at the request of the

[046-081]boroughreeve and constables of Manchester, he contrived an oscillating

[046-082]and rotating wet gas meter of a new kind, which enabled them to sell

[046-083]gas by measure.  This was the first meter in which a water lute was

[046-084]applied to prevent the escape of gas by the index shaft, the want of

[046-085]which, as well as its great complexity, had prevented the only other

[046-086]gas meter then in existence from working satisfactorily.  The water

[046-087]lute was immediately adopted by the patentee of that meter.  The

[046-088]planing machine, though claimed, as we have seen, by many inventors,

[046-089]was constructed by Mr. Roberts after an original plan of his own in

[046-090]1817, and became the tool most generally employed in mechanical

[046-091]workshops--acting by means of a chain and rack--though it has since

[046-092]been superseded to some extent by the planing machine of Whitworth,

[046-093]which works both ways upon an endless screw.  Improvements followed in

[046-094]the slide-lathe (giving a large range of speed with increased diameters

[046-095]for the same size of headstocks, &c.), in the wheel-cutting engine, in

[046-096]the scale-beam (by which, with a load of 2 oz.  on each end, the

[046-097]fifteen-hundredth part of a grain could be indicated), in the

[046-098]broaching-machine, the slotting-machine, and other engines.

[046-099]But the inventions by which his fame became most extensively known

[046-100]arose out of circumstances connected with the cotton manufactures of

[046-101]Manchester and the neighbourhood.  The great improvements which he

[046-102]introduced in the machine for making weavers' reeds, led to the

[046-103]formation of the firm of Sharp, Roberts, and Co., of which Mr. Roberts

[046-104]was the acting mechanical partner for many years.  Not less important

[046-105]were his improvements in [[power-loom|power-looms]] for weaving fustians, which were

[046-106]extensively adopted.  But by far the most famous of his inventions was

[046-107]unquestionably his Self-acting Mule, one of the most elaborate and

[046-108]beautiful pieces of machinery ever contrived.  Before its invention,

[046-109]the working of the entire machinery of the cotton-mill, as well as the

[046-110]employment of the piecers, cleaners, and other classes of operatives,

[046-111]depended upon the spinners, who, though receiving the highest rates of

[046-112]pay, were by much the most given to strikes; and they were frequently

[046-113]accustomed to turn out in times when trade was brisk, thereby bringing

[046-114]the whole operations of the manufactories to a standstill, and throwing

[046-115]all the other operatives out of employment.  A long-continued strike of

[046-116]this sort took place in 1824, when the idea occurred to the masters

[046-117]that it might be possible to make the spinning-mules run out and in at

[046-118]the proper speed by means of self-acting machinery, and thus render

[046-119]them in some measure independent of the more refractory class of their

[046-120]workmen.  It seemed, however, to be so very difficult a problem, that

[046-121]they were by no means sanguine of success in its solution.  Some time

[046-122]passed before they could find any mechanic willing so much as to

[046-123]consider the subject.  Mr. Ashton of Staley-bridge made every effort

[046-124]with this object, but the answer he got was uniformly the same.  The

[046-125]thing was declared to be impracticable and impossible.  Mr. Ashton,

[046-126]accompanied by two other leading spinners, called on Sharp, Roberts,

[046-127]and Co., to seek an interview with Mr. Roberts.  They introduced the

[046-128]subject to him, but he would scarcely listen to their explanations,

[046-129]cutting them short with the remark that he knew nothing whatever about

[046-130][[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]].  They insisted, nevertheless, on explaining to him

[046-131]what they required, but they went away without being able to obtain

[046-132]from him any promise of assistance in bringing out the required machine.

[046-133]The strike continued, and the manufacturers again called upon Mr.

[046-134]Roberts, but with no better result.  A third time they called and

[046-135]appealed to [[thomas-sharp|Mr. Sharp]], the capitalist of the firm, who promised to use

[046-136]his best endeavours to induce his mechanical partner to take the matter

[046-137]in hand.  But Mr. Roberts, notwithstanding his reticence, had been

[046-138]occupied in carefully pondering the subject since Mr. Ashton's first

[046-139]interview with him.  The very difficulty of the problem to be solved

[046-140]had tempted him boldly to grapple with it, though he would not hold out

[046-141]the slightest expectation to the cotton-spinners of his being able to

[046-142]help them in their emergency until he saw his way perfectly clear.

[046-143]That time had now come; and when [[thomas-sharp|Mr. Sharp]] introduced the subject, he

[046-144]said he had turned the matter over and thought he could construct the

[046-145]required self-acting machinery.  It was arranged that he should proceed

[046-146]with it at once, and after a close study of four months he brought out

[046-147]the machine now so extensively known as the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]].  The

[046-148]invention was patented in 1825, and was perfected by subsequent

[046-149]additions, which were also patented.

[046-150]Like so many other inventions, the idea of the self-acting mule was not

[046-151]new.  Thus [[william-strutt|Mr. William Strutt]] of [[derby|Derby]], the father of Lord Belper,

[046-152]invented a machine of this sort at an early period; Mr. William Belly,

[046-153]of the [[new-lanark-mills|[[new-lanark-mills|New Lanark]] Mills]], invented a second; and various other

[046-154]projectors tried their skill in the same direction; but none of these

[046-155]inventions came into practical use.  In such cases it has become

[046-156]generally admitted that the real inventor is not the person who

[046-157]suggests the idea of the invention, but he who first works it out into

[046-158]a practicable process, and so makes it of practical and commercial

[046-159]value.  This was accomplished by Mr. Roberts, who, working out the idea

[046-160]after his own independent methods, succeeded in making the first

[046-161]self-acting mule that would really act as such; and he is therefore

[046-162]fairly entitled to be regarded as its inventor.

[046-163]By means of this beautiful contrivance, spindle-carriages; bearing

[046-164]hundreds of spindles, run themselves out and in by means of automatic

[046-165]machinery, at the proper speed, without a hand touching them; the only

[046-166]labour required being that of a few boys and girls to watch them and

[046-167]mend the broken threads when the carriage recedes from the roller beam,

[046-168]and to stop it when the cop is completely formed, as is indicated by

[046-169]the bell of the counter attached to the working gear.  Mr. Baines

[046-170]describes the self-acting mule while at work as "drawing out, twisting,

[046-171]and winding up many thousand threads, with unfailing precision and

[046-172]indefatigable patience and strength--a scene as magical to the eye

[046-173]which is not familiarized with it, as the effects have been marvellous

[046-174]in augmenting the wealth and population of the country." [5]

[046-175]Mr. Roberts's great success with the self-acting mule led to his being

[046-176]often appealed to for help in the mechanics of manufacturing.  In 1826,

[046-177]the year after his patent was taken out, he was sent for to Mulhouse,

[046-178]in Alsace, to design and arrange the machine establishment of Andre

[046-179]Koechlin and Co.; and in that and the two subsequent years he fairly

[046-180]set the works a-going, instructing the workmen in the manufacture of

[046-181]spinning-machinery, and thus contributing largely to the success of the

[046-182]French cotton manufacture.  In 1832 he patented his invention of the

[046-183]Radial Arm for "winding on" in the self-acting mule, now in general

[046-184]use; and in future years he took out sundry patents for roving,

[046-185]slubbing, spinning, and doubling cotton and other fibrous materials;

[046-186]and for weaving, beetling, and mangling fabrics of various sorts.
