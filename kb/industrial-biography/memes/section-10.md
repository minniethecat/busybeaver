[010-001]The town of [[dudley|Dudley]] was already a centre of the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]], though

[010-002]chiefly of small wares, such as nails, horse-shoes, keys, locks, and

[010-003]common agricultural tools; and it was estimated that there were about

[010-004]20,000 smiths and workers in iron of various kinds living within a

[010-005]circuit of ten miles of [[dudley|Dudley]] Castle.  But, as in the southern

[010-006]counties, the production of iron had suffered great diminution from the

[010-007]want of fuel in the district, though formerly a mighty woodland

[010-008]country; and many important branches of the local trade were brought

[010-009]almost to a stand-still.  Yet there was an extraordinary abundance of

[010-010]coal to be met with in the neighbourhood--coal in some places lying in

[010-011]seams ten feet thick--ironstone four feet thick immediately under the

[010-012]coal, with limestone conveniently adjacent to both.  The conjunction

[010-013]seemed almost providential--"as if," observes Dud, "God had decreed the

[010-014]time when and how these smiths should be supplied, and this island

[010-015]also, with iron, and most especially that this cole and ironstone

[010-016]should give the first and just occasion for the invention of smelting

[010-017]iron with pit-cole;" though, as we have already seen, all attempts

[010-018]heretofore made with that object had practically failed.

[010-019]Dud was a special favourite of the Earl his father, who encouraged his

[010-020]speculations with reference to the improvement of the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]],

[010-021]and gave him an education calculated to enable him to turn his

[010-022]excellent practical abilities to account.  He was studying at Baliol

[010-023]College, Oxford, in the year 1619, when the Earl sent for him to take

[010-024]charge of an iron furnace and two forges in the chase of Pensnet in

[010-025][[worcestershire|Worcestershire]].  He was no sooner installed manager of the works, than,

[010-026]feeling hampered by the want of wood for fuel, his attention was

[010-027]directed to the employment of pit-coal as a substitute.  He altered his

[010-028]furnace accordingly, so as to adapt it to the new process, and the

[010-029]result of the first trial was such as to induce him to persevere.  It

[010-030]is nowhere stated in [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s Treatise what was the precise nature

[010-031]of the method adopted by him; but it is most probable that, in

[010-032]endeavouring to substitute coal for wood as fuel, he would subject the

[010-033]coal to a process similar to that of charcoal-burning.  The result

[010-034]would be what is called Coke; and as Dudley informs us that he followed

[010-035]up his first experiment with a second blast, by means of which he was

[010-036]enabled to produce good marketable iron, the presumption is that his

[010-037]success was also due to an improvement of the blast which he contrived

[010-038]for the purpose of keeping up the active combustion of the fuel.

[010-039]Though the quantity produced by the new process was comparatively

[010-040]small--not more than three tons a week from each furnace--Dudley

[010-041]anticipated that greater experience would enable him to increase the

[010-042]quantity; and at all events he had succeeded in proving the

[010-043]practicability of smelting iron with fuel made from pit-coal, which so

[010-044]many before him had tried in vain.

[010-045]Immediately after the second trial had been made with such good issue,

[010-046]Dud wrote to his father the Earl, then in [[london|London]], informing him what he

[010-047]had done, and desiring him at once to obtain a patent for the invention

[010-048]from King James.  This was readily granted, and the patent (No. 18),

[010-049]dated the 22nd February, 1620, was taken out in the name of Lord Dudley

[010-050]himself.

[010-051]Dud proceeded with the manufacture of iron at Pensnet, and also at

[010-052]Cradley in [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], where he erected another furnace; and a year

[010-053]after the patent was granted he was enabled to send up to [[tower-of-london|the Tower]], by

[010-054]the King's command, a considerable quantity of the new iron for trial.

[010-055]Many experiments were made with it:  its qualities were fairly tested,

[010-056]and it was pronounced "good merchantable iron."  Dud adds, in his

[010-057]Treatise, that his brother-in-law, Richard Parkshouse, of Sedgeley,[5]

[010-058]"had a fowling-gun there made of the Pit-cole iron," which was "well

[010-059]approved."  There was therefore every prospect of the new method of

[010-060]manufacture becoming fairly established, and with greater experience

[010-061]further improvements might with confidence be anticipated, when a

[010-062]succession of calamities occurred to the inventor which involved him in

[010-063]difficulties and put an effectual stop to the progress of his

[010-064]enterprise.

[010-065]The new works had been in successful operation little more than a year,

[010-066]when a flood, long after known as the "Great May-day Flood," swept away

[010-067]Dudley's principal works at Cradley, and otherwise inflicted much

[010-068]damage throughout the district.  "At the market town called

[010-069][[stourbridge|Stourbridge]]," says Dud, in the course of his curious narrative,

[010-070]"although the author sent with speed to preserve the people from

[010-071]drowning, and one resolute man was carried from the bridge there in the

[010-072]day-time, the nether part of the town was so deep in water that the

[010-073]people had much ado to preserve their lives in the uppermost rooms of

[010-074]their houses."  Dudley himself received very little sympathy for his

[010-075]losses.  On the contrary, the iron-smelters of the district rejoiced

[010-076]exceedingly at the destruction of his works by the flood.  They had

[010-077]seen him making good iron by his new patent process, and selling it

[010-078]cheaper than they could afford to do.  They accordingly put in

[010-079]circulation all manner of disparaging reports about his iron.  It was

[010-080]bad iron, not fit to be used; indeed no iron, except what was smelted

[010-081]with charcoal of wood, could be good.  To smelt it with coal was a

[010-082]dangerous innovation, and could only result in some great public

[010-083]calamity.  The ironmasters even appealed to King James to put a stop to

[010-084]Dud's manufacture, alleging that his iron was not merchantable.  And

[010-085]then came the great flood, which swept away his works; the hostile

[010-086]ironmasters now hoping that there was an end for ever of Dudley's

[010-087]pit-coal iron.

[010-088]But Dud, with his wonted energy, forthwith set to work and repaired his

[010-089]furnaces and forges, though at great cost; and in the course of a short

[010-090]time the new manufacture was again in full progress.  The ironmasters

[010-091]raised a fresh outcry against him, and addressed another strong

[010-092]memorial against Dud and his iron to King James.  This seems to have

[010-093]taken effect; and in order to ascertain the quality of the article by

[010-094]testing it upon a large scale, the King commanded Dudley to send up to

[010-095]the [[tower-of-london|Tower of London]], with every possible speed, quantities of all the

[010-096]sorts of bar-iron made by him, fit for the "making of muskets,

[010-097]carbines, and iron for great bolts for shipping; which iron," continues

[010-098]Dud, "being so tried by artists and smiths, the ironmasters and

[010-099]iron-mongers were all silenced until the 21st year of King James's

[010-100]reign."  The ironmasters then endeavoured to get the Dudley patent

[010-101]included in the monopolies to be abolished by the statute of that year;

[010-102]but all they could accomplish was the limitation of the patent to

[010-103]fourteen years instead of thirty-one; the special exemption of the

[010-104]patent from the operation of the statute affording a sufficient

[010-105]indication of the importance already attached to the invention.  After

[010-106]that time Dudley "went on with his invention cheerfully, and made

[010-107]annually great store of iron, good and merchantable, and sold it unto

[010-108]diverse men at twelve pounds per ton." "I also," said he, "made all

[010-109]sorts of [[cast-iron|cast-iron ware]]s, as brewing cisterns, pots, mortars, &c.,

[010-110]better and cheaper than any yet made in these nations with charcoal,

[010-111]some of which are yet to be seen by any man (at the author's house in

[010-112]the city of [[worcestershire|Worcester]]) that desires to be satisfied of the truth of the

[010-113]invention."

[010-114]Notwithstanding this decided success, Dudley encountered nothing but

[010-115]trouble and misfortune.  The ironmasters combined to resist his

[010-116]invention; they fastened lawsuit's upon him, and succeeded in getting

[010-117]him ousted from his works at Cradley.  From thence he removed to Himley

[010-118]in the county of Stafford, where he set up a pit-coal furnace; but

[010-119]being without the means of forging the iron into bars, he was

[010-120]constrained to sell the pig-iron to the charcoal-ironmasters, "who did

[010-121]him much prejudice, not only by detaining his stock, but also by

[010-122]disparaging his iron."  He next proceeded to erect a large new furnace

[010-123]at Hasco Bridge, near Sedgeley, in the same county, for the purpose of

[010-124]carrying out the manufacture on the most improved principles.  This

[010-125]furnace was of stone, twenty-seven feet square, provided with unusually

[010-126]large bellows; and when in full work he says he was enabled to turn out

[010-127]seven tons of iron per week, "the greatest quantity of pit-coal iron

[010-128]ever yet made in Great Britain."  At the same place he discovered and

[010-129]opened out new workings of coal ten feet thick, lying immediately over

[010-130]the ironstone, and he prepared to carry on his operations on a large

[010-131]scale; but the new works were scarcely finished when a mob of rioters,

[010-132]instigated by the charcoal-ironmasters, broke in upon them, cut in

[010-133]pieces the new bellows, destroyed the machinery, and laid the results

[010-134]of all his deep-laid ingenuity and persevering industry in ruins.  From

[010-135]that time forward Dudley was allowed no rest nor peace:  he was

[010-136]attacked by mobs, worried by lawsuits, and eventually overwhelmed by

[010-137]debts.  He was then seized by his creditors and sent up to [[london|London]],

[010-138]where he was held a prisoner in the Comptoir for several thousand

[010-139]pounds.  The charcoal-iron men thus for a time remained masters of the

[010-140]field.

[010-141]Charles I. seems to have taken pity on the suffering inventor; and on

[010-142]his earnest petition, setting forth the great advantages to the nation

[010-143]of his invention, from which he had as yet derived no advantage, but

[010-144]only losses, sufferings, and persecution, the King granted him a

[010-145]renewal of his patent[6] in the year 1638; three other gentlemen

[010-146]joining him as partners, and doubtless providing the requisite capital

[010-147]for carrying on the manufacture after the plans of the inventor.  But

[010-148]Dud's evil fortune continued to pursue him.  The patent had scarcely

[010-149]been securedere the Civil War broke out, and the arts of peace must at

[010-150]once perforce give place to the arts of war.  Dud's nature would not

[010-151]suffer him to be neutral at such a time; and when the nation divided

[010-152]itself into two hostile camps, his predilections being strongly

[010-153]loyalist, he took the side of the King with his father.  It would

[010-154]appear from a petition presented by him to Charles II. in 1660, setting

[010-155]forth his sufferings in the royal cause, and praying for restoral to

[010-156]certain offices which he had enjoyed under Charles I., that as early as

[010-157]the year 1637 he had been employed by the King on a mission into

[010-158][[scotland|Scotland]],[7] in the train of the Marquis of Hamilton, the King's

[010-159]Commissioner.  Again in 1639, leaving his ironworks and partners, he

[010-160]accompanied Charles on his expedition across the Scotch border, and was

[010-161]present with the army until its discomfiture at Newburn near [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]]

[010-162]in the following year.

[010-163]The sword was now fairly drawn, and Dud seems for a time to have

[010-164]abandoned his iron-works and followed entirely the fortunes of the

[010-165]king.  He was sworn surveyor of the Mews or Armoury in 1640, but being

[010-166]unable to pay for the patent, another was sworn in in his place.  Yet

[010-167]his loyalty did not falter, for in the beginning of 1642, when Charles

[010-168]set out from London, shortly after the fall of Strafford and Laud, Dud

[010-169]went with him.[8]  He was present before Hull when Sir John Hotham shut

[010-170]its gates in the king's face; at York when the royal commissions of

[010-171]array were sent out enjoining all loyal subjects to send men, arms,

[010-172]money, and horses, for defence of the king and maintenance of the law;

[010-173]at [[nottingham|Nottingham]], where the royal standard was raised; at Coventry, where

[010-174]the townspeople refused the king entrance and fired upon his troops

[010-175]from the walls; at Edgehill, where the first great but indecisive

[010-176]battle was fought between the contending parties; in short, as Dud

[010-177]Dudley states in his petition, he was "in most of the battailes that

[010-178]year, and also supplyed his late sacred Majestie's magazines of

[010-179]Stafford, Worcester, Dudley Castle, and Oxford, with arms, shot,

[010-180]drakes, and cannon; and also, became major unto Sir Frauncis Worsley's

[010-181]regiment, which was much decaied."
