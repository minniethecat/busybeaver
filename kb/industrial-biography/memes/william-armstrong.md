## Early Life and Career

William George Armstrong was born in 1810 in [[Newcastle upon Tyne]]. Trained initially as a solicitor, his mechanical interests led him away from the law and toward engineering. He became one of the most prominent British engineers and industrialists of the nineteenth century, with contributions spanning hydraulic machinery, armaments, and shipbuilding. (031-044)

## Hydraulic Engineering

Armstrong made his most lasting contributions in the field of hydraulic engineering. He developed hydraulic cranes, hoists, and dock machinery that were widely adopted in British ports and industrial installations. His hydraulic accumulator, invented in 1846, provided a means of storing power and delivering it on demand, enabling the practical application of hydraulic power at an industrial scale. (034-103)

The principles of hydraulic power that Armstrong advanced were anticipated in earlier mechanical inventions. [[Joseph Bramah]], working decades before Armstrong, had conceived of transmitting power through hydraulic systems—a concept that Armstrong would later develop into practical industrial machinery. As [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]] noted, Bramah had "shot ahead of the mechanical necessities of his time" in this as in many other matters. (034-105)

## Armaments and the Elswick Works

Armstrong is perhaps most widely known for his contributions to armaments manufacturing. He founded the [[Elswick Works]] near [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]], which became one of the world's leading manufacturers of artillery and naval guns. His breech-loading rifled cannon represented a significant advance in ordnance technology. Smiles observed that the principle of the breech-loading cannon embodying Armstrong's modern idea, "though in a rude form," had earlier precedents—weapons from the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries in the Arsenal at Venice already embodied similar concepts. (031-044)

## Legacy

Sir William Armstrong was elevated to the peerage as Baron Armstrong of Bamburgh and Cragside. His country house, [[Cragside]] in [[northumberland|Northumberland]], was the first house in the world to be lit by hydroelectric power. Armstrong's engineering firm, later Armstrong Whitworth, became a major force in British heavy engineering, shipbuilding, and armaments throughout the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.
