# Portsmouth

Portsmouth was the site of one of the most celebrated engineering installations of the early nineteenth century: the block-making machinery at [[portsmouth-dockyard|Portsmouth Dockyard]], designed by [[marc-isambard-brunel|Marc Isambard Brunel]] and built by [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]. The dockyard was the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]]'s premier manufacturing facility for over a century. (019-052) (038-105)

## Industrial History

### The Royal Dockyard

[[portsmouth-dockyard|Portsmouth Dockyard]] was a principal establishment of the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]], serving as the construction and maintenance base for the fleet. Its strategic location on the south coast made it essential to British naval power from the Tudor period onward, and by the eighteenth century it was the largest industrial complex in the country. (038-105)

The dockyard regularly employed hundreds of skilled artisans: shipwrights, smiths, block-makers, and riggers. It was for upwards of fifty years the centre of the Navy's block supply, with blocks — the pulleys essential to ship rigging — manufactured there in vast quantities for the entire fleet. (039-003)

### Brunel's [[block-machinery|Block Machinery]]

The block-making machinery installed at [[portsmouth-dockyard|Portsmouth Dockyard]] was one of the engineering marvels of its age. [[marc-isambard-brunel|Marc Isambard Brunel]] designed a system of machines that could produce ships' blocks with unprecedented speed and precision, replacing the labour of scores of skilled block-makers. The machinery was built by [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] and installed at Portsmouth beginning in 1803. (038-092)

The Portsmouth block machinery was a landmark in the history of [[machine-tool|machine tools]], demonstrating the potential of mechanised production for precision manufacturing. The system remained in service for well over a century and was admired by engineers worldwide. (038-105)

### Earlier History

The iron-working connection to Portsmouth predated Brunel's machinery: as early as 1628, the failure of a cast-iron gun on board a ship of the [[george-villiers|Duke of Buckingham]] at Portsmouth was traced to inferior iron founding, revealing the importance of quality control in military metalwork. (019-052) This early instance of metallurgical failure analysis linked Portsmouth to the broader history of [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]].
