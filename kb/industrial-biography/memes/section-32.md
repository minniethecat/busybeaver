[032-001]Watt endeavoured to remedy the defect by keeping certain sets of

[032-002]workmen to special classes of work, allowing them to do nothing else.

[032-003]Fathers were induced to bring up their sons at the same bench with

[032-004]themselves, and initiate them in the dexterity which they had acquired

[032-005]by experience; and at Soho it was not unusual for the same precise line

[032-006]of work to be followed by members of the same family for three

[032-007]generations.  In this way as great a degree of accuracy of a mechanical

[032-008]kind was arrived at was practicable under the circumstances.  But

[032-009]notwithstanding all this care, accuracy of fitting could not be secured

[032-010]so long as the manufacture of steam-engines was conducted mainly by

[032-011]hand.  There was usually a considerable waste of steam, which the

[032-012]expedients of chewed paper and greased hat packed outside the piston

[032-013]were insufficient to remedy; and it was not until the invention of

[032-014]automatic [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] by the mechanical engineers about to be

[032-015]mentioned, that the manufacture of the [[steam-engine]] became a matter of

[032-016]comparative ease and certainty.  Watt was compelled to rest satisfied

[032-017]with imperfect results, arising from imperfect workmanship.  Thus,

[032-018]writing to Dr. Small respecting a cylinder 18 inches in diameter, he

[032-019]said, "at the worst place the long diameter exceeded the short by only

[032-020]three-eighths of an inch."  How different from the state of things at

[032-021]this day, when a cylinder five feet wide will be rejected as a piece of

[032-022]imperfect workmanship if it be found to vary in any part more than the

[032-023]80th part of an inch in diameter!

[032-024]Not fifty years since it was a matter of the utmost difficulty to set

[032-025]an engine to work, and sometimes of equal difficulty to keep it going.

[032-026]Though fitted by competent workmen, it often would not go at all.  Then

[032-027]the foreman of the factory at which it was made was sent for, and he

[032-028]would almost live beside the engine for a month or more; and after

[032-029]easing her here and screwing her up there, putting in a new part and

[032-030]altering an old one, packing the piston and tightening the valves, the

[032-031]machine would at length begot to work.[17]  Now the case is altogether

[032-032]different.  The perfection of modern [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] is such that the

[032-033]utmost possible precision is secured, and the mechanical engineer can

[032-034]calculate on a degree of exactitude that does not admit of a deviation

[032-035]beyond the thousandth part of an inch.  When the powerful oscillating

[032-036]engines of the 'Warrior' were put on board that ship, the parts,

[032-037]consisting of some five thousand separate pieces, were brought from the

[032-038]different workshops of the Messrs. Penn and Sons, where they had been

[032-039]made by workmen who knew not the places they were to occupy, and fitted

[032-040]together with such precision that so soon as the steam was raised and

[032-041]let into the cylinders, the immense machine began as if to breathe and

[032-042]move like a living creature, stretching its huge arms like a new-born

[032-043]giant, and then, after practising its strength a little and proving its

[032-044]soundness in body and limb, it started off with the power of above a

[032-045]thousand horses to try its strength in breasting the billows of the

[032-046]North Sea.

[032-047]Such are among the triumphs of modern mechanical engineering, due in a

[032-048]great measure to the perfection of the tools by means of which all

[032-049]works in metal are now fashioned.  These tools are themselves among the

[032-050]most striking results of the mechanical invention of the day.  They are

[032-051]automata of the most perfect kind, rendering the engine and

[032-052]machine-maker in a great measure independent of inferior workmen.  For

[032-053]the machine tools have no unsteady hand, are not careless nor clumsy,

[032-054]do not work by rule of thumb, and cannot make mistakes.  They will

[032-055]repeat their operations a thousand times without tiring, or varying one

[032-056]hair's breadth in their action; and will turn out, without complaining,

[032-057]any quantity of work, all of like accuracy and finish.  Exercising as

[032-058]they do so remarkable an influence on the development of modern

[032-059]industry, we now propose, so far as the materials at our disposal will

[032-060]admit, to give an account of their principal inventors, beginning with

[032-061]the school of Bramah.

[032-144]CHAPTER XI.

[032-145]JOSEPH BRAMAH.

[032-146]"The great Inventor is one who has walked forth upon the industrial

[032-147]world, not from universities, but from hovels; not as clad in silks and

[032-148]decked with honours, but as clad in fustian and grimed with soot and

[032-149]oil."--ISAAC TAYLOR, Ultimate Civilization.

[032-150]The inventive faculty is so strong in some men that it may be said to

[032-151]amount to a passion, and cannot be restrained.  The saying that the

[032-152]poet is born, not made, applies with equal force to the inventor, who,

[032-153]though indebted like the other to culture and improved opportunities,

[032-154]nevertheless invents and goes on inventing mainly to gratify his own

[032-155]instinct.  The inventor, however, is not a creator like the poet, but

[032-156]chiefly a finder-out.  His power consists in a great measure in quick

[032-157]perception and accurate observation, and in seeing and foreseeing the

[032-158]effects of certain mechanical combinations.  He must possess the gift

[032-159]of insight, as well as of manual dexterity, combined with the

[032-160]indispensable qualities of patience and perseverance,--for though

[032-161]baffled, as he often is, he must be ready to rise up again unconquered

[032-162]even in the moment of defeat.  This is the stuff of which the greatest

[032-163]inventors have been made.  The subject of the following memoir may not

[032-164]be entitled to take rank as a first-class inventor, though he was a

[032-165]most prolific one; but, as the founder of a school from which proceeded

[032-166]some of the most distinguished mechanics of our time, he is entitled to

[032-167]a prominent place in this series of memoirs.

[032-168][[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] was born in 1748 at the village of Stainborough, near

[032-169]Barnsley in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], where his father rented a small farm under Lord

[032-170]Strafford.  Joseph was the eldest of five children, and was early

[032-171]destined to follow the plough.  After receiving a small amount of

[032-172]education at the village school, he was set to work upon the farm.

[032-173]From an early period he showed signs of constructive skill.  When a

[032-174]mere boy, he occupied his leisure hours in making musical instruments,

[032-175]and he succeeded in executing some creditable pieces of work with very

[032-176]imperfect tools.  A violin, which he made out of a solid block of wood,

[032-177]was long preserved as a curiosity.  He was so fortunate as to make a

[032-178]friend of the village blacksmith, whose smithy he was in the practice

[032-179]of frequenting.  The smith was an ingenious workman, and, having taken

[032-180]a liking for the boy, he made sundry tools for him out of old files and

[032-181]razor blades; and with these his fiddle and other pieces of work were

[032-182]mainly executed.
