Stephenson's *Rocket* was the [[locomotive|steam locomotive]] designed and built by [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]] and his son [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] that won the celebrated [[rainhill-trials|Rainhill Trials]] of October 1829. The competition, organized by the directors of the [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|[[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway]], was the decisive test that established the steam locomotive as the motive power for railways.

## The Rainhill Trials

The [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|Liverpool and Manchester Railway]] directors appointed three judges to adjudicate the locomotive competition at Rainhill. Among them was [[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]] of Manchester, the cotton-spinner and self-educated engineer whose "thorough knowledge of mechanical engineering" qualified him for the task. By these steps, Smiles noted, "did this poor Scotch boy become one of the leading men of Manchester." (054-100)

## Design and Significance

The *Rocket* incorporated several key innovations that distinguished it from its competitors: a multi-tubular boiler designed by [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] that greatly increased the heating surface; a separate firebox; and the use of the exhaust steam blast to increase the draught through the fire. These features, combined in the *Rocket*, set the pattern for steam locomotive design for the following century.

The *Rocket*'s victory at Rainhill inaugurated the railway age. The [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|Liverpool and Manchester Railway]], when it opened in 1830, became the model for railways throughout Britain and the world.
