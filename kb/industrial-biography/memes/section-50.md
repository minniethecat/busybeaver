[050-001]On reaching [[england|England]], Mr. Nasmyth at once wrote to his partner telling

[050-002]him what he had seen, and urging that the taking out of a patent for

[050-003]the protection of the invention ought no longer to be deferred.  But

[050-004]trade was still very much depressed, and as the Patricroft firm needed

[050-005]all their capital to carry on their business, Mr. Gaskell objected to

[050-006]lock any of it up in engineering novelties.  Seeing himself on the

[050-007]brink of losing his property in the invention, Mr. Nasmyth applied to

[050-008]his brother-in-law, William Bennett, Esq., who advanced him the

[050-009]requisite money for the purpose--about 280L.,--and the patent was

[050-010]secured in June 1840.  The first hammer, of 30 cwt., was made for the

[050-011]Patricroft works, with the consent of the partners; and in the course

[050-012]of a few weeks it was in full work.  The precision and beauty of its

[050-013]action--the perfect ease with which it was managed, and the untiring

[050-014]force of its percussive blows--were the admiration of all who saw it;

[050-015]and from that moment the steam-hammer became a recognised power in

[050-016]modern mechanics.  The variety or gradation of its blows was such, that

[050-017]it was found practicable to manipulate a hammer of ten tons as easily

[050-018]as if it had only been of ten ounces weight.  It was under such

[050-019]complete control that while descending with its greatest momentum, it

[050-020]could be arrested at any point with even greater ease than any

[050-021]instrument used by hand.  While capable of forging an Armstrong

[050-022]hundred-pounder, or the sheet-anchor for a ship of the line, it could

[050-023]hammer a nail, or crack a nut without bruising the kernel.  When it

[050-024]came into general use, the facilities which it afforded for executing

[050-025]all kinds of forging had the effect of greatly increasing the quantity

[050-026]of work done, at the same time that expense was saved.  The cost of

[050-027]making anchors was reduced by at least 50 per cent., while the quality

[050-028]of the forging was improved.  Before its invention the manufacture of a

[050-029]shaft of 15 or 20 cwt. required the concentrated exertions of a large

[050-030]establishment, and its successful execution was regarded as a great

[050-031]triumph of skill; whereas forgings of 20 and 30 tons weight are now

[050-032]things of almost every-day occurrence.  Its advantages were so obvious,

[050-033]that its adoption soon became general, and in the course of a few years

[050-034]Nasmyth steam-hammers were to be found in every well-appointed workshop

[050-035]both at home and abroad.  Many modifications have been made in the

[050-036]tool, by Condie, Morrison, Naylor, Rigby, and others; but Nasmyth's was

[050-037]the father of them all, and still holds its ground.[5]

[050-038]Among the important uses to which this hammer has of late years been

[050-039]applied, is the manufacture of iron plates for covering our ships of

[050-040]war, and the fabrication of the immense wrought-iron ordnance of

[050-041]Armstrong, Whitworth, and Blakely.  But for the steam-hammer, indeed,

[050-042]it is doubtful whether such weapons could have been made.  It is also

[050-043]used for the re-manufacture of iron in various other forms, to say

[050-044]nothing of the greatly extended use which it has been the direct means

[050-045]of effecting in wrought-iron and steel forgings in every description of

[050-046]machinery, from the largest marine steam-engines to the most nice and

[050-047]delicate parts of textile mechanism.  "It is not too much to say,"

[050-048]observes a writer in the Engineer, "that, without Nasmyth's

[050-049]steam-hammer, we must have stopped short in many of those gigantic

[050-050]engineering works which, but for the decay of all wonder in us, would

[050-051]be the perpetual wonder of this age, and which have enabled our modern

[050-052]engineers to take rank above the gods of all mythologies.  There is one

[050-053]use to which the steam-hammer is now becoming extensively applied by

[050-054]some of our manufacturers that deserves especial mention, rather for

[050-055]the prospect which it opens to us than for what has already been

[050-056]actually accomplished.  We allude to the manufacture of large articles

[050-057]in DIES.  At one manufactory in the country, railway wheels, for

[050-058]example, are being manufactured with enormous economy by this means.

[050-059]The various parts of the wheels are produced in quantity either by

[050-060]rolling or by dies under the hammer; these parts are brought together

[050-061]in their relative positions in a mould, heated to a welding heat, and

[050-062]then by a blow of the steam hammer, furnished with dies, are stamped

[050-063]into a complete and all but finished wheel.  It is evident that

[050-064]wherever wrought-iron articles of a manageable size have to be produced

[050-065]in considerable quantities, the same process may be adopted, and the

[050-066]saving effected by the substitution of this for the ordinary forging

[050-067]process will doubtless ere long prove incalculable.  For this, as for

[050-068]the many other advantageous uses of the steam-hammer, we are primarily

[050-069]and mainly indebted to Mr. Nasmyth.  It is but right, therefore, that

[050-070]we should hold his name in honour.  In fact, when we think of the

[050-071]universal service which this machine is rendering us, we feel that some

[050-072]special expression of our indebtedness to him would be a reasonable and

[050-073]grateful service.  The benefit which he has conferred upon us is so

[050-074]great as to justly entitle him to stand side by side with the few men

[050-075]who have gained name and fame as great inventive engineers, and to whom

[050-076]we have testified our gratitude--usually, unhappily, when it was too

[050-077]late for them to enjoy it."

[050-078]Mr. Nasmyth subsequently applied the principle of the steam-hammer in

[050-079]the pile driver, which he invented in 1845.  Until its production, all

[050-080]piles had been driven by means of a small mass of iron falling upon the

[050-081]head of the pile with great velocity from a considerable height,--the

[050-082]raising of the iron mass by means of the "monkey" being an operation

[050-083]that occupied much time and labour, with which the results were very

[050-084]incommensurate.  Pile-driving was, in Mr. Nasmyth's words, conducted on

[050-085]the artillery or cannon-ball principle; the action being excessive and

[050-086]the mass deficient, and adapted rather for destructive than impulsive

[050-087]action.  In his new and beautiful machine, he applied the elastic force

[050-088]of steam in raising the ram or driving block, on which, the block being

[050-089]disengaged, its whole weight of three tons descended on the head of the

[050-090]pile, and the process being repeated eighty times in the minute, the

[050-091]pile was sent home with a rapidity that was quite marvellous compared

[050-092]with the old-fashioned system.  In forming coffer-dams for the piers

[050-093]and abutments of bridges, quays, and harbours, and in piling the

[050-094]foundations of all kinds of masonry, the steam pile driver was found of

[050-095]invaluable use by the engineer.  At the first experiment made with the

[050-096]machine, Mr. Nasmyth drove a 14-inch pile fifteen feet into hard ground

[050-097]at the rate of 65 blows a minute.  The driver was first used in forming

[050-098]the great steam dock at [[devon|Devon]]port, where the results were very

[050-099]striking; and it was shortly after employed by [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] in

[050-100]piling the foundations of the great High Level Bridge at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]], and

[050-101]the Border Bridge at Berwick, as well as in several other of his great

[050-102]works.  The saving of time effected by this machine was very

[050-103]remarkable, the ratio being as 1 to 1800; that is, a pile could be

[050-104]driven in four minutes that before required twelve hours.  One of the

[050-105]peculiar features of the invention was that of employing the pile

[050-106]itself as the support of the steam-hammer part of the apparatus while

[050-107]it was being driven, so that the pile had the percussive action of the

[050-108]dead weight of the hammer as well as its lively blows to induce it to

[050-109]sink into the ground.  The steam-hammer sat as it were on the shoulders

[050-110]of the pile, while it dealt forth its ponderous blows on the pile-head

[050-111]at the rate of 80 a minute, and as the pile sank, the hammer followed

[050-112]it down with never relaxing activity until it was driven home to the

[050-113]required depth.  One of the most ingenious contrivances employed in the

[050-114]driver, which was also adopted in the hammer, was the use of steam as a

[050-115]buffer in the upper part of the cylinder, which had the effect of a

[050-116]recoil spring, and greatly enhanced the force of the downward blow.

[050-117]In 1846, Mr. Nasmyth designed a form of [[steam-engine]] after that of his

[050-118]steam-hammer, which has been extensively adopted all over the world for

[050-119]screw-ships of all sizes.  The pyramidal form of this engine, its great

[050-120]simplicity and GET-AT-ABILITY of parts, together with the circumstance

[050-121]that all the weighty parts of the engine are kept low, have rendered it

[050-122]a universal favourite.  Among the other labour-saving tools invented by

[050-123]Mr. Nasmyth, may be mentioned the well-known planing machine for small

[050-124]work, called "Nasmyth's Steam Arm," now used in every large workshop.

[050-125]It was contrived for the purpose of executing a large order for

[050-126]locomotives received from the [[great-western-railway|Great Western Railway]], and was found of

[050-127]great use in accelerating the work, especially in planing the links,

[050-128]levers, connecting rods, and smaller kinds of wrought-iron work in

[050-129]those engines.  His circular cutter for toothed wheels was another of

[050-130]his handy inventions, which shortly came into general use.  In

[050-131]iron-founding also he introduced a valuable practical improvement.  The

[050-132]old mode of pouring the molten metal into the moulds was by means of a

[050-133]large ladle with one or two cross handles and levers; but many dreadful

[050-134]accidents occurred through a slip of the hand, and Mr. Nasmyth

[050-135]resolved, if possible, to prevent them.  The plan he adopted was to fix

[050-136]a worm-wheel on the side of the ladle, into which a worm was geared,

[050-137]and by this simple contrivance one man was enabled to move the largest

[050-138]ladle on its axis with perfect ease and safety.  By this means the work

[050-139]was more promptly performed, and accidents entirely avoided.

[050-140]Mr. Nasmyth's skill in invention was backed by great energy and a large

[050-141]fund of common sense--qualities not often found united.  These proved

[050-142]of much service to the concern of which he was the head, and indeed

[050-143]constituted the vital force.  The firm prospered as it deserved; and

[050-144]they executed orders not only for [[england|England]], but for most countries in

[050-145]the civilized world.  Mr. Nasmyth had the advantage of being trained in

[050-146]a good school--that of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]--where he had not only learnt

[050-147]handicraft under the eye of that great mechanic, but the art of

[050-148]organizing labour, and (what is of great value to an employer)

[050-149]knowledge of the characters of workmen.  Yet the Nasmyth firm were not

[050-150]without their troubles as respected the mechanics in their employment,

[050-151]and on one occasion they had to pass through the ordeal of a very

[050-152]formidable strike.  The manner in which the inventor of the

[050-153]steam-hammer literally "Scotched" this strike was very characteristic.

[050-154]A clever young man employed by the firm as a brass founder, being found

[050-155]to have a peculiar capacity for skilled mechanical work, had been

[050-156]advanced to the lathe.  The other men objected to his being so employed

[050-157]on the ground that it was against the rules of the trade.  "But he is a

[050-158]first-rate workman," replied the employers, "and we think it right to

[050-159]advance a man according to his conduct and his merits."  "No matter,"

[050-160]said the workmen, "it is against the rules, and if you do not take the

[050-161]man from the lathe, we must turn out."  "Very well; we hold to our

[050-162]right of selecting the best men for the best places, and we will not

[050-163]take the man from the lathe."  The consequence was a general turn out.

[050-164]Pickets were set about the works, and any stray men who went thither to

[050-165]seek employment were waylaid, and if not induced to turn back, were

[050-166]maltreated or annoyed until they were glad to leave.  The works were

[050-167]almost at a standstill.  This state of things could not be allowed to

[050-168]go on, and the head of the firm bestirred himself accordingly with his

[050-169]usual energy.  He went down to [[scotland|Scotland]], searched all the best

[050-170]mechanical workshops there, and after a time succeeded in engaging

[050-171]sixty-four good hands.  He forbade them coming by driblets, but held

[050-172]them together until there was a full freight; and then they came, with

[050-173]their wives, families, chests of drawers, and eight-day clocks, in a

[050-174]steamboat specially hired for their transport from [[greenock|Greenock]] to

[050-175][[liverpool|Liverpool]].  From thence they came by special train to Patricroft, where

[050-176]houses were in readiness for their reception.  The arrival of so

[050-177]numerous, well-dressed, and respectable a corps of workmen and their

[050-178]families was an event in the neighbourhood, and could not fail to

[050-179]strike the "pickets" with surprise.  Next morning the sixty-four

[050-180]Scotchmen assembled in the yard at Patricroft, and after giving "three

[050-181]cheers," went quietly to their work.  The "picketing" went on for a

[050-182]little while longer, but it was of no use against a body of strong men

[050-183]who stood "shouther to shouther," as the new hands did.  It was even

[050-184]bruited about that there were more trains to follow!  It very soon

[050-185]became clear that the back of the strike was broken.  The men returned

[050-186]to their work, and the clever brass founder continued at his

[050-187]turning-lathe, from which he speedily rose to still higher employment.
