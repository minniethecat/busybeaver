# Henry Bessemer

Henry Bessemer (1813–1898) was an English inventor and engineer whose Bessemer process revolutionised steel production.

Smiles notes that among the improved processes for manufacturing steel, "those of Heath, Mushet, and Bessemer" were the most significant of the era. (021-002)

The Bessemer process converted molten [[pig-iron|pig iron]] into steel by blowing air through it, dramatically reducing the cost of steel production.

## Early Life and Career

Born in Charlton, Hertfordshire, Bessemer was a prolific inventor from an early age. Before his steelmaking breakthrough, he had already devised improvements to typesetting machines, sugar-cane processing, and artillery projectiles.

## The Bessemer Process

Bessemer's key insight — patented in 1856 — was that blowing atmospheric air through molten pig iron would oxidise impurities while the heat of oxidation would keep the iron molten. The process could produce large quantities of steel in minutes rather than days.

Bessemer described the process in his own words as "the true components of cast-steel and soft [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]]." (021-023) The Bessemer processes are now in successful operation in [[sheffield|Sheffield]], where they have transformed the steel industry. (021-025)

Yet, like every other invention, this of Mr. Bessemer had long been anticipated. (021-030) Similar ideas had been explored before, as detailed in the "Papers on Iron and Steel." Nevertheless, Mr. Bessemer was the first to bring the process to industrial fruition. (021-035)

## Anticipations and Predecessors

The Bessemer process, while named for its inventor, had precursors. The principle of decarbonising iron by air-blast had been described before — Jemcott's work in particular anticipated key aspects of the discovery. (021-030)

What distinguished Bessemer was not the raw idea, but the determination and engineering skill to convert a laboratory insight into a working industrial process capable of producing steel at unprecedented scale and cost.

## Legacy

Henry Bessemer died in 1898 at the age of 85, having been knighted for his contributions to industry. The Bessemer process marked the beginning of the age of mass-produced steel, enabling railways, skyscrapers, and the infrastructure of the modern world.
