# Iron Bridge

The application of iron to bridge construction — a revolution in civil engineering that began with the world's first iron bridge at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1779 and culminated in the great tubular and suspension bridges of the Victorian era, transforming the scale and durability of infrastructure.

## Definition

An iron bridge is a bridge whose primary structural elements are made of [[pig-iron|cast iron]] (brittle, strong in compression) or wrought iron (tough, strong in tension), or a combination of both. The introduction of iron as a bridge-building material broke free from the limitations of stone and timber — stone arches required massive abutments and could only span limited distances; timber bridges rotted and burned. Iron offered strength, durability, and the ability to be cast or forged into complex structural shapes. (056-063)

The first generation of iron bridges used [[pig-iron|cast iron]] for the main arch members, exploiting [[pig-iron|cast iron]]'s compressive strength while avoiding its brittleness in tension. Later bridges used wrought iron for tension members (chains, ties) and eventually for entire structures, as the properties of wrought iron — toughness and ductility — were better suited to the dynamic loads experienced by bridges.

## Historical Development

### [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]: The First Iron Bridge

The first iron bridge in the world was erected across the [[severn|River Severn]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1779. **[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]]II** (grandson of the coke-smelting pioneer) undertook the project, casting the bridge components at the family's [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] Iron Works. The bridge spans 100 feet (30 metres) in a single elegant arch, constructed from five parallel cast-iron ribs. (001-180)

"It is worthy of note that the first iron bridge ever erected was" built entirely of [[pig-iron|cast iron]], using the same material and techniques that the Darby family had pioneered in iron smelting. The bridge demonstrated that iron could be used structurally on a grand scale. (017-015)

### Telford and the Menai Suspension Bridge

**[[thomas-telford|Thomas Telford]]** designed the Menai Suspension Bridge (1826), spanning 579 feet across the Menai Strait. This was the first major suspension bridge to use wrought-iron chains, exploiting the material's tensile strength. Telford's iron bridges "across the Straits of Menai" were engineering marvels of their age. (056-071)

### Stephenson's [[tubular-bridge|Tubular Bridge]]s

**[[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]]** took iron bridge construction to its ultimate 19th-century form with the tubular bridges at Conway (1848) and the [[tubular-bridge|Britannia Bridge]] across the Menai Strait (1850). These were wrought-iron boxes through which trains ran — a completely new structural concept. Stephenson's "direction was the preparation of the design of a cast-iron bridge" that evolved into the tubular concept after extensive experimentation. (054-037)

### The Iron Bridge Age

By the 1850s, iron bridges had transformed the landscape. Bridges could span wider rivers and gorges than ever before, carry heavier loads (particularly railways), and be erected faster than masonry structures. The "iron-and-crystal palaces, iron churches, and iron bridges" of the age symbolised the triumph of industrial materials over traditional craftsmanship. (056-063)

## Structural Principles

Iron bridges exploited the distinct properties of the two main iron materials:

- **Cast iron**: Excellent in compression (200+ MPa), used for arches, columns, and compression members. However, brittle — sudden failure without warning if overloaded in tension.
- **Wrought iron**: Excellent in tension (300+ MPa), tough and ductile, used for chains, tie rods, and later for entire girder sections. Could be forge-welded into large assemblies.

The arch form was natural for [[pig-iron|cast iron]], as it kept the material in compression. The suspension form exploited wrought iron's tensile strength. Tubular and girder bridges used both materials strategically.

## Economic and Industrial Impact

Iron bridges transformed 19th-century infrastructure:

- **Railways**: Iron bridges carried railways across valleys and rivers, making the railway network physically possible
- **Canals**: Iron aqueducts (like Telford's Pontcysyllte) carried canals across valleys
- **Urban growth**: Iron bridges connected growing cities across rivers, enabling suburban expansion
- **Empire**: British iron bridges were exported worldwide, from India to Australia

The iron bridge industry drove demand for iron production, stimulated innovation in foundry practice and forging, and created a new profession — the civil engineer — whose expertise in structural ironwork shaped the built environment of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].
