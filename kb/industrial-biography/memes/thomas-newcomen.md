Thomas Newcomen (1664–1729) was an English ironmonger and [[inventor]] who built the first practical atmospheric steam engine. His pumping engine, first deployed in 1712, became the foundation of steam power for over half a century and was used extensively in [[coal-mining]] and metal-mining districts across Britain. Newcomen's engine was the crucial link in the chain that led from Thomas Savary to [[James Watt]].

## Early Life and Background

Newcomen was born in Dartmouth, [[devon|Devon]], in 1664. He was by trade an ironmonger — a dealer in iron goods — which brought him into contact with the mining districts of [[cornwall|Cornwall]] and Devon, where the problem of flooding in deep mine workings was a constant and costly challenge. The mines of the southwest, particularly the tin and copper mines, required ever more powerful pumping as they reached greater depths.

## The [[atmospheric-engine|Atmospheric Engine]]

### The Problem of Mine Drainage

By the early eighteenth century, many mines in Britain faced abandonment because existing methods of pumping — horse-gins, water-wheels, and chain-pumps — could not lift water from depths below about 150 feet. Thomas Savary had patented a steam pump in 1698, but it was limited in lift height and prone to boiler explosions at the high pressures required. Newcomen's insight was to use atmospheric pressure rather than high-pressure steam to drive the piston. (016-097)

### The [[atmospheric-engine|Newcomen Engine]]

Newcomen's engine worked by admitting low-pressure steam into a vertical cylinder beneath a piston. The steam was then condensed by a jet of cold water, creating a partial vacuum. Atmospheric pressure drove the piston down, and this motion was transmitted by a massive overhead beam to pump rods in the mine shaft. The engine was safe, reliable, and could lift water from any depth.

Newcomen erected his first working engine at the Coneygree Colliery near [[dudley|Dudley]] Castle in 1712. The engine was an immediate success, and within a few years Newcomen engines were pumping mines across Britain and in continental Europe. Many were manufactured at the [[Coalbrookdale]] ironworks, where the skill of the Quaker ironmasters in casting large cylinders proved essential. (016-097) (034-024)

> "There was a considerable demand for pumping-engines of Newcomen's construction, many of which were made at the Coalbrookdale Works." (016-097)

## Relationship to Later Inventors

Newcomen's engine remained the dominant prime mover in Britain for over sixty years. The [[Newcomen engine]] was still in widespread use when [[James Watt]], a mathematical instrument maker at [[glasgow|Glasgow]], began his experiments. The deficiencies of the Newcomen engine — particularly its enormous fuel consumption — were what stimulated Watt's inventive work. Watt's separate condenser, patented in 1769, was the single greatest improvement ever made to the steam engine, but it rested entirely on the foundation Newcomen had laid. (025-056) (025-060)

Smiles places Newcomen in a direct lineage of inventive progress: "Thus Savary and Newcomen led up to Watt; Cugnot, [[william-murdock|Murdock]], and [[Richard Trevithick|Trevithick]] to the Stephensons; and Maudslay to Clement, Roberts, Nasmyth, Whitworth, and many more mechanical worthies." (031-158) (031-159)

Newcomen's engine was described (in retrospect) by some as a "monstrous" thing, but it was in truth one of the greatest single inventions in the history of technology. It turned the latent power of coal into useful mechanical work for the first time on an industrial scale. (034-024)
