**Ironstone** — iron-bearing sedimentary rock found abundantly in Britain's Coal Measures and other geological formations — was the primary raw material of the nation's iron industry. Unlike rich foreign ores, the lean British ironstone required substantial processing, but its proximity to [[coal-mining|Coal]] and [[limestone|Limestone]] gave Britain a decisive industrial advantage.

## Definition

Ironstone is a clayey carbonate of iron, typically containing 25–35% metallic iron, interbedded with coal seams and shales. "Persons unacquainted with minerals would be unable to discover the slightest affinity between the rough ironstone as brought up from the mine, and the iron or steel of commerce." (003-126) Unlike the rich oxide ores of Sweden or Spain, British ironstone required more fuel and furnace capacity to reduce — but it was available in vast quantities, close to the coal that fuelled its smelting.

## Mining and Processing

In early times, ironstone was obtained from shallow "bell pits" — circular shafts sunk into the ironstone beds. In [[leeds|Leeds]], "many 'bell pits' have been brought to light, from which ironstone has been removed." (007-037) In the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] and other ancient iron districts, later ironmasters "instead of digging for ironstone, resorted to the beds of ancient scoriae for their principal supply" — re-smelting the slag heaps left by Roman and medieval furnaces, which still contained substantial iron. (008-097)

The cinders from Roman workings were particularly valued: "these cinders have they which make the prime and best iron, and with much less charcoal than doth the ironstone." (006-061) This practice — recorded by [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]] in 1677 — continued well into the 18th century.

## Industrial Significance

The geological coincidence of ironstone, coal, and limestone in close proximity within Britain's Carboniferous formations was arguably the single most important natural endowment of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. It eliminated transport costs between mine, fuel source, and furnace flux — an advantage no Continental iron district could match until the railway age.

The Cleveland ironstone field, discovered and exploited from the 1850s, became the foundation of the [[middlesbrough|Middlesbrough]] iron district and the [[bolckow-vaughan|Bolckow & Vaughan]] empire, producing pig iron on a scale that eclipsed all earlier British iron regions.
