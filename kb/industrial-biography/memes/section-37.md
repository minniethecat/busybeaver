[037-001]A very few words of explanation will serve to illustrate the importance

[037-002]of Maudslay's invention.  Every person is familiar with the uses of the

[037-003]common turning-lathe.  It is a favourite machine with amateur

[037-004]mechanics, and its employment is indispensable for the execution of all

[037-005]kinds of rounded work in wood and metal.  Perhaps there is no

[037-006]contrivance by which the skill of the handicraftsman has been more

[037-007]effectually aided than by this machine.  Its origin is lost in the

[037-008]shades of antiquity.  Its most ancient form was probably the potter's

[037-009]wheel, from which it advanced, by successive improvements, to its

[037-010]present highly improved form.  It was found that, by whatever means a

[037-011]substance capable of being cut could be made to revolve with a circular

[037-012]motion round a fixed right line as a centre, a cutting tool applied to

[037-013]its surface would remove the inequalities so that any part of such

[037-014]surface should be equidistant from that centre.  Such is the

[037-015]fundamental idea of the ordinary turning-lathe.  The ingenuity and

[037-016]experience of mechanics working such an instrument enabled them to add

[037-017]many improvements to it; until the skilful artisan at length produced

[037-018]not merely circular turning of the most beautiful and accurate

[037-019]description, but exquisite figure-work, and complicated geometrical

[037-020]designs, depending upon the cycloidal and eccentric movements which

[037-021]were from time to time added to the machine.

[037-022]The artisans of the Middle Ages were very skilful in the use of the

[037-023]lathe, and turned out much beautiful screen and stall work, still to be

[037-024]seen in our cathedrals, as well as twisted and swash-work for the

[037-025]balusters of staircases and other ornamental purposes.  English

[037-026]mechanics seem early to have distinguished themselves as improvers of

[037-027]the lathe; and in Moxon's 'Treatise on Turning,' published in 1680, we

[037-028]find Mr. Thomas Oldfield, at the sign of the Flower-de-Luce, near the

[037-029]Savoy in the Strand, named as an excellent maker of oval-engines and

[037-030]swash-engines, showing that such machines were then in some demand.

[037-031]The French writer Plumier[3] also mentions an ingenious modification of

[037-032]the lathe by means of which any kind of reticulated form could be given

[037-033]to the work; and, from it's being employed to ornament the handles of

[037-034]knives, it was called by him the "Machine a manche de Couteau

[037-035]d'Angleterre."  But the French artisans were at that time much better

[037-036]skilled than the English in the use of tools, and it is most probable

[037-037]that we owe to the Flemish and French Protestant workmen who flocked

[037-038]into [[england|England]] in such large numbers during the religious persecutions of

[037-039]the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the improvement, if not the

[037-040]introduction, of the art of turning, as well as many other arts

[037-041]hereafter to be referred to.  It is certain that at the period to which

[037-042]we refer numerous treatises were published in France on the art of

[037-043]turning, some of them of a most elaborate character.  Such were the

[037-044]works of De la Hire,[4] who described how every kind of polygon might

[037-045]be made by the lathe; De la Condamine,[5] who showed how a lathe could

[037-046]turn all sorts of irregular figures by means of tracers; and of Grand

[037-047]Jean, Morin,[6] Plumier, Bergeron, and many other writers.

[037-048]The work of Plumier is especially elaborate, entering into the

[037-049]construction of the lathe in its various parts, the making of the tools

[037-050]and cutters, and the different motions to be given to the machine by

[037-051]means of wheels, eccentrics, and other expedients, amongst which may be

[037-052]mentioned one very much resembling the slide rest and planing-machine

[037-053]combined.[7]  From this work it appears that turning had long been a

[037-054]favourite pursuit in France with amateurs of all ranks, who spared no

[037-055]expense in the contrivance and perfection of elaborate machinery for

[037-056]the production of complex figures.[8]  There was at that time a great

[037-057]passion for automata in France, which gave rise to many highly

[037-058]ingenious devices, such as Camus's miniature carriage (made for Louis

[037-059]XIV. when a child), Degennes' mechanical peacock, Vancanson's duck, and

[037-060]Maillardet's conjuror.  It had the effect of introducing among the

[037-061]higher order of artists habits of nice and [[precision-engineering|accurate workmanship]] in

[037-062]executing delicate pieces of machinery; and the same combination of

[037-063]mechanical powers which made the steel spider crawl, the duck quack, or

[037-064]waved the tiny rod of the magician, contributed in future years to

[037-065]purposes of higher import,--the wheels and pinions, which in these

[037-066]automata almost eluded the human senses by their minuteness,

[037-067]reappearing in modern times in the stupendous mechanism of our

[037-068]self-acting lathes, spinning-mules, and steam-engines.

[037-069]"In our own country," says Professor Willis, "the literature of this

[037-070]subject is so defective that it is very difficult to discover what

[037-071]progress we were making during the seventeenth and eighteenth

[037-072]centuries." [9]  We believe the fact to be, that the progress made in

[037-073][[england|England]] down to the end of last century had been very small indeed, and

[037-074]that the lathe had experienced little or no improvement until Maudslay

[037-075]took it in hand.  Nothing seems to have been known of the slide rest

[037-076]until he re-invented it and applied it to the production of machinery

[037-077]of a far more elaborate character than had ever before been

[037-078]contemplated as possible.  Professor Willis says that Bramah's, in

[037-079]other words Maudslay's, slide rest of 1794 is so different from that

[037-080]described in the French 'Encyclopedie in 1772, that the two could not

[037-081]have had a common origin.  We are therefore led to the conclusion that

[037-082]Maudslay's invention was entirely independent of all that had gone

[037-083]before, and that he contrived it for the special purpose of overcoming

[037-084]the difficulties which he himself experienced in turning out duplicate

[037-085]parts in large numbers.  At all events, he was so early and zealous a

[037-086]promoter of its use, that we think he may, in the eyes of all practical

[037-087]mechanics, stand as the parent of its introduction to the workshops of

[037-088]England.

[037-089]It is unquestionable that at the time when Maudslay began the

[037-090]improvement of [[machine-tool|machine-tools]], the methods of working in wood and metals

[037-091]were exceedingly imperfect.  Mr. [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] has stated that when

[037-092]he first became acquainted with mechanical engineering, about sixty

[037-093]years ago, there were no self-acting tools; everything was executed by

[037-094]hand.  There were neither planing, slotting, nor [[shaping-machine|shaping machine]]s; and

[037-095]the whole stock of an engineering or machine establishment might be

[037-096]summed up in a few ill-constructed lathes, and a few drills and boring

[037-097]machines of rude construction.[10]  Our mechanics were equally backward

[037-098]in contrivances for working in wood.  Thus, when [[samuel-bentham|Sir Samuel Bentham]]

[037-099]made a tour through the manufacturing districts of England in 1791, he

[037-100]was surprised to find how little had been done to substitute the

[037-101]invariable accuracy of machinery for the uncertain dexterity of the

[037-102]human hand.  Steam-power was as yet only employed in driving

[037-103]spinning-machines, rolling metals, pumping water, and such like

[037-104]purposes.  In the working of wood no machinery had been introduced

[037-105]beyond the common turning-lathe and some saws, and a few boring tools

[037-106]used in making blocks for the navy.  Even saws worked by inanimate

[037-107]force for slitting timber, though in extensive use in foreign

[037-108]countries, were nowhere to be found in Great Britain.[11]  As

[037-109]everything depended on the dexterity of hand and correctness of eye of

[037-110]the workmen, the work turned out was of very unequal merit, besides

[037-111]being exceedingly costly.  Even in the construction of comparatively

[037-112]simple machines, the expense was so great as to present a formidable

[037-113]obstacle to their introduction and extensive use; and but for the

[037-114]invention of  machine-making tools, the use of the [[steam-engine]] in the

[037-115]various forms in which it is now applied for the production of power

[037-116]could never have become general.

[037-117]In turning a piece of work on the old-fashioned lathe, the workman

[037-118]applied and guided his tool by means of muscular strength.  The work

[037-119]was made to revolve, and the turner, holding the cutting tool firmly

[037-120]upon the long, straight, guiding edge of the rest, along which he

[037-121]carried it, and pressing its point firmly against the article to be

[037-122]turned, was thus enabled to reduce its surface to the required size and

[037-123]shape.  Some dexterous turners were able, with practice and

[037-124]carefulness, to execute very clever pieces of work by this simple

[037-125]means.  But when the article to be turned was of considerable size, and

[037-126]especially when it was of metal, the expenditure of muscular strength

[037-127]was so great that the workman soon became exhausted.  The slightest

[037-128]variation in the pressure of the tool led to an irregularity of

[037-129]surface; and with the utmost care on the workman's part, he could not

[037-130]avoid occasionally cutting a little too deep, in consequence of which

[037-131]he must necessarily go over the surface again, to reduce the whole to

[037-132]the level of that accidentally cut too deep; and thus possibly the job

[037-133]would be altogether spoiled by the diameter of the article under

[037-134]operation being made too small for its intended purpose.

[037-135]The introduction of the slide rest furnished a complete remedy for this

[037-136]source of imperfection.  The principle of the invention consists in

[037-137]constructing and fitting the rest so that, instead of being screwed

[037-138]down to one place, and the tool in the hands of the workman travelling

[037-139]over it, the rest shall itself hold the cutting tool firmly fixed in

[037-140]it, and slide along the surface of the bench in a direction exactly

[037-141]parallel with the axis of the work.  Before its invention various

[037-142]methods had been tried with the object of enabling the work to be

[037-143]turned true independent of the dexterity of the workman.  Thus, a

[037-144]square steel cutter used to be firmly fixed in a bed, along which it

[037-145]was wedged from point to point of the work, and tolerable accuracy was

[037-146]in this way secured.  But the slide rest was much more easily managed,

[037-147]and the result was much more satisfactory.  All that the workman had to

[037-148]do, after the tool was firmly fitted into the rest, was merely to turn

[037-149]a screw-handle, and thus advance the cutter along the face of the work

[037-150]as required, with an expenditure of strength so slight as scarcely to

[037-151]be appreciable.  And even this labour has now been got rid of; for, by

[037-152]an arrangement of the gearing, the slide itself has been made

[037-153]self-acting, and advances with the revolution of the work in the lathe,

[037-154]which thus supplies the place of the workman's hand.  The accuracy of

[037-155]the turning done by this beautiful yet simple arrangement is as

[037-156]mechanically perfect as work can be.  The pair of steel fingers which

[037-157]hold the cutting tool firmly in their grasp never tire, and it moves

[037-158]along the metal to be cut with an accuracy and precision which the

[037-159]human hand, however skilled, could never equal.

[037-160]The effects of the introduction of the slide rest were very shortly

[037-161]felt in all departments of mechanism.  Though it had to encounter some

[037-162]of the ridicule with which new methods of working are usually received,

[037-163]and for a time was spoken of in derision as "Maudslay's Go-cart,"--its

[037-164]practical advantages were so decided that it gradually made its way,

[037-165]and became an established tool in all the best mechanical workshops.

[037-166]It was found alike capable of executing the most delicate and the most

[037-167]ponderous pieces of machinery; and as slide-lathes could be

[037-168]manufactured to any extent, machinery, steam-engines, and all kinds of

[037-169]metal work could now be turned out in a quantity and at a price that,

[037-170]but for its use, could never have been practicable.  In course of time

[037-171]various modifications of the machine were introduced--such as the

[037-172]planing machine, the wheel-cutting machine, and other beautiful tools

[037-173]on the slide-rest principle,--the result of which has been that

[037-174]extraordinary development of mechanical production and power which is

[037-175]so characteristic a feature of the age we live in.

[037-176]"It is not, indeed, saying at all too much to state," says Mr.

[037-177]Nasmyth,[12] a most competent judge in such a matter, "that its

[037-178]influence in improving and extending the use of machinery has been as

[037-179]great as that produced by the improvement of the steam-engine in

[037-180]respect to perfecting manufactures and extending commerce, inasmuch as

[037-181]without the aid of the vast accession to our power of producing perfect

[037-182]mechanism which it at once supplied, we could never have worked out

[037-183]into practical and profitable forms the conceptions of those master

[037-184]minds who, during the last half century, have so successfully pioneered

[037-185]the way for mankind.  The steam-engine itself, which supplies us with

[037-186]such unbounded power, owes its present perfection to this most

[037-187]admirable means of giving to metallic objects the most precise and

[037-188]perfect geometrical forms.  How could we, for instance, have good

[037-189]steam-engines if we had not the means of boring out a true cylinder, or

[037-190]turning a true piston-rod, or planing a valve face? It is this alone

[037-191]which has furnished us with the means of carrying into practice the

[037-192]accumulated result's of scientific investigation on mechanical

[037-193]subjects.  It would be blamable indeed," continues Mr. Nasmyth, "after

[037-194]having endeavoured to set forth the vast advantages which have been

[037-195]conferred on the mechanical world, and therefore on mankind generally,

[037-196]by the invention and introduction of the [[slide-rest|Slide Rest]], were I to suppress

[037-197]the name of that admirable individual to whom we are indebted for this

[037-198]powerful agent towards the attainment of mechanical perfection.  I

[037-199]allude to [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], whose useful life was enthusiastically

[037-200]devoted to the grand object of improving our means of producing perfect

[037-201]workmanship and machinery:  to him we are certainly indebted for the

[037-202]slide rest, and, consequently, to say the least, we are indirectly so

[037-203]for the vast benefits which have resulted from the introduction of so

[037-204]powerful an agent in perfecting our machinery and mechanism generally.

[037-205]The indefatigable care which he took in inculcating and diffusing among

[037-206]his workmen, and mechanical men generally, sound ideas of practical

[037-207]knowledge and refined views of construction, have rendered and ever

[037-208]will continue to render his name identified with all that is noble in

[037-209]the ambition of a lover of mechanical perfection."
