The spinning frame (also known as the water frame) was a powered spinning machine invented by [[Richard Arkwright]] and patented in 1769. It used a system of rollers rotating at different speeds to draw out and twist cotton fibers into yarn, producing stronger thread than the manual spinning jenny and enabling the rise of the water-powered cotton factory.

## Invention Background

Before Arkwright's invention, cotton spinning was done by hand or with [[James Hargreaves]]' spinning jenny (1764), which could spin multiple threads but produced relatively weak yarn suitable only for weft. A machine that could produce strong warp yarn was needed to enable all-cotton cloth production. Arkwright's machinery addressed this gap. (029-055)

## Inventor

- **Inventor**: [[Richard Arkwright]]
- **Patent Year**: 1769
- **Related Inventors**: [[James Hargreaves]], [[Samuel Crompton]], [[Richard Roberts]]

The invention of the spinning machine has been variously attributed to Wyatt, Hargreaves, Higley, and Arkwright. (031-125) Arkwright's specific contribution was the roller-drawing system that produced yarn strong enough for warp threads, combined with water-power driving to enable factory-scale production.

## Working Principle

Arkwright's spinning frame passed cotton rovings through successive pairs of rollers, each rotating faster than the previous pair, thereby drawing the fibers out into finer and finer strands. The drawn roving was then twisted by a rotating spindle to lock the fibers together. The entire machine was initially powered by horse (hence "spinning frame") and later by water wheels (hence "water frame").

## Technical Significance

Arkwright's spinning frame was a foundational invention of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. It enabled the first water-powered cotton mills and the [[industrial-revolution|factory system]] that transformed textile production. Employment from the great firms of Arkwright and his partners spread through [[lancashire|Lancashire]], [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]], and [[scotland|Scotland]]. (045-068)

The spinning frame was later combined with features of the spinning jenny by [[Samuel Crompton]] in his [[mule-spinning|spinning mule]] (1779), and eventually fully automated by [[Richard Roberts]] in the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] (1825).

## Later Improvements

The roller-drawing principle remained central to cotton spinning throughout the 19th century. Arkwright's frame evolved through the mule and ring-spinning frame, but the fundamental mechanism—roller drafting followed by spindle twisting—persisted into modern textile machinery.
