# Tubular Bridge

The tubular bridge — a railway bridge formed of a continuous wrought-iron rectangular tube, through which the train runs as if through a tunnel — was one of the boldest structural innovations of the railway age. Conceived for the Chester and Holyhead Railway's crossings of the Conway River and the Menai Strait, the Britannia and Conway tubular bridges demonstrated that wrought iron could span distances previously achievable only with suspension chains.

## Background

When the Chester and Holyhead Railway reached the Menai Strait in the 1840s, [[Robert Stephenson]] faced a formidable challenge. The strait was a busy shipping channel; an arch bridge would obstruct navigation, and a suspension bridge was considered too flexible for the weight and vibration of railway trains.

Stephenson turned to [[William Fairbairn]], the leading authority on iron construction, to investigate whether a wrought-iron tube of sufficient strength could carry a railway across such spans. Fairbairn conducted extensive experiments on the strength of wrought-iron plates and girders at his Millwall works.

## Inventor

- **Originator of the concept**: "The original idea of the tubular bridge belongs to Mr. Fairbairn." (056-023)
- **Chief engineer**: [[Robert Stephenson]] directed the overall design and construction
- **Ironwork**: Supplied and erected by Fairbairn's firm

## Design and Construction

The tubular bridge was essentially a rectangular wrought-iron box girder, large enough for a train to pass through. The top and bottom of the tube acted as the compression and tension flanges of a beam; the sides provided shear resistance and prevented buckling.

The Britannia Bridge (across the Menai Strait) and the Conway Bridge were the first and most famous examples. Both comprised pairs of tubes, one for each track, fabricated from wrought-iron plates riveted together.

The construction of these bridges was the spur that drove [[Richard Roberts]] to invent his [[Punching Machine|Jacquard punching machine]]. During assembly of the Conway bridge girders in 1848, labour disputes and the slow pace of hand-punching thousands of rivet holes threatened the contract deadline. Roberts responded with the self-acting punching machine that "enabled the contractors to proceed with the punching of the plates independent of the refractory men." (047-043)

## Significance

The tubular bridges demonstrated several principles that became central to structural engineering:

- **Wrought iron as a structural material**: The bridges proved that iron could be used for the largest engineering structures, not just machines
- **Scientific design**: Fairbairn's experiments established the practice of testing materials and structural forms before construction — a forerunner of modern civil engineering methodology
- **Precision manufacture**: The thousands of rivet holes in bridge plates required a new level of accuracy in fabrication, spurring the development of the [[punching-machine|Jacquard punching machine]] and [[Riveting Machine]]

The tubular bridge form was superseded within a generation by open-web girder and truss designs, but its influence on structural thinking and fabrication methods endured.
