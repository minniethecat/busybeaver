[040-001]One of the things in which Mr. Maudslay took just pride was in the

[040-002]excellence of his work.  In designing and executing it, his main object

[040-003]was to do it in the best possible style and finish, altogether

[040-004]irrespective of the probable pecuniary results.  This he regarded in

[040-005]the light of a duty he could not and would not evade, independent of

[040-006]its being a good investment for securing a future reputation; and the

[040-007]character which he thus obtained, although at times purchased at great

[040-008]cost, eventually justified the soundness of his views.  As the eminent

[040-009]Mr. Penn, the head of the great engineering firm, is accustomed to say,

[040-010]"I cannot afford to turn out second-rate work," so Mr. Maudslay found

[040-011]both character and profit in striving after the highest excellence in

[040-012]his productions.  He was particular even in the minutest details.  Thus

[040-013]one of the points on which he insisted--apparently a trivial matter,

[040-014]but in reality of considerable importance in mechanical

[040-015]construction--was the avoidance of sharp interior angles in ironwork,

[040-016]whether wrought or cast; for he found that in such interior angles

[040-017]cracks were apt to originate; and when the article was a tool, the

[040-018]sharp angle was less pleasant to the hand as well as to the eye.  In

[040-019]the application of his favourite round or hollow corner system--as, for

[040-020]instance, in the case of the points of junction of the arms of a wheel

[040-021]with its centre and rim--he used to illustrate its superiority by

[040-022]holding up his hand and pointing out the nice rounded hollow at the

[040-023]junction of the fingers, or by referring to the junction of the

[040-024]branches to the stem of a tree.  Hence he made a point of having all

[040-025]the angles of his machine framework nicely rounded off on their

[040-026]exterior, and carefully hollowed in their interior angles.  In forging

[040-027]such articles he would so shape his metal before bending that the

[040-028]result should be the right hollow or rounded corner when bent; the

[040-029]anticipated external angle falling into its proper place when the bar

[040-030]so shaped was brought to its ultimate form.  In all such matters of

[040-031]detail he was greatly assisted by his early dexterity as a blacksmith;

[040-032]and he used to say that to be a good smith you must be able to SEE in

[040-033]the bar of iron the object proposed to be got out of it by the hammer

[040-034]or the tool, just as the sculptor is supposed to see in the block of

[040-035]stone the statue which he proposes to bring forth from it by his mind

[040-036]and his chisel.

[040-037]Mr. Maudslay did not allow himself to forget his skill in the use of

[040-038]the hammer, and to the last he took pleasure in handling it, sometimes

[040-039]in the way of business, and often through sheer love of his art.  Mr

[040-040]Nasmyth says, "It was one of my duties, while acting as assistant in

[040-041]his beautiful little workshop, to keep up a stock of handy bars of lead

[040-042]which he had placed on a shelf under his work-bench, which was of thick

[040-043]slate for the more ready making of his usual illustrative sketches of

[040-044]machinery in chalk.  His love of iron-forging led him to take delight

[040-045]in forging the models of work to be ultimately done in iron; and cold

[040-046]lead being of about the same malleability as red-hot iron, furnished a

[040-047]convenient material for illustrating the method to be adopted with the

[040-048]large work.  I well remember the smile of satisfaction that lit up his

[040-049]honest face when he met with a good excuse for 'having a go at' one of

[040-050]the bars of lead with hammer and anvil as if it were a bar of iron; and

[040-051]how, with a few dexterous strokes, punchings of holes, and rounded

[040-052]notches, he would give the rough bar or block its desired form.  He

[040-053]always aimed at working it out of the solid as much as possible, so as

[040-054]to avoid the risk of any concealed defect, to which ironwork built up

[040-055]of welded parts is so liable; and when he had thus cleverly finished

[040-056]his model, he used forthwith to send for the foreman of smiths, and

[040-057]show him how he was to instruct his men as to the proper forging of the

[040-058]desired object."  One of Mr. Maudslay's old workmen, when informing us

[040-059]of the skilful manner in which he handled the file, said, "It was a

[040-060]pleasure to see him handle a tool of any kind, but he was QUITE

[040-061]SPLENDID with an eighteen-inch file!" The vice at which he worked was

[040-062]constructed by himself, and it was perfect of its kind.  It could be

[040-063]turned round to any position on the bench; the jaws would turn from the

[040-064]horizontal to the perpendicular or any other position--upside-down if

[040-065]necessary--and they would open twelve inches parallel.

[040-066]Mr. Nasmyth furnishes the following further recollections of Mr.

[040-067]Maudslay, which will serve in some measure to illustrate his personal

[040-068]character.  "[[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]," he says, "lived in the days of

[040-069]snuff-taking, which unhappily, as I think, has given way to the

[040-070]cigar-smoking system.  He enjoyed his occasional pinch very much.  It

[040-071]generally preceded the giving out of a new notion or suggestion for an

[040-072]improvement or alteration of some job in hand.  As with most of those

[040-073]who enjoy their pinch, about three times as much was taken between the

[040-074]fingers as was utilized by the nose, and the consequence was that a

[040-075]large unconsumed surplus collected in the folds of the master's

[040-076]waistcoat as he sat working at his bench.  Sometimes a file, or a tool,

[040-077]or some small piece of work would drop, and then it was my duty to go

[040-078]down on my knees and fetch it up.  On such occasions, while waiting for

[040-079]the article, he would take the opportunity of pulling down his

[040-080]waistcoat front, which had become disarranged by his energetic working

[040-081]at the bench; and many a time have I come up with the dropped article,

[040-082]half-blinded by the snuff jerked into my eyes from off his waistcoat

[040-083]front.

[040-084]"All the while he was at work he would be narrating some incident in

[040-085]his past life, or describing the progress of some new and important

[040-086]undertaking, in illustrating which he would use the bit of chalk ready

[040-087]to his hand upon the slate bench before him, which was thus in almost

[040-088]constant use.  One of the pleasures he indulged in while he sat at work

[040-089]was Music, of which he was very fond,--more particularly of melodies

[040-090]and airs which took a lasting hold on his mind.  Hence he was never

[040-091]without an assortment of musical boxes, some of which were of a large

[040-092]size.  One of these he would set agoing on his library table, which was

[040-093]next to his workshop, and with the door kept open, he was thus enabled

[040-094]to enjoy the music while he sat working at his bench.  Intimate friends

[040-095]would frequently call upon him and sit by the hour, but though talking

[040-096]all the while he never dropped his work, but continued employed on it

[040-097]with as much zeal as if he were only beginning life.  His old friend

[040-098][[samuel-bentham|Sir Samuel Bentham]] was a frequent caller in this way, as well as Sir

[040-099]Isambard Brunel while occupied with his [[thames-tunnel|[[thames|Thames]] Tunnel]] works[15] and Mr.

[040-100]Chantrey, who was accustomed to consult him about the casting of his

[040-101]bronze statuary.  Mr. Barton of the [[royal-mint|Royal Mint]], and [[bryan-donkin|Mr. Donkin]] the

[040-102]engineer, with whom Mr. Barton was associated in ascertaining and

[040-103]devising a correct system of dividing the Standard Yard, and many

[040-104]others, had like audience of Mr. Maudslay in his little workshop, for

[040-105]friendly converse, for advice, or on affairs of business.

[040-106]"It was a special and constant practice with him on a workman's

[040-107]holiday, or on a Sunday morning, to take a walk through his workshops

[040-108]when all was quiet, and then and there examine the various jobs in

[040-109]hand.  On such occasions he carried with him a piece of chalk, with

[040-110]which, in a neat and very legible hand, he would record his remarks in

[040-111]the most pithy and sometimes caustic terms.  Any evidence of want of

[040-112]correctness in setting things square, or in 'flat filing,' which he

[040-113]held in high esteem, or untidiness in not sweeping down the bench and

[040-114]laying the tools in order, was sure to have a record in chalk made on

[040-115]the spot.  If it was a mild case, the reproof was recorded in gentle

[040-116]terms, simply to show that the master's eye was on the workman; but

[040-117]where the case deserved hearty approbation or required equally hearty

[040-118]reproof, the words employed were few, but went straight to the mark.

[040-119]These chalk jottings on the bench were held in the highest respect by

[040-120]the workmen themselves, whether they conveyed praise or blame, as they

[040-121]were sure to be deserved; and when the men next assembled, it soon

[040-122]became known all over the shop who had received the honour or otherwise

[040-123]of one of the master's bench memoranda in chalk."

[040-124]The vigilant, the critical, and yet withal the generous eye of the

[040-125]master being over all his workmen, it will readily be understood how

[040-126]Maudslay's works came to be regarded as a first-class school for

[040-127]mechanical engineers.  Every one felt that the quality of his

[040-128]workmanship was fully understood; and, if he had the right stuff in

[040-129]him, and was determined to advance, that his progress in skill would be

[040-130]thoroughly appreciated.  It is scarcely necessary to point out how this

[040-131]feeling, pervading the establishment, must have operated, not only in

[040-132]maintaining the quality of the work, but in improving the character of

[040-133]the workmen.  The results were felt in the increased practical ability

[040-134]of a large number of artisans, some of whom subsequently rose to the

[040-135]highest distinction.  Indeed it may be said that what Oxford and

[040-136]Cambridge are in letters, workshops such as Maudslay's and Penn's are

[040-137]in mechanics.  Nor can Oxford and Cambridge men be prouder of the

[040-138]connection with their respective colleges than mechanics such as

[040-139]Whitworth, Nasmyth, Roberts, Muir, and Lewis, are of their connection

[040-140]with the school of Maudslay.  For all these distinguished engineers at

[040-141]one time or another formed part of his working staff, and were trained

[040-142]to the exercise of their special abilities under his own eye.  The

[040-143]result has been a development of mechanical ability the like of which

[040-144]perhaps is not to be found in any age or country.

[040-145]Although Mr. Maudslay was an unceasing inventor, he troubled himself

[040-146]very little about patenting his inventions.  He considered that the

[040-147]superiority of his tools and the excellence of his work were his surest

[040-148]protection.  Yet he had sometimes the annoyance of being threatened

[040-149]with actions by persons who had patented the inventions which he

[040-150]himself had made.[16]  He was much beset by inventors, sometimes sadly

[040-151]out at elbows, but always with a boundless fortune looming before them.

[040-152]To such as applied to him for advice in a frank and candid spirit, he

[040-153]did not hesitate to speak freely, and communicate the results of his

[040-154]great experience in the most liberal manner; and to poor and deserving

[040-155]men of this class he was often found as ready to help them with his

[040-156]purse as with his still more valuable advice.  He had a singular way of

[040-157]estimating the abilities of those who thus called upon him about their

[040-158]projects.  The highest order of man was marked in his own mind at 100

[040-159]degrees; and by this ideal standard he measured others, setting them

[040-160]down at 90 degrees, 80 degrees, and so on.  A very first-rate man he

[040-161]would set down at 95 degrees, but men of this rank were exceedingly

[040-162]rare.  After an interview with one of the applicants to him for advice,

[040-163]he would say to his pupil Nasmyth, "Jem, I think that man may be set

[040-164]down at 45 degrees, but he might be WORKED UP TO 60 degrees"--a common

[040-165]enough way of speaking of the working of a [[steam-engine]], but a somewhat

[040-166]novel though by no means an inexpressive method of estimating the

[040-167]powers of an individual.
