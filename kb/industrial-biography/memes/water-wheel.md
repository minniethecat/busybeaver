# Water Wheel

A rotary mechanical device driven by the weight or impulse of falling water, converting hydraulic energy into shaft power to drive mills, hammers, bellows, and early factory machinery — the dominant source of industrial power in Britain before the widespread adoption of the [[Steam Engine]].

## Definition

A water wheel consists of a large wheel with paddles, buckets, or vanes around its circumference, mounted on a horizontal axle. Water directed onto the wheel causes it to rotate, and the rotating shaft transmits power via gears, belts, or cams to machinery. Water wheels are classified by where the water strikes the wheel: overshot (water fed onto the top), breastshot (water fed at axle height), and undershot (wheel dipped into flowing stream). (008-088)

## Historical Development

### Medieval Origins

Water wheels have powered mills in Britain since Roman times, but their widespread application to industry beyond corn-grinding began in the Middle Ages. By the 13th century, water wheels drove fulling mills in the wool industry and trip hammers in iron forges.

> The upright drill worked by the water-wheel, which was used to bore out wooden water-pipes from elm trunks, represents the earliest form of machine-tool in which the work-piece was rotated against a fixed cutting tool. (007-193)

### Improvements in the 18th Century

The 18th century saw systematic study and improvement of water-wheel design. [[John Smeaton]] (1724–1792), the first civil engineer, conducted experiments on water-wheel efficiency and established that overshot wheels were about twice as efficient as undershot wheels:

> Smeaton's improvements in water-wheels and his application of cast-iron to their construction greatly increased both their power and their durability. (002-176)

### The Iron Water Wheel

The introduction of [[Cast Iron]] transformed water-wheel construction. Iron wheels could be built larger, run faster, and transmit more power than traditional wooden wheels. Cast-iron axles, gears, and bearing housings replaced wooden counterparts, reducing friction and maintenance.

> The velocity with which the water-wheel revolved was governed by the head of water and the diameter of the wheel. The introduction of iron gearing allowed the power to be transmitted to machinery at any desired speed. (008-090)

### Competition with Steam

Through the early 19th century, water power remained competitive with steam in districts with reliable water supply. Many early textile mills in [[lancashire|Lancashire]] and [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] were water-powered. The [[Tilt Hammer]] and early iron forges relied on water wheels into the 1840s:

> The tilt-hammer was usually driven by a water-wheel, or by oxen or horses. The tilt-hammer continued in use for forging heavy masses of iron long after the introduction of the steam hammer. (049-050)

By the 1850s, the [[Steam Engine]] had largely displaced water wheels for new industrial installations, though water wheels continued in use in rural districts and in older mills where the water rights were already established.

## Types of Water Wheels

| Type | Efficiency | Head Required | Typical Use |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| Overshot | 60–80% | 3–10 m | Mills, forges — highest efficiency |
| Breastshot | 40–60% | 1.5–3 m | Textile mills — medium head |
| Undershot | 20–30% | < 1.5 m | Lowland rivers — lowest efficiency |
| Pitchback | 60–75% | 3–8 m | Variant of overshot, reverses direction |

## Industrial Applications

Water wheels powered a remarkable range of industrial equipment well into the 19th century:

- **Blast furnace bellows**: Water wheels drove the great leather bellows that supplied air to early blast furnaces
- **Forge hammers**: Both tilt hammers and later [[tilt-hammer|helve hammer]]s were water-powered
- **Rolling mills**: Water wheels provided the torque for rolling [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] and plate
- **Boring and drilling**: Early cannon-boring mills were water-driven
- **Grinding**: [[cutlery|Cutlery]] grinding wheels in [[sheffield|Sheffield]] were water-powered into the 19th century
- **Textile machinery**: Water frames ([[Richard Arkwright]]) and early mules were water-driven

## Economic Significance

The availability of water-power sites fundamentally shaped the geography of early British industrialization. The fast-flowing streams of the Pennines, the Welsh valleys, and the [[highlands|Scottish Highlands]] determined where the first factories could be built — before the steam engine freed industry from its dependence on rivers.

Water power was a renewable resource with zero fuel cost, an advantage that kept it economically viable well into the steam era. A water wheel, once constructed, consumed only water — no coal, no boiler, no fireman. Its limitation was seasonality: summer drought and winter ice could stop production when steam would keep running.
