[016-001]Darby proceeded to make arrangements for carrying on the manufacture

[016-002]upon a large scale at the Baptist Mills; but the other partners

[016-003]hesitated to embark more capital in the concern, and at length refused

[016-004]their concurrence.  Determined not to be baulked in his enterprise,

[016-005]Darby abandoned the [[bristol|Bristol]] firm; and in the year 1709 he removed to

[016-006][[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in [[shropshire|Shropshire]], with the intention of prosecuting the

[016-007]enterprise on his own account.  He took the lease of a little furnace

[016-008]which had existed at the place for more than a century, as the records

[016-009]exist of a "smethe" or "smeth-house" at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in the time of

[016-010]the Tudors.  The woods of oak and hazel which at that time filled the

[016-011]beautiful dingles of the dale, and spread in almost a continuous forest

[016-012]to the base of the Wrekin, furnished abundant fuel for the smithery.

[016-013]As the trade of the Coalbrookdale firm extended, these woods became

[016-014]cleared, until the same scarcity of fuel began to be experienced that

[016-015]had already desolated the forests of [[sussex|Sussex]], and brought the

[016-016]manufacture of iron in that quarter to a stand-still.

[016-017]It appears from the '[[blast-furnace|Blast Furnace]] Memorandum Book' of Abraham Darby,

[016-018]which we have examined, that the make of iron at the Coalbrookdale

[016-019]foundry, in 1713, varied from five to ten tons a week.  The principal

[016-020]articles cast were pots, kettles, and other "hollow ware," direct from

[016-021]the smelting-furnace; the rest of the metal was run into pigs.  In

[016-022]course of time we find that other castings were turned out:  a few

[016-023]grates, smoothing-irons, door-frames, weights, baking-plates,

[016-024]cart-bushes, iron pestles and mortars, and occasionally a tailor's

[016-025]goose.  The trade gradually increased, until we find as many as 150

[016-026]pots and kettles cast in a week.

[016-027]The fuel used in the furnaces appears, from the Darby Memorandum-Book,

[016-028]to have been at first entirely charcoal; but the growing scarcity of

[016-029]wood seems to have gradually led to the use of coke, brays or small

[016-030]coke, and peat.  An abundance of coals existed in the neighbourhood: by

[016-031]rejecting those of inferior quality, and coking the others with great

[016-032]care, a combustible was obtained better fitted even than charcoal

[016-033]itself for the fusion of that particular kind of ore which is found in

[016-034]the coal-measures.  Thus we find Darby's most favourite charge for his

[016-035]furnaces to have been five baskets of coke, two of brays, and one of

[016-036]peat; next followed the ore, and then the limestone.  The use of

[016-037]charcoal was gradually given up as the art of smelting with coke and

[016-038]brays improved, most probably aided by the increased power of the

[016-039]furnace-blast, until at length we find it entirely discontinued.

[016-040]The castings of Coalbrookdale gradually acquired a reputation, and the

[016-041]trade of Abraham Darby continued to increase until the date of his

[016-042]death, which occurred at Madeley Court in 1717.  His sons were too

[016-043]young at the time to carry on the business which he had so successfully

[016-044]started, and several portions of the works were sold at a serious

[016-045]sacrifice.  But when the sons had grown up to manhood, they too entered

[016-046]upon the business of iron-founding; and Abraham Darby's son and

[016-047]grandson, both of the same name, largely extended the operations of the

[016-048]firm, until Coalbrookdale, or, as it was popularly called, "Bedlam,"

[016-049]became the principal seat of one of the most important branches of the

[016-050][[iron-industry|iron trade]].

[016-051]There seems to be some doubt as to the precise time when pit-coal was

[016-052]first regularly employed at Coalbrookdale in smelting the ore.  Mr.

[016-053]Scrivenor says, "pit-coal was first used by Mr. Abraham Darby, in his

[016-054]furnace at Coalbrookdale, in 1713;" [4] but we can find no confirmation

[016-055]of this statement in the records of the Company.  It is probable that

[016-056]Mr. Darby used raw coal, as was done in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] at the same

[016-057]time,[5] in the process of calcining the ore; but it would appear from

[016-058]his own Memoranda that coke only was used in the process of smelting.

[016-059]We infer from other circumstances that pit-coal was not employed for

[016-060]the latter purpose until a considerably later period.  The merit of its

[016-061]introduction, and its successful use in iron-smelting, is due to Mr.

[016-062]Richard Ford, who had married a daughter of Abraham Darby, and managed

[016-063]the Coalbrookdale works in 1747.  In a paper by the Rev. Mr. Mason,

[016-064]Woodwardian Professor at Cambridge, given in the 'Philosophical

[016-065]Transactions' for that year,[6] the first account of its successful

[016-066]employment is stated as follows:--"Several attempts have been made to

[016-067]run iron-ore with pit-coal:  he (Mr. Mason) thinks it has not succeeded

[016-068]anywhere, as we have had no account of its being practised; but Mr.

[016-069]Ford, of Coalbrookdale in [[shropshire|Shropshire]], from iron-ore and coal, both got

[016-070]in the same dale, makes iron brittle or tough as he pleases, there

[016-071]being cannon thus cast so soft as to bear turning like wrought-iron."

[016-072]Most probably, however, it was not until the time of [[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]],

[016-073]who succeeded Abraham Darby the second in the management of the works

[016-074]in 1757, that pit-coal came into large and regular use in the

[016-075]blasting-furnaces as well as the fineries of Coalbrookdale.

[016-076][[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]] was born at [[bristol|Bristol]] in 1735.  His parents, like the

[016-077]Darbys, belonged to the Society of Friends, and he was educated in that

[016-078]persuasion.  Being a spirited, lively youth, the "old Adam"

[016-079]occasionally cropped out in him; and he is even said, when a young man,

[016-080]to have been so much fired by the heroism of the soldier's character

[016-081]that he felt a strong desire to embrace a military career; but this

[016-082]feeling soon died out, and he dropped into the sober and steady rut of

[016-083]the Society.  After serving an apprenticeship in his native town, he

[016-084]was sent to Coalbrookdale on a mission of business, where he became

[016-085]acquainted with the Darby family, and shortly after married Hannah, the

[016-086]daughter of Abraham the second.  He then entered upon the conduct of

[016-087]the iron and coal works at [[ketley|Ketley]] and Horsehay, where he resided for

[016-088]six years, removing to Coalbrookdale in 1763, to take charge of the

[016-089]works there, on the death of his father-in-law.

[016-090]By the exertions and enterprise of the Darbys, the Coalbrookdale Works

[016-091]had become greatly enlarged, giving remunerative employment to a large

[016-092]and increasing population.  The firm had extended their operations far

[016-093]beyond the boundaries of the Dale:  they had established foundries at

[016-094][[london|London]], Bristol, and [[liverpool|Liverpool]], and agencies at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] and Truro for

[016-095]the disposal of steam-engines and other iron machinery used in the deep

[016-096]mines of those districts.  Watt had not yet perfected his [[steam-engine]];

[016-097]but there was a considerable demand for pumping-engines of Newcomen's

[016-098]construction, many of which were made at the Coalbrookdale Works.  The

[016-099]increasing demand for iron gave an impetus to coal-mining, which in its

[016-100]turn stimulated inventors in their improvement of the power of the

[016-101]steam-engine; for the coal could not be worked quickly and

[016-102]advantageously unless the pits could be kept clear of water.  Thus one

[016-103]invention stimulates another; and when the steam-engine had been

[016-104]perfected by Watt, and enabled powerful-blowing apparatus to be worked

[016-105]by its agency, we shall find that the production of iron by means of

[016-106]pit-coal being rendered cheap and expeditious, soon became enormously

[016-107]increased.

[016-108]We are informed that it was while Richard Reynolds had charge of the

[016-109]Coalbrookdale works that a further important improvement was effected

[016-110]in the manufacture of iron by pit-coal.  Up to this time the conversion

[016-111]of crude or [[pig-iron|cast iron]] into malleable or [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] had been effected

[016-112]entirely by means of charcoal.  The process was carried on in a fire

[016-113]called a finery, somewhat like that of a smith's forge; the iron being

[016-114]exposed to the blast of powerful bellows, and in constant contact with

[016-115]the fuel.  In the first process of fusing the ironstone, coal had been

[016-116]used for some time with increasing success; but the question arose,

[016-117]whether coal might not also be used with effect in the second or

[016-118]refining stage.  Two of the foremen, named Cranege, suggested to Mr.

[016-119]Reynolds that this might be performed in what is called a reverberatory

[016-120]furnace,[7] in which the iron should not mix with the coal, but be

[016-121]heated solely by the flame.  [[richard-reynolds|Mr. Reynolds]] greatly doubted the

[016-122]feasibility of the operation, but he authorized the Cranege, to make an

[016-123]experiment of their process, the result of which will be found

[016-124]described in the following extract of a letter from Mr. Reynolds to Mr.

[016-125]Thomas Goldney of Bristol, dated "Coalbrookdale, 25th April, 1766":--

[016-126].... "I come now to what I think a matter of very great consequence.

[016-127]It is some time since Thos. Cranege, who works at Bridgenorth Forge,

[016-128]and his brother George, of the Dale, spoke to me about a notion they

[016-129]had conceived of making [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] without wood charcoal.  I told them,

[016-130]consistent with the notion I had adopted in common with all others I

[016-131]had conversed with, that I thought it impossible, because the vegetable

[016-132]salts in the charcoal being an alkali acted as an absorbent to the

[016-133]sulphur of the iron, which occasions the red-short quality of the iron,

[016-134]and pit coal abounding with sulphur would increase it.  This specious

[016-135]answer, which would probably have appeared conclusive to most, and

[016-136]which indeed was what I really thought, was not so to them.  They

[016-137]replied that from the observations they had made, and repeated

[016-138]conversations together, they were both firmly of opinion that the

[016-139]alteration from the quality of pig iron into that of bar iron was

[016-140]effected merely by heat, and if I would give them leave, they would

[016-141]make a trial some day.  I consented, but, I confess, without any great

[016-142]expectation of their success; and so the matter rested some weeks, when

[016-143]it happening that some repairs had to be done at Bridgenorth, Thomas

[016-144]came up to the Dale, and, with his brother, made a trial in Thos.

[016-145]Tilly's air-furnace with such success as I thought would justify the

[016-146]erection of a small air-furnace at the Forge for the more perfectly

[016-147]ascertaining the merit of the invention.  This was accordingly done,

[016-148]and a trial of it has been made this week, and the success has

[016-149]surpassed the most sanguine expectations.  The iron put into the

[016-150]furnace was old Bushes, which thou knowest are always made of hard

[016-151]iron, and the iron drawn out is the toughest I ever saw.  A bar 1 1/4

[016-152]inch square, when broke, appears to have very little cold short in it.

[016-153]I look upon it as one of the most important discoveries ever made, and

[016-154]take the liberty of recommending thee and earnestly requesting thou

[016-155]wouldst take out a patent for it immediately....  The specification of

[016-156]the invention will be comprised in a few words, as it will only set

[016-157]forth that a reverberatory furnace being built of a proper

[016-158]construction, the pig or [[pig-iron|cast iron]] is put into it, and without the

[016-159]addition of anything else than common raw pit coal, is converted into

[016-160]good [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]], and, being taken red-hot from the reverberatory

[016-161]furnace to the [[tilt-hammer|forge hammer]], is drawn out into bars of various shapes

[016-162]and sizes, according to the will of the workmen."

[016-163]Mr. Reynolds's advice was implicitly followed.  A patent was secured in

[016-164]the name of the brothers Cranege, dated the 17th June, 1766; and the

[016-165]identical words in the above letter were adopted in the specification

[016-166]as descriptive of the process.  By this method of [[puddling-process|puddling]], as it is

[016-167]termed, the manufacturer was thenceforward enabled to produce iron in

[016-168]increased quantity at a large reduction in price; and though the

[016-169]invention of [[cranege-brothers|the Craneges]] was greatly improved upon by Onions, and

[016-170]subsequently by Cort, there can be no doubt as to the originality and

[016-171]the importance of their invention.  Mr. Tylor states that he was

[016-172]informed by the son of Richard Reynolds that the wrought iron made at

[016-173]Coalbrookdale by the Cranege process "was very good, quite tough, and

[016-174]broke with a long, bright, fibrous fracture:  that made by Cort

[016-175]afterwards was quite different." [8]  Though Mr. Reynolds's generosity

[016-176]to the Craneges is apparent; in the course which he adopted in securing

[016-177]for them a patent for the invention in their own names, it does not

[016-178]appear to have proved of much advantage to them; and they failed to

[016-179]rise above the rank which they occupied when their valuable discovery

[016-180]was patented.  This, however, was no fault of Richard Reynolds, but was

[016-181]mainly attributable to the circumstance of other inventions in a great

[016-182]measure superseding their process, and depriving them of the benefits

[016-183]of their ingenuity.
