## Early Life and Career

Little is recorded of Joshua Field's early years. He rose to prominence as a partner of [[Henry Maudslay]], the celebrated inventor of the slide-rest and founder of modern machine-tool engineering. The firm of Maudslay, Field, and Co. became one of the most respected engineering establishments in [[london|London]], known for precision workmanship and the training of a generation of mechanical engineers. (001-052)

## Partnership with Henry Maudslay

Field was closely associated with Maudslay throughout his later career. In 1824, Maudslay and Field jointly took out a patent for preventing the concentration of brine in boilers — an important innovation for marine steam engines, where salt accumulation had been a persistent problem limiting the efficiency and reliability of steam propulsion at sea. (041-125)

The partnership's works became a training ground for many distinguished engineers. After Maudslay's death in 1831, [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], the future inventor of the steam hammer, passed into the service of Joshua Field and acted as his draughtsman, "much to my advantage," as Nasmyth later recalled. (049-002)

## Contributions to Industrial Biography

Field was among the gentlemen who personally assisted [[Samuel Smiles]] in preparing the memoirs contained in *Industrial Biography: Iron Workers and Tool Makers*. The materials for the biography of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] were "partly supplied by the late Mr. Joshua Field, F.R.S. (his partner), but principally by Mr. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], C.E., his distinguished pupil." (001-052)

His contributions as a first-hand witness to Maudslay's methods and character were invaluable to the historical record of early machine-tool development. Smiles noted that Field had "died since the date at which he communicated his recollections," making his testimony all the more significant as one of the last living links to the pioneering era of precision engineering. (001-084)

## Legacy

As a Fellow of the [[royal-society|Royal Society]], Field represented the growing recognition of engineering as a scientific discipline in the early nineteenth century. His firm, Maudslay, Field, and Co., continued Maudslay's tradition of precision manufacturing and served as a bridge between the first generation of machine-tool builders and the mature engineering industry of the Victorian era. (049-002)
