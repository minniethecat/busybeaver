# Darby [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]

The Darby family enterprise at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] spanned three generations of Quaker ironmasters. It began with [[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]], who successfully smelted iron with coke in 1709 — a breakthrough that freed the iron industry from charcoal dependence.

## History

[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]], originally a brass-founder at [[bristol|Bristol]], took the lease of an old furnace at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1709. His [[bristol|Bristol]] partners had refused to invest further in the venture. (016-005)

The furnace had existed for over a century — records mention a "smethe" at Coalbrookdale as early as Tudor times. The surrounding woods of oak and hazel initially provided charcoal, but as the business grew these were rapidly cleared. (016-013)

Darby was granted a patent by Queen Anne for casting iron pots in sand without loam or clay. (015-179) His Memorandum Book records that by 1713 the Coalbrookdale furnace was producing five to ten tons of iron per week, mostly cast as pots, kettles, and hollow ware. (016-017)

The enterprise passed to [[abraham-darby-ii|Abraham Darby II]], who expanded production, and then to [[abraham-darby-iii|Abraham Darby III]], who built the world's first [[iron-bridge|cast-iron bridge]] across the [[severn|Severn]] in 1779.

## Significance

The Darby enterprise proved that coke-smelted iron could be commercially viable. By the early 18th century, charcoal shortages had reduced English furnaces dramatically — only ten remained in blast in the whole [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] by the 1720s. (015-118)

The Coalbrookdale works became the model for the coke-fired iron industry that powered the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], establishing [[shropshire|Shropshire]] as a centre of iron production that rivalled the older charcoal districts of the south.
