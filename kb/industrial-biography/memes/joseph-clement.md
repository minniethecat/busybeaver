Joseph Clement was a British mechanical engineer and draughtsman whose career spanned the formative decades of precision engineering in Britain.

Born at Great Ashby in Westmoreland in 1779, Clement rose from humble origins — his father was a hand-loom weaver — to become foreman at [[Joseph Bramah]]'s workshop and a draughtsman at [[Henry Maudslay|Maudslay]] and Field's, two of the premier engineering establishments of the era.

## Early Life and Education

Clement's father, Thomas Clement, was a hand-loom weaver by trade but a man of remarkable culture for his station in life. He was an ardent student of natural history, particularly entomology, and maintained an extensive collection of beetles and insects. "Old Clement" was also an amateur mechanic who worked a lathe of his own construction for turning bobbins and other projects. (041-141)

Young Joseph received very little formal education, learning only the rudiments of reading and writing at the village school. He worked alongside his father at the loom for some years, but as handloom weaving was gradually being displaced by improved machinery, his father resolved to apprentice him to a better trade. Joseph was trained as a slater, working at that trade for five years between the ages of eighteen and twenty-three. (041-142)

## [[apprenticeship|Apprenticeship]] and Early Mechanical Work

Like his father, Clement had a strong inclination for mechanics. The slating trade did not keep him in regular employment, especially during winter, giving him ample opportunity to pursue his mechanical interests. He befriended the village blacksmith and learned to work at the forge, handling hammer and file, and soon became adept at shoeing horses. (041-143)

A turning point came when a cousin named Farer, a clock and watchmaker, returned from [[london|London]] with books on mechanics which he lent to Joseph. These kindled an ardent desire to become a mechanic. With the aid of the village blacksmith, Clement built his own turning-lathe, superior to his father's, and used it to make flutes, clarinets, and even a pair of [[northumberland|Northumberland]] bagpipes. He also constructed a microscope and a reflecting telescope. During this early period (1804), he directed his attention to screw-making and produced a pair of satisfactory die-stocks, reportedly without having seen such a contrivance before. (041-144)

## Career as a Mechanic

Clement gave up slating and found employment at a small factory in Kirby Stephen, some thirteen miles from his home village, where he worked at making [[power-loom|power-looms]]. His earnings, recorded in his own handwriting, were 3s. 6d. a day for "fitting the first set of iron loames" and "making moddles" in 1805. (041-145)

He subsequently moved to [[carlisle|Carlisle]], working for Forster and Sons for two years at similar work. He then proceeded to [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1807, where he worked as a turner. While there, Clement took drawing lessons from Peter Nicholson, the well-known writer on carpentry. Nicholson, impressed by Clement's talent—particularly when Clement produced a flawless copy of a complex drawing—offered him gratuitous lessons, famously declaring, "Young man, YOU'LL DO!" (041-146)

After about a year in [[glasgow|Glasgow]], Clement moved to [[aberdeen|Aberdeen]] with Messrs. Leys, Masson, and Co., where his wages rose from one and a half guineas to three guineas a week. His principal work involved designing and making machinery. (041-147)

## [[london|London]] and Bramah's Workshop

Clement eventually made his way to [[london|London]], where he was first employed by [[Alexander Galloway]], a leading mechanical engineer. He subsequently joined the workshop of [[Joseph Bramah]] at Pimlico, where his skill was quickly recognised and he was advanced to the position of foreman. Bramah's workshop was a renowned training ground for first-class mechanics, and Clement was among its most distinguished alumni. (034-149)

## Draughtsman at Maudslay and Field

After his time with Bramah, Clement became a draughtsman at [[Henry Maudslay|Maudslay]] and Field's, one of the premier engineering establishments of the era. He was part of the remarkable generation of mechanics—including [[Henry Maudslay]], [[Joseph Whitworth]], [[James Nasmyth]], and [[William Fairbairn]]—who carried the mechanical arts to still higher perfection and gave an impulse to the manufacturing industry of Great Britain. (039-130)

Clement's particular expertise in screw-making and precision engineering contributed to the advancing standards of machine-tool construction in the early nineteenth century. His career exemplified the self-taught mechanical genius that characterised the [[Industrial Revolution]].
