[051-001]Notwithstanding the losses and suffering occasioned by strikes, Mr.

[051-002]Nasmyth holds the opinion that they have on the whole produced much

[051-003]more good than evil.  They have served to stimulate invention in an

[051-004]extraordinary degree.  Some of the most important labour-saving

[051-005]processes now in common use are directly traceable to them.  In the

[051-006]case of many of our most potent self-acting tools and machines,

[051-007]manufacturers could not be induced to adopt them until compelled to do

[051-008]so by strikes.  This was the ease with the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]], the

[051-009]wool-combing machine, the planing machine, the slotting machine,

[051-010]Nasmyth's steam arm, and many others.  Thus, even in the mechanical

[051-011]world, there may be "a soul of goodness in things evil."

[051-012]Mr. Nasmyth retired from business in December, 1856.  He had the moral

[051-013]courage to come out of the groove which he had so laboriously made for

[051-014]himself, and to leave a large and prosperous business, saying, "I have

[051-015]now enough of this world's goods; let younger men have their chance."

[051-016]He settled down at his rural retreat in [[kent|Kent]], but not to lead a life of

[051-017]idle ease.  Industry had become his habit, and active occupation was

[051-018]necessary to his happiness.  He fell back upon the cultivation of those

[051-019]artistic tastes which are the heritage of his family.  When a boy at

[051-020]the High School of [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], he was so skilful in making pen and ink

[051-021]illustrations on the margins of the classics, that he thus often

[051-022]purchased from his monitors exemption from the lessons of the day.  Nor

[051-023]had he ceased to cultivate the art during his residence at Patricroft,

[051-024]but was accustomed to fall back upon it for relaxation and enjoyment

[051-025]amid the pursuits of trade.  That he possesses remarkable fertility of

[051-026]imagination, and great skill in architectural and landscape drawing, as

[051-027]well as in the much more difficult art of delineating the human figure,

[051-028]will be obvious to any one who has seen his works,--more particularly

[051-029]his "City of St.  Ann's," "The Fairies," and "Everybody for ever!"

[051-030]which last was exhibited in Pall Mail, among the recent collection of

[051-031]works of Art by amateurs and others, for relief of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]]

[051-032]distress.  He has also brought his common sense to bear on such

[051-033]unlikely subject's as the origin of the cuneiform character.  The

[051-034]possession of a brick from Babylon set him a thinking.  How had it been

[051-035]manufactured? Its under side was clearly marked by the sedges of the

[051-036]Euphrates upon which it had been laid to dry and bake in the sun.  But

[051-037]how about those curious cuneiform characters? How had writing assumed

[051-038]so remarkable a form?  His surmise was this:  that the brickmakers, in

[051-039]telling their tale of bricks, used the triangular corner of another

[051-040]brick, and by pressing it down upon the soft clay, left behind it the

[051-041]triangular mark which the cuneiform character exhibits.  Such marks

[051-042]repeated, and placed in different relations to each other, would

[051-043]readily represent any number.  From the use of the corner of a brick in

[051-044]writing, the transition was easy to a pointed stick with a triangular

[051-045]end, by the use of which all the cuneiform characters can readily be

[051-046]produced upon the soft clay.  This curious question formed the subject

[051-047]of an interesting paper read by Mr. Nasmyth before the British

[051-048]Association at Cheltenham.

[051-049]But the most engrossing of Mr. Nasmyth's later pursuits has been the

[051-050]science of astronomy, in which, by bringing a fresh, original mind to

[051-051]the observation of celestial phenomena, he has succeeded in making some

[051-052]of the most remarkable discoveries of our time.  Astronomy was one of

[051-053]his favourite pursuits at Patricroft, and on his retirement became his

[051-054]serious study.  By repeated observations with a powerful reflecting

[051-055]telescope of his own construction, he succeeded in making a very

[051-056]careful and minute painting of the craters, cracks, mountains, and

[051-057]valleys in the moon's surface, for which a Council Medal was awarded

[051-058]him at the [[great-exhibition-1851|Great Exhibition]] of 1851.  But the most striking discovery

[051-059]which he has made by means of big telescope--the result of patient,

[051-060]continuous, and energetic observation--has been that of the nature of

[051-061]the sun's surface, and the character of the extraordinary light-giving

[051-062]bodies, apparently possessed of voluntary motion, moving across it,

[051-063]sometimes forming spots or hollows of more than a hundred thousand

[051-064]miles in diameter.

[051-065]The results of these observations were of so novel a character that

[051-066]astronomers for some time hesitated to receive them as facts.[6]  Yet

[051-067]so eminent an astronomer as Sir John Herschel does not hesitate now to

[051-068]describe them as "a most wonderful discovery."  "According to Mr.

[051-069]Nasmyth's observations," says he, "made with a very fine telescope of

[051-070]his own making, the bright surface of the sun consists of separate,

[051-071]insulated, individual objects or things, all nearly or exactly of one

[051-072]certain definite size and shape, which is more like that of a willow

[051-073]leaf, as he describes them, than anything else.  These leaves or scales

[051-074]are not arranged in any order (as those on a butterfly's wing are), but

[051-075]lie crossing one another in all directions, like what are called spills

[051-076]in the game of spillikins; except at the borders of a spot, where they

[051-077]point for the most part inwards towards the middle of the spot,[7]

[051-078]presenting much the sort of appearance that the small leaves of some

[051-079]water-plants or sea-weeds do at the edge of a deep hole of clear water.

[051-080]The exceedingly definite shape of these objects, their exact similarity

[051-081]one to another, and the way in which they lie across and athwart each

[051-082]other (except where they form a sort of bridge across a spot, in which

[051-083]case they seem to affect a common direction, that, namely, of the

[051-084]bridge itself),--all these characters seem quite repugnant to the

[051-085]notion of their being of a vaporous, a cloudy, or a fluid nature.

[051-086]Nothing remains but to consider them as separate and independent

[051-087]sheets, flakes, or scales, having some sort of solidity.  And these

[051-088]flakes, be they what they may, and whatever may be said about the

[051-089]dashing of meteoric stones into the sun's atmosphere, &c., are

[051-090]evidently THE IMMEDIATE SOURCES OF THE SOLAR LIGHT AND HEAT, by

[051-091]whatever mechanism or whatever processes they may be enabled to

[051-092]develope and, as it were, elaborate these elements from the bosom of

[051-093]the non-luminous fluid in which they appear to float.  Looked at in

[051-094]this point of view, we cannot refuse to regard them as organisms of

[051-095]some peculiar and amazing kind; and though it would be too daring to

[051-096]speak of such organization as partaking of the nature of life, yet we

[051-097]do know that vital action is competent to develop heat and light, as

[051-098]well as electricity.  These wonderful objects have been seen by others

[051-099]as well as Mr. Nasmyth, so that them is no room to doubt of their

[051-100]reality." [8]

[051-101]Such is the marvellous discovery made by the inventor of the

[051-102]steam-hammer, as described by the most distinguished astronomer of the

[051-103]age.  A writer in the [[edinburgh-review|[[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] Review]], referring to the subject in a

[051-104]recent number, says it shows him "to possess an intellect as profound

[051-105]as it is expert."  Doubtless his training as a mechanic, his habits of

[051-106]close observation and his ready inventiveness, which conferred so much

[051-107]power on him as an engineer, proved of equal advantage to him when

[051-108]labouring in the domain of physical science.  Bringing a fresh mind, of

[051-109]keen perception, to his new studies, and uninfluenced by preconceived

[051-110]opinions, he saw them in new and original lights; and hence the

[051-111]extraordinary discovery above described by Sir John Herschel.

[051-112]Some two hundred years since, a member of the Nasmyth family, Jean

[051-113]Nasmyth of Hamilton, was burnt for a witch--one of the last martyrs to

[051-114]ignorance and superstition in [[scotland|Scotland]]--because she read her Bible with

[051-115]two pairs of spectacles.  Had Mr. Nasmyth himself lived then, he might,

[051-116]with his two telescopes of his own making, which bring the sun and moon

[051-117]into his chamber for him to examine and paint, have been taken for a

[051-118]sorcerer.  But fortunately for him, and still more so for us, Mr.

[051-119]Nasmyth stands before the public of this age as not only one of its

[051-120]ablest mechanics, but as one of the most accomplished and original of

[051-121]scientific observers.

[051-168]CHAPTER XVI.

[051-169]WILLIAM FAIRBAIRN.

[051-170]"In science there is work for all hands, more or less skilled; and he

[051-171]is usually the most fit to occupy the higher posts who has risen from

[051-172]the ranks, and has experimentally acquainted himself with the nature of

[051-173]the work to be done in each and every, even the humblest department."

[051-174]J. D. Forbes.

[051-175]The development of the mechanical industry of [[england|England]] has been so

[051-176]rapid, especially as regards the wonders achieved by the [[machine-tool|machine-tools]]

[051-177]above referred to, that it may almost be said to have been accomplished

[051-178]within the life of the present generation.  "When I first entered this

[051-179]city," said Mr. Fairbairn, in his inaugural address as President of the

[051-180][[british-association|British Association]] at Manchester in 1861, "the whole of the machinery

[051-181]was executed by hand.  There were neither planing, slotting, nor

[051-182][[shaping-machine|shaping machine]]s; and, with the exception of very imperfect lathes and

[051-183]a few drills, the preparatory operations of construction were effected

[051-184]entirely by the hands of the workmen.  Now, everything is done by

[051-185][[machine-tool|machine-tools]] with a degree of accuracy which the unaided hand could

[051-186]never accomplish.  The automaton or self-acting machine-tool has within

[051-187]itself an almost creative power; in fact, so great are its powers of

[051-188]adaptation, that there is no operation of the human hand that it does

[051-189]not imitate."  In a letter to the author, Mr. Fairbairn says, "The

[051-190]great pioneers of machine-tool-making were Maudslay, Murray of [[leeds|Leeds]],

[051-191]Clement and Fox of [[derby|Derby]], who were ably followed by Nasmyth, Roberts,

[051-192]and Whitworth, of Manchester, and [[peter-fairbairn|Sir Peter Fairbairn]] of [[leeds|Leeds]]; and Mr.

[051-193]Fairbairn might well have added, by himself,--for he has been one of

[051-194]the most influential and successful of mechanical engineers.
