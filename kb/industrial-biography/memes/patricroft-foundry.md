# Patricroft [[foundry|Foundry]]

The Patricroft Foundry near [[manchester|Manchester]] was established by [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] in 1836 after his apprenticeship with [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]. The works became the birthplace of the steam hammer, one of the most transformative inventions in heavy engineering.

## Founding and Early Years

Nasmyth established the Patricroft works during a period of economic depression, making the early years challenging. (050-004) The firm needed every shilling of capital it could command, and Nasmyth's partner Gaskell objected to locking up capital in what he regarded as engineering novelties. (050-004)

Despite these constraints, Nasmyth built up the works at Patricroft into a thriving concern. The foundry became known for its innovative approach to manufacturing, producing machine tools, locomotives, and eventually the steam hammer that would make the works famous worldwide.

## The [[steam-hammer|Steam Hammer]]

The steam hammer, Nasmyth's most famous invention, was first built at Patricroft. Upon returning from a visit to France where he saw the need for such a tool, Nasmyth urged his partner to take out a patent. (050-004)

Seeing himself on the brink of losing his property in the invention, Nasmyth applied to his brother-in-law William Bennett, who advanced him about £280, and the patent was secured in June 1840. (050-007)

The first hammer, of 30 cwt., was made for the Patricroft works with the consent of the partners, and within a few weeks it was in full work. The precision and beauty of its action, the perfect ease with which it was managed, and the untiring force of its percussive blows were the admiration of all who saw it. (050-011)

## Global Impact

The advantages of the steam hammer were so obvious that its adoption soon became general. In the course of a few years, Nasmyth steam-hammers were to be found in every well-appointed workshop both at home and abroad. (050-034)

Many modifications were made by Condie, Morrison, Naylor, Rigby, and others; but Nasmyth's was the father of them all, and still held its ground. (050-034)

Nasmyth later recalled putting his anxiety at rest one evening — he went out to Patricroft and took a look at the works, finding comfort in the sight of what he had built. (049-015)

A writer in *The Engineer* observed that "without Nasmyth's steam-hammer, we must have stopped short in many of those gigantic engineering works which would be the perpetual wonder of this age." (050-048)

## The Steam Pile-Driver

Nasmyth subsequently applied the principle of the steam-hammer in the pile-driver, which he invented in 1845. Until its production, pile-driving had been conducted on what Nasmyth called the artillery or cannon-ball principle — the action being excessive and the mass deficient. (050-084)

In his new machine, he applied the elastic force of steam to deliver heavy but controlled blows. The driver was first used in forming the great steam dock at [[devon|Devon]]port, where the results were striking. It was shortly after employed by [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] in piling the foundations of the great High Level Bridge at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] and the Border Bridge at Berwick. (050-099)

## Visitors and Recognition

The Patricroft works attracted distinguished visitors from across the world. A party from the Creusot Iron Works in France called at the Patricroft works together to see Nasmyth's methods. (049-155)

On another occasion, visitors came from [[liverpool|Liverpool]] by special train to Patricroft, where the Scotchmen assembled in the yard gave "three cheers" for the works. (050-175) (050-180)

Even after Nasmyth retired, the pursuits he had cultivated during his residence at Patricroft remained central to his life and became his principal occupation in retirement. (051-023) (051-053)
