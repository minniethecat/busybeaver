# Menai Suspension Bridge 1826

In 1826, [[thomas-telford|Thomas Telford]] completed the Menai Suspension Bridge, spanning 579 feet with wrought-iron chains across the Menai Strait between [[wales|Wales]] and [[anglesey|Anglesey]]. It was the largest suspension bridge in the world at the time and demonstrated the structural potential of wrought iron on an unprecedented scale.

## Engineering Achievement

The bridge's main span of 579 feet was the longest in the world when completed, surpassing all previous suspension structures. The sixteen wrought-iron chains, each made of eye-bar links, supported a roadway 100 feet above the water, allowing sailing ships to pass beneath.

The Menai crossing was part of Telford's larger programme to improve the [[london|London]]–[[holyhead|Holyhead]] road, the principal route for mail and passengers to [[ireland|Ireland]].

## Legacy

The Menai Bridge's success encouraged further [[iron-bridge|iron bridge construction]] across Britain. Just two decades later, the same strait was crossed by the [[britannia-bridge-1850|Britannia Tubular Bridge]] (1850), a wrought-iron box-girder design by [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]] with assistance from [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]. (056-018)

Together, the Menai Suspension Bridge and the Britannia [[tubular-bridge|Tubular Bridge]] made the Menai Strait a showcase for the two great iron bridge technologies of the 19th century: the suspension chain and the wrought-iron tube.
