William Fairbairn (1787–1874) was a Scottish [[engineer]], [[millwright]], and iron [[shipbuilder]] who became one of the most influential figures in nineteenth-century British engineering. Starting with almost no capital, he built a firm in [[Manchester]] that pioneered the use of [[iron]] in ships, bridges, and mill construction. His investigations into the strength of materials—particularly [[wrought-iron]]—established fundamental principles of [[structural-engineering]] that guided the design of bridges, buildings, and boilers throughout the Victorian era.

## Early Life

William Fairbairn was born at Kelso, Roxburghshire, on the 19th of February, 1787. His father, Andrew Fairbairn, was a farm-labourer and the son of a gardener; through his mother's line, the family claimed descent from the ancient Border house of Douglas. (052-001) (052-003) A curious connection linked the Fairbairns to the literary world: Andrew Fairbairn, as a strong youth of fifteen or sixteen, used to carry the young Walter Scott—then recovering from a diseased hip—about the village of Smailholm in his arms. (052-013)

The family removed to Moy in Ross-shire in 1799, where Andrew Fairbairn took a farm of about three hundred acres on the banks of the river Conan. (052-049) The farm proved a harsh school. The boys could not be spared for schooling; they worked in the fields or helped nurse the younger children at home. (052-076) Yet William's mechanical instincts surfaced early. At Moy, tasked with carrying his infant brother Peter, he built a small four-wheeled waggon to wheel him about, using only a knife, a gimlet, and an old saw—boring the axle-holes with a red-hot poker. (052-091) His mother, a woman of "patient industry, thrift, discreetness, and piety," taught her children lessons of character that "probably far more than compensated for the boys' loss of school-culture." (052-079)

## Career

### [[apprenticeship|Apprenticeship]] and Wanderjahre

At the age of fourteen Fairbairn was apprenticed to a millwright at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|[[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] upon Tyne]]. He afterwards travelled south, working at various places and gaining the broad practical experience that German tradesmen call their *Wandertschaft*. By the time he reached [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1814, aged twenty-four, he had acquired a thorough command of his trade without possessing any capital beyond his own skill and energy. (054-007)

### Manchester and Partnership with [[james-lillie|James Lillie]]

In [[manchester|Manchester]], Fairbairn worked for two years as a journeyman millwright, saved sufficient money to furnish a two-roomed cottage, and married at the end of 1816. (054-028) In 1817 he entered into partnership with [[james-lillie|James Lillie]], a fellow-workman, and the two rented a small shed at twelve shillings a week, setting up a lathe of their own making. (054-055)

Their first major contract came from Messrs. Adam and George Murray, large cotton-spinners, who entrusted the young firm—despite their having only a single lathe—with renewing the entire horizontal cross-shafting of a large mule-spinning mill. Fairbairn and Lillie worked from five in the morning until nine at night "for a considerable period" and completed the work to the Murrays' entire satisfaction. (054-086) The Murrays recommended them to [[John Kennedy]], of the firm MacConnel and Kennedy, then the largest spinners in the kingdom, and orders multiplied rapidly. (054-096)

### Iron Shipbuilding and Structural Engineering

The partnership with Lillie dissolved amicably in 1832, and Fairbairn carried on the business alone, turning increasingly to the design and construction of [[iron-shipbuilding|iron steamships]]. His millwright's knowledge of the strength of materials, combined with methodical experimentation, made him one of the foremost authorities on [[wrought-iron]] construction in Britain. He conducted extensive tests on the strength of iron plates, riveted joints, and tubular girders, and the results of his investigations were published in papers to the [[Royal Society]], of which he was elected a Fellow. (024-012) (029-056)

> "In those days a good millwright was a man of large resources; he was generally well educated, and could draw out his own designs and work at the lathe; he had a knowledge of mill machinery, pumps, and cranes, and could turn his hand to the bench or the forge with equal adroitness and facility." — William Fairbairn, on the millwright's craft. (054-011)

Fairbairn's most enduring contribution lay in his systematic investigation of the strength of materials. His experiments on the transverse strength of hot and cold blast iron, the resistance of tubes to collapse, and the behaviour of riveted plates under load provided the empirical foundation upon which Victorian structural engineering was built. (045-049)

## Influence and Legacy

Fairbairn's influence extended well beyond his own firm. His brother, [[peter-fairbairn|Sir Peter Fairbairn]], became a prominent machine-tool builder and mayor of [[leeds|Leeds]]. (052-104) The [[manchester|Manchester]] engineering tradition that Fairbairn helped to found—alongside figures such as [[Joseph Whitworth]], [[James Nasmyth]], and [[Richard Roberts]]—made the city one of the world's great centres of mechanical innovation in the middle decades of the nineteenth century. (045-050)

> "In his inaugural address as president of the [[british-association|British Association]] at Manchester, Mr. Fairbairn observed that the city's pre-eminence in the mechanical arts rested upon the skill, energy, and practical knowledge of the men who had grown up with its industries." (051-179)

Fairbairn was elected a Fellow of the [[Royal Society]] and created a baronet in 1869 in recognition of his services to engineering and science. He died on 18 August 1874, leaving behind a body of published work—including treatises on iron shipbuilding, millwork, and the strength of iron—that remained standard references for a generation of engineers.
