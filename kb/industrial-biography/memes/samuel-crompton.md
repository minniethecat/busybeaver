## Early Life

Samuel Crompton was born in 1753 at Firwood Fold, near [[bolton|Bolton]], [[lancashire|Lancashire]]. He grew up in the heart of the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] cotton district during the first stirrings of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], when hand-spinning was still the dominant method of producing cotton yarn. (055-019)

## The Spinning Mule

Crompton's great invention, the Spinning Mule (completed in 1779), combined the principles of [[Richard Arkwright]]'s water frame (which produced strong but coarse thread) with [[James Hargreaves]]'s spinning jenny (which produced fine but weak thread). The mule produced yarn that was both fine and strong, enabling the manufacture of muslins and other high-quality cotton fabrics that had previously only been imported from India. (055-019)

> The invention of the Mule by Samuel Crompton was for a long time the only record which the public possessed of the merits and claims of that distinguished inventor. — John Kennedy, *Proceedings of the Literary and Philosophical Society of [[manchester|Manchester]]* (055-019)

## Later Life and Recognition

Unlike many inventors of his era, Crompton received little financial reward from his invention during his lifetime. He had been unable to afford a patent and was largely dependent on the goodwill of manufacturers, some of whom made substantial fortunes from his machine while he struggled in relative poverty. (055-019)

[[john-kennedy|John Kennedy]], the [[manchester|Manchester]] cotton-spinner and engineer, prepared a paper on Crompton for the Literary and Philosophical Society of [[manchester|Manchester]], which was for many years the only public record of his contributions. Kennedy's account helped secure Crompton's reputation, and in 1812 [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] awarded him a grant of £5,000 in belated recognition of his invention's importance to British industry. (055-019)
