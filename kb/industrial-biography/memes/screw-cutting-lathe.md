# Screw-Cutting [[lathe|Lathe]]

[[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]'s precision [[screw-cutting|screw-cutting lathe]] — the machine that made interchangeable threaded fasteners possible, produced the leadscrews that gave all subsequent machine tools their accuracy, and established the principle of mechanically constrained tool movement that defined precision engineering.

## Background

Before Maudslay, screw threads were cut by hand. A skilled workman filed or chiselled the helical groove by eye, and each nut had to be individually fitted to its bolt. There were no standard pitches, no standard thread forms, and no interchangeability. For precision instruments, astronomical devices, and scientific apparatus, the inability to produce accurate screws was a fundamental limitation.

The critical need came from the nascent machine tool industry itself: to build self-acting lathes, planers, and other automatic machines required leadscrews of precise and consistent pitch. The screw was the metrological foundation of mechanical engineering — and it was still being made by hand.

## Inventor

- **Inventor**: [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] (1771–1831)
- **Developed**: c. 1800 at Maudslay's [[lambeth|Lambeth]] works, [[london|London]]
- **Basis**: Combined the slide rest with a geared leadscrew
- **Trained**: [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] (1789–1797), then independent

## How It Works

Maudslay's lathe synchronises tool movement with workpiece rotation through a positive mechanical drive:

1. The workpiece rotates in the lathe spindle
2. A **master leadscrew** — a precision screw of known pitch, mounted along the lathe bed — rotates at a fixed ratio to the spindle through change gears
3. A **split nut** engages the leadscrew, driving the tool carriage along the bed at an exact linear speed proportional to spindle rotation
4. The cutting tool, held rigidly in the **slide rest**, traces a mathematically perfect helix on the workpiece

To cut a different thread pitch, the operator swaps the change gears to alter the ratio between spindle and leadscrew speeds. This gear train is the "program" of the lathe: the gear ratio directly determines the thread pitch.

Maudslay's lathe cut threads with "accuracy and precision which the hand of man could never equal." The same leadscrew principle was later applied to planing machines, milling machines, and every other machine tool requiring coordinated movement. (037-158)

## Significance

The [[screw-cutting|screw-cutting lathe]] was self-amplifying: it could cut a leadscrew more accurate than itself, which could then be installed in a better lathe, which could cut an even more accurate leadscrew. This virtuous cycle of improving precision drove the entire machine tool industry forward for a century. Its impacts included:

- **Interchangeable fasteners**: Bolts and nuts became commodity items
- **Precision leadscrews** for all subsequent machine tools
- **Measuring instruments**: Micrometers and other precision instruments
- **Standardisation**: Made Whitworth's standard thread system possible

## Later Improvements

- **[[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]]**: Standardised thread forms (BSW, 1841) and improved lathe accuracy with true surface plates
- Modern CNC lathes still use the leadscrew principle, now driven by servo motors and ball screws rather than change gears
