[019-004]CHAPTER VI.

[019-005]INVENTION OF CAST STEEL--BENJAMIN HUNTSMAN.

[019-006]"It may be averred that as certainly as the age of iron superseded that

[019-007]of bronze, so will the age of steel reign triumphant over iron."--HENRY

[019-008]BESSEMER.

[019-009]"Aujourd'hui la revolution que devait amener en Grande-Bretagne la

[019-010]memorable decouverte de [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]] est tout a fait accomplie, et

[019-011]chaque jour les consequetces sen feront plus vivement sentir sur le

[019-012]confinent."--LE PLAY, Sur la Fabrication de l' Acier en [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]].

[019-013]Iron, besides being used in various forms as bar and [[pig-iron|cast iron]], is also

[019-014]used in various forms as bar and cast steel; and it is principally

[019-015]because of its many admirable qualities in these latter forms that iron

[019-016]maintains its supremacy over all the other metals.

[019-017]The process of converting iron into steel had long been known among the

[019-018]Eastern nations before it was introduced into Europe.  The Hindoos were

[019-019]especially skilled in the art of making steel, as indeed they are to

[019-020]this day; and it is supposed that the tools with which the Egyptians

[019-021]covered their obelisks and temples of porphyry and syenite with

[019-022]hieroglyphics were made of Indian steel, as probably no other metal was

[019-023]capable of executing such work.  The art seems to have been well known

[019-024]in Germany in the Middle Ages, and the process is on the whole very

[019-025]faithfully described by Agricola in his great work on Metallurgy.[1]

[019-026][[england|England]] then produced very little steel, and was mainly dependent for

[019-027]its supply of the article upon the continental makers.

[019-028]From an early period [[sheffield|Sheffield]] became distinguished for its manufacture

[019-029]of iron and steel into various useful articles.  We find it mentioned

[019-030]in the thirteenth century as a place where the best arrowheads were

[019-031]made,--the Earl of Richmond owing his success at the battle of Bosworth

[019-032]partly to their superior length, sharpness, and finish.  The

[019-033]manufactures of the town became of a more pacific character in the

[019-034]following centuries, during which knives, tools, and implements of

[019-035]husbandry became the leading articles.

[019-036]Chaucer's reference to the '[[sheffield|Sheffield]] thwytel' (or case-knife) in his

[019-037]Canterbury Tales, written about the end of the fourteenth century,

[019-038]shows that the place had then become known for its manufacture of

[019-039]knives.  In 1575 we find the Earl of Shrewsbury presenting to his

[019-040]friend Lord Burleigh "a case of Hallamshire whittells, being such

[019-041]fruites as his pore cuntrey affordeth with fame throughout the realme."

[019-042]Fuller afterwards speaks of the Sheffield knives as "for common use of

[019-043]the country people," and he cites an instance of a knave who cozened

[019-044]him out of fourpence for one when it was only worth a penny.

[019-045]In 1600 Sheffield became celebrated for its tobacco-boxes and

[019-046]Jew's-harps.  The town was as yet of small size and population; for

[019-047]when a survey of it was made in 1615 it was found to contain not more

[019-048]than 2207 householders, of whom one-third, or 725, were "not able to

[019-049]live without the charity of their neighbours:  these are all Begging

[019-050]poor." [2]  It must, however, have continued its manufacture of knives;

[019-051]for we find that the knife with which Felton stabbed the Duke of

[019-052]Buckingham at [[portsmouth|Portsmouth]] in 1628 was traced to Sheffield.  The knife

[019-053]was left sticking in the duke's body, and when examined was found to

[019-054]bear the Sheffield corporation mark.  It was ultimately ascertained to

[019-055]have been made by one Wild, a cutler, who had sold the knife for

[019-056]tenpence to Felton when recruiting in the town.  At a still later

[019-057]period, the manufacture of clasp or spring knives was introduced into

[019-058]Sheffield by Flemish workmen.  Harrison says this trade was begun in

[019-059]1650.  The clasp-knife was commonly known in the North as a jocteleg.

[019-060]Hence Burns, describing the famous article treasured by Captain Grose

[019-061]the antiquarian, says that--

[019-062]"It was a faulding jocteleq,

[019-063]Or lang-kail gully;"

[019-064]the word being merely a corruption of Jacques de Liege, a famous

[019-065]foreign cutler, whose knives were as well known throughout Europe as

[019-066]those of Rogers or Mappin are now.  Scythes and sickles formed other

[019-067]branches of manufacture introduced by the Flemish artisans, the makers

[019-068]of the former principally living in the parish of Norton, those of the

[019-069]latter in Eckington.

[019-070]Many improvements were introduced from time to time in the material of

[019-071]which these articles were made.  Instead of importing the German steel,

[019-072]as it was called, the Sheffield manufacturers began to make it

[019-073]themselves, principally from Dannemora iron imported from Sweden.  The

[019-074]first English manufacturer of the article was one Crowley, a [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]]

[019-075]man; and the Sheffield makers shortly followed his example.  We may

[019-076]here briefly state that the ordinary method of preparing this valuable

[019-077]material of manufactures is by exposing iron bars, placed in contact

[019-078]with roughly-granulated charcoal, to an intense heat,--the process

[019-079]lasting for about a week, more or less, according to the degree of

[019-080]carbonization required.  By this means, what is called BLISTERED STEEL

[019-081]is produced, and it furnishes the material out of which razors, files,

[019-082]knives, swords, and various articles of hardware are manufactured.  A

[019-083]further process is the manufacture of the metal thus treated into SHEAR

[019-084]STEEL, by exposing a fasciculus of the blistered steel rods, with sand

[019-085]scattered over them for the purposes of a flux, to the heat of a

[019-086]wind-furnace until the whole mass becomes of a welding heat, when it is

[019-087]taken from the fire and drawn out under a forge-hammer,--the process of

[019-088]welding being repeated, after which the steel is reduced to the

[019-089]required sizes.  The article called FAGGOT steel is made after a

[019-090]somewhat similar process.

[019-091]But the most valuable form in which steel is now used in the

[019-092]manufactures of Sheffield is that of cast-steel, in which iron is

[019-093]presented in perhaps its very highest state of perfection.  Cast-steel

[019-094]consists of iron united to carbon in an elastic state together with a

[019-095]small portion of oxygen; whereas crude or pig iron consists of iron

[019-096]combined with carbon in a material state.[3]  Chief merits of

[019-097]cast-steel consist in its possessing great cohesion and closeness of

[019-098]grain, with an astonishing degree of tenacity and

[019-099]flexibility,--qualities which render it of the highest value in all

[019-100]kinds of tools and instruments where durability, polish, and fineness

[019-101]of edge are essential requisites.  It is to this material that we are

[019-102]mainly indebted for the exquisite cutting instrument of the surgeon,

[019-103]the chisel of the sculptor, the steel plate on which the engraver

[019-104]practises his art, the cutting tools employed in the various processes

[019-105]of skilled handicraft, down to the common saw or the axe used by the

[019-106]backwoodsman in levelling the primeval forest.

[019-107]The invention of cast-steel is due to [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]], of

[019-108][[attercliffe|Attercliffe]], near Sheffield.  M. Le Play, Professor of Metallurgy in

[019-109]the Royal School of Mines of France, after making careful inquiry and

[019-110]weighing all the evidence on the subject, arrived at the conclusion

[019-111]that the invention fairly belongs to Huntsman.  The French professor

[019-112]speaks of it as a "memorable discovery," made and applied with

[019-113]admirable perseverance; and he claims for its inventor the

[019-114]distinguished merit of advancing the steel manufactures of [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] to

[019-115]the first rank, and powerfully contributing to the establishment on a

[019-116]firm foundation of the industrial and commercial supremacy of Great

[019-117]Britain.  It is remarkable that a French writer should have been among

[019-118]the first to direct public attention to the merits of this inventor,

[019-119]and to have first published the few facts known as to his history in a

[019-120]French Government Report,--showing the neglect which men of this class

[019-121]have heretofore received at home, and the much greater esteem in which

[019-122]they are held by scientific foreigners.[4]  Le Play, in his

[019-123]enthusiastic admiration of the discoverer of so potent a metal as

[019-124]cast-steel, paid a visit to Huntsman's grave in Atterclifle Churchyard,

[019-125]near Sheffield, and from the inscription on his tombstone recites the

[019-126]facts of his birth, his death, and his brief history.  With the

[019-127]assistance of his descendants, we are now enabled to add the following

[019-128]record of the life and labours of this remarkable but almost forgotten

[019-129]man.

[019-130]Benjamin Huntsman was born in Lincolnshire in the year 1704.  His

[019-131]parents were of German extraction, and had settled in this country only

[019-132]a few years previous to his birth.  The boy being of an ingenious turn,

[019-133]was bred to a mechanical calling; and becoming celebrated for his

[019-134]expertness in repairing clocks, he eventually set up in business as a

[019-135]clock maker and mender in the town of Doncaster.  He also undertook

[019-136]various other kinds of metal work, such as the making and repairing of

[019-137]locks, smoke-jacks, roasting-jacks, and other articles requiring

[019-138]mechanical skill.  He was remarkably shrewd, observant, thoughtful, and

[019-139]practical; so much so that he came to be regarded as the "wise man" of

[019-140]his neighbourhood, and was not only consulted as to the repairs of

[019-141]machinery, but also of the human frame.  He practised surgery with

[019-142]dexterity, though after an empirical fashion, and was held in especial

[019-143]esteem as an oculist.  His success was such that his advice was sought

[019-144]in many surgical diseases, and he was always ready to give it, but

[019-145]declined receiving any payment in return.

[019-146]In the exercise of his mechanical calling, he introduced several

[019-147]improved tools, but was much hindered by the inferior quality of the

[019-148]metal supplied to him, which was common German steel.  He also

[019-149]experienced considerable difficulty in finding a material suitable for

[019-150]the springs and pendulums of his clocks.  These circumstances induced

[019-151]him to turn his attention to the making of a better kind of steel than

[019-152]was then procurable, for the purposes of his trade.  His first

[019-153]experiments were conducted at Doncaster;[5] but as fuel was difficult

[019-154]to be had at that place, he determined, for greater convenience, to

[019-155]remove to the neighbourhood of Sheffield, which he did in 1740.  He

[019-156]first settled at Handsworth, a few miles to the south of that town, and

[019-157]there pursued his investigations in secret.  Unfortunately, no records

[019-158]have been preserved of the methods which he adopted in overcoming the

[019-159]difficulties he had necessarily to encounter.  That they must have been

[019-160]great is certain, for the process of manufacturing cast-steel of a

[019-161]first-rate quality even at this day is of a most elaborate and delicate

[019-162]character, requiring to be carefully watched in its various stages.  He

[019-163]had not only to discover the fuel and flux suitable for his purpose,

[019-164]but to build such a furnace and make such a crucible as should sustain

[019-165]a heat more intense than any then known in metallurgy.  Ingot-moulds

[019-166]had not yet been cast, nor were there hoops and wedges made that would

[019-167]hold them together, nor, in short, were any of those materials at his

[019-168]disposal which are now so familiar at every melting-furnace.

[019-169]Huntsman's experiments extended over many years before the desired

[019-170]result was achieved.  Long after his death, the memorials of the

[019-171]numerous failures through which he toilsomely worked his way to

[019-172]success, were brought to light in the shape of many hundredweights of

[019-173]steel, found buried in the earth in different places about his

[019-174]manufactory.  From the number of these wrecks of early experiments, it

[019-175]is clear that he had worked continuously upon his grand idea of

[019-176]purifying the raw steel then in use, by melting it with fluxes at an

[019-177]intense heat in closed earthen crucibles.  The buried masses were found

[019-178]in various stages of failure, arising from imperfect melting, breaking

[019-179]of crucibles, and bad fluxes; and had been hid away as so much spoiled

[019-180]steel of which nothing could be made.  At last his perseverance was

[019-181]rewarded, and his invention perfected; and though a hundred years have

[019-182]passed since Huntsman's discovery, the description of fuel (coke) which

[019-183]he first applied for the purpose of melting the steel, and the

[019-184]crucibles and furnaces which he used, are for the most part similar to

[019-185]those in use at the present day.  Although the making of cast-steel is

[019-186]conducted with greater economy and dexterity, owing to increased

[019-187]experience, it is questionable whether any maker has since been able to

[019-188]surpass the quality of Huntsman's manufacture.
