# Scotland

Scotland was transformed by the iron industry in the 18th and 19th centuries. Before the establishment of the [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]], Scotland was entirely dependent upon other countries for its supply of iron. (025-143) The rise of the Scotch [[iron-industry|iron trade]], driven by Carron, the discovery of the Black Band ironstone by [[david-mushet|David Mushet]], and the invention of the hot blast by [[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]], turned Scotland into one of the world's great iron-producing regions. (025-154) (025-155) (025-157)

## Industrial History

### The [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]]

The [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]], established near [[falkirk|Falkirk]] in Stirlingshire, marked the birth of large-scale iron-making in Scotland. The first furnace was blown at Carron on the first day of January, 1760, and in the course of the same year the works turned out 1,500 tons of iron — then the whole annual produce of Scotland. (025-009) (025-010) (025-011) The works were founded on an abundant supply of water and local raw materials, and their main product was castings, for which the peculiar quality of Scotch iron admirably adapted it. (024-194) (025-025) (025-026)

> "The well-known Carronades, or short ship's guns, first invented by General Robert Melville, were cast at the Carron Works." (025-026)

From the Carron enterprise, in a great measure, sprang the [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]] Canal]], the first artificial navigation in Scotland. (025-032) (025-033) The Carron foundry provided training and experience for a generation of Scottish ironmasters and engineers, and until at length the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of Scotland assumed such a magnitude that its manufacturers were among the largest in the world. (025-148) (025-149)

### The Black Band and [[hot-blast|Hot Blast]]

The extraordinary expansion of the Scotch [[iron-industry|iron trade]] of late years was mainly due to the discovery by [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] of the Black Band ironstone, and the invention of the hot blast by [[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]] in 1828. (025-154) (025-155) (025-157) [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] was born at Dalkeith, near [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]], and his discovery of the Black Band — a carboniferous ironstone that could be smelted with far less fuel — revolutionised Scottish iron-making. (025-157)

[[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]] was born at Shettleston, a roadside village near [[glasgow|Glasgow]]. (027-070) His invention of the hot blast — pre-heating the air blown into the furnace — dramatically reduced fuel consumption and made it possible to smelt the Black Band ironstone profitably. The coals of Scotland were extensively developed in the midland Scotch counties, where coal and iron measures stretched across the country from the Firth of Forth to the Irish Channel at the Firth of [[clyde|Clyde]]. (029-003) (029-015) (029-017)

### Growth and Scale

The impact of Mushet's discovery and Neilson's invention was dramatic. In the year after Neilson's patent was taken out, the total make of Scotland was still modest. But in Ayrshire and Lanarkshire new works were erected, until by 1845 the production of Scotch pig-iron had increased to 475,000 tons. (029-021) (029-023) (029-024) At the peak, there were 125 furnaces in blast throughout Scotland, and the wealth and resources of the Scotch iron districts advanced with extraordinary rapidity. (029-028) (029-030) By the later 19th century, Scotland every furnace — with the exception of one at Carron — used the hot blast, and Scotch produce was above £650,000 sterling per annum. (029-076) (029-079)
