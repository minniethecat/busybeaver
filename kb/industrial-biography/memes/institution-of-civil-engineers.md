# Institution of Civil Engineers

The Institution of Civil Engineers (ICE) is a British professional engineering institution founded in 1818. It is the world's first professional body for civil engineers and received its Royal Charter in 1828. The ICE established professional standards and recognition for civil engineering as a distinct discipline.

## Founding

The ICE was founded by a small group of young engineers meeting in [[london|London]] coffee houses, including Henry Robinson Palmer and James Jones. The institution was formally established on 2 January 1818 at the Kendal Coffee House in Fleet Street. [[thomas-telford|Thomas Telford]], the pre-eminent civil engineer of the era, became the ICE's first president in 1820, lending his immense prestige to the fledgling organisation.

## Role and Recognition

### Professional Recognition

The ICE grew to become the leading body for the civil engineering profession in Britain and across the British Empire. Election to membership or fellowship became a mark of professional standing. [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]] notes that engineers of distinction received recognition "by many eminent bodies — by the Institution of Civil Engineers, the Chemical Society, and others." (028-173)

### Knowledge Sharing

The ICE published proceedings and papers that disseminated engineering knowledge, technical standards, and case studies. Its library and meetings provided a forum where engineers could share innovations and lessons learned from major construction projects including railways, docks, bridges, and waterworks.

## Legacy

The ICE continues as a major professional engineering institution, with headquarters at One Great George Street in Westminster. Its model of professional organisation for engineers was widely imitated internationally, contributing to the standardisation of engineering practice and education worldwide.
