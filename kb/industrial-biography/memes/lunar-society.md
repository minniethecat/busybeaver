The **Lunar Society** of [[birmingham|Birmingham]] was an informal dining club and learned society that met monthly on the Monday nearest the full moon (hence the "lunar" name). It brought together leading figures of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] and the Midlands Enlightenment, including inventors, scientists, manufacturers, and intellectuals.

## Members and Influence

The Society's core membership included some of the most influential figures of the Industrial Revolution. [[[james-watt|James Watt]]](/james-watt) attended meetings during his partnership with [[[matthew-boulton|Matthew Boulton]]](/matthew-boulton) in [[birmingham|Birmingham]]. [Joseph Priestley](/joseph-priestley), the chemist and discoverer of oxygen, was a prominent member. The physician and poet Erasmus Darwin (grandfather of Charles Darwin) and the industrialist Josiah Wedgwood also participated.

The Society's experiments were not without controversy — the painter Sir William Beechey begged the Lunar Society to desist from certain chemical experiments, warning that if the process succeeded it would ruin his livelihood as a portraitist dependent on paint pigments (032-102).

## Legacy

Though informal, the Lunar Society exemplified the cross-pollination of science and industry that drove the Industrial Revolution. Its members' collaborations led to advances in steam power, chemistry, manufacturing, and canal engineering. The Society dissolved around 1813, but its model of informal scientific exchange influenced later bodies like the [[British Association for the Advancement of Science]].
