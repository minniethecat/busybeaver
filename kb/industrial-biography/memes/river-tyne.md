# River Tyne

The River Tyne was the industrial artery of North East [[england|England]], supporting a concentration of coal-mining, iron-works, and engineering enterprises centred on [[newcastle-upon-tyne|[[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] upon Tyne]]. The river's navigable channel made it one of Britain's busiest coal-export routes during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. (023-176)

## Industrial History

### Newcastle's Iron and Engineering Works

[[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle upon Tyne]], situated on the Tyne, became a major centre of iron production and heavy engineering. Iron-works were founded at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] in the late eighteenth century, drawing on the rich coal seams of the Durham and [[northumberland|Northumberland]] coalfields that were accessible by river transport. (023-176)

The engineer [[william-armstrong|William Armstrong]] established his engineering works on the banks of the Tyne at Elswick, which grew into one of the world's largest manufacturers of hydraulic machinery, armaments, and ships. Armstrong's works made the Tyne synonymous with heavy engineering in the Victorian era. (052-147)

### Engineering Institutions

The industrial importance of the Tyne was reflected in its professional culture. The [[institution-of-mechanical-engineers|Institution of Mechanical Engineers]] held meetings at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle upon Tyne]], where leading figures of the profession gathered to discuss advances in [[steam-engine|steam power]], iron shipbuilding, and machine tools. (056-182) The [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] engineers formed part of a network that connected the Tyne to engineering centres in [[manchester|Manchester]], [[leeds|Leeds]], and [[birmingham|Birmingham]].

### Coal and Shipping

The Tyne's coal trade was the foundation of its industrial wealth. Collieries along the river shipped coal to [[london|London]] and the south, and the demand for mining machinery stimulated local engineering. The river also became a centre of iron shipbuilding in the later nineteenth century, drawing on the region's iron production and skilled workforce.
