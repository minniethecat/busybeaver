Worcestershire, in the West Midlands of [[england|England]], was one of the earliest centres of iron-making in Britain.

The county's iron industry flourished from Roman times, drawing on local iron-ore deposits and abundant woodland for charcoal fuel. During the seventeenth century, Worcestershire became closely associated with the [[dudley|Dudley]] family of ironmasters, particularly [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] (1600–1684), who pioneered the use of pit-coal in iron-smelting.

## Geography and Resources

Worcestershire's iron industry benefited from its geography. The [[severn|River Severn]] provided transport links to [[bristol|Bristol]] and the sea, while the coalfields of the Black Country lay immediately to the north. The county sat at the intersection of the major iron-producing regions of the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], South [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], and [[shropshire|Shropshire]]. (012-037)

## Iron-making History

The [[iron-industry|iron trade]] in Worcestershire was well established by the early modern period. The county's ironworks drew on ore from the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] to the south-west and local coal measures. By the early seventeenth century, furnaces and forges operated along the [[severn|Severn]] valley, supplying iron to [[birmingham|Birmingham]], [[bristol|Bristol]], and the wider Midlands metal trades. (010-025)

## The [[dudley|Dudley]] Family and the Civil War

Worcestershire is inseparable from the history of the [[dudley|Dudley]] family. [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]], the fourth of eleven children of Edward Sutton, Lord [[dudley|Dudley]], was born at [[dudley|Dudley]] Castle. He claimed to have successfully smelted iron with pit-coal as early as 1619, decades before Abraham Darby's more famous achievement at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]. (009-175)

During the English Civil War (1642–1651), Worcestershire became contested ground. [[dudley|Dudley]] Castle was fortified for the Royalist cause, and [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] himself served as a Royalist engineer, fortifying Worcester and Stafford. (011-003) After the [[house-of-commons|Parliament]]arian victory, [[dudley|Dudley]]'s ironworks were swept away by a flood — deliberately, he claimed, by political enemies — and his house in Worcester was seized. (011-064) (011-072)

The final defeat of the Royalists at the Battle of Worcester in 1651 marked the end of the first phase of the [[dudley|Dudley]] ironmaking enterprise. [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] gradually emerged from obscurity in the Restoration period, retiring to St. Helen's in Worcestershire. (011-080) (011-153)

## Notable Ironworks and Ironmasters

Several Worcestershire ironworks operated during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]] (1616–1684), another notable Worcestershire figure, was apprenticed to a Worcester linendraper before becoming an engineer and ironmaster. (012-088) (012-089) In 1652, Yarranton established ironworks at Ashley, near Bewdley. (012-109)

The Foley family, prominent Midlands ironmasters, also had connections to the Worcester area. Richard Foley (1580–1657) began his career in [[stourbridge|Stourbridge]], close to the Worcestershire border, and the family's network of furnaces and slitting mills extended through the county.
