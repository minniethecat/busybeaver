## Overview

Cardiff, situated on the [[bristol|Bristol]] Channel in [[south-wales|South Wales]], became one of the world's most important coal and iron exporting ports during the nineteenth century. The town's rise was driven by the rapid expansion of the [[south-wales|South Wales]] iron industry, which drew [[sussex|Sussex]] ironmasters to [[glamorgan|Glamorganshire]] in the sixteenth century and flourished through the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Early [[iron-industry|Iron Industry]] in South Wales

The iron industry of [[south-wales|South Wales]] traces its origins to the late sixteenth century, when ironmasters from [[sussex|Sussex]] migrated to [[glamorgan|Glamorgan]]shire. [[sussex|The Weald]]'s charcoal-based iron industry was declining as timber resources depleted, and [[south-wales|South Wales]] offered abundant woodland, iron-ore, and water-power. (008-141) By the early 1700s, most of the early Welsh ironworks had been razed to the ground and rebuilt on a larger scale. (008-159)

## The Rise of the Great Ironworks

[[south-wales|South Wales]] became home to some of the largest ironworks in the world during the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The [[dowlais-ironworks|[[dowlais-ironworks|Dowlais]] Ironworks]], established near [[merthyr-tydfil|Merthyr Tydfil]], grew to become the largest iron-producing enterprise in the world under the management of the Guest family. The Crawshay family's [[cyfarthfa-ironworks|Cyfarthfa Ironworks]] rivalled Dowlais in scale and output. Together, the [[south-wales|South Wales]] ironworks produced rails, plates, and structural iron that supplied railways and engineering works across the globe. (026-157)

[[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] maintained close connections with the [[south-wales|South Wales]] [[iron-industry|iron trade]], and the Homfray family were among the great Welsh ironmasters who shaped the industry's development. (022-084) (024-080)

## Cardiff as Iron and Coal Port

Cardiff's transformation from a small market town into a major industrial port was driven by the need to export the output of the [[south-wales|South Wales]] ironworks and, increasingly, the steam coal of the Welsh valleys. The construction of the Glamorganshire Canal in 1794 and the Taff Vale Railway in 1840 connected Cardiff to the Merthyr iron district, enabling the efficient shipment of iron and coal.

By the mid-nineteenth century, Cardiff's Bute Docks were among the busiest in the world. The port handled millions of tons of coal and iron annually, supplying steamships, railways, and manufacturing enterprises worldwide. The city's rapid growth reflected the scale of the [[south-wales|South Wales]] mineral wealth that flowed through its docks. (029-042)

## Engineering Connections

The [[south-wales|South Wales]] iron and coal trade attracted engineers from across Britain. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] travelled through [[south-wales|South Wales]], visiting ironworks and studying their methods, while the great railway engineers of the age — including [[isambard-kingdom-brunel|Isambard Kingdom Brunel]] — depended on Welsh iron for their constructions. (053-170) (053-174)
