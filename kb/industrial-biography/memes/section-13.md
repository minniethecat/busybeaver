[013-001]Another of Yarranton's far-sighted schemes of a similar kind was one to

[013-002]connect the [[thames|Thames]] with the [[severn|Severn]] by means of an artificial cut, at

[013-003]the very place where, more than a century after his death, it was

[013-004]actually carried out by modern engineers.  This canal, it appears, was

[013-005]twice surveyed under his direction by his son.  He did, however,

[013-006]succeed in his own time in opening up the navigation of the [[river-avon|Avon]], and

[013-007]was the first to carry barges upon its waters from Tewkesbury to

[013-008]Stratford.

[013-009]The improvement of agriculture, too, had a share of Yarranton's

[013-010]attention.  He saw the soil exhausted by long tillage and constantly

[013-011]repeated crops of rye, and he urged that the land should have rest or

[013-012]at least rotation of crop.  With this object he introduced clover-seed,

[013-013]and supplied it largely to the farmers of the western counties, who

[013-014]found their land doubled in value by the new method of husbandry, and

[013-015]it shortly became adopted throughout the country.  Seeing how commerce

[013-016]was retarded by the small accommodation provided for shipping at the

[013-017]then principal ports, Yarranton next made surveys and planned docks for

[013-018]the city of [[london|London]]; but though he zealously advocated the subject, he

[013-019]found few supporters, and his plans proved fruitless.  In this respect

[013-020]he was nearly a hundred and fifty years before his age, and the [[london|London]]

[013-021]importers continued to conduct their shipping business in the crowded

[013-022]tideway of the [[thames|Thames]] down even to the beginning of the present century.

[013-023]While carrying on his iron works, it occurred to Yarranton that it

[013-024]would be of great national advantage if the manufacture of tin-plate

[013-025]could be introduced into [[england|England]].  Although the richest tin mines then

[013-026]known existed in this country, the mechanical arts were at so low an

[013-027]ebb that we were almost entirely dependent upon foreigners for the

[013-028]supply of the articles manufactured from the metal.  The Saxons were

[013-029]the principal consumers of English tin, and we obtained from them in

[013-030]return nearly the whole of our tin-plates.  All attempts made to

[013-031]manufacture them in [[england|England]] had hitherto failed; the beating out of the

[013-032]iron by hammers into laminae sufficiently thin and smooth, and the

[013-033]subsequent distribution and fixing of the film of tin over the surface

[013-034]of the iron, proving difficulties which the English manufacturers were

[013-035]unable to overcome.  To master these difficulties the indefatigable

[013-036]Yarranton set himself to work.  "Knowing," says he, "the usefulness of

[013-037]tin-plates and the goodness of our metals for that purpose, I did,

[013-038]about sixteen years since (i.e.  about 1665), endeavour to find out the

[013-039]way for making thereof; whereupon I acquainted a person of much riches,

[013-040]and one that was very understanding in the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]], who was

[013-041]pleased to say that he had often designed to get the trade into

[013-042]England, but never could find out the way.  Upon which it was agreed

[013-043]that a sum of monies should be advanced by several persons,[13] for the

[013-044]defraying of my charges of travelling to the place where these plates

[013-045]are made, and from thence to bring away the art of making them.  Upon

[013-046]which, an able fire-man, that well understood the nature of iron, was

[013-047]made choice of to accompany me; and being fitted with an ingenious

[013-048]interpreter that well understood the language, and that had dealt much

[013-049]in that commodity, we marched first for Hamburgh, then to Leipsic, and

[013-050]from thence to Dresden, the Duke of Saxony's court, where we had notice

[013-051]of the place where the plates were made; which was in a large tract of

[013-052]mountainous land, running from a place called Seger-Hutton unto a town

[013-053]called Awe [Au], being in length about twenty miles." [14]

[013-054]It is curious to find how much the national industry of England has

[013-055]been influenced by the existence from time to time of religious

[013-056]persecutions abroad, which had the effect of driving skilled Protestant

[013-057]artisans, more particularly from Flanders and France, into England,

[013-058]where they enjoyed the special protection of successive English

[013-059]Governments, and founded various important branches of manufacture.

[013-060]But it appears from the history of the tin manufactures of Saxony, that

[013-061]that country also had profited in like manner by the religious

[013-062]persecutions of Germany, and even of England itself.  Thus we are told

[013-063]by Yarranton that it was a Cornish miner, a Protestant, banished out of

[013-064]England for his religion in Queen Mary's time, who discovered the tin

[013-065]mines at Awe, and that a Romish priest of Bohemia, who had been

[013-066]converted to Lutheranism and fled into Saxony for refuge, "was the

[013-067]chief instrument in the manufacture until it was perfected."  These two

[013-068]men were held in great regard by the Duke of Saxony as well as by the

[013-069]people of the country; for their ingenuity and industry proved the

[013-070]source of great prosperity and wealth, "several fine cities," says

[013-071]Yarranton, "having been raised by the riches proceeding from the

[013-072]tin-works"--not less than 80,000 men depending upon the trade for their

[013-073]subsistence; and when Yarranton visited Awe, he found that a statue had

[013-074]been erected to the memory of the Cornish miner who first discovered

[013-075]the tin.

[013-076]Yarranton was very civilly received by the miners, and, contrary to his

[013-077]expectation, he was allowed freely to inspect the tin-works and examine

[013-078]the methods by which the iron-plates were rolled out, as well as the

[013-079]process of tinning them.  He was even permitted to engage a number of

[013-080]skilled workmen, whom he brought over with him to England for the

[013-081]purpose of starting the manufacture in this country.  A beginning was

[013-082]made, and the tin-plates manufactured by Yarranton's men were

[013-083]pronounced of better quality even than those made in Saxony.  "Many

[013-084]thousand plates," Yarranton says, "were made from iron raised in the

[013-085][[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], and were tinned over with Cornish tin; and the plates

[013-086]proved far better than the German ones, by reason of the toughness and

[013-087]flexibleness of our forest iron.  One Mr. Bison, a tinman in [[worcestershire|Worcester]],

[013-088]Mr. Lydiate near Fleet Bridge, and Mr. Harrison near the King's Bench,

[013-089]have wrought many, and know their goodness." As Yarranton's account was

[013-090]written and published during the lifetime of the parties, there is no

[013-091]reason to doubt the accuracy of his statement.

[013-092]Arrangements were made to carry on the manufacture upon a large scale;

[013-093]but the secret having got wind, a patent was taken out, or "trumpt up"

[013-094]as Yarranton calls it, for the manufacture, "the patentee being

[013-095]countenanced by some persons of quality," and Yarranton was precluded

[013-096]from carrying his operations further.  It is not improbable that the

[013-097]patentee in question was [[william-chamberlaine|William Chamberlaine]], [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s quondam

[013-098]partner in the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]].[15]  "What with the patent being in

[013-099]our way," says Yarranton, "and the richest of our partners being afraid

[013-100]to offend great men in power, who had their eye upon us, it caused the

[013-101]thing to cool, and the making of the tin-plates was neither proceeded

[013-102]in by us, nor possibly could be by him that had the patent; because

[013-103]neither he that hath the patent, nor those that have countenanced him,

[013-104]can make one plate fit for use." Yarranton's labours were thus lost to

[013-105]the English public for a time; and we continued to import all our

[013-106]tin-plates from Germany until about sixty years later, when a tin-plate

[013-107]manufactory was established by Capel Hanbury at [[pontypool|Pontypool]] in

[013-108][[newport|Monmouthshire]], where it has since continued to be successfully carried

[013-109]on.

[013-110]We can only briefly refer to the subsequent history of Andrew

[013-111]Yarranton.  Shortly after his journey into Saxony, he proceeded to

[013-112]Holland to examine the inland navigations of the Dutch, to inspect

[013-113]their linen and other manufactures, and to inquire into the causes of

[013-114]the then extraordinary prosperity of that country compared with

[013-115]England.  Industry was in a very languishing state at home.  "People

[013-116]confess they are sick," said Yarranton, "that trade is in a

[013-117]consumption, and the whole nation languishes."  He therefore determined

[013-118]to ascertain whether something useful might not be learnt from the

[013-119]example of Holland.  The Dutch were then the hardest working and the

[013-120]most thriving people in Europe.  They were manufacturers and carriers

[013-121]for the world.  Their fleets floated on every known sea; and their

[013-122]herring-busses swarmed along our coasts as far north as the Hebrides.

[013-123]The Dutch supplied our markets with fish caught within sight of our own

[013-124]shores, while our coasting population stood idly looking on.  Yarranton

[013-125]regarded this state of things as most discreditable, and he urged the

[013-126]establishment of various branches of home industry as the best way of

[013-127]out-doing the Dutch without fighting them.

[013-128]Wherever he travelled abroad, in Germany or in Holland, he saw industry

[013-129]attended by wealth and comfort, and idleness by poverty and misery.

[013-130]The same pursuits, he held, would prove as beneficial to England as

[013-131]they were abundantly proved to have been to Holland.  The healthy life

[013-132]of work was good for all--for individuals as for the whole nation; and

[013-133]if we would out-do the Dutch, he held that we must out-do them in

[013-134]industry.  But all must be done honestly and by fair means.  "Common

[013-135]Honesty," said Yarranton, "is as necessary and needful in kingdoms and

[013-136]commonwealths that depend upon Trade, as discipline is in an army; and

[013-137]where there is want of common Honesty in a kingdom or commonwealth,

[013-138]from thence Trade shall depart.  For as the Honesty of all governments

[013-139]is, so shall be their Riches; and as their Honour, Honesty, and Riches

[013-140]are, so will be their Strength; and as their Honour, Honesty, Riches,

[013-141]and Strength are, so will be their Trade.  These are five sisters that

[013-142]go hand in hand, and must not be parted." Admirable sentiments, which

[013-143]are as true now as they were two hundred years ago, when Yarranton

[013-144]urged them upon the attention of the English public.

[013-145]On his return from Holland, he accordingly set on foot various schemes

[013-146]of public utility.  He stirred up a movement for the encouragement of

[013-147]the British fisheries.  He made several journeys into Ireland for the

[013-148]purpose of planting new manufactures there.  He surveyed the River

[013-149]Slade with the object of rendering it navigable, and proposed a plan

[013-150]for improving the harbour of Dublin.  He also surveyed the Dee in

[013-151]England with a view to its being connected with the [[severn|Severn]].  Chambers

[013-152]says that on the decline of his popularity in 1677, he was taken by

[013-153]Lord Clarendon to Salisbury to survey the [[river-avon|River Avon]], and find out how

[013-154]that river might be made navigable, and also whether a safe harbour for

[013-155]ships could be made at Christchurch; and that having found where he

[013-156]thought safe anchorage might be obtained, his Lordship proceeded to act

[013-157]upon Yarranton's recommendations.[16]

[013-158]Another of his grand schemes was the establishment of the linen

[013-159]manufacture in the central counties of England, which, he showed, were

[013-160]well adapted for the growth of flax; and he calculated that if success

[013-161]attended his efforts, at least two millions of money then sent out of

[013-162]the country for the purchase of foreign linen would be retained at

[013-163]home, besides increasing the value of the land on which the flax was

[013-164]grown, and giving remunerative employment to our own people, then

[013-165]emigrating for want of work.  "Nothing but Sloth or Envy," he said,

[013-166]"can possibly hinder my labours from being crowned with the wished for

[013-167]success; our habitual fondness for the one hath already brought us to

[013-168]the brink of ruin, and our proneness to the other hath almost

[013-169]discouraged all pious endeavours to promote our future happiness."

[013-170]In 1677 he published the first part of his England's Improvement by Sea

[013-171]and Land--a very remarkable book, full of sagacious insight as

[013-172]respected the future commercial and manufacturing greatness of England.

[013-173]Mr. Dove says of this book that "Yarranton chalks out in it the future

[013-174]course of Britain with as free a hand as if second-sight had revealed

[013-175]to him those expansions of her industrial career which never fail to

[013-176]surprise us, even when we behold them realized." Besides his extensive

[013-177]plans for making harbours and improving internal navigation with the

[013-178]object of creating new channels for domestic industry, his schemes for

[013-179]extending the iron and the woollen trades, establishing the linen

[013-180]manufacture, and cultivating the home fisheries, we find him throwing

[013-181]out various valuable suggestions with reference to the means of

[013-182]facilitating commercial transactions, some of winch have only been

[013-183]carried out in our own day.  One of his grandest ideas was the

[013-184]establishment of a public bank, the credit of which, based upon the

[013-185]security of freehold land,[17] should enable its paper "to go in trade

[013-186]equal with ready money."  A bank of this sort formed one of the

[013-187]principal means by which the Dutch had been enabled to extend their

[013-188]commercial transactions, and Yarranton accordingly urged its

[013-189]introduction into England.  Part of his scheme consisted of a voluntary

[013-190]register of real property, for the purpose of effecting simplicity of

[013-191]title, and obtaining relief from the excessive charges for law,[18] as

[013-192]well as enabling money to be readily raised for commercial purposes on

[013-193]security of the land registered.
