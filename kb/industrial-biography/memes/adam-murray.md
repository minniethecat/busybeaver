## Early Life

Little is recorded of Adam Murray's early years. He rose to prominence as a large cotton-spinner and manufacturer in [[Manchester]] during the early decades of the nineteenth century, partnering with his brother George Murray in the firm of Adam and George Murray. (054-068)

## Cotton-Spinning Enterprise

The firm of Adam and George Murray was among the substantial [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]] establishments of [[manchester|Manchester]], operating at a time when the cotton industry was undergoing rapid mechanisation and expansion. The mills required constant maintenance and upgrading of their power transmission systems, which created opportunities for a new generation of mechanical engineers. (054-068)

## Patronage of William Fairbairn

Adam Murray played a significant role in the early career of [[William Fairbairn]], the celebrated engineer. In 1817, Fairbairn and his partner [[james-lillie|James Lillie]], a young firm without capital and almost without plant, were seeking work. Among others, Fairbairn called upon the Messrs. Adam and George Murray, bringing with him the designs of his iron bridge. (054-068)

Mr. Adam Murray received Fairbairn kindly, heard his explanations, and invited him to call on the following day with his partner. The manufacturer was favourably impressed, for the next day he took them over his mill and asked whether they felt themselves competent to renew with horizontal cross-shafts the whole of the work by which the mule-spinning machinery was turned. (054-069)

This was a formidable enterprise for a young firm to undertake, but Fairbairn and Lillie had confidence in themselves and boldly replied that they were willing and able to execute the work. Murray, prudently, called at their workshop to satisfy himself that they possessed the means of undertaking such an order. Despite finding only a single lathe—the one the partners had just built and set up—he was more favourably impressed by the individual merits of the partners than by the excellence of their [[machine-tool|machine-tools]], and he gave them the order. (054-070)

Fairbairn and Lillie worked "late and early—from 5 in the morning until 9 at night for a considerable period" and succeeded in completing the alterations within the time specified, and to Mr. Murray's entire satisfaction. This first contract proved the practical skill of the young men and established their reputation, launching Fairbairn on the path to becoming one of the foremost engineers of his generation. (054-071)

## Personal Character

Adam Murray appears in the historical record as a discerning judge of ability and character, willing to take a chance on unknown young mechanics when he recognised their merits. His decision to entrust Fairbairn and Lillie with a substantial contract, despite their meagre resources, proved to be a pivotal moment in British engineering history. (054-072)

[[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] later recalled Adam Murray as "one of his most intimate acquaintances," indicating a friendship that endured well beyond their initial business relationship. (054-188)
