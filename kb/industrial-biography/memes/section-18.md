[018-001]The future management of his extensive ironworks being thus placed in

[018-002]able hands, [[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]] finally left [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1804, for

[018-003][[bristol|Bristol]], his native town, where he spent the remainder of his life in

[018-004]works of charity and mercy.  Here we might leave the subject, but

[018-005]cannot refrain from adding a few concluding words as to the moral

[018-006]characteristics of this truly good man.  Though habitually religious,

[018-007]he was neither demure nor morose, but cheerful, gay, and humorous.  He

[018-008]took great interest in the pleasures of the young people about him, and

[018-009]exerted himself in all ways to promote their happiness.  He was fond of

[018-010]books, pictures, poetry, and music, though the indulgence of artistic

[018-011]tastes is not thought becoming in the Society to which he belonged.

[018-012]His love for the beauties of nature amounted almost to a passion, and

[018-013]when living at The Bank, near [[ketley|Ketley]], it was his great delight in the

[018-014]summer evenings to retire with his pipe to a rural seat commanding a

[018-015]full view of the Wrekin, the Ercall Woods, with Cader Idris and the

[018-016]Montgomeryshire hills in the distance, and watch the sun go down in the

[018-017]west in his glory.  Once in every year he assembled a large party to

[018-018]spend a day with him on the Wrekin, and amongst those invited were the

[018-019]principal clerks in the company's employment, together with their

[018-020]families.  At Madeley, near [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], where he bought a property,

[018-021]he laid out, for the express use of the workmen, extensive walks

[018-022]through the woods on Lincoln Hill, commanding beautiful views.  They

[018-023]were called "The Workmen's Walks," and were a source of great enjoyment

[018-024]to them and their families, especially on Sunday afternoons.

[018-025]When [[richard-reynolds|Mr. Reynolds]] went to [[london|London]] on business, he was accustomed to make

[018-026]a round of visits, on his way home, to places remarkable for their

[018-027]picturesque beauty, such as Stowe, Hagley Park, and the Leasowes.

[018-028]After a visit to the latter place in 1767, he thus, in a letter to his

[018-029]friend John Maccappen, vindicated his love for the beautiful in

[018-030]nature:--"I think it not only lawful but expedient to cultivate a

[018-031]disposition to be pleased with the beauties of nature, by frequent

[018-032]indulgences for that purpose.  The mind, by being continually applied

[018-033]to the consideration of ways and means to gain money, contracts an

[018-034]indifferency if not an insensibility to the profusion of beauties which

[018-035]the benevolent Creator has impressed upon every part of the material

[018-036]creation.  A sordid love of gold, the possession of what gold can

[018-037]purchase, and the reputation of being rich, have so depraved the finer

[018-038]feelings of some men, that they pass through the most delightful grove,

[018-039]filled with the melody of nature, or listen to the murmurings of the

[018-040]brook in the valley, with as little pleasure and with no more of the

[018-041]vernal delight which Milton describes, than they feel in passing

[018-042]through some obscure alley in a town."

[018-043]When in the prime of life, Mr. Reynolds was an excellent rider,

[018-044]performing all his journeys on horseback.  He used to give a ludicrous

[018-045]account of a race he once ran with another youth, each having a lady

[018-046]seated on a pillion behind him; Mr. Reynolds reached the goal first,

[018-047]but when he looked round he found that he had lost his fair companion,

[018-048]who had fallen off in the race! On another occasion he had a hard run

[018-049]with Lord Thurlow during a visit paid by the latter to the [[ketley|Ketley]]

[018-050]Iron-Works.  Lord Thurlow pulled up his horse first, and observed,

[018-051]laughing, "I think, Mr. Reynolds, this is probably the first time that

[018-052]ever a Lord Chancellor rode a race with a Quaker!" But a stranger

[018-053]rencontre was one which befel Mr. Reynolds on Blackheath.  Though he

[018-054]declined Government orders for cannon, he seems to have had a secret

[018-055]hankering after the "pomp and circumstance" of military life.  At all

[018-056]event's he was present on Blackheath one day when George III. was

[018-057]reviewing some troops.  Mr. Reynold's horse, an old trooper, no sooner

[018-058]heard the sound of the trumpet than he started off at full speed, and

[018-059]made directly for the group of officers before whom the troops were

[018-060]defiling.  Great was the surprise of the King when he saw the Quaker

[018-061]draw up alongside of him, but still greater, perhaps, was the confusion

[018-062]of the Quaker at finding himself in such company.

[018-063]During the later years of his life, while living at [[bristol|Bristol]], his hand

[018-064]was in every good work; and it was often felt where it was not seen.

[018-065]For he carefully avoided ostentation, and preferred doing his good in

[018-066]secret.  He strongly disapproved of making charitable bequests by will,

[018-067]which he observed in many cases to have been the foundation of enormous

[018-068]abuses, but held it to be the duty of each man to do all the possible

[018-069]good that he could during his lifetime.  Many were the instances of his

[018-070]princely, though at the time unknown, munificence.  Unwilling to be

[018-071]recognised as the giver of large sums, he employed agents to dispense

[018-072]his anonymous benefactions.  He thus sent 20,000L. to [[london|London]] to be

[018-073]distributed during the distress of 1795.  He had four almoners

[018-074]constantly employed in Bristol, finding out cases of distress,

[018-075]relieving them, and presenting their accounts to him weekly, with

[018-076]details of the cases relieved.  He searched the debtors' prisons, and

[018-077]where, as often happened, deserving but unfortunate men were found

[018-078]confined for debt, he paid the claims against them and procured their

[018-079]release.  Such a man could not fail to be followed with blessings and

[018-080]gratitude; but these he sought to direct to the Giver of all Good.  "My

[018-081]talent," said he to a friend, "is the meanest of all talents--a little

[018-082]sordid dust; but as the man in the parable who had but one talent was

[018-083]held accountable, I also am accountable for the talent that I possess,

[018-084]humble as it is, to the great Lord of all."  On one occasion the case

[018-085]of a poor orphan boy was submitted to him, whose parents, both dying

[018-086]young, had left him destitute, on which Mr. Reynolds generously offered

[018-087]to place a sum in the names of trustees for his education and

[018-088]maintenance until he could be apprenticed to a business.  The lady who

[018-089]represented the case was so overpowered by the munificence of the act

[018-090]that she burst into tears, and, struggling to express her gratitude,

[018-091]concluded with--"and when the dear child is old enough, I will teach

[018-092]him to thank his benefactor."  "Thou must teach him to look higher,"

[018-093]interrupted Reynolds:  "Do we thank the clouds for rain? When the child

[018-094]grows up, teach him to thank Him who sendeth both the clouds and the

[018-095]rain."  Reynolds himself deplored his infirmity of temper, which was by

[018-096]nature hasty; and, as his benevolence was known, and appeals were made

[018-097]to him at all times, seasonable and unseasonable, he sometimes met them

[018-098]with a sharp word, which, however, he had scarcely uttered before he

[018-099]repented of it:  and he is known to have followed a poor woman to her

[018-100]home and ask forgiveness for having spoken hastily in answer to her

[018-101]application for help.

[018-102]This "great good man" died on the 10th of September, 1816, in the 81st

[018-103]year of his age.  At his funeral the poor of Bristol were the chief

[018-104]mourners.  The children of the benevolent societies which he had

[018-105]munificently supported during his lifetime, and some of which he had

[018-106]founded, followed his body to the grave.  The procession was joined by

[018-107]the clergy and ministers of all denominations, and by men of all

[018-108]classes and persuasions.  And thus was [[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]] laid to his

[018-109]rest, leaving behind him a name full of good odour, which will long be

[018-110]held in grateful remembrance by the inhabitants of Bristol.

[018-141][7] Reverberatory, so called because the flame or current of heated

[018-142]gases from the fuel is caused to be reverberated or reflected down upon

[018-143]the substance under operation before passing into the chimney.  It is

[018-144]curious that Rovenson, in his Treatise of Metallica of 1613, describes

[018-145]a reverberatory furnace in which iron was to be smelted by pit-coal,

[018-146]though it does not appear that he succeeded in perfecting his

[018-147]invention.  Dr. Percy, in his excellent work on Metallurgy, thus

[018-148]describes a reverberatory furnace:--"It consists essentially of three

[018-149]parts--a fireplace at one end, a stack or chimney at the other, and a

[018-150]bed between both on which the matter is heated.  The fireplace is

[018-151]separated from the bed by a low partition wall called the fire-bridge,

[018-152]and both are covered by an arched roof which rises from the end wall of

[018-153]the fireplace and gradually dips toward the furthest end of the bed

[018-154]connected with the stack.  On one or both sides of the bed, or at the

[018-155]end near the stack, may be openings through which the ore spread over

[018-156]the surface of the bed may be stirred about and exposed to the action

[018-157]of the air.  The matter is heated in such a furnace by flame, and is

[018-158]kept from contact with the solid fuel.  The flame in its course from

[018-159]the fireplace to the stack is reflected downwards or REVERBERATED on

[018-160]the matter beneath, whence the name REVERBERATORY furnace."
