# Calder Iron Works

The Calder Iron Works, located near the river Calder in the parish of Old Monkland, [[scotland|Scotland]], played a pivotal role in the history of British iron-making. It was here that [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] made one of the most important discoveries in metallurgical history — the Black Band ironstone — and where the [[hot-blast|hot blast]] process was first trialled, transforming the economics of iron production. (026-090) (028-123)

## Industrial Significance

### Discovery of Black Band [[ironstone|Ironstone]]

In 1801, while engaged in erecting the Calder Iron Works for himself and his partners, [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] discovered a new variety of ironstone in the bed of the river Calder. Mushet observed that while crossing the river Calder in the parish of Old Monkland, he found a specimen in the river-bed which proved to be an ironstone of extraordinary richness. (026-090) (027-032)

This discovery — which Mushet named the Black Band ironstone — proved to be one of the richest sources of iron yet found. Seams of black band were subsequently discovered throughout the Scottish lowlands, and the ironstone was remarkable in that it required almost no addition of coal for smelting. (027-043)

### Commercial Obstacles

Despite the value of his discovery, Mushet faced great prejudice from the established ironmasters of the district. His eventual separation from the Calder Iron Works lost him possession of extensive tracts of Black Band ironstone, and for several years after its discovery, its use was confined to the Calder Iron Works alone. (026-117) (027-053) The ironmasters had been accustomed to using richer and more tractable ores and were slow to recognise the value of the Black Band.

### The [[hot-blast|Hot Blast]] Trials

The Calder Iron Works later became one of the two sites — alongside [[clyde-iron-works|[[clyde|Clyde]] Iron Works]] — where the first trials of [[james-neilson|[[james-beaumont-neilson|James Neilson]]'s]] hot blast process were made. The success of these trials led to the rapid spread of the hot blast throughout the iron-mining districts of [[scotland|Scotland]] and beyond. (028-123) (028-148)

## Key Figures

- [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] — metallurgist who discovered Black Band ironstone at Calder (026-090)
- [[james-neilson|James Neilson]] — inventor of the hot blast, first trialled at Calder and [[clyde|Clyde]] (028-123)
