[043-001]As with many other original-minded mechanics, invention became a habit

[043-002]with him, and by study and labour he rarely failed in attaining the

[043-003]object which he had bent his mind upon accomplishing.  Indeed, nothing

[043-004]pleased him better than to have what he called "a tough job;" as it

[043-005]stimulated his inventive faculty, in the exercise of which he took the

[043-006]highest pleasure.  Hence mechanical schemers of all kinds were

[043-007]accustomed to resort to Clement for help when they had found an idea

[043-008]which they desired to embody in a machine.  If there was any value in

[043-009]their idea, none could be more ready than he to recognise its merit,

[043-010]and to work it into shape; but if worthless, he spoke out his mind at

[043-011]once, dissuading the projector from wasting upon it further labour or

[043-012]expense.

[043-013]One of the important branches of practical mechanics to which Clement

[043-014]continued through life to devote himself, was the improvement of

[043-015]self-acting tools, more especially of the slide-lathe.  He introduced

[043-016]various improvements in its construction and arrangement, until in his

[043-017]hands it became as nearly perfect as it was possible to be.  In 1818,

[043-018]he furnished the lathe with a slide rest twenty-two inches long, for

[043-019]the purpose of cutting screws, provided with the means of

[043-020]self-correction; and some years later, in 1827, the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]]

[043-021]awarded him their gold Isis medal for his improved turning-lathe, which

[043-022]embodied many ingenious contrivances calculated to increase its

[043-023]precision and accuracy in large surface-turning.

[043-024]The beautiful arrangements embodied in Mr. Clement's improved lathe can

[043-025]with difficulty be described in words; but its ingenuity may be

[043-026]inferred from a brief statement of the defects which it was invented to

[043-027]remedy, and which it successfully overcame.  When the mandrill of a

[043-028]lathe, having a metal plate fixed to it, turns round with a uniform

[043-029]motion, and the slide rest which carries the cutter is moving from the

[043-030]circumference of the work to the centre, it will be obvious that the

[043-031]quantity of metal passing over the edge of the cutter at each

[043-032]revolution, and therefore at equal intervals of time, is continually

[043-033]diminishing, in exact proportion to the spiral line described by the

[043-034]cutter on the face of the work.  But in turning metal plates it is

[043-035]found very in expedient to increase the speed of the work beyond a

[043-036]certain quantity; for when this happens, and the tool passes the work

[043-037]at too great a velocity, it heats, softens, and is ground away, the

[043-038]edge of the cutter becomes dull, and the surface of the plate is

[043-039]indented and burnished, instead of being turned.  Hence loss of time on

[043-040]the part of the workman, and diminished work on the part of the tool,

[043-041]results which, considering the wages of the one and the capital

[043-042]expended on the construction of the other, are of no small importance;

[043-043]for the prime objects of all improvement of tools are, economy of time

[043-044]and economy of capital--to minimize labour and cost, and maximize

[043-045]result.

[043-046]The defect to which we have referred was almost the only remaining

[043-047]imperfection in the lathe, and Mr. Clement overcame it by making the

[043-048]machine self-regulating; so that, whatever might be the situation of

[043-049]the cutter, equal quantities of metal should pass over it in equal

[043-050]times,--the speed at the centre not exceeding that suited to the work

[043-051]at the circumference,--while the workman was enabled to convert the

[043-052]varying rate of the mandrill into a uniform one whenever he chose.

[043-053]Thus the expedients of wheels, riggers, and drums, of different

[043-054]diameters, by which it had been endeavoured to alter the speed of the

[043-055]lathe-mandrill, according to the hardness of the metal and the diameter

[043-056]of the thing to be turned, were effectually disposed of.  These, though

[043-057]answering very well where cylinders of equal diameter had to be bored,

[043-058]and a uniform motion was all that was required, were found very

[043-059]inefficient where a Plane surface had to be turned; and it was in such

[043-060]cases that Mr. Clement's lathe was found so valuable.  By its means

[043-061]surfaces of unrivalled correctness were produced, and the slide-lathe,

[043-062]so improved, became recognised and adopted as the most accurate and

[043-063]extensively applicable of all [[machine-tool|machine-tools]].

[043-064]The year after Mr. Clement brought out his improved turning-lathe, he

[043-065]added to it his self-adjusting double driving centre-chuck, for which

[043-066]the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]] awarded him their silver medal in 1828.  In

[043-067]introducing this invention to the notice of the Society, Mr. Clement

[043-068]said, "Although I have been in the habit of turning and making

[043-069]turning-lathes and other machinery for upwards of thirty-five years,

[043-070]and have examined the best turning-lathes in the principal

[043-071]manufactories throughout Great Britain, I find it universally regretted

[043-072]by all practical men that they cannot turn anything perfectly true

[043-073]between the centres of the lathe."  It was found by experience, that

[043-074]there was a degree of eccentricity, and consequently of imperfection,

[043-075]in the figure of any long cylinder turned while suspended between the

[043-076]centres of the lathe, and made to revolve by the action of a single

[043-077]driver.  Under such circumstances the pressure of the tool tended to

[043-078]force the work out of the right line and to distribute the strain

[043-079]between the driver and the adjacent centre, so that one end of the

[043-080]cylinder became eccentric with respect to the other.  By Mr. Clement's

[043-081]invention of the two-armed driver, which was self-adjusting, the strain

[043-082]was taken from the centre and divided between the two arms, which being

[043-083]equidistant from the centre, effectually corrected all eccentricity in

[043-084]the work.  This invention was found of great importance in ensuring the

[043-085]true turning of large machinery, which before had been found a matter

[043-086]of considerable difficulty.

[043-087]In the same year (1828) Mr. Clement began the making of fluted taps and

[043-088]dies, and he established a mechanical practice with reference to the

[043-089]pitch of the screw, which proved of the greatest importance in the

[043-090]economics of manufacture.  Before his time, each mechanical engineer

[043-091]adopted a thread of his own; so that when a piece of work came under

[043-092]repair, the screw-hob had usually to be drilled out, and a new thread

[043-093]was introduced according to the usage which prevailed in the shop in

[043-094]which the work was executed.  Mr. Clement saw a great waste of labour

[043-095]in this practice, and he promulgated the idea that every screw of a

[043-096]particular length ought to be furnished with its appointed number of

[043-097]threads of a settled pitch.  Taking the inch as the basis of his

[043-098]calculations, he determined the number of threads in each case; and the

[043-099]practice thus initiated by him, recommended as it was by convenience

[043-100]and economy, was very shortly adopted throughout the trade.  It may be

[043-101]mentioned that one of Clement's ablest journeymen, Mr. Whitworth, has,

[043-102]since his time, been mainly instrumental in establishing the settled

[043-103]practice; and Whitworth's thread (initiated by Clement) has become

[043-104]recognised throughout the mechanical world.  To carry out his idea,

[043-105]Clement invented his screw-engine lathe, with gearing, mandrill, and

[043-106]sliding-table wheel-work, by means of which he first cut the inside

[043-107]screw-tools from the left-handed hobs--the reverse mode having before

[043-108]been adopted,--while in [[shaping-machine|shaping machine]]s he was the first to use the

[043-109]revolving cutter attached to the slide rest.  Then, in 1828, he fluted

[043-110]the taps for the first time with a revolving cutter,--other makers

[043-111]having up to that time only notched them.  Among his other inventions

[043-112]in screws may be mentioned his headless tap, which, according to Mr.

[043-113]Nasmyth, is so valuable an invention, that, "if he had done nothing

[043-114]else, it ought to immortalize him among mechanics.  It passed right

[043-115]through the hole to be tapped, and was thus enabled to do the duty of

[043-116]three ordinary screws."  By these improvements much greater precision

[043-117]was secured in the manufacture of tools and machinery, accompanied by a

[043-118]greatly reduced cost of production; the results of which are felt to

[043-119]this day.

[043-120]Another of Mr. Clement's ingenious inventions was his [[planing-machine|Planing Machine]],

[043-121]by means of which metal plates of large dimensions were planed with

[043-122]perfect truth and finished with beautiful accuracy.  There is perhaps

[043-123]scarcely a machine about which there has been more controversy than

[043-124]this; and we do not pretend to be able to determine the respective

[043-125]merits of the many able mechanics who have had a hand in its invention.

[043-126]It is exceedingly probable that others besides Clement worked out the

[043-127]problem in their own way, by independent methods; and this is confirmed

[043-128]by the circumstance that though the results achieved by the respective

[043-129]inventors were the same, the methods employed by them were in many

[043-130]respects different.  As regards Clement, we find that previous to the

[043-131]year 1820 he had a machine in regular use for planing the triangular

[043-132]bars of lathes and the sides of weaving-looms.  This instrument was

[043-133]found so useful and so economical in its working, that Clement

[043-134]proceeded to elaborate a planing machine of a more complete kind, which

[043-135]he finished and set to work in the year 1825.  He prepared no model of

[043-136]it, but made it direct from the working drawings; and it was so nicely

[043-137]constructed, that when put together it went without a hitch, and has

[043-138]continued steadily working for more than thirty years down to the

[043-139]present day.

[043-140]Clement took out no patent for his invention, relying for protection

[043-141]mainly on his own and his workmen's skill in using it.  We therefore

[043-142]find no specification of his machine at the [[patent-office|Patent Office]], as in the

[043-143]case of most other capital inventions; but a very complete account of

[043-144]it is to be found in the Transactions of the Society of Arts for 1832,

[043-145]as described by Mr. Varley.  The practical value of the [[planing-machine|Planing Machine]]

[043-146]induced the Society to apply to Mr. Clement for liberty to publish a

[043-147]full description of it; and Mr. Varley's paper was the result.[3]  It

[043-148]may be briefly stated that this engineer's plane differs greatly from

[043-149]the carpenter's plane, the cutter of which is only allowed to project

[043-150]so far as to admit of a thin shaving to be sliced off,--the plane

[043-151]working flat in proportion to the width of the tool, and its length and

[043-152]straightness preventing the cutter from descending into any hollows in

[043-153]the wood.  The engineer's plane more resembles the turning-lathe, of

[043-154]which indeed it is but a modification, working up on the same

[043-155]principle, on flat surfaces.  The tools or cutters in Clement's machine

[043-156]were similar to those used in the lathe, varying in like manner, but

[043-157]performing their work in right lines,--the tool being stationary and

[043-158]the work moving under it, the tool only travelling when making lateral

[043-159]cuts.  To save time two cutters were mounted, one to cut the work while

[043-160]going, the other while returning, both being so arranged and held as to

[043-161]be presented to the work in the firmest manner, and with the least

[043-162]possible friction.  The bed of the machine, on which the work was laid,

[043-163]passed under the cutters on perfectly true rollers or wheels, lodged

[043-164]and held in their bearings as accurately as the best mandrill could be,

[043-165]and having set-screws acting against their ends totally preventing all

[043-166]end-motion.  The machine was bedded on a massive and solid foundation

[043-167]of masonry in heavy blocks, the support at all points being so complete

[043-168]as effectually to destroy all tendency to vibration, with the object of

[043-169]securing full, round, and quiet cuts.  The rollers on which the

[043-170]planing-machine travelled were so true, that Clement himself used to

[043-171]say of them, "If you were to put but a paper shaving under one of the

[043-172]rollers, it would at once stop all the rest."  Nor was this any

[043-173]exaggeration--the entire mechanism, notwithstanding its great size,

[043-174]being as true and accurate as that of a watch.

[043-175]By an ingenious adaptation of the apparatus, which will also be found

[043-176]described in the Society of Arts paper, the planing machine might be

[043-177]fitted with a lathe-bed, either to hold two centres, or a head with a

[043-178]suitable mandrill.  When so fitted, the machine was enabled to do the

[043-179]work of a turning-lathe, though in a different way, cutting cylinders

[043-180]or cones in their longitudinal direction perfectly straight, as well as

[043-181]solids or prisms of any angle, either by the longitudinal or lateral

[043-182]motion of the cutter; whilst by making the work revolve, it might be

[043-183]turned as in any other lathe.  This ingenious machine, as contrived by

[043-184]Mr. Clement, therefore represented a complete union of the

[043-185]turning-lathe with the planing machine and dividing engine, by which

[043-186]turning of the most complicated kind might readily be executed.  For

[043-187]ten years after it was set in motion, Clement's was the only machine of

[043-188]the sort available for planing large work; and being consequently very

[043-189]much in request, it was often kept going night and day,--the earnings

[043-190]by the planing machine alone during that time forming the principal

[043-191]income of its inventor.  As it took in a piece of work six feet square,

[043-192]and as his charge for planing was three-halfpence the square inch, or

[043-193]eighteen shillings the square foot, he could thus earn by his machine

[043-194]alone some ten pounds for every day's work of twelve hours.  We may add

[043-195]that since planing machines in various forms have become common in

[043-196]mechanical workshops, the cost of planing does not amount to more than

[043-197]three-halfpence the square foot.
