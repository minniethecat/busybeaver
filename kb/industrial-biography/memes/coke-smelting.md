# Coke Smelting

The substitution of coke (derived from pit-coal) for charcoal in iron smelting — a pivotal innovation that liberated the iron industry from its dependence on dwindling wood supplies and enabled the vast expansion of British iron production in the 18th century.

## Definition

Coke smelting is the process of reducing iron ore to metallic iron in a blast furnace using coke — a carbon-rich fuel produced by heating coal in the absence of air — instead of traditional charcoal. Unlike charcoal, which requires vast quantities of timber and is limited by the availability of woodlands, coke could be produced from abundant pit-coal. The fuel does not come into direct contact with the iron ore; it provides both heat and the reducing gases (carbon monoxide) that strip oxygen from the iron oxide. (001-126)

The key challenge in early coke smelting was the quality of the coke and the behaviour of impurities: early attempts suffered from sulphur contamination from the coal and inconsistent furnace operation. Overcoming these technical barriers took over a century of experimentation, from [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s first attempts in the 1620s to Abraham Darby's successful commercial process at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1709.

## Historical Development

### Early Attempts: [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]

The first serious attempt to smelt iron with pit-coal was made by **[[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]**, the illegitimate son of the Earl of [[dudley|Dudley]], in the early 17th century. [[dudley|Dudley]] claimed to have successfully smelted iron using coke made from pit-coal around 1620, producing iron of marketable quality. He described his method in his treatise *Metallum Martis*. However, his success was intermittent — his works were repeatedly destroyed, once by a flood and several times by rioting ironworkers who feared for their livelihoods. (001-129) (001-130)

[[dudley|Dudley]] faced enormous opposition: "the charcoal ironmasters... did all they could to hinder the use of pit-coal," and his commercial ventures never achieved lasting success. Nevertheless, his pioneering work demonstrated the theoretical possibility of coke smelting.

### Commercial Success: Abraham Darby

The credit for successfully commercialising coke smelting belongs to **[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]]**, a Quaker ironmaster who established the [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] Iron Works in 1709. Darby's crucial innovation was not just the use of coke but the production of a superior quality of coke from the local [[shropshire|Shropshire]] coal, which happened to be low in sulphur. At [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], Darby "succeeded in smelting iron ore with pit-coal" and established the first sustained commercial coke-iron operation. (021-122)

Darby's "method of smelting iron" with coke was initially limited to producing cast-iron goods — pots, kettles, and other hollow-ware — rather than [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. It was his son, **[[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]]I**, who extended the process and whose works supplied the iron for the world's first iron bridge, constructed across the [[severn|Severn]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1779. (001-173) (001-180)

### Wider Adoption

Despite Darby's success, widespread adoption of coke smelting was slow. The process was initially "constrained to sell the pig-iron to the charcoal-ironmasters," as coke-smelted pig iron was considered inferior for conversion to [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. (010-120) It was not until [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]'s [[puddling-process|puddling]] process (1783–1784) provided an effective method of converting coke-smelted pig iron into quality [[wrought-iron|bar iron]] that coke smelting became truly dominant. By the early 19th century, the practicability of "smelting iron with fuel made from pit-coal" was firmly established. (010-043)

## Process

In coke smelting, the blast furnace is charged from the top with alternating layers of iron ore, coke, and limestone flux. A powerful blast of air — originally from water-powered bellows, later from steam-powered blowing engines — is forced into the bottom of the furnace. The coke burns, generating intense heat (over 1,500°C) and producing carbon monoxide, which reduces the iron oxide to metallic iron. The limestone combines with impurities to form slag, which floats on top of the molten iron. The iron is periodically tapped from the bottom as molten pig iron.

The shift from charcoal to coke required modifications to the furnace design. Coke is denser and stronger than charcoal, so it could support taller furnace stacks without crushing — enabling larger, more productive furnaces. The stronger blast needed for coke combustion also drove the development of more powerful blowing engines, notably improved by [[john-smeaton|John Smeaton]] and later by [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s steam engine.

## Economic and Industrial Impact

Coke smelting transformed the [[iron-industry|British iron industry]]. Before its adoption, iron production was limited by the availability of timber for charcoal — a single furnace could consume the wood from several square miles of forest each year. The shift to coal-based fuel removed this bottleneck: "the smelting of iron by means of coke and coal was first successfully accomplished at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]]," and the process spread rapidly thereafter. (015-143)

The economic consequences were profound:
- British pig iron production rose from approximately 17,000 tons in 1740 to over 250,000 tons by 1805
- Britain became self-sufficient in iron, ending dependence on imports from Sweden and Russia
- The price of iron fell dramatically, making it available for construction, machinery, and infrastructure
- [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] became the cradle of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] in iron

The combination of coke smelting (Darby, 1709), [[puddling-process|puddling]] (Cort, 1784), and the hot blast (Neilson, 1828) constituted the three foundational innovations that made iron the defining material of the Industrial Revolution.
