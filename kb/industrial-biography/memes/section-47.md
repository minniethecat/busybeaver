[047-001]A considerable branch of business carried on by the firm of Sharp,

[047-002]Roberts, and Co. was the manufacture of iron billiard-tables, which

[047-003]were constructed with almost perfect truth by means of Mr. Roberts's

[047-004]planing-machine, and became a large article of export.  But a much more

[047-005]important and remunerative department was the manufacture of

[047-006]locomotives, which was begun by the firm shortly after the opening of

[047-007]the [[liverpool-and-manchester-railway|[[liverpool|Liverpool]] and [[manchester|Manchester]] Railway]] had marked this as one of the

[047-008]chief branches of future mechanical engineering.  Mr. Roberts adroitly

[047-009]seized the opportunity presented by this new field of invention and

[047-010]enterprise, and devoted himself for a time to the careful study of the

[047-011]locomotive and its powers.  As early as the year 1829 we find him

[047-012]presenting to the [[manchester-mechanics-institute|Manchester Mechanics' Institute]] a machine exhibiting

[047-013]the nature of friction upon railroads, in solution of the problem then

[047-014]under discussion in the scientific journals.  In the following year he

[047-015]patented an arrangement for communicating power to both driving-wheels

[047-016]of the locomotive, at all times in the exact proportions required when

[047-017]turning to the right or left,--an arrangement which has since been

[047-018]adopted in many [[locomotive|road locomotive]]s and agricultural engines.  In the same

[047-019]patent will be found embodied his invention of the steam-brake, which

[047-020]was also a favourite idea of [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]], since elaborated by Mr.

[047-021]MacConnell of the [[london|London]] and North-Western Railway.  In 1834, Sharp,

[047-022]Roberts, and Co. began the manufacture of locomotives on a large scale;

[047-023]and the compactness of their engines, the excellence of their

[047-024]workmanship, and the numerous original improvements introduced in them,

[047-025]speedily secured for the engines of the Atlas firm a high reputation

[047-026]and a very large demand.  Among Mr. Roberts's improvements may be

[047-027]mentioned his method of manufacturing the crank axle, of welding the

[047-028]rim and tyres of the wheels, and his arrangement and form of the

[047-029]wrought-iron framing and axle-guards.  His system of templets and

[047-030]gauges, by means of which every part of an engine or tender

[047-031]corresponded with that of every other engine or tender of the same

[047-032]class, was as great an improvement as Maudslay's system of uniformity

[047-033]of parts in other descriptions of machinery.

[047-034]In connection with the subject of railways, we may allude in passing to

[047-035]Mr. Roberts's invention of the [[punching-machine|Jacquard punching machine]]--a self-acting

[047-036]tool of great power, used for punching any required number of holes, of

[047-037]any pitch and to any pattern, with mathematical accuracy, in bridge or

[047-038]boiler plates.  The origin of this invention was somewhat similar to

[047-039]that of the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]].  The contractors for the Conway Tubular

[047-040]Bridge while under construction, in 1848, were greatly hampered by

[047-041]combinations amongst the workmen, and they despaired of being able to

[047-042]finish the girders within the time specified in the contract.  The

[047-043]punching of the iron plates by hand was a tedious and expensive as well

[047-044]as an inaccurate process; and the work was proceeding so slowly that

[047-045]the contractors found it absolutely necessary to adopt some new method

[047-046]of punching if they were to finish the work in time.  In their

[047-047]emergency they appealed to Mr. Roberts, and endeavoured to persuade him

[047-048]to take the matter up.  He at length consented to do so, and evolved

[047-049]the machine in question during his evening's leisure--for the most part

[047-050]while quietly sipping his tea.  The machine was produced, the

[047-051]contractors were enabled to proceed with the punching of the plates

[047-052]independent of the refractory men, and the work was executed with a

[047-053]despatch, accuracy, and excellence that would not otherwise have been

[047-054]possible.  Only a few years since Mr. Roberts added a useful companion

[047-055]to the [[punching-machine|Jacquard punching machine]], in his combined self-acting machine

[047-056]for shearing iron and punching both webs of angle or T iron

[047-057]simultaneously to any required pitch; though this machine, like others

[047-058]which have proceeded from his fertile brain, is ahead even of this

[047-059]fast-manufacturing age, and has not yet come into general use, but is

[047-060]certain to do so before many years have elapsed.

[047-061]These inventions were surely enough for one man to have accomplished;

[047-062]but we have not yet done.  The mere enumeration of his other inventions

[047-063]would occupy several pages.  We shall merely allude to a few of them.

[047-064]One was his Turret Clock, for which he obtained the medal at the Great

[047-065]Exhibition of 1851.  Another was his Prize Electro-Magnet of 1845.

[047-066]When this subject was first mentioned to him, he said he did not know

[047-067]anything of the theory or practice of electro-magnetism, but he would

[047-068]try and find out.  The result of his trying was that he won the prize

[047-069]for the most powerful electro-magnet:  one is placed in the museum at

[047-070]Peel Park, [[manchester|Manchester]], and another with the Scottish [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]],

[047-071][[edinburgh|Edinburgh]].  In 1846 he perfected an American invention for making

[047-072]cigars by machinery; enabling a boy, working one of his cigar-engines,

[047-073]to make as many as 5000 in a day.  In 1852 he patented improvements in

[047-074]the construction, propelling, and equipment of steamships, which have,

[047-075]we believe, been adopted to a certain extent by the [[admiralty|Admiralty]]; and a

[047-076]few years later, in 1855, we find him presenting the Secretary of War

[047-077]with plans of elongated rifle projectiles to be used in smooth-bore

[047-078]ordnance with a view to utilize the old-pattern gun.  His head, like

[047-079]many inventors of the time, being full of the mechanics of war, he went

[047-080]so far as to wait upon Louis Napoleon, and laid before him a plan by

[047-081]which Sebastopol was to be blown down.  In short, upon whatever subject

[047-082]he turned his mind, he left the impress of his inventive faculty.  If

[047-083]it was imperfect, he improved it; if incapable of improvement, and

[047-084]impracticable, he invented something entirely new, superseding it

[047-085]altogether.  But with all his inventive genius, in the exercise of

[047-086]which Mr. Roberts has so largely added to the productive power of the

[047-087]country, we regret to say that he is not gifted with the commercial

[047-088]faculty.  He has helped others in their difficulties, but forgotten

[047-089]himself.  Many have profited by his inventions, without even

[047-090]acknowledging the obligations which they owed to him.  They have used

[047-091]his brains and copied his tools, and the "sucked orange" is all but

[047-092]forgotten.  There may have been a want of worldly wisdom on his part,

[047-093]but it is lamentable to think that one of the most prolific and useful

[047-094]inventors of his time should in his old age be left to fight with

[047-095]poverty.

[047-096]Mr. Whitworth is another of the first-class tool-makers of Manchester

[047-097]who has turned to excellent account his training in the workshops of

[047-098]Maudslay and Clement.  He has carried fully out the system of

[047-099]uniformity in Screw Threads which they initiated; and he has still

[047-100]further improved the mechanism of the planing machine, enabling it to

[047-101]work both backwards and forwards by means of a screw and roller motion.

[047-102]His "Jim Crow Machine," so called from its peculiar motion in reversing

[047-103]itself and working both ways, is an extremely beautiful tool, adapted

[047-104]alike for horizontal, vertical, or angular motions.  The minute

[047-105]accuracy of Mr. Whitworth's machines is not the least of their merits;

[047-106]and nothing will satisfy him short of perfect truth.  At the meeting of

[047-107]the Institute of Mechanical Engineers at [[glasgow|Glasgow]] in 1856 he read a

[047-108]paper on the essential importance of possessing a true plane as a

[047-109]standard of reference in mechanical constructions, and he described

[047-110]elaborately the true method of securing it,--namely, by scraping,

[047-111]instead of by the ordinary process of grinding.  At the same meeting he

[047-112]exhibited a machine of his invention by which he stated that a

[047-113]difference of the millionth part of an inch in length could at once be

[047-114]detected.  He also there urged his favourite idea of uniformity, and

[047-115]proper gradations of size of parts, in all the various branches of the

[047-116]mechanical arts, as a chief means towards economy of production--a

[047-117]principle, as he showed, capable of very extensive application.  To

[047-118]show the progress of tools and machinery in his own time, Mr. Whitworth

[047-119]cited the fact that thirty years since the cost of labour for making a

[047-120]surface of cast-iron true--one of the most important operations in

[047-121]mechanics--by chipping and filing by the hand, was 12s. a square foot;

[047-122]whereas it is now done by the planing machine at a cost for labour of

[047-123]less than a penny.  Then in machinery, pieces of 74 reed

[047-124]printing-cotton cloth of 29 yards each could not be produced at less

[047-125]cost than 30s. 6d. per piece; whereas the same description is now sold

[047-126]for 3s. 9d.  Mr. Whitworth has been among the most effective workers in

[047-127]this field of improvement, his tools taking the first place in point of

[047-128]speed, accuracy, and finish of work, in which respects they challenge

[047-129]competition with the world.  Mr. Whitworth has of late years been

[047-130]applying himself with his accustomed ardour to the development of the

[047-131]powers of rifled guns and projectiles,--a branch of mechanical science

[047-132]in which he confessedly holds a foremost place, and in perfecting which

[047-133]he is still occupied.

[047-160]CHAPTER XV.

[047-161]JAMES NASMYTH.

[047-162]"By Hammer and Hand

[047-163]All Arts doth stand."

[047-164]Hammermen's Motto.

[047-165]The founder Of the Scotch family of Naesmyth is said to have derived

[047-166]his name from the following circumstance.  In the course of the feuds

[047-167]which raged for some time between the Scotch kings and their powerful

[047-168]subjects the Earls of Douglas, a rencontre took place one day on the

[047-169]outskirts of a Border village, when the king's adherents were worsted.

[047-170]One of them took refuge in the village smithy, where, hastily

[047-171]disguising himself, and donning a spare leathern apron, he pretended to

[047-172]be engaged in assisting the smith with his work, when a party of the

[047-173]Douglas followers rushed in.  They glanced at the pretended workman at

[047-174]the anvil, and observed him deliver a blow upon it so unskilfully that

[047-175]the hammer-shaft broke in his hand.  On this one of the Douglas men

[047-176]rushed at him, calling out, "Ye're nae smyth!" The assailed man seized

[047-177]his sword, which lay conveniently at hand, and defended himself so

[047-178]vigorously that he shortly killed his assailant, while the smith

[047-179]brained another with his hammer; and, a party of the king's men having

[047-180]come to their help, the rest were speedily overpowered.  The royal

[047-181]forces then rallied, and their temporary defeat was converted into a

[047-182]victory.  The king bestowed a grant of land on his follower "Nae

[047-183]Smyth," who assumed for his arms a sword between two hammers with

[047-184]broken shafts, and the motto "Non arte sed Marte," as if to disclaim

[047-185]the art of the Smith, in which he had failed, and to emphasize the

[047-186]superiority of the warrior.  Such is said to be the traditional origin

[047-187]of the family of Naesmyth of Posso in Peeblesshire, who continue to

[047-188]bear the same name and arms.
