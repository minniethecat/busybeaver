[035-001][4] A Letter to the Right Hon. Sir James Eyre, Lord Chief Justice of

[035-002]the Common Pleas, on the subject of the cause [[boulton-and-watt|Boulton and Watt]] v.

[035-003][[jonathan-hornblower|Hornblower]] and Maberly, for Infringement on Mr. Watt's Patent for an

[035-004]Improvement of the [[steam-engine|Steam Engine]].  By [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]], Engineer.  [[london|London]],

[035-005]1797.

[035-006][5] [[samuel-bentham|Sir Samuel Bentham]] and [[marc-isambard-brunel|Marc Isambard Brunel]] subsequently

[035-007]distinguished themselves by the invention of wood-working machinery,

[035-008]full accounts of which will be found in the Memoirs of the former by

[035-009]Lady Bentham, and in the Life of the latter by Mr. Beamish.

[035-010][6] "Record of the International Exhibition, 1862."  Practical

[035-011]Mechanic's Journal, 293.

[035-012][7] In this, as in other methods of employing power, the moderns had

[035-013]been anticipated by the ancients; and though hydraulic machinery is a

[035-014]comparatively recent invention in [[england|England]], it had long been in use

[035-015]abroad.  Thus we find in Dr. Bright's Travels in Lower Hungary a full

[035-016]description of the powerful hydraulic machinery invented by M. Holl,

[035-017]Chief Engineer of the Imperial Mines, which had been in use since the

[035-018]year 1749, in pumping water from a depth of 1800 feet, from the silver

[035-019]and gold mines of Schemnitz and Kremnitz.  A head of water was

[035-020]collected by forming a reservoir along the mountain side, from which it

[035-021]was conducted through water-tight cast-iron pipes erected

[035-022]perpendicularly in the mine-shaft.  [[About]] forty-five fathoms down, the

[035-023]water descending through the pipe was forced by the weight of the

[035-024]column above it into the bottom of a perpendicular cylinder, in which

[035-025]it raised a water-tight piston.  When forced up to a given point a

[035-026]self-acting stop-cock shut off the pressure of the descending column,

[035-027]while a self-acting valve enabled the water contained in the cylinder

[035-028]to be discharged, on which the piston again descended, and the process

[035-029]was repeated like the successive strokes of a steam-engine.  Pump-rods

[035-030]were attached to this hydraulic apparatus, which were carried to the

[035-031]bottom of the shaft, and each worked a pump at different levels,

[035-032]raising the water stage by stage to the level of the main adit.  The

[035-033]pumps of these three several stages each raised 1790 cubic feet of

[035-034]water from a depth of 600 feet in the hour.  The regular working of the

[035-035]machinery was aided by the employment of a balance-beam connected by a

[035-036]chain with the head of the large piston and pump-rods; and the whole of

[035-037]these powerful machines by means of three of which as much as 789,840

[035-038]gallons of water were pumped out of the mines every 24 hours--were set

[035-039]in operation and regulated merely by the turning of a stopcock.  It

[035-040]will be observed that the arrangement thus briefly described was

[035-041]equally applicable to the working of machinery of all kinds, cranes,

[035-042]&c., as well as pumps; and it will be noted that, notwithstanding the

[035-043]ingenuity of Bramah, Armstrong, and other eminent English mechanics,

[035-044]the Austrian engineer Holl was thus decidedly beforehand with them in

[035-045]the practical application of the principles of hydrostatics.

[035-046][8] Dr. Brown published a brief memoir of his friend in the New Monthly

[035-047]Magazine for April, 1815, which has been the foundation of all the

[035-048]notices of Bramah's life that have heretofore appeared.

[035-049][9] Notwithstanding his well-known religious character, Bramah seems to

[035-050]have fallen under the grievous displeasure of William Huntington, S.S.

[035-051](Sinner Saved), described by Macaulay in his youth as "a worthless ugly

[035-052]lad of the name of Hunter," and in his manhood as "that remarkable

[035-053]impostor" (Essays, 1 vol. ed. 529).  It seems that Huntington sought

[035-054]the professional services of Bramah when re-edifying his chapel in

[035-055]1793; and at the conclusion of the work, the engineer generously sent

[035-056]the preacher a cheque for 8L. towards defraying the necessary expenses.

[035-057]Whether the sum was less than Huntington expected, or from whatever

[035-058]cause, the S.S.  contemptuously flung back the gift, as proceeding from

[035-059]an Arian whose religion was "unsavoury," at the same time hurling at

[035-060]the giver a number of texts conveying epithets of an offensive

[035-061]character.  Bramah replied to the farrago of nonsense, which he

[035-062]characterised as "unmannerly, absurd, and illiterate that it must have

[035-063]been composed when the writer was intoxicated, mad, or under the

[035-064]influence of Lucifer," and he threatened that unless Huntington

[035-065]apologised for his gratuitous insults, he (Bramah) would assuredly

[035-066]expose him.  The mechanician nevertheless proceeded gravely to explain

[035-067]and defend his "profession of faith," which was altogether unnecessary.

[035-068]On this Huntington returned to the charge, and directed against the

[035-069]mechanic a fresh volley of Scripture texts and phraseology, not without

[035-070]humour, if profanity be allowable in controversy, as where he says,

[035-071]"Poor man!  he makes a good patent lock, but cuts a sad figure with the

[035-072]keys of the Kingdom of Heaven!"  "What Mr. Bramah is," says S.S., "In

[035-073]respect to his character or conduct in life, as a man, a tradesman, a

[035-074]neighbour, a gentleman, a husband, friend, master, or subject, I know

[035-075]not.  In all these characters he may shine as a comet for aught I know;

[035-076]but he appears to me to be as far from any resemblance to a poor

[035-077]penitent or broken-hearted sinner as Jannes, Jambres, or Alexander the

[035-078]coppersmith!"  Bramah rejoined by threatening to publish his

[035-079]assailant's letters, but Huntington anticipated him in A Feeble Dispute

[035-080]with a Wise and Learned Man, 8vo.  [[london|London]], 1793, in which, whether

[035-081]justly or not, Huntington makes Bramah appear to murder the king's

[035-082]English in the most barbarous manner.

[035-083]CHAPTER XII.

[035-084]HENRY MAUDSLAY.

[035-085]"The successful construction of all machinery depends on the perfection

[035-086]of the tools employed; and whoever is a master in the arts of

[035-087]tool-making possesses the key to the construction of all machines.....

[035-088]The contrivance and construction of tools must therefore ever stand at

[035-089]the head of the industrial arts."--C. BABBAGE, Exposition of 1851.

[035-090][[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] was born at [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]] towards the end of last century, in

[035-091]a house standing in the court at the back of the Salutation Inn, the

[035-092]entrance to which is nearly opposite the Arsenal gates.  His father was

[035-093]a native of [[lancashire|Lancashire]], descended from an old family of the same name,

[035-094]the head of which resided at Mawdsley Hall near Ormskirk at the

[035-095]beginning of the seventeenth century.  The family were afterwards

[035-096]scattered, and several of its members became workmen.  William

[035-097]Maudslay, the father of Henry, belonged to the neighbourhood of [[bolton|Bolton]],

[035-098]where he was brought up to the trade of a joiner.  His principal

[035-099]employment, while working at his trade in [[lancashire|Lancashire]], consisted in

[035-100]making the wood framing of cotton machinery, in the construction of

[035-101]which cast-iron had not yet been introduced.  Having got into some

[035-102]trouble in his neighbourhood, through some alleged LIAISON, William

[035-103]enlisted in the Royal Artillery, and the corps to which he belonged was

[035-104]shortly after sent out to the West Indies.  He was several times

[035-105]engaged in battle, and in his last action he was hit by a musket-bullet

[035-106]in the throat.  The soldier's stock which he wore had a piece cut out

[035-107]of it by the ball, the direction of which was diverted, and though

[035-108]severely wounded, his life was saved.  He brought home the stock and

[035-109]preserved it as a relic, afterwards leaving it to his son.  Long after,

[035-110]the son would point to the stock, hung up against his wall, and say

[035-111]"But for that bit of leather there would have been no [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]."

[035-112]The wounded artilleryman was invalided and sent home to [[woolwich|Woolwich]], the

[035-113]headquarters of his corps, where he was shortly after discharged.

[035-114]Being a handy workman, he sought and obtained employment at the

[035-115]Arsenal.  He was afterwards appointed a storekeeper in the Dockyard.

[035-116]It was during the former stage of William Maudslay's employment at

[035-117][[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]], that the subject of this memoir was born in the house in the

[035-118]court above mentioned, on the 22nd of August, 1771.

[035-119]The boy was early set to work.  When twelve years old he was employed

[035-120]as a "powder-monkey," in making and filling cartridges.  After two

[035-121]years, he was passed on to the carpenter's shop where his father

[035-122]worked, and there he became acquainted with tools and the art of

[035-123]working in wood and iron.  From the first, the latter seems to have had

[035-124]by far the greatest charms for him.  The blacksmiths' shop was close to

[035-125]the carpenters', and Harry seized every opportunity that offered of

[035-126]plying the hammer, the file, and the chisel, in preference to the saw

[035-127]and the plane.  Many a cuff did the foreman of carpenters give him for

[035-128]absenting himself from his proper shop and stealing off to the smithy.

[035-129]His propensity was indeed so strong that, at the end of a year, it was

[035-130]thought better, as he was a handy, clever boy, to yield to his earnest

[035-131]desire to be placed in the smithy, and he was removed thither

[035-132]accordingly in his fifteenth year.

[035-133]His heart being now in his work, he made rapid progress, and soon

[035-134]became an expert smith and metal worker.  He displayed his skill

[035-135]especially in forging light ironwork; and a favourite job of his was

[035-136]the making of "Trivets" out of the solid, which only the "dab hands" of

[035-137]the shop could do, but which he threw off with great rapidity in first

[035-138]rate style.  These "Trivets" were made out of Spanish iron bolts--rare

[035-139]stuff, which, though exceedingly tough, forged like wax under the

[035-140]hammer.  Even at the close of his life, when he had acquired eminent

[035-141]distinction as an inventor, and was a large employer of skilled labour,

[035-142]he looked back with pride to the forging of his early days in Woolwich

[035-143]Arsenal.  He used to describe with much gusto, how the old experienced

[035-144]hands, with whom he was a great favourite, would crowd about him when

[035-145]forging his "Trivets," some of which may to this day be in use among

[035-146]Woolwich housewives for supporting the toast-plate before the bright

[035-147]fire against tea time.  This was, however, entirely contraband work,

[035-148]done "on the sly," and strictly prohibited by the superintending

[035-149]officer, who used kindly to signal his approach by blowing his nose in

[035-150]a peculiar manner, so that all forbidden jobs might be put out of the

[035-151]way by the time he entered the shop.

[035-152]We have referred to Maudslay's early dexterity in trivet-making--a

[035-153]circumstance trifling enough in itself--for the purpose of illustrating

[035-154]the progress which he had made in a branch of his art of the greatest

[035-155]importance in tool and machine making.  Nothing pleased him more in his

[035-156]after life than to be set to work upon an unusual piece of forging, and

[035-157]to overcome, as none could do so cleverly as he, the difficulties which

[035-158]it presented.  The pride of art was as strong in him as it must have

[035-159]been in the mediaeval smiths, who turned out those beautiful pieces of

[035-160]workmanship still regarded as the pride of our cathedrals and old

[035-161]mansions.  In Maudslay's case, his dexterity as a smith was eventually

[035-162]directed to machinery, rather than ornamental work; though, had the

[035-163]latter been his line of labour, we do not doubt that he would have

[035-164]reached the highest distinction.

[035-165]The manual skill which our young blacksmith had acquired was such as to

[035-166]give him considerable reputation in his craft, and he was spoken of

[035-167]even in the London shops as one of the most dexterous hands in the

[035-168]trade.  It was this circumstance that shortly after led to his removal

[035-169]from the smithy in [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich Arsenal]] to a sphere more suitable for the

[035-170]development of his mechanical ability.

[035-171]We have already stated in the preceding memoir, that [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] took

[035-172]out the first patent for his lock in 1784, and a second for its

[035-173]improvement several years later; but notwithstanding the acknowledged

[035-174]superiority of the new lock over all others, Bramah experienced the

[035-175]greatest difficulty in getting it manufactured with sufficient

[035-176]precision, and at such a price as to render it an article of extensive

[035-177]commerce.  This arose from the generally inferior character of the

[035-178]workmanship of that day, as well as the clumsiness and uncertainty of

[035-179]the tools then in use.  Bramah found that even the best manual

[035-180]dexterity was not to be trusted, and yet it seemed to be his only

[035-181]resource; for [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] of a superior kind had not yet been

[035-182]invented.  In this dilemma he determined to consult an ingenious old

[035-183]German artisan, then working with William Moodie, a general blacksmith

[035-184]in Whitechapel.  This German was reckoned one of the most ingenious

[035-185]workmen in London at the time.  Bramah had several long interviews with

[035-186]him, with the object of endeavouring to solve the difficult problem of

[035-187]how to secure precise workmanship in lock-making.  But they could not

[035-188]solve it; they saw that without better tools the difficulty was

[035-189]insuperable; and then Bramah began to fear that his lock would remain a

[035-190]mere mechanical curiosity, and be prevented from coming into general

[035-191]use.
