# Henry Maudslay

Apprenticed to [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] as a blacksmith, Henry Maudslay became the greatest machine tool builder of his age. His slide rest (c. 1798) mechanically constrained the cutting tool to move with geometric accuracy. His [[screw-cutting|screw-cutting lathe]] made interchangeable threaded fasteners possible. Maudslay's [[lambeth|Lambeth]] works trained Whitworth, Nasmyth, and Roberts. (032-033)

## Early Life and Career

Henry Maudslay was born in 1771 at [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]], where his father worked as a wheelwright in the [[woolwich-arsenal|Royal Arsenal]]. At age twelve he joined the [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich Arsenal]] as a "powder monkey," filling cartridges. His mechanical aptitude was already evident: he built a model forge from scraps and learned smithing from the arsenal blacksmiths.

At eighteen, Maudslay sought a position with [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]], the leading [[london|London]] engineer. Bramah was impressed by the young man's skill with a file and hired him as a journeyman blacksmith at his Pimlico works. Maudslay quickly rose to become Bramah's chief mechanic, building the special machinery that produced Bramah's celebrated lock. (032-024)

In 1797, after eight years with Bramah, Maudslay left to establish his own workshop. The break was partly over salary — Bramah refused to raise Maudslay's wages above thirty shillings a week — and partly over ambition. Maudslay rented a small smithy in Wells Street and began independent work.

## Major Contributions

Maudslay's defining contribution was the [[slide-rest|slide rest]], developed around 1798. Before it, metal turning was done with hand-held tools; the turner controlled the cut by feel alone. The slide rest constrained the tool to a geometrically perfect path using a mechanical slide on accurately fitted flat surfaces.

It "has done more for the mechanical arts than any invention since the printing-press." (037-158)

His second great contribution was the [[screw-cutting-lathe|screw-cutting lathe]]. By connecting the slide rest's feed to the lathe spindle through a leadscrew and change gears, Maudslay made a lathe that cut screw threads of any pitch with mathematical precision. This enabled interchangeable threaded fasteners on which modern machinery depends.

Maudslay also pioneered true flat surfaces as the basis of accurate machine construction. He developed the technique of scraping two surfaces together with marking compound, removing high spots until they matched perfectly. This method of creating flat surfaces by hand scraping was the foundation of precision engineering for a century.

## Legacy

Maudslay's Lambeth works became the training ground for a generation of machine tool builders. [[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]] learned the importance of true planes and precise measurement, then established the Whitworth thread standard. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] absorbed Maudslay's method of systematic design and later invented the [[steam-hammer|steam hammer]].

[[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]] applied Maudslay's principles to textile machinery and the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]]. His influence extended beyond his pupils: every machine shop in the 19th century operated on principles established at Lambeth. Henry Maudslay died in 1831, leaving a firm that continued as [[maudslay-sons-and-field|Maudslay, Sons and Field]], marine engineers of distinction.
