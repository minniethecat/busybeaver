Iron shipbuilding was the revolutionary practice of constructing ocean-going vessels with [[wrought-iron]] hulls and frames instead of timber.

First undertaken on a commercial scale in the 1830s, iron construction transformed shipbuilding from a traditional craft into a branch of [[structural-engineering]], requiring precise knowledge of the strength of materials, the behaviour of riveted joints, and the distribution of stress throughout a hollow beam-like structure.

[[William Fairbairn]] of [[Manchester]] was the central figure in this transformation: his experiments on the [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]] Canal]], his establishment of the first large iron shipbuilding yard at Millwall on the [[thames|Thames]], and his systematic investigations into the properties of iron plates and riveted connections laid the empirical foundation upon which the Victorian iron shipbuilding industry was built.

## Origins: From Wood to Iron

The idea of building ships of iron rather than timber was initially met with widespread scepticism. Wooden ships had carried the world's trade for millennia; they were well understood, their materials were abundant, and the skills for their construction were widely distributed. Iron, by contrast, was heavier than water—a fact that seemed to many a fatal objection to its use for hulls. Early experiments with iron vessels, such as the canal lighter *Trial* built by [[john-wilkinson|John Wilkinson]] at [[Coalbrookdale]] in 1787, were regarded as curiosities rather than harbingers of a new technology.

The first serious commercial application of iron to shipbuilding came in the 1820s, when light iron boats were built for service on canals and inland waterways. The breakthrough was prompted, paradoxically, by the apparent failure of Mr. Houston's experiments on the Ardrossan Canal in 1829. Houston discovered, to his puzzlement, that the horses towing his experimental light boats "had to perform much greater labour at six or seven, than at nine miles an hour." (055-155) The anomaly prompted the Council of the [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and Clyde Canal]] to invite [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]—already known as a "scientific mechanic"—to investigate the law of traction.

## Fairbairn and the Industrial Breakthrough

Fairbairn's canal experiments, conducted over a series of years at a cost of several thousand pounds, began with wooden vessels but "eventually led to the construction of iron vessels upon a large scale and on an entirely new principle of construction, with angle iron ribs and wrought-iron sheathing plates." (055-171) The results "had the effect of specially directing the attention of naval engineers to the employment of iron in ship building." (055-175)

In 1831, Fairbairn constructed an iron vessel at his works in [[manchester|Manchester]], which "went to sea the same year." Its success was immediate and convincing. Shortly afterwards he began iron shipbuilding on a large scale, at approximately the same time as the Messrs. Laird commenced similar work at Birkenhead. (055-178) In 1835, Fairbairn established extensive works at Millwall on the [[thames|Thames]]—a site later occupied by Scott Russell, in whose yard the celebrated *Great Eastern* steamship was erected. (055-182)

> "In the course of some fourteen years he built upwards of a hundred and twenty iron ships, some of them above 2000 tons burden. It was in fact the first great iron shipbuilding yard in Britain, and led the way in a branch of business which has since become of first-rate magnitude and importance." (055-185)

## Construction Methods: Riveting and Boilers

The transition from timber to iron construction demanded new manufacturing processes as well as new design principles. The traditional method of riveting—two men with hand-hammers, working on opposite sides of a plate—was "tedious and expensive, as well as clumsy and imperfect." [[About]] 1839, Fairbairn invented a machine for riveting boiler plates by steam power, enabling the rivet to be "driven into its place and firmly fastened there by a couple of strokes." (056-001) Combined with [[Richard Roberts]]'s Jacquard punching-machine, the [[riveting-machine|steam riveter]] reduced the fastening of the largest iron plates to "one of the simplest operations in iron-manufacturing." (056-011)

These practical innovations were underpinned by a sustained programme of experimental research. Fairbairn investigated the strength of different kinds of riveted joints compared with the solid plate, the distribution of material throughout the structure of the vessel, and the optimal form of the hull itself. "It would indeed be difficult to over-estimate the value of his investigations on these points in the earlier stages of this now highly important branch of the national industry." (055-189)

## From Ships to Bridges: The [[tubular-bridge|Tubular Bridge]]s

Fairbairn's knowledge of the strength of [[wrought-iron]] "in the form of the hollow beam (which a wrought-iron ship really is)" led directly to his involvement in one of the great civil-engineering projects of the Victorian age. [[Robert Stephenson]] consulted Fairbairn on the structures needed to span the estuary of the Conway and the Menai Strait, and the result was the [[Britannia Bridge 1850|Conway and Britannia Tubular Bridges]]. Smiles concludes that "by far the largest share of the merit of working out the practical details of those structures, and thus realizing [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]]'s magnificent idea of the tubular bridge, belongs to Mr. Fairbairn." (056-014)

## Legacy

By the 1860s, iron had become the standard material for ocean-going merchant vessels and warships alike, and the skills developed in the iron shipyards—riveting, plate-forming, stress analysis—had transferred to bridge-building, boiler-making, and structural engineering. The Millwall yard that Fairbairn had founded became one of the nurseries of the industry, and the experimental methods he introduced—testing materials to destruction, measuring stress under load, publishing the results openly—established a pattern of scientific engineering that became characteristic of British practice throughout the Victorian period.
