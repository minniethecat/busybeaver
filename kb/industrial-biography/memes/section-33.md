[033-001]Joseph might have remained a ploughman for life, but for an accident

[033-002]which happened to his right ankle at the age of 16, which unfitted him

[033-003]for farm-work.  While confined at home disabled he spent his time in

[033-004]carving and making things in wood; and then it occurred to him that,

[033-005]though he could not now be a ploughman, he might be a mechanic.  When

[033-006]sufficiently recovered, he was accordingly put apprentice to one

[033-007]Allott, the village carpenter, under whom he soon became an expert

[033-008]workman.  He could make ploughs, window-frames, or fiddles, with equal

[033-009]dexterity.  He also made violoncellos, and was so fortunate as to sell

[033-010]one of his making for three guineas, which is still reckoned a good

[033-011]instrument.  He doubtless felt within him the promptings of ambition,

[033-012]such as every good workman feels, and at all events entertained the

[033-013]desire of rising in his trade.  When his time was out, he accordingly

[033-014]resolved to seek work in [[london|London]], whither he made the journey on foot.

[033-015]He soon found work at a cabinet-maker's, and remained with him for some

[033-016]time, after which he set up business in a very small way on his own

[033-017]account.  An accident which happened to him in the course of his daily

[033-018]work, again proved his helper, by affording him a degree of leisure

[033-019]which he at once proceeded to turn to some useful account.  Part of his

[033-020]business consisted in putting up [[water-closet|water-closet]]s, after a method invented

[033-021]or improved by a Mr. Allen; but the article was still very imperfect;

[033-022]and Bramah had long resolved that if he could only secure some leisure

[033-023]for the purpose, he would contrive something that should supersede it

[033-024]altogether.  A severe fall which occurred to him in the course of his

[033-025]business, and laid him up, though very much against his will, now

[033-026]afforded him the leisure which he desired, and he proceeded to make his

[033-027]proposed invention.  He took out a patent for it in 1778, describing

[033-028]himself in the specification as "of Cross Court, Carnaby Market [Golden

[033-029]Square], Middlesex, Cabinet Maker."  He afterwards removed to a shop in

[033-030]Denmark Street, St.  Giles's, and while there he made a further

[033-031]improvement in his invention by the addition of a water cock, which he

[033-032]patented in 1783.  The merits of the machine were generally recognised,

[033-033]and before long it came into extensive use, continuing to be employed,

[033-034]with but few alterations, until the present day.  His circumstances

[033-035]improving with the increased use of his invention, Bramah proceeded to

[033-036]undertake the manufacture of the pumps, pipes, &c., required for its

[033-037]construction; and, remembering his friend the [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] blacksmith, who

[033-038]had made his first tools for him out of the old files and razor-blades,

[033-039]he sent for him to [[london|London]] to take charge of his blacksmith's

[033-040]department, in which he proved a most useful assistant.  As usual, the

[033-041]patent was attacked by pirates so soon as it became productive, and

[033-042]Bramah was under the necessity, on more than one occasion, of defending

[033-043]his property in the invention, in which he was completely successful.

[033-044]We next find Bramah turning his attention to the invention of a lock

[033-045]that should surpass all others then known.  The locks then in use were

[033-046]of a very imperfect character, easily picked by dexterous thieves,

[033-047]against whom they afforded little protection.  Yet locks are a very

[033-048]ancient invention, though, as in many other cases, the art of making

[033-049]them seems in a great measure to have become lost, and accordingly had

[033-050]to be found out anew.  Thus the tumbler lock--which consists in the use

[033-051]of moveable impediments acted on by the proper key only, as

[033-052]contradistinguished from the ordinary ward locks, where the impediments

[033-053]are fixed--appears to have been well known to the ancient Egyptians,

[033-054]the representation of such a lock being found sculptured among the

[033-055]bas-reliefs which decorate the great temple at Karnak.  This kind of

[033-056]lock was revived, or at least greatly improved, by a Mr. Barron in

[033-057]1774, and it was shortly after this time that Bramah directed his

[033-058]attention to the subject.  After much study and many experiments, he

[033-059]contrived a lock more simple, more serviceable, as well as more secure,

[033-060]than Barron's, as is proved by the fact that it has stood the test of

[033-061]nearly eighty years' experience,[1] and still holds its ground.  For a

[033-062]long time, indeed, Bramah's lock was regarded as absolutely inviolable,

[033-063]and it remained unpicked for sixty-seven years until Hobbs the American

[033-064]mastered it in 1851.  A notice had long been exhibited in Bramah's

[033-065]shop-window in Piccadilly, offering 200L. to any one who should succeed

[033-066]in picking the patent lock.  Many tried, and all failed, until Hobbs

[033-067]succeeded, after sixteen days' manipulation of it with various

[033-068]elaborate instruments.  But the difficulty with which the lock was

[033-069]picked showed that, for all ordinary purposes, it might be pronounced

[033-070]impregnable.

[033-071]The new locks were machines of the most delicate kind, the action of

[033-072]which depended in a great measure upon the precision with which the

[033-073]springs, sliders, levers, barrels, and other parts were finished.  The

[033-074]merits of the invention being generally admitted, there was a

[033-075]considerable demand for the locks, and the necessity thus arose for

[033-076]inventing a series of original [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] to enable them to be

[033-077]manufactured in sufficient quantities to meet the demand.  It is

[033-078]probable, indeed, that, but for the contrivance of such tools, the lock

[033-079]could never have come in to general use, as the skill of hand-workmen,

[033-080]no matter how experienced, could not have been relied upon for turning

[033-081]out the article with that degree of accuracy and finish in all the

[033-082]parts which was indispensable for its proper action.  In conducting the

[033-083]manufacture throughout, Bramah was greatly assisted by [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]],

[033-084]his foreman, to whom he was in no small degree indebted for the

[033-085]contrivance of those tool-machines which enabled him to carry on the

[033-086]business of lock-making with advantage and profit.

[033-087]Bramah's indefatigable spirit of invention was only stimulated to fresh

[033-088]efforts by the success of his lock; and in the course of a few years we

[033-089]find him entering upon a more important and original line of action

[033-090]than he had yet ventured on.  His patent of 1785 shows the direction of

[033-091]his studies.  Watt had invented his [[steam-engine]], which was coming into

[033-092]general use; and the creation of motive-power in various other forms

[033-093]became a favourite subject of inquiry with inventors.  Bramah's first

[033-094]invention with this object was his Hydrostatic Machine, founded on the

[033-095]doctrine of the equilibrium of pressure in fluids, as exhibited in the

[033-096]well known 'hydrostatic paradox.' In his patent of 1785, in which he no

[033-097]longer describes himself as Cabinet maker, but 'Engine maker' of

[033-098]Piccadilly, he indicated many inventions, though none of them came into

[033-099]practical use,--such as a Hydrostatical Machine and Boiler, and the

[033-100]application of the power produced by them to the drawing of carriages,

[033-101]and the propelling of ships by a paddle-wheel fixed in the stern of the

[033-102]vessel, of which drawings are annexed to the specification; but it was

[033-103]not until 1795 that he patented his Hydrostatic or [[hydraulic-press|Hydraulic Press]].

[033-104]Though the principle on which the [[hydraulic-press|Hydraulic Press]] is founded had long

[033-105]been known, and formed the subject of much curious speculation, it

[033-106]remained unproductive of results until a comparatively recent period,

[033-107]when the idea occurred of applying it to mechanical purposes.  A

[033-108]machine of the kind was indeed proposed by Pascal, the eminent

[033-109]philosopher, in 1664, but more than a century elapsed before the

[033-110]difficulties in the way of its construction were satisfactorily

[033-111]overcome.  Bramah's machine consists of a large and massive cylinder,

[033-112]in which there works an accurately-fitted solid piston or plunger.  A

[033-113]forcing-pump of very small bore communicates with the bottom of the

[033-114]cylinder, and by the action of the pump-handle or lever, exceeding

[033-115]small quantities of water are forced in succession beneath the piston

[033-116]in the large cylinder, thus gradually raising it up, and compressing

[033-117]bodies whose bulk or volume it is intended to reduce.  Hence it is most

[033-118]commonly used as a packing-press, being superior to every other

[033-119]contrivance of the kind that has yet been invented; and though

[033-120]exercising a prodigious force, it is so easily managed that a boy can

[033-121]work it.  The machine has been employed on many extraordinary occasions

[033-122]in preference to other methods of applying power.  Thus Robert

[033-123]Stephenson used it to hoist the gigantic tubes of the [[tubular-bridge|Britannia Bridge]]

[033-124]into their bed,[2] and Brunel to launch the Great Eastern steamship

[033-125]from her cradles.  It has also been used to cut bars of iron, to draw

[033-126]the piles driven in forming coffer dams, and to wrench up trees by the

[033-127]roots, all of which feats it accomplishes with comparative ease.

[033-128]The principal difficulty experienced in constructing the hydraulic

[033-129]press before the time of Bramah arose from the tremendous pressure

[033-130]exercised by the pump, which forced the water through between the solid

[033-131]piston and the side of the cylinder in which it worked in such

[033-132]quantities as to render the press useless for practical purposes.

[033-133]Bramah himself was at first completely baffled by this difficulty.  It

[033-134]will be observed that the problem was to secure a joint sufficiently

[033-135]free to let the piston slide up through it, and at the same time so

[033-136]water-tight as to withstand the internal force of the pump.  These two

[033-137]conditions seemed so conflicting that Bramah was almost at his wit's

[033-138]end, and for a time despaired of being able to bring the machine to a

[033-139]state of practical efficiency.  None but those who have occupied

[033-140]themselves in the laborious and often profitless task of helping the

[033-141]world to new and useful machines can have any idea of the tantalizing

[033-142]anxiety which arises from the apparently petty stumbling-blocks which

[033-143]for awhile impede the realization of a great idea in mechanical

[033-144]invention.  Such was the case with the water-tight arrangement in the

[033-145]hydraulic press.  In his early experiments, Bramah tried the expedient

[033-146]of the ordinary stuffing-box for the purpose of securing the required

[033-147]water tightness' That is, a coil of hemp on leather washers was placed

[033-148]in a recess, so as to fit tightly round the moving ram or piston, and

[033-149]it was further held in its place by means of a compressing collar

[033-150]forced hard down by strong screws.  The defect of this arrangement was,

[033-151]that, even supposing the packing could be made sufficiently tight to

[033-152]resist the passage of the water urged by the tremendous pressure from

[033-153]beneath, such was the grip which the compressed material took of the

[033-154]ram of the press, that it could not be got to return down after the

[033-155]water pressure had been removed.

[033-156]In this dilemma, Bramah's ever-ready workman, [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], came to

[033-157]his rescue.  The happy idea occurred to him of employing the pressure

[033-158]of the water itself to give the requisite water-tightness to the

[033-159]collar.  It was a flash of common-sense genius--beautiful through its

[033-160]very simplicity.  The result was Maudslay's self-tightening collar, the

[033-161]action of which a few words of description will render easily

[033-162]intelligible.  A collar of sound leather, the convex side upwards and

[033-163]the concave downwards, was fitted into the recess turned out in the

[033-164]neck of the press-cylinder, at the place formerly used as a

[033-165]stuffing-box.  Immediately on the high pressure water being turned on,

[033-166]it forced its way into the leathern concavity and 'flapped out' the

[033-167]bent edges of the collar; and, in so doing, caused the leather to apply

[033-168]itself to the surface of the rising ram with a degree of closeness and

[033-169]tightness so as to seal up the joint the closer exactly in proportion

[033-170]to the pressure of the water in its tendency to escape.  On the other

[033-171]hand, the moment the  pressure was let off and the ram desired to

[033-172]return, the collar collapsed and the ram slid gently down, perfectly

[033-173]free and yet perfectly water-tight.  Thus, the former tendency of the

[033-174]water to escape by the side of the piston was by this most simple and

[033-175]elegant self-adjusting contrivance made instrumental to the perfectly

[033-176]efficient action of the machine; and from the moment of its invention

[033-177]the hydraulic press took its place as one of the grandest agents for

[033-178]exercising power in a concentrated and tranquil form.
