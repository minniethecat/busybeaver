Richard Trevithick (1771–1833) was a Cornish [[engineer]] and [[inventor]] who pioneered the use of high-pressure steam. He built the first railway [[locomotive|steam locomotive]] that successfully ran on rails, constructed for [[samuel-homfray|Mr. Homfray]]'s ironworks in 1803, and his double-cylinder engine designs directly influenced later locomotive builders including [[George Stephenson]] and [[Matthew Murray]]. Smiles places Trevithick in the direct inventive lineage from Cugnot and [[william-murdock|Murdock]] to the Stephensons. (031-159)

## Early Life

Trevithick was born in 1771 at Illogan, [[cornwall|Cornwall]], the heart of Britain's copper and tin mining district. His father was a mine manager, or "captain," and the young Trevithick grew up surrounded by the massive beam engines that pumped the Cornish mines — almost all of them of the [[Newcomen engine|Newcomen]] and later [[James Watt|Watt]] types. His early intimate knowledge of mine engineering shaped his lifelong commitment to improving the steam engine.

## Career and Inventions

### High-Pressure Steam

Trevithick's great departure from the engine-building orthodoxy of his day was his championing of high-pressure steam. While [[James Watt]] had always insisted on low-pressure operation — fearing boiler explosions — Trevithick saw that higher pressures could make engines smaller, lighter, and more powerful. His "strong steam" engines dispensed with the massive beam and separate condenser, using steam pressure directly against the piston.

### The First Railway [[locomotive|Locomotive]]

In 1803, Trevithick constructed a [[locomotive|steam locomotive]] for [[samuel-homfray|Mr. Homfray]]'s ironworks at Penydarren, [[south-wales|South Wales]]. This was the first locomotive to run on a railway, successfully hauling a load of ten tons of iron and seventy men over a distance of nine miles. Unfortunately, the cast-iron tram-plates of the day proved too weak to bear the engine's weight, and the locomotive was withdrawn from service. Nevertheless, Trevithick had demonstrated the fundamental feasibility of steam railway traction. (024-108) (024-109) (024-110) (024-111)

> "The first locomotive run upon a railroad was that constructed by Trevithick for [[samuel-homfray|Mr. Homfray]] in 1803, which was employed to bring down metal from the furnaces to the Old Forge. The engine was taken off the road because the tram-plates were found too weak to bear its weight without breaking." (024-108) (024-110) (024-111)

### Influence on Later Engineers

Trevithick's influence on subsequent locomotive design was substantial. [[Matthew Murray]] of [[leeds|Leeds]] improved upon Trevithick's design by introducing the double cylinder — Trevithick's engine had only one cylinder, the defects of which Murray supplemented with a fly-wheel to carry the crank over the dead points. The double-cylinder arrangement became standard for all later locomotives. (045-165) (045-171) (045-172)

The blast-pipe, a crucial device for increasing locomotive boiler efficiency by directing exhaust steam up the chimney, was claimed for Trevithick as well as for [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]], Goldsworthy Gurney, and Timothy Hackworth — a disputed priority that reflects the intense collaborative competition of early locomotive development. (031-129) (031-144)

## Influence and Legacy

Trevithick's career did not bring him lasting wealth. He died in poverty at Dartford in 1833, working to the last on new inventions. Yet his contributions to the steam engine and to railway locomotion were fundamental. The lineage Smiles traces — "Cugnot, Murdock, and Trevithick to the Stephensons" — rightly places him as a key transitional figure between the low-pressure beam engines of the eighteenth century and the high-pressure locomotives that powered the railway age. (031-159)
