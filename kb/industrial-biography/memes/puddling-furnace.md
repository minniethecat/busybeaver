# Puddling Furnace

The [[puddling-process|[[puddling-process|puddling]] furnace]] is a reverberatory furnace for converting [[pig-iron|pig iron]] into [[wrought-iron|wrought iron]] by stirring the molten metal on a sand-lined hearth.

The metal is exposed to an oxidising flame that burns off carbon and other impurities. Patented by [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] in 1784, it replaced the charcoal-fired finery and made possible the mass production of [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] using coal fuel.

## Background

Before Cort's innovation, converting pig iron into wrought iron required charcoal, which was increasingly scarce and expensive.

Several ironmasters had attempted coal-based alternatives: [[peter-onions|Peter Onions]] patented a furnace in 1783 in which the metal was stirred on an air-blown hearth, and the [[cranege-brothers|Cranege brothers]] had earlier dispensed with the blast altogether. (021-156)

Cort's puddling furnace separated the fuel from the metal — the fire burned in a separate chamber and the flame reflected from the arched roof onto the iron below. This prevented the sulphurous coal fumes from contaminating the metal, a problem that had defeated earlier attempts.

## The [[puddling-process|Puddling Process]]

The workman charged the furnace with pig iron and, as it melted, stirred the metal with a long bar through a working door. The stirring exposed fresh surfaces of molten iron to the oxidising flame. As carbon burned off, the metal became less fluid and "thickened into a kind of froth, which the workman, by opening the door, must turn and stir with a bar." (021-144)

As the stirring continued, the iron particles began to adhere into a pasty mass. The workman collected these into lumps or "puddle balls," which were then raised to a white heat and forged at the [[forge-hammer|forge hammer]] into malleable [[wrought-iron|bar iron]]. (021-150)

## Significance

Cort's puddling process, combined with his [[grooved-rollers|grooved rolling mill]] (patented 1783), established "quite a new era in the history of the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]." (021-158)

Within a few years of 1786, British iron production rose dramatically, freeing the country from dependence on imported Swedish and Russian bar iron. The puddling furnace remained the dominant method of wrought iron production worldwide until well into the 20th century.
