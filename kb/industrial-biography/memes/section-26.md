[026-001]He first set up his crucible upon the bridge of the reverberatory

[026-002]furnace used for melting pig-iron, and filled it with a mixture

[026-003]carefully compounded according to the formula of the books; but,

[026-004]notwithstanding the shelter of a brick, placed before it to break the

[026-005]action of the flame, the crucible generally split in two, and not

[026-006]unfrequently melted and disappeared altogether.  To obtain better

[026-007]results if possible, he next had recourse to the ordinary smith's fire,

[026-008]carrying on his experiments in the evenings after office-hours.  He set

[026-009]his crucible upon the fire on a piece of fire brick, opposite the

[026-010]nozzle of the bellows; covering the whole with coke, and then exciting

[026-011]the flame by blowing.  This mode of operating produced somewhat better

[026-012]results, but still neither the iron nor the cinder obtained resembled

[026-013]the pig or scoria of the blast-furnace, which it was his ambition to

[026-014]imitate.  From the irregularity of the results, and the frequent

[026-015]failure of the crucibles, he came to the conclusion that either his

[026-016]furnace, or his mode of fluxing, was in fault, and he looked about him

[026-017]for a more convenient means of pursuing his experiments.  A small

[026-018]square furnace had been erected in the works for the purpose of heating

[026-019]the rivets used for the repair of [[steam-engine]] boilers; the furnace had

[026-020]for its chimney a cast-iron pipe six or seven inches in diameter and

[026-021]nine feet long.  After a few trials with it, he raised the heat to such

[026-022]an extent that the lower end of the pipe was melted off, without

[026-023]producing any very satisfactory results on the experimental crucible,

[026-024]and his operations were again brought to a standstill.  A chimney of

[026-025]brick having been substituted for the cast-iron pipe, he was, however,

[026-026]enabled to proceed with his trials.

[026-027]He continued to pursue his experiments in assaying for about two years,

[026-028]during which he had been working entirely after the methods described

[026-029]in books; but, feeling the results still unsatisfactory, he determined

[026-030]to borrow no more from the books, but to work out a system of his own,

[026-031]which should ensure results similar to those produced at the

[026-032]blast-furnace.  This he eventually succeeded in effecting by numerous

[026-033]experiments performed in the night; as his time was fully occupied by

[026-034]his office-duties during the day.  At length these patient experiments

[026-035]bore their due fruits.  [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] became the most skilled assayer at

[026-036]the works; and when a difficulty occurred in smelting a quantity of new

[026-037]ironstone which had been contracted for, the manager himself resorted

[026-038]to the bookkeeper for advice and information; and the skill and

[026-039]experience which he had gathered during his nightly labours, enabled

[026-040]him readily and satisfactorily to solve the difficulty and suggest a

[026-041]suitable remedy.  His reward for this achievement was the permission,

[026-042]which was immediately granted him by the manager, to make use of his

[026-043]own assay-furnace, in which he thenceforward continued his

[026-044]investigations, at the same time that he instructed the manager's son

[026-045]in the art of assaying.  This additional experience proved of great

[026-046]benefit to him; and he continued to prosecute his inquiries with much

[026-047]zeal, sometimes devoting entire nights to experiments in assaying,

[026-048]roasting and cementing iron-ores and ironstone, decarbonating cast-iron

[026-049]for steel and bar-iron, and various like operations.  His general

[026-050]practice, however, at that time was, to retire between two and three

[026-051]o'clock in the morning, leaving directions with the engine-man to call

[026-052]him at half-past five, so as to be present in the office at six.  But

[026-053]these praiseworthy experiments were brought to a sudden end, as thus

[026-054]described by himself:--

[026-055]"In the midst of my career of investigation," says he,[6] "and without

[026-056]a cause being assigned, I was stopped short.  My furnaces, at the order

[026-057]of the manager, were pulled in pieces, and an edict was passed that

[026-058]they should never be erected again.  Thus terminated my researches at

[026-059]the [[clyde-iron-works|[[clyde|Clyde]] Iron Works]].  It happened at a time when I was interested--and

[026-060]I had been two years previously occupied--in an attempt to convert

[026-061]cast-iron into steel, without fusion, by a process of cementation,

[026-062]which had for its object the dispersion or absorption of the

[026-063]superfluous carbon contained in the cast-iron,--an object which at that

[026-064]time appeared to me of so great importance, that, with the consent of a

[026-065]friend, I erected an assay and cementing Furnace at the distance of

[026-066]about two miles from the [[clyde|Clyde]] Works.  Thither I repaired at night, and

[026-067]sometimes at the breakfast and dinner hours during the day.  This plan

[026-068]of operation was persevered in for the whole of one summer, but was

[026-069]found too uncertain and laborious to be continued.  At the latter end

[026-070]of the year 1798 I left my chambers, and removed from the Clyde Works

[026-071]to the distance of about a mile, where I constructed several furnaces

[026-072]for assaying and cementing, capable of exciting a greater temperature

[026-073]than any to which I before had access; and thus for nearly two years I

[026-074]continued to carry on my investigations connected with iron and the

[026-075]alloys of the metals.

[026-076]"Though operating in a retired manner, and holding little communication

[026-077]with others, my views and opinions upon the RATIONALE of iron-making

[026-078]spread over the establishment.  I was considered forward in affecting

[026-079]to see and explain matters in a different way from others who were much

[026-080]my seniors, and who were content to be satisfied with old methods of

[026-081]explanation, or with no explanation at all.....  Notwithstanding these

[026-082]early reproaches, I have lived to see the nomenclature of my youth

[026-083]furnish a vocabulary of terms in the art of iron-making, which is used

[026-084]by many of the ironmasters of the present day with freedom and effect,

[026-085]in communicating with each other on the subject of their respective

[026-086]manufactures.  Prejudices seldom outlive the generation to which they

[026-087]belong, when opposed by a more rational system of explanation.  In this

[026-088]respect, Time (as my Lord Bacon says) is the greatest of all innovators.

[026-089]"In a similar manner, Time operated in my favour in respect to the

[026-090]Black Band [[ironstone|Ironstone]].[7]  The discovery of this was made in 1801, when

[026-091]I was engaged in erecting for myself and partners the Calder Iron

[026-092]Works.  Great prejudice was excited against me by the ironmasters and

[026-093]others of that day in presuming to class the WILD COALS of the country

[026-094](as Black Band was called) with ironstone fit and proper for the blast

[026-095]furnace.  Yet that discovery has elevated [[scotland|Scotland]] to a considerable

[026-096]rank among the iron-making nations of Europe, with resources still in

[026-097]store that may be considered inexhaustible.  But such are the

[026-098]consolatory effects of Time, that the discoverer of 1801 is no longer

[026-099]considered the intrusive visionary of the laboratory, but the

[026-100]acknowledged benefactor of his country at large, and particularly of an

[026-101]extensive class of coal and mine proprietors and iron masters, who have

[026-102]derived, and are still deriving, great wealth from this important

[026-103]discovery; and who, in the spirit of grateful acknowledgment, have

[026-104]pronounced it worthy of a crown of gold, or a monumental record on the

[026-105]spot where the discovery was first made.

[026-106]"At an advanced period of life, such considerations are soothing and

[026-107]satisfactory.  Many under similar circumstances have not, in their own

[026-108]lifetime, had that measure of justice awarded to them by their country

[026-109]to which they were equally entitled.  I accept it, however, as a boon

[026-110]justly due to me, and as an equivalent in some degree for that

[026-111]laborious course of investigation which I had prescribed for myself,

[026-112]and which, in early life, was carried on under circumstances of

[026-113]personal exposure and inconvenience, which nothing but a frame of iron

[026-114]could have supported.  They atone also, in part, for that

[026-115]disappointment sustained in early life by the speculative habits of one

[026-116]partner, and the constitutional nervousness of another, which

[026-117]eventually occasioned my separation from the [[calder-ironworks|Calder Iron Works]], and

[026-118]lost me the possession of extensive tracts of Black Band iron-stone,

[026-119]which I had secured while the value of the discovery was known only to

[026-120]myself."

[026-121][[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] published the results of his laborious investigations in a

[026-122]series of papers in the Philosophical Magazine,--afterwards reprinted

[026-123]in a collected form in 1840 under the title of "Papers on Iron and

[026-124]Steel."  These papers are among the most valuable original

[026-125]contributions to the literature of the iron-manufacture that have yet

[026-126]been given to the world.  They contain the germs of many inventions and

[026-127]discoveries in iron and steel, some of which were perfected by Mr.

[026-128]Mushet himself, while others were adopted and worked out by different

[026-129]experimenters.  In 1798 some of the leading French chemists were

[026-130]endeavouring to prove by experiment that steel could be made by contact

[026-131]of the diamond with bar-iron in the crucible, the carbon of the diamond

[026-132]being liberated and entering into combination with the iron, forming

[026-133]steel.  In the animated controversy which occurred on the subject, Mr.

[026-134]Mushet's name was brought into considerable notice; one of the subjects

[026-135]of his published experiments having been the conversion of bar-iron

[026-136]into steel in the crucible by contact with regulated proportions of

[026-137]charcoal.  The experiments which he made in connection with this

[026-138]controversy, though in themselves unproductive of results, led to the

[026-139]important discovery by Mr. Mushet of the certain fusibility of

[026-140][[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] at a suitable temperature.

[026-141]Among the other important results of Mr. Mushet's lifelong labours, the

[026-142]following may be summarily mentioned:  The preparation of steel from

[026-143]bar-iron by a direct process, combining the iron with carbon; the

[026-144]discovery of the beneficial effects of oxide of manganese on iron and

[026-145]steel; the use of oxides of iron in the [[puddling-process|puddling]]-furnace in various

[026-146]modes of appliance; the production of pig-iron from the blast-furnace,

[026-147]suitable for [[puddling-process|puddling]], without the intervention of the refinery; and

[026-148]the application of the hot blast to anthracite coal in iron-smelting.

[026-149]For the process of combining iron with carbon for the production of

[026-150]steel, Mr. Mushet took out a patent in November, 1800; and many years

[026-151]after, when he had discovered the beneficial effects of oxide of

[026-152]manganese on steel, Mr. [[josiah-heath|Josiah Heath]] founded upon it his celebrated

[026-153]patent for the making of cast-steel, which had the effect of raising

[026-154]the annual production of that metal in [[sheffield|Sheffield]] from 3000 to 100,000

[026-155]tons.  His application of the hot blast to anthracite coal, after a

[026-156]process invented by him and adopted by the Messrs. Hill of the Plymouth

[026-157]Iron Works, [[south-wales|South Wales]], had the effect of producing savings equal to

[026-158]about 20,000L. a year at those works; and yet, strange to say, Mr.

[026-159]Mushet himself never received any consideration for his invention.

[026-160]The discovery of Titanium by Mr. Mushet in the hearth of a

[026-161]blast-furnace in 1794 would now be regarded as a mere isolated fact,

[026-162]inasmuch as Titanium was not placed in the list of recognised metals

[026-163]until Dr. Wollaston, many years later, ascertained its qualities.  But

[026-164]in connection with the fact, it may be mentioned that Mr. Mushet's

[026-165]youngest son, Robert, reasoning on the peculiar circumstances of the

[026-166]discovery in question, of which ample record is left, has founded upon

[026-167]it his Titanium process, which is expected by him eventually to

[026-168]supersede all other methods of manufacturing steel, and to reduce very

[026-169]materially the cost of its production.

[026-170]While he lived, Mr. Mushet was a leading authority on all matters

[026-171]connected with Iron and Steel, and he contributed largely to the

[026-172]scientific works of his time.  Besides his papers in the Philosophical

[026-173]Journal, he wrote the article "Iron" for Napiers Supplement to the

[026-174]Encyclopaedia Britannica; and the articles "[[blast-furnace|Blast Furnace]]" and "Blowing

[026-175]Machine" for Rees's Cyclopaedia.  The two latter articles had a

[026-176]considerable influence on the opposition to the intended tax upon iron

[026-177]in 1807, and were frequently referred to in the discussions on the

[026-178]subject in [[house-of-commons|Parliament]].  Mr. Mushet died in 1847.
