[036-001]He was indeed sorely puzzled what next to do, when one of the hammermen

[036-002]in Moodie's shop ventured to suggest that there was a young man in the

[036-003][[woolwich-arsenal|[[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]] Arsenal]] smithy, named Maudslay, who was so ingenious in such

[036-004]matters that "nothing bet him," and he recommended that Mr. Bramah

[036-005]should have a talk with him upon the subject of his difficulty.

[036-006]Maudslay was at once sent for to Bramah's workshop, and appeared before

[036-007]the lock-maker, a tall, strong, comely young fellow, then only eighteen

[036-008]years old.  Bramah was almost ashamed to lay his case before such a

[036-009]mere youth; but necessity constrained him to try all methods of

[036-010]accomplishing his object, and Maudslay's suggestions in reply to his

[036-011]statement of the case were so modest, so sensible, and as the result

[036-012]proved, so practical, that the master was constrained to admit that the

[036-013]lad before him had an old head though set on young shoulders.  Bramah

[036-014]decided to adopt the youth's suggestions, made him a present on the

[036-015]spot, and offered to give him a job if he was willing to come and work

[036-016]in a town shop.  Maudslay gladly accepted the offer, and in due time

[036-017]appeared before Bramah to enter upon his duties.

[036-018]As Maudslay had served no regular apprenticeship, and was of a very

[036-019]youthful appearance, the foreman of the shop had considerable doubts as

[036-020]to his ability to take rank alongside his experienced hands.  But

[036-021]Maudslay soon set his master's and the foreman's mind at rest.

[036-022]Pointing to a worn-out vice-bench, he said to Bramah, "Perhaps if I can

[036-023]make that as good as new by six o'clock to-night, it will satisfy your

[036-024]foreman that I am entitled to rank as a tradesman and take my place

[036-025]among your men, even though I have not served a seven years'

[036-026]apprenticeship."  There was so much self-reliant ability in the

[036-027]proposal, which was moreover so reasonable, that it was at once acceded

[036-028]to.  Off went Maudslay's coat, up went his shirt sleeves, and to work

[036-029]he set with a will upon the old bench.  The vice-jaws were re-steeled

[036-030]"in no time," filed up, re-cut, all the parts cleaned and made trim,

[036-031]and set into form again.  By six o'clock, the old vice was screwed up

[036-032]to its place, its jaws were hardened and "let down" to proper temper,

[036-033]and the old bench was made to look so smart and neat that it threw all

[036-034]the neighbouring benches into the shade!  Bramah and his foreman came

[036-035]round to see it, while the men of the shop looked admiringly on.  It

[036-036]was examined and pronounced "a first-rate job." This diploma piece of

[036-037]work secured Maudslay's footing, and next Monday morning he came on as

[036-038]one of the regular hands.

[036-039]He soon took rank in the shop as a first-class workman.  Loving his

[036-040]art, he aimed at excellence in it, and succeeded.  For it must be

[036-041]understood that the handicraftsman whose heart is in his calling, feels

[036-042]as much honest pride in turning out a piece of thoroughly good

[036-043]workmanship, as the sculptor or the painter does in executing a statue

[036-044]or a picture.  In course of time, the most difficult and delicate jobs

[036-045]came to be entrusted to Maudslay; and nothing gave him greater pleasure

[036-046]than to be set to work upon an entirely new piece of machinery.  And

[036-047]thus he rose, naturally and steadily, from hand to head work.  For his

[036-048]manual dexterity was the least of his gifts.  He possessed an intuitive

[036-049]power of mechanical analysis and synthesis.  He had a quick eye to

[036-050]perceive the arrangements requisite to effect given purposes; and

[036-051]whenever a difficulty arose, his inventive mind set to work to overcome

[036-052]it.

[036-053]His fellow-workmen were not slow to recognise his many admirable

[036-054]qualities, of hand, mind, and heart; and he became not only the

[036-055]favourite, but the hero of the shop.  Perhaps he owed something to his

[036-056]fine personal appearance.  Hence on gala-days, when the men turned out

[036-057]in procession, "Harry" was usually selected to march at their head and

[036-058]carry the flag.  His conduct as a son, also, was as admirable as his

[036-059]qualities as a workman.  His father dying shortly after Maudslay

[036-060]entered Bramah's concern, he was accustomed to walk down to [[woolwich|Woolwich]]

[036-061]every Saturday night, and hand over to his mother, for whom he had the

[036-062]tenderest regard, a considerable share of his week's wages, and this he

[036-063]continued to do as long as she lived.

[036-064]Notwithstanding his youth, he was raised from one post to another,

[036-065]until he was appointed, by unanimous consent, the head foreman of the

[036-066]works; and was recognised by all who had occasion to do business there

[036-067]as "Bramah's right-hand man."  He not only won the heart of his master,

[036-068]but--what proved of far greater importance to him--he also won the

[036-069]heart of his master's pretty housemaid, Sarah Tindel by name, whom he

[036-070]married, and she went hand-in-hand with him through life, an admirable

[036-071]"help meet," in every way worthy of the noble character of the great

[036-072]mechanic.  Maudslay was found especially useful by his master in

[036-073]devising the tools for making his patent locks; and many were the

[036-074]beautiful contrivances which he invented for the purpose of ensuring

[036-075]their more accurate and speedy manufacture, with a minimum degree of

[036-076]labour, and without the need of any large amount of manual dexterity on

[036-077]the part of the workman.  The lock was so delicate a machine, that the

[036-078]identity of the several parts of which it was composed was found to be

[036-079]an absolute necessity.  Mere handicraft, however skilled, could not

[036-080]secure the requisite precision of workmanship; nor could the parts be

[036-081]turned out in sufficient quantity to meet any large demand.  It was

[036-082]therefore requisite to devise [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] which should not blunder,

[036-083]nor turn out imperfect work;--machines, in short, which should be in a

[036-084]great measure independent of the want of dexterity of individual

[036-085]workmen, but which should unerringly labour in their prescribed track,

[036-086]and do the work set them, even in the minutest details, after the

[036-087]methods designed by their inventor.  In this department Maudslay was

[036-088]eminently successful, and to his laborious ingenuity, as first

[036-089]displayed in Bramah's workshops, and afterwards in his own

[036-090]establishment, we unquestionably owe much of the power and accuracy of

[036-091]our present self-acting machines.

[036-092]Bramah himself was not backward in admitting that to [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]'s

[036-093]practical skill in contriving the machines for manufacturing his locks

[036-094]on a large scale, the success of his invention was in a great degree

[036-095]attributable.  In further proof of his manual dexterity, it may be

[036-096]mentioned that he constructed with his own hands the identical padlock

[036-097]which so severely tested the powers of Mr. Hobbs in 1851.  And when it

[036-098]is considered that the lock had been made for more than half a century,

[036-099]and did not embody any of the modern improvements, it will perhaps be

[036-100]regarded not only as creditable to the principles on which it was

[036-101]constructed, but to the workmanship of its maker, that it should so

[036-102]long have withstood the various mechanical dexterity to which it was

[036-103]exposed.

[036-104]Besides the invention of improved [[machine-tool|machine-tools]] for the manufacture of

[036-105]locks, Maudslay was of further service to Bramah in applying the

[036-106]expedient to his famous [[hydraulic-press|Hydraulic Press]], without which it would

[036-107]probably have remained an impracticable though a highly ingenious

[036-108]machine.  As in other instances of great inventions, the practical

[036-109]success of the whole is often found to depend upon the action of some

[036-110]apparently trifling detail.  This was especially the case with the

[036-111]hydraulic press; to which Maudslay added the essential feature of the

[036-112]self-tightening collar, above described in the memoir of Bramah.  Mr.

[036-113][[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]] is our authority for ascribing this invention to

[036-114]Maudslay, who was certainly quite competent to have made it; and it is

[036-115]a matter of fact that Bramah's specification of the press says nothing

[036-116]of the hollow collar,[1] on which its efficient action mainly depends.

[036-117]Mr. Nasmyth says--"Maudslay himself told me, or led me to believe, that

[036-118]it was he who invented the self-tightening collar for the hydraulic

[036-119]press, without which it would never have been a serviceable machine.

[036-120]As the self-tightening collar is to the hydraulic press, so is the

[036-121]steamblast to the locomotive.  It is the one thing needful that has

[036-122]made it effective in practice.  If Maudslay was the inventor of the

[036-123]collar, that one contrivance ought to immortalize him.  He used to tell

[036-124]me of it with great gusto, and I have no reason to doubt the

[036-125]correctness of his statement."  Whoever really struck out the idea of

[036-126]the collar, displayed the instinct of the true inventor, who invariably

[036-127]seeks to accomplish his object by the adoption of the simplest possible

[036-128]means.

[036-129]During the time that Maudslay held the important office of manager of

[036-130]Bramah's works, his highest wages were not more than thirty shillings

[036-131]a-week.  He himself thought that he was worth more to his master--as

[036-132]indeed he was,--and he felt somewhat mortified that he should have to

[036-133]make an application for an advance; but the increasing expenses of his

[036-134]family compelled him in a measure to do so.  His application was

[036-135]refused in such a manner as greatly to hurt his sensitive feelings; and

[036-136]the result was that he threw up his situation, and determined to begin

[036-137]working on his own account.

[036-138]His first start in business was in the year 1797, in a small workshop

[036-139]and smithy situated in Wells Street, Oxford Street.  It was in an awful

[036-140]state of dirt and dilapidation when he became its tenant.  He entered

[036-141]the place on a Friday, but by the Saturday evening, with the help of

[036-142]his excellent wife, he had the shop thoroughly cleaned, whitewashed,

[036-143]and put in readiness for beginning work on the next Monday morning.  He

[036-144]had then the pleasure of hearing the roar of his own forge-fire, and

[036-145]the cheering ring of the hammer on his own anvil; and great was the

[036-146]pride he felt in standing for the first time within his own smithy and

[036-147]executing orders for customers on his own account.  His first customer

[036-148]was an artist, who gave him an order to execute the iron work of a

[036-149]large easel, embodying some new arrangements; and the work was

[036-150]punctually done to his employer's satisfaction.  Other orders followed,

[036-151]and he soon became fully employed.  His fame as a first-rate workman

[036-152]was almost as great as that of his former master; and many who had been

[036-153]accustomed to do business with him at Pimlico followed him to Wells

[036-154]Street.  Long years after, the thought of these early days of

[036-155]self-dependence and hard work used to set him in a glow, and he would

[036-156]dilate to his intimate friends up on his early struggles and his first

[036-157]successes, which were much more highly prized by him than those of his

[036-158]maturer years.

[036-159]With a true love of his craft, Maudslay continued to apply himself, as

[036-160]he had done whilst working as Bramah's foreman, to the best methods of

[036-161]ensuring accuracy and finish of work, so as in a measure to be

[036-162]independent of the carelessness or want of dexterity of the workman.

[036-163]With this object he aimed at the contrivance of improved machine-tools,

[036-164]which should be as much self-acting and self-regulating as possible;

[036-165]and it was while pursuing this study that he wrought out the important

[036-166]mechanical invention with which his name is usually identified--that of

[036-167]the [[slide-rest|Slide Rest]].  It continued to be his special delight, when engaged

[036-168]in the execution of any piece of work in which he took a personal

[036-169]interest, to introduce a system of identity of parts, and to adapt for

[036-170]the purpose some one or other of the mechanical contrivances with which

[036-171]his fertile brain was always teeming.  Thus it was from his desire to

[036-172]leave nothing to the chance of mere individual dexterity of hand that

[036-173]he introduced the slide rest in the lathe, and rendered it one of the

[036-174]most important of machine-tools.  The first device of this kind was

[036-175]contrived by him for Bramah, in whose shops it continued in practical

[036-176]use long after he had begun business for himself.  "I have seen the

[036-177]slide rest," says Mr. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], "the first that [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]]

[036-178]made, in use at Messrs. Bramah's workshops, and in it were all those

[036-179]arrangements which are to be found in the most modern slide rest of our

[036-180]own day,[2] all of which are the legitimate offspring of Maudslay's

[036-181]original rest.  If this tool be yet extant, it ought to be preserved

[036-182]with the greatest care, for it was the beginning of those mechanical

[036-183]triumphs which give to the days in which we live so much of their

[036-184]distinguishing character."
