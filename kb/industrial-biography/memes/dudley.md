# Dudley

Dudley in the West Midlands was the centre of [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s experiments with [[coke-smelting|pit-coal smelting]] in the early 17th century. The region became one of Britain's principal iron-producing districts, known as the Black Country for its coal mines, ironworks, and foundries. (001-129)

## Early [[iron-industry|Iron Industry]]

By the early 1600s, the town of Dudley was already a centre of the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]], chiefly producing small wares such as nails, horse-shoes, keys, locks, and common agricultural tools. It was estimated that about 20,000 smiths and workers in iron of various kinds lived within a circuit of ten miles of Dudley Castle. (010-001)

Iron production in the district had suffered severely from the want of fuel. Though formerly a mighty woodland country, the forests around Dudley had been largely consumed by the ironworks. (010-006)

## Coal and Revival

Despite the timber shortage, there was an extraordinary abundance of coal in the neighbourhood — in some places lying in seams ten yards thick. (010-010) This abundance of coal, combined with the iron-ore deposits of [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]], made Dudley the natural birthplace of attempts to smelt iron with mineral fuel.

## [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s Experiments

[[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] (1599–1684), the natural son of Edward Lord Dudley of Dudley Castle, made the first serious attempts to smelt iron with pit-coal in the 1620s. (009-174)

Though his works were destroyed by flood and the Civil War, his experiments anticipated by nearly a century the coke-smelting process later developed by [[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]].
