John Kennedy (1769–1855) was a Scottish [[cotton-spinner]] and [[machine-maker]] who co-founded the firm of MacConnel and Kennedy, at one time the largest [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]] enterprise in the United Kingdom. A self-educated man of wide interests, Kennedy was one of the three judges at the celebrated [[Rainhill Trials]] of 1829, an early director of the [[Liverpool and Manchester Railway]], and a friend and correspondent of [[James Watt]]. His memoir of [[Samuel Crompton]] and the invention of the [[Mule Spinning|spinning mule]] long remained the only published record of that invention.

## Early Life

John Kennedy was born in 1769 at Knocknalling, in the stewartry of Kirkcudbright, [[scotland|Scotland]]. His parents were "respectable peasants, possessed of a little bit of ground on which they contrived to live, and that was all." (054-123) He was one of a family of five sons and two daughters; his father died early, leaving the burden of raising the children to his mother, a strict disciplinarian who impressed upon her sons that "they had their own way to make in the world." (054-126)

The parish-school was six miles distant and the teaching inferior, so the Kennedy children attended only a few months each summer. "What they had acquired by the end of one season was often forgotten by the beginning of the next." (054-142) Yet Kennedy's early education, meagre as it was, did not prevent him from later becoming one of the most literate and intellectually curious manufacturers of his generation.

Kennedy was one of five young Scots of nearly the same age who came from the same neighbourhood and eventually settled in [[manchester|Manchester]] as cotton-spinners about the end of the eighteenth century. The others were his brother James, his future partner [[james-macconel|James MacConnel]], and the brothers Adam and George Murray—who would later give [[William Fairbairn]] his first large contract. (054-118)

## Career

### Founding of MacConnel and Kennedy

His apprenticeship completed, Kennedy began business in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1791 in partnership with two other workmen, Sandford and MacConnel. The firm combined machine-making with mule-spinning, Kennedy taking charge of the machine department. (055-001) They started modestly, putting up their mules "in any convenient garrets they could hire at a low rental," before moving to a small factory in Canal Street. (055-005) The business grew steadily, and Kennedy and MacConnel went on to build mills and extend their operations on an ever-larger scale. (055-008)

By the early decades of the nineteenth century, MacConnel and Kennedy had become the largest cotton-spinners in the kingdom—a remarkable ascent for a group of young men who had arrived in [[manchester|Manchester]] with nothing but their mechanical skill and determination. (054-096)

### Patronage of William Fairbairn

In 1818, the firm commissioned [[William Fairbairn]] and [[james-lillie|James Lillie]]—then an entirely unknown partnership with a single lathe—to erect the machinery for a large new mill. Kennedy added to Fairbairn's improved gearing an original invention of his own: a system of "double speeds" designed to give an increased quantity of twist in the finer descriptions of mule yarn. (055-075) The success of this installation launched Fairbairn's reputation, and "Mr. Kennedy's good word was of itself a passport to fame and business." (055-083)

### The Rainhill Trials

Kennedy's reputation for mechanical judgment and impartiality led to his appointment as one of the three judges at the [[Rainhill Trials]] of October 1829, where competing locomotives—including [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]]'s *Rocket*—were tested to determine the motive power for the [[Liverpool and Manchester Railway]]. Kennedy served alongside John Rastrick and Nicholas Wood. (055-026) His selection for this role reflected the high esteem in which his engineering knowledge was held by contemporaries such as [[George Stephenson]], whom he later numbered among his personal friends.

> "By these successive steps did this poor Scotch boy become one of the leading men of Manchester, closing his long and useful life in 1855 at an advanced age, his mental faculties remaining clear and unclouded to the last." (055-031)

## Literary and Scientific Contributions

Kennedy overcame the deficiencies of his early education to become one of the few men of his class distinguished for literary labours. Towards the close of his life he prepared several papers of great interest for the [[Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society]], published in their *Proceedings*. (055-014)

His most historically significant paper was a memoir on the invention of the [[mule-spinning|spinning mule]] by [[Samuel Crompton]], which "was for a long time the only record which the public possessed of the merits and claims of that distinguished inventor." (055-019) Kennedy's knowledge of the history of the cotton manufacture in its various stages, and of mechanical inventions generally, was described by contemporaries as "most extensive and accurate." (055-021)

## Influence and Legacy

Kennedy's influence extended across several spheres. As a manufacturer, he helped to make [[manchester|Manchester]] the world centre of [[mule-spinning|cotton-spinning]]. As a patron of engineering talent, he gave [[William Fairbairn]] the early break that launched a career of national importance. As a railway director and Rainhill judge, he helped to shape the development of steam locomotion. And as a man of letters, he preserved for posterity the story of [[Samuel Crompton]] and the [[Mule Spinning|spinning mule]]—an invention that, more than any other, powered the transformation of [[lancashire|Lancashire]] into the workshop of the world.

He died at an advanced age in 1855; his end was described as "happy and tranquil—so easy that it was for a time doubtful whether he was dead or asleep." (055-034)
