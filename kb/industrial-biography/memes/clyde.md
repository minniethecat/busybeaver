# River Clyde

The River Clyde, flowing through [[glasgow|Glasgow]] to the Firth of Clyde, was one of [[scotland|Scotland]]'s principal industrial corridors during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]]. The Clyde valley became the site of pioneering iron-works, notably the [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]], and later the heart of British shipbuilding. (005-170)

## Industrial History

### Early [[iron-industry|Iron Industry]]

The Clyde valley's industrial potential was recognised from an early period. [[ironstone|Ironstone]] deposits in the valleys of the [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Clyde]] attracted the attention of ironmasters who understood the region's combined advantages of mineral wealth and water transport. (002-284) (005-170)

From these beginnings sprang the great iron industry of the west of [[scotland|Scotland]], anchored by works at Clyde Cleugh, Muirkirk, and [[devon|Devon]], alongside the [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]] near [[glasgow|Glasgow]]. (025-146) (025-160)

### The [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]]

The [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]], situated on the river near [[glasgow|Glasgow]], became one of the most important centres of Scottish iron production. Mr. Colin Dunlop of the [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]] was among the industrialists who supported the development of the [[neilson-hot-blast|hot blast]] process, which was brought into a definite shape at the [[clyde-iron-works|Clyde Iron Works]] and revolutionised iron-smelting. (028-069) (028-091)

The hot blast invention later spread from the Clyde and Calder to iron-works across Britain and the continent. (028-123) It happened at a critical moment: a depression in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] had forced the closure of many furnaces, and the Clyde Works itself faced difficult times before the new process restored its competitiveness. (026-059) (026-066) (026-070)

### [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and Clyde Canal]]

The [[forth-and-clyde-canal|Forth and Clyde Canal]], which joined the Clyde to the Firth of Forth, provided essential water communication across the Central Belt of [[scotland|Scotland]]. (025-032) The canal and the river together formed an integrated transport network that sustained the region's iron industry through the nineteenth century. (055-163)
