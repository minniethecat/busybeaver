## Early Life

James Fox, the founder of the [[derby|Derby]] firm of mechanical engineers, began his career in an unlikely occupation—as a butler in the service of the Rev. Thomas Gisborne of Foxhall Lodge, [[staffordshire|Staffordshire]]. Though domestic service might seem unfavourable for the display of mechanical ability, Fox's instinct for handicraft was so strong that it could not be repressed. His master not only encouraged him in the handling of tools during his leisure hours, but had so genuine an admiration of his skill and excellent qualities of character that he eventually furnished him with the means of beginning in business on his own account. (045-053)

## Career in [[derby|Derby]]

The growth and extension of the cotton, silk, and lace trades in the neighbourhood of [[derby|Derby]] provided Fox with ample opportunities for the exercise of his mechanical skill. His lace machinery became celebrated, and he supplied it extensively to the neighbouring town of [[Nottingham]]. He also obtained considerable employment from the great firms of [[Richard Arkwright|Arkwright]] and [[Jedediah [[william-strutt|Strutt]]|Strutt]], the founders of the modern cotton manufacture. (045-054)

Fox became particularly renowned for his lathes, which were of excellent quality and maintained a high reputation over many years. Besides supplying the home demand, he exported considerable machinery abroad to France, Russia, and the Mauritius. (045-055)

## [[machine-tool|Machine Tool]] Inventions

The firm of Fox of [[derby|Derby]], continued by his descendants, claim for their grandfather that he made the first planing machine in 1814. According to Samuel Hall, a former workman at the firm, the machine was "essentially the same in principle as the planing machine now in general use, although differing in detail." It featured a self-acting ratchet motion for moving the slides of a compound slide rest and a self-acting reversing tackle consisting of three bevel wheels with a clutch box mechanism. (045-090)

The same James Fox is also credited with having invented, at a very early period, a screw-cutting machine, an engine for accurately dividing and cutting the teeth of wheels, and a self-acting lathe. These inventions placed Fox among the leading machine-tool makers of his generation, alongside contemporaries such as [[Henry Maudslay]], [[Richard Roberts]], and [[Joseph Whitworth]]. (045-091)

## Legacy

James Fox was one of a group of mechanics of almost equal eminence who flourished in the northern manufacturing towns during the early nineteenth century, alongside [[matthew-murray|Matthew Murray]] and [[peter-fairbairn|Peter Fairbairn]] of [[leeds|Leeds]], and [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]], [[joseph-whitworth|Joseph Whitworth]], [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], and [[William Fairbairn]] of [[manchester|Manchester]]. As [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]] noted, "to all of whom the manufacturing industry of Great Britain stands in the highest degree indebted." (045-048)

The Fox firm continued under his successors, maintaining the reputation for quality machine tools that their founder had established.
