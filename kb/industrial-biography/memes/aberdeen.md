# Aberdeen

Aberdeen, the principal city of north-east [[scotland|Scotland]], played a significant role in the development of precision engineering in the early 19th century. Known as the "Granite City" for its distinctive stone architecture, Aberdeen was home to engineering workshops, a university, and a thriving community of skilled mechanics. Its most notable association was with [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]], who worked and studied there before becoming one of [[london|London]]'s most celebrated precision engineers. (002-075) (002-076) (042-048)

## Engineering and Education

### [[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]]'s Aberdeen Years

[[joseph-clement|Joseph Clement]], born in Westmorland in 1779, removed to Aberdeen as a young man in search of opportunity. There, Peter Nicholson, the distinguished architectural draughtsman and mathematician, taught him drawing — a skill that would prove fundamental to his later career as a precision machine-maker. (002-066) (002-074) (002-075) Clement worked as a mechanic while attending the city's college, demonstrating the combination of practical and theoretical education that characterised the best of the Scottish Enlightenment tradition. (002-076)

At Aberdeen, Clement was employed by the firm of Masson and Co., where he began at a guinea and a half a week, gradually rising to two and then three guineas as his skill was recognised. (042-048) (042-049) His principal work consisted of designing and making machinery for the firm, and it was at Aberdeen that he made a turning-lathe with a sliding rest — a precision machine tool that foreshadowed his later innovations. (042-050) (042-055)

> "At Aberdeen, he made a turning-lathe with a sliding rest, an early precision machine tool that presaged his later innovations in [[london|London]]." (042-055)

Clement attended the natural philosophy lectures at Aberdeen in the session of 1812–13, and we do not doubt that our mechanic was among the most diligent of his fellow students. (042-066) (042-067) After his time in Aberdeen, Clement removed to [[london|London]], where he became one of the most celebrated precision engineers of his age. (002-077)

### Spring of Aberdeen

The engineering workshops of Aberdeen were part of a network of Scottish and northern English machine-makers that drove the industrial revolution. The firm of Spring of Aberdeen, alongside [[matthew-murray|Matthew Murray]] of [[leeds|Leeds]] and Clement himself, contributed to the development of steam engines and machine tools. (031-154)

## Industrial Significance

The hot blast, invented by [[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]], was recognised in Aberdeen as one of the greatest technical advances in iron-making. A contemporary observer from Aberdeen characterized it as "the greatest improvement with which the [[iron-industry|iron trade]] had ever been blessed." (029-052) This reflected the city's engagement with the technological transformations reshaping Scottish industry.
