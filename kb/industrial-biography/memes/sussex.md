# Sussex

Sussex was the earliest great iron-making district of [[england|England]]. The Weald of Sussex, with its abundant woodlands for charcoal and its accessible iron ores, was the principal source of English iron from Roman times through to the 18th century, when the exhaustion of its timber and the rise of coal-based smelting in the [[midlands|Midlands]] and [[scotland|Scotland]] brought its furnaces to a halt. (001-115) (001-122)

## Historical Development

### Roman and Medieval Origins

Iron-making in Sussex dated back to the Roman occupation, when the Romans had established extensive works in the district. (007-083) Smelting operations were carried on to a substantial extent in the neighbourhood of Lewes in the 13th and 14th centuries, where the iron was principally made into nails and horse-shoes. (007-084) (007-085) (007-086) The ores of Sussex were extensively worked, as appeared from the cinder heaps still to be found all over the Sussex iron district. (004-137) (007-103)

### The Great Age of the Ironmasters

The [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]s of Sussex reached their peak in the 16th and 17th centuries. The district produced wealthy ironmasters, among them the founder of the Gale family, who made their fortunes from the Sussex furnaces. (001-117) (001-118) Sussex was a major centre for the manufacture of cannon and ordnance, with extensive exports of English guns to the Continent. (001-116) (001-119) The Weald of Sussex, together with [[surrey|Surrey]] and [[kent|Kent]], formed a continuous iron-working region that supplied much of [[england|England]]'s iron. (006-148)

> "He that well observes it, and hath known the welds of Sussex, Surry, and [[kent|Kent]], shall find the iron-works to be the great making of the gentry." (006-148)

### Decline

The ironworks of Sussex were among the most important in [[england|England]] before the discovery of smelting by pit-coal. (007-096) (007-097) (007-098) However, the destruction of timber in iron-smelting placed the manufacture under restrictions, as the depletion of the Sussex woods for charcoal became unsustainable. (001-120) (001-121) The Sussex furnaces were eventually blown out, and by the late 18th century iron-making had shifted decisively to the coal-rich districts of the [[midlands|Midlands]], the [[north-of-england|North]], and [[scotland|Scotland]]. (001-122)

## Key Features

- The Weald — the wooded iron district stretching across Sussex, [[surrey|Surrey]], and Kent
- Roman bloomeries and medieval forges — some of the earliest iron-working sites in Britain
- Cannon foundries — Sussex was the arsenal of Tudor and Stuart [[england|England]]
