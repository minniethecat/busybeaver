[001-007]by [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]]

[001-009]PREFACE.

[001-010]The Author offers the following book as a continuation, in a more

[001-011]generally accessible form, of the Series of Memoirs of Industrial Men

[001-012]introduced in his Lives of the Engineers.  While preparing that work he

[001-013]frequently came across the tracks of celebrated inventors, mechanics,

[001-014]and iron-workers--the founders, in a great measure, of the modern

[001-015]industry of Britain--whose labours seemed to him well worthy of being

[001-016]traced out and placed on record, and the more so as their lives

[001-017]presented many points of curious and original interest.  Having been

[001-018]encouraged to prosecute the subject by offers of assistance from some

[001-019]of the most eminent living mechanical engineers, he is now enabled to

[001-020]present the following further series of memoirs to the public.

[001-021]Without exaggerating the importance of this class of biography, it may

[001-022]at least be averred that it has not yet received its due share of

[001-023]attention.  While commemorating the labours and honouring the names of

[001-024]those who have striven to elevate man above the material and

[001-025]mechanical, the labours of the important industrial class to whom

[001-026]society owes so much of its comfort and well-being are also entitled to

[001-027]consideration.  Without derogating from the biographic claims of those

[001-028]who minister to intellect and taste, those who minister to utility need

[001-029]not be overlooked.  When a Frenchman was praising to Sir John Sinclair

[001-030]the artist who invented ruffles, the Baronet shrewdly remarked that

[001-031]some merit was also due to the man who added the shirt.

[001-032]A distinguished living mechanic thus expresses himself to the Author on

[001-033]this point:--"Kings, warriors, and statesmen have heretofore

[001-034]monopolized not only the pages of history, but almost those of

[001-035]biography.  Surely some niche ought to be found for the Mechanic,

[001-036]without whose skill and labour society, as it is, could not exist.  I

[001-037]do not begrudge destructive heroes their fame, but the constructive

[001-038]ones ought not to be forgotten; and there IS a heroism of skill and

[001-039]toil belonging to the latter class, worthy of as grateful record,--less

[001-040]perilous and romantic, it may be, than that of the other, but not less

[001-041]full of the results of human energy, bravery, and character.  The lot

[001-042]of labour is indeed often a dull one; and it is doing a public service

[001-043]to endeavour to lighten it up by records of the struggles and triumphs

[001-044]of our more illustrious workers, and the results of their labours in

[001-045]the cause of human advancement."

[001-046]As respects the preparation of the following memoirs, the Author's

[001-047]principal task has consisted in selecting and arranging the materials

[001-048]so liberally placed at his disposal by gentlemen for the most part

[001-049]personally acquainted with the subjects of them, and but for whose

[001-050]assistance the book could not have been written.  The materials for the

[001-051]biography of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], for instance, have been partly supplied by

[001-052]the late Mr. [[joshua-field|[[joshua-field|Joshua Field]], F.R.S.]] (his partner), but principally by Mr.

[001-053][[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], C.E., his distinguished pupil.  In like manner Mr. John

[001-054]Penn, C.E., has supplied the chief materials for the memoir of Joseph

[001-055]Clement, assisted by Mr. Wilkinson, Clement's nephew.  The Author has

[001-056]also had the valuable assistance of Mr. [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]], F.R.S., Mr.

[001-057]J. O. March, tool manufacturer (Mayor of [[leeds|Leeds]]), Mr. [[richard-roberts|Richard Roberts]],

[001-058]C.E., Mr. [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], C.E., and Mr. J. Kitson, Jun., iron

[001-059]manufacturer, [[leeds|Leeds]], in the preparation of the other memoirs of

[001-060]mechanical engineers included in this volume.

[001-061]The materials for the memoirs of the early iron-workers have in like

[001-062]manner been obtained for the most part from original sources; those of

[001-063]the Darbys and Reynoldses from Mr. Dickinson of [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]], Mr.

[001-064][[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] of Coed-du, and Mr. William G. Norris of the former

[001-065]place, as well as from Mr. Anstice of Madeley Wood, who has kindly

[001-066]supplied the original records of the firm.  The substance of the

[001-067]biography of [[benjamin-huntsman|Benjamin Huntsman]], the inventor of cast-steel, has been

[001-068]furnished by his lineal representatives; and the facts embodied in the

[001-069]memoirs of [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] and [[david-mushet|David Mushet]] have been supplied by the sons

[001-070]of those inventors.  To Mr. Anderson Kirkwood of [[glasgow|Glasgow]] the Author is

[001-071]indebted for the memoir of [[james-beaumont-neilson|James Beaumont Neilson]], inventor of the hot

[001-072]blast; and to Mr. Ralph Moore, Inspector of Mines in [[scotland|Scotland]], for

[001-073]various information relative to the progress of the Scotch iron

[001-074]manufacture.

[001-075]The memoirs of [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] and [[andrew-yarranton|Andrew Yarranton]] are almost the only ones

[001-076]of the series in preparing which material assistance has been derived

[001-077]from books; but these have been largely illustrated by facts contained

[001-078]in original documents preserved in the State Paper Office, the careful

[001-079]examination of which has been conducted by Mr. W. Walker Wilkins.

[001-080]It will thus be observed that most of the information embodied in this

[001-081]volume, more especially that relating to the inventors of tools and

[001-082]machines, has heretofore existed only in the memories of the eminent

[001-083]mechanical engineers from whom it has been collected.  The estimable

[001-084]Joshua Field has died since the date at which he communicated his

[001-085]recollections; and in a few more years many of the facts which have

[001-086]been caught and are here placed on record would, probably, in the

[001-087]ordinary course of things, have passed into oblivion.  As it is, the

[001-088]Author feels that there are many gaps yet to be filled up; but the

[001-089]field of Industrial Biography is a wide one, and is open to all who

[001-090]will labour in it.

[001-091][[london|London]], October, 1863.
