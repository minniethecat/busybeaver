# Edinburgh

Edinburgh was the intellectual hub of [[scotland|Scotland]] during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], its university providing scientific education to a generation of engineers, chemists, and ironmasters who shaped British industry. The city and its environs were the birthplace and training ground of figures including [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], [[david-mushet|David Mushet]], and [[james-watt|James Watt]]'s patron [[joseph-black|Joseph Black]]. (024-133)

## Industrial History

### University and Scientific Culture

The University of Edinburgh was a centre of scientific inquiry that directly influenced industrial development. The chemistry lectures delivered there attracted students from across Britain, and the university's professors — including Joseph Black, who discovered latent heat — provided the theoretical foundation for many practical advances in engineering and metallurgy. (024-133) (048-085) Dr. [[john-roebuck|John Roebuck]], who later founded the [[carron-iron-works|Carron Iron Works]], contracted many intimate friendships at Edinburgh that proved of great service in his subsequent career. (024-136)

### Engineering and Industry

Edinburgh's workshop culture nurtured practical talent alongside academic learning. [[james-nasmyth|James Nasmyth]], inventor of the [[steam-hammer|steam hammer]], was born in Edinburgh on 19 August 1808, the son of Alexander Nasmyth the painter. His father maintained a workshop where the young Nasmyth made model [[steam-engine]]s with his own hands, laying the foundation for his later mechanical career. (048-017) (048-027) (051-128)

[[david-mushet|David Mushet]], the metallurgist who discovered titanium and pioneered the use of black-band ironstone, was born at Dalkeith near Edinburgh in 1772. (025-157) The city also supplied skilled labour to distant enterprises: [[james-watt|James Watt]], preparing for his [[london|London]] apprenticeship, paused in Edinburgh to construct a small stock of engineering tools for the university. (049-004) (049-008)

### Edinburghshire Iron

Iron deposits were discovered and successfully worked in Edinburghshire, contributing to the region's early metal industry before the centre of Scottish iron production shifted westward to the [[clyde|Clyde]] valley and [[carron-iron-works|Carron]]. (027-043) The city's foundries continued to supply smaller engineering works through the nineteenth century.
