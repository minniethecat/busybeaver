William Chamberlaine appears in the historical record as a patentee and [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]r in Restoration [[england|England]].

## Early Life

Little is recorded of Chamberlaine's origins. He emerges in the historical record during the reign of Charles II as an [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]r and patentee, associated with [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] in the early tin-plate industry.

## Career

### [[patents|Patents]] and Iron Manufacture

In 1661, a patent was granted to William Chamberlaine and [[dudley|Dudley]] (possibly [[Dud Dudley]], the pioneer of iron-smelting with coal) for the sole use of their new invention of plating steel and tinning the said plates. This was one of the earliest English patents relating to the tin-plate industry, at a time when [[england|England]] was almost entirely dependent on German imports for tin-plated ironware. (011-139)

Chamberlaine subsequently obtained a sole patent in 1673 for "plating and tinning iron, copper, &c.," which extended his earlier joint patent with [[dudley|Dudley]]. (015-001)

### Conflict with Andrew Yarranton

Chamberlaine's patents brought him into conflict with [[Andrew Yarranton]], the enterprising iron-master and engineer who had independently developed a practical method for manufacturing tin-plates in [[england|England]]. Yarranton had made arrangements to carry on the manufacture upon a large scale using [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] iron tinned with Cornish tin, but his plans were thwarted when a patent was taken out — or "trumpt up," as Yarranton called it — for the same manufacture. (013-092)

It is not improbable, as the sources suggest, that the patentee in question was William Chamberlaine, [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s former partner in the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]. Yarranton bitterly observed that the patent, backed by "persons of quality," prevented him from proceeding, though he believed that neither the patentee nor those who countenanced him could make one plate fit for use. (013-097)

Yarranton later reflected on the experience in his treatise on trade, citing Chamberlaine's patent as an example of how patents could have "the tendency to drive trade out of the kingdom," and this thwarted enterprise delayed the establishment of an English tin-plate industry for some sixty years. (014-063)

## Legacy

Chamberlaine's role in the early history of the English tin-plate industry is that of a controversial figure. While his patents represented early attempts to establish domestic manufacture of a strategically important product, they also exemplified the [[patents|patent system]]'s potential to obstruct independent innovation. The tin-plate manufactory was eventually established by Capel Hanbury at [[pontypool|Pontypool]] in [[monmouthshire|Monmouthshire]] around 1730, where it continued to be successfully carried on. (013-105)
