Richard Roberts (1789–1864) was a British [[engineer]] and inventor based in [[Manchester]], described by [[samuel-smiles|Samuel Smiles]] as "one of the most prolific of modern inventors" and "almost regarded as the Admirable Crichton of modern mechanics." (046-003) (046-011) His inventions ranged from the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] for textile spinning to planing machines, gas-meters, and electro-magnets, placing him among the most versatile mechanical innovators of the early nineteenth century.

## Early Life

Richard Roberts was born in 1789 at Carreghova in the parish of Llanymynech, on the border between [[shropshire|Shropshire]] and Montgomeryshire. His father was a shoemaker who occasionally also served as a toll-keeper. The house of his birth stood exactly on the county line: the front door opened in one county, the back door in the other. (046-012) (046-013) (046-015) (046-016)

Roberts received almost no formal education. As soon as he was of working age he was put to common labour, working for some time in a quarry near his father's dwelling. But his mechanical instincts soon showed themselves: during his leisure hours he made various articles of mechanism, and his first achievement as a quarryman was building a spinning-wheel, which was considered "a good job" by those who saw it. (046-018) (046-019) (046-020) (046-022) (046-024)

## Career

### [[apprenticeship|Apprenticeship]] and Early Work

Seeking better opportunities, Roberts found work as a pattern-maker at Bradley, near Bilston, under the famous ironmaster [[John Wilkinson]] — the first man who could bore a [[steam-engine]] cylinder with accuracy for [[James Watt]]. After gaining practical knowledge of working in both wood and iron, Roberts moved to [[Birmingham]], passing through different shops and gaining experience at many kinds of work. He became known as a sort of jack-of-all-trades: a good turner, a tolerable wheelwright, and capable of repairing mill-work. (046-028) (046-029) (046-034) (046-035) (046-036) (046-038)

Roberts next worked at the Horsley ironworks at Tipton, but was forced to leave when drawn for the militia. Determined not to be a soldier, he fled north on foot, reaching [[Liverpool]] and then [[Manchester]] — arriving at dusk, weary and mud-spattered from the wretched roads. Knowing no one in the town, he famously approached an apple-stall to ask the stall-keeper if she knew of any work. (046-040) (046-041) (046-045) (046-047) (046-050)

### Major Inventions

Roberts established himself in [[manchester|Manchester]] and became one of the city's leading mechanical engineers. His range of inventions was extraordinary: the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] transformed cotton spinning; his planing machine was fundamental to [[machine-tool]] development; and he also devised the best electro-magnet of his day, wet gas-meters, iron billiard-tables, turret-clocks, the centrifugal railway, the drill slotting-machine, and machinery for steamship propulsion. (046-004) (046-005) (046-007) (046-008) (046-009) (046-010)

He was a partner in the firm of Sharp, Roberts and Co., which became one of [[manchester|Manchester]]'s most important engineering establishments. His work on the [[mule-spinning|self-acting mule]] alone placed him in the front rank of textile-machinery inventors alongside figures such as [[John Kennedy]].

## Influence and Legacy

Roberts belongs to the remarkable generation of [[manchester|Manchester]]-based mechanical engineers that included [[Joseph Whitworth]], [[James Nasmyth]], and [[William Fairbairn]]. Together they made [[manchester|Manchester]] one of the world's great centres of mechanical innovation in the middle decades of the nineteenth century. (045-049) (045-050)

Though Roberts never achieved the wealth or fame of some of his contemporaries, his versatility and prolific inventiveness earned him a permanent place in the history of British engineering. He died in 1864.
