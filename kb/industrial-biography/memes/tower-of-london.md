The Tower of [[london|London]], the royal fortress on the north bank of the [[thames|River Thames]], played an important role in the testing and storage of iron cannon and artillery from the early modern period through the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

## Role in Iron Manufacturing

The Tower served as the royal armoury and proving ground for iron cannon. Old hooped guns preserved in the Tower date from the reign of Henry VI, and the first cast-iron cannons of English manufacture were tested there. Small pieces of artillery, specimens of which are still preserved in the museum at [[woolwich-arsenal|[[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]] Arsenal]] and at the Tower, document the evolution of English iron-founding. (001-056)

## [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]]'s Iron Testing

Early in the 17th century, the Tower became the site of a pivotal test of English iron. After [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] secured a patent for smelting iron with pit-coal, the King commanded [[dudley|Dudley]] "to send up to the Tower of London, with every possible speed, quantities of the new iron for trial." Dudley accordingly dispatched a considerable quantity, and the iron was subjected to large-scale testing by the royal ordnance authorities. (001-132)

The Tower's association with iron manufacturing illustrates the close relationship between military demand and the development of the [[iron-industry|British iron industry]]. The need for reliable cannon and shot drove innovation in casting and smelting, and the Tower's role as the ultimate proving authority gave it a unique place in the history of iron-making.
