Iron containing 2–4% carbon, produced directly from the [[Blast Furnace]] and cast into moulds — the material that transformed British industry by enabling cheap mass production of everything from pots and cannons to bridges and railway components.

## Definition

Cast iron is a ferrous alloy with carbon content between 2% and 4%, along with silicon (1–3%) and traces of manganese, sulphur, and phosphorus. Its high carbon content gives it a relatively low melting point (about 1,150°C) and excellent fluidity when molten, making it ideal for casting into complex shapes. However, it is brittle and cannot be forged or rolled. (003-137) It is distinct from [[Wrought Iron]] (which has < 0.1% carbon) and from [[Steel]] (0.1–2% carbon).

## Historical Development

### Early Production

Cast iron was known in China as early as the 5th century BC, but in Britain its widespread use began with the blast furnace in the late 15th century. The first English cast-iron cannons were made at Buxted in [[sussex|Sussex]] in 1543 under the direction of Ralph Hogge:

> The first cast-iron cannons of English manufacture were made at Buxted, in the Weald of [[sussex|Sussex]], by Ralf Hogge, in 1543: "Master Huggett and his man John, they did cast the first cannon." (007-137)

[[sussex|The Weald]]en iron industry, based on charcoal blast furnaces, produced cast-iron guns that gained a reputation for quality:

> English ships came to rely on the excellence of their cast-iron guns. The Hogges of Sussex were among the principal founders. (007-158)

### Abraham Darby and Cast-Iron Pots

A major breakthrough came in the early 18th century when [[Abraham Darby I]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] perfected the technique of casting iron in sand moulds using coke-smelted iron:

> His manufacture of cast-iron pots at [[bristol|Bristol]] was highly successful, and led to the establishment of the [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] ironworks. (001-171)

> At that period cast-iron pots were in very general use, and Darby's thin, light castings were greatly superior to the thick, heavy pots previously made. (015-156)

### Cast Iron in Architecture and Engineering

The late 18th century saw [[pig-iron|cast iron]] applied to structural uses on an unprecedented scale. [[Richard Reynolds]] pioneered the use of cast-iron rails for mine tramways in 1767:

> Invention of cast-iron rails by Reynolds at Coalbrookdale. These were first laid down for the use of the horses drawing the coal waggons. (001-179)

The most dramatic application was the [[Iron Bridge]] at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] (1779), the first iron bridge in the world:

> It was proposed to introduce cast-iron in the arch of a bridge over the [[severn|Severn]] near Coalbrookdale... The idea that the manipulation of cast-iron was then so imperfectly understood makes the achievement all the more remarkable. (017-049, 017-071)

> A bridge of cast-iron of one arch only was first put across a navigable river at Coalbrookdale in 1779. (017-085)

## Manufacturing

The standard method of producing [[pig-iron|cast iron]] goods involved:

1. **Smelting**: Iron ore was smelted in the blast furnace with coke and limestone
2. **Tapping**: Molten pig iron was drawn from the furnace hearth
3. **Casting**: The molten metal was either poured directly into moulds at the furnace or cast into "pigs" (ingots) for remelting at a separate foundry

> Sorts of cast-iron wares, as brewing cisterns, pots, pans, and fire-backs, were produced in increasing quantities as the demand for domestic iron goods expanded. (010-109)

For large castings such as bridge arches or engine cylinders, the iron was melted in a [[Foundry]] cupola and poured into sand or loam moulds:

> A mould of cast-iron previously prepared: here it remained until sufficiently cool, when the mould was removed and the casting finished. (020-120)

## Economic and Industrial Impact

Cast iron democratized durable goods. Before cheap [[pig-iron|cast iron]], cooking pots, stoves, and structural hardware were expensive items accessible mainly to the wealthy. By the late 18th century, cast-iron cookware was common in working-class households.

The material also enabled entirely new types of infrastructure: iron bridges, cast-iron aqueducts, iron-framed buildings, and iron rails for railways. The [[Iron Industry]] became one of the pillars of the [[Industrial Revolution]].

> Crawshay the cast-iron king — the "Iron Cyfarthfa" — supplied the government with cannon during the Napoleonic wars. (023-102)
