Mule spinning was the dominant mechanised technique for spinning cotton yarn throughout the first half of the nineteenth century, and the effort to make the machine fully automatic—the self-acting mule—drove advances in precision engineering that shaped the entire [[machine-tool]] industry. Invented by [[Samuel Crompton]] in 1779, the spinning mule combined the drafting rollers of [[Richard Arkwright]]'s water frame with the movable carriage of [[james-hargreaves|James Hargreaves]]'s spinning jenny, producing yarn that was finer and stronger than either machine could achieve alone. Its perfection and automation became a central concern of the [[manchester|Manchester]] engineering community and linked the careers of figures as diverse as [[John Kennedy]], [[Richard Roberts]], and [[William Fairbairn]].

## Origins: Crompton's Mule (1779)

[[samuel-crompton|Samuel Crompton]], a weaver and spinner of [[bolton|Bolton]], [[lancashire|Lancashire]], completed his first spinning mule in 1779 after five years of patient work. The machine was so-called because, like a mule, it was a hybrid—combining the roller-drawing principle of Arkwright's water frame with the intermittent twisting and winding action of Hargreaves's jenny. Crompton lacked the funds to patent his invention, and the machine was rapidly copied and adopted throughout the [[lancashire|Lancashire]] cotton district. "The mule," Smiles notes, "enabled spinners to produce yarn of a fineness and quality far exceeding anything that had been produced before." The invention of the mule is generally regarded, alongside the steam engine and the [[power-loom|power loom]], as one of the three great mechanical innovations that created the [[industrial-revolution|factory system]] of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]].

The earliest mules were hand-operated and contained only a few spindles—often no more than twenty or thirty. Yet even in this primitive form they proved capable of producing yarn fine enough for the weaving of muslins, a manufacture previously dependent on hand-spun yarn imported from India. The rapid spread of mule-spinning in the 1780s and 1790s transformed [[Manchester]] and the surrounding towns into the cotton-manufacturing centre of the world. (055-019)

## John Kennedy and the Mechanisation of Mule Spinning

The transition from hand-operated mules to power-driven factory machinery was accomplished by men such as [[John Kennedy]], who began mule-spinning in [[manchester|Manchester]] in 1791 in partnership with Sandford and [[james-macconel|James MacConnel]]. Kennedy took charge of the machine department, improving the design of the mules and the gearing that drove them. (055-001) By the early decades of the nineteenth century, MacConnel and Kennedy had become the largest cotton-spinners in the United Kingdom, operating hundreds of power-driven mules in their [[manchester|Manchester]] mills.

Kennedy's contribution extended beyond manufacture. His memoir of [[Samuel Crompton]], published in the *Proceedings* of the [[Manchester Literary and Philosophical Society]], "was for a long time the only record which the public possessed of the merits and claims of that distinguished inventor." (055-019) Kennedy added his own invention—a system of "double speeds" that gave "an increased quantity of twist in the finer descriptions of mule yarn"—when he collaborated with [[William Fairbairn]] on the machinery for the firm's new mill in 1818. (055-077)

## The [[self-acting-mule|[[self-acting|Self-Acting]] Mule]]

The effort to make the mule fully automatic—to dispense with the skilled operative who guided the carriage and regulated the winding—was one of the great mechanical challenges of the 1820s and 1830s. An effective self-acting mule required delicate, precisely-timed sequences of motions: drawing out the roving, twisting it into yarn, backing off the tension, and winding the finished yarn onto the spindle. All of this had to happen at high speed, over hundreds of spindles simultaneously, without breaking the fine cotton thread.

> "It was considered next to impossible to make the spinning-mules run out and in at the proper speed by the action of a self-contained mechanism, without the intervention of the skilled hand and eye of the operative." (046-117)

The engineer who succeeded was [[Richard Roberts]] of [[manchester|Manchester]]. Roberts's self-acting mule of 1825—later improved in 1830—was regarded by contemporaries as a mechanical masterpiece. Smiles describes it at work as "drawing out, twisting, and winding up the thread upon the spindle without the aid of human hands." (046-170) The invention transformed the cotton industry, enabling the operation of mules containing up to a thousand spindles by a single minder. Roberts's success with the self-acting mule "led to his being extensively employed by the leading machine-makers of [[lancashire|Lancashire]]." (046-175)

The indirect consequences of the self-acting mule were equally profound. The precision required to make its mechanism reliable spurred the development of [[planing machines]], [[slide rest]] lathes, and [[screw-cutting]] apparatus—the foundational tools of the machine-tool industry. The self-acting mule thus stands as a case study in how a manufacturing challenge in one sector (textiles) could drive innovation in another (mechanical engineering).

## Impact on the Cotton Industry

By the 1830s the self-acting mule had become the standard spinning machine in [[lancashire|Lancashire]] mills, driving an enormous expansion of cotton manufacture. The number of spindles in the United Kingdom rose from roughly 2 million in 1800 to over 20 million by 1850, and the mule produced the vast majority of the yarn. The technology underpinned the prosperity of [[Manchester]] and its satellite towns—Bolton, Oldham, Rochdale, Preston—and made cotton Britain's largest export industry by value.

The mule's success also had an important political dimension. Like the [[Steam Hammer]] and the [[planing machine]], the self-acting mule was adopted by manufacturers "only when compelled to do so by strikes" of skilled operatives who had previously controlled the pace of production. (051-008) The machine thus became both a symbol of industrial progress and a focus of labour conflict, illustrating the complex relationship between mechanisation, skill, and class in the Industrial Revolution.
