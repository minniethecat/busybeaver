The Fourdrinier machine was a continuous paper-making machine developed in the early 19th century by the brothers Henry and Sealy Fourdrinier, with the engineering expertise of [[Bryan Donkin]]. It replaced the traditional hand-made process—where each sheet was individually formed on a wire mould—with a continuous mechanized web, transforming paper from a luxury craft product into an affordable industrial commodity. (034-095)

## Invention Background

Before the Fourdrinier machine, all paper was made by hand: a vatman dipped a wire mould into a pulp suspension, shook it to interlock the fibers, and couched the wet sheet onto felt. The process was slow, labor-intensive, and limited the availability of paper for printing, packaging, and administration.

A Frenchman, Nicholas-Louis Robert, first patented a continuous paper-making machine in 1799. His design was acquired by the Fourdrinier brothers in [[london|London]], who funded its further development.

## Inventor

- **Inventors**: Henry Fourdrinier, Sealy Fourdrinier
- **Engineer**: [[Bryan Donkin]]
- **Patent Year**: ~1803–1807 (successive improvements)

The Fourdriniers provided the capital and commercial drive, but the machine's practical success depended on the mechanical skill of [[bryan-donkin|Bryan Donkin]], who refined the design and manufactured the working machines. The inventions of Fourdrinier and Donkin in this field were widely adopted. (034-095)

## Working Principle

The Fourdrinier machine operated by flowing a dilute pulp suspension onto a moving continuous wire mesh belt. Water drained through the mesh while the fibers formed a wet web. The web then passed through a series of press rolls to squeeze out additional water, followed by heated drying cylinders. The finished paper emerged as a continuous reel, ready for cutting into sheets.

Early machines were complex and temperamental. Donkin's improvements to the wire mesh drive, the press section, and the drying arrangement were essential to making the process reliable. (045-032)

## Technical Significance

The Fourdrinier machine transformed paper-making from a craft into an industry. Paper that had been scarce and expensive became abundant and cheap, fueling the growth of newspapers, books, and commercial printing in the 19th century.

The machine's influence extended beyond paper: Donkin's work on it established him as a precision engineer of the first rank, leading to his later involvement with the [[Royal Mint]] and other industrial projects. (040-101)

## Later Improvements

The basic principle of the Fourdrinier machine—a moving wire mesh forming a continuous web—remains the foundation of modern paper-making. Successive improvements by the Messrs. Donkin and later engineers increased machine speed, width, and reliability. (045-032) The design was so sound that Fourdrinier-type machines continued to dominate the paper industry into the 20th century.
