# Thomas Telford

Thomas Telford (1757–1834) was a Scottish civil engineer whose works helped define the infrastructure of early industrial Britain. Smiles notes that while the Menai Suspension Bridge was in progress, "Mr. Telford, the well-known engineer" inspected the work and praised its quality. (017-082) Telford also "gracefully acknowledged the engineer" of the Caledonian Canal. (017-172)

## Early Life and Career

Born in Eskdale, Dumfriesshire, Telford began his career as a stonemason before moving to [[london|London]] and eventually becoming one of Britain's most respected civil engineers. He was a founding member of the [[institution-of-civil-engineers|Institution of Civil Engineers]], serving as its first president.

## Major Projects

Telford's engineering portfolio includes the [[ellesmere-canal|Ellesmere Canal]] (featuring the Pontcysyllte Aqueduct), the Caledonian Canal in [[scotland|Scotland]], the Menai Suspension Bridge connecting Anglesey to mainland Wales (completed 1826), and over 1,200 miles of roads in the [[highlands|Scottish Highlands]].

## Legacy

Thomas Telford died in 1834 and is buried in Westminster Abbey. His pioneering use of iron in bridge construction influenced subsequent generations of engineers, and his work established civil engineering as a recognised profession in Britain.
