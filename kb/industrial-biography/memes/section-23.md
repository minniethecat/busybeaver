[023-001]Although the Cort patents expired in 1796 and 1798 respectively, they

[023-002]continued the subject of public discussion for some time after, more

[023-003]particularly in connection with the defalcations of the deceased Adam

[023-004]Jellicoe.  It does not appear that more than 2654L. was realised by the

[023-005]Government from the Cort estate towards the loss sustained by the

[023-006]public, as a balance of 24,846L. was still found standing to the debit

[023-007]of Jellicoe in 1800, when the deficiencies in the naval account's

[023-008]became matter of public inquiry.  A few years later, in 1805, the

[023-009]subject was again revived in a remarkable manner.  In that year, the

[023-010]Whigs, Perceiving the bodily decay of Mr. Pitt, and being too eager to

[023-011]wait for his removal by death, began their famous series of attacks

[023-012]upon his administration.  Fearing to tackle the popular statesman

[023-013]himself, they inverted the ordinary tactics of an opposition, and fell

[023-014]foul of Dundas, Lord Melville, then Treasurer of the Navy, who had

[023-015]successfully carried the country through the great naval war with

[023-016]revolutionary France.  They scrupled not to tax him with gross

[023-017]peculation, and exhibited articles of impeachment against him, which

[023-018]became the subject of elaborate investigation, the result of which is

[023-019]matter of history.  In those articles, no reference whatever was made

[023-020]to Lord Melville's supposed complicity with Jellicoe; nor, on the trial

[023-021]that followed, was any reference made to the defalcations of that

[023-022]official.  But when Mr. Whitbread, on the 8th of April, 1805, spoke to

[023-023]the "Resolutions" in the Commons for impeaching the Treasurer of the

[023-024]Navy, he thought proper to intimate that he "had a strong suspicion

[023-025]that Jellicoe was in the same partnership with Mark Sprott, Alexander

[023-026]Trotter, and Lord Melville.  He had been suffered to remain a public

[023-027]debtor for a whole year after he was known to be in arrears upwards of

[023-028]24,000L.  During next year 11,000L. more had accrued.  It would not

[023-029]have been fair to have turned too short on an old companion.  It would

[023-030]perhaps, too, have been dangerous, since unpleasant discoveries might

[023-031]have met the public eye.  It looked very much as if, mutually conscious

[023-032]of criminality, they had agreed to be silent, and keep their own

[023-033]secrets."

[023-034]In making these offensive observations Whitbread was manifestly

[023-035]actuated by political enmity.  They were utterly unwarrantable.  In the

[023-036]first place, Melville had been formally acquitted of Jellicoe's

[023-037]deficiency by a writ of Privy Seal, dated 31st May, 1800; and secondly,

[023-038]the committee appointed in that very year (1805) to reinvestigate the

[023-039]naval accounts, had again exonerated him, but intimated that they were

[023-040]of opinion there was remissness on his part in allowing Jellicoe to

[023-041]remain in his office after the discovery of his defalcations.

[023-042]the report made by the commissioners to the Houses of [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] in

[023-043]1805,[10] the value of Corts patents was estimated at only 100L.

[023-044]Referring to the schedule of Jellicoe's alleged assets, they say "Many

[023-045]of the debts are marked as bad; and we apprehend that the debt from Mr.

[023-046][[henry-cort|Henry Cort]], not so marked, of 54,000L. and upwards, is of that

[023-047]description."  As for poor bankrupt [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]], these discussions

[023-048]availed nothing.  On the death of Jellicoe, he left his iron works,

[023-049]feeling himself a ruined man.  He made many appeals to the Government

[023-050]of the day for restoral of his patents, and offered to find security

[023-051]for payment of the debt due by his firm to the Crown, but in vain.  In

[023-052]1794, an appeal was made to Mr. Pitt by a number of influential members

[023-053]of [[house-of-commons|Parliament]], on behalf of the inventor and his destitute family of

[023-054]twelve children, when a pension of 200L. a-year was granted him.  This

[023-055]Mr. Cort enjoyed until the year 1800, when he died, broken in health

[023-056]and spirit, in his sixtieth year.  He was buried in Hampstead

[023-057]Churchyard, where a stone marking the date of his death is still to be

[023-058]seen.  A few years since it was illegible, but it has recently been

[023-059]restored by his surviving son.

[023-060]Though Cort thus died in comparative poverty, he laid the foundations

[023-061]of many gigantic fortunes.  He may be said to have been in a great

[023-062]measure the author of our modern iron aristocracy, who still

[023-063]manufacture after the processes which he invented or perfected, but for

[023-064]which they never paid him a shilling of royalty.  These men of gigantic

[023-065]fortunes have owed much--we might almost say everything--to the ruined

[023-066]projector of "the little mill at Fontley."  Their wealth has enriched

[023-067]many families of the older aristocracy, and has been the foundation of

[023-068]several modern peerages.  Yet Henry Cort, the rock from which they were

[023-069]hewn, is already all but forgotten; and his surviving children, now

[023-070]aged and infirm, are dependent for their support upon the slender

[023-071]pittance wrung by repeated entreaty and expostulation from the state.

[023-072]The career of [[richard-crawshay|Richard Crawshay]], the first of the great ironmasters who

[023-073]had the sense to appreciate and adopt the methods of manufacturing iron

[023-074]invented by Henry Cort, is a not unfitting commentary on the sad

[023-075]history we have thus briefly described.  It shows how, as respects mere

[023-076]money-making, shrewdness is more potent than invention, and business

[023-077]faculty than manufacturing skill.  [[richard-crawshay|Richard Crawshay]] was born at

[023-078]Normanton near [[leeds|Leeds]], the son of a small [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] farmer.  When a

[023-079]youth, he worked on his father's farm, and looked forward to occupying

[023-080]the same condition in life; but a difference with his father unsettled

[023-081]his mind, and at the age of fifteen he determined to leave his home,

[023-082]and seek his fortune elsewhere.  Like most unsettled and enterprising

[023-083]lads, he first made for [[london|London]], riding to town on a pony of his own,

[023-084]which, with the clothes on his back, formed his entire fortune.  It

[023-085]took him a fortnight to make the journey, in consequence of the badness

[023-086]of the roads.  Arrived in [[london|London]], he sold his pony for fifteen pounds,

[023-087]and the money kept him until he succeeded in finding employment.  He

[023-088]was so fortunate as to be taken upon trial by a Mr. Bicklewith, who

[023-089]kept an ironmonger's shop in York Yard, Upper [[thames|Thames]] Street; and his

[023-090]first duty there was to clean out the office, put the stools and desks

[023-091]in order for the other clerks, run errands, and act as porter when

[023-092]occasion required.  Young Crawshay was very attentive, industrious, and

[023-093]shrewd; and became known in the office as "The [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]] Boy." Chiefly

[023-094]because of his "cuteness," his master appointed him to the department

[023-095]of selling flat irons.  The London washerwomen of that day were very

[023-096]sharp and not very honest, and it used to be said of them that where

[023-097]they bought one flat iron they generally contrived to steal two.  Mr.

[023-098]Bicklewith thought he could not do better than set the Yorkshireman to

[023-099]watch the washerwomen, and, by way of inducement to him to be vigilant,

[023-100]he gave young Crawshay an interest in that branch of the business,

[023-101]which was soon found to prosper under his charge.  After a few more

[023-102]years, Mr. Bicklewith retired, and left to Crawshay the cast-iron

[023-103]business in York Yard.  This he still further increased, There was not

[023-104]at that time much enterprise in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]], but Crawshay

[023-105]endeavoured to connect himself with what there was of it.  The price of

[023-106]iron was then very high, and the best sorts were still imported from

[023-107]abroad; a good deal of the foreign iron and steel being still landed at

[023-108]the Steelyard on the [[thames|Thames]], in the immediate neighbourhood of

[023-109]Crawshay's ironmongery store.

[023-110]It seems to have occurred to some London capitalists that money was

[023-111]then to be made in the [[iron-industry|iron trade]], and that [[south-wales|South Wales]] was a good

[023-112]field for an experiment.  The soil there was known to be full of coal

[023-113]and ironstone, and several small iron works had for some time been

[023-114]carried on, which were supposed to be doing well.  Merthyr Tydvil was

[023-115]one of the places at which operations had been begun, but the place

[023-116]being situated in a hill district, of difficult access, and the

[023-117]manufacture being still in a very imperfect state, the progress made

[023-118]was for some time very slow.  Land containing coal and iron was deemed

[023-119]of very little value, as maybe inferred from the fact that in the year

[023-120]1765, Mr. [[anthony-bacon|Anthony Bacon]], a man of much foresight, took a lease from

[023-121]Lord Talbot, for 99 years, of the minerals under forty square miles of

[023-122]country surrounding the then insignificant hamlet of Merthyr Tydvil, at

[023-123]the trifling rental of 200L. a-year.  There he erected iron works, and

[023-124]supplied the Government with considerable quantities of cannon and iron

[023-125]for different purposes; and having earned a competency, he retired from

[023-126]business in 1782, subletting his mineral tract in four divisions--the

[023-127][[dowlais-ironworks|Dowlais]], the [[penydarran|Penydarran]], the Cyfartha, and the Plymouth Works, north,

[023-128]east, west, and south, of Merthyr Tydvil.

[023-129]Mr. Richard Crawshay became the lessee of what [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] has called

[023-130]"the Cyfartha flitch of the great Bacon domain."  There he proceeded to

[023-131]carry on the works established by [[anthony-bacon|Mr. Bacon]] with increased spirit; his

[023-132]son William, whom he left in charge of the ironmongery store in London,

[023-133]supplying him with capital to put into the iron works as fast as he

[023-134]could earn it by the retail trade.  In 1787, we find Richard Crawshay

[023-135]manufacturing with difficulty ten tons of bar-iron weekly, and it was

[023-136]of a very inferior character,[11]--the means not having yet been

[023-137]devised at Cyfartha for malleableizing the pit-coal cast-iron with

[023-138]economy or good effect.  Yet Crawshay found a ready market for all the

[023-139]iron he could make, and he is said to have counted the gains of the

[023-140]forge-hammer close by his house at the rate of a penny a stroke.  In

[023-141]course of time he found it necessary to erect new furnaces, and, having

[023-142]adopted the processes invented by Henry Cort, he was thereby enabled

[023-143]greatly to increase the production of his forges, until in 1812 we find

[023-144]him stating to a committee of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] that he was making

[023-145]ten thousand tons of bar-iron yearly, or an average produce of two

[023-146]hundred tons a week.  But this quantity, great though it was, has since

[023-147]been largely increased, the total produce of the Crawshay furnaces of

[023-148]Cyfartha, Ynysfach, and Kirwan, being upwards of 50,000 tons of

[023-149]bar-iron yearly.

[023-150]The distance of Merthyr from [[cardiff|Cardiff]], the nearest port, being

[023-151]considerable, and the cost of carriage being very great by reason of

[023-152]the badness of the roads, [[richard-crawshay|Mr. Crawshay]] set himself to overcome this

[023-153]great impediment to the prosperity of the Merthyr Tydvil district; and,

[023-154]in conjunction with [[samuel-homfray|Mr. Homfray]] of the [[penydarran|[[penydarran|Penydarran]] Works]], he planned and

[023-155]constructed the canal[12] to [[cardiff|Cardiff]], the opening of which, in 1795,

[023-156]gave an immense impetus to the iron trade of the neighbourhood.

[023-157]Numerous other extensive iron works became established there, until

[023-158]Merthyr Tydvil attained the reputation of being at once the richest and

[023-159]the dirtiest district in all Britain.  Mr. Crawshay became known in the

[023-160]west of [[england|England]] as the "Iron King," and was quoted as the highest

[023-161]authority in all questions relating to the trade.  Mr. George Crawshay,

[023-162]recently describing the founder of the family at a social meeting at

[023-163][[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]], said,--"In these days a name like ours is lost in the

[023-164]infinity of great manufacturing firms which exist through out the land;

[023-165]but in those early times the man who opened out the iron district of

[023-166]Wales stood upon an eminence seen by all the world.  It is preserved in

[023-167]the traditions of the family that when the 'Iron King' used to drive

[023-168]from home in his coach-and-four into Wales, all the country turned out

[023-169]to see him, and quite a commotion took place when he passed through

[023-170][[bristol|Bristol]] on his way to the works.  My great grandfather was succeeded by

[023-171]his son, and by his grandson; the Crawshays have followed one another

[023-172]for four generations in the iron trade in Wales, and there they still

[023-173]stand at the head of the trade."  The occasion on which these words

[023-174]were uttered was at a Christmas party, given to the men, about 1300 in

[023-175]number, employed at the iron works of Messrs. [[john-hawks|Hawks]], Crawshay, and Co.,

[023-176]at [[river-tyne|[[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]]-upon-Tyne]].  These works were founded in 1754 by William

[023-177]Hawks, a blacksmith, whose principal trade consisted in making

[023-178]claw-hammers for joiners.  He became a thriving man, and eventually a

[023-179]large manufacturer of bar-iron.  Partners joined him, and in the course

[023-180]of the changes wrought by time, one of the Crawshays, in 1842, became a

[023-181]principal partner in the firm.
