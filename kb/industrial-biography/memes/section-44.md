[044-001]The excellence of Mr. Clement's tools, and his well-known skill in

[044-002]designing and executing work requiring unusual accuracy and finish, led

[044-003]to his being employed by Mr. Babbage to make his celebrated Calculating

[044-004]or Difference Engine.  The contrivance of a machine that should work

[044-005]out complicated sums in arithmetic with perfect precision, was, as may

[044-006]readily be imagined, one of the most difficult feats of the mechanical

[044-007]intellect.  To do this was in an especial sense to stamp matter with

[044-008]the impress of mind, and render it subservient to the highest thinking

[044-009]faculty.  Attempts had been made at an early period to perform

[044-010]arithmetical calculations by mechanical aids more rapidly and precisely

[044-011]than it was possible to do by the operations of the individual mind.

[044-012]The preparation of arithmetical tables of high numbers involved a vast

[044-013]deal of labour, and even with the greatest care errors were unavoidable

[044-014]and numerous.  Thus in a multipltcation-table prepared by a man so

[044-015]eminent as Dr. Hutton for the [[board-of-longitude|Board of Longitude]], no fewer than forty

[044-016]errors were discovered in a single page taken at random.  In the tables

[044-017]of the Nautical Almanac, where the greatest possible precision was

[044-018]desirable and necessary, more than five hundred errors were detected by

[044-019]one person; and the Tables of the Board of Longitude were found equally

[044-020]incorrect.  But such errors were impossible to be avoided so long as

[044-021]the ordinary modes of calculating, transcribing, and printing continued

[044-022]in use.

[044-023]The earliest and simplest form of calculating apparatus was that

[044-024]employed by the schoolboys of ancient Greece, called the Abacus;

[044-025]consisting of a smooth board with a narrow rim, on which they were

[044-026]taught to compute by means of progressive rows of pebbles, bits of bone

[044-027]or ivory, or pieces of silver coin, used as counters.  The same board,

[044-028]strewn over with sand, was used for teaching the rudiments of writing

[044-029]and the principles of geometry.  The Romans subsequently adopted the

[044-030]Abacus, dividing it by means of perpendicular lines or bars, and from

[044-031]the designation of calculus which they gave to each pebble or counter

[044-032]employed on the board, we have derived our English word to calculate.

[044-033]The same instrument continued to be employed during the middle ages,

[044-034]and the table used by the English Court of Exchequer was but a modified

[044-035]form of the Greek Abacus, the chequered lines across it giving the

[044-036]designation to the Court, which still survives.  Tallies, from the

[044-037]French word tailler to cut, were another of the mechanical methods

[044-038]employed to record computations, though in a very rude way.  Step by

[044-039]step improvements were made; the most important being that invented by

[044-040]Napier of Merchiston, the inventor of logarithms, commonly called

[044-041]Napier's bones, consisting of a number of rods divided into ten equal

[044-042]squares and numbered, so that the whole when placed together formed the

[044-043]common multiplication table.  By these means various operations in

[044-044]multiplication and division were performed.  Sir Samuel Morland,

[044-045]Gunter, and Lamb introduced other contrivances, applicable to

[044-046]trigonometry; Gunter's scale being still in common use.  The

[044-047]calculating machines of Gersten and Pascal were of a different kind,

[044-048]working out arithmetical calculations by means of trains of wheels and

[044-049]other arrangements; and that contrived by Lord Stanhope for the purpose

[044-050]of verifying his calculations with respect to the National Debt was of

[044-051]like character.  But none of these will bear for a moment to be

[044-052]compared with the machine designed by Mr. Babbage for performing

[044-053]arithmetical calculations and mathematical analyses, as well as for

[044-054]recording the calculations when made, thereby getting rid entirely of

[044-055]individual error in the operations of calculation, transcription, and

[044-056]printing.

[044-057]The French government, in their desire to promote the extension of the

[044-058]decimal system, had ordered the construction of logarithmical tables of

[044-059]vast extent; but the great labour and expense involved in the

[044-060]undertaking prevented the design from being carried out.  It was

[044-061]reserved for Mr. Babbage to develope the idea by means of a machine

[044-062]which he called the Difference Engine.  This machine is of so

[044-063]complicated a character that it would be impossible for us to give any

[044-064]intelligible description of it in words.  Although Dr. Lardner was

[044-065]unrivalled in the art of describing mechanism, he occupied twenty-five

[044-066]pages of the '[[edinburgh-review|[[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] Review]]' (vol.59) in endeavouring to describe

[044-067]its action, and there were several features in it which he gave up as

[044-068]hopeless.  Some parts of the apparatus and modes of action are indeed

[044-069]extraordinary and perhaps none more so than that for ensuring accuracy

[044-070]in the calculated results,--the machine actually correcting itself, and

[044-071]rubbing itself back into accuracy, when the disposition to err occurs,

[044-072]by the friction of the adjacent machinery!  When an error is made, the

[044-073]wheels become locked and refuse to proceed; thus the machine must go

[044-074]rightly or not at all,--an arrangement as nearly resembling volition as

[044-075]anything that brass and steel are likely to accomplish.

[044-076]This intricate subject was taken up by Mr. Babbage in 1821, when he

[044-077]undertook to superintend for the British government the construction of

[044-078]a machine for calculating and printing mathematical and astronomical

[044-079]tables.  The model first constructed to illustrate the nature of his

[044-080]invention produced figures at the rate of 44 a minute.  In 1823 the

[044-081][[royal-society|Royal Society]] was requested to report upon the invention, and after

[044-082]full inquiry the committee recommended it as one highly deserving of

[044-083]public encouragement.  A sum of 1500L. was then placed at Mr. Babbage's

[044-084]disposal by the Lords of the Treasury for the purpose of enabling him

[044-085]to perfect his invention.  It was at this time that he engaged Mr.

[044-086]Clement as draughtsman and mechanic to embody his ideas in a working

[044-087]machine.  Numerous tools were expressly contrived by the latter for

[044-088]executing the several parts, and workmen were specially educated for

[044-089]the purpose of using them.  Some idea of the elaborate character of the

[044-090]drawings may be formed from the fact that those required for the

[044-091]calculating machinery alone--not to mention the printing machinery,

[044-092]which was almost equally elaborate--covered not less than four hundred

[044-093]square feet of surface!  The cost of executing the calculating machine

[044-094]was of course very great, and the progress of the work was necessarily

[044-095]slow.  The consequence was that the government first became impatient,

[044-096]and then began to grumble at the expense.  At the end of seven years

[044-097]the engineer's bills alone were found to amount to nearly 7200L., and

[044-098]Mr. Babbage's costs out of pocket to 7000L. more.  In order to make

[044-099]more satisfactory progress, it was determined to remove the works to

[044-100]the neighbourhood of Mr. Babbage's own residence; but as Clement's

[044-101]claims for conducting the operations in the new premises were thought

[044-102]exorbitant, and as he himself considered that the work did not yield

[044-103]him the average profit of ordinary employment in his own trade, he

[044-104]eventually withdrew from the enterprise, taking with him the tools

[044-105]which he had constructed for executing the machine.  The government

[044-106]also shortly after withdrew from it, and from that time the scheme was

[044-107]suspended, the Calculating Engine remaining a beautiful but unfinished

[044-108]fragment of a great work.  Though originally intended to go as far as

[044-109]twenty figures, it was only completed to the extent of being capable of

[044-110]calculating to the depth of five figures, and two orders of

[044-111]differences; and only a small part of the proposed printing machinery

[044-112]was ever made.  The engine was placed in the museum of King's College

[044-113]in 1843, enclosed in a glass case, until the year 1862, when it was

[044-114]removed for a time to the [[great-exhibition-1851|Great Exhibition]], where it formed perhaps the

[044-115]most remarkable and beautifully executed piece of mechanism the

[044-116]combined result of intellectual and mechanical contrivance--in the

[044-117]entire collection.[4]

[044-118]Clement was on various other occasions invited to undertake work

[044-119]requiring extra skill, which other mechanics were unwilling or unable

[044-120]to execute.  He was thus always full of employment, never being under

[044-121]the necessity of canvassing for customers.  He was almost constantly in

[044-122]his workshop, in which he took great pride.  His dwelling was over the

[044-123]office in the yard, and it was with difficulty he could be induced to

[044-124]leave the premises.  On one occasion Mr. Brunel of the Great Western

[044-125]Railway called upon him to ask if he could supply him with a superior

[044-126][[steam-whistle]] for his locomotives, the whistles which they were using

[044-127]giving forth very little sound.  Clement examined the specimen brought

[044-128]by Brunel, and pronounced it to be "mere tallow-chandler's work."  He

[044-129]undertook to supply a proper article, and after his usual fashion he

[044-130]proceeded to contrive a machine or tool for the express purpose of

[044-131]making steam-whistles.  They were made and supplied, and when mounted

[044-132]on the locomotive the effect was indeed "screaming."  They were heard

[044-133]miles off, and Brunel, delighted, ordered a hundred.  But when the bill

[044-134]came in, it was found that the charge made for them was very high--as

[044-135]much as 40L. the set.  The company demurred at the price,--Brunel

[044-136]declaring it to be six times more than the price they had before been

[044-137]paying.  "That may be;" rejoined Clement, "but mine are more than six

[044-138]times better.  You ordered a first-rate article, and you must be

[044-139]content to pay for it." The matter was referred to an arbitrator, who

[044-140]awarded the full sum claimed.  Mr. Weld mentions a similar case of an

[044-141]order which Clement received from America to make a large screw of

[044-142]given dimensions "in the best possible manner," and he accordingly

[044-143]proceeded to make one with the greatest mathematical accuracy.  But his

[044-144]bill amounted to some hundreds of pounds, which completely staggered

[044-145]the American, who did not calculate on having to pay more than 20L. at

[044-146]the utmost for the screw.  The matter was, however, referred to

[044-147]arbitrators, who gave their decision, as in the former case, in favour

[044-148]of the mechanic.[5]

[044-149]One of the last works which Clement executed as a matter of pleasure,

[044-150]was the building of an organ for his own use.  It will be remembered

[044-151]that when working as a slater at Great Ashby, he had made flutes and

[044-152]clarinets, and now in his old age he determined to try his skill at

[044-153]making an organ--in his opinion the king of musical instruments.  The

[044-154]building of it became his hobby, and his greatest delight was in

[044-155]superintending its progress.  It cost him about two thousand pounds in

[044-156]labour alone, but he lived to finish it, and we have been informed that

[044-157]it was pronounced a very excellent instrument.

[044-158]Clement was a heavy-browed man, without any polish of manner or speech;

[044-159]for to the last he continued to use his strong Westmoreland dialect.

[044-160]He was not educated in a literary sense; for he read but little, and

[044-161]could write with difficulty.  He was eminently a mechanic, and had

[044-162]achieved his exquisite skill by observation, experience, and

[044-163]reflection.  His head was a complete repertory of inventions, on which

[044-164]he was constantly drawing for the improvement of mechanical practice.

[044-165]Though he had never more than thirty workmen in his factory, they were

[044-166]all of the first class; and the example which Clement set before them

[044-167]of extreme carefulness and accuracy in execution rendered his shop one

[044-168]of the best schools of its time for the training of thoroughly

[044-169]accomplished mechanics.  Mr. Clement died in 1844, in his sixty-fifth

[044-170]year; after which his works were carried on by Mr. Wilkinson, one of

[044-171]his nephews; and his planing machine still continues in useful work.

[044-172][1] On one occasion Galloway had a cast-iron roof made for his

[044-173]workshop, so flat and so independent of ties that the wonder was that

[044-174]it should have stood an hour.  One day Peter Keir, an engineer much

[044-175]employed by the government--a clever man, though some what

[044-176]eccentric--was taken into the shop by Galloway to admire the new roof.

[044-177]Keir, on glancing up at it, immediately exclaimed, "Come outside, and

[044-178]let us speak about it there!"  All that he could say to Galloway

[044-179]respecting the unsoundness of its construction was of no avail.  The

[044-180]fact was that, however Keir might argue about its not being able to

[044-181]stand, there it was actually standing, and that was enough for

[044-182]Galloway.  Keir went home, his mind filled with Galloway's most

[044-183]unprincipled roof.  "If that stands," said he to himself, "all that I

[044-184]have been learning and doing for thirty years has been wrong."  That

[044-185]night he could not sleep for thinking about it.  In the morning he

[044-186]strolled up Primrose Hill, and returned home still muttering to himself

[044-187]about "that roof."  "What," said his wife to him, "are you thinking of

[044-188]Galloway's roof?"  "Yes," said he.  "Then you have seen the papers?"

[044-189]"No--what about them?"  "Galloway's roof has fallen in this morning,

[044-190]and killed eight or ten of the men!" Keir immediately went to bed, and

[044-191]slept soundly till next morning.
