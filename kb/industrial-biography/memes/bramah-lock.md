# Bramah Lock

A high-security lock designed by [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]], incorporating a complex tumbler mechanism that remained unpickable for over 60 years and established Bramah's reputation as a precision engineer

## Background
In the 18th century, most locks were simple ward locks easily picked or forced. Bramah, a cabinet-maker turned engineer, recognised the need for a lock based on mechanical principles that could not be defeated by conventional picking methods.

## Inventor
- **Inventor**: [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] (1748-1814)
- **Patent**: 1784
- **Public challenge**: Bramah offered 200 guineas to anyone who could pick his lock. It remained unpicked for 67 years, until an American locksmith succeeded at the [[great-exhibition-1851|Great Exhibition]] of 1851 after 51 hours of effort.

## How It Works
The Bramah lock used a cylindrical key with longitudinal slots of different depths pressed into a rotating barrel containing sliding plates. Only when all plates were depressed to their correct depths by the key could the barrel rotate and disengage the bolt. The mechanism required precision machining far beyond that needed for ward locks.

## Significance
The Bramah lock established Bramah's reputation and led directly to his engineering career. The precision required to manufacture the lock mechanisms drove Bramah to develop improved machine tools and to hire [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], whose work at Bramah's shop launched the machine tool revolution. The lock thus indirectly contributed to the birth of precision engineering.
