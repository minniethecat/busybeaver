# Ketley

Ketley in [[shropshire|Shropshire]] was one of the principal ironworks of the Reynolds family, associates of the Darbys at [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]].

## History

[[richard-reynolds|Richard Reynolds]] entered upon the conduct of the iron and coal works at Ketley and Horsehay after marrying Hannah Darby, daughter of [[abraham-darby-i|Abraham Darby I]]I. He resided at Ketley for six years before removing to [[coalbrookdale|Coalbrookdale]] in 1763 to take charge of the works there on the death of his father-in-law. (016-087)

Reynolds later determined to give up his shares in the ironworks at Ketley to his sons William and Joseph, who continued to carry them on. [[william-reynolds|William Reynolds]] was a man of eminent ability, well versed in science, and an excellent mechanic. (017-150) He introduced great improvements in the working of the coal and iron operations.

## Inclined Plane Innovation

It was William Reynolds of Ketley who stepped from the accustomed path of canal engineering and constructed the first inclined plane, introducing boats of five tons. This was deemed a visionary project by many, particularly by the Duke of Bridgewater, who was partial to locks; yet the inclined plane also succeeded. (017-167) William Reynolds also provided valuable assistance in planning the iron aqueduct by means of which the [[ellesmere-canal|Ellesmere Canal]] was carried over the Pont Cysylltau, executing the necessary castings for the purpose at the Ketley foundry. (017-176)

## Puddling Trials

The Ketley works played a role in the development of the [[puddling-process|puddling]] process. When [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] was promoting his new method of manufacturing [[wrought-iron|bar iron]], Richard Reynolds invited him to conduct a trial of his process at Ketley. (022-100) William Reynolds recorded in his memorandum-book that he witnessed Cort at Ketley on December 17, 1784, produce from the same pig iron both cold short and tough iron by a variation of the process used in reducing cast-iron to malleable bar-iron. (024-047)

## Life at The Bank

When living at The Bank, near Ketley, it was Richard Reynolds's great delight in the summer evenings to retire with his pipe to a rural seat commanding a full view of the Wrekin, the Ercall Woods, with Cader Idris and the Montgomeryshire hills in the distance, and watch the sun go down. (018-013) Lord Thurlow once paid a visit to the Ketley Iron-Works and rode a race with Reynolds — observing afterwards, laughing, that it was probably the first time a Lord Chancellor had ridden a race with a Quaker. (018-049)

## Industrial Significance

An important site in the industrial geography of Britain during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], Ketley was a center of both iron production and canal-engineering innovation under the Reynolds family.
