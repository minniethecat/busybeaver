The refined, malleable form of iron that was the primary structural metal of the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] — produced from [[pig-iron|cast iron]] by removing carbon and impurities through [[puddling-process|puddling]], then forged or rolled into bars, plates, and sections for construction, shipbuilding, and machinery.

## Definition

Wrought iron is a low-carbon iron alloy (typically less than 0.1% carbon) containing dispersed slag fibres that give it a distinctive fibrous grain structure. Unlike brittle [[pig-iron|cast iron]] (2–4% carbon) or hard steel (0.2–2% carbon), wrought iron is tough, ductile, and can be forged, rolled, and welded. The term "wrought" comes from the past participle of "work" — it is iron that has been worked or forged into shape. (019-095)

The conversion of "crude or [[pig-iron|cast iron]] into malleable or bar iron" was the central technical challenge of the iron industry before the 19th century. Until the [[puddling-process|puddling]] process was perfected, this conversion was slow, expensive, and dependent on charcoal. (016-111) The "alteration from the quality of pig iron into that of bar iron" required removing most of the carbon — a chemical transformation that was poorly understood but empirically mastered over centuries. (016-139)

## Historical Development

### Pre-Puddling Methods

Before the 18th century, wrought iron was produced in two stages:
1. **The Bloomery**: The earliest method, in which iron ore was reduced directly to a spongy mass of iron (the bloom) in a small hearth. The bloom was then repeatedly heated and hammered to expel slag. This was slow, labour-intensive, and could only produce small quantities.
2. **The Finery Forge**: A two-stage process where pig iron from the blast furnace was remelted in a charcoal-fired finery hearth. An oxidising blast burned off carbon, producing a "loop" or bloom of malleable iron, which was then forged under a water-powered hammer.

Both methods required charcoal, which limited production scale. The breakthrough came with the recognition that pit-coal could be used if the fuel was kept separate from the metal.

### The Puddling Revolution

The [[puddling-process|puddling]] process, perfected by [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]] in 1784, transformed wrought iron production. In Cort's method, pig iron was melted in a reverberatory furnace where the fuel (coal) did not contact the metal. A workman stirred the molten iron, exposing it to oxidising gases that burned off carbon and silicon. As the carbon content dropped, the melting point rose and the iron "came to nature" — solidifying into pasty lumps. (016-160)

These lumps were gathered into balls, forged under a hammer to squeeze out slag, and then passed through grooved rollers to form uniform bars. The entire sequence — "[[puddling-process|puddling]], piling, welding, and working the bar-iron through grooved rollers" — was perfected by Cort and remained essentially unchanged for over a century. (022-042)

### Mass Production Era

By the mid-19th century, the production of wrought iron had reached industrial scale. The steps "by which the manufacture of bar-iron by means of pit-coal" had been established — coke smelting of ore → pig iron → [[puddling-process|puddling]] → shingling → rolling — constituted a complete industrial system. (021-120) Britain's annual production of wrought iron rose from tens of thousands of tons in 1800 to over three million tons by 1870.

## Properties and Uses

Wrought iron's unique properties made it the premier structural material of the 19th century:

- **Toughness and ductility**: Unlike [[pig-iron|cast iron]], wrought iron bends before breaking — critical for beams, chains, and ships' hulls
- **Weldability**: Wrought iron can be forge-welded at white heat, enabling the fabrication of large assemblies from smaller pieces
- **Corrosion resistance**: The slag fibres create a protective layer that resists atmospheric corrosion better than mild steel
- **Workability**: Can be forged, rolled, drawn into wire, or hammered into sheets

Major applications included:
- Railway rails and bridges (the [[tubular-bridge|Britannia Bridge]], the Forth Bridge)
- Ship hulls and boilers (the SS Great Britain, the first iron ocean liner)
- Structural beams and columns for mills, warehouses, and later skyscrapers
- Chains, anchors, and rigging for the [[admiralty|Royal Navy]]
- Ornamental ironwork — gates, railings, and architectural details

Experiments on the "strength of wrought iron" were conducted by engineers including Bramah and Telford, establishing the engineering data needed for safe structural design. (002-179)

## Decline

Wrought iron was gradually superseded by mild steel after the Bessemer process (1856) and the open-hearth furnace (1860s) made cheap, high-quality steel available in bulk. Mild steel offered similar ductility with greater strength and consistency, and could be produced at lower cost. By the early 20th century, wrought iron production had largely ceased, though it remains prized for restoration work and ornamental applications.
