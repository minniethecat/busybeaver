The Scottish Highlands were a region of traditional iron-working and armour-making that contributed skilled labour to the Scottish [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]], particularly through the migration of craftsmen to centres such as [[Carron Iron Works]].

## Geography and Early Iron-Working

The Highlands offered iron-ore deposits and wood fuel that supported small-scale smelting and smithing from an early period. Iron tools and weapons were produced across the region long before the rise of coke-fired blast furnaces in the Lowlands. (002-155)

## Armourer Tradition

In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the Highlands supported a notable armourer craft. Ironmongery and weapon-making were practised across the region, and skilled Highland armourers gained a reputation for quality. (005-083) One great armourer working in the Highlands employed a number of craftsmen, producing arms that were valued beyond the region. (005-091) (005-106)

## Connection to the Industrial Revolution

By the late eighteenth century, Highland iron-working communities became a source of labour for the expanding iron industry further south. Workers travelled from remote parts of the Highlands to find employment at the Carron Iron Works and other Lowland industrial sites. (027-165) During the early industrial era, William Fairbairn's mother, while in the Highlands, made her own anchors and demonstrated the self-reliant craft tradition of the region. (052-087)

Andrew Yarranton visited the Highlands as well as the Lowlands in 1637 during his survey of British iron-making, noting the region's industrial potential. (011-181)
