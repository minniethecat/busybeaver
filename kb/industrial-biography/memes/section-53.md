[053-001]In due course of time our young engineer was removed from the workshop,

[053-002]and appointed to take charge of the pumps of the mine and the

[053-003][[steam-engine]] by which they were kept in work.  This employment was more

[053-004]to his taste, gave him better "insight," and afforded him greater

[053-005]opportunities for improvement.  The work was, however, very trying, and

[053-006]at times severe, especially in winter, the engineer being liable to be

[053-007]drenched with water every time that he descended the shaft to regulate

[053-008]the working of the pumps; but, thanks to a stout constitution, he bore

[053-009]through these exposures without injury, though others sank under them.

[053-010]At this period he had the advantage of occasional days of leisure, to

[053-011]which he was entitled by reason of his nightwork; and during such

[053-012]leisure he usually applied himself to reading and study.

[053-013]It was about this time that [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]] made the acquaintance of

[053-014][[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]], while the latter was employed in working the

[053-015]ballast-engine at Willington Quay.  He greatly admired George as a

[053-016]workman, and was accustomed in the summer evenings to go over to the

[053-017]Quay occasionally and take charge of George's engine, to enable him to

[053-018]earn a few shillings extra by heaving ballast out of the collier

[053-019]vessels.  Stephenson's zeal in the pursuit of mechanical knowledge

[053-020]probably was not without its influence in stimulating [[william-fairbairn|William Fairbairn]]

[053-021]himself to carry on so diligently the work of self-culture.  But little

[053-022]could the latter have dreamt, while serving his apprenticeship at Percy

[053-023]Main, that his friend [[george-stephenson|George Stephenson]], the brakesman, should yet be

[053-024]recognised as among the greatest engineers of his age, and that he

[053-025]himself should have the opportunity, in his capacity of President of

[053-026]the Institute of Mechanical Engineers at [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]], of making public

[053-027]acknowledgment of the opportunities for education which he had enjoyed

[053-028]in that neighbourhood in his early years.[2]

[053-029]Having finished his five years' apprenticeship at Percy Main, by which

[053-030]time he had reached his twenty-first year, William Fairbairn shortly

[053-031]after determined to go forth into the world in search of experience.

[053-032]At [[newcastle-upon-tyne|Newcastle]] he found employment as a millwright for a few weeks,

[053-033]during which he worked at the erection of a sawmill in the Close.  From

[053-034]thence he went to Bedlington at an advanced wage.  He remained there

[053-035]for six months, during which he was so fortunate as to make the

[053-036]acquaintance of Miss Mar, who five years after, when his wanderings had

[053-037]ceased, became his wife.  On the completion of the job on which he had

[053-038]been employed, our engineer prepared to make another change.  Work was

[053-039]difficult to be had in the North, and, joined by a comrade, he resolved

[053-040]to try his fortune in [[london|London]].  Adopting the cheapest route, he took

[053-041]passage by a Shields collier, in which he sailed for the [[thames|Thames]] on the

[053-042]11th of December, 1811.  It was then war-time, and the vessel was very

[053-043]short-handed, the crew consisting only of three old men and three boys,

[053-044]with the skipper and mate; so that the vessel was no sooner fairly at

[053-045]sea than both the passenger youths had to lend a hand in working her,

[053-046]and this continued for the greater part of the voyage.  The weather was

[053-047]very rough, and in consequence of the captain's anxiety to avoid

[053-048]privateers he hugged the shore too close, and when navigating the

[053-049]inside passage of the Swin, between Yarmouth and the Nore, the vessel

[053-050]very narrowly escaped shipwreck.  After beating about along shore, the

[053-051]captain half drunk the greater part of the time, the vessel at last

[053-052]reached the [[thames|Thames]] with loss of spars and an anchor, after a tedious

[053-053]voyage of fourteen days.

[053-054]On arriving off Blackwall the captain went ashore ostensibly in search

[053-055]of the Coal Exchange, taking our young engineer with him.  The former

[053-056]was still under the influence of drink; and though he failed to reach

[053-057]the Exchange that night, he succeeded in reaching a public house in

[053-058]Wapping, beyond which he could not be got.  At ten o'clock the two

[053-059]started on their return to the ship; but the captain took the

[053-060]opportunity of the darkness to separate from his companion, and did not

[053-061]reach the ship until next morning.  It afterwards came out that he had

[053-062]been taken up and lodged in the watch-house.  The youth, left alone in

[053-063]the streets of the strange city, felt himself in an awkward dilemma.

[053-064]He asked the next watchman he met to recommend him to a lodging, on

[053-065]which the man took him to a house in New Gravel Lane, where he

[053-066]succeeded in finding accommodation.  What was his horror next morning

[053-067]to learn that a whole family--the Williamsons--had been murdered in the

[053-068]very next house during the night!  Making the best of his way back to

[053-069]the ship, he found that his comrade, who had suffered dreadfully from

[053-070]sea-sickness during the voyage, had nearly recovered, and was able to

[053-071]accompany him into the City in search of work.  They had between them a

[053-072]sum of only about eight pounds, so that it was necessary for them to

[053-073]take immediate steps to obtain employment.

[053-074]They thought themselves fortunate in getting the promise of a job from

[053-075][[george-rennie|Mr. Rennie]], the celebrated engineer, whose works were situated at the

[053-076]south end of [[blackfriars-bridge|Blackfriars Bridge]].  [[george-rennie|Mr. Rennie]] sent the two young men to

[053-077]his foreman, with the request that he should set them to work.  The

[053-078]foreman referred them to the secretary of the [[millwright|Millwright]]s' Society, the

[053-079]shop being filled with Union men, who set their shoulders together to

[053-080]exclude those of their own grade, however skilled, who could not

[053-081]produce evidence that they had complied with the rules of the trade.

[053-082]Describing his first experience of [[london|London]] Unionists, nearly half a

[053-083]century later, before an assembly of working men at [[derby|Derby]], Mr.

[053-084]Fairbairn said, "When I first entered London, a young man from the

[053-085]country had no chance whatever of success, in consequence of the trade

[053-086]guilds and unions.  I had no difficulty in finding employment, but

[053-087]before I could begin work I had to run the gauntlet of the trade

[053-088]societies; and after dancing attendance for nearly six weeks, with very

[053-089]little money in my pocket, and having to 'box Harry' all the time, I

[053-090]was ultimately declared illegitimate, and sent adrift to seek my

[053-091]fortune elsewhere.  There were then three millwright societies in

[053-092]London:  one called the Old Society, another the New Society, and a

[053-093]third the Independent Society.  These societies were not founded for

[053-094]the protection of the trade, but for the maintenance of high wages, and

[053-095]for the exclusion of all those who could not assert their claims to

[053-096]work in London and other corporate towns.  Laws of a most arbitrary

[053-097]character were enforced, and they were governed by cliques of

[053-098]self-appointed officers, who never failed to take care of their own

[053-099]interests." [3]

[053-100]Their first application for leave to work in London having thus

[053-101]disastrously ended, the two youths determined to try their fortune in

[053-102]the country, and with aching hearts they started next morning before

[053-103]daylight.  Their hopes had been suddenly crushed, their slender funds

[053-104]were nearly exhausted, and they scarce knew where to turn.  But they

[053-105]set their faces bravely northward, and pushed along the high road,

[053-106]through slush and snow, as far as Hertford, which they reached after

[053-107]nearly eight hours' walking, on the moderate fare during their journey

[053-108]of a penny roll and a pint of ale each.  Though wet to the skin, they

[053-109]immediately sought out a master millwright, and applied for work.  He

[053-110]said he had no job vacant at present; but, seeing their sorry plight,

[053-111]he had compassion upon them, and said, "Though I cannot give you

[053-112]employment, you seem to be two nice lads;" and he concluded by offering

[053-113]Fairbairn a half-crown.  But his proud spirit revolted at taking money

[053-114]which he had not earned; and he declined the proffered gift with

[053-115]thanks, saying he was sorry they could not have work.  He then turned

[053-116]away from the door, on which his companion, mortified by his refusal to

[053-117]accept the half-crown at a time when they were reduced almost to their

[053-118]last penny, broke out in bitter remonstrances and regrets.  Weary, wet,

[053-119]and disheartened, the two turned into Hertford churchyard, and rested

[053-120]for a while upon a tombstone, Fairbairn's companion relieving himself

[053-121]by a good cry, and occasional angry outbursts of "Why didn't you take

[053-122]the half-crown?"  "Come, come, man!" said Fairbairn, "it's of no use

[053-123]crying; cheer up; let's try another road; something must soon cast up."

[053-124]They rose, and set out again, but when they reached the bridge, the

[053-125]dispirited youth again broke down; and, leaning his back against the

[053-126]parapet, said, "I winna gang a bit further; let's get back to London."

[053-127]Against this Fairbairn remonstrated, saying "It's of no use lamenting;

[053-128]we must try what we can do here; if the worst comes to the worst, we

[053-129]can 'list; you are a strong chap--they'll soon take you; and as for me,

[053-130]I'll join too; I think I could fight a bit."  After this council of

[053-131]war, the pair determined to find lodgings in the town for the night,

[053-132]and begin their search for work anew on the morrow.

[053-133]Next day, when passing along one of the back streets of Hertford, they

[053-134]came to a wheelwright's shop, where they made the usual enquiries.  The

[053-135]wheelwright, said that he did not think there was any job to be had in

[053-136]the town; but if the two young men pushed on to Cheshunt, he thought

[053-137]they might find work at a windmill which was under contract to be

[053-138]finished in three weeks, and where the millwright wanted hands.  Here

[053-139]was a glimpse of hope at last; and the strength and spirits of both

[053-140]revived in an instant.  They set out immediately; walked the seven

[053-141]miles to Cheshunt; succeeded in obtaining the expected employment;

[053-142]worked at the job a fortnight; and entered London again with nearly

[053-143]three pounds in their pockets.

[053-144]Our young millwright at length succeeded in obtaining regular

[053-145]employment in the metropolis at good wages.  He worked first at

[053-146]Grundy's Patent Ropery at Shadwell, and afterwards at Mr. Penn's of

[053-147][[greenwich|Greenwich]], gaining much valuable insight, and sedulously improving his

[053-148]mind by study in his leisure hours.  Among the acquaintances he then

[053-149]made was an enthusiastic projector of the name of Hall, who had taken

[053-150]out one patent for making hemp from bean-stalks, and contemplated

[053-151]taking out another for effecting spade tillage by steam.  The young

[053-152]engineer was invited to make the requisite model, which he did, and it

[053-153]cost him both time and money, which the out-at-elbows projector was

[053-154]unable to repay; and all that came of the project was the exhibition of

[053-155]the model at the [[society-of-arts|Society of Arts]] and before the Board of Agriculture,

[053-156]in whose collection it is probably still to be found.  Another more

[053-157]successful machine constructed By Mr. Fairbairn about the same time was

[053-158]a sausage-chopping machine, which he contrived and made for a

[053-159]pork-butcher for 33l.  It was the first order he had ever had on his

[053-160]own account; and, as the machine when made did its work admirably, he

[053-161]was naturally very proud of it.  The machine was provided with a

[053-162]fly-wheel and double crank, with connecting rods which worked a cross

[053-163]head.  It contained a dozen knives crossing each other at right angles

[053-164]in such a way as to enable them to mince or divide the meat on a

[053-165]revolving block.  Another part of the apparatus accomplished the

[053-166]filling of the sausages in a very expert manner, to the entire

[053-167]satisfaction of the pork-butcher.

[053-168]As work was scarce in London at the time, and our engineer was bent on

[053-169]gathering further experience in his trade, he determined to make a tour

[053-170]in the South of [[england|England]] and [[south-wales|South Wales]]; and set out from London in

[053-171]April 1813 with 7L. in his pocket.  After visiting Bath and Frome, he

[053-172]settled to work for six weeks at Bathgate; after which he travelled by

[053-173][[bradford|Bradford]] and Trowbridge--always on foot--to [[bristol|Bristol]].  From thence he

[053-174]travelled through [[south-wales|South Wales]], spending a few days each at [[newport|Newport]],

[053-175]Llandaff, and [[cardiff|Cardiff]], where he took ship for Dublin.  By the time he

[053-176]reached Ireland his means were all but exhausted, only three-halfpence

[053-177]remaining in his pocket; but, being young, hopeful, skilful, and

[053-178]industrious, he was light of heart, and looked cheerfully forward.  The

[053-179]next day he succeeded in finding employment at Mr. Robinson's, of the

[053-180]Phoenix [[foundry|Foundry]], where he was put to work at once upon a set of

[053-181]patterns for some nail-machinery.  Mr. Robinson was a man of spirit and

[053-182]enterprise, and, seeing the quantities of English machine-made nails

[053-183]imported into Ireland, he was desirous of giving Irish industry the

[053-184]benefit of the manufacture.  The construction of the nail-making

[053-185]machinery occupied Mr. Fairbairn the entire summer; and on its

[053-186]completion he set sail in the month of October for [[liverpool|Liverpool]].  It may

[053-187]be added, that, notwithstanding the expense incurred by Mr. Robinson in

[053-188]setting up the new nail-machinery, his workmen threatened him with a

[053-189]strike if he ventured to use it.  As he could not brave the opposition

[053-190]of the Unionists, then all-powerful in Dublin, the machinery was never

[053-191]set to work; the nail-making trade left Ireland, never to return; and

[053-192]the Irish market was thenceforward supplied entirely with English-made

[053-193]nails.  The Dublin iron-manufacture was ruined in the same way; not

[053-194]through any local disadvantages, but solely by the prohibitory

[053-195]regulations enforced by the workmen of the Trades Unions.
