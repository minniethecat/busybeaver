# [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich]]

Woolwich, on the south bank of the [[thames|Thames]] below [[london|London]], was the site of the [[woolwich-arsenal|Royal Arsenal]] and the birthplace of [[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]], the founding father of machine-tool engineering. The Arsenal smithy where Maudslay began his career was one of the most important centres of mechanical innovation in late eighteenth-century Britain. (035-090) (036-003)

## Industrial Significance

### [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich Arsenal]]

The [[woolwich-arsenal|Royal Arsenal]] at Woolwich was a vast government manufactory employing thousands of workmen in the production of ordnance, small arms, and military stores. A powder-boy was employed in the Arsenal as early as the [[james-brindley|Brindley]] era, and the site maintained its pre-eminence through successive generations of military engineers. (002-029)

The Arsenal's workshops contained some of the earliest applications of steam power and precision machinery to government manufacturing. The principle of [[henry-maudslay|Bramah]]'s hydraulic press was demonstrated at [[woolwich-arsenal|Woolwich Arsenal]], and the museum there held important collections of early ordnance. (034-050) (012-007)

### Maudslay's Early Years

[[henry-maudslay|Henry Maudslay]] was born at Woolwich towards the end of the eighteenth century, the son of a skilled workman at the Arsenal. He began his working life in the smithy of the Arsenal, where he acquired the forging skills that would later distinguish his engineering career. (035-090) (035-142)

After being invalided from sea service, Maudslay returned to Woolwich, where the subject of this memoir began to attract attention for the extraordinary quality of his smith's work. The Woolwich housewives in particular supported the young smith, commissioning ironwork that displayed his growing mastery. (035-112) (035-117) (035-146)

### Later Connection

Maudslay retained a lifelong affection for Woolwich. He was accustomed to walk down to Woolwich from [[london|London]] in his later years, and after his death he was buried there at his own request, having ever a warm heart for the place of his birth and early training. (036-060) (041-035) (041-037) (041-048)
