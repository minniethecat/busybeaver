[004-001]We can only conjecture how the art of smelting iron was discovered.

[004-002]Who first applied fire to the ore, and made it plastic; who discovered

[004-003]fire itself, and its uses in metallurgy? No one can tell.  Tradition

[004-004]says that the metal was discovered through the accidental burning of a

[004-005]wood in Greece.  [[david-mushet|Mr. Mushet]] thinks it more probable that the discovery

[004-006]was made on the conversion of wood into charcoal for culinary or

[004-007]chamber purposes.  "If a mass of ore," he says, "accidentally dropped

[004-008]into the middle of the burning pile during a period of neglect, or

[004-009]during the existence of a thorough draught, a mixed mass, partly earthy

[004-010]and partly metallic, would be obtained, possessing ductility and

[004-011]extension under pressure.  But if the conjecture is pushed still

[004-012]further, and we suppose that the ore was not an oxide, but rich in

[004-013]iron, magnetic or spicular, the result would in all probability be a

[004-014]mass of perfectly [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]].  I have seen this fact illustrated in

[004-015]the roasting of a species of iron-stone, which was united with a

[004-016]considerable mass of bituminous matter.  After a high temperature had

[004-017]been excited in the interior of the pile, plates of [[wrought-iron|malleable iron]] of a

[004-018]tough and flexible nature were formed, and under circumstances where

[004-019]there was no fuel but that furnished by the ore itself." [9]

[004-020]The metal once discovered, many attempts would be made to give to that

[004-021]which had been the effect of accident a more unerring result.  The

[004-022]smelting of ore in an open heap of wood or charcoal being found tedious

[004-023]and wasteful, as well as uncertain, would naturally lead to the

[004-024]invention of a furnace; with the object of keeping the ore surrounded

[004-025]as much as possible with fuel while the process of conversion into iron

[004-026]was going forward.  The low conical furnaces employed at this day by

[004-027]some of the tribes of Central and Southern Africa, are perhaps very

[004-028]much the same in character as those adopted by the early tribes of all

[004-029]countries where iron was first made.  Small openings at the lower end

[004-030]of the cone to admit the air, and a larger orifice at the top, would,

[004-031]with charcoal, be sufficient to produce the requisite degree of heat

[004-032]for the reduction of the ore.  To this the foot-blast was added, as

[004-033]still used in Ceylon and in India; and afterwards the water-blast, as

[004-034]employed in Spain (where it is known as the Catalan forge), along the

[004-035]coasts of the Mediterranean, and in some parts of America.

[004-036]It is worthy of remark, that the ruder the method employed for the

[004-037]reduction of the ore, the better the quality of the iron usually is.

[004-038]Where the art is little advanced, only the most tractable ores are

[004-039]selected; and as charcoal is the only fuel used, the quality of the

[004-040]metal is almost invariably excellent.  The ore being long exposed to

[004-041]the charcoal fire, and the quantity made small, the result is a metal

[004-042]having many of the qualities of steel, capable of being used for

[004-043]weapons or tools after a comparatively small amount of forging.  Dr.

[004-044]Livingstone speaks of the excellent quality of the iron made by the

[004-045]African tribes on the Zambesi, who refuse to use ordinary English iron,

[004-046]which they consider "rotten." [10]  Du Chaillu also says of the Fans,

[004-047]that, in making their best knives and arrow-heads, they will not use

[004-048]European or American iron, greatly preferring their own.  The

[004-049]celebrated wootz or steel of India, made in little cakes of only about

[004-050]two pounds weight, possesses qualities which no European steel can

[004-051]surpass.  Out of this material the famous Damascus sword-blades were

[004-052]made; and its use for so long a period is perhaps one of the most

[004-053]striking proofs of the ancient civilization of India.

[004-054]The early history of iron in Britain is necessarily very obscure.  When

[004-055]the Romans invaded the country, the metal seems to have been already

[004-056]known to the tribes along the coast.  The natives had probably smelted

[004-057]it themselves in their rude bloomeries, or obtained it from the

[004-058]Phoenicians in small quantities in exchange for skins and food, or tin.

[004-059]We must, however, regard the stories told of the ancient British

[004-060]chariots armed with swords or scythes as altogether apocryphal.  The

[004-061]existence of iron in sufficient quantity to be used for such a purpose

[004-062]is incompatible with contemporary facts, and unsupported by a single

[004-063]vestige remaining to our time.  The country was then mostly forest, and

[004-064]the roads did not as yet exist upon which chariots could be used;

[004-065]whilst iron was too scarce to be mounted as scythes upon chariots, when

[004-066]the warriors themselves wanted it for swords.  The orator Cicero, in a

[004-067]letter to Trebatius, then serving with the army in Britain,

[004-068]sarcastically advised him to capture and convey one of these vehicles

[004-069]to Italy for exhibition; but we do not hear that any specimen of the

[004-070]British war-chariot was ever seen in Rome.

[004-071]It is only in the tumuli along the coast, or in those of the

[004-072]Romano-British period, that iron implements are ever found; whilst in

[004-073]the ancient burying places of the interior of the country they are

[004-074]altogether wanting.  Herodian says of the British pursued by Severus

[004-075]through the fens and marshes of the east coast, that they wore iron

[004-076]hoops round their middles and their necks, esteeming them as ornaments

[004-077]and tokens of riches, in like manner as other barbarous people then

[004-078]esteemed ornaments of silver and gold.  Their only money, according to

[004-079]Caesar, consisted of pieces of brass or iron, reduced to a certain

[004-080]standard weight.[11]  It is particularly important to observe, says M.

[004-081]Worsaae, that all the antiquities which have hitherto been found in the

[004-082]large burying places of the Iron period, in Switzerland, Bavaria,

[004-083]Baden, France, [[england|England]], and the North, exhibit traces more or less of

[004-084]Roman influence.[12]  The Romans themselves used weapons of bronze when

[004-085]they could not obtain iron in sufficient quantity, and many of the

[004-086]Roman weapons dug out of the ancient tumuli are of that metal.  They

[004-087]possessed the art of tempering and hardening bronze to such a degree as

[004-088]to enable them to manufacture swords with it of a pretty good edge; and

[004-089]in those countries which they penetrated, their bronze implements

[004-090]gradually supplanted those which had been previously fashioned of

[004-091]stone.  Great quantities of bronze tools have been found in different

[004-092]parts of [[england|England]],--sometimes in heaps, as if they had been thrown away

[004-093]in basketfuls as things of little value.  It has been conjectured that

[004-094]when the Romans came into Britain they found the inhabitants,

[004-095]especially those to the northward, in very nearly the same state as

[004-096]Captain Cook and other voyagers found the inhabitants of the South Sea

[004-097]Islands; that the Britons parted with their food and valuables for

[004-098]tools of inferior metal made in imitation of their stone ones; but

[004-099]finding themselves cheated by the Romans, as the natives of Otaheite

[004-100]have been cheated by Europeans, the Britons relinquished the bad tools

[004-101]when they became acquainted with articles made of better metal.[13]

[004-102]The Roman colonists were the first makers of iron in Britain on any

[004-103]large scale.  They availed themselves of the mineral riches of the

[004-104]country wherever they went.  Every year brings their extraordinary

[004-105]industrial activity more clearly to light.  They not only occupied the

[004-106]best sites for trade, intersected the land with a complete system of

[004-107]well-constructed roads, studded our hills and valleys with towns,

[004-108]villages, and pleasure-houses, and availed themselves of our medicinal

[004-109]springs for purposes of baths to an extent not even exceeded at this

[004-110]day, but they explored our mines and quarries, and carried on the

[004-111]smelting and manufacture of metals in nearly all parts of the island.

[004-112]The heaps of mining refuse left by them in the valleys and along the

[004-113]hill-sides of North [[derbyshire|Derbyshire]] are still spoken of by the country

[004-114]people as "old man," or the "old man's work."  Year by year, from

[004-115]Dartmoor to the Moray Firth, the plough turns up fresh traces of their

[004-116]indefatigable industry and enterprise, in pigs of lead, implements of

[004-117]iron and bronze, vessels of pottery, coins, and sculpture; and it is a

[004-118]remarkable circumstance that in several districts where the existence

[004-119]of extensive iron beds had not been dreamt of until within the last

[004-120]twenty years, as in Northamptonshire and North [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], the remains

[004-121]of ancient workings recently discovered show that the Roman colonists

[004-122]were fully acquainted with them.

[004-123]But the principal iron mines worked by that people were those which

[004-124]were most conveniently situated for purposes of exportation, more

[004-125]especially in the southern counties and on the borders of Wales.  The

[004-126]extensive cinder heaps found in the--[[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]]--which formed the

[004-127]readiest resource of the modern iron-smelter when improved processes

[004-128]enabled him to reduce them--show that their principal [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]s

[004-129]were carried on in that quarter.[14]  It is indeed matter of history,

[004-130]that about seventeen hundred years since (A.D. 120) the Romans had

[004-131]forges in the West of England, both in the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] and in South

[004-132]Wales; and that they sent the metal from thence to [[bristol|Bristol]], where it

[004-133]was forged and made into weapons for the use of the troops.  Along the

[004-134]banks of the Wye, the ground is in many places a continuous bed of iron

[004-135]cinders, in which numerous remains have been found, furnishing

[004-136]unmistakeable proofs of the Roman furnaces.  At the same time, the iron

[004-137]ores of [[sussex|Sussex]] were extensively worked, as appears from the cinder

[004-138]heaps found at Maresfield and several places in that county, intermixed

[004-139]with Roman pottery, coins, and other remains.  In a bed of scoriae

[004-140]several acres in extent, at Old Land Farm in Maresfield, the Rev. Mr.

[004-141]Turner found the remains of Roman pottery so numerous that scarcely a

[004-142]barrow-load of cinders was removed that did not contain several

[004-143]fragments, together with coins of the reigns of Nero, Vespasian, and

[004-144]Dioclesian.[15]  In the turbulent infancy of nations it is to be

[004-145]expected that we should hear more of the Smith, or worker in iron, in

[004-146]connexion with war, than with more peaceful pursuits.  Although he was

[004-147]a nail-maker and a horse-shoer--made axes, chisels, saws, and hammers

[004-148]for the artificer--spades and hoes for the farmer--bolts and fastenings

[004-149]for the lord's castle-gates, and chains for his draw-bridge--it was

[004-150]principally because of his skill in armour-work that he was esteemed.

[004-151]He made and mended the weapons used in the chase and in war--the

[004-152]gavelocs, bills, and battle-axes; he tipped the bowmen's arrows, and

[004-153]furnished spear-heads for the men-at-arms; but, above all, he forged

[004-154]the mail-coats and cuirasses of the chiefs, and welded their swords, on

[004-155]the temper and quality of which, life, honour, and victory in battle

[004-156]depended.  Hence the great estimation in which the smith was held in

[004-157]the Anglo-Saxon times.  His person was protected by a double penalty.

[004-158]He was treated as an officer of the highest rank, and awarded the first

[004-159]place in precedency.  After him ranked the maker of mead, and then the

[004-160]physician.  In the royal court of Wales he sat in the great hall with

[004-161]the king and queen, next to the domestic chaplain; and even at that

[004-162]early day there seems to have been a hot spark in the smith's throat

[004-163]which needed much quenching; for he was "entitled to a draught of every

[004-164]kind of liquor that was brought into the hall."

[004-165]The smith was thus a mighty man.  The Saxon Chronicle describes the

[004-166]valiant knight himself as a "mighty war-smith."  But the smith was

[004-167]greatest of all in his forging of swords; and the bards were wont to

[004-168]sing the praises of the knight's "good sword" and of the smith who made

[004-169]it, as well as of the knight himself who wielded it in battle.  The

[004-170]most extraordinary powers were attributed to the weapon of steel when

[004-171]first invented.  Its sharpness seemed so marvellous when compared with

[004-172]one of bronze, that with the vulgar nothing but magic could account for

[004-173]it.  Traditions, enshrined in fairy tales, still survive in most

[004-174]countries, illustrative of its magical properties.  The weapon of

[004-175]bronze was dull; but that of steel was bright--the "white sword of

[004-176]light," one touch of which broke spells, liberated enchanted

[004-177]princesses, and froze giants' marrow.  King Arthur's magic sword

[004-178]"Excalibur" was regarded as almost heroic in the romance of

[004-179]chivalry.[16]  So were the swords "Galatin" of Sir Gawain, and

[004-180]"Joyeuse" of Charlemagne, both of which were reputed to be the work of

[004-181]Weland the Smith, about whose name clusters so much traditional glory

[004-182]as an ancient worker in metals.[17]  The heroes of the Northmen in like

[004-183]manner wielded magic swords.  Olave the Norwegian possessed the sword

[004-184]"Macabuin," forged by the dark smith of Drontheim, whose feats are

[004-185]recorded in the tales of the Scalds.  And so, in like manner,

[004-186]traditions of the supernatural power of the blacksmith are found

[004-187]existing to this day all over the [[highlands|Scottish Highlands]].[18]  When William

[004-188]the Norman invaded Britain, he was well supplied with smiths.  His

[004-189]followers were clad in armour of steel, and furnished with the best

[004-190]weapons of the time.  Indeed, their superiority in this respect is

[004-191]supposed to have been the principal cause of William's victory over

[004-192]Harold; for the men of both armies were equal in point of bravery.  The

[004-193]Normans had not only smiths to attend to the arms of the knights, but

[004-194]farriers to shoe their horses.  Henry de Femariis, or Ferrers,

[004-195]"prefectus fabrorum," was one of the principal officers entrusted with

[004-196]the supervision of the Conqueror's ferriery department; and long after

[004-197]the earldom was founded his descendants continued to bear on their coat

[004-198]of arms the six horse-shoes indicative of their origin.[19]  William

[004-199]also gave the town of Northampton, with the hundred of Fackley, as a

[004-200]fief to Simon St.  Liz, in consideration of his providing shoes for his

[004-201]horses.[20]  But though the practice of horse-shoeing is said to have

[004-202]been introduced to this country at the time of the Conquest, it is

[004-203]probably of an earlier date; as, according to Dugdale, an old Saxon

[004-204]tenant in capite of Welbeck in [[nottingham|Nottingham]]shire, named Gamelbere, held

[004-205]two carucates of land by the service of shoeing the king's palfrey on

[004-206]all four feet with the king's nails, as oft as the king should lie at

[004-207]the neighbouring manor of Mansfield.
