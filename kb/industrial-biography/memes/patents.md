# Patents

The legal mechanism of granting temporary monopolies to inventors — a system that both stimulated the flood of innovation during the [[industrial-revolution|Industrial Revolution]] and generated some of the bitterest disputes over who truly invented the transformative technologies of the age.

## Definition

A patent (letters patent) is a grant from the Crown giving an inventor the exclusive right to exploit an invention for a specified period — typically 14 years in 18th-century Britain. In exchange, the inventor must publicly disclose the invention in a written specification, so that others may learn from it after the patent expires. The patent system was intended to balance two competing interests: rewarding inventors for their ingenuity and ensuring that knowledge eventually entered the public domain.

Patents were expensive and cumbersome to obtain in the 18th century. An inventor had to petition through multiple offices, pay substantial fees, and navigate a legal system that was ill-equipped to judge novelty. Nevertheless, patents were taken out for nearly every major invention of the era, from Darby's coke smelting to Cort's [[puddling-process|puddling]] process to Neilson's hot blast.

## Historical Development

### The Statute of Monopolies (1624)

The foundation of English patent law was the Statute of Monopolies of 1624, which abolished the Crown's practice of granting monopolies to favourites but carved out an exception for "letters patent for the term of 14 years" for "the true and first inventor" of "any manner of new manufactures." This established the principle that patents should reward genuine invention, not royal favouritism.

### Patents in the [[iron-industry|Iron Industry]]

The iron industry was shaped by patents. Abraham Darby's coke-smelting process, the Cranege brothers' [[puddling-process|puddling]] method (1766), [[peter-onions|Peter Onions]]' reverberatory furnace (1783), [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]'s [[puddling-process|puddling]] and rolling patents (1783–1784), [[james-beaumont-neilson|James Neilson]]'s hot blast (1828) — all were secured by patents. Each patent represented years of experimentation and significant financial risk.

But patents also generated conflict. [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]]'s patents were challenged by [[cranege-brothers|the Craneges]] and Onions, who claimed priority. Neilson's hot blast patent was litigated for years, with rival ironmasters arguing that preheating blast air was an obvious improvement. The patent system thus both protected and obstructed innovation — a duality that persists to this day.

### Patent Disputes and Realities

The patent system's weaknesses were evident: wealthy infringers could out-litigate poor inventors; genuine inventors sometimes lost their rights through legal technicalities; and the slow, expensive patent process meant that many inventors operated without protection. [[henry-cort|Henry Cort]], despite his revolutionary patents, died impoverished after his patents were challenged and his business ruined.

## Economic Impact

The patent system's effects on the Industrial Revolution are debated:
- **Pro**: Patents provided the security that encouraged inventors to invest years of labour and capital in developing new technologies
- **Con**: Patents created monopolies that slowed the diffusion of innovations and raised prices for consumers
- **Reality**: Most inventors failed to profit from their patents, but the public disclosure requirement spread technical knowledge widely

The system's real value may have been in forcing inventors to write down and publish their methods — creating a technical literature that accelerated innovation across the industry.
