# Tempering

A heat treatment in which hardened steel is reheated to a controlled temperature below its critical point, then cooled — reducing the extreme brittleness of fully hardened steel while preserving most of its hardness, essential to the production of durable cutting tools, springs, and machine parts.

## Definition

Tempering is the second stage of the hardening-and-tempering heat treatment cycle. Steel is first hardened by heating to a bright red heat (above its upper critical temperature) and quenching rapidly in water or oil, which produces a very hard but extremely brittle structure called martensite. The steel is then tempered by reheating to a moderate temperature — typically between 200°C and 350°C for tools — which allows some of the internal stresses to relax and transforms a portion of the martensite into tougher structures. (004-087)

## Historical Development

### Ancient Origins

The art of tempering metals is ancient. Bronze weapons were work-hardened and heat-treated in the Mediterranean world by 1000 BC. Iron and steel tempering became critically important with the spread of iron weapons and tools:

> The ancient metallurgists of the classical world possessed the art of tempering and hardening bronze to such a degree that their chisels could cut hard stone, and their swords retained an edge through long campaigns. (004-087)

### The Craftsman's Art

Before modern metallurgy, tempering was an empirical art passed from master to apprentice. The smith judged temperature by the colour of the oxide film that forms on a cleaned steel surface as it is heated:

| Colour | Temperature (°C) | Typical Application |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Pale straw | 230 | Cutting tools, razors |
| Dark straw | 250 | Drills, milling cutters |
| Brown | 270 | Axes, chisels |
| Purple | 290 | Springs, saws |
| Dark blue | 310 | Screwdrivers, springs |
| Light blue | 340 | Soft springs |

> The nicety of the operation of tempering, as well as the judgment required to select the proper temperature for each tool, could only be acquired by long practice. (005-110)

### Industrial Application

By the 19th century, tempering was applied systematically in the manufacture of machine tools, cutlery, and armaments. The [[Cutlery]] trade of [[sheffield|Sheffield]] relied on precise tempering to produce blades that were hard enough to hold an edge yet tough enough not to snap in use.

## The Tempering Process

1. **Hardening**: The steel is heated to a bright cherry-red and quenched in water, oil, or brine. This produces maximum hardness but extreme brittleness.

2. **Cleaning**: The quenched surface is polished to bare metal so the temper colour can be observed.

3. **Reheating**: The piece is slowly and evenly reheated. The smith watches the oxide colours run across the surface.

4. **Quenching**: When the desired colour reaches the critical area (e.g., the cutting edge), the piece is quenched again to stop the tempering process.

5. **Testing**: The finished tool is tested for hardness and toughness. A file that skates across the surface without biting indicates proper hardness.

## Applications in Iron-Working

Tempering was essential to every edge tool, spring, and wear surface produced by the iron-working trades:

- **[[cutlery|Cutlery]]**: Knife blades tempered to dark straw for a combination of sharpness and toughness
- **Machine tools**: [[lathe|Lathe]] cutting tools tempered to pale straw for maximum hardness
- **Springs**: Tempered to blue for maximum resilience
- **Files**: The teeth of files were hardened but given only a very light temper, remaining nearly fully hard
