[030-001]It will be observed, however, that the new method must have seriously

[030-002]interfered with the labour of the hand-sawyers; and it was but natural

[030-003]that they should regard the establishment of the saw-mills with

[030-004]suspicion and hostility.  Hence a long period elapsed before the

[030-005]hand-sawyers would permit the new machinery to be set up and worked.

[030-006]The first saw-mill in [[england|England]] was erected by a Dutchman, near [[london|London]],

[030-007]in 1663, but was shortly abandoned in consequence of the determined

[030-008]hostility of the workmen.  More than a century passed before a second

[030-009]saw-mill was set up; when, in 1767, Mr. John Houghton, a [[london|London]]

[030-010]timber-merchant, by the desire and with the approbation of the Society

[030-011]of Arts, erected one at Limehouse, to be driven by wind.  The work was

[030-012]directed by one James Stansfield, who had gone over to Holland for the

[030-013]purpose of learning the art of constructing and managing the sawing

[030-014]machinery.  But the mill was no sooner erected than a mob assembled and

[030-015]razed it to the ground.  The principal rioters having been punished,

[030-016]and the loss to the proprietor having been made good by the nation, a

[030-017]new mill was shortly after built, and it was suffered to work without

[030-018]further molestation.

[030-019]Improved methods of manufacture have usually had to encounter the same

[030-020]kind of opposition.  Thus, when the Flemish weavers came over to

[030-021][[england|England]] in the seventeenth century, bringing with them their skill and

[030-022]their industry, they excited great jealousy and hostility amongst the

[030-023]native workmen.  Their competition as workmen was resented as an

[030-024]injury, but their improved machinery was regarded as a far greater

[030-025]source of mischief.  In a memorial presented to the king in 1621 we

[030-026]find the London weavers complaining of the foreigners' competition, but

[030-027]especially that "they have made so bould of late as to devise engines

[030-028]for working of tape, lace, ribbin, and such like, wherein one man doth

[030-029]more among them than 7 Englishe men can doe; so as their cheap sale of

[030-030]commodities beggereth all our Englishe artificers of that trade, and

[030-031]enricheth them." [2]

[030-032]At a much more recent period new inventions have had to encounter

[030-033]serious rioting and machine-breaking fury.  Kay of the fly-shuttle,

[030-034]Hargreaves of the spinning-jenny, and Arkwright of the spinning-frame,

[030-035]all had to fly from [[lancashire|Lancashire]], glad to escape with their lives.

[030-036]Indeed, says Mr. Bazley, "so jealous were the people, and also the

[030-037]legislature, of everything calculated to supersede men's labour, that

[030-038]when the Sankey Canal, six miles long, near Warrington, was authorized

[030-039]about the middle of last century, it was on the express condition that

[030-040]the boats plying on it should be drawn by men only!" [3]  Even improved

[030-041]agricultural tools and machines have had the same opposition to

[030-042]encounter; and in our own time bands of rural labourers have gone from

[030-043]farm to farm breaking drill-ploughs, winnowing, threshing, and other

[030-044]machines, down even to the common drills,--not perceiving that if their

[030-045]policy had proved successful, and tools could have been effectually

[030-046]destroyed, the human race would at once have been reduced to their

[030-047]teeth and nails, and civilization summarily abolished.[4]  It is, no

[030-048]doubt, natural that the ordinary class of workmen should regard with

[030-049]prejudice, if not with hostility, the introduction of machines

[030-050]calculated to place them at a disadvantage and to interfere with their

[030-051]usual employments; for to poor and not very far-seeing men the loss of

[030-052]daily bread is an appalling prospect.  But invention does not stand

[030-053]still on that account.  Human brains WILL work.  Old tools are improved

[030-054]and new ones invented, superseding existing methods of production,

[030-055]though the weak and unskilled may occasionally be pushed aside or even

[030-056]trodden under foot.  The consolation which remains is, that while the

[030-057]few suffer, society as a whole is vastly benefitted by the improved

[030-058]methods of production which are suggested, invented, and perfected by

[030-059]the experience of successive generations.

[030-060]The living race is the inheritor of the industry and skill of all past

[030-061]times; and the civilization we enjoy is but the sum of the useful

[030-062]effects of labour during the past centuries.  Nihil per saltum.  By

[030-063]slow and often painful steps Nature's secrets have been mastered.  Not

[030-064]an effort has been made but has had its influence.  For no human labour

[030-065]is altogether lost; some remnant of useful effect surviving for the

[030-066]benefit of the race, if not of the individual.  Even attempts

[030-067]apparently useless have not really been so, but have served in some way

[030-068]to advance man to higher knowledge, skill, or discipline.  "The loss of

[030-069]a position gained," says Professor Thomson, "is an event unknown in the

[030-070]history of man's struggle with the forces of inanimate nature."  A

[030-071]single step won gives a firmer foothold for further effort.  The man

[030-072]may die, but the race survives and continues the work,--to use the

[030-073]poet's simile, mounting on stepping-stones of dead selves to higher

[030-074]selves.

[030-075]Philarete Chasles, indeed, holds that it is the Human Race that is your

[030-076]true inventor:  "As if to unite all generations," he says, "and to show

[030-077]that man can only act efficiently by association with others, it has

[030-078]been ordained that each inventor shall only interpret the first word of

[030-079]the problem he sets himself to solve, and that every great idea shall

[030-080]be the RESUME of the past at the same time that it is the germ of the

[030-081]future."  And rarely does it happen that any discovery or invention of

[030-082]importance is made by one man alone.  The threads of inquiry are taken

[030-083]up and traced, one labourer succeeding another, each tracing it a

[030-084]little further, often without apparent result.  This goes on sometimes

[030-085]for centuries, until at length some man, greater perhaps than his

[030-086]fellows, seeking to fulfil the needs of his time, gathers the various

[030-087]threads together, treasures up the gain of past successes and failures,

[030-088]and uses them as the means for some solid achievement, Thus Newton

[030-089]discovered the law of gravitation, and thus [[james-watt|James Watt]] invented the

[030-090][[steam-engine]].  So also of the [[locomotive|Locomotive]], of which [[robert-stephenson|Robert Stephenson]]

[030-091]said, "It has not been the invention of any one man, but of a race of

[030-092]mechanical engineers."  Or, as [[joseph-bramah|Joseph Bramah]] observed, in the preamble

[030-093]to his second Lock patent, "Among the number of patents granted there

[030-094]are comparatively few which can be called original so that it is

[030-095]difficult to say where the boundary of one ends and where that of

[030-096]another begins."

[030-097]The arts are indeed reared but slowly; and it was a wise observation of

[030-098]Lord Bacon that we are too apt to pass those ladders by which they have

[030-099]been reared, and reflect the whole merit on the last new performer.

[030-100]Thus, what is hailed as an original invention is often found to be but

[030-101]the result of a long succession of trials and experiments gradually

[030-102]following each other, which ought rather to be considered as a

[030-103]continuous series of achievements of the human mind than as the

[030-104]conquest of any single individual.  It has sometimes taken centuries of

[030-105]experience to ascertain the value of a single fact in its various

[030-106]bearings.  Like man himself, experience is feeble and apparently

[030-107]purposeless in its infancy, but acquires maturity and strength with

[030-108]age.  Experience, however, is not limited to a lifetime, but is the

[030-109]stored-up wealth and power of our race.  Even amidst the death of

[030-110]successive generations it is constantly advancing and accumulating,

[030-111]exhibiting at the same time the weakness and the power, the littleness

[030-112]and the greatness of our common humanity.  And not only do we who live

[030-113]succeed to the actual results of our predecessors' labours,--to their

[030-114]works of learning and of art, their inventions and discoveries, their

[030-115]tools and machines, their roads, bridges, canals, and railways,--but to

[030-116]the inborn aptitudes of blood and brain which they bequeath to us, to

[030-117]that "educability," so to speak, which has been won for us by the

[030-118]labours of many generations, and forms our richest natural heritage.

[030-119]The beginning of most inventions is very remote.  The first idea, born

[030-120]within some unknown brain, passes thence into others, and at last comes

[030-121]forth complete, after a parturition, it may be, of centuries.  One

[030-122]starts the idea, another developes it, and so on progressively until at

[030-123]last it is elaborated and worked out in practice; but the first not

[030-124]less than the last is entitled to his share in the merit of the

[030-125]invention, were it only possible to measure and apportion it duly.

[030-126]Sometimes a great original mind strikes upon some new vein of hidden

[030-127]power, and gives a powerful impulse to the inventive faculties of man,

[030-128]which lasts through generations.  More frequently, however, inventions

[030-129]are not entirely new, but modifications of contrivances previously

[030-130]known, though to a few, and not yet brought into practical use.

[030-131]Glancing back over the history of mechanism, we occasionally see an

[030-132]invention seemingly full born, when suddenly it drops out of sight, and

[030-133]we hear no more of it for centuries.  It is taken up de novo by some

[030-134]inventor, stimulated by the needs of his time, and falling again upon

[030-135]the track, he recovers the old footmarks, follows them up, and

[030-136]completes the work.

[030-137]There is also such a thing as inventions being born before their

[030-138]time--the advanced mind of one generation projecting that which cannot

[030-139]be executed for want of the requisite means; but in due process of

[030-140]time, when mechanism has got abreast of the original idea, it is at

[030-141]length carried out; and thus it is that modern inventors are enabled to

[030-142]effect many objects which their predecessors had tried in vain to

[030-143]accomplish.  As Louis Napoleon has said, "Inventions born before their

[030-144]time must remain useless until the level of common intellects rises to

[030-145]comprehend them."  For this reason, misfortune is often the lot of the

[030-146]inventor before his time, though glory and profit may belong to his

[030-147]successors.  Hence the gift of inventing not unfrequently involves a

[030-148]yoke of sorrow.  Many of the greatest inventors have lived neglected

[030-149]and died unrequited, before their merits could be recognised and

[030-150]estimated.  Even if they succeed, they often raise up hosts of enemies

[030-151]in the persons whose methods they propose to supersede.  Envy, malice,

[030-152]and detraction meet them in all their forms; they are assailed by

[030-153]combinations of rich and unscrupulous persons to wrest from them the

[030-154]profits of their ingenuity; and last and worst of all, the successful

[030-155]inventor often finds his claims to originality decried, and himself

[030-156]branded as a copyist and a pirate.

[030-157]Among the inventions born out of time, and before the world could make

[030-158]adequate use of them, we can only find space to allude to a few, though

[030-159]they are so many that one is almost disposed to accept the words of

[030-160]Chaucer as true, that "There is nothing new but what has once been

[030-161]old;" or, as another writer puts it, "There is nothing new but what has

[030-162]before been known and forgotten;" or, in the words of Solomon, "The

[030-163]thing that hath been is that which shall be, and there is no new thing

[030-164]under the sun."  One of the most important of these is the use of

[030-165]Steam, which was well known to the ancients; but though it was used to

[030-166]grind drugs, to turn a spit, and to excite the wonder and fear of the

[030-167]credulous, a long time elapsed before it became employed as a useful

[030-168]motive-power.  The inquiries and experiments on the subject extended

[030-169]through many ages.  Friar Bacon, who flourished in the thirteenth

[030-170]century, seems fully to have anticipated, in the following remarkable

[030-171]passage, nearly all that steam could accomplish, as well as the

[030-172]hydraulic engine and the diving-bell, though the flying machine yet

[030-173]remains to be invented:--

[030-174]"I will now," says the Friar, "mention some of the wonderful works of

[030-175]art and nature in which there is nothing of magic, and which magic

[030-176]could not perform.  Instruments may be made by which the largest ships,

[030-177]with only one man guiding them, will be carried with greater velocity

[030-178]than if they were full of sailors.  Chariots may be constructed that

[030-179]will move with incredible rapidity, without the help of animals.

[030-180]Instruments of flying may be formed, in which a man, sitting at his

[030-181]ease and meditating on any subject, may beat the air with his

[030-182]artificial wings, after the manner of birds.  A small instrument may be

[030-183]made to raise or depress the greatest weights.  An instrument may be

[030-184]fabricated by which one man may draw a thousand men to him by force and

[030-185]against their will; as also machines which will enable men to walk at

[030-186]the bottom of seas or rivers without danger." It is possible that Friar

[030-187]Bacon derived his knowledge of the powers which he thus described from

[030-188]the traditions handed down of former inventions which had been

[030-189]neglected and allowed to fall into oblivion; for before the invention

[030-190]of printing, which enabled the results of investigation and experience

[030-191]to be treasured up in books, there was great risk of the inventions of

[030-192]one age being lost to the succeeding generations.  Yet Disraeli the

[030-193]elder is of opinion that the Romans had invented printing without being

[030-194]aware of it; or perhaps the senate dreaded the inconveniences attending

[030-195]its use, and did not care to deprive a large body of scribes of their

[030-196]employment.  They even used stereotypes, or immovable printing-types,

[030-197]to stamp impressions on their pottery, specimens of which still exist.

[030-198]In China the art of printing is of great antiquity.  Lithography was

[030-199]well known in Germany, by the very name which it still bears, nearly

[030-200]three hundred years before Senefelder reinvented it; and specimens of

[030-201]the ancient art are yet to be seen in the Royal Museum at Munich.[5]
