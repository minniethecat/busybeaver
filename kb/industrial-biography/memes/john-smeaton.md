## Early Life and Career

John Smeaton was born in 1724 at Austhorpe, near [[leeds|Leeds]]. He was educated at [[leeds|Leeds]] Grammar School and later studied law, but his natural inclination was toward mechanics and engineering. He established himself as a mathematical instrument maker in [[london|London]] before turning to civil engineering, a profession that he effectively founded in Britain. He was among the first to call himself a "civil engineer," distinguishing his profession from military engineering. (025-029)

## Work at Carron Iron Works

Smeaton was called upon by [[John Roebuck|Dr. John Roebuck]] to assist at the [[Carron Iron Works]], the great Scottish iron-founding enterprise. To increase the power of the blowing apparatus at Carron, Smeaton contrived and erected "the most perfect apparatus of the kind then in existence." The Carron Works was primarily known for its castings; the peculiar quality of Scotch iron admirably suited it for this purpose. The well-known Carronades, or "Smashers" as they were named, were cast in large numbers there. (025-029)

## The Forth and [[clyde|Clyde]] Canal

Out of the Carron enterprise sprang the [[Forth and Clyde Canal]], the first artificial navigation in [[scotland|Scotland]]. The [[carron-company|Carron Company]], with a view to securing an improved communication with [[Glasgow]], themselves surveyed a line for the canal. Although the project was initially given up due to the determined opposition of landowners, it was eventually revived and carried out after the designs of Smeaton and [[James Brindley]]. (025-038)

## [[steam-engine|Steam Engine]] Improvements

Smeaton also made important contributions to the development of the [[steam-engine]]. He conducted systematic experiments on the [[Newcomen engine|Newcomen atmospheric engine]] and made improvements that significantly increased its efficiency. Even after [[James Watt]] had removed to [[Birmingham]] and had the assistance of [[Matthew Boulton|Boulton]]'s best workmen, Smeaton expressed the opinion, when he saw Watt's engine at work, that "notwithstanding the ingenuity of the mechanism, it was too complex and too expensive to be generally adopted"—a judgement that history would prove mistaken, though it reflected the concerns of his era. (031-195)

## Legacy

John Smeaton is remembered as the father of civil engineering in Britain. He was a founder of the Society of Civil Engineers (the "Smeatonian Society"), which later became the [[institution-of-civil-engineers|Institution of Civil Engineers]]. His works included numerous bridges, canals, harbours, and lighthouses—most notably the [[Eddystone Lighthouse]], completed in 1759, which established principles of lighthouse construction that endured for over a century. His methodical, experimental approach to engineering problems set a standard for the profession.
