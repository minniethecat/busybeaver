[009-002][2] Life of St. Egwin, in Capgrave's Nova Legenda Anglioe.  Alcester

[009-003]was, as its name indicates, an old Roman settlement (situated on the

[009-004]Icknild Street), where the art of working in iron was practised from an

[009-005]early period.  It was originally called Alauna, being situated on the

[009-006]river Alne in [[warwickshire|Warwickshire]].  It is still a seat of the needle

[009-007]manufacture.

[009-028]It is probable that the "blomes" referred to in this agreement were the

[009-029]bloomeries or fires in which the iron was made; and that the "olyveres"

[009-030]were forges or erections, each of which contained so many bloomeries,

[009-031]but were of limited durability, and probably perished in the using.

[009-073]CHAPTER III.

[009-074]IRON-SMELTING BY PIT-COAL--DUD DUDLEY.

[009-075]"God of his Infinite goodness (if we will but take notice of his

[009-076]goodness unto this Nation) hath made this Country a very Granary for

[009-077]the supplying of Smiths with Iron, Cole, and Lime made with cole, which

[009-078]hath much supplied these men with Corn also of late; and from these men

[009-079]a great part, not only of this Island, but also of his Majestie's other

[009-080]Kingdoms and Territories, with Iron wares have their supply, and Wood

[009-081]in these parts almost exhausted, although it were of late a mighty

[009-082]woodland country."--DUDLEY's Metallum Martis, 1665.

[009-083]The severe restrictions enforced by the legislature against the use of

[009-084]wood in iron-smelting had the effect of almost extinguishing the

[009-085]manufacture.  New furnaces ceased to be erected, and many of the old

[009-086]ones were allowed to fall into decay, until it began to be feared that

[009-087]this important branch of industry would become completely lost.  The

[009-088]same restrictions alike affected the operations of the glass

[009-089]manufacture, which, with the aid of foreign artisans, had been

[009-090]gradually established in [[england|England]], and was becoming a thriving branch of

[009-091]trade.  It was even proposed that the smelting of iron should be

[009-092]absolutely prohibited:  "many think," said a contemporary writer, "that

[009-093]there should be NO WORKS ANYWHERE--they do so devour the woods."

[009-094]The use of iron, however, could not be dispensed with.  The very

[009-095]foundations of society rested upon an abundant supply of it, for tools

[009-096]and implements of peace, as well as for weapons of war.  In the dearth

[009-097]of the article at home, a supply of it was therefore sought for abroad;

[009-098]and both iron and steel came to be imported in largely-increased

[009-099]quantities.  This branch of trade was principally in the hands of the

[009-100]Steelyard Company of Foreign Merchants, established in Upper [[thames|Thames]]

[009-101]Street, a little above [[london-bridge|London Bridge]]; and they imported large

[009-102]quantities of iron and steel from foreign countries, principally from

[009-103]Sweden, Germany, and Spain.  The best iron came from Spain, though the

[009-104]Spaniards on their part coveted our English made cannons, which were

[009-105]better manufactured than theirs; while the best steel came from Germany

[009-106]and Sweden.[1]

[009-107]Under these circumstances, it was natural that persons interested in

[009-108]the English [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]] should turn their attention to some other

[009-109]description of fuel which should serve as a substitute for the

[009-110]prohibited article.  There was known to be an abundance of coal in the

[009-111]northern and midland counties, and it occurred to some speculators more

[009-112]than usually daring, to propose it as a substitute for the charcoal

[009-113]fuel made from wood.  But the same popular prejudice which existed

[009-114]against the use of coal for domestic purposes, prevented its being

[009-115]employed for purposes of manufacture; and they were thought very

[009-116]foolish persons indeed who first promulgated the idea of smelting iron

[009-117]by means of pit-coal.  The old manufacturers held it to be impossible

[009-118]to reduce the ore in any other way than by means of charcoal of wood.

[009-119]It was only when the wood in the neighbourhood of the ironworks had

[009-120]been almost entirely burnt up, that the manufacturers were driven to

[009-121]entertain the idea of using coal as a substitute; but more than a

[009-122]hundred years passed before the practice of smelting iron by its means

[009-123]became general.

[009-124]The first who took out a patent for the purpose was one Simon

[009-125]Sturtevant, a German skilled in mining operations; the professed object

[009-126]of his invention being "to neale, melt, and worke all kind of metal

[009-127]oares, irons, and steeles with sea-coale, pit-coale, earth-coale, and

[009-128]brush fewell."  The principal end of his invention, he states in his

[009-129]Treatise of Metallica,[2] is to save the consumption and waste of the

[009-130]woods and timber of the country; and, should his design succeed, he

[009-131]holds that it "will prove to be the best and most profitable business

[009-132]and invention that ever was known or invented in [[england|England]] these many

[009-133]yeares."  He says he has already made trial of the process on a small

[009-134]scale, and is confident that it will prove equally successful on a

[009-135]large one.  Sturtevant was not very specific as to his process; but it

[009-136]incidentally appears to have been his purpose to reduce the coal by an

[009-137]imperfect combustion to the condition of coke, thereby ridding it of

[009-138]"those malignant proprieties which are averse to the nature of

[009-139]metallique substances." The subject was treated by him, as was

[009-140]customary in those days, as a great mystery, made still more mysterious

[009-141]by the multitude of learned words under which he undertook to describe

[009-142]his "Ignick Invention" All the operations of industry were then treated

[009-143]as secrets.  Each trade was a craft, and those who followed it were

[009-144]called craftsmen.  Even the common carpenter was a handicraftsman; and

[009-145]skilled artisans were "cunning men."  But the higher branches of work

[009-146]were mysteries, the communication of which to others was carefully

[009-147]guarded by the regulations of the trades guilds.  Although the early

[009-148]patents are called specifications, they in reality specify nothing.

[009-149]They are for the most part but a mere haze of words, from which very

[009-150]little definite information can be gleaned as to the processes

[009-151]patented.  It may be that Sturtevant had not yet reduced his idea to

[009-152]any practicable method, and therefore could not definitely explain it.

[009-153]However that may be, it is certain that his process failed when tried

[009-154]on a large scale, and Sturtevant's patent was accordingly cancelled at

[009-155]the end of a year.

[009-156]The idea, however, had been fairly born, and repeated patents were

[009-157]taken out with the same object from time to time.  Thus, immediately on

[009-158]Sturtevant's failure becoming known, one John Rovenzon, who had been

[009-159]mixed up with the other's adventure, applied for a patent for making

[009-160]iron by the same process, which was granted him in 1613.  His 'Treatise

[009-161]of Metallica'[3] shows that Rovenzon had a true conception of the

[009-162]method of manufacture.  Nevertheless he, too, failed in carrying out

[009-163]the invention in practice, and his patent was also cancelled.  Though

[009-164]these failures were very discouraging, like experiments continued to be

[009-165]made and patents taken out,--principally by Dutchmen and

[009-166]Germans,[4]--but no decided success seems to have attended their

[009-167]efforts until the year 1620, when Lord [[dudley|Dudley]] took out his patent "for

[009-168]melting iron ore, making bar-iron, &c., with coal, in furnaces, with

[009-169]bellows." This patent was taken out at the instance of his son Dud

[009-170][[dudley|Dudley]], whose story we gather partly from his treatise entitled

[009-171]'Metallum Martis,' and partly from various petitions presented by him

[009-172]to the king, which are preserved in the State Paper Office, and it runs

[009-173]as follows:--

[009-174][[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] was born in 1599, the natural son of Edward Lord Dudley of

[009-175]Dudley Castle in the county of [[worcestershire|Worcester]].  He was the fourth of eleven

[009-176]children by the same mother, who is described in the pedigree of the

[009-177]family given in the Herald's visitation of the county of Stafford in

[009-178]the year 1663, signed by [[dud-dudley|Dud Dudley]] himself, as "Elizabeth, daughter of

[009-179]William Tomlinson of Dudley, concubine of Edward Lord Dudley." Dud's

[009-180]eldest brother is described in the same pedigree as Robert Dudley,

[009-181]Squire, of Netherton Hall; and as his sisters mostly married well,

[009-182]several of them county gentlemen, it is obvious that the family,

[009-183]notwithstanding that the children were born out of wedlock, held a good

[009-184]position in their neighbourhood, and were regarded with respect.  Lord

[009-185]Dudley, though married and having legitimate heirs at the time, seems

[009-186]to have attended to the up-bringing of his natural children; educating

[009-187]them carefully, and afterwards employing them in confidential offices

[009-188]connected with the management of his extensive property.  Dud describes

[009-189]himself as taking great delight, when a youth, in his father's

[009-190]iron-works near Dudley, where he obtained considerable knowledge of the

[009-191]various processes of the manufacture.
