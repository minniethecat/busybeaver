[007-001]But the first reception given to John Wesley by the miners of the

[007-002][[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]], more than a thousand years later, was perhaps scarcely

[007-003]more gratifying than that given to St.  Egwin.

[007-004]That working in iron was regarded as an honourable and useful calling

[007-005]in the Middle Ages, is apparent from the extent to which it was

[007-006]followed by the monks, some of whom were excellent craftsmen.  Thus St.

[007-007]Dunstan, who governed [[england|England]] in the time of Edwy the Fair, was a

[007-008]skilled blacksmith and metallurgist.  He is said to have had a forge

[007-009]even in his bedroom, and it was there that his reputed encounter with

[007-010]Satan occurred, in which of course the saint came off the victor.

[007-011]There was another monk of St.  Alban's, called Anketil, who flourished

[007-012]in the twelfth century, so famous for his skill as a worker in iron,

[007-013]silver, gold, jewelry, and gilding, that he was invited by the king of

[007-014]Denmark to be his goldsmith and banker.  A pair of gold and silver

[007-015]candlesticks of his manufacture, presented by the abbot of St.  Alban's

[007-016]to Pope Adrian IV., were so much esteemed for their exquisite

[007-017]workmanship that they were consecrated to St.  Peter, and were the

[007-018]means of obtaining high ecclesiastical distinction for the abbey.

[007-019]We also find that the abbots of monasteries situated in the iron

[007-020]districts, among their other labours, devoted themselves to the

[007-021]manufacture of iron from the ore.  The extensive beds of cinders still

[007-022]found in the immediate neighbourhood of Rievaulx and Hackness, in

[007-023][[yorkshire|Yorkshire]], show that the monks were well acquainted with the art of

[007-024]forging, and early turned to account the riches of the Cleveland

[007-025]ironstone.  In the [[forest-of-dean|Forest of Dean]] also, the abbot of Flaxley was

[007-026]possessed of one stationary and one itinerant forge, by grant from

[007-027]Henry II, and he was allowed two oaks weekly for fuel,--a privilege

[007-028]afterwards commuted, in 1258, for Abbot's Wood of 872 acres, which was

[007-029]held by the abbey until its dissolution in the reign of Henry VIII.  At

[007-030]the same time the Earl of Warwick had forges at work in his woods at

[007-031][[lydney|Lydney]]; and in 1282, as many as 72 forges were leased from the Crown by

[007-032]various iron-smelters in the same Forest of Dean.

[007-033]There are numerous indications of iron-smelting having been conducted

[007-034]on a considerable scale at some remote period in the neighbourhood of

[007-035][[leeds|Leeds]], in [[yorkshire|Yorkshire]].  In digging out the foundations of houses in

[007-036]Briggate, the principal street of that town, many "bell pits" have been

[007-037]brought to light, from which ironstone has been removed.  The new

[007-038]cemetery at Burmandtofts, in the same town, was in like manner found

[007-039]pitted over with these ancient holes.  The miner seems to have dug a

[007-040]well about 6 feet in diameter, and so soon as he reached the mineral,

[007-041]he worked it away all round, leaving the bell-shaped cavities in

[007-042]question.  He did not attempt any gallery excavations, but when the pit

[007-043]was exhausted, a fresh one was sunk.  The ore, when dug, was

[007-044]transported, most probably on horses' backs, to the adjacent districts

[007-045]for the convenience of fuel.  For it was easier to carry the mineral to

[007-046]the wood--then exclusively used for smelting'--than to bring the wood

[007-047]to the mineral.  Hence the numerous heaps of scoriae found in the

[007-048]neighbourhood of [[leeds|Leeds]],--at Middleton, Whitkirk, and Horsforth--all

[007-049]within the borough.  At Horsforth, they are found in conglomerated

[007-050]masses from 30 to 40 yards long, and of considerable width and depth.

[007-051]The remains of these cinder-beds in various positions, some of them

[007-052]near the summit of the hill, tend to show, that as the trees were

[007-053]consumed, a new wind furnace was erected in another situation, in order

[007-054]to lessen the labour of carrying the fuel.  There are also deposits of

[007-055]a similar kind at Kirkby Overblow, a village a few miles to the

[007-056]north-east of Leeds; and Thoresby states that the place was so called

[007-057]because it was the village of the "Ore blowers,"--hence the corruption

[007-058]of "Overblow."  A discovery has recently been made among the papers of

[007-059]the Wentworth family, of a contract for supplying wood and ore for iron

[007-060]"blomes" at Kirskill near Otley, in the fourteenth century;[3] though

[007-061]the manufacture near that place has long since ceased.

[007-062]Although the making of iron was thus carried on in various parts of

[007-063][[england|England]] in the Middle Ages, the quantity produced was altogether

[007-064]insufficient to meet the ordinary demand, as it appears from our early

[007-065]records to have long continued one of the principal articles imported

[007-066]from foreign countries.  English iron was not only dearer, but it was

[007-067]much inferior in quality to that manufactured abroad; and hence all the

[007-068]best arms and tools continued to be made of foreign iron.  Indeed the

[007-069]scarcity of this metal occasionally led to great inconvenience, and to

[007-070]prevent its rising in price [[house-of-commons|Parliament]] enacted, in 1354, that no iron,

[007-071]either wrought or unwrought, should be exported, under heavy penalties.

[007-072]For nearly two hundred years--that is, throughout the fourteenth and

[007-073]fifteenth centuries--the English market was principally supplied with

[007-074]iron and steel from Spain and Germany; the foreign merchants of the

[007-075]Steelyard doing a large and profitable trade in those commodities.

[007-076]While the woollen and other branches of trade were making considerable

[007-077]progress, the manufacture of iron stood still.  Among the lists of

[007-078]articles, the importation of which was prohibited in Edward IV.'s

[007-079]reign, with a view to the protection of domestic manufactures, we find

[007-080]no mention of iron, which was still, as a matter of necessity, allowed

[007-081]to come freely from abroad.

[007-082]The first indications of revival in the [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]] showed

[007-083]themselves in [[sussex|Sussex]], a district in which the Romans had established

[007-084]extensive works, and where smelting operations were carried on to a

[007-085]partial extent in the neighbourhood of Lewes, in the thirteenth and

[007-086]fourteenth centuries, where the iron was principally made into nails

[007-087]and horse-shoes.  The county abounds in ironstone, which is contained

[007-088]in the sandstone beds of the Forest ridge, lying between the chalk and

[007-089]oolite of the district, called by geologists the Hastings sand.  The

[007-090]beds run in a north-westerly direction, by Ashburnham and Heathfield,

[007-091]to Crowborough and thereabouts.  In early times the region was covered

[007-092]with wood, and was known as the Great Forest of Anderida.  [[sussex|The Weald]],

[007-093]or wild wood, abounded in oaks of great size, suitable for smelting

[007-094]ore; and the proximity of the mineral to the timber, as well as the

[007-095]situation of the district in the neighbourhood of the capital,

[007-096]sufficiently account for the [[sussex|Sussex]] iron-works being among the most

[007-097]important which existed in England previous to the discovery of

[007-098]smelting by pit-coal.

[007-099]The [[iron-industry|iron manufacture]]rs of the south were especially busy during the

[007-100]fifteenth and sixteenth centuries.  Their works were established near

[007-101]to the beds of ore, and in places where water-power existed, or could

[007-102]be provided by artificial means.  Hence the numerous artificial ponds

[007-103]which are still to be found all over the Sussex iron district.  Dams of

[007-104]earth, called "pond-bays," were thrown across watercourses, with

[007-105]convenient outlets built of masonry, wherein was set the great wheel

[007-106]which worked the hammer or blew the furnace.  Portions of the adjoining

[007-107]forest-land were granted or leased to the iron-smelters; and the many

[007-108]places still known by the name of "Chart" in the Weald, probably mark

[007-109]the lands chartered for the purpose of supplying the iron-works with

[007-110]their necessary fuel.  The cast-iron tombstones and slabs in many

[007-111]Sussex churchyards,--the andirons and chimney backs[4] still found in

[007-112]old Sussex mansions and farm-houses, and such names as Furnace Place,

[007-113]Cinder Hill, Forge Farm, and Hammer Pond, which are of very frequent

[007-114]occurrence throughout the county, clearly mark the extent and activity

[007-115]of this ancient branch of industry.[5]  Steel was also manufactured at

[007-116]several places in the county, more particularly at Steel-Forge Land,

[007-117]Warbleton, and at Robertsbridge.  The steel was said to be of good

[007-118]quality, resembling Swedish--both alike depending for their excellence

[007-119]on the exclusive use of charcoal in smelting the ore,--iron so produced

[007-120]maintaining its superiority over coal-smelted iron to this day.

[007-121]When cannon came to be employed in war, the nearness of Sussex to

[007-122][[london|London]] and the Cinque Forts gave it a great advantage over the remoter

[007-123]iron-producing districts in the north and west of England, and for a

[007-124]long time the iron-works of this county enjoyed almost a monopoly of

[007-125]the manufacture.  The metal was still too precious to be used for

[007-126]cannon balls, which were hewn of stone from quarries on Maidstone

[007-127]Heath.  Iron was only available, and that in limited quantities, for

[007-128]the fabrication of the cannon themselves, and wrought-iron was chiefly

[007-129]used for the purpose.  An old mortar which formerly lay on Eridge

[007-130]Green, near Frant, is said to have been the first mortar made in

[007-131]England;[6] only the chamber was cast, while the tube consisted of bars

[007-132]strongly hooped together.  Although the local distich says that

[007-133]"Master Huggett and his man John

[007-134]They did cast the first cannon,"

[007-135]there is every reason to believe that both cannons and mortars were

[007-136]made in Sussex before Huggett's time; the old hooped guns in [[tower-of-london|the Tower]]

[007-137]being of the date of Henry VI.  The first cast-iron cannons of English

[007-138]manufacture were made at Buxtead, in Sussex, in 1543, by Ralph Hogge,

[007-139]master founder, who employed as his principal assistant one Peter

[007-140]Baude, a Frenchman.  Gun-founding was a French invention, and Mr. Lower

[007-141]supposes that Hogge brought over Baude from France to teach his workmen

[007-142]the method of casting the guns.  [[About]] the same time Hogge employed a

[007-143]skilled Flemish gunsmith named Peter Van Collet, who, according to

[007-144]Stowe, "devised or caused to be made certain mortar pieces, being at

[007-145]the mouth from eleven to nine inches wide, for the use whereof the said

[007-146]Peter caused to be made certain hollow shot of cast-iron to be stuffed

[007-147]with fyrework, whereof the bigger sort for the same has screws of iron

[007-148]to receive a match to carry fyre for to break in small pieces the said

[007-149]hollow shot, whereof the smallest piece hitting a man would kill or

[007-150]spoil him."  In short, Peter Van Collet here introduced the manufacture

[007-151]of the explosive shell in the form in which it continued to be used

[007-152]down to our own day.

[007-153]Baude, the Frenchman, afterwards set up business on his own account,

[007-154]making many guns, both of brass and iron, some of which are still

[007-155]preserved in the Tower.[7]  Other workmen, learning the trade from him,

[007-156]also began to manufacture on their own account; one of Baude's

[007-157]servants, named John Johnson, and after him his son Thomas, becoming

[007-158]famous for the excellence of their cast-iron guns.  The Hogges

[007-159]continued the business for several generations, and became a wealthy

[007-160]county family.  Huggett was another cannon maker of repute; and Owen

[007-161]became celebrated for his brass culverins.  Mr. Lower mentions, as a

[007-162]curious instance of the tenacity with which families continue to follow

[007-163]a particular vocation, that many persons of the name of Huggett still

[007-164]carry on the trade of blacksmith in East Sussex.  But most of the early

[007-165]workmen at the Sussex iron-works, as in other branches of skilled

[007-166]industry in England during the sixteenth century, were

[007-167]foreigners--Flemish and French--many of whom had taken refuge in this

[007-168]country from the religious persecutions then raging abroad, while

[007-169]others, of special skill, were invited over by the iron manufacturers

[007-170]to instruct their workmen in the art of metal-founding.[8]

[007-171]As much wealth was gained by the pursuit of the revived iron

[007-172]manufacture in Sussex, iron-mills rapidly extended over the

[007-173]ore-yielding district.  The landed proprietors entered with zeal into

[007-174]this new branch of industry, and when wood ran short, they did not

[007-175]hesitate to sacrifice their ancestral oaks to provide fuel for the

[007-176]furnaces.  Mr. Lower says even the most ancient families, such as the

[007-177]Nevilles, Howards, Percys, Stanleys, Montagues, Pelhams, Ashburnhams,

[007-178]Sidneys, Sackvilles, Dacres, and Finches, prosecuted the manufacture

[007-179]with all the apparent ardour of [[birmingham|Birmingham]] and [[wolverhampton|Wolverhampton]] men in

[007-180]modern times.  William Penn, the courtier Quaker, had iron-furnaces at

[007-181]Hawkhurst and other places in Sussex.  The ruins of the Ashburnham

[007-182]forge, situated a few miles to the north-east of Battle, still serve to

[007-183]indicate the extent of the manufacture.  At the upper part of the

[007-184]valley in which the works were situated, an artificial lake was formed

[007-185]by constructing an embankment across the watercourse descending from

[007-186]the higher ground,[9] and thus a sufficient fall of water was procured

[007-187]for the purpose of blowing the furnaces, the site of which is still

[007-188]marked by surrounding mounds of iron cinders and charcoal waste.  Three

[007-189]quarters of a mile lower down the valley stood the forge, also provided

[007-190]with water-power for working the hammer; and some of the old buildings

[007-191]are still standing, among others the boring-house, of small size, now

[007-192]used as an ordinary labourer's cottage, where the guns were bored.  The

[007-193]machine was a mere upright drill worked by the water-wheel, which was

[007-194]only eighteen inches across the breast.  The property belonged, as it

[007-195]still does, to the Ashburnham family, who are said to have derived

[007-196]great wealth from the manufacture of guns at their works, which were

[007-197]among the last carried on in Sussex.  The Ashburnham iron was

[007-198]distinguished for its toughness, and was said to be equal to the best

[007-199]Spanish or Swedish iron.
