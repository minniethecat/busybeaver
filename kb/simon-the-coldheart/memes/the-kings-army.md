# The King's Army

**The King's Army** was the English military force commanded by [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]] during the second Norman campaign of 1417, the phase of the [[hundred-years-war|Hundred Years' War]] in which English forces systematically subdued the Duchy of Normandy. The army appears throughout the latter half of the novel as the institutional framework within which [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] rose from knight to baron to Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches of Normandy.

## Supreme Command: King Henry V

The army was the personal instrument of [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]], who directed strategy and delegated command to his principal lords. Henry had fought alongside his men at [[agincourt|Agincourt]] in 1415 and remained an active campaigner. Simon of Beauvallet had "learned the art of war under Henry, and he followed his precepts strictly, with the result that he lost no men, save by sickness, during all that weary siege" (013-011). This apprenticeship under the King himself was the foundation of Simon's military method.

Henry's strategic mind was evident in his coordinated assignment of independent commands. While he himself besieged [[falaise|Falaise]], he dispatched Simon to [[belremy|Belrémy]], Gloucester to the Cotentin, and Huntingdon to Coutances — a distribution of force that prevented the remaining French strongholds from reinforcing one another. The King knew his commanders intimately, naming [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]], [[alan-of-montlice|Alan of Montlice]], and Simon "my Soldier, my Knight, and my Poet" (012-021).

## Structure and Divisions

The army was organized into three major divisions. During the 1417 campaign, [[thomas-duke-of-clarence|Thomas, Duke of Clarence]], the King's brother, "commanded one of the three divisions of the army, and whose task it was to prepare for the advance on Rouen" (029-003). The threefold structure allowed each division to operate as a semi-independent force in the dispersed Norman campaign.

### Simon's Contingent at Belrémy

The clearest depiction of army organization comes from Simon's force at the [[siege-of-belremy|Siege of Belrémy]]:

> "Midway through October in that year of grace, 1417, Simon appeared before the town of Belrémy, with an army of fifteen hundred strong, Geoffrey of Malvallet leading the van, John Holland, Earl of Huntingdon, the left wing, and himself the right, Alan of Montlice with him, acting Master of Simon's Horse. Two squires came in Simon's train, Cedric of Gountray and Edmund Marnet. In the rear, with the ordnance and provisions, were the surgeons, the priest, and one John Tarbury, with his officers, as Master of Works." (013-003)

This passage reveals the standard organization of a fifteenth-century English field army:

- **Van** (forward/assault element): [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]]
- **Left Wing**: [[john-holland-earl-of-huntingdon|John Holland, Earl of Huntingdon]], assigned to "cover the western side" (013-008)
- **Right Wing**: Simon himself with [[alan-of-montlice|Alan of Montlice]] as Master of Horse
- **Rear/Support**: Ordnance, provisions, medical staff (surgeons), spiritual support (priest), and engineering (John Tarbury, Master of Works)

### The Wider Campaign Structure

Beyond Simon's force, the campaign included:
- The King's own division besieging Falaise
- [[humphrey-duke-of-gloucester|Humphrey, Duke of Gloucester]], operating in the Cotentin
- Forces dispatched to Domfront, Coutances, and other strongholds

## The Siege Train

The army's siege capability was a crucial element of the Norman campaign. Simon's force at Belrémy included a dedicated siege train with specialized personnel and equipment:

The ordnance included cannon — Holland "had trained his one cannon on to the western wall the better to attract attention" (014-098) — and a breaching tower for the assault. The English had "three towers of archers" (014-005) as mobile elevated firing platforms.

> "Shall we sit down before this town forever? To what avail your bombardment? The walls of Belrémy seem made of granite! They laugh at thy guns! I tell thee, Simon, this is waste of time!" (013-015)

The siege train also supported the mining operation, the most innovative element of Simon's approach. John Tarbury, Master of Works, supervised the digging of a tunnel from the English camp beneath the town walls to "a corner of waste ground within the town" (014-003).

### Field Works

Upon arrival at Belrémy, the army constructed substantial field fortifications:

> "Within an hour the army was at work, under Simon's direction. His men were set to build wooden huts, for Simon anticipated a prolonged siege, and winter was drawing on. Trenches were dug, and palisades erected for the protection of the army, and until these were finished, some ten days later, the camp was hard at work, both officers and men." (013-009)

## The Men of the Army

### Composition

The army was a mixture of feudal levies and professional soldiers. Fulk of Montlice's contingent at Shrewsbury (1403) offers a representative snapshot: "six score men-at-arms I can muster, and eight score archers, under Francis of Dalley. There is John the Marshal, and Vincent, my captain" (003-034). By the time of the Normandy campaign, Simon had built his own force at [[house-of-beauvallet|Beauvallet]]:

> "Simon set about reforming his men-at-arms, and archers, with so much success that within the space of six months he had a very fair army at his beck and call, composed of peasants' sons, and some wandering soldiers. Walter of Santoy proved himself an admirable captain." (010-115)

The army included men-at-arms (heavily armoured mounted soldiers), archers (English longbowmen, the decisive arm), horse-archers "mounted on trained chargers" (014-127), engineers, surgeons, priests, and servants.

### The Beauvallet Contingent

Simon's personal force wore green-and-russet, the Beauvallet colours. At the Battle of Belrémy marketplace, the army was a "medley of fighting men, a blaze of colour" — Montlice red-and-gold, Malvallet black, Beauvallet green-and-russet, and the King's men (014-142). This visual diversity reflected the composite nature of the English host, in which each lord brought his own retinue in his own livery.

## Discipline and Conduct

The novel places notable emphasis on military discipline under Henry V and the commanders who followed his precepts. Simon's orders before the assault on Belrémy set the standard:

> "If any man strike down a woman or child in the fight, or offer injury where none is courted, his life will I take, and that right speedily. I will have no burning or pillaging, but order and chivalry." (014-038)

This policy of mercy toward civilians was reinforced by Simon's refusal to starve Belrémy into submission: "Were I to hold this town till starvation came, I should enter it over children's bodies. I war not with babes" (013-055). Quarter was granted everywhere when the French broke (014-154), and the townsfolk were "amazed at thy clemency" (016-061).

## The Army in Battle

### The Belrémy Assault

The army's tactical sophistication was demonstrated in the taking of Belrémy, a coordinated three-pronged operation:

1. Huntingdon's diversionary assault on the western gate at dawn, with cannon, archers, and a breaching tower
2. Simon's mine party of twelve men, who infiltrated through a tunnel and opened the southern gates
3. Malvallet's charge through the open gates, riding westward to relieve Huntingdon, while Alan pressed to the castle

> "Plunging into Belrémy came the English, Malvallet at their head, unmistakable by his black plumes and surcoat." (014-122)

The fighting lasted an hour before the French broke. Alan's horse-archers followed behind Geoffrey, "red plumes waving" in the breeze. The English roared out the Agincourt song as they swept through the streets (014-149).

### Shrewsbury (1403)

Earlier in Simon's career, the army fought at the [[shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]] against Hotspur's rebellion. The King's army there numbered "fourteen to fifteen hundred strong, north to Hayteley-hill" (003-170), and was deployed with "the Prince's standard on the left wing" under which Fulk of Montlice served (003-167). The battle demonstrated the army's capacity to fight a major field engagement alongside its siege capabilities.

## After the Campaign: The Military Government

Following the success of the Norman campaign, Henry V established a permanent military administration. Simon was appointed Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches of Normandy, with Geoffrey of Malvallet and Alan of Montlice as his principal lieutenants (029-031). The army thus transitioned from a campaigning force into a garrison establishment, maintaining English control over the conquered duchy while the King prepared to advance on Rouen.
