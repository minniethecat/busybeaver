## Overview

Beauvallet is the Suffolk barony and estate granted to Simon of Beauvallet by King Henry IV as a reward for his services to the Crown. Originally known as Fair Pastures, the estate was the property of a rebel lord attainted for treason and subsequently bestowed upon the newly-knighted Simon. The name "Beauvallet" -- which Simon had already chosen as his own surname -- becomes the name of his domain, fusing his personal identity with the land he rules. The estate's transformation from a neglected rebel holding into a model of feudal order mirrors Simon's own rise from nameless adventurer to respected baron.

> "And ye shall call it Beauvallet and be yourself Lord of Beauvallet."

> -- [007-095]

## Origin of the Name

### An Assumed Identity

The name Beauvallet first appears not as a place but as a personal alias. When the boy who would become Simon arrives at [[montlice|Montlice]], he is asked his name. He replies: "I call myself Beauvallet, sir" (001-087). It is an invented name, a shield against the shame of his bastard birth and the enmity of his father, the Lord of [[malvallet|Malvallet]]. The name, meaning "Fair Vallet" in the Norman-French of the period, carries overtones of beauty and worth -- qualities the boy claims for himself by the very act of naming.

### From Fair Pastures to Beau Vallet

Years later, when the King determines to reward Simon's service, the chosen estate bears a name that seems almost providential: Fair Pastures (005-021). "Fair Pastures -- Beau Vallet. Ay, that is fitting," the King observes, drawing the connection between the estate's name and Simon's chosen surname (007-095). The linguistic echo between the English "Fair Pastures" and the French "Beau Vallet" seals the union of man and land. Simon, who once had no name and no home, becomes the lord of a barony that bears the very alias he invented for himself as a homeless boy.

> "'It is known as Fair Pastures, my lord.'"

> -- [005-021]

> "Fair Pastures -- Beau Vallet. Ay, that is fitting."

> -- [007-095]

## The Grant and Its Circumstances

### A Traitor's Lands

Fair Pastures was formerly the property of a lord who joined the Percy rebellion against King Henry IV. Following the rebel's attainder, the estate reverted to the Crown. Its location in Suffolk placed it within a region where loyalties were uncertain and where a strong royal presence was needed to prevent further risings. By granting the estate to Simon -- a proven soldier of unquestionable loyalty -- the King served both his own interests and the cause of regional stability.

> "There will be little fear of risings near Beauvallet now. This gift is to mine own benefit as well as to thine."

> -- [007-101]

### The Creation of a Baron

The formal elevation of Simon to the barony takes place with royal ceremony. "The King made Simon Baron of Beauvallet, and gave him a land called Fair Pastures, which was once the estate of a rebel lord attainted" (008-063). For the boy who arrived at Montlice without name or station, this moment represents the culmination of his long struggle for recognition. He is no longer merely "Simon of Beauvallet" by his own invention, but Simon, Lord of Beauvallet, by the King's express will.

> "The King made Simon Baron of Beauvallet, and gave him a land called Fair Pastures."

> -- [008-063]

## Taking Possession

### The Arrival

Simon and his half-brother [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] ride through the lands of Fair Pastures towards the castle, surveying the domain that is now Simon's own (008-098). The estate includes a castle with kitchens, great hall, stables, and surrounding tenanted farms, villages, and woodlands. At the castle, they are met by Master Hubert, the steward who has been managing the estate in the absence of a resident lord (009-003).

> "Charles of Granmere and Simon of Beauvallet rode through Montlice towards his new lands, looking about them with eager eyes."

> -- [008-098]

### Confronting the Steward

Master Hubert presents Simon with the estate accounts, but the new lord quickly perceives that all is not well. The steward has grown comfortable in his unsupervised authority and proves reluctant to yield control to the young baron. Simon's questioning is methodical and relentless, revealing that the estate has been poorly managed and that the steward has profited from his master's absence.

## Reform and Governance

### Order Out of Chaos

Simon wastes no time in imposing his will upon his new domain. The steward, Nicholas, is dismissed "in an hour, calling down curses on his head" (010-018). This swift removal of the old order is followed by a thorough overhaul of the estate's governance. "Strenuous changes were wrought in Beauvallet. Malefactors were brought to justice, and the lawless were brought to heel" (010-019). The new lord's reputation as a stern and efficient commander, earned in the wars, now serves him in the administration of peace.

> "Strenuous changes were wrought in Beauvallet. Malefactors were brought to justice."

> -- [010-019]

> "In a surprisingly short time the men of Beauvallet settled down under the new regime."

> -- [010-021]

### Knowing Every Man

Simon's approach to lordship is personal and direct. When his man Bernard asks in wonder, "Do ye know every man in Beauvallet, sir?" (009-105), the question reveals the new baron's method: he has taken the time to learn the names, circumstances, and characters of those he rules. This intimate knowledge of his domain -- a habit formed in the close-knit [[montlice|Montlice]] household -- becomes a hallmark of his governance at Beauvallet.

> "Do ye know every man in Beauvallet, sir?" asked Bernard wonderingly.

> -- [009-105]

## The Baronial Household

### The Beauvallet Colours

The men of Beauvallet wear green and russet, colours that distinguish them on the battlefield and in the lists (012-006). These liveried retainers -- some veterans of Shrewsbury who fought alongside Simon, some newly recruited from the estate -- form a fighting force whose loyalty to their lord is absolute. When Simon later marches to war in Normandy, the Beauvallet contingent rides behind their distinctive banner, a visible symbol of the barony's martial strength.

> "What! Do ye think Beauvallet hath no tenderness within him?"

> -- [012-007]

### The Castle Community

The castle at Beauvallet becomes a centre of hospitality and counsel. Alan of Montlice visits for a week, and Simon is "rather pleased" to receive his old friend (010-102). Guests and messengers come and go, bearing news from the wider world. The household includes pages, squires, men-at-arms, servants, and the various craftsmen and labourers who keep a great estate functioning. Simon governs it all with the same cool efficiency he brought to the Shrewsbury campaign.

> "Alan remained at Beauvallet a week, and Simon was rather pleased to have him there."

> -- [010-102]

## The Promise of a Lady Baroness

### A Future Mistress

When Alan asks Simon, "Who shall be mistress of Beauvallet, Simon?" (008-090), the question touches on the one element still missing from the baron's achievement. Simon's answer is characteristically measured: he will bring no strangers to Beauvallet, but for now will make shift with his own governance (008-107). Yet the question plants a seed that will grow through the later chapters of the novel.

Later, when Simon captures the formidable [[lady-margaret-of-belremy|Lady Margaret of Belremy]] and brings her as hostage to England, the arc of the story bends towards a resolution in which the Lady Margaret will become the mistress that Beauvallet lacks. "Thou shalt be the Lady Baroness of Beauvallet" becomes the implicit promise that closes the romantic arc of the narrative.

> "Who shall be mistress of Beauvallet, Simon?"

> -- [008-090]

> "I will bring no strangers into Beauvallet."

> -- [008-107]

## Military Role in the Later Novel

### The Normandy Campaign

When Simon is appointed Lieutenant-General of the Lands and Marches of Normandy, the men of Beauvallet form the core of his expeditionary force. "These picked men of Beauvallet would cheerfully have died so that their lord might live" (014-081). They fight under the green-and-russet Beauvallet colours at the [[siege-of-belremy|Siege of Belremy]], where Simon's cry of "Way for Beauvallet!" becomes a battle-call that his enemies learn to fear (014-140).

> "Way for Beauvallet! Follow the Gilded Armour!"

> -- [014-141]

### Home Base

Throughout the Normandy campaign, Beauvallet remains Simon's English base, the place to which dispatches are sent and from which supplies and reinforcements are drawn. When he writes to the King, he signs himself "Simon of Beauvallet" -- the name that once was an invention is now a title recognized across two kingdoms (005-101). The estate continues to function smoothly in his absence, a testament to the reforms he implemented when he first took possession.
