# Turincel

**Turincel** was the castle and ducal seat of [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand, Duke of Turincel]], situated in the Norman countryside roughly ten leagues from [[belremy|Belrémy]]. The [[stronghold|castle]] served as a regional power centre, its lord commanding enough authority that [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret of Belrémy]] staked her escape upon his promised aid. During the Lancastrian campaign to subdue the rebellious [[belremy|Belrémy]] region, Turincel became a focal point of diplomatic and military manoeuvre when [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] traced Margaret's flight to its gates, only to find that the Duke had submitted and the fugitive had already moved on.

## Geography and Location

### Distance from Belrémy

Turincel lay approximately ten leagues from [[belremy|Belrémy]], a distance that shaped both Margaret's desperate flight and the strategic calculations of the pursuing forces. [[the-lady-margaret|The Lady Margaret]] estimated she could cover the distance on foot in three days:

> "I would go to Fernand de Turincel. Nay, but listen! A pageboy even an I walk to Turincel I can reach it within three days." (019-049)

By horse, the journey was considerably shorter. [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]], riding with their retinue, reached Turincel within a single day's hard ride from [[belremy|Belrémy]]:

> Within the hour they were riding out of Belrémy, black plumes and green side by side, bearing for Turincel. They reached it in the evening, but Simon went at once to the Castle, only to be told that Fernand de Turincel was abroad. (023-095)

### Regional Position

The [[stronghold|castle]]'s position in [[normandy|Normandy]] placed it within the network of feudal holdings whose lords' allegiances could determine the balance of power in local conflicts. Near enough to [[belremy|Belrémy]] to serve as an escape route for a fugitive countess, yet independent enough to offer sanctuary and potentially martial support, Turincel represented the classic Norman castle-holding of the [[hundred-years-war|Hundred Years' War]] period — a fortified seat whose political loyalties were as important as its military strength.

### The Road to Turincel

The journey to Turincel from [[belremy|Belrémy]] was marked by anxiety and urgent calculation. Margaret and her small party, travelling on foot and in disguise, asked repeatedly for directions and estimated the remaining distance. At a moment of rare optimism, one of the party declared:

> "And another day will see us at Turincel." (020-023)

This confident assessment, however, masked the real danger of the journey. The countryside between Belrémy and Turincel was not entirely secure, and the party's dependence on the goodwill of strangers made their progress uncertain. Margaret's hopes rested on feudal bonds that might or might not hold under [[house-of-lancaster|Lancastrian]] pressure. The servant [[gaston|Gaston]], whose loyalty to Margaret was absolute, noted his own uncertainty about the guide: "Perchance I will take him as far as Turincel. I hope he is not a rogue, come to betray us" (020-155). This fragile trust — Gaston hoping the guide was not a traitor, Margaret hoping Gaston himself was true — epitomises the atmosphere of suspicion and risk that surrounded the entire flight to Turincel.

## The Lady Margaret's Flight

### Escape from Belrémy

During her flight from [[belremy|Belrémy]], [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]] repeatedly identified Turincel as her destination and her salvation. Desperate to throw off the Lancastrian yoke imposed by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]], she placed all her hopes in the aid of [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand de Turincel]]:

> Margaret fretted. "If I could but reach Turincel!" (017-030)

Her companion questioned whether they could manage the journey, but Margaret's conviction rested on her faith in Fernand's loyalty:

> "Turincel! Why, chérie, it is ten leagues distant!"
> "If I could reach it, Fernand de Turincel would aid me! Aid me to throw off this hated yoke!" (017-031/017-032)

She envisioned Turincel not merely as refuge but as the source of a counter-stroke against Simon's occupation, musing defiantly: "I wonder what he will say when I bring Turincel about his ears?" (020-043).

### Arrival at the Castle

Margaret and her servant [[jeanne|Jeanne]], aided by the loyal [[gaston|Gaston]], reached Turincel after a gruelling journey. They arrived shortly before three in the afternoon (021-039), having paused only for a frugal meal along the way. The chapter heading — "How the Lady Margaret Came to Turincel" (021-002) — signals the narrative significance of this arrival. At the castle gate, the porter's blunt question carried the weight of potential betrayal:

> "What do ye at Turincel?" (021-013)

Margaret's response — that she sought Fernand de Turincel — was a gamble. The porter might have turned her away; he might have alerted the Lancastrian authorities. Instead, he admitted her, and [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand de Turincel]] received the exhausted Countess with formal courtesy:

> "Is it indeed so? I am Fernand, Duke of Turincel, at your service, Countess." (021-056)

The reception suggested a nobleman bound by the codes of [[chivalry|chivalric]] hospitality, whatever his ultimate political calculations might be.

### Departure Southward

After two days' rest at Turincel, Margaret departed, provisioned by Fernand for a longer southward journey (022-003). Whether Fernand actively aided her escape or merely granted her the courtesy of shelter before sending her on is left ambiguous in the narrative, but the provisioning suggests at minimum a benevolent neutrality towards her cause. Margaret's intended route led southward towards [[vazincourt|Vazincourt]] and the Sieur de Larousie — a journey that would prove even more perilous than the flight to Turincel, for it passed through the lawless lands of [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]].

The danger of the route south from Turincel was underscored by Fernand himself, who warned Margaret of what lay ahead (021-107). His warning, combined with his provision of supplies, suggests a man who wished Margaret well but was unwilling to commit his own forces to her cause — a pragmatic calculation characteristic of the feudal politics of the period.

## Fernand's Submission

### The Envoy's Discovery

When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] arrived at Turincel in pursuit of Margaret, they received news that fundamentally altered the strategic picture. The Duke had submitted to royal authority:

> "Turincel has submitted. Where next did ye go?" (023-081)

The timing of this submission remains unclear — whether Fernand had yielded to pressure from [[house-of-lancaster|Lancastrian]] forces or had pre-emptively distanced himself from Margaret's cause. What mattered was that Turincel, as a potential centre of resistance, had been neutralised.

### A Night at the Castle

Simon and Geoffrey spent the night within Turincel's walls, seeking information about Margaret's whereabouts. The castle's servants confirmed her recent presence and departure:

> "Then they slept in Turincel that night," Simon said. "Your servant, sir." (023-118)

Geoffrey, exasperated by the trail going cold, remarked: "They are surely in Turincel. Where else should they be?" (023-125). The frustration of the pursuit underscored the castle's role as a pivot point in the wider campaign — a place where the fugitive's trail might end, yet where political realities forced the pursuers to accept the limits of their reach.

### The Betrayal of Trust

From Margaret's perspective, Fernand's submission constituted nothing less than a betrayal. When she learned the truth, her despair was absolute:

> "All in vain!" Margaret whispered. "Fernand turned traitor." (021-075)

The word "traitor" is deeply significant. Margaret had staked not only her personal safety but her entire political strategy on Fernand's loyalty. She had promised herself that Turincel would provide the counter-stroke that would "bring Turincel about his ears" (020-043). Fernand's submission extinguished that hope before it could take shape. The feudal bonds of [[chivalry|honour]] and obligation that Margaret had counted upon — bonds that might have obligated Fernand to aid a fellow noble against an invader — proved insufficient against the pressure exerted by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] in the name of [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]].

## Architecture and Layout

### The Castle Structure

Turincel, like many Norman castles of the period, was likely a stone [[stronghold|fortress]] raised on a slope or eminence, with a curtain wall, gatehouse, and central keep. Although the text provides no detailed architectural description, its designation as the seat of a Duke implies a structure of considerable size, capable of housing a noble household, servants, and a garrison. The castle was substantial enough that [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand]] could receive a Countess with appropriate ceremony and accommodate both her party and, later, the [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]-led envoy.

### Household and Garrison

As a ducal seat, Turincel would have maintained a permanent household staff and a small garrison of men-at-arms. The ease with which Margaret was received — and the readiness with which Simon was later told that Fernand was "abroad" — suggests an orderly establishment accustomed to the comings and goings of noble visitors, characteristic of a well-run feudal [[stronghold|castle]] in [[normandy|Normandy]].

## Strategic Significance

### Feudal Allegiances

Turincel exemplified the network of feudal loyalties that defined Norman political life. [[the-lady-margaret|Margaret]]'s certainty that [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand]] would aid her speaks to bonds of allegiance — perhaps kinship, perhaps feudal obligation, perhaps shared antipathy to [[house-of-lancaster|Lancastrian]] rule — that tied the houses of [[belremy|Belrémy]] and Turincel together. That Fernand ultimately submitted rather than fought illustrates the effectiveness of the royal authority exercised through [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]].

### Role in the Belrémy Campaign

Turincel's submission marked a turning point in the [[surrender-of-belremy|Belrémy campaign]]. With the region's most significant neighbouring lord neutralised, Margaret's hope of a counter-offensive collapsed. The castle's strategic importance was thus ultimately political rather than military — its lord's allegiance, not its walls, determined the outcome. In this sense, Turincel serves as a case study in how fifteenth-century [[stronghold|castle]]s functioned as seats of lordship whose loyalties were won or lost through diplomacy and pressure as much as through siege.
