## Overview

Normandy is the French duchy that serves as the principal theatre of military operations in the latter half of the novel. Once the ancestral homeland of William the Conqueror, Normandy in the early fifteenth century is a contested territory, claimed by both the English Crown and the French monarchy. King Henry V's campaign to reclaim the duchy provides the historical backdrop against which Simon of Beauvallet's military career reaches its zenith. From his first crossing into Normandy to his eventual appointment as Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches, Simon's fortunes are inseparable from this embattled region.

> "'Our King went forth to Normandy' -- have at ye now! 'With grace and might of chivalry.'"

> -- [014-152]

## Historical Context

### The Lancastrian Claim

The English claim to Normandy traces back to the Norman Conquest of 1066 and the subsequent entanglement of the English and French crowns. By the reign of Henry V, the duchy had been largely lost to the French during the previous century's conflicts. Henry's determination to recover these lost territories -- asserting his rights as Duke of Normandy as well as King of England -- drove the military campaigns that dominate the novel's second half.

### A Contested Land

The Normandy of the novel is a land divided. Some strongholds, such as Falaise and Belremy, remain in the hands of French lords and ladies loyal to the Dauphin. Others, like Bayeux, have fallen to the English advance. The countryside between these fortified places is roamed by bandits such as Raoul the Terrible, who exploit the chaos of war for their own enrichment. It is into this fractured landscape that Simon rides, charged with bringing order through force of arms.

## The English Campaign

### Crossing into Normandy

The novel's twelfth chapter, titled "How He Came to Normandy" (012-002), marks the transition from the English scenes of Simon's rise to the French theatre of his greatest achievements. The King has resolved upon a full-scale invasion, and Simon -- now Lord of [[beauvallet|Beauvallet]] -- is summoned to join the expedition. His crossing with the royal army places him at the centre of one of the most ambitious military enterprises of the Lancastrian era.

### Key Strongholds

The campaign targets a chain of fortified towns and castles across Lower Normandy. The King himself undertakes the siege of Falaise, the birthplace of William the Conqueror and a symbolically crucial prize (012-099). Simon is assigned the more difficult task: the reduction of Belremy, a town built upon a slight incline and defended by the formidable [[lady-margaret-of-belremy|Lady Margaret of Belremy]] (013-004). The division of these objectives reflects the Crown's confidence in Simon's abilities -- the harder citadel goes to the more capable commander.

> "So if the King take Falaise, Simon shall take Belremy."

> -- [012-099]

> "Belremy is the harder task, sire, saving your presence."

> -- [012-103]

## Lower Normandy: The Theatre of War

### Geography and Distances

Lower Normandy, the western portion of the duchy, encompasses the region from [[bayeux|Bayeux]] in the north to the borderlands further south and east. The novel describes Belremy as "commanding the centre of Lower Normandy some twenty-five leagues from Bayeux" (029-003). This geography shapes the campaign: distances between fortified places are measured in days of hard riding, and control of the roads is as vital as control of the walls.

> "Belremy was situated commanding the centre of Lower Normandy, some twenty-five leagues from Bayeux."

> -- [029-003]

### Bayeux: The Royal Headquarters

The city of Bayeux serves as the English headquarters during the campaign. It is to Bayeux that Simon rides "slowly at the head of his men, Alan at his side" to report to the King (029-003). The city functions as the administrative and military nerve-centre of the English occupation, where royal writs are issued, dispatches are received, and the strategy of conquest is debated in council. Simon's journeys to and from Bayeux form the connective tissue of the campaign narrative.

## Simon's Command in Normandy

### Appointment as Warden

Simon's greatest military achievement in the novel is his appointment as "Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches of Normandy" (031-056). This commission, issued by the King in formal and grandiose terms, places Simon in command of the English military presence throughout the duchy. The appointment is both a reward for past service and a recognition that no other commander possesses the combination of tactical skill, ruthless efficiency, and political judgment required to hold the conquered territory.

> "In grandiose terms it created him Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches of Normandy."

> -- [031-056]

### The Troops Under Command

With the King's departure from Normandy, Simon is left as the senior English commander in the duchy. "My King hath made me lieutenant of the troops he leaves in Normandy" (032-050). These forces include not only his own Beauvallet men but also contingents from other English lords, garrison troops in captured towns, and the various support elements required to sustain an occupying army in hostile territory. The responsibility is immense: the entire English position in Normandy depends upon Simon's leadership.

> "My King hath made me lieutenant of the troops he leaves in Normandy, Margot."

> -- [032-050]

## The Siege of Belremy

### Encamping Before the Town

The siege of [[belremy|Belremy]] is the centrepiece of Simon's Normandy campaign. He appears before the town "with an army of fifteen hundred men" and establishes his siege lines (013-003). Belremy stands upon a slight incline, its walls seemingly "made of granite," defying the English bombardment. The defenders, led by the Lady Margaret, return "a hot answer" to Simon's summons to surrender, declaring that he shall enter Belremy only over her dead body (013-010).

> "Belremy stood upon a slight incline, its walls seemed made of granite!"

> -- [013-004]

### The Taking of the Town

The fall of Belremy is achieved through a combination of mining, assault, and the relentless pressure of Simon's will. "Plunging into Belremy came the English" at the climax of the siege, their battle-cry of "Way for Beauvallet!" echoing through the breached streets (014-122, 014-140). The capture of the town, and of the Lady Margaret herself, marks the turning point of the campaign and sets in motion the events that will lead to Simon's ultimate triumph.

> "We meet within Belremy, Beauvallet. God be with you and keep you."

> -- [014-042]

## Pacification and Governance

### Clearing the Bandits

With the major strongholds reduced, Simon turns his attention to the lawless elements that prey upon the Norman countryside. The most notorious of these is Raoul, a bandit whose depredations have terrorized the region. Simon tracks him down and eliminates him with characteristic thoroughness. "And so have rid Normandy of a very foul rogue," he reports, with the satisfied understatement of a man who has done a necessary and unpleasant job (029-012).

> "'And so have rid Normandy of a very foul rogue.' I thank you, sir."

> -- [029-012]

### The Duchy Under English Rule

The administration of conquered Normandy requires more than military force. Simon's role as Warden encompasses judicial authority, the collection of revenues, the maintenance of garrisons, and the delicate management of relations between the English occupiers and the Norman population. The seal of the Duchy, held by Morgan; the Seneschal, Luttrell; and the various lieutenants disposed across the region all operate under Simon's overarching command (031-015). The novel portrays this governance as efficient and firm -- a reflection of Simon's own character.

> "Morgan is to hold the seal of the Duchy, Luttrell is to be Seneschal."

> -- [031-015]

## Other Operations

### The Siege of Cherbourg

While Simon secures the centre of Lower Normandy, other English commanders prosecute operations elsewhere. Gloucester is tasked with the siege of [[cherbourg|Cherbourg]] in the Cotentin peninsula (029-054). The strength of Cherbourg's fortifications makes it a stubborn objective, but the overall English strategy -- to take every walled place in the duchy, one by one -- is methodical and inexorable.

### Domfront and the Southern Approaches

Domfront, another strongpoint in the Norman defences, is noted as likely to fall before Cherbourg loses "one stone from its walls" (031-032). The assessment reflects the varying quality of fortifications and defenders across the duchy, and the practical military judgment that Simon and his fellow commanders must exercise in determining the sequence of operations.

## Lasting Significance

### Normandy in Simon's Arc

Normandy is the place where Simon of Beauvallet's transformation is completed. He arrived in England as a nameless boy; he crossed to France as a baron and knight; he will return as a conqueror, a lieutenant of the Crown, and the captor of the woman who will become his wife. The duchy is the forge in which his final identity as a great lord is hammered into shape -- a place of trial, triumph, and the beginning of his personal as much as his political fulfilment.

> "Thus shall he remain in Normandy. Art thou satisfied?"

> -- [031-015]

### The Margaret Connection

It is in Normandy that Simon first encounters the Lady Margaret of Belremy, and it is from Normandy that he brings her as a hostage to England -- and ultimately to [[beauvallet|Beauvallet]] as its Lady Baroness. The French duchy thus supplies not only the military climax of the novel but also the romantic one. The conquest of Normandy and the conquest of the Lady Margaret are intertwined, twin strands of a single narrative of mastery and domestication.
