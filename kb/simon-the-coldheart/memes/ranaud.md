Ranaud is a giant, red-bearded Frenchman who travels with the [[the-lady-margaret|Lady Margaret]] and [[jeanne|Jeanne]] during their flight from [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]]'s lands. Fiercely independent, he describes himself with the memorable declaration: "I am Ranaud, and I go where I please, and do what I please" (021-031). He later pledges his service to [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], recognising in him a lord worthy of his loyalty. A man of immense physical strength, shrewd intelligence, and rough good humour, Ranaud is one of the novel's most vivid secondary characters.

## Physical appearance and demeanour

### Stature and presence

Ranaud is a man of overwhelming physical presence, described as a giant whose sheer bulk dominates any space he enters. When he returns to a small room, he seems "to fill it with his great body" (021-096). His physicality defines him: his "great fist" comes down upon tables with crushing force (020-119), and he moves with a power that renders even hostile innkeepers cautious, for "Ranaud was too formidable for his taste" (020-121). His voice is a "deep bass which seemed to come from a bottomless cavern within him" (022-003), and he sings cheerfully while travelling, his rumbling tones accompanying the party on their arduous journey.

### Character and temperament

Ranaud is defined by his fierce independence, his courage, and his unshakeable good humour. He hums to himself in quiet moments (021-003), chuckles at adversity (021-007), and shows no trace of fear in the face of any foe. His little eyes "blaze fiercely" when he is angered (020-125), yet he is equally quick to kindness and protective instinct.

Beneath his rough and ready exterior lies a man of genuine shrewdness. Margaret herself observes this quality when she notes "what a shrewd glance it was" that Ranaud turned upon her (020-134). He penetrates her disguise with ease, having been "once at Belrémy" and recognising the Countess despite her boy's clothing (021-027). He speaks in a calm and composed manner even when revealing his knowledge: "So I thought," he says placidly when Margaret confesses her true identity (021-020), and "Well, I know that," he answers when she presses him further (021-025).

## Role in the narrative

### First encounter at the inn

Ranaud first appears at a roadside inn where [[the-lady-margaret|Margaret]], disguised as a boy, is being harassed by fellow travellers on the road to [[turincel|Turincel]]. When one of the men jostles her roughly, Ranaud intervenes, thrusting Margaret into her seat with a protective gesture (020-116). He brings "his great fist down on the table" and bellows at the landlord, silencing the commotion (020-119). The landlord draws back muttering, recognising that Ranaud is not a man to be trifled with.

That evening, Ranaud escorts Margaret and [[jeanne|Jeanne]] to the stable-loft for the night, climbing the ladder with a lantern to light their way (020-147). When Margaret asks whether the hay is soft enough, he grunts "Soft enough" and clambered down again, a man of few words but reliable deeds (020-150).

### Journey to Turincel

The next morning, Ranaud voluntarily joins Margaret and Jeanne on their journey. "And I will come too," he says simply (020-128), and the trio sets out together, "Jeanne's hand clinging to Margaret's, and Ranaud striding along beside them" (020-129).

During a roadside stop, Margaret reveals her true identity to him. Ranaud has already guessed it, explaining that he recognised her dagger. "I was once at Belrémy," he tells her, revealing his prior connection to her household (021-027). He agrees to help her, though he makes his position clear: he wants no reward, asserting his independence with the declaration "I am Ranaud, and I go where I please, and do what I please. And I have taken a liking to thee" (021-031). Margaret is journeying to find [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand de Turincel]], her uncle, at his stronghold of [[turincel|Turincel]].

Throughout the journey, Ranaud proves an invaluable companion. He carries Jeanne in his great arms when the child tires, humming as he walks (022-003). He steals provisions from inn cellars to sustain the party, prompting Jeanne to exclaim "Gaston, how should we fare without thee?" (021-104). When Margaret asks him whether he will stay with them, he answers in his characteristic manner: "I will think on it. The lass is not the only one who hath taken a liking to thee" (021-035).

As they pass into the dangerous lands of Raoul, Ranaud remarks laconically: "Bad land" (022-004). His instincts prove correct.

### Capture by Raoul's men

The journey meets catastrophe when [[jeanne|Jeanne]] is seized by the men of [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]]. Ranaud acts instantly: he crashes into the bushes through which the attackers appeared, his quarterstaff gripped firmly, and "thundered down upon this group of men" (022-019). Despite his ferocity, he is overwhelmed by superior numbers. "Half-a-dozen men closed in upon Ranaud, swords drawn" (022-021), and though he fights with the fury of a cornered beast, he is beaten to the ground and bound.

Even in defeat, Ranaud remains defiant. Brought before Raoul, he roars his contempt with unforgettable venom:

> "Toad, toad! Misshapen toad! God's curse be upon thee!" (022-022)

And later, with equal ferocity:

> "And I will kill thee yet! Fat, shapeless spider!" (022-030)

During Margaret's interrogation by Raoul, Ranaud—still bound and helpless—cries out to strengthen her resolve:

> "Trust him not! Make no terms!" (022-084)

His loyalty to Margaret never wavers, even in the direst circumstances.

### Rescue and the fight for freedom

The dramatic rescue led by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] turns the tide. In the chaos that ensues, the two men holding Ranaud lose their heads—and Ranaud seizes his moment. He snatches a sword and falls to work, "using it like a quarterstaff, and causing considerable damage among his erstwhile captors" (024-010).

Ranaud fights his way out with the rest of the party. He follows the retreating group, "bringing up the rear, singing as he went" (024-020). The image of the wounded giant singing in the midst of combat perfectly captures his indomitable spirit. When they reach a gate, "Ranaud wrenched the gate open" with his bare strength (025-047), clearing the path to freedom and toward the safety of [[malvallet|Malvallet]].

The escape leaves him badly wounded. Simon undertakes to find "a surgeon for the wounded and very much shaken Ranaud" (024-124), ensuring his loyal companion receives the care he needs.

### Service to Simon of Beauvallet

Ranaud's story culminates in the final chapter. Recovered from his wounds, he comes before [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] and asks to join his guard. Simon commends him:

> "Ye did well, Ranaud, though I slew Raoul. Art a brave man, methinks." (028-023)

Ranaud responds with characteristic directness, smiting his thigh in emphasis (028-024) and explaining his choice:

> "Thought I to myself, by God, this is a fit master for me!" (028-025)

Simon accepts him into service, and Ranaud is formally enrolled into the household (028-029). His journey—from independent wanderer to loyal retainer—is complete. The free-spirited Frenchman who served no master and went where he pleased has found a lord worthy of his devotion.

## Name and alias

Ranaud travels under the alias "Gaston" while escorting the [[the-lady-margaret|Lady Margaret]] in disguise. Margaret addresses him by this name throughout their journey (021-004, 021-012, 021-107). The alias serves a practical purpose—concealing his identity in hostile territory—and he resumes his true name when he enters [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s service. The contrast between the two names mirrors his character arc: Gaston is the wandering adventurer, Ranaud is the sworn man.

## Relationships

### The Lady Margaret

Ranaud's primary loyalty throughout the novel is to [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]]. He protects her with a fierce, almost paternal devotion, guiding her through danger and bolstering her courage when her own resolve falters. His toast to her captures the essence of their bond:

> "Here's to a right brave lady." (021-143)

He drinks it standing, a gesture of genuine respect for the woman he has served.

### Jeanne

Ranaud shares a warm rapport with [[jeanne|Jeanne]], the child who accompanies Margaret. He carries her in his arms when she wearies, steals food to feed her, and seems to understand her without words—when "Jeanne shrugged, and Ranaud seemed to understand, for he nodded his great head wisely" (021-097). Their relationship brings out the gentler side of the giant.

### Simon of Beauvallet

Ranaud's relationship with [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] represents his character's resolution. Simon earns Ranaud's respect through his courage and competence in the rescue at [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]]'s castle, and Ranaud—who serves no one except by his own choice—elects to follow him. The final exchange between the two men is one of mutual recognition and respect.

### Raoul the Terrible

Ranaud's hatred for [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]] is expressed with savage eloquence. His insults—"Toad, toad! Misshapen toad!" (022-022) and "Fat, shapeless spider!" (022-030)—are among the novel's most memorable lines, embodying righteous fury in the face of tyranny.

## Themes and significance

Ranaud embodies the ideal of the free companion in medieval literature—the man who serves by choice rather than by obligation. His declaration "I am Ranaud, and I serve whom I please" (028-028) is the key to his character: his loyalty is not commanded but given, and therefore all the more valuable. In a novel centrally concerned with questions of fealty, service, and the bonds between lords and men, Ranaud offers a model in which service is freely chosen, rooted in genuine respect rather than inherited duty. His choice to follow [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] contrasts with the hereditary allegiance that binds figures like [[alan-of-montlice|Alan of Montlice]] to their lords.

His physicality—the giant frame, the great fist, the bottomless voice—also serves a thematic purpose, grounding the novel's action in the visceral reality of medieval life. Where [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] represents cold, calculated competence, Ranaud represents warm, uncalculating courage: the heart to Simon's head.
