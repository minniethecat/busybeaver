House of Montlice is the noble house into which Simon enters as a landless boy seeking service and from which he emerges, three years later, as a knight bound for greater things. It is the crucible of his early life, the institution that shapes his skills and his character, and the base from which he rides to the battle that will make his name.

## Nature and Allegiance

### A Northern Martial House

The House of Montlice is one of the great fighting houses of the English North. Its lord, Fulk of Montlice, is known as "Fulk the Lion" — a title that captures both his ferocity in battle and the leonine crest that his men carry. Fulk is described as "a giant of a man, deep-chested and massive," sitting at his ease with "the remains of my lord's breakfast still stood upon the table" (001-043) — a picture of the rough, physical, unceremonious nobility of the borderlands.

> "Leaning back at his ease, sat Fulk of Montlice, a giant of a man, deep-chested and massive." — [001-043]

The Montlice colors are red and gold (002-003), and the house's reputation for martial prowess is such that the mere name commands respect — and sometimes fear — throughout the North. When Alan of Montlice accosts a stranger at the gate, the man's reaction to the name is telling: "He is to be feared," Alan says of his own father (001-038), and the sentiment is clearly shared by those who know Fulk's reputation.

### Fealty to the Crown

Like all noble houses in the novel's England, House of Montlice owes fealty to the Crown. When the King summons his lords to Shrewsbury to face the rebellion of Hotspur, Fulk answers immediately and with enthusiasm — "Fulk, ever-ready for war, had chafed and fretted for some such summons" (003-004). He is a king's man not out of political calculation but out of temperament: war is what he does, and a king who offers war is a king worth serving.

The house's allegiance is put to the test at Shrewsbury, where Fulk leads his men into the thickest of the fighting. He fights under the Prince of Wales, placing his house and its men at the service of the royal cause. Alan, his son, "must lead our men back to Montlice" (003-246) if Fulk falls — a glimpse of the feudal chain that runs from father to son, from lord to heir, sustaining the house across generations.

## Key Members

### Fulk of Montlice, Lord and Patriarch

Fulk of Montlice is the heart of the house and the force that gives it its character. He is described as a giant of a man — physically imposing, loud, quick to rage and quick to laugh. His treatment of those around him alternates between roaring intimidation and rough affection, and his servants learn to navigate his moods with care.

> "What I have I hold!" Fulk roared at him once, pointing a thick finger. — [002-064]

His relationship with Simon is the defining one of Simon's early life. Fulk recognizes the boy's quality immediately — "he knew him for a soldier born" (002-086) — and though he tests him harshly, he also protects him and ultimately delivers the knighthood that Simon has earned. Fulk's acknowledgment that Simon is his half-brother — "the late lord's bastard son" (003-272) — adds a dimension of family obligation to the feudal bond between them.

The men who serve under Fulk respect him as a commander. "Montlice, leading his men in place of another who had fallen, drove deep into the press" (003-083) — this is leadership from the front, the kind of generalship that inspires men to follow into the most dangerous fighting. At Shrewsbury, his soldiers fight with the ferocity of men who know their lord expects nothing less.

### Alan of Montlice, Heir and Friend

Alan is Fulk's son and heir, a young man of gentler temperament than his father but no less courageous. When Simon first arrives at Montlice, it is Alan who meets him at the gate: "I am Alan of Montlice. What want you of us?" (001-029). The wary formality of that first encounter gives way, over time, to one of the novel's most important friendships.

> "I am Alan of Montlice," he said. "What want you of us?" — [001-029]

Alan and Simon are companions throughout the early part of the novel — practicing together in the yard, hunting, playing music in the turret room. Their friendship is a study in contrasts: Alan is emotional and expressive where Simon is cold and reserved; Alan plays the harp where Simon sharpens his sword. Yet the bond between them is genuine, forged in the shared experience of growing up in Fulk's formidable household.

Alan's role in the house is that of heir apparent. When Fulk is wounded at Shrewsbury, it is Alan who must "lead our men back to Montlice. If Fulk is dead, then do I owe allegiance to Alan, his son" (003-246). The transfer of authority from father to son is the mechanism by which noble houses survive across generations.

### Simon of Beauvallet, Fosterling and Half-Brother

Though Simon is not born a Montlice, his years in the household make him, for all practical purposes, a member of it. He sleeps "on a hard pallet across Fulk's threshold" (002-003), serves as Fulk's squire, fights under the Montlice banner, and is treated — after a fashion — as part of the family. When Fulk presents Simon to the Prince as "my half-brother, the late lord's bastard son" (003-272), he is publicly acknowledging a blood tie that complements the feudal bond.

Simon's relationship with the household is complex. He accepts Fulk's blows "in an acquiescence that surprised even Fulk himself" (002-005), but he also refuses to cringe or plead for mercy. The other squires learn to fear him, for "woe betide the squire or serf who crossed his path with mockery or insult" (002-005). He is both insider and outsider — a half-brother who does not carry the Montlice name, a squire who commands more respect than many knights.

### Eleanor of Montlice

Eleanor is Fulk's lady, the mistress of the Montlice household. She is mentioned in connection with Simon's knighting, when Fulk presents the newly dubbed knight to her: "I present thee Sir Simon of Beauvallet, my lady" (004-073). Her response — "Now art thou honoured indeed, lad!" (004-076) — captures the maternal pride that the household feels for Simon's achievement.

## Role in the Story

### The Furnace of Formation

The House of Montlice serves primarily as the environment in which Simon's character is forged. His three years there — from his arrival as a half-grown boy demanding service to his departure as a knight bound for the King's court — constitute the apprenticeship that prepares him for everything that follows.

Fulk's household is a harsh school. The lord is demanding and occasionally brutal; the other squires are jealous and hostile; the work is hard and the discipline severe. But it is precisely this harshness that Simon needs. A softer household would have softened him; Montlice hardens him, tests him, and ultimately proves him.

> "Fulk knew him for a soldier born, and spread the mantle of his protection about his squire, wondering how he would maintain his iron self-control." — [002-086]

"Watching his squire's iron equanimity with an appreciative eye" (002-063), Fulk sees the making of a great soldier — and perhaps of something more.

### The Battlefield Brotherhood

At Shrewsbury, the House of Montlice functions as a military unit. Fulk leads his men — his household knights, his squires, his men-at-arms — into the battle, and they fight as a single coordinated force. The battle is the supreme test of the house's martial character, and the Montlice men acquit themselves with distinction.

> "Montlice and Malvallet galloped forward, and the men behind them, cheering, followed." — [003-187]

The partnership between Montlice and Malvallet on the battlefield foreshadows the friendship between Simon and Geoffrey of Malvallet that will be one of the novel's enduring relationships. The older generation's alliance is replicated in the younger, binding the two houses together across the generations.

### A Launching Platform

The House of Montlice is ultimately a launching platform for Simon's greater destiny. Fulk knows this, and he does not stand in Simon's way when the King offers greater opportunities. When Simon leaves Montlice to take up his new estate at Beauvallet, he leaves behind the household that made him — but he carries its lessons with him, and his own house will bear the unmistakable imprint of Montlice's harsh but effective training.

## Structure

The Montlice household is organized around the person of Fulk himself. He sits at the center of a web of relationships that includes his son Alan, his wife Eleanor, his squires (chief among them Simon), his household knights, his men-at-arms, and the domestic servants who maintain the castle. The household's physical center is the great hall, where Fulk holds court, where meals are taken, and where the daily business of the lordship is transacted.

The hierarchy is clear and strictly enforced. Fulk's word is absolute law within his domain; even his son Alan kneels before him (001-049). Below Fulk come the knights and gentlemen of his retinue, then the squires, then the men-at-arms, then the servants and serfs. Simon's rise through this hierarchy — from the nameless boy at the gate to the knight who commands his own squire — is a demonstration of the mobility that the feudal system could offer to men of exceptional ability.
