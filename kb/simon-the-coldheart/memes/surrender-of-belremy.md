# Surrender of Belrémy

The **Surrender of Belrémy** was the culminating event of the [[siege-of-belremy|Siege of Belrémy]], occurring in 1405 when [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret of Belrémy]] yielded the keys of the town and castle to [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], acting in the name of [[king-henry-v|King Henry V of England]]. The surrender unfolded in two stages---first the town, then the castle proper---and represented a decisive moment in Simon's military career, establishing him as a commander capable of reducing a fortified French stronghold.

## Prelude: The Demand for Surrender

### Simon's First Overture

After [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] had positioned his forces at [[belremy|Belrémy]] and begun the siege in earnest, he made a formal demand for the town's capitulation. The response he received was defiant and contemptuous, setting the tone for what would become a bitter and protracted struggle.

> Simon sent a herald to the town, bidding them surrender, but the Lady Margaret returned a hot answer, that he should enter Belrémy over her dead body. (013-010)

The Lady Margaret's defiance was not mere bravado. She was a woman of fierce pride and indomitable will, and she had prepared Belrémy's defenses with care. Her refusal to yield to a mere English knight, however formidable his reputation, reflected both her personal courage and her determination to hold the castle that was her birthright.

### Geoffrey's Grim Oath

As the assault began, [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]], Simon's half-brother and trusted lieutenant, swore a solemn oath. The bond between the two men, forged on the field at Shrewsbury, had deepened with shared hardship, and Geoffrey's words carried the weight of that brotherhood:

> "Simon, if so be they slay thee before thou hast flung open the gates, Belrémy shall have no quarter. That I swear." (014-033)

This oath underscored the personal stakes of the siege. For Geoffrey, the campaign was not merely a matter of duty to the Crown but a test of loyalty to his half-brother. His promise of merciless retribution hung over the defenders like a sword.

## The Fall of the Town

### Street-by-Street Fighting

The fighting that followed the initial breach was fierce and chaotic. Simon's men poured through the broken gates and spread through the narrow streets of Belrémy, meeting determined resistance from the French defenders at every corner. The battle raged from house to house, square to square, as the English forces methodically pushed toward the marketplace.

> Fully an hour the fight lasted, all over the town, before the French broke. First in one place, and then in another, the French cried for quarter. Quarter was granted everywhere. (014-154)

The marketplace became the focal point of the struggle. Simon's men fought their way through a "medley of fighting men" until, by sheer weight of arms, the French were driven back and the marketplace fell to the English (014-142..014-143). The fall of the marketplace signaled the collapse of organized resistance in the town.

### The Keys of the Town

With the town's defenses shattered and quarter having been granted to the surviving defenders, the civic authorities of Belrémy made their submission:

> Soon the sheriff sent to Simon, who had pushed his way back to the marketplace, surrendering the keys of the town. (014-154)

Simon accepted the keys with his characteristic calm, his cold grey eyes betraying no emotion. He did not gloat over the victory or berate the defeated; he simply received their submission and moved on to the next phase of the operation. By late that same day, the marketplace was almost cleared of the dead and wounded, and Simon's men had secured the town (014-161).

### Establishing Headquarters

With the town in English hands, Simon established his command post at the justice-house, a suitable building from which to govern the occupied territory and coordinate the final assault on the castle itself. It was from this position that he would direct the next, and more delicate, phase of the operation: dealing with the Lady Margaret (015-004).

## The Castle Confrontation

### The Formal Ultimatum

The castle proper remained defiant even after the town had fallen. Its walls were strong, its garrison still intact, and its commander---the Lady Margaret herself---showed no sign of yielding. Simon therefore delivered a formal ultimatum, couched in the precise language of royal authority:

> "In the name of His Most Gracious Majesty, King Henry the Fifth of England and France, I, Simon of Beauvallet, command that ye surrender the keys of the Castle of Belrémy within the hour, swearing fealty to His Majesty King Henry, and delivering the keys into my hands." (015-009)

He further specified the terms of compliance: the castle was to be surrendered within twelve hours, with the keys delivered to Ferdinand de Valmé, Simon's sheriff---and no harm was to come to Sir Alan of Montlice, who was held prisoner within (015-017).

### The Hostage Crisis

The Lady Margaret did not yield easily. In a desperate move, she threatened the lives of hostages---children of the castle household---if Simon pressed his demand. Simon responded not with threats but with cold precision, making it clear that the consequences of continued defiance would fall not upon the hostages but upon the entire population of Belrémy:

> "If ye surrender not this castle, and Sir Alan of Montlice, then will ye indeed be fools. Ye will see my soldiers burn Belrémy to the ground, and slay all those who dwell therein." (015-099)

### The Terms of Clemency

Simon's approach was not merely coercive; it was also pragmatic. He offered genuine clemency to those who submitted, distinguishing himself from commanders who might have been tempted to sack the town and put the garrison to the sword:

> "If ye do surrender unto me the keys of this castle, and do swear fealty to my master, King Henry — I can offer you his gracious protection and clemency." (015-079)

This dual approach---the credible threat of annihilation balanced against the promise of mercy---was characteristic of Simon's cold, calculating style of command. He did not make threats he was not prepared to carry out, and he did not offer terms he did not intend to honor.

## The Lady Margaret Yields

### The Final Confrontation

The decisive confrontation between Simon and Margaret came when Simon spoke to her directly, his voice as cold as his grey eyes. When she challenged his authority and questioned his willingness to act, he answered without hesitation:

> "An ye fail to order your men to submit, madame, ye will see how much I dare." (015-127)

He demanded that she command her soldiers formally: "Bid thy men swear before God to submit themselves" (015-140). Margaret, cornered and outmaneuvered, protested that he was forcing her hand through pity for those who would suffer if she continued to resist: "Ye seek to force me to yield through pity!" (015-146).

### The Capitulation

The final push came not from Simon but from Margaret's own cousin, who recognized that further resistance was futile: "Cousin, thou must yield!" (015-152). At last, the capitulation came---offered, with studied formality, not by Margaret herself but through her representative:

> "Then I submit, milor', in the name of the Countess Margaret." (015-158)

The phrasing was significant. By having her submission delivered through an intermediary, Margaret preserved a shred of her dignity even as she surrendered everything else. She was the Countess of Belrémy, and she would not abase herself before an English knight, however complete her defeat.

## Aftermath and Significance

### Occupation and Control

The surrender of Belrémy was complete. The town and castle passed into Simon's control, held in the name of [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]]. For [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]], the victory established him unequivocally as a military commander of proven capability. He had taken a fortified town by siege, forced the submission of a determined and resourceful opponent, and done so with a minimum of unnecessary bloodshed. His reputation, already considerable, was now formidable.

### The Role of Alan of Montlice

The fate of [[alan-of-montlice|Sir Alan of Montlice]], held prisoner by the Lady Margaret during the siege, was a critical factor in the surrender negotiations. Alan, the gentle and chivalric friend of [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]], had been captured early in the siege and his life hung in the balance throughout. Simon's ultimatum explicitly tied the terms of surrender to Alan's safety: no terms would be honoured if harm came to the prisoner (015-017). Margaret's willingness to use Alan as a hostage — and her ultimate decision not to harm him — shaped the moral calculus of the surrender. Alan's survival was a testament both to Margaret's residual [[chivalry|honour]] and to Simon's refusal to abandon his friend, even when military logic might have dictated otherwise.

### The Transfer of the Keys

The formal mechanism of surrender was the delivery of the castle keys into Simon's hands. This act, freighted with medieval symbolism, represented the transfer of lordship from the house of [[belremy|Belrémy]] to the Crown. [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] received the keys not as a personal prize but as the King's representative, and his subsequent administration of the captured territory — establishing a sheriff, quartering his troops, and maintaining order — demonstrated the transition from military conquest to civil governance that characterised effective occupation. The marketplace, where the town keys had been surrendered hours earlier (014-154), became Simon's temporary seat of authority, a visible symbol of the changed order.

### The Submission to the Crown

The wider significance of the surrender extended beyond Belrémy itself. By reducing this fortress, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] had demonstrated that Lancastrian authority could be projected deep into [[normandy|Normandy]], overcoming not only military resistance but also the feudal loyalties that might have been expected to sustain a rebellious lord. The later submission of [[turincel|Turincel]] (023-081) can be seen as a direct consequence: when [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand de Turincel]] saw that Belrémy had fallen, he calculated that his own resistance would be futile. The surrender of Belrémy thus had a cascading effect on the political landscape of the region, neutralising not only the immediate target but also its potential allies.

### The Seeds of Further Conflict

For [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]], the submission was bitter but not absolute. Her pride had been wounded, her authority usurped, and her castle occupied. But her will remained unbroken. Within days of the surrender, she would begin plotting her escape, setting in motion events that would lead to her flight from Belrémy and a pursuit across the French countryside that would test Simon's resolve in ways the siege itself had not. The surrender of Belrémy was thus not an ending but a beginning---the opening move in a contest of wills between Simon and Margaret that would define the latter half of the novel.
