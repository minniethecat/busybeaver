# Siege of Belrémy

The **Siege of Belrémy** was a military campaign conducted in Normandy between October and December 1417, during the [[normandy|Norman]] phase of the [[hundred-years-war|Hundred Years' War]]. Commanded by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], the siege was his first major independent command and resulted in the capture of one of the strongest fortresses remaining in French hands after [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]]'s second invasion of France.

## Strategic Context

### The Norman Campaign of 1417

By 1417, Henry V had returned to France for his second major campaign, following the victory at [[agincourt|Agincourt]] two years earlier. The English strategy was to subdue Normandy systematically, reducing towns and strongholds one by one before pressing on to Paris. Simon of Beauvallet, now past thirty and "famous for his generalship," was "spoken of as the Fifth Henry's friend, and the Iron Lord" (012-006).

### Umfraville's Defeat

Before Simon received the assignment, [[gilbert-of-umfraville|Gilbert of Umfraville]] had attempted and failed to take Belrémy. When the King summoned Simon to his quarters at Alençon, Umfraville was present and the failure hung over the council. Umfraville himself acknowledged Simon's superior ability:

> "Unlike me, thou'lt be like unto Caesar, Simon... To Belrémy wilt thou go, and where I saw, thou wilt also conquer." (012-066)

### The Assignment

Simon was blunt with the King about the cause of Umfraville's defeat: "Ye did underrate the enemy. The task had been too easy before" (012-072). Henry's response gave the campaign its memorable name: "And now a woman baulks me. So I send her Simon the Coldheart" (012-073).

The King was simultaneously besieging [[falaise|Falaise]], held by the Sire de Mauny. The dual campaign — Henry at Falaise, Simon at Belrémy — was a coordinated effort to seal Norman resistance. Alan of Montlice observed that Belrémy was "the harder task" (012-103) because "a man holds Falaise, and a woman, Belrémy. I would sooner fight a man than a woman" (012-110).

## Forces and Deployment

### The English Army

Simon appeared before Belrémy with "an army of fifteen hundred strong" (013-003). The force was organized in three divisions:

- **Van**: [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]], Simon's half-brother, commanding the forward division
- **Left Wing**: [[john-holland-earl-of-huntingdon|John Holland, Earl of Huntingdon]], a young but fiercely brave nobleman
- **Right Wing**: Simon himself, with [[alan-of-montlice|Alan of Montlice]] acting as Master of Simon's Horse
- **Rear**: [[walter-of-santoy|Walter of Santoy]] with the ordnance and provisions, including surgeons, a priest, and [[john-tarbury|John Tarbury]] as Master of Works
- **Squires**: [[cedric-of-gountray|Cedric of Gountray]] and Edmund Marnet (013-003)

### The Fortress of Belrémy

"Belrémy stood upon a slight incline, with its castle frowning down upon this force, and its grey walls sullen and forbidding" (013-004). The town was considered exceptionally well-fortified. Umfraville had warned Simon that "the town is so strongly fortified that 'twould take thee months to batter down the walls. Provisions they seem to have in plenty" (012-078). Alan's first reaction upon seeing it — "God's my life! I like not this place!" — was met by Simon's tiger-snarl of a smile, and Montlice "shivered a little, pitying Belrémy" (013-005--013-006).

## The Siege

### Encampment and Preparations

Within an hour of arrival, Simon set his army to work:

> "His men were set to build wooden huts, for Simon anticipated a prolonged siege, and winter was drawing on. Trenches were dug, and palisades erected for the protection of the army, and until these were finished, some ten days later, the camp was hard at work, both officers and men." (013-009)

This methodical preparation reflected Henry V's teachings. Simon "had learned the art of war under Henry, and he followed his precepts strictly" (013-011).

### The Lady Margaret's Defiance

Simon sent a herald demanding surrender. The Lady Margaret's answer was immediate and defiant: "he should enter Belrémy over her dead body. Simon had no taste for heroics, and he received this answer indifferently" (013-010).

### The Blockade

The siege proceeded as a slow strangulation. The English bombardment proved ineffective against Belrémy's granite walls. Geoffrey, ever impetuous, confronted Simon:

> "Shall we sit down before this town forever? To what avail your bombardment? The walls of Belrémy seem made of granite! They laugh at thy guns! I tell thee, Simon, this is waste of time!" (013-015)

Simon's strategic reply revealed his methodology: "So that I may weaken their fortifications, and, by hunger, weaken the soldiers" (013-018). He refused to lose patience, though "Geoffrey of Malvallet was nigh to weeping from boredom and inactivity" (013-011).

When Geoffrey complained that his men "grow troublesome, and murmur," Simon's rebuke was cold: "Check their murmuring, then. 'Twere to more avail than this whining in mine ear" (013-030). Geoffrey submitted: "Thou art right, Simon. I will mend my ways. Thy pardon" (013-037).

### The Mine

The key to Simon's strategy lay underground. John Tarbury, Master of Works, supervised the digging of a mine — a tunnel running from the English camp beneath the town walls to a corner of waste ground within Belrémy itself (014-003). The mine was not designed to collapse the walls but to provide a covert infiltration route.

### The Christmas Pledge

To steady morale through the grinding weeks of blockade, Simon made a definitive promise to his half-brother Geoffrey:

> "Thou shalt feast at Christmas within those walls. I pledge thee my word." (013-026)

Alan's faith in this pledge was absolute: "If he told me he would march into Hell by Christmas and enslave the Devil, I would follow him" (013-070).

### The Humanitarian Principle

When Alan suggested simply holding the siege until the defenders starved, Simon's reply revealed an unexpected dimension of his character:

> "Fool! Were I to hold this town till starvation came, I should enter it over children's bodies. I war not with babes." (013-055)

This refusal to wage war against non-combatants would be reinforced by his orders of mercy on the day of the assault itself.

## The Assault

### Planning

Simon struck "a week before the promised date" (014-003), with a plan of elegant coordination. He called a council of his captains — Huntingdon, Malvallet, and Alan — and laid out a three-pronged attack (014-005):

1. **Huntingdon's Diversion**: At seven in the morning, a full-force assault on the western gate "with much to-do and noise," drawing the garrison's attention and fire. The wall there had been weakened by cannon bombardment.
2. **The Mine Party**: At five o'clock, twelve men would creep through the mine, break through the earth crust inside the town, wait for Huntingdon's signal, then race to the southern gates and open them.
3. **Malvallet's Charge**: With the gates open, Geoffrey would charge the van through, ride westward to relieve Huntingdon, while Alan pressed to the centre.

### Simon as the Twelfth Man

Simon assigned himself the most dangerous task — leading the mine party personally:

> "I lead those who enter the town by the mine. I am the twelfth man." (014-012)

His captains protested vehemently. Huntingdon offered to take Simon's place; Alan begged "for the love that lies betwixt us two assign thy task to me" (014-024). Simon refused with a single word: "Nay" (014-017). When asked what would happen if he fell, he answered: "Then shall ye be commander in my room. I fall not" (014-019).

### The Sealed Will

Before the assault, Simon produced a sealed document dividing his wealth between Geoffrey and Alan, with specific bequests to his marshal Maurice of Gountray. He asked that they care for Cedric and Edmund. Geoffrey was alarmed: "Simon, ye have never spoken thus before. Not in all our fights. What ill-omen dost thou feel, my brother?" (014-047). Simon acknowledged only that "the fight will be stern" (014-049).

### Orders of Mercy

Simon's final command to his captains typified the discipline he demanded:

> "If any man strike down a woman or child in the fight, or offer injury where none is courted, his life will I take, and that right speedily. I will have no burning or pillaging, but order and chivalry." (014-038)

### Through the Mine

Before dawn, Simon and his hand-picked party entered the mine, walking "bent almost double" through the "dank, earth-smelling passage" (014-072). At the end, Malcolm Clayton's pick broke through the crust. Simon peered out, nodded, and the men banked earth into a step.

There he discovered a thirteenth man — his young squire Cedric, who had disobeyed orders to join him. "You and I shall have a reckoning to settle for this, Cedric" (014-076), Simon said, but did not send him back.

### The Gates

At seven o'clock, Huntingdon's horn sounded thrice. Simon led his party onto waste ground, then raced for the southern gates shouting "The Seneschal! The Seneschal!" to deceive the sentries (014-108). They fought up the gate-tower stairs, overwhelmed the small garrison left to guard the gate, and Simon himself wrenched the lever to lower the drawbridge.

> "Plunging into Belrémy came the English, Malvallet at their head, unmistakable by his black plumes and surcoat." (014-122)

Geoffrey brought Simon's black charger. Alan followed with the horse-archers, red plumes waving. Walter of Santoy brought up the rearguard.

### The Battle

The marketplace became a "medley of fighting men, a blaze of colour" — Montlice red-and-gold, Malvallet black, Beauvallet green-and-russet, and the King's men (014-142). Simon reorganized his forces into two divisions and "charged after the great golden figure ahead, bearing down upon the enemy like a battering ram" (014-143).

During the street fighting, a child ran "almost beneath the plunging hoofs of Simon's horse." He wrenched back the animal, stooped, hoisted the child up, and cut his way to the terrified mother before handing the child down (014-144--014-146). Simon then cried "For St. George and the King!" and his men roared out the Agincourt song (014-149).

After an hour of fighting, the French broke and cried for quarter. Quarter was granted everywhere. The sheriff surrendered the keys of the town (014-154).

### Alan's Capture

Triumph turned to crisis when Geoffrey returned with devastating news: "Simon, Simon! Alan is taken! Taken by that she-devil, and carried into her stronghold!" (014-162). Alan, wounded in the fighting, had been overcome by the Lady Margaret's remaining men-at-arms and held as a hostage within the castle keep.

Simon's response was a whispered oath: "If I have not Alan by nightfall, may my soul wither in hell!" (014-164).

## The Surrender of the Castle

### The Exchange of Ultimatums

By noon, Simon held the town but not the castle. He held one crucial advantage: Huntingdon had captured the Lady Margaret's uncle, the Sire de Galledemaine, her Marshal. Simon dictated a formal demand for surrender and Alan's delivery (015-009).

The Lady Margaret replied with defiance: if the English did not withdraw within twelve hours and surrender the town keys, Alan would "swing from the ramparts in thy sight" (015-017).

Simon's counter-threat was terrifying in its calculated cruelty. He swore by the Rood that if any harm came to Alan, he would execute Galledemaine, slay every third breadwinner in the town, and ultimately "raze this city to the ground, slaying all who dwell therein and sparing neither woman nor child" (015-022). He added a piece of stagecraft — six children would die before the castle gates if she did not surrender — and instructed his herald:

> "If the Lady Margaret should speak with thee, asking what manner of man I may be, thou wilt tell her that what I say I will do, I do." (015-025)

### The Parley

The Lady Margaret offered to negotiate — if Simon came alone, under truce, into the castle. Geoffrey protested it was a trap. Simon disagreed: "She dare not harm me, or detain me" (015-034). He left instructions: if he did not return within the hour, Galledemaine was to be executed, then the town sacked, then the castle stormed.

Simon entered the audience chamber in full gilded armour. The Lady Margaret stood "like a pillar of ice" in wine-red silk beside her foppish cousin, the Chevalier de Fleurival (015-073). When she refused to submit and threatened to have Alan killed, Simon drew his sword and placed its point at her breast:

> "One step more, and your mistress dies. The truce is at an end." (015-117)

When Margaret ordered her men to kill Alan, they refused — "Madame, I dare not" (015-135). The Chevalier took charge: "Then I submit, milor', in the name of the Countess Margaret" (015-158).

### Alan's Rescue

Margaret was forced to lead Simon to Alan, who lay wounded in a tower chamber. Alan's first reaction upon seeing his rescuer was anguish: "Simon! Ah, no, Simon! Not thou! Death were easier!" (015-182). He had assumed Simon had been captured too.

### The Lady Margaret as Hostage

Simon brought the Lady Margaret to the justice-house as his hostage. The town was "amazed at thy clemency" (016-061). But the Lady Margaret was not yet vanquished:

> "Let there be an understanding between us, sir... I give no parole, I swear no allegiance. It is war between us to the death, for I am not vanquished yet, nor will be! Ye would do well to beware my vengeance, Lord of Beauvallet!" (016-040--016-042)

As Simon himself reflected: "Belrémy is not wholly mine yet, though I hold the town and the castle" (016-047). The military phase was complete; the political and personal consequences would unfold through the novel's later chapters.

## Aftermath and Significance

The Siege of Belrémy established Simon's reputation as the foremost siege commander in Henry V's army. The campaign demonstrated every principle of his military art: methodical preparation, preservation of his men through disciplined blockade rather than rash assault, coordinated multi-point attacks, personal leadership in the most dangerous role, and a policy of mercy toward civilians that contrasted with the brutal norms of fifteenth-century warfare.

Simon had kept his Christmas pledge: Alan and Geoffrey did indeed feast within Belrémy's walls by Christmas Day. The siege also introduced the volatile relationship between Simon and the Lady Margaret, Countess of Belrémy — a relationship that would prove to be the catalyst for awakening the sleeping passion that Henry V had long predicted lay dormant in Simon the Coldheart.
