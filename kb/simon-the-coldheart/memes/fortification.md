Fortifications in the novel are the physical embodiment of military power. Castles like [[belremy|Belrémy]] and [[montlice|Montlice]] are not merely settings but strategic assets — their walls, towers, and keeps define the terms of siege warfare that drives much of the plot.

## In the Novel

When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] surveys [[belremy|Belrémy]] before the siege, he assesses its fortifications with a professional eye: "So that I may weaken their fortifications, and, by hunger, weaken the soldiers" (013-018). His methodical approach treats fortifications as problems to be solved through engineering and patience rather than reckless assault.

The novel's fortifications range from the imposing castle of Belrémy, held by the Lady Margaret, to [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]]'s stronghold at [[turincel|Turincel]], from which Simon must stage a daring rescue. Each fortified location carries its own strategic character and narrative weight. The walls of Belrémy, in particular, become a symbol of resistance — both Margaret's defiance of English rule and Simon's determination to fulfil his king's command.

## Historical Background

Early 15th-century fortifications represented centuries of military architectural development. Concentric castles with multiple layers of defence, machicolations, and improved gatehouses had evolved in response to increasingly powerful siege engines. The novel's period sits at the cusp of change — gunpowder artillery was beginning to render traditional castle fortifications obsolete, though the process would take decades to complete.
