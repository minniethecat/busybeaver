## Introduction

King Henry V is the sovereign authority who presides over the entire world of Simon's public life. Though a real historical figure, he appears in the novel as a character in his own right: a keen judge of men, a shrewd political operator, and a monarch who values competence above flattery. His relationship with Simon forms the axis around which Simon's career as knight and governor turns. From the moment Simon kneels to receive the accolade at Shrewsbury, through the grant of lands in Normandy, to the final summons that sends Simon to reclaim Beauvallet, every major event in Simon's external life bears the King's seal.

## First Encounters and Knighthood

The King first enters the narrative indirectly, through the political world Simon is about to join. In the year 1403, "Bolingbroke had sat upon the throne for four years, and his son, Henry of Monmouth, had held the reins of government in the Welsh Marches" (003-003). The young Prince — not yet king — is already a formidable presence. When Simon asks what manner of man the King is, Fulk replies with blunt admiration: "Nay, by the Rood! Henry is a man, even as his son" (003-109). This early characterisation establishes the Lancastrian line as men of action, not mere ornament.

After the Battle of Shrewsbury, where Simon distinguishes himself, the King knights him on the field. The moment is stark and moving:

> "Rise, Sir Simon of Beauvallet," Simon came to his feet and stood before the King. "I have heard yet more of your doings, Sir Simon," Henry said, twinkling. "Paul of Lendar is well pleased with you." (004-041, 004-042)

The King's tone — twinkling, pleased, familiar — signals that Simon has already entered royal favour. This is not routine; it is personal recognition.

## The Audience at Westminster

The novel's most extended portrait of Henry comes in Chapter 7, when Simon presents himself at Westminster for a formal audience. The King receives him not in open court but in a private chamber, attended by "six or seven gentlemen of the Council" (007-036). The atmosphere is serious but not hostile. Henry interrogates Simon on his fitness to hold the captured lands, and Simon answers with characteristic directness.

A revealing exchange occurs when the King asks Simon to name his desire. Simon requests the grant of a lordship — and adds that he would wish to be known by an epithet:

> "That is a strange wish!" Henry said. "Wherefore? Dost like to be called Simon?" (011-007)

When Simon explains his preference for plainness, the King offers a compromise richer than Simon imagined:

> "Thou shalt have it, then," Henry promised. "Simon of the Gilded Girdle." (011-009)

When Simon demurs — the girdle is Alan's gift, not his own doing — the King revises:

> "Then shall I be foolish," Henry said, "for I shall call thee Simon the Coldheart." (011-011)

Thus the novel's title, the protagonist's defining epithet, originates in the King's amused but perceptive judgment.

## Judge of Men and Merit

Henry's most striking quality in the novel is his capacity to read men. He is not a remote figure issuing decrees from a throne; he interrogates, observes, and forms his own conclusions. When Granmere comes to court with accusations against Simon, the King hears him out but is not swayed:

> "I cannot believe so empty a tale," the Duke of York said hotly. "Perhaps," Henry said. He gave a short, half-sceptical laugh. (006-075, 006-078)

The King's studied neutrality is itself a judgment. He knows that men bring him self-serving tales and he has learned to sift truth from ambition. His preference is for men who speak plainly and act effectively — a preference that Simon satisfies completely.

The theme recurs when the Council debates Simon's fitness. One councillor whispers sceptically to his neighbour about Simon's request, and the King responds with measured deliberation: "It seems not much to ask," Henry said slowly. He looked at one of his Council. "What know ye of this place?" (007-088). He will not be rushed; he gathers facts and decides on merit.

## Military Strategy and the Norman Campaign

In the novel's later sections, the King directs the broader strategy of the Norman conquest. He entrusts the Cotenin campaign to his brother, the Duke of Gloucester, while Simon serves as a trusted captain within that theatre. Gloucester "plans to lay siege to Cherbourg" (029-054), and Simon's mission is to subdue outlying strongholds one by one. The King's warrant gives Simon's authority its legitimacy — when Simon presents himself at the gates of enemy castles, he does so "in the name of King Henry of England" (023-170).

The King's military acumen is not described in tactical detail but is implied by results. His captains win; his strategy works. He delegates authority to competent men and gives them latitude. The Duke of Clarence commands on one front; Gloucester on another; Simon serves both, always with the King's commission behind him.

When Simon finally departs to reclaim his own ancestral lands, the King's approval is essential. Simon's mission is a royal one — he goes bearing the King's authority, and his success serves the Crown's interests in pacifying Normandy.

## Relationship with Simon

The bond between King and knight rests on a foundation of mutual respect that is rare in the novel's world. Simon never flatters; the King never demands flattery. When Simon stands before the Council and refuses to embellish his claims, the King is amused rather than offended. "And to your advancement," Henry said in amusement at one exchange (007-080) — a dry humour that Simon reciprocates with his own solemnity.

This relationship matures across the novel. In the early chapters, it is the King who tests and measures Simon. By the later campaigns, Simon has become a known quantity — a man the King can dispatch on delicate missions with confidence. The warrant Henry issues for Simon to reclaim his lands is not a gift but an investment: the King knows his man will deliver.

The King's final appearance in the novel carries this theme of settled trust. When news arrives from Gloucester — "Simon is alive and well" (031-004) — the brief report is sufficient. The King does not need to see Simon to know that his servant is doing his work.

## Depiction as a Historical Character

Georgette Heyer's Henry V is consistent with the historical portrait found in chronicles: a disciplined, intelligent ruler who transformed himself from a dissolute youth (the "Prince Hal" of legend) into a formidable warrior-king. The novel alludes to this transformation obliquely. When Simon first hears of "young Henry of Monmouth," Fulk tells him the Prince is "one year thy junior" (003-101). The implication is that the King himself was young once, and that his rise to authority parallels Simon's own.

The novel does not pretend to historical completeness. Henry's great victory at Agincourt, his marriage to Katherine of Valois, his tragic early death from dysentery — none of these are narrated. Instead, the King appears only when Simon's story intersects with royal authority. The effect is to make Henry a presence rather than a protagonist: he is the fixed star by which Simon navigates.

## Significance and Themes

King Henry V embodies the novel's ideal of legitimate authority. He is contrasted implicitly with the "usurper" epithet used by his enemies and explicitly with the rebels Simon suppresses. His rule is orderly, meritocratic, and effective. He rewards competence; he punishes rebellion. His portrait validates Simon's choice to serve a master — and to serve that master well.

The King's role also anchors the novel in its historical moment. Without him, the story would be a purely fictional tale of a knight in Normandy. With him, it becomes a window into the Lancastrian enterprise in France — the ambition, the violence, and the precarious order imposed by a king who demanded loyalty and returned it with patronage.
