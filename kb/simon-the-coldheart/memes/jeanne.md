## Identity and First Appearance

Jeanne de Faucourt is the principal lady-in-waiting to [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret of Belremy]], introduced in the aftermath of Simon's parley as the Countess's closest confidante. Her surname is used precisely once in the narrative — "'But still a man,' nodded Jeanne de Faucourt. 'He hath thy measure, Margot'" (017-011) — establishing her noble lineage while allowing the intimate "Jeanne" and the affectionate "Jeannette" (017-028) to dominate subsequent references.

Margaret's first words to her establish their bond of trust: "'Jeanne, stay with me'" (017-004). It is a command and a plea, and Jeanne's response — "Jeanne folded her hands. A smile hovered at the corners of her mouth" (017-008) — reveals the quiet self-possession that defines her character. She is not awed by her mistress's grandeur; she has known Margaret too long and too intimately for that.

> "But still a man," nodded Jeanne de Faucourt, with the calm assessment of one who has watched her mistress navigate the currents of power and passion for years. "He hath thy measure, Margot." (017-011)

## Gentle Adviser to the Countess

### The Voice of Reason

Jeanne's primary narrative function is to provide a counterweight to Margaret's proud intransigence. Where the Countess rages, Jeanne counsels patience; where Margaret declares undying hatred, Jeanne offers the gentle suggestion that "hate is akin to love" (025-089). Her role is not to challenge Margaret's authority but to temper it with the wisdom of someone who knows her mistress better than she knows herself.

When Margaret, humiliated by Simon's rough treatment, insists she could break him, Jeanne responds with the wry observation of a confidante who has seen her mistress's strengths and knows their limits: "'But thou art a woman, cherie,' Jeanne said gently" (017-036). She does not deny Margaret's power — she acknowledges it — but she refuses to indulge the fantasy that force alone can solve what force has created.

> "Thou art too warlike," Jeanne chided her, and knelt by the chair, bringing herself to Margaret's level not as a servant but as a sister. (017-029)

Jeanne's wisdom in these exchanges is both practical and psychological. When Margaret rages against Simon — "'until I have avenged myself! Jeanne, Jeanne, have ye forgotten how he served me?'" (017-048) — Jeanne does not argue the merits of revenge. Instead she observes, quietly and devastatingly: "'I would have found another way. He is not easily turned from his purpose'" (017-054). The observation is not a criticism of Margaret but a recognition of Simon's quality — and through that recognition, an implicit invitation for Margaret to see the English lord as something other than a pure enemy.

### The Confidante's Test

Jeanne's loyalty is absolute, but it is not blind. When Margaret's schemes seem to place principle above prudence, Jeanne's unvarnished counsel reveals the depth of her devotion. After Margaret's attempt on Simon's life fails, "Margaret had cast herself into Jeanne's arms in a fit of wild weeping" (017-138), and Jeanne's response is calm and knowing: "'I knew thou couldst not slay him. I saw thee creep into his room'" (017-140). She has watched her mistress attempt the impossible and is not surprised by the failure — only moved by the pain it has caused.

> "Oh, my dear, my dear!" Jeanne cried, and flung her arms about Margaret, the formal courtly distance collapsing into the embrace of one woman who loves another beyond rank or station. (017-143)

Throughout Margaret's captivity and the turbulent months that follow, Jeanne remains steadfastly at her side. She accompanies the Countess during the disguised flight from [[belremy|Belremy]], endures the terrifying journey through [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]]'s lands, and is present at every crucial turning point — from the Chevalier's furtive visits to Margaret's chambers to the final, tearful parting with [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]].

## The Terrace Courtship

### Geoffrey's Arrival

Chapter 18, titled "How He Found Geoffrey and Jeanne on the Terrace," contains Jeanne's most extended and charming scene. The chapter opens with an image of domestic tranquillity: "On the broad terrace Jeanne sat sewing, a fur cloak about her" (018-003). The pastoral peace is broken by Geoffrey's entrance, dressed to impress in "crimson velvet and gold lacing" and greeted with Jeanne's characteristic mockery: "'Oh, fie! The soldier turned popinjay, i' faith. He shames the sun'" (018-004).

What follows is a masterpiece of flirtatious indirection conducted in the elaborate idiom of courtly love. Geoffrey tells an allegorical tale of a "great, stupid Englishman" who has lost his heart to a "little lady with the big blue eyes and the pretty dimples" (018-037) — a description transparent to both speaker and listener but maintained with elaborate pretense.

> "And French." Jeanne nodded dreamily. "An enemy. I have always thought I could never love an Englishman." (018-040)

Jeanne parries each advance with feigned incomprehension, playing the part of the reluctant lady with delightfully transparent artifice. "'Jeanne,' Geoffrey said pleadingly" (018-017), and she deflects. "'Nay, Jeanne!'" (018-021) he protests, and she retreats further into her sewing: "'He will certainly be pricked,' Jeanne said, plying her needle faster" (018-022).

### The Confession

Beneath the teasing, Jeanne's own feelings gradually emerge. When Geoffrey describes his allegorical heart, she offers her own version:

> "Perhaps, after all — it was still a virgin heart. The — the lady's, I mean." (018-051)

The hesitation — "The — the lady's" — is the sound of Jeanne's guard dropping, the confession hidden within a denial. Geoffrey presses, and Jeanne, still holding her needlework as a shield, continues her counter-allegory:

> "It was such a timid little heart, that the man — he was a great, stupid Englishman — never knew that it was in his pocket, but besought the lady to give it to him." (018-060)

The form is courtly indirection, but the substance is a woman admitting she has loved the man before her all along. When Geoffrey finally gathers her into his arms, Jeanne's admission is sweetened by continued teasing: "'And might it be won, Jeanne?' he asked" (018-053). "'By an English lover, Jeanne'" (018-056). And her reply acknowledges both his persistence and his character: "'He was strong and masterful withal,' Jeanne murmured, and let her stitchery fall" (018-062). The dropped sewing is the dropped guard, the final surrender of playful resistance to acknowledged love.

> "He was strong and masterful withal," Jeanne murmured, and let her stitchery fall — the symbol of her careful self-containment abandoned in the face of a love she could no longer pretend to resist. (018-062)

### The Question of Loyalty

The courtship scene ends on a note of genuine moral gravity. Between Geoffrey's kisses, Jeanne stirs the question that will govern the rest of the narrative:

> "Oh, but we are traitors, both! Thou to the Lord Beauvallet, I to the Lady Margaret! While these two stay at enmity I must cleave to the one, and thou to the other." (018-078)

This is not coyness but principle. Jeanne's love for Geoffrey is real, but her loyalty to Margaret is absolute. The two loyalties are in tension, and Jeanne makes clear that, should the conflict prove irreconcilable, Margaret comes first. It is this integrity — the refusal to be a traitor to her mistress even for love — that gives Jeanne her moral stature, and that makes her eventual acceptance of the English presence in [[belremy|Belremy]], once the central enmity is resolved, a meaningful marker of the novel's resolution rather than a simple romantic payoff.

## The Flight and Rescue

### Portrait of a Confidante

When Margaret decides to flee [[belremy|Belremy]] disguised as a page's sister, Jeanne joins her without hesitation. Margaret's excited planning reveals their intimate bond: "'Smile, Jeannette! It means escape, and help!'" (019-051). The diminutive "Jeannette" — used in moments of heightened emotion — speaks of a relationship that predates and surpasses their formal roles.

The escape itself is harrowing. Margaret whispers to Jeanne: "'Thou silly Jeannette! — till we came to the brook'" (020-019), and later, exhausted, confesses: "'We'll have covered five leagues, Jeannette, and another day will see us at the coast'" (020-023). But it is Jeanne's physical limits, not Margaret's, that define the ordeal: "'Lean on me, Jeannette. I should not have brought thee on this wild venture'" (020-028). Margaret, the tigress who thought herself invincible, learns that her softer companion is both her responsibility and her conscience.

### Through Raoul's Lands

The journey through [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]]'s domain brings the women to their lowest point. Captured and brought before Raoul, Jeanne watches in horror as the brute attacks Margaret. "Jeanne sprang to her aid, eluding her guard" (024-003) — an act of physical courage that defies her delicate appearance and proves that gentleness is not weakness.

Simon's rescue transforms the ordeal into deliverance. Geoffrey carries Jeanne "in his arms through the palace corridors" (024-019), and later, on horseback, he wraps her in his cloak and "kissed her repeatedly" (024-051). The terror of the flight accelerates their courtship: by the time they reach safety, their union is an accomplished fact. Geoffrey proposes with characteristic bluntness — "'I will wed thee'" — and Jeanne replies with a final, affectionate mockery: "'Thou wilt bear me, then, to the altar by force, sir'" (024-062), spoken in "keenest admiration" (024-064).

> "Oh, Jeannette, Jeannette, thou art too brave for me!" Margaret cried during the flight, and in that cry the hierarchy of mistress and servant dissolved into the bond of two women holding each other upright through a nightmare. (021-093)

## Loyalty and Resistance

### The Terrace Confrontation

Jeanne's loyalty is tested directly when [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]], finding her alone on the terrace, attempts to recruit her as an ally in winning Margaret's submission. His approach is characteristically direct: "'I but ask thy persuasion, lady'" (018-130). Her refusal — "'You ask in vain, sir'" (018-131) — reveals the steel beneath Jeanne's gentle exterior.

When Simon hints darkly that the Countess could face execution, Jeanne does not flinch. Instead she appeals to his English sense of justice:

> "Yet would ye not slay the Countess, for ye are English, and I have heard that their justice is great." (018-137)

This exchange establishes Jeanne not as a mere romantic interest but as a character of moral weight — someone who will not be intimidated by "the icy Lord of Beauvallet" (018-069), and whose appeal to justice constitutes both a test of Simon's character and a declaration of her own.

### The Watcher's Instinct

Jeanne's moral clarity extends to her perception of other characters. When [[the-chevalier|the Chevalier]] hovers at the edges of the court, it is "Jeanne who looked coldly at him" (025-102). She "rose to admit the Chevalier" when he comes to Margaret's chambers (017-056), performing the mechanical duty while her face registers her distrust. Unlike others who dismiss the Chevalier as merely tiresome, Jeanne perceives the malice beneath the affectation — her instincts about his character are sound, and her wariness proves prophetic.

## Witness to Resolution

In the novel's closing chapters, Jeanne serves as both witness and facilitator to the major resolutions. She is present in Margaret's chamber when Simon delivers his farewell, and after his departure, it is Jeanne who enters softly, "passing an arm about her waist" (028-047) to comfort her weeping mistress.

> "Jeanne, stay with me," Margaret had said in their first scene together, and here, at the end, Jeanne is still there — the constant through every crisis, the witness to every transformation. (028-047)

It is Jeanne whom Margaret sends to summon [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]], initiating the chain of events that leads to the Chevalier's banishment and the final reconciliation of the English and French households. The novel's last glimpse of her finds Jeanne in Geoffrey's arms, being kissed "meekly" before delivering her mistress's message — a small domestic comedy that confirms her passage from French lady-in-waiting to English bride, and that closes the narrative on a note of hard-won warmth.

## The Bridge Between Two Worlds

Jeanne's function in the novel extends beyond her individual characterization. Her romance with [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] provides the symbolic bridge between the French and English worlds, conducted in the common language of love that transcends political divisions. When Jeanne admits that she "hated" Geoffrey when she first saw him from the tower window, and then immediately qualifies it — "one does not hate a helpless man" (018-071) — she articulates the novel's argument that personal feeling, honestly acknowledged, dissolves the artificial barriers of nationality and enmity.

Her final allegiance spans both worlds: to Geoffrey as her betrothed, to Margaret as her mistress, with the two loyalties reconciled when Margaret herself makes peace with the English. Jeanne's story traces the arc from war to peace, from hatred to love, from division to union — the same arc that governs the novel as a whole.

> "Not 'poor,' Jeanne, for she hath all a man's heart could desire," Geoffrey said, meaning Margaret but looking at Jeanne, and in that double meaning lay the promise that the great romance of the central pair would find its echo in the quiet, tender love of the Lady's waiting-woman and the King's Soldier. (018-029)
