## Historical Context

The Battle of Shrewsbury was fought on 21 July 1403 between the forces of King Henry IV and a rebel army led by Sir Henry Percy, known to history as [[hotspur|Hotspur]]. The Percy family, who had been instrumental in placing Henry Bolingbroke on the throne four years earlier, had grown disillusioned with his rule. Hotspur raised his standard in the North alongside his father, the Earl of Northumberland, and his uncle, [[thomas-percy-earl-of-worcester|Thomas Percy, Earl of Worcester]], and marched south with the intention of joining forces with the Welsh rebel [[owen-glyndourdy|Owen Glyndourdy]] (003-003).

> "Percy, the redoubtable Hotspur, had, with his father, the Earl of Northumberland, and his uncle, the Earl of Worcester, raised his standard in the North against the King, and was on the point of marching to join Glyndourdy in Wales." (003-003)

The King, recognising the mortal danger of allowing the Percy forces to link with the Welsh uprising, raced to intercept Hotspur before the junction could occur. The town of [[shrewsbury|Shrewsbury]], situated on the River Severn and commanding the approach from Cheshire into Wales, became the flashpoint where the two armies converged.

### The Strategic Situation

The strategic calculus was stark. Hotspur's army was reported to be approximately fourteen hundred strong, and if Glyndourdy joined him the royalist position would become untenable (003-105). The King's urgency was therefore paramount: he must bring Hotspur to battle before the Welsh could reinforce the rebel standard. The Prince of Wales, the young Henry of Monmouth, was already at Shrewsbury with an insufficient force and dwindling supplies when the crisis broke (003-004).

## The Novel's Depiction

### The March to Shrewsbury

In the novel, the Shrewsbury campaign serves as [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]]'s introduction to war. When a King's Messenger arrives at [[montlice|Montlice]] bearing a royal writ summoning [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]] to join the King at Shrewsbury, Simon, then seventeen, reacts with characteristic composure (003-019). Fulk, by contrast, is in a ferment of eagerness, but Simon counsels calm:

> "'There will be more done, and that expeditiously, if a head is kept firm upon one's shoulders.'" (003-022)

Fulk assembles a force of six score men-at-arms and eight score archers under Francis of Dalley, with John the Marshal and Vincent as his captains (003-034). Alan, Fulk's son, is denied permission to join the campaign -- being deemed too young and temperamentally unsuited -- and Simon is chosen to accompany Fulk in his stead (003-029). After three days of preparation, the Montlice contingent departs on the arduous march to Shrewsbury.

### Arrival at Shrewsbury

The Montlice force reaches Shrewsbury at the end of the week, one day ahead of the King (003-081). Fulk's irritability, which had plagued him during the period of uncertainty at home, evaporates the moment he is in action. The men are quartered well and received royally. Almost immediately, Fulk is summoned to wait upon the Prince of Wales -- a boy of sixteen who has already led a punitive expedition into North Wales and earned Fulk's genuine admiration (003-098).

While Fulk attends his audience, Simon encounters his half-brother, [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]], for the first time. Geoffrey, who has led his father's men to Shrewsbury in place of the ailing Lord of Malvallet, initially dismisses Simon as "one of the Montlice brood," but their tense first meeting is charged with the uneasy recognition of kinship and the rivalry of ancient family enmity (003-088).

### Simon's Sharp Eyes

On the day following their arrival, Simon goes to the battlements on the east side of Shrewsbury and mounts the parapet to scan the surrounding country. His exceptionally keen eyesight -- a trait remarked upon throughout the novel -- allows him to detect movement on the distant horizon long before anyone else. He observes "a glistening like a tiny star" which he correctly identifies as sunlight on armour (003-136).

Captain Paul of [[lenoir|Lenoir]], who at first scoffs, eventually confirms Simon's observation. Simon further demonstrates his strategic acumen by deducing that the approaching force is the King's army, not Hotspur's, since Hotspur would come from the north where he was known to be marching towards Chester (003-148). The sighting triggers celebration in Shrewsbury, and the King's army reaches the gates shortly after dusk (003-163).

### The Eve of Battle

A council is summoned within the hour of the King's arrival, and Fulk does not return until well into the night (003-164). The next morning brings news that Percy has appeared before the walls but, seeing the royal banner, has withdrawn his forces to a defensive position. The King holds another council, and the decision is made to march forth and engage the rebels on Hayteley-hill, where Hotspur has drawn up his army (003-170).

The royal army, numbering fourteen to fifteen hundred men, is divided into three divisions: Stafford leads the van, the King commands the right wing, and the Prince of Wales takes the left (003-167). Montlice and Malvallet are both assigned to the Prince's command. Before battle is joined, a herald is sent to treat with Percy, and Thomas of Worcester rides out under a flag of truce, but the negotiations fail (003-173).

### The Battle

What follows is one of the most vividly rendered battle sequences in the novel. The terrain is difficult: ponds run along the foot of the hill, and thick rows of peas grow in front of the rebel position, hampering movement (003-172). Stafford's van charges through these obstructions and engages Hotspur's forces descending the hill. The archers on both wings loose volleys that darken the air.

> "Suddenly the air seemed thick with flying arrows, and alive with cries and the clash of arms. Among the ponds and beyond them the vans of the two armies engaged, and for a while nothing could be seen save a medley of soldiers fighting together in growing disorder." (003-182)

Stafford falls early, and the royal lines waver. Then the Prince is struck in the face by an arrow. The moment is critical: the young Henry plucks the arrow from his cheek, staunches the blood, and rises in his stirrups to rally his wavering troops.

> "'Onward, onward!' he shouted. 'Follow me!' He set spur to his horse and charged forward. 'St. George, St. George for us!' he cried." (003-186)

This act of personal heroism turns the tide. The men surge forward, shouting "The Prince, the Prince!" and follow him up the hill, rolling back the enemy's right flank between themselves and the King's division (003-189).

### Simon's Valour

In the chaos of the melee, Simon distinguishes himself with extraordinary feats of arms. He fights his way to the aid of Geoffrey of Malvallet, whose horse has been killed and who stands hard-pressed by Percy's men. Simon charges into the group at a full gallop and strikes with such force that his "huge sword with all his iron strength behind it descended on one hapless shoulder where it joined his victim's neck, and cleaved through the sheltering armour as though it had been so much cardboard" (003-190).

Dismounting, Simon gives his own horse to Geoffrey and continues fighting on foot. In a remarkable display of versatility, he calmly kneels and uses his treasured bow -- which Fulk had remonstrated with him for bringing -- to pick off the rebels surrounding Malvallet:

> "Calmly, too, he took aim, and bent that mighty weapon. The arrow sang forth, but so sure was Simon of his skill, equal, Fulk said, to that of the best bowman in all Cheshire, that he paused not to see it hit its mark." (003-193)

The tide of the battle shifts decisively when news spreads that Hotspur has fallen. The cry goes up: "Hotspur has fallen! Hotspur is dead! Hurrah for St. George of England!" (003-203). The rebel army loses its zest for the fight, and by dusk the battle is over.

### Aftermath and Knighthood

When the fighting ceases, Simon sits on his jaded horse surveying the field with an expression of "detached interest" (003-205). To Geoffrey's question about what he thinks of it all, Simon reflects that the battlefield is "disorderly" and that he would help "tidy it" -- a response that prompts Geoffrey to call him a "cold-blooded tiger-cub" (003-215). Simon's reply encapsulates his philosophy:

> "'One must die,' Simon answered. 'And I would deem this a good death. Why should I pity them?'" (003-216)

Simon's valour does not go unnoticed. The Prince of Wales, riding by with a bandage across his wounded face, personally acknowledges Simon's bravery and promises not to forget him (003-258). This promise is kept: at the Prince's recommendation, Simon is among the dozen knights created by the King on the field.

On the day of the knighting ceremony, Simon kneels as the thirteenth candidate, a number that Fulk finds inauspicious but that Simon claims as lucky (004-039). At the King's words "Rise, Sir Simon of Beauvallet," Simon comes to his feet, and Geoffrey, standing among the Prince's retinue, smiles and salutes him (004-041). The young Prince Henry comments on Simon's lynx-eyes, citing Paul of Lenoir's report, and Simon modestly demurs (004-042).

### The Departure

Two days after the knighting, the Montlice contingent leaves Shrewsbury with the King's army, journeying south before branching off towards Cambridge (004-058). Fulk's losses have been few, and Simon steps into the role of the fallen captain Vincent, proving himself thoroughly equal to the task. They return to Montlice early in August, after an absence of nearly a month, where Simon is greeted not as a squire but as a knight -- Sir Simon of Beauvallet.

## Military Significance

### Tactical Analysis

The battle represents a classic case of royalist forces exploiting interior lines and superior speed. The King's rapid march to Shrewsbury preempted the junction of Hotspur with Glyndourdy, transforming what would have been a desperate defensive action into a winnable pitched battle. The choice of ground by Hotspur -- a hill protected by ponds and thick vegetation -- was tactically sound but was overcome by the personal leadership of the young Prince of Wales.

The novel's depiction emphasises the critical importance of individual leadership in medieval warfare. The Prince's dramatic rally after being wounded in the face is treated as the turning point of the engagement. Similarly, Simon's intervention to save Geoffrey demonstrates how single combat actions could have outsized effects on local engagements within the larger melee.

### Casualties and Consequences

The principal casualty was Hotspur himself, whose death effectively ended the Percy rebellion. Thomas of Worcester, the Earl, was captured along with the Scottish Earl Archibald Douglas, who had thrown in his lot with the rebels (004-020). The Percy threat to the Lancastrian crown was decisively broken, though Glyndourdy remained at large in Wales and would continue to trouble the realm for years to come.

## Character Development

### Simon's Baptism of Fire

The Battle of Shrewsbury is the crucible in which Simon of Beauvallet's character is forged. He enters Shrewsbury as an unknown squire and leaves it as Sir Simon of Beauvallet, a knight dubbed by the King's own hand. The battle reveals every quality that will define him: his preternatural calm under fire, his physical prowess, his tactical intelligence (displayed in his reconnaissance from the battlements), and his disconcerting emotional detachment from the horrors around him.

His coldness is not cruelty but a kind of existential pragmatism. When Geoffrey asks if he has no compassion for the wounded, Simon answers that he would help them so they might "fight again" but would not pity them, "for that is foolish" (003-218). This philosophy -- practical engagement with suffering without sentimental wallowing -- will characterise Simon's approach to governance as well as war.

### The Bond with Geoffrey

The battle also establishes the fraternal bond between Simon and Geoffrey of Malvallet. Though they meet as strangers bound to rival houses, their mutual rescue on the field creates a connection that transcends feudal enmity. Geoffrey acknowledges Simon as "brother" and offers his hand (003-232). Simon accepts the friendship but refuses to let it alter his present allegiance to Fulk of Montlice. The tension between blood kinship and sworn loyalty, first crystallised at Shrewsbury, will run through the remainder of the novel.

### The Prince's Notice

The Prince of Wales's recognition of Simon is another seed planted at Shrewsbury that will bear fruit throughout the narrative. Henry's observation that "there is that in him that pleases" and his wish to "call him friend" (003-277) presage the deep bond that will develop between the cold-hearted knight and the future [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]].
