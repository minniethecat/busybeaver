## Identity and Portrayal

Fulk of Montlice is first introduced seated at a table in his own hall, "leaning back at his ease, a giant of a man, deep-chested and long-limbed, with a shock of grizzled red hair and a beard to match" (001-043). He is the Earl of Montlice, lord of [[montlice|Montlice Castle]] and its surrounding domain, a veteran of many campaigns whose great frame, roaring voice, and red-eyed rages conceal a shrewd judge of character beneath. The sobriquet "Fulk the Lion" — used even by the boy [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] — captures his combination of ferocity and kingly presence (001-037).

The Montlice household colours are red and gold, and by the second PN section Simon himself is "decked out in Montlice red and gold. Very brave he looked" (002-003). These colours mark the identity Fulk extends to those under his protection. Fulk's manner is rough and direct; he snaps his fingers to jeer (001-070), brings his fist crashing down on the table to make a point (003-031), and roars for his squire before he has even set foot on the ground of a campaign (003-009). Yet this bluster is not brutality — it is the style of a man who has commanded men in war and sees no need for the refinements of court.

## Relationship with Simon

### The Arrival at Montlice

Simon's first encounter with Fulk sets the pattern for their entire relationship. When the fourteen-year-old boy arrives at [[montlice|Montlice]] and asks to see "the lion," Alan brings him before his father. Montlice snaps his fingers jeeringly and demands to know what this strange, cold-eyed youth wants at his table (001-070). Simon's answer — that he has come to serve — draws a great laugh from the earl. My Lord of Montlice laughed at "such temerity" (001-049); Montlice "flung back his head and laughed" (001-052). Fulk is simultaneously amused and intrigued by a boy who shows neither fear nor deference.

The examination of Simon's golden ring reveals Fulk's deeper perceptiveness. "Montlice looked at it long and curiously" (001-102), and the revelation of Simon's birth drives him to an unusual stillness: "Montlice sank back again" (001-098). In this moment the roaring earl becomes a judge, weighing what he has learned about the boy. When he speaks again it is with recognition: "'Here's a philosophy!' Montlice became aware of his son, still standing" (001-105). He offers Simon a place with a typical warning — "'I am no easy master,' Montlice warned him" (001-127) — and seals it with a blow of approval: "Montlice smote him on the shoulder" (001-135). Thus it was that "Simon came to Fulk of Montlice" (001-137).

### The Taming of the Wildcat

The early months of Simon's service are marked by physical conflict that paradoxically deepens their bond. Fulk beats Simon when provoked, and Simon endures it with his characteristic stillness. "From Fulk he bore blows in an acquiescent silence" (002-005). At one point Fulk roars, "What I have I hold!" and points at the boy (002-064) as if asserting ownership over this strange creature who refuses to break. When pressed beyond endurance, "Fulk's eyes showed red a moment" (002-066) and the struggle escalates.

The pivotal confrontation comes when Fulk cries out, "I will tame you, wildcat!" and draws back his fist to strike (002-069). Simon does not flinch. "'Dare ye defy me?' roared Fulk, and closed his huge hand on him" (002-074). But the boy "not so much as wince, but held Fulk's look steadily" (002-077). It is in this moment that something shifts. "Slowly the giant's grip relaxed" and Fulk acknowledges, "Ay, ye dare" (002-078). The admission leads not to more violence but to transformation: "At that Fulk broke into a great laugh" (002-080) and the beating turns to approval. "Whereat Fulk laughed the more and liked him the better" (002-084).

> "What I have I hold!" Fulk roared at him once, pointing a great finger at him, his voice thick with wine. "Serve me well and I will cherish thee; betray me once and I will break thee in two!"

Simon's silence in the face of Fulk's fury is his greatest weapon. In these scenes Fulk reveals himself as a man who respects resistance — who recognises in the boy's iron composure a quality worth more than servile obedience.

### Mentor and Champion

Having recognised Simon's quality, Fulk extends his full protection. "True it was that Fulk cared for him more than for his own son" (002-063), and Alan himself acknowledges the particular fondness. "Fulk knew him for a soldier born" (002-086), and watching Simon's iron equanimity, Fulk "smote his thigh" with satisfaction (002-099).

The relationship deepens when "state affairs first affected Montlice" with the summons to campaign (003-004). A messenger arrives at Montlice, "covered with dust, dropping with fatigue" (003-005), and Fulk's first instinct is to roar for his squire: "the Messenger departed and Fulk roaring for his squire" (003-009). Fulk hands Simon the King's writ and Simon reads it in silence before returning it with a frown: "We can make ready to start in three days" (003-017). "Fulk laughed, stuffing the parchment away" (003-018) — delighted that his squire's first response is not excitement or fear but calculation.

When Alan protests the dangers of war, Fulk is scathing. "'What a captain thou wouldst make!' jeered Fulk. 'Paling at every sound, wearing thy heart upon thy sleeve'" (003-026). He turns instead to Simon, whose cold competence he trusts implicitly: "'It is Simon I will have beside me'" (003-029). "Fulk brought his fist down on the table" (003-031) to silence his son's protests, and "'To Warwick I will go!' declared Fulk obstinately" (003-037).

> "It is Simon I will have beside me," Fulk replied, cutting through Alan's protest with the flat certainty of a man who has weighed both his son and his squire and found one wanting.

After the battle, Fulk emerges "in rare good spirits" (003-097), shooting Simon "a sidelong glance" of approval (003-104). And when Simon proves himself on the field, Fulk delivers the ultimate endorsement: "Shalt have thy knighthood, lad, an thou'lt stay yet a while" (004-029). When Simon is finally knighted, Fulk's exclamation carries the pride of a mentor vindicated: "Now art thou honoured indeed, lad!" (004-076).

## Warlord and Campaigner

Fulk of Montlice is a soldier through and through. "Although for some time past Fulk, ever-ready for war, had chafed" at inaction, state affairs finally gave him a campaign (003-004). He rides to war with his household following: "Three days later Fulk left Montlice with his following" (003-081), suffering some losses on the march but arriving with his core strength intact (003-082).

In the field, Fulk fights as he lives — ferociously and directly. During battle his men are recognisable to allies and enemies alike: "'Ha, one of the Montlice brood!' he said, and would have struck" (003-088).

> "I serve Montlice, Highness," Simon answered, and the Prince nodded slowly, understanding that here was a loyalty not lightly given nor lightly withdrawn. (003-254)

When Malvallet and Montlice both take the field, "Montlice and Malvallet galloped forward" together (003-187), united in their charge. But the campaign extracts its price — Fulk is gravely wounded. By the time Simon finds him after the fighting, he is "stretched upon a bed, and swathed in bandages" at his lodging (004-003). Even in his wounded state he manages "a nod" (004-007) and retains enough spirit to shoot "an upward glance" at Simon (004-024).

> "Montlice? I saw him fall a while since," the Prince said, and Simon's heart turned cold within him. (003-255)

## Household and Domain

The Montlice household revolves around Fulk's direct, personal rule. The great hall at [[montlice|Montlice]] is where he holds court, and his authority is absolute within its walls. The "leathern curtain" that hung at the doorway when Simon first arrived remained in place through Simon's years of service (003-007), a small detail marking the unchanging character of Fulk's domain.

His relationship with his son [[alan-of-montlice|Alan]] is complex. Fulk is openly critical of Alan's softer nature, jeering at his fears and dismissing his pretensions to command. Yet Alan is his heir, and Fulk's gruffness conceals a father's concern. Alan remarks on "my Lord of Montlice's" fondness for Simon with a mixture of affection and hurt jealousy (002-035).

His bond with [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] is that of equals — old friends and comrades who fight side by side and understand each other without words. "Couldst thou leave Montlice today without one pang of regret?" Malvallet asks (002-041), probing the limits of his friend's attachment to place and person alike. Simon answers for Fulk by implication: a lord who would never abandon what he holds.

## Personality and Temperament

Fulk's rages are legendary — "Fulk's eyes showed red" (002-066), he roars and brings fists down on tables — but they are matched by equally formidable laughter. No one "laughed out as did my Lord of Montlice" (002-085). From his first great laugh at Simon's temerity (001-052) to his delight at the boy's defiance (002-080), Fulk's humour tempers his violence. His contradictions make him fully human: the brute who cherishes, the roaring lord who extends protection, the impatient warrior who can wait for his squire to mature.

> "Wise boy!" Fulk shook with laughter. "Eh, but thou hast a way with thee that pleaseth me mightily!" (003-023)

When the campaign is over and Simon must think of his future, Fulk's acknowledgment of what he has gained and lost in the boy is tinged with gruff affection. He cannot hold Simon forever — a squire must become a knight, a servant must become a lord in his own right — but the bond forged in blows and laughter will outlast the service itself.

## Significance in the Narrative

Fulk of Montlice serves as the novel's model of feudal lordship at its best — personal, demanding, protective, and ultimately generous. He is not a refined courtier or a calculating politician but a man who rules through bonds of loyalty cemented by shared danger and mutual respect. His recognition of Simon's quality, despite the boy's coldness, lack of birth, and refusal to flatter, marks Fulk as a superior judge of character. In taking in the strange boy who sought "the lion" at Montlice gates, Fulk sets in motion the entire arc of Simon's rise — from squire to soldier, from soldier to knight, and eventually to lord in his own right. Without Fulk of Montlice there is no Simon of Beauvallet.
