## Character Overview

The Lady Margaret, Countess of [[belremy|Belrémy]], is the most formidable figure in the novel apart from [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] himself — and the only one who consistently meets him as an equal. Where others shrink from the Coldheart's stare, she returns it with one of her own. The narrative introduces her as an unseen threat long before she appears in person: when the King's men at Westminster debate assignments, [[edmund-of-fenton|Edmund of Fenton]] warns [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] to "pit thy strength against the Lady Margaret of Belrémy" (012-047), framing the encounter as a test of more than military skill. [[gilbert|Gilbert of Umfraville]] adds that a woman rules Belrémy — and he "would sooner fight a man than a woman" (012-110), signaling how Margaret's gender unsettles the masculine logic of conquest.

When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] first sees her, seated in a "throne-like chair" within her own hall, she is described as "like a pillar of ice" (015-073). The image is deliberate: she mirrors Simon's own coldness, suggesting that the two are cut from the same stone. Her rule of Belrémy is one of order and competence — she has defeated Umfraville in the field, governs her lands justly, and when summoned to surrender, returns "a hot answer, that he should enter Belrémy over her dead body" (013-010).

> "Madame Margaret, Countess of Belrémy, will treat with the Lord of Beauvallet for the safety of her town and her dependants, but for her own person she will make no terms." (015-032)

## Personality and Temperament

Margaret defies easy categorization. To her enemies she is "Margaret the Amazon" (012-085), "the vixen Margaret" (014-051), and "the Devil" (013-073). To her own people — her ladies, her page Léon, and later the destitute giant [[ranaud|Ranaud]] — she is generous, loyal, and capable of great tenderness. This duality is central to her characterization: she is fierce because she must be, having ruled alone since her father's death, and gentle because she chooses to be with those she loves.

Her pride is immense. When [[the-chevalier|Victor]] offers her a jewelled dagger and suggests she stab [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] in the back, she recoils with disgust: "Stab him in the back? Pah!" (017-066). But pride is also her vulnerability. Cornered in [[turincel|Turincel]] after discovering that [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand]] has submitted to [[king-henry-v|King Henry]], she refuses to return defeated. "Shall I be unfaithful to the name I bear? What says my motto? — 'Conquest or death' — well, I will conquer" (021-081).

> "He scorns me because I am a woman. I will show him what a woman can do!" (017-144)

Her temper is volcanic. When taunted by [[alan-of-montlice|Alan]] about her men having scarred his forehead, she strikes [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] across the mouth (017-130). When cornered by [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]], she drives her fist into his grinning face (022-098). Yet she is also capable of rapid self-reproach. After snapping at [[jeanne]] in a fit of distress, she "ran to her, and caught her in her arms" (017-023), begging forgiveness.

## Military Command and the Defense of Belrémy

Before [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] arrives, Margaret has already proven herself a capable military leader. She defeated [[gilbert-of-umfraville|Umfraville]] in the field, led her men in person, and built Belrémy into an independent stronghold. When Simon lays siege, she refuses surrender and his army labors against walls that "seem made of granite" (013-015). The siege proceeds until Simon uses a ruse — mining the walls, then personally leading a storming party through the breach early on Christmas morning.

> "Ye have defeated Umfraville, and would have defeated you, had you not taken Belrémy by a trick!" (017-110)

After the fall of [[belremy|Belrémy]], Simon keeps her as a hostage in her own castle. The dynamic between them during her captivity is charged: she is physically confined but spiritually unbroken. When [[the-chevalier|Victor]] attempts to manipulate her into assassination, her refusal is rooted not in cowardice but in a warrior's sense of honor — if she kills Simon, " 'twill not be from behind" (017-075).

## The Assassination Attempt

The climactic scene of the captivity section occurs in Chapter 17, "How the Lady Margaret Could Not Stab Him." Donning a dagger hidden in her dress, Margaret descends in silence to the great hall where Simon sits writing, his back turned. The prose is taut with suspense as she creeps "inch by inch" closer, dagger ready (017-082). Then Simon speaks without lifting his head:

> "Strike where the neck joins the shoulder, my lady," (017-084)

Simon had not heard her approach. He relied, as he explains, on "the sense that warns me of danger" (017-100). He goes further, taking her wrist and guiding the dagger's point to his own neck, daring her: "Push home, my lady" (017-089). She cannot. The dagger falls. It is the turning point — she has been faced with the chance to kill him under conditions of her choosing, and something within her refused. Simon understands what this means even if she does not: "Thou wilt never do it now, lady. The time is past, and thy courage forsook thee" (017-093).

## Escape, Journey, and Capture

Margaret's escape is a feat of audacity and cross-dressing resourcefulness. She obtains young Léon's pass, forges "and sister" onto it in Simon's own secretary's hand, and disguises herself as a pageboy in brown tunic and hose (019-046). Her delight in the transformation is infectious:

> "Am I not brave? Sister, I salute thee! These clothes make me smaller, but 'tis no matter. Jeanne, Jeanne, look not so horrified!" (019-090)

She cuts a fine figure, and when her own lady-in-waiting [[jeanne|Hélène de Courvonne]] fails to recognize her, the disguise is proven (019-116). She and [[jeanne]] depart before dawn, and Simon — who has left at noon for Sal-de-lac — does not discover the escape until his return two days later (023-003).

The journey is a trial of endurance. They tramp through [[balderin]] and [[razincourt]], eat coarse fare in rough taverns, and sleep in a stable loft at [[tourdelonne]] (020-128). At the Tourdelonne inn, they acquire a protector: a red-bearded giant named [[ranaud|Gaston Ranaud]], who recognizes Margaret despite her disguise and insists on accompanying them (021-025). He proves invaluable when they reach [[turincel|Turincel]] only to learn that [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand]] has submitted to the English King. Margaret's rage and despair are raw: "Fernand turned traitor... What shall I do? What can I do?" (021-075)

Undeterred, she resolves to march to [[vazincourt|Vazincourt]] to seek aid from Arnaud de Larousie — even at the price of marriage. This means crossing the lands of [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul the Terrible]], a debauched lord in the mold of Gilles de Rais. The choice proves catastrophic: Raoul's hunting party captures them. He recognizes Ranaud from an old score, flings them into a dungeon, and the next day brings them before him in a chamber carpeted with animal skins. He discovers Margaret's sex, recognizes her value as a political pawn — and desires her for himself.

> "Belrémy, eh? Now I have an old, old score to settle 'gainst thy land. Methinks I have found a way. Would it hurt the proud burghers of thy land to see thee my chattel, I wonder? Or I might wed thee..." (022-095)

When he kisses her, she strikes his face. He responds with another kiss, gloating that she is "a silly fly in his web" (022-102). The scene is genuinely menacing, and it is at this moment that Simon arrives.

## Rescue and the Death of Raoul

Simon, having tracked the escape party across [[turincel|Turincel]] and into Raoul's domain, arrives at the hunting lodge with [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]], posing as the King's envoy. The steward announces him, the door opens — and Simon sees Margaret in Raoul's arms, fighting.

> With an oath Raoul let Margaret go, pushing her from him so that she fell on to the ground... But Simon had seen, and the sight of Margaret's slim figure, fighting madly with this deformed, evil creature, awoke some hitherto dormant emotion within him. Rage surged up, and suddenly everything grew red. (024-006)

What follows is the novel's most visceral moment. Simon abandons all caution, seizes Raoul, bends him backwards over his knee, and stabs him with Margaret's own dagger — the same weapon she could not use against Simon himself. "Die, thou dog! Die!" he roars (024-008). The death of [[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]] is more than a rescue; it is the first time Simon of Beauvallet loses control, and it is Margaret who provokes that loss.

The desperate escape that follows — fighting through the palace, bolting doors, finding the kitchen stairs, racing to horses — is the novel's most sustained action sequence. Margaret fights alongside the men, wielding Raoul's dress-sword, and when they gallop free, she lies "limp in his arms, half-conscious, knowing herself safe at last" (024-044).

### Simon's Loss of Control

The slaying of Raoul marks a decisive fracture in Simon's characterization. Throughout the novel, he is defined by emotional restraint and strategic calculation. Yet here, "for the first time in his life he forgot caution" (024-007). The trigger is unambiguous: seeing Margaret — his prisoner, his adversary — in another man's grasp. [[jeanne|Jeanne]] later explains to [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]] that Simon "meant not to kill Raoul, but when he saw my lady in his arms, he forgot caution, and only thought of vengeance" (025-049). When [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] teases Simon afterward — "this is the first time — and the last, please God — that I have turned my back on the enemy" — Simon does not deny it (024-041). The Coldheart has discovered that he is not so cold after all, and the revelation changes the calculus of conquest into something far more personal.

## Submission and the Genesis of Love

Back at [[belremy|Belrémy]], Simon carries her to her chamber himself, brushing past her protesting cousin. That night he tells his astonished foster-father [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]]: "That, my lord, is the lady whom I will one day take to wife" (024-127).

The submission scene in Chapter 25 is a negotiation between equals. Margaret, dressed in a red robe and horned headdress, begins formally: "I have first — to thank you — for — what you did — yesterday" (025-065). She offers her formal submission to the King. Simon demands more:

> "Thy life, ay. All of thee." Suddenly he bent forward, and kissed her, full on her red lips. (025-070)

When she protests, he announces his intention: "I want thy hand in marriage" (025-073). Her fury returns — "Think ye I would wed — an English boor?" (025-075) — but Simon is unmoved. After she signs the parchment of submission, he delivers his ultimatum: "Willingly shalt thou come to me, and willingly give thy heart. For I will have all or nothing" (025-083).

Jeanne's quiet observation to Margaret — "Pride dies hard... Thou art torn between love and hate" (025-094) — names the central dynamic. Margaret's emotional trajectory from sworn enemy to wife is not a softening but a recognition. The woman who could not stab Simon in the back has already, in her heart, surrendered.

## Character Significance

Margaret is not merely a love interest in the conventional sense. She is the only character whose will matches Simon's, and her arc transforms the novel from a simple conquest narrative into something more complex. The Coldheart conquers Belrémy by force, but he cannot conquer Margaret by force — he must wait for her to choose. That she does choose, on terms she can respect, is what makes her a fitting partner: "He called me the Amazon. Full well will I merit that title" (021-083).

In [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]]'s rueful words, she is "a tigress in her fierceness" (017-135). But in [[jeanne]]'s correction: "She is the sweetest, dearest lady! She shows herself tigerish to you, because you seek to conquer her!" (018-084). Both judgments are true. The novel's resolution — Margaret's submission not as defeat but as free choice — validates her complexity. She is not conquered; she consents.
