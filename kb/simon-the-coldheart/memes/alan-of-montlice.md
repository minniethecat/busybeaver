## Identity and First Impression

Alan of Montlice is the son and heir of [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk, Earl of Montlice]], and the first character to greet the fourteen-year-old [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] when he arrives at the castle gates. His introduction is direct and unguarded: "'I am Alan of Montlice,' he said. 'What want you of my father?'" (001-029). Already a young man on the verge of knighthood, Alan is everything his father is not — slender where Fulk is massive, hesitant where Fulk is decisive, and gentle where Fulk is ferocious.

His first response to Simon is telling: "Alan of Montlice hesitated" (001-032), weighing whether to admit the strange, cold-eyed boy. When he decides to let him pass, "standing aside for the young Montlice to pass through the doorway" (001-034), he "shot him an awkward glance" — a mix of curiosity and wariness that would soon give way to the deepest friendship of his life.

> "I am Alan of Montlice," he said. "What want you of my father?" Then his gaze fell upon the youth's still, cold face, and he fell back a pace, wondering. (001-029)

## The Harp and the Arrow

From their earliest days together, Alan and Simon present a study in contrasts that Heyer makes vivid through a recurring tableau. The novel's most tranquil scene places them "high up in one of the turrets, Alan playing his harp, and Simon fashioning arrows" (002-009). This image — the poet and the warrior, music and war, creation and destruction side by side — defines their friendship from the beginning.

Alan's musicality is no mere pastime. It is the outward sign of a temperament fundamentally unsuited to the world of iron in which he has been born. "Alan played a soft chord or two" (002-012), then "put his harp away and crossed to the window" (002-015) where his silent friend stood. He "watched him for a moment" (002-016), then asked softly, "'What dost thou like the best, Simon?'" (002-019). The question is pure Alan — reaching for connection, probing the depths of a friend who offers no easy answers.

> Alan played a soft chord or two, then fell silent, watching his friend's still profile against the fading light. In that high tower room, with the harp's last note still trembling in the air, there was no need for speech between them. (002-012)

Simon answers, and "Alan sighed" (002-024). Then "Alan laughed at that" (002-027) — the quick shift between melancholy and laughter characteristic of his emotional openness. Later, "Again Alan sighed" (002-032), and eventually the conversation turns to the future: "'Couldst thou leave Montlice today without one pang of regret?' demanded Alan" (002-041). He "sat down again, twanging his harp" (002-043), unable to be still, needing to occupy his hands as his mind wrestled with the knowledge that his friend might one day leave.

## The Reversal of Positions

The narrative is explicit about the power dynamic between the two friends:

> "Between Alan and Simon positions were very soon reversed. Alan was the elder, but from the first Simon dominated him." (002-007)

This is not a relationship Alan resents. He submits to Simon's stronger will not from weakness but from recognition. When Simon rebukes him — "'Art like a whining babe, Alan'" (002-035) — Alan does not take offence. He "stretched out his hands" (002-036) in a gesture of surrender and acceptance. Simon "looked thoughtfully at Alan, so that the boy sprang up" (002-038), and the tension dissolves into the rhythm of their peculiar intimacy.

Alan is the one who voices the questions Simon will not ask. "'What will be thy life?' asked Alan" (002-058), looking into a future he cannot yet see but already fears will separate them. He also perceives what others miss: "'I know that he watches thee always,'" (002-054) Alan tells Simon of his father's quiet observation. His gentleness is paired with sharp perception.

## Father and Son

### The Lion's Disappointment

The relationship between Alan and [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]] is marked by tension and disappointment on both sides. The narrative states it plainly: "The lion-spirit was not in Alan, and between him and his father there was little sympathy" (002-063). Fulk, the roaring warrior, cannot understand a son who plays the harp and dreams of songs rather than campaigns.

This dynamic plays out vividly when the summons to war arrives at [[montlice|Montlice]]. While Fulk roars for his squire (003-009), "Alan sat in a great chair by the embrasure" (003-009) — passive, watching, already sensing the danger ahead. When Fulk mocks his anxiety — "'See our Alan there. The lad's in a ferment'" (003-023) — "Alan flushed at the taunt" (003-024). The mockery escalates as Fulk jeers at Alan's military pretensions: "'What a captain thou wouldst make! Paling at every sound, wearing thy heart upon thy sleeve'" (003-026).

> "Up sprang Alan in a rage" at his father's taunts, his gentle nature for once incandescent with fury — but it was Simon's quiet intercession that cooled the quarrel, not any yielding on Fulk's part. (003-027)

The cruellest blow comes when Fulk declares "'It is Simon I will take'" (003-029). "Alan went sulkily to his chair" (003-033), defeated. But his father is not entirely blind to his son's worth. When the campaign strategy is discussed, Alan makes a valid point — "'Lichfield, sir,' remarked Alan languidly" (003-040) — and over time, hard experience transforms the "silly boy" into a proven knight.

## My Poet: The King's Trio

King Henry V himself gives voice to the three companions' distinct natures. The King christens [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] "my Soldier," Simon "my Knight," and Alan "my Poet" (012-021). The epithets stick. The description that follows codifies their identities:

> "And as such they were known, close friends all three, and each one unlike the other. Geoffrey was a fire-eater, all dash and ardour, romance and an impetuous spirit; Alan, the Poet, was a dreamer, unfit for wars, yet unafraid." (012-023)

The phrase "unfit for wars, yet unafraid" is the novel's definitive judgment on Alan. He does not belong on the battlefield by temperament — he is too sensitive, too gentle, too given to dreams — but he never falters when placed there. The King's affection for him is genuine. "'Dost thou go with Simon, my Poet?' Henry asked. 'Who then will sing to me of an evening?'" (012-083). And when Alan does depart: "'Is that thy reason for taking my Poet from me?'" (012-091) the King demands of Simon, half in jest and half in genuine regret.

When Henry reappears in the narrative, his first instinct is to ask for both friends together: "'And my Poet! Where is my Knight?'" (029-005). Alan's reply earns a royal laugh: "'The Poet hath spoken,' Henry laughed" (029-030). And later still: "'Come hither, my Poet. These came today from my brother'" (031-004), and the King shares his strategic thinking: "'I have thought deeply on it, my Poet, and at last I have seen how the thing must be done'" (031-012). Alan is not merely a court ornament — he is a trusted confidant to the King.

> "Three have I about me!" King Henry exclaimed, his eye travelling from one to the next with the satisfaction of a commander who knows the worth of every man in his service. "My Soldier, my Knight, and my Poet." (012-021)

## The Capture at Belremy

Alan's most dramatic personal arc comes during the siege of [[belremy|Belremy]]. Captured and held within the castle by the Lady of Belremy, Alan becomes the pivot upon which Simon's entire campaign turns. Simon's ultimatum to the Lady is delivered with the cold fury of a man whose friend's life hangs in the balance:

> "If ye surrender not this castle, and Sir Alan of Montlice, then will ye indeed learn what manner of man I am." (015-099)

The siege is no longer merely a military objective — it is a matter of personal honour, and Simon's coldness, which Alan has always seen through to the warmth beneath, hardens into something terrible. When Simon finally breaches Belremy, Alan is found not in a dungeon but treated with some care: they led Simon's men to "Alan who lay in a fair chamber in the castle" (016-060). The Lady, for all her defiance, has respected her prisoner's rank and lineage.

Alan's recovery from captivity is slow. He is "very much shaken" by the ordeal (024-124), and the experience leaves its mark. Yet he survives, and his survival is a testament not to his martial prowess but to the tenacity of a gentle spirit that refuses to break.

## Growth Into Manhood

### From Silly Boy to Proven Knight

One of the novel's most moving transformations is Alan's growth from the timid youth who flushed at his father's taunts into a knight of proven courage. When [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]] sees his son after a long separation, he marvels:

> "What hath come to mine Alan? He left Montlice a silly boy, since then I have not seen him. And yesterday all day I have heard of naught save thy prowess." (024-117)

The father who despaired of his gentle son now hears the boy's name spoken with respect across the camp. Alan has earned his knighthood not through inherited nobility but through deeds performed alongside [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] on the hard ground of France.

Yet Alan remains fundamentally Alan. He is still the one who "laughed softly" and who can sit on a terrace "fitting a new string to his harp" (025-003) even after the carnage of siege warfare. His growth is not into hardness but into a fuller, more complete version of himself — the poet who can also fight, the dreamer who does not flinch.

> "Alan is very wise in all matters of the heart," Simon said of him once, and in that single sentence the cold-hearted knight paid his warm-hearted friend the truest tribute. (012-019)

When the novel reaches its final pages, Alan is there — "Fulk was standing by Alan, one arm about him" (032-053). The father who once despaired of his son now wraps an arm around him; the boy who was a "silly boy" is now a man his father can embrace with pride. And Simon, looking on, sees the circle of loyalty and affection that began at [[montlice|Montlice]] completed.

## Alan as Counterweight

Throughout the novel, Alan serves as Simon's moral and emotional counterweight. He is the friend Simon must rescue, the companion Simon must protect, the voice that reminds the reader Simon cares about something beyond cold ambition. In a narrative dominated by sieges, campaigns, and the cold calculus of power, Alan represents the human cost and the human warmth that make the coldness bearable. Without Alan, Simon would be merely formidable; with Alan, he becomes comprehensible.

The friendship endures through every trial. When Alan "slipped his arm in Simon's, looking up at him with a smile" (012-042), the gesture speaks of an intimacy earned through years of shared danger. Their bond is the novel's quiet centre — a friendship that needs no declarations because it has been tested in fire.
