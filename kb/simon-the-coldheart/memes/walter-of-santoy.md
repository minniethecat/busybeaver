Walter of Santoy is the Captain of the Guard at [[montlice|Montlice]] under [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], serving as the military backbone of Simon's household forces during the consolidation of the Beauvallet estates and the subsequent [[campaign-in-normandy|Campaign in Normandy]]. Appointed to replace the incompetent [[maurice-of-gountray|Maurice of Gountray]], Walter proves himself a capable and reliable officer whose steady competence earns Simon's trust and increasing responsibility throughout the novel.

## Appointment as Captain

### Dismissal of Gountray

Walter's appointment as captain follows Simon's swift dismissal of his predecessor, [[maurice-of-gountray|Maurice of Gountray]]. Simon, having assumed lordship of [[montlice|Montlice]] after the death of [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]], finds the garrison in a lamentable state. The men-at-arms are ill-disciplined, carousing and idle under Gountray's negligent command. Simon confronts Gountray directly, dismisses him from service with the declaration "I want true men about me, not brawling drunkards" (009-151), and immediately turns his mind to finding a replacement.

It is in this context that Simon asks his companions for their assessment:

> "Nay? How read ye Walter of Santoy?" (009-104)

The question reveals Simon's characteristic method of evaluating men: he gathers opinions quietly, weighs character, and acts decisively. Walter of Santoy is his choice.

### Restoring discipline

When Walter assumes command, he inherits a garrison that has grown soft under Gountray's lax leadership. The men are "murmurous and ill-at-ease, for they had a new captain in Walter of Santoy who was busily employed in disciplining them" (010-004). Walter's approach is methodical and relentless: he imposes rigorous drill, demanding standards, and a clear chain of command.

The men groan under the new regime, but they submit—and soon enough, they improve:

> "Walter of Santoy had orders to drill his men, and although they groaned under it, they submitted, and very soon put some life into their labours, for no one knew when Simon would appear upon the scene, watching closely from under his jutting brows, chary of praise, but giving it where it was due." (010-019)

Walter's effectiveness as a captain proves such that Simon is able to delegate military oversight entirely and redirect his attention to the cultivation of his lands. The narrative notes that "Walter of Santoy proved himself an admirable captain, so that Simon relaxed some of his vigilance, and turned his attention to the cultivation of his land" (010-115). This is high praise indeed from a lord as exacting as [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]].

## The Siege of Belrémy

### Arrival with the rearguard

Walter plays a significant role in the latter phase of the [[siege-of-belremy|Siege of Belrémy]], arriving as commander of the rearguard force that reinforces Simon's position:

> "At last came Walter of Santoy, in green-and-russet, Beauvallet colours, riding at the head of the rearguard, some score and a half men-at-arms mounted. They halted within the town, and spread quickly to guard the bridge at a sharp command from Santoy." (014-131)

The detail of the Beauvallet colours—green-and-russet—marks Walter as fully integrated into Simon's household, bearing his lord's livery with pride. His men deploy efficiently at his command, securing the bridge, a key tactical point.

### Securing the watch-tower

Walter's men are immediately put to use in the most dangerous post: the watch-tower that overlooks the contested bridge. They relieve the exhausted troops who had held the position through the first stages of the assault. The men Walter sends up are described as "wounded every one, but dauntless" (014-134), a testament both to the ferocity of the fighting and to the quality of soldiers Walter commands and leads.

When one of Simon's men, a soldier named Malcolm, is wounded in the fighting, it is Walter's surgeon who attends him (014-136). This detail underscores Walter's preparedness as a commander: he brings not only fighting men but the medical support needed to sustain them.

## The Normandy Campaign

### Guarding Simon's quarters

During the [[campaign-in-normandy|Campaign in Normandy]], Walter's responsibilities expand from garrison commander to field officer entrusted with critical security duties. When Simon secures quarters at [[belremy|Belrémy]], his first concern is the safekeeping of his position and his prisoner. He turns at once to Walter:

> "Go prepare me two rooms above," he said. "Let Walter of Santoy set a guard of mine own men upon them, so that not a mouse may creep out unseen. Hasten." (016-017)

The order is characteristic of the trust Simon places in Walter: the guard must be of Simon's "own men," personally reliable, and Walter is the officer who can ensure it. The instruction that "not a mouse may creep out unseen" conveys the seriousness of the assignment.

### The escort at Turincel

Walter's most significant moment in the campaign comes at [[turincel|Turincel]], where Simon and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] enter the castle as envoys. Before dismounting, Simon gives his orders:

> "Walter, Sir Geoffrey and I enter alone. No danger awaits us, for I go as an envoy." He dismounted and gave his horse into Walter's care. (023-163–164)

This brief exchange encapsulates the nature of Walter's role. He is the man who holds the line while his lord advances into the unknown, the steady hand who waits with the horses and the men, ready to act if the situation turns. Simon's confidence in leaving his horse—and implicitly his line of retreat—in Walter's care speaks to their relationship of absolute trust.

In the final charge at [[belremy|Belrémy]]'s gate, Walter is positioned to the right of the main force: "To the right was Santoy, with his men" (024-031). Simon directs him during the engagement: "Bear to the right, Santoy" (024-043). After the battle, it is Santoy who takes charge of the wounded (024-124), demonstrating once again his comprehensive competence as a military officer.

## Character and military ethos

Walter of Santoy is portrayed as the ideal professional soldier: competent, dependable, efficient, and devoid of courtly pretension. His character emerges not through lengthy description but through the accumulation of small, telling details. His "laughing glance" at [[maurice-of-gountray|Gountray]] (011-029) suggests a dry, sardonic humour—the amusement of a capable man observing the follies of the incompetent. His rapid execution of orders, from drilling to deployment to guard duty, marks him as a man of action rather than words.

Walter's relationship with [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] is founded on mutual professional respect. Simon recognised Walter's quality early, asking his companions for their reading of the man before confirming the appointment. In turn, Walter proved himself worthy of that assessment through sustained, reliable performance. In the military world of the novel, where figures like [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] represent the chivalric ideal and [[maurice-of-gountray|Gountray]] represents its decay, Walter of Santoy represents a third type: the unglamorous but indispensable professional upon whom all military enterprises ultimately depend.

## Significance in the narrative

Walter of Santoy functions in the novel as a measure of [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s judgement and leadership. Simon's ability to identify, appoint, and retain capable subordinates like Walter is what enables him to extend his reach beyond what any single commander could achieve alone. The garrison at [[montlice|Montlice]] thrives not because Simon is everywhere at once—he is not—but because he installs men like Walter in positions of authority and gives them the trust and resources to succeed.

Walter also represents the meritocratic dimension of Simon's household. Unlike the hereditary allegiances that structure much of the novel's political landscape, Walter's position is earned through demonstrated competence. He is not a nobleman; he is a professional soldier who rises on his own merits under a lord capable of recognising them. In this respect, his career parallels the upward mobility that [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] himself represents—the advancement of talent over birth.

### The Meritorious Ideal

Walter of Santoy embodies a particular ideal within the military world of the novel: the competent subordinate officer who makes the commander possible. While [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] is the protagonist whose name fills the chronicles, men like Walter are the sinews that connect command to execution. His trajectory — from an unknown soldier recommended by his peers, to garrison captain, to field officer trusted with independent command — mirrors in miniature the rise of Simon himself. Both men earn their places through demonstrated ability rather than inherited rank, and both find in each other the recognition that merit deserves.

In a narrative world where [[chivalry|chivalric]] ideals are tested against the hard realities of fifteenth-century warfare, Walter represents the unglamorous but essential truth that battles are won not only by the cold-eyed commander who plans them but by the captains who drill the men, hold the bridge, and tend the wounded when the fighting is done.
