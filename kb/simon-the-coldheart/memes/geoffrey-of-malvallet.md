## Character Overview

Geoffrey of Malvallet is the legitimate son of the Lord of Malvallet and the half-brother of [[simon-of-beauvallet]], the novel's protagonist. Born into the nobility of the Malvallet house, Geoffrey embodies the warm, spirited counterpoint to Simon's cold and calculating nature. Where Simon's eyes are calculating and remote, Geoffrey's sparkled with youthful fire and vitality (003-085). He is nineteen years old when he first appears in the narrative, already entrusted with leading his father's men to join Prince Henry at [[shrewsbury]] (003-083).

Geoffrey's physical appearance marks him as Simon's opposite in almost every way — dark where Simon is fair, warm where Simon is cold, and openly expressive where Simon is guarded (003-085, 003-271). Despite these contrasts, the two half-brothers forge a bond of deep mutual respect, bound together by the exchange of life-debts on the battlefield of Shrewsbury.

> "But where Simon's eyes were cold, Geoffrey's sparkled; and where Simon's mouth was stern, Geoffrey's curved in a smile." (003-085)

## Role in the Narrative

### First Meeting at Shrewsbury

Simon first meets his half-brother at the army camp outside Shrewsbury, where the forces of the Prince of Wales are gathering to confront the rebel [[henry-percy-hotspur]]. Geoffrey had arrived not twenty-four hours before Montlice's company, leading his men in place of his father (003-083). When he encounters Simon — who at that time calls himself "Nobody" — Geoffrey is sauntering through the great hall, a young nobleman at ease in his surroundings (003-084).

The meeting is marked by Geoffrey's immediate recognition of the red and gold device on Simon's shield — the Montlice colours — and his quizzical, mocking humour when Simon refuses to give his name (003-087, 003-092). From that first encounter, a wary curiosity is established between the two young men, each the son of a different father but sharing the blood of Malvallet.

> "I think I am Nobody, Sir Geoffrey." (003-092)

> "Why so do I! And being Nobody, beware how ye cross my path again." (003-093)

### The Battle of Shrewsbury

Geoffrey plays a vital role in the climactic battle of Chapter 3, fighting shoulder to shoulder with Simon in the chaos of the Shrewsbury field. When Simon saves his life in the press of combat, Geoffrey gasps his thanks — "At my back!" (003-192) — and the two fight on together. Later, Geoffrey is separated from Simon and watches from a distance as his half-brother fights with what appears to be supernatural ferocity (003-206).

The bond between the two is sealed when, at a critical moment, Geoffrey returns the favour, rescuing Simon from an enemy attack. "For which I thank thee, brother," Simon says, holding out his mailed hand (003-232). Geoffrey's reply — "Remember I am in thy debt!" (003-265) — establishes the central dynamic of their relationship: an ongoing exchange of life-debts, each brother saving the other in turn.

> "Canst see Hotspur?" panted Geoffrey once. "Methought I heard a shout." (003-202)

After the battle, Geoffrey is present when Simon is brought before the Prince of Wales. He smiles at Simon and makes a little encouraging gesture, standing at the Prince's side as Simon receives commendation (004-040, 004-041). When the Prince remarks on Simon's remarkable night vision, Geoffrey confirms with a shrug that he "had naught to say in the matter" of Simon's service — an understatement that hints at his growing respect for his half-brother's independence (004-045, 004-051).

### Campaigns in Wales and France

Following the victory at Shrewsbury, Geoffrey continues to serve the Lancastrian cause. In March of the following year, Simon joins him at his side in [[wales]], "engaged in hard fighting" against Welsh rebels (011-003). The two brothers fight together through the Welsh campaign, with Geoffrey and [[alan-of-montlice]] riding on either side of Simon when he returns home (011-012).

Later in the narrative, Geoffrey writes a letter to Simon urging him to join fresh military enterprises — a letter that speaks to the growing bond between the two half-brothers and Geoffrey's desire to have Simon at his side (010-131). The letter is delivered by their father, the Lord of [[malvallet]], who visits Simon bearing Geoffrey's message (010-126, 010-128).

> "My brother, I do write to urge thee that thou shouldst come to me." (010-131)

Geoffrey rises in prominence during the French campaign of 1417, where he leads the vanguard — "three hundred strong, Geoffrey of Malvallet leading the van" — alongside John Holland, Earl of Huntingdon (013-003). This marks a significant evolution from the young man who led his father's men at Shrewsbury to a commander entrusted with the front line of a major expedition.

### Belremy and Normandy

At the siege and storming of [[belremy]], Geoffrey of Malvallet is unmistakable at the head of the English forces as they enter the town (014-122). He fights alongside Simon throughout the campaign, and the two brothers share the dangers and triumphs of the French wars.

By the closing chapters, Geoffrey has been appointed, alongside Sir [[alan-of-montlice]], as a Lieutenant and Warden of the Lands and Marches of Normandy under Simon's overall command (031-056). This appointment represents the culmination of Geoffrey's military career within the novel — trusted with significant authority in the administration of conquered territories, serving at his half-brother's side.

## Character Analysis

Geoffrey of Malvallet represents a vital narrative counterweight to [[simon-of-beauvallet]]. Where Simon embodies the cold, calculating warrior — the "cold-blooded tiger-cub" — Geoffrey supplies the warmth, humour, and emotional expressiveness that Simon lacks (003-215, 003-085). His eyes "sparkled" and his mouth "curved in a smile" where Simon's were cold and stern, providing the reader with a character who feels and expresses emotion openly.

Geoffrey is no less brave than Simon. At Shrewsbury he fights ferociously, "wielding his sword like one possessed," and he proves his courage again and again throughout the Welsh and French campaigns (003-190). But his courage is of a different quality — warmer, more spontaneous, and less chilling than Simon's unblinking ferocity.

> "Thou cold-blooded tiger-cub! Thou hast no compassion!" (003-215)

His relationship with Simon evolves from wary curiosity — the strange young man who refuses to give his name — to deep fraternal loyalty. The exchange of life-debts at Shrewsbury binds them in a way that transcends their different fathers and their contrasting temperaments. The Malvallet blood that runs in both of them is acknowledged repeatedly: "In me, my lord, is Malvallet blood. A Malvallet asks no favours" (004-164).

Geoffrey also shares a warm relationship with Alan of Montlice, the son of Fulk the Lion. The two are raised in proximity, and by the novel's end they serve together as Simon's lieutenants in Normandy — a testament to the enduring bond between the houses of Malvallet and Montlice (002-006, 031-056, 011-012).

## Key Relationships

- **[[simon-of-beauvallet]]** — Half-brother. Bound by an exchange of life-debts on the Shrewsbury field. Geoffrey is the warm, open counterpart to Simon's coldness, and their bond deepens through the Welsh and French campaigns. Geoffrey addresses Simon as "brother" and seeks his companionship in arms (003-232, 010-131).

- **Lord of Malvallet** — Geoffrey's father, the head of the noble house. Geoffrey leads his father's men at Shrewsbury and later delivers his father's letters to Simon (003-083, 010-128).

- **[[alan-of-montlice]]** — Son of Fulk of Montlice, raised alongside Geoffrey. The two serve together as lieutenants under Simon in Normandy, and they ride together at Simon's side on his return from Wales (011-012, 031-056).

- **[[fulk-of-montlice]]** — The Lion of Montlice, who raises Simon and is father to Alan. Geoffrey moves in Fulk's orbit through his connection to Alan and to Simon (002-006).

- **[[henry-v-of-england]]** — Prince of Wales and later King. Geoffrey serves under Henry at Shrewsbury, stands at his side when Simon is commended, and fights in Henry's French campaigns (004-040, 013-011).

## Historical Context

Geoffrey of Malvallet is a fictional character, but his military career closely mirrors the historical trajectory of a young Lancastrian knight in the early fifteenth century. He fights at the Battle of Shrewsbury (1403), historically a decisive victory for Henry IV against the rebel Percy forces and a battle in which the future [[henry-v-of-england]], then Prince Hal, was famously wounded in the face by an arrow (003-082).

His subsequent service in Wales reflects the historical Glyndwr Rising, the Welsh rebellion led by Owain Glyndwr that occupied English forces for over a decade (011-003). His role in the 1417 French campaign and the siege of [[belremy]] mirrors Henry V's historical conquest of Normandy, which culminated in the Treaty of Troyes and Henry's recognition as heir to the French throne (013-003, 014-122).

Geoffrey's final appointment as Lieutenant and Warden of the Marches of Normandy reflects the historical practice of English military administration in occupied France — trusted commanders were appointed to hold and govern conquered territories, often serving alongside family members or close allies (031-056).
