Knighthood in the world of the novel is at once a military rank, a social status, and a deeply personal identity. It is the threshold that separates the boy from the man, the squire from the warrior, the follower from the leader. For Simon of Beauvallet, knighthood is the goal toward which every action of his early life is directed — and the achievement of it marks the point at which his destiny passes into his own hands.

## The Meaning of Knighthood

### A Military Rank

At its core, knighthood is a martial qualification. A knight is a mounted warrior trained in the use of arms, capable of leading men in battle and fighting as heavy cavalry. The novel treats knighthood as a credential earned through demonstrated competence: a man does not inherit the right to be a knight, nor is he knighted as a mere courtesy. He must prove himself in the field.

Simon's entire apprenticeship at Montlice is preparation for knighthood. He serves as Fulk's squire for three years, learning the skills of war under the tutelage of one of the most formidable warriors in England. When the test comes, at the battle of Shrewsbury, Simon's performance is so remarkable that the Prince of Wales himself takes note and recommends him for knighthood. "The Prince hath recommended thee for knighthood" (004-026), Fulk tells him — a commendation from the highest military authority in the land.

### A Social Threshold

Knighthood also marks a definitive change in social standing. Before his knighting, Simon is a landless squire, a man of uncertain parentage who "calls himself Beauvallet" (003-272). After the ceremony, he is Sir Simon of Beauvallet — a titled gentleman with the right to bear arms, to command men, and to be received in the halls of kings.

> "Rise, Sir Simon of Beauvallet." Simon came to his feet. The rest of the ceremony passed in a kind of haze. — [004-041]

The transformation is immediate and visible. His companion Geoffrey greets him with wonder: "Hast been knighted! Simon, Simon, is't true indeed?" (004-072). Fulk confirms it with gruff pride: "Ay, knighted he is, and by the King himself" (004-073). From that moment forward, Simon is addressed by a title, and the social gulf between him and the common men of Montlice is now formalized in law and custom.

## Simon's Path to Knighthood

### The Squire's Ambition

From his first arrival at Montlice, Simon makes his ambition for knighthood explicit. When questioned by Fulk about why he has come, Simon answers with a directness that sets the tone for their entire relationship: "Ye think to earn knighthood with me?" Fulk asks. Simon's reply is the declaration of a man who has already mapped out his life: he will earn it through service, and he will accept no lesser outcome (001-129).

The question is not whether Simon will be knighted but when and by whom. Fulk recognizes Simon's quality almost immediately — "he knew him for a soldier born, and spread the mantle of his protection about his squire" (002-086) — but the timing of the knighthood is a matter of politics and opportunity as much as merit. A knighthood conferred too early might be seen as favoritism; one deferred too long might be seen as an insult.

> "Ye think to earn knighthood with me?" Fulk demanded. Simon met his eyes unflinchingly. — [001-129]

### The Battle of Shrewsbury

The battle of Shrewsbury is the crucible in which Simon's knighthood is forged. Fulk, leading his men in place of another commander who has fallen, drives deep into the enemy line. Simon fights at his side with a ferocity that astonishes the veteran soldiers around him.

> "Montlice, leading his men in place of another who had fallen, drove deep into the press, and at his side, fighting with a cold and terrible fury, went his young squire, Simon of Beauvallet." — [003-083]

The Prince of Wales witnesses Simon's conduct and is impressed. When Fulk later presents Simon to the Prince, the royal commendation is immediate: the Prince "hath recommended thee for knighthood" (004-026). This is the turning point — the recognition by the heir to the throne that Simon has earned the rank not by birth or patronage but by deeds of arms performed under the most demanding conditions.

### The Ceremony of Dubbing

The ceremony itself takes place on the day following the battle. It is a formal ritual conducted by King Henry himself, whose participation elevates the event from a routine military promotion to a royal recognition of exceptional merit.

> "The ceremony of knighting took place on the following day. King Henry himself performed the office." — [004-039]

The ceremony follows the traditional form. Simon kneels before the King, who strikes him with the flat of the sword — the accolade, or "dubbing" — and speaks the words that transform him: "Rise, Sir Simon of Beauvallet" (004-041). At that moment, the boy who once demanded entry to Fulk's castle becomes a knight of England, entitled to wear the golden spurs and to take his place among the warrior elite. The "rest of the ceremony passed in a kind of haze" (004-041), suggesting that for Simon, the ritual is less important than the reality it has created — he is now what he has always intended to be.

Fulk's lady, Eleanor of Montlice, marks the occasion with words that underline the social dimension of the transformation: "Now art thou honoured indeed, lad! My lady, is there a braver knight than our Simon?" (004-076). The knighthood is not merely a personal achievement but a source of pride for the entire household that has fostered him.

## Knighthood and the King's Favor

King Henry's personal involvement in Simon's knighthood has consequences far beyond the ceremony itself. When Simon later presents himself at court, the King remembers him specifically as "Simon of Beauvallet whom you knighted at Shrewsbury after the battle in last July" (006-034). This royal recollection is the foundation upon which Simon's subsequent career is built.

> "Perchance remember one Simon of Beauvallet whom you knighted at Shrewsbury after the battle in last July?" — [006-034]

The King's interest in Simon goes beyond mere recollection. When Henry learns that Simon is "the thirteenth knight" from Shrewsbury, he is eager to meet the young warrior whose reputation has preceded him (006-062). This royal attention transforms Simon's knighthood from a personal milestone into a matter of state — the recognition of a warrior whose value to the crown has been demonstrated on the field and who may now be called upon for greater service.

The King also notes approvingly that Simon wears "a ring that Richard was wont to give his knights, and which are now seen in England no more" (006-077) — a detail that connects Simon to an older and grander tradition of knightly honor, associated with King Richard the Lionheart himself.

## The Rewards of Knighthood

Knighthood brings tangible rewards. After the ceremony, Simon is "given a squire of his own and a page" and receives "a goodly sum each month as wage for his services" (004-081). He is no longer the servant but the served; no longer the follower but the leader. These material perquisites are the outward signs of the inward transformation — the knighthood has made him a man of substance in the feudal world.

But the greatest reward of Simon's knighthood is not material. It is the respect of his peers and the opening of a path to greater things. Within a short time, the King will make him Baron of Beauvallet, giving him lands and a lordship of his own. The knighthood was the necessary prerequisite — without it, no barony could have followed. As the King himself notes, the gift of Beauvallet is "for his services" (008-063) — services that could only have been rendered by a knight.

## Knighthood and Character

The novel uses knighthood not merely as a plot device but as a lens through which to examine character. For Simon, knighthood is the fulfillment of a purpose he has pursued with single-minded intensity since boyhood. He does not seek it for glory or for the trappings of rank but because it is the station to which his abilities entitle him and from which he can exercise his full capacity for leadership.

Fulk's attitude toward knighthood reflects his own rough-hewn character. He promises it to Simon bluntly — "Shalt have thy knighthood, lad, an thou'lt stay yet a while with me" (004-029) — and delivers it without ceremony or sentimentality. For Fulk, knighthood is a tool: it is what a man needs to command men and to claim his place in the world.

Alan's reaction to Simon's knighthood is a mixture of pride and wistfulness. He is glad for his friend but aware that the knighthood will take Simon away from Montlice and into a wider world in which Alan himself may have a smaller part to play.

## Historical Background

In 15th-century England, knighthood was both a military and a social institution. The ceremony of dubbing followed an ancient form: the candidate knelt, the lord or king struck him with the flat of a sword — the accolade — and spoke the words of investiture. The new knight received golden spurs, a symbol of his mounted status, and swore an oath to uphold the ideals of chivalry: to defend the Church, to protect the weak, and to serve his lord faithfully.

King Henry V was a notably active promoter of knights. His campaigns in France created many opportunities for men of talent to distinguish themselves and earn knighthood through military service. The "thirteenth knight" detail (006-062) reflects the historical reality that knighthoods were often conferred in batches after major battles, when the King recognized multiple warriors whose conduct had been commended by their commanders.

The novel's treatment of knighthood accurately reflects the meritocratic dimension of the late medieval English military system. While knighthood could be inherited, the period also saw many men — particularly younger sons and bastards — elevated for distinguished service. This was the path that Simon follows, and the novel presents it as both honorable and just.
