## The Chivalric Code: Ideal and Reality

The chivalric code that governed medieval knighthood was never a single written law but rather a constellation of expectations — prowess in arms, loyalty to one's lord, defence of the Church and the helpless, and the practice of courtesy, especially toward women. By the 15th century of the novel's setting, chivalry had become highly formalised through institutions like the Order of the Garter (founded 1348), yet it was also under pressure from the professionalisation of warfare and the centralising power of monarchs such as [[king-henry-v|Henry V]].

The novel engages with chivalry not as a romantic backdrop but as a living code that characters must interpret and negotiate. Through [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], it explores the tension between chivalric display and practical substance — a knight who fulfills every military obligation of chivalry while dispensing almost entirely with its ceremonial and courtly aspects. His coldness is not a rejection of chivalry but a particular, disciplined interpretation of it: one that privileges order, effectiveness, and honour over gallantry and show.

> "I will have no burning or pillaging, but order and chivalry. Ye do understand?" (014-038)

This command, issued during the final assault on [[belremy|Belrémy]], distills Simon's version of chivalry to its essence. Chivalry here is not about courtly manners or romantic quests — it is about the discipline that separates a soldier from a brigand. The pairing of "order and chivalry" reveals Simon's conviction that the two are inseparable: chivalry without order is mere sentiment, and order without chivalry is mere tyranny.

## Chivalry in Practice: The Knighting of Simon

### From Page to Knight

Simon's path to knighthood illustrates the practical machinery behind the chivalric ideal. The novel traces the full arc of his ascent: arriving at [[montlice|Montlice]] as a nameless boy (001-137), serving as [[alan-of-montlice|Alan]]'s page and then [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]]'s (002-003), and distinguishing himself through martial competence rather than high birth.

The question of knighthood appears early. When the boy Simon first presents himself, [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]] asks with characteristic directness:

> "Ye think to earn knighthood with me?" (001-129)

The question is prophetic. Simon does earn knighthood through [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]]'s patronage — but not through favour alone. He earns it on the battlefield at [[shrewsbury|Shrewsbury]], where his conduct draws the attention of no less a figure than [[king-henry-v|Henry of Monmouth]] himself. Fulk's prediction proves accurate:

> "Perchance he will knight thee soon." (002-054)

When the moment of knighthood arrives, it is [[king-henry-v|the King]] who performs the ceremony — an honour that places Simon among the "dozen knights" created after the battle (004-022). The ceremony itself is described with the novel's characteristic economy:

> "The ceremony of knighting took place on the following day." (004-039)

Simon's response to his knighthood is striking for its lack of exuberance. When congratulated by those who trained him, he is described as "unimpressed by the honour" (004-019) — not ungrateful, but unmoved by the ceremony that surrounds the substance. This is the pattern that will define his entire relationship to chivalry: accepting its obligations while remaining indifferent to its ornaments.

### The Squire and the Page

The chivalric hierarchy is further revealed through Simon's treatment of his own dependents. After his knighting, he is "given a squire of his own and a page" (004-081) — the young Roger who worships him and the small Arnold who follows like a shadow. Simon maintains these relationships with characteristic reserve:

> "None, my lord, save Roger, my squire, and little Arnold, my page." (008-105)

The loyalty these youths show Simon — Roger in a gloomy mood at their departure from [[montlice|Montlice]] (005-005), the pages "following him like a troop" (011-027) — testifies that Simon's coldness does not preclude the bonds of obligation and care that the chivalric relationship between knight and squire demands.

## Chivalry and Combat: The Code on the Battlefield

### Honour in Arms

The novel depicts several moments where chivalric norms govern battlefield conduct. Simon's understanding of honour in combat is distinctive — he insists that honour belongs to the strategist, not merely the executor:

> "To me is small honour due, for by chance alone did I strike the vital spot." (007-048)

And further:

> "Yet to him whom the finger of God hath guided to the vital spot has honour ever been due." (007-049)

When [[king-henry-v|Henry V]] tests this reasoning — asking whether honour is due to "him whose brain planned the deed" — Simon's assent (007-071) reveals a philosophy of chivalric merit that values intellect over brawn. This is a departure from the conventional knightly ideal that prized physical prowess above all, and it marks Simon as a soldier of the new, more professional type that the 15th century was producing.

### The Paradox of the Cold-Hearted Knight

The novel's central chivalric paradox is embodied in Simon himself. He is described with an epithet that seems to contradict the chivalric virtues of warmth and generosity:

> "Simon, what hast thou in place of a heart?" (002-033)

Alan's question goes to the core of the novel's engagement with chivalry. Simon is undeniably a great knight — he protects the helpless (rescuing the damsel in distress at 005-048), serves his lord faithfully, fights with unmatched skill — yet he lacks the emotional warmth that chivalric literature typically associates with the ideal knight. He is, as Alan observes:

> "Thou art so strange, Simon, and so cold." (002-044)

The novel suggests that this coldness is not a defect but a different kind of strength. Simon's composure under provocation — "they never disturbed his cold composure, nor awakened any feeling of anger in him" (002-005) — makes him a more effective commander and a more reliable ally than the hot-blooded knights of romance. His is a chivalry stripped of sentiment, reduced to its operational core: protect the weak, serve the lord, fight honourably, and maintain order.

### The Knight-Errant and the Practical Soldier

The novel carefully distinguishes Simon from the romantic figure of the knight-errant. When he encounters a damsel in distress, the text is explicit:

> "Simon was no knight-errant, but he went forward quickly." (005-048)

He rescues the woman not because he is seeking adventure or romantic glory but because it is the practical obligation of a man who carries a sword. The distinction is crucial: Simon fulfills the chivalric duty of protecting the weak without any of the chivalric posturing that accompanies it in romance.

## Courtly Chivalry and Its Limits

### The Language of Gallantry

The novel is not without characters who embody the courtly aspects of chivalry. [[alan-of-montlice|Alan of Montlice]], with his "slender grace" and attire of "silks" (012-042), moves through the world with the ease of a court-bred knight. His kneeling is graceful (024-122, 031-016), his manners polished, his instincts romantic.

[[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] represents another variant — the hot-blooded young knight whose chivalry is expressed through impetuosity rather than calculation. When he urges Simon to "storm it now in full force" at Belrémy (013-021), he reveals a conception of chivalry that values daring over prudence.

The contrast between these figures and Simon structures the novel's examination of chivalry itself. Simon's men, observing their "shining armour and their disciplined appearance" (014-130), recognise a different kind of knighthood — one defined not by individual feats of arms but by collective order and effectiveness.

### The Chevalier: Chivalry as Performance

The novel's most pointed critique of chivalric pretension appears in the figure of [[the-chevalier|the Chevalier de Fleurival]]. Here is a knight who has mastered every external form of chivalry — the costume, the manner, the courtly address — while being entirely hollow within. When a character quotes "With grace and might of chivalry" (014-152), the irony is unmistakable: the Chevalier's version of chivalry is pure theatre, a costume worn to impress rather than a code lived.

Simon's treatment of the Chevalier throughout the novel — his cold contempt, his refusal to be impressed by chivalric display — represents the novel's judgment on those who use the forms of chivalry to mask the absence of its substance.

## The Sunset of Chivalry

### A Code Under Pressure

The novel is set at a historical moment when chivalry was increasingly under pressure from new forms of warfare and politics. The reign of [[king-henry-v|Henry V]] represented both the apex of chivalric kingship — Henry was himself a keen patron of chivalric culture — and the beginning of its decline, as professional armies and gunpowder artillery began to erode the military monopoly of the mounted knight.

The novel captures this tension in a moment of startling directness:

> "Truly, why, then, the time for chivalry is past." (015-176)

This declaration, made during the tense confrontation within [[belremy|Belrémy]], acknowledges that the code which has governed the novel's world may be reaching its endpoint. Yet the response — "Lead on, Sir Chevalier" — reasserts chivalric identity even as it concedes the code's fragility. The novel offers no simple resolution: chivalry is both fading and enduring, both essential and inadequate.

Simon himself embodies this ambiguity. He is simultaneously the novel's greatest knight and the character who most thoroughly dismantles the romantic pretensions of knighthood. His final resolution — to lay siege to his own rightful holdings (031-006) — shows him applying chivalric methods to chivalric ends, with the same cold efficiency he has displayed throughout.

### The Enduring Substance

What survives in the novel's treatment of chivalry is not the ceremony or the courtly graces but the core of order, loyalty, and protection that the code was always meant to secure. When Simon demands "order and chivalry" of his men, he is not invoking a dead ideal but a living standard — one measured not in fine speeches but in disciplined conduct, not in the trappings of knighthood but in the quality of the deeds that underpin it.

## Historical Background

The code of chivalry developed in medieval Europe from the 12th century onward, fusing three distinct elements: the warrior ethos of the Germanic tribes, Christian moral teaching (particularly the Peace and Truce of God movements), and the courtly culture of the Provencal troubadours. By the 15th century, chivalry had become institutionalised through orders of knighthood — England's Order of the Garter (founded by Edward III in 1348), Burgundy's Order of the Golden Fleece (1430), and others — which formalised honour as a system of recognition tied to military and political service.

The novel's setting during the reign of [[king-henry-v|Henry V]] (1413–1422) places it at a pivotal moment. Henry was both a practitioner of chivalric kingship — he founded the Brigittine abbey at Syon as a chivalric-pious act, challenged the Dauphin to single combat in the chivalric tradition — and a modernising monarch whose military reforms (systematic recruitment, disciplined ordinance, artillery integration) anticipated the professional armies that would eventually make the knight obsolete. The tension between these two aspects of Henry's rule mirrors the novel's own ambivalent treatment of chivalry.
