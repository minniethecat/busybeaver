Fealty is the solemn and binding oath by which a man pledges his loyalty and service to a lord. It is the fundamental bond of feudal society, a reciprocal relationship in which the vassal offers military service and counsel, and the lord in return grants protection and the right to hold land. In the novel, fealty is not an abstract legal concept but a living reality that shapes the destiny of every character — from the squire who carries his lord's lance to the king before whom conquered garrisons kneel.

## Definition

Fealty in the medieval world was the formal acknowledgment of a hierarchical bond. A man swore fealty by kneeling before his lord, placing his hands between the lord's hands, and speaking the words of the oath. The ceremony created obligations on both sides: the vassal owed military service, counsel, and loyalty; the lord owed protection, justice, and the grant of land to sustain the vassal. The bond was considered sacred — to break it was both a legal felony and a mortal sin.

In the novel's world, fealty is the architecture of all political relationships. Every character exists within a chain of fealty that runs upward from the common soldier to the king. The novel repeatedly tests whether these bonds will hold under the strain of war, ambition, and conflicting loyalties. When Simon arrives at Montlice as an unknown boy, the first question asked of him is "Whose man are you?" (003-253) — because in this world, to be someone is to belong to someone.

> "Whose man are you, Simon of Beauvallet?" — [003-253]

Simon's reply is simple and absolute: "I serve Montlice, Highness" (003-254). That declaration of fealty places him within the feudal order and determines his subsequent path through the novel. It gives the Prince the information he needs to classify this formidable young warrior, and it establishes the chain of obligation that Simon will later transcend when the King makes him a baron in his own right.

> "In that long grip was his allegiance to Simon sealed." — [009-191]

This moment between Simon and Geoffrey of Malvallet captures fealty in its most instinctive form: no oath is spoken, no ceremony conducted, yet the bond formed in that hand-grip is as binding as any formal vow. The novel repeatedly shows that the deepest fealties are those freely chosen and sealed by action rather than by words.

## The Oath of Fealty at Belremy

The most explicit demonstration of fealty in the novel occurs at the fall of Belremy. After Simon captures the castle, he demands that its defenders swear fealty to King Henry as a condition of their lives and liberty. This is fealty as a political instrument — the formal submission of a conquered people to a new sovereign.

> "I, Simon of Beauvallet, do swear by the Rood and by all the blessed saints that if ye will deliver up the keys of this castle, and do swear fealty to my master, King Henry" — [015-079]

The scene at Belremy illustrates two distinct aspects of fealty. First, fealty is the mechanism by which conquest is legitimized. The garrison's oath transforms military occupation into lawful rule. Second, fealty can be offered as an alternative to destruction — Simon extends "protection to all who would swear allegiance to Henry" (015-003), converting enemies into subjects through the ritual of oath-taking. For the most part, the garrison accepted these terms, choosing fealty to a new king over death or perpetual imprisonment.

The Lady Margaret's position complicates the matter. As the chatelaine of Belremy, she has her own fealty to consider — to her dead husband's house and to the French cause. Simon demands that she "swear fealty to my master, King Henry" (015-079) and "deliver the knight, Sir Alan of Montlice, into my hands" (015-009). Her resistance to this demand drives the central conflict of the novel's middle chapters, demonstrating that fealty cannot simply be extorted — it must either be freely given or secured through negotiation.

### Negotiated Fealty

Fealty in the novel is not always straightforward. When Geoffrey of Malvallet and Simon negotiate with the Lady Margaret, they propose a conditional arrangement: if Simon will "withdraw your men from Belremy, swearing never to return, she will..." (015-104) This is fealty as diplomacy — a negotiated settlement in which both sides calibrate their obligations. The proposal ultimately fails, but it shows that the novel's characters understand fealty not as a rigid absolute but as a bond that can be shaped by circumstance.

## Fealty in the Chain of Command

### Squire to Lord

The novel traces Simon's movement through several levels of the fealty chain. When he first arrives at Montlice, he is a landless boy seeking service. His declaration "I would carry your lance and squire you, sir" (001-069) is an offer of fealty in its most personal form — the squire binding himself to a single knight. Fulk initially scoffs: "I've no need of squires" (001-076), but ultimately accepts Simon into his household.

Simon's fealty to Fulk is not merely formal; it becomes the defining structure of his young life. He serves three years as Fulk's squire, and during that time his loyalty never wavers. When the Prince of Wales asks him whose man he is, Simon answers without hesitation that he serves Montlice. Asked whether he could leave Fulk's service for the Prince's father, Simon's allegiance remains firm — "for the present I owe allegiance to Montlice" (003-244).

### Baron to King

Simon's elevation to the barony of Beauvallet transforms his fealty relationship. The King makes him Baron of Beauvallet "for his services" (008-063), and in doing so places Simon at the head of his own chain of fealty. He is no longer merely a vassal; he is now a lord with vassals of his own. The men of Beauvallet owe fealty to him, and he in turn owes fealty to the Crown.

This elevation is driven by the King's strategic judgment: "I have little fear of risings near Beauvallet now. This gift is to mine own advantage" (007-101). The King grants Beauvallet because he knows that Simon's fealty is worth having — that a strong and capable lord on that land will secure the peace more effectively than a distant royal steward could. Fealty here is a two-way instrument of governance.

> "For his services the King made Simon Baron of Beauvallet, and gave him a land called Fair Pastures, which was once the estate of a rebellious lord" — [008-063]

## Tests of Fealty

### Alan of Montlice and Conflicting Obligations

Alan of Montlice faces the classic feudal dilemma: fealty to his father, to his friend, and to his king pull in different directions. When Simon is knighted and departs Montlice, Alan must choose where his primary fealty lies. "If Fulk is dead, then do I owe allegiance to Alan, his son. But I do not..." (003-246) — this fragment of reasoning captures the web of obligation that fealty creates.

Alan ultimately proves true to both his father and his friend, but the tension is real. The novel recognizes that fealty is not always a simple chain but a network, and that wise men must navigate among competing claims without breaking any of them.

### The Lady Margaret's Resistance

The Lady Margaret's refusal to swear fealty to Henry V is the most sustained test of the fealty system in the novel. She rejects the entire premise of English sovereignty, and in doing so places herself outside the protection that fealty would afford her. Simon must deal with her not as a rebellious vassal but as an enemy combatant, because she has refused the very bond that would make her a subject.

When Simon asks, "Thou wilt trust to a woman's honour?" (015-035) he is questioning whether a person who rejects fealty can be bound by any obligation at all. Her reply — "Mine honour? What of thine own, Simon of Beauvallet?" (017-102) — turns the question back on him, challenging whether a conqueror who imposes fealty by force can himself claim the moral standing that fealty confers.

### Ranaud: Fealty Freely Chosen

The character of Ranaud presents the novel's most interesting commentary on fealty. Ranaud is a fiercely independent giant who owes fealty to no man.

> "I give no parole, I swear no allegiance. It is war between us to the death." — [016-042]

This is fealty refused in the starkest terms — a declaration that sets Ranaud apart from the novel's feudal framework entirely. Yet he ultimately chooses to serve Simon, acknowledging him as "a fit master for me" (028-025).

The novel thus presents a hierarchy of fealty relationships. At the bottom is the compelled oath of the conquered garrison; in the middle is the inherited fealty of the born vassal; at the top is the freely chosen fealty of men like Ranaud, who serve not because they must but because they have found a lord they respect. It is this last form of fealty that the novel most admires, and it is this form that Simon most consistently inspires.

## Historical Background

In 15th-century England, fealty was the central institution of feudal governance. The oath of fealty (fidelitas) was distinct from the act of homage (homagium), though the two were often performed together. Homage was the physical act of submission — the vassal kneeling, placing his hands between the lord's, and receiving the kiss of peace. Fealty was the spoken oath, typically sworn on a Bible or relic, in which the vassal promised to be faithful, to do no harm to his lord, and to perform the services owed.

The legal consequence of breaking fealty was forfeiture — the loss of all lands held from the lord. This is precisely why the King's grant of Beauvallet to Simon involved the confiscation of lands from a "rebellious lord" (008-063): that lord had broken his fealty, and the penalty was the loss of everything he held. The King's ability to grant those same lands to a loyal servant was the engine that drove the entire feudal economy of loyalty.
