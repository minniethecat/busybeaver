## Overview

The knighting of Simon of Beauvallet is a pivotal event that transforms the protagonist from a nameless bastard squire into a recognized knight of the realm. The ceremony takes place at Shrewsbury on the day following the great battle, when King Henry IV bestows knighthood upon a select group of men who distinguished themselves in the fighting. Simon, having fought with extraordinary prowess alongside his half-brother [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] and saved the life of the [[prince-of-wales|Prince of Wales]], is among those chosen for the honor. The event also sees the public revelation of Simon's parentage when both [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]] and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] claim kinship to him before the King.

## Prelude to Knighthood

### The March to Shrewsbury

Simon arrived at Shrewsbury as a squire in the retinue of Fulk of Montlice, having endured the arduous march from Montlice to join the Prince of Wales on the Marches. The journey was perilous, and skirmishes broke out before the army even reached its destination. As Fulk observed, it was just as well that some fighting occurred on the road, for it allowed his men to whet their appetites for the greater conflict ahead (003-081..003-082). Simon, armed with his great cross-hilted sword and his bow, proved himself in these preliminary encounters, displaying the cold, efficient fury in battle that would later earn him renown (003-169).

### Valour on the Battlefield

The Battle of Shrewsbury itself saw Simon distinguish himself beyond all expectation. Fighting alongside Geoffrey of Malvallet, his half-brother whom he had only just met, Simon wielded his two-edged sword with devastating precision. When Geoffrey was hard-pressed, Simon came to his aid, and the two fought back to back against the press of men. Even after his sword-arm grew weary and he was forced to change to his left hand, Simon's prowess did not diminish (003-201). Geoffrey, watching him fight, was struck by the cold, methodical fury with which Simon dispatched his enemies (003-206).

> The memory of war was as the memory of yesterday. He had been in the thick of the fight, had borne himself well. (003-188)

### The Last to Kneel

The formal ceremony of knighting took place on the day after the battle. A dozen men were to be honored, and Simon was the last of them. Fulk had brought him the news earlier that morning, telling Simon that the King intended to make knights of those who had served with exceptional valor (004-022..004-023). Simon's response was characteristically restrained; he spent the morning polishing his sword and armor in quiet anticipation, while Fulk rode off to attend the King (004-021).

## The Ceremony

### Before the King

When his turn came, Simon was the last to kneel before Henry IV. The King, who had already heard accounts of Simon's deeds on the field, looked upon the young man with interest. Fulk had recommended Simon for [[knighthood]], and the King, impressed by what he had heard of the boy's courage, was inclined to grant the request (004-026).

> The ceremony of knighting took place on the following day, and Simon was the last to kneel before the King. (004-039..004-040)

Henry IV bestowed the accolade upon Simon with the traditional words, tapping each shoulder with the flat of his sword. As the King spoke the final words---"Rise, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Sir Simon of Beauvallet]]"---Simon came to his feet, no longer a nameless squire but a knight of the realm (004-041).

### The King's Words

After the ceremony, Henry spoke to the newly-made knight with a twinkle in his eye, remarking that he had heard yet more of Simon's doings on the battlefield. The King's manner was warm and approving, a mark of genuine respect for the young man's achievements (004-042). Simon, ever sparing of words, answered the King with his customary briefness, but the honor had been conferred and could not be undone.

> The King's last words to him: "Rise, Sir Simon of Beauvallet," Simon came to his feet. (004-041)

## The Contest of Kinship

### Fulk's Claim

No sooner had Simon risen from his knees than a remarkable scene unfolded. Fulk of Montlice, who had been watching the ceremony with pride, stepped forward and presented the newly-knighted Simon to his lady wife, saying with evident satisfaction, "I present thee Sir Simon of Beauvallet, my lady" (004-073). Fulk's tone made it clear that he considered Simon to be in his service, and he intended for all present to know it.

### Geoffrey's Counter-Claim

But Fulk was not to have the last word. Geoffrey of Malvallet, who had also been knighted that day, stepped forward and publicly declared his own tie to Simon. Geoffrey asserted that Simon was his half-brother, a son of the late Lord Malvallet, and therefore kin to him by blood. The revelation caused a stir among those assembled (004-090..004-093).

> Fulk answered him, calm and possessive: "Simon of Beauvallet is in my service." (004-093)

### The King's Interest

Henry IV watched the contest with evident amusement. Two of his newly-made knights, both distinguished warriors, were openly wrangling over the right to claim Simon as their own. Fulk insisted that Simon was his man by oath of service, while Geoffrey countered that blood was thicker than any bond of fealty. The King, recognizing the value of a man who inspired such loyalty in others, observed the exchange with keen interest, perhaps already calculating how Simon of Beauvallet might be useful to the Crown.

## Aftermath and Significance

### A New Name, A New Life

The knighting ceremony marked the irrevocable transformation of Simon's station. He had arrived at Montlice as a nameless boy who called himself Beauvallet after the lands of his unknown father's family (001-087). He had served Fulk as a squire, enduring blows and hard labor with the same stoic acceptance he brought to everything. Now he was Sir Simon of Beauvallet, with a name that commanded respect and a reputation that would open doors across England and France.

> "Ay, knighted he is," said Fulk, "and by the King's own hand." (004-073)

### Departure from Shrewsbury

Two days after the knighting, the army departed Shrewsbury (004-058). Simon rode out not as a squire in Fulk's train but as a knight in his own right, wearing the golden spurs that marked his new station. The boy who had once declared "Needs must I win my spurs one day" had made good on his quiet ambition (003-048). Ahead lay service to the King, the siege of Belremy, and encounters with the Lady Margaret---but the foundation of it all was this moment, when a bastard son of Malvallet was recognized by his King as a knight worthy of the name.

### Long-Term Consequences

The knighting had far-reaching consequences. When Simon later presented himself before the King once more to deliver captured dispatches, Henry remembered him as "one Simon of Beauvallet whom you knighted at Shrewsbury after the battle" (006-034). That personal recognition from the King would prove crucial in Simon's subsequent career, granting him access to royal favor and eventually to the command that would see him lay siege to [[belremy|Belremy]] and subdue the proud [[lady-margaret-of-belremy|Lady Margaret]].
