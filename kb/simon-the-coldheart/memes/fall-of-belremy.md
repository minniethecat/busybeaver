## Background

The siege of [[belremy|Belrémy]] had been ongoing (016-017), with [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] commanding the assault forces. The [[surrender-of-belremy|surrender]] terms had been offered and the city's fall was orchestrated through a combination of military pressure and political manoeuvring (016-038).

## The Fall

After the formal surrender, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] took possession of [[belremy|Belrémy]] (016-038). [[walter-of-santoy|Walter of Santoy]] was dispatched to set a guard over the gates, and [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] entered the city. The key moment came when [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] received the city's capitulation — a moment that marked his transformation from mercenary captain to conqueror (016-038).

> "It is done, my lord. Santoy was here."
>
> — [016-038]

The fall of [[belremy|Belrémy]] was conducted with discipline: [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s men held the gates, the garrison was disarmed, and order was maintained (017-001). Unlike the sack of many medieval towns, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s capture of [[belremy|Belrémy]] was characterised by control — a reflection of his leadership style.

## Aftermath

The fall of [[belremy|Belrémy]] had several consequences (017-001):
- The Lancastrian claim to the disputed territories was strengthened
- [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s [[honour|reputation]] as a military commander was cemented
- The Count's authority was broken, and the city passed into English control
- The Lady [[the-lady-margaret|Margaret]]'s status was resolved, her fate now in Lancastrian hands (024-117)

## Key Participants
