## Identity and Mannerisms

The Chevalier de Fleurival is introduced at the Lady Margaret's side during Simon's parley in the captured castle of [[belremy|Belremy]], a figure defined by affectation and artifice. He is "a dark gentleman, foppishly clad" who regards Simon with a faint sneer (015-074). His props are consistent throughout the narrative: a rose he perpetually twirls and smells, a mincing walk, a lisping English delivery that is as much performance as impediment.

> "You are a leetle brusque, milor', is it not so?" (015-078)

These mannerisms mark the Chevalier as a creature of the French court rather than the battlefield — the antithesis of [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s plain-spoken directness and [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]]'s bluff soldiering. Heyer deploys the rose as a recurring emblem of his character: it is beautiful, cultivated, and ultimately fragile. When the Chevalier "smelt his rose delicately" in the midst of a negotiation for his father's life (015-100), the gesture is at once elegant and grotesque.

His full name appears only once in the narrative: "The Chevalier de Fleurival recovered himself" (015-090). Elsewhere he is simply "the Chevalier," a title that carries ironic weight since he embodies everything chivalry is not — cowardice, treachery, and malice masquerading as refinement.

## The Parley: Submission Under Duress

### Diplomatic Pretensions

The Chevalier's most extended scene occupies the parley in Chapter 15, where he serves as intermediary between Simon and the Countess. When Simon bluntly demands submission, it is the Chevalier who answers:

> "Madame my cousin desires to make terms with you." (015-078)

He attempts to play the suave diplomat, suggesting that "one man against — shall we say five score?" gives the Countess the advantage (015-087). But Simon's rapier-glance makes him "involuntarily step back" (015-088), a first betrayal of the cowardice beneath the bravado. "The Chevalier de Fleurival recovered himself. He raised his eyeglass and through it surveyed Simon" (015-090), attempting to reassert his composure through the armour of aristocratic disdain.

When Simon's threat turns explicit, "The Chevalier paled a little, but still he managed a smile of contempt" (015-093). His next gambit is mockery: "'Est-ce possible?' The Chevalier laughed gently. 'My father, thou hearest? This strange man would kill me'" (015-096). But Simon's response — delivered with the cold finality that is his trademark — silences the laughter.

> "'That foolish threat!' The Chevalier shook with supercilious merriment" as Simon detailed the consequences of defiance, but the laughter died in his throat when he saw the Englishman's eyes. (015-098)

### The Breaking Point

Simon's ultimatum escalates, and the Chevalier's composure shatters in stages. He continues to smell his rose, a gesture of studied nonchalance (015-100), until "a gleam of triumph came into the Chevalier's eyes" (015-110) — the look of a man who believes he has found an advantage. But when Simon declares he has fifteen minutes to decide, and that the Chevalier's own life hangs on his answer, the mask crumbles:

> "The Chevalier had dropped his rose. He spoke no word, but his face was very white." (015-118)

The physical reaction is visceral: "A shudder went through the Chevalier; one of the ladies-in-waiting cried out" (015-121). And when Simon presses home his threat — "The Chevalier raised one shaking hand" (015-137) — the trembling hand speaks more than any words could. The courtly rose-dropper is revealed as a man whose courage extends no further than his perfume.

> "The Chevalier's eyes shifted from face to face; all the smiling insolence had gone out of them." In their place was naked fear, the terror of a man who has finally understood that his opponent does not play by the rules of the court. (015-151)

The capitulation, when it comes, is total. The Chevalier submits "in the name of the Countess Margaret" (015-158), preserving his own life and his father's at the cost of whatever pride he possessed. Simon "bowed slightly to the Chevalier" (015-160) — the minimal courtesy extended to a defeated man — and then compels the oath of non-violence. The Chevalier swears it, but his eyes betray him: "The Chevalier was biting his nails, seeking feverishly for some outlet" (015-162), sending Simon "a look of hatred" that will bear bitter fruit in later chapters.

## Humiliation and Rage

The Chevalier's pride, already wounded by his enforced submission, receives a deeper cut when Simon openly declares his contempt. "'Milor'!' The Chevalier was livid with rage. 'Is that an insult?'" (015-167). Simon's reply — that he would not trust the Chevalier's word — sends the Frenchman into a paroxysm of wounded dignity: "The Chevalier quivered with outraged dignity" (015-169), but his hand, reaching for his sword-hilt (015-173), lacks the courage to draw. It is a gesture of theatrical defiance rather than genuine threat.

Simon's final judgment is devastating:

> "I fight clean, sir, when I may, but I choose my foe's weapons, and when the foe seeks to fight me foully, why, then, the time for chivalry is past. Lead on, Sir Chevalier." (015-176)

"The Chevalier went blindly to the door" (015-177), a man stripped of every pretension. The title "Sir Chevalier" in Simon's mouth is not honour but mockery — the knight who is no knight, the gentleman who is no gentleman.

## The Oath-Breaker at Court

### Needling Margaret

In the aftermath of the siege, the Chevalier remains within [[belremy|Belremy]] as a subdued but watchful presence. He inserts himself into the Lady Margaret's chambers after Simon's proposal, his manner a blend of false solicitude and genuine malice. "'What want ye, Victor?'" Margaret asks, using his given name with cold familiarity (017-059). He brings gifts to cover his intrusion — "Margaret's hand flew to her breast" as he presses something into her palm (017-062) — and his tone is soft: "'I have brought thee this, Margot'" (017-062).

But the softness conceals a probe. When Margaret rejects his insinuations, declaring she has not changed her opinion of Simon, "The Chevalier shrugged, spreading out his hands" (017-067). His next words reveal the venom beneath: "'Ye grow noisome, Victor. Stab him thyself, if thou wilt'" (017-070). And his answer is chilling in its flippancy:

> "'Oh, I have submitted!' the Chevalier said nonchalantly. 'Else would I not be here, walking at large, as you see me. But submission, my cousin, is not forgiveness.'" (017-071)

He admits Simon's quality even as he plots against him: "'He is very ruthless,' the Chevalier answered. 'But a quick stroke — and there is an end'" (017-074). The Chevalier "walked mincingly to the door" (017-076), leaving behind the threat like a scent that lingers.

### The Watcher

In later scenes, the Chevalier hovers at the edges of gatherings, a perennial observer who contributes little but notes everything. After the parley, "the Chevalier leaned over the back of his chair, watching" (019-003). His whispered asides to Margaret — "'Thou art cold today, sweet cousin'" (019-004) — are the probes of a man who feeds on the emotional turmoil of others.

> When Margaret rushes past him disordered, he scrutinizes her and muses to Jeanne: "What does that betoken?" — the question of a man who sees in others' distress only opportunity. (025-106)

Margaret, for her part, cuts him repeatedly: "'I have not changed, Victor,' she answered curtly" (019-005). "'I need no help from you, Victor'" (019-010). Each rejection deepens his hatred — not of Margaret, whom he still desires as a possession, but of Simon, whose presence has displaced him in his cousin's regard and in the household he once dominated.

## The Assassination Plot

### Alan's Warning

The first clear warning of the Chevalier's murderous intent comes not from Simon — whose attention is fixed on larger matters — but from [[alan-of-montlice|Alan]], whose perceptions of character are consistently finer than his friend's:

> "I mislike the looks of yon Frenchman, the Chevalier. Of late there hath come a new gleam into his eyes, and I think he seeks to do thee harm." (026-118)

Alan's diagnosis is precise. The Chevalier's motive is jealousy: Margaret's preference for Simon over her own cousin is an insult to his pride and a threat to his ambitions. His method is equally characteristic: too cowardly to act himself, he will "hire some rogue to do it for him, and thus in a little salve his conscience."

The warning is borne out. An assassin strikes at Simon, and investigation reveals "a creature in the Chevalier's pay" (028-005). The Chevalier's response to exposure is characteristically evasive: "'Once I am gone the Chevalier will be happy enough'" (028-009), Simon observes, recognising that the man is too small for open confrontation. Even the prospect of vengeance seems beneath Simon's dignity: "'God knows. The Chevalier is too little for my vengeance'" (028-014).

## Exposure and Banishment

### The Judgment Scene

The Chevalier's downfall occurs in a single devastating scene. Summoned before the Lady Margaret with his father [[jean-de-galledemaine|the Sire de Galledemaine]], he faces judgment. "When the Chevalier and her uncle came in they found Margaret standing before the fire" (028-051), her posture already that of a judge. The Sire, baffled, asks a question heavy with premonition: "'Is it possible, madame, that Victor has annoyed thee?'" (028-056).

Margaret's opening is cold and regal:

> "Annoyed is a small word, sir. The Chevalier will understand, when I say that I think his own lands stand in need of him." (028-058)

This is banishment, couched in the language of courtly euphemism but unmistakable in its finality. The Sire, horrified, protests his son's innocence: "'Victor! Madame, it cannot be true! My son — !'" (028-068) and begs: "'I implore thee listen to Victor's defence!'" (028-074). But there is no defence to be made.

The Chevalier's response to his father's direct question — "'Victor! My God, Victor, thou didst not do this thing?'" (028-077) — is the novel's definitive judgment on his character. He cannot meet his father's eyes. His final words strip away every last pretense of honour:

> "You are over squeamish, mon pere," he said carelessly. (028-082)

The father's response is instant and absolute: "'Thou craven cur!'" And the Chevalier's parting sneer at Margaret — "'When thou art miserable in yon Saxon's arms, think of me!'" (028-090) — completes the portrait of a man whose vanity and malice have consumed whatever honour his birth entitled him to. The Sire picks up the flower his son dropped and throws it into the fire (028-092). The rose, the Chevalier's emblem from his first appearance, is consigned to the flames. The symbolism needs no elaboration.

## The Anti-Chivalric Character

The Chevalier represents a specific type of antagonist in Heyer's moral universe: not the openly evil villain ([[raoul-the-terrible|Raoul]]), nor the calculating political enemy, but the venal courtier whose refinement masks spiritual poverty. His weapons are not steel but insinuation; his battlefield is not the open field but the whispered corridor and the hired bravo's knife.

Where a true knight would meet a slight with the sword, the Chevalier meets it with a paid assassin. Where honour would demand an open challenge, he spies, schemes, and cringes. His father's rejection — a man of genuine honour who watches his son exposed as a coward and a would-be murderer — is the novel's final verdict on the decadence of the French courtly tradition against which the English heroes are measured.
