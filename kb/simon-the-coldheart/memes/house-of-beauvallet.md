House of Beauvallet is the noble house and barony that King Henry V creates for Simon in recognition of his extraordinary military services. It is the only house in the novel's world that is founded in the course of the story rather than inherited from ancestral lines — a lordship won by the sword rather than by blood.

## Nature and Allegiance

### A Crown Creation

The House of Beauvallet is a direct creation of the English Crown. King Henry V, seeking to reward a young knight whose services have proven invaluable and to secure a strategically important tract of land, elevates Simon to the rank of baron and grants him the estate known as Fair Pastures. The King's motivation is both gratitude and calculation: "I have little fear of risings near Beauvallet now. This gift is to mine own advantage" (007-101).

> "For his services the King made Simon Baron of Beauvallet, and gave him a land called Fair Pastures, which was once the estate of a rebellious lord." — [008-063]

The lands of Beauvallet were formerly the property of a lord who rebelled against the Crown — his lands forfeit under feudal law, his house extinguished. The King's grant transforms these confiscated lands into a new lordship, symbolic of the Crown's power to make and unmake noble houses. The name itself is the King's invention: "Fair Pastures — Beau Vallet. Ay, that is it" (007-095), linking the French description of the land to Simon's own chosen surname and creating a house name that is at once descriptive and personal.

### Feudal Obligations

As a baron, Simon owes fealty and military service to the Crown. His house sits within the feudal hierarchy of England — below the King and the great magnates, above the knights and gentry of his own domain. The barony carries the obligation to maintain order on its lands, to provide men-at-arms when the King calls, and to administer justice to the inhabitants.

The men of Beauvallet, in turn, owe fealty to Simon. "In a surprisingly short time the men of Beauvallet settled down under the new regime" (010-021), accepting their new lord and the order he imposes. The house thus functions as the local government, with Simon as its head and its steward and officers as the instruments of his rule.

## Key Members

### Simon of Beauvallet, Baron and Founder

Simon is not merely the leader of the house — he is its founder, its reason for being, and the force that gives it character. He takes possession of his new domain with the same cold efficiency he brings to everything. The steward of the former lord is dismissed, the malefactors are brought to justice, and a new order is imposed with a speed that astonishes the countryside.

> "Multitudinous changes were wrought in Beauvallet. Malefactors were brought to justice, the old steward dismissed, and the whole estate reorganized under Simon's iron hand." — [010-019]

Simon knows every man in Beauvallet by name — "Do ye know every man in Beauvallet, sir?" Bernard asks wonderingly (009-105) — and governs with an intimate knowledge of his domain that the previous lord, whoever he was, never possessed. He is a hands-on ruler, inspecting his lands, judging disputes, and ensuring that his commands are obeyed absolutely.

### Master Hubert, the Steward

Master Hubert is the steward Simon finds in place when he first arrives at what was then Fair Pastures. He is described as sitting "in the kitchens at the Castle of Fair Pastures, now known as Beauvallet" (009-003), a detail that suggests the shambolic state of the estate under its previous management. Simon assesses Hubert quickly and finds him wanting — the steward is replaced as part of the sweeping reorganization that follows.

### Bernard

Bernard is a man of Beauvallet who emerges as one of Simon's trusted followers. His wondering observation that Simon knows every man's name (009-105) testifies to the new lord's remarkable attention to detail and his determination to govern his domain personally rather than through intermediaries.

### Nicholas, the Defiant Servant

Nicholas is the "one person in all Beauvallet who would openly defy Simon" (010-035). When Simon dismisses him from service, Nicholas departs "in an hour, calling down curses upon the head of his new lord" (010-018). This episode demonstrates that Simon's rule, while effective, is not universally popular — and that the new baron will tolerate no open defiance from those who serve him.

## Role in the Story

### The Rise of a Bastard

The creation of the House of Beauvallet is the crowning event of Simon's rise from obscurity to prominence. He enters Montlice as a landless boy of uncertain parentage, calling himself "Beauvallet" — literally "beautiful valley," a name that points to no known lineage. Fulk of Montlice confirms that he is Simon's half-brother: "He calls himself Beauvallet, sir, and is my half-brother. The late lord's bastard son" (003-272).

From these unpromising beginnings, Simon rises through sheer ability and force of will. He earns his knighthood at Shrewsbury, distinguishes himself in the King's service, and is rewarded with a barony of his own. The House of Beauvallet is thus the tangible proof of the novel's central thesis: that merit can overcome birth, and that a man who is willing to fight for his place in the world may ultimately claim it.

> "I am come to see my son, Sir Simon of Beauvallet." — [004-090]

When Fulk speaks these words after Simon's knighting, he publicly acknowledges the bond that birth and service have created between them. But Simon has already outgrown the role of "Fulk's son" — he is now a knight and soon to be a baron in his own right, the founder of a house that will owe nothing to Montlice.

### Governance and Reform

The second role of the House of Beauvallet in the story is to demonstrate Simon's capacity for governance. The novel devotes considerable attention to the transformation of Fair Pastures under Simon's rule. The old order is swept away; justice is imposed; the men of the domain learn to live under a master who expects obedience and rewards industry.

> "Malefactors were brought to justice, and in a surprisingly short time the men of Beauvallet settled down under the new regime." — [010-019]

This interlude of governance establishes Simon as more than a warrior. He is an administrator, a judge, a builder of order. The skills he displayed on the battlefield — decisiveness, clarity of purpose, intolerance of incompetence — prove equally effective in the management of an estate. The House of Beauvallet is not merely a title but a functioning political entity, and Simon proves himself capable of running it.

### A Strategic Asset

The King's grant of Beauvallet is not charity but strategy. Henry places a capable and loyal lord on lands that were formerly held by a rebel, ensuring that the region will remain peaceful and that the Crown's authority will be respected. "I have little fear of risings near Beauvallet now" (007-101) — the King speaks as a ruler who understands that strong local lords are the foundation of royal power.

> "My Lord of Beauvallet, forsooth! Little did I think to see thee raised so high!" — [008-080]

The words capture the astonishment of those who knew Simon as a landless squire and now see him riding as a baron through the very lands that are now his own. The House of Beauvallet, in its rapid rise from nothing, becomes a symbol of [[king-henry-v|King Henry]]'s meritocratic kingship.

## Structure

The House of Beauvallet follows the standard structure of a 15th-century English barony. At its head stands Simon, the baron, holding his lands directly from the King. Beneath him are the officers of his household: the steward who manages the estate's day-to-day affairs, the bailiffs who oversee the manors, and the men-at-arms who form the military backbone of his retinue.

The castle of Beauvallet — formerly the castle of Fair Pastures — serves as the administrative and military center of the domain. It is from here that Simon governs, judges, and prepares for the campaigns that will occupy the latter part of the novel. The castle is described as standing in a fertile valley, its lands well-watered and productive — a fitting seat for a house whose name means "beautiful valley."

The household also includes a domestic staff: cooks, varlets, grooms, and pages — the infrastructure of daily life in a medieval noble residence. These servants are mentioned in passing but contribute to the novel's sense of a lived-in world in which noble houses are not abstractions but functioning communities.
