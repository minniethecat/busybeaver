# Montlice

**Montlice** is the baronial castle and seat of [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]], a "giant of a man, deep-chested and big-limbed" (001-043), situated in the Cambridgeshire region of eastern England. It is the place where [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] came as a nameless boy of fifteen and where he was forged into the soldier and leader who would become the Iron Lord. For three formative years, Montlice was Simon's home, and its great hall witnessed his transformation from a proud, untamed youth into a knight and baron.

## Geography and Setting

Montlice lies in the eastern counties of England, near [[cambridge|Cambridge]]. Its location places it within reach of the old Roman road that runs south-west toward [[london|London]] (006-029), making it connected to the political heart of the Lancastrian kingdom while remaining sufficiently remote to serve as a self-contained baronial domain. The castle itself is approached by a winding slope leading up to a great door (001-017), surrounded by the lands and villages from which Fulk of Montlice draws his men and resources.

The castle is a working stronghold, not merely a noble residence. When Simon rode through its lands on his return from London, "all along the road men came out to cheer him, and women dropped him shy curtseys" (008-003). This landscape of fields, villages, and dependent folk forms the feudal backdrop against which Simon's early manhood unfolds.

### The Great Hall

The heart of Montlice is its great hall, described as a chamber where Fulk sits "at his ease" after supper, with "long trestles already cleared" and a single lamp burning (001-043). The hall is hung with "paintings, biblical and historical" (001-043), suggesting a cultured household despite Fulk's rough exterior. It is here that Simon first presented himself before Fulk, and here that the great lord would later receive news of wars, plots, and his ward's extraordinary rise.

### The Approach and Walls

The castle is accessed by a drawbridge and defended by men-at-arms. When Simon returned after his London mission and knighthood, he and his companions "arrived at the drawbridge and went over, saluted by some half-dozen men-at-arms, who one and all gave Simon welcome" (008-006). The walls and battlements of Montlice, though not described in exhaustive detail, clearly constituted a defensible fortification capable of housing a lord's retinue and resisting attack.

## Simon's Arrival at Montlice

The first chapter of the novel is titled "How He Came to Fulk of Montlice" (001-002), establishing the castle as the destination of Simon's youthful journey. "So at length he came to Montlice, which was his goal" (001-007). Simon arrived on foot, a boy of fifteen, having walked alone across England seeking service with a great lord.

At the castle gate, Simon met Alan of Montlice, Fulk's son. "I am Alan of Montlice," the youth said. "What want you of me?" (001-029). Alan, initially skeptical, "hesitated" (001-032) before "standing aside for the young Montlice to pass through the doorway" (001-034) — a gesture of acceptance that presaged the deep bond that would form between the two young men.

When Simon came before Fulk, the great lord was amused by the boy's temerity. "My Lord of Montlice laughed" (001-049), and when Simon made his bold request to enter Fulk's service, "Montlice flung back his head and laughed" again (001-052). This laughter, far from dismissive, marked the beginning of a profound mutual respect.

> Montlice snapped his fingers, jeering. (001-070)

## The Montlice Household

### Fulk of Montlice

[[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]] was a warrior lord of the old stamp — loud, fierce, but fundamentally just. He met Simon's pride with mockery and provocation, yet was also capable of deep feeling. When he looked at Simon's golden ring — the sole token of the boy's unknown parentage — he studied it with keen interest (001-102), sensing there was more to this youth than met the eye.

> "Here's a philosophy!" Montlice became aware of his son, still kneeling beside Simon. (001-105)

> "I am no easy master," Montlice warned him. (001-127)

The years proved this true — Fulk's rages were legendary, and he could be as stubborn as Simon himself. Yet when Simon proved himself, Fulk responded with approval: "Montlice smote him on the shoulder" (001-135). The phrase "Thus it was that Simon came to Fulk of Montlice" (001-137) closes the first chapter with a sense of destined belonging.

### Alan of Montlice

Alan, Fulk's son and heir, became Simon's closest friend. From their first meeting at the castle gate, through years of companionship, to their shared campaigns in France, Alan was the gentler counterpoint to Simon's iron resolve. King Henry V called his three companions "my Soldier, my Knight, and my Poet" (012-021), and Alan was the Poet — a dreamer unfit for war yet drawn to it by loyalty.

Alan's lighthearted question to Simon — "Couldst thou leave Montlice today without one pang of regret?" (002-041) — foreshadowed the eventual parting when Simon left to rule his own barony. Yet their bond, forged within Montlice's walls, endured across distance and years.

## Montlice as a Military Household

Montlice was not merely a noble residence but a working military establishment. Simon "was decked out in Montlice red and gold" (002-003), the household colours that identified him as Fulk's man. The castle maintained a company of men-at-arms and archers, and when state affairs demanded, Fulk would lead his following to war.

A breathless messenger reached the castle bringing news that would alter everything:

> "State affairs first affected Montlice" early in the month, when a courier arrived at the castle, "covered with dust, dropping with weariness." (003-004, 003-005)

News of the Percy rebellion and the coming campaign at Shrewsbury had reached the castle, stirring the household to action.

"Three days later Fulk left Montlice with his following" (003-081), marching to join the King's forces. At the Battle of Shrewsbury, Fulk fell wounded, and "not twenty-four hours before Montlice, leading his men in place of his fallen lord" (003-083), Simon took command of the Montlice contingent. This moment — Simon leading Montlice's men into battle — was a critical step in his rise.

A wounded soldier, asked if he came "with Montlice," replied in the affirmative (003-125), testifying to the loyalty the castle's name inspired. In the thick of battle, "Montlice and Malvallet galloped forward together" (003-187), the two houses united in the King's cause.

## The Return to Montlice

Chapter 8, titled "How He Returned to Montlice," recounts Simon's homecoming after his London mission and knighthood. "A week later, Charles of Granmere and Simon of Beauvallet rode through Montlice towards the castle, their squires behind them" (008-003). The welcome Simon received demonstrated the regard in which the castle's people held him.

Fulk's greeting was characteristically explosive:

> "Hey, Simon lad! Hey, thou rascally, turbulent, naughty knave! How darest thou stay away all these weeks! Hast no regard for me at all, cub? Praise be to God, no harm has come to thee! Holy Virgin, I would they had clapped thee up for a mad rogue! I might have known thou'dst return to enrage me further, small thanks to thee for doing it!" (008-009)

Despite his bluster, Fulk "discarded his stick and caught Simon in a large embrace, kissing him loudly on both cheeks" (008-009). The scene exemplifies the unique dynamic of the Montlice household — outwardly rough, inwardly deeply affectionate.

It was here, at Montlice, that Simon's elevation was first proclaimed. Roger, Simon's squire, burst out with "My Lord of Beauvallet, sir" (008-058), and Fulk's astonishment — "Lord of Beauvallet, Lord of Beauvallet! What means the boy?" (008-061) — gave way to the news that "the King made Simon Baron of Beauvallet, and gave him a land called Fair Pastures" (008-063).

### Fulk's Farewell

The grant of Beauvallet meant Simon must leave Montlice. Fulk, though proud, felt the loss keenly: "It is great news, lad, and I am glad for thy sake. But it means that I must lose thee, and I like it not" (008-068). He asked: "who's to take thy place here, my lion-cub?" (008-070). Simon's answer — "Alan is of an age now, my lord" (008-071) — acknowledged that the Montlice legacy would pass to Fulk's true son, even as the foster-son rode out to build his own.

## Convalescence and Counsel

After the Shrewsbury campaign, Fulk convalesced at Montlice. When Malvallet arrived, the scene was tense — "Malvallet came to Montlice" (004-082), and "One of Montlice's varlets, astonished at his coming, ran to warn my lord" (004-083). The visit concerned Geoffrey's parentage and Simon's place in the Malvallet inheritance, setting the stage for Simon's eventual choice to forge his own path rather than claim what was not fully his.

Leeches attended Fulk through his recovery: "He shot an upward glance at Montlice. Fulk saw the glance and laughed" (004-024). The laughter, so characteristic of Montlice, bound together moments of pain and healing alike.

Later, when Simon was established at Beauvallet, "Montlice rode over to see Simon" (010-022), demonstrating the enduring connection between the two households. Simon "rode next day to Montlice to take counsel of Fulk" (010-140) when difficult decisions faced him, showing that even as a baron in his own right, he still valued the wisdom of the man who had given him his first chance.

## The Montlice Legacy

Montlice represents more than a physical location in Simon's story. It is the forge in which his character was tempered. "Without the years he had been at Montlice" Simon might never have possessed "the iron muscle" and "the calmness of temper" that defined him (002-006, 003-194). The castle's name — though Simon bore it only by adoption — became woven into the identity of one of England's greatest soldiers.

In the later Normandy campaign, when Fulk himself arrived in France, he came "roaring" (029-046), still the same lord of Montlice despite age and gout. And when the King appointed Simon as Warden of Normandy, he had "the men of Beauvallet and some of Montlice behind him" (031-024), the two households now marching as one.

Alan, who remained at Montlice after Simon's departure (010-115), would eventually inherit the castle and its lordship. The bond between Montlice and Beauvallet, forged in Simon's youth, continued through Alan's friendship and Fulk's enduring paternal pride.
