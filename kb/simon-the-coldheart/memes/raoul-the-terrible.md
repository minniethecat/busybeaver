## Character Overview

Raoul the Terrible is a renegade nobleman and robber-baron whose lawless territory lies across the route from Montlice to Turincel. Described as a petty tyrant who rules through fear and casual cruelty, he epitomizes the breakdown of feudal order that Simon of Beauvallet is repeatedly called upon to confront. Unlike the political antagonists Simon faces — rebellious barons, French garrisons — Raoul is a purely personal evil: a predator who takes what he wants without pretense of legal or moral justification.

His lands are notorious for their lawlessness. As one character observes, "All goes to ruin while Raoul feeds his pleasure" (022-004). His epithet "the Terrible" is explicitly earned through reputation — his name alone causes travellers to fear, and even seasoned soldiers blanch at the prospect of crossing his domain (021-118).

## Role in the Narrative

### Capture of the Lady Margaret

Raoul enters the story when the Lady Margaret of Belrémy, fleeing from Simon after her failed assassination attempt, attempts to cross his territory en route to her cousin at Turincel. Her party is intercepted by Raoul's men in a brutal hunting-style ambush.

> "Raoul! Raoul! The devil hunts!" (022-020)

The capture scene establishes Raoul's character through action rather than exposition. When a captured member of Margaret's party defies him, Raoul's response is chilling in its casualness:

> "Truss him," Raoul purred, and let his small, heavy-lidded eyes rest on the man dispassionately. (022-023)

Raoul is not a raging berserker but a predator who enjoys the slow exercise of power. His dialogue is marked by softness — he "purrs," he murmurs, he smiles — creating a deliberate contrast with the violence he commands. When he interrogates Margaret, the menace is in his gentleness:

> "No peasant wench thou," Raoul went on. "So what do ye in my lands, lady?" (022-076)

### The Demand for Ransom

Once Raoul learns his captive's identity — Margaret of Belrémy — his demeanor shifts from casual cruelty to calculated greed. His demand for ransom sets the terms for the novel's climactic sequence. Unlike a conventional nobleman who might treat a high-born captive with some deference, Raoul treats Margaret as a commodity:

> "I bargain not," Raoul smiled. "Thou wilt tell." (022-083)

When her identity is formally revealed — "the Lady Margaret of Belrémy, Lord Raoul" (022-093) — Raoul's smile only widens, recognizing the value of his prize.

### Confrontation with Simon

News of Margaret's capture reaches Simon and Geoffrey indirectly. A witness reports hearing the name "Raoul the Terrible" spoken in panic (023-128). Geoffrey's immediate recognition — "Raoul!" (023-129) — underscores the man's infamous reputation. Simon's pursuit of Raoul becomes the action that drives the novel's final movement, transforming what began as a punitive expedition against a rebellious castle into a rescue mission with deeply personal stakes.

## Character Analysis

### Cruelty as Performance

Raoul's cruelty is performative as much as practical. His soft-spoken manner, his "gentle smile" while threatening captives, his theatrical displays of power — all suggest a man who derives aesthetic pleasure from domination. He does not simply kill or imprison; he stages encounters designed to maximize the psychological distress of his victims.

> Raoul smiled gently upon his prisoners. (022-070)

This gentleness in the midst of threat is a hallmark of Raoul's characterization. He does not need to shout because his reputation precedes him; his softness is itself a threat, implying a man so secure in his power that he need not demonstrate it loudly.

### The Failure of Feudal Order

Raoul represents a systemic failure in the feudal order. His very existence — a lord who preys upon travellers, who captures noblewomen for ransom, who "feeds his pleasure" while his lands fall into ruin — is an indictment of the decentralized power structure that allows such men to operate with impunity. Simon's campaign against Raoul is thus both a personal quest (to rescue Margaret) and a political act (to restore order to a lawless region).

His isolated stronghold, described in terms that emphasize its grim and foreboding nature, is the physical manifestation of his moral character: a fortress built not to protect the realm but to enable predation.

## Historical Context

Raoul belongs to a recognizable type in medieval history and literature: the robber-baron who exploits weak central authority to establish a personal fiefdom of terror. The early 15th century, particularly in the contested regions of France, saw numerous such figures emerge in the gaps between royal jurisdictions. Raoul's characterization draws on this historical reality while heightening it for dramatic effect.
