## Character Overview

Simon of Beauvallet is [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] eponymous protagonist, introduced as a fourteen-year-old wanderer walking from Bedford into Cambridge with nothing but a knapsack, a quarterstaff, and a fierce determination. His defining trait is coldness — not cruelty, but an iron self-discipline that governs his every action and masks deeper passions. Over [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] course [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] novel, he rises from squire to [[knighthood|knight]] to territorial lord, his ascent driven by merit ra[[raoul-the-terrible|the]]r than birth.

> He came walking from Bedford into Cambridge one morning when the sun was still young and the dew scarce gone from the grass. (001-003)

## Biography

### Origins and Arrival at Montlice

Simon arrives at [[montlice|Montlice]] [[stronghold|castle]] at age fourteen, carrying his worldly possessions in an old knapsack (001-003). When challenged by [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] guard, he declares simply: "I seek [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] lion" — referring to [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]], [[fulk-of-montlice|Earl of Montlice]] — and walks forward across [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] drawbridge without hesitation (001-010). He asks to serve as [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]]'s squire: "I would carry your lance and squire you, sir" (001-069).

> "I seek the lion," he said, and walked calmly forward to the drawbridge. (001-010)

His ambition is stated plainly to Lord Fulk: "I hope one day to be a man, my lord, even as you" (001-051). And when Fulk questions whether he knows how hard it is to enter his service, Simon responds: "Ay, so is it. Ye like the harder task, babe?" — and Simon answers coolly: "I do, my lord" (001-121/001-122).

## Character Analysis

Simon represents a distinctive type [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] hero — nei[[raoul-the-terrible|the]]r [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] hot-blooded warrior (like [[hotspur|Hotspur]]) nor [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] courtly [[knighthood|knight]] [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] romance, but a cold, calculating figure whose power lies in discipline and restraint. His coldness is not absence [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] feeling but mastery over it. Throughout [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] novel, he refuses honours he has not earned, power he has not won, and wealth he has not taken by his own hand. This relentless self-reliance makes him respected by kings and feared by enemies.

His philosophy is perhaps best captured in his response to Fulk's surprise that he has never loved:

> "I have never loved, nor ever will," Simon answered. "For I want some years to full manhood. And I am happy as I am." (002-042)

And his response to those who would persuade him to marry: "I will take no woman to wife" (010-097) — a declaration that proves prophetic until the events of the Belrémy campaign alter the course of his emotional life.
