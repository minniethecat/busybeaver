# Plot

## The Baron of Crowburg's Conspiracy

[[the-chevalier|The]] plot was uncovered when [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] intercepted a letter during [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[rescue-of-the-damsel|Rescue of the Damsel]]:

> The letter was couched in fair terms, and it assured my lord the Baron of Crowburg, faithful adherent to the true king, Richard by the Grace of God, lately escaped into Scotland, that despite the lying reports of his death, set about by the usurper ... (005-075)

This letter reveals [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] structure [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] a Ricardian plot: a network [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] loyalists who refused to accept [[king-henry-v|Henry V]]'s legitimacy, maintaining that [[king-henry-v|Richard II]] had escaped to [[london|Scotland]] and that reports [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] his death were Lancastrian propaganda. [[the-chevalier|The]] Baron [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] Crowburg was one node in this network; [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] letter's courier was ano[[raoul-the-terrible|the]]r.

## The Nature of Medieval Conspiracy

[[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s handling [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] plot demonstrates both his instinct for statecraft and his directness. When he interrogated [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] prisoner at Gountray, "he had surprised a pretty plot, and his eyes kindled a little" (005-076). [[the-chevalier|The]] phrase "pretty plot" captures [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] mix [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] danger and intellectual satisfaction in uncovering such schemes.

[[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]] showed a lord's wariness [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] court intrigue:

> "Thou must poke and pry into plots and other such treasonable matters? Let well alone, boy." (006-016)

[[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk]]'s instinct was to avoid entanglement; [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s was to pursue [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] matter to its conclusion. This tension — between [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] provincial lord's desire for quiet and [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] active servant's duty to [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] Crown — reflects [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] practical realities [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] medieval political life.

## At the Royal Court

When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] brought [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] matter to [[london|Westminster]], [[king-henry-v|the King]] took it seriously. A council was convened:

> As before, he was conducted to the King's closet, but this time he found some six or seven gentlemen of the Council there beside the King. (007-036)

Throughout [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] novel, plots and counter-plots provide narrative momentum.
