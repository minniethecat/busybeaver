# London

**London** was the capital of England and the seat of the Lancastrian monarchy under [[king-henry-v|King Henry V]]. In the novel, London serves as the political centre to which [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] rode to deliver his treasonous prisoner, where he was received and thanked by the King, and where he petitioned for — and received — the barony that became [[house-of-beauvallet|Beauvallet]]. London functions less as a described physical setting than as the symbol and mechanism of legitimate royal authority.

## The Ride to London

### Departure from Saltpetres

After the [[rescue-of-the-damsel|Rescue of the Damsel]], Simon wrote to Fulk of Montlice from the tavern of the Ox at Saltpetres, announcing his intention: "I am bound for London, having taken a man prisoner here who bears traitorous dispatches concerning the late King" (005-100). He sent his squire Roger back to Montlice with instructions to bring reinforcements and a fresh horse for the journey:

> "Look ye, Roger, thou must ride back to Montlice at once, and deliver this into my lord's own hands. Then change thy horse for another — Sultan or Rover — and bring with thee my mare, Fleet-foot. Gregory will come back with thee, and thou shalt take my horse Cedric to Montlice again. We ride to London on the morrow." (005-104)

### The Roman Road

The journey followed the old Roman road, the ancient artery connecting the provinces to the capital:

> "Thence they dined and rested, and again set off down the old Roman road to London. They lay that night at a village some fifteen miles from their destination, and were early afoot on the following morn." (006-029)

Such roads were the arteries of medieval English communication. The party included Simon, his squire Roger, Gregory of the Mount with six men-at-arms sent by Fulk, and the prisoner himself. The urgency of the mission was such that Simon would not wait for escort — he set out with only Roger, and the rest caught up by forced ride.

### Purpose of the Journey

Simon's purpose was to transfer the captured conspirator from personal custody into royal justice. Fulk's instructions were precise:

> "Ye will deliver him to whoever shall come from London with orders from the King, or from me." (006-008)

The delivery to London was essential because the prisoner carried dispatches relating to the Serle conspiracy — a plot to place a counterfeit Richard II on the Scottish throne and foment rebellion against the Lancastrian regime. The conspiracy implicated figures across England, and only the Crown had the authority and apparatus to investigate it fully.

## The City and Its Geography

### The Strand

Simon and his party approached London from the west, riding "down the Strand, past the greater palaces till they came upon one that was less magnificent, and bore the name of Granmere Hall" (006-041). The Strand, which then ran alongside the Thames, was home to the town palaces of the nobility, their river gates giving direct access to the waterway. This juxtaposition — greater palaces passed by, a more modest hall as destination — reflected the multi-layered geography of power in the medieval capital.

### Granmere Hall

[[charles-of-granmere|Charles of Granmere]], Fulk's cousin, maintained Granmere Hall as his London residence. Simon carried a letter of introduction from Fulk:

> "If thou stand in need of a friend, or a lodging, repair to my cousin, Charles of Granmere, whose house is called Granmere Hall, situate in the Strand, which is in London, and show him this letter. He will give thee aid." (006-018)

Granmere proved an invaluable host, introducing Simon to the gentlemen of the court during the fortnight of waiting for the King's return from the Welsh Marches. The hall provided a base from which Simon could observe the political currents of the capital.

### The Palace of Westminster

The royal palace — where Simon was twice received by the King — was the centre of Lancastrian government. Simon was "conducted to the King's closet" (007-036) for his second audience, an intimate space where Henry met with his Council of "some six or seven gentlemen" (007-036). The King's closet, as opposed to the great hall, signalled the confidential and strategic nature of their discussion.

## The King's Absence

When Simon arrived in London, the King was not present. Henry, "having wintered in London, was now back on the Marches" (006-031), dealing with the persistent threat on the Welsh border. This detail illustrates the peripatetic nature of medieval kingship — the monarch moved constantly between his palaces and the frontiers of his realm, and petitioners might wait weeks for an audience.

The fortnight of waiting was not wasted. Granmere ensured that Simon was presented to many guests:

> "Much time he spent in exploring the city, and my Lord of Granmere contrived to keep him occupied by inviting many guests to his house, to all of whom he presented Simon. And if some of these gentlemen did not like the silent, direct young man whom they met, at least they were not in danger of easily forgetting his strangely forceful personality." (007-003)

Simon's refusal to write to Fulk during this period — "He knows me too well to think that... What I set out to do, I do" (007-009, 007-018) — reveals both his self-containment and his assumption that his success was a foregone conclusion.

## The King's Reception

### The First Audience

Simon's first meeting with Henry V established the climate of mutual respect that would characterize their relationship. The King examined the treasonable documents with the Duke of York:

> "A long silence fell upon the closet while the King and the Duke slit them open one after the other and read their contents." (006-072)

### The Second Audience

The second audience, held after the investigation was complete, was the scene of the King's formal thanks. Henry acknowledged the significance of what Simon had uncovered:

> "The messenger whom ye waylaid came safely to London, but methinks he was something stiff of limb, and sore in every part of his worthless carcase." (007-043)

The King's quizzical look and Simon's short laugh (007-043) marked the moment of rapport between monarch and subject. Henry further observed:

> "It was not chance alone which brought you safe to London, and which has brought your prisoner, too." (007-058)

This remark — suggesting providence at work in Simon's safe arrival — elevates London beyond mere geography to a place where destiny manifests.

### The Grant of Beauvallet

It was in London, at the Palace of Westminster, that Simon made his boldest petition: the grant of Fair Pastures. Standing before the King and Council, Simon described the derelict estate and asked for it directly:

> "Sire, in Cambridge, to the south and east of Montlice, is a fair barony of little size, but, as I judge, of passing great wealth. It is named Fair Pastures... Give this land to me, sire, and I will bring law and order into it, and hold it as mine own, myself owning allegiance to you!" (007-087)

The King's response — "Fair Pastures — Beau Vallet. Ay, that is a good omen. Ye shall have that land, Sir Simon, and ye shall call it Beauvallet and be yourself Lord of Beauvallet" (007-095) — was made in London, and the deed of gift was dispatched to Granmere Hall. London was thus the birthplace, in legal terms, of House of Beauvallet.

## Political Geography

### London as the Seat of Legitimate Authority

London in the novel is not described in extensive physical detail but functions as a symbol of legitimate authority. The prisoner's delivery to London represents the channeling of provincial justice into royal jurisdiction. The capital is the destination that validates Simon's actions, transforming a local rescue into an act of service to the Crown.

The delivery of the conspirator was the culmination of Simon's independent initiative, and its success brought him into the King's direct notice. As Simon told Granmere's steward: "The prisoner is brought safe to London, that is all, sir. It is our duty to do no less" (006-095).

### Contrast with the Norman Periphery

The contrast between London and the Norman strongholds — [[belremy|Belrémy]], [[turincel|Turincel]], [[montlice|Montlice]] — maps the political geography of the novel: the settled English centre versus the contested Norman periphery. London is the fixed point from which royal authority emanates outward, while the castles of Normandy represent the unstable frontier where that authority must be imposed by force.

This contrast is underscored when Granmere tells Fulk, "Methinks they could hear thy voice in London" (008-025) — a jest that simultaneously acknowledges Fulk's roaring presence and the distance between the provincial castle and the metropolitan centre.

### Westminster and the Strand

The two poles of the novel's London are Westminster (the royal palace, seat of authority) and the Strand (the noble quarter, seat of social connection). Simon moved between them — staying at Granmere Hall on the Strand, presenting himself at Westminster to the King — and this movement traced the path from social introduction to royal favour that structured Lancastrian political advancement.

## London as a Moral and Narrative Centre

The London sequence serves several narrative functions beyond its geographical role. It establishes Simon in the King's eyes, setting the stage for his later rise. It introduces the character of Granmere, who would become a recurring ally. It demonstrates Simon's competence beyond the battlefield — his ability to navigate the political world of the court — and it reveals the King's wisdom in recognizing and rewarding merit regardless of birth.

More subtly, the London chapters establish that Simon's coldness is not a defect but a form of self-sufficiency that the King recognizes and values. When Granmere questioned Simon about whether he would persist in an impossible task, Simon replied: "That were the action of a fool, my lord, and I do not think I am one" (007-026). Granmere's response — "By God, I believe that thou wouldst!" (007-034) — confirmed that London's political elite saw in Simon what Fulk had seen: a man of extraordinary capability.
