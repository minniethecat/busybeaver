## Definition

An oath in [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] medieval world [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] novel is a binding verbal contract, sworn before witnesses or before God, that creates obligations no honourable person may breach. It is simultaneously a legal instrument (binding parties to specific terms), a social bond (cementing relationships [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[allegiance|fealty]] and service), and a test [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] character (as oath-breaking marks one as beyond [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] pale [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[knighthood|knight]]ly society). [[the-chevalier|The]] novel deploys oaths at critical junctures to bind characters to courses [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] action from which [[raoul-the-terrible|the]]y cannot honourably retreat.

## In the Novel

[[the-chevalier|The]] oath [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] submission extracted from [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[the-chevalier|Chevalier]] [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[belremy|Belrémy]] marks a pivotal moment in [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s campaign. After [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] fall [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[stronghold|castle]], [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[the-chevalier|Chevalier]] swears an oath [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] submission — yet [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s pragmatic caution is evident in his observation: "[[the-chevalier|The Chevalier]] hath sworn an oath [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] submission, but I trust him not" (016-060). This skepticism toward sworn words from a defeated enemy reveals [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s characteristic blend [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] honour and hardheadedness — [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] oath is necessary as a formal instrument, but insufficient as a guarantee.

[[the-chevalier|The]] most dramatic oath sequence involves [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[the-lady-margaret|Lady Margaret]]. When she declares "You have mine oath" in a matter concerning her cousin, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] seizes upon [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] formal pledge immediately: "Thus shall I also have thine oath" (015-167, 015-168). [[the-chevalier|The]] exchange is not merely a negotiation but a ritual — [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] giving and receiving [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] oaths establishes mutual obligation, transforming antagonists into parties bound by a shared commitment.

Oaths also serve as outlets for strong emotion. [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] lets fly a great oath upon receiving news, and claps his hand to his sword — [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] oath here functioning as a verbal release [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] tension before action (015-019). Elsewhere, a character rolls out a huge oath, which seemed slightly to relieve him — confirming [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] almost physical pressure that oaths can discharge (008-065).

[[the-chevalier|The]] novel even attributes oath-like agency to inanimate forces. Hunger, personified, is said to have made solemn oath unto itself that it would devour [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] land — an image that captures how deeply [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] language [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] oath-taking permeates [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] novel's conceptual world (018-033).

## Historical Background

In 15th-century England, oaths were central to [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] feudal system. [[the-chevalier|The]] oath [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[allegiance|fealty]] bound vassal to lord; [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] coronation oath bound king to realm; judicial oaths determined legal truth. Oath-breaking (perjury) carried both legal penalties and social death — a [[knighthood|knight]] who broke his sworn word forfeited his honour, which was tantamount to forfeiting his identity. [[the-chevalier|The]] period's religious culture reinforced [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] gravity [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] oaths: to swear was to call God as witness, and to break an oath was to imperil one's soul.
