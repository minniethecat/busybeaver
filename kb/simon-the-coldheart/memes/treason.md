Treason — the betrayal of one's sworn lord or sovereign — was the gravest political crime in medieval law, punishable by death. In the novel, the spectre of treason hangs over the political landscape, shaping alliances and determining fates.

## In the Novel

The novel's most notable act of treachery occurs when [[fernand-de-turincel|Fernand de Turincel]], from whom [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]] sought aid against the English, proves a traitor to her cause. His betrayal leaves Margaret stunned: "Fernand turned traitor… What shall I do? What can I do?" (021-074). This personal act of treason leaves her isolated and desperate, forcing her to continue her flight through hostile territory.

The King's Council meeting at [[london|Westminster]] also involves the discovery of a treasonable plot against the Crown (006-044–047), demonstrating that treason threatens authority at the highest levels. [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s role in revealing this plot cements his reputation as a loyal servant of the King.

Treason is the implied charge behind many political threats throughout the novel. In a world structured by oaths of fealty and allegiance, any breach of sworn loyalty carries the stain of treason — a crime that threatens not just power but the moral order of feudal society.

## Historical Background

Under medieval English law, treason was defined by the Statute of Treasons (1351). High treason encompassed plotting the king's death, levying war against the king, or adhering to the king's enemies. The 15th century saw frequent accusations of treason as the Houses of Lancaster and York contended for the throne, and as English-held territories in France faced constant threats of rebellion.
