## Overview

Belrémy is a fortified castle and town in Normandy that serves as one of the central settings in the novel. Standing upon a slight incline, it is the seat of [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]], Countess of Belrémy, a fierce and proud noblewoman who rules the domain in her own right. In the autumn of 1417, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] is dispatched by King [[henry-v-of-england|Henry V]] to subdue the stronghold, a task that proves far more demanding than the parallel siege of Falaise.

> "Belrémy stood upon a slight incline, ..." — 013-004

The capture of Belrémy becomes the defining military achievement of Simon's early career, testing not only his tactical ingenuity but also his capacity for mercy and his complex relationship with its formidable ruler.

## Geography and Fortifications

### Location and Natural Defences

Belrémy is situated on gently rising ground in Normandy, its position chosen to command the surrounding countryside. The slight incline upon which it stands gives it a natural defensive advantage against any approaching force.

> "Belrémy stood upon a slight incline..." — 013-004

The town is enclosed by formidable walls, their strength so exceptional that they provoke dismay even among Simon's seasoned soldiers. When the English bombardment begins, the defenders find cause for grim confidence.

> "...our bombardment? The walls of Belrémy seem made of granite! They laugh at our cannon!" — 013-015

### The Castle and Town

Belrémy comprises both a fortified town and an inner castle, the latter serving as the residence and seat of power for its ruler. The castle houses a great hall where [[the-lady-margaret|the Lady Margaret]] receives emissaries, seated upon a throne-like chair. The streets of the town, through which the English troops move after the capture, are lined with the dwellings of townsfolk and tradespeople. Separate administrative structures also exist, including a guardroom where prisoners may be held.

> "Through the streets of Belrémy, past staring townsfolk and soldiers..." — 016-003

## The Siege of Belrémy (1417)

### Simon's Commission

In 1417, as part of King Henry V's campaign in Normandy, Simon of Beauvallet is assigned the task of taking Belrémy. The assignment is made deliberately by the King, who sees in Simon a commander capable of achieving what others might find impossible.

> "...Caesar, Simon," he said. "To Belrémy wilt thou go, and where I saw..." — 012-066

Simon's comrade [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]] volunteers to accompany him, and the two set out together for the campaign. The King's council acknowledges the difficulty of the task: Falaise is to be taken by another commander, while Simon is given the harder prize.

> "Belrémy is the harder task, sire, saving your presence..." — 012-103

### The Encampment and Bombardment

Simon appears before Belrémy in October 1417 with an army of fifteen hundred men. The chapter titled "How He Encamped Before Belrémy" (013-002) details the initial investment of the town. Despite a sustained bombardment, the walls prove remarkably resistant, and the besiegers are forced to reconsider their tactics. Simon's men grow restless as the siege drags on through the autumn and into winter.

> "So would we all. Belrémy will be hell enough, God wot!" — 013-072

Alan, one of Simon's lieutenants, records Simon's determination, noting that Simon has given his word that they shall feast within Belrémy by Christmas Day.

> "...gave me his word we shall feast in Belrémy on Christmas Day, Alan." — 013-045

### Mining Operations

Frustrated by the failure of direct bombardment, Simon turns to mining — digging beneath the walls to undermine them. The operation is slow and dangerous, and the men are uncertain whether it will succeed. Simon himself maintains an outward calm, but the strain is evident.

> "...Mayhap we are to enter Belrémy through this mine he digs so patiently." — 013-064

The miners work under cover of night, and the goal is clear: to enter Belrémy by Christmastide, fulfilling Simon's promised timeline.

> "That we enter Belrémy by Christmastide. What Simon wills, he does." — 013-069

### The Lady Margaret's Defence

The Lady Margaret of Belrémy refuses all calls for surrender. When Simon sends a herald demanding the town's capitulation, her response is defiant and unambiguous: he shall enter Belrémy only over her dead body. Her leadership stiffens the resolve of the garrison, and no help comes to relieve them — yet they do not yield.

> "...she returned answer, that he should enter Belrémy over her dead body." — 013-010

The absence of external relief is noted grimly as the siege wears on. The defenders of Belrémy stand alone, sustained only by their walls and their lady's iron will.

> "...No help came to them." — 013-048

## The Storm and Capture

### The Plan of Attack

The chapter titled "How He Took Belrémy" (014-002) recounts the decisive assault. Simon, working from a rough plan of the town, devises a two-pronged attack involving a coordinated night assault. He makes a pact with Geoffrey of Malvallet: the two shall meet within the gates of Belrémy.

> "...meet me within the gates of Belrémy, Geoffrey. My hand on it." — 014-031

Simon issues a grim ultimatum: if the gates are flung open by force, Belrémy shall receive no quarter.

> "...thou hast flung open the gates, Belrémy shall have no quarter. That I swear." — 014-033

Before the assault begins, Simon and Geoffrey exchange a final word, each knowing the peril of what they are about to attempt.

> "We meet within Belrémy, Beauvallet. God be with you..." — 014-042

### The Assault

The English forces plunge into Belrémy in a coordinated assault. Malvallet leads one column, while Simon commands the other. The attack achieves surprise and the garrison is overwhelmed. Simon's soldiers sweep through the town, and the defenders are scattered.

> "Plunging into Belrémy came the English, Malvallet at their head..." — 014-122

In the aftermath, Simon reminds his men of their duty, cautioning them against wanton destruction now that the town is under his command.

> "Remember, ye are soldiers of Belrémy," he reminded them. "Spread abroad no disorder..." — 014-102

### Aftermath and Occupation

Following the capture, Simon devotes himself to restoring order. He imposes discipline on the occupying force, negotiates with the Lady Margaret, and seeks to bring the shattered town back to a functioning state.

> "...had brought some semblance of order into Belrémy and had held a long parley..." — 015-003

From the Castle of Belrémy, Simon writes to the Lady Margaret, formally offering terms of surrender under the authority of Henry V. The letter is dated the twenty-first of December.

> "Written at my Castle of Belrémy this twenty-first day of December..." — 015-018

## The Lady Margaret and Belrémy

### The Countess in Her Domain

The Lady Margaret is inseparable from Belrémy. She is not merely its ruler but its embodiment — proud, defiant, and unyielding. Her authority within the castle is absolute, and even after its capture, she continues to exert a powerful influence over events.

> "...my sister is serving-maid to the Lady Margaret of Belrémy," she said daringly. — 020-073

Throughout the novel, characters refer to her as "Margaret of Belrémy," as though the place and the person are one and the same.

> "Ye know not Margaret of Belrémy, sir!" — 016-035

### A Castle Divided

Even after the military conquest, Belrémy is not wholly at peace. Simon acknowledges that the loyalty of the people remains with their former mistress, and the occupation is fragile.

> "...Belrémy is not wholly mine yet, though I hold it." — 016-047

The tension between the English occupiers and the native population simmers beneath the surface. Simon is warned that pushing too hard against the Lady Margaret could provoke a general uprising.

> "Ye would have all Belrémy about your ears, like hornets!" — 018-135

### Departure and Legacy

Later in the story, Simon departs Belrémy to report to King Henry, leaving the garrison under trusted command. The Lady Margaret herself eventually leaves the castle, though the question of whether she will ever return to rule hangs over the latter portion of the narrative. The memory of the siege and the fall of Belrémy becomes a cornerstone of Simon's reputation.

> "He left Belrémy at noon on the day of the Lady..." — 023-003

## Strategic Significance

### Belrémy in the Normandy Campaign

Belrémy occupies a strategic position in the broader context of Henry V's 1417 Normandy campaign. Its capture, alongside Falaise and other strongholds, consolidates English control over the region. Simon's success at Belrémy is noted with approval at the English court, where it is recalled as a model of siege warfare conducted with patience and resolve.

> "...we sat down before Belrémy until Christmastide, and lived on short rations..." — 029-035

The King himself takes an interest in the Lady Margaret of Belrémy, inquiring after her when Simon presents himself at court.

> "It is the Lady Margaret of Belrémy, sire." — 029-066

### Comparison with Falaise

Throughout the narrative, Belrémy is explicitly contrasted with Falaise, another Norman stronghold under siege at the same time. Falaise is held by the Sieur de Mauny, while Belrémy is held by the Lady Margaret. The commanders note the irony: a man holds one fortress, a woman the other, yet the woman's fortress proves the harder prize.

> "...Mauny holds Falaise, and the Lady Margaret rules Belrémy." — 012-106

Gilbert's remark that "Simon shall take Belrémy" while "Raoul shall take Falaise" (012-099) establishes the parallel assignments, and Simon's own wry observation underscores the perception of the task.

> "...a man holds Falaise, and a woman, Belrémy. I would sooner fight a man than a woman." — 012-110

## People of Belrémy

### Key Figures

- **[[the-lady-margaret|Lady Margaret]]**, Countess of Belrémy — the formidable ruler who defies the English siege and refuses to surrender her birthright.
- **[[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]]** — the English commander who captures Belrémy and later develops a complex bond with its lady.
- **[[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey of Malvallet]]** — Simon's steadfast companion and fellow commander during the siege and assault.
- **Alan** — one of Simon's trusted lieutenants who records Simon's determination to take the town by Christmas.

### The Garrison and Townsfolk

The soldiers of Belrémy — those who man its walls and fight its defence — are loyal to the Lady Margaret. After the capture, Simon addresses them directly, reminding them that they are now under English authority and must conduct themselves accordingly. The townsfolk, staring at the English troops as they march through the streets, represent the civilian population caught between the old regime and the new.
