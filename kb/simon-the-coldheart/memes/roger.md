# Roger

**Roger** (also referred to as Roger of Maitland) was squire to [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] during [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] years 1404–1405, serving as his close [[squire|attendant]] through [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[rescue-of-the-damsel|Rescue of the Damsel]], [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]], and [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] early [[campaign-in-normandy|Campaign in Normandy]].

## In the Service of Simon

Roger first appears in [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] company [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] in Chapter V, riding out from [[montlice|Montlice]] "in a gloomy mood" following a severe reprimand from his master (005-005). [[the-chevalier|The]] relationship between squire and [[knighthood|knight]] was defined by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s exacting standards and Roger's imperfect attempts to meet [[raoul-the-terrible|the]]m.

> Simon rode out one morning in the year 1404, bearing to the southeast. With him went Roger, his squire, in a gloomy mood, for he had fallen foul of Simon that very day and had received a severe reprimand, accompanied by a searching, flaming glance which he had learned to dread. (005-005)

Roger's physical condition [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]]ten reflected [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] strain [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] his service:

> He had worked him hard this week, and Roger's eyes were black-ringed from fatigue, his movements slow. (005-033)

Despite [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] rigours, Roger remained dutiful. When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]]fered him respite, he answered: "Yes, sir," "devoutly thankful" (005-035).

## The Rescue of the Damsel

Roger played a supporting role during [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[rescue-of-the-damsel|Rescue of the Damsel]] in [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] forest. When [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] sound [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] footsteps approached through [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] wood, it was Roger who came racing to his master's assistance (005-068). After [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] rescue, Roger attempted to comfort [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] weeping girl (005-076) and helped carry [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] unconscious abductor to [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] inn at [[maurice-of-gountray|Gountray]] (005-078).

[[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] entrusted Roger with [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] critical task [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] delivering a letter to [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]]:

> "Look ye, Roger, thou must ride back to Montlice at once, and deliver this into my lord's own hands." (005-104)

Roger's reaction captures his character precisely:

> Roger heaved a sullen, weary sigh. ... Roger flushed to the roots of his curly hair. ... Roger swallowed hard and sent a fleeting glance upwards. (005-108–116)

## The London Mission

By [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] following morning, Roger had returned with Gregory, [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s lieutenant, and six men, demonstrating his reliability in executing his master's orders (006-003). When [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] party arrived in [[london|London]], Roger was in "a twitter [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] excitement" and implored [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] to let him walk out after supper to see [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] town — a request [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] denied (006-030).

Later, when summoned to ride to [[london|Westminster]], Roger was "delighted at [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] prospect [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] seeing more [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] town" and "ran to do his bidding, wrea[[raoul-the-terrible|the]]d in smiles" (006-040).

## Later Service

During [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s assumption [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[barony|lordship]] at [[montlice|Montlice]], Roger continued in his duties. When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] sent him to summon [[maurice|Maurice]], Roger returned alone, having been unable to find him (009-120). When [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] prepared to ride out alone with [[alan-of-montlice|Alan]], Roger attempted to follow:

> Roger ran after him. "Sir, let me ride with you!" (009-132)

[[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s refusal — "Thou didst hear me, Roger?" (009-133) — and Roger's subsequent sigh (009-134) exemplify [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] dynamic [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]]ir relationship.

At [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] [[house-of-beauvallet|Beauvallet]] armoury, Roger was present during [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] selection [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s [[armour|gilded armour]], tasked with keeping young [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|Geoffrey]] from tumbling over [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s sword (011-028). His complaint about [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] press [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] eager [[squire|attendant]]s captures [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] squire's perspective: "[[the-chevalier|The]]y cluster about my lord so that [[raoul-the-terrible|the]]re is naught for us poor [[squire|squires]] to do" (011-034).

## Character

Roger is characterised throughout as loyal, long-suffering, somewhat timid, but ultimately dependable. His emotional transparency — sighing, flushing, swallowing nervously — provides a human counterpoint to [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s controlled demeanour. He represents [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] ordinary man serving extraordinary masters in [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] military world [[geoffrey-of-malvallet|of]] [[raoul-the-terrible|the]] early fifteenth century.
