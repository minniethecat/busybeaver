# Henry IV Bolingbroke

**Henry IV** (Henry Bolingbroke) was King of England from 1399 to 1413, the sovereign who seized the throne from [[richard-ii|Richard II]] and established the Lancastrian dynasty. He appears in the early chapters of the novel as the monarch whom [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] serves, granting the young knight an audience after his exploits at the [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]] and receiving intelligence of a conspiracy against his crown. In the world of the novel, Henry is the embodiment of established — if contested — royal authority, the power that Simon serves loyally and that his enemies seek to undermine through rebellion and plot.

## The Usurper King

### Rise to the Throne

Henry came to the throne by deposing his cousin [[richard-ii|Richard II]], an act that earned him the epithet "the Usurper" among his enemies and initiated the Lancastrian phase of the [[hundred-years-war|Hundred Years' War]] era. The novel opens in 1403, four years into his reign:

> It was in the year 1403, when Bolingbroke had sat upon the throne for four years, and his son, Henry of Monmouth, had held the reins of government in Wales. (003-003)

His reign was marked by rebellion from the outset — most notably the Percy uprising that culminated at the [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]], where the novel's opening military action takes place. The Percys, who had helped place Bolingbroke on the throne, turned against him, and the battle that followed was as much a test of Henry's legitimacy as it was a military engagement.

### Character and Reputation

The novel presents Henry through the filter of others' perceptions, most notably through the question posed by [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]], the blunt-spoken young soldier who has never met his sovereign:

> "What manner of man is this Henry of Bolingbroke?" asked Simon. "Is he one to follow?" (003-108)

The response he receives is telling: "Nay, by the Rood! Henry is a man, even as his son" (003-109). This comparison to the Prince of Wales — the vigorous and charismatic [[king-henry-v|Henry of Monmouth]] — places the King in a favourable light, as a man of action rather than a remote figurehead. Yet the cloud of usurpation hangs over him. Throughout the novel, his enemies refer to him dismissively as "the usurper, Henry Bolingbroke, called the Fourth of England" (005-075), reminding the reader that his crown was won by deposition and that his legitimacy remained contested throughout his reign.

The court over which Henry presided was one of competing factions and watchful nobles. [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]]'s own assessment, delivered with characteristic bluntness to [[charles-of-granmere]] when Granmere defends the King's accessibility, draws a careful distinction:

> "But Henry is a man," Granmere answered. (006-064)
> "It is the young Henry who is a man," Simon said. (006-065)

This remark suggests that the King, though competent, lacks the youthful vigour and martial magnetism of his eldest son — a distinction that would prove fateful when the Prince succeeded as [[king-henry-v|Henry V]] and eclipsed his father's military achievements.

## Audience with Simon of Beauvallet

### The First Audience at Shrewsbury

After the [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]], [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon]] — still an unknown knight — was brought before the King. The audience scene occupies much of Chapter 3 and establishes the relationship between monarch and subject that would define Simon's career. Henry received the young knight with royal gravity, questioning him about the battle, his origins, and his service to [[fulk-of-montlice|Fulk of Montlice]].

The King's judgement of Simon was favourable and perceptive. He recognised in the young man — cold-eyed, blunt-speaking, ferociously competent — the same kind of fierce capability that had secured his own throne. The audience concluded with Henry granting Simon a place in the royal service, acknowledging the knight's worth despite his lack of noble lineage or courtly polish.

### The Plot at Granmere Hall

The second audience between Henry and Simon occurred after Simon's discovery of a conspiracy against the crown. While visiting [[charles-of-granmere|Charles of Granmere]] at [[granmere-hall|Granmere Hall]] during his journey south, Simon uncovered evidence of a plot against the King's life. Riding to [[london|London]] with the intelligence, Simon was granted a second royal audience:

> Accosted by a strange knight with news of a plot, the King received him at once, and in the King's chamber, where he found Henry and the old Duke of York. (006-067)

The presence of the Duke of York in this scene is significant, signaling the factional tensions that characterised Henry's court. The King received the intelligence gravely, rewarding Simon with royal thanks and entrusting him with further responsibilities. This second audience cemented Simon's position as a trusted servant of the Lancastrian crown and set in motion the chain of events that would lead him to the [[siege-of-belremy|siege of Belrémy]] and the [[campaign-in-normandy|Normandy campaign]].

### The Duke of York at Court

Henry's court included powerful magnates whose presence at key moments underscored the precarious balance of power. The Duke of York, who appears beside the King when Simon delivers the plot warning (006-067), was a figure of immense influence whose loyalties — like those of many nobles in the post-deposition years — could not be taken entirely for granted. Henry's ability to maintain the allegiance of such men, even as he faced rebellion from the Percys and others, speaks to his political skill as much as his military capacity.

## Relationship with Henry of Monmouth

### Father and Heir

The relationship between Henry IV and his eldest son, [[king-henry-v|Henry of Monmouth]] (the future Henry V), forms an important backdrop to the novel's political world. The Prince appears prominently in the early chapters, fighting alongside Simon at [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Shrewsbury]] and proving himself a charismatic and capable leader. The King is seen receiving his son publicly after the battle:

> Henry came at last, and publicly embraced his son. (003-163)

The public nature of this embrace — in full view of the army and the court — was an important political gesture. Henry IV needed to demonstrate not only paternal affection but also confidence in his heir, whose reputation for wildness in his youth had caused some to question his fitness to rule. By the time of the novel, the Prince had proven himself in the Welsh campaigns and at Shrewsbury, and the King's pride in his son's achievements is evident.

### Contrasting Reputations

The novel draws a subtle contrast between father and son. Where the Prince is vigorous, magnetic, and youthful — "a man," as Simon says (006-065) — the King, though respected, bears the weight of contested legitimacy. The epithet "usurper" follows him through the narrative, a reminder that his crown was won by deposition and defended by force. His son, by contrast, would inherit a throne more securely Lancastrian than his father ever held, and would achieve the military glories in France that eluded Henry IV during his illness-plagued final years.

## Historical Context

### The Lancastrian Usurpation

The historical Henry IV reigned from 1399 to 1413. His deposition of [[richard-ii|Richard II]] initiated the Lancastrian phase of the late medieval period and set the stage for both the Agincourt campaign and, ultimately, the Wars of the Roses. In deposing Richard, Henry broke the principle of hereditary succession, establishing the precedent that a monarch could be removed by force — a precedent that would ultimately consume his own dynasty.

The novel references the contested nature of Henry's rule explicitly through the words of his enemies:

> "The injustice set about by the usurper, Henry Bolingbroke, called the Fourth of England." (005-075)

This language, typical of Lancastrian-era propaganda from the opposing faction, captures the central tension of Henry's reign: a capable ruler whose authority was never universally accepted.

### The Percy Rebellion and Shrewsbury

The [[battle-of-shrewsbury|Battle of Shrewsbury]] (1403) was the defining military crisis of Henry's reign. The Percy family — the Earl of Northumberland, his brother the Earl of Worcester, and his son [[hotspur|Henry "Hotspur" Percy]] — had been instrumental in placing Bolingbroke on the throne but turned against him over perceived slights and unpaid debts. The rebellion nearly toppled the Lancastrian regime in its infancy, and the royal victory at Shrewsbury, fought in July 1403, secured Henry's hold on the crown.

In the novel, Shrewsbury serves as the crucible in which [[simon-of-beauvallet|Simon of Beauvallet]] proves his martial worth. The King's personal presence at the battle is documented in the narrative, which notes that Henry was on the field with his forces at Shrewsbury, where his leadership helped secure the Lancastrian victory (003-003). Henry narrowly escaped death during the battle when several knights wearing royal armour were cut down by Percy's men — demonstrating the active, campaigning kingship that characterised the Lancastrian style.

### Legacy in the Novel

In the world of *Simon the Coldheart*, Henry IV serves as the embodiment of established royal authority — the power that Simon serves without question and that his enemies seek to undermine. The King's role, while limited to the early chapters, is foundational: he is the sovereign who validates Simon's rise, the authority in whose name Belrémy is taken, and the father of the Prince under whom Simon will fight his future campaigns. His contested legitimacy and eventual success in holding the throne mirror, at the political level, the novel's central theme of a man who wins his place through force of arms and force of will.
