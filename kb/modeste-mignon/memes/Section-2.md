# Section 2

[002-001]In 1829 one of the last houses looking toward the sea, and which in

[002-002]all probability stands about the centre of the [[ingouville|Ingouville]] to-day, was

[002-003]called, and perhaps is still called, “[[chalet|the Chalet]].” Originally it was a

[002-004]porter’s lodge with a trim little garden in front of it. The owner of

[002-005]the villa to which it belonged,--a mansion with park, gardens, aviaries,

[002-006]hot-houses, and lawns--took a fancy to put the little dwelling more in

[002-007]keeping with the splendor of his own abode, and he reconstructed it on

[002-008]the model of an ornamental cottage. He divided this cottage from his own

[002-009]lawn, which was bordered and set with flower-beds and formed the terrace

[002-010]of his villa, by a low wall along which he planted a concealing hedge.

[002-011]Behind the cottage (called, in spite of all his efforts to prevent it,

[002-012]the Chalet) were the orchards and kitchen gardens of the villa. The

[002-013]Chalet, without cows or dairy, is separated from the roadway by a wooden

[002-014]fence whose palings are hidden under a luxuriant hedge. On the other

[002-015]side of the road the opposite house, subject to a legal privilege, has

[002-016]a similar hedge and paling, so as to leave an unobstructed view of [[provincial-life|Havre]]

[002-017]to the Chalet.

[002-018]This little dwelling was the torment of the present proprietor of the

[002-019]villa, Monsieur Vilquin; and here is the why and the wherefore. The

[002-020]original creator of the villa, whose sumptuous details cry aloud,

[002-021]“Behold our millions!” extended his park far into the country for the

[002-022]purpose, as he averred, of getting his gardeners out of his pockets; and

[002-023]so, when the Chalet was finished, none but a friend could be allowed to

[002-024]inhabit it. Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], the next owner of the property, was very

[002-025]much attached to his cashier, [[dumay|Dumay]], and the following history will

[002-026]prove that the attachment was mutual; to him therefore he offered

[002-027]the little dwelling. Dumay, a stickler for legal methods, insisted on

[002-028]signing a lease for three hundred francs for twelve years, and Monsieur

[002-029]Mignon willingly agreed, remarking,--

[002-030]“My dear Dumay, remember, you have now bound yourself to live with me

[002-031]for twelve years.”

[002-032]In consequence of certain events which will presently be related, the

[002-033]estates of Monsieur Mignon, formerly the richest [[commerce|merchant]] in Havre, were

[002-034]sold to Vilquin, one of his business competitors. In his joy at getting

[002-035]possession of the celebrated villa Mignon, the latter forgot to demand

[002-036]the cancelling of the lease. Dumay, anxious not to hinder the sale,

[002-037]would have signed anything Vilquin required, but the sale once made, he

[002-038]held to his lease like a vengeance. And there he remained, in Vilquin’s

[002-039]pocket as it were; at the heart of Vilquin’s family life, observing

[002-040]Vilquin, irritating Vilquin,--in short, the gadfly of all the Vilquins.

[002-041]Every morning, when he looked out of his window, Vilquin felt a violent

[002-042]shock of annoyance as his eye lighted on the little gem of a building,

[002-043]the Chalet, which had cost sixty thousand francs and sparkled like a

[002-044]ruby in the sun. That comparison is very nearly exact. The architect has

[002-045]constructed the cottage of brilliant red brick pointed with white.

[002-046]The window-frames are painted of a lively green, the woodwork is brown

[002-047]verging on yellow. The roof overhangs by several feet. A pretty gallery,

[002-048]with open-worked balustrade, surmounts the lower floor and projects

[002-049]at the centre of the facade into a veranda with glass sides. The

[002-050]ground-floor has a charming salon and a dining-room, separated from

[002-051]each other by the landing of a staircase built of wood, designed

[002-052]and decorated with elegant simplicity. The kitchen is behind the

[002-053]dining-room, and the corresponding room back of the salon, formerly a

[002-054]study, is now the bedroom of Monsieur and Madame Dumay. On the upper

[002-055]floor the architect has managed to get two large bedrooms, each with a

[002-056]dressing-room, to which the veranda serves as a salon; and above this

[002-057]floor, under the eaves, which are tipped together like a couple of

[002-058]cards, are two servants’ rooms with mansard roofs, each lighted by a

[002-059]circular window and tolerably spacious.

[002-060]Vilquin has been petty enough to build a high wall on the side toward

[002-061]the orchard and kitchen garden; and in consequence of this piece

[002-062]of spite, the few square feet which the lease secured to the Chalet

[002-063]resembled a Parisian garden. The out-buildings, painted in keeping

[002-064]with the cottage, stood with their backs to the wall of the adjoining

[002-065]property.

[002-066]The interior of this charming dwelling harmonized with its exterior.

[002-067]The salon, floored entirely with iron-wood, was painted in a style that

[002-068]suggested the beauties of Chinese lacquer. On black panels edged with

[002-069]gold, birds of every color, foliage of impossible greens, and fantastic

[002-070]oriental designs glowed and shimmered. The dining-room was entirely

[002-071]sheathed in Northern woods carved and cut in open-work like the

[002-072]beautiful Russian chalets. The little antechamber formed by the landing

[002-073]and the well of the staircase was painted in old oak to represent Gothic

[002-074]ornament. The bedrooms, hung with chintz, were charming in their costly

[002-075]simplicity. The study, where the cashier and his wife now slept, was

[002-076]panelled from top to bottom, on the walls and ceiling, like the cabin of

[002-077]a steamboat. These luxuries of his predecessor excited Vilquin’s wrath.

[002-078]He would fain have lodged his daughter and her husband in the cottage.

[002-079]This desire, well known to Dumay, will presently serve to illustrate the

[002-080]Breton obstinacy of the latter.

[002-081]The entrance to the Chalet is by a little trellised iron door, the

[002-082]uprights of which, ending in lance-heads, show for a few inches above

[002-083]the fence and its hedge. The little garden, about as wide as the more

[002-084]pretentious lawn, was just now filled with flowers, roses, and dahlias

[002-085]of the choicest kind, and many rare products of the hot-houses, for

[002-086](another Vilquinard grievance) the elegant little hot-house, a very whim

[002-087]of a hot-house, a hot-house representing dignity and style, belonged

[002-088]to the Chalet, and separated, or if you prefer, united it to the villa

[002-089]Vilquin. Dumay consoled himself for the toils of business in taking care

[002-090]of this hot-house, whose exotic treasures were one of [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s joys.

[002-091]The billiard-room of the villa Vilquin, a species of gallery, formerly

[002-092]communicated through an immense aviary with this hot-house. But after

[002-093]the building of the wall which deprived him of a view into the orchards,

[002-094]Dumay bricked up the door of communication. “Wall for wall!” he said.

[002-095]In 1827 Vilquin offered Dumay a salary of six thousand francs, and ten

[002-096]thousand more as indemnity, if he would give up the lease. The cashier

[002-097]refused; though he had but three thousand francs from Gobenheim, a

[002-098]former clerk of his master. Dumay was a Breton transplanted by fate into

[002-099]Normandy. Imagine therefore the hatred conceived for the tenants of the

[002-100]Chalet by the Norman Vilquin, a man worth three millions! What criminal

[002-101]leze-million on the part of a cashier, to hold up to the eyes of such

[002-102]a man the impotence of his wealth! Vilquin, whose desperation in the

[002-103]matter made him the talk of Havre, had just proposed to give Dumay a

[002-104]pretty house of his own, and had again been refused. Havre itself began

[002-105]to grow uneasy at the man’s obstinacy, and a good many persons explained

[002-106]it by the phrase, “Dumay is a Breton.” As for the cashier, he thought

[002-107]Madame and Mademoiselle Mignon would be ill-lodged elsewhere. His

[002-108]two idols now inhabited a temple worthy of them; the sumptuous little

[002-109]cottage gave them a home, where these dethroned royalties could keep the

[002-110]semblance of majesty about them,--a species of dignity usually denied to

[002-111]those who have seen better days.

[002-112]Perhaps as the story goes on, the reader will not regret having learned

[002-113]in advance a few particulars as to the home and the habitual companions

[002-114]of Modeste Mignon, for, at her age, people and things have as much

[002-115]influence upon the future life as a person’s own character,--indeed,

[002-116]character often receives ineffaceable impressions from its surroundings.

[002-117]CHAPTER II. A PORTRAIT FROM LIFE

[002-118]From the manner with which the Latournelles entered the Chalet a

[002-119]stranger would readily have guessed that they came there every evening.

[002-120]“Ah, you are here already,” said the notary, perceiving the young banker

[002-121]Gobenheim, a connection of Gobenheim-Keller, the head of the great

[002-122]banking house in [[paris|Paris]].

[002-123]This young man with a livid face--a blonde of the type with black eyes,

[002-124]whose immovable glance has an indescribable fascination, sober in speech

[002-125]as in conduct, dressed in black, lean as a consumptive, but nevertheless

[002-126]vigorously framed--visited the family of his former master and the house

[002-127]of his cashier less from affection than from self-interest. Here they

[002-128]played whist at two sous a point; a dress-coat was not required; he

[002-129]accepted no refreshment except “eau sucree,” and consequently had

[002-130]no civilities to return. This apparent devotion to the Mignon family

[002-131]allowed it to be supposed that Gobenheim had a heart; it also released

[002-132]him from the necessity of going into the society of Havre and incurring

[002-133]useless expenses, thus upsetting the orderly economy of his domestic

[002-134]life. This disciple of the golden calf went to bed at half-past ten

[002-135]o’clock and got up at five in the morning. Moreover, being perfectly

[002-136]sure of Latournelle’s and Butscha’s discretion, he could talk over

[002-137]difficult business matters, obtain the advice of the notary gratis,

[002-138]and get an inkling of the real truth of the gossip of the street. This

[002-139]stolid gold-glutton (the epithet is Butscha’s) belonged by nature to

[002-140]the class of substances which chemistry terms absorbents. Ever since the

[002-141]catastrophe of the house of Mignon, where the Kellers had placed him to

[002-142]learn the principles of maritime commerce, no one at the Chalet had ever

[002-143]asked him to do the smallest thing, no matter what; his reply was too

[002-144]well known. The young fellow looked at Modeste precisely as he would

[002-145]have looked at a cheap lithograph.

[002-146]“He’s one of the pistons of the big engine called ‘Commerce,’” said poor

[002-147]Butscha, whose clever mind made itself felt occasionally by such little

[002-148]sayings timidly jerked out.

[002-149]The four Latournelles bowed with the most respectful deference to an

[002-150]old lady dressed in black velvet, who did not rise from the armchair in

[002-151]which she was seated, for the reason that both eyes were covered with

[002-152]the yellow film produced by cataract. Madame Mignon may be sketched in

[002-153]one sentence. Her august countenance of the mother of a family attracted

[002-154]instant notice as that of one whose irreproachable life defies the

[002-155]assaults of destiny, which nevertheless makes her the target of its

[002-156]arrows and a member of the unnumbered tribe of Niobes. Her blonde wig,

[002-157]carefully curled and well arranged upon her head, became the cold white

[002-158]face which resembled that of some burgomaster’s wife painted by Hals or

[002-159]Mirevelt. The extreme neatness of her dress, the velvet boots, the lace

[002-160]collar, the shawl evenly folded and put on, all bore testimony to the

[002-161]solicitous care which Modeste bestowed upon her mother.

[002-162]When silence was, as the notary had predicted, restored in the pretty

[002-163]salon, Modeste, sitting beside her mother, for whom she was embroidering

[002-164]a kerchief, became for an instant the centre of observation. This

[002-165]curiosity, barely veiled by the commonplace salutations and inquiries

[002-166]of the visitors, would have revealed even to an indifferent person the

[002-167]existence of the domestic plot to which Modeste was expected to fall

[002-168]a victim; but Gobenheim, more than indifferent, noticed nothing, and

[002-169]proceeded to light the candles on the card-table. The behavior of Dumay

[002-170]made the whole scene terrifying to Butscha, to the Latournelles, and

[002-171]above all to Madame Dumay, who knew her husband to be capable of firing

[002-172]a pistol at Modeste’s [[love|lover]] as coolly as though he were a mad dog.

[002-173]After dinner that day the cashier had gone to walk followed by two

[002-174]magnificent Pyrenees hounds, whom he suspected of betraying him, and

[002-175]therefore left in charge of a farmer, a former tenant of Monsieur

[002-176]Mignon. On his return, just before the arrival of the Latournelles,

[002-177]he had taken his pistols from his bed’s head and placed them on the

[002-178]chimney-piece, concealing this action from Modeste. The young girl took

[002-179]no notice whatever of these preparations, singular as they were.
