# Section 29

[029-001]“Your conscience, my friend, strikes me as nothing more nor less than a

[029-002]dread of losing the pleasures of vanity, and some very real advantages

[029-003]and habits by sacrificing the affections of Madame de Chaulieu; for, if

[029-004]you were sure of succeeding with [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], you would renounce without the

[029-005]slightest compunction the wilted aftermath of a passion that has been

[029-006]mown and well-raked for the last eight years. If you simply mean that

[029-007]you are afraid of displeasing your protectress, should she find out the

[029-008]object of your stay here, I believe you. To renounce the duchess and yet

[029-009]not succeed at [[chalet|the Chalet]] is too heavy a risk. You take the anxiety of

[029-010]this alternative for remorse.”

[029-011]“You have no comprehension of feelings,” said [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]], irritably, like

[029-012]a man who hears truth when he expects a compliment.

[029-013]“That is what a bigamist should tell the jury,” retorted La Briere,

[029-014]laughing.

[029-015]This epigram made another disagreeable impression on Canalis. He began

[029-016]to think La Briere too witty and too free for a secretary.

[029-017]The arrival of an elegant caleche, driven by a coachman in the Canalis

[029-018]livery, made the more excitement at the Chalet because the two suitors

[029-019]were expected, and all the personages of this history were assembled to

[029-020]receive them, except the duke and Butscha.

[029-021]“Which is the [[poetry|poet]]?” asked Madame Latournelle of [[dumay|Dumay]] in the embrasure

[029-022]of a window, where she stationed herself as soon as she heard the

[029-023]wheels.

[029-024]“The one who walks like a drum-major,” answered the lieutenant.

[029-025]“Ah!” said the notary’s wife, examining Canalis, who was swinging his

[029-026]body like a man who knows he is being looked at. The fault lay with the

[029-027]great lady who flattered him incessantly and spoiled him,--as all women

[029-028]older than their adorers invariably spoil and flatter them; Canalis in

[029-029]his moral being was a sort of Narcissus. When a woman of a certain age

[029-030]wishes to attach a man forever, she begins by deifying his defects, so

[029-031]as to cut off all possibility of rivalry; for a rival is never, at the

[029-032]first approach, aware of the super-fine flattery to which the man is

[029-033]accustomed. Coxcombs are the product of this feminine manoeuvre, when

[029-034]they are not fops by nature. Canalis, taken young by the handsome

[029-035]duchess, vindicated his affectations to his own mind by telling himself

[029-036]that they pleased that “grande dame,” whose taste was law. Such shades

[029-037]of character may be excessively faint, but it is improper for the

[029-038]historian not to point them out. For instance, Melchior possessed a

[029-039]talent for reading which was greatly admired, and much injudicious

[029-040]praise had given him a habit of exaggeration, which neither poets nor

[029-041]actors are willing to check, and which made people say of him (always

[029-042]through De Marsay) that he no longer declaimed, he bellowed his verses;

[029-043]lengthening the sounds that he might listen to himself. In the slang of

[029-044]the green-room, Canalis “dragged the time.” He was fond of exchanging

[029-045]glances with his hearers, throwing himself into postures of

[029-046]self-complacency and practising those tricks of demeanor which actors

[029-047]call “balancoires,”--the picturesque phrase of an artistic people.

[029-048]Canalis had his imitators, and was in fact the head of a school of

[029-049]his kind. This habit of declamatory chanting slightly affected his

[029-050]conversation, as we have seen in his interview with Dumay. The moment

[029-051]the mind becomes finical the manners follow suit, and the great poet

[029-052]ended by studying his demeanor, inventing attitudes, looking furtively

[029-053]at himself in mirrors, and suiting his discourse to the particular

[029-054]pose which he happened to have taken up. He was so preoccupied with the

[029-055]effect he wished to produce, that a practical joke, Blondet, had bet

[029-056]once or twice, and won the wager, that he could nonplus him at any

[029-057]moment by merely looking fixedly at his hair, or his boots, or the tails

[029-058]of his coats.

[029-059]These airs and graces, which started in life with a passport of flowery

[029-060]youth, now seemed all the more stale and old because Melchior himself

[029-061]was waning. Life in the world of fashion is quite as exhausting to men

[029-062]as it is to women, and perhaps the twenty years by which the duchess

[029-063]exceeded her [[love|lover]]’s age, weighed more heavily upon him than upon her;

[029-064]for to the eyes of the world she was always handsome,--without rouge,

[029-065]without wrinkles, and without heart. Alas! neither men nor women have

[029-066]friends who are friendly enough to warn them of the moment when the

[029-067]fragrance of their modesty grows stale, when the caressing glance is

[029-068]but an echo of the stage, when the expression of the face changes from

[029-069]sentiment to sentimentality, and the artifices of the mind show their

[029-070]rusty edges. Genius alone renews its skin like a snake; and in the

[029-071]matter of charm, as in everything else, it is only the heart that never

[029-072]grows old. People who have hearts are simple in all their ways. Now

[029-073]Canalis, as we know, had a shrivelled heart. He misused the beauty of

[029-074]his glance by giving it, without adequate reason, the fixity that comes

[029-075]to the eyes in meditation. In short, applause was to him a [[commerce|business]], in

[029-076]which he was perpetually on the lookout for gain. His style of paying

[029-077]compliments, charming to superficial people, seemed insulting to

[029-078]others of more delicacy, by its triteness and the cool assurance of

[029-079]its cut-and-dried flattery. As a matter of fact, Melchior lied like a

[029-080]courtier. He remarked without blushing to the Duc de Chaulieu, who made

[029-081]no impression whatever when he was obliged to address the Chamber as

[029-082]minister of foreign affairs, “Your excellency was truly sublime!” Many

[029-083]men like Canalis are purged of their affectations by the administration

[029-084]of non-success in little doses.

[029-085]These defects, slight in the gilded salons of the faubourg

[029-086]Saint-Germain, where every one contributes his or her quota of

[029-087]absurdity, and where these particular forms of exaggerated speech

[029-088]and affected diction--magniloquence, if you please to call it so--are

[029-089]surrounded by excessive luxury and sumptuous toilettes, which are to

[029-090]some extent their excuse, were certain to be far more noticed in the

[029-091]provinces, whose own absurdities are of a totally different type.

[029-092]Canalis, by nature over-strained and artificial, could not change his

[029-093]form; in fact, he had had time to grow stiff in the mould into which the

[029-094]duchess had poured him; moreover, he was thoroughly Parisian, or, if

[029-095]you prefer it, truly French. The Parisian is amazed that everything

[029-096]everywhere is not as it in [[paris|Paris]]; the Frenchman, as it is in France.

[029-097]Good taste, on the contrary, demands that we adapt ourselves to the

[029-098]customs of foreigners without losing too much of our own character,--as

[029-099]did Alcibiades, that model of a gentleman. True grace is elastic; it

[029-100]lends itself to circumstances; it is in harmony with all social centres;

[029-101]it wears a robe of simple material in the streets, noticeable only by

[029-102]its cut, in preference to the feathers and flounces of middle-class

[029-103]vulgarity. Now Canalis, instigated by a woman who loved herself much

[029-104]more than she loved him, wished to lay down the law and be, everywhere,

[029-105]such as he himself might see fit to be. He believed he carried his own

[029-106]public with him wherever he went,--an error shared by several of the

[029-107]great men of Paris.

[029-108]While the poet made a studied and effective entrance into the salon of

[029-109]the Chalet, La Briere slipped in behind him like a person of no account.

[029-110]“Ha! do I see my soldier?” said Canalis, perceiving Dumay, after

[029-111]addressing a compliment to Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], and bowing to the other women.

[029-112]“Your anxieties are relieved, are they not?” he said, offering his hand

[029-113]effusively; “I comprehend them to their fullest extent after seeing

[029-114]mademoiselle. I spoke to you of terrestrial creatures, not of angels.”

[029-115]All present seemed by their attitudes to ask the meaning of this speech.

[029-116]“I shall always consider it a triumph,” resumed the poet, observing that

[029-117]everybody wished for an explanation, “to have stirred to mention one

[029-118]of those men of iron whom Napoleon had the eye to find and make the

[029-119]supporting piles on which he tried to build an empire, too colossal

[029-120]to be lasting: for such structures time alone is the cement. But this

[029-121]triumph--why should I be proud of it?--I count for nothing. It was the

[029-122]triumph of ideas over facts. Your battles, my dear Monsieur Dumay, your

[029-123]heroic charges, Monsieur le comte, nay, war itself was the form in which

[029-124]Napoleon’s idea clothed itself. Of all of these things, what remains?

[029-125]The sod that covers them knows nothing; harvests come and go without

[029-126]revealing their resting-place; were it not for the historian, the

[029-127]writer, futurity would have no knowledge of those heroic days. Therefore

[029-128]your fifteen years of war are now ideas and nothing more; that which

[029-129]preserves the Empire forever is the poem that the poets make of them. A

[029-130]nation that can win such battles must know how to sing them.”

[029-131]Canalis paused, to gather by a glance that ran round the circle the

[029-132]tribute of amazement which he expected of provincials.

[029-133]“You must be aware, monsieur, of the regret I feel at not seeing you,”

[029-134]said Madame Mignon, “since you compensate me with the pleasure of

[029-135]hearing you.”

[029-136]Modeste, determined to think Canalis sublime, sat motionless with

[029-137]amazement; the embroidery slipped from her fingers, which held it only

[029-138]by the needleful of thread.

[029-139]“Modeste, this is Monsieur [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere. Monsieur Ernest, my

[029-140]daughter,” said the count, thinking the secretary too much in the

[029-141]background.

[029-142]The young girl bowed coldly, giving Ernest a glance that was meant to

[029-143]prove to every one present that she saw him for the first time.

[029-144]“Pardon me, monsieur,” she said without blushing; “the great admiration

[029-145]I feel for the greatest of our poets is, in the eyes of my friends, a

[029-146]sufficient excuse for seeing only him.”

[029-147]The pure, fresh voice, with accents like that of Mademoiselle Mars,

[029-148]charmed the poor secretary, already dazzled by Modeste’s beauty, and

[029-149]in his sudden surprise he answered by a phrase that would have been

[029-150]sublime, had it been true.

[029-151]“He is my friend,” he said.

[029-152]“Ah, then you do pardon me,” she replied.

[029-153]“He is more than a friend,” cried Canalis taking Ernest by the shoulder

[029-154]and leaning upon it like Alexander on Hephaestion, “we love each other

[029-155]as though we were brothers--”

[029-156]Madame Latournelle cut short the poet’s speech by pointing to Ernest

[029-157]and saying aloud to her husband, “Surely that is the gentleman we saw at

[029-158]church.”

[029-159]“Why not?” said Charles Mignon, quickly, observing that Ernest reddened.

[029-160]Modeste coldly took up her embroidery.

[029-161]“Madame may be right; I have been twice in [[provincial-life|Havre]] lately,” replied La

[029-162]Briere, sitting down by Dumay.

[029-163]Canalis, charmed with Modeste’s beauty, mistook the admiration she

[029-164]expressed, and flattered himself he had succeeded in producing his

[029-165]desired effects.

[029-166]“I should think a man without heart, if he had no devoted friend near

[029-167]him,” said Modeste, to pick up the conversation interrupted by Madame

[029-168]Latournelle’s awkwardness.

[029-169]“Mademoiselle, Ernest’s devotion makes me almost think myself worth

[029-170]something,” said Canalis; “for my dear Pylades is full of talent; he

[029-171]was the right hand of the greatest minister we have had since the peace.

[029-172]Though he holds a fine position, he is good enough to be my tutor in the

[029-173]science of politics; he teaches me to conduct affairs and feeds me with

[029-174]his experience, when all the while he might aspire to a much better

[029-175]situation. Oh! he is worth far more than I.” At a gesture from Modeste

[029-176]he continued gracefully: “Yes, the poetry that I express he carries in

[029-177]his heart; and if I speak thus openly before him it is because he has

[029-178]the modesty of a nun.”

[029-179]“Enough, oh, enough!” cried La Briere, who hardly knew which way to

[029-180]look. “My dear Canalis, you remind me of a mother who is seeking to

[029-181]marry off her daughter.”

[029-182]“How is it, monsieur,” said Charles Mignon, addressing Canalis, “that

[029-183]you can even think of becoming a political character?”
