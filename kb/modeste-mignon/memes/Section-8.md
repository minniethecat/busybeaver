# Section 8

[008-001]Nevertheless, this drama of a poor seduced sister returning to die under

[008-002]a roof of elegant poverty, the failure of her father, the baseness of

[008-003]her betrothed, the blindness of her mother caused by grief, had touched

[008-004]the surface only of [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s life, by which alone the Dumays and the

[008-005]Latournelles judged her; for no devotion of friends can take the place

[008-006]of a mother’s eye. The monotonous life in the dainty little Chalet,

[008-007]surrounded by the choice flowers which [[dumay|Dumay]] cultivated; the family

[008-008]customs, as regular as clock-work, the [[provincial-life|provincial]] decorum, the games

[008-009]at whist while the mother knitted and the daughter sewed, the silence,

[008-010]broken only by the roar of the sea in the equinoctial storms,--all this

[008-011]monastic tranquillity did in fact hide an inner and tumultuous life, the

[008-012]life of ideas, the life of the spiritual being. We sometimes wonder how

[008-013]it is possible for young girls to do wrong; but such as do so have no

[008-014]blind mother to send her plummet line of intuition to the depths of the

[008-015]subterranean fancies of a virgin heart. The Dumays slept when Modeste

[008-016]opened her window, as it were to watch for the passing of a man,--the

[008-017]man of her dreams, the expected knight who was to mount her behind him

[008-018]and ride away under the fire of Dumay’s pistols.

[008-019]During the depression caused by her sister’s death Modeste flung herself

[008-020]into the practice of reading, until her mind became sodden in it. Born

[008-021]to the use of two languages, she could speak and read German quite as

[008-022]well as French; she had also, together with her sister, learned English

[008-023]from Madame Dumay. Being very little overlooked in the matter of reading

[008-024]by the people about her, who had no literary knowledge, Modeste fed her

[008-025]soul on the modern masterpieces of three literatures, English, French,

[008-026]and German. Lord Byron, Goethe, Schiller, Walter Scott, Hugo, Lamartine,

[008-027]Crabbe, Moore, the great works of the 17th and 18th centuries, history,

[008-028]drama, and fiction, from Astraea to Manon Lescaut, from Montaigne’s

[008-029]Essays to Diderot, from the Fabliaux to the Nouvelle Heloise,--in short,

[008-030]the thought of three lands crowded with confused images that girlish

[008-031]head, august in its cold guilelessness, its native chastity, but from

[008-032]which there sprang full-armed, brilliant, sincere, and strong, an

[008-033]overwhelming admiration for genius. To Modeste a new book was an event;

[008-034]a masterpiece that would have horrified Madame Latournelle made her

[008-035]happy,--equally unhappy if the great work did not play havoc with her

[008-036]heart. A lyric instinct bubbled in that girlish soul, so full of the

[008-037]beautiful illusions of its youth. But of this radiant existence not a

[008-038]gleam reached the surface of daily life; it escaped the ken of Dumay and

[008-039]his wife and the Latournelles; the ears of the blind mother alone caught

[008-040]the crackling of its flame.

[008-041]The profound disdain which Modeste now conceived for ordinary men gave

[008-042]to her face a look of pride, an inexpressible untamed shyness, which

[008-043]tempered her Teutonic simplicity, and accorded well with a peculiarity

[008-044]of her head. The hair growing in a point above the forehead seemed the

[008-045]continuation of a slight line which thought had already furrowed between

[008-046]the eyebrows, and made the expression of untameability perhaps a

[008-047]shade too strong. The voice of this charming child, whom her father,

[008-048]delighting in her wit, was wont to call his “little proverb of Solomon,”

[008-049]had acquired a precious flexibility of organ through the practice of

[008-050]three languages. This advantage was still further enhanced by a

[008-051]natural bell-like tone both sweet and fresh, which touched the heart as

[008-052]delightfully as it did the ear. If the mother could no longer see the

[008-053]signs of a noble destiny upon her daughter’s brow, she could study

[008-054]the transitions of her soul’s development in the accents of that voice

[008-055]attuned to [[love]].

[008-056]CHAPTER VI. A MAIDEN’S FIRST ROMANCE

[008-057]To this period of Modeste’s eager rage for reading succeeded the

[008-058]exercise of a strange faculty given to vigorous imaginations,--the

[008-059]power, namely, of making herself an actor in a dream-existence; of

[008-060]representing to her own mind the things desired, with so vivid a

[008-061]conception that they seemed actually to attain reality; in short, to

[008-062]enjoy by thought,--to live out her years within her mind; to marry;

[008-063]to grow old; to attend her own funeral like Charles V.; to play within

[008-064]herself the comedy of life and, if need be, that of death. Modeste was

[008-065]indeed playing, but all alone, the comedy of Love. She fancied herself

[008-066]adored to the summit of her wishes in many an imagined phase of

[008-067]social life. Sometimes as the heroine of a dark romance, she loved the

[008-068]executioner, or the wretch who ended her days upon the scaffold, or,

[008-069]like her sister, some Parisian youth without a penny, whose struggles

[008-070]were all beneath a garret-roof. Sometimes she was Ninon, scorning men

[008-071]amid continual fetes; or some applauded actress, or gay adventuress,

[008-072]exhausting in her own behalf the luck of Gil Blas, or the triumphs

[008-073]of Pasta, Malibran, and Florine. Then, weary of the horrors and

[008-074]excitements, she returned to actual life. She married a notary, she ate

[008-075]the plain brown bread of honest everyday life, she saw herself a Madame

[008-076]Latournelle; she accepted a painful existence, she bore all the trials

[008-077]of a struggle with [[commerce|fortune]]. After that she went back to the romances:

[008-078]she was loved for her beauty; a son of a peer of France, an eccentric,

[008-079]artistic young man, divined her heart, recognized the star which the

[008-080]genius of a De Stael had planted on her brow. Her father returned,

[008-081]possessing millions. With his permission, she put her various lovers

[008-082]to certain tests (always carefully guarding her own independence); she

[008-083]owned a magnificent estate and castle, servants, horses, carriages, the

[008-084]choicest of everything that luxury could bestow, and kept her suitors

[008-085]uncertain until she was forty years old, at which age she made her

[008-086]choice.

[008-087]This edition of the Arabian Nights in a single copy lasted nearly a

[008-088]year, and taught Modeste the sense of satiety through thought. She held

[008-089]her life too often in her hand, she said to herself philosophically and

[008-090]with too real a bitterness, too seriously, and too often, “Well, what

[008-091]is it, after all?” not to have plunged to her waist in the deep disgust

[008-092]which all men of genius feel when they try to complete by intense toil

[008-093]the work to which they have devoted themselves. Her youth and her rich

[008-094]nature alone kept Modeste at this period of her life from seeking to

[008-095]enter a cloister. But this sense of satiety cast her, saturated as

[008-096]she still was with Catholic spirituality, into the love of Good, the

[008-097]infinite of heaven. She conceived of charity, service to others, as the

[008-098]true occupation of life; but she cowered in the gloomy dreariness of

[008-099]finding in it no food for the fancy that lay crouching in her heart like

[008-100]an insect at the bottom of a calyx. Meanwhile she sat tranquilly sewing

[008-101]garments for the children of the poor, and listening abstractedly to the

[008-102]grumblings of Monsieur Latournelle when Dumay held the thirteenth card

[008-103]or drew out his last trump.

[008-104]Her religious faith drove Modeste for a time into a singular track

[008-105]of thought. She imagined that if she became sinless (speaking

[008-106]ecclesiastically) she would attain to such a condition of sanctity that

[008-107]God would hear her and accomplish her desires. “Faith,” she thought,

[008-108]“can move mountains; Christ has said so. The Saviour led his apostle

[008-109]upon the waters of the lake Tiberias; and I, all I ask of God is a

[008-110]husband to love me; that is easier than walking upon the sea.” She

[008-111]fasted through the next Lent, and did not commit a single sin; then she

[008-112]said to herself that on a certain day coming out of church she should

[008-113]meet a handsome young man who was worthy of her, whom her mother would

[008-114]accept, and who would fall madly in love with her. When the day came on

[008-115]which she had, as it were, summoned God to send her an angel, she was

[008-116]persistently followed by a rather disgusting beggar; moreover, it rained

[008-117]heavily, and not a single young man was in the streets. On another

[008-118]occasion she went to walk on the jetty to see the English travellers

[008-119]land; but each Englishman had an Englishwoman, nearly as handsome as

[008-120]Modeste herself, who saw no one at all resembling a wandering Childe

[008-121]Harold. Tears overcame her, as she sat down like Marius on the ruins of

[008-122]her imagination. But on the day when she subpoenaed God for the third

[008-123]time she firmly believed that the Elect of her dreams was within the

[008-124]church, hiding, perhaps out of delicacy, behind one of the pillars,

[008-125]round all of which she dragged Madame Latournelle on a tour of

[008-126]inspection. After this failure, she deposed the Deity from omnipotence.

[008-127]Many were her conversations with the imaginary lover, for whom she

[008-128]invented questions and answers, bestowing upon him a great deal of wit

[008-129]and intelligence.

[008-130]The high ambitions of her heart hidden within these romances were

[008-131]the real explanation of the prudent conduct which the good people who

[008-132]watched over Modeste so much admired; they might have brought her any

[008-133]number of young Althors or Vilquins, and she would never have stooped to

[008-134]such clowns. She wanted, purely and simply, a man of genius,--talent she

[008-135]cared little for; just as a lawyer is of no account to a girl who aims

[008-136]for an ambassador. Her only desire for wealth was to cast it at the feet

[008-137]of her idol. Indeed, the golden background of these visions was far less

[008-138]rich than the treasury of her own heart, filled with womanly delicacy;

[008-139]for its dominant desire was to make some Tasso, some Milton, a

[008-140]Jean-Jacques Rousseau, a Murat, a Christopher Columbus happy.

[008-141]Commonplace miseries did not seriously touch this youthful soul, who

[008-142]longed to extinguish the fires of the martyrs ignored and rejected in

[008-143]their own day. Sometimes she imagined balms of Gilead, soothing melodies

[008-144]which might have allayed the savage misanthropy of Rousseau. Or she

[008-145]fancied herself the wife of Lord Byron; guessing intuitively his

[008-146]contempt for the real, she made herself as fantastic as the [[poetry]]

[008-147]of Manfred, and provided for his scepticism by making him a Catholic.

[008-148]Modeste attributed Moliere’s melancholy to the women of the seventeenth

[008-149]century. “Why is there not some one woman,” she asked herself, “loving,

[008-150]beautiful, and rich, ready to stand beside each man of genius and be

[008-151]his slave, like Lara, the mysterious page?” She had, as the reader

[008-152]perceives, fully understood “il pianto,” which the English poet chanted

[008-153]by the mouth of his Gulmare. Modeste greatly admired the behavior of

[008-154]the young Englishwoman who offered herself to Crebillon, the son, who

[008-155]married her. The story of Sterne and Eliza Draper was her life and her

[008-156]happiness for several months. She made herself ideally the heroine of a

[008-157]like romance, and many a time she rehearsed in imagination the

[008-158]sublime role of Eliza. The sensibility so charmingly expressed in that

[008-159]delightful [[epistolary-novel|correspondence]] filled her eyes with tears which, it is said,

[008-160]were lacking in those of the wittiest of English writers.

[008-161]Modeste existed for some time on a comprehension, not only of the works,

[008-162]but of the characters of her favorite authors,--Goldsmith, the author

[008-163]of Obermann, Charles Nodier, Maturin. The poorest and the most suffering

[008-164]among them were her deities; she guessed their trials, initiated herself

[008-165]into a destitution where the thoughts of genius brooded, and poured upon

[008-166]it the treasures of her heart; she fancied herself the giver of material

[008-167]comfort to these great men, martyrs to their own faculty. This noble

[008-168]compassion, this intuition of the struggles of toilers, this worship

[008-169]of genius, are among the choicest perceptions that flutter through the

[008-170]souls of women. They are, in the first place, a secret between the woman

[008-171]and God, for they are hidden; in them there is nothing striking, nothing

[008-172]that gratifies the vanity,--that powerful auxiliary to all action among

[008-173]the French.
