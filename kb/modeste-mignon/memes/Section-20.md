# Section 20

[020-001]“Do you venture to question me after all I have done you the [[honor]] to

[020-002]say, and to ask of you?”

[020-003]Butscha bowed without another word, and departed to find his master, in

[020-004]all the rapture of being taken into the service of his goddess.

[020-005]Half an hour later, Monsieur and Madame Latournelle came to fetch

[020-006][[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], who complained of a horrible toothache.

[020-007]“I really have not had the courage to dress myself,” she said.

[020-008]“Well then,” replied the worthy chaperone, “stay at home.”

[020-009]“Oh, no!” said Modeste. “I would rather not. I have bundled myself up,

[020-010]and I don’t think it will do me any harm to go out.”

[020-011]And Mademoiselle [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] marched off beside Latournelle, refusing to take

[020-012]his arm lest she should be questioned about the outward trembling which

[020-013]betrayed her inward agitation at the thought of at last seeing her great

[020-014][[poetry|poet]]. One look, the first,--was it not about to decide her fate?

[020-015]CHAPTER XIII. A FULL-LENGTH PORTRAIT OF MONSIEUR DE LA BRIERE

[020-016]Is there in the life of man a more delightful moment than that of a

[020-017]first rendezvous? Are the sensations then hidden at the bottom of our

[020-018]hearts and finding their first expression ever renewed? Can we feel

[020-019]again the nameless pleasures that we felt when, like [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de

[020-020]La Briere, we looked up our sharpest razors, our finest shirt, an

[020-021]irreproachable collar, and our best clothes? We deify the garments

[020-022]associated with that all-supreme moment. We weave within us poetic

[020-023]fancies quite equal to those of the woman; and the day when either party

[020-024]guesses them they take wings to themselves and fly away. Are not such

[020-025]things like the flower of wild fruits, bitter-sweet, grown in the heart

[020-026]of a forest, the joy of the scant sun-rays, the joy, as [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] says in

[020-027]the “Maiden’s Song,” of the plant itself whose eyes unclosing see its

[020-028]own image within its breast?

[020-029]Such emotions, now taking place in La Briere, tend to show that, like

[020-030]other poor fellows for whom life begins in toil and care, he had never

[020-031]yet been loved. Arriving at [[provincial-life|Havre]] overnight, he had gone to bed at once,

[020-032]like a true coquette, to obliterate all traces of fatigue; and now,

[020-033]after taking his bath, he had put himself into a costume carefully

[020-034]adapted to show him off to the best advantage. This is, perhaps, the

[020-035]right moment to exhibit a full-length portrait of him, if only to

[020-036]justify the last letter that Modeste was still to write to him.

[020-037]Born of a good family in Toulouse, and allied by [[marriage]] to the

[020-038]minister who first took him under his protection, Ernest had that air of

[020-039]good-breeding which comes of an education begun in the cradle; and the

[020-040]habit of managing [[commerce|business]] affairs gave him a certain sedateness which

[020-041]was not pedantic,--though pedantry is the natural outgrowth of premature

[020-042]gravity. He was of ordinary height; his face, which won upon all who saw

[020-043]him by its delicacy and sweetness, was warm in the flesh-tints, though

[020-044]without color, and relieved by a small moustache and imperial a la

[020-045]Mazarin. Without this evidence of virility he might have resembled a

[020-046]young woman in disguise, so refined was the shape of his face and the

[020-047]cut of his lips, so feminine the transparent ivory of a set of teeth,

[020-048]regular enough to have seemed artificial. Add to these womanly points a

[020-049]habit of speech as gentle as the expression of the face; as gentle,

[020-050]too, as the blue eyes with their Turkish eyelids, and you will readily

[020-051]understand how it was that the minister occasionally called his young

[020-052]secretary Mademoiselle de La Briere. The full, clear forehead, well

[020-053]framed by abundant black hair, was dreamy, and did not contradict the

[020-054]character of the face, which was altogether melancholy. The prominent

[020-055]arch of the upper eyelid, though very beautifully cut, overshadowed

[020-056]the glance of the eye, and added a physical sadness,--if we may so call

[020-057]it,--produced by the droop of the lid over the eyeball. This inward

[020-058]doubt or eclipse--which is put into language by the word modesty--was

[020-059]expressed in his whole person. Perhaps we shall be able to make his

[020-060]appearance better understood if we say that the logic of design required

[020-061]greater length in the oval of his head, more space between the chin,

[020-062]which ended abruptly, and the forehead, which was reduced in height

[020-063]by the way in which the hair grew. The face had, in short, a rather

[020-064]compressed appearance. Hard work had already drawn furrows between the

[020-065]eyebrows, which were somewhat too thick and too near together, like

[020-066]those of a jealous nature. Though La Briere was then slight, he belonged

[020-067]to the class of temperaments which begin, after they are thirty, to take

[020-068]on an unexpected amount of flesh.

[020-069]The young man would have seemed to a student of French history a very

[020-070]fair representative of the royal and almost inconceivable figure of

[020-071]Louis XIII.,--that historical figure of melancholy modesty without

[020-072]known cause; pallid beneath the crown; loving the dangers of war and

[020-073]the fatigues of hunting, but hating work; timid with his mistress to the

[020-074]extent of keeping away from her; so indifferent as to allow the head

[020-075]of his friend to be cut off,--a figure that nothing can explain but his

[020-076]remorse for having avenged his father on his mother. Was he a Catholic

[020-077]Hamlet, or merely the victim of incurable disease? But the undying worm

[020-078]which gnawed at the king’s vitals was in Ernest’s case simply distrust

[020-079]of himself,--the timidity of a man to whom no woman had ever said, “Ah,

[020-080]how I [[love]] thee!” and, above all, the spirit of self-devotion without

[020-081]an object. After hearing the knell of the monarchy in the fall of his

[020-082]patron’s ministry, the poor fellow had next fallen upon a rock covered

[020-083]with exquisite mosses, named Canalis; he was, therefore, still seeking

[020-084]a power to love, and this spaniel-like search for a master gave him

[020-085]outwardly the air of a king who has met with his. This play of feeling,

[020-086]and a general tone of suffering in the young man’s face made it more

[020-087]really beautiful than he was himself aware of; for he had always

[020-088]been annoyed to find himself classed by women among the “handsome

[020-089]disconsolate,”--a class which has passed out of fashion in these days,

[020-090]when every man seeks to blow his own trumpet and put himself in the

[020-091]advance.

[020-092]The self-distrustful Ernest now rested his immediate hopes on the

[020-093]fashionable clothes he intended to wear. He put on, for this sacred

[020-094]interview, where everything depended on a first impression, a pair

[020-095]of black trousers and carefully polished boots, a sulphur-colored

[020-096]waistcoat, which left to sight an exquisitely fine shirt with opal

[020-097]buttons, a black cravat, and a small blue surtout coat which seemed

[020-098]glued to his back and shoulders by some newly-invented process.

[020-099]The ribbon of the Legion of honor was in his buttonhole. He wore a

[020-100]well-fitting pair of kid gloves of the Florentine bronze color, and

[020-101]carried his cane and hat in the left hand with a gesture and air that

[020-102]was worthy of the Grand Monarch, and enabled him to show, as the

[020-103]sacred precincts required, his bare head with the light falling on his

[020-104]carefully arranged hair. He stationed himself before the service began

[020-105]in the church porch, from whence he could examine the church, and the

[020-106]Christians--more particularly the female Christians--who dipped their

[020-107]fingers in the holy water.

[020-108]An inward voice cried to Modeste as she entered, “It is he!” That

[020-109]surtout, and indeed the whole bearing of the young man were essentially

[020-110]Parisian; the ribbon, the gloves, the cane, the very perfume of his hair

[020-111]were not of Havre. So when La Briere turned about to examine the

[020-112]tall and imposing Madame Latournelle, the notary, and the bundled-up

[020-113](expression sacred to women) figure of Modeste, the poor child, though

[020-114]she had carefully tutored herself for the event, received a violent blow

[020-115]on her heart when her eyes rested on this poetic figure, illuminated by

[020-116]the full light of day as it streamed through the open door. She could

[020-117]not be mistaken; a small white rose nearly hid the ribbon of the Legion.

[020-118]Would he recognize his unknown mistress muffled in an old bonnet with

[020-119]a double veil? Modeste was so in fear of love’s clairvoyance that she

[020-120]began to stoop in her walk like an old woman.

[020-121]“Wife,” said little Latournelle as they took their seats, “that

[020-122]gentleman does not belong to Havre.”

[020-123]“So many strangers come here,” answered his wife.

[020-124]“But,” said the notary, “strangers never come to look at a church like

[020-125]ours, which is less than two centuries old.”

[020-126]Ernest remained in the porch throughout the service without seeing any

[020-127]woman who realized his hopes. Modeste, on her part, could not control

[020-128]the trembling of her limbs until Mass was nearly over. She was in the

[020-129]grasp of a joy that none but she herself could depict. At last she heard

[020-130]the foot-fall of a gentleman on the pavement of the aisle. The service

[020-131]over, La Briere was making a circuit of the church, where no one now

[020-132]remained but the punctiliously pious, whom he proceeded to subject to

[020-133]a shrewd and keen analysis. Ernest noticed that a prayer-book shook

[020-134]violently in the hands of a veiled woman as he passed her; as she alone

[020-135]kept her face hidden his suspicions were aroused, and then confirmed by

[020-136]Modeste’s dress, which the lover’s eye now scanned and noted. He left

[020-137]the church with the Latournelles and followed them at a distance to

[020-138]the rue Royale, where he saw them enter a house accompanied by Modeste,

[020-139]whose custom it was to stay with her friends till the hour of vespers.

[020-140]After examining the little house, which was ornamented with scutcheons,

[020-141]he asked the name of the owner, and was told that he was Monsieur

[020-142]Latournelle, the chief notary in Havre. As Ernest lounged along the rue

[020-143]Royale hoping for a glimpse into the house, Modeste caught sight of him,

[020-144]and thereupon declared herself too ill to go to vespers. Poor

[020-145]Ernest thus had his trouble for his pains. He dared not wander about

[020-146][[ingouville|Ingouville]]; moreover, he made it a point of honor to obey orders, and

[020-147]he therefore went back to [[paris|Paris]], previously writing a letter which

[020-148]Francoise Cochet duly delivered on the morrow with the Havre postmark.

[020-149]It was the custom of Monsieur and Madame Latournelle to dine at the

[020-150]Chalet every Sunday when they brought back Modeste after vespers. So, as

[020-151]soon as the invalid felt a little better, they started for Ingouville,

[020-152]accompanied by Butscha. Once at home, the happy Modeste forgot her

[020-153]pretended illness and her disguise, and dressed herself charmingly,

[020-154]humming as she came down to dinner,--

[020-155]“Nought is sleeping--Heart! awaking,

[020-156]Lift thine incense to the skies.”

[020-157]Butscha shuddered slightly when he caught sight of her, so changed did

[020-158]she seem to him. The wings of love were fastened to her shoulders; she

[020-159]had the air of a nymph, a Psyche; her cheeks glowed with the divine

[020-160]color of happiness.

[020-161]“Who wrote the words to which you have put that pretty [[music]]?” asked her

[020-162]mother.

[020-163]“Canalis, mamma,” she answered, flushing rosy red from her throat to her

[020-164]forehead.

[020-165]“Canalis!” cried the dwarf, to whom the inflections of the girl’s voice

[020-166]and her blush told the only thing of which he was still ignorant. “He,

[020-167]that great poet, does he write songs?”

[020-168]“They are only simple verses,” she said, “which I have ventured to set

[020-169]to German airs.”

[020-170]“No, no,” interrupted Madame Mignon, “the music is your own, my

[020-171]daughter.”

[020-172]Modeste, feeling that she grew more and more crimson, went off into the

[020-173]garden, calling Butscha after her.

[020-174]“You can do me a great service,” she said. “[[dumay|Dumay]] is keeping a secret

[020-175]from my mother and me as to the [[social-mobility|fortune]] which my father is bringing back

[020-176]with him; and I want to know what it is. Did not Dumay send papa when

[020-177]he first went away over five hundred thousand francs? Yes. Well, papa is

[020-178]not the kind of man to stay away four years and only double his capital.

[020-179]It seems he is coming back on a ship of his own, and Dumay’s share

[020-180]amounts to almost six hundred thousand francs.”
