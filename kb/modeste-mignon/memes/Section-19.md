# Section 19

[019-001]“Well, what’s the matter with you, Butscha?” cried Madame Latournelle;

[019-002]“one would really think you hadn’t a friend in the world.”

[019-003]Tears shone in the eyes of the poor fellow, who was the son of a Swedish

[019-004]sailor, and whose mother was dead.

[019-005]“I have no one in the world but you,” he answered with a troubled voice;

[019-006]“and your compassion is so much a part of your religion that I can never

[019-007]lose it--and I will never deserve to lose it.”

[019-008]This answer struck the sensitive chord of true delicacy in the minds of

[019-009]all present.

[019-010]“We [[love]] you, Monsieur Butscha,” said Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], with much feeling

[019-011]in her voice.

[019-012]“I’ve six hundred thousand francs of my own, this day,” cried [[dumay|Dumay]],

[019-013]“and you shall be a notary and the successor of Latournelle.”

[019-014]The American wife took the hand of the poor hunchback and pressed it.

[019-015]“What! you have six hundred thousand francs!” exclaimed Latournelle,

[019-016]pricking up his ears as Dumay let fall the words; “and you allow these

[019-017]ladies to live as they do! [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] ought to have a fine horse; and why

[019-018]doesn’t she continue to take lessons in [[music]], and painting, and--”

[019-019]“Why, he has only had the money a few hours!” cried the little wife.

[019-020]“Hush!” murmured Madame Mignon.

[019-021]While these words were exchanged, Butscha’s august mistress turned

[019-022]towards him, preparing to make a speech:--

[019-023]“My son,” she said, “you are so surrounded by true affection that I

[019-024]never thought how my thoughtless use of that familiar phrase might be

[019-025]construed; but you must thank me for my little blunder, because it has

[019-026]served to show you what friends your noble qualities have won.”

[019-027]“Then you must have news from Monsieur Mignon,” resumed the notary.

[019-028]“He is on his way home,” said Madame Mignon; “but let us keep the secret

[019-029]to ourselves. When my husband learns how faithful Butscha has been

[019-030]to us, how he has shown us the warmest and the most disinterested

[019-031]friendship when others have given us the cold shoulder, he will not let

[019-032]you alone provide for him, Dumay. And so, my friend,” she added, turning

[019-033]her blind face toward Butscha; “you can begin at once to negotiate with

[019-034]Latournelle.”

[019-035]“He’s of legal age, twenty-five and a half years. As for me, it will

[019-036]be paying a debt, my boy, to make the purchase easy for you,” said the

[019-037]notary.

[019-038]Butscha was kissing Madame Mignon’s hand, and his face was wet with

[019-039]tears as Modeste opened the door of the salon.

[019-040]“What are you doing to my Black Dwarf?” she demanded. “Who is making him

[019-041]unhappy?”

[019-042]“Ah! Mademoiselle Mignon, do we luckless fellows, cradled in misfortune,

[019-043]ever weep for grief? They have just shown me as much affection as I

[019-044]could feel for them if they were indeed my own relations. I’m to be a

[019-045]notary; I shall be rich. Ha! ha! the poor Butscha may become the rich

[019-046]Butscha. You don’t know what audacity there is in this abortion,” he

[019-047]cried.

[019-048]With that he gave himself a resounding blow on the cavity of his chest

[019-049]and took up a position before the fireplace, after casting a glance at

[019-050]Modeste, which slipped like a ray of light between his heavy half-closed

[019-051]eyelids. He perceived, in this unexpected incident, a chance of

[019-052]interrogating the heart of his sovereign. Dumay thought for a moment

[019-053]that the clerk dared to aspire to Modeste, and he exchanged a rapid

[019-054]glance with the others, who understood him, and began to eye the little

[019-055]man with a species of terror mingled with curiosity.

[019-056]“I, too, have my dreams,” said Butscha, not taking his eyes from

[019-057]Modeste.

[019-058]The young girl lowered her eyelids with a movement that was a revelation

[019-059]to the young man.

[019-060]“You love romance,” he said, addressing her. “Let me, in this moment of

[019-061]happiness, tell you mine; and you shall tell me in return whether the

[019-062]conclusion of the tale I have invented for my life is possible. To me

[019-063]wealth would bring greater happiness than to other men; for the highest

[019-064]happiness I can imagine would be to enrich the one I loved. You,

[019-065]mademoiselle, who know so many things, tell me if it is possible for a

[019-066]man to make himself beloved independently of his person, be it handsome

[019-067]or ugly, and for his spirit only?”

[019-068]Modeste raised her eyes and looked at Butscha. It was a piercing and

[019-069]questioning glance; for she shared Dumay’s suspicion of Butscha’s

[019-070]motive.

[019-071]“Let me be rich, and I will seek some beautiful poor girl, abandoned

[019-072]like myself, who has suffered, who knows what misery is. I will write

[019-073]to her and console her, and be her guardian spirit; she shall read my

[019-074]heart, my soul; she shall possess by double wealth, my two wealths,--my

[019-075]gold, delicately offered, and my thought robed in all the splendor which

[019-076]the accident of birth has denied to my grotesque body. But I myself

[019-077]shall remain hidden like the cause that science seeks. God himself may

[019-078]not be glorious to the eye. Well, naturally, the maiden will be curious;

[019-079]she will wish to see me; but I shall tell her that I am a monster of

[019-080]ugliness; I shall picture myself hideous.”

[019-081]At these words Modeste gave Butscha a glance that looked him through and

[019-082]through. If she had said aloud, “What do you know of my love?” she could

[019-083]not have been more explicit.

[019-084]“If I have the [[honor]] of being loved for the poem of my heart, if some

[019-085]day such love may make a woman think me only slightly deformed, I

[019-086]ask you, mademoiselle, shall I not be happier than the handsomest of

[019-087]men,--as happy as a man of genius beloved by some celestial being like

[019-088]yourself.”

[019-089]The color which suffused the young girl’s face told the cripple nearly

[019-090]all he sought to know.

[019-091]“Well, if that be so,” he went on, “if we enrich the one we love, if

[019-092]we please the spirit and withdraw the body, is not that the way to make

[019-093]one’s self beloved? At any rate it is the dream of your poor dwarf,--a

[019-094]dream of yesterday; for to-day your mother gives me the key to future

[019-095]wealth by promising me the means of buying a practice. But before I

[019-096]become another Gobenheim, I seek to know whether this dream could be

[019-097]really carried out. What do you say, mademoiselle, _you_?”

[019-098]Modeste was so astonished that she did not notice the question. The trap

[019-099]of the lover was much better baited than that of the soldier, for the

[019-100]poor girl was rendered speechless.

[019-101]“Poor Butscha!” whispered Madame Latournelle to her husband. “Do you

[019-102]think he is going mad?”

[019-103]“You want to realize the story of Beauty and the Beast,” said Modeste at

[019-104]length; “but you forget that the Beast turned into Prince Charming.”

[019-105]“Do you think so?” said the dwarf. “Now I have always thought that

[019-106]that transformation meant the phenomenon of the soul made visible,

[019-107]obliterating the form under the light of the spirit. If I were not loved

[019-108]I should stay hidden, that is all. You and yours, madame,” he continued,

[019-109]addressing his mistress, “instead of having a dwarf at your service,

[019-110]will now have a life and a [[commerce|fortune]].”

[019-111]So saying, Butscha resumed his seat, remarking to the three

[019-112]whist-players with an assumption of calmness, “Whose deal is it?” but

[019-113]within his soul he whispered sadly to himself: “She wants to be loved

[019-114]for herself; she corresponds with some pretended great man; how far has

[019-115]it gone?”

[019-116]“Dear mamma, it is nearly ten o’clock,” said Modeste.

[019-117]Madame Mignon said good-night to her friends, and went to bed.

[019-118]They who wish to love in secret may have Pyrenean hounds, mothers,

[019-119]Dumays, and Latournelles to spy upon them, and yet not be in any danger;

[019-120]but when it comes to a lover!--ah! that is diamond cut diamond, flame

[019-121]against flame, mind to mind, an equation whose terms are mutual.

[019-122]On Sunday morning Butscha arrived at [[chalet|the Chalet]] before Madame

[019-123]Latournelle, who always came to take Modeste to church, and he proceeded

[019-124]to blockade the house in expectation of the postman.

[019-125]“Have you a letter for Mademoiselle Mignon?” he said to that humble

[019-126]functionary when he appeared.

[019-127]“No, monsieur, none.”

[019-128]“This house has been a good customer to the post of late,” remarked the

[019-129]clerk.

[019-130]“You may well say that,” replied the man.

[019-131]Modeste both heard and saw the little colloquy from her chamber window,

[019-132]where she always posted herself behind the blinds at this particular

[019-133]hour to watch for the postman. She ran downstairs, went into the little

[019-134]garden, and called in an imperative voice:--

[019-135]“Monsieur Butscha!”

[019-136]“Here am I, mademoiselle,” said the cripple, reaching the gate as

[019-137]Modeste herself opened it.

[019-138]“Will you be good enough to tell me whether among your various titles to

[019-139]a woman’s affection you count that of the shameless spying in which you

[019-140]are now engaged?” demanded the girl, endeavoring to crush her slave with

[019-141]the glance and gesture of a queen.

[019-142]“Yes, mademoiselle,” he answered proudly. “Ah! I never expected,”

[019-143]he continued in a low tone, “that the grub could be of service to a

[019-144]star,--but so it is. Would you rather that your mother and Monsieur

[019-145]Dumay and Madame Latournelle had guessed your secret than one, excluded

[019-146]as it were from life, who seeks to be to you one of those flowers that

[019-147]you cut and wear for a moment? They all know you love; but I, I alone,

[019-148]_know how_. Use me as you would a vigilant watch-dog; I will obey you,

[019-149]protect you, and never bark; neither will I condemn you. I ask only

[019-150]to be of service to you. Your father has made Dumay keeper of the

[019-151]hen-roost, take Butscha to watch outside,--poor Butscha, who doesn’t ask

[019-152]for anything, not so much as a bone.”

[019-153]“Well, I’ve give you a trial,” said Modeste, whose strongest desire was

[019-154]to get rid of so clever a watcher. “Please go at once to all the hotels

[019-155]in Graville and in [[provincial-life|Havre]], and ask if a gentleman has arrived from

[019-156]England named Monsieur Arthur--”

[019-157]“Listen to me, mademoiselle,” said Butscha, interrupting Modeste

[019-158]respectfully. “I will go and take a walk on the seashore, for you don’t

[019-159]want me to go to church to-day; that’s what it is.”

[019-160]Modeste looked at her dwarf with a perfectly stupid astonishment.

[019-161]“Mademoiselle, you have wrapped your face in cotton-wool and a silk

[019-162]handkerchief, but there’s nothing the matter with you; and you have put

[019-163]that thick veil on your bonnet to see some one yourself without being

[019-164]seen.”

[019-165]“Where did you acquire all that perspicacity?” cried Modeste, blushing.

[019-166]“Moreover, mademoiselle, you have not put on your corset; a cold in the

[019-167]head wouldn’t oblige you to disfigure your waist and wear half a dozen

[019-168]petticoats, nor hide your hands in these old gloves, and your pretty

[019-169]feet in those hideous shoes, nor dress yourself like a beggar-woman,

[019-170]nor--”

[019-171]“That’s enough,” she said. “How am I to be certain that you will obey

[019-172]me?”

[019-173]“My master is obliged to go to Sainte-Adresse. He does not like it, but

[019-174]he is so truly good he won’t deprive me of my Sunday; I will offer to go

[019-175]for him.”

[019-176]“Go, and I will trust you.”

[019-177]“You are sure I can do nothing for you in Havre?”

[019-178]“Nothing. Hear me, mysterious dwarf,--look,” she continued, pointing to

[019-179]the cloudless sky; “can you see a single trace of that bird that flew

[019-180]by just now? No; well then, my actions are pure as the air is pure, and

[019-181]leave no stain behind them. You may reassure Dumay and the Latournelles,

[019-182]and my mother. That hand,” she said, holding up a pretty delicate hand,

[019-183]with the points of the rosy fingers, through which the light shone,

[019-184]slightly turning back, “will never be given, it will never even be

[019-185]kissed by what people call a lover until my father has returned.”

[019-186]“Why don’t you want me in the church to-day?”
