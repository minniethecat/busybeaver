# Section 31

[031-001]“You?”

[031-002]“I.”

[031-003]“And how, pray?”

[031-004]“Ha, that’s it!” said the little hunchback, plucking a blade of grass.

[031-005]“See here! this herb believes that men build palaces for it to grow in;

[031-006]it wedges its way between the closest blocks of marble, and brings

[031-007]them down, just as the masses forced into the edifice of feudality have

[031-008]brought it to the ground. The power of the feeble life that can creep

[031-009]everywhere is greater than that of the mighty behind their cannons. I

[031-010]am one of three who have sworn that [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] shall be happy, and we would

[031-011]sell our [[honor]] for her. Adieu, monsieur. If you truly [[love]] Mademoiselle

[031-012]de La Bastie, forget this conversation and shake hands with me, for I

[031-013]think you’ve got a heart. I longed to see [[chalet|the Chalet]], and I got here

[031-014]just as SHE was putting out her light. I saw the dogs rush at you, and

[031-015]I overheard your words, and that is why I take the liberty of saying we

[031-016]serve in the same regiment--that of loyal devotion.”

[031-017]“Monsieur,” said La Briere, wringing the hunchback’s hand, “would you

[031-018]have the friendliness to tell me if Mademoiselle Modeste ever loved any

[031-019]one WITH LOVE before she wrote to [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]?”

[031-020]“Oh!” exclaimed Butscha in an altered voice; “that thought is an insult.

[031-021]And even now, who knows if she really loves? does she know herself?

[031-022]She is enamored of genius, of the soul and intellect of that seller of

[031-023]verses, that literary quack; but she will study him, we shall all study

[031-024]him; and I know how to make the man’s real character peep out from under

[031-025]that turtle-shell of fine manners,--we’ll soon see the petty little head

[031-026]of his ambition and his vanity!” cried Butscha, rubbing his hands. “So,

[031-027]unless mademoiselle is desperately taken with him--”

[031-028]“Oh! she was seized with admiration when she saw him, as if he were

[031-029]something marvellous,” exclaimed La Briere, letting the secret of his

[031-030]jealousy escape him.

[031-031]“If he is a loyal, honest fellow, and loves her; if he is worthy of

[031-032]her; if he renounces his duchess,” said Butscha,--“then I’ll manage the

[031-033]duchess! Here, my dear sir, take this road, and you will get home in ten

[031-034]minutes.”

[031-035]But as they parted, Butscha turned back and hailed poor [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], who,

[031-036]as a true lover, would gladly have stayed there all night talking of

[031-037]Modeste.

[031-038]“Monsieur,” said Butscha, “I have not yet had the honor of seeing our

[031-039]great [[poetry|poet]]. I am very curious to observe that magnificent phenomenon

[031-040]in the exercise of his functions. Do me the favor to bring him to the

[031-041]Chalet to-morrow evening, and stay as long as possible; for it takes

[031-042]more than an hour for a man to show himself for what he is. I shall be

[031-043]the first to see if he loves, if he can love, or if he ever will love

[031-044]Mademoiselle Modeste.”

[031-045]“You are very young to--”

[031-046]“--to be a professor,” said Butscha, cutting short La Briere. “Ha,

[031-047]monsieur, deformed folks are born a hundred years old. And besides, a

[031-048]sick man who has long been sick, knows more than his doctor; he knows

[031-049]the disease, and that is more than can be said for the best of doctors.

[031-050]Well, so it is with a man who cherishes a woman in his heart when the

[031-051]woman is forced to disdain him for his ugliness or his deformity; he

[031-052]ends by knowing so much of love that he becomes seductive, just as the

[031-053]sick man recovers his health; stupidity alone is incurable. I have

[031-054]had neither father nor mother since I was six years old; I am now

[031-055]twenty-five. Public charity has been my mother, the procureur du roi my

[031-056]father. Oh! don’t be troubled,” he added, seeing Ernest’s gesture; “I am

[031-057]much more lively than my situation. Well, for the last six years, ever

[031-058]since a woman’s eye first told me I had no right to love, I do love, and

[031-059]I study women. I began with the ugly ones, for it is best to take the

[031-060]bull by the horns. So I took my master’s wife, who has certainly been

[031-061]an angel to me, for my first study. Perhaps I did wrong; but I couldn’t

[031-062]help it. I passed her through my alembic and what did I find? this

[031-063]thought, crouching at the bottom of her heart, ‘I am not so ugly as they

[031-064]think me’; and if a man were to work upon that thought he could bring

[031-065]her to the edge of the abyss, pious as she is.”

[031-066]“And have you studied Modeste?”

[031-067]“I thought I told you,” replied Butscha, “that my life belongs to her,

[031-068]just as France belongs to the king. Do you now understand what you

[031-069]called my spying in [[paris|Paris]]? No one but me really knows what nobility,

[031-070]what pride, what devotion, what mysterious grace, what unwearying

[031-071]kindness, what true religion, gaiety, wit, delicacy, knowledge, and

[031-072]courtesy there are in the soul and in the heart of that adorable

[031-073]creature!”

[031-074]Butscha drew out his handkerchief and wiped his eyes, and La Briere

[031-075]pressed his hand for a long time.

[031-076]“I live in the sunbeam of her existence; it comes from her, it is

[031-077]absorbed in me; that is how we are united,--as nature is to God, by the

[031-078]Light and by the Word. Adieu, monsieur; never in my life have I talked

[031-079]in this way; but seeing you beneath her windows, I felt in my heart that

[031-080]you loved her as I love her.”

[031-081]Without waiting for an answer Butscha quitted the poor lover, into whose

[031-082]heart his words had put an inexpressible balm. Ernest resolved to make

[031-083]a friend of him, not suspecting that the chief object of the clerk’s

[031-084]loquacity was to gain communication with some one connected with

[031-085]Canalis. Ernest was rocked to sleep that night by the ebb and flow

[031-086]of thoughts and resolutions and plans for his future conduct, whereas

[031-087]Canalis slept the sleep of the conqueror, which is the sweetest of

[031-088]slumbers after that of the just.

[031-089]At breakfast next morning, the friends agreed to spend the evening

[031-090]of the following day at the Chalet and initiate themselves into the

[031-091]delights of [[provincial-life|provincial]] whist. To get rid of the day they ordered their

[031-092]horses, purchased by Germain at a large price, and started on a voyage

[031-093]of discovery round the country, which was quite as unknown to them

[031-094]as China; for the most foreign thing to Frenchmen in France is France

[031-095]itself.

[031-096]By dint of reflecting on his position as an unfortunate and despised

[031-097]lover, Ernest went through something of the same process as Modeste’s

[031-098]first letter had forced upon him. Though sorrow is said to develop

[031-099]virtue, it only develops it in virtuous persons; that cleaning-out of

[031-100]the conscience takes place only in persons who are by nature clean.

[031-101]La Briere vowed to endure his sufferings in Spartan silence, to act

[031-102]worthily, and give way to no baseness; while Canalis, fascinated by the

[031-103]enormous “dot,” was telling himself to take every means of captivating

[031-104]the heiress. Selfishness and devotion, the key-notes of the two

[031-105]characters, therefore took, by the action of a moral law which is often

[031-106]very odd in its effects, certain measures that were contrary to their

[031-107]respective natures. The selfish man put on self-abnegation; the man who

[031-108]thought chiefly of others took refuge on the Aventinus of pride. That

[031-109]phenomenon is often seen in political life. Men frequently turn their

[031-110]characters wrong side out, and it sometimes happens that the public is

[031-111]unable to tell which is the right side.

[031-112]After dinner the two friends heard of the arrival of the grand equerry,

[031-113]who was presented at the Chalet the same evening by Latournelle.

[031-114]Mademoiselle d’Herouville had contrived to wound that worthy man by

[031-115]sending a footmen to tell him to come to her, instead of sending her

[031-116]nephew in person; thus depriving the notary of a distinguished visit he

[031-117]would certainly have talked about for the rest of his natural life. So

[031-118]Latournelle curtly informed the grand equerry, when he proposed to

[031-119]drive him to the Chalet, that he was engaged to take Madame Latournelle.

[031-120]Guessing from the little man’s sulky manner that there was some blunder

[031-121]to repair, the duke said graciously:--

[031-122]“Then I shall have the pleasure, if you will allow me, of taking Madame

[031-123]Latournelle also.”

[031-124]Disregarding Mademoiselle d’Herouville’s haughty shrug, the duke left

[031-125]the room with the notary. Madame Latournelle, half-crazed with joy at

[031-126]seeing the gorgeous carriage at her door, with footmen in royal livery

[031-127]letting down the steps, was too agitated on hearing that the grand

[031-128]equerry had called for her, to find her gloves, her parasol, her

[031-129]absurdity, or her usual air of pompous dignity. Once in the carriage,

[031-130]however, and while expressing confused thanks and civilities to the

[031-131]little duke, she suddenly exclaimed, from a thought in her kind heart,--

[031-132]“But Butscha, where is he?”

[031-133]“Let us take Butscha,” said the duke, smiling.

[031-134]When the people on the quays, attracted in groups by the splendor of the

[031-135]royal equipage, saw the funny spectacle, the three little men with the

[031-136]spare gigantic woman, they looked at one another and laughed.

[031-137]“If you melt all three together, they might make one man fit to mate

[031-138]with that big cod-fish,” said a sailor from Bordeaux.

[031-139]“Is there any other thing you would like to take with you, madame?”

[031-140]asked the duke, jestingly, while the footman awaited his orders.

[031-141]“No, monseigneur,” she replied, turning scarlet and looking at her

[031-142]husband as much as to say, “What did I do wrong?”

[031-143]“Monsieur le duc honors me by considering that I am a thing,” said

[031-144]Butscha; “a poor clerk is usually thought to be a nonentity.”

[031-145]Though this was said with a laugh, the duke colored and did not answer.

[031-146]Great people are to blame for joking with their social inferiors.

[031-147]Jesting is a game, and games presuppose equality; it is to obviate any

[031-148]inconvenient results of this temporary equality that players have the

[031-149]right, after the game is over, not to recognize each other.

[031-150]The visit of the grand equerry had the ostensible excuse of an important

[031-151]piece of [[commerce|business]]; namely, the retrieval of an immense tract of waste

[031-152]land left by the sea between the mouths of the two rivers, which

[031-153]tract had just been adjudged by the Council of State to the house of

[031-154]Herouville. The matter was nothing less than putting flood-gates with

[031-155]double bridges, draining three or four hundred acres, cutting canals,

[031-156]and laying out roadways. When the duke had explained the condition of

[031-157]the land, [[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]] remarked that time must be allowed for the

[031-158]soil, which was still moving, to settle and grow solid in a natural way.

[031-159]“Time, which has providentially enriched your house, Monsieur le duc,

[031-160]can alone complete the work,” he said, in conclusion. “It would be

[031-161]prudent to let fifty years elapse before you reclaim the land.”

[031-162]“Do not let that be your final word, Monsieur le comte,” said the duke.

[031-163]“Come to Herouville and see things for yourself.”

[031-164]Charles Mignon replied that every capitalist should take time to examine

[031-165]into such matters with a cool head, thus giving the duke a pretext for

[031-166]his visits to the Chalet. The sight of Modeste made a lively impression

[031-167]on the young man, and he asked the favor of receiving her at Herouville

[031-168]with her father, saying that his sister and his aunt had heard much of

[031-169]her, and wished to make her acquaintance. On this the count proposed

[031-170]to present his daughter to those ladies himself, and invited the whole

[031-171]party to dinner on the day of his return to the villa. The duke accepted

[031-172]the invitation. The blue ribbon, the title, and above all, the ecstatic

[031-173]glances of the noble gentleman had an effect upon Modeste; but she

[031-174]appeared to great advantage in carriage, dignity, and conversation. The

[031-175]duke withdrew reluctantly, carrying with him an invitation to visit the

[031-176]Chalet every evening,--an invitation based on the impossibility of a

[031-177]courtier of Charles X. existing for a single evening without his rubber.
