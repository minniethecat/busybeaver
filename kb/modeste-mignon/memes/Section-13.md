# Section 13

[013-001]Ah! you poor women of France, try to remain hidden if you can; try

[013-002]to weave the least little romance about your lives in the midst of

[013-003]a civilization which posts in the public streets the hours when the

[013-004]coaches arrive and depart; which counts all [[epistolary-novel|letters]] and stamps them

[013-005]twice over, first with the hour when they are thrown into the boxes, and

[013-006]next with that of their delivery; which numbers the houses, prints the

[013-007]tax of every tenant on a metal register at the doors (after verifying

[013-008]its particulars), and will soon possess one vast register of every

[013-009]inch of its territory down to the smallest parcel of land, and the most

[013-010]insignificant features of it,--a giant work ordained by a giant. Try,

[013-011]imprudent young ladies, to escape not only the eye of the police, but

[013-012]the incessant chatter which takes place in a country town about the

[013-013]veriest trifles,--how many dishes the prefect has at his dessert,

[013-014]how many slices of melon are left at the door of some small

[013-015]householder,--which strains its ear to catch the chink of the gold a

[013-016]thrifty man lays by, and spends its evenings in calculating the incomes

[013-017]of the village and the town and the department. It was mere chance

[013-018]that enabled [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] to escape discovery through [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]]’s reconnoitring

[013-019]expedition,--a step which he already regretted; but what Parisian can

[013-020]allow himself to be the dupe of a little country girl? Incapable of

[013-021]being duped! that horrid maxim is the dissolvent of all noble sentiments

[013-022]in man.

[013-023]We can readily guess the struggle of feeling to which this honest young

[013-024]fellow fell a prey when we read the letter that he now indited, in which

[013-025]every stroke of the flail which scourged his conscience will be found to

[013-026]have left its trace.

[013-027]This is what Modeste read a few days later, as she sat by her window on

[013-028]a fine summer’s day:--

[013-029]Mademoiselle,--Without hypocrisy or evasion, _yes_, if I had been

[013-030]certain that you possessed an immense [[commerce|fortune]] I should have acted

[013-031]differently. Why? I have searched for the reason; here it is. We

[013-032]have within us an inborn feeling, inordinately developed by social

[013-033]life, which drives us to the pursuit and to the possession of

[013-034]happiness. Most men confound happiness with the means that lead to

[013-035]it; money in their eyes is the chief element of happiness. I

[013-036]should, therefore, have endeavored to win you, prompted by that

[013-037]social sentiment which has in all ages made wealth a religion. At

[013-038]least, I think I should. It is not to be expected of a man still

[013-039]young that he can have the wisdom to substitute sound sense for

[013-040]the pleasure of the senses; within sight of a prey the brutal

[013-041]instincts hidden in the heart of man drive him on. Instead of that

[013-042]lesson, I should have sent you compliments and flatteries. Should

[013-043]I have kept my own esteem in so doing? I doubt it. Mademoiselle,

[013-044]in such a case success brings absolution; but happiness? That is

[013-045]another thing. Should I have distrusted my wife had I won her in

[013-046]that way? Most assuredly I should. Your advance on me would sooner

[013-047]or later have come between us. Your husband, however grand your

[013-048]fancy may make him, would have ended by reproaching you for having

[013-049]abased him. You, yourself, might have come, sooner or later, to

[013-050]despise him. The strong man forgives, but [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]] whines. Such,

[013-051]mademoiselle, is the answer which my honesty compels me to make to

[013-052]you.

[013-053]And now, listen to me. You have the triumph of forcing me to

[013-054]reflect deeply,--first on you, whom I do not sufficiently know;

[013-055]next, on myself, of whom I knew too little. You have had the power

[013-056]to stir up many of the evil thoughts which crouched in my heart,

[013-057]as in all hearts; but from them something good and generous has

[013-058]come forth, and I salute you with my most fervent benedictions,

[013-059]just as at sea we salute the lighthouse which shows the rocks on

[013-060]which we were about to perish. Here is my confession, for I would

[013-061]not lose your esteem nor my own for all the treasures of earth.

[013-062]I wished to know who you are. I have just returned from [[provincial-life|Havre]],

[013-063]where I saw Francoise Cochet, and followed her to [[ingouville|Ingouville]]. You

[013-064]are as beautiful as the woman of a [[poetry|poet]]’s dream; but I do not know

[013-065]if you are Mademoiselle Vilquin concealed under Mademoiselle

[013-066]d’Herouville, or Mademoiselle d’Herouville hidden under

[013-067]Mademoiselle Vilquin. Though all is fair in war, I blushed at such

[013-068]spying and stopped short in my inquiries. You have roused my

[013-069]curiosity; forgive me for being somewhat of a woman; it is, I

[013-070]believe, the privilege of a poet.

[013-071]Now that I have laid bare my heart and allowed you to read it, you

[013-072]will believe in the sincerity of what I am about to add. Though

[013-073]the glimpse I had of you was all too rapid, it has sufficed to

[013-074]modify my opinion of your conduct. You are a poet and a poem, even

[013-075]more than you are a woman. Yes, there is in you something more

[013-076]precious than beauty; you are the beautiful Ideal of art, of

[013-077]fancy. The step you took, blamable as it would be in an ordinary

[013-078]young girl, allotted to an every-day destiny, has another aspect

[013-079]if endowed with the nature which I now attribute to you. Among the

[013-080]crowd of beings flung by fate into the social life of this planet

[013-081]to make up a generation there are exceptional souls. If your

[013-082]letter is the outcome of long poetic reveries on the fate which

[013-083]conventions bring to women, if, constrained by the impulse of a

[013-084]lofty and intelligent mind, you have wished to understand the life

[013-085]of a man to whom you attribute the gift of genius, to the end that

[013-086]you may create a friendship withdrawn from the ordinary relations

[013-087]of life, with a soul in communion with your own, disregarding thus

[013-088]the ordinary trammels of your sex,--then, assuredly, you are an

[013-089]exception. The law which rightly limits the actions of the crowd

[013-090]is too limited for you. But in that case, the remark in my first

[013-091]letter returns in greater force,--you have done too much or not

[013-092]enough.

[013-093]Accept once more my thanks for the service you have rendered me,

[013-094]that of compelling me to sound my heart. You have corrected in me

[013-095]the false idea, only too common in France, that [[marriage]] should be

[013-096]a means of [[social-mobility|fortune]]. While I struggled with my conscience a sacred

[013-097]voice spoke to me. I swore solemnly to make my fortune myself, and

[013-098]not be led by motives of cupidity in choosing the companion of my

[013-099]life. I have also reproached myself for the blamable curiosity you

[013-100]have excited in me. You have not six millions. There is no

[013-101]concealment possible in Havre for a young lady who possesses such

[013-102]a fortune; you would be discovered at once by the pack of hounds

[013-103]of great families whom I see in [[paris|Paris]] on the hunt after heiresses,

[013-104]and who have already sent one, the grand equerry, the young duke,

[013-105]among the Vilquins. Therefore, believe me, the sentiments I have

[013-106]now expressed are fixed in my mind as a rule of life, from which I

[013-107]have abstracted all influences of romance or of actual fact. Prove

[013-108]to me, therefore, that you have one of those souls which may be

[013-109]forgiven for its disobedience to the common law, by perceiving and

[013-110]comprehending the spirit of this letter as you did that of my

[013-111]first letter. If you are destined to a middle-class life, obey the

[013-112]iron law which holds society together. Lifted in mind above other

[013-113]women, I admire you; but if you seek to obey an impulse which you

[013-114]ought to repress, I pity you. The all-wise moral of that great

[013-115]domestic epic “Clarissa Harlowe” is that legitimate and honorable

[013-116][[love]] led the poor victim to her ruin because it was conceived,

[013-117]developed, and pursued beyond the boundaries of family restraint.

[013-118]The family, however cruel and even foolish it may be, is in the

[013-119]right against the Lovelaces. The family is Society. Believe me,

[013-120]the glory of a young girl, of a woman, must always be that of

[013-121]repressing her most ardent impulses within the narrow sphere of

[013-122]conventions. If I had a daughter able to become a Madame de Stael

[013-123]I should wish her dead at fifteen. Can you imagine a daughter of

[013-124]yours flaunting on the stage of fame, exhibiting herself to win

[013-125]the plaudits of a crowd, and not suffer anguish at the thought? No

[013-126]matter to what heights a woman can rise by the inward poetry of

[013-127]her soul, she must sacrifice the outer signs of superiority on the

[013-128]altar of her home. Her impulse, her genius, her aspirations toward

[013-129]Good, the whole poem of a young girl’s being, should belong to the

[013-130]man she accepts and the children whom she brings into the world. I

[013-131]think I perceive in you a secret desire to widen the narrow circle

[013-132]of the life to which all women are condemned, and to put love and

[013-133]passion into marriage. Ah! it is a lovely dream! it is not

[013-134]impossible; it is difficult, but if realized, may it not be to the

[013-135]despair of souls--forgive me the hackneyed word--“incompris”?

[013-136]If you seek a platonic friendship it will be to your sorrow in

[013-137]after years. If your letter was a jest, discontinue it. Perhaps

[013-138]this little romance is to end here--is it? It has not been without

[013-139]fruit. My sense of duty is aroused, and you, on your side, will

[013-140]have learned something of Society. Turn your thoughts to real

[013-141]life; throw the enthusiasms you have culled from literature into

[013-142]the virtues of your sex.

[013-143]Adieu, mademoiselle. Do me the [[honor]] to grant me your esteem.

[013-144]Having seen you, or one whom I believe to be you, I have known

[013-145]that your letter was simply natural; a flower so lovely turns to

[013-146]the sun--of poetry. Yes, love poetry as you love flowers, [[music]],

[013-147]the grandeur of the sea, the beauties of nature; love them as an

[013-148]adornment of the soul, but remember what I have had the honor of

[013-149]telling you as to the nature of poets. Be cautious not to marry,

[013-150]as you say, a dunce, but seek the partner whom God has made for

[013-151]you. There are souls, believe me, who are fit to appreciate you,

[013-152]and to make you happy. If I were rich, if you were poor, I would

[013-153]lay my heart and my fortunes at your feet; for I believe your soul

[013-154]to be full of riches and of loyalty; to you I could confide my

[013-155]life and my honor in absolute security.

[013-156]Once more, adieu, adieu, fairest daughter of Eve the fair.

[013-157]The reading of this letter, swallowed like a drop of water in the

[013-158]desert, lifted the mountain which weighed heavily on Modeste’s heart:

[013-159]then she saw the mistake she had made in arranging her plan, and

[013-160]repaired it by giving Francoise some envelopes directed to herself, in

[013-161]which the maid could put the letters which came from Paris and drop them

[013-162]again into the box. Modeste resolved to receive the postman herself on

[013-163]the steps of [[chalet|the Chalet]] at the hour when he made his delivery.

[013-164]As to the feelings that this reply, in which the noble heart of poor

[013-165]La Briere beat beneath the brilliant phantom of Canalis, excited in

[013-166]Modeste, they were as multifarious and confused as the waves which

[013-167]rushed to die along the shore while with her eyes fixed on the wide

[013-168]ocean she gave herself up to the joy of having (if we dare say so)

[013-169]harpooned an angelic soul in the Parisian Gulf, of having divined that

[013-170]hearts of price might still be found in harmony with genius, and, above

[013-171]all, for having followed the magic voice of intuition.
