# The Betrothal

Modeste Mignon's betrothal to Ernest de La Brière — the resolution of the
letter-plot deception and the novel's movement from romantic illusion to mature
commitment.

## Summary

After the revelation that Ernest, not Canalis, authored the letters that won her
heart, Modeste Mignon accepts Ernest de La Brière as her betrothed. The
engagement takes place at the Chalet with the consent of Charles Mignon, who has
returned from exile a wealthy man and recognizes in Ernest the integrity his
daughter deserves. The betrothal represents not merely a marriage plot resolution
but Balzac's argument that true love must be grounded in authentic character
rather than idealized image.

## Context

The betrothal is made possible by the unraveling of Canalis's deception, Charles
Mignon's return with his restored fortune, and Modeste's own moral education
through the crisis of the correspondence. Ernest's quiet virtue — his refusal to
claim the letters, his willingness to lose Modeste rather than deceive her
further — proves him worthy. Charles Mignon, who has built a fortune through
shrewd judgment, applies the same discernment to his daughter's suitor.

## Consequences

The betrothal closes the novel's central conflict and establishes the social
order of the Chalet on a new foundation. It rewards Ernest's integrity over
Canalis's celebrity, affirming the novel's moral thesis. The engagement also
signals the Mignon family's full restoration: Charles Mignon's return, his
recovered fortune, and his daughter's worthy marriage form a triple resolution.

## Narrative Function

As the novel's denouement, the betrothal transforms the romantic comedy of
mistaken identity into a realist assertion: love succeeds not through poetic
idealism but through the recognition of genuine worth. Balzac uses the
engagement to close the gap between epistolary romance and practical marriage,
a recurring concern across *La Comédie Humaine*.
