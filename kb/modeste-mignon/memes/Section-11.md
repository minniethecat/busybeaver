# Section 11

[011-001]But after the arrival of [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s letter [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] deceived himself no

[011-002]longer as to [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]. The pair had just finished breakfast and were

[011-003]talking together in the [[poetry|poet]]’s study, which was on the ground-floor of a

[011-004]house standing back in a court-yard, and looked into a garden.

[011-005]“There!” exclaimed Canalis, “I was telling Madame de Chaulieu the

[011-006]other day that I ought to bring out another poem; I knew admiration was

[011-007]running short, for I have had no anonymous [[epistolary-novel|letters]] for a long time.”

[011-008]“Is it from an unknown woman?”

[011-009]“Unknown? yes!--a D’Este, in [[provincial-life|Havre]]; evidently a feigned name.”

[011-010]Canalis passed the letter to La Briere. The little poem, with all its

[011-011]hidden enthusiasms, in short, poor Modeste’s heart, was disdainfully

[011-012]handed over, with the gesture of a spoiled dandy.

[011-013]“It is a fine thing,” said the lawyer, “to have the power to attract

[011-014]such feelings; to force a poor woman to step out of the habits which

[011-015]nature, education, and the world dictate to her, to break through

[011-016]conventions. What privileges genius wins! A letter such as this, written

[011-017]by a young girl--a genuine young girl--without hidden meanings, with

[011-018]real enthusiasm--”

[011-019]“Well, what?” said Canalis.

[011-020]“Why, a man might suffer as much as Tasso and yet feel recompensed,”

[011-021]cried La Briere.

[011-022]“So he might, my dear fellow, by a first letter of that kind, and even a

[011-023]second; but how about the thirtieth? And suppose you find out that these

[011-024]young enthusiasts are little jades? Or imagine a poet rushing along the

[011-025]brilliant path in search of her, and finding at the end of it an old

[011-026]Englishwoman sitting on a mile-stone and offering you her hand! Or

[011-027]suppose this post-office angel should really be a rather ugly girl in

[011-028]quest of a husband? Ah, my boy! the effervescence then goes down.”

[011-029]“I begin to perceive,” said La Briere, smiling, “that there is something

[011-030]poisonous in glory, as there is in certain dazzling flowers.”

[011-031]“And then,” resumed Canalis, “all these women, even when they are

[011-032]simple-minded, have ideals, and you can’t satisfy them. They never say

[011-033]to themselves that a poet is a vain man, as I am accused of being; they

[011-034]can’t conceive what it is for an author to be at the mercy of a feverish

[011-035]excitement, which makes him disagreeable and capricious; they want him

[011-036]always grand, noble; it never occurs to them that genius is a disease,

[011-037]or that Nathan lives with Florine; that D’Arthez is too fat, and Joseph

[011-038]Bridau is too thin; that Beranger limps, and that their own particular

[011-039]deity may have the snuffles! A Lucien de Rubempre, poet and cupid, is a

[011-040]phoenix. And why should I go in search of compliments only to pull the

[011-041]string of a shower-bath of horrid looks from some disillusioned female?”

[011-042]“Then the true poet,” said La Briere, “ought to remain hidden, like God,

[011-043]in the centre of his worlds, and be only seen in his own creations.”

[011-044]“Glory would cost too dear in that case,” answered Canalis. “There is

[011-045]some good in life. As for that letter,” he added, taking a cup of tea,

[011-046]“I assure you that when a noble and beautiful woman loves a poet she

[011-047]does not hide in the corner boxes, like a duchess in [[love]] with an actor;

[011-048]she feels that her beauty, her [[commerce|fortune]], her name are protection enough,

[011-049]and she dares to say openly, like an epic poem: ‘I am the nymph Calypso,

[011-050]enamored of Telemachus.’ Mystery and feigned names are the resources of

[011-051]little minds. For my part I no longer answer masks--”

[011-052]“I should love a woman who came to seek me,” cried La Briere. “To all

[011-053]you say I reply, my dear Canalis, that it cannot be an ordinary girl who

[011-054]aspires to a distinguished man; such a girl has too little trust, too

[011-055]much vanity; she is too faint-hearted. Only a star, a--”

[011-056]“--princess!” cried Canalis, bursting into a shout of laughter; “only a

[011-057]princess can descend to him. My dear fellow, that doesn’t happen once

[011-058]in a hundred years. Such a love is like that flower that blossoms every

[011-059]century. Princesses, let me tell you, if they are young, rich, and

[011-060]beautiful, have something else to think of; they are surrounded

[011-061]like rare plants by a hedge of fools, well-bred idiots as hollow as

[011-062]elder-bushes! My dream, alas! the crystal of my dream, garlanded from

[011-063]hence to the Correze with roses--ah! I cannot speak of it--it is in

[011-064]fragments at my feet, and has long been so. No, no, all anonymous

[011-065]letters are begging letters; and what sort of begging? Write yourself to

[011-066]that young woman, if you suppose her young and pretty, and you’ll find

[011-067]out. There is nothing like experience. As for me, I can’t reasonably be

[011-068]expected to love every woman; Apollo, at any rate he of Belvedere, is a

[011-069]delicate consumptive who must take care of his health.”

[011-070]“But when a woman writes to you in this way her excuse must certainly

[011-071]be in her consciousness that she is able to eclipse in tenderness and

[011-072]beauty every other woman,” said Ernest, “and I should think you might

[011-073]feel some curiosity--”

[011-074]“Ah,” said Canalis, “permit me, my juvenile friend, to abide by the

[011-075]beautiful duchess who is all my joy.”

[011-076]“You are right, you are right!” cried Ernest. However, the young

[011-077]secretary read and re-read Modeste’s letter, striving to guess the mind

[011-078]of its hidden writer.

[011-079]“There is not the least fine-writing here,” he said, “she does not even

[011-080]talk of your genius; she speaks to your heart. In your place I should

[011-081]feel tempted by this fragrance of modesty,--this proposed agreement--”

[011-082]“Then, sign it!” cried Canalis, laughing; “answer the letter and go to

[011-083]the end of the adventure yourself. You shall tell me the results three

[011-084]months hence--if the affair lasts so long.”

[011-085]Four days later Modeste received the following letter, written on

[011-086]extremely fine paper, protected by two envelopes, and sealed with the

[011-087]arms of Canalis.

[011-088]Mademoiselle,--The admiration for fine works (allowing that my

[011-089]books are such) implies something so lofty and sincere as to

[011-090]protect you from all light jesting, and to justify before the

[011-091]sternest judge the step you have taken in writing to me.

[011-092]But first I must thank you for the pleasure which such proofs of

[011-093]sympathy afford, even though we may not merit them,--for the maker

[011-094]of verses and the true poet are equally certain of the intrinsic

[011-095]worth of their writings,--so readily does self-esteem lend itself

[011-096]to praise. The best proof of friendship that I can give to an

[011-097]unknown lady in exchange for a faith which allays the sting of

[011-098]criticism, is to share with her the harvest of my own experience,

[011-099]even at the risk of dispelling her most vivid illusions.

[011-100]Mademoiselle, the noblest adornment of a young girl is the flower

[011-101]of a pure and saintly and irreproachable life. Are you alone in

[011-102]the world? If you are, there is no need to say more. But if you

[011-103]have a family, a father or a mother, think of all the sorrow that

[011-104]might come to them from such a letter as yours addressed to a poet

[011-105]of whom you know nothing personally. All writers are not angels;

[011-106]they have many defects. Some are frivolous, heedless, foppish,

[011-107]ambitious, dissipated; and, believe me, no matter how imposing

[011-108]innocence may be, how chivalrous a poet is, you will meet with

[011-109]many a degenerate troubadour in [[paris|Paris]] ready to cultivate your

[011-110]affection only to betray it. By such a man your letter would be

[011-111]interpreted otherwise than it is by me. He would see a thought

[011-112]that is not in it, which you, in your innocence, have not

[011-113]suspected. There are as many natures as there are writers. I am

[011-114]deeply flattered that you have judged me capable of understanding

[011-115]you; but had you, perchance, fallen upon a hypocrite, a scoffer,

[011-116]one whose books may be melancholy but whose life is a perpetual

[011-117]carnival, you would have found as the result of your generous

[011-118]imprudence an evil-minded man, the frequenter of green-rooms,

[011-119]perhaps a hero of some gay resort. In the bower of clematis where

[011-120]you dream of poets, can you smell the odor of the cigar which

[011-121]drives all poetry from the manuscript?

[011-122]But let us look still further. How could the dreamy, solitary life

[011-123]you lead, doubtless by the sea-shore, interest a poet, whose

[011-124]mission it is to imagine all, and to paint all? What reality can

[011-125]equal imagination? The young girls of the poets are so [[the-ideal|ideal]] that

[011-126]no living daughter of Eve can compete with them. And now tell me,

[011-127]what will you gain,--you, a young girl, brought up to be the

[011-128]virtuous mother of a family,--if you learn to comprehend the

[011-129]terrible agitations of a poet’s life in this dreadful capital,

[011-130]which may be defined by one sentence,--the hell in which men love.

[011-131]If the desire to brighten the monotonous existence of a young girl

[011-132]thirsting for knowledge has led you to take your pen in hand and

[011-133]write to me, has not the step itself the appearance of

[011-134]degradation? What meaning am I to give to your letter? Are you one

[011-135]of a rejected caste, and do you seek a friend far away from you?

[011-136]Or, are you afflicted with personal ugliness, yet feeling within

[011-137]you a noble soul which can give and receive a confidence? Alas,

[011-138]alas, the conclusion to be drawn is grievous. You have said too

[011-139]much, or too little; you have gone too far, or not far enough.

[011-140]Either let us drop this correspondence, or, if you continue it,

[011-141]tell me more than in the letter you have now written me.

[011-142]But, mademoiselle, if you are young, if you are beautiful, if you

[011-143]have a home, a family, if in your heart you have the precious

[011-144]ointment, the spikenard, to pour out, as did Magdalene on the feet

[011-145]of Jesus, let yourself be won by a man worthy of you; become what

[011-146]every pure young girl should be,--a good woman, the virtuous

[011-147]mother of a family. A poet is the saddest conquest that a girl can

[011-148]make; he is full of vanity, full of angles that will sharply wound

[011-149]a woman’s proper pride, and kill a tenderness which has no

[011-150]experience of life. The wife of a poet should love him long before

[011-151]she marries him; she must train herself to the charity of angels,

[011-152]to their forbearance, to all the virtues of motherhood. Such

[011-153]qualities, mademoiselle, are but germs in a young girl.

[011-154]Hear the whole truth,--do I not owe it to you in return for your

[011-155]intoxicating flattery? If it is a glorious thing to marry a great

[011-156]renown, remember also that you must soon discover a superior man

[011-157]to be, in all that makes a man, like other men. He therefore

[011-158]poorly realizes the hopes that attach to him as a phoenix. He

[011-159]becomes like a woman whose beauty is over-praised, and of whom we

[011-160]say: “I thought her far more lovely.” She has not warranted the

[011-161]portrait painted by the fairy to whom I owe your letter,--the

[011-162]fairy whose name is Imagination.

[011-163]Believe me, the qualities of the mind live and thrive only in a

[011-164]sphere invisible, not in daily life; the wife of a poet bears the

[011-165]burden; she sees the jewels manufactured, but she never wears

[011-166]them. If the glory of the position fascinates you, hear me now

[011-167]when I tell you that its pleasures are soon at an end. You will

[011-168]suffer when you find so many asperities in a nature which, from a

[011-169]distance, you thought equable, and such coldness at the shining

[011-170]summit. Moreover, as women never set their feet within the world

[011-171]of real difficulties, they cease to appreciate what they once

[011-172]admired as soon as they think they see the inner mechanism of it.

[011-173]I close with a last thought, in which there is no disguised

[011-174]entreaty; it is the counsel of a friend. The exchange of souls can

[011-175]take place only between persons who are resolved to hide nothing

[011-176]from each other. Would you show yourself for such as you are to an

[011-177]unknown man? I dare not follow out the consequences of that idea.
