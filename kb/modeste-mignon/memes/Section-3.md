# Section 3

[003-001]Though short, thick-set, pockmarked, and speaking always in a low voice

[003-002]as if listening to himself, this Breton, a former lieutenant in the

[003-003]Guard, showed the evidence of such resolution, such sang-froid on his

[003-004]face that throughout life, even in the army, no one had ever ventured

[003-005]to trifle with him. His little eyes, of a calm blue, were like bits of

[003-006]steel. His ways, the look on his face, his speech, his carriage, were

[003-007]all in keeping with the short name of [[dumay|Dumay]]. His physical strength,

[003-008]well-known to every one, put him above all danger of attack. He was able

[003-009]to kill a man with a blow of his fist, and had performed that feat at

[003-010]Bautzen, where he found himself, unarmed, face to face with a Saxon

[003-011]at the rear of his company. At the present moment the usually firm

[003-012]yet gentle expression of the man’s face had risen to a sort of tragic

[003-013]sublimity; his lips were pale as the rest of his face, indicating a

[003-014]tumult within him mastered by his Breton will; a slight sweat, which

[003-015]every one noticed and guessed to be cold, moistened his brow. The notary

[003-016]knew but too well that these signs might result in a drama before the

[003-017]criminal courts. In fact the cashier was playing a part in connection

[003-018]with [[modeste-mignon|Modeste Mignon]], which involved to his mind sentiments of [[honor]] and

[003-019]loyalty of far greater importance than mere social laws; and his present

[003-020]conduct proceeded from one of those compacts which, in case disaster

[003-021]came of it, could be judged only in a higher court than one of earth.

[003-022]The majority of dramas lie really in the ideas which we make to

[003-023]ourselves about things. Events which seem to us dramatic are nothing

[003-024]more than subjects which our souls convert into tragedy or comedy

[003-025]according to the bent of our characters.

[003-026]Madame Latournelle and Madame Dumay, who were appointed to watch

[003-027]Modeste, had a certain assumed stiffness of demeanor and a quiver in

[003-028]their voices, which the suspected party did not notice, so absorbed

[003-029]was she in her embroidery. Modeste laid each thread of cotton with a

[003-030]precision that would have made an ordinary workwoman desperate. Her face

[003-031]expressed the pleasure she took in the smooth petals of the flower

[003-032]she was working. The dwarf, seated between his mistress and Gobenheim,

[003-033]restrained his emotion, trying to find means to approach Modeste and

[003-034]whisper a word of warning in her ear.

[003-035]By taking a position in front of Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], Madame Latournelle, with

[003-036]the diabolical intelligence of conscientious duty, had isolated Modeste.

[003-037]Madame Mignon, whose blindness always made her silent, was even paler

[003-038]than usual, showing plainly that she was aware of the test to which

[003-039]her daughter was about to be subjected. Perhaps at the last moment she

[003-040]revolted from the stratagem, necessary as it might seem to her. Hence

[003-041]her silence; she was weeping inwardly. Exupere, the spring of the

[003-042]trap, was wholly ignorant of the piece in which he was to play a

[003-043]part. Gobenheim, by reason of his character, remained in a state of

[003-044]indifference equal to that displayed by Modeste. To a spectator who

[003-045]understood the situation, this contrast between the ignorance of some

[003-046]and the palpitating interest of others would have seemed quite [[poetry|poetic]].

[003-047]Nowadays romance-writers arrange such effects; and it is quite within

[003-048]their [[provincial-life|province]] to do so, for nature in all ages takes the liberty to be

[003-049]stronger than they. In this instance, as you will see, nature, social

[003-050]nature, which is a second nature within nature, amused herself by making

[003-051]truth more interesting than fiction; just as mountain torrents describe

[003-052]curves which are beyond the skill of painters to convey, and accomplish

[003-053]giant deeds in displacing or smoothing stones which are the wonder of

[003-054]architects and sculptors.

[003-055]It was eight o’clock. At that season twilight was still shedding its

[003-056]last gleams; there was not a cloud in the sky; the balmy air caressed

[003-057]the earth, the flowers gave forth their fragrance, the steps of

[003-058]pedestrians turning homeward sounded along the gravelly road, the sea

[003-059]shone like a mirror, and there was so little wind that the wax candles

[003-060]upon the card-tables sent up a steady flame, although the windows were

[003-061]wide open. This salon, this evening, this dwelling--what a frame for the

[003-062]portrait of the young girl whom these persons were now studying with the

[003-063]profound attention of a painter in presence of the Margharita Doni, one

[003-064]of the glories of the Pitti palace. Modeste,--blossom enclosed, like

[003-065]that of Catullus,--was she worth all these precautions?

[003-066]You have seen the cage; behold the bird! Just twenty years of age,

[003-067]slender and delicate as the sirens which English designers invent for

[003-068]their “Books of Beauty,” Modeste was, like her mother before her, the

[003-069]captivating embodiment of a grace too little understood in France, where

[003-070]we choose to call it sentimentality, but which among German women is

[003-071]the poetry of the heart coming to the surface of the being and spending

[003-072]itself--in affectations if the owner is silly, in divine charms of

[003-073]manner if she is “spirituelle” and intelligent. Remarkable for her pale

[003-074]golden hair, Modeste belonged to the type of woman called, perhaps in

[003-075]memory of Eve, the celestial blonde; whose satiny skin is like a silk

[003-076]paper applied to the flesh, shuddering at the winter of a cold look,

[003-077]expanding in the sunshine of a loving glance,--teaching the hand to be

[003-078]jealous of the eye. Beneath her hair, which was soft and feathery and

[003-079]worn in many curls, the brow, which might have been traced by a compass

[003-080]so pure was its modelling, shone forth discreet, calm to placidity,

[003-081]and yet luminous with thought: when and where could another be found so

[003-082]transparently clear or more exquisitely smooth? It seemed, like a pearl,

[003-083]to have its orient. The eyes, of a blue verging on gray and limpid

[003-084]as the eyes of a child, had all the mischief, all the innocence of

[003-085]childhood, and they harmonized well with the arch of the eyebrows,

[003-086]faintly indicated by lines like those made with a brush on Chinese

[003-087]faces. This candor of the soul was still further evidenced around the

[003-088]eyes, in their corners, and about the temples, by pearly tints threaded

[003-089]with blue, the special privilege of these delicate complexions. The

[003-090]face, whose oval Raphael so often gave to his Madonnas, was remarkable

[003-091]for the sober and virginal tone of the cheeks, soft as a Bengal rose,

[003-092]upon which the long lashes of the diaphanous eyelids cast shadows that

[003-093]were mingled with light. The throat, bending as she worked, too delicate

[003-094]perhaps, and of milky whiteness, recalled those vanishing lines that

[003-095]Leonardo loved. A few little blemishes here and there, like the patches

[003-096]of the eighteenth century, proved that Modeste was indeed a child of

[003-097]earth, and not a creation dreamed of in Italy by the angelic school. Her

[003-098]lips, delicate yet full, were slightly mocking and somewhat sensuous;

[003-099]the waist, which was supple and yet not fragile, had no terrors for

[003-100]maternity, like those of girls who seek beauty by the fatal pressure of

[003-101]a corset. Steel and dimity and lacings defined but did not create the

[003-102]serpentine lines of the elegant figure, graceful as that of a young

[003-103]poplar swaying in the wind.

[003-104]A pearl-gray dress with crimson trimmings, made with a long waist,

[003-105]modestly outlined the bust and covered the shoulders, still rather thin,

[003-106]with a chemisette which left nothing to view but the first curves of

[003-107]the throat where it joined the shoulders. From the aspect of the young

[003-108]girl’s face, at once ethereal and intelligent, where the delicacy of a

[003-109]Greek nose with its rosy nostrils and firm modelling marked something

[003-110]positive and defined; where the poetry enthroned upon an almost mystic

[003-111]brow seemed belied at times by the pleasure-loving expression of the

[003-112]mouth; where candor claimed the depths profound and varied of the

[003-113]eye, and disputed them with a spirit of irony that was trained and

[003-114]educated,--from all these signs an observer would have felt that this

[003-115]young girl, with the keen, alert ear that waked at every sound, with

[003-116]a nostril open to catch the fragrance of the celestial flower of the

[003-117]Ideal, was destined to be the battle-ground of a struggle between

[003-118]the poesies of the dawn and the labors of the day; between fancy

[003-119]and reality, the spirit and the life. Modeste was a pure young girl,

[003-120]inquisitive after knowledge, understanding her destiny, and filled with

[003-121]chastity,--the Virgin of Spain rather than the Madonna of Raphael.

[003-122]She raised her head when she heard Dumay say to Exupere, “Come here,

[003-123]young man.” Seeing them together in the corner of the salon she supposed

[003-124]they were talking of some commission in [[paris|Paris]]. Then she looked at

[003-125]the friends who surrounded her, as if surprised by their silence, and

[003-126]exclaimed in her natural manner, “Why are you not playing?”--with a

[003-127]glance at the green table which the imposing Madame Latournelle called

[003-128]the “altar.”

[003-129]“Yes, let us play,” said Dumay, having sent off Exupere.

[003-130]“Sit there, Butscha,” said Madame Latournelle, separating the head-clerk

[003-131]from the group around Madame Mignon and her daughter by the whole width

[003-132]of the table.

[003-133]“And you, come over here,” said Dumay to his wife, making her sit close

[003-134]by him.

[003-135]Madame Dumay, a little American about thirty-six years of age, wiped her

[003-136]eyes furtively; she adored Modeste, and feared a catastrophe.

[003-137]“You are not very lively this evening,” remarked Modeste.

[003-138]“We are playing,” said Gobenheim, sorting his cards.

[003-139]No matter how interesting this situation may appear, it can be made

[003-140]still more so by explaining Dumay’s position towards Modeste. If the

[003-141]brevity of this explanation makes it seem rather dry, the reader must

[003-142]pardon its dryness in view of our desire to get through with these

[003-143]preliminaries as speedily as possible, and the necessity of relating the

[003-144]main circumstances which govern all dramas.

[003-145]CHAPTER III. PRELIMINARIES

[003-146]Jean Francois Bernard Dumay, born at Vannes, started as a soldier for

[003-147]the army of Italy in 1799. His father, president of the revolutionary

[003-148]tribunal of that town, had displayed so much energy in his office

[003-149]that the place had become too hot to hold the son when the parent, a

[003-150]pettifogging lawyer, perished on the scaffold after the ninth Thermidor.

[003-151]On the death of his mother, who died of the grief this catastrophe

[003-152]occasioned, Jean sold all that he possessed and rushed to Italy at the

[003-153]age of twenty-two, at the very moment when our armies were beginning to

[003-154]yield. On the way he met a young man in the department of Var, who

[003-155]for reasons analogous to his own was in search of glory, believing a

[003-156]battle-field less perilous than his own Provence. Charles Mignon, the

[003-157]last scion of an ancient family, which gave its name to a street in

[003-158]Paris and to a mansion built by Cardinal Mignon, had a shrewd and

[003-159]calculating father, whose one idea was to save his feudal estate of La

[003-160]Bastie in the Comtat from the claws of the Revolution. Like all timid

[003-161]folk of that day, the Comte de La Bastie, now citizen Mignon, found it

[003-162]more wholesome to cut off other people’s heads than to let his own be

[003-163]cut off. The sham terrorist disappeared after the 9th Thermidor, and was

[003-164]then inscribed on the list of emigres. The estate of La Bastie was sold;

[003-165]the towers and bastions of the old castle were pulled down, and citizen

[003-166]Mignon was soon after discovered at Orleans and put to death with his

[003-167]wife and all his children except Charles, whom he had sent to find a

[003-168]refuge for the family in the Upper Alps.

[003-169]Horrorstruck at the news, Charles waited for better times in a valley of

[003-170]Mont Genevra; and there he remained till 1799, subsisting on a few

[003-171]louis which his father had put into his hand at starting. Finally,

[003-172]when twenty-three years of age, and without other [[commerce|fortune]] than his fine

[003-173]presence and that southern beauty which, when it reaches perfection,

[003-174]may be called sublime (of which Antinous, the favorite of Adrian, is the

[003-175]type), Charles resolved to wager his Provencal audacity--taking it, like

[003-176]many another youth, for a vocation--on the red cloth of war. On his

[003-177]way to the base of the army at Nice he met the Breton. The pair became

[003-178]intimate, partly from the contrasts in their characters; they drank from

[003-179]the same cup at the wayside torrents, broke the same biscuit, and were

[003-180]both made sergeants at the peace which followed the battle of Marengo.
