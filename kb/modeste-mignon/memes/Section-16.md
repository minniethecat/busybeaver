# Section 16

[016-001]But my proceedings, dear Chrysale, have at least the merit of not

[016-002]binding us personally. I know that [[love]] has its illusions, and

[016-003]every [[illusion]] its to-morrow. That is why there are so many

[016-004]partings among lovers vowed to each other for life. The proof of

[016-005]love lies in two things,--suffering and happiness. When, after

[016-006]passing through these double trials of life two beings have shown

[016-007]each other their defects as well as their good qualities, when

[016-008]they have really observed each other’s character, then they may go

[016-009]to their grave hand in hand. My dear Argante, who told you that

[016-010]our little drama thus begun was to have no future? In any case

[016-011]shall we not have enjoyed the pleasures of our [[epistolary-novel|correspondence]]?

[016-012]I await your orders, monseigneur, and I am with all my heart,

[016-013]Your handmaiden,

[016-014]O. d’Este M.

[016-015]To Mademoiselle O. d’Este M.,--You are a witch, a spirit, and I

[016-016]love you! Is that what you desire of me, most original of girls?

[016-017]Perhaps you are only seeking to amuse your [[provincial-life|provincial]] leisure with

[016-018]the follies which are you able to make a [[poetry|poet]] commit. If so, you

[016-019]have done a bad deed. Your two letters have enough of the spirit

[016-020]of mischief in them to force this doubt into the mind of a

[016-021]Parisian. But I am no longer master of myself; my life, my future

[016-022]depend on the answer you will make me. Tell me if the certainty of

[016-023]an unbounded affection, oblivious of all social conventions, will

[016-024]touch you,--if you will suffer me to seek you. There is anxiety

[016-025]enough and uncertainty enough in the question as to whether I can

[016-026]personally please you. If your reply is favorable I change my

[016-027]life, I bid adieu to all the irksome pleasures which we have the

[016-028]folly to call happiness. Happiness, my dear and beautiful unknown,

[016-029]is what you dream it to be,--a fusion of feelings, a perfect

[016-030]accordance of souls, the imprint of a noble [[the-ideal|ideal]] (such as God

[016-031]does permit us to form in this low world) upon the trivial round

[016-032]of daily life whose habits we must needs obey, a constancy of

[016-033]heart more precious far than what we call fidelity. Can we say

[016-034]that we make sacrifices when the end in view is our eternal good,

[016-035]the dream of poets, the dream of maidens, the poem which, at the

[016-036]entrance of life when thought essays its wings, each noble

[016-037]intellect has pondered and caressed only to see it shivered to

[016-038]fragments on some stone of stumbling as hard as it is vulgar?--for

[016-039]to the great majority of men, the foot of reality steps instantly

[016-040]on that mysterious egg so seldom hatched.

[016-041]I cannot speak to you any more of myself; not of my past life, nor

[016-042]of my character, nor of an affection almost maternal on one side,

[016-043]filial on mine, which you have already seriously changed--an

[016-044]effect upon my life which must explain my use of the word

[016-045]“sacrifice.” You have already rendered me forgetful, if not

[016-046]ungrateful; does that satisfy you? Oh, speak! Say to me one word,

[016-047]and I will love you till my eyes close in death, as the Marquis de

[016-048]Pescaire loved his wife, as Romeo loved Juliet, and faithfully.

[016-049]Our life will be, for me at least, that “felicity untroubled”

[016-050]which Dante made the very element of his Paradiso,--a poem far

[016-051]superior to his Inferno. Strange, it is not myself that I doubt in

[016-052]the long reverie through which, like you, I follow the windings of

[016-053]a dreamed existence; it is you. Yes, dear, I feel within me the

[016-054]power to love, and to love endlessly,--to march to the grave with

[016-055]gentle slowness and a smiling eye, with my beloved on my arm, and

[016-056]with never a cloud upon the sunshine of our souls. Yes, I dare to

[016-057]face our mutual old age, to see ourselves with whitening heads,

[016-058]like the venerable historian of Italy, inspired always with the

[016-059]same affection but transformed in soul by our life’s seasons. Hear

[016-060]me, I can no longer be your friend only. Though Chrysale, Geronte,

[016-061]and Argante re-live, you say, in me, I am not yet old enough to

[016-062]drink from the cup held to my lips by the sweet hands of a veiled

[016-063]woman without a passionate desire to tear off the domino and the

[016-064]mask and see the face. Either write me no more, or give me hope.

[016-065]Let me see you, or let me go. Must I bid you adieu? Will you

[016-066]permit me to sign myself,

[016-067]Your Friend?

[016-068]To Monsieur de [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]],--What flattery! with what rapidity is the

[016-069]grave Anselme transformed into a handsome Leander! To what must I

[016-070]attribute such a change? to this black which I put upon this

[016-071]white? to these ideas which are to the flowers of my soul what a

[016-072]rose drawn in charcoal is to the roses in the garden? Or is it to

[016-073]a recollection of the young girl whom you took for me, and who is

[016-074]personally as like me as a waiting-woman is like her mistress?

[016-075]Have we changed roles? Have I the sense? have you the fancy? But a

[016-076]truce with jesting.

[016-077]Your letter has made me know the elating pleasures of the soul;

[016-078]the first that I have known outside of my family affections. What,

[016-079]says a poet, are the ties of blood which are so strong in ordinary

[016-080]minds, compared to those divinely forged within us by mysterious

[016-081]sympathies? Let me thank you--no, we must not thank each other for

[016-082]such things--but God bless you for the happiness you have given

[016-083]me; be happy in the joy you have shed into my soul. You explain to

[016-084]me some of the apparent injustices in social life. There is

[016-085]something, I know not what, so dazzling, so virile in glory, that

[016-086]it belongs only to man; God forbids us women to wear its halo, but

[016-087]he makes love our portion, giving us the tenderness which soothes

[016-088]the brow scorched by his lightnings. I have felt my mission, and

[016-089]you have now confirmed it.

[016-090]Sometimes, my friend, I rise in the morning in a state of

[016-091]inexpressible sweetness; a sort of peace, tender and divine, gives

[016-092]me an idea of heaven. My first thought is then like a benediction.

[016-093]I call these mornings my little German wakings, in opposition to

[016-094]my Southern sunsets, full of heroic deeds, battles, Roman fetes

[016-095]and ardent poems. Well, after reading your letter, so full of

[016-096]feverish impatience, I felt in my heart all the freshness of my

[016-097]celestial wakings, when I love the air about me and all nature,

[016-098]and fancy that I am destined to die for one I love. One of your

[016-099]poems, “The Maiden’s Song,” paints these delicious moments, when

[016-100]gaiety is tender, when aspiration is a need; it is one of my

[016-101]favorites. Do you want me to put all my flatteries into one?--well

[016-102]then, I think you worthy to be _me_!

[016-103]Your letter, though short, enables me to read within you. Yes, I

[016-104]have guessed your tumultuous struggles, your piqued curiosity,

[016-105]your projects; but I do not yet know you well enough to satisfy

[016-106]your wishes. Hear me, dear; the mystery in which I am shrouded

[016-107]allows me to use that word, which lets you see to the bottom of my

[016-108]heart. Hear me: if we once meet, adieu to our mutual

[016-109]comprehension! Will you make a compact with me? Was the first

[016-110]disadvantageous to you? But remember it won you my esteem, and it

[016-111]is a great deal, my friend, to gain an admiration lined throughout

[016-112]with esteem. Here is the compact: write me your life in a few

[016-113]words; then tell me what you do in [[paris|Paris]], day by day, with no

[016-114]reservations, and as if you were talking to some old friend. Well,

[016-115]having done that, I will take a step myself--I will see you, I

[016-116]promise you that. And it is a great deal.

[016-117]This, dear, is no intrigue, no adventure; no gallantry, as you men

[016-118]say, can come of it, I warn you frankly. It involves my life, and

[016-119]more than that,--something that causes me remorse for the many

[016-120]thoughts that fly to you in flocks--it involves my father’s and my

[016-121]mother’s life. I adore them, and my choice must please them; they

[016-122]must find a son in you.

[016-123]Tell me, to what extent can the superb spirits of your kind, to

[016-124]whom God has given the wings of his angels, without always adding

[016-125]their amiability,--how far can they bend under a family yoke, and

[016-126]put up with its little miseries? That is a text I have meditated

[016-127]upon. Ah! though I said to my heart before I came to you, Forward!

[016-128]Onward! it did not tremble and palpitate any the less on the way;

[016-129]and I did not conceal from myself the stoniness of the path nor

[016-130]the Alpine difficulties I had to encounter. I thought of all in my

[016-131]long, long meditations. Do I not know that eminent men like you

[016-132]have known the love they have inspired quite as well as that which

[016-133]they themselves have felt; that they have had many romances in

[016-134]their lives,--you particularly, who send forth those airy visions

[016-135]of your soul that women rush to buy? Yet still I cried to myself,

[016-136]“Onward!” because I have studied, more than you give me credit

[016-137]for, the geography of the great summits of humanity, which you

[016-138]tell me are so cold. Did you not say that Goethe and Byron were

[016-139]the colossi of egoism and poetry? Ah, my friend, there you shared

[016-140]a mistake into which superficial minds are apt to fall; but in you

[016-141]perhaps it came from generosity, false modesty, or the desire to

[016-142]escape from me. Vulgar minds may mistake the effect of toil for

[016-143]the development of personal character, but you must not. Neither

[016-144]Lord Byron, nor Goethe, nor Walter Scott, nor Cuvier, nor any

[016-145]inventor, belongs to himself, he is the slave of his idea. And

[016-146]this mysterious power is more jealous than a woman; it sucks their

[016-147]blood, it makes them live, it makes them die for its sake. The

[016-148]visible developments of their hidden existence do seem, in their

[016-149]results, like egotism; but who shall dare to say that the man who

[016-150]has abnegated self to give pleasure, instruction, or grandeur to

[016-151]his epoch, is an egoist? Is a mother selfish when she immolates

[016-152]all things to her child? Well, the detractors of genius do not

[016-153]perceive its fecund maternity, that is all. The life of a poet is

[016-154]so perpetual a sacrifice that he needs a gigantic organization to

[016-155]bear even the ordinary pleasures of life. Therefore, into what

[016-156]sorrows may he not fall when, like Moliere, he wishes to live the

[016-157]life of feeling in its most poignant crises; to me, remembering

[016-158]his personal life, Moliere’s comedy is horrible.

[016-159]The generosity of genius seems to me half divine; and I place you

[016-160]in this noble family of alleged egoists. Ah! if I had found

[016-161]self-interest, ambition, a seared nature where I now can see my

[016-162]best loved flowers of the soul, you know not what long anguish I

[016-163]should have had to bear. I met with disappointment before I was

[016-164]sixteen. What would have become of me had I learned at twenty that

[016-165]fame is a lie, that he whose books express the feelings hidden in

[016-166]my heart was incapable of feeling them himself? Oh! my friend, do

[016-167]you know what would have become of me? Shall I take you into the

[016-168]recesses of my soul? I should have gone to my father and said,

[016-169]“Bring me the son-in-law whom you desire; my will abdicates,--marry

[016-170]me to whom you please.” And the man might have been a notary,

[016-171]banker, miser, fool, dullard, wearisome as a rainy day, common as

[016-172]the usher of a school, a manufacturer, or some brave soldier without

[016-173]two ideas,--he would have had a resigned and attentive servant in

[016-174]me. But what an awful suicide! never could my soul have expanded

[016-175]in the life-giving rays of a beloved sun. No murmur should have

[016-176]revealed to my father, or my mother, or my children the suicide of

[016-177]the creature who at this instant is shaking her fetters, casting

[016-178]lightnings from her eyes, and flying towards you with eager wing.

[016-179]See, she is there, at the angle of your desk, like Polyhymnia,

[016-180]breathing the air of your presence, and glancing about her with a

[016-181]curious eye. Sometimes in the fields where my husband would have

[016-182]taken me to walk, I should have wept, apart and secretly, at sight

[016-183]of a glorious morning; and in my heart, or hidden in a

[016-184]bureau-drawer, I might have kept some treasure, the comfort of poor

[016-185]girls ill-used by love, sad, poetic souls,--but ah! I have _you_, I

[016-186]believe in _you_, my friend. That belief straightens all my thoughts

[016-187]and fancies, even the most fantastic, and sometimes--see how far

[016-188]my frankness leads me--I wish I were in the middle of the book we

[016-189]are just beginning; such persistency do I feel in my sentiments,

[016-190]such strength in my heart to love, such constancy sustained by

[016-191]reason, such heroism for the duties for which I was created,--if

[016-192]indeed love can ever be transmuted into duty.
