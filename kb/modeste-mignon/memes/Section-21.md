# Section 21

[021-001]“There is no need to question [[dumay|Dumay]],” said Butscha. “Your father lost,

[021-002]as you know, about four millions when he went away, and he has doubtless

[021-003]recovered them. He would of course give Dumay ten per cent of his

[021-004]profits; the worthy man admitted the other day how much it was, and my

[021-005]master and I think that in that case the colonel’s [[commerce|fortune]] must amount

[021-006]to six or seven millions--”

[021-007]“Oh, papa!” cried [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], crossing her hands on her breast and looking

[021-008]up to heaven, “twice you have given me life!”

[021-009]“Ah, mademoiselle!” said Butscha, “you [[love]] a [[poetry|poet]]. That kind of man

[021-010]is more or less of a Narcissus. Will he know how to love you? A

[021-011]phrase-maker, always busy in fitting words together, must be a bore.

[021-012]Mademoiselle, a poet is no more poetry than a seed is a flower.”

[021-013]“Butscha, I never saw so handsome a man.”

[021-014]“Beauty is a veil which often serves to hide imperfections.”

[021-015]“He has the most angelic heart of heaven--”

[021-016]“I pray God you may be right,” said the dwarf, clasping his hands,

[021-017]“--and happy! That man shall have, as you have, a servant in Jean

[021-018]Butscha. I will not be notary; I shall give that up; I shall study the

[021-019]sciences.”

[021-020]“Why?”

[021-021]“Ah, mademoiselle, to train up your children, if you will deign to make

[021-022]me their tutor. But, oh! if you would only listen to some advice. Let

[021-023]me take up this matter; let me look into the life and habits of this

[021-024]man,--find out if he is kind, or bad-tempered, or gentle, if he commands

[021-025]the respect which you merit in a husband, if he is able to love utterly,

[021-026]preferring you to everything, even his own talent--”

[021-027]“What does that signify if I love him?”

[021-028]“Ah, true!” cried the dwarf.

[021-029]At that instant Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] was saying to her friends,--

[021-030]“My daughter saw the man she loves this morning.”

[021-031]“Then it must have been that sulphur waistcoat which puzzled you so,

[021-032]Latournelle,” said his wife. “The young man had a pretty white rose in

[021-033]his buttonhole.”

[021-034]“Ah!” sighed the mother, “the sign of recognition.”

[021-035]“And he also wore the ribbon of an officer of the Legion of [[honor]]. He is

[021-036]a charming young man. But we are all deceiving ourselves; Modeste

[021-037]never raised her veil, and her clothes were huddled on like a

[021-038]beggar-woman’s--”

[021-039]“And she said she was ill,” cried the notary; “but she has taken off her

[021-040]mufflings and is just as well as she ever was.”

[021-041]“It is incomprehensible!” said Dumay.

[021-042]“Not at all,” said the notary; “it is now as clear as day.”

[021-043]“My child,” said Madame Mignon to Modeste, as she came into the room,

[021-044]followed by Butscha, “did you see a well-dressed young man at church

[021-045]this morning, with a white rose in his button-hole?”

[021-046]“I saw him,” said Butscha quickly, perceiving by everybody’s strained

[021-047]attention that Modeste was likely to fall into a trap. “It was

[021-048]Grindot, the famous architect, with whom the town is in treaty for the

[021-049]restoration of the church. He has just come from [[paris|Paris]], and I met

[021-050]him this morning examining the exterior as I was on my way to

[021-051]Sainte-Adresse.”

[021-052]“Oh, an architect, was he? he puzzled me,” said Modeste, for whom

[021-053]Butscha had thus gained time to recover herself.

[021-054]Dumay looked askance at Butscha. Modeste, fully warned, recovered her

[021-055]impenetrable composure. Dumay’s distrust was now thoroughly aroused, and

[021-056]he resolved to go the mayor’s office early in the morning and ascertain

[021-057]if the architect had really been in [[provincial-life|Havre]] the previous day. Butscha,

[021-058]on the other hand, was equally determined to go to Paris and find out

[021-059]something about [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]].

[021-060]Gobenheim came to play whist, and by his presence subdued and compressed

[021-061]all this fermentation of feelings. Modeste awaited her mother’s bedtime

[021-062]with impatience. She intended to write, but never did so except at

[021-063]night. Here is the letter which love dictated to her while all the world

[021-064]was sleeping:--

[021-065]To Monsieur de Canalis,--Ah! my friend, my well-beloved! What

[021-066]atrocious falsehoods those portraits in the shop-windows are! And

[021-067]I, who made that horrible lithograph my joy!--I am humbled at the

[021-068]thought of loving one so handsome. No; it is impossible that those

[021-069]Parisian women are so stupid as not to have seen their dreams

[021-070]fulfilled in you. You neglected! you unloved! I do not believe a

[021-071]word of all that you have written me about your lonely and obscure

[021-072]life, your hunger for an idol,--sought in vain until now. You have

[021-073]been too well loved, monsieur; your brow, white and smooth as a

[021-074]magnolia leaf, reveals it; and it is I who must be neglected,--for

[021-075]who am I? Ah! why have you called me to life? I felt for a moment

[021-076]as though the heavy burden of the flesh was leaving me; my soul

[021-077]had broken the crystal which held it captive; it pervaded my whole

[021-078]being; the cold silence of material things had ceased; all things

[021-079]in nature had a voice and spoke to me. The old church was

[021-080]luminous. It’s arched roof, brilliant with gold and azure like

[021-081]those of an Italian cathedral, sparkled above my head. Melodies

[021-082]such as the angels sang to martyrs, quieting their pains, sounded

[021-083]from the organ. The rough pavements of Havre seemed to my feet a

[021-084]flowery mead; the sea spoke to me with a voice of sympathy, like

[021-085]an old friend whom I had never truly understood. I saw clearly how

[021-086]the roses in my garden had long adored me and bidden me love; they

[021-087]lifted their heads and smiled as I came back from church. I heard

[021-088]your name, “Melchior,” chiming in the flower-bells; I saw it

[021-089]written on the clouds. Yes, yes, I live, I am living, thanks to

[021-090]thee,--my poet, more beautiful than that cold, conventional Lord

[021-091]Byron, with a face as dull as the English climate. One glance of

[021-092]thine, thine Orient glance, pierced through my double veil and

[021-093]sent thy blood to my heart, and from thence to my head and feet.

[021-094]Ah! that is not the life our mother gave us. A hurt to thee would

[021-095]hurt me too at the very instant it was given,--my life exists by

[021-096]thy thought only. I know now the purpose of the divine faculty of

[021-097][[music]]; the angels invented it to utter love. Ah, my Melchior, to

[021-098]have genius and to have beauty is too much; a man should be made

[021-099]to choose between them at his birth.

[021-100]When I think of the treasures of tenderness and affection which

[021-101]you have given me, and more especially for the last month, I ask

[021-102]myself if I dream. No, but you hide some mystery; what woman can

[021-103]yield you up to me and not die? Ah! jealousy has entered my heart

[021-104]with love,--love in which I could not have believed. How could I

[021-105]have imagined so mighty a conflagration? And now--strange and

[021-106]inconceivable revulsion!--I would rather you were ugly.

[021-107]What follies I committed after I came home! The yellow dahlias

[021-108]reminded me of your waistcoat, the white roses were my loving

[021-109]friends; I bowed to them with a look that belonged to you, like

[021-110]all that is of me. The very color of the gloves, moulded to hands

[021-111]of a gentleman, your step along the nave,--all, all, is so printed

[021-112]on my memory that sixty years hence I shall see the veriest

[021-113]trifles of this day of days,--the color of the atmosphere, the ray

[021-114]of sunshine that flickered on a certain pillar; I shall hear the

[021-115]prayer your step interrupted; I shall inhale the incense of the

[021-116]altar; forever I shall feel above our heads the priestly hands

[021-117]that blessed us both as you passed by me at the closing

[021-118]benediction. The good Abbe Marcelin married us then! The

[021-119]happiness, above that of earth, which I feel in this new world of

[021-120]unexpected emotions can only be equalled by the joy of telling it

[021-121]to you, of sending it back to him who poured it into my heart with

[021-122]the lavishness of the sun itself. No more veils, no more

[021-123]disguises, my beloved. Come back to me, oh, come back soon. With

[021-124]joy I now unmask.

[021-125]You have no doubt heard of the house of Mignon in Havre? Well, I

[021-126]am, through an irreparable misfortune, its sole heiress. But you

[021-127]are not to look down upon us, descendant of an Auvergne knight;

[021-128]the arms of the Mignon de La Bastie will do no dishonor to those

[021-129]of Canalis. We bear gules, on a bend sable four bezants or;

[021-130]quarterly four crosses patriarchal or; a cardinal’s hat as crest,

[021-131]and the fiocchi for supports. Dear, I will be faithful to our

[021-132]motto: “Una fides, unus Dominus!”--the true faith, and one only

[021-133]Master.

[021-134]Perhaps, my friend, you will find some irony in my name, after all

[021-135]that I have done, and all that I herein avow. I am named Modeste.

[021-136]Therefore I have not deceived you by signing “O. d’Este M.”

[021-137]Neither have I misled you about our [[social-mobility|fortune]]; it will amount, I

[021-138]believe, to the sum which rendered you so virtuous. I know that to

[021-139]you money is a consideration of small importance; therefore I

[021-140]speak of it without reserve. Let me tell you how happy it makes me

[021-141]to give freedom of action to our happiness,--to be able to say,

[021-142]when the fancy for travel takes us, “Come, let us go in a

[021-143]comfortable carriage, sitting side by side, without a thought of

[021-144]money”--happy, in short, to tell the king, “I have the fortune

[021-145]which you require in your peers.” Thus Modeste Mignon can be of

[021-146]service to you, and her gold will have the noblest of uses.

[021-147]As to your servant herself,--you did see her once, at her window.

[021-148]Yes, “the fairest daughter of Eve the fair” was indeed your

[021-149]unknown damozel; but how little the Modeste of to-day resembles

[021-150]her of that long past era! That one was in her shroud, this one

[021-151]--have I made you know it?--has received from you the life of life.

[021-152]Love, pure, and sanctioned, the love my father, now returning

[021-153]rich and prosperous, will authorize, has raised me with its

[021-154]powerful yet childlike hand from the grave in which I slept. You

[021-155]have wakened me as the sun wakens the flowers. The eyes of your

[021-156]beloved are no longer those of the little Modeste so daring in her

[021-157]ignorance,--no, they are dimmed with the sight of happiness, and

[021-158]the lids close over them. To-day I tremble lest I can never

[021-159]deserve my fate. The king has come in his glory; my lord has now a

[021-160]subject who asks pardon for the liberties she has taken, like the

[021-161]gambler with loaded dice after cheating Monsieur de Grammont.

[021-162]My cherished poet! I will be thy Mignon--happier far than the

[021-163]Mignon of Goethe, for thou wilt leave me in mine own land,--in thy

[021-164]heart. Just as I write this pledge of our betrothal a nightingale

[021-165]in the Vilquin park answers for thee. Ah, tell me quick that his

[021-166]note, so pure, so clear, so full, which fills my heart with joy

[021-167]and love like an Annunciation, does not lie to me.

[021-168]My father will pass through Paris on his way from Marseilles; the

[021-169]house of Mongenod, with whom he corresponds, will know his

[021-170]address. Go to him, my Melchior, tell him that you love me; but do

[021-171]not try to tell him how I love you,--let that be forever between

[021-172]ourselves and God. I, my dear one, am about to tell everything to

[021-173]my mother. Her heart will justify my conduct; she will rejoice in

[021-174]our secret poem, so romantic, human and divine in one.

[021-175]You have the confession of the daughter; you must now obtain the

[021-176]consent of the Comte de La Bastie, father of your

[021-177]Modeste.

[021-178]P.S.--Above all, do not come to Havre without having first

[021-179]obtained my father’s consent. If you love me you will not fail to

[021-180]find him on his way through Paris.
