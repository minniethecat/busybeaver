# Music

Music in *Modeste Mignon* represents the most natural and unpremeditated form of artistic expression — a gift Modeste possesses without formal training, reflecting her innate sensibility.

## Definition

Balzac presents music as prior to and more fundamental than formal art. The narrator states: "Melody is to music what imagery and sentiment are to poetry, a flower that blossoms spontaneously. Consequently, nations have had melodies before harmony — botany comes later than the flower" (007-040). This botanical metaphor positions music, like poetry, as a natural effusion of the soul rather than a learned technique. Modeste's musicality is presented as inseparable from her character — she is "born a musician" (007-040) who "played to enliven her mother" and "sang by nature" (007-040).

## In the Novel

### Manifestations

Modeste's musical activity serves multiple functions in the narrative. It provides solace to her blind mother: "Modeste often conjured away her troubles by practising, without a master" (007-040). It also becomes a vehicle for her romantic awakening — she composes "tender little lyric melodies" whose "poetry and meaning had roused the attention of her mother, already surprised by her sudden eagerness for composition and her fancy for putting airs into certain verses" (007-048). Music thus becomes the medium through which Modeste's inner emotional life first becomes legible to others.

The German Lieder that Madame Mignon teaches her daughter connect the household to a broader European cultural tradition. Modeste "loved the German airs which her mother taught her" (007-040), linking the domestic sphere of the Chalet to the romantic sensibility associated with German culture in Balzac's France.

## In Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*

Music appears throughout Balzac's work as a marker of refined sensibility and emotional depth. In *Modeste Mignon*, however, it takes on a specifically feminine and domestic character — Modeste's music-making is private, amateur, and deeply connected to her role as caregiver to her blind mother. This contrasts with the public, professional world of the male poet Canalis, whose art is commodified.
