# Section 25

[025-001]A few moments before the breakfast-hour at [[chalet|the Chalet]], the clacking of

[025-002]a postilion’s whip apprised the family that the two soldiers were

[025-003]arriving; only a father’s joy at returning after long absence could be

[025-004]heralded with such clatter, and it brought all the women to the garden

[025-005]gate. There is many a father and many a child--perhaps more fathers than

[025-006]children--who will understand the delights of such an arrival, and that

[025-007]happy fact shows that literature has no need to depict it. Perhaps all

[025-008]gentle and tender emotions are beyond the range of literature.

[025-009]Not a word that could trouble the peace of the family was uttered on

[025-010]this joyful day. Truce was tacitly established between father, mother,

[025-011]and child as to the so-called mysterious [[love]] which had paled [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s

[025-012]cheeks,--for this was the first day she had left her bed since [[dumay|Dumay]]’s

[025-013]departure for [[paris|Paris]]. The colonel, with the charming delicacy of a

[025-014]true soldier, never left his wife’s side nor released her hand; but he

[025-015]watched Modeste with delight, and was never weary of noting her refined,

[025-016]elegant, and [[poetry|poetic]] beauty. Is it not by such seeming trifles that we

[025-017]recognize a man of feeling? Modeste, who feared to interrupt the subdued

[025-018]joy of the husband and wife kept at a little distance, coming from time

[025-019]to time to kiss her father’s forehead, and when she kissed it overmuch

[025-020]she seemed to mean that she was kissing it for two,--for Bettina and

[025-021]herself.

[025-022]“Oh, my darling, I understand you,” said the colonel, pressing her hand

[025-023]as she assailed him with kisses.

[025-024]“Hush!” whispered the young girl, glancing at her mother.

[025-025]Dumay’s rather sly and pregnant silence made Modeste somewhat uneasy as

[025-026]to the upshot of his journey to Paris. She looked at him furtively

[025-027]every now and then, without being able to get beneath his epidermis.

[025-028]The colonel, like a prudent father, wanted to study the character of

[025-029]his only daughter, and above all consult his wife, before entering on a

[025-030]conference upon which the happiness of the whole family depended.

[025-031]“To-morrow, my precious child,” he said as they parted for the night,

[025-032]“get up early, and we will go and take a walk on the seashore. We have

[025-033]to talk about your poems, Mademoiselle de La Bastie.”

[025-034]His last words, accompanied by a smile, which reappeared like an echo

[025-035]on Dumay’s lips, were all that gave Modeste any clew to what was coming;

[025-036]but it was enough to calm her uneasiness and keep her awake far into the

[025-037]night with her head full of suppositions; this, however, did not prevent

[025-038]her from being dressed and ready in the morning long before the colonel.

[025-039]“You know all, my kind papa?” she said as soon as they were on the road

[025-040]to the beach.

[025-041]“I know all, and a good deal more than you do,” he replied.

[025-042]After that remark father and daughter went some little way in silence.

[025-043]“Explain to me, my child, how it happens that a girl whom her mother

[025-044]idolizes could have taken such an important step as to write to a

[025-045]stranger without consulting her.”

[025-046]“Oh, papa! because mamma would never have allowed it.”

[025-047]“And do you think, my daughter, that that was proper? Though you have

[025-048]been educating your mind in this fatal way, how is it that your good

[025-049]sense and your intellect did not, in default of modesty, step in and

[025-050]show you that by acting as you did you were throwing yourself at a man’s

[025-051]head. To think that my daughter, my only remaining child, should lack

[025-052]pride and delicacy! Oh, Modeste, you made your father pass two hours in

[025-053]hell when he heard of it; for, after all, your conduct has been the

[025-054]same as Bettina’s without the excuse of a heart’s seduction; you were

[025-055]a coquette in cold blood, and that sort of coquetry is head-love, the

[025-056]worst vice of French women.”

[025-057]“I, without pride!” said Modeste, weeping; “but _he_ has not yet seen

[025-058]me.”

[025-059]“_He_ knows your name.”

[025-060]“I did not tell it to him till my eyes had vindicated the

[025-061][[epistolary-novel|correspondence]], lasting three months, during which our souls had spoken

[025-062]to each other.”

[025-063]“Oh, my dear misguided angel, you have mixed up a species of reason

[025-064]with a folly that has compromised your own happiness and that of your

[025-065]family.”

[025-066]“But, after all, papa, happiness is the absolution of my temerity,” she

[025-067]said, pouting.

[025-068]“Oh! your conduct is temerity, is it?”

[025-069]“A temerity that my mother practised before me,” she retorted quickly.

[025-070]“Rebellious child! your mother after seeing me at a ball told her

[025-071]father, who adored her, that she thought she could be happy with me. Be

[025-072]honest, Modeste; is there any likeness between a love hastily conceived,

[025-073]I admit, but under the eyes of a father, and your mad action of writing

[025-074]to a stranger?”

[025-075]“A stranger, papa? say rather one of our greatest poets, whose character

[025-076]and whose life are exposed to the strongest light of day, to detraction,

[025-077]to calumny,--a man robed in fame, and to whom, my dear father, I was a

[025-078]mere literary and dramatic personage, one of Shakespeare’s women, until

[025-079]the moment when I wished to know if the man himself were as beautiful as

[025-080]his soul.”

[025-081]“Good God! my poor child, you are turning [[marriage]] into poetry. But if,

[025-082]from time immemorial, girls have been cloistered in the bosom of their

[025-083]families, if God, if social laws put them under the stern yoke of

[025-084]parental sanction, it is, mark my words, to spare them the misfortunes

[025-085]that this very poetry which charms and dazzles you, and which you are

[025-086]therefore unable to judge of, would entail upon them. Poetry is indeed

[025-087]one of the pleasures of life, but it is not life itself.”

[025-088]“Papa, that is a suit still pending before the Court of Facts; the

[025-089]struggle is forever going on between our hearts and the claims of

[025-090]family.”

[025-091]“Alas for the child that finds her happiness in resisting them,” said

[025-092]the colonel, gravely. “In 1813 I saw one of my comrades, the Marquis

[025-093]d’Aiglemont, marry his cousin against the wishes of her father, and the

[025-094]pair have since paid dear for the obstinacy which the young girl took

[025-095]for love. The family must be sovereign in marriage.”

[025-096]“My poet has told me all that,” she answered. “He played Orgon for some

[025-097]time; and he was brave enough to disparage the personal lives of poets.”

[025-098]“I have read your letters,” said [[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]], with the flicker of a

[025-099]malicious smile on his lips that made Modeste very uneasy, “and I ought

[025-100]to remark that your last epistle was scarcely permissible in any woman,

[025-101]even a Julie d’Etanges. Good God! what harm novels do!”

[025-102]“We should live them, my dear father, whether people wrote them or not;

[025-103]I think it is better to read them. There are not so many adventures in

[025-104]these days as there were under Louis XIV. and Louis XV., and so they

[025-105]publish fewer novels. Besides, if you have read those letters, you must

[025-106]know that I have chosen the most angelic soul, the most sternly upright

[025-107]man for your son-in-law, and you must have seen that we love one another

[025-108]at least as much as you and mamma love each other. Well, I admit that it

[025-109]was not all exactly conventional; I did, if you _will_ have me say so,

[025-110]wrong--”

[025-111]“I have read your letters,” said her father, interrupting her, “and I

[025-112]know exactly how far your lover justified you in your own eyes for a

[025-113]proceeding which might be permissible in some woman who understood life,

[025-114]and who was led away by strong passion, but which in a young girl of

[025-115]twenty was a monstrous piece of wrong-doing.”

[025-116]“Yes, wrong-doing for commonplace people, for the narrow-minded

[025-117]Gobenheims, who measure life with a square rule. Please let us keep to

[025-118]the artistic and poetic life, papa. We young girls have only two ways to

[025-119]act; we must let a man know we love him by mincing and simpering, or we

[025-120]must go to him frankly. Isn’t the last way grand and noble? We French

[025-121]girls are delivered over by our families like so much merchandise, at

[025-122]sixty days’ sight, sometimes thirty, like Mademoiselle Vilquin; but in

[025-123]England, and Switzerland, and Germany, they follow very much the plan

[025-124]I have adopted. Now what have you got to say to that? Am I not half

[025-125]German?”

[025-126]“Child!” cried the colonel, looking at her; “the supremacy of France

[025-127]comes from her sound common-sense, from the logic to which her noble

[025-128]language constrains her mind. France is the reason of the whole world.

[025-129]England and Germany are romantic in their marriage customs,--though even

[025-130]there noble families follow our customs. You certainly do not mean to

[025-131]deny that your parents, who know life, who are responsible for your

[025-132]soul and for your happiness, have no right to guard you from the

[025-133]stumbling-blocks that are in your way? Good heavens!” he continued,

[025-134]speaking half to himself, “is it their fault, or is it ours? Ought we

[025-135]to hold our children under an iron yoke? Must we be punished for the

[025-136]tenderness that leads us to make them happy, and teaches our hearts how

[025-137]to do so?”

[025-138]Modeste watched her father out of the corner of her eye as she listened

[025-139]to this species of invocation, uttered in a broken voice.

[025-140]“Was it wrong,” she said, “in a girl whose heart was free, to choose for

[025-141]her husband not only a charming companion, but a man of noble genius,

[025-142]born to an honorable position, a gentleman; the equal of myself, a

[025-143]gentlewoman?”

[025-144]“You love him?” asked her father.

[025-145]“Father!” she said, laying her head upon his breast, “would you see me

[025-146]die?”

[025-147]“Enough!” said the old soldier. “I see your love is inextinguishable.”

[025-148]“Yes, inextinguishable.”

[025-149]“Can nothing change it?”

[025-150]“Nothing.”

[025-151]“No circumstances, no treachery, no betrayal? You mean that you will

[025-152]love him in spite of everything, because of his personal attractions?

[025-153]Even though he proved a D’Estourny, would you love him still?”

[025-154]“Oh, my father! you do not know your daughter. Could I love a coward, a

[025-155]man without [[honor]], without faith?”

[025-156]“But suppose he had deceived you?”

[025-157]“He? that honest, candid soul, half melancholy? You are joking, father,

[025-158]or else you have never met him.”

[025-159]“But you see now that your love is not inextinguishable, as you chose

[025-160]to call it. I have already made you admit that circumstances could alter

[025-161]your poem; don’t you now see that fathers are good for something?”

[025-162]“You want to give me a lecture, papa; it is positively l’Ami des Enfants

[025-163]over again.”

[025-164]“Poor deceived girl,” said her father, sternly; “it is no lecture of

[025-165]mine, I count for nothing in it; indeed, I am only trying to soften the

[025-166]blow.”

[025-167]“Father, don’t play tricks with my life,” exclaimed Modeste, turning

[025-168]pale.

[025-169]“Then, my daughter, summon all your courage. It is you who have been

[025-170]playing tricks with your life, and life is now tricking you.”

[025-171]Modeste looked at her father in stupid amazement.

[025-172]“Suppose that young man whom you love, whom you saw four days ago at

[025-173]church in [[provincial-life|Havre]], was a deceiver?”

[025-174]“Never!” she cried; “that noble head, that pale face full of poetry--”

[025-175]“--was a lie,” said the colonel interrupting her. “He was no more

[025-176]Monsieur de [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] than I am that sailor over there putting out to

[025-177]sea.”

[025-178]“Do you know what you are killing in me?” she said in a low voice.

[025-179]“Comfort yourself, my child; though accident has put the punishment of

[025-180]your fault into the fault itself, the harm done is not irreparable.

[025-181]The young man whom you have seen, and with whom you exchanged hearts

[025-182]by correspondence, is a loyal and honorable fellow; he came to me and

[025-183]confided everything. He loves you, and I have no objection to him as a

[025-184]son-in-law.”

[025-185]“If he is not Canalis, who is he then?” said Modeste in a changed voice.
