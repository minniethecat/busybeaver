# Section 39

[039-001]“The sun is the inexhaustible palette,” said [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], looking at the

[039-002][[poetry|poet]] in a species of bewilderment.

[039-003]A remark that she presently made on his absence of mind gave him

[039-004]an opportunity of saying that he was just then absorbed in his own

[039-005]thoughts,--an excuse that authors have more reason for giving than other

[039-006]men.

[039-007]“Are we really made happy by carrying our lives into the midst of

[039-008]the world, and swelling them with all sorts of fictitious wants and

[039-009]over-excited vanities?” said Modeste, moved by the aspect of the fertile

[039-010]and billowy country to long for a philosophically tranquil life.

[039-011]“That is a bucolic, mademoiselle, which is only written on tablets of

[039-012]gold,” said [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]].

[039-013]“And sometimes under garret-roofs,” remarked the colonel.

[039-014]Modeste threw a piercing glance at Canalis, which he was unable to

[039-015]sustain; she was conscious of a ringing in her ears, darkness seemed to

[039-016]spread before her, and then she suddenly exclaimed in icy tones:--

[039-017]“Ah! it is Wednesday!”

[039-018]“I do not say this to flatter your passing caprice, mademoiselle,” said

[039-019]the duke, to whom the little scene, so tragical for Modeste, had left

[039-020]time for thought; “but I declare I am so profoundly disgusted with the

[039-021]world and the Court and [[paris|Paris]] that had I a Duchesse d’Herouville, gifted

[039-022]with the wit and graces of mademoiselle, I would gladly bind myself to

[039-023]live like a philosopher at my chateau, doing good around me, draining my

[039-024]marshes, educating my children--”

[039-025]“That, Monsieur le duc, will be set to the account of your great

[039-026]goodness,” said Modeste, letting her eyes rest steadily on the noble

[039-027]gentleman. “You flatter me in not thinking me frivolous, and in

[039-028]believing that I have enough resources within myself to be able to live

[039-029]in solitude. It is perhaps my lot,” she added, glancing at Canalis, with

[039-030]an expression of pity.

[039-031]“It is the lot of all insignificant fortunes,” said the poet. “Paris

[039-032]demands Babylonian splendor. Sometimes I ask myself how I have ever

[039-033]managed to keep it up.”

[039-034]“The king does that for both of us,” said the duke, candidly; “we live

[039-035]on his Majesty’s bounty. If my family had not been allowed, after the

[039-036]death of Monsieur le Grand, as they call Cinq-Mars, to keep his office

[039-037]among us, we should have been obliged to sell Herouville to the Black

[039-038]Brethren. Ah, believe me, mademoiselle, it is a bitter humiliation to me

[039-039]to have to think of money in marrying.”

[039-040]The simple honesty of this confession came from his heart, and the

[039-041]regret was so sincere that it touched Modeste.

[039-042]“In these days,” said the poet, “no man in France, Monsieur le duc, is

[039-043]rich enough to marry a woman for herself, her personal worth, her grace,

[039-044]or her beauty--”

[039-045]The colonel looked at Canalis with a curious eye, after first watching

[039-046]Modeste, whose face no longer expressed the slightest astonishment.

[039-047]“For persons of high [[honor]],” he said slowly, “it is a noble employment

[039-048]of wealth to repair the ravages of time and destiny, and restore the old

[039-049]historic families.”

[039-050]“Yes, papa,” said Modeste, gravely.

[039-051]The colonel invited the duke and Canalis to dine with him sociably

[039-052]in their riding-dress, promising them to make no change himself.

[039-053]When Modeste went to her room to make her toilette, she looked at the

[039-054]jewelled whip she had disdained in the morning.

[039-055]“What workmanship they put into such things nowadays!” she said to

[039-056]Francoise Cochet, who had become her waiting-maid.

[039-057]“That poor young man, mademoiselle, who has got a fever--”

[039-058]“Who told you that?”

[039-059]“Monsieur Butscha. He came here this afternoon and asked me to say to

[039-060]you that he hoped you would notice he had kept his word on the appointed

[039-061]day.”

[039-062]Modeste came down into the salon dressed with royal simplicity.

[039-063]“My dear father,” she said aloud, taking the colonel by the arm, “please

[039-064]go and ask after Monsieur de La Briere’s health, and take him back his

[039-065]present. You can say that my small means, as well as my natural tastes,

[039-066]forbid my wearing ornaments which are only fit for queens or courtesans.

[039-067]Besides, I can only accept gifts from a bridegroom. Beg him to keep the

[039-068]whip until you know whether you are rich enough to buy it back.”

[039-069]“My little girl has plenty of good sense,” said the colonel, kissing his

[039-070]daughter on the forehead.

[039-071]Canalis took advantage of a conversation which began between the duke

[039-072]and Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] to escape to the terrace, where Modeste joined him,

[039-073]influenced by curiosity, though the poet believed her desire to become

[039-074]Madame de Canalis had brought her there. Rather alarmed at the indecency

[039-075]with which he had just executed what soldiers call a “volte-face,” and

[039-076]which, according to the laws of ambition, every man in his position

[039-077]would have executed quite as brutally, he now endeavored, as the

[039-078]unfortunate Modeste approached him, to find plausible excuses for his

[039-079]conduct.

[039-080]“Dear Modeste,” he began, in a coaxing tone, “considering the terms on

[039-081]which we stand to each other, shall I displease you if I say that

[039-082]your replies to the Duc d’Herouville were very painful to a man in

[039-083][[love]],--above all, to a poet whose soul is feminine, nervous, full of the

[039-084]jealousies of true passion. I should make a poor diplomatist indeed if

[039-085]I had not perceived that your first coquetries, your little premeditated

[039-086]inconsistencies, were only assumed for the purpose of studying our

[039-087]characters--”

[039-088]Modeste raised her head with the rapid, intelligent, half-coquettish

[039-089]motion of a wild animal, in whom instinct produces such miracles of

[039-090]grace.

[039-091]“--and therefore when I returned home and thought them over, they never

[039-092]misled me. I only marvelled at a cleverness so in harmony with your

[039-093]character and your countenance. Do not be uneasy, I never doubted that

[039-094]your assumed duplicity covered an angelic candor. No, your mind, your

[039-095]education, have in no way lessened the precious innocence which we

[039-096]demand in a wife. You are indeed a wife for a poet, a diplomatist, a

[039-097]thinker, a man destined to endure the chances and changes of life; and

[039-098]my admiration is equalled only by the attachment I feel to you. I now

[039-099]entreat you--if yesterday you were not playing a little comedy when

[039-100]you accepted the love of a man whose vanity will change to pride if

[039-101]you accept him, one whose defects will become virtues under your divine

[039-102]influence--I entreat you do not excite a passion which, in him, amounts

[039-103]to vice. Jealousy is a noxious element in my soul, and you have revealed

[039-104]to me its strength; it is awful, it destroys everything--Oh! I do not

[039-105]mean the jealousy of an Othello,” he continued, noticing Modeste’s

[039-106]gesture. “No, no; my thoughts were of myself: I have been so indulged

[039-107]on that point. You know the affection to which I owe all the happiness I

[039-108]have ever enjoyed,--very little at the best” (he sadly shook his head).

[039-109]“Love is symbolized among all nations as a child, because it fancies

[039-110]the world belongs to it, and it cannot conceive otherwise. Well, Nature

[039-111]herself set the limit to that sentiment. It was still-born. A tender,

[039-112]maternal soul guessed and calmed the painful constriction of my

[039-113]heart,--for a woman who feels, who knows, that she is past the joys of

[039-114]love becomes angelic in her treatment of others. The duchess has never

[039-115]made me suffer in my sensibilities. For ten years not a word, not a

[039-116]look, that could wound me! I attach more value to words, to thoughts,

[039-117]to looks, than ordinary men. If a look is to me a treasure beyond all

[039-118]price, the slightest doubt is deadly poison; it acts instantaneously,

[039-119]my love dies. I believe--contrary to the mass of men, who delight in

[039-120]trembling, hoping, expecting--that love can only exist in perfect,

[039-121]infantile, and infinite security. The exquisite purgatory, where women

[039-122]delight to send us by their coquetry, is a base happiness to which I

[039-123]will not submit: to me, love is either heaven or hell. If it is hell,

[039-124]I will have none of it. I feel an affinity with the azure skies of

[039-125]Paradise within my soul. I can give myself without reserve, without

[039-126]secrets, doubts or deceptions, in the life to come; and I demand

[039-127]reciprocity. Perhaps I offend you by these doubts. Remember, however,

[039-128]that I am only talking of myself--”

[039-129]“--a good deal, but never too much,” said Modeste, offended in every

[039-130]hole and corner of her pride by this discourse, in which the Duchesse de

[039-131]Chaulieu served as a dagger. “I am so accustomed to admire you, my dear

[039-132]poet.”

[039-133]“Well, then, can you promise me the same canine fidelity which I offer

[039-134]to you? Is it not beautiful? Is it not just what you have longed for?”

[039-135]“But why, dear poet, do you not marry a deaf-mute, and one who is also

[039-136]something of an idiot? I ask nothing better than to please my husband.

[039-137]But you threaten to take away from a girl the very happiness you so

[039-138]kindly arrange for her; you are tearing away every gesture, every word,

[039-139]every look; you cut the wings of your bird, and then expect it to

[039-140]hover about you. I know poets are accused of inconsistency--oh! very

[039-141]unjustly,” she added, as Canalis made a gesture of denial; “that alleged

[039-142]defect which comes from the brilliant activity of their minds which

[039-143]commonplace people cannot take into account. I do not believe, however,

[039-144]that a man of genius can invent such irreconcilable conditions and call

[039-145]his invention life. You are requiring the impossible solely for

[039-146]the pleasure of putting me in the wrong,--like the enchanters in

[039-147]fairy-tales, who set tasks to persecuted young girls whom the good

[039-148]fairies come and deliver.”

[039-149]“In this case the good fairy would be true love,” said Canalis in a curt

[039-150]tone, aware that his elaborate excuse for a rupture was seen through by

[039-151]the keen and delicate mind which Butscha had piloted so well.

[039-152]“My dear poet, you remind me of those fathers who inquire into a

[039-153]girl’s ‘dot’ before they are willing to name that of their son. You are

[039-154]quarrelling with me without knowing whether you have the slightest right

[039-155]to do so. Love is not gained by such dry arguments as yours. The poor

[039-156]duke on the contrary abandons himself to it like my Uncle Toby; with

[039-157]this difference, that I am not the Widow Wadman,--though widow indeed

[039-158]of many illusions as to poetry at the present moment. Ah, yes, we young

[039-159]girls will not believe in anything that disturbs our world of fancy! I

[039-160]was warned of all this beforehand. My dear poet, you are attempting to

[039-161]get up a quarrel which is unworthy of you. I no longer recognize the

[039-162]Melchior of yesterday.”

[039-163]“Because Melchior has discovered a spirit of ambition in you which--”

[039-164]Modeste looked at him from head to foot with an imperial eye.

[039-165]“But I shall be peer of France and ambassador as well as he,” added

[039-166]Canalis.

[039-167]“Do you take me for a [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]],” she said, beginning to mount the steps

[039-168]of the portico; but she instantly turned back and added, “That is less

[039-169]impertinent than to take me for a fool. The change in your conduct comes

[039-170]from certain silly rumors which you have heard in [[provincial-life|Havre]], and which my

[039-171]maid Francoise has repeated to me.”

[039-172]“Ah, Modeste, how can you think it?” said Canalis, striking a dramatic

[039-173]attitude. “Do you think me capable of marrying you only for your money?”

[039-174]“If I do you that wrong after your edifying remarks on the banks of the

[039-175]Seine can you easily undeceive me,” she said, annihilating him with her

[039-176]scorn.

[039-177]“Ah!” thought the poet, as he followed her into the house, “if you

[039-178]think, my little girl, that I’m to be caught in that net, you take me

[039-179]to be younger than I am. Dear, dear, what a fuss about an artful little

[039-180]thing whose esteem I value about as much as that of the king of Borneo.

[039-181]But she has given me a good reason for the rupture by accusing me of

[039-182]such unworthy sentiments. Isn’t she sly? La Briere will get a burden on

[039-183]his back--idiot that he is! And five years hence it will be a good joke

[039-184]to see them together.”
