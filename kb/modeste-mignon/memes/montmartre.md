# Montmartre

Montmartre, the celebrated hill overlooking Paris, appears in the novel's opening pages as a single comparative touchstone: "Ingouville is to Havre what Montmartre is to Paris" (001-141).

## Geography

Rising to 130 meters above the Seine plain, Montmartre is the highest point in Paris. In Balzac's era, it was still a semi-rural village on the outskirts of the capital, known for its windmills, vineyards, and panoramic views over the city below. Its position — a high hill at the city's edge — made it the natural reference point for describing Ingouville's relationship to Le Havre.

## In the Novel

The Montmartre reference occurs in the novel's opening geographical exposition, as Balzac establishes the topography of Le Havre and its surroundings. Ingouville sits on a hillside overlooking the port, just as Montmartre overlooks Paris. The comparison is precise and economical: in a single phrase, it gives the reader both the physical layout and the social connotation — a hillside suburb from which one looks down upon commercial bustle below (001-141, 001-146).

## Thematic Significance

The Montmartre comparison encapsulates the novel's persistent mapping of provincial Normandy onto Parisian coordinates. By describing Ingouville through a Parisian lens, Balzac simultaneously elevates his Norman setting (worthy of such a comparison) and diminishes it (defined only by reference to the capital). The gesture is characteristic of the novel's provincial consciousness — always measuring itself against Paris, always finding itself a "lesser" version.

## Historical Context

In the 1820s–1830s, Montmartre was an independent commune outside the Paris city walls, not annexed until 1860 under Baron Haussmann. Its windmills, including the famous Moulin de la Galette, and its gypsum quarries gave it a distinct identity. The hill was a popular destination for Sunday excursions by Parisians seeking fresh air and views. Balzac's use of Montmartre as a ready geographical reference assumes his readers' familiarity with this notable feature of the Parisian landscape.
