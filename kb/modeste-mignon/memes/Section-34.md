# Section 34

[034-001]Instead of endeavoring to appear more amiable and wittier than his

[034-002]rival, [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] imitated the Duc d’Herouville, and was gloomy, anxious,

[034-003]and watchful; but whereas the courier studied the freaks of the young

[034-004]heiress, Ernest simply fell a prey to the pains of dark and concentrated

[034-005]jealousy. He had not yet been able to obtain a glance from his idol.

[034-006]After a while he left the room with Butscha.

[034-007]“It is all over!” he said; “she is caught by him; I am more disagreeable

[034-008]to her, and moreover, she is right. [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] is charming; there’s

[034-009]intellect in his silence, passion in his eyes, [[poetry]] in his

[034-010]rhodomontades.”

[034-011]“Is he an honest man?” asked Butscha.

[034-012]“Oh, yes,” replied La Briere. “He is loyal and chivalrous, and capable

[034-013]of getting rid, under [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s influence, of those affectations which

[034-014]Madame de Chaulieu has taught him.”

[034-015]“You are a fine fellow,” said the hunchback; “but is he capable of

[034-016]loving,--will he [[love]] her?”

[034-017]“I don’t know,” answered La Briere. “Has she said anything about me?” he

[034-018]asked after a moment’s silence.

[034-019]“Yes,” said Butscha, and he repeated Modeste’s speech about disguises.

[034-020]Poor Ernest flung himself upon a bench and held his head in his hands.

[034-021]He could not keep back his tears, and he did not wish Butscha to see

[034-022]them; but the dwarf was the very man to guess his emotion.

[034-023]“What troubles you?” he asked.

[034-024]“She is right!” cried Ernest, springing up; “I am a wretch.”

[034-025]And he related the deception into which Canalis had led him when

[034-026]Modeste’s first letter was received, carefully pointing out to Butscha

[034-027]that he had wished to undeceive the young girl before she herself

[034-028]took off the mask, and apostrophizing, in rather juvenile fashion, his

[034-029]luckless destiny. Butscha sympathetically understood the love in the

[034-030]flavor and vigor of his simple language, and in his deep and genuine

[034-031]anxiety.

[034-032]“But why don’t you show yourself to Mademoiselle Modeste for what you

[034-033]are?” he said; “why do you let your rival do his exercises?”

[034-034]“Have you never felt your throat tighten when you wished to speak to

[034-035]her?” cried La Briere; “is there never a strange feeling in the roots of

[034-036]your hair and on the surface of your skin when she looks at you,--even

[034-037]if she is thinking of something else?”

[034-038]“But you had sufficient judgment to show displeasure when she as good as

[034-039]told her excellent father that he was a dolt.”

[034-040]“Monsieur, I love her too well not to have felt a knife in my heart when

[034-041]I heard her contradicting her own perfections.”

[034-042]“Canalis supported her.”

[034-043]“If she had more self-love than heart there would be nothing for a man

[034-044]to regret in losing her,” answered La Briere.

[034-045]At this moment, Modeste, followed by Canalis, who had lost the rubber,

[034-046]came out with her father and Madame [[dumay|Dumay]] to breathe the fresh air of

[034-047]the starry night. While his daughter walked about with the poet, Charles

[034-048][[charles-mignon|Mignon]] left her and came up to La Briere.

[034-049]“Your friend, monsieur, ought to have been a lawyer,” he said, smiling

[034-050]and looking attentively at the young man.

[034-051]“You must not judge a poet as you would an ordinary man,--as you would

[034-052]me, for example, Monsieur le comte,” said La Briere. “A poet has a

[034-053]mission. He is obliged by his nature to see the poetry of questions,

[034-054]just as he expresses that of things. When you think him inconsistent

[034-055]with himself he is really faithful to his vocation. He is a painter

[034-056]copying with equal truth a Madonna and a courtesan. Moliere is as true

[034-057]to nature in his old men as in his young ones, and Moliere’s judgment

[034-058]was assuredly a sound and healthy one. These witty paradoxes might be

[034-059]dangerous for second-rate minds, but they have no real influence on the

[034-060]character of great men.”

[034-061]Charles Mignon pressed La Briere’s hand.

[034-062]“That adaptability, however, leads a man to excuse himself in his own

[034-063]eyes for actions that are diametrically opposed to each other; above

[034-064]all, in politics.”

[034-065]“Ah, mademoiselle,” Canalis was at this moment saying, in a caressing

[034-066]voice, replying to a roguish remark of Modeste, “do not think that a

[034-067]multiplicity of emotions can in any way lessen the strength of feelings.

[034-068]Poets, even more than other men, must needs love with constancy and

[034-069]faith. You must not be jealous of what is called the Muse. Happy is the

[034-070]wife of a man whose days are occupied. If you heard the complaints of

[034-071]women who have to endure the burden of an idle husband, either a man

[034-072]without duties, or one so rich as to have nothing to do, you would know

[034-073]that the highest happiness of a Parisian wife is freedom,--the right

[034-074]to rule in her own home. Now we writers and men of functions and

[034-075]occupations, we leave the sceptre to our wives; we cannot descend to

[034-076]the tyranny of little minds; we have something better to do. If I ever

[034-077]marry,--which I assure you is a catastrophe very remote at the present

[034-078]moment,--I should wish my wife to enjoy the same moral freedom that

[034-079]a mistress enjoys, and which is perhaps the real source of her

[034-080]attraction.”

[034-081]Canalis talked on, displaying the warmth of his fancy and all his

[034-082]graces, for Modeste’s benefit, as he spoke of love, [[marriage]], and the

[034-083]adoration of women, until Monsieur Mignon, who had rejoined them, seized

[034-084]the opportunity of a slight pause to take his daughter’s arm and lead

[034-085]her up to Ernest de La Briere, whom he had been advising to seek an open

[034-086]explanation with her.

[034-087]“Mademoiselle,” said Ernest, in a voice that was scarcely his own,

[034-088]“it is impossible for me to remain any longer under the weight of

[034-089]your displeasure. I do not defend myself; I do not seek to justify my

[034-090]conduct; I desire only to make you see that _before_ reading your most

[034-091]flattering letter, addressed to the individual and no longer to the

[034-092]poet,--the last which you sent to me,--I wished, and I told you in my

[034-093]note written at [[provincial-life|Havre]] that I wished, to correct the error under which

[034-094]you were acting. All the feelings that I have had the happiness to

[034-095]express to you are sincere. A hope dawned on me in [[paris|Paris]] when your

[034-096]father told me he was comparatively poor,--but now that all is lost, now

[034-097]that nothing is left for me but endless regrets, why should I stay

[034-098]here where all is torture? Let me carry away with me one smile to live

[034-099]forever in my heart.”

[034-100]“Monsieur,” answered Modeste, who seemed cold and absent-minded, “I am

[034-101]not the mistress of this house; but I certainly should deeply regret to

[034-102]retain any one where he finds neither pleasure nor happiness.”

[034-103]She left La Briere and took Madame Dumay’s arm to re-enter the house. A

[034-104]few moments later all the actors in this domestic scene reassembled in

[034-105]the salon, and were a good deal surprised to see Modeste sitting beside

[034-106]the Duc d’Herouville and coquetting with him like an accomplished

[034-107]Parisian woman. She watched his play, gave him the advice he wanted, and

[034-108]found occasion to say flattering things by ranking the merits of noble

[034-109]birth with those of genius and beauty. Canalis thought he knew the

[034-110]reason of this change; he had tried to pique Modeste by calling marriage

[034-111]a catastrophe, and showing that he was aloof from it; but like others

[034-112]who play with fire, he had burned his fingers. Modeste’s pride and her

[034-113]present disdain frightened him, and he endeavored to recover his ground,

[034-114]exhibiting a jealousy which was all the more visible because it was

[034-115]artificial. Modeste, implacable as an angel, tasted the sweets of power,

[034-116]and, naturally enough, abused it. The Duc d’Herouville had never known

[034-117]such a happy evening; a woman smiled on him! At eleven o’clock, an

[034-118]unheard-of hour at [[chalet|the Chalet]], the three suitors took their leave,--the

[034-119]duke thinking Modeste charming, Canalis believing her excessively

[034-120]coquettish, and La Briere heart-broken by her cruelty.

[034-121]For eight days the heiress continued to be to her three lovers very

[034-122]much what she had been during that evening; so that the poet appeared

[034-123]to carry the day against his rivals, in spite of certain freaks and

[034-124]caprices which from time to time gave the Duc d’Herouville a little

[034-125]hope. The disrespect she showed to her father, and the great liberties

[034-126]she took with him; her impatience with her blind mother, to whom she

[034-127]seemed to grudge the little services which had once been the delight

[034-128]of her filial piety,--seemed the result of a capricious nature and a

[034-129]heedless gaiety indulged from childhood. When Modeste went too far, she

[034-130]turned round and openly took herself to task, ascribing her impertinence

[034-131]and levity to a spirit of independence. She acknowledged to the duke

[034-132]and Canalis her distaste for obedience, and professed to regard it as an

[034-133]obstacle to her marriage; thus investigating the nature of her suitors,

[034-134]after the manner of those who dig into the earth in search of metals,

[034-135]coal, tufa, or water.

[034-136]“I shall never,” she said, the evening before the day on which the

[034-137]family were to move into the villa, “find a husband who will put up with

[034-138]my caprices as my father does; his kindness never flags. I am sure no

[034-139]one will ever be as indulgent to me as my precious mother.”

[034-140]“They know that you love them, mademoiselle,” said La Briere.

[034-141]“You may be very sure, mademoiselle, that your husband will know the

[034-142]full value of his treasure,” added the duke.

[034-143]“You have spirit and resolution enough to discipline a husband,” cried

[034-144]Canalis, laughing.

[034-145]Modeste smiled as Henri IV. must have smiled after drawing out the

[034-146]characters of his three principal ministers, for the benefit of a

[034-147]foreign ambassador, by means of three answers to an insidious question.

[034-148]On the day of the dinner, Modeste, led away by the preference she

[034-149]bestowed on Canalis, walked alone with him up and down the gravelled

[034-150]space which lay between the house and the lawn with its flower-beds.

[034-151]From the gestures of the poet, and the air and manner of the young

[034-152]heiress, it was easy to see that she was listening favorably to him.

[034-153]The two demoiselles d’Herouville hastened to interrupt the scandalous

[034-154]tete-a-tete; and with the natural cleverness of women under such

[034-155]circumstances, they turned the conversation on the court, and the

[034-156]distinction of an appointment under the crown,--pointing out the

[034-157]difference that existed between appointments in the household of the

[034-158]king and those of the crown. They tried to intoxicate Modeste’s mind by

[034-159]appealing to her pride, and describing one of the highest stations to

[034-160]which a woman could aspire.

[034-161]“To have a duke for a son,” said the elder lady, “is an actual

[034-162]advantage. The title is a [[commerce|fortune]] that we secure to our children without

[034-163]the possibility of loss.”

[034-164]“How is it, then,” said Canalis, displeased at his tete-a-tete being

[034-165]thus broken in upon, “that Monsieur le duc has had so little success in

[034-166]a matter where his title would seem to be of special service to him?”

[034-167]The two ladies cast a look at Canalis as full of venom as the tooth of a

[034-168]snake, and they were so disconcerted by Modeste’s amused smile that they

[034-169]were actually unable to reply.

[034-170]“Monsieur le duc has never blamed you,” she said to Canalis, “for the

[034-171]humility with which you bear your fame; why should you attack him for

[034-172]his modesty?”

[034-173]“Besides, we have never yet met a woman worthy of my nephew’s rank,”

[034-174]said Mademoiselle d’Herouville. “Some had only the wealth of the

[034-175]position; others, without [[social-mobility|fortune]], had the wit and birth. I must admit

[034-176]that we have done well to wait till God granted us an opportunity to

[034-177]meet one in whom we find the noble blood, the mind, and fortune of a

[034-178]Duchesse d’Herouville.”

[034-179]“My dear Modeste,” said Helene d’Herouville, leading her new friend

[034-180]apart, “there are a thousand barons in the kingdom, just as there are a

[034-181]hundred poets in Paris, who are worth as much as he; he is so little of

[034-182]a great man that even I, a poor girl forced to take the veil for want

[034-183]of a ‘dot,’ I would not take him. You don’t know what a young man is who

[034-184]has been for ten years in the hands of a Duchesse de Chaulieu. None but

[034-185]an old woman of sixty could put up with the little ailments of which,

[034-186]they say, the great poet is always complaining,--a habit in Louis XIV.

[034-187]that became a perfectly insupportable annoyance. It is true the duchess

[034-188]does not suffer from it as much as a wife, who would have him always

[034-189]about her.”
