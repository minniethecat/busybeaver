# Section 23

[023-001]At the hour when La Briere was inquiring about the father of his beloved

[023-002]from the head of the house of Mongenod, and getting information that

[023-003]might be useful to him in his strange position, a scene was taking place

[023-004]in [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]’s study which the ex-lieutenant’s hasty departure from [[provincial-life|Havre]]

[023-005]may have led the reader to foresee.

[023-006]Like a true soldier of the imperial school, [[dumay|Dumay]], whose Breton blood

[023-007]had boiled all the way to [[paris|Paris]], considered a [[poetry|poet]] to be a poor stick of

[023-008]a fellow, of no consequence whatever,--a buffoon addicted to choruses,

[023-009]living in a garret, dressed in black clothes that were white at every

[023-010]seam, wearing boots that were occasionally without soles, and linen that

[023-011]was unmentionable, and whose fingers knew more about ink than soap; in

[023-012]short, one who looked always as if he had tumbled from the moon,

[023-013]except when scribbling at a desk, like Butscha. But the seething of the

[023-014]Breton’s heart and brain received a violent application of cold water

[023-015]when he entered the courtyard of the pretty house occupied by the poet

[023-016]and saw a groom washing a carriage, and also, through the windows of a

[023-017]handsome dining-room, a valet dressed like a banker, to whom the groom

[023-018]referred him, and who answered, looking the stranger over from head to

[023-019]foot, that Monsieur le baron was not visible. “There is,” added the man,

[023-020]“a meeting of the council of state to-day, at which Monsieur le baron is

[023-021]obliged to be present.”

[023-022]“Is this really the house of Monsieur Canalis,” said Dumay, “a writer of

[023-023]poetry?”

[023-024]“Monsieur le baron de Canalis,” replied the valet, “is the great poet

[023-025]of whom you speak; but he is also the president of the court of Claims

[023-026]attached to the ministry of foreign affairs.”

[023-027]Dumay, who had come to box the ears of a scribbling nobody, found

[023-028]himself confronted by a high functionary of the state. The salon

[023-029]where he was told to wait offered, as a topic for his meditations, the

[023-030]insignia of the Legion of [[honor]] glittering on a black coat which the

[023-031]valet had left upon a chair. Presently his eyes were attracted by the

[023-032]beauty and brilliancy of a silver-gilt cup bearing the words “Given by

[023-033]_Madame_.” Then he beheld before him, on a pedestal, a Sevres vase on

[023-034]which was engraved, “The gift of Madame la _Dauphine_.”

[023-035]These mute admonitions brought Dumay to his senses while the valet went

[023-036]to ask his master if he would receive a person who had come from Havre

[023-037]expressly to see him,--a stranger named Dumay.

[023-038]“What sort of a man?” asked Canalis.

[023-039]“He is well-dressed, and wears the ribbon of the Legion of honor.”

[023-040]Canalis made a sign of assent, and the valet retreated, and then

[023-041]returned and announced, “Monsieur Dumay.”

[023-042]When he heard himself announced, when he was actually in presence of

[023-043]Canalis, in a study as gorgeous as it was elegant, with his feet on a

[023-044]carpet far handsomer than any in the house of [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], and when he met

[023-045]the studied glance of the poet who was playing with the tassels of a

[023-046]sumptuous dressing-gown, Dumay was so completely taken aback that he

[023-047]allowed the great poet to have the first word.

[023-048]“To what do I owe the honor of your visit, monsieur?”

[023-049]“Monsieur,” began Dumay, who remained standing.

[023-050]“If you have a good deal to say,” interrupted Canalis, “I must ask you

[023-051]to be seated.”

[023-052]And Canalis himself plunged into an armchair a la Voltaire, crossed his

[023-053]legs, raised the upper one to the level of his eye and looked fixedly at

[023-054]Dumay, who became, to use his own martial slang, “bayonetted.”

[023-055]“I am listening, monsieur,” said the poet; “my time is precious,--the

[023-056]ministers are expecting me.”

[023-057]“Monsieur,” said Dumay, “I shall be brief. You have seduced--how, I do

[023-058]not know--a young lady in Havre, young, beautiful, and rich; the

[023-059]last and only hope of two noble families; and I have come to ask your

[023-060]intentions.”

[023-061]Canalis, who had been busy during the last three months with serious

[023-062]matters of his own, and was trying to get himself made commander of the

[023-063]Legion of honor and minister to a German court, had completely forgotten

[023-064][[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s letter.”

[023-065]“I!” he exclaimed.

[023-066]“You!” repeated Dumay.

[023-067]“Monsieur,” answered Canalis, smiling; “I know no more of what you are

[023-068]talking about than if you had said it in Hebrew. I seduce a young

[023-069]girl! I, who--” and a superb smile crossed his features. “Come, come,

[023-070]monsieur, I’m not such a child as to steal fruit over the hedges when I

[023-071]have orchards and gardens of my own where the finest peaches ripen. All

[023-072]Paris knows where my affections are set. Very likely there may be some

[023-073]young girl in Havre full of enthusiasm for my verses,--of which they are

[023-074]not worthy; that would not surprise me at all; nothing is more common.

[023-075]See! look at that lovely coffer of ebony inlaid with mother-of-pearl,

[023-076]and edged with that iron-work as fine as lace. That coffer belonged

[023-077]to Pope Leo X., and was given to me by the Duchesse de Chaulieu, who

[023-078]received it from the king of Spain. I use it to hold the [[epistolary-novel|letters]] I

[023-079]receive from ladies and young girls living in every quarter of Europe.

[023-080]Oh! I assure you I feel the utmost respect for these flowers of the

[023-081]soul, cut and sent in moments of enthusiasm that are worthy of all

[023-082]reverence. Yes, to me the impulse of a heart is a noble and sublime

[023-083]thing! Others--scoffers--light their cigars with such letters, or give

[023-084]them to their wives for curl-papers; but I, who am a bachelor, monsieur,

[023-085]I have too much delicacy not to preserve these artless offerings--so

[023-086]fresh, so disinterested--in a tabernacle of their own. In fact, I guard

[023-087]them with a species of veneration, and at my death they will be burned

[023-088]before my eyes. People may call that ridiculous, but I do not care. I am

[023-089]grateful; these proofs of devotion enable me to bear the criticisms and

[023-090]annoyances of a literary life. When I receive a shot in the back from

[023-091]some enemy lurking under cover of a daily paper, I look at that casket

[023-092]and think,--here and there in this wide world there are hearts whose

[023-093]wounds have been healed, or soothed, or dressed by me!”

[023-094]This bit of poetry, declaimed with all the talent of a great actor,

[023-095]petrified the lieutenant, whose eyes opened to their utmost extent, and

[023-096]whose astonishment delighted the poet.

[023-097]“I will permit you,” continued the peacock, spreading his tail, “out of

[023-098]respect for your position, which I fully appreciate, to open that coffer

[023-099]and look for the letter of your young lady. Though I know I am right, I

[023-100]remember names, and I assure you you are mistaken in thinking--”

[023-101]“And this is what a poor child comes to in this gulf of Paris!” cried

[023-102]Dumay,--“the darling of her parents, the joy of her friends, the hope

[023-103]of all, petted by all, the pride of a family, who has six persons so

[023-104]devoted to her that they would willingly make a rampart of their lives

[023-105]and fortunes between her and sorrow. Monsieur,” Dumay remarked after a

[023-106]pause, “you are a great poet, and I am only a poor soldier. For fifteen

[023-107]years I served my country in the ranks; I have had the wind of many a

[023-108]bullet in my face; I have crossed Siberia and been a prisoner there; the

[023-109]Russians flung me on a kibitka, and God knows what I suffered. I have

[023-110]seen thousands of my comrades die,--but you, you have given me a chill

[023-111]to the marrow of my bones, such as I never felt before.”

[023-112]Dumay fancied that his words moved the poet, but in fact they only

[023-113]flattered him,--a thing which at this period of his life had become

[023-114]almost an impossibility; for his ambitious mind had long forgotten the

[023-115]first perfumed phial that praise had broken over his head.

[023-116]“Ah, my soldier!” he said solemnly, laying his hand on Dumay’s shoulder,

[023-117]and thinking to himself how droll it was to make a soldier of the empire

[023-118]tremble, “this young girl may be all in all to you, but to society at

[023-119]large what is she? nothing. At this moment the greatest mandarin in

[023-120]China may be yielding up the ghost and putting half the universe in

[023-121]mourning, and what is that to you? The English are killing thousands of

[023-122]people in India more worthy than we are; why, at this very moment while

[023-123]I am speaking to you some ravishing woman is being burned alive,--did

[023-124]that make you care less for your cup of coffee this morning at

[023-125]breakfast? Not a day passes in Paris that some mother in rags does not

[023-126]cast her infant on the world to be picked up by whoever finds it; and

[023-127]yet see! here is this delicious tea in a cup that cost five louis, and

[023-128]I write verses which Parisian women rush to buy, exclaiming, ‘Divine!

[023-129]delicious! charming! food for the soul!’ Social nature, like Nature

[023-130]herself, is a great forgetter. You will be quite surprised ten years

[023-131]hence at what you have done to-day. You are here in a city where people

[023-132]die, where they marry, where they adore each other at an assignation,

[023-133]where young girls suffocate themselves, where the man of genius with

[023-134]his cargo of thoughts teeming with humane beneficence goes to the

[023-135]bottom,--all side by side, sometimes under the same roof, and yet

[023-136]ignorant of each other, ignorant and indifferent. And here you come

[023-137]among us and ask us to expire with grief at this commonplace affair.”

[023-138]“You call yourself a poet!” cried Dumay, “but don’t you feel what you

[023-139]write?”

[023-140]“Good heavens! if we endured the joys or the woes we sing we should

[023-141]be as worn out in three months as a pair of old boots,” said the poet,

[023-142]smiling. “But stay, you shall not come from Havre to Paris to see

[023-143]Canalis without carrying something back with you. Warrior!” (Canalis had

[023-144]the form and action of an Homeric hero) “learn this from the poet: Every

[023-145]noble sentiment in man is a poem so exclusively individual that his

[023-146]nearest friend, his other self, cares nothing for it. It is a treasure

[023-147]which is his alone, it is--”

[023-148]“Forgive me for interrupting you,” said Dumay, who was gazing at the

[023-149]poet with horror, “but did you ever come to Havre?”

[023-150]“I was there for a day and a night in the spring of 1824 on my way to

[023-151]London.”

[023-152]“You are a man of honor,” continued Dumay; “will you give me your word

[023-153]that you do not know Mademoiselle Modeste Mignon?”

[023-154]“This is the first time that name ever struck my ear,” replied Canalis.

[023-155]“Ah, monsieur!” said Dumay, “into what dark intrigue am I about to

[023-156]plunge? Can I count upon you to help me in my inquiries?--for I am

[023-157]certain that some one has been using your name. You ought to have had a

[023-158]letter yesterday from Havre.”

[023-159]“I received none. Be sure, monsieur, that I will help you,” said

[023-160]Canalis, “so far as I have the opportunity of doing so.”

[023-161]Dumay withdrew, his heart torn with anxiety, believing that the wretched

[023-162]Butscha had worn the skin of the poet to deceive Modeste; whereas

[023-163]Butscha himself, keen-witted as a prince seeking revenge, and far

[023-164]cleverer than any paid spy, was ferretting out the life and actions

[023-165]of Canalis, escaping notice by his insignificance, like an insect that

[023-166]bores its way into the sap of a tree.

[023-167]The Breton had scarcely left the poet’s house when La Briere entered his

[023-168]friend’s study. Naturally, Canalis told him of the visit of the man from

[023-169]Havre.

[023-170]“Ha!” said [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], “Modeste Mignon; that is just what I have come to

[023-171]speak of.”

[023-172]“Ah, bah!” cried Canalis; “have I had a triumph by proxy?”

[023-173]“Yes; and here is the key to it. My friend, I am loved by the sweetest

[023-174]girl in all the world,--beautiful enough to shine beside the greatest

[023-175]beauties in Paris, with a heart and mind worthy of Clarissa. She has

[023-176]seen me; I have pleased her, and she thinks me the great Canalis. But

[023-177]that is not all. Modeste Mignon is of high birth, and Mongenod has just

[023-178]told me that her father, the Comte de La Bastie, has something like six

[023-179]millions. The father is here now, and I have asked him through Mongenod

[023-180]for an interview at two o’clock. Mongenod is to give him a hint, just

[023-181]a word, that it concerns the happiness of his daughter. But you will

[023-182]readily understand that before seeing the father I feel I ought to make

[023-183]a clean breast of it to you.”
