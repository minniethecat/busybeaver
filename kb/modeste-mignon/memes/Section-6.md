# Section 6

[006-001]On sunny days, between breakfast and dinner, Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] and Madame

[006-002][[dumay|Dumay]] took a little walk toward the sea. [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] accompanied them, for

[006-003]two arms were needed to support the blind mother. [[About]] a month before

[006-004]the scene to which this explanation is a parenthesis, Madame Mignon

[006-005]had taken counsel with her friends, Madame Latournelle, the notary, and

[006-006]Dumay, while Madame Dumay carried Modeste in another direction for a

[006-007]longer walk.

[006-008]“Listen to what I have to say,” said the blind woman. “My daughter is in

[006-009][[love]]. I feel it; I see it. A singular change has taken place within her,

[006-010]and I do not see how it is that none of you have perceived it.”

[006-011]“In the name of all that’s honorable--” cried the lieutenant.

[006-012]“Don’t interrupt me, Dumay. For the last two months Modeste has taken as

[006-013]much care of her personal appearance as if she expected to meet a lover.

[006-014]She has grown extremely fastidious about her shoes; she wants to set off

[006-015]her pretty feet; she scolds Madame Gobet, the shoemaker. It is the

[006-016]same thing with her milliner. Some days my poor darling is absorbed in

[006-017]thought, evidently expectant, as if waiting for some one. Her voice has

[006-018]curt tones when she answers a question, as though she were interrupted

[006-019]in the current of her thoughts and secret expectations. Then, if this

[006-020]awaited lover has come--”

[006-021]“Good heavens!”

[006-022]“Sit down, Dumay,” said the blind woman. “Well, then Modeste is gay. Oh!

[006-023]she is not gay to your sight; you cannot catch these gradations; they

[006-024]are too delicate for eyes that see only the outside of nature. Her

[006-025]gaiety is betrayed to me by the tones of her voice, by certain accents

[006-026]which I alone can catch and understand. Modeste then, instead of sitting

[006-027]still and thoughtful, gives vent to a wild, inward activity by impulsive

[006-028]movements,--in short, she is happy. There is a grace, a charm in the

[006-029]very ideas she utters. Ah, my friends, I know happiness as well as I

[006-030]know sorrow; I know its signs. By the kiss my Modeste gives me I can

[006-031]guess what is passing within her. I know whether she has received what

[006-032]she was looking for, or whether she is uneasy or expectant. There are

[006-033]many gradations in a kiss, even in that of an innocent young girl, and

[006-034]Modeste is innocence itself; but hers is the innocence of knowledge,

[006-035]not of ignorance. I may be blind, but my tenderness is all-seeing, and I

[006-036]charge you to watch over my daughter.”

[006-037]Dumay, now actually ferocious, the notary, in the character of a

[006-038]man bound to ferret out a mystery, Madame Latournelle, the deceived

[006-039]chaperone, and Madame Dumay, alarmed for her husband’s safety, became

[006-040]at once a set of spies, and Modeste from this day forth was never left

[006-041]alone for an instant. Dumay passed nights under her window wrapped in

[006-042]his cloak like a jealous Spaniard; but with all his military sagacity

[006-043]he was unable to detect the least suspicious sign. Unless she loved the

[006-044]nightingales in the villa park, or some fairy prince, Modeste could have

[006-045]seen no one, and had neither given nor received a signal. Madame Dumay,

[006-046]who never went to bed till she knew Modeste was asleep, watched the

[006-047]road from the upper windows of [[chalet|the Chalet]] with a vigilance equal to her

[006-048]husband’s. Under these eight Argus eyes the blameless child, whose

[006-049]every motion was studied and analyzed, came out of the ordeal so fully

[006-050]acquitted of all criminal conversation that the four friends declared

[006-051]to each other privately that Madame Mignon was foolishly over-anxious.

[006-052]Madame Latournelle, who always took Modeste to church and brought her

[006-053]back again, was commissioned to tell the mother that she was mistaken

[006-054]about her daughter.

[006-055]“Modeste,” she said, “is a young girl of very exalted ideas; she works

[006-056]herself into enthusiasm for the [[poetry]] of one writer or the prose of

[006-057]another. You have only to judge by the impression made upon her by

[006-058]that scaffold symphony, ‘The Last Hours of a Convict’” (the saying was

[006-059]Butscha’s, who supplied wit to his benefactress with a lavish hand);

[006-060]“she seemed to me all but crazy with admiration for that Monsieur Hugo.

[006-061]I’m sure I don’t know where such people” (Victor Hugo, Lamartine, Byron

[006-062]being _such people_ to the Madame Latournelles of the [[bourgeoisie]]) “get

[006-063]their ideas. Modeste kept talking to me of Childe Harold, and as I did

[006-064]not wish to get the worst of the argument I was silly enough to try

[006-065]to read the thing. Perhaps it was the fault of the translator, but it

[006-066]actually turned my stomach; I was dazed; I couldn’t possibly finish it.

[006-067]Why, the man talks about comparisons that howl, rocks that faint, and

[006-068]waves of war! However, he is only a travelling Englishman, and we must

[006-069]expect absurdities,--though his are really inexcusable. He takes you to

[006-070]Spain, and sets you in the clouds above the Alps, and makes the torrents

[006-071]talk, and the stars; and he says there are too many virgins! Did you

[006-072]ever hear the like? Then, after Napoleon’s campaigns, the lines are full

[006-073]of sonorous brass and flaming cannon-balls, rolling along from page to

[006-074]page. Modeste tells me that all that bathos is put in by the translator,

[006-075]and that I ought to read the book in English. But I certainly sha’n’t

[006-076]learn English to read Lord Byron when I didn’t learn it to teach

[006-077]Exupere. I much prefer the novels of Ducray-Dumenil to all these

[006-078]English romances. I’m too good a Norman to fall in love with foreign

[006-079]things,--above all when they come from England.”

[006-080]Madame Mignon, notwithstanding her melancholy, could not help smiling at

[006-081]the idea of Madame Latournelle reading Childe Harold. The stern scion of

[006-082]a parliamentary house accepted the smile as an approval of her doctrine.

[006-083]“And, therefore, my dear Madame Mignon,” she went on, “you have taken

[006-084]Modeste’s fancies, which are nothing but the results of her reading,

[006-085]for a love-affair. Remember, she is just twenty. Girls fall in love with

[006-086]themselves at that age; they dress to see themselves well-dressed. I

[006-087]remember I used to make my little sister, now dead, put on a man’s hat

[006-088]and pretend we were monsieur and madame. You see, you had a very happy

[006-089]youth in Frankfort; but let us be just,--Modeste is living here without

[006-090]the slightest amusement. Although, to be sure, her every wish is

[006-091]attended to, still she knows she is shut up and watched, and the life

[006-092]she leads would give her no pleasure at all if it were not for the

[006-093]amusement she gets out of her books. Come, don’t worry yourself; she

[006-094]loves nobody but you. You ought to be very glad that she goes into these

[006-095]enthusiasms for the corsairs of Byron and the heroes of Walter Scott and

[006-096]your own Germans, Egmont, Goethe, Werther, Schiller, and all the other

[006-097]‘ers.’”

[006-098]“Well, madame, what do you say to that?” asked Dumay, respectfully,

[006-099]alarmed at Madame Mignon’s silence.

[006-100]“Modeste is not only inclined to love, but she loves some man,” answered

[006-101]the mother, obstinately.

[006-102]“Madame, my life is at stake, and you must allow me--not for my sake,

[006-103]but for my wife, my colonel, for all of us--to probe this matter to the

[006-104]bottom, and find out whether it is the mother or the watch-dog who is

[006-105]deceived.”

[006-106]“It is you who are deceived, Dumay. Ah! if I could but see my daughter!”

[006-107]cried the poor woman.

[006-108]“But whom is it possible for her to love?” asked the notary. “I’ll

[006-109]answer for my Exupere.”

[006-110]“It can’t be Gobenheim,” said Dumay, “for since the colonel’s departure

[006-111]he has not spent nine hours a week in this house. Besides, he doesn’t

[006-112]even notice Modeste--that five-franc piece of a man! His uncle

[006-113]Gobenheim-Keller is all the time writing him, ‘Get rich enough to marry

[006-114]a Keller.’ With that idea in his mind you may be sure he doesn’t know

[006-115]which sex Modeste belongs to. No other men ever come here,--for of

[006-116]course I don’t count Butscha, poor little fellow; I love him! He is your

[006-117]Dumay, madame,” said the cashier to Madame Latournelle. “Butscha knows

[006-118]very well that a mere glance at Modeste would cost him a Breton ducking.

[006-119]Not a soul has any communication with this house. Madame Latournelle who

[006-120]takes Modeste to church ever since your--your misfortune, madame, has

[006-121]carefully watched her on the way and all through the service, and has

[006-122]seen nothing suspicious. In short, if I must confess the truth, I have

[006-123]myself raked all the paths about the house every evening for the last

[006-124]month, and found no trace of footsteps in the morning.”

[006-125]“Rakes are neither costly nor difficult to handle,” remarked the

[006-126]daughter of Germany.

[006-127]“But the dogs?” cried Dumay.

[006-128]“Lovers have philters even for dogs,” answered Madame Mignon.

[006-129]“If you are right, my [[honor]] is lost! I may as well blow my brains out,”

[006-130]exclaimed Dumay.

[006-131]“Why so, Dumay?” said the blind woman.

[006-132]“Ah, madame, I could never meet my colonel’s eye if he did not find his

[006-133]daughter--now his only daughter--as pure and virtuous as she was when

[006-134]he said to me on the vessel, ‘Let no fear of the scaffold hinder you,

[006-135]Dumay, if the honor of my Modeste is at stake.’”

[006-136]“Ah! I recognize you both,” said Madame Mignon in a voice of strong

[006-137]emotion.

[006-138]“I’ll wager my salvation that Modeste is as pure as she was in her

[006-139]cradle,” exclaimed Madame Dumay.

[006-140]“Well, I shall make certain of it,” replied her husband, “if Madame

[006-141]la Comtesse will allow me to employ certain means; for old troopers

[006-142]understand strategy.”

[006-143]“I will allow you to do anything that shall enlighten us, provided it

[006-144]does no injury to my last child.”

[006-145]“What are you going to do, Jean?” asked Madame Dumay; “how can you

[006-146]discover a young girl’s secret if she means to hide it?”

[006-147]“Obey me, all!” cried the lieutenant, “I shall need every one of you.”

[006-148]If this rapid sketch were clearly developed it would give a whole

[006-149]picture of manners and customs in which many a family could recognize

[006-150]the events of their own history; but it must suffice as it is to explain

[006-151]the importance of the few details heretofore given about persons and

[006-152]things on the memorable evening when the old soldier had made ready his

[006-153]plot against the young girl, intending to wrench from the recesses of

[006-154]her heart the secret of a love and a lover seen only by a blind mother.

[006-155]CHAPTER V. THE PROBLEM STILL UNSOLVED

[006-156]An hour went by in solemn stillness broken only by the cabalistic

[006-157]phrases of the whist-players: “Spades!” “Trumped!” “Cut!” “How are

[006-158]honors?” “Two to four.” “Whose deal?”--phrases which represent in these

[006-159]days the higher emotions of the European [[aristocracy]]. Modeste continued

[006-160]to work, without seeming to be surprised at her mother’s silence.

[006-161]Madame Mignon’s handkerchief slipped from her lap to the floor; Butscha

[006-162]precipitated himself upon it, picked it up, and as he returned it

[006-163]whispered in Modeste’s ear, “Take care!” Modeste raised a pair of

[006-164]wondering eyes, whose puzzled glance filled the poor cripple with joy

[006-165]unspeakable. “She is not in love!” he whispered to himself, rubbing his

[006-166]hands till the skin was nearly peeled off. At this moment Exupere

[006-167]tore through the garden and the house, plunged into the salon like an

[006-168]avalanche, and said to Dumay in an audible whisper, “The young man is

[006-169]here!” Dumay sprang for his pistols and rushed out.

[006-170]“Good God! suppose he kills him!” cried Madame Dumay, bursting into

[006-171]tears.

[006-172]“What is the matter?” asked Modeste, looking innocently at her friends

[006-173]and not betraying the slightest fear.

[006-174]“It is all about a young man who is hanging round the house,” cried

[006-175]Madame Latournelle.

[006-176]“Well!” said Modeste, “why should Dumay kill him?”

[006-177]“Sancta simplicita!” ejaculated Butscha, looking at his master as

[006-178]proudly as Alexander is made to contemplate Babylon in Lebrun’s great

[006-179]picture.

[006-180]“Where are you going, Modeste?” asked the mother as her daughter rose to

[006-181]leave the room.
