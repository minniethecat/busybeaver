# Section 26

[026-001]“The secretary; his name is [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere. He is not a nobleman;

[026-002]but he is one of those plain men with fixed principles and sound

[026-003]morality who satisfy parents. However, that is not the point; you

[026-004]have seen him and nothing can change your heart; you have chosen him,

[026-005]comprehend his soul, it is as beautiful as he himself.”

[026-006]The count was interrupted by a heavy sigh from [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]. The poor girl

[026-007]sat with her eyes fixed on the sea, pale and rigid as death, as if a

[026-008]pistol shot had struck her in those fatal words, _a plain man, with

[026-009]fixed principles and sound morality_.

[026-010]“Deceived!” she said at last.

[026-011]“Like your poor sister, but less fatally.”

[026-012]“Let us go home, father,” she said, rising from the hillock on which

[026-013]they were sitting. “Papa, hear me, I swear before God to obey your

[026-014]wishes, whatever they may be, in the _affair_ of my [[marriage]].”

[026-015]“Then you don’t [[love]] him any longer?” asked her father.

[026-016]“I loved an honest man, with no falsehood on his face, upright as

[026-017]yourself, incapable of disguising himself like an actor, with the paint

[026-018]of another man’s glory on his cheeks.”

[026-019]“You said nothing could change you”; remarked the colonel, ironically.

[026-020]“Ah, do not trifle with me!” she exclaimed, clasping her hands and

[026-021]looking at her father in distressful anxiety; “don’t you see that you

[026-022]are wringing my heart and destroying my beliefs with your jokes.”

[026-023]“God forbid! I have told you the exact truth.”

[026-024]“You are very kind, father,” she said after a pause, and with a sort of

[026-025]solemnity.

[026-026]“He has kept your [[epistolary-novel|letters]],” resumed the colonel; “now suppose the rash

[026-027]caresses of your soul had fallen into the hands of one of those poets

[026-028]who, as [[dumay|Dumay]] says, light their cigars with them?”

[026-029]“Oh!--you are going too far.”

[026-030]“[[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] told him so.”

[026-031]“Has Dumay seen Canalis?”

[026-032]“Yes,” answered her father.

[026-033]The two walked along in silence.

[026-034]“So that is why that _gentleman_,” resumed Modeste, “told me so much

[026-035]harm of poets and [[poetry]]; no wonder the little secretary said--Why,” she

[026-036]added, interrupting herself, “his virtues, his noble qualities, his fine

[026-037]sentiments are nothing but an epistolary theft! The man who steals glory

[026-038]and a name may very likely--”

[026-039]“--break locks, steal purses, and cut people’s throats on the highway,”

[026-040]cried the colonel. “Ah, you young girls, that’s just like you,--with

[026-041]your peremptory opinions and your ignorance of life. A man who once

[026-042]deceives a woman was born under the scaffold on which he ought to die.”

[026-043]This ridicule stopped Modeste’s effervescence for a moment and least,

[026-044]and again there was silence.

[026-045]“My child,” said the colonel, presently, “men in society, as in nature

[026-046]everywhere, are made to win the hearts of women, and women must

[026-047]defend themselves. You have chosen to invert the parts. Was that wise?

[026-048]Everything is false in a false position. The first wrong-doing was

[026-049]yours. No, a man is not a monster because he seeks to please a woman; it

[026-050]is our right to win her by aggression with all its consequences, short

[026-051]of crime and cowardice. A man may have many virtues even if he does

[026-052]deceive a woman; if he deceives her, it is because he finds her wanting

[026-053]in some of the treasures that he sought in her. None but a queen, an

[026-054]actress, or a woman placed so far above a man that she seems to him a

[026-055]queen, can go to him of herself without incurring blame--and for a young

[026-056]girl to do it! Why, she is false to all that God has given her that is

[026-057]sacred and lovely and noble,--no matter with what grace or what poetry

[026-058]or what precautions she surrounds her fault.”

[026-059]“To seek the master and find the servant!” she said bitterly, “oh! I can

[026-060]never recover from it!”

[026-061]“Nonsense! Monsieur Ernest de La Briere is, to my thinking, fully the

[026-062]equal of the Baron de Canalis. He was private secretary of a cabinet

[026-063]minister, and he is now counsel for the Court of Claims; he has a heart,

[026-064]and he adores you, but--he _does not write verses_. No, I admit, he is

[026-065]not a poet; but for all that he may have a heart full of poetry. At

[026-066]any rate, my dear girl,” added her father, as Modeste made a gesture of

[026-067]disgust, “you are to see both of them, the sham and the true Canalis--”

[026-068]“Oh, papa!--”

[026-069]“Did you not swear just now to obey me in everything, even in the

[026-070]_affair_ of your marriage? Well, I allow you to choose which of the two

[026-071]you like best for a husband. You have begun by a poem, you shall finish

[026-072]with a bucolic, and try if you can discover the real character of these

[026-073]gentlemen here, in the country, on a few hunting or fishing excursions.”

[026-074]Modeste bowed her head and walked home with her father, listening to

[026-075]what he said but replying only in monosyllables.

[026-076]CHAPTER XVI. DISENCHANTED

[026-077]The poor girl had fallen humiliated from the alp she had scaled in

[026-078]search of her eagle’s nest, into the mud of the swamp below, where (to

[026-079]use the poetic language of an author of our day) “after feeling the

[026-080]soles of her feet too tender to tread the broken glass of reality,

[026-081]Imagination--which in that delicate bosom united the whole of womanhood,

[026-082]from the violet-hidden reveries of a chaste young girl to the passionate

[026-083]desires of the sex--had led her into enchanted gardens where, oh, bitter

[026-084]sight! she now saw, springing from the ground, not the sublime flower

[026-085]of her fancy, but the hairy, twisted limbs of the black mandragora.”

[026-086]Modeste suddenly found herself brought down from the mystic heights of

[026-087]her love to a straight, flat road bordered with ditches,--in short the

[026-088]work-day path of common life. What ardent, aspiring soul would not have

[026-089]been bruised and broken by such a fall? Whose feet were these at which

[026-090]she had shed her thoughts? The Modeste who re-entered [[chalet|the Chalet]] was no

[026-091]more the Modeste who had left it two hours earlier than an actress in

[026-092]the street is like an actress on the boards. She fell into a state of

[026-093]numb depression that was pitiful to see. The sun was darkened, nature

[026-094]veiled itself, even the flowers no longer spoke to her. Like all young

[026-095]girls with a tendency to extremes, she drank too deeply of the cup of

[026-096]disillusion. She fought against reality, and would not bend her neck

[026-097]to the yoke of family and conventions; it was, she felt, too heavy,

[026-098]too hard, too crushing. She would not listen to the consolations of her

[026-099]father and mother, and tasted a sort of savage pleasure in letting her

[026-100]soul suffer to the utmost.

[026-101]“Poor Butscha was right,” she said one evening.

[026-102]The words indicate the distance she travelled in a short space of time

[026-103]and in gloomy sadness across the barren plain of reality. Sadness, when

[026-104]caused by the overgrowth of hope, is a disease,--sometimes a fatal one.

[026-105]It would be no mean object for physiology to search out in what ways

[026-106]and by what means Thought produces the same internal disorganization as

[026-107]poison; and how it is that despair affects the appetite, destroys the

[026-108]pylorus, and changes all the physical conditions of the strongest life.

[026-109]Such was the case with Modeste. In three short days she became the image

[026-110]of morbid melancholy; she did not sing, she could not be made to smile.

[026-111][[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]], becoming uneasy at the non-arrival of the two friends,

[026-112]thought of going to fetch them, when, on the evening of the fifth day,

[026-113]he received news of their movements through Latournelle.

[026-114]Canalis, excessively delighted at the idea of a rich marriage, was

[026-115]determined to neglect nothing that might help him to cut out La Briere,

[026-116]without, however, giving La Briere a chance to reproach him for having

[026-117]violated the laws of friendship. The poet felt that nothing would lower

[026-118]a lover so much in the eyes of a young girl as to exhibit him in a

[026-119]subordinate position; and he therefore proposed to La Briere, in the

[026-120]most natural manner, to take a little country-house at [[ingouville|Ingouville]] for a

[026-121]month, and live there together on pretence of requiring sea-air. As

[026-122]soon as La Briere, who at first saw nothing amiss in the proposal, had

[026-123]consented, Canalis declared that he should pay all expenses, and he sent

[026-124]his valet to [[provincial-life|Havre]], telling him to see Monsieur Latournelle and get

[026-125]his assistance in choosing the house,--well aware that the notary would

[026-126]repeat all particulars to the Mignons. Ernest and Canalis had, as may

[026-127]well be supposed, talked over all the aspects of the affair, and the

[026-128]rather prolix Ernest had given a good many useful hints to his rival.

[026-129]The valet, understanding his master’s wishes, fulfilled them to the

[026-130]letter; he trumpeted the arrival of the great poet, for whom the doctors

[026-131]advised sea-air to restore his health, injured as it was by the double

[026-132]toils of literature and politics. This important personage wanted a

[026-133]house, which must have at least such and such a number of rooms, as he

[026-134]would bring with him a secretary, cook, two servants, and a coachman,

[026-135]not counting himself, Germain Bonnet, the valet. The carriage, selected

[026-136]and hired for a month by Canalis, was a pretty one; and Germain

[026-137]set about finding a pair of fine horses which would also answer as

[026-138]saddle-horses,--for, as he said, monsieur le baron and his secretary

[026-139]took horseback exercise. Under the eyes of little Latournelle, who went

[026-140]with him to various houses, Germain made a good deal of talk about the

[026-141]secretary, rejecting two or three because there was no suitable room for

[026-142]Monsieur de La Briere.

[026-143]“Monsieur le baron,” he said to the notary, “makes his secretary quite

[026-144]his best friend. Ah! I should be well scolded if Monsieur de La Briere

[026-145]was not as well treated as monsieur le baron himself; and after all, you

[026-146]know, Monsieur de La Briere is a lawyer in my master’s court.”

[026-147]Germain never appeared in public unless punctiliously dressed in

[026-148]black, with spotless gloves, well-polished boots, and otherwise as well

[026-149]apparelled as a lawyer. Imagine the effect he produced in Havre, and the

[026-150]idea people took of the great poet from this sample of him! The valet

[026-151]of a man of wit and intellect ends by getting a little wit and intellect

[026-152]himself which has rubbed off from his master. Germain did not overplay

[026-153]his part; he was simple and good-humored, as Canalis had instructed him

[026-154]to be. Poor La Briere was in blissful ignorance of the harm Germain

[026-155]was doing to his prospects, and the depreciation his consent to the

[026-156]arrangement had brought upon him; it is, however, true that some inkling

[026-157]of the state of things rose to Modeste’s ears from these lower regions.

[026-158]Canalis had arranged to bring his secretary in his own carriage, and

[026-159]Ernest’s unsuspicious nature did not perceive that he was putting

[026-160]himself in a false position until too late to remedy it. The delay in

[026-161]the arrival of the pair which had troubled Charles Mignon was caused by

[026-162]the painting of the Canalis arms on the panels of the carriage, and by

[026-163]certain orders given to a tailor; for the poet neglected none of the

[026-164]innumerable details which might, even the smallest of them, influence a

[026-165]young girl.

[026-166]“It is all right,” said Latournelle to Mignon on the sixth day. “The

[026-167]baron’s valet has hired Madame Amaury’s villa at Sanvic, all furnished,

[026-168]for seven hundred francs; he has written to his master that he may

[026-169]start, and that all will be ready on his arrival. So the two gentlemen

[026-170]will be here Sunday. I have also had a letter from Butscha; here it is;

[026-171]it’s not long: ‘My dear master,--I cannot get back till Sunday. Between

[026-172]now and then I have some very important inquiries to make which concern

[026-173]the happiness of a person in whom you take an interest.’”
