# Section 27

[027-001]The announcement of this arrival did not rouse [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] from her gloom;

[027-002]the sense of her fall and the bewilderment of her mind were still

[027-003]too great, and she was not nearly as much of a coquette as her father

[027-004]thought her to be. There is, in truth, a charming and permissible

[027-005]coquetry, that of the soul, which may claim to be [[love]]’s politeness.

[027-006][[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]], when scolding his daughter, failed to distinguish

[027-007]between the mere desire of pleasing and the love of the mind,--the

[027-008]thirst for love, and the thirst for admiration. Like every true colonel

[027-009]of the Empire he saw in this [[epistolary-novel|correspondence]], rapidly read, only the

[027-010]young girl who had thrown herself at the head of a [[poetry|poet]]; but in the

[027-011]letters which we were forced to lack of space to suppress, a better

[027-012]judge would have admired the dignified and gracious reserve which

[027-013]Modeste had substituted for the rather aggressive and light-minded tone

[027-014]of her first letters. The father, however, was only too cruelly right on

[027-015]one point. Modeste’s last letter, which we have read, had indeed spoken

[027-016]as though the [[marriage]] were a settled fact, and the remembrance of that

[027-017]letter filled her with shame; she thought her father very harsh and

[027-018]cruel to force her to receive a man unworthy of her, yet to whom

[027-019]her soul had flown, as it were, bare. She questioned [[dumay|Dumay]] about his

[027-020]interview with [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]], she inveigled him into relating its every

[027-021]detail, and she did not think Canalis as barbarous as the lieutenant had

[027-022]declared him. The thought of the beautiful casket which held the letters

[027-023]of the thousand and one women of this literary Don Juan made her smile,

[027-024]and she was strongly tempted to say to her father: “I am not the only

[027-025]one to write to him; the elite of my sex send their leaves for the

[027-026]laurel wreath of the poet.”

[027-027]During this week Modeste’s character underwent a transformation. The

[027-028]catastrophe--and it was a great one to her poetic nature--roused a

[027-029]faculty of discernment and also the malice latent in her girlish heart,

[027-030]in which her suitors were about to encounter a formidable adversary. It

[027-031]is a fact that when a young woman’s heart is chilled her head becomes

[027-032]clear; she observes with great rapidity of judgment, and with a tinge of

[027-033]pleasantry which Shakespeare’s Beatrice so admirably represents in “Much

[027-034]Ado about Nothing.” Modeste was seized with a deep disgust for men, now

[027-035]that the most distinguished among them had betrayed her hopes. When a

[027-036]woman loves, what she takes for disgust is simply the ability to see

[027-037]clearly; but in matters of sentiment she is never, especially if she is

[027-038]a young girl, in a condition to see clearly. If she cannot admire, she

[027-039]despises. And so, after passing through terrible struggles of the soul,

[027-040]Modeste necessarily put on the armor on which, as she had once declared,

[027-041]the word “Disdain” was engraved. After reaching that point she was able,

[027-042]in the character of uninterested spectator, to take part in what she was

[027-043]pleased to call the “farce of the suitors,” a performance in which she

[027-044]herself was about to play the role of heroine. She particularly set

[027-045]before her mind the satisfaction of humiliating Monsieur de La Briere.

[027-046]“Modeste is saved,” said Madame Mignon to her husband; “she wants to

[027-047]revenge herself on the false Canalis by trying to love the real one.”

[027-048]Such in truth was Modeste’s plan. It was so utterly commonplace that her

[027-049]mother, to whom she confided her griefs, advised her on the contrary to

[027-050]treat Monsieur de La Briere with extreme politeness.

[027-051]CHAPTER XVII. A THIRD SUITOR

[027-052]“Those two young men,” said Madame Latournelle, on the Saturday evening,

[027-053]“have no idea how many spies they have on their tracks. We are eight in

[027-054]all, on the watch.”

[027-055]“Don’t say two young men, wife; say three!” cried little Latournelle,

[027-056]looking round him. “Gobenheim is not here, so I can speak out.”

[027-057]Modeste raised her head, and everybody, imitating Modeste, raised theirs

[027-058]and looked at the notary.

[027-059]“Yes, a third lover--and he is something like a lover--offers himself as

[027-060]a candidate.”

[027-061]“Bah!” exclaimed the colonel.

[027-062]“I speak of no less a person,” said Latournelle, pompously, “than

[027-063]Monsieur le Duc d’Herouville, Marquis de Saint-Sever, Duc de Nivron,

[027-064]Comte de Bayeux, Vicomte d’Essigny, grand equerry and peer of France,

[027-065]knight of the Spur and the Golden Fleece, grandee of Spain, and son of

[027-066]the last governor of Normandy. He saw Mademoiselle Modeste at the time

[027-067]when he was staying with the Vilquins, and he regretted then--as his

[027-068]notary, who came from Bayeux yesterday, tells me--that she was not

[027-069]rich enough for him; for his father recovered nothing but the estate of

[027-070]Herouville on his return to France, and that is saddled with a sister.

[027-071]The young duke is thirty-three years old. I am definitively charged to

[027-072]lay these proposals before you, Monsieur le comte,” added the notary,

[027-073]turning respectfully to the colonel.

[027-074]“Ask Modeste if she wants another bird in her cage,” replied the count;

[027-075]“as far as I am concerned, I am willing that my lord the grand equerry

[027-076]shall pay her attention.”

[027-077]Notwithstanding the care with which Charles Mignon avoided seeing

[027-078]people, and though he stayed in [[chalet|the Chalet]] and never went out without

[027-079]Modeste, Gobenheim had reported Dumay’s wealth; for Dumay had said to

[027-080]him when giving up his position as cashier: “I am to be bailiff for my

[027-081]colonel, and all my [[commerce|fortune]], except what my wife needs, is to go to

[027-082]the children of our little Modeste.” Every one in [[provincial-life|Havre]] had therefore

[027-083]propounded the same question that the notary had already put to himself:

[027-084]“If Dumay’s share in the profits is six hundred thousand francs, and

[027-085]he is going to be Monsieur Mignon’s bailiff, then Monsieur Mignon must

[027-086]certainly have a colossal [[social-mobility|fortune]]. He arrived at Marseilles on a ship of

[027-087]his own, loaded with indigo; and they say at the Bourse that the cargo,

[027-088]not counting the ship, is worth more than he gives out as his whole

[027-089]fortune.”

[027-090]The colonel was unwilling to dismiss the servants he had brought back

[027-091]with him, whom he had chosen with care during his travels; and he

[027-092]therefore hired a house for them in the lower part of [[ingouville|Ingouville]], where

[027-093]he installed his valet, cook, and coachman, all Negroes, and three

[027-094]mulattos on whose fidelity he could rely. The coachman was told to

[027-095]search for saddle-horses for Mademoiselle and for his master, and for

[027-096]carriage-horses for the caleche in which the colonel and the lieutenant

[027-097]had returned to Havre. That carriage, bought in [[paris|Paris]], was of the

[027-098]latest fashion, and bore the arms of La Bastie, surmounted by a count’s

[027-099]coronet. These things, insignificant in the eyes of a man who for four

[027-100]years had been accustomed to the unbridled luxury of the Indies and of

[027-101]the English merchants at Canton, were the subject of much comment

[027-102]among the business men of Havre and the inhabitants of Ingouville and

[027-103]Graville. Before five days had elapsed the rumor of them ran from one

[027-104]end of Normandy to the other like a train of gunpowder touched by fire.

[027-105]“Monsieur Mignon has come back from China with millions,” some one said

[027-106]in Rouen; “and it seems he was made a count in mid-ocean.”

[027-107]“But he was the Comte de La Bastie before the Revolution,” answered

[027-108]another.

[027-109]“So they call him a liberal just because he was plain Charles Mignon for

[027-110]twenty-five years! What are we coming to?” said a third.

[027-111]Modeste was considered, therefore, notwithstanding the silence of her

[027-112]parents and friends, as the richest heiress in Normandy, and all eyes

[027-113]began once more to see her merits. The aunt and sister of the Duc

[027-114]d’Herouville confirmed in the aristocratic salons of Bayeux Monsieur

[027-115]Charles Mignon’s right to the title and arms of count, derived from

[027-116]Cardinal Mignon, for whom the Cardinal’s hat and tassels were added as a

[027-117]crest. They had seen Mademoiselle de La Bastie when they were staying

[027-118]at the Vilquins, and their solicitude for the impoverished head of their

[027-119]house now became active.

[027-120]“If Mademoiselle de La Bastie is really as rich as she is beautiful,”

[027-121]said the aunt of the young duke, “she is the best match in the province.

[027-122]_She_ at least is noble.”

[027-123]The last words were aimed at the Vilquins, with whom they had not been

[027-124]able to come to terms, after incurring the humiliation of staying in

[027-125]that [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] household.

[027-126]Such were the little events which, contrary to the rules of Aristotle

[027-127]and of Horace, precede the introduction of another person into our

[027-128]story; but the portrait and the biography of this personage, this

[027-129]late arrival, shall not be long, taking into consideration his own

[027-130]diminutiveness. The grand equerry shall not take more space here than

[027-131]he will take in history. Monsieur le Duc d’Herouville, offspring of the

[027-132]matrimonial autumn of the last governor of Normandy, was born during the

[027-133]emigration in 1799, at Vienna. The old marechal, father of the present

[027-134]duke, returned with the king in 1814, and died in 1819, before he was

[027-135]able to marry his son. He could only leave him the vast chateau of

[027-136]Herouville, the park, a few dependencies, and a farm which he had bought

[027-137]back with some difficulty; all of which returned a rental of about

[027-138]fifteen thousand francs a year. Louis XVIII. gave the post of grand

[027-139]equerry to the son, who, under Charles X., received the usual pension of

[027-140]twelve thousand francs which was granted to the pauper peers of France.

[027-141]But what were these twenty-seven thousand francs a year and the salary

[027-142]of grand equerry to such a family? In Paris, of course, the young duke

[027-143]used the king’s coaches, and had a mansion provided for him in the rue

[027-144]Saint-Thomas-du-Louvre, near the royal stables; his salary paid for

[027-145]his winters in the city, and his twenty-seven thousand francs for the

[027-146]summers in Normandy. If this noble personage was still a bachelor he was

[027-147]less to blame than his aunt, who was not versed in La Fontaine’s fables.

[027-148]Mademoiselle d’Herouville made enormous pretensions wholly out of

[027-149]keeping with the spirit of the times; for great names, without the money

[027-150]to keep them up, can seldom win rich heiresses among the higher French

[027-151]nobility, who are themselves embarrassed to provide for their sons under

[027-152]the new law of the equal division of property. To marry the young Duc

[027-153]d’Herouville, it was necessary to conciliate the great banking-houses;

[027-154]but the haughty pride of the daughter of the house alienated these

[027-155]people by cutting speeches. During the first years of the Restoration,

[027-156]from 1817 to 1825, Mademoiselle d’Herouville, though in quest of

[027-157]millions, refused, among others, the daughter of Mongenod the banker,

[027-158]with whom Monsieur de Fontaine afterwards contented himself.

[027-159]At last, having lost several good opportunities to establish her nephew,

[027-160]entirely through her own fault, she was just considering whether the

[027-161]property of the Nucingens was not too basely acquired, or whether she

[027-162]should lend herself to the ambition of Madame de Nucingen, who wished

[027-163]to make her daughter a duchess. The king, anxious to restore the

[027-164]d’Herouvilles to their former splendor, had almost brought about this

[027-165]marriage, and when it failed he openly accused Mademoiselle d’Herouville

[027-166]of folly. In this way the aunt made the nephew ridiculous, and the

[027-167]nephew, in his own way, was not less absurd. When great things disappear

[027-168]they leave crumbs, “frusteaux,” Rabelais would say, behind them; and

[027-169]the French nobility of this century has left us too many such fragments.

[027-170]Neither the clergy nor the nobility have anything to complain of in this

[027-171]long history of manners and customs. Those great and magnificent social

[027-172]necessities have been well represented; but we ought surely to renounce

[027-173]the noble title of historian if we are not impartial, if we do not here

[027-174]depict the present degeneracy of the race of nobles, although we have

[027-175]already done so elsewhere,--in the character of the Comte de Mortsauf

[027-176](in “The Lily of the Valley”), in the “Duchesse de Langeais,” and the

[027-177]very nobleness of the nobility in the “Marquis d’Espard.” How then could

[027-178]it be that the race of heroes and valiant men belonging to the proud

[027-179]house of Herouville, who gave the famous marshal to the nation,

[027-180]cardinals to the church, great leaders to the Valois, knights to Louis

[027-181]XIV., was reduced to a little fragile being smaller than Butscha? That

[027-182]is a question which we ask ourselves in more than one salon in Paris

[027-183]when we hear the greatest names of France announced, and see the

[027-184]entrance of a thin, pinched, undersized young man, scarcely possessing

[027-185]the breath of life, or a premature old one, or some whimsical creature

[027-186]in whom an observer can with great difficulty trace the signs of a past

[027-187]grandeur. The dissipations of the reign of Louis XV., the orgies of that

[027-188]fatal and egotistic period, have produced an effete generation, in which

[027-189]manners alone survive the nobler vanished qualities,--forms, which are

[027-190]the sole heritage our nobles have preserved. The abandonment in which

[027-191]Louis XVI. was allowed to perish may thus be explained, with some slight

[027-192]reservations, as a wretched result of the reign of Madame de Pompadour.
