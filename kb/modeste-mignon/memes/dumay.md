# Dumay

Dumay, Charles Mignon's former lieutenant, is the guardian of the Chalet household — a soldier whose martial virtues of loyalty, vigilance, and strategic thinking are redirected to protecting the Mignon women during his colonel's absence.

## Role in the Novel

Dumay serves as protector, conspirator, and comic foil. His military background — "a former lieutenant in the Guard, showed the evidence of such resolution, such sang-froid" (003-046) — makes him simultaneously formidable and slightly ridiculous in the domestic sphere. He co-authors the "mouse-trap" conspiracy with Charles Mignon to test Modeste's suitors (001-048).

## Biography

### Background & Origins

A Breton of humble origins, Dumay served under Charles Mignon in the Imperial Guard. After the wars, he followed his colonel into civilian life, becoming the household's protector. His devotion to the Mignon children — "Dumay loved these little ones without having any personal interest in them" (004-046) — defines his character.

### Key Actions

Dumay's warning — "When a Breton girl sings, the lover is not far off" (007-040) — is one of the novel's most memorable lines, encapsulating his role as vigilant guardian. His participation in the "mouse-trap" conspiracy demonstrates his willingness to subordinate himself entirely to the Mignon family's interests.

### Fate / Resolution

Dumay's loyalty is rewarded; he remains an integral member of the household through the novel's resolution.

## Character Traits

- **Defining qualities**: Absolute loyalty (004-046), military discipline (003-046), protective instinct, blunt honesty
- **Social Type**: The devoted soldier-servant — a Napoleonic type transposed into Restoration domesticity

## Balzacian Context

Appears only in *Modeste Mignon*. Dumay belongs to Balzac's recurring type of the loyal subordinate whose military past gives him a code of honor that civilian society lacks.
