# Section 7

[007-001]“To get ready for your bedtime, mamma,” answered [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], in a voice as

[007-002]pure as the tones of an instrument.

[007-003]“You haven’t paid your expenses,” said the dwarf to [[dumay|Dumay]] when he

[007-004]returned.

[007-005]“Modeste is as pure as the Virgin on our altar,” cried Madame

[007-006]Latournelle.

[007-007]“Good God! such excitements wear me out,” said Dumay; “and yet I’m a

[007-008]strong man.”

[007-009]“May I lose that twenty-five sous if I have the slightest idea what you

[007-010]are about,” remarked Gobenheim. “You seem to me to be crazy.”

[007-011]“And yet it is all about a treasure,” said Butscha, standing on tiptoe

[007-012]to whisper in Gobenheim’s ear.

[007-013]“Dumay, I am sorry to say that I am still almost certain of what I told

[007-014]you,” persisted Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]].

[007-015]“The burden of proof is now on you, madame,” said Dumay, calmly; “it is

[007-016]for you to prove that we are mistaken.”

[007-017]Discovering that the matter in question was only Modeste’s [[honor]],

[007-018]Gobenheim took his hat, made his bow, and walked off, carrying his ten

[007-019]sous with him,--there being evidently no hope of another rubber.

[007-020]“Exupere, and you too, Butscha, may leave us,” said Madame Latournelle.

[007-021]“Go back to [[provincial-life|Havre]]; you will get there in time for the last piece at the

[007-022]theatre. I’ll pay for your tickets.”

[007-023]When the four friends were alone with Madame Mignon, Madame Latournelle,

[007-024]after looking at Dumay, who being a Breton understood the mother’s

[007-025]obstinacy, and at her husband who was fingering the cards, felt herself

[007-026]authorized to speak up.

[007-027]“Madame Mignon, come now, tell us what decisive thing has struck your

[007-028]mind.”

[007-029]“Ah, my good friend, if you were a [[music|musician]] you would have heard, as I

[007-030]have, the language of [[love]] that Modeste speaks.”

[007-031]The piano of the demoiselles Mignon was among the few articles of

[007-032]furniture which had been moved from the town-house to [[chalet|the Chalet]].

[007-033]Modeste often conjured away her troubles by practising, without a

[007-034]master. Born a musician, she played to enliven her mother. She sang

[007-035]by nature, and loved the German airs which her mother taught her. From

[007-036]these lessons and these attempts at self-instruction came a phenomenon

[007-037]not uncommon to natures with a musical vocation; Modeste composed, as

[007-038]far as a person ignorant of the laws of harmony can be said to compose,

[007-039]tender little lyric melodies. Melody is to music what imagery and

[007-040]sentiment are to [[poetry]], a flower that blossoms spontaneously.

[007-041]Consequently, nations have had melodies before harmony,--botany comes

[007-042]later than the flower. In like manner, Modeste, who knew nothing of

[007-043]the painter’s art except what she had seen her sister do in the way of

[007-044]water-color, would have stood subdued and fascinated before the

[007-045]pictures of Raphael, Titian, Rubens, Murillo, Rembrandt, Albert Durer,

[007-046]Holbein,--in other words, before the great ideals of many lands. Lately,

[007-047]for at least a month, Modeste had warbled the songs of nightingales,

[007-048]musical rhapsodies whose poetry and meaning had roused the attention of

[007-049]her mother, already surprised by her sudden eagerness for composition

[007-050]and her fancy for putting airs into certain verses.

[007-051]“If your suspicions have no other foundation,” said Latournelle to

[007-052]Madame Mignon, “I pity your susceptibilities.”

[007-053]“When a Breton girl sings,” said Dumay gloomily, “the lover is not far

[007-054]off.”

[007-055]“I will let you hear Modeste when she is improvising,” said the mother,

[007-056]“and you shall judge for yourselves--”

[007-057]“Poor girl!” said Madame Dumay, “If she only knew our anxiety she would

[007-058]be deeply distressed; she would tell us the truth,--especially if she

[007-059]thought it would save Dumay.”

[007-060]“My friends, I will question my daughter to-morrow,” said Madame Mignon;

[007-061]“perhaps I shall obtain more by tenderness than you have discovered by

[007-062]trickery.”

[007-063]Was the comedy of the “Fille mal Gardee” being played here,--as it is

[007-064]everywhere and forever,--under the noses of these faithful spies, these

[007-065]honest Bartholos, these Pyrenean hounds, without their being able to

[007-066]ferret out, detect, nor even surmise the lover, the love-affair, or

[007-067]the smoke of the fire? At any rate it was certainly not the result of a

[007-068]struggle between the jailers and the prisoner, between the despotism of

[007-069]a dungeon and the liberty of a victim,--it was simply the never-ending

[007-070]repetition of the first scene played by man when the curtain of the

[007-071]Creation rose; it was Eve in Paradise.

[007-072]And now, which of the two, the mother or the watch-dog, had the right of

[007-073]it?

[007-074]None of the persons who were about Modeste could understand that maiden

[007-075]heart--for the soul and the face we have described were in harmony. The

[007-076]girl had transported her existence into another world, as much denied

[007-077]and disbelieved in in these days of ours as the new world of Christopher

[007-078]Columbus in the sixteenth century. Happily, she kept her own counsel,

[007-079]or they would have thought her crazy. But first we must explain the

[007-080]influence of the past upon her nature.

[007-081]Two events had formed the soul and developed the mind of this young

[007-082]girl. Monsieur and Madame Mignon, warned by the fate that overtook

[007-083]Bettina, had resolved, just before the failure, to marry Modeste. They

[007-084]chose the son of a rich banker, formerly of Hamburg, but established in

[007-085]Havre since 1815,--a man, moreover, who was under obligations to them.

[007-086]The young man, whose name was Francois Althor, the dandy of Havre,

[007-087]blessed with a certain vulgar beauty in which the middle classes

[007-088]delight, well-made, well-fleshed, and with a fine complexion, abandoned

[007-089]his betrothed so hastily on the day of her father’s failure that neither

[007-090]Modeste nor her mother nor either of the Dumays had seen him since.

[007-091]Latournelle ventured a question on the subject to Jacob Althor, the

[007-092]father; but he only shrugged his shoulders and replied, “I really don’t

[007-093]know what you mean.”

[007-094]This answer, told to Modeste to give her some experience of life, was

[007-095]a lesson which she learned all the more readily because Latournelle

[007-096]and Dumay made many and long comments on the cowardly desertion. The

[007-097]daughters of Charles Mignon, like spoiled children, had all their wishes

[007-098]gratified; they rode on horseback, kept their own horses and grooms, and

[007-099]otherwise enjoyed a perilous liberty. Seeing herself in possession of

[007-100]an official lover, Modeste had allowed Francisque to kiss her hand, and

[007-101]take her by the waist to mount her. She accepted his flowers and all the

[007-102]little proofs of tenderness with which it is proper to surround the

[007-103]lady of our choice; she even worked him a purse, believing in such

[007-104]ties,--strong indeed to noble souls, but cobwebs for the Gobenheims, the

[007-105]Vilquins, and the Althors.

[007-106]Some time during the spring which followed the removal of Madame Mignon

[007-107]and her daughter to the Chalet, Francisque Althor came to dine with

[007-108]the Vilquins. Happening to see Modeste over the wall at the foot of the

[007-109]lawn, he turned away his head. Six weeks later he married the eldest

[007-110]Mademoiselle Vilquin. In this way Modeste, young, beautiful, and of high

[007-111]birth, learned the lesson that for three whole months of her engagement

[007-112]she had been nothing more than Mademoiselle Million. Her poverty, well

[007-113]known to all, became a sentinel defending the approaches to the Chalet

[007-114]fully as well as the prudence of the Latournelles or the vigilance of

[007-115]Dumay. The talk of the town ran for a time on Mademoiselle Mignon’s

[007-116]position only to insult her.

[007-117]“Poor girl! what will become of her?--an old maid, of course.”

[007-118]“What a fate! to have had the world at her feet; to have had the chance

[007-119]to marry Francisque Althor,--and now, nobody willing to take her!”

[007-120]“After a life of luxury, to come down to such poverty--”

[007-121]And these insults were not uttered in secret or left to Modeste’s

[007-122]imagination; she heard them spoken more than once by the young men and

[007-123]the young women of Havre as they walked to [[ingouville|Ingouville]], and, knowing that

[007-124]Madame Mignon and her daughter lived at the Chalet, talked of them as

[007-125]they passed the house. Friends of the Vilquins expressed surprise that

[007-126]the mother and daughter were willing to live on among the scenes of

[007-127]their former splendor. From her open window behind the closed blinds

[007-128]Modeste sometimes heard such insolence as this:--

[007-129]“I am sure I can’t think how they can live there,” some one would say

[007-130]as he paced the villa lawn,--perhaps to assist Vilquin in getting rid of

[007-131]his tenant.

[007-132]“What do you suppose they live on? they haven’t any means of earning

[007-133]money.”

[007-134]“I am told the old woman has gone blind.”

[007-135]“Is Mademoiselle Mignon still pretty? Dear me, how dashing she used to

[007-136]be! Well, she hasn’t any horses now.”

[007-137]Most young girls on hearing these spiteful and silly speeches, born of

[007-138]an envy that now rushed, peevish and drivelling, to avenge the past,

[007-139]would have felt the blood mount to their foreheads; others would have

[007-140]wept; some would have undergone spasms of anger; but Modeste smiled, as

[007-141]we smile at the theatre while watching the actors. Her pride could not

[007-142]descend so low as the level of such speeches.

[007-143]The other event was more serious than these mercenary meannesses.

[007-144]Bettina Caroline died in the arms of her younger sister, who had nursed

[007-145]her with the devotion of girlhood, and the curiosity of an untainted

[007-146]imagination. In the silence of long nights the sisters exchanged many a

[007-147]confidence. With what dramatic interest was poor Bettina invested in the

[007-148]eyes of the innocent Modeste? Bettina knew love through sorrow only, and

[007-149]she was dying of it. Among young girls every man, scoundrel though he

[007-150]be, is still a lover. Passion is the one thing absolutely real in the

[007-151]things of life, and it insists on its supremacy. Charles d’Estourny,

[007-152]gambler, criminal, and debauchee, remained in the memory of the

[007-153]sisters, the elegant Parisian of the fetes of Havre, the admired of the

[007-154]womenkind. Bettina believed she had carried him off from the coquettish

[007-155]Madame Vilquin, and to Modeste he was her sister’s happy lover. Such

[007-156]adoration in young girls is stronger than all social condemnations. To

[007-157]Bettina’s thinking, justice had been deceived; if not, how could it

[007-158]have sentenced a man who had loved her for six months?--loved her to

[007-159]distraction in the hidden retreat to which he had taken her,--that he

[007-160]might, we may add, be at liberty to go his own way. Thus the dying girl

[007-161]inoculated her sister with love. Together they talked of the great drama

[007-162]which imagination enhances; and Bettina carried with her to the grave

[007-163]her sister’s ignorance, leaving her, if not informed, at least thirsting

[007-164]for information.

[007-165]Nevertheless, remorse had set its fangs too sharply in Bettina’s heart

[007-166]not to force her to warn her sister. In the midst of her own confessions

[007-167]she had preached duty and implicit obedience to Modeste. On the evening

[007-168]of her death she implored her to remember the tears that soaked her

[007-169]pillow, and not to imitate a conduct which even suffering could not

[007-170]expiate. Bettina accused herself of bringing a curse upon the family,

[007-171]and died in despair at being unable to obtain her father’s pardon.

[007-172]Notwithstanding the consolations which the ministers of religion,

[007-173]touched by her repentance, freely gave her, she cried in heartrending

[007-174]tones with her latest breath: “Oh father! father!” “Never give your

[007-175]heart without your hand,” she said to Modeste an hour before she died;

[007-176]“and above all, accept no attentions from any man without telling

[007-177]everything to papa and mamma.”

[007-178]These words, so earnest in their practical meaning, uttered in the hour

[007-179]of death, had more effect upon Modeste than if Bettina had exacted a

[007-180]solemn oath. The dying girl, farseeing as prophet, drew from beneath her

[007-181]pillow a ring which she had sent by her faithful maid, Francoise Cochet,

[007-182]to be engraved in Havre with these words, “Think of Bettina, 1827,” and

[007-183]placed it on her sister’s finger, begging her to keep it there until

[007-184]she married. Thus there had been between these two young girls a strange

[007-185]commingling of bitter remorse and the artless visions of a fleeting

[007-186]spring-time too early blighted by the keen north wind of desertion; yet

[007-187]all their tears, regrets and memories were always subordinate to their

[007-188]horror of evil.
