# Commerce

Commerce in *Modeste Mignon* is the invisible engine of the plot — the source of the Mignon fortune, the basis of Modeste's desirability as an heiress, and the force that destabilizes the old aristocratic order.

## Definition

Balzac presents commerce as both necessity and contamination. Charles Mignon made his fortune through trade in the Levant, dealing in goods that the novel only vaguely specifies — an obscurity that suggests commerce is not quite respectable enough to describe in detail. Yet this commercial wealth is what makes the Mignon family's social position possible. The tension between aristocratic blood (the "de La Bastie" title) and mercantile wealth runs throughout the novel, reflecting the broader transformation of French society under the Restoration, where old nobility and new money competed for dominance.

## In the Novel

### Manifestations

Commerce appears most concretely in the figure of Gobenheim, the banker who visits the Chalet — a man of pure calculation who represents the commercial world in its least romantic form. The Havre setting is itself meaningful: as a major port, Havre is a commercial city, and the Mignon wealth is tied to maritime trade. Modeste's marriageability is expressed in commercial terms throughout the novel — she is worth "a million," and her suitors calculate accordingly.

The novel also treats poetry as a form of commerce: Canalis "pays ten thousand francs for every volume of poetry" (009-101), and his literary reputation is managed as carefully as a business. The conflation of art and commerce — the poet as businessman — is one of the novel's sharpest satirical observations.

## In Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*

Commerce is central to Balzac's fictional universe, from the perfume trade of *César Birotteau* to the banking houses of *La Maison Nucingen*. In *Modeste Mignon*, commerce is treated more gently than in these darker works — it is a fact of life rather than a source of tragedy, consistent with the novel's comic mode.
