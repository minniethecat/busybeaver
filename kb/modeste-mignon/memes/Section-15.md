# Section 15

[015-001]Do you really believe, mademoiselle, that [[epistolary-novel|letters]], more or less

[015-002]true in relation to the life of the writers, more or less

[015-003]insincere,--for those which we write to each other are the

[015-004]expressions of the moment at which we pen them, and not of the

[015-005]general tenor of our lives,--do you believe, I say, that beautiful

[015-006]as they may be, they can at all replace the representation that we

[015-007]could make of ourselves to each other by the revelations of daily

[015-008]intercourse? Man is dual. There is a life invisible, that of the

[015-009]heart, to which letters may suffice; and there is a life material,

[015-010]to which more importance is, alas, attached than you are aware of

[015-011]at your age. These two existences must, however, be made to

[015-012]harmonize in [[the-ideal|the ideal]] which you cherish; and this, I may remark

[015-013]in passing, is very rare.

[015-014]The pure, spontaneous, disinterested homage of a solitary soul

[015-015]which is both educated and chaste, is one of those celestial

[015-016]flowers whose color and fragrance console for every grief, for

[015-017]every wound, for every betrayal which makes up the life of a

[015-018]literary man; and I thank you with an impulse equal to your own.

[015-019]But after this poetical exchange of my griefs for the pearls of

[015-020]your charity, what next? what do you expect? I have neither the

[015-021]genius nor the splendid position of Lord Byron; above all, I have

[015-022]not the halo of his fictitious damnation and his false social

[015-023]woes. But what could you have hoped from him in like

[015-024]circumstances? His friendship? Well, he who ought to have felt

[015-025]only pride was eaten up by vanity of every kind,--sickly,

[015-026]irritable vanity which discouraged friendship. I, a thousand-fold

[015-027]more insignificant than he, may I not have discordances of

[015-028]character, and make friendship a burden heavy indeed to bear? In

[015-029]exchange for your reveries, what will you gain? The

[015-030]dissatisfaction of a life which will not be wholly yours. The

[015-031]compact is madness. Let me tell you why. In the first place, your

[015-032]projected poem is a plagiarism. A young German girl, who was not,

[015-033]like you, semi-German, but altogether so, adored Goethe with the

[015-034]rash intoxication of girlhood. She made him her friend, her

[015-035]religion, her god, knowing at the same time that he was married.

[015-036]Madame Goethe, a worthy German woman, lent herself to this worship

[015-037]with a sly good-nature which did not cure Bettina. But what was

[015-038]the end of it all? The young ecstatic married a man who was

[015-039]younger and handsomer than Goethe. Now, between ourselves, let us

[015-040]admit that a young girl who should make herself the handmaid of a

[015-041]man of genius, his equal through comprehension, and should piously

[015-042]worship him till death, like one of those divine figures sketched

[015-043]by the masters on the shutters of their mystic shrines, and who,

[015-044]when Germany lost him, should have retired to some solitude away

[015-045]from men, like the friend of Lord Bolingbroke,--let us admit, I

[015-046]say, that the young girl would have lived forever, inlaid in the

[015-047]glory of [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]] as Mary Magdalene in the cross and triumph of

[015-048]our Lord. If that is sublime, what say you to the reverse of the

[015-049]picture? As I am neither Goethe nor Lord Byron, the colossi of

[015-050][[poetry]] and egotism, but simply the author of a few esteemed

[015-051]verses, I cannot expect the honors of a cult. Neither am I

[015-052]disposed to be a martyr. I have ambition, and I have a heart; I am

[015-053]still young and I have my career to make. See me for what I am.

[015-054]The bounty of the king and the protection of his ministers give me

[015-055]sufficient means of living. I have the outward bearing of a very

[015-056]ordinary man. I go to the soirees in [[paris|Paris]] like any other

[015-057]empty-headed fop; and if I drive, the wheels of my carriage do not

[015-058]roll on the solid ground, absolutely indispensable in these days,

[015-059]of property invested in the funds. But if I am not rich, neither do

[015-060]I have the reliefs and consolations of life in a garret, the toil

[015-061]uncomprehended, the fame in penury, which belong to men who are

[015-062]worth far more than I,--D’Arthez, for instance.

[015-063]Ah! what prosaic conclusions will your young enthusiasm find to

[015-064]these enchanting visions. Let us stop here. If I have had the

[015-065]happiness of seeming to you a terrestrial paragon, you have been

[015-066]to me a thing of light and a beacon, like those stars that shine

[015-067]for a moment and disappear. May nothing ever tarnish this episode

[015-068]of our lives. Were we to continue it I might [[love]] you; I might

[015-069]conceive one of those mad passions which rend all obstacles, which

[015-070]light fires in the heart whose violence is greater than their

[015-071]duration. And suppose I succeeded in pleasing you? we should end

[015-072]our tale in the common vulgar way,--[[marriage]], a household,

[015-073]children, Belise and Henriette Chrysale together!--could it be?

[015-074]Therefore, adieu.

[015-075]CHAPTER X. THE MARRIAGE OF SOULS

[015-076]To Monsieur de Canalis:

[015-077]My Friend,--Your letter gives me as much pain as pleasure. But

[015-078]perhaps some day we shall find nothing but pleasure in writing to

[015-079]each other. Understand me thoroughly. The soul speaks to God and

[015-080]asks him for many things; he is mute. I seek to obtain in you the

[015-081]answers that God does not make to me. Cannot the friendship of

[015-082]Mademoiselle de Gournay and Montaigne be revived in us? Do you not

[015-083]remember the household of Sismonde de Sismondi in Geneva? The most

[015-084]lovely home ever known, as I have been told; something like that

[015-085]of the Marquis de Pescaire and his wife,--happy to old age. Ah!

[015-086]friend, is it impossible that two hearts, two harps, should exist

[015-087]as in a symphony, answering each other from a distance, vibrating

[015-088]with delicious [[music|melody]] in unison? Man alone of all creation is in

[015-089]himself the harp, the musician, and the listener. Do you think to

[015-090]find me uneasy and jealous like ordinary women? I know that you go

[015-091]into the world and meet the handsomest and the wittiest women in

[015-092]Paris. May I not suppose that some one of those mermaids has

[015-093]deigned to clasp you in her cold and scaly arms, and that she has

[015-094]inspired the answer whose prosaic opinions sadden me? There is

[015-095]something in life more beautiful than the garlands of Parisian

[015-096]coquetry; there grows a flower far up those Alpine peaks called

[015-097]men of genius, the glory of humanity, which they fertilize with

[015-098]the dews their lofty heads draw from the skies. I seek to

[015-099]cultivate that flower and make it bloom; for its wild yet gentle

[015-100]fragrance can never fail,--it is eternal.

[015-101]Do me the [[honor]] to believe that there is nothing low or

[015-102]commonplace in me. Were I Bettina, for I know to whom you allude,

[015-103]I should never have become Madame von Arnim; and had I been one of

[015-104]Lord Byron’s many loves, I should be at this moment in a cloister.

[015-105]You have touched me to the quick. You do not know me, but you

[015-106]shall know me. I feel within me something that is sublime, of

[015-107]which I dare speak without vanity. God has put into my soul the

[015-108]roots of that Alpine flower born on the summits of which I speak,

[015-109]and I cannot plant it in an earthen pot upon my window-sill and

[015-110]see it die. No, that glorious flower-cup, single in its beauty,

[015-111]intoxicating in its fragrance, shall not be dragged through the

[015-112]vulgarities of life! it is yours--yours, before any eye has

[015-113]blighted it, yours forever! Yes, my poet, to you belong my

[015-114]thoughts,--all, those that are secret, those that are gayest; my

[015-115]heart is yours without reserve and with its infinite affection. If

[015-116]you should personally not please me, I shall never marry. I can

[015-117]live in the life of the heart, I can exist on your mind, your

[015-118]sentiments; they please me, and I will always be what I am, your

[015-119]friend. Yours is a noble moral nature; I have recognized it, I

[015-120]have appreciated it, and that suffices me. In that is all my

[015-121]future. Do not laugh at a young and pretty handmaiden who shrinks

[015-122]not from the thought of being some day the old companion of a

[015-123]poet,--a sort of mother perhaps, or a housekeeper; the guide of

[015-124]his judgment and a source of his wealth. This handmaiden--so

[015-125]devoted, so precious to the lives of such as you--is Friendship,

[015-126]pure, disinterested friendship, to whom you will tell all, who

[015-127]listens and sometimes shakes her head; who knits by the light of

[015-128]the lamp and waits to be present when the poet returns home soaked

[015-129]with rain, or vexed in mind. Such shall be my destiny if I do not

[015-130]find that of a happy wife attached forever to her husband; I smile

[015-131]alike at the thought of either fate. Do you believe France will be

[015-132]any the worse if Mademoiselle d’Este does not give it two or three

[015-133]sons, and never becomes a Madame Vilquin-something-or-other? As

[015-134]for me, I shall never be an old maid. I shall make myself a

[015-135]mother, by taking care of others and by my secret co-operation in

[015-136]the existence of a great man, to whom also I shall carry all my

[015-137]thoughts and all my earthly efforts.

[015-138]I have the deepest horror of commonplaceness. If I am free, if I

[015-139]am rich (and I know that I am young and pretty), I will never

[015-140]belong to any ninny just because he is the son of a peer of

[015-141]France, nor to a [[commerce|merchant]] who could ruin himself and me in a day,

[015-142]nor to a handsome creature who would be a sort of woman in the

[015-143]household, nor to a man of any kind who would make me blush twenty

[015-144]times a day for being his. Make yourself easy on that point. My

[015-145]father adores my wishes; he will never oppose them. If I please my

[015-146]poet, and he pleases me, the glorious structure of our love shall

[015-147]be built so high as to be inaccessible to any kind of misfortune.

[015-148]I am an eaglet; and you will see it in my eyes.

[015-149]I shall not repeat what I have already said, but I will put its

[015-150]substance in the least possible number of words, and confess to

[015-151]you that I should be the happiest of women if I were imprisoned by

[015-152]love as I am now imprisoned by the wish and will of a father. Ah!

[015-153]my friend, may we bring to a real end the romance that has come to

[015-154]us through the first exercise of my will: listen to its

[015-155]argument:--

[015-156]A young girl with a lively imagination, locked up in a tower, is

[015-157]weary with longing to run loose in the park where her eyes only

[015-158]are allowed to rove. She invents a way to loosen her bars; she

[015-159]jumps from the casement; she scales the park wall; she frolics

[015-160]along the neighbor’s sward--it is the Everlasting comedy. Well,

[015-161]that young girl is my soul, the neighbor’s park is your genius. Is

[015-162]it not all very natural? Was there ever a neighbor that did not

[015-163]complain that unknown feet broke down his trellises? I leave it to

[015-164]my poet to answer.

[015-165]But does the lofty reasoner after the fashion of Moliere want

[015-166]still better reasons? Well, here they are. My dear Geronte,

[015-167]marriages are usually made in defiance of common-sense. Parents

[015-168]make inquiries about a young man. If the Leander--who is supplied

[015-169]by some friend, or caught in a ball-room--is not a thief, and has

[015-170]no visible rent in his reputation, if he has the necessary

[015-171][[social-mobility|fortune]], if he comes from a college or a law-school and so fulfils

[015-172]the popular ideas of education, and if he wears his clothes with a

[015-173]gentlemanly air, he is allowed to meet the young lady, whose

[015-174]mother has ordered her to guard her tongue, to let no sign of her

[015-175]heart or soul appear on her face, which must wear the smile of a

[015-176]danseuse finishing a pirouette. These commands are coupled with

[015-177]instructions as to the danger of revealing her real character, and

[015-178]the additional advice of not seeming alarmingly well educated. If

[015-179]the settlements have all been agreed upon, the parents are

[015-180]good-natured enough to let the pair see each other for a few

[015-181]moments; they are allowed to talk or walk together, but always

[015-182]without the slightest freedom, and knowing that they are bound by

[015-183]rigid rules. The man is as much dressed up in soul as he is in body,

[015-184]and so is the young girl. This pitiable comedy, mixed with bouquets,

[015-185]jewels, and theatre-parties is called “paying your addresses.” It

[015-186]revolts me: I desire that actual marriage shall be the result of a

[015-187]previous and long marriage of souls. A young girl, a woman, has

[015-188]throughout her life only this one moment when reflection, second

[015-189]sight, and experience are necessary to her. She plays her liberty,

[015-190]her happiness, and she is not allowed to throw the dice; she risks

[015-191]her all, and is forced to be a mere spectator. I have the right,

[015-192]the will, the power to make my own unhappiness, and I use them, as

[015-193]did my mother, who, won by beauty and led by instinct, married the

[015-194]most generous, the most liberal, the most loving of men. I know

[015-195]that you are free, a poet, and noble-looking. Be sure that I

[015-196]should not have chosen one of your brothers in Apollo who was

[015-197]already married. If my mother was won by beauty, which is perhaps

[015-198]the spirit of form, why should I not be attracted by the spirit

[015-199]and the form united? Shall I not know you better by studying you

[015-200]in this correspondence than I could through the vulgar experience

[015-201]of “receiving your addresses”? This is the question, as Hamlet

[015-202]says.
