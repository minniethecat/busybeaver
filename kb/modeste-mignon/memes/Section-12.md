# Section 12

[012-001]Deign to accept, mademoiselle, the homage which we owe to all

[012-002]women, even those who are disguised and masked.

[012-003]So this was the letter she had worn between her flesh and her corset

[012-004]above her palpitating heart throughout one whole day! For this she had

[012-005]postponed the reading until the midnight hour when the household slept,

[012-006]waiting for the solemn silence with the eager anxiety of an imagination

[012-007]on fire! For this she had blessed [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]] by anticipation, reading a

[012-008]thousand [[epistolary-novel|letters]] ere she opened one,--fancying all things, except this

[012-009]drop of cold water falling upon the vaporous forms of her [[illusion]], and

[012-010]dissolving them as prussic acid dissolves life. What could she do but

[012-011]hide herself in her bed, blow out her candle, bury her face in the

[012-012]sheets and weep?

[012-013]All this happened during the first days of July. But [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] presently

[012-014]got up, walked across the room and opened the window. She wanted air.

[012-015]The fragrance of the flowers came to her with the peculiar freshness of

[012-016]the odors of the night. The sea, lighted by the moon, sparkled like a

[012-017]mirror. A nightingale was singing in a tree. “Ah, there is the [[poetry|poet]]!”

[012-018]thought Modeste, whose anger subsided at once. Bitter reflections chased

[012-019]each other through her mind. She was cut to the quick; she wished to

[012-020]re-read the letter, and lit a candle; she studied the sentences so

[012-021]carefully studied when written; and ended by hearing the wheezing voice

[012-022]of the outer world.

[012-023]“He is right, and I am wrong,” she said to herself. “But who could ever

[012-024]believe that under the starry mantle of a poet I should find nothing but

[012-025]one of Moliere’s old men?”

[012-026]When a woman or young girl is taken in the act, “flagrante delicto,” she

[012-027]conceives a deadly hatred to the witness, the author, or the object of

[012-028]her fault. And so the true, the single-minded, the untamed and untamable

[012-029]Modeste conceived within her soul an unquenchable desire to get

[012-030]the better of that righteous spirit, to drive him into some fatal

[012-031]inconsistency, and so return him blow for blow. This girl, this

[012-032]child, as we may call her, so pure, whose head alone had been

[012-033]misguided,--partly by her reading, partly by her sister’s sorrows, and

[012-034]more perhaps by the dangerous meditations of her solitary life,--was

[012-035]suddenly caught by a ray of sunshine flickering across her face. She had

[012-036]been standing for three hours on the shores of the vast sea of Doubt.

[012-037]Nights like these are never forgotten. Modeste walked straight to

[012-038]her little Chinese table, a gift from her father, and wrote a letter

[012-039]dictated by the infernal spirit of vengeance which palpitates in the

[012-040]hearts of young girls.

[012-041]CHAPTER VIII. BLADE TO BLADE

[012-042]To Monsieur de Canalis:

[012-043]Monsieur,--You are certainly a great poet, and you are something

[012-044]more,--an honest man. After showing such loyal frankness to a

[012-045]young girl who was stepping to the verge of an abyss, have you

[012-046]enough left to answer without hypocrisy or evasion the following

[012-047]question?

[012-048]Would you have written the letter I now hold in answer to mine,

[012-049]--would your ideas, your language have been the same,--had some

[012-050]one whispered in your ear (what may prove true), Mademoiselle O.

[012-051]d’Este M. has six millions and does intend to have a dunce for a

[012-052]master?

[012-053]Admit the supposition for a moment. Be with me what you are with

[012-054]yourself; fear nothing. I am wiser than my twenty years; nothing

[012-055]that is frank can hurt you in my mind. When I have read your

[012-056]confidence, if you deign to make it, you shall receive from me an

[012-057]answer to your first letter.

[012-058]Having admired your talent, often so sublime, permit me to do

[012-059]homage to your delicacy and your integrity, which force me to

[012-060]remain always,

[012-061]Your humble servant, O. d’Este M.

[012-062]When [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere had held this letter in his hands for some

[012-063]little time he went to walk along the boulevards, tossed in mind like a

[012-064]tiny vessel by a tempest when the wind is blowing from all points of the

[012-065]compass. Most young men, specially true Parisians, would have settled

[012-066]the matter in a single phrase, “The girl is a little hussy.” But for

[012-067]a youth whose soul was noble and true, this attempt to put him, as it

[012-068]were, upon his oath, this appeal to truth, had the power to awaken the

[012-069]three judges hidden in the conscience of every man. [[honor|Honor]], Truth,

[012-070]and Justice, getting on their feet, cried out in their several ways

[012-071]energetically.

[012-072]“Ah, my dear Ernest,” said Truth, “you never would have read that lesson

[012-073]to a rich heiress. No, my boy; you would have gone in hot haste to [[provincial-life|Havre]]

[012-074]to find out if the girl were handsome, and you would have been very

[012-075]unhappy indeed at her preference for genius; and if you could

[012-076]have tripped up your friend and supplanted him in her affections,

[012-077]Mademoiselle d’Este would have been a divinity.”

[012-078]“What?” cried Justice, “are you not always bemoaning yourselves, you

[012-079]penniless men of wit and capacity, that rich girls marry beings whom you

[012-080]wouldn’t take as your servants? You rail against the materialism of the

[012-081]century which hastens to join wealth to wealth, and never marries some

[012-082]fine young man with brains and no money to a rich girl. What an outcry

[012-083]you make about it; and yet here is a young woman who revolts against

[012-084]that very spirit of the age, and behold! the poet replies with a blow at

[012-085]her heart!”

[012-086]“Rich or poor, young or old, ugly or handsome, the girl is right; she

[012-087]has sense and judgment, she has tripped you over into the slough of

[012-088]self-interest and lets you know it,” cried Honor. “She deserves an

[012-089]answer, a sincere and loyal and frank answer, and, above all, the honest

[012-090]expression of your thought. Examine yourself! sound your heart and purge

[012-091]it of its meannesses. What would Moliere’s Alceste say?”

[012-092]And La Briere, having started from the boulevard Poissoniere, walked so

[012-093]slowly, absorbed in these reflections, that he was more than an hour in

[012-094]reaching the boulevard des Capucines. Then he followed the quays, which

[012-095]led him to the Cour des Comptes, situated in that time close to the

[012-096]Saint-Chapelle. Instead of beginning on the accounts as he should have

[012-097]done, he remained at the mercy of his perplexities.

[012-098]“One thing is evident,” he said to himself; “she hasn’t six millions;

[012-099]but that’s not the point--”

[012-100]Six days later, Modeste received the following letter:

[012-101]Mademoiselle,--You are not a D’Este. The name is a feigned one to

[012-102]conceal your own. Do I owe the revelations which you solicit to a

[012-103]person who is untruthful about herself? Question for question: Are

[012-104]you of an illustrious family? or a noble family? or a middle-class

[012-105]family? Undoubtedly ethics and morality cannot change; they are

[012-106]one: but obligations vary in the different states of life. Just as

[012-107]the sun lights up a scene diversely and produces differences which

[012-108]we admire, so morality conforms social duty to rank, to position.

[012-109]The peccadillo of a soldier is a crime in a general, and

[012-110]vice-versa. Observances are not alike in all cases. They are not

[012-111]the same for the gleaner in the field, for the girl who sews at

[012-112]fifteen sous a day, for the daughter of a petty shopkeeper, for

[012-113]the young bourgoise, for the child of a rich [[commerce|merchant]], for the

[012-114]heiress of a noble family, for a daughter of the house of Este. A

[012-115]king must not stoop to pick up a piece of gold, but a laborer

[012-116]ought to retrace his steps to find ten sous; though both are

[012-117]equally bound to obey the laws of economy. A daughter of Este, who

[012-118]is worth six millions, has the right to wear a broad-brimmed hat

[012-119]and plume, to flourish her whip, press the flanks of her barb, and

[012-120]ride like an amazon decked in gold lace, with a lackey behind her,

[012-121]into the presence of a poet and say: “I [[love]] poetry; and I would

[012-122]fain expiate Leonora’s cruelty to Tasso!” but a daughter of the

[012-123]people would cover herself with ridicule by imitating her. To what

[012-124]class do you belong? Answer sincerely, and I will answer the

[012-125]question you have put to me.

[012-126]As I have not the honor of knowing you personally, and yet am

[012-127]bound to you, in a measure, by the ties of poetic communion, I am

[012-128]unwilling to offer any commonplace compliments. Perhaps you have

[012-129]already won a malicious victory by thus embarrassing a maker of

[012-130]books.

[012-131]The young man was certainly not wanting in the sort of shrewdness which

[012-132]is permissible to a man of honor. By return courier he received an

[012-133]answer:--

[012-134]To Monsieur de Canalis,--You grow more and more sensible, my dear

[012-135]poet. My father is a count. The chief glory of our house was a

[012-136]cardinal, in the days when cardinals walked the earth by the side

[012-137]of kings. I am the last of our family, which ends in me; but I

[012-138]have the necessary quarterings to make my entry into any court or

[012-139]chapter-house in Europe. We are quite the equals of the Canalis.

[012-140]You will be so kind as to excuse me from sending you our arms.

[012-141]Endeavor to answer me as truthfully as I have now answered you. I

[012-142]await your response to know if I can then sign myself as I do now,

[012-143]Your servant, O. d’Este M.

[012-144]“The little mischief! how she abuses her privileges,” cried La Briere;

[012-145]“but isn’t she frank!”

[012-146]No young man can be four years private secretary to a cabinet minister,

[012-147]and live in [[paris|Paris]] and observe the carrying on of many intrigues, with

[012-148]perfect impunity; in fact, the purest soul is more or less intoxicated

[012-149]by the heady atmosphere of the imperial city. Happy in the thought

[012-150]that he was not Canalis, our young secretary engaged a place in the

[012-151]mail-coach for Havre, after writing a letter in which he announced that

[012-152]the promised answer would be sent a few days later,--excusing the delay

[012-153]on the ground of the importance of the confession and the pressure of

[012-154]his duties at the ministry.

[012-155]He took care to get from the director-general of the post-office a note

[012-156]to the postmaster at Havre, requesting secrecy and attention to his

[012-157]wishes. Ernest was thus enabled to see Francoise Cochet when she came

[012-158]for the letters, and to follow her without exciting observation. Guided

[012-159]by her, he reached [[ingouville|Ingouville]] and saw Modeste [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] at the window of

[012-160][[chalet|the Chalet]].

[012-161]“Well, Francoise?” he heard the young girl say, to which the maid

[012-162]responded,--

[012-163]“Yes, mademoiselle, I have one.”

[012-164]Struck by the girl’s great beauty, Ernest retraced his steps and asked a

[012-165]man on the street the name of the owner of that magnificent estate.

[012-166]“That?” said the man, nodding to the villa.

[012-167]“Yes, my friend.”

[012-168]“Oh, that belongs to Monsieur Vilquin, the richest shipping merchant in

[012-169]Havre, so rich he doesn’t know what he is worth.”

[012-170]“There is no Cardinal Vilquin that I know of in history,” thought

[012-171]Ernest, as he walked back to Havre for the night mail to Paris.

[012-172]Naturally he questioned the postmaster about the Vilquin family, and

[012-173]learned that it possessed an enormous [[social-mobility|fortune]]. Monsieur Vilquin had

[012-174]a son and two daughters, one of whom was married to Monsieur Althor,

[012-175]junior. Prudence kept La Briere from seeming anxious about the Vilquins;

[012-176]the postmaster was already looking at him slyly.

[012-177]“Is there there any one staying with them at the present moment,” he

[012-178]asked, “besides the family?”

[012-179]“The d’Herouville family is there just now. They do talk of a [[marriage]]

[012-180]between the young duke and the remaining Mademoiselle Vilquin.”

[012-181]“Ha!” thought Ernest; “there was a celebrated Cardinal d’Herouville

[012-182]under the Valois, and a terrible marshal whom they made a duke in the

[012-183]time of Henri IV.”

[012-184]Ernest returned to Paris having seen enough of Modeste to dream of her,

[012-185]and to think that, whether she were rich or whether she were poor, if

[012-186]she had a noble soul he would like to make her Madame de La Briere; and

[012-187]so thinking, he resolved to continue the correspondence.
