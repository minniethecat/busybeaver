# Section 17

[017-001]If you were able to follow me to the exquisite retreat where I

[017-002]fancy ourselves happy, if you knew my plans and projects, the

[017-003]dreadful word “folly!” might escape you, and I should be cruelly

[017-004]punished for sending [[poetry]] to a poet. Yes, I wish to be a spring

[017-005]of waters inexhaustible as a fertile land for the twenty years

[017-006]that nature allows me to shine. I want to drive away satiety by

[017-007]charm. I mean to be courageous for my friend as most women are for

[017-008]the world. I wish to vary happiness. I wish to put intelligence

[017-009]into tenderness, and to give piquancy to fidelity. I am filled

[017-010]with ambition to kill the rivals of the past, to conjure away all

[017-011]outside griefs by a wife’s gentleness, by her proud abnegation, to

[017-012]take a lifelong care of the nest,--such as birds can only take for

[017-013]a few weeks.

[017-014]Tell me, do you now think me to blame for my first letter? The

[017-015]mysterious wind of will drove me to you, as the tempest brings the

[017-016]little rose-tree to the pollard window. In your letter, which I

[017-017]hold here upon my heart, you cried out, like your ancestor when he

[017-018]departed for the Crusades, “God wills it.”

[017-019]Ah! but you will cry out, “What a chatterbox!” All the people

[017-020]round me say, on the contrary, “Mademoiselle is very taciturn.”

[017-021]O. d’Este M.

[017-022]CHAPTER XI. WHAT COMES OF CORRESPONDENCE

[017-023]The foregoing [[epistolary-novel|letters]] seemed very original to the persons from whom the

[017-024]author of the “Comedy of Human Life” obtained them; but their interest

[017-025]in this duel, this crossing of pens between two minds, may not be

[017-026]shared. For every hundred readers, eighty might weary of the battle.

[017-027]The respect due to the majority in every nation under a constitutional

[017-028]government, leads us, therefore, to suppress eleven other letters

[017-029]exchanged between [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] and [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] during the month of September. If,

[017-030]later on, some flattering majority should arise to claim them, let us

[017-031]hope that we can then find means to insert them in their proper place.

[017-032]Urged by a mind that seemed as aggressive as the heart was lovable, the

[017-033]truly chivalrous feelings of the poor secretary gave themselves free

[017-034]play in these suppressed letters, which seem, perhaps, more beautiful

[017-035]than they really are, because the imagination is charmed by a sense of

[017-036]the communion of two free souls. Ernest’s whole life was now wrapped up

[017-037]in these sweet scraps of paper; they were to him what banknotes are to a

[017-038]miser; while in Modeste’s soul a deep [[love]] took the place of her delight

[017-039]in agitating a glorious life, and being, in spite of distance, its

[017-040]mainspring. Ernest’s heart was the complement of [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]’s glory. Alas!

[017-041]it often takes two men to make a perfect lover, just as in literature

[017-042]we compose a type by collecting the peculiarities of several similar

[017-043]characters. How many a time a woman has been heard to say in her own

[017-044]salon after close and intimate conversations:--

[017-045]“Such a one is my [[the-ideal|ideal]] as to soul, and I love the other who is only a

[017-046]dream of the senses.”

[017-047]The last letter written by Modeste, which here follows, gives us

[017-048]a glimpse of the enchanted isle to which the meanderings of this

[017-049]correspondence had led the two lovers.

[017-050]To Monsieur de Canalis,--Be at [[provincial-life|Havre]] next Sunday; go to church;

[017-051]after the morning service, walk once or twice round the nave, and

[017-052]go out without speaking to any one; but wear a white rose in your

[017-053]button-hole. Then return to [[paris|Paris]], where you shall receive an

[017-054]answer. I warn you that this answer will not be what you wish;

[017-055]for, as I told you, the future is not yet mine. But should I not

[017-056]indeed be mad and foolish to say yes without having seen you? When

[017-057]I have seen you I can say no without wounding you; I can make sure

[017-058]that you shall not see me.

[017-059]This letter had been sent off the evening before the day when the

[017-060]abortive struggle between [[dumay|Dumay]] and Modeste had taken place. The happy

[017-061]girl was impatiently awaiting Sunday, when her eyes were to vindicate or

[017-062]condemn her heart and her actions,--a solemn moment in the life of any

[017-063]woman, and which three months of close communion of souls now rendered

[017-064]as romantic as the most imaginative maiden could have wished. Every one,

[017-065]except the mother, had taken this torpor of expectation for the calm of

[017-066]innocence. No matter how firmly family laws and religious precepts may

[017-067]bind, there will always be the Clarissas and the Julies, whose souls

[017-068]like flowing cups o’erlap the brim under some spiritual pressure.

[017-069]Modeste was glorious in the savage energy with which she repressed her

[017-070]exuberant youthful happiness and remained demurely quiet. Let us say

[017-071]frankly that the memory of her sister was more potent upon her than any

[017-072]social conventions; her will was iron in the resolve to bring no grief

[017-073]upon her father and her mother. But what tumultuous heavings were within

[017-074]her breast! no wonder that a mother guessed them.

[017-075]On the following day Modeste and Madame Dumay took Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] about

[017-076]mid-day to a seat in the sun among the flowers. The blind woman turned

[017-077]her wan and blighted face toward the ocean; she inhaled the odors of

[017-078]the sea and took the hand of her daughter who remained beside her. The

[017-079]mother hesitated between forgiveness and remonstrance ere she put the

[017-080]important question; for she comprehended the girl’s love and recognized,

[017-081]as the pretended Canalis had done, that Modeste was exceptional in

[017-082]nature.

[017-083]“God grant that your father return in time! If he delays much longer he

[017-084]will find none but you to love him. Modeste, promise me once more never

[017-085]to leave him,” she said in a fond maternal tone.

[017-086]Modeste lifted her mother’s hands to her lips and kissed them gently,

[017-087]replying: “Need I say it again?”

[017-088]“Ah, my child! I did this thing myself. I left my father to follow my

[017-089]husband; and yet my father was all alone; I was all the child he had. Is

[017-090]that why God has so punished me? What I ask of you is to marry as your

[017-091]father wishes, to cherish him in your heart, not to sacrifice him to

[017-092]your own happiness, but to make him the centre of your home. Before

[017-093]losing my sight, I wrote him all my wishes, and I know he will execute

[017-094]them. I enjoined him to keep his property intact and in his own hands;

[017-095]not that I distrust you, my Modeste, for a moment, but who can be sure

[017-096]of a son-in-law? Ah! my daughter, look at me; was I reasonable? One

[017-097]glance of the eye decided my life. Beauty, so often deceitful, in my

[017-098]case spoke true; but even were it the same with you, my poor child,

[017-099]swear to me that you will let your father inquire into the character,

[017-100]the habits, the heart, and the previous life of the man you distinguish

[017-101]with your love--if, by chance, there is such a man.”

[017-102]“I will never marry without the consent of my father,” answered Modeste.

[017-103]“You see, my darling,” said Madame Mignon after a long pause, “that if I

[017-104]am dying by inches through Bettina’s wrong-doing, your father would not

[017-105]survive yours, no, not for a moment. I know him; he would put a pistol

[017-106]to his head,--there could be no life, no happiness on earth for him.”

[017-107]Modeste walked a few steps away from her mother, but immediately came

[017-108]back.

[017-109]“Why did you leave me?” demanded Madame Mignon.

[017-110]“You made me cry, mamma,” answered Modeste.

[017-111]“Ah, my little darling, kiss me. You love no one here? you have no

[017-112]lover, have you?” she asked, holding Modeste on her lap, heart to heart.

[017-113]“No, my dear mamma,” said the little Jesuit.

[017-114]“Can you swear it?”

[017-115]“Oh, yes!” cried Modeste.

[017-116]Madame Mignon said no more; but she still doubted.

[017-117]“At least, if you do choose your husband, you will tell your father?”

[017-118]she resumed.

[017-119]“I promised that to my sister, and to you, mother. What evil do you

[017-120]think I could commit while I wear that ring upon my finger and read

[017-121]those words: ‘Think of Bettina?’ Poor sister!”

[017-122]At these words a truce of silence came between the pair; the mother’s

[017-123]blighted eyes rained tears which Modeste could not check, though she

[017-124]threw herself upon her knees, and cried: “Forgive me! oh, forgive me,

[017-125]mother!”

[017-126]At this instant the excellent Dumay was coming up the hill of [[ingouville|Ingouville]]

[017-127]on the double-quick,--a fact quite abnormal in the present life of the

[017-128]cashier.

[017-129]Three letters had brought ruin to the Mignons; a single letter now

[017-130]restored their fortunes. Dumay had received from a sea-captain just

[017-131]arrived from the China Seas the following letter containing the first

[017-132]news of his patron and friend, Charles Mignon:--

[017-133]To Monsieur Jean Dumay:

[017-134]My Dear Dumay,--I shall quickly follow, barring the chances of the

[017-135]voyage, the vessel which carries this letter. In fact, I should

[017-136]have taken it, but I did not wish to leave my own ship to which I

[017-137]am accustomed.

[017-138]I told you that no new was to be good news. But the first words of

[017-139]this letter ought to make you a happy man. I have made seven

[017-140]millions at the least. I am bringing back a large part of it in

[017-141]indigo, one third in safe London securities, and another third in

[017-142]good solid gold. Your remittances helped me to make the sum I had

[017-143]settled in my own mind much sooner than I expected. I wanted two

[017-144]millions for my daughters and a competence for myself.

[017-145]I have been engaged in the opium [[commerce|trade]] with the largest houses in

[017-146]Canton, all ten times richer than ever I was. You have no idea, in

[017-147]Europe, what these rich East India merchants are. I went to Asia

[017-148]Minor and purchased opium at low prices, and from thence to Canton

[017-149]where I delivered my cargoes to the companies who control the

[017-150]trade. My last expedition was to the Philippine Islands where I

[017-151]exchanged opium for indigo of the first quality. In fact, I may

[017-152]have half a million more than I stated, for I reckoned the indigo

[017-153]at what it cost me. I have always been well in health; not the

[017-154]slightest illness. That is the result of working for one’s

[017-155]children. Since the second year I have owned a pretty little brig

[017-156]of seven hundred tons, called the “Mignon.” She is built of oak,

[017-157]double-planked, and copper-fastened; and all the interior fittings

[017-158]were done to suit me. She is, in fact, an additional piece of

[017-159]property.

[017-160]A sea-life and the active habits required by my business have kept

[017-161]me in good health. To tell you all this is the same as telling it

[017-162]to my two daughters and my dear wife. I trust that the wretched

[017-163]man who took away my Bettina deserted her when he heard of my

[017-164]ruin; and that I shall find the poor lost lamb at [[chalet|the Chalet]]. My

[017-165]three dear women and my Dumay! All four of you have been ever

[017-166]present in my thoughts for the last three years. You are a rich

[017-167]man, now, Dumay. Your share, outside of my own [[social-mobility|fortune]], amounts to

[017-168]five hundred and sixty thousand francs, for which I send you

[017-169]herewith a check, which can only be paid to you in person by the

[017-170]Mongenods, who have been duly advised from New York.

[017-171]A few short months, and I shall see you all again, and all well, I

[017-172]trust. My dear Dumay, if I write this letter to you it is because

[017-173]I am anxious to keep my fortune a secret for the present. I

[017-174]therefore leave to you the happiness of preparing my dear angels

[017-175]for my return. I have had enough of commerce; and I am resolved to

[017-176]leave Havre. My intention is to buy back the estate of La Bastie,

[017-177]and to entail it, so as to establish an estate yielding at least a

[017-178]hundred thousand francs a year, and then to ask the king to grant

[017-179]that one of my sons-in-law may succeed to my name and title. You

[017-180]know, my poor Dumay, what a terrible misfortune overtook us

[017-181]through the fatal [[honor|reputation]] of a large fortune,--my daughter’s

[017-182]honor was lost. I have therefore resolved that the amount of my

[017-183]present fortune shall not be known. I shall not disembark at

[017-184]Havre, but at Marseilles. I shall sell my indigo, and negotiate

[017-185]for the purchase of La Bastie through the house of Mongenod in

[017-186]Paris. I shall put my funds in the Bank of France and return to

[017-187]the Chalet giving out that I have a considerable fortune in

[017-188]merchandise. My daughters will be supposed to have two or three

[017-189]hundred thousand francs. To choose which of my sons-in-law is

[017-190]worthy to succeed to my title and estates and to live with us, is

[017-191]now the object of my life; but both of them must be, like you and

[017-192]me, honest, loyal, and firm men, and absolutely honorable.
