# Section 45

[045-001]CHAPTER XXIX. CONCLUSION

[045-002]“Ladies,” said the Prince de Cadignan, as the guests were about to

[045-003]separate for the night, “I know that several of you propose to follow

[045-004]the hounds with us to-morrow, and it becomes my duty to tell you that if

[045-005]you will be Dianas you must rise, like Diana, with the dawn. The meet is

[045-006]for half-past eight o’clock. I have in the course of my life seen many

[045-007]women display greater courage than men, but for a few seconds only; and

[045-008]you will need a strong dose of resolution to keep you on horseback the

[045-009]whole day, barring a halt for breakfast, which we shall take, like true

[045-010]hunters and huntresses, on the nail. Are you still determined to show

[045-011]yourselves trained horse-women?”

[045-012]“Prince, it is necessary for me to do so,” said [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], adroitly.

[045-013]“I answer for myself,” said the Duchesse de Chaulieu.

[045-014]“And I for my daughter Diane; she is worthy of her name,” added the

[045-015]prince. “So, then, you all persist in your intentions? However, I shall

[045-016]arrange, for the sake of Madame and Mademoiselle de Verneuil and others

[045-017]of the party who stay at home, to drive the stag to the further end of

[045-018]the pond.”

[045-019]“Make yourself quite easy, mesdames,” said the Prince de Loudon, when

[045-020]the Royal Huntsman had left the room; “that breakfast ‘on the nail’ will

[045-021]take place under a comfortable tent.”

[045-022]The next day, at dawn, all signs gave promise of a glorious day. The

[045-023]skies, veiled by a slight gray vapor, showed spaces of purest blue, and

[045-024]would surely be swept clear before mid-day by the northwest wind, which

[045-025]was already playing with the fleecy cloudlets. As the hunting party left

[045-026]the chateau, the Master of the Hunt, the Duc de Rhetore, and the Prince

[045-027]de Loudon, who had no ladies to escort, rode in the advance, noticing

[045-028]the white masses of the chateau, with its rising chimneys relieved

[045-029]against the brilliant red-brown foliage which the trees in Normandy put

[045-030]on at the close of a fine autumn.

[045-031]“The ladies are fortunate in their weather,” remarked the Duc de

[045-032]Rhetore.

[045-033]“Oh, in spite of all their boasting,” replied the Prince de Cadignan, “I

[045-034]think they will let us hunt without them!”

[045-035]“So they might, if each had not a squire,” said the duke.

[045-036]At this moment the attention of these determined huntsmen--for the

[045-037]Prince de Loudon and the Duc de Rhetore are of the race of Nimrod, and

[045-038]the best shots of the faubourg Saint-Germain--was attracted by a loud

[045-039]altercation; and they spurred their horses to an open space at the

[045-040]entrance to the forest of Rosembray, famous for its mossy turf, which

[045-041]was appointed for the meet. The cause of the quarrel was soon apparent.

[045-042]The Prince de Loudon, afflicted with anglomania, had brought out his own

[045-043]hunting establishment, which was exclusively Britannic, and placed it

[045-044]under orders of the Master of the Hunt. Now, one of his men, a little

[045-045]Englishman,--fair, pale, insolent, and phlegmatic, scarcely able to

[045-046]speak a word of French, and dressed with a neatness which distinguishes

[045-047]all Britons, even those of the lower classes,--had posted himself on one

[045-048]side of this open space. John Barry wore a short frock-coat, buttoned

[045-049]tightly at the waist, made of scarlet cloth, with buttons bearing the De

[045-050]Verneuil arms, white leather breeches, top-boots, a striped waistcoat,

[045-051]and a collar and cape of black velvet. He held in his hand a small

[045-052]hunting-whip, and hanging to his wrist by a silken cord was a

[045-053]brass horn. This man, the first whipper-in, was accompanied by two

[045-054]thorough-bred dogs,--fox-hounds, white, with liver spots, long in the

[045-055]leg, fine in the muzzle, with slender heads, and little ears at their

[045-056]crests. The huntsman--famous in the English county from which the

[045-057]Prince de Loudon had obtained him at great cost--was in charge of an

[045-058]establishment of fifteen horses and sixty English hounds, which cost the

[045-059]Duc de Verneuil, who was nothing of a huntsman, but chose to indulge his

[045-060]son in this essentially royal taste, an enormous sum of money to keep

[045-061]up.

[045-062]Now, when John arrived on the ground, he found himself forestalled by

[045-063]three other whippers-in, in charge of two of the royal packs of hounds

[045-064]which had been brought there in carts. They were the three best huntsmen

[045-065]of the Prince de Cadignan, and presented, both in character and in their

[045-066]distinctively French costume, a marked contrast to the representative

[045-067]of insolent Albion. These favorites of the Prince, each wearing

[045-068]full-brimmed, three-cornered hats, very flat and very wide-spreading,

[045-069]beneath which grinned their swarthy, tanned, and wrinkled faces, lighted

[045-070]by three pairs of twinkling eyes, were noticeably lean, sinewy, and

[045-071]vigorous, like men in whom sport had become a passion. All three were

[045-072]supplied with immense horns of Dampierre, wound with green worsted

[045-073]cords, leaving only the brass tubes visible; but they controlled their

[045-074]dogs by the eye and voice. Those noble animals were far more faithful

[045-075]and submissive subjects than the human lieges whom the king was at that

[045-076]moment addressing; all were marked with white, black, or liver spots,

[045-077]each having as distinctive a countenance as the soldiers of Napoleon,

[045-078]their eyes flashing like diamonds at the slightest noise. One of them,

[045-079]brought from Poitou, was short in the back, deep in the shoulder,

[045-080]low-jointed, and lop-eared; the other, from England, white, fine as a

[045-081]greyhound with no belly, small ears, and built for running. Both were

[045-082]young, impatient, and yelping eagerly, while the old hounds, on the

[045-083]contrary, covered with scars, lay quietly with their heads on their

[045-084]forepaws, and their ears to the earth like savages.

[045-085]As the Englishman came up, the royal dogs and huntsmen looked at each

[045-086]other as though they said, “If we cannot hunt by ourselves his Majesty’s

[045-087]service is insulted.”

[045-088]Beginning with jests, the quarrel presently grew fiercer between

[045-089]Monsieur Jacquin La Roulie, the old French whipper-in, and John Barry,

[045-090]the young islander. The two princes guessed from afar the subject of

[045-091]the altercation, and the Master of the Hunt, setting spurs to his horse,

[045-092]brought it to an end by saying, in a voice of authority:--

[045-093]“Who drew the wood?”

[045-094]“I, monseigneur,” said the Englishman.

[045-095]“Very good,” said the Prince de Cadignan, proceeding to take Barry’s

[045-096]report.

[045-097]Dogs and men became silent and respectful before the Royal Huntsman, as

[045-098]though each recognized his dignity as supreme. The prince laid out the

[045-099]day’s work; for it is with a hunt as it is with a battle, and the

[045-100]Master of Charles X.’s hounds was the Napoleon of forests. Thanks to the

[045-101]admirable system which he has introduced into French venery, he was able

[045-102]to turn his thoughts exclusively to the science and strategy of it.

[045-103]He now quietly assigned a special duty to the Prince de Loudon’s

[045-104]establishment, that of driving the stag to water, when, as he expected,

[045-105]the royal hounds had sent it into the Crown forest which outlined the

[045-106]horizon directly in front of the chateau. The prince knew well how to

[045-107]soothe the self-[[love]] of his old huntsmen by giving them the most arduous

[045-108]part of the work, and also that of the Englishman, whom he employed at

[045-109]his own speciality, affording him a chance to show the fleetness of his

[045-110]horses and dogs in the open. The two national systems were thus face to

[045-111]face and allowed to do their best under each other’s eyes.

[045-112]“Does monseigneur wish us to wait any longer?” said La Roulie,

[045-113]respectfully.

[045-114]“I know what you mean, old friend,” said the prince. “It is late, but--”

[045-115]“Here come the ladies,” said the second whipper-in.

[045-116]At that moment the cavalcade of sixteen riders was seen to approach

[045-117]at the head of which were the green veils of the four ladies. Modeste,

[045-118]accompanied by her father, the grand equerry, and La Briere, was in the

[045-119]advance, beside the Duchesse de Maufrigneuse whom the Vicomte de Serizy

[045-120]escorted. Behind them rode the Duchesse de Chaulieu, flanked by [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]],

[045-121]on whom she was smiling without a trace of rancor. When they had reached

[045-122]the open space where the huntsmen with their red coats and brass bugles,

[045-123]surrounded by the hounds, made a picture worthy of Van der Meulen, the

[045-124]Duchesse de Chaulieu, who, in spite of her embonpoint, sat her horse

[045-125]admirably, rode up to Modeste, finding it more for her dignity not to

[045-126]avoid that young person, to whom the evening before she had not said a

[045-127]single word.

[045-128]When the Master of the Hunt finished his compliments to the ladies on

[045-129]their amazing punctuality, Eleonore deigned to observe the magnificent

[045-130]whip which sparked in Modeste’s little hand, and graciously asked leave

[045-131]to look at it.

[045-132]“I have never seen anything of the kind more beautiful,” she said,

[045-133]showing it to Diane de Maufrigneuse. “It is in keeping with its

[045-134]possessor,” she added, returning it to Modeste.

[045-135]“You must admit, Madame la duchesse,” answered Mademoiselle de La

[045-136]Bastie, with a tender and malicious glance at La Briere, “that it is a

[045-137]rather strange gift from the hand of a future husband.”

[045-138]“I should take it,” said Madame de Maufrigneuse, “as a declaration of my

[045-139]rights, in remembrance of Louis XIV.”

[045-140]La Briere’s eyes were suffused, and for a moment he dropped his

[045-141]reins; but a second glance from Modeste ordered him not to betray his

[045-142]happiness. The hunt now began.

[045-143]The Duc d’Herouville took occasion to say in a low voice to his

[045-144]fortunate rival; “Monsieur, I hope that you will make your wife happy;

[045-145]if I can be useful to you in any way, command my services; I should be

[045-146]only too glad to contribute to the happiness of so charming a pair.”

[045-147]This great day, in which such vast interests of heart and [[commerce|fortune]] were

[045-148]decided, caused but one anxiety to the Master of the Hunt,--namely,

[045-149]whether or not the stag would cross the pond and be killed on the

[045-150]lawn before the house; for huntsmen of his calibre are like great

[045-151]chess-players who can predict a checkmate under certain circumstances.

[045-152]The happy old man succeeded to the height of his wishes; the run was

[045-153]magnificent, and the ladies released him from his attendance upon them

[045-154]for the hunt of the next day but one,--which, however, turned out to be

[045-155]rainy.

[045-156]The Duc de Verneuil’s guests stayed five days at Rosembray. On the last

[045-157]day the Gazette de France announced the appointment of Monsieur le Baron

[045-158]de Canalis to the rank of commander of the Legion of [[honor]], and to the

[045-159]post of minister at Carlsruhe.

[045-160]When, early in the month of December, Madame de La Bastie, operated upon

[045-161]by Desplein, recovered her sight and saw [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere for the

[045-162]first time, she pressed Modeste’s hand and whispered in her ear, “I

[045-163]should have chosen him myself.”

[045-164]Toward the last of February all the deeds for the estates in Provence

[045-165]were signed by Latournelle, and about that time the family of La Bastie

[045-166]obtained the marked honor of the king’s signature to the [[marriage]]

[045-167]contract and to the ordinance transmitting their title and arms to La

[045-168]Briere, who henceforth took the name of La Briere-La Bastie. The estate

[045-169]of La Bastie was entailed by [[epistolary-novel|letters]]-patent issued about the end of

[045-170]April. La Briere’s witnesses on the occasion of his marriage were

[045-171]Canalis and the minister whom he had served for five years as secretary.

[045-172]Those of the bride were the Duc d’Herouville and Desplein, whom the

[045-173]Mignons long held in grateful remembrance, after giving him magnificent

[045-174]and substantial proofs of their regard.

[045-175]Later, in the course of this long history of our manners and customs, we

[045-176]may again meet Monsieur and Madame de La Briere-La Bastie; and those

[045-177]who have the eyes to see, will then behold how sweet, how easy, is the

[045-178]marriage yoke with an educated and intelligent woman; for Modeste, who

[045-179]had the wit to avoid the follies of pedantry, is the pride and happiness

[045-180]of her husband, as she is of her family and of all those who surround

[045-181]her.
