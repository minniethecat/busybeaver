# Section 10

[010-001]Your servant, O. d’Este M.

[010-002]P.S.--If you do me the favor to answer this letter address your

[010-003]reply, if you please, to Mademoiselle F. Cochet, “poste restante,”

[010-004][[provincial-life|Havre]].

[010-005]CHAPTER VII. A POET OF THE ANGELIC SCHOOL

[010-006]All young girls, romantic or otherwise, can imagine the impatience in

[010-007]which [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] lived for the next few days. The air was full of tongues

[010-008]of fire. The trees were like a plumage. She was not conscious of a body;

[010-009]she hovered in space, the earth melted away under her feet. Full of

[010-010]admiration for the post-office, she followed her little sheet of paper

[010-011]on its way; she was happy, as we all are happy at twenty years of age,

[010-012]in the first exercise of our will. She was possessed, as in the middle

[010-013]ages. She made pictures in her mind of [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]]’s abode, of his

[010-014]study; she saw him unsealing her letter; and then followed myriads of

[010-015]suppositions.

[010-016]After sketching the [[poetry]] we cannot do less than give the profile of

[010-017]the poet. Canalis is a short, spare man, with an air of good-breeding, a

[010-018]dark-complexioned, moon-shaped face, and a rather mean head like that

[010-019]of a man who has more vanity than pride. He loves luxury, rank, and

[010-020]splendor. Money is of more importance to him than to most men. Proud of

[010-021]his birth, even more than of his talent, he destroys the value of his

[010-022]ancestors by making too much of them in the present day,--after all,

[010-023]the Canalis are not Navarreins, nor Cadignans, nor Grandlieus. Nature,

[010-024]however, helps him out in his pretensions. He has those eyes of Eastern

[010-025]effulgence which we demand in a poet, a delicate charm of manner, and a

[010-026]vibrant voice; yet a taint of natural charlatanism destroys the effect

[010-027]of nearly all these advantages; he is a born comedian. If he puts

[010-028]forward his well-shaped foot, it is because the attitude has become

[010-029]a habit; if he uses exclamatory terms they are part of himself; if he

[010-030]poses with high dramatic action he has made that deportment his second

[010-031]nature. Such defects as these are not incompatible with a general

[010-032]benevolence and a certain quality of errant and purely [[the-ideal|ideal]] chivalry,

[010-033]which distinguishes the paladin from the knight. Canalis has not

[010-034]devotion enough for a Don Quixote, but he has too much elevation of

[010-035]thought not to put himself on the nobler side of questions and things.

[010-036]His poetry, which takes the town by storm on all profitable occasions,

[010-037]really injures the man as a poet; for he is not without mind, but

[010-038]his talent prevents him from developing it; he is overweighted by his

[010-039][[honor|reputation]], and is always aiming to make himself appear greater than he

[010-040]has the credit of being. Thus, as often happens, the man is entirely out

[010-041]of keeping with the products of his thought. The author of these naive,

[010-042]caressing, tender little lyrics, these calm idylls pure and cold as the

[010-043]surface of a lake, these verses so essentially feminine, is an ambitious

[010-044]little creature in a tightly buttoned frock-coat, with the air of

[010-045]a diplomat seeking political influence, smelling of the musk of

[010-046][[aristocracy]], full of pretension, thirsting for money, already spoiled by

[010-047]success in two directions, and wearing the double wreath of myrtle and

[010-048]of laurel. A government situation worth eight thousand francs, three

[010-049]thousand francs’ annuity from the literary fund, two thousand from the

[010-050]Academy, three thousand more from the paternal estate (less the taxes

[010-051]and the cost of keeping it in order),--a total fixed income of fifteen

[010-052]thousand francs, plus the ten thousand bought in, one year with another,

[010-053]by his poetry; in all twenty-five thousand francs,--this for Modeste’s

[010-054]hero was so precarious and insufficient an income that he usually spent

[010-055]five or six thousand francs more every year; but the king’s privy purse

[010-056]and the secret funds of the foreign office had hitherto supplied the

[010-057]deficit. He wrote a hymn for the king’s coronation which earned him a

[010-058]whole silver service,--having refused a sum of money on the ground that

[010-059]a Canalis owed his duty to his sovereign.

[010-060]But about this time Canalis had, as the journalists say, exhausted his

[010-061]budget. He felt himself unable to invent any new form of poetry; his

[010-062]lyre did not have seven strings, it had one; and having played on that

[010-063]one string so long, the public allowed him no other alternative but to

[010-064]hang himself with it, or to hold his tongue. De Marsay, who did not

[010-065]like Canalis, made a remark whose poisoned shaft touched the poet to

[010-066]the quick of his vanity. “Canalis,” he said, “always reminds me of that

[010-067]brave man whom Frederic the Great called up and commended after a battle

[010-068]because his trumpet had never ceased tooting its one little tune.”

[010-069]Canalis’s ambition was to enter political life, and he made capital of a

[010-070]journey he had taken to Madrid as secretary to the embassy of the Duc

[010-071]de Chaulieu, though it was really made, according to Parisian gossip, in

[010-072]the capacity of “attache to the duchess.” How many times a sarcasm or a

[010-073]single speech has decided the whole course of a man’s life. Colla,

[010-074]the late president of the Cisalpine republic, and the best lawyer in

[010-075]Piedmont, was told by a friend when he was forty years of age that

[010-076]he knew nothing of botany. He was piqued, became a second Jussieu,

[010-077]cultivated flowers, and compiled and published “The Flora of Piedmont,”

[010-078]in Latin, a labor of ten years. “I’ll master De Marsay some of these

[010-079]days!” thought the crushed poet; “after all, Canning and Chateaubriand

[010-080]are both in politics.”

[010-081]Canalis would gladly have brought forth some great political poem, but

[010-082]he was afraid of the French press, whose criticisms are savage upon any

[010-083]writer who takes four alexandrines to express one idea. Of all the poets

[010-084]of our day only three, Hugo, Theophile Gautier, and De Vigny, have been

[010-085]able to win the double glory of poet and prose-writer, like Racine and

[010-086]Voltaire, Moliere, and Rabelais,--a rare distinction in the literature

[010-087]of France, which ought to give a man a right to the crowning title of

[010-088]poet.

[010-089]So then, the bard of the faubourg Saint-Germain was doing a wise thing

[010-090]in trying to house his little chariot under the protecting roof of the

[010-091]present government. When he became president of the court of Claims at

[010-092]the foreign office, he stood in need of a secretary,--a friend who could

[010-093]take his place in various ways; cook up his interests with publishers,

[010-094]see to his glory in the newspapers, help him if need be in politics,--in

[010-095]short, a cat’s paw and satellite. In [[paris|Paris]] many men of celebrity in art,

[010-096]science, and literature have one or more train-bearers, captains of

[010-097]the guard, chamberlains as it were, who live in the sunshine of their

[010-098]presence,--aides-de-camp entrusted with delicate missions, allowing

[010-099]themselves to be compromised if necessary; workers round the pedestal

[010-100]of the idol; not exactly his servants, nor yet his equals; bold in

[010-101]his defence, first in the breach, covering all retreats, busy with his

[010-102][[commerce|business]], and devoted to him just so long as their illusions last,

[010-103]or until the moment when they have got all they wanted. Some of these

[010-104]satellites perceive the ingratitude of their great man; others feel that

[010-105]they are simply made tools of; many weary of the life; very few remain

[010-106]contented with that sweet equality of feeling and sentiment which is

[010-107]the only reward that should be looked for in an intimacy with a superior

[010-108]man,--a reward that contented Ali when Mohammed raised him to himself.

[010-109]Many of these men, misled by vanity, think themselves quite as capable

[010-110]as their patron. Pure devotion, such as Modeste conceived it, without

[010-111]money and without price, and more especially without hope, is rare.

[010-112]Nevertheless there are Mennevals to be found, more perhaps in Paris

[010-113]than elsewhere, men who value a life in the background with its peaceful

[010-114]toil; these are the wandering Benedictines of our social world, which

[010-115]offers them no other monastery. These brave, meek hearts live, by their

[010-116]actions and in their hidden lives, the poetry that poets utter. They

[010-117]are poets themselves in soul, in tenderness, in their lonely vigils and

[010-118]meditations,--as truly poets as others of the name on paper, who fatten

[010-119]in the fields of literature at so much a verse; like Lord Byron, like

[010-120]all who live, alas, by ink, the Hippocrene water of to-day, for want of

[010-121]a better.

[010-122]Attracted by the fame of Canalis, also by the prospect of political

[010-123]interest, and advised thereto by Madame d’Espard, who acted in the

[010-124]matter for the Duchesse de Chaulieu, a young lawyer of the court

[010-125]of Claims became secretary and confidential friend of the poet, who

[010-126]welcomed and petted him very much as a broker caresses his first dabbler

[010-127]in the funds. The beginning of this companionship bore a very fair

[010-128]resemblance to friendship. The young man had already held the same

[010-129]relation to a minister, who went out of office in 1827, taking care

[010-130]before he did so to appoint his young secretary to a place in the

[010-131]foreign office. [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere, then about twenty-seven years of

[010-132]age, was decorated with the Legion of honor but was without other means

[010-133]than his salary; he was accustomed to the management of business and

[010-134]had learned a good deal of life during his four years in a minister’s

[010-135]cabinet. Kindly, amiable, and over-modest, with a heart full of pure and

[010-136]sound feelings, he was averse to putting himself in the foreground. He

[010-137]loved his country, and wished to serve her, but notoriety abashed him.

[010-138]To him the place of secretary to a Napoleon was far more desirable

[010-139]than that of the minister himself. As soon as he became the friend and

[010-140]secretary of Canalis he did a great amount of labor for him, but by the

[010-141]end of eighteen months he had learned to understand the barrenness of

[010-142]a nature that was poetic through literary expression only. The truth of

[010-143]the old proverb, “The cowl doesn’t make the monk,” is eminently shown in

[010-144]literature. It is extremely rare to find among literary men a nature

[010-145]and a talent that are in perfect accord. The faculties are not the man

[010-146]himself. This disconnection, whose phenomena are amazing, proceeds

[010-147]from an unexplored, possibly an unexplorable mystery. The brain and its

[010-148]products of all kinds (for in art the hand of man is a continuation of

[010-149]his brain) are a world apart, which flourishes beneath the cranium in

[010-150]absolute independence of sentiments, feelings, and all that is called

[010-151]virtue, the virtue of citizens, fathers, and private life. This, however

[010-152]true, is not absolutely so; nothing is absolutely true of man. It is

[010-153]certain that a debauched man will dissipate his talent, that a drunkard

[010-154]will waste it in libations; while, on the other hand, no man can give

[010-155]himself talent by wholesome living: nevertheless, it is all but proved

[010-156]that Virgil, the painter of [[love]], never loved a Dido, and that

[010-157]Rousseau, the model citizen, had enough pride to had furnished forth an

[010-158]aristocracy. On the other hand Raphael and Michael Angelo do present the

[010-159]glorious conjunction of genius with the lines of character. Talent

[010-160]in men is therefore, in all moral points, very much what beauty is in

[010-161]women,--simply a promise. Let us, therefore, doubly admire the man in

[010-162]whom both heart and character equal the perfection of his genius.

[010-163]When Ernest discovered within his poet an ambitious egoist, the worst

[010-164]species of egoist (for there are some amiable forms of the vice), he

[010-165]felt a delicacy in leaving him. Honest natures cannot easily break the

[010-166]ties that bind them, especially if they have tied them voluntarily. The

[010-167]secretary was therefore still living in domestic relations with the

[010-168]poet when Modeste’s letter arrived,--in such relations, be it said, as

[010-169]involved a perpetual sacrifice of his feelings. La Briere admitted the

[010-170]frankness with which Canalis had laid himself bare before him. Moreover,

[010-171]the defects of the man, who will always be considered a great poet

[010-172]during his lifetime and flattered as Martmontel was flattered, were only

[010-173]the wrong side of his brilliant qualities. Without his vanity and his

[010-174]magniloquence it is possible that he might never have acquired the

[010-175]sonorous elocution which is so useful and even necessary an instrument

[010-176]in political life. His cold-bloodedness touched at certain points on

[010-177]rectitude and loyalty; his ostentation had a lining of generosity.

[010-178]Results, we must remember, are to the profit of society; motives concern

[010-179]God.
