# Gobenheim

A Le Havre banker and merchant, Gobenheim represents the calculating, indifferent face of provincial bourgeois commerce — a man whose heart, if he ever had one, has been cauterized by the relentless pursuit of profit.

## Role in the Novel

Gobenheim serves as a minor but sharply-drawn figure in the social world of the Chalet. He is a regular at the nightly whist games that form the ritual center of the Mignon household's provincial routine (003-138). Balzac uses him to illustrate a particular type of merchant psychology: the man so thoroughly absorbed in business that human connection becomes merely another transaction. His presence in the novel adds texture to the commercial milieu of Le Havre and provides a counterpoint to the more emotionally engaged characters like Dumay and Madame Latournelle.

## Biography

### Background & Origins

Gobenheim is connected to the banking house of Gobenheim-Keller, a firm of sufficient standing to be a creditor to Charles Mignon (002-121). He belongs to the commercial bourgeoisie of Le Havre, a port city whose fortunes rise and fall with maritime trade. His exact origins are not detailed, but his name and banking connections suggest a family of established financial interests.

### Key Actions

Gobenheim was one of the creditors to whom Charles Mignon owed approximately three thousand francs before his departure for the East (002-097). He is a fixture at the Chalet, often accompanying Madame Latournelle on her visits (005-165). During the nightly whist games, he maintains his characteristic detachment — "more than indifferent, noticing everything" (002-168). His presence at the Chalet is tolerated rather than welcomed; the household accommodates him as one does a useful but unlovable piece of furniture.

### Fate / Resolution

Gobenheim profits from Dumay's financial struggles (005-038), exemplifying the merchant's credo: profit before sentiment. His role in the novel's conclusion is minimal, as the focus shifts to Modeste's romantic resolution. He remains what he has always been: a fixture of Le Havre commerce, unchanged and unchanging.

## Character Traits

- **Defining qualities**: Cold, calculating, observant, wholly absorbed in commerce, emotionally cauterized (002-131)
- **Flaws / Contradictions**: His indifference is so complete it becomes a kind of negative presence; Balzac notes that the death of his business standing "allowed it to be supposed that Gobenheim had a heart" (002-131), suggesting that even the loss of money could not truly animate him
- **Social Type**: The provincial banker — Balzac's recurring portrait of the man whose humanity has been subsumed by the ledger

## Balzacian Context

Gobenheim appears only in *Modeste Mignon*. He belongs to Balzac's extensive gallery of bankers and moneylenders — figures like Nucingen, du Tillet, and the Kellers — who embody the rising power of finance capital in post-Napoleonic France. Gobenheim is a provincial variant of this type: smaller in scale but no less illustrative of the commercial soul.
