# Section 4

[004-001]When the war recommenced, [[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]] was promoted into the cavalry

[004-002]and lost sight of his comrade. In 1812 the last of the Mignon de La

[004-003]Bastie was an officer of the Legion of [[honor]] and major of a regiment

[004-004]of cavalry. Taken prisoner by the Russians he was sent, like so

[004-005]many others, to Siberia. He made the journey in company with another

[004-006]prisoner, a poor lieutenant, in whom he recognized his old friend Jean

[004-007][[dumay|Dumay]], brave, neglected, undecorated, unhappy, like a million of other

[004-008]woollen epaulets, rank and file--that canvas of men on which

[004-009]Napoleon painted the picture of the Empire. While in Siberia, the

[004-010]lieutenant-colonel, to kill time, taught writing and arithmetic to the

[004-011]Breton, whose early education had seemed a useless waste of time to Pere

[004-012]Scevola. Charles found in the old comrade of his marching days one of

[004-013]those rare hearts into which a man can pour his griefs while telling his

[004-014]joys.

[004-015]The young Provencal had met the fate which attends all handsome

[004-016]bachelors. In 1804, at Frankfort on the Main, he was adored by Bettina

[004-017]Wallenrod, only daughter of a banker, and he married her with all the

[004-018]more enthusiasm because she was rich and a noted beauty, while he was

[004-019]only a lieutenant with no prospects but the extremely problematical

[004-020]future of a soldier of [[commerce|fortune]] of that day. Old Wallenrod, a decayed

[004-021]German baron (there is always a baron in a German bank) delighted to

[004-022]know that the handsome lieutenant was the sole representative of the

[004-023]Mignon de La Bastie, approved the [[love]] of the blonde Bettina, whose

[004-024]beauty an artist (at that time there really was one in Frankfort) had

[004-025]lately painted as an [[the-ideal|ideal]] head of Germany. Wallenrod invested enough

[004-026]money in the French funds to give his daughter thirty thousand francs a

[004-027]year, and settled it on his anticipated grandsons, naming them counts of

[004-028]La Bastie-Wallenrod. This “dot” made only a small hole in his cash-box,

[004-029]the value of money being then very low. But the Empire, pursuing a

[004-030]policy often attempted by other debtors, rarely paid its dividends; and

[004-031]Charles was rather alarmed at this investment, having less faith than

[004-032]his father-in-law in the imperial eagle. The phenomenon of belief, or of

[004-033]admiration which is ephemeral belief, is not so easily maintained when

[004-034]in close quarters with the idol. The mechanic distrusts the machine

[004-035]which the traveller admires; and the officers of the army might be

[004-036]called the stokers of the Napoleonic engine,--if, indeed, they were not

[004-037]its fuel.

[004-038]However, the Baron Wallenrod-Tustall-Bartenstild promised to come if

[004-039]necessary to the help of the household. Charles loved Bettina Wallenrod

[004-040]as much as she loved him, and that is saying a good deal; but when a

[004-041]Provencal is moved to enthusiasm all his feelings and attachments are

[004-042]genuine and natural. And how could he fail to adore that blonde beauty,

[004-043]escaping, as it were, from the canvas of Durer, gifted with an angelic

[004-044]nature and endowed with Frankfort wealth? The pair had four children, of

[004-045]whom only two daughters survived at the time when he poured his griefs

[004-046]into the Breton’s heart. Dumay loved these little ones without having

[004-047]seen them, solely through the sympathy so well described by Charlet,

[004-048]which makes a soldier the father of every child. The eldest, named

[004-049]Bettina Caroline, was born in 1805; the other, Marie [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], in 1808.

[004-050]The unfortunate lieutenant-colonel, long without tidings of these

[004-051]cherished darlings, was sent, at the peace of 1814, across Russia

[004-052]and Prussia on foot, accompanied by the lieutenant. No difference of

[004-053]epaulets could count between the two friends, who reached Frankfort just

[004-054]as Napoleon was disembarking at Cannes.

[004-055]Charles found his wife in Frankfort, in mourning for her father, who had

[004-056]always idolized her and tried to keep a smile upon her lips, even by

[004-057]his dying bed. Old Wallenrod was unable to survive the disasters of the

[004-058]Empire. At seventy years of age he speculated in cottons, relying on the

[004-059]genius of Napoleon without comprehending that genius is quite as often

[004-060]beyond as at the bottom of current events. The old man had purchased

[004-061]nearly as many bales of cotton as the Emperor had lost men during his

[004-062]magnificent campaign in France. “I tie in goddon,” said the father to

[004-063]the daughter, a father of the Goriot type, striving to quiet a grief

[004-064]which distressed him. “I owe no mann anything--” and he died, still

[004-065]trying to speak to his daughter in the language that she loved.

[004-066]Thankful to have saved his wife and daughters from the general

[004-067]wreck, Charles Mignon returned to [[paris|Paris]], where the Emperor made him

[004-068]lieutenant-colonel in the cuirassiers of the Guard and commander of the

[004-069]Legion of honor. The colonel dreamed of being count and general

[004-070]after the first victory. Alas! that hope was quenched in the blood of

[004-071]Waterloo. The colonel, slightly wounded, retired to the Loire, and left

[004-072]Tours before the disbandment of the army.

[004-073]In the spring of 1816 Charles sold his wife’s property out of the funds

[004-074]to the amount of nearly four hundred thousand francs, intending to seek

[004-075]his [[social-mobility|fortune]] in America, and abandon his own country where persecution

[004-076]was beginning to lay a heavy hand on the soldiers of Napoleon. He went

[004-077]to [[provincial-life|Havre]] accompanied by Dumay, whose life he had saved at Waterloo

[004-078]by taking him on the crupper of his saddle in the hurly-burly of the

[004-079]retreat. Dumay shared the opinions and the anxieties of his colonel; the

[004-080]poor fellow idolized the two little girls and followed Charles like

[004-081]a spaniel. The latter, confident that the habit of obedience, the

[004-082]discipline of subordination, and the honesty and affection of the

[004-083]lieutenant would make him a useful as well as a faithful retainer,

[004-084]proposed to take him with him in a civil capacity. Dumay was only too

[004-085]happy to be adopted into the family, to which he resolved to cling like

[004-086]the mistletoe to an oak.

[004-087]While waiting for an opportunity to embark, at the same time making

[004-088]choice of a ship and reflecting on the chances offered by the various

[004-089]ports for which they sailed, the colonel heard much talk about the

[004-090]brilliant future which the peace seemed to promise to Havre. As he

[004-091]listened to these conversations among the merchants, he foresaw the

[004-092]means of fortune, and without loss of time he set about making himself

[004-093]the owner of landed property, a banker, and a shipping-merchant. He

[004-094]bought land and houses in the town, and despatched a vessel to New York

[004-095]freighted with silks purchased in Lyons at reduced prices. He sent Dumay

[004-096]on the ship as his agent; and when the latter returned, after making a

[004-097]double profit by the sale of the silks and the purchase of cottons at

[004-098]a low valuation, he found the colonel installed with his family in

[004-099]the handsomest house in the rue Royale, and studying the principles of

[004-100]banking with the prodigious activity and intelligence of a native of

[004-101]Provence.

[004-102]This double operation of Dumay’s was worth a fortune to the house of

[004-103]Mignon. The colonel purchased the villa at [[ingouville|Ingouville]] and rewarded his

[004-104]agent with the gift of a modest little house in the rue Royale. The

[004-105]poor toiler had brought back from New York, together with his cottons,

[004-106]a pretty little wife, attracted it would seem by his French nature. Miss

[004-107]Grummer was worth about four thousand dollars (twenty thousand francs),

[004-108]which sum Dumay placed with his colonel, to whom he now became an alter

[004-109]ego. In a short time he learned to keep his patron’s books, a science

[004-110]which, to use his own expression, pertains to the sergeant-majors of

[004-111]commerce. The simple-hearted soldier, whom fortune had forgotten for

[004-112]twenty years, thought himself the happiest man in the world as the owner

[004-113]of the little house (which his master’s liberality had furnished), with

[004-114]twelve hundred francs a year from money in the funds, and a salary of

[004-115]three thousand six hundred. Never in his dreams had Lieutenant Dumay

[004-116]hoped for a situation so good as this; but greater still was the

[004-117]satisfaction he derived from the knowledge that his lucky enterprise had

[004-118]been the pivot of good fortune to the richest commercial house in Havre.

[004-119]Madame Dumay, a rather pretty little American, had the misfortune to

[004-120]lose all her children at their birth; and her last confinement was so

[004-121]disastrous as to deprive her of the hope of any other. She therefore

[004-122]attached herself to the two little Mignons, whom Dumay himself loved,

[004-123]or would have loved, even better than his own children had they lived.

[004-124]Madame Dumay, whose parents were farmers accustomed to a life of

[004-125]economy, was quite satisfied to receive only two thousand four hundred

[004-126]francs of her own and her household expenses; so that every year Dumay

[004-127]laid by two thousand and some extra hundreds with the house of Mignon.

[004-128]When the yearly accounts were made up the colonel always added something

[004-129]to this little store by way of acknowledging the cashier’s services,

[004-130]until in 1824 the latter had a credit of fifty-eight thousand francs. In

[004-131]was then that Charles Mignon, Comte de La Bastie, a title he never used,

[004-132]crowned his cashier with the final happiness of residing at [[chalet|the Chalet]],

[004-133]where at the time when this story begins Madame Mignon and her daughter

[004-134]were living in obscurity.

[004-135]The deplorable state of Madame Mignon’s health was caused in part by the

[004-136]catastrophe to which the absence of her husband was due. Grief had taken

[004-137]three years to break down the docile German woman; but it was a grief

[004-138]that gnawed at her heart like a worm at the core of a sound fruit. It

[004-139]is easy to reckon up its obvious causes. Two children, dying in infancy,

[004-140]had a double grave in a soul that could never forget. The exile of her

[004-141]husband to Siberia was to such a woman a daily death. The failure of

[004-142]the rich house of Wallenrod, and the death of her father, leaving his

[004-143]coffers empty, was to Bettina, then uncertain about the fate of her

[004-144]husband, a terrible blow. The joy of Charles’s return came near killing

[004-145]the tender German flower. After that the second fall of the Empire and

[004-146]the proposed expatriation acted on her feelings like a renewed attack

[004-147]of the same fever. At last, however, after ten years of continual

[004-148]prosperity, the comforts of her house, which was the finest in Havre,

[004-149]the dinners, balls, and fetes of a prosperous merchant, the splendors of

[004-150]the villa Mignon, the unbounded respect and consideration enjoyed by her

[004-151]husband, his absolute affection, giving her an unrivalled love in return

[004-152]for her single-minded love for him,--all these things brought the woman

[004-153]back to life. At the moment when her doubts and fears at last left her,

[004-154]when she could look forward to the bright evening of her stormy life, a

[004-155]hidden catastrophe, buried in the heart of the family, and of which we

[004-156]shall presently make mention, came as the precursor of renewed trials.

[004-157]In January, 1826, on the day when Havre had unanimously chosen Charles

[004-158]Mignon as its deputy, three [[epistolary-novel|letters]], arriving from New York, Paris, and

[004-159]London, fell with the destruction of a hammer upon the crystal palace

[004-160]of his prosperity. In an instant ruin like a vulture swooped down upon

[004-161]their happiness, just as the cold fell in 1812 upon the grand army in

[004-162]Russia. One night sufficed Charles Mignon to decide upon his course,

[004-163]and he spent it in settling his accounts with Dumay. All he owned, not

[004-164]excepting his furniture, would just suffice to pay his creditors.

[004-165]“Havre shall never see me doing nothing,” said the colonel to the

[004-166]lieutenant. “Dumay, I take your sixty thousand francs at six per cent.”

[004-167]“Three, my colonel.”

[004-168]“At nothing, then,” cried Mignon, peremptorily; “you shall have your

[004-169]share in the profits of what I now undertake. The ‘Modeste,’ which is no

[004-170]longer mine, sails to-morrow, and I sail in her. I commit to you my wife

[004-171]and daughter. I shall not write. No news must be taken as good news.”
