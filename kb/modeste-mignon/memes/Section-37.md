# Section 37

[037-001]“Monsieur knows my attachment to him,” said Germain, perceiving that the

[037-002][[poetry|poet]] was quite thrown off his balance; “he will not be surprised if I

[037-003]give him a word of advice. There is that clerk; try to get the truth out

[037-004]of him. Perhaps he’ll unbutton after a bottle or two of champagne, or at

[037-005]any rate a third. It would be strange indeed if monsieur, who will one

[037-006]day be ambassador, as Philoxene has heard Madame la duchesse say time

[037-007]and time again, couldn’t turn a little notary’s clerk inside out.”

[037-008]CHAPTER XXIII. BUTSCHA DISTINGUISHES HIMSELF

[037-009]At this instant Butscha, the hidden prompter of the fishing part, was

[037-010]requesting the secretary to say nothing about his trip to [[paris|Paris]], and not

[037-011]to interfere in any way with what he, Butscha, might do. The dwarf

[037-012]had already made use of an unfavorable feeling lately roused against

[037-013]Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] in [[provincial-life|Havre]] in consequence of his reserve and his

[037-014]determination to keep silence as to the amount of his [[commerce|fortune]]. The

[037-015]persons who were most bitter against him even declared calumniously that

[037-016]he had made over a large amount of property to [[dumay|Dumay]] to save it from the

[037-017]just demands of his associates in China. Butscha took advantage of this

[037-018]state of feeling. He asked the fishermen, who owed him many a good turn,

[037-019]to keep the secret and lend him their tongues. They served him well.

[037-020]The captain of the fishing-smack told Germain that one of his cousins,

[037-021]a sailor, had just returned from Marseilles, where he had been paid off

[037-022]from the brig in which Monsieur Mignon returned to France. The brig had

[037-023]been sold to the account of some other person than Monsieur Mignon, and

[037-024]the cargo was only worth three or four hundred thousand francs at the

[037-025]utmost.

[037-026]“Germain,” said [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], as the valet was leaving the room, “serve

[037-027]champagne and claret. A member of the legal fraternity of Havre must

[037-028]carry away with him proper ideas of a poet’s hospitality. Besides, he

[037-029]has got a wit that is equal to Figaro’s,” added Canalis, laying his

[037-030]hand on the dwarf’s shoulder, “and we must make it foam and sparkle with

[037-031]champagne; you and I, [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], will not spare the bottle either. Faith,

[037-032]it is over two years since I’ve been drunk,” he added, looking at La

[037-033]Briere.

[037-034]“Not drunk with wine, you mean,” said Butscha, looking keenly at him,

[037-035]“yes, I can believe that. You get drunk every day on yourself, you drink

[037-036]in so much praise. Ha, you are handsome, you are a poet, you are famous

[037-037]in your lifetime, you have the gift of an eloquence that is equal to

[037-038]your genius, and you please all women,--even my master’s wife. Admired

[037-039]by the finest sultana-valide that I ever saw in my life (and I never

[037-040]saw but her) you can, if you choose, marry Mademoiselle de La Bastie.

[037-041]Goodness! the mere inventory of your present advantages, not to speak

[037-042]of the future (a noble title, peerage, embassy!), is enough to make me

[037-043]drunk already,--like the men who bottle other men’s wine.”

[037-044]“All such social distinctions,” said Canalis, “are of little use without

[037-045]the one thing that gives them value,--wealth. Here we can talk as men

[037-046]with men; fine sentiments only do in verse.”

[037-047]“That depends on circumstances,” said the dwarf, with a knowing gesture.

[037-048]“Ah! you writer of conveyances,” said the poet, smiling at the

[037-049]interruption, “you know as well as I do that ‘cottage’ rhymes with

[037-050]‘pottage,’--and who would like to live on that for the rest of his

[037-051]days?”

[037-052]At table Butscha played the part of Trigaudin, in the “Maison en

[037-053]loterie,” in a way that alarmed Ernest, who did not know the waggery of

[037-054]a lawyer’s office, which is quite equal to that of an atelier. Butscha

[037-055]poured forth the scandalous gossip of Havre, the private history of

[037-056][[social-mobility|fortune]] and boudoirs, and the crimes committed code in hand, which are

[037-057]called in Normandy, “getting out of a thing as best you can.” He spared

[037-058]no one; and his liveliness increased with the torrents of wine which

[037-059]poured down his throat like rain through a gutter.

[037-060]“Do you know, La Briere,” said Canalis, filling Butscha’s glass, “that

[037-061]this fellow would make a capital secretary to the embassy?”

[037-062]“And oust his chief!” cried the dwarf flinging a look at Canalis whose

[037-063]insolence was lost in the gurgling of carbonic acid gas. “I’ve little

[037-064]enough gratitude and quite enough scheming to get astride of your

[037-065]shoulders. Ha, ha, a poet carrying a hunchback! that’s been seen, often

[037-066]seen--on book-shelves. Come, don’t look at me as if I were swallowing

[037-067]swords. My dear great genius, you’re a superior man; you know that

[037-068]gratitude is the word of fools; they stick it in the dictionary, but it

[037-069]isn’t in the human heart; pledges are worth nothing, except on a certain

[037-070]mount that is neither Pindus nor Parnassus. You think I owe a great deal

[037-071]to my master’s wife, who brought me up. Bless you, the whole town has

[037-072]paid her for that in praises, respect, and admiration,--the very best

[037-073]of coin. I don’t recognize any service that is only the capital of

[037-074]self-[[love]]. Men make a commerce of their services, and gratitude

[037-075]goes down on the debit side,--that’s all. As to schemes, they are my

[037-076]divinity. What?” he exclaimed, at a gesture of Canalis, “don’t you

[037-077]admire the faculty which enables a wily man to get the better of a

[037-078]man of genius? it takes the closest observation of his vices, and his

[037-079]weaknesses, and the wit to seize the happy moment. Ask diplomacy if

[037-080]its greatest triumphs are not those of craft over force? If I were your

[037-081]secretary, Monsieur le baron, you’d soon be prime-minister, because

[037-082]it would be my interest to have you so. Do you want a specimen of my

[037-083]talents in that line? Well then, listen; you love [[modeste-mignon|Mademoiselle Modeste]]

[037-084]distractedly, and you’ve good reason to do so. The girl has my fullest

[037-085]esteem; she is a true Parisian. Sometimes we get a few real Parisians

[037-086]born down here in the provinces. Well, Modeste is just the woman to help

[037-087]a man’s career. She’s got _that_ in her,” he cried, with a turn of his

[037-088]wrist in the air. “But you’ve a dangerous competitor in the duke; what

[037-089]will you give me to get him out of Havre within three days?”

[037-090]“Finish this bottle,” said the poet, refilling Butscha’s glass.

[037-091]“You’ll make me drunk,” said the dwarf, tossing off his ninth glass of

[037-092]champagne. “Have you a bed where I could sleep it off? My master is

[037-093]as sober as the camel that he is, and Madame Latournelle too. They are

[037-094]brutal enough, both of them, to scold me; and they’d have the rights of

[037-095]it too--there are those deeds I ought to be drawing!--” Then, suddenly

[037-096]returning to his previous ideas, after the fashion of a drunken man, he

[037-097]exclaimed, “and I’ve such a memory; it is on a par with my gratitude.”

[037-098]“Butscha!” cried the poet, “you said just now you had no gratitude; you

[037-099]contradict yourself.”

[037-100]“Not at all,” he replied. “To forget a thing means almost always

[037-101]recollecting it. Come, come, do you want me to get rid of the duke? I’m

[037-102]cut out for a secretary.”

[037-103]“How could you manage it?” said Canalis, delighted to find the

[037-104]conversation taking this turn of its own accord.

[037-105]“That’s none of your business,” said the dwarf, with a portentous

[037-106]hiccough.

[037-107]Butscha’s head rolled between his shoulders, and his eyes turned from

[037-108]Germain to La Briere, and from La Briere to Canalis, after the manner of

[037-109]men who, knowing they are tipsy, wish to see what other men are thinking

[037-110]of them; for in the shipwreck of drunkenness it is noticeable that

[037-111]self-love is the last thing that goes to the bottom.

[037-112]“Ha! my great poet, you’re a pretty good trickster yourself; but you

[037-113]are not deep enough. What do you mean by taking me for one of your own

[037-114]readers,--you who sent your friend to Paris, full gallop, to inquire

[037-115]into the property of the Mignon family? Ha, ha! I hoax, thou hoaxest, we

[037-116]hoax--Good! But do me the [[honor]] to believe that I’m deep enough to keep

[037-117]the secrets of my own business. As the head-clerk of a notary, my heart

[037-118]is a locked box, padlocked! My mouth never opens to let out anything

[037-119]about a client. I know all, and I know nothing. Besides, my passion is

[037-120]well known. I love Modeste; she is my pupil, and she must make a good

[037-121][[marriage]]. I’ll fool the duke, if need be; and you shall marry--”

[037-122]“Germain, coffee and liqueurs,” said Canalis.

[037-123]“Liqueurs!” repeated Butscha with a wave of his hand, and the air of a

[037-124]sham virgin repelling seduction; “Ah, those poor deeds! one of ‘em was a

[037-125]marriage contract; and that second clerk of mine is as stupid as--as--an

[037-126]epithalamium, and he’s capable of digging his penknife right through the

[037-127]bride’s paraphernalia; he thinks he’s a handsome man because he’s five

[037-128]feet six,--idiot!”

[037-129]“Here is some creme de the, a liqueur of the West Indies,” said Canalis.

[037-130]“You, whom Mademoiselle Modeste consults--”

[037-131]“Yes, she consults me.”

[037-132]“Well, do you think she loves me?” asked the poet.

[037-133]“Loves you? yes, more than she loves the duke,” answered the dwarf,

[037-134]rousing himself from a stupor which was admirably played. “She loves

[037-135]you for your disinterestedness. She told me she was ready to make the

[037-136]greatest sacrifices for your sake; to give up dress and spend as little

[037-137]as possible on herself, and devote her life to showing you that in

[037-138]marrying her you hadn’t done so” (hiccough) “bad a thing for yourself.

[037-139]She’s as right as a trivet,--yes, and well informed. She knows

[037-140]everything, that girl.”

[037-141]“And she has three hundred thousand francs?”

[037-142]“There may be quite as much as that,” cried the dwarf, enthusiastically.

[037-143]“Papa Mignon,--mignon by name, mignon by nature, and that’s why I

[037-144]respect him,--well, he would rob himself of everything to marry his

[037-145]daughter. Your Restoration” (hiccough) “has taught him how to live on

[037-146]half-pay; he’d be quite content to live with Dumay on next to nothing,

[037-147]if he could rake and scrape enough together to give the little one three

[037-148]hundred thousand francs. But don’t let’s forget that Dumay is going to

[037-149]leave all his money to Modeste. Dumay, you know, is a Breton, and

[037-150]that fact clinches the matter; he won’t go back from his word, and

[037-151]his fortune is equal to the colonel’s. But I don’t approve of Monsieur

[037-152]Mignon’s taking back that villa, and, as they often ask my advice, I

[037-153]told them so. ‘You sink too much in it,’ I said; ‘if Vilquin does not

[037-154]buy it back there’s two hundred thousand francs which won’t bring you a

[037-155]penny; it only leaves you a hundred thousand to get along with, and it

[037-156]isn’t enough.’ The colonel and Dumay are consulting about it now. But

[037-157]nevertheless, between you and me, Modeste is sure to be rich. I hear

[037-158]talk on the quays against it; but that’s all nonsense; people are

[037-159]jealous. Why, there’s no such ‘dot’ in Havre,” cried Butscha, beginning

[037-160]to count on his fingers. “Two to three hundred thousand in ready money,”

[037-161]bending back the thumb of his left hand with the forefinger of his

[037-162]right, “that’s one item; the reversion of the villa Mignon, that’s

[037-163]another; ‘tertio,’ Dumay’s property!” doubling down his middle finger.

[037-164]“Ha! little Modeste may count upon her six hundred thousand francs

[037-165]as soon as the two old soldiers have got their marching orders for

[037-166]eternity.”

[037-167]This coarse and candid statement, intermingled with a variety of

[037-168]liqueurs, sobered Canalis as much as it appeared to befuddle Butscha.

[037-169]To the latter, a young provincial, such a fortune must of course seem

[037-170]colossal. He let his head fall into the palm of his right hand, and

[037-171]putting his elbows majestically on the table, blinked his eyes and

[037-172]continued talking to himself:--
