# Section 32

[032-001]The following evening, therefore, [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] was to see all three of her

[032-002]lovers. No matter what young girls may say, and though the logic of

[032-003]the heart may lead them to sacrifice everything to preference, it is

[032-004]extremely flattering to their self-[[love]] to see a number of rival

[032-005]adorers around them,--distinguished or celebrated men, or men of ancient

[032-006]lineage,--all endeavoring to shine and to please. Suffer as Modeste may

[032-007]in general estimation, it must be told she subsequently admitted that

[032-008]the sentiments expressed in her [[epistolary-novel|letters]] paled before the pleasure of

[032-009]seeing three such different minds at war with one another,--three men

[032-010]who, taken separately, would each have done [[honor]] to the most exacting

[032-011]family. Yet this luxury of self-love was checked by a misanthropical

[032-012]spitefulness, resulting from the terrible wound she had

[032-013]received,--although by this time she was beginning to think of that

[032-014]wound as a disappointment only. So when her father said to her,

[032-015]laughing, “Well, Modeste, do you want to be a duchess?” she answered,

[032-016]with a mocking curtsey,--

[032-017]“Sorrows have made me philosophical.”

[032-018]“Do you mean to be only a baroness?” asked Butscha.

[032-019]“Or a viscountess?” said her father.

[032-020]“How could that be?” she asked quickly.

[032-021]“If you accept Monsieur de La Briere, he has enough merit and influence

[032-022]to obtain permission from the king to bear my titles and arms.”

[032-023]“Oh, if it comes to disguising himself, _he_ will not make any

[032-024]difficulty,” said Modeste, scornfully.

[032-025]Butscha did not understand this epigram, whose meaning could only be

[032-026]guessed by Monsieur and Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] and [[dumay|Dumay]].

[032-027]“When it is a question of [[marriage]], all men disguise themselves,”

[032-028]remarked Latournelle, “and women set them the example. I’ve heard

[032-029]it said ever since I came into the world that ‘Monsieur this or

[032-030]Mademoiselle that has made a good marriage,’--meaning that the other

[032-031]side had made a bad one.”

[032-032]“Marriage,” said Butscha, “is like a lawsuit; there’s always one side

[032-033]discontented. If one dupes the other, certainly half the husbands in the

[032-034]world are playing a comedy at the expense of the other half.”

[032-035]“From which you conclude, Sieur Butscha?” inquired Modeste.

[032-036]“To pay the utmost attention to the manoeuvres of the enemy,” answered

[032-037]the clerk.

[032-038]“What did I tell you, my darling?” said Charles Mignon, alluding to

[032-039]their conversation on the seashore.

[032-040]“Men play as many parts to get married as mothers make their daughters

[032-041]play to get rid of them,” said Latournelle.

[032-042]“Then you approve of stratagems?” said Modeste.

[032-043]“On both sides,” cried Gobenheim, “and that brings it even.”

[032-044]This conversation was carried on by fits and starts, as they say, in the

[032-045]intervals of cutting and dealing the cards; and it soon turned chiefly

[032-046]on the merits of the Duc d’Herouville, who was thought very good-looking

[032-047]by little Latournelle, little Dumay, and little Butscha. Without the

[032-048]foregoing discussion on the lawfulness of matrimonial tricks, the

[032-049]reader might possibly find the forthcoming account of the evening so

[032-050]impatiently awaited by Butscha, somewhat too long.

[032-051]Desplein, the famous surgeon, arrived the next morning, and stayed only

[032-052]long enough to send to [[provincial-life|Havre]] for fresh horses and have them put-to,

[032-053]which took about an hour. After examining Madame Mignon’s eyes, he

[032-054]decided that she could recover her sight, and fixed a suitable time, a

[032-055]month later, to perform the operation. This important consultation took

[032-056]place before the assembled members of [[chalet|the Chalet]], who stood trembling

[032-057]and expectant to hear the verdict of the prince of science. That

[032-058]illustrious member of the Academy of Sciences put about a dozen brief

[032-059]questions to the blind woman as he examined her eyes in the strong light

[032-060]from a window. Modeste was amazed at the value which a man so celebrated

[032-061]attached to time, when she saw the travelling-carriage piled with books

[032-062]which the great surgeon proposed to read during the journey; for he had

[032-063]left [[paris|Paris]] the evening before, and had spent the night in sleeping and

[032-064]travelling. The rapidity and clearness of Desplein’s judgment on each

[032-065]answer made by Madame Mignon, his succinct tone, his decisive manner,

[032-066]gave Modeste her first real idea of a man of genius. She perceived

[032-067]the enormous difference between a second-rate man, like [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], and

[032-068]Desplein, who was even more than a superior man. A man of genius finds

[032-069]in the consciousness of his talent and in the solidity of his fame an

[032-070]arena of his own, where his legitimate pride can expand and exercise

[032-071]itself without interfering with others. Moreover, his perpetual struggle

[032-072]with men and things leave them no time for the coxcombry of fashionable

[032-073]genius, which makes haste to gather in the harvests of a fugitive

[032-074]season, and whose vanity and self-love are as petty and exacting as a

[032-075]custom-house which levies tithes on all that comes in its way.

[032-076]Modeste was the more enchanted by this great practical genius, because

[032-077]he was evidently charmed with the exquisite beauty of Modeste,--he,

[032-078]through whose hands so many women had passed, and who had long since

[032-079]examined the sex, as it were, with magnifier and scalpel.

[032-080]“It would be a sad pity,” he said, with an air of gallantry which he

[032-081]occasionally put on, and which contrasted with his assumed brusqueness,

[032-082]“if a mother were deprived of the sight of so charming a daughter.”

[032-083]Modeste insisted on serving the simple breakfast which was all the

[032-084]great surgeon would accept. She accompanied her father and Dumay to the

[032-085]carriage stationed at the garden-gate, and said to Desplein at parting,

[032-086]her eyes shining with hope,--

[032-087]“And will my dear mamma really see me?”

[032-088]“Yes, my little sprite, I’ll promise you that,” he answered, smiling;

[032-089]“and I am incapable of deceiving you, for I, too, have a daughter.”

[032-090]The horses started and carried him off as he uttered the last words with

[032-091]unexpected grace and feeling. Nothing is more charming than the peculiar

[032-092]unexpectedness of persons of talent.

[032-093]CHAPTER XX. THE POET DOES HIS EXERCISES

[032-094]This visit of the great surgeon was the event of the day, and it left a

[032-095]luminous trace in Modeste’s soul. The young enthusiast ardently admired

[032-096]the man whose life belonged to others, and in whom the habit of studying

[032-097]physical suffering had destroyed the manifestations of egoism. That

[032-098]evening, when Gobenheim, the Latournelles, and Butscha, Canalis,

[032-099][[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], and the Duc d’Herouville were gathered in the salon, they all

[032-100]congratulated the Mignon family on the hopes which Desplein encouraged.

[032-101]The conversation, in which the Modeste of her letters was once more in

[032-102]the ascendant, turned naturally on the man whose genius, unfortunately

[032-103]for his fame, was appreciable only by the faculty and men of science.

[032-104]Gobenheim contributed a phrase which is the sacred chrism of genius as

[032-105]interpreted in these days by public economists and bankers,--

[032-106]“He makes a mint of money.”

[032-107]“They say he is very grasping,” added Canalis.

[032-108]The praises which Modeste showered on Desplein had annoyed the [[poetry|poet]].

[032-109]Vanity acts like a woman,--they both think they are defrauded when love

[032-110]or praise is bestowed on others. Voltaire was jealous of the wit of a

[032-111]roue whom Paris admired for two days; and even a duchess takes offence

[032-112]at a look bestowed upon her maid. The avarice excited by these two

[032-113]sentiments is such that a fraction of them given to the poor is thought

[032-114]robbery.

[032-115]“Do you think, monsieur,” said Modeste, smiling, “that we should judge

[032-116]genius by ordinary standards?”

[032-117]“Perhaps we ought first of all to define the man of genius,” replied

[032-118]Canalis. “One of the conditions of genius is invention,--invention of a

[032-119]form, a system, a force. Napoleon was an inventor, apart from his other

[032-120]conditions of genius. He invented his method of making war. Walter Scott

[032-121]is an inventor, Linnaeus is an inventor, Geoffrey Saint-Hilaire and

[032-122]Cuvier are inventors. Such men are men of genius of the first rank. They

[032-123]renew, increase, or modify both science and art. But Desplein is merely

[032-124]a man whose vast talent consists in properly applying laws already

[032-125]known; in observing, by means of a natural gift, the limits laid down

[032-126]for each temperament, and the time appointed by Nature for an operation.

[032-127]He has not founded, like Hippocrates, the science itself. He has

[032-128]invented no system, as did Galen, Broussais, and Rasori. He is merely an

[032-129]executive genius, like Moscheles on the piano, Paganini on the violin,

[032-130]or Farinelli on his own larynx,--men who have developed enormous

[032-131]faculties, but who have not created [[music]]. You must permit me to

[032-132]discriminate between Beethoven and la Catalani: to one belongs the

[032-133]immortal crown of genius and of martyrdom, to the other innumerable

[032-134]five-franc pieces; one we can pay in coin, but the world remains

[032-135]throughout all time a debtor to the other. Each day increases our debt

[032-136]to Moliere, but Baron’s comedies have been overpaid.”

[032-137]“I think you make the prerogative of ideas too exclusive,” said Ernest

[032-138]de La Briere, in a quiet and melodious voice, which formed a sudden

[032-139]contrast to the peremptory tones of the poet, whose flexible organ had

[032-140]abandoned its caressing notes for the strident and magisterial voice

[032-141]of the rostrum. “Genius must be estimated according to its utility;

[032-142]and Parmentier, who brought potatoes into general use, Jacquart, the

[032-143]inventor of silk looms; Papin, who first discovered the elastic quality

[032-144]of steam, are men of genius, to whom statues will some day be erected.

[032-145]They have changed, or they will change in a certain sense, the face of

[032-146]the State. It is in that sense that Desplein will always be considered

[032-147]a man of genius by thinkers; they see him attended by a generation of

[032-148]sufferers whose pains are stifled by his hand.”

[032-149]That Ernest should give utterance to this opinion was enough to make

[032-150]Modeste oppose it.

[032-151]“If that be so, monsieur,” she said, “then the man who could discover a

[032-152]way to mow wheat without injuring the straw, by a machine that could do

[032-153]the work of ten men, would be a man of genius.”

[032-154]“Yes, my daughter,” said Madame Mignon; “and the poor would bless him

[032-155]for cheaper bread,--he that is blessed by the poor is blessed of God.”

[032-156]“That is putting utility above art,” said Modeste, shaking her head.

[032-157]“Without utility what would become of art?” said Charles Mignon. “What

[032-158]would it rest on? what would it live on? Where would you lodge, and how

[032-159]would you pay the poet?”

[032-160]“Oh! my dear papa, such opinions are fearfully flat and antediluvian!

[032-161]I am not surprised that Gobenheim and Monsieur de La Briere, who are

[032-162]interested in the solution of social problems should think so; but you,

[032-163]whose life has been the most useless poetry of the century,--useless

[032-164]because the blood you shed all over Europe, and the horrible sufferings

[032-165]exacted by your colossus, did not prevent France from losing ten

[032-166]departments acquired under the Revolution,--how can _you_ give in to

[032-167]such excessively pig-tail notions, as the idealists say? It is plain

[032-168]you’ve just come from China.”

[032-169]The impertinence of Modeste’s speech was heightened by a little air

[032-170]of contemptuous disdain which she purposely put on, and which fairly

[032-171]astounded Madame Mignon, Madame Latournelle, and Dumay. As for Madame

[032-172]Latournelle, she opened her eyes so wide she no longer saw anything.

[032-173]Butscha, whose alert attention was comparable to that of a spy, looked

[032-174]at Monsieur Mignon, expecting to see him flush with sudden and violent

[032-175]indignation.

[032-176]“A little more, young lady, and you will be wanting in respect for your

[032-177]father,” said the colonel, smiling, and noticing Butscha’s look. “See

[032-178]what it is to spoil one’s children!”

[032-179]“I am your only child,” she said saucily.

[032-180]“Child, indeed,” remarked the notary, significantly.

[032-181]“Monsieur,” said Modeste, turning upon him, “my father is delighted to

[032-182]have me for his governess; he gave me life and I give him knowledge; he

[032-183]will soon owe me something.”

[032-184]“There seems occasion for it,” said Madame Mignon.

[032-185]“But mademoiselle is right,” said Canalis, rising and standing before

[032-186]the fireplace in one of the finest attitudes of his collection. “God,

[032-187]in his providence, has given food and clothing to man, but he has not

[032-188]directly given him art. He says to man: ‘To live, thou must bow thyself

[032-189]to earth; to think, thou shalt lift thyself to Me.’ We have as much need

[032-190]of the life of the soul as of the life of the body,--hence, there are

[032-191]two utilities. It is true we cannot be shod by books or clothed by

[032-192]poems. An epic song is not, if you take the utilitarian view, as useful

[032-193]as the broth of a charity kitchen. The noblest ideas will not sail a

[032-194]vessel in place of canvas. It is quite true that the cotton-gin gives us

[032-195]calicoes for thirty sous a yard less than we ever paid before; but that

[032-196]machine and all other industrial perfections will not breathe the breath

[032-197]of life into a people, will not tell futurity of a civilization that

[032-198]once existed. Art, on the contrary, Egyptian, Mexican, Grecian, Roman

[032-199]art, with their masterpieces--now called useless!--reveal the existence

[032-200]of races back in the vague immense of time, beyond where the great

[032-201]intermediary nations, denuded of men of genius, have disappeared,

[032-202]leaving not a line nor a trace behind them! The works of genius are the

[032-203]‘summum’ of civilization, and presuppose utility. Surely a pair of boots

[032-204]are not as agreeable to your eyes as a fine play at the theatre; and you

[032-205]don’t prefer a windmill to the church of Saint-Ouen, do you? Well then,

[032-206]nations are imbued with the same feelings as the individual man, and

[032-207]the man’s cherished desire is to survive himself morally just as he

[032-208]propagates himself physically. The survival of a people is the work of

[032-209]its men of genius. At this very moment France is proving, energetically,

[032-210]the truth of that theory. She is, undoubtedly, excelled by England in

[032-211][[commerce]], industry, and navigation, and yet she is, I believe, at the

[032-212]head of the world,--by reason of her artists, her men of talent, and the

[032-213]good taste of her products. There is no artist and no superior intellect

[032-214]that does not come to Paris for a diploma. There is no school of

[032-215]painting at this moment but that of France; and we shall reign far

[032-216]longer and perhaps more securely by our books than by our swords. In La

[032-217]Briere’s system, on the other hand, all that is glorious and lovely must

[032-218]be suppressed,--woman’s beauty, music, painting, poetry. Society will

[032-219]not be overthrown, that is true, but, I ask you, who would willingly

[032-220]accept such a life? All useful things are ugly and forbidding. A kitchen

[032-221]is indispensable, but you take care not to sit there; you live in the

[032-222]salon, which you adorn, like this, with superfluous things. Of what

[032-223]_use_, let me ask you, are these charming wall-paintings, this carved

[032-224]wood-work? There is nothing beautiful but that which seems to us

[032-225]useless. We called the sixteenth century the Renascence with admirable

[032-226]truth of language. That century was the dawn of a new era. Men will

[032-227]continue to speak of it when all remembrance of anterior centuries had

[032-228]passed away,--their only merit being that they once existed, like the

[032-229]million beings who count as the rubbish of a generation.”
