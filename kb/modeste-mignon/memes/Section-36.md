# Section 36

[036-001]“Then I flatter you?” said the young man, looking up at her with a face

[036-002]that was illuminated like a city for a festival.

[036-003]“You?” she said; “you give me the most precious of all friendships,--a

[036-004]feeling as disinterested as that of a mother for her child. Compare

[036-005]yourself to no one; for even my father is obliged to be devoted to me.”

[036-006]She paused. “I cannot say that I [[love]] you, in the sense which men give

[036-007]to that word, but what I do give you is eternal and can know no change.”

[036-008]“Then,” said Butscha, stooping to pick up a pebble that he might kiss

[036-009]the hem of her garment, “suffer me to watch over you as a dragon guards

[036-010]a treasure. The [[poetry|poet]] was covering you just now with the lace-work of his

[036-011]precious phrases, the tinsel of his promises; he chanted his love on the

[036-012]best strings of his lyre, I know he did. If, as soon as this noble lover

[036-013]finds out how small your [[commerce|fortune]] is, he makes a sudden change in his

[036-014]behavior, and is cold and embarrassed, will you still marry him? shall

[036-015]you still esteem him?”

[036-016]“He would be another Francisque Althor,” she said, with a gesture of

[036-017]bitter disgust.

[036-018]“Let me have the pleasure of producing that change of scene,” said

[036-019]Butscha. “Not only shall it be sudden, but I believe I can change it

[036-020]back and make your poet as loving as before,--nay, it is possible

[036-021]to make him blow alternately hot and cold upon your heart, just as

[036-022]gracefully as he has talked on both sides of an argument in one evening

[036-023]without ever finding it out.”

[036-024]“If you are right,” she said, “who can be trusted?”

[036-025]“One who truly loves you.”

[036-026]“The little duke?”

[036-027]Butscha looked at [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]. The pair walked some distance in silence; the

[036-028]girl was impenetrable and not an eyelash quivered.

[036-029]“Mademoiselle, permit me to be the exponent of the thoughts that are

[036-030]lying at the bottom of your heart like sea-mosses under the waves, and

[036-031]which you do not choose to gather up.”

[036-032]“Eh!” said Modeste, “so my intimate friend and counsellor thinks himself

[036-033]a mirror, does he?”

[036-034]“No, an echo,” he answered, with a gesture of sublime humility.

[036-035]“The duke loves you, but he loves you too much. If I, a dwarf, have

[036-036]understood the infinite delicacy of your heart, it would be repugnant

[036-037]to you to be worshipped like a saint in her shrine. You are eminently a

[036-038]woman; you neither want a man perpetually at your feet of whom you

[036-039]are eternally sure, nor a selfish egoist like [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], who will always

[036-040]prefer himself to you. Why? ah, that I don’t know. But I will make

[036-041]myself a woman, an old woman, and find out the meaning of the plan which

[036-042]I have read in your eyes, and which perhaps is in the heart of every

[036-043]girl. Nevertheless, in your great soul you feel the need of worshipping.

[036-044]When a man is at your knees, you cannot put yourself at his. You can’t

[036-045]advance in that way, as Voltaire might say. The little duke has too many

[036-046]genuflections in his moral being and the poet has too few,--indeed, I

[036-047]might say, none at all. Ha, I have guessed the mischief in your smiles

[036-048]when you talk to the grand equerry, and when he talks to you and you

[036-049]answer him. You would never be unhappy with the duke, and everybody will

[036-050]approve your choice, if you do choose him; but you will never love

[036-051]him. The ice of egotism, and the burning heat of ecstasy both produce

[036-052]indifference in the heart of every woman. It is evident to my mind that

[036-053]no such perpetual worship will give you the infinite delights which you

[036-054]are dreaming of in [[marriage]],--in some marriage where obedience will be

[036-055]your pride, where noble little sacrifices can be made and hidden,

[036-056]where the heart is full of anxieties without a cause, and successes are

[036-057]awaited with eager hope, where each new chance for magnanimity is hailed

[036-058]with joy, where souls are comprehended to their inmost recesses, and

[036-059]where the woman protects with her love the man who protects her.”

[036-060]“You are a sorcerer!” exclaimed Modeste.

[036-061]“Neither will you find that sweet equality of feeling, that continual

[036-062]sharing of each other’s life, that certainty of pleasing which makes

[036-063]marriage tolerable, if you take Canalis,--a man who thinks of himself

[036-064]only, whose ‘I’ is the one string to his lute, whose mind is so fixed

[036-065]on himself that he has hitherto taken no notice of your father or the

[036-066]duke,--a man of second-rate ambitions, to whom your dignity and your

[036-067]devotion will matter nothing, who will make you a mere appendage to

[036-068]his household, and who already insults you by his indifference to your

[036-069]behavior; yes, if you permitted yourself to go so far as to box your

[036-070]mother’s ears Canalis would shut his eyes to it, and deny your

[036-071]crime even to himself, because he thirsts for your money. And so,

[036-072]mademoiselle, when I spoke of the man who truly loves you I was not

[036-073]thinking of the great poet who is nothing but a little comedian, nor of

[036-074]the duke, who might be a good marriage for you, but never a husband--”

[036-075]“Butscha, my heart is a blank page on which you are yourself writing all

[036-076]that you read there,” cried Modeste, interrupting him. “You are carried

[036-077]away by your [[provincial-life|provincial]] hatred for everything that obliges you to

[036-078]look higher than your own head. You can’t forgive a poet for being a

[036-079]statesman, for possessing the gift of speech, for having a noble future

[036-080]before him,--and you calumniate his intentions.”

[036-081]“His!--mademoiselle, he will turn his back upon you with the baseness of

[036-082]an Althor.”

[036-083]“Make him play that pretty little comedy, and--”

[036-084]“That I will! he shall play it through and through within three

[036-085]days,--on Wednesday,--recollect, Wednesday! Until then, mademoiselle,

[036-086]amuse yourself by listening to the little tunes of the lyre, so that the

[036-087]discords and the false notes may come out all the more distinctly.”

[036-088]Modeste ran gaily back to the salon, where La Briere, who was sitting by

[036-089]the window, where he had doubtless been watching his idol, rose to his

[036-090]feet as if a groom of the chambers had suddenly announced, “The Queen.”

[036-091]It was a movement of spontaneous respect, full of that living eloquence

[036-092]that lies in gesture even more than in speech. Spoken love cannot

[036-093]compare with acts of love; and every young girl of twenty has the

[036-094]wisdom of fifty in applying the axiom. In it lies the great secret of

[036-095]attraction. Instead of looking Modeste in the face, as Canalis who paid

[036-096]her public homage would have done, the neglected lover followed her with

[036-097]a furtive look between his eyelids, humble after the manner of Butscha,

[036-098]and almost timid. The young heiress observed it, as she took her place

[036-099]by Canalis, to whose game she proceeded to pay attention. During a

[036-100]conversation which ensued, La Briere heard Modeste say to her father

[036-101]that she should ride out for the first time on the following Wednesday;

[036-102]and she also reminded him that she had no whip in keeping with her new

[036-103]equipments. The young man flung a lightning glance at the dwarf, and a

[036-104]few minutes later the two were pacing the terrace.

[036-105]“It is nine o’clock,” cried [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]]. “I shall start for [[paris|Paris]] at full

[036-106]gallop; I can get there to-morrow morning by ten. My dear Butscha, from

[036-107]you she will accept anything, for she is attached to you; let me give

[036-108]her a riding-whip in your name. If you will do me this immense kindness,

[036-109]you shall have not only my friendship but my devotion.”

[036-110]“Ah, you are very happy,” said Butscha, ruefully; “you have money, you!”

[036-111]“Tell Canalis not to expect me, and that he must find some pretext to

[036-112]account for my absence.”

[036-113]An hour later Ernest had ridden out of Havre. He reached Paris in twelve

[036-114]hours, where his first act was to secure a place in the mail-coach

[036-115]for Havre on the following evening. Then he went to three of the chief

[036-116]jewellers in Paris and compared all the whip-handles that they could

[036-117]offer; he was in search of some artistic treasure that was regally

[036-118]superb. He found one at last, made by Stidmann for a Russian, who was

[036-119]unable to pay for it when finished,--a fox-head in gold, with a ruby of

[036-120]exorbitant value; all his savings went into the purchase, the cost of

[036-121]which was seven thousand francs. Ernest gave a drawing of the arms of

[036-122]La Bastie, and allowed the shop-people twenty hours to engrave them.

[036-123]The handle, a masterpiece of delicate workmanship, was fitted to an

[036-124]india-rubber whip and put into a morocco case lined with velvet, on

[036-125]which two M.’s interlaced were stamped in gold.

[036-126]La Briere got back to Havre by the mail-coach Wednesday morning in

[036-127]time to breakfast with Canalis. The poet had concealed his secretary’s

[036-128]absence by declaring that he was busy with some work sent from Paris.

[036-129]Butscha, who met La Briere at the coach-door, took the box containing

[036-130]the precious work of art to Francoise Cochet, with instructions to place

[036-131]it on Modeste’s dressing-table.

[036-132]“Of course you will accompany Mademoiselle Modeste on her ride to-day?”

[036-133]said Butscha, who went to Canalis’s house to let La Briere know by a

[036-134]wink that the whip had gone to its destination.

[036-135]“I?” answered Ernest; “no, I am going to bed.”

[036-136]“Bah!” exclaimed Canalis, looking at him. “I don’t know what to make of

[036-137]you.”

[036-138]Breakfast was then served, and the poet naturally invited their visitor

[036-139]to stay and take it. Butscha complied, having seen in the expression of

[036-140]the valet’s face the success of a trick in which we shall see the first

[036-141]fruits of his promise to Modeste.

[036-142]“Monsieur is very right to detain the clerk of Monsieur Latournelle,”

[036-143]whispered Germain in his master’s ear.

[036-144]Canalis and Germain went into the salon on a sign that passed between

[036-145]them.

[036-146]“I went out this morning to see the men fish, monsieur,” said the

[036-147]valet,--“an excursion proposed to me by the captain of a smack, whose

[036-148]acquaintance I have made.”

[036-149]Germain did not acknowledge that he had the bad taste to play billiards

[036-150]in a cafe,--a fact of which Butscha had taken advantage to surround him

[036-151]with friends of his own and manage him as he pleased.

[036-152]“Well?” said Canalis, “to the point,--quick!”

[036-153]“Monsieur le baron, I heard a conversation about Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], which

[036-154]I encouraged as far as I could; for no one, of course, knew that I

[036-155]belong to you. Ah! monsieur, judging by the talk of the quays, you are

[036-156]running your head into a noose. The [[social-mobility|fortune]] of Mademoiselle de La Bastie

[036-157]is, like her name, modest. The vessel on which the father returned does

[036-158]not belong to him, but to rich China merchants to whom he renders an

[036-159]account. They even say things that are not at all flattering to Monsieur

[036-160]Mignon’s [[honor]]. Having heard that you and Monsieur le duc were rivals

[036-161]for Mademoiselle de La Bastie’s hand, I have taken the liberty to warn

[036-162]you; of the two, wouldn’t it be better that his lordship should gobble

[036-163]her? As I came home I walked round the quays, and into that theatre-hall

[036-164]where the merchants meet; I slipped boldly in and out among them. Seeing

[036-165]a well-dressed stranger, those worthy fellows began to talk to me of

[036-166]Havre, and I got them, little by little, to speak of Colonel Mignon.

[036-167]What they said only confirms the stories the fishermen told me; and I

[036-168]feel that I should fail in my duty if I keep silence. That is why I did

[036-169]not get home in time to dress monsieur this morning.”

[036-170]“What am I to do?” cried Canalis, who remembered his proposals to

[036-171]Modeste the night before, and did not see how he could get out of them.
