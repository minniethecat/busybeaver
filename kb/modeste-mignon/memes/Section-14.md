# Section 14

[014-001]A vast interest was now about to animate her life. The wires of her cage

[014-002]were broken: the bolts and bars of the pretty Chalet--where were they?

[014-003]Her thoughts took wings.

[014-004]“Oh, father!” she cried, looking out to the horizon. “Come back and make

[014-005]us rich and happy.”

[014-006]The answer which [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] de La Briere received some five days later will

[014-007]tell the reader more than any elaborate disquisition of ours.

[014-008]CHAPTER IX. THE POWER OF THE UNSEEN

[014-009]To Monsieur de [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]:

[014-010]My friend,--Suffer me to give you that name,--you have delighted

[014-011]me; I would not have you other than you are in this letter, the

[014-012]first--oh, may it not be the last! Who but a [[poetry|poet]] could have

[014-013]excused and understood a young girl so delicately?

[014-014]I wish to speak with the sincerity that dictated the first lines

[014-015]of your letter. And first, let me say that most fortunately you do

[014-016]not know me. I can joyfully assure you than I am neither that

[014-017]hideous Mademoiselle Vilquin nor the very noble and withered

[014-018]Mademoiselle d’Herouville who floats between twenty and forty

[014-019]years of age, unable to decide on a satisfactory date. The

[014-020]Cardinal d’Herouville flourished in the history of the Church at

[014-021]least a century before the cardinal of whom we boast as our only

[014-022]family glory,--for I take no account of lieutenant-generals, and

[014-023]abbes who write trumpery little verses.

[014-024]Moreover, I do not live in the magnificent villa Vilquin; there is

[014-025]not in my veins, thank God, the ten-millionth of a drop of that

[014-026]chilly blood which flows behind a counter. I come on one side from

[014-027]Germany, on the other from the south of France; my mind has a

[014-028]Teutonic [[love]] of reverie, my blood the vivacity of Provence. I am

[014-029]noble on my father’s and on my mother’s side. On my mother’s I

[014-030]derive from every page of the Almanach de Gotha. In short, my

[014-031]precautions are well taken. It is not in any man’s power, nor even

[014-032]in the power of the law, to unmask my incognito. I shall remain

[014-033]veiled, unknown.

[014-034]As to my person and as to my “belongings,” as the Normans say,

[014-035]make yourself easy. I am at least as handsome as the little girl

[014-036](ignorantly happy) on whom your eyes chanced to light during your

[014-037]visit to [[provincial-life|Havre]]; and I do not call myself poverty-stricken,

[014-038]although ten sons of peers may not accompany me on my walks. I

[014-039]have seen the humiliating comedy of the heiress sought for her

[014-040]millions played on my account. In short, make no attempt, even on

[014-041]a wager, to reach me. Alas! though free as air, I am watched and

[014-042]guarded,--by myself, in the first place, and secondly, by people

[014-043]of nerve and courage who would not hesitate to put a knife in your

[014-044]heart if you tried to penetrate my retreat. I do not say this to

[014-045]excite your courage or stimulate your curiosity; I believe I have

[014-046]no need of such incentives to interest you and attach you to me.

[014-047]I will now reply to the second edition, considerably enlarged, of

[014-048]your first sermon.

[014-049]Will you have a confession? I said to myself when I saw you so

[014-050]distrustful, and mistaking me for Corinne (whose improvisations

[014-051]bore me dreadfully), that in all probability dozes of Muses had

[014-052]already led you, rashly curious, into their valleys, and begged

[014-053]you to taste the fruits of their boarding-school Parnassus. Oh!

[014-054]you are perfectly safe with me, my friend; I may love poetry, but

[014-055]I have no little verses in my pocket-book, and my stockings are,

[014-056]and will remain, immaculately white. You shall not be pestered

[014-057]with the “Flowers of my Heart” in one or more volumes. And,

[014-058]finally, should it ever happen that I say to you the word “Come!”

[014-059]you will not find--you know it now--an old maid, no, nor a poor

[014-060]and ugly one.

[014-061]Ah! my friend, if you only knew how I regret that you came to

[014-062]Havre! You have lowered the charm of what you call my romance. God

[014-063]alone knew the treasure I was reserving for the man noble enough,

[014-064]and trusting enough, and perspicacious enough to come--having

[014-065]faith in my [[epistolary-novel|letters]], having penetrated step by step into the

[014-066]depths of my heart--to come to our first meeting with the

[014-067]simplicity of a child: for that was what I dreamed to be the

[014-068]innocence of a man of genius. And now you have spoiled my

[014-069]treasure! But I forgive you; you live in [[paris|Paris]] and, as you say,

[014-070]there is always a man within a poet.

[014-071]Because I tell you this will you think me some little girl who

[014-072]cultivates a garden-full of illusions? You, who are witty and

[014-073]wise, have you not guessed that when Mademoiselle d’Este received

[014-074]your pedantic lesson she said to herself: “No, dear poet, my first

[014-075]letter was not the pebble which a vagabond child flings about the

[014-076]highway to frighten the owner of the adjacent fruit-trees, but a

[014-077]net carefully and prudently thrown by a fisherman seated on a rock

[014-078]above the sea, hoping and expecting a miraculous draught.”

[014-079]All that you say so beautifully about the family has my approval.

[014-080]The man who is able to please me, and of whom I believe myself

[014-081]worthy, will have my heart and my life,--with the consent of my

[014-082]parents, for I will neither grieve them, nor take them unawares:

[014-083]happily, I am certain of reigning over them; and, besides, they

[014-084]are wholly without prejudice. Indeed, in every way, I feel myself

[014-085]protected against any delusions in my dream. I have built the

[014-086]fortress with my own hands, and I have let it be fortified by the

[014-087]boundless devotion of those who watch over me as if I were a

[014-088]treasure,--not that I am unable to defend myself in the open, if

[014-089]need be; for, let me say, circumstances have furnished me with

[014-090]armor of proof on which is engraved the word “Disdain.” I have the

[014-091]deepest horror of all that is calculating,--of all that is not

[014-092]pure, disinterested, and wholly noble. I worship the beautiful,

[014-093][[the-ideal|the ideal]], without being romantic; though I HAVE been, in my heart

[014-094]of hearts, in my dreams. But I recognize the truth of the various

[014-095]things, just even to vulgarity, which you have written me about

[014-096]Society and social life.

[014-097]For the time being we are, and we can only be, two friends. Why

[014-098]seek an unseen friend? you ask. Your person may be unknown to me,

[014-099]but your mind, your heart I _know_; they please me, and I feel an

[014-100]infinitude of thoughts within my soul which need a man of genius

[014-101]for their confidant. I do not wish the poem of my heart to be

[014-102]wasted; I would have it known to you as it is to God. What a

[014-103]precious thing is a true comrade, one to whom we can tell all! You

[014-104]will surely not reject the unpublished leaflets of a young girl’s

[014-105]thoughts when they fly to you like the pretty insects fluttering

[014-106]to the sun? I am sure you have never before met with this good

[014-107][[commerce|fortune]] of the soul,--the honest confidences of an honest girl.

[014-108]Listen to her prattle; accept the [[music]] that she sings to you in

[014-109]her own heart. Later, if our souls are sisters, if our characters

[014-110]warrant the attempt, a white-haired old serving-man shall await

[014-111]you by the wayside and lead you to the cottage, the villa, the

[014-112]castle, the palace--I don’t know yet what sort of bower it will

[014-113]be, nor what its color, nor whether this conclusion will ever be

[014-114]possible; but you will admit, will you not? that it is poetic, and

[014-115]that Mademoiselle d’Este has a complying disposition. Has she not

[014-116]left you free? Has she gone with jealous feet to watch you in the

[014-117]salons of Paris? Has she imposed upon you the labors of some high

[014-118]emprise, such as paladins sought voluntarily in the olden time?

[014-119]No, she asks a perfectly spiritual and mystic alliance. Come to me

[014-120]when you are unhappy, wounded, weary. Tell me all, hide nothing; I

[014-121]have balms for all your ills. I am twenty years of age, dear

[014-122]friend, but I have the sense of fifty, and unfortunately I have

[014-123]known through the experience of another all the horrors and the

[014-124]delights of love. I know what baseness the human heart can

[014-125]contain, what infamy; yet I myself am an honest girl. No, I have

[014-126]no illusions; but I have something better, something real,--I have

[014-127]beliefs and a religion. See! I open the ball of our confidences.

[014-128]Whoever I marry--provided I choose him for myself--may sleep in

[014-129]peace or go to the East Indies sure that he will find me on his

[014-130]return working at the tapestry which I began before he left me;

[014-131]and in every stitch he shall read a verse of the poem of which he

[014-132]has been the hero. Yes, I have resolved within my heart never to

[014-133]follow my husband where he does not wish me to go. I will be the

[014-134]divinity of his hearth. That is my religion of humanity. But why

[014-135]should I not test and choose the man to whom I am to be like the

[014-136]life to the body? Is a man ever impeded by life? What can that

[014-137]woman be who thwarts the man she loves?--an illness, a disease,

[014-138]not life. By life, I mean that joyous health which makes each hour

[014-139]a pleasure.

[014-140]But to return to your letter, which will always be precious to me.

[014-141]Yes, jesting apart, it contains that which I desired, an

[014-142]expression of prosaic sentiments which are as necessary to family

[014-143]life as air to the lungs; and without which no happiness is

[014-144]possible. To act as an honest man, to think as a poet, to love as

[014-145]women love, that is what I longed for in my friend, and it is now

[014-146]no longer a chimera.

[014-147]Adieu, my friend. I am poor at this moment. That is one of the

[014-148]reasons why I cling to my concealment, my mask, my impregnable

[014-149]fortress. I have read your last verses in the “Revue,”--ah! with

[014-150]what delight, now that I am initiated in the austere loftiness of

[014-151]your secret soul.

[014-152]Will it make you unhappy to know that a young girl prays for you;

[014-153]that you are her solitary thought,--without a rival except in her

[014-154]father and mother? Can there be any reason why you should reject

[014-155]these pages full of you, written for you, seen by no eye but

[014-156]yours? Send me their counterpart. I am so little of a woman yet

[014-157]that your confidences--provided they are full and true--will

[014-158]suffice for the happiness of your

[014-159]O. d’Este M.

[014-160]“Good heavens! can I be in love already?” cried the young secretary,

[014-161]when he perceived that he had held the above letter in his hands more

[014-162]than an hour after reading it. “What shall I do? She thinks she is

[014-163]writing to the great poet! Can I continue the deception? Is she a woman

[014-164]of forty, or a girl of twenty?”

[014-165]Ernest was now fascinated by the great gulf of the unseen. The unseen

[014-166]is the obscurity of infinitude, and nothing is more alluring. In that

[014-167]sombre vastness fires flash, and furrow and color the abyss with fancies

[014-168]like those of Martin. For a busy man like Canalis, an adventure of this

[014-169]kind is swept away like a harebell by a mountain torrent, but in the

[014-170]more unoccupied life of the young secretary, this charming girl, whom

[014-171]his imagination persistently connected with the blonde beauty at

[014-172]the window, fastened upon his heart, and did as much mischief in his

[014-173]regulated life as a fox in a poultry-yard. La Briere allowed himself

[014-174]to be preoccupied by this mysterious correspondent; and he answered her

[014-175]last letter with another, a pretentious and carefully studied epistle,

[014-176]in which, however, passion begins to reveal itself through pique.

[014-177]Mademoiselle,--Is it quite loyal in you to enthrone yourself in

[014-178]the heart of a poor poet with a latent intention of abandoning him

[014-179]if he is not exactly what you wish, leaving him to endless

[014-180]regrets,--showing him for a moment an image of perfection, were it

[014-181]only assumed, and at any rate giving him a foretaste of happiness?

[014-182]I was very short-sighted in soliciting this letter, in which you

[014-183]have begun to unfold the elegant fabric of your thoughts. A man

[014-184]can easily become enamored with a mysterious unknown who combines

[014-185]such fearlessness with such originality, so much imagination with

[014-186]so much feeling. Who would not wish to know you after reading your

[014-187]first confidence? It requires a strong effort on my part to retain

[014-188]my senses in thinking of you, for you combine all that can trouble

[014-189]the head or the heart of man. I therefore make the most of the

[014-190]little self-possession you have left me to offer you my humble

[014-191]remonstrances.
