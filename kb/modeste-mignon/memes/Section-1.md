# Section 1

[001-001]MODESTE MIGNON

[001-002]By Honore De Balzac

[001-003]Translated by Katharine Prescott Wormeley

[001-004]DEDICATION

[001-005]To a Polish Lady.

[001-006]Daughter of an enslaved land, angel through [[love]], witch through

[001-007]fancy, child by faith, aged by experience, man in brain, woman in

[001-008]heart, giant by hope, mother through sorrows, [[poetry|poet]] in thy dreams,

[001-009]--to _thee_ belongs this book, in which thy love, thy fancy, thy

[001-010]experience, thy sorrow, thy hope, thy dreams, are the warp through

[001-011]which is shot a woof less brilliant than the poesy of thy soul,

[001-012]whose expression, when it shines upon thy countenance, is, to

[001-013]those who love thee, what the characters of a lost language are to

[001-014]scholars.

[001-015]De Balzac.

[001-016]MODESTE MIGNON

[001-017]CHAPTER I. THE CHALET

[001-018]At the beginning of October, 1829, Monsieur Simon Babylas Latournelle,

[001-019]notary, was walking up from [[provincial-life|Havre]] to [[ingouville|Ingouville]], arm in arm with his

[001-020]son and accompanied by his wife, at whose side the head clerk of the

[001-021]lawyer’s office, a little hunchback named Jean Butscha, trotted along

[001-022]like a page. When these four personages (two of whom came the same way

[001-023]every evening) reached the elbow of the road where it turns back upon

[001-024]itself like those called in Italy “cornice,” the notary looked about to

[001-025]see if any one could overhear him either from the terrace above or

[001-026]the path beneath, and when he spoke he lowered his voice as a further

[001-027]precaution.

[001-028]“Exupere,” he said to his son, “you must try to carry out intelligently

[001-029]a little manoeuvre which I shall explain to you, but you are not to ask

[001-030]the meaning of it; and if you guess the meaning I command you to toss

[001-031]it into that Styx which every lawyer and every man who expects to have

[001-032]a hand in the government of his country is bound to keep within him for

[001-033]the secrets of others. After you have paid your respects and compliments

[001-034]to Madame and Mademoiselle [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], to Monsieur and Madame [[dumay|Dumay]], and

[001-035]to Monsieur Gobenheim if he is at [[chalet|the Chalet]], and as soon as quiet

[001-036]is restored, Monsieur Dumay will take you aside; you are then to look

[001-037]attentively at [[modeste-mignon|Mademoiselle Modeste]] (yes, I am willing to allow it)

[001-038]during the whole time he is speaking to you. My worthy friend will ask

[001-039]you to go out and take a walk; at the end of an hour, that is, about

[001-040]nine o’clock, you are to come back in a great hurry; try to puff as if

[001-041]you were out of breath, and whisper in Monsieur Dumay’s ear, quite low,

[001-042]but so that Mademoiselle Modeste is sure to overhear you, these words:

[001-043]‘The young man has come.’”

[001-044]Exupere was to start the next morning for [[paris|Paris]] to begin the study of

[001-045]law. This impending departure had induced Latournelle to propose him

[001-046]to his friend Dumay as an accomplice in the important conspiracy which

[001-047]these directions indicate.

[001-048]“Is Mademoiselle Modeste suspected of having a lover?” asked Butscha in

[001-049]a timid voice of Madame Latournelle.

[001-050]“Hush, Butscha,” she replied, taking her husband’s arm.

[001-051]Madame Latournelle, the daughter of a clerk of the supreme court,

[001-052]feels that her birth authorizes her to claim issue from a parliamentary

[001-053]family. This conviction explains why the lady, who is somewhat blotched

[001-054]as to complexion, endeavors to assume in her own person the majesty of

[001-055]a court whose decrees are recorded in her father’s pothooks. She

[001-056]takes snuff, holds herself as stiff as a ramrod, poses for a person

[001-057]of consideration, and resembles nothing so much as a mummy brought

[001-058]momentarily to life by galvanism. She tries to give high-bred tones to

[001-059]her sharp voice, and succeeds no better in doing that than in hiding

[001-060]her general lack of breeding. Her social usefulness seems, however,

[001-061]incontestable when we glance at the flower-bedecked cap she wears,

[001-062]at the false front frizzling around her forehead, at the gowns of her

[001-063]choice; for how could shopkeepers dispose of those products if there

[001-064]were no Madame Latournelle? All these absurdities of the worthy woman,

[001-065]who is truly pious and charitable, might have passed unnoticed, if

[001-066]nature, amusing herself as she often does by turning out these ludicrous

[001-067]creations, had not endowed her with the height of a drum-major, and thus

[001-068]held up to view the comicalities of her provincial nature. She has

[001-069]never been out of Havre; she believes in the infallibility of Havre; she

[001-070]proclaims herself Norman to the very tips of her fingers; she venerates

[001-071]her father, and adores her husband.

[001-072]Little Latournelle was bold enough to marry this lady after she had

[001-073]attained the anti-matrimonial age of thirty-three, and what is more, he

[001-074]had a son by her. As he could have got the sixty thousand francs of

[001-075]her “dot” in several other ways, the public assigned his uncommon

[001-076]intrepidity to a desire to escape an invasion of the Minotaur, against

[001-077]whom his personal qualifications would have insufficiently protected him

[001-078]had he rashly dared his fate by bringing home a young and pretty wife.

[001-079]The fact was, however, that the notary recognized the really fine

[001-080]qualities of Mademoiselle Agnes (she was called Agnes) and reflected to

[001-081]himself that a woman’s beauty is soon past and gone to a husband. As

[001-082]to the insignificant youth on whom the clerk of the court bestowed in

[001-083]baptism his Norman name of “Exupere,” Madame Latournelle is still so

[001-084]surprised at becoming his mother, at the age of thirty-five years and

[001-085]seven months, that she would still provide him, if it were necessary,

[001-086]with her breast and her milk,--an hyperbole which alone can fully

[001-087]express her impassioned maternity. “How handsome he is, that son of

[001-088]mine!” she says to her little friend Modeste, as they walk to church,

[001-089]with the beautiful Exupere in front of them. “He is like you,” Modeste

[001-090]Mignon answers, very much as she might have said, “What horrid

[001-091]weather!” This silhouette of Madame Latournelle is quite important as an

[001-092]accessory, inasmuch as for three years she has been the chaperone of the

[001-093]young girl against whom the notary and his friend Dumay are now plotting

[001-094]to set up what we have called, in the “Physiologie du Mariage,” a

[001-095]“mouse-trap.”

[001-096]As for Latournelle, imagine a worthy little fellow as sly as the purest

[001-097][[honor]] and uprightness would allow him to be,--a man whom any stranger

[001-098]would take for a rascal at sight of his queer physiognomy, to which,

[001-099]however, the inhabitants of Havre were well accustomed. His eyesight,

[001-100]said to be weak, obliged the worthy man to wear green goggles for the

[001-101]protection of his eyes, which were constantly inflamed. The arch of each

[001-102]eyebrow, defined by a thin down of hair, surrounded the tortoise-shell

[001-103]rim of the glasses and made a couple of circles as it were, slightly

[001-104]apart. If you have never observed on the human face the effect produced

[001-105]by these circumferences placed one within the other, and separated by a

[001-106]hollow space or line, you can hardly imagine how perplexing such a face

[001-107]will be to you, especially if pale, hollow-cheeked, and terminating in a

[001-108]pointed chin like that of Mephistopheles,--a type which painters give

[001-109]to cats. This double resemblance was observable on the face of Babylas

[001-110]Latournelle. Above the atrocious green spectacles rose a bald crown,

[001-111]all the more crafty in expression because a wig, seemingly endowed with

[001-112]motion, let the white hairs show on all sides of it as it meandered

[001-113]crookedly across the forehead. An observer taking note of this excellent

[001-114]Norman, clothed in black and mounted on his two legs like a beetle on

[001-115]a couple of pins, and knowing him to be one of the most trustworthy

[001-116]of men, would have sought, without finding it, for the reason of such

[001-117]physical misrepresentation.

[001-118]Jean Butscha, a natural son abandoned by his parents and taken care of

[001-119]by the clerk of the court and his daughter, and now, through sheer hard

[001-120]work, head-clerk to the notary, fed and lodged by his master, who

[001-121]gave him a salary of nine hundred francs, almost a dwarf, and with

[001-122]no semblance of youth,--Jean Butscha made Modeste his idol, and would

[001-123]willingly have given his life for hers. The poor fellow, whose eyes

[001-124]were hollowed beneath their heavy lids like the touch-holes of a cannon,

[001-125]whose head overweighted his body, with its shock of crisp hair, and

[001-126]whose face was pock-marked, had lived under pitying eyes from the time

[001-127]he was seven years of age. Is not that enough to explain his whole

[001-128]being? Silent, self-contained, pious, exemplary in conduct, he went

[001-129]his way over that vast tract of country named on the map of the heart

[001-130]Love-without-Hope, the sublime and arid steppes of Desire. Modeste had

[001-131]christened this grotesque little being her “Black Dwarf.” The nickname

[001-132]sent him to the pages of Walter Scott’s novel, and he one day said

[001-133]to Modeste: “Will you accept a rose against the evil day from your

[001-134]mysterious dwarf?” Modeste instantly sent the soul of her adorer to its

[001-135]humble mud-cabin with a terrible glance, such as young girls bestow

[001-136]on the men who cannot please them. Butscha’s conception of himself was

[001-137]lowly, and, like the wife of his master, he had never been out of Havre.

[001-138]Perhaps it will be well, for the sake of those who have never seen

[001-139]that city, to say a few words as to the present destination of the

[001-140]Latournelle family,--the head clerk being included in the latter term.

[001-141]Ingouville is to Havre what Montmartre is to Paris,--a high hill at the

[001-142]foot of which the city lies; with this difference, that the hill and the

[001-143]city are surrounded by the sea and the Seine, that Havre is helplessly

[001-144]circumscribed by enclosing fortifications, and, in short, that the mouth

[001-145]of the river, the harbor, and the docks present a very different aspect

[001-146]from the fifty thousand houses of Paris. At the foot of Montmartre an

[001-147]ocean of slate roofs lies in motionless blue billows; at Ingouville the

[001-148]sea is like the same roofs stirred by the wind. This eminence, or line

[001-149]of hills, which coasts the Seine from Rouen to the seashore, leaving a

[001-150]margin of valley land more or less narrow between itself and the river,

[001-151]and containing in its cities, its ravines, its vales, its meadows,

[001-152]veritable treasures of the picturesque, became of enormous value in

[001-153]and about Ingouville, after the year 1816, the period at which the

[001-154]prosperity of Havre began. This township has become since that time

[001-155]the Auteuil, the Ville-d’Avray, the Montmorency, in short, the suburban

[001-156]residence of the merchants of Havre. Here they build their houses on

[001-157]terraces around its ampitheatre of hills, and breathe the sea air

[001-158]laden with the fragrance of their splendid gardens. Here these bold

[001-159]speculators cast off the burden of their counting-rooms and the

[001-160]atmosphere of their city houses, which are built closely together

[001-161]without open spaces, often without court-yards,--a vice of construction

[001-162]with the increasing population of Havre, the inflexible line of the

[001-163]fortifications, and the enlargement of the docks has forced upon them.

[001-164]The result is, weariness of heart in Havre, cheerfulness and joy at

[001-165]Ingouville. The law of social development has forced up the suburb of

[001-166]Graville like a mushroom. It is to-day more extensive than Havre itself,

[001-167]which lies at the foot of its slopes like a serpent.

[001-168]At the crest of the hill Ingouville has but one street, and (as in all

[001-169]such situations) the houses which overlook the river have an immense

[001-170]advantage over those on the other side of the road, whose view they

[001-171]obstruct, and which present the effect of standing on tip-toe to look

[001-172]over the opposing roofs. However, there exist here, as elsewhere,

[001-173]certain servitudes. Some houses standing at the summit have a finer

[001-174]position or possess legal rights of view which compel their opposite

[001-175]neighbors to keep their buildings down to a required height. Moreover,

[001-176]the openings cut in the capricious rock by roads which follow its

[001-177]declensions and make the ampitheatre habitable, give vistas through

[001-178]which some estates can see the city, or the river, or the sea. Instead

[001-179]of rising to an actual peak, the hill ends abruptly in a cliff. At the

[001-180]end of the street which follows the line of the summit, ravines appear

[001-181]in which a few villages are clustered (Sainte-Adresse and two or three

[001-182]other Saint-somethings) together with several creeks which murmur and

[001-183]flow with the tides of the sea. These half-deserted slopes of Ingouville

[001-184]form a striking contrast to the terraces of fine villas which overlook

[001-185]the valley of the Seine. Is the wind on this side too strong for

[001-186]vegetation? Do the merchants shrink from the cost of terracing it?

[001-187]However this may be, the traveller approaching Havre on a steamer is

[001-188]surprised to find a barren coast and tangled gorges to the west of

[001-189]Ingouville, like a beggar in rags beside a perfumed and sumptuously

[001-190]apparelled rich man.
