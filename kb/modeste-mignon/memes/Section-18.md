# Section 18

[018-001]My dear old fellow, I have never doubted you for a moment. We have

[018-002]gone through wars and [[commerce]] together and now we will undertake

[018-003]agriculture; you shall be my bailiff. You will like that, will you

[018-004]not? And so, old friend, I leave it to your discretion to tell

[018-005]what you think best to my wife and daughters; I rely upon your

[018-006]prudence. In four years great changes may have taken place in

[018-007]their characters.

[018-008]Adieu, my old [[dumay|Dumay]]. Say to my daughters and to my wife that I

[018-009]have never failed to kiss them in my thoughts morning and evening

[018-010]since I left them. The second check for forty thousand francs

[018-011]herewith enclosed is for my wife and children.

[018-012]Till we meet.--Your colonel and friend,

[018-013][[charles-mignon|Charles Mignon]].

[018-014]“Your father is coming,” said Madame Mignon to her daughter.

[018-015]“What makes you think so, mamma?” asked [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]].

[018-016]“Nothing else could make Dumay hurry himself.”

[018-017]“Victory! victory!” cried the lieutenant as soon as he reached the

[018-018]garden gate. “Madame, the colonel has not been ill a moment; he is

[018-019]coming back--coming back on the ‘Mignon,’ a fine ship of his own, which

[018-020]together with its cargo is worth, he tells me, eight or nine hundred

[018-021]thousand francs. But he requires secrecy from all of us; his heart is

[018-022]still wrung by the misfortunes of our dear departed girl.”

[018-023]“He has still to learn her death,” said Madame Mignon.

[018-024]“He attributes her disaster, and I think he is right, to the rapacity of

[018-025]young men after great fortunes. My poor colonel expects to find the lost

[018-026]sheep here. Let us be happy among ourselves but say nothing to any

[018-027]one, not even to Latournelle, if that is possible. Mademoiselle,” he

[018-028]whispered in Modeste’s ear, “write to your father and tell him of his

[018-029]loss and also the terrible results on your mother’s health and eyesight;

[018-030]prepare him for the shock he has to meet. I will engage to get the

[018-031]letter into his hands before he reaches [[provincial-life|Havre]], for he will have to pass

[018-032]through [[paris|Paris]] on his way. Write him a long letter; you have plenty of

[018-033]time. I will take the letter on Monday; Monday I shall probably go to

[018-034]Paris.”

[018-035]Modeste was so afraid that [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] and Dumay would meet that she started

[018-036]hastily for the house to write to her [[poetry|poet]] and put off the rendezvous.

[018-037]“Mademoiselle,” said Dumay, in a very humble manner and barring

[018-038]Modeste’s way, “may your father find his daughter with no other feelings

[018-039]in her heart than those she had for him and for her mother before he was

[018-040]obliged to leave her.”

[018-041]“I have sworn to myself, to my sister, and to my mother to be the

[018-042]joy, the consolation, and the glory of my father, and _I shall keep my

[018-043]oath_!” replied Modeste with a haughty and disdainful glance at Dumay.

[018-044]“Do not trouble my delight in the thought of my father’s return

[018-045]with insulting suspicions. You cannot prevent a girl’s heart from

[018-046]beating--you don’t want me to be a mummy, do you?” she said. “My hand

[018-047]belongs to my family, but my heart is my own. If I [[love]] any one, my

[018-048]father and my mother will know it. Does that satisfy you, monsieur?”

[018-049]“Thank you, mademoiselle; you restore me to life,” said Dumay, “but you

[018-050]might still call me Dumay, even when you box my ears!”

[018-051]“Swear to me,” said her mother, “that you have not engaged a word or a

[018-052]look with any young man.”

[018-053]“I can swear that, my dear mother,” said Modeste, laughing, and looking

[018-054]at Dumay who was watching her and smiling to himself like a mischievous

[018-055]girl.

[018-056]“She must be false indeed if you are right,” cried Dumay, when Modeste

[018-057]had left them and gone into the house.

[018-058]“My daughter Modeste may have faults,” said her mother, “but falsehood

[018-059]is not one of them; she is incapable of saying what is not true.”

[018-060]“Well! then let us feel easy,” continued Dumay, “and believe that

[018-061]misfortune has closed his account with us.”

[018-062]“God grant it!” answered Madame Mignon. “You will see _him_, Dumay; but

[018-063]I shall only hear him. There is much of sadness in my joy.”

[018-064]CHAPTER XII. A DECLARATION OF LOVE,--SET TO MUSIC

[018-065]At this moment Modeste, happy as she was in the return of her father,

[018-066]was, nevertheless, pacing her room disconsolate as Perrette on seeing

[018-067]her eggs broken. She had hoped her father would bring back a much larger

[018-068][[social-mobility|fortune]] than Dumay had mentioned. Nothing could satisfy her new-found

[018-069]ambition on behalf of her poet less than at least half the six millions

[018-070]she had talked of in her second letter. Trebly agitated by her two joys

[018-071]and the grief caused by her comparative poverty, she seated herself at

[018-072]the piano, that confidant of so many young girls, who tell out their

[018-073]wishes and provocations on the keys, expressing them by the notes and

[018-074]tones of their [[music]]. Dumay was talking with his wife in the garden

[018-075]under the windows, telling her the secret of their own wealth, and

[018-076]questioning her as to her desires and her intentions. Madame Dumay had,

[018-077]like her husband, no other family than the Mignons. Husband and wife

[018-078]agreed, therefore, to go and live in Provence, if the Comte de La Bastie

[018-079]really meant to live in Provence, and to leave their money to whichever

[018-080]of Modeste’s children might need it most.

[018-081]“Listen to Modeste,” said Madame Mignon, addressing them. “None but a

[018-082]girl in love can compose such airs without having studied music.”

[018-083]Houses may burn, fortunes be engulfed, fathers return from distant

[018-084]lands, empires may crumble away, the cholera may ravage cities, but a

[018-085]maiden’s love wings its way as nature pursues hers, or that alarming

[018-086]acid which chemistry has lately discovered, and which will presently eat

[018-087]through the globe, if nothing stops it.

[018-088]Modeste, under the inspiration of her present situation, was putting to

[018-089]music certain stanzas which we are compelled to quote here--albeit

[018-090]they are printed in the second volume of the edition Dauriat had

[018-091]mentioned--because, in order to adapt them to her music, which had the

[018-092]inexpressible charm of sentiment so admired in great singers, Modeste

[018-093]had taken liberties with the lines in a manner that may astonish the

[018-094]admirers of a poet so famous for the correctness, sometimes too precise,

[018-095]of his measures.

[018-096]THE MAIDEN’S SONG

[018-097]Hear, arise! the lark is shaking

[018-098]Sunlit wings that heavenward rise;

[018-099]Sleep no more; the violet, waking,

[018-100]Wafts her incense to the skies.

[018-101]Flowers revived, their eyes unclosing,

[018-102]See themselves in drops of dew

[018-103]In each calyx-cup reposing,

[018-104]Pearls of a day their mirror true.

[018-105]Breeze divine, the god of roses,

[018-106]Passed by night to bless their bloom;

[018-107]See! for him each bud uncloses,

[018-108]Glows, and yields its rich perfume.

[018-109]Then arise! the lark is shaking

[018-110]Sunlit wings that heavenward rise;

[018-111]Nought is sleeping--Heart, awaking,

[018-112]Lift thine incense to the skies.

[018-113]“It is very pretty,” said Madame Dumay. “Modeste is a musician, and

[018-114]that’s the whole of it.”

[018-115]“The devil is in her!” cried the cashier, into whose heart the suspicion

[018-116]of the mother forced its way and made him shiver.

[018-117]“She loves,” persisted Madame Mignon.

[018-118]By succeeding, through the undeniable testimony of the song, in making

[018-119]the cashier a sharer in her belief as to the state of Modeste’s heart,

[018-120]Madame Mignon destroyed the happiness the return and the prosperity of

[018-121]his master had brought him. The poor Breton went down the hill to Havre

[018-122]and to his desk in Gobenheim’s counting-room with a heavy heart; then,

[018-123]before returning to dinner, he went to see Latournelle, to tell his

[018-124]fears, and beg once more for the notary’s advice and assistance.

[018-125]“Yes, my dear friend,” said Dumay, when they parted on the steps of the

[018-126]notary’s door, “I now agree with madame; she loves,--yes, I am sure of

[018-127]it; and the devil knows the rest. I am dishonored.”

[018-128]“Don’t make yourself unhappy, Dumay,” answered the little notary. “Among

[018-129]us all we can surely get the better of the little puss; sooner or later,

[018-130]every girl in love betrays herself,--you may be sure of that. But we

[018-131]will talk about it this evening.”

[018-132]Thus it happened that all those devoted to the Mignon family were fully

[018-133]as disquieted and uncertain as they were before the old soldier tried

[018-134]the experiment which he expected would be so decisive. The ill-success

[018-135]of his past efforts so stimulated Dumay’s sense of duty, that he

[018-136]determined not to go to Paris to see after his own fortune as announced

[018-137]by his patron, until he had guessed the riddle of Modeste’s heart. These

[018-138]friends, to whom feelings were more precious than interests, well knew

[018-139]that unless the daughter were pure and innocent, the father would die of

[018-140]grief when he came to know the death of Bettina and the blindness of

[018-141]his wife. The distress of poor Dumay made such an impression on the

[018-142]Latournelles that they even forgot their parting with Exupere, whom they

[018-143]had sent off that morning to Paris. During dinner, while the three were

[018-144]alone, Monsieur and Madame Latournelle and Butscha turned the problem

[018-145]over and over in their minds, and discussed every aspect of it.

[018-146]“If Modeste loved any one in Havre she would have shown some fear

[018-147]yesterday,” said Madame Latournelle; “her lover, therefore, lives

[018-148]somewhere else.”

[018-149]“She swore to her mother this morning,” said the notary, “in presence

[018-150]of Dumay, that she had not exchanged a look or a word with any living

[018-151]soul.”

[018-152]“Then she loves after my fashion!” exclaimed Butscha.

[018-153]“And how is that, my poor lad?” asked Madame Latournelle.

[018-154]“Madame,” said the little cripple, “I love alone and afar--oh! as far as

[018-155]from here to the stars.”

[018-156]“How do you manage it, you silly fellow?” said Madame Latournelle,

[018-157]laughing.

[018-158]“Ah, madame!” said Butscha, “what you call my hump is the socket of my

[018-159]wings.”

[018-160]“So that is the explanation of your seal, is it?” cried the notary.

[018-161]Butscha’s seal was a star, and under it the words “Fulgens,

[018-162]sequar,”--“Shining One, I follow thee,”--the motto of the house of

[018-163]Chastillonest.

[018-164]“A beautiful woman may feel as distrustful as the ugliest,” said

[018-165]Butscha, as if speaking to himself; “Modeste is clever enough to fear

[018-166]she may be loved only for her beauty.”

[018-167]Hunchbacks are extraordinary creations, due entirely to society for,

[018-168]according to Nature’s plan, feeble or aborted beings ought to perish.

[018-169]The curvature or distortion of the spinal column creates in these

[018-170]outwardly deformed subjects as it were a storage-battery, where

[018-171]the nerve currents accumulate more abundantly than under normal

[018-172]conditions,--where they develop, and whence they are emitted, so to say,

[018-173]in lightning flashes, to energize the interior being. From this, forces

[018-174]result which are sometimes brought to light by magnetism, though they

[018-175]are far more frequently lost in the vague spaces of the spiritual world.

[018-176]It is rare to find a deformed person who is not gifted with some special

[018-177]faculty,--a whimsical or sparkling gaiety perhaps, an utter malignity,

[018-178]or an almost sublime goodness. Like instruments which the hand of art

[018-179]can never fully waken, these beings, highly privileged though they know

[018-180]it not, live within themselves, as Butscha lived, provided their natural

[018-181]forces so magnificently concentrated have not been spent in the struggle

[018-182]they have been forced to maintain, against tremendous odds, to keep

[018-183]alive. This explains many superstitions, the popular legends of gnomes,

[018-184]frightful dwarfs, deformed fairies,--all that race of bottles, as

[018-185]Rabelais called them, containing elixirs and precious balms.

[018-186]Butscha, therefore, had very nearly found the key to the puzzle. With

[018-187]all the anxious solicitude of a hopeless lover, a vassal ever ready to

[018-188]die,--like the soldiers alone and abandoned in the snows of Russia, who

[018-189]still cried out, “Long live the Emperor,”--he meditated how to capture

[018-190]Modeste’s secret for his own private knowledge. So thinking, he followed

[018-191]his patrons to [[chalet|the Chalet]] that evening, with a cloud of care upon his

[018-192]brow: for he knew it was most important to hide from all these watchful

[018-193]eyes and ears the net, whatever it might be, in which he should entrap

[018-194]his lady. It would have to be, he thought, by some intercepted glance,

[018-195]some sudden start or quiver, as when a surgeon lays his finger on a

[018-196]hidden sore. That evening Gobenheim did not appear, and Butscha was

[018-197]Dumay’s partner against Monsieur and Madame Latournelle. During the few

[018-198]moment’s of Modeste’s absence, about nine o’clock, to prepare for her

[018-199]mother’s bedtime, Madame Mignon and her friends spoke openly to one

[018-200]another; but the poor clerk, depressed by the conviction of Modeste’s

[018-201]love, which had now seized upon him as upon the rest, seemed as remote

[018-202]from the discussion as Gobenheim had been the night before.
