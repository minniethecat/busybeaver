# Section 40

[040-001]The coldness which this altercation produced between [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] and [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]

[040-002]was visible to all eyes that evening. The [[poetry|poet]] went off early, on the

[040-003]ground of La Briere’s illness, leaving the field to the grand equerry.

[040-004][[About]] eleven o’clock Butscha, who had come to walk home with Madame

[040-005]Latournelle, whispered in Modeste’s ear, “Was I right?”

[040-006]“Alas, yes,” she said.

[040-007]“But I hope you have left the door half open, so that he can come back;

[040-008]we agreed upon that, you know.”

[040-009]“Anger got the better of me,” said Modeste. “Such meanness sent the

[040-010]blood to my head and I told him what I thought of him.”

[040-011]“Well, so much the better. When you are both so angry that you can’t

[040-012]speak civilly to each other I engage to make him desperately in [[love]] and

[040-013]so pressing that you will be deceived yourself.”

[040-014]“Come, come, Butscha; he is a great poet; he is a gentleman; he is a man

[040-015]of intellect.”

[040-016]“Your father’s eight millions are more to him than all that.”

[040-017]“Eight millions!” exclaimed Modeste.

[040-018]“My master, who has sold his practice, is going to Provence to attend

[040-019]to the purchase of lands which your father’s agent has suggested to him.

[040-020]The sum that is to be paid for the estate of La Bastie is four millions;

[040-021]your father has agreed to it. You are to have a ‘dot’ of two millions

[040-022]and another million for an establishment in [[paris|Paris]], a hotel and

[040-023]furniture. Now, count up.”

[040-024]“Ah! then I can be Duchesse d’Herouville!” cried Modeste, glancing at

[040-025]Butscha.

[040-026]“If it hadn’t been for that comedian of a Canalis you would have kept

[040-027]HIS whip, thinking it came from me,” said the dwarf, indirectly pleading

[040-028]La Briere’s cause.

[040-029]“Monsieur Butscha, may I ask if I am to marry to please you?” said

[040-030]Modeste, laughing.

[040-031]“That fine fellow loves you as well as I do,--and you loved him for

[040-032]eight days,” retorted Butscha; “and HE has got a heart.”

[040-033]“Can he compete, pray, with an office under the Crown? There are but

[040-034]six, grand almoner, chancellor, grand chamberlain, grand master, high

[040-035]constable, grand admiral,--but they don’t appoint high constables any

[040-036]longer.”

[040-037]“In six months, mademoiselle, the masses--who are made up of wicked

[040-038]Butschas--could send all those grand dignities to the winds. Besides,

[040-039]what signifies nobility in these days? There are not a thousand real

[040-040]noblemen in France. The d’Herouvilles are descended from a tipstaff

[040-041]in the time of Robert of Normandy. You will have to put up with many a

[040-042]vexation from the old aunt with the furrowed face. Look here,--as you

[040-043]are so anxious for the title of duchess,--you belong to the Comtat, and

[040-044]the Pope will certainly think as much of you as he does of all those

[040-045]merchants down there; he’ll sell you a duchy with some name ending in

[040-046]‘ia’ or ‘agno.’ Don’t play away your happiness for an office under the

[040-047]Crown.”

[040-048]CHAPTER XXV. A DIPLOMATIC LETTER

[040-049]The poet’s reflections during the night were thoroughly matter of fact.

[040-050]He sincerely saw nothing worse in life than the situation of a married

[040-051]man without money. Still trembling at the danger he had been led into by

[040-052]his vanity, his desire to get the better of the duke, and his belief in

[040-053]the [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] millions, he began to ask himself what the duchess must be

[040-054]thinking of his stay in [[provincial-life|Havre]], aggravated by the fact that he had not

[040-055]written to her for fourteen days, whereas in Paris they exchanged four

[040-056]or five [[epistolary-novel|letters]] a week.

[040-057]“And that poor woman is working hard to get me appointed commander of

[040-058]the Legion and ambassador to the Court of Baden!” he cried.

[040-059]Thereupon, with that promptitude of decision which results--in poets as

[040-060]well as in speculators--from a lively intuition of the future, he sat

[040-061]down and composed the following letter:--

[040-062]To Madame la Duchesse de Chaulieu:

[040-063]My dear Eleonore,--You have doubtless been surprised at not

[040-064]hearing from me; but the stay I am making in this place is not

[040-065]altogether on account of my health. I have been trying to do a

[040-066]good turn to our little friend La Briere. The poor fellow has

[040-067]fallen in love with a certain Mademoiselle Modeste de La Bastie, a

[040-068]rather pale, insignificant, and thread-papery little thing, who,

[040-069]by the way, has the vice of liking literature, and calls herself a

[040-070]poet to excuse the caprices and humors of a rather sullen nature.

[040-071]You know [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]],--he is so easy to catch that I have been afraid

[040-072]to leave him to himself. Mademoiselle de La Bastie was inclined to

[040-073]coquet with your Melchior, and was only too ready to become your

[040-074]rival, though her arms are thin, and she has no more bust than

[040-075]most girls; moreover, her hair is as dead and colorless as that of

[040-076]Madame de Rochefide, and her eyes small, gray, and very

[040-077]suspicious. I put a stop--perhaps rather brutally--to the

[040-078]attentions of Mademoiselle Immodeste; but love, such as mine for

[040-079]you, demanded it. What care I for all the women on earth,

[040-080]--compared to you, what are they?

[040-081]The people with whom I pass my time, and who form the circle round

[040-082]the heiress, are so thoroughly [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] that they almost turn my

[040-083]stomach. Pity me; imagine! I pass my evenings with notaries,

[040-084]notaresses, cashiers, provincial money-lenders--ah! what a change

[040-085]from my evenings in the rue de Grenelle. The alleged [[commerce|fortune]] of

[040-086]the father, lately returned from China, has brought to Havre that

[040-087]indefatigable suitor, the grand equerry, hungry after the

[040-088]millions, which he wants, they say, to drain his marshes. The king

[040-089]does not know what a fatal present he made the duke in those waste

[040-090]lands. His Grace, who has not yet found out that the lady had only

[040-091]a small [[social-mobility|fortune]], is jealous of _me_; for La Briere is quietly making

[040-092]progress with his idol under cover of his friend, who serves as a

[040-093]blind.

[040-094]Notwithstanding Ernest’s romantic ecstasies, I myself, a poet,

[040-095]think chiefly of the essential thing, and I have been making some

[040-096]inquiries which darken the prospects of our friend. If my angel

[040-097]would like absolution for some of our little sins, she will try to

[040-098]find out the facts of the case by sending for Mongenod, the

[040-099]banker, and questioning him, with the dexterity that characterizes

[040-100]her, as to the father’s fortune? Monsieur Mignon, formerly colonel

[040-101]of cavalry in the Imperial guard, has been for the last seven

[040-102]years a correspondent of the Mongenods. It is said that he gives

[040-103]his daughter a “dot” of two hundred thousand francs, and before I

[040-104]make the offer on Ernest’s behalf I am anxious to get the rights

[040-105]of the story. As soon as the affair is arranged I shall return to

[040-106]Paris. I know a way to settle everything to the advantage of our

[040-107]young lover,--simply by the transmission of the father-in-law’s

[040-108]title, and no one, I think, can more readily obtain that favor

[040-109]than Ernest, both on account of his own services and the influence

[040-110]which you and I and the duke can exert for him. With his tastes,

[040-111]Ernest, who of course will step into my office when I go to Baden,

[040-112]will be perfectly happy in Paris with twenty-five thousand francs

[040-113]a year, a permanent place, and a wife--luckless fellow!

[040-114]Ah, dearest, how I long for the rue de Grenelle! Fifteen days of

[040-115]absence! when they do not kill love, they revive all the ardor of

[040-116]its earlier days, and you know, better than I, perhaps, the

[040-117]reasons that make my love eternal,--my bones will love thee in the

[040-118]grave! Ah! I cannot bear this separation. If I am forced to stay

[040-119]here another ten days, I shall make a flying visit of a few hours

[040-120]to Paris.

[040-121]Has the duke obtained for me the thing we wanted; and shall you,

[040-122]my dearest life, be ordered to drink the Baden waters next year?

[040-123]The billing and cooing of the “handsome disconsolate,” compared

[040-124]with the accents of our happy love--so true and changeless for now

[040-125]ten years!--have given me a great contempt for [[marriage]]. I had

[040-126]never seen the thing so near. Ah, dearest! what the world calls a

[040-127]“false step” brings two beings nearer together than the law--does

[040-128]it not?

[040-129]The concluding idea served as a text for two pages of reminiscences and

[040-130]aspirations a little too confidential for publication.

[040-131]The evening before the day on which Canalis put the above epistle into

[040-132]the post, Butscha, under the name of Jean Jacmin, had received a letter

[040-133]from his fictitious cousin, Philoxene, and had mailed his answer, which

[040-134]thus preceded the letter of the poet by about twelve hours. Terribly

[040-135]anxious for the last two weeks, and wounded by Melchior’s silence,

[040-136]the duchess herself dictated Philoxene’s letter to her cousin, and

[040-137]the moment she had read the answer, rather too explicit for her

[040-138]quinquagenary vanity, she sent for the banker and made close inquiries

[040-139]as to the exact fortune of Monsieur Mignon. Finding herself betrayed and

[040-140]abandoned for the millions, Eleonore gave way to a paroxysm of anger,

[040-141]hatred, and cold vindictiveness. Philoxene knocked at the door of the

[040-142]sumptuous room, and entering found her mistress with her eyes full

[040-143]of tears,--so unprecedented a phenomenon in the fifteen years she had

[040-144]waited upon her that the woman stopped short stupefied.

[040-145]“We expiate the happiness of ten years in ten minutes,” she heard the

[040-146]duchess say.

[040-147]“A letter from Havre, madame.”

[040-148]Eleonore read the poet’s prose without noticing the presence of

[040-149]Philoxene, whose amazement became still greater when she saw the dawn

[040-150]of fresh serenity on the duchess’s face as she read further and further

[040-151]into the letter. Hold out a pole no thicker than a walking-stick to

[040-152]a drowning man, and he will think it a high-road of safety. The happy

[040-153]Eleonore believed in Canalis’s good faith when she had read through the

[040-154]four pages in which love and business, falsehood and truth, jostled

[040-155]each other. She who, a few moments earlier, had sent for her husband

[040-156]to prevent Melchior’s appointment while there was still time, was now

[040-157]seized with a spirit of generosity that amounted almost to the sublime.

[040-158]“Poor fellow!” she thought; “he has not had one faithless thought; he

[040-159]loves me as he did on the first day; he tells me all--Philoxene!”

[040-160]she cried, noticing her maid, who was standing near and pretending to

[040-161]arrange the toilet-table.

[040-162]“Madame la duchesse?”

[040-163]“A mirror, child!”

[040-164]Eleonore looked at herself, saw the fine razor-like lines traced on her

[040-165]brow, which disappeared at a little distance; she sighed, and in that

[040-166]sigh she felt she bade adieu to love. A brave thought came into her

[040-167]mind, a manly thought, outside of all the pettiness of women,--a

[040-168]thought which intoxicates for a moment, and which explains, perhaps,

[040-169]the clemency of the Semiramis of Russia when she married her young and

[040-170]beautiful rival to Momonoff.

[040-171]“Since he has not been faithless, he shall have the girl and her

[040-172]millions,” she thought,--“provided Mademoiselle Mignon is as ugly as he

[040-173]says she is.”

[040-174]Three raps, circumspectly given, announced the duke, and his wife went

[040-175]herself to the door to let him in.

[040-176]“Ah! I see you are better, my dear,” he cried, with the counterfeit

[040-177]joy that courtiers assume so readily, and by which fools are so readily

[040-178]taken in.

[040-179]“My dear Henri,” she answered, “why is it you have not yet obtained that

[040-180]appointment for Melchior,--you who sacrificed so much to the king in

[040-181]taking a ministry which you knew could only last one year.”

[040-182]The duke glanced at Philoxene, who showed him by an almost imperceptible

[040-183]sign the letter from Havre on the dressing-table.

[040-184]“You would be terribly bored at Baden and come back at daggers drawn

[040-185]with Melchior,” said the duke.

[040-186]“Pray why?”

[040-187]“Why, you would always be together,” said the former diplomat, with

[040-188]comic good-humor.

[040-189]“Oh, no,” she said; “I am going to marry him.”

[040-190]“If we can believe d’Herouville, our dear Canalis stands in no need

[040-191]of your help in that direction,” said the duke, smiling. “Yesterday

[040-192]Grandlieu read me some passages from a letter the grand equerry had

[040-193]written him. No doubt they were dictated by the aunt for the express

[040-194]purpose of their reaching you, for Mademoiselle d’Herouville, always on

[040-195]the scent of a ‘dot,’ knows that Grandlieu and I play whist nearly every

[040-196]evening. That good little d’Herouville wants the Prince de Cadignan to

[040-197]go down and give a royal hunt in Normandy, and endeavor to persuade the

[040-198]king to be present, so as to turn the head of the damozel when she sees

[040-199]herself the object of such a grand affair. In short, two words from

[040-200]Charles X. would settle the matter. D’Herouville says the girl has

[040-201]incomparable beauty--”
