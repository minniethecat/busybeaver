# Social Mobility

Social mobility in *Modeste Mignon* is achieved through commerce, marriage, and talent — three paths that Balzac examines with characteristic attention to the interplay of money, rank, and merit.

## Definition

The novel presents social mobility as the central anxiety of Restoration France. Charles Mignon, though of noble extraction ("de La Bastie"), makes his fortune through commerce in the East — a path that mingles aristocratic blood with mercantile wealth (004-039). His daughter Modeste's marriage prospects are evaluated entirely in financial terms: "an heiress worth a million will be as rare as generosity in a money-lender" (038-008). The novel's world is one where birth, wealth, and talent compete for precedence, and where the outcome of that competition determines individual fates.

## In the Novel

### Manifestations

Charles Mignon's trajectory exemplifies mobility through commerce: he loses his fortune, goes into exile, rebuilds his wealth through trade, and returns to reclaim his social position. Dumay, by contrast, achieves his position through military loyalty rather than commerce — he is "a former lieutenant in the Guard" (003-046) whose social standing derives entirely from his attachment to the Mignon family.

The marriage market is the novel's primary mechanism of social mobility. The Duc d'Hérouville, despite his rank, seeks Modeste's fortune to restore his family's position. Canalis, a celebrated poet, weighs Modeste's wealth against his literary fame. La Brière, a functionary without fortune, represents the path of merit — his value lies in character rather than wealth or title.

## In Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*

Social mobility is one of Balzac's master themes, treated tragically in *Le Père Goriot* and *Lost Illusions*. In *Modeste Mignon*, the treatment is comic — the right people end up with the right partners, and mobility serves to correct rather than destroy. This optimistic resolution is rare in Balzac and marks the novel's generic identity as romantic comedy.
