# Section 38

[038-001]“In twenty years, thanks to that Code, which pillages fortunes under

[038-002]what they call ‘Successions,’ an heiress worth a million will be as rare

[038-003]as generosity in a money-lender. Suppose [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] does want to spend all

[038-004]the interest of her own money,--well, she is so pretty, so sweet and

[038-005]pretty; why she’s--you poets are always after metaphors--she’s a weasel

[038-006]as tricky as a monkey.”

[038-007]“How came you to tell me she had six millions?” said [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] to La

[038-008]Briere, in a low voice.

[038-009]“My friend,” said [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], “I do assure you that I was bound to silence

[038-010]by an oath; perhaps, even now, I ought not to say as much as that.”

[038-011]“Bound! to whom?”

[038-012]“To Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]].”

[038-013]“Ernest! you who know how essential [[commerce|fortune]] is to me--”

[038-014]Butscha snored.

[038-015]“--who know my situation, and all that I shall lose in the Duchesse de

[038-016]Chaulieu, by this attempt at marrying, YOU could coldly let me plunge

[038-017]into such a thing as this?” exclaimed Canalis, turning pale. “It was a

[038-018]question of friendship; and ours was a compact entered into long before

[038-019]you ever saw that crafty Mignon.”

[038-020]“My dear fellow,” said Ernest, “I [[love]] Modeste too well to--”

[038-021]“Fool! then take her,” cried the [[poetry|poet]], “and break your oath.”

[038-022]“Will you promise me on your word of [[honor]] to forget what I now tell

[038-023]you, and to behave to me as though this confidence had never been made,

[038-024]whatever happens?”

[038-025]“I’ll swear that, by my mother’s memory.”

[038-026]“Well then,” said La Briere, “Monsieur Mignon told me in [[paris|Paris]] that he

[038-027]was very far from having the colossal [[social-mobility|fortune]] which the Mongenods told

[038-028]me about and which I mentioned to you. The colonel intends to give two

[038-029]hundred thousand francs to his daughter. And now, Melchior, I ask you,

[038-030]was the father really distrustful of us, as you thought; or was he

[038-031]sincere? It is not for me to answer those questions. If Modeste without

[038-032]a fortune deigns to choose me, she will be my wife.”

[038-033]“A blue-stocking! educated till she is a terror! a girl who has read

[038-034]everything, who knows everything,--in theory,” cried Canalis, hastily,

[038-035]noticing La Briere’s gesture, “a spoiled child, brought up in luxury in

[038-036]her childhood, and weaned of it for five years. Ah! my poor friend, take

[038-037]care what you are about.”

[038-038]“Ode and Code,” said Butscha, waking up, “you do the ode and I the code;

[038-039]there’s only a C’s difference between us. Well, now, code comes from

[038-040]‘coda,’ a tail,--mark that word! See here! a bit of good advice is worth

[038-041]your wine and your cream of tea. Father Mignon--he’s cream, too; the

[038-042]cream of honest men--he is going with his daughter on this riding party;

[038-043]do you go up frankly and talk ‘dot’ to him. He’ll answer plainly, and

[038-044]you’ll get at the truth, just as surely as I’m drunk, and you’re a great

[038-045]poet,--but no matter for that; we are to leave [[provincial-life|Havre]] together, that’s

[038-046]settled, isn’t it? I’m to be your secretary in place of that little

[038-047]fellow who sits there grinning at me and thinking I’m drunk. Come, let’s

[038-048]go, and leave him to marry the girl.”

[038-049]Canalis rose to leave the room to dress for the excursion.

[038-050]“Hush, not a word,--he is going to commit suicide,” whispered Butscha,

[038-051]sober as a judge, to La Briere as he made the gesture of a street boy

[038-052]at Canalis’s back. “Adieu, my chief!” he shouted, in stentorian tones,

[038-053]“will you allow me to take a snooze in that kiosk down in the garden?”

[038-054]“Make yourself at home,” answered the poet.

[038-055]Butscha, pursued by the laughter of the three servants of the

[038-056]establishment, gained the kiosk by walking over the flower-beds and

[038-057]round the vases with the perverse grace of an insect describing its

[038-058]interminable zig-zags as it tries to get out of a closed window. When he

[038-059]had clambered into the kiosk, and the servants had retired, he sat down

[038-060]on a wooden bench and wallowed in the delights of his triumph. He had

[038-061]completely fooled a great man; he had not only torn off his mask, but

[038-062]he had made him untie the strings himself; and he laughed like an author

[038-063]over his own play,--that is to say, with a true sense of the immense

[038-064]value of his “vis comica.”

[038-065]“Men are tops!” he cried, “you’ve only to find the twine to wind ‘em

[038-066]up with. But I’m like my fellows,” he added, presently. “I should faint

[038-067]away if any one came and said to me ‘Mademoiselle Modeste has been

[038-068]thrown from her horse, and has broken her leg.’”

[038-069]CHAPTER XXIV. THE POET FEELS THAT HE IS LOVED TOO WELL

[038-070]An hour later, Modeste, charmingly equipped in a bottle-green cassimere

[038-071]habit, a small hat with a green veil, buckskin gloves, and velvet boots

[038-072]which met the lace frills of her drawers, and mounted on an elegantly

[038-073]caparisoned little horse, was exhibiting to her father and the Duc

[038-074]d’Herouville the beautiful present she had just received; she was

[038-075]evidently delighted with an attention of a kind that particularly

[038-076]flatters women.

[038-077]“Did it come from you, Monsieur le duc?” she said, holding the sparkling

[038-078]handle toward him. “There was a card with it, saying, ‘Guess if you

[038-079]can,’ and some asterisks. Francoise and [[dumay|Dumay]] credit Butscha with this

[038-080]charming surprise; but my dear Butscha is not rich enough to buy such

[038-081]rubies. And as for papa (to whom I said, as I remember, on Sunday

[038-082]evening, that I had no whip), he sent to Rouen for this one,”--pointing

[038-083]to a whip in her father’s hand, with a top like a cone of turquoise, a

[038-084]fashion then in vogue which has since become vulgar.

[038-085]“I would give ten years of my old age, mademoiselle, to have the right

[038-086]to offer you that beautiful jewel,” said the duke, courteously.

[038-087]“Ah, here comes the audacious giver!” cried Modeste, as Canalis rode

[038-088]up. “It is only a poet who knows where to find such choice things.

[038-089]Monsieur,” she said to Melchior, “my father will scold you, and say that

[038-090]you justify those who accuse you of extravagance.”

[038-091]“Oh!” exclaimed Canalis, with apparent simplicity, “so that is why La

[038-092]Briere rode at full gallop from Havre to Paris?”

[038-093]“Does your secretary take such liberties?” said Modeste, turning pale,

[038-094]and throwing the whip to Francoise with an impetuosity that expressed

[038-095]scorn. “Give me your whip, papa.”

[038-096]“Poor Ernest, who lies there on his bed half-dead with fatigue!” said

[038-097]Canalis, overtaking the girl, who had already started at a gallop. “You

[038-098]are pitiless, mademoiselle. ‘I have’ (the poor fellow said to me) ‘only

[038-099]this one chance to remain in her memory.’”

[038-100]“And should you think well of a woman who could take presents from half

[038-101]the parish?” said Modeste.

[038-102]She was surprised to receive no answer to this inquiry, and attributed

[038-103]the poet’s inattention to the noise of the horse’s feet.

[038-104]“How you delight in tormenting those who love you,” said the duke. “Your

[038-105]nobility of soul and your pride are so inconsistent with your faults

[038-106]that I begin to suspect you calumniate yourself, and do those naughty

[038-107]things on purpose.”

[038-108]“Ah! have you only just found that out, Monsieur le duc?” she exclaimed,

[038-109]laughing. “You have the sagacity of a husband.”

[038-110]They rode half a mile in silence. Modeste was a good deal astonished not

[038-111]to receive the fire of the poet’s eyes. The evening before, as she was

[038-112]pointing out to him an admirable effect of setting sunlight across the

[038-113]water, she had said, remarking his inattention, “Well, don’t you see

[038-114]it?”--to which he replied, “I can see only your hand”; but now his

[038-115]admiration for the beauties of nature seemed a little too intense to be

[038-116]natural.

[038-117]“Does Monsieur de La Briere know how to ride?” she asked, for the

[038-118]purpose of teasing him.

[038-119]“Not very well, but he gets along,” answered the poet, cold as Gobenheim

[038-120]before the colonel’s return.

[038-121]At a cross-road, which Monsieur Mignon made them take through a lovely

[038-122]valley to reach a height overlooking the Seine, Canalis let Modeste and

[038-123]the duke pass him, and then reined up to join the colonel.

[038-124]“Monsieur le comte,” he said, “you are an open-hearted soldier, and

[038-125]I know you will regard my frankness as a title to your esteem. When

[038-126]proposals of [[marriage]], with all their brutal,--or, if you please, too

[038-127]civilized--discussions, are carried on by third parties, it is an injury

[038-128]to all. We are both gentlemen, and both discreet; and you, like myself,

[038-129]have passed beyond the age of surprises. Let us therefore speak as

[038-130]intimates. I will set you the example. I am twenty-nine years old,

[038-131]without landed estates, and full of ambition. Mademoiselle Modeste,

[038-132]as you must have perceived, pleases me extremely. Now, in spite of the

[038-133]little defects which your dear girl likes to assume--”

[038-134]“--not counting those she really possesses,” said the colonel,

[038-135]smiling,--

[038-136]“--I should gladly make her my wife, and I believe I could render her

[038-137]happy. The question of money is of the utmost importance to my future,

[038-138]which hangs to-day in the balance. All young girls expect to be loved

[038-139]_whether or no_--fortune or no fortune. But you are not the man to marry

[038-140]your dear Modeste without a ‘dot,’ and my situation does not allow me

[038-141]to make a marriage of what is called love unless with a woman who has

[038-142]a fortune at least equal to mine. I have, from my emoluments and

[038-143]sinecures, from the Academy and from my works, about thirty thousand

[038-144]francs a year, a large income for a bachelor. If my wife brought me as

[038-145]much more, I should still be in about the same condition that I am now.

[038-146]Shall you give Mademoiselle a million?”

[038-147]“Ah, monsieur, we have not reached that point as yet,” said the colonel,

[038-148]Jesuitically.

[038-149]“Then suppose,” said Canalis, quickly, “that we go no further; we will

[038-150]let the matter drop. You shall have no cause to complain of me, Monsieur

[038-151]le comte; the world shall consider me among the unfortunate suitors of

[038-152]your charming daughter. Give me your word of honor to say nothing on

[038-153]the subject to any one, not even to Mademoiselle Modeste, because,” he

[038-154]added, throwing a word of promise to the ear, “my circumstances may so

[038-155]change that I can ask you for her without ‘dot.’”

[038-156]“I promise you that,” said the colonel. “You know, monsieur, with what

[038-157]assurance the public, both in Paris and the provinces, talk of fortunes

[038-158]that they make and unmake. People exaggerate both happiness and

[038-159]unhappiness; we are never so fortunate nor so unfortunate as people say

[038-160]we are. There is nothing sure and certain in business except investments

[038-161]in land. I am awaiting the accounts of my agents with very great

[038-162]impatience. The sale of my merchandise and my ship, and the settlement

[038-163]of my affairs in China, are not yet concluded; and I cannot know the

[038-164]full amount of my fortune for at least six months. I did, however, say

[038-165]to Monsieur de La Briere in Paris that I would guarantee a ‘dot’ of two

[038-166]hundred thousand francs in ready money. I wish to entail my estates, and

[038-167]enable my grandchildren to inherit my arms and title.”

[038-168]Canalis did not listen to this statement after the opening sentence.

[038-169]The four riders, having now reached a wider road, went abreast and soon

[038-170]reached a stretch of table-land, from which the eye took in on one side

[038-171]the rich valley of the Seine toward Rouen, and on the other an horizon

[038-172]bounded only by the sea.

[038-173]“Butscha was right, God is the greatest of all landscape painters,” said

[038-174]Canalis, contemplating the view, which is unique among the many fine

[038-175]scenes that have made the shores of the Seine so justly celebrated.

[038-176]“Above all do we feel that, my dear baron,” said the duke, “on

[038-177]hunting-days, when nature has a voice, and a lively tumult breaks

[038-178]the silence; at such times the landscape, changing rapidly as we ride

[038-179]through it, seems really sublime.”
