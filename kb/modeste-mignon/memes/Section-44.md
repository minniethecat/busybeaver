# Section 44

[044-001]“Gaspard,” said the duchess, calling her son to her. The young

[044-002]prince came at once, and his mother continued, motioning to [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]],

[044-003]“Mademoiselle de La Bastie, my friend.”

[044-004]The heir presumptive, whose [[marriage]] with Desplein’s only daughter had

[044-005]lately been arranged, bowed to the young girl without seeming struck,

[044-006]as his father had been, with her beauty. Modeste was thus enabled to

[044-007]compare the youth of to-day with the old age of a past epoch; for the

[044-008]old Prince de Cadignan had already said a few words which made her feel

[044-009]that he rendered as true a homage to womanhood as to royalty. The Duc de

[044-010]Rhetore, the eldest son of the Duchesse de Chaulieu, chiefly remarkable

[044-011]for manners that were equally impertinent and free and easy, bowed

[044-012]to Modeste rather cavalierly. The reason of this contrast between the

[044-013]fathers and the sons is to be found, probably, in the fact that young

[044-014]men no longer feel themselves great beings, as their forefathers did,

[044-015]and they dispense with the duties of greatness, knowing well that they

[044-016]are now but the shadow of it. The fathers retain the inherent politeness

[044-017]of their vanished grandeur, like the mountain-tops still gilded by the

[044-018]sun when all is twilight in the valley.

[044-019][[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] was at last able to slip a word into Modeste’s ear, and she rose

[044-020]immediately.

[044-021]“My dear,” said the duchesse, thinking she was going to dress, and

[044-022]pulling a bell-rope, “they shall show you your apartment.”

[044-023]Ernest accompanied Modeste to the foot of the grand staircase,

[044-024]presenting the request of the luckless [[poetry|poet]], and endeavoring to touch

[044-025]her feelings by describing Melchior’s agony.

[044-026]“You see, he loves--he is a captive who thought he could break his

[044-027]chain.”

[044-028]“[[love|Love]] in such a rapid seeker after [[commerce|fortune]]!” retorted Modeste.

[044-029]“Mademoiselle, you are at the entrance of life; you do not know its

[044-030]defiles. The inconsistencies of a man who falls under the dominion of a

[044-031]woman much older than himself should be forgiven, for he is really not

[044-032]accountable. Think how many sacrifices [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] has made to her. He

[044-033]has sown too much seed of that kind to resign the harvest; the duchess

[044-034]represents to him ten years of devotion and happiness. You made him

[044-035]forget all that, and unfortunately, he has more vanity than pride; he

[044-036]did not reflect on what he was losing until he met Madame Chaulieu here

[044-037]to-day. If you really understood him, you would help him. He is a child,

[044-038]always mismanaging his life. You call him a seeker after [[social-mobility|fortune]], but

[044-039]he seeks very badly; like all poets, he is a victim of sensations; he

[044-040]is childish, easily dazzled like a child by anything that shines, and

[044-041]pursuing its glitter. He used to love horses and pictures, and he craved

[044-042]fame,--well, he sold his pictures to buy armor and old furniture of the

[044-043]Renaissance and Louis XV.; just now he is seeking political power. Admit

[044-044]that his hobbies are noble things.”

[044-045]“You have said enough,” replied Modeste; “come,” she added, seeing her

[044-046]father, whom she called with a motion of her head to give her his arm;

[044-047]“come with me, and I will give you that scrap of paper; you shall carry

[044-048]it to the great man and assure him of my condescension to his wishes,

[044-049]but on one condition,--you must thank him in my name for the pleasure I

[044-050]have taken in seeing one of the finest of the German plays performed in

[044-051]my [[honor]]. I have learned that Goethe’s masterpiece is neither Faust

[044-052]nor Egmont--” and then, as Ernest looked at the malicious girl with a

[044-053]puzzled air, she added: “It is Torquato Tasso! Tell Monsieur de Canalis

[044-054]to re-read it,” she added smiling; “I particularly desire that you

[044-055]will repeat to your friend word for word what I say; for it is not an

[044-056]epigram, it is the justification of his conduct,--with this trifling

[044-057]difference, that he will, I trust, become more and more reasonable,

[044-058]thanks to the folly of his Eleonore.”

[044-059]The duchess’s head-woman conducted Modeste and her father to their

[044-060]apartment, where Francoise Cochet had already put everything in order,

[044-061]and the choice elegance of which astounded the colonel, more especially

[044-062]after he heard from Francoise that there were thirty other apartments in

[044-063]the chateau decorated with the same taste.

[044-064]“This is what I call a proper country-house,” said Modeste.

[044-065]“The Comte de La Bastie must build you one like it,” replied her father.

[044-066]“Here, monsieur,” said Modeste, giving the bit of paper to Ernest;

[044-067]“carry it to our friend and put him out of his misery.”

[044-068]The word _our_ friend struck the young man’s heart. He looked at Modeste

[044-069]to see if there was anything real in the community of interests which

[044-070]she seemed to admit, and she, understanding perfectly what his look

[044-071]meant, added, “Come, go at once, your friend is waiting.”

[044-072]La Briere colored excessively, and left the room in a state of doubt and

[044-073]anxiety less endurable than despair. The path that approaches happiness

[044-074]is, to the true lover, like the narrow way which Catholic poetry has

[044-075]called the entrance to Paradise,--expressing thus a dark and gloomy

[044-076]passage, echoing with the last cries of earthly anguish.

[044-077]An hour later this illustrious company were all assembled in the

[044-078]salon; some were playing whist, others conversing; the women had their

[044-079]embroideries in hand, and all were waiting the announcement of dinner.

[044-080]The Prince de Cadignan was drawing Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] out upon China,

[044-081]and his campaigns under the empire, and making him talk about the

[044-082]Portendueres, the L’Estorades, and the Maucombes, Provencal families; he

[044-083]blamed him for not seeking service, and assured him that nothing would

[044-084]be easier than to restore him to his rank as colonel of the Guard.

[044-085]“A man of your birth and your fortune ought not to belong to the present

[044-086]Opposition,” said the prince, smiling.

[044-087]This society of distinguished persons not only pleased Modeste, but it

[044-088]enabled her to acquire, during her stay, a perfection of manners which

[044-089]without this revelation she would have lacked all her life. Show a clock

[044-090]to an embryo mechanic, and you reveal to him the whole mechanism; he

[044-091]thus develops the germs of his faculty which lie dormant within him.

[044-092]In like manner Modeste had the instinct to appropriate the distinctive

[044-093]qualities of Madame de Maufrigneuse and Madame de Chaulieu. For her, the

[044-094]sight of these women was an education; whereas a [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] would merely

[044-095]have ridiculed their ways or made them absurd by clumsy imitation. A

[044-096]well-born, well-educated, and right-minded young woman like Modeste

[044-097]fell naturally into connection with these people, and saw at once the

[044-098]differences that separate the aristocratic world from the bourgeois

[044-099]world, the provinces from the faubourg Saint-Germain; she caught the

[044-100]almost imperceptible shadings; in short, she perceived the grace of the

[044-101]“grande dame” without doubting that she could herself acquire it. She

[044-102]noticed also that her father and La Briere appeared infinitely better

[044-103]in this Olympus than Canalis. The great poet, abdicating his real

[044-104]and incontestable power, that of the mind, became nothing more than

[044-105]a courtier seeking a ministry, intriguing for an order, and forced to

[044-106]please the whole galaxy. Ernest de La Briere, without ambitions, was

[044-107]able to be himself; while Melchior became, to use a vulgar expression,

[044-108]a mere toady, and courted the Prince de Loudon, the Duc de Rhetore, the

[044-109]Vicomte de Serizy, or the Duc de Maufrigneuse, like a man not free

[044-110]to assert himself, as did Colonel Mignon, who was justly proud of his

[044-111]campaigns, and of the confidence of the Emperor Napoleon. Modeste took

[044-112]note of the strained efforts of the man of real talent, seeking some

[044-113]witticism that should raise a laugh, some clever speech, some compliment

[044-114]with which to flatter these grand personages, whom it was his interest

[044-115]to please. In a word, to Modeste’s eyes the peacock plucked out his

[044-116]tail-feathers.

[044-117]Toward the middle of the evening the young girl sat down with the grand

[044-118]equerry in a corner of the salon. She led him there purposely to end

[044-119]a suit which she could no longer encourage if she wished to retain her

[044-120]self-respect.

[044-121]“Monsieur le duc, if you really knew me,” she said, “you would

[044-122]understand how deeply I am touched by your attentions. It is because of

[044-123]the profound respect I feel for your character, and the friendship which

[044-124]a soul like yours inspires in mine, that I cannot endure to wound your

[044-125]self-love. Before your arrival in [[provincial-life|Havre]] I loved sincerely, deeply, and

[044-126]forever, one who is worthy of being loved, and my affection for whom

[044-127]is still a secret; but I wish you to know--and in saying this I am

[044-128]more sincere than most young girls--that had I not already formed this

[044-129]voluntary attachment, you would have been my choice, for I recognize

[044-130]your noble and beautiful qualities. A few words which your aunt and

[044-131]sister have said to me as to your intentions lead me to make this frank

[044-132]avowal. If you think it desirable, a letter from my mother shall recall

[044-133]me, on pretence of her illness, to-morrow morning before the hunt

[044-134]begins. Without your consent I do not choose to be present at a fete

[044-135]which I owe to your kindness, and where, if my secret should escape me,

[044-136]you might feel hurt and defrauded. You will ask me why I have come here

[044-137]at all. I could not withstand the invitation. Be generous enough not to

[044-138]reproach me for what was almost a necessary curiosity. But this is not

[044-139]the chief, not the most delicate thing I have to say to you. You have

[044-140]firm friends in my father and myself,--more so than perhaps you realize;

[044-141]and as my fortune was the first cause that brought you to me, I wish

[044-142]to say--but without intending to use it as a sedative to calm the grief

[044-143]which gallantry requires you to testify--that my father has thought

[044-144]over the affair of the marshes, his friend [[dumay|Dumay]] thinks your project

[044-145]feasible, and they have already taken steps to form a company.

[044-146]Gobenheim, Dumay, and my father have subscribed fifteen hundred thousand

[044-147]francs, and undertake to get the rest from capitalists, who will feel

[044-148]it in their interest to take up the matter. If I have not the honor

[044-149]of becoming the Duchesse d’Herouville, I have almost the certainty of

[044-150]enabling you to choose her, free from all trammels in your choice,

[044-151]and in a higher sphere than mine. Oh! let me finish,” she cried, at a

[044-152]gesture from the duke.

[044-153]“Judging by my nephew’s emotion,” whispered Mademoiselle d’Herouville to

[044-154]her niece, “it is easy to see you have a sister.”

[044-155]“Monsieur le duc, all this was settled in my mind the day of our first

[044-156]ride, when I heard you deplore your situation. This is what I have

[044-157]wished to say to you. That day determined my future life. Though you

[044-158]did not make the conquest of a woman, you have at least gained faithful

[044-159]friends at [[ingouville|Ingouville]]--if you will deign to accord us that title.”

[044-160]This little discourse, which Modeste had carefully thought over,

[044-161]was said with so much charm of soul that the tears came to the grand

[044-162]equerry’s eyes; he seized her hand and kissed it.

[044-163]“Stay during the hunt,” he said; “my want of merit has accustomed me

[044-164]to these refusals; but while accepting your friendship and that of the

[044-165]colonel, you must let me satisfy myself by the judgment of competent

[044-166]scientific men, that the draining of those marshes will be no risk to

[044-167]the company you speak of, before I agree to the generous offer of your

[044-168]friends. You are a noble girl, and though my heart aches to think I can

[044-169]only be your friend, I will glory in that title, and prove it to you at

[044-170]all times and in all seasons.”

[044-171]“In that case, Monsieur le duc, let us keep our secret. My choice will

[044-172]not be known, at least I think not, until after my mother’s complete

[044-173]recovery. I should like our first blessing to come from her eyes.”
