# Section 35

[035-001]Then, practising a well-known manoeuvre peculiar to her sex, Helene

[035-002]d’Herouville repeated in a low voice all the calumnies which women

[035-003]jealous of the Duchesse de Chaulieu were in the habit of spreading about

[035-004][[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]]. This little incident, common as it is in the intercourse of

[035-005]women, will serve to show with what fury the hounds were after [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s

[035-006]wealth.

[035-007]Ten days saw a great change in the opinions at [[chalet|the Chalet]] as to the

[035-008]three suitors for Mademoiselle de La Bastie’s hand. This change,

[035-009]which was much to the disadvantage of Canalis, came about through

[035-010]considerations of a nature which ought to make the holders of any kind

[035-011]of fame pause, and reflect. No one can deny, if we remember the passion

[035-012]with which people seek for autographs, that public curiosity is greatly

[035-013]excited by celebrity. Evidently most provincials never form an exact

[035-014]idea in their own minds of how illustrious Parisians put on their

[035-015]cravats, walk on the boulevards, stand gaping at nothing, or eat

[035-016]a cutlet; because, no sooner do they perceive a man clothed in the

[035-017]sunbeams of fashion or resplendent with some dignity that is more or

[035-018]less fugitive (though always envied), than they cry out, “Look at

[035-019]that!” “How queer!” and other depreciatory exclamations. In a word, the

[035-020]mysterious charm that attaches to every kind of fame, even that which

[035-021]is most justly due, never lasts. It is, and especially with superficial

[035-022]people who are envious or sarcastic, a sensation which passes off with

[035-023]the rapidity of lightning, and never returns. It would seem as though

[035-024]fame, like the sun, hot and luminous at a distance, is cold as the

[035-025]summit of an alp when you approach it. Perhaps man is only really great

[035-026]to his peers; perhaps the defects inherent in his constitution disappear

[035-027]sooner to the eyes of his equals than to those of vulgar admirers. A

[035-028][[poetry|poet]], if he would please in ordinary life, must put on the fictitious

[035-029]graces of those who are able to make their insignificances forgotten

[035-030]by charming manners and complying speeches. The poet of the faubourg

[035-031]Saint-Germain, who did not choose to bow before this social dictum, was

[035-032]made before long to feel that an insulting [[provincial-life|provincial]] indifference

[035-033]had succeeded to the dazed fascination of the earlier evenings. The

[035-034]prodigality of his wit and wisdom had produced upon these worthy souls

[035-035]somewhat the effect which a shopful of glass-ware produces on the eye;

[035-036]in other words, the fire and brilliancy of Canalis’s eloquence soon

[035-037]wearied people who, to use their own words, “cared more for the solid.”

[035-038]Forced after a while to behave like an ordinary man, the poet found an

[035-039]unexpected stumbling-block on ground where La Briere had already won the

[035-040]suffrage of the worthy people who at first had thought him sulky. They

[035-041]felt the need of compensating themselves for Canalis’s [[honor|reputation]] by

[035-042]preferring his friend. The best of men are influenced by such feelings

[035-043]as these. The simple and straightforward young fellow jarred no one’s

[035-044]self-[[love]]; coming to know him better they discovered his heart, his

[035-045]modesty, his silent and sure discretion, and his excellent bearing.

[035-046]The Duc d’Herouville considered him, as a political element, far above

[035-047]Canalis. The poet, ill-balanced, ambitious, and restless as Tasso,

[035-048]loved luxury, grandeur, and ran into debt; while the young lawyer,

[035-049]whose character was equable and well-balanced, lived soberly, was useful

[035-050]without proclaiming it, awaited rewards without begging for them, and

[035-051]laid by his money.

[035-052]Canalis had moreover laid himself open in a special way to the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]]

[035-053]eyes that were watching him. For two or three days he had shown signs

[035-054]of impatience; he had given way to depression, to states of melancholy

[035-055]without apparent reason, to those capricious changes of temper which

[035-056]are the natural results of the nervous temperament of poets. These

[035-057]originalities (we use the provincial word) came from the uneasiness

[035-058]that his conduct toward the Duchesse de Chaulieu which grew daily less

[035-059]explainable, caused him. He knew he ought to write to her, but could not

[035-060]resolve on doing so. All these fluctuations were carefully remarked

[035-061]and commented on by the gentle American, and the excellent Madame

[035-062]Latournelle, and they formed the topic of many a discussion between

[035-063]these two ladies and Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]]. Canalis felt the effects of these

[035-064]discussions without being able to explain them. The attention paid

[035-065]to him was not the same, the faces surrounding him no longer wore the

[035-066]entranced look of the earlier days; while at the same time [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] was

[035-067]evidently gaining ground.

[035-068]For the last two days the poet had endeavored to fascinate Modeste only,

[035-069]and he took advantage of every moment when he found himself alone with

[035-070]her, to weave the web of passionate language around his love. Modeste’s

[035-071]blush, as she listened to him on the occasion we have just mentioned,

[035-072]showed the demoiselles d’Herouville the pleasure with which she was

[035-073]listening to sweet conceits that were sweetly said; and they, horribly

[035-074]uneasy at the sight, had immediate recourse to the “ultima ratio” of

[035-075]women in such cases, namely, those calumnies which seldom miss their

[035-076]object. Accordingly, when the party met at the dinner-table the poet

[035-077]saw a cloud on the brow of his idol; he knew that Mademoiselle

[035-078]d’Herouville’s malignity allowed him to lose no time, and he resolved

[035-079]to offer himself as a husband at the first moment when he could find

[035-080]himself alone with Modeste.

[035-081]Overhearing a few acid though polite remarks exchanged between the poet

[035-082]and the two noble ladies, Gobenheim nudged Butscha with his elbow,

[035-083]and said in an undertone, motioning towards the poet and the grand

[035-084]equerry,--

[035-085]“They’ll demolish one another!”

[035-086]“Canalis has genius enough to demolish himself all alone,” answered the

[035-087]dwarf.

[035-088]CHAPTER XXII. A RIDDLE GUESSED

[035-089]During the dinner, which was magnificent and admirably well served, the

[035-090]duke obtained a signal advantage over Canalis. Modeste, who had received

[035-091]her habit and other equestrian equipments the night before, spoke of

[035-092]taking rides about the country. A turn of the conversation led her to

[035-093]express the wish to see a hunt with hounds, a pleasure she had never yet

[035-094]enjoyed. The duke at once proposed to arrange a hunt in one of the crown

[035-095]forests, which lay a few leagues from Havre. Thanks to his intimacy

[035-096]with the Prince de Cadignan, Master of the Hunt, he saw his chance of

[035-097]displaying an almost regal pomp before Modeste’s eyes, and alluring her

[035-098]with a glimpse of court fascinations, to which she could be introduced

[035-099]by [[marriage]]. Glances were exchanged between the duke and the two

[035-100]demoiselles d’Herouville, which plainly said, “The heiress is ours!”

[035-101]and the poet, who detected them, and who had nothing but his personal

[035-102]splendors to depend on, determined all the more firmly to obtain some

[035-103]pledge of affection at once. Modeste, on the other hand, half-frightened

[035-104]at being thus pushed beyond her intentions by the d’Herouvilles, walked

[035-105]rather markedly apart with Melchior, when the company adjourned to the

[035-106]park after dinner. With the pardonable curiosity of a young girl, she

[035-107]let him suspect the calumnies which Helene had poured into her ears; but

[035-108]on Canalis’s exclamation of anger, she begged him to keep silence about

[035-109]them, which he promised.

[035-110]“These stabs of the tongue,” he said, “are considered fair in the great

[035-111]world. They shock your upright nature; but as for me, I laugh at them; I

[035-112]am even pleased. These ladies must feel that the duke’s interests are in

[035-113]great peril, when they have recourse to such warfare.”

[035-114]Making the most of the advantage Modeste had thus given him, Canalis

[035-115]entered upon his defence with such warmth, such eagerness, and with a

[035-116]passion so exquisitely expressed, as he thanked her for a confidence in

[035-117]which he could venture to see the dawn of love, that she found herself

[035-118]suddenly as much compromised with the poet as she feared to be with the

[035-119]grand equerry. Canalis, feeling the necessity of prompt action, declared

[035-120]himself plainly. He uttered vows and protestations in which his poetry

[035-121]shone like a moon, invoked for the occasion, and illuminating his

[035-122]allusions to the beauty of his mistress and the charms of her evening

[035-123]dress. This counterfeit enthusiasm, in which the night, the foliage,

[035-124]the heavens and the earth, and Nature herself played a part, carried the

[035-125]eager lover beyond all bounds; for he dwelt on his disinterestedness,

[035-126]and revamped in his own charming style, Diderot’s famous apostrophe

[035-127]to “Sophie and fifteen hundred francs!” and the well-worn “love in

[035-128]a cottage” of every lover who knows perfectly well the length of the

[035-129]father-in-law’s purse.

[035-130]“Monsieur,” said Modeste, after listening with delight to the [[music|melody]] of

[035-131]this concerto; “the freedom granted to me by my parents has allowed me

[035-132]to listen to you; but it is to them that you must address yourself.”

[035-133]“But,” exclaimed Canalis, “tell me that if I obtain their consent, you

[035-134]will ask nothing better than to obey them.”

[035-135]“I know beforehand,” she replied, “that my father has certain fancies

[035-136]which may wound the proper pride of an old family like yours. He wishes

[035-137]to have his own title and name borne by his grandsons.”

[035-138]“Ah! dear Modeste, what sacrifices would I not make to commit my life to

[035-139]the guardian care of an angel like you.”

[035-140]“You will permit me not to decide in a moment the fate of my whole

[035-141]life,” she said, turning to rejoin the demoiselles d’Herouville.

[035-142]Those noble ladies were just then engaged in flattering the vanity

[035-143]of little Latournelle, intending to win him over to their interests.

[035-144]Mademoiselle d’Herouville, to whom we shall in future confine the family

[035-145]name, to distinguish her from her niece Helene, was giving the notary to

[035-146]understand that the post of judge of the Supreme Court in Havre, which

[035-147]Charles X. would bestow as she desired, was an office worthy of his

[035-148]legal talent and his well-known probity. Butscha, meanwhile, who had

[035-149]been walking about with La Briere, was greatly alarmed at the progress

[035-150]Canalis was evidently making, and he waylaid Modeste at the lower step

[035-151]of the portico when the whole party returned to the house to endure the

[035-152]torments of their inevitable whist.

[035-153]“Mademoiselle,” he said, in a low whisper, “I do hope you don’t call him

[035-154]Melchior.”

[035-155]“I’m very near it, my Black Dwarf,” she said, with a smile that might

[035-156]have made an angel swear.

[035-157]“Good God!” exclaimed Butscha, letting fall his hands, which struck the

[035-158]marble steps.

[035-159]“Well! and isn’t he worth more than that spiteful and gloomy secretary

[035-160]in whom you take such an interest?” she retorted, assuming, at the mere

[035-161]thought of Ernest, the haughty manner whose secret belongs exclusively

[035-162]to young girls,--as if their virginity lent them wings to fly to heaven.

[035-163]“Pray, would your little La Briere accept me without a [[commerce|fortune]]?” she

[035-164]said, after a pause.

[035-165]“Ask your father,” replied Butscha, who walked a few steps from the

[035-166]house, to get Modeste at a safe distance from the windows. “Listen to

[035-167]me, mademoiselle. You know that he who speaks to you is ready to give

[035-168]not only his life but his honor for you, at any moment, and at all

[035-169]times. Therefore you may believe in him; you can confide to him that

[035-170]which you may not, perhaps, be willing to say to your father. Tell me,

[035-171]has that sublime Canalis been making you the disinterested offer that

[035-172]you now fling as a reproach at poor Ernest?”

[035-173]“Yes.”

[035-174]“Do you believe it?”

[035-175]“That question, my manikin,” she replied, giving him one of the ten or

[035-176]a dozen nicknames she had invented for him, “strikes me as undervaluing

[035-177]the strength of my self-love.”

[035-178]“Ah, you are laughing, my dear Mademoiselle Modeste; then there’s no

[035-179]danger: I hope you are only making a fool of him.”

[035-180]“Pray what would you think of me, Monsieur Butscha, if I allowed myself

[035-181]to make fun of those who do me the honor to wish to marry me? You ought

[035-182]to know, master Jean, that even if a girl affects to despise the most

[035-183]despicable attentions, she is flattered by them.”
