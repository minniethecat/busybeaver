# Section 24

[024-001]“Among the plants whose flowers bloom in the sunshine of fame,” said

[024-002][[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], impressively, “there is one, and the most magnificent, which

[024-003]bears like the orange-tree a golden fruit amid the mingled perfumes of

[024-004]beauty and of mind; a lovely plant, a true tenderness, a perfect bliss,

[024-005]and--it eludes me.” Canalis looked at the carpet that [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] might

[024-006]not read his eyes. “Could I,” he continued after a pause to regain his

[024-007]self-possession, “how could I have divined that flower from a pretty

[024-008]sheet of perfumed paper, that true heart, that young girl, that woman in

[024-009]whom [[love]] wears the livery of flattery, who loves us for ourselves, who

[024-010]offers us felicity? It needed but an angel or a demon to perceive

[024-011]her; and what am I but the ambitious head of a Court of Claims! Ah, my

[024-012]friend, fame makes us the target of a thousand arrows. One of us

[024-013]owes his rich [[marriage]] to an hydraulic piece of [[poetry]], while I, more

[024-014]seductive, more a woman’s man than he, have missed mine,--for, do you

[024-015]love her, poor girl?” he said, looking up at La Briere.

[024-016]“Oh!” ejaculated the young man.

[024-017]“Well then,” said the poet, taking his secretary’s arm and leaning

[024-018]heavily upon it, “be happy, Ernest. By a mere accident I have been not

[024-019]ungrateful to you. You are richly rewarded for your devotion, and I will

[024-020]generously further your happiness.”

[024-021]Canalis was furious; but he could not behave otherwise than with

[024-022]propriety, and he made the best of his disappointment by mounting it as

[024-023]a pedestal.

[024-024]“Ah, Canalis, I have never really known you till this moment.”

[024-025]“Did you expect to? It takes some time to go round the world,” replied

[024-026]the poet with his pompous irony.

[024-027]“But think,” said La Briere, “of this enormous [[commerce|fortune]].”

[024-028]“Ah, my friend, is it not well invested in you?” cried Canalis,

[024-029]accompanying the words with a charming gesture.

[024-030]“Melchior,” said La Briere, “I am yours for life and death.”

[024-031]He wrung the poet’s hand and left him abruptly, for he was in haste to

[024-032]meet Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]].

[024-033]CHAPTER XV. A FATHER STEPS IN

[024-034]The Comte de La Bastie was at this moment overwhelmed with the sorrows

[024-035]which lay in wait for him as their prey. He had learned from his

[024-036]daughter’s letter of Bettina’s death and of his wife’s infirmity, and

[024-037][[dumay|Dumay]] related to him, when they met, his terrible perplexity as to

[024-038][[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]’s love affairs.

[024-039]“Leave me to myself,” he said to his faithful friend.

[024-040]As the lieutenant closed the door, the unhappy father threw himself on a

[024-041]sofa, with his head in his hands, weeping those slow, scanty tears which

[024-042]suffuse the eyes of a man of sixty, but do not fall,--tears soon dried,

[024-043]yet quick to start again,--the last dews of the human autumn.

[024-044]“To have children, to have a wife, to adore them--what is it but to have

[024-045]many hearts and bare them to a dagger?” he cried, springing up with the

[024-046]bound of a tiger and walking up and down the room. “To be a father is

[024-047]to give one’s self over, bound hand and foot to sorrow. If I meet that

[024-048]D’Estourny I will kill him. To have daughters!--one gives her life to a

[024-049]scoundrel, the other, my Modeste, falls a victim to whom? a coward, who

[024-050]deceives her with the gilded paper of a poet. If it were Canalis himself

[024-051]it might not be so bad; but that Scapin of a lover!--I will strangle him

[024-052]with my two hands,” he cried, making an involuntary gesture of furious

[024-053]determination. “And what then? suppose my Modeste were to die of grief?”

[024-054]He gazed mechanically out of the windows of the hotel des Princes, and

[024-055]then returned to the sofa, where he sat motionless. The fatigues of

[024-056]six voyages to India, the anxieties of speculation, the dangers he

[024-057]had encountered and evaded, and his many griefs, had silvered Charles

[024-058]Mignon’s head. His handsome soldierly face, so pure in outline and now

[024-059]bronzed by the suns of China and the southern seas, had acquired an air

[024-060]of dignity which his present grief rendered almost sublime.

[024-061]“Mongenod told me he felt confidence in the young man who is coming to

[024-062]ask me for my daughter,” he thought at last; and at this moment Ernest

[024-063]de La Briere was announced by one of the servants whom Monsieur de La

[024-064]Bastie had attached to himself during the last four years.

[024-065]“You have come, monsieur, from my friend Mongenod?” he said.

[024-066]“Yes,” replied Ernest, growing timid when he saw before him a face as

[024-067]sombre as Othello’s. “My name is Ernest de La Briere, related to the

[024-068]family of the late cabinet minister, and his private secretary during

[024-069]his term of office. On his dismissal, his Excellency put me in the Court

[024-070]of Claims, to which I am legal counsel, and where I may possibly succeed

[024-071]as chief--”

[024-072]“And how does all this concern Mademoiselle de La Bastie?” asked the

[024-073]count.

[024-074]“Monsieur, I love her; and I have the unhoped-for happiness of being

[024-075]loved by her. Hear me, monsieur,” cried Ernest, checking a violent

[024-076]movement on the part of the angry father. “I have the strangest

[024-077]confession to make to you, a shameful one for a man of [[honor]]; but the

[024-078]worst punishment of my conduct, natural enough in itself, is not

[024-079]the telling of it to you; no, I fear the daughter even more than the

[024-080]father.”

[024-081]Ernest then related simply, and with the nobleness that comes of

[024-082]sincerity, all the facts of his little drama, not omitting the twenty or

[024-083]more [[epistolary-novel|letters]], which he had brought with him, nor the interview which he

[024-084]had just had with Canalis. When Monsieur Mignon had finished reading the

[024-085]letters, the unfortunate lover, pale and suppliant, actually trembled

[024-086]under the fiery glance of the Provencal.

[024-087]“Monsieur,” said the latter, “in this whole matter there is but one

[024-088]error, but that is cardinal. My daughter will not have six millions;

[024-089]at the utmost, she will have a marriage portion of two hundred thousand

[024-090]francs, and very doubtful expectations.”

[024-091]“Ah, monsieur!” cried Ernest, rising and grasping Monsieur Mignon’s

[024-092]hand; “you take a load from my breast. Nothing can now hinder my

[024-093]happiness. I have friends, influence; I shall certainly be chief of

[024-094]the Court of Claims. Had Mademoiselle Mignon no more than ten thousand

[024-095]francs, if I had even to make a settlement on her, she should still be

[024-096]my wife; and to make her happy as you, monsieur, have made your wife

[024-097]happy, to be to you a real son (for I have no father), are the deepest

[024-098]desires of my heart.”

[024-099]Charles Mignon stepped back three paces and fixed upon La Briere a look

[024-100]which entered the eyes of the young man as a dagger enters its sheath;

[024-101]he stood silent a moment, recognizing the absolute candor, the pure

[024-102]truthfulness of that open nature in the light of the young man’s

[024-103]inspired eyes. “Is fate at last weary of pursuing me?” he asked himself.

[024-104]“Am I to find in this young man the pearl of sons-in-law?” He walked up

[024-105]and down the room in strong agitation.

[024-106]“Monsieur,” he said at last, “you are bound to submit wholly to the

[024-107]judgment which you have come here to seek, otherwise you are now playing

[024-108]a farce.”

[024-109]“Oh, monsieur!”

[024-110]“Listen to me,” said the father, nailing La Briere where he stood with a

[024-111]glance. “I shall be neither harsh, nor hard, nor unjust. You shall have

[024-112]the advantages and the disadvantages of the false position in which you

[024-113]have placed yourself. My daughter believes that she loves one of the

[024-114]great poets of the day, whose fame is really that which has attracted

[024-115]her. Well, I, her father, intend to give her the opportunity to choose

[024-116]between the celebrity which has been a beacon to her, and the poor

[024-117]reality which the irony of fate has flung at her feet. Ought she not

[024-118]to choose between Canalis and yourself? I rely upon your honor not to

[024-119]repeat what I have told you as to the state of my affairs. You may each

[024-120]come, I mean you and your friend the Baron de Canalis, to [[provincial-life|Havre]] for the

[024-121]last two weeks of October. My house will be open to both of you, and my

[024-122]daughter must have an opportunity to study you. You must yourself bring

[024-123]your rival, and not disabuse him as to the foolish tales he will hear

[024-124]about the wealth of the Comte de La Bastie. I go to Havre to-morrow, and

[024-125]I shall expect you three days later. Adieu, monsieur.”

[024-126]Poor La Briere went back to Canalis with a dragging step. The poet,

[024-127]meantime, left to himself, had given way to a current of thought out

[024-128]of which had come that secondary impulse which Monsieur de Talleyrand

[024-129]valued so much. The first impulse is the voice of nature, the second

[024-130]that of society.

[024-131]“A girl worth six millions,” he thought to himself, “and my eyes were

[024-132]not able to see that gold shining in the darkness! With such a [[social-mobility|fortune]]

[024-133]I could be peer of France, count, marquis, ambassador. I’ve replied

[024-134]to middle-class women and silly women, and crafty creatures who wanted

[024-135]autographs; I’ve tired myself to death with masked-ball intrigues,--at

[024-136]the very moment when God was sending me a soul of price, an angel with

[024-137]golden wings! Bah! I’ll make a poem on it, and perhaps the chance will

[024-138]come again. Heavens! the luck of that little La Briere,--strutting about

[024-139]in my lustre--plagiarism! I’m the cast and he’s to be the statue, is

[024-140]he? It is the old fable of Bertrand and Raton. Six millions, a beauty,

[024-141]a Mignon de La Bastie, an aristocratic divinity loving poetry and the

[024-142]poet! And I, who showed my muscle as man of the world, who did those

[024-143]Alcide exercises to silence by moral force the champion of physical

[024-144]force, that old soldier with a heart, that friend of this very young

[024-145]girl, whom he’ll now go and tell that I have a heart of iron!--I, to

[024-146]play Napoleon when I ought to have been seraphic! Good heavens! True, I

[024-147]shall have my friend. Friendship is a beautiful thing. I have kept him,

[024-148]but at what a price! Six millions, that’s the cost of it; we can’t have

[024-149]many friends if we pay all that for them.”

[024-150]La Briere entered the room as Canalis reached this point in his

[024-151]meditations. He was gloom personified.

[024-152]“Well, what’s the matter?” said Canalis.

[024-153]“The father exacts that his daughter shall choose between the two

[024-154]Canalis--”

[024-155]“Poor boy!” cried the poet, laughing, “he’s a clever fellow, that

[024-156]father.”

[024-157]“I have pledged my honor that I will take you to Havre,” said La Briere,

[024-158]piteously.

[024-159]“My dear fellow,” said Canalis, “if it is a question of your honor you

[024-160]may count on me. I’ll ask for leave of absence for a month.”

[024-161]“Modeste is so beautiful!” exclaimed La Briere, in a despairing tone.

[024-162]“You will crush me out of sight. I wondered all along that fate should

[024-163]be so kind to me; I knew it was all a mistake.”

[024-164]“Bah! we will see about that,” said Canalis with inhuman gaiety.

[024-165]That evening, after dinner, Charles Mignon and Dumay, were flying,

[024-166]by virtue of three francs to each postilion, from [[paris|Paris]] to Havre.

[024-167]The father had eased the watch-dog’s mind as to Modeste and her love

[024-168]affairs; the guard was relieved, and Butscha’s innocence established.

[024-169]“It is all for the best, my old Dumay,” said the count, who had been

[024-170]making certain inquiries of Mongenod respecting Canalis and La Briere.

[024-171]“We are going to have two actors for one part!” he cried gaily.

[024-172]Nevertheless, he requested his old comrade to be absolutely silent about

[024-173]the comedy which was now to be played at [[chalet|the Chalet]],--a comedy it might

[024-174]be, but also a gentle punishment, or, if you prefer it, a lesson given

[024-175]by the father to the daughter.

[024-176]The two friends kept up a long conversation all the way from Paris to

[024-177]Havre, which put the colonel in possession of the facts relating to his

[024-178]family during the past four years, and informing Dumay that Desplein,

[024-179]the great surgeon, was coming to Havre at the end of the present month

[024-180]to examine the cataract on Madame Mignon’s eyes, and decide if it were

[024-181]possible to restore her sight.
