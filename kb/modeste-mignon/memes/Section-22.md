# Section 22

[022-001]“What are you doing, up at this hour, [[modeste-mignon|Mademoiselle Modeste]]?” said the

[022-002]voice of [[dumay|Dumay]] at her door.

[022-003]“Writing to my father,” she answered; “did you not tell me you should

[022-004]start in the morning?”

[022-005]Dumay had nothing to say to that, and he went to bed, while Modeste

[022-006]wrote another long letter, this time to her father.

[022-007]On the morrow, Francois Cochet, terrified at seeing the [[provincial-life|Havre]] postmark

[022-008]on the envelope which [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] had mailed the night before, brought her

[022-009]young mistress the following letter and took away the one which Modeste

[022-010]had written:--

[022-011]To Mademoiselle O. d’Este M.,--My heart tells me that you were the

[022-012]woman so carefully veiled and disguised, and seated between

[022-013]Monsieur and Madame Latournelle, who have but one child, a son.

[022-014]Ah, my [[love]], if you have only a modest station, without

[022-015]distinction, without importance, without money even, you do not

[022-016]know how happy that would make me. You ought to understand me by

[022-017]this time; why will you not tell me the truth? I am no [[poetry|poet]],

[022-018]--except in heart, through love, through you. Oh! what power of

[022-019]affection there is in me to keep me here in this hotel, instead of

[022-020]mounting to [[ingouville|Ingouville]] which I can see from my windows. Will you

[022-021]ever love me as I love you? To leave Havre in such uncertainty! Am

[022-022]I not punished for loving you as if I had committed a crime? But I

[022-023]obey you blindly. Let me have a letter quickly, for if you have

[022-024]been mysterious, I have returned you mystery for mystery, and I

[022-025]must at last throw off my disguise, show you [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]] that I am,

[022-026]and abdicate my borrowed glory.

[022-027]This letter made Modeste terribly uneasy. She could not get back the

[022-028]one which Francoise had carried away before she came to the last words,

[022-029]whose meaning she now sought by reading them again and again; but

[022-030]she went to her own room and wrote an answer in which she demanded an

[022-031]immediate explanation.

[022-032]CHAPTER XIV. MATTERS GROWN COMPLICATED

[022-033]During these little events other little events were going on in Havre,

[022-034]which caused Modeste to forget her present uneasiness. Dumay went down

[022-035]to Havre early in the morning, and soon discovered that no architect had

[022-036]been in town the day before. Furious at Butscha’s lie, which revealed a

[022-037]conspiracy of which he was resolved to know the meaning, he rushed from

[022-038]the mayor’s office to his friend Latournelle.

[022-039]“Where’s your Master Butscha?” he demanded of the notary, when he saw

[022-040]that the clerk was not in his place.

[022-041]“Butscha, my dear fellow, has gone to [[paris|Paris]]. He heard some news of his

[022-042]father this morning on the quays, from a Swedish sailor. It seems the

[022-043]father went to the Indies and served a prince, or something, and he is

[022-044]now in Paris.”

[022-045]“Lies! it’s all a trick! infamous! I’ll find that damned cripple if I’ve

[022-046]got to go express to Paris for him,” cried Dumay. “Butscha is deceiving

[022-047]us; he knows something about Modeste, and hasn’t told us. If he meddles

[022-048]in this thing he shall never be a notary. I’ll roll him in the mud from

[022-049]which he came, I’ll--”

[022-050]“Come, come, my friend; never hang a man before you try him,” said

[022-051]Latournelle, frightened at Dumay’s rage.

[022-052]After stating the facts on which his suspicions were founded, Dumay

[022-053]begged Madame Latournelle to go and stay at [[chalet|the Chalet]] during his

[022-054]absence.

[022-055]“You will find the colonel in Paris,” said the notary. “In the shipping

[022-056]news quoted this morning in the Journal of [[commerce|Commerce]], I found under

[022-057]the head of Marseilles--here, see for yourself,” he said, offering the

[022-058]paper. “‘The Bettina [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], Captain Mignon, arrived October 6’; it is

[022-059]now the 17th, and the colonel is sure to be in Paris.”

[022-060]Dumay requested Gobenheim to do without him in future, and then went

[022-061]back to the Chalet, which he reached just as Modeste was sealing her two

[022-062][[epistolary-novel|letters]], to her father and Canalis. Except for the address the letters

[022-063]were precisely alike both in weight and appearance. Modeste thought she

[022-064]had laid that to her father over that to her Melchior, but had, in fact,

[022-065]done exactly the reverse. This mistake, so often made in the little

[022-066]things of life, occasioned the discovery of her secret by Dumay and her

[022-067]mother. The former was talking vehemently to Madame Mignon in the salon,

[022-068]and revealing to her his fresh fears caused by Modeste’s duplicity and

[022-069]Butscha’s connivance.

[022-070]“Madame,” he cried, “he is a serpent whom we have warmed in our bosoms;

[022-071]there’s no place in his contorted little body for a soul!”

[022-072]Modeste put the letter for her father into the pocket of her apron,

[022-073]supposing it to be that for Canalis, and came downstairs with the letter

[022-074]for her lover in her hand, to see Dumay before he started for Paris.

[022-075]“What has happened to my Black Dwarf? why are you talking so loud!” she

[022-076]said, appearing at the door.

[022-077]“Mademoiselle, Butscha has gone to Paris, and you, no doubt, know

[022-078]why,--to carry on that affair of the little architect with the sulphur

[022-079]waistcoat, who, unluckily for the hunchback’s lies, has never been

[022-080]here.”

[022-081]Modeste was struck dumb; feeling sure that the dwarf had departed on

[022-082]a mission of inquiry as to her poet’s morals, she turned pale, and sat

[022-083]down.

[022-084]“I’m going after him; I shall find him,” continued Dumay. “Is that the

[022-085]letter for your father, mademoiselle?” he added, holding out his hand.

[022-086]“I will take it to the Mongenods. God grant the colonel and I may not

[022-087]pass each other on the road.”

[022-088]Modeste gave him the letter. Dumay looked mechanically at the address.

[022-089]“‘Monsieur le Baron de Canalis, rue de Paradis-Poissoniere, No. 29’!” he

[022-090]cried out; “what does that mean?”

[022-091]“Ah, my daughter! that is the man you love,” exclaimed Madame Mignon;

[022-092]“the stanzas you set to [[music]] were his--”

[022-093]“And that’s his portrait that you have in a frame upstairs,” added

[022-094]Dumay.

[022-095]“Give me back that letter, Monsieur Dumay,” said Modeste, erecting

[022-096]herself like a lioness defending her cubs.

[022-097]“There it is, mademoiselle,” he replied.

[022-098]Modeste put it into the bosom of her dress, and gave Dumay the one

[022-099]intended for her father.

[022-100]“I know what you are capable of, Dumay,” she said; “and if you take

[022-101]one step against Monsieur de Canalis, I shall take another out of this

[022-102]house, to which I will never return.”

[022-103]“You will kill your mother, mademoiselle,” replied Dumay, who left the

[022-104]room and called his wife.

[022-105]The poor mother was indeed half-fainting,--struck to the heart by

[022-106]Modeste’s words.

[022-107]“Good-bye, wife,” said the Breton, kissing the American. “Take care of

[022-108]the mother; I go to save the daughter.”

[022-109]He made his preparations for the journey in a few minutes, and started

[022-110]for Havre. An hour later he was travelling post to Paris, with the haste

[022-111]that nothing but passion or speculation can get out of wheels.

[022-112]Recovering herself under Modeste’s tender care, Madame Mignon went up to

[022-113]her bedroom leaning on the arm of her daughter, to whom she said, as her

[022-114]sole reproach, when they were alone:--

[022-115]“My unfortunate child, see what you have done! Why did you conceal

[022-116]anything from me? Am I so harsh?”

[022-117]“Oh! I was just going to tell it to you comfortably,” sobbed Modeste.

[022-118]She thereupon related everything to her mother, read her the letters

[022-119]and their answers, and shed the rose of her poem petal by petal into the

[022-120]heart of the kind German woman. When this confidence, which took half

[022-121]the day, was over, when she saw something that was almost a smile on the

[022-122]lips of the too indulgent mother, Modeste fell upon her breast in tears.

[022-123]“Oh, mother!” she said amid her sobs, “you, whose heart, all gold and

[022-124]poetry, is a chosen vessel, chosen of God to hold a sacred love, a

[022-125]single and celestial love that endures for life; you, whom I wish to

[022-126]imitate by loving no one but my husband,--you will surely understand

[022-127]what bitter tears I am now shedding. This butterfly, this Psyche of my

[022-128]thoughts, this dual soul which I have nurtured with maternal care, my

[022-129]love, my sacred love, this living mystery of mysteries--it is about to

[022-130]fall into vulgar hands, and they will tear its diaphanous wings and rend

[022-131]its veil under the miserable pretext of enlightening me, of discovering

[022-132]whether genius is as prudent as a banker, whether my Melchior has saved

[022-133]his money, or whether he has some entanglement to shake off; they

[022-134]want to find out if he is guilty to [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois]] eyes of youthful

[022-135]indiscretions,--which to the sun of our love are like the clouds of the

[022-136]dawn. Oh! what will come of it? what will they do? See! feel my hand, it

[022-137]burns with fever. Ah! I shall never survive it.”

[022-138]And Modeste, really taken with a chill, was forced to go to bed, causing

[022-139]serious uneasiness to her mother, Madame Latournelle, and Madame Dumay,

[022-140]who took good care of her during the journey of the lieutenant to

[022-141]Paris,--to which city the logic of events compels us to transport our

[022-142]drama for a moment.

[022-143]Truly modest minds, like that of Ernest de La Briere, but especially

[022-144]those who, knowing their own value, also know that they are neither

[022-145]loved nor appreciated, can understand the infinite joy to which the

[022-146]young secretary abandoned himself on reading Modeste’s letter. Could

[022-147]it be that after thinking him lofty and witty in soul, his young, his

[022-148]artless, his tricksome mistress now thought him handsome? This flattery

[022-149]is the flattery supreme. And why? Beauty is, undoubtedly, the signature

[022-150]of the master to the work into which he has put his soul; it is the

[022-151]divine spirit manifested. And to see it where it is not, to create it by

[022-152]the power of an inward look,--is not that the highest reach of love?

[022-153]And so the poor youth cried aloud with all the rapture of an applauded

[022-154]author, “At last I am beloved!” When a woman, be she maid, wife, or

[022-155]widow, lets the charming words escape her, “Thou art handsome,” the

[022-156]words may be false, but the man opens his thick skull to their subtle

[022-157]poison, and thenceforth he is attached by an everlasting tie to the

[022-158]pretty flatterer, the true or the deceived judge; she becomes his

[022-159]particular world, he thirsts for her continual testimony, and he never

[022-160]wearies of it, even if he is a crowned prince. Ernest walked proudly

[022-161]up and down his room; he struck a three-quarter, full-face, and profile

[022-162]attitude before the glass; he tried to criticise himself; but a voice,

[022-163]diabolically persuasive, whispered to him, “Modeste is right.” He took

[022-164]up her letter and re-read it; he saw his fairest of the fair; he talked

[022-165]with her; then, in the midst of his ecstacy, a dreadful thought came to

[022-166]him:--

[022-167]“She thinks me Canalis, and she has a million of money!”

[022-168]Down went his happiness, just as a somnambulist, having attained the

[022-169]peak of a roof, hears a voice, awakes, and falls crushed upon the

[022-170]pavement.

[022-171]“Without the halo of fame I shall be hideous in her eyes,” he cried;

[022-172]“what a maddening situation I have put myself in!”

[022-173]La Briere was too much the man of his letters which we have read, his

[022-174]heart was too noble and pure to allow him to hesitate at the call of

[022-175][[honor]]. He at once resolved to find Modeste’s father, if he were in

[022-176]Paris, and confess all to him, and to let Canalis know the serious

[022-177]results of their Parisian jest. To a sensitive nature like his,

[022-178]Modeste’s large [[social-mobility|fortune]] was in itself a determining reason. He could not

[022-179]allow it to be even suspected that the ardor of the correspondence, so

[022-180]sincere on his part, had in view the capture of a “dot.” Tears were in

[022-181]his eyes as he made his way to the rue Chantereine to find the banker

[022-182]Mongenod, whose fortune and business connections were partly the work of

[022-183]the minister to whom Ernest owed his start in life.
