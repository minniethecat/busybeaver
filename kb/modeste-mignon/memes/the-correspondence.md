# The Correspondence

a three-month secret exchange of letters between Modeste Mignon and the poet
Canalis — letters actually written by his secretary, Ernest de La Brière — that
becomes the central plot engine of Balzac's *Modeste Mignon*.

## Summary

Modeste Mignon, the romantic young daughter of the exiled Charles Mignon, writes
a fan letter to the celebrated poet Melchior de Canalis. Canalis, absorbed in
political ambition, delegates the reply to his secretary Ernest de La Brière.
What begins as a literary admiration quickly deepens into an intimate
correspondence lasting three months (008-159). Modeste, inspired by the story of
Sterne and Eliza Draper, pours her soul into the letters, unaware that the man
writing back is not the poet she idolizes but his loyal, honorable secretary.

## Context

Charles Mignon has been absent for years, rebuilding the family fortune in the
East. His wife and daughter live quietly at the Chalet in Ingouville, near Le
Havre. Modeste, confined to provincial life and nourished on Romantic literature,
seeks an ideal love beyond the practical marriages she observes around her. The
correspondence becomes her secret life — a realm where her intelligence,
sensibility, and wit can flourish without the constraints of her social position.

## Consequences

The correspondence transforms Modeste from a dreamy provincial girl into a young
woman confident in her own judgment. It sets in motion the novel's central
conflict: when Canalis visits the Chalet in person, Modeste must reconcile the
man before her with the soul she encountered in the letters. The revelation that
Ernest — not Canalis — wrote the letters reshapes her understanding of love,
talent, and authenticity.

## Narrative Function

The correspondence serves as the inciting incident and structural backbone of the
novel. It allows Balzac to explore the gap between idealized literary identity
and lived reality, a theme central to *La Comédie Humaine*. The letters also
function as a testing ground where character is revealed through language rather
than action.
