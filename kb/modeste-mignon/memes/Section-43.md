# Section 43

[043-001]Mademoiselle d’Herouville said in a low voice and with much meaning

[043-002]to the Duchesse de Verneuil, “Eleonore receives her Melchior very

[043-003]ungraciously.”

[043-004]“The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse thinks there is a coolness between them,”

[043-005]said Laure de Verneuil, with simplicity.

[043-006]Charming phrase! so often used in the world of society,--how the north

[043-007]wind blows through it.

[043-008]“Why so?” asked [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] of the pretty young girl who had lately left the

[043-009]Sacre-Coeur.

[043-010]“The great [[poetry|poet]],” said the pious duchess--making a sign to her daughter

[043-011]to be silent--“left Madame de Chaulieu without a letter for more than

[043-012]two weeks after he went to [[provincial-life|Havre]], having told her that he went there for

[043-013]his health--”

[043-014]Modeste made a hasty movement, which caught the attention of Laure,

[043-015]Helene, and Mademoiselle d’Herouville.

[043-016]“--and during that time,” continued the devout duchess, “she was

[043-017]endeavoring to have him appointed commander of the Legion of [[honor]], and

[043-018]minister at Baden.”

[043-019]“Oh, that was shameful in [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]; he owes everything to her,” exclaimed

[043-020]Mademoiselle d’Herouville.

[043-021]“Why did not Madame de Chaulieu come to Havre?” asked Modeste of Helene,

[043-022]innocently.

[043-023]“My dear,” said the Duchesse de Verneuil, “she would let herself be cut

[043-024]in little pieces without saying a word. Look at her,--she is regal; her

[043-025]head would smile, like Mary Stuart’s, after it was cut off; in fact, she

[043-026]has some of that blood in her veins.”

[043-027]“Did she not write to him?” asked Modeste.

[043-028]“Diane tells me,” answered the duchess, prompted by a nudge from

[043-029]Mademoiselle d’Herouville, “that in answer to Canalis’s first letter she

[043-030]made a cutting reply a few days ago.”

[043-031]This explanation made Modeste blush with shame for the man before her;

[043-032]she longed, not to crush him under her feet, but to revenge herself by

[043-033]one of those malicious acts that are sharper than a dagger’s thrust. She

[043-034]looked haughtily at the Duchesse de Chaulieu--

[043-035]“Monsieur Melchior!” she said.

[043-036]All the women snuffed the air and looked alternately at the duchess, who

[043-037]was talking in an undertone to Canalis over the embroidery-frame,

[043-038]and then at the young girl so ill brought up as to disturb a lovers’

[043-039]meeting,--a thing not permissible in any society. Diane de Maufrigneuse

[043-040]nodded, however, as much as to say, “The child is in the right of it.”

[043-041]All the women ended by smiling at each other; they were enraged with a

[043-042]woman who was fifty-six years old and still handsome enough to put her

[043-043]fingers into the treasury and steal the dues of youth. Melchior looked

[043-044]at Modeste with feverish impatience, and made the gesture of a master

[043-045]to a valet, while the duchess lowered her head with the movement of a

[043-046]lioness disturbed at a meal; her eyes, fastened on the canvas, emitted

[043-047]red flames in the direction of the poet, which stabbed like epigrams,

[043-048]for each word revealed to her a triple insult.

[043-049]“Monsieur Melchior!” said Modeste again in a voice that asserted its

[043-050]right to be heard.

[043-051]“What, mademoiselle?” demanded the poet.

[043-052]Forced to rise, he remained standing half-way between the embroidery

[043-053]frame, which was near a window, and the fireplace where Modeste was

[043-054]seated with the Duchesse de Verneuil on a sofa. What bitter reflections

[043-055]came into his ambitious mind, as he caught a glance from Eleonore. If

[043-056]he obeyed Modeste all was over, and forever, between himself and his

[043-057]protectress. Not to obey her was to avow his slavery, to lose the

[043-058]chances of his twenty-five days of base manoeuvring, and to disregard

[043-059]the plainest laws of decency and civility. The greater the folly, the

[043-060]more imperatively the duchess exacted it. Modeste’s beauty and money

[043-061]thus pitted against Eleonore’s rights and influence made this hesitation

[043-062]between the man and his honor as terrible to witness as the peril of

[043-063]a matador in the arena. A man seldom feels such palpitations as those

[043-064]which now came near causing Canalis an aneurism, except, perhaps, before

[043-065]the green table, where his [[commerce|fortune]] or his ruin is about to be decided.

[043-066]“Mademoiselle d’Herouville hurried me from the carriage, and I left

[043-067]behind me,” said Modeste to Canalis, “my handkerchief--”

[043-068]Canalis shrugged his shoulders significantly.

[043-069]“And,” continued Modeste, taking no notice of his gesture, “I had tied

[043-070]into one corner of it the key of a desk which contains the fragment of

[043-071]an important letter; have the kindness, Monsieur Melchior, to get it for

[043-072]me.”

[043-073]Between an angel and a tiger equally enraged Canalis, who had turned

[043-074]livid, no longer hesitated,--the tiger seemed to him the least dangerous

[043-075]of the two; and he was about to do as he was told, and commit himself

[043-076]irretrievably, when La Briere appeared at the door of the salon, seeming

[043-077]to his anguished mind like the archangel Gabriel tumbling from heaven.

[043-078]“[[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], here, Mademoiselle de La Bastie wants you,” said the poet,

[043-079]hastily returning to his chair by the embroidery frame.

[043-080]Ernest rushed to Modeste without bowing to any one; he saw only her,

[043-081]took his commission with undisguised joy, and darted from the room, with

[043-082]the secret approbation of every woman present.

[043-083]“What an occupation for a poet!” said Modeste to Helene d’Herouville,

[043-084]glancing toward the embroidery at which the duchess was now working

[043-085]savagely.

[043-086]“If you speak to her, if you ever look at her, all is over between us,”

[043-087]said the duchess to the poet in a low voice, not at all satisfied with

[043-088]the very doubtful termination which Ernest’s arrival had put to the

[043-089]scene; “and remember, if I am not present, I leave behind me eyes that

[043-090]will watch you.”

[043-091]So saying, the duchess, a woman of medium height, but a little too

[043-092]stout, like all women over fifty who retain their beauty, rose and

[043-093]walked toward the group which surrounded Diane de Maufrigneuse, stepping

[043-094]daintily on little feet that were as slender and nervous as a deer’s.

[043-095]Beneath her plumpness could be seen the exquisite delicacy of such

[043-096]women, which comes from the vigor of their nervous systems controlling

[043-097]and vitalizing the development of flesh. There is no other way to

[043-098]explain the lightness of her step, and the incomparable nobility of her

[043-099]bearing. None but the women whose quarterings begin with Noah know,

[043-100]as Eleonore did, how to be majestic in spite of a buxom tendency. A

[043-101]philosopher might have pitied Philoxene, while admiring the graceful

[043-102]lines of the bust and the minute care bestowed upon a morning dress,

[043-103]which was worn with the elegance of a queen and the easy grace of a

[043-104]young girl. Her abundant hair, still undyed, was simply wound about her

[043-105]head in plaits; she bared her snowy throat and shoulders, exquisitely

[043-106]modelled, and her celebrated hand and arm, with pardonable pride.

[043-107]Modeste, together with all other antagonists of the duchess, recognized

[043-108]in her a woman of whom they were forced to say, “She eclipses us.” In

[043-109]fact, Eleonore was one of the “grandes dames” now so rare. To endeavor

[043-110]to explain what august quality there was in the carriage of the head,

[043-111]what refinement and delicacy in the curve of the throat, what harmony in

[043-112]her movements, and nobility in her bearing, what grandeur in the perfect

[043-113]accord of details with the whole being, and in the arts, now a second

[043-114]nature, which render a woman grand and even sacred,--to explain all

[043-115]these things would simply be to attempt to analyze the sublime. People

[043-116]enjoy such poetry as they enjoy that of Paganini; they do not explain to

[043-117]themselves the medium, they know the cause is in the spirit that remains

[043-118]invisible.

[043-119]Madame de Chaulieu bowed her head in salutation of Helene and her aunt;

[043-120]then, saying to Diane, in a pure and equable tone of voice, without a

[043-121]trace of emotion, “Is it not time to dress, duchess?” she made her exit,

[043-122]accompanied by her daughter-in-law and Mademoiselle d’Herouville. As she

[043-123]left the room she spoke in an undertone to the old maid, who pressed her

[043-124]arm, saying, “You are charming,”--which meant, “I am all gratitude

[043-125]for the service you have just done us.” After that, Mademoiselle

[043-126]d’Herouville returned to the salon to play her part of spy, and her

[043-127]first glance apprised Canalis that the duchess had made him no empty

[043-128]threat. That apprentice in diplomacy became aware that his science was

[043-129]not sufficient for a struggle of this kind, and his wit served him

[043-130]to take a more honest position, if not a worthier one. When Ernest

[043-131]returned, bringing Modeste’s handkerchief, the poet seized his arm and

[043-132]took him out on the terrace.

[043-133]“My dear friend,” he said, “I am not only the most unfortunate man in

[043-134]the world, but I am also the most ridiculous; and I come to you to get

[043-135]me out of the hornet’s nest into which I have run myself. Modeste is a

[043-136]demon; she sees my difficulty and she laughs at it; she has just spoken

[043-137]to me of a fragment of a letter of Madame de Chaulieu, which I had

[043-138]the folly to give her; if she shows it I can never make my peace with

[043-139]Eleonore. Therefore, will you at once ask Modeste to send me back that

[043-140]paper, and tell her, from me, that I make no pretensions to her hand.

[043-141]Say I count upon her delicacy, upon her propriety as a young girl, to

[043-142]behave to me as if we had never known each other. I beg her not to speak

[043-143]to me; I implore her to treat me harshly,--though I hardly dare ask her

[043-144]to feign a jealous anger, which would help my interests amazingly. Go, I

[043-145]will wait here for an answer.”

[043-146]CHAPTER XXVIII. MODESTE BEHAVES WITH DIGNITY

[043-147]On re-entering the salon Ernest de La Briere found a young officer of

[043-148]the company of the guard d’Havre, the Vicomte de Serizy, who had just

[043-149]arrived from Rosny to announce that _Madame_ was obliged to be present

[043-150]at the opening of the Chambers. We know the importance then attached to

[043-151]this constitutional solemnity, at which Charles X. delivered his speech,

[043-152]surrounded by the royal family,--Madame la Dauphine and _Madame_ being

[043-153]present in their gallery. The choice of the emissary charged with the

[043-154]duty of expressing the princess’s regrets was an attention to Diane,

[043-155]who was then an object of adoration to this charming young man, son of

[043-156]a minister of state, gentleman in ordinary of the chamber, only son and

[043-157]heir to an immense [[social-mobility|fortune]]. The Duchesse de Maufrigneuse permitted his

[043-158]attentions solely for the purpose of attracting notice to the age of

[043-159]his mother, Madame de Serizy, who was said, in those chronicles that

[043-160]are whispered behind the fans, to have deprived her of the heart of the

[043-161]handsome Lucien de Rubempre.

[043-162]“You will do us the pleasure, I hope, to remain at Rosembray,” said the

[043-163]severe duchess to the young officer.

[043-164]While giving ear to every scandal, the devout lady shut her eyes to the

[043-165]derelictions of her guests who had been carefully selected by the duke;

[043-166]indeed, it is surprising how much these excellent women will tolerate

[043-167]under pretence of bringing the lost sheep back to the fold by their

[043-168]indulgence.

[043-169]“We reckoned without our constitutional government,” said the grand

[043-170]equerry; “and Rosembray, Madame la duchesse, will lose a great honor.”

[043-171]“We shall be more at our ease,” said a tall thin old man, about

[043-172]seventy-five years of age, dressed in blue cloth, and wearing his

[043-173]hunting-cap by permission of the ladies. This personage, who closely

[043-174]resembled the Duc de Bourbon, was no less than the Prince de Cadignan,

[043-175]Master of the Hunt, and one of the last of the great French lords.

[043-176]Just as La Briere was endeavoring to slip behind the sofa and obtain a

[043-177]moment’s intercourse with Modeste, a man of thirty-eight, short, fat,

[043-178]and very common in appearance, entered the room.

[043-179]“My son, the Prince de Loudon,” said the Duchesse de Verneuil to

[043-180]Modeste, who could not restrain the expression of amazement that

[043-181]overspread her young face on seeing the man who bore the historical name

[043-182]that the hero of La Vendee had rendered famous by his bravery and the

[043-183]martyrdom of his death.
