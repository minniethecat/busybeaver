# House of Herouville

One of the oldest noble families of [[Normandy]], the House of Herouville enters *Modeste Mignon* as a declining aristocratic power seeking to restore its fortunes through strategic marriage. The family's presence in the novel illuminates the tension between ancestral prestige and commercial wealth that defines Balzac's Comédie Humaine.

## Overview

The Herouvilles trace their lineage to "the famous marshal" of the house (027-179) and boast "a celebrated Cardinal d'Herouville" who "flourished in the history of the Church" (012-181, 014-020). Despite this illustrious past, by the 1820s the family faced extinction: "the house of Herouville had already been threatened with extinction" (028-005). The family's estate at Herouville in Normandy includes "the park, a few dependencies" and a castle (027-136).

## In the Novel

### The Duc d'Herouville

Monsieur le Duc d'Herouville, Marquis de Saint-Sever, Duc de Nivron (027-063) returned to France after the Restoration and was appointed to high office — a position he "justified" in the eyes of observers (028-029). He was short in stature, nicknamed "the little Duc d'Herouville" (028-094), and sought [[Modeste Mignon]]'s hand as a means of restoring his family's declining wealth.

### Mademoiselle d'Herouville

The Duke's sister, Mademoiselle d'Herouville, was a formidable presence: she "floats between twenty and forty" in terms of apparent age (014-018) and "made enormous pretensions" (027-148). For years she had been "in quest of" a marriage that would restore the family fortunes (027-156), and she openly resented any threat to her brother's marital prospects. She "had contrived to wound" with her haughty demeanor (031-114), and when the Duke left to pursue Modeste, Mademoiselle d'Herouville gave a "haughty shrug" (031-124).

### The Marriage Strategy

The Herouville strategy was clear: "it was necessary to conciliate the great banking-houses" (027-153) — a frank acknowledgment that aristocratic prestige alone could not sustain the family. The Duke's courtship of Modeste, "the richest heiress in Normandy" (027-112), was a direct attempt to marry ancestral name to commercial fortune. When this failed, the house's prospects dimmed considerably.

## Historical Context

The House of Herouville represents the post-Revolutionary French aristocracy's dilemma. Stripped of feudal revenues but retaining social prestige, families like the Herouvilles sought survival through strategic marriages with the commercial bourgeoisie. Balzac portrays this not as corruption but as historical necessity — the old order adapting or perishing. The Herouvilles' failure to secure Modeste's hand is a small but significant defeat for the ancien régime in the novel's moral economy.
