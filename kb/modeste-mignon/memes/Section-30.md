# Section 30

[030-001]“It is abdication,” said [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], “for a [[poetry|poet]]; politics are the resource

[030-002]of matter-of-fact men.”

[030-003]“Ah, mademoiselle, the rostrum is to-day the greatest theatre of the

[030-004]world; it has succeeded the tournaments of chivalry, it is now the

[030-005]meeting-place for all intellects, just as the army has been the

[030-006]rallying-point of courage.”

[030-007][[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]] stuck spurs into his charger and talked for ten minutes on

[030-008]political life: “Poetry was but a preface to the statesman.” “To-day the

[030-009]orator has become a sublime reasoner, the shepherd of ideas.” “A poet

[030-010]may point the way to nations or individuals, but can he ever cease to be

[030-011]himself?” He quoted Chateaubriand and declared that he would one day be

[030-012]greater on the political side than on the literary. “The forum of France

[030-013]was to be the pharos of humanity.” “Oral battles supplanted fields of

[030-014]battle: there were sessions of the Chamber finer than any Austerlitz,

[030-015]and orators were seen to be as lofty as generals; they spent their

[030-016]lives, their courage, their strength, as freely as those who went to

[030-017]war.” “Speech was surely one of the most prodigal outlets of the vital

[030-018]fluid that man had ever known,” etc.

[030-019]This improvisation of modern commonplaces, clothed in sonorous phrases

[030-020]and newly invented words, and intended to prove that the Comte de

[030-021]Canalis was becoming one of the glories of the French government, made

[030-022]a deep impression upon the notary and Gobenheim, and upon Madame

[030-023]Latournelle and Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]]. Modeste looked as though she were at the

[030-024]theatre, in an attitude of enthusiasm for an actor,--very much like

[030-025]that of [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] toward herself; for though the secretary knew all these

[030-026]high-sounding phrases by heart, he listened through the eyes, as it

[030-027]were, of the young girl, and grew more and more madly in [[love]] with

[030-028]her. To this true lover, Modeste was eclipsing all the Modestes he had

[030-029]created as he read her [[epistolary-novel|letters]] and answered them.

[030-030]This visit, the length of which was predetermined by Canalis, careful

[030-031]not to allow his admirers a chance to get surfeited, ended by an

[030-032]invitation to dinner on the following Monday.

[030-033]“We shall not be at [[chalet|the Chalet]],” said the Comte de La Bastie. “[[dumay|Dumay]]

[030-034]will have sole possession of it. I return to the villa, having bought it

[030-035]back under a deed of redemption within six months, which I have to-day

[030-036]signed with Monsieur Vilquin.”

[030-037]“I hope,” said Dumay, “that Vilquin will not be able to return to you

[030-038]the sum you have just lent him, and that the villa will remain yours.”

[030-039]“It is an abode in keeping with your [[commerce|fortune]],” said Canalis.

[030-040]“You mean the [[social-mobility|fortune]] that I am supposed to have,” replied Charles

[030-041]Mignon, hastily.

[030-042]“It would be too sad,” said Canalis, turning to Modeste with a charming

[030-043]little bow, “if this Madonna were not framed in a manner worthy of her

[030-044]divine perfections.”

[030-045]That was the only thing Canalis said to Modeste. He affected not to

[030-046]look at her, and behaved like a man to whom all idea of [[marriage]] was

[030-047]interdicted.

[030-048]“Ah! my dear Madame Mignon,” cried the notary’s wife, as soon as the

[030-049]gravel was heard to grit under the feet of the Parisians, “what an

[030-050]intellect!”

[030-051]“Is he rich?--that is the question,” said Gobenheim.

[030-052]Modeste was at the window, not losing a single movement of the great

[030-053]poet, and paying no attention to his companion. When Monsieur Mignon

[030-054]returned to the salon, and Modeste, having received a last bow from the

[030-055]two friends as the carriage turned, went back to her seat, a weighty

[030-056]discussion took place, such as provincials invariably hold over

[030-057]Parisians after a first interview. Gobenheim repeated his phrase, “Is

[030-058]he rich?” as a chorus to the songs of praise sung by Madame Latournelle,

[030-059]Modeste, and her mother.

[030-060]“Rich!” exclaimed Modeste; “what can that signify! Do you not see that

[030-061]Monsieur de Canalis is one of those men who are destined for the highest

[030-062]places in the State. He has more than fortune; he possesses that which

[030-063]gives fortune.”

[030-064]“He will be minister or ambassador,” said Monsieur Mignon.

[030-065]“That won’t hinder tax-payers from having to pay the costs of his

[030-066]funeral,” remarked the notary.

[030-067]“How so?” asked Charles Mignon.

[030-068]“He strikes me as a man who will waste all the fortunes with whose gifts

[030-069]Mademoiselle Modeste so liberally endows him,” answered Latournelle.

[030-070]“Modeste can’t avoid being liberal to a poet who called her a Madonna,”

[030-071]said Dumay, sneering, and faithful to the repulsion with which Canalis

[030-072]had originally inspired him.

[030-073]Gobenheim arranged the whist-table with all the more persistency

[030-074]because, since the return of Monsieur Mignon, Latournelle and Dumay had

[030-075]allowed themselves to play for ten sous points.

[030-076]“Well, my little darling,” said the father to the daughter in the

[030-077]embrasure of a window. “Admit that papa thinks of everything. If you

[030-078]send your orders this evening to your former dressmaker in [[paris|Paris]], and

[030-079]all your other furnishing people, you shall show yourself eight days

[030-080]hence in all the splendor of an heiress. Meantime we will install

[030-081]ourselves in the villa. You already have a pretty horse, now order a

[030-082]habit; you owe that amount of civility to the grand equerry.”

[030-083]“All the more because there will be a number of us to ride,” said

[030-084]Modeste, who was recovering the colors of health.

[030-085]“The secretary did not say much,” remarked Madame Mignon.

[030-086]“A little fool,” said Madame Latournelle; “the poet has an attentive

[030-087]word for everybody. He thanked Monsieur Latournelle for his help in

[030-088]choosing the house; and said he must have taken counsel with a woman of

[030-089]good taste. But the other looked as gloomy as a Spaniard, and kept his

[030-090]eyes fixed on Modeste as though he would like to swallow her whole. If

[030-091]he had even looked at me I should have been afraid of him.”

[030-092]“He had a pleasant voice,” said Madame Mignon.

[030-093]“No doubt he came to [[provincial-life|Havre]] to inquire about the Mignons in the interests

[030-094]of his friend the poet,” said Modeste, looking furtively at her father.

[030-095]“It was certainly he whom we saw in church.”

[030-096]Madame Dumay and Monsieur and Madame Latournelle, accepted this as the

[030-097]natural explanation of Ernest’s journey.

[030-098]CHAPTER XIX. OF WHICH THE AUTHOR THINKS A GOOD DEAL

[030-099]“Do you know, Ernest,” cried Canalis, when they had driven a short

[030-100]distance from the house, “I don’t see any marriageable woman in society

[030-101]in Paris who compares with that adorable girl.”

[030-102]“Ah, that ends it!” replied Ernest. “She loves you, or she will love you

[030-103]if you desire it. Your fame won half the battle. Well, you may now

[030-104]have it all your own way. You shall go there alone in future. Modeste

[030-105]despises me; she is right to do so; and I don’t see any reason why I

[030-106]should condemn myself to see, to love, desire, and adore that which I

[030-107]can never possess.”

[030-108]After a few consoling remarks, dashed with his own satisfaction at

[030-109]having made a new version of Caesar’s phrase, Canalis divulged a desire

[030-110]to break with the Duchesse de Chaulieu. La Briere, totally unable to

[030-111]keep up the conversation, made the beauty of the night an excuse to be

[030-112]set down, and then rushed like one possessed to the seashore, where he

[030-113]stayed till past ten, in a half-demented state, walking hurriedly up

[030-114]and down, talking aloud in broken sentences, sometimes standing still

[030-115]or sitting down, without noticing the uneasiness of two custom-house

[030-116]officers who were on the watch. After loving Modeste’s wit and

[030-117]intellect and her aggressive frankness, he now joined adoration of her

[030-118]beauty--that is to say, love without reason, love inexplicable--to all

[030-119]the other reasons which had drawn him ten days earlier, to the church in

[030-120]Havre.

[030-121]He returned to the Chalet, where the Pyrenees hounds barked at him till

[030-122]he was forced to relinquish the pleasure of gazing at Modeste’s windows.

[030-123]In love, such things are of no more account to the lover than the work

[030-124]which is covered by the last layer of color is to an artist; yet they

[030-125]make up the whole of love, just as the hidden toil is the whole of art.

[030-126]Out of them arise the great painter and the true lover whom the woman

[030-127]and the public end, sometimes too late, by adoring.

[030-128]“Well then!” he cried aloud, “I will stay, I will suffer, I will love

[030-129]her for myself only, in solitude. Modeste shall be my sun, my life; I

[030-130]will breathe with her breath, rejoice in her joys and bear her griefs,

[030-131]be she even the wife of that egoist, Canalis.”

[030-132]“That’s what I call loving, monsieur,” said a voice which came from a

[030-133]shrub by the side of the road. “Ha, ha, so all the world is in love with

[030-134]Mademoiselle de La Bastie?”

[030-135]And Butscha suddenly appeared and looked at La Briere. La Briere checked

[030-136]his anger when, by the light of the moon, he saw the dwarf, and he made

[030-137]a few steps without replying.

[030-138]“Soldiers who serve in the same company ought to be good comrades,”

[030-139]remarked Butscha. “You don’t love Canalis; neither do I.”

[030-140]“He is my friend,” replied Ernest.

[030-141]“Ha, you are the little secretary?”

[030-142]“You are to know, monsieur, that I am no man’s secretary. I have the

[030-143][[honor]] to be of counsel to a supreme court of this kingdom.”

[030-144]“I have the honor to salute Monsieur de La Briere,” said Butscha. “I

[030-145]myself have the honor to be head clerk to Latournelle, chief councillor

[030-146]of Havre, and my position is a better one than yours. Yes, I have had

[030-147]the happiness of seeing Mademoiselle Modeste de La Bastie nearly every

[030-148]evening for the last four years, and I expect to live near her, as a

[030-149]king’s servant lives in the Tuileries. If they offered me the throne of

[030-150]Russia I should answer, ‘I love the sun too well.’ Isn’t that telling

[030-151]you, monsieur, that I care more for her than for myself? I am looking

[030-152]after her interests with the most honorable intentions. Do you believe

[030-153]that the proud Duchesse de Chaulieu would cast a favorable eye on the

[030-154]happiness of Madame de Canalis if her waiting-woman, who is in love with

[030-155]Monsieur Germain, not liking that charming valet’s absence in Havre,

[030-156]were to say to her mistress while brushing her hair--”

[030-157]“Who do you know about all this?” said La Briere, interrupting Butscha.

[030-158]“In the first place, I am clerk to a notary,” answered Butscha. “But

[030-159]haven’t you seen my hump? It is full of resources, monsieur. I have made

[030-160]myself cousin to Mademoiselle Philoxene Jacmin, born at Honfleur, where

[030-161]my mother was born, a Jacmin,--there are eight branches of the Jacmins

[030-162]at Honfleur. So my cousin Philoxene, enticed by the bait of a highly

[030-163]improbable fortune, has told me a good many things.”

[030-164]“The duchess is vindictive?” said La Briere.

[030-165]“Vindictive as a queen, Philoxene says; she has never yet forgiven the

[030-166]duke for being nothing more than her husband,” replied Butscha. “She

[030-167]hates as she loves. I know all about her character, her tastes, her

[030-168]toilette, her religion, and her manners; for Philoxene stripped her for

[030-169]me, soul and corset. I went to the opera expressly to see her, and I

[030-170]didn’t grudge the ten francs it cost me--I don’t mean the play. If my

[030-171]imaginary cousin had not told me the duchess had seen her fifty summers,

[030-172]I should have thought I was over-generous in giving her thirty; she has

[030-173]never known a winter, that duchess!”

[030-174]“Yes,” said La Briere, “she is a cameo--preserved because it is stone.

[030-175]Canalis would be in a bad way if the duchess were to find out what he

[030-176]is doing here; and I hope, monsieur, that you will go no further in this

[030-177]business of spying, which is unworthy of an honest man.”

[030-178]“Monsieur,” said Butscha, proudly; “for me Modeste is my country. I do

[030-179]not spy; I foresee, I take precautions. The duchess will come here if

[030-180]it is desirable, or she will stay tranquilly where she is, according to

[030-181]what I judge best.”
