# Bourgeoisie

The bourgeoisie in *Modeste Mignon* represents the ascendant commercial class of Restoration France — figures of material comfort, social aspiration, and the limitations of provincial respectability.

## Definition

Balzac portrays the bourgeoisie through characters like the Latournelles, whose social world is defined by notarial respectability, precise routines, and a narrow horizon of concerns. The narrator describes how "the Madame Latournelles of the bourgeoisie 'get their living by the sweat of their brow'" (006-062), capturing both the moral self-regard and the limited imagination of this class. The bourgeois household is characterized by material comfort without elegance, social conformity without distinction, and a deep suspicion of anything that transcends the ordinary (027-125).

## In the Novel

### Manifestations

The Latournelle household serves as the novel's primary specimen of provincial bourgeois life. Monsieur Latournelle, the notary, walks the same route from Havre to Ingouville every evening (001-048). His wife embodies the curiosity and moral surveillance that characterize small-town bourgeois society — she is among the first to suspect Modeste's secret correspondence.

The Chalet household itself exists in tension with bourgeois norms. Charles Mignon, though wealthy from commerce, is a former soldier of the Empire with aristocratic pretensions (the "de La Bastie" title). Dumay, the loyal lieutenant turned family protector, despises the bourgeois world he is forced to inhabit. The Gobenheim character represents the purely commercial dimension of the bourgeoisie — calculating, detached from sentiment, focused on transactions.

## In Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*

The critique of the bourgeoisie is one of Balzac's great themes, developed most fully in *César Birotteau* and *Les Petits Bourgeois*. In *Modeste Mignon*, the treatment is lighter — the bourgeoisie is satirized rather than anatomized, serving as comic relief and social backdrop rather than as the object of tragic analysis. This reflects the novel's generic identity as a romantic comedy.
