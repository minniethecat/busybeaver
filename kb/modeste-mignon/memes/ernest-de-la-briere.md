# Ernest de La Brière

Ernest de La Brière is the novel's true romantic hero — a man whose sincerity, expressed through letters he writes under another man's name, ultimately wins Modeste's heart over the celebrated poet she thought she loved.

## Role in the Novel

La Brière functions as Canalis's moral foil. Where Canalis performs genius, La Brière embodies virtue. His ghost-writing of Modeste's love letters creates the novel's central irony: she falls in love with the letters' sincerity while attributing them to the wrong man. His eventual vindication — Modeste chooses him — is the novel's moral climax.

## Biography

### Background & Origins

A young man of modest means employed as Canalis's secretary, La Brière lacks the poet's fame and the duke's rank. His virtue is his only asset. When Canalis delegates Modeste's correspondence to him, La Brière writes with genuine feeling, creating letters of an authenticity that Canalis could not produce.

### Key Actions

La Brière writes the letters that make Modeste fall in love — letters attributed to Canalis but infused with La Brière's own sensibility (010-053). When the deception is revealed, his honesty in acknowledging his role and his feelings distinguishes him from Canalis's evasions. He competes with both the poet and the Duc d'Hérouville for Modeste's hand.

### Fate / Resolution

La Brière wins Modeste. The novel's resolution affirms that character — not fame, not rank, not wealth — is the proper basis for marriage.

## Character Traits

- **Defining qualities**: Sincerity, loyalty, moral courage, emotional honesty
- **Social Type**: The virtuous functionary — a man of merit without fortune in a world that values both

## Balzacian Context

Appears only in *Modeste Mignon*. La Brière represents a rare type in Balzac: the genuinely good man who succeeds. Most Balzacian heroes of virtue (David Séchard, Père Goriot) are destroyed; La Brière is rewarded.
