# Versailles

The Palace of Versailles, the former seat of French royal power, appears in *Modeste Mignon* not as a setting but as a standard of comparison — the ultimate measure of architectural and landscape grandeur against which Norman provincial estates are judged and found to be imitations.

## Geography

Located approximately 20 kilometers southwest of Paris, Versailles was the principal royal residence of France from 1682 until the Revolution of 1789. The palace is renowned for its vast scale, its Hall of Mirrors, its formal gardens designed by André Le Nôtre, and the Grand Canal — a monumental water feature that extends the palace's axis into the landscape. Balzac's readers would have recognized Versailles as the supreme expression of French classical grandeur.

## In the Novel

### Narrative Function

Versailles appears in Section 42 exclusively as a comparative reference. A provincial estate — likely the property of the Vilquin family or another Norman grandee — is described through a series of Versailles comparisons: its courtyard is "like that at Versailles, with magnificent" proportions (042-086), its water feature is measured against "the grand canal at Versailles" (042-105), and the whole ensemble is characterized as "this lesser Versailles" (042-125). The triple comparison establishes both the ambition of the provincial estate and its inevitable secondariness — it can only ever be a "lesser" version of the real thing.

A female character, upon entering this provincial imitation, is struck by its grandeur: "she suddenly" beholds the scale of the imitation and reacts (042-125). The Versailles comparison thus functions as a social marker — those who recognize the reference demonstrate their familiarity with the highest registers of French culture.

## Thematic Significance

Versailles serves as the novel's ultimate symbol of aristocratic pretension. In post-Revolutionary France, the palace had lost its political function but retained its cultural authority as the gold standard of grandeur. By measuring a Norman estate against Versailles, Balzac exposes the provincial bourgeoisie's aspiration to aristocratic legitimacy — the Vilquins and their peers can build courtyards and dig canals, but they can only ever produce a "lesser" version. The Versailles reference thus encapsulates the novel's broader concern with social climbing and the gap between provincial reality and Parisian-aristocratic ideals.

## Historical Context

In Balzac's era (~1829–1844), Versailles was no longer a royal residence. Louis-Philippe, the "Citizen King" who reigned from 1830 to 1848, had transformed the palace into a national museum dedicated "to all the glories of France" — the Musée de l'Histoire de France, inaugurated in 1837. This transformation from seat of royal power to public monument shaped how Balzac's readers would have understood Versailles: as a site of historical memory rather than living power. The "lesser Versailles" of the novel thus imitates something already consigned to history — a doubly nostalgic gesture.
