# Section 28

[028-001]The grand equerry, a fair young man with blue eyes and a pallid face,

[028-002]was not without a certain dignity of thought; but his thin, undersized

[028-003]figure, and the follies of his aunt who had taken him to the Vilquins

[028-004]and elsewhere to pay his court, rendered him extremely diffident. The

[028-005]house of Herouville had already been threatened with extinction by the

[028-006]deed of a deformed being (see the “Enfant Maudit” in “Philosophical

[028-007]Studies”). The grand marshal, that being the family term for the member

[028-008]who was made duke by Louis XIII., married at the age of eighty. The

[028-009]young duke admired women, but he placed them too high and respected them

[028-010]too much; in fact, he adored them, and was only at his ease with those

[028-011]whom he could not respect. This characteristic caused him to lead a

[028-012]double life. He found compensation with women of easy virtue for the

[028-013]worship to which he surrendered himself in the salons, or, if you like,

[028-014]the boudoirs, of the faubourg Saint-Germain. Such habits and his puny

[028-015]figure, his suffering face with its blue eyes turning upward in ecstasy,

[028-016]increased the ridicule already bestowed upon him,--very unjustly

[028-017]bestowed, as it happened, for he was full of wit and delicacy; but his

[028-018]wit, which never sparkled, only showed itself when he felt at ease.

[028-019]Fanny Beaupre, an actress who was supposed to be his nearest friend (at

[028-020]a price), called him “a sound wine so carefully corked that you break

[028-021]all your corkscrews.” The beautiful Duchesse de Maufrigneuse, whom the

[028-022]grand equerry could only worship, annihilated him with a speech which,

[028-023]unfortunately, was repeated from mouth to mouth, like all such pretty

[028-024]and malicious sayings.

[028-025]“He always seems to me,” she said, “like one of those jewels of fine

[028-026]workmanship which we exhibit but never wear, and keep in cotton-wool.”

[028-027]Everything about him, even to his absurdly contrasting title of

[028-028]grand equerry, amused the good-natured king, Charles X., and made him

[028-029]laugh,--although the Duc d’Herouville justified his appointment in the

[028-030]matter of being a fine horseman. Men are like books, often understood

[028-031]and appreciated too late. [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] had seen the duke during his fruitless

[028-032]visit to the Vilquins, and many of these reflections passed through

[028-033]her mind as she watched him come and go. But under the circumstances in

[028-034]which she now found herself, she saw plainly that the courtship of

[028-035]the Duc d’Herouville would save her from being at the mercy of either

[028-036][[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]].

[028-037]“I see no reason,” she said to Latournelle, “why the Duc d’Herouville

[028-038]should not be received. I have passed, in spite of our indigence,” she

[028-039]continued, with a mischievous look at her father, “to the condition

[028-040]of heiress. Haven’t you observed Gobenheim’s glances? They have quite

[028-041]changed their character within a week. He is in despair at not being

[028-042]able to make his games of whist count for mute adoration of my charms.”

[028-043]“Hush, my darling!” cried Madame Latournelle, “here he comes.”

[028-044]“Old Althor is in despair,” said Gobenheim to Monsieur [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] as he

[028-045]entered.

[028-046]“Why?” asked the count.

[028-047]“Vilquin is going to fail; and the Bourse thinks you are worth several

[028-048]millions. What ill-luck for his son!”

[028-049]“No one knows,” said Charles Mignon, coldly, “what my liabilities in

[028-050]India are; and I do not intend to take the public into my confidence as

[028-051]to my private affairs. [[dumay|Dumay]],” he whispered to his friend, “if Vilquin

[028-052]is embarrassed we could get back the villa by paying him what he gave

[028-053]for it.”

[028-054]Such was the general state of things, due chiefly to accident, when on

[028-055]Sunday morning Canalis and La Briere arrived, with a courier in advance,

[028-056]at the villa of Madame Amaury. It was known that the Duc d’Herouville,

[028-057]his sister, and his aunt were coming the following Tuesday to occupy,

[028-058]also under pretext of ill-health, a hired house at Graville. This

[028-059]assemblage of suitors made the wits of the Bourse remark that, thanks to

[028-060]Mademoiselle Mignon, rents would rise at [[ingouville|Ingouville]]. “If this goes on,

[028-061]she will have a hospital here,” said the younger Mademoiselle Vilquin,

[028-062]vexed at not becoming a duchess.

[028-063]The everlasting comedy of “The Heiress,” about to be played at the

[028-064]Chalet, might very well be called, in view of Modeste’s frame of mind,

[028-065]“The Designs of a Young Girl”; for since the overthrow of her illusions

[028-066]she had fully made up her mind to give her hand to no man whose

[028-067]qualifications did not fully satisfy her.

[028-068]The two rivals, still intimate friends, intended to pay their first

[028-069]visit at [[chalet|the Chalet]] on the evening of the day succeeding their arrival.

[028-070]They had spent Sunday and part of Monday in unpacking and arranging

[028-071]Madame Amaury’s house for a month’s stay. The [[poetry|poet]], always calculating

[028-072]effects, wished to make the most of the probable excitement which his

[028-073]arrival would case in [[provincial-life|Havre]], and which would of course echo up to the

[028-074]Mignons. Therefore, in his role of a man needing rest, he did not leave

[028-075]the house. La Briere went twice to walk past the Chalet, though always

[028-076]with a sense of despair, for he feared to displease Modeste, and the

[028-077]future seemed to him dark with clouds. The two friends came down to

[028-078]dinner on Monday dressed for the momentous visit. La Briere wore the

[028-079]same clothes he had so carefully selected for the famous Sunday; but

[028-080]he now felt like the satellite of a planet, and resigned himself to

[028-081]the uncertainties of his situation. Canalis, on the other hand, had

[028-082]carefully attended to his black coat, his orders, and all those little

[028-083]drawing-room elegancies, which his intimacy with the Duchesse de

[028-084]Chaulieu and the fashionable world of the faubourg had brought to

[028-085]perfection. He had gone into the minutiae of dandyism, while poor La

[028-086]Briere was about to present himself with the negligence of a man without

[028-087]hope. Germain, as he waited at dinner could not help smiling to himself

[028-088]at the contrast. After the second course, however, the valet came in

[028-089]with a diplomatic, that is to say, uneasy air.

[028-090]“Does Monsieur le baron know,” he said to Canalis in a low voice, “that

[028-091]Monsieur the grand equerry is coming to Graville to get cured of the

[028-092]same illness which has brought Monsieur de La Briere and Monsieur le

[028-093]baron to the sea-shore?”

[028-094]“What, the little Duc d’Herouville?”

[028-095]“Yes, monsieur.”

[028-096]“Is he coming for Mademoiselle de La Bastie?” asked La Briere, coloring.

[028-097]“So it appears, monsieur.”

[028-098]“We are cheated!” cried Canalis looking at La Briere.

[028-099]“Ah!” retorted [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]] quickly, “that is the first time you have said,

[028-100]‘we’ since we left [[paris|Paris]]: it has been ‘I’ all along.”

[028-101]“You understood me,” cried Canalis, with a burst of laughter. “But

[028-102]we are not in a position to struggle against a ducal coronet, nor the

[028-103]duke’s title, nor against the waste lands which the Council of State

[028-104]have just granted, on my report, to the house of Herouville.”

[028-105]“His grace,” said La Briere, with a spice of malice that was

[028-106]nevertheless serious, “will furnish you with compensation in the person

[028-107]of his sister.”

[028-108]At this instant, the Comte de La Bastie was announced; the two young men

[028-109]rose at once, and La Briere hastened forward to present Canalis.

[028-110]“I wished to return the visit that you paid me in Paris,” said the count

[028-111]to the young lawyer, “and I knew that by coming here I should have the

[028-112]double pleasure of greeting one of our great living poets.”

[028-113]“Great!--Monsieur,” replied the poet, smiling, “no one can be great in a

[028-114]century prefaced by the reign of a Napoleon. We are a tribe of would-be

[028-115]great poets; besides, second-rate talent imitates genius nowadays, and

[028-116]renders real distinction impossible.”

[028-117]“Is that the reason why you have thrown yourself into politics?” asked

[028-118]the count.

[028-119]“It is the same thing in that sphere,” said the poet; “there are no

[028-120]statesmen in these days, only men who handle events more or less. Look

[028-121]at it, monsieur; under the system of government that we derive from the

[028-122]Charter, which makes a tax-list of more importance than a coat-of-arms,

[028-123]there is absolutely nothing solid except that which you went to seek in

[028-124]China,--wealth.”

[028-125]Satisfied with himself and with the impression he was making on the

[028-126]prospective father-in-law, Canalis turned to Germain.

[028-127]“Serve the coffee in the salon,” he said, inviting Monsieur de La Bastie

[028-128]to leave the dining-room.

[028-129]“I thank you for this visit, monsieur le comte,” said La Briere; “it

[028-130]saves me from the embarrassment of presenting my friend to you in your

[028-131]own house. You have a heart, and you have also a quick mind.”

[028-132]“Bah! the ready wit of Provence, that is all,” said Charles Mignon.

[028-133]“Ah, do you come from Provence?” cried Canalis.

[028-134]“You must pardon my friend,” said La Briere; “he has not studied, as I

[028-135]have, the history of La Bastie.”

[028-136]At the word _friend_ Canalis threw a searching glance at Ernest.

[028-137]“If your health will allow,” said the count to the poet, “I shall hope

[028-138]to receive you this evening under my roof; it will be a day to mark,

[028-139]as the old writer said ‘albo notanda lapillo.’ Though we cannot duly

[028-140]receive so great a fame in our little house, yet your visit will gratify

[028-141]my daughter, whose admiration for your poems has even led her to set

[028-142]them to [[music]].”

[028-143]“You have something better than fame in your house,” said Canalis; “you

[028-144]have beauty, if I am to believe Ernest.”

[028-145]“Yes, a good daughter; but you will find her rather countrified,” said

[028-146]Charles Mignon.

[028-147]“A country girl sought by the Duc d’Herouville,” remarked Canalis,

[028-148]dryly.

[028-149]“Oh!” replied Monsieur Mignon, with the perfidious good-humor of a

[028-150]Southerner, “I leave my daughter free. Dukes, princes, commoners,--they

[028-151]are all the same to me, even men of genius. I shall make no pledges, and

[028-152]whoever my Modeste chooses will be my son-in-law, or rather my son,” he

[028-153]added, looking at La Briere. “It could not be otherwise. Madame de La

[028-154]Bastie is German. She has never adopted our etiquette, and I let my

[028-155]two women lead me their own way. I have always preferred to sit in

[028-156]the carriage rather than on the box. I can make a joke of all this at

[028-157]present, for we have not yet seen the Duc d’Herouville, and I do not

[028-158]believe in marriages arranged by proxy, any more than I believe in

[028-159]choosing my daughter’s husband.”

[028-160]“That declaration is equally encouraging and discouraging to two young

[028-161]men who are searching for the philosopher’s stone of happiness in

[028-162][[marriage]],” said Canalis.

[028-163]“Don’t you consider it useful, necessary, and even politic to stipulate

[028-164]for perfect freedom of action for parents, daughters, and suitors?”

[028-165]asked Charles Mignon.

[028-166]Canalis, at a sign from La Briere, kept silence. The conversation

[028-167]presently became unimportant, and after a few turns round the garden the

[028-168]count retired, urging the visit of the two friends.

[028-169]“That’s our dismissal,” cried Canalis; “you saw it as plainly as I did.

[028-170]Well, in his place, I should not hesitate between the grand equerry and

[028-171]either of us, charming as we are.”

[028-172]“I don’t think so,” said La Briere. “I believe that frank soldier came

[028-173]here to satisfy his desire to see you, and to warn us of his neutrality

[028-174]while receiving us in his house. Modeste, in [[love]] with your fame, and

[028-175]misled by my person, stands, as it were, between the real and [[the-ideal|the ideal]],

[028-176]between poetry and prose. I am, unfortunately, the prose.”

[028-177]“Germain,” said Canalis to the valet, who came to take away the coffee,

[028-178]“order the carriage in half an hour. We will take a drive before we go

[028-179]to the Chalet.”

[028-180]CHAPTER XVIII. A SPLENDID FIRST APPEARANCE

[028-181]The two young men were equally impatient to see Modeste, but La Briere

[028-182]dreaded the interview, while Canalis approached it with the confidence

[028-183]of self-conceit. The eagerness with which La Briere had met the

[028-184]father, and the flattery of his attention to the family pride of the

[028-185]ex-[[commerce|merchant]], showed Canalis his own maladroitness, and determined him to

[028-186]select a special role. The great poet resolved to pretend indifference,

[028-187]though all the while displaying his seductive powers; to appear to

[028-188]disdain the young lady, and thus pique her self-love. Trained by

[028-189]the handsome Duchesse de Chaulieu, he was bound to be worthy of his

[028-190][[honor|reputation]] as a man who knew women, when, in fact, he did not know them

[028-191]at all,--which is often the case with those who are the happy victims

[028-192]of an exclusive passion. While poor Ernest, gloomily ensconced in his

[028-193]corner of the caleche, gave way to the terrors of genuine love, and

[028-194]foresaw instinctively the anger, contempt, and disdain of an injured and

[028-195]offended young girl, Canalis was preparing himself, not less silently,

[028-196]like an actor making ready for an important part in a new play;

[028-197]certainly neither of them presented the appearance of a happy man.

[028-198]Important interests were involved for Canalis. The mere suggestion of

[028-199]his desire to marry would bring about a rupture of the tie which had

[028-200]bound him for the last ten years to the Duchesse de Chaulieu. Though

[028-201]he had covered the purpose of his journey with the vulgar pretext of

[028-202]needing rest,--in which, by the bye, women never believe, even when

[028-203]it is true,--his conscience troubled him somewhat; but the word

[028-204]“conscience” seemed so Jesuitical to La Briere that he shrugged his

[028-205]shoulders when the poet mentioned his scruples.
