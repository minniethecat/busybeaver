# Canton

Canton (present-day Guangzhou) was the sole Chinese port open to Western trade during Charles Mignon's exile, and the specific location where his commercial genius rebuilt the family fortune.

## Geography

Located on the Pearl River in southern China, Canton was the hub of the Canton System (1757–1842), the Qing dynasty's framework for controlling foreign trade. All Western merchants — British, French, Dutch, and American — were confined to a designated factory district on the city's waterfront. For Mignon, Canton represented the easternmost node of a global commercial network spanning Le Havre, New York, India, and China.

## In the Novel

Canton appears in the context of Charles Mignon's commercial operations. It is described as "ten times richer than ever" (017-146), a hyperbole that conveys the scale of the China trade's profitability. Goods move "from thence to Canton" at advantageous prices (017-148), exploiting the arbitrage between European and Chinese markets. The "English merchants at Canton" are referenced as subjects of commercial discussion (027-101), situating Mignon's activities within the competitive landscape of the early 19th-century China trade.

## Thematic Significance

Canton represents the farthest point of Mignon's commercial reach and the source of his restored wealth. Its remoteness from Normandy — geographical, cultural, commercial — measures the distance he traveled, both literally and figuratively, to redeem his name. The city's association with immense riches ("ten times richer") reinforces the novel's fascination with the transformative power of commerce.

## Historical Context

The Canton System confined all Western trade to Guangzhou from 1757 until the Treaty of Nanking (1842), which ended the First Opium War and opened additional Chinese ports. During Mignon's exile (approximately 1820s–1830s), Canton was at the height of its monopoly, with tea, silk, and porcelain flowing to Europe. The reference to "English merchants" reflects the British East India Company's dominance, though French traders — including private merchants from Le Havre and Marseille — also participated in the Canton trade.
