# Section 9

[009-001]Out of this third period of the development of her ideas, there came to

[009-002][[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] a passionate desire to penetrate to the heart of one of these

[009-003]abnormal beings; to understand the working of the thoughts and the

[009-004]hidden griefs of genius,--to know not only what it wanted but what it

[009-005]was. At the period when this story begins, these vagaries of fancy,

[009-006]these excursions of her soul into the void, these feelers put forth into

[009-007]the darkness of the future, the impatience of an ungiven [[love]] to find

[009-008]its goal, the nobility of all her thoughts of life, the decision of her

[009-009]mind to suffer in a sphere of higher things rather than flounder in the

[009-010]marshes of [[provincial-life|provincial life]] like her mother, the pledge she had made to

[009-011]herself never to fail in conduct, but to respect her father’s hearth and

[009-012]bring it happiness,--all this world of feeling and sentiment had lately

[009-013]come to a climax and taken shape. Modeste wished to be the friend and

[009-014]companion of a [[poetry|poet]], an artist, a man in some way superior to the crowd

[009-015]of men. But she intended to choose him,--not to give him her heart, her

[009-016]life, her infinite tenderness freed from the trammels of passion, until

[009-017]she had carefully and deeply studied him.

[009-018]She began this pretty romance by simply enjoying it. Profound

[009-019]tranquillity settled down upon her soul. Her cheeks took on a soft

[009-020]color; and she became the beautiful and noble image of Germany, such as

[009-021]we have lately seen her, the glory of [[chalet|the Chalet]], the pride of Madame

[009-022]Latournelle and the Dumays. Modeste was living a double existence. She

[009-023]performed with humble, loving care all the minute duties of the homely

[009-024]life at the Chalet, using them as a rein to guide the poetry of her

[009-025][[the-ideal|ideal life]], like the Carthusian monks who labor methodically on material

[009-026]things to leave their souls the freer to develop in prayer. All great

[009-027]minds have bound themselves to some form of mechanical toil to obtain

[009-028]greater mastery of thought. Spinosa ground glasses for spectacles; Bayle

[009-029]counted the tiles on the roof; Montesquieu gardened. The body being thus

[009-030]subdued, the soul could spread its wings in all security.

[009-031]Madame [[charles-mignon|Mignon]], reading her daughter’s soul, was therefore right. Modeste

[009-032]loved; she loved with that rare platonic love, so little understood, the

[009-033]first [[illusion]] of a young girl, the most delicate of all sentiments, a

[009-034]very dainty of the heart. She drank deep draughts from the chalice of

[009-035]the unknown, the vague, the visionary. She admired the blue plumage of

[009-036]the bird that sings afar in the paradise of young girls, which no hand

[009-037]can touch, no gun can cover, as it flits across the sight; she loved

[009-038]those magic colors, like sparkling jewels dazzling to the eye, which

[009-039]youth can see, and never sees again when Reality, the hideous hag,

[009-040]appears with witnesses accompanied by the mayor. To live the very poetry

[009-041]of love and not to see the lover--ah, what sweet intoxication! what

[009-042]visionary rapture! a chimera with flowing man and outspread wings!

[009-043]The following is the puerile and even silly event which decided the

[009-044]future life of this young girl.

[009-045]Modeste happened to see in a bookseller’s window a lithographic portrait

[009-046]of one of her favorites, [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]]. We all know what lies such pictures

[009-047]tell,--being as they are the result of a shameless speculation, which

[009-048]seizes upon the personality of celebrated individuals as if their faces

[009-049]were public property.

[009-050]In this instance Canalis, sketched in a Byronic pose, was offering to

[009-051]public admiration his dark locks floating in the breeze, a bare throat,

[009-052]and the unfathomable brow which every bard ought to possess. Victor

[009-053]Hugo’s forehead will make more persons shave their heads than the

[009-054]number of incipient marshals ever killed by the glory of Napoleon.

[009-055]This portrait of Canalis (poetic through mercantile necessity) caught

[009-056]Modeste’s eye. The day on which it caught her eye one of Arthez’s best

[009-057]books happened to be published. We are compelled to admit, though it may

[009-058]be to Modeste’s injury, that she hesitated long between the illustrious

[009-059]poet and the illustrious prose-writer. Which of these celebrated men was

[009-060]free?--that was the question.

[009-061]Modeste began by securing the co-operation of Francoise Cochet, a maid

[009-062]taken from Havre and brought back again by poor Bettina, whom Madame

[009-063]Mignon and Madame [[dumay|Dumay]] now employed by the day, and who lived in Havre.

[009-064]Modeste took her to her own room and assured her that she would never

[009-065]cause her parents any grief, never pass the bounds of a young girl’s

[009-066]propriety, and that as to Francoise herself she would be well provided

[009-067]for after the return of Monsieur Mignon, on condition that she would do

[009-068]a certain service and keep it an inviolable secret. What was it? Why, a

[009-069]nothing--perfectly innocent. All that Modeste wanted of her accomplice

[009-070]was to put certain [[epistolary-novel|letters]] into the post at Havre and to bring some

[009-071]back which would be directed to herself, Francoise Cochet. The treaty

[009-072]concluded, Modeste wrote a polite note to Dauriat, publisher of the

[009-073]poems of Canalis, asking, in the interest of that great poet, for some

[009-074]particulars about him, among others if he were married. She requested

[009-075]the publisher to address his answer to Mademoiselle Francoise, “poste

[009-076]restante,” Havre.

[009-077]Dauriat, incapable of taking the epistle seriously, wrote a reply in

[009-078]presence of four or five journalists who happened to be in his office

[009-079]at the time, each of whom added his particular stroke of wit to the

[009-080]production.

[009-081]Mademoiselle,--Canalis (Baron of), Constant Cys Melchior, member

[009-082]of the French Academy, born in 1800, at Canalis (Correze), five

[009-083]feet four inches in height, of good standing, vaccinated, spotless

[009-084]birth, has given a substitute to the conscription, enjoys perfect

[009-085]health, owns a small patrimonial estate in the Correze, and wishes

[009-086]to marry, but the lady must be rich.

[009-087]He beareth per pale, gules an axe or, sable three escallops

[009-088]argent, surmounted by a baron’s coronet; supporters, two larches,

[009-089]vert. Motto: “Or et fer” (no allusion to Ophir or auriferous).

[009-090]The original Canalis, who went to the Holy Land with the First

[009-091]Crusade, is cited in the chronicles of Auvergne as being armed

[009-092]with an axe on account of the family indigence, which to this day

[009-093]weighs heavily on the race. This noble baron, famous for

[009-094]discomfiting a vast number of infidels, died, without “or” or

[009-095]“fer,” as naked as a worm, near Jerusalem, on the plains of

[009-096]Ascalon, ambulances not being then invented.

[009-097]The chateau of Canalis (the domain yields a few chestnuts)

[009-098]consists of two dismantled towers, united by a piece of wall

[009-099]covered by a fine ivy, and is taxed at twenty-two francs.

[009-100]The undersigned (publisher) calls attention to the fact that he

[009-101]pays ten thousand francs for every volume of poetry written by

[009-102]Monsieur de Canalis, who does not give his shells, or his nuts

[009-103]either, for nothing.

[009-104]The chanticler of the Correze lives in the rue de

[009-105]Paradis-Poissoniere, number 29, which is a highly suitable

[009-106]location for a poet of the angelic school. Letters must be

[009-107]_post-paid_.

[009-108]Noble dames of the faubourg Saint-Germain are said to take the

[009-109]path to Paradise and protect its god. The king, Charles X., thinks

[009-110]so highly of this great poet as to believe him capable of

[009-111]governing the country; he has lately made him officer of the

[009-112]Legion of [[honor]], and (what pays him better) president of the court

[009-113]of Claims at the foreign office. These functions do not hinder

[009-114]this great genius from drawing an annuity out of the fund for the

[009-115]encouragement of the arts and belles letters.

[009-116]The last edition of the works of Canalis, printed on vellum, royal

[009-117]8vo, from the press of Didot, with illustrations by Bixiou, Joseph

[009-118]Bridau, Schinner, Sommervieux, etc., is in five volumes, price,

[009-119]nine francs post-paid.

[009-120]This letter fell like a cobble-stone on a tulip. A poet, secretary

[009-121]of claims, getting a stipend in a public office, drawing an

[009-122]annuity, seeking a decoration, adored by the women of the faubourg

[009-123]Saint-Germain--was that the muddy minstrel lingering along the quays,

[009-124]sad, dreamy, worn with toil, and re-entering his garret fraught with

[009-125]poetry? However, Modeste perceived the irony of the envious bookseller,

[009-126]who dared to say, “I invented Canalis; I made Nathan!” Besides, she

[009-127]re-read her hero’s poems,--verses extremely seductive, insincere, and

[009-128]hypocritical, which require a word of analysis, were it only to explain

[009-129]her infatuation.

[009-130]Canalis may be distinguished from Lamartine, chief of the angelic

[009-131]school, by a wheedling tone like that of a sick-nurse, a treacherous

[009-132]sweetness, and a delightful correctness of diction. If the chief with

[009-133]his strident cry is an eagle, Canalis, rose and white, is a flamingo.

[009-134]In him women find the friend they seek, their interpreter, a being who

[009-135]understands them, who explains them to themselves, and a safe confidant.

[009-136]The wide margins given by Didot to the last edition were crowded with

[009-137]Modeste’s pencilled sentiments, expressing her sympathy with this tender

[009-138]and dreamy spirit. Canalis does not possess the gift of life; he cannot

[009-139]breathe existence into his creations; but he knows how to calm vague

[009-140]sufferings like those which assailed Modeste. He speaks to young girls

[009-141]in their own language; he can allay the anguish of a bleeding wound

[009-142]and lull the moans, even the sobs of woe. His gift lies not in stirring

[009-143]words, nor in the remedy of strong emotions, he contents himself with

[009-144]saying in harmonious tones which compel belief, “I suffer with you; I

[009-145]understand you; come with me; let us weep together beside the brook,

[009-146]beneath the willows.” And they follow him! They listen to his empty and

[009-147]sonorous poetry like infants to a nurse’s lullaby. Canalis, like Nodier,

[009-148]enchants the reader by an artlessness which is genuine in the prose

[009-149]writer and artificial in the poet, by his tact, his smile, the shedding

[009-150]of his rose-leaves, in short by his infantile philosophy. He imitates

[009-151]so well the language of our early youth that he leads us back to

[009-152]the prairie-land of our illusions. We can be pitiless to the eagles,

[009-153]requiring from them the quality of the diamond, incorruptible

[009-154]perfection; but as for Canalis, we take him for what he is and let the

[009-155]rest go. He seems a good fellow; the affectations of the angelic school

[009-156]have answered his purpose and succeeded, just as a woman succeeds when

[009-157]she plays the ingenue cleverly, and simulates surprise, youth, innocence

[009-158]betrayed, in short, the wounded angel.

[009-159]Modeste, recovering her first impression, renewed her confidence in

[009-160]that soul, in that countenance as ravishing as the face of Bernardin de

[009-161]Saint-Pierre. She paid no further attention to the publisher. And so,

[009-162]about the beginning of the month of August she wrote the following

[009-163]letter to this Dorat of the sacristy, who still ranks as a star of the

[009-164]modern Pleiades.

[009-165]To Monsieur de Canalis,--Many a time, monsieur, I have wished to

[009-166]write to you; and why? Surely you guess why,--to tell you how much

[009-167]I admire your genius. Yes, I feel the need of expressing to you

[009-168]the admiration of a poor country girl, lonely in her little

[009-169]corner, whose only happiness is to read your thoughts. I have read

[009-170]Rene, and I come to you. Sadness leads to reverie. How many other

[009-171]women are sending you the homage of their secret thoughts? What

[009-172]chance have I for notice among so many? This paper, filled with my

[009-173]soul,--can it be more to you than the perfumed letters which

[009-174]already beset you. I come to you with less grace than others, for

[009-175]I wish to remain unknown and yet to receive your entire confidence

[009-176]--as though you had long known me.

[009-177]Answer my letter and be friendly with me. I cannot promise to make

[009-178]myself known to you, though I do not positively say I will not

[009-179]some day do so.

[009-180]What shall I add? Read between the lines of this letter, monsieur,

[009-181]the great effort which I am making: permit me to offer you my

[009-182]hand,--that of a friend, ah! a true friend.
