# Section 42

[042-001]“Before we part forever,” said [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]], interrupting a la [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], and

[042-002]speaking in a voice of some emotion, “I wish to ask you one thing; and

[042-003]though you once disguised yourself, I think you cannot be so base as to

[042-004]deceive me now.”

[042-005]The taunt made him turn pale, and he cried out, “Oh, you are pitiless!”

[042-006]“Will you be frank?”

[042-007]“You have the right to ask me that degrading question,” he said, in a

[042-008]voice weakened by the violent palpitation of his heart.

[042-009]“Well, then, did you read my [[epistolary-novel|letters]] to Monsieur de Canalis?”

[042-010]“No, mademoiselle; and I allowed your father to read them it was to

[042-011]justify my [[love]] by showing him how it was born, and how sincere my

[042-012]efforts were to cure you of your fancy.”

[042-013]“But how came the idea of that unworthy masquerade ever to arise?” she

[042-014]said, with a sort of impatience.

[042-015]La Briere related truthfully the scene in the [[poetry|poet]]’s study which

[042-016]Modeste’s first letter had occasioned, and the sort of challenge that

[042-017]resulted from his expressing a favorable opinion of a young girl thus

[042-018]led toward a poet’s fame, as a plant seeks its share of the sun.

[042-019]“You have said enough,” said Modeste, restraining some emotion. “If you

[042-020]have not my heart, monsieur, you have at least my esteem.”

[042-021]These simple words gave the young man a violent shock; feeling himself

[042-022]stagger, he leaned against a tree, like a man deprived for a moment of

[042-023]reason. Modest, who had left him, turned her head and came hastily back.

[042-024]“What is the matter?” she asked, taking his hand to prevent him from

[042-025]falling.

[042-026]“Forgive me--I thought you despised me.”

[042-027]“But,” she answered, with a distant and disdainful manner, “I did not

[042-028]say that I loved you.”

[042-029]And she left him again. But this time, in spite of her harshness, La

[042-030]Briere thought he walked on air; the earth softened under his feet, the

[042-031]trees bore flowers; the skies were rosy, the air cerulean, as they are

[042-032]in the temples of Hymen in those fairy pantomimes which finish happily.

[042-033]In such situations every woman is a Janus, and sees behind her without

[042-034]turning round; and thus Modeste perceived on the face of her lover the

[042-035]indubitable symptoms of a love like Butscha’s,--surely the “ne plus

[042-036]ultra” of a woman’s hope. Moreover, the great value which La Briere

[042-037]attached to her opinion filled Modeste with an emotion that was

[042-038]inestimably sweet.

[042-039]“Mademoiselle,” said Canalis, leaving the colonel and waylaying Modeste,

[042-040]“in spite of the little value you attach to my sentiments, my [[honor]] is

[042-041]concerned in effacing a stain under which I have suffered too long. Here

[042-042]is a letter which I received from the Duchesse de Chaulieu five days

[042-043]after my arrival in [[provincial-life|Havre]].”

[042-044]He let Modeste read the first lines of the letter we have seen, which

[042-045]the duchess began by saying that she had seen Mongenod, and now wished

[042-046]to marry her poet to Modeste; then he tore that passage from the body of

[042-047]the letter, and placed the fragment in her hand.

[042-048]“I cannot let you read the rest,” he said, putting the paper in his

[042-049]pocket; “but I confide these few lines to your discretion, so that you

[042-050]may verify the writing. A young girl who could accuse me of ignoble

[042-051]sentiments is quite capable of suspecting some collusion, some trickery.

[042-052]Ah, Modeste,” he said, with tears in his voice, “your poet, the poet of

[042-053]Madame de Chaulieu, has no less poetry in his heart than in his mind.

[042-054]You are about to see the duchess; suspend your judgment of me till

[042-055]then.”

[042-056]He left Modeste half bewildered.

[042-057]“Oh, dear!” she said to herself; “it seems they are all angels--and not

[042-058]marriageable; the duke is the only one that belongs to humanity.”

[042-059]“Mademoiselle Modeste,” said Butscha, appearing with a parcel under his

[042-060]arm, “this hunt makes me very uneasy. I dreamed your horse ran away with

[042-061]you, and I have been to Rouen to see if I could get a Spanish bit which,

[042-062]they tell me, a horse can’t take between his teeth. I entreat you to

[042-063]use it. I have shown it to the colonel, and he has thanked me more than

[042-064]there is any occasion for.”

[042-065]“Poor, dear Butscha!” cried Modeste, moved to tears by this maternal

[042-066]care.

[042-067]Butscha went skipping off like a man who has just heard of the death of

[042-068]a rich uncle.

[042-069]“My dear father,” said Modeste, returning to the salon; “I should like

[042-070]to have that beautiful whip,--suppose you were to ask Monsieur de La

[042-071]Briere to exchange it for your picture by Van Ostade.”

[042-072]Modeste looked furtively at [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], while the colonel made him this

[042-073]proposition, standing before the picture which was the sole thing he

[042-074]possessed in memory of his campaigns, having bought it of a burgher

[042-075]at Rabiston; and she said to herself as La Briere left the room

[042-076]precipitately, “He will be at the hunt.”

[042-077]A curious thing happened. Modeste’s three lovers each and all went to

[042-078]Rosembray with their hearts full of hope, and captivated by her many

[042-079]perfections.

[042-080]Rosembray,--an estate lately purchased by the Duc de Verneuil, with the

[042-081]money which fell to him as his share of the thousand millions voted as

[042-082]indemnity for the sale of the lands of the emigres,--is remarkable for

[042-083]its chateau, whose magnificence compares only with that of Mesniere or

[042-084]of Balleroy. This imposing and noble edifice is approached by a wide

[042-085]avenue of four rows of venerable elms, from which the visitor enters

[042-086]an immense rising court-yard, like that at Versailles, with magnificent

[042-087]iron railings and two lodges, and adorned with rows of large

[042-088]orange-trees in their tubs. Facing this court-yard, the chateau

[042-089]presents, between two fronts of the main building which retreat on

[042-090]either side of this projection, a double row of nineteen tall windows,

[042-091]with carved arches and diamond panes, divided from each other by a

[042-092]series of fluted pilasters surmounted by an entablature which hides an

[042-093]Italian roof, from which rise several stone chimneys masked by

[042-094]carved trophies of arms. Rosembray was built, under Louis XIV., by a

[042-095]“fermier-general” named Cottin. The facade toward the park differs from

[042-096]that on the court-yard by having a narrower projection in the centre,

[042-097]with columns between five windows, above which rises a magnificent

[042-098]pediment. The family of Marigny, to whom the estates of this Cottin were

[042-099]brought in [[marriage]] by Mademoiselle Cottin, her father’s sole heiress,

[042-100]ordered a sunrise to be carved on this pediment by Coysevox. Beneath it

[042-101]are two angels unwinding a scroll, on which is cut this motto in honor

[042-102]of the Grand Monarch, “Sol nobis benignus.”

[042-103]From the portico, reached by two grand circular and balustraded flights

[042-104]of steps, the view extends over an immense fish-pond, as long and wide

[042-105]as the grand canal at Versailles, beginning at the foot of a grass-plot

[042-106]which compares well with the finest English lawns, and bordered with

[042-107]beds and baskets now filled with the brilliant flowers of autumn. On

[042-108]either side of the piece of water two gardens, laid out in the French

[042-109]style, display their squares and long straight paths, like brilliant

[042-110]pages written in the ciphers of Lenotre. These gardens are backed to

[042-111]their whole length by a border of nearly thirty acres of woodland. From

[042-112]the terrace the view is bounded by a forest belonging to Rosembray and

[042-113]contiguous to two other forests, one of which belongs to the Crown, the

[042-114]other to the State. It would be difficult to find a nobler landscape.

[042-115]CHAPTER XXVII. A GIRL’S REVENGE

[042-116]Modeste’s arrival at Rosembray made a certain sensation in the avenue

[042-117]when the carriage with the liveries of France came in sight, accompanied

[042-118]by the grand equerry, the colonel, Canalis, and La Briere on

[042-119]horseback, preceded by an outrider in full dress, and followed by six

[042-120]servants,--among whom were the Negroes and the mulatto,--and the britzka

[042-121]of the colonel for the two waiting-women and the luggage. The carriage

[042-122]was drawn by four horses, ridden by postilions dressed with an elegance

[042-123]specially commanded by the grand equerry, who was often better served

[042-124]than the king himself. As Modeste, dazzled by the magnificence of the

[042-125]great lords, entered and beheld this lesser Versailles, she suddenly

[042-126]remembered her approaching interview with the celebrated duchesses, and

[042-127]began to fear that she might seem awkward, or provincial, or parvenue;

[042-128]in fact, she lost her self-possession, and heartily repented having

[042-129]wished for a hunt.

[042-130]Fortunately, however, as the carriage drew up, Modeste saw an old man,

[042-131]in a blond wig frizzed into little curls, whose calm, plump, smooth face

[042-132]wore a fatherly smile and an expression of monastic cheerfulness which

[042-133]the half-veiled glance of the eye rendered almost noble. This was the

[042-134]Duc de Verneuil, master of Rosembray. The duchess, a woman of extreme

[042-135]piety, the only daughter of a rich and deceased chief-justice, spare

[042-136]and erect, and the mother of four children, resembled Madame

[042-137]Latournelle,--if the imagination can go so far as to adorn the notary’s

[042-138]wife with the graces of a bearing that was truly abbatial.

[042-139]“Ah, good morning, dear Hortense!” said Mademoiselle d’Herouville,

[042-140]kissing the duchess with the sympathy that united their haughty

[042-141]natures; “let me present to you and to the dear duke our little angel,

[042-142]Mademoiselle de La Bastie.”

[042-143]“We have heard so much of you, mademoiselle,” said the duchess, “that we

[042-144]were in haste to receive you.”

[042-145]“And regret the time lost,” added the Duc de Verneuil, with courteous

[042-146]admiration.

[042-147]“Monsieur le Comte de La Bastie,” said the grand equerry, taking the

[042-148]colonel by the arm and presenting him to the duke and duchess, with an

[042-149]air of respect in his tone and gesture.

[042-150]“I am glad to welcome you, Monsieur le comte!” said Monsieur de

[042-151]Verneuil. “You possess more than one treasure,” he added, looking at

[042-152]Modeste.

[042-153]The duchess took Modeste under her arm and led her into an immense

[042-154]salon, where a dozen or more women were grouped about the fireplace. The

[042-155]men of the party remained with the duke on the terrace, except Canalis,

[042-156]who respectfully made his way to the superb Eleonore. The Duchesse de

[042-157]Chaulieu, seated at an embroidery-frame, was showing Mademoiselle de

[042-158]Verneuil how to shade a flower.

[042-159]If Modeste had run a needle through her finger when handling a

[042-160]pin-cushion she could not have felt a sharper prick than she received

[042-161]from the cold and haughty and contemptuous stare with which Madame de

[042-162]Chaulieu favored her. For an instant she saw nothing but that one woman,

[042-163]and she saw through her. To understand the depths of cruelty to which

[042-164]these charming creatures, whom our passions deify, can go, we must see

[042-165]women with each other. Modeste would have disarmed almost any other than

[042-166]Eleonore by the perfectly stupid and involuntary admiration which

[042-167]her face betrayed. Had she not known the duchess’s age she would have

[042-168]thought her a woman of thirty-six; but other and greater astonishments

[042-169]awaited her.

[042-170]The poet had run plump against a great lady’s anger. Such anger is the

[042-171]worst of sphinxes; the face is radiant, all the rest menacing. Kings

[042-172]themselves cannot make the exquisite politeness of a mistress’s cold

[042-173]anger capitulate when she guards it with steel armor. Canalis tried to

[042-174]cling to the steel, but his fingers slipped on the polished surface,

[042-175]like his words on the heart; and the gracious face, the gracious words,

[042-176]the gracious bearing of the duchess hid the steel of her wrath, now

[042-177]fallen to twenty-five below zero, from all observers. The appearance

[042-178]of Modeste in her sublime beauty, and dressed as well as Diane de

[042-179]Maufrigneuse herself, had fired the train of gunpowder which reflection

[042-180]had been laying in Eleonore’s mind.

[042-181]All the women had gone to the windows to see the new wonder get out of

[042-182]the royal carriage, attended by her three suitors.

[042-183]“Do not let us seem so curious,” Madame de Chaulieu had said, cut to the

[042-184]heart by Diane’s exclamation,--“She is divine! where in the world

[042-185]does she come from?”--and with that the bevy flew back to their seats,

[042-186]resuming their composure, though Eleonore’s heart was full of hungry

[042-187]vipers all clamorous for a meal.
