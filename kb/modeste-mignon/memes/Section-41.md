# Section 41

[041-001]“Henri, let us go to [[provincial-life|Havre]]!” cried the duchess, interrupting him.

[041-002]“Under what pretext?” said her husband, gravely; he was one of the

[041-003]confidants of Louis XVIII.

[041-004]“I never saw a hunt.”

[041-005]“It would be all very well if the king went; but it is a terrible bore

[041-006]to go so far, and he will not do it; I have just been speaking with him

[041-007]about it.”

[041-008]“Perhaps _Madame_ would go?”

[041-009]“That would be better,” returned the duke, “I dare say the Duchesse de

[041-010]Maufrigneuse would help you to persuade her from Rosny. If she goes the

[041-011]king will not be displeased at the use of his hunting equipage. Don’t

[041-012]go to Havre, my dear,” added the duke, paternally, “that would be giving

[041-013]yourself away. Come, here’s a better plan, I think. Gaspard’s chateau of

[041-014]Rosembray is on the other side of the forest of Brotonne; why not give

[041-015]him a hint to invite the whole party?”

[041-016]“He invite them?” said Eleonore.

[041-017]“I mean, of course, the duchess; she is always engaged in pious works

[041-018]with Mademoiselle d’Herouville; give that old maid a hint, and get her

[041-019]to speak to Gaspard.”

[041-020]“You are a [[love]] of a man,” cried Eleonore; “I’ll write to the old maid

[041-021]and to Diane at once, for we must get hunting things made,--a riding hat

[041-022]is so becoming. Did you win last night at the English embassy?”

[041-023]“Yes,” said the duke; “I cleared myself.”

[041-024]“Henri, above all things, stop proceedings about Melchior’s two

[041-025]appointments.”

[041-026]After writing half a dozen lines to the beautiful Diane de Maufrigneuse,

[041-027]and a short hint to Mademoiselle d’Herouville, Eleonore sent the

[041-028]following answer like the lash of a whip through [[melchior-de-canalis|the poet]]’s lies.

[041-029]To Monsieur le Baron de Canalis:--

[041-030]My dear [[poetry|poet]],--Mademoiselle de La Bastie is very beautiful;

[041-031]Mongenod has proved to me that her father has millions. I did

[041-032]think of marrying you to her; I am therefore much displeased at

[041-033]your want of confidence. If you had any intention of marrying La

[041-034]Briere when you went to Havre it is surprising that you said

[041-035]nothing to me about it before you started. And why have you

[041-036]omitted writing to a friend who is so easily made anxious as I?

[041-037]Your letter arrived a trifle late; I had already seen the banker.

[041-038]You are a child, Melchior, and you are playing tricks with us. It

[041-039]is not right. The duke himself is quite indignant at your

[041-040]proceedings; he thinks you less than a gentleman, which casts some

[041-041]reflections on your mother’s [[honor]].

[041-042]Now, I intend to see things for myself. I shall, I believe, have

[041-043]the honor of accompanying _Madame_ to the hunt which the Duc

[041-044]d’Herouville proposes to give for Mademoiselle de La Bastie. I

[041-045]will manage to have you invited to Rosembray, for the meet will

[041-046]probably take place in Duc de Verneuil’s park.

[041-047]Pray believe, my dear poet, that I am none the less, for life,

[041-048]Your friend, Eleonore de M.

[041-049]“There, [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]], just look at that!” cried Canalis, tossing the letter

[041-050]at Ernest’s nose across the breakfast-table; “that’s the two thousandth

[041-051]love-letter I have had from that woman, and there isn’t even a ‘thou’ in

[041-052]it. The illustrious Eleonore has never compromised herself more than she

[041-053]does there. Marry, and try your luck! The worst [[marriage]] in the world

[041-054]is better than this sort of halter. Ah, I am the greatest Nicodemus that

[041-055]ever tumbled out of the moon! [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] has millions, and I’ve lost

[041-056]her; for we can’t get back from the poles, where we are to-day, to the

[041-057]tropics, where we were three days ago! Well, I am all the more anxious

[041-058]for your triumph over the grand equerry, because I told the duchess I

[041-059]came here only for your sake; and so I shall do my best for you.”

[041-060]“Alas, Melchior, Modeste must needs have so noble, so grand, so

[041-061]well-balanced a nature to resist the glories of the Court, and all these

[041-062]splendors cleverly displayed for her honor and glory by the duke, that I

[041-063]cannot believe in the existence of such perfection,--and yet, if she is

[041-064]still the Modeste of her [[epistolary-novel|letters]], there might be hope!”

[041-065]“Well, well, you are a happy fellow, you young Boniface, to see the

[041-066]world and your mistress through green spectacles!” cried Canalis,

[041-067]marching off to pace up and down the garden.

[041-068]Caught between two lies, the poet was at a loss what to do.

[041-069]“Play by rule, and you lose!” he cried presently, sitting down in the

[041-070]kiosk. “Every man of sense would have acted as I did four days ago, and

[041-071]got himself out of the net in which I saw myself. At such times people

[041-072]don’t disentangle nets, they break through them! Come, let us be calm,

[041-073]cold, dignified, affronted. Honor requires it; English stiffness is the

[041-074]only way to win her back. After all, if I have to retire finally, I can

[041-075]always fall back on my old happiness; a fidelity of ten years can’t go

[041-076]unrewarded. Eleonore will arrange me some good marriage.”

[041-077]CHAPTER XXVI. TRUE LOVE

[041-078]The hunt was destined to be not only a meet of the hounds, but a meeting

[041-079]of all the passions excited by the colonel’s millions and Modeste’s

[041-080]beauty; and while it was in prospect there was truce between the

[041-081]adversaries. During the days required for the arrangement of this

[041-082]forestrial solemnity, the salon of the villa [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] presented the

[041-083]tranquil picture of a united family. Canalis, cut short in his role of

[041-084]injured love by Modeste’s quick perceptions, wished to appear courteous;

[041-085]he laid aside his pretensions, gave no further specimens of his

[041-086]oratory, and became, what all men of intellect can be when they renounce

[041-087]affectation, perfectly charming. He talked finances with Gobenheim, and

[041-088]war with the colonel, Germany with Madame Mignon, and housekeeping with

[041-089]Madame Latournelle,--endeavoring to bias them all in favor of La Briere.

[041-090]The Duc d’Herouville left the field to his rivals, for he was obliged

[041-091]to go to Rosembray to consult with the Duc de Verneuil, and see that the

[041-092]orders of the Royal Huntsman, the Prince de Cadignan, were carried out.

[041-093]And yet the comic element was not altogether wanting. Modeste found

[041-094]herself between the depreciatory hints of Canalis as to the gallantry

[041-095]of the grand equerry, and the exaggerations of the two Mesdemoiselles

[041-096]d’Herouville, who passed every evening at the villa. Canalis made

[041-097]Modeste take notice that, instead of being the heroine of the hunt, she

[041-098]would be scarcely noticed. _Madame_ would be attended by the Duchesse de

[041-099]Maufrigneuse, daughter-in-law of the Prince de Cadignan, by the Duchesse

[041-100]de Chaulieu, and other great ladies of the Court, among whom she could

[041-101]produce no sensation; no doubt the officers in garrison at Rouen would

[041-102]be invited, etc. Helene, on the other hand, was incessantly telling her

[041-103]new friend, whom she already looked upon as a sister-in-law, that she

[041-104]was to be presented to _Madame_; undoubtedly the Duc de Verneuil would

[041-105]invite her father and herself to stay at Rosembray; if the colonel

[041-106]wished to obtain a favor of the king,--a peerage, for instance,--the

[041-107]opportunity was unique, for there was hope of the king himself being

[041-108]present on the third day; she would be delighted with the charming

[041-109]welcome with which the beauties of the Court, the Duchesses de Chaulieu,

[041-110]de Maufrigneuse, de Lenoncourt-Chaulieu, and other ladies, were prepared

[041-111]to meet her. It was in fact an excessively amusing little warfare, with

[041-112]its marches and countermarches and stratagems,--all of which were keenly

[041-113]enjoyed by the Dumays, the Latournelles, Gobenheim, and Butscha, who,

[041-114]in conclave assembled, said horrible things of these noble personages,

[041-115]cruelly noting and intelligently studying all their little meannesses.

[041-116]The promises on the d’Herouville side were, however, confirmed by the

[041-117]arrival of an invitation, couched in flattering terms, from the Duc de

[041-118]Verneuil and the Master of the Hunt to Monsieur le Comte de La Bastie

[041-119]and his daughter, to stay at Rosembray and be present at a grand hunt on

[041-120]the seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth, of November following.

[041-121]La Briere, full of dark presentiments, craved the presence of Modeste

[041-122]with an eagerness whose bitter joys are known only to lovers who feel

[041-123]that they are parted, and parted fatally from those they love. Flashes

[041-124]of joy came to him intermingled with melancholy meditations on the one

[041-125]theme, “I have lost her,” and made him all the more interesting to those

[041-126]who watched him, because his face and his whole person were in keeping

[041-127]with his profound feeling. There is nothing more poetic than a living

[041-128]elegy, animated by a pair of eyes, walking about, and sighing without

[041-129]rhymes.

[041-130]The Duc d’Herouville arrived at last to arrange for Modeste’s departure;

[041-131]after crossing the Seine she was to be conveyed in the duke’s caleche,

[041-132]accompanied by the Demoiselles d’Herouville. The duke was charmingly

[041-133]courteous, he begged Canalis and La Briere to be of the party, assuring

[041-134]them, as he did the colonel, that he had taken particular care that

[041-135]hunters should be provided for them. The colonel invited the three

[041-136]lovers to breakfast on the morning of the start. Canalis then began to

[041-137]put into execution a plan that he had been maturing in his own mind for

[041-138]the last few days; namely, to quietly reconquer Modeste, and throw over

[041-139]the duchess, La Briere, and the duke. A graduate of diplomacy could

[041-140]hardly remain stuck in the position in which he found himself. On the

[041-141]other hand La Briere had come to the resolution of bidding Modeste an

[041-142]eternal farewell. Each suitor was therefore on the watch to slip in a

[041-143]last word, like the defendant’s counsel to the court before judgment is

[041-144]pronounced; for all felt that the three weeks’ struggle was approaching

[041-145]its conclusion. After dinner on the evening before the start was to be

[041-146]made, the colonel had taken his daughter by the arm and made her feel

[041-147]the necessity of deciding.

[041-148]“Our position with the d’Herouville family will be quite intolerable at

[041-149]Rosembray,” he said to her. “Do you mean to be a duchess?”

[041-150]“No, father,” she answered.

[041-151]“Then do you love Canalis?”

[041-152]“No, papa, a thousand times no!” she exclaimed with the impatience of a

[041-153]child.

[041-154]The colonel looked at her with a sort of joy.

[041-155]“Ah, I have not influenced you,” cried the true father, “and I will

[041-156]now confess that I chose my son-in-law in [[paris|Paris]] when, having made him

[041-157]believe that I had but little [[commerce|fortune]], he grasped my hand and told me I

[041-158]took a weight from his mind--”

[041-159]“Who is it you mean?” asked Modeste, coloring.

[041-160]“_The man of fixed principles and sound moralities_,” said her father,

[041-161]slyly, repeating the words which had dissolved poor Modeste’s dream on

[041-162]the day after his return.

[041-163]“I was not even thinking of him, papa. Please leave me at liberty to

[041-164]refuse the duke myself; I understand him, and I know how to soothe him.”

[041-165]“Then your choice is not made?”

[041-166]“Not yet; there is another syllable or two in the charade of my destiny

[041-167]still to be guessed; but after I have had a glimpse of court life at

[041-168]Rosembray I will tell you my secret.”

[041-169]“Ah! Monsieur de La Briere,” cried the colonel, as the young man

[041-170]approached them along the garden path in which they were walking, “I

[041-171]hope you are going to this hunt?”

[041-172]“No, colonel,” answered Ernest. “I have come to take leave of you and of

[041-173]mademoiselle; I return to Paris--”

[041-174]“You have no curiosity,” said Modeste, interrupting, and looking at him.

[041-175]“A wish--that I cannot expect--would suffice to keep me,” he replied.

[041-176]“If that is all, you must stay to please me; I wish it,” said the

[041-177]colonel, going forward to meet Canalis, and leaving his daughter and La

[041-178]Briere together for a moment.

[041-179]“Mademoiselle,” said the young man, raising his eyes to hers with the

[041-180]boldness of a man without hope, “I have an entreaty to make to you.”

[041-181]“To me?”

[041-182]“Let me carry away with me your forgiveness. My life can never be happy;

[041-183]it must be full of remorse for having lost my happiness--no doubt by my

[041-184]own fault; but, at least,--”
