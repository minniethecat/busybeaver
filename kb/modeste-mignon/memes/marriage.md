# Marriage

Marriage in *Modeste Mignon* is never merely a union of individuals — it is a strategic alliance involving fortune, rank, and family honor, whose successful negotiation constitutes the novel's comic resolution.

## Definition

Balzac treats marriage as the central social institution of Restoration France, simultaneously inevitable and fraught with peril. The novel opens with a marriage plot in embryo — Modeste is of marriageable age, and the community observes her with proprietary interest. The notary Latournelle's son is being deployed as a "conspiracy" participant (001-048), suggesting that marriage in Havre is a communal affair orchestrated by the older generation. Yet Modeste disrupts this provincial marriage economy by initiating her own romantic correspondence, threatening to choose her own husband outside the established channels.

## In the Novel

### Manifestations

Marriage structures the novel's plot as both goal and threat. Modeste's three suitors — Canalis, La Brière, and the Duc d'Hérouville — represent three different marriage economies: marriage to genius (Canalis), marriage to virtue (La Brière), and marriage to rank (d'Hérouville). The novel's resolution endorses the second option while acknowledging the claims of the other two.

The mésalliance — marriage across class lines — is a persistent anxiety. Charles Mignon, though noble, married Bettina Wallenrod for love, and his commercial activities blur his aristocratic identity. The prospect of Modeste marrying beneath her station haunts the family's calculations, even as the novel ultimately validates love over rank.

## In Balzac's *La Comédie Humaine*

Marriage is Balzac's great sociological subject, examined across dozens of novels from *Physiologie du mariage* to *Béatrix*. *Modeste Mignon* belongs to the subset of Balzac's works — like *Eugénie Grandet* — that center on a young woman's marriage choice and its implications for family fortune and social position. Unlike Eugénie, however, Modeste is permitted a happy ending: she marries the right man.
