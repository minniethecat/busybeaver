# Pride in Modeste Mignon

Pride is the novel's most morally ambiguous quality. It can be the noble self-respect of [[Charles Mignon]], who refuses to declare bankruptcy dishonorably, or the brittle vanity of [[Melchior de Canalis]], whose pride is indistinguishable from egotism. Balzac draws a line through the heart of the concept: pride can elevate or destroy, depending on what it is pride IN.

## Role in the Novel

### Noble Pride

Charles Mignon embodies pride in its honorable form. His refusal to accept financial ruin passively — his departure for [[China]] to rebuild his fortune from nothing — is an act of pride: he will not be seen as a failure. "The model citizen had enough pride to furnish forth an entire aristocracy" (010-157). This pride is the foundation of his moral authority and the reason [[Dumay]] serves him with absolute loyalty.

### Modeste's Pride

Modeste's pride is more complicated. "Her pride could not endure" the humiliation of being deceived (007-141). Her dignity is real but precarious — it depends on being RIGHT about her judgments. When she discovers Canalis's deception, her pride is wounded but also vindicated: she was right to be suspicious, right to demand more than surface charm. "I, without pride!" she weeps — but the tears themselves are an assertion of wounded pride (025-057).

### Pride vs. Vanity

Balzac's most important distinction is between pride and [[vanity]]. "A man who has more vanity than pride" (010-019) — this is Canalis in a sentence. Vanity is pride's counterfeit: it seeks external validation rather than internal worth. The novel's moral taxonomy sorts characters by which of these two qualities dominates them. [[Butscha]] has pride without vanity; Canalis has vanity without pride; Modeste must learn to distinguish the two.

## Thematic Significance

### Pride as Social Marker

In the novel's social world, pride maps onto class. The aristocracy has ancestral pride; the bourgeoisie has commercial pride (or vanity disguised as pride). Mignon, the self-made man, has earned pride — a Balzacian synthesis of the best of both worlds. The [[d'Herouville family]] has inherited pride that is becoming hollow; the [[Vilquin family]] has pride that is really vanity.
