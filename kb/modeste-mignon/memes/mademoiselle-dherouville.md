# Mademoiselle d'Hérouville

The sister of the Duc d'Hérouville, Mademoiselle d'Hérouville is an aging aristocrat of formidable pretensions who channels the faded grandeur of her ancient Norman family into the singular project of securing a wealthy bride for her brother.

## Role in the Novel

Mademoiselle d'Hérouville functions as her brother's agent in the marriage market — a matchmaker whose ambition far exceeds her family's diminished means. She "made enormous pretensions" (027-148) in her quest to restore the d'Hérouville name to its former glory through strategic alliance. Her efforts on behalf of her brother form part of the novel's broader anatomy of marriage as a financial and social transaction in Restoration France. She represents the old nobility's desperate adaptation to a world in which titles without fortunes are increasingly worthless.

## Biography

### Background & Origins

Mademoiselle d'Hérouville belongs to one of Normandy's most distinguished families, whose lineage includes a celebrated Cardinal d'Hérouville (014-020). Now past her prime — she "floats between twenty and forty" (014-018), a Balzacian euphemism for a woman approaching middle age — she has devoted herself not to her own marriage prospects but to her brother's. Her identity is inseparable from the family name she bears and the aristocratic pride it confers.

### Key Actions

From 1817 to 1825, Mademoiselle d'Hérouville was "in quest of" a suitable match for her brother, though her efforts yielded no result (027-156). Her pretensions — "enormous" by Balzac's account (027-148) — made it "necessary to conciliate" her (027-153), suggesting that her demands in the marriage market outpaced what the d'Hérouville name alone could command. The Mignon fortune, newly restored by Charles Mignon's return from the East, represented precisely the kind of bourgeois wealth that could restore the d'Hérouvilles "to their former splendor" (027-164).

### Fate / Resolution

Mademoiselle d'Hérouville's matchmaking fails. Modeste Mignon chooses Ernest de La Brière, and the d'Hérouville hopes of restoration through marriage are dashed. Her fate mirrors her brother's — the old nobility's tools of name and lineage prove insufficient in a France where capital has become the true coin of social exchange.

## Character Traits

- **Defining qualities**: Ambitious, proud, relentless in pursuit of family restoration, conscious of lineage to the point of delusion (027-148)
- **Flaws / Contradictions**: Her "enormous pretensions" blind her to the shifting social realities of post-Napoleonic France; she acts as though the d'Hérouville name alone should command deference, while the marriage market demands cash as well as crests
- **Social Type**: The aristocratic spinster-sister — a recurring Balzacian figure, the woman who sublates her own life into the project of family advancement

## Balzacian Context

Mademoiselle d'Hérouville does not appear in other novels of *La Comédie Humaine*, but she belongs to Balzac's extensive portrait gallery of aristocratic women negotiating the collision between old wealth (land, name, lineage) and new wealth (commerce, capital, speculation). Her type recurs throughout the *Comédie Humaine*: the sister who manages her brother's marital affairs because the family name must not die.
