# Duc d'Hérouville

A Norman aristocrat of the highest lineage, the Duc d'Hérouville serves as one of Modeste Mignon's suitors, representing the old nobility's bid to reclaim its place in post-Napoleonic France through strategic marriage.

## Role in the Novel

The Duc d'Hérouville embodies the aristocratic suitor archetype — a man whose illustrious family name and ancient lineage make him a socially desirable match, yet whose personal qualities and diminished fortune reveal the hollow core of Restoration-era nobility. His courtship of Modeste Mignon exposes the transactional nature of aristocratic marriage, where a bride's dowry matters as much as her bloodline (027-063). He serves as a foil to both the poetic Canalis and the steadfast Ernest de La Brière, representing a third path that Modeste ultimately rejects.

## Biography

### Background & Origins

The Duc d'Hérouville belongs to one of Normandy's most storied families. His lineage includes a celebrated Cardinal d'Hérouville who "flourished in the history of the Church" (014-020). The family seat at Hérouville boasts a park and historic dependencies, though by the Restoration period the estate's grandeur had diminished (027-136). Born around 1792, he inherited the title and the burden of restoring the family's fortunes after the upheavals of the Revolution and Empire.

### Key Actions

Upon returning to France, the Duc established himself in Norman society, where the d'Hérouville name still commanded respect (027-070). His pursuit of Modeste Mignon was driven by necessity as much as inclination — the d'Hérouville fortune required a wealthy marriage to restore the family "to their former splendor" (027-164). The Duc's suit was approved by Modeste's father Charles Mignon, who saw in the match an alliance with one of France's oldest houses, confirming the "aristocratic" prestige that Charles himself, a wealthy but untitled merchant, could not claim (027-114).

### Fate / Resolution

The Duc d'Hérouville's suit ultimately fails. Modeste, having discovered the truth about Canalis's duplicity and Ernest de La Brière's genuine worth, chooses Ernest. The Duc returns to his Norman estate, his hopes of financial restoration through marriage dashed. His fate illustrates Balzac's recurring theme: in the new France of talent and capital, an ancient name alone is no longer enough.

## Character Traits

- **Defining qualities**: Conscious of lineage, dignified, pragmatic about marriage as a financial transaction, a representative of the old Norman aristocracy (027-131)
- **Flaws / Contradictions**: His title outweighs his substance; the family's diminished wealth forces him to treat marriage as commerce, undermining the very aristocratic pride he embodies
- **Social Type**: The Restoration aristocrat — a noble of ancient lineage but depleted resources, forced to negotiate with the new bourgeoisie for survival

## Balzacian Context

The Duc d'Hérouville does not appear in other novels of *La Comédie Humaine*; however, the d'Hérouville family and the theme of aristocratic decline in Normandy recur across Balzac's work. The character belongs to the type of Restoration noble that Balzac anatomizes throughout his fiction — the titled but impoverished aristocrat who must marry a bourgeois fortune.
