# Section 5

[005-001][[dumay|Dumay]], always subordinate, asked no questions of his colonel. “I think,”

[005-002]he said to Latournelle with a knowing little glance, “that my colonel

[005-003]has a plan laid out.”

[005-004]The following day at dawn he accompanied his master on board the

[005-005]“[[modeste-mignon|Modeste]]” bound for Constantinople. There, on the poop of the vessel,

[005-006]the Breton said to the Provencal,--

[005-007]“What are your last commands, my colonel?”

[005-008]“That no man shall enter [[chalet|the Chalet]],” cried the father with strong

[005-009]emotion. “Dumay, guard my last child as though you were a bull-dog.

[005-010]Death to the man who seduces another daughter! Fear nothing, not even

[005-011]the scaffold--I will be with you.”

[005-012]“My colonel, go in peace. I understand you. You shall find Mademoiselle

[005-013][[charles-mignon|Mignon]] on your return such as you now give her to me, or I shall be

[005-014]dead. You know me, and you know your Pyrenees hounds. No man shall reach

[005-015]your daughter. Forgive me for troubling you with words.”

[005-016]The two soldiers clasped arms like men who had learned to understand

[005-017]each other in the solitudes of Siberia.

[005-018]On the same day the [[provincial-life|Havre]] “Courier” published the following terrible,

[005-019]simple, energetic, and honorable notice:--

[005-020]“The house of Charles Mignon suspends payment. But the

[005-021]undersigned, assignees of the estate, undertake to pay all

[005-022]liabilities. On and after this date, holders of notes may obtain

[005-023]the usual discount. The sale of the landed estates will fully

[005-024]cover all current indebtedness.

[005-025]“This notice is issued for the [[honor]] of the house, and to prevent

[005-026]any disturbance in the money-market of this town.

[005-027]“Monsieur Charles Mignon sailed this morning on the ‘Modeste’ for

[005-028]Asia Minor, leaving full powers with the undersigned to sell his

[005-029]whole property, both landed and personal.

[005-030]“DUMAY, assignee of the Bank accounts,

[005-031]LATOURNELLE, notary, assignee of the city and villa property,

[005-032]GOBENHEIM, assignee of the commercial property.”

[005-033]Latournelle owed his prosperity to the kindness of Monsieur Mignon,

[005-034]who lent him one hundred thousand francs in 1817 to buy the finest law

[005-035]practice in Havre. The poor man, who had no pecuniary means, was nearly

[005-036]forty years of age and saw no prospect of being other than head-clerk

[005-037]for the rest of his days. He was the only man in Havre whose devotion

[005-038]could be compared with Dumay’s. As for Gobenheim, he profited by the

[005-039]liquidation to get a part of Monsieur Mignon’s [[commerce|business]], which lifted

[005-040]his own little bank into prominence.

[005-041]While unanimous regrets for the disaster were expressed in

[005-042]counting-rooms, on the wharves, and in private houses, where praises of

[005-043]a man so irreproachable, honorable, and beneficent filled every mouth,

[005-044]Latournelle and Dumay, silent and active as ants, sold land, turned

[005-045]property into money, paid the debts, and settled up everything.

[005-046]Vilquin showed a good deal of generosity in purchasing the villa, the

[005-047]town-house, and a farm; and Latournelle made the most of his liberality

[005-048]by getting a good price out of him. Society wished to show civilities to

[005-049]Madame and Mademoiselle Mignon; but they had already obeyed the father’s

[005-050]last wishes and taken refuge in the Chalet, where they went on the very

[005-051]morning of his departure, the exact hour of which had been concealed

[005-052]from them. Not to be shaken in his resolution by his grief at parting,

[005-053]the brave man said farewell to his wife and daughter while they slept.

[005-054]Three hundred visiting cards were left at the house. A fortnight later,

[005-055]just as Charles had predicted, complete forgetfulness settled down upon

[005-056]the Chalet, and proved to these women the wisdom and dignity of his

[005-057]command.

[005-058]Dumay sent agents to represent his master in New York, [[paris|Paris]], and

[005-059]London, and followed up the assignments of the three banking-houses

[005-060]whose failure had caused the ruin of the Havre house, thus realizing

[005-061]five hundred thousand francs between 1826 and 1828, an eighth of

[005-062]Charles’s whole [[social-mobility|fortune]]; then, according to the latter’s directions

[005-063]given on the night of his departure, he sent that sum to New York

[005-064]through the house of Mongenod to the credit of Monsieur Charles Mignon.

[005-065]All this was done with military obedience, except in a matter of

[005-066]withholding thirty thousand francs for the personal expenses of Madame

[005-067]and Mademoiselle Mignon as the colonel had ordered him to do, but

[005-068]which Dumay did not do. The Breton sold his own little house for twenty

[005-069]thousand francs, which sum he gave to Madame Mignon, believing that the

[005-070]more capital he sent to his colonel the sooner the latter would return.

[005-071]“He might perish for the want of thirty thousand francs,” Dumay remarked

[005-072]to Latournelle, who bought the little house at its full value, where an

[005-073]apartment was always kept ready for the inhabitants of the Chalet.

[005-074]CHAPTER IV. A SIMPLE STORY

[005-075]Such was the result to the celebrated house of Mignon at Havre of

[005-076]the crisis of 1825-26, which convulsed many of the principal business

[005-077]centres in Europe and caused the ruin of several Parisian bankers, among

[005-078]them (as those who remember that crisis will recall) the president of

[005-079]the chamber of commerce.

[005-080]We can now understand how this great disaster, coming suddenly at the

[005-081]close of ten years of domestic happiness, might well have been the death

[005-082]of Bettina Mignon, again separated from her husband and ignorant of his

[005-083]fate,--to her as adventurous and perilous as the exile to Siberia. But

[005-084]the grief which was dragging her to the grave was far other than these

[005-085]visible sorrows. The caustic that was slowly eating into her heart lay

[005-086]beneath a stone in the little graveyard of [[ingouville|Ingouville]], on which was

[005-087]inscribed:--

[005-088]BETTINA CAROLINE MIGNON

[005-089]Died aged twenty-two.

[005-090]Pray for her.

[005-091]This inscription is to the young girl whom it covered what many another

[005-092]epitaph has been for the dead lying beneath them,--a table of contents

[005-093]to a hidden book. Here is the book, in its dreadful brevity; and it will

[005-094]explain the oath exacted and taken when the colonel and the lieutenant

[005-095]bade each other farewell.

[005-096]A young man of charming appearance, named Charles d’Estourny, came to

[005-097]Havre for the commonplace purpose of being near the sea, and there he

[005-098]saw Bettina Mignon. A “soi-disant” fashionable Parisian is never without

[005-099]introductions, and he was invited at the instance of a friend of the

[005-100]Mignons to a fete given at Ingouville. He fell in [[love]] with Bettina and

[005-101]with her fortune, and in three months he had done the work of seduction

[005-102]and enticed her away. The father of a family of daughters should no more

[005-103]allow a young man whom he does not know to enter his home than he should

[005-104]leave books and papers lying about which he has not read. A young girl’s

[005-105]innocence is like milk, which a small matter turns sour,--a clap of

[005-106]thunder, an evil odor, a hot day, a mere breath.

[005-107]When Charles Mignon read his daughter’s letter of farewell he instantly

[005-108]despatched Madame Dumay to Paris. The family gave out that a journey

[005-109]to another climate had suddenly been advised for Caroline by their

[005-110]physician; and the physician himself sustained the excuse, though unable

[005-111]to prevent some gossip in the society of Havre. “Such a vigorous young

[005-112]girl! with the complexion of a Spaniard, and that black hair!--she

[005-113]consumptive!” “Yes, they say she committed some imprudence.” “Ah, ah!”

[005-114]cried a Vilquin. “I am told she came back bathed in perspiration after

[005-115]riding on horseback, and drank iced water; at least, that is what Dr.

[005-116]Troussenard says.”

[005-117]By the time Madame Dumay returned to Havre the catastrophe of the

[005-118]failure had taken place, and society paid no further attention to the

[005-119]absence of Bettina or the return of the cashier’s wife. At the beginning

[005-120]of 1827 the newspapers rang with the trial of Charles d’Estourny, who

[005-121]was found guilty of cheating at cards. The young corsair escaped into

[005-122]foreign parts without taking thought of Mademoiselle Mignon, who was of

[005-123]little value to him since the failure of the bank. Bettina heard of his

[005-124]infamous desertion and of her father’s ruin almost at the same time. She

[005-125]returned home struck by death, and wasted away in a short time at the

[005-126]Chalet. Her death at least protected her reputation. The illness that

[005-127]Monsieur Mignon alleged to be the cause of her absence, and the doctor’s

[005-128]order which sent her to Nice were now generally believed. Up to the last

[005-129]moment the mother hoped to save her daughter’s life. Bettina was her

[005-130]darling and Modeste was the father’s. There was something touching in

[005-131]the two preferences. Bettina was the image of Charles, just as Modeste

[005-132]was the reproduction of her mother. Both parents continued their love

[005-133]for each other in their children. Bettina, a daughter of Provence,

[005-134]inherited from her father the beautiful hair, black as a raven’s wing,

[005-135]which distinguishes the women of the South, the brown eye, almond-shaped

[005-136]and brilliant as a star, the olive tint, the velvet skin as of some

[005-137]golden fruit, the arched instep, and the Spanish waist from which the

[005-138]short basque skirt fell crisply. Both mother and father were proud of

[005-139]the charming contrast between the sisters. “A devil and an angel!” they

[005-140]said to each other, laughing, little thinking it prophetic.

[005-141]After weeping for a month in the solitude of her chamber, where she

[005-142]admitted no one, the mother came forth at last with injured eyes. Before

[005-143]losing her sight altogether she persisted, against the wishes of her

[005-144]friends, in visiting her daughter’s grave, on which she riveted her gaze

[005-145]in contemplation. That image remained vivid in the darkness which now

[005-146]fell upon her, just as the red spectrum of an object shines in our eyes

[005-147]when we close them in full daylight. This terrible and double misfortune

[005-148]made Dumay, not less devoted, but more anxious about Modeste, now the

[005-149]only daughter of the father who was unaware of his loss. Madame Dumay,

[005-150]idolizing Modeste, like other women deprived of their children, cast her

[005-151]motherliness about the girl,--yet without disregarding the commands

[005-152]of her husband, who distrusted female intimacies. Those commands were

[005-153]brief. “If any man, of any age, or any rank,” Dumay said, “speaks to

[005-154]Modeste, ogles her, makes love to her, he is a dead man. I’ll blow his

[005-155]brains out and give myself to the authorities; my death may save her. If

[005-156]you don’t wish to see my head cut off, do you take my place in watching

[005-157]her when I am obliged to go out.”

[005-158]For the last three years Dumay had examined his pistols every night. He

[005-159]seemed to have put half the burden of his oath upon the Pyrenean hounds,

[005-160]two animals of uncommon sagacity. One slept inside the Chalet, the

[005-161]other was stationed in a kennel which he never left, and where he never

[005-162]barked; but terrible would have been the moment had the pair made their

[005-163]teeth meet in some unknown adventurer.

[005-164]We can now imagine the sort of life led by mother and daughter at the

[005-165]Chalet. Monsieur and Madame Latournelle, often accompanied by Gobenheim,

[005-166]came to call and play whist with Dumay nearly every evening. The

[005-167]conversation turned on the gossip of Havre and the petty events of

[005-168]provincial life. The little company separated between nine and ten

[005-169]o’clock. Modeste put her mother to bed, and together they said their

[005-170]prayers, kept up each other’s courage, and talked of the dear absent

[005-171]one, the husband and father. After kissing her mother for good-night,

[005-172]the girl went to her own room about ten o’clock. The next morning she

[005-173]prepared her mother for the day with the same care, the same prayers,

[005-174]the same prattle. To her praise be it said that from the day when the

[005-175]terrible infirmity deprived her mother of a sense, Modeste had been like

[005-176]a servant to her, displaying at all times the same solicitude; never

[005-177]wearying of the duty, never thinking it monotonous. Such constant

[005-178]devotion, combined with a tenderness rare among young girls, was

[005-179]thoroughly appreciated by those who witnessed it. To the Latournelle

[005-180]family, and to Monsieur and Madame Dumay, Modeste was, in soul, the

[005-181]pearl of price.
