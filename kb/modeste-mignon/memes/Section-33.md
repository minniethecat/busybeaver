# Section 33

[033-001]“Rubbish! yes, that may be, but my rubbish is dear to me,” said the

[033-002]Duc d’Herouville, laughing, during the silent pause which followed the

[033-003][[poetry|poet]]’s pompous oration.

[033-004]“Let me ask,” said Butscha, attacking [[melchior-de-canalis|Canalis]], “does art, the sphere in

[033-005]which, according to you, genius is required to evolve itself, exist at

[033-006]all? Is it not a splendid lie, a delusion, of the social man? Do I want

[033-007]a landscape scene of Normandy in my bedroom when I can look out and see

[033-008]a better one done by God himself? Our dreams make poems more glorious

[033-009]than Iliads. For an insignificant sum of money I can find at Valogne, at

[033-010]Carentan, in Provence, at Arles, many a Venus as beautiful as those

[033-011]of Titian. The police gazette publishes tales, differing somewhat

[033-012]from those of Walter Scott, but ending tragically with blood, not ink.

[033-013]Happiness and virtue exist above and beyond both art and genius.”

[033-014]“Bravo, Butscha!” cried Madame Latournelle.

[033-015]“What did he say?” asked Canalis of La Briere, failing to gather from

[033-016]the eyes and attitude of Mademoiselle [[charles-mignon|Mignon]] the usual signs of artless

[033-017]admiration.

[033-018]The contemptuous indifference which [[modeste-mignon|Modeste]] had exhibited toward La

[033-019]Briere, and above all, her disrespectful speeches to her father, so

[033-020]depressed the young man that he made no answer to Canalis; his eyes,

[033-021]fixed sorrowfully on Modeste, were full of deep meditation. The Duc

[033-022]d’Herouville took up Butscha’s argument and reproduced it with much

[033-023]intelligence, saying finally that the ecstasies of Saint-Theresa were

[033-024]far superior to the creations of Lord Byron.

[033-025]“Oh, Monsieur le duc,” exclaimed Modeste, “hers was a purely personal

[033-026]poetry, whereas the genius of Lord Byron and Moliere benefit the world.”

[033-027]“How do you square that opinion with those of Monsieur le baron?” cried

[033-028]Charles Mignon, quickly. “Now you are insisting that genius must be

[033-029]useful, and benefit the world as though it were cotton,--but perhaps you

[033-030]think logic as antediluvian as your poor old father.”

[033-031]Butscha, La Briere, and Madame Latournelle exchanged glances that were

[033-032]more than half derisive, and drove Modeste to a pitch of irritation that

[033-033]kept her silent for a moment.

[033-034]“Mademoiselle, do not mind them,” said Canalis, smiling upon her, “we

[033-035]are neither beaten, nor caught in a contradiction. Every work of art,

[033-036]let it be in literature, [[music]], painting, sculpture, or architecture,

[033-037]implies a positive social utility, equal to that of all other commercial

[033-038]products. Art is pre-eminently [[commerce]]; presupposes it, in short. An

[033-039]author pockets ten thousand francs for his book; the making of books

[033-040]means the manufactory of paper, a foundry, a printing-office, a

[033-041]bookseller,--in other words, the employment of thousands of men. The

[033-042]execution of a symphony of Beethoven or an opera by Rossini requires

[033-043]human arms and machinery and manufactures. The cost of a monument is

[033-044]an almost brutal case in point. In short, I may say that the works of

[033-045]genius have an extremely costly basis and are, necessarily, useful to

[033-046]the workingman.”

[033-047]Astride of that theme, Canalis spoke for some minutes with a fine luxury

[033-048]of metaphor, and much inward complacency as to his phrases; but it

[033-049]happened with him, as with many another great speaker, that he found

[033-050]himself at last at the point from which the conversation started, and in

[033-051]full agreement with La Briere without perceiving it.

[033-052]“I see with much pleasure, my dear baron,” said the little duke, slyly,

[033-053]“that you will make an admirable constitutional minister.”

[033-054]“Oh!” said Canalis, with the gesture of a great man, “what is the use

[033-055]of all these discussions? What do they prove?--the eternal verity of one

[033-056]axiom: All things are true, all things are false. Moral truths as well

[033-057]as human beings change their aspect according to their surroundings, to

[033-058]the point of being actually unrecognizable.”

[033-059]“Society exists through settled opinions,” said the Duc d’Herouville.

[033-060]“What laxity!” whispered Madame Latournelle to her husband.

[033-061]“He is a poet,” said Gobenheim, who overheard her.

[033-062]Canalis, who was ten leagues above the heads of his audience, and who

[033-063]may have been right in his last philosophical remark, took the sort

[033-064]of coldness which now overspread the surrounding faces of a symptom of

[033-065][[provincial-life|provincial]] ignorance; but seeing that Modeste understood him, he

[033-066]was content, being wholly unaware that monologue is particularly

[033-067]disagreeable to country-folk, whose principal desire it is to exhibit

[033-068]the manner of life and the wit and wisdom of the provinces to Parisians.

[033-069]“It is long since you have seen the Duchesse de Chaulieu?” asked the

[033-070]duke, addressing Canalis, as if to change the conversation.

[033-071]“I left her about six days ago.”

[033-072]“Is she well?” persisted the duke.

[033-073]“Perfectly well.”

[033-074]“Have the kindness to remember me to her when you write.”

[033-075]“They say she is charming,” remarked Modeste, addressing the duke.

[033-076]“Monsieur le baron can speak more confidently than I,” replied the grand

[033-077]equerry.

[033-078]“More than charming,” said Canalis, making the best of the duke’s

[033-079]perfidy; “but I am partial, mademoiselle; she has been a friend to me

[033-080]for the last ten years; I owe all that is good in me to her; she has

[033-081]saved me from the dangers of the world. Moreover, Monsieur le Duc de

[033-082]Chaulieu launched me in my present career. Without the influence of that

[033-083]family the king and the princesses would have forgotten a poor poet

[033-084]like me; therefore my affection for the duchess must always be full of

[033-085]gratitude.”

[033-086]His voice quivered.

[033-087]“We ought to [[love]] the woman who has led you to write those sublime

[033-088]poems, and who inspires you with such noble feelings,” said Modeste,

[033-089]quite affected. “Who can think of a poet without a muse!”

[033-090]“He would be without a heart,” replied Canalis. “He would write barren

[033-091]verses like Voltaire, who never loved any one but Voltaire.”

[033-092]“I thought you did me the [[honor]] to say, in [[paris|Paris]],” interrupted [[dumay|Dumay]],

[033-093]“that you never felt the sentiments you expressed.”

[033-094]“The shoe fits, my soldier,” replied the poet, smiling; “but let me tell

[033-095]you that it is quite possible to have a great deal of feeling both in

[033-096]the intellectual life and in real life. My good friend here, La Briere,

[033-097]is madly in love,” continued Canalis, with a fine show of generosity,

[033-098]looking at Modeste. “I, who certainly love as much as he,--that is, I

[033-099]think so unless I delude myself,--well, I can give to my love a literary

[033-100]form in harmony with its character. But I dare not say, mademoiselle,”

[033-101]he added, turning to Modeste with too studied a grace, “that to-morrow I

[033-102]may not be without inspiration.”

[033-103]Thus the poet triumphed over all obstacles. In honor of his love he

[033-104]rode a-tilt at the hindrances that were thrown in his way, and Modeste

[033-105]remained wonder-struck at the Parisian wit that scintillated in his

[033-106]declamatory discourse, of which she had hitherto known little or

[033-107]nothing.

[033-108]“What an acrobat!” whispered Butscha to Latournelle, after listening

[033-109]to a magnificent tirade on the Catholic religion and the happiness

[033-110]of having a pious wife,--served up in response to a remark by Madame

[033-111]Mignon.

[033-112]Modeste’s eyes were blindfolded as it were; Canalis’s elocution and the

[033-113]close attention which she was predetermined to pay to him prevented her

[033-114]from seeing that Butscha was carefully noting the declamation, the want

[033-115]of simplicity, the emphasis that took the place of feeling, and the

[033-116]curious incoherencies in the poet’s speech which led the dwarf to make

[033-117]his rather cruel comment. At certain points of Canalis’s discourse, when

[033-118]Monsieur Mignon, Dumay, Butscha, and Latournelle wondered at the

[033-119]man’s utter want of logic, Modeste admired his suppleness, and said to

[033-120]herself, as she dragged him after her through the labyrinth of fancy,

[033-121]“He loves me!” Butscha, in common with the other spectators of what

[033-122]we must call a stage scene, was struck with the radiant defect of all

[033-123]egoists, which Canalis, like many men accustomed to perorate, allowed to

[033-124]be too plainly seen. Whether he understood beforehand what the person he

[033-125]was speaking to meant to say, whether he was not listening, or whether

[033-126]he had the faculty of listening when he was thinking of something

[033-127]else, it is certain that Melchior’s face wore an absent-minded look in

[033-128]conversation, which disconcerted the ideas of others and wounded their

[033-129]vanity. Not to listen is not merely a want of politeness, it is a mark

[033-130]of disrespect. Canalis pushed this habit too far; for he often forgot

[033-131]to answer a speech which required an answer, and passed, without the

[033-132]ordinary transitions of courtesy, to the subject, whatever it was, that

[033-133]preoccupied him. Though such impertinence is accepted without protest

[033-134]from a man of marked distinction, it stirs a leaven of hatred and

[033-135]vengeance in many hearts; in those of equals it even goes so far as to

[033-136]destroy a friendship. If by chance Melchior was forced to listen, he

[033-137]fell into another fault; he merely lent his attention, and never

[033-138]gave it. Though this may not be so mortifying, it shows a kind of

[033-139]semi-concession which is almost as unsatisfactory to the hearer and

[033-140]leaves him dissatisfied. Nothing brings more profit in the commerce

[033-141]of society than the small change of attention. He that heareth let him

[033-142]hear, is not only a gospel precept, it is an excellent speculation;

[033-143]follow it, and all will be forgiven you, even vice. Canalis took a great

[033-144]deal of trouble in his anxiety to please Modeste; but though he was

[033-145]compliant enough with her, he fell back into his natural self with the

[033-146]others.

[033-147]Modeste, pitiless for the ten martyrs she was making, begged Canalis to

[033-148]read some of his poems; she wanted, she said, a specimen of his gift for

[033-149]reading, of which she had heard so much. Canalis took the volume which

[033-150]she gave him, and cooed (for that is the proper word) a poem which is

[033-151]generally considered his finest,--an imitation of Moore’s “Loves of the

[033-152]Angels,” entitled “Vitalis,” which Monsieur and Madame Dumay, Madame

[033-153]Latournelle, and Gobenheim welcomed with a few yawns.

[033-154]“If you are a good whist-player, monsieur,” said Gobenheim, flourishing

[033-155]five cards held like a fan, “I must say I have never met a man as

[033-156]accomplished as you.”

[033-157]The remark raised a laugh, for it was the translation of everybody’s

[033-158]thought.

[033-159]“I play it sufficiently well to live in the provinces for the rest of my

[033-160]days,” replied Canalis. “That, I think, is enough, and more than enough

[033-161]literature and conversation for whist-players,” he added, throwing the

[033-162]volume impatiently on a table.

[033-163]This little incident serves to show what dangers environ a drawing-room

[033-164]hero when he steps, like Canalis, out of his sphere; he is like the

[033-165]favorite actor of a second-rate audience, whose talent is lost when he

[033-166]leaves his own boards and steps upon those of an upper-class theatre.

[033-167]CHAPTER XXI. MODESTE PLAYS HER PART

[033-168]The game opened with the baron and the duke, Gobenheim and Latournelle

[033-169]as partners. Modeste took a seat near the poet, to [[ernest-de-la-briere|Ernest]]’s deep

[033-170]disappointment; he watched the face of the wayward girl, and marked the

[033-171]progress of the fascination which Canalis exerted over her. La

[033-172]Briere had not the gift of seduction which Melchior possessed. Nature

[033-173]frequently denies it to true hearts, who are, as a rule, timid. This

[033-174]gift demands fearlessness, an alacrity of ways and means that might be

[033-175]called the trapeze of the mind; a little mimicry goes with it; in fact

[033-176]there is always, morally speaking, something of the comedian in a poet.

[033-177]There is a vast difference between expressing sentiments we do not feel,

[033-178]though we may imagine all their variations, and feigning to feel them

[033-179]when bidding for success on the theatre of private life. And yet, though

[033-180]the necessary hypocrisy of a man of the world may have gangrened a poet,

[033-181]he ends by carrying the faculties of his talent into the expression of

[033-182]any required sentiment, just as a great man doomed to solitude ends by

[033-183]infusing his heart into his mind.

[033-184]“He is after the millions,” thought La Briere, sadly; “and he can play

[033-185]passion so well that Modeste will believe him.”
