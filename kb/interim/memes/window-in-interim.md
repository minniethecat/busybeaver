# Window in *Interim*

If doors in *Interim* mark thresholds crossed, windows mark thresholds gazed through. They are the membrane between the domestic interior and the urban exterior — admitting light and weather, framing views, registering the difference between inside warmth and outside cold. Richardson's attention to windows is part of her larger attention to the sensory boundaries that shape consciousness.

## In the Novel

Windows appear throughout the novel in several characteristic modes: the curtain-screened windows of the dining room [001-004], the open windows that let in street sounds [001-012], the French windows of the drawing-room [002-008], the "low barred window" of a basement [005-001], and the "grey window" through which sunlight pours [008-002]. Each window is a different relationship between inside and outside.

> A row of narrow curtain-screened windows behind Grace, the clean gleam of the glass. [001-004]

The windows are "curtain-screened" — mediated, softened — but the "clean gleam of the glass" is still visible. Richardson registers the window both as a barrier (the curtain) and as a surface (the gleaming glass), the two qualities coexisting.

## Windows and Their Orientations

| Window | Location | What It Admits |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| Dining-room windows | Banbury Park, curtain-screened | Filtered daylight, the "clean gleam" of glass |
| Open windows | Upper rooms, "open at the top" [001-012] | Air, street sounds, the outside world |
| French windows | Drawing-room, floor-length [002-008] | Garden light, the outside made elegant |
| Barred window | Basement, "low barred window" [005-001] | Restricted light, the below-ground perspective |
| Grey window | Bedroom, "through her grey window" [008-002] | Morning sunlight, the promise of the day |

> She strolled to the window, finding renewal in the familiar morning light. [008-021]

The window as source of renewal. The "familiar morning light" — light that is known, that repeats — offers a small restoration. To stroll to the window is to seek out the outside from within, to refresh the interior self with exterior light.

## Key Passages

> If she stayed on in the cold window space she would have the beginning of a headache. [002-004]

The "cold window space" — the zone near the glass where the outside temperature penetrates. Richardson is attentive to the microclimates of rooms: the warmth near the fire, the cold near the window. The window creates a boundary not just of light but of temperature, a gradient of comfort.

> Pressed against the windows, filtered out into the quiet street. [002-009]

The pressure here is from inside out — sound, presence, life pressing against the glass and "filtering" into the street. The window is permeable in both directions: the street enters the house as sound, and the house enters the street as light.

> The window was blocked with flowers in jars. [006-014]

A window filled not with a view but with flowers — the outside brought inside and arranged on the sill. The "blocked" window is a window that has been domesticated, turned from a transparency into a display. The flowers mediate between the garden they came from and the room they now occupy.

> Through her grey window she could see the sunlight pouring down on the pavement. [008-002]

The "grey window" — grey with city grime, with the residue of weather — filters the sunlight, but does not stop it. "Pouring down" suggests abundance, generosity. The window is grey but the light is golden; the dingy medium does not diminish the radiance it transmits.

## Significance

Windows in *Interim* embody the novel's central spatial dynamic: the tension between enclosure and exposure, between the shelter of the interior and the presence of the world outside. Richardson's protagonist lives largely indoors — in boarding-houses, drawing-rooms, bedrooms — and the window is her primary connection to the outside. To look through a window is to be inside and outside simultaneously, to inhabit the threshold.

This is the phenomenological condition the novel explores throughout: consciousness as a kind of window, a permeable membrane between inner and outer worlds. Richardson does not state this analogy; she builds it, window by window, across the texture of the narrative.
