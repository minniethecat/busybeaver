# Dream in *Interim*

In a novel built around the minute registration of waking consciousness, dream and dreaming mark the boundary where conscious control gives way. Richardson uses dream not as surreal escape but as another register of experience — a space where what waking life suppresses can surface, and where the boundary between inner and outer worlds becomes permeable.

## In the Novel

Dream in *Interim* operates on a spectrum from literal sleep to waking reverie. Miriam drifts between states: the "dream" of a train journey back toward London [004-011], the "dreamy" absorption in work [008-002], the "dreamily" smiling presence of others [008-034]. Dream is less a discrete state than a quality of attention — or its relaxation.

> She felt she must have dreamed the news. Eve, not a governess any more — the fact seemed to belong to another order of reality. [004-004]

The news of Eve's changed circumstances feels "dreamed" because it does not fit the known pattern of the world. Dream here is not escapism but a category for experiences that resist assimilation to waking logic.

## The Dream Spectrum

| State | Example | Function |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Night dream | "a scene she recognised; in a dream" [008-248] | Subconscious processing; the return of the repressed |
| Daydream | "dreaming and brooding was a sort of affection" [004-010] | Emotional sustenance; the inner life as resource |
| Reverie | "smiling dreamily" [008-034] | Social withdrawal; presence without participation |
| Aspiration | "the shipwreck of all your dreams" [008-020] | Hope and its loss; dream as life-plan |

The novel moves fluidly between these registers. A scene "recognised; in a dream" [008-248] blurs the line between memory and dream: what is recognised may have been dreamed before, or may only feel that way.

## Key Passages

> She was called back towards London in a dream. Her compartment was empty. [004-011]

The journey "in a dream" is not unreal — it is a real train journey experienced with the heightened, slightly unreal quality of a significant transition. The empty compartment intensifies the dream-feeling: isolation within movement, consciousness suspended between two places.

> Dreaming and brooding was a sort of affection in your life. [004-010]

This remarkable sentence equates "dreaming and brooding" with affection — not as its substitute but as its equivalent. The inner life, Richardson suggests, is a form of relationship: to oneself, to one's past, to the people held in memory.

> She felt the work was unreal, as easy as a dream. [008-002]

Work experienced "as easy as a dream" suggests not difficulty but its opposite — a state of flow where conscious effort dissolves. The comparison to dream implies a loss of the usual friction between self and task.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of dream is characteristically modernist in its refusal to draw a hard line between sleeping and waking, inner and outer, real and unreal. Consciousness in *Interim* does not switch off during dream; it shifts register. The dream-state — whether literal sleep or waking reverie — is not the opposite of the novel's primary subject (conscious experience) but its extension.

This is of a piece with Richardson's larger project: to render the full spectrum of consciousness, from sharp social attention to the drifting edge of sleep. In a novel that already privileges [[silence-in-interim|silence]] over speech and interior perception over external event, dream is the logical horizon — the point where consciousness turns entirely inward.
