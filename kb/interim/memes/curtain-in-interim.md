# Curtain in _Interim_

**Curtains** in _Interim_ mark the boundary between interior and exterior, between one room and another, between the seen and the hidden. They are among the most frequently mentioned features of domestic space, and Richardson invests them with an almost architectural significance — curtains shape light, frame views, and govern access.

## Curtains and Light

The primary function of curtains in the novel is the modulation of light. The "thick close-drawn curtains that made the perfect velvet darkness" [PN=001-012] transform the bedroom into a sealed chamber, the quality of darkness itself described as luxurious — "velvet." Morning light "filtering in through the curtains hanging motionless" [PN=008-014] registers the curtain as a membrane, partially permeable, softening the outside world before it enters the room.

The "little sharp bow of narrow curtain-screened windows" [PN=001-004] makes the window itself seem tentative, the curtain narrowing its aperture. A "small uncurtained lattice" [PN=001-015] admits unmediated light, the absence of a curtain creating a different quality of brightness — harsher, more direct.

> …the thick close-drawn curtains that made the perfect velvet darkness of the room… [PN=001-012]

## Curtains and Social Thresholds

Curtains also function as social barriers. The "mysterious Bailey curtains partly screening the passage" [PN=008-023] mark a zone of restricted access — what lies beyond the curtain is private, the domain of the family. A man who "disappeared between curtains at the end of the room" [PN=005-002] passes from public performance into backstage space, the curtain swallowing him from view.

Mr. Bowdoin "coming back through the curtains and turning up an end of the table" [PN=005-003] reverses the movement — emerging from behind curtains into the social space of the room. The curtain is a membrane crossed in both directions.

## Curtains as Domestic Texture

The "little Madras muslin curtains" [PN=002-007] appear twice in the novel, their specific fabric named — muslin, Madras — as part of Richardson's exacting attention to the material qualities of domestic objects. These are not generic curtains but particular ones, with a weight and transparency that belong to a specific textile. The "dingy cream lace curtains almost meeting across" the window [PN=002-008] register age and use, the dinginess of the lace telling a history of accumulated days.

Someone playing a piano "even behind a curtain" [PN=008-145] imagines music coming from hidden space, the curtain creating a separation between performer and audience that is also a kind of protection.
