# Room in *Interim*

The boarding-house at Banbury Park is a collection of rooms, and each room in *Interim* has its own sensory identity. The dining room with its gaslight and long table; the drawing-room with its fire and armchairs; the bedrooms with their privacy and cold; the kitchen with its labour — these spaces are not interchangeable backdrops but distinct environments that shape the consciousness within them.

## In the Novel

Richardson's method is spatial before it is temporal. The novel's action is organised not by plot but by movement between rooms — the passage from dining room to drawing-room, from drawing-room to bedroom, from inside to street and back. Each room-change registers as a shift in the quality of consciousness, a new configuration of light, sound, and social demand.

> The bright light of the gas and the soft light of the reading-lamp shone upon perfect order. [002-002]

This is a room in its ideal state: ordered, lit, waiting. The two qualities of light — "bright" and "soft" — coexist within the same space, creating zones of different affective temperature. The room is not one thing but several, depending on where one sits and what light one sits under.

## Rooms and Their Atmospheres

| Room | Light | Social Function | Affective Quality |
|------|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| Dining room | Gaslight, bright | Meals, public gathering | Exposure, performance |
| Drawing-room | Firelight, lamps | Evening conversation, music | Intimacy, reverie |
| Bedroom | Twilight, dark | Solitude, sleep | Privacy, vulnerability |
| Kitchen | Unseen, implied | Labour, preparation | The novel's hidden space |
| Hall/Passage | Transitional light | Movement between rooms | Suspension, interim |

> The dining-room door was ajar showing a faint light. [004-013]

The door ajar is the room partially disclosed — its "faint light" a muted signal of what lies within. Richardson rarely gives us a room in full description; she gives us glimpses, thresholds, the quality of light at the edge of the door.

## Key Passages

> The room was lit only by the firelight. From its wide clear core streamed a steady radiance. [004-007]

The room reduced to one light source — fire only, no gas — becomes a different kind of space. The "wide clear core" of the fire replaces the diffused brightness of gas with a centred, directional glow. This is a room for solitude or intimate talk, a room stripped of public-facing illumination.

> The room was full of light, the sun pouring in across the table. [005-003]

Morning light transforms the room: the sun "pouring in" replaces the previous night's firelight and gaslight. Rooms in *Interim* change character with the time of day; the same space is different at breakfast than at dinner, different in winter twilight than in morning sun.

> She could feel the cold window space pressing against the warmth of the firelit room. [paraphrase of 002-004]

The room is not thermally uniform. The "cold window space" and the "warmth of the firelit room" create a gradient — warmth at the hearth, cold at the glass. Richardson's attention to temperature is as precise as her attention to light, and together they define the room as a sensory field.

> Miriam going with her to the door — No, you're to rest. [001-014]

The room as the place where one rests or does not rest. The offer to walk someone to the door and the refusal — "you're to rest" — locate the body in the room as a place of prescribed stillness. The room is not just a space but a command: stay here, rest.

## Significance

Rooms in *Interim* are not settings but conditions of consciousness. Each room — with its particular light, its temperature, its social expectation — shapes what can be thought and felt within it. To move from the dining room to the drawing-room is not just to change location but to change the kind of attention one can pay.

This spatial sensitivity is central to Richardson's modernist project. She does not describe rooms as objective spaces; she renders them as they are experienced — through the body, through the senses, through the consciousness that inhabits them. The room is not outside the self but around it, pressing in with its light and warmth and expectation.
