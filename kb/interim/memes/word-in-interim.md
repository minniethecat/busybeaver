# Word in _Interim_

Words in _Interim_ are never transparent vehicles of meaning. They are physical events — sounds shaped by lips and tongue, substances with texture and weight. Richardson treats language as a sensory medium, and Miriam Henderson's relationship to words is at once practical (she must speak them to others) and phenomenological (she feels them in her body).

## The Physical Word

In a striking passage on pronunciation, Miriam attends to the material production of speech: "Hold the lips tight before the b and explode the word up into the nose partly closing the back of the throat and mouth" [003-011]. The word is not an idea but an action — a controlled explosion, a coordination of lips and breath and nasal cavity. This attention to the mechanics of utterance is characteristic of Richardson's commitment to rendering experience from the inside.

The word "conjoint" arrives in a moment of desperate social performance. When Dr. von Heber is speaking, Miriam searches for a response: "he had not been going to stop speaking but Miriam found an immense welcoming space for the word she summoned in vain desperately from far away Wimpole Street. The conjoint she declared at last eagerly, almost before the word reached her consciousness" [004-013]. The word arrives from somewhere outside her — "from far away Wimpole Street" — and she seizes it "almost before the word reached her consciousness." Language here precedes thought; the word is a bodily reflex before it is a meaning.

## Words as Social Currency

Words circulate in the boarding-house world as a form of social currency. "What had come now was the fulfilment of the apprehension she had had when Mrs. Bailey had spoken the word boarders" [002-006]. A single word — "boarders" — carries an entire social structure within it: the sharing of space, the performance of respectability, the negotiation of intimacy and distance. Words in _Interim_ are never neutral; they are loaded with class, gender, and the history of their use.

The word is also a marker of exclusion. "Foreign languages had a beauty that could not be found in English, and the world would be ruled by the kind of English people who could never get the sound of a foreign word" [003-011]. The inability to produce foreign sounds is an "appalling obliviousness" — a failure of perception that has political consequences. The word, properly spoken, opens worlds; the word, mangled by English mouths, closes them.

## Words and Reading

When Eve Henderson's letter arrives announcing her move to London, Miriam is "too excited to read word by word" [004-004]. She scans the pages, extracting meaning in bursts rather than through the disciplined sequence of words. The phrase "word by word" implies a normative mode of reading — patient, sequential, attentive — that excitement overrides. Reading, like speaking, is a bodily practice subject to the pressures of emotion.
