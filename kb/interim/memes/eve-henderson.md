# Eve Henderson

Eve Henderson is a character in [[Dorothy Richardson]]'s novel *Interim*, part of the *[[Pilgrimage]]* sequence. She is [[Miriam Henderson]]'s sister, distinguished by her independence and her position as the proprietor of a [[west-end-shop|West End shop]] — a rare example of autonomous womanhood in the novel's social landscape. Eve's presence weaves through the narrative as both a real figure and a projected image of what Miriam might become. Her relationships with [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]] and the [[wimpole-street|Wimpole Street]] household situate her within the novel's key interpersonal networks.

## Character Overview

Eve represents a model of financial and personal independence unusual for women of her era. Unlike Miriam, who struggles through governessing and boarding-house privations, Eve has established herself in business — her "west-end blaze of a shop" marking her as a public, economically self-sufficient figure. Through Miriam's consciousness, Eve appears as a point of comparison and aspiration, her letters and arrivals punctuating the narrative as moments of reckoning with alternative life paths.

## In the Novel

Eve exists largely offstage in *Interim*, her presence mediated through letters, reported conversations, and brief appearances. She does not live at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] or [[tansley-street|Tansley Street]] but moves independently in London.

> A letter; from Eve; witnessing her discomfort; she was coming up; coming home to England and things were not going well. [004-003]

Her planned visit to the Henderson household becomes a focal point of anticipation in Chapter IV, with Miriam preparing her room and imagining the encounter.

> Then Eve would not come this winter. There would only be Eve and the accomplishment of having her — herself enduring the discomfort of having not come. [004-007]

## Key Relationships

### Miriam Henderson

Eve serves as Miriam's confidante and mirror. Miriam tells Eve things she cannot tell anyone else, and Eve's life choices — particularly her business independence — represent roads not taken.

> "I was right when I told Eve I had sold my soul to the devil," — a confession that reveals the depth of their intimacy as sisters. [008-020]

### Dr. von Heber

Eve is romantically linked to [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]], the German dentist who fascinates the [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] household. Their relationship is the subject of gossip and speculation, particularly as Dr. von Heber's departure for Canada unfolds.

> This woman had opened Dr. von Heber's letter; knew he was coming to see Eve in her shop. [010-030]

### The Bailey Household

[[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] witnesses Eve's arrival at the station, a moment that brings Eve into the orbit of the wider boarding-house community.

## The West End Shop

Eve's shop is the concrete symbol of her difference. Located in London's commercial West End, it is described with ambivalence — both a "blaze" of success and a site where Eve stands "badly in a droopy black dress," suggesting the cost of her independence.

> There was Eve; standing badly in a droopy black dress, not the Eve of the letters from abroad. [006-015]

The shop marks Eve as a public woman in an era when gentility demanded domestic enclosure. Her presence there — managing, visible, commercial — is her triumph and her vulnerability.

## Significance

Eve Henderson serves as a foil to Miriam: where Miriam is introspective and uncertain, Eve is outwardly directed and economically active. She embodies the question that haunts the novel — what kind of life can a woman of intelligence and sensitivity construct for herself? Eve's answer (commerce, independence, romantic entanglement with a cosmopolitan foreigner) is different from Miriam's, and the novel holds both possibilities in tension.

> "The divine curiosity of Eve … that's the answer to the Moderns." [008-126]

This line, spoken by a character in the novel, links Eve's name to the biblical Eve — the first woman, the original seeker of knowledge. The allusion reinforces Eve Henderson's symbolic role as a pathfinder, a woman who dared to step outside the domestic sphere and into the world.
