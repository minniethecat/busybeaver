# Women in _Interim_

**Women** in _Interim_ are the fabric of the novel's social world — observed in drawing rooms, on streets, at concerts, and in domestic labour. Richardson's narrator is an acute anthropologist of feminine behaviour, registering the performances, constraints, and solidarities that shape women's lives in late-Victorian and Edwardian London.

## Women Among Themselves

### Women Together

The novel's most concentrated observation of women occurs when men leave the room. "Presently there were only the women left in the room. Miriam felt" (006-019) marks the threshold between mixed company and female space. The shift in atmosphere is palpable — a different kind of talk, a different quality of attention. Richardson is alert to what happens "on the faces of some of the women caught as she glanced" (004-009) — the fleeting expressions that escape performance.

> …Presently there were only the women left in the room. Miriam felt…
> (006-019)

This spatial segregation of the sexes — men in one part of the room, women in another — is not incidental but structural. Richardson makes visible what the characters themselves take for granted, the unremarked architecture of gender that organises every social gathering.

### Women Observed and Observing

Throughout, Miriam is both observer and observed. She watches "women in white satin evening dresses" (006-014) with the novelist's eye for costume and class, and she herself is subject to the same gaze. The novel's feminism is not programmatic but perceptual — it proceeds by noticing, by refusing to take the arrangement of the sexes as natural or invisible.

The visual regime of femininity is rendered precisely: "thin hard fingers of women chattering and tweaking" (008-148) — the physicality of women's social performance rendered in a single sentence. Fingers, voice, gesture, dress — all are enlisted in the work of presentation, and Richardson registers each element with the same descriptive precision she brings to rooms and music.

## Women and Men

The relations between women and men are scrutinised with a critical eye. Miriam notes "the men were apart from the women; to join the men would be easy" (005-004), the segregated social geometry of the gathering made visible. She registers "a demeaning kind of men in relation to women, thinking and believing" (006-018) — a critique of paternalism — and observes that "most of the women were there with men, somehow" (006-028), the "somehow" carrying the weight of unexamined social arrangement.

The novel also tracks women's physical positioning relative to men: "the tall man and the two women were marching along ahead" (005-005) — procession as hierarchy, the women following in the wake of male presence. Against this, Richardson sets moments of female self-assertion: "You rate men lower than women in power to endure pain" (008-098), a direct challenge to the era's default assumptions about gendered suffering.

## Women's Labour and Critique

### Women's Work and Talk

Gossip, in _Interim_, is gendered labour. "Men are worse gossips than old women. They can't keep their hands" (007-008) reverses the conventional charge, while "men talk about women gossiping. My word" (008-126) records the double standard with ironic precision. Beyond talk, the novel notes women's professional worlds — "the Canadian women's way" (007-005) of management, "the world of women's doctors" (007-009) — spheres of competence that exist alongside and sometimes in tension with domestic expectation.

The gendered economy of performance is particularly acute: "women succeeding only by jesting" (007-006) captures the pressure to entertain as a condition of social survival. Women must be amusing, must smooth the edges of conversation, must manage the emotional temperature of rooms — labour that is simultaneously demanded and devalued.

### The Critique Within

Richardson's narrator does not exempt women from her critical scrutiny. Life can be "imprisoned by those women, consciously goody conscienti" (006-003) — the domestic tyrant disguised as virtue. She registers a world where "women succeeding only by jesting" (007-006), the pressure to entertain as a condition of social survival. The observation that "thin hard fingers of women chattering and tweaking" (008-148) captures the physicality of women's social performance — fingers, voice, gesture all enlisted in the work of presentation. And in a blunt exchange: "'You rate men lower than women in power to endure pain'" (008-098), a challenge that reverses the era's default hierarchy of suffering and toughness.

## Women's Moral Worlds

### Women and Moral Discourse

The novel exposes the gendered distribution of moral language. One character's resentment extends to "the sufferings of other women and to make a fuss over men" (008-103), diagnosing a pattern of sentimental attention that prioritises male comfort while policing female complaint. The narrator's own awareness spans transatlantic comparisons — "those women. But that must be by an Ameri" (008-215) — and Continental observations: "discreet women are sly. To get on in the wor" (009-080), a cynical aphorism that doubles as sociological observation.

Richardson also captures the search for feminine ideals that escape the existing repertoire: "looking in vain in the world for women who were like the women described" (006-025) — the gap between cultural image and lived reality, between the women that books describe and the women one actually encounters. This search for adequate models is one of the novel's quiet preoccupations, a restlessness that cannot be satisfied by the available types. The novel also briefly gestures toward the collective sound of women's public presence: "amidst the shouting of women and the interwoven dark thick" (006-026) — the street as a space where women's voices, usually contained within domestic interiors, become momentarily audible in public. Throughout, _Interim_ presents women's moral life not as a simple story of oppression and resistance but as a dense web of strategy, compromise, and intermittent lucidity.

### Women Across Borders

Richardson extends her observation beyond English drawing rooms. The "Canadian women's way" (007-005) of management suggests alternative models of feminine competence, while "the world of women's doctors" (007-009) maps a transnational professional sphere. The "regiment of women" (009-081) — "proper men can't bring the w" — suggests women as a coherent social body with its own rules, its own forms of discipline and solidarity. This military metaphor for feminine collectivity is not accidental; it recurs in the novel's lexicon, pointing to the organised, disciplined nature of women's social existence.
