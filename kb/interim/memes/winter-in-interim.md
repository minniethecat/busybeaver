# Winter in _Interim_

**Winter** in _Interim_ is the season of darkness made brilliant. Richardson's London winter is not the bleak deprivation of a rural cold season but an urban winter lit by gas, fire, and the peculiar luminosity of the city after dark. The winter setting concentrates the novel's attention on interiors, lamplight, and the warmth that gathers indoors against the cold outside.

## Winter Blackness and Brilliance

The novel's most compressed winter image is a paradox. "The gas flared out making a winter brilliance" [PN=008-020] — winter's darkness is not an absence but a backdrop against which artificial light declares itself more vividly. Earlier, Miriam registers that a lamp "shone brilliantly in this darkest winter blackness" [PN=001-012], the superlatives ("brilliantly," "darkest") reinforcing each other. Winter heightens the drama of light.

> …shone brilliantly in this darkest winter blackness. It was more than winter… [PN=001-012]

## The Firelit Winter Interior

"In the familiar firelit winter darkness was a faint dry warmth" [PN=004-007] — the winter interior is defined by firelight and its specific qualities: "familiar," "faint," "dry." The fire does not banish winter darkness but transforms it into something inhabited. Richardson's winter is not a season to be overcome but an atmosphere to be lived inside, the fire a companion rather than a conquest.

## Winter as Endurance

Winter also marks the limits of bodily comfort. "But I should freeze in the winter; because I shouldn't be able to stand the long evenings" [PN=008-068] — a visiting speaker confesses his fear of the English winter, the "long evenings" as threatening as the cold itself. An English house in winter "'d take some beating" [PN=008-056], the season becoming a test of architecture and national character alike. "Your winters don't call for them. It's the damp" [PN=008-071] distinguishes the English winter's particular hostility — not the dry cold of continental Europe but a penetrating damp that seeps through walls.

## Winter and Memory

Winter and summer are repeatedly paired as a temporal frame. "Cheerfulness and seriousness and winter and summer. They would not know" [PN=006-011] presents the seasons as complementary registers of experience, the cycle of winter and summer standing for the full span of a life. Yet "it is a mistake to imagine that the winter is gone; but it is gone in you" [PN=006-023] suggests winter's internal persistence — the season, once experienced, becomes part of the self.
