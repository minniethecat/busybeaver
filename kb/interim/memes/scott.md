# Miss Scott

Miss Scott is a boarder at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park establishment in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. She is marked by her Scotch nationality — a detail that Richardson returns to several times — and by a forthrightness of opinion that distinguishes her from the more guarded personalities of the boarding-house circle. She appears most frequently in the dinner-table scenes and in the gossip that circulates among the household.

## Character Overview

Miss Scott is introduced early as a fixture of the Bailey household, one of the women whose presence and personality contribute to the dense social texture of the novel. Her defining trait is her nationality: she is repeatedly identified as "Scotch," a marker of difference within the English boarding-house that carries implications of temperament, speech, and social perspective.

> Miss Scott was Scotch. [004-008]

The simplicity of the statement — a standalone sentence, unadorned — is characteristic of Richardson's method. Nationality is not elaborated or psychologised; it is stated as a fact, a piece of social data that the reader (like Miriam) must interpret.

## In the Novel

### At the Dinner Table

Miss Scott appears regularly at the social gatherings that structure the novel's action. Her position at the table is precisely mapped, part of the careful choreography of the boarding-house dinner.

> Then came Miss Scott on Sissie's left; then Sissie… [006-004]

The seating arrangement places her in proximity to [[sissie|Sissie]], and the two are frequently mentioned together in the novel's social scenes. This pairing — the Scotch woman and the young pupil of French — suggests the heterogeneous character of the Bailey household, where nationalities and ages and social positions mingle around a common table.

### Opinions and Conversation

Miss Scott is not a silent presence. She has opinions, and she expresses them. Her conversation is noted — sometimes approvingly, sometimes as material for mockery.

> "That's what Miss Scott says." [008-181]

The reported speech — someone quoting Miss Scott, perhaps with a touch of irony — suggests that her pronouncements are sufficiently distinctive to be remembered and repeated. She is someone whose words carry weight, or at least entertainment value, within the household's oral culture.

> …both snatching at scraps of Scott and Sissie and Gunner chaff… [009-098]

In this passage, "Scott" has become a subject of conversation — "scraps of Scott" — her name and opinions reduced to material for dinner-table banter. The verb "snatching" suggests eagerness, even voracity: the household feeds on talk of its own members.

## Key Relationships

### Sissie

Miss Scott's most consistent pairing is with [[sissie|Sissie]], the two women seated together at dinner, their names linked in the gossip that circulates through the household. Their dynamic — the older Scotch woman and the younger, more irreverent Sissie — embodies the cross-generational and cross-cultural currents of the boarding-house.

### Mr. Gunner

Miss Scott is also associated with [[gunner|Mr. Gunner]], the two sometimes mentioned in the same breath in the novel's more satirical passages.

> …the spectacle of Mr. Gunner, or Miss Scott, following her to Ruscino's… [009-036]

The word "spectacle" casts both Gunner and Scott in a slightly comedic light — figures whose behaviour is observed and remarked upon by the other boarders with a mixture of amusement and detachment.

### Miriam Henderson

For [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]], Miss Scott is an object of observation and occasional inquiry.

> "D'you like London, Miss Scott?" said Miriam uncontrollably… [004-003]

The adverb "uncontrollably" is telling — Miriam's question bursts out of her, a social impulse she cannot suppress. Miss Scott, as a Scotch woman in London, is a natural recipient for such a question; her answer, whatever it is, will reflect the experience of displacement that Miriam herself knows intimately.

## Significance

Miss Scott exemplifies Richardson's method of building character through accumulation of small facts — nationality, a seating position, an attributed opinion, a name dropped into gossip. She is not the subject of any dramatic arc. She does not confess, or transform, or depart. She is simply present — Scotch, opinionated, quotable — contributing her particular note to the boarding-house's polyphony.

In a novel structured around Miriam Henderson's consciousness, characters like Miss Scott function as fixtures in the perceptual field: consistent, recognisable, part of the furniture of daily life. They matter not because they change but because they endure — the familiar faces around the dinner table, night after night, whose presence is the ground against which the novel's more disruptive events take shape.
