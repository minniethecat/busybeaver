# Love in *Interim*

Love in *Interim* is a subject Miriam Henderson approaches indirectly, even evasively. The word appears, but its appearances are hedged — qualified, questioned, or deflected into other terms. Richardson does not give us a love story; she gives us a consciousness considering the possibility of love and finding it difficult to locate.

## In the Novel

The novel's few direct invocations of love are characteristically ambivalent. "There was no love in her nature" [004-011] — the statement is too absolute to be trusted, the kind of thing one thinks about oneself in a moment of loneliness. Elsewhere, love appears as "tyrannous love" [001-017] — the possessive affection of family that demands and constrains.

> In my secret self I should love a prison. Walls. What are walls. [003-001]

This is love as metaphor: the "prison" that would be loved for its walls, its enclosure, its protection from the open. The sentence fragment "Walls." — isolated, emphatic — suggests a longing for containment that is also a longing for love, or for something like it.

## Love's Ambiguities

| Aspect | Manifestation | Emotional Register |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| Familial love | "Tyrannous love" of mothers and sisters [001-017] | Obligation, demand, suffocation |
| Romantic possibility | Conversations with men, glances, evenings out | Uncertainty, deferral |
| Love's absence | "There was no love in her nature" [004-011] | Self-doubt, loneliness |
| Love as idea | "Falling in love … just thinking" [007-004] | Intellectual consideration, detachment |
| Practical love | "Love was actual and practical" [004-010] | A rebuttal to romance, an ethic of action |

> Love was actual and practical, moving about among your fellows. [004-010]

This is the novel's most considered statement about love, and it is a quiet manifesto against romance. Love is "actual and practical" — not a feeling but a practice, a way of moving among others. The phrase is a rebuke to the sentimental tradition and an assertion of Richardson's ethical realism.

## Key Passages

> She glanced at him with a touch of weakness. What a lovely mild evening. [006-023]

The deflection is the point. A "touch of weakness" — a moment of possible feeling — is immediately covered by the weather: "What a lovely mild evening." The word "lovely" migrates from the person to the evening, from the dangerous territory of attraction to the safe ground of atmospheric description. This is how Richardson writes love: as a pressure that is felt and redirected.

> There was no love in her nature. If there were she would know it. [004-011]

The logic is circular — "if there were she would know it" — and that is its truth. Miriam's uncertainty about her own capacity for love is the most honest thing that can be said about it. She does not know whether she can love, and her not-knowing is the form her honesty takes.

> Their beloved Bunnikin, as smartly dressed as ever. [007-010]

"Beloved Bunnikin" — the family nickname, the diminutive, the affection embedded in the name. This is love as it actually appears in the novel: not in declarations but in epithets, in the small linguistic gestures of attachment that circulate through domestic life.

> He would love laughter. Freed from worries. [006-019]

"He would love laughter" — the sentence imagines another person's capacity for joy. It is not "he loves" but "he would love," the conditional marking the gap between what is and what might be. Love in the optative mood: a hope, not a fact.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of love is of a piece with her modernist refusal of narrative convention. The marriage plot — the engine of so much nineteenth-century fiction — is simply absent. In its place is a consciousness that considers love, doubts it, deflects it, and occasionally, in the small gestures of domestic affection, enacts it without naming it. This is love stripped of romance and restored to the texture of ordinary life: "actual and practical, moving about among your fellows."
