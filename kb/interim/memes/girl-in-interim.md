# Girl in *Interim*

The figure of the girl runs through Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* as both a social category and a site of memory. "Girl" is a word applied to daughters, to schoolfellows, to Miriam herself by older women — and rejected by Miriam when it threatens to define her. The novel registers girlhood as a condition one inhabits, remembers, and eventually outgrows, tracing the boundary between the girl one was and the woman one is becoming.

## The Bailey Girls

The most sustained representation of girls in the novel is the Bailey daughters, observed by Miriam at the boarding-house table in [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]]. They are present as physical facts — bodies, hair, eyes — rendered with Richardson's characteristic attention to surface detail.

> Miriam's glance elastic in the warmth coming from the room swept from the flood of yellow hair on the back of the youngest Bailey girl sitting close at her mother's left hand, across to the far side of the table. [002-006]

The Bailey girls are seen before they are heard. The youngest is defined by her yellow hair and physical proximity to her mother; the eldest by her "pale grey-blue eyes" and the "unseeing look" she directs towards the bread and butter [002-006]. These girls are caught in the act of being daughters — sitting, reaching, watching — and Miriam registers them as elements in a domestic composition.

At a dinner party later in the novel, the Bailey girls reappear with "shiny flushed cheeks and bright eyes," watching a male guest [003-002]. Here they are spectators of adult social performance, their flushed cheeks marking the excitement of proximity to adult masculine presence. Their role in the novel is largely passive: they are present, they observe, they are observed. Richardson gives them no interiority — only surfaces, the way a lodger might see the landlady's daughters.

## "Giddy Girl": Girlishness as Social Performance

Early in the novel, Miriam's behaviour earns a particular epithet. After an evening of animated talk about childhood toys, Miriam springs up and dances about the room.

> Giddy girl, chuckled Mrs. Philps affectionately. [001-011]

The phrase "giddy girl" is a social tag — affectionate, dismissive, mildly corrective. It names Miriam's behaviour as girlish excess, the kind of spiritedness that older women both enjoy and gently police. Mrs. Philps's chuckle places the remark in the register of fond indulgence, but the word "giddy" carries its own judgment: lightness, lack of seriousness, a failure of adult composure. Miriam's dancing is a release after the strain of social performance; Mrs. Philps recontains it with a label.

Girlishness appears elsewhere as a quality attributed rather than claimed. Mrs. Bailey, in a moment of disclosure, "flushed a girlish pink" [009-039] — the blush of a grown woman reverting to a younger self under emotional pressure. A "bulky woman" at a gathering speaks "in a high pure girlish voice" [006-005], the voice at odds with the body, girlishness as a sonic performance that does not align with physical presence. In each case, the girlish is a layer — a register one can slip into or have attributed — rather than a fixed identity.

## Schoolgirls: Past and Present Identity

The word "schoolgirl" carries particular weight in the novel. It names a past that is not fully past, a self that persists beneath the present one.

> Closing in upon her from the schoolgirl pieces still echoing in the room came sudden abrupt little scenes from all the levels of her life, deep-rooted moments still alive within her challenging and promising as when she had left them, driven relentlessly on. [002-009]

The "schoolgirl pieces" are piano pieces — music learned in girlhood, still capable of summoning the girl who learned them. The passage registers girlhood as a layer of consciousness that can be reactivated by sensory triggers: a piece of music, a room's atmosphere. The "deep-rooted moments still alive within her" suggest that the schoolgirl self is not discarded but stored, waiting to be called up.

Elsewhere the schoolgirl appears as a figure of judgment. Walking the streets of north London on Christmas Day, Miriam is flooded with "innumerable impressions gathered on walks with the schoolgirls or in lonely wanderings" [001-015] — the schoolgirl walks are memories of a shared social world, a collective girlhood defined by companionship and shared perception. These memories crowd in on the present, "oppressing and darkening the crisp light air," until Miriam fights them off. The schoolgirl past is not only nostalgic; it can be claustrophobic, a set of perceptions one must escape.

At the dinner table, a guest is described with the word used as pure physiognomic descriptor: "an enormous woman with a large schoolgirl face, fair straight and schoolgirl hair" [009-098]. Here "schoolgirl" is not memory but type — a face that has not aged into its years, hair worn in a style associated with youth. The woman's face and hair are "schoolgirl," but her arms are "brawny" and her eating "angry." The dissonance between the schoolgirl surface and the adult body beneath it makes the description unsettling.

## Miriam Between Girlhood and Womanhood

Miriam occupies an ambiguous position in the novel's taxonomy of age and gender. She is no longer a girl — she works, she lives independently in London, she moves through adult social worlds. But she is also not yet fully claimed by womanhood. This betweenness is dramatised in an exchange about the Brooms' former pupils.

> —They always send you their love and say you are to go and see them—Oh yes, I must go; some time—They are wonderfully fond of their girls. … It's one of the greatest pleasures of their lives keeping up with the old girls—Fatigue was returning upon Miriam; her face flushed and her hands were large and cold. [001-013]

The phrase "old girls" — the Brooms' term for their former pupils — lands on Miriam with uncomfortable weight. The category is meant to include her, but she resists it.

> A mild yes would bring the sitting to an end.—But you see I'm not an old girl she said impatiently. [001-013]

Miriam's impatience is significant. She refuses the label "old girl" — refuses to be folded back into the collective of former schoolfellows, into a category that would define her by her school past. The refusal is a bid for a different kind of womanhood, one not organised around the institutions of girlhood.

Elsewhere, Miriam observes that she is unlike "the kind of girl who after good schooling could spend a year in France and come back unable to speak French" [002-007]. The comment is dismissive — the girl who fails to learn from her opportunities — and marks Miriam's distance from a certain type of middle-class girlish trajectory. She is not that kind of girl. But what kind of girl she is remains an open question, one the novel does not fully resolve.

Beyond Miriam's individual case, the novel registers "girl" as a social classification with its own rules and expectations. Mrs. Philps speaks of the Brooms' commitment to "keeping up with the old girls" [001-013] — the maintenance of a network of former pupils as a social and perhaps moral project. The "Girls' Friendly" [006-003] appears as an institutional name, evoking organised benevolence directed at young women. An "Irish girl" is cited as a source of opinion [009-100], her nationality and her girlhood both relevant to the social point being made.

The word operates differently depending on who uses it and about whom. When Mrs. Philps calls Miriam a "giddy girl" [001-011], it is affectionate diminution. When Miriam refuses to call herself "an old girl" [001-013], it is a rejection of containment. When von Heber is seen sitting opposite a "fair young English girl" [006-019], the phrase carries the weight of a romantic tableau — girl as object of male attention, fair and English as modifiers that place her in a national-aesthetic tradition.

## Significance

The figure of the girl in *Interim* is not a theme so much as a register: a way of naming a stage of life that the novel's central consciousness is in the process of leaving. Richardson treats girlhood as both a remembered condition (the schoolgirl self, the Bailey girls) and a social attribution that others impose (the "giddy girl," the "old girl"). Miriam's relationship to the category is characteristically double — she inhabits it in memory, resists it in the present, and observes it in others with the detached attention she brings to all social forms.

The novel's treatment of the girl sits at the intersection of [[young-in-interim|youth]], [[woman-in-interim|womanhood]], and [[memory-in-interim|memory]]. To be a girl in *Interim* is to be positioned on a threshold — no longer a [[child-in-interim|child]], not yet a woman, visible to others in ways one does not fully control.
