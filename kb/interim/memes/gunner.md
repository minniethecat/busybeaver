# Mr. Gunner

Mr. Gunner is a boarder at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park establishment in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. He belongs to the small population of young men in a household otherwise dominated by women, and his presence — loud, laughing, socially conspicuous — provides both energy and friction in the novel's dinner-table scenes. He is, in the taxonomy of the boarding-house, a character defined by performance: his laughter, his smiles, his place at the table are all enacted before an audience.

## Character Overview

Gunner is introduced through his laughter — a bodily, unselfconscious sound that seems to fill the space around him.

> Mr. Gunner's laughter flung back his head… [006-009]

The verb "flung" suggests something involuntary, almost violent: laughter that possesses the laugher, that operates on him rather than through him. This is not polite amusement but a physical release, and it marks Gunner as someone whose emotions are close to the surface.

> Gunner was smiling sideways down the table… [006-011]

The sideways smile, directed down the length of the table, captures Gunner's social mode: aware of his audience, performing for a distributed gaze. He is not simply feeling; he is communicating feeling, using his face and body as instruments of social engagement.

## In the Novel

### Dinner-Table Dynamics

Gunner's appearances are concentrated in the evening scenes, where the boarding-house community gathers for dinner. His presence is registered by the novel's protagonist, Miriam Henderson, with a mixture of amusement and critical distance.

> …taking of the Baileys and Mr. Gunner for granted… [006-019]

The phrase "taking for granted" positions Gunner as part of the furniture of the Bailey world — sufficiently established, sufficiently familiar, that his presence no longer requires comment or justification. He has become part of the household's taken-for-granted reality.

### Gossip and Speculation

Like [[scott|Miss Scott]] and [[sissie|Sissie]], Gunner is a subject of the boarding-house's informal oral culture. His behaviour is observed, discussed, and woven into the ongoing narrative the boarders collectively construct about one another.

> "…what happened, that's between Mr. Gunner and Miss S. They got to know each other…" [009-035]

The elliptical reference — "Miss S." — and the knowing tone capture the gossip economy of the boarding-house. Gunner's romantic or quasi-romantic entanglements are public property, material for speculation and narrative construction by the other boarders.

> …the spectacle of Mr. Gunner, or Miss Scott, following her to Ruscino's… [009-036]

The word "spectacle" is significant. Gunner is not simply observed; he is watched, his actions framed as performance. The boarding-house is a theatre, and Gunner is one of its more vivid performers.

### Work and Daily Life

Gunner is not only a presence at dinner. He has a life beyond the boarding-house, though the novel shows it to us only in fragments.

> "Rodkin and Helsing and Gunner's out all day." [010-089]

The brief mention — Gunner's name listed alongside colleagues (or employers) — sketches the outlines of a working life that exists off-stage. Gunner is "out all day," part of the London world of work and commerce that the novel's women observe from within the domestic sphere.

## Key Relationships

### Miss Scott

Gunner and Miss Scott appear together in the novel's gossip, their names linked by the speculative energy of the dinner table. Both are boarders; both are observed and commented upon; both become "spectacle" for the other inhabitants of the household.

### Dr. von Heber

Gunner shares the dinner table with [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]], the charismatic German dentist. Where von Heber dominates through musical performance and romantic intrigue, Gunner's mode of social presence is more straightforward — laughter, smiles, the simple fact of being young and male in a house of women.

### Mrs. Bailey

As the landlady, [[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] presides over the social world in which Gunner moves. His presence at her table, his position within the household's economy of hospitality, are part of the larger pattern she orchestrates.

## Significance

Mr. Gunner is a minor character, but his narrative function is precise. He brings masculine energy into a predominantly feminine domestic space. His laughter and smiles provide contrast to the more introspective mode of Miriam's consciousness. And his role as an object of gossip illustrates the novel's deep interest in how people become stories — how individual actions are observed, narrated, and transformed into the collective oral text of the boarding-house's social life.
