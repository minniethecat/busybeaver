# Joy in *Interim*

Joy in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* is rarely a public emotion. It arrives in solitary moments — waking in the dark to distant music, walking alone through London streets, speaking in a foreign tongue — and it is almost always held back, kept private, contained within Miriam Henderson's consciousness rather than expressed outwardly. The novel distinguishes carefully between joy as an inner state and the social performance of "enjoying" oneself, a distinction that goes to the heart of Richardson's modernist method.

## In the Novel

Joy appears in *Interim* most vividly in moments of solitude. Miriam wakes in the night at the Brooms' house to hear the Christmas waits playing outside.

> She held in her joy to listen. There was no sadness in the curious sorrowful little air. It drew her out into the quiet neighbourhood... [001-012]

The verb "held in" is decisive: joy is not something to be released or shared but something to be contained, controlled, the better to attend to the experience that produced it. The joy is private — Miriam lies in darkness, alone, listening to music played "to no one, out into the darkness." The music reaches "the secret happy interior of the life of each sleeping form," and this "secret happy interior" is precisely where joy resides in Richardson's fictional world — hidden within, inaccessible to others, discovered only in solitude.

Later that same Christmas morning, Miriam experiences a different kind of joy — the joy of being with the Brooms — but even this she must qualify and manage. "There was something that she had forgotten, some point that was being missed, something that must be said at this moment to pin down the happiness of everything" [001-013]. The impulse is to "pin down" joy, to fix it in language, but the attempt fails: "It was going away." Joy is fugitive; the moment it is named or sought it retreats.

## Joy vs. Social Enjoyment

The novel draws a sustained distinction between joy and social enjoyment. When Mrs. Philps declares "You never know when you're happy," Grace protests "Oh, I don't *know* aunt, I think you *do*," and Miriam responds with the deflecting chirp: "I'm very happy, thank you — aren't we all happy dear brethren?" [001-013]. The exchange is comic but the point is serious: public declarations of happiness are performance, not experience. Real joy resists being stated.

At a musical evening with Mr. Bowdoin, the men talk "in eager happy sentences and laughing happily" [005-004]. The doubling of "happy" — in sentences and in laughter — makes it a quality of surface, of social display. Miriam observes it but does not share it; her own joy is elsewhere, in the solitary walk home afterward:

> the joy of the need for the understanding sweeping glance from right to left as she moved across [the road], the sense of being swept across in an easy curve drawn by the kindly calculable swing of the traffic [005-005]

This is joy as physical fluency, as harmony with the urban environment — solitary, wordless, felt in the body rather than spoken in company.

The distinction is pressed hardest in a later passage where Miriam contrasts Mrs. Bailey's moral judgment of Eleanor with her own private feeling:

> It might end in nothing and the mean selfish joy would still be waiting upstairs as soon as one had forgotten that it was mean and selfish. [010-013]

Here joy is frankly acknowledged as "mean and selfish" — a guilty secret, something one keeps for oneself in defiance of social obligation and moral approval. The phrase "mean selfish joy" strips away the social veneer of happiness and reveals joy as a fundamentally private possession, indifferent to others' claims.

## The Joy of Saying and Hearing

One of the novel's most complex treatments of joy involves language itself. When Miriam engages in French conversation with Bernard Mendizabal, she experiences a specific form of delight:

> ...she slid down her sentence to leap, flouting them, forsaking her crowding thoughts, and catch at any cost, the joy of saying and hearing no matter what, in foreign speech. She would pay for the moment any price to make it sound and keep it sounding in the room. [003-014]

The "joy of saying and hearing" is a joy specific to language — not to what is said but to the act of speaking and being understood in another tongue. It is a "forgotten joy so unexpectedly renewed," a recovery of something lost from earlier life. The phrase "at any cost" and "any price" underscore the intensity of the need: this joy is worth whatever social price it exacts (here, affronting Sissie and Emile). And it is, again, something she wants to "keep it sounding in the room" — to prolong, to hold, to dwell in.

Later, Richardson gives us the phrase "driven joyfully forth" [008-013], describing Miriam after dinner, "united with everyone in the room as her feet carried her step by step without destination." Here joy is fused with movement — not a static emotion but a propulsion, a being-carried. It is also characteristically ambiguous: she is both "remaining behind" and "going everywhere," both solitary and united. Joy in *Interim* lives in these paradoxes.

## Joy Constrained, Joy Kept Private

The novel's most characteristic treatment of joy is as something held back. Miriam wakes from deep sleep to music and immediately "held in her joy to listen" [001-012]. She experiences the "mean selfish joy" that waits upstairs [010-013]. She offers Mrs. Bailey "the joy of her journey down" [009-003] — joy offered as a gift to another, a social translation of private experience.

Even in her most exuberant moments, joy is qualified. Walking alone at night, she feels "an inexhaustible blissfulness, everything melted away into it" [005-005] — but this comes after the constraint of an evening spent among others, unable to speak or be herself. The blissfulness is the release from constraint, not its opposite.

Richardson's treatment of joy is consistent with her larger modernist project. Just as [[conversation-in-interim|conversation]] in the novel is never merely informational, joy is never merely a feeling. It is always relational — a negotiation between private experience and social surface, between the "secret happy interior" and the public performance of enjoyment. Joy must be held in, because to release it would be to lose it.

## Key Passages

> ...she came so gently up from her deep sleep. She held in her joy to listen. There was no sadness in the curious sorrowful little air. [001-012]

The novel's foundational image of joy: solitary, nocturnal, awakened by music, and immediately held back.

> ...catch at any cost, the joy of saying and hearing no matter what, in foreign speech. She would pay for the moment any price to make it sound and keep it sounding in the room. [003-014]

Joy defined as linguistic pleasure — the delight not in what is said but in the act of speaking and being heard.

> It might end in nothing and the mean selfish joy would still be waiting upstairs as soon as one had forgotten that it was mean and selfish. [010-013]

Joy acknowledged as morally compromised, private, indifferent to social judgment — and for that very reason, the more real.

## Significance

Joy in *Interim* is a private, interior emotion that resists social articulation. It is distinguished from the public performance of enjoyment and located instead in solitary moments: waking in darkness, walking alone at night, speaking a foreign language, sitting in a restaurant where the waiter's kindness transforms an intimidating space into "a bit of her own London home" [004-012]. The novel's treatment of joy mirrors its treatment of [[secret-in-interim|secrecy]], [[silence-in-interim|silence]], and [[music-in-interim|music]] — all modes of experience that exist most fully when not shared.

Richardson's achievement is to render joy not as a simple positive emotion but as a complex negotiation between inner abundance and outer constraint. Joy must be held in — and the holding-in, paradoxically, is part of what makes it joy.
