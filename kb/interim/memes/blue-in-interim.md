# Blue in _Interim_

**Blue** in _Interim_ is the novel's most recurrent colour, appearing in eyes, dresses, streetlight, and sky. Richardson deploys blue across a wide tonal range — from the "pale waxy blue" of strange eyes [004-012] to the "blue-lit" vastness of a London street at dusk [005-005] — making it the chromatic signature of the novel's visual world.

## Blue Eyes

Blue eyes proliferate in the novel's character descriptions, but they are rarely simply "blue." Sissie has "pale steady grey-blue eyes" [002-007], the compound adjective registering uncertainty — are they grey or blue? The waitress at the restaurant has "curious pale waxy blue eyes" [004-012], the waxiness making them strange, slightly inhuman. Heber's are "a hard opaque pale blue" [006-009], the hardness suggesting malice beneath the surface. Richardson's blue eyes are never merely beautiful; they are diagnostic, revealing character through the precise quality of their colouring.

Miriam's own self-assessment comes refracted through a mirror: "her blue eyes set judiciously on the face of the important Duchesse mirror" [009-090]. The mirror reflects the blue eyes back at their owner, the adjective "judiciously" suggesting appraisal — Miriam seeing herself as another might see her.

## Blue Light

Blue in the novel is as much an atmospheric condition as a colour of objects. Miriam steps out into "the high quiet empty blue-lit street" [005-005], the compound adjective capturing the peculiar quality of evening light in the city — not darkness but a blue-tinged luminosity suspended between day and night. Indoors, "the faint blue light coming up from the street lit up the outer edges of the grey stone windowsills" [008-021], the blue seeping upward into rooms, colouring the built environment.

Against this urban blue, the novel's one evocation of Mediterranean blue stands out: "white plaster houses against the blue of the Mediterranean, grey mimosa trees, green-feathered lilac of wisteria" [008-008]. This is blue as escape — the saturated colour of a southern sky remembered from within grey London.

## Blue Objects

Blue marks objects of modest significance. The "skimpy blue serge dresses" [002-006] of the Bailey children are practical and unlovely — blue as the colour of school uniforms and economy. The pamphlet on the table is "coloured; blue" [009-100], its blueness its most notable feature, catching the lamplight. The note Miriam finds is on "Silurian blue" paper [011-002], the geological adjective lending the colour a learned gravity — this is not just blue but Silurian blue, a colour named with the precision of someone who cares about distinctions.

## Blue as Emotional Register

The "blue-lit" street [005-005] into which Miriam steps is also an emotional space — "tranquil immensity" following a difficult evening. Blue light here offers relief, a cool expanse after the heat of social tension. The blue of the pamphlet under the streetlamp is an anchor for attention in a dim room. Blue in *Interim* functions less as a symbol than as a sensory fact that carries feeling — the colour of threshold states, of evenings, of things seen in uncertain light.
