## Voices as Character Portraits

Richardson characterises individuals through their voices with remarkable economy. The "feeble voice like a chant" [001-010] that punctuates a moment of domestic drama and the "hurried low reproving voice" [001-014] that emerges onto a landing convey character through sound alone.

Florrie's "most grinding voice" provokes laughter [001-013], the harshness of the sound becoming the point of the social exchange.

> …Florrie in her most grinding voice and laughed. Two voices broke… [001-013]

Professional and social roles are registered vocally. A "dry professional official voice" [004-013] speaking at a hearth carries the weight of institutional authority. The "low quiet glad confident voice steadying the aspect of the world" [004-014] conveys reassurance and presence.

The "quiet weary voice" [004-003] of a speaker offering to take "the liberty of w…" suggests exhaustion beneath politeness.

## Voice and Interiority

For Miriam, voices carry an almost physical presence. The "hovering haunting consoling voice pure and clear, in a shape" [002-009] that she perceives suggests voice as a kind of material substance, something that occupies space and touches the listener.

> …a voice thrilling with stored up laughter racing through her mind… [001-015]

A "voice thrilling with stored up laughter" [001-015] registers in Miriam's mind as a physical sensation — the voice not merely heard but felt. The "shapeless colourless voice murmuring from the back of her head" [003-011] captures the interior quality of recalled speech, a voice half-heard in memory rather than present reality.

The "smooth-voiced world" [001-017] that Miriam imagines as a "passport" into social belonging reveals voice as a marker of class aspiration — the quality of one's voice determines access to certain social spheres.

## The Sound-World of the Boarding House

The Baileys' boarding house is defined as much by its soundscape as by its physical spaces. "Voices and laughter" around a table create "an uproar" [006-003], while "voices and the creaking open of doors" drift up from the street below [002-003].

This layering of sound — voices from different rooms, from the street, from memory — is central to Richardson's rendering of consciousness as a receptive surface continually impacted by sensory input.

> …darkness from the street below, voices and the creaking open of doo… [002-003]

A voice "sounded in the hall" [002-006] — the domestic space amplifying and distributing sound through its architecture. The "educative voice" [004-009] that Miriam anticipates in one scene links vocal authority to knowledge and social learning.

## Voice and Social Performance

The contrast between cultivated and unselfconscious voices runs through the novel. A woman speaking "in a high pure girlish voice" [006-005] registers differently from the voice that is "refined and she talked easily" [005-004] — each tonal quality carrying implications about class, education, and self-presentation.

> …a woman in a high pure girlish voice, "I doan blame 'im." [006-005]

The "adenoid voice and a narrow sniggering laugh" [006-004] of a minor character shows how Richardson uses vocal description as shorthand for moral judgement. Dante's voice [004-010], listened to by an attentive audience, represents the authority of high culture rendered audible.
