## Introduction

Interiority is the central fact of *Interim*. The novel's action is not external — not plot, not event — but the movement of Miriam Henderson's consciousness as she navigates London lodgings, family obligations, and the social surface of Edwardian life. The narrative stays close to her thoughts, her half-formed impressions, her sudden revelatory moments.

> The inner life withheld from others. [001-011]

This is the novel's true subject. Everything that happens happens first inside Miriam, and the prose registers this inwardness with unprecedented fidelity.

## The Narrative of Consciousness

Richardson's method is to follow consciousness wherever it goes.

> Things were coming to her out of the fire, fresh and new, seen for the first time; a flood of images. [001-011]

The mind does not narrate — it receives. Images arrive unbidden, and the prose records their passage. This is not introspection in the traditional sense (a character thinking about their thoughts) but something more radical: the rendering of consciousness as a continuous present tense of perception.

Against this inward life, social existence appears as surface. Social life is "a sort of state that made social life a talkative nothingness sliding" [004-006]. The contrast between the rich interior stream and the "talkative nothingness" of social exchange is one of the novel's persistent tensions.

## Light as Inner Weather

Richardson uses light as an index of interior states. Gaslight, firelight, twilight, and the changing London daylight register the novel's emotional weather — each quality of light corresponding to a state of Miriam Henderson's consciousness.

> The frosted globe preluding freedom and rest. [001-015]

The lamp illuminates the room but also the mood — readiness for solitude, for the inner life to resume after social performance. Light in *Interim* is never neutral; it is always the light of a particular state of mind.

## Voice and Interiority

The novel's famous style — sometimes called "stream of consciousness" — is better understood as a rendering of inner voice. Miriam's perceptions arrive in fragments, in rhythms that match breathing, in sentences that swerve mid-course. The prose does not explain the inner life; it performs it.

> Her thoughts went homelessly to and fro. [001-014]

The adverb does the work of interiority, making thought visible as movement through space. Thoughts go "homelessly" — seeking a place to settle, finding none, continuing their restless traverse. Interiority in Richardson's hands is not a settled condition but a continuous search.

## The Inward Turn

At night, consciousness turns fully inward. The darkness outside mirrors the inward turn — a distinctively modernist use of darkness, not as symbol but as condition. Unlike the Victorian night of danger or romance, Richardson's night is phenomenological: it is simply the condition in which consciousness becomes most fully aware of its own operations.

> Her mind became a blank. Her body was heavy on her chair, ill-clothed. [001-017]

Interiority is not always rich with content. It can empty out. The mind that one moment races with "topics… thrilling with stored up laughter" [001-015] can the next moment become a blank, leaving only the weight of the body in its chair. Richardson's fidelity to this intermittency — the ebb and flow of inner life — is what distinguishes her fictional method from all that preceded it.

## Significance

| Aspect | Victorian Novel | Richardson's Method |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Access to mind | Omniscient narrator summarizes thoughts | Direct rendering of consciousness in process |
| Inner/outer balance | External events drive narrative; thoughts comment on them | Inner life IS the narrative; external events register through it |
| Temporality | Linear, chronological | Layered — past, present, future coexist in consciousness |
| Epistemology | Narrator knows and explains | Consciousness discovers; nothing is fully known |

Richardson's achievement is to have made the novel a record of experiencing rather than of experience. By refusing the omniscient narrator's claims to knowledge, she created a fiction truer to the way consciousness actually operates — partially, intermittently, richly, and always from the inside.
