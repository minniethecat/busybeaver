# Strength in _Interim_

**Strength** in _Interim_ is never taken for granted. Richardson's narrator observes it in others — in Grace Broom's personality, in von Heber's physique, in the clear light of a room — and registers its absence in herself. Strength is at once physical, moral, and social: a quality that some possess and others lack, and that the narrator navigates between.

## Thematic Dimensions

### Strength as Physical Vitality

The novel registers strength as bodily presence. "Her body was full of sleep and strength; all one strength from head to foot" [011-001] captures an integrated physical wholeness — strength not as effort but as fullness. Von Heber is described through "the strength and sunshine of him" [007-007], his physical vigour inseparable from his social magnetism. The comparison with another man who lacks "von Heber's strength nor his manner" [007-009] makes strength a distinguishing feature, a quality that sets one person above another.

### Strength of Personality

Grace Broom embodies strength of will: "All the strength of Grace's personality was quiet and strong" [001-017]. The doubling of "strength" and "strong" emphasises the completeness of her character — she does not perform strength; she is strength. The observation that "there was never enough time and strength to make everything clear" [001-010] reveals the narrator's own sense of insufficiency, the lack of the very quality she observes in Grace.

### Strength as Illumination

Richardson extends strength beyond the human into the atmospheric. "The room was full of clear strength" [002-002] makes strength a property of space — not metaphorical but felt, a quality of light and clarity that inhabits a room. The phrase "clear strength" recurs, linking strength to lucidity rather than force.

### Strength and Social Judgment

Strength is also a social quality, observed and compared. The "curious blending of sturdy strength and polished refinement" [003-009] captures the social evaluation of a man's bearing — strength must be mediated by refinement to be socially legible. When the narrator gives "all her strength and interest" [006-015] to a task, strength becomes directed will, the capacity to commit oneself fully.

## Key Passages

- **[002-002]** "The room was full of clear strength. There must always be a clear…" — Strength as an atmospheric quality, divorced from human agency.
- **[007-007]** "In the strength and sunshine of him there is his general effect" — Von Heber's physical presence rendered as elemental force.
- **[011-001]** "Her body was full of sleep and strength; all one strength from head to foot" — Bodily wholeness as the experience of strength.
- **[001-017]** "All the strength of Grace's personality was quiet and strong" — Strength of character as silent, self-contained power.
- **[001-010]** "There was never enough time and strength to make everything clear" — The narrator's acknowledgment of her own limits.
