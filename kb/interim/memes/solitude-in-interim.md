## Introduction

Solitude in *Interim* is neither affliction nor achievement — it is the novel's baseline condition. Miriam Henderson lives among others — boarders, family, the passing figures of London streets — but her consciousness is fundamentally solitary. The novel tracks the texture of this solitude: how it feels to be alone in a room, alone in a crowd, alone with one's thoughts in the aftermath of social gatherings. Solitude is not loneliness (though it can shade into it) but the ground from which perception and reflection arise.

## The Room as Solitude's Architecture

Miriam's rented room is the spatial expression of her solitude. Returned from an evening of talk, she finds "the freshness and quietude of the room enfolded Miriam, smoothing away grubbiness and fatigue" [001-011]. The room does not isolate her — it enfolds her, offering the quiet in which consciousness can gather itself. This is solitude as refuge, not deprivation.

## Solitude and Social Life

The novel draws a sharp distinction between the solitary self and the social self. At the Brooms', Miriam performs sociability — talks, laughs, tells stories — but even in company she is aware of the gap between what she shows and what she feels. "Giving an opinion opened so many things. It was impossible to show everything, the more opinions you expressed the more you misled people and the further you got away from them." [001-011] Solitude is where the self can stop performing.

## Walking Alone

London streets provide another register of solitude. Miriam walks alone through "the empty stretch of lamplit pavement leading on and on," her thoughts moving with her footsteps. Street solitude is different from room solitude — more exposed, more porous to the city's stimuli — but it shares the same essential quality: the self in motion, unaccompanied, thinking its own thoughts. Richardson's London walking scenes are among the novel's richest renderings of solitary consciousness.

## Solitude and Freedom

The novel's most important insight about solitude is its link to freedom. The solitary self is free — not from obligation (the obligations remain) but from the demand to be legible to others. "Outside one need do nothing but what was expected of one, asking nothing for oneself but freedom to return, to the centre" [002-002]. Solitude is the centre, the place consciousness returns to after its dispersals.
