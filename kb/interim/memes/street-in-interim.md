# Street in *Interim*

The street in *Interim* is the necessary outside — the zone Miriam Henderson crosses to reach other interiors, the space where the city's weather, light, and population press against her consciousness. Richardson's streets are never neutral thoroughfares; they are sensory environments, charged with the difference between the enclosed warmth of the boarding-house and the exposed public realm.

## In the Novel

The novel's geography is organised around a series of street-crossings. Miriam moves between Banbury Park and the Corries' house, between the boarding-house and the railway station, between the familiar and the unfamiliar. Each street journey is rendered with the same attention to sensory texture that Richardson gives to interiors: the quality of lamplight, the sound of footsteps, the temperature of the air.

> She wandered slowly home through the drizzling rain. [004-012]

The street here is neither destination nor obstacle but medium — the element through which Miriam moves, "slowly" and "wandering" rather than directly. The drizzling rain softens the street's edges, makes it a space of suspension rather than transit.

> Through the darkness from the street below, voices and the creaking open of doors. [002-003]

The street penetrates the house. Even indoors, the street is present — through windows, through sounds that rise from below. Richardson's domestic interiors are never sealed; they are permeable to the city, and the street is the medium of that permeability.

## Key Passages

> The icy wind drove against her as she turned into a maze of side streets. [002-005]

The "maze of side streets" suggests both the complexity of London's geography and Miriam's uncertain navigation of it. The icy wind is an antagonist — the city's weather as a force to be resisted. Richardson's streets are physical before they are symbolic.

> When they drifted out into the street seemed all to be going the same way. [005-005]

The street unifies. After an evening indoors, the party spills into the street and discovers a common direction — "all to be going the same way." The street here is a space of collective movement, the shared outside that dissolves the separateness of the interior.

> She might get away and back home alone up a side street. Amidst the shouting of women. [006-026]

The side street as escape route. "Alone" is the operative word — the side street offers solitude where the main thoroughfare offers crowd. The "shouting of women" is the noise of working-class London, the life of the street from which Miriam withdraws.

> Far away behind her the black and gold of the streets. [006-028]

"Black and gold" — the street's colours at night. Black for darkness, gold for lamplight: the urban night rendered in two pigments. The phrase is characteristic of Richardson's impressionist method — colour standing for the whole sensory complex of the street after dark.

## Significance

The street in *Interim* is the novel's most important non-domestic space. It is where the private consciousness of the boarding-house interior meets the public world of the city. Richardson's streets are not described panoramically — she does not give us London as a view — but phenomenologically, as a series of sensory encounters: the feel of wind, the sound of voices, the quality of lamplight on wet pavement.

This treatment of the street is part of Richardson's larger project of rendering consciousness through its sensory environment. The street is not a setting but an experience — something that happens to Miriam, something she moves through and is changed by.
