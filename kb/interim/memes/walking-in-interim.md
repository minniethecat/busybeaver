# Walking in *Interim*

Walking is the physical activity most closely associated with Miriam Henderson's consciousness in Dorothy Richardson's novel *Interim*. Through the streets of London — along the [[farringdon-road|Farringdon Road]], past [[st-pancras|St. Pancras Church]], toward [[wimpole-street|Wimpole Street]] — Miriam walks and thinks, her footsteps providing a rhythm for the associative movement of her mind.

## Walking and Consciousness

Walking in the novel is never merely transportation. It is a mode of being — the physical correlative of the stream of consciousness. The rhythm of walking (step, step, step) mirrors the rhythm of thought (one impression leading to another), and the passing cityscape provides a continuous stream of sensory stimuli that triggers memory and reflection.

> Rapid walking in the rain-freshened air. The pavement leading on and on. [004-012]

The "pavement leading on and on" captures the forward momentum of both walking and thinking — the sense of a journey without a fixed destination, driven by internal rather than external necessity.

## Walking as Freedom

For Miriam, walking represents a form of freedom unavailable within domestic space. On the street, she is neither daughter nor governess nor boarder — she is simply a body in motion, a consciousness observing the world.

> She had not felt that they were walking along, outside London, in some strange unknown country. [005-002]

The transformation of familiar London into "some strange unknown country" through the act of walking reveals the perceptual novelty that walking enables — the city made strange by the rhythm of pedestrian movement.

## Significance

Walking in *Interim* is part of a broader literary tradition — the urban pedestrian as observer, the *flâneur* — but with a crucial difference: Richardson's walker is a woman, and her freedom to walk the city alone is itself a political fact. Miriam's walks are acts of independence in a world where women's mobility was constrained by convention and danger.

The walking motif also serves a formal function: it provides the novel with a principle of movement that replaces plot. Where another novel might advance through events, *Interim* advances through walks — each outing a new occasion for consciousness to encounter the world.
