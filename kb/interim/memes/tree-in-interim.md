# Tree in _Interim_

Trees in _Interim_ punctuate the London streetscape as living presences — reminders of a natural world that persists within the city's masonry. Richardson's trees are never mere scenery; they are agents of perception, markers of time, and figures for human consciousness itself.

## London's Trees

The novel's trees appear most vividly at twilight, when their forms soften and merge with the darkening sky. "Presently the sky would be mother-of-pearl above a soft dark mass and then pure evening grey outlining the dark feathery tree tops of a London square turning to green below in the lamplight" [008-019]. The trees form the boundary between sky and street, their "feathery" tops a dark scrim against the fading light. They are both architecture and organism — "the lamplit green trees; plane-tree shadows on the narrow pavement" [008-021] link the built environment to the living world.

The plane tree, London's characteristic street tree, appears in its distinctive dappled bark and broad leaves. Its shadows fall across the pavement as Miriam walks, creating a pattern of light and dark that mirrors the novel's own rhythmic alternation between illumination and obscurity.

## Tree as Figure

Richardson twice uses the tree as a simile for Miriam's own bearing. "She drew herself upright, careless, like a tree in the wind" [010-031] — the tree is rooted yet flexible, standing firm while bending to force. The image recurs: "her bearing altered forever, held up by him, like a tree in the wind, everyone powerless to embarrass her" [010-031]. The tree is resilience made visible, a natural uprightness that withstands external pressure without rigidity.

This figurative use of the tree is characteristic of Richardson's method: the natural world provides a vocabulary for states of consciousness that resist direct description. Miriam does not "feel" like a tree; she stands like one, her body finding a posture that corresponds to an inner equilibrium.

## Trees and Time

The trees of the London squares mark the passage of seasons — bare in winter, "turning to green" [008-019] in spring, heavy with leaf in summer. For Miriam, walking through the squares, the trees provide a temporal register different from the social clock of meals and appointments. They measure time in the slow rhythm of growth and decay, offering a counterpoint to the novel's diurnal structure.
