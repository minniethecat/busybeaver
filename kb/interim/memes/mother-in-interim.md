## Introduction

The mother in *Interim* is both an absence and a presence. Miriam's own mother is dead — "it must always have been there since her mother's death" [002-006] — and her absence is the ground note beneath all of Miriam's interactions with maternal figures. Mrs. Bailey, the landlady of the boarding house, becomes a surrogate mother, her warmth and authority filling the domestic space. The mother-daughter relationship reverberates through the novel in registers of tenderness, distance, and unspoken understanding.

## Mrs. Bailey as Surrogate Mother

Mrs. Bailey's maternal presence defines the boarding house. She calls Miriam "cold mother darling" and pulls her chair "nearer the fire" [003-009], the endearment blending affection with a mother's practical concern for warmth. Her voice — "the voice would sound again" [003-009] — is the voice of the house itself, inquiring, reassuring. She manages the household with the quiet authority of a mother who knows each boarder's needs without asking.

## The Absent Mother

Miriam's mother died before the events of *Interim*, and the loss is felt as a permanent condition. The memory of her mother's hats, her presence, her way of arranging people, surfaces in Miriam's consciousness as a standard against which present social arrangements are measured. The past — the "picturesque suburb," the "pretty corner house standing alone in its garden" [001-017] — is a maternal landscape now inaccessible.

## Mother-Daughter Dynamics

The Bailey daughters sit "close against their mother" [002-006] at the dinner table, the physical proximity speaking of an intimacy Miriam observes from outside. Between Mrs. Bailey and Miriam, there is conspiratorial warmth — "she conspired with an easy contemptuous conscience and they stood murmuring" [002-007]. The maternal bond is recreated in whispers, in shared glances, in the small intimacies of domestic life that Miriam, motherless, receives as gifts.

## The Maternal Gaze

Mrs. Bailey's attention is maternal in its completeness — she sees Miriam in ways that others do not. Her questionings are gentle but persistent; she notices cold hands, tiredness, the need for tea. The maternal gaze in *Interim* is not the biological mother's but the landlady's — a professional motherliness that is no less real for being embedded in the economics of the boarding house.
