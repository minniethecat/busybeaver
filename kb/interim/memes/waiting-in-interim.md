## Introduction

Waiting pervades the world of *Interim* as both a physical state and an emotional condition. Characters pause at thresholds, linger for responses, and suspend themselves between the present moment and an anticipated future. The boarding-house at Tansley Street becomes a stage where waiting is the default posture — for meals, for conversation, for something unnamed to arrive.

## Domestic Suspension

The house itself embodies waiting. "All the furniture seemed to be waiting for someone or something" [002-008], as if the domestic interior shares the emotional atmosphere of its inhabitants. Miriam watches the dying fire while "waiting tremulously for a verdict" [001-017], the household objects around her mirroring her suspense. The house "had been her own; waiting for her when she found it" [002-006] — a space defined as much by anticipation as by ownership.

## Social Anticipation

Waiting shapes the social fabric of the novel. Miriam follows the postman "waiting for the eyes to lift" [001-014], caught in the delicate choreography of human encounter. At the Gower Street curb, characters stand "waiting to plunge through the passing" traffic [006-024], their physical pause charged with social tension. Mr. Mendizabal is found "waiting unconcernedly outside the chemist" [006-025], his composure a counterpoint to the anxious waiting that characterizes Miriam's inner life.

## Temporal Threshold

Waiting in *Interim* marks the boundary between present and future. "The year that was waiting far off, invisible behind the" horizon [001-015] suggests a temporal dimension beyond immediate domestic routines. Miriam watches from the window "sullenly waiting for schooldays to be at an end" [007-001], her waiting directed toward a threshold she cannot yet cross. This temporal waiting gives the novel its characteristic atmosphere of suspension — between girlhood and womanhood, between the known and the unknown.
