## Overview

Alienation in *Interim* pervades Miriam Henderson's experience of London as she moves through spaces where she is physically present but socially and emotionally absent. The boarding-house on Tansley Street, where residents live side by side without genuine connection, becomes a microcosm of modern estrangement, while Miriam's role as a dental assistant keeps her a perpetual observer rather than a participant in social life. Her experience of spending New Year's Eve alone in a cold bedroom [002-002] crystallizes the novel's central tension between proximity and isolation.

## Thematic Dimensions

Social alienation runs through the novel as Miriam stands outside the circles of conventional social life. When she reflects that "no one had missed her" [002-002], the absence of social recognition becomes a quiet wound. Yet she also perceives the hollowness of such rituals: "Social life, any sort of social life anywhere would not help" [002-006], and she registers "that sort of state that made social life a talkative nothingness" [004-006]. This double movement — excluded from social life while simultaneously rejecting its emptiness — defines her alienated position.

Emotional alienation deepens within the domestic spaces that should offer belonging. The boarding-house transforms into a site of estrangement where "They were becoming strangers in their own dining-room" [004-003], and the sudden appearance of "a stranger sitting at the fireside" [003-002] disrupts whatever fragile sense of home exists. Miriam's solitude in the dental practice further reinforces her distance, positioning her behind a professional mask that separates her from both patients and the current of ordinary life.

Existential alienation emerges through the novel's rendering of Miriam's solitary movement through London streets. The experience of "pressing alone wearily on through the dying" [001-011] conveys a metaphysical weariness, while being "Left alone with silence all along the street" [001-015] transforms urban space into a site of radical isolation. Miriam questions the very meaning of her condition: "What did people mean about loneliness? It was always the people arr..." [004-006], a truncated thought that mirrors the incompleteness and fragmentation of alienated consciousness.

## Key Passages

- **[002-002]** "No one had missed her" — Miriam's recognition of her social invisibility, intensified by the fact that she spent New Year's Eve alone in a cold bedroom, an emblematic scene of alienation at the threshold of a new year.
- **[002-006]** "Social life, any sort of social life anywhere would not help" — The rejection of social remedy, suggesting that her estrangement is not circumstantial but existential.
- **[003-002]** "There was a stranger sitting at the fireside" — The intrusion of the unfamiliar into the domestic interior, rendering even the hearth alien.
- **[004-003]** "They were becoming strangers in their own dining-room" — The metamorphosis of intimate space into territory of estrangement, capturing the relational decay within the boarding-house.
- **[001-015]** "Left alone with silence all along the street" — The urban landscape as a mirror of inner solitude, where the city's expanse offers no consolation.
