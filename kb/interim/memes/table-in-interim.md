# Table in _Interim_

The **table** in _Interim_ is more than furniture; it is the organising centre of domestic and social life. Meals are served at it, letters arrive on it, conversations circle around it. As a material object that gathers people and objects into relation, the table anchors the novel's repeated scenes of communal domesticity at the Baileys' boarding house.

## The Table as Social Stage

The supper table is the first interior object Miriam navigates in the novel, as she "got herself round the supper table to stand with Mrs. Philps on the hearthrug" [PN=001-001]. The table here is both obstacle and gathering-point — you must move around it to reach the fire, the true centre of warmth and sociality. Florrie speaks "from across the table" [PN=001-010], the table mediating conversation as both distance and connection.

At the Baileys', the dining table is where the household coheres. The instruction "It's time to lay the table" [PN=003-011] marks the daily ritual that transforms the room from neutral space to staged intimacy. The "tablecloth flountering out across" [PN=003-013] registers the physical actions that produce this transformation — domestic labour made visible.

## Tables and Objects

Richardson attends to the material surfaces of tables — the "smeary marble of the hall table" [PN=003-002], the "console table" that Miriam notes with her newly acquired vocabulary of furniture [PN=002-008]. These details are not decorative; they register social class, aesthetic sensibility, and Miriam's growing fluency in reading domestic interiors.

The hall table is also where letters arrive — "On the hall table lay a letter; from Eve" [PN=004-003] — making it a threshold between the private world of the house and the wider network of relationships beyond. The table thus functions as a surface of communication as well as consumption.

## The Table and Conversation

The table governs the geometry of talk. At the Baileys' dinners, characters are arranged around it in fixed positions that shape who can speak to whom. Miriam finds herself "glancing up the table towards the centre of a conflict" [006-003], the table's length becoming the axis along which conversational alliances and tensions align. Voices travel "from across the table" [001-010] and "down the table" [006-011], the table surface serving as both conduit and barrier.

The table also frames performance. Mr. Gunner sits "with his elbows on the table" [006-013], his bodily claim on the surface asserting a masculine ease. Miriam retreats "to the far end of the table taking her place on Sissie's right hand" [006-019], her position at the margin reflecting her uncertain social footing. When she and another guest meet "at the end of the table standing one each side the angle of the fireside corner" [004-013], the table angle creates an alcove of intimacy — "smiling as if her murmured response to his greeting had been a speech in a play ready-made to bring them together" [004-013].

## Tables and Bohemian Identity

A different kind of table appears in the bohemian spaces Miriam visits. A "long kitchen table lit by a low barred window" [005-001] belongs to a world of artistic poverty and freedom — the table bare and functional rather than set for display. Miriam registers the difference: "It was Bohemia; the table and chairs were bohemian" [005-003]. Even "the empty white coffee cups standing about on the table caught the light" [005-004], their aesthetic quality transforming utilitarian objects.

The florist's workshop table is another variant: "Cut flowers in stone jam pots, masses of greenery lying on a wet table" [006-015]. Here the table is a surface of production, wet with labour, strewn with raw materials rather than the finished arrangements of the dining room.

## Work and Display

The table also serves as workspace. Miriam brings "the large book to the table" and begins "wretchedly turning" pages [PN=002-006] — the table becoming a desk, a site of solitary intellectual effort within the communal space. The "twisted cone of serviettes" whisked from the table [PN=004-012] and the "decorated table for dinner each night" [PN=004-004] mark the table's oscillation between practical surface and aesthetic display.
