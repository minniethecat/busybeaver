# Life in _Interim_

**Life** is the word Richardson's narrator returns to most often as a philosophical question. In _Interim_, life is not a given but a problem — something to be felt, examined, and occasionally enjoyed despite the pressure of its circumstances. "You know you enjoy life tremendously" (001-013) is spoken to Miriam as if it were news to her, and the novel's work is to test the proposition.

## Life as Inner Source

The novel's most lyric passage on life describes "nothing but the cool sense of life pouring from some inner source" (001-012). Life here is not external event but inward flow — a bodily conviction that precedes thought. This is Richardson's essential claim: life is felt before it is understood, and the feeling is the ground of everything else. "The glow of life was left there at the heart" (003-010) images life as ember, a residual warmth that persists even when circumstances are cold.

> …nothing but the cool sense of life pouring from some inner source…
> (001-012)

Against this inward conviction, external life exerts its own claims. "Something in the centre of life had steadied and clarified" (004-014) — a rare moment of resolution, when inner and outer briefly align. The novel does not promise that such moments last; it only records them when they come.

## Life in Motion and Texture

### Life as Movement

Life in _Interim_ is figured as motion. "Stepping observantly in through the edge of an unknown life" (001-014) describes the threshold experience of entering a world not one's own. "On and on as they went through life" (001-017) makes life a traversal, a going-forward. And the "old life, the only life known to those" (001-016) acknowledges the gravitational pull of habit and origin — the life one is "drawn back into" against the forward impulse.

The novel also offers moments of quiet acceptance: "Liking the prices of her new life. Accepting them without" (006-015) — life as economic reality, its costs acknowledged and borne without complaint. This is the life of the working woman, measured in wages and hours, distinct from the philosophical meditations but no less real.

### Life's Texture and Value

Richardson attends to life's sensory texture — "the room was full of life and warmth and golden light" (005-003) — a moment where life is simply present, filling a room with light. And yet she maintains the critical distance that allows her to see how "life arranged itself" (005-005), as if life had its own compositional intelligence independent of human will. The novel's gift is to hold both perspectives at once: life as something one suffers and life as something one shapes, the cool inner source and the warm gold of a sunlit room.

## Life in the Social World

### Life and Social Performance

Against this inward life, Richardson sets the social version — "a sort of state that made social life a talkative nothingness sliding" (004-006). Social life is surface, chatter, the friction of gatherings. "The sense of a complex London life crowded with engagements" (004-008) names the external busyness that fills time without necessarily touching the inner source. The narrator moves between these two registers, aware of their incommensurability.

### Life and Exclusion

Not everyone participates equally in life. Richardson registers exclusion with precision: "powerless to spread into her life. They and their world were" (001-015) — the social world that cannot penetrate the self's boundaries. The narrator knows the feeling of being "left out of life forever, because it was bette" (002-006), an exclusion that is also, paradoxically, a judgment of superiority. She observes life from the margins of spaces — "considered attics, apart from the life that was beginning to flow" (002-007) — where architecture mirrors psychology, the upper rooms of the house also being the rooms farthest from the social flow.

The novel also registers domestic imprisonment: "her life was imprisoned" (006-003) — life constricted by the expectations of those who claim to care for it. And the conditional "there would be no outside life for her" (006-003) marks the boundary between what life is and what it might have been. Against all this stands the conviction that "there was no affection in your life, that dreaming and brooding" (004-010), a diagnosis delivered as if from the outside, naming the cost of detachment.

## Life and Consciousness

### Life and Thought

Richardson is alert to the gap between living and thinking about living. "It was only in thoughts not in life that these things existed" (003-016) draws a sharp distinction: mental constructions are not life, however vivid they may be. "Explaining things, making life stop, while reality went on" (002-002) captures the paradox of reflection — to explain life is to arrest it, to step outside the flow one is trying to describe.

This gap between thought and life is not merely philosophical — it shapes the novel's narrative method. Richardson's sentences do not explain life; they register its passage, its textures, its interruptions. The narrator "could imagine nothing in life or in the world that could" (006-016) — the sentence breaks off, because life exceeds what can be imagined of it.

### Life's Multiple Levels

The novel's deepest insight about life is its simultaneity. The narrator experiences "deep-rooted moments still" (002-009) rising "from all the levels of her life" (002-009) — past and present, public and private, all intersecting in the single moment of consciousness. The everyday carries on alongside the profound: "in the hurrying confusion of everyday life something was being done" (003-011), a quiet undercurrent beneath the surface noise. And "the spaces of her separate life in the house became a background" (003-014) — life compartmentalised into rooms and hours, each space holding its own version of the self. This layered quality — life not as a single narrative but as simultaneous strata — is arguably Richardson's most significant formal innovation.
