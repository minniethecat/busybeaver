# Emile

Emile is the waiter at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park boarding-house in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. His French nationality and his role as a servant in an English domestic establishment make him a figure of particular interest: simultaneously inside and outside the household, present at the most intimate social moments yet separated by the boundary of service. His interactions with the boarders — particularly with Sissie — introduce a subtle erotic charge into the novel's dinner-table scenes.

## Character Overview

Emile is defined by his professional function — he lays the table, serves the meal, busies himself with the physical tasks that sustain the boarding-house's social life. But Richardson invests these mundane actions with significance, using Emile's movements around the table as part of the choreography of the evening.

> …she had come in and found Emile laying the table. There was no… [006-001]

The act of laying the table is a threshold moment — the preparation of the domestic stage before the actors arrive. Emile's presence at this moment, alone with the table, marks him as part of the household's infrastructure: the invisible labour that enables the visible social performance.

## In the Novel

### Service and Spectacle

Emile's work positions him at the boundary between servant and participant. He is in the room but not of the company; he moves through the social space without fully inhabiting it.

> Emile's vegetable dishes had been replenished… [006-011]

The detail — vegetable dishes replenished — is domestic and precise. Emile's labour is material: food, dishes, the physical requirements of a shared meal. His role is to ensure the dinner proceeds smoothly, that the boarders are fed, that the social ritual of the evening is physically sustained.

> Emile busied himself to lay him a place… [006-013]

The verb "busied" captures Emile's characteristic mode of action: purposeful, efficient, slightly self-effacing. He does not draw attention to himself; he facilitates the attention others receive.

### The French Connection

Emile's nationality is significant. In a novel interested in linguistic and cultural difference — the French language, the German dentist von Heber, the Scotch Miss Scott — Emile represents a specifically French presence within the English domestic sphere.

His French identity is not foregrounded in dramatic declarations but registered through his name and his implicit connection to the French language that threads through the novel. [[sissie|Sissie]] takes French lessons from Miriam; Emile is the actual Frenchman in the room. The two currents — the studied French of the pupil and the native French of the waiter — run parallel, occasionally converging.

### Emile and Sissie

The most vivid passages involving Emile concern his dynamic with Sissie, the young boarder who is learning French. Their interactions carry an undercurrent of flirtation, of glances exchanged and tensions unspoken.

> Sissie and Emile were back again in the room having a scene… [003-013]

> She had a sense of Emile and Sissie glancing and affronted while… [003-014]

The word "affronted" suggests a breach of decorum — a glance that pushed a boundary, an interaction that drew attention. Miriam's "sense" of the dynamic is characteristic of her observational mode: she registers the tension without being directly party to it. Emile and Sissie, in their glancing and affronted intimacy, form a secondary drama playing out at the margins of the dinner table.

> …and Sissie and Emile would forget her desperate onlooking… [003-016]

The phrase is poignant: Sissie and Emile are absorbed in one another; Miriam is "desperately onlooking." The waiter and the pupil have become a closed circuit, their mutual attention excluding the observer. In this triangle — Miriam watching Sissie and Emile watching each other — Richardson captures the loneliness that coexists with social proximity.

## Significance

Emile is a character of the margins — defined by service, by nationality, by his place at the edge of the dinner table. But marginality in Richardson's novel is not insignificance. Emile's presence introduces difference into the household: the difference of language, of nationality, of a young man's body moving through a space of women. His glancing encounters with Sissie are small events, but in the miniature world of the boarding-house, even a glance can constitute a scene.
