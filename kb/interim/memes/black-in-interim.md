## Introduction

Black is the dominant colour of *Interim*'s nocturnal world — the darkness of January streets, the black dress of a woman at work, the black wood of Victorian furniture, the black of moustaches and silk ties and high-shouldered engines. It is both absence and presence: the blackness that obscures and the black objects that populate the visible world with weight and solidity.

## Nocturnal London

The novel's London is saturated in blackness. Miriam "stared into the velvety blackness trying to see the furniture" [001-012], the darkness a material presence rather than mere absence of light. "Darkness was beginning; a hard black January darkness, utterly different to the friendly exciting twilights of the old year" [002-005] — black is seasonal, temporal, carrying the weight of winter. Against this darkness, Miriam flings herself "forward to the sense of sunshine, sudden beautiful things, unreasonable secret happiness, waiting somewhere beyond the blackness, to come again" [002-006]. Blackness becomes the condition against which hope is measured.

## Dress and Identity

Black clothes recur as markers of professional identity and social position. Miriam wears "her black dress with her clumsy golf-cape thrown back from her shoulders" [004-009], hoping she "could perhaps pass for a cultured person" in the soft evening light. Eve stands "badly in a droopy black dress on a bare wet wooden floor" [006-015] in a west-end shop, the black dress failing to confer the dignity it promises. "White muslin wings on a black straw hat, a well-cut check costume" [006-028] — the black-and-white contrast becomes a visual shorthand for social aspiration and its fragility.

## The Material World

Black in *Interim* is also a colour of objects: "a chiffonier in black wood supporting and reflecting in its little mirror a large square deeply carved dusty brown wooden box" [002-008], the black wood anchoring the Victorian interior. "Black bars" stripe the "wide clear core" of the fire's glow [004-007], the black of the grate framing the rose-gold warmth. Even the train arrives behind "its beloved black dumpy high-shouldered engine" [004-008], the adjective "beloved" transforming the black machine into something almost tender. At the chapter's close, Miriam wanders "out through the misty smoke-wreathed golden light, threading her way amongst the tables towards the black and gold of the streets" [006-028] — black and gold together, the night city a composition in two colours.
