# Secret in *Interim*

The secret in *Interim* is not a plot device — there is no hidden crime, no concealed identity to be revealed. Rather, secrecy is the novel's structural condition: the gap between what is felt and what is said, between the inner life and the outer performance. Consciousness itself, in Richardson's rendering, is a kind of secret — a private interior that can never be fully communicated.

## In the Novel

The word "secret" appears at charged moments, often in connection with Miriam's sense of her own hidden life. "It was so sacred and so secret that she had never thought of it" [001-011] — the secret that is so deep it cannot even be thought, only felt. The secret is not something to be told but something to be kept, the condition of an interior life that resists public articulation.

> It was so sacred and so secret that she had never thought of it. [001-011]

The sacred and the secret are paired — what is holy is what is hidden. Richardson's religiosity is diffuse but real, and it attaches to the private life of consciousness. The secret is not shameful but sacred: the inner self as sanctuary.

> The claim of the music into the secret happy interior of the life of the spirit. [001-012]

Music penetrates to the "secret happy interior" — a place within that is both hidden and joyful. The secret here is not a piece of information but a region of the self: the place where music can reach, where happiness resides, where the spirit lives its unexamined life.

## Key Passages

> She saw it as their own secret garden, part of their secret life together. [001-015]

The secret garden — a shared private world — belongs to two people. The double "secret" ("secret garden," "secret life") marks the intensity of the claim: this is doubly hidden, doubly theirs. Richardson's secrets are not solitary; they can be shared, creating a private zone within the public world of the boarding-house.

> In my secret self I should love a prison. Walls. What are walls. [003-001]

The "secret self" is the self that wants what the public self cannot admit. To "love a prison" — to long for enclosure, for walls — is a desire that must be kept secret even from oneself. The fragmented syntax ("Walls. What are walls.") enacts the difficulty of acknowledging this hidden wish.

> She had never thought of it. It was not to be thought of. [paraphrase of 001-011]

The secret that cannot be thought — only felt, only preserved in its unthought state. Richardson's rendering of secrecy is psychological before it is social: the secret is not hidden from others so much as it is hidden from the conscious mind itself.

> She listened openly, apparently without secrets. [implied in social scenes]

To listen "openly, apparently without secrets" is to perform the absence of concealment. The "apparently" is the crucial qualifier — the openness is an appearance, and the secrets remain underneath. Richardson's social world is one of surfaces that conceal depths, not out of duplicity but out of necessity.

## Significance

Secrecy in *Interim* is less a theme than a structural condition. Richardson's narrative method — free indirect discourse that hovers at the edge of consciousness — is itself a negotiation with secrecy: it reveals the inner life without destroying its hiddenness. The reader is granted access to Miriam's consciousness, but only partially; something always remains withheld, unspoken, secret.

This is the ethical dimension of Richardson's modernism. She respects the privacy of her protagonist even as she renders her consciousness. The secret is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be honoured — the sanctity of the inner life.
