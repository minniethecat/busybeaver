## Introduction

Gender in *Interim* is not a thesis but a texture — the accumulated weight of small observations about how men and women move, speak, and occupy space differently. Miriam Henderson's consciousness registers gender difference with the same sensory precision it brings to light, sound, and weather. The novel offers no programmatic feminism; instead, it renders the felt experience of living in a female body in a world structured by male confidence, male incomes, and male freedom of movement.

## Gender and Space

The novel maps a gendered geography of London. Men move freely through the city's streets, restaurants, and concert halls; women navigate a more restricted territory, their movements constrained by propriety, safety, and economic limitation. Miriam's walks through London are acts of quiet transgression — a woman alone on the streets after dark, claiming the freedom of movement that men take for granted. The boarding-house itself is a gendered space: a domestic interior managed by women but populated by both sexes, where the rituals of shared meals enact the daily negotiation of gender relations.

## Masculine Confidence

Richardson renders masculine confidence as a physical quality — the ease with which men occupy rooms, the authority of their opinions, the unselfconsciousness of their bodies. The "thin hard fingers of women chattering and tweaking" [008-148] are contrasted with the solidity of male presence. Miriam observes this confidence with a mixture of resentment and fascination: it is what she lacks and what she questions, the unearned social capital that gender distributes unevenly.

## Performing Femininity

The visual regime of femininity is rendered with ethnographic precision. Dress, gesture, the management of voice and gaze — these are the techniques by which femininity is performed and policed. Miriam is acutely aware of the expectation to perform a certain version of womanhood and equally aware of her distance from that performance. Her golf-cape "thrown over her arm" becomes a "sort of hallmark" of her difference from conventional femininity — a small rebellion registered in the draping of fabric. Richardson refuses to separate gender from the material conditions in which it is lived: clothing, income, the architecture of rooms, the protocols of social encounter.
