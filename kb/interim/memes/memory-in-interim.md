## Introduction

Memory in *Interim* is not a faculty one exercises but an atmosphere one inhabits. The past does not stay in the past — it "would come again" [001-015], surging into the present unbidden, brought by a scent, a quality of light, a phrase overheard. Miriam Henderson lives simultaneously in multiple temporalities: the immediate present of her London life, the remembered past of childhood, and the anticipated future toward which both reach. Richardson's rendering of memory is one of her most radical formal achievements.

## Memory's Triggers

Memory arrives through the senses — the smell of a doll's house interior, the "sudden light in the frosted globe" [001-015], the sound of paper scraping over a wooden roller in a toy theatre. These sensory triggers are not symbols; they are portals. Through them, "the nearer past, her years of London work set in the air, framed and contemplable like the pictures on the wall, and beside them the early golden years in snatches" [001-015] become present. The past is not remembered — it is re-inhabited.

> Things came nearer and nearer, nearer and nearer. Everything that had ever been and everything that was to come poured through her as she lay, happy and still. [001-012]

This passage from the Christmas night vigil captures the way memory in Richardson's hands dissolves temporal distance. The past arrives not as recollection but as presence — "nearer and nearer" — until the boundary between what was and what is becomes indistinguishable.

## Childhood as Living Past

Childhood memories occupy a privileged place in the novel's architecture of recollection. The Noah's ark, the doll's house, the kaleidoscope — these objects from Miriam's early years surface at the Brooms' fireside with extraordinary vividness.

> I can smell the smell of the inside at this moment. [001-011]

The present tense of memory makes the past more real than the room in which Miriam sits telling the story. The dolls — Dutch dolls with wooden bodies, composition dolls with "soft bluey grey" eyes that have "an *expression*; looking at something, looking at the same thing you looked at yourself" [001-011] — are not nostalgia-objects but presences. Their eyes meet Miriam's across decades, and the look is mutual.

## Memory and Identity

The novel's treatment of memory suggests that identity is cumulative. Miriam is not the person she was as a child, but neither has she left that person behind.

| Temporal Layer | Content | Narrative Function |
|---------------|---------|-------------------|
| Childhood | Toys, family home, "Little Miriam" identity | Foundation of self; sensory richness |
| Recent past | London work years, boarding-house life | "Framed and contemplable" — available for reflection |
| Immediate present | The Brooms' Christmas visit | The surface on which memory breaks through |
| Anticipated future | "Scenes from the future… moving in boundless backgrounds" | Memory enables projection; past patterns shape future vision |

"The early golden years in snatches, chosen pictures from here and there" [001-015] coexist with her present self. Memory makes the self porous to its own history. This is not the Proustian recovery of lost time but something closer to a continuous present in which past and present are layered rather than sequential.

## The Past as Future

Memory in *Interim* also points forward. "Scenes from the future, moving in boundless backgrounds came streaming unsummoned into her mind, making her surroundings suddenly unfamiliar… the past would come again." [001-015] Past and future are not opposites but phases of the same movement. The novel's final implication is that memory — the capacity to hold the past within the present — is what makes it possible to imagine a future.
