## Overview

Pages — of magazines, notebooks, letters, and books — recur throughout *Interim* as sites where Miriam Henderson's consciousness meets the written word. The physical act of turning a page is often charged with emotion: anxiety, anticipation, or revelation. The page is both a material object and a metaphor for the movement of thought.

## Manifestations

Pages appear in moments of reading and waiting. Miriam sits "anxiously deep in the pages of the magazine" [001-014] in a domestic scene, and later begins "wretchedly turning the pages with her gloved hands" [002-006], the physical gesture conveying emotional discomfort. A decisive moment occurs when, "tearing off the page she laid it on the sofa-head" [003-011], she externalises thought onto paper.

The page also figures as a surface for discovery: "on some turned page something would shine out and trouble the darkness" [006-016]. In a notebook, "she turned the pages of her notebook and came upon a passage" [006-019], linking the page to memory and self-reflection. The sensory quality of pages varies: the "thin bible page" [007-001] admits light, while elsewhere a page comes alive "like a woven carpet" [008-020].

## Key Passages

- [008-020] "It made the page alive … a woven carpet, on one side a pattern, on the other a blank" — the page as metaphor for consciousness.
- [006-016] "On some turned page something would shine out" — the page as site of revelation.
- [001-014] Miriam "anxiously deep in the pages of the magazine" — pages and emotional state.
