# Euston Road

Euston Road is one of the London streets most vividly rendered in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. A major east-west artery running through north-central London, it appears in the novel as a site of urban wandering — a hot, narrow channel through which Miriam Henderson moves, observes, and registers the sensory texture of the modern city.

## Overview

The Euston Road of *Interim* is a place of transit and threshold. Miriam walks along it, waits at its edges, crosses its pavements. It is not a destination but a corridor — the connective tissue between the boarding-house at Banbury Park, the railway stations, and the other scattered points of her London geography.

> The Euston Road was a narrow hot channel of narrow hot sunlight… [008-009]

The repetition of "narrow hot" compresses the street into a sensory intensity: heat, constriction, the density of urban experience. The Euston Road is not a picturesque London landmark; it is the working city, the thoroughfare of potato-men and ironmongers and June dust.

## In the Novel

### Wandering and Waiting

Miriam's relationship to the Euston Road is that of a walker — someone who traverses rather than inhabits, who uses the street to think and to feel.

> She wandered along the Euston Road hoping to meet a potato-man. [004-012]

The "potato-man" — a street vendor, a figure of the urban working class — grounds the Euston Road in the everyday economy of the city. Miriam's wandering is not aimless tourism; it is the movement of someone who lives in London, who knows its streets and their inhabitants, whose walks are threaded with small practical hopes.

> …when they turned into the Euston Road and ironically watched the meagre… [005-005]

The adverb "ironically" marks the Euston Road as a site of critical observation. Miriam and her companion do not simply see the street; they interpret it, read its meagreness, bring to it a consciousness that transforms ordinary urban spectacle into material for reflection.

### Heat and Dust

The Euston Road of Richardson's rendering is inseparable from its weather. It appears in summer, in heat, in the particular oppressiveness of London at midday.

> Out in the Euston Road along the pavements shadowed… [008-022]

The pavements "shadowed" — the sense of partial relief, of light and dark alternating along the walking path — capture the street's physical texture. Richardson's method is precise: she registers the quality of light, the temperature, the feel of the pavement underfoot.

> …the June dust and heat of the Euston Road had made her forget it. [008-245]

The street has the power to absorb consciousness — the "dust and heat" are not merely background but active forces, capable of "making" Miriam forget. The Euston Road is a character in the novel's sensory drama, as present and effective as any human figure.

## Significance

Euston Road exemplifies Richardson's treatment of urban space. It is not symbolic — it does not "represent" anything beyond itself. It is real, specific, sensory: hot, dusty, narrow, alive with the movements of working people. In a novel built around Miriam Henderson's consciousness, streets like the Euston Road are the ground against which interior experience occurs. To walk along it is to be in London, fully and irreducibly.
