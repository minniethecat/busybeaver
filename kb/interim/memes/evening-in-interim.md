# Evening in *Interim*

Evening is the dominant temporal setting of Dorothy Richardson's novel *Interim*, structuring its social encounters, its characters' introspection, and its representation of everyday domestic life. More than a mere backdrop, evening functions as a narrative principle — the threshold between day and night mirrors the novel's own position between realist chronicle and modernist interiority. From the dinner gatherings at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] to the solitary walks through London streets, evening is when the novel's characters come together, speak their minds, and confront their solitude.

## The Rhythm and Ritual of Evening

### Temporal Rhythm of the Novel

The narrative of *Interim* follows a diurnal rhythm in which evening consistently provides the stage for the novel's most significant encounters. The working day — with its professional obligations, its errands, its dispersed activities — recedes, and the characters converge around dinner tables, in drawing rooms, and on evening walks.

- **Evening gatherings** structure the social world of [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]], where boarders assemble for dinner and after-dinner conversation
- **Evening walks** provide Miriam with moments of solitary reflection, often through the streets near [[st-pancras|St. Pancras]] and [[wimpole-street|Wimpole Street]]
- **Evening music** occasions the piano performances that bring the household together around shared aesthetic experience, as explored in [[music-in-interim|Music in *Interim*]]

### Evening and Social Ritual

Evening in *Interim* is indistinguishable from the rituals that fill it. The evening meal, the after-dinner conversation, the piano performance, the final dispersal to bedrooms — these repeated forms give the novel its structural backbone.

| Evening Ritual | Typical Setting | Social Function |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Afternoon tea | Drawing room | Transition from day to evening; informal gathering |
| Dinner | Dining room | Formal assembly of boarders; conversation and debate |
| Music | Drawing room | Shared aesthetic experience; emotional release |
| Evening walk | London streets | Solitary reflection; observation of city life |
| Late-night conversation | Bedroom, hallway | Intimate exchange; emotional disclosure |

## Evening and Interiority

Her walk through the London evening becomes a journey into consciousness itself, the darkening streets a metaphor for the descent into the self. The "map of London at night" she consults is as much psychological as geographical [005-005].

## Evening Moments

### St. Pancras Clock

The [[st-pancras|St. Pancras]] clock serves as the novel's timekeeper, its strikes punctuating the evening hours. Miriam listens to it from her window, its chimes measuring the passage of time with a regularity that contrasts with the fluid, associative movement of her thoughts.

> She stood listening to St. Pancras clock striking midnight. [004-013]

The clock's midnight strike marks the end of evening and the threshold of a new day — a temporal boundary that the novel repeatedly approaches but never quite crosses into morning.

### Key Passages

The novel's evening scenes combine social observation with philosophical reflection, the outer world of manners and conversation interwoven with Miriam's inner stream of consciousness.

> She had spent New Year's Eve alone in a cold bedroom. Why had she not gone out into the streets? [002-002]

New Year's Eve — the evening of evenings — becomes a moment of existential solitude. Miriam's question to herself captures the novel's preoccupation with the gap between social expectation and individual experience.

> That evening both Dr. Heber and Dr. Hurd appeared at dinner. [006-019]

Dinner at Banbury Park is the novel's primary social theater. The arrival of the two doctors — [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]] and Dr. Hurd — transforms an ordinary evening meal into an occasion charged with intellectual and romantic possibility.

> Even with Bohemians evenings did not begin until late. [005-005]

This aside reveals the narrator's awareness of alternative temporalities — the "Bohemian" evenings that operate on a different clock from the respectable routines of the boarding house.

## Significance

Evening in *Interim* is more than setting — it is a mode of attention. The fading light, the cessation of daytime business, the gathering of the household: these conditions create the distinctive narrative texture of the novel. Richardson's use of evening aligns her with a broader modernist preoccupation with twilight consciousness — the half-light in which the boundaries between self and world, past and present, become permeable.

Unlike the morning, which demands action, or the night, which brings sleep, evening is the time of reflection — and *Interim* is, above all, a novel of reflection. That it takes place so consistently in the evening hours is no accident: evening is the temporal equivalent of the novel's own epistemological stance, suspended between the known and the unknown.
