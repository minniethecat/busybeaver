# Circle in _Interim_

The **circle** in _Interim_ is a figure of enclosure — sometimes protective, sometimes confining. Richardson's narrator draws circles around families, sits within circles of light, and observes the social circles from which she feels excluded. The circle is the shape of belonging and its opposite: the boundary that holds some in and keeps others out.

## Thematic Dimensions

### Circle as Social Boundary

The circle appears first as a figure of inclusion — and implicit exclusion. "Her mind drew a little circle round the family group" [001-017] images the family as a bounded unit, the circle's edge marking where belonging ends. The narrator is outside, drawing the circle she observes. Similarly, "the little circle where she sat enclosed" [002-009] places the sitter within the circle but emphasises its smallness — the circle encloses but also limits.

### Circle as Space and Architecture

The novel extends the circle into physical space. "An open space of light, set in a circle of balconied gloom" [006-027] renders architecture as geometry — light enclosed by darkness, the circle as a spatial form that organises perception. "Surrounded by a distant circle of palms" [006-028] makes the circle decorative and distant, a frame that sets the scene without touching it.

### Circle as Social World

Richardson also registers the circle as a metaphor for social worlds. "Only lately joined musical circles" [005-004] uses "circles" in its familiar social sense — a group defined by shared interest. But the narrator is sceptical: "All 'circles' must be like that in some way" [005-005] generalises the observation, making all social circles variations on the same pattern of inclusion and performance.

### Circle and the Body

The circle also appears at the scale of the body. "That would follow Florrie's encirclement of her waist" [001-014] makes the circle an embrace, a physical encircling. The body becomes a circle-maker, drawing boundaries through touch.

## Key Passages

- **[001-017]** "Her mind drew a little circle round the family group" — The circle as mental boundary, enclosing the family unit.
- **[002-009]** "The little circle where she sat enclosed" — Sitting within a bounded social space.
- **[006-027]** "An open space of light, set in a circle of balconied gloom" — Architectural circle as spatial organisation.
- **[005-004]** "Only lately joined musical circles" — Social circles as exclusive groups.
