# Garden in *Interim*

The garden in *Interim* is nature at a manageable scale. Unlike the sea — vast, uncontained, absent — the garden is present, visible, bounded. It appears through windows, in memories, as a "little square" of green amid the London brick. Richardson's gardens are not pastoral escapes but urban compromises: patches of soil and foliage that soften the city without denying it.

## In the Novel

Gardens appear at the edges of the novel's domestic spaces — seen from windows, walked through in memory, associated with childhood and summer. They are spaces of transition between the full interiority of the house and the full exteriority of the street.

> The little square of summer garden showed brilliantly in the sun. [001-012]

The garden is "little" and "square" — diminutive, geometric — but it "showed brilliantly." The brilliance is real even if the garden is small. Richardson does not need a landscape; a square of garden in sunlight is enough to shift the novel's register from the gaslit interior to the natural world.

## The Garden's Dimensions

| Aspect | Manifestation | Significance |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| Spatial | "Little square," bounded, visible | Nature contained, made domestic |
| Seasonal | Summer garden, spring rain on foliage | The cycle of growth and renewal |
| Mnemonic | Childhood gardens, the "frilled shirts" on the line [001-013] | The garden as a site of memory |
| Social | Garden parties, the "league garden party" [001-017] | The garden as public/private threshold |
| Sensory | Rain on foliage, the scent of leaves | The garden registered through the senses |

> She imagined the snow on the garden where the frilled shirts used to hang. [001-013]

Memory superimposes weather: the present snow on the remembered garden, the "frilled shirts" of a lost domestic routine. The garden is a palimpsest — the present season written over past ones, the current self looking back at an earlier one.

## Key Passages

> Blackness in large gardens and over the bridge. [001-012]

The "large gardens" of London — public squares, private enclosures — are zones of "blackness" at night. The garden after dark is not the bright square of summer but an absence, a void between streetlamps. Richardson registers the garden's nocturnal transformation as part of the city's larger rhythm of light and dark.

> The rush of spring rain on the garden foliage, and presently a voice from within the house. [001-015]

Rain on foliage — the garden as sensory surface — is interrupted by a voice from inside. The garden exists in counterpoint to the house: the natural sound of rain against the human sound of speech. Miriam hears both, suspended between them.

> She saw it as their own secret garden, part of their secret life together. [001-015]

The garden as "secret" space — not literally hidden but privately claimed, a shared imaginary territory. The phrase "secret garden" carries the weight of the literary tradition (the enclosed garden as sanctuary) but Richardson treats it lightly, as a passing figure of speech that reveals the emotional claim underneath.

## Significance

The garden in *Interim* is Richardson's most concentrated image of nature made compatible with domestic life. It is not wilderness — it is bounded, tended, urban — but it allows into the novel something the boarding-house interiors cannot: the presence of growth, of weather, of seasonal change. The garden is where the natural cycle registers within the urban domestic world.

This is consistent with Richardson's method throughout: she does not seek the sublime in nature but the sensory — the feel of rain, the gleam of sunlight on leaves, the blackness of foliage at night. The garden is enough.
