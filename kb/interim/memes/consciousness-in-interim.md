# Consciousness in *Interim*

Consciousness is the fundamental medium of *Interim*. Dorothy Richardson's novel does not have a plot in the conventional sense — it has a mind. Every event, every character, every room and street and quality of light reaches the reader through the streaming awareness of [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]]. Consciousness is not merely what the novel is about; it is what the novel *is*. The prose follows the movements of a single perceiving mind, registering its shifts of attention, its sudden associations, its withdrawals and returns.

## In the Novel

Miriam's consciousness is restless, mobile, and rarely fixed on any single object for long. At the Brooms' dinner table, she listens to "the tones, glancing intelligence accordingly, umpiring the contest, her mind wandering blissfully about" [001-013]. Social participation and inner drift happen simultaneously — the mind attending to the room while also departing from it. This simultaneity is the novel's signature effect.

The novel traces consciousness across its full spectrum: from sharp social attention to the threshold of sleep. Miriam slips "back into unconsciousness" [001-012] after the midnight waits, then surfaces into the clarity of morning. Consciousness moves between the "flood of images" that comes "fresh and new, seen for the first time" [001-011] at the fireside, and the blank weight of a body that becomes "heavy on her chair, ill-clothed" [001-017], the mind suddenly emptied of its contents.

> Topics racing through her mind in a voice thrilling with stored up laughter. [001-015]

This passage catches the characteristic velocity of Miriam's consciousness — thoughts racing, a voice within that thrills with energy not yet released. The mind is not a container but a current.

> Her mind drew a little circle round the family group. It spun round them on and on as they went through life. [001-017]

Here consciousness operates as an act of imaginative framing — drawing circles, spinning them, making sense of social data through a kind of intellectual motion. Consciousness in Richardson's hands is not passive reception but an active, shaping force.

## Consciousness and Social Life

| Aspect of Consciousness | Social Context | Narrative Effect |
|------------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Wandering attention | Dinner-table conversation | Double registration: outer talk and inner drift |
| Painful self-awareness | Being questioned about loyalties | "desperation to her painful consciousness that nothing she saw and felt was visible" |
| Withdrawal into silence | Social gatherings | Consciousness as refuge from social demand |
| Imaginative framing | Observing others | Mind drawing circles, organizing social data |
| Blankness and heaviness | End of an evening | Consciousness giving way to bodily fatigue |

The tension between inner and outer — between what consciousness registers and what social life permits to be expressed — is one of the novel's persistent dramas. Miriam feels "desperation to her painful consciousness that nothing she saw and felt was visible to the three pairs of differently watchful eyes" [001-013]. Consciousness isolates her even as it enriches her. The inner wealth cannot be communicated; the social surface cannot be avoided.

This isolation is intensified by Miriam's awareness of the *unconsciousness* of others. The well-to-do family Grace describes possess a quality of "unconsciousness, their lives as they looked to themselves" [001-017] — an unreflective self-satisfaction that Miriam's own consciousness can never achieve. She sees them as "unconscious unbroken and maddeningly unquestioning and unaware" [001-017], their social ease purchased at the cost of genuine perception.

## Key Passages

> Everything that passed into her mind out and out towards a centre. [006-017]

Richardson describes consciousness as centripetal — all experience is drawn inward toward a centre of awareness. This is the novel's deep structure: nothing exists in *Interim* until it has been pulled through Miriam's consciousness and organized around its core.

> She had heard nothing of the various conversations sprouting easily all round the table. [006-019]

Consciousness is selective. It hears what it attends to and discards the rest. Miriam's failure to hear the conversations around her is not a failure of perception but its necessary condition — to attend to everything would be to attend to nothing.

> She felt the work was unreal, as easy as a dream. [008-002]

The boundary between waking consciousness and dream is porous. Work can feel "as easy as a dream" when consciousness flows without friction. Richardson treats dream-states not as the opposite of consciousness but as another of its registers — a point where deliberate attention relaxes into something more fluid.

## Understanding Richardson's Rendering of Consciousness

What distinguishes Richardson's treatment of consciousness from the psychological novel that preceded her is its refusal to summarize. A nineteenth-century novelist might describe a character as "lost in thought" or "deeply moved." Richardson instead renders the texture of thought itself — its rhythms, its swerves, its sudden illuminations and sudden blanknesses.

When Miriam's "mind became a blank. Her body was heavy on her chair, ill-clothed" [001-017], Richardson records the disappearance of mental content with the same precision she brings to its presence. Consciousness is not a steady state; it fluctuates. It can race with "topics" one moment and empty out the next. This fidelity to the actual texture of awareness — its intermittency, its variability — is what makes Richardson's method feel modern even now.

The technique has been called "stream of consciousness," but the metaphor is somewhat misleading. A stream flows continuously in one direction. Miriam's consciousness is more like weather — changing light, sudden gusts of memory, patches of stillness. "Things were coming to her out of the fire, fresh and new, seen for the first time; a flood of images" [001-011]. The images arrive unbidden, called up not by will but by the quality of attention that firelight induces. Consciousness in Richardson's hands is receptive rather than directive — it is what happens when the mind stops trying and begins to see.

This has implications for how the novel asks to be read. A reader seeking plot, event, dramatic confrontation will find little purchase. The novel's drama is entirely internal — the movement of one mind through the textures of a particular Christmas visit, registering everything (the quality of gaslight, the sound of sausages mentioned at table, the way a servant's hands move) and organizing it into the shape of a life.

## Significance

Richardson's rendering of consciousness in *Interim* represents a decisive moment in the history of the novel. By making consciousness not the subject matter but the medium — by insisting that everything passes through a single perceiving mind before it reaches the page — she shifted the novel's centre of gravity from external event to internal experience. This shift would prove enormously influential, shaping the work of Virginia Woolf, James Joyce, and the modernist tradition that followed.

But *Interim* does not treat consciousness as a technique alone. The novel's deeper subject is what it costs to be so aware — the strain of consciousness unrelieved by the unreflective ease that Miriam envies in others. Consciousness in *Interim* is both gift and burden: the source of the novel's extraordinary perceptual richness, and the source of its protagonist's isolation. To see everything is to be separate from those who see less.
