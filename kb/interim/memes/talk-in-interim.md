# Talk in _Interim_

**Talk** fills _Interim_ — at dinner tables, on doorsteps, in drawing rooms, across the arena of a concert hall. Richardson's narrator is a connoisseur of talk, alive to its textures, its failures, and the social worlds it builds and demolishes. "Talking as people talk" (001-016) is both description and critique: talk is what people do, but it is also a performance that can be done badly or well.

## Talk and Social Life

### Talk as Social Fabric

The novel registers talk as the medium through which social life happens. "The Baileys laughing and talking on their doorstep" (002-003) — talk here is the sound of belonging, the audible sign of a household at ease. "Long talks on the seat at the end" (001-015) images talk as leisure, the extended conversation that fills an afternoon. And "talking derisively across at Mr. M" (004-003) shows talk as social weapon, the barb disguised as banter.

> …the Baileys laughing and talking on their doorstep. A smooth…
> (002-003)

Talk also serves as the sign of social inclusion — or its absence. "Presently they would all be talking and would have forgotten her" (006-001) captures the fear of being left out of the conversational circle, of talk continuing without you. The Baileys' gatherings are thick with talk: "sincere in their laughter and talk. They all had some sort of" (006-011) — sincerity in talk is a quality the narrator registers carefully, distinguishing performed warmth from the real thing.

### The Unevenness of Talk

Richardson is acutely aware that not everyone talks from the same position. The narrator wishes proximity to fluent speakers "might make her able to talk like they did, but never to t" (004-009) — the but marking an insurmountable distance between her own speech and theirs. The question of who controls talk arises forcefully: "Could not Mrs. Bailey make him go on talking? A cold gloom spread" (003-009) registers both the desire for talk to continue and the powerlessness of being unable to sustain it. Talk in _Interim_ is never democratic — some voices fill rooms, others are barely audible. The man whose talk dominates dinner commentary speaks "through his talk he smiled a quizzical pleased" (004-013), the pleasure of the talker visible in the act, indifferent to his listeners' needs.

This asymmetry is rendered most vividly in the image of the "eagerly talking woman" — "the onslaught of the eagerly talking woman. The woman was in the" (005-004) — where talk becomes assault, the speaker's eagerness experienced by the listener as something to be endured rather than shared.

### Talk About Talk

Richardson's characters are themselves theorists of talk. "People talked about these things" (004-006) observes the meta-discourse — talk that takes itself as its subject. "I never understand all that talk about the affection for rag dolls" (001-011) dismisses a whole category of sentimental discourse. And "those people were right who talked about Anglo-Saxon supremacy" (003-011) shows talk hardening into ideology, the casual conversation that carries political freight.

The novel also registers talk about books and art as a distinct social register: "people who talked about the book probably understood" (006-016) — literary talk as a marker of cultural competence, a way of signalling membership in a certain kind of intellectual community. And "people talked about cosmopolis" (003-016) captures the aspirational quality of certain kinds of talk — the desire to sound worldly, to gesture toward a larger stage.

## The Limits of Talk

### Talk as Inadequate

Throughout the novel, talk repeatedly fails its speakers. "Talking, explaining things, making life stop, while reality went on" (002-002) is the harshest verdict: talk arrests experience, replacing the flow of living with the sediment of description. Miriam "reawakened the laughter and talk, but" (001-013) — the "but" hangs there, the limit of talk even when it succeeds. For a novel so saturated with speech, _Interim_ is profoundly sceptical about whether talk can ever say what needs saying.

The inadequacy of talk is also registered through its physical aftermath. After conversation, "a cold gloom spread" (003-009) — the silence after talk feels colder than the silence before it, because talk has raised expectations it cannot fulfil. The desire to sustain conversation — "Could not Mrs. Bailey make him go on talking?" (003-009) — reveals the anxiety of conversational breakdown, the fear of returning to silence.

### Talk and Silence

The novel is equally attentive to what talk displaces. "Why did he talk like that?" (001-010) is the narrator's puzzled response to a manner of speech that feels foreign. "She imagined them moving and talking in all manner of circumstances" (004-012) reveals talk as the stuff of social imagination — the narrator mentally rehearsing conversations, hearing voices before they speak. "Wait till we can talk she sighed" (001-014) suggests that real talk requires conditions — privacy, leisure, the right moment — that are not always available.

## The Varieties of Talk

### Talk in Motion

Talk in _Interim_ is not confined to interiors. One of the novel's most characteristic images is of conversation continuing through the street: "Still talking, Mr. Bowdoin went up the r" (005-001) — talk as accompaniment to walking, the sentence trailing off as the speaker moves out of earshot. The domestic scene of "dresses they had worn at the dance talking to their mother indoors" (001-017) places talk within family ritual, the recounting of the evening's events.

The novel also gives us talk in transit: "Walking out to Highgate, 'talking all the time'" (005-005) — talk sustained across miles, the rhythm of walking and the rhythm of speech merged. And talk at the concert: "people who stand in the arena and talk about the music and know that" (004-001) — talk about art as social positioning, the conversation that uses culture as currency. In each case, Richardson embeds talk in physical situation — talk is never pure discourse but always located, always carried by bodies in specific places.

### Talk and Excitation

Richardson attends closely to the emotional states that accompany speech — or precede it. The narrator registers her own "outbreak of excited talk and loud laughter" (005-002) with retrospective unease, the excessive talk that reveals more than it intends. "She had talked excitedly and scrappily" (006-002) — the self-assessment after the fact, talk that felt like communication at the time but in memory reads as incoherence. These moments of excited speech are the novel's way of marking the gap between what talk achieves in the moment and what it leaves behind in recollection.
