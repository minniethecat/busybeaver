# Face in *Interim*

Miriam Henderson is a reader of faces. Throughout *Interim*, she registers the expressions of those around her — the "heavy sallow sullen face" of a servant [001-011], the "intelligent face" of a companion [001-013], the "angry handsome face" of a waiter [004-012] — with an attention that is at once social and phenomenological. Faces in Richardson's novel are neither transparent windows onto character nor opaque masks, but something more complex: surfaces shaped by feeling, class, and the pressure of observation.

## In the Novel

The novel's faces are rarely described in full. Richardson tends to register a single quality — sallowness, squareness, pallor — and let it stand for the whole. This is consistent with her narrative method: consciousness perceives selectively, seizing on the detail that matters and leaving the rest in shadow.

> Miriam ate rapidly glancing from face to face. [001-010]

The rapid glance "from face to face" — a survey of the dinner table — captures Miriam's characteristic mode of social perception: watchful, scanning, assembling the room from its faces. She does not participate in conversation so much as read it from the faces of those speaking.

> Something in Mrs. Bailey's face when she said I was wondering. [002-007]

The sentence trails off — Richardson's characteristic ellipsis — because what matters is not the completed thought but the face that accompanied it. "Something in Mrs. Bailey's face" is the real communication; the words are merely its vehicle. Miriam reads the face before and beneath the speech.

## Key Passages

> Her face felt drawn and weariness was upon her. [001-015]

Miriam's face is known to her not by sight but by sensation — it "felt drawn," the tightness of fatigue registered from within. This is Richardson's phenomenological method applied to the self: one's own face is not an image but a feeling, a zone of bodily awareness.

> The Bailey girls were watching Miriam's face. [003-002]

The gaze is reciprocal. While Miriam reads the faces of others, she is herself being read. The sentence's simplicity — subject, verb, object — enacts the directness of the scrutiny. To be watched is to become a face, a surface offered to interpretation.

> Dark forms and phantom white faces of waiting passengers. [004-008]

At the railway station, faces become "phantom white" — bleached by platform lighting, disembodied by darkness. The face here is the only visible part of the person, a floating oval of identity in the surrounding dark. It is Richardson's most expressionist moment: faces as the last residue of the human in the impersonal space of transit.

> His face seemed to be laughing ironically at something he was saying. [004-003]

The face "seemed" — the qualifier is everything. Miriam interprets the expression but does not claim certainty. Richardson's faces are always mediated by the perceiving consciousness; we see not the face itself but Miriam's seeing of it.

## Significance

Faces in *Interim* are where the novel's social and phenomenological interests converge. To read a face is both a social act — interpreting another person's feeling and intention — and a perceptual one — registering light, shape, and movement. Richardson treats the face not as a symbol of identity but as a sensory surface, as real and as partial as a curtain or a door. Miriam's constant glancing from face to face is not idle observation but a form of knowing — the face as the closest available approach to another consciousness.
