# Farringdon Road

Farringdon Road appears in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* as the setting for a walking conversation — one of those scenes of pedestrian talk that Richardson uses to braid together interior consciousness, social interaction, and the physical environment of London. It is a street of the city's working edge, where the built environment and the social world press upon one another.

## Overview

Farringdon Road runs north from the City of London through Clerkenwell, an area associated in the late Victorian period with commerce, printing, and the working classes. In *Interim*, it appears as a route walked in company — a space of conversation conducted in motion, where the rhythm of walking shapes the rhythm of talk.

> …conversation walking along the Farringdon Road. It was strange enough to know… [005-001]

The syntax — "conversation walking" — fuses talk and movement into a single activity. The walking is not incidental to the conversation; the conversation is not a pause from the walking. They are one thing: the Farringdon Road becomes the medium through which social exchange occurs.

## In the Novel

### A Worldly Street

Farringdon Road is explicitly identified with the world beyond the domestic sphere. It is "in the world" — a phrase that marks the distinction between the protected interior of the boarding-house and the broader social reality of the city.

> …had said that Farringdon Road was in the world and that he… [005-002]

To be "in the world" is to be exposed to class difference, to commerce, to the rough texture of urban life. Farringdon Road, in this formulation, is not a neutral thoroughfare but a site of social encounter — a place where the sheltered consciousness of the middle-class boarder meets the harder reality of the working city.

### Walking in Company

The walking conversation on Farringdon Road involves three figures — Miriam, Mr. Bowdoin, and another companion — a triangulation of talk that mirrors the dinner-table groupings at Banbury Park.

> …found herself walking along the Farringdon Road between Mr. Bowdoin and the s… [005-005]

The preposition "between" is spatially and socially precise. Miriam is flanked, caught in the middle of a conversational pairing that she may or may not have chosen. The Farringdon Road walking scene reproduces, in outdoor space, the dynamics of the indoor dinner table: proximity, arrangement, the politics of who walks beside whom.

## Significance

Farringdon Road is a minor location in the novel, but its narrative function is precise. It is where the social world of the boarding-house extends into the street — where the indoor dynamics of talk and position are transposed onto the urban pavement. In Richardson's London, streets are not inert; they are stages, charged with the social meanings that walkers bring to them.
