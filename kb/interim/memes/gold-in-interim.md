## Introduction

Gold in *Interim* is the counterpoint to blackness — the colour of gaslight, of jewellery, of certain charged moments when the ordinary world becomes luminous. It appears in watch-chains and gold bars, in the rose-gold glow of a fire, in the golden light of a warmly lit room, and in the vast gold-lit sky that opens above twilight streets. Gold is the visual signature of what Miriam reaches toward: warmth, beauty, a life that gleams.

## Light and Space

Golden light transforms the spaces it touches. "The room was full of life and warmth and golden light" [005-003], the three qualities fused into a single experience. "The room beamed softly in its golden light. From the heart of the golden light Mrs. Bailey was hurrying towards her with good tidings" [006-011] — light becomes a medium of communication, carrying warmth before words arrive. In the evening, "a great gold pink glow stood above and showed through the feathery upper branches of the trees in Endsleigh Gardens" [008-014], the gold-rose sky opening a vista of "green peace" [008-014]. The sky turns "rosy gold" that deepens and spreads [008-016], then fades into "a soft mistiness spreading through the deepening rose" [008-019]. The sequence is a slow unfolding — gold as an event, something that happens.

## Adornment and Value

Gold objects punctuate the novel's social world. Miriam's vision of a cultivated past includes "the delicate old gold chain, the little pearled cross, the old fine delicate rings" [001-017] — gold jewellery as inheritance, carrying "centuries of shadowy ecclesiasticism" [001-017]. The young Norwegian gentleman at the lecture wears "a narrow gold bar set aslant in the soft mass of black silk tie" [002-006], the gold a small bright signal against the dark formality. A gold watch-chain "gleams" against crimson cloth [003-002], the flash of gold marking masculine presence and prosperity.

## Firelight and the City

The most powerful gold in *Interim* comes from fire and gaslight. The fire's "wide clear core striped by black bars" sends "a broad rose-gold shaft" glowing "out across the room" [004-007], the rose-gold light touching copper vessels and mirrors, "bringing a sense of the freedom of wealth to have things out of season" [004-007]. In the city streets, "golden gaslight" pours "over the white tablecloth" [006-001], and Miriam walks through "the mingled darkness and gold of the coming streets" [006-025]. At the novel's close, "heavy on the golden balcony" [008-147], she faces "the rose-gold world, blossoming under her eyes" [008-147] — the golden light become a weight she leans into, a world she can just bear to face.
