# Bernard Mendizabal

Bernard Mendizabal is among the most cosmopolitan figures in *Interim*. His name — Spanish, European, un-English — marks him as an outsider in the Banbury Park boarding-house, and his intellectual interests (philosophy, music, cosmopolitanism itself) set him apart from the novel's more parochial characters.

## Character Overview

Mendizabal is a talker — one of the figures who animates the drawing-room conversations that structure the novel's evenings. He brings [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]] into the household, plays music, and engages in the kind of speculative conversation that Miriam Henderson finds alternately stimulating and exhausting.

> Sitting down almost the moment Mr. Mendizabal brought him into the room and playing Wagner. With many wrong notes. [004-001]

This is Mendizabal's entrance — bringing a guest, initiating music, setting a cultural tone. The "many wrong notes" are characteristic of Richardson's deflationary touch: the cosmopolitan intellectual's musical offering is imperfect, human, slightly absurd. But the impulse — to bring music into the room, to create a cultural occasion — is genuine.

## In the Novel

Mendizabal functions as a conductor of intellectual energy. His conversations range across philosophy, art, and the nature of civilisation, and his presence raises the temperature of the drawing-room talk. He is associated with Wagner, with European culture, and with the idea — which he both embodies and articulates — of a cosmopolitan world beyond national boundaries.

> There is no Cosmopolis. Bernard Mendizabal. [003-013]

The statement is striking: "There is no Cosmopolis" — no world-city, no universal society — and it is attributed to Mendizabal by name. The full stop after "Cosmopolis" and the separate sentence "Bernard Mendizabal" give his name the weight of an aphorism's author. He is the character who can say such things, who carries the intellectual authority to pronounce on the world's organisation.

## Key Relationships

### Dr. von Heber

Mendizabal brings von Heber to Banbury Park and shares with him a European cultural background. The two men form an intellectual alliance within the household — two foreigners, two cosmopolitans, two figures who stand slightly apart from the English domestic world.

### Mr. Bowdoin

[[bowdoin|Mr. Bowdoin]] is part of the same intellectual circle, and the two men appear together in several scenes. Their conversations — about art, about ideas, about the world beyond the boarding-house — constitute the novel's most sustained intellectual exchanges.

### Miriam Henderson

Miriam observes Mendizabal with her characteristic mixture of interest and reserve. His European sophistication is both attractive and alienating; his talk both stimulates and exhausts. He represents a kind of intellectual life that Miriam is drawn to but cannot fully inhabit.

## Notable Passages

> "No miss!" shouted Mr. Mendizabal. She cast a fierce general scowl around the room. [003-015]

The shouted "No miss!" and the "fierce general scowl" reveal another side of Mendizabal — not the cosmopolitan intellectual but the irritable boarder, capable of sudden vehemence. Richardson never lets a character settle into a single register; Mendizabal is both sage and scowler.

## Significance

Mendizabal represents the intellectual current in the boarding-house world. Without him — and without the conversations he initiates — the novel's social texture would be thinner, its intellectual range narrower. He brings Wagner, philosophy, and the idea of Cosmopolis into the English drawing-room, and the novel is richer for his presence.
