## Introduction

Power in *Interim* operates on multiple registers: the physical power of movement, the social power of status and personality, and the inward power — or powerlessness — of consciousness itself. Characters move "powerfully about the room" [002-002] or sit drained of "resisting power" [002-006]; facts and memories "tug at the jungle of feelings that had the power to lead one back" [001-016]. Power is both an outward force and an inward constraint, something Miriam observes in others and struggles to locate in herself.

## Physical Power and Movement

The body's power is felt in motion. Miriam imagines moving like the wind, "a steady human southwest wind" [002-002], the rhythm of movement becoming a source of strength. She steps "lightly and powerfully about the room" [002-002], the two adverbs — lightness and power — held in balance. Power in the body is not aggression but capability, the freedom to move through space without hesitation.

## The Power of Personality

Mr. Bowdoin radiates a quiet authority: "In the strength and sunshine of him there is power" [007-007]. His power is not in what he says but in what he has done — "the things he has done are the power in him; no need to know the gossipy details" [007-007]. This is power as accumulated presence, the weight of a life. Mrs. Bailey's power, by contrast, is social — the power to orchestrate the room, to arrange people and conversation.

## Powerlessness

Powerlessness haunts Miriam's inner life. Her lips are "powerless" [001-014] to speak in a charged moment; she feels "no energy no initiative no hopefulness no resisting power" [002-006]. Yet powerlessness can become a kind of possession: past suffering, though "banished and powerless" [001-015], is something she carries forward. To hate the past would be "to allow them to spoil her day" — she retains the power of refusal.

## Power and Perception

The most elusive power in the novel is the power of perception itself. Miriam's consciousness registers every flicker of social hierarchy, every subtle shift of authority between speakers. She sees the "same power" that shapes Miss Rogers's great aureole of hair [005-003], the shaping force of identity. To perceive power clearly is itself a form of power — the power of the observer.
