## Introduction

Sound saturates the narrative of *Interim*, filtered through Miriam Henderson's acutely sensitive perception. From the scrape of paper on cardboard to the distant murmur of London streets, the novel registers the acoustic environment with unusual fidelity. Sound operates as a bridge between inner consciousness and the external world, and as a measure of emotional atmosphere. Richardson's treatment of sound anticipates the modernist preoccupation with subjective sensory experience — the world is not what is seen but what is heard, and hearing is never neutral.

## Music and Voice

Music provides a recurring framework for auditory experience. Miriam imagines that "everything in experience sound like music in a dream" [002-003], collapsing the boundary between perception and art. The piano at the Broom's becomes a site where "Wagner writing down the world in sound" [004-002] meets domestic performance. Voices carry as much meaning as music: Mrs. Bailey's "shapeless colourless voice murmuring from the back of her throat" [003-011] reveals character through sound quality rather than content alone.

The distinction between music as art and music as social performance structures the novel's treatment of auditory experience. When Miriam attends a brass band concert at the Albert Hall, the gap between her musical sensibility and the event's reality is registered through sound — the "brass band" that others hear as "a concert" [006-018] becomes an index of class and cultural difference. Music, in *Interim*, is never simply heard; it is filtered through social awareness, memory, and the perpetual motion of consciousness.

## Household Acoustics

The boarding-house interior generates its own soundscape. Miriam hears "the sound of the paper scraping over the cardboard stage" [001-011] during an evening game, a small noise magnified by her attention. Her footsteps "made no sound along the carpeted hall" [004-007], allowing her to move as a silent observer through the house. "Some little sound they could both hear" [001-017] becomes a shared secret, a point of connection between characters otherwise separated by convention.

These small domestic sounds — the scrape, the footfall, the shared whisper — constitute what might be called an acoustic vernacular of the boarding-house. Richardson's attention to them is democratic: no sound is too insignificant for notation. The "scraping of chairs" [008-031] when conversations shift, the "rattle of teacups" that punctuates domestic ritual — each carries information about social relationships and emotional states that visual description alone cannot convey.

## Silence and Its Meanings

Silence in *Interim* is not the absence of sound but a distinct acoustic condition with its own emotional weight. When conversation falters or a room empties, silence descends not as peace but as pressure — a space that must be filled or endured. Miriam's own silence, her habit of listening rather than speaking, becomes a form of social strategy: by not adding to the noise, she gains a position from which to observe it.

The contrast between the "tumult" [001-010] of the boarding-house and the quiet of solitary moments structures the novel's acoustic rhythm. Richardson moves between noise and quiet as she moves between social and private consciousness, the volume of the world rising and falling with Miriam's attention.

## The Street Beyond

Beyond the house, London's acoustic panorama presses in. Miriam registers "the sound of carts and near footfalls" [006-017] as she walks, the city's rhythm threading through her thoughts. "The tide of light and sound raising them into a companionship" [006-025] on Tottenham Court Road fuses sensory experience with social belonging. The "distant murmuring tide of sound passing through her" [005-005] suggests an immersive auditory field that dissolves the boundary between self and city.

The street soundscape differs from the domestic in scale and quality: it is continuous rather than episodic, anonymous rather than personal. Yet Richardson treats it with the same attentive particularity. The "rattle of omnibus wheels," the "cry of a costermonger," the "distant hum of traffic" — these are not background noise but the audible texture of urban modernity, as integral to Miriam's experience as the voices of the drawing-room.

## Sound as Memory and Association

Sound in *Interim* frequently triggers memory and associative thought. A particular voice or musical phrase can carry Miriam backward in time, collapsing the distance between present perception and past experience. This is consistent with Richardson's broader commitment to rendering consciousness as a stream in which sensory stimuli — especially auditory ones — serve as portals between temporal layers. A sound heard in the present is never only present; it resonates with every previous hearing.
