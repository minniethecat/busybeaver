## Introduction

The boarding house is the novel's primary social architecture. At Banbury Park, Mrs. Bailey's establishment houses a shifting population of boarders — young women working in London, transient lodgers, the permanent residents who form the house's emotional core. The boarding house is neither home nor institution but something between: a place where private lives intersect under conditions of enforced proximity, where meals are shared and secrets kept, where the rituals of domestic life are performed by strangers.

## The Architecture of Proximity

The boarding house brings people together who would not otherwise meet. "The Bailey girls were watching Miriam's face" [003-002] — in a house where everyone eats together, everyone is watched. The architecture itself enforces a kind of intimacy: shared staircases, overheard conversations, the constant negotiation of private and common space. Miriam's room — "tidied until nothing was visible but the permanent shining gleaming furniture" [001-015] — is the one space she can withdraw to, but even there the sounds of the house penetrate.

## Meals as Social Theatre

Dinner at the boarding house is the novel's recurring set-piece. Around the table, characters perform themselves: the witty anecdote, the careful question, the strategic silence. Meals are where the house's social order is most visible — who speaks, who listens, who is deferred to. The food itself (sausages, blancmange, bananas) is rendered with the same attention Richardson gives to consciousness, because what people eat and how they eat it tells you everything about who they are.

## The Boarders

The boarding house population forms a microcosm of Edwardian London: young women in offices and shops, older residents who have made the house their permanent home, the transient figures who pass through and are remembered only by a phrase or a gesture. Each boarder brings their own history, their own way of navigating the house's collective life. The Bailey sisters — shy, watchful, "making a bright little world in there by feeling themselves surrounded by people who would be shocked" [003-002] — are the house's emotional centre, but the novel gives each figure their own register.

## Between Home and Institution

The boarding house occupies an ambiguous position in the novel's geography of dwelling. It is not home — Miriam "had no home" — but neither is it the cold institution of a hotel or lodging-house. It provides shelter, company, the daily rituals of meals and fires, without demanding the emotional obligations of family. For the novel's women — working, unmarried, making their way in London — this ambiguity is a form of freedom. The boarding house is where they can live in the interim.
