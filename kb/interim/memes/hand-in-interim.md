## Gesture and Social Meaning

Hands in the novel are rarely idle; their positions and movements convey social relationships and emotional states. When Miriam observes a man sitting "with his elbows on his knees and his hands clasped" [003-002], the posture signals ease and belonging.

The same man later stands "with his hands in his pockets" [003-016], a gesture that Miriam reads as a form of informal masculine confidence. These small physical details accumulate into a language of gesture that Richardson renders with precision.

At moments of emotional intensity, hands become expressive. Grace presses Miriam's hand during a charged conversation [001-017], and Miriam turns from the fire in response — the brief contact carrying a weight of unspoken feeling. The "handmade embroidery" on a bag [001-001] registers the tactile quality of crafted objects, part of the novel's wider attention to texture and materiality.

> …said Grace pressing her hand. Miriam turned from the fire… [001-017]

## Hands and Character

Richardson uses hand descriptions to characterise individuals. Eve's "firm small hands" reaching out to "take hold of London" [004-004] convey determination and vitality. Mrs. Corrie, brushing at Miriam's skirt with her handkerchief [003-003], reveals fussiness and a proprietary concern through manual gesture.

> …stretching out her firm small hands and taking hold of London… [004-004]

The "small rough reddened hand" of a servant [003-009] registers class and labour through physical marks. A hand "superciliously grasping the handle of the large coffeepot" [001-013] encodes social attitude in a domestic gesture.

The piano performances that punctuate the novel are rendered partly through the movement of hands: "his hands were on the piano again" [004-002] and the hand upon "the low velvet back of a chair" [002-008] weave manual gesture into the texture of social scenes.

## Hands and Materiality

Hands in *Interim* mediate between the self and the material world. Turning pages "with her gloved hands" [002-006] suggests a barrier between touch and object. The "heavy softly closing loose-handled door" [002-009] registers manual interaction with domestic architecture.

> …turning the pages with her gloved hands. Here it was… [002-006]

A man drying his hands as he crosses a room [004-006] captures the hand in a moment of mundane transition, while the hand "with a clue in one's hand" [001-016] suggests agency and orientation — the hand as instrument of purposeful action.

## Hands and Interiority

For Miriam herself, hands are objects of self-scrutiny. "Her hands went out … to look at them now" [001-014] describes a moment of self-consciousness, the hands becoming external objects of contemplation. The hands "clasped upon her knees" [001-015] register a posture of patient observation, characteristic of Miriam's role as witness and reflector throughout the novel.

> …her hands went out … to look at them now… [001-014]
