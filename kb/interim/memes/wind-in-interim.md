## Introduction

Wind in *Interim* is an invisible but forceful presence — a carrier of song, a metaphor for liberated motion, and a sensory assault on the body that anchors interior experience to the physical world. Unlike the enclosing domestic spaces of the boarding-house, wind belongs to the outdoors, to movement, and to Miriam's yearning for a life unfettered by social constraint.

## Song and Vitality

Wind enters Miriam's consciousness as music. "Life danced and sang within her; shreds of song; the sense of the singing of the wind; clear bright light streaming through large houses" [001-017] — the singing wind is inseparable from the vitality that surges through her. It carries light and motion together, dissolving walls and opening interior spaces to brightness. The association is liberatory: "One would move like the wind always, a steady human southwest wind, alive, without personality or speech" [002-002]. To move like the wind is to escape the burden of self-presentation — to exist purely as force and direction, unselfconscious and free.

## Bleak Encounter

Wind is not always singing. "Turning northwards toward the Marylebone Road she met a bleak wind and turned back" [002-005] — the wind here is an antagonist, an obstacle that redirects her path. "The icy wind drove against her all the way" [002-005], the physical assault matching the harshness of the January darkness that surrounds her. This wind offers no transcendence, only the bracing reality of a cold London winter. The contrast between the singing wind of inner vitality and the bleak wind of Marylebone Road maps the distance between aspiration and circumstance.

## Motion and Freedom

Wind in *Interim* is consistently linked to movement. Unlike the static interiors of Tansley Street — the furniture that "seemed to be waiting" [002-008] — wind is pure passage. Its freedom is impersonal: "without personality or speech" [002-002], it escapes the social performances that constrain the novel's characters. Wind does not converse, does not judge, does not wait. It merely moves, and in its movement Miriam finds an image of the self she wishes to inhabit.
