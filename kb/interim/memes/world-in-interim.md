## Overview

The concept of "the world" in *Interim* operates on multiple scales: the intimate domestic world of the boarding-house, the social world of London, and the larger world of Europe and ideas that enters through cosmopolitan visitors and Miriam's reading. The novel traces how these worlds intersect, collide, and reshape each other in Miriam's consciousness.

## Domestic and Cosmopolitan Worlds

The boarding-house becomes a site where different worlds converge. Mr. Mendizabal, the cosmopolitan foreigner, brings Continental sophistication into the Tansley Street dining-room, challenging the provincial assumptions of the English boarders.

> I edit their newspaper. I conduct a café. (003-006)

His declaration becomes a refrain that opens Miriam's imagination to worlds beyond London.

> Oh yes, there is a cosmopolis argued Miriam looking up and out from a whirl of convincing images. (003-014)

The tension between the "new cold world" pressing forward and the remembered warmth of familiar spaces runs through the novel.

> Everyone even the Brooms were being pushed forward into a new cold world. (001-013)

## The World of Work and Money

Miriam's world is shaped by the economic realities of working life. The dental practice on Wimpole Street represents one version of the world — professional, hierarchical, defined by roles and routines — while the boarding-house represents another. Between them, Miriam navigates a London where each person inhabits their own world of destinations and purposes.

## Inner and Outer Worlds

Richardson's narrative method dissolves the boundary between inner and outer worlds.

> Her thoughts went homelessly to and fro between her various world and the lumpy figure moving sullenly along the edge of an unknown life. (001-014)

The novel's achievement is to render these worlds not as separate domains but as simultaneous layers of experience, with the visible world always shadowed by the invisible one of thought and feeling. Miriam's consciousness becomes the meeting-point where these multiple worlds — domestic, professional, cosmopolitan, remembered, imagined — converge and reshape one another.

## Key Passages

- "Oh yes, there is a cosmopolis" — Mr. Mendizabal's cosmopolitan assertion that opens a window from the provincial dining-room to a larger world (003-014).
- Miriam's "thoughts went homelessly to and fro between her various worlds and the lumpy figure moving sullenly along the edge of" perception, capturing the novel's constant movement between inner consciousness and outer reality (001-014).
- "Everyone even the Brooms were being pushed forward into a new cold world" — the intrusion of a larger, impersonal modernity into even the most intimate domestic spaces (001-013).
- "I edit their newspaper. I conduct a café" — Mendizabal's clipped, declarative statements that condense a whole cosmopolitan life into two sentences (003-006).
