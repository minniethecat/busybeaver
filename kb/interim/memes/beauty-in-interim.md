## Introduction

Beauty in *Interim* is something sought and something stumbled upon — never a possession, always an encounter. Miriam finds it in "the beauty of the blackness outside" the carriage window [004-011], in the "beauty that could not be found in English" foreign languages [003-011], in the "soft-toned" beauty of a remembered landscape [004-007]. Beauty is fugitive, appearing at the edges of perception and vanishing when grasped.

## Beauty and Language

"Foreign languages had a beauty that could not be found in English" [003-011] — for Miriam, beauty is partly a matter of distance. Words in French or German carry a glamour that English, with its over-familiarity and its association with the constraining world of family and school, cannot match. The foreign represents a world where Miriam might be someone else, where the social codes that weigh on her carry none of their native weight. Beauty, in this sense, is escape.

## Beauty in Darkness

The novel locates beauty in unexpected places. Miriam cannot see "the beauty of the blackness outside" [004-011] the carriage — but she knows it is there, the January darkness itself beautiful in its depth and completeness. Later, walking through London streets, she finds "the vista it opened growing in beauty as she walked" [006-025], beauty not as a static quality but as something that grows, develops, emerges. The pose of listening — "the features and the hair and made beauty" [005-003] — turns an ordinary face into something luminous.

## The Inexpressible

The novel's most characteristic beauty is the beauty that cannot be spoken. Miriam contemplates "the unspoken inexpressible beauty of Newlands" [004-007], her family's former home — beauty as memory, as loss, as something that exists only in the gap between what can be said and what can be felt. This inexpressible beauty is the novel's own mode: it does not describe beauty so much as gesture toward it, leaving the reader in the same position as Miriam — aware of something just beyond reach.
