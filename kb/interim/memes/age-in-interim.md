## Overview

Age in *Interim* is less a fixed datum than a relational field: characters are defined by their position in generational time, and Miriam Henderson navigates a boarding-house world stratified by years. The novel registers age through physical surfaces — faces, voices, the way a body occupies a chair — and through the social roles that different ages confer. Miriam herself, a young woman working in London, occupies an ambiguous generational position: neither the girl she was nor yet the settled woman she sees in others.

## Thematic Dimensions

The boarding-house at Tansley Street gathers characters of distinct generations into shared domestic space. Mrs. Philps, elderly and watchful, speaks from the authority of long residence — "Bring her in scolded Mrs. Philps from the dining" [001-001] — her voice carrying the weight of seniority. The youngest Bailey girl sits "close against her mother" [002-006], her youth registered in physical proximity and dependence. Between these poles, Miriam observes age as a visible condition: someone "looked very young, a child speaking on sufferance" [001-013], while "a tall thin pale-faced young man in dark clothes" [002-006] is defined in part by the adjective that precedes him.

The experience of age is double for Miriam. She can feel very young — performing for older women, dancing about a room to be called "giddy girl" [001-011] — yet also "old beyond her years" in the weariness of work and the burden of independent life. Years themselves are registered not as chronology but as felt accumulation: "she mentioned it to herself all these years" [001-011], the phrase suggesting how the past layers itself inside consciousness. The seasons of years press forward: "amongst the seasons of the years. It sent her forward to tomorrow" [001-012], age as propulsion into an uncertain future.

Youth in the novel is both promise and burden. The youth who comes "flourishing in" [003-011] carries vitality that Miriam observes with a mixture of attraction and detachment. Youth is physical — a "slender young man" moving noiselessly [003-011] — but it is also a social category that exposes the young to the appraisal of their elders. Mrs. Philps's "giddy girl" [001-011] is affectionate but also containing: the phrase names youth as a condition to be indulged and gently corrected.

## Key Passages

- **[001-011]**: "she mentioned it to herself all these years" — years as accumulated memory, the long backward glance.
- **[001-013]**: "She looked very young, a child speaking on sufferance" — age perceived in another's face, youth as diminished social standing.
- **[002-006]**: "a tall thin pale-faced young man in dark clothes and a light" — generation marked in physical description, youth as a visible type.
- **[003-011]**: "persisted Miriam. The youth came flourishing in" — the energy of young adulthood entering a room, contrasted with Miriam's own persistence.
- **[001-012]**: "amongst the seasons of the years. It sent her forward to tomorrow" — age as temporal momentum, the forward pressure of passing time.
