# Bed in _Interim_

The **bed** in _Interim_ occupies a distinctive position in the novel's spatial imagination: it is the site where public consciousness recedes and private feeling emerges. Unlike the dining table or the fireside — spaces of communal gathering — the bed is associated with solitude, reflection, and the threshold between waking and sleep.

## Bed and Domestic Ritual

Bed in the novel is embedded in the rhythms of domestic routine. The recognition that "it was time to go to bed" provokes "hilarious replies" [PN=001-013], the announcement of bedtime becoming a social performance in itself — a moment of collective recognition that the evening has ended. Miriam's offer "Let me come and help make my bed" [PN=001-014] registers bed-making as a small domestic ritual that enacts care and competence.

The "opposing bedroom doors" that "flooded their light" across the landing [PN=001-015] create a geography of bedrooms as adjacent privacies, adjoining spaces of withdrawal from the communal life below. The "bedroom crockery cold in the midmorning light" [PN=002-009] captures the particular atmosphere of the bedroom by day — functional, exposed, stripped of the intimacy it holds at night.

> …the landing where the opposing bedroom doors flooded their light across… [PN=001-015]

## Bed and Interiority

The bed is where Miriam's consciousness turns inward. The prospect of "New Year's Eve alone in a cold bedroom" [PN=002-002] registers isolation in spatial terms — the cold bedroom becomes a figure for emotional solitude. Preparing "for bed, going serenely through all the little offices" [PN=004-014] describes the transition to sleep as a deliberate winding-down, a sequence of small actions that compose a private ritual.

Agreeing to go "cheerfully to bed, with no thoughts" [PN=004-013] presents bed as escape — a surrender to unconsciousness that is also a relief from the demands of social awareness. The youngest girl being "sent to bed" [PN=003-009] marks a different relationship to bedtime: bed as discipline, the withdrawal of children from adult space.

## Bed as Physical Object

Richardson also attends to the bed as material thing. Miriam throws her bag "on to the bed" on arrival [PN=001-001], the gesture claiming the room as her own. The bed is at once the most personal piece of furniture and the most anonymous — it belongs to no one permanently in the boarding-house world, yet it is where the most private hours are passed.

## Bed and Social Care

The bed also functions as a site where care and concern are enacted between the novel's characters. When Grace offers "You shall have breakfast in bed if you like" [PN=001-011], the gesture moves beyond mere hospitality — it positions the bed as a place where the ordinary demands of social life are suspended and one is permitted to receive rather than perform. The offer is simultaneously intimate and public, made before the assembled company, and Miriam responds with a "brisk grimace" that deflects the attention even as it acknowledges the kindness.

Mrs. Philps's observation — "She's tired; she wants to go to bed" [PN=001-011] — demonstrates how the bed becomes a social token: the statement is less a diagnosis than a gentle dismissal that permits Miriam's withdrawal from the evening's demands. In the economy of the boarding-house drawing room, invoking bed authorises a character's retreat from the communal space.

## Bed in Discourse and Health

References to bed circulate in the novel's social conversation in ways that reveal prevailing attitudes toward sleep, health, and conduct. The dinner-table story of the Hindu guest who could not "find thee bed at once" [PN=001-010] turns the bed into a comic prop — the anecdote's humour depends on the absurdity of losing something as fixed and domestic as a bed, transforming the familiar into the foreign.

Elsewhere, bed appears in a register of health advice: "You're not tempted to read in bed and you go to sleep in healthy bracing air; it keeps you hardy" [PN=008-062]. This remark — reported rather than spoken directly — aligns the bed with a discourse of vigour and self-discipline, where the temptation to read in bed is a minor moral failing and the properly ventilated bedroom is a guarantor of hardiness. Miriam's own declaration "I'm going to bed" [PN=009-100], stated while "advancing towards the windows," asserts a different relationship: bed as a destination chosen by the self, not prescribed by others.
