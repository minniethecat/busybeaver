# Light in *Interim*

Light in *Interim* is never neutral. Gaslight, firelight, twilight, and the changing London daylight register the novel's emotional weather — each quality of light corresponding to a state of Miriam Henderson's consciousness. Richardson uses light not as backdrop but as a sensory index of inner life.

## In the Novel

The novel's interiors are lit by gas and fire — the gaslight of the dining room casting its "frosted globe" glow over the dinner table [001-001], the firelight of the drawing room where characters gather after meals [004-007]. These artificial lights create self-contained worlds, each room a distinct atmosphere separate from the dark streets outside.

> The room was lit only by the firelight. From its wide clear core streamed a steady radiance. [004-007]

The boarding-house at Banbury Park is a patchwork of different lights: the "gaslight pouring down on to the smeary breakfast cloth" [003-002], the "landing skylight" against which Eve's rain patters [004-004], and the "platform lights shining murkily" at railway stations [004-008].

## Light as Emotional Register

| Quality of Light | Setting | Emotional Register |
|-----------------|---------|-------------------|
| Gaslight | Dining room, hallways | Public exposure, social visibility |
| Firelight | Drawing room, bedrooms | Warmth, intimacy, reverie |
| Twilight | Streets, transitional moments | Ambiguity, possibility, endings |
| Sunlight | Outdoor scenes, memories | Vitality, clarity, past happiness |
| Lamplight | Reading scenes, solitary moments | Concentration, interior life |

The "bright light of the gas and the soft light of the reading-lamp" [002-002] in the same room at [[evening-in-interim|evening]] captures the duality Richardson traces throughout: public brightness and private glow, social demand and solitary absorption. Miriam moves between these zones of light as she moves between outer and inner worlds.

## Key Passages

> She passed through the changing lights of the passage, up the little staircase. [001-015]

The "changing lights" of the passage mark transitions — between rooms, between conversations, between states of mind. Richardson's passages and staircases are often zones of shifting awareness, and light is the medium of that shift.

> The bright light of the gas and the soft light of the reading-lamp shone upon perfect order. [002-002]

Two qualities of light in a single sentence: the public "bright light" and the private "soft light." The reading-lamp creates a circle of concentration within the larger room — a spatial metaphor for consciousness itself, which selects and illuminates a small portion of the available world.

> A cold grey twilight was blotting out the warm brown dusk of the old year. [002-006]

Twilight here is temporal as much as visual — the "cold grey" of the new year erasing the "warm brown" of the old. Light marks the passage not just of a day but of a year, and with it the novel's deeper preoccupation with time and change.

## Understanding Light as Narrative Method

What Richardson does with light is not simply descriptive — it is structural. Readers coming to *Interim* from more conventional fiction may notice that there are few passages of straightforward scene-setting. Richardson rarely tells us what a room looks like in objective terms. Instead, she registers light as Miriam perceives it, and that perception carries the emotional meaning that another novelist might communicate through plot or direct statement of feeling.

Think of it this way: in a typical novel, a character who is happy might be described as feeling happy. In *Interim*, Richardson shows Miriam registering "the bright light of the gas" or "the soft light of the reading-lamp," and the reader understands her state of mind through the quality of light she notices. This is not symbolism in the conventional sense — light does not "stand for" an emotion. Rather, Richardson treats perception itself as the primary reality. What Miriam sees is what she feels, and light — because it is everywhere, because it conditions every visual experience — becomes the novel's most flexible vocabulary for inner states.

This technique has a powerful cumulative effect. Over the course of the novel, certain qualities of light become associated with certain emotional registers — firelight with intimacy, twilight with ambiguity, gaslight with exposure — until the reader, like Miriam, learns to read the emotional weather through the light that illuminates each scene. It is a method that asks the reader to pay attention in the way Miriam herself pays attention: not to what is explained, but to what is perceived.

## Significance

Light in Richardson's novel functions as what modernist criticism would call an "objective correlative" — an external sensory detail that carries emotional weight without naming the emotion directly. When Miriam registers "the changing lights of the passage" [001-015] or the "cold grey twilight" [002-006], she is registering her own shifting consciousness through the medium of perception.

This attention to light is central to Richardson's narrative method. Her protagonist does not announce her feelings; she perceives a world saturated with them. Light — its quality, its source, its intensity — becomes a vocabulary for states of being that resist direct articulation.
