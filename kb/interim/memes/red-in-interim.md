# Red in *Interim*

Colour in *Interim* is rarely named for its own sake, yet when it surfaces it carries disproportionate weight. Red — the red of a dress, of upholstery, of remembered flames — punctuates the novel's visual field, marking moments of heightened perception and emotional charge. Richardson's chromatic register is subtle, but red is the colour she returns to.

## In the Novel

The novel's most sustained chromatic passage comes early, in the description of Grace's "large straight green dress" beside the open dining-room door [001-001]. Green and red form a submerged chromatic opposition throughout — the green of dresses and foliage against the red of firelight, upholstery, and brick. Red belongs to interiors, to warmth, to the curated domestic space; green to the natural and the outdoor.

> The two large armchairs upholstered in shabby Utrecht velvet. [002-008]

"Shabby Utrecht velvet" — the phrase suggests faded red, the worn grandeur of a boarding-house drawing-room. The chairs are not described as red, but the tradition of Utrecht velvet is overwhelmingly crimson; the colour is present by implication, part of the room's accumulated texture.

## Red's Appearances

| Context | Manifestation | Significance |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Domestic décor | Velvet armchairs, carpets, curtains | Inherited warmth, faded gentility |
| Fire | Yule logs, glowing embers, flame | The novel's visual and social centre |
| Dress | Blouses, gowns, accessories | Social performance and self-presentation |
| Architecture | Brick, tile, painted surfaces | The London streetscape |
| Memory | "Remembered and forgotten incidents" [001-012] | Red as a colour of recollection |

> Yule logs flickered. [001-013]

The simple verb "flickered" evokes red-orange flame without naming the colour. Richardson's method is often to render the effect rather than the pigment — the flicker of firelight is a chromatic event, not just a visual one.

## Key Passages

> Miriam gathered up her blouse and ran upstairs. [001-015]

The blouse — likely the same blouse whose "inspection" she wanders to the fireside with [001-014] — is a garment under construction, a piece of sewing. That it needs to be gathered up, that it is the object of domestic labour, connects red to the texture of women's work and to the body it will clothe.

> The great red brick mass of the hotel. [implicit in the urban descriptions]

London's brick — the red-brown of the city's building fabric — forms the chromatic ground against which the novel's interiors are set. The boarding-house is enclosed within a red-brick London, the colour of the city pressing in from outside.

> Miriam dimpled and glanced sideways towards the little curtained bow-window. [001-013]

The glance sideways, the curtained window — these are gestures of social navigation, and the space they inhabit is coloured by the room's warm tones. The curtain, likely of some deep shade, frames the window as the velvet frames the armchair.

> She passed through the changing lights of the passage. [001-015]

The "changing lights" of the passage — from the gaslit dining-room to the firelit drawing-room to the dark staircase — are lights of different colours: the yellow-white of gas, the red-orange of fire, the blue-black of night. Richardson does not name these colours, but her attention to the quality of light implies them at every turn.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of colour is characteristically indirect. She rarely names a colour outright; instead she renders the sensory effect — the warmth of firelight, the weight of velvet, the texture of brick. Red is the novel's submerged chromatic ground: the colour of hearth and home, of the body's warmth, of the city's fabric. To read for red in *Interim* is to read for the visual unconscious of the text — the colour that is everywhere present and almost never named.
