## Overview

Stone appears throughout *Interim* as a defining material of London's urban fabric. Grey stone pavements, church walls, and station entrances form the tactile background against which Miriam Henderson's consciousness unfolds. Stone signals permanence, weight, and the impersonal solidity of the city.

## Manifestations

The novel's London is built of stone. Miriam observes the "cool stone of St. Pancras Church" [008-009] and the "dirty grey stone" of buildings "railed by a balustrade of dark spikes" [008-014]. The material creates a distinct chromatic register: "the shadowless stone grey of the pavement" [008-008] blends with the summer air.

Stone also appears in domestic interiors. A "white stone-pillared balustrade" [001-016] looks down upon imagined scenes, and "grey stone framed days" [001-017] evoke a sense of waiting and enclosure. The material metaphor extends to social friction: an interruption is likened to "tripping over a stone" [004-009].

At Euston, "the high stone entrance of Euston Station" [008-022] represents the monumental public face of the city that Miriam passes through daily.

## Stone and Consciousness

Stone in *Interim* is not only a physical material but a register of Miriam's mental states. The image of "grey stone framed days" [001-017] turns stone from a building material into a metaphor for temporal experience — days held within grey frames as if the passage of time itself were masonry. The simile of a social interruption as "tripping over a stone" [004-009] transposes the physical hazard of stumbling into the psychological domain, suggesting that social life has its own hard, unyielding edges. In these instances, stone becomes the medium through which inner experience is made tangible — weight made word.

> …grey stone framed days upon a jewelled scene… [001-017]

The phrase "cut flowers in stone jam pots" [006-015] introduces a different register: the domestic and the makeshift. Here stone is not monumental but quotidian — a heavy container for something fragile and temporary. The juxtaposition of cut flowers (organic, transient) with stone (inorganic, durable) captures the tension that runs through the novel between fleeting perception and enduring material reality.

> Cut flowers in stone jam pots, masses of greenery… [006-015]

## Key Passages

- [008-009] The street "ending in the cool stone of St. Pancras Church" — stone as terminus and sanctuary.
- [008-014] The "dirty grey stone" with its "balustrade of dark spikes" — London's austere beauty.
- [008-022] "The high stone entrance of Euston Station" — metropolitan monumentality.
