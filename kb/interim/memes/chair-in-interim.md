# Chair in _Interim_

The **chair** in _Interim_ is never merely furniture. It is a social instrument: where a character sits, in what kind of chair, and how they occupy it, all register position within the shifting geography of the [[room-in-interim|boarding-house drawing-room]]. Richardson's chairs carry political meaning — they encode class, gender, and the distribution of ease within domestic space.

## Chair and Social Placement

Miriam's first evening establishes the chair as a site of belonging. She sinks into "the largest of the velvet armchairs" [PN=001-001], and the chair's yielding slope becomes a physical metaphor for acceptance into the household. When she pats "the arm of the vacant armchair" [PN=001-011] and declares "I like this best," the gesture is proprietorial — claiming a place among strangers by claiming a chair.

The chair also registers exclusion. Miriam's eyes turning "towards the chair in the corner half-hidden" [PN=001-014] mark a peripheral seat, a position outside the central circle of talk. In the Bailey household, where one sits is whom one is.

> …She sank into the largest of the velvet armchairs. Its back sloped away from her… [PN=001-001]

## Chair and the Body

Richardson attends closely to how bodies occupy chairs. Miriam sits "back in her chair searching her face and form" [PN=001-017], the chair becoming a frame for self-scrutiny. A character "lounging back in his chair with his hands in his pockets" [PN=004-003] signals ease — a physical confidence that Miriam herself rarely achieves.

Mrs. Bailey "piloting her to a chair in front of the fire" [PN=003-002] performs a small act of placement, steering the newcomer toward warmth and visibility. The chair by the fire is the chair of welcome, and the verb "piloting" suggests both guidance and gentle control — Miriam is being placed, not placing herself.

## Chairs as Domestic Texture

Chairs also contribute to the novel's thick description of interiors. "Kitchen chairs standing sparsely about" [PN=005-002] in an unfamiliar room evoke austerity and impermanence. The declaration "It was Bohemia; the table and chairs were bohemian" [PN=005-003] invests furniture with cultural meaning — chairs signify a way of life. The "little circular chair" with its "narrow circular strip of velvet" [PN=002-008] is attended to as an object of tactile and visual interest, part of the sensory fabric of the room.

Richardson's treatment of furniture belongs to a tradition of domestic realism that reads character through environment. But her innovation is to render the chair not as static property but as dynamic participant in social interaction — the chair is not simply there; it is occupied, vacated, moved toward, turned away from, struck, stroked, and claimed.

## Chair and Gendered Performance

The way chairs are occupied in _Interim_ reveals a quiet but consistent code of gendered behaviour. Dr. Hurd repeatedly strikes his chair in conversation — a "thwack on the arm of his chair as he flung back his head for his laugh" [PN=008-031], and again he "struck his chair and sat forward" [PN=008-036]. These percussive gestures treat the chair as an extension of masculine self-assertion, a surface to be hit as punctuation to speech. When he "flopped down in the easy chair" [PN=008-023], the verb registers an unselfconscious physicality, a body taking possession of furniture without hesitation.

Miriam's relationship with chairs is altogether different. She sits "limply down on the nearest chair" [PN=010-009], the adverb suggesting exhaustion or defeat rather than ownership. When presented with a chair, she is directed: "Take this chair he pursued and sat near as she sat down in a little stiff chair" [PN=008-023]. The "little stiff chair" — diminutive, rigid — is the chair assigned to her, while the men occupy the "easy chair" nearby. The asymmetry is spatial and social: men sprawl and strike; women are placed and perched.

This gendered chair-politics extends to the novel's broader treatment of domestic space. The easy chair is a masculine prerogative; the stiff chair, the corner chair, the chair one is "piloted" toward — these are feminine seating assignments, and Miriam's consciousness registers their constraints with the same acuity it brings to conversational nuance.

## Chair Arrangements and Conversation

Richardson's interiors are choreographed through furniture placement, and chairs are the primary instruments of this choreography. The "little comfortable circular velvet chair stood in the middle of the row, conversationally facing the high-backed wicker chair" [PN=008-014] — here chairs are arranged not for decoration but for talk, their mutual orientation creating a conversational dyad within the larger room.

The social dynamics of chair placement extend to the drawing-room at evening. A visitor "crossed over to a chair near Miss Rogers and was in eager conversation with her" [PN=005-004], the movement toward a specific chair enacting social choice. And when a new arrival enters "sliding into a chair, his hat sticking out from the hand of the arm" [PN=009-099], the verb "sliding" captures the fluidity of confident social movement — no hesitation, no search for the right seat, just an easy insertion into the available space.

## Chairs and the Reading Body

A chair is also, critically, where one reads. Miriam's most private moments occur in chairs — reading letters, turning pages, absorbed in thought while physically anchored to a particular seat. The chair supports the life of the mind even as it constrains the body. Richardson's chairs thus mediate between the physical and the intellectual, the social and the solitary, in a way that no other domestic object in *Interim* quite achieves.
