# Dr. Hurd

Dr. Hurd is a minor but distinctly rendered character in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*, one of the professional men who board at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park establishment. As a physician, he belongs to the same medical world as [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]], though his temperament and narrative function differ markedly from the charismatic German dentist.

## Character Overview

Dr. Hurd appears largely in the novel's evening scenes — at dinner, in the drawing-room, in the threshold moments of arrival and departure. He is characterised by a certain reserve, a professional decorum that distinguishes him from the more flamboyant personalities of the boarding-house circle.

> Dr. Hurd held the door wide for Miriam. [006-018]

The small gesture — holding the door — establishes his manner: courteous, unobtrusive, present without demanding attention. He is the kind of character Richardson excels at rendering: not central to the plot but essential to the texture of the social world.

## In the Novel

Dr. Hurd's appearances are concentrated in the middle chapters, where he participates in the evening gatherings that structure the novel's social life.

> Both Dr. Heber and Dr. Hurd appeared at dinner. Mrs. Bailey was in high spirits. [006-019]

The pairing with Dr. von Heber is significant: two doctors, two foreigners (Heber is German; Hurd's nationality is less marked), two professional men in a household of women. Their joint appearance at dinner elevates the occasion — "Mrs. Bailey was in high spirits" — suggesting the social weight that male professionals carry in this domestic world.

## Key Relationships

### Dr. von Heber

The two doctors form a natural pair within the boarding-house, united by profession and gender. But where von Heber is voluble, musical, and romantically entangled with Eve Henderson, Hurd is quieter — an observer rather than a performer.

### Mrs. Bailey

As the boarding-house keeper, [[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] orchestrates the social occasions at which Dr. Hurd appears. His presence at dinner is part of the economy of hospitality she manages.

### Miriam Henderson

Miriam registers Dr. Hurd with her characteristic attention to social nuance. His door-holding, his impressions, his quiet presence — these are noted not because they are dramatic but because they are part of the sensory and social texture she is constantly recording.

## Notable Passages

> Dr. Hurd's impressions had had no effect on her. [007-004]

This line suggests a moment of assessment — Dr. Hurd has formed impressions, has perhaps expressed them, and Miriam has registered and dismissed them. The sentence captures the asymmetry of boarding-house relationships: people observe and judge one another, their impressions landing or not landing with varying force.

> Dr. Hurd closed the door behind them. [008-023]

Another door held, another threshold crossed. The repetition of this gesture — Dr. Hurd at the door — makes it his signature action: the man who facilitates passages, who eases transitions, who stands at the edge of the scene rather than its centre.

## Significance

Dr. Hurd exemplifies Richardson's method of characterisation through accumulation of small gestures. He does not have a dramatic arc; he does not declare himself in speech or action. He is known through what he does at thresholds — holding doors, appearing at dinner, forming and expressing impressions. This is character as social texture, not dramatic function.
