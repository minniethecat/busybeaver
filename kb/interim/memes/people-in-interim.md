## Overview

"People" in *Interim* are simultaneously objects of Miriam's acute observation and subjects whose inner lives remain fundamentally unknowable. The novel's social world — the boarders, the dental patients, the visitors to the drawing-room, the strangers on omnibuses — provides an endless field for Miriam's consciousness to explore, classify, and wonder at. Her relationship to "people" oscillates between fascinated engagement and a retreat into private judgment.

## The Spectacle of Others

Miriam watches people with an almost ethnographic attention. The boarders at Tansley Street — Mrs. Bailey, Sissie, the various temporary residents — become characters in a continuous social drama that Miriam both participates in and observes from a distance. "You do come across some funny people," Mrs. Philps remarks, and the novel delights in the sheer variety of human types that pass through Miriam's field of vision.

The opening scene establishes the warm, tactile social world Miriam enters:

> Bring her in scolded Mrs. Philps from the dining-room door. (001-001)

> Mrs. Philps patted her arm and cheek. (001-001)

Each person is a world, and the boarding-house is a theatre of worlds colliding — intimate gestures and casual remarks carrying the weight of whole personalities.

## The Limits of Knowledge

For all her powers of observation, Miriam confronts the impossibility of truly knowing other people.

> It was impossible to show everything, the more opinions you expressed the more you misled people and the further you got away from them. (001-011)

This recognition — that social performance creates distance rather than connection — is central to the novel's understanding of human relations. The novel asks: what can we know of another person? Richardson's answer, embedded in the narrative method itself, is that we can know surfaces, gestures, fragments of speech — but the inner life of others remains a mystery that even the most sensitive observer cannot penetrate.

> Why would people insist upon talking about things—when nothing can ever be communicated. (001-013)

## People and Solitude

Miriam's relationship to people is defined by a paradoxical need for both connection and solitude. She values the anonymity of the crowd — the "relief to be in London; surrounded by people who did not know what one was really like" — that liberates her from the demands of social performance. At the same time, the novel registers the loneliness of being surrounded by people with whom one cannot truly connect. The boarding-house, where residents live side by side without genuine connection, becomes an emblem of modern social life itself.

## Key Passages

- "You do come across some funny people" — Mrs. Philps's remark captures the novel's comic and curious attention to human variety.
- "The more opinions you expressed the more you misled people and the further you got away from them" — Miriam's recognition of the gap between social speech and authentic connection (001-011).
- "Why would people insist upon talking about things — when nothing can ever be communicated" — a radical skepticism about the possibility of genuine communication through language (001-013).
- "Mrs. Philps patted her arm and cheek" — the physical intimacy of the boarding-house world, where affection is expressed through small gestures rather than declarations (001-001).
