# Wall in _Interim_

**Walls** in _Interim_ are not neutral backdrops but charged surfaces — bearing pictures, reflecting light, containing and separating lives. Richardson's attention to walls registers the novel's deep interest in the psychology of domestic space.

## Walls as Surfaces

Walls in the novel are described through their coverings and adornments. The "sheeny brown-yellow varnish of the wallpaper above the mantelpiece" [PN=002-003] captures a specific materiality — varnished, reflective, amber-toned. Pictures hang on walls as objects of contemplation: "the Shakespeare on the wall opposite" [PN=001-004] and the "large portrait" lit by the piano [PN=005-003]. The walls "sent back sheeny reflections" [PN=006-001] between the pictures, turning the room into a chamber of light.

> …like the pictures on the wall, and beside them the early going to and fro… [PN=001-015]

## Walls and Light

Richardson frequently couples walls with light effects. "The light ran up the wall" [PN=005-003] describes the glow cast upward by a piano lamp, while Miriam turns to "consult the pictures and the wallpaper" [PN=004-013] as if they held answers. The "cool dark walls holding darkness in far corners" [PN=008-010] show walls as containers of shadow as much as light.

## Walls as Containment

Walls mark the boundary between shelter and exposure. The "large houses, quickening on walls and stairways and across wide-open spaces" [PN=001-017] suggest walls transmitting life rather than merely enclosing it. Miriam's fleeting thought — "a prison. Walls. What are walls?" [PN=003-001] — crystallises the ambivalence: walls both protect and confine.
