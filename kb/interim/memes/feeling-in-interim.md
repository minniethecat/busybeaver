## Overview

Feeling in *Interim* is not a secondary quality of experience but its primary substance. Miriam Henderson moves through the world not by reasoning but by feeling — registering the emotional temperature of rooms, the affective charge of encounters, the bodily sensations that precede and exceed thought. Richardson's narrative method makes feeling the very medium of consciousness, dissolving the conventional boundary between emotion and perception.

## The Body as Register

Feeling in the novel is fundamentally embodied.

> She could not think of anything that could be made to follow it with any show of sincerity and sat feeling large and conspicuous. (001-013)

Miriam's physical self-awareness is inseparable from her emotional state — the body registers social discomfort before the mind can articulate it.

> She hurried along feeling out towards them through downcast eyes. (001-015)

The act of perception itself becomes a mode of feeling — reaching towards the world through sensation rather than detached observation. The novel insists that consciousness is not a disembodied mind but a feeling body moving through a felt world.

## The Texture of Emotion

Richardson renders emotional states with an unusual fidelity to their complexity and ambiguity. Feelings are rarely pure or simple: they shade into one another, contradict each other, emerge from and dissolve into bodily sensation.

> Moszkowski's Serenade sounded fearfully pathetic; as if the piano were heartbroken. (002-009)

Music is translated into feeling with an immediacy that bypasses intellectual interpretation. Throughout the novel, Miriam's emotional life is rendered not through named emotions — joy, sorrow, anger — but through the textured registration of affective states that resist simple categorization.

## The Sensory World

Feeling in *Interim* extends beyond emotion to encompass the full sensory spectrum.

> The rich strong coffee was flowing into the cups. In a moment Grace would be handing plates of rashers and Florrie would have finished extracting the eggs from the boiler. (001-013)

The sensory anticipation of food and warmth becomes a moment of pure bodily feeling — the domestic world rendered through the language of physical sensation. Richardson's prose demonstrates that the most ordinary moments are saturated with feeling when registered by a consciousness fully present to its own experience.

## Feeling and Thought

The relationship between feeling and thought in *Interim* is not hierarchical but reciprocal. Miriam "sat feeling large and conspicuous" — the physical sensation of social discomfort is itself a form of thought, a judgment about her place in the social world that precedes and exceeds any articulate formulation. Richardson's innovation is to show that feeling is not the opposite of thought but its inseparable companion.

## Key Passages

- "The rich strong coffee was flowing into the cups. In a moment Grace would be handing plates of rashers" — the sensory anticipation of food and warmth rendered as a moment of pure bodily feeling (001-013).
- Miriam "sat feeling large and conspicuous" — physical self-consciousness as social emotion, the body registering what the mind cannot yet articulate (001-013).
- "She hurried along feeling out towards them through downcast eyes" — perception as a form of emotional reaching, the eyes feeling rather than merely seeing (001-015).
- "Moszkowski's Serenade sounded fearfully pathetic; as if the piano were heartbroken" — music translated directly into the language of feeling (002-009).
