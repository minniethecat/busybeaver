# Weather in _Interim_

**Weather** in _Interim_ is far more than meteorological background. It is the default currency of small talk, the physical atmosphere that shapes the novel's sensory world, and — in one of Richardson's most pointed insights — a mechanism for avoiding genuine communication. When the characters talk about the weather, they are almost always talking around something else.

## Weather as Conversational Currency

The novel opens with weather talk. At the dinner table, characters make "sudden statements about the weather one after the other" [006-001], the weather functioning as a shared surface beneath which private thoughts run undisturbed. This is social speech at its most automatic — words exchanged not to communicate but to fill the space between people with something safe.

> They were all talking about the weather, making sudden statements about the weather one after the other. They were feeling the weather, wrapped in it, and sending out these statements like signals. [006-001]

The simile is precise: weather statements are "signals," not conversations. They register presence rather than convey meaning. Each speaker confirms they are there, that they share the same atmosphere, that they are willing to participate in the ritual of sociability — and nothing more.

The weather question becomes a bridge across awkward silence. Mrs. Bailey "waited silent a moment before asking if it rained" [004-013], and the question's function is not informational but relational — a way of re-entering conversation after a pause threatened to become a gap. Miriam responds in kind: "In a dry professional official voice she said gazing at the hearthrug ... no, at least oh yes, I think it is raining" [004-013]. Her uncertainty about the actual weather reveals the question's true nature. Nobody really needs to know if it is raining; they need to keep talking.

## Weather as Deflection

Weather's most significant narrative function in _Interim_ is its role as a barrier to genuine feeling. The novel's treatment of love contains the most compressed instance:

> She glanced at him with a touch of weakness. What a lovely mild evening. [006-023]

A "touch of weakness" — a flicker of possible attraction, of emotional vulnerability — is immediately covered by the weather. "What a lovely mild evening." The word "lovely" migrates from the person to the atmosphere, from the dangerous territory of feeling to the safe ground of meteorological description. This is how Richardson writes emotional life: as a pressure that is felt and redirected. Weather talk is the mechanism of that redirection.

The pattern recurs throughout the novel. When conversation approaches the intimate or the difficult, someone remarks on the weather and the moment passes. Weather is the social equivalent of a door quietly closed — a way of returning to safer ground without acknowledging the retreat.

## The Great Weather Debate

In the novel's eighth chapter, weather becomes the subject of sustained philosophical argument. Miriam defends English weather against the preference for predictable continental climates, and the debate reveals character with unusual directness.

> "English weather does not want a champion. It's so wonderful. Perhaps you are thinking of Italian skies and that sort of thing; in countries where the weather does not change or not suddenly; only at fixed seasons. That's very nice in a way. You can make plans. But I know I should long for grey days and changes in the sky. A grey day is not melancholy; it's exciting. You can see everything. The sun makes everything pale and blinds you." [008-038]

This is the novel's most extended aesthetic statement about weather, and it is also a statement about Miriam herself. Her preference for variability over predictability, for grey over gold, for English mutability over continental stability, is a preference for a world that cannot be planned for — a world in which one must remain alert. "A grey day is not melancholy; it's exciting" is a small manifesto for attention over comfort.

The debate continues with fog:

> "I think a real London fog is perfection; everything and the shapes and outlines of things looming up only as you pass them. Wonderful." [008-040]

Fog transforms the known city into an unknown one. Objects emerge from obscurity only as one approaches them — a perceptual condition that mirrors the novel's own method of gradual revelation. The fog is "perfection" because it makes the familiar strange, restoring mystery to streets walked every day.

Not everyone agrees. Another voice objects: "Town fog's not a natural phenomenon. It's just, town dirt" [008-042], and another: "I can't see anything either beautiful or in the least wonderful in your town fogs" [008-041]. The disagreement is not really about fog. It is about whether one finds value in opacity or insists on clarity — about two ways of being in the world. The weather debate is, like all the novel's conversations, a debate about perception itself.

Miriam's position is summed up in a line that could stand as the novel's aesthetic creed:

> "In a way the weather's enough, in itself, without anything else." [008-043]

The weather is enough. Not as background, not as metaphor, not as small talk — but as experience in its own right. This is the logic of Richardson's entire method: the surface of life, attended to with sufficient intensity, needs no deeper meaning.

## London Weather as Atmosphere

London weather in _Interim_ is specific and sensory: the "drizzling rain" [004-012] through which Miriam walks home, the "bleak wind" [002-005] at the Marylebone Road that forces her to turn back, the "icy wind" [002-005] that drives against her. The novel's weather is cold, damp, grey, and dark — a London winter rendered in its physical particulars.

This weather is not decorative. It shapes action — Miriam turns back from a walk because of wind, hurries home through drizzle, endures cold bedrooms — and it conditions perception. The "grey brilliance of the sky was upon the pavement and in the gutters" [008-008] after rain, and the "vast fresh gold-lit sky somewhere behind the twilight" [006-017] after a storm clears, are not mere description but registrations of how the physical world presses on consciousness.

The boarding-house itself is defined against the weather outside. Inside, there is firelight and gaslight and the "familiar firelit winter darkness" [004-007] with its "faint dry warmth." Outside, there is the cold, the rain, the wind, the fog. The threshold between inside and outside is also a threshold between warmth and exposure, between the social and the solitary, between the protected and the vulnerable. Weather is what the house keeps out.

The imprint of weather on the material world is registered with characteristic precision. A "weatherworn felt hat softened perhaps" [004-009] carries the accumulated exposure of its wearer's life — the hat shaped not only by use but by the elements it has passed through. "Weatherworn" is a word about time. It records the slow action of atmosphere on material, the way exposure wears surfaces into character. This attention to the weathering of objects is part of Richardson's larger attention to the material world as a register of lived experience. The weatherworn hat, like the grey window filmed with city grime, like the rain-streaked pavement, testifies to the passage of time through physical surfaces. Weather is not an event in the novel; it is a continuous process that leaves its mark on everything it touches.

## Significance

Weather in _Interim_ operates at three levels simultaneously. As conversational filler, it enables social life to proceed without the risk of genuine exchange. As physical atmosphere, it saturates the novel's sensory world and shapes the bodily experience of its characters. And as aesthetic value — "the weather's enough, in itself" — it models the novel's deepest commitment: that the surface of experience, attended to with sufficient care, requires no transcendence. The weather is not a symbol of something else. The weather is the weather, and that is enough.
