# Night in *Interim*

Night in *Interim* is the hour of unaccompanied consciousness. When the boarding-house falls quiet — the dinner cleared, the fires banked, the other residents retired — Miriam Henderson is left alone with the heightened awareness that darkness brings. Richardson's nights are not escapes from the day's experience but intensifications of it.

## In the Novel

The novel's evening scenes extend into night, and the transition matters. The drawing-room conversation that fills the early evening gives way to the solitary hours when Miriam lies awake or moves through the darkened house. These nocturnal passages are where the novel's modernist project is most fully realised — consciousness stripped of social occasion.

> She could come here every night … filling her life with green chartreuse. [008-014]

The ellipsis is Richardson's — a pause in which the possibility opens. "Every night" suggests a rhythm, a repetition, a life shaped by nocturnal habit. The "green chartreuse" is specific, sensory, and slightly decadent: the night as a time of permitted indulgence.

> It was nearly midnight. The grey of tomorrow morning was already in the sky. [003-010]

Midnight is not an ending but a threshold — "the grey of tomorrow morning" already visible. Richardson's temporal awareness is always layered: the present moment contains the seed of what comes next.

## Key Passages

> Sounds came from far away in the night straight into her heart. [001-012]

The night amplifies. Daytime sounds are filtered through social awareness; night sounds travel "straight into her heart" — unmediated, direct. The sentence enacts what it describes: the simple declarative structure carries the sound without obstacle from the outer world to the inner.

> She dreaded waking in the night with gnawing hunger to keep her awake until the morning. [004-012]

Night here is not romantic solitude but physical vulnerability — "gnawing hunger," the dread of wakefulness, the long wait for morning. Richardson does not sentimentalise the night; she records its bodily reality.

> Tonight, going strong and steady to her own front door. [008-017]

The shift in tone is striking. After passages of nocturnal anxiety, this night-walk is purposeful: "strong and steady." The "own front door" — the word "own" doing quiet work — suggests a self-possession earned through the long nights of uncertainty.

> She began the new year with a long night's rest in a tidy room with everything put away for the night. [002-002]

The satisfaction here is domestic and temporal. A "long night's rest" is both physical recovery and symbolic renewal — the new year beginning with order, with "everything put away." The night as container, as closure.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of night is central to her rendering of consciousness. The social self that performs at dinner and converses in the drawing-room retires; what remains is a more exposed awareness — more vulnerable to memory and sensation, but also more honest. Night strips away the scaffolding of social identity and leaves consciousness alone with itself.

This is a distinctively modernist use of night. Unlike the Victorian night of danger or romance, Richardson's night is phenomenological: it is simply the condition in which consciousness becomes most fully aware of its own operations. The darkness outside mirrors the inward turn.
