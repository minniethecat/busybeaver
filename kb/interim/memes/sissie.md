# Sissie

Sissie is a boarder at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park establishment and one of the young women whose daily life forms the social texture of Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. Her most distinctive narrative function is as Miriam Henderson's pupil in French, a relationship that weaves together the novel's interests in language, social class, and the quiet dramas of boarding-house intimacy.

## Character Overview

Sissie belongs to the circle of young women around the Bailey dinner table — not a central protagonist like [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]], but a recurring presence whose interactions illuminate the social dynamics of the household. She is inquisitive, occasionally irreverent, and sufficiently at ease in the boarding-house to demand attention when she wants it.

> "Where's Mr. Mendizzable?" demanded Sissie. [006-003]

The demand — and the deliberate mangling of "Mendizabal" — reveals Sissie's character in miniature: forward, playful with names, unwilling to be a silent observer. Her mispronunciation of the Spanish dentist's name is a small act of irreverence, a refusal of the formality that foreign names might impose.

## Role in the Narrative

Sissie's appearances span the novel, from early chapters through to the final gathering scenes.

### French Lessons

Sissie's French lessons with Miriam form one of the novel's recurring minor through-lines. Miriam takes on the lessons partly for income — the money helps sustain her precarious position — and partly as an entry into the social rhythms of the Bailey household.

> "…wondering if you would give Sissie a few French lessons" spoke the voice. [002-007]

> …any part of it was bought by Sissie's French lessons and being Sissie's friend… [003-001]

The French lessons are not simply a transaction; they bind Miriam to the household, give her a role beyond that of a paying boarder. That the lessons also "bought" friendship suggests the fluid boundary between professional and personal relations in this world.

> Sissie read her set of phrases in her clear young voice… [003-011]

The lessons themselves are rendered with Richardson's characteristic attention to sound and presence — the "clear young voice," the physical proximity of pupil and teacher, the awkwardness and intimacy of learning.

### Dinner-Table Presence

At dinner, Sissie is an active participant in the social choreography. Her seating position is noted with precision, as Richardson maps the table's geography.

> …the forms about the table besides Sissie sitting at the far end opposite… [006-001]

The dinner table in *Interim* is a stage, and Sissie's place at the far end positions her as part of the ensemble. She is not the star but an essential player in the evening's collective performance.

## Key Relationships

### Emile

Sissie and [[emile|Emile]], the waiter, share a particular dynamic — the young woman taking French lessons and the young man who is himself French. Richardson suggests a flirtatious undercurrent between them.

> Sissie and Emile were back again in the room having a scene… [003-013]

> She had a sense of Emile and Sissie glancing and affronted while… [003-014]

The pairing of Sissie and Emile — both young, both positioned somewhat at the margins of the English boarding-house, both connected to the French language — creates a secondary current of social possibility running alongside the novel's main concerns.

### Miss Scott

Sissie's position at the table places her in proximity to [[scott|Miss Scott]], and the two are often mentioned together in the gossip that circulates through the household.

> Then came Miss Scott on Sissie's left; then Sissie… [006-004]

### Miriam Henderson

For Miriam, Sissie is pupil, companion, and occasional confidante. Miriam observes Sissie with the same intense attention she brings to all the personalities around her, noting social nuances and unspoken dynamics.

> …round them. Miriam consulted Sissie, probing enviously for the data… [004-003]

## Notable Passages

> …and Sissie and Emile would forget her desperate onlooking… [003-016]

The phrase "desperate onlooking" captures the particular loneliness of Miriam's position — even when included, she is watching from the outside. Sissie and Emile, absorbed in their own dynamic, do not notice.

> Mrs. Bailey looked sharply up. Sissie was ambling hurriedly out of the room… [009-099]

Mrs. Bailey's sharp look and Sissie's hurried exit suggest a moment of tension — a reprimand, perhaps, or a social misstep. The scene is typical of Richardson's method: drama conveyed through glances and movements rather than explicit narration.

## Significance

Sissie is a character of the middle distance — neither foreground protagonist nor background extra. She is defined by sociability, by her place in the boarding-house ecosystem, by the particular connection she shares with Miriam through the French lessons. In the economy of Richardson's novel, characters like Sissie do the essential work of building the social world: they talk, they demand, they mispronounce, they hurry out of rooms. They make the boarding-house feel inhabited.
