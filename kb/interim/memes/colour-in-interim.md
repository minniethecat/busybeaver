# Colour in _Interim_

**Colour** in _Interim_ is at once a fact of perception and a vehicle of meaning. Richardson's descriptive palette registers colour with attentive precision — the "cream-coloured basins" of the washstand [001-001], the "colourless railed-in gardens" [001-015] of a London thoroughfare, the "coloured glass windows" of a remembered church [001-011]. Colour saturates the visual field, yet Richardson's treatment is rarely decorative; colour is an index of mood, class, and the quality of attention itself.

## Colour and Perception

Miriam's perception of colour is shaped by her emotional and physical state. The "cream-coloured basins" [001-001] she notices while pouring hot water are not neutral observations — the cream colour belongs to a world of domestic comfort, of gleaming surfaces and careful furnishing that her eye runs over appreciatively. When the world feels hostile or indifferent, colour drains from it: "eddies of colourless dust swirled about the pavements" [002-005], the adjective "colourless" naming a world stripped of visual interest and affective charge.

The "colourless voice murmuring from the back of her throat" [003-011] extends the vocabulary of colour into the domain of sound — a voice without timbre, without animation, as if speech itself had lost its chromatic range. Colour becomes a measure of presence: the vivid world is coloured; the diminished world is colourless.

## The Secret of Beauty

Richardson offers a rare aesthetic pronouncement: "The secret of beauty is colour and texture" [008-214]. This aphorism, attributed to a character in conversation, distils a principle that the novel's own descriptive practice bears out. Beauty inheres not in form alone but in the interplay of surface — the way light catches a coloured surface, the texture that gives colour its material ground.

The "coloured; blue" pamphlet on the table "under the window that was the most brightly lit by the streetlamps" [009-100] exemplifies this principle in miniature: a single coloured object, caught in lamplight, draws the eye and concentrates meaning. The blue pamphlet is an island of colour in a dim room.

## Watercolour Sketches

Colour in _Interim_ is not only observed but made. Miriam examines "the little watercolour sketches one by one" [005-003], an encounter with coloured images rendered by hand that introduces the act of making colour into the novel's visual field. Watercolour as a medium — translucent, layered, fugitive — mirrors the novel's own descriptive method: colour that does not declare itself heavily but seeps into perception, staining the visual field with delicate wash. The sketches are small, handled individually, demanding the close attention that is Miriam's characteristic mode. In a novel where colour frequently arrives as an attribute of domestic objects or natural light, the watercolour sketches mark a deliberate human intervention — colour produced, not merely received.

> She took the little watercolour sketches one by one and listened…
> (005-003)

## Colourlessness and Modern Life

The urban landscape of *Interim* is frequently colourless. The "almost colourless" iron railings "with black grime" [008-014], the "colourless figure" [006-028] of a man seen at a café — Richardson's London is a city of subdued tones, of grime and greyness. Colourlessness is the visual signature of modernity: the city's architecture and atmosphere strip the world of hue, leaving only the monochrome of stone, soot, and fading light.

Against this chromatic poverty, isolated moments of colour — the "cream-coloured" porcelain, the "coloured glass" of the church, the blue pamphlet — stand out as acts of resistance, assertions of the aesthetic in a world tending toward grey.
