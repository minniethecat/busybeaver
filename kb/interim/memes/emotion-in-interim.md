## Introduction

Emotion in *Interim* is rarely named directly. Richardson's method is to render feeling through the texture of perception — a quality of light, a physical sensation, a half-formed thought — rather than through the conventional vocabulary of emotional states. This indirection is a deliberate modernist technique: emotion is too fluid, too layered, to be captured by labels like "happy" or "sad." The novel's prose tracks the movement of feeling as it passes through consciousness, registering its effects without reducing it to a category.

## The Objective Correlative

Light in Richardson's novel functions as what modernist criticism would call an "objective correlative" — an external sensory detail that carries emotional weight without naming the emotion directly. A change in lamplight, the quality of dusk in a London street, the "velvet lustre" of eyes darkened by fear [001-014]: these sensory details become the vehicle for emotional states that the narrative voice does not state. Emotion is shown through its effects on perception, not through introspection about feeling.

## Emotion and the Body

Richardson registers emotion through bodily sensation. Fear manifests as physical constriction; joy as a lightness of movement, a propulsion forward. The body in *Interim* is not separate from consciousness but its instrument — what it feels, consciousness registers as emotion. This embodied approach to feeling resists the mind-body dualism that would treat emotion as purely mental. For Miriam Henderson, to feel is to inhabit the body in a particular way.

## The Social Regulation of Feeling

Emotion in the boarding-house world is subject to constant social regulation. At the dinner table, feeling must be performed appropriately — cheerfulness, interest, the right degree of amusement. Private emotion, by contrast, is released in the solitude of the rented room, where "the freshness and quietude of the room enfolded Miriam, smoothing away grubbiness and fatigue" [001-011]. The novel tracks the gap between public emotional performance and private emotional experience, finding in that gap the seeds of both alienation and self-knowledge.
