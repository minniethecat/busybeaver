# Laughter in *Interim*

Laughter in *Interim* is rarely innocent. Around the dinner table at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]], laughter signals inclusion or exclusion, marks gender boundaries, and registers the constant social negotiation of the boarding-house world. Miriam Henderson — often silent, often observing — experiences the laughter of others as something to navigate rather than simply share.

## In the Novel

Laughter fills the novel's social spaces: the dining room "in an uproar of voices and laughter" [006-003], the drawing room where guests sit "perfectly sincere in their laughter and talk" [006-011], the hallway where conversations break "into shouts of laughter" [004-003]. It is the sound of collective life, the audible evidence that a social world exists beyond Miriam's private consciousness.

> Miriam sat stiffly through the laughter of the Baileys. Her refusal to join exposed the distance between her consciousness and their easy sociability. [003-009]

Miriam's relationship to laughter is often one of observation and resistance. She "flushed through her laughter" [001-014], caught between the impulse to join and the awareness of her separateness. The novel tracks not just when Miriam laughs, but when she cannot.

## Laughter as Social Marker

| Function | Example | Significance |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Group bonding | "the general laughter" at dinner [006-019] | Creates the collective "we" Miriam stands outside |
| Gendered performance | Mr. Gunner's laughter "flung back his head" [006-009] | Male laughter as display; female laughter as response |
| Exclusion | "the laughter of the Baileys" [003-009] | Miriam's non-participation marks her outsider status |
| Release | "her laughter-shaken person" [004-003] | Eve's laughter as emotional freedom Miriam envies |
| Deflection | "bathed in laughter heaped up" [007-006] | Laughter as a way of not speaking; a social shield |

The novel's men — the doctors, Mr. Gunner — laugh performatively. The women laugh in response or, like Miriam, resist laughter as a form of self-protection.

## Key Passages

> She walks in Beauty like the night — he roared out words and laughter together. [011-002]

This late-novel scene crystallizes the fusion of speech and laughter. Dr. von Heber recites Byron through laughter — the line itself ("She walks in Beauty") contrasts with the roaring delivery, suggesting that even poetry must pass through the filter of social performance at the boarding-house.

> The world burst into laughter; his face turned slowly. [006-028]

The world's laughter here is almost cosmic — the universe mocking. In a single sentence, laughter transforms from social signal to existential commentary.

> She heard nothing of the various conversations sprouting easily all round the table. [006-019]

In the very scene where "general laughter" fills the dining room, Miriam hears nothing. Her exclusion from laughter is inseparable from her absorption in consciousness itself.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of laughter — as social performance, as gender marker, as exclusion mechanism — is of a piece with her larger modernist project. Just as [[conversation-in-interim|conversation]] in the novel is never merely informational, laughter is never merely joyful. It is always also relational: a negotiation of who belongs and who does not, who speaks and who is spoken about.
