# Conversation in *Interim*

Conversation is the primary social activity in Dorothy Richardson's novel *Interim*, occurring at the dinner table, during [[tea-in-interim|tea]], and on [[walking-in-interim|walks]] through London streets. In a novel built around Miriam Henderson's consciousness, conversation serves as the interface between inner and outer worlds — the mechanism through which other characters reveal themselves and Miriam's own thoughts find or resist expression.

## In the Novel

The novel's conversations unfold primarily at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]], where the boarding-house dinner table becomes a theater of competing voices. Mrs. Bailey presides, the doctors debate, and Miriam listens — often more participant in her own mind than in the spoken exchange.

> The dining-room was a buzz of conversation. The table was packed save for one place. [009-098]

This "buzz of conversation" is the novel's soundtrack — the ambient noise of collective life against which individual consciousness defines itself.

> She had heard nothing of the various conversations sprouting easily all round the table. [006-019]

Miriam's detachment from conversation is as significant as her participation. Her failure to hear — her absorption in her own thoughts — marks the distance between social surface and inner depth.

## Conversation as Social Performance

| Type of Conversation | Setting | Function |
|---------------------|---------|----------|
| Dinner-table debate | Dining room | Intellectual display; male professional performance |
| Intimate exchange | Hallway, bedroom | Emotional disclosure; female confidence |
| Formal introduction | Drawing room | Social positioning; class and nationality marked |
| Walking talk | London streets | Less formal; movement enabling speech |

Conversation in the novel is not neutral — it is gendered, classed, and nationalized. The male doctors ([[von-heber|von Heber]], Hurd) dominate dinner-table talk while the women (Miriam, [[grace-broom|Grace Broom]]) more often listen or speak in private.

## Key Passages

> "Do sit down," said Mrs. Philps, her voice emerging from the midst of conversation. [001-011]

Mrs. Philps's voice "emerging from the midst of conversation" captures the texture of social speech in the novel — voices surfacing from and submerging back into the collective hum.

> He was speaking low, making a separate conversation. The others were talking together. [008-256]

The "separate conversation" — the private channel opened within the public gathering — is a recurring pattern. It is in these side conversations that the novel's emotional disclosures occur.

## Understanding Conversation in Richardson's Method

Richardson treats conversation not as a transparent window through which characters reveal themselves, but as an opaque social surface that Miriam's consciousness must interpret. This is different from how most novels handle dialogue.

In a conventional nineteenth-century novel, conversation advances the plot and reveals character directly — a character says what they think, or at least what they intend others to believe. In *Interim*, by contrast, conversation is primarily something Miriam *overhears and filters*. The reader rarely gets direct dialogue; instead, we receive Miriam's consciousness of speech: fragments of overheard talk, tones of voice, the feeling a remark produces in her. The actual words are often less significant than the atmosphere they create.

This technique — rendering conversation through a single perceiving consciousness rather than as objective dialogue — is one of Richardson's central contributions to modernist fiction. It shifts the novel's subject from what is said to what is *experienced in the hearing of it*. The dinner table at Banbury Park is not a stage where characters perform for the reader; it is a sensory field that Miriam navigates, sometimes participating, often withdrawing into the richer world of her own thoughts.

For the modern reader, Richardson's approach may feel unfamiliar at first. There are few dramatic confrontations, few revelatory speeches. Instead, there is the texture of social life as it is actually experienced — fragmentary, ambiguous, half-heard. Conversation matters in *Interim* not for what it communicates but for what it reveals about the difficulty of communication itself.

## Significance

Conversation in *Interim* is never merely informational. It is always also relational — a negotiation of status, intimacy, and power. Richardson's rendering of conversation — fragmentary, interrupted, filtered through Miriam's consciousness — anticipates the modernist treatment of dialogue not as transparent communication but as opaque social performance.

The novel's preoccupation with conversation reflects its deeper theme: the difficulty of genuine connection between separate consciousnesses. Words pass between characters, but understanding is never guaranteed.
