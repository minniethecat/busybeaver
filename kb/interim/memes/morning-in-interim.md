# Morning in _Interim_

**Morning** in _Interim_ is a time of renewed perception and daily ritual. As one of the temporal markers that structure the novel's stream of consciousness, morning carries associations of clarity, work, and the resumption of social life. Richardson renders morning not as an abstract time-unit but as a quality of light, a bodily sensation, and a prompt for action. In a novel so attuned to the rhythms of domestic time, morning is the hinge between the private world of sleep and the public world of social obligation.

## Morning Light

Morning in the novel is first registered through its distinctive light. The "morning light" that tells a character "with shouts" [PN=001-012] captures the abrupt, declarative quality of dawn — light that insists on wakefulness. The personification of light as a shouting presence is characteristic of Richardson's method: sensory phenomena are treated as active agents rather than passive conditions. The "midmorning light" cold on bedroom crockery [PN=002-009] conveys the unflattering clarity of daytime interiors, a sensory sharpness that contrasts with the softer illumination of evening and fireside.

> …sitting her upright in morning light telling her with shouts… [PN=001-012]

The "isolated morning figures" that "hurried across the park" in "sunlit green and gold" [PN=001-015] combine temporal and visual registers — morning is not only a time but a colour and a mood. This synthesis of temporal and sensory experience is characteristic of Richardson's method: the world is never simply chronological; it is chromatically, acoustically, and kinesthetically layered.

## Work and Domestic Routine

Morning in the Baileys' household is defined by work. "A steady morning's work disposed of the correspondence" [PN=002-004] registers morning as the productive portion of the day. Miriam's "morning's work" is "distracted" by thoughts that press upon her attention [PN=003-011] — the tension between the demands of employment and the movements of consciousness is most acute in the morning hours, when the mind must discipline itself into professional shape after the freedom of sleep.

The domestic rhythm of waking is rendered through small sensory details. Getting up "as usual the next morning" [PN=005-005] establishes routine as a background against which departures and exceptions register. The "faint square of brighter light" that drags one "up exhausted in the morning" [PN=004-012] captures the reluctant transition from sleep to consciousness — morning as physical demand rather than invitation.

## Morning and Anticipation

Morning also carries forward the emotional residue of the previous night. "The grey of tomorrow morning kept pressing on her attention" [PN=003-010] describes how morning can feel like a weight rather than a release — the new day brings not relief but the continuation of unresolved feeling. The "first thing in the morning" [PN=004-005] marks the beginning of social time, the moment when the household comes alive with interaction and obligation.

This forward pressure distinguishes Richardson's treatment of morning from the conventional literary association of dawn with renewal. In *Interim*, morning is as likely to bring anxiety as hope — the "grey" of the coming day is not a blank slate but a surface already inscribed with yesterday's unfinished business.

## Morning and the Body

Morning is also a bodily condition. Richardson registers the physical sensations of waking: the stiffness of limbs, the resistance of the eyelids, the gradual re-inhabitation of the body after the abandonment of sleep. These corporeal details root the abstract category of "morning" in the concrete experience of a specific body in a specific room. The morning body is a body in transition — from horizontal to vertical, from private to public, from unconscious to alert — and Richardson traces each stage of this transition with characteristic fidelity to sensory detail.

## Morning as Narrative Structure

Across *Interim*, morning scenes function as structural markers — points of restart that punctuate the narrative flow. Each new morning re-establishes the coordinates of time, place, and social situation, orienting the reader within the novel's densely textured temporal web. The recurrence of morning scenes creates a diurnal rhythm that is one of the novel's most important formal features, anchoring its stream-of-consciousness method in the reliable cycle of the day.
