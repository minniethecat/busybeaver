## Overview

Strangeness pervades *Interim* as both a perceptual mode and a social condition. Things become strange when seen freshly; people become strangers when familiar rituals fail. Miriam's narrative consciousness is drawn repeatedly to moments where the ordinary turns unfamiliar — a room fills with "strange dim lights," music becomes "some strange beast in the room," and acquaintances become "strangers in their own dining-room." Strangeness is the novel's word for the uncanny within the domestic.

## Manifestations

The strange arrives first through perception. Miriam sees "strange dim lights coming in through the uncurtained window" [001-015], a room transformed by twilight into something unfamiliar. Music conjures strangeness: "It was so strange. It came from far back amongst scenes that were gone" [001-012] — sound as archaeological retrieval, the past made strange by its sudden proximity.

Social situations generate their own strangeness. At the Baileys', "There was a stranger sitting at the fireside" [003-002] — the introduction of a new character reshapes the room's geometry. In a more unsettling register, the hosts themselves become alien: "They were becoming strangers in their own dining-room, with far-off thoughts" [004-003]. Miriam reflects "no wonder she had found herself a stranger with these people" [005-005] — strangeness as the felt gap between inner experience and social performance.

The strange also describes the city itself. Walking "the strange dead road east of Bloomsbury" [005-002], Miriam encounters London as a place of suspended familiarity. The "strange new sense of the London medical world" [004-014] marks an encounter with a professional milieu she observes from outside. Even the intimate gathering becomes "forgetfulness amongst strangers near and intimate" [006-028] — a paradox that captures the novel's central insight: closeness and strangeness are not opposites but companions.

## Key Passages

- **[001-015]**: "The room was full of strange dim lights" — the domestic space made unfamiliar by changing light.
- **[001-012]**: "It was so strange. It came from far back amongst scenes that were gone" — music as strangeness, the past made present.
- **[004-003]**: "They were becoming strangers in their own dining-room" — social alienation within the familiar.
- **[005-002]**: "the strange dead road east of Bloomsbury" — the city as site of the uncanny.
- **[006-028]**: "forgetfulness amongst strangers near and intimate" — the paradox of intimacy and estrangement.
