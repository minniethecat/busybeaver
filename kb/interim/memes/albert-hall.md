# Albert Hall

The Royal Albert Hall appears in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* as the site of a failed social outing — a Sunday afternoon excursion to a brass band concert that Miriam Henderson endures rather than enjoys. The episode crystallises the novel's treatment of class, musical taste, gendered expectation, and the awkwardness of social performance. Opened in 1871 as a monument to Victorian cultural aspiration, the Albert Hall was by the early twentieth century a venue of heterogeneous programming — from classical concerts to brass band competitions — and its mixed cultural status makes it an apt setting for the novel's dramatisation of contested taste.

## In the Novel

### The Invitation

Dr. Hurd, a Canadian boarder at the Baileys', proposes taking Miriam to a concert at the Albert Hall. His manner transforms the invitation into something that makes her uncomfortable:

> "I'll get a silk hat before Sunday"; he would prepare for her to go all the way down to the Albert Hall as a young lady being taken to a concert; the Albert Hall on Sunday was brass bands; he thought they were a concert. [006-018]

The gap between Dr. Hurd's understanding and Miriam's is immediately apparent. He sees the Albert Hall as a grand venue warranting a "silk hat" — a gesture of formal courtship that casts Miriam in the role of "a young lady being taken to a concert." Miriam realises that Sunday at the Albert Hall means brass bands, not the orchestral concert Dr. Hurd imagines. His conflation of brass bands with "a concert" marks him as culturally naive in her eyes, while his assumption of a romantic framework imposes a social script she resists. The silk hat — an object of conspicuous formality — becomes an emblem of the mismatch: he dresses for an occasion she does not recognise.

### The Outing

The actual journey to the Albert Hall is rendered as an ordeal:

> Forced to go on and on isolated with the brick-red grin and the splintering green eyes through the afternoon heat, in the midst of a glare of omnibus people, on their way to a brass band in the Albert Hall, thinking they were going to a concert. [007-005]

Richardson's free indirect discourse captures Miriam's entrapment: "forced to go on and on isolated" in the crowded omnibus, surrounded by people but fundamentally alone. The "brick-red grin" and "splintering green eyes" reduce Dr. Hurd to a caricature of masculine confidence — immovable, oblivious, "there for the afternoon." The repetition of "thinking they were going to a concert" underscores the gulf between his perception and hers, a gulf that is simultaneously comic and painful.

The omnibus itself becomes a site of exposure: the "glare of omnibus people" suggests a public visibility that Miriam finds oppressive. She is not merely traveling to a concert; she is being seen traveling, on display as part of a couple whose mismatch is apparent to her if not to her companion.

## Class, Taste, and Cultural Consumption

The Albert Hall episode is, at its deepest level, about the class dimensions of cultural taste. Dr. Hurd's inability to distinguish between a brass band and a concert is not simply a failure of musical knowledge — it is a failure to recognise the distinctions that organise cultural life along class lines. Miriam, whose musical sensibility has been formed by genuine engagement with art music, experiences the brass band as an affront not to snobbery but to sincerity. She does not reject the brass band because it is low; she rejects it because Dr. Hurd cannot tell the difference.

This distinction matters for understanding Richardson's treatment of class in *Interim*. Miriam's cultural discernment is not snobbery — it is a form of perceptual ethics, a commitment to seeing (and hearing) things as they actually are. The Albert Hall episode exposes not Miriam's elitism but the gap between genuine aesthetic experience and the social performance of culture.

## Gender and the Forced Outing

The Albert Hall episode is also a study in gendered constraint. Miriam does not want to go, but she goes. She does not enjoy the concert, but she endures it. The entire outing unfolds under the sign of obligation rather than choice — she is "forced to go on and on" not by physical compulsion but by the social expectation that a young woman must accept a gentleman's invitation graciously. The episode thus dramatises a specifically feminine predicament: the obligation to perform enjoyment, to suppress one's own taste in deference to male initiative, to smile through an afternoon one would rather spend anywhere else.

## Significance

The Albert Hall episode distills a recurring pattern in *Interim*: social occasions that promise connection but deliver isolation. The venue itself — grand, institutional, a monument to Victorian cultural aspiration — becomes the stage for a mismatch of expectations. Dr. Hurd's brass band is not Miriam's concert; his silk hat is not her idea of an afternoon's music. The Albert Hall, like Oxford Street in a different register, exposes the gap between London's promise and its delivery. And in the gendered and class-inflected dynamics of the failed outing, Richardson captures something essential about the social world her protagonist must navigate: its constraints are felt most acutely not in moments of open conflict but in the quiet, enforced endurance of an afternoon that was supposed to be a treat.
