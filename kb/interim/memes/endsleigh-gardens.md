# Endsleigh Gardens

Endsleigh Gardens is the London address where Miriam Henderson lives during the events of Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*. It is a "quiet thoroughfare" — tree-lined, residential, removed from the commercial bustle of the main roads — and its grey house-fronts and upper branches form the visual boundary of Miriam's domestic world. More than a mere street name, Endsleigh Gardens functions in the novel as a sensory and psychological threshold: the place she returns to, the place she leaves from, the fixed point around which her urban movements orbit.

## Overview

Endsleigh Gardens is introduced early as the site of Miriam's lodgings — her room, her latchkey, her private space within the larger city. It is distinguished from the Banbury Park boarding-house, which belongs to the social world of Mrs. Bailey and her boarders; Endsleigh Gardens is Miriam's alone.

> …from the quiet thoroughfare of Endsleigh Gardens, her triumphant faithful latchkey… [002-006]

The "quiet thoroughfare" establishes Endsleigh Gardens as a residential street — not a destination, not a place of commerce or spectacle, but a place of return. The "triumphant faithful latchkey" makes the street personal: it is where Miriam possesses a key, where she has the power to enter and to close the door behind her.

## In the Novel

### A Street of Trees

Richardson returns several times to the trees of Endsleigh Gardens. They are upper branches — visible from windows, framing the sky — and they represent a particular kind of urban nature: managed, contained, but real.

> …the upper branches of the trees in Endsleigh Gardens. A number of people must have… [008-014]

The trees are both intimate and public. Their upper branches are visible to anyone who looks up, and "a number of people" must have seen them. But they are also part of Miriam's private landscape — the view from her room, the green that edges her consciousness.

> …leathery green floored woods of Endsleigh Gardens edged by grey house fronts… [008-009]

The description is dense with sensory language: "leathery green," "floored woods," "grey house fronts." The trees are not pastoral — they are urban trees, their green "leathery" rather than lush, their woods "floored" by pavement. The grey house-fronts edge them, contain them, make them part of the built environment. This is nature as it exists in London: present but domesticated, glimpsed between buildings.

### Threshold and Return

Endsleigh Gardens functions primarily as a place of return — the street Miriam comes back to, the door she unlocks, the room she inhabits when the social performances of the day are done. It is not where the novel's dramatic scenes occur; it is where the protagonist recovers from them.

The latchkey is the symbol of this: "triumphant" because it means possession, autonomy, a space that is hers. In a novel structured around Miriam's movements through boarding-houses, dinner tables, and London streets, Endsleigh Gardens is the still centre — the place that is not performed but simply inhabited.

## Significance

Endsleigh Gardens is the novel's most important domestic location after Banbury Park, and it serves a complementary function. Where Banbury Park is social, collective, and charged with the dynamics of the boarding-house, Endsleigh Gardens is private, solitary, and quiet. The two locations define the poles of Miriam's London existence: the public self that performs at dinner and the private self that unlocks a door in a quiet thoroughfare and is, at last, alone.
