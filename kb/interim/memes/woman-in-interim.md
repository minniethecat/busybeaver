## Overview

While *Interim* gives sustained attention to women collectively — their social worlds, labour, and relationships — it also trains its gaze on the singular woman: the individual female figure caught in a moment of perception, a gesture, a sentence. This entry tracks the singular, as distinct from the collective registered in [[women-in-interim]]. The individual woman appears as narrator (Miriam observing herself), as object of observation (other women in rooms), and as ideal (womanhood as cultural aspiration).

Richardson's treatment of the individual woman is characteristically double: the observing consciousness is itself female, yet it observes other women with a detachment that borders on ethnographic. This doubleness — woman as both subject and object of the novel's gaze — is one of *Interim*'s most distinctive formal features.

## Manifestations

The novel opens with a typology: "No woman who did typewriting from morning till night" [001-014] — woman defined by labour, by the absence of leisure that the Baileys' world assumes. This working woman is the novel's baseline, the condition against which other femininities are measured.

Miriam observes other women with the same intensity she applies to rooms and music. "This dingy woman playing with the directness and precision of a man was like some strange beast in the room" [002-009] — the woman pianist is rendered strange, powerful, almost inhuman in her competence. At another gathering, "the woman had crossed over to a chair near the window" [005-004] — a simple movement rendered significant by the quality of attention it receives.

The novel also registers womanhood as cultural category: "white gracious adorable womanhood given her by Dr. Heber" [006-026] — an ideal bestowed from outside, a costume as much as an identity. The "gentlewoman" [006-003] appears as a class-inflected category, marking the distance between Miriam's working life and the world she moves through. The "bulky woman in a high pure girlish voice" [006-005] captures the dissonance between body and performance, between what a woman is and how she sounds.

Against these observed figures stands Miriam's own self-consciousness: "She was an Englishwoman. But her mind was so at home elsewhere" [004-009] — national and gendered identity as a fact one inhabits but does not fully own.

## The Observed Woman

Richardson consistently registers the physical presence of individual women with a painter's attention to detail. On a journey, Miriam's attention is "drawn towards the face of the woman sitting opposite to her; a shadowy face" [004-011] — the woman on the train, anonymous yet fully present, a face that becomes a study. At a party, "there were two men and another woman. They stood about in the gloomy firelight" [005-003] — the woman positioned by negation, "another," defined by her relation to the group.

These observed women are rarely given names. They are figures in a composition, their significance deriving from the quality of Miriam's attention rather than from narrative function. The "stout Italian woman" at the lodging house [008-020] appears in a rush of multilingual sound — "Gemma; Bayatrichay" — a fragment of foreign life glimpsed and gone. The "simple woman" [008-160] and the "Canadian woman" with "that circular jaw movement" [009-098] are each caught in a single characteristic gesture, a moment of perception that defines them.

## Intersections with Class

The individual woman in *Interim* is always also a classed woman. The "gentlewoman" [006-003] inhabits a different category from the woman who types. The "widow woman" whom lodgers exploit [010-093] belongs to yet another economic reality. Richardson refuses to separate gender from the material conditions in which it is lived.

This intersection is sharpest in Miriam herself: a woman who works for wages, moves through middle-class drawing rooms, and carries within her a consciousness shaped by books, music, and solitude. She is, in the novel's own terms, both the observer and the observed — the woman who sees and the woman who is seen.

## Womanhood as Performance and Constraint

Throughout the novel, womanhood appears as something performed under observation. The Banbury Park gatherings display women performing for one another: voices, postures, opinions calibrated to the room. The woman pianist at the Brooms' is judged by how she plays — "the directness and precision of a man" [002-009] — the compliment itself revealing the gendered expectations it defies.

Miriam is acutely aware of womanhood as constraint: "a woman must march with her regiment" [009-080]. This military metaphor for social conformity captures the disciplined nature of feminine performance. Yet the novel also records moments of escape: "She was an Englishwoman. But her mind was so at home elsewhere" [004-009] — interiority as freedom, the mind wandering beyond the categories that contain the body.

The doctor's lecture on "the Mosaic fable about woman" [008-126] introduces a mythic dimension — woman as theological problem, as ancient story. Miriam's interest is characteristic: she listens, registers, does not argue. The novel's feminism, if it can be called that, operates through accumulation of observed detail rather than argument.

- **[002-009]**: "This dingy woman playing with the directness and precision of a man" — the woman pianist, competence made strange.
- **[004-009]**: "She was an Englishwoman. But her mind was so at home elsewhere" — national-gendered identity and its limits.

- **[006-026]**: "white gracious adorable womanhood" — womanhood as bestowed ideal, external and performed.
