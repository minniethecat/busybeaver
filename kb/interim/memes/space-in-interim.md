# Space in *Interim*

Space in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* is never a passive container for action. It is a sensory environment that shapes consciousness — warm or cold, bright or dim, enclosed or exposed, private or public. The novel moves through a geography of spaces: the boarding-house rooms at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]], the London streets between them, and the thresholds — [[door-in-interim|doors]], [[window-in-interim|windows]], staircases — that connect one space to another.

## In the Novel

The novel's spatial world is built around a fundamental division: inside and outside. Inside, there are the rooms of domestic life — dining room, drawing-room, bedrooms, kitchen. Outside, there are the London streets, the gardens glimpsed through windows, the "maze of little roads" [001-015] that Miriam walks through. Between them stand the thresholds: the front door that shuts with a "shallow suburban slam" [001-001], the "cold window space" [002-004] where interior warmth meets exterior chill, the "stained glass door of the little greenhouse" [001-015] that admits a strange dim light.

> There was nothing but the cool sense of life pouring from some inner source and the deep fresh spaces of the darkness all round her. [001-012]

This is space as Richardson renders it: not measured but felt, not described but inhabited. The "deep fresh spaces of the darkness" are outside — the garden at night, the quiet neighbourhood beyond the curtained windows — and the "inner source" is inside, the self from which life pours. The two spaces, inner and outer, interpenetrate.

## Domestic Space at Banbury Park

The boarding-house at Banbury Park is the novel's primary spatial container. It is a house of distinct zones, each with its own character.

| Space | Character | Social Register |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Dining room | Gaslight, long table, "buzz of conversation" | Public, shared, performative |
| Drawing-room | Firelight, armchairs, piano | Semi-private, intimate |
| Bedrooms | Privacy, cold, "the freshness and quietude of the room" [001-001] | Private, solitary |
| Kitchen | Unseen labour, Christine's domain | Hidden, service |
| Hall and passage | Transitional, "the smiling familiar surprise of the hall" [011-002] | Liminal, between spaces |
| Staircase | Vertical movement between public and private | Threshold between social and solitary |

> When the door was shut the freshness and quietude of the room enfolded Miriam, smoothing away grubbiness and fatigue. [001-001]

The bedroom, once the door is shut, becomes a space of restoration. The room does not merely contain Miriam — it enfolded her, actively smoothing away the residue of the journey. Space in Richardson is not passive; it acts upon the person within it.

The distinction between shared and private space is central to the boarding-house experience. The dining room is a space of exposure, where Miriam must perform sociability at the communal table. The bedroom is a space of retreat, where she can be alone. The movement between these spaces — navigated through the hall, the [[stair-in-interim|staircase]], the "door of retreat always open" [001-016] — is what makes the boarding-house livable. Knowing one can withdraw is what permits one to stay.

> Left alone with silence all along the street, Christine inaudible in the kitchen, dead silence in the house, Miriam gathered up her blouse and ran upstairs. [001-015]

The house in silence is a different kind of space — emptied, quiet, a container for solitude. The three spatial zones — street, kitchen, house — are named in sequence, each with its own register of sound or silence. Miriam's movement upstairs is a movement toward the private, toward the space where she can be alone.

## London as Spatial Experience

The street is the novel's essential outside. Richardson renders London not as a panorama but as a series of sensory encounters with specific places and spaces: the Farringdon Road, Oxford Street, Wimpole Street, the squares and side streets that Miriam walks through.

> It drew her out into the quiet neighbourhood... misty darkness along empty roads, plaques of lamplight here and there on pavements and across house fronts... blackness in large gardens and over the bridge. [001-012]

The street is rendered as a sequence of spatial impressions — darkness, lamplight, gardens, bridge — each marking a different quality of the urban night. Richardson's London is not the London of landmarks and monuments but of side streets and residential roads, the spaces between familiar interiors.

> She wandered along the little roads turning and turning until she came to a broad open thoroughfare lined with high grey houses standing back behind colourless railed-in gardens. [001-015]

The movement from narrow to broad, from "little roads" to "broad open thoroughfare," registers a change in spatial scale that is also a change in social world. The "high grey houses" with their "railed-in gardens" signal a grander, more imposing London. Space in the novel carries class meaning: the size of a house, the presence of railings, the width of a street — these are not neutral architectural facts but social indices.

> He had not felt that they were walking along, outside London, in blank space, free, and exactly alike in their thoughts. [005-002]

Walking outside London, in what feels like "blank space," the city's spatial order dissolves. The familiar geography of neighbourhoods and streets gives way to an undifferentiated outside. This is a moment of spatial freedom — "free," "exactly alike" — in which the constraints of urban domestic space are temporarily suspended.

## Interior vs. Exterior

The boundary between inside and outside is one of the novel's persistent preoccupations. Windows, doors, and walls define this boundary, and Richardson is attentive to what passes across it: light, sound, cold, the presence of other people.

> Behind the curtains and the Venetian blinds the windows were open at the top letting in the garden air. [001-012]

The window open at the top — a small aperture, a modest concession to the outside — lets the garden air into the bedroom. The curtains and blinds are barriers, but they are permeable. The outside is never fully excluded from interior space.

> Triumphant echoes filled its wide spaces, pressed against the windows, filtered out into the quiet street out and away into London. [002-009]

Here the movement is from inside out: music fills the "wide spaces" of the room, presses against the windows, filters through into the street, and dissipates into the larger city. Space in the novel is not sealed but connected. The music played in the drawing-room travels outward, linking domestic interior to urban exterior.

> She paused at the open drawing-room door to listen to the faint faraway subterranean sounds coming from the kitchen. [002-008]

The house is a vertical stack of spaces, and sound travels between them. The "faint faraway subterranean sounds" from the kitchen reach the drawing-room door — domestic labour audible at the threshold of leisure. Space in Richardson connects as much as it separates. The kitchen, though unseen, is never entirely absent from the rooms above.

> The empty room was waiting for the next event, a spread meal, voices sounding towards a centre, distracting attention from its increasing shabbiness... there was never long for it to remain sounding its shabbiness, the sound of dust, into the empty space. [010-001]

An empty room is not neutral. It "was waiting" — possessed of a kind of intentionality, oriented toward the next social gathering that will fill it. And it has a sound: "the sound of dust, into the empty space." Richardson's attention to the acoustic quality of empty rooms — the specific silence of a room that is ordinarily full — is part of her larger rendering of space as sensorily alive.

## Spatial Thresholds

Thresholds — doors, windows, staircases, the conservatory — are the novel's most charged spaces. They are neither fully inside nor fully outside, and it is in these liminal zones that consciousness is most alert.

The conservatory or "little greenhouse" at Banbury Park is a threshold space between house and garden, domestic interior and natural exterior. It is neither room nor outdoors, and its light is neither daylight nor gaslight but a "strange dim light" filtered through stained glass.

> The room was full of strange dim lights coming in through the stained glass door of the little greenhouse. [001-015]

The "strange dim lights" — plural, not singular — suggest a space of multiple sources and uncertain illumination. The conservatory transforms light, and the light it transmits transforms the room. This is Richardson's spatial method in miniature: space is not a fixed container but a field of changing sensory qualities.

> She pushed open the glass door turning the light to a soft green and sat sociably down in a low chair her hands clasped upon her knees, topics racing through her mind in a voice thrilling with stored up laughter. [001-015]

Pushing open the glass door changes the colour of the light — "turning the light to a soft green." Space is altered by the action of passing through it, and the alteration is registered through the senses. The "soft green" light creates a particular mood, a particular kind of space, in which Miriam sits "sociably" — alone but oriented toward the company that will return.

The staircase is another threshold space, marking the boundary between downstairs sociability and upstairs privacy. To go upstairs is to withdraw from the collective into the solitary; to come downstairs is to re-enter the social world.

> They all went busily upstairs. Even Grace did not linger. [001-014]

The collective movement upstairs is a ritual of dispersal — the end of an evening, the close of a shared time. The staircase is where characters shed their public selves and return to the private self of the bedroom.

> She crept downstairs through the silent empty house, pausing at the open drawing-room door to listen to the faint faraway subterranean sounds coming from the kitchen. [002-008]

Creeping downstairs through a silent house — the movement is stealthy, almost transgressive. The house when empty is a different spatial order from the house when full. Miriam moves through it differently, and her pause at the open door — suspended between the staircase and the drawing-room, between upstairs and downstairs — is a moment of pure spatial attention.

> A door opened as she turned the staircase. [002-006]

The synchronisation of door and staircase — one opens as the other is turned — suggests a house organised around thresholds, a space where transitions between zones are charged with significance. Richardson's doors and staircases mark not just architectural divisions but shifts in the quality of experience.

## The "Cold Window Space"

One of the novel's most distinctive spatial phenomena is the "cold window space" — the zone near the glass where the outside temperature penetrates. This microclimate within the room is a recurring motif, registering Richardson's attention to the thermal gradient of interior space.

> She sat working until she grew so cold that she knew if she stayed on in the cold window space she would have the beginning of a cold. [002-004]

The "cold window space" is a distinct spatial zone within the room — not the room as a whole but the area near the window, where the outside cold presses through the glass. Richardson's attention to this microclimate reveals her phenomenological method: space is not uniform but differentiated, with zones of warmth and cold, comfort and discomfort.

## Significance

Space in *Interim* is the medium in which consciousness unfolds. The novel's movement between rooms, between inside and outside, between public and private, is not incidental but structural — it is the principle by which the narrative organises itself in the absence of conventional plot.

Richardson's attention to spatial thresholds — doors, windows, staircases, the conservatory — reveals her deeper interest in states of betweenness. The novel's title names a temporal interim, but the novel itself is saturated with spatial interims: the doorway where one is neither in nor out, the window where one is both inside and outside at once, the staircase where one is suspended between public and private. These liminal spaces are the novel's true territory, and in them Richardson finds the conditions for the modernist consciousness she is inventing: alert, permeable, suspended between states.

This treatment of space is central to what makes Richardson a modernist. She does not describe rooms; she renders them as they are experienced — through the body, through the senses, through the consciousness that inhabits them. Space is not outside the self but around it, pressing in with its light and warmth and expectation, shaping what can be thought and felt within it.
