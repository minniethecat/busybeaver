# Gower Street

Gower Street appears in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* primarily as a railway station — a point of arrival and departure, a threshold where the underground railway surfaces into the London street. It is part of the city's commuting geography, familiar to Miriam Henderson as a landmark in her daily movements through the urban landscape.

## Overview

Gower Street station, on what is now part of the London Underground's Metropolitan line, serves as a node in Miriam's London. It is where she alights, where she waits, where the subterranean journey gives way to the street-level city. Richardson renders it with characteristic sensory precision: the platform, the light, the particular quality of air that belongs to underground stations.

> …up and down the platform at Gower Street. Its familiar sulphurous gloom… [004-008]

The "sulphurous gloom" is precise and evocative — the smell of coal smoke and steam, the dimness of a covered platform, the particular atmosphere of Victorian railway infrastructure. The gloom is "familiar," which is key: Gower Street is not a discovery but a known quantity, a landmark embedded in the routine of Miriam's commuting life.

## In the Novel

### A Temporal Marker

Gower Street stations appears at moments of temporal awareness — it is where Miriam registers the time, notes the hour, situates herself in the diurnal rhythm.

> At Gower Street it was eleven o'clock. She wandered… [004-012]

The sentence links place and time directly: at Gower Street, at eleven o'clock. The station is a clock — a point in the city where time is marked and registered. From here, Miriam's wandering begins, her trajectory through the Euston Road and beyond.

### Urban Threshold

The area around Gower Street serves as a threshold between different zones of the city — and different modes of Miriam's experience.

> They had reached the Gower Street curb and stood waiting to plunge… [006-024]

The "curb" is a literal edge — the boundary between pavement and roadway — but also a figurative one. To "stand waiting to plunge" is to hesitate at a threshold, to gather oneself before entering the flow of urban traffic. Gower Street is the place where this hesitation occurs.

### A Room in Gower Street

The street is also the site of domestic space — a large room mentioned in conversation, part of the geography of residences and visits that extends beyond the Banbury Park boarding-house.

> …a curtain; in a large room in Gower Street, I can't imagine why you say… [008-145]

The "large room in Gower Street" is glimpsed rather than described — a space whose existence is noted but whose interior remains opaque to the reader. This is typical of Richardson's urban representation: places exist in the novel as nodes in a network, their interiors only occasionally illuminated.

## Significance

Gower Street exemplifies the role of transport infrastructure in Richardson's London. Stations, platforms, curbs — these are not merely background but active elements of urban experience. The "sulphurous gloom" of Gower Street is as much a character in the novel as any human figure: familiar, consistent, marking the transitions between underground and surface, between journey and arrival, between the public city and the private interior.
