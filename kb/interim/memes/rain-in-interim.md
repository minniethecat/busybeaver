## Introduction

Rain is a persistent atmospheric presence in *Interim*, threading through the novel as both a literal weather condition and a carrier of memory and mood. It accompanies arrivals, punctuates departures, and saturates the London streetscape that Miriam traverses. More than mere background, rain becomes a marker of interior states — anticipation, solitude, renewal.

## Arrival and Welcome

Miriam arrives at the Tansley Street boarding-house in the rain. "Never mind the mud, come in out of the rain, scolded Mrs. Philps backing towards the fire" [001-001], the landlady's scolding carrying the warmth of welcome. The domestic hearth and the wet outdoors are set in immediate contrast. In her memory, rain is associated with possibility: "In her ears was the rush of spring rain on the garden foliage, and presently a voice saying where are we going this summer?" [001-015]. The sound of rain opens a portal to anticipated pleasures — the garden leaves "washed clean by yesterday's rain" [001-015] gleaming "crisply, their yellow and green enamel" refreshed.

## Solitude and the City

Rain isolates and accompanies Miriam through London's streets. Walking home from an evening engagement, "rapid walking in the rain-freshened air relieved her faintness but she dreaded waking in the night" [004-012] — the rain both revives and exposes her vulnerability. "She wandered slowly home through the drizzling rain warmed and fed and with a glow at her heart" [004-012], the drizzle becoming a gentle companion. Eve's departure brings a different rain: "The rain gently thudding on the roof and rattling against the landing skylight was Eve's rain" [004-004], the possessive "Eve's rain" marking how rain absorbs the emotional colour of each moment.

## Social Threshold

Rain hovers at the edges of social interaction. Mrs. Bailey "waited silent a moment before asking if it rained" [004-013], the question a tentative bridge across an awkward silence. Miriam, distracted, "glanced at the outside London world and was lost in interchanging scenes" [004-013], the rain outside dissolving the boundary between inner and outer experience. "Was it raining? … In a dry professional official voice she said gazing at the hearthrug … no, at least oh yes, I think it is raining" [004-013], her uncertainty revealing how the weather has become secondary to the currents of consciousness flowing beneath.
