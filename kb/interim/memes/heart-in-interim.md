# Heart in _Interim_

The **heart** in _Interim_ functions as the novel's central metaphor for emotional interiority — not the sentimental organ of romance but a physical register through which feeling is experienced in the body. Miriam's consciousness repeatedly returns to the beating heart as the site where external events register as physiological sensation.

## Heart as Emotional Barometer

The heart in Richardson's narrative registers emotional shifts before conscious thought can articulate them. When Miriam feels an evening revelation strike "straight into her heart" [PN=001-012], the heart receives what the mind has not yet named — the joy of belonging or the ache of exclusion arrives as a bodily event. A heavy mood "settling heavily down upon her heart" [PN=001-013] transforms diffuse unease into a located physical weight, the heart bearing what consciousness cannot yet process.

At moments of heightened awareness, Miriam listens "with loudly beating heart" [PN=002-008], the heart's acceleration marking the threshold between ordinary perception and charged anticipation. The heart beats loudest in silence, when the social world recedes and the body's own rhythms become audible.

> …she looked up into a little face whose every feature was ready, attentive, and could not speak for the beating of her heart… [PN=004-012]

## Heart and Music

The piano becomes a conduit for heart-feeling. Miriam perceives music as if "the piano were heartbroken" [PN=002-009], the instrument transmitting emotional states that words cannot carry. The heartbreak of the piano is not personal grief but a quality of sound — music as the expression of feeling that has no other outlet.

This connection between heart and music links the private body to the shared space of the [[music-in-interim|drawing-room]], where feeling circulates without being named.

## Heart and Social Encounter

The heart in *Interim* is also the organ of social vulnerability. When Miriam enters the doctor's consulting room, she is "hardly able to speak for the beating of her heart" [004-012], her body betraying an agitation that her composed face conceals. The heart beats recklessly at the thought of writing a letter: "her heart beat recklessly round the certainty of writing and posting her letter" [009-098] — anticipation and intention registering in the pulse before they reach the hand that will write.

In conversation, the heart reveals what speech withholds. The elderly couple's familiar banter — "Bless my heart! They talked familiarly on, two tired worn people in a little blaze of mutual congratulation" [009-099] — shows the heart invoked in ordinary idiom, worn familiarity taking the place of the romantic palpitations the word might suggest.

## The Heart as Centre and Margin

Richardson deploys the heart spatially as well as emotionally. The "glow of life was left there at the heart of the circle by the fire" [003-010], locating the heart at the domestic hearth — a centre of warmth around which the Bailey household arranges itself. Yet the heart can also lie outside oneself: Miriam perceives another presence "alive and real in the heart of the London darkness" [005-005], projecting the heart-centre outward into the city's vast obscurity. The heart maps not just inner states but spatial and social geometries.

## The Metaphorical Heart

Beyond the literal organ, Richardson uses "heart" to designate centres of intensity. The fire at the "heart of the circle" [PN=003-010] in the [[room-in-interim|Bailey sitting-room]] names a spatial centre — the hearth as the gathering point around which domestic life arranges itself. The presence of another person, "alive and real in the heart of the London darkness" [PN=005-005], places the heart at the convergence of intimacy and the vast indifferent city.
