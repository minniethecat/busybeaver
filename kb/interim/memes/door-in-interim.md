# Door in *Interim*

In the confined geography of the boarding-house, doors matter. They separate rooms and the lives within them; they admit or exclude; they frame the moment of entry — into a conversation, a confidence, a solitude. Richardson's attention to doors is part of her larger attention to the sensory texture of domestic space, and the thresholds they mark are rarely neutral.

## In the Novel

The novel begins at a doorstep — "Gladstone bag down on to the doorstep" [001-001] — and doors remain structurally active throughout. Characters knock, wait, enter, close doors behind them. Each door marks a boundary between one social zone and another: the dining room with its public gaslight, the drawing room with its fireside intimacy, the bedrooms with their private consciousness.

> The door of retreat always open. [001-016]

This is the novel's most concentrated statement about doors. The "door of retreat" — open, available, never closed — is the condition that makes social life bearable. Knowing one can leave is what permits one to stay.

## Doors and Their Meanings

| Door | Setting | What It Marks |
|------|---------|--------------|
| Front doorstep | Arrival, departure | The boundary between street and house |
| Dining-room door | Meals, gatherings | Entry into shared domestic ritual |
| Drawing-room door | Evenings, conversation | The threshold of social intimacy |
| Bedroom doors | "Opposing bedroom doors" [001-015] | Privacy, the separateness of lives |
| Frosted door | Restaurants, public spaces | The cold barrier between inside and outside |

> A door opened as she turned the staircase. [002-006]

The door opens not in response to a knock but as Miriam passes — a synchronisation of movement and opening that suggests a world alert to her presence. Richardson's doors often open at charged moments, as if the house itself is aware.

## Key Passages

> Miriam going with her to the door — No, you're to rest — I don't want you to come to the door with me. [001-014]

The small ritual of seeing someone to the door — the offer, the refusal, the negotiation — is rendered with the attention usually reserved for significant plot events. In Richardson's method, the minor social exchange at the threshold carries as much weight as any melodramatic crisis.

> She paused at the open drawing-room door to listen to the faint faraway music. [002-008]

The open door as frame: Miriam does not enter but pauses at the threshold, listening. The door marks the boundary between the hallway's transit and the room's enclosure, and she occupies that boundary — neither in nor out, suspended in the interim.

> The dining-room door was ajar showing a faint light. [004-013]

The door "ajar" — neither open nor closed — is the novel's characteristic door. It suggests a world that is neither fully public nor fully private, a domestic space of partial disclosures. The "faint light" visible through the gap is invitation and mystery in equal measure.

> Dr. Hurd closed the door behind them. [008-023]

Sometimes a door closed is the significant action. The closing marks the beginning of a private conversation, a zone of confidence. What happens after the door closes is different in kind from what happens before — the threshold changes the nature of the exchange.

## Significance

Doors in *Interim* are not decorative. They are the structural joints of the novel's domestic world, and they carry the emotional weight of transitions. To arrive at a door, to knock, to wait, to enter, to close — these are acts charged with social meaning, and Richardson registers them with the precision she brings to all sensory experience.

This attention to thresholds is part of what makes Richardson a modernist. She does not simply describe rooms; she describes the movement between them, the moment of crossing. The door is where consciousness shifts — from outside to inside, from solitude to company, from one version of the self to another.
