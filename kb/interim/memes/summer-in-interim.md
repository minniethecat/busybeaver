# Summer in *Interim*

*Interim* takes place in winter — the weeks around Christmas and New Year — yet summer is everywhere present as anticipation and memory. Characters ask "where are we going this summer?" [001-015]; Miriam remembers "the little square of summer garden" [001-012]; the season to come structures the present with the promise of change. Summer is the novel's temporal horizon — the future that makes the winter interim bearable.

## In the Novel

Summer appears in three modes: as memory of past summers, as anticipation of the summer to come, and as an implied contrast to the present cold. Each mode carries a different emotional charge, but all three share a common function: summer is what winter is not. It represents warmth where the novel gives us cold, freedom where it gives us enclosure, light where it gives us early darkness.

> Where are we going this summer? [001-015]

The question — casual, social — carries existential weight. "Where are we going" is both a practical query about holiday plans and an unspoken question about the direction of a life. Summer is the season when such questions become answerable.

## Summer's Modalities

| Mode | Manifestation | Emotional Register |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| Anticipated summer | "Where are we going this summer?" [001-015] | Hope, uncertainty |
| Remembered summer | "The little square of summer garden" [001-012] | Nostalgia, warmth |
| Contrasted summer | The cold, dark present measured against remembered warmth | Longing, endurance |
| Social summer | "The league garden party in the summer" [001-017] | Obligation, performance |
| Natural summer | Sunlight, foliage, open windows | Sensory abundance |

> By the time they came back she would be there in the spring. [001-015]

Summer is approached through spring — "by the time they came back" the season will have turned. The sentence locates Miriam in a future that is already being imagined, a temporal position the novel frequently occupies: the present experienced as what will have been, the future already taking shape in anticipation.

## Key Passages

> The little square of summer garden showed brilliantly in the sun. [001-012]

Summer compressed into a "little square" — the garden as the season's visible token. The brilliance of the remembered sun is undiminished by the smallness of the space; summer is not measured in acres but in intensity of light.

> The league garden party in the summer. She could have warned Grace. [001-017]

The garden party — summer's social ritual — remembered with a hint of social anxiety. "She could have warned Grace" suggests some past awkwardness, a summer occasion that did not go as planned. Summer is not idyllic in Richardson; it has its own social pressures, its own failures of ease.

> The rush of spring rain on the garden foliage. [001-015]

Spring rain — not yet summer but the promise of it. The "rush" of rain on foliage is a sound of abundance, of growth, of the natural world stirring toward summer's fullness. Richardson hears summer before she sees it.

## Significance

Summer in *Interim* is the season that is not there — the absent presence that frames the novel's winter world. Its function is structural: by keeping summer in view as memory and anticipation, Richardson prevents the winter interim from becoming the whole of existence. There will be a summer; the cold will end; the question "where are we going this summer?" will be answered.

This is the temporal equivalent of the novel's spatial dynamic — the sea as the absent horizon, summer as the absent season. Both exist in the same mode: as what is not present but is nonetheless real, shaping the present by their imagined eventual arrival.
