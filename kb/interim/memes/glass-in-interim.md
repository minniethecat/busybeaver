# Glass in _Interim_

**Glass** in _Interim_ is a persistent material presence that mediates between interior and exterior worlds. Richardson's narrator registers glass not as neutral transparency but as a surface that frames, distorts, or distances — whether as window, drinking vessel, spectacle lens, or frosted door. Glass both enables perception and announces its limits.

## Glass Windows and Coloured Light

The novel's most vivid glass image is architectural and ecclesiastical. Miriam imagines "a little alabaster church with coloured glass windows and a place inside for a candle" [PN=001-011], the stained glass transforming light into colour and the church interior into a space of transformed vision. The passage links glass to the aesthetic desire for filtered, beautified light — a contrast with the novel's more common grey London daylight.

> …a little alabaster church with coloured glass windows and a place inside for a candle… [PN=001-011]

## Frosted Glass and Social Opacity

Glass that obscures rather than reveals carries social meaning. "Behind them was again frosted glass. It was not so grand as Baker Street" [PN=004-012] uses frosted glass as a class marker — the semi-opaque surface signalling a setting less refined than the transparent shopfronts of a better district. Glass here is not a window but a screen, maintaining the separation between private and public space.

## Glasses and the Framed Gaze

Spectacles recur as a perceptual frame. "Her glasses, the mystery of her rigid smallness" [PN=001-017] renders glasses as an alienating device, part of the strangeness Miriam perceives in another woman. Later, a face appears "lost beyond the rim of her glasses" [PN=008-014], the spectacle lens functioning as a boundary that the gaze cannot cross. "The high polished glass-doored bookcase, scenes from a former life" [PN=001-015] extends the glass motif to domestic furniture, where glass protects and distances cherished objects.

## Glass as Domestic Surface

Miriam's world is full of glass objects noted in passing — the "glass-doored bookcase" [PN=001-015], drinking glasses on tables, the glint of glass in lamplight. These surfaces catch and redirect light, creating the shimmer that is characteristic of Richardson's impressionist method. Glass is the medium through which the world is seen and the surface on which light declares itself.
