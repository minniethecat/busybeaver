# Dance in _Interim_

**Dance** in _Interim_ operates on multiple registers — as literal physical movement, as social ritual marked by dress and occasion, and as a metaphor for the rhythmic flow of life itself. Richardson's narrator moves between these levels fluidly, a danced blouse becoming, within the same paragraph, a figure for how one might move through a life.

## Dance as Playful Gesture

The novel's first dance reference is intimate and unguarded. Miriam "took the blouse by its shoulders and danced it up and down in time to the music" [PN=001-013] — a private pantomime performed alone in a room. The dance here is not a social event but a spontaneous bodily response to music, a moment of unselfconscious play rare in the novel's more guarded emotional landscape.

> …took the blouse by its shoulders and danced it up and down in time to the music… [PN=001-013]

## Dance Dresses and Social Occasion

The dance as formal event surfaces through its costume. The narrator recalls "the dresses they had worn at the dance talking to their mother indoors" [PN=001-017], the dance existing not as choreography but as the memory of fabric and conversation. The dresses hold the event, carrying its social meaning long after the music has stopped. "Tight dance buttonholes pinned on" [PN=006-014] likewise registers the dance through its material accessories — the buttonhole that marks a man as a dancer.

## Dance as Life-Metaphor

Richardson's most expansive use of dance is metaphorical. To "dance and sing through life to a handful of tunes" [PN=006-019] imagines existence itself as a kind of choreography — a set of movements performed to a limited repertoire. The metaphor is both liberating (life as dance) and constraining (only a "handful of tunes"). Later, "it was like a sort of dance, everyone coming and going politely" [PN=006-028] extends the figure to social interaction itself, the comings and goings of daily life acquiring the formal grace of partnered movement.

## The Body in Motion

Throughout these variations, dance anchors the novel's attention to the body. Whether a blouse animated by music or a life lived as movement, dance insists that consciousness is not disembodied — it moves, gestures, and responds to rhythm. The danced blouse is a small rebellion against the novel's more frequent scenes of sitting still, waiting, and enduring.
