# Rose in _Interim_

The rose in _Interim_ is less a botanical fact than a quality of perception — a colour that emerges at the intersection of light and matter, warmth and beauty. Richardson's rose is the glow of firelight on copper, the faded silk of a drawing-room piano, the Mystic Rose of Dante's heaven glimpsed through a lecture on Italian poetry.

## Rose-Gold Light

The novel's most sustained rose imagery belongs to firelight. "From its wide clear core striped by black bars a broad rose-gold shaft glowed out across the room reaching the copper vessels on the black oak sideboard" [004-007]. The fire's rose-gold is a transformative light — it touches ordinary objects and makes them radiant, "bringing a sense of the freedom of wealth to have things out of season" [004-007].

This rose-gold light recurs throughout the novel as a signature of heightened perception. "The rosy gold was deepening and spreading" [008-016] — the light is always in motion, never static, a process rather than a state. At the novel's close, Miriam faces "the rose-gold world, blossoming under her eyes" [008-147], the rose-gold having expanded from a single fireside shaft to encompass the entire visible world.

## The Piano's Rose

The drawing-room piano is described through rose materials: "the rosewood case, the faded rose silk pleating strained taut, its margin hidden under a rosewood trellis" [002-008]. The rose here is not colour but substance — the piano is made of rose, its case and silk and trellis all variations on the same tonal family. The piano is an English domestic object, "perfectly wrought and finished, music swathed and hidden in elegance" [002-008], and the rose materials are part of its genteel femininity.

## The Mystic Rose

In a passage that sweeps through Dante scholarship, "the Mystic Rose" [008-020] appears as a theological image — the celestial rose of Paradise into which the blessed are gathered. But Richardson immediately brings it down to earth: Gemma Donati, Dante's wife, is imagined "busily making puddings in the world lit by the light of the Mystic Rose" [008-020], then "a silent Italian woman in a hot kitchen scolding, left out of the mystic rose" [008-020]. The sacred image is juxtaposed with domestic labour, the transcendent with the mundane. Rose is both heaven and kitchen, vision and exclusion.

## Rose as Adornment

Eve Henderson appears with "her crimson-rose bloom" [006-017] — the rose as complexion, the flush of youth and beauty. And Dr. Heber's vision of Miriam as an "ideal old-English rose and white gracious adorable womanhood" [006-026] uses the rose as a category of feminine beauty — the English rose, pale and blooming, an ideal that Miriam carries within her as a "secret unfathomable abundance" [006-026] but cannot inhabit.
