## Introduction

Fear in *Interim* is rarely dramatic. It flickers at the edges of social occasions, darkens the "velvet lustre" of a visitor's eyes [001-014], and lives in the small anxieties of domestic life. Miriam's fear is the fear of being "left out of things" [002-002], of being seen and misunderstood, of the silence that follows her piano playing. It is an atmospheric emotion, more weather than event.

## Social Fear

The fear of social exposure runs through the novel. Grace asks "with fear in her eyes" [001-017] whether her aunt will call, the domestic hierarchy producing real anxiety. Miriam fears that her piano playing has been "a sentimental exhibition of unneeded life," that she has "exposed herself and it meant nothing" [002-009]. The fear is not of danger but of irrelevance — of being present without mattering. Even Mrs. Bailey, "producing one by one, fearfully, in a low motherly encouraging tone the things she had arranged beforehand" [002-007], reveals the fear beneath social performance.

## The Fear of Being Left Out

Aloneness and fear intertwine. Miriam reflects that "the only misery in being alone was the fear of being left out of things" [002-002] — solitude itself is tolerable, but the exclusion it implies is not. This fear is tied to her position as a boarder, a young woman without family or fixed place, moving through other people's houses and other people's lives. The "fearful clangour" of St. Pancras bells [002-003] externalizes this inner state, the city's noise a reminder of all the life happening elsewhere.

## Fear and Beauty

The novel does not separate fear from beauty. Moszkowski's Serenade on the Baileys' piano sounds "fearfully pathetic" [002-009] — the adverb "fearfully" meaning both "dreadfully" and "with something of fear about it." Music, in its capacity to make feeling audible, reveals that fear and beauty are not opposites but companions. Miriam plays on, the fear not overcome but incorporated, made part of the sound.
