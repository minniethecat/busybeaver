# Book in _Interim_

The **book** in _Interim_ represents intellectual life, private contemplation, and the world of ideas that Miriam Henderson inhabits alongside the social world of the Baileys' boarding house. Books appear as physical objects, as topics of conversation, and as markers of cultural identity throughout the novel.

## Reading and Solitude

Books in the novel are strongly associated with solitary experience. Miriam consulting "the small chair and the large book" at a table [PN=002-006] registers reading as a posture of withdrawal and concentration. The image of "sitting in a corner dressed in white, reading a book" [PN=008-162] presents reading as a form of self-contained composure, a retreat from social demands into private mental space.

The physical presence of books in the novel's interiors is substantial: "inlaid and strewn with dingy books" occupying the end of a room [PN=002-008] conveys the material weight of accumulated reading. The "high polished glass-doored bookcase" [PN=001-015] glimpsed in a domestic scene suggests books as objects of display as well as use.

## The Exercise Book

Miriam is not only a reader but a writer in embryo. The moment she finds "an exercise book and wrote them down" [PN=002-002] captures the transition from passive consumption to active production. An exercise book — blank, provisional, intended for practice rather than publication — is the appropriate vessel for thoughts still taking shape. The act of writing in it bridges the world of reading and the world of making, and it registers Miriam's impulse to fix ideas before they slip away. In a novel so saturated with the consciousness of a reader, the exercise book stands as the quiet obverse: the book one writes in, not the book one reads.

> She found an exercise book and wrote them down. There they were…
> (002-002)

## Books and Conversation

Books serve as conversational currency among the novel's characters. Miriam's observations about "statements in books and in the conversation of others" [PN=006-018] acknowledge books as a source of knowledge that circulates socially. The question of "answering questions about books" [PN=006-019] and Dr. Hurd's declared indifference to reading suggest that books function as markers of intellectual identity and points of social distinction.

Miriam's own practice of reading is rendered with specificity. Discovery of "a Havet and a phrase book" [PN=002-007] connects reading to the practical project of learning French — part of Miriam's broader intellectual ambition.

> She understood what women in books meant by sacred… [PN=006-025]

## Literary Consciousness

Richardson's protagonist is a reader, and her reading shapes her perception. When Miriam finds characters in a novel "familiar; as if she had met them" [PN=006-016], the boundary between literary and lived experience dissolves — a reflection of the novel's own stream-of-consciousness method. The prayerbook that Miriam finds and inscribes with her name [PN=007-001] bridges the sacred and the personal, a book that is both institutional text and private object.

## Books and Gender

The novel registers a quiet but persistent awareness of how books are gendered. The reflection "Why did men write books? Modern men? The book was open" [PN=008-020] interrupts the act of reading with a question about authorship — who produces the books that circulate in this world, and why. The question is left unanswered, hanging in the space between the open page and the reader's consciousness.

In counterpoint, the mention of a woman who has "written a book called 'The Purple Shawl of Ceremony'" [PN=008-167] inserts a female author into the narrative's periphery — a reminder that women do write, even if the novel's central intellectual canon appears male-dominated. The dismissive judgment of "Holmes' worst book" and the classification of it as "a book written on purpose, to prove something" [PN=008-257] registers critical discernment: not all books merit the same respect, and the instrumental book — the book with an agenda — is suspect.

## Books as Objects of Exchange

Books move through the novel not only as texts to be read but as physical objects that mediate social relationships. A "book package, with a note" [PN=011-002] arrives as a gift, its material presence accompanied by the intimacy of handwriting. The wondering "How had she done it? When? Carrying off a book" [PN=011-002] frames the movement of a book between people as a small mystery — an act that requires initiative and occasions surprise. The book here is less a source of ideas than a token of connection, its physical passage between hands enacting a relationship that the narrative does not fully explain.
