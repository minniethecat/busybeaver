## Introduction

Softness in *Interim* is a quality of light, of sound, of colour, of touch. It names the gentler registers of perception — the "soft smooth flurring" of a duster over polished surfaces, the "soft light" of a reading lamp in a cold bedroom, the "soft shout" of recognition at the supper table. Against the novel's hardness — the black January darkness, the heavy Victorian furniture, the social stiffness Miriam navigates — softness offers relief, a mode of being that does not demand performance.

## Light and Colour

Softness is most frequently a property of light. Miriam's room is transformed when the gaslight is turned "to a soft green" [001-015], the colour itself a softening of the harshness of ordinary illumination. The "soft light of the reading-lamp" [002-002] creates a private sphere within the large cold bedroom, a circle of warmth that makes solitude bearable. Describing dolls from her childhood, Miriam remembers them as "painted a soft colour, very roundly moulded" [001-011] — softness as the quality of things remembered tenderly, their edges blurred by time.

## Sound and Silence

Softness also registers in sound. Miriam listens "to the soft smooth flurring of the duster over the highly polished surfaces" [001-014], the duster's softness domestic and reassuring, the rhythm of housework rendered almost musical. In the parlour, she hears Grace's "soft voice" [001-015] against the harder tones of Mrs. Philps. The piano's soft pedal produces "a woolly sweetness" [002-009] — softness as a mode of sound that wraps rather than penetrates, that invites rather than demands.
