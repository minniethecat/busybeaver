# Antoine Bowdoin

Antoine Bowdoin is one of the more vividly sketched minor characters in *Interim*. With his French name, his artistic inclinations, and his distinct physical presence, he stands out from the general run of boarding-house residents. He is seen through Miriam Henderson's observant consciousness — drawn, noted, assessed — but he remains opaque in ways that are characteristic of Richardson's method.

## Character Overview

Bowdoin is associated with art — he sketches, he draws, he carries the identity of an artist within the household. His name, "Antoine Bowdoin," combines the French (Antoine) with the Anglo-Saxon (Bowdoin), suggesting a mixed cultural identity that aligns him with the novel's other cosmopolitan figures: [[mendizabal|Mendizabal]], [[von-heber|von Heber]].

> Antoine Bowdoin. If she had had a solemn letter from him she would have been impressed. [004-008]

The name is presented as a kind of talisman — "Antoine Bowdoin" in its own sentence, as if the name itself carries weight. But the narrator's reflection — "if she had had a solemn letter from him she would have been impressed" — gently deflates the pretension. The name is impressive; the reality of the man may be less so.

## In the Novel

Bowdoin's appearances are marked by physical detail. He is seen from behind, his posture noted, his mannerisms observed. The other boarders draw pictures of him — "drawing pictures of Mr. Bowdoin's back view and noting his movements" [004-003] — which makes him both subject and object of the novel's own descriptive project.

> They were drawing pictures of Mr. Bowdoin's back view and noting his movements. [004-003]

The scene is almost meta-fictional: the characters themselves are engaged in the work of character-sketching, "drawing pictures" and "noting movements" — precisely what Richardson herself is doing. Bowdoin, seen from behind, studied by his fellow boarders, becomes a figure for the novel's own method of indirect portraiture.

## Key Relationships

### Bernard Mendizabal

Bowdoin and [[mendizabal|Mendizabal]] form a pair within the boarding-house — two intellectual men, both with European cultural associations, both participants in the drawing-room conversations that constitute the novel's intellectual life.

### Dr. von Heber

Bowdoin is part of the masculine circle that includes [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]]. The three men — Mendizabal, Bowdoin, von Heber — form a loose alliance of outsiders within the predominantly female domestic space of the boarding-house.

### Miriam Henderson

Miriam observes Bowdoin with her characteristic attention to physical detail and social manner. He is one of the figures through whom she registers the texture of the boarding-house world — not a love interest, not a confidant, but a presence, a surface to be read.

## Notable Passages

> The engagement with Mr. Bowdoin would remain untouched. [004-013]

The "engagement" — presumably a social arrangement, an evening plan — is left "untouched," neither confirmed nor cancelled. The phrase captures the provisional quality of boarding-house social life: plans are made and unmade, engagements float in a state of suspension. Bowdoin is part of this suspended sociality.

> Mr. Bowdoin went up the rubbish-strewn steps and opened the dusty blistered door with his latchkey. [004-008]

The "rubbish-strewn steps" and "dusty blistered door" are the unglamorous reality of Bowdoin's London life. The latchkey — sign of independence, of coming and going at will — contrasts with the shabbiness of the setting. He is a man with a key to a dusty door, an artist in a rubbish-strewn street.

## Significance

Bowdoin, like Mendizabal, represents the cultural ambition of the boarding-house world. He sketches, he converses, he carries a European name into an English drawing-room. But Richardson never lets the cultural ambition obscure the material reality: the dusty door, the rubbish-strewn steps, the back view that others draw. Bowdoin is both an artist and a boarder, a cosmopolitan and a man with a latchkey to a shabby London house.
