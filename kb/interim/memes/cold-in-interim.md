## Introduction

Cold pervades *Interim* as both a literal condition and an emotional register. The boarding-house rooms are perpetually under-heated, the bedrooms frigid, and the characters move through spaces that offer little physical warmth. But cold also describes the emotional distances between people — the reserve, the withheld affection, the "cold mother" who cannot offer comfort. Weather and feeling converge in this persistent motif.

## Physical Cold

The material reality of cold shapes daily life in the boarding-house. Miriam sits working "until she grew so cold that she knew if she stayed on" she would suffer [002-004]. She spends New Year's Eve "alone in a cold bedroom" [002-002], the temperature compounding her isolation. Upstairs, "in her cold room everything vanished" [004-004] — the physical chill erasing the warmth of social contact below. The cold is not abstract but palpable: "a cold grey twilight was blotting out" the house's interior [002-006], and the "cold china knob" of a door "told her callously" [002-009] that the house offers no comfort.

## Emotional Cold

Coldness extends beyond temperature into human relationships. Mrs. Bailey's voice provokes the memory of "You cold mother" [003-009], a phrase that collapses physical sensation and emotional withholding into a single judgment. The world outside is "cold and desolate and harsh and dreary" [001-015], an external landscape matching internal states. At the hostel, Miriam endures "the horrible stony cold dark restaurant" [006-001] — coldness mapped onto an institutional space that offers sustenance without warmth.

## Social Distinction

Cold also marks social difference. The young women wash "in cold water" [006-003] as a matter of course, their poverty inscribed in the temperature of their morning routine. The insistence that "a cold bedroom keeps you hardy" [008-060] recasts deprivation as virtue, a rationalization that cannot disguise the underlying chill. When characters move "out of their warm parlours into cold corridors" [008-061], they cross a thermal boundary that is also a social one.
