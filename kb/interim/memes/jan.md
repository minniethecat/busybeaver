# Jan

Jan is a friend of Miriam Henderson in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim*, appearing primarily in conversational scenes that occur outside the Banbury Park boarding-house. Unlike the boarders — whose relationships with Miriam are mediated by the social rituals of the Bailey household — Jan belongs to a different sphere of intimacy: the private conversation between friends, where Miriam can voice doubts and seek reassurance.

## Character Overview

Jan is introduced obliquely, through a reference to "Mag's and Jan's boarding house," which situates her within the novel's geography of rented rooms and transient domestic arrangements.

> It was at Mag's and Jan's boarding house. Mrs. Philps… [001-010]

The mention of Jan alongside Mag suggests a partnership — two women running a boarding establishment, a parallel to the Bailey household at Banbury Park. But Jan's narrative function is not as a landlady; it is as a friend, someone to whom Miriam turns when the pressures of her own situation become acute.

## In the Novel

### A Confidante

Jan's most significant appearances are in dialogue — direct address, question and response, the rhythms of intimate conversation. Miriam speaks to Jan with a frankness she does not always permit herself in the more public setting of the Bailey dinner table.

> "Can you see her difficulty, Jan?" [008-145]

> "I'm not morbid. Am I, Jan?" [008-164]

The repeated invocation of Jan's name in direct address is striking. Miriam is not simply talking to Jan; she is appealing to her, seeking corroboration. "Am I, Jan?" — the question is not rhetorical; it wants an answer. Jan functions as a mirror, a source of confirmation, someone whose judgment Miriam trusts.

> "Don't be a fool to hesitate, don't you, Jan?" [008-158]

The syntax here — the colloquial imperative softened by the tag question "don't you" — captures the texture of their intimacy. Miriam is chiding Jan, but gently; there is affection in the phrasing, a shared idiom that marks their friendship as close and longstanding.

### Shared Laughter

The friendship is not all earnest questioning. Jan and Miriam share moments of amusement, a private comic sphere that relieves the novel's more introspective passages.

> "I think it's awfully clever. Jan and I roared over your Madeleine…" [008-171]

The verb "roared" — loud, bodily, unselfconscious laughter — offers a counterpoint to Miriam's more characteristic mode of silent observation. With Jan, Miriam can laugh. The friendship provides momentary release from the pressure of consciousness that otherwise defines her experience.

## Key Relationships

### Miriam Henderson

Jan's primary narrative function is as [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]]'s friend and confidante. Their conversations offer the reader access to a more relaxed, more openly emotional Miriam — a version of the protagonist that the boarding-house, with its social performance and constant observation, rarely permits.

### Mag

Jan's association with Mag — mentioned together as joint keepers of a boarding-house — hints at a life and partnership that exist largely off-stage. The novel does not develop Mag as a character, but her name alongside Jan's sketches a domestic arrangement parallel to the Bailey household.

### Mrs. Philps

The link between Jan and [[mrs-philps|Mrs. Philps]] — "It was at Mag's and Jan's boarding house. Mrs. Philps…" — suggests overlapping social circles, the dense network of acquaintance that connects the novel's various domestic spaces.

## Significance

Jan is a minor character — she appears in only a handful of passages — but her function is important. She represents friendship as a distinct mode of relation: not the charged observation of the boarding-house dinner table, not the economic transaction of the French lesson or the rented room, but the simple, sustaining intimacy of conversation between equals. In a novel so deeply invested in Miriam's interiority, Jan offers an outside — someone to whom interior experience can be spoken, tested, and sometimes laughed at.
