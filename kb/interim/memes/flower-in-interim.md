## Overview

Flowers recur in *Interim* as markers of beauty, commerce, and sensory pleasure within the urban landscape. They appear in West End shop displays, in domestic arrangements, and in Miriam Henderson's reflections on nature and artifice. Flowers mediate between the natural world and the constructed city.

## Manifestations

The novel's most vivid floral imagery centres on a West End flower shop. "The window was blocked with flowers" [006-014] describes the visual excess of a shop display, while inside "cut flowers in stone jam pots, masses of greenery" [006-015] evoke abundance and impermanence. This shop, connected to "Madame Leroy a friend of Mrs. Green who had a flower shop in Bruton Street" [004-004], links the floral to social networks.

Flowers also appear in reflective moments. Miriam understands "why people bought flowers" [004-006] and imagines a room "fragrant with clusters of winter flowers" [004-007]. The "blazing flower-baskets" [005-003] of London streets inject colour into the grey cityscape. In a garden, she feels that "the fields and the flowers would make her cry" [004-011], revealing the emotional charge flowers carry.

## Key Passages

- [006-014] "The window was blocked with flowers" — the West End flower shop as urban spectacle.
- [006-015] "Cut flowers in stone jam pots, masses of greenery" — the sensory texture of the shop interior.
- [004-007] "Fragrant with clusters of winter flowers" — imagined floral abundance.
