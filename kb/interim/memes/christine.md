# Christine

Christine is the servant at Mrs. Bailey's Banbury Park boarding-house — a figure whose labour is constantly referenced but whose interior life remains entirely outside the novel's reach. She clears tables, lights fires, answers doors, and prepares rooms, her presence registered through the effects of her work rather than through direct characterisation.

## Character Overview

Christine belongs to the novel's hidden economy of domestic labour. While Miriam Henderson and the other boarders converse in the drawing-room, Christine works in the kitchen and the passages. She is seen briefly — "Christine inaudible in the kitchen" [001-015] — and heard through the sounds of her activity: the clearing of plates, the lighting of fires, the answering of bells.

> Don't forget to tell Christine how we like them done. [001-004]

Grace Broom's instruction to tell Christine "how we like them done" — the "them" unspecified but clearly some domestic preparation — captures the servant's position: she is the recipient of instructions, the executor of preferences she has not chosen. The sentence registers both Christine's necessary presence and her complete exclusion from the conversation.

## In the Novel

Christine appears at the functional margins of the novel's domestic scenes. She clears the supper things [001-011], answers the front door bell [001-010], and prepares the rooms for the boarders' use. Her name is spoken more often than she herself appears — characters refer to her, give instructions concerning her, but rarely address her directly.

> There's Christine shall we have her in to change the plates. [001-010]

The casual "shall we have her in" — as if Christine were a resource to be summoned rather than a person to be consulted — reveals the class relations that structure the boarding-house world. The Broom sisters and Mrs. Bailey speak of Christine with the unconscious authority of employers.

## Key Relationships

### The Broom Sisters

[[florrie-broom|Florrie]] and [[grace-broom|Grace Broom]] give Christine instructions about domestic arrangements. Their relationship with her is defined by the class distance between the daughters of the house and the servant.

### Mrs. Bailey

[[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]], as the boarding-house keeper, is Christine's direct employer. The management of Christine's labour — what she cooks, how she cleans, when she appears — is part of Mrs. Bailey's larger management of the household.

### Miriam Henderson

Miriam notices Christine only in passing — a figure in the kitchen, a name spoken at table. This inattention is itself significant: the novel's commitment to rendering consciousness has a class boundary, and Christine lies beyond it.

## Significance

Christine represents the limit of Richardson's narrative method. The novel's free indirect discourse — its commitment to rendering consciousness from within — can follow Miriam into her deepest reflections but cannot cross the class boundary that separates her from the servant. Christine is present only as an effect: the cleared table, the lit fire, the answered door.

This limitation is not a failure but a structural honesty. Richardson does not pretend to know Christine's interiority; she records only what Miriam can see and hear of her. The servant's invisibility within the narrative mirrors her social invisibility — a silence that the novel registers without attempting to fill.
