## Domestic Architecture as Social Marker

Houses in *Interim* serve as markers of social position. The "white façade of a large London house" [001-016] contrasts with the boarding house at "Mag's and Jan's" [001-010], establishing a hierarchy of domestic spaces.

The "big houses" carry an anxiety of exclusion — "the anxiety in the big houses" [001-013] — while smaller shared spaces like the boarding house create intimacy through proximity.

> …the white façade of a large London house the laurels looked down through… [001-016]

The distinction between house types maps onto class consciousness. Miriam is acutely aware of the social signals embedded in architecture: "the crown of the large house and the reality of all the un…" [002-007] suggests both aspiration and the impossibility of full belonging.

## The House as Psychological Space

The house is not merely physical setting but a projection of Miriam's inner state. "Dead silence in the house" [001-015] externalises her solitude, while "the pretty corner house standing alone in its garden" [001-017] mirrors her own sense of apartness from the social world.

> …dead silence in the house, Miriam gathered up her blouse… [001-015]

This psychological dimension deepens when Miriam finds herself alone: "The whole house was hers; she was a boarder" [003-001]. The parenthetical qualifier "she was a boarder" undercuts the initial freedom — the house is hers only conditionally, always subject to the terms of her economic position.

## Silence and Solitude

Throughout the novel, the house registers states of emptiness and quiet. "Inside the house a cold grey twilight was blot…" [002-006] evokes a liminal domestic atmosphere. "The house was silent. Her footsteps mad…" [004-007] renders the house as an acoustic space that amplifies Miriam's aloneness.

> After only two days in the house she seemed already more at home… [004-003]

Miriam's rapid acclimatisation to new houses speaks to her adaptability but also to a rootlessness — she fits into houses quickly because she has learned not to expect permanence. The silence of the house becomes "a background for this…" [003-014] — a neutral canvas against which her inner life unfolds.

## Domestic Ritual and Daily Life

The house structures daily rhythms. "Don't run about the house, come down to supper" [001-001] establishes the house as a space governed by domestic ritual and adult authority. These small disciplinary moments accumulate across the narrative, shaping Miriam's experience of domestic space as both shelter and constraint.

The house also mediates social performance: "chairs and telling the whole house of an extraordinary impulse" [001-014] shows how domestic interiors amplify private moments into public declarations. The house is never fully private; sound and behaviour travel through its structure, making solitude fragile.
