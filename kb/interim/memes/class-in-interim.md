## Introduction

Social class in *Interim* is neither abstract category nor political argument — it is the felt texture of daily life. Miriam Henderson moves through a London stratified by accent, dress, address, and manners, and her consciousness registers class difference as a physical sensation: the "shouting of women" in working-class streets, the awkwardness of musical performance before a mixed audience, the confidence of bodies that move through the world without self-consciousness. Richardson's treatment of class is modernist in its indirection — class is not discussed but perceived, a quality of the sensory world.

## The Boarding-House as Class Microcosm

Banbury Park assembles a cross-section of the lower-middle and middle classes: clerks, teachers, dental students, women of precarious independence. The dinner table enacts class distinction through manners, taste, and the subtle hierarchies of address. Who speaks first, who defers, whose opinion on music or politics carries weight — these are class performances as much as personal attributes. The boarding-house, like the novel itself, contains class difference within a single frame, making it visible through juxtaposition.

## The Texture of Working-Class London

The life of the London street brings Miriam into proximity with working-class existence. The "shouting of women" is the noise of working-class neighbourhoods, the life of the street from which Miriam withdraws into the quiet of her room. Street vendors, shop assistants, the bodies of labourers — these figures populate the novel's periphery, reminders of a world whose rough texture both attracts and unsettles Miriam. To be "in the world" is to be exposed to class difference, to commerce, to the casual physicality of working-class life.

## Class and Language

Words in *Interim* are never neutral; they are loaded with class, gender, and the history of their use. The speech of the boarding-house carries class markers — pronunciations, idioms, the confidence or hesitancy with which opinions are offered. Miriam's own relationship to language is shaped by class aspiration and class anxiety: she hears her own speech as provincial, as marked, as something to be overcome. The novel's prose style — its careful rendering of the texture of speech — is itself a class performance, a demonstration of the linguistic sophistication that class mobility demands.
