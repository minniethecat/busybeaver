# Ruscino's

Ruscino's is a café or restaurant in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* that serves as an off-stage social venue — a destination for the boarders of Banbury Park when they venture beyond the domestic sphere. Unlike the boarding-house dinner table, which is structured by Mrs. Bailey's domestic authority, Ruscino's represents a more fluid, cosmopolitan space: a place of evening gatherings, French proprietorship, and the social possibilities of the city after dark.

## Overview

Ruscino's is known to us primarily through conversation — it is mentioned, asked about, speculated upon — rather than through extended descriptive passages. This is characteristic of Richardson's method: places are registered through the talk that circulates about them, their significance shaped by who goes there and what it means to go there.

> "Do you still go to Ruscino's every night, Miriam?" [008-177]

The question — "every night" — establishes Ruscino's as a habitual destination, part of the rhythm of evening life. The fact that someone needs to ask, that going to Ruscino's is a practice that can be continued or discontinued, also hints at its social significance: it is a choice, a marker of lifestyle.

## In the Novel

### A Cosmopolitan Space

Ruscino's is presided over by a French proprietor — "Ruscino's mademoiselle" — which marks it as distinct from the English domesticity of Banbury Park. In a novel where French language and culture represent a current of continental sophistication, a French-run establishment carries connotations of cosmopolitanism, of a world larger than the boarding-house.

> Here is Ruscino's mademoiselle, allons, we will… [006-027]

The French imperative "allons" — let's go — signals a shift in register. At Ruscino's, French is spoken, continental manners prevail. The café is a pocket of Europe in London, a space where the novel's interest in linguistic and cultural difference finds physical expression.

### The Social Stage

Ruscino's functions as a secondary social stage, complementing the boarding-house dinner table. It is where boarders go together, where relationships are observed and commented upon, where the gossip economy of the household extends into the city.

> "So you don't go to Ruscino's every evening?" [008-188]

The follow-up question, pressing for confirmation, suggests that attendance at Ruscino's is socially legible — that going or not going means something, can be read by others as a sign of mood, attachment, or social availability.

### Spectacle and Observation

The gossip that surrounds Ruscino's mirrors the gossip that surrounds the dinner table. Who goes with whom, who follows whom — these are matters of interest and speculation.

> …Miss Scott, following her to Ruscino's. They had told Mrs. Bailey… [009-036]

The act of "following" someone to Ruscino's — the pursuit, the trailing — turns the café into a site of intrigue. And the report back to Mrs. Bailey closes the loop: Ruscino's may be beyond the domestic walls, but it is not beyond the reach of household knowledge. What happens there comes home, becomes material for talk, feeds the social narrative the boarders collectively construct.

### A Place of Arrival

For [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]], Ruscino's represents arrival — a place where he is "at home," where he belongs.

> He was here, in Ruscino's quite simply. He sat at home… [006-028]

The phrase "quite simply" is significant. Ruscino's strips away social complexity; it is a place where being is not performance but presence. For von Heber, a German in London, Ruscino's offers the comfort of the continental — a space where his foreignness does not mark him as other.

## Significance

Ruscino's is more than a café. It is an extension of the novel's social geography, a place where the hierarchies and intimacies of the boarding-house are renegotiated on neutral ground. Under the French proprietor's gaze, the English boarders become slightly different versions of themselves — more continental, more adventurous, more visible. Ruscino's is where the household goes to become a public.
