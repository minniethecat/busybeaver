# Mirror in _Interim_

**Mirrors** in _Interim_ are sites where self-perception and social performance intersect. Richardson's narrator repeatedly catches her own reflection or notes the reflections of others, making the mirror a threshold where the private sense of self meets its public image. The mirror is never neutral: it judges, surprises, or confirms.

## The Mirror as Self-Examination

The novel opens with a mirror scene that sets the tone for the whole.

> …In the large square mirror her dingy blouse and tie looked somehow old and wrong… [PN=001-001]

The mirror does not flatter but exposes, making Miriam see herself as others might. The mirror's judgment is immediate and bodily, tied to clothing and class. The "dingy" quality the glass reveals is inseparable from social position — the blouse and tie that felt adequate before the mirror now register as evidence of not quite belonging.

## The Narrow Strip of Mirror

Richardson uses mirror placement with architectural precision.

> In the narrow strip of mirror that ran from the table high up the wall. [PN=002-008]

This captures the fragmentary nature of self-perception — the self is seen in slices, never whole. The narrow mirror above the table frames only part of the body, just as consciousness frames only part of experience. Miriam's world is one of partial views, and the narrow mirror literalises this epistemology.

## The Long Mirror Between Windows

> The lower part of the long mirror between the windows. [PN=004-007]

Richardson situates the mirror spatially between two sources of light, making it a third aperture — one that reflects rather than admits. The long mirror between windows is a standard piece of Victorian furnishing, but Richardson invests it with phenomenological weight: it doubles the room's space while also doubling the self who inhabits it.

## Smeary Mirrors and Social Space

Not all mirrors in the novel are domestic.

> The smeary mirrors and unpolished furniture. [PN=006-001]

These appear in a public dining room, the degraded reflection marking the space as transient and uncherished. The smear on the mirror is a trace of all the previous gazes that have passed across its surface — a kind of social history written in smudged glass.

## Mirror as Narrative Method

| Mirror Function | Novel Instance | Narrative Significance |
|----------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Self-judgment | Square mirror (001-001) | Class anxiety made visible |
| Fragmented perception | Narrow strip (002-008) | Consciousness sees in pieces |
| Spatial doubling | Long mirror (004-007) | The self and its reflection coexist |
| Social trace | Smeary mirrors (006-001) | Reflections carry collective history |

The mirror motif in *Interim* is more than decoration. It is Richardson's way of making visible the invisible work of self-perception — the constant glancing, judging, and adjusting that constitutes social consciousness. Every mirror in the novel is also a lens through which Miriam sees herself being seen.
