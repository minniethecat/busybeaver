# Train in _Interim_

The train in _Interim_ is not merely a mode of transport — it is a distinct psychological space, a compartment of consciousness suspended between departure and arrival. Richardson's Metropolitan Railway journey is rendered as a passage through darkness, a zone where the external world falls away and Miriam Henderson's interior life asserts itself.

## The Metropolitan Journey

The novel's most extended train passage occurs when Miriam travels to hear a Dante lecture. The journey begins at Gower Street station, whose "familiar sulphurous gloom" and "platform lights shining murkily from the midst of slowly rolling clouds of grey smoke" [004-008] establish the train as an underworld — a space of dimness and anonymity. The "dark forms and phantom white faces of waiting passengers emerging suddenly as she threaded the darkness" [004-008] turn fellow travellers into apparitions, barely present.

The train itself arrives as a living presence: "its beloved black dumpy high-shouldered engine with its large unshrieking mushroom bell-whistle" [004-008]. The engine is affectionately personified — "beloved," "dumpy," "high-shouldered" — a creature of the industrial world that Miriam regards with something like fondness. The journey transforms: what began as "an expedition to a spot within five minutes' walk of Sarah's" becomes "a journey on the Metropolitan; going indeed outside the radius into blackness" [004-008].

## Darkness and Consciousness

The train's passage through darkness is the literal correlative of a psychological state. Richardson describes "the sense of the train going along in the darkness" [004-011] as something one must relinquish in order to engage with other people. The darkness of the tunnel is a refuge — a place where the demands of social performance are suspended:

> It meant that now at this moment one must give up the sense of the train going along in the darkness and the sense of the dark streets waiting lamplit under the dark sky and go out to the people in the carriage. [004-011]

The rhythm of the train — its steady forward movement through blackness — provides a somatic ground for thought. Miriam's consciousness moves associatively, and the train's motion mirrors this: forward, unstoppable, passing through zones of darkness punctuated by brief illuminations.

## Between Worlds

The train journey is a threshold experience. Departing from Gower Street, Miriam leaves behind the known world of the boarding house; arriving at her destination, she must re-enter the social world of the lecture, the audience, the obligation to listen and respond. The train car itself is neither here nor there — a transitional space where identity is temporarily suspended.

This betweenness is characteristic of Richardson's modernism. The train is a technology of modernity that fragments experience — the passenger is at once stationary (seated in the carriage) and in motion (hurtling through darkness), alone and among strangers, in one place and already arriving at another. For Miriam, whose consciousness is itself a kind of perpetual transit between inner and outer worlds, the train is the perfect vehicle.
