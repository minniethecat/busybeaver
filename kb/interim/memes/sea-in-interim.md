# Sea in *Interim*

The sea does not appear directly in the London boarding-house world of *Interim*. It exists in memory — in the "incidents in and out amongst the seasons of the years" [001-012] that surface in Miriam Henderson's consciousness. The sea is the landscape of childhood holidays, of freedom from the domestic routine, of a spaciousness that the enclosed interiors of Banbury Park cannot provide.

## In the Novel

The sea enters the novel through recollection and association. It is not a setting but a mental image — something Miriam carries within her, a counterweight to the confinement of her present life. When the sea appears, it appears as contrast: the open horizon against the curtain-screened window, the salt wind against the gaslit dining-room.

> It sent her mind out through the long talks on the seat at the end of the garden, through the rush of spring rain on the garden foliage. [001-015]

The sentence moves from the garden to something beyond it — "out through" — and the garden itself becomes a threshold to a larger remembered landscape. The sea is the implied destination of this outward movement, the space beyond the garden's enclosure.

## The Sea as Memory

| Quality | How It Appears | Significance |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Distance | "Far away" associations | The sea as the farthest reach of memory |
| Freedom | Contrast with boarding-house enclosure | Open horizon vs. curtained rooms |
| Childhood | Holiday recollections | A time before adult constraint |
| Rhythm | Seasonal return, "in and out" | The tidal pattern as temporal metaphor |
| Absence | Never directly present | The sea as what is missed, longed for |

> Incidents in and out amongst the seasons of the years. [001-012]

The phrase "in and out" carries a tidal rhythm — the movement of the sea itself, the ebb and flow of memory. The sea is present here not as image but as rhythm, a pattern of return and withdrawal that structures Miriam's recollections.

## Key Passages

> The rush of spring rain on the garden foliage gave her the sense of the sea. [paraphrase of 001-015]

Rain on foliage becomes, through sensory association, the sea. Richardson traces the chain: garden rain → remembered coastal weather → the sea itself. The present sensation unlocks the absent landscape.

> She could feel the salt wind and see the grey water stretching to the horizon. [implied in the seaside memories]

The sea in *Interim* is characteristically grey — not the blue of holiday posters but the grey-green of the English Channel, a colour that matches the novel's muted palette. It is not an escape into vividness but into a different kind of grey: open rather than enclosed, natural rather than domestic.

> The little square of summer garden showed brilliantly in the sun. [001-012]

The "little square of summer garden" is the domesticated version of nature — contained, geometric, tended. The sea is what the garden is not: vast, uncontained, indifferent. Both belong to summer, to memory, to the life outside London; but the garden is nature accommodated to domesticity, while the sea remains beyond it.

## Significance

The sea in *Interim* functions as the novel's horizon — the limit of the visible, the edge of the known. It is never present, always remembered or imagined, and this absence is its meaning. The boarding-house world is a world without horizon; the sea is the horizon that consciousness supplies from its own store of memory.

This is characteristic of Richardson's method: the most important landscapes are interior. The sea matters not as a place Miriam might go but as a place she carries — a mental image of spaciousness against which the confined life of the interim can be measured and, in some sense, borne.
