# Darkness in _Interim_

**Darkness** in _Interim_ is far more than the absence of light. Richardson's narrator moves through darkness — of rooms, staircases, London streets at night — and finds in it a heightened state of perception. "Miriam emerged smoothly into the darkness and lay radiant" (001-012) captures the paradox: darkness as the medium in which interior radiance becomes possible.

## Darkness as Physical Space

The novel's architecture is scored by darkness. The "staircase beckoned her up into darkness" (003-002), a recurring threshold experience in which ascending into the unlit becomes a minor act of courage. "The cold darkness waiting upstairs" (003-009) personifies darkness as a patient presence that must be met. Inside rooms, "cool walls holding darkness in far corners" (008-010) give darkness volume and depth — it is not flat but spatial, pooling in architectural recesses.

> …the staircase beckoned her up into darkness. A few steps and she would be…
> (003-002)

## Seasonal Darkness

Richardson distinguishes between qualities of darkness. "A hard black January darkness, utterly different to the friendly" (002-005) ranks darkness by its emotional temperature — January darkness is hostile, a "cold dreadful darkness" (002-006) that the subject endures without fully knowing it. By contrast, the "familiar firelit winter darkness" (004-007) is domesticated, warmed by the hearth, carrying "a faint dry warm scent" that makes darkness inhabitable rather than threatening.

## Darkness and Perception

Darkness in _Interim_ sharpens rather than dulls the senses. "As she threaded the darkness, revived her" (004-008) suggests that navigating darkness is itself invigorating. The visual world does not disappear in darkness — it transforms. "Here and there in the darkness of the opposite house fronts" (008-021) shows the narrator's eye still registering detail across the dark street. Darkness heightens what remains visible: a single star, a lit window, the outline of trees.

## Darkness and Interior Life

The novel's deepest darkness is psychological. "It was wonderful; but all in a darkness" (006-016) suggests that aesthetic experience — music, a concert — exists within a surrounding darkness that is the condition of modern subjectivity. To "walk on through the darkness towards the mingled darkness" (006-025) describes not only London streets but a mode of being: moving forward without full visibility, the darkness ahead merging with the darkness behind.

## Threshold Darkness

Darkness in _Interim_ repeatedly marks thresholds between states. The concert scene contains a compressed instance: "plunged it into a darkness that still held the threat of" (001-016) — darkness that retains menace even as it surrounds the listener. Gazing "past her into the darkness beyond the range of the firel" (001-017) makes darkness the space just outside the circle of warmth, defining the edge of domestic safety. Staring "out towards the darkness" (004-011) becomes a gesture of unanswered questioning, the dark as the void into which questions are thrown. And in the novel's most liberating darkness, Miriam reaches "the free space of darkness she had reached" (006-026), darkness as emancipation from enclosure — a space not of confinement but of release.

## Darkness and the Face

Richardson registers darkness through the human face, making physiognomy a site where inner and outer dark meet. At the dinner table, "Mrs. Philps' face had grown dark and old" (001-013), the darkening of a face marking a shift in social atmosphere that Miriam registers before she understands its cause. In a more intimate register, "she came back with fear darkening the velvet lustre of her eyes" (001-014) shows emotion itself as a darkening — fear as a shadow that passes across the eyes, dimming their light from within. Both moments reveal darkness as not merely environmental but as something that surfaces in the body, visible to the watchful observer.

> …Mrs. Philps' face had grown dark and old. Miriam glanced restlessly…
> (001-013)

## Darkness and the Natural World

Beyond London's built environment, Richardson finds darkness shaped by vegetation and landscape. The portico holds "palm ferns standing in semidarkness just inside the portico, the" (006-027), where the half-dark of exotic plants creates an ambiguous interior threshold between street and building. Summer brings its own nocturnal texture: "summer leaves flickering in the darkness, the trailing of dusk across" (002-009) captures the animated quality of foliage at night — darkness not as stillness but as the medium through which the leaves' movement becomes visible. In the final chapter, trees cast their own darkness: "shadowed by trees and left in darkness by the high spindling shaded" (008-022), darkness given a source and shape by the natural form that produces it.
