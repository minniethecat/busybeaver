# Time in *Interim*

Time in *Interim* is experienced, not measured. Richardson's narrative traces the texture of duration — the stretch of an evening, the weight of a season, the sudden awareness of years passing — through Miriam Henderson's consciousness. Clock-time barely registers; what matters is the felt quality of temporal passage.

## In the Novel

The novel unfolds across a single winter visit — Miriam's stay at the Banbury Park boarding-house over Christmas and New Year — yet within this compressed calendar, Richardson opens an expansive interior temporality. The narrative moves between the immediate present of conversation and dinner, the remembered past of earlier visits and lost homes, and the anticipated future of return and change.

> There was never enough time and strength to make everything perfect. [001-010]

This early observation — Mrs. Philps's complaint about housekeeping — resonates beyond its domestic occasion. "Never enough time" is the novel's temporal ground-bass: the sense that life's demands exceed the hours available, that perfection recedes with every passing moment.

## Temporal Dimensions

| Dimension | Manifestation | Significance |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| Cyclical time | Meals, evenings, the daily round | The boarding-house rhythm that contains consciousness |
| Seasonal time | Christmas, New Year, winter darkness | Ritual punctuation; the weight of shared festivity |
| Retrospective time | Memories of home, earlier visits, childhood | The past as a living presence within the present |
| Anticipatory time | Miriam's uncertain return, plans deferred | The future as an open question, a source of anxiety |
| Suspended time | Hours by the fire, the interval before sleep | Moments when clock-time dissolves into pure awareness |

Christmas itself is the novel's central temporal marker: "At Christmas-time one should be where one belongs" [001-016]. The sentence registers both the pull of tradition and Miriam's ambiguous position — a guest in someone else's household, belonging nowhere in particular.

## Key Passages

> By the time they came back she would be there in the spring. [001-015]

Time here is measured not in dates but in returns — "by the time they came back" — and seasons — "in the spring." Richardson's temporal markers are relational: one event locates another, one season anticipates the next.

> The bright light of the gas and the soft light of the reading-lamp shone upon perfect order. [002-002]

The "perfect order" of this moment is a stilled time — the room arranged, the lamps lit, the household suspended between the activity of day and the quiet of night. This is the interstitial time the novel's title names: *Interim*.

> She had laughed, several times during the past hour. She had not known it. [003-009]

The curious doubling here — Miriam registers her own laughter only in retrospect — captures Richardson's treatment of time and consciousness: experience precedes awareness. One lives first, knows later.

> A cold grey twilight was blotting out the warm brown dusk of the old year. [002-006]

The transition from old year to new is rendered as a contest of light — "cold grey" erasing "warm brown." Temporal passage is sensory, almost tactile, not abstract.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of time is central to her modernist method. By refusing the clock-time of conventional narrative — the ordered sequence of events — she opens fiction to the temporality of consciousness itself: associative, recursive, layered. A single evening can contain childhood memories, present sensations, and future anxieties, all held in suspension.

The boarding-house setting amplifies this temporal texture. It is a temporary home, an interim space, where time feels both suspended and urgent — the visit will end, the future is uncertain, and every moment carries the weight of its own passing.
