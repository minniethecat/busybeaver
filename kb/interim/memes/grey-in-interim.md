# Grey in _Interim_

**Grey** is the dominant colour of _Interim_'s visual world, saturating its architecture, weather, and emotional atmosphere. Richardson deploys grey not as mere absence of colour but as an active quality — the "pressing grey of an outside world" (001-013) that bears down on interior life, the grey of stone and twilight that frames the novel's scenes.

## Grey in the City

### Grey Architecture

The London of _Interim_ is built of grey. The "thoroughfare lined with high grey houses standing back" (001-015) establishes the city's characteristic surface, while "the grey heights of University College" (006-025) and "grey house fronts" (008-009) repeat the motif. Even the domestic interior participates: "Inside the house a cold grey twilight was blotting out" (002-006), the grey seeping through walls. In the final chapter, "the rain-cool grey façades of the opposite houses" (008-010) look back across the street, grey answering grey.

The architectural grey extends to the very stone the city walks on: "an irregular row on the dirty grey stone, railed by a balustrade" (008-014) — grey as the ground of urban passage, marked by use. And "the grey stone windowsills" (008-021) where "the air" touches the building's edge — grey as the interface between inside and out.

### Grey in Motion

Richardson repeatedly places grey in contexts of movement through the city. The outdoor evening scene is defined by "slowly rolling clouds of grey smoke, the dark forms and" (004-008), smoke animating the grey air. Walking home, Miriam is "led on again into the lamplit greyness leading along to Donizett" (005-005), where greyness becomes a directional medium — something travelled through.

The street surface itself transforms under changing light: "the brilliant copper grey" of wet pavement (008-008) — grey as copper, metallic and luminous, the city's ground made strange by rain. Even the interior participates: "the grey space of dusty floor" (008-022) — grey as the unnoticed, the space one walks across without seeing. And at evening, "pure evening grey outlining the dark feathery" (008-019) — grey as the last light, the colour of the world before it goes dark.

## Grey Light and Weather

The novel's weather is persistently grey. "It was nearly midnight. The grey of tomorrow morning kept pressing" (003-010) makes grey a temporal register — the colour of the approaching day. "Through her grey window she could see the sunlight" (008-002) places grey between the perceiver and the world. "The street was a livid grey, brilliant with hidden sunlight" (008-004) captures the paradox Richardson often returns to: grey that is nevertheless luminous, the colour of light diffused through cloud and stone.

The novel's most extended meditation on grey weather comes late: "I should long for grey days and changes in the sky" (008-038), followed by the clarifying "the grey days for their own sake" (008-040). This is an aesthetic defence of grey — not as tolerable default but as positive value, the English light that the narrator would miss if it were gone. The "slowly rolling clouds of grey smoke" (004-008) animate the air itself, grey becoming movement.

## Grey in the Human Figure

### Faces and Eyes in Grey

Richardson extends grey to the human face. An "old man sitting alone with a grey face and an extinguished eye" (006-028) presents grey as the colour of depleted life — the face drained of vitality. Yet grey eyes are different: "soft bluey grey with an expression" (001-011) suggests depth and gentleness, while "Sissie's pale steady grey-blue eyes, her characterless" (002-007) uses grey to register personality's absence.

The human grey extends beyond the face. At the novel's opening, Miriam imagines arriving "cool and grey clad at the morning door" (008-017) — grey not as depletion but as composure, the colour of professional readiness. And a character appears with "massy grey hair; unspectacled smiling" (009-098) — grey hair signalling age and authority, worn without apology.

> …an old man sitting alone with a grey face and an extinguished eye…
> (006-028)

### Grey in Dress and Objects

Grey appears not only in architecture and weather but in the material details of clothing and décor. One of the novel's most vivid costume descriptions places grey at its centre: "shemy stripy harsh pale grey, a crimson waistcoat showing" (003-002) — the grey suit set against a flash of crimson. The masculine elegance of hats receives similar treatment: a man's "clear grey and black of the hat he was" (006-021) and later "his black banded grey hat. 'Jaunty and debonair'" (006-023), the grey lending sobriety to the dandyish accessory.

The novel also registers grey in the texture of ordinary domestic labour: an "ugly greyish stripe and badly cut" (009-089) blouse — grey as the colour of poverty, of garments that cannot afford distinction. And "the grey felt hat I saw go down" (008-170) — a hat disappearing, grey as the colour of things lost to view. These sartorial greys ground the colour in the texture of everyday Edwardian London — grey is not only landscape but costume, not only atmosphere but economy.

> …shemy stripy harsh pale grey, a crimson waistcoat showing… (003-002)

## The Meaning of Grey

### Grey as Emotional Register

The "pressing grey of an outside world" (001-013) is Richardson's most compressed statement of grey's emotional function. It is not simply the colour of things but the weight of externality itself — the world as pressure, the outside as a greyness that must be endured. Yet this grey is not despairing; it is the given condition, the visual truth of a London winter from which the novel's small warmths emerge.

### Grey as Aesthetic Principle

Richardson's treatment of grey amounts to an aesthetic argument. When the narrator declares she would miss "the grey days for their own sake" (008-040), she is asserting the value of the muted, the atmospheric, the non-spectacular. This is also the novel's own aesthetic: prose that refuses the bright colours of dramatic incident in favour of the grey gradations of consciousness. Grey in _Interim_ is never nothing — it is always the specific quality of a particular stone, a particular sky, a particular hour. Its value lies in its refusal to announce itself.
