# Fire in *Interim*

In the winter world of *Interim*, fire is the novel's centripetal force. Characters gather around it, gaze into it, warm themselves by it. The fire structures domestic space and social ritual alike — the drawing-room fire where evening conversation unfolds, the dying embers that witness solitary reflection, the "extravagant blaze" that marks moments of heightened feeling.

## In the Novel

The novel opens with Mrs. Philps "backing towards the fire" [001-001] — the hearth already established as the room's orienting point. Throughout the narrative, characters move toward or away from fires, and the quality of a fire mirrors the quality of a scene: blazing for arrival and excitement, dying for departure and melancholy.

> The fire had not yet conquered the musty chill of the little drawing-room. [001-015]

This early description establishes fire as an active agent — it must "conquer" the cold, and its progress maps the room's gradual habitation. A fire that has not yet warmed the room is a room not yet alive.

## The Hearth as Social Centre

| Function | Description | Key PN |
|----------|------------|--------|
| Gathering point | Characters "sitting by the fire" is the novel's default social posture | [001-011] |
| Threshold marker | Arrivals and departures orient around the fireside | [001-013] |
| Intimacy enabler | The dying fire permits quieter, more personal talk | [001-017] |
| Temporal anchor | "Christmas lingered" in the fire's warmth past the holiday | [002-002] |
| Solitude's companion | Miriam alone "by the dying fire unwearied" | [003-009] |

The fireside circle creates what the dinner table cannot: an informal, self-selecting gathering where talk can drift and silences are permitted. "In front of her about the fireside were two large armchairs" [002-008] — the furniture arranges itself around the hearth as social life arranges itself around the fire.

## Key Passages

> The room was lit only by the firelight. From its wide clear core streamed a steady radiance. [004-007]

This is the novel's most concentrated firelight passage. The room stripped of gaslight, reduced to firelight alone, becomes a space of pure sensory presence. The "wide clear core" and "steady radiance" suggest a centredness — visual and spiritual — that gaslight, with its public glare, cannot provide.

> The fire still blazed its extravagant welcome. [001-013]

Fire as welcome, as greeting. The blaze is "extravagant" — excessive, generous — matching the emotional pitch of arrival. Richardson's fires are never merely functional; they carry affective charge.

> She was waiting tremulously in front of the caverns of the dying fire. [001-017]

The dying fire's "caverns" — hollows of collapsing ember — mirror a state of suspension and inwardness. Waiting "tremulously" before a dying fire: this is the novel's characteristic posture, the interim between event and event, between one state of being and the next.

> People, sitting in the winter by fires and worrying about right and wrong. [006-016]

The compression here is characteristic: winter, fires, moral worry — the domestic hearth as the setting for existential unease. Richardson does not separate inner life from its physical setting.

## Significance

Fire in *Interim* functions as more than setting. It is the sensory anchor of the novel's domestic world — the source of light, warmth, and gathering. But it is also a register of emotional temperature: blazing for arrival, steady for intimacy, dying for departure. To track the fires through the novel is to track the rhythm of its feeling.

This is modernist domestic realism of a particular kind: the hearth is not sentimentalised but rendered as a phenomenological fact — the thing toward which bodies orient themselves in winter, the thing whose light alters the quality of a face and a conversation. Richardson's attention to fire is part of her larger project: to record consciousness as it is shaped by the ordinary sensory world.
