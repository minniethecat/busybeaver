## Overview

The word "young" recurs through *Interim* as a form of address, a social category, and a self-perception. Miriam is repeatedly called "young lady" by the Baileys, by visitors, by strangers — a phrase that marks her position in a generational and gendered hierarchy. Yet the novel also explores the interior experience of youth: its vulnerability, its performance, its strange combination of exposure and invisibility.

## Manifestations

The phrase "young lady" functions almost as a refrain, appearing across multiple chapters with varying inflections. Mrs. Bailey uses it with maternal authority: "Here you are young lady sit here" [003-002], and later, more intimately, "Well young lady, I want to talk to you" [009-009]. Male visitors deploy it differently — Mr. Mendizabal's "you didn't expect to see me, young lady" [002-007] carries a teasing, proprietary tone, while the doctor's "That's all in order young lady" [010-019] is clinical and dismissive.

Youth is also something Miriam observes in others. She notices that someone "looked very young, a child speaking on sufferance" [001-013], and the "youngest girl was sent to bed" at ten o'clock [003-009]. These observations externalise what Miriam herself experiences: youth as a state of being managed by others.

The motif extends to appearance and social type: "a tall thin pale-faced young man in dark clothes" [002-006], "a slender young man darted noiselessly" [003-011], and "the young women in the sitting-room" [006-003] — youth as a visible category that determines how one is seen and treated. A darker note emerges in Miriam's fantasy of dying "young and unknown to anyone in the street" [008-025], where youth becomes synonymous with anonymity and unfulfilment.

## Key Passages

- **[003-002]**: "Here you are young lady sit here — said Mrs. Bailey" — the foundational instance of "young lady" as a domestic address that positions Miriam within the household order.
- **[008-025]**: "would suddenly fall down, dead; young and unknown to anyone in the street" — youth as existential vulnerability, the terror of dying before being fully known.
- **[009-009]**: "Well, young lady, I want to talk to you" — Mrs. Bailey's intimate yet authoritative tone, the phrase becoming a vehicle for serious conversation.
- **[001-013]**: "She looked very young, a child speaking on sufferance" — youth observed from outside, reduced to a condition of tolerated speech.
