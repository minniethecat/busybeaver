# Dinner in *Interim*

Dinner at [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] is the novel's central social institution — the daily gathering that brings the boarding-house residents together around a common table. More than a meal, dinner is a ritual of communal life, a theater of manners, and the primary stage on which the novel's characters perform their social identities.

## The Dinner Table as Social Theater

The dinner table at Banbury Park is where the household assembles — where the doctors debate, where [[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] presides, and where [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]] observes. The seating arrangement, the serving of food, and the flow of conversation all encode the social hierarchies of the boarding house.

> After dinner Miriam left the dining-room. [008-013]

This simple sentence — "after dinner" — marks the transition from public to private, from the collective ritual to individual solitude. The dinner provides the temporal structure within which the evening unfolds.

## Dinner and Class

The dinner table reveals class in its smallest details — who can afford what, who serves whom, who speaks and who listens. The "shilling dinners" that one character resorts to are a reminder that the respectable dinner table is an economic achievement, not a natural fact.

> That evening both Dr. Heber and Dr. Hurd appeared at dinner. Mrs. Bailey tumultuously arranged the seating. [006-019]

The doctors' appearance at dinner is an event — their professional status demanding special arrangement, their conversation dominating the table. Dinner is where professional masculinity asserts itself.

## Significance

Dinner in *Interim* exemplifies Richardson's method: the ordinary ritual rendered significant through the quality of attention paid to it. The dinner table is where the novel's themes — class, gender, nationality, the possibility of genuine connection — converge in the simple act of shared eating.

Like [[tea-in-interim|tea]] and [[evening-in-interim|evening]], dinner is one of the ritual structures that give the novel its form. Without plot in the conventional sense, the novel relies on these repeated gatherings to create pattern and meaning.
