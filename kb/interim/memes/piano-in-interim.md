## The Piano as Social Centre

The piano occupies a central place in the social life of *Interim*. It is the object around which gatherings form — "the group about the piano were taken by surprise" [004-002] — and the instrument through which characters express what they cannot say in words. The "cottage piano standing at right angles" [005-002] in the boarding house parlour is both furniture and focal point.

> The group about the piano were taken by surprise, waiting… [004-002]

## Music and Emotion

The piano channels emotion that exceeds language. Miriam perceives it as "fearfully pathetic; as if the piano were heartbroken" [002-009], projecting her own affective state onto the instrument. The "quiet shock of it standing" [002-008] registers the piano's presence as almost animate.

> …fearfully pathetic; as if the piano were heartbroken. It could be… [002-009]

The piano also represents a lost competence Miriam envies — she imagines knowing "piano scores and get them and play" [004-001] as a form of cultural belonging she lacks.

## Performance and Identity

Who plays the piano, and how, reveals social identity in the boarding house. Grace casually "striking random notes on the piano" [001-017] signals ease and ownership of the communal space, while Miriam's self-conscious distance from the instrument marks her outsider status. The "good piano" [004-008] — the adjective quietly implying a judgment about quality and class — becomes the measure of a performance's legitimacy. When a character "sat busily down to the piano again, angrily recording his" music [007-002], the adverb captures the emotional stakes invested in musical competence. The piano is not just furniture but a stage on which the boarding house residents perform their claims to cultural refinement.

> …striking random notes on the piano. Grace laughed. [001-017]

> …but the music on the good piano and now when she had forgotten… [004-008]
