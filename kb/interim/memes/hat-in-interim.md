## Introduction

Hats in *Interim* are never merely accessories. They frame faces, signal class and personality, and mark the threshold between indoors and outdoors, private and public. The novel opens with Florrie appearing "from under the wide brim of a heather mixture hat" [001-001], the hat becoming the first visual detail — before the face, before the room. Throughout the narrative, hats are removed, smoothed, packed into boxes, worn at angles; each gesture of hat-wearing or hat-removing carries social meaning.

## The Opening Scene

The novel's first hat is Florrie's: "smiling brilliantly from under the wide brim of a heather mixture hat" [001-001]. The wide brim frames her brilliance, the heather mixture suggesting taste and a certain rural solidity. Moments later, Grace is "smoothing tissue paper into a large hat box" [001-001], preparing a hat — "it must be an enormous one — It is; it's huge" — for an occasion. The hat box conversation between Grace and Miriam establishes the hat as an object of admiration, aspiration, and female intimacy.

## Hats and Social Identity

Hats mark distinctions of class and age. The "heather mixture" of Florrie's hat speaks of country practicality; the enormity of the hat in Grace's box suggests a more theatrical, urban sensibility. Miriam recalls warning Grace "if she had heard about the hats" [001-017] — hats here standing in for the entire system of social grouping that Grace had not yet learned. "Grace had not yet found out that people were arranged in groups" [001-017], and hats are one of the visible markers of that arrangement.

## The Hat as Frame

A hat frames the face, altering perception. Miriam's "hair had been flattened by her hat becomingly over the broad top of her head, and its mass pushed down in a loose careless bundle" [001-001]. The hat shapes the hair, the hair shapes the face, and the face carries the identity. Removing a hat is an act of arrival — crossing the threshold into domestic space. The hat is the last piece of the outside world to be shed.
