# Clock in _Interim_

**Clocks** in _Interim_ mark the hours with quiet insistence, anchoring the narrative's fluid consciousness in the grid of measured time. Richardson's clocks are nearly always heard before they are seen — striking, chiming, sounding from church towers — making time an acoustic presence in the novel's world.

## St. Pancras Clock

The clock of St. Pancras Church is the novel's dominant timekeeper, heard rather than viewed. Its chime "striking down the chimney" [PN=002-003] enters the domestic interior from outside, a public sound penetrating private space. Later, Miriam waits "for the striking of nine o'clock from St. Pancras church" [PN=008-017], the chime functioning as a cue for permission or release. The clock links interior time-consciousness to the city's collective rhythm.

> …St. Pancras clock — striking down the chimney… [PN=002-003]

## Domestic Clocks

Clocks within rooms carry a different emotional weight. "The clock on the mantelpiece was telling…" [PN=010-001] stands as an unfinished sentence, the clock's message suspended. Miriam "turn[s] round to the clock and back again to consult the pictures and the wallpaper" [PN=004-013], checking the time as part of her restless survey of a room. "At ten o'clock the youngest girl was sent to…" [PN=003-009] shows the clock governing the household's daily dispatch.

## Clock-Time and Hunger

The novel repeatedly couples clock-time with bodily hunger. "It was eleven o'clock. She was faint with hunger" [PN=004-012] makes the clock's announcement immediately physical. The alignment of social time ("eleven o'clock") with bodily need ("faint with hunger") is characteristic of Richardson's method — abstract measurement answered by urgent sensation.

## The Clock and Perception

Looking at a clock, in Richardson's rendering, is rarely just about knowing the hour. The act of looking up becomes a hinge between inner and outer awareness. "In looking up at the clock she had become aware of detailed people grouped at tables" [PN=006-016] captures this precisely: the clock-check is the gesture that opens perception outward, the upward glance at the timepiece suddenly revealing the social scene that was already there. The clock, paradoxically, returns the observer to the present moment rather than abstracting her from it.

Elsewhere, the absence of a visible clock produces a different kind of temporal awareness. "It must be only about nine o'clock… the gas flared out making a winter brilliance" [PN=008-020] — here time is estimated not from a dial but from atmospheric cues, the quality of light standing in for the missing clock. The gaslight's "winter brilliance" serves as an index of the hour, linking time-sense to the sensory texture of the room.

## Public Clocks and the Urban Landscape

Beyond St. Pancras, clocks appear as features of the London streetscape, anchoring the narrative in a recognisable city. A "lit building with a frosted lower window and a clock… a post office" [PN=008-022] catches Miriam's attention during a nocturnal walk, the clock here part of the municipal infrastructure — functional, impersonal, always visible. Such public timepieces form a network of temporal markers across the city, each one a reminder that private consciousness moves within a shared grid of measured hours.

## Announcing the Hour

To speak the time aloud, in the novel's social world, can be an act of force. "'Eleven o'clock.' She had shattered the silence he had built" [PN=008-026] — the declaration of the hour is not neutral information but a rupture, an intrusion of temporal order into a carefully maintained quiet. The clock here is invoked as a weapon, its authority borrowed to break a mood. The sentence captures the double life of clock-time in _Interim_: it is at once the most ordinary of observations and, in the right context, an act of social aggression.
