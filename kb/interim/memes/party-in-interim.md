# Party in _Interim_

The **party** in _Interim_ is a charged social arena — a space where Miriam must perform, observe, and endure. Whether a birthday celebration, a garden party, or a formal dinner, the gathering concentrates the novel's themes of social performance, class anxiety, and self-consciousness into a single room. Parties are events that Miriam prepares for and recovers from, their anticipation and aftermath often more vivid than the event itself. Richardson's treatment of the party belongs to a recognisably modernist disillusionment with social ritual — the party promises communion but delivers performance.

## The Birthday Party

The novel's most explicit party reference is a "birthday party; twenty-seex wheeskies and soda" [PN=001-010], heard through the noise of a boarding-house. The party is registered as sound before it is seen — "the tumult" that rises from another room. The specific count of whiskies ("twenty-seex") suggests both the scale of the event and the observing narrator's detached, almost anthropological notation of its consumption. The phonetic rendering of "six" as "seex" captures the accent and register of the reporting voice, embedding class and regional markers within the act of observation itself.

> …a birthday party; twenty-seex wheeskies and soda… [PN=001-010]

The birthday party is experienced from outside — Miriam hears it rather than participates in it. This position of auditory eavesdropping is characteristic of her relationship to social gatherings throughout the novel: she is proximate to festivity but not absorbed by it, close enough to register its details but distant enough to analyse them.

## The Garden Party

A remembered garden party appears through the filter of clothing: "the dress she had worn at the league garden party in the summer" [PN=001-017]. The party is not narrated directly but reconstructed through its sartorial trace — the dress that was chosen, worn, and now remembered. This is characteristic of Richardson's method: the social event is present not as scene but as its emotional and material residue. The dress carries the party forward in time, making the past gathering available to present consciousness through the sensory memory of what was worn.

The garden party, as a specifically outdoor and summertime variant of social gathering, also carries class connotations — it is the party form of a certain kind of English middle-class leisure, and Miriam's attendance registers her ambiguous position within that world.

## The Dinner Party

The dinner party represents the formal extreme of social gathering. "How did the dinner-party go off?" [PN=008-203] is asked as a direct question, the phrase "go off" suggesting the explosive potential of the event — a dinner party can succeed or fail, and its outcome is never guaranteed. Elsewhere, Miriam anticipates having to "sit, with a party, and afterwards find something to say" [PN=004-008], the party demanding both physical endurance (sitting still) and verbal performance (finding something to say). The dinner party, more than any other social form, imposes a script: courses must be served, conversations must be sustained, the performance must not falter.

## The Party as Composition

The party is also a visual arrangement. A dining group is described as a composition: "made up the party" [PN=006-004], the phrase suggesting the party is assembled like a still-life or a theatrical tableau. Each diner is a component, and the success of the whole depends on their arrangement. The party, like a painting, must balance its elements — and Miriam, ever the observer, is as aware of the composition as she is of her place within it.

## Aftermath and Emotional Residue

Parties in *Interim* do not end when the guests depart. They persist in the consciousness as emotional residue — relief, exhaustion, self-reproach, or rare satisfaction. Miriam's post-party reflections are often more narratively significant than the parties themselves, for it is in the aftermath that she processes what the gathering revealed about herself and others. This structural pattern — event followed by extended reflection — mirrors the novel's broader commitment to consciousness as its primary subject: the party matters not for what happened but for what it means, and meaning unfolds slowly, in the quiet hours after the last guest has gone.
