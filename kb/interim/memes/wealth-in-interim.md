## Overview

Wealth in *Interim* is observed rather than possessed. Miriam Henderson, a dental assistant living at a Tansley Street boarding-house, moves through a London where money is the invisible architecture of daily life — determining who lives where, who speaks to whom, and what futures are imaginable. She is neither wealthy nor destitute, and this in-between position gives her consciousness its particular acuity: she sees the economic texture of things precisely because she must work for her own.

## Thematic Dimensions

Wealth appears first as sensory abundance. At the Brooms' comfortable home, Miriam is "ensconced in her wealth" [001-015], the verb suggesting enclosure and protection — wealth as a warm room one inhabits. The "rich strong coffee" [001-013] served there is wealth made liquid, tasted, shared. Against this stands poverty, which Richardson renders as paralysis: "someone sitting paralyzed; poverty, everything being different" [001-013]. Poverty is not a condition but a transformation of the entire world — "everything" reshaped by lack. The economic anxieties of the boarding-house are the novel's bass note: residents who "lived in a poor lodging" yet could somehow present themselves otherwise [001-014], the "polished hair" that was "poor and worn" [002-006], the constant negotiation between appearance and reality that money — or its absence — demands.

Money and independence are knotted together in Miriam's consciousness. She notes with satisfaction that others have "regular off times and plenty of money to buy clothes and presents" [001-011], the phrase carrying both envy and appraisal. Yet wealth is also suspect: the novel registers "the new definition of vice... money can't produce money" [004-010], a cryptic moral judgment that separates earned living from the corruptions of capital. Miriam's own position — working, self-supporting, neither dependent nor secure — is the novel's ethical centre. She has witnessed "her theft from the wealth they had provided" [003-009], the language of theft and provision framing wealth as obligation and betrayal as well as comfort. The social performance of having and lacking runs through every dinner-table scene: who can afford ease, whose clothes announce their means, whose future is secured by inheritance and whose depends on the next week's wages.

## Key Passages

- **[001-015]** "ensconced in her wealth" — Wealth as enclosure, a protective shell. The Brooms' home offers Miriam a glimpse of comfort she does not herself possess.
- **[001-013]** "someone sitting paralyzed; poverty, everything being different" — Poverty as ontological transformation, not mere shortage. The world itself changes texture.
- **[001-011]** "regular off times and plenty of money to buy clothes and presents" — Miriam's appraisal of others' economic circumstances, the observational precision of someone who must budget her own.
- **[004-010]** "the new definition of vice... money can't produce money" — A moral calculus that distinguishes earned living from the abstract circulations of wealth.
- **[003-009]** "witnessed her theft from the wealth they had provided" — Wealth as gift and burden, the obligations that come with receiving.
