## Introduction

French runs through *Interim* as a secondary current — the language of lessons, of cultural contrast, and of quiet intimacy between women. Sissie, the young boarder at Banbury Park, takes French lessons from Miriam Henderson, and these lessons become the occasion for a relationship that the novel treats with characteristic delicacy. Beyond the lessons, French appears as a point of cultural reference — what the English are not, what the continent represents — in a novel otherwise steeped in London's particular Englishness.

## The French Lessons

Sissie's most distinctive narrative function is as Miriam's pupil in French, "a relationship that weaves together the novel's interests in language, social class, and the quiet dramas of boarding-house intimacy." The lessons are not merely pedagogic — they are a form of closeness, a shared activity that allows two women who live in the same house but occupy different social positions to meet on neutral ground. French becomes a space apart from the hierarchies of the boarding-house.

## French as Cultural Contrast

The novel's glancing references to French culture serve as foils for English life. A character observes of continental doctors: "the Germans and the French, you have only to look at them. They are brutal." [008-108] The judgment is harsh but revealing — it names an English suspicion of continental directness, a preference for the veiled and the indirect that the novel itself both practises and anatomises. French represents a frankness that English social life forbids.

## Language and Intimacy

Learning a language together creates a particular kind of intimacy. Miriam and Sissie's French lessons are moments when the usual social distances — teacher and pupil, older and younger woman, established boarder and newcomer — are temporarily suspended. In speaking French, they are both learners, both reaching toward something outside themselves. The language lesson becomes, in Richardson's rendering, one of the novel's quiet forms of connection.
