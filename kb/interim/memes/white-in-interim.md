## Introduction

White recurs throughout *Interim* as a colour that carries contradictory associations: purity and pallor, light and blankness, domestic order and unsettling stillness. From white tablecloths to white faces, from the white façade of a London house to white moonlight, the colour operates as a visual register of the novel's emotional and social atmosphere.

## Faces and Bodies

White most frequently describes the human face, and here its meaning is ambivalent. Dr. Wayneflete's "white criminal face" [007-009] links pallor to moral unease, while the "white brow" [003-010] of an imagined skater suggests refinement. "Mr. Mendizabal's white face unconscious under his" composure [006-023] makes whiteness a sign of withdrawal — the face as mask. The "phantom white faces of waiting passengers" [004-008] at the station transform human figures into ghostly apparitions, the colour draining them of individuality.

## Domestic Surfaces

White also marks the domestic interior. "The empty white coffee cups standing about" [005-004] after a gathering register absence rather than fullness. The "golden gaslight pouring over the white tablecloth" [006-001] creates a luminous domestic scene — whiteness as a surface that receives and transforms light. A "white apron" [004-012] on a hurrying man signals service, labour, and the ordered household.

## Architecture and Light

Beyond the interior, white marks the built environment. "The white façade of a large London house" [001-016] caught in sunlight suggests solidity and social standing. Euston Station "shone white across the way" [008-022], the public building made luminous and inviting. "White plaster houses against the blue" sky [008-008] and "white houses" with geranium balconies [008-012] evoke a Mediterranean idyll that contrasts with London's greyness — white as a colour of elsewhere, of aspiration.
