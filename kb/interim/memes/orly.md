# Mrs. Orly

The Orly family appear in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* as an alternative domestic presence — a family household set against the boarding-house world of Banbury Park. They are known to Miriam Henderson through social connections, and their home represents a different kind of English domesticity: careful, respectable, family-centred.

## Character Overview

The Orlys are always referred to collectively — "the Orlys" — rather than as distinct individuals. This plural naming is itself significant: they function in the novel as a unit, a family system, rather than as a collection of separate personalities. Mrs. Orly is the only member named individually, and even then, her identity is inseparable from the family entity she heads.

> …and careful people like Mrs. Orly could buy it… [004-007]

The adjective "careful" is telling. In a novel where money is a constant subtext — the cost of rooms, the price of meals, the precarious economy of the boarding-house — Mrs. Orly's carefulness marks her as a competent domestic manager. She belongs to the world of solvent respectability, the people who can "buy it."

## In the Novel

### A Social Contrast

The Orlys serve partly as a point of contrast to the Bailey household. Where Banbury Park is a collection of unrelated boarders — Germans, Scotch, Canadians, young women and professional men thrown together by economic necessity — the Orlys represent the normative family unit.

> …cheered by the protests of the Orlys and ending with warm hours in… [002-004]

The "protests of the Orlys" suggest lively disagreement, a family that debates and discusses. Their protests are "cheering" — invigorating rather than alienating — which distinguishes them from the more guarded social performances at the Bailey dinner table.

### Miriam's Relationship

Miriam's connection to the Orlys is one of social acquaintance. She visits their home; she registers their way of life as distinct from her own.

> "You really like the Orlys, don't you?" [008-229]

The question — put to Miriam by another character — suggests that her fondness for the Orlys is visible, perhaps even notable. The Orlys represent something Miriam values: domestic stability, familial warmth, a mode of living that the boarding-house can approximate but never fully reproduce.

## The Orly Household

> …thing in the morning and the Orlys during the day. [004-005]

The temporal division — morning for one thing, "the Orlys during the day" — suggests the rhythm of a visit. The Orlys are not a permanent presence in Miriam's life; they are an intermittent one, a household she goes to and returns from. Their home is a destination, not a residence.

> Of course the Orlys immediately said Oh how nice… [004-006]

The phrase "of course" implies predictability — the Orlys can be counted on for a certain kind of response. Their "Oh how nice" is the polite reflex of respectable domesticity, the social script that governs family-home interactions. The Orlys are reliable, which in the uncertain world of the boarding-house is itself a form of value.

## Significance

The Orlys are a minor presence in the novel, but their narrative function is precise. They embody domestic normality — the family household, the careful management of money, the predictable social responses — against which the more fluid and precarious arrangements of the boarding-house can be measured. In a novel deeply concerned with how people live together, the Orlys provide one answer: the family, careful and respectable, buying things and saying "Oh how nice."
