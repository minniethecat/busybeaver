# Coat in _Interim_

**Coats** and outerwear in _Interim_ appear at moments of arrival and departure, on thresholds between indoor and outdoor life. As the outermost layer of dress, coats function in the novel as social signals — marking class, profession, and the transition from private to public self.

## Coats and Departure

Coats signal imminent movement. Miriam "lifted her coat from her knees and stood up" [PN=002-002], the gesture closing one scene and opening the next. The coat on the knee is a held readiness, a promise of departure. In the novel's world of visits and calls, putting on or taking off a coat is a small social ritual repeated across chapters.

> She lifted her coat from her knees and stood up… [PN=002-002]

## Coats and Male Identity

Men's coats in the novel do descriptive work, sketching character through clothing. Mr. Mendizabal wears "a funny little short overcoat" [PN=006-019] — the adjective "funny" doing double duty as description and judgment. A stranger is fixed in perception by "a thin faded grey overcoat, a sharp greyish yellow profile" [PN=006-028], coat and face forming a single impression. "His brown beard and his frock-coat just for the time he was talking" [PN=007-009] suggests the frock-coat as temporary role rather than permanent identity.

A more imposing figure is rendered through his coat: "a large expensive spread of smooth well-cut black coat; a huge figure, sitting tall, with easily moving head reared high" [PN=009-098]. Here the coat is not a separate garment but an extension of the man's physical presence — the words "spread" and "smooth" and "well-cut" making the coat a second skin of wealth and authority. In contrast, a "striped cotton jacket" [PN=003-011] worn indoors signals a different relationship to labour and class, the cotton jacket a working garment rather than a social costume.

> …men in dark clothes and a light waistcoat flashed past her and leaped lightly down the stairs… [PN=002-006]

## Fur and Fantasy

A surprising cluster of references to fur coats appears in speculative mode. "Snowy weather and put on a fur coat to go out; but I should be so…" [PN=008-064] and "wouldn't be able to wear a fur coat" [PN=008-068] suggest Miriam imagining alternative selves through clothing — the fur coat as fantasy of luxury and self-transformation.

## The Ritual of Arrival and Removal

Outerwear in _Interim_ participates in a repeated choreography of arrival. "Let me take your cloak" [PN=005-002] is the host's script, the offer that transforms a visitor into a guest. The removal of the outer layer is a small threshold — the point at which the public self is shed and the domestic self admitted. "Miriam slipped off her golf-cape and he disappeared between curtains" [PN=005-002] captures the swiftness of this transition: the cape is removed, and immediately the host has vanished into another room, the ritual completed almost before it registers.

Richardson is attentive to how outerwear conveys character even — perhaps especially — in stillness. "The very hang of her cloak" [PN=004-012] suggests that a garment's drape can reveal what words do not, the cloak's fall communicating something essential about its wearer. A "clumsy golf-cape thrown back from her shoulders" [PN=004-009] registers not only the garment but the casual confidence of its wearer — "thrown back" implying ease and indifference to effect.

## Coats and Memory

A coat can also function as a vessel of recollection. The question "Do you remember him coming out into the hall one evening when you were brushing your coat?" [PN=009-029] embeds the coat in shared memory. The act of brushing a coat — a mundane maintenance of clothing — becomes a scene around which a social memory crystallises. The coat here is not only what was worn but what was witnessed: a figure emerging into a hall, a moment held between two people through the shared image of coat-brushing.
