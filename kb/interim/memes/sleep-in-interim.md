# Sleep in _Interim_

**Sleep** in _Interim_ occupies the boundary between the social world of waking life and the private realm of unconsciousness. Richardson attends not to dreams but to the physical experience of sleep itself — its approach, its quality, and the renewal it brings to the body.

## The Threshold of Sleep

Sleep arrives in the novel as a gentle recession from awareness. Miriam is described as coming "so gently up from her deep sleep" [PN=001-012], the adverb registering the gradual, almost reluctant return to consciousness. Waking is not a sharp break but a slow surfacing, the body still heavy with sleep's residue.

The conventional question "Did you sleep well" [PN=001-013] appears as a piece of morning ritual — Mrs. Philps's inquiry is at once genuine care and social formula. Miriam's "sleepily feeling for a thread of conversation" [PN=001-017] captures the peculiar mental state between sleep and full wakefulness, when thought moves slowly and the demands of sociability feel remote.

> …she came so gently up from her deep sleep. She held in her joy to listen… [PN=001-012]

## Sleep and Bodily Renewal

Sleep in Richardson's treatment is fundamentally a bodily experience. Miriam's "body was full of sleep and strength; all one strength" [PN=011-001] describes sleep not as absence but as accumulation — the body replenished, unified, rendered powerful by rest. This is sleep as physiological fact rather than psychological symbol.

After a period of rest, she finds herself "rested as if by sleep" [PN=008-017], the simile suggesting that certain waking states can approximate the restorative quality of sleep. The distinction between sleep and deep rest blurs.

## Sleep and the Rhythms of the Household

Sleep in *Interim* also marks the temporal architecture of domestic life. The speculation that "they must both have gone to sleep again" [001-010] after a morning disturbance registers sleep as a shared household rhythm — who is asleep and who is awake defines the distribution of awareness and responsibility in the domestic space. The youngest girl is "sent to bed" [003-009], sleep imposed by adult authority as a disciplinary boundary.

The morning inquiry — "Did you sleep well" [001-013] — recurs as a ritual of re-entry into the social world. The question is less about the fact of sleep than about readiness: has the night's restoration prepared the body for another day of social performance? Sleep is the private interlude between public selves.

## Sleep, Consciousness, and the Narrative

Sleep poses a representational problem for the stream-of-consciousness novel: consciousness ceases, yet the narrative must bridge the gap. Richardson meets this challenge by treating sleep as a presence marked by its edges — the approach to sleep and the return from it. The "slumber" into which a profile "smouldered" [003-011] during a lesson suggests consciousness sinking not into nothingness but into a kind of low flame, a diminished awareness that still registers warmth.

The evening world prepares for sleep with its own rituals. The "London square turning to green below in the lamplight, sinking to sleep, deeply breathing out its freshness" [008-019] extends sleep from the individual body to the city itself — London as a breathing organism settling into nightly repose.

## Sleep Environments

Richardson notes the material conditions of sleep. The bedroom that "keeps you hardy and you sleep better" [PN=008-060] links the quality of sleep to the physical environment — cold air, open windows, the bracing conditions of the English bedroom. Reading "in bed and you go to sleep in healthy bracing air" [PN=008-062] positions sleep as the natural terminus of the evening's reading, the book dropping from the hand as consciousness fades.

The youngest girl being "sent to bed" [PN=003-009] registers sleep from outside — the adult imposition of bedtime, sleep as discipline rather than desire.
