# Sky in _Interim_

**Sky** in _Interim_ serves as a recurring atmospheric register, marking shifts in time, mood, and Miriam's perceptual attention. Richardson's narrator looks upward at key moments, using the sky's changing states — from "gold-lit" to "mother-of-pearl" — as an index of inner weather.

## The Evening Sky

The sky at dusk is the novel's most sustained skyscape. "The grey brilliance of the sky was upon the pavement" [PN=008-008] captures the peculiar luminosity of a London evening, while "presently the sky would be mother-of-pearl above the shaded lamplight" [PN=008-019] registers the gradual colour-shift with painterly precision. Miriam offers "her evening sky as a full answer" [PN=008-018], as if the visible heavens could substitute for speech.

> …the sky soared up high above the growing dusk… [PN=001-015]

## Sky and Weather

Individual weather events are tracked against the sky. "The grey brilliance of the sky was upon the pavement and in the gutters" [PN=008-008] follows rain, while "a vast fresh gold-lit sky somewhere behind the twilight" [PN=006-017] suggests clearing after storm. Richardson draws a firm distinction between sky and mere weather: "A grey day is not melancholy" [PN=008-038], insisting on sky's emotional neutrality against the pathetic fallacy.

## Sky Through Windows

Like much of the novel's visual experience, sky is often framed by windows. Miriam turns "towards the glowing sky with its back to the rest of the room" [PN=008-014], and the sky seen through glass becomes both picture and escape. The "tilted lengthways across the sky" view [PN=002-003] shows how architecture shapes even this most unbounded element.
