## Introduction

Family in *Interim* is defined by absence rather than presence. Miriam Henderson's family — the Newlands home, her mother, her sisters — exists in memory and in brief letters, not in daily proximity. The novel opens with her "alone in a cold bedroom" on New Year's Eve [002-002], a fact that marks her as unmoored from family and conventional festivity. The boarding-house at Banbury Park provides a makeshift substitute, but it is a family of strangers, governed by economic transaction rather than blood.

## The Lost Newlands

The family home at Newlands haunts the novel as an idyll of irretrievable domestic wholeness. Miriam contemplates "the unspoken inexpressible beauty of Newlands" [004-007], her family's former home — beauty as memory, as loss, as something that exists only in the gap between what can be said and what can be felt. Newlands is not merely a house but the symbolic centre of a life that no longer exists: the family gathered, the garden tended, the social world intact. Its loss is the novel's founding wound.

## The Boarding-House as Substitute Family

Banbury Park assembles a household of unrelated adults who approximate familial roles without the bonds of kinship. Mrs. Bailey presides as a maternal figure; the boarders gather at meals like a family at table; the routines of arrival, departure, and shared domestic space echo family rhythms. But the boarding-house family is provisional and commercial — each member pays for their place, and the relationships are contingent. Miriam navigates this arrangement with the awareness that it is both liberating and hollow: freedom from family constraint, but also freedom from family belonging.

## Family and Language

The lost family world is associated with a lost language. Words in French or German carry a glamour that English, with its over-familiarity and its association with the constraining world of family and school, cannot match. The family's linguistic world — English, provincial, gendered — is one Miriam seeks to escape even as she mourns its loss. The novel's modernist project of rendering consciousness is itself a kind of departure from the family's linguistic inheritance.
