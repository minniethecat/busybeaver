## Overview

In *Interim*, physical appearance functions as a charged social surface — clothing, posture, and the act of looking are never neutral. Miriam's consciousness registers dress and presentation with acute sensitivity, both in herself and in others. The boarding-house setting intensifies this scrutiny, making every entrance and encounter a moment of mutual appraisal.

## Thematic Dimensions

Clothing operates as a social signifier throughout the novel. Miriam's "dingy blouse and tie" which "looked quite bright under the gaslight" [001-001] reveals her awareness of how artificial conditions transform appearance — what looks presentable in one light fails in another. Florrie appears "in her outdoor clothes smiling brilliantly" [001-001], her dress marking the boundary between inside and outside, the private and the public self. The economic dimension is never far: having "plenty of money to buy clothes and presents" [001-011] registers as a form of freedom that Miriam herself does not fully possess.

The act of seeing and being seen structures the novel's social encounters. Miriam "ignor[es] the invariable appearance of a peeping form" [002-006] at the boarding-house, yet the very notation betrays her heightened awareness of being watched. The foreign perspective intrudes tellingly when she wonders "how spanking would look to a Hindu" [001-010], destabilising the naturalness of English social practices by imagining them through alien eyes.

Miriam's self-consciousness extends beyond clothing to her whole presentation — her hat, her posture, the way she moves through rooms. She moves "with an appearance of briskness" [001-017], a performance of purpose that may or may not correspond to inner feeling. Appearance in *Interim* is thus both armour and exposure: a surface that simultaneously conceals and betrays the self beneath.

## Key Passages

- **[001-001]** Miriam's "dingy blouse and tie looked quite bright under the gaslight" — the opening pages establish her acute awareness of how lighting and context transform appearance.
- **[001-001]** "a large straight green dress appeared beside her" — dress itself becomes an event, clothing preceding full recognition of the person wearing it.
- **[001-010]** "how spanking would look to a Hindu" — a defamiliarising moment that reveals the cultural contingency of English bodily norms.
- **[001-017]** "with an appearance of briskness" — appearance as deliberate performance, the outer self managed for social consumption.
- **[002-006]** "ignoring the invariable appearance of a peeping form" — the boarding-house as theatre of mutual watching, where being seen is inescapable.
