# Ink in _Interim_

**Ink** in _Interim_ appears as the material residue of intellectual and emotional labour. Unlike speech, which vanishes into air, ink leaves a permanent trace — on the page, in the school essay, on the letter that will cross the city. Richardson registers ink not as a metaphor but as a physical substance, the medium through which thought becomes legible.

## Ink and Education

Ink enters the novel most directly through the world of schoolwork. Miriam recalls "the pages of a school essay, page after page, no red-ink corrections" [009-101], the absence of the teacher's corrective red ink marking a perfect piece of writing. The essay's "last page galloping along one long sentence; 'until England shall have recognised her cruel folly'" [009-101] — scored a perfect ten — suggests that ink, when flowing freely, can carry political conviction as well as academic exercise. The red ink of correction and the black ink of composition form a pedagogical system: one writes, the other judges.

## Ink and Letters

The world of letters in the novel depends on ink as its enabling medium. When Miriam's "mind flew, flaming, over the gossiping household, over Mrs. Bailey's thoughts as she pondered the evidence" [009-064], the unspoken context is a letter written and sent — ink having carried words across distances, making private thoughts available to unintended readers. Ink is the medium of both intimacy and risk: what is written cannot be unwritten.

## Ink and the Written Word

Unlike the novel's many oral exchanges — conversations that fade, voices that dissolve — writing with ink produces a durable object. The "strange indignation and certainty, coming suddenly while writing; there for always" [009-101] names the permanence that ink confers: once set down, thought hardens into a form that can be returned to, challenged, corrected. In a novel so invested in the ephemeral flux of consciousness, ink represents the counter-principle: the fixity of the written, the commitment of word to page.
