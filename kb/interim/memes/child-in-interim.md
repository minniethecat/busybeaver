# Child in _Interim_

The **child** in _Interim_ appears in several registers: as a remembered self, as a figure of vulnerability, and as a social category subject to adult authority. Richardson treats childhood not as a nostalgic ideal but as a state of diminished power — the child is someone spoken about, sent to bed, and used as the object of moral comparison.

## The Child as Vulnerable Self

Miriam is herself described in terms that evoke childhood. She looks "very young, a child speaking on sufferance, saying the first thing that occurs lest someone should remark that it was time to go to bed" [001-013]. The child here is not innocent but strategic — speaking to avoid being dismissed, navigating adult conversation from a position of precarity. The child-adult is a recurring figure: someone who has the intellectual equipment of adulthood but the social standing of a minor.

The remark "I should not have known I was not simple unless she had told me; that child" [007-010] captures the retrospective recognition of one's own childhood naivety — the child-self seen from the distance of experience as a distinct and slightly foreign being.

## Children as Social Category

Children in the novel are largely peripheral — glimpsed in passing or mentioned as constraints. The vague "two vague furtive children in skimpy blue serge dresses" [002-006] are barely individuated; they are elements of the Bailey household's economy rather than characters in their own right. "Children and housework and a selfish husband and nothing in life of her own" [004-011] names children as one term in a list of domestic burdens — the child as responsibility rather than joy.

The remark about how "the way we treat our children" [009-100] introduces a political dimension — the Irish narrator's comment about English child-rearing practices makes the treatment of children an index of national character. The child becomes the site where cultural norms are revealed and contested.

## The Child and Moral Hierarchy

The novel records a striking moral calculus: "I'd rather see a woman suffer than a man and a man rather than a child and a child rather than an animal" [008-097]. This descending scale — woman, man, child, animal — positions the child as more worthy of protection than adults, though less than animals. The hierarchy suggests an ethical sensibility in which innocence and vulnerability command increasing care as one descends the scale of power.
