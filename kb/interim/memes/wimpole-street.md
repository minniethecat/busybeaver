# Wimpole Street

Wimpole Street is a London thoroughfare in the Marylebone district, famous in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as a center of medical and dental practice. In Dorothy Richardson's novel *Interim*, Wimpole Street appears as the professional anchor of the Henderson family's world — the address where dental appointments are kept, where professional identities are maintained, and from which news and letters radiate outward to the domestic sphere of [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]].

## Location and Historical Context

Wimpole Street runs north-south through Marylebone, connecting [[harley-street|Harley Street]] (the most famous medical address in London) to the broader network of professional streets in the district. By the late Victorian period, Wimpole Street had become synonymous with private medical practice — a landscape of consulting rooms, waiting rooms, and the quiet comings and goings of patients.

The street's architecture — Georgian terraces converted to professional use — reflects the transformation of domestic space into commercial-medical space that characterized the professionalization of medicine and dentistry in the period. For the characters of *Interim*, Wimpole Street is both a workplace and a symbol of professional aspiration.

## In the Novel

Wimpole Street functions in the novel as the bridge between the domestic world and the professional world. It is where appointments are kept, where professional judgments are rendered, and where the economic realities that underwrite boarding-house life are transacted.

> Glancing through it at Wimpole Street. There was no need to have brought it. [002-006]

The glance toward Wimpole Street is casual — part of the texture of daily movement — but it carries the weight of professional obligation. The street is a destination, a point on the map of daily errands that structures the characters' movements through London.

> With a heavy price, here and at Wimpole Street … it's us; our family; always paying. [003-001]

This fragment reveals the economic dimension of Wimpole Street: it is a site of expense, of the "heavy price" that professional life exacts from the family. The dental practice that sustains the Hendersons also consumes them.

## The Weekend at Wimpole Street

One of the novel's most extended engagements with Wimpole Street comes in the context of a weekend gathering — a social occasion that blurs the boundary between professional and domestic space.

> The weekend still going on at Wimpole Street; her renewed sense of the simplicity of life there. [009-003]

The weekend at Wimpole Street represents an alternative mode of domesticity — simpler, perhaps, than the complex social negotiations of the boarding house. Miriam's "renewed sense" of this simplicity suggests that Wimpole Street holds a nostalgic or utopian charge for her.

> Tonight, going strong and straight to the morning door of Wimpole Street. [008-017]

The journey toward Wimpole Street is rendered as purposeful — "strong and straight" — in contrast to the more wandering, associative movements that characterize Miriam's other journeys through London.

## Wimpole Street and the Medical World

The medical character of Wimpole Street is central to its role in the novel. The Hendersons' identity as a dental family — with its particular professional status, its economic precariousness, its claim to gentility — is spatially anchored in this address. Wimpole Street is where professional identity is performed and validated.

For the novel's doctors — [[von-heber|von Heber]], Hurd, Winchester — Wimpole Street and its neighboring streets form the professional geography within which their careers and reputations are built. The consulting room is as much a stage as the drawing room, and Wimpole Street is its backdrop.

## Significance

Wimpole Street in *Interim* represents the world of professional work that exists alongside but separate from the domestic world of the boarding house. It is a place of appointments, fees, and professional judgment — the economic infrastructure that makes the domestic world possible.

The spatial relationship between Wimpole Street and the novel's other locations — [[st-pancras|St. Pancras]] to the east, [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] to the north — maps the characters' daily trajectories between work and home, professionalism and domesticity. In a novel concerned above all with a woman's consciousness, the male professional world of Wimpole Street is both adjacent and alien — a realm Miriam can visit but not inhabit.
