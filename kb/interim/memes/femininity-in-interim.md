## Introduction

Femininity in *Interim* is rendered as material culture — the objects, garments, and gestures through which womanhood is performed and perceived. Richardson's narrative consciousness registers the physical details of feminine presentation with the same precision it brings to weather, architecture, and the quality of light: "thin hard fingers of women chattering and tweaking" [008-148]. Femininity is not an essence but a practice, a set of embodied habits that the novel observes with ethnographic detachment.

## Dress and Identity

Clothing in the novel is never merely decorative. Miriam's golf-cape "thrown over her arm" signals her difference from the conventional femininity of the boarding-house world — it "became a sort of hallmark" of her uneasy relationship to feminine self-presentation. Dress is a language that the body speaks whether it intends to or not, broadcasting class affiliation, sexual availability, and the wearer's degree of investment in the performance of womanhood. Miriam navigates this sartorial semiotics with the awkwardness of someone who understands the code but cannot quite bring herself to comply with it.

## Domestic Objects and Gendered Space

The domestic interiors of the novel are thick with objects that carry gendered associations. The piano — "perfectly wrought and finished, music swathed and hidden in elegance" [002-008] — is a quintessentially feminine domestic object, its rose materials part of "its genteel femininity." Such objects structure feminine space: they are the material of the drawing-room, the accomplishments of the marriageable daughter, the décor of a domesticity that Miriam simultaneously inhabits and questions. Richardson's attention to these objects is neither celebration nor critique but a rendering of the material world in which feminine subjectivity takes shape.

## Femininity and Modernity

Miriam's uneasy relationship to conventional femininity is part of the novel's larger engagement with modernity. The New Woman of the late-Victorian and Edwardian periods — educated, mobile, economically semi-independent — represented a challenge to the established codes of feminine behaviour, and Miriam embodies this challenge in her daily life. She works for wages, walks alone in the city, thinks thoughts that do not serve the demands of feminine charm. Yet she is not immune to the pull of those codes — the desire to be seen as attractive, the pleasure of a well-chosen garment, the pull of the drawing-room's warmth. The novel's treatment of femininity holds these contradictions in suspension, refusing to resolve them.
