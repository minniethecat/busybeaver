## Introduction

Work is the steady bass note beneath the surface currents of *Interim*. As a dental secretary managing correspondence, accounts, and surgeries, Miriam's days are shaped by the rhythm of labour — the "steady morning's work" [002-004] that fills the hours between meals and social calls. Work is both a discipline and a refuge, a structure that holds her in place while her consciousness roams elsewhere.

## The Daily Round

Miriam's work is methodical and absorbing. "Her resolutions kept her at work on Saturday afternoon" [002-004], the discipline self-imposed. "A steady morning's work disposed of the correspondence and the inrush of paid accounts" [002-004], the language matter-of-fact, describing a competence she takes quiet pride in. "After lunch she worked in the surgeries until they were ready for Monday morning and made an attack on the mass of clerical work that remained from the old year" [002-004] — the verb "attack" revealing the energy she brings to the task. Work, in these passages, is not drudgery but a form of agency.

## Distraction and Drift

Yet work is also a surface beneath which deeper currents flow. "Miriam found herself in the midst of a train of thought that had distracted her during her morning's work" [003-011], the inner life continually breaking through the discipline of the desk. Her employer Mr. Hancock recognises the quality of that distraction — "the simple stupendous fact" [004-006] of her presence — even as she "busied herself with her dusting" [004-006]. The tension between outward industry and inward wandering is the novel's central rhythm.

## Work as Calling

Work in *Interim* carries spiritual weight. "Prayer was the work to do in life, nothing else" [004-011], Miriam reflects, elevating daily labour into a form of devotion. And yet she recoils from the narrowness of that vision: "Nothing beyond one's daily work and all the rest of the time being all things to all men" [004-011]. The day's work can feel "unreal, as easy as a dream" [008-002] or, by evening, leave behind "a cool solid clear head and rested limbs and the feeling that the strain of work was so far away that it could never return" [008-017]. Work is both burden and balm — the rhythm that makes the stillness of evening possible.
