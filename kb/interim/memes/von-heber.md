# Dr. von Heber

Dr. von Heber is a German dentist and resident of the [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]] boarding house in Dorothy Richardson's novel *Interim*. Soft-spoken, formally courteous, and marked by a "brown beard and frock coat," he represents a European cosmopolitanism that both attracts and unsettles the English characters around him. His romantic connection to [[eve-henderson|Eve Henderson]] and his abrupt departure for Canada make him one of the novel's most enigmatic figures — a man whose silence carries as much weight as his speech.

## Character Overview

Von Heber is introduced as a fellow lodger at Banbury Park, where his professional status as a dentist and his Continental manners set him apart from the English boarders. His name — the German "von" suggesting aristocratic lineage — prompts both curiosity and a faint xenophobia among the other characters. He is consistently described through his physical stillness and his smile: a "welcoming smile" that Miriam finds both disarming and, at times, unsettling.

## In the Novel

Von Heber appears primarily in the dinner-table scenes at Banbury Park, where conversations about science, religion, and society unfold. His quiet, measured speech contrasts with the more voluble English characters. He is a listener as much as a speaker, and his occasional interventions carry disproportionate weight.

> "My name's von Heber," he announced quietly, his reserve marking him immediately as a foreigner among the English boarders. [004-013]

His dental practice provides him with a professional identity that the novel treats with a mixture of respect and mild mockery — dentistry being, in the period, a newly professionalizing field.

## Key Relationships

### Eve Henderson

Von Heber's most significant relationship is with [[eve-henderson|Eve Henderson]], Miriam's independent sister. Their connection is conducted largely offstage, visible only through fragments: a letter opened by another character, a rushed departure, a confession of feeling.

> "That is so. It's von Heber I'm so mad about." [009-070]

Eve's admission reveals the depth of the attachment, but the relationship remains unconsummated in the narrative — von Heber leaves for Canada before anything is resolved.

### Miriam Henderson

Miriam observes von Heber with her characteristic intensity, reading his silences and gestures as texts to be interpreted. She finds in him both a potential ally and a figure of masculine opacity.

> Dr. von Heber's welcoming smile stood at the end of the room. They would remember. [008-248]

### Dr. Winchester

Von Heber's professional colleague Dr. Winchester acts as his advocate and interpreter within the household, describing him as "one in a thousand." [009-049]

### The Banbury Park Circle

At [[banbury-park|Banbury Park]], von Heber navigates the complex social dynamics of the boarding house — the [[bailey|Mrs. Bailey]]'s maternal oversight, the other lodgers' curiosity, the evening gatherings that structure domestic life.

## Departure for Canada

Von Heber's sudden departure for Canada is the novel's central romantic disruption. Without a farewell word to Eve, he leaves — and the narrative circles around the meaning of his absence.

> Dr. von Heber gone without a word. Perhaps it was better so. [009-040]

The departure becomes a prism through which Miriam examines questions of commitment, freedom, and the different trajectories available to men and women.

> "Von Heber's a man who'll carve his way." [007-007]

This judgment — admiring but also distancing — captures the double valence of von Heber's character: admirable in his competence, troubling in his opacity.

## Significance

Von Heber embodies several of the novel's core themes. As a German in England, he represents the foreign, the not-quite-assimilable — a figure through whom the novel explores national identity and cosmopolitanism. As a professional man, he stands for masculine competence and its limits. And as the object of Eve's romantic feeling, he catalyzes the novel's examination of love, choice, and the different freedoms available to men and women.

His silence — his refusal or inability to speak his feelings — makes him a character defined as much by absence as presence. In a novel built from the heroine's consciousness, von Heber is one of the figures who remains resolutely external, unknowable, and therefore magnetic.
