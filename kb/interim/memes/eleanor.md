# Eleanor Dear

Eleanor Dear appears late in *Interim* as a figure from the world of Canadian society — a reminder that the boarding-house at Banbury Park, for all its insularity, is connected to a larger social geography. She is associated with the warmth and possibility of colonial society, a contrast to the constrained English world of the novel's main settings.

## Character Overview

Eleanor's appearances are concentrated in the novel's later chapters, where she figures in conversations about Canada — the destination toward which several characters, including Dr. von Heber, are gravitating. She represents the social world that awaits across the Atlantic: warmer, more open, less constrained by English class distinctions.

> "Eleanor!" [009-088]

The single-word exclamation — her name called out in recognition — suggests a moment of social encounter, a greeting across a room. Richardson gives us the name and the moment but little more; Eleanor remains a sketch, a gesture toward a world the novel cannot fully enter.

## In the Novel

Eleanor's most significant passage involves her dress and appearance — the "tie" she arranges, the social self she presents.

> However Eleanor arranged the tie she could not get it to look like anything really new. [009-095]

The detail is domestic and sartorial — a tie that will not sit right, a look that will not cohere. Richardson renders Eleanor through this small failure of self-presentation: the desire to appear "really new" and the inability to achieve it. The tie becomes a minor symbol of the gap between social aspiration and bodily reality.

> Eleanor Dear was feeling at its doors the warm glow of Canadian society. [009-098]

The "warm glow of Canadian society" — the phrase suggests both genuine warmth and a certain ironic distance. Canada, for the characters of *Interim*, is a land of promise: Dr. von Heber departs for it, and Eleanor embodies its social possibilities. But "feeling at its doors" also suggests exclusion — she is at the threshold, not yet inside.

## Key Relationships

### Dr. von Heber

Eleanor is linked to [[von-heber|Dr. von Heber]] through the Canadian connection. Both belong to the novel's intimation of a world beyond England — a colonial modernity that offers escape from the constraints of the boarding-house and the class system it miniaturises.

### Eve Henderson

Like [[eve-henderson|Eve Henderson]], Eleanor represents an alternative model of womanhood — more socially mobile, less defined by domestic enclosure. But where Eve's independence is economic (the West End shop), Eleanor's is social: she moves in a world of "Canadian society" that promises a different kind of life.

## Significance

Eleanor Dear is a minor character in the strictest sense — she appears briefly, is sketched lightly, and vanishes. But her presence serves an important structural function: she extends the novel's social geography beyond London, beyond England, toward the colonial world that represents both escape and uncertainty. The "warm glow of Canadian society" is the novel's farthest horizon — a place where the constraints of the interim might finally loosen.
