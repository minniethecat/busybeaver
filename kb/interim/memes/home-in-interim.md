# Home in *Interim*

Miriam Henderson has no home. She moves between other people's houses — the Bailey boarding-house at Banbury Park, the Corries' in Harlesden, her sisters' flats — a visitor everywhere, belonging nowhere. The novel's title names her condition: she lives in the interim, between homes, between settled states. And from this unmoored position, Richardson builds her deepest inquiry into what home means.

## In the Novel

The question of home surfaces early and persists. "Probably she had some sort of home" [001-011] — the narrator's conjecture about Miriam, hedged with "probably" and "some sort of," captures the uncertainty. Home is not a fact but a hypothesis, something that may or may not exist.

> Her thoughts went homelessly to and fro between her sisters' homes. [001-014]

The adverb "homelessly" does the work here. Miriam's thoughts themselves are homeless — they have no fixed ground, no place to settle. They shuttle between the homes of others, belonging to none.

## Dimensions of Home

| Aspect | Manifestation in the Novel | Emotional Register |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Home as place | Banbury Park, Harlesden, remembered childhood houses | Ambiguous — none fully hers |
| Home as people | Sisters, the boarding-house circle | Provisional belonging |
| Home as ritual | Christmas, meals, evenings by the fire | Longing for tradition |
| Home as memory | The past that can't be returned to | Nostalgia and loss |
| Home as absence | What the novel's title names | The interim condition |

> At Christmas-time one should be where one belongs. [001-016]

Christmas crystallises the question of home. The "should" is moral — belonging somewhere is an obligation as much as a desire. And Miriam, a guest at someone else's Christmas, belongs nowhere in particular. The sentence is less a complaint than a statement of fact: the world is arranged so that one should belong somewhere, and she does not.

## Key Passages

> She seemed already more at home than the Baileys. [004-003]

The irony is pointed: the guest is more at home than the hosts. This is not a claim of superiority but an observation about ease — Miriam, precisely because she is a perpetual guest, has learned to inhabit temporary spaces fully. Being "at home" is a skill she has developed through its absence.

> It was her only home, and she must have all her meals there. [006-003]

The boarding-house as "only home" — the phrase carries both resignation and resolve. If this provisional place is all she has, then she will claim it fully. "She must have all her meals there" — the practical detail carries existential weight.

> But her mind was so at home in the Middle Ages that she seemed hardly aware of the modern woman talking to her. [004-009]

Home here is not a place but a mental habitation. To be "at home in the Middle Ages" while sitting in a modern drawing-room is to live in multiple temporalities at once — to be physically present but mentally elsewhere. This is the condition the novel explores throughout.

> She wandered slowly home through the drizzling rain. [004-012]

"Home" in this sentence means the boarding-house — a temporary shelter called home by default. The slow wandering, the drizzling rain, the reluctance: the journey homeward carries none of the comfort the word "home" conventionally promises.

## Understanding Home as Modernist Theme

Richardson's treatment of home distinguishes *Interim* from both the nineteenth-century domestic novel and the masculinist modernism of exile that was emerging in the same period.

In the Victorian novel, home is typically either a place of security (the happy ending the protagonist earns) or a place of confinement (the domestic prison from which the heroine must escape). Richardson refuses both patterns. Home in *Interim* is neither achieved nor rejected — it is simply absent, and the novel's interest is not in resolving that absence but in exploring what consciousness becomes when it has no fixed domestic ground.

This is different, too, from the modernist myth of the artist as voluntary exile — the Joyce or Pound who leaves home to find artistic freedom. Miriam has not chosen homelessness; it is her economic and social condition as a single working woman. Richardson treats this condition not as tragedy but as a distinctive vantage point. From the position of the perpetual guest, Miriam sees domestic life with a clarity unavailable to those inside it. The boarding-house, as a setting, is uniquely suited to this perspective: it is domestic without being private, social without being intimate, a place where the structures of home life are visible precisely because they are provisional.

For the reader encountering *Interim* for the first time, the novel's refusal to provide the comfort of a settled home may feel unsettling. But this is precisely Richardson's point: the interim condition — the state of being between homes, between identities, between certainties — is not a problem to be solved. It is the ground of a particular kind of attention, and the novel is the record of that attention.

## Significance

Richardson's treatment of home is quietly radical. In refusing to give her protagonist a settled domestic location, she refuses the narrative closure that home conventionally provides. Miriam's homelessness is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be explored — the ground from which a particular kind of consciousness emerges.

The boarding-house, as a setting, is the perfect expression of this condition. It is home-like without being a home: meals are provided, fires are lit, conversations unfold — but the arrangements are commercial, the belonging provisional. In this interim space, Richardson finds the conditions for the modernist consciousness she is inventing: alert, unmoored, attentive to the texture of the temporary.
