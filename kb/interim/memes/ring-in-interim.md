# Ring in _Interim_

The **ring** in _Interim_ — whether of a doorbell, a musical note, or a voice — functions as a signal that reorganises social space. A ring interrupts, summons attention, and redistributes the energies of a room. Richardson attends to rings as acoustic events that demand response, structuring the rhythm of domestic life.

## The Doorbell as Social Summons

The doorbell's ring is the novel's primary sound of arrival and departure. "There was a subdued ring at the front door bell. — There's Christine shall we have her in to change the plates" [001-010]: the ring announces a person before they appear, creating a moment of anticipation and negotiation. The household must decide — shall she come in now? The ring does not command; it proposes, and the residents must interpret.

The bell is also a signal of domestic authority. When "Florrie got up briskly and rang the bell" [001-013] to summon a servant, the ring is an act of command disguised as a simple physical gesture. The bell-ring divides the household into those who ring and those who answer.

## Musical Rings

Beyond the doorbell, Richardson uses "ring" to describe the quality of sound itself — its resonance, its carrying power, its emotional timbre. Miriam imagines "a sonata ringing out into" a room [002-009], the verb capturing the way music fills and transforms space. A laugh that "rang in her head; a single lively deep-chested note followed by a falsetto note that curved hysterically up" [003-002] records the after-effect of sound — the ring that persists in memory long after the sound itself has ceased.

Even voices have their ring. "Somewhere in her voice was something that rang restoringly round the world" [003-009]: the voice's ring is its power to reach beyond its immediate context, to sound in the listener's inner ear. The foreign voice "rang whatever it said in bright sunshine" [003-009], the ring of the voice creating an atmosphere independent of the words spoken.

## The Ring of Exhaustion

At the opposite extreme, the ring can signal depletion rather than vitality. "The exhausted air rang with lifeless strident voices in shoutings and heavy thick flattened unconcerned speech" [006-026] describes a room where sound has become oppressive — ringing not with music but with noise, the acoustic environment registering social fatigue. The ring here is the reverberation of a world that has talked itself hollow.
