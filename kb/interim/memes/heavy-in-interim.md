## Introduction

Heaviness in *Interim* names a pervasive condition — the weight of furniture, the heaviness of a body on a chair, the heavy dish of food passed at the supper table, the heavy movements of a person uncertain in a strange house. It is at once physical and emotional, a word that bridges the material world and the interior life with quiet insistence.

## Physical Weight

The novel's domestic interiors are saturated with heaviness. Furniture presses back: Miriam's "body was heavy on her chair, ill-clothed" [001-017], the chair itself offering no comfort, her body a weight she cannot escape. The servants too are heavy — Christine's "heavy sallow sullen face" [001-011] makes her presence material and opaque, her movements "heavy" when compared to the light efficiency of the Baileys' household. Objects carry their weight as presence: "the heavy dish of bananas" [001-010] circulates at the supper table, food itself becoming a social burden.

## Emotional Gravity

Heaviness extends inward. Miriam sits "with the large volume heavy on her knees" [002-006], the book a physical anchor for mental wandering. In the drawing-room, she confronts "the heavy self-conscious reflection of the elegant jug" [002-008] in a narrow mirror — the adjective "self-conscious" applied to an object's heaviness, as if things too feel their own weight. Even gestures carry this gravity: "Halfway downstairs she restrained her heavy movements" [002-006], the body slowed by awareness, by the need to move quietly through someone else's house.
