# Hair in _Interim_

**Hair** in _Interim_ functions as a concentrated site of physical description and characterisation. Richardson registers hair with the same precision she brings to voices, hands, and clothing — as a visible signature of identity, class, and temperament. A character's hair, in the novel's economy of detail, often stands for the whole person.

## Hair as Character Portrait

Richardson uses hair to fix characters in the reader's perception with remarkable economy. Eve is remembered through "the thatch of soft fine fair hair" [PN=004-003] — the description combining texture, colour, and abundance in a single phrase. A man seen in passing is defined by "the soft crisply ridged black hair" [PN=003-008], and the speculation "Perhaps he was Dutch" follows directly from this physical detail, as if hair were a clue to national identity.

The "characterless hair" of a minor figure [PN=002-007] does descriptive work by negation — the hair's lack of distinction becomes itself a form of characterisation, marking the person as visually unremarkable. The back of a man's hair, glanced at across a room [PN=005-002], triggers a train of imaginative speculation about his life and habits.

> …her characterless hair, her thickset swiftly ambling figure… [PN=002-007]

## Hair, Light, and Atmosphere

Hair in the novel is never described in isolation; it is always seen in relation to light and setting. The firelight "defining her brow and hair, shedding a warmth" [PN=002-003] makes hair part of a composed visual scene. This integration of hair into the play of light and shadow is typical of Richardson's descriptive method — physical details are always situated, never abstracted.

## Hair and Self-Presentation

For Miriam, hair is an object of social awareness. The care with which she observes others' hair registers her sensitivity to the codes of feminine self-presentation. The absence of elaborate description of her own hair — in contrast to the detailed renderings of others' — suggests her characteristic position as observer rather than observed, a consciousness directed outward rather than preoccupied with its own appearance.
