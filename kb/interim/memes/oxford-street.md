# Oxford Street

Oxford Street appears in Dorothy Richardson's *Interim* as a London street that embodies the gap between the city's promised glamour and its everyday reality. Miriam Henderson encounters it in two contrasting passages: first during a solitary walk suffused with intellectual possibility, and later during an evening outing that reveals the street's more confining character.

## In the Novel

### Solitary Reverie

Miriam's first encounter with Oxford Street occurs as she leaves a café, carrying a volume of Ibsen. The street opens before her as a space of expansion:

> She wandered out into Oxford Street. There was a vast fresh gold-lit sky somewhere behind the twilight. [006-017]

Walking with Ibsen held against her side, Miriam experiences the street not as a shopping destination but as a corridor for thought — a place where the mind can wander outward "towards a centre in Norway." The "gold-lit sky" suggests a city beautiful with evening light, and the act of reading while walking transforms the commercial thoroughfare into a space of intellectual freedom.

> Walking along Oxford Street with a read volume of Ibsen held against you is walking along with something precious between two covers which makes you know you are rich and free. [006-017]

### Urban Disillusionment

The second passage reverses this impression. Walking with Mr. Mendizabal towards the street, Miriam experiences Oxford Street very differently:

> At the turning into Oxford Street they lost each other. [006-026]

The loss of her companion at the street's threshold marks a shift. The "clear space" ahead, which "once had seemed part of the golden west-end," now presses upon her "without ray or break":

> Oxford Street was not the west-end. Oxford Street was like a prison… [006-026]

The street that had been expansive and golden during her solitary walk has become confining, even oppressive, when approached as part of a social outing. The "prison" metaphor captures the gap between expectation and experience — the west-end's glamour recedes, leaving only the hard reality of the commercial street.

## Significance

Oxford Street in *Interim* functions as a gauge of Miriam's interior state. The same physical location yields radically different experiences depending on her mood and circumstances: liberation when alone with a book, confinement when entangled in social performance. Richardson uses the street to map the psychological topography of London — a city whose spaces are never neutral, always shaped by the consciousness moving through them.
