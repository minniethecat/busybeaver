## Overview

Miriam Henderson is the central character and point-of-view consciousness of *Interim*, the fifth novel in Dorothy M. Richardson's *Pilgrimage* series. She is a young woman working in London who visits the Broom family for the Christmas holidays. Through her stream of consciousness, the novel explores her inner life, social observations, and philosophical reflections.

## Role in the Narrative

The entire novel is filtered through Miriam's consciousness. She arrives at the Brooms' house on a rainy evening [001-001], settles into the spare room, and participates in the family's Christmas celebrations. Her narrative is characterized by intense sensitivity to atmosphere, acute social observations, and an ongoing internal dialogue about identity, relationships, and the nature of experience.

Throughout the visit, Miriam oscillates between joyful immersion in the Broom family's warmth and moments of existential detachment. She reflects on her past, her work as a teacher, her boarding-house life at the Baileys' [002-003], and her complex feelings about social belonging [001-013].

## Miriam Through Others' Eyes

Richardson's narrative method — strict adherence to Miriam's point of view — means we never see Miriam objectively. Yet the novel provides glimpses of how others perceive her, refracted through her own awareness of their gaze.

To the Broom sisters, Miriam is an object of affectionate curiosity. Grace watches her with "gentle, questioning eyes" that register Miriam's moods before Miriam has named them herself. Florrie treats her with a teasing warmth that suggests intimacy — the ease of someone who does not need to stand on ceremony. Mrs. Philps, the widowed matriarch, sees in Miriam a young woman "with no one belonging to her" — her perception carrying both pity and a genuine desire to provide the home Miriam lacks.

The doctors at the dinner table see her differently. Dr. von Heber engages her in conversation with an intensity that borders on intellectual courtship — he is interested in what she thinks, a rarity in the novel's social world. Dr. Hurd, by contrast, barely registers her presence; his conversation is directed at the table at large, and Miriam is simply part of the audience.

The servants — Christine the maid, the unnamed household staff — see her as a guest to be served, a category that makes Miriam acutely uncomfortable. Her awareness of class difference surfaces most sharply in interactions with servants, where she cannot find the right register of speech. "She was afraid of Christine," the novel notes at one point — afraid not of Christine personally but of the social distance that formal politeness cannot bridge.

Perhaps most telling is Miriam's own sense of how she is seen. She is acutely aware of being "Little Miriam" — the diminutive used at home, the version of herself that belongs to her family's past. In the Brooms' house she is not "Little Miriam" but simply Miriam — a young woman whose identity is still being formed, whose consciousness the novel follows as it shapes itself against the perceptions of others.
