## Introduction

The mind — Miriam Henderson's mind — is the primary medium of *Interim*. The novel registers not external events but their passage through consciousness: thoughts race, shrink, wander, and draw circles around experience. Dorothy Richardson's narrative method makes mental process itself the subject, anticipating the stream-of-consciousness technique that would define literary modernism.

## The Moving Mind

Miriam's mind is rarely still. "Topics racing through her mind in a voice thrilling with" suppressed energy [001-015] describes the characteristic velocity of her thought. At the dinner table, her "mind wandering blissfully about" [001-013] drifts away from the conversation even as she appears to participate. This mental mobility is not distraction but a mode of being — the mind as a sensorium that refuses to fix on any single object. When her "mind shrank from" a prospect [003-009], the recoil is as physical as any bodily movement.

## Consciousness as Filter

Everything in the novel reaches the reader through Miriam's mental filter. "Everything that passed into her mind out and out towards a centre" [006-017] describes the centripetal force of her consciousness — all experience is drawn inward, organized, and transformed. What "had risen in her mind as she came into the room" [002-008] matters more than what anyone said or did. The mind does not simply receive; it actively shapes, connecting "interchanging scenes" while "her mind split up, pressing several ways" [004-013].

## Interior Judgment

The mind in *Interim* is also the seat of moral and social judgment. "She cast about in her mind for something from her own" experience [001-014] to offer in conversation, the mind functioning as a storehouse of social currency. "Her shocked mind still condemning her" [004-010] shows consciousness doubling back on itself in self-critique. The ability to "make up one's mind" [002-002] — to decide, to commit — becomes an existential act in a world where mental states determine reality.
