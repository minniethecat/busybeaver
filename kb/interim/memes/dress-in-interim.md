## Social Signification

Dress in the novel serves as an immediate index of class and respectability. Miriam's descriptions of clothing are rarely neutral: they encode judgments about taste, wealth, and belonging. The "large straight green dress" worn by Grace at the novel's opening [001-001] establishes dress as the first visual detail in a character introduction.

Miriam's own garments register economic constraint — "her good, once best stuff dress, two years old in its features" [001-013] — the careful notation of age and condition reflecting an awareness of clothing as economic biography.

> Grace in a large straight green dress appeared beside her from the darkness. [001-001]

This attention to the social life of clothing extends beyond the household. Dr. Hurd's observation about secularists — "That is why they dress without style" [004-011] — connects dress directly to ideological identity.

## Self-Presentation and Performance

Miriam's relationship to her own clothing is characterised by ambivalence. She is acutely aware of how her garments position her socially, yet she resists the idea that dress should define identity.

The "small sheeny satin dress" she consults [004-003] and the "black dress with her clumsy golf-cape" [004-009] represent different modes of self-presentation — one aligned with feminine display, the other with practical independence. The golf-cape "thrown over her arm" becomes a "sort of hallmark" of her difference from conventional femininity.

Formal dress marks the boundary between everyday life and special occasion. The vision of "women in white satin evening dresses with trains, bowing from platforms" [006-014] belongs to a world of performance and spectacle. The "droopy black dress on a bare wet wooden floor" [006-015] registers discomfort and exposure — dress failing to confer dignity.

## Domestic and Seasonal Dress

Within the domestic sphere, dress marks the rhythms of daily life. The instruction "let 'er change her dress" during a family Christmas scene [001-010] signals the domestic ritual of changing for meals or company.

The sight of "a summer dress, summer light coming through" a hall [008-002] links clothing to season and atmosphere, part of Richardson's larger project of rendering sensory experience through material detail. "Muslin dresses indoors in snowy weather" [008-064] evokes a specific tactile and thermal contrast — the thin fabric against winter — that registers bodily comfort through clothing.

## Dress and Social Occasion

Special occasions crystallise around dress as a marker of ritual. The sisters appear "in the dresses they had worn at the dance" [001-017], the garment carrying the memory of the social event forward into domestic space.

> …the sisters in the dresses they had worn at the dance… [001-017]

A figure "as smartly dressed as Mrs. Corrie" [007-010] uses dress as shorthand for social aspiration and emulation. The "children in skimpy blue serge dresses" [002-006] register childhood through uniform, part of the novel's broader attention to how clothing defines life stages.
