# Section 54

[053-001]‘But, all the same, he ought to feel pleasure in your good fortune.’

[053-002][[marian-yule|Marian]] turned to another subject.

[053-003]‘Think of the Reardons; what a change all at once! What will they do, I

[053-004]wonder? Surely they won’t continue to live apart?’

[053-005]‘We shall hear from [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]].’

[053-006]Whilst they were discussing the affairs of that branch of the family,

[053-007]Maud returned. There was ill-humour on her handsome face, and she

[053-008]greeted Marian but coldly. Throwing off her hat and gloves and mantle

[053-009]she listened to the repeated story of [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s bequests.

[053-010]‘But why ever has [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]] so much more than anyone else?’ she asked.

[053-011]‘We can only suppose it is because she was the favourite child of the

[053-012]brother he liked best. Yet at her wedding he gave her nothing, and spoke

[053-013]contemptuously of her for marrying a literary man.’

[053-014]‘Fortunate for her poor husband that her uncle was able to forgive her.

[053-015]I wonder what’s the date of the will? Who knows but he may have rewarded

[053-016]her for quarrelling with [[edwin-reardon|Mr Reardon]].’

[053-017]This excited a laugh.

[053-018]‘I don’t know when the will was made,’ said Marian. ‘And I don’t know

[053-019]whether uncle had even heard of the Reardons’ misfortunes. I suppose he

[053-020]must have done. My cousin John was at the funeral, but not my aunt. I

[053-021]think it most likely father and John didn’t speak a word to each other.

[053-022]Fortunately the relatives were lost sight of in the great crowd of

[053-023][[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] people; there was an enormous procession, of course.’

[053-024]Maud kept glancing at her sister. The ill-humour had not altogether

[053-025]passed from her face, but it was now blended with reflectiveness.

[053-026]A few moments more, and Marian had to hasten home. When she was gone the

[053-027]sisters looked at each other.

[053-028]‘Five thousand pounds,’ murmured the elder. ‘I suppose that is

[053-029]considered nothing.’

[053-030]‘I suppose so.--He was here when Marian came, but didn’t stay.’

[053-031]‘Then you’ll take him the news this evening?’

[053-032]‘Yes,’ replied [[dora-milvain|Dora]]. Then, after musing, ‘He seemed annoyed that you

[053-033]were at the Lanes’ again.’

[053-034]Maud made a movement of indifference.

[053-035]‘What has been putting you out?’

[053-036]‘Things were rather stupid. Some people who were to have come didn’t

[053-037]turn up. And--well, it doesn’t matter.’

[053-038]She rose and glanced at herself in the little oblong mirror over the

[053-039]mantelpiece.

[053-040]‘Did Jasper ever speak to you of a Miss Rupert?’ asked Dora.

[053-041]‘Not that I remember.’

[053-042]‘What do you think? He told me in the calmest way that he didn’t see

[053-043]why Marian should think of him as anything but the most ordinary

[053-044]friend--said he had never given her reason to think anything else.’

[053-045]‘Indeed! And Miss Rupert is someone who has the honour of his

[053-046]preference?’

[053-047]‘He says she is about thirty, and rather masculine, but a great heiress.

[053-048]Jasper is shameful!’

[053-049]‘What do you expect? I consider it is your duty to let Marian know

[053-050]everything he says. Otherwise you help to deceive her. He has no sense

[053-051]of honour in such things.’

[053-052]Dora was so impatient to let her brother have the news that she left

[053-053]the house as soon as she had had tea on the chance of finding Jasper

[053-054]at home. She had not gone a dozen yards before she encountered him in

[053-055]person.

[053-056]‘I was afraid Marian might still be with you,’ he said, laughing.

[053-057]‘I should have asked the landlady. Well?’

[053-058]‘We can’t stand talking here. You had better come in.’

[053-059]He was in too much excitement to wait.

[053-060]‘Just tell me. What has she?’

[053-061]Dora walked quickly towards the house, looking annoyed.

[053-062]‘Nothing at all? Then what has her father?’

[053-063]‘He has nothing,’ replied his sister, ‘and she has five thousand

[053-064]pounds.’

[053-065]Jasper walked on with bent head. He said nothing more until he was

[053-066]upstairs in the sitting-room, where Maud greeted him carelessly.

[053-067]‘Mrs Reardon anything?’

[053-068]Dora informed him.

[053-069]‘What?’ he cried incredulously. ‘Ten thousand? You don’t say so!’

[053-070]He burst into uproarious laughter.

[053-071]‘So Reardon is rescued from the slum and the clerk’s desk! Well, I’m

[053-072]glad; by Jove, I am. I should have liked it better if Marian had had the

[053-073]ten thousand and he the five, but it’s an excellent joke. Perhaps the

[053-074]next thing will be that he’ll refuse to have anything to do with his

[053-075]wife’s [[money-in-literature|money]]; that would be just like him.’ After amusing himself with

[053-076]this subject for a few minutes more, he turned to the window and stood

[053-077]there in silence.

[053-078]‘Are you going to have tea with us?’ Dora inquired.

[053-079]He did not seem to hear her. On a repetition of the inquiry, he answered

[053-080]absently:

[053-081]‘Yes, I may as well. Then I can go home and get to work.’

[053-082]During the remainder of his stay he talked very little, and as Maud also

[053-083]was in an abstracted mood, tea passed almost in silence. On the point of

[053-084]departing he asked:

[053-085]‘When is Marian likely to come here again?’

[053-086]‘I haven’t the least idea,’ answered Dora.

[053-087]He nodded, and went his way.

[053-088]It was necessary for him to work at a magazine article which he had

[053-089]begun this morning, and on reaching home he spread out his papers in

[053-090]the usual businesslike fashion. The subject out of which he was

[053-091]manufacturing ‘copy’ had its difficulties, and was not altogether

[053-092]congenial to him; this morning he had laboured with unwonted effort to

[053-093]produce about a page of manuscript, and now that he tried to resume the

[053-094]task his thoughts would not centre upon it. Jasper was too young to have

[053-095]thoroughly mastered the art of somnambulistic composition; to write,

[053-096]he was still obliged to give exclusive attention to the matter under

[053-097]treatment. Dr Johnson’s saying, that a man may write at any time if he

[053-098]will set himself doggedly to it, was often upon his lips, and had even

[053-099]been of help to him, as no doubt it has to many another man obliged to

[053-100]compose amid distracting circumstances; but the formula had no efficacy

[053-101]this evening. Twice or thrice he rose from his chair, paced the room

[053-102]with a determined brow, and sat down again with vigorous clutch of the

[053-103]pen; still he failed to excogitate a single sentence that would serve

[053-104]his purpose.

[053-105]‘I must have it out with myself before I can do anything,’ was his

[053-106]thought as he finally abandoned the endeavour. ‘I must make up my mind.’

[053-107]To this end he settled himself in an easy-chair and began to smoke

[053-108]cigarettes. Some dozen of these aids to reflection only made him so

[053-109]nervous that he could no longer remain alone. He put on his hat and

[053-110]overcoat and went out--to find that it was raining heavily. He returned

[053-111]for an umbrella, and before long was walking aimlessly about the Strand,

[053-112]unable to make up his mind whether to turn into a theatre or not.

[053-113]Instead of doing so, he sought a certain upper room of a familiar

[053-114]restaurant, where the day’s papers were to be seen, and perchance an

[053-115]acquaintance might be met. Only half-a-dozen men were there, reading and

[053-116]smoking, and all were unknown to him. He drank a glass of lager beer,

[053-117]skimmed the news of the evening, and again went out into the bad

[053-118]weather.

[053-119]After all it was better to go home. Everything he encountered had an

[053-120]unsettling effect upon him, so that he was further than ever from the

[053-121]decision at which he wished to arrive. In [[mornington-road|Mornington Road]] he came upon

[053-122][[whelpdale|Whelpdale]], who was walking slowly under an umbrella.

[053-123]‘I’ve just called at your place.’

[053-124]‘All right; come back if you like.’

[053-125]‘But perhaps I shall waste your time?’ said Whelpdale, with unusual

[053-126]diffidence.

[053-127]Reassured, he gladly returned to the house. Milvain acquainted him

[053-128]with the fact of John [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s death, and with its result so far as it

[053-129]concerned the Reardons. They talked of how the couple would probably

[053-130]behave under this decisive change of circumstances.

[053-131]‘[[harold-biffen|Biffen]] professes to know nothing about Mrs Reardon,’ said Whelpdale. ‘I

[053-132]suspect he keeps his knowledge to himself, out of regard for Reardon. It

[053-133]wouldn’t surprise me if they live apart for a long time yet.’

[053-134]‘Not very likely. It was only want of money.’

[053-135]‘They’re not at all suited to each other. Mrs Reardon, no doubt, repents

[053-136]her [[literary-marriage|marriage]] bitterly, and I doubt whether Reardon cares much for his

[053-137]wife.’

[053-138]‘As there’s no way of getting divorced they’ll make the best of it. Ten

[053-139]thousand pounds produce about four hundred a year; it’s enough to live

[053-140]on.’

[053-141]‘And be miserable on--if they no longer love each other.’

[053-142]‘You’re such a sentimental fellow!’ cried Jasper. ‘I believe you

[053-143]seriously think that love--the sort of frenzy you understand by

[053-144]it--ought to endure throughout married life. How has a man come to your

[053-145]age with such primitive ideas?’

[053-146]‘Well, I don’t know. Perhaps you err a little in the opposite

[053-147]direction.’

[053-148]‘I haven’t much faith in marrying for love, as you know. What’s more,

[053-149]I believe it’s the very rarest thing for people to be in love with each

[053-150]other. Reardon and his wife perhaps were an instance; perhaps--I’m

[053-151]not quite sure about her. As a rule, marriage is the result of a mild

[053-152]preference, encouraged by circumstances, and deliberately heightened

[053-153]into strong sexual feeling. You, of all men, know well enough that the

[053-154]same kind of feeling could be produced for almost any woman who wasn’t

[053-155]repulsive.’

[053-156]‘The same kind of feeling; but there’s vast difference of degree.’

[053-157]‘To be sure. I think it’s only a matter of degree. When it rises to the

[053-158]point of frenzy people may strictly be said to be in love; and, as I

[053-159]tell you, I think that comes to pass very rarely indeed. For my own

[053-160]part, I have no experience of it, and think I never shall have.’

[053-161]‘I can’t say the same.’

[053-162]They laughed.

[053-163]‘I dare say you have imagined yourself in love--or really been so for

[053-164]aught I know--a dozen times. How the deuce you can attach any importance

[053-165]to such feeling where marriage is concerned I don’t understand.’

[053-166]‘Well, now,’ said Whelpdale, ‘I have never upheld the theory--at least

[053-167]not since I was sixteen--that a man can be in love only once, or that

[053-168]there is one particular woman if he misses whom he can never be happy.

[053-169]There may be thousands of women whom I could love with equal sincerity.’

[053-170]‘I object to the word “love” altogether. It has been vulgarised. Let us

[053-171]talk about compatibility. Now, I should say that, no doubt, and speaking

[053-172]scientifically, there is one particular woman supremely fitted to

[053-173]each man. I put aside consideration of circumstances; we know that

[053-174]circumstances will disturb any degree of abstract fitness. But in the

[053-175]nature of things there must be one woman whose nature is specially well

[053-176]adapted to harmonise with mine, or with yours. If there were any means

[053-177]of discovering this woman in each case, then I have no doubt it would

[053-178]be worth a man’s utmost effort to do so, and any amount of erotic

[053-179]jubilation would be reasonable when the discovery was made. But

[053-180]the thing is impossible, and, what’s more, we know what ridiculous

[053-181]fallibility people display when they imagine they have found the best

[053-182]substitute for that indiscoverable. This is what makes me impatient with

[053-183]sentimental talk about marriage. An educated man mustn’t play so into

[053-184]the hands of ironic destiny. Let him think he wants to marry a woman;

[053-185]but don’t let him exaggerate his feelings or idealise their nature.’

[053-186]‘There’s a good deal in all that,’ admitted Whelpdale, though

[053-187]discontentedly.

[053-188]‘There’s more than a good deal; there’s the last word on the subject.

[053-189]The days of romantic love are gone by. The scientific spirit has put

[053-190]an end to that kind of self-deception. Romantic love was inextricably

[053-191]blended with all sorts of superstitions--belief in personal immortality,

[053-192]in superior beings, in--all the rest of it. What we think of now is

[053-193]moral and intellectual and physical compatibility; I mean, if we are

[053-194]reasonable people.’
