# Section 45

[044-001]‘I don’t. I said that I had offered her the best home I could. I know

[044-002]it’s impossible, of course.’

[044-003]Either he must speak thus, or break into senseless wrath. It was hard to

[044-004]hold back the angry words that were on his lips, but he succeeded, and

[044-005]he was glad he had done so.

[044-006]‘Then it doesn’t depend on [[amy-reardon|Amy]],’ said John.

[044-007]‘I suppose not.’

[044-008]‘You see no reason, then, why she shouldn’t live as at present for an

[044-009]indefinite time?’

[044-010]To John, whose perspicacity was not remarkable, [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s changed

[044-011]tone conveyed simply an impression of bland impudence. He eyed his

[044-012]brother-in-law rather haughtily.

[044-013]‘I can only say,’ returned the other, who was become wearily

[044-014]indifferent, ‘that as soon as I can afford a decent home I shall give my

[044-015]wife the opportunity of returning to me.’

[044-016]‘But, pray, when is that likely to be?’

[044-017]John had passed the bounds; his manner was too frankly contemptuous.

[044-018]‘I see no right you have to examine me in this fashion,’ Reardon

[044-019]exclaimed. ‘With Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] I should have done my best to be patient if

[044-020]she had asked these questions; but you are not justified in putting

[044-021]them, at all events not in this way.’

[044-022]‘I’m very sorry you speak like this, Reardon,’ said the other, with calm

[044-023]insolence. ‘It confirms unpleasant ideas, you know.’

[044-024]‘What do you mean?’

[044-025]‘Why, one can’t help thinking that you are rather too much at your ease

[044-026]under the circumstances. It isn’t exactly an everyday thing, you know,

[044-027]for a man’s wife to be sent back to her own people--’

[044-028]Reardon could not endure the sound of these words. He interrupted hotly.

[044-029]‘I can’t discuss it with you. You are utterly unable to comprehend me

[044-030]and my position, utterly! It would be useless to defend myself. You must

[044-031]take whatever view seems to you the natural one.’

[044-032]John, having finished his cigarette, rose.

[044-033]‘The natural view is an uncommonly disagreeable one,’ he said. ‘However,

[044-034]I have no intention of quarrelling with you. I’ll only just say that,

[044-035]as I take a share in the expenses of my mother’s house, this question

[044-036]decidedly concerns me; and I’ll add that I think it ought to concern you

[044-037]a good deal more than it seems to.’

[044-038]Reardon, ashamed already of his violence, paused upon these remarks.

[044-039]‘It shall,’ he uttered at length, coldly. ‘You have put it clearly

[044-040]enough to me, and you shan’t have spoken in vain. Is there anything else

[044-041]you wish to say?’

[044-042]‘Thank you; I think not.’

[044-043]They parted with distant civility, and Reardon closed the door behind

[044-044]his visitor.

[044-045]He knew that his character was seen through a distorting medium by Amy’s

[044-046]relatives, to some extent by Amy herself; but hitherto the reflection

[044-047]that this must always be the case when a man of his kind is judged by

[044-048]people of the world had strengthened him in defiance. An endeavour

[044-049]to explain himself would be maddeningly hopeless; even Amy did not

[044-050]understand aright the troubles through which his intellectual and moral

[044-051]nature was passing, and to speak of such experiences to Mrs Yule or to

[044-052]John would be equivalent to addressing them in alien tongues; he and

[044-053]they had no common criterion by reference to which he could make

[044-054]himself intelligible. The practical tone in which John had explained the

[044-055]opposing view of the situation made it impossible for him to proceed as

[044-056]he had purposed. Amy would never come to him in his poor lodgings; her

[044-057]mother, her brother, all her advisers would regard such a thing as out

[044-058]of the question. Very well; recognising this, he must also recognise his

[044-059]wife’s claim upon him for material support. It was not in his power to

[044-060]supply her with means sufficient to live upon, but what he could afford

[044-061]she should have.

[044-062]When he went out, it was with a different purpose from that of half

[044-063]an hour ago. After a short search in the direction of Edgware Road, he

[044-064]found a dealer in second-hand furniture, whom he requested to come as

[044-065]soon as possible to the flat on a matter of business. An hour later the

[044-066]man kept his appointment. Having brought him into the study, Reardon

[044-067]said:

[044-068]‘I wish to sell everything in this flat, with a few exceptions that I’ll

[044-069]point out to you’.

[044-070]‘Very good, sir,’ was the reply. ‘Let’s have a look through the rooms.’

[044-071]That the price offered would be strictly a minimum Reardon knew well

[044-072]enough. The dealer was a rough and rather dirty fellow, with the

[044-073]distrustful glance which distinguishes his [[social-class|class]]. Men of Reardon’s type,

[044-074]when hapless enough to be forced into vulgar commerce, are doubly at a

[044-075]disadvantage; not only their ignorance, but their sensitiveness, makes

[044-076]them ready victims of even the least subtle man of business. To deal

[044-077]on equal terms with a person you must be able to assert with calm

[044-078]confidence that you are not to be cheated; Reardon was too well aware

[044-079]that he would certainly be cheated, and shrank scornfully from the

[044-080]higgling of the market. Moreover, he was in a half-frenzied state of

[044-081]mind, and cared for little but to be done with the hateful details of

[044-082]this process of ruin.

[044-083]He pencilled a list of the articles he must retain for his own use; it

[044-084]would of course be cheaper to take a bare room than furnished

[044-085]lodgings, and every penny he could save was of importance to him. The

[044-086]chair-bedstead, with necessary linen and blankets, a table, two chairs,

[044-087]a looking-glass--strictly the indispensable things; no need to complete

[044-088]the list. Then there were a few valuable wedding-presents, which

[044-089]belonged rather to Amy than to him; these he would get packed and send

[044-090]to Westbourne Park.

[044-091]The dealer made his calculation, with many side-glances at the vendor.

[044-092]‘And what may you ask for the lot?’

[044-093]‘Please to make an offer.’

[044-094]‘Most of the things has had a good deal of wear--’

[044-095]‘I know, I know. Just let me hear what you will give.’

[044-096]‘Well, if you want a valuation, I say eighteen pound ten.’

[044-097]It was more than Reardon had expected, though much less than a man who

[044-098]understood such affairs would have obtained.

[044-099]‘That’s the most you can give?’

[044-100]‘Wouldn’t pay me to give a sixpence more. You see--’

[044-101]He began to point out defects, but Reardon cut him short.

[044-102]‘Can you take them away at once?’

[044-103]‘At wunst? Would two o’clock do?’

[044-104]‘Yes, it would.’

[044-105]‘And might you want these other things takin’ anywheres?’

[044-106]‘Yes, but not till to-morrow. They have to go to [[islington|Islington]]. What would

[044-107]you do it for?’

[044-108]This bargain also was completed, and the dealer went his way. Thereupon

[044-109]Reardon set to work to dispose of his books; by half-past one he had

[044-110]sold them for a couple of guineas. At two came the cart that was to take

[044-111]away the furniture, and at four o’clock nothing remained in the flat

[044-112]save what had to be removed on the morrow.

[044-113]The next thing to be done was to go to Islington, forfeit a week’s rent

[044-114]for the two rooms he had taken, and find a single room at the lowest

[044-115]possible cost. On the way, he entered an eating-house and satisfied his

[044-116]hunger, for he had had nothing since breakfast. It took him a couple of

[044-117]hours to discover the ideal garret; it was found at length in a narrow

[044-118]little by-way running out of Upper Street. The rent was half-a-crown a

[044-119]week.

[044-120]At seven o’clock he sat down in what once was called his study, and

[044-121]wrote the following letter:

[044-122]‘Enclosed in this envelope you will find twenty pounds. I have been

[044-123]reminded that your relatives will be at the expense of your support;

[044-124]it seemed best to me to sell the furniture, and now I send you all

[044-125]the [[money-in-literature|money]] I can spare at present. You will receive to-morrow a box

[044-126]containing several things I did not feel justified in selling. As soon

[044-127]as I begin to have my payment from [[carter|Carter]], half of it shall be sent

[044-128]to you every week. My address is: 5 Manville Street, Upper Street,

[044-129]Islington.--EDWIN REARDON.’

[044-130]He enclosed the money, in notes and gold, and addressed the envelope to

[044-131]his wife. She must receive it this very night, and he knew not how to

[044-132]ensure that save by delivering it himself. So he went to Westbourne Park

[044-133]by train, and walked to Mrs Yule’s house.

[044-134]At this hour the family were probably at dinner; yes, the window of the

[044-135]dining-room showed lights within, whilst those of the drawing-room were

[044-136]in shadow. After a little hesitation he rang the servants’ bell. When

[044-137]the door opened, he handed his letter to the girl, and requested that it

[044-138]might be given to Mrs Reardon as soon as possible. With one more hasty

[044-139]glance at the window--Amy was perhaps enjoying her unwonted comfort--he

[044-140]walked quickly away.

[044-141]As he re-entered what had been his home, its bareness made his heart

[044-142]sink. An hour or two had sufficed for this devastation; nothing remained

[044-143]upon the uncarpeted floors but the needments he would carry with him

[044-144]into the wilderness, such few evidences of civilisation as the poorest

[044-145]cannot well dispense with. Anger, revolt, a sense of outraged love--all

[044-146]manner of confused passions had sustained him throughout this day of

[044-147]toil; now he had leisure to know how faint he was. He threw himself upon

[044-148]his chair-bedstead, and lay for more than an hour in torpor of body and

[044-149]mind.

[044-150]But before he could sleep he must eat. Though it was cold, he could

[044-151]not exert himself to light a fire; there was some food still in the

[044-152]cupboard, and he consumed it in the fashion of a tired labourer, with

[044-153]the plate on his lap, using his fingers and a knife. What had he to do

[044-154]with delicacies?

[044-155]He felt utterly alone in the world. Unless it were [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], what mortal

[044-156]would give him kindly welcome under any roof? These stripped rooms

[044-157]were symbolical of his life; losing money, he had lost everything. ‘Be

[044-158]thankful that you exist, that these morsels of food are still granted

[044-159]you. Man has a right to nothing in this world that he cannot pay for.

[044-160]Did you imagine that love was an exception? Foolish idealist! Love is

[044-161]one of the first things to be frightened away by [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. Go and live

[044-162]upon your twelve-and-sixpence a week, and on your memories of the past.’

[044-163]In this room he had sat with Amy on their return from the wedding

[044-164]holiday. ‘Shall you always love me as you do now?’--‘For ever! for

[044-165]ever!’--‘Even if I disappointed you? If I failed?’--‘How could that

[044-166]affect my love?’ The voices seemed to be lingering still, in a sad,

[044-167]faint echo, so short a time it was since those words were uttered.

[044-168]His own fault. A man has no business to fail; least of all can he expect

[044-169]others to have time to look back upon him or pity him if he sink under

[044-170]the stress of conflict. Those behind will trample over his body; they

[044-171]can’t help it; they themselves are borne onwards by resistless pressure.

[044-172]He slept for a few hours, then lay watching the light of dawn as it

[044-173]revealed his desolation.

[044-174]The morning’s post brought him a large heavy envelope, the aspect of

[044-175]which for a moment puzzled him. But he recognised the handwriting, and

[044-176]understood. The editor of [[the-wayside|The Wayside]], in a pleasantly-written note,

[044-177]begged to return the paper on Pliny’s Letters which had recently

[044-178]been submitted to him; he was sorry it did not strike him as quite so

[044-179]interesting as the other contributions from Reardon’s pen.

[044-180]This was a trifle. For the first time he received a rejected piece of

[044-181]writing without distress; he even laughed at the artistic completeness

[044-182]of the situation. The money would have been welcome, but on that very

[044-183]account he might have known it would not come.

[044-184]The cart that was to transfer his property to the room in Islington

[044-185]arrived about mid-day. By that time he had dismissed the last details of

[044-186]business in relation to the flat, and was free to go back to the obscure

[044-187]world whence he had risen. He felt that for two years and a half he had

[044-188]been a pretender. It was not natural to him to live in the manner of

[044-189]people who enjoy an assured income; he belonged to the class of casual

[044-190]wage-earners. Back to obscurity!
