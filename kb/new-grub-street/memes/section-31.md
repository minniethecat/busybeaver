# Section 31

[030-001]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] did not venture to intrude upon her daughter’s privacy. That

[030-002][[marian-yule|Marian]] neither went out nor showed herself in the house proved her

[030-003]troubled state, but the mother had no confidence in her power to

[030-004]comfort. At the usual time she presented herself in the study with her

[030-005]husband’s coffee; the face which was for an instant turned to her did

[030-006]not invite conversation, but distress obliged her to speak.

[030-007]‘Why are you cross with Marian, Alfred?’

[030-008]‘You had better ask what she means by her extraordinary behaviour.’

[030-009]A word of harsh rebuff was the most she had expected. Thus encouraged,

[030-010]she timidly put another question.

[030-011]‘How has she behaved?’

[030-012]‘I suppose you have ears?’

[030-013]‘But wasn’t there something before that? You spoke so angry to her.’

[030-014]‘Spoke so angry, did I? She is out, I suppose?’

[030-015]‘No, she hasn’t gone out.’

[030-016]‘That’ll do. Don’t disturb me any longer.’

[030-017]She did not venture to linger.

[030-018]The breakfast next morning seemed likely to pass without any interchange

[030-019]of words. But when Yule was pushing back his chair, Marian--who looked

[030-020]pale and ill--addressed a question to him about the work she would

[030-021]ordinarily have pursued to-day at the Reading-room. He answered in a

[030-022]matter-of-fact tone, and for a few minutes they talked on the subject

[030-023]much as at any other time. Half an hour after, Marian set forth for the

[030-024]Museum in the usual way. Her father stayed at home.

[030-025]It was the end of the episode for the present. Marian felt that the

[030-026]best thing would be to ignore what had happened, as her father evidently

[030-027]purposed doing. She had asked his forgiveness, and it was harsh in him

[030-028]to have repelled her; but by now she was able once more to take into

[030-029]consideration all his trials and toils, his embittered temper and the

[030-030]new wound he had received. That he should resume his wonted manner was

[030-031]sufficient evidence of regret on his part. Gladly she would have unsaid

[030-032]her resentful words; she had been guilty of a childish outburst of

[030-033]temper, and perhaps had prepared worse sufferings for the future.

[030-034]And yet, perhaps it was as well that her father should be warned. She

[030-035]was not all submission, he might try her beyond endurance; there might

[030-036]come a day when perforce she must stand face to face with him, and make

[030-037]it known she had her own claims upon life. It was as well he should hold

[030-038]that possibility in view.

[030-039]This evening no work was expected of her. Not long after dinner she

[030-040]prepared for going out; to her mother she mentioned she should be back

[030-041]about ten o’clock.

[030-042]‘Give my kind regards to them, dear--if you like to,’ said Mrs Yule just

[030-043]above her breath.

[030-044]‘Certainly I will.’

[030-045]CHAPTER XIV. RECRUITS

[030-046]Marian walked to the nearest point of Camden Road, and there waited for

[030-047]an omnibus, which conveyed her to within easy reach of the street where

[030-048]Maud and [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]] had their lodgings. This was at the north-east of

[030-049]Regent’s Park, and no great distance from [[mornington-road|Mornington Road]], where [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]

[030-050]still dwelt.

[030-051]On learning that the young ladies were at home and alone, she ascended

[030-052]to the second floor and knocked.

[030-053]‘That’s right!’ exclaimed Dora’s pleasant voice, as the door opened and

[030-054]the visitor showed herself. And then came the friendly greeting which

[030-055]warmed Marian’s heart, the greeting which until lately no house in

[030-056][[london|London]] could afford her.

[030-057]The girls looked oddly out of place in this second-floor sitting-room,

[030-058]with its vulgar furniture and paltry ornaments. Maud especially so, for

[030-059]her fine figure was well displayed by the dress of mourning, and

[030-060]her pale, handsome face had as little congruence as possible with a

[030-061]background of humble circumstances.

[030-062]Dora impressed one as a simpler nature, but she too had distinctly the

[030-063]note of refinement which was out of harmony with these surroundings.

[030-064]They occupied only two rooms, the sleeping-chamber being double-bedded;

[030-065]they purchased food for themselves and prepared their own meals,

[030-066]excepting dinner. During the first week a good many tears were shed

[030-067]by both of them; it was not easy to transfer themselves from the

[030-068]comfortable country home to this bare corner of lodgers’ London. Maud,

[030-069]as appeared at the first glance, was less disposed than her sister to

[030-070]make the best of things; her countenance wore an expression rather of

[030-071]discontent than of sorrow, and she did not talk with the same readiness

[030-072]as Dora.

[030-073]On the round table lay a number of books; when disturbed, the sisters

[030-074]had been engaged in studious reading.

[030-075]‘I’m not sure that I do right in coming again so soon,’ said Marian as

[030-076]she took off her things. ‘Your time is precious.’

[030-077]‘So are you,’ replied Dora, laughing. ‘It’s only under protest that we

[030-078]work in the evening when we have been hard at it all day.’

[030-079]‘We have news for you, too,’ said Maud, who sat languidly on an uneasy

[030-080]chair.

[030-081]‘Good, I hope?’

[030-082]‘Someone called to see us yesterday. I dare say you can guess who it

[030-083]was.’

[030-084]‘[[amy-reardon|Amy]], perhaps?’

[030-085]‘Yes.’

[030-086]‘And how did you like her?’

[030-087]The sisters seemed to have a difficulty in answering. Dora was the first

[030-088]to speak.

[030-089]‘We thought she was sadly out of spirits. Indeed she told us that she

[030-090]hasn’t been very well lately. But I think we shall like her if we come

[030-091]to know her better.’

[030-092]‘It was rather awkward, Marian,’ the elder sister explained. ‘We felt

[030-093]obliged to say something about [[edwin-reardon|Mr Reardon]]’s books, but we haven’t read

[030-094]any of them yet, you know, so I just said that I hoped soon to read his

[030-095]new novel. “I suppose you have seen reviews of it?” she asked at once.

[030-096]Of course I ought to have had the courage to say no, but I admitted

[030-097]that I had seen one or two--Jasper showed us them. She looked very much

[030-098]annoyed, and after that we didn’t find much to talk about.’

[030-099]‘The reviews are very disagreeable,’ said Marian with a troubled face.

[030-100]‘I have read the book since I saw you the other day, and I am afraid it

[030-101]isn’t good, but I have seen many worse novels more kindly reviewed.’

[030-102]‘Jasper says it’s because Mr Reardon has no friends among the

[030-103]journalists.’

[030-104]‘Still,’ replied Marian, ‘I’m afraid they couldn’t have given the book

[030-105]much praise, if they wrote honestly. Did Amy ask you to go and see her?’

[030-106]‘Yes, but she said it was uncertain how long they would be living at

[030-107]their present address. And really, we can’t feel sure whether we should

[030-108]be welcome or not just now.’

[030-109]Marian listened with bent head. She too had to make known to her friends

[030-110]that they were not welcome in her own home; but she knew not how to

[030-111]utter words which would sound so unkind.

[030-112]‘Your brother,’ she said after a pause, ‘will soon find suitable friends

[030-113]for you.’

[030-114]‘Before long,’ replied Dora, with a look of amusement, ‘he’s going to

[030-115]take us to call on Mrs Boston Wright. I hardly thought he was serious at

[030-116]first, but he says he really means it.’

[030-117]Marian grew more and more silent. At home she had felt that it would not

[030-118]be difficult to explain her troubles to these sympathetic girls, but now

[030-119]the time had come for speaking, she was oppressed by shame and anxiety.

[030-120]True, there was no absolute necessity for making the confession this

[030-121]evening, and if she chose to resist her father’s prejudice, things might

[030-122]even go on in a seemingly natural way. But the loneliness of her life

[030-123]had developed in her a sensitiveness which could not endure situations

[030-124]such as the present; difficulties which are of small account to

[030-125]people who take their part in active social life, harassed her to the

[030-126]destruction of all peace. Dora was not long in noticing the dejected

[030-127]mood which had come upon her friend.

[030-128]‘What’s troubling you, Marian?’

[030-129]‘Something I can hardly bear to speak of. Perhaps it will be the end of

[030-130]your friendship for me, and I should find it very hard to go back to my

[030-131]old solitude.’

[030-132]The girls gazed at her, in doubt at first whether she spoke seriously.

[030-133]‘What can you mean?’ Dora exclaimed. ‘What crime have you been

[030-134]committing?’

[030-135]Maud, who leaned with her elbows on the table, searched Marian’s face

[030-136]curiously, but said nothing.

[030-137]‘Has Mr Milvain shown you the new number of [[the-current|The Current]]?’ Marian went on

[030-138]to ask.

[030-139]They replied with a negative, and Maud added:

[030-140]‘He has nothing in it this month, except a review.’

[030-141]‘A review?’ repeated Marian in a low voice.

[030-142]‘Yes; of somebody’s novel.’

[030-143]‘Markland’s,’ supplied Dora.

[030-144]Marian drew a breath, but remained for a moment with her eyes cast down.

[030-145]‘Do go on, dear,’ urged Dora. ‘Whatever are you going to tell us?’

[030-146]‘There’s a notice of father’s book,’ continued the other, ‘a very

[030-147]ill-natured one; it’s written by the editor, Mr [[fadge|Fadge]]. Father and he

[030-148]have been very unfriendly for a long time. Perhaps Mr Milvain has told

[030-149]you something about it?’

[030-150]Dora replied that he had.

[030-151]‘I don’t know how it is in other professions,’ Marian resumed, ‘but I

[030-152]hope there is less envy, hatred and malice than in this of ours. The

[030-153]name of literature is often made hateful to me by the things I hear

[030-154]and read. My father has never been very fortunate, and many things have

[030-155]happened to make him bitter against the men who succeed; he has often

[030-156]quarrelled with people who were at first his friends, but never so

[030-157]seriously with anyone as with Mr Fadge. His feeling of enmity goes so

[030-158]far that it includes even those who are in any way associated with Mr

[030-159]Fadge. I am sorry to say’--she looked with painful anxiety from one to

[030-160]the other of her hearers--‘this has turned him against your brother,

[030-161]and--’

[030-162]Her voice was checked by agitation.

[030-163]‘We were afraid of this,’ said Dora, in a tone of sympathy.

[030-164]‘Jasper feared it might be the case,’ added Maud, more coldly, though

[030-165]with friendliness.

[030-166]‘Why I speak of it at all,’ Marian hastened to say, ‘is because I am so

[030-167]afraid it should make a difference between yourselves and me.’

[030-168]‘Oh! don’t think that!’ Dora exclaimed.

[030-169]‘I am so ashamed,’ Marian went on in an uncertain tone, ‘but I think

[030-170]it will be better if I don’t ask you to come and see me. It sounds

[030-171]ridiculous; it is ridiculous and shameful. I couldn’t complain if you

[030-172]refused to have anything more to do with me.’

[030-173]‘Don’t let it trouble you,’ urged Maud, with perhaps a trifle more of

[030-174]magnanimity in her voice than was needful. We quite understand. Indeed,

[030-175]it shan’t make any difference to us.’

[030-176]But Marian had averted her face, and could not meet these assurances

[030-177]with any show of pleasure. Now that the step was taken she felt that

[030-178]her behaviour had been very weak. Unreasonable harshness such as her

[030-179]father’s ought to have been met more steadily; she had no right to make

[030-180]it an excuse for such incivility to her friends. Yet only in some

[030-181]such way as this could she make known to Jasper Milvain how her father

[030-182]regarded him, which she felt it necessary to do. Now his sisters would

[030-183]tell him, and henceforth there would be a clear understanding on both

[030-184]sides. That state of things was painful to her, but it was better than

[030-185]ambiguous relations.

[030-186]‘Jasper is very sorry about it,’ said Dora, glancing rapidly at Marian.

[030-187]‘But his connection with Mr Fadge came about in such a natural way,’

[030-188]added the eldest sister. ‘And it was impossible for him to refuse

[030-189]opportunities.’

[030-190]‘Impossible; I know,’ Marian replied earnestly. ‘Don’t think that I

[030-191]wish to justify my father. But I can understand him, and it must be very

[030-192]difficult for you to do so. You can’t know, as I do, how intensely he

[030-193]has suffered in these wretched, ignoble quarrels. If only you will let

[030-194]me come here still, in the same way, and still be as friendly to me. My

[030-195]home has never been a place to which I could have invited friends

[030-196]with any comfort, even if I had had any to invite. There were always

[030-197]reasons--but I can’t speak of them.’
