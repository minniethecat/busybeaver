# Section 29

[028-001]Neither [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] nor his wife spoke.

[028-002]‘Of course,’ went on [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]], looking at the former, ‘if you had rather

[028-003]I left it alone--’

[028-004]‘I had much rather. Please don’t say anything about it.’

[028-005]There was an awkward silence. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] broke it by saying:

[028-006]‘Are your sisters in town, Mr Milvain?’

[028-007]‘Yes. We came up two days ago. I found lodgings for them not far from

[028-008][[mornington-road|Mornington Road]]. Poor girls! they don’t quite know where they are, yet.

[028-009]Of course they will keep very quiet for a time, then I must try to get

[028-010]friends for them. Well, they have one already--your cousin, [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]].

[028-011]She has already been to see them.’

[028-012]‘I’m very glad of that.’

[028-013]Amy took an opportunity of studying his face. There was again a

[028-014]silence as if of constraint. Reardon, glancing at his wife, said with

[028-015]hesitation:

[028-016]‘When they care to see other visitors, I’m sure Amy would be very

[028-017]glad--’

[028-018]‘Certainly!’ his wife added.

[028-019]‘Thank you very much. Of course I knew I could depend on Mrs Reardon to

[028-020]show them kindness in that way. But let me speak frankly of something.

[028-021]My sisters have made quite a friend of Miss [[alfred-yule|Yule]], since she was down

[028-022]there last year. Wouldn’t that’--he turned to Amy--‘cause you a little

[028-023]awkwardness?’

[028-024]Amy had a difficulty in replying. She kept her eyes on the ground.

[028-025]‘You have had no quarrel with your cousin,’ remarked Reardon.

[028-026]‘None whatever. It’s only my mother and my uncle.’

[028-027]‘I can’t imagine Miss Yule having a quarrel with anyone,’ said Jasper.

[028-028]Then he added quickly: ‘Well, things must shape themselves naturally. We

[028-029]shall see. For the present they will be fully occupied. Of course it’s

[028-030]best that they should be. I shall see them every day, and Miss Yule will

[028-031]come pretty often, I dare say.’

[028-032]Reardon caught Amy’s eye, but at once looked away again.

[028-033]‘My word!’ exclaimed Milvain, after a moment’s meditation. ‘It’s well

[028-034]this didn’t happen a year ago. The girls have no income; only a little

[028-035]cash to go on with. We shall have our work set. It’s a precious lucky

[028-036]thing that I have just got a sort of footing.’

[028-037]Reardon muttered an assent.

[028-038]‘And what are you doing now?’ Jasper inquired suddenly.

[028-039]‘Writing a one-volume story.’

[028-040]‘I’m glad to hear that. Any special plan for its publication?’

[028-041]‘No.’

[028-042]‘Then why not offer it to [[jedwood|Jedwood]]? He’s [[publishing-trade|publishing]] a series of

[028-043]one-volume novels. You know of Jedwood, don’t you? He was Culpepper’s

[028-044]manager; started business about half a year ago, and it looks as if he

[028-045]would do well. He married that woman--what’s her name?--Who wrote “Mr

[028-046]Henderson’s Wives”?’

[028-047]‘Never heard of it.’

[028-048]‘Nonsense!--Miss Wilkes, of course. Well, she married this fellow

[028-049]Jedwood, and there was a great row about something or other between

[028-050]him and her publishers. Mrs Boston Wright told me all about it. An

[028-051]astonishing woman that; a cyclopaedia of the day’s small talk. I’m quite

[028-052]a favourite with her; she’s promised to help the girls all she can.

[028-053]Well, but I was talking about Jedwood. Why not offer him this book of

[028-054]yours? He’s eager to get hold of the new writers. Advertises hugely; he

[028-055]has the whole back page of [[the-study|The Study]] about every other week. I suppose

[028-056]Miss Wilkes’s profits are paying for it. He has just given Markland two

[028-057]hundred pounds for a paltry little tale that would scarcely swell out

[028-058]to a volume. Markland told me himself. You know that I’ve scraped an

[028-059]acquaintance with him? Oh! I suppose I haven’t seen you since then. He’s

[028-060]a dwarfish fellow with only one eye. Mrs Boston Wright cries him up at

[028-061]every opportunity.’

[028-062]‘Who IS Mrs Boston Wright?’ asked Reardon, laughing impatiently.

[028-063]‘Edits The English Girl, you know. She’s had an extraordinary life.

[028-064]Was born in Mauritius--no, Ceylon--I forget; some such place. Married a

[028-065]sailor at fifteen. Was shipwrecked somewhere, and only restored to life

[028-066]after terrific efforts;--her story leaves it all rather vague. Then she

[028-067]turns up as a newspaper correspondent at the Cape. Gave up that, and

[028-068]took to some kind of farming, I forget where. Married again (first

[028-069]husband lost in aforementioned shipwreck), this time a Baptist minister,

[028-070]and began to devote herself to soup-kitchens in Liverpool. Husband

[028-071]burned to death, somewhere. She’s next discovered in the thick of

[028-072]literary society in [[london|London]]. A wonderful woman, I assure you. Must be

[028-073]nearly fifty, but she looks twenty-five.’

[028-074]He paused, then added impulsively:

[028-075]‘Let me take you to one of her evenings--nine on Thursday. Do persuade

[028-076]him, Mrs Reardon?’

[028-077]Reardon shook his head.

[028-078]‘No, no. I should be horribly out of my element.’

[028-079]‘I can’t see why. You would meet all sorts of well-known people; those

[028-080]you ought to have met long ago. Better still, let me ask her to send

[028-081]an invitation for both of you. I’m sure you’d like her, Mrs Reardon.

[028-082]There’s a good deal of humbug about her, it’s true, but some solid

[028-083]qualities as well. No one has a word to say against her. And it’s a

[028-084]splendid advertisement to have her for a friend. She’ll talk about your

[028-085]books and articles till all is blue.’

[028-086]Amy gave a questioning look at her husband. But Reardon moved in an

[028-087]uncomfortable way.

[028-088]‘We’ll see about it,’ he said. ‘Some day, perhaps.’

[028-089]‘Let me know whenever you feel disposed. But about Jedwood: I happen to

[028-090]know a man who reads for him.’

[028-091]‘Heavens!’ cried Reardon. ‘Who don’t you know?’

[028-092]‘The simplest thing in the world. At present it’s a large part of my

[028-093]business to make acquaintances. Why, look you; a man who has to live

[028-094]by miscellaneous writing couldn’t get on without a vast variety of

[028-095]acquaintances. One’s own brain would soon run dry; a clever fellow knows

[028-096]how to use the brains of other people.’

[028-097]Amy listened with an unconscious smile which expressed keen interest.

[028-098]‘Oh,’ pursued Jasper, ‘when did you see [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] last?’

[028-099]‘Haven’t seen him for a long time.’

[028-100]‘You don’t know what he’s doing? The fellow has set up as a “literary

[028-101]adviser.” He has an advertisement in The Study every week. “To Young

[028-102]Authors and Literary Aspirants”--something of the kind. “Advice given on

[028-103]choice of subjects, MSS. read, corrected, and recommended to publishers.

[028-104]Moderate terms.” A fact! And what’s more, he made six guineas in the

[028-105]first fortnight; so he says, at all events. Now that’s one of the finest

[028-106]jokes I ever heard. A man who can’t get anyone to publish his own books

[028-107]makes a living by telling other people how to write!’

[028-108]‘But it’s a confounded swindle!’

[028-109]‘Oh, I don’t know. He’s capable of correcting the grammar of “literary

[028-110]aspirants,” and as for recommending to publishers--well, anyone can

[028-111]recommend, I suppose.’

[028-112]Reardon’s indignation yielded to laughter.

[028-113]‘It’s not impossible that he may thrive by this kind of thing.’

[028-114]‘Not at all,’ assented Jasper.

[028-115]Shortly after this he looked at his watch.

[028-116]‘I must be off, my friends. I have something to write before I can go to

[028-117]my truckle-bed, and it’ll take me three hours at least.

[028-118]Good-bye, old man. Let me know when your story’s finished, and we’ll

[028-119]talk about it. And think about Mrs Boston Wright; oh, and about that

[028-120]review in [[the-current|The Current]]. I wish you’d let me do it. Talk it over with your

[028-121]guide, philosopher, and friend.’

[028-122]He indicated Amy, who laughed in a forced way.

[028-123]When he was gone, the two sat without speaking for several minutes.

[028-124]‘Do you care to make friends with those girls?’ asked Reardon at length.

[028-125]‘I suppose in decency I must call upon them?’

[028-126]‘I suppose so.’

[028-127]‘You may find them very agreeable.’

[028-128]‘Oh yes.’

[028-129]They conversed with their own thoughts for a while. Then Reardon burst

[028-130]out laughing.

[028-131]‘Well, there’s the successful man, you see. Some day he’ll live in a

[028-132]mansion, and dictate literary opinions to the universe.’

[028-133]‘How has he offended you?’

[028-134]‘Offended me? Not at all. I am glad of his cheerful prospects.’

[028-135]‘Why should you refuse to go among those people? It might be good for

[028-136]you in several ways.’

[028-137]‘If the chance had come when I was publishing my best work, I dare say I

[028-138]shouldn’t have refused. But I certainly shall not present myself as the

[028-139]author of “[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]],” and the rubbish I’m now writing.’

[028-140]‘Then you must cease to write rubbish.’

[028-141]‘Yes. I must cease to write altogether.’

[028-142]‘And do what?’

[028-143]‘I wish to Heaven I knew!’

[028-144]CHAPTER XIII. A WARNING

[028-145]In the spring list of Mr Jedwood’s publications, announcement was

[028-146]made of a new work by Alfred Yule. It was called ‘English Prose in the

[028-147]Nineteenth Century,’ and consisted of a number of essays (several of

[028-148]which had already seen the light in periodicals) strung into continuity.

[028-149]The final chapter dealt with contemporary writers, more especially those

[028-150]who served to illustrate the author’s theme--that [[journalism-trade|journalism]] is the

[028-151]destruction of prose style: on certain popular writers of the day there

[028-152]was an outpouring of gall which was not likely to be received as though

[028-153]it were sweet ointment. The book met with rather severe treatment in

[028-154]critical columns; it could scarcely be ignored (the safest mode of

[028-155]attack when one’s author has no expectant public), and only the most

[028-156]skilful could write of it in a hostile spirit without betraying that

[028-157]some of its strokes had told. An evening newspaper which piqued itself

[028-158]on independence indulged in laughing appreciation of the polemical

[028-159]chapter, and the next day printed a scornful letter from a

[028-160]thinly-disguised correspondent who assailed both book and reviewer. For

[028-161]the moment people talked more of Alfred Yule than they had done since

[028-162]his memorable conflict with Clement [[fadge|Fadge]].

[028-163]The publisher had hoped for this. Mr Jedwood was an energetic and

[028-164]sanguine man, who had entered upon his business with a determination to

[028-165]rival in a year or so the houses which had slowly risen into commanding

[028-166]stability. He had no great capital, but the stroke of fortune which had

[028-167]wedded him to a popular novelist enabled him to count on steady profit

[028-168]from one source, and boundless faith in his own judgment urged him to an

[028-169]initial outlay which made the prudent shake their heads. He talked much

[028-170]of ‘the new era,’ foresaw revolutions in publishing and book-selling,

[028-171]planned every week a score of untried ventures which should appeal to

[028-172]the democratic generation just maturing; in the meantime, was ready to

[028-173]publish anything which seemed likely to get talked about.

[028-174]The May number of The Current, in its article headed ‘Books of the

[028-175]Month,’ devoted about half a page to ‘English Prose in the Nineteenth

[028-176]Century.’ This notice was a consummate example of the flippant style of

[028-177]attack. Flippancy, the most hopeless form of intellectual vice, was a

[028-178]characterising note of Mr Fadge’s periodical; his monthly comments on

[028-179]publications were already looked for with eagerness by that growing

[028-180][[social-class|class]] of readers who care for nothing but what can be made matter of

[028-181]ridicule. The hostility of other reviewers was awkward and ineffectual

[028-182]compared with this venomous banter, which entertained by showing that in

[028-183]the book under notice there was neither entertainment nor any other kind

[028-184]of interest. To assail an author without increasing the number of his

[028-185]readers is the perfection of journalistic skill, and The Current, had

[028-186]it stood alone, would fully have achieved this end. As it was, silence

[028-187]might have been better tactics. But Mr Fadge knew that his enemy would

[028-188]smart under the poisoned pin-points, and that was something gained.

[028-189]On the day that The Current appeared, its treatment of Alfred Yule was

[028-190]discussed in Mr Jedwood’s private office. Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]], who had intimate

[028-191]relations with the publisher, happened to look in just as a young man

[028-192](one of Mr Jedwood’s ‘readers’) was expressing a doubt whether Fadge

[028-193]himself was the author of the review.

[028-194]‘But there’s Fadge’s thumb-mark all down the page,’ cried Mr Quarmby.

[028-195]‘He inspired the thing, of course; but I rather think it was written by

[028-196]that fellow Milvain.’
