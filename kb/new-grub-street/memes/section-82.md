# Section 82

[081-001]The subject of this panegyric coloured a little and laughed.

[081-002]Unmistakably she was pleased.

[081-003]‘Look here, [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]],’ said [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]], ‘I can’t have this; [[dora-milvain|Dora]]’s conceit,

[081-004]please to remember, is, to begin with, only a little less than my own,

[081-005]and you will make her unendurable. Her tale is well enough in its way,

[081-006]but then its way is a very humble one.’

[081-007]‘I deny it!’ cried the other, excitedly. ‘How can it be called a humble

[081-008]line of work to provide reading, which is at once intellectual and

[081-009]moving and exquisitely pure, for the most important part of the

[081-010]population--the educated and refined young people who are just passing

[081-011]from girlhood to womanhood?’

[081-012]‘The most important fiddlestick!’

[081-013]‘You are grossly irreverent, my dear Milvain. I cannot appeal to your

[081-014]sister, for she’s too modest to rate her own sex at its true value, but

[081-015]the vast majority of thoughtful men would support me. You yourself do,

[081-016]though you affect this profane way of speaking. And we know,’ he looked

[081-017]at Dora, ‘that he wouldn’t talk like this if [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]] were present.’

[081-018]Jasper changed the topic of conversation, and presently Whelpdale was

[081-019]able to talk with more calmness. The young man, since his association

[081-020]with Fleet & Co., had become fertile in suggestions of literary

[081-021]enterprise, and at present he was occupied with a project of special

[081-022]hopefulness.

[081-023]‘I want to find a capitalist,’ he said, ‘who will get possession of that

[081-024]paper Chat, and transform it according to an idea I have in my head. The

[081-025]thing is doing very indifferently, but I am convinced it might be made

[081-026]splendid property, with a few changes in the way of conducting it.’

[081-027]‘The paper is rubbish,’ remarked Jasper, ‘and the kind of rubbish--oddly

[081-028]enough--which doesn’t attract people.’

[081-029]‘Precisely, but the rubbish is capable of being made a very valuable

[081-030]article, if it were only handled properly. I have talked to the people

[081-031]about it again and again, but I can’t get them to believe what I say.

[081-032]Now just listen to my notion. In the first place, I should slightly

[081-033]alter the name; only slightly, but that little alteration would in

[081-034]itself have an enormous effect. Instead of Chat I should call it

[081-035][[chit-chat|Chit-Chat]]!’

[081-036]Jasper exploded with mirth.

[081-037]‘That’s brilliant!’ he cried. ‘A stroke of [[literary-genius|genius]]!’

[081-038]‘Are you serious? Or are you making fun of me? I believe it is a stroke

[081-039]of genius. Chat doesn’t attract anyone, but Chit-Chat would sell like

[081-040]hot cakes, as they say in America. I know I am right; laugh as you

[081-041]will.’

[081-042]‘On the same principle,’ cried Jasper, ‘if The Tatler were changed to

[081-043]Tittle-Tattle, its circulation would be trebled.’

[081-044]Whelpdale smote his knee in delight.

[081-045]‘An admirable idea! Many a true word uttered in joke, and this is an

[081-046]instance! Tittle-Tattle--a magnificent title; the very thing to catch

[081-047]the multitude.’

[081-048]Dora was joining in the merriment, and for a minute or two nothing but

[081-049]bursts of laughter could be heard.

[081-050]‘Now do let me go on,’ implored the man of projects, when the noise

[081-051]subsided. ‘That’s only one change, though a most important one. What

[081-052]I next propose is this:--I know you will laugh again, but I will

[081-053]demonstrate to you that I am right. No article in the paper is to

[081-054]measure more than two inches in length, and every inch must be broken

[081-055]into at least two paragraphs.’

[081-056]‘Superb!’

[081-057]‘But you are joking, Mr Whelpdale!’ exclaimed Dora.

[081-058]‘No, I am perfectly serious. Let me explain my principle. I would have

[081-059]the paper address itself to the quarter-educated; that is to say, the

[081-060]great new generation that is being turned out by the Board schools, the

[081-061]young men and women who can just read, but are incapable of sustained

[081-062]attention. People of this kind want something to occupy them in trains

[081-063]and on ‘buses and trams. As a rule they care for no newspapers except

[081-064]the Sunday ones; what they want is the lightest and frothiest of

[081-065]chit-chatty information--bits of stories, bits of description, bits of

[081-066]scandal, bits of jokes, bits of statistics, bits of foolery. Am I not

[081-067]right? Everything must be very short, two inches at the utmost; their

[081-068]attention can’t sustain itself beyond two inches. Even chat is too solid

[081-069]for them: they want chit-chat.’

[081-070]Jasper had begun to listen seriously.

[081-071]‘There’s something in this, Whelpdale,’ he remarked.

[081-072]‘Ha! I have caught you?’ cried the other delightedly. ‘Of course there’s

[081-073]something in it?’

[081-074]‘But--’ began Dora, and checked herself.

[081-075]‘You were going to say--’ Whelpdale bent towards her with deference.

[081-076]‘Surely these poor, silly people oughtn’t to be encouraged in their

[081-077]weakness.’

[081-078]Whelpdale’s countenance fell. He looked ashamed of himself. But Jasper

[081-079]came speedily to the rescue.

[081-080]‘That’s twaddle, Dora. Fools will be fools to the world’s end. Answer

[081-081]a fool according to his folly; supply a simpleton with the reading he

[081-082]craves, if it will put [[money-in-literature|money]] in your pocket. You have discouraged poor

[081-083]Whelpdale in one of the most notable projects of modern times.’

[081-084]‘I shall think no more of it,’ said Whelpdale, gravely. ‘You are right,

[081-085]Miss Dora.’

[081-086]Again Jasper burst into merriment. His sister reddened, and looked

[081-087]uncomfortable. She began to speak timidly:

[081-088]‘You said this was for reading in trains and ‘buses?’

[081-089]Whelpdale caught at hope.

[081-090]‘Yes. And really, you know, it may be better at such times to read

[081-091]chit-chat than to be altogether vacant, or to talk unprofitably. I am

[081-092]not sure; I bow to your opinion unreservedly.’

[081-093]‘So long as they only read the paper at such times,’ said Dora, still

[081-094]hesitating. ‘One knows by experience that one really can’t fix one’s

[081-095]attention in travelling; even an article in a newspaper is often too

[081-096]long.’

[081-097]‘Exactly! And if you find it so, what must be the case with the mass

[081-098]of untaught people, the quarter-educated? It might encourage in some of

[081-099]them a taste for reading--don’t you think?’

[081-100]‘It might,’ assented Dora, musingly. ‘And in that case you would be

[081-101]doing good!’

[081-102]‘Distinct good!’

[081-103]They smiled joyfully at each other. Then Whelpdale turned to Jasper:

[081-104]‘You are convinced that there is something in this?’

[081-105]‘Seriously, I think there is. It would all depend on the skill of the

[081-106]fellows who put the thing together every week. There ought always to be

[081-107]one strongly sensational item--we won’t call it article. For instance,

[081-108]you might display on a placard: “What the Queen eats!” or “How

[081-109]Gladstone’s collars are made!”--things of that kind.’

[081-110]‘To be sure, to be sure. And then, you know,’ added Whelpdale, glancing

[081-111]anxiously at Dora, ‘when people had been attracted by these devices,

[081-112]they would find a few things that were really profitable. We would give

[081-113]nicely written little accounts of exemplary careers, of heroic

[081-114]deeds, and so on. Of course nothing whatever that could be really

[081-115]demoralising--_cela va sans dire_. Well, what I was going to say was this:

[081-116]would you come with me to the office of Chat, and have a talk with my

[081-117]friend Lake, the sub-editor? I know your time is very valuable, but

[081-118]then you’re often running into the Will-o’-the-Wisp, and Chat is just

[081-119]upstairs, you know.’

[081-120]‘What use should I be?’

[081-121]‘Oh, all the use in the world. Lake would pay most respectful attention

[081-122]to your opinion, though he thinks so little of mine. You are a man of

[081-123]note, I am nobody. I feel convinced that you could persuade the

[081-124]Chat people to adopt my idea, and they might be willing to give me a

[081-125]contingent share of contingent profits, if I had really shown them the

[081-126]way to a good thing.’

[081-127]Jasper promised to think the matter over. Whilst their talk still ran on

[081-128]this subject, a packet that had come by post was brought into the room.

[081-129]Opening it, Milvain exclaimed:

[081-130]‘Ha! this is lucky. There’s something here that may interest you,

[081-131]Whelpdale.’

[081-132]‘Proofs?’

[081-133]‘Yes. A paper I have written for [[the-wayside|The Wayside]].’ He looked at Dora, who

[081-134]smiled. ‘How do you like the title?--“The Novels of [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]]!”’

[081-135]‘You don’t say so!’ cried the other. ‘What a good-hearted fellow you

[081-136]are, Milvain! Now that’s really a kind thing to have done. By Jove!

[081-137]I must shake hands with you; I must indeed! Poor Reardon! Poor old

[081-138]fellow!’

[081-139]His eyes gleamed with moisture. Dora, observing this, looked at him so

[081-140]gently and sweetly that it was perhaps well he did not meet her eyes;

[081-141]the experience would have been altogether too much for him.

[081-142]‘It has been written for three months,’ said Jasper, ‘but we have held

[081-143]it over for a practical reason. When I was engaged upon it, I went to

[081-144]see Mortimer, and asked him if there was any chance of a new edition of

[081-145]Reardon’s books. He had no idea the poor fellow was dead, and the news

[081-146]seemed really to affect him. He promised to consider whether it would be

[081-147]worth while trying a new issue, and before long I heard from him that

[081-148]he would bring out the two best books with a decent cover and so on,

[081-149]provided I could get my article on Reardon into one of the monthlies.

[081-150]This was soon settled. The editor of The Wayside answered at once, when

[081-151]I wrote to him, that he should be very glad to print what I proposed,

[081-152]as he had a real respect for Reardon. Next month the books will be

[081-153]out--“Neutral Ground,” and “Hubert Reed.” Mortimer said he was sure

[081-154]these were the only ones that would pay for themselves. But we shall

[081-155]see. He may alter his opinion when my article has been read.’

[081-156]‘Read it to us now, Jasper, will you?’ asked Dora.

[081-157]The request was supported by Whelpdale, and Jasper needed no pressing.

[081-158]He seated himself so that the lamplight fell upon the pages, and read

[081-159]the article through. It was an excellent piece of writing (see The

[081-160]Wayside, June 1884), and in places touched with true emotion. Any

[081-161]intelligent reader would divine that the author had been personally

[081-162]acquainted with the man of whom he wrote, though the fact was nowhere

[081-163]stated. The praise was not exaggerated, yet all the best points of

[081-164]Reardon’s work were admirably brought out. One who knew Jasper might

[081-165]reasonably have doubted, before reading this, whether he was capable of

[081-166]so worthily appreciating the nobler man.

[081-167]‘I never understood Reardon so well before,’ declared Whelpdale, at the

[081-168]close. ‘This is a good thing well done. It’s something to be proud of,

[081-169]Miss Dora.’

[081-170]‘Yes, I feel that it is,’ she replied.

[081-171]‘[[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]] ought to be very grateful to you, Milvain. By-the-by, do

[081-172]you ever see her?’

[081-173]‘I have met her only once since his death--by chance.’

[081-174]‘Of course she will marry again. I wonder who’ll be the fortunate man?’

[081-175]‘Fortunate, do you think?’ asked Dora quietly, without looking at him.

[081-176]‘Oh, I spoke rather cynically, I’m afraid,’ Whelpdale hastened to reply.

[081-177]‘I was thinking of her money. Indeed, I knew Mrs Reardon only very

[081-178]slightly.’

[081-179]‘I don’t think you need regret it,’ Dora remarked.

[081-180]‘Oh, well, come, come!’ put in her brother. ‘We know very well that

[081-181]there was little enough blame on her side.’

[081-182]‘There was great blame!’ Dora exclaimed. ‘She behaved shamefully!

[081-183]I wouldn’t speak to her; I wouldn’t sit down in her company!’

[081-184]‘Bosh! What do you know about it? Wait till you are married to a man

[081-185]like Reardon, and reduced to utter [[literary-poverty|penury]].’

[081-186]‘Whoever my husband was, I would stand by him, if I starved to death.’

[081-187]‘If he ill-used you?’

[081-188]‘I am not talking of such cases. Mrs Reardon had never anything of the

[081-189]kind to fear. It was impossible for a man such as her husband to behave

[081-190]harshly. Her conduct was cowardly, faithless, unwomanly!’

[081-191]‘Trust one woman for thinking the worst of another,’ observed Jasper

[081-192]with something like a sneer.

[081-193]Dora gave him a look of strong disapproval; one might have suspected

[081-194]that brother and sister had before this fallen into disagreement on the

[081-195]delicate topic. Whelpdale felt obliged to interpose, and had of course

[081-196]no choice but to support the girl.
