# Literary Marriage

In *New Grub Street*, marriage is never merely a domestic arrangement. It is an economic partnership, a career strategy, and — most often — a casualty of the literary marketplace. Gissing traces how the pressures of Grub Street infiltrate the most intimate relationships, making marriage another arena where artistic integrity and commercial survival collide.

## Reardon and Amy: The Marriage as Economic Unit

Edwin Reardon's marriage to Amy is the novel's central case study. At the time of their union, Reardon had no commercial prospects — a friend observes, "Had Mr Reardon any hopes of that kind at the time of his marriage, do you think?" and receives the blunt reply: "Good heavens, no!" (001-003–001-005). The marriage begins in love but founders on poverty.

Reardon himself recognizes the structural problem: "Even in mid-rapture of his marriage month he had foreseen this" (009-062) — the financial impossibility of supporting a wife on literary earnings. Amy, for her part, cannot accept the social descent that Reardon's artistic scruples entail. When she inherits money, the power dynamic reverses, and the marriage becomes unsustainable. Gissing's analysis is ruthlessly materialist: love cannot survive in a system where "writing is a business" (001-119) and the business is failing.

## Marriage as Escape

For the Milvain sisters, marriage is the primary escape route from Grub Street. Maud marries into wealth and thereafter speaks "with laughing scorn of the days when she inhabited Grub Street" (085-036). Dora, too, is positioned for an advantageous match. Jasper Milvain, the novel's most clear-eyed strategist, states his intentions plainly: "When I have a decent income of my own, I shall marry a woman with an income somewhat larger, so that casualties may be provided for" (P02-206–P02-208). Marriage is explicitly treated as a financial instrument.

## Marian Yule and the Marriage That Fails

Marian Yule's broken engagement to Jasper Milvain is the novel's most painful marriage plot. Marian is the novel's most sympathetic character — intelligent, capable, loyal — and her inheritance (or lack thereof) determines her marital fate. When John Yule's will does not provide for her, Milvain withdraws. The marriage that might have been is revealed as having always been contingent on money.

## The Structural Argument

Gissing's treatment of marriage is a structural critique. In a literary economy that cannot reliably support a writer, marriage becomes impossible for anyone who refuses to treat writing as a trade. Reardon's marriage fails because he cannot commodify his art; Milvain's marriage strategy succeeds because he treats everything — including love — as a market transaction. There is no third option.
