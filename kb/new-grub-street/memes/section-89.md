# Section 89

[088-001]‘No, I didn’t know that.’

[088-002]‘He will neither see me nor hear of me. Merely because of my connection

[088-003]with [[fadge|Fadge]]. Think of that poor girl thus situated. And I could so easily

[088-004]put her at rest by renouncing all claim upon her.’

[088-005]‘I surmise that--that you yourself would also be put at rest by such a

[088-006]decision?’

[088-007]‘Don’t look at me with that ironical smile,’ he pleaded. ‘What you have

[088-008]said is true. And really, why should I not be glad of it? I couldn’t go

[088-009]about declaring that I was heartbroken, in any event; I must be content

[088-010]for people to judge me according to their disposition, and judgments are

[088-011]pretty sure to be unfavourable. What can I do? In either case I must to

[088-012]a certain extent be in the wrong. To tell the truth, I was wrong from

[088-013]the first.’

[088-014]There was a slight movement about [[amy-reardon|Amy]]’s lips as these words were

[088-015]uttered: she kept her eyes down, and waited before replying.

[088-016]‘The case is too delicate, I fear, for my advice.’

[088-017]‘Yes, I feel it; and perhaps I oughtn’t to have spoken of it at all.

[088-018]Well, I’ll go back to my scribbling. I am so very glad to have seen you

[088-019]again.’

[088-020]‘It was good of you to take the trouble to come--whilst you have so much

[088-021]on your mind.’

[088-022]Again [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] held the white, soft hand for a superfluous moment.

[088-023]The next morning it was he who had to wait at the rendezvous; he was

[088-024]pacing the pathway at least ten minutes before the appointed time.

[088-025]When [[marian-yule|Marian]] joined him, she was panting from a hurried walk, and this

[088-026]affected Jasper disagreeably; he thought of Amy [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s air of repose,

[088-027]and how impossible it would be for that refined person to fall into such

[088-028]disorder. He observed, too, with more disgust than usual, the signs in

[088-029]Marian’s attire of encroaching [[literary-poverty|poverty]]--her unsatisfactory gloves, her

[088-030]mantle out of fashion. Yet for such feelings he reproached himself, and

[088-031]the reproach made him angry.

[088-032]They walked together in the same direction as when they met here before.

[088-033]Marian could not mistake the air of restless trouble on her companion’s

[088-034]smooth countenance. She had divined that there was some grave reason

[088-035]for this summons, and the panting with which she had approached was half

[088-036]caused by the anxious beats of her heart. Jasper’s long silence again

[088-037]was ominous. He began abruptly:

[088-038]‘You’ve heard that [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]] has committed suicide?’

[088-039]‘No!’ she replied, looking shocked.

[088-040]‘Poisoned himself. You’ll find something about it in today’s Telegraph.’

[088-041]He gave her such details as he had obtained, then added:

[088-042]‘There are two of my companions fallen in the battle. I ought to think

[088-043]myself a lucky fellow, Marian. What?’

[088-044]‘You are better fitted to fight your way, Jasper.’

[088-045]‘More of a brute, you mean.’

[088-046]‘You know very well I don’t. You have more energy and more intellect.’

[088-047]‘Well, it remains to be seen how I shall come out when I am weighted

[088-048]with graver cares than I have yet known.’

[088-049]She looked at him inquiringly, but said nothing.

[088-050]‘I have made up my mind about our affairs,’ he went on presently.

[088-051]‘Marian, if ever we are to be married, it must be now.’

[088-052]The words were so unexpected that they brought a flush to her cheeks and

[088-053]neck.

[088-054]‘Now?’

[088-055]‘Yes. Will you marry me, and let us take our chance?’

[088-056]Her heart throbbed violently.

[088-057]‘You don’t mean at once, Jasper? You would wait until I know what

[088-058]father’s fate is to be?’

[088-059]‘Well, now, there’s the point. You feel yourself indispensable to your

[088-060]father at present?’

[088-061]‘Not indispensable, but--wouldn’t it seem very unkind? I should be so

[088-062]afraid of the effect upon his health, Jasper. So much depends, we are

[088-063]told, upon his general state of mind and body. It would be dreadful if I

[088-064]were the cause of--’

[088-065]She paused, and looked up at him touchingly.

[088-066]‘I understand that. But let us face our position. Suppose the operation

[088-067]is successful; your father will certainly not be able to use his eyes

[088-068]much for a long time, if ever; and perhaps he would miss you as much

[088-069]then as now. Suppose he does not regain his sight; could you then leave

[088-070]him?’

[088-071]‘Dear, I can’t feel it would be my duty to renounce you because my

[088-072]father had become blind. And if he can see pretty well, I don’t think I

[088-073]need remain with him.’

[088-074]‘Has one thing occurred to you? Will he consent to receive an allowance

[088-075]from a person whose name is Mrs Milvain?’

[088-076]‘I can’t be sure,’ she replied, much troubled.

[088-077]‘And if he obstinately refuses--what then? What is before him?’

[088-078]Marian’s head sank, and she stood still.

[088-079]‘Why have you changed your mind so, Jasper?’ she inquired at length.

[088-080]‘Because I have decided that the indefinitely long engagement would be

[088-081]unjust to you--and to myself. Such engagements are always dangerous;

[088-082]sometimes they deprave the character of the man or woman.’

[088-083]She listened anxiously and reflected.

[088-084]‘Everything,’ he went on, ‘would be simple enough but for your domestic

[088-085]difficulties. As I have said, there is the very serious doubt whether

[088-086]your father would accept [[money-in-literature|money]] from you when you are my wife. Then

[088-087]again, shall we be able to afford such an allowance?’

[088-088]‘I thought you felt sure of that?’

[088-089]‘I’m not very sure of anything, to tell the truth. I am harassed.

[088-090]I can’t get on with my work.’

[088-091]‘I am very, very sorry.’

[088-092]‘It isn’t your fault, Marian, and--Well, then, there’s only one thing

[088-093]to do. Let us wait, at all events, till your father has undergone the

[088-094]operation. Whichever the result, you say your own position will be the

[088-095]same.’

[088-096]‘Except, Jasper, that if father is helpless, I must find means of

[088-097]assuring his support.’

[088-098]‘In other words, if you can’t do that as my wife, you must remain Marian

[088-099][[alfred-yule|Yule]].’

[088-100]After a silence, Marian regarded him steadily.

[088-101]‘You see only the difficulties in our way,’ she said, in a colder voice.

[088-102]‘They are many, I know. Do you think them insurmountable?’

[088-103]‘Upon my word, they almost seem so,’ Jasper exclaimed, distractedly.

[088-104]‘They were not so great when we spoke of [[literary-marriage|marriage]] a few years hence.’

[088-105]‘A few years!’ he echoed, in a cheerless voice. ‘That is just what I

[088-106]have decided is impossible. Marian, you shall have the plain truth. I

[088-107]can trust your faith, but I can’t trust my own. I will marry you now,

[088-108]but--years hence--how can I tell what may happen? I don’t trust myself.’

[088-109]‘You say you “will” marry me now; that sounds as if you had made up your

[088-110]mind to a sacrifice.’

[088-111]‘I didn’t mean that. To face difficulties, yes.’

[088-112]Whilst they spoke, the sky had grown dark with a heavy cloud, and now

[088-113]spots of rain began to fall. Jasper looked about him in annoyance as he

[088-114]felt the moisture, but Marian did not seem aware of it.

[088-115]‘But shall you face them willingly?’

[088-116]‘I am not a man to repine and grumble. Put up your umbrella, Marian.’

[088-117]‘What do I care for a drop of rain,’ she exclaimed with passionate

[088-118]sadness, ‘when all my life is at stake! How am I to understand you?

[088-119]Every word you speak seems intended to dishearten me. Do you no longer

[088-120]love me? Why need you conceal it, if that is the truth? Is that what you

[088-121]mean by saying you distrust yourself?

[088-122]If you do so, there must be reason for it in the present. Could I

[088-123]distrust myself? Can I force myself in any manner to believe that I

[088-124]shall ever cease to love you?’

[088-125]Jasper opened his umbrella.

[088-126]‘We must see each other again, Marian. We can’t stand and talk in the

[088-127]rain--confound it! Cursed climate, where you can never be sure of a

[088-128]clear sky for five minutes!’

[088-129]‘I can’t go till you have spoken more plainly, Jasper! How am I to live

[088-130]an hour in such uncertainty as this? Do you love me or not? Do you wish

[088-131]me to be your wife, or are you sacrificing yourself?’

[088-132]‘I do wish it!’ Her emotion had an effect upon him, and his voice

[088-133]trembled. ‘But I can’t answer for myself--no, not for a year. And how

[088-134]are we to marry now, in face of all these--’

[088-135]‘What can I do? What can I do?’ she sobbed. ‘Oh, if I were but heartless

[088-136]to everyone but to you! If I could give you my money, and leave my

[088-137]father and mother to their fate! Perhaps some could do that. There is

[088-138]no natural law that a child should surrender everything for her parents.

[088-139]You know so much more of the world than I do; can’t you advise me? Is

[088-140]there no way of providing for my father?’

[088-141]‘Good God! This is frightful, Marian. I can’t stand it. Live as you are

[088-142]doing. Let us wait and see.’

[088-143]‘At the cost of losing you?’

[088-144]‘I will be faithful to you!’

[088-145]‘And your voice says you promise it out of pity.’

[088-146]He had made a pretence of holding his umbrella over her, but Marian

[088-147]turned away and walked to a little distance, and stood beneath the

[088-148]shelter of a great tree, her face averted from him. Moving to follow, he

[088-149]saw that her frame was shaken by soundless sobbing. When his footsteps

[088-150]came close to her, she again looked at him.

[088-151]‘I know now,’ she said, ‘how foolish it is when they talk of love being

[088-152]unselfish. In what can there be more selfishness? I feel as if I could

[088-153]hold you to your promise at any cost, though you have made me understand

[088-154]that you regard our engagement as your great misfortune. I have felt it

[088-155]for weeks--oh, for months! But I couldn’t say a word that would seem to

[088-156]invite such misery as this. You don’t love me, Jasper, and that’s an end

[088-157]of everything.

[088-158]I should be shamed if I married you.’

[088-159]‘Whether I love you or not, I feel as if no sacrifice would be too great

[088-160]that would bring you the happiness you deserve.’

[088-161]‘Deserve!’ she repeated bitterly. ‘Why do I deserve it? Because I long

[088-162]for it with all my heart and soul? There’s no such thing as deserving.

[088-163]Happiness or misery come to us by fate.’

[088-164]‘Is it in my power to make you happy?’

[088-165]‘No; because it isn’t in your power to call dead love to life again. I

[088-166]think perhaps you never loved me. Jasper, I could give my right hand if

[088-167]you had said you loved me before--I can’t put it into words; it sounds

[088-168]too base, and I don’t wish to imply that you behaved basely. But if you

[088-169]had said you loved me before that, I should have it always to remember.’

[088-170]‘You will do me no wrong if you charge me with baseness,’ he replied

[088-171]gloomily. ‘If I believe anything, I believe that I did love you. But I

[088-172]knew myself and I should never have betrayed what I felt, if for once in

[088-173]my life I could have been honourable.’

[088-174]The rain pattered on the leaves and the grass, and still the sky

[088-175]darkened.

[088-176]‘This is wretchedness to both of us,’ Jasper added. ‘Let us part now,

[088-177]Marian. Let me see you again.’

[088-178]‘I can’t see you again. What can you say to me more than you have said

[088-179]now? I should feel like a beggar coming to you. I must try and keep some

[088-180]little self-respect, if I am to live at all.’

[088-181]‘Then let me help you to think of me with indifference. Remember me as a

[088-182]man who disregarded priceless love such as yours to go and make himself

[088-183]a proud position among fools and knaves--indeed that’s what it comes to.

[088-184]It is you who reject me, and rightly. One who is so much at the mercy

[088-185]of a vulgar [[literary-ambition|ambition]] as I am, is no fit husband for you. Soon enough you

[088-186]would thoroughly despise me, and though I should know it was merited,

[088-187]my perverse pride would revolt against it. Many a time I have tried to

[088-188]regard life practically as I am able to do theoretically, but it always

[088-189]ends in hypocrisy. It is men of my kind who succeed; the conscientious,

[088-190]and those who really have a high ideal, either perish or struggle on in

[088-191]neglect.’
