# Section 68

[067-001]It was about a week after this, midway in November, that there at length

[067-002]came to Manville Street a letter addressed in [[amy-reardon|Amy]]’s hand. It arrived

[067-003]at three one afternoon; [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] heard the postman, but he had ceased to

[067-004]rush out on every such occasion, and to-day he was feeling ill. Lying

[067-005]upon the bed, he had just raised his head wearily when he became aware

[067-006]that someone was mounting to his room. He sprang up, his face and neck

[067-007]flushing.

[067-008]This time Amy began ‘Dear Edwin’; the sight of those words made his

[067-009]brain swim.

[067-010]‘You must, of course, have heard [she wrote] that my uncle John has left

[067-011]me ten thousand pounds. It has not yet come into my possession, and

[067-012]I had decided that I would not write to you till that happened, but

[067-013]perhaps you may altogether misunderstand my silence.

[067-014]‘If this [[money-in-literature|money]] had come to me when you were struggling so hard to earn

[067-015]a living for us, we should never have spoken the words and thought the

[067-016]thoughts which now make it so difficult for me to write to you. What I

[067-017]wish to say is that, although the property is legally my own, I quite

[067-018]recognise that you have a right to share in it. Since we have lived

[067-019]apart you have sent me far more than you could really afford, believing

[067-020]it your duty to do so; now that things are so different I wish you, as

[067-021]well as myself, to benefit by the change.

[067-022]‘I said at our last meeting that I should be quite prepared to return to

[067-023]you if you took that position at Croydon. There is now no need for you

[067-024]to pursue a kind of work for which you are quite unfitted, and I repeat

[067-025]that I am willing to live with you as before. If you will tell me where

[067-026]you would like to make a new home I shall gladly agree. I do not think

[067-027]you would care to leave [[london|London]] permanently, and certainly I should not.

[067-028]‘Please to let me hear from you as soon as possible. In writing like

[067-029]this I feel that I have done what you expressed a wish that I should

[067-030]do. I have asked you to put an end to our separation, and I trust that I

[067-031]have not asked in vain.

[067-032]‘Yours always,

[067-033]‘AMY REARDON.’

[067-034]The letter fell from his hand. It was such a letter as he might have

[067-035]expected, but the beginning misled him, and as his agitation throbbed

[067-036]itself away he suffered an encroachment of despair which made him for a

[067-037]time unable to move or even think.

[067-038]His reply, written by the dreary twilight which represented sunset, ran

[067-039]thus.

[067-040]‘Dear Amy,--I thank you for your letter, and I appreciate your motive in

[067-041]writing it. But if you feel that you have “done what I expressed a wish

[067-042]that you should do,” you must have strangely misunderstood me.

[067-043]‘The only one thing that I wished was, that by some miracle your love

[067-044]for me might be revived. Can I persuade myself that this is the letter

[067-045]of a wife who desires to return to me because in her heart she loves me?

[067-046]If that is the truth you have been most unfortunate in trying to express

[067-047]yourself.

[067-048]‘You have written because it seemed your duty to do so. But, indeed, a

[067-049]sense of duty such as this is a mistaken one. You have no love for me,

[067-050]and where there is no love there is no mutual obligation in [[literary-marriage|marriage]].

[067-051]Perhaps you think that regard for social conventions will necessitate

[067-052]your living with me again. But have more courage; refuse to act

[067-053]falsehoods; tell society it is base and brutal, and that you prefer to

[067-054]live an honest life.

[067-055]‘I cannot share your wealth, dear. But as you have no longer need of my

[067-056]help--as we are now quite independent of each other--I shall cease to

[067-057]send the money which hitherto I have considered yours. In this way I

[067-058]shall have enough, and more than enough, for my necessities, so that you

[067-059]will never have to trouble yourself with the thought that I am suffering

[067-060]privations. At Christmas I go to Croydon, and I will then write to you

[067-061]again.

[067-062]‘For we may at all events be friendly. My mind is relieved from

[067-063]ceaseless anxiety on your account. I know now that you are safe from

[067-064]that accursed [[literary-poverty|poverty]] which is to blame for all our sufferings. You I do

[067-065]not blame, though I have sometimes done so. My own experience teaches

[067-066]me how kindness can be embittered by misfortune. Some great and noble

[067-067]sorrow may have the effect of drawing hearts together, but to struggle

[067-068]against destitution, to be crushed by care about shillings and

[067-069]sixpences--that must always degrade.

[067-070]‘No other reply than this is possible, so I beg you not to write in this

[067-071]way again. Let me know if you go to live elsewhere. I hope Willie is

[067-072]well, and that his growth is still a delight and happiness to you.

[067-073]‘EDWIN REARDON.’

[067-074]That one word ‘dear,’ occurring in the middle of the letter, gave him

[067-075]pause as he read the lines over. Should he not obliterate it, and even

[067-076]in such a way that Amy might see what he had done? His pen was dipped in

[067-077]the ink for that purpose, but after all he held his hand. Amy was

[067-078]still dear to him, say what he might, and if she noted the word--if she

[067-079]pondered over it--

[067-080]A street gas lamp prevented the room from becoming absolutely dark. When

[067-081]he had closed the envelope he lay down on his bed again, and watched the

[067-082]flickering yellowness upon the ceiling. He ought to have some tea before

[067-083]going to the hospital, but he cared so little for it that the trouble of

[067-084]boiling water was too great.

[067-085]The flickering light grew fainter; he understood at length that this was

[067-086]caused by fog that had begun to descend. The fog was his enemy; it would

[067-087]be wise to purchase a respirator if this hideous weather continued, for

[067-088]sometimes his throat burned, and there was a rasping in his chest which

[067-089]gave disagreeable admonition.

[067-090]He fell asleep for half an hour, and on awaking he was feverish, as

[067-091]usual at this time of day. Well, it was time to go to his work. Ugh!

[067-092]That first mouthful of fog!

[067-093]CHAPTER XXVIII. INTERIM

[067-094]The rooms which [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] had taken for himself and his sisters were

[067-095]modest, but more expensive than their old quarters. As the change was

[067-096]on his account he held himself responsible for the extra outlay. But for

[067-097]his immediate prospects this step would have been unwarrantable, as

[067-098]his earnings were only just sufficient for his needs on the previous

[067-099]footing. He had resolved that his marriage must take place before

[067-100]Christmas; till that event he would draw when necessary upon the girls’

[067-101]little store, and then repay them out of [[marian-yule|Marian]]’s dowry.

[067-102]‘And what are we to do when you are married?’ asked [[dora-milvain|Dora]].

[067-103]The question was put on the first evening of their being all under the

[067-104]same roof. The trio had had supper in the girls’ sitting-room, and

[067-105]it was a moment for frank conversation. Dora rejoiced in the coming

[067-106]marriage; her brother had behaved honourably, and Marian, she trusted,

[067-107]would be very happy, notwithstanding disagreement with her father, which

[067-108]seemed inevitable. Maud was by no means so well pleased, though she

[067-109]endeavoured to wear smiles. It looked to her as if Jasper had been

[067-110]guilty of a kind of weakness not to be expected in him. Marian, as an

[067-111]individual, could not be considered an appropriate wife for such a

[067-112]man with such a future; and as for her five thousand pounds, that was

[067-113]ridiculous. Had it been ten--something can be made of ten thousand; but

[067-114]a paltry five! Maud’s ideas on such subjects had notably expanded of

[067-115]late, and one of the results was that she did not live so harmoniously

[067-116]with her sister as for the first few months of their London career.

[067-117]‘I have been thinking a good deal about that,’ replied Jasper to the

[067-118]younger girl’s question. He stood with his back to the fire and smoked a

[067-119]cigarette. ‘I thought at first of taking a flat; but then a flat of the

[067-120]kind I should want would be twice the rent of a large house. If we have

[067-121]a house with plenty of room in it you might come and live with us after

[067-122]a time. At first I must find you decent lodgings in our neighbourhood.’

[067-123]‘You show a good deal of generosity, Jasper,’ said Maud, ‘but pray

[067-124]remember that Marian isn’t bringing you five thousand a year.’

[067-125]‘I regret to say that she isn’t. What she brings me is five hundred a

[067-126]year for ten years--that’s how I look at it. My own income will make

[067-127]it something between six or seven hundred at first, and before long

[067-128]probably more like a thousand. I am quite cool and collected. I

[067-129]understand exactly where I am, and where I am likely to be ten years

[067-130]hence. Marian’s money is to be spent in obtaining a position for myself.

[067-131]At present I am spoken of as a “smart young fellow,” and that kind of

[067-132]thing; but no one would offer me an editorship, or any other serious

[067-133]help. Wait till I show that I have helped myself and hands will be

[067-134]stretched to me from every side. ‘Tis the way of the world. I shall

[067-135]belong to a club; I shall give nice, quiet little dinners to selected

[067-136]people; I shall let it be understood by all and sundry that I have a

[067-137][[social-class|social position]]. Thenceforth I am quite a different man, a man to be

[067-138]taken into account. And what will you bet me that I don’t stand in the

[067-139]foremost rank of literary reputabilities ten years hence?’

[067-140]‘I doubt whether six or seven hundred a year will be enough for this.’

[067-141]‘If not, I am prepared to spend a thousand. Bless my soul! As if two or

[067-142]three years wouldn’t suffice to draw out the mean qualities in the kind

[067-143]of people I am thinking of! I say ten, to leave myself a great margin.’

[067-144]‘Marian approves this?’

[067-145]‘I haven’t distinctly spoken of it. But she approves whatever I think

[067-146]good.’

[067-147]The girls laughed at his way of pronouncing this.

[067-148]‘And let us just suppose that you are so unfortunate as to fail?’

[067-149]‘There’s no supposing it, unless, of course, I lose my health. I am not

[067-150]presuming on any wonderful development of powers. Such as I am now, I

[067-151]need only to be put on the little pedestal of a decent independence and

[067-152]plenty of people will point fingers of admiration at me. You don’t fully

[067-153]appreciate this. Mind, it wouldn’t do if I had no qualities. I have the

[067-154]qualities; they only need bringing into prominence. If I am an unknown

[067-155]man, and publish a wonderful book, it will make its way very slowly, or

[067-156]not at all. If I, become a known man, publish that very same book, its

[067-157]praise will echo over both hemispheres. I should be within the truth

[067-158]if I had said “a vastly inferior book,” But I am in a bland mood at

[067-159]present. Suppose poor Reardon’s novels had been published in the full

[067-160]light of [[literary-success|reputation]] instead of in the struggling dawn which was never to

[067-161]become day, wouldn’t they have been magnified by every critic? You have

[067-162]to become famous before you can secure the attention which would give

[067-163]fame.’

[067-164]He delivered this apophthegm with emphasis, and repeated it in another

[067-165]form.

[067-166]‘You have to obtain reputation before you can get a fair hearing for

[067-167]that which would justify your repute. It’s the old story of the French

[067-168]publisher who said to Dumas: “Make a name, and I’ll publish anything you

[067-169]write.” “But how the diable,” cries the author, “am I to make a name

[067-170]if I can’t get published?” If a man can’t hit upon any other way of

[067-171]attracting attention, let him dance on his head in the middle of the

[067-172]street; after that he may hope to get consideration for his volume of

[067-173]poems. I am speaking of men who wish to win reputation before they are

[067-174]toothless. Of course if your work is strong, and you can afford to wait,

[067-175]the probability is that half a dozen people will at last begin to shout

[067-176]that you have been monstrously neglected, as you have. But that happens

[067-177]when you are hoary and sapless, and when nothing under the sun delights

[067-178]you.’
