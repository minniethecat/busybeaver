# Section 32

[031-001]‘My dear [[marian-yule|Marian]],’ appealed [[dora-milvain|Dora]], ‘don’t distress yourself so! Do believe

[031-002]that nothing whatever has happened to change our feeling to you. Has

[031-003]there, Maud?’

[031-004]‘Nothing whatever. We are not unreasonable girls, Marian.’

[031-005]‘I am more grateful to you than I can say.’

[031-006]It had seemed as if Marian must give way to the emotions which all but

[031-007]choked her voice; she overcame them, however, and presently was able

[031-008]to talk in pretty much her usual way, though when she smiled it was

[031-009]but faintly. Maud tried to lead her thoughts in another direction by

[031-010]speaking of work in which she and Dora were engaged. Already the sisters

[031-011]were doing a new piece of compilation for Messrs Jolly and Monk; it was

[031-012]more exacting than their initial task for the book market, and would

[031-013]take a much longer time.

[031-014]A couple of hours went by, and Marian had just spoken of taking her

[031-015]leave, when a man’s step was heard rapidly ascending the nearest flight

[031-016]of stairs.

[031-017]‘Here’s [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]],’ remarked Dora, and in a moment there sounded a short,

[031-018]sharp summons at the door.

[031-019]Jasper it was; he came in with radiant face, his eyes blinking before

[031-020]the lamplight.

[031-021]‘Well, girls! Ha! how do you do, Miss [[alfred-yule|Yule]]? I had just the vaguest sort

[031-022]of expectation that you might be here. It seemed a likely night; I

[031-023]don’t know why. I say, Dora, we really must get two or three decent

[031-024]easy-chairs for your room. I’ve seen some outside a second-hand

[031-025]furniture shop in Hampstead Road, about six shillings apiece. There’s no

[031-026]sitting on chairs such as these.’

[031-027]That on which he tried to dispose himself, when he had flung aside his

[031-028]trappings, creaked and shivered ominously.

[031-029]‘You hear? I shall come plump on to the floor, if I don’t mind. My word,

[031-030]what a day I have had! I’ve just been trying what I really could do

[031-031]in one day if I worked my hardest. Now just listen; it deserves to be

[031-032]chronicled for the encouragement of aspiring youth. I got up at 7.30,

[031-033]and whilst I breakfasted I read through a volume I had to review. By

[031-034]10.30 the review was written--three-quarters of a column of the Evening

[031-035]Budget.’

[031-036]‘Who is the unfortunate author?’ interrupted Maud, caustically.

[031-037]‘Not unfortunate at all. I had to crack him up; otherwise I couldn’t

[031-038]have done the job so quickly. It’s the easiest thing in the world to

[031-039]write laudation; only an inexperienced grumbler would declare it was

[031-040]easier to find fault. The book was Billington’s “Vagaries”; pompous

[031-041]idiocy, of course, but he lives in a big house and gives dinners. Well,

[031-042]from 10.30 to 11, I smoked a cigar and reflected, feeling that the day

[031-043]wasn’t badly begun. At eleven I was ready to write my Saturday causerie

[031-044]for the Will o’ the Wisp; it took me till close upon one o’clock, which

[031-045]was rather too long. I can’t afford more than an hour and a half

[031-046]for that job. At one, I rushed out to a dirty little eating-house

[031-047]in Hampstead Road. Was back again by a quarter to two, having in the

[031-048]meantime sketched a paper for [[the-west-end|The West End]]. Pipe in mouth, I sat down

[031-049]to leisurely artistic work; by five, half the paper was done; the

[031-050]other half remains for to-morrow. From five to half-past I read four

[031-051]newspapers and two magazines, and from half-past to a quarter to six I

[031-052]jotted down several ideas that had come to me whilst reading. At six I

[031-053]was again in the dirty eating-house, satisfying a ferocious hunger. Home

[031-054]once more at 6.45, and for two hours wrote steadily at a long affair I

[031-055]have in hand for [[the-current|The Current]]. Then I came here, thinking hard all the

[031-056]way. What say you to this? Have I earned a night’s repose?’

[031-057]‘And what’s the value of it all?’ asked Maud.

[031-058]‘Probably from ten to twelve guineas, if I calculated.’

[031-059]‘I meant, what was the literary value of it?’ said his sister, with a

[031-060]smile.

[031-061]‘Equal to that of the contents of a mouldy nut.’

[031-062]‘Pretty much what I thought.’

[031-063]‘Oh, but it answers the purpose,’ urged Dora, ‘and it does no one any

[031-064]harm.’

[031-065]‘Honest journey-work!’ cried Jasper. ‘There are few men in [[london|London]]

[031-066]capable of such a feat. Many a fellow could write more in quantity, but

[031-067]they couldn’t command my market. It’s rubbish, but rubbish of a very

[031-068]special kind, of fine quality.’

[031-069]Marian had not yet spoken, save a word or two in reply to Jasper’s

[031-070]greeting; now and then she just glanced at him, but for the most part

[031-071]her eyes were cast down. Now Jasper addressed her.

[031-072]‘A year ago, Miss Yule, I shouldn’t have believed myself capable of such

[031-073]activity. In fact I wasn’t capable of it then.’

[031-074]‘You think such work won’t be too great a strain upon you?’ she asked.

[031-075]‘Oh, this isn’t a specimen day, you know. To-morrow I shall very likely

[031-076]do nothing but finish my West End article, in an easy two or three

[031-077]hours. There’s no knowing; I might perhaps keep up the high pressure

[031-078]if I tried. But then I couldn’t dispose of all the work. Little by

[031-079]little--or perhaps rather quicker than that--I shall extend my scope.

[031-080]For instance, I should like to do two or three leaders a week for one of

[031-081]the big dailies. I can’t attain unto that just yet.’

[031-082]‘Not political leaders?’

[031-083]‘By no means. That’s not my line. The kind of thing in which one makes a

[031-084]column out of what would fill six lines of respectable prose. You call

[031-085]a cigar a “convoluted weed,” and so on, you know; that passes for

[031-086]facetiousness. I’ve never really tried my hand at that style yet; I

[031-087]shouldn’t wonder if I managed it brilliantly. Some day I’ll write a few

[031-088]exercises; just take two lines of some good prose writer, and expand

[031-089]them into twenty, in half-a-dozen different ways. Excellent mental

[031-090]gymnastics!’

[031-091]Marian listened to his flow of talk for a few minutes longer, then took

[031-092]the opportunity of a brief silence to rise and put on her hat. Jasper

[031-093]observed her, but without rising; he looked at his sisters in a

[031-094]hesitating way. At length he stood up, and declared that he too must be

[031-095]off. This coincidence had happened once before when he met Marian here

[031-096]in the evening.

[031-097]‘At all events, you won’t do any more work to-night,’ said Dora.

[031-098]‘No; I shall read a page of something or other over a glass of whisky,

[031-099]and seek the sleep of a man who has done his duty.’

[031-100]‘Why the whisky?’ asked Maud.

[031-101]‘Do you grudge me such poor solace?’

[031-102]‘I don’t see the need of it.’

[031-103]‘Nonsense, Maud!’ exclaimed her sister. ‘He needs a little stimulant

[031-104]when he works so hard.’

[031-105]Each of the girls gave Marian’s hand a significant pressure as she took

[031-106]leave of them, and begged her to come again as soon as she had a free

[031-107]evening. There was gratitude in her eyes.

[031-108]The evening was clear, and not very cold.

[031-109]‘It’s rather late for you to go home,’ said Jasper, as they left the

[031-110]house. ‘May I walk part of the way with you?’

[031-111]Marian replied with a low ‘Thank you.’

[031-112]‘I think you get on pretty well with the girls, don’t you?’

[031-113]‘I hope they are as glad of my friendship as I am of theirs.’

[031-114]‘Pity to see them in a place like that, isn’t it? They ought to have a

[031-115]good house, with plenty of servants. It’s bad enough for a civilised

[031-116]man to have to rough it, but I hate to see women living in a sordid way.

[031-117]Don’t you think they could both play their part in a drawing-room, with

[031-118]a little experience?’

[031-119]‘Surely there’s no doubt of it.’

[031-120]‘Maud would look really superb if she were handsomely dressed. She

[031-121]hasn’t a common face, by any means. And Dora is pretty, I think. Well,

[031-122]they shall go and see some people before long. The difficulty is, one

[031-123]doesn’t like it to be known that they live in such a crib; but I daren’t

[031-124]advise them to go in for expense. One can’t be sure that it would repay

[031-125]them, though--Now, in my own case, if I could get hold of a few thousand

[031-126]pounds I should know how to use it with the certainty of return; it

[031-127]would save me, probably, a clear ten years of life; I mean, I should go

[031-128]at a jump to what I shall be ten years hence without the help of [[money-in-literature|money]].

[031-129]But they have such a miserable little bit of capital, and everything is

[031-130]still so uncertain. One daren’t speculate under the circumstances.’

[031-131]Marian made no reply.

[031-132]‘You think I talk of nothing but money?’ Jasper said suddenly, looking

[031-133]down into her face.

[031-134]‘I know too well what it means to be without money.’

[031-135]‘Yes, but--you do just a little despise me?’

[031-136]‘Indeed, I don’t, Mr Milvain.’

[031-137]‘If that is sincere, I’m very glad. I take it in a friendly sense. I am

[031-138]rather despicable, you know; it’s part of my business to be so. But

[031-139]a friend needn’t regard that. There is the man apart from his

[031-140]necessities.’

[031-141]The silence was then unbroken till they came to the lower end of Park

[031-142]Street, the junction of roads which lead to Hampstead, to Highgate, and

[031-143]to Holloway.

[031-144]‘Shall you take an omnibus?’ Jasper asked.

[031-145]She hesitated.

[031-146]‘Or will you give me the pleasure of walking on with you? You are tired,

[031-147]perhaps?’

[031-148]‘Not the least.’

[031-149]For the rest of her answer she moved forward, and they crossed into the

[031-150]obscurity of Camden Road.

[031-151]‘Shall I be doing wrong, Mr Milvain,’ Marian began in a very low

[031-152]voice, ‘if I ask you about the authorship of something in this month’s

[031-153]Current?’

[031-154]‘I’m afraid I know what you refer to. There’s no reason why I shouldn’t

[031-155]answer a question of the kind.’

[031-156]‘It was Mr [[fadge|Fadge]] himself who reviewed my father’s book?’

[031-157]‘It was--confound him! I don’t know another man who could have done the

[031-158]thing so vilely well.’

[031-159]‘I suppose he was only replying to my father’s attack upon him and his

[031-160]friends.’

[031-161]‘Your father’s attack is honest and straightforward and justifiable and

[031-162]well put. I read that chapter of his book with huge satisfaction.

[031-163]But has anyone suggested that another than Fadge was capable of that

[031-164]masterpiece?’

[031-165]‘Yes. I am told that Mr [[jedwood|Jedwood]], the publisher, has somehow made a

[031-166]mistake.’

[031-167]‘Jedwood? And what mistake?’

[031-168]‘Father heard that you were the writer.’

[031-169]‘I?’ Jasper stopped short. They were in the rays of a street-lamp, and

[031-170]could see each other’s faces. ‘And he believes that?’

[031-171]‘I’m afraid so.’

[031-172]‘And you believe--believed it?’

[031-173]‘Not for a moment.’

[031-174]‘I shall write a note to Mr Yule.’

[031-175]Marian was silent a while, then said:

[031-176]‘Wouldn’t it be better if you found a way of letting Mr Jedwood know the

[031-177]truth?’

[031-178]‘Perhaps you are right.’

[031-179]Jasper was very grateful for the suggestion. In that moment he had

[031-180]reflected how rash it would be to write to Alfred Yule on such a

[031-181]subject, with whatever prudence in expressing himself. Such a letter,

[031-182]coming under the notice of the great Fadge, might do its writer serious

[031-183]harm.

[031-184]‘Yes, you are right,’ he repeated. ‘I’ll stop that rumour at its source.

[031-185]I can’t guess how it started; for aught I know, some enemy hath done

[031-186]this, though I don’t quite discern the motive. Thank you very much for

[031-187]telling me, and still more for refusing to believe that I could treat Mr

[031-188]Yule in that way, even as a matter of business. When I said that I was

[031-189]despicable, I didn’t mean that I could sink quite to such a point as

[031-190]that. If only because it was your father--’

[031-191]He checked himself and they walked on for several yards without

[031-192]speaking.

[031-193]‘In that case,’ Jasper resumed at length, ‘your father doesn’t think of

[031-194]me in a very friendly way?’
