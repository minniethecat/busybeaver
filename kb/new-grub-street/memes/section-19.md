# Section 19

[018-001]‘It might have been very different with me,’ he exclaimed abruptly, as

[018-002]if they had already been conversing on the subject. ‘When you think

[018-003]of my failures--and you must often do so now you are grown up and

[018-004]understand things--don’t forget the obstacles that have been in my way.

[018-005]I don’t like you to look upon your father as a thickhead who couldn’t

[018-006]be expected to succeed. Look at [[fadge|Fadge]]. He married a woman of good social

[018-007]position; she brought him friends and influence. But for that he would

[018-008]never have been editor of [[the-study|The Study]], a place for which he wasn’t in the

[018-009]least fit. But he was able to give dinners; he and his wife went into

[018-010]society; everybody knew him and talked of him. How has it been with

[018-011]me? I live here like an animal in its hole, and go blinking about if

[018-012]by chance I find myself among the people with whom I ought naturally to

[018-013]associate. If I had been able to come in direct contact with Rackett and

[018-014]other men of that kind, to dine with them, and have them to dine with

[018-015]me, to belong to a club, and so on, I shouldn’t be what I am at my age.

[018-016]My one opportunity--when I edited The Balance--wasn’t worth much; there

[018-017]was no [[money-in-literature|money]] behind the paper; we couldn’t hold out long enough. But

[018-018]even then, if I could have assumed my proper social standing, if I could

[018-019]have opened my house freely to the right kind of people--How was it

[018-020]possible?’

[018-021][[marian-yule|Marian]] could not raise her head. She recognised the portion of truth in

[018-022]what he said, but it shocked her that he should allow himself to speak

[018-023]thus. Her silence seemed to remind him how painful it must be to her to

[018-024]hear these accusations of her mother, and with a sudden ‘Good-night’ he

[018-025]dismissed her.

[018-026]She went up to her room, and wept over the wretchedness of all their

[018-027]lives. Her loneliness had seemed harder to bear than ever since that

[018-028]last holiday. For a moment, in the lanes about Finden, there had come to

[018-029]her a vision of joy such as fate owed her youth; but it had faded, and

[018-030]she could no longer hope for its return. She was not a woman, but a mere

[018-031]machine for reading and writing. Did her father never think of this? He

[018-032]was not the only one to suffer from the circumstances in which [[literary-poverty|poverty]]

[018-033]had involved him.

[018-034]She had no friends to whom she could utter her thoughts. [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]]

[018-035]had written a second time, and more recently had come a letter from

[018-036]Maud; but in replying to them she could not give a true account of

[018-037]herself. Impossible, to them. From what she wrote they would imagine her

[018-038]contentedly busy, absorbed in the affairs of literature. To no one could

[018-039]she make known the aching sadness of her heart, the dreariness of life

[018-040]as it lay before her.

[018-041]That beginning of half-confidence between her and her mother had led to

[018-042]nothing. Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] found no second opportunity of speaking to her husband

[018-043]about [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]], and purposely she refrained from any further hint

[018-044]or question to Marian. Everything must go on as hitherto.

[018-045]The days darkened. Through November rains and fogs Marian went her usual

[018-046]way to the Museum, and toiled there among the other toilers. Perhaps

[018-047]once a week she allowed herself to stray about the alleys of the

[018-048]Reading-room, scanning furtively those who sat at the desks, but the

[018-049]face she might perchance have discovered was not there.

[018-050]One day at the end of the month she sat with books open before her, but

[018-051]by no effort could fix her attention upon them. It was gloomy, and one

[018-052]could scarcely see to read; a taste of fog grew perceptible in the warm,

[018-053]headachy air. Such profound discouragement possessed her that she

[018-054]could not even maintain the pretence of study; heedless whether anyone

[018-055]observed her, she let her hands fall and her head droop. She kept asking

[018-056]herself what was the use and purpose of such a life as she was condemned

[018-057]to lead. When already there was more good literature in the world than

[018-058]any mortal could cope with in his lifetime, here was she exhausting

[018-059]herself in the manufacture of printed stuff which no one even pretended

[018-060]to be more than a commodity for the day’s market. What unspeakable

[018-061]folly! To write--was not that the joy and the privilege of one who had

[018-062]an urgent message for the world?

[018-063]Her father, she knew well, had no such message; he had abandoned all

[018-064]thought of original production, and only wrote about writing.

[018-065]She herself would throw away her pen with joy but for the need of

[018-066]earning money. And all these people about her, what aim had they save to

[018-067]make new books out of those already existing, that yet newer books

[018-068]might in turn be made out of theirs? This huge library, growing into

[018-069]unwieldiness, threatening to become a trackless desert of print--how

[018-070]intolerably it weighed upon the spirit!

[018-071]Oh, to go forth and labour with one’s hands, to do any poorest,

[018-072]commonest work of which the world had truly need! It was ignoble to sit

[018-073]here and support the paltry pretence of intellectual dignity. A few

[018-074]days ago her startled eye had caught an advertisement in the newspaper,

[018-075]headed ‘Literary Machine’; had it then been invented at last, some

[018-076]automaton to supply the place of such poor creatures as herself to

[018-077]turn out books and articles? Alas! the machine was only one for holding

[018-078]volumes conveniently, that the work of literary manufacture might be

[018-079]physically lightened. But surely before long some Edison would make the

[018-080]true automaton; the problem must be comparatively such a simple one.

[018-081]Only to throw in a given number of old books, and have them reduced,

[018-082]blended, modernised into a single one for to-day’s consumption.

[018-083]The fog grew thicker; she looked up at the windows beneath the dome and

[018-084]saw that they were a dusky yellow. Then her eye discerned an official

[018-085]walking along the upper gallery, and in pursuance of her grotesque

[018-086]humour, her mocking misery, she likened him to a black, lost soul,

[018-087]doomed to wander in an eternity of vain research along endless shelves.

[018-088]Or again, the readers who sat here at these radiating lines of desks,

[018-089]what were they but hapless flies caught in a huge web, its nucleus the

[018-090]great circle of the Catalogue? Darker, darker. From the towering wall of

[018-091]volumes seemed to emanate visible motes, intensifying the obscurity;

[018-092]in a moment the book-lined circumference of the room would be but a

[018-093]featureless prison-limit.

[018-094]But then flashed forth the sputtering whiteness of the electric light,

[018-095]and its ceaseless hum was henceforth a new source of headache. It

[018-096]reminded her how little work she had done to-day; she must, she must

[018-097]force herself to think of the task in hand. A machine has no business to

[018-098]refuse its duty. But the pages were blue and green and yellow before her

[018-099]eyes; the uncertainty of the light was intolerable. Right or wrong she

[018-100]would go home, and hide herself, and let her heart unburden itself of

[018-101]tears.

[018-102]On her way to return books she encountered Jasper Milvain. Face to face;

[018-103]no possibility of his avoiding her.

[018-104]And indeed he seemed to have no such wish. His countenance lighted up

[018-105]with unmistakable pleasure.

[018-106]‘At last we meet, as they say in the melodramas. Oh, do let me help you

[018-107]with those volumes, which won’t even let you shake hands. How do you do?

[018-108]How do you like this weather? And how do you like this light?’

[018-109]‘It’s very bad.’

[018-110]‘That’ll do both for weather and light, but not for yourself. How glad I

[018-111]am to see you! Are you just going?’

[018-112]‘Yes.’

[018-113]‘I have scarcely been here half-a-dozen times since I came back to

[018-114][[london|London]].’

[018-115]‘But you are writing still?’

[018-116]‘Oh yes! But I draw upon my [[literary-genius|genius]], and my stores of observation, and

[018-117]the living world.’

[018-118]Marian received her vouchers for the volumes, and turned to face Jasper

[018-119]again. There was a smile on her lips.

[018-120]‘The fog is terrible,’ Milvain went on. ‘How do you get home?’

[018-121]‘By omnibus from [[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]].’

[018-122]‘Then do let me go a part of the way with you. I live in Mornington

[018-123]Road--up yonder, you know. I have only just come in to waste half an

[018-124]hour, and after all I think I should be better at home. Your father is

[018-125]all right, I hope?’

[018-126]‘He is not quite well.’

[018-127]‘I’m sorry to hear that. You are not exactly up to the mark, either.

[018-128]What weather! What a place to live in, this London, in winter! It would

[018-129]be a little better down at Finden.’

[018-130]‘A good deal better, I should think. If the weather were bad, it would

[018-131]be bad in a natural way; but this is artificial misery.’

[018-132]‘I don’t let it affect me much,’ said Milvain. ‘Just of late I have

[018-133]been in remarkably good spirits. I’m doing a lot of work. No end of

[018-134]work--more than I’ve ever done.’

[018-135]‘I am very glad.’

[018-136]‘Where are your out-of-door things? I think there’s a ladies’ vestry

[018-137]somewhere, isn’t there?’

[018-138]‘Oh yes.’

[018-139]‘Then will you go and get ready? I’ll wait for you in the hall. But,

[018-140]by-the-bye, I am taking it for granted that you were going alone.’

[018-141]‘I was, quite alone.’

[018-142]The ‘quite’ seemed excessive; it made Jasper smile.

[018-143]‘And also,’ he added, ‘that I shall not annoy you by offering my

[018-144]company?’

[018-145]‘Why should it annoy me?’

[018-146]‘Good!’

[018-147]Milvain had only to wait a minute or two. He surveyed Marian from head

[018-148]to foot when she appeared--an impertinence as unintentional as that

[018-149]occasionally noticeable in his speech--and smiled approval. They went

[018-150]out into the fog, which was not one of London’s densest, but made

[018-151]walking disagreeable enough.

[018-152]‘You have heard from the girls, I think?’ Jasper resumed.

[018-153]‘Your sisters? Yes; they have been so kind as to write to me.’

[018-154]‘Told you all about their great work? I hope it’ll be finished by the

[018-155]end of the year. The bits they have sent me will do very well indeed. I

[018-156]knew they had it in them to put sentences together. Now I want them to

[018-157]think of patching up something or other for The English Girl; you know

[018-158]the paper?’

[018-159]‘I have heard of it.’

[018-160]‘I happen to know Mrs Boston Wright, who edits it. Met her at a house

[018-161]the other day, and told her frankly that she would have to give my

[018-162]sisters something to do. It’s the only way to get on; one has to take it

[018-163]for granted that people are willing to help you. I have made a host of

[018-164]new acquaintances just lately.’

[018-165]‘I’m glad to hear it,’ said Marian.

[018-166]‘Do you know--but how should you? I am going to write for the new

[018-167]magazine, [[the-current|The Current]].’

[018-168]‘Indeed!’

[018-169]‘Edited by that man Fadge.’

[018-170]‘Yes.’

[018-171]‘Your father has no affection for him, I know.’

[018-172]‘He has no reason to have, Mr Milvain.’

[018-173]‘No, no. Fadge is an offensive fellow, when he likes; and I fancy he

[018-174]very often does like. Well, I must make what use of him I can.

[018-175]You won’t think worse of me because I write for him?’

[018-176]‘I know that one can’t exercise choice in such things.’

[018-177]‘True. I shouldn’t like to think that you regard me as a Fadge-like

[018-178]individual, a natural Fadgeite.’

[018-179]Marian laughed.

[018-180]‘There’s no danger of my thinking that.’

[018-181]But the fog was making their eyes water and getting into their throats.

[018-182]By when they reached Tottenham Court Road they were both thoroughly

[018-183]uncomfortable. The ‘bus had to be waited for, and in the meantime they

[018-184]talked scrappily, coughily. In the vehicle things were a little better,

[018-185]but here one could not converse with freedom.

[018-186]‘What pestilent conditions of life!’ exclaimed Jasper, putting his face

[018-187]rather near to Marian’s. ‘I wish to goodness we were back in those quiet

[018-188]fields--you remember?--with the September sun warm about us. Shall you

[018-189]go to Finden again before long?’
