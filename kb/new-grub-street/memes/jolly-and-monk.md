# Jolly and Monk

Messrs Jolly and Monk was a [[london|London]] [[publishing-trade|publishing]] firm that represented the lower tier of the Victorian [[publishing-trade|publishing]] industry — producing compilations, popular histories, and cheap books for the mass market rather than literary fiction. In [[New Grub Street]], the firm provides piece-work employment to the [[Milvain sisters]] and exemplifies the commodification of literary labour.

## Role in the Novel

Jolly and Monk appears throughout the novel as an employer of literary piece-workers. The firm's market position is frankly commercial: [[jasper-milvain]] investigates "the state of the market as to such wares as Jolly and Monk dispose of" [012-131] — the dismissive "such wares" capturing the contempt that literary aspirants felt toward the firm's output even as they depended on it for income.

The [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] sisters — [[dora-milvain|Dora]] and [[maud-milvain|Maud]] — become the firm's most visible employees, working on English histories and compilations. [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] negotiates their first commission: "Jolly and Monk offer thirty pounds for the little book, if it suits them when finished" [015-007], and later reports they are "doing a new piece of compilation for Messrs Jolly and Monk; it was more exacting than their initial task" [031-011].

The terms are revealing. Thirty pounds for a book-length compilation represents subsistence wages, but the sisters — living with their mother on limited means — have few alternatives. By autumn, "the two girls made good progress with the book they were manufacturing for Messrs Jolly and Monk" [047-032], the verb "manufacturing" deliberately chosen to suggest industrial production rather than literary creation.

## The Compilation Economy

Jolly and Monk's business model relied on the "compilation" — books assembled from existing sources rather than original research or creative work. This model employed a growing [[social-class|class]] of literary workers who, like the [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] sisters, could produce serviceable prose on demand without specialist knowledge. [[jasper-milvain]] also considers writing for "The English Girl" and other markets, noting he "can hit upon a second idea that will appeal to Jolly and Monk" [019-184], treating the firm as one more outlet in his diversified literary portfolio.

## Theme: Literary Commercialization

Jolly and Monk embodies the central theme of [[literature-as-trade]] in the novel. The firm represents the complete industrialization of writing: authors are not creators but "manufacturers," books are "wares," and the publisher's only criterion is marketability. The contrast with [[mudies-library]] is instructive — [[mudies-library|Mudie's]] shaped high [[literary-culture|literary culture]] through its circulating library monopoly; Jolly and Monk served the mass market at the bottom, indifferent to literary quality.
