# Section 7

[006-001]And as he talked the man kept describing imaginary geometrical figures

[006-002]with the end of his walking-stick; he very seldom raised his eyes

[006-003]from the ground, and the stoop in his shoulders grew more and more

[006-004]pronounced, until at a little distance one might have taken him for a

[006-005]hunchback. At one point [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] made a pause to speak of the pleasant

[006-006]wooded prospect that lay before them; his companion regarded it

[006-007]absently, and in a moment or two asked:

[006-008]‘Did you ever come across Cottle’s poem on the Malvern Hills? No?

[006-009]It contains a couple of the richest lines ever put into print:

[006-010]It needs the evidence of close deduction

[006-011]To know that I shall ever reach the top.

[006-012]Perfectly serious poetry, mind you!’

[006-013]He barked in laughter. Impossible to interest him in anything apart from

[006-014]literature; yet one saw him to be a man of solid understanding, and

[006-015]not without perception of humour. He had read vastly; his memory was a

[006-016]literary cyclopaedia. His failings, obvious enough, were the results

[006-017]of a strong and somewhat pedantic individuality ceaselessly at conflict

[006-018]with unpropitious circumstances.

[006-019]Towards the young man his demeanour varied between a shy cordiality and

[006-020]a dignified reserve which was in danger of seeming pretentious. On the

[006-021]homeward part of the walk he made a few discreet inquiries regarding

[006-022]Milvain’s literary achievements and prospects, and the frank

[006-023]self-confidence of the replies appeared to interest him. But he

[006-024]expressed no desire to number Jasper among his acquaintances in town,

[006-025]and of his own professional or private concerns he said not a word.

[006-026]‘Whether he could be any use to me or not, I don’t exactly know,’ Jasper

[006-027]remarked to his mother and sisters at dinner. ‘I suspect it’s as much as

[006-028]he can do to keep a footing among the younger tradesmen. But I think he

[006-029]might have said he was willing to help me if he could.’

[006-030]‘Perhaps,’ replied Maud, ‘your large way of talking made him think any

[006-031]such offer superfluous.’

[006-032]‘You have still to learn,’ said Jasper, ‘that modesty helps a man in no

[006-033]department of modern life. People take you at your own valuation. It’s

[006-034]the men who declare boldly that they need no help to whom practical

[006-035]help comes from all sides. As likely as not [[alfred-yule|Yule]] will mention my name

[006-036]to someone. “A young fellow who seems to see his way pretty clear before

[006-037]him.” The other man will repeat it to somebody else, “A young fellow

[006-038]whose way is clear before him,” and so I come to the ears of a man who

[006-039]thinks “Just the fellow I want; I must look him up and ask him if he’ll

[006-040]do such-and-such a thing.” But I should like to see these Yules at home;

[006-041]I must fish for an invitation.’

[006-042]In the afternoon, Miss Harrow and [[marian-yule|Marian]] came at the expected hour.

[006-043]Jasper purposely kept out of the way until he was summoned to the

[006-044]tea-table.

[006-045]The Milvain girls were so far from effusive, even towards old

[006-046]acquaintances, that even the people who knew them best spoke of them

[006-047]as rather cold and perhaps a trifle condescending; there were people

[006-048]in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] who declared their airs of superiority ridiculous and

[006-049]insufferable. The truth was that nature had endowed them with a larger

[006-050]share of brains than was common in their circle, and had added that

[006-051]touch of pride which harmonised so ill with the restrictions of

[006-052][[literary-poverty|poverty]]. Their life had a tone of melancholy, the painful reserve which

[006-053]characterises a certain clearly defined [[social-class|class]] in the present day. Had

[006-054]they been born twenty years earlier, the children of that veterinary

[006-055]surgeon would have grown up to a very different, and in all probability

[006-056]a much happier, existence, for their education would have been

[006-057]limited to the strictly needful, and--certainly in the case of the

[006-058]girls--nothing would have encouraged them to look beyond the simple life

[006-059]possible to a poor man’s offspring. But whilst Maud and [[dora-milvain|Dora]] were still

[006-060]with their homely schoolmistress, Wattleborough saw fit to establish

[006-061]a Girls’ High School, and the moderateness of the fees enabled these

[006-062]sisters to receive an intellectual training wholly incompatible with the

[006-063]material conditions of their life. To the relatively poor (who are so

[006-064]much worse off than the poor absolutely) education is in most cases

[006-065]a mocking cruelty. The burden of their brother’s support made it very

[006-066]difficult for Maud and Dora even to dress as became their intellectual

[006-067]station; amusements, holidays, the purchase of such simple luxuries as

[006-068]were all but indispensable to them, could not be thought of. It resulted

[006-069]that they held apart from the society which would have welcomed them,

[006-070]for they could not bear to receive without offering in turn. The

[006-071]necessity of giving lessons galled them; they felt--and with every

[006-072]reason--that it made their position ambiguous. So that, though they

[006-073]could not help knowing many people, they had no intimates; they

[006-074]encouraged no one to visit them, and visited other houses as little as

[006-075]might be.

[006-076]In Marian Yule they divined a sympathetic nature. She was unlike any

[006-077]girl with whom they had hitherto associated, and it was the impulse of

[006-078]both to receive her with unusual friendliness. The habit of reticence

[006-079]could not be at once overcome, and Marian’s own timidity was an obstacle

[006-080]in the way of free intercourse, but Jasper’s conversation at tea helped

[006-081]to smooth the course of things.

[006-082]‘I wish you lived anywhere near us,’ Dora said to their visitor, as the

[006-083]three girls walked in the garden afterwards, and Maud echoed the wish.

[006-084]‘It would be very nice,’ was Marian’s reply. ‘I have no friends of my

[006-085]own age in [[london|London]].’

[006-086]‘None?’

[006-087]‘Not one!’

[006-088]She was about to add something, but in the end kept silence.

[006-089]‘You seem to get along with Miss Yule pretty well, after all,’ said

[006-090]Jasper, when the family were alone again.

[006-091]‘Did you anticipate anything else?’ Maud asked.

[006-092]‘It seemed doubtful, up at Yule’s house. Well, get her to come here

[006-093]again before I go. But it’s a pity she doesn’t play the piano,’ he

[006-094]added, musingly.

[006-095]For two days nothing was seen of the Yules. Jasper went each afternoon

[006-096]to the stream in the valley, but did not again meet Marian. In the

[006-097]meanwhile he was growing restless. A fortnight always exhausted his

[006-098]capacity for enjoying the companionship of his mother and sisters, and

[006-099]this time he seemed anxious to get to the end of his holiday. For all

[006-100]that, there was no continuance of the domestic bickering which had

[006-101]begun. Whatever the reason, Maud behaved with unusual mildness to her

[006-102]brother, and Jasper in turn was gently disposed to both the girls.

[006-103]On the morning of the third day--it was Saturday--he kept silence

[006-104]through breakfast, and just as all were about to rise from the table, he

[006-105]made a sudden announcement:

[006-106]‘I shall go to London this afternoon.’

[006-107]‘This afternoon?’ all exclaimed. ‘But Monday is your day.’

[006-108]‘No, I shall go this afternoon, by the 2.45.’

[006-109]And he left the room. Mrs Milvain and the girls exchanged looks.

[006-110]‘I suppose he thinks the Sunday will be too wearisome,’ said the mother.

[006-111]‘Perhaps so,’ Maud agreed, carelessly.

[006-112]Half an hour later, just as Dora was ready to leave the house for her

[006-113]engagements in Wattleborough, her brother came into the hall and took

[006-114]his hat, saying:

[006-115]‘I’ll walk a little way with you, if you don’t mind.’

[006-116]When they were in the road, he asked her in an offhand manner:

[006-117]‘Do you think I ought to say good-bye to the Yules? Or won’t it

[006-118]signify?’

[006-119]‘I should have thought you would wish to.’

[006-120]‘I don’t care about it. And, you see, there’s been no hint of a wish on

[006-121]their part that I should see them in London. No, I’ll just leave you to

[006-122]say good-bye for me.’

[006-123]‘But they expect to see us to-day or to-morrow. You told them you were

[006-124]not going till Monday, and you don’t know but Mr Yule might mean to say

[006-125]something yet.’

[006-126]‘Well, I had rather he didn’t,’ replied Jasper, with a laugh.

[006-127]‘Oh, indeed?’

[006-128]‘I don’t mind telling you,’ he laughed again. ‘I’m afraid of that girl.

[006-129]No, it won’t do! You understand that I’m a practical man, and I shall

[006-130]keep clear of dangers. These days of holiday idleness put all sorts of

[006-131]nonsense into one’s head.’

[006-132]Dora kept her eyes down, and smiled ambiguously.

[006-133]‘You must act as you think fit,’ she remarked at length.

[006-134]‘Exactly. Now I’ll turn back. You’ll be with us at dinner?’

[006-135]They parted. But Jasper did not keep to the straight way home. First of

[006-136]all, he loitered to watch a reaping-machine at work; then he turned into

[006-137]a lane which led up the hill on which was [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s house. Even if he

[006-138]had purposed making a farewell call, it was still far too early; all he

[006-139]wanted to do was to pass an hour of the morning, which threatened to lie

[006-140]heavy on his hands. So he rambled on, and went past the house, and took

[006-141]the field-path which would lead him circuitously home again.

[006-142]His mother desired to speak to him. She was in the dining-room; in the

[006-143]parlour Maud was practising music.

[006-144]‘I think I ought to tell you of something I did yesterday, Jasper,’ Mrs

[006-145]Milvain began. ‘You see, my dear, we have been rather straitened lately,

[006-146]and my health, you know, grows so uncertain, and, all things considered,

[006-147]I have been feeling very anxious about the girls. So I wrote to your

[006-148]uncle William, and told him that I must positively have that [[money-in-literature|money]]. I

[006-149]must think of my own children before his.’

[006-150]The matter referred to was this. The deceased Mr Milvain had a brother

[006-151]who was a struggling shopkeeper in a Midland town. Some ten years ago,

[006-152]William Milvain, on the point of bankruptcy, had borrowed a hundred

[006-153]and seventy pounds from his brother in Wattleborough, and this debt was

[006-154]still unpaid; for on the death of Jasper’s father repayment of the loan

[006-155]was impossible for William, and since then it had seemed hopeless that

[006-156]the sum would ever be recovered. The poor shopkeeper had a large family,

[006-157]and Mrs Milvain, notwithstanding her own position, had never felt able

[006-158]to press him; her relative, however, often spoke of the business, and

[006-159]declared his intention of paying whenever he could.

[006-160]‘You can’t recover by law now, you know,’ said Jasper.

[006-161]‘But we have a right to the money, law or no law. He must pay it.’

[006-162]‘He will simply refuse--and be justified. Poverty doesn’t allow of

[006-163]honourable feeling, any more than of compassion. I’m sorry you wrote

[006-164]like that. You won’t get anything, and you might as well have enjoyed

[006-165]the [[literary-success|reputation]] of forbearance.’

[006-166]Mrs Milvain was not able to appreciate this characteristic remark.

[006-167]Anxiety weighed upon her, and she became irritable.

[006-168]‘I am obliged to say, Jasper, that you seem rather thoughtless. If

[006-169]it were only myself. I would make any sacrifice for you; but you must

[006-170]remember--’

[006-171]‘Now listen, mother,’ he interrupted, laying a hand on her shoulder;

[006-172]‘I have been thinking about all this, and the fact of the matter is,

[006-173]I shall do my best to ask you for no more money. It may or may not be

[006-174]practicable, but I’ll have a try. So don’t worry. If uncle writes that

[006-175]he can’t pay, just explain why you wrote, and keep him gently in mind of

[006-176]the thing, that’s all. One doesn’t like to do brutal things if one can

[006-177]avoid them, you know.’

[006-178]The young man went to the parlour and listened to Maud’s music for

[006-179]awhile. But restlessness again drove him forth. Towards eleven o’clock

[006-180]he was again ascending in the direction of John Yule’s house. Again

[006-181]he had no intention of calling, but when he reached the iron gates he

[006-182]lingered.

[006-183]‘I will, by Jove!’ he said within himself at last. ‘Just to prove I

[006-184]have complete command of myself. It’s to be a display of strength, not

[006-185]weakness.’
