# Section 22

[021-001]The impossible; for he could no longer deceive himself with a hope of

[021-002]genuine [[literary-ambition|success]]. If he earned a bare living, that would be the utmost.

[021-003]And with bare livelihood [[amy-reardon|Amy]] would not, could not, be content.

[021-004]If he were to die a natural death it would be well for all. His wife and

[021-005]the child would be looked after; they could live with [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]],

[021-006]and certainly it would not be long before Amy married again, this time a

[021-007]man of whose competency to maintain her there would be no doubt. His own

[021-008]behaviour had been cowardly selfishness. Oh yes, she had loved him, had

[021-009]been eager to believe in him. But there was always that voice of warning

[021-010]in his mind; he foresaw--he knew--

[021-011]And if he killed himself? Not here; no lurid horrors for that poor girl

[021-012]and her relatives; but somewhere at a distance, under circumstances

[021-013]which would render the recovery of his body difficult, yet would leave

[021-014]no doubt of his death. Would that, again, be cowardly? The opposite,

[021-015]when once it was certain that to live meant [[literary-poverty|poverty]] and wretchedness.

[021-016]Amy’s grief, however sincere, would be but a short trial compared with

[021-017]what else might lie before her. The burden of supporting her and Willie

[021-018]would be a very slight one if she went to live in her mother’s house.

[021-019]He considered the whole matter night after night, until perchance it

[021-020]happened that sleep had pity upon him for an hour before the time of

[021-021]rising.

[021-022]Autumn was passing into winter. Dark days, which were always an

[021-023]oppression to his mind, began to be frequent, and would soon succeed

[021-024]each other remorselessly. Well, if only each of them represented four

[021-025]written slips.

[021-026][[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]’s advice to him had of course proved useless. The sensational

[021-027]title suggested nothing, or only ragged shapes of incomplete humanity

[021-028]that fluttered mockingly when he strove to fix them. But he had decided

[021-029]upon a story of the kind natural to him; a ‘thin’ story, and one which

[021-030]it would be difficult to spin into three volumes. His own, at all

[021-031]events. The title was always a matter for head-racking when the book was

[021-032]finished; he had never yet chosen it before beginning.

[021-033]For a week he got on at the desired rate; then came once more the crisis

[021-034]he had anticipated.

[021-035]A familiar symptom of the malady which falls upon outwearied

[021-036]imagination. There were floating in his mind five or six possible

[021-037]subjects for a book, all dating back to the time when he first began

[021-038]novel-writing, when ideas came freshly to him. If he grasped desperately

[021-039]at one of these, and did his best to develop it, for a day or two he

[021-040]could almost content himself; characters, situations, lines of motive,

[021-041]were laboriously schemed, and he felt ready to begin writing. But

[021-042]scarcely had he done a chapter or two when all the structure fell into

[021-043]flatness. He had made a mistake. Not this story, but that other one, was

[021-044]what he should have taken. The other one in question, left out of mind

[021-045]for a time, had come back with a face of new possibility; it invited

[021-046]him, tempted him to throw aside what he had already written. Good;

[021-047]now he was in more hopeful train. But a few days, and the experience

[021-048]repeated itself. No, not this story, but that third one, of which he

[021-049]had not thought for a long time. How could he have rejected so hopeful a

[021-050]subject?

[021-051]For months he had been living in this way; endless circling, perpetual

[021-052]beginning, followed by frustration. A sign of exhaustion, it of course

[021-053]made exhaustion more complete. At times he was on the border-land of

[021-054]imbecility; his mind looked into a cloudy chaos, a shapeless whirl of

[021-055]nothings. He talked aloud to himself, not knowing that he did so. Little

[021-056]phrases which indicated dolorously the subject of his preoccupation

[021-057]often escaped him in the street: ‘What could I make of that, now?’

[021-058]‘Well, suppose I made him--?’ ‘But no, that wouldn’t do,’ and so on.

[021-059]It had happened that he caught the eye of some one passing fixed in

[021-060]surprise upon him; so young a man to be talking to himself in evident

[021-061]distress!

[021-062]The expected crisis came, even now that he was savagely determined to

[021-063]go on at any cost, to write, let the result be what it would. His will

[021-064]prevailed. A day or two of anguish such as there is no describing to the

[021-065]inexperienced, and again he was dismissing slip after slip, a sigh of

[021-066]thankfulness at the completion of each one. It was a fraction of the

[021-067]whole, a fraction, a fraction.

[021-068]The ordering of his day was thus. At nine, after breakfast, he sat down

[021-069]to his desk, and worked till one. Then came dinner, followed by a walk.

[021-070]As a rule he could not allow Amy to walk with him, for he had to think

[021-071]over the remainder of the day’s toil, and companionship would have been

[021-072]fatal. At about half-past three he again seated himself; and wrote until

[021-073]half-past six, when he had a meal. Then once more to work from half-past

[021-074]seven to ten. Numberless were the experiments he had tried for the day’s

[021-075]division. The slightest interruption of the order for the time being put

[021-076]him out of gear; Amy durst not open his door to ask however necessary a

[021-077]question.

[021-078]Sometimes the three hours’ labour of a morning resulted in half-a-dozen

[021-079]lines, corrected into illegibility. His brain would not work; he could

[021-080]not recall the simplest synonyms; intolerable faults of composition

[021-081]drove him mad. He would write a sentence beginning thus: ‘She took a

[021-082]book with a look of--;’ or thus: ‘A revision of this decision would

[021-083]have made him an object of derision.’ Or, if the period were otherwise

[021-084]inoffensive, it ran in a rhythmic gallop which was torment to the ear.

[021-085]All this, in spite of the fact that his former books had been noticeably

[021-086]good in style. He had an appreciation of shapely prose which made him

[021-087]scorn himself for the kind of stuff he was now turning out. ‘I can’t

[021-088]help it; it must go; the time is passing.’

[021-089]Things were better, as a rule, in the evening. Occasionally he wrote a

[021-090]page with fluency which recalled his fortunate years; and then his heart

[021-091]gladdened, his hand trembled with joy.

[021-092]Description of locality, deliberate analysis of character or motive,

[021-093]demanded far too great an effort for his present condition. He kept as

[021-094]much as possible to dialogue; the space is filled so much more quickly,

[021-095]and at a pinch one can make people talk about the paltriest incidents of

[021-096]life.

[021-097]There came an evening when he opened the door and called to Amy.

[021-098]‘What is it?’ she answered from the bedroom. ‘I’m busy with Willie.’

[021-099]‘Come as soon as you are free.’

[021-100]In ten minutes she appeared. There was apprehension on her face; she

[021-101]feared he was going to lament his inability to work. Instead of that, he

[021-102]told her joyfully that the first volume was finished.

[021-103]‘Thank goodness!’ she exclaimed. ‘Are you going to do any more

[021-104]to-night?’

[021-105]‘I think not--if you will come and sit with me.’

[021-106]‘Willie doesn’t seem very well. He can’t get to sleep.’

[021-107]‘You would like to stay with him?’

[021-108]‘A little while. I’ll come presently.’

[021-109]She closed the door. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] brought a high-backed chair to the

[021-110]fireside, and allowed himself to forget the two volumes that had still

[021-111]to be struggled through, in a grateful sense of the portion that

[021-112]was achieved. In a few minutes it occurred to him that it would be

[021-113]delightful to read a scrap of the ‘Odyssey’; he went to the shelves on

[021-114]which were his classical books, took the desired volume, and opened it

[021-115]where Odysseus speaks to Nausicaa:

[021-116]‘For never yet did I behold one of mortals like to thee, neither man nor

[021-117]woman; I am awed as I look upon thee. In Delos once, hard by the altar

[021-118]of Apollo, I saw a young palm-tree shooting up with even such a grace.’

[021-119]Yes, yes; THAT was not written at so many pages a day, with a workhouse

[021-120]clock clanging its admonition at the poet’s ear. How it freshened the

[021-121]soul! How the eyes grew dim with a rare joy in the sounding of those

[021-122]nobly sweet hexameters!

[021-123]Amy came into the room again.

[021-124]‘Listen,’ said Reardon, looking up at her with a bright smile. ‘Do you

[021-125]remember the first time that I read you this?’

[021-126]And he turned the speech into free prose. Amy laughed.

[021-127]‘I remember it well enough. We were alone in the drawing-room; I had

[021-128]told the others that they must make shift with the dining-room for that

[021-129]evening. And you pulled the book out of your pocket unexpectedly. I

[021-130]laughed at your habit of always carrying little books about.’

[021-131]The cheerful news had brightened her. If she had been summoned to hear

[021-132]lamentations her voice would not have rippled thus soothingly. Reardon

[021-133]thought of this, and it made him silent for a minute.

[021-134]‘The habit was ominous,’ he said, looking at her with an uncertain

[021-135]smile. ‘A practical literary man doesn’t do such things.’

[021-136]‘Milvain, for instance. No.’

[021-137]With curious frequency she mentioned the name of Milvain. Her

[021-138]unconsciousness in doing so prevented Reardon from thinking about the

[021-139]fact; still, he had noted it.

[021-140]‘Did you understand the phrase slightingly?’ he asked.

[021-141]‘Slightingly? Yes, a little, of course. It always has that sense on your

[021-142]lips, I think.’

[021-143]In the light of this answer he mused upon her readily-offered instance.

[021-144]True, he had occasionally spoken of Jasper with something less than

[021-145]respect, but Amy was not in the habit of doing so.

[021-146]‘I hadn’t any such meaning just then,’ he said. ‘I meant quite

[021-147]simply that my bookish habits didn’t promise much for my [[literary-success|success]] as a

[021-148]novelist.’

[021-149]‘I see. But you didn’t think of it in that way at the time.’

[021-150]He sighed.

[021-151]‘No. At least--no.’

[021-152]‘At least what?’

[021-153]‘Well, no; on the whole I had good hope.’

[021-154]Amy twisted her fingers together impatiently.

[021-155]‘Edwin, let me tell you something. You are getting too fond of speaking

[021-156]in a discouraging way. Now, why should you do so? I don’t like it. It

[021-157]has one disagreeable effect on me, and that is, when people ask me about

[021-158]you, how you are getting on, I don’t quite know how to answer. They

[021-159]can’t help seeing that I am uneasy. I speak so differently from what I

[021-160]used to.’

[021-161]‘Do you, really?’

[021-162]‘Indeed I can’t help it. As I say, it’s very much your own fault.’

[021-163]‘Well, but granted that I am not of a very sanguine nature, and that I

[021-164]easily fall into gloomy ways of talk, what is Amy here for?’

[021-165]‘Yes, yes. But--’

[021-166]‘But?’

[021-167]‘I am not here only to try and keep you in good spirits, am I?’

[021-168]She asked it prettily, with a smile like that of maidenhood.

[021-169]‘Heaven forbid! I oughtn’t to have put it in that absolute way. I was

[021-170]half joking, you know. But unfortunately it’s true that I can’t be as

[021-171]light-spirited as I could wish. Does that make you impatient with me?’

[021-172]‘A little. I can’t help the feeling, and I ought to try to overcome it.

[021-173]But you must try on your side as well. Why should you have said that

[021-174]thing just now?’

[021-175]‘You’re quite right. It was needless.’

[021-176]‘A few weeks ago I didn’t expect you to be cheerful. Things began

[021-177]to look about as bad as they could. But now that you’ve got a volume

[021-178]finished, there’s hope once more.’

[021-179]Hope? Of what quality? Reardon durst not say what rose in his thoughts.

[021-180]‘A very small, poor hope. Hope of [[money-in-literature|money]] enough to struggle through

[021-181]another half year, if indeed enough for that.’ He had learnt that Amy

[021-182]was not to be told the whole truth about anything as he himself saw it.

[021-183]It was a pity. To the ideal wife a man speaks out all that is in him;

[021-184]she had infinitely rather share his full conviction than be treated as

[021-185]one from whom facts must be disguised. She says: ‘Let us face the worst

[021-186]and talk of it together, you and I.’ No, Amy was not the ideal wife

[021-187]from that point of view. But the moment after this half-reproach had

[021-188]traversed his consciousness he condemned himself; and looked with the

[021-189]joy of love into her clear eyes.
