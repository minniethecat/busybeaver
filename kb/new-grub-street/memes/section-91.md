# Section 91

[090-001]‘I wish it very, very often,’ [[dora-milvain|Dora]] replied in a moved voice.

[090-002]‘We haven’t done badly, drawbacks considered. Now, you may speak of

[090-003][[money-in-literature|money]] as scornfully as you like; but suppose you had married a man who

[090-004]could only keep you in lodgings! How would life look to you?’

[090-005]‘Who ever disputed the value of money? But there are things one mustn’t

[090-006]sacrifice to gain it.’

[090-007]‘I suppose so. Well, I have some news for you, Dora. I am thinking of

[090-008]following your example.’

[090-009]Dora’s face changed to grave anticipation.

[090-010]‘And who is it?’

[090-011]‘[[amy-reardon|Amy Reardon]].’

[090-012]His sister turned away, with a look of intense annoyance.

[090-013]‘You see, I am disinterested myself,’ he went on. ‘I might find a wife

[090-014]who had wealth and social standing. But I choose Amy deliberately.’

[090-015]‘An abominable choice!’

[090-016]‘No; an excellent choice. I have never yet met a woman so well fitted

[090-017]to aid me in my career. She has a trifling sum of money, which will be

[090-018]useful for the next year or two--’

[090-019]‘What has she done with the rest of it, then?’

[090-020]‘Oh, the ten thousand is intact, but it can’t be seriously spoken of. It

[090-021]will keep up appearances till I get my editorship and so on. We shall be

[090-022]married early in August, I think. I want to ask you if you will go and

[090-023]see her.’

[090-024]‘On no account! I couldn’t be civil to her.’

[090-025][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]’s brows blackened.

[090-026]‘This is idiotic prejudice, Dora. I think I have some claim upon you; I

[090-027]have shown some kindness--’

[090-028]‘You have, and I am not ungrateful. But I dislike Mrs [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], and I

[090-029]couldn’t bring myself to be friendly with her.’

[090-030]‘You don’t know her.’

[090-031]‘Too well. You yourself have taught me to know her. Don’t compel me to

[090-032]say what I think of her.’

[090-033]‘She is beautiful, and high-minded, and warm-hearted. I don’t know

[090-034]a womanly quality that she doesn’t possess. You will offend me most

[090-035]seriously if you speak a word against her.’

[090-036]‘Then I will be silent. But you must never ask me to meet her.’

[090-037]‘Never?’

[090-038]‘Never!’

[090-039]‘Then we shall quarrel. I haven’t deserved this, Dora. If you refuse

[090-040]to meet my wife on terms of decent friendliness, there’s no more

[090-041]intercourse between your house and mine. You have to choose. Persist in

[090-042]this fatuous obstinacy, and I have done with you!’

[090-043]‘So be it!’

[090-044]‘That is your final answer?’

[090-045]Dora, who was now as angry as he, gave a short affirmative, and Jasper

[090-046]at once left her.

[090-047]But it was very unlikely that things should rest at this pass. The

[090-048]brother and sister were bound by a strong mutual affection, and

[090-049][[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] was not long in effecting a compromise.

[090-050]‘My dear wife,’ he exclaimed, in despair at the threatened calamity,

[090-051]‘you are right, a thousand times, but it’s impossible for you to be

[090-052]on ill terms with Jasper. There’s no need for you to see much of Mrs

[090-053]Reardon--’

[090-054]‘I hate her! She killed her husband; I am sure of it.’

[090-055]‘My darling!’

[090-056]‘I mean by her base conduct. She is a cold, cruel, unprincipled

[090-057]creature! Jasper makes himself more than ever contemptible by marrying

[090-058]her.’

[090-059]All the same, in less than three weeks Mrs Whelpdale had called upon

[090-060]Amy, and the call was returned. The two women were perfectly conscious

[090-061]of reciprocal dislike, but they smothered the feeling beneath

[090-062]conventional suavities. Jasper was not backward in making known his

[090-063]gratitude for Dora’s concession, and indeed it became clear to all his

[090-064]intimates that this [[literary-marriage|marriage]] would be by no means one of mere interest;

[090-065]the man was in love at last, if he had never been before.

[090-066]Let lapse the ensuing twelve months, and come to an evening at the end

[090-067]of July, 1886. Mr and Mrs Milvain are entertaining a small and select

[090-068]party of friends at dinner. Their house in [[bayswater|Bayswater]] is neither large

[090-069]nor internally magnificent, but it will do very well for the temporary

[090-070]sojourn of a young man of letters who has much greater things in

[090-071]confident expectation, who is a good deal talked of, who can gather

[090-072]clever and worthy people at his table, and whose matchless wife would

[090-073]attract men of taste to a very much poorer abode.

[090-074]Jasper had changed considerably in appearance since that last holiday

[090-075]that he spent in his mother’s house at Finden. At present he would have

[090-076]been taken for five-and-thirty, though only in his twenty-ninth year;

[090-077]his hair was noticeably thinning; his moustache had grown heavier;

[090-078]a wrinkle or two showed beneath his eyes; his voice was softer, yet

[090-079]firmer. It goes without saying that his evening uniform lacked no point

[090-080]of perfection, and somehow it suggested a more elaborate care than that

[090-081]of other men in the room. He laughed frequently, and with a throwing

[090-082]back of the head which seemed to express a spirit of triumph.

[090-083]Amy looked her years to the full, but her type of beauty, as you

[090-084]know, was independent of youthfulness. That suspicion of masculinity

[090-085]observable in her when she became Reardon’s wife impressed one now only

[090-086]as the consummate grace of a perfectly-built woman. You saw that at

[090-087]forty, at fifty, she would be one of the stateliest of dames. When she

[090-088]bent her head towards the person with whom she spoke, it was an act of

[090-089]queenly favour. Her words were uttered with just enough deliberation to

[090-090]give them the value of an opinion; she smiled with a delicious shade

[090-091]of irony; her glance intimated that nothing could be too subtle for her

[090-092]understanding.

[090-093]The guests numbered six, and no one of them was insignificant. Two of

[090-094]the men were about Jasper’s age, and they had already made their mark

[090-095]in literature; the third was a novelist of circulating fame, spirally

[090-096]crescent. The three of the stronger sex were excellent modern types,

[090-097]with sweet lips attuned to epigram, and good broad brows.

[090-098]The novelist at one point put an interesting question to Amy.

[090-099]‘Is it true that [[fadge|Fadge]] is leaving [[the-current|The Current]]?’

[090-100]‘It is rumoured, I believe.’

[090-101]‘Going to one of the quarterlies, they say,’ remarked a lady. ‘He is

[090-102]getting terribly autocratic. Have you heard the delightful story of

[090-103]his telling Mr Rowland to persevere, as his last work was one of

[090-104]considerable promise?’

[090-105]Mr Rowland was a man who had made a merited [[literary-success|reputation]] when Fadge was

[090-106]still on the lower rungs of [[journalism-trade|journalism]]. Amy smiled and told another

[090-107]anecdote of the great editor. Whilst speaking, she caught her husband’s

[090-108]eye, and perhaps this was the reason why her story, at the close, seemed

[090-109]rather amiably pointless--not a common fault when she narrated.

[090-110]When the ladies had withdrawn, one of the younger men, in a conversation

[090-111]about a certain magazine, remarked:

[090-112]‘Thomas always maintains that it was killed by that solemn old stager,

[090-113][[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]]. By the way, he is dead himself, I hear.’

[090-114]Jasper bent forward.

[090-115]‘Alfred Yule is dead?’

[090-116]‘So [[jedwood|Jedwood]] told me this morning. He died in the country somewhere,

[090-117]blind and fallen on evil days, poor old fellow.’

[090-118]All the guests were ignorant of any tie of kindred between their host

[090-119]and the man spoken of.

[090-120]‘I believe,’ said the novelist, ‘that he had a clever daughter who used

[090-121]to do all the work he signed. That used to be a current bit of scandal

[090-122]in Fadge’s circle.’

[090-123]‘Oh, there was much exaggeration in that,’ remarked Jasper, blandly.

[090-124]‘His daughter assisted him, doubtless, but in quite a legitimate way.

[090-125]One used to see her at the Museum.’

[090-126]The subject was dropped.

[090-127]An hour and a half later, when the last stranger had taken his leave,

[090-128]Jasper examined two or three letters which had arrived since dinner-time

[090-129]and were lying on the hall table. With one of them open in his hand, he

[090-130]suddenly sprang up the stairs and leaped, rather than stepped, into the

[090-131]drawing-room. Amy was reading an evening paper.

[090-132]‘Look at this!’ he cried, holding the letter to her.

[090-133]It was a communication from the [[publishing-trade|publishers]] who owned The Current; they

[090-134]stated that the editorship of that review would shortly be resigned

[090-135]by Mr Fadge, and they inquired whether Milvain would feel disposed to

[090-136]assume the vacant chair.

[090-137]Amy sprang up and threw her arms about her husband’s neck, uttering a

[090-138]cry of delight.

[090-139]‘So soon! Oh, this is great! this is glorious!’

[090-140]‘Do you think this would have been offered to me but for the spacious

[090-141]life we have led of late? Never! Was I right in my calculations, Amy?’

[090-142]‘Did I ever doubt it?’

[090-143]He returned her embrace ardently, and gazed into her eyes with profound

[090-144]tenderness.

[090-145]‘Doesn’t the future brighten?’

[090-146]‘It has been very bright to me, Jasper, since I became your wife.’

[090-147]‘And I owe my fortune to you, dear girl. Now the way is smooth!’

[090-148]They placed themselves on a settee, Jasper with an arm about his wife’s

[090-149]waist, as if they were newly plighted lovers. When they had talked for a

[090-150]long time, Milvain said in a changed tone:

[090-151]‘I am told that your uncle is dead.’

[090-152]He mentioned how the news had reached him.

[090-153]‘I must make inquiries to-morrow. I suppose there will be a notice in

[090-154][[the-study|The Study]] and some of the other papers. I hope somebody will make it an

[090-155]opportunity to have a hit at that ruffian Fadge. By-the-by, it doesn’t

[090-156]much matter now how you speak of Fadge; but I was a trifle anxious when

[090-157]I heard your story at dinner.’

[090-158]‘Oh, you can afford to be more independent.--What are you thinking

[090-159]about?’

[090-160]‘Nothing.’

[090-161]‘Why do you look sad?--Yes, I know, I know. I’ll try to forgive you.’

[090-162]‘I can’t help thinking at times of the poor girl, Amy. Life will be

[090-163]easier for her now, with only her mother to support. Someone spoke of

[090-164]her this evening, and repeated Fadge’s lie that she used to do all her

[090-165]father’s writing.’

[090-166]‘She was capable of doing it. I must seem to you rather a poor-brained

[090-167]woman in comparison. Isn’t it true?’

[090-168]‘My dearest, you are a perfect woman, and poor [[marian-yule|Marian]] was only a clever

[090-169]school-girl. Do you know, I never could help imagining that she had

[090-170]ink-stains on her fingers. Heaven forbid that I should say it unkindly!

[090-171]It was touching to me at the time, for I knew how fearfully hard she

[090-172]worked.’

[090-173]‘She nearly ruined your life; remember that.’

[090-174]Jasper was silent.

[090-175]‘You will never confess it, and that is a fault in you.’

[090-176]‘She loved me, Amy.’

[090-177]‘Perhaps! as a school-girl loves. But you never loved her.’

[090-178]‘No.’

[090-179]Amy examined his face as he spoke.

[090-180]‘Her image is very faint before me,’ Jasper pursued, ‘and soon I shall

[090-181]scarcely be able to recall it. Yes, you are right; she nearly ruined

[090-182]me. And in more senses than one. Poverty and struggle, under such

[090-183]circumstances, would have made me a detestable creature. As it is, I am

[090-184]not such a bad fellow, Amy.’

[090-185]She laughed, and caressed his cheek.

[090-186]‘No, I am far from a bad fellow. I feel kindly to everyone who deserves

[090-187]it. I like to be generous, in word and deed. Trust me, there’s many

[090-188]a man who would like to be generous, but is made despicably mean by

[090-189]necessity. What a true sentence that is of Landor’s: “It has been

[090-190]repeated often enough that vice leads to misery; will no man declare

[090-191]that misery leads to vice?” I have much of the weakness that might

[090-192]become viciousness, but I am now far from the possibility of being

[090-193]vicious. Of course there are men, like Fadge, who seem only to grow

[090-194]meaner the more prosperous they are; but these are exceptions. Happiness

[090-195]is the nurse of virtue.’

[090-196]‘And independence the root of happiness.’

[090-197]‘True. “The glorious privilege of being independent”--yes, Burns

[090-198]understood the matter. Go to the piano, dear, and play me something.

[090-199]If I don’t mind, I shall fall into Whelpdale’s vein, and talk about my

[090-200]“blessedness”. Ha! isn’t the world a glorious place?’
