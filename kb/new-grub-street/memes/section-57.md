# Section 57

[056-001]‘Narrow-minded?’ He clutched at the word. ‘You admit they are that?’

[056-002]‘I feel very sure that Mr [[fadge|Fadge]] is.’

[056-003]‘Then you are not on his side against me?’

[056-004]‘How could you suppose such a thing?’

[056-005]‘Well, well; we won’t talk of that. Perhaps it isn’t a great matter.

[056-006]No--from a philosophical point of view, such things are unspeakably

[056-007]petty. But I am not much of a philosopher.’ He laughed, with a break in

[056-008]his voice. ‘Defeat in life is defeat, after all; and unmerited failure

[056-009]is a bitter curse. You see, I am not too old to do something yet. My

[056-010]sight is failing, but I can take care of it. If I had my own review, I

[056-011]would write every now and then a critical paper in my very best style.

[056-012]You remember poor old [[hinks|Hinks]]’s note about me in his book? We laughed at

[056-013]it, but he wasn’t so far wrong. I have many of those qualities. A man is

[056-014]conscious of his own merits as well as of his defects. I have done a few

[056-015]admirable things. You remember my paper on Lord Herbert of Cherbury? No

[056-016]one ever wrote a more subtle piece of [[reviewing-criticism|criticism]]; but it was swept aside

[056-017]among the rubbish of the magazines. And it’s just because of my pungent

[056-018]phrases that I have excited so much enmity. Wait! Wait! Let me have my

[056-019]own review, and leisure, and satisfaction of mind--heavens! what I will

[056-020]write! How I will scarify!’

[056-021]‘That is unworthy of you. How much better to ignore your enemies!

[056-022]In such a position, I should carefully avoid every word that betrayed

[056-023]personal feeling.’

[056-024]‘Well, well; you are of course right, my good girl. And I believe I

[056-025]should do injustice to myself if I made you think that those ignoble

[056-026]motives are the strongest in me. No; it isn’t so. From my boyhood I

[056-027]have had a passionate desire of literary fame, deep down below all the

[056-028]surface faults of my character. The best of my life has gone by, and it

[056-029]drives me to despair when I feel that I have not gained the position due

[056-030]to me. There is only one way of doing this now, and that is by becoming

[056-031]the editor of an important periodical. Only in that way shall I succeed

[056-032]in forcing people to pay attention to my claims. Many a man goes to

[056-033]his grave unrecognised, just because he has never had a fair judgment.

[056-034]Nowadays it is the unscrupulous men of business who hold the attention

[056-035]of the public; they blow their trumpets so loudly that the voices of

[056-036]honest men have no chance of being heard.’

[056-037][[marian-yule|Marian]] was pained by the humility of his pleading with her--for what was

[056-038]all this but an endeavour to move her sympathies?--and by the necessity

[056-039]she was under of seeming to turn a deaf ear. She believed that there

[056-040]was some truth in his estimate of his own powers; though as an editor

[056-041]he would almost certainly fail, as a man of letters he had probably

[056-042]done far better work than some who had passed him by on their way to

[056-043]popularity. Circumstances might enable her to assist him, though not in

[056-044]the way he proposed. The worst of it was that she could not let him see

[056-045]what was in her mind. He must think that she was simply balancing

[056-046]her own satisfaction against his, when in truth she suffered from the

[056-047]conviction that to yield would be as unwise in regard to her father’s

[056-048]future as it would be perilous to her own prospect of happiness.

[056-049]‘Shall we leave this to be talked of when the [[money-in-literature|money]] has been paid over

[056-050]to me?’ she said, after a silence.

[056-051]‘Yes. Don’t suppose I wish to influence you by dwelling on my

[056-052]own hardships. That would be contemptible. I have only taken this

[056-053]opportunity of making myself better known to you. I don’t readily talk

[056-054]of myself and in general my real feelings are hidden by the faults of

[056-055]my temper. In suggesting how you could do me a great service, and at the

[056-056]same time reap advantage for yourself I couldn’t but remember how little

[056-057]reason you have to think kindly of me. But we will postpone further

[056-058]talk. You will think over what I have said?’

[056-059]Marian promised that she would, and was glad to bring the conversation

[056-060]to an end.

[056-061]When Sunday came, [[alfred-yule|Yule]] inquired of his daughter if she had any

[056-062]engagement for the afternoon.

[056-063]‘Yes, I have,’ she replied, with an effort to disguise her

[056-064]embarrassment.

[056-065]‘I’m sorry. I thought of asking you to come with me to [[quarmby|Quarmby]]’s. Shall

[056-066]you be away through the evening?’

[056-067]‘Till about nine o’clock, I think.’

[056-068]‘Ah! Never mind, never mind.’

[056-069]He tried to dismiss the matter as if it were of no moment, but Marian

[056-070]saw the shadow that passed over his countenance. This was just after

[056-071]breakfast. For the remainder of the morning she did not meet him, and at

[056-072]the mid-day dinner he was silent, though he brought no book to the table

[056-073]with him, as he was wont to do when in his dark moods. Marian

[056-074]talked with her mother, doing her best to preserve the appearance of

[056-075]cheerfulness which was natural since the change in Yule’s demeanour.

[056-076]She chanced to meet her father in the passage just as she was going

[056-077]out. He smiled (it was more like a grin of pain) and nodded, but said

[056-078]nothing.

[056-079]When the front door closed, he went into the parlour. Mrs Yule was

[056-080]reading, or, at all events, turning over a volume of an illustrated

[056-081]magazine.

[056-082]‘Where do you suppose she has gone?’ he asked, in a voice which was only

[056-083]distant, not offensive.

[056-084]‘To the Miss Milvains, I believe,’ Mrs Yule answered, looking aside.

[056-085]‘Did she tell you so?’

[056-086]‘No. We don’t talk about it.’

[056-087]He seated himself on the corner of a chair and bent forward, his chin in

[056-088]his hand.

[056-089]‘Has she said anything to you about the review?’

[056-090]‘Not a word.’

[056-091]She glanced at him timidly, and turned a few pages of her book.

[056-092]‘I wanted her to come to Quarmby’s, because there’ll be a man there who

[056-093]is anxious that [[jedwood|Jedwood]] should start a magazine, and it would be useful

[056-094]for her to hear practical opinions. There’d be no harm if you just spoke

[056-095]to her about it now and then. Of course if she has made up her mind

[056-096]to refuse me it’s no use troubling myself any more. I should think you

[056-097]might find out what’s really going on.’

[056-098]Only dire stress of circumstances could have brought Alfred Yule to make

[056-099]distinct appeal for his wife’s help. There was no underhand plotting

[056-100]between them to influence their daughter; Mrs Yule had as much desire

[056-101]for the happiness of her husband as for that of Marian, but she felt

[056-102]powerless to effect anything on either side.

[056-103]‘If ever she says anything, I’ll let you know.’

[056-104]‘But it seems to me that you have a right to question her.’

[056-105]‘I can’t do that, Alfred.’

[056-106]‘Unfortunately, there are a good many things you can’t do.’ With that

[056-107]remark, familiar to his wife in substance, though the tone of it was

[056-108]less caustic than usual, he rose and sauntered from the room. He spent

[056-109]a gloomy hour in the study, then went off to join the literary circle at

[056-110]Mr Quarmby’s.

[056-111]CHAPTER XXIV. JASPER’S MAGNANIMITY

[056-112]Occasionally [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] met his sisters as they came out of church on

[056-113]Sunday morning, and walked home to have dinner with them. He did so

[056-114]to-day, though the sky was cheerless and a strong north-west wind made

[056-115]it anything but agreeable to wait about in open spaces.

[056-116]‘Are you going to Mrs Wright’s this afternoon?’ he asked, as they went

[056-117]on together.

[056-118]‘I thought of going,’ replied Maud. ‘Marian will be with [[dora-milvain|Dora]].’

[056-119]‘You ought both to go. You mustn’t neglect that woman.’

[056-120]He said nothing more just then, but when presently he was alone with

[056-121]Dora in the sitting-room for a few minutes, he turned with a peculiar

[056-122]smile and remarked quietly:

[056-123]‘I think you had better go with Maud this afternoon.’

[056-124]‘But I can’t. I expect Marian at three.’

[056-125]‘That’s just why I want you to go.’

[056-126]She looked her surprise.

[056-127]‘I want to have a talk with Marian. We’ll manage it in this way. At a

[056-128]quarter to three you two shall start, and as you go out you can tell the

[056-129]landlady that if Miss Yule comes she is to wait for you, as you won’t be

[056-130]long. She’ll come upstairs, and I shall be there. You see?’

[056-131]Dora turned half away, disturbed a little, but not displeased.

[056-132]‘And what about Miss Rupert?’ she asked.

[056-133]‘Oh, Miss Rupert may go to Jericho for all I care. I’m in a magnanimous

[056-134]mood.’

[056-135]‘Very, I’ve no doubt.’

[056-136]‘Well, you’ll do this? One of the results of [[literary-poverty|poverty]], you see; one can’t

[056-137]even have a private conversation with a friend without plotting to get

[056-138]the use of a room. But there shall be an end of this state of things.’

[056-139]He nodded significantly. Thereupon Dora left the room to speak with her

[056-140]sister.

[056-141]The device was put into execution, and Jasper saw his sisters depart

[056-142]knowing that they were not likely to return for some three hours. He

[056-143]seated himself comfortably by the fire and mused. Five minutes had

[056-144]hardly gone by when he looked at his watch, thinking Marian must be

[056-145]unpunctual. He was nervous, though he had believed himself secure

[056-146]against such weakness. His presence here with the purpose he had in his

[056-147]mind seemed to him distinctly a concession to impulses he ought to have

[056-148]controlled; but to this resolve he had come, and it was now too late to

[056-149]recommence the arguments with himself. Too late? Well, not strictly so;

[056-150]he had committed himself to nothing; up to the last moment of freedom he

[056-151]could always--

[056-152]That was doubtless Marian’s knock at the front door. He jumped up,

[056-153]walked the length of the room, sat down on another chair, returned to

[056-154]his former seat. Then the door opened and Marian came in.

[056-155]She was not surprised; the landlady had mentioned to her that Mr Milvain

[056-156]was upstairs, waiting the return of his sisters.

[056-157]‘I am to make Dora’s excuses,’ Jasper said. ‘She begged you would

[056-158]forgive her--that you would wait.’

[056-159]‘Oh yes.’

[056-160]‘And you were to be sure to take off your hat,’ he added in a laughing

[056-161]tone; ‘and to let me put your umbrella in the corner--like that.’

[056-162]He had always admired the shape of Marian’s head, and the beauty of her

[056-163]short, soft, curly hair. As he watched her uncovering it, he was pleased

[056-164]with the grace of her arms and the pliancy of her slight figure.

[056-165]‘Which is usually your chair?’

[056-166]‘I’m sure I don’t know.’

[056-167]‘When one goes to see a friend frequently, one gets into regular

[056-168]habits in these matters. In [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]’s garret I used to have the most

[056-169]uncomfortable chair it was ever my lot to sit upon; still, I came to

[056-170]feel an affection for it. At [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s I always had what was supposed to

[056-171]be the most luxurious seat, but it was too small for me, and I eyed it

[056-172]resentfully on sitting down and rising.’

[056-173]‘Have you any news about the Reardons?’

[056-174]‘Yes. I am told that Reardon has had the offer of a secretaryship to a

[056-175]boys’ home, or something of the kind, at Croydon. But I suppose there’ll

[056-176]be no need for him to think of that now.’

[056-177]‘Surely not!’

[056-178]‘Oh there’s no saying.’

[056-179]‘Why should he do work of that kind now?’

[056-180]‘Perhaps his wife will tell him that she wants her money all for

[056-181]herself.’

[056-182]Marian laughed. It was very rarely that Jasper had heard her laugh at

[056-183]all, and never so spontaneously as this. He liked the music.

[056-184]‘You haven’t a very good opinion of [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]],’ she said.
