# Section 12

[011-001]She spoke with such a sweet decision. Her ‘I love you!’ was a bond with

[011-002]eternity. In the simplest as in the greatest things she saw his wish

[011-003]and acted frankly upon it. No pretty petulance, no affectation of

[011-004]silly-sweet languishing, none of the weaknesses of woman. And so

[011-005]exquisitely fresh in her twenty years of maidenhood, with bright young

[011-006]eyes that seemed to bid defiance to all the years to come.

[011-007]He went about like one dazzled with excessive light. He talked as he had

[011-008]never talked before, recklessly, exultantly, insolently--in the nobler

[011-009]sense. He made friends on every hand; he welcomed all the world to his

[011-010]bosom; he felt the benevolence of a god.

[011-011]‘I love you!’ It breathed like music at his ears when he fell asleep

[011-012]in weariness of joy; it awakened him on the morrow as with a glorious

[011-013]ringing summons to renewed life.

[011-014]Delay? Why should there be delay? [[amy-reardon|Amy]] wished nothing but to become his

[011-015]wife. Idle to think of his doing any more work until he sat down in the

[011-016]home of which she was mistress. His brain burned with visions of the

[011-017]books he would henceforth write, but his hand was incapable of anything

[011-018]but a love-letter. And what letters! [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] never published anything

[011-019]equal to those. ‘I have received your poem,’ Amy replied to one of them.

[011-020]And she was right; not a letter, but a poem he had sent her, with every

[011-021]word on fire.

[011-022]The hours of talk! It enraptured him to find how much she had read, and

[011-023]with what clearness of understanding. Latin and Greek, no. Ah! but

[011-024]she should learn them both, that there might be nothing wanting in the

[011-025]communion between his thought and hers. For he loved the old writers

[011-026]with all his heart; they had been such strength to him in his days of

[011-027]misery.

[011-028]They would go together to the charmed lands of the South. No, not now

[011-029]for their [[literary-marriage|marriage]] holiday--Amy said that would be an imprudent

[011-030]expense; but as soon as he had got a good price for a book. Will not the

[011-031][[publishing-trade|publishers]] be kind? If they knew what happiness lurked in embryo within

[011-032]their foolish cheque-books!

[011-033]He woke of a sudden in the early hours of one morning, a week before the

[011-034]wedding-day. You know that kind of awaking, so complete in an instant,

[011-035]caused by the pressure of some troublesome thought upon the dreaming

[011-036]brain. ‘Suppose I should not succeed henceforth? Suppose I could never

[011-037]get more than this poor hundred pounds for one of the long books which

[011-038]cost me so much labour? I shall perhaps have children to support; and

[011-039]Amy--how would Amy bear [[literary-poverty|poverty]]?’

[011-040]He knew what poverty means. The chilling of brain and heart, the

[011-041]unnerving of the hands, the slow gathering about one of fear and shame

[011-042]and impotent wrath, the dread feeling of helplessness, of the world’s

[011-043]base indifference. Poverty! Poverty!

[011-044]And for hours he could not sleep. His eyes kept filling with tears, the

[011-045]beating of his heart was low; and in his solitude he called upon Amy

[011-046]with pitiful entreaty: ‘Do not forsake me! I love you! I love you!’

[011-047]But that went by. Six days, five days, four days--will one’s heart burst

[011-048]with happiness? The flat is taken, is furnished, up there towards the

[011-049]sky, eight flights of stone steps.

[011-050]‘You’re a confoundedly lucky fellow, Reardon,’ remarked [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]], who had

[011-051]already become very intimate with his new friend. ‘A good fellow, too,

[011-052]and you deserve it.’

[011-053]‘But at first I had a horrible suspicion.’

[011-054]‘I guess what you mean. No; I wasn’t even in love with her, though I

[011-055]admired her. She would never have cared for me in any case; I am not

[011-056]sentimental enough.’

[011-057]‘The deuce!’

[011-058]‘I mean it in an inoffensive sense. She and I are rather too much alike,

[011-059]I fancy.’

[011-060]‘How do you mean?’ asked Reardon, puzzled, and not very well pleased.

[011-061]‘There’s a great deal of pure intellect about [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]], you know. She

[011-062]was sure to choose a man of the passionate kind.’

[011-063]‘I think you are talking nonsense, my dear fellow.’

[011-064]‘Well, perhaps I am. To tell you the truth, I have by no means completed

[011-065]my study of women yet. It is one of the things in which I hope to be a

[011-066]specialist some day, though I don’t think I shall ever make use of it in

[011-067]novels--rather, perhaps, in life.’

[011-068]Three days--two days--one day.

[011-069]Now let every joyous sound which the great globe can utter ring forth

[011-070]in one burst of harmony! Is it not well done to make the village-bells

[011-071]chant merrily when a marriage is over? Here in [[london|London]] we can have no

[011-072]such music; but for us, my dear one, all the roaring life of the great

[011-073]city is wedding-hymn. Sweet, pure face under its bridal-veil! The face

[011-074]which shall, if fate spare it, be as dear to me many a long year hence

[011-075]as now at the culminating moment of my life!

[011-076]As he trudged on in the dark, his tortured memory was living through

[011-077]that time again. The images forced themselves upon him, however much he

[011-078]tried to think of quite other things--of some fictitious story on which

[011-079]he might set to work. In the case of his earlier books he had waited

[011-080]quietly until some suggestive ‘situation,’ some group of congenial

[011-081]characters, came with sudden delightfulness before his mind and urged

[011-082]him to write; but nothing so spontaneous could now be hoped for. His

[011-083]brain was too weary with months of fruitless, harassing endeavour;

[011-084]moreover, he was trying to devise a ‘plot,’ the kind of literary

[011-085]Jack-in-the-box which might excite interest in the mass of readers, and

[011-086]this was alien to the natural working of his imagination. He suffered

[011-087]the torments of nightmare--an oppression of the brain and heart which

[011-088]must soon be intolerable.

[011-089]CHAPTER VI. THE PRACTICAL FRIEND

[011-090]When her husband had set forth, Amy seated herself in the study and took

[011-091]up a new library volume as if to read. But she had no real intention of

[011-092]doing so; it was always disagreeable to her to sit in the manner of one

[011-093]totally unoccupied, with hands on lap, and even when she consciously

[011-094]gave herself up to musing an open book was generally before her. She did

[011-095]not, in truth, read much nowadays; since the birth of her child she had

[011-096]seemed to care less than before for disinterested study. If a new

[011-097]novel that had succeeded came into her hands she perused it in a very

[011-098]practical spirit, commenting to Reardon on the features of the work

[011-099]which had made it popular; formerly, she would have thought much more of

[011-100]its purely literary merits, for which her eye was very keen. How often

[011-101]she had given her husband a thrill of exquisite pleasure by pointing

[011-102]to some merit or defect of which the common reader would be totally

[011-103]insensible! Now she spoke less frequently on such subjects. Her

[011-104]interests were becoming more personal; she liked to hear details of the

[011-105][[literary-ambition|success]] of popular authors--about their wives or husbands, as the case

[011-106]might be, their arrangements with publishers, their methods of work.

[011-107]The gossip columns of literary papers--and of some that were not

[011-108]literary--had an attraction for her. She talked of questions such

[011-109]as international [[money-in-literature|copyright]], was anxious to get an insight into the

[011-110]practical conduct of journals and magazines, liked to know who ‘read’

[011-111]for the publishing-houses. To an impartial observer it might have

[011-112]appeared that her intellect was growing more active and mature.

[011-113]More than half an hour passed. It was not a pleasant train of thought

[011-114]that now occupied her. Her lips were drawn together, her brows were

[011-115]slightly wrinkled; the self-control which at other times was agreeably

[011-116]expressed upon her features had become rather too cold and decided. At

[011-117]one moment it seemed to her that she heard a sound in the bedroom--the

[011-118]doors were purposely left ajar--and her head turned quickly to listen,

[011-119]the look in her eyes instantaneously softening; but all remained quiet.

[011-120]The street would have been silent but for a cab that now and then

[011-121]passed--the swing of a hansom or the roll of a four-wheeler--and within

[011-122]the buildings nothing whatever was audible.

[011-123]Yes, a footstep, briskly mounting the stone stairs. Not like that of the

[011-124]postman. A visitor, perhaps, to the other flat on the topmost landing.

[011-125]But the final pause was in this direction, and then came a sharp rat-tat

[011-126]at the door. Amy rose immediately and went to open.

[011-127]Jasper Milvain raised his urban silk hat, then held out his hand with

[011-128]the greeting of frank friendship. His inquiries were in so loud a voice

[011-129]that Amy checked him with a forbidding gesture.

[011-130]‘You’ll wake Willie!’

[011-131]‘By Jove! I always forget,’ he exclaimed in subdued tones. ‘Does the

[011-132]infant flourish?’

[011-133]‘Oh, yes!’

[011-134]‘Reardon out? I got back on Saturday evening, but couldn’t come round

[011-135]before this.’ It was Monday. ‘How close it is in here! I suppose the

[011-136]roof gets so heated during the day. Glorious weather in the country! And

[011-137]I’ve no end of things to tell you. He won’t be long, I suppose?’

[011-138]‘I think not.’

[011-139]He left his hat and stick in the passage, came into the study, and

[011-140]glanced about as if he expected to see some change since he was last

[011-141]here, three weeks ago.

[011-142]‘So you have been enjoying yourself?’ said Amy as, after listening for a

[011-143]moment at the door, she took a seat.

[011-144]‘Oh, a little freshening of the faculties. But whose acquaintance do you

[011-145]think I have made?’

[011-146]‘Down there?’

[011-147]‘Yes. Your uncle [[alfred-yule|Alfred]] and his daughter were staying at [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s,

[011-148]and I saw something of them. I was invited to the house.’

[011-149]‘Did you speak of us?’

[011-150]‘To Miss Yule only. I happened to meet her on a walk, and in a

[011-151]blundering way I mentioned Reardon’s name. But of course it didn’t

[011-152]matter in the least. She inquired about you with a good deal of

[011-153]interest--asked if you were as beautiful as you promised to be years

[011-154]ago.’

[011-155]Amy laughed.

[011-156]‘Doesn’t that proceed from your fertile invention, Mr Milvain?’

[011-157]‘Not a bit of it! By-the-bye, what would be your natural question

[011-158]concerning her? Do you think she gave promise of good looks?’

[011-159]‘I’m afraid I can’t say that she did. She had a good face, but--rather

[011-160]plain.’

[011-161]‘I see.’ Jasper threw back his head and seemed to contemplate an object

[011-162]in memory. ‘Well, I shouldn’t wonder if most people called her a trifle

[011-163]plain even now; and yet--no, that’s hardly possible, after all. She has

[011-164]no colour. Wears her hair short.’

[011-165]‘Short?’

[011-166]‘Oh, I don’t mean the smooth, boyish hair with a parting--not the

[011-167]kind of hair that would be lank if it grew long. Curly all over. Looks

[011-168]uncommonly well, I assure you. She has a capital head. Odd girl; very

[011-169]odd girl! Quiet, thoughtful--not very happy, I’m afraid. Seems to think

[011-170]with dread of a return to books.’

[011-171]‘Indeed! But I had understood that she was a reader.’

[011-172]‘Reading enough for six people, probably. Perhaps her health is not

[011-173]very robust. Oh, I knew her by sight quite well--had seen her at the

[011-174]Reading-room. She’s the kind of girl that gets into one’s head, you

[011-175]know--suggestive; much more in her than comes out until one knows her

[011-176]very well.’

[011-177]‘Well, I should hope so,’ remarked Amy, with a peculiar smile.

[011-178]‘But that’s by no means a matter of course. They didn’t invite me to

[011-179]come and see them in London.’

[011-180]‘I suppose Marian mentioned your acquaintance with this branch of the

[011-181]family?’

[011-182]‘I think not. At all events, she promised me she wouldn’t.’

[011-183]Amy looked at him inquiringly, in a puzzled way.

[011-184]‘She promised you?’

[011-185]‘Voluntarily. We got rather sympathetic. Your uncle--Alfred, I mean--is

[011-186]a remarkable man; but I think he regarded me as a youth of no particular

[011-187]importance. Well, how do things go?’
