# Section 76

[075-001][[marian-yule|Marian]] withdrew. She went to the sitting-room, where an ochreous

[075-002]daylight was beginning to diffuse itself and to render the lamp

[075-003]superfluous. With the dissipation of the fog rain had set in; its

[075-004]splashing upon the muddy pavement was audible.

[075-005]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]], still with a duster in her hand, sat on the sofa. Marian took

[075-006]a place beside her. They talked in low, broken tones, and wept together

[075-007]over their miseries.

[075-008]CHAPTER XXXI. A RESCUE AND A SUMMONS

[075-009]The chances are that you have neither understanding nor sympathy for men

[075-010]such as [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]] and [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]]. They merely provoke you.

[075-011]They seem to you inert, flabby, weakly envious, foolishly obstinate,

[075-012]impiously mutinous, and many other things. You are made angrily

[075-013]contemptuous by their failure to get on; why don’t they bestir

[075-014]themselves, push and bustle, welcome kicks so long as halfpence follow,

[075-015]make place in the world’s eye--in short, take a leaf from the book of Mr

[075-016][[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]?

[075-017]But try to imagine a personality wholly unfitted for the rough and

[075-018]tumble of the world’s labour-market. From the familiar point of view

[075-019]these men were worthless; view them in possible relation to a humane

[075-020]order of Society, and they are admirable citizens. Nothing is easier

[075-021]than to condemn a type of character which is unequal to the coarse

[075-022]demands of life as it suits the average man. These two were richly

[075-023]endowed with the kindly and the imaginative virtues; if fate threw them

[075-024]amid incongruous circumstances, is their endowment of less value? You

[075-025]scorn their passivity; but it was their nature and their merit to be

[075-026]passive.

[075-027]Gifted with independent means, each of them would have taken quite

[075-028]a different aspect in your eyes. The sum of their faults was their

[075-029]inability to earn [[money-in-literature|money]]; but, indeed, that inability does not call for

[075-030]unmingled disdain.

[075-031]It was very weak of Harold Biffen to come so near perishing of hunger as

[075-032]he did in the days when he was completing his novel. But he would have

[075-033]vastly preferred to eat and be satisfied had any method of obtaining

[075-034]food presented itself to him. He did not starve for the pleasure of the

[075-035]thing, I assure you. Pupils were difficult to get just now, and writing

[075-036]that he had sent to magazines had returned upon his hands. He pawned

[075-037]such of his possessions as he could spare, and he reduced his meals to

[075-038]the minimum. Nor was he uncheerful in his cold garret and with his empty

[075-039]stomach, for ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer,’ drew steadily to an end.

[075-040]He worked very slowly. The book would make perhaps two volumes of

[075-041]ordinary novel size, but he had laboured over it for many months,

[075-042]patiently, affectionately, scrupulously. Each sentence was as good as

[075-043]he could make it, harmonious to the ear, with words of precious meaning

[075-044]skilfully set. Before sitting down to a chapter he planned it minutely

[075-045]in his mind; then he wrote a rough draft of it; then he elaborated the

[075-046]thing phrase by phrase. He had no thought of whether such toil would be

[075-047]recompensed in coin of the realm; nay, it was his conviction that, if

[075-048]with difficulty published, it could scarcely bring him money. The work

[075-049]must be significant, that was all he cared for. And he had no society of

[075-050]admiring friends to encourage him. Reardon understood the merit of the

[075-051]workmanship, but frankly owned that the book was repulsive to him.

[075-052]To the public it would be worse than repulsive--tedious, utterly

[075-053]uninteresting. No matter; it drew to its end.

[075-054]The day of its completion was made memorable by an event decidedly more

[075-055]exciting, even to the author.

[075-056]At eight o’clock in the evening there remained half a page to be

[075-057]written. Biffen had already worked about nine hours, and on breaking

[075-058]off to appease his hunger he doubted whether to finish to-night or to

[075-059]postpone the last lines till tomorrow. The discovery that only a small

[075-060]crust of bread lay in the cupboard decided him to write no more; he

[075-061]would have to go out to purchase a loaf and that was disturbance.

[075-062]But stay; had he enough money? He searched his pockets. Two pence and

[075-063]two farthings; no more.

[075-064]You are probably not aware that at bakers’ shops in the poor quarters

[075-065]the price of the half-quartern loaf varies sometimes from week to week.

[075-066]At present, as Biffen knew, it was twopence three-farthings, a

[075-067]common figure. But Harold did not possess three farthings, only two.

[075-068]Reflecting, he remembered to have passed yesterday a shop where the

[075-069]bread was marked twopence halfpenny; it was a shop in a very obscure

[075-070]little street off Hampstead Road, some distance from Clipstone Street.

[075-071]Thither he must repair. He had only his hat and a muffler to put on, for

[075-072]again he was wearing his overcoat in default of the under one, and his

[075-073]ragged umbrella to take from the corner; so he went forth.

[075-074]To his delight the twopence halfpenny announcement was still in the

[075-075]baker’s window. He obtained a loaf wrapped it in the piece of paper he

[075-076]had brought--small bakers decline to supply paper for this purpose--and

[075-077]strode joyously homeward again.

[075-078]Having eaten, he looked longingly at his manuscript. But half a page

[075-079]more. Should he not finish it to-night? The temptation was irresistible.

[075-080]He sat down, wrought with unusual speed, and at half-past ten wrote with

[075-081]magnificent flourish ‘The End.’

[075-082]His fire was out and he had neither coals nor wood. But his feet were

[075-083]frozen into lifelessness. Impossible to go to bed like this; he must

[075-084]take another turn in the streets. It would suit his humour to ramble a

[075-085]while. Had it not been so late he would have gone to see Reardon, who

[075-086]expected the communication of this glorious news.

[075-087]So again he locked his door. Half-way downstairs he stumbled over

[075-088]something or somebody in the dark.

[075-089]‘Who is that?’ he cried.

[075-090]The answer was a loud snore. Biffen went to the bottom of the house and

[075-091]called to the landlady.

[075-092]‘Mrs Willoughby! Who is asleep on the stairs?’

[075-093]‘Why, I ‘spect it’s Mr Briggs,’ replied the woman, indulgently. ‘Don’t

[075-094]you mind him, Mr Biffen. There’s no ‘arm: he’s only had a little too

[075-095]much. I’ll go up an’ make him go to bed as soon as I’ve got my ‘ands

[075-096]clean.’

[075-097]‘The necessity for waiting till then isn’t obvious,’ remarked the

[075-098]realist with a chuckle, and went his way.

[075-099]He walked at a sharp pace for more than an hour, and about midnight drew

[075-100]near to his own quarter again. He had just turned up by the Middlesex

[075-101]Hospital, and was at no great distance from Clipstone Street, when a

[075-102]yell and scamper caught his attention; a group of loafing blackguards on

[075-103]the opposite side of the way had suddenly broken up, and as they rushed

[075-104]off he heard the word ‘Fire!’ This was too common an occurrence to

[075-105]disturb his equanimity; he wondered absently in which street the fire

[075-106]might be, but trudged on without a thought of making investigation.

[075-107]Repeated yells and rushes, however, assailed his apathy. Two women came

[075-108]tearing by him, and he shouted to them: ‘Where is it?’

[075-109]‘In Clipstone Street, they say,’ one screamed back.

[075-110]He could no longer be unconcerned. If in his own street the

[075-111]conflagration might be in the very house he inhabited, and in that

[075-112]case---- He set off at a run. Ahead of him was a thickening throng, its

[075-113]position indicating the entrance to Clipstone Street. Soon he found his

[075-114]progress retarded; he had to dodge this way and that, to force progress,

[075-115]to guard himself against overthrows by the torrent of ruffiandom which

[075-116]always breaks forth at the cry of fire. He could now smell the smoke,

[075-117]and all at once a black volume of it, bursting from upper windows,

[075-118]alarmed his sight. At once he was aware that, if not his own dwelling,

[075-119]it must be one of those on either side that was in flames. As yet no

[075-120]engine had arrived, and straggling policemen were only just beginning to

[075-121]make their way to the scene of uproar. By dint of violent effort Biffen

[075-122]moved forward yard by yard. A tongue of flame which suddenly illumined

[075-123]the fronts of the houses put an end to his doubt.

[075-124]‘Let me get past!’ he shouted to the gaping and swaying mass of people

[075-125]in front of him. ‘I live there! I must go upstairs to save something!’

[075-126]His educated accent moved attention. Repeating the demand again and

[075-127]again he succeeded in getting forward, and at length was near enough

[075-128]to see that people were dragging articles of furniture out on to the

[075-129]pavement.

[075-130]‘That you, Mr Biffen?’ cried someone to him.

[075-131]He recognised the face of a fellow-lodger.

[075-132]‘Is it possible to get up to my room?’ broke frantically from his lips.

[075-133]‘You’ll never get up there. It’s that--Briggs’--the epithet was

[075-134]alliterative--‘’as upset his lamp, and I ‘ope he’ll--well get roasted to

[075-135]death.’

[075-136]Biffen leaped on to the threshold, and crashed against Mrs Willoughby,

[075-137]the landlady, who was carrying a huge bundle of household linen.

[075-138]‘I told you to look after that drunken brute;’ he said to her. ‘Can I

[075-139]get upstairs?’

[075-140]‘What do I care whether you can or not!’ the woman shrieked. ‘My God!

[075-141]And all them new chairs as I bought--!’

[075-142]He heard no more, but bounded over a confusion of obstacles, and in a

[075-143]moment was on the landing of the first storey. Here he encountered a

[075-144]man who had not lost his head, a stalwart mechanic engaged in slipping

[075-145]clothes on to two little children.

[075-146]‘If somebody don’t drag that fellow Briggs down he’ll be dead,’ observed

[075-147]the man. ‘He’s layin’ outside his door. I pulled him out, but I can’t do

[075-148]no more for him.’

[075-149]Smoke grew thick on the staircase. Burning was as yet confined to that

[075-150]front room on the second floor tenanted by Briggs the disastrous, but

[075-151]in all likelihood the ceiling was ablaze, and if so it would be all but

[075-152]impossible for Biffen to gain his own chamber, which was at the back on

[075-153]the floor above. No one was making an attempt to extinguish the fire;

[075-154]personal safety and the rescue of their possessions alone occupied the

[075-155]thoughts of such people as were still in the house. Desperate with the

[075-156]dread of losing his manuscript, his toil, his one hope, the realist

[075-157]scarcely stayed to listen to a warning that the fumes were impassable;

[075-158]with head bent he rushed up to the next landing. There lay Briggs,

[075-159]perchance already stifled, and through the open door Biffen had a

[075-160]horrible vision of furnace fury. To go yet higher would have been

[075-161]madness but for one encouragement: he knew that on his own storey was a

[075-162]ladder giving access to a trap-door, by which he might issue on to the

[075-163]roof, whence escape to the adjacent houses would be practicable. Again a

[075-164]leap forward!

[075-165]In fact, not two minutes elapsed from his commencing the ascent of the

[075-166]stairs to the moment when, all but fainting, he thrust the key into his

[075-167]door and fell forward into purer air. Fell, for he was on his knees, and

[075-168]had begun to suffer from a sense of failing power, a sick whirling of

[075-169]the brain, a terror of hideous death. His manuscript was on the table,

[075-170]where he had left it after regarding and handling it with joyful

[075-171]self-congratulation; though it was pitch dark in the room, he could at

[075-172]once lay his hand on the heap of paper. Now he had it; now it was jammed

[075-173]tight under his left arm; now he was out again on the landing, in smoke

[075-174]more deadly than ever.

[075-175]He said to himself: ‘If I cannot instantly break out by the trap-door

[075-176]it’s all over with me.’ That the exit would open to a vigorous thrust

[075-177]he knew, having amused himself not long ago by going on to the roof. He

[075-178]touched the ladder, sprang upwards, and felt the trap above him. But he

[075-179]could not push it back. ‘I’m a dead man,’ flashed across his mind, ‘and

[075-180]all for the sake of “Mr Bailey, Grocer.”’ A frenzied effort, the last of

[075-181]which his muscles were capable, and the door yielded. His head was now

[075-182]through the aperture, and though the smoke swept up about him, that gasp

[075-183]of cold air gave him strength to throw himself on the flat portion of

[075-184]the roof that he had reached.
