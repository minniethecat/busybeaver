# New Grub Street

*New Grub Street* is George Gissing's ninth novel, published in 1891 by Smith, Elder & Co. Set in the literary world of late Victorian London, it traces the contrasting careers of [[Jasper Milvain]], a cynical and pragmatic young writer who rises through clever networking, and [[Edwin Reardon]], a novelist of genuine artistic ambition who descends into poverty and failure. The novel anatomizes the commercialization of literature with an unflinching eye, examining how the marketplace reshapes literary production, personal relationships, and creative identity.

## The Title

The title invokes the original Grub Street — the London street that became synonymous with hack writing in the eighteenth century — and reframes it for the modern age. One character explicitly draws this lineage: "if he lived in Sam Johnson's Grub Street. But our Grub Street of today is quite a different place" (P02-195). The "new" Grub Street is not a single location but a dispersed network of periodical offices, reading rooms, and cheap lodgings where writers compete for a precarious living. Gissing maps this geography across areas like [[Tottenham Court Road]], [[Hampstead]], and the [[British Museum]] Reading Room.

## Themes

The novel's central concern is the tension between artistic integrity and commercial viability. Milvain, who treats literature as a trade, flourishes; Reardon, who cannot compromise his standards, fails. Gissing offers no simple moral — Milvain is not a villain, and Reardon is not a martyr [^1]. The novel portrays the literary marketplace as a system that rewards certain temperaments while destroying others, a dynamic embodied in the three-volume publishing format that structures the characters' economic realities.
