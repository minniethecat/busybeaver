# Reviewing and Criticism

In *New Grub Street*, the review is the quintessential Grub Street product: quickly written, paid by the column, shaped by commercial pressures rather than critical judgment, and consumed and forgotten within a week. The practice of literary criticism — which might have served as a check on the commercial degradation of literature — has instead become another arm of the marketplace.

## The Economy of Reviewing

Reviewing provides steady income for characters who cannot earn enough from their own creative work. Alfred Yule has spent decades writing reviews and critical pieces — a kind of literary piecework that keeps him alive but leaves him bitter and unfulfilled. The review is the emblematic form of journalistic writing: ephemeral, reactive, and fundamentally commercial.

The novel's account of reviewing is notably cynical. Opinion is for sale; praise and blame are distributed according to editorial policy and personal connections rather than genuine critical assessment. The literary world is depicted as a network of mutual back-scratching, where "the most malicious man in the literary world" (003-175) can destroy a reputation with a well-placed notice.

## Criticism as Gatekeeping

Reviews determine not just reputation but survival. A bad review can doom a novel to commercial failure; a good one can create a market. The power of reviewers is disproportionate to their expertise or insight, and the novel shows this power being exercised arbitrarily. Reardon's *The Optimist* fails partly because it receives no influential support — not because it is bad, but because Reardon lacks the social connections that generate favorable notices.

## The Missing Alternative

Conspicuously absent from the novel is any model of genuine critical engagement — the kind of serious, sustained literary criticism that might challenge the market's judgments. The absence is itself Gissing's argument: the conditions of periodical publication make serious criticism impossible. The reviewer must produce copy quickly, on assignment, for a market that rewards provocation and dismisses nuance.
