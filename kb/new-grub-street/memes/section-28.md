# Section 28

[027-001]Meanwhile the first number of [[the-current|The Current]] had appeared, and [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]

[027-002]Milvain had made a palpable hit. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] spoke very often of the article

[027-003]called ‘Typical Readers,’ and her interest in its author was freely

[027-004]manifested. Whenever a mention of Jasper came under her notice she read

[027-005]it out to her husband. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] smiled and appeared glad, but he did not

[027-006]care to discuss Milvain with the same frankness as formerly.

[027-007]One evening at the end of January he told Amy what he had been writing

[027-008]at the Museum, and asked her if she would care to hear it read.

[027-009]‘I began to wonder what you were doing,’ she replied.

[027-010]‘Then why didn’t you ask me?’

[027-011]‘I was rather afraid to.’

[027-012]‘Why afraid?’

[027-013]‘It would have seemed like reminding you that--you know what I mean.’

[027-014]‘That a month or two more will see us at the same crisis again. Still, I

[027-015]had rather you had shown an interest in my doings.’

[027-016]After a pause Amy asked:

[027-017]‘Do you think you can get a paper of this kind accepted?’

[027-018]‘It isn’t impossible. I think it’s rather well done. Let me read you a

[027-019]page--’

[027-020]‘Where will you send it?’ she interrupted.

[027-021]‘To [[the-wayside|The Wayside]].’

[027-022]‘Why not try The Current? Ask Milvain to introduce you to Mr [[fadge|Fadge]]. They

[027-023]pay much better, you know.’

[027-024]‘But this isn’t so well suited for Fadge. And I much prefer to be

[027-025]independent, as long as it’s possible.’

[027-026]‘That’s one of your faults, Edwin,’ remarked his wife, mildly. ‘It’s

[027-027]only the strongest men that can make their way independently. You ought

[027-028]to use every means that offers.’

[027-029]‘Seeing that I am so weak?’

[027-030]‘I didn’t think it would offend you. I only meant---’

[027-031]‘No, no; you are quite right. Certainly, I am one of the men who need

[027-032]all the help they can get. But I assure you, this thing won’t do for The

[027-033]Current.’

[027-034]‘What a pity you will go back to those musty old times! Now think of

[027-035]that article of Milvain’s. If only you could do something of that kind!

[027-036]What do people care about Diogenes and his tub and his lantern?’

[027-037]‘My dear girl, Diogenes Laertius had neither tub nor lantern, that I

[027-038]know of. You are making a mistake; but it doesn’t matter.’

[027-039]‘No, I don’t think it does.’ The caustic note was not very pleasant on

[027-040]Amy’s lips. ‘Whoever he was, the mass of readers will be frightened by

[027-041]his name.’

[027-042]‘Well, we have to recognise that the mass of readers will never care for

[027-043]anything I do.’

[027-044]‘You will never convince me that you couldn’t write in a popular way if

[027-045]you tried. I’m sure you are quite as clever as Milvain--’

[027-046]Reardon made an impatient gesture.

[027-047]‘Do leave Milvain aside for a little! He and I are as unlike as two

[027-048]men could be. What’s the use of constantly comparing us?’

[027-049]Amy looked at him. He had never spoken to her so brusquely.

[027-050]‘How can you say that I am constantly comparing you?’

[027-051]‘If not in spoken words, then in your thoughts.’

[027-052]‘That’s not a very nice thing to say, Edwin.’

[027-053]‘You make it so unmistakable, Amy. What I mean is, that you are always

[027-054]regretting the difference between him and me. You lament that I can’t

[027-055]write in that attractive way. Well, I lament it myself--for your sake. I

[027-056]wish I had Milvain’s peculiar talent, so that I could get [[literary-success|reputation]] and

[027-057][[money-in-literature|money]]. But I haven’t, and there’s an end of it. It irritates a man to be

[027-058]perpetually told of his disadvantages.’

[027-059]‘I will never mention Milvain’s name again,’ said Amy coldly.

[027-060]‘Now that’s ridiculous, and you know it.’

[027-061]‘I feel the same about your irritation. I can’t see that I have given

[027-062]any cause for it.’

[027-063]‘Then we’ll talk no more of the matter.’

[027-064]Reardon threw his manuscript aside and opened a book. Amy never asked

[027-065]him to resume his intention of reading what he had written.

[027-066]However, the paper was accepted. It came out in The Wayside for March,

[027-067]and Reardon received seven pounds ten for it. By that time he had

[027-068]written another thing of the same gossipy kind, suggested by Pliny’s

[027-069]Letters. The pleasant occupation did him good, but there was no

[027-070]possibility of pursuing this course. ‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]]’ would be published

[027-071]in April; he might get the five-and-twenty pounds contingent upon a

[027-072]certain sale, yet that could in no case be paid until the middle of the

[027-073]year, and long before then he would be penniless. His respite drew to an

[027-074]end.

[027-075]But now he took counsel of no one; as far as it was possible he lived in

[027-076]solitude, never seeing those of his acquaintances who were outside the

[027-077]literary world, and seldom even his colleagues. Milvain was so busy that

[027-078]he had only been able to look in twice or thrice since Christmas, and

[027-079]Reardon nowadays never went to Jasper’s lodgings.

[027-080]He had the conviction that all was over with the happiness of his

[027-081]married life, though how the events which were to express this ruin

[027-082]would shape themselves he could not foresee. Amy was revealing that

[027-083]aspect of her character to which he had been blind, though a practical

[027-084]man would have perceived it from the first; so far from helping him to

[027-085]support [[literary-poverty|poverty]], she perhaps would even refuse to share it with him.

[027-086]He knew that she was slowly drawing apart; already there was a divorce

[027-087]between their minds, and he tortured himself in uncertainty as to how

[027-088]far he retained her affections. A word of tenderness, a caress, no

[027-089]longer met with response from her; her softest mood was that of mere

[027-090]comradeship. All the warmth of her nature was expended upon the child;

[027-091]Reardon learnt how easy it is for a mother to forget that both parents

[027-092]have a share in her offspring.

[027-093]He was beginning to dislike the child. But for Willie’s existence Amy

[027-094]would still love him with undivided heart; not, perhaps, so passionately

[027-095]as once, but still with lover’s love. And Amy understood--or, at all

[027-096]events, remarked--this change in him. She was aware that he seldom asked

[027-097]a question about Willie, and that he listened with indifference when she

[027-098]spoke of the little fellow’s progress. In part offended, she was also in

[027-099]part pleased.

[027-100]But for the child, mere poverty, he said to himself, should never have

[027-101]sundered them. In the strength of his passion he could have overcome all

[027-102]her disappointments; and, indeed, but for that new care, he would

[027-103]most likely never have fallen to this extremity of helplessness. It is

[027-104]natural in a weak and sensitive man to dream of possibilities disturbed

[027-105]by the force of circumstance. For one hour which he gave to conflict

[027-106]with his present difficulties, Reardon spent many in contemplation of

[027-107]the happiness that might have been.

[027-108]Even yet, it needed but a little money to redeem all. Amy had no

[027-109]extravagant aspirations; a home of simple refinement and freedom from

[027-110]anxiety would restore her to her nobler self. How could he find fault

[027-111]with her? She knew nothing of such sordid life as he had gone through,

[027-112]and to lack money for necessities seemed to her degrading beyond

[027-113]endurance. Why, even the ordinary artisan’s wife does not suffer such

[027-114]privations as hers at the end of the past year. For lack of that little

[027-115]money his life must be ruined. Of late he had often thought about the

[027-116]rich uncle, [[john-yule|John Yule]], who might perhaps leave something to Amy; but the

[027-117]hope was so uncertain. And supposing such a thing were to happen; would

[027-118]it be perfectly easy to live upon his wife’s bounty--perhaps exhausting

[027-119]a small capital, so that, some years hence, their position would be

[027-120]no better than before? Not long ago, he could have taken anything from

[027-121]Amy’s hand; would it be so simple since the change that had come between

[027-122]them?

[027-123]Having written his second magazine-article (it was rejected by two

[027-124]editors, and he had no choice but to hold it over until sufficient time

[027-125]had elapsed to allow of his again trying The Wayside), he saw that he

[027-126]must perforce plan another novel. But this time he was resolute not to

[027-127]undertake three volumes. The advertisements informed him that numbers of

[027-128]authors were abandoning that procrustean system; hopeless as he was, he

[027-129]might as well try his chance with a book which could be written in a

[027-130]few weeks. And why not a glaringly artificial story with a sensational

[027-131]title? It could not be worse than what he had last written.

[027-132]So, without a word to Amy, he put aside his purely intellectual work

[027-133]and began once more the search for a ‘plot.’ This was towards the end of

[027-134]February. The proofs of ‘Margaret Home’ were coming in day by day; Amy

[027-135]had offered to correct them, but after all he preferred to keep his

[027-136]shame to himself as long as possible, and with a hurried reading he

[027-137]dismissed sheet after sheet. His imagination did not work the more

[027-138]happily for this repugnant task; still, he hit at length upon a

[027-139]conception which seemed absurd enough for the purpose before him.

[027-140]Whether he could persevere with it even to the extent of one volume was

[027-141]very doubtful. But it should not be said of him that he abandoned his

[027-142]wife and child to penury without one effort of the kind that Milvain and

[027-143]Amy herself had recommended.

[027-144]Writing a page or two of manuscript daily, and with several holocausts

[027-145]to retard him, he had done nearly a quarter of the story when there came

[027-146]a note from Jasper telling of Mrs Milvain’s death. He handed it across

[027-147]the breakfast-table to Amy, and watched her as she read it.

[027-148]‘I suppose it doesn’t alter his position,’ Amy remarked, without much

[027-149]interest.

[027-150]‘I suppose not appreciably. He told me once his mother had a sufficient

[027-151]income; but whatever she leaves will go to his sisters, I should think.

[027-152]He has never said much to me.’

[027-153]Nearly three weeks passed before they heard anything more from Jasper

[027-154]himself; then he wrote, again from the country, saying that he purposed

[027-155]bringing his sisters to live in [[london|London]]. Another week, and one evening he

[027-156]appeared at the door.

[027-157]A want of heartiness in Reardon’s reception of him might have been

[027-158]explained as gravity natural under the circumstances. But Jasper had

[027-159]before this become conscious that he was not welcomed here quite so

[027-160]cheerily as in the old days. He remarked it distinctly on that evening

[027-161]when he accompanied Amy home from Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s; since then he had allowed

[027-162]his pressing occupations to be an excuse for the paucity of his visits.

[027-163]It seemed to him perfectly intelligible that Reardon, sinking into

[027-164]literary insignificance, should grow cool to a man entering upon a

[027-165]successful career; the vein of cynicism in Jasper enabled him to pardon

[027-166]a weakness of this kind, which in some measure flattered him. But he

[027-167]both liked and respected Reardon, and at present he was in the mood to

[027-168]give expression to his warmer feelings.

[027-169]‘Your book is announced, I see,’ he said with an accent of pleasure, as

[027-170]soon as he had seated himself.

[027-171]‘I didn’t know it.’

[027-172]‘Yes. “New novel by the author of ‘[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]].’” Down for the

[027-173]sixteenth of April. And I have a proposal to make about it. Will you

[027-174]let me ask Fadge to have it noticed in “Books of the Month,” in the May

[027-175]Current?’

[027-176]‘I strongly advise you to let it take its chance. The book isn’t worth

[027-177]special notice, and whoever undertook to review it for Fadge would

[027-178]either have to lie, or stultify the magazine.’

[027-179]Jasper turned to Amy.

[027-180]‘Now what is to be done with a man like this? What is one to say to him,

[027-181]Mrs Reardon?’

[027-182]‘Edwin dislikes the book,’ Amy replied, carelessly.

[027-183]‘That has nothing to do with the matter. We know quite well that in

[027-184]anything he writes there’ll be something for a well-disposed reviewer

[027-185]to make a good deal of. If Fadge will let me, I should do the thing

[027-186]myself.’
