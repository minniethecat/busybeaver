# Section 85

[084-001]‘Mr [[hinks|Hinks]] and Mr Gorbutt inquired very kindly after you to-day,’ said

[084-002]the girl, as she seated herself.

[084-003]‘Oh, is Hinks out again?’

[084-004]‘Yes, but he looks very ill.’

[084-005]They conversed of such matters until Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]--now her own

[084-006]servant--brought in the dinner. After the meal, [[marian-yule|Marian]] was in her

[084-007]bedroom for about an hour; then she went to her father, who sat in

[084-008]idleness, smoking.

[084-009]‘What is your mother doing?’ he asked, as she entered.

[084-010]‘Some needlework.’

[084-011]‘I had perhaps better say’--he spoke rather stiffly, and with averted

[084-012]face--‘that I make no exclusive claim to the use of this room. As I

[084-013]can no longer pretend to study, it would be idle to keep up the show

[084-014]of privacy that mustn’t be disturbed. Perhaps you will mention to your

[084-015]mother that she is quite at liberty to sit here whenever she chooses.’

[084-016]It was characteristic of him that he should wish to deliver this

[084-017]permission by proxy. But Marian understood how much was implied in such

[084-018]an announcement.

[084-019]‘I will tell mother,’ she said. ‘But at this moment I wished to speak to

[084-020]you privately. How would you advise me to invest my [[money-in-literature|money]]?’

[084-021]Yule looked surprised, and answered with cold dignity.

[084-022]‘It is strange that you should put such a question to me. I should have

[084-023]supposed your interests were in the hands of--of some competent person.’

[084-024]‘This will be my private affair, father. I wish to get as high a rate of

[084-025]interest as I safely can.’

[084-026]‘I really must decline to advise, or interfere in any way. But, as you

[084-027]have introduced this subject, I may as well put a question which is

[084-028]connected with it. Could you give me any idea as to how long you are

[084-029]likely to remain with us?’

[084-030]‘At least a year,’ was the answer, ‘and very likely much longer.’

[084-031]‘Am I to understand, then, that your [[literary-marriage|marriage]] is indefinitely

[084-032]postponed?’

[084-033]‘Yes, father.’

[084-034]‘And will you tell me why?’

[084-035]‘I can only say that it has seemed better--to both of us.’

[084-036]Yule detected the sorrowful emotion she was endeavouring to suppress.

[084-037]His conception of [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]’s character made it easy for him to form a

[084-038]just surmise as to the reasons for this postponement; he was gratified

[084-039]to think that Marian might learn how rightly he had judged her wooer,

[084-040]and an involuntary pity for the girl did not prevent his hoping that

[084-041]the detestable alliance was doomed. With difficulty he refrained from

[084-042]smiling.

[084-043]‘I will make no comment on that,’ he remarked, with a certain emphasis.

[084-044]‘But do you imply that this investment of which you speak is to be

[084-045]solely for your own advantage?’

[084-046]‘For mine, and for yours and mother’s.’

[084-047]There was a silence of a minute or two. As yet it had not been necessary

[084-048]to take any steps for raising money, but a few months more would see the

[084-049]family without resources, save those provided by Marian, who, without

[084-050]discussion, had been simply setting aside what she received for her

[084-051]work.

[084-052]‘You must be well aware,’ said Yule at length, ‘that I cannot consent to

[084-053]benefit by any such offer. When it is necessary, I shall borrow on the

[084-054]security of--’

[084-055]‘Why should you do that, father?’ Marian interrupted. ‘My money is

[084-056]yours. If you refuse it as a gift, then why may not I lend to you

[084-057]as well as a stranger? Repay me when your eyes are restored. For the

[084-058]present, all our anxieties are at an end. We can live very well until

[084-059]you are able to write again.’

[084-060]For his sake she put it in his way. Supposing him never able to earn

[084-061]anything, then indeed would come a time of hardship; but she could

[084-062]not contemplate that. The worst would only befall them in case she was

[084-063]forsaken by Jasper, and if that happened all else would be of little

[084-064]account.

[084-065]‘This has come upon me as a surprise,’ said Yule, in his most reserved

[084-066]tone. ‘I can give no definite reply; I must think of it.’

[084-067]‘Should you like me to ask mother to bring her sewing here now?’ asked

[084-068]Marian, rising.

[084-069]‘Yes, you may do so.’

[084-070]In this way the awkwardness of the situation was overcome, and when

[084-071]Marian next had occasion to speak of money matters no serious objection

[084-072]was offered to her proposal.

[084-073][[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]] of course learnt what had come to pass; to anticipate

[084-074][[reviewing-criticism|criticism]], her brother imparted to her the decision at which Marian and

[084-075]he had arrived. She reflected with an air of discontent.

[084-076]‘So you are quite satisfied,’ was her question at length, ‘that Marian

[084-077]should toil to support her parents as well as herself?’

[084-078]‘Can I help it?’

[084-079]‘I shall think very ill of you if you don’t marry her in a year at

[084-080]latest.’

[084-081]‘I tell you, Marian has made a deliberate choice. She understands me

[084-082]perfectly, and is quite satisfied with my projects. You will have the

[084-083]kindness, Dora, not to disturb her faith in me.’

[084-084]‘I agree to that; and in return I shall let you know when she begins to

[084-085]suffer from hunger. It won’t be very long till then, you may be sure.

[084-086]How do you suppose three people are going to live on a hundred a year?

[084-087]And it’s very doubtful indeed whether Marian can earn as much as fifty

[084-088]pounds. Never mind; I shall let you know when she is beginning to

[084-089]starve, and doubtless that will amuse you.’

[084-090]At the end of July Maud was married. Between Mr Dolomore and

[084-091]Jasper existed no superfluous kindness, each resenting the other’s

[084-092]self-sufficiency; but Jasper, when once satisfied of his proposed

[084-093]brother-in-law’s straightforwardness, was careful not to give offence to

[084-094]a man who might some day serve him. Provided this marriage resulted in

[084-095]moderate happiness to Maud, it was undoubtedly a magnificent stroke of

[084-096]luck. Mrs Lane, the lady who has so often been casually mentioned, took

[084-097]upon herself those offices in connection with the ceremony which

[084-098]the bride’s mother is wont to perform; at her house was held the

[084-099]wedding-breakfast, and such other absurdities of usage as recommend

[084-100]themselves to Society. Dora of course played the part of a bridesmaid,

[084-101]and Jasper went through his duties with the suave seriousness of a man

[084-102]who has convinced himself that he cannot afford to despise anything that

[084-103]the world sanctions.

[084-104][[About]] the same time occurred another event which was to have more

[084-105]importance for this aspiring little family than could as yet be

[084-106]foreseen. [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]]’s noteworthy idea triumphed; the weekly paper called

[084-107]Chat was thoroughly transformed, and appeared as [[chit-chat|Chit-Chat]]. From the

[084-108]first number, the [[literary-ambition|success]] of the enterprise was beyond doubt; in a

[084-109]month’s time all England was ringing with the fame of this noble

[084-110]new development of [[journalism-trade|journalism]]; the proprietor saw his way to a solid

[084-111]fortune, and other men who had money to embark began to scheme imitative

[084-112]publications. It was clear that the quarter-educated would soon be

[084-113]abundantly provided with literature to their taste.

[084-114]Whelpdale’s exultation was unbounded, but in the fifth week of the life

[084-115]of Chit-Chat something happened which threatened to overturn his sober

[084-116]reason. Jasper was walking along the Strand one afternoon, when he

[084-117]saw his ingenious friend approaching him in a manner scarcely to be

[084-118]accounted for, unless Whelpdale’s abstemiousness had for once given way

[084-119]before convivial invitation. The young man’s hat was on the back of his

[084-120]head, and his coat flew wildly as he rushed forwards with perspiring

[084-121]face and glaring eyes. He would have passed without observing Jasper,

[084-122]had not the latter called to him; then he turned round, laughed

[084-123]insanely, grasped his acquaintance by the wrists, and drew him aside

[084-124]into a court.

[084-125]‘What do you think?’ he panted. ‘What do you think has happened?’

[084-126]‘Not what one would suppose, I hope. You seem to have gone mad.’

[084-127]‘I’ve got Lake’s place on Chit-Chat!’ cried the other hoarsely. ‘Two

[084-128]hundred and fifty a year! Lake and the editor quarrelled--pummelled each

[084-129]other--neither know nor care what it was about. My fortune’s made!’

[084-130]‘You’re a modest man,’ remarked Jasper, smiling.

[084-131]‘Certainly I am. I have always admitted it. But remember that there’s

[084-132]my connection with Fleet as well; no need to give that up. Presently I

[084-133]shall be making a clear six hundred, my dear sir!

[084-134]A clear six hundred, if a penny!’

[084-135]‘Satisfactory, so far.’

[084-136]‘But you must remember that I’m not a big gun, like you! Why, my dear

[084-137]Milvain, a year ago I should have thought an income of two hundred a

[084-138]glorious competence. I don’t aim at such things as are fit for you. You

[084-139]won’t be content till you have thousands; of course I know that. But I’m

[084-140]a humble fellow. Yet no; by Jingo, I’m not! In one way I’m not--I must

[084-141]confess it.’

[084-142]‘In what instance are you arrogant?’

[084-143]‘I can’t tell you--not yet; this is neither time nor place. I say,

[084-144]when will you dine with me? I shall give a dinner to half a dozen of my

[084-145]acquaintances somewhere or other. Poor old [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] must come. When can

[084-146]you dine?’

[084-147]‘Give me a week’s notice, and I’ll fit it in.’

[084-148]That dinner came duly off. On the day that followed, Jasper and Dora

[084-149]left town for their holiday; they went to the Channel Islands, and spent

[084-150]more than half of the three weeks they had allowed themselves in Sark.

[084-151]Passing over from Guernsey to that island, they were amused to see a

[084-152]copy of Chit-Chat in the hands of an obese and well-dressed man.

[084-153]‘Is he one of the quarter-educated?’ asked Dora, laughing.

[084-154]‘Not in Whelpdale’s sense of the word. But, strictly speaking, no doubt

[084-155]he is. The quarter-educated constitute a very large [[social-class|class]] indeed; how

[084-156]large, the huge [[literary-success|success]] of that paper is demonstrating. I’ll write to

[084-157]Whelpdale, and let him know that his benefaction has extended even to

[084-158]Sark.’

[084-159]This letter was written, and in a few days there came a reply.

[084-160]‘Why, the fellow has written to you as well!’ exclaimed Jasper, taking

[084-161]up a second letter; both were on the table of their sitting-room when

[084-162]they came to their lodgings for lunch. ‘That’s his hand.’

[084-163]‘It looks like it.’

[084-164]Dora hummed an air as she regarded the envelope, then she took it away

[084-165]with her to her room upstairs.

[084-166]‘What had he to say?’ Jasper inquired, when she came down again and

[084-167]seated herself at the table.

[084-168]‘Oh, a friendly letter. What does he say to you?’

[084-169]Dora had never looked so animated and fresh of colour since leaving

[084-170][[london|London]]; her brother remarked this, and was glad to think that the air of

[084-171]the Channel should be doing her so much good. He read Whelpdale’s letter

[084-172]aloud; it was facetious, but oddly respectful.

[084-173]‘The reverence that fellow has for me is astonishing,’ he observed with

[084-174]a laugh. ‘The queer thing is, it increases the better he knows me.’

[084-175]Dora laughed for five minutes.

[084-176]‘Oh, what a splendid epigram!’ she exclaimed. ‘It is indeed a queer

[084-177]thing, Jasper! Did you mean that to be a good joke, or was it better

[084-178]still by coming out unintentionally?’

[084-179]‘You are in remarkable spirits, old girl. By-the-by, would you mind

[084-180]letting me see that letter of yours?’

[084-181]He held out his hand.

[084-182]‘I left it upstairs,’ Dora replied carelessly.

[084-183]‘Rather presumptuous in him, it seems to me.’

[084-184]‘Oh, he writes quite as respectfully to me as he does to you,’ she

[084-185]returned, with a peculiar smile.

[084-186]‘But what business has he to write at all? It’s confounded impertinence,

[084-187]now I come to think of it. I shall give him a hint to remember his

[084-188]position.’

[084-189]Dora could not be quite sure whether he spoke seriously or not. As both

[084-190]of them had begun to eat with an excellent appetite, a few moments were

[084-191]allowed to pass before the girl again spoke.

[084-192]‘His position is as good as ours,’ she said at length.

[084-193]‘As good as ours? The “sub.” of a paltry rag like Chit-Chat, and

[084-194]assistant to a literary agency!’
