# Section 72

[071-001]Their eyes met, and the look of each seemed to fascinate the other.

[071-002]‘If you have made up your mind to that,’ said [[marian-yule|Marian]] in a shaking

[071-003]voice, ‘I can remain here no longer. Such words are senselessly cruel.

[071-004]To-morrow I shall leave the house.’

[071-005]‘I repeat that you are of age, and perfectly independent. It can be

[071-006]nothing to me how soon you go. You have given proof that I am of less

[071-007]than no account to you, and doubtless the sooner we cease to afflict

[071-008]each other the better.’

[071-009]It seemed as if the effect of these conflicts with her father were to

[071-010]develop in Marian a vehemence of temper which at length matched that

[071-011]of which [[alfred-yule|Yule]] was the victim. Her face, outlined to express a gentle

[071-012]gravity, was now haughtily passionate; nostrils and lips thrilled with

[071-013]wrath, and her eyes were magnificent in their dark fieriness.

[071-014]‘You shall not need to tell me that again,’ she answered, and

[071-015]immediately left him.

[071-016]She went into the sitting-room, where Mrs Yule was awaiting the result

[071-017]of the interview.

[071-018]‘Mother,’ she said, with stern gentleness, ‘this house can no longer be

[071-019]a home for me. I shall go away to-morrow, and live in lodgings until the

[071-020]time of my [[literary-marriage|marriage]].’

[071-021]Mrs Yule uttered a cry of pain, and started up.

[071-022]‘Oh, don’t do that, Marian! What has he said to you? Come and talk to

[071-023]me, darling--tell me what he’s said--don’t look like that!’

[071-024]She clung to the girl despairingly, terrified by a transformation she

[071-025]would have thought impossible.

[071-026]‘He says that if I marry [[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]] he hopes never to see my face again.

[071-027]I can’t stay here. You shall come and see me, and we will be the same

[071-028]to each other as always. But father has treated me too unjustly. I can’t

[071-029]live near him after this.’

[071-030]‘He doesn’t mean it,’ sobbed her mother. ‘He says what he’s sorry for

[071-031]as soon as the words are spoken. He loves you too much, my darling, to

[071-032]drive you away like that. It’s his disappointment, Marian; that’s all it

[071-033]is. He counted on it so much. I’ve heard him talk of it in his sleep;

[071-034]he made so sure that he was going to have that new magazine, and the

[071-035]disappointment makes him that he doesn’t know what he’s saying. Only

[071-036]wait and see; he’ll tell you he didn’t mean it, I know he will. Only

[071-037]leave him alone till he’s had time to get over it. Do forgive him this

[071-038]once.’

[071-039]‘It’s like a madman to talk in that way,’ said the girl, releasing

[071-040]herself. ‘Whatever his disappointment, I can’t endure it. I have worked

[071-041]hard for him, very hard, ever since I was old enough, and he owes me

[071-042]some kindness, some respect. It would be different if he had the least

[071-043]reason for his hatred of Jasper. It is nothing but insensate prejudice,

[071-044]the result of his quarrels with other people. What right has he to

[071-045]insult me by representing my future husband as a scheming hypocrite?’

[071-046]‘My love, he has had so much to bear--it’s made him so quick-tempered.’

[071-047]‘Then I am quick-tempered too, and the sooner we are apart the better,

[071-048]as he said himself.’

[071-049]‘Oh, but you have always been such a patient girl.’

[071-050]‘My patience is at an end when I am treated as if I had neither rights

[071-051]nor feelings. However wrong the choice I had made, this was not the way

[071-052]to behave to me. His disappointment? Is there a natural law, then, that

[071-053]a daughter must be sacrificed to her father? My husband will have as

[071-054]much need of that [[money-in-literature|money]] as my father has, and he will be able to make

[071-055]far better use of it. It was wrong even to ask me to give my money away

[071-056]like that. I have a right to happiness, as well as other women.’

[071-057]She was shaken with hysterical passion, the natural consequence of this

[071-058]outbreak in a nature such as hers. Her mother, in the meantime,

[071-059]grew stronger by force of profound love that at length had found its

[071-060]opportunity of expression. Presently she persuaded Marian to come

[071-061]upstairs with her, and before long the overburdened breast was relieved

[071-062]by a flow of tears. But Marian’s purpose remained unshaken.

[071-063]‘It is impossible for us to see each other day after day,’ she said when

[071-064]calmer. ‘He can’t control his anger against me, and I suffer too much

[071-065]when I am made to feel like this. I shall take a lodging not far off;

[071-066]where you can see me often.’

[071-067]‘But you have no money, Marian,’ replied Mrs Yule, miserably.

[071-068]‘No money? As if I couldn’t borrow a few pounds until all my own comes

[071-069]to me! [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]] can lend me all I shall want; it won’t make the

[071-070]least difference to her. I must have my money very soon now.’

[071-071]At about half-past eleven Mrs Yule went downstairs, and entered the

[071-072]study.

[071-073]‘If you are coming to speak about Marian,’ said her husband, turning

[071-074]upon her with savage eyes, ‘you can save your breath. I won’t hear her

[071-075]name mentioned.’

[071-076]She faltered, but overcame her weakness.

[071-077]‘You are driving her away from us, Alfred. It isn’t right! Oh, it isn’t

[071-078]right!’

[071-079]‘If she didn’t go I should, so understand that! And if I go, you have

[071-080]seen the last of me. Make your choice, make your choice!’

[071-081]He had yielded himself to that perverse frenzy which impels a man to

[071-082]acts and utterances most wildly at conflict with reason. His sense of

[071-083]the monstrous irrationality to which he was committed completed what was

[071-084]begun in him by the bitterness of a great frustration.

[071-085]‘If I wasn’t a poor, helpless woman,’ replied his wife, sinking upon a

[071-086]chair and crying without raising her hands to her face, ‘I’d go and live

[071-087]with her till she was married, and then make a home for myself. But I

[071-088]haven’t a penny, and I’m too old to earn my own living; I should only be

[071-089]a burden to her.’

[071-090]‘That shall be no hindrance,’ cried Yule. ‘Go, by all means; you shall

[071-091]have a sufficient allowance as long as I can continue to work, and when

[071-092]I’m past that, your lot will be no harder than mine. Your daughter had

[071-093]the chance of making provision for my old age, at no expense to herself.

[071-094]But that was asking too much of her. Go, by all means, and leave me to

[071-095]make what I can of the rest of my life; perhaps I may save a few years

[071-096]still from the curse brought upon me by my own folly.’

[071-097]It was idle to address him. Mrs Yule went into the sitting-room, and

[071-098]there sat weeping for an hour. Then she extinguished the lights, and

[071-099]crept upstairs in silence.

[071-100]Yule passed the night in the study. Towards morning he slept for an hour

[071-101]or two, just long enough to let the fire go out and to get thoroughly

[071-102]chilled. When he opened his eyes a muddy twilight had begun to show at

[071-103]the window; the sounds of a clapping door within the house, which had

[071-104]probably awakened him, made him aware that the servant was already up.

[071-105]He drew up the blind. There seemed to be a frost, for the moisture

[071-106]of last night had all disappeared, and the yard upon which the window

[071-107]looked was unusually clean. With a glance at the black grate he

[071-108]extinguished his lamp, and went out into the passage. A few minutes’

[071-109]groping for his overcoat and hat, and he left the house.

[071-110]His purpose was to warm himself with a vigorous walk, and at the

[071-111]same time to shake off if possible, the nightmare of his rage and

[071-112]hopelessness. He had no distinct feeling with regard to his behaviour of

[071-113]the past evening; he neither justified nor condemned himself; he did not

[071-114]ask himself whether Marian would to-day leave her home, or if her mother

[071-115]would take him at his word and also depart. These seemed to be details

[071-116]which his brain was too weary to consider. But he wished to be away from

[071-117]the wretchedness of his house, and to let things go as they would

[071-118]whilst he was absent. As he closed the front door he felt as if he were

[071-119]escaping from an atmosphere that threatened to stifle him.

[071-120]His steps directing themselves more by habit than with any deliberate

[071-121]choice, he walked towards Camden Road. When he had reached [[camden-town|Camden Town]]

[071-122]railway-station he was attracted by a coffee-stall; a draught of

[071-123]the steaming liquid, no matter its quality, would help his blood to

[071-124]circulate. He laid down his penny, and first warmed his hands by holding

[071-125]them round the cup. Whilst standing thus he noticed that the objects at

[071-126]which he looked had a blurred appearance; his eyesight seemed to have

[071-127]become worse this morning. Only a result of his insufficient sleep

[071-128]perhaps. He took up a scrap of newspaper that lay on the stall; he could

[071-129]read it, but one of his eyes was certainly weaker than the other; trying

[071-130]to see with that one alone, he found that everything became misty.

[071-131]He laughed, as if the threat of new calamity were an amusement in his

[071-132]present state of mind. And at the same moment his look encountered that

[071-133]of a man who had drawn near to him, a shabbily-dressed man of middle

[071-134]age, whose face did not correspond with his attire.

[071-135]‘Will you give me a cup of coffee?’ asked the stranger, in a low voice

[071-136]and with shamefaced manner. ‘It would be a great kindness.’

[071-137]The accent was that of good breeding. Yule hesitated in surprise for a

[071-138]moment, then said:

[071-139]‘Have one by all means. Would you care for anything to eat?’

[071-140]‘I am much obliged to you. I think I should be none the worse for one of

[071-141]those solid slices of bread and butter.’

[071-142]The stall-keeper was just extinguishing his lights; the frosty sky

[071-143]showed a pale gleam of sunrise.

[071-144]‘Hard times, I’m afraid,’ remarked Yule, as his beneficiary began to eat

[071-145]the luncheon with much appearance of grateful appetite.

[071-146]‘Very hard times.’ He had a small, thin, colourless countenance, with

[071-147]large, pathetic eyes; a slight moustache and curly beard. His clothes

[071-148]were such as would be worn by some very poor clerk. ‘I came here an

[071-149]hour ago,’ he continued, ‘with the hope of meeting an acquaintance who

[071-150]generally goes from this station at a certain time. I have missed

[071-151]him, and in doing so I missed what I had thought my one chance of a

[071-152]breakfast. When one has neither dined nor supped on the previous day,

[071-153]breakfast becomes a meal of some importance.’

[071-154]‘True. Take another slice.’

[071-155]‘I am greatly obliged to you.’

[071-156]‘Not at all. I have known hard times myself, and am likely to know

[071-157]worse.’

[071-158]‘I trust not. This is the first time that I have positively begged.

[071-159]I should have been too much ashamed to beg of the kind of men who are

[071-160]usually at these places; they certainly have no money to spare. I was

[071-161]thinking of making an appeal at a baker’s shop, but it is very likely I

[071-162]should have been handed over to a policeman. Indeed I don’t know what I

[071-163]should have done; the last point of endurance was almost reached. I have

[071-164]no clothes but these I wear, and they are few enough for the season.

[071-165]Still, I suppose the waistcoat must have gone.’

[071-166]He did not talk like a beggar who is trying to excite compassion, but

[071-167]with a sort of detached curiosity concerning the difficulties of his

[071-168]position.

[071-169]‘You can find nothing to do?’ said the man of letters.

[071-170]‘Positively nothing. By profession I am a surgeon, but it’s a long time

[071-171]since I practised. Fifteen years ago I was comfortably established at

[071-172]Wakefield; I was married and had one child. But my capital ran out, and

[071-173]my practice, never anything to boast of, fell to nothing. I succeeded

[071-174]in getting a place as an assistant to a man at Chester. We sold up, and

[071-175]started on the journey.’
