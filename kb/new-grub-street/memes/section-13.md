# Section 13

[012-001][[amy-reardon|Amy]] shook her head.

[012-002]‘No progress?’

[012-003]‘None whatever. He can’t work; I begin to be afraid that he is really

[012-004]ill. He must go away before the fine weather is over. Do persuade him

[012-005]to-night! I wish you could have had a holiday with him.’

[012-006]‘Out of the question now, I’m sorry to say. I must work savagely. But

[012-007]can’t you all manage a fortnight somewhere--Hastings, Eastbourne?’

[012-008]‘It would be simply rash. One goes on saying, “What does a pound or two

[012-009]matter?”--but it begins at length to matter a great deal.’

[012-010]‘I know, confound it all! Think how it would amuse some rich grocer’s

[012-011]son who pitches his half-sovereign to the waiter when he has dined

[012-012]himself into good humour! But I tell you what it is: you must really try

[012-013]to influence him towards practicality. Don’t you think--?’

[012-014]He paused, and Amy sat looking at her hands.

[012-015]‘I have made an attempt,’ she said at length, in a distant undertone.

[012-016]‘You really have?’

[012-017][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] leaned forward, his clasped hands hanging between his knees. He

[012-018]was scrutinising her face, and Amy, conscious of the too fixed regard,

[012-019]at length moved her head uneasily.

[012-020]‘It seems very clear to me,’ she said, ‘that a long book is out of the

[012-021]question for him at present. He writes so slowly, and is so fastidious.

[012-022]It would be a fatal thing to hurry through something weaker even than

[012-023]the last.’

[012-024]‘You think “The Optimist” weak?’ Jasper asked, half absently.

[012-025]‘I don’t think it worthy of [[edwin-reardon|Edwin]]; I don’t see how anyone can.

[012-026]‘I have wondered what your opinion was. Yes, he ought to try a new tack,

[012-027]I think.’

[012-028]Just then there came the sound of a latch-key opening the outer door.

[012-029]Jasper lay back in his chair and waited with a smile for his expected

[012-030]friend’s appearance; Amy made no movement.

[012-031]‘Oh, there you are!’ said Reardon, presenting himself with the dazzled

[012-032]eyes of one who has been in darkness; he spoke in a voice of genial

[012-033]welcome, though it still had the note of depression. ‘When did you get

[012-034]back?’

[012-035]Milvain began to recount what he had told in the first part of his

[012-036]conversation with Amy. As he did so, the latter withdrew, and was absent

[012-037]for five minutes; on reappearing she said:

[012-038]‘You’ll have some supper with us, Mr Milvain?’

[012-039]‘I think I will, please.’

[012-040]Shortly after, all repaired to the eating-room, where conversation

[012-041]had to be carried on in a low tone because of the proximity of the

[012-042]bedchamber in which lay the sleeping child. Jasper began to tell of

[012-043]certain things that had happened to him since his arrival in town.

[012-044]‘It was a curious coincidence--but, by-the-bye, have you heard of what

[012-045][[the-study|The Study]] has been doing?’

[012-046]‘I should rather think so,’ replied Reardon, his face lighting up. ‘With

[012-047]no small satisfaction.’

[012-048]‘Delicious, isn’t it?’ exclaimed his wife. ‘I thought it too good to be

[012-049]true when Edwin heard of it from [[harold-biffen|Mr Biffen]].’

[012-050]All three laughed in subdued chorus. For the moment, Reardon became a

[012-051]new man in his exultation over the contradictory reviewers.

[012-052]‘Oh, Biffen told you, did he? Well,’ continued Jasper, ‘it was an odd

[012-053]thing, but when I reached my lodgings on Saturday evening there lay

[012-054]a note from [[horace-barlow|Horace Barlow]], inviting me to go and see him on Sunday

[012-055]afternoon out at Wimbledon, the special reason being that the editor of

[012-056]The Study would be there, and Barlow thought I might like to meet him.

[012-057]Now this letter gave me a fit of laughter; not only because of those

[012-058]precious reviews, but because [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] had been telling me all about

[012-059]this same editor, who rejoices in the name of [[fadge|Fadge]]. Your uncle, Mrs

[012-060]Reardon, declares that Fadge is the most malicious man in the literary

[012-061]profession; though that’s saying such a very great deal--well, never

[012-062]mind! Of course I was delighted to go and meet Fadge. At Barlow’s I

[012-063]found the queerest collection of people, most of them women of the

[012-064]inkiest description. The great Fadge himself surprised me; I expected

[012-065]to see a gaunt, bilious man, and he was the rosiest and dumpiest little

[012-066]dandy you can imagine; a fellow of forty-five, I dare say, with thin

[012-067]yellow hair and blue eyes and a manner of extreme innocence. Fadge

[012-068]flattered me with confidential chat, and I discovered at length why

[012-069]Barlow had asked me to meet him; it’s Fadge that is going to edit

[012-070]Culpepper’s new monthly--you’ve heard about it?--and he had actually

[012-071]thought it worth while to enlist me among contributors! Now, how’s that

[012-072]for a piece of news?’

[012-073]The speaker looked from Reardon to Amy with a smile of vast

[012-074]significance.

[012-075]‘I rejoice to hear it!’ said Reardon, fervently.

[012-076]‘You see! you see!’ cried Jasper, forgetting all about the infant in the

[012-077]next room, ‘all things come to the man who knows how to wait. But I’m

[012-078]hanged if I expected a thing of this kind to come so soon! Why, I’m a

[012-079]man of distinction! My doings have been noted; the admirable qualities

[012-080]of my style have drawn attention; I’m looked upon as one of the coming

[012-081]men! Thanks, I confess, in some measure, to old Barlow; he seems to have

[012-082]amused himself with cracking me up to all and sundry. That last thing

[012-083]of mine in [[the-west-end|The West End]] has done me a vast amount of good, it seems. And

[012-084]Alfred Yule himself had noticed that paper in [[the-wayside|The Wayside]]. That’s how

[012-085]things work, you know; [[literary-success|reputation]] comes with a burst, just when you’re

[012-086]not looking for anything of the kind.’

[012-087]‘What’s the new magazine to be called?’ asked Amy.

[012-088]‘Why, they propose [[the-current|The Current]]. Not bad, in a way; though you imagine

[012-089]a fellow saying “Have you seen the current Current?” At all events, the

[012-090]tone is to be up to date, and the articles are to be short; no padding,

[012-091]merum sal from cover to cover. What do you think I have undertaken to

[012-092]do, for a start? A paper consisting of sketches of typical readers of

[012-093]each of the principal daily and weekly papers. A deuced good idea, you

[012-094]know--my own, of course--but deucedly hard to carry out. I shall rise

[012-095]to the occasion, see if I don’t. I’ll rival Fadge himself in

[012-096]maliciousness--though I must confess I discovered no particular malice

[012-097]in the fellow’s way of talking. The article shall make a sensation. I’ll

[012-098]spend a whole month on it, and make it a perfect piece of satire.’

[012-099]‘Now that’s the kind of thing that inspires me with awe and envy,’

[012-100]said Reardon. ‘I could no more write such a paper than an article on

[012-101]Fluxions.’

[012-102]‘’Tis my vocation, Hal! You might think I hadn’t experience enough,

[012-103]to begin with. But my intuition is so strong that I can make a little

[012-104]experience go an immense way. Most people would imagine I had been

[012-105]wasting my time these last few years, just sauntering about, reading

[012-106]nothing but periodicals, making acquaintance with loafers of every

[012-107]description. The truth is, I have been collecting ideas, and ideas

[012-108]that are convertible into coin of the realm, my boy; I have the special

[012-109]faculty of an extempore writer. Never in my life shall I do anything of

[012-110]solid literary value; I shall always despise the people I write for. But

[012-111]my path will be that of [[literary-ambition|success]]. I have always said it, and now I’m sure

[012-112]of it.’

[012-113]‘Does Fadge retire from The Study, then?’ inquired Reardon, when he had

[012-114]received this tirade with a friendly laugh.

[012-115]‘Yes, he does. Was going to, it seems, in any case. Of course I heard

[012-116]nothing about the two reviews, and I was almost afraid to smile whilst

[012-117]Fadge was talking with me, lest I should betray my thought. Did you know

[012-118]anything about the fellow before?’

[012-119]‘Not I. Didn’t know who edited The Study.’

[012-120]‘Nor I either. Remarkable what a number of illustrious obscure are going

[012-121]about. But I have still something else to tell you. I’m going to set my

[012-122]sisters afloat in literature.’

[012-123]‘How!’

[012-124]‘Well, I don’t see why they shouldn’t try their hands at a little

[012-125]writing, instead of giving lessons, which doesn’t suit them a bit. Last

[012-126]night, when I got back from Wimbledon, I went to look up Davies. Perhaps

[012-127]you don’t remember my mentioning him; a fellow who was at Jolly and

[012-128]Monk’s, the [[publishing-trade|publishers]], up to a year ago. He edits a trade journal now,

[012-129]and I see very little of him. However, I found him at home, and had

[012-130]a long practical talk with him. I wanted to find out the state of the

[012-131]market as to such wares as Jolly and Monk dispose of. He gave me some

[012-132]very useful hints, and the result was that I went off this morning and

[012-133]saw Monk himself--no Jolly exists at present. “Mr Monk,” I began, in my

[012-134]blandest tone--you know it--“I am requested to call upon you by a lady

[012-135]who thinks of preparing a little volume to be called ‘A Child’s History

[012-136]of the English Parliament.’ Her idea is, that”--and so on. Well, I

[012-137]got on admirably with Monk, especially when he learnt that I was to be

[012-138]connected with Culpepper’s new venture; he smiled upon the project, and

[012-139]said he should be very glad to see a specimen chapter; if that pleased

[012-140]him, we could then discuss terms.’

[012-141]‘But has one of your sisters really begun such a book?’ inquired Amy.

[012-142]‘Neither of them knows anything of the matter, but they are certainly

[012-143]capable of doing the kind of thing I have in mind, which will consist

[012-144]largely of anecdotes of prominent statesmen. I myself shall write the

[012-145]specimen chapter, and send it to the girls to show them what I propose.

[012-146]I shouldn’t wonder if they make some fifty pounds out of it. The few

[012-147]books that will be necessary they can either get at a [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]]

[012-148]library, or I can send them.’

[012-149]‘Your energy is remarkable, all of a sudden,’ said Reardon.

[012-150]‘Yes. The hour has come, I find. “There is a tide”--to quote something

[012-151]that has the charm of freshness.’

[012-152]The supper--which consisted of bread and butter, cheese, sardines,

[012-153]cocoa--was now over, and Jasper, still enlarging on his recent

[012-154]experiences and future prospects, led the way back to the sitting-room.

[012-155]Not very long after this, Amy left the two friends to their pipes; she

[012-156]was anxious that her husband should discuss his affairs privately with

[012-157]Milvain, and give ear to the practical advice which she knew would be

[012-158]tendered him.

[012-159]‘I hear that you are still stuck fast,’ began Jasper, when they had

[012-160]smoked awhile in silence.

[012-161]‘Yes.’

[012-162]‘Getting rather serious, I should fear, isn’t it?’

[012-163]‘Yes,’ repeated Reardon, in a low voice.

[012-164]‘Come, come, old man, you can’t go on in this way. Would it, or wouldn’t

[012-165]it, be any use if you took a seaside holiday?’

[012-166]‘Not the least. I am incapable of holiday, if the opportunity were

[012-167]offered. Do something I must, or I shall fret myself into imbecility.’

[012-168]‘Very well. What is it to be?’

[012-169]‘I shall try to manufacture two volumes. They needn’t run to more than

[012-170]about two hundred and seventy pages, and those well spaced out.’

[012-171]‘This is refreshing. This is practical. But look now: let it be

[012-172]something rather sensational. Couldn’t we invent a good title--something

[012-173]to catch eye and ear? The title would suggest the story, you know.’

[012-174]Reardon laughed contemptuously, but the scorn was directed rather

[012-175]against himself than Milvain.

[012-176]‘Let’s try,’ he muttered.

[012-177]Both appeared to exercise their minds on the problem for a few minutes.

[012-178]Then Jasper slapped his knee.

[012-179]‘How would this do: “The Weird Sisters”? Devilish good, eh? Suggests all

[012-180]sorts of things, both to the vulgar and the educated. Nothing brutally

[012-181]clap-trap about it, you know.’

[012-182]‘But--what does it suggest to you?’

[012-183]‘Oh, witch-like, mysterious girls or women. Think it over.’

[012-184]There was another long silence. Reardon’s face was that of a man in

[012-185]blank misery.
