# Section 46

[045-001]Carrying a bag which contained a few things best kept in his own care,

[045-002]he went by train to King’s Cross, and thence walked up Pentonville

[045-003]Hill to Upper Street and his own little by-way. Manville Street was not

[045-004]unreasonably squalid; the house in which he had found a home was not

[045-005]alarming in its appearance, and the woman who kept it had an honest

[045-006]face. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] would have shrunk in apprehension, but to one who had

[045-007]experience of [[london|London]] garrets this was a rather favourable specimen of

[045-008]its kind. The door closed more satisfactorily than poor [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]’s, for

[045-009]instance, and there were not many of those knot-holes in the floor which

[045-010]gave admission to piercing little draughts; not a pane of the window

[045-011]was cracked, not one. A man might live here comfortably--could memory be

[045-012]destroyed.

[045-013]‘There’s a letter come for you,’ said the landlady as she admitted him.

[045-014]‘You’ll find it on your mantel.’

[045-015]He ascended hastily. The letter must be from Amy, as no one else knew

[045-016]his address. Yes, and its contents were these:

[045-017]‘As you have really sold the furniture, I shall accept half this [[money-in-literature|money]]

[045-018]that you send. I must buy clothing for myself and Willie. But the other

[045-019]ten pounds I shall return to you as soon as possible. As for your

[045-020]offer of half what you are to receive from Mr [[carter|Carter]], that seems to me

[045-021]ridiculous; in any case, I cannot take it. If you seriously abandon

[045-022]all further hope from literature, I think it is your duty to make every

[045-023]effort to obtain a position suitable to a man of your education.--AMY

[045-024]REARDON.’

[045-025]Doubtless Amy thought it was her duty to write in this way. Not a word

[045-026]of sympathy; he must understand that no one was to blame but himself;

[045-027]and that her hardships were equal to his own.

[045-028]In the bag he had brought with him there were writing materials.

[045-029]Standing at the mantelpiece, he forthwith penned a reply to this letter:

[045-030]‘The money is for your support, as far as it will go. If it comes back

[045-031]to me I shall send it again. If you refuse to make use of it, you

[045-032]will have the kindness to put it aside and consider it as belonging

[045-033]to Willie. The other money of which I spoke will be sent to you once a

[045-034]month. As our concerns are no longer between us alone, I must protect

[045-035]myself against anyone who would be likely to accuse me of not giving you

[045-036]what I could afford. For your advice I thank you, but remember that in

[045-037]withdrawing from me your affection you have lost all right to offer me

[045-038]counsel.’

[045-039]He went out and posted this at once.

[045-040]By three o’clock the furniture of his room was arranged. He had not kept

[045-041]a carpet; that was luxury, and beyond his due. His score of volumes must

[045-042]rank upon the mantelpiece; his clothing must be kept in the trunk. Cups,

[045-043]plates, knives, forks, and spoons would lie in the little open cupboard,

[045-044]the lowest section of which was for his supply of coals. When everything

[045-045]was in order he drew water from a tap on the landing and washed himself;

[045-046]then, with his bag, went out to make purchases. A loaf of bread, butter,

[045-047]sugar, condensed milk; a remnant of tea he had brought with him. On

[045-048]returning, he lit as small a fire as possible, put on his kettle, and

[045-049]sat down to meditate.

[045-050]How familiar it all was to him! And not unpleasant, for it brought

[045-051]back the days when he had worked to such good purpose. It was like a

[045-052]restoration of youth.

[045-053]Of Amy he would not think. Knowing his bitter misery, she could write

[045-054]to him in cold, hard words, without a touch even of womanly feeling. If

[045-055]ever they were to meet again, the advance must be from her side. He had

[045-056]no more tenderness for her until she strove to revive it.

[045-057]Next morning he called at the hospital to see Carter. The secretary’s

[045-058]peculiar look and smile seemed to betray a knowledge of what had been

[045-059]going on since Sunday, and his first words confirmed this impression of

[045-060][[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s.

[045-061]‘You have removed, I hear?’

[045-062]‘Yes; I had better give you my new address.’

[045-063]Reardon’s tone was meant to signify that further remark on the subject

[045-064]would be unwelcome. Musingly, Carter made a note of the address.

[045-065]‘You still wish to go on with this affair?’

[045-066]‘Certainly.’

[045-067]‘Come and have some lunch with me, then, and afterwards we’ll go to the

[045-068]City Road and talk things over on the spot.’

[045-069]The vivacious young man was not quite so genial as of wont, but he

[045-070]evidently strove to show that the renewal of their relations as employer

[045-071]and clerk would make no difference in the friendly intercourse which

[045-072]had since been established; the invitation to lunch evidently had this

[045-073]purpose.

[045-074]‘I suppose,’ said Carter, when they were seated in a restaurant, ‘you

[045-075]wouldn’t object to anything better, if a chance turned up?’

[045-076]‘I should take it, to be sure.’

[045-077]‘But you don’t want a job that would occupy all your time? You’re going

[045-078]on with writing, of course?’

[045-079]‘Not for the present, I think.’

[045-080]‘Then you would like me to keep a look-out? I haven’t anything in

[045-081]view--nothing whatever. But one hears of things sometimes.’

[045-082]‘I should be obliged to you if you could help me to anything

[045-083]satisfactory.’

[045-084]Having brought himself to this admission, Reardon felt more at ease. To

[045-085]what purpose should he keep up transparent pretences? It was manifestly

[045-086]his duty to earn as much money as he could, in whatever way. Let the

[045-087]man of letters be forgotten; he was seeking for remunerative employment,

[045-088]just as if he had never written a line.

[045-089]Amy did not return the ten pounds, and did not write again. So,

[045-090]presumably, she would accept the moiety of his earnings; he was glad

[045-091]of it. After paying half-a-crown for rent, there would be left ten

[045-092]shillings. Something like three pounds that still remained to him he

[045-093]would not reckon; this must be for casualties.

[045-094]Half-a-sovereign was enough for his needs; in the old times he had

[045-095]counted it a competency which put his mind quite at rest.

[045-096]The day came, and he entered upon his duties in City Road. It needed but

[045-097]an hour or two, and all the intervening time was cancelled; he was

[045-098]back once more in the days of no [[literary-success|reputation]], a harmless clerk, a decent

[045-099]wage-earner.

[045-100]CHAPTER XX. THE END OF WAITING

[045-101]It was more than a fortnight after Reardon’s removal to [[islington|Islington]] when

[045-102][[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]] heard for the first time of what had happened. He was

[045-103]coming down from the office of the Will-o’-the-Wisp one afternoon,

[045-104]after a talk with the editor concerning a paragraph in his last week’s

[045-105]causerie which had been complained of as libellous, and which would

[045-106]probably lead to the ‘case’ so much desired by everyone connected with

[045-107]the paper, when someone descending from a higher storey of the building

[045-108]overtook him and laid a hand on his shoulder. He turned and saw

[045-109][[whelpdale|Whelpdale]].

[045-110]‘What brings you on these premises?’ he asked, as they shook hands.

[045-111]‘A man I know has just been made sub-editor of Chat, upstairs. He has

[045-112]half promised to let me do a column of answers to correspondents.’

[045-113]‘Cosmetics? Fashions? Cookery?’

[045-114]‘I’m not so versatile as all that, unfortunately. No, the general

[045-115]information column. “Will you be so good as to inform me, through the

[045-116]medium of your invaluable paper, what was the exact area devastated

[045-117]by the Great Fire of London?”--that kind of thing, you know.

[045-118]Hopburn--that’s the fellow’s name--tells me that his predecessor always

[045-119]called the paper Chat-moss, because of the frightful difficulty he had

[045-120]in filling it up each week. By-the-bye, what a capital column that is of

[045-121]yours in Will-o’-the-Wisp. I know nothing like it in English [[journalism-trade|journalism]];

[045-122]upon my word I don’t!’

[045-123]‘Glad you like it. Some people are less fervent in their admiration.’

[045-124]Jasper recounted the affair which had just been under discussion in the

[045-125]office.

[045-126]‘It may cost a couple of thousands, but the advertisement is worth that,

[045-127]Patwin thinks. Barlow is delighted; he wouldn’t mind paying double the

[045-128]money to make those people a laughing-stock for a week or two.’

[045-129]They issued into the street, and walked on together; Milvain, with

[045-130]his keen eye and critical smile, unmistakably the modern young man who

[045-131]cultivates the art of [[literary-ambition|success]]; his companion of a less pronounced type,

[045-132]but distinguished by a certain subtlety of countenance, a blending of

[045-133]the sentimental and the shrewd.

[045-134]‘Of course you know all about the Reardons?’ said Whelpdale.

[045-135]‘Haven’t seen or heard of them lately. What is it?’

[045-136]‘Then you don’t know that they have parted?’

[045-137]‘Parted?’

[045-138]‘I only heard about it last night; Biffen told me. Reardon is doing

[045-139]clerk’s work at a hospital somewhere in the East-end, and his wife has

[045-140]gone to live at her mother’s house.’

[045-141]‘Ho, ho!’ exclaimed Jasper, thoughtfully. ‘Then the crash has come. Of

[045-142]course I knew it must be impending. I’m sorry for Reardon.’

[045-143]‘I’m sorry for his wife.’

[045-144]‘Trust you for thinking of women first, Whelpdale.’

[045-145]‘It’s in an honourable way, my dear fellow. I’m a slave to women, true,

[045-146]but all in an honourable way. After that last adventure of mine most

[045-147]men would be savage and cynical, wouldn’t they, now? I’m nothing of the

[045-148]kind. I think no worse of women--not a bit. I reverence them as much as

[045-149]ever. There must be a good deal of magnanimity in me, don’t you think?’

[045-150]Jasper laughed unrestrainedly.

[045-151]‘But it’s the simple truth,’ pursued the other. ‘You should have

[045-152]seen the letter I wrote to that girl at Birmingham--all charity and

[045-153]forgiveness. I meant it, every word of it. I shouldn’t talk to everyone

[045-154]like this, you know; but it’s as well to show a friend one’s best

[045-155]qualities now and then.’

[045-156]‘Is Reardon still living at the old place?’

[045-157]‘No, no. They sold up everything and let the flat. He’s in lodgings

[045-158]somewhere or other. I’m not quite intimate enough with him to go and see

[045-159]him under the circumstances. But I’m surprised you know nothing about

[045-160]it.’

[045-161]‘I haven’t seen much of them this year. Reardon--well, I’m afraid he

[045-162]hasn’t very much of the virtue you claim for yourself. It rather annoys

[045-163]him to see me going ahead.’

[045-164]‘Really? His character never struck me in that way.’

[045-165]‘You haven’t come enough in contact with him. At all events, I can’t

[045-166]explain his change of manner in any other way. But I’m sorry for him;

[045-167]I am, indeed. At a hospital? I suppose Carter has given him the old job

[045-168]again?’

[045-169]‘Don’t know. Biffen doesn’t talk very freely about it; there’s a good

[045-170]deal of delicacy in Biffen, you know. A thoroughly good-hearted fellow.

[045-171]And so is Reardon, I believe, though no doubt he has his weaknesses.’

[045-172]‘Oh, an excellent fellow! But weakness isn’t the word. Why, I foresaw

[045-173]all this from the very beginning. The first hour’s talk I ever had

[045-174]with him was enough to convince me that he’d never hold his own. But he

[045-175]really believed that the future was clear before him; he imagined he’d

[045-176]go on getting more and more for his books. An extraordinary thing that

[045-177]that girl had such faith in him!’

[045-178]They parted soon after this, and Milvain went homeward, musing upon what

[045-179]he had heard. It was his purpose to spend the whole evening on some

[045-180]work which pressed for completion, but he found an unusual difficulty

[045-181]in settling to it. [[About]] eight o’clock he gave up the effort, arrayed

[045-182]himself in the costume of black and white, and journeyed to Westbourne

[045-183]Park, where his destination was the house of [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]]. Of the

[045-184]servant who opened to him he inquired if Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] was at home, and

[045-185]received an answer in the affirmative.
