# Section 81

[080-001]‘I feared this,’ he replied under his breath. ‘He was ill when I saw him

[080-002]off at [[london|London]] Bridge. But Willie is better, I trust?’

[080-003][[amy-reardon|Amy]] tried to answer, but tears filled her eyes and her head drooped.

[080-004][[harold-biffen|Harold]] was overcome with a sense of fatality; grief and dread held him

[080-005]motionless.

[080-006]They conversed brokenly for a few minutes, then left the house, Biffen

[080-007]carrying the hand-bag with which he had travelled hither. When they

[080-008]reached the hotel he waited apart until it was ascertained whether he

[080-009]could enter the sick-room. Amy rejoined him and said with a faint smile:

[080-010]‘He is conscious, and was very glad to hear that you had come. But don’t

[080-011]let him try to speak much.’

[080-012]The change that had come over his friend’s countenance was to Harold, of

[080-013]course, far more gravely impressive than to those who had watched at the

[080-014]bedside. In the drawn features, large sunken eyes, thin and discoloured

[080-015]lips, it seemed to him that he read too surely the presage of doom.

[080-016]After holding the shrunken hand for a moment he was convulsed with an

[080-017]agonising sob, and had to turn away.

[080-018]Amy saw that her husband wished to speak to her; she bent over him.

[080-019]‘Ask him to stay, dear. Give him a room in the hotel.’

[080-020]‘I will.’

[080-021]Biffen sat down by the bedside, and remained for half an hour. His

[080-022]friend inquired whether he had yet heard about the novel; the answer was

[080-023]a shake of the head. When he rose, [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] signed to him to bend down,

[080-024]and whispered:

[080-025]‘It doesn’t matter what happens; she is mine again.’

[080-026]The next day was very cold, but a blue sky gleamed over land and sea.

[080-027]The drives and promenades were thronged with people in exuberant health

[080-028]and spirits. Biffen regarded this spectacle with resentful scorn; at

[080-029]another time it would have moved him merely to mirth, but not even the

[080-030]sound of the breakers when he had wandered as far as possible from human

[080-031]contact could help him to think with resignation of the injustice which

[080-032]triumphs so flagrantly in the destinies of men. Towards Amy he had no

[080-033]shadow of unkindness; the sight of her in tears had impressed him as

[080-034]profoundly, in another way, as that of his friend’s wasted features. She

[080-035]and Reardon were again one, and his love for them both was stronger than

[080-036]any emotion of tenderness he had ever known.

[080-037]In the afternoon he again sat by the bedside. Every symptom of the

[080-038]sufferer’s condition pointed to an approaching end: a face that had

[080-039]grown cadaverous, livid lips, breath drawn in hurrying gasps. Harold

[080-040]despaired of another look of recognition. But as he sat with his

[080-041]forehead resting on his hand Amy touched him; Reardon had turned his

[080-042]face in their direction, and with a conscious gaze.

[080-043]‘I shall never go with you to Greece,’ he said distinctly.

[080-044]There was silence again. Biffen did not move his eyes from the deathly

[080-045]mask; in a minute or two he saw a smile soften its lineaments, and

[080-046]Reardon again spoke:

[080-047]‘How often you and I have quoted it!--“We are such stuff as dreams are

[080-048]made on, and our--“’

[080-049]The remaining words were indistinguishable, and, as if the effort of

[080-050]utterance had exhausted him, his eyes closed, and he sank into lethargy.

[080-051]When he came down from his bedroom on the following morning, Biffen was

[080-052]informed that his friend had died between two and three o’clock. At the

[080-053]same time he received a note in which Amy requested him to come and see

[080-054]her late in the afternoon. He spent the day in a long walk along the

[080-055]eastward cliffs; again the sun shone brilliantly, and the sea was

[080-056]flecked with foam upon its changing green and azure. It seemed to him

[080-057]that he had never before known solitude, even through all the years of

[080-058]his lonely and sad existence.

[080-059]At sunset he obeyed Amy’s summons. He found her calm, but with the signs

[080-060]of long weeping.

[080-061]‘At the last moment,’ she said, ‘he was able to speak to me, and you

[080-062]were mentioned. He wished you to have all that he has left in his room

[080-063]at [[islington|Islington]]. When I come back to London, will you take me there and let

[080-064]me see the room just as when he lived in it? Let the people in the house

[080-065]know what has happened, and that I am responsible for whatever will be

[080-066]owing.’

[080-067]Her resolve to behave composedly gave way as soon as Harold’s broken

[080-068]voice had replied. Hysterical sobbing made further speech from her

[080-069]impossible, and Biffen, after holding her hand reverently for a moment,

[080-070]left her alone.

[080-071]CHAPTER XXXIII. THE SUNNY WAY

[080-072]On an evening of early summer, six months after the death of Edwin

[080-073]Reardon, [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] of the facile pen was bending over his desk, writing

[080-074]rapidly by the warm western light which told that sunset was near. Not

[080-075]far from him sat his younger sister; she was reading, and the book in

[080-076]her hand bore the title, ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer.’

[080-077]‘How will this do?’ Jasper exclaimed, suddenly throwing down his pen.

[080-078]And he read aloud a critical notice of the book with which [[dora-milvain|Dora]] was

[080-079]occupied; a notice of the frankly eulogistic species, beginning with:

[080-080]‘It is seldom nowadays that the luckless reviewer of novels can draw

[080-081]the attention of the public to a new work which is at once powerful and

[080-082]original;’ and ending: ‘The word is a bold one, but we do not hesitate

[080-083]to pronounce this book a masterpiece.’

[080-084]‘Is that for [[the-current|The Current]]?’ asked Dora, when he had finished.

[080-085]‘No, for [[the-west-end|The West End]]. [[fadge|Fadge]] won’t allow anyone but himself to be lauded

[080-086]in that style. I may as well do the notice for The Current now, as I’ve

[080-087]got my hand in.’

[080-088]He turned to his desk again, and before daylight failed him had produced

[080-089]a piece of more cautious writing, very favourable on the whole, but with

[080-090]reserves and slight censures. This also he read to Dora.

[080-091]‘You wouldn’t suspect they were written by the same man, eh?’

[080-092]‘No. You have changed the style very skilfully.’

[080-093]‘I doubt if they’ll be much use. Most people will fling the book down

[080-094]with yawns before they’re half through the first volume. If I knew a

[080-095]doctor who had many cases of insomnia in hand, I would recommend “Mr

[080-096]Bailey” to him as a specific.’

[080-097]‘Oh, but it is really clever, Jasper!’

[080-098]‘Not a doubt of it. I half believe what I have written. And if only we

[080-099]could get it mentioned in a leader or two, and so on, old Biffen’s fame

[080-100]would be established with the better sort of readers. But he won’t

[080-101]sell three hundred copies. I wonder whether Robertson would let me do a

[080-102]notice for his paper?’

[080-103]‘Biffen ought to be grateful to you, if he knew,’ said Dora, laughing.

[080-104]‘Yet, now, there are people who would cry out that this kind of thing is

[080-105]disgraceful. It’s nothing of the kind. Speaking seriously, we know that

[080-106]a really good book will more likely than not receive fair treatment from

[080-107]two or three reviewers; yes, but also more likely than not it will be

[080-108]swamped in the flood of literature that pours forth week after week, and

[080-109]won’t have attention fixed long enough upon it to establish its repute.

[080-110]The struggle for existence among books is nowadays as severe as among

[080-111]men. If a writer has friends connected with the press, it is the plain

[080-112]duty of those friends to do their utmost to help him. What matter if

[080-113]they exaggerate, or even lie? The simple, sober truth has no chance

[080-114]whatever of being listened to, and it’s only by volume of shouting that

[080-115]the ear of the public is held. What use is it to Biffen if his work

[080-116]struggles to slow recognition ten years hence? Besides, as I say, the

[080-117]growing flood of literature swamps everything but works of primary

[080-118][[literary-genius|genius]]. If a clever and conscientious book does not spring to [[literary-ambition|success]]

[080-119]at once, there’s precious small chance that it will survive. Suppose it

[080-120]were possible for me to write a round dozen reviews of this book, in as

[080-121]many different papers, I would do it with satisfaction. Depend upon

[080-122]it, this kind of thing will be done on that scale before long. And

[080-123]it’s quite natural. A man’s friends must be helped, by whatever means,

[080-124]_quocunque modo_, as Biffen himself would say.’

[080-125]‘I dare say he doesn’t even think of you as a friend now.’

[080-126]‘Very likely not. It’s ages since I saw him. But there’s much

[080-127]magnanimity in my character, as I have often told you. It delights me to

[080-128]be generous, whenever I can afford it.’

[080-129]Dusk was gathering about them. As they sat talking, there came a tap at

[080-130]the door, and the summons to enter was obeyed by [[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]].

[080-131]‘I was passing,’ he said in his respectful voice, ‘and couldn’t resist

[080-132]the temptation.’

[080-133]Jasper struck a match and lit the lamp. In this clearer light Whelpdale

[080-134]was exhibited as a young man of greatly improved exterior; he wore a

[080-135]cream-coloured waistcoat, a necktie of subtle hue, and delicate gloves;

[080-136]prosperity breathed from his whole person. It was, in fact, only a

[080-137]moderate prosperity to which he had as yet attained, but the future

[080-138]beckoned to him flatteringly.

[080-139]Early in this year, his enterprise as ‘literary adviser’ had brought

[080-140]him in contact with a man of some pecuniary resources, who proposed to

[080-141]establish an agency for the convenience of authors who were not skilled

[080-142]in disposing of their productions to the best advantage. Under the name

[080-143]of Fleet & Co., this business was shortly set on foot, and Whelpdale’s

[080-144]services were retained on satisfactory terms. The birth of the syndicate

[080-145]system had given new scope to literary agencies, and Mr Fleet was a man

[080-146]of keen eye for commercial opportunities.

[080-147]‘Well, have you read Biffen’s book?’ asked Jasper.

[080-148]‘Wonderful, isn’t it! A work of genius, I am convinced. Ha! you have it

[080-149]there, Miss Dora. But I’m afraid it is hardly for you.’

[080-150]‘And why not, Mr Whelpdale?’

[080-151]‘You should only read of beautiful things, of happy lives. This book

[080-152]must depress you.’

[080-153]‘But why will you imagine me such a feeble-minded person?’ asked Dora.

[080-154]‘You have so often spoken like this. I have really no ambition to be a

[080-155]doll of such superfine wax.’

[080-156]The habitual flatterer looked deeply concerned.

[080-157]‘Pray forgive me!’ he murmured humbly, leaning forwards towards the girl

[080-158]with eyes which deprecated her displeasure. ‘I am very far indeed from

[080-159]attributing weakness to you. It was only the natural, unreflecting

[080-160]impulse; one finds it so difficult to associate you, even as merely a

[080-161]reader, with such squalid scenes.

[080-162]The ignobly decent, as poor Biffen calls it, is so very far from that

[080-163]sphere in which you are naturally at home.’

[080-164]There was some slight affectation in his language, but the tone attested

[080-165]sincere feeling. Jasper was watching him with half an eye, and glancing

[080-166]occasionally at Dora.

[080-167]‘No doubt,’ said the latter, ‘it’s my story in The English Girl that

[080-168]inclines you to think me a goody-goody sort of young woman.’

[080-169]‘So far from that, Miss Dora, I was only waiting for an opportunity to

[080-170]tell you how exceedingly delighted I have been with the last two weeks’

[080-171]instalments. In all seriousness, I consider that story of yours the best

[080-172]thing of the kind that ever came under my notice. You seem to me to

[080-173]have discovered a new genre; such writing as this has surely never been

[080-174]offered to girls, and all the readers of the paper must be immensely

[080-175]grateful to you. I run eagerly to buy the paper each week; I assure you

[080-176]I do. The stationer thinks I purchase it for a sister, I suppose. But

[080-177]each section of the story seems to be better than the last. Mark the

[080-178]prophecy which I now make: when this tale is published in a volume its

[080-179][[literary-success|success]] will be great. You will be recognised, Miss Dora, as the new

[080-180]writer for modern English girls.’
