# Section 77

[076-001]So for a minute or two he lay. Then he was able to stand, to survey

[076-002]his position, and to walk along by the parapet. He looked down upon the

[076-003]surging and shouting crowd in Clipstone Street, but could see it only at

[076-004]intervals, owing to the smoke that rolled from the front windows below

[076-005]him.

[076-006]What he had now to do he understood perfectly. This roof was divided

[076-007]from those on either hand by a stack of chimneys; to get round the end

[076-008]of these stacks was impossible, or at all events too dangerous a feat

[076-009]unless it were the last resource, but by climbing to the apex of the

[076-010]slates he would be able to reach the chimney-pots, to drag himself up

[076-011]to them, and somehow to tumble over on to the safer side. To this

[076-012]undertaking he forthwith addressed himself. Without difficulty he

[076-013]reached the ridge; standing on it he found that only by stretching his

[076-014]arm to the utmost could he grip the top of a chimney-pot. Had he the

[076-015]strength necessary to raise himself by such a hold? And suppose the pot

[076-016]broke?

[076-017]His life was still in danger; the increasing volumes of smoke warned him

[076-018]that in a few minutes the uppermost storey might be in flames. He

[076-019]took off his overcoat to allow himself more freedom of action; the

[076-020]manuscript, now an encumbrance, must precede him over the chimney-stack,

[076-021]and there was only one way of effecting that. With care he stowed

[076-022]the papers into the pockets of the coat; then he rolled the garment

[076-023]together, tied it up in its own sleeves, took a deliberate aim--and the

[076-024]bundle was for the present in safety.

[076-025]Now for the gymnastic endeavour. Standing on tiptoe, he clutched the

[076-026]rim of the chimney-pot, and strove to raise himself. The hold was firm

[076-027]enough, but his arms were far too puny to perform such work, even

[076-028]when death would be the penalty of failure. Too long he had lived on

[076-029]insufficient food and sat over the debilitating desk. He swung this way

[076-030]and that, trying to throw one of his knees as high as the top of the

[076-031]brickwork, but there was no chance of his succeeding. Dropping on to the

[076-032]slates, he sat there in perturbation.

[076-033]He must cry for help. In front it was scarcely possible to stand by the

[076-034]parapet, owing to the black clouds of smoke, now mingled with sparks;

[076-035]perchance he might attract the notice of some person either in the yards

[076-036]behind or at the back windows of other houses. The night was so obscure

[076-037]that he could not hope to be seen; voice alone must be depended upon,

[076-038]and there was no certainty that it would be heard far enough. Though he

[076-039]stood in his shirt-sleeves in a bitter wind no sense of cold affected

[076-040]him; his face was beaded with perspiration drawn forth by his futile

[076-041]struggle to climb. He let himself slide down the rear slope, and,

[076-042]holding by the end of the chimney brickwork, looked into the yards. At

[076-043]the same instant a face appeared to him--that of a man who was trying to

[076-044]obtain a glimpse of this roof from that of the next house by thrusting

[076-045]out his head beyond the block of chimneys.

[076-046]‘Hollo!’ cried the stranger. ‘What are you doing there?’

[076-047]‘Trying to escape, of course. Help me to get on to your roof.’

[076-048]‘By God! I expected to see the fire coming through already. Are you

[076-049]the--as upset his lamp an’ fired the bloomin’ ‘ouse?’

[076-050]‘Not I! He’s lying drunk on the stairs; dead by this time.’

[076-051]‘By God! I wouldn’t have helped you if you’d been him. How are you

[076-052]coming round? Blest if I see! You’ll break your bloomin’ neck if you try

[076-053]this corner. You’ll have to come over the chimneys; wait till I get a

[076-054]ladder.’

[076-055]‘And a rope,’ shouted [[harold-biffen|Biffen]].

[076-056]The man disappeared for five minutes. To Biffen it seemed half an hour;

[076-057]he felt, or imagined he felt, the slates getting hot beneath him, and

[076-058]the smoke was again catching his breath. But at length there was a shout

[076-059]from the top of the chimney-stack. The rescuer had seated himself on one

[076-060]of the pots, and was about to lower on Biffen’s side a ladder which had

[076-061]enabled him to ascend from the other. Biffen planted the lowest rung

[076-062]very carefully on the ridge of the roof, climbed as lightly as possible,

[076-063]got a footing between two pots; the ladder was then pulled over, and

[076-064]both men descended in safety.

[076-065]‘Have you seen a coat lying about here?’ was Biffen’s first question. ‘I

[076-066]threw mine over.’

[076-067]‘What did you do that for?’

[076-068]‘There are some valuable papers in the pockets.’

[076-069]They searched in vain; on neither side of the roof was the coat

[076-070]discoverable.

[076-071]‘You must have pitched it into the street,’ said the man.

[076-072]This was a terrible blow; Biffen forgot his rescue from destruction

[076-073]in lament for the loss of his manuscript. He would have pursued the

[076-074]fruitless search, but his companion, who feared that the fire might

[076-075]spread to adjoining houses, insisted on his passing through the

[076-076]trap-door and descending the stairs.’If the coat fell into the street,’

[076-077]Biffen said, when they were down on the ground floor, ‘of course it’s

[076-078]lost; it would be stolen at once. But may not it have fallen into your

[076-079]back yard?’

[076-080]He was standing in the midst of a cluster of alarmed people, who stared

[076-081]at him in astonishment, for the reek through which he had fought his way

[076-082]had given him the aspect of a sweep. His suggestion prompted someone to

[076-083]run into the yard, with the result that a muddy bundle was brought in

[076-084]and exhibited to him.

[076-085]‘Is this your coat, Mister?’

[076-086]‘Heaven be thanked! That’s it! There are valuable papers in the

[076-087]pockets.’

[076-088]He unrolled the garment, felt to make sure that ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]]’ was safe,

[076-089]and finally put it on.

[076-090]‘Will anyone here let me sit down in a room and give me a drink of

[076-091]water?’ he asked, feeling now as if he must drop with exhaustion.

[076-092]The man who had rescued him performed this further kindness, and for

[076-093]half an hour, whilst tumult indescribable raged about him, Biffen sat

[076-094]recovering his strength. By that time the firemen were hard at work, but

[076-095]one floor of the burning house had already fallen through, and it was

[076-096]probable that nothing but the shell would be saved. After giving a full

[076-097]account of himself to the people among whom he had come, Harold declared

[076-098]his intention of departing; his need of repose was imperative, and he

[076-099]could not hope for it in this proximity to the fire. As he had no [[money-in-literature|money]],

[076-100]his only course was to inquire for a room at some house in the immediate

[076-101]neighbourhood, where the people would receive him in a charitable

[076-102]spirit.

[076-103]With the aid of the police he passed to where the crowd was thinner, and

[076-104]came out into Cleveland Street. Here most of the house-doors were open,

[076-105]and he made several applications for hospitality, but either his story

[076-106]was doubted or his grimy appearance predisposed people against him. At

[076-107]length, when again his strength was all but at an end, he made appeal to

[076-108]a policeman.

[076-109]‘Surely you can tell,’ he protested, after explaining his position,

[076-110]‘that I don’t want to cheat anybody. I shall have money to-morrow. If

[076-111]no one will take me in you must haul me on some charge to the

[076-112]police-station; I shall have to lie down on the pavement in a minute.’

[076-113]The officer recognised a man who was standing half-dressed on a

[076-114]threshold close by; he stepped up to him and made representations

[076-115]which were successful. In a few minutes Biffen took possession of an

[076-116]underground room furnished as a bedchamber, which he agreed to rent for

[076-117]a week. His landlord was not ungracious, and went so far as to supply

[076-118]him with warm water, that he might in a measure cleanse himself. This

[076-119]operation rapidly performed, the hapless author flung himself into bed,

[076-120]and before long was fast asleep.

[076-121]When he went upstairs about nine o’clock in the morning he discovered

[076-122]that his host kept an oil-shop.

[076-123]‘Lost everything, have you?’ asked the man sympathetically.

[076-124]‘Everything, except the clothes I wear and some papers that I managed to

[076-125]save. All my books burnt!’

[076-126]Biffen shook his head dolorously.

[076-127]‘Your account-books!’ cried the dealer in oil. ‘Dear, dear!--and what

[076-128]might your business be?’

[076-129]The author corrected this misapprehension. In the end he was invited to

[076-130]break his fast, which he did right willingly. Then, with assurances

[076-131]that he would return before nightfall, he left the house. His steps were

[076-132]naturally first directed to Clipstone Street; the familiar abode was a

[076-133]gruesome ruin, still smoking. Neighbours informed him that Mr Briggs’s

[076-134]body had been brought forth in a horrible condition; but this was the

[076-135]only loss of life that had happened.

[076-136]Thence he struck eastward, and at eleven came to Manville Street,

[076-137][[islington|Islington]]. He found [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] by the fireside, looking very ill, and

[076-138]speaking with hoarseness.

[076-139]‘Another cold?’

[076-140]‘It looks like it. I wish you would take the trouble to go and buy me

[076-141]some vermin-killer. That would suit my case.’

[076-142]‘Then what would suit mine? Behold me, undeniably a philosopher; in the

[076-143]literal sense of the words omnia _mea mecum porto_.’

[076-144]He recounted his adventures, and with such humorous vivacity that when

[076-145]he ceased the two laughed together as if nothing more amusing had ever

[076-146]been heard.

[076-147]‘Ah, but my books, my books!’ exclaimed Biffen, with a genuine groan.

[076-148]‘And all my notes! At one fell swoop! If I didn’t laugh, old friend, I

[076-149]should sit down and cry; indeed I should. All my classics, with years of

[076-150]scribbling in the margins! How am I to buy them again?’

[076-151]‘You rescued “Mr Bailey.” He must repay you.’

[076-152]Biffen had already laid the manuscript on the table; it was dirty and

[076-153]crumpled, but not to such an extent as to render copying necessary.

[076-154]Lovingly he smoothed the pages and set them in order, then he wrapped

[076-155]the whole in a piece of brown paper which Reardon supplied, and wrote

[076-156]upon it the address of a firm of [[publishing-trade|publishers]].

[076-157]‘Have you note-paper? I’ll write to them; impossible to call in my

[076-158]present guise.’

[076-159]Indeed his attire was more like that of a bankrupt costermonger than of

[076-160]a man of letters. Collar he had none, for the griminess of that he wore

[076-161]last night had necessitated its being thrown aside; round his throat

[076-162]was a dirty handkerchief. His coat had been brushed, but its recent

[076-163]experiences had brought it one stage nearer to that dissolution which

[076-164]must very soon be its fate. His grey trousers were now black, and his

[076-165]boots looked as if they had not been cleaned for weeks.

[076-166]‘Shall I say anything about the character of the book?’ he asked,

[076-167]seating himself with pen and paper. ‘Shall I hint that it deals with the

[076-168]ignobly decent?’

[076-169]‘Better let them form their own judgment,’ replied Reardon, in his

[076-170]hoarse voice.

[076-171]‘Then I’ll just say that I submit to them a novel of modern life, the

[076-172]scope of which is in some degree indicated by its title. Pity they can’t

[076-173]know how nearly it became a holocaust, and that I risked my life to save

[076-174]it. If they’re good enough to accept it I’ll tell them the story. And

[076-175]now, Reardon, I’m ashamed of myself, but can you without inconvenience

[076-176]lend me ten shillings?’

[076-177]‘Easily.’

[076-178]‘I must write to two pupils, to inform them of my change of

[076-179]address--from garret to cellar. And I must ask help from my prosperous

[076-180]brother. He gives it me unreluctantly, I know, but I am always loth to

[076-181]apply to him. May I use your paper for these purposes?’

[076-182]The brother of whom he spoke was employed in a house of business at

[076-183]Liverpool; the two had not met for years, but they corresponded,

[076-184]and were on terms such as Harold indicated. When he had finished his

[076-185]letters, and had received the half-sovereign from Reardon, he went his

[076-186]way to deposit the brown-paper parcel at the publishers’. The clerk who

[076-187]received it from his hands probably thought that the author might have

[076-188]chosen a more respectable messenger.
