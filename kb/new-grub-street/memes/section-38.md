# Section 38

[037-001]He opened his cupboard, and brought out a loaf of bread and a saucer of

[037-002]dripping, with salt and pepper.

[037-003]‘Better dripping this than I’ve had for a long time. I get it at Mr

[037-004]Bailey’s--that isn’t his real name, of course. He assures me it comes

[037-005]from a large hotel where his wife’s sister is a kitchen-maid, and that

[037-006]it’s perfectly pure; they very often mix flour with it, you know, and

[037-007]perhaps more obnoxious things that an economical man doesn’t care

[037-008]to reflect upon. Now, with a little pepper and salt, this bread and

[037-009]dripping is as appetising food as I know. I often make a dinner of it.’

[037-010]‘I have done the same myself before now. Do you ever buy pease-pudding?’

[037-011]‘I should think so! I get magnificent pennyworths at a shop in Cleveland

[037-012]Street, of a very rich quality indeed. Excellent faggots they have

[037-013]there, too. I’ll give you a supper of them some night before you go.’

[037-014][[harold-biffen|Biffen]] rose to enthusiasm in the contemplation of these dainties.

[037-015]He ate his bread and dripping with knife and fork; this always made the

[037-016]fare seem more substantial.

[037-017]‘Is it very cold out?’ he asked, rising from the table. ‘Need I put my

[037-018]overcoat on?’

[037-019]This overcoat, purchased second-hand three years ago, hung on a

[037-020]door-nail. Comparative ease of circumstances had restored to the

[037-021]realist his ordinary indoor garment--a morning coat of the cloth called

[037-022]diagonal, rather large for him, but in better preservation than the

[037-023]other articles of his attire.

[037-024][[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] judging the overcoat necessary, his friend carefully brushed it

[037-025]and drew it on with a caution which probably had reference to

[037-026]starting seams. Then he put into the pocket his pipe, his pouch, his

[037-027]tobacco-stopper, and his matches, murmuring to himself a Greek iambic

[037-028]line which had come into his head a propos of nothing obvious.

[037-029]‘Go out,’ he said, ‘and then I’ll extinguish the lamp. Mind the second

[037-030]step down, as usual.’

[037-031]They issued into Clipstone Street, turned northward, crossed Euston

[037-032]Road, and came into Albany Street, where, in a house of decent exterior,

[037-033][[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]] had his present abode. A girl who opened the door requested

[037-034]them to walk up to the topmost storey.

[037-035]A cheery voice called to them from within the room at which they

[037-036]knocked. This lodging spoke more distinctly of civilisation than that

[037-037]inhabited by Biffen; it contained the minimum supply of furniture needed

[037-038]to give it somewhat the appearance of a study, but the articles were in

[037-039]good condition. One end of the room was concealed by a chintz curtain;

[037-040]scrutiny would have discovered behind the draping the essential

[037-041]equipments of a bedchamber.

[037-042]Mr Whelpdale sat by the fire, smoking a cigar. He was a plain-featured

[037-043]but graceful and refined-looking man of thirty, with wavy chestnut

[037-044]hair and a trimmed beard which became him well. At present he wore a

[037-045]dressing-gown and was without collar.

[037-046]‘Welcome, gents both!’ he cried facetiously. ‘Ages since I saw you,

[037-047]Reardon. I’ve been reading your new book. Uncommonly good things in it

[037-048]here and there--uncommonly good.’

[037-049]Whelpdale had the weakness of being unable to tell a disagreeable

[037-050]truth, and a tendency to flattery which had always made Reardon rather

[037-051]uncomfortable in his society. Though there was no need whatever of his

[037-052]mentioning ‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]],’ he preferred to frame smooth fictions rather

[037-053]than keep a silence which might be construed as unfavourable [[reviewing-criticism|criticism]].

[037-054]‘In the last volume,’ he went on, ‘I think there are one or two things

[037-055]as good as you ever did; I do indeed.’

[037-056]Reardon made no acknowledgment of these remarks. They irritated him, for

[037-057]he knew their insincerity. Biffen, understanding his friend’s silence,

[037-058]struck in on another subject.

[037-059]‘Who is this lady of whom you write to me?’

[037-060]‘Ah, quite a story! I’m going to be married, Reardon. A serious

[037-061][[literary-marriage|marriage]]. Light your pipes, and I’ll tell you all about it. Startled

[037-062]you, I suppose, Biffen? Unlikely news, eh? Some people would call it a

[037-063]rash step, I dare say. We shall just take another room in this house,

[037-064]that’s all. I think I can count upon an income of a couple of guineas

[037-065]a week, and I have plans without end that are pretty sure to bring in

[037-066]coin.’

[037-067]Reardon did not care to smoke, but Biffen lit his pipe and waited with

[037-068]grave interest for the romantic narrative. Whenever he heard of a poor

[037-069]man’s persuading a woman to share his [[literary-poverty|poverty]] he was eager of details;

[037-070]perchance he himself might yet have that heavenly good fortune.

[037-071]‘Well,’ began Whelpdale, crossing his legs and watching a wreath he had

[037-072]just puffed from the cigar, ‘you know all about my literary advisership.

[037-073]The business goes on reasonably well. I’m going to extend it in ways

[037-074]I’ll explain to you presently. [[About]] six weeks ago I received a letter

[037-075]from a lady who referred to my advertisements, and said she had the

[037-076]manuscript of a novel which she would like to offer for my opinion. Two

[037-077][[publishing-trade|publishers]] had refused it, but one with complimentary phrases, and she

[037-078]hoped it mightn’t be impossible to put the thing into acceptable shape.

[037-079]Of course I wrote optimistically, and the manuscript was sent to me.

[037-080]Well, it wasn’t actually bad--by Jove! you should have seen some of

[037-081]the things I have been asked to recommend to publishers! It wasn’t

[037-082]hopelessly bad by any means, and I gave serious thought to it. After

[037-083]exchange of several letters I asked the authoress to come and see me,

[037-084]that we might save postage stamps and talk things over. She hadn’t

[037-085]given me her address: I had to direct to a stationer’s in [[bayswater|Bayswater]]. She

[037-086]agreed to come, and did come. I had formed a sort of idea, but of course

[037-087]I was quite wrong. Imagine my excitement when there came in a

[037-088]very beautiful girl, a tremendously interesting girl, about

[037-089]one-and-twenty--just the kind of girl that most strongly appeals to

[037-090]me; dark, pale, rather consumptive-looking, slender--no, there’s no

[037-091]describing her; there really isn’t! You must wait till you see her.’

[037-092]‘I hope the consumption was only a figure of speech,’ remarked Biffen in

[037-093]his grave way.

[037-094]‘Oh, there’s nothing serious the matter, I think. A slight cough, poor

[037-095]girl.’

[037-096]‘The deuce!’ interjected Reardon.

[037-097]‘Oh, nothing, nothing! It’ll be all right. Well, now, of course we

[037-098]talked over the story--in good earnest, you know. Little by little I

[037-099]induced her to speak of herself--this, after she’d come two or three

[037-100]times--and she told me lamentable things. She was absolutely alone in

[037-101][[london|London]], and hadn’t had sufficient food for weeks; had sold all she could

[037-102]of her clothing; and so on. Her home was in Birmingham; she had been

[037-103]driven away by the brutality of a stepmother; a friend lent her a few

[037-104]pounds, and she came to London with an unfinished novel. Well, you know,

[037-105]this kind of thing would be enough to make me soft-hearted to any girl,

[037-106]let alone one who, to begin with, was absolutely my ideal. When she

[037-107]began to express a fear that I was giving too much time to her, that she

[037-108]wouldn’t be able to pay my fees, and so on, I could restrain myself

[037-109]no longer. On the spot I asked her to marry me. I didn’t practise any

[037-110]deception, mind. I told her I was a poor devil who had failed as a

[037-111]realistic novelist and was earning bread in haphazard ways; and I

[037-112]explained frankly that I thought we might carry on various kinds of

[037-113]business together: she might go on with her novel-writing, and--so on.

[037-114]But she was frightened; I had been too abrupt. That’s a fault of mine,

[037-115]you know; but I was so confoundedly afraid of losing her. And I told her

[037-116]as much, plainly.’

[037-117]Biffen smiled.

[037-118]‘This would be exciting,’ he said, ‘if we didn’t know the end of the

[037-119]story.’

[037-120]‘Yes. Pity I didn’t keep it a secret. Well, she wouldn’t say yes, but

[037-121]I could see that she didn’t absolutely say no. “In any case,” I said,

[037-122]“you’ll let me see you often? Fees be hanged! I’ll work day and night

[037-123]for you. I’ll do my utmost to get your novel accepted.” And I implored

[037-124]her to let me lend her a little [[money-in-literature|money]]. It was very difficult to persuade

[037-125]her, but at last she accepted a few shillings. I could see in her face

[037-126]that she was hungry. Just imagine! A beautiful girl absolutely hungry;

[037-127]it drove me frantic!

[037-128]But that was a great point gained. After that we saw each other almost

[037-129]every day, and at last--she consented! Did indeed! I can hardly believe

[037-130]it yet. We shall be married in a fortnight’s time.’

[037-131]‘I congratulate you,’ said Reardon.

[037-132]‘So do I,’ sighed Biffen.

[037-133]‘The day before yesterday she went to Birmingham to see her father and

[037-134]tell him all about the affair. I agreed with her it was as well; the old

[037-135]fellow isn’t badly off; and he may forgive her for running away, though

[037-136]he’s under his wife’s thumb, it appears. I had a note yesterday. She had

[037-137]gone to a friend’s house for the first day. I hoped to have heard again

[037-138]this morning--must to-morrow, in any case. I live, as you may imagine,

[037-139]in wild excitement. Of course, if the old man stumps up a wedding

[037-140]present, all the better. But I don’t care; we’ll make a living somehow.

[037-141]What do you think I’m writing just now? An author’s Guide. You know the

[037-142]kind of thing; they sell splendidly. Of course I shall make it a good

[037-143]advertisement of my business. Then I have a splendid idea. I’m going to

[037-144]advertise: “Novel-writing taught in ten lessons!” What do you think

[037-145]of that? No swindle; not a bit of it. I am quite capable of giving the

[037-146]ordinary man or woman ten very useful lessons. I’ve been working out the

[037-147]scheme; it would amuse you vastly, Reardon. The first lesson deals with

[037-148]the question of subjects, local colour--that kind of thing. I gravely

[037-149]advise people, if they possibly can, to write of the wealthy middle

[037-150][[social-class|class]]; that’s the popular subject, you know. Lords and ladies are all

[037-151]very well, but the real thing to take is a story about people who have

[037-152]no titles, but live in good Philistine style. I urge study of horsey

[037-153]matters especially; that’s very important. You must be well up, too,

[037-154]in military grades, know about Sandhurst, and so on. Boating is an

[037-155]important topic. You see? Oh, I shall make a great thing of this. I

[037-156]shall teach my wife carefully, and then let her advertise lessons to

[037-157]girls; they’ll prefer coming to a woman, you know.’

[037-158]Biffen leant back and laughed noisily.

[037-159]‘How much shall you charge for the course?’ asked Reardon.

[037-160]‘That’ll depend. I shan’t refuse a guinea or two; but some people may be

[037-161]made to pay five, perhaps.’

[037-162]Someone knocked at the door, and a voice said:

[037-163]‘A letter for you, Mr Whelpdale.’

[037-164]He started up, and came back into the room with face illuminated.

[037-165]‘Yes, it’s from Birmingham; posted this morning. Look what an exquisite

[037-166]hand she writes!’

[037-167]He tore open the envelope. In delicacy Reardon and Biffen averted their

[037-168]eyes. There was silence for a minute, then a strange ejaculation from

[037-169]Whelpdale caused his friends to look up at him. He had gone pale, and

[037-170]was frowning at the sheet of paper which trembled in his hand.

[037-171]‘No bad news, I hope?’ Biffen ventured to say.

[037-172]Whelpdale let himself sink into a chair.

[037-173]‘Now if this isn’t too bad!’ he exclaimed in a thick voice. ‘If

[037-174]this isn’t monstrously unkind! I never heard anything so gross as

[037-175]this--never!’

[037-176]The two waited, trying not to smile.

[037-177]‘She writes--that she has met an old lover--in Birmingham--that it was

[037-178]with him she had quarrelled-not with her father at all--that she ran

[037-179]away to annoy him and frighten him--that she has made it up again, and

[037-180]they’re going to be married!’
