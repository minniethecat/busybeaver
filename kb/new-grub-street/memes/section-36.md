# Section 36

[035-001]‘Will you let me give one to Mrs [[carter|Carter]]?’

[035-002]‘As you please.’

[035-003]He took one set and added it to the row of his productions which stood

[035-004]on a topmost shelf [[amy-reardon|Amy]] laid her hand upon his shoulder and contemplated

[035-005]the effect of this addition.

[035-006]‘The works of [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]],’ she said, with a smile.

[035-007]‘The work, at all events--rather a different thing, unfortunately. Amy,

[035-008]if only I were back at the time when I wrote “[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]],” and

[035-009]yet had you with me! How full my mind was in those days! Then I had only

[035-010]to look, and I saw something; now I strain my eyes, but can make out

[035-011]nothing more than nebulous grotesques. I used to sit down knowing

[035-012]so well what I had to say; now I strive to invent, and never come at

[035-013]anything. Suppose you pick up a needle with warm, supple fingers; try to

[035-014]do it when your hand is stiff and numb with cold; there’s the difference

[035-015]between my manner of work in those days and what it is now.’

[035-016]‘But you are going to get back your health. You will write better than

[035-017]ever.’

[035-018]‘We shall see. Of course there was a great deal of miserable struggle

[035-019]even then, but I remember it as insignificant compared with the hours of

[035-020]contented work. I seldom did anything in the mornings except think and

[035-021]prepare; towards evening I felt myself getting ready, and at last I sat

[035-022]down with the first lines buzzing in my head. And I used to read a great

[035-023]deal at the same time. Whilst I was writing “On Neutral Ground” I went

[035-024]solidly through the “Divina Commedia,” a canto each day. Very often I

[035-025]wrote till after midnight, but occasionally I got my quantum finished

[035-026]much earlier, and then I used to treat myself to a ramble about the

[035-027]streets. I can recall exactly the places where some of my best ideas

[035-028]came to me. You remember the scene in Prendergast’s lodgings? That

[035-029]flashed on me late one night as I was turning out of Leicester Square

[035-030]into the slum that leads to Clare Market; ah, how well I remember! And

[035-031]I went home to my garret in a state of delightful fever, and scribbled

[035-032]notes furiously before going to bed.’

[035-033]‘Don’t trouble; it’ll all come back to you.’

[035-034]‘But in those days I hadn’t to think of [[money-in-literature|money]]. I could look forward and

[035-035]see provision for my needs. I never asked myself what I should get for

[035-036]the book; I assure you, that never came into my head--never. The work

[035-037]was done for its own sake. No hurry to finish it; if I felt that I

[035-038]wasn’t up to the mark, I just waited till the better mood returned. “On

[035-039]Neutral Ground” took me seven months; now I have to write three volumes

[035-040]in nine weeks, with the lash stinging on my back if I miss a day.’

[035-041]He brooded for a little.

[035-042]‘I suppose there must be some rich man somewhere who has read one or two

[035-043]of my books with a certain interest. If only I could encounter him and

[035-044]tell him plainly what a cursed state I am in, perhaps he would help me

[035-045]to some means of earning a couple of pounds a week. One has heard of

[035-046]such things.’

[035-047]‘In the old days.’

[035-048]‘Yes. I doubt if it ever happens now. Coleridge wouldn’t so easily meet

[035-049]with his Gillman nowadays. Well, I am not a Coleridge, and I don’t ask

[035-050]to be lodged under any man’s roof; but if I could earn money enough to

[035-051]leave me good long evenings unspoilt by fear of the workhouse--’

[035-052]Amy turned away, and presently went to look after her little boy.

[035-053]A few days after this they had a visit from [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]. He came about ten

[035-054]o’clock in the evening.

[035-055]‘I’m not going to stay,’ he announced. ‘But where’s my copy of “Margaret

[035-056]Home”? I am to have one, I suppose?’

[035-057]‘I have no particular desire that you should read it,’ returned Reardon.

[035-058]‘But I HAVE read it, my dear fellow. Got it from the library on the day

[035-059]of publication; I had a suspicion that you wouldn’t send me a copy. But

[035-060]I must possess your opera omnia.’

[035-061]‘Here it is. Hide it away somewhere.--You may as well sit down for a few

[035-062]minutes.’

[035-063]‘I confess I should like to talk about the book, if you don’t mind.

[035-064]It isn’t so utterly and damnably bad as you make out, you know. The

[035-065]misfortune was that you had to make three volumes of it. If I had leave

[035-066]to cut it down to one, it would do you credit.

[035-067]The motive is good enough.’

[035-068]‘Yes. Just good enough to show how badly it’s managed.’

[035-069]Milvain began to expatiate on that well-worn topic, the evils of the

[035-070][[three-volume-system|three-volume system]].

[035-071]‘A triple-headed monster, sucking the blood of English novelists.

[035-072]One might design an allegorical cartoon for a comic literary paper.

[035-073]By-the-bye, why doesn’t such a thing exist?--a weekly paper treating of

[035-074]things and people literary in a facetious spirit. It would be caviare

[035-075]to the general, but might be supported, I should think. The editor would

[035-076]probably be assassinated, though.’

[035-077]‘For anyone in my position,’ said Reardon, ‘how is it possible to

[035-078]abandon the three volumes? It is a question of payment. An author of

[035-079]moderate repute may live on a yearly three-volume novel--I mean the man

[035-080]who is obliged to sell his book out and out, and who gets from one to

[035-081]two hundred pounds for it. But he would have to produce four one-volume

[035-082]novels to obtain the same income; and I doubt whether he could get so

[035-083]many published within the twelve months. And here comes in the benefit

[035-084]of the libraries; from the commercial point of view the libraries are

[035-085]indispensable. Do you suppose the public would support the present

[035-086]number of novelists if each book had to be purchased? A sudden change to

[035-087]that system would throw three-fourths of the novelists out of work.’

[035-088]‘But there’s no reason why the libraries shouldn’t circulate novels in

[035-089]one volume.’

[035-090]‘Profits would be less, I suppose. People would take the minimum

[035-091]subscription.’

[035-092]‘Well, to go to the concrete, what about your own one-volume?’

[035-093]‘All but done.’

[035-094]‘And you’ll offer it to [[jedwood|Jedwood]]? Go and see him personally. He’s a very

[035-095]decent fellow, I believe.’

[035-096]Milvain stayed only half an hour. The days when he was wont to sit and

[035-097]talk at large through a whole evening were no more; partly because of

[035-098]his diminished leisure, but also for a less simple reason--the growth of

[035-099]something like estrangement between him and Reardon.

[035-100]‘You didn’t mention your plans,’ said Amy, when the visitor had been

[035-101]gone some time.

[035-102]‘No.’

[035-103]Reardon was content with the negative, and his wife made no further

[035-104]remark.

[035-105]The result of advertising the flat was that two or three persons called

[035-106]to make inspection. One of them, a man of military appearance, showed

[035-107]himself anxious to come to terms; he was willing to take the tenement

[035-108]from next quarter-day (June), but wished, if possible, to enter upon

[035-109]possession sooner than that.

[035-110]‘Nothing could be better,’ said Amy in colloquy with her husband. ‘If he

[035-111]will pay for the extra time, we shall be only too glad.’

[035-112]Reardon mused and looked gloomy. He could not bring himself to regard

[035-113]the experiment before him with hopefulness, and his heart sank at the

[035-114]thought of parting from Amy.

[035-115]‘You are very anxious to get rid of me,’ he answered, trying to smile.

[035-116]‘Yes, I am,’ she exclaimed; ‘but simply for your own good, as you know

[035-117]very well.’

[035-118]‘Suppose I can’t sell this book?’

[035-119]‘You will have a few pounds. Send your “Pliny” article to [[the-wayside|The Wayside]].

[035-120]If you come to an end of all your money, mother shall lend you some.’

[035-121]‘I am not very likely to do much work in that case.’

[035-122]‘Oh, but you will sell the book. You’ll get twenty pounds for it, and

[035-123]that alone would keep you for three months. Think--three months of the

[035-124]best part of the year at the seaside! Oh, you will do wonders!’

[035-125]The furniture was to be housed at Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s. Neither of them durst

[035-126]speak of selling it; that would have sounded too ominous. As for the

[035-127]locality of Reardon’s retreat, Amy herself had suggested Worthing, which

[035-128]she knew from a visit a few years ago; the advantages were its proximity

[035-129]to [[london|London]], and the likelihood that very cheap lodgings could be found

[035-130]either in the town or near it. One room would suffice for the hapless

[035-131]author, and his expenses, beyond a trifling rent, would be confined to

[035-132]mere food.

[035-133]Oh yes, he might manage on considerably less than a pound a week.

[035-134]Amy was in much better spirits than for a long time; she appeared to

[035-135]have convinced herself that there was no doubt of the issue of this

[035-136]perilous scheme; that her husband would write a notable book, receive a

[035-137]satisfactory price for it, and so re-establish their home. Yet her moods

[035-138]varied greatly. After all, there was delay in the letting of the flat,

[035-139]and this caused her annoyance. It was whilst the negotiations were still

[035-140]pending that she made her call upon Maud and [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]]; Reardon did

[035-141]not know of her intention to visit them until it had been carried out.

[035-142]She mentioned what she had done in almost a casual manner.

[035-143]‘I had to get it over,’ she said, when Reardon exhibited surprise, ‘and

[035-144]I don’t think I made a very favourable impression.’

[035-145]‘You told them, I suppose, what we are going to do?’

[035-146]‘No; I didn’t say a word of it.’

[035-147]‘But why not? It can’t be kept a secret. Milvain will have heard of it

[035-148]already, I should think, from your mother.’

[035-149]‘From mother? But it’s the rarest thing for him to go there. Do you

[035-150]imagine he is a constant visitor? I thought it better to say nothing

[035-151]until the thing is actually done. Who knows what may happen?’

[035-152]She was in a strange, nervous state, and Reardon regarded her uneasily.

[035-153]He talked very little in these days, and passed hours in dark reverie.

[035-154]His book was finished, and he awaited the publisher’s decision.

[035-155]PART THREE

[035-156]CHAPTER XVI. REJECTION

[035-157]One of Reardon’s minor worries at this time was the fear that by chance

[035-158]he might come upon a review of ‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]].’ Since the publication of

[035-159]his first book he had avoided as far as possible all knowledge of what

[035-160]the [[reviewing-criticism|critics]] had to say about him; his nervous temperament could not bear

[035-161]the agitation of reading these remarks, which, however inept, define

[035-162]an author and his work to so many people incapable of judging for

[035-163]themselves. No man or woman could tell him anything in the way of praise

[035-164]or blame which he did not already know quite well; commendation was

[035-165]pleasant, but it so often aimed amiss, and censure was for the most part

[035-166]so unintelligent. In the case of this latest novel he dreaded the

[035-167]sight of a review as he would have done a gash from a rusty knife.

[035-168]The judgments could not but be damnatory, and their expression in

[035-169]journalistic phrase would disturb his mind with evil rancour. No one

[035-170]would have insight enough to appreciate the nature and cause of his

[035-171]book’s demerits; every comment would be wide of the mark; sneer,

[035-172]ridicule, trite objection, would but madden him with a sense of

[035-173]injustice.

[035-174]His position was illogical--one result of the moral weakness which was

[035-175]allied with his aesthetic sensibility. Putting aside the worthlessness

[035-176]of current reviewing, the critic of an isolated book has of course

[035-177]nothing to do with its author’s state of mind and body any more than

[035-178]with the condition of his purse. Reardon would have granted this, but he

[035-179]could not command his emotions. He was in passionate revolt against

[035-180]the base necessities which compelled him to put forth work in no way

[035-181]representing his healthy powers, his artistic criterion. Not he had

[035-182]written this book, but his accursed [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. To assail him as the author

[035-183]was, in his feeling, to be guilty of brutal insult. When by ill-hap a

[035-184]notice in one of the daily papers came under his eyes, it made his blood

[035-185]boil with a fierceness of hatred only possible to him in a profoundly

[035-186]morbid condition; he could not steady his hand for half an hour after.

[035-187]Yet this particular critic only said what was quite true--that the novel

[035-188]contained not a single striking scene and not one living character;

[035-189]Reardon had expressed himself about it in almost identical terms. But

[035-190]he saw himself in the position of one sickly and all but destitute man

[035-191]against a relentless world, and every blow directed against him appeared

[035-192]dastardly. He could have cried ‘Coward!’ to the writer who wounded him.
