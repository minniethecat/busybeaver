# Section 67

[066-001]He had worked himself to such intensity of feeling that at length his

[066-002]voice choked and tears burst from his eyes.

[066-003]‘Come out, and let us have a walk,’ said [[harold-biffen|Biffen]].

[066-004]On leaving the house they found themselves in a thick fog, through which

[066-005]trickled drops of warm rain. Nevertheless, they pursued their purpose,

[066-006]and presently were seated in one of the boxes of a small coffee-shop.

[066-007]Their only companion in the place was a cab-driver, who had just

[066-008]finished a meal, and was now nodding into slumber over his plate and

[066-009]cup. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] ordered fried ham and eggs, the luxury of the poor, and

[066-010]when the attendant woman was gone away to execute the order, he burst

[066-011]into excited laughter.

[066-012]‘Here we sit, two literary men! How should we be regarded by--’

[066-013]He named two or three of the successful novelists of the day.

[066-014]‘With what magnificent scorn they would turn from us and our squalid

[066-015]feast! They have never known struggle; not they. They are public-school

[066-016]men, University men, club men, society men. An income of less than three

[066-017]or four hundred a year is inconceivable to them; that seems the minimum

[066-018]for an educated man’s support. It would be small-minded to think of them

[066-019]with rancour, but, by Apollo! I know that we should change places with

[066-020]them if the work we have done were justly weighed against theirs.’

[066-021]‘What does it matter? We are different types of intellectual workers. I

[066-022]think of them savagely now and then, but only when hunger gets a

[066-023]trifle too keen. Their work answers a demand; ours--or mine at all

[066-024]events--doesn’t. They are in touch with the reading multitude; they have

[066-025]the sentiments of the respectable; they write for their [[social-class|class]]. Well, you

[066-026]had your circle of readers, and, if things hadn’t gone against you, by

[066-027]this time you certainly could have counted on your three or four hundred

[066-028]a year.’

[066-029]‘It’s unlikely that I should ever have got more than two hundred pounds

[066-030]for a book; and, to have kept at my best, I must have been content to

[066-031]publish once every two or three years. The position was untenable with

[066-032]no private income. And I must needs marry a wife of dainty instincts!

[066-033]What astounding impudence! No wonder Fate pitched me aside into the

[066-034]gutter.’

[066-035]They ate their ham and eggs, and exhilarated themselves with a cup

[066-036]of chicory--called coffee. Then Biffen drew from the pocket of his

[066-037]venerable overcoat the volume of Euripides he had brought, and their

[066-038]talk turned once more to the land of the sun. Only when the coffee-shop

[066-039]was closed did they go forth again into the foggy street, and at the

[066-040]top of Pentonville Hill they stood for ten minutes debating a metrical

[066-041]effect in one of the Fragments.

[066-042]Day after day Reardon went about with a fever upon him. By evening his

[066-043]pulse was always rapid, and no extremity of weariness brought him a

[066-044]refreshing sleep. In conversation he seemed either depressed or

[066-045]excited, more often the latter. Save when attending to his duties at the

[066-046]hospital, he made no pretence of employing himself; if at home, he sat

[066-047]for hours without opening a book, and his walks, excepting when they led

[066-048]him to Clipstone Street, were aimless.

[066-049]The hours of postal delivery found him waiting in an anguish of

[066-050]suspense. At eight o’clock each morning he stood by his window,

[066-051]listening for the postman’s knock in the street. As it approached he

[066-052]went out to the head of the stairs, and if the knock sounded at the door

[066-053]of his house, he leaned over the banisters, trembling in expectation.

[066-054]But the letter was never for him. When his agitation had subsided he

[066-055]felt glad of the disappointment, and laughed and sang.

[066-056]One day [[carter|Carter]] appeared at the City Road establishment, and made an

[066-057]opportunity of speaking to his clerk in private.

[066-058]‘I suppose,’ he said with a smile, ‘they’ll have to look out for someone

[066-059]else at Croydon?’

[066-060]‘By no means! The thing is settled. I go at Christmas.’

[066-061]‘You really mean that?’

[066-062]‘Undoubtedly.’

[066-063]Seeing that Reardon was not disposed even to allude to private

[066-064]circumstances, the secretary said no more, and went away convinced that

[066-065]misfortunes had turned the poor fellow’s brain.

[066-066]Wandering in the city, about this time, Reardon encountered his friend

[066-067]the realist.

[066-068]‘Would you like to meet Sykes?’ asked Biffen. ‘I am just going to see

[066-069]him.’

[066-070]‘Where does he live?’

[066-071]‘In some indiscoverable hole. To save fuel, he spends his mornings at

[066-072]some reading-rooms; the admission is only a penny, and there he can see

[066-073]all the papers and do his writing and enjoy a grateful temperature.’

[066-074]They repaired to the haunt in question. A flight of stairs brought them

[066-075]to a small room in which were exposed the daily newspapers; another

[066-076]ascent, and they were in a room devoted to magazines, chess, and

[066-077]refreshments; yet another, and they reached the department of weekly

[066-078]publications; lastly, at the top of the house, they found a lavatory,

[066-079]and a chamber for the use of those who desired to write. The walls

[066-080]of this last retreat were of blue plaster and sloped inwards from the

[066-081]floor; along them stood school desks with benches, and in one place was

[066-082]suspended a ragged and dirty card announcing that paper and envelopes

[066-083]could be purchased downstairs. An enormous basket full of waste-paper,

[066-084]and a small stove, occupied two corners; ink blotches, satirical

[066-085]designs, and much scribbling in pen and pencil served for mural

[066-086]adornment. From the adjacent lavatory came sounds of splashing and

[066-087]spluttering, and the busy street far below sent up its confused noises.

[066-088]Two persons only sat at the desks. One was a hunger-bitten, out-of-work

[066-089]clerk, evidently engaged in replying to advertisements; in front of him

[066-090]lay two or three finished letters, and on the ground at his feet were

[066-091]several crumpled sheets of note-paper, representing abortive essays in

[066-092]composition. The other man, also occupied with the pen, looked about

[066-093]forty years old, and was clad in a very rusty suit of tweeds; on the

[066-094]bench beside him lay a grey overcoat and a silk hat which had for

[066-095]some time been moulting. His face declared the habit to which he was a

[066-096]victim, but it had nothing repulsive in its lineaments and expression;

[066-097]on the contrary, it was pleasing, amiable, and rather quaint. At this

[066-098]moment no one would have doubted his sobriety. With coat-sleeve turned

[066-099]back, so as to give free play to his right hand and wrist, revealing

[066-100]meanwhile a flannel shirt of singular colour, and with his collar

[066-101]unbuttoned (he wore no tie) to leave his throat at ease as he bent

[066-102]myopically over the paper, he was writing at express speed, evidently

[066-103]in the full rush of the ardour of composition. The veins of his forehead

[066-104]were dilated, and his chin pushed forward in a way that made one think

[066-105]of a racing horse.

[066-106]‘Are you too busy to talk?’ asked Biffen, going to his side.

[066-107]‘I am! Upon my soul I am!’ exclaimed the other looking up in alarm. ‘For

[066-108]the love of Heaven don’t put me out! A quarter of an hour!’

[066-109]‘All right. I’ll come up again.’

[066-110]The friends went downstairs and turned over the papers.

[066-111]‘Now let’s try him again,’ said Biffen, when considerably more than

[066-112]the requested time had elapsed. They went up, and found Mr Sykes in an

[066-113]attitude of melancholy meditation. He had turned back his coat

[066-114]sleeve, had buttoned his collar, and was eyeing the slips of completed

[066-115]manuscript. Biffen presented his companion, and Mr Sykes greeted the

[066-116]novelist with much geniality.

[066-117]‘What do you think this is?’ he exclaimed, pointing to his work. ‘The

[066-118]first instalment of my autobiography for the “Shropshire Weekly Herald.”

[066-119]Anonymous, of course, but strictly veracious, with the omission of

[066-120]sundry little personal failings which are nothing to the point. I call

[066-121]it “Through the Wilds of Literary [[london|London]].” An old friend of mine edits

[066-122]the “Herald,” and I’m indebted to him for the suggestion.’

[066-123]His voice was a trifle husky, but he spoke like a man of education.

[066-124]‘Most people will take it for fiction. I wish I had inventive power

[066-125]enough to write fiction anything like it. I have published novels, Mr

[066-126]Reardon, but my experience in that branch of literature was peculiar--as

[066-127]I may say it has been in most others to which I have applied myself. My

[066-128]first stories were written for “The Young Lady’s Favourite,” and most

[066-129]remarkable productions they were, I promise you. That was fifteen years

[066-130]ago, in the days of my versatility. I could throw off my supplemental

[066-131]novelette of fifteen thousand words without turning a hair, and

[066-132]immediately after it fall to, fresh as a daisy, on the “Illustrated

[066-133]History of the United States,” which I was then doing for Edward

[066-134]Coghlan. But presently I thought myself too good for the “Favourite”; in

[066-135]an evil day I began to write three-volume novels, aiming at [[literary-success|reputation]].

[066-136]It wouldn’t do. I persevered for five years, and made about five

[066-137]failures. Then I went back to Bowring. “Take me on again, old man, will

[066-138]you?” Bowring was a man of few words; he said, “Blaze away, my boy.” And

[066-139]I tried to. But it was no use; I had got out of the style; my writing

[066-140]was too literary by a long chalk. For a whole year I deliberately strove

[066-141]to write badly, but Bowring was so pained with the feebleness of my

[066-142]efforts that at last he sternly bade me avoid his sight. “What the

[066-143]devil,” he roared one day, “do you mean by sending me stories about

[066-144]men and women? You ought to know better than that, a fellow of your

[066-145]experience!” So I had to give it up, and there was an end of my career

[066-146]as a writer of fiction.’

[066-147]He shook his head sadly.

[066-148]‘Biffen,’ he continued, ‘when I first made his acquaintance, had an idea

[066-149]of writing for the working classes; and what do you think he was going

[066-150]to offer them? Stories about the working classes! Nay, never hang your

[066-151]head for it, old boy; it was excusable in the days of your youth. Why,

[066-152]Mr Reardon, as no doubt you know well enough, nothing can induce working

[066-153]men or women to read stories that treat of their own world. They are

[066-154]the most consumed idealists in creation, especially the women. Again

[066-155]and again work-girls have said to me: “Oh, I don’t like that book; it’s

[066-156]nothing but real life.”’

[066-157]‘It’s the fault of women in general,’ remarked Reardon.

[066-158]‘So it is, but it comes out with delicious naivete in the working

[066-159]classes. Now, educated people like to read of scenes that are familiar

[066-160]to them, though I grant you that the picture must be idealised if you’re

[066-161]to appeal to more than one in a thousand. The working classes detest

[066-162]anything that tries to represent their daily life. It isn’t because that

[066-163]life is too painful; no, no; it’s downright snobbishness. Dickens goes

[066-164]down only with the best of them, and then solely because of his strength

[066-165]in farce and his melodrama.’

[066-166]Presently the three went out together, and had dinner at an a la mode

[066-167]beef shop. Mr Sykes ate little, but took copious libations of porter at

[066-168]twopence a pint. When the meal was over he grew taciturn.

[066-169]‘Can you walk westwards?’ Biffen asked.

[066-170]‘I’m afraid not, afraid not. In fact I have an appointment at two--at

[066-171]Aldgate station.’

[066-172]They parted from him.

[066-173]‘Now he’ll go and soak till he’s unconscious,’ said Biffen. ‘Poor

[066-174]fellow! Pity he ever earns anything at all. The workhouse would be

[066-175]better, I should think.’

[066-176]‘No, no! Let a man drink himself to death rather. I have a horror of the

[066-177]workhouse. Remember the clock at [[marylebone|Marylebone]] I used to tell you about.’

[066-178]‘Unphilosophic. I don’t think I should be unhappy in the workhouse.

[066-179]I should have a certain satisfaction in the thought that I had forced

[066-180]society to support me. And then the absolute freedom from care! Why,

[066-181]it’s very much the same as being a man of independent fortune.’
