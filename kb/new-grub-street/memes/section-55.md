# Section 55

[054-001]‘And if we are not so unfortunate as to fall in love with an

[054-002]incompatible,’ added [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]], laughing.

[054-003]‘Well, that is a form of unreason--a blind desire which science could

[054-004]explain in each case. I rejoice that I am not subject to that form of

[054-005]epilepsy.’

[054-006]‘You positively never were in love!’

[054-007]‘As you understand it, never. But I have felt a very distinct

[054-008]preference.’

[054-009]‘Based on what you think compatibility?’

[054-010]‘Yes. Not strong enough to make me lose sight of prudence and advantage.

[054-011]No, not strong enough for that.’

[054-012]He seemed to be reassuring himself.

[054-013]‘Then of course that can’t be called love,’ said Whelpdale.

[054-014]‘Perhaps not. But, as I told you, a preference of this kind can be

[054-015]heightened into emotion, if one chooses. In the case of which I am

[054-016]thinking it easily might be. And I think it very improbable indeed that

[054-017]I should repent it if anything led me to indulge such an impulse.’

[054-018]Whelpdale smiled.

[054-019]‘This is very interesting. I hope it may lead to something.’

[054-020]‘I don’t think it will. I am far more likely to marry some woman for

[054-021]whom I have no preference, but who can serve me materially.’

[054-022]‘I confess that amazes me. I know the value of [[money-in-literature|money]] as well as you do,

[054-023]but I wouldn’t marry a rich woman for whom I had no preference. By Jove,

[054-024]no!’

[054-025]‘Yes, yes. You are a consistent sentimentalist.’

[054-026]‘Doomed to perpetual disappointment,’ said the other, looking

[054-027]disconsolately about the room.

[054-028]‘Courage, my boy! I have every hope that I shall see you marry and

[054-029]repent.’

[054-030]‘I admit the danger of that. But shall I tell you something I have

[054-031]observed? Each woman I fall in love with is of a higher type than the

[054-032]one before.’

[054-033][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] roared irreverently, and his companion looked hurt.

[054-034]‘But I am perfectly serious, I assure you. To go back only three or four

[054-035]years. There was the daughter of my landlady in Barham Street; well, a

[054-036]nice girl enough, but limited, decidedly limited.

[054-037]Next came that girl at the stationer’s--you remember? She was distinctly

[054-038]an advance, both in mind and person. Then there was Miss Embleton; yes,

[054-039]I think she made again an advance. She had been at Bedford College,

[054-040]you know, and was really a girl of considerable attainments; morally,

[054-041]admirable. Afterwards--’

[054-042]He paused.

[054-043]‘The maiden from Birmingham, wasn’t it?’ said Jasper, again exploding.

[054-044]‘Yes, it was. Well, I can’t be quite sure. But in many respects that

[054-045]girl was my ideal; she really was.’

[054-046]‘As you once or twice told me at the time.’

[054-047]‘I really believe she would rank above Miss Embleton--at all events from

[054-048]my point of view. And that’s everything, you know. It’s the effect a

[054-049]woman produces on one that has to be considered.’

[054-050]‘The next should be a paragon,’ said Jasper.

[054-051]‘The next?’

[054-052]Whelpdale again looked about the room, but added nothing, and fell into

[054-053]a long silence.

[054-054]When left to himself Jasper walked about a little, then sat down at his

[054-055]writing-table, for he felt easier in mind, and fancied that he might

[054-056]still do a couple of hours’ work before going to bed. He did in fact

[054-057]write half-a-dozen lines, but with the effort came back his former mood.

[054-058]Very soon the pen dropped, and he was once more in the throes of anxious

[054-059]mental debate.

[054-060]He sat till after midnight, and when he went to his bedroom it was with

[054-061]a lingering step, which proved him still a prey to indecision.

[054-062]PART FOUR

[054-063]CHAPTER XXIII. A PROPOSED INVESTMENT

[054-064][[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]]’s behaviour under his disappointment seemed to prove that

[054-065]even for him the uses of adversity could be sweet. On the day after his

[054-066]return home he displayed a most unwonted mildness in such remarks as

[054-067]he addressed to his wife, and his bearing towards [[marian-yule|Marian]] was gravely

[054-068]gentle. At meals he conversed, or rather monologised, on literary

[054-069]topics, with occasionally one of his grim jokes, pointed for Marian’s

[054-070]appreciation. He became aware that the girl had been overtaxing her

[054-071]strength of late, and suggested a few weeks of recreation among new

[054-072]novels. The coldness and gloom which had possessed him when he made a

[054-073]formal announcement of the news appeared to have given way before the

[054-074]sympathy manifested by his wife and daughter; he was now sorrowful, but

[054-075]resigned.

[054-076]He explained to Marian the exact nature of her legacy. It was to be paid

[054-077]out of her uncle’s share in a wholesale stationery business, with which

[054-078][[john-yule|John Yule]] had been connected for the last twenty years, but from which

[054-079]he had not long ago withdrawn a large portion of his invested capital.

[054-080]This house was known as ‘Turberville & Co.,’ a name which Marian now

[054-081]heard for the first time.

[054-082]‘I knew nothing of his association with them,’ said her father. ‘They

[054-083]tell me that seven or eight thousand pounds will be realised from that

[054-084]source; it seems a pity that the investment was not left to you intact.

[054-085]Whether there will be any delay in withdrawing the money I can’t say.’

[054-086]The executors were two old friends of the deceased, one of them a former

[054-087]partner in his paper-making concern.

[054-088]On the evening of the second day, about an hour after dinner was over,

[054-089]Mr [[hinks|Hinks]] called at the house; as usual, he went into the study. Before

[054-090]long came a second visitor, Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]], who joined Yule and Hinks. The

[054-091]three had all sat together for some time, when Marian, who happened to

[054-092]be coming down stairs, saw her father at the study door.

[054-093]‘Ask your mother to let us have some supper at a quarter to ten,’ he

[054-094]said urbanely. ‘And come in, won’t you? We are only gossiping.’

[054-095]It had not often happened that Marian was invited to join parties of

[054-096]this kind.

[054-097]‘Do you wish me to come?’ she asked.

[054-098]‘Yes, I should like you to, if you have nothing particular to do.’

[054-099]Marian informed Mrs Yule that the visitors would have supper, and then

[054-100]went to the study. Mr Quarmby was smoking a pipe; Mr Hinks, who on

[054-101]grounds of economy had long since given up tobacco, sat with his hands

[054-102]in his trouser pockets, and his long, thin legs tucked beneath the

[054-103]chair; both rose and greeted Marian with more than ordinary warmth.

[054-104]‘Will you allow me five or six more puffs?’ asked Mr Quarmby, laying one

[054-105]hand on his ample stomach and elevating his pipe as if it were a glass

[054-106]of beaded liquor. ‘I shall then have done.’

[054-107]‘As many more as you like,’ Marian replied.

[054-108]The easiest chair was placed for her, Mr Hinks hastening to perform this

[054-109]courtesy, and her father apprised her of the topic they were discussing.

[054-110]‘What’s your view, Marian? Is there anything to be said for the

[054-111]establishment of a literary academy in England?’

[054-112]Mr Quarmby beamed benevolently upon her, and Mr Hinks, his scraggy neck

[054-113]at full length, awaited her reply with a look of the most respectful

[054-114]attention.

[054-115]‘I really think we have quite enough literary quarrelling as it is,’ the

[054-116]girl replied, casting down her eyes and smiling.

[054-117]Mr Quarmby uttered a hollow chuckle, Mr Hinks laughed thinly and

[054-118]exclaimed, ‘Very good indeed! Very good!’ Yule affected to applaud with

[054-119]impartial smile.

[054-120]‘It wouldn’t harmonise with the Anglo-Saxon spirit,’ remarked Mr Hinks,

[054-121]with an air of diffident profundity.

[054-122]Yule held forth on the subject for a few minutes in laboured phrases.

[054-123]Presently the conversation turned to periodicals, and the three men were

[054-124]unanimous in an opinion that no existing monthly or quarterly could be

[054-125]considered as representing the best literary opinion.

[054-126]‘We want,’ remarked Mr Quarmby, ‘we want a monthly review which

[054-127]shall deal exclusively with literature. The Fortnightly, the

[054-128]Contemporary--they are very well in their way, but then they are mere

[054-129]miscellanies. You will find one solid literary article amid a confused

[054-130]mass of politics and economics and general clap-trap.’

[054-131]‘Articles on the currency and railway statistics and views of

[054-132]evolution,’ said Mr Hinks, with a look as if something were grating

[054-133]between his teeth.

[054-134]‘The quarterlies?’ put in Yule. ‘Well, the original idea of the

[054-135]quarterlies was that there are not enough important books published to

[054-136]occupy solid reviewers more than four times a year. That may be true,

[054-137]but then a literary monthly would include much more than professed

[054-138]reviews. Hinks’s essays on the historical drama would have come out in

[054-139]it very well; or your “Spanish Poets,” Quarmby.’

[054-140]‘I threw out the idea to [[jedwood|Jedwood]] the other day,’ said Mr Quarmby, ‘and

[054-141]he seemed to nibble at it.’

[054-142]‘Yes, yes,’ came from Yule; ‘but Jedwood has so many irons in the fire.

[054-143]I doubt if he has the necessary capital at command just now. No doubt

[054-144]he’s the man, if some capitalist would join him.’

[054-145]‘No enormous capital needed,’ opined Mr Quarmby. ‘The thing would

[054-146]pay its way almost from the first. It would take a place between the

[054-147]literary weeklies and the quarterlies. The former are too academic,

[054-148]the latter too massive, for multitudes of people who yet have strong

[054-149]literary tastes. Foreign publications should be liberally dealt with.

[054-150]But, as Hinks says, no meddling with the books that are no books--biblia

[054-151]abiblia; nothing about essays on bimetallism and treatises for or

[054-152]against vaccination.’

[054-153]Even here, in the freedom of a friend’s study, he laughed his

[054-154]Reading-room laugh, folding both hands upon his expansive waistcoat.

[054-155]‘Fiction? I presume a serial of the better kind might be admitted?’ said

[054-156]Yule.

[054-157]‘That would be advisable, no doubt. But strictly of the better kind.’

[054-158]‘Oh, strictly of the better kind,’ chimed in Mr Hinks.

[054-159]They pursued the discussion as if they were an editorial committee

[054-160]planning a review of which the first number was shortly to appear.

[054-161]It occupied them until Mrs Yule announced at the door that supper was

[054-162]ready.

[054-163]During the meal Marian found herself the object of unusual attention;

[054-164]her father troubled to inquire if the cut of cold beef he sent her was

[054-165]to her taste, and kept an eye on her progress. Mr Hinks talked to her in

[054-166]a tone of respectful sympathy, and Mr Quarmby was paternally jovial when

[054-167]he addressed her. Mrs Yule would have kept silence, in her ordinary way,

[054-168]but this evening her husband made several remarks which he had adapted

[054-169]to her intellect, and even showed that a reply would be graciously

[054-170]received.

[054-171]Mother and daughter remained together when the men withdrew to their

[054-172]tobacco and toddy. Neither made allusion to the wonderful change, but

[054-173]they talked more light-heartedly than for a long time.

[054-174]On the morrow Yule began by consulting Marian with regard to the

[054-175]disposition of matter in an essay he was writing. What she said he

[054-176]weighed carefully, and seemed to think that she had set his doubts at

[054-177]rest.

[054-178]‘Poor old Hinks!’ he said presently, with a sigh. ‘Breaking up, isn’t

[054-179]he? He positively totters in his walk. I’m afraid he’s the kind of

[054-180]man to have a paralytic stroke; it wouldn’t astonish me to hear at any

[054-181]moment that he was lying helpless.’

[054-182]‘What ever would become of him in that case?’

[054-183]‘Goodness knows! One might ask the same of so many of us. What would

[054-184]become of me, for instance, if I were incapable of work?’

[054-185]Marian could make no reply.

[054-186]‘There’s something I’ll just mention to you,’ he went on in a lowered

[054-187]tone, ‘though I don’t wish you to take it too seriously. I’m beginning

[054-188]to have a little trouble with my eyes.’

[054-189]She looked at him, startled.

[054-190]‘With your eyes?’

[054-191]‘Nothing, I hope; but--well, I think I shall see an oculist. One doesn’t

[054-192]care to face a prospect of failing sight, perhaps of cataract, or

[054-193]something of that kind; still, it’s better to know the facts, I should

[054-194]say.’

[054-195]‘By all means go to an oculist,’ said Marian, earnestly.

[054-196]‘Don’t disturb yourself about it. It may be nothing at all. But in any

[054-197]case I must change my glasses.’
