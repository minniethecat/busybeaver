# Section 10

[009-001]He stood and regarded her. His expression was one of pained perplexity.

[009-002]‘You mustn’t forget, [[amy-reardon|Amy]], that it needs a particular kind of faculty to

[009-003]write stories of this sort. The invention of a plot is just the thing I

[009-004]find most difficult.’

[009-005]‘But the plot may be as silly as you like, providing it holds the

[009-006]attention of vulgar readers. Think of “The Hollow Statue”, what could be

[009-007]more idiotic? Yet it sells by thousands.’

[009-008]‘I don’t think I can bring myself to that,’ [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] said, in a low

[009-009]voice.

[009-010]‘Very well, then will you tell me what you propose to do?’

[009-011]‘I might perhaps manage a novel in two volumes, instead of three.’

[009-012]He seated himself at the writing-table, and stared at the blank sheets

[009-013]of paper in an anguish of hopelessness.

[009-014]‘It will take you till Christmas,’ said Amy, ‘and then you will get

[009-015]perhaps fifty pounds for it.’

[009-016]‘I must do my best. I’ll go out and try to get some ideas. I--’

[009-017]He broke off and looked steadily at his wife.

[009-018]‘What is it?’ she asked.

[009-019]‘Suppose I were to propose to you to leave this flat and take cheaper

[009-020]rooms?’

[009-021]He uttered it in a shamefaced way, his eyes falling. Amy kept silence.

[009-022]‘We might sublet it,’ he continued, in the same tone, ‘for the last year

[009-023]of the lease.’

[009-024]‘And where do you propose to live?’ Amy inquired, coldly.

[009-025]‘There’s no need to be in such a dear neighbourhood. We could go to one

[009-026]of the outer districts. One might find three unfurnished rooms for about

[009-027]eight-and-sixpence a week--less than half our rent here.’

[009-028]‘You must do as seems good to you.’

[009-029]‘For Heaven’s sake, Amy, don’t speak to me in that way! I can’t stand

[009-030]that! Surely you can see that I am driven to think of every possible

[009-031]resource. To speak like that is to abandon me. Say you can’t or won’t do

[009-032]it, but don’t treat me as if you had no share in my miseries!’

[009-033]She was touched for the moment.

[009-034]‘I didn’t mean to speak unkindly, dear. But think what it means, to give

[009-035]up our home and position. That is open confession of failure. It would

[009-036]be horrible.’

[009-037]‘I won’t think of it. I have three months before Christmas, and I will

[009-038]finish a book!’

[009-039]‘I really can’t see why you shouldn’t. Just do a certain number of pages

[009-040]every day. Good or bad, never mind; let the pages be finished. Now you

[009-041]have got two chapters--’

[009-042]‘No; that won’t do. I must think of a better subject.’

[009-043]Amy made a gesture of impatience.

[009-044]‘There you are! What does the subject matter? Get this book finished and

[009-045]sold, and then do something better next time.’

[009-046]‘Give me to-night, just to think. Perhaps one of the old stories I have

[009-047]thrown aside will come back in a clearer light. I’ll go out for an hour;

[009-048]you don’t mind being left alone?’

[009-049]‘You mustn’t think of such trifles as that.’

[009-050]‘But nothing that concerns you in the slightest way is a trifle to

[009-051]me--nothing! I can’t bear that you should forget that. Have patience

[009-052]with me, darling, a little longer.’

[009-053]He knelt by her, and looked up into her face.

[009-054]‘Say only one or two kind words--like you used to!’

[009-055]She passed her hand lightly over his hair, and murmured something with a

[009-056]faint smile.

[009-057]Then Reardon took his hat and stick and descended the eight flights

[009-058]of stone steps, and walked in the darkness round the outer circle

[009-059]of Regent’s Park, racking his fagged brain in a hopeless search for

[009-060]characters, situations, motives.

[009-061]CHAPTER V. THE WAY HITHER

[009-062]Even in mid-rapture of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]] month he had foreseen this

[009-063]possibility; but fate had hitherto rescued him in sudden ways when he

[009-064]was on the brink of self-abandonment, and it was hard to imagine that

[009-065]this culmination of triumphant joy could be a preface to base miseries.

[009-066]He was the son of a man who had followed many different pursuits, and

[009-067]in none had done much more than earn a livelihood. At the age of

[009-068]forty--when Edwin, his only child, was ten years old--Mr Reardon

[009-069]established himself in the town of Hereford as a photographer, and there

[009-070]he abode until his death, nine years after, occasionally risking some

[009-071]speculation not inconsistent with the photographic business, but always

[009-072]with the result of losing the little capital he ventured. Mrs Reardon

[009-073]died when Edwin had reached his fifteenth year. In breeding and

[009-074]education she was superior to her husband, to whom, moreover, she had

[009-075]brought something between four and five hundred pounds; her temper was

[009-076]passionate in both senses of the word, and the marriage could hardly be

[009-077]called a happy one, though it was never disturbed by serious discord.

[009-078]The photographer was a man of whims and idealisms; his wife had a

[009-079]strong vein of [[literary-ambition|worldly ambition]]. They made few friends, and it was Mrs

[009-080]Reardon’s frequently expressed desire to go and live in [[london|London]], where

[009-081]fortune, she thought, might be kinder to them. Reardon had all but made

[009-082]up his mind to try this venture when he suddenly became a widower; after

[009-083]that he never summoned energy to embark on new enterprises.

[009-084]The boy was educated at an excellent local school; at eighteen he had

[009-085]a far better acquaintance with the ancient classics than most lads

[009-086]who have been expressly prepared for a university, and, thanks to an

[009-087]anglicised Swiss who acted as an assistant in Mr Reardon’s business,

[009-088]he not only read French, but could talk it with a certain haphazard

[009-089]fluency. These attainments, however, were not of much practical use; the

[009-090]best that could be done for Edwin was to place him in the office of

[009-091]an estate agent. His health was indifferent, and it seemed likely

[009-092]that open-air exercise, of which he would have a good deal under the

[009-093]particular circumstances of the case, might counteract the effects of

[009-094]study too closely pursued.

[009-095]At his father’s death he came into possession (practically it was put at

[009-096]his disposal at once, though he was little more than nineteen) of

[009-097]about two hundred pounds--a life-insurance for five hundred had been

[009-098]sacrificed to exigencies not very long before. He had no difficulty in

[009-099]deciding how to use this [[money-in-literature|money]]. His mother’s desire to live in London

[009-100]had in him the force of an inherited motive; as soon as possible he

[009-101]released himself from his uncongenial occupations, converted into money

[009-102]all the possessions of which he had not immediate need, and betook

[009-103]himself to the metropolis.

[009-104]To become a literary man, of course.

[009-105]His capital lasted him nearly four years, for, notwithstanding his age,

[009-106]he lived with painful economy. The strangest life, of almost absolute

[009-107]loneliness. From a certain point of [[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]] there is

[009-108]visible a certain garret window in a certain street which runs parallel

[009-109]with that thoroughfare; for the greater part of these four years the

[009-110]garret in question was Reardon’s home. He paid only three-and-sixpence

[009-111]a week for the privilege of living there; his food cost him about a

[009-112]shilling a day; on clothing and other unavoidable expenses he laid

[009-113]out some five pounds yearly. Then he bought books--volumes which cost

[009-114]anything between twopence and two shillings; further than that he durst

[009-115]not go. A strange time, I assure you.

[009-116]When he had completed his twenty-first year, he desired to procure a

[009-117]reader’s ticket for the [[british-museum|British Museum]]. Now this was not such a simple

[009-118]matter as you may suppose; it was necessary to obtain the signature of

[009-119]some respectable householder, and Reardon was acquainted with no such

[009-120]person. His landlady was a decent woman enough, and a payer of rates and

[009-121]taxes, but it would look odd, to say the least of it, to present oneself

[009-122]in Great Russell Street armed with this person’s recommendation. There

[009-123]was nothing for it but to take a bold step, to force himself upon the

[009-124]attention of a stranger--the thing from which his pride had always

[009-125]shrunk. He wrote to a well-known novelist--a man with whose works he had

[009-126]some sympathy. ‘I am trying to prepare myself for a literary career.

[009-127]I wish to study in the Reading-room of the British Museum, but have

[009-128]no acquaintance to whom I can refer in the ordinary way. Will you help

[009-129]me--I mean, in this particular only?’ That was the substance of his

[009-130]letter. For reply came an invitation to a house in the West-end. With

[009-131]fear and trembling Reardon answered the summons. He was so shabbily

[009-132]attired; he was so diffident from the habit of living quite alone; he

[009-133]was horribly afraid lest it should be supposed that he looked for other

[009-134]assistance than he had requested. Well, the novelist was a rotund and

[009-135]jovial man; his dwelling and his person smelt of money; he was so happy

[009-136]himself that he could afford to be kind to others.

[009-137]‘Have you published anything?’ he inquired, for the young man’s letter

[009-138]had left this uncertain.

[009-139]‘Nothing. I have tried the magazines, but as yet without [[literary-success|success]].’

[009-140]‘But what do you write?’

[009-141]‘Chiefly essays on literary subjects.’

[009-142]‘I can understand that you would find a difficulty in disposing of them.

[009-143]That kind of thing is supplied either by men of established reputation,

[009-144]or by anonymous writers who have a regular engagement on papers and

[009-145]magazines. Give me an example of your topics.’

[009-146]‘I have written something lately about Tibullus.’

[009-147]‘Oh, dear! Oh, dear!--Forgive me, Mr Reardon; my feelings were too much

[009-148]for me; those names have been my horror ever since I was a schoolboy.

[009-149]Far be it from me to discourage you, if your line is to be solid

[009-150][[reviewing-criticism|literary criticism]]; I will only mention, as a matter of fact, that such

[009-151]work is indifferently paid and in very small demand. It hasn’t occurred

[009-152]to you to try your hand at fiction?’

[009-153]In uttering the word he beamed; to him it meant a thousand or so a year.

[009-154]‘I am afraid I have no talent for that.’

[009-155]The novelist could do no more than grant his genial signature for the

[009-156]specified purpose, and add good wishes in abundance. Reardon went home

[009-157]with his brain in a whirl. He had had his first glimpse of what was

[009-158]meant by literary success. That luxurious study, with its shelves of

[009-159]handsomely-bound books, its beautiful pictures, its warm, fragrant

[009-160]air--great heavens! what might not a man do who sat at his ease amid

[009-161]such surroundings!

[009-162]He began to work at the Reading-room, but at the same time he thought

[009-163]often of the novelist’s suggestion, and before long had written two or

[009-164]three short stories. No editor would accept them; but he continued to

[009-165]practise himself in that art, and by degrees came to fancy that,

[009-166]after all, perhaps he had some talent for fiction. It was significant,

[009-167]however, that no native impulse had directed him to novel-writing. His

[009-168]intellectual temper was that of the student, the scholar, but strongly

[009-169]blended with a love of independence which had always made him think

[009-170]with distaste of a teacher’s life. The stories he wrote were scraps

[009-171]of immature psychology--the last thing a magazine would accept from an

[009-172]unknown man.

[009-173]His money dwindled, and there came a winter during which he suffered

[009-174]much from cold and hunger. What a blessed refuge it was, there under the

[009-175]great dome, when he must else have sat in his windy garret with the

[009-176]mere pretence of a fire! The Reading-room was his true home; its warmth

[009-177]enwrapped him kindly; the peculiar odour of its atmosphere--at first a

[009-178]cause of headache--grew dear and delightful to him. But he could not sit

[009-179]here until his last penny should be spent. Something practical must be

[009-180]done, and practicality was not his strong point.

[009-181]Friends in London he had none; but for an occasional conversation with

[009-182]his landlady he would scarcely have spoken a dozen words in a week.

[009-183]His disposition was the reverse of democratic, and he could not make

[009-184]acquaintances below his own intellectual level. Solitude fostered

[009-185]a sensitiveness which to begin with was extreme; the lack of stated

[009-186]occupation encouraged his natural tendency to dream and procrastinate

[009-187]and hope for the improbable. He was a recluse in the midst of millions,

[009-188]and viewed with dread the necessity of going forth to fight for daily

[009-189]food.
