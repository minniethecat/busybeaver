# Section 15

[014-001]‘I’ve had a talk with Nathaniel Walker,’ he continued; ‘a long talk--a

[014-002]talk of vast importance. You know Walker? No, no; how should you? He’s a

[014-003]man of business; close friend of Rackett’s--Rackett, you know, the owner

[014-004]of [[the-study|The Study]].’

[014-005]Upon this he made a grave pause, and glared more excitedly than ever.

[014-006]‘I have heard of Mr Rackett,’ said [[marian-yule|Marian]].

[014-007]‘Of course, of course. And you must also have heard that [[fadge|Fadge]] leaves

[014-008]The Study at the end of this year, eh?’

[014-009]‘Father told me it was probable.’

[014-010]‘Rackett and he have done nothing but quarrel for months; the paper is

[014-011]falling off seriously. Well, now, when I came across Nat Walker this

[014-012]afternoon, the first thing he said to me was, “You know [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]]

[014-013]pretty well, I think?” “Pretty well,” I answered; “why?” “I’ll tell

[014-014]you,” he said, “but it’s between you and me, you understand. Rackett is

[014-015]thinking about him in connection with The Study.” “I’m delighted to hear

[014-016]it.” “To tell you the truth,” went on Nat, “I shouldn’t wonder if Yule

[014-017]gets the editorship; but you understand that it would be altogether

[014-018]premature to talk about it.” Now what do you think of this, eh?’

[014-019]‘It’s very good news,’ answered Marian.

[014-020]‘I should think so! Ho, ho!’

[014-021]Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]] laughed in a peculiar way, which was the result of long years

[014-022]of mirth-subdual in the Reading-room.

[014-023]‘But not a breath to anyone but your father. He’ll be here to-morrow?

[014-024]Break it gently to him, you know; he’s an excitable man; can’t take

[014-025]things quietly, like I do. Ho, ho!’

[014-026]His suppressed laugh ended in a fit of coughing--the Reading-room cough.

[014-027]When he had recovered from it, he pressed Marian’s hand with paternal

[014-028]fervour, and waddled off to chatter with someone else.

[014-029]Marian replaced several books on the reference-shelves, returned others

[014-030]to the central desk, and was just leaving the room, when again a voice

[014-031]made demand upon her attention.

[014-032]‘Miss Yule! One moment, if you please!’

[014-033]It was a tall, meagre, dry-featured man, dressed with the painful

[014-034]neatness of self-respecting [[literary-poverty|poverty]]: the edges of his coat-sleeves were

[014-035]carefully darned; his black necktie and a skull-cap which covered

[014-036]his baldness were evidently of home manufacture. He smiled softly and

[014-037]timidly with blue, rheumy eyes. Two or three recent cuts on his chin and

[014-038]neck were the result of conscientious shaving with an unsteady hand.

[014-039]‘I have been looking for your father,’ he said, as Marian turned. ‘Isn’t

[014-040]he here?’

[014-041]‘He has gone, Mr [[hinks|Hinks]].’

[014-042]‘Ah, then would you do me the kindness to take a book for him? In fact,

[014-043]it’s my little “Essay on the Historical Drama,” just out.’

[014-044]He spoke with nervous hesitation, and in a tone which seemed to make

[014-045]apology for his existence.

[014-046]‘Oh, father will be very glad to have it.’

[014-047]‘If you will kindly wait one minute, Miss Yule. It’s at my place over

[014-048]there.’

[014-049]He went off with long strides, and speedily came back panting, in his

[014-050]hand a thin new volume.

[014-051]‘My kind regards to him, Miss Yule. You are quite well, I hope? I won’t

[014-052]detain you.’

[014-053]And he backed into a man who was coming inobservantly this way.

[014-054]Marian went to the ladies’ cloak-room, put on her hat and jacket, and

[014-055]left the Museum. Some one passed out through the swing-door a moment

[014-056]before her, and as soon as she had issued beneath the portico, she saw

[014-057]that it was [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]; she must have followed him through the hall,

[014-058]but her eyes had been cast down. The young man was now alone; as he

[014-059]descended the steps he looked to left and right, but not behind him.

[014-060]Marian followed at a distance of two or three yards. Nearing the

[014-061]gateway, she quickened her pace a little, so as to pass out into the

[014-062]street almost at the same moment as Milvain. But he did not turn his

[014-063]head.

[014-064]He took to the right. Marian had fallen back again, but she still

[014-065]followed at a very little distance. His walk was slow, and she might

[014-066]easily have passed him in quite a natural way; in that case he could not

[014-067]help seeing her. But there was an uneasy suspicion in her mind that he

[014-068]really must have noticed her in the Reading-room. This was the first

[014-069]time she had seen him since their parting at Finden. Had he any reason

[014-070]for avoiding her? Did he take it ill that her father had shown no desire

[014-071]to keep up his acquaintance?

[014-072]She allowed the interval between them to become greater. In a minute or

[014-073]two Milvain turned up Charlotte Street, and so she lost sight of him.

[014-074]In [[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]] she waited for an omnibus that would take her

[014-075]to the remoter part of [[camden-town|Camden Town]]; obtaining a corner seat, she drew as

[014-076]far back as possible, and paid no attention to her fellow-passengers.

[014-077]At a point in Camden Road she at length alighted, and after ten

[014-078]minutes’ walk reached her destination in a quiet by-way called St Paul’s

[014-079]Crescent, consisting of small, decent houses. That at which she paused

[014-080]had an exterior promising comfort within; the windows were clean and

[014-081]neatly curtained, and the polishable appurtenances of the door gleamed

[014-082]to perfection. She admitted herself with a latch-key, and went straight

[014-083]upstairs without encountering anyone.

[014-084]Descending again in a few moments, she entered the front room on the

[014-085]ground-floor. This served both as parlour and dining-room; it was

[014-086]comfortably furnished, without much attempt at adornment. On the walls

[014-087]were a few autotypes and old engravings. A recess between fireplace and

[014-088]window was fitted with shelves, which supported hundreds of volumes,

[014-089]the overflow of Yule’s library. The table was laid for a meal. It best

[014-090]suited the convenience of the family to dine at five o’clock; a long

[014-091]evening, so necessary to most literary people, was thus assured.

[014-092]Marian, as always when she had spent a day at the Museum, was faint with

[014-093]weariness and hunger; she cut a small piece of bread from a loaf on the

[014-094]table, and sat down in an easy chair.

[014-095]Presently appeared a short, slight woman of middle age, plainly dressed

[014-096]in serviceable grey. Her face could never have been very comely, and it

[014-097]expressed but moderate intelligence; its lines, however, were those of

[014-098]gentleness and good feeling. She had the look of one who is making

[014-099]a painful effort to understand something; this was fixed upon her

[014-100]features, and probably resulted from the peculiar conditions of her

[014-101]life.

[014-102]‘Rather early, aren’t you, Marian?’ she said, as she closed the door and

[014-103]came forward to take a seat.

[014-104]‘Yes; I have a little headache.’

[014-105]‘Oh, dear! Is that beginning again?’

[014-106]Mrs Yule’s speech was seldom ungrammatical, and her intonation was not

[014-107]flagrantly vulgar, but the accent of the [[london|London]] poor, which brands as

[014-108]with hereditary baseness, still clung to her words, rendering futile

[014-109]such propriety of phrase as she owed to years of association with

[014-110]educated people. In the same degree did her bearing fall short of that

[014-111]which distinguishes a lady. The London work-girl is rarely capable of

[014-112]raising herself, or being raised, to a place in life above that to which

[014-113]she was born; she cannot learn how to stand and sit and move like a

[014-114]woman bred to refinement, any more than she can fashion her tongue

[014-115]to graceful speech. Mrs Yule’s behaviour to Marian was marked with a

[014-116]singular diffidence; she looked and spoke affectionately, but not with a

[014-117]mother’s freedom; one might have taken her for a trusted servant waiting

[014-118]upon her mistress. Whenever opportunity offered, she watched the girl

[014-119]in a curiously furtive way, that puzzled look on her face becoming very

[014-120]noticeable. Her consciousness was never able to accept as a familiar and

[014-121]unimportant fact the vast difference between herself and her daughter.

[014-122]Marian’s superiority in native powers, in delicacy of feeling, in the

[014-123]results of education, could never be lost sight of. Under ordinary

[014-124]circumstances she addressed the girl as if tentatively; however sure of

[014-125]anything from her own point of view, she knew that Marian, as often

[014-126]as not, had quite a different criterion. She understood that the

[014-127]girl frequently expressed an opinion by mere reticence, and hence the

[014-128]carefulness with which, when conversing, she tried to discover the real

[014-129]effect of her words in Marian’s features.

[014-130]‘Hungry, too,’ she said, seeing the crust Marian was nibbling. ‘You

[014-131]really must have more lunch, dear. It isn’t right to go so long; you’ll

[014-132]make yourself ill.’

[014-133]‘Have you been out?’ Marian asked.

[014-134]‘Yes; I went to Holloway.’

[014-135]Mrs Yule sighed and looked very unhappy. By ‘going to Holloway’ was

[014-136]always meant a visit to her own relatives--a married sister with three

[014-137]children, and a brother who inhabited the same house. To her husband

[014-138]she scarcely ever ventured to speak of these persons; Yule had

[014-139]no intercourse with them. But Marian was always willing to listen

[014-140]sympathetically, and her mother often exhibited a touching gratitude for

[014-141]this condescension--as she deemed it.

[014-142]‘Are things no better?’ the girl inquired.

[014-143]‘Worse, as far as I can see. John has begun his drinking again, and him

[014-144]and Tom quarrel every night; there’s no peace in the ‘ouse.’

[014-145]If ever Mrs Yule lapsed into gross errors of pronunciation or phrase, it

[014-146]was when she spoke of her kinsfolk. The subject seemed to throw her back

[014-147]into a former condition.

[014-148]‘He ought to go and live by himself’ said Marian, referring to her

[014-149]mother’s brother, the thirsty John.

[014-150]‘So he ought, to be sure. I’m always telling them so. But there!

[014-151]you don’t seem to be able to persuade them, they’re that silly and

[014-152]obstinate. And Susan, she only gets angry with me, and tells me not to

[014-153]talk in a stuck-up way. I’m sure I never say a word that could offend

[014-154]her; I’m too careful for that. And there’s Annie; no doing anything with

[014-155]her! She’s about the streets at all hours, and what’ll be the end of

[014-156]it no one can say. They’re getting that ragged, all of them. It isn’t

[014-157]Susan’s fault; indeed it isn’t. She does all that woman can. But Tom

[014-158]hasn’t brought home ten shillings the last month, and it seems to me as

[014-159]if he was getting careless. I gave her half-a-crown; it was all I could

[014-160]do. And the worst of it is, they think I could do so much more if I

[014-161]liked. They’re always hinting that we are rich people, and it’s no good

[014-162]my trying to persuade them. They think I’m telling falsehoods, and it’s

[014-163]very hard to be looked at in that way; it is, indeed, Marian.’

[014-164]‘You can’t help it, mother. I suppose their suffering makes them unkind

[014-165]and unjust.’

[014-166]‘That’s just what it does, my dear; you never said anything truer.

[014-167]Poverty will make the best people bad, if it gets hard enough. Why

[014-168]there’s so much of it in the world, I’m sure I can’t see.’

[014-169]‘I suppose father will be back soon?’

[014-170]‘He said dinner-time.’

[014-171]‘Mr Quarmby has been telling me something which is wonderfully good news

[014-172]if it’s really true; but I can’t help feeling doubtful.

[014-173]He says that father may perhaps be made editor of The Study at the end

[014-174]of this year.’

[014-175]Mrs Yule, of course, understood, in outline, these affairs of the

[014-176]literary world; she thought of them only from the pecuniary point of

[014-177]view, but that made no essential distinction between her and the mass of

[014-178]literary people.

[014-179]‘My word!’ she exclaimed. ‘What a thing that would be for us!’

[014-180]Marian had begun to explain her reluctance to base any hopes on Mr

[014-181]Quarmby’s prediction, when the sound of a postman’s knock at the

[014-182]house-door caused her mother to disappear for a moment.

[014-183]‘It’s for you,’ said Mrs Yule, returning. ‘From the country.’

[014-184]Marian took the letter and examined its address with interest.

[014-185]‘It must be one of the Miss Milvains. Yes; [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]].’

[014-186]After Jasper’s departure from Finden his sisters had seen Marian several

[014-187]times, and the mutual liking between her and them had been confirmed by

[014-188]opportunity of conversation. The promise of correspondence had hitherto

[014-189]waited for fulfilment. It seemed natural to Marian that the younger

[014-190]of the two girls should write; Maud was attractive and agreeable, and

[014-191]probably clever, but Dora had more spontaneity in friendship.
