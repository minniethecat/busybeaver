# Section 83

[082-001]‘I can only say,’ he remarked with a smile, ‘that Miss [[dora-milvain|Dora]] takes a very

[082-002]noble point of view. One feels that a wife ought to be staunch. But

[082-003]it’s so very unsafe to discuss matters in which one cannot know all the

[082-004]facts.’

[082-005]‘We know quite enough of the facts,’ said Dora, with delightful

[082-006]pertinacity.

[082-007]‘Indeed, perhaps we do,’ assented her slave. Then, turning to her

[082-008]brother, ‘Well, once more I congratulate you. I shall talk of your

[082-009]article incessantly, as soon as it appears. And I shall pester every one

[082-010]of my acquaintances to buy [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s books--though it’s no use to him,

[082-011]poor fellow. Still, he would have died more contentedly if he could have

[082-012]foreseen this. By-the-by, [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] will be profoundly grateful to you, I’m

[082-013]sure.’

[082-014]‘I’m doing what I can for him, too. Run your eye over these slips.’

[082-015][[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] exhausted himself in terms of satisfaction.

[082-016]‘You deserve to get on, my dear fellow. In a few years you will be the

[082-017]Aristarchus of our literary world.’

[082-018]When the visitor rose to depart, [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] said he would walk a short

[082-019]distance with him. As soon as they had left the house, the future

[082-020]Aristarchus made a confidential communication.

[082-021]‘It may interest you to know that my sister Maud is shortly to be

[082-022]married.’

[082-023]‘Indeed! May I ask to whom?’

[082-024]‘A man you don’t know. His name is Dolomore--a fellow in society.’

[082-025]‘Rich, then, I hope?’

[082-026]‘Tolerably well-to-do. I dare say he has three or four thousand a year!’

[082-027]‘Gracious heavens! Why, that’s magnificent.’

[082-028]But Whelpdale did not look quite so much satisfaction as his words

[082-029]expressed.

[082-030]‘Is it to be soon?’ he inquired.

[082-031]‘At the end of the season. Make no difference to Dora and me, of

[082-032]course.’

[082-033]‘Oh? Really? No difference at all? You will let me come and see

[082-034]you--both--just in the old way, Milvain?’

[082-035]‘Why the deuce shouldn’t you?’

[082-036]‘To be sure, to be sure. By Jove! I really don’t know how I should get

[082-037]on if I couldn’t look in of an evening now and then. I have got so much

[082-038]into the habit of it. And--I’m a lonely beggar, you know. I don’t go

[082-039]into society, and really--’

[082-040]He broke off, and Jasper began to speak of other things.

[082-041]When Milvain re-entered the house, Dora had gone to her own

[082-042]sitting-room. It was not quite ten o’clock. Taking one set of the proofs

[082-043]of his ‘Reardon’ article, he put it into a large envelope; then he

[082-044]wrote a short letter, which began ‘Dear [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]],’ and ended ‘Very

[082-045]sincerely yours,’ the communication itself being as follows:

[082-046]‘I venture to send you the proofs of a paper which is to appear in next

[082-047]month’s Wayside, in the hope that it may seem to you not badly done, and

[082-048]that the reading of it may give you pleasure. If anything occurs to you

[082-049]which you would like me to add, or if you desire any omission, will you

[082-050]do me the kindness to let me know of it as soon as possible, and your

[082-051]suggestion shall at once be adopted. I am informed that the new edition

[082-052]of “[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]]” and “Hubert Reed” will be ready next month. Need

[082-053]I say how glad I am that my friend’s work is not to be forgotten?’

[082-054]This note he also put into the envelope, which he made ready for

[082-055]posting. Then he sat for a long time in profound thought.

[082-056]Shortly after eleven his door opened, and Maud came in. She had been

[082-057]dining at Mrs Lane’s. Her attire was still simple, but of quality which

[082-058]would have signified recklessness, but for the outlook whereof Jasper

[082-059]spoke to Whelpdale. The girl looked very beautiful. There was a flush of

[082-060]health and happiness on her cheek, and when she spoke it was in a voice

[082-061]that rang quite differently from her tones of a year ago; the pride

[082-062]which was natural to her had now a firm support; she moved and uttered

[082-063]herself in queenly fashion.

[082-064]‘Has anyone been?’ she asked.

[082-065]‘Whelpdale.’

[082-066]‘Oh! I wanted to ask you, Jasper: do you think it wise to let him come

[082-067]quite so often?’

[082-068]‘There’s a difficulty, you see. I can hardly tell him to sheer off. And

[082-069]he’s really a decent fellow.’

[082-070]‘That may be. But--I think it’s rather unwise. Things are changed. In a

[082-071]few months, Dora will be a good deal at my house, and will see all sorts

[082-072]of people.’

[082-073]‘Yes; but what if they are the kind of people she doesn’t care anything

[082-074]about? You must remember, old girl, that her tastes are quite different

[082-075]from yours. I say nothing, but--perhaps it’s as well they should be.’

[082-076]‘You say nothing, but you add an insult,’ returned Maud, with a smile of

[082-077]superb disregard. ‘We won’t reopen the question.’

[082-078]‘Oh dear no! And, by-the-by, I have a letter from Dolomore. It came just

[082-079]after you left.’

[082-080]‘Well?’

[082-081]‘He is quite willing to settle upon you a third of his income from

[082-082]the collieries; he tells me it will represent between seven and eight

[082-083]hundred a year. I think it rather little, you know; but I congratulate

[082-084]myself on having got this out of him.’

[082-085]‘Don’t speak in that unpleasant way! It was only your abruptness that

[082-086]made any kind of difficulty.’

[082-087]‘I have my own opinion on that point, and I shall beg leave to keep it.

[082-088]Probably he will think me still more abrupt when I request, as I am now

[082-089]going to do, an interview with his solicitors.’

[082-090]‘Is that allowable?’ asked Maud, anxiously. ‘Can you do that with any

[082-091]decency?’

[082-092]‘If not, then I must do it with indecency. You will have the goodness

[082-093]to remember that if I don’t look after your interests, no one else will.

[082-094]It’s perhaps fortunate for you that I have a good deal of the man of

[082-095]business about me. Dolomore thought I was a dreamy, literary fellow.

[082-096]I don’t say that he isn’t entirely honest, but he shows something of a

[082-097]disposition to play the autocrat, and I by no means intend to let

[082-098]him. If you had a father, Dolomore would have to submit his affairs to

[082-099]examination.

[082-100]I stand to you in loco parentis, and I shall bate no jot of my rights.’

[082-101]‘But you can’t say that his behaviour hasn’t been perfectly

[082-102]straightforward.’

[082-103]‘I don’t wish to. I think, on the whole, he has behaved more honourably

[082-104]than was to be expected of a man of his kind. But he must treat me with

[082-105]respect. My position in the world is greatly superior to his. And, by

[082-106]the gods! I will be treated respectfully! It wouldn’t be amiss, Maud, if

[082-107]you just gave him a hint to that effect.’

[082-108]‘All I have to say is, Jasper, don’t do me an irreparable injury. You

[082-109]might, without meaning it.’

[082-110]‘No fear whatever of it. I can behave as a gentleman, and I only expect

[082-111]Dolomore to do the same.’

[082-112]Their conversation lasted for a long time, and when he was again left

[082-113]alone Jasper again fell into a mood of thoughtfulness.

[082-114]By a late post on the following day he received this letter:

[082-115]‘DEAR MR MILVAIN,--I have received the proofs, and have just read them;

[082-116]I hasten to thank you with all my heart. No suggestion of mine could

[082-117]possibly improve this article; it seems to me perfect in taste, in

[082-118]style, in matter. No one but you could have written this, for no one

[082-119]else understood Edwin so well, or had given such thought to his work. If

[082-120]he could but have known that such justice would be done to his memory!

[082-121]But he died believing that already he was utterly forgotten, that his

[082-122]books would never again be publicly spoken of. This was a cruel fate. I

[082-123]have shed tears over what you have written, but they were not only tears

[082-124]of bitterness; it cannot but be a consolation to me to think that, when

[082-125]the magazine appears, so many people will talk of Edwin and his books.

[082-126]I am deeply grateful to Mr Mortimer for having undertaken to republish

[082-127]those two novels; if you have an opportunity, will you do me the great

[082-128]kindness to thank him on my behalf? At the same time, I must remember

[082-129]that it was you who first spoke to him on this subject. You say that it

[082-130]gladdens you to think Edwin will not be forgotten, and I am very sure

[082-131]that the friendly office you have so admirably performed will in itself

[082-132]reward you more than any poor expression of gratitude from me. I write

[082-133]hurriedly, anxious to let you hear as soon as possible.

[082-134]‘Believe me, dear Mr Milvain,

[082-135]‘Yours sincerely,

[082-136]‘AMY REARDON.’

[082-137]CHAPTER XXXIV. A CHECK

[082-138][[marian-yule|Marian]] was at work as usual in the Reading-room. She did her best,

[082-139]during the hours spent here, to convert herself into the literary

[082-140]machine which it was her hope would some day be invented for

[082-141]construction in a less sensitive material than human tissue. Her eyes

[082-142]seldom strayed beyond the limits of the desk; and if she had occasion to

[082-143]rise and go to the reference shelves, she looked at no one on the way.

[082-144]Yet she herself was occasionally an object of interested regard. Several

[082-145]readers were acquainted with the chief facts of her position; they knew

[082-146]that her father was now incapable of work, and was waiting till his

[082-147]diseased eyes should be ready for the operator; it was surmised,

[082-148]moreover, that a good deal depended upon the girl’s literary exertions.

[082-149]Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]] and his gossips naturally took the darkest view of things;

[082-150]they were convinced that [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] could never recover his sight,

[082-151]and they had a dolorous satisfaction in relating the story of Marian’s

[082-152]legacy. Of her relations with Jasper Milvain none of these persons had

[082-153]heard; Yule had never spoken of that matter to any one of his friends.

[082-154]Jasper had to look in this morning for a hurried consultation of certain

[082-155]encyclopaedic volumes, and it chanced that Marian was standing before

[082-156]the shelves to which his business led him. He saw her from a little

[082-157]distance, and paused; it seemed as if he would turn back; for a moment

[082-158]he wore a look of doubt and worry. But after all he proceeded. At the

[082-159]sound of his ‘Good-morning,’ Marian started--she was standing with an

[082-160]open book in hand--and looked up with a gleam of joy on her face.

[082-161]‘I wanted to see you to-day,’ she said, subduing her voice to the tone

[082-162]of ordinary conversation. ‘I should have come this evening.’

[082-163]‘You wouldn’t have found me at home. From five to seven I shall be

[082-164]frantically busy, and then I have to rush off to dine with some people.’

[082-165]‘I couldn’t see you before five?’

[082-166]‘Is it something important?’

[082-167]‘Yes, it is.’

[082-168]‘I tell you what. If you could meet me at Gloucester Gate at four, then

[082-169]I shall be glad of half an hour in the park. But I mustn’t talk now; I’m

[082-170]driven to my wits’ end. Gloucester Gate, at four sharp. I don’t think

[082-171]it’ll rain.’

[082-172]He dragged out a tome of the ‘Britannica.’ Marian nodded, and returned

[082-173]to her seat.

[082-174]At the appointed hour she was waiting near the entrance of Regent’s

[082-175]Park which Jasper had mentioned. Not long ago there had fallen a light

[082-176]shower, but the sky was clear again. At five minutes past four she

[082-177]still waited, and had begun to fear that the passing rain might have

[082-178]led Jasper to think she would not come. Another five minutes, and from a

[082-179]hansom that rattled hither at full speed, the familiar figure alighted.

[082-180]‘Do forgive me!’ he exclaimed. ‘I couldn’t possibly get here before. Let

[082-181]us go to the right.’

[082-182]They betook themselves to that tree-shadowed strip of the park which

[082-183]skirts the canal.

[082-184]‘I’m so afraid that you haven’t really time,’ said Marian, who was

[082-185]chilled and confused by this show of hurry. She regretted having made

[082-186]the appointment; it would have been much better to postpone what she had

[082-187]to say until Jasper was at leisure. Yet nowadays the hours of leisure

[082-188]seemed to come so rarely.
