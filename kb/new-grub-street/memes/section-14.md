# Section 14

[013-001]‘I have been trying,’ he said at length, after an attempt to speak which

[013-002]was checked by a huskiness in his throat, ‘to explain to myself how this

[013-003]state of things has come about. I almost think I can do so.’

[013-004]‘How?’

[013-005]‘That half-year abroad, and the extraordinary shock of happiness which

[013-006]followed at once upon it, have disturbed the balance of my nature.

[013-007]It was adjusted to circumstances of hardship, privation, struggle.

[013-008]A temperament like mine can’t pass through such a violent change of

[013-009]conditions without being greatly affected; I have never since been the

[013-010]man I was before I left England. The stage I had then reached was the

[013-011]result of a slow and elaborate building up; I could look back and see

[013-012]the processes by which I had grown from the boy who was a mere bookworm

[013-013]to the man who had all but succeeded as a novelist. It was a perfectly

[013-014]natural, sober development. But in the last two years and a half I can

[013-015]distinguish no order. In living through it, I have imagined from time

[013-016]to time that my powers were coming to their ripest; but that was mere

[013-017]delusion. Intellectually, I have fallen back. The probability is that

[013-018]this wouldn’t matter, if only I could live on in peace of mind; I should

[013-019]recover my equilibrium, and perhaps once more understand myself. But the

[013-020]due course of things is troubled by my [[literary-poverty|poverty]].’

[013-021]He spoke in a slow, meditative way, in a monotonous voice, and without

[013-022]raising his eyes from the ground.

[013-023]‘I can understand,’ put in [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]], ‘that there may be philosophical

[013-024]truth in all this. All the same, it’s a great pity that you should

[013-025]occupy your mind with such thoughts.’

[013-026]‘A pity--no! I must remain a reasoning creature. Disaster may end by

[013-027]driving me out of my wits, but till then I won’t abandon my heritage of

[013-028]thought.’

[013-029]‘Let us have it out, then. You think it was a mistake to spend those

[013-030]months abroad?’

[013-031]‘A mistake from the practical point of view. That vast broadening of my

[013-032]horizon lost me the command of my literary resources. I lived in

[013-033]Italy and Greece as a student, concerned especially with the old

[013-034]civilisations; I read little but Greek and Latin. That brought me out of

[013-035]the track I had laboriously made for myself I often thought with disgust

[013-036]of the kind of work I had been doing; my novels seemed vapid stuff, so

[013-037]wretchedly and shallowly modern. If I had had the means, I should have

[013-038]devoted myself to the life of a scholar. That, I quite believe, is my

[013-039]natural life; it’s only the influence of recent circumstances that has

[013-040]made me a writer of novels. A man who can’t journalise, yet must earn

[013-041]his bread by literature, nowadays inevitably turns to fiction, as the

[013-042]Elizabethan men turned to the drama. Well, but I should have got back, I

[013-043]think, into the old line of work. It was my [[literary-marriage|marriage]] that completed what

[013-044]the time abroad had begun.’

[013-045]He looked up suddenly, and added:

[013-046]‘I am speaking as if to myself. You, of course, don’t misunderstand me,

[013-047]and think I am accusing my wife.’

[013-048]‘No, I don’t take you to mean that, by any means.’

[013-049]‘No, no; of course not. All that’s wrong is my accursed want of [[money-in-literature|money]].

[013-050]But that threatens to be such a fearful wrong, that I begin to wish I

[013-051]had died before my marriage-day. Then [[amy-reardon|Amy]] would have been saved. The

[013-052]Philistines are right: a man has no business to marry unless he has a

[013-053]secured income equal to all natural demands. I behaved with the grossest

[013-054]selfishness. I might have known that such happiness was never meant for

[013-055]me.’

[013-056]‘Do you mean by all this that you seriously doubt whether you will ever

[013-057]be able to write again?’

[013-058]‘In awful seriousness, I doubt it,’ replied [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], with haggard face.

[013-059]‘It strikes me as extraordinary. In your position I should work as I

[013-060]never had done before.’

[013-061]‘Because you are the kind of man who is roused by necessity. I am

[013-062]overcome by it. My nature is feeble and luxurious. I never in my life

[013-063]encountered and overcame a practical difficulty.’

[013-064]‘Yes; when you got the work at the hospital.’

[013-065]‘All I did was to write a letter, and chance made it effective.’

[013-066]‘My view of the case, Reardon, is that you are simply ill.’

[013-067]‘Certainly I am; but the ailment is desperately complicated. Tell me: do

[013-068]you think I might possibly get any kind of stated work to do? Should I

[013-069]be fit for any place in a newspaper office, for instance?’

[013-070]‘I fear not. You are the last man to have anything to do with

[013-071][[journalism-trade|journalism]].’

[013-072]‘If I appealed to my [[publishing-trade|publishers]], could they help me?’

[013-073]‘I don’t see how. They would simply say: Write a book and we’ll buy it.’

[013-074]‘Yes, there’s no help but that.’

[013-075]‘If only you were able to write short stories, [[fadge|Fadge]] might be useful.’

[013-076]‘But what’s the use? I suppose I might get ten guineas, at most, for

[013-077]such a story. I need a couple of hundred pounds at least. Even if

[013-078]I could finish a three-volume book, I doubt if they would give me a

[013-079]hundred again, after the failure of “The Optimist”; no, they wouldn’t.’

[013-080]‘But to sit and look forward in this way is absolutely fatal, my

[013-081]dear fellow. Get to work at your two-volume story. Call it “The Weird

[013-082]Sisters,” or anything better that you can devise; but get it done, so

[013-083]many pages a day. If I go ahead as I begin to think I shall, I shall

[013-084]soon be able to assure you good notices in a lot of papers. Your

[013-085]misfortune has been that you had no influential friends. By-the-bye, how

[013-086]has [[the-study|The Study]] been in the habit of treating you?’

[013-087]‘Scrubbily.’

[013-088]‘I’ll make an opportunity of talking about your books to Fadge. I think

[013-089]Fadge and I shall get on pretty well together. [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] hates the man

[013-090]fiercely, for some reason or other. By the way, I may as well tell you

[013-091]that I broke short off with the Yules on purpose.’

[013-092]‘Oh?’

[013-093]‘I had begun to think far too much about the girl. Wouldn’t do, you

[013-094]know. I must marry someone with money, and a good deal of it.

[013-095]That’s a settled point with me.’

[013-096]‘Then you are not at all likely to meet them in [[london|London]]?’

[013-097]‘Not at all. And if I get allied with Fadge, no doubt Yule will involve

[013-098]me in his savage feeling. You see how wisely I acted. I have a scent for

[013-099]the prudent course.’

[013-100]They talked for a long time, but again chiefly of Milvain’s affairs.

[013-101]Reardon, indeed, cared little to say anything more about his own. Talk

[013-102]was mere vanity and vexation of spirit, for the spring of his volition

[013-103]seemed to be broken, and, whatever resolve he might utter, he knew that

[013-104]everything depended on influences he could not even foresee.

[013-105]CHAPTER VII. MARIAN’S HOME

[013-106]Three weeks after her return from the country--which took place a week

[013-107]later than that of Jasper Milvain--[[marian-yule|Marian Yule]] was working one afternoon

[013-108]at her usual place in the Museum Reading-room. It was three o’clock, and

[013-109]with the interval of half an hour at midday, when she went away for a

[013-110]cup of tea and a sandwich, she had been closely occupied since half-past

[013-111]nine. Her task at present was to collect materials for a paper on

[013-112]‘French Authoresses of the Seventeenth Century,’ the kind of thing

[013-113]which her father supplied on stipulated terms for anonymous publication.

[013-114]Marian was by this time almost able to complete such a piece of

[013-115]manufacture herself and her father’s share in it was limited to a few

[013-116]hints and corrections. The greater part of the work by which Yule earned

[013-117]his moderate income was anonymous: volumes and articles which bore his

[013-118]signature dealt with much the same subjects as his unsigned matter, but

[013-119]the writing was laboured with a conscientiousness unusual in men of his

[013-120]position. The result, unhappily, was not correspondent with the efforts.

[013-121]Alfred Yule had made a recognisable name among the critical writers of

[013-122]the day; seeing him in the title-lists of a periodical, most people knew

[013-123]what to expect, but not a few forbore the cutting open of the pages he

[013-124]occupied. He was learned, copious, occasionally mordant in style; but

[013-125]grace had been denied to him. He had of late begun to perceive the fact

[013-126]that those passages of Marian’s writing which were printed just as they

[013-127]came from her pen had merit of a kind quite distinct from anything of

[013-128]which he himself was capable, and it began to be a question with

[013-129]him whether it would not be advantageous to let the girl sign these

[013-130]compositions. A matter of business, to be sure--at all events in the

[013-131]first instance.

[013-132]For a long time Marian had scarcely looked up from the desk, but at this

[013-133]moment she found it necessary to refer to the invaluable Larousse. As so

[013-134]often happened, the particular volume of which she had need was not upon

[013-135]the shelf; she turned away, and looked about her with a gaze of weary

[013-136]disappointment. At a little distance were standing two young men,

[013-137]engaged, as their faces showed, in facetious colloquy; as soon as she

[013-138]observed them, Marian’s eyes fell, but the next moment she looked again

[013-139]in that direction. Her face had wholly changed; she wore a look of timid

[013-140]expectancy.

[013-141]The men were moving towards her, still talking and laughing. She turned

[013-142]to the shelves, and affected to search for a book. The voices drew near,

[013-143]and one of them was well known to her; now she could hear every word;

[013-144]now the speakers were gone by. Was it possible that Mr Milvain had not

[013-145]recognised her? She followed him with her eyes, and saw him take a seat

[013-146]not far off; he must have passed without even being aware of her.

[013-147]She went back to her place and for some minutes sat trifling with a pen.

[013-148]When she made a show of resuming work, it was evident that she could no

[013-149]longer apply herself as before. Every now and then she glanced at people

[013-150]who were passing; there were intervals when she wholly lost herself in

[013-151]reverie. She was tired, and had even a slight headache. When the hand of

[013-152]the clock pointed to half-past three, she closed the volume from which

[013-153]she had been copying extracts, and began to collect her papers.

[013-154]A voice spoke close behind her.

[013-155]‘Where’s your father, Miss Yule?’

[013-156]The speaker was a man of sixty, short, stout, tonsured by the hand of

[013-157]time. He had a broad, flabby face, the colour of an ancient turnip,

[013-158]save where one of the cheeks was marked with a mulberry stain; his

[013-159]eyes, grey-orbed in a yellow setting, glared with good-humoured

[013-160]inquisitiveness, and his mouth was that of the confirmed gossip. For

[013-161]eyebrows he had two little patches of reddish stubble; for moustache,

[013-162]what looked like a bit of discoloured tow, and scraps of similar

[013-163]material hanging beneath his creasy chin represented a beard. His garb

[013-164]must have seen a great deal of Museum service; it consisted of a jacket,

[013-165]something between brown and blue, hanging in capacious shapelessness,

[013-166]a waistcoat half open for lack of buttons and with one of the pockets

[013-167]coming unsewn, a pair of bronze-hued trousers which had all run to

[013-168]knee. Necktie he had none, and his linen made distinct appeal to the

[013-169]laundress.

[013-170]Marian shook hands with him.

[013-171]‘He went away at half-past two,’ was her reply to his question.

[013-172]‘How annoying! I wanted particularly to see him. I have been running

[013-173]about all day, and couldn’t get here before. Something important--most

[013-174]important. At all events, I can tell you. But I entreat that you won’t

[013-175]breathe a word save to your father.’

[013-176]Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]]--that was his name--had taken a vacant chair and drawn it

[013-177]close to Marian’s. He was in a state of joyous excitement, and talked

[013-178]in thick, rather pompous tones, with a pant at the end of a sentence. To

[013-179]emphasise the extremely confidential nature of his remarks, he brought

[013-180]his head almost in contact with the girl’s, and one of her thin,

[013-181]delicate hands was covered with his red, podgy fingers.
