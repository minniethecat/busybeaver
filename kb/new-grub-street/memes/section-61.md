# Section 61

[060-001]‘I have just invented a riddle; see if you can guess it. Why is a [[london|London]]

[060-002]lodging-house like the human body?’

[060-003][[harold-biffen|Biffen]] looked with some concern at his friend, so unwonted was a sally

[060-004]of this kind.

[060-005]‘Why is a London lodging-house--? Haven’t the least idea.’

[060-006]‘Because the brains are always at the top. Not bad, I think, eh?’

[060-007]‘Well, no; it’ll pass. Distinctly professional though. The general

[060-008]public would fail to see the point, I’m afraid. But what has come to

[060-009]you?’

[060-010]‘Good tidings. [[carter|Carter]] has offered me a place which will be a decided

[060-011]improvement. A house found--or rooms, at all events--and salary a

[060-012]hundred and fifty a year.

[060-013]‘By Plutus! That’s good hearing. Some duties attached, I suppose?’

[060-014]‘I’m afraid that was inevitable, as things go. It’s the secretaryship of

[060-015]a home for destitute boys at Croydon. The post is far from a sinecure,

[060-016]Carter assures me. There’s a great deal of purely secretarial work,

[060-017]and there’s a great deal of practical work, some of it rather rough,

[060-018]I fancy. It seems doubtful whether I am exactly the man. The present

[060-019]holder is a burly fellow over six feet high, delighting in gymnastics,

[060-020]and rather fond of a fight now and then when opportunity offers. But he

[060-021]is departing at Christmas--going somewhere as a missionary; and I can

[060-022]have the place if I choose.’

[060-023]‘As I suppose you do?’

[060-024]‘Yes. I shall try it, decidedly.’

[060-025]Biffen waited a little, then asked:

[060-026]‘I suppose your wife will go with you?’

[060-027]‘There’s no saying.’

[060-028][[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] tried to answer indifferently, but it could be seen that he was

[060-029]agitated between hopes and fears.

[060-030]‘You’ll ask her, at all events?’

[060-031]‘Oh yes,’ was the half-absent reply.

[060-032]‘But surely there can be no doubt that she’ll come. A hundred and fifty

[060-033]a year, without rent to pay. Why, that’s affluence!’

[060-034]‘The rooms I might occupy are in the home itself. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] won’t take very

[060-035]readily to a dwelling of that kind. And Croydon isn’t the most inviting

[060-036]locality.’

[060-037]‘Close to delightful country.’

[060-038]‘Yes, yes; but Amy doesn’t care about that.’

[060-039]‘You misjudge her, Reardon. You are too harsh. I implore you not to lose

[060-040]the chance of setting all right again! If only you could be put into my

[060-041]position for a moment, and then be offered the companionship of such a

[060-042]wife as yours!’

[060-043]Reardon listened with a face of lowering excitement.

[060-044]‘I should be perfectly within my rights,’ he said sternly, ‘if I merely

[060-045]told her when I have taken the position, and let her ask me to take her

[060-046]back--if she wishes.’

[060-047]‘You have changed a great deal this last year,’ replied Biffen, shaking

[060-048]his head, ‘a great deal. I hope to see you your old self again before

[060-049]long. I should have declared it impossible for you to become so rugged.

[060-050]Go and see your wife, there’s a good fellow.’

[060-051]‘No; I shall write to her.’

[060-052]‘Go and see her, I beg you! No good ever came of letter-writing between

[060-053]two people who have misunderstood each other. Go to Westbourne Park

[060-054]to-morrow. And be reasonable; be more than reasonable. The happiness

[060-055]of your life depends on what you do now. Be content to forget whatever

[060-056]wrong has been done you. To think that a man should need persuading to

[060-057]win back such a wife!’

[060-058]In truth, there needed little persuasion. Perverseness, one of the forms

[060-059]or issues of self-pity, made him strive against his desire, and caused

[060-060]him to adopt a tone of acerbity in excess of what he felt; but already

[060-061]he had made up his mind to see Amy. Even if this excuse had not

[060-062]presented itself he must very soon have yielded to the longing for

[060-063]a sight of his wife’s face which day by day increased among all the

[060-064]conflicting passions of which he was the victim. A month or two ago,

[060-065]when the summer sunshine made his confinement to the streets a daily

[060-066]torture, he convinced himself that there remained in him no trace of his

[060-067]love for Amy; there were moments when he thought of her with repugnance,

[060-068]as a cold, selfish woman, who had feigned affection when it seemed her

[060-069]interest to do so, but brutally declared her true self when there was

[060-070]no longer anything to be hoped from him. That was the self-deception of

[060-071]misery. Love, even passion, was still alive in the depths of his being;

[060-072]the animation with which he sped to his friend as soon as a new hope had

[060-073]risen was the best proof of his feeling.

[060-074]He went home and wrote to Amy.

[060-075]‘I have a reason for wishing to see you. Will you have the kindness to

[060-076]appoint an hour on Sunday morning when I can speak with you in private?

[060-077]It must be understood that I shall see no one else.’

[060-078]She would receive this by the first post to-morrow, Saturday, and

[060-079]doubtless would let him hear in reply some time in the afternoon.

[060-080]Impatience allowed him little sleep, and the next day was a long

[060-081]weariness of waiting. The evening he would have to spend at the

[060-082]hospital; if there came no reply before the time of his leaving home, he

[060-083]knew not how he should compel himself to the ordinary routine of work.

[060-084]Yet the hour came, and he had heard nothing. He was tempted to go at

[060-085]once to Westbourne Park, but reason prevailed with him. When he again

[060-086]entered the house, having walked at his utmost speed from the City Road,

[060-087]the letter lay waiting for him; it had been pushed beneath his door, and

[060-088]when he struck a match he found that one of his feet was upon the white

[060-089]envelope.

[060-090]Amy wrote that she would be at home at eleven to-morrow morning. Not

[060-091]another word.

[060-092]In all probability she knew of the offer that had been made to him; Mrs

[060-093]Carter would have told her. Was it of good or of ill omen that she wrote

[060-094]only these half-dozen words? Half through the night he plagued himself

[060-095]with suppositions, now thinking that her brevity promised a welcome,

[060-096]now that she wished to warn him against expecting anything but a cold,

[060-097]offended demeanour. At seven he was dressed; two hours and a half had

[060-098]to be killed before he could start on his walk westward. He would have

[060-099]wandered about the streets, but it rained.

[060-100]He had made himself as decent as possible in appearance, but he must

[060-101]necessarily seem an odd Sunday visitor at a house such as Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s.

[060-102]His soft felt hat, never brushed for months, was a greyish green, and

[060-103]stained round the band with perspiration. His necktie was discoloured

[060-104]and worn. Coat and waistcoat might pass muster, but of the trousers the

[060-105]less said the better. One of his boots was patched, and both were all

[060-106]but heelless.

[060-107]Very well; let her see him thus. Let her understand what it meant to

[060-108]live on twelve and sixpence a week.

[060-109]Though it was cold and wet he could not put on his overcoat. Three

[060-110]years ago it had been a fairly good ulster; at present, the edges of the

[060-111]sleeves were frayed, two buttons were missing, and the original hue of

[060-112]the cloth was indeterminable.

[060-113]At half-past nine he set out and struggled with his shabby umbrella

[060-114]against wind and rain. Down Pentonville Hill, up Euston Road, all

[060-115]along [[marylebone|Marylebone]] Road, then north-westwards towards the point of his

[060-116]destination. It was a good six miles from the one house to the other,

[060-117]but he arrived before the appointed time, and had to stray about until

[060-118]the cessation of bell-clanging and the striking of clocks told him it

[060-119]was eleven. Then he presented himself at the familiar door.

[060-120]On his asking for Mrs Reardon, he was at once admitted and led up to the

[060-121]drawing-room; the servant did not ask his name.

[060-122]Then he waited for a minute or two, feeling himself a squalid wretch

[060-123]amid the dainty furniture. The door opened. Amy, in a simple but very

[060-124]becoming dress, approached to within a yard of him; after the first

[060-125]glance she had averted her eyes, and she did not offer to shake hands.

[060-126]He saw that his muddy and shapeless boots drew her attention.

[060-127]‘Do you know why I have come?’ he asked.

[060-128]He meant the tone to be conciliatory, but he could not command his

[060-129]voice, and it sounded rough, hostile.

[060-130]‘I think so,’ Amy answered, seating herself gracefully. She would have

[060-131]spoken with less dignity but for that accent of his.

[060-132]‘The Carters have told you?’

[060-133]‘Yes; I have heard about it.’

[060-134]There was no promise in her manner. She kept her face turned away, and

[060-135]Reardon saw its beautiful profile, hard and cold as though in marble.

[060-136]‘It doesn’t interest you at all?’

[060-137]‘I am glad to hear that a better prospect offers for you.’

[060-138]He did not sit down, and was holding his rusty hat behind his back.

[060-139]‘You speak as if it in no way concerned yourself. Is that what you wish

[060-140]me to understand?’

[060-141]‘Won’t it be better if you tell me why you have come here? As you are

[060-142]resolved to find offence in whatever I say, I prefer to keep silence.

[060-143]Please to let me know why you have asked to see me.’

[060-144]Reardon turned abruptly as if to leave her, but checked himself at a

[060-145]little distance.

[060-146]Both had come to this meeting prepared for a renewal of amity, but in

[060-147]these first few moments each was so disagreeably impressed by the look

[060-148]and language of the other that a revulsion of feeling undid all the more

[060-149]hopeful effects of their long severance. On entering, Amy had meant to

[060-150]offer her hand, but the unexpected meanness of Reardon’s aspect shocked

[060-151]and restrained her. All but every woman would have experienced that

[060-152]shrinking from the livery of [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. Amy had but to reflect, and she

[060-153]understood that her husband could in no wise help this shabbiness;

[060-154]when he parted from her his wardrobe was already in a long-suffering

[060-155]condition, and how was he to have purchased new garments since then?

[060-156]None the less such attire degraded him in her eyes; it symbolised the

[060-157]melancholy decline which he had suffered intellectually. On Reardon his

[060-158]wife’s elegance had the same repellent effect, though this would not

[060-159]have been the case but for the expression of her countenance. Had it

[060-160]been possible for them to remain together during the first five minutes

[060-161]without exchange of words, sympathies might have prevailed on both

[060-162]sides; the first speech uttered would most likely have harmonised with

[060-163]their gentler thoughts. But the mischief was done so speedily.

[060-164]A man must indeed be graciously endowed if his personal appearance can

[060-165]defy the disadvantage of cheap modern clothing worn into shapelessness.

[060-166]Reardon had no such remarkable physique, and it was not wonderful that

[060-167]his wife felt ashamed of him. Strictly ashamed; he seemed to her a

[060-168]social inferior; the impression was so strong that it resisted all

[060-169]memory of his spiritual qualities. She might have anticipated this state

[060-170]of things, and have armed herself to encounter it, but somehow she had

[060-171]not done so. For more than five months she had been living among people

[060-172]who dressed well; the contrast was too suddenly forced upon her. She was

[060-173]especially susceptible in such matters, and had become none the less

[060-174]so under the demoralising influence of her misfortunes. True, she soon

[060-175]began to feel ashamed of her shame, but that could not annihilate the

[060-176]natural feeling and its results.

[060-177]‘I don’t love him. I can’t love him.’ Thus she spoke to herself, with

[060-178]immutable decision. She had been doubtful till now, but all doubt was at

[060-179]an end. Had Reardon been practical man enough to procure by hook or

[060-180]by crook a decent suit of clothes for this interview, that ridiculous

[060-181]trifle might have made all the difference in what was to result.

[060-182]He turned again, and spoke with the harshness of a man who feels that he

[060-183]is despised, and is determined to show an equal contempt.
