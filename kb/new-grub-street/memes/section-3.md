# Section 3

[002-001][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] mused concerning them. When he had walked a few yards, he looked

[002-002]back; at the same moment the unknown man also turned his head.

[002-003]‘Where the deuce have I seen them--him and the girl too?’ Milvain asked

[002-004]himself.

[002-005]And before he reached home the recollection he sought flashed upon his

[002-006]mind.

[002-007]‘The Museum Reading-room, of course!’

[002-008]CHAPTER II. THE HOUSE OF YULE

[002-009]‘I think’ said Jasper, as he entered the room where his mother and Maud

[002-010]were busy with plain needlework, ‘I must have met [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] and his

[002-011]daughter.’

[002-012]‘How did you recognise them?’ Mrs Milvain inquired.

[002-013]‘I passed an old buffer and a pale-faced girl whom I know by sight at

[002-014]the [[british-museum|British Museum]]. It wasn’t near Yule’s house, but they were taking a

[002-015]walk.’

[002-016]‘They may have come already. When Miss Harrow was here last, she said

[002-017]“in about a fortnight.”’

[002-018]‘No mistaking them for people of these parts, even if I hadn’t

[002-019]remembered their faces. Both of them are obvious dwellers in the valley

[002-020]of the shadow of books.’

[002-021]‘Is [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]] such a fright then?’ asked Maud.

[002-022]‘A fright! Not at all. A good example of the modern literary girl. I

[002-023]suppose you have the oddest old-fashioned ideas of such people. No,

[002-024]I rather like the look of her. Simpatica, I should think, as that ass

[002-025][[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] would say. A very delicate, pure complexion, though morbid;

[002-026]nice eyes; figure not spoilt yet. But of course I may be wrong about

[002-027]their identity.’

[002-028]Later in the afternoon Jasper’s conjecture was rendered a certainty.

[002-029]Maud had walked to [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]], where she would meet [[dora-milvain|Dora]] on the

[002-030]latter’s return from her teaching, and Mrs Milvain sat alone, in a

[002-031]mood of depression; there was a ring at the door-bell, and the servant

[002-032]admitted Miss Harrow.

[002-033]This lady acted as housekeeper to Mr [[john-yule|John Yule]], a wealthy resident in

[002-034]this neighbourhood; she was the sister of his deceased wife--a thin,

[002-035]soft-speaking, kindly woman of forty-five. The greater part of her life

[002-036]she had spent as a governess; her position now was more agreeable, and

[002-037]the removal of her anxiety about the future had developed qualities of

[002-038]cheerfulness which formerly no one would have suspected her to possess.

[002-039]The acquaintance between Mrs Milvain and her was only of twelve months’

[002-040]standing; prior to that, Mr Yule had inhabited a house at the end of

[002-041]Wattleborough remote from Finden.

[002-042]‘Our [[london|London]] visitors came yesterday,’ she began by saying.

[002-043]Mrs Milvain mentioned her son’s encounter an hour or two ago.

[002-044]‘No doubt it was they,’ said the visitor. ‘Mrs Yule hasn’t come; I

[002-045]hardly expected she would, you know. So very unfortunate when there are

[002-046]difficulties of that kind, isn’t it?’

[002-047]She smiled confidentially.

[002-048]‘The poor girl must feel it,’ said Mrs Milvain.

[002-049]‘I’m afraid she does. Of course it narrows the circle of her friends at

[002-050]home. She’s a sweet girl, and I should so like you to meet her. Do come

[002-051]and have tea with us to-morrow afternoon, will you? Or would it be too

[002-052]much for you just now?’

[002-053]‘Will you let the girls call? And then perhaps Miss Yule will be so good

[002-054]as to come and see me?’

[002-055]‘I wonder whether Mr Milvain would like to meet her father? I have

[002-056]thought that perhaps it might be some advantage to him. Alfred is so

[002-057]closely connected with literary people, you know.’

[002-058]‘I feel sure he would be glad,’ replied Mrs Milvain. ‘But--what of

[002-059]Jasper’s friendship with [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]] and the Reardons? Mightn’t it

[002-060]be a little awkward?’

[002-061]‘Oh, I don’t think so, unless he himself felt it so. There would be no

[002-062]need to mention that, I should say. And, really, it would be so much

[002-063]better if those estrangements came to an end. John makes no scruple of

[002-064]speaking freely about everyone, and I don’t think Alfred regards Mrs

[002-065]Edmund with any serious unkindness. If Mr Milvain would walk over with

[002-066]the young ladies to-morrow, it would be very pleasant.’

[002-067]‘Then I think I may promise that he will. I’m sure I don’t know where he

[002-068]is at this moment. We don’t see very much of him, except at meals.’

[002-069]‘He won’t be with you much longer, I suppose?’

[002-070]‘Perhaps a week.’

[002-071]Before Miss Harrow’s departure Maud and Dora reached home. They were

[002-072]curious to see the young lady from the valley of the shadow of books,

[002-073]and gladly accepted the invitation offered them.

[002-074]They set out on the following afternoon in their brother’s company. It

[002-075]was only a quarter of an hour’s walk to Mr Yule’s habitation, a small

[002-076]house in a large garden. Jasper was coming hither for the first time;

[002-077]his sisters now and then visited Miss Harrow, but very rarely saw Mr

[002-078]Yule himself who made no secret of the fact that he cared little for

[002-079]female society. In Wattleborough and the neighbourhood opinions varied

[002-080]greatly as to this gentleman’s character, but women seldom spoke

[002-081]very favourably of him. Miss Harrow was reticent concerning her

[002-082]brother-in-law; no one, however, had any reason to believe that she

[002-083]found life under his roof disagreeable. That she lived with him at all

[002-084]was of course occasionally matter for comment, certain Wattleborough

[002-085]ladies having their doubts regarding the position of a deceased wife’s

[002-086]sister under such circumstances; but no one was seriously exercised

[002-087]about the relations between this sober lady of forty-five and a man of

[002-088]sixty-three in broken health.

[002-089]A word of the family history.

[002-090]John, Alfred, and Edmund Yule were the sons of a Wattleborough

[002-091]stationer. Each was well educated, up to the age of seventeen, at the

[002-092]town’s grammar school. The eldest, who was a hot-headed lad, but showed

[002-093]capacities for business, worked at first with his father, endeavouring

[002-094]to add a bookselling department to the trade in stationery; but the life

[002-095]of home was not much to his taste, and at one-and-twenty he obtained a

[002-096]clerk’s place in the office of a London newspaper. Three years after,

[002-097]his father died, and the small patrimony which fell to him he used

[002-098]in making himself practically acquainted with the details of paper

[002-099]manufacture, his aim being to establish himself in partnership with an

[002-100]acquaintance who had started a small paper-mill in Hertfordshire.

[002-101]His speculation succeeded, and as years went on he became a thriving

[002-102]manufacturer. His brother Alfred, in the meantime, had drifted from work

[002-103]at a London bookseller’s into [[grub-street|the modern Grub Street]], his adventures in

[002-104]which region will concern us hereafter.

[002-105]Edmund carried on the Wattleborough business, but with small [[literary-ambition|success]].

[002-106]Between him and his eldest brother existed a good deal of affection,

[002-107]and in the end John offered him a share in his flourishing paper works;

[002-108]whereupon Edmund married, deeming himself well established for life. But

[002-109]John’s temper was a difficult one; Edmund and he quarrelled, parted; and

[002-110]when the younger died, aged about forty, he left but moderate provision

[002-111]for his widow and two children.

[002-112]Only when he had reached middle age did John marry; the experiment

[002-113]could not be called successful, and Mrs Yule died three years later,

[002-114]childless.

[002-115]At fifty-four John Yule retired from active business; he came back to

[002-116]the scenes of his early life, and began to take an important part in the

[002-117]municipal affairs of Wattleborough. He was then a remarkably robust man,

[002-118]fond of out-of-door exercise; he made it one of his chief efforts to

[002-119]encourage the local Volunteer movement, the cricket and football clubs,

[002-120]public sports of every kind, showing no sympathy whatever with those

[002-121]persons who wished to establish free libraries, lectures, and the like.

[002-122]At his own expense he built for the Volunteers a handsome drill-shed;

[002-123]he founded a public gymnasium; and finally he allowed it to be rumoured

[002-124]that he was going to present the town with a park. But by presuming too

[002-125]far upon the bodily vigour which prompted these activities, he passed of

[002-126]a sudden into the state of a confirmed invalid. On an autumn expedition

[002-127]in the Hebrides he slept one night under the open sky, with the result

[002-128]that he had an all but fatal attack of rheumatic fever. After that,

[002-129]though the direction of his interests was unchanged, he could no longer

[002-130]set the example to Wattleborough youth of muscular manliness. The

[002-131]infliction did not improve his temper; for the next year or two he was

[002-132]constantly at warfare with one or other of his colleagues and friends,

[002-133]ill brooking that the familiar control of various local interests should

[002-134]fall out of his hands. But before long he appeared to resign himself

[002-135]to his fate, and at present Wattleborough saw little of him. It seemed

[002-136]likely that he might still found the park which was to bear his name;

[002-137]but perhaps it would only be done in consequence of directions in his

[002-138]will. It was believed that he could not live much longer.

[002-139]With his kinsfolk he held very little communication. Alfred Yule, a

[002-140]battered man of letters, had visited Wattleborough only twice (including

[002-141]the present occasion) since John’s return hither. Mrs Edmund Yule, with

[002-142]her daughter--now [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]]--had been only once, three years ago.

[002-143]These two families, as you have heard, were not on terms of amity with

[002-144]each other, owing to difficulties between Mrs Alfred and Mrs Edmund; but

[002-145]John seemed to regard both impartially. Perhaps the only real warmth of

[002-146]feeling he had ever known was bestowed upon Edmund, and Miss Harrow had

[002-147]remarked that he spoke with somewhat more interest of Edmund’s daughter,

[002-148]Amy, than of Alfred’s daughter, Marian. But it was doubtful whether the

[002-149]sudden disappearance from the earth of all his relatives would greatly

[002-150]have troubled him. He lived a life of curious self-absorption, reading

[002-151]newspapers (little else), and talking with old friends who had stuck to

[002-152]him in spite of his irascibility.

[002-153]Miss Harrow received her visitors in a small and soberly furnished

[002-154]drawing-room. She was nervous, probably because of Jasper Milvain, whom

[002-155]she had met but once--last spring--and who on that occasion had struck

[002-156]her as an alarmingly modern young man. In the shadow of a window-curtain

[002-157]sat a slight, simply-dressed girl, whose short curly hair and thoughtful

[002-158]countenance Jasper again recognised. When it was his turn to be

[002-159]presented to Miss Yule, he saw that she doubted for an instant whether

[002-160]or not to give her hand; yet she decided to do so, and there was

[002-161]something very pleasant to him in its warm softness. She smiled with a

[002-162]slight embarrassment, meeting his look only for a second.

[002-163]‘I have seen you several times, Miss Yule,’ he said in a friendly way,

[002-164]‘though without knowing your name. It was under the great dome.’

[002-165]She laughed, readily understanding his phrase.

[002-166]‘I am there very often,’ was her reply.

[002-167]‘What great dome?’ asked Miss Harrow, with surprise.

[002-168]‘That of the British Museum Reading-room,’ explained Jasper; ‘known to

[002-169]some of us as the valley of the shadow of books. People who often work

[002-170]there necessarily get to know each other by sight.

[002-171]In the same way I knew Miss Yule’s father when I happened to pass him in

[002-172]the road yesterday.’

[002-173]The three girls began to converse together, perforce of trivialities.

[002-174]Marian Yule spoke in rather slow tones, thoughtfully, gently; she had

[002-175]linked her fingers, and laid her hands, palms downwards, upon her lap--a

[002-176]nervous action. Her accent was pure, unpretentious; and she used none of

[002-177]the fashionable turns of speech which would have suggested the habit of

[002-178]intercourse with distinctly metropolitan society.

[002-179]‘You must wonder how we exist in this out-of-the-way place,’ remarked

[002-180]Maud.

[002-181]‘Rather, I envy you,’ Marian answered, with a slight emphasis.

[002-182]The door opened, and Alfred Yule presented himself. He was tall, and his

[002-183]head seemed a disproportionate culmination to his meagre body, it was so

[002-184]large and massively featured. Intellect and uncertainty of temper were

[002-185]equally marked upon his visage; his brows were knitted in a permanent

[002-186]expression of severity. He had thin, smooth hair, grizzled whiskers, a

[002-187]shaven chin. In the multitudinous wrinkles of his face lay a history of

[002-188]laborious and stormy life; one readily divined in him a struggling and

[002-189]embittered man. Though he looked older than his years, he had by no

[002-190]means the appearance of being beyond the ripeness of his mental vigour.
