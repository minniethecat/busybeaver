# Section 25

[024-001]‘Then, so long as you let me come here now and then, I must give in. I

[024-002]promise not to trouble you with any more complaining. But how you can

[024-003]live such a life I don’t know. I consider myself more of a reader than

[024-004]women generally are, and I should be mortally offended if anyone called

[024-005]me frivolous; but I must have a good deal of society. Really and truly,

[024-006]I can’t live without it.’

[024-007]‘No?’ said [[amy-reardon|Amy]], with a smile which meant more than Edith could

[024-008]interpret. It seemed slightly condescending.

[024-009]‘There’s no knowing; perhaps if I had married a literary man---’ She

[024-010]paused, smiling and musing. ‘But then I haven’t, you see.’ She laughed.

[024-011]‘Albert is anything but a bookworm, as you know.’

[024-012]‘You wouldn’t wish him to be.’

[024-013]‘Oh no! Not a bookworm. To be sure, we suit each other very well indeed.

[024-014]He likes society just as much as I do. It would be the death of him if

[024-015]he didn’t spend three-quarters of every day with lively people.’

[024-016]‘That’s rather a large portion. But then you count yourself among the

[024-017]lively ones.’

[024-018]They exchanged looks, and laughed together.

[024-019]‘Of course you think me rather silly to want to talk so much with silly

[024-020]people,’ Edith went on. ‘But then there’s generally some amusement to

[024-021]be got, you know. I don’t take life quite so seriously as you do. People

[024-022]are people, after all; it’s good fun to see how they live and hear how

[024-023]they talk.’

[024-024]Amy felt that she was playing a sorry part. She thought of sour grapes,

[024-025]and of the fox who had lost his tail. Worst of all, perhaps Edith

[024-026]suspected the truth. She began to make inquiries about common

[024-027]acquaintances, and fell into an easier current of gossip.

[024-028]A quarter of an hour after the visitor’s departure [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] came back.

[024-029]Amy had guessed aright; the necessity of selling his books weighed upon

[024-030]him so that for the present he could do nothing. The evening was spent

[024-031]gloomily, with very little conversation.

[024-032]Next day came the bookseller to make his inspection. Reardon had

[024-033]chosen out and ranged upon a table nearly a hundred volumes. With a few

[024-034]exceptions, they had been purchased second-hand. The tradesman examined

[024-035]them rapidly.

[024-036]‘What do you ask?’ he inquired, putting his head aside.

[024-037]‘I prefer that you should make an offer,’ Reardon replied, with the

[024-038]helplessness of one who lives remote from traffic.

[024-039]‘I can’t say more than two pounds ten.’

[024-040]‘That is at the rate of sixpence a volume---?’

[024-041]‘To me that’s about the average value of books like these.’

[024-042]Perhaps the offer was a fair one; perhaps it was not. Reardon had

[024-043]neither time nor spirit to test the possibilities of the market; he was

[024-044]ashamed to betray his need by higgling.

[024-045]‘I’ll take it,’ he said, in a matter-of-fact voice.

[024-046]A messenger was sent for the books that afternoon. He stowed them

[024-047]skilfully in two bags, and carried them downstairs to a cart that was

[024-048]waiting.

[024-049]Reardon looked at the gaps left on his shelves. Many of those vanished

[024-050]volumes were dear old friends to him; he could have told you where he

[024-051]had picked them up and when; to open them recalled a past moment of

[024-052]intellectual growth, a mood of hope or despondency, a stage of struggle.

[024-053]In most of them his name was written, and there were often pencilled

[024-054]notes in the margin. Of course he had chosen from among the most

[024-055]valuable he possessed; such a multitude must else have been sold to make

[024-056]this sum of two pounds ten. Books are cheap, you know. At need, one can

[024-057]buy a Homer for fourpence, a Sophocles for sixpence. It was not rubbish

[024-058]that he had accumulated at so small expenditure, but the library of a

[024-059]poor student--battered bindings, stained pages, supplanted editions.

[024-060]He loved his books, but there was something he loved more, and when Amy

[024-061]glanced at him with eyes of sympathy he broke into a cheerful laugh.

[024-062]‘I’m only sorry they have gone for so little. Tell me when the [[money-in-literature|money]]

[024-063]is nearly at an end again, and you shall have more. It’s all right; the

[024-064]novel will be done soon.’

[024-065]And that night he worked until twelve o’clock, doggedly, fiercely.

[024-066]The next day was Sunday. As a rule he made it a day of rest, and almost

[024-067]perforce, for the depressing influence of Sunday in [[london|London]] made work too

[024-068]difficult. Then, it was the day on which he either went to see his own

[024-069]particular friends or was visited by them.

[024-070]‘Do you expect anyone this evening?’ Amy inquired.

[024-071]‘[[harold-biffen|Biffen]] will look in, I dare say. Perhaps [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]].’

[024-072]‘I think I shall take Willie to mother’s. I shall be back before eight.’

[024-073]‘Amy, don’t say anything about the books.’

[024-074]‘No, no.’

[024-075]‘I suppose they always ask you when we think of removing over the way?’

[024-076]He pointed in a direction that suggested [[marylebone|Marylebone]] Workhouse. Amy tried

[024-077]to laugh, but a woman with a child in her arms has no keen relish for

[024-078]such jokes.

[024-079]‘I don’t talk to them about our affairs,’ she said.

[024-080]‘That’s best.’

[024-081]She left home about three o’clock, the servant going with her to carry

[024-082]the child.

[024-083]At five a familiar knock sounded through the flat; it was a heavy rap

[024-084]followed by half-a-dozen light ones, like a reverberating echo, the last

[024-085]stroke scarcely audible. Reardon laid down his book, but kept his pipe

[024-086]in his mouth, and went to the door. A tall, thin man stood there, with a

[024-087]slouch hat and long grey overcoat. He shook hands silently, hung his hat

[024-088]in the passage, and came forward into the study.

[024-089]His name was Harold Biffen, and, to judge from his appearance, he did

[024-090]not belong to the race of common mortals. His excessive meagreness would

[024-091]all but have qualified him to enter an exhibition in the capacity of

[024-092]living skeleton, and the garments which hung upon this framework would

[024-093]perhaps have sold for three-and-sixpence at an old-clothes dealer’s. But

[024-094]the man was superior to these accidents of flesh and raiment. He had

[024-095]a fine face: large, gentle eyes, nose slightly aquiline, small and

[024-096]delicate mouth. Thick black hair fell to his coat-collar; he wore a

[024-097]heavy moustache and a full beard. In his gait there was a singular

[024-098]dignity; only a man of cultivated mind and graceful character could move

[024-099]and stand as he did.

[024-100]His first act on entering the room was to take from his pocket a pipe,

[024-101]a pouch, a little tobacco-stopper, and a box of matches, all of which

[024-102]he arranged carefully on a corner of the central table. Then he drew

[024-103]forward a chair and seated himself.

[024-104]‘Take your top-coat off;’ said Reardon.

[024-105]‘Thanks, not this evening.’

[024-106]‘Why the deuce not?’

[024-107]‘Not this evening, thanks.’

[024-108]The reason, as soon as Reardon sought for it, was obvious. Biffen had

[024-109]no ordinary coat beneath the other. To have referred to this fact would

[024-110]have been indelicate; the novelist of course understood it, and smiled,

[024-111]but with no mirth.

[024-112]‘Let me have your Sophocles,’ were the visitor’s next words.

[024-113]Reardon offered him a volume of the [[oxford|Oxford]] Pocket Classics.

[024-114]‘I prefer the Wunder, please.’

[024-115]‘It’s gone, my boy.’

[024-116]‘Gone?’

[024-117]‘Wanted a little cash.’

[024-118]Biffen uttered a sound in which remonstrance and sympathy were blended.

[024-119]‘I’m sorry to hear that; very sorry. Well, this must do. Now, I want to

[024-120]know how you scan this chorus in the “Oedipus Rex.”’

[024-121]Reardon took the volume, considered, and began to read aloud with metric

[024-122]emphasis.

[024-123]‘Choriambics, eh?’ cried the other. ‘Possible, of course; but treat them

[024-124]as Ionics a minore with an anacrusis, and see if they don’t go better.’

[024-125]He involved himself in terms of pedantry, and with such delight that his

[024-126]eyes gleamed. Having delivered a technical lecture, he began to read in

[024-127]illustration, producing quite a different effect from that of the

[024-128]rhythm as given by his friend. And the reading was by no means that of a

[024-129]pedant, rather of a poet.

[024-130]For half an hour the two men talked Greek metres as if they lived in a

[024-131]world where the only hunger known could be satisfied by grand or sweet

[024-132]cadences.

[024-133]They had first met in an amusing way. Not long after the publication of

[024-134]his book ‘[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]]’ Reardon was spending a week at Hastings.

[024-135]A rainy day drove him to the circulating library, and as he was looking

[024-136]along the shelves for something readable a voice near at hand asked the

[024-137]attendant if he had anything ‘by Edwin Reardon.’ The novelist turned in

[024-138]astonishment; that any casual mortal should inquire for his books seemed

[024-139]incredible. Of course there was nothing by that author in the library,

[024-140]and he who had asked the question walked out again. On the morrow

[024-141]Reardon encountered this same man at a lonely part of the shore; he

[024-142]looked at him, and spoke a word or two of common civility; they got into

[024-143]conversation, with the result that Edwin told the story of yesterday.

[024-144]The stranger introduced himself as Harold Biffen, an author in a small

[024-145]way, and a teacher whenever he could get pupils; an abusive review had

[024-146]interested him in Reardon’s novels, but as yet he knew nothing of them

[024-147]but the names.

[024-148]Their tastes were found to be in many respects sympathetic, and after

[024-149]returning to London they saw each other frequently. Biffen was always in

[024-150]dire [[literary-poverty|poverty]], and lived in the oddest places; he had seen harder trials

[024-151]than even Reardon himself. The teaching by which he partly lived was of

[024-152]a kind quite unknown to the respectable tutorial world. In these days

[024-153]of examinations, numbers of men in a poor position--clerks

[024-154]chiefly--conceive a hope that by ‘passing’ this, that, or the other

[024-155]formal test they may open for themselves a new career. Not a few such

[024-156]persons nourish preposterous ambitions; there are warehouse clerks

[024-157]privately preparing (without any means or prospect of them) for a

[024-158]call to the Bar, drapers’ assistants who ‘go in’ for the preliminary

[024-159]examination of the College of Surgeons, and untaught men innumerable who

[024-160]desire to procure enough show of education to be eligible for a curacy.

[024-161]Candidates of this stamp frequently advertise in the newspapers for

[024-162]cheap tuition, or answer advertisements which are intended to appeal to

[024-163]them; they pay from sixpence to half-a-crown an hour--rarely as much as

[024-164]the latter sum. Occasionally it happened that Harold Biffen had three or

[024-165]four such pupils in hand, and extraordinary stories he could draw from

[024-166]his large experience in this sphere.

[024-167]Then as to his authorship.--But shortly after the discussion of Greek

[024-168]metres he fell upon the subject of his literary projects, and, by no

[024-169]means for the first time, developed the theory on which he worked.

[024-170]‘I have thought of a new way of putting it. What I really aim at is an

[024-171][[literary-realism|absolute realism]] in the sphere of the ignobly decent. The field, as I

[024-172]understand it, is a new one; I don’t know any writer who has treated

[024-173]ordinary vulgar life with fidelity and seriousness. Zola writes

[024-174]deliberate tragedies; his vilest figures become heroic from the

[024-175]place they fill in a strongly imagined drama. I want to deal with the

[024-176]essentially unheroic, with the day-to-day life of that vast majority of

[024-177]people who are at the mercy of paltry circumstance. Dickens understood

[024-178]the possibility of such work, but his tendency to melodrama on the one

[024-179]hand, and his humour on the other, prevented him from thinking of it. An

[024-180]instance, now. As I came along by Regent’s Park half an hour ago a man

[024-181]and a girl were walking close in front of me, love-making; I passed them

[024-182]slowly and heard a good deal of their talk--it was part of the situation

[024-183]that they should pay no heed to a stranger’s proximity. Now, such

[024-184]a love-scene as that has absolutely never been written down; it was

[024-185]entirely decent, yet vulgar to the nth power. Dickens would have made

[024-186]it ludicrous--a gross injustice. Other men who deal with low-[[social-class|class]] life

[024-187]would perhaps have preferred idealising it--an absurdity. For my

[024-188]own part, I am going to reproduce it verbatim, without one single

[024-189]impertinent suggestion of any point of view save that of honest

[024-190]reporting. The result will be something unutterably tedious. Precisely.

[024-191]That is the stamp of the ignobly decent life. If it were anything but

[024-192]tedious it would be untrue. I speak, of course, of its effect upon the

[024-193]ordinary reader.’
