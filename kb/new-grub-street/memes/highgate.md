# Highgate

Highgate is the northern companion to [[Hampstead]] — the two villages sit on the same green ridge that rises above the sprawl of Victorian London. In *New Grub Street*, the ridge is described as "the green ridge from Hampstead to Highgate" (007-076), a landscape feature visible from the upper windows of houses on the city's northern edge. Where Hampstead appears frequently as a place of residence, Highgate is more distant — glimpsed, mentioned as a destination, a point on the horizon.

## Role in the Novel

Highgate appears in the novel's geographical imagination rather than as a developed setting. The "roads which lead to Hampstead, to Highgate" (031-142) mark the northern escape routes from literary London — paths that lead away from the [[grub-street|Grub Street]] grind toward cleaner air and quieter streets. For characters whose lives are circumscribed by the [[British Museum|Reading Room]] and cheap lodgings, the mention of Highgate carries the suggestion of a different kind of life.

The village was known in the late Victorian period for its healthy elevation, its cemetery (the final resting place of Karl Marx, among other notables), and its character as a semi-rural retreat within reach of the city. Gissing uses it sparingly — a geographical marker rather than a stage — but its pairing with Hampstead in the novel's topographical vocabulary is significant: together they form the northern boundary of the literary world the novel inhabits.
