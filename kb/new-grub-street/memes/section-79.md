# Section 79

[078-001]‘Perhaps--perhaps--perhaps!’ cried [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], contemptuously. ‘There goes

[078-002]the razor again! Take the plain, human construction of what happens. Ask

[078-003]yourself what the vulgar man would do, and do likewise; that’s the only

[078-004]safe rule for you.’

[078-005]They were both hoarse with too much talking, and for the last half of

[078-006]the drive neither spoke.

[078-007]At the railway-station they ate and drank together, but with poor

[078-008]pretence of appetite. As long as possible they kept within the warmed

[078-009]rooms. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] was pale, and had anxious, restless eyes; he could not

[078-010]remain seated, though when he had walked about for a few minutes the

[078-011]trembling of his limbs obliged him to sink down. It was an unutterable

[078-012]relief to both when the moment of the train’s starting approached.

[078-013]They clasped hands warmly, and exchanged a few last requests and

[078-014]promises.

[078-015]‘Forgive my plain speech, old fellow,’ said Biffen. ‘Go and be happy!’

[078-016]Then he stood alone on the platform, watching the red light on the last

[078-017]carriage as the train whirled away into darkness and storm.

[078-018]CHAPTER XXXII. REARDON BECOMES PRACTICAL

[078-019]Reardon had never been to [[brighton|Brighton]], and of his own accord never would

[078-020]have gone; he was prejudiced against the place because its name has

[078-021]become suggestive of fashionable imbecility and the snobbishness which

[078-022]tries to model itself thereon; he knew that the town was a mere portion

[078-023]of [[london|London]] transferred to the sea-shore, and as he loved the strand and

[078-024]the breakers for their own sake, to think of them in such connection

[078-025]could be nothing but a trial of his temper. Something of this species of

[078-026]irritation affected him in the first part of his journey, and disturbed

[078-027]the mood of kindliness with which he was approaching [[amy-reardon|Amy]]; but towards

[078-028]the end he forgot this in a growing desire to be beside his wife in her

[078-029]trouble. His impatience made the hour and a half seem interminable.

[078-030]The fever which was upon him had increased. He coughed frequently; his

[078-031]breathing was difficult; though constantly moving, he felt as if, in

[078-032]the absence of excitement, his one wish would have been to lie down and

[078-033]abandon himself to lethargy. Two men who sat with him in the third-[[social-class|class]]

[078-034]carriage had spread a rug over their knees and amused themselves with

[078-035]playing cards for trifling sums of [[money-in-literature|money]]; the sight of their foolish

[078-036]faces, the sound of their laughs, the talk they interchanged,

[078-037]exasperated him to the last point of endurance; but for all that he

[078-038]could not draw his attention from them. He seemed condemned by some

[078-039]spiritual tormentor to take an interest in their endless games, and to

[078-040]observe their visages until he knew every line with a hateful intimacy.

[078-041]One of the men had a moustache of unusual form; the ends curved upward

[078-042]with peculiar suddenness, and Reardon was constrained to speculate as

[078-043]to the mode of training by which this singularity had been produced. He

[078-044]could have shed tears of nervous distraction in his inability to turn

[078-045]his thoughts upon other things.

[078-046]On alighting at his journey’s end he was seized with a fit of shivering,

[078-047]an intense and sudden chill which made his teeth chatter. In an

[078-048]endeavour to overcome this he began to run towards the row of cabs, but

[078-049]his legs refused such exercise, and coughing compelled him to pause for

[078-050]breath. Still shaking, he threw himself into a vehicle and was driven to

[078-051]the address Amy had mentioned. The snow on the ground lay thick, but no

[078-052]more was falling.

[078-053]Heedless of the direction which the cab took, he suffered his physical

[078-054]and mental unrest for another quarter of an hour, then a stoppage told

[078-055]him that the house was reached. On his way he had heard a clock strike

[078-056]eleven.

[078-057]The door opened almost as soon as he had rung the bell. He mentioned

[078-058]his name, and the maid-servant conducted him to a drawing-room on the

[078-059]ground-floor. The house was quite a small one, but seemed to be well

[078-060]furnished. One lamp burned on the table, and the fire had sunk to a red

[078-061]glow. Saying that she would inform Mrs Reardon at once, the servant left

[078-062]him alone.

[078-063]He placed his bag on the floor, took off his muffler, threw back his

[078-064]overcoat, and sat waiting. The overcoat was new, but the garments

[078-065]beneath it were his poorest, those he wore when sitting in his garret,

[078-066]for he had neither had time to change them, nor thought of doing so.

[078-067]He heard no approaching footstep but Amy came into the room in a way

[078-068]which showed that she had hastened downstairs. She looked at him, then

[078-069]drew near with both hands extended, and laid them on his shoulders, and

[078-070]kissed him. Reardon shook so violently that it was all he could do to

[078-071]remain standing; he seized one of her hands, and pressed it against his

[078-072]lips.

[078-073]‘How hot your breath is!’ she said. ‘And how you tremble! Are you ill?’

[078-074]‘A bad cold, that’s all,’ he answered thickly, and coughed. ‘How is

[078-075]Willie?’

[078-076]‘In great danger. The doctor is coming again to-night; we thought that

[078-077]was his ring.’

[078-078]‘You didn’t expect me to-night?’

[078-079]‘I couldn’t feel sure whether you would come.’

[078-080]‘Why did you send for me, Amy? Because Willie was in danger, and you

[078-081]felt I ought to know about it?’

[078-082]‘Yes--and because I--’

[078-083]She burst into tears. The display of emotion came very suddenly; her

[078-084]words had been spoken in a firm voice, and only the pained knitting of

[078-085]her brows had told what she was suffering.

[078-086]‘If Willie dies, what shall I do? Oh, what shall I do?’ broke forth

[078-087]between her sobs.

[078-088]Reardon took her in his arms, and laid his hand upon her head in the old

[078-089]loving way.

[078-090]‘Do you wish me to go up and see him, Amy?’

[078-091]‘Of course. But first, let me tell you why we are here. Edith--Mrs

[078-092][[carter|Carter]]--was coming to spend a week with her mother, and she pressed

[078-093]me to join her. I didn’t really wish to; I was unhappy, and felt how

[078-094]impossible it was to go on always living away from you. Oh, that I had

[078-095]never come! Then Willie would have been as well as ever.’

[078-096]‘Tell me when and how it began.’

[078-097]She explained briefly, then went on to tell of other circumstances.

[078-098]‘I have a nurse with me in the room. It’s my own bedroom, and this house

[078-099]is so small it will be impossible to give you a bed here, Edwin. But

[078-100]there’s an hotel only a few yards away.’

[078-101]‘Yes, yes; don’t trouble about that.’

[078-102]‘But you look so ill--you are shaking so. Is it a cold you have had

[078-103]long?’

[078-104]‘Oh, my old habit; you remember. One cold after another, all through the

[078-105]accursed winter. What does that matter when you speak kindly to me once

[078-106]more? I had rather die now at your feet and see the old gentleness when

[078-107]you look at me, than live on estranged from you. No, don’t kiss me, I

[078-108]believe these vile sore-throats are contagious.’

[078-109]‘But your lips are so hot and parched! And to think of your coming this

[078-110]journey, on such a night!’

[078-111]‘Good old Biffen came to the station with me. He was angry because I had

[078-112]kept away from you so long. Have you given me your heart again, Amy?’

[078-113]‘Oh, it has all been a wretched mistake! But we were so poor. Now all

[078-114]that is over; if only Willie can be saved to me! I am so anxious for

[078-115]the doctor’s coming; the poor little child can hardly draw a breath. How

[078-116]cruel it is that such suffering should come upon a little creature who

[078-117]has never done or thought ill!’

[078-118]‘You are not the first, dearest, who has revolted against nature’s

[078-119]cruelty.’

[078-120]‘Let us go up at once, Edwin. Leave your coat and things here. Mrs

[078-121]Winter--Edith’s mother--is a very old lady; she has gone to bed. And I

[078-122]dare say you wouldn’t care to see Mrs Carter to-night?’

[078-123]‘No, no! only you and Willie.’

[078-124]‘When the doctor comes hadn’t you better ask his advice for yourself?’

[078-125]‘We shall see. Don’t trouble about me.’

[078-126]They went softly up to the first floor, and entered a bedroom.

[078-127]Fortunately the light here was very dim, or the nurse who sat by the

[078-128]child’s bed must have wondered at the eccentricity with which her

[078-129]patient’s father attired himself. Bending over the little sufferer,

[078-130]Reardon felt for the first time since Willie’s birth a strong fatherly

[078-131]emotion; tears rushed to his eyes, and he almost crushed Amy’s hand as

[078-132]he held it during the spasm of his intense feeling.

[078-133]He sat here for a long time without speaking. The warmth of the chamber

[078-134]had the reverse of an assuaging effect upon his difficult breathing and

[078-135]his frequent short cough--it seemed to oppress and confuse his brain. He

[078-136]began to feel a pain in his right side, and could not sit upright on the

[078-137]chair.

[078-138]Amy kept regarding him, without his being aware of it.

[078-139]‘Does your head ache?’ she whispered.

[078-140]He nodded, but did not speak.

[078-141]‘Oh, why doesn’t the doctor come? I must send in a few minutes.’

[078-142]But as soon as she had spoken a bell rang in the lower part of the

[078-143]house. Amy had no doubt that it announced the promised visit.

[078-144]She left the room, and in a minute or two returned with the medical

[078-145]man. When the examination of the child was over, Reardon requested a few

[078-146]words with the doctor in the room downstairs.

[078-147]‘I’ll come back to you,’ he whispered to Amy.

[078-148]The two descended together, and entered the drawing-room.

[078-149]‘Is there any hope for the little fellow?’ Reardon asked.

[078-150]Yes, there was hope; a favourable turn might be expected.

[078-151]‘Now I wish to trouble you for a moment on my own account. I shouldn’t

[078-152]be surprised if you tell me that I have congestion of the lungs.’

[078-153]The doctor, a suave man of fifty, had been inspecting his interlocutor

[078-154]with curiosity. He now asked the necessary questions, and made an

[078-155]examination.

[078-156]‘Have you had any lung trouble before this?’ he inquired gravely.

[078-157]‘Slight congestion of the right lung not many weeks ago.’

[078-158]‘I must order you to bed immediately. Why have you allowed your symptoms

[078-159]to go so far without--’

[078-160]‘I have just come down from London,’ interrupted Reardon.

[078-161]‘Tut, tut, tut! To bed this moment, my dear sir! There is inflammation,

[078-162]and--’

[078-163]‘I can’t have a bed in this house; there is no spare room. I must go to

[078-164]the nearest hotel.’

[078-165]‘Positively? Then let me take you. My carriage is at the door.’

[078-166]‘One thing--I beg you won’t tell my wife that this is serious. Wait till

[078-167]she is out of her anxiety about the child.’

[078-168]‘You will need the services of a nurse. A most unfortunate thing that

[078-169]you are obliged to go to the hotel.’

[078-170]‘It can’t be helped. If a nurse is necessary, I must engage one.’

[078-171]He had the strange sensation of knowing that whatever was needful could

[078-172]be paid for; it relieved his mind immensely. To the rich, illness has

[078-173]none of the worst horrors only understood by the poor.

[078-174]‘Don’t speak a word more than you can help,’ said the doctor as he

[078-175]watched Reardon withdraw.

[078-176]Amy stood on the lower stairs, and came down as soon as her husband

[078-177]showed himself.

[078-178]‘The doctor is good enough to take me in his carriage,’ he whispered.

[078-179]‘It is better that I should go to bed, and get a good night’s rest. I

[078-180]wish I could have sat with you, Amy.’

[078-181]‘Is it anything? You look worse than when you came, Edwin.’

[078-182]‘A feverish cold. Don’t give it a thought, dearest. Go to Willie.

[078-183]Good-night!’

[078-184]She threw her arms about him.

[078-185]‘I shall come to see you if you are not able to be here by nine in the

[078-186]morning,’ she said, and added the name of the hotel to which he was to

[078-187]go.
