# Amy Reardon

The novel's most contested female character — vilified by some readers as mercenary, defended by others as realistic. Amy Reardon is a woman of intelligence and taste who marries a promising novelist and finds herself trapped in poverty she cannot bear. Gissing's treatment of her is notably even-handed: her position is as sympathetically rendered as her husband's.

## Character Overview

Amy is introduced as Edwin Reardon's wife, living with him in a modest flat. She has "cultivated tastes" and "middle-class expectations" — phrases that are not ironic in Gissing's rendering. She married Reardon expecting a life of literary society and modest comfort; she finds herself in grinding poverty, watching her husband's career collapse and her social standing erode. Her response — withdrawal, resentment, and eventual flight — is depicted as comprehensible rather than villainous.

## Marriage and Poverty

The Reardon marriage is the novel's central domestic tragedy. Reardon himself acknowledges that "even in mid-rapture of his marriage month he had foreseen this" (009-062) — the financial impossibility of the life they had planned. Amy's inability to accept their circumstances is not greed but the rational response of someone who expected a middle-class life and received something closer to destitution. When she inherits money, the power dynamic between husband and wife reverses, and the marriage becomes unsustainable.

Gissing's genius in this portrait is his refusal to take sides. Amy is not wrong to want financial security; Reardon is not wrong to pursue artistic integrity. The tragedy is structural — the marriage cannot survive in a system where literature does not pay.

## Key Relationships

- **Edwin Reardon**: Her husband. Their marriage is a love match that founders on economics. Amy's departure from Reardon is the novel's most painful domestic rupture.

- **Jasper Milvain**: Amy and Jasper share a pragmatic understanding of the literary marketplace. Her later residence with her mother — and Milvain's visits — suggest an alignment of values that Reardon cannot share.

## Thematic Significance

Amy Reardon represents the domestic cost of literary failure. She is not an antagonist but a victim of the same system that destroys her husband — a woman whose entirely reasonable expectations are incompatible with the economic realities of Grub Street.
