# Section 58

[057-001]‘She is a difficult person to judge. I never disliked her, by any means;

[057-002]but she was decidedly out of place as the wife of a struggling author.

[057-003]Perhaps I have been a little prejudiced against her since [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]

[057-004]quarrelled with me on her account.’

[057-005][[marian-yule|Marian]] was astonished at this unlooked-for explanation of the rupture

[057-006]between [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] and his friend. That they had not seen each other for

[057-007]some months she knew from Jasper himself but no definite cause had been

[057-008]assigned.

[057-009]‘I may as well let you know all about it,’ Milvain continued, seeing

[057-010]that he had disconcerted the girl, as he meant to. ‘I met Reardon not

[057-011]long after they had parted, and he charged me with being in great part

[057-012]the cause of his troubles.’

[057-013]The listener did not raise her eyes.

[057-014]‘You would never imagine what my fault was. Reardon declared that the

[057-015]tone of my conversation had been morally injurious to his wife. He said

[057-016]I was always glorifying worldly [[literary-ambition|success]], and that this had made her

[057-017]discontented with her lot. Sounds rather ludicrous, don’t you think?’

[057-018]‘It was very strange.’

[057-019]‘Reardon was in desperate earnest, poor fellow. And, to tell you the

[057-020]truth, I fear there may have been something in his complaint.

[057-021]I told him at once that I should henceforth keep away from Mrs Edmund

[057-022][[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s; and so I have done, with the result, of course, that they

[057-023]suppose I condemn [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]]’s behaviour. The affair was a nuisance,

[057-024]but I had no choice, I think.’

[057-025]‘You say that perhaps your talk really was harmful to her.’

[057-026]‘It may have been, though such a danger never occurred to me.’

[057-027]‘Then Amy must be very weak-minded.’

[057-028]‘To be influenced by such a paltry fellow?’

[057-029]‘To be influenced by anyone in such a way.’

[057-030]‘You think the worse of me for this story?’ Jasper asked.

[057-031]‘I don’t quite understand it. How did you talk to her?’

[057-032]‘As I talk to everyone. You have heard me say the same things many a

[057-033]time. I simply declare my opinion that the end of literary work--unless

[057-034]one is a man of [[literary-genius|genius]]--is to secure comfort and repute. This doesn’t

[057-035]seem to me very scandalous. But Mrs Reardon was perhaps too urgent in

[057-036]repeating such views to her husband. She saw that in my case they were

[057-037]likely to have solid results, and it was a misery to her that Reardon

[057-038]couldn’t or wouldn’t work in the same practical way.

[057-039]‘It was very unfortunate.’

[057-040]‘And you are inclined to blame me?’

[057-041]‘No; because I am so sure that you only spoke in the way natural to you,

[057-042]without a thought of such consequences.’

[057-043]Jasper smiled.

[057-044]‘That’s precisely the truth. Nearly all men who have their way to make

[057-045]think as I do, but most feel obliged to adopt a false tone, to talk

[057-046]about literary conscientiousness, and so on. I simply say what I think,

[057-047]with no pretences. I should like to be conscientious, but it’s a luxury

[057-048]I can’t afford. I’ve told you all this often enough, you know.’

[057-049]‘Yes.’

[057-050]‘But it hasn’t been morally injurious to you,’ he said with a laugh.

[057-051]‘Not at all. Still I don’t like it.’

[057-052]Jasper was startled. He gazed at her. Ought he, then, to have dealt

[057-053]with her less frankly? Had he been mistaken in thinking that the

[057-054]unusual openness of his talk was attractive to her? She spoke with quite

[057-055]unaccustomed decision; indeed, he had noticed from her entrance that

[057-056]there was something unfamiliar in her way of conversing. She was so much

[057-057]more self-possessed than of wont, and did not seem to treat him with the

[057-058]same deference, the same subdual of her own personality.

[057-059]‘You don’t like it?’ he repeated calmly. ‘It has become rather tiresome

[057-060]to you?’

[057-061]‘I feel sorry that you should always represent yourself in an

[057-062]unfavourable light.’

[057-063]He was an acute man, but the self-confidence with which he had entered

[057-064]upon this dialogue, his conviction that he had but to speak when he

[057-065]wished to receive assurance of Marian’s devotion, prevented him from

[057-066]understanding the tone of independence she had suddenly adopted. With

[057-067]more modesty he would have felt more subtly at this juncture, would have

[057-068]divined that the girl had an exquisite pleasure in drawing back now that

[057-069]she saw him approaching her with unmistakable purpose, that she wished

[057-070]to be wooed in less off-hand fashion before confessing what was in her

[057-071]heart. For the moment he was disconcerted. Those last words of hers had

[057-072]a slight tone of superiority, the last thing he would have expected upon

[057-073]her lips.

[057-074]‘Yet I surely haven’t always appeared so--to you?’ he said.

[057-075]‘No, not always.’

[057-076]‘But you are in doubt concerning the real man?’

[057-077]‘I’m not sure that I understand you. You say that you do really think as

[057-078]you speak.’

[057-079]‘So I do. I think that there is no choice for a man who can’t bear

[057-080][[literary-poverty|poverty]]. I have never said, though, that I had pleasure in mean

[057-081]necessities; I accept them because I can’t help it.’

[057-082]It was a delight to Marian to observe the anxiety with which he turned

[057-083]to self-defence. Never in her life had she felt this joy of holding a

[057-084]position of command. It was nothing to her that Jasper valued her more

[057-085]because of her [[money-in-literature|money]]; impossible for it to be otherwise. Satisfied that

[057-086]he did value her, to begin with, for her own sake, she was very willing

[057-087]to accept money as her ally in the winning of his love. He scarcely

[057-088]loved her yet, as she understood the feeling, but she perceived her

[057-089]power over him, and passion taught her how to exert it.

[057-090]‘But you resign yourself very cheerfully to the necessity,’ she said,

[057-091]looking at him with merely intellectual eyes.

[057-092]‘You had rather I lamented my fate in not being able to devote myself to

[057-093]nobly unremunerative work?’

[057-094]There was a note of irony here. It caused her a tremor, but she held her

[057-095]position.

[057-096]‘That you never do so would make one think--but I won’t speak unkindly.’

[057-097]‘That I neither care for good work nor am capable of it,’ Jasper

[057-098]finished her sentence. ‘I shouldn’t have thought it would make you think

[057-099]so.’

[057-100]Instead of replying she turned her look towards the door. There was a

[057-101]footstep on the stairs, but it passed.

[057-102]‘I thought it might be [[dora-milvain|Dora]],’ she said.

[057-103]‘She won’t be here for another couple of hours at least,’ replied Jasper

[057-104]with a slight smile.

[057-105]‘But you said--?’

[057-106]‘I sent her to Mrs Boston Wright’s that I might have an opportunity of

[057-107]talking to you. Will you forgive the stratagem?’

[057-108]Marian resumed her former attitude, the faintest smile hovering about

[057-109]her lips.

[057-110]‘I’m glad there’s plenty of time,’ he continued. ‘I begin to suspect

[057-111]that you have been misunderstanding me of late. I must set that right.’

[057-112]‘I don’t think I have misunderstood you.’

[057-113]‘That may mean something very disagreeable. I know that some people whom

[057-114]I esteem have a very poor opinion of me, but I can’t allow you to be one

[057-115]of them. What do I seem to you? What is the result on your mind of all

[057-116]our conversations?’

[057-117]‘I have already told you.’

[057-118]‘Not seriously. Do you believe I am capable of generous feeling?’

[057-119]‘To say no, would be to put you in the lowest [[social-class|class]] of men, and that a

[057-120]very small one.’

[057-121]‘Good! Then I am not among the basest. But that doesn’t give me very

[057-122]distinguished claims upon your consideration. Whatever I am, I am high

[057-123]in some of my ambitions.’

[057-124]‘Which of them?’

[057-125]‘For instance, I have been daring enough to hope that you might love

[057-126]me.’

[057-127]Marian delayed for a moment, then said quietly:

[057-128]‘Why do you call that daring?’

[057-129]‘Because I have enough of old-fashioned thought to believe that a woman

[057-130]who is worthy of a man’s love is higher than he, and condescends in

[057-131]giving herself to him.’

[057-132]His voice was not convincing; the phrase did not sound natural on his

[057-133]lips. It was not thus that she had hoped to hear him speak. Whilst he

[057-134]expressed himself thus conventionally he did not love her as she desired

[057-135]to be loved.

[057-136]‘I don’t hold that view,’ she said.

[057-137]‘It doesn’t surprise me. You are very reserved on all subjects, and we

[057-138]have never spoken of this, but of course I know that your thought is

[057-139]never commonplace. Hold what view you like of woman’s position, that

[057-140]doesn’t affect mine.’

[057-141]‘Is yours commonplace, then?’

[057-142]‘Desperately. Love is a very old and common thing, and I believe I love

[057-143]you in the old and common way. I think you beautiful, you seem to me

[057-144]womanly in the best sense, full of charm and sweetness. I know myself a

[057-145]coarse being in comparison. All this has been felt and said in the same

[057-146]way by men infinite in variety. Must I find some new expression before

[057-147]you can believe me?’

[057-148]Marian kept silence.

[057-149]‘I know what you are thinking,’ he said. ‘The thought is as inevitable

[057-150]as my consciousness of it.’

[057-151]For an instant she looked at him.

[057-152]‘Yes, you look the thought. Why have I not spoken to you in this

[057-153]way before? Why have I waited until you are obliged to suspect my

[057-154]sincerity?’

[057-155]‘My thought is not so easily read, then,’ said Marian.

[057-156]‘To be sure it hasn’t a gross form, but I know you wish--whatever your

[057-157]real feeling towards me--that I had spoken a fortnight ago. You would

[057-158]wish that of any man in my position, merely because it is painful to you

[057-159]to see a possible insincerity. Well, I am not insincere. I have thought

[057-160]of you as of no other woman for some time. But--yes, you shall have the

[057-161]plain, coarse truth, which is good in its way, no doubt. I was afraid to

[057-162]say that I loved you. You don’t flinch; so far, so good. Now what harm

[057-163]is there in this confession? In the common course of things I shouldn’t

[057-164]be in a position to marry for perhaps three or four years, and even then

[057-165][[literary-marriage|marriage]] would mean difficulties, restraints, obstacles. I have always

[057-166]dreaded the thought of marriage with a poor income. You remember?

[057-167]Love in a hut, with water and a crust,

[057-168]Is--Love forgive us!--cinders, ashes, dust.

[057-169]You know that is true.’

[057-170]‘Not always, I dare say.’

[057-171]‘But for the vast majority of mortals. There’s the instance of the

[057-172]Reardons. They were in love with each other, if ever two people were;

[057-173]but poverty ruined everything. I am not in the confidence of either of

[057-174]them, but I feel sure each has wished the other dead. What else was

[057-175]to be expected? Should I have dared to take a wife in my present

[057-176]circumstances--a wife as poor as myself?’

[057-177]‘You will be in a much better position before long,’ said Marian.

[057-178]‘If you loved me, why should you have been afraid to ask me to have

[057-179]confidence in your future?’

[057-180]‘It’s all so uncertain. It may be another ten years before I can count

[057-181]on an income of five or six hundred pounds--if I have to struggle on in

[057-182]the common way.’

[057-183]‘But tell me, what is your aim in life? What do you understand by

[057-184][[literary-success|success]]?’

[057-185]‘Yes, I will tell you. My aim is to have easy command of all the

[057-186]pleasures desired by a cultivated man. I want to live among beautiful

[057-187]things, and never to be troubled by a thought of vulgar difficulties.

[057-188]I want to travel and enrich my mind in foreign countries. I want to

[057-189]associate on equal terms with refined and interesting people. I want to

[057-190]be known, to be familiarly referred to, to feel when I enter a room that

[057-191]people regard me with some curiosity.’

[057-192]He looked steadily at her with bright eyes.

[057-193]‘And that’s all?’ asked Marian.

[057-194]‘That is very much. Perhaps you don’t know how I suffer in feeling

[057-195]myself at a disadvantage. My instincts are strongly social, yet I can’t

[057-196]be at my ease in society, simply because I can’t do justice to myself.

[057-197]Want of money makes me the inferior of the people I talk with, though

[057-198]I might be superior to them in most things. I am ignorant in many

[057-199]ways, and merely because I am poor. Imagine my never having been out of

[057-200]England! It shames me when people talk familiarly of the Continent. So

[057-201]with regard to all manner of amusements and pursuits at home. Impossible

[057-202]for me to appear among my acquaintances at the theatre, at concerts.

[057-203]I am perpetually at a disadvantage; I haven’t fair play. Suppose me

[057-204]possessed of money enough to live a full and active life for the next

[057-205]five years; why, at the end of that time my position would be secure. To

[057-206]him that hath shall be given--you know how universally true that is.’
