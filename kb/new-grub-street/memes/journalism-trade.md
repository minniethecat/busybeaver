# Journalism as Trade

If the three-volume novel represents the old economy of Victorian literature, journalism represents the new. In *New Grub Street*, periodical writing — reviews, leaders, gossip columns, society notes — is the dominant form of literary production. It is faster, more reliably paid, and fundamentally incompatible with the slow, painstaking craft of the serious novelist.

## The Economics of Speed

Journalism pays by the column-inch, and speed is the primary professional virtue. Jasper Milvain embodies this logic: he writes for multiple periodicals, diversifies his output, and treats each piece as a transaction. His ambition is not to produce lasting work but to "make his thousand a year" (P02-204) through sheer volume and versatility.

The contrast with Reardon is explicit. Reardon writes novels slowly, painfully, revising endlessly. Journalism demands the opposite: quick opinions, disposable prose, constant output. The novel's title itself — *New* Grub Street — suggests that the periodical press has transformed the old world of hack writing into something more efficient and more mercenary.

## The Periodical Ecosystem

The novel is dense with named periodicals: *Chat*, *Chit*, *The Study*, *The Current*, *The West End*. Each represents a different niche in the literary marketplace — from serious quarterly reviews to society gossip sheets. Milvain navigates this ecosystem expertly, moving between publications as opportunities arise. His final triumph is an editorial appointment at a fashionable paper — journalism as career, not calling.

Alfred Yule, by contrast, has spent decades writing for periodicals without ever mastering the commercial game. His bitterness is that of the lifelong journalist who has produced immense quantities of prose without achieving either wealth or literary distinction — "A life of toil! A life of toil!" (055-018).

## Journalism and Literary Form

Gissing suggests that journalism not only competes economically with serious fiction but also degrades literary standards. The demand for constant novelty, provocative opinion, and crowd-pleasing content shapes what gets written and what gets read. The "modern Grub Street" with its "telegraphic communication" (P02-196) is above all a journalistic world — networked, reactive, and indifferent to artistic quality.
