# Section 26

[025-001]‘I couldn’t do it,’ said [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]].

[025-002]‘Certainly you couldn’t. You--well, you are a psychological realist in

[025-003]the [[literary-culture|sphere of culture]]. You are impatient of vulgar circumstances.’

[025-004]‘In a great measure because my life has been martyred by them.’

[025-005]‘And for that very same reason I delight in them,’ cried [[harold-biffen|Biffen]].

[025-006]‘You are repelled by what has injured you; I am attracted by it. This

[025-007]divergence is very interesting; but for that, we should have resembled

[025-008]each other so closely. You know that by temper we are rabid idealists,

[025-009]both of us.’

[025-010]‘I suppose so.’

[025-011]‘But let me go on. I want, among other things, to insist upon the

[025-012]fateful power of trivial incidents. No one has yet dared to do this

[025-013]seriously. It has often been done in farce, and that’s why farcical

[025-014]writing so often makes one melancholy. You know my stock instances

[025-015]of the kind of thing I mean. There was poor Allen, who lost the most

[025-016]valuable opportunity of his life because he hadn’t a clean shirt to put

[025-017]on; and Williamson, who would probably have married that rich girl but

[025-018]for the grain of dust that got into his eye, and made him unable to say

[025-019]or do anything at the critical moment.’

[025-020]Reardon burst into a roar of laughter.

[025-021]‘There you are!’ cried Biffen, with friendly annoyance. ‘You take the

[025-022]conventional view. If you wrote of these things you would represent them

[025-023]as laughable.’

[025-024]‘They are laughable,’ asserted the other, ‘however serious to the

[025-025]persons concerned. The mere fact of grave issues in life depending on

[025-026]such paltry things is monstrously ludicrous. Life is a huge farce, and

[025-027]the advantage of possessing a sense of humour is that it enables one to

[025-028]defy fate with mocking laughter.’

[025-029]‘That’s all very well, but it isn’t an original view. I am not lacking

[025-030]in sense of humour, but I prefer to treat these aspects of life from

[025-031]an impartial standpoint. The man who laughs takes the side of a cruel

[025-032]omnipotence, if one can imagine such a thing.

[025-033]I want to take no side at all; simply to say, Look, this is the kind of

[025-034]thing that happens.’

[025-035]‘I admire your honesty, Biffen,’ said Reardon, sighing. ‘You will

[025-036]never sell work of this kind, yet you have the courage to go on with it

[025-037]because you believe in it.’

[025-038]‘I don’t know; I may perhaps sell it some day.’

[025-039]‘In the meantime,’ said Reardon, laying down his pipe, ‘suppose we eat a

[025-040]morsel of something. I’m rather hungry.’

[025-041]In the early days of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]] Reardon was wont to offer the friends

[025-042]who looked in on Sunday evening a substantial supper; by degrees the

[025-043]meal had grown simpler, until now, in the depth of his [[literary-poverty|poverty]], he made

[025-044]no pretence of hospitable entertainment. It was only because he knew

[025-045]that Biffen as often as not had nothing whatever to eat that he did not

[025-046]hesitate to offer him a slice of bread and butter and a cup of tea. They

[025-047]went into the back room, and over the Spartan fare continued to discuss

[025-048]aspects of fiction.

[025-049]‘I shall never,’ said Biffen, ‘write anything like a dramatic scene.

[025-050]Such things do happen in life, but so very rarely that they are nothing

[025-051]to my purpose. Even when they happen, by-the-bye, it is in a shape that

[025-052]would be useless to the ordinary novelist; he would have to cut away

[025-053]this circumstance, and add that. Why? I should like to know. Such

[025-054]conventionalism results from stage necessities. Fiction hasn’t yet

[025-055]outgrown the influence of the stage on which it originated. Whatever a

[025-056]man writes FOR EFFECT is wrong and bad.’

[025-057]‘Only in your view. There may surely exist such a thing as the ART of

[025-058]fiction.’

[025-059]‘It is worked out. We must have a rest from it. You, now--the best

[025-060]things you have done are altogether in conflict with novelistic

[025-061]conventionalities. It was because that blackguard review of “On Neutral

[025-062]Ground” clumsily hinted this that I first thought of you with interest.

[025-063]No, no; let us copy life. When the man and woman are to meet for a

[025-064]great scene of passion, let it all be frustrated by one or other of

[025-065]them having a bad cold in the head, and so on. Let the pretty girl get

[025-066]a disfiguring pimple on her nose just before the ball at which she is

[025-067]going to shine. Show the numberless repulsive features of common decent

[025-068]life. Seriously, coldly; not a hint of facetiousness, or the thing

[025-069]becomes different.’

[025-070][[About]] eight o’clock Reardon heard his wife’s knock at the door. On

[025-071]opening he saw not only [[amy-reardon|Amy]] and the servant, the latter holding Willie

[025-072]in her arms, but with them [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]].

[025-073]‘I have been at Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s,’ Jasper explained as he came in. ‘Have you

[025-074]anyone here?’

[025-075]‘Biffen.’

[025-076]‘Ah, then we’ll discuss [[literary-realism|realism]].’

[025-077]‘That’s over for the evening. Greek metres also.’

[025-078]‘Thank Heaven!’

[025-079]The three men seated themselves with joking and laughter, and the smoke

[025-080]of their pipes gathered thickly in the little room. It was half an

[025-081]hour before Amy joined them. Tobacco was no disturbance to her, and

[025-082]she enjoyed the kind of talk that was held on these occasions; but

[025-083]it annoyed her that she could no longer play the hostess at a merry

[025-084]supper-table.

[025-085]‘Why ever are you sitting in your overcoat, Mr Biffen?’ were her first

[025-086]words when she entered.

[025-087]‘Please excuse me, Mrs Reardon. It happens to be more convenient this

[025-088]evening.’

[025-089]She was puzzled, but a glance from her husband warned her not to pursue

[025-090]the subject.

[025-091]Biffen always behaved to Amy with a sincerity of respect which had made

[025-092]him a favourite with her. To him, poor fellow, Reardon seemed supremely

[025-093]blessed. That a struggling man of letters should have been able to

[025-094]marry, and such a wife, was miraculous in Biffen’s eyes. A woman’s love

[025-095]was to him the unattainable ideal; already thirty-five years old, he had

[025-096]no prospect of ever being rich enough to assure himself a daily dinner;

[025-097]marriage was wildly out of the question. Sitting here, he found it very

[025-098]difficult not to gaze at Amy with uncivil persistency. Seldom in his

[025-099]life had he conversed with educated women, and the sound of this clear

[025-100]voice was always more delightful to him than any music.

[025-101]Amy took a place near to him, and talked in her most charming way of

[025-102]such things as she knew interested him. Biffen’s deferential attitude

[025-103]as he listened and replied was in strong contrast with the careless

[025-104]ease which marked Jasper Milvain. The realist would never smoke in Amy’s

[025-105]presence, but Jasper puffed jovial clouds even whilst she was conversing

[025-106]with him.

[025-107]‘[[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] came to see me last night,’ remarked Milvain, presently.

[025-108]‘His novel is refused on all hands. He talks of earning a living as a

[025-109]commission agent for some sewing-machine people.’

[025-110]‘I can’t understand how his book should be positively refused,’ said

[025-111]Reardon. ‘The last wasn’t altogether a failure.’

[025-112]‘Very nearly. And this one consists of nothing but a series of

[025-113]conversations between two people. It is really a dialogue, not a novel

[025-114]at all. He read me some twenty pages, and I no longer wondered that he

[025-115]couldn’t sell it.’

[025-116]‘Oh, but it has considerable merit,’ put in Biffen. ‘The talk is

[025-117]remarkably true.’

[025-118]‘But what’s the good of talk that leads to nothing?’ protested Jasper.

[025-119]‘It’s a bit of real life.’

[025-120]‘Yes, but it has no market value. You may write what you like, so long

[025-121]as people are willing to read you. Whelpdale’s a clever fellow, but he

[025-122]can’t hit a practical line.’

[025-123]‘Like some other people I have heard of;’ said Reardon, laughing.

[025-124]‘But the odd thing is, that he always strikes one as practical-minded.

[025-125]Don’t you feel that, Mrs Reardon?’

[025-126]He and Amy talked for a few minutes, and Reardon, seemingly lost in

[025-127]meditation, now and then observed them from the corner of his eye.

[025-128]At eleven o’clock husband and wife were alone again.

[025-129]‘You don’t mean to say,’ exclaimed Amy, ‘that Biffen has sold his coat?’

[025-130]‘Or pawned it.’

[025-131]‘But why not the overcoat?’

[025-132]‘Partly, I should think, because it’s the warmer of the two; partly,

[025-133]perhaps, because the other would fetch more.’

[025-134]‘That poor man will die of starvation, some day, Edwin.’

[025-135]‘I think it not impossible.’

[025-136]‘I hope you gave him something to eat?’

[025-137]‘Oh yes. But I could see he didn’t like to take as much as he wanted.

[025-138]I don’t think of him with so much pity as I used to; that’s a result of

[025-139]suffering oneself.’

[025-140]Amy set her lips and sighed.

[025-141]CHAPTER XI. RESPITE

[025-142]The last volume was written in fourteen days. In this achievement

[025-143]Reardon rose almost to heroic pitch, for he had much to contend with

[025-144]beyond the mere labour of composition. Scarcely had he begun when a

[025-145]sharp attack of lumbago fell upon him; for two or three days it was

[025-146]torture to support himself at the desk, and he moved about like a

[025-147]cripple. Upon this ensued headaches, sore-throat, general enfeeblement.

[025-148]And before the end of the fortnight it was necessary to think of raising

[025-149]another small sum of [[money-in-literature|money]]; he took his watch to the pawnbroker’s (you

[025-150]can imagine that it would not stand as security for much), and sold a

[025-151]few more books. All this notwithstanding, here was the novel at length

[025-152]finished. When he had written ‘The End’ he lay back, closed his eyes,

[025-153]and let time pass in blankness for a quarter of an hour.

[025-154]It remained to determine the title. But his brain refused another

[025-155]effort; after a few minutes’ feeble search he simply took the name of

[025-156]the chief female character, [[margaret-home|Margaret Home]]. That must do for the book.

[025-157]Already, with the penning of the last word, all its scenes, personages,

[025-158]dialogues had slipped away into oblivion; he knew and cared nothing more

[025-159]about them.

[025-160]‘Amy, you will have to correct the proofs for me. Never as long as I

[025-161]live will I look upon a page of this accursed novel. It has all but

[025-162]killed me.’

[025-163]‘The point is,’ replied Amy, ‘that here we have it complete. Pack it up

[025-164]and take it to the [[publishing-trade|publishers]]’ to-morrow morning.’

[025-165]‘I will.’

[025-166]‘And--you will ask them to advance you a few pounds?’

[025-167]‘I must.’

[025-168]But that undertaking was almost as hard to face as a rewriting of

[025-169]the last volume would have been. Reardon had such superfluity of

[025-170]sensitiveness that, for his own part, he would far rather have gone

[025-171]hungry than ask for money not legally his due. To-day there was no

[025-172]choice. In the ordinary course of business it would be certainly a month

[025-173]before he heard the publishers’ terms, and perhaps the Christmas season

[025-174]might cause yet more delay. Without borrowing, he could not provide for

[025-175]the expenses of more than another week or two.

[025-176]His parcel under his arm, he entered the ground-floor office, and

[025-177]desired to see that member of the firm with whom he had previously had

[025-178]personal relations. This gentleman was not in town; he would be away for

[025-179]a few days. Reardon left the manuscript, and came out into the street

[025-180]again.

[025-181]He crossed, and looked up at the publishers’ windows from the opposite

[025-182]pavement. ‘Do they suspect in what wretched circumstances I am? Would

[025-183]it surprise them to know all that depends upon that budget of paltry

[025-184]scribbling? I suppose not; it must be a daily experience with them.

[025-185]Well, I must write a begging letter.’

[025-186]It was raining and windy. He went slowly homewards, and was on the point

[025-187]of entering the public door of the flats when his uneasiness became so

[025-188]great that he turned and walked past. If he went in, he must at

[025-189]once write his appeal for money, and he felt that he could not. The

[025-190]degradation seemed too great.

[025-191]Was there no way of getting over the next few weeks? Rent, of course,

[025-192]would be due at Christmas, but that payment might be postponed; it was

[025-193]only a question of buying food and fuel. Amy had offered to ask her

[025-194]mother for a few pounds; it would be cowardly to put this task upon her

[025-195]now that he had promised to meet the difficulty himself. What man in

[025-196]all [[london|London]] could and would lend him money? He reviewed the list of his

[025-197]acquaintances, but there was only one to whom he could appeal with the

[025-198]slightest hope--that was [[carter|Carter]].
