# Section 34

[033-001][[amy-reardon|Amy]] was oftener from home than had been her custom.

[033-002]Occasionally she went away soon after breakfast, and spent the whole day

[033-003]at her mother’s house. ‘It saves food,’ she said with a bitter laugh,

[033-004]when [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] once expressed surprise that she should be going again so

[033-005]soon.

[033-006]‘And gives you an opportunity of bewailing your hard fate,’ he returned

[033-007]coldly.

[033-008]The reproach was ignoble, and he could not be surprised that Amy left

[033-009]the house without another word to him. Yet he resented that, as he

[033-010]had resented her sorrowful jest. The feeling of unmanliness in his own

[033-011]position tortured him into a mood of perversity. Through the day he

[033-012]wrote only a few lines, and on Amy’s return he resolved not to speak

[033-013]to her. There was a sense of repose in this change of attitude; he

[033-014]encouraged himself in the view that Amy was treating him with cruel

[033-015]neglect. She, surprised that her friendly questions elicited no answer,

[033-016]looked into his face and saw a sullen anger of which hitherto Reardon

[033-017]had never seemed capable. Her indignation took fire, and she left him to

[033-018]himself.

[033-019]For a day or two he persevered in his muteness, uttering a word only

[033-020]when it could not be avoided. Amy was at first so resentful that she

[033-021]contemplated leaving him to his ill-temper and dwelling at her mother’s

[033-022]house until he chose to recall her. But his face grew so haggard in

[033-023]fixed misery that compassion at length prevailed over her injured

[033-024]pride. Late in the evening she went to the study, and found him sitting

[033-025]unoccupied.

[033-026]‘Edwin--’

[033-027]‘What do you want?’ he asked indifferently.

[033-028]‘Why are you behaving to me like this?’

[033-029]‘Surely it makes no difference to you how I behave? You can easily

[033-030]forget that I exist, and live your own life.’

[033-031]‘What have I done to make this change in you?’

[033-032]‘Is it a change?’

[033-033]‘You know it is.’

[033-034]‘How did I behave before?’ he asked, glancing at her.

[033-035]‘Like yourself--kindly and gently.’

[033-036]‘If I always did so, in spite of things that might have embittered

[033-037]another man’s temper, I think it deserved some return of kindness from

[033-038]you.’

[033-039]‘What “things” do you mean?’

[033-040]‘Circumstances for which neither of us is to blame.’

[033-041]‘I am not conscious of having failed in kindness,’ said Amy, distantly.

[033-042]‘Then that only shows that you have forgotten your old self, and utterly

[033-043]changed in your feeling to me. When we first came to live here could you

[033-044]have imagined yourself leaving me alone for long, miserable days, just

[033-045]because I was suffering under misfortunes? You have shown too plainly

[033-046]that you don’t care to give me the help even of a kind word. You get

[033-047]away from me as often as you can, as if to remind me that we have no

[033-048]longer any interests in common. Other people are your confidants; you

[033-049]speak of me to them as if I were purposely dragging you down into a mean

[033-050]condition.’

[033-051]‘How can you know what I say about you?’

[033-052]‘Isn’t it true?’ he asked, flashing an angry glance at her.

[033-053]‘It is not true. Of course I have talked to mother about our

[033-054]difficulties; how could I help it?’

[033-055]‘And to other people.’

[033-056]‘Not in a way that you could find fault with.’

[033-057]‘In a way that makes me seem contemptible to them. You show them that

[033-058]I have made you poor and unhappy, and you are glad to have their

[033-059]sympathy.’

[033-060]‘What you mean is, that I oughtn’t to see anyone. There’s no other way

[033-061]of avoiding such a reproach as this. So long as I don’t laugh and sing

[033-062]before people, and assure them that things couldn’t be more hopeful, I

[033-063]shall be asking for their sympathy, and against you. I can’t understand

[033-064]your unreasonableness.’

[033-065]‘I’m afraid there is very little in me that you can understand. So long

[033-066]as my prospects seemed bright, you could sympathise readily enough; as

[033-067]soon as ever they darkened, something came between us. Amy, you haven’t

[033-068]done your duty. Your love hasn’t stood the test as it should have done.

[033-069]You have given me no help; besides the burden of cheerless work I have

[033-070]had to bear that of your growing coldness. I can’t remember one instance

[033-071]when you have spoken to me as a wife might--a wife who was something

[033-072]more than a man’s housekeeper.’

[033-073]The passion in his voice and the harshness of the accusation made her

[033-074]unable to reply.

[033-075]‘You said rightly,’ he went on, ‘that I have always been kind and

[033-076]gentle. I never thought I could speak to you or feel to you in any other

[033-077]way. But I have undergone too much, and you have deserted me. Surely it

[033-078]was too soon to do that. So long as I endeavoured my utmost, and loved

[033-079]you the same as ever, you might have remembered all you once said to me.

[033-080]You might have given me help, but you haven’t cared to.’

[033-081]The impulses which had part in this outbreak were numerous and complex.

[033-082]He felt all that he expressed, but at the same time it seemed to him

[033-083]that he had the choice between two ways of uttering his emotion--the

[033-084]tenderly appealing and the sternly reproachful: he took the latter

[033-085]course because it was less natural to him than the former. His desire

[033-086]was to impress Amy with the bitter intensity of his sufferings; pathos

[033-087]and loving words seemed to have lost their power upon her, but perhaps

[033-088]if he yielded to that other form of passion she would be shaken out of

[033-089]her coldness. The stress of injured love is always tempted to speech

[033-090]which seems its contradiction. Reardon had the strangest mixture of pain

[033-091]and pleasure in flinging out these first words of wrath that he had ever

[033-092]addressed to Amy; they consoled him under the humiliating sense of his

[033-093]weakness, and yet he watched with dread his wife’s countenance as she

[033-094]listened to him. He hoped to cause her pain equal to his own, for then

[033-095]it would be in his power at once to throw off this disguise and soothe

[033-096]her with every softest word his heart could suggest. That she had really

[033-097]ceased to love him he could not, durst not, believe; but his nature

[033-098]demanded frequent assurance of affection. Amy had abandoned too soon the

[033-099]caresses of their ardent time; she was absorbed in her maternity, and

[033-100]thought it enough to be her husband’s friend. Ashamed to make appeal

[033-101]directly for the tenderness she no longer offered, he accused her of

[033-102]utter indifference, of abandoning him and all but betraying him, that in

[033-103]self-defence she might show what really was in her heart.

[033-104]But Amy made no movement towards him.

[033-105]‘How can you say that I have deserted you?’ she returned, with cold

[033-106]indignation. ‘When did I refuse to share your [[literary-poverty|poverty]]? When did I

[033-107]grumble at what we have had to go through?’

[033-108]‘Ever since the troubles really began you have let me know what your

[033-109]thoughts were, even if you didn’t speak them. You have never shared my

[033-110]lot willingly. I can’t recall one word of encouragement from you, but

[033-111]many, many which made the struggle harder for me.’

[033-112]‘Then it would be better for you if I went away altogether, and left you

[033-113]free to do the best for yourself. If that is what you mean by all this,

[033-114]why not say it plainly? I won’t be a burden to you. Someone will give me

[033-115]a home.’

[033-116]‘And you would leave me without regret? Your only care would be that you

[033-117]were still bound to me?’

[033-118]‘You must think of me what you like. I don’t care to defend myself.’

[033-119]‘You won’t admit, then, that I have anything to complain of? I seem to

[033-120]you simply in a bad temper without a cause?’

[033-121]‘To tell you the truth, that’s just what I do think. I came here to ask

[033-122]what I had done that you were angry with me, and you break out furiously

[033-123]with all sorts of vague reproaches. You have much to endure, I know

[033-124]that, but it’s no reason why you should turn against me. I have never

[033-125]neglected my duty. Is the duty all on my side? I believe there are very

[033-126]few wives who would be as patient as I have been.’

[033-127]Reardon gazed at her for a moment, then turned away. The distance

[033-128]between them was greater than he had thought, and now he repented of

[033-129]having given way to an impulse so alien to his true feelings; anger only

[033-130]estranged her, whereas by speech of a different kind he might have won

[033-131]the caress for which he hungered.

[033-132]Amy, seeing that he would say nothing more, left him to himself.

[033-133]It grew late in the night. The fire had gone out, but Reardon still sat

[033-134]in the cold room. Thoughts of self-destruction were again haunting him,

[033-135]as they had done during the black months of last year. If he had lost

[033-136]Amy’s love, and all through the mental impotence which would make it

[033-137]hard for him even to earn bread, why should he still live? Affection for

[033-138]his child had no weight with him; it was Amy’s child rather than his,

[033-139]and he had more fear than pleasure in the prospect of Willie’s growing

[033-140]to manhood.

[033-141]He had just heard the workhouse clock strike two, when, without the

[033-142]warning of a footstep, the door opened. Amy came in; she wore her

[033-143]dressing-gown, and her hair was arranged for the night.

[033-144]‘Why do you stay here?’ she asked.

[033-145]It was not the same voice as before. He saw that her eyes were red and

[033-146]swollen.

[033-147]‘Have you been crying, Amy?’

[033-148]‘Never mind. Do you know what time it is?’

[033-149]He went towards her.

[033-150]‘Why have you been crying?’

[033-151]‘There are many things to cry for.’

[033-152]‘Amy, have you any love for me still, or has poverty robbed me of it

[033-153]all?’

[033-154]‘I have never said that I didn’t love you. Why do you accuse me of such

[033-155]things?’

[033-156]He took her in his arms and held her passionately and kissed her face

[033-157]again and again. Amy’s tears broke forth anew.

[033-158]‘Why should we come to such utter ruin?’ she sobbed. ‘Oh, try, try if

[033-159]you can’t save us even yet! You know without my saying it that I do love

[033-160]you; it’s dreadful to me to think all our happy life should be at an

[033-161]end, when we thought of such a future together. Is it impossible? Can’t

[033-162]you work as you used to and succeed as we felt confident you would?

[033-163]Don’t despair yet, Edwin; do, do try, whilst there is still time!’

[033-164]‘Darling, darling--if only I COULD!’

[033-165]‘I have thought of something, dearest. Do as you proposed last year;

[033-166]find a tenant for the flat whilst we still have a little [[money-in-literature|money]], and

[033-167]then go away into some quiet country place, where you can get back your

[033-168]health and live for very little, and write another book--a good book,

[033-169]that’ll bring you [[literary-success|reputation]] again. I and Willie can go and live at

[033-170]mother’s for the summer months. Do this! It would cost you so little,

[033-171]living alone, wouldn’t it? You would know that I was well cared for;

[033-172]mother would be willing to have me for a few months, and it’s easy to

[033-173]explain that your health has failed, that you’re obliged to go away for

[033-174]a time.’

[033-175]‘But why shouldn’t you go with me, if we are to let this place?’

[033-176]‘We shouldn’t have enough money. I want to free your mind from the

[033-177]burden whilst you are writing. And what is before us if we go on in this

[033-178]way? You don’t think you will get much for what you’re writing now, do

[033-179]you?’

[033-180]Reardon shook his head.

[033-181]‘Then how can we live even till the end of the year? Something must be

[033-182]done, you know. If we get into poor lodgings, what hope is there that

[033-183]you’ll be able to write anything good?’
