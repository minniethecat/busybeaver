# Section 21

[020-001]‘How do their friends in the country think of it?’

[020-002]‘Very dubiously; but then what else was to be expected? Of course, the

[020-003]respectable and intelligible path marked out for both of them points

[020-004]to a lifetime of governessing. But the girls have no relish for that;

[020-005]they’d rather do almost anything. We talked over all the aspects of the

[020-006]situation seriously enough--it is desperately serious, no doubt of that.

[020-007]I told them fairly all the hardships they would have to face--described

[020-008]the typical [[london|London]] lodgings, and so on. Still, there’s an adventurous

[020-009]vein in them, and they decided for the risk. If it came to the worst I

[020-010]suppose they could still find governess work.’

[020-011]‘Let us hope better things.’

[020-012]‘Yes. But now, I should have felt far more reluctant to let them come

[020-013]here in this way hadn’t it been that they regard you as a friend.

[020-014]To-morrow morning you will probably hear from one or both of them.

[020-015]Perhaps it would have been better if I had left them to tell you all

[020-016]this, but I felt I should like to see you and--put it in my own way. I

[020-017]think you’ll understand this feeling, [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]]. I wanted, in fact, to

[020-018]hear from yourself that you would be a friend to the poor girls.’

[020-019]‘Oh, you already know that! I shall be so very glad to see them often.’

[020-020]Marian’s voice lent itself very naturally and sweetly to the expression

[020-021]of warm feeling. Emphasis was not her habit; it only needed that she

[020-022]should put off her ordinary reserve, utter quietly the emotional thought

[020-023]which so seldom might declare itself, and her tones had an exquisite

[020-024]womanliness.

[020-025][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] looked full into her face.

[020-026]‘In that case they won’t miss the comfort of home so much. Of course

[020-027]they will have to go into very modest lodgings indeed. I have already

[020-028]been looking about. I should like to find rooms for them somewhere near

[020-029]my own place; it’s a decent neighbourhood, and the park is at hand,

[020-030]and then they wouldn’t be very far from you. They thought it might be

[020-031]possible to make a joint establishment with me, but I’m afraid that’s

[020-032]out of the question.

[020-033]The lodgings we should want in that case, everything considered, would

[020-034]cost more than the sum of our expenses if we live apart. Besides,

[020-035]there’s no harm in saying that I don’t think we should get along very

[020-036]well together. We’re all of us rather quarrelsome, to tell the truth,

[020-037]and we try each other’s tempers.’

[020-038]Marian smiled and looked puzzled.

[020-039]‘Shouldn’t you have thought that?’

[020-040]‘I have seen no signs of quarrelsomeness.’

[020-041]‘I’m not sure that the worst fault is on my side. Why should one condemn

[020-042]oneself against conscience? Maud is perhaps the hardest to get along

[020-043]with. She has a sort of arrogance, an exaggeration of something I am

[020-044]quite aware of in myself. You have noticed that trait in me?’

[020-045]‘Arrogance--I think not. You have self-confidence.’

[020-046]‘Which goes into extremes now and then. But, putting myself aside, I

[020-047]feel pretty sure that the girls won’t seem quarrelsome to you; they

[020-048]would have to be very fractious indeed before that were possible.’

[020-049]‘We shall continue to be friends, I am sure.’

[020-050]Jasper let his eyes wander about the room.

[020-051]‘This is your father’s study?’

[020-052]‘Yes.’

[020-053]‘Perhaps it would have seemed odd to [[alfred-yule|Mr Yule]] if I had come in and begun

[020-054]to talk to him about these purely private affairs. He knows me so very

[020-055]slightly. But, in calling here for the first time--’

[020-056]An unusual embarrassment checked him.

[020-057]‘I will explain to father your very natural wish to speak of these

[020-058]things,’ said Marian, with tact.

[020-059]She thought uneasily of her mother in the next room. To her there

[020-060]appeared no reason whatever why Jasper should not be introduced to Mrs

[020-061]Yule, yet she could not venture to propose it. Remembering her father’s

[020-062]last remarks about Milvain in connection with [[fadge|Fadge]]’s magazine, she must

[020-063]wait for distinct permission before offering the young man encouragement

[020-064]to repeat his visit. Perhaps there was complicated trouble in store

[020-065]for her; impossible to say how her father’s deep-rooted and rankling

[020-066]antipathies might affect her intercourse even with the two girls. But

[020-067]she was of independent years; she must be allowed the choice of her

[020-068]own friends. The pleasure she had in seeing Jasper under this roof, in

[020-069]hearing him talk with such intimate friendliness, strengthened her to

[020-070]resist timid thoughts.

[020-071]‘When will your sisters arrive?’ she asked.

[020-072]‘I think in a very few days. When I have fixed upon lodgings for them I

[020-073]must go back to Finden; then they will return with me as soon as we

[020-074]can get the house emptied. It’s rather miserable selling things one has

[020-075]lived among from childhood. A friend in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] will house for us

[020-076]what we really can’t bear to part with.’

[020-077]‘It must be very sad,’ Marian murmured.

[020-078]‘You know,’ said the other suddenly, ‘that it’s my fault the girls are

[020-079]left in such a hard position?’

[020-080]Marian looked at him with startled eyes. His tone was quite unfamiliar

[020-081]to her.

[020-082]‘Mother had an annuity,’ he continued. ‘It ended with her life, but if

[020-083]it hadn’t been for me she could have saved a good deal out of it. Until

[020-084]the last year or two I have earned nothing, and I have spent more

[020-085]than was strictly necessary. Well, I didn’t live like that in mere

[020-086]recklessness; I knew I was preparing myself for remunerative work. But

[020-087]it seems too bad now. I’m sorry for it. I wish I had found some way of

[020-088]supporting myself. The end of mother’s life was made far more unhappy

[020-089]than it need have been. I should like you to understand all this.’

[020-090]The listener kept her eyes on the ground.

[020-091]‘Perhaps the girls have hinted it to you?’ Jasper added.

[020-092]‘No.’

[020-093]‘Selfishness--that’s one of my faults. It isn’t a brutal kind of

[020-094]selfishness; the thought of it often enough troubles me. If I were rich,

[020-095]I should be a generous and good man; I know I should. So would many

[020-096]another poor fellow whose worst features come out under hardship. This

[020-097]isn’t a heroic type; of course not. I am a civilised man, that’s all.’

[020-098]Marian could say nothing.

[020-099]‘You wonder why I am so impertinent as to talk about myself like this.

[020-100]I have gone through a good deal of mental pain these last few weeks,

[020-101]and somehow I can’t help showing you something of my real thoughts. Just

[020-102]because you are one of the few people I regard with sincere respect.

[020-103]I don’t know you very well, but quite well enough to respect you. My

[020-104]sisters think of you in the same way. I shall do many a base thing in

[020-105]life, just to get [[money-in-literature|money]] and [[literary-success|reputation]]; I tell you this that you mayn’t

[020-106]be surprised if anything of that kind comes to your ears. I can’t afford

[020-107]to live as I should like to.’

[020-108]She looked up at him with a smile.

[020-109]‘People who are going to live unworthily don’t declare it in this way.’

[020-110]‘I oughtn’t to; a few minutes ago I had no intention of saying such

[020-111]things. It means I am rather overstrung, I suppose; but it’s all true,

[020-112]unfortunately.’

[020-113]He rose, and began to run his eye along the shelves nearest to him.

[020-114]‘Well, now I will go, Miss Yule.’

[020-115]Marian stood up as he approached.

[020-116]‘It’s all very well,’ he said, smiling, ‘for me to encourage my sisters

[020-117]in the hope that they may earn a living; but suppose I can’t even do it

[020-118]myself? It’s by no means certain that I shall make ends meet this year.’

[020-119]‘You have every reason to hope, I think.’

[020-120]‘I like to hear people say that, but it’ll mean savage work. When we

[020-121]were all at Finden last year, I told the girls that it would be another

[020-122]twelve months before I could support myself. Now I am forced to do

[020-123]it. And I don’t like work; my nature is lazy. I shall never write for

[020-124]writing’s sake, only to make money. All my plans and efforts will have

[020-125]money in view--all. I shan’t allow anything to come in the way of my

[020-126]material advancement.’

[020-127]‘I wish you every [[literary-ambition|success]],’ said Marian, without looking at him, and

[020-128]without a smile.

[020-129]‘Thank you. But that sounds too much like good-bye. I trust we are to be

[020-130]friends, for all that?’

[020-131]‘Indeed, I hope we may be.’

[020-132]They shook hands, and he went towards the door. But before opening it,

[020-133]he asked:

[020-134]‘Did you read that thing of mine in [[the-current|The Current]]?’

[020-135]‘Yes, I did.’

[020-136]‘It wasn’t bad, I think?’

[020-137]‘It seemed to me very clever.’

[020-138]‘Clever--yes, that’s the word. It had a success, too. I have as good a

[020-139]thing half done for the April number, but I’ve felt too heavy-hearted to

[020-140]go on with it. The girls shall let you know when they are in town.’

[020-141]Marian followed him into the passage, and watched him as he opened the

[020-142]front door. When it had closed, she went back into the study for a few

[020-143]minutes before rejoining her mother.

[020-144]CHAPTER IX. INVITA MINERVA

[020-145]After all, there came a day when [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]] found himself regularly

[020-146]at work once more, ticking off his stipulated quantum of manuscript each

[020-147]four-and-twenty hours. He wrote a very small hand; sixty written slips

[020-148]of the kind of paper he habitually used would represent--thanks to the

[020-149]astonishing system which prevails in such matters: large type, wide

[020-150]spacing, frequency of blank pages--a passable three-hundred-page volume.

[020-151]On an average he could write four such slips a day; so here we have

[020-152]fifteen days for the volume, and forty-five for the completed book.

[020-153]Forty-five days; an eternity in the looking forward. Yet the calculation

[020-154]gave him a faint-hearted encouragement. At that rate he might have

[020-155]his book sold by Christmas. It would certainly not bring him a hundred

[020-156]pounds; seventy-five perhaps. But even that small sum would enable him

[020-157]to pay the quarter’s rent, and then give him a short time, if only two

[020-158]or three weeks, of mental rest. If such rest could not be obtained all

[020-159]was at an end with him. He must either find some new means of supporting

[020-160]himself and his family, or--have done with life and its responsibilities

[020-161]altogether.

[020-162]The latter alternative was often enough before him. He seldom slept for

[020-163]more than two or three consecutive hours in the night, and the time

[020-164]of wakefulness was often terrible. The various sounds which marked the

[020-165]stages from midnight to dawn had grown miserably familiar to him; worst

[020-166]torture to his mind was the chiming and striking of clocks. Two of these

[020-167]were in general audible, that of [[marylebone|Marylebone]] parish church, and that of

[020-168]the adjoining workhouse; the latter always sounded several minutes after

[020-169]its ecclesiastical neighbour, and with a difference of note which seemed

[020-170]to Reardon very appropriate--a thin, querulous voice, reminding one of

[020-171]the community it represented. After lying awake for awhile he would hear

[020-172]quarters sounding; if they ceased before the fourth he was glad, for

[020-173]he feared to know what time it was. If the hour was complete, he waited

[020-174]anxiously for its number. Two, three, even four, were grateful; there

[020-175]was still a long time before he need rise and face the dreaded task, the

[020-176]horrible four blank slips of paper that had to be filled ere he might

[020-177]sleep again. But such restfulness was only for a moment; no sooner had

[020-178]the workhouse bell become silent than he began to toil in his weary

[020-179]imagination, or else, incapable of that, to vision fearful hazards of

[020-180]the future. The soft breathing of [[amy-reardon|Amy]] at his side, the contact of her

[020-181]warm limbs, often filled him with intolerable dread. Even now he did not

[020-182]believe that Amy loved him with the old love, and the suspicion was like

[020-183]a cold weight at his heart that to retain even her wifely sympathy, her

[020-184]wedded tenderness, he must achieve the impossible.
