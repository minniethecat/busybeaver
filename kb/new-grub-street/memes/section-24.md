# Section 24

[023-001]She knew so well how ‘people’ were talking of him and her. Even her

[023-002]unliterary acquaintances understood that [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s last novel had been

[023-003]anything but successful, and they must of course ask each other how

[023-004]the Reardons were going to live if the business of novel-writing proved

[023-005]unremunerative. Her pride took offence at the mere thought of such

[023-006]conversations. Presently she would become an object of pity; there would

[023-007]be talk of ‘poor [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]].’ It was intolerable.

[023-008]So during the last half year she had withheld as much as possible from

[023-009]the intercourse which might have been one of her chief pleasures. And to

[023-010]disguise the true cause she made pretences which were a satire upon her

[023-011]state of mind--alleging that she had devoted herself to a serious course

[023-012]of studies, that the care of house and child occupied all the time she

[023-013]could spare from her intellectual pursuits. The worst of it was, she

[023-014]had little faith in the efficacy of these fictions; in uttering them she

[023-015]felt an unpleasant warmth upon her cheeks, and it was not difficult to

[023-016]detect a look of doubt in the eyes of the listener. She grew angry

[023-017]with herself for being dishonest, and with her husband for making such

[023-018]dishonesty needful.

[023-019]The female friend with whom she had most trouble was Mrs [[carter|Carter]]. You

[023-020]remember that on the occasion of Reardon’s first meeting with his future

[023-021]wife, at the Grosvenor Gallery, there were present his friend Carter

[023-022]and a young lady who was shortly to bear the name of that spirited

[023-023]young man. The Carters had now been married about a year; they lived

[023-024]in [[bayswater|Bayswater]], and saw much of a certain world which imitates on a lower

[023-025]plane the amusements and affectations of society proper. Mr Carter was

[023-026]still secretary to the hospital where Reardon had once earned his twenty

[023-027]shillings a week, but by voyaging in the seas of charitable enterprise

[023-028]he had come upon supplementary sources of income; for instance, he held

[023-029]the post of secretary to the Barclay Trust, a charity whose moderate

[023-030]funds were largely devoted to the support of gentlemen engaged in

[023-031]administering it. This young man, with his air of pleasing vivacity, had

[023-032]early ingratiated himself with the kind of people who were likely to be

[023-033]of use to him; he had his reward in the shape of offices which are only

[023-034]procured through private influence. His wife was a good-natured, lively,

[023-035]and rather clever girl; she had a genuine regard for Amy, and much

[023-036]respect for Reardon. Her [[literary-ambition|ambition]] was to form a circle of distinctly

[023-037]intellectual acquaintances, and she was constantly inviting the Reardons

[023-038]to her house; a real live novelist is not easily drawn into the world

[023-039]where Mrs Carter had her being, and it annoyed her that all attempts to

[023-040]secure Amy and her husband for five-o’clock teas and small parties had

[023-041]of late failed.

[023-042]On the afternoon when Reardon had visited a second-hand bookseller with

[023-043]a view of raising [[money-in-literature|money]]--he was again shut up in his study, dolorously

[023-044]at work--Amy was disturbed by the sound of a visitor’s rat-tat; the

[023-045]little servant went to the door, and returned followed by Mrs Carter.

[023-046]Under the best of circumstances it was awkward to receive any but

[023-047]intimate friends during the hours when Reardon sat at his desk. The

[023-048]little dining-room (with its screen to conceal the kitchen range)

[023-049]offered nothing more than homely comfort; and then the servant had to

[023-050]be disposed of by sending her into the bedroom to take care of Willie.

[023-051]Privacy, in the strict sense, was impossible, for the servant

[023-052]might listen at the door (one room led out of the other) to all the

[023-053]conversation that went on; yet Amy could not request her visitors to

[023-054]speak in a low tone. For the first year these difficulties had not

[023-055]been felt; Reardon made a point of leaving the front room at his wife’s

[023-056]disposal from three to six; it was only when dread of the future began

[023-057]to press upon him that he sat in the study all day long. You see how

[023-058]complicated were the miseries of the situation; one torment involved

[023-059]another, and in every quarter subjects of discontent were multiplied.

[023-060]Mrs Carter would have taken it ill had she known that Amy did not

[023-061]regard her as strictly an intimate. They addressed each other by their

[023-062]Christian names, and conversed without ceremony; but Amy was always

[023-063]dissatisfied when the well-dressed young woman burst with laughter and

[023-064]animated talk into this abode of concealed [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. Edith was not the

[023-065]kind of person with whom one can quarrel; she had a kind heart, and was

[023-066]never disagreeably pretentious. Had circumstances allowed it, Amy would

[023-067]have given frank welcome to such friendship; she would have been glad

[023-068]to accept as many invitations as Edith chose to offer. But at present

[023-069]it did her harm to come in contact with Mrs Carter; it made her envious,

[023-070]cold to her husband, resentful against fate.

[023-071]‘Why can’t she leave me alone?’ was the thought that rose in her mind as

[023-072]Edith entered. ‘I shall let her see that I don’t want her here.’

[023-073]‘Your husband at work?’ Edith asked, with a glance in the direction of

[023-074]the study, as soon as they had exchanged kisses and greetings.

[023-075]‘Yes, he is busy.’

[023-076]‘And you are sitting alone, as usual. I feared you might be out; an

[023-077]afternoon of sunshine isn’t to be neglected at this time of year.’

[023-078]‘Is there sunshine?’ Amy inquired coldly.

[023-079]‘Why, look! Do you mean to say you haven’t noticed it? What a comical

[023-080]person you are sometimes! I suppose you have been over head and ears in

[023-081]books all day. How is Willie?’

[023-082]‘Very well, thank you.’

[023-083]‘Mayn’t I see him?’

[023-084]‘If you like.’

[023-085]Amy stepped to the bedroom door and bade the servant bring Willie for

[023-086]exhibition. Edith, who as yet had no child of her own, always showed the

[023-087]most flattering admiration of this infant; it was so manifestly sincere

[023-088]that the mother could not but be moved to a grateful friendliness

[023-089]whenever she listened to its expression. Even this afternoon the usual

[023-090]effect followed when Edith had made a pretty and tender fool of herself

[023-091]for several minutes. Amy bade the servant make tea.

[023-092]At this moment the door from the passage opened, and Reardon looked in.

[023-093]‘Well, if this isn’t marvellous!’ cried Edith. ‘I should as soon have

[023-094]expected the heavens to fall!’

[023-095]‘As what?’ asked Reardon, with a pale smile.

[023-096]‘As you to show yourself when I am here.’

[023-097]‘I should like to say that I came on purpose to see you, Mrs Carter,

[023-098]but it wouldn’t be true. I’m going out for an hour, so that you can take

[023-099]possession of the other room if you like, Amy.’

[023-100]‘Going out?’ said Amy, with a look of surprise.

[023-101]‘Nothing--nothing. I mustn’t stay.’

[023-102]He just inquired of Mrs Carter how her husband was, and withdrew. The

[023-103]door of the flat was heard to close after him.

[023-104]‘Let us go into the study, then,’ said Amy, again in rather a cold

[023-105]voice.

[023-106]On Reardon’s desk were lying slips of blank paper. Edith, approaching on

[023-107]tiptoe with what was partly make believe, partly genuine, awe, looked at

[023-108]the literary apparatus, then turned with a laugh to her friend.

[023-109]‘How delightful it must be to sit down and write about people one has

[023-110]invented! Ever since I have known you and Mr Reardon I have been tempted

[023-111]to try if I couldn’t write a story.’

[023-112]‘Have you?’

[023-113]‘And I’m sure I don’t know how you can resist the temptation. I feel

[023-114]sure you could write books almost as clever as your husband’s.’

[023-115]‘I have no intention of trying.’

[023-116]‘You don’t seem very well to-day, Amy.’

[023-117]‘Oh, I think I am as well as usual.’

[023-118]She guessed that her husband was once more brought to a standstill, and

[023-119]this darkened her humour again.

[023-120]‘One of my reasons for coming,’ said Edith, ‘was to beg and entreat and

[023-121]implore you and Mr Reardon to dine with us next Wednesday. Now, don’t

[023-122]put on such a severe face! Are you engaged that evening?’

[023-123]‘Yes; in the ordinary way. Edwin can’t possibly leave his work.’

[023-124]‘But for one poor evening! It’s such ages since we saw you.’

[023-125]‘I’m very sorry. I don’t think we shall ever be able to accept

[023-126]invitations in future.’

[023-127]Amy spoke thus at the prompting of a sudden impulse. A minute ago, no

[023-128]such definite declaration was in her mind.

[023-129]‘Never?’ exclaimed Edith. ‘But why? Whatever do you mean?’

[023-130]‘We find that social engagements consume too much time,’ Amy replied,

[023-131]her explanation just as much of an impromptu as the announcement had

[023-132]been. ‘You see, one must either belong to society or not. Married people

[023-133]can’t accept an occasional invitation from friends and never do their

[023-134]social duty in return.

[023-135]We have decided to withdraw altogether--at all events for the present. I

[023-136]shall see no one except my relatives.’

[023-137]Edith listened with a face of astonishment.

[023-138]‘You won’t even see ME?’ she exclaimed.

[023-139]‘Indeed, I have no wish to lose your friendship. Yet I am ashamed to ask

[023-140]you to come here when I can never return your visits.’

[023-141]‘Oh, please don’t put it in that way! But it seems so very strange.’

[023-142]Edith could not help conjecturing the true significance of this resolve.

[023-143]But, as is commonly the case with people in easy circumstances, she

[023-144]found it hard to believe that her friends were so straitened as to

[023-145]have a difficulty in supporting the ordinary obligations of a civilised

[023-146]state.

[023-147]‘I know how precious your husband’s time is,’ she added, as if to remove

[023-148]the effect of her last remark. ‘Surely, there’s no harm in my saying--we

[023-149]know each other well enough--you wouldn’t think it necessary to devote

[023-150]an evening to entertaining us just because you had given us the pleasure

[023-151]of your company. I put it very stupidly, but I’m sure you understand me,

[023-152]Amy. Don’t refuse just to come to our house now and then.’

[023-153]‘I’m afraid we shall have to be consistent, Edith.’

[023-154]‘But do you think this is a WISE thing to do?’

[023-155]‘Wise?’

[023-156]‘You know what you once told me, about how necessary it was for a

[023-157]novelist to study all sorts of people. How can Mr Reardon do this if he

[023-158]shuts himself up in the house? I should have thought he would find it

[023-159]necessary to make new acquaintances.’

[023-160]‘As I said,’ returned Amy, ‘it won’t be always like this. For the

[023-161]present, Edwin has quite enough “material.”’

[023-162]She spoke distantly; it irritated her to have to invent excuses for the

[023-163]sacrifice she had just imposed on herself. Edith sipped the tea which

[023-164]had been offered her, and for a minute kept silence.

[023-165]‘When will Mr Reardon’s next book be published?’ she asked at length.

[023-166]‘I’m sure I don’t know. Not before the spring.’

[023-167]‘I shall look so anxiously for it. Whenever I meet new people I always

[023-168]turn the conversation to novels, just for the sake of asking them if

[023-169]they know your husband’s books.’

[023-170]She laughed merrily.

[023-171]‘Which is seldom the case, I should think,’ said Amy, with a smile of

[023-172]indifference.

[023-173]‘Well, my dear, you don’t expect ordinary novel-readers to know about Mr

[023-174]Reardon. I wish my acquaintances were a better kind of people; then, of

[023-175]course, I should hear of his books more often. But one has to make the

[023-176]best of such society as offers. If you and your husband forsake me, I

[023-177]shall feel it a sad loss; I shall indeed.’

[023-178]Amy gave a quick glance at the speaker’s face.

[023-179]‘Oh, we must be friends just the same,’ she said, more naturally than

[023-180]she had spoken hitherto. ‘But don’t ask us to come and dine just now.

[023-181]All through this winter we shall be very busy, both of us. Indeed, we

[023-182]have decided not to accept any invitations at all.’
