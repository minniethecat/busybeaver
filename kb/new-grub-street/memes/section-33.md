# Section 33

[032-001]‘He scarcely could--’

[032-002]‘No, no. And I quite understand that the mere fact of my working for

[032-003][[fadge|Fadge]] would prejudice him against me. But that’s no reason, I hope, why

[032-004]you and I shouldn’t be friends?’

[032-005]‘I hope not.’

[032-006]‘I don’t know that my friendship is worth much,’ [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] continued,

[032-007]talking into the upper air, a habit of his when he discussed his own

[032-008]character. ‘I shall go on as I have begun, and fight for some of the

[032-009]good things of life. But your friendship is valuable. If I am sure of

[032-010]it, I shall be at all events within sight of the better ideals.’

[032-011][[marian-yule|Marian]] walked on with her eyes upon the ground. To her surprise she

[032-012]discovered presently that they had all but reached St Paul’s Crescent.

[032-013]‘Thank you for having come so far,’ she said, pausing.

[032-014]‘Ah, you are nearly home. Why, it seems only a few minutes since we left

[032-015]the girls. Now I’ll run back to the whisky of which Maud disapproves.’

[032-016]‘May it do you good!’ said Marian with a laugh.

[032-017]A speech of this kind seemed unusual upon her lips. Jasper smiled as he

[032-018]held her hand and regarded her.

[032-019]‘Then you can speak in a joking way?’

[032-020]‘Do I seem so very dull?’

[032-021]‘Dull, by no means. But sage and sober and reticent--and exactly what

[032-022]I like in my friend, because it contrasts with my own habits. All the

[032-023]better that merriment lies below it. Goodnight, Miss [[alfred-yule|Yule]].’

[032-024]He strode off and in a minute or two turned his head to look at the

[032-025]slight figure passing into darkness.

[032-026]Marian’s hand trembled as she tried to insert her latch-key. When

[032-027]she had closed the door very quietly behind her she went to the

[032-028]sitting-room; Mrs Yule was just laying aside the sewing on which she had

[032-029]occupied herself throughout the lonely evening.

[032-030]‘I’m rather late,’ said the girl, in a voice of subdued joyousness.

[032-031]‘Yes; I was getting a little uneasy, dear.’

[032-032]‘Oh, there’s no danger.’

[032-033]‘You have been enjoying yourself, I can see.’

[032-034]‘I have had a pleasant evening.’

[032-035]In the retrospect it seemed the pleasantest she had yet spent with her

[032-036]friends, though she had set out in such a different mood. Her mind was

[032-037]relieved of two anxieties; she felt sure that the girls had not

[032-038]taken ill what she told them, and there was no longer the least doubt

[032-039]concerning the authorship of that review in [[the-current|The Current]].

[032-040]She could confess to herself now that the assurance from Jasper’s

[032-041]lips was not superfluous. He might have weighed profit against other

[032-042]considerations, and have written in that way of her father; she had not

[032-043]felt that absolute confidence which defies every argument from human

[032-044]frailty. And now she asked herself if faith of that unassailable kind is

[032-045]ever possible; is it not only the poet’s dream, the far ideal?

[032-046]Marian often went thus far in her speculation. Her candour was allied

[032-047]with clear insight into the possibilities of falsehood; she was not

[032-048]readily the victim of illusion; thinking much, and speaking little, she

[032-049]had not come to her twenty-third year without perceiving what a distance

[032-050]lay between a girl’s dream of life as it might be and life as it is. Had

[032-051]she invariably disclosed her thoughts, she would have earned the repute

[032-052]of a very sceptical and slightly cynical person.

[032-053]But with what rapturous tumult of the heart she could abandon herself to

[032-054]a belief in human virtues when their suggestion seemed to promise her a

[032-055]future of happiness!

[032-056]Alone in her room she sat down only to think of Jasper Milvain, and

[032-057]extract from the memory of his words, his looks, new sustenance for

[032-058]her hungry heart. Jasper was the first man who had ever evinced a

[032-059]man’s interest in her. Until she met him she had not known a look

[032-060]of compliment or a word addressed to her emotions. He was as far as

[032-061]possible from representing the lover of her imagination, but from the

[032-062]day of that long talk in the fields near [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] the thought of

[032-063]him had supplanted dreams. On that day she said to herself: I could love

[032-064]him if he cared to seek my love. Premature, perhaps; why, yes, but one

[032-065]who is starving is not wont to feel reluctance at the suggestion of

[032-066]food. The first man who had approached her with display of feeling and

[032-067]energy and youthful self-confidence; handsome too, it seemed to her. Her

[032-068]womanhood went eagerly to meet him.

[032-069]Since then she had made careful study of his faults. Each conversation

[032-070]had revealed to her new weakness and follies. With the result that her

[032-071]love had grown to a reality.

[032-072]He was so human, and a youth of all but monastic seclusion had prepared

[032-073]her to love the man who aimed with frank energy at the joys of life.

[032-074]A taint of pedantry would have repelled her. She did not ask for high

[032-075]intellect or great attainments; but vivacity, courage, determination to

[032-076]succeed, were delightful to her senses. Her ideal would not have been

[032-077]a literary man at all; certainly not a man likely to be prominent

[032-078]in [[journalism-trade|journalism]]; rather a man of action, one who had no restraints of

[032-079]commerce or official routine. But in Jasper she saw the qualities that

[032-080]attracted her apart from the accidents of his position. Ideal personages

[032-081]do not descend to girls who have to labour at the [[british-museum|British Museum]]; it

[032-082]seemed a marvel to her, and of good augury, that even such a man as

[032-083]Jasper should have crossed her path.

[032-084]It was as though years had passed since their first meeting. Upon her

[032-085]return to [[london|London]] had followed such long periods of hopelessness. Yet

[032-086]whenever they encountered each other he had look and speech for her with

[032-087]which surely he did not greet every woman. From the first his way of

[032-088]regarding her had shown frank interest. And at length had come the

[032-089]confession of his ‘respect,’ his desire to be something more to her than

[032-090]a mere acquaintance. It was scarcely possible that he should speak as he

[032-091]several times had of late if he did not wish to draw her towards him.

[032-092]That was the hopeful side of her thoughts. It was easy to forget for a

[032-093]time those words of his which one might think were spoken as distinct

[032-094]warning; but they crept into the memory, unwelcome, importunate, as soon

[032-095]as imagination had built its palace of joy. Why did he always recur to

[032-096]the subject of [[money-in-literature|money]]? ‘I shall allow nothing to come in my way;’ he once

[032-097]said that as if meaning, ‘certainly not a love affair with a girl who

[032-098]is penniless.’ He emphasised the word ‘friend,’ as if to explain that he

[032-099]offered and asked nothing more than friendship.

[032-100]But it only meant that he would not be in haste to declare himself. Of

[032-101]a certainty there was conflict between his [[literary-ambition|ambition]] and his love, but

[032-102]she recognised her power over him and exulted in it. She had observed

[032-103]his hesitancy this evening, before he rose to accompany her from

[032-104]the house; her heart laughed within her as the desire drew him. And

[032-105]henceforth such meetings would be frequent, with each one her influence

[032-106]would increase. How kindly fate had dealt with her in bringing Maud and

[032-107][[dora-milvain|Dora]] to London!

[032-108]It was within his reach to marry a woman who would bring him wealth.

[032-109]He had that in mind; she understood it too well. But not one moment’s

[032-110]advantage would she relinquish. He must choose her in her [[literary-poverty|poverty]], and

[032-111]be content with what his talents could earn for him. Her love gave her

[032-112]the right to demand this sacrifice; let him ask for her love, and the

[032-113]sacrifice would no longer seem one, so passionately would she reward

[032-114]him.

[032-115]He would ask it. To-night she was full of a rich confidence, partly, no

[032-116]doubt, the result of reaction from her miseries. He had said at parting

[032-117]that her character was so well suited to his; that he liked her. And

[032-118]then he had pressed her hand so warmly. Before long he would ask her

[032-119]love.

[032-120]The unhoped was all but granted her. She could labour on in the valley

[032-121]of the shadow of books, for a ray of dazzling sunshine might at any

[032-122]moment strike into its musty gloom.

[032-123]CHAPTER XV. THE LAST RESOURCE

[032-124]The past twelve months had added several years to [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]]’s

[032-125]seeming age; at thirty-three he would generally have been taken for

[032-126]forty. His bearing, his personal habits, were no longer those of a

[032-127]young man; he walked with a stoop and pressed noticeably on the stick

[032-128]he carried; it was rare for him to show the countenance which tells of

[032-129]present cheerfulness or glad onward-looking; there was no spring in his

[032-130]step; his voice had fallen to a lower key, and often he spoke with

[032-131]that hesitation in choice of words which may be noticed in persons whom

[032-132]defeat has made self-distrustful. Ceaseless perplexity and dread gave a

[032-133]wandering, sometimes a wild, expression to his eyes.

[032-134]He seldom slept, in the proper sense of the word; as a rule he was

[032-135]conscious all through the night of ‘a kind of fighting’ between physical

[032-136]weariness and wakeful toil of the mind. It often happened that some

[032-137]wholly imaginary obstacle in the story he was writing kept him under

[032-138]a sense of effort throughout the dark hours; now and again he woke,

[032-139]reasoned with himself, and remembered clearly that the torment was

[032-140]without cause, but the short relief thus afforded soon passed in the

[032-141]recollection of real distress. In his unsoothing slumber he talked

[032-142]aloud, frequently wakening [[amy-reardon|Amy]]; generally he seemed to be holding a

[032-143]dialogue with someone who had imposed an intolerable task upon him; he

[032-144]protested passionately, appealed, argued in the strangest way about

[032-145]the injustice of what was demanded. Once Amy heard him begging for

[032-146]money--positively begging, like some poor wretch in the street; it

[032-147]was horrible, and made her shed tears; when he asked what he had been

[032-148]saying, she could not bring herself to tell him.

[032-149]When the striking clocks summoned him remorselessly to rise and work

[032-150]he often reeled with dizziness. It seemed to him that the greatest

[032-151]happiness attainable would be to creep into some dark, warm corner, out

[032-152]of the sight and memory of men, and lie there torpid, with a blessed

[032-153]half-consciousness that death was slowly overcoming him. Of all the

[032-154]sufferings collected into each four-and-twenty hours this of rising to a

[032-155]new day was the worst.

[032-156]The one-volume story which he had calculated would take him four or five

[032-157]weeks was with difficulty finished in two months. March winds made an

[032-158]invalid of him; at one time he was threatened with bronchitis, and for

[032-159]several days had to abandon even the effort to work. In previous winters

[032-160]he had been wont to undergo a good deal of martyrdom from the London

[032-161]climate, but never in such a degree as now; mental illness seemed to

[032-162]have enfeebled his body.

[032-163]It was strange that he succeeded in doing work of any kind, for he had

[032-164]no hope from the result. This one last effort he would make, just to

[032-165]complete the undeniableness of his failure, and then literature should

[032-166]be thrown behind him; what other pursuit was possible to him he knew

[032-167]not, but perhaps he might discover some mode of earning a livelihood.

[032-168]Had it been a question of gaining a pound a week, as in the old days,

[032-169]he might have hoped to obtain some clerkship like that at the hospital,

[032-170]where no commercial experience or aptitude was demanded; but in his

[032-171]present position such an income would be useless. Could he take Amy

[032-172]and the child to live in a garret? On less than a hundred a year it was

[032-173]scarcely possible to maintain outward decency. Already his own clothing

[032-174]began to declare him poverty-stricken, and but for gifts from her

[032-175]mother Amy would have reached the like pass. They lived in dread of

[032-176]the pettiest casual expense, for the day of pennilessness was again

[032-177]approaching.
