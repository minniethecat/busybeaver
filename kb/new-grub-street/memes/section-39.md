# Section 39

[038-001]He let the sheet fall, and looked so utterly woebegone that his friends

[038-002]at once exerted themselves to offer such consolation as the case

[038-003]admitted of. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] thought better of [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] for this emotion; he

[038-004]had not believed him capable of it.

[038-005]‘It isn’t a case of vulgar cheating!’ cried the forsaken one presently.

[038-006]‘Don’t go away thinking that. She writes in real distress and

[038-007]penitence--she does indeed. Oh, the devil! Why did I let her go to

[038-008]Birmingham? A fortnight more, and I should have had her safe. But it’s

[038-009]just like my luck. Do you know that this is the third time I’ve been

[038-010]engaged to be married?--no, by Jove, the fourth! And every time the girl

[038-011]has got out of it at the last moment. What an unlucky beast I am! A girl

[038-012]who was positively my ideal! I haven’t even a photograph of her to show

[038-013]you; but you’d be astonished at her face. Why, in the devil’s name, did

[038-014]I let her go to Birmingham?’

[038-015]The visitors had risen. They felt uncomfortable, for it seemed as if

[038-016]Whelpdale might find vent for his distress in tears.

[038-017]‘We had better leave you,’ suggested [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]. ‘It’s very hard--it is

[038-018]indeed.’

[038-019]‘Look here! Read the letter for yourselves! Do!’

[038-020]They declined, and begged him not to insist.

[038-021]‘But I want you to see what kind of girl she is. It isn’t a case of

[038-022]farcical deceiving--not a bit of it! She implores me to forgive her, and

[038-023]blames herself no end. Just my luck! The third--no, the fourth time, by

[038-024]Jove! Never was such an unlucky fellow with women. It’s because I’m so

[038-025]damnably poor; that’s it, of course!’

[038-026]Reardon and his companion succeeded at length in getting away, though

[038-027]not till they had heard the virtues and beauty of the vanished girl

[038-028]described again and again in much detail. Both were in a state of

[038-029]depression as they left the house.

[038-030]‘What think you of this story?’ asked Biffen. ‘Is this possible in a

[038-031]woman of any merit?’

[038-032]‘Anything is possible in a woman,’ Reardon replied, harshly.

[038-033]They walked in silence as far as Portland Road Station. There, with an

[038-034]assurance that he would come to a garret-supper before leaving [[london|London]],

[038-035]Reardon parted from his friend and turned westward.

[038-036]As soon as he had entered, [[amy-reardon|Amy]]’s voice called to him:

[038-037]‘Here’s a letter from [[jedwood|Jedwood]], Edwin!’

[038-038]He stepped into the study.

[038-039]‘It came just after you went out, and it has been all I could do to

[038-040]resist the temptation to open it.’

[038-041]‘Why shouldn’t you have opened it?’ said her husband, carelessly.

[038-042]He tried to do so himself, but his shaking hand thwarted him at first.

[038-043]Succeeding at length, he found a letter in the publisher’s own writing,

[038-044]and the first word that caught his attention was ‘regret.’ With an angry

[038-045]effort to command himself he ran through the communication, then held it

[038-046]out to Amy.

[038-047]She read, and her countenance fell. Mr Jedwood regretted that the story

[038-048]offered to him did not seem likely to please that particular public to

[038-049]whom his series of one-volume novels made appeal. He hoped it would

[038-050]be understood that, in declining, he by no means expressed an adverse

[038-051]judgment on the story itself &c.

[038-052]‘It doesn’t surprise me,’ said Reardon. ‘I believe he is quite right.

[038-053]The thing is too empty to please the better kind of readers, yet not

[038-054]vulgar enough to please the worse.’

[038-055]‘But you’ll try someone else?’

[038-056]‘I don’t think it’s much use.’

[038-057]They sat opposite each other, and kept silence. Jedwood’s letter slipped

[038-058]from Amy’s lap to the ground.

[038-059]‘So,’ said Reardon, presently, ‘I don’t see how our plan is to be

[038-060]carried out.’

[038-061]‘Oh, it must be!’

[038-062]‘But how?’

[038-063]‘You’ll get seven or eight pounds from [[the-wayside|The Wayside]]. And--hadn’t we

[038-064]better sell the furniture, instead of--’

[038-065]His look checked her.

[038-066]‘It seems to me, Amy, that your one desire is to get away from me, on

[038-067]whatever terms.’

[038-068]‘Don’t begin that over again!’ she exclaimed, fretfully. ‘If you don’t

[038-069]believe what I say--’

[038-070]They were both in a state of intolerable nervous tension. Their voices

[038-071]quivered, and their eyes had an unnatural brightness.

[038-072]‘If we sell the furniture,’ pursued Reardon, ‘that means you’ll never

[038-073]come back to me. You wish to save yourself and the child from the hard

[038-074]life that seems to be before us.’

[038-075]‘Yes, I do; but not by deserting you. I want you to go and work for us

[038-076]all, so that we may live more happily before long. Oh, how wretched this

[038-077]is!’

[038-078]She burst into hysterical weeping. But Reardon, instead of attempting to

[038-079]soothe her, went into the next room, where he sat for a long time in

[038-080]the dark. When he returned Amy was calm again; her face expressed a cold

[038-081]misery.

[038-082]‘Where did you go this morning?’ he asked, as if wishing to talk of

[038-083]common things.

[038-084]‘I told you. I went to buy those things for Willie.’

[038-085]‘Oh yes.’

[038-086]There was a silence.

[038-087]‘Biffen passed you in [[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]],’ he added.

[038-088]‘I didn’t see him.’

[038-089]‘No; he said you didn’t.’

[038-090]‘Perhaps,’ said Amy, ‘it was just when I was speaking to [[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]].’

[038-091]‘You met Milvain?’

[038-092]‘Yes.’

[038-093]‘Why didn’t you tell me?’

[038-094]‘I’m sure I don’t know. I can’t mention every trifle that happens.’

[038-095]‘No, of course not.’

[038-096]Amy closed her eyes, as if in weariness, and for a minute or two Reardon

[038-097]observed her countenance.

[038-098]‘So you think we had better sell the furniture.’

[038-099]‘I shall say nothing more about it. You must do as seems best to you,

[038-100]Edwin.’

[038-101]‘Are you going to see your mother to-morrow?’

[038-102]‘Yes. I thought you would like to come too.’

[038-103]‘No; there’s no good in my going.’

[038-104]He again rose, and that night they talked no more of their difficulties,

[038-105]though on the morrow (Sunday) it would be necessary to decide their

[038-106]course in every detail.

[038-107]CHAPTER XVII. THE PARTING

[038-108]Amy did not go to church. Before her [[literary-marriage|marriage]] she had done so as a mere

[038-109]matter of course, accompanying her mother, but Reardon’s attitude with

[038-110]regard to the popular religion speedily became her own; she let the

[038-111]subject lapse from her mind, and cared neither to defend nor to attack

[038-112]where dogma was concerned. She had no sympathies with mysticism; her

[038-113]nature was strongly practical, with something of zeal for intellectual

[038-114]attainment superadded.

[038-115]This Sunday morning she was very busy with domestic minutiae. Reardon

[038-116]noticed what looked like preparations for packing, and being as little

[038-117]disposed for conversation as his wife, he went out and walked for a

[038-118]couple of hours in the Hampstead region. Dinner over, Amy at once made

[038-119]ready for her journey to Westbourne Park.

[038-120]‘Then you won’t come?’ she said to her husband.

[038-121]‘No. I shall see your mother before I go away, but I don’t care to till

[038-122]you have settled everything.’

[038-123]It was half a year since he had met Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]. She never came to their

[038-124]dwelling, and Reardon could not bring himself to visit her.

[038-125]‘You had very much rather we didn’t sell the furniture?’ Amy asked.

[038-126]‘Ask your mother’s opinion. That shall decide.’

[038-127]‘There’ll be the expense of moving it, you know. Unless [[money-in-literature|money]] comes from

[038-128]The Wayside, you’ll only have two or three pounds left.’

[038-129]Reardon made no reply. He was overcome by the bitterness of shame.

[038-130]‘I shall say, then,’ pursued Amy, who spoke with averted face, ‘that I

[038-131]am to go there for good on Tuesday? I mean, of course, for the summer

[038-132]months.’

[038-133]‘I suppose so.’

[038-134]Then he turned suddenly upon her.

[038-135]‘Do you really imagine that at the end of the summer I shall be a rich

[038-136]man? What do you mean by talking in this way? If the furniture is sold

[038-137]to supply me with a few pounds for the present, what prospect is there

[038-138]that I shall be able to buy new?’

[038-139]‘How can we look forward at all?’ replied Amy. ‘It has come to the

[038-140]question of how we are to subsist. I thought you would rather get money

[038-141]in this way than borrow of mother--when she has the expense of keeping

[038-142]me and Willie.’

[038-143]‘You are right,’ muttered Reardon. ‘Do as you think best.’ Amy was in

[038-144]her most practical mood, and would not linger for purposeless talk. A

[038-145]few minutes, and Reardon was left alone.

[038-146]He stood before his bookshelves and began to pick out the volumes which

[038-147]he would take away with him. Just a few, the indispensable companions of

[038-148]a bookish man who still clings to life--his Homer, his Shakespeare--

[038-149]The rest must be sold. He would get rid of them to-morrow morning. All

[038-150]together they might bring him a couple of sovereigns.

[038-151]Then his clothing. Amy had fulfilled all the domestic duties of a wife;

[038-152]his wardrobe was in as good a state as circumstances allowed. But there

[038-153]was no object in burdening himself with winter garments, for, if he

[038-154]lived through the summer at all, he would be able to repurchase such few

[038-155]poor things as were needful; at present he could only think of how to

[038-156]get together a few coins. So he made a heap of such things as might be

[038-157]sold.

[038-158]The furniture? If it must go, the price could scarcely be more than ten

[038-159]or twelve pounds; well, perhaps fifteen. To be sure, in this way his

[038-160]summer’s living would be abundantly provided for.

[038-161]He thought of Biffen enviously. Biffen, if need be, could support life

[038-162]on three or four shillings a week, happy in the thought that no mortal

[038-163]had a claim upon him. If he starved to death--well, many another lonely

[038-164]man has come to that end. If he preferred to kill himself, who would be

[038-165]distressed? Spoilt child of fortune!

[038-166]The bells of St [[marylebone|Marylebone]] began to clang for afternoon service. In

[038-167]the idleness of dull pain his thoughts followed their summons, and he

[038-168]marvelled that there were people who could imagine it a duty or find it

[038-169]a solace to go and sit in that twilight church and listen to the droning

[038-170]of prayers. He thought of the wretched millions of mankind to whom life

[038-171]is so barren that they must needs believe in a recompense beyond the

[038-172]grave. For that he neither looked nor longed. The bitterness of his

[038-173]lot was that this world might be a sufficing paradise to him if only he

[038-174]could clutch a poor little share of current coin. He had won the world’s

[038-175]greatest prize--a woman’s love--but could not retain it because his

[038-176]pockets were empty.

[038-177]That he should fail to make a great name, this was grievous

[038-178]disappointment to Amy, but this alone would not have estranged her. It

[038-179]was the dread and shame of [[literary-poverty|penury]] that made her heart cold to him. And

[038-180]he could not in his conscience scorn her for being thus affected by the

[038-181]vulgar circumstances of life; only a few supreme natures stand unshaken

[038-182]under such a trial, and though his love of Amy was still passionate, he

[038-183]knew that her place was among a certain [[social-class|class]] of women, and not on the

[038-184]isolated pinnacle where he had at first visioned her. It was entirely

[038-185]natural that she shrank at the test of squalid suffering. A little

[038-186]money, and he could have rested secure in her love, for then he would

[038-187]have been able to keep ever before her the best qualities of his heart

[038-188]and brain. Upon him, too, penury had its debasing effect; as he now

[038-189]presented himself he was not a man to be admired or loved. It was all

[038-190]simple and intelligible enough--a situation that would be misread only

[038-191]by shallow idealism.

[038-192]Worst of all, she was attracted by Jasper Milvain’s energy and promise

[038-193]of [[literary-ambition|success]]. He had no ignoble suspicions of Amy, but it was impossible

[038-194]for him not to see that she habitually contrasted the young journalist,

[038-195]who laughingly made his way among men, with her grave, dispirited

[038-196]husband, who was not even capable of holding such position as he had

[038-197]gained. She enjoyed Milvain’s conversation, it put her into a good

[038-198]humour; she liked him personally, and there could be no doubt that she

[038-199]had observed a jealous tendency in Reardon’s attitude to his former

[038-200]friend--always a harmful suggestion to a woman. Formerly she had

[038-201]appreciated her husband’s superiority; she had smiled at Milvain’s

[038-202]commoner stamp of mind and character. But tedious repetition of failure

[038-203]had outwearied her, and now she saw Milvain in the sunshine of progress,

[038-204]dwelt upon the worldly advantages of gifts and a temperament such as

[038-205]his. Again, simple and intelligible enough.
