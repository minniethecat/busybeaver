# Money in Literature

Money is the novel's true protagonist. *New Grub Street* tracks income, expenses, debts, advances, and inheritances with an accountant's precision. The question "how much?" — how much does a novel earn, how much does a marriage cost, how much is a writer worth — pervades every scene, and the answers determine who lives, who marries, and who dies.

## The Arithmetic of Literary Life

Gissing is relentlessly specific about sums. Reardon earns fifty pounds for the copyright of a novel. A hack writer can earn "about a hundred a year in Grub Street" (005-105). Milvain aims for "a thousand a year" (P02-204). These figures are not decorative — they are the novel's real structure. Characters are sorted by income bracket as precisely as by moral quality.

The economics of publishing are anatomized in detail. An author may receive a percentage "if the sale in three-volume form should reach a certain number" (026-142), but such contingent payments rarely materialize. The circulating libraries — above all Mudie's — are the real purchasers of three-volume novels, and their buying decisions determine which authors survive. The publisher, not the writer, controls the revenue.

## Money and Marriage

Every marriage plot in the novel is also a financial plot. Milvain explicitly plans to "marry a woman with an income somewhat larger, so that casualties may be provided for" (P02-206–P02-208). Reardon's marriage to Amy founders on poverty: she cannot accept the social descent that his literary earnings cannot prevent. John Yule's will — a sum of money, distributed or withheld — determines Marian's marital fate. Love is contingent on income.

## Money and Artistic Integrity

The novel's central equation is that artistic integrity costs money. Reardon writes slowly and carefully and earns almost nothing; Milvain writes rapidly and commercially and prospers. There is no middle ground — "writing is a business" (001-119), and those who refuse to treat it as such are punished economically. The novel makes visible the cost of art in a system where art is not valued.

## The Inheritance Plot

Inheritances — John Yule's will, Amy Reardon's legacy — function as *deus ex machina* events that reveal the true financial logic of the characters' relationships. When Marian Yule fails to inherit, Milvain withdraws from their engagement. When Amy inherits, her marriage to Reardon becomes terminally unbalanced. Money, not love, is the ultimate arbiter of relationships in Gissing's world.
