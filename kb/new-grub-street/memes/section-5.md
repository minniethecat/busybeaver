# Section 5

[004-001]It was agreed that Miss Harrow and [[marian-yule|Marian]] should come on the second

[004-002]day after to have tea with the Milvains. And when [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] took leave of

[004-003][[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]], the latter expressed a wish that they might have a walk

[004-004]together one of these mornings.

[004-005]CHAPTER III. HOLIDAY

[004-006]Jasper’s favourite walk led him to a spot distant perhaps a mile and a

[004-007]half from home. From a tract of common he turned into a short lane which

[004-008]crossed the Great Western railway, and thence by a stile into certain

[004-009]meadows forming a compact little valley. One recommendation of this

[004-010]retreat was that it lay sheltered from all winds; to Jasper a wind was

[004-011]objectionable. Along the bottom ran a clear, shallow stream, overhung

[004-012]with elder and hawthorn bushes; and close by the wooden bridge which

[004-013]spanned it was a great ash tree, making shadow for cows and sheep when

[004-014]the sun lay hot upon the open field. It was rare for anyone to come

[004-015]along this path, save farm labourers morning and evening.

[004-016]But to-day--the afternoon that followed his visit to [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s

[004-017]house--he saw from a distance that his lounging-place on the wooden

[004-018]bridge was occupied. Someone else had discovered the pleasure there was

[004-019]in watching the sun-flecked sparkle of the water as it flowed over the

[004-020]clean sand and stones. A girl in a yellow-straw hat; yes, and precisely

[004-021]the person he had hoped, at the first glance, that it might be. He

[004-022]made no haste as he drew nearer on the descending path. At length his

[004-023]footstep was heard; Marian Yule turned her head and clearly recognised

[004-024]him.

[004-025]She assumed an upright position, letting one of her hands rest upon

[004-026]the rail. After the exchange of ordinary greetings, Jasper leaned back

[004-027]against the same support and showed himself disposed for talk.

[004-028]‘When I was here late in the spring,’ he said, ‘this ash was only just

[004-029]budding, though everything else seemed in full leaf.’

[004-030]‘An ash, is it?’ murmured Marian. ‘I didn’t know. I think an oak is the

[004-031]only tree I can distinguish. Yet,’ she added quickly, ‘I knew that the

[004-032]ash was late; some lines of Tennyson come to my memory.’

[004-033]‘Which are those?’

[004-034]‘Delaying, as the tender ash delays

[004-035]To clothe herself when all the woods are green,

[004-036]somewhere in the “Idylls.”’

[004-037]‘I don’t remember; so I won’t pretend to--though I should do so as a

[004-038]rule.’

[004-039]She looked at him oddly, and seemed about to laugh, yet did not.

[004-040]‘You have had little experience of the country?’ Jasper continued.

[004-041]‘Very little. You, I think, have known it from childhood?’

[004-042]‘In a sort of way. I was born in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]], and my people have

[004-043]always lived here. But I am not very rural in temperament. I have really

[004-044]no friends here; either they have lost interest in me, or I in them.

[004-045]What do you think of the girls, my sisters?’

[004-046]The question, though put with perfect simplicity, was embarrassing.

[004-047]‘They are tolerably intellectual,’ Jasper went on, when he saw that it

[004-048]would be difficult for her to answer. ‘I want to persuade them to try

[004-049]their hands at literary work of some kind or other. They give lessons,

[004-050]and both hate it.’

[004-051]‘Would literary work be less--burdensome?’ said Marian, without looking

[004-052]at him.

[004-053]‘Rather more so, you think?’

[004-054]She hesitated.

[004-055]‘It depends, of course, on--on several things.’

[004-056]‘To be sure,’ Jasper agreed. ‘I don’t think they have any marked faculty

[004-057]for such work; but as they certainly haven’t for teaching, that doesn’t

[004-058]matter. It’s a question of learning a business. I am going through my

[004-059]apprenticeship, and find it a long affair. Money would shorten it, and,

[004-060]unfortunately, I have none.’

[004-061]‘Yes,’ said Marian, turning her eyes upon the stream, ‘[[money-in-literature|money]] is a help

[004-062]in everything.’

[004-063]‘Without it, one spends the best part of one’s life in toiling for that

[004-064]first foothold which money could at once purchase. To have money is

[004-065]becoming of more and more importance in a literary career; principally

[004-066]because to have money is to have friends. Year by year, such influence

[004-067]grows of more account. A lucky man will still occasionally succeed by

[004-068]dint of his own honest perseverance, but the chances are dead against

[004-069]anyone who can’t make private interest with influential people; his work

[004-070]is simply overwhelmed by that of the men who have better opportunities.’

[004-071]‘Don’t you think that, even to-day, really good work will sooner or

[004-072]later be recognised?’

[004-073]‘Later, rather than sooner; and very likely the man can’t wait; he

[004-074]starves in the meantime. You understand that I am not speaking of

[004-075][[literary-genius|genius]]; I mean marketable literary work. The quantity turned out is

[004-076]so great that there’s no hope for the special attention of the public

[004-077]unless one can afford to advertise hugely. Take the instance of a

[004-078]successful all-round man of letters; take Ralph Warbury, whose name

[004-079]you’ll see in the first magazine you happen to open. But perhaps he is a

[004-080]friend of yours?’

[004-081]‘Oh no!’

[004-082]‘Well, I wasn’t going to abuse him. I was only going to ask: Is there

[004-083]any quality which distinguishes his work from that of twenty struggling

[004-084]writers one could name? Of course not. He’s a clever, prolific man; so

[004-085]are they. But he began with money and friends; he came from [[oxford|Oxford]] into

[004-086]the thick of advertised people; his name was mentioned in print six

[004-087]times a week before he had written a dozen articles. This kind of thing

[004-088]will become the rule. Men won’t succeed in literature that they may

[004-089]get into society, but will get into society that they may succeed in

[004-090]literature.’

[004-091]‘Yes, I know it is true,’ said Marian, in a low voice.

[004-092]‘There’s a friend of mine who writes novels,’ Jasper pursued. ‘His

[004-093]books are not works of genius, but they are glaringly distinct from the

[004-094]ordinary circulating novel. Well, after one or two attempts, he made

[004-095]half a [[literary-ambition|success]]; that is to say, the [[publishing-trade|publishers]] brought out a second

[004-096]edition of the book in a few months. There was his opportunity. But he

[004-097]couldn’t use it; he had no friends, because he had no money. A book of

[004-098]half that merit, if written by a man in the position of Warbury when

[004-099]he started, would have established the [[literary-success|reputation]] of a lifetime. His

[004-100]influential friends would have referred to it in leaders, in magazine

[004-101]articles, in speeches, in sermons. It would have run through numerous

[004-102]editions, and the author would have had nothing to do but to write

[004-103]another book and demand his price. But the novel I’m speaking of was

[004-104]practically forgotten a year after its appearance; it was whelmed

[004-105]beneath the flood of next season’s literature.’

[004-106]Marian urged a hesitating objection.

[004-107]‘But, under the circumstances, wasn’t it in the author’s power to make

[004-108]friends? Was money really indispensable?’

[004-109]‘Why, yes--because he chose to marry. As a bachelor he might possibly

[004-110]have got into the right circles, though his character would in any case

[004-111]have made it difficult for him to curry favour.

[004-112]But as a married man, without means, the situation was hopeless. Once

[004-113]married you must live up to the standard of the society you frequent;

[004-114]you can’t be entertained without entertaining in return. Now if his wife

[004-115]had brought him only a couple of thousand pounds all might have been

[004-116]well. I should have advised him, in sober seriousness, to live for two

[004-117]years at the rate of a thousand a year. At the end of that time he would

[004-118]have been earning enough to continue at pretty much the same rate of

[004-119]expenditure.’

[004-120]‘Perhaps.’

[004-121]‘Well, I ought rather to say that the average man of letters would be

[004-122]able to do that. As for [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]--’

[004-123]He stopped. The name had escaped him unawares.

[004-124]‘Reardon?’ said Marian, looking up. ‘You are speaking of him?’

[004-125]‘I have betrayed myself Miss Yule.’

[004-126]‘But what does it matter? You have only spoken in his favour.’

[004-127]‘I feared the name might affect you disagreeably.’

[004-128]Marian delayed her reply.

[004-129]‘It is true,’ she said, ‘we are not on friendly terms with my cousin’s

[004-130]family. I have never met Mr Reardon. But I shouldn’t like you to think

[004-131]that the mention of his name is disagreeable to me.’

[004-132]‘It made me slightly uncomfortable yesterday--the fact that I am well

[004-133]acquainted with [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]], and that Reardon is my friend. Yet

[004-134]I didn’t see why that should prevent my making your father’s

[004-135]acquaintance.’

[004-136]‘Surely not. I shall say nothing about it; I mean, as you uttered the

[004-137]name unintentionally.’

[004-138]There was a pause in the dialogue. They had been speaking almost

[004-139]confidentially, and Marian seemed to become suddenly aware of an oddness

[004-140]in the situation. She turned towards the uphill path, as if thinking of

[004-141]resuming her walk.

[004-142]‘You are tired of standing still,’ said Jasper. ‘May I walk back a part

[004-143]of the way with you?’

[004-144]‘Thank you; I shall be glad.’

[004-145]They went on for a few minutes in silence.

[004-146]‘Have you published anything with your signature, Miss Yule?’ Jasper at

[004-147]length inquired.

[004-148]‘Nothing. I only help father a little.’

[004-149]The silence that again followed was broken this time by Marian.

[004-150]‘When you chanced to mention Mr Reardon’s name,’ she said, with a

[004-151]diffident smile in which lay that suggestion of humour so delightful

[004-152]upon a woman’s face, ‘you were going to say something more about him?’

[004-153]‘Only that--’ he broke off and laughed. ‘Now, how boyish it was, wasn’t

[004-154]it? I remember doing just the same thing once when I came home

[004-155]from school and had an exciting story to tell, with preservation of

[004-156]anonymities. Of course I blurted out a name in the first minute or two,

[004-157]to my father’s great amusement. He told me that I hadn’t the diplomatic

[004-158]character. I have been trying to acquire it ever since.

[004-159]‘But why?’

[004-160]‘It’s one of the essentials of success in any kind of public life. And

[004-161]I mean to succeed, you know. I feel that I am one of the men who do

[004-162]succeed. But I beg your pardon; you asked me a question. Really, I was

[004-163]only going to say of Reardon what I had said before: that he hasn’t the

[004-164]tact requisite for acquiring popularity.’

[004-165]‘Then I may hope that it isn’t his [[literary-marriage|marriage]] with my cousin which has

[004-166]proved a fatal misfortune?’

[004-167]‘In no case,’ replied Milvain, averting his look, ‘would he have used

[004-168]his advantages.’

[004-169]‘And now? Do you think he has but poor prospects?’

[004-170]‘I wish I could see any chance of his being estimated at his right

[004-171]value. It’s very hard to say what is before him.’

[004-172]‘I knew my cousin [[amy-reardon|Amy]] when we were children,’ said Marian, presently.

[004-173]‘She gave promise of beauty.’

[004-174]‘Yes, she is beautiful.’

[004-175]‘And--the kind of woman to be of help to such a husband?’

[004-176]‘I hardly know how to answer, Miss Yule,’ said Jasper, looking frankly

[004-177]at her. ‘Perhaps I had better say that it’s unfortunate they are poor.’

[004-178]Marian cast down her eyes.

[004-179]‘To whom isn’t it a misfortune?’ pursued her companion. ‘Poverty is the

[004-180]root of all social ills; its existence accounts even for the ills that

[004-181]arise from wealth. The poor man is a man labouring in fetters. I declare

[004-182]there is no word in our language which sounds so hideous to me as

[004-183]“Poverty.”’

[004-184]Shortly after this they came to the bridge over the railway line. Jasper

[004-185]looked at his watch.

[004-186]‘Will you indulge me in a piece of childishness?’ he said. ‘In less than

[004-187]five minutes a [[london|London]] express goes by; I have often watched it here, and

[004-188]it amuses me. Would it weary you to wait?’

[004-189]‘I should like to,’ she replied with a laugh.

[004-190]The line ran along a deep cutting, from either side of which grew hazel

[004-191]bushes and a few larger trees. Leaning upon the parapet of the bridge,

[004-192]Jasper kept his eye in the westward direction, where the gleaming rails

[004-193]were visible for more than a mile. Suddenly he raised his finger.
