# Social Class

*New Grub Street* is saturated with class consciousness, though it is rarely discussed explicitly. Every character's position — their income, their address, their marriage prospects, their literary ambitions — is shaped by class. The literary marketplace, Gissing shows, is not a meritocracy but a class system in which some writers can afford to fail while others cannot afford to try.

## The Upper Middle-Class of Intellect

The novel's characters occupy an ambiguous class position. They are educated — they read Greek, discuss literary theory, move in circles of intellectual aspiration — but they are poor. Jasper Milvain describes himself and his peers as "the upper middle-class of intellect, the people who" (001-142) — the incomplete quotation itself suggesting the instability of the category. They possess cultural capital but not economic capital, and the tension between these two forms of value drives the novel's social dynamics.

## Class and Marriage

Marriage in the novel is class endogamy enforced by financial necessity. Milvain plans to marry "a woman with an income somewhat larger" (P02-207) — class consolidation through marriage. Maud's marriage to a wealthy man allows her to escape Grub Street entirely, speaking afterward "with laughing scorn of the days when she inhabited" it (085-036). Amy Reardon's inability to accept poverty is a class response — she has the expectations of the middle class without the income to support them.

## Class and Literary Form

Class even determines what kind of writing is possible. The three-volume novel was a middle-class form, distributed through circulating libraries to middle-class readers. Journalism was a lower-status form, associated with Grub Street hacks. Milvain's ambition to rise from journalism to editorship is a class journey as much as a literary one. Reardon's commitment to serious fiction is, in class terms, an aspiration to status that his income cannot support.

## The Working Class Absence

The working class is almost entirely absent from the novel, appearing only as servants or objects of occasional reference. Gissing's world is the anxious lower-middle class of struggling professionals — people who have enough education to aspire but not enough capital to be secure. This class precarity is the unspoken condition of every character's literary life.
