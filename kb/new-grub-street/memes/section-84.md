# Section 84

[083-001]‘If I get home at five, it’ll be all right,’ he replied. ‘What have you

[083-002]to tell me, [[marian-yule|Marian]]?’

[083-003]‘We have heard about the [[money-in-literature|money]], at last.’

[083-004]‘Oh?’ He avoided looking at her. ‘And what’s the upshot?’

[083-005]‘I shall have nearly fifteen hundred pounds.’

[083-006]‘So much as that? Well, that’s better than nothing, isn’t it?’

[083-007]‘Very much better.’

[083-008]They walked on in silence. Marian stole a glance at her companion.

[083-009]‘I should have thought it a great deal,’ she said presently, ‘before I

[083-010]had begun to think of thousands.’

[083-011]‘Fifteen hundred. Well, it means fifty pounds a year, I suppose.’

[083-012]He chewed the end of his moustache.

[083-013]‘Let us sit down on this bench. Fifteen hundred--h’m! And nothing more

[083-014]is to be hoped for?’

[083-015]‘Nothing. I should have thought men would wish to pay their debts, even

[083-016]after they had been bankrupt; but they tell us we can’t expect anything

[083-017]more from these people.’

[083-018]‘You are thinking of Walter Scott, and that kind of thing’--[[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]

[083-019]laughed. ‘Oh, that’s quite unbusinesslike; it would be setting a

[083-020]pernicious example nowadays. Well, and what’s to be done?’

[083-021]Marian had no answer for such a question. The tone of it was a new stab

[083-022]to her heart, which had suffered so many during the past half-year.

[083-023]‘Now, I’ll ask you frankly,’ Jasper went on, ‘and I know you will reply

[083-024]in the same spirit: would it be wise for us to marry on this money?’

[083-025]‘On this money?’

[083-026]She looked into his face with painful earnestness.

[083-027]‘You mean,’ he said, ‘that it can’t be spared for that purpose?’

[083-028]What she really meant was uncertain even to herself. She had wished to

[083-029]hear how Jasper would receive the news, and thereby to direct her own

[083-030]course. Had he welcomed it as offering a possibility of their [[literary-marriage|marriage]],

[083-031]that would have gladdened her, though it would then have been necessary

[083-032]to show him all the difficulties by which she was beset; for some time

[083-033]they had not spoken of her father’s position, and Jasper seemed willing

[083-034]to forget all about that complication of their troubles. But marriage

[083-035]did not occur to him, and he was evidently quite prepared to hear that

[083-036]she could no longer regard this money as her own to be freely disposed

[083-037]of. This was on one side a relief but on the other it confirmed her

[083-038]fears. She would rather have heard him plead with her to neglect her

[083-039]parents for the sake of being his wife. Love excuses everything, and his

[083-040]selfishness would have been easily lost sight of in the assurance that

[083-041]he still desired her.

[083-042]‘You say,’ she replied, with bent head, ‘that it would bring us fifty

[083-043]pounds a year. If another fifty were added to that, my father and mother

[083-044]would be supported in case the worst comes. I might earn fifty pounds.’

[083-045]‘You wish me to understand, Marian, that I mustn’t expect that you will

[083-046]bring me anything when we are married.’

[083-047]His tone was that of acquiescence; not by any means of displeasure. He

[083-048]spoke as if desirous of saying for her something she found a difficulty

[083-049]in saying for herself.

[083-050]‘Jasper, it is so hard for me! So hard for me! How could I help

[083-051]remembering what you told me when I promised to be your wife?’

[083-052]‘I spoke the truth rather brutally,’ he replied, in a kind voice. ‘Let

[083-053]all that be unsaid, forgotten. We are in quite a different position now.

[083-054]Be open with me, Marian; surely you can trust my common sense and good

[083-055]feeling. Put aside all thought of things I have said, and don’t be

[083-056]restrained by any fear lest you should seem to me unwomanly--you can’t

[083-057]be that. What is your own wish? What do you really wish to do, now that

[083-058]there is no uncertainty calling for postponements?’

[083-059]Marian raised her eyes, and was about to speak as she regarded him; but

[083-060]with the first accent her look fell.

[083-061]‘I wish to be your wife.’

[083-062]He waited, thinking and struggling with himself.

[083-063]‘Yet you feel that it would be heartless to take and use this money for

[083-064]our own purposes?’

[083-065]‘What is to become of my parents, Jasper?’

[083-066]‘But then you admit that the fifteen hundred pounds won’t support them.

[083-067]You talk of earning fifty pounds a year for them.’

[083-068]‘Need I cease to write, dear, if we were married? Wouldn’t you let me

[083-069]help them?’

[083-070]‘But, my dear girl, you are taking for granted that we shall have enough

[083-071]for ourselves.’

[083-072]‘I didn’t mean at once,’ she explained hurriedly. ‘In a short time--in

[083-073]a year. You are getting on so well. You will soon have a sufficient

[083-074]income, I am sure.’

[083-075]Jasper rose.

[083-076]‘Let us walk as far as the next seat. Don’t speak. I have something to

[083-077]think about.’

[083-078]Moving on beside him, she slipped her hand softly within his arm; but

[083-079]Jasper did not put the arm into position to support hers, and her hand

[083-080]fell again, dropped suddenly. They reached another bench, and again

[083-081]became seated.

[083-082]‘It comes to this, Marian,’ he said, with portentous gravity. ‘Support

[083-083]you, I could--I have little doubt of that. Maud is provided for, and

[083-084][[dora-milvain|Dora]] can make a living for herself. I could support you and leave you

[083-085]free to give your parents whatever you can earn by your own work. But--’

[083-086]He paused significantly. It was his wish that Marian should supply the

[083-087]consequence, but she did not speak.

[083-088]‘Very well,’ he exclaimed. ‘Then when are we to be married?’

[083-089]The tone of resignation was too marked. Jasper was not good as a

[083-090]comedian; he lacked subtlety.

[083-091]‘We must wait,’ fell from Marian’s lips, in the whisper of despair.

[083-092]‘Wait? But how long?’ he inquired, dispassionately.

[083-093]‘Do you wish to be freed from your engagement, Jasper?’

[083-094]He was not strong enough to reply with a plain ‘Yes,’ and so have done

[083-095]with his perplexities. He feared the girl’s face, and he feared his own

[083-096]subsequent emotions.

[083-097]‘Don’t talk in that way, Marian. The question is simply this: Are we

[083-098]to wait a year, or are we to wait five years? In a year’s time, I shall

[083-099]probably be able to have a small house somewhere out in the suburbs. If

[083-100]we are married then, I shall be happy enough with so good a wife, but my

[083-101]career will take a different shape. I shall just throw overboard certain

[083-102]of my ambitions, and work steadily on at earning a livelihood. If we

[083-103]wait five years, I may perhaps have obtained an editorship, and in that

[083-104]case I should of course have all sorts of better things to offer you.’

[083-105]‘But, dear, why shouldn’t you get an editorship all the same if you are

[083-106]married?’

[083-107]‘I have explained to you several times that [[literary-ambition|success]] of that kind is

[083-108]not compatible with a small house in the suburbs and all the ties of a

[083-109]narrow income. As a bachelor, I can go about freely, make acquaintances,

[083-110]dine at people’s houses, perhaps entertain a useful friend now and

[083-111]then--and so on. It is not merit that succeeds in my line; it is merit

[083-112]plus opportunity. Marrying now, I cut myself off from opportunity,

[083-113]that’s all.’

[083-114]She kept silence.

[083-115]‘Decide my fate for me, Marian,’ he pursued, magnanimously. ‘Let us make

[083-116]up our minds and do what we decide to do. Indeed, it doesn’t concern me

[083-117]so much as yourself. Are you content to lead a simple, unambitious life?

[083-118]Or should you prefer your husband to be a man of some distinction?’

[083-119]‘I know so well what your own wish is. But to wait for years--you will

[083-120]cease to love me, and will only think of me as a hindrance in your way.’

[083-121]‘Well now, when I said five years, of course I took a round number.

[083-122]Three--two might make all the difference to me.’

[083-123]‘Let it be just as you wish. I can bear anything rather than lose your

[083-124]love.’

[083-125]‘You feel, then, that it will decidedly be wise not to marry whilst we

[083-126]are still so poor?’

[083-127]‘Yes; whatever you are convinced of is right.’

[083-128]He again rose, and looked at his watch.

[083-129]‘Jasper, you don’t think that I have behaved selfishly in wishing to let

[083-130]my father have the money?’

[083-131]‘I should have been greatly surprised if you hadn’t wished it. I

[083-132]certainly can’t imagine you saying: “Oh, let them do as best they can!”

[083-133]That would have been selfish with a vengeance.’

[083-134]‘Now you are speaking kindly! Must you go, Jasper?’

[083-135]‘I must indeed. Two hours’ work I am bound to get before seven o’clock.’

[083-136]‘And I have been making it harder for you, by disturbing your mind.’

[083-137]‘No, no; it’s all right now. I shall go at it with all the more energy,

[083-138]now we have come to a decision.’

[083-139]‘Dora has asked me to go to Kew on Sunday. Shall you be able to come,

[083-140]dear?’

[083-141]‘By Jove, no! I have three engagements on Sunday afternoon. I’ll try and

[083-142]keep the Sunday after; I will indeed.’

[083-143]‘What are the engagements?’ she asked timidly.

[083-144]As they walked back towards Gloucester Gate, he answered her question,

[083-145]showing how unpardonable it would be to neglect the people concerned.

[083-146]Then they parted, Jasper going off at a smart pace homewards.

[083-147]Marian turned down Park Street, and proceeded for some distance along

[083-148]Camden Road. The house in which she and her parents now lived was not

[083-149]quite so far away as St Paul’s Crescent; they rented four rooms, one

[083-150]of which had to serve both as [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]]’s sitting-room and for

[083-151]the gatherings of the family at meals. Mrs Yule generally sat in

[083-152]the kitchen, and Marian used her bedroom as a study. [[About]] half the

[083-153]collection of books had been sold; those that remained were still a

[083-154]respectable library, almost covering the walls of the room where their

[083-155]disconsolate possessor passed his mournful days.

[083-156]He could read for a few hours a day, but only large type, and fear of

[083-157]consequences kept him well within the limit of such indulgence laid down

[083-158]by his advisers. Though he inwardly spoke as if his case were hopeless,

[083-159]Yule was very far from having resigned himself to this conviction;

[083-160]indeed, the prospect of spending his latter years in darkness and

[083-161]idleness was too dreadful to him to be accepted so long as a glimmer of

[083-162]hope remained. He saw no reason why the customary operation should not

[083-163]restore him to his old pursuits, and he would have borne it ill if his

[083-164]wife or daughter had ever ceased to oppose the despair which it pleased

[083-165]him to affect.

[083-166]On the whole, he was noticeably patient. At the time of their removal to

[083-167]these lodgings, seeing that Marian prepared herself to share the change

[083-168]as a matter of course, he let her do as she would without comment; nor

[083-169]had he since spoken to her on the subject which had proved so dangerous.

[083-170]Confidence between them there was none; Yule addressed his daughter in

[083-171]a grave, cold, civil tone, and Marian replied gently, but without

[083-172]tenderness. For Mrs Yule the disaster to the family was distinctly a

[083-173]gain; she could not but mourn her husband’s affliction, yet he no longer

[083-174]visited her with the fury or contemptuous impatience of former days.

[083-175]Doubtless the fact of needing so much tendance had its softening

[083-176]influence on the man; he could not turn brutally upon his wife when

[083-177]every hour of the day afforded him some proof of her absolute devotion.

[083-178]Of course his open-air exercise was still unhindered, and in this season

[083-179]of the returning sun he walked a great deal, decidedly to the advantage

[083-180]of his general health--which again must have been a source of benefit

[083-181]to his temper. Of evenings, Marian sometimes read to him. He never

[083-182]requested this, but he did not reject the kindness.

[083-183]This afternoon Marian found her father examining a volume of prints

[083-184]which had been lent him by Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]]. The table was laid for dinner

[083-185](owing to Marian’s frequent absence at the Museum, no change had been

[083-186]made in the order of meals), and Yule sat by the window, his book

[083-187]propped on a second chair. A whiteness in his eyes showed how the

[083-188]disease was progressing, but his face had a more wholesome colour than a

[083-189]year ago.
