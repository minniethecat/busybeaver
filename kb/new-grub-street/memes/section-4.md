# Section 4

[003-001]‘It pleases me to meet you, [[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]],’ he said, as he stretched out

[003-002]his bony hand. ‘Your name reminds me of a paper in [[the-wayside|The Wayside]] a month

[003-003]or two ago, which you will perhaps allow a veteran to say was not ill

[003-004]done.’

[003-005]‘I am grateful to you for noticing it,’ replied Jasper.

[003-006]There was positively a touch of visible warmth upon his cheek. The

[003-007]allusion had come so unexpectedly that it caused him keen pleasure.

[003-008][[alfred-yule|Mr Yule]] seated himself awkwardly, crossed his legs, and began to stroke

[003-009]the back of his left hand, which lay on his knee. He seemed to have

[003-010]nothing more to say at present, and allowed Miss Harrow and the girls to

[003-011]support conversation. Jasper listened with a smile for a minute or two,

[003-012]then he addressed the veteran.’Have you seen [[the-study|The Study]] this week, Mr

[003-013]Yule?’

[003-014]‘Yes.’

[003-015]‘Did you notice that it contains a very favourable review of a novel

[003-016]which was tremendously abused in the same columns three weeks ago?’

[003-017]Mr Yule started, but Jasper could perceive at once that his emotion was

[003-018]not disagreeable.

[003-019]‘You don’t say so.’

[003-020]‘Yes. The novel is Miss Hawk’s “On the Boards.” How will the editor get

[003-021]out of this?’

[003-022]‘H’m! Of course Mr [[fadge|Fadge]] is not immediately responsible; but it’ll be

[003-023]unpleasant for him, decidedly unpleasant.’ He smiled grimly. ‘You hear

[003-024]this, [[marian-yule|Marian]]?’

[003-025]‘How is it explained, father?’

[003-026]‘May be accident, of course; but--well, there’s no knowing. I think

[003-027]it very likely this will be the end of Mr Fadge’s tenure of office.

[003-028]Rackett, the proprietor, only wants a plausible excuse for making a

[003-029]change. The paper has been going downhill for the last year; I know of

[003-030]two [[publishing-trade|publishing houses]] who have withdrawn their advertising from it, and

[003-031]who never send their books for review. Everyone foresaw that kind of

[003-032]thing from the day Mr Fadge became editor. The tone of his paragraphs

[003-033]has been detestable. Two reviews of the same novel, eh? And

[003-034]diametrically opposed? Ha! Ha!’

[003-035]Gradually he had passed from quiet appreciation of the joke to

[003-036]undisguised mirth and pleasure. His utterance of the name ‘Mr Fadge’

[003-037]sufficiently intimated that he had some cause of personal discontent

[003-038]with the editor of The Study.

[003-039]‘The author,’ remarked Milvain, ‘ought to make a good thing out of

[003-040]this.’

[003-041]‘Will, no doubt. Ought to write at once to the papers, calling attention

[003-042]to this sample of critical impartiality. Ha! ha!’

[003-043]He rose and went to the window, where for several minutes he stood

[003-044]gazing at vacancy, the same grim smile still on his face. Jasper in the

[003-045]meantime amused the ladies (his sisters had heard him on the subject

[003-046]already) with a description of the two antagonistic notices. But he

[003-047]did not trust himself to express so freely as he had done at home his

[003-048]opinion of [[reviewing-criticism|reviewing]] in general; it was more than probable that both

[003-049]Yule and his daughter did a good deal of such work.

[003-050]‘Suppose we go into the garden,’ suggested Miss Harrow, presently. ‘It

[003-051]seems a shame to sit indoors on such a lovely afternoon.’

[003-052]Hitherto there had been no mention of the master of the house. But Mr

[003-053]Yule now remarked to Jasper:

[003-054]‘My brother would be glad if you would come and have a word with him. He

[003-055]isn’t quite well enough to leave his room to-day.’

[003-056]So, as the ladies went gardenwards, Jasper followed the man of letters

[003-057]upstairs to a room on the first floor. Here, in a deep cane chair, which

[003-058]was placed by the open window, sat [[john-yule|John Yule]]. He was completely dressed,

[003-059]save that instead of coat he wore a dressing-gown. The facial

[003-060]likeness between him and his brother was very strong, but John’s

[003-061]would universally have been judged the finer countenance; illness

[003-062]notwithstanding, he had a complexion which contrasted in its pure colour

[003-063]with Alfred’s parchmenty skin, and there was more finish about his

[003-064]features. His abundant hair was reddish, his long moustache and trimmed

[003-065]beard a lighter shade of the same hue.

[003-066]‘So you too are in league with the doctors,’ was his bluff greeting,

[003-067]as he held a hand to the young man and inspected him with a look of

[003-068]slighting good-nature.

[003-069]‘Well, that certainly is one way of regarding the literary profession,’

[003-070]admitted Jasper, who had heard enough of John’s way of thinking to

[003-071]understand the remark.

[003-072]‘A young fellow with all the world before him, too. Hang it, Mr Milvain,

[003-073]is there no less pernicious work you can turn your hand to?’

[003-074]‘I’m afraid not, Mr Yule. After all, you know, you must be held in a

[003-075]measure responsible for my depravity.’

[003-076]‘How’s that?’

[003-077]‘I understand that you have devoted most of your life to the making

[003-078]of paper. If that article were not so cheap and so abundant, people

[003-079]wouldn’t have so much temptation to scribble.’

[003-080]Alfred Yule uttered a short laugh.

[003-081]‘I think you are cornered, John.’

[003-082]‘I wish,’ answered John, ‘that you were both condemned to write on such

[003-083]paper as I chiefly made; it was a special kind of whitey-brown, used by

[003-084]shopkeepers.’

[003-085]He chuckled inwardly, and at the same time reached out for a box of

[003-086]cigarettes on a table near him. His brother and Jasper each took one as

[003-087]he offered them, and began to smoke.

[003-088]‘You would like to see literary production come entirely to an end?’

[003-089]said Milvain.

[003-090]‘I should like to see the business of literature abolished.’

[003-091]‘There’s a distinction, of course. But, on the whole, I should say that

[003-092]even the business serves a good purpose.’

[003-093]‘What purpose?’

[003-094]‘It helps to spread civilisation.’

[003-095]‘Civilisation!’ exclaimed John, scornfully. ‘What do you mean by

[003-096]civilisation? Do you call it civilising men to make them weak, flabby

[003-097]creatures, with ruined eyes and dyspeptic stomachs? Who is it that

[003-098]reads most of the stuff that’s poured out daily by the ton from the

[003-099]printing-press? Just the men and women who ought to spend their leisure

[003-100]hours in open-air exercise; the people who earn their bread by sedentary

[003-101]pursuits, and who need to live as soon as they are free from the desk

[003-102]or the counter, not to moon over small print. Your Board schools, your

[003-103]popular press, your spread of education! Machinery for ruining the

[003-104]country, that’s what I call it.’

[003-105]‘You have done a good deal, I think, to counteract those influences in

[003-106][[wattleborough|Wattleborough]].’

[003-107]‘I hope so; and if only I had kept the use of my limbs I’d have done a

[003-108]good deal more. I have an idea of offering substantial prizes to men

[003-109]and women engaged in sedentary work who take an oath to abstain from all

[003-110]reading, and keep it for a certain number of years. There’s a good deal

[003-111]more need for that than for abstinence from strong liquor. If I could

[003-112]have had my way I would have revived prize-fighting.’

[003-113]His brother laughed with contemptuous impatience.

[003-114]‘You would doubtless like to see military conscription introduced into

[003-115]England?’ said Jasper.

[003-116]‘Of course I should! You talk of civilising; there’s no such way of

[003-117]civilising the masses of the people as by fixed military service. Before

[003-118]mental training must come training of the body. Go about the Continent,

[003-119]and see the effect of military service on loutish peasants and the

[003-120]lowest classes of town population. Do you know why it isn’t even more

[003-121]successful? Because the damnable education movement interferes. If

[003-122]Germany would shut up her schools and universities for the next quarter

[003-123]of a century and go ahead like blazes with military training there’d be

[003-124]a nation such as the world has never seen. After that, they might begin

[003-125]a little book-teaching again--say an hour and a half a day for everyone

[003-126]above nine years old. Do you suppose, Mr Milvain, that society is going

[003-127]to be reformed by you people who write for [[money-in-literature|money]]? Why, you are the very

[003-128]first [[social-class|class]] that will be swept from the face of the earth as soon as the

[003-129]reformation really begins!’

[003-130]Alfred puffed at his cigarette. His thoughts were occupied with Mr

[003-131]Fadge and The Study. He was considering whether he could aid in bringing

[003-132]public contempt upon that literary organ and its editor. Milvain

[003-133]listened to the elder man’s diatribe with much amusement.

[003-134]‘You, now,’ pursued John, ‘what do you write about?’

[003-135]‘Nothing in particular. I make a salable page or two out of whatever

[003-136]strikes my fancy.’

[003-137]‘Exactly! You don’t even pretend that you’ve got anything to say. You

[003-138]live by inducing people to give themselves mental indigestion--and

[003-139]bodily, too, for that matter.’

[003-140]‘Do you know, Mr Yule, that you have suggested a capital idea to me? If

[003-141]I were to take up your views, I think it isn’t at all unlikely that

[003-142]I might make a good thing of writing against writing. It should be my

[003-143]literary specialty to rail against literature. The reading public should

[003-144]pay me for telling them that they oughtn’t to read. I must think it

[003-145]over.’

[003-146]‘Carlyle has anticipated you,’ threw in Alfred.

[003-147]‘Yes, but in an antiquated way. I would base my polemic on the newest

[003-148]philosophy.’

[003-149]He developed the idea facetiously, whilst John regarded him as he might

[003-150]have watched a performing monkey.

[003-151]‘There again! your new philosophy!’ exclaimed the invalid. ‘Why, it

[003-152]isn’t even wholesome stuff, the kind of reading that most of you

[003-153]force on the public. Now there’s the man who has married one of my

[003-154]nieces--poor lass! [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], his name is. You know him, I dare say.

[003-155]Just for curiosity I had a look at one of his books; it was called “The

[003-156]Optimist.” Of all the morbid trash I ever saw, that beat everything. I

[003-157]thought of writing him a letter, advising a couple of anti-bilious pills

[003-158]before bedtime for a few weeks.’

[003-159]Jasper glanced at Alfred Yule, who wore a look of indifference.

[003-160]‘That man deserves penal servitude in my opinion,’ pursued John. ‘I’m

[003-161]not sure that it isn’t my duty to offer him a couple of hundred a year

[003-162]on condition that he writes no more.’

[003-163]Milvain, with a clear vision of his friend in [[london|London]], burst into

[003-164]laughter. But at that point Alfred rose from his chair.

[003-165]‘Shall we rejoin the ladies?’ he said, with a certain pedantry

[003-166]of phrase and manner which often characterised him.

[003-167]‘Think over your ways whilst you’re still young,’ said John as he shook

[003-168]hands with his visitor.

[003-169]‘Your brother speaks quite seriously, I suppose?’ Jasper remarked when

[003-170]he was in the garden with Alfred.

[003-171]‘I think so. It’s amusing now and then, but gets rather tiresome when

[003-172]you hear it often. By-the-bye, you are not personally acquainted with Mr

[003-173]Fadge?’

[003-174]‘I didn’t even know his name until you mentioned it.’

[003-175]‘The most malicious man in the literary world. There’s no

[003-176]uncharitableness in feeling a certain pleasure when he gets into a

[003-177]scrape. I could tell you incredible stories about him; but that kind of

[003-178]thing is probably as little to your taste as it is to mine.’

[003-179]Miss Harrow and her companions, having caught sight of the pair, came

[003-180]towards them. Tea was to be brought out into the garden.

[003-181]‘So you can sit with us and smoke, if you like,’ said Miss Harrow to

[003-182]Alfred. ‘You are never quite at your ease, I think, without a pipe.’

[003-183]But the man of letters was too preoccupied for society. In a few minutes

[003-184]he begged that the ladies would excuse his withdrawing; he had two or

[003-185]three letters to write before post-time, which was early at Finden.

[003-186]Jasper, relieved by the veteran’s departure, began at once to make

[003-187]himself very agreeable company. When he chose to lay aside the topic of

[003-188]his own difficulties and ambitions, he could converse with a spontaneous

[003-189]gaiety which readily won the good-will of listeners. Naturally

[003-190]he addressed himself very often to Marian Yule, whose attention

[003-191]complimented him. She said little, and evidently was at no time a free

[003-192]talker, but the smile on her face indicated a mood of quiet enjoyment.

[003-193]When her eyes wandered, it was to rest on the beauties of the garden,

[003-194]the moving patches of golden sunshine, the forms of gleaming cloud.

[003-195]Jasper liked to observe her as she turned her head: there seemed to him

[003-196]a particular grace in the movement; her head and neck were admirably

[003-197]formed, and the short hair drew attention to this.
