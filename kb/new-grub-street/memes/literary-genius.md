# Literary Genius

"Putting aside men of genius, who may succeed by mere cosmic force" — Jasper Milvain's famous concession (P02-183) is the novel's key to understanding genius as a category. Genius is the exception that proves the rule: the one kind of writer who can ignore the market and still succeed. But the novel systematically undermines this exception, suggesting that in the modern Grub Street, even genius cannot survive without commercial accommodation.

## The Exception Clause

Milvain's rhetorical strategy is revealing. He invokes genius only to dismiss it — "putting aside men of genius" — and then proceeds to describe a literary world in which genius is irrelevant. His "skilful tradesman" model of authorship has no place for the divinely gifted outsider. The phrase "mere cosmic force" is deliberately reductive: genius is reduced to a natural phenomenon, like weather, rather than a human achievement.

## Reardon as Failed Genius

Edwin Reardon is the novel's test case for the genius exception. He has genuine literary talent — his early work showed real promise, and even his commercial failure *The Optimist* has artistic merit. But his talent is not "cosmic force" — it is fragile, dependent on conditions (financial security, domestic peace, intellectual stimulation) that the market does not provide. Reardon's failure suggests that even genuine literary gifts cannot survive the grinding pressures of commercial authorship.

The novel notes that Reardon had a moment "when as yet no mission of literary realism had been imposed upon him, and when his passions were still soothed by natural" feeling (087-022) — a prelapsarian state before the demands of the market drove him into artistic paralysis. Genius, in this account, is not an innate force but a fragile capacity that can be destroyed by circumstance.

## Biffen and the Genius of Refusal

Harold Biffen may be the novel's truest genius — not because of commercial or critical success but because his artistic vision is genuinely original. His "absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent" (024-171) is a concept no previous writer had explored. But Biffen's genius brings him starvation, not success. The novel's implicit argument is devastating: in the modern literary marketplace, artistic originality is a liability.

## The Disenchantment of Genius

Gissing demystifies genius. It is not a magical exemption from economic reality but merely another variable in the commercial equation — and often a disadvantageous one. The novel's world has no room for the Romantic conception of the inspired artist; that figure belongs to "Sam Johnson's Grub Street" (P02-195), not the telegraph-connected marketplace of the 1880s.
