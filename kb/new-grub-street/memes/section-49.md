# Section 49

[048-001]‘Well, have you enjoyed yourself?’

[048-002]‘Oh yes!’ she replied, carelessly. ‘This letter for me? What has Mrs

[048-003]Haynes got to say, I wonder?’

[048-004]She opened the envelope, and began to glance hurriedly over the sheet of

[048-005]paper. Then her face changed.

[048-006]‘What do you think? [[alfred-yule|Mr Yule]] is dead!’

[048-007][[dora-milvain|Dora]] uttered an exclamation; [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] displayed the keenest interest.

[048-008]‘He died yesterday--no, it would be the day before yesterday. He had a

[048-009]fit of some kind at a public meeting, was taken to the hospital because

[048-010]it was nearest, and died in a few hours. So that has come, at last! Now

[048-011]what’ll be the result of it, I wonder?’

[048-012]‘When shall you be seeing [[marian-yule|Marian]]?’ asked her brother.

[048-013]‘She might come to-morrow evening.’

[048-014]‘But won’t she go to the funeral?’ suggested Dora.

[048-015]‘Perhaps; there’s no saying. I suppose her father will, at all events.

[048-016]The day before yesterday? Then the funeral will be on Saturday, I should

[048-017]think.’

[048-018]‘Ought I to write to Marian?’ asked Dora.

[048-019]‘No; I wouldn’t,’ was Jasper’s reply. ‘Better wait till she lets you

[048-020]hear. That’s sure to be soon. She may have gone to [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] this

[048-021]afternoon, or be going to-morrow morning.’

[048-022]The letter from Mrs Haynes was passed from hand to hand. ‘Everybody

[048-023]feels sure,’ it said, ‘that a great deal of his [[money-in-literature|money]] will be left for

[048-024]public purposes. The ground for the park being already purchased, he is

[048-025]sure to have made provision for carrying out his plans connected with

[048-026]it. But I hope your friends in [[london|London]] may benefit.’

[048-027]It was some time before Jasper could put an end to the speculative

[048-028]conversation and betake himself homewards. And even on getting back to

[048-029]his lodgings he was little disposed to go to bed. This event of John

[048-030]Yule’s death had been constantly in his mind, but there was always a

[048-031]fear that it might not happen for long enough; the sudden announcement

[048-032]excited him almost as much as if he were a relative of the deceased.

[048-033]‘Confound his public purposes!’ was the thought upon which he at length

[048-034]slept.

[048-035]CHAPTER XXI. MR YULE LEAVES TOWN

[048-036]Since the domestic incidents connected with that unpleasant review in

[048-037][[the-current|The Current]], the relations between Alfred Yule and his daughter had

[048-038]suffered a permanent change, though not in a degree noticeable by any

[048-039]one but the two concerned. To all appearances, they worked together and

[048-040]conversed very much as they had been wont to do; but Marian was made

[048-041]to feel in many subtle ways that her father no longer had complete

[048-042]confidence in her, no longer took the same pleasure as formerly in the

[048-043]skill and conscientiousness of her work, and Yule on his side perceived

[048-044]too clearly that the girl was preoccupied with something other than

[048-045]her old wish to aid and satisfy him, that she had a new life of her own

[048-046]alien to, and in some respects irreconcilable with, the existence

[048-047]in which he desired to confirm her. There was no renewal of open

[048-048]disagreement, but their conversations frequently ended by tacit mutual

[048-049]consent, at a point which threatened divergence; and in Yule’s case

[048-050]every such warning was a cause of intense irritation. He feared to

[048-051]provoke Marian, and this fear was again a torture to his pride.

[048-052]Beyond the fact that his daughter was in constant communication with

[048-053]the Miss Milvains, he knew, and could discover, nothing of the terms on

[048-054]which she stood with the girls’ brother, and this ignorance was harder

[048-055]to bear than full assurance of a disagreeable fact would have been. That

[048-056]a man like Jasper Milvain, whose name was every now and then forced

[048-057]upon his notice as a rising periodicalist and a faithful henchman of

[048-058]the unspeakable [[fadge|Fadge]]--that a young fellow of such excellent prospects

[048-059]should seriously attach himself to a girl like Marian seemed to him

[048-060]highly improbable, save, indeed, for the one consideration, that

[048-061]Milvain, who assuredly had a very keen eye to chances, might regard the

[048-062]girl as a niece of old [[john-yule|John Yule]], and therefore worth holding in view

[048-063]until it was decided whether or not she would benefit by her uncle’s

[048-064]decease. Fixed in his antipathy to the young man, he would not allow

[048-065]himself to admit any but a base motive on Milvain’s side, if, indeed,

[048-066]Marian and Jasper were more to each other than slight acquaintances; and

[048-067]he persuaded himself that anxiety for the girl’s welfare was at least

[048-068]as strong a motive with him as mere prejudice against the ally of Fadge,

[048-069]and, it might be, the reviewer of ‘English Prose.’ Milvain was quite

[048-070]capable of playing fast and loose with a girl, and Marian, owing to the

[048-071]peculiar circumstances of her position, would easily be misled by the

[048-072]pretence of a clever speculator.

[048-073]That she had never spoken again about the review in The Current might

[048-074]receive several explanations. Perhaps she had not been able to convince

[048-075]herself either for or against Milvain’s authorship; perhaps she had

[048-076]reason to suspect that the young man was the author; perhaps she merely

[048-077]shrank from reviving a discussion in which she might betray what she

[048-078]desired to keep secret. This last was the truth. Finding that her father

[048-079]did not recur to the subject, Marian concluded that he had found himself

[048-080]to be misinformed. But Yule, though he heard the original rumour denied

[048-081]by people whom in other matters he would have trusted, would not lay

[048-082]aside the doubt that flattered his prejudices. If Milvain were not the

[048-083]writer of the review, he very well might have been; and what certainty

[048-084]could be arrived at in matters of literary gossip?

[048-085]There was an element of jealousy in the father’s feeling. If he did not

[048-086]love Marian with all the warmth of which a parent is capable, at least

[048-087]he had more affection for her than for any other person, and of this he

[048-088]became strongly aware now that the girl seemed to be turning from him.

[048-089]If he lost Marian, he would indeed be a lonely man, for he considered

[048-090]his wife of no account.

[048-091]Intellectually again, he demanded an entire allegiance from his

[048-092]daughter; he could not bear to think that her zeal on his behalf was

[048-093]diminishing, that perhaps she was beginning to regard his work as futile

[048-094]and antiquated in comparison with that of the new generation. Yet this

[048-095]must needs be the result of frequent intercourse with such a man as

[048-096]Milvain. It seemed to him that he remarked it in her speech and manner,

[048-097]and at times he with difficulty restrained himself from a reproach or a

[048-098]sarcasm which would have led to trouble.

[048-099]Had he been in the habit of dealing harshly with Marian, as with her

[048-100]mother, of course his position would have been simpler. But he had

[048-101]always respected her, and he feared to lose that measure of respect with

[048-102]which she repaid him. Already he had suffered in her esteem, perhaps

[048-103]more than he liked to think, and the increasing embitterment of his

[048-104]temper kept him always in danger of the conflict he dreaded. Marian was

[048-105]not like her mother; she could not submit to tyrannous usage. Warned

[048-106]of that, he did his utmost to avoid an outbreak of discord, constantly

[048-107]hoping that he might come to understand his daughter’s position, and

[048-108]perhaps discover that his greatest fear was unfounded.

[048-109]Twice in the course of the summer he inquired of his wife whether she

[048-110]knew anything about the Milvains. But Mrs Yule was not in Marian’s

[048-111]confidence.

[048-112]‘I only know that she goes to see the young ladies, and that they do

[048-113]writing of some kind.’

[048-114]‘She never even mentions their brother to you?’

[048-115]‘Never. I haven’t heard his name from her since she told me the Miss

[048-116]Milvains weren’t coming here again.’

[048-117]He was not sorry that Marian had taken the decision to keep her friends

[048-118]away from St Paul’s Crescent, for it saved him a recurring annoyance;

[048-119]but, on the other hand, if they had continued to come, he would not

[048-120]have been thus completely in the dark as to her intercourse with Jasper;

[048-121]scraps of information must now and then have been gathered by his wife

[048-122]from the girls’ talk.

[048-123]Throughout the month of July he suffered much from his wonted bilious

[048-124]attacks, and Mrs Yule had to endure a double share of his ill-temper,

[048-125]that which was naturally directed against her, and that of which Marian

[048-126]was the cause. In August things were slightly better; but with the

[048-127]return to labour came a renewal of Yule’s sullenness and savageness.

[048-128]Sundry pieces of ill-luck of a professional kind--warnings, as he too

[048-129]well understood, that it was growing more and more difficult for him

[048-130]to hold his own against the new writers--exasperated his quarrel with

[048-131]destiny. The gloom of a cold and stormy September was doubly wretched

[048-132]in that house on the far borders of [[camden-town|Camden Town]], but in October the sun

[048-133]reappeared and it seemed to mollify the literary man’s mood. Just when

[048-134]Mrs Yule and Marian began to hope that this long distemper must surely

[048-135]come to an end, there befell an incident which, at the best of times,

[048-136]would have occasioned misery, and which in the present juncture proved

[048-137]disastrous.

[048-138]It was one morning about eleven. Yule was in his study; Marian was at

[048-139]the Museum; Mrs Yule had gone shopping. There came a sharp knock at

[048-140]the front door, and the servant, on opening, was confronted with a

[048-141]decently-dressed woman, who asked in a peremptory voice if Mrs Yule was

[048-142]at home.

[048-143]‘No? Then is Mr Yule?’

[048-144]‘Yes, mum, but I’m afraid he’s busy.’

[048-145]‘I don’t care, I must see him. Say that Mrs Goby wants to see him at

[048-146]once.’

[048-147]The servant, not without apprehensions, delivered this message at the

[048-148]door of the study.

[048-149]‘Mrs Goby? Who is Mrs Goby?’ exclaimed the man of letters, irate at the

[048-150]disturbance.

[048-151]There sounded an answer out of the passage, for the visitor had followed

[048-152]close.

[048-153]‘I am Mrs Goby, of the ‘Olloway Road, wife of Mr C. O. Goby,

[048-154]‘aberdasher. I just want to speak to you, Mr Yule, if you please, seeing

[048-155]that Mrs Yule isn’t in.’

[048-156]Yule started up in fury, and stared at the woman, to whom the servant

[048-157]had reluctantly given place.

[048-158]‘What business can you have with me? If you wish to see Mrs Yule, come

[048-159]again when she is at home.’

[048-160]‘No, Mr Yule, I will not come again!’ cried the woman, red in the face.

[048-161]‘I thought I might have had respectable treatment here, at all events;

[048-162]but I see you’re pretty much like your relations in the way of behaving

[048-163]to people, though you do wear better clothes, and--I s’pose--call

[048-164]yourself a gentleman. I won’t come again, and you shall just hear what

[048-165]I’ve got to say.

[048-166]She closed the door violently, and stood in an attitude of robust

[048-167]defiance.

[048-168]‘What’s all this about?’ asked the enraged author, overcoming an impulse

[048-169]to take Mrs Goby by the shoulders and throw her out--though he might

[048-170]have found some difficulty in achieving this feat. ‘Who are you? And why

[048-171]do you come here with your brawling?’

[048-172]‘I’m the respectable wife of a respectable man--that’s who I am, Mr

[048-173]Yule, if you want to know. And I always thought Mrs Yule was the same,

[048-174]from the dealings we’ve had with her at the shop, though not knowing any

[048-175]more of her, it’s true, except that she lived in St Paul’s Crezzent.

[048-176]And so she may be respectable, though I can’t say as her husband behaves

[048-177]himself very much like what he pretends to be. But I can’t say as much

[048-178]for her relations in Perker Street, ‘Olloway, which I s’pose they’re

[048-179]your relations as well, at least by [[literary-marriage|marriage]]. And if they think they’re

[048-180]going to insult me, and use their blackguard tongues--’

[048-181]‘What are you talking about?’ shouted Yule, who was driven to frenzy by

[048-182]the mention of his wife’s humble family. ‘What have I to do with these

[048-183]people?’
