# Section 73

[072-001]He paused, looking at [[alfred-yule|Yule]] in a strange way.

[072-002]‘What happened then?’

[072-003]‘You probably don’t remember a railway accident that took place near

[072-004]Crewe in that year--it was 1869? I and my wife and child were alone in

[072-005]a carriage that was splintered. One moment I was talking with them, in

[072-006]fairly good spirits, and my wife was laughing at something I had said;

[072-007]the next, there were two crushed, bleeding bodies at my feet. I had a

[072-008]broken arm, that was all. Well, they were killed on the instant; they

[072-009]didn’t suffer. That has been my one consolation.’

[072-010]Yule kept the silence of sympathy.

[072-011]‘I was in a lunatic asylum for more than a year after that,’ continued

[072-012]the man. ‘Unhappily, I didn’t lose my senses at the moment; it took two

[072-013]or three weeks to bring me to that pass. But I recovered, and there has

[072-014]been no return of the disease. Don’t suppose that I am still of unsound

[072-015]mind. There can be little doubt that [[literary-poverty|poverty]] will bring me to that again

[072-016]in the end; but as yet I am perfectly sane. I have supported myself in

[072-017]various ways.

[072-018]No, I don’t drink; I see the question in your face. But I am physically

[072-019]weak, and, to quote Mrs Gummidge, “things go contrary with me.” There’s

[072-020]no use lamenting; this breakfast has helped me on, and I feel in much

[072-021]better spirits.’

[072-022]‘Your surgical knowledge is no use to you?’

[072-023]The other shook his head and sighed.

[072-024]‘Did you ever give any special attention to diseases of the eyes?’

[072-025]‘Special, no. But of course I had some acquaintance with the subject.’

[072-026]‘Could you tell by examination whether a man was threatened with

[072-027]cataract, or anything of that kind?’

[072-028]‘I think I could.’

[072-029]‘I am speaking of myself.’

[072-030]The stranger made a close scrutiny of Yule’s face, and asked certain

[072-031]questions with reference to his visual sensations.

[072-032]‘I hardly like to propose it,’ he said at length, ‘but if you were

[072-033]willing to accompany me to a very poor room that I have not far from

[072-034]here, I could make the examination formally.’

[072-035]‘I will go with you.’

[072-036]They turned away from the stall, and the ex-surgeon led into a

[072-037]by-street. Yule wondered at himself for caring to seek such a singular

[072-038]consultation, but he had a pressing desire to hear some opinion as to

[072-039]the state of his eyes. Whatever the stranger might tell him, he would

[072-040]afterwards have recourse to a man of recognised standing; but just now

[072-041]companionship of any kind was welcome, and the poor hungry fellow, with

[072-042]his dolorous life-story, had made appeal to his sympathies. To give

[072-043][[money-in-literature|money]] under guise of a fee would be better than merely offering alms.

[072-044]‘This is the house,’ said his guide, pausing at a dirty door. ‘It isn’t

[072-045]inviting, but the people are honest, so far as I know. My room is at the

[072-046]top.’

[072-047]‘Lead on,’ answered Yule.

[072-048]In the room they entered was nothing noticeable; it was only the poorest

[072-049]possible kind of bed-chamber, or all but the poorest possible. Daylight

[072-050]had now succeeded to dawn, yet the first thing the stranger did was to

[072-051]strike a match and light a candle.

[072-052]‘Will you kindly place yourself with your back to the window?’ he

[072-053]said. ‘I am going to apply what is called the catoptric test. You have

[072-054]probably heard of it?’

[072-055]‘My ignorance of scientific matters is fathomless.’

[072-056]The other smiled, and at once offered a simple explanation of the term.

[072-057]By the appearance of the candle as it reflected itself in the patient’s

[072-058]eye it was possible, he said, to decide whether cataract had taken hold

[072-059]upon the organ.

[072-060]For a minute or two he conducted his experiment carefully, and Yule was

[072-061]at no loss to read the result upon his face.

[072-062]‘How long have you suspected that something was wrong?’ the surgeon

[072-063]asked, as he put down the candle.

[072-064]‘For several months.’

[072-065]‘You haven’t consulted anyone?’

[072-066]‘No one. I have kept putting it off. Just tell me what you have

[072-067]discovered.’

[072-068]‘The back of the right lens is affected beyond a doubt.’

[072-069]‘That means, I take it, that before very long I shall be practically

[072-070]blind?’

[072-071]‘I don’t like to speak with an air of authority. After all, I am only a

[072-072]surgeon who has bungled himself into pauperdom. You must see a competent

[072-073]man; that much I can tell you in all earnestness.

[072-074]Do you use your eyes much?’

[072-075]‘Fourteen hours a day, that’s all.’

[072-076]‘H’m! You are a literary man, I think?’

[072-077]‘I am. My name is Alfred Yule.’

[072-078]He had some faint hope that the name might be recognised; that would

[072-079]have gone far, for the moment, to counteract his trouble. But not even

[072-080]this poor satisfaction was to be granted him; to his hearer the name

[072-081]evidently conveyed nothing.

[072-082]‘See a competent man, Mr Yule. Science has advanced rapidly since the

[072-083]days when I was a student; I am only able to assure you of the existence

[072-084]of disease.’

[072-085]They talked for half an hour, until both were shaking with cold. Then

[072-086]Yule thrust his hand into his pocket.

[072-087]‘You will of course allow me to offer such return as I am able,’ he

[072-088]said. ‘The information isn’t pleasant, but I am glad to have it.’

[072-089]He laid five shillings on the chest of drawers--there was no table. The

[072-090]stranger expressed his gratitude.

[072-091]‘My name is Duke,’ he said, ‘and I was christened Victor--possibly

[072-092]because I was doomed to defeat in life. I wish you could have associated

[072-093]the memory of me with happier circumstances.’

[072-094]They shook hands, and Yule quitted the house.

[072-095]He came out again by [[camden-town|Camden Town]] station. The coffee-stall had

[072-096]disappeared; the traffic of the great highway was growing uproarious.

[072-097]Among all the strugglers for existence who rushed this way and that,

[072-098]Alfred Yule felt himself a man chosen for fate’s heaviest infliction. He

[072-099]never questioned the accuracy of the stranger’s judgment, and he hoped

[072-100]for no mitigation of the doom it threatened. His life was over--and

[072-101]wasted.

[072-102]He might as well go home, and take his place meekly by the fireside.

[072-103]He was beaten. Soon to be a useless old man, a burden and annoyance to

[072-104]whosoever had pity on him.

[072-105]It was a curious effect of the imagination that since coming into the

[072-106]open air again his eyesight seemed to be far worse than before. He

[072-107]irritated his nerves of vision by incessant tests, closing first one eye

[072-108]then the other, comparing his view of nearer objects with the appearance

[072-109]of others more remote, fancying an occasional pain--which could have had

[072-110]no connection with his disease. The literary projects which had stirred

[072-111]so actively in his mind twelve hours ago were become an insubstantial

[072-112]memory; to the one crushing blow had succeeded a second, which was

[072-113]fatal. He could hardly recall what special piece of work he had been

[072-114]engaged upon last night. His thoughts were such as if actual blindness

[072-115]had really fallen upon him.

[072-116]At half-past eight he entered the house. Mrs Yule was standing at the

[072-117]foot of the stairs; she looked at him, then turned away towards the

[072-118]kitchen. He went upstairs. On coming down again he found breakfast ready

[072-119]as usual, and seated himself at the table. Two letters waited for him

[072-120]there; he opened them.

[072-121]When Mrs Yule came into the room a few moments later she was astonished

[072-122]by a burst of loud, mocking laughter from her husband, excited, as it

[072-123]appeared, by something he was reading.

[072-124]‘Is [[marian-yule|Marian]] up?’ he asked, turning to her.

[072-125]‘Yes.’

[072-126]‘She is not coming to breakfast?’

[072-127]‘No.’

[072-128]‘Then just take that letter to her, and ask her to read it.’

[072-129]Mrs Yule ascended to her daughter’s bedroom. She knocked, was bidden

[072-130]enter, and found Marian packing clothes in a trunk. The girl looked as

[072-131]if she had been up all night; her eyes bore the traces of much weeping.

[072-132]‘He has come back, dear,’ said Mrs Yule, in the low voice of

[072-133]apprehension, ‘and he says you are to read this letter.’

[072-134]Marian took the sheet, unfolded it, and read. As soon as she had reached

[072-135]the end she looked wildly at her mother, seemed to endeavour vainly to

[072-136]speak, then fell to the floor in unconsciousness. The mother was only

[072-137]just able to break the violence of her fall. Having snatched a pillow

[072-138]and placed it beneath Marian’s head, she rushed to the door and called

[072-139]loudly for her husband, who in a moment appeared.

[072-140]‘What is it?’ she cried to him. ‘Look, she has fallen down in a faint.

[072-141]Why are you treating her like this?’

[072-142]‘Attend to her,’ Yule replied roughly. ‘I suppose you know better than I

[072-143]do what to do when a person faints.’

[072-144]The swoon lasted for several minutes.

[072-145]‘What’s in the letter?’ asked Mrs Yule whilst chafing the lifeless

[072-146]hands.

[072-147]‘Her money’s lost. The people who were to pay it have just failed.’

[072-148]‘She won’t get anything?’

[072-149]‘Most likely nothing at all.’

[072-150]The letter was a private communication from one of [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s

[072-151]executors. It seemed likely that the demand upon Turberville & Co. for

[072-152]an account of the deceased partner’s share in their business had helped

[072-153]to bring about a crisis in affairs that were already unstable. Something

[072-154]might be recovered in the legal proceedings that would result, but there

[072-155]were circumstances which made the outlook very doubtful.

[072-156]As Marian came to herself her father left the room. An hour afterwards

[072-157]Mrs Yule summoned him again to the girl’s chamber; he went, and found

[072-158]Marian lying on the bed, looking like one who had been long ill.

[072-159]‘I wish to ask you a few questions,’ she said, without raising herself.

[072-160]‘Must my legacy necessarily be paid out of that investment?’

[072-161]‘It must. Those are the terms of the will.’

[072-162]‘If nothing can be recovered from those people, I have no remedy?’

[072-163]‘None whatever that I can see.’

[072-164]‘But when a firm is bankrupt they generally pay some portion of their

[072-165]debts?’

[072-166]‘Sometimes. I know nothing of the case.’

[072-167]‘This of course happens to me,’ Marian said, with intense bitterness.

[072-168]‘None of the other legatees will suffer, I suppose?’

[072-169]‘Someone must, but to a very small extent.’

[072-170]‘Of course. When shall I have direct information?’

[072-171]‘You can write to Mr Holden; you have his address.’

[072-172]‘Thank you. That’s all.’

[072-173]He was dismissed, and went quietly away.

[072-174]PART FIVE

[072-175]CHAPTER XXX. WAITING ON DESTINY

[072-176]Throughout the day Marian kept her room. Her intention to leave the

[072-177]house was, of course, abandoned; she was the prisoner of fate. Mrs Yule

[072-178]would have tended her with unremitting devotion, but the girl desired to

[072-179]be alone. At times she lay in silent anguish; frequently her tears broke

[072-180]forth, and she sobbed until weariness overcame her. In the afternoon she

[072-181]wrote a letter to Mr Holden, begging that she might be kept constantly

[072-182]acquainted with the progress of things.

[072-183]At five her mother brought tea.

[072-184]‘Wouldn’t it be better if you went to bed now, Marian?’ she suggested.

[072-185]‘To bed? But I am going out in an hour or two.’

[072-186]‘Oh, you can’t, dear! It’s so bitterly cold. It wouldn’t be good for

[072-187]you.’

[072-188]‘I have to go out, mother, so we won’t speak of it.’

[072-189]It was not safe to reply. Mrs Yule sat down, and watched the girl raise

[072-190]the cup to her mouth with trembling hand.

[072-191]‘This won’t make any difference to you--in the end, my darling,’ the

[072-192]mother ventured to say at length, alluding for the first time to the

[072-193]effect of the catastrophe on Marian’s immediate prospects.

[072-194]‘Of course not,’ was the reply, in a tone of self-persuasion.

[072-195]‘[[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]] is sure to have plenty of money before long.’

[072-196]‘Yes.’

[072-197]‘You feel much better now, don’t you?’

[072-198]‘Much. I am quite well again.’

[072-199]At seven, Marian went out. Finding herself weaker than she had thought,

[072-200]she stopped an empty cab that presently passed her, and so drove to

[072-201]the Milvains’ lodgings. In her agitation she inquired for Mr Milvain,

[072-202]instead of for [[dora-milvain|Dora]], as was her habit; it mattered very little, for

[072-203]the landlady and her servants were of course under no misconception

[072-204]regarding this young lady’s visits.
