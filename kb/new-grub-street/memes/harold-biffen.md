# Harold Biffen

The novel's purest artistic conscience — a writer whose commitment to literary realism is absolute, and whose fate demonstrates that absolute artistic integrity is fatal in the modern literary marketplace. Biffen's story is the novel's most uncompromising argument about the cost of art in a commercial world.

## Character Overview

Biffen is Reardon's closest friend and intellectual companion. He shares Reardon's commitment to serious fiction but is even more uncompromising. Where Reardon at least attempts to produce commercially viable work, Biffen refuses every compromise. His artistic credo — "absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent" (024-171) — is genuinely original and intellectually serious. It also guarantees his commercial failure.

## Artistic Vision

Biffen's realism is a project of radical ordinariness. He wants to depict the lives of people who are neither heroic nor villainous — the "ignobly decent" who have never been treated as worthy of serious artistic attention. The ambition is significant: he believes he has discovered a genuinely new subject for fiction. "The field, as I understand it, is a new one; I don't know any writer who has treated it" (024-171–024-172).

This artistic seriousness is both Biffen's achievement and his doom. The market has no category for "absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent" — no readership, no advance, no review attention. His commitment to an art that no one wants to buy is a form of martyrdom.

## Poverty and Death

Biffen's poverty is the most extreme in the novel. He lives on almost nothing, and his death — by suicide, implied though not directly stated — is the novel's darkest moment. His fate is the logical endpoint of the argument Gissing has been building: in a literary marketplace that rewards only commercial success, the uncompromising artist has no place.

## Key Relationships

- **Edwin Reardon**: His closest friend. Their conversations about realism, Greek metres, and the literary life are the novel's most sustained depiction of intellectual friendship. They sustain each other through poverty and failure — until poverty kills them both.

## Thematic Significance

Biffen is the novel's most extreme test case. If Reardon represents the difficulty of combining artistic integrity with commercial survival, Biffen represents the impossibility of artistic integrity without any commercial accommodation at all. His death is the novel's final word on the cost of art.
