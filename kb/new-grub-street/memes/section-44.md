# Section 44

[043-001]How clean and sweet everything was! It is often said, by people who are

[043-002]exquisitely ignorant of the matter, that cleanliness is a luxury within

[043-003]reach even of the poorest. Very far from that; only with the utmost

[043-004]difficulty, with wearisome exertion, with harassing sacrifice, can

[043-005]people who are pinched for [[money-in-literature|money]] preserve a moderate purity in their

[043-006]persons and their surroundings. By painful degrees [[amy-reardon|Amy]] had accustomed

[043-007]herself to compromises in this particular which in the early days of her

[043-008]married life would have seemed intensely disagreeable, if not revolting.

[043-009]A housewife who lives in the country, and has but a patch of back

[043-010]garden, or even a good-sized kitchen, can, if she thinks fit, take her

[043-011]place at the wash-tub and relieve her mind on laundry matters; but to

[043-012]the inhabitant of a miniature flat in the heart of [[london|London]] anything of

[043-013]that kind is out of the question.

[043-014]When Amy began to cut down her laundress’s bill, she did it with a

[043-015]sense of degradation. One grows accustomed, however, to such unpleasant

[043-016]necessities, and already she had learnt what was the minimum of

[043-017]expenditure for one who is troubled with a lady’s instincts.

[043-018]No, no; cleanliness is a costly thing, and a troublesome thing when

[043-019]appliances and means have to be improvised. It was, in part, the

[043-020]understanding she had gained of this side of the life of [[literary-poverty|poverty]] that

[043-021]made Amy shrink in dread from the still narrower lodgings to which

[043-022][[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] invited her. She knew how subtly one’s self-respect can be

[043-023]undermined by sordid conditions. The difference between the life of

[043-024]well-to-do educated people and that of the uneducated poor is not

[043-025]greater in visible details than in the minutiae of privacy, and Amy

[043-026]must have submitted to an extraordinary change before it would have been

[043-027]possible for her to live at ease in the circumstances which satisfy a

[043-028]decent working-[[social-class|class]] woman. She was prepared for final parting from her

[043-029]husband rather than try to effect that change in herself.

[043-030]She undressed at leisure, and stretched her limbs in the cold, soft,

[043-031]fragrant bed. A sigh of profound relief escaped her. How good it was to

[043-032]be alone!

[043-033]And in a quarter of an hour she was sleeping as peacefully as the child

[043-034]who shared her room.

[043-035]At breakfast in the morning she showed a bright, almost a happy face. It

[043-036]was long, long since she had enjoyed such a night’s rest, so undisturbed

[043-037]with unwelcome thoughts on the threshold of sleep and on awaking. Her

[043-038]life was perhaps wrecked, but the thought of that did not press upon

[043-039]her; for the present she must enjoy her freedom. It was like a recovery

[043-040]of girlhood. There are few married women who would not, sooner or later,

[043-041]accept with joy the offer of some months of a maidenly liberty. Amy

[043-042]would not allow herself to think that her wedded life was at an end.

[043-043]With a woman’s strange faculty of closing her eyes against facts that

[043-044]do not immediately concern her, she tasted the relief of the present and

[043-045]let the future lie unregarded. Reardon would get out of his difficulties

[043-046]sooner or later; somebody or other would help him; that was the dim

[043-047]background of her agreeable sensations.

[043-048]He suffered, no doubt. But then it was just as well that he should.

[043-049]Suffering would perhaps impel him to effort. When he communicated to her

[043-050]his new address--he could scarcely neglect to do that--she would send a

[043-051]not unfriendly letter, and hint to him that now was his opportunity for

[043-052]writing a book, as good a book as those which formerly issued from his

[043-053]garret-solitude. If he found that literature was in truth a thing of the

[043-054]past with him, then he must exert himself to obtain a position worthy of

[043-055]an educated man. Yes, in this way she would write to him, without a word

[043-056]that could hurt or offend.

[043-057]She ate an excellent breakfast, and made known her enjoyment of it.

[043-058]‘I am so glad!’ replied her mother. ‘You have been getting quite thin

[043-059]and pale.’

[043-060]‘Quite consumptive,’ remarked John, looking up from his newspaper.

[043-061]‘Shall I make arrangements for a daily landau at the livery stables

[043-062]round here?’

[043-063]‘You can if you like,’ replied his sister; ‘it would do both mother and

[043-064]me good, and I have no doubt you could afford it quite well.’

[043-065]‘Oh, indeed! You’re a remarkable young woman, let me tell you.

[043-066]By-the-bye, I suppose your husband is breakfasting on bread and water?’

[043-067]‘I hope not, and I don’t think it very likely.’

[043-068]‘Jack, Jack!’ interposed Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]], softly.

[043-069]Her son resumed his paper, and at the end of the meal rose with an

[043-070]unwonted briskness to make his preparations for departure.

[043-071]CHAPTER XIX. THE PAST REVIVED

[043-072]Nor would it be true to represent Edwin Reardon as rising to the new day

[043-073]wholly disconsolate. He too had slept unusually well, and with returning

[043-074]consciousness the sense of a burden removed was more instant than that

[043-075]of his loss and all the dreary circumstances attaching to it. He had no

[043-076]longer to fear the effects upon Amy of such a grievous change as from

[043-077]their homelike flat to the couple of rooms he had taken in [[islington|Islington]];

[043-078]for the moment, this relief helped him to bear the pain of all that had

[043-079]happened and the uneasiness which troubled him when he reflected that

[043-080]his wife was henceforth a charge to her mother.

[043-081]Of course for the moment only. He had no sooner begun to move about, to

[043-082]prepare his breakfast (amid the relics of last evening’s meal), to think

[043-083]of all the detestable work he had to do before to-morrow night, than his

[043-084]heart sank again. His position was well-nigh as dolorous as that of any

[043-085]man who awoke that morning to the brutal realities of life. If only for

[043-086]the shame of it! How must they be speaking of him, Amy’s relatives,

[043-087]and her friends? A novelist who couldn’t write novels; a husband

[043-088]who couldn’t support his wife and child; a literate who made eager

[043-089]application for illiterate work at paltry wages--how interesting it

[043-090]would all sound in humorous gossip! And what hope had he that things

[043-091]would ever be better with him?

[043-092]Had he done well? Had he done wisely? Would it not have been better to

[043-093]have made that one last effort? There came before him a vision of quiet

[043-094]nooks beneath the Sussex cliffs, of the long lines of green breakers

[043-095]bursting into foam; he heard the wave-music, and tasted the briny

[043-096]freshness of the sea-breeze. Inspiration, after all, would perchance

[043-097]have come to him.

[043-098]If Amy’s love had but been of more enduring quality; if she had

[043-099]strengthened him for this last endeavour with the brave tenderness of

[043-100]an ideal wife! But he had seen such hateful things in her eyes. Her love

[043-101]was dead, and she regarded him as the man who had spoilt her hopes of

[043-102]happiness. It was only for her own sake that she urged him to strive on;

[043-103]let his be the toil, that hers might be the advantage if he succeeded.

[043-104]‘She would be glad if I were dead. She would be glad.’

[043-105]He had the conviction of it. Oh yes, she would shed tears; they come so

[043-106]easily to women. But to have him dead and out of her way; to be saved

[043-107]from her anomalous position; to see once more a chance in life; she

[043-108]would welcome it.

[043-109]But there was no time for brooding. To-day he had to sell all the things

[043-110]that were superfluous, and to make arrangements for the removal of his

[043-111]effects to-morrow. By Wednesday night, in accordance with his agreement,

[043-112]the flat must be free for the new occupier.

[043-113]He had taken only two rooms, and fortunately as things were. Three would

[043-114]have cost more than he was likely to be able to afford for a long time.

[043-115]The rent of the two was to be six-and-sixpence; and how, if Amy had

[043-116]consented to come, could he have met the expenses of their living out

[043-117]of his weekly twenty-five shillings? How could he have pretended to do

[043-118]literary work in such cramped quarters, he who had never been able to

[043-119]write a line save in strict seclusion? In his despair he had faced the

[043-120]impossible. Amy had shown more wisdom, though in a spirit of unkindness.

[043-121]Towards ten o’clock he was leaving the flat to go and find people who

[043-122]would purchase his books and old clothing and other superfluities; but

[043-123]before he could close the door behind him, an approaching step on

[043-124]the stairs caught his attention. He saw the shining silk hat of a

[043-125]well-equipped gentleman. It was [[john-yule|John Yule]].

[043-126]‘Ha! Good-morning!’ John exclaimed, looking up. ‘A minute or two and I

[043-127]should have been too late, I see.’

[043-128]He spoke in quite a friendly way, and, on reaching the landing, shook

[043-129]hands.

[043-130]‘Are you obliged to go at once? Or could I have a word with you?’

[043-131]‘Come in.’

[043-132]They entered the study, which was in some disorder; Reardon made no

[043-133]reference to circumstances, but offered a chair, and seated himself.

[043-134]‘Have a cigarette?’ said Yule, holding out a box of them.

[043-135]‘No, thank you; I don’t smoke so early.’

[043-136]‘Then I’ll light one myself; it always makes talk easier to me. You’re

[043-137]on the point of moving, I suppose?’

[043-138]‘Yes, I am.’

[043-139]Reardon tried to speak in quite a simple way, with no admission of

[043-140]embarrassment. He was not successful, and to his visitor the tone seemed

[043-141]rather offensive.

[043-142]‘I suppose you’ll let Amy know your new address?’

[043-143]‘Certainly. Why should I conceal it?’

[043-144]‘No, no; I didn’t mean to suggest that. But you might be taking it for

[043-145]granted that--that the rupture was final, I thought.’

[043-146]There had never been any intimacy between these two men. Reardon

[043-147]regarded his wife’s brother as rather snobbish and disagreeably selfish;

[043-148]John Yule looked upon the novelist as a prig, and now of late as

[043-149]a shuffling, untrustworthy fellow. It appeared to John that his

[043-150]brother-in-law was assuming a manner wholly unjustifiable, and he had a

[043-151]difficulty in behaving to him with courtesy. Reardon, on the other hand,

[043-152]felt injured by the turn his visitor’s remarks were taking, and began to

[043-153]resent the visit altogether.

[043-154]‘I take nothing for granted,’ he said coldly. ‘But I’m afraid nothing is

[043-155]to be gained by a discussion of our difficulties. The time for that is

[043-156]over.

[043-157]‘I can’t quite see that. It seems to me that the time has just come.’

[043-158]‘Please tell me, to begin with, do you come on Amy’s behalf?’

[043-159]‘In a way, yes. She hasn’t sent me, but my mother and I are so

[043-160]astonished at what is happening that it was necessary for one or other

[043-161]of us to see you.’

[043-162]‘I think it is all between Amy and myself.’

[043-163]‘Difficulties between husband and wife are generally best left to

[043-164]the people themselves, I know. But the fact is, there are peculiar

[043-165]circumstances in the present case. It can’t be necessary for me to

[043-166]explain further.’

[043-167]Reardon could find no suitable words of reply. He understood what Yule

[043-168]referred to, and began to feel the full extent of his humiliation.

[043-169]‘You mean, of course--’ he began; but his tongue failed him.

[043-170]‘Well, we should really like to know how long it is proposed that Amy

[043-171]shall remain with her mother.’

[043-172]John was perfectly self-possessed; it took much to disturb his

[043-173]equanimity. He smoked his cigarette, which was in an amber mouthpiece,

[043-174]and seemed to enjoy its flavour. Reardon found himself observing the

[043-175]perfection of the young man’s boots and trousers.

[043-176]‘That depends entirely on my wife herself;’ he replied mechanically.

[043-177]‘How so?’

[043-178]‘I offer her the best home I can.’

[043-179]Reardon felt himself a poor, pitiful creature, and hated the

[043-180]well-dressed man who made him feel so.

[043-181]‘But really, Reardon,’ began the other, uncrossing and recrossing his

[043-182]legs, ‘do you tell me in seriousness that you expect Amy to live in such

[043-183]lodgings as you can afford on a pound a week?’
