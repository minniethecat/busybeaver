[064-006]‘Then you have abandoned your old ambitions?’ [[amy-reardon|Amy]] pursued.

[064-007]‘Not a bit of it. I am on the way to achieve them.’

[064-023]In the length of [[gorer-street|Gower Street]] they talked of several other things less

[064-030]‘You will let us know when that takes place?’

[064-033]CHAPTER XXVII. THE LONELY MAN

[064-034]A touch of congestion in the right lung was a warning to [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] that

[064-035]his half-year of insufficient food and general waste of strength would

[064-036]make the coming winter a hard time for him, worse probably than the

[064-037]last. [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], responding in person to the summons, found him in bed,

[064-038]waited upon by a gaunt, dry, sententious woman of sixty--not the

[064-039]landlady, but a lodger who was glad to earn one meal a day by any means

[064-040]that offered.

[064-041]‘It wouldn’t be very nice to die here, would it?’ said the sufferer,

[064-042]with a laugh which was cut short by a cough. ‘One would like a

[064-043]comfortable room, at least. Why, I don’t know. I dreamt last night that

[064-044]I was in a ship that had struck something and was going down; and it

[064-045]wasn’t the thought of death that most disturbed me, but a horror of

[064-046]being plunged in the icy water. In fact, I have had just the same

[064-047]feeling on shipboard. I remember waking up midway between Corfu and

[064-048]Brindisi, on that shaky tub of a Greek boat; we were rolling a good

[064-049]deal, and I heard a sort of alarmed rush and shouting up on deck. It

[064-050]was so warm and comfortable in the berth, and I thought with intolerable

[064-051]horror of the possibility of sousing into the black depths.’

[064-052]‘Don’t talk, my boy,’ advised Biffen. ‘Let me read you the new chapter

[064-053]of “[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]].” It may induce a refreshing slumber.’

[064-054]Reardon was away from his duties for a week; he returned to them with a

[064-055]feeling of extreme shakiness, an indisposition to exert himself, and

[064-056]a complete disregard of the course that events were taking. It was

[064-057]fortunate that he had kept aside that small store of [[money-in-literature|money]] designed

[064-058]for emergencies; he was able to draw on it now to pay his doctor, and

[064-059]provide himself with better nourishment than usual. He purchased new

[064-060]boots, too, and some articles of warm clothing of which he stood in

[064-061]need--an alarming outlay.

[064-062]A change had come over him; he was no longer rendered miserable by

[064-063]thoughts of Amy--seldom, indeed, turned his mind to her at all.

[064-064]His secretaryship at Croydon was a haven within view; the income of

[064-065]seventy-five pounds (the other half to go to his wife) would support him

[064-066]luxuriously, and for anything beyond that he seemed to care little. Next

[064-067]Sunday he was to go over to Croydon and see the institution.

[064-068]One evening of calm weather he made his way to Clipstone Street and

[064-069]greeted his friend with more show of light-heartedness than he had been

[064-070]capable of for at least two years.

[064-071]‘I have been as nearly as possible a happy man all to-day,’ he said,

[064-072]when his pipe was well lit. ‘Partly the sunshine, I suppose. There’s

[064-073]no saying if the mood will last, but if it does all is well with me. I

[064-074]regret nothing and wish for nothing.’

[064-075]‘A morbid state of mind,’ was Biffen’s opinion.

[064-076]‘No doubt of that, but I am content to be indebted to morbidness. One

[064-077]must have a rest from misery somehow. Another kind of man would have

[064-078]taken to drinking; that has tempted me now and then, I assure you. But

[064-079]I couldn’t afford it. Did you ever feel tempted to drink merely for the

[064-080]sake of forgetting trouble?’

[064-081]‘Often enough. I have done it. I have deliberately spent a certain

[064-082]proportion of the money that ought to have gone for food in the cheapest

[064-083]kind of strong liquor.’

[064-084]‘Ha! that’s interesting. But it never got the force of a habit you had

[064-085]to break?’

[064-086]‘No. Partly, I dare say, because I had the warning of poor Sykes before

[064-087]my eyes.’

[064-088]‘You never see that poor fellow?’

[064-089]‘Never. He must be dead, I think. He would die either in the hospital or

[064-090]the workhouse.’

[064-091]‘Well,’ said Reardon, musing cheerfully, ‘I shall never become a

[064-092]drunkard; I haven’t that diathesis, to use your expression. Doesn’t it

[064-093]strike you that you and I are very respectable persons? We really have

[064-094]no vices. Put us on a social pedestal, and we should be shining lights

[064-095]of morality. I sometimes wonder at our inoffensiveness. Why don’t we run

[064-096]amuck against law and order? Why, at the least, don’t we become savage

[064-097]revolutionists, and harangue in Regent’s Park of a Sunday?’

[064-098]‘Because we are passive beings, and were meant to enjoy life very

[064-099]quietly. As we can’t enjoy, we just suffer quietly, that’s all.

[064-100]By-the-bye, I want to talk about a difficulty in one of the Fragments of

[064-101]Euripides. Did you ever go through the Fragments?’

[064-102]This made a diversion for half an hour. Then Reardon returned to his

[064-103]former line of thought.

[064-104]‘As I was entering patients yesterday, there came up to the table a

[064-105]tall, good-looking, very quiet girl, poorly dressed, but as neat as

[064-106]could be. She gave me her name, then I asked “Occupation?” She said

[064-107]at once, “I’m unfortunate, sir.” I couldn’t help looking up at her in

[064-108]surprise; I had taken it for granted she was a dressmaker or something

[064-109]of the kind. And, do you know, I never felt so strong an impulse to

[064-110]shake hands, to show sympathy, and even respect, in some way. I should

[064-111]have liked to say, “Why, I am unfortunate, too!” such a good, patient

[064-112]face she had.’

[064-113]‘I distrust such appearances,’ said Biffen in his quality of realist.

[064-114]‘Well, so do I, as a rule. But in this case they were convincing. And

[064-115]there was no need whatever for her to make such a declaration; she might

[064-116]just as well have said anything else; it’s the merest form. I shall

[064-117]always hear her voice saying, “I’m unfortunate, sir.” She made me feel

[064-118]what a mistake it was for me to marry such a girl as Amy. I ought to

[064-119]have looked about for some simple, kind-hearted work-girl; that was

[064-120]the kind of wife indicated for me by circumstances. If I had earned a

[064-121]hundred a year she would have thought we were well-to-do. I should have

[064-122]been an authority to her on everything under the sun--and above it. No

[064-123]ambition would have unsettled her. We should have lived in a couple of

[064-124]poor rooms somewhere, and--we should have loved each other.’

[064-125]‘What a shameless idealist you are!’ said Biffen, shaking his head. ‘Let

[064-126]me sketch the true issue of such a marriage. To begin with, the girl

[064-127]would have married you in firm persuasion that you were a “gentleman”

[064-128]in temporary difficulties, and that before long you would have plenty

[064-129]of money to dispose of. Disappointed in this hope, she would have grown

[064-130]sharp-tempered, querulous, selfish. All your endeavours to make her

[064-131]understand you would only have resulted in widening the impassable

[064-132]gulf. She would have misconstrued your every sentence, found food for

[064-133]suspicion in every harmless joke, tormented you with the vulgarest forms

[064-134]of jealousy. The effect upon your nature would have been degrading. In

[064-135]the end, you must have abandoned every effort to raise her to your own

[064-136]level, and either have sunk to hers or made a rupture. Who doesn’t know

[064-137]the story of such attempts? I myself ten years ago, was on the point of

[064-138]committing such a folly, but, Heaven be praised! an accident saved me.’

[064-139]‘You never told me that story.’

[064-140]‘And don’t care to now. I prefer to forget it.’

[064-141]‘Well, you can judge for yourself but not for me. Of course I might have

[064-142]chosen the wrong girl, but I am supposing that I had been fortunate. In

[064-143]any case there would have been a much better chance than in the marriage

[064-144]that I made.’

[064-145]‘Your marriage was sensible enough, and a few years hence you will be a

[064-146]happy man again.’

[064-147]‘You seriously think Amy will come back to me?’

[064-148]‘Of course I do.’

[064-149]‘Upon my word, I don’t know that I desire it.’

[064-150]‘Because you are in a strangely unhealthy state.’

[064-151]‘I rather think I regard the matter more sanely than ever yet. I

[064-152]am quite free from sexual bias. I can see that Amy was not my fit

[064-153]intellectual companion, and all emotion at the thought of her has gone

[064-154]from me. The word “love” is a weariness to me. If only our idiotic laws

[064-155]permitted us to break the legal bond, how glad both of us would be!’

[064-156]‘You are depressed and anaemic. Get yourself in flesh, and view things

[064-157]like a man of this world.’

[064-158]‘But don’t you think it the best thing that can happen to a man if he

[064-159]outgrows passion?’

[064-160]‘In certain circumstances, no doubt.’

[064-161]‘In all and any. The best moments of life are those when we contemplate

[064-162]beauty in the purely artistic spirit--objectively. I have had such

[064-163]moments in Greece and Italy; times when I was a free spirit, utterly

[064-164]remote from the temptations and harassings of sexual emotion. What we

[064-165]call love is mere turmoil. Who wouldn’t release himself from it for

[064-166]ever, if the possibility offered?’

[064-167]‘Oh, there’s a good deal to be said for that, of course.’

[064-168]Reardon’s face was illumined with the glow of an exquisite memory.

[064-169]‘Haven’t I told you,’ he said, ‘of that marvellous sunset at Athens? I

[064-170]was on the Pnyx; had been rambling about there the whole afternoon. For

[064-171]I dare say a couple of hours I had noticed a growing rift of light in

[064-172]the clouds to the west; it looked as if the dull day might have a rich

[064-173]ending. That rift grew broader and brighter--the only bit of light in

[064-174]the sky. On Parnes there were white strips of ragged mist, hanging very

[064-175]low; the same on Hymettus, and even the peak of Lycabettus was just

[064-176]hidden. Of a sudden, the sun’s rays broke out. They showed themselves

[064-177]first in a strangely beautiful way, striking from behind the seaward

[064-178]hills through the pass that leads to Eleusis, and so gleaming on the

[064-179]nearer slopes of Aigaleos, making the clefts black and the rounded parts

[064-180]of the mountain wonderfully brilliant with golden colour. All the rest

[064-181]of the landscape, remember, was untouched with a ray of light. This

[064-182]lasted only a minute or two, then the sun itself sank into the open

[064-183]patch of sky and shot glory in every direction; broadening beams smote

[064-184]upwards over the dark clouds, and made them a lurid yellow. To the left

[064-185]of the sun, the gulf of Aegina was all golden mist, the islands floating

[064-186]in it vaguely. To the right, over black Salamis, lay delicate strips of

[064-187]pale blue--indescribably pale and delicate.’
