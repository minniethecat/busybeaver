# Section 11

[010-001]Little by little he had ceased to hold any correspondence with his

[010-002]former friends at Hereford. The only person to whom he still wrote and

[010-003]from whom he still heard was his mother’s father--an old man who lived

[010-004]at Derby, retired from the business of a draper, and spending his last

[010-005]years pleasantly enough with a daughter who had remained single. [[edwin-reardon|Edwin]]

[010-006]had always been a favourite with his grandfather, though they had met

[010-007]only once or twice during the past eight years. But in writing he did

[010-008]not allow it to be understood that he was in actual want, and he felt

[010-009]that he must come to dire extremities before he could bring himself to

[010-010]beg assistance.

[010-011]He had begun to answer advertisements, but the state of his wardrobe

[010-012]forbade his applying for any but humble positions. Once or twice he

[010-013]presented himself personally at offices, but his reception was so

[010-014]mortifying that death by hunger seemed preferable to a continuance of

[010-015]such experiences. The injury to his pride made him savagely arrogant;

[010-016]for days after the last rejection he hid himself in his garret, hating

[010-017]the world.

[010-018]He sold his little collection of books, and of course they brought only

[010-019]a trifling sum. That exhausted, he must begin to sell his clothes. And

[010-020]then--?

[010-021]But help was at hand. One day he saw it advertised in a newspaper that

[010-022]the secretary of a hospital in the north of [[london|London]] was in need of a

[010-023]clerk; application was to be made by letter. He wrote, and two days

[010-024]later, to his astonishment, received a reply asking him to wait upon

[010-025]the secretary at a certain hour. In a fever of agitation he kept the

[010-026]appointment, and found that his business was with a young man in the

[010-027]very highest spirits, who walked up and down a little office (the

[010-028]hospital was of the ‘special’ order, a house of no great size), and

[010-029]treated the matter in hand as an excellent joke.

[010-030]‘I thought, you know, of engaging someone much younger--quite a lad, in

[010-031]fact. But look there! Those are the replies to my advertisement.’

[010-032]He pointed to a heap of five or six hundred letters, and laughed

[010-033]consumedly.

[010-034]‘Impossible to read them all, you know. It seemed to me that the fairest

[010-035]thing would be to shake them together, stick my hand in, and take out

[010-036]one by chance. If it didn’t seem very promising, I would try a second

[010-037]time. But the first letter was yours, and I thought the fair thing to do

[010-038]was at all events to see you, you know. The fact is, I am only able to

[010-039]offer a pound a week.’

[010-040]‘I shall be very glad indeed to take that,’ said Reardon, who was bathed

[010-041]in perspiration.

[010-042]‘Then what about references, and so on?’ proceeded the young man,

[010-043]chuckling and rubbing his hands together.

[010-044]The applicant was engaged. He had barely strength to walk home; the

[010-045]sudden relief from his miseries made him, for the first time, sensible

[010-046]of the extreme physical weakness into which he had sunk. For the next

[010-047]week he was very ill, but he did not allow this to interfere with his

[010-048]new work, which was easily learnt and not burdensome.

[010-049]He held this position for three years, and during that time

[010-050]important things happened. When he had recovered from his state of

[010-051]semi-starvation, and was living in comfort (a pound a week is a very

[010-052]large sum if you have previously had to live on ten shillings), Reardon

[010-053]found that the impulse to literary production awoke in him more strongly

[010-054]than ever. He generally got home from the hospital about six o’clock,

[010-055]and the evening was his own. In this leisure time he wrote a novel in

[010-056]two volumes; one publisher refused it, but a second offered to bring it

[010-057]out on the terms of half profits to the author. The book appeared, and

[010-058]was well spoken of in one or two papers; but profits there were none

[010-059]to divide. In the third year of his clerkship he wrote a novel in three

[010-060]volumes; for this his [[publishing-trade|publishers]] gave him twenty-five pounds, with again

[010-061]a promise of half the profits after deduction of the sum advanced. Again

[010-062]there was no pecuniary [[literary-ambition|success]]. He had just got to work upon a third

[010-063]book, when his grandfather at Derby died and left him four hundred

[010-064]pounds.

[010-065]He could not resist the temptation to recover his freedom. Four hundred

[010-066]pounds, at the rate of eighty pounds a year, meant five years of

[010-067]literary endeavour. In that period he could certainly determine whether

[010-068]or not it was his destiny to live by the pen.

[010-069]In the meantime his relations with the secretary of the hospital, [[carter|Carter]]

[010-070]by name, had grown very friendly. When Reardon began to publish books,

[010-071]the high-spirited Mr Carter looked upon him with something of awe; and

[010-072]when the literary man ceased to be a clerk, there was nothing to prevent

[010-073]association on equal terms between him and his former employer. They

[010-074]continued to see a good deal of each other, and Carter made Reardon

[010-075]acquainted with certain of his friends, among whom was one [[john-yule|John Yule]],

[010-076]an easy-going, selfish, semi-intellectual young man who had a place in

[010-077]a Government office. The time of solitude had gone by for Reardon. He

[010-078]began to develop the power that was in him.

[010-079]Those two books of his were not of a kind to win popularity. They dealt

[010-080]with no particular [[social-class|class]] of society (unless one makes a distinct class

[010-081]of people who have brains), and they lacked local colour. Their interest

[010-082]was almost purely psychological. It was clear that the author had no

[010-083]faculty for constructing a story, and that pictures of active life were

[010-084]not to be expected of him; he could never appeal to the multitude.

[010-085]But strong characterisation was within his scope, and an intellectual

[010-086]fervour, appetising to a small section of refined readers, marked all

[010-087]his best pages.

[010-088]He was the kind of man who cannot struggle against adverse conditions,

[010-089]but whom prosperity warms to the exercise of his powers. Anything

[010-090]like the cares of responsibility would sooner or later harass him into

[010-091]unproductiveness. That he should produce much was in any case out of the

[010-092]question; possibly a book every two or three years might not prove too

[010-093]great a strain upon his delicate mental organism, but for him to attempt

[010-094]more than that would certainly be fatal to the peculiar merit of his

[010-095]work. Of this he was dimly conscious, and, on receiving his legacy, he

[010-096]put aside for nearly twelve months the new novel he had begun. To give

[010-097]his mind a rest he wrote several essays, much maturer than those which

[010-098]had formerly failed to find acceptance, and two of these appeared in

[010-099]magazines.

[010-100]The [[money-in-literature|money]] thus earned he spent--at a tailor’s. His friend Carter

[010-101]ventured to suggest this mode of outlay.

[010-102]His third book sold for fifty pounds. It was a great improvement on its

[010-103]predecessors, and the reviews were generally favourable. For the story

[010-104]which followed, ‘[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]],’ he received a hundred pounds. On

[010-105]the strength of that he spent six months travelling in the South of

[010-106]Europe.

[010-107]He returned to London at mid-June, and on the second day after his

[010-108]arrival befell an incident which was to control the rest of his life.

[010-109]Busy with the pictures in the Grosvenor Gallery, he heard himself

[010-110]addressed in a familiar voice, and on turning he was aware of Mr Carter,

[010-111]resplendent in fashionable summer attire, and accompanied by a young

[010-112]lady of some charms. Reardon had formerly feared encounters of this

[010-113]kind, too conscious of the defects of his attire; but at present there

[010-114]was no reason why he should shirk social intercourse. He was passably

[010-115]dressed, and the half-year of travel had benefited his appearance in

[010-116]no slight degree. Carter presented him to the young lady, of whom the

[010-117]novelist had already heard as affianced to his friend.

[010-118]Whilst they stood conversing, there approached two ladies, evidently

[010-119]mother and daughter, whose attendant was another of Reardon’s

[010-120]acquaintances, Mr John [[alfred-yule|Yule]]. This gentleman stepped briskly forward and

[010-121]welcomed the returned wanderer.

[010-122]‘Let me introduce you,’ he said, ‘to my mother and sister. Your fame has

[010-123]made them anxious to know you.’

[010-124]Reardon found himself in a position of which the novelty was

[010-125]embarrassing, but scarcely disagreeable. Here were five people

[010-126]grouped around him, all of whom regarded him unaffectedly as a man of

[010-127]importance; for though, strictly speaking, he had no ‘fame’ at all,

[010-128]these persons had kept up with the progress of his small repute,

[010-129]and were all distinctly glad to number among their acquaintances an

[010-130]unmistakable author, one, too, who was fresh from Italy and Greece. Mrs

[010-131]Yule, a lady rather too pretentious in her tone to be attractive to a

[010-132]man of Reardon’s refinement, hastened to assure him how well his books

[010-133]were known in her house, ‘though for the run of ordinary novels we don’t

[010-134]care much.’ [[marian-yule|Miss Yule]], not at all pretentious in speech, and seemingly

[010-135]reserved of disposition, was good enough to show frank interest in the

[010-136]author. As for the poor author himself, well, he merely fell in love

[010-137]with Miss Yule at first sight, and there was an end of the matter.

[010-138]A day or two later he made a call at their house, in the region

[010-139]of Westbourne Park. It was a small house, and rather showily than

[010-140]handsomely furnished; no one after visiting it would be astonished to

[010-141]hear that [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]] had but a small income, and that she was often

[010-142]put to desperate expedients to keep up the gloss of easy circumstances.

[010-143]In the gauzy and fluffy and varnishy little drawing-room Reardon found

[010-144]a youngish gentleman already in conversation with the widow and her

[010-145]daughter. This proved to be one Mr [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]], also a man of

[010-146]letters. Mr Milvain was glad to meet Reardon, whose books he had read

[010-147]with decided interest.

[010-148]‘Really,’ exclaimed Mrs Yule, ‘I don’t know how it is that we have had

[010-149]to wait so long for the pleasure of knowing you, Mr Reardon. If

[010-150]John were not so selfish he would have allowed us a share in your

[010-151]acquaintance long ago.’

[010-152]Ten weeks thereafter, Miss Yule became [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]].

[010-153]It was a time of frantic exultation with the poor fellow. He had always

[010-154]regarded the winning of a beautiful and intellectual wife as the crown

[010-155]of a successful literary career, but he had not dared to hope that such

[010-156]a triumph would be his. Life had been too hard with him on the whole.

[010-157]He, who hungered for sympathy, who thought of a woman’s love as the

[010-158]prize of mortals supremely blessed, had spent the fresh years of his

[010-159]youth in monkish solitude. Now of a sudden came friends and flattery,

[010-160]ay, and love itself. He was rapt to the seventh heaven.

[010-161]Indeed, it seemed that the girl loved him. She knew that he had but a

[010-162]hundred pounds or so left over from that little inheritance, that his

[010-163]books sold for a trifle, that he had no wealthy relatives from whom he

[010-164]could expect anything; yet she hesitated not a moment when he asked her

[010-165]to marry him.

[010-166]‘I have loved you from the first.’

[010-167]‘How is that possible?’ he urged. ‘What is there lovable in me? I

[010-168]am afraid of waking up and finding myself in my old garret, cold and

[010-169]hungry.’

[010-170]‘You will be a great man.’

[010-171]‘I implore you not to count on that! In many ways I am wretchedly weak.

[010-172]I have no such confidence in myself.’

[010-173]‘Then I will have confidence for both.’

[010-174]‘But can you love me for my own sake--love me as a man?’

[010-175]‘I love you!’

[010-176]And the words sang about him, filled the air with a mad pulsing of

[010-177]intolerable joy, made him desire to fling himself in passionate humility

[010-178]at her feet, to weep hot tears, to cry to her in insane worship. He

[010-179]thought her beautiful beyond anything his heart had imagined; her warm

[010-180]gold hair was the rapture of his eyes and of his reverent hand. Though

[010-181]slenderly fashioned, she was so gloriously strong. ‘Not a day of illness

[010-182]in her life,’ said Mrs Yule, and one could readily believe it.
