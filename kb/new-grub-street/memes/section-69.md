# Section 69

[068-001]He lit a new cigarette.

[068-002]‘Now I, my dear girls, am not a man who can afford to wait. First of

[068-003]all, my qualities are not of the kind which demand the recognition of

[068-004]posterity. My writing is for to-day, most distinctly hodiernal. It has

[068-005]no value save in reference to to-day. The question is: How can I get

[068-006]the eyes of men fixed upon me? The answer: By pretending I am quite

[068-007]independent of their gaze. I shall succeed, without any kind of doubt;

[068-008]and then I’ll have a medal struck to celebrate the day of my [[literary-marriage|marriage]].’

[068-009]But [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] was not quite so well assured of the prudence of what he was

[068-010]about to do as he wished his sisters to believe. The impulse to which he

[068-011]had finally yielded still kept its force; indeed, was stronger than

[068-012]ever since the intimacy of lovers’ dialogue had revealed to him more of

[068-013][[marian-yule|Marian]]’s heart and mind. Undeniably he was in love. Not passionately,

[068-014]not with the consuming desire which makes every motive seem paltry

[068-015]compared with its own satisfaction; but still quite sufficiently in love

[068-016]to have a great difficulty in pursuing his daily tasks. This did not

[068-017]still the voice which bade him remember all the opportunities and hopes

[068-018]he was throwing aside. Since the plighting of troth with Marian he had

[068-019]been over to Wimbledon, to the house of his friend and patron Mr Horace

[068-020]Barlow, and there he had again met with Miss Rupert. This lady had no

[068-021]power whatever over his emotions, but he felt assured that she

[068-022]regarded him with strong interest. When he imagined the possibility of

[068-023]contracting a marriage with Miss Rupert, who would make him at once

[068-024]a man of solid means, his head drooped, and he wondered at his

[068-025]precipitation. It had to be confessed that he was the victim of a vulgar

[068-026]weakness. He had declared himself not of the first order of progressive

[068-027]men.

[068-028]The conversation with [[amy-reardon|Amy Reardon]] did not tend to put his mind at rest.

[068-029]Amy was astonished at so indiscreet a step in a man of his calibre. Ah!

[068-030]if only Amy herself were free, with her ten thousand pounds to dispose

[068-031]of! She, he felt sure, did not view him with indifference. Was there not

[068-032]a touch of pique in the elaborate irony with which she had spoken of his

[068-033]choice?--But it was idle to look in that direction.

[068-034]He was anxious on his sisters’ account. They were clever girls, and with

[068-035]energy might before long earn a bare subsistence; but it began to be

[068-036]doubtful whether they would persevere in literary work. Maud, it was

[068-037]clear, had conceived hopes of quite another kind. Her intimacy with Mrs

[068-038]Lane was effecting a change in her habits, her dress, even her modes of

[068-039]speech. A few days after their establishment in the new lodgings, Jasper

[068-040]spoke seriously on this subject with the younger girl.

[068-041]‘I wonder whether you could satisfy my curiosity in a certain matter,’

[068-042]he said. ‘Do you, by chance, know how much Maud gave for that new jacket

[068-043]in which I saw her yesterday?’

[068-044][[dora-milvain|Dora]] was reluctant to answer.

[068-045]‘I don’t think it was very much.’

[068-046]‘That is to say, it didn’t cost twenty guineas. Well, I hope not.

[068-047]I notice, too, that she has been purchasing a new hat.’

[068-048]‘Oh, that was very inexpensive. She trimmed it herself.’

[068-049]‘Did she? Is there any particular, any quite special, reason for this

[068-050]expenditure?’

[068-051]‘I really can’t say, Jasper.’

[068-052]‘That’s ambiguous, you know. Perhaps it means you won’t allow yourself

[068-053]to say?’

[068-054]‘No, Maud doesn’t tell me about things of that kind.’

[068-055]He took opportunities of investigating the matter, with the result that

[068-056]some ten days after he sought private colloquy with Maud herself. She

[068-057]had asked his opinion of a little paper she was going to send to a

[068-058]ladies’ illustrated weekly, and he summoned her to his own room.

[068-059]‘I think this will do pretty well,’ he said. ‘There’s rather too much

[068-060]thought in it, perhaps. Suppose you knock out one or two of the less

[068-061]obvious reflections, and substitute a wholesome commonplace? You’ll have

[068-062]a better chance, I assure you.’

[068-063]‘But I shall make it worthless.’

[068-064]‘No; you’ll probably make it worth a guinea or so. You must remember

[068-065]that the people who read women’s papers are irritated, simply irritated,

[068-066]by anything that isn’t glaringly obvious. They hate an unusual

[068-067]thought. The art of writing for such papers--indeed, for the public in

[068-068]general--is to express vulgar thought and feeling in a way that flatters

[068-069]the vulgar thinkers and feelers. Just abandon your mind to it, and then

[068-070]let me see it again.’

[068-071]Maud took up the manuscript and glanced over it with a contemptuous

[068-072]smile. Having observed her for a moment, Jasper threw himself back in

[068-073]the chair and said, as if casually:

[068-074]‘I am told that Mr Dolomore is becoming a great friend of yours.’

[068-075]The girl’s face changed. She drew herself up, and looked away towards

[068-076]the window.

[068-077]‘I don’t know that he is a “great” friend.’

[068-078]‘Still, he pays enough attention to you to excite remark.’

[068-079]‘Whose remark?’

[068-080]‘That of several people who go to Mrs Lane’s.’

[068-081]‘I don’t know any reason for it,’ said Maud coldly.

[068-082]‘Look here, Maud, you don’t mind if I give you a friendly warning?’

[068-083]She kept silence, with a look of superiority to all monition.

[068-084]‘Dolomore,’ pursued her brother, ‘is all very well in his way, but

[068-085]that way isn’t yours. I believe he has a good deal of [[money-in-literature|money]], but he

[068-086]has neither brains nor principle. There’s no harm in your observing

[068-087]the nature and habits of such individuals, but don’t allow yourself to

[068-088]forget that they are altogether beneath you.’

[068-089]‘There’s no need whatever for you to teach me self-respect,’ replied the

[068-090]girl.

[068-091]‘I’m quite sure of that; but you are inexperienced. On the whole, I do

[068-092]rather wish that you would go less frequently to Mrs Lane’s.

[068-093]It was rather an unfortunate choice of yours. Very much better if you

[068-094]could have got on a good footing with the Barnabys. If you are generally

[068-095]looked upon as belonging to the Lanes’ set it will make it difficult for

[068-096]you to get in with the better people.’

[068-097]Maud was not to be drawn into argument, and Jasper could only hope that

[068-098]his words would have some weight with her. The Mr Dolomore in question

[068-099]was a young man of rather offensive type--athletic, dandiacal, and

[068-100]half-educated. It astonished Jasper that his sister could tolerate such

[068-101]an empty creature for a moment; who has not felt the like surprise with

[068-102]regard to women’s inclinations? He talked with Dora about it, but she

[068-103]was not in her sister’s confidence.

[068-104]‘I think you ought to have some influence with her,’ Jasper said.

[068-105]‘Maud won’t allow anyone to interfere in--her private affairs.’

[068-106]‘It would be unfortunate if she made me quarrel with her.’

[068-107]‘Oh, surely there isn’t any danger of that?’

[068-108]‘I don’t know, she mustn’t be obstinate.’

[068-109]Jasper himself saw a good deal of miscellaneous society at this time. He

[068-110]could not work so persistently as usual, and with wise tactics he used

[068-111]the seasons of enforced leisure to extend his acquaintance. Marian and

[068-112]he were together twice a week, in the evening.

[068-113]Of his old Bohemian associates he kept up intimate relations with one

[068-114]only, and that was [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]]. This was in a measure obligatory, for

[068-115]Whelpdale frequently came to see him, and it would have been difficult

[068-116]to repel a man who was always making known how highly he esteemed the

[068-117]privilege of Milvain’s friendship, and whose company on the whole was

[068-118]agreeable enough. At the present juncture Whelpdale’s cheery flattery

[068-119]was a distinct assistance; it helped to support Jasper in his

[068-120]self-confidence, and to keep the brightest complexion on the prospect to

[068-121]which he had committed himself.

[068-122]‘Whelpdale is anxious to make Marian’s acquaintance,’ Jasper said to his

[068-123]sisters one day. ‘Shall we have him here tomorrow evening?’

[068-124]‘Just as you like,’ Maud replied.

[068-125]‘You won’t object, Dora?’

[068-126]‘Oh no! I rather like Mr Whelpdale.’

[068-127]‘If I were to repeat that to him he’d go wild with delight. But don’t

[068-128]be afraid; I shan’t. I’ll ask him to come for an hour, and trust to his

[068-129]discretion not to bore us by staying too long.’

[068-130]A note was posted to Whelpdale; he was invited to present himself at

[068-131]eight o’clock, by which time Marian would have arrived. Jasper’s room

[068-132]was to be the scene of the assembly, and punctual to the minute the

[068-133]literary adviser appeared. He was dressed with all the finish his

[068-134]wardrobe allowed, and his face beamed with gratification; it was rapture

[068-135]to him to enter the presence of these three girls, one of whom he had,

[068-136]_more suo_, held in romantic remembrance since his one meeting with her at

[068-137]Jasper’s old lodgings. His eyes melted with tenderness as he approached

[068-138]Dora and saw her smile of gracious recognition. By Maud he was

[068-139]profoundly impressed. Marian inspired him with no awe, but he fully

[068-140]appreciated the charm of her features and her modest gravity. After all,

[068-141]it was to Dora that his eyes turned again most naturally. He thought

[068-142]her exquisite, and, rather than be long without a glimpse of her, he

[068-143]contented himself with fixing his eyes on the hem of her dress and the

[068-144]boot-toe that occasionally peeped from beneath it.

[068-145]As was to be expected in such a circle, conversation soon turned to the

[068-146]subject of literary struggles.

[068-147]‘I always feel it rather humiliating,’ said Jasper, ‘that I have gone

[068-148]through no very serious hardships. It must be so gratifying to say to

[068-149]young fellows who are just beginning:

[068-150]“Ah, I remember when I was within an ace of starving to death,” and

[068-151]then come out with [[grub-street|Grub Street]] reminiscences of the most appalling kind.

[068-152]Unfortunately, I have always had enough to eat.’

[068-153]‘I haven’t,’ exclaimed Whelpdale. ‘I have lived for five days on a few

[068-154]cents’ worth of pea-nuts in the States.’

[068-155]‘What are pea-nuts, Mr Whelpdale?’ asked Dora.

[068-156]Delighted with the question, Whelpdale described that undesirable

[068-157]species of food.

[068-158]‘It was in Troy,’ he went on, ‘Troy, N.Y. To think that a man should

[068-159]live on pea-nuts in a town called Troy!’

[068-160]‘Tell us those adventures,’ cried Jasper. ‘It’s a long time since I

[068-161]heard them, and the girls will enjoy it vastly.’

[068-162]Dora looked at him with such good-humoured interest that the traveller

[068-163]needed no further persuasion.

[068-164]‘It came to pass in those days,’ he began, ‘that I inherited from my

[068-165]godfather a small, a very small, sum of money. I was making strenuous

[068-166]efforts to write for magazines, with absolutely no encouragement.

[068-167]As everybody was talking just then of the Centennial Exhibition at

[068-168]Philadelphia, I conceived the brilliant idea of crossing the Atlantic,

[068-169]in the hope that I might find valuable literary material at the

[068-170]Exhibition--or Exposition, as they called it--and elsewhere. I won’t

[068-171]trouble you with an account of how I lived whilst I still had money;

[068-172]sufficient that no one would accept the articles I sent to England,

[068-173]and that at last I got into perilous straits. I went to New York, and

[068-174]thought of returning home, but the spirit of adventure was strong in me.

[068-175]“I’ll go West,” I said to myself. “There I am bound to find material.”

[068-176]And go I did, taking an emigrant ticket to Chicago. It was December, and

[068-177]I should like you to imagine what a journey of a thousand miles by an

[068-178]emigrant train meant at that season. The cars were deadly cold, and what

[068-179]with that and the hardness of the seats I found it impossible to sleep;

[068-180]it reminded me of tortures I had read about; I thought my brain would

[068-181]have burst with the need of sleeping. At Cleveland, in Ohio, we had to

[068-182]wait several hours in the night; I left the station and wandered about

[068-183]till I found myself on the edge of a great cliff that looked over Lake

[068-184]Erie. A magnificent picture! Brilliant moonlight, and all the lake away

[068-185]to the horizon frozen and covered with snow. The clocks struck two as I

[068-186]stood there.’
