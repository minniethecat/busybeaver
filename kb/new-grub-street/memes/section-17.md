# Section 17

[016-001]Unfortunately, [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] was not so grateful as he might have been.

[016-002]His [[literary-marriage|marriage]] proved far from unsuccessful; he might have found himself

[016-003]united to a vulgar shrew, whereas the girl had the great virtues of

[016-004]humility and kindliness. She endeavoured to learn of him, but her

[016-005]dulness and his impatience made this attempt a failure; her human

[016-006]qualities had to suffice. And they did, until Yule began to lift his

[016-007]head above the literary mob. Previously, he often lost his temper with

[016-008]her, but never expressed or felt repentance of his marriage; now he

[016-009]began to see only the disadvantages of his position, and, forgetting the

[016-010]facts of the case, to imagine that he might well have waited for a wife

[016-011]who could share his intellectual existence. Mrs Yule had to pass through

[016-012]a few years of much bitterness. Already a martyr to dyspepsia, and often

[016-013]suffering from bilious headaches of extreme violence, her husband now

[016-014]and then lost all control of his temper, all sense of kind feeling,

[016-015]even of decency, and reproached the poor woman with her ignorance, her

[016-016]stupidity, her low origin. Naturally enough she defended herself with

[016-017]such weapons as a sense of cruel injustice supplied. More than once

[016-018]the two all but parted. It did not come to an actual rupture, chiefly

[016-019]because Yule could not do without his wife; her tendance had become

[016-020]indispensable. And then there was the child to consider.

[016-021]From the first it was Yule’s dread lest [[marian-yule|Marian]] should be infected with

[016-022]her mother’s faults of speech and behaviour. He would scarcely permit

[016-023]his wife to talk to the child. At the earliest possible moment Marian

[016-024]was sent to a day-school, and in her tenth year she went as weekly

[016-025]boarder to an establishment at Fulham; any sacrifice of [[money-in-literature|money]] to insure

[016-026]her growing up with the tongue and manners of a lady. It can scarcely

[016-027]have been a light trial to the mother to know that contact with her was

[016-028]regarded as her child’s greatest danger; but in her humility and her

[016-029]love for Marian she offered no resistance. And so it came to pass

[016-030]that one day the little girl, hearing her mother make some flagrant

[016-031]grammatical error, turned to the other parent and asked gravely: ‘Why

[016-032]doesn’t mother speak as properly as we do?’ Well, that is one of the

[016-033]results of such marriages, one of the myriad miseries that result from

[016-034][[literary-poverty|poverty]].

[016-035]The end was gained at all hazards. Marian grew up everything that her

[016-036]father desired. Not only had she the bearing of refinement, but it early

[016-037]became obvious that nature had well endowed her with brains. From the

[016-038]nursery her talk was of books, and at the age of twelve she was already

[016-039]able to give her father some assistance as an amanuensis.

[016-040]At that time Edmund Yule was still living; he had overcome his

[016-041]prejudices, and there was intercourse between his household and that of

[016-042]the literary man. Intimacy it could not be called, for Mrs Edmund (who

[016-043]was the daughter of a law-stationer) had much difficulty in behaving to

[016-044]Mrs Alfred with show of suavity. Still, the cousins [[amy-reardon|Amy]] and Marian from

[016-045]time to time saw each other, and were not unsuitable companions. It was

[016-046]the death of Amy’s father that brought these relations to an end; left

[016-047]to the control of her own affairs Mrs Edmund was not long in giving

[016-048]offence to Mrs Alfred, and so to Alfred himself. The man of letters

[016-049]might be inconsiderate enough in his behaviour to his wife, but as

[016-050]soon as anyone else treated her with disrespect that was quite another

[016-051]matter. Purely on this account he quarrelled violently with his

[016-052]brother’s widow, and from that day the two families kept apart.

[016-053]The chapter of quarrels was one of no small importance in Alfred’s life;

[016-054]his difficult temper, and an ever-increasing sense of neglected merit,

[016-055]frequently put him at war with [[publishing-trade|publishers]], editors, fellow-authors, and

[016-056]he had an unhappy trick of exciting the hostility of men who were most

[016-057]likely to be useful to him. With Mr Polo, for instance, who held him

[016-058]in esteem, and whose [[literary-success|commercial success]] made him a valuable connection,

[016-059]Alfred ultimately broke on a trifling matter of personal dignity. Later

[016-060]came the great quarrel with Clement [[fadge|Fadge]], an affair of considerable

[016-061]advantage in the way of advertisement to both the men concerned. It

[016-062]happened in the year 1873. At that time Yule was editor of a weekly

[016-063]paper called The Balance, a literary organ which aimed high, and failed

[016-064]to hit the circulation essential to its existence. Fadge, a younger man,

[016-065]did [[reviewing-criticism|reviewing]] for The Balance; he was in needy circumstances, and had

[016-066]wrought himself into Yule’s good opinion by judicious flattery. But with

[016-067]a clear eye for the main chance Mr Fadge soon perceived that Yule

[016-068]could only be of temporary use to him, and that the editor of a

[016-069]well-established weekly which lost no opportunity of throwing scorn

[016-070]upon Yule and all his works would be a much more profitable conquest.

[016-071]He succeeded in transferring his services to the more flourishing

[016-072]paper, and struck out a special line of work by the free exercise of

[016-073]a malicious flippancy which was then without rival in the periodical

[016-074]press. When he had thoroughly got his hand in, it fell to Mr Fadge,

[016-075]in the mere way of business, to review a volume of his old editor’s,

[016-076]a rather pretentious and longwinded but far from worthless essay ‘On

[016-077]Imagination as a National Characteristic.’ The notice was a masterpiece;

[016-078]its exquisite virulence set the literary circles chuckling. Concerning

[016-079]the authorship there was no mystery, and Alfred Yule had the

[016-080]indiscretion to make a violent reply, a savage assault upon Fadge, in

[016-081]the columns of The Balance. Fadge desired nothing better; the uproar

[016-082]which arose--chaff, fury, grave comments, sneering spite--could only

[016-083]result in drawing universal attention to his anonymous cleverness, and

[016-084]throwing ridicule upon the heavy, conscientious man. Well, you

[016-085]probably remember all about it. It ended in the disappearance of Yule’s

[016-086]struggling paper, and the establishment on a firm basis of Fadge’s

[016-087]reputation.

[016-088]It would be difficult to mention any department of literary endeavour in

[016-089]which Yule did not, at one time or another, try his fortune. Turn to

[016-090]his name in the Museum Catalogue; the list of works appended to it

[016-091]will amuse you. In his thirtieth year he published a novel; it failed

[016-092]completely, and the same result awaited a similar experiment five years

[016-093]later. He wrote a drama of modern life, and for some years strove to

[016-094]get it acted, but in vain; finally it appeared ‘for the closet’--giving

[016-095]Clement Fadge such an opportunity as he seldom enjoyed. The one

[016-096]noteworthy thing about these productions, and about others of equally

[016-097]mistaken direction, was the sincerity of their workmanship. Had Yule

[016-098]been content to manufacture a novel or a play with due disregard for

[016-099]literary honour, he might perchance have made a mercantile [[literary-ambition|success]]; but

[016-100]the poor fellow had not pliancy enough for this. He took his efforts

[016-101]au grand serieux; thought he was producing works of art; pursued his

[016-102]ambition in a spirit of fierce conscientiousness. In spite of all, he

[016-103]remained only a journeyman. The kind of work he did best was poorly

[016-104]paid, and could bring no fame. At the age of fifty he was still living

[016-105]in a poor house in an obscure quarter. He earned enough for his actual

[016-106]needs, and was under no pressing fear for the morrow, so long as his

[016-107]faculties remained unimpaired; but there was no disguising from himself

[016-108]that his life had been a failure. And the thought tormented him.

[016-109]Now there had come unexpectedly a gleam of hope. If indeed, the man

[016-110]Rackett thought of offering him the editorship of [[the-study|The Study]] he might

[016-111]even yet taste the triumphs for which he had so vehemently longed. The

[016-112]Study was a weekly paper of fair repute. Fadge had harmed it, no doubt

[016-113]of that, by giving it a tone which did not suit the majority of its

[016-114]readers--serious people, who thought that the criticism of contemporary

[016-115]writing offered an opportunity for something better than a display of

[016-116]malevolent wit. But a return to the old earnestness would doubtless set

[016-117]all right again. And the joy of sitting in that dictatorial chair! The

[016-118]delight of having his own organ once more, of making himself a power in

[016-119]the world of letters, of emphasising to a large audience his developed

[016-120]methods of criticism!

[016-121]An embittered man is a man beset by evil temptations. The Study

[016-122]contained each week certain columns of flying gossip, and when he

[016-123]thought of this, Yule also thought of Clement Fadge, and sundry other

[016-124]of his worst enemies. How the gossip column can be used for hostile

[016-125]purposes, yet without the least overt offence, he had learnt only too

[016-126]well. Sometimes the mere omission of a man’s name from a list of authors

[016-127]can mortify and injure. In our day the manipulation of such paragraphs

[016-128]has become a fine art; but you recall numerous illustrations. Alfred

[016-129]knew well enough how incessantly the tempter would be at his ear;

[016-130]he said to himself that in certain instances yielding would be no

[016-131]dishonour. He himself had many a time been mercilessly treated; in the

[016-132]very interest of the public it was good that certain men should suffer a

[016-133]snubbing, and his fingers itched to have hold of the editorial pen. Ha,

[016-134]ha! Like the war-horse he snuffed the battle afar off.

[016-135]No work this evening, though there were tasks which pressed for

[016-136]completion. His study--the only room on the ground level except the

[016-137]dining-room--was small, and even a good deal of the floor was encumbered

[016-138]with books, but he found space for walking nervously hither and thither.

[016-139]He was doing this when, about half-past nine, his wife appeared at the

[016-140]door, bringing him a cup of coffee and some biscuits, his wonted supper.

[016-141]Marian generally waited upon him at this time, and he asked why she had

[016-142]not come.

[016-143]‘She has one of her headaches again, I’m sorry to say,’ Mrs Yule

[016-144]replied. ‘I persuaded her to go to bed early.’

[016-145]Having placed the tray upon the table--books had to be pushed aside--she

[016-146]did not seem disposed to withdraw.

[016-147]‘Are you busy, Alfred?’

[016-148]‘Why?’

[016-149]‘I thought I should like just to speak of something.’

[016-150]She was using the opportunity of his good humour. Yule spoke to her with

[016-151]the usual carelessness, but not forbiddingly.

[016-152]‘What is it? Those Holloway people, I’ll warrant.’

[016-153]‘No, no! It’s about Marian. She had a letter from one of those young

[016-154]ladies this afternoon.’

[016-155]‘What young ladies?’ asked Yule, with impatience of this circuitous

[016-156]approach.

[016-157]‘The Miss Milvains.’

[016-158]‘Well, there’s no harm that I know of. They’re decent people.’

[016-159]‘Yes; so you told me. But she began to speak about their brother, and--’

[016-160]‘What about him? Do say what you want to say, and have done with it!’

[016-161]‘I can’t help thinking, Alfred, that she’s disappointed you didn’t ask

[016-162]him to come here.’

[016-163]Yule stared at her in slight surprise. He was still not angry, and

[016-164]seemed quite willing to consider this matter suggested to him so

[016-165]timorously.

[016-166]‘Oh, you think so? Well, I don’t know. Why should I have asked him?

[016-167]It was only because Miss Harrow seemed to wish it that I saw him down

[016-168]there. I have no particular interest in him. And as for--’

[016-169]He broke off and seated himself. Mrs Yule stood at a distance.

[016-170]‘We must remember her age,’ she said.

[016-171]‘Why yes, of course.’

[016-172]He mused, and began to nibble a biscuit.

[016-173]‘And you know, Alfred, she never does meet any young men. I’ve often

[016-174]thought it wasn’t right to her.’

[016-175]‘H’m! But this lad [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] is a very doubtful sort of customer. To begin

[016-176]with, he has nothing, and they tell me his mother for the most part

[016-177]supports him. I don’t quite approve of that. She isn’t well off, and he

[016-178]ought to have been making a living by now.
