# Literature as Trade

The proposition that literature has become "a trade" — that the writer is no longer an artist serving truth but a tradesman serving the market — is the ideological fulcrum of *New Grub Street*. Every character in the novel is positioned relative to this claim, and their fates are determined by how they negotiate it. [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] embraces the logic and prospers; [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] resists it and dies; [[alfred-yule]] accepts it resentfully and wears out his life in meaningless toil.

## Milvain's Gospel

[[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]] delivers the novel's most famous speech — a lucid, unapologetic account of what it means to treat writing as commerce:

> "Literature nowadays is a trade. Putting aside men of genius, who may succeed by mere cosmic force, your successful man of letters is your skilful tradesman. He thinks first and foremost of the markets; when one kind of goods begins to go off slackly, he is ready with something new and appetising." [P02-183–P02-187]

Milvain's metaphor is relentlessly commercial: literary works are "goods," the audience is a "market," the writer is a "tradesman." His genius — and his amorality — lies in his willingness to accept this logic without nostalgia or regret. He is "slowly, but surely, learning the business" (P02-201), treating literature as one might treat any other trade.

| **Critical distance** | [[marian-yule]] | Survival without prosperity |
| **Inherited wealth** | [[amy-reardon]] (after inheritance) | Freedom from the trade entirely |

## Reardon's Refusal

[[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]] embodies the opposite response. He cannot treat writing as a trade because he experiences it as a vocation — slow, painful, driven by artistic conscience rather than market demand. His tragedy is that he lives in a world where "writing is a business" (001-119) but cannot adapt to that reality. [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] describes Reardon as someone who "can't supply the market" (P02-181) — the key verb is "supply," treating literary creation as commodity production.

## The Old Guard's Ambivalence

[[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] represents an older generation that is neither as cynical as [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] nor as principled as [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]. He has spent decades in [[grub-street]] as a "gazetteer" (055-016), producing literary hack-work without either commercial success or artistic satisfaction. His bitterness — "A life of toil! A life of toil!" (055-018) — is the product of a system that demands ceaseless production without offering either wealth or meaning.

## The Gissing Paradox

Gissing's own position is complex. The novel anatomizes the commercial literary world with sociological precision, yet it also suggests that the older model of literary patronage and artistic autonomy was already an illusion. There is no escape from the market — only degrees of accommodation. [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] prospers, [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] dies, and the novel refuses to offer easy moral judgments about either outcome.
