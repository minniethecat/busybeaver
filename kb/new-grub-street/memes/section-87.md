# Section 87

[086-001]A fatal day. There was an end of all his peace, all his capacity

[086-002]for labour, his patient endurance of [[literary-poverty|penury]]. Once, when he was about

[086-003]three-and-twenty, he had been in love with a girl of gentle nature and

[086-004]fair intelligence; on account of his poverty, he could not even hope

[086-005]that his love might be returned, and he went away to bear the misery as

[086-006]best he might. Since then the life he had led precluded the forming of

[086-007]such attachments; it would never have been possible for him to support

[086-008]a wife of however humble origin. At intervals he felt the full weight

[086-009]of his loneliness, but there were happily long periods during which his

[086-010]Greek studies and his efforts in [[literary-realism|realistic fiction]] made him indifferent

[086-011]to the curse laid upon him. But after that hour of intimate speech with

[086-012][[amy-reardon|Amy]], he never again knew rest of mind or heart.

[086-013]Accepting what [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] had bequeathed to him, he removed the books and

[086-014]furniture to a room in that part of the town which he had found most

[086-015]convenient for his singular tutorial pursuits. The winter did not pass

[086-016]without days of all but starvation, but in March he received his fifteen

[086-017]pounds for ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]],’ and this was a fortune, putting him beyond the

[086-018]reach of hunger for full six months. Not long after that he yielded to

[086-019]a temptation that haunted him day and night, and went to call upon Amy,

[086-020]who was still living with her mother at Westbourne Park. When he

[086-021]entered the drawing-room Amy was sitting there alone; she rose with an

[086-022]exclamation of frank pleasure.

[086-023]‘I have often thought of you lately, [[harold-biffen|Mr Biffen]]. How kind to come and see

[086-024]me!’

[086-025]He could scarcely speak; her beauty, as she stood before him in the

[086-026]graceful black dress, was anguish to his excited nerves, and her voice

[086-027]was so cruel in its conventional warmth. When he looked at her eyes,

[086-028]he remembered how their brightness had been dimmed with tears, and the

[086-029]sorrow he had shared with her seemed to make him more than an ordinary

[086-030]friend. When he told her of his [[literary-ambition|success]] with the [[publishing-trade|publishers]], she was

[086-031]delighted.

[086-032]‘Oh, when is it to come out? I shall watch the advertisements so

[086-033]anxiously.’

[086-034]‘Will you allow me to send you a copy, Mrs Reardon?’

[086-035]‘Can you really spare one?’

[086-036]Of the half-dozen he would receive, he scarcely knew how to dispose of

[086-037]three. And Amy expressed her gratitude in the most charming way. She had

[086-038]gained much in point of manner during the past twelve months; her ten

[086-039]thousand pounds inspired her with the confidence necessary to a perfect

[086-040]demeanour. That slight hardness which was wont to be perceptible in

[086-041]her tone had altogether passed away; she seemed to be cultivating

[086-042]flexibility of voice.

[086-043]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] came in, and was all graciousness. Then two callers presented

[086-044]themselves. Biffen’s pleasure was at an end as soon as he had to adapt

[086-045]himself to polite dialogue; he escaped as speedily as possible.

[086-046]He was not the kind of man that deceives himself as to his own aspect

[086-047]in the eyes of others. Be as kind as she might, Amy could not set him

[086-048]strutting Malvolio-wise; she viewed him as a poor devil who often had

[086-049]to pawn his coat--a man of parts who would never get on in the world--a

[086-050]friend to be thought of kindly because her dead husband had valued

[086-051]him. Nothing more than that; he understood perfectly the limits of her

[086-052]feeling. But this could not put restraint upon the emotion with which

[086-053]he received any most trifling utterance of kindness from her. He did not

[086-054]think of what was, but of what, under changed circumstances, might be.

[086-055]To encourage such fantasy was the idlest self-torment, but he had gone

[086-056]too far in this form of indulgence. He became the slave of his inflamed

[086-057]imagination.

[086-058]In that letter with which he replied to her praises of his book,

[086-059]perchance he had allowed himself to speak too much as he thought.

[086-060]He wrote in reckless delight, and did not wait for the prudence of a

[086-061]later hour. When it was past recall, he would gladly have softened

[086-062]many of the expressions the letter contained. ‘I value it more than the

[086-063]praises of all the reviewers in existence’--would Amy be offended at

[086-064]that? ‘Yours in gratitude and reverence,’ he had signed himself--the

[086-065]kind of phrase that comes naturally to a passionate man, when he would

[086-066]fain say more than he dares. To what purpose this half-revelation?

[086-067]Unless, indeed, he wished to learn once and for ever, by the gentlest

[086-068]of repulses, that his homage was only welcome so long as it kept well

[086-069]within conventional terms.

[086-070]He passed a month of distracted idleness, until there came a day

[086-071]when the need to see Amy was so imperative that it mastered every

[086-072]consideration. He donned his best clothes, and about four o’clock

[086-073]presented himself at Mrs Yule’s house. By ill luck there happened to be

[086-074]at least half a dozen callers in the drawing-room; the strappado would

[086-075]have been preferable, in his eyes, to such an ordeal as this. Moreover,

[086-076]he was convinced that both Amy and her mother received him with far less

[086-077]cordiality than on the last occasion. He had expected it, but he bit

[086-078]his lips till the blood came. What business had he among people of this

[086-079]kind? No doubt the visitors wondered at his comparative shabbiness, and

[086-080]asked themselves how he ventured to make a call without the regulation

[086-081]chimney-pot hat. It was a wretched and foolish mistake.

[086-082]Ten minutes saw him in the street again, vowing that he would never

[086-083]approach Amy more. Not that he found fault with her; the blame was

[086-084]entirely his own.

[086-085]He lived on the third floor of a house in Goodge Street, above a baker’s

[086-086]shop. The bequest of Reardon’s furniture was a great advantage to him,

[086-087]as he had only to pay rent for a bare room; the books, too, came as a

[086-088]godsend, since the destruction of his own. He had now only one pupil,

[086-089]and was not exerting himself to find others; his old energy had forsaken

[086-090]him.

[086-091]For the failure of his book he cared nothing. It was no more than he

[086-092]anticipated. The work was done--the best he was capable of--and this

[086-093]satisfied him.

[086-094]It was doubtful whether he loved Amy, in the true sense of exclusive

[086-095]desire. She represented for him all that is lovely in womanhood; to his

[086-096]starved soul and senses she was woman, the complement of his frustrate

[086-097]being. Circumstance had made her the means of exciting in him that

[086-098]natural force which had hitherto either been dormant or had yielded to

[086-099]the resolute will.

[086-100]Companionless, inert, he suffered the tortures which are so ludicrous

[086-101]and contemptible to the happily married. Life was barren to him, and

[086-102]would soon grow hateful; only in sleep could he cast off the unchanging

[086-103]thoughts and desires which made all else meaningless. And rightly

[086-104]meaningless: he revolted against the unnatural constraints forbidding

[086-105]him to complete his manhood.

[086-106]By what fatality was he alone of men withheld from the winning of a

[086-107]woman’s love?

[086-108]He could not bear to walk the streets where the faces of beautiful women

[086-109]would encounter him. When he must needs leave the house, he went about

[086-110]in the poor, narrow ways, where only spectacles of coarseness, and

[086-111]want, and toil would be presented to him. Yet even here he was too often

[086-112]reminded that the poverty-stricken of the [[social-class|class]] to which poverty is

[086-113]natural were not condemned to endure in solitude. Only he who belonged

[086-114]to no class, who was rejected alike by his fellows in privation and by

[086-115]his equals in intellect, must die without having known the touch of a

[086-116]loving woman’s hand.

[086-117]The summer went by, and he was unconscious of its warmth and light. How

[086-118]his days passed he could not have said.

[086-119]One evening in early autumn, as he stood before the book-stall at the

[086-120]end of Goodge Street, a familiar voice accosted him. It was [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]]’s.

[086-121]A month or two ago he had stubbornly refused an invitation to dine

[086-122]with Whelpdale and other acquaintances--you remember what the occasion

[086-123]was--and since then the prosperous young man had not crossed his path.

[086-124]‘I’ve something to tell you,’ said the assailer, taking hold of his

[086-125]arm. ‘I’m in a tremendous state of mind, and want someone to share my

[086-126]delight. You can walk a short way, I hope? Not too busy with some new

[086-127]book?’

[086-128]Biffen gave no answer, but went whither he was led.

[086-129]‘You are writing a new book, I suppose? Don’t be discouraged, old

[086-130]fellow. “Mr Bailey” will have his day yet; I know men who consider it an

[086-131]undoubted work of [[literary-genius|genius]]. What’s the next to deal with?’

[086-132]‘I haven’t decided yet,’ replied Harold, merely to avoid argument. He

[086-133]spoke so seldom that the sound of his own voice was strange to him.

[086-134]‘Thinking over it, I suppose, in your usual solid way. Don’t be hurried.

[086-135]But I must tell you of this affair of mine. You know [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]]? I

[086-136]have asked her to marry me, and, by the Powers! she has given me an

[086-137]encouraging answer. Not an actual yes, but encouraging! She’s away in

[086-138]the Channel Islands, and I wrote--’

[086-139]He talked on for a quarter of an hour. Then, with a sudden movement, the

[086-140]listener freed himself.

[086-141]‘I can’t go any farther,’ he said hoarsely. ‘Good-bye!’

[086-142]Whelpdale was disconcerted.

[086-143]‘I have been boring you. That’s a confounded fault of mine; I know it.’

[086-144]Biffen had waved his hand, and was gone.

[086-145]A week or two more would see him at the end of his [[money-in-literature|money]]. He had no

[086-146]lessons now, and could not write; from his novel nothing was to be

[086-147]expected. He might apply again to his brother, but such dependence was

[086-148]unjust and unworthy. And why should he struggle to preserve a life which

[086-149]had no prospect but of misery?

[086-150]It was in the hours following his encounter with Whelpdale that he first

[086-151]knew the actual desire of death, the simple longing for extinction. One

[086-152]must go far in suffering before the innate will-to-live is thus truly

[086-153]overcome; weariness of bodily anguish may induce this perversion of

[086-154]the instincts; less often, that despair of suppressed emotion which

[086-155]had fallen upon Harold. Through the night he kept his thoughts fixed on

[086-156]death in its aspect of repose, of eternal oblivion. And herein he had

[086-157]found solace.

[086-158]The next night it was the same. Moving about among common needs and

[086-159]occupations, he knew not a moment’s cessation of heart-ache, but when

[086-160]he lay down in the darkness a hopeful summons whispered to him. Night,

[086-161]which had been the worst season of his pain, had now grown friendly; it

[086-162]came as an anticipation of the sleep that is everlasting.

[086-163]A few more days, and he was possessed by a calm of spirit such as he had

[086-164]never known. His resolve was taken, not in a moment of supreme conflict,

[086-165]but as the result of a subtle process by which his imagination had

[086-166]become in love with death. Turning from contemplation of life’s one

[086-167]rapture, he looked with the same intensity of desire to a state that had

[086-168]neither fear nor hope.

[086-169]One afternoon he went to the Museum Reading-room, and was busy for a few

[086-170]minutes in consultation of a volume which he took from the shelves

[086-171]of medical literature. On his way homeward he entered two or three

[086-172]chemists’ shops. Something of which he had need could be procured only

[086-173]in very small quantities; but repetition of his demand in different

[086-174]places supplied him sufficiently. When he reached his room, he emptied

[086-175]the contents of sundry little bottles into one larger, and put this in

[086-176]his pocket. Then he wrote rather a long letter, addressed to his brother

[086-177]at Liverpool.

[086-178]It had been a beautiful day, and there wanted still a couple of hours

[086-179]before the warm, golden sunlight would disappear. Harold stood and

[086-180]looked round his room. As always, it presented a neat, orderly aspect,

[086-181]but his eye caught sight of a volume which stood upside down, and this

[086-182]fault--particularly hateful to a bookish man--he rectified. He put

[086-183]his blotting-pad square on the table, closed the lid of the inkstand,

[086-184]arranged his pens. Then he took his hat and stick, locked the door

[086-185]behind him, and went downstairs. At the foot he spoke to his landlady,

[086-186]and told her that he should not return that night. As soon as possible

[086-187]after leaving the house he posted his letter.
