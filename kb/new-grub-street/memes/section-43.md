# Section 43

[042-001]‘Things’ were not quite satisfactory, as it proved. You should have

[042-002]heard the change that came in that sweetly plaintive voice when it

[042-003]addressed the luckless housemaid. It was not brutal; not at all. But

[042-004]so sharp, hard, unrelenting--the voice of the goddess Poverty herself

[042-005]perhaps sounds like that.

[042-006]Mad? Was he to be spoken of in a low voice, and with finger pointing to

[042-007]the forehead? There was something ridiculous, as well as repugnant, in

[042-008]such a thought; but it kept possession of [[amy-reardon|Amy]]’s mind. She was brooding

[042-009]upon it when her mother came into the drawing-room.

[042-010]‘And he positively refused to carry out the former plan?’

[042-011]‘Refused. Said it was useless.’

[042-012]‘How could it be useless? There’s something so unaccountable in his

[042-013]behaviour.’

[042-014]‘I don’t think it unaccountable,’ replied Amy. ‘It’s weak and selfish,

[042-015]that’s all. He takes the first miserable employment that offers rather

[042-016]than face the hard work of writing another book.’

[042-017]She was quite aware that this did not truly represent her husband’s

[042-018]position. But an uneasiness of conscience impelled her to harsh speech.

[042-019]‘But just fancy!’ exclaimed her mother. ‘What can he mean by asking you

[042-020]to go and live with him on twenty-five shillings a week? Upon my word.

[042-021]if his mind isn’t disordered he must have made a deliberate plan to get

[042-022]rid of you.’

[042-023]Amy shook her head.

[042-024]‘You mean,’ asked Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]], ‘that he really thinks it possible for all

[042-025]of you to be supported on those wages?’

[042-026]The last word was chosen to express the utmost scorn.

[042-027]‘He talked of earning fifty pounds a year by writing.’

[042-028]‘Even then it could only make about a hundred a year. My dear child,

[042-029]it’s one of two things: either he is out of his mind, or he has

[042-030]purposely cast you off.’

[042-031]Amy laughed, thinking of her husband in the light of the latter

[042-032]alternative.

[042-033]‘There’s no need to seek so far for explanations,’ she said. ‘He has

[042-034]failed, that’s all; just like a man might fail in any other business. He

[042-035]can’t write like he used to. It may be all the result of ill-health; I

[042-036]don’t know. His last book, you see, is positively refused. He has made

[042-037]up his mind that there’s nothing but [[literary-poverty|poverty]] before him, and he can’t

[042-038]understand why I should object to live like the wife of a working-man.’

[042-039]‘Well, I only know that he has placed you in an exceedingly difficult

[042-040]position. If he had gone away to Worthing for the summer we might have

[042-041]made it seem natural; people are always ready to allow literary men to

[042-042]do rather odd things--up to a certain point. We should have behaved as

[042-043]if there were nothing that called for explanation. But what are we to do

[042-044]now?’

[042-045]Like her multitudinous kind, Mrs Yule lived only in the opinions of

[042-046]other people. What others would say was her ceaseless preoccupation.

[042-047]She had never conceived of life as something proper to the individual;

[042-048]independence in the directing of one’s course seemed to her only

[042-049]possible in the case of very eccentric persons, or of such as were

[042-050]altogether out of society. Amy had advanced, intellectually, far beyond

[042-051]this standpoint, but lack of courage disabled her from acting upon her

[042-052]convictions.

[042-053]‘People must know the truth, I suppose,’ she answered dispiritedly.

[042-054]Now, confession of the truth was the last thing that would occur to Mrs

[042-055]Yule when social relations were concerned. Her whole existence was based

[042-056]on bold denial of actualities. And, as is natural in such persons, she

[042-057]had the ostrich instinct strongly developed; though very acute in

[042-058]the discovery of her friends’ shams and lies, she deceived herself

[042-059]ludicrously in the matter of concealing her own embarrassments.

[042-060]‘But the fact is, my dear,’ she answered, ‘we don’t know the truth

[042-061]ourselves. You had better let yourself be directed by me. It will be

[042-062]better, at first, if you see as few people as possible. I suppose you

[042-063]must say something or other to two or three of your own friends; if you

[042-064]take my advice you’ll be rather mysterious. Let them think what they

[042-065]like; anything is better than to say plainly. “My husband can’t support

[042-066]me, and he has gone to work as a clerk for weekly wages.” Be mysterious,

[042-067]darling; depend upon it, that’s the safest.’

[042-068]The conversation was pursued, with brief intervals, all through the

[042-069]day. In the afternoon two ladies paid a call, but Amy kept out of

[042-070]sight. Between six and seven [[john-yule|John Yule]] returned from his gentlemanly

[042-071]occupations. As he was generally in a touchy temper before dinner had

[042-072]soothed him, nothing was said to him of the latest development of his

[042-073]sister’s affairs until late in the evening; he was allowed to suppose

[042-074]that [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s departure for the seaside had taken place a day sooner

[042-075]than had been arranged.

[042-076]Behind the dining-room was a comfortable little chamber set apart as

[042-077]John’s sanctum; here he smoked and entertained his male friends, and

[042-078]contemplated the portraits of those female ones who would not have been

[042-079]altogether at their ease in Mrs Yule’s drawing-room. Not long after

[042-080]dinner his mother and sister came to talk with him in this retreat.

[042-081]With some nervousness Mrs Yule made known to him what had taken place.

[042-082]Amy, the while, stood by the table, and glanced over a magazine that she

[042-083]had picked up.

[042-084]‘Well, I see nothing to be surprised at,’ was John’s first remark. ‘It

[042-085]was pretty certain he’d come to this. But what I want to know is, how

[042-086]long are we to be at the expense of supporting Amy and her youngster?’

[042-087]This was practical, and just what Mrs Yule had expected from her son.

[042-088]‘We can’t consider such things as that,’ she replied. ‘You don’t wish, I

[042-089]suppose, that Amy should go and live in a back street at [[islington|Islington]], and

[042-090]be hungry every other day, and soon have no decent clothes?’

[042-091]‘I don’t think Jack would be greatly distressed,’ Amy put in quietly.

[042-092]‘This is a woman’s way of talking,’ replied John. ‘I want to know what

[042-093]is to be the end of it all? I’ve no doubt it’s uncommonly pleasant for

[042-094]Reardon to shift his responsibilities on to our shoulders. At this rate

[042-095]I think I shall get married, and live beyond my means until I can hold

[042-096]out no longer, and then hand my wife over to her relatives, with my

[042-097]compliments. It’s about the coolest business that ever came under my

[042-098]notice.’

[042-099]‘But what is to be done?’ asked Mrs Yule. ‘It’s no use talking

[042-100]sarcastically, John, or making yourself disagreeable.’

[042-101]‘We are not called upon to find a way out of the difficulty. The fact of

[042-102]the matter is, Reardon must get a decent berth. Somebody or other must

[042-103]pitch him into the kind of place that suits men who can do nothing in

[042-104]particular. [[carter|Carter]] ought to be able to help, I should think.’

[042-105]‘You know very well,’ said Amy, ‘that places of that kind are not to be

[042-106]had for the asking. It may be years before any such opportunity offers.’

[042-107]‘Confound the fellow! Why the deuce doesn’t he go on with his

[042-108]novel-writing? There’s plenty of [[money-in-literature|money]] to be made out of novels.’

[042-109]‘But he can’t write, Jack. He has lost his talent.’

[042-110]‘That’s all bosh, Amy. If a fellow has once got into the swing of it he

[042-111]can keep it up if he likes. He might write his two novels a year easily

[042-112]enough, just like twenty other men and women. Look here, I could do it

[042-113]myself if I weren’t too lazy. And that’s what’s the matter with Reardon.

[042-114]He doesn’t care to work.’

[042-115]‘I have thought that myself;’ observed Mrs Yule. ‘It really is too

[042-116]ridiculous to say that he couldn’t write some kind of novels if he

[042-117]chose. Look at Miss Blunt’s last book; why, anybody could have written

[042-118]that. I’m sure there isn’t a thing in it I couldn’t have imagined

[042-119]myself.’

[042-120]‘Well, all I want to know is, what’s Amy going to do if things don’t

[042-121]alter?’

[042-122]‘She shall never want a home as long as I have one to share with her.’

[042-123]John’s natural procedure, when beset by difficulties, was to find

[042-124]fault with everyone all round, himself maintaining a position of

[042-125]irresponsibility.

[042-126]‘It’s all very well, mother, but when a girl gets married she takes her

[042-127]husband, I have always understood, for better or worse, just as a man

[042-128]takes his wife. To tell the truth, it seems to me Amy has put herself in

[042-129]the wrong. It’s deuced unpleasant to go and live in back streets, and

[042-130]to go without dinner now and then, but girls mustn’t marry if they’re

[042-131]afraid to face these things.’

[042-132]‘Don’t talk so monstrously, John!’ exclaimed his mother. ‘How could Amy

[042-133]possibly foresee such things? The case is quite an extraordinary one.’

[042-134]‘Not so uncommon, I assure you. Some one was telling me the other day of

[042-135]a married lady--well educated and blameless--who goes to work at a shop

[042-136]somewhere or other because her husband can’t support her.’

[042-137]‘And you wish to see Amy working in a shop?’

[042-138]‘No, I can’t say I do. I’m only telling you that her bad luck isn’t

[042-139]unexampled. It’s very fortunate for her that she has good-natured

[042-140]relatives.’

[042-141]Amy had taken a seat apart. She sat with her head leaning on her hand.

[042-142]‘Why don’t you go and see Reardon?’ John asked of his mother.

[042-143]‘What would be the use? Perhaps he would tell me to mind my own

[042-144]business.’

[042-145]‘By jingo! precisely what you would be doing. I think you ought to see

[042-146]him and give him to understand that he’s behaving in a confoundedly

[042-147]ungentlemanly way. Evidently he’s the kind of fellow that wants stirring

[042-148]up. I’ve half a mind to go and see him myself. Where is this slum that

[042-149]he’s gone to live in?’

[042-150]‘We don’t know his address yet.’

[042-151]‘So long as it’s not the kind of place where one would be afraid of

[042-152]catching a fever, I think it wouldn’t be amiss for me to look him up.’

[042-153]‘You’ll do no good by that,’ said Amy, indifferently.

[042-154]‘Confound it! It’s just because nobody does anything that things have

[042-155]come to this pass!’

[042-156]The conversation was, of course, profitless. John could only return

[042-157]again and again to his assertion that Reardon must get ‘a decent berth.’

[042-158]At length Amy left the room in weariness and disgust.

[042-159]‘I suppose they have quarrelled terrifically,’ said her brother, as soon

[042-160]as she was gone.

[042-161]‘I am afraid so.’

[042-162]‘Well, you must do as you please. But it’s confounded hard lines that

[042-163]you should have to keep her and the kid. You know I can’t afford to

[042-164]contribute.’

[042-165]‘My dear, I haven’t asked you to.’

[042-166]‘No, but you’ll have the devil’s own job to make ends meet; I know that

[042-167]well enough.’

[042-168]‘I shall manage somehow.’

[042-169]‘All right; you’re a plucky woman, but it’s too bad. Reardon’s a humbug,

[042-170]that’s my opinion. I shall have a talk with Carter about him. I suppose

[042-171]he has transferred all their furniture to the slum?’

[042-172]‘He can’t have removed yet. It was only this morning that he went to

[042-173]search for lodgings.’

[042-174]‘Oh, then I tell you what it is: I shall look in there the first thing

[042-175]to-morrow morning, and just talk to him in a fatherly way. You needn’t

[042-176]say anything to Amy. But I see he’s just the kind of fellow that,

[042-177]if everyone leaves him alone, he’ll be content with Carter’s

[042-178]five-and-twenty shillings for the rest of his life, and never trouble

[042-179]his head about how Amy is living.’

[042-180]To this proposal Mrs Yule readily assented. On going upstairs she found

[042-181]that Amy had all but fallen asleep upon a settee in the drawing-room.

[042-182]‘You are quite worn out with your troubles,’ she said. ‘Go to bed, and

[042-183]have a good long sleep.’

[042-184]‘Yes, I will.’

[042-185]The neat, fresh bedchamber seemed to Amy a delightful haven of rest. She

[042-186]turned the key in the door with an enjoyment of the privacy thus secured

[042-187]such as she had never known in her life; for in maidenhood safe solitude

[042-188]was a matter of course to her, and since [[literary-marriage|marriage]] she had not passed a

[042-189]night alone. Willie was fast asleep in a little bed shadowed by her own.

[042-190]In an impulse of maternal love and gladness she bent over the child and

[042-191]covered his face with kisses too gentle to awaken him.
