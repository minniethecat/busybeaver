# Section 60

[059-001]It was by force of commiserating his own lot that [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]]

[059-002]continued to live through the first month after his parting from [[amy-reardon|Amy]].

[059-003]Once or twice a week, sometimes early in the evening, sometimes at

[059-004]midnight or later, he haunted the street at Westbourne Park where his

[059-005]wife was dwelling, and on each occasion he returned to his garret with

[059-006]a fortified sense of the injustice to which he was submitted, of revolt

[059-007]against the circumstances which had driven him into outer darkness, of

[059-008]bitterness against his wife for saving her own comfort rather than

[059-009]share his downfall. At times he was not far from that state of sheer

[059-010]distraction which [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]] preferred to suppose that he had

[059-011]reached. An extraordinary arrogance now and then possessed him; he stood

[059-012]amid his poor surroundings with the sensations of an outraged exile, and

[059-013]laughed aloud in furious contempt of all who censured or pitied him.

[059-014]On hearing from [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]] that Amy had fallen ill, or at all

[059-015]events was suffering in health from what she had gone through, he felt

[059-016]a momentary pang which all but determined him to hasten to her side. The

[059-017]reaction was a feeling of distinct pleasure that she had her share of

[059-018]pain, and even a hope that her illness might become grave; he pictured

[059-019]himself summoned to her sick chamber, imagined her begging his

[059-020]forgiveness. But it was not merely, nor in great part, a malicious

[059-021]satisfaction; he succeeded in believing that Amy suffered because she

[059-022]still had a remnant of love for him. As the days went by and he heard

[059-023]nothing, disappointment and resentment occupied him. At length he ceased

[059-024]to haunt the neighbourhood. His desires grew sullen; he became fixed in

[059-025]the resolve to hold entirely apart and doggedly await the issue.

[059-026]At the end of each month he sent half the [[money-in-literature|money]] he had received from

[059-027][[carter|Carter]], simply enclosing postal orders in an envelope addressed to his

[059-028]wife. The first two remittances were in no way acknowledged; the third

[059-029]brought a short note from Amy:

[059-030]‘As you continue to send these sums of money, I had perhaps better let

[059-031]you know that I cannot use them for any purposes of my own. Perhaps a

[059-032]sense of duty leads you to make this sacrifice, but I am afraid it

[059-033]is more likely that you wish to remind me every month that you are

[059-034]undergoing privations, and to pain me in this way. What you have sent I

[059-035]have deposited in the Post Office Savings’ Bank in Willie’s name, and I

[059-036]shall continue to do so.--A.R.’

[059-037]For a day or two Reardon persevered in an intention of not replying, but

[059-038]the desire to utter his turbid feelings became in the end too strong. He

[059-039]wrote:

[059-040]‘I regard it as quite natural that you should put the worst

[059-041]interpretation on whatever I do. As for my privations, I think very

[059-042]little of them; they are a trifle in comparison with the thought that

[059-043]I am forsaken just because my pocket is empty. And I am far indeed from

[059-044]thinking that you can be pained by whatever I may undergo; that would

[059-045]suppose some generosity in your nature.’

[059-046]This was no sooner posted than he would gladly have recalled it. He knew

[059-047]that it was undignified, that it contained as many falsehoods as lines,

[059-048]and he was ashamed of himself for having written so. But he could not

[059-049]pen a letter of retractation, and there remained with him a new cause of

[059-050]exasperated wretchedness.

[059-051]Excepting the people with whom he came in contact at the hospital, he

[059-052]had no society but that of [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]. The realist visited him once a

[059-053]week, and this friendship grew closer than it had been in the time of

[059-054]Reardon’s prosperity. Biffen was a man of so much natural delicacy, that

[059-055]there was a pleasure in imparting to him the details of private sorrow;

[059-056]though profoundly sympathetic, he did his best to oppose Reardon’s

[059-057]harsher judgments of Amy, and herein he gave his friend a satisfaction

[059-058]which might not be avowed.

[059-059]‘I really do not see,’ he exclaimed, as they sat in the garret one night

[059-060]of midsummer, ‘how your wife could have acted otherwise. Of course I

[059-061]am quite unable to judge the attitude of her mind, but I think, I can’t

[059-062]help thinking, from what I knew of her, that there has been strictly a

[059-063]misunderstanding between you.

[059-064]It was a hard and miserable thing that she should have to leave you for

[059-065]a time, and you couldn’t face the necessity in a just spirit. Don’t you

[059-066]think there’s some truth in this way of looking at it?’

[059-067]‘As a woman, it was her part to soften the hateful necessity; she made

[059-068]it worse.’

[059-069]‘I’m not sure that you don’t demand too much of her. Unhappily, I know

[059-070]little or nothing of delicately-bred women, but I have a suspicion that

[059-071]one oughtn’t to expect heroism in them, any more than in the women of

[059-072]the lower classes. I think of women as creatures to be protected. Is a

[059-073]man justified in asking them to be stronger than himself?’

[059-074]‘Of course,’ replied Reardon, ‘there’s no use in demanding more than

[059-075]a character is capable of. But I believed her of finer stuff. My

[059-076]bitterness comes of the disappointment.’

[059-077]‘I suppose there were faults of temper on both sides, and you saw at

[059-078]last only each other’s weaknesses.’

[059-079]‘I saw the truth, which had always been disguised from me.’ Biffen

[059-080]persisted in looking doubtful, and in secret Reardon thanked him for it.

[059-081]As the realist progressed with his novel, ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer,’ he read

[059-082]the chapters to Reardon, not only for his own satisfaction, but in great

[059-083]part because he hoped that this example of productivity might in the end

[059-084]encourage the listener to resume his own literary tasks. Reardon found

[059-085]much to criticise in his friend’s work; it was noteworthy that he

[059-086]objected and condemned with much less hesitation than in his better

[059-087]days, for sensitive reticence is one of the virtues wont to be assailed

[059-088]by suffering, at all events in the weaker natures. Biffen purposely

[059-089]urged these discussions as far as possible, and doubtless they benefited

[059-090]Reardon for the time; but the defeated novelist could not be induced to

[059-091]undertake another practical illustration of his own views. Occasionally

[059-092]he had an impulse to plan a story, but an hour’s turning it over in his

[059-093]mind sufficed to disgust him. His ideas seemed barren, vapid; it would

[059-094]have been impossible for him to write half a dozen pages, and the mere

[059-095]thought of a whole book overcame him with the dread of insurmountable

[059-096]difficulties, immeasurable toil.

[059-097]In time, however, he was able to read. He had a pleasure in

[059-098]contemplating the little collection of sterling books that alone

[059-099]remained to him from his library; the sight of many volumes would have

[059-100]been a weariness, but these few--when he was again able to think

[059-101]of books at all--were as friendly countenances. He could not read

[059-102]continuously, but sometimes he opened his Shakespeare, for instance,

[059-103]and dreamed over a page or two. From such glimpses there remained in

[059-104]his head a line or a short passage, which he kept repeating to himself

[059-105]wherever he went; generally some example of sweet or sonorous metre

[059-106]which had a soothing effect upon him.

[059-107]With odd result on one occasion. He was walking in one of the back

[059-108]streets of [[islington|Islington]], and stopped idly to gaze into the window of some

[059-109]small shop. Standing thus, he forgot himself and presently recited

[059-110]aloud:

[059-111]‘Caesar, ‘tis his schoolmaster:

[059-112]An argument that he is pluck’d, when hither

[059-113]He sends so poor a pinion of his wing,

[059-114]Which had superfluous kings for messengers

[059-115]Not many moons gone by.’

[059-116]The last two lines he uttered a second time, enjoying their magnificent

[059-117]sound, and then was brought back to consciousness by the loud mocking

[059-118]laugh of two men standing close by, who evidently looked upon him as a

[059-119]strayed lunatic.

[059-120]He kept one suit of clothes for his hours of attendance at the hospital;

[059-121]it was still decent, and with much care would remain so for a long time.

[059-122]That which he wore at home and in his street wanderings declared [[literary-poverty|poverty]]

[059-123]at every point; it had been discarded before he left the old abode. In

[059-124]his present state of mind he cared nothing how disreputable he looked to

[059-125]passers-by. These seedy habiliments were the token of his degradation,

[059-126]and at times he regarded them (happening to see himself in a shop

[059-127]mirror) with pleasurable contempt. The same spirit often led him for a

[059-128]meal to the poorest of eating-houses, places where he rubbed elbows with

[059-129]ragged creatures who had somehow obtained the price of a cup of coffee

[059-130]and a slice of bread and butter. He liked to contrast himself with these

[059-131]comrades in misfortune. ‘This is the rate at which the world esteems

[059-132]me; I am worth no better provision than this.’ Or else, instead of

[059-133]emphasising the contrast, he defiantly took a place among the miserables

[059-134]of the nether world, and nursed hatred of all who were well-to-do.

[059-135]One of these he desired to regard with gratitude, but found it difficult

[059-136]to support that feeling. Carter, the vivacious, though at first

[059-137]perfectly unembarrassed in his relations with the City Road clerk,

[059-138]gradually exhibited a change of demeanour. Reardon occasionally found

[059-139]the young man’s eye fixed upon him with a singular expression, and the

[059-140]secretary’s talk, though still as a rule genial, was wont to suffer

[059-141]curious interruptions, during which he seemed to be musing on something

[059-142]Reardon had said, or on some point of his behaviour. The explanation of

[059-143]this was that Carter had begun to think there might be a foundation for

[059-144]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s hypothesis--that the novelist was not altogether in his sound

[059-145]senses. At first he scouted the idea, but as time went on it seemed

[059-146]to him that Reardon’s countenance certainly had a gaunt wildness which

[059-147]suggested disagreeable things. Especially did he remark this after his

[059-148]return from an August holiday in Norway. On coming for the first time

[059-149]to the City Road branch he sat down and began to favour Reardon with

[059-150]a lively description of how he had enjoyed himself abroad; it never

[059-151]occurred to him that such talk was not likely to inspirit the man

[059-152]who had passed his August between the garret and the hospital, but he

[059-153]observed before long that his listener was glancing hither and thither

[059-154]in rather a strange way.

[059-155]‘You haven’t been ill since I saw you?’ he inquired.

[059-156]‘Oh no!’

[059-157]‘But you look as if you might have been. I say, we must manage for you

[059-158]to have a fortnight off, you know, this month.’

[059-159]‘I have no wish for it,’ said Reardon. ‘I’ll imagine I have been to

[059-160]Norway. It has done me good to hear of your holiday.’

[059-161]‘I’m glad of that; but it isn’t quite the same thing, you know, as

[059-162]having a run somewhere yourself.’

[059-163]‘Oh, much better! To enjoy myself may be mere selfishness, but to enjoy

[059-164]another’s enjoyment is the purest satisfaction, good for body and soul.

[059-165]I am cultivating altruism.’

[059-166]‘What’s that?’

[059-167]‘A highly rarefied form of happiness. The curious thing about it is

[059-168]that it won’t grow unless you have just twice as much faith in it as is

[059-169]required for assent to the Athanasian Creed.’

[059-170]‘Oh!’

[059-171]Carter went away more than puzzled. He told his wife that evening that

[059-172]Reardon had been talking to him in the most extraordinary fashion--no

[059-173]understanding a word he said.

[059-174]All this time he was on the look-out for employment that would be more

[059-175]suitable to his unfortunate clerk. Whether slightly demented or not,

[059-176]Reardon gave no sign of inability to discharge his duties; he was

[059-177]conscientious as ever, and might, unless he changed greatly, be relied

[059-178]upon in positions of more responsibility than his present one. And at

[059-179]length, early in October, there came to the secretary’s knowledge an

[059-180]opportunity with which he lost no time in acquainting Reardon. The

[059-181]latter repaired that evening to Clipstone Street, and climbed to

[059-182]Biffen’s chamber. He entered with a cheerful look, and exclaimed:
