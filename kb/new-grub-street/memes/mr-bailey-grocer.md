# Mr Bailey, Grocer

[[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]]'s novel of uncompromising [[literary-realism|realism]], depicting the true story of a grocer's [[literary-marriage|marriage]] and domestic life. The novel embodies [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s artistic creed of absolute fidelity to ordinary life, and its fate in the [[grub-street|literary marketplace]] serves as a central commentary on the tension between artistic integrity and commercial viability.

## Overview

"Mr Bailey, Grocer" is a novel by [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]], a struggling author and close friend of [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]]. [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] describes it as an experiment in [[literary-realism|absolute realism]]: "Bailey is a grocer in a little street" [036-129], and the novel aims "to tell the true story of [[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]]'s [[literary-marriage|marriage]] and of his" life with unvarnished fidelity [036-136]. The work progresses slowly over many months as [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] labours under conditions of extreme [[literary-poverty|poverty]], often going without food or fuel to continue writing [025-045].

## Composition and Realism

[[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s artistic method in "Mr Bailey, Grocer" is defined by his refusal to romanticise or sensationalise his subject. He proudly declares that he will "never write anything like a dramatic" scene, insisting that absolute truthfulness to ordinary experience is the highest literary aim [025-049]. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], though personally fond of [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], privately questions whether such relentless [[literary-realism|realism]] can find an audience: "I admire your honesty, [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]" [025-035].

The novel draws steadily to completion despite [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s deteriorating circumstances. By Chapter 75, "Mr Bailey, Grocer" is nearing its end, and [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] makes "a frenzied effort" to finish it despite his failing health [075-180]. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] rescues the manuscript from a fire in [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s lodgings [076-088], and [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] later tells [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], "You rescued '[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]].' He must repay you" [076-151].

## Publication and Reception

[[harold-biffen|Biffen]] harbours "a superstitious faith in '[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]]'" [077-015], believing it will bring him both critical recognition and financial reward. [[amy-reardon|Amy Reardon]], after handling the manuscript, notes that "her hand bore the title, 'Mr Bailey, Grocer'" [080-076]. The novel is eventually sold, though the terms are modest. The final chapters record that [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] received a modest sum of "pounds for '[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]]'" [086-017], an outcome that underscores the commercial indifference to uncompromising literary [[literary-realism|realism]].

## Thematic Significance

"Mr Bailey, Grocer" functions as the novel's emblem of pure artistic integrity. [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s commitment to realist truth — even at the cost of commercial failure — stands in pointed contrast to [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]'s calculating literary careerism. The novel-within-the-novel asks whether literature should serve truth or the market, a question that lies at the heart of New [[grub-street|Grub Street]]'s exploration of [[literary-culture|literary culture]]. [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s faith that his grocer's story matters, despite its lack of sensational incident, represents Gissing's own sympathy with the realist tradition.
