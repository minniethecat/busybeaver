# Section 64

[063-001]Edith mused. Such speculations were daring, but she had grown accustomed

[063-002]to think of [[amy-reardon|Amy]] as an ‘advanced’ woman, and liked to imitate her in this

[063-003]respect.

[063-004]‘It does seem reasonable,’ she murmured.

[063-005]‘The law ought to encourage such separations, instead of forbidding

[063-006]them,’ Amy pursued. ‘If a husband and wife find that they have made

[063-007]a mistake, what useless cruelty it is to condemn them to suffer the

[063-008]consequences for the whole of their lives!’

[063-009]‘I suppose it’s to make people careful,’ said Edith, with a laugh.

[063-010]‘If so, we know that it has always failed, and always will fail; so the

[063-011]sooner such a profitless law is altered the better. Isn’t there some

[063-012]society for getting that kind of reform? I would subscribe fifty pounds

[063-013]a year to help it. Wouldn’t you?’

[063-014]‘Yes, if I had it to spare,’ replied the other.

[063-015]Then they both laughed, but Edith the more naturally.

[063-016]‘Not on my own account, you know,’ she added.

[063-017]‘It’s because women who are happily married can’t and won’t understand

[063-018]the position of those who are not that there’s so much difficulty in

[063-019]reforming [[literary-marriage|marriage]] laws.’

[063-020]‘But I understand you, Amy, and I grieve about you. What you are to do I

[063-021]can’t think.’

[063-022]‘Oh, it’s easy to see what I shall do. Of course I have no choice

[063-023]really. And I ought to have a choice; that’s the hardship and the wrong

[063-024]of it. Perhaps if I had, I should find a sort of pleasure in sacrificing

[063-025]myself.’

[063-026]There were some new novels on the table; Amy took up a volume presently,

[063-027]and glanced over a page or two.

[063-028]‘I don’t know how you can go on reading that sort of stuff, book after

[063-029]book,’ she exclaimed.

[063-030]‘Oh, but people say this last novel of Markland’s is one of his best.’

[063-031]‘Best or worst, novels are all the same. Nothing but love, love, love;

[063-032]what silly nonsense it is! Why don’t people write about the really

[063-033]important things of life? Some of the French novelists do; several of

[063-034]Balzac’s, for instance. I have just been reading his “Cousin Pons,” a

[063-035]terrible book, but I enjoyed it ever so much because it was nothing like

[063-036]a love story. What rubbish is printed about love!’

[063-037]‘I get rather tired of it sometimes,’ admitted Edith with amusement.

[063-038]‘I should hope you do, indeed. What downright lies are accepted as

[063-039]indisputable! That about love being a woman’s whole life; who believes

[063-040]it really? Love is the most insignificant thing in most women’s lives.

[063-041]It occupies a few months, possibly a year or two, and even then I doubt

[063-042]if it is often the first consideration.’

[063-043]Edith held her head aside, and pondered smilingly.

[063-044]‘I’m sure there’s a great opportunity for some clever novelist who will

[063-045]never write about love at all.’

[063-046]‘But then it does come into life.’

[063-047]‘Yes, for a month or two, as I say. Think of the biographies of men and

[063-048]women; how many pages are devoted to their love affairs? Compare those

[063-049]books with novels which profess to be biographies, and you see how false

[063-050]such pictures are. Think of the very words “novel,” “romance”--what do

[063-051]they mean but exaggeration of one bit of life?’

[063-052]‘That may be true. But why do people find the subject so interesting?’

[063-053]‘Because there is so little love in real life. That’s the truth of

[063-054]it. Why do poor people care only for stories about the rich? The same

[063-055]principle.’

[063-056]‘How clever you are, Amy!’

[063-057]‘Am I? It’s very nice to be told so. Perhaps I have some cleverness of a

[063-058]kind; but what use is it to me? My life is being wasted. I ought to

[063-059]have a place in the society of clever people. I was never meant to live

[063-060]quietly in the background. Oh, if I hadn’t been in such a hurry, and so

[063-061]inexperienced!’

[063-062]‘Oh, I wanted to ask you,’ said Edith, soon after this. ‘Do you wish

[063-063]Albert to say anything about you--at the hospital?’

[063-064]‘There’s no reason why he shouldn’t.’

[063-065]‘You won’t even write to say--?’

[063-066]‘I shall do nothing.’

[063-067]Since the parting from her husband, there had proceeded in Amy

[063-068]a noticeable maturing of intellect. Probably the one thing was a

[063-069]consequence of the other. During that last year in the flat her mind

[063-070]was held captive by material cares, and this arrest of her natural

[063-071]development doubtless had much to do with the appearance of acerbity

[063-072]in a character which had displayed so much sweetness, so much womanly

[063-073]grace. Moreover, it was arrest at a critical point. When she fell in

[063-074]love with [[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]] her mind had still to undergo the [[literary-culture|culture]] of

[063-075]circumstances; though a woman in years she had seen nothing of life but

[063-076]a few phases of artificial society, and her education had not progressed

[063-077]beyond the final schoolgirl stage. Submitting herself to Reardon’s

[063-078]influence, she passed through what was a highly useful training of the

[063-079]intellect; but with the result that she became clearly conscious of the

[063-080]divergence between herself and her husband. In endeavouring to imbue her

[063-081]with his own literary tastes, Reardon instructed Amy as to the natural

[063-082]tendencies of her mind, which till then she had not clearly understood.

[063-083]When she ceased to read with the eyes of passion, most of the things

[063-084]which were Reardon’s supreme interests lost their value for her. A sound

[063-085]intelligence enabled her to think and feel in many directions, but the

[063-086]special line of her growth lay apart from that in which the novelist and

[063-087]classical scholar had directed her.

[063-088]When she found herself alone and independent, her mind acted like a

[063-089]spring when pressure is removed. After a few weeks of desoeuvrement she

[063-090]obeyed the impulse to occupy herself with a kind of reading alien

[063-091]to Reardon’s sympathies. The solid periodicals attracted her, and

[063-092]especially those articles which dealt with themes of social science.

[063-093]Anything that savoured of newness and boldness in philosophic thought

[063-094]had a charm for her palate. She read a good deal of that kind of

[063-095]literature which may be defined as specialism popularised; writing which

[063-096]addresses itself to educated, but not strictly studious, persons, and

[063-097]which forms the reservoir of conversation for society above the sphere

[063-098]of turf and west-endism. Thus, for instance, though she could not

[063-099]undertake the volumes of Herbert Spencer, she was intelligently

[063-100]acquainted with the tenor of their contents; and though she had never

[063-101]opened one of Darwin’s books, her knowledge of his main theories and

[063-102]illustrations was respectable. She was becoming a typical woman of the

[063-103]new time, the woman who has developed concurrently with journalistic

[063-104]enterprise.

[063-105]Not many days after that conversation with Edith [[carter|Carter]], she had

[063-106]occasion to visit Mudie’s, for the new number of some periodical which

[063-107]contained an appetising title. As it was a sunny and warm day she walked

[063-108]to New [[oxford|Oxford]] Street from the nearest Metropolitan station. Whilst

[063-109]waiting at the library counter, she heard a familiar voice in her

[063-110]proximity; it was that of [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]], who stood talking with a

[063-111]middle-aged lady. As Amy turned to look at him his eye met hers; clearly

[063-112]he had been aware of her. The review she desired was handed to her; she

[063-113]moved aside, and turned over the pages. Then Milvain walked up.

[063-114]He was armed cap-a-pie in the fashions of suave society; no Bohemianism

[063-115]of garb or person, for Jasper knew he could not afford that kind of

[063-116]economy. On her part, Amy was much better dressed than usual, a costume

[063-117]suited to her position of bereaved heiress.

[063-118]‘What a time since we met!’ said Jasper, taking her delicately gloved

[063-119]hand and looking into her face with his most effective smile.

[063-120]‘And why?’ asked Amy.

[063-121]‘Indeed, I hardly know. I hope Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] is well?’

[063-122]‘Quite, thank you.’

[063-123]It seemed as if he would draw back to let her pass, and so make an end

[063-124]of the colloquy. But Amy, though she moved forward, added a remark:

[063-125]‘I don’t see your name in any of this month’s magazines.’

[063-126]‘I have nothing signed this month. A short review in [[the-current|The Current]], that’s

[063-127]all.’

[063-128]‘But I suppose you write as much as ever?’

[063-129]‘Yes; but chiefly in weekly papers just now. You don’t see the

[063-130]Will-o’-the-Wisp?’

[063-131]‘Oh yes. And I think I can generally recognise your hand.’

[063-132]They issued from the library.

[063-133]‘Which way are you going?’ Jasper inquired, with something more of the

[063-134]old freedom.

[063-135]‘I walked from [[gorer-street|Gower Street]] station, and I think, as it’s so fine, I

[063-136]shall walk back again.’

[063-137]He accompanied her. They turned up Museum Street, and Amy, after a short

[063-138]silence, made inquiry concerning his sisters.

[063-139]‘I am sorry I saw them only once, but no doubt you thought it better to

[063-140]let the acquaintance end there.’

[063-141]‘I really didn’t think of it in that way at all,’ Jasper replied.

[063-142]’We naturally understood it so, when you even ceased to call, yourself.’

[063-143]‘But don’t you feel that there would have been a good deal of

[063-144]awkwardness in my coming to Mrs Yule’s?’

[063-145]‘Seeing that you looked at things from my husband’s point of view?’

[063-146]‘Oh, that’s a mistake! I have only seen your husband once since he went

[063-147]to [[islington|Islington]].’

[063-148]Amy gave him a look of surprise.

[063-149]‘You are not on friendly terms with him?’

[063-150]‘Well, we have drifted apart. For some reason he seemed to think that my

[063-151]companionship was not very profitable. So it was better, on the whole,

[063-152]that I should see neither you nor him.’

[063-153]Amy was wondering whether he had heard of her legacy. He might have been

[063-154]informed by a [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] correspondent, even if no one in [[london|London]] had

[063-155]told him.

[063-156]‘Do your sisters keep up their friendship with my cousin [[marian-yule|Marian]]?’ she

[063-157]asked, quitting the previous difficult topic.

[063-158]‘Oh yes!’ He smiled. ‘They see a great deal of each other.’

[063-159]‘Then of course you have heard of my uncle’s death?’

[063-160]‘Yes. I hope all your difficulties are now at an end.’

[063-161]Amy delayed a moment, then said: ‘I hope so,’ without any emphasis.

[063-162]‘Do you think of spending this winter abroad?’

[063-163]It was the nearest he could come to a question concerning the future of

[063-164]Amy and her husband.

[063-165]‘Everything is still quite uncertain. But tell me something about our

[063-166]old acquaintances. How does [[harold-biffen|Mr Biffen]] get on?’

[063-167]‘I scarcely ever see him, but I think he pegs away at an interminable

[063-168]novel, which no one will publish when it’s done. [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] I meet

[063-169]occasionally.’

[063-170]He talked of the latter’s projects and achievements in a lively strain.

[063-171]‘Your own prospects continue to brighten, no doubt,’ said Amy.

[063-172]‘I really think they do. Things go fairly well. And I have lately

[063-173]received a promise of very valuable help.’

[063-174]‘From whom?’

[063-175]‘A relative of yours.’

[063-176]Amy turned to interrogate him with a look.

[063-177]‘A relative? You mean--?’

[063-178]‘Yes; Marian.’

[063-179]They were passing Bedford Square. Amy glanced at the trees, now

[063-180]almost bare of foliage; then her eyes met Jasper’s, and she smiled

[063-181]significantly.

[063-182]‘I should have thought your aim would have been far more ambitious,’ she

[063-183]said, with distinct utterance.

[063-184]‘Marian and I have been engaged for some time--practically.’

[063-185]‘Indeed? I remember now how you once spoke of her. And you will be

[063-186]married soon?’

[063-187]‘Probably before the end of the year. I see that you are criticising my

[063-188]motives. I am quite prepared for that in everyone who knows me and the

[063-189]circumstances. But you must remember that I couldn’t foresee anything of

[063-190]this kind. It enables us to marry sooner, that’s all.’

[063-191]‘I am sure your motives are unassailable,’ replied Amy, still with a

[063-192]smile. ‘I imagined that you wouldn’t marry for years, and then some

[063-193]distinguished person. This throws new light upon your character.’

[063-194]‘You thought me so desperately scheming and cold-blooded?’

[063-195]‘Oh dear no! But--well, to be sure, I can’t say that I know Marian. I

[063-196]haven’t seen her for years and years. She may be admirably suited to

[063-197]you.’
