# Putney

Putney is a district in southwest [[london|London]], south of the River Thames, that appears in the closing chapters of the novel as the site of [[harold-biffen]]'s suicide. Unlike other [[london|London]] locations that represent the daily grind of literary labour, Putney marks the terminal point — a place at the edge of the city where a character's struggle comes to its abrupt and tragic end.

## 位置与特征

Putney lies on the south bank of the Thames, reached by crossing the river from Fulham. Putney Hill rises from the river valley, and Putney Heath stretches beyond — open ground on the edge of the city. The area's semi-rural character, removed from the press of central [[london|London]], makes it a place of isolation rather than community.

## 小说中的出场

Putney appears in a single concentrated sequence. A character "crossed the Thames to Putney" (087-004), making his way "up Putney Hill" (087-007), walking slowly. The destination is Putney Heath, where "poison had been found" (087-080) and papers in the deceased's pocket identify him. The geography of the sequence — river crossing, hill ascent, heath — traces a progress from the city toward its margin, from life toward its end.

## 叙事功能

Putney's narrative function is starkly singular: it is the place where [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]], a scholar and writer who has been [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]'s closest friend, takes his own life. The choice of Putney Heath — open, exposed, on the edge of [[london|London]] — mirrors [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]'s own position: a man of genuine learning and sensibility who has been pushed to the margins of a literary world that values commerce over craft. That the novel's geography culminates in this southwest [[london|London]] heath underscores the fatal logic of [[literary-poverty]].

## 象征意义

Putney represents the literal and figurative end of the road. The crossing of the Thames is a passage to the other side — the far bank from which there is no return. Putney Hill, climbed slowly, is a final ascent. Putney Heath, where the body is found, is open ground without shelter or concealment. Together these elements form a geography of finality that gives physical shape to the novel's bleakest thematic conclusion.
