# Section 6

[005-001]‘You hear?’

[005-002][[marian-yule|Marian]] had just caught the far-off sound of the train. She looked

[005-003]eagerly, and in a few moments saw it approaching. The front of the

[005-004]engine blackened nearer and nearer, coming on with dread force and

[005-005]speed. A blinding rush, and there burst against the bridge a great

[005-006]volley of sunlit steam. [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] and his companion ran to the opposite

[005-007]parapet, but already the whole train had emerged, and in a few seconds

[005-008]it had disappeared round a sharp curve. The leafy branches that grew out

[005-009]over the line swayed violently backwards and forwards in the perturbed

[005-010]air.

[005-011]‘If I were ten years younger,’ said Jasper, laughing, ‘I should say that

[005-012]was jolly! It enspirits me. It makes me feel eager to go back and plunge

[005-013]into the fight again.’

[005-014]‘Upon me it has just the opposite effect,’ fell from Marian, in very low

[005-015]tones.

[005-016]‘Oh, don’t say that! Well, it only means that you haven’t had enough

[005-017]holiday yet. I have been in the country more than a week; a few days

[005-018]more and I must be off. How long do you think of staying?’

[005-019]‘Not much more than a week, I think.’

[005-020]‘By-the-bye, you are coming to have tea with us to-morrow,’ Jasper

[005-021]remarked a propos of nothing. Then he returned to another subject that

[005-022]was in his thoughts.

[005-023]‘It was by a train like that that I first went up to [[london|London]]. Not really

[005-024]the first time; I mean when I went to live there, seven years ago.

[005-025]What spirits I was in! A boy of eighteen going to live independently in

[005-026]London; think of it!’

[005-027]‘You went straight from school?’

[005-028]‘I was for two years at Redmayne College after leaving [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]]

[005-029]Grammar School. Then my father died, and I spent nearly half a year at

[005-030]home. I was meant to be a teacher, but the prospect of entering a school

[005-031]by no means appealed to me. A friend of mine was studying in London for

[005-032]some Civil Service exam., so I declared that I would go and do the same

[005-033]thing.’

[005-034]‘Did you succeed?’

[005-035]‘Not I! I never worked properly for that kind of thing. I read

[005-036]voraciously, and got to know London. I might have gone to the dogs, you

[005-037]know; but by when I had been in London a year a pretty clear purpose

[005-038]began to form in me. Strange to think that you were growing up there all

[005-039]the time. I may have passed you in the street now and then.’

[005-040]Marian laughed.

[005-041]‘And I did at length see you at the [[british-museum|British Museum]], you know.’

[005-042]They turned a corner of the road, and came full upon Marian’s father,

[005-043]who was walking in this direction with eyes fixed upon the ground.

[005-044]‘So here you are!’ he exclaimed, looking at the girl, and for the moment

[005-045]paying no attention to Jasper. ‘I wondered whether I should meet you.’

[005-046]Then, more dryly, ‘How do you do, Mr Milvain?’

[005-047]In a tone of easy indifference Jasper explained how he came to be

[005-048]accompanying Miss [[alfred-yule|Yule]].

[005-049]‘Shall I walk on with you, father?’ Marian asked, scrutinising his

[005-050]rugged features.

[005-051]‘Just as you please; I don’t know that I should have gone much further.

[005-052]But we might take another way back.’

[005-053]Jasper readily adapted himself to the wish he discerned in Mr Yule; at

[005-054]once he offered leave-taking in the most natural way. Nothing was said

[005-055]on either side about another meeting.

[005-056]The young man proceeded homewards, but, on arriving, did not at once

[005-057]enter the house. Behind the garden was a field used for the grazing of

[005-058]horses; he entered it by the unfastened gate, and strolled idly hither

[005-059]and thither, now and then standing to observe a poor worn-out beast, all

[005-060]skin and bone, which had presumably been sent here in the hope that a

[005-061]little more labour might still be exacted from it if it were suffered

[005-062]to repose for a few weeks. There were sores upon its back and legs; it

[005-063]stood in a fixed attitude of despondency, just flicking away troublesome

[005-064]flies with its grizzled tail.

[005-065]It was tea-time when he went in. Maud was not at home, and Mrs Milvain,

[005-066]tormented by a familiar headache, kept her room; so Jasper and [[dora-milvain|Dora]] sat

[005-067]down together. Each had an open book on the table; throughout the meal

[005-068]they exchanged only a few words.

[005-069]‘Going to play a little?’ Jasper suggested when they had gone into the

[005-070]sitting-room.

[005-071]‘If you like.’

[005-072]She sat down at the piano, whilst her brother lay on the sofa, his

[005-073]hands clasped beneath his head. Dora did not play badly, but an

[005-074]absentmindedness which was commonly observable in her had its effect

[005-075]upon the music. She at length broke off idly in the middle of a passage,

[005-076]and began to linger on careless chords. Then, without turning her head,

[005-077]she asked:

[005-078]‘Were you serious in what you said about writing storybooks?’

[005-079]‘Quite. I see no reason why you shouldn’t do something in that way. But

[005-080]I tell you what; when I get back, I’ll inquire into the state of the

[005-081]market. I know a man who was once engaged at Jolly & Monk’s--the chief

[005-082][[publishing-trade|publishers]] of that kind of thing, you know; I must look him up--what a

[005-083]mistake it is to neglect any acquaintance!--and get some information out

[005-084]of him. But it’s obvious what an immense field there is for anyone who

[005-085]can just hit the taste of the new generation of Board school children.

[005-086]Mustn’t be too goody-goody; that kind of thing is falling out of date.

[005-087]But you’d have to cultivate a particular kind of vulgarity.

[005-088]There’s an idea, by-the-bye. I’ll write a paper on the characteristics

[005-089]of that new generation; it may bring me a few guineas, and it would be a

[005-090]help to you.’

[005-091]‘But what do you know about the subject?’ asked Dora doubtfully.

[005-092]‘What a comical question! It is my business to know something about

[005-093]every subject--or to know where to get the knowledge.’

[005-094]‘Well,’ said Dora, after a pause, ‘there’s no doubt Maud and I ought

[005-095]to think very seriously about the future. You are aware, Jasper, that

[005-096]mother has not been able to save a penny of her income.’

[005-097]‘I don’t see how she could have done. Of course I know what you’re

[005-098]thinking; but for me, it would have been possible. I don’t mind

[005-099]confessing to you that the thought troubles me a little now and then;

[005-100]I shouldn’t like to see you two going off governessing in strangers’

[005-101]houses. All I can say is, that I am very honestly working for the end

[005-102]which I am convinced will be most profitable.

[005-103]I shall not desert you; you needn’t fear that. But just put your heads

[005-104]together, and cultivate your writing faculty. Suppose you could both

[005-105]together earn about a hundred a year in [[grub-street|Grub Street]], it would be better

[005-106]than governessing; wouldn’t it?’

[005-107]‘You say you don’t know what Miss Yule writes?’

[005-108]‘Well, I know a little more about her than I did yesterday. I’ve had an

[005-109]hour’s talk with her this afternoon.’

[005-110]‘Indeed?’

[005-111]‘Met her down in the Leggatt fields. I find she doesn’t write

[005-112]independently; just helps her father. What the help amounts to I can’t

[005-113]say. There’s something very attractive about her. She quoted a line or

[005-114]two of Tennyson; the first time I ever heard a woman speak blank verse

[005-115]with any kind of decency.’

[005-116]‘She was walking alone?’

[005-117]‘Yes. On the way back we met old Yule; he seemed rather grumpy, I

[005-118]thought. I don’t think she’s the kind of girl to make a paying business

[005-119]of literature. Her qualities are personal. And it’s pretty clear to

[005-120]me that the valley of the shadow of books by no means agrees with her

[005-121]disposition. Possibly old Yule is something of a tyrant.’

[005-122]‘He doesn’t impress me very favourably. Do you think you will keep up

[005-123]their acquaintance in London?’

[005-124]‘Can’t say. I wonder what sort of a woman that mother really is? Can’t

[005-125]be so very gross, I should think.’

[005-126]‘Miss Harrow knows nothing about her, except that she was a quite

[005-127]uneducated girl.’

[005-128]‘But, dash it! by this time she must have got decent manners. Of course

[005-129]there may be other objections. [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]] knows nothing against her.’

[005-130]Midway in the following morning, as Jasper sat with a book in the

[005-131]garden, he was surprised to see Alfred Yule enter by the gate.

[005-132]‘I thought,’ began the visitor, who seemed in high spirits, ‘that you

[005-133]might like to see something I received this morning.’

[005-134]He unfolded a London evening paper, and indicated a long letter from

[005-135]a casual correspondent. It was written by the authoress of ‘On the

[005-136]Boards,’ and drew attention, with much expenditure of witticism, to the

[005-137]conflicting notices of that book which had appeared in [[the-study|The Study]]. Jasper

[005-138]read the thing with laughing appreciation.

[005-139]‘Just what one expected!’

[005-140]‘And I have private letters on the subject,’ added Mr Yule.

[005-141]‘There has been something like a personal conflict between [[fadge|Fadge]] and the

[005-142]man who looks after the minor notices. Fadge, more so, charged the other

[005-143]man with a design to damage him and the paper. There’s talk of legal

[005-144]proceedings. An immense joke!’

[005-145]He laughed in his peculiar croaking way.

[005-146]‘Do you feel disposed for a turn along the lanes, Mr Milvain?’

[005-147]‘By all means.--There’s my mother at the window; will you come in for a

[005-148]moment?’

[005-149]With a step of quite unusual sprightliness Mr Yule entered the house.

[005-150]He could talk of but one subject, and Mrs Milvain had to listen to a

[005-151]laboured account of the blunder just committed by The Study. It was

[005-152]Alfred’s Yule’s characteristic that he could do nothing lighthandedly.

[005-153]He seemed always to converse with effort; he took a seat with stiff

[005-154]ungainliness; he walked with a stumbling or sprawling gait.

[005-155]When he and Jasper set out for their ramble, his loquacity was in strong

[005-156]contrast with the taciturn mood he had exhibited yesterday and the day

[005-157]before. He fell upon the general aspects of contemporary literature.

[005-158]‘... The evil of the time is the multiplication of ephemerides. Hence a

[005-159]demand for essays, descriptive articles, fragments of [[reviewing-criticism|criticism]], out of

[005-160]all proportion to the supply of even tolerable work. The men who have

[005-161]an aptitude for turning out this kind of thing in vast quantities are

[005-162]enlisted by every new periodical, with the result that their productions

[005-163]are ultimately watered down into worthlessness.... Well now, there’s

[005-164]Fadge. Years ago some of Fadge’s work was not without a certain--a

[005-165]certain conditional promise of--of comparative merit; but now his

[005-166]writing, in my opinion, is altogether beneath consideration; how Rackett

[005-167]could be so benighted as to give him The Study--especially after a man

[005-168]like Henry Hawkridge--passes my comprehension. Did you read a paper of

[005-169]his, a few months back, in [[the-wayside|The Wayside]], a preposterous rehabilitation of

[005-170]Elkanah Settle? Ha! Ha! That’s what such men are driven to. Elkanah

[005-171]Settle! And he hadn’t even a competent acquaintance with his paltry

[005-172]subject. Will you credit that he twice or thrice referred to Settle’s

[005-173]reply to “Absalom and Achitophel” by the title of “Absalom Transposed,”

[005-174]when every schoolgirl knows that the thing was called “Achitophel

[005-175]Transposed”! This was monstrous enough, but there was something still

[005-176]more contemptible. He positively, I assure you, attributed the play of

[005-177]“Epsom Wells” to Crowne! I should have presumed that every student of

[005-178]even the most trivial primer of literature was aware that “Epsom Wells”

[005-179]was written by Shadwell.... Now, if one were to take Shadwell for the

[005-180]subject of a paper, one might very well show how unjustly his name has

[005-181]fallen into contempt. It has often occurred to me to do this. “But

[005-182]Shadwell never deviates into sense.” The sneer, in my opinion, is

[005-183]entirely unmerited. For my own part, I put Shadwell very high among the

[005-184]dramatists of his time, and I think I could show that his absolute worth

[005-185]is by no means inconsiderable. Shadwell has distinct vigour of dramatic

[005-186]conception; his dialogue....’
