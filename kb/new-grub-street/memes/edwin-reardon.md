# Edwin Reardon

The novel's central figure of artistic integrity and commercial failure, [[edwin-reardon]] is a gifted writer who cannot — or will not — treat [[literature-as-trade|literature as a trade]], and the modern [[grub-street]] has no place for him. His trajectory from promising novelist to destitute clerk to early death is the novel's tragedy, and Gissing renders it with a specificity that makes it feel inevitable rather than melodramatic. Reardon stands in stark opposition to his friend [[jasper-milvain]], embodying the fatal incompatibility between artistic conscience and commercial survival in the [[publishing-trade]].

## Character Overview

Reardon possesses genuine literary talent — his early work showed real promise, and his novel *The Optimist* has artistic merit despite its commercial failure. But his method is slow and painstaking; he revises endlessly, cannot write to order, and produces too little to support himself, let alone his wife [[amy-reardon|Amy]]. The novel tracks his decline with painful precision: the loss of income, the loss of domestic peace, the loss of health, and finally the loss of life itself.

Milvain captures the contrast bluntly: "the difference between a man like Reardon and a man like me is this — Reardon takes things hard, I take them easy" (002-178). This temperamental difference is not incidental but fundamental: Reardon is constitutionally incapable of the detachment required for commercial success.

## Family and Background

Reardon's background is modest but respectable. He came to London with literary ambitions and initially seemed likely to succeed. His marriage to [[amy-reardon|Amy]] — a woman of cultivated tastes and middle-class expectations — was a love match, but "even in mid-rapture of his marriage month he had foreseen this" (009-062): the financial impossibility of supporting a wife on the uncertain earnings of a serious novelist.

Amy's mother had brought her to London only once before the marriage (002-142), and Amy's expectations of metropolitan life were shaped by imagination rather than experience. The Reardons' domestic life on [[mornington-road]] is rendered with excruciating detail — the small humiliations of genteel poverty accumulating until the marriage cracks under their weight.

## Career and Artistic Method

Reardon's writing method is the opposite of [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]'s. He writes slowly, painfully, driven by artistic conscience. He cannot produce on demand; he cannot write to market specifications. "Writing is a business" (001-119), but Reardon cannot treat it as one. His novel *The Optimist* is a three-volume work of genuine literary ambition — and a commercial failure. The market rewards speed and marketability, neither of which Reardon can supply.

His friend Milvain says of him: "Reardon works hard all the year round and gets nothing for it" (001-052), diagnosing the problem as one of method rather than talent. But for Reardon, the method is inseparable from the art: "as for Reardon—" (004-122), the unfinished sentence speaks to a career whose trajectory everyone can see but no one can alter.

His moment of artistic freedom is described poignantly: there was a time "when as yet no mission of literary realism had been imposed upon him, and when his passions were still soothed by natural" feeling (087-022). The "mission" of realism is experienced as a burden — a vocation that chose him rather than a career he chose.

## Key Relationships

- **[[amy-reardon|Amy Reardon]]**: His wife. The marriage founders on poverty: Amy cannot accept the social descent that Reardon's literary earnings cannot prevent. When she inherits money from her uncle [[john-yule]], the power dynamic inverts, and the marriage becomes terminally unbalanced. Gissing's treatment of their relationship is notably even-handed — Amy's position is as sympathetically rendered as Reardon's.

- **[[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]**: His friend and opposite. Milvain genuinely likes Reardon but cannot respect his commercial incompetence. Their friendship is the novel's structural axis — art versus commerce, failure versus success. "I could only get that into poor Reardon's head" (001-125), Milvain reflects, lamenting his friend's inability to grasp the market logic that comes naturally to himself.

- **[[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]]**: His closest friend and fellow failure. Biffen's commitment to "absolute realism" (024-171) mirrors Reardon's own artistic seriousness, and their shared poverty creates a bond of mutual recognition. Biffen is the only person who understands Reardon's artistic commitment without needing to be persuaded of its value.

## Thematic Significance

Reardon is the novel's argument that artistic integrity is incompatible with survival in the modern literary marketplace. He is not a hero in the conventional sense — his failure is partly self-inflicted, his paralysis partly a failure of will — but his death is the novel's most powerful indictment of a system that has no use for serious art.

> Reardon is "absurd enough to be" (002-148) — the sentence from Milvain's perspective trails off, but its implication is clear: absurd enough to be honest, absurd enough to starve for his art, absurd enough to die rather than compromise. In the world of New [[grub-street]], this is not virtue but pathology.
