# Section 90

[089-001][[marian-yule|Marian]] had overcome her excess of emotion.

[089-002]‘There is no need to disparage yourself’ she said. ‘What can be simpler

[089-003]than the truth? You loved me, or thought you did, and now you love me

[089-004]no longer. It is a thing that happens every day, either in man or woman,

[089-005]and all that honour demands is the courage to confess the truth. Why

[089-006]didn’t you tell me as soon as you knew that I was burdensome to you?’

[089-007]‘Marian, will you do this?--will you let our engagement last for another

[089-008]six months, but without our meeting during that time?’

[089-009]‘But to what purpose?’

[089-010]‘Then we would see each other again, and both would be able to speak

[089-011]calmly, and we should both know with certainty what course we ought to

[089-012]pursue.’

[089-013]‘That seems to me childish. It is easy for you to contemplate months of

[089-014]postponement. There must be an end now; I can bear it no longer.’

[089-015]The rain fell unceasingly, and with it began to mingle an autumnal mist.

[089-016][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] delayed a moment, then asked calmly:

[089-017]‘Are you going to the Museum?’

[089-018]‘Yes.’

[089-019]‘Go home again for this morning, Marian. You can’t work--’

[089-020]‘I must; and I have no time to lose. Good-bye!’

[089-021]She gave him her hand. They looked at each other for an instant, then

[089-022]Marian left the shelter of the tree, opened her umbrella, and walked

[089-023]quickly away. Jasper did not watch her; he had the face of a man who is

[089-024]suffering a severe humiliation.

[089-025]A few hours later he told [[dora-milvain|Dora]] what had come to pass, and without

[089-026]extenuation of his own conduct. His sister said very little, for she

[089-027]recognised genuine suffering in his tones and aspect. But when it was

[089-028]over, she sat down and wrote to Marian.

[089-029]‘I feel far more disposed to congratulate you than to regret what has

[089-030]happened. Now that there is no necessity for silence, I will tell you

[089-031]something which will help you to see Jasper in his true light. A few

[089-032]weeks ago he actually proposed to a woman for whom he does not pretend

[089-033]to have the slightest affection, but who is very rich, and who seemed

[089-034]likely to be foolish enough to marry him. Yesterday morning he received

[089-035]her final answer--a refusal. I am not sure that I was right in keeping

[089-036]this a secret from you, but I might have done harm by interfering. You

[089-037]will understand (though surely you need no fresh proof) how utterly

[089-038]unworthy he is of you. You cannot, I am sure you cannot, regard it as a

[089-039]misfortune that all is over between you. Dearest Marian, do not cease to

[089-040]think of me as your friend because my brother has disgraced himself. If

[089-041]you can’t see me, at least let us write to each other. You are the only

[089-042]friend I have of my own sex, and I could not bear to lose you.’

[089-043]And much more of the same tenor.

[089-044]Several days passed before there came a reply. It was written with

[089-045]undisturbed kindness of feeling, but in few words.

[089-046]‘For the present we cannot see each other, but I am very far from

[089-047]wishing that our friendship should come to an end. I must only ask that

[089-048]you will write to me without the least reference to these troubles; tell

[089-049]me always about yourself, and be sure that you cannot tell me too much.

[089-050]I hope you may soon be able to send me the news which was foreshadowed

[089-051]in our last talk--though “foreshadowed” is a wrong word to use of coming

[089-052]happiness, isn’t it? That paper I sent to Mr Trenchard is accepted, and

[089-053]I shall be glad to have your [[reviewing-criticism|criticism]] when it comes out; don’t spare

[089-054]my style, which needs a great deal of chastening. I have been thinking:

[089-055]couldn’t you use your holiday in Sark for a story? To judge from your

[089-056]letters, you could make an excellent background of word-painting.’

[089-057]Dora sighed, and shook her little head, and thought of her brother with

[089-058]unspeakable disdain.

[089-059]CHAPTER XXXVII. REWARDS

[089-060]When the fitting moment arrived, [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]] underwent an operation

[089-061]for cataract, and it was believed at first that the result would be

[089-062]favourable. This hope had but short duration; though the utmost prudence

[089-063]was exercised, evil symptoms declared themselves, and in a few months’

[089-064]time all prospect of restoring his vision was at an end. Anxiety, and

[089-065]then the fatal assurance, undermined his health; with blindness, there

[089-066]fell upon him the debility of premature old age.

[089-067]The position of the family was desperate. Marian had suffered much all

[089-068]the winter from attacks of nervous disorder, and by no effort of will

[089-069]could she produce enough literary work to supplement adequately the

[089-070]income derived from her fifteen hundred pounds. In the summer of 1885

[089-071]things were at the worst; Marian saw no alternative but to draw upon her

[089-072]capital, and so relieve the present at the expense of the future. She

[089-073]had a mournful warning before her eyes in the case of poor [[hinks|Hinks]] and his

[089-074]wife, who were now kept from the workhouse only by charity. But at this

[089-075]juncture the rescuer appeared. Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]] and certain of his friends

[089-076]were already making a subscription for the Yules’ benefit, when one of

[089-077]their number--Mr [[jedwood|Jedwood]], the publisher--came forward with a proposal

[089-078]which relieved the minds of all concerned. Mr Jedwood had a brother who

[089-079]was the director of a public library in a provincial town, and by this

[089-080]means he was enabled to offer Marian Yule a place as assistant in that

[089-081]institution; she would receive seventy-five pounds a year, and thus,

[089-082]adding her own income, would be able to put her parents beyond the reach

[089-083]of want. The family at once removed from [[london|London]], and the name of Yule

[089-084]was no longer met with in periodical literature.

[089-085]By an interesting coincidence, it was on the day of this departure that

[089-086]there appeared a number of [[the-west-end|The West End]] in which the place of honour,

[089-087]that of the week’s Celebrity, was occupied by Clement [[fadge|Fadge]]. A coloured

[089-088]portrait of this illustrious man challenged the admiration of all who

[089-089]had literary tastes, and two columns of panegyric recorded his career

[089-090]for the encouragement of aspiring youth. This article, of course

[089-091]unsigned, came from the pen of Jasper Milvain.

[089-092]It was only by indirect channels that Jasper learnt how Marian and her

[089-093]parents had been provided for. Dora’s correspondence with her friend

[089-094]soon languished; in the nature of things this could not but happen; and

[089-095]about the time when Alfred Yule became totally blind the girls ceased to

[089-096]hear anything of each other. An event which came to pass in the spring

[089-097]sorely tempted Dora to write, but out of good feeling she refrained.

[089-098]For it was then that she at length decided to change her name for

[089-099]that of [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]]. Jasper could not quite reconcile himself to this

[089-100]condescension; in various discourses he pointed out to his sister how

[089-101]much higher she might look if she would only have a little patience.

[089-102]‘Whelpdale will never be a man of any note. A good fellow, I admit, but

[089-103]borne in all senses. Let me impress upon you, my dear girl, that I have

[089-104]a future before me, and that there is no reason--with your charm of

[089-105]person and mind--why you should not marry brilliantly. Whelpdale can

[089-106]give you a decent home, I admit, but as regards society he will be a

[089-107]drag upon you.’

[089-108]‘It happens, Jasper, that I have promised to marry him,’ replied Dora,

[089-109]in a significant tone.

[089-110]‘Well, I regret it, but--you are of course your own mistress. I shall

[089-111]make no unpleasantness. I don’t dislike Whelpdale, and I shall remain on

[089-112]friendly terms with him.’

[089-113]‘That is very kind of you,’ said his sister suavely.

[089-114]Whelpdale was frantic with exultation. When the day of the wedding had

[089-115]been settled, he rushed into Jasper’s study and fairly shed tears before

[089-116]he could command his voice.

[089-117]‘There is no mortal on the surface of the globe one-tenth so happy as

[089-118]I am!’ he gasped. ‘I can’t believe it! Why in the name of sense and

[089-119]justice have I been suffered to attain this blessedness? Think of the

[089-120]days when I all but starved in my Albany Street garret, scarcely better

[089-121]off than poor, dear old [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]! Why should I have come to this, and

[089-122]Biffen have poisoned himself in despair? He was a thousand times a

[089-123]better and cleverer fellow than I. And poor old [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], dead in misery!

[089-124]Could I for a moment compare with him?’

[089-125]‘My dear fellow,’ said Jasper, calmly, ‘compose yourself and be logical.

[089-126]In the first place, [[literary-ambition|success]] has nothing whatever to do with moral

[089-127]deserts; and then, both Reardon and Biffen were hopelessly unpractical.

[089-128]In such an admirable social order as ours, they were bound to go to the

[089-129]dogs. Let us be sorry for them, but let us recognise causas rerum, as

[089-130]Biffen would have said. You have exercised ingenuity and perseverance;

[089-131]you have your reward.’

[089-132]‘And when I think that I might have married fatally on thirteen or

[089-133]fourteen different occasions. By-the-by, I implore you never to tell

[089-134]Dora those stories about me. I should lose all her respect. Do you

[089-135]remember the girl from Birmingham?’ He laughed wildly. ‘Heaven be

[089-136]praised that she threw me over! Eternal gratitude to all and sundry of

[089-137]the girls who have plunged me into wretchedness!’

[089-138]‘I admit that you have run the gauntlet, and that you have had

[089-139]marvellous escapes. But be good enough to leave me alone for the

[089-140]present. I must finish this review by midday.’

[089-141]‘Only one word. I don’t know how to thank Dora, how to express my

[089-142]infinite sense of her goodness. Will you try to do so for me? You can

[089-143]speak to her with calmness. Will you tell her what I have said to you?’

[089-144]‘Oh, certainly.--I should recommend a cooling draught of some kind. Look

[089-145]in at a chemist’s as you walk on.’

[089-146]The heavens did not fall before the [[literary-marriage|marriage]]-day, and the wedded pair

[089-147]betook themselves for a few weeks to the Continent. They had been back

[089-148]again and established in their house at Earl’s Court for a month, when

[089-149]one morning about twelve o’clock Jasper dropped in, as though casually.

[089-150]Dora was writing; she had no thought of entirely abandoning literature,

[089-151]and had in hand at present a very pretty tale which would probably

[089-152]appear in The English Girl. Her boudoir, in which she sat, could

[089-153]not well have been daintier and more appropriate to the charming

[089-154]characteristics of its mistress.

[089-155]Mrs Whelpdale affected no literary slovenliness; she was dressed in

[089-156]light colours, and looked so lovely that even Jasper paused on the

[089-157]threshold with a smile of admiration.

[089-158]‘Upon my word,’ he exclaimed, ‘I am proud of my sisters! What did you

[089-159]think of Maud last night? Wasn’t she superb?’

[089-160]‘She certainly did look very well. But I doubt if she’s very happy.’

[089-161]‘That is her own look out; I told her plainly enough my opinion of

[089-162]Dolomore. But she was in such a tremendous hurry.’

[089-163]‘You are detestable, Jasper! Is it inconceivable to you that a man or

[089-164]woman should be disinterested when they marry?’

[089-165]‘By no means.’

[089-166]‘Maud didn’t marry for [[money-in-literature|money]] any more than I did.’

[089-167]‘You remember the Northern Farmer: “Doan’t thou marry for money, but

[089-168]go where money is.” An admirable piece of advice. Well, Maud made a

[089-169]mistake, let us say. Dolomore is a clown, and now she knows it. Why,

[089-170]if she had waited, she might have married one of the leading men of the

[089-171]day. She is fit to be a duchess, as far as appearance goes; but I was

[089-172]never snobbish. I care very little about titles; what I look to is

[089-173]intellectual distinction.’

[089-174]‘Combined with financial [[literary-success|success]].’

[089-175]‘Why, that is what distinction means.’ He looked round the room with a

[089-176]smile. ‘You are not uncomfortable here, old girl. I wish mother could

[089-177]have lived till now.’
