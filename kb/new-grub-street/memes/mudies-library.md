# Mudie's Library

Mudie's Select Library was the most powerful [[circulating library]] in Victorian [[london|London]], whose dominance of the [[three-volume-system|three-volume novel]] market directly shaped the [[publishing-trade|publishing]] economy that entraps the struggling writers of [[New Grub Street]].

## Role in the Novel

Mudie's appears at key moments in the narrative, always as a barometer of [[literary-success|literary success]] and failure. When [[jasper-milvain]] praises a forthcoming novel, he casually offers to "send scores of people to Mudie's after it" [026-031], treating the circulating library as the ultimate distribution channel for fiction. The casualness of the remark underscores how completely Mudie's controlled the market for new novels.

Later, [[dora-milvain|Dora]] visits Mudie's "for the new number of some periodical which contained an appetising title" [063-106], and overhears a conversation at the counter that [[money-in-literature|advances]] the social plotting of the novel. The library counter serves as both a physical and social crossroads.

In the novel's closing chapters, the embittered [[edwin-reardon]] imagines sending someone to "Mudie's, and make inquiry if my novels ever by any chance leave the shelves" [077-022], fully expecting the answer to be that he is "quite forgotten." For [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], Mudie's represents the ultimate verdict on a writer's career — circulation at Mudie's meant cultural survival; absence meant oblivion.

## The Three-Volume System

Mudie's business model was inseparable from the "[[three-volume-system|three-volume system]]" — the practice of [[publishing-trade|publishing]] new novels in three volumes at 31s 6d, a price that made purchase prohibitive for individual readers but ideal for circulating libraries. Mudie's, as the largest subscriber library, effectively dictated what could be published by its willingness or refusal to stock a title. [[harold-biffen]]'s grim assessment that a writer "may live on a yearly [[three-volume-system|three-volume novel]]" [035-079] reflects the system's economic logic: Mudie's guaranteed a baseline sale, but also capped [[literary-ambition|ambition]] at the commercially safe.

## The Circulating Library as Gatekeeper

Mudie's functioned as the primary gatekeeper of Victorian fiction. Publishers would not accept a novel unless they believed Mudie's would order copies in bulk. This meant that Mudie's moral standards — the library refused anything it deemed unsuitable for [[social-class|middle-class]] family reading — became de facto censorship over the [[grub-street|literary marketplace]]. [[alfred-yule]]'s bitterness about literary commerce and [[edwin-reardon]]'s struggles both trace in part to a system where a single institution's purchasing decisions determined a writer's fate.
