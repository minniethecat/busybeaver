# Section 35

[034-001]‘But, [[amy-reardon|Amy]], I have no faith in my power of--’

[034-002]‘Oh, it would be different! A few days--a week or a fortnight of real

[034-003]holiday in this spring weather. Go to some seaside place. How is it

[034-004]possible that all your talent should have left you? It’s only that you

[034-005]have been so anxious and in such poor health. You say I don’t love you,

[034-006]but I have thought and thought what would be best for you to do, how

[034-007]you could save yourself. How can you sink down to the position of a poor

[034-008]clerk in some office? That CAN’T be your fate, [[edwin-reardon|Edwin]]; it’s incredible.

[034-009]Oh, after such bright hopes, make one more effort! Have you forgotten

[034-010]that we were to go to the South together--you were to take me to Italy

[034-011]and Greece? How can that ever be if you fail utterly in literature? How

[034-012]can you ever hope to earn more than bare sustenance at any other kind of

[034-013]work?’

[034-014]He all but lost consciousness of her words in gazing at the face she

[034-015]held up to his.

[034-016]‘You love me? Say again that you love me!’

[034-017]‘Dear, I love you with all my heart. But I am so afraid of the future.

[034-018]I can’t bear [[literary-poverty|poverty]]; I have found that I can’t bear it. And I dread to

[034-019]think of your becoming only an ordinary man--’

[034-020]Reardon laughed.

[034-021]‘But I am NOT “only an ordinary man,” Amy! If I never write another

[034-022]line, that won’t undo what I have done. It’s little enough, to be sure;

[034-023]but you know what I am. Do you only love the author in me? Don’t you

[034-024]think of me apart from all that I may do or not do? If I had to earn my

[034-025]living as a clerk, would that make me a clerk in soul?’

[034-026]‘You shall not fall to that! It would be too bitter a shame to lose all

[034-027]you have gained in these long years of work. Let me plan for you; do as

[034-028]I wish. You are to be what we hoped from the first. Take all the summer

[034-029]months. How long will it be before you can finish this short book?’

[034-030]‘A week or two.’

[034-031]‘Then finish it, and see what you can get for it. And try at once

[034-032]to find a tenant to take this place off our hands; that would be

[034-033]twenty-five pounds saved for the rest of the year. You could live on so

[034-034]little by yourself, couldn’t you?’

[034-035]‘Oh, on ten shillings a week, if need be.’

[034-036]‘But not to starve yourself, you know. Don’t you feel that my plan is a

[034-037]good one? When I came to you to-night I meant to speak of this, but you

[034-038]were so cruel--’

[034-039]‘Forgive me, dearest love! I was half a madman. You have been so cold to

[034-040]me for a long time.’

[034-041]‘I have been distracted. It was as if we were drawing nearer and nearer

[034-042]to the edge of a cataract.’

[034-043]‘Have you spoken to your mother about this?’ he asked uneasily.

[034-044]‘No--not exactly this. But I know she will help us in this way.’

[034-045]He had seated himself and was holding her in his arms, his face laid

[034-046]against hers.

[034-047]‘I shall dread to part from you, Amy. That’s such a dangerous thing to

[034-048]do. It may mean that we are never to live as husband and wife again.’

[034-049]‘But how could it? It’s just to prevent that danger. If we go on here

[034-050]till we have no [[money-in-literature|money]]--what’s before us then? Wretched lodgings at the

[034-051]best. And I am afraid to think of that. I can’t trust myself if that

[034-052]should come to pass.’

[034-053]‘What do you mean?’ he asked anxiously.

[034-054]‘I hate poverty so. It brings out all the worst things in me; you know I

[034-055]have told you that before, Edwin?’

[034-056]‘But you would never forget that you are my wife?’

[034-057]‘I hope not. But--I can’t think of it; I can’t face it! That would be

[034-058]the very worst that can befall us, and we are going to try our utmost to

[034-059]escape from it. Was there ever a man who did as much as you have done in

[034-060]literature and then sank into hopeless poverty?’

[034-061]‘Oh, many!’

[034-062]‘But at your age, I mean. Surely not at your age?’

[034-063]‘I’m afraid there have been such poor fellows. Think how often one hears

[034-064]of hopeful beginnings, new reputations, and then--you hear no more. Of

[034-065]course it generally means that the man has gone into a different career;

[034-066]but sometimes, sometimes--’

[034-067]‘What?’

[034-068]‘The abyss.’ He pointed downward. ‘Penury and despair and a miserable

[034-069]death.’

[034-070]‘Oh, but those men haven’t a wife and child! They would struggle--’

[034-071]‘Darling, they do struggle. But it’s as if an ever-increasing weight

[034-072]were round their necks; it drags them lower and lower. The world has no

[034-073]pity on a man who can’t do or produce something it thinks worth money.

[034-074]You may be a divine poet, and if some good fellow doesn’t take pity on

[034-075]you you will starve by the roadside. Society is as blind and brutal as

[034-076]fate. I have no right to complain of my own ill-fortune; it’s my own

[034-077]fault (in a sense) that I can’t continue as well as I began; if I could

[034-078]write books as good as the early ones I should earn money. For all that,

[034-079]it’s hard that I must be kicked aside as worthless just because I don’t

[034-080]know a trade.’

[034-081]‘It shan’t be! I have only to look into your face to know that you will

[034-082]succeed after all. Yours is the kind of face that people come to know in

[034-083]portraits.’

[034-084]He kissed her hair, and her eyes, and her mouth.

[034-085]‘How well I remember your saying that before! Why have you grown so good

[034-086]to me all at once, my Amy? Hearing you speak like that I feel there’s

[034-087]nothing beyond my reach. But I dread to go away from you. If I find that

[034-088]it is hopeless; if I am alone somewhere, and know that the effort is all

[034-089]in vain--’

[034-090]‘Then?’

[034-091]‘Well, I can leave you free. If I can’t support you, it will be only

[034-092]just that I should give you back your freedom.’

[034-093]‘I don’t understand--’

[034-094]She raised herself and looked into his eyes.

[034-095]‘We won’t talk of that. If you bid me go on with the struggle, I shall

[034-096]do so.’

[034-097]Amy had hidden her face, and lay silently in his arms for a minute or

[034-098]two. Then she murmured:

[034-099]‘It is so cold here, and so late. Come!’

[034-100]‘So early. There goes three o’clock.’

[034-101]The next day they talked much of this new project. As there was sunshine

[034-102]Amy accompanied her husband for his walk in the afternoon; it was long

[034-103]since they had been out together. An open carriage that passed, followed

[034-104]by two young girls on horseback, gave a familiar direction to Reardon’s

[034-105]thoughts.

[034-106]‘If one were as rich as those people! They pass so close to us; they see

[034-107]us, and we see them; but the distance between is infinity. They don’t

[034-108]belong to the same world as we poor wretches. They see everything in a

[034-109]different light; they have powers which would seem supernatural if we

[034-110]were suddenly endowed with them.’

[034-111]‘Of course,’ assented his companion with a sigh.

[034-112]‘Just fancy, if one got up in the morning with the thought that no

[034-113]reasonable desire that occurred to one throughout the day need remain

[034-114]ungratified! And that it would be the same, any day and every day, to

[034-115]the end of one’s life! Look at those houses; every detail, within and

[034-116]without, luxurious. To have such a home as that!’

[034-117]‘And they are empty creatures who live there.’

[034-118]‘They do live, Amy, at all events. Whatever may be their faculties, they

[034-119]all have free scope. I have often stood staring at houses like these

[034-120]until I couldn’t believe that the people owning them were mere human

[034-121]beings like myself. The power of money is so hard to realise; one who

[034-122]has never had it marvels at the completeness with which it transforms

[034-123]every detail of life. Compare what we call our home with that of rich

[034-124]people; it moves one to scornful laughter. I have no sympathy with the

[034-125]stoical point of view; between wealth and poverty is just the difference

[034-126]between the whole man and the maimed. If my lower limbs are paralysed

[034-127]I may still be able to think, but then there is such a thing in life as

[034-128]walking. As a poor devil I may live nobly; but one happens to be made

[034-129]with faculties of enjoyment, and those have to fall into atrophy. To be

[034-130]sure, most rich people don’t understand their happiness; if they did,

[034-131]they would move and talk like gods--which indeed they are.’

[034-132]Amy’s brow was shadowed. A wise man, in Reardon’s position, would not

[034-133]have chosen this subject to dilate upon.

[034-134]‘The difference,’ he went on, ‘between the man with money and the man

[034-135]without is simply this: the one thinks, “How shall I use my life?” and

[034-136]the other, “How shall I keep myself alive?” A physiologist ought to be

[034-137]able to discover some curious distinction between the brain of a person

[034-138]who has never given a thought to the means of subsistence, and that of

[034-139]one who has never known a day free from such cares. There must be some

[034-140]special cerebral development representing the mental anguish kept up by

[034-141]poverty.’

[034-142]‘I should say,’ put in Amy, ‘that it affects every function of the

[034-143]brain. It isn’t a special point of suffering, but a misery that colours

[034-144]every thought.’

[034-145]‘True. Can I think of a single subject in all the sphere of my

[034-146]experience without the consciousness that I see it through the medium of

[034-147]poverty? I have no enjoyment which isn’t tainted by that thought, and I

[034-148]can suffer no pain which it doesn’t increase. The curse of poverty is to

[034-149]the modern world just what that of slavery was to the ancient. Rich and

[034-150]destitute stand to each other as free man and bond. You remember the

[034-151]line of Homer I have often quoted about the demoralising effect of

[034-152]enslavement; poverty degrades in the same way.’

[034-153]‘It has had its effect upon me--I know that too well,’ said Amy, with

[034-154]bitter frankness.

[034-155]Reardon glanced at her, and wished to make some reply, but he could not

[034-156]say what was in his thoughts.

[034-157]He worked on at his story. Before he had reached the end of it,

[034-158]‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]]’ was published, and one day arrived a parcel containing

[034-159]the six copies to which an author is traditionally entitled. Reardon was

[034-160]not so old in authorship that he could open the packet without a slight

[034-161]flutter of his pulse. The book was tastefully got up; Amy exclaimed with

[034-162]pleasure as she caught sight of the cover and lettering:

[034-163]‘It may succeed, Edwin. It doesn’t look like a book that fails, does

[034-164]it?’

[034-165]She laughed at her own childishness. But Reardon had opened one of the

[034-166]volumes, and was glancing over the beginning of a chapter.

[034-167]‘Good God!’ he cried. ‘What hellish torment it was to write that page!

[034-168]I did it one morning when the fog was so thick that I had to light the

[034-169]lamp. It brings cold sweat to my forehead to read the words. And to

[034-170]think that people will skim over it without a suspicion of what it

[034-171]cost the writer!--What execrable style! A potboy could write better

[034-172]narrative.’

[034-173]‘Who are to have copies?’

[034-174]‘No one, if I could help it. But I suppose your mother will expect one?’

[034-175]‘And--[[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]?’

[034-176]‘I suppose so,’ he replied indifferently. ‘But not unless he asks for

[034-177]it. Poor old [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], of course; though it’ll make him despise me. Then

[034-178]one for ourselves. That leaves two--to light the fire with. We have

[034-179]been rather short of fire-paper since we couldn’t afford our daily

[034-180]newspaper.’
