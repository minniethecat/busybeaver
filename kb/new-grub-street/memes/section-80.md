# Section 80

[079-001]At this establishment the doctor was well known. By midnight [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]

[079-002]lay in a comfortable room, a huge cataplasm fixed upon him, and other

[079-003]needful arrangements made. A waiter had undertaken to visit him at

[079-004]intervals through the night, and the man of medicine promised to return

[079-005]as soon as possible after daybreak.

[079-006]What sound was that, soft and continuous, remote, now clearer, now

[079-007]confusedly murmuring? He must have slept, but now he lay in sudden

[079-008]perfect consciousness, and that music fell upon his ears. Ah! of course

[079-009]it was the rising tide; he was near the divine sea.

[079-010]The night-light enabled him to discern the principal objects in the

[079-011]room, and he let his eyes stray idly hither and thither. But this moment

[079-012]of peacefulness was brought to an end by a fit of coughing, and he

[079-013]became troubled, profoundly troubled, in mind. Was his illness really

[079-014]dangerous? He tried to draw a deep breath, but could not. He found that

[079-015]he could only lie on his right side with any ease. And with the effort

[079-016]of turning he exhausted himself; in the course of an hour or two all

[079-017]his strength had left him. Vague fears flitted harassingly through his

[079-018]thoughts. If he had inflammation of the lungs--that was a disease of

[079-019]which one might die, and speedily. Death? No, no, no; impossible at such

[079-020]a time as this, when [[amy-reardon|Amy]], his own dear wife, had come back to him, and

[079-021]had brought him that which would insure their happiness through all the

[079-022]years of a long life.

[079-023]He was still quite a young man; there must be great reserves of strength

[079-024]in him. And he had the will to live, the prevailing will, the passionate

[079-025]all-conquering desire of happiness.

[079-026]How he had alarmed himself! Why, now he was calmer again, and again

[079-027]could listen to the music of the breakers. Not all the folly and

[079-028]baseness that paraded along this strip of the shore could change the

[079-029]sea’s eternal melody. In a day or two he would walk on the sands with

[079-030]Amy, somewhere quite out of sight of the repulsive town. But Willie was

[079-031]ill; he had forgotten that. Poor little boy! In future the child should

[079-032]be more to him; though never what the mother was, his own love, won

[079-033]again and for ever.

[079-034]Again an interval of unconsciousness, brought to an end by that aching

[079-035]in his side. He breathed very quickly; could not help doing so. He had

[079-036]never felt so ill as this, never. Was it not near morning?

[079-037]Then he dreamt. He was at Patras, was stepping into a boat to be rowed

[079-038]out to the steamer which would bear him away from Greece. A magnificent

[079-039]night, though at the end of December; a sky of deep blue, thick set with

[079-040]stars. No sound but the steady splash of the oars, or perhaps a voice

[079-041]from one of the many vessels that lay anchored in the harbour, each

[079-042]showing its lantern-gleams. The water was as deep a blue as the sky, and

[079-043]sparkled with reflected radiance.

[079-044]And now he stood on deck in the light of early morning. Southward lay

[079-045]the Ionian Islands; he looked for Ithaca, and grieved that it had been

[079-046]passed in the hours of darkness. But the nearest point of the main shore

[079-047]was a rocky promontory; it reminded him that in these waters was fought

[079-048]the battle of Actium.

[079-049]The glory vanished. He lay once more a sick man in a hired chamber,

[079-050]longing for the dull English dawn.

[079-051]At eight o’clock came the doctor. He would allow only a word or two to

[079-052]be uttered, and his visit was brief. Reardon was chiefly anxious to have

[079-053]news of the child, but for this he would have to wait.

[079-054]At ten Amy entered the bedroom. Reardon could not raise himself, but he

[079-055]stretched out his hand and took hers, and gazed eagerly at her. She must

[079-056]have been weeping, he felt sure of that, and there was an expression on

[079-057]her face such as he had never seen there.

[079-058]‘How is Willie?’

[079-059]‘Better, dear; much better.’

[079-060]He still searched her face.

[079-061]‘Ought you to leave him?’

[079-062]‘Hush! You mustn’t speak.’

[079-063]Tears broke from her eyes, and Reardon had the conviction that the child

[079-064]was dead.

[079-065]‘The truth, Amy!’

[079-066]She threw herself on her knees by the bedside, and pressed her wet cheek

[079-067]against his hand.

[079-068]‘I am come to nurse you, dear husband,’ she said a moment after,

[079-069]standing up again and kissing his forehead. ‘I have only you now.’

[079-070]His heart sank, and for a moment so great a terror was upon him that he

[079-071]closed his eyes and seemed to pass into utter darkness. But those

[079-072]last words of hers repeated themselves in his mind, and at length they

[079-073]brought a deep solace. Poor little Willie had been the cause of the

[079-074]first coldness between him and Amy; her love for him had given place to

[079-075]a mother’s love for the child. Now it would be as in the first days of

[079-076]their [[literary-marriage|marriage]]; they would again be all in all to each other.

[079-077]‘You oughtn’t to have come, feeling so ill,’ she said to him. ‘You

[079-078]should have let me know, dear.’

[079-079]He smiled and kissed her hand.

[079-080]‘And you kept the truth from me last night, in kindness.’

[079-081]She checked herself, knowing that agitation must be harmful to him. She

[079-082]had hoped to conceal the child’s death, but the effort was too much for

[079-083]her overstrung nerves. And indeed it was only possible for her to remain

[079-084]an hour or two by this sick-bed, for she was exhausted by her night

[079-085]of watching, and the sudden agony with which it had concluded. Shortly

[079-086]after Amy’s departure, a professional nurse came to attend upon what the

[079-087]doctor had privately characterised as a very grave case.

[079-088]By the evening its gravity was in no respect diminished. The sufferer

[079-089]had ceased to cough and to make restless movements, and had become

[079-090]lethargic; later, he spoke deliriously, or rather muttered, for his

[079-091]words were seldom intelligible. Amy had returned to the room at four

[079-092]o’clock, and remained till far into the night; she was physically

[079-093]exhausted, and could do little but sit in a chair by the bedside

[079-094]and shed silent tears, or gaze at vacancy in the woe of her sudden

[079-095]arrive in [[brighton|Brighton]] to-morrow morning; the child’s funeral would probably

[079-096]be on the third day from this.

[079-097]When she rose to go away for the night, leaving the nurse in attendance,

[079-098]Reardon seemed to lie in a state of unconsciousness, but just as she was

[079-099]turning from the bed, he opened his eyes and pronounced her name.

[079-100]‘I am here, Edwin,’ she answered, bending over him.

[079-101]‘Will you let [[harold-biffen|Biffen]] know?’ he said in low but very clear tones.

[079-102]‘That you are ill dear? I will write at once, or telegraph, if you like.

[079-103]What is his address?’

[079-104]He had closed his eyes again, and there came no reply. Amy repeated her

[079-105]question twice; she was turning from him in hopelessness when his voice

[079-106]became audible.

[079-107]‘I can’t remember his new address. I know it, but I can’t remember.’

[079-108]She had to leave him thus.

[079-109]The next day his breathing was so harassed that he had to be raised

[079-110]against pillows. But throughout the hours of daylight his mind was

[079-111]clear, and from time to time he whispered words of tenderness in reply

[079-112]to Amy’s look. He never willingly relinquished her hand, and repeatedly

[079-113]he pressed it against his cheek or lips. Vainly he still endeavoured to

[079-114]recall his friend’s address.

[079-115]‘Couldn’t Mr [[carter|Carter]] discover it for you?’ Amy asked.

[079-116]‘Perhaps. You might try.’

[079-117]She would have suggested applying to [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]], but that name must

[079-118]not be mentioned. [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]], also, would perchance know where Biffen

[079-119]lived, but Whelpdale’s address he had also forgotten.

[079-120]At night there were long periods of delirium; not mere confused

[079-121]muttering, but continuous talk which the listeners could follow

[079-122]perfectly.

[079-123]For the most part the sufferer’s mind was occupied with revival of the

[079-124]distress he had undergone whilst making those last efforts to write

[079-125]something worthy of himself. Amy’s heart was wrung as she heard him

[079-126]living through that time of supreme misery--misery which she might have

[079-127]done so much to alleviate, had not selfish fears and irritated pride

[079-128]caused her to draw further and further from him. Hers was the kind of

[079-129]penitence which is forced by sheer stress of circumstances on a nature

[079-130]which resents any form of humiliation; she could not abandon herself to

[079-131]unreserved grief for what she had done or omitted, and the sense of this

[079-132]defect made a great part of her affliction. When her husband lay in mute

[079-133]lethargy, she thought only of her dead child, and mourned the loss; but

[079-134]his delirious utterances constrained her to break from that bittersweet

[079-135]preoccupation, to confuse her mourning with self-reproach and with

[079-136]fears.

[079-137]Though unconsciously, he was addressing her: ‘I can do no more, Amy. My

[079-138]brain seems to be worn out; I can’t compose, I can’t even think. Look! I

[079-139]have been sitting here for hours, and I have done only that little bit,

[079-140]half a dozen lines. Such poor stuff too! I should burn it, only I can’t

[079-141]afford. I must do my regular quantity every day, no matter what it is.’

[079-142]The nurse, who was present when he talked in this way, looked to Amy for

[079-143]an explanation.

[079-144]‘My husband is an author,’ Amy answered. ‘Not long ago he was obliged to

[079-145]write when he was ill and ought to have been resting.’

[079-146]‘I always thought it must be hard work writing books,’ said the nurse

[079-147]with a shake of her head.

[079-148]‘You don’t understand me,’ the voice pursued, dreadful as a voice always

[079-149]is when speaking independently of the will. ‘You think I am only a poor

[079-150]creature, because I can do nothing better than this. If only I had [[money-in-literature|money]]

[079-151]enough to rest for a year or two, you should see. Just because I have no

[079-152]money I must sink to this degradation. And I am losing you as well; you

[079-153]don’t love me!’

[079-154]He began to moan in anguish.

[079-155]But a happy change presently came over his dreaming. He fell into

[079-156]animated description of his experiences in Greece and Italy, and after

[079-157]talking for a long time, he turned his head and said in a perfectly

[079-158]natural tone:

[079-159]‘Amy, do you know that Biffen and I are going to Greece?’

[079-160]She believed he spoke consciously, and replied:

[079-161]‘You must take me with you, Edwin.’

[079-162]He paid no attention to this remark, but went on with the same deceptive

[079-163]accent.

[079-164]‘He deserves a holiday after nearly getting burnt to death to save

[079-165]his novel. Imagine the old fellow plunging headlong into the flames to

[079-166]rescue his manuscript! Don’t say that authors can’t be heroic!’

[079-167]And he laughed gaily.

[079-168]Another morning broke. It was possible, said the doctors (a second had

[079-169]been summoned), that a crisis which drew near might bring the favourable

[079-170]turn; but Amy formed her own opinion from the way in which the

[079-171]nurse expressed herself. She felt sure that the gravest fears were

[079-172]entertained. Before noon Reardon awoke from what had seemed natural

[079-173]sleep--save for the rapid breathing--and of a sudden recollected the

[079-174]number of the house in Cleveland Street at which Biffen was now living.

[079-175]He uttered it without explanation. Amy at once conjectured his meaning,

[079-176]her husband’s friend.

[079-177]That evening, as Amy was on the point of returning to the sick-room

[079-178]after having dined at her friend’s house, it was announced that

[079-179]a gentleman named Biffen wished to see her. She found him in the

[079-180]dining-room, and, even amid her distress, it was a satisfaction to her

[079-181]that he presented a far more conventional appearance than in the old

[079-182]days. All the garments he wore, even his hat, gloves, and boots,

[079-183]were new; a surprising state of things, explained by the fact of his

[079-184]commercial brother having sent him a present of ten pounds, a practical

[079-185]expression of sympathy with him in his recent calamity. Biffen could

[079-186]not speak; he looked with alarm at Amy’s pallid face. In a few words she

[079-187]told him of Reardon’s condition.
