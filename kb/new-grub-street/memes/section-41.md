# Section 41

[040-001]‘You refuse to live with me, then?’

[040-002]‘Yes, if this is the kind of life you offer me.’

[040-003]‘You would be more ashamed to share your husband’s misfortunes than to

[040-004]declare to everyone that you had deserted him?’

[040-005]‘I shall “declare to everyone” the simple truth. You have the

[040-006]opportunity of making one more effort to save us from degradation. You

[040-007]refuse to take the trouble; you prefer to drag me down into a lower rank

[040-008]of life. I can’t and won’t consent to that. The disgrace is yours; it’s

[040-009]fortunate for me that I have a decent home to go to.’

[040-010]‘Fortunate for you!--you make yourself unutterably contemptible. I have

[040-011]done nothing that justifies you in leaving me. It is for me to judge

[040-012]what I can do and what I can’t. A good woman would see no degradation in

[040-013]what I ask of you. But to run away from me just because I am poorer than

[040-014]you ever thought I should be--’

[040-015]He was incoherent. A thousand passionate things that he wished to say

[040-016]clashed together in his mind and confused his speech. Defeated in

[040-017]the attempt to act like a strong man, he could not yet recover

[040-018]standing-ground, knew not how to tone his utterances.

[040-019]‘Yes, of course, that’s how you will put it,’ said [[amy-reardon|Amy]]. ‘That’s how you

[040-020]will represent me to your friends. My friends will see it in a different

[040-021]light.’

[040-022]‘They will regard you as a martyr?’

[040-023]‘No one shall make a martyr of me, you may be sure. I was unfortunate

[040-024]enough to marry a man who had no delicacy, no regard for my feelings.--I

[040-025]am not the first woman who has made a mistake of this kind.’

[040-026]‘No delicacy? No regard for your feelings?--Have I always utterly

[040-027]misunderstood you? Or has [[literary-poverty|poverty]] changed you to a woman I can’t

[040-028]recognise?’

[040-029]He came nearer, and gazed desperately into her face. Not a muscle of it

[040-030]showed susceptibility to the old influences.

[040-031]‘Do you know, Amy,’ he added in a lower voice, ‘that if we part now, we

[040-032]part for ever?’

[040-033]‘I’m afraid that is only too likely.’

[040-034]She moved aside.

[040-035]‘You mean that you wish it. You are weary of me, and care for nothing

[040-036]but how to make yourself free.’

[040-037]‘I shall argue no more. I am tired to death of it.’

[040-038]‘Then say nothing, but listen for the last time to my view of the

[040-039]position we have come to. When I consented to leave you for a time, to

[040-040]go away and try to work in solitude, I was foolish and even insincere,

[040-041]both to you and to myself. I knew that I was undertaking the impossible.

[040-042]It was just putting off the evil day, that was all--putting off the time

[040-043]when I should have to say plainly: “I can’t live by literature, so I

[040-044]must look out for some other employment.” I shouldn’t have been so weak

[040-045]but that I knew how you would regard such a decision as that. I was

[040-046]afraid to tell the truth--afraid. Now, when [[carter|Carter]] of a sudden put this

[040-047]opportunity before me, I saw all the absurdity of the arrangements we

[040-048]had made. It didn’t take me a moment to make up my mind. Anything was

[040-049]to be chosen rather than a parting from you on false pretences, a

[040-050]ridiculous affectation of hope where there was no hope.’

[040-051]He paused, and saw that his words had no effect upon her.

[040-052]‘And a grievous share of the fault lies with you, Amy. You remember very

[040-053]well when I first saw how dark the future was. I was driven even to say

[040-054]that we ought to change our mode of living; I asked you if you would be

[040-055]willing to leave this place and go into cheaper rooms. And you know what

[040-056]your answer was. Not a sign in you that you would stand by me if the

[040-057]worst came. I knew then what I had to look forward to, but I durst not

[040-058]believe it. I kept saying to myself: “She loves me, and as soon as she

[040-059]really understands--” That was all self-deception. If I had been a wise

[040-060]man, I should have spoken to you in a way you couldn’t mistake. I should

[040-061]have told you that we were living recklessly, and that I had determined

[040-062]to alter it. I have no delicacy? No regard for your feelings? Oh, if

[040-063]I had had less! I doubt whether you can even understand some of the

[040-064]considerations that weighed with me, and made me cowardly--though I once

[040-065]thought there was no refinement of sensibility that you couldn’t enter

[040-066]into. Yes, I was absurd enough to say to myself: “It will look as if I

[040-067]had consciously deceived her; she may suffer from the thought that I won

[040-068]her at all hazards, knowing that I should soon expose her to poverty and

[040-069]all sorts of humiliation.” Impossible to speak of that again; I had to

[040-070]struggle desperately on, trying to hope. Oh! if you knew--’

[040-071]His voice gave way for an instant.

[040-072]‘I don’t understand how you could be so thoughtless and heartless. You

[040-073]knew that I was almost mad with anxiety at times. Surely, any woman must

[040-074]have had the impulse to give what help was in her power. How could you

[040-075]hesitate? Had you no suspicion of what a relief and encouragement it

[040-076]would be to me, if you said: “Yes, we must go and live in a simpler

[040-077]way?” If only as a proof that you loved me, how I should have welcomed

[040-078]that! You helped me in nothing. You threw all the responsibility upon

[040-079]me--always bearing in mind, I suppose, that there was a refuge for you.

[040-080]Even now, I despise myself for saying such things of you, though I know

[040-081]so bitterly that they are true. It takes a long time to see you as such

[040-082]a different woman from the one I worshipped. In passion, I can fling out

[040-083]violent words, but they don’t yet answer to my actual feeling. It will

[040-084]be long enough yet before I think contemptuously of you. You know that

[040-085]when a light is suddenly extinguished, the image of it still shows

[040-086]before your eyes. But at last comes the darkness.’

[040-087]Amy turned towards him once more.

[040-088]‘Instead of saying all this, you might be proving that I am wrong. Do

[040-089]so, and I will gladly confess it.’

[040-090]‘That you are wrong? I don’t see your meaning.’

[040-091]‘You might prove that you are willing to do your utmost to save me from

[040-092]humiliation.’

[040-093]‘Amy, I have done my utmost. I have done more than you can imagine.’

[040-094]‘No. You have toiled on in illness and anxiety--I know that. But a

[040-095]chance is offered you now of working in a better way. Till that is

[040-096]tried, you have no right to give all up and try to drag me down with

[040-097]you.’

[040-098]‘I don’t know how to answer. I have told you so often--You can’t

[040-099]understand me!’

[040-100]‘I can! I can!’ Her voice trembled for the first time. ‘I know that you

[040-101]are so ready to give in to difficulties. Listen to me, and do as I bid

[040-102]you.’ She spoke in the strangest tone of command.

[040-103]It was command, not exhortation, but there was no harshness in her

[040-104]voice. ‘Go at once to Mr Carter. Tell him you have made a ludicrous

[040-105]mistake--in a fit of low spirits; anything you like to say. Tell him you

[040-106]of course couldn’t dream of becoming his clerk. To-night; at once! You

[040-107]understand me, [[edwin-reardon|Edwin]]? Go now, this moment.’

[040-108]‘Have you determined to see how weak I am? Do you wish to be able to

[040-109]despise me more completely still?’

[040-110]‘I am determined to be your friend, and to save you from yourself. Go at

[040-111]once! Leave all the rest to me. If I have let things take their course

[040-112]till now, it shan’t be so in future. The responsibility shall be with

[040-113]me. Only do as I tell you.’

[040-114]‘You know it’s impossible--’

[040-115]‘It is not! I will find [[money-in-literature|money]]. No one shall be allowed to say that we

[040-116]are parting; no one has any such idea yet. You are going away for

[040-117]your health, just three summer months. I have been far more careful of

[040-118]appearances than you imagine, but you give me credit for so little. I

[040-119]will find the money you need, until you have written another book. I

[040-120]promise; I undertake it. Then I will find another home for us, of the

[040-121]proper kind. You shall have no trouble. You shall give yourself entirely

[040-122]to intellectual things.

[040-123]But Mr Carter must be told at once, before he can spread a report. If he

[040-124]has spoken, he must contradict what he has said.’

[040-125]‘But you amaze me, Amy. Do you mean to say that you look upon it as a

[040-126]veritable disgrace, my taking this clerkship?’

[040-127]‘I do. I can’t help my nature. I am ashamed through and through that you

[040-128]should sink to this.’

[040-129]‘But everyone knows that I was a clerk once!’

[040-130]‘Very few people know it. And then that isn’t the same thing. It

[040-131]doesn’t matter what one has been in the past. Especially a literary man;

[040-132]everyone expects to hear that he was once poor. But to fall from the

[040-133]position you now have, and to take weekly wages--you surely can’t know

[040-134]how people of my world regard that.’

[040-135]‘Of your world? I had thought your world was the same as mine, and knew

[040-136]nothing whatever of these imbecilities.’

[040-137]‘It is getting late. Go and see Mr Carter, and afterwards I will talk as

[040-138]much as you like.’

[040-139]He might perhaps have yielded, but the unemphasised contempt in that

[040-140]last sentence was more than he could bear. It demonstrated to him more

[040-141]completely than set terms could have done what a paltry weakling he

[040-142]would appear in Amy’s eyes if he took his hat down from the peg and set

[040-143]out to obey her orders.

[040-144]‘You are asking too much,’ he said, with unexpected coldness. ‘If my

[040-145]opinions are so valueless to you that you dismiss them like those of a

[040-146]troublesome child, I wonder you think it worth while to try and keep up

[040-147]appearances about me. It is very simple: make known to everyone that you

[040-148]are in no way connected with the disgrace I have brought upon myself.

[040-149]Put an advertisement in the newspapers to that effect, if you like--as

[040-150]men do about their wives’ debts. I have chosen my part. I can’t stultify

[040-151]myself to please you.’

[040-152]She knew that this was final. His voice had the true ring of shame in

[040-153]revolt.

[040-154]‘Then go your way, and I will go mine!’

[040-155]Amy left the room.

[040-156]When Reardon went into the bedchamber an hour later, he unfolded a

[040-157]chair-bedstead that stood there, threw some rugs upon it, and so lay

[040-158]down to pass the night. He did not close his eyes. Amy slept for an hour

[040-159]or two before dawn, and on waking she started up and looked anxiously

[040-160]about the room. But neither spoke.

[040-161]There was a pretence of ordinary breakfast; the little servant

[040-162]necessitated that. When she saw her husband preparing to go out, Amy

[040-163]asked him to come into the study.

[040-164]‘How long shall you be away?’ she asked, curtly.

[040-165]‘It is doubtful. I am going to look for rooms.’

[040-166]‘Then no doubt I shall be gone when you come back. There’s no object,

[040-167]now, in my staying here till to-morrow.’

[040-168]‘As you please.’

[040-169]‘Do you wish Lizzie still to come?’

[040-170]‘No. Please to pay her wages and dismiss her. Here is some money.’

[040-171]‘I think you had better let me see to that.’

[040-172]He flung the coin on to the table and opened the door. Amy stepped

[040-173]quickly forward and closed it again.

[040-174]‘This is our good-bye, is it?’ she asked, her eyes on the ground.

[040-175]‘As you wish it--yes.’

[040-176]‘You will remember that I have not wished it.’
