# Publishing Trade

Between the writer and the reader stands the publisher — and in *New Grub Street*, the publisher is the dominant partner in the relationship. The novel anatomizes the commercial apparatus of late-Victorian publishing with sociological precision, showing how the business decisions of publishers and booksellers determine the literary output of an entire culture.

## The Publisher as Gatekeeper

Publishers in the novel are not patrons of literature but commercial operators making calculations about marketability. Reardon cannot find a publisher willing to take a chance on his serious fiction; Milvain, who supplies exactly what the market wants, has no such difficulty. The publisher's judgment is not literary but commercial — what will sell, not what is good.

The novel's specific references to "two publishing houses who have withdrawn" (003-030) from certain kinds of publishing illustrate the contraction of the market for serious fiction. Publishers are consolidating around sure commercial bets, leaving writers like Reardon with nowhere to go.

## Mudie's and the Circulating Libraries

Charles Mudie's circulating library — and its competitors — were the primary purchasers of three-volume novels in Victorian Britain. Mudie's buying power effectively determined which novels reached readers and which did not. Gissing's characters are acutely aware of this dependency: a novel rejected by Mudie's is commercially dead, regardless of its literary merit. The circulating library system centralized literary gatekeeping in a single commercial entity whose tastes shaped the entire market.

## Booksellers and the Retail Chain

The bookseller — from the London retailer through which Alfred Yule's brother "drifted from work at a London bookseller's into the modern Grub Street" (002-102–002-103) to the provincial shops that stock or decline to stock new titles — represents the final link in the commercial chain. The novelist is not a free agent but a producer in a vertically integrated industry, and at every level, commercial logic prevails over literary value.

## The Disappearing Middle

Gissing depicts a publishing industry that is both more efficient and less hospitable to serious literature than ever before. The old system of patronage and subscription is gone; the new system of mass-market publishing has not yet arrived. In the transitional moment of the 1880s, the publishing trade can sustain hacks and sensation-mongers, but the serious novelist who refuses to commodify his work has no institutional support.
