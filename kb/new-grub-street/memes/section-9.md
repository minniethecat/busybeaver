# Section 9

[008-001]‘Don’t use that word, [[amy-reardon|Amy]]. I hate it!’

[008-002]‘You can’t afford to hate it,’ was her rejoinder, in very practical

[008-003]tones. ‘However it was before, you must write for the market now. You

[008-004]have admitted that yourself.’

[008-005]He kept silence.

[008-006]‘Where are you?’ she went on to ask. ‘What have you actually done?’

[008-007]‘Two short chapters of a story I can’t go on with. The three volumes lie

[008-008]before me like an interminable desert. Impossible to get through them.

[008-009]The idea is stupidly artificial, and I haven’t a living character in

[008-010]it.’

[008-011]‘The public don’t care whether the characters are living or not.--Don’t

[008-012]stand behind me, like that; it’s such an awkward way of talking. Come

[008-013]and sit down.’

[008-014]He drew away, and came to a position whence he could see her face, but

[008-015]kept at a distance.

[008-016]‘Yes,’ he said, in a different way, ‘that’s the worst of it.’

[008-017]‘What is?’

[008-018]‘That you--well, it’s no use.’

[008-019]‘That I--what?’

[008-020]She did not look at him; her lips, after she had spoken, drew in a

[008-021]little.

[008-022]‘That your disposition towards me is being affected by this miserable

[008-023]failure. You keep saying to yourself that I am not what you thought me.

[008-024]Perhaps you even feel that I have been guilty of a sort of deception. I

[008-025]don’t blame you; it’s natural enough.’

[008-026]‘I’ll tell you quite honestly what I do think,’ she replied, after a

[008-027]short silence. ‘You are much weaker than I imagined. Difficulties crush

[008-028]you, instead of rousing you to struggle.’

[008-029]‘True. It has always been my fault.’

[008-030]‘But don’t you feel it’s rather unmanly, this state of things? You say

[008-031]you love me, and I try to believe it. But whilst you are saying so, you

[008-032]let me get nearer and nearer to miserable, hateful [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. What is to

[008-033]become of me--of us? Shall you sit here day after day until our last

[008-034]shilling is spent?’

[008-035]‘No; of course I must do something.’

[008-036]‘When shall you begin in earnest? In a day or two you must pay this

[008-037]quarter’s rent, and that will leave us just about fifteen pounds in the

[008-038]world. Where is the rent at Christmas to come from?

[008-039]What are we to live upon? There’s all sorts of clothing to be bought;

[008-040]there’ll be all the extra expenses of winter. Surely it’s bad enough

[008-041]that we have had to stay here all the summer; no holiday of any kind. I

[008-042]have done my best not to grumble about it, but I begin to think that it

[008-043]would be very much wiser if I did grumble.’

[008-044]She squared her shoulders, and gave her head just a little shake, as if

[008-045]a fly had troubled her.

[008-046]‘You bear everything very well and kindly,’ said [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]. ‘My behaviour

[008-047]is contemptible; I know that. Good heavens! if I only had some business

[008-048]to go to, something I could work at in any state of mind, and make [[money-in-literature|money]]

[008-049]out of! Given this chance, I would work myself to death rather than you

[008-050]should lack anything you desire. But I am at the mercy of my brain; it

[008-051]is dry and powerless. How I envy those clerks who go by to their offices

[008-052]in the morning! There’s the day’s work cut out for them; no question

[008-053]of mood and feeling; they have just to work at something, and when the

[008-054]evening comes, they have earned their wages, they are free to rest and

[008-055]enjoy themselves. What an insane thing it is to make literature one’s

[008-056]only means of support! When the most trivial accident may at any time

[008-057]prove fatal to one’s power of work for weeks or months. No, that is the

[008-058]unpardonable sin! To make a trade of an art! I am rightly served for

[008-059]attempting such a brutal folly.’

[008-060]He turned away in a passion of misery.

[008-061]‘How very silly it is to talk like this!’ came in Amy’s voice, clearly

[008-062]critical. ‘Art must be practised as a trade, at all events in our time.

[008-063]This is the age of trade. Of course if one refuses to be of one’s time,

[008-064]and yet hasn’t the means to live independently, what can result but

[008-065]breakdown and wretchedness? The fact of the matter is, you could do

[008-066]fairly good work, and work which would sell, if only you would bring

[008-067]yourself to look at things in a more practical way. It’s what [[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]]

[008-068]is always saying, you know.’

[008-069]‘Milvain’s temperament is very different from mine. He is naturally

[008-070]light-hearted and hopeful; I am naturally the opposite.

[008-071]What you and he say is true enough; the misfortune is that I can’t act

[008-072]upon it. I am no uncompromising artistic pedant; I am quite willing to

[008-073]try and do the kind of work that will sell; under the circumstances it

[008-074]would be a kind of insanity if I refused. But power doesn’t answer

[008-075]to the will. My efforts are utterly vain; I suppose the prospect of

[008-076]pennilessness is itself a hindrance; the fear haunts me. With such

[008-077]terrible real things pressing upon me, my imagination can shape nothing

[008-078]substantial. When I have laboured out a story, I suddenly see it in

[008-079]a light of such contemptible triviality that to work at it is an

[008-080]impossible thing.’

[008-081]‘You are ill, that’s the fact of the matter. You ought to have had a

[008-082]holiday. I think even now you had better go away for a week or two. Do,

[008-083]Edwin!’

[008-084]‘Impossible! It would be the merest pretence of holiday. To go away and

[008-085]leave you here--no!’

[008-086]‘Shall I ask mother or Jack to lend us some money?’

[008-087]‘That would be intolerable.’

[008-088]‘But this state of things is intolerable!’

[008-089]Reardon walked the length of the room and back again.

[008-090]‘Your mother has no money to lend, dear, and your brother would do it so

[008-091]unwillingly that we can’t lay ourselves under such an obligation.’

[008-092]‘Yet it will come to that, you know,’ remarked Amy, calmly.

[008-093]‘No, it shall not come to that. I must and will get something done long

[008-094]before Christmas. If only you--’

[008-095]He came and took one of her hands.

[008-096]‘If only you will give me more sympathy, dearest. You see, that’s one

[008-097]side of my weakness. I am utterly dependent upon you. Your kindness is

[008-098]the breath of life to me. Don’t refuse it!’

[008-099]‘But I have done nothing of the kind.’

[008-100]‘You begin to speak very coldly. And I understand your feeling of

[008-101]disappointment. The mere fact of your urging me to do anything that will

[008-102]sell is a proof of bitter disappointment. You would have looked with

[008-103]scorn at anyone who talked to me like that two years ago. You were proud

[008-104]of me because my work wasn’t altogether common, and because I had never

[008-105]written a line that was meant to attract the vulgar. All that’s over

[008-106]now. If you knew how dreadful it is to see that you have lost your hopes

[008-107]of me!’

[008-108]‘Well, but I haven’t--altogether,’ Amy replied, meditatively. ‘I know

[008-109]very well that, if you had a lot of money, you would do better things

[008-110]than ever.’

[008-111]‘Thank you a thousand times for saying that, my dearest.’

[008-112]‘But, you see, we haven’t money, and there’s little chance of our

[008-113]getting any. That scrubby old uncle won’t leave anything to us; I feel

[008-114]too sure of it. I often feel disposed to go and beg him on my knees to

[008-115]think of us in his will.’ She laughed. ‘I suppose it’s impossible, and

[008-116]would be useless; but I should be capable of it if I knew it would bring

[008-117]money.’

[008-118]Reardon said nothing.

[008-119]‘I didn’t think so much of money when we were married,’ Amy

[008-120]continued. ‘I had never seriously felt the want of it, you know. I did

[008-121]think--there’s no harm in confessing it--that you were sure to be rich

[008-122]some day; but I should have married you all the same if I had known that

[008-123]you would win only [[literary-success|reputation]].’

[008-124]‘You are sure of that?’

[008-125]‘Well, I think so. But I know the value of money better now. I know it

[008-126]is the most powerful thing in the world. If I had to choose between

[008-127]a glorious reputation with poverty and a contemptible popularity with

[008-128]wealth, I should choose the latter.’

[008-129]‘No!’

[008-130]‘I should.’

[008-131]‘Perhaps you are right.’

[008-132]He turned away with a sigh.

[008-133]‘Yes, you are right. What is reputation? If it is deserved, it

[008-134]originates with a few score of people among the many millions who would

[008-135]never have recognised the merit they at last applaud. That’s the lot of

[008-136]a great [[literary-genius|genius]]. As for a mediocrity like me--what ludicrous absurdity to

[008-137]fret myself in the hope that half-a-dozen folks will say I am “above the

[008-138]average!” After all, is there sillier vanity than this? A year after I

[008-139]have published my last book, I shall be practically forgotten; ten years

[008-140]later, I shall be as absolutely forgotten as one of those novelists of

[008-141]the early part of this century, whose names one doesn’t even recognise.

[008-142]What fatuous posing!’

[008-143]Amy looked askance at him, but replied nothing.

[008-144]‘And yet,’ he continued, ‘of course it isn’t only for the sake of

[008-145]reputation that one tries to do uncommon work. There’s the shrinking

[008-146]from conscious insincerity of workmanship--which most of the writers

[008-147]nowadays seem never to feel. “It’s good enough for the market”; that

[008-148]satisfies them. And perhaps they are justified.

[008-149]I can’t pretend that I rule my life by absolute ideals; I admit that

[008-150]everything is relative. There is no such thing as goodness or badness,

[008-151]in the absolute sense, of course. Perhaps I am absurdly inconsistent

[008-152]when--though knowing my work can’t be first rate--I strive to make it as

[008-153]good as possible. I don’t say this in irony, Amy; I really mean it. It

[008-154]may very well be that I am just as foolish as the people I ridicule for

[008-155]moral and religious superstition. This habit of mine is superstitious.

[008-156]How well I can imagine the answer of some popular novelist if he heard

[008-157]me speak scornfully of his books. “My dear fellow,” he might say, “do

[008-158]you suppose I am not aware that my books are rubbish? I know it just

[008-159]as well as you do. But my vocation is to live comfortably. I have a

[008-160]luxurious house, a wife and children who are happy and grateful to me

[008-161]for their happiness. If you choose to live in a garret, and, what’s

[008-162]worse, make your wife and children share it with you, that’s your

[008-163]concern.” The man would be abundantly right.’

[008-164]‘But,’ said Amy, ‘why should you assume that his books are rubbish? Good

[008-165]work succeeds--now and then.’

[008-166]‘I speak of the common kind of [[literary-ambition|success]], which is never due to

[008-167]literary merit. And if I speak bitterly, well, I am suffering from my

[008-168]powerlessness. I am a failure, my poor girl, and it isn’t easy for me to

[008-169]look with charity on the success of men who deserved it far less than I

[008-170]did, when I was still able to work.’

[008-171]‘Of course, Edwin, if you make up your mind that you are a failure,

[008-172]you will end by being so. But I’m convinced there’s no reason that you

[008-173]should fail to make a living with your pen. Now let me advise you; put

[008-174]aside all your strict ideas about what is worthy and what is unworthy,

[008-175]and just act upon my advice. It’s impossible for you to write a

[008-176][[three-volume-system|three-volume novel]]; very well, then do a short story of a kind that’s

[008-177]likely to be popular. You know Mr Milvain is always saying that the long

[008-178]novel has had its day, and that in future people will write shilling

[008-179]books. Why not try?

[008-180]Give yourself a week to invent a sensational plot, and then a fortnight

[008-181]for the writing. Have it ready for the new season at the end of October.

[008-182]If you like, don’t put your name to it; your name certainly would have

[008-183]no weight with this sort of public. Just make it a matter of business,

[008-184]as Mr Milvain says, and see if you can’t earn some money.’
