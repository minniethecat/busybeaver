# Section 53

[052-001]‘[[marian-yule|Marian]] hasn’t been yet.’

[052-002][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] seemed to pay no attention; she looked up at him, and saw that he

[052-003]was in thought.

[052-004]‘Did you go to those people last night?’ she inquired.

[052-005]‘Yes. By-the-bye, Miss Rupert was there.’

[052-006]He spoke as if the name would be familiar to his hearer, but [[dora-milvain|Dora]] seemed

[052-007]at a loss.

[052-008]‘Who is Miss Rupert?’

[052-009]‘Didn’t I tell you about her? I thought I did. Oh, I met her first of

[052-010]all at Barlow’s, just after we got back from the seaside. Rather an

[052-011]interesting girl. She’s a daughter of Manton Rupert, the advertising

[052-012]agent. I want to get invited to their house; useful people, you know.’

[052-013]‘But is an advertising agent a gentleman?’

[052-014]Jasper laughed.

[052-015]‘Do you think of him as a bill-poster? At all events he is enormously

[052-016]wealthy, and has a magnificent house at Chislehurst. The girl goes about

[052-017]with her stepmother. I call her a girl, but she must be nearly thirty,

[052-018]and Mrs Rupert looks only two or three years older. I had quite a long

[052-019]talk with her--Miss Rupert, I mean--last night. She told me she was

[052-020]going to stay next week with the Barlows, so I shall have a run out to

[052-021]Wimbledon one afternoon.’

[052-022]Dora looked at him inquiringly.

[052-023]‘Just to see Miss Rupert?’ she asked, meeting his eyes.

[052-024]‘To be sure. Why not?’

[052-025]‘Oh!’ ejaculated his sister, as if the question did not concern her.

[052-026]‘She isn’t exactly good-looking,’ pursued Jasper, meditatively, with a

[052-027]quick glance at the listener, ‘but fairly intellectual. Plays very well,

[052-028]and has a nice contralto voice; she sang that new thing of Tosti’s--what

[052-029]do you call it? I thought her rather masculine when I first saw her, but

[052-030]the impression wears off when one knows her better. She rather takes to

[052-031]me, I fancy.’

[052-032]‘But--’ began Dora, after a minute’s silence.

[052-033]‘But what?’ inquired her brother with an air of interest.

[052-034]‘I don’t quite understand you.’

[052-035]‘In general, or with reference to some particular?’

[052-036]‘What right have you to go to places just to see this Miss Rupert?’

[052-037]‘What right?’ He laughed. ‘I am a young man with my way to make. I can’t

[052-038]afford to lose any opportunity. If Miss Rupert is so good as to take an

[052-039]interest in me, I have no objection. She’s old enough to make friends

[052-040]for herself.’

[052-041]‘Oh, then you consider her simply a friend?’

[052-042]‘I shall see how things go on.’

[052-043]‘But, pray, do you consider yourself perfectly free?’ asked Dora, with

[052-044]some indignation.

[052-045]‘Why shouldn’t I?’

[052-046]‘Then I think you have been behaving very strangely.’

[052-047]Jasper saw that she was in earnest. He stroked the back of his head and

[052-048]smiled at the wall.

[052-049]‘With regard to Marian, you mean?’

[052-050]‘Of course I do.’

[052-051]‘But Marian understands me perfectly. I have never for a moment tried

[052-052]to make her think that--well, to put it plainly, that I was in love with

[052-053]her. In all our conversations it has been my one object to afford her

[052-054]insight into my character, and to explain my position. She has no excuse

[052-055]whatever for misinterpreting me. And I feel assured that she has done

[052-056]nothing of the kind.’

[052-057]‘Very well, if you feel satisfied with yourself--’

[052-058]‘But come now, Dora; what’s all this about? You are Marian’s friend,

[052-059]and, of course, I don’t wish you to say a word about her.

[052-060]But let me explain myself. I have occasionally walked part of the way

[052-061]home with Marian, when she and I have happened to go from here at the

[052-062]same time; now there was nothing whatever in our talk at such times that

[052-063]anyone mightn’t have listened to. We are both intellectual people, and

[052-064]we talk in an intellectual way. You seem to have rather old-fashioned

[052-065]ideas--provincial ideas. A girl like Marian [[alfred-yule|Yule]] claims the new

[052-066]privileges of woman; she would resent it if you supposed that she

[052-067]couldn’t be friendly with a man without attributing “intentions” to

[052-068]him--to use the old word. We don’t live in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]], where liberty

[052-069]is rendered impossible by the cackling of gossips.’

[052-070]‘No, but--’

[052-071]‘Well?’

[052-072]‘It seems to me rather strange, that’s all. We had better not talk about

[052-073]it any more.’

[052-074]‘But I have only just begun to talk about it; I must try to make

[052-075]my position intelligible to you. Now, suppose--a quite impossible

[052-076]thing--that Marian inherited some twenty or thirty thousand pounds; I

[052-077]should forthwith ask her to be my wife.’

[052-078]‘Oh indeed!’

[052-079]‘I see no reason for sarcasm. It would be a most rational proceeding.

[052-080]I like her very much; but to marry her (supposing she would have me)

[052-081]without [[money-in-literature|money]] would he a gross absurdity, simply spoiling my career, and

[052-082]leading to all sorts of discontents.’

[052-083]‘No one would suggest that you should marry as things are.’

[052-084]‘No; but please to bear in mind that to obtain money somehow or

[052-085]other--and I see no other way than by [[literary-marriage|marriage]]--is necessary to me, and

[052-086]that with as little delay as possible. I am not at all likely to get a

[052-087]big editorship for some years to come, and I don’t feel disposed to make

[052-088]myself prematurely old by toiling for a few hundreds per annum in the

[052-089]meantime. Now all this I have frankly and fully explained to Marian. I

[052-090]dare say she suspects what I should do if she came into possession of

[052-091]money; there’s no harm in that. But she knows perfectly well that, as

[052-092]things are, we remain intellectual friends.’

[052-093]‘Then listen to me, Jasper. If we hear that Marian gets nothing from her

[052-094]uncle, you had better behave honestly, and let her see that you haven’t

[052-095]as much interest in her as before.’

[052-096]‘That would be brutality.’

[052-097]‘It would be honest.’

[052-098]‘Well, no, it wouldn’t. Strictly speaking, my interest in Marian

[052-099]wouldn’t suffer at all. I should know that we could be nothing but

[052-100]friends, that’s all. Hitherto I haven’t known what might come to pass;

[052-101]I don’t know yet. So far from following your advice, I shall let Marian

[052-102]understand that, if anything, I am more her friend than ever, seeing

[052-103]that henceforth there can be no ambiguities.’

[052-104]‘I can only tell you that Maud would agree with me in what I have been

[052-105]saying.’

[052-106]‘Then both of you have distorted views.’

[052-107]‘I think not. It’s you who are unprincipled.’

[052-108]‘My dear girl, haven’t I been showing you that no man could be more

[052-109]above-board, more straightforward?’

[052-110]‘You have been talking nonsense, Jasper.’

[052-111]‘Nonsense? Oh, this female lack of logic! Then my argument has been

[052-112]utterly thrown away. Now that’s one of the things I like in Miss Rupert;

[052-113]she can follow an argument and see consequences. And for that matter so

[052-114]can Marian. I only wish it were possible to refer this question to her.’

[052-115]There was a tap at the door. Dora called ‘Come in!’ and Marian herself

[052-116]appeared.

[052-117]‘What an odd thing!’ exclaimed Jasper, lowering his voice. ‘I was that

[052-118]moment saying I wished it were possible to refer a question to you.’

[052-119]Dora reddened, and stood in an embarrassed attitude.

[052-120]‘It was the old dispute whether women in general are capable of logic.

[052-121]But pardon me, Miss Yule; I forget that you have been occupied with sad

[052-122]things since I last saw you.’

[052-123]Dora led her to a chair, asking if her father had returned.

[052-124]‘Yes, he came back yesterday.’

[052-125]Jasper and his sister could not think it likely that Marian had suffered

[052-126]much from grief at her uncle’s death; practically [[john-yule|John Yule]] was a

[052-127]stranger to her. Yet her face bore the signs of acute mental trouble,

[052-128]and it seemed as if some agitation made it difficult for her to speak.

[052-129]The awkward silence that fell upon the three was broken by Jasper, who

[052-130]expressed a regret that he was obliged to take his leave.

[052-131]‘Maud is becoming a young lady of society,’ he said--just for the sake

[052-132]of saying something--as he moved towards the door. ‘If she comes back

[052-133]whilst you are here, Miss Yule, warn her that that is the path of

[052-134]destruction for literary people.’

[052-135]‘You should bear that in mind yourself’ remarked Dora, with a

[052-136]significant look.

[052-137]‘Oh, I am cool-headed enough to make society serve my own ends.’

[052-138]Marian turned her head with a sudden movement which was checked before

[052-139]she had quite looked round to him. The phrase he uttered last appeared

[052-140]to have affected her in some way; her eyes fell, and an expression of

[052-141]pain was on her brows for a moment.

[052-142]‘I can only stay a few minutes,’ she said, bending with a faint smile

[052-143]towards Dora, as soon as they were alone. ‘I have come on my way from

[052-144]the Museum.’

[052-145]‘Where you have tired yourself to death as usual, I can see.’

[052-146]‘No; I have done scarcely anything. I only pretended to read; my mind is

[052-147]too much troubled. Have you heard anything about my uncle’s will?’

[052-148]‘Nothing whatever.’

[052-149]‘I thought it might have been spoken of in Wattleborough, and some

[052-150]friend might have written to you. But I suppose there has hardly been

[052-151]time for that. I shall surprise you very much. Father receives nothing,

[052-152]but I have a legacy of five thousand pounds.’

[052-153]Dora kept her eyes down.

[052-154]‘Then--what do you think?’ continued Marian. ‘My cousin [[amy-reardon|Amy]] has ten

[052-155]thousand pounds.’

[052-156]‘Good gracious! What a difference that will make!’

[052-157]‘Yes, indeed. And her brother John has six thousand. But nothing to

[052-158]their mother. There are a good many other legacies, but most of

[052-159]the property goes to the Wattleborough park--“Yule Park” it will be

[052-160]called--and to the volunteers, and things of that kind. They say he

[052-161]wasn’t as rich as people thought.’

[052-162]‘Do you know what Miss Harrow gets?’

[052-163]‘She has the house for her life, and fifteen hundred pounds.’

[052-164]‘And your father nothing whatever?’

[052-165]‘Nothing. Not a penny. Oh I am so grieved! I think it so unkind, so

[052-166]wrong. Amy and her brother to have sixteen thousand pounds and father

[052-167]nothing! I can’t understand it. There was no unkind feeling between him

[052-168]and father. He knew what a hard life father has had. Doesn’t it seem

[052-169]heartless?’

[052-170]‘What does your father say?’

[052-171]‘I think he feels the unkindness more than he does the disappointment;

[052-172]of course he must have expected something. He came into the room where

[052-173]mother and I were, and sat down, and began to tell us about the will

[052-174]just as if he were speaking to strangers about something he had read in

[052-175]the newspaper--that’s the only way I can describe it. Then he got up and

[052-176]went away into the study. I waited a little, and then went to him there;

[052-177]he was sitting at work, as if he hadn’t been away from home at all. I

[052-178]tried to tell him how sorry I was, but I couldn’t say anything. I began

[052-179]to cry foolishly. He spoke kindly to me, far more kindly than he has

[052-180]done for a long time; but he wouldn’t talk about the will, and I had to

[052-181]go away and leave him. Poor mother! for all she was afraid that we were

[052-182]going to be rich, is broken-hearted at his disappointment.’

[052-183]‘Your mother was afraid?’ said Dora.

[052-184]‘Because she thought herself unfitted for life in a large house, and

[052-185]feared we should think her in our way.’ She smiled sadly. ‘Poor mother!

[052-186]she is so humble and so good. I do hope that father will be kinder to

[052-187]her. But there’s no telling yet what the result of this may be. I feel

[052-188]guilty when I stand before him.’

[052-189]‘But he must feel glad that you have five thousand pounds.’

[052-190]Marian delayed her reply for a moment, her eyes down.

[052-191]‘Yes, perhaps he is glad of that.’

[052-192]‘Perhaps!’

[052-193]‘He can’t help thinking, Dora, what use he could have made of it.

[052-194]It has always been his greatest wish to have a literary paper of his

[052-195]own--like [[the-study|The Study]], you know. He would have used the money in that way,

[052-196]I am sure.’
