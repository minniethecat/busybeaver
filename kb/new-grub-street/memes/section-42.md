# Section 42

[041-001]‘In that case, you have only to go with me to the new home.’

[041-002]‘I can’t.’

[041-003]‘Then you have made your choice.’

[041-004]She did not prevent his opening the door this time, and he passed out

[041-005]without looking at her.

[041-006]His return was at three in the afternoon. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] and the child were gone;

[041-007]the servant was gone. The table in the dining-room was spread as if for

[041-008]one person’s meal.

[041-009]He went into the bedroom. Amy’s trunks had disappeared. The child’s cot

[041-010]was covered over. In the study, he saw that the sovereign he had thrown

[041-011]on to the table still lay in the same place.

[041-012]As it was a very cold day he lit a fire. Whilst it burnt up he sat

[041-013]reading a torn portion of a newspaper, and became quite interested in

[041-014]the report of a commercial meeting in the City, a thing he would never

[041-015]have glanced at under ordinary circumstances. The fragment fell at

[041-016]length from his hands; his head drooped; he sank into a troubled sleep.

[041-017][[About]] six he had tea, then began the packing of the few books that were

[041-018]to go with him, and of such other things as could be enclosed in box

[041-019]or portmanteau. After a couple of hours of this occupation he could no

[041-020]longer resist his weariness, so he went to bed. Before falling asleep

[041-021]he heard the two familiar clocks strike eight; this evening they were

[041-022]in unusual accord, and the querulous notes from the workhouse sounded

[041-023]between the deeper ones from St [[marylebone|Marylebone]]. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] tried to remember

[041-024]when he had last observed this; the matter seemed to have a peculiar

[041-025]interest for him, and in dreams he worried himself with a grotesque

[041-026]speculation thence derived.

[041-027]CHAPTER XVIII. THE OLD HOME

[041-028]Before her [[literary-marriage|marriage]] [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]] was one of seven motherless sisters

[041-029]who constituted the family of a dentist slenderly provided in the matter

[041-030]of income. The pinching and paring which was a chief employment of her

[041-031]energies in those early days had disagreeable effects upon a character

[041-032]disposed rather to generosity than the reverse; during her husband’s

[041-033]lifetime she had enjoyed rather too eagerly all the good things which he

[041-034]put at her command, sometimes forgetting that a wife has duties as

[041-035]well as claims, and in her widowhood she indulged a pretentiousness

[041-036]and querulousness which were the natural, but not amiable, results of

[041-037]suddenly restricted circumstances.

[041-038]Like the majority of [[london|London]] people, she occupied a house of which the

[041-039]rent absurdly exceeded the due proportion of her income, a pleasant

[041-040]foible turned to such good account by London landlords. Whereas she

[041-041]might have lived with a good deal of modest comfort, her existence was a

[041-042]perpetual effort to conceal the squalid background of what was meant for

[041-043]the eyes of her friends and neighbours. She kept only two servants, who

[041-044]were so ill paid and so relentlessly overworked that it was seldom they

[041-045]remained with her for more than three months. In dealings with other

[041-046]people whom she perforce employed, she was often guilty of incredible

[041-047]meanness; as, for instance, when she obliged her half-starved dressmaker

[041-048]to purchase material for her, and then postponed payment alike for

[041-049]that and for the work itself to the last possible moment. This was not

[041-050]heartlessness in the strict sense of the word; the woman not only knew

[041-051]that her behaviour was shameful, she was in truth ashamed of it and

[041-052]sorry for her victims. But life was a battle. She must either crush or

[041-053]be crushed. With sufficient means, she would have defrauded no one, and

[041-054]would have behaved generously to many; with barely enough for her needs,

[041-055]she set her face and defied her feelings, inasmuch as she believed there

[041-056]was no choice.

[041-057]She would shed tears over a pitiful story of want, and without shadow of

[041-058]hypocrisy. It was hard, it was cruel; such things oughtn’t to be allowed

[041-059]in a world where there were so many rich people. The next day she would

[041-060]argue with her charwoman about halfpence, and end by paying the poor

[041-061]creature what she knew was inadequate and unjust. For the simplest

[041-062]reason: she hadn’t more to give, without submitting to privations which

[041-063]she considered intolerable.

[041-064]But whilst she could be a positive hyena to strangers, to those who were

[041-065]akin to her, and those of whom she was fond, her affectionate kindness

[041-066]was remarkable. One observes this peculiarity often enough; it reminds

[041-067]one how savage the social conflict is, in which those little groups of

[041-068]people stand serried against their common enemies; relentless to all

[041-069]others, among themselves only the more tender and zealous because of

[041-070]the ever-impending danger. No mother was ever more devoted. Her son, a

[041-071]gentleman of quite noteworthy selfishness, had board and lodging beneath

[041-072]her roof on nominal terms, and under no stress of pecuniary trouble had

[041-073]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] called upon him to make the slightest sacrifice on her behalf.

[041-074]Her daughter she loved with profound tenderness, and had no will that

[041-075]was opposed to Amy’s. And it was characteristic of her that her children

[041-076]were never allowed to understand of what baseness she often became

[041-077]guilty in the determination to support appearances. [[john-yule|John Yule]] naturally

[041-078]suspected what went on behind the scenes; on one occasion--since Amy’s

[041-079]marriage--he had involuntarily overheard a dialogue between his mother

[041-080]and a servant on the point of departing which made even him feel

[041-081]ashamed. But from Amy every paltriness and meanness had always been

[041-082]concealed with the utmost care; Mrs Yule did not scruple to lie

[041-083]heroically when in danger of being detected by her daughter.

[041-084]Yet this energetic lady had no social ambitions that pointed above her

[041-085]own stratum. She did not aim at intimacy with her superiors; merely at

[041-086]superiority among her intimates. Her circle was not large, but in that

[041-087]circle she must be regarded with the respect due to a woman of refined

[041-088]tastes and personal distinction. Her little dinners might be of rare

[041-089]occurrence, but to be invited must be felt a privilege. ‘Mrs Edmund

[041-090]Yule’ must sound well on people’s lips; never be the occasion of those

[041-091]peculiar smiles which she herself was rather fond of indulging at the

[041-092]mention of other people’s names.

[041-093]The question of Amy’s marriage had been her constant thought from the

[041-094]time when the little girl shot into a woman grown. For Amy no common

[041-095]match, no acceptance of a husband merely for [[money-in-literature|money]] or position. Few men

[041-096]who walked the earth were mates for Amy. But years went on, and the man

[041-097]of undeniable distinction did not yet present himself. Suitors offered,

[041-098]but Amy smiled coldly at their addresses, in private not seldom

[041-099]scornfully, and her mother, though growing anxious, approved. Then of a

[041-100]sudden appeared Edwin Reardon.

[041-101]A literary man? Well, it was one mode of distinction. Happily, a

[041-102]novelist; novelists now and then had considerable social [[literary-ambition|success]].

[041-103]Mr Reardon, it was true, did not impress one as a man likely to push

[041-104]forward where the battle called for rude vigour, but Amy soon assured

[041-105]herself that he would have a [[literary-success|reputation]] far other than that of the

[041-106]average successful storyteller. The best people would regard him; he

[041-107]would be welcomed in the penetralia of [[literary-culture|culture]]; superior persons would

[041-108]say: ‘Oh, I don’t read novels as a rule, but of course Mr Reardon’s--’

[041-109]If that really were to be the case, all was well; for Mrs Yule could

[041-110]appreciate social and intellectual differences.

[041-111]Alas! alas! What was the end of those shining anticipations?

[041-112]First of all, Mrs Yule began to make less frequent mention of ‘my

[041-113]son-in-law, Mr Edwin Reardon.’ Next, she never uttered his name save

[041-114]when inquiries necessitated it. Then, the most intimate of her intimates

[041-115]received little hints which were not quite easy to interpret.

[041-116]‘Mr Reardon is growing so very eccentric--has an odd distaste for

[041-117]society--occupies himself with all sorts of out-of-the-way interests.

[041-118]No, I’m afraid we shan’t have another of his novels for some time.

[041-119]I think he writes anonymously a good deal. And really, such curious

[041-120]eccentricities!’ Many were the tears she wept after her depressing

[041-121]colloquies with Amy; and, as was to be expected, she thought severely

[041-122]of the cause of these sorrows. On the last occasion when he came to

[041-123]her house she received him with such extreme civility that Reardon

[041-124]thenceforth disliked her, whereas before he had only thought her a

[041-125]good-natured and silly woman.

[041-126]Alas for Amy’s marriage with a man of distinction! From step to step of

[041-127]descent, till here was downright catastrophe. Bitter enough in itself,

[041-128]but most lamentable with reference to the friends of the family. How was

[041-129]it to be explained, this return of Amy to her home for several months,

[041-130]whilst her husband was no further away than Worthing? The bald, horrible

[041-131]truth--impossible! Yet [[jasper-milvain|Mr Milvain]] knew it, and the Carters must guess

[041-132]it. What colour could be thrown upon such vulgar distress?

[041-133]The worst was not yet. It declared itself. this May morning, when, quite

[041-134]unexpectedly, a cab drove up to the house, bringing Amy and her child,

[041-135]and her trunks, and her band-boxes, and her what-nots.

[041-136]From the dining-room window Mrs Yule was aware of this arrival, and in a

[041-137]few moments she learnt the unspeakable cause.

[041-138]She burst into tears, genuine as ever woman shed.

[041-139]‘There’s no use in that, mother,’ said Amy, whose temper was in a

[041-140]dangerous state. ‘Nothing worse can happen, that’s one consolation.’

[041-141]‘Oh, it’s disgraceful! disgraceful!’ sobbed Mrs Yule. ‘What we are to

[041-142]say I can NOT think.’

[041-143]‘I shall say nothing whatever. People can scarcely have the impertinence

[041-144]to ask us questions when we have shown that they are unwelcome.’

[041-145]‘But there are some people I can’t help giving some explanation to. My

[041-146]dear child, he is not in his right mind. I’m convinced of it, there! He

[041-147]is not in his right mind.’

[041-148]‘That’s nonsense, mother. He is as sane as I am.’

[041-149]‘But you have often said what strange things he says and does; you know

[041-150]you have, Amy. That talking in his sleep; I’ve thought a great deal of

[041-151]it since you told me about that. And--and so many other things. My love,

[041-152]I shall give it to be understood that he has become so very odd in his

[041-153]ways that--’

[041-154]‘I can’t have that,’ replied Amy with decision. ‘Don’t you see that in

[041-155]that case I should be behaving very badly?’

[041-156]‘I can’t see that at all. There are many reasons, as you know very well,

[041-157]why one shouldn’t live with a husband who is at all suspected of mental

[041-158]derangement. You have done your utmost for him. And this would be some

[041-159]sort of explanation, you know. I am so convinced that there is truth in

[041-160]it, too.’

[041-161]‘Of course I can’t prevent you from saying what you like, but I think it

[041-162]would be very wrong to start a rumour of this kind.’

[041-163]There was less resolve in this utterance. Amy mused, and looked

[041-164]wretched.

[041-165]‘Come up to the drawing-room, dear,’ said her mother, for they had held

[041-166]their conversation in the room nearest to the house-door. ‘What a state

[041-167]your mind must be in! Oh dear! Oh dear!’

[041-168]She was a slender, well-proportioned woman, still pretty in face, and

[041-169]dressed in a way that emphasised her abiding charms. Her voice had

[041-170]something of plaintiveness, and altogether she was of frailer type than

[041-171]her daughter.

[041-172]‘Is my room ready?’ Amy inquired on the stairs.

[041-173]‘I’m sorry to say it isn’t, dear, as I didn’t expect you till tomorrow.

[041-174]But it shall be seen to immediately.’

[041-175]This addition to the household was destined to cause grave difficulties

[041-176]with the domestic slaves. But Mrs Yule would prove equal to the

[041-177]occasion. On Amy’s behalf she would have worked her servants till they

[041-178]perished of exhaustion before her eyes.

[041-179]‘Use my room for the present,’ she added. ‘I think the girl has finished

[041-180]up there. But wait here; I’ll just go and see to things.’
