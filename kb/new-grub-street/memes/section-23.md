# Section 23

[022-001]‘Yes, there’s hope once more, my dearest. No more gloomy talk to-night!

[022-002]I have read you something, now you shall read something to me; it is a

[022-003]long time since I delighted myself with listening to you. What shall it

[022-004]be?’

[022-005]‘I feel rather too tired to-night.’

[022-006]‘Do you?’

[022-007]‘I have had to look after Willie so much. But read me some more Homer; I

[022-008]shall be very glad to listen.’

[022-009][[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] reached for the book again, but not readily. His face showed

[022-010]disappointment. Their evenings together had never been the same since

[022-011]the birth of the child; Willie was always an excuse--valid enough--for

[022-012][[amy-reardon|Amy]]’s feeling tired. The little boy had come between him and the mother,

[022-013]as must always be the case in poor homes, most of all where the [[literary-poverty|poverty]]

[022-014]is relative. Reardon could not pass the subject without a remark, but he

[022-015]tried to speak humorously.

[022-016]‘There ought to be a huge public creche in [[london|London]]. It’s monstrous that

[022-017]an educated mother should have to be nursemaid.’

[022-018]‘But you know very well I think nothing of that. A creche, indeed! No

[022-019]child of mine should go to any such place.’

[022-020]There it was. She grudged no trouble on behalf of the child. That was

[022-021]love; whereas--But then maternal love was a mere matter of course.

[022-022]‘As soon as you get two or three hundred pounds for a book,’ she added,

[022-023]laughing, ‘there’ll be no need for me to give so much time.’

[022-024]‘Two or three hundred pounds!’ He repeated it with a shake of the head.

[022-025]‘Ah, if that were possible!’

[022-026]‘But that’s really a paltry sum. What would fifty novelists you could

[022-027]name say if they were offered three hundred pounds for a book? How much

[022-028]do you suppose even Markland got for his last?’

[022-029]‘Didn’t sell it at all, ten to one. Gets a royalty.’

[022-030]‘Which will bring him five or six hundred pounds before the book ceases

[022-031]to be talked of.’

[022-032]‘Never mind. I’m sick of the word “pounds.”’

[022-033]‘So am I.’

[022-034]She sighed, commenting thus on her acquiescence.

[022-035]‘But look, Amy. If I try to be cheerful in spite of natural dumps,

[022-036]wouldn’t it be fair for you to put aside thoughts of [[money-in-literature|money]]?’

[022-037]‘Yes. Read some Homer, dear. Let us have Odysseus down in Hades, and

[022-038]Ajax stalking past him. Oh, I like that!’

[022-039]So he read, rather coldly at first, but soon warming. Amy sat with

[022-040]folded arms, a smile on her lips, her brows knitted to the epic humour.

[022-041]In a few minutes it was as if no difficulties threatened their life.

[022-042]Every now and then Reardon looked up from his translating with a

[022-043]delighted laugh, in which Amy joined.

[022-044]When he had returned the book to the shelf he stepped behind his wife’s

[022-045]chair, leaned upon it, and put his cheek against hers.

[022-046]‘Amy!’

[022-047]‘Yes, dear?’

[022-048]‘Do you still love me a little?’

[022-049]‘Much more than a little.’

[022-050]‘Though I am sunk to writing a wretched pot-boiler?’

[022-051]‘Is it so bad as all that?’

[022-052]‘Confoundedly bad. I shall be ashamed to see it in print; the proofs

[022-053]will be a martyrdom.’

[022-054]‘Oh, but why? why?’

[022-055]‘It’s the best I can do, dearest. So you don’t love me enough to hear

[022-056]that calmly.’

[022-057]‘If I didn’t love you, I might be calmer about it, Edwin. It’s dreadful

[022-058]to me to think of what they will say in the reviews.’

[022-059]‘Curse the reviews!’

[022-060]His mood had changed on the instant. He stood up with darkened face,

[022-061]trembling angrily.

[022-062]‘I want you to promise me something, Amy. You won’t read a single one

[022-063]of the notices unless it is forced upon your attention. Now, promise me

[022-064]that. Neglect them absolutely, as I do. They’re not worth a glance of

[022-065]your eyes. And I shan’t be able to bear it if I know you read all the

[022-066]contempt that will be poured on me.’

[022-067]‘I’m sure I shall be glad enough to avoid it; but other people, our

[022-068]friends, read it. That’s the worst.’

[022-069]‘You know that their praise would be valueless, so have strength to

[022-070]disregard the blame. Let our friends read and talk as much as they like.

[022-071]Can’t you console yourself with the thought that I am not contemptible,

[022-072]though I may have been forced to do poor work?’

[022-073]‘People don’t look at it in that way.’

[022-074]‘But, darling,’ he took her hands strongly in his own, ‘I want you to

[022-075]disregard other people. You and I are surely everything to each other?

[022-076]Are you ashamed of me, of me myself?’

[022-077]‘No, not ashamed of you. But I am sensitive to people’s talk and

[022-078]opinions.’

[022-079]‘But that means they make you feel ashamed of me. What else?’

[022-080]There was silence.

[022-081]‘Edwin, if you find you are unable to do good work, you mustn’t do bad.

[022-082]We must think of some other way of making a living.’

[022-083]‘Have you forgotten that you urged me to write a trashy sensational

[022-084]story?’

[022-085]She coloured and looked annoyed.

[022-086]‘You misunderstood me. A sensational story needn’t be trash. And then,

[022-087]you know, if you had tried something entirely unlike your usual work,

[022-088]that would have been excuse enough if people had called it a failure.’

[022-089]‘People! People!’

[022-090]‘We can’t live in solitude, Edwin, though really we are not far

[022-091]from it.’ He did not dare to make any reply to this. Amy was so

[022-092]exasperatingly womanlike in avoiding the important issue to which he

[022-093]tried to confine her; another moment, and his tone would be that of

[022-094]irritation. So he turned away and sat down to his desk, as if he had

[022-095]some thought of resuming work.

[022-096]‘Will you come and have some supper?’ Amy asked, rising.

[022-097]‘I have been forgetting that to-morrow morning’s chapter has still to be

[022-098]thought out.’

[022-099]‘Edwin, I can’t think this book will really be so poor. You couldn’t

[022-100]possibly give all this toil for no result.’

[022-101]‘No; not if I were in sound health. But I am far from it.’

[022-102]‘Come and have supper with me, dear, and think afterwards.’

[022-103]He turned and smiled at her.

[022-104]‘I hope I shall never be able to resist an invitation from you, sweet.’

[022-105]The result of all this was, of course, that he sat down in anything but

[022-106]the right mood to his work next morning. Amy’s anticipation of [[reviewing-criticism|criticism]]

[022-107]had made it harder than ever for him to labour at what he knew to be

[022-108]bad. And, as ill-luck would have it, in a day or two he caught his

[022-109]first winter’s cold. For several years a succession of influenzas,

[022-110]sore-throats, lumbagoes, had tormented him from October to May; in

[022-111]planning his present work, and telling himself that it must be finished

[022-112]before Christmas, he had not lost sight of these possible interruptions.

[022-113]But he said to himself: ‘Other men have worked hard in seasons of

[022-114]illness; I must do the same.’ All very well, but Reardon did not belong

[022-115]to the heroic [[social-class|class]]. A feverish cold now put his powers and resolution

[022-116]to the test. Through one hideous day he nailed himself to the desk--and

[022-117]wrote a quarter of a page. The next day Amy would not let him rise from

[022-118]bed; he was wretchedly ill. In the night he had talked about his work

[022-119]deliriously, causing her no slight alarm.

[022-120]‘If this goes on,’ she said to him in the morning, ‘you’ll have brain

[022-121]fever. You must rest for two or three days.’

[022-122]‘Teach me how to. I wish I could.’

[022-123]Rest had indeed become out of the question. For two days he could not

[022-124]write, but the result upon his mind was far worse than if he had been at

[022-125]the desk. He looked a haggard creature when he again sat down with the

[022-126]accustomed blank slip before him.

[022-127]The second volume ought to have been much easier work than the first; it

[022-128]proved far harder. Messieurs and mesdames the critics are wont to point

[022-129]out the weakness of second volumes; they are generally right, simply

[022-130]because a story which would have made a tolerable book (the common run

[022-131]of stories) refuses to fill three books. Reardon’s story was in itself

[022-132]weak, and this second volume had to consist almost entirely of laborious

[022-133]padding. If he wrote three slips a day he did well.

[022-134]And the money was melting, melting, despite Amy’s efforts at economy.

[022-135]She spent as little as she could; not a luxury came into their home;

[022-136]articles of clothing all but indispensable were left unpurchased. But

[022-137]to what purpose was all this? Impossible, now, that the book should be

[022-138]finished and sold before the money had all run out.

[022-139]At the end of November, Reardon said to his wife one morning:

[022-140]‘To-morrow I finish the second volume.’

[022-141]‘And in a week,’ she replied, ‘we shan’t have a shilling left.’

[022-142]He had refrained from making inquiries, and Amy had forborne to tell

[022-143]him the state of things, lest it should bring him to a dead stop in his

[022-144]writing. But now they must needs discuss their position.

[022-145]‘In three weeks I can get to the end,’ said Reardon, with unnatural

[022-146]calmness. ‘Then I will go personally to the [[publishing-trade|publishers]], and beg them to

[022-147]advance me something on the manuscript before they have read it.’

[022-148]‘Couldn’t you do that with the first two volumes?’

[022-149]‘No, I can’t; indeed I can’t. The other thing will be bad enough; but to

[022-150]beg on an incomplete book, and such a book--I can’t!’

[022-151]There were drops on his forehead.

[022-152]‘They would help you if they knew,’ said Amy in a low voice.

[022-153]‘Perhaps; I can’t say. They can’t help every poor devil. No; I will sell

[022-154]some books. I can pick out fifty or sixty that I shan’t much miss.’

[022-155]Amy knew what a wrench this would be. The imminence of distress seemed

[022-156]to have softened her.

[022-157]‘Edwin, let me take those two volumes to the publishers, and ask--’

[022-158]‘Heavens! no. That’s impossible. Ten to one you will be told that my

[022-159]work is of such doubtful value that they can’t offer even a guinea till

[022-160]the whole book has been considered. I can’t allow you to go, dearest.

[022-161]This morning I’ll choose some books that I can spare, and after dinner

[022-162]I’ll ask a man to come and look at them. Don’t worry yourself; I can

[022-163]finish in three weeks, I’m sure I can. If I can get you three or four

[022-164]pounds you could make it do, couldn’t you?’

[022-165]‘Yes.’

[022-166]She averted her face as she spoke.

[022-167]‘You shall have that.’ He still spoke very quietly. ‘If the books won’t

[022-168]bring enough, there’s my watch--oh, lots of things.’

[022-169]He turned abruptly away, and Amy went on with her household work.

[022-170]CHAPTER X. THE FRIENDS OF THE FAMILY

[022-171]It was natural that Amy should hint dissatisfaction with the loneliness

[022-172]in which her days were mostly spent. She had never lived in a large

[022-173]circle of acquaintances; the narrowness of her mother’s means restricted

[022-174]the family to intercourse with a few old friends and such new ones as

[022-175]were content with teacup entertainment; but her tastes were social,

[022-176]and the maturing process which followed upon her [[literary-marriage|marriage]] made her more

[022-177]conscious of this than she had been before. Already she had allowed her

[022-178]husband to understand that one of her strongest motives in marrying

[022-179]him was the belief that he would achieve distinction. At the time

[022-180]she doubtless thought of his coming fame only--or principally--as it

[022-181]concerned their relations to each other; her pride in him was to be one

[022-182]phase of her love. Now she was well aware that no degree of distinction

[022-183]in her husband would be of much value to her unless she had the pleasure

[022-184]of witnessing its effect upon others; she must shine with reflected

[022-185]light before an admiring assembly.

[022-186]The more conscious she became of this requirement of her nature, the

[022-187]more clearly did she perceive that her hopes had been founded on an

[022-188]error. Reardon would never be a great man; he would never even occupy

[022-189]a prominent place in the estimation of the public. The two things, Amy

[022-190]knew, might be as different as light and darkness; but in the grief of

[022-191]her disappointment she would rather have had him flare into a worthless

[022-192]popularity than flicker down into total extinction, which it almost

[022-193]seemed was to be his fate.
