# Section 20

[019-001]‘I really don’t know.’

[019-002]‘I’m sorry to say my mother is far from well. In any case I must go at

[019-003]Christmas, but I’m afraid it won’t be a cheerful visit.’

[019-004]Arrived in Hampstead Road he offered his hand for good-bye.

[019-005]‘I wanted to talk about all sorts of things. But perhaps I shall find

[019-006]you again some day.’

[019-007]He jumped out, and waved his hat in the lurid fog.

[019-008]Shortly before the end of December appeared the first number of The

[019-009]Current. [[alfred-yule|Yule]] had once or twice referred to the forthcoming magazine

[019-010]with acrid contempt, and of course he did not purchase a copy.

[019-011]‘So young [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] has joined [[fadge|Fadge]]’s hopeful standard,’ he remarked,

[019-012]a day or two later, at breakfast. ‘They say his paper is remarkably

[019-013]clever; I could wish it had appeared anywhere else.

[019-014]Evil communications, &c.’

[019-015]‘But I shouldn’t think there’s any personal connection,’ said [[marian-yule|Marian]].

[019-016]‘Very likely not. But Milvain has been invited to contribute, you see.

[019-017]‘Do you think he ought to have refused?’

[019-018]‘Oh no. It’s nothing to me; nothing whatever.’

[019-019]Mrs Yule glanced at her daughter, but Marian seemed unconcerned. The

[019-020]subject was dismissed. In introducing it Yule had had his purpose;

[019-021]there had always been an unnatural avoidance of Milvain’s name in

[019-022]conversation, and he wished to have an end of this. Hitherto he had felt

[019-023]a troublesome uncertainty regarding his position in the matter. From

[019-024]what his wife had told him it seemed pretty certain that Marian was

[019-025]disappointed by the abrupt closing of her brief acquaintance with the

[019-026]young man, and Yule’s affection for his daughter caused him to feel

[019-027]uneasy in the thought that perhaps he had deprived her of a chance of

[019-028]happiness. His conscience readily took hold of an excuse for justifying

[019-029]the course he had followed. Milvain had gone over to the enemy. Whether

[019-030]or not the young man understood how relentless the hostility was between

[019-031]Yule and Fadge mattered little; the probability was that he knew

[019-032]all about it. In any case intimate relations with him could not have

[019-033]survived this alliance with Fadge, so that, after all, there had been

[019-034]wisdom in letting the acquaintance lapse. To be sure, nothing could have

[019-035]come of it. Milvain was the kind of man who weighed opportunities; every

[019-036]step he took would be regulated by considerations of advantage; at all

[019-037]events that was the impression his character had made upon Yule. Any

[019-038]hopes that Marian might have been induced to form would assuredly have

[019-039]ended in disappointment. It was kindness to interpose before things had

[019-040]gone so far.

[019-041]Henceforth, if Milvain’s name was unavoidable, it should be mentioned

[019-042]just like that of any other literary man. It seemed very unlikely indeed

[019-043]that Marian would continue to think of him with any special and personal

[019-044]interest. The fact of her having got into correspondence with his

[019-045]sisters was unfortunate, but this kind of thing rarely went on for very

[019-046]long.

[019-047]Yule spoke of the matter with his wife that evening.

[019-048]‘By-the-bye, has Marian heard from those girls at Finden lately?’

[019-049]‘She had a letter one afternoon last week.’

[019-050]‘Do you see these letters?’

[019-051]‘No; she told me what was in them at first, but now she doesn’t.’

[019-052]‘She hasn’t spoken to you again of Milvain?’

[019-053]‘Not a word.’

[019-054]‘Well, I understood what I was about,’ Yule remarked, with the confident

[019-055]air of one who doesn’t wish to remember that he had ever felt doubtful.

[019-056]‘There was no good in having the fellow here.

[019-057]He has got in with a set that I don’t at all care for. If she ever says

[019-058]anything--you understand--you can just let me know.’

[019-059]Marian had already procured a copy of [[the-current|The Current]], and read it

[019-060]privately. Of the cleverness of Milvain’s contribution there could be

[019-061]no two opinions; it drew the attention of the public, and all notices

[019-062]of the new magazine made special reference to this article. With keen

[019-063]interest Marian sought after comments of the press; when it was possible

[019-064]she cut them out and put them carefully away.

[019-065]January passed, and February. She saw nothing of Jasper. A letter from

[019-066][[dora-milvain|Dora]] in the first week of March made announcement that the ‘Child’s

[019-067]History of the English Parliament’ would be published very shortly; it

[019-068]told her, too, that Mrs Milvain had been very ill indeed, but that she

[019-069]seemed to recover a little strength as the weather improved. Of Jasper

[019-070]there was no mention.

[019-071]A week later came the news that Mrs Milvain had suddenly died.

[019-072]This letter was received at breakfast-time. The envelope was an ordinary

[019-073]one, and so little did Marian anticipate the nature of its contents that

[019-074]at the first sight of the words she uttered an exclamation of pain.

[019-075]Her father, who had turned from the table to the fireside with his

[019-076]newspaper, looked round and asked what was the matter.

[019-077]‘Mrs Milvain died the day before yesterday.’

[019-078]‘Indeed!’

[019-079]He averted his face again and seemed disposed to say no more. But in a

[019-080]few moments he inquired:

[019-081]‘What are her daughters likely to do?’

[019-082]‘I have no idea.’

[019-083]‘Do you know anything of their circumstances?’

[019-084]‘I believe they will have to depend upon themselves.’

[019-085]Nothing more was said. Afterwards Mrs Yule made a few sympathetic

[019-086]inquiries, but Marian was very brief in her replies.

[019-087]Ten days after that, on a Sunday afternoon when Marian and her mother

[019-088]were alone in the sitting-room, they heard the knock of a visitor at the

[019-089]front door. Yule was out, and there was no likelihood of the visitor’s

[019-090]wishing to see anyone but him. They listened; the servant went to the

[019-091]door, and, after a murmur of voices, came to speak to her mistress.

[019-092]‘It’s a gentleman called Mr Milvain,’ the girl reported, in a way that

[019-093]proved how seldom callers presented themselves. ‘He asked for Mr Yule,

[019-094]and when I said he was out, then he asked for Miss Yule.’ Mother and

[019-095]daughter looked anxiously at each other. Mrs Yule was nervous and

[019-096]helpless.

[019-097]‘Show Mr Milvain into the study,’ said Marian, with sudden decision.

[019-098]‘Are you going to see him there?’ asked her mother in a hurried whisper.

[019-099]‘I thought you would prefer that to his coming in here.’

[019-100]‘Yes--yes. But suppose father comes back before he’s gone?’

[019-101]‘What will it matter? You forget that he asked for father first.’

[019-102]‘Oh yes! Then don’t wait.’

[019-103]Marian, scarcely less agitated than her mother, was just leaving the

[019-104]room, when she turned back again.

[019-105]‘If father comes in, you will tell him before he goes into the study?’

[019-106]‘Yes, I will.’

[019-107]The fire in the study was on the point of extinction; this was the first

[019-108]thing Marian’s eye perceived on entering, and it gave her assurance that

[019-109]her father would not be back for some hours. Evidently he had intended

[019-110]it to go out; small economies of this kind, unintelligible to people who

[019-111]have always lived at ease, had been the life-long rule with him. With a

[019-112]sensation of gladness at having free time before her, Marian turned to

[019-113]where Milvain was standing, in front of one of the bookcases. He wore no

[019-114]symbol of mourning, but his countenance was far graver than usual, and

[019-115]rather paler. They shook hands in silence.

[019-116]‘I am so grieved--’ Marian began with broken voice.

[019-117]‘Thank you. I know the girls have told you all about it. We knew for the

[019-118]last month that it must come before long, though there was a deceptive

[019-119]improvement just before the end.’

[019-120]‘Please to sit down, Mr Milvain. Father went out not long ago, and I

[019-121]don’t think he will be back very soon.’

[019-122]‘It was not really Mr Yule I wished to see,’ said Jasper, frankly. ‘If

[019-123]he had been at home I should have spoken with him about what I have

[019-124]in mind, but if you will kindly give me a few minutes it will be much

[019-125]better.’

[019-126]Marian glanced at the expiring fire. Her curiosity as to what Milvain

[019-127]had to say was mingled with an anxious doubt whether it was not too late

[019-128]to put on fresh coals; already the room was growing very chill, and this

[019-129]appearance of inhospitality troubled her.

[019-130]‘Do you wish to save it?’ Jasper asked, understanding her look and

[019-131]movement.

[019-132]‘I’m afraid it has got too low.’

[019-133]‘I think not. Life in lodgings has made me skilful at this kind of

[019-134]thing; let me try my hand.’

[019-135]He took the tongs and carefully disposed small pieces of coal upon

[019-136]the glow that remained. Marian stood apart with a feeling of shame

[019-137]and annoyance. But it is so seldom that situations in life arrange

[019-138]themselves with dramatic propriety; and, after all, this vulgar

[019-139]necessity made the beginning of the conversation easier.

[019-140]‘That will be all right now,’ said Jasper at length, as little tongues

[019-141]of flame began to shoot here and there.

[019-142]Marian said nothing, but seated herself and waited.

[019-143]‘I came up to town yesterday,’ Jasper began. ‘Of course we have had a

[019-144]great deal to do and think about. Miss Harrow has been very kind indeed

[019-145]to the girls; so have several of our old friends in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]]. It

[019-146]was necessary to decide at once what Maud and Dora are going to do, and

[019-147]it is on their account that I have come to see you.

[019-148]The listener kept silence, with a face of sympathetic attention.

[019-149]‘We have made up our minds that they may as well come to [[london|London]]. It’s a

[019-150]bold step; I’m by no means sure that the result will justify it. But I

[019-151]think they are perhaps right in wishing to try it.’

[019-152]‘They will go on with literary work?’

[019-153]‘Well, it’s our hope that they may be able to. Of course there’s no

[019-154]chance of their earning enough to live upon for some time. But the

[019-155]matter stands like this. They have a trifling sum of [[money-in-literature|money]], on which,

[019-156]at a pinch, they could live in London for perhaps a year and a half. In

[019-157]that time they may find their way to a sort of income; at all events,

[019-158]the chances are that a year and a half hence I shall be able to help

[019-159]them to keep body and soul together.’

[019-160]The money of which he spoke was the debt owed to their father by William

[019-161]Milvain. In consequence of Mrs Milvain’s pressing application, half of

[019-162]this sum had at length been paid and the remainder was promised in a

[019-163]year’s time, greatly to Jasper’s astonishment. In addition, there would

[019-164]be the trifle realised by the sale of furniture, though most of this

[019-165]might have to go in payment of rent unless the house could be relet

[019-166]immediately.

[019-167]‘They have made a good beginning,’ said Marian.

[019-168]She spoke mechanically, for it was impossible to keep her thoughts under

[019-169]control. If Maud and Dora came to live in London it might bring about

[019-170]a most important change in her life; she could scarcely imagine the

[019-171]happiness of having two such friends always near. On the other hand, how

[019-172]would it be regarded by her father? She was at a loss amid conflicting

[019-173]emotions.

[019-174]‘It’s better than if they had done nothing at all,’ Jasper replied to

[019-175]her remark. ‘And the way they knocked that trifle together promises

[019-176]well. They did it very quickly, and in a far more workmanlike way than I

[019-177]should have thought possible.’

[019-178]‘No doubt they share your own talent.’

[019-179]‘Perhaps so. Of course I know that I have talent of a kind, though

[019-180]I don’t rate it very high. We shall have to see whether they can do

[019-181]anything more than mere [[publishing-trade|booksellers]]’ work; they are both very young,

[019-182]you know. I think they may be able to write something that’ll do for The

[019-183]English Girl, and no doubt I can hit upon a second idea that will appeal

[019-184]to Jolly and Monk. At all events, they’ll have books within reach, and

[019-185]better opportunities every way than at Finden.’
