# Grub Street

The symbolic heart of the commercial literary world in *New Grub Street* — both a literal reference to the historical London street associated with hack writers, and Gissing's metaphor for the modern literary marketplace where ambition, poverty, and artistic integrity collide.

## Defining the Metaphor

Gissing appropriates the historical Grub Street — the eighteenth-century haunt of Samuel Johnson and impoverished scribblers — and reimagines it for the 1880s. In the novel's opening, Jasper Milvain draws the crucial distinction between old and new:

> "Reardon can't do that kind of thing, he's behind his age; he sells a manuscript as if he lived in Sam Johnson's Grub Street. But our Grub Street of to-day is quite a different place: it is supplied with telegraphic communication, it knows what literary fare is in demand in every part of the world, its inhabitants are men of business, however seedy." [P02-194–P02-198]

The modern Grub Street is not a place of garret poverty but a networked marketplace — connected by telegraph, responsive to demand, fundamentally commercial. Milvain's key insight is that even its "seedy" inhabitants are now "men of business."

## Literature as a Trade

The novel's central argument, voiced most explicitly by Milvain, is that literature has become a trade rather than a calling:

> "Literature nowadays is a trade. Putting aside men of genius, who may succeed by mere cosmic force, your successful man of letters is your skilful tradesman. He thinks first and foremost of the markets; when one kind of goods begins to go off slackly, he is ready with something new and appetising." [P02-183–P02-187]

This redefinition of literary work as commercial production is the ideological core of the novel. Milvain, the novel's anti-hero, embraces this logic entirely; Edwin Reardon, the protagonist, cannot — and his failure is the novel's tragedy.

## The Inhabitants of Grub Street

### The Businessman

Jasper Milvain is the self-conscious modern Grub Street operator. He studies the market, diversifies his output, and plans his career as a commercial enterprise: "I am slowly, but surely, learning the business. My line won't be novels; I have failed in that direction" [P02-201–P02-202]. His trajectory from struggling journalist to editor of a society paper embodies the Grub Street success story — at the cost of any artistic commitment.

### The Veteran

Alfred Yule represents the old guard of Grub Street — the career literary laborer who has spent decades in grinding poverty without Milvain's commercial instincts. He describes himself as having been "a Grub Street gazetteer" when he first met his friend Quarmby, reflecting: "A life of toil! A life of toil!" [055-016–055-018]. Yule's bitterness and pedantry are the psychological costs of survival in the marketplace without worldly success.

### The Arriviste

Maud Milvain, Jasper's sister, uses her literary connections to marry into wealth and then distances herself from Grub Street origins entirely. After her advantageous marriage, she speaks "with laughing scorn of the days when she inhabited Grub Street; her literary tastes were henceforth to serve as merely a note of distinction" [085-036–085-037]. Grub Street becomes a class stigma to be escaped.

### The Casualty

Edwin Reardon cannot be a Grub Street tradesman. He writes slowly, painfully, with artistic conscience — and starves for it. His brother-in-law Alfred Yule "had drifted from work at a London bookseller's into the modern Grub Street, his adventures in which region will concern us hereafter" [002-102–002-104] — a prefiguring of Reardon's own trajectory into failure.

## Grub Street as Social World

Grub Street in the novel is more than a metaphor — it is a tangible social network. Its inhabitants meet at the British Museum Reading Room, dine at each other's shabby lodgings, trade gossip about editors and advances. Whelpdale, at a dinner party, is asked to recount his "Grub Street reminiscences of the most appalling kind" (068-151) — near-starvation stories have become a form of social currency within the circle.

This social dimension gives the metaphor its specificity. Grub Street is not an abstract "literary world" but a particular milieu of struggling journalists, failed novelists, and precarious editors — a world where "earning about a hundred a year in Grub Street" (005-105) represents a modest but real achievement.

## Historical Context

The original Grub Street (later renamed Milton Street) was a London thoroughfare near Moorfields, known from the seventeenth century as a quarter of impoverished hack writers. Samuel Johnson, in his *Dictionary* (1755), defined "Grubstreet" as "a street in London much inhabited by writers of small histories, dictionaries, and temporary poems." Gissing's innovation is to claim this historical term for the late-Victorian present — and to argue that the commercial forces Johnson's era knew only in embryo had, by the 1880s, transformed literary life entirely.
