# Section 27

[026-001]Half an hour later he entered that same hospital door through which,

[026-002]some years ago, he had passed as a half-starved applicant for work. The

[026-003]matron met him.

[026-004]‘Is Mr [[carter|Carter]] here?’

[026-005]‘No, sir. But we expect him any minute. Will you wait?’

[026-006]He entered the familiar office, and sat down. At the table where he had

[026-007]been wont to work, a young clerk was writing. If only all the events of

[026-008]the last few years could be undone, and he, with no soul dependent upon

[026-009]him, be once more earning his pound a week in this room! What a happy

[026-010]man he was in those days!

[026-011]Nearly half an hour passed. It is the common experience of beggars

[026-012]to have to wait. Then Carter came in with quick step; he wore a heavy

[026-013]ulster of the latest fashion, new gloves, a resplendent silk hat; his

[026-014]cheeks were rosy from the east wind.

[026-015]‘Ha, [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]! How do? how do? Delighted to see you!’

[026-016]‘Are you very busy?’

[026-017]‘Well, no, not particularly. A few cheques to sign, and we’re just

[026-018]getting out our Christmas appeals. You remember?’

[026-019]He laughed gaily. There was a remarkable freedom from snobbishness in

[026-020]this young man; the fact of Reardon’s intellectual superiority had long

[026-021]ago counteracted Carter’s social prejudices.

[026-022]‘I should like to have a word with you.’

[026-023]‘Right you are!’

[026-024]They went into a small inner room. Reardon’s pulse beat at fever-rate;

[026-025]his tongue was cleaving to his palate.

[026-026]‘What is it, old man?’ asked the secretary, seating himself and flinging

[026-027]one of his legs over the other. ‘You look rather seedy, do you know. Why

[026-028]the deuce don’t you and your wife look us up now and then?’

[026-029]‘I’ve had a hard pull to finish my novel.’

[026-030]‘Finished, is it? I’m glad to hear that. When’ll it be out? I’ll send

[026-031]scores of people to Mudie’s after it.

[026-032]‘Thanks; but I don’t think much of it, to tell you the truth.’

[026-033]‘Oh, we know what that means.’

[026-034]Reardon was talking like an automaton. It seemed to him that he turned

[026-035]screws and pressed levers for the utterance of his next words.

[026-036]‘I may as well say at once what I have come for. Could you lend me ten

[026-037]pounds for a month--in fact, until I get the [[money-in-literature|money]] for my book?’

[026-038]The secretary’s countenance fell, though not to that expression of utter

[026-039]coldness which would have come naturally under the circumstances to a

[026-040]great many vivacious men. He seemed genuinely embarrassed.

[026-041]‘By Jove! I--confound it! To tell you the truth, I haven’t ten pounds

[026-042]to lend. Upon my word, I haven’t, Reardon! These infernal housekeeping

[026-043]expenses! I don’t mind telling you, old man, that Edith and I have been

[026-044]pushing the pace rather.’ He laughed, and thrust his hands down into

[026-045]his trousers-pockets. ‘We pay such a darned rent, you know--hundred and

[026-046]twenty-five. We’ve only just been saying we should have to draw it mild

[026-047]for the rest of the winter. But I’m infernally sorry; upon my word I

[026-048]am.’

[026-049]‘And I am sorry to have annoyed you by the unseasonable request.’

[026-050]‘Devilish seasonable, Reardon, I assure you!’ cried the secretary, and

[026-051]roared at his joke. It put him into a better temper than ever, and he

[026-052]said at length: ‘I suppose a fiver wouldn’t be much use?--For a month,

[026-053]you say?--I might manage a fiver, I think.’

[026-054]‘It would be very useful. But on no account if----’

[026-055]‘No, no; I could manage a fiver, for a month. Shall I give you a

[026-056]cheque?’

[026-057]‘I’m ashamed----’

[026-058]‘Not a bit of it! I’ll go and write the cheque.’

[026-059]Reardon’s face was burning. Of the conversation that followed when

[026-060]Carter again presented himself he never recalled a word. The bit of

[026-061]paper was crushed together in his hand. Out in the street again, he all

[026-062]but threw it away, dreaming for the moment that it was a ‘bus ticket or

[026-063]a patent medicine bill.

[026-064]He reached home much after the dinner-hour. [[amy-reardon|Amy]] was surprised at his

[026-065]long absence.

[026-066]‘Got anything?’ she asked.

[026-067]‘Yes.’

[026-068]It was half his intention to deceive her, to say that the [[publishing-trade|publishers]] had

[026-069]advanced him five pounds. But that would be his first word of untruth

[026-070]to Amy, and why should he be guilty of it? He told her all that had

[026-071]happened. The result of this frankness was something that he had not

[026-072]anticipated; Amy exhibited profound vexation.

[026-073]‘Oh, you SHOULDN’T have done that!’ she exclaimed. ‘Why didn’t you come

[026-074]home and tell me? I would have gone to mother at once.’

[026-075]‘But does it matter?’

[026-076]‘Of course it does,’ she replied sharply. ‘Mr Carter will tell his wife,

[026-077]and how pleasant that is?’

[026-078]‘I never thought of that. And perhaps it wouldn’t have seemed to me so

[026-079]annoying as it does to you.’

[026-080]‘Very likely not.’

[026-081]She turned abruptly away, and stood at a distance in gloomy muteness.

[026-082]‘Well,’ she said at length, ‘there’s no helping it now. Come and have

[026-083]your dinner.’

[026-084]‘You have taken away my appetite.’

[026-085]‘Nonsense! I suppose you’re dying of hunger.’

[026-086]They had a very uncomfortable meal, exchanging few words. On Amy’s face

[026-087]was a look more resembling bad temper than anything Reardon had ever

[026-088]seen there. After dinner he went and sat alone in the study. Amy did

[026-089]not come near him. He grew stubbornly angry; remembering the pain he had

[026-090]gone through, he felt that Amy’s behaviour to him was cruel. She must

[026-091]come and speak when she would.

[026-092]At six o’clock she showed her face in the doorway and asked if he would

[026-093]come to tea.

[026-094]‘Thank you,’ he replied, ‘I had rather stay here.’

[026-095]‘As you please.’

[026-096]And he sat alone until about nine. It was only then he recollected that

[026-097]he must send a note to the publishers, calling their attention to the

[026-098]parcel he had left. He wrote it, and closed with a request that they

[026-099]would let him hear as soon as they conveniently could. As he was

[026-100]putting on his hat and coat to go out and post the letter Amy opened the

[026-101]dining-room door.

[026-102]‘You’re going out?’

[026-103]‘Yes.’

[026-104]‘Shall you be long?’

[026-105]‘I think not.’

[026-106]He was away only a few minutes. On returning he went first of all into

[026-107]the study, but the thought of Amy alone in the other room would not let

[026-108]him rest. He looked in and saw that she was sitting without a fire.

[026-109]‘You can’t stay here in the cold, Amy.’

[026-110]‘I’m afraid I must get used to it,’ she replied, affecting to be closely

[026-111]engaged upon some sewing.

[026-112]That strength of character which it had always delighted him to read in

[026-113]her features was become an ominous hardness. He felt his heart sink as

[026-114]he looked at her.

[026-115]‘Is [[literary-poverty|poverty]] going to have the usual result in our case?’ he asked,

[026-116]drawing nearer.

[026-117]‘I never pretended that I could be indifferent to it.’

[026-118]‘Still, don’t you care to try and resist it?’

[026-119]She gave no answer. As usual in conversation with an aggrieved woman it

[026-120]was necessary to go back from the general to the particular.

[026-121]‘I’m afraid,’ he said, ‘that the Carters already knew pretty well how

[026-122]things were going with us.’

[026-123]‘That’s a very different thing. But when it comes to asking them for

[026-124]money--’

[026-125]‘I’m very sorry. I would rather have done anything if I had known how it

[026-126]would annoy you.’

[026-127]‘If we have to wait a month, five pounds will be very little use to us.’

[026-128]She detailed all manner of expenses that had to be met--outlay there was

[026-129]no possibility of avoiding so long as their life was maintained on its

[026-130]present basis.

[026-131]‘However, you needn’t trouble any more about it. I’ll see to it. Now you

[026-132]are free from your book try to rest.’

[026-133]‘Come and sit by the fire. There’s small chance of rest for me if we are

[026-134]thinking unkindly of each other.’

[026-135]A doleful Christmas. Week after week went by and Reardon knew that Amy

[026-136]must have exhausted the money he had given her. But she made no more

[026-137]demands upon him, and necessaries were paid for in the usual way. He

[026-138]suffered from a sense of humiliation; sometimes he found it difficult to

[026-139]look in his wife’s face.

[026-140]When the publishers’ letter came it contained an offer of seventy-five

[026-141]pounds for the copyright of ‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]],’ twenty-five more to be

[026-142]paid if the sale in [[three-volume-system|three-volume form]] should reach a certain number of

[026-143]copies.

[026-144]Here was failure put into unmistakable figures. Reardon said to himself

[026-145]that it was all over with his profession of authorship. The book could

[026-146]not possibly succeed even to the point of completing his hundred pounds;

[026-147]it would meet with universal contempt, and indeed deserved nothing

[026-148]better.

[026-149]‘Shall you accept this?’ asked Amy, after dreary silence.

[026-150]‘No one else would offer terms as good.’

[026-151]‘Will they pay you at once?’

[026-152]‘I must ask them to.’

[026-153]Well, it was seventy-five pounds in hand. The cheque came as soon as

[026-154]it was requested, and Reardon’s face brightened for the moment. Blessed

[026-155]money! root of all good, until the world invent some saner economy.

[026-156]‘How much do you owe your mother?’ he inquired, without looking at Amy.

[026-157]‘Six pounds,’ she answered coldly.

[026-158]‘And five to Carter; and rent, twelve pounds ten. We shall have a matter

[026-159]of fifty pounds to go on with.’

[026-160]CHAPTER XII. WORK WITHOUT HOPE

[026-161]The prudent course was so obvious that he marvelled at Amy’s failing

[026-162]to suggest it. For people in their circumstances to be paying a rent

[026-163]of fifty pounds when a home could be found for half the money was

[026-164]recklessness; there would be no difficulty in letting the flat for this

[026-165]last year of their lease, and the cost of removal would be trifling. The

[026-166]mental relief of such a change might enable him to front with courage

[026-167]a problem in any case very difficult, and, as things were, desperate.

[026-168]Three months ago, in a moment of profoundest misery, he had proposed

[026-169]this step; courage failed him to speak of it again, Amy’s look and voice

[026-170]were too vivid in his memory. Was she not capable of such a sacrifice

[026-171]for his sake? Did she prefer to let him bear all the responsibility of

[026-172]whatever might result from a futile struggle to keep up appearances?

[026-173]Between him and her there was no longer perfect confidence. Her silence

[026-174]meant reproach, and--whatever might have been the case before--there was

[026-175]no doubt that she now discussed him with her mother, possibly with other

[026-176]people. It was not likely that she concealed his own opinion of the book

[026-177]he had just finished; all their acquaintances would be prepared to greet

[026-178]its publication with private scoffing or with mournful shaking of the

[026-179]head. His feeling towards Amy entered upon a new phase. The stability of

[026-180]his love was a source of pain; condemning himself, he felt at the same

[026-181]time that he was wronged. A coldness which was far from representing

[026-182]the truth began to affect his manner and speech, and Amy did not seem

[026-183]to notice it, at all events she made no kind of protest. They no longer

[026-184]talked of the old subjects, but of those mean concerns of material life

[026-185]which formerly they had agreed to dismiss as quickly as possible. Their

[026-186]relations to each other--not long ago an inexhaustible topic--would not

[026-187]bear spoken comment; both were too conscious of the danger-signal when

[026-188]they looked that way.

[026-189]In the time of waiting for the publishers’ offer, and now again when he

[026-190]was asking himself how he should use the respite granted him, Reardon

[026-191]spent his days at the [[british-museum|British Museum]]. He could not read to much purpose,

[026-192]but it was better to sit here among strangers than seem to be idling

[026-193]under Amy’s glance. Sick of imaginative writing, he turned to the

[026-194]studies which had always been most congenial, and tried to shape out a

[026-195]paper or two like those he had formerly disposed of to editors. Among

[026-196]his unused material lay a mass of notes he had made in a reading of

[026-197]Diogenes Laertius, and it seemed to him now that he might make something

[026-198]salable out of these anecdotes of the philosophers. In a happier mood he

[026-199]could have written delightfully on such a subject--not learnedly, but in

[026-200]the strain of a modern man whose humour and sensibility find free play

[026-201]among the classic ghosts; even now he was able to recover something of

[026-202]the light touch which had given value to his published essays.
