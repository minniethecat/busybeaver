# Section 40

[039-001]Living apart from her husband, she could not be expected to forswear

[039-002]society, and doubtless she would see [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] pretty often. He called

[039-003]occasionally at Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s, and would not do so less often when he knew

[039-004]that [[amy-reardon|Amy]] was to be met there. There would be chance encounters like that

[039-005]of yesterday, of which she had chosen to keep silence.

[039-006]A dark fear began to shadow him. In yielding thus passively to stress of

[039-007]circumstances, was he not exposing his wife to a danger which outweighed

[039-008]all the ills of [[literary-poverty|poverty]]? As one to whom she was inestimably dear, was

[039-009]he right in allowing her to leave him, if only for a few months? He knew

[039-010]very well that a man of strong character would never have entertained

[039-011]this project. He had got into the way of thinking of himself as too weak

[039-012]to struggle against the obstacles on which Amy insisted, and of looking

[039-013]for safety in retreat; but what was to be the end of this weakness if

[039-014]the summer did not at all advance him? He knew better than Amy could how

[039-015]unlikely it was that he should recover the energies of his mind in

[039-016]so short a time and under such circumstances; only the feeble man’s

[039-017]temptation to postpone effort had made him consent to this step, and

[039-018]now that he was all but beyond turning back, the perils of which he had

[039-019]thought too little forced themselves upon his mind.

[039-020]He rose in anguish, and stood looking about him as if aid might

[039-021]somewhere be visible.

[039-022]Presently there was a knock at the front door, and on opening he beheld

[039-023]the vivacious Mr [[carter|Carter]]. This gentleman had only made two or three calls

[039-024]here since [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s [[literary-marriage|marriage]]; his appearance was a surprise.

[039-025]‘I hear you are leaving town for a time,’ he exclaimed. ‘Edith told me

[039-026]yesterday, so I thought I’d look you up.’

[039-027]He was in spring costume, and exhaled fresh odours. The contrast between

[039-028]his prosperous animation and Reardon’s broken-spirited quietness could

[039-029]not have been more striking.

[039-030]‘Going away for your health, they tell me. You’ve been working too hard,

[039-031]you know. You mustn’t overdo it. And where do you think of going to?’

[039-032]‘It isn’t at all certain that I shall go,’ Reardon replied. ‘I thought

[039-033]of a few weeks--somewhere at the seaside.’

[039-034]‘I advise you to go north,’ went on Carter cheerily. ‘You want a tonic,

[039-035]you know. Get up into Scotland and do some boating and fishing--that

[039-036]kind of thing. You’d come back a new man. Edith and I had a turn up

[039-037]there last year, you know; it did me heaps of good.’

[039-038]‘Oh, I don’t think I should go so far as that.’

[039-039]‘But that’s just what you want--a regular change, something bracing. You

[039-040]don’t look at all well, that’s the fact. A winter in [[london|London]] tries any

[039-041]man--it does me, I know. I’ve been seedy myself these last few weeks.

[039-042]Edith wants me to take her over to Paris at the end of this month, and

[039-043]I think it isn’t a bad idea; but I’m so confoundedly busy. In the autumn

[039-044]we shall go to Norway, I think; it seems to be the right thing to do

[039-045]nowadays. Why shouldn’t you have a run over to Norway? They say it can

[039-046]be done very cheaply; the steamers take you for next to nothing.’

[039-047]He talked on with the joyous satisfaction of a man whose income is

[039-048]assured, and whose future teems with a succession of lively holidays.

[039-049]Reardon could make no answer to such suggestions; he sat with a fixed

[039-050]smile on his face.

[039-051]‘Have you heard,’ said Carter, presently, ‘that we’re opening a branch

[039-052]of the hospital in the City Road?’

[039-053]‘No; I hadn’t heard of it.’

[039-054]‘It’ll only be for out-patients. Open three mornings and three evenings

[039-055]alternately.’

[039-056]‘Who’ll represent you there?’

[039-057]‘I shall look in now and then, of course; there’ll be a clerk, like at

[039-058]the old place.’

[039-059]He talked of the matter in detail--of the doctors who would attend, and

[039-060]of certain new arrangements to be tried.

[039-061]‘Have you engaged the clerk?’ Reardon asked.

[039-062]‘Not yet. I think I know a man who’ll suit me, though.’

[039-063]‘You wouldn’t be disposed to give me the chance?’

[039-064]Reardon spoke huskily, and ended with a broken laugh.

[039-065]‘You’re rather above my figure nowadays, old man!’ exclaimed Carter,

[039-066]joining in what he considered the jest.

[039-067]‘Shall you pay a pound a week?’

[039-068]‘Twenty-five shillings. It’ll have to be a man who can be trusted to

[039-069]take [[money-in-literature|money]] from the paying patients.’

[039-070]‘Well, I am serious. Will you give me the place?’

[039-071]Carter gazed at him, and checked another laugh.

[039-072]‘What the deuce do you mean?’

[039-073]‘The fact is,’ Reardon replied, ‘I want variety of occupation. I can’t

[039-074]stick at writing for more than a month or two at a time. It’s because I

[039-075]have tried to do so that--well, practically, I have broken down. If you

[039-076]will give me this clerkship, it will relieve me from the necessity of

[039-077]perpetually writing novels; I shall be better for it in every way. You

[039-078]know that I’m equal to the job; you can trust me; and I dare say I shall

[039-079]be more useful than most clerks you could get.’

[039-080]It was done, most happily done, on the first impulse. A minute more of

[039-081]pause, and he could not have faced the humiliation. His face burned, his

[039-082]tongue was parched.

[039-083]‘I’m floored!’ cried Carter. ‘I shouldn’t have thought--but of course,

[039-084]if you really want it. I can hardly believe yet that you’re serious,

[039-085]Reardon.’

[039-086]‘Why not? Will you promise me the work?’

[039-087]‘Well, yes.’

[039-088]‘When shall I have to begin?’

[039-089]‘The place’ll be opened to-morrow week. But how about your holiday?’

[039-090]‘Oh, let that stand over. It’ll be holiday enough to occupy myself in a

[039-091]new way. An old way, too; I shall enjoy it.’

[039-092]He laughed merrily, relieved beyond measure at having come to what

[039-093]seemed an end of his difficulties. For half an hour they continued to

[039-094]talk over the affair.

[039-095]‘Well, it’s a comical idea,’ said Carter, as he took his leave, ‘but you

[039-096]know your own business best.’

[039-097]When Amy returned, Reardon allowed her to put the child to bed before he

[039-098]sought any conversation. She came at length and sat down in the study.

[039-099]‘Mother advises us not to sell the furniture,’ were her first words.

[039-100]‘I’m glad of that, as I had quite made up my mind not to.’ There was a

[039-101]change in his way of speaking which she at once noticed.

[039-102]‘Have you thought of something?’

[039-103]‘Yes. Carter has been here, and he happened to mention that they’re

[039-104]opening an out-patient department of the hospital, in the City Road.

[039-105]He’ll want someone to help him there. I asked for the post, and he

[039-106]promised it me.’

[039-107]The last words were hurried, though he had resolved to speak with

[039-108]deliberation. No more feebleness; he had taken a decision, and would act

[039-109]upon it as became a responsible man.

[039-110]‘The post?’ said Amy. ‘What post?’

[039-111]‘In plain English, the clerkship. It’ll be the same work as I used to

[039-112]have--registering patients, receiving their “letters,” and so on. The

[039-113]pay is to be five-and-twenty shillings a week.’

[039-114]Amy sat upright and looked steadily at him.

[039-115]‘Is this a joke?’

[039-116]‘Far from it, dear. It’s a blessed deliverance.’

[039-117]‘You have asked Mr Carter to take you back as a clerk?’

[039-118]‘I have.’

[039-119]‘And you propose that we shall live on twenty-five shillings a week?’

[039-120]‘Oh no! I shall be engaged only three mornings in the week and three

[039-121]evenings. In my free time I shall do literary work, and no doubt I can

[039-122]earn fifty pounds a year by it--if I have your sympathy to help me.

[039-123]To-morrow I shall go and look for rooms some distance from here; in

[039-124][[islington|Islington]], I think. We have been living far beyond our means; that must

[039-125]come to an end. We’ll have no more keeping up of sham appearances. If I

[039-126]can make my way in literature, well and good; in that case our position

[039-127]and prospects will of course change. But for the present we are poor

[039-128]people, and must live in a poor way. If our friends like to come and see

[039-129]us, they must put aside all snobbishness, and take us as we are. If they

[039-130]prefer not to come, there’ll be an excuse in our remoteness.’

[039-131]Amy was stroking the back of her hand. After a long silence, she said in

[039-132]a very quiet, but very resolute tone:

[039-133]‘I shall not consent to this.’

[039-134]‘In that case, Amy, I must do without your consent. The rooms will be

[039-135]taken, and our furniture transferred to them.’

[039-136]‘To me that will make no difference,’ returned his wife, in the same

[039-137]voice as before. ‘I have decided--as you told me to--to go with Willie

[039-138]to mother’s next Tuesday. You, of course, must do as you please. I

[039-139]should have thought a summer at the seaside would have been more helpful

[039-140]to you; but if you prefer to live in Islington--’

[039-141]Reardon approached her, and laid a hand on her shoulder.

[039-142]‘Amy, are you my wife, or not?’

[039-143]‘I am certainly not the wife of a clerk who is paid so much a week.’

[039-144]He had foreseen a struggle, but without certainty of the form Amy’s

[039-145]opposition would take. For himself he meant to be gently resolute,

[039-146]calmly regardless of protest. But in a man to whom such self-assertion

[039-147]is a matter of conscious effort, tremor of the nerves will always

[039-148]interfere with the line of conduct he has conceived in advance.

[039-149]Already Reardon had spoken with far more bluntness than he proposed;

[039-150]involuntarily, his voice slipped from earnest determination to the

[039-151]note of absolutism, and, as is wont to be the case, the sound of these

[039-152]strange tones instigated him to further utterances of the same kind.

[039-153]He lost control of himself. Amy’s last reply went through him like an

[039-154]electric shock, and for the moment he was a mere husband defied by

[039-155]his wife, the male stung to exertion of his brute force against the

[039-156]physically weaker sex.

[039-157]‘However you regard me, you will do what I think fit. I shall not argue

[039-158]with you. If I choose to take lodgings in Whitechapel, there you will

[039-159]come and live.’

[039-160]He met Amy’s full look, and was conscious of that in it which

[039-161]corresponded to his own brutality. She had become suddenly a much

[039-162]older woman; her cheeks were tight drawn into thinness, her lips were

[039-163]bloodlessly hard, there was an unknown furrow along her forehead, and

[039-164]she glared like the animal that defends itself with tooth and claw.

[039-165]‘Do as YOU think fit? Indeed!’

[039-166]Could Amy’s voice sound like that? Great Heaven! With just such accent

[039-167]he had heard a wrangling woman retort upon her husband at the street

[039-168]corner. Is there then no essential difference between a woman of this

[039-169]world and one of that? Does the same nature lie beneath such unlike

[039-170]surfaces?

[039-171]He had but to do one thing: to seize her by the arm, drag her up

[039-172]from the chair, dash her back again with all his force--there, the

[039-173]transformation would be complete, they would stand towards each other

[039-174]on the natural footing. With an added curse perhaps--Instead of that, he

[039-175]choked, struggled for breath, and shed tears.

[039-176]Amy turned scornfully away from him. Blows and a curse would have

[039-177]overawed her, at all events for the moment; she would have felt: ‘Yes,

[039-178]he is a man, and I have put my destiny into his hands.’ His tears

[039-179]moved her to a feeling cruelly exultant; they were the sign of her

[039-180]superiority. It was she who should have wept, and never in her life had

[039-181]she been further from such display of weakness.

[039-182]This could not be the end, however, and she had no wish to terminate

[039-183]the scene. They stood for a minute without regarding each other, then

[039-184]Reardon faced to her.
