# Section 71

[070-001]‘Those paragraphs on the Rota Club strike me as singularly happy,’ he

[070-002]said, tapping the manuscript with the mouthpiece of his pipe. ‘Perhaps

[070-003]you might say a word or two more about Cyriac Skinner; one mustn’t be

[070-004]too allusive with general readers, their ignorance is incredible. But

[070-005]there is so little to add to this paper--so little to alter--that I

[070-006]couldn’t feel justified in sending it as my own work. I think it is

[070-007]altogether too good to appear anonymously. You must sign it, [[marian-yule|Marian]], and

[070-008]have the credit that is due to you.’

[070-009]‘Oh, do you think it’s worth while?’ answered the girl, who was far from

[070-010]easy under this praise. Of late there had been too much of it; it made

[070-011]her regard her father with suspicions which increased her sense of

[070-012]trouble in keeping a momentous secret from him.

[070-013]‘Yes, yes; you had better sign it. I’ll undertake there’s no other girl

[070-014]of your age who could turn out such a piece of work. I think we may

[070-015]fairly say that your apprenticeship is at an end. Before long,’ he

[070-016]smiled anxiously, ‘I may be counting upon you as a valued contributor.

[070-017]And that reminds me; would you be disposed to call with me on the

[070-018]Jedwoods at their house next Sunday?’

[070-019]Marian understood the intention that lay beneath this proposal. She

[070-020]saw that her father would not allow himself to seem discouraged by the

[070-021]silence she maintained on the great subject which awaited her decision.

[070-022]He was endeavouring gradually to involve her in his ambitions, to carry

[070-023]her forward by insensible steps. It pained her to observe the suppressed

[070-024]eagerness with which he looked for her reply.

[070-025]‘I will go if you wish, father, but I had rather not.’

[070-026]‘I feel sure you would like Mrs [[jedwood|Jedwood]]. One has no great opinion of her

[070-027]novels, but she is a woman of some intellect. Let me book you for next

[070-028]Sunday; surely I have a claim to your companionship now and then.’

[070-029]Marian kept silence. [[alfred-yule|Yule]] puffed at his pipe, then said with a

[070-030]speculative air:

[070-031]‘I suppose it has never even occurred to you to try your hand at

[070-032]fiction?’

[070-033]‘I haven’t the least inclination that way.’

[070-034]‘You would probably do something rather good if you tried. But I don’t

[070-035]urge it. My own efforts in that line were a mistake, I’m disposed to

[070-036]think. Not that the things were worse than multitudes of books which

[070-037]nowadays go down with the many-headed. But I never quite knew what I

[070-038]wished to be at in fiction. I wasn’t content to write a mere narrative

[070-039]of the exciting kind, yet I couldn’t hit upon subjects of intellectual

[070-040]cast that altogether satisfied me. Well, well; I have tried my hand

[070-041]at most kinds of literature. Assuredly I merit the title of man of

[070-042]letters.’

[070-043]‘You certainly do.’

[070-044]‘By-the-by, what should you think of that title for a review--Letters?

[070-045]It has never been used, so far as I know. I like the word “letters.”

[070-046]How much better “a man of letters” than “a literary man”! And apropos of

[070-047]that, when was the word “literature” first used in our modern sense

[070-048]to signify a body of writing? In Johnson’s day it was pretty much the

[070-049]equivalent of our “[[literary-culture|culture]].” You remember his saying, “It is surprising

[070-050]how little literature people have.” His dictionary, I believe, defines

[070-051]the word as “learning, skill in letters”--nothing else.’

[070-052]It was characteristic of Yule to dwell with gusto on little points such

[070-053]as this; he prosed for a quarter of an hour, with a pause every now and

[070-054]then whilst he kept his pipe alight.

[070-055]‘I think Letters wouldn’t be amiss,’ he said at length, returning to

[070-056]the suggestion which he wished to keep before Marian’s mind. ‘It would

[070-057]clearly indicate our scope. No articles on bimetallism, as [[quarmby|Quarmby]]

[070-058]said--wasn’t it Quarmby?’

[070-059]He laughed idly.

[070-060]‘Yes, I must ask Jedwood how he likes the name.’

[070-061]Though Marian feared the result, she was glad when [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] made up his

[070-062]mind to write to her father. Since it was determined that her [[money-in-literature|money]]

[070-063]could not be devoted to establishing a review, the truth ought to be

[070-064]confessed before Yule had gone too far in nursing his dangerous hope.

[070-065]Without the support of her love and all the prospects connected with it,

[070-066]she would hardly have been capable of giving a distinct refusal when her

[070-067]reply could no longer be postponed; to hold the money merely for her own

[070-068]benefit would have seemed to her too selfish, however slight her faith

[070-069]in the project on which her father built so exultantly. When it was

[070-070]declared that she had accepted an offer of [[literary-marriage|marriage]], a sacrifice of that

[070-071]kind could no longer be expected of her. Opposition must direct itself

[070-072]against the choice she had made. It would be stern, perhaps relentless;

[070-073]but she felt able to face any extremity of wrath. Her nerves quivered,

[070-074]but in her heart was an exhaustless source of courage.

[070-075]That a change had somehow come about in the girl Yule was aware. He

[070-076]observed her with the closest study day after day. Her health seemed

[070-077]to have improved; after a long spell of work she had not the air of

[070-078]despondent weariness which had sometimes irritated him, sometimes

[070-079]made him uneasy. She was more womanly in her bearing and speech, and

[070-080]exercised an independence, appropriate indeed to her years, but such

[070-081]as had not formerly declared itself The question with her father

[070-082]was whether these things resulted simply from her consciousness of

[070-083]possessing what to her seemed wealth, or something else had happened

[070-084]of the nature that he dreaded. An alarming symptom was the increased

[070-085]attention she paid to her personal appearance; its indications were

[070-086]not at all prominent, but Yule, on the watch for such things, did not

[070-087]overlook them. True, this also might mean nothing but a sense of relief

[070-088]from narrow means; a girl would naturally adorn herself a little under

[070-089]the circumstances.

[070-090]His doubts came to an end two days after that proposal of a title for

[070-091]the new review. As he sat in his study the servant brought him a letter

[070-092]delivered by the last evening post. The handwriting was unknown to him;

[070-093]the contents were these:

[070-094]‘DEAR MR YULE,--It is my desire to write to you with perfect frankness

[070-095]and as simply as I can on a subject which has the deepest interest for

[070-096]me, and which I trust you will consider in that spirit of kindness with

[070-097]which you received me when we first met at Finden.

[070-098]‘On the occasion of that meeting I had the happiness of being presented

[070-099]to Miss Yule. She was not totally a stranger to me; at that time I used

[070-100]to work pretty regularly in the Museum Reading-room, and there I had

[070-101]seen Miss Yule, had ventured to observe her at moments with a young

[070-102]man’s attention, and had felt my interest aroused, though I did not

[070-103]know her name. To find her at Finden seemed to me a very unusual and

[070-104]delightful piece of good fortune.

[070-105]When I came back from my holiday I was conscious of a new purpose in

[070-106]life, a new desire and a new motive to help me on in my chosen career.

[070-107]‘My mother’s death led to my sisters’ coming to live in [[london|London]]. Already

[070-108]there had been friendly correspondence between Miss Yule and the two

[070-109]girls, and now that the opportunity offered they began to see each other

[070-110]frequently. As I was often at my sisters’ lodgings it came about that

[070-111]I met Miss Yule there from time to time. In this way was confirmed my

[070-112]attachment to your daughter. The better I knew her, the more worthy I

[070-113]found her of reverence and love.

[070-114]‘Would it not have been natural for me to seek a renewal of the

[070-115]acquaintance with yourself which had been begun in the country? Gladly I

[070-116]should have done so. Before my sisters’ coming to London I did call one

[070-117]day at your house with the desire of seeing you, but unfortunately you

[070-118]were not at home. Very soon after that I learnt to my extreme regret

[070-119]that my connection with [[the-current|The Current]] and its editor would make any

[070-120]repetition of my visit very distasteful to you. I was conscious of

[070-121]nothing in my literary life that could justly offend you--and at this

[070-122]day I can say the same--but I shrank from the appearance of importunity,

[070-123]and for some months I was deeply distressed by the fear that what I most

[070-124]desired in life had become unattainable. My means were very slight; I

[070-125]had no choice but to take such work as offered, and mere chance had put

[070-126]me into a position which threatened ruin to the hope that you would some

[070-127]day regard me as a not unworthy suitor for your daughter’s hand.

[070-128]‘Circumstances have led me to a step which at that time seemed

[070-129]impossible. Having discovered that Miss Yule returned the feeling

[070-130]I entertained for her, I have asked her to be my wife, and she has

[070-131]consented. It is now my hope that you will permit me to call upon you.

[070-132]Miss Yule is aware that I am writing this letter; will you not let her

[070-133]plead for me, seeing that only by an unhappy chance have I been kept

[070-134]aloof from you? Marian and I are equally desirous that you should

[070-135]approve our union; without that approval, indeed, something will be

[070-136]lacking to the happiness for which we hope.

[070-137]‘Believe me to be sincerely yours,

[070-138]‘JASPER MILVAIN.’

[070-139]Half an hour after reading this Yule was roused from a fit of the

[070-140]gloomiest brooding by Marian’s entrance. She came towards him timidly,

[070-141]with pale countenance. He had glanced round to see who it was, but at

[070-142]once turned his head again.

[070-143]‘Will you forgive me for keeping this secret from you, father?’

[070-144]‘Forgive you?’ he replied in a hard, deliberate voice. ‘I assure you it

[070-145]is a matter of perfect indifference to me. You are long since of age,

[070-146]and I have no power whatever to prevent your falling a victim to any

[070-147]schemer who takes your fancy. It would be folly in me to discuss the

[070-148]question. I recognise your right to have as many secrets as may seem

[070-149]good to you. To talk of forgiveness is the merest affectation.’

[070-150]‘No, I spoke sincerely. If it had seemed possible I should gladly have

[070-151]let you know about this from the first. That would have been natural and

[070-152]right. But you know what prevented me.’

[070-153]‘I do. I will try to hope that even a sense of shame had something to do

[070-154]with it.’

[070-155]‘That had nothing to do with it,’ said Marian, coldly. ‘I have never had

[070-156]reason to feel ashamed.’

[070-157]‘Be it so. I trust you may never have reason to feel repentance. May I

[070-158]ask when you propose to be married?’

[070-159]‘I don’t know when it will take place.’

[070-160]‘As soon, I suppose, as your uncle’s executors have discharged a piece

[070-161]of business which is distinctly germane to the matter?’

[070-162]‘Perhaps.’

[070-163]‘Does your mother know?’

[070-164]‘I have just told her.’

[070-165]‘Very well, then it seems to me that there’s nothing more to be said.’

[070-166]‘Do you refuse to see Mr Milvain?’

[070-167]‘Most decidedly I do. You will have the goodness to inform him that that

[070-168]is my reply to his letter.’

[070-169]‘I don’t think that is the behaviour of a gentleman,’ said Marian, her

[070-170]eyes beginning to gleam with resentment.

[070-171]‘I am obliged to you for your instruction.’

[070-172]‘Will you tell me, father, in plain words, why you dislike Mr Milvain?’

[070-173]‘I am not inclined to repeat what I have already fruitlessly told you.

[070-174]For the sake of a clear understanding, however, I will let you know the

[070-175]practical result of my dislike. From the day of your marriage with that

[070-176]man you are nothing to me. I shall distinctly forbid you to enter my

[070-177]house. You make your choice, and go your own way. I shall hope never to

[070-178]see your face again.’
