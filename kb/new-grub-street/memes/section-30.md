# Section 30

[029-001]‘Think so?’ asked the publisher.

[029-002]‘Well, I know with certainty that the notice of Markland’s novel is his

[029-003]writing, and I have reasons for suspecting that he did [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s book as

[029-004]well.’

[029-005]‘Smart youngster, that,’ remarked Mr [[jedwood|Jedwood]]. ‘Who is he, by-the-bye?’

[029-006]‘Somebody’s illegitimate son, I believe,’ replied the source of

[029-007]trustworthy information, with a laugh. ‘Denham says he met him in New

[029-008]York a year or two ago, under another name.

[029-009]‘Excuse me,’ interposed Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]], ‘there’s some mistake in all that.’

[029-010]He went on to state what he knew, from Yule himself, concerning

[029-011][[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]’s history. Though in this instance a corrector, Mr Quarmby took

[029-012]an opportunity, a few hours later, of informing Mr [[hinks|Hinks]] that the attack

[029-013]on Yule in [[the-current|The Current]] was almost certainly written by young Milvain,

[029-014]with the result that when the rumour reached Yule’s ears it was

[029-015]delivered as an undoubted and well-known fact.

[029-016]It was a month prior to this that Milvain made his call upon [[marian-yule|Marian]]

[029-017]Yule, on the Sunday when her father was absent. When told of the visit,

[029-018]Yule assumed a manner of indifference, but his daughter understood that

[029-019]he was annoyed. With regard to the sisters who would shortly be living

[029-020]in [[london|London]], he merely said that Marian must behave as discretion directed

[029-021]her. If she wished to invite the Miss Milvains to St Paul’s Crescent,

[029-022]he only begged that the times and seasons of the household might not be

[029-023]disturbed.

[029-024]As her habit was, Marian took refuge in silence. Nothing could have been

[029-025]more welcome to her than the proximity of Maud and [[dora-milvain|Dora]], but she foresaw

[029-026]that her own home would not be freely open to them; perhaps it might be

[029-027]necessary to behave with simple frankness, and let her friends know the

[029-028]embarrassments of the situation. But that could not be done in the first

[029-029]instance; the unkindness would seem too great. A day after the arrival

[029-030]of the girls, she received a note from Dora, and almost at once replied

[029-031]to it by calling at her friends’ lodgings. A week after that, Maud and

[029-032]Dora came to St Paul’s Crescent; it was Sunday, and Mr Yule purposely

[029-033]kept away from home. They had only been once to the house since then,

[029-034]again without meeting Mr Yule. Marian, however, visited them at their

[029-035]lodgings frequently; now and then she met Jasper there. The latter never

[029-036]spoke of her father, and there was no question of inviting him to repeat

[029-037]his call.

[029-038]In the end, Marian was obliged to speak on the subject with her mother.

[029-039]Mrs Yule offered an occasion by asking when the Miss Milvains were

[029-040]coming again.

[029-041]‘I don’t think I shall ever ask them again,’ Marian replied.

[029-042]Her mother understood, and looked troubled.

[029-043]‘I must tell them how it is, that’s all,’ the girl went on. ‘They are

[029-044]sensible; they won’t be offended with me.’

[029-045]‘But your father has never had anything to say against them,’ urged Mrs

[029-046]Yule. ‘Not a word to me, Marian. I’d tell you the truth if he had.’

[029-047]‘It’s too disagreeable, all the same. I can’t invite them here with

[029-048]pleasure. Father has grown prejudiced against them all, and he won’t

[029-049]change. No, I shall just tell them.’

[029-050]‘It’s very hard for you,’ sighed her mother. ‘If I thought I could do

[029-051]any good by speaking--but I can’t, my dear.’

[029-052]‘I know it, mother. Let us go on as we did before.’

[029-053]The day after this, when Yule came home about the hour of dinner, he

[029-054]called Marian’s name from within the study. Marian had not left the

[029-055]house to-day; her work had been set, in the shape of a long task

[029-056]of copying from disorderly manuscript. She left the sitting-room in

[029-057]obedience to her father’s summons.

[029-058]‘Here’s something that will afford you amusement,’ he said, holding

[029-059]to her the new number of The Current, and indicating the notice of his

[029-060]book.

[029-061]She read a few lines, then threw the thing on to the table.

[029-062]‘That kind of writing sickens me,’ she exclaimed, with anger in her

[029-063]eyes. ‘Only base and heartless people can write in that way. You surely

[029-064]won’t let it trouble you?’

[029-065]‘Oh, not for a moment,’ her father answered, with exaggerated show of

[029-066]calm. ‘But I am surprised that you don’t see the literary merit of the

[029-067]work. I thought it would distinctly appeal to you.’

[029-068]There was a strangeness in his voice, as well as in the words, which

[029-069]caused her to look at him inquiringly. She knew him well enough to

[029-070]understand that such a notice would irritate him profoundly; but why

[029-071]should he go out of his way to show it her, and with this peculiar

[029-072]acerbity of manner?

[029-073]‘Why do you say that, father?’

[029-074]‘It doesn’t occur to you who may probably have written it?’

[029-075]She could not miss his meaning; astonishment held her mute for a moment,

[029-076]then she said:

[029-077]‘Surely Mr [[fadge|Fadge]] wrote it himself?’

[029-078]‘I am told not. I am informed on very good authority that one of his

[029-079]young gentlemen has the credit of it.’

[029-080]‘You refer, of course, to Mr Milvain,’ she replied quietly. ‘But I think

[029-081]that can’t be true.’

[029-082]He looked keenly at her. He had expected a more decided protest.

[029-083]‘I see no reason for disbelieving it.’

[029-084]‘I see every reason, until I have your evidence.’

[029-085]This was not at all Marian’s natural tone in argument with him. She was

[029-086]wont to be submissive.

[029-087]‘I was told,’ he continued, hardening face and voice, ‘by someone who

[029-088]had it from Jedwood.’

[029-089]Yule was conscious of untruth in this statement, but his mood would not

[029-090]allow him to speak ingenuously, and he wished to note the effect upon

[029-091]Marian of what he said. There were two beliefs in him: on the one hand,

[029-092]he recognised Fadge in every line of the writing; on the other, he had a

[029-093]perverse satisfaction in convincing himself that it was Milvain who had

[029-094]caught so successfully the master’s manner. He was not the kind of man

[029-095]who can resist an opportunity of justifying, to himself and others, a

[029-096]course into which he has been led by mingled feelings, all more or less

[029-097]unjustifiable.

[029-098]‘How should Jedwood know?’ asked Marian.

[029-099]Yule shrugged his shoulders.

[029-100]‘As if these things didn’t get about among editors and [[publishing-trade|publishers]]!’

[029-101]‘In this case, there’s a mistake.’

[029-102]‘And why, pray?’ His voice trembled with choler. ‘Why need there be a

[029-103]mistake?’

[029-104]‘Because Mr Milvain is quite incapable of [[reviewing-criticism|reviewing]] your book in such a

[029-105]spirit.’

[029-106]‘There is your mistake, my girl. Milvain will do anything that’s asked

[029-107]of him, provided he’s well enough paid.’

[029-108]Marian reflected. When she raised her eyes again they were perfectly

[029-109]calm.

[029-110]‘What has led you to think that?’

[029-111]‘Don’t I know the type of man? Noscitur ex sociis--have you Latin enough

[029-112]for that?’

[029-113]‘You’ll find that you are misinformed,’ Marian replied, and therewith

[029-114]went from the room.

[029-115]She could not trust herself to converse longer. A resentment such as her

[029-116]father had never yet excited in her--such, indeed, as she had seldom, if

[029-117]ever, conceived--threatened to force utterance for itself in words which

[029-118]would change the current of her whole life. She saw her father in his

[029-119]worst aspect, and her heart was shaken by an unnatural revolt from him.

[029-120]Let his assurance of what he reported be ever so firm, what right had

[029-121]he to make this use of it? His behaviour was spiteful. Suppose he

[029-122]entertained suspicions which seemed to make it his duty to warn her

[029-123]against Milvain, this was not the way to go about it. A father actuated

[029-124]by simple motives of affection would never speak and look thus.

[029-125]It was the hateful spirit of literary rancour that ruled him; the spirit

[029-126]that made people eager to believe all evil, that blinded and maddened.

[029-127]Never had she felt so strongly the unworthiness of the existence to

[029-128]which she was condemned. That contemptible review, and now her father’s

[029-129]ignoble passion--such things were enough to make all literature appear a

[029-130]morbid excrescence upon human life.

[029-131]Forgetful of the time, she sat in her bedroom until a knock at the

[029-132]door, and her mother’s voice, admonished her that dinner was waiting. An

[029-133]impulse all but caused her to say that she would rather not go down

[029-134]for the meal, that she wished to be left alone. But this would be weak

[029-135]peevishness. She just looked at the glass to see that her face bore no

[029-136]unwonted signs, and descended to take her place as usual.

[029-137]Throughout the dinner there passed no word of conversation. Yule was at

[029-138]his blackest; he gobbled a few mouthfuls, then occupied himself with the

[029-139]evening paper. On rising, he said to Marian:

[029-140]‘Have you copied the whole of that?’

[029-141]The tone would have been uncivil if addressed to an impertinent servant.

[029-142]‘Not much more than half,’ was the cold reply.

[029-143]‘Can you finish it to-night?’

[029-144]‘I’m afraid not. I am going out.’

[029-145]‘Then I must do it myself’

[029-146]And he went to the study.

[029-147]Mrs Yule was in an anguish of nervousness.

[029-148]‘What is it, dear?’ she asked of Marian, in a pleading whisper. ‘Oh,

[029-149]don’t quarrel with your father! Don’t!’

[029-150]‘I can’t be a slave, mother, and I can’t be treated unjustly.’

[029-151]‘What is it? Let me go and speak to him.’

[029-152]‘It’s no use. We CAN’T live in terror.’

[029-153]For Mrs Yule this was unimaginable disaster. She had never dreamt that

[029-154]Marian, the still, gentle Marian, could be driven to revolt. And it had

[029-155]come with the suddenness of a thunderclap. She wished to ask what had

[029-156]taken place between father and daughter in the brief interview before

[029-157]dinner; but Marian gave her no chance, quitting the room upon those last

[029-158]trembling words.

[029-159]The girl had resolved to visit her friends, the sisters, and tell them

[029-160]that in future they must never come to see her at home. But it was no

[029-161]easy thing for her to stifle her conscience, and leave her father to

[029-162]toil over that copying which had need of being finished. Not her will,

[029-163]but her exasperated feeling, had replied to him that she would not do

[029-164]the work; already it astonished her that she had really spoken such

[029-165]words. And as the throbbing of her pulses subsided, she saw more clearly

[029-166]into the motives of this wretched tumult which possessed her. Her

[029-167]mind was harassed with a fear lest in defending Milvain she had spoken

[029-168]foolishly. Had he not himself said to her that he might be guilty of

[029-169]base things, just to make his way? Perhaps it was the intolerable pain

[029-170]of imagining that he had already made good his words, which robbed her

[029-171]of self-control and made her meet her father’s rudeness with defiance.

[029-172]Impossible to carry out her purpose; she could not deliberately leave

[029-173]the house and spend some hours away with the thought of such wrath and

[029-174]misery left behind her. Gradually she was returning to her natural self;

[029-175]fear and penitence were chill at her heart.

[029-176]She went down to the study, tapped, and entered.

[029-177]‘Father, I said something that I did not really mean. Of course I shall

[029-178]go on with the copying and finish it as soon as possible.’

[029-179]‘You will do nothing of the kind, my girl.’ He was in his usual place,

[029-180]already working at Marian’s task; he spoke in a low, thick voice. ‘Spend

[029-181]your evening as you choose, I have no need of you.’

[029-182]‘I behaved very ill-temperedly. Forgive me, father.’

[029-183]‘Have the goodness to go away. You hear me?’

[029-184]His eyes were inflamed, and his discoloured teeth showed themselves

[029-185]savagely. Marian durst not, really durst not approach him. She

[029-186]hesitated, but once more a sense of hateful injustice moved within her,

[029-187]and she went away as quietly as she had entered.

[029-188]She said to herself that now it was her perfect right to go whither

[029-189]she would. But the freedom was only in theory; her submissive and timid

[029-190]nature kept her at home--and upstairs in her own room; for, if she

[029-191]went to sit with her mother, of necessity she must talk about what had

[029-192]happened, and that she felt unable to do. Some friend to whom she could

[029-193]unbosom all her sufferings would now have been very precious to her, but

[029-194]Maud and Dora were her only intimates, and to them she might not make

[029-195]the full confession which gives solace.
