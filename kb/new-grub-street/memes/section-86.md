# Section 86

[085-001]‘He makes considerably more [[money-in-literature|money]] than we do.’

[085-002]‘Money! What’s money?’

[085-003][[dora-milvain|Dora]] was again mirthful.

[085-004]‘Oh, of course money is nothing! We write for honour and glory. Don’t

[085-005]forget to insist on that when you reprove [[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]]; no doubt it will

[085-006]impress him.’

[085-007]Late in the evening of that day, when the brother and sister had

[085-008]strolled by moonlight up to the windmill which occupies the highest

[085-009]point of Sark, and as they stood looking upon the pale expanse of sea,

[085-010]dotted with the gleam of light-houses near and far, Dora broke the

[085-011]silence to say quietly:

[085-012]‘I may as well tell you that Mr Whelpdale wants to know if I will marry

[085-013]him.’

[085-014]‘The deuce he does!’ cried [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]], with a start. ‘If I didn’t half

[085-015]suspect something of that kind! What astounding impudence!’

[085-016]‘You seriously think so?’

[085-017]‘Well, don’t you? You hardly know him, to begin with. And then--oh,

[085-018]confound it!’

[085-019]‘Very well, I’ll tell him that his impudence astonishes me.’

[085-020]‘You will?’

[085-021]‘Certainly. Of course in civil terms. But don’t let this make any

[085-022]difference between you and him. Just pretend to know nothing about it;

[085-023]no harm is done.’

[085-024]‘You are speaking in earnest?’

[085-025]‘Quite. He has written in a very proper way, and there’s no reason

[085-026]whatever to disturb our friendliness with him. I have a right to give

[085-027]directions in a matter like this, and you’ll please to obey them.’

[085-028]Before going to bed Dora wrote a letter to Mr Whelpdale, not,

[085-029]indeed, accepting his offer forthwith, but conveying to him with much

[085-030]gracefulness an unmistakable encouragement to persevere. This was posted

[085-031]on the morrow, and its writer continued to benefit most remarkably by

[085-032]the sun and breezes and rock-scrambling of Sark.

[085-033]Soon after their return to [[london|London]], Dora had the satisfaction of paying

[085-034]the first visit to her sister at the Dolomores’ house in Ovington

[085-035]Square. Maud was established in the midst of luxuries, and talked with

[085-036]laughing scorn of the days when she inhabited [[grub-street|Grub Street]]; her literary

[085-037]tastes were henceforth to serve as merely a note of distinction, an

[085-038]added grace which made evident her superiority to the well-attired and

[085-039]smooth-tongued people among whom she was content to shine. On the one

[085-040]hand, she had contact with the world of fashionable literature, on

[085-041]the other with that of fashionable ignorance. Mrs Lane’s house was a

[085-042]meeting-point of the two spheres.

[085-043]‘I shan’t be there very often,’ remarked Jasper, as Dora and he

[085-044]discussed their sister’s magnificence. ‘That’s all very well in its way,

[085-045]but I aim at something higher.’

[085-046]‘So do I,’ Dora replied.

[085-047]‘I’m very glad to hear that. I confess it seemed to me that you were

[085-048]rather too cordial with Whelpdale yesterday.’

[085-049]‘One must behave civilly. Mr Whelpdale quite understands me.’

[085-050]‘You are sure of that? He didn’t seem quite so gloomy as he ought to

[085-051]have been.’

[085-052]‘The [[literary-ambition|success]] of [[chit-chat|Chit-Chat]] keeps him in good spirits.’

[085-053]It was perhaps a week after this that Mrs Dolomore came quite

[085-054]unexpectedly to the house by Regent’s Park, as early as eleven o’clock

[085-055]in the morning. She had a long talk in private with Dora. Jasper was not

[085-056]at home; when he returned towards evening, Dora came to his room with a

[085-057]countenance which disconcerted him.

[085-058]‘Is it true,’ she asked abruptly, standing before him with her hands

[085-059]strained together, ‘that you have been representing yourself as no

[085-060]longer engaged to [[marian-yule|Marian]]?’

[085-061]‘Who has told you so?’

[085-062]‘That doesn’t matter. I have heard it, and I want to know from you that

[085-063]it is false.’

[085-064]Jasper thrust his hands into his pockets and walked apart.

[085-065]‘I can take no notice,’ he said with indifference, ‘of anonymous

[085-066]gossip.’

[085-067]‘Well, then, I will tell you how I have heard. Maud came this morning,

[085-068]and told me that Mrs Betterton had been asking her about it. Mrs

[085-069]Betterton had heard from Mrs Lane.’

[085-070]‘From Mrs Lane? And from whom did she hear, pray?’

[085-071]‘That I don’t know. Is it true or not?’

[085-072]‘I have never told anyone that my engagement was at an end,’ replied

[085-073]Jasper, deliberately.

[085-074]The girl met his eyes.

[085-075]‘Then I was right,’ she said. ‘Of course I told Maud that it was

[085-076]impossible to believe this for a moment. But how has it come to be

[085-077]said?’

[085-078]‘You might as well ask me how any lie gets into circulation among people

[085-079]of that sort. I have told you the truth, and there’s an end of it.’

[085-080]Dora lingered for a while, but left the room without saying anything

[085-081]more.

[085-082]She sat up late, mostly engaged in thinking, though at times an open

[085-083]book was in her hand. It was nearly half-past twelve when a very light

[085-084]rap at the door caused her to start. She called, and Jasper came in.

[085-085]‘Why are you still up?’ he asked, avoiding her look as he moved forward

[085-086]and took a leaning attitude behind an easy-chair.

[085-087]‘Oh, I don’t know. Do you want anything?’

[085-088]There was a pause; then Jasper said in an unsteady voice:

[085-089]‘I am not given to lying, Dora, and I feel confoundedly uncomfortable

[085-090]about what I said to you early this evening. I didn’t lie in the

[085-091]ordinary sense; it’s true enough that I have never told anyone that

[085-092]my engagement was at an end. But I have acted as if it were, and it’s

[085-093]better I should tell you.’

[085-094]His sister gazed at him with indignation.

[085-095]‘You have acted as if you were free?’

[085-096]‘Yes. I have proposed to Miss Rupert. How Mrs Lane and that lot have

[085-097]come to know anything about this I don’t understand. I am not aware of

[085-098]any connecting link between them and the Ruperts, or the Barlows either.

[085-099]Perhaps there are none; most likely the rumour has no foundation in

[085-100]their knowledge. Still, it is better that I should have told you. Miss

[085-101]Rupert has never heard that I was engaged, nor have her friends the

[085-102]Barlows--at least I don’t see how they could have done. She may have

[085-103]told Mrs Barlow of my proposal--probably would; and this may somehow

[085-104]have got round to those other people. But Maud didn’t make any mention

[085-105]of Miss Rupert, did she?’

[085-106]Dora replied with a cold negative.

[085-107]‘Well, there’s the state of things. It isn’t pleasant, but that’s what I

[085-108]have done.’

[085-109]‘Do you mean that Miss Rupert has accepted you?’

[085-110]‘No. I wrote to her. She answered that she was going to Germany for a

[085-111]few weeks, and that I should have her reply whilst she was away. I am

[085-112]waiting.’

[085-113]‘But what name is to be given to behaviour such as this?’

[085-114]‘Listen: didn’t you know perfectly well that this must be the end of

[085-115]it?’

[085-116]‘Do you suppose I thought you utterly shameless and cruel beyond words?’

[085-117]‘I suppose I am both. It was a moment of desperate temptation, though.

[085-118]I had dined at the Ruperts’--you remember--and it seemed to me there was

[085-119]no mistaking the girl’s manner.’

[085-120]‘Don’t call her a girl!’ broke in Dora, scornfully. ‘You say she is

[085-121]several years older than yourself.’

[085-122]‘Well, at all events, she’s intellectual, and very rich. I yielded to

[085-123]the temptation.’

[085-124]‘And deserted Marian just when she has most need of help and

[085-125]consolation? It’s frightful!’

[085-126]Jasper moved to another chair and sat down. He was much perturbed.

[085-127]‘Look here, Dora, I regret it; I do, indeed. And, what’s more, if that

[085-128]woman refuses me--as it’s more than likely she will--I will go to Marian

[085-129]and ask her to marry me at once. I promise that.’

[085-130]His sister made a movement of contemptuous impatience.

[085-131]‘And if the woman doesn’t refuse you?’

[085-132]‘Then I can’t help it. But there’s one thing more I will say. Whether I

[085-133]marry Marian or Miss Rupert, I sacrifice my strongest feelings--in the

[085-134]one case to a sense of duty, in the other to worldly advantage. I was

[085-135]an idiot to write that letter, for I knew at the time that there was a

[085-136]woman who is far more to me than Miss Rupert and all her money--a woman

[085-137]I might, perhaps, marry. Don’t ask any questions; I shall not answer

[085-138]them. As I have said so much, I wished you to understand my position

[085-139]fully. You know the promise I have made. Don’t say anything to Marian;

[085-140]if I am left free I shall marry her as soon as possible.’

[085-141]And so he left the room.

[085-142]For a fortnight and more he remained in uncertainty. His life was very

[085-143]uncomfortable, for Dora would only speak to him when necessity compelled

[085-144]her; and there were two meetings with Marian, at which he had to act

[085-145]his part as well as he could. At length came the expected letter. Very

[085-146]nicely expressed, very friendly, very complimentary, but--a refusal.

[085-147]He handed it to Dora across the breakfast-table, saying with a pinched

[085-148]smile:

[085-149]‘Now you can look cheerful again. I am doomed.’

[085-150]CHAPTER XXXV. FEVER AND REST

[085-151]Milvain’s skilful efforts notwithstanding, ‘[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer,’ had no

[085-152][[literary-success|success]]. By two [[publishing-trade|publishers]] the book had been declined; the firm which

[085-153]brought it out offered the author half profits and fifteen pounds on

[085-154]account, greatly to [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]]’s satisfaction. But reviewers in

[085-155]general were either angry or coldly contemptuous. ‘Let Mr Biffen bear in

[085-156]mind,’ said one of these sages, ‘that a novelist’s first duty is to tell

[085-157]a story.’ ‘Mr Biffen,’ wrote another, ‘seems not to understand that

[085-158]a work of art must before everything else afford amusement.’ ‘A

[085-159]pretentious book of the genre _ennuyant_,’ was the brief comment of a

[085-160]Society journal. A weekly of high standing began its short notice in a

[085-161]rage: ‘Here is another of those intolerable productions for which we

[085-162]are indebted to the spirit of [[literary-realism|grovelling realism]]. This author, let it be

[085-163]said, is never offensive, but then one must go on to describe his work

[085-164]by a succession of negatives; it is never interesting, never profitable,

[085-165]never--’ and the rest. The eulogy in [[the-west-end|The West End]] had a few timid

[085-166]echoes. That in [[the-current|The Current]] would have secured more imitators, but

[085-167]unfortunately it appeared when most of the [[reviewing-criticism|reviewing]] had already

[085-168]been done. And, as Jasper truly said, only a concurrence of powerful

[085-169]testimonials could have compelled any number of people to affect an

[085-170]interest in this book. ‘The first duty of a novelist is to tell a

[085-171]story:’ the perpetual repetition of this phrase is a warning to all

[085-172]men who propose drawing from the life. Biffen only offered a slice of

[085-173]biography, and it was found to lack flavour.

[085-174]He wrote to [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]]: ‘I cannot thank you enough for this very kind

[085-175]letter about my book; I value it more than I should the praises of all

[085-176]the reviewers in existence. You have understood my aim. Few people

[085-177]will do that, and very few indeed could express it with such clear

[085-178]conciseness.’

[085-179]If Amy had but contented herself with a civil acknowledgment of the

[085-180]volumes he sent her! She thought it a kindness to write to him so

[085-181]appreciatively, to exaggerate her approval. The poor fellow was so

[085-182]lonely. Yes, but his loneliness only became intolerable when a beautiful

[085-183]woman had smiled upon him, and so forced him to dream perpetually of

[085-184]that supreme joy of life which to him was forbidden.

[085-185]It was a fatal day, that on which Amy put herself under his guidance

[085-186]to visit [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s poor room at [[islington|Islington]]. In the old times, Harold had

[085-187]been wont to regard his friend’s wife as the perfect woman; seldom in

[085-188]his life had he enjoyed female society, and when he first met Amy it

[085-189]was years since he had spoken with any woman above the rank of a

[085-190]lodging-house keeper or a needle-plier. Her beauty seemed to him of a

[085-191]very high order, and her mental endowments filled him with an exquisite

[085-192]delight, not to be appreciated by men who have never been in his

[085-193]position. When the rupture came between Amy and her husband, Harold

[085-194]could not believe that she was in any way to blame; held to Reardon by

[085-195]strong friendship, he yet accused him of injustice to Amy. And what

[085-196]he saw of her at [[brighton|Brighton]] confirmed him in this judgment. When he

[085-197]accompanied her to Manville Street, he allowed her, of course, to remain

[085-198]alone in the room where Reardon had lived; but Amy presently summoned

[085-199]him, and asked him questions. Every tear she shed watered a growth of

[085-200]passionate tenderness in the solitary man’s heart. Parting from her at

[085-201]length, he went to hide his face in darkness and think of her--think of

[085-202]her.
