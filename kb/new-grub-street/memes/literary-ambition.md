# Literary Ambition

Every major character in *New Grub Street* is driven by ambition, but the novel distinguishes sharply between kinds. There is the ambition for artistic achievement, the ambition for worldly success, and the ambition simply to survive — and the literary marketplace, as Gissing depicts it, rewards only the second.

## Milvain's Worldly Ambition

Jasper Milvain is ambition personified — candid, systematic, and unburdened by artistic conscience. He studies the market, cultivates connections, and treats every social interaction as a career investment. His ambition is explicitly commercial: "In ten years, I repeat, I shall be making my thousand a year" (P02-204). The precision of the figure — a thousand a year — is characteristic. Milvain's ambition is quantifiable, scalable, and entirely successful.

His worldly ambition extends beyond money to social position. He cultivates "a strong vein of worldly ambition" (009-079), making few friends and keeping his eyes on the main chance. The novel does not condemn Milvain for this — Gissing's treatment is sociological rather than moralistic. Milvain succeeds because his ambition is perfectly calibrated to the market he inhabits.

## Reardon's Artistic Ambition

Edwin Reardon's ambition is the mirror image of Milvain's. He wants to write serious, lasting fiction — to be recognized as an artist, not a tradesman. But in the modern Grub Street, artistic ambition is a liability. Reardon's slow, painstaking method cannot compete with Milvain's rapid-fire journalism. His ambition for quality makes him slow, and slowness is fatal in a marketplace that rewards speed.

The tragedy is that Reardon's ambition was once rewarded — his early work showed promise. But the demands of commercial production eroded his gift, and by the time of the novel's main action, his ambition has become a kind of paralysis.

## Biffen's Pure Ambition

Harold Biffen represents a third kind of ambition — the ambition for absolute artistic integrity, regardless of recognition or reward. His goal of "absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent" (024-171) is genuine and original. But this pure ambition is the most punishing of all: it offers neither money nor recognition, only the satisfaction of the work itself. Biffen starves for his art.

## The Ambition Hierarchy

Gissing's implicit argument is that the literary marketplace enforces a hierarchy of ambitions. Worldly ambition is rewarded; artistic ambition is tolerated only if it produces marketable results; pure ambition is crushed. There is no path that combines artistic and worldly success — the novel is a sustained argument against that reconciliation.
