# Literary Realism

The aesthetic commitment to representing life as it actually is — without idealization, without romance, without concession to popular taste — is both the highest artistic value in *New Grub Street* and the surest path to commercial failure. Gissing explores realism not as an abstract literary doctrine but as a lived conflict between artistic conscience and economic survival.

## Biffen's Manifesto

The novel's most explicit statement of realist doctrine comes from Harold Biffen, the impoverished writer who is Reardon's closest friend. Biffen articulates his artistic aim with memorable precision:

> "What I really aim at is an absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent. The field, as I understand it, is a new one; I don't know any writer who has treated it." [024-171–024-172]

Biffen's phrase "the ignobly decent" captures the realist project: to render ordinary, unheroic lives with artistic seriousness. His ambition is genuinely original — he seeks a subject matter that no previous writer has explored. Yet Biffen is also the novel's most materially desperate character, and his commitment to realism brings him starvation, not recognition.

## Realism and the Marketplace

The commercial literary world, as Gissing depicts it, has no use for realism. A reviewer in the novel dismisses a realist work as one of "those intolerable productions for which we are indebted to the spirit of grovelling realism" (085-162). The phrase "grovelling realism" — used by a critic writing for the market — captures the contempt with which commercial literary culture regards unvarnished truth-telling.

Reardon himself had, as a younger writer, a moment before "any mission of literary realism had been imposed upon him" (087-022) — a poignant phrase suggesting that realism is experienced as a burden, a vocation that chooses the writer rather than the reverse.

## The Cost of Truth

Realism in the novel is not merely an aesthetic position but an ethical one. To write truthfully about the "ignobly decent" is to refuse the market's demand for sensation, romance, and flattery. The realist pays for this refusal in poverty and obscurity. Biffen's fate — and Reardon's — suggests that Gissing saw realism as a kind of martyrdom: the artist's integrity preserved at the cost of everything else.
