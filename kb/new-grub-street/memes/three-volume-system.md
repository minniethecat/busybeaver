# Three-Volume System

The "three-decker" or three-volume novel was the standard format for new fiction in Victorian Britain — a commercial structure that profoundly shaped the literary marketplace depicted in *New Grub Street*. For Gissing's characters, the three-volume system is both an economic reality and a symbol of the constraints that commerce imposes on art.

## The Economics of Three Volumes

The system was straightforward but rigid: a new novel was published in three separate volumes, sold at 31 shillings and 6 pence (a guinea and a half) — far beyond the means of most individual readers. The primary customers were circulating libraries, above all Mudie's, which bought in bulk. This created an artificial market where novels were written to fill three volumes regardless of what the story required.

In the novel, the economics are treated as common knowledge. A character observes that a novelist "may live on a yearly three-volume novel — I mean the man who is well established" (035-079), while noting that the fees for authors are meagre: "if I could finish a three-volume book, I doubt if they would give me fifty pounds for the copyright" (013-078). Reardon's novel *The Optimist* is a three-volume work (008-176), and its commercial failure — despite literary merit — demonstrates how the format rewarded prolific output over quality.

## The System as Creative Constraint

The three-volume format demanded a specific kind of narrative: plots needed to stretch across three physical volumes, each ending at a point of suspense to ensure the library subscriber would request the next. Characters frequently padded their work to meet the volume requirement. Biffen, the novel's realist purist, reflects bitterly that he began "to write three-volume novels, aiming at reputation" only to find that "an evil day" followed — the system rewarded neither reputation nor realism (066-135).

The format was already in decline when Gissing wrote the novel (1891). By the mid-1890s, the single-volume novel at six shillings would displace the three-decker entirely. The characters in *New Grub Street* inhabit this transitional moment — Milvain, with his commercial instincts, navigates the changing market, while Reardon remains trapped in the assumptions of the old system.

## Beyond the Format

The three-volume system serves in the novel as a synecdoche for the entire commercial apparatus that mediates between writer and reader. When characters discuss "the trade," "the market," or "the business," the three-volume system is the unspoken architecture beneath these abstractions. Its decline mirrors the broader transformation of literary life that Gissing anatomizes.
