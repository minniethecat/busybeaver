# Section 52

[051-001]‘Perhaps so.’

[051-002]The evening passed drearily. Fatigued with her emotions, [[marian-yule|Marian]] went

[051-003]early to bed; she even slept later than usual in the morning, and on

[051-004]descending she found her father already at the breakfast-table. No

[051-005]greeting passed, and there was no conversation during the meal. Marian

[051-006]noticed that her mother kept glancing at her in a peculiarly grave way;

[051-007]but she felt ill and dejected, and could fix her thoughts on no subject.

[051-008]As he left the table [[alfred-yule|Yule]] said to her:

[051-009]‘I want to speak to you for a moment. I shall be in the study.’

[051-010]She joined him there very soon. He looked coldly at her, and said in a

[051-011]distant tone:

[051-012]‘Dead!’

[051-013]‘He died of apoplexy, at a meeting in [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]]. I shall go down

[051-014]this morning, and of course remain till after the funeral. I see no

[051-015]necessity for your going, unless, of course, it is your desire to do

[051-016]so.’

[051-017]‘No; I should do as you wish.’

[051-018]‘I think you had better not go to the Museum whilst I am away. You will

[051-019]occupy yourself as you think fit.’

[051-020]‘I shall go on with the Harrington notes.’

[051-021]‘As you please. I don’t know what mourning it would be decent for you to

[051-022]wear; you must consult with your mother about that. That is all I wished

[051-023]to say.’

[051-024]His tone was dismissal. Marian had a struggle with herself but she could

[051-025]find nothing to reply to his cold phrases. And an hour or two afterwards

[051-026]Yule left the house without leave-taking.

[051-027]Soon after his departure there was a visitor’s rat-tat at the door;

[051-028]it heralded Mrs Goby. In the interview which then took place Marian

[051-029]assisted her mother to bear the vigorous onslaughts of the haberdasher’s

[051-030]wife. For more than two hours Mrs Goby related her grievances, against

[051-031]the fugitive servant, against Mrs Yule, against Mr Yule; meeting with no

[051-032]irritating opposition, she was able in this space of time to cool down

[051-033]to the temperature of normal intercourse, and when she went forth from

[051-034]the house again it was in a mood of dignified displeasure which she felt

[051-035]to be some recompense for the injuries of yesterday.

[051-036]A result of this annoyance was to postpone conversation between mother

[051-037]and daughter on the subject of [[john-yule|John Yule]]’s death until a late hour of

[051-038]the afternoon. Marian was at work in the study, or endeavouring to work,

[051-039]for her thoughts would not fix themselves on the matter in hand for

[051-040]many minutes together, and Mrs Yule came in with more than her customary

[051-041]diffidence.

[051-042]‘Have you nearly done for to-day, dear?’

[051-043]‘Enough for the present, I think.’

[051-044]She laid down her pen, and leant back in the chair.

[051-045]‘Marian, do you think your father will be rich?’

[051-046]‘I have no idea, mother. I suppose we shall know very soon.’

[051-047]Her tone was dreamy. She seemed to herself to be speaking of something

[051-048]which scarcely at all concerned her, of vague possibilities which did

[051-049]not affect her habits of thought.

[051-050]‘If that happens,’ continued Mrs Yule, in a low tone of distress, ‘I

[051-051]don’t know what I shall do.’

[051-052]Marian looked at her questioningly.

[051-053]‘I can’t wish that it mayn’t happen,’ her mother went on; ‘I can’t, for

[051-054]his sake and for yours; but I don’t know what I shall do. He’d think me

[051-055]more in his way than ever. He’d wish to have a large house, and live

[051-056]in quite a different way; and how could I manage then? I couldn’t show

[051-057]myself; he’d be too much ashamed of me. I shouldn’t be in my place; even

[051-058]you’d feel ashamed of me.’

[051-059]‘You mustn’t say that, mother. I have never given you cause to think

[051-060]that.’

[051-061]‘No, my dear, you haven’t; but it would be only natural. I couldn’t live

[051-062]the kind of life that you’re fit for. I shall be nothing but a hindrance

[051-063]and a shame to both of you.’

[051-064]‘To me you would never be either hindrance or shame; be quite sure of

[051-065]that. And as for father, I am all but certain that, if he became rich,

[051-066]he would be a very much kinder man, a better man in every way. It is

[051-067][[literary-poverty|poverty]] that has made him worse than he naturally is; it has that effect

[051-068]on almost everybody. Money does harm, too, sometimes; but never, I

[051-069]think, to people who have a good heart and a strong mind. Father is

[051-070]naturally a warm-hearted man; riches would bring out all the best in

[051-071]him. He would be generous again, which he has almost forgotten how to

[051-072]be among all his disappointments and battlings. Don’t be afraid of that

[051-073]change, but hope for it.’

[051-074]Mrs Yule gave a troublous sigh, and for a few minutes pondered

[051-075]anxiously.

[051-076]‘I wasn’t thinking so much about myself’ she said at length. ‘It’s the

[051-077]hindrance I should be to father. Just because of me, he mightn’t be able

[051-078]to use his [[money-in-literature|money]] as he’d wish. He’d always be feeling that if it wasn’t

[051-079]for me things would be so much better for him and for you as well.’

[051-080]‘You must remember,’ Marian replied, ‘that at father’s age people don’t

[051-081]care to make such great changes. His home life, I feel sure, wouldn’t be

[051-082]so very different from what it is now; he would prefer to use his money

[051-083]in starting a paper or magazine. I know that would be his first thought.

[051-084]If more acquaintances came to his house, what would that matter? It

[051-085]isn’t as if he wished for fashionable society. They would be literary

[051-086]people, and why ever shouldn’t you meet with them?’

[051-087]‘I’ve always been the reason why he couldn’t have many friends.’

[051-088]‘That’s a great mistake. If father ever said that, in his bad temper, he

[051-089]knew it wasn’t the truth. The chief reason has always been his poverty.

[051-090]It costs money to entertain friends; time as well. Don’t think in this

[051-091]anxious way, mother. If we are to be rich, it will be better for all of

[051-092]us.’

[051-093]Marian had every reason for seeking to persuade herself that this was

[051-094]true. In her own heart there was a fear of how wealth might affect her

[051-095]father, but she could not bring herself to face the darker prospect. For

[051-096]her so much depended on that hope of a revival of generous feeling under

[051-097]sunny influences.

[051-098]It was only after this conversation that she began to reflect on all the

[051-099]possible consequences of her uncle’s death. As yet she had been too much

[051-100]disturbed to grasp as a reality the event to which she had often looked

[051-101]forward, though as to something still remote, and of quite uncertain

[051-102]results. Perhaps at this moment, though she could not know it, the

[051-103]course of her life had undergone the most important change. Perhaps

[051-104]there was no more need for her to labour upon this ‘article’ she was

[051-105]manufacturing.

[051-106]She did not think it probable that she herself would benefit directly by

[051-107]John Yule’s will. There was no certainty that even her father would, for

[051-108]he and his brother had never been on cordial terms. But on the whole it

[051-109]seemed likely that he would inherit money enough to free him from the

[051-110]toil of writing for periodicals. He himself anticipated that. What else

[051-111]could be the meaning of those words in which (and it was before

[051-112]the arrival of the news) he had warned her against ‘people who made

[051-113]connections only with self-interest in view?’ This threw a sudden light

[051-114]upon her father’s attitude towards [[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]. Evidently he thought

[051-115]that Jasper regarded her as a possible heiress, sooner or later.

[051-116]That suspicion was rankling in his mind; doubtless it intensified the

[051-117]prejudice which originated in literary animosity.

[051-118]Was there any truth in his suspicion? She did not shrink from admitting

[051-119]that there might be. Jasper had from the first been so frank with her,

[051-120]had so often repeated that money was at present his chief need. If her

[051-121]father inherited substantial property, would it induce Jasper to declare

[051-122]himself more than her friend? She could view the possibility of that,

[051-123]and yet not for a moment be shaken in her love. It was plain that

[051-124]Jasper could not think of marrying until his position and prospects were

[051-125]greatly improved; practically, his sisters depended upon him. What folly

[051-126]it would be to draw back if circumstances led him to avow what hitherto

[051-127]he had so slightly disguised! She had the conviction that he valued her

[051-128]for her own sake; if the obstacle between them could only be removed,

[051-129]what matter how?

[051-130]Would he be willing to abandon Clement [[fadge|Fadge]], and come over to her

[051-131]father’s side? If Yule were able to found a magazine?

[051-132]Had she read or heard of a girl who went so far in concessions, Marian

[051-133]would have turned away, her delicacy offended. In her own case she could

[051-134]indulge to the utmost that practicality which colours a woman’s thought

[051-135]even in mid passion. The cold exhibition of ignoble scheming will repel

[051-136]many a woman who, for her own heart’s desire, is capable of that same

[051-137]compromise with her strict sense of honour.

[051-138]Marian wrote to [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]], telling her what had happened. But she

[051-139]refrained from visiting her friends.

[051-140]Each night found her more restless, each morning less able to employ

[051-141]herself. She shut herself in the study merely to be alone with her

[051-142]thoughts, to be able to walk backwards and forwards, or sit for hours in

[051-143]feverish reverie. From her father came no news. Her mother was suffering

[051-144]dreadfully from suspense, and often had eyes red with weeping. Absorbed

[051-145]in her own hopes and fears, whilst every hour harassed her more

[051-146]intolerably, Marian was unable to play the part of an encourager; she

[051-147]had never known such exclusiveness of self-occupation.

[051-148]Yule’s return was unannounced. Early in the afternoon, when he had been

[051-149]absent five days, he entered the house, deposited his travelling-bag in

[051-150]the passage, and went upstairs. Marian had come out of the study just

[051-151]in time to see him up on the first landing; at the same moment Mrs Yule

[051-152]ascended from the kitchen.

[051-153]‘Wasn’t that father?’

[051-154]‘Yes, he has gone up.’

[051-155]‘Did he say anything?’

[051-156]Marian shook her head. They looked at the travelling-bag, then went into

[051-157]the parlour and waited in silence for more than a quarter of an hour.

[051-158]Yule’s foot was heard on the stairs; he came down slowly, paused in the

[051-159]passage, entered the parlour with his usual grave, cold countenance.

[051-160]CHAPTER XXII. THE LEGATEES

[051-161]Each day Jasper came to inquire of his sisters if they had news from

[051-162]Wattleborough or from Marian Yule. He exhibited no impatience, spoke of

[051-163]the matter in a disinterested tone; still, he came daily.

[051-164]One afternoon he found Dora working alone. Maud, he was told, had gone

[051-165]to lunch at Mrs Lane’s.

[051-166]‘So soon again? She’s getting very thick with those people. And why

[051-167]don’t they ask you?’

[051-168]‘Maud has told them that I don’t care to go out.’

[051-169]‘It’s all very well, but she mustn’t neglect her work. Did she write

[051-170]anything last night or this morning?’

[051-171]Dora bit the end of her pen and shook her head.

[051-172]‘Why not?’

[051-173]‘The invitation came about five o’clock, and it seemed to unsettle her.’

[051-174]‘Precisely. That’s what I’m afraid of. She isn’t the kind of girl to

[051-175]stick at work if people begin to send her invitations. But I tell you

[051-176]what it is, you must talk seriously to her; she has to get her living,

[051-177]you know. Mrs Lane and her set are not likely to be much use, that’s the

[051-178]worst of it; they’ll merely waste her time, and make her discontented.’

[051-179]His sister executed an elaborate bit of cross-hatching on some waste

[051-180]paper. Her lips were drawn together, and her brows wrinkled. At length

[051-181]she broke the silence by saying:
