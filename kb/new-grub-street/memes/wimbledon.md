Wimbledon is a suburban district in southwest London, featured in George Gissing's *New Grub Street* (1891) as the residence of Horace Barlow, a patron and friend to several of the novel's literary characters.

## Location and Character

Wimbledon lies beyond central London, accessible by an afternoon's journey from the city. In the novel, it represents a space of suburban retreat where literary networking and social encounters occur away from the pressures of Grub Street.

## Associations in the Novel

### Horace Barlow's Residence

Wimbledon is primarily known in the novel as the home of Horace Barlow. Barlow hosts several gatherings at his Wimbledon house, connecting literary figures in a more relaxed suburban setting.

> Mr Horace Barlow invited Edwin Reardon to visit him at Wimbledon, where the editor of *The Study* would also be present — an opportunity for professional networking that Barlow arranged for his friend's benefit (012-055).

### Social Encounters

Wimbledon serves as the setting for significant meetings between characters. Edwin Reardon visits Barlow at Wimbledon early in the novel, receiving an invitation that initially provokes his laughter at the perceived incongruity of his circumstances (012-055). After one such visit, Reardon seeks out Davies, a former colleague at the publishing firm of Jolly and Monk (012-126).

Later in the novel, Jasper Milvain also travels to Wimbledon to visit Horace Barlow, where he again encounters Miss Rupert — a meeting that carries implications for Milvain's marital ambitions (068-019). Dora Milvain, too, mentions an excursion to Wimbledon while staying with the Barlows (052-021).

## Significance

As one of the few named suburban locations in the novel, Wimbledon contrasts with the central London settings (the British Museum Reading Room, Grub Street, and various lodgings) where most of the literary struggle takes place. It represents the periphery of literary society — a place where connections can be cultivated in more comfortable circumstances than the grinding poverty faced by characters like Reardon and Biffen.
