# Section 70

[069-001]He was interrupted by the entrance of a servant who brought coffee.

[069-002]‘Nothing could be more welcome,’ cried [[dora-milvain|Dora]]. ‘[[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]] makes one

[069-003]feel quite chilly.’

[069-004]There was laughter and chatting whilst Maud poured out the beverage.

[069-005]Then Whelpdale pursued his narrative.

[069-006]‘I reached Chicago with not quite five dollars in my pockets, and, with

[069-007]a courage which I now marvel at, I paid immediately four dollars and a

[069-008]half for a week’s board and lodging. “Well,” I said to myself, “for a

[069-009]week I am safe. If I earn nothing in that time, at least I shall owe

[069-010]nothing when I have to turn out into the streets.” It was a rather dirty

[069-011]little boarding-house, in Wabash Avenue, and occupied, as I soon found,

[069-012]almost entirely by actors. There was no fireplace in my bedroom, and

[069-013]if there had been I couldn’t have afforded a fire. But that mattered

[069-014]little; what I had to do was to set forth and discover some way of

[069-015]making [[money-in-literature|money]]. Don’t suppose that I was in a desperate state of mind;

[069-016]how it was, I don’t quite know, but I felt decidedly cheerful. It was

[069-017]pleasant to be in this new region of the earth, and I went about the

[069-018]town like a tourist who has abundant resources.’

[069-019]He sipped his coffee.

[069-020]‘I saw nothing for it but to apply at the office of some newspaper, and

[069-021]as I happened to light upon the biggest of them first of all, I put on

[069-022]a bold face, marched in, asked if I could see the editor. There was no

[069-023]difficulty whatever about this; I was told to ascend by means of the

[069-024]“elevator” to an upper storey, and there I walked into a comfortable

[069-025]little room where a youngish man sat smoking a cigar at a table covered

[069-026]with print and manuscript. I introduced myself, stated my business. “Can

[069-027]you give me work of any kind on your paper?” “Well, what experience have

[069-028]you had?” “None whatever.” The editor smiled. “I’m very much afraid you

[069-029]would be no use to us. But what do you think you could do?” Well now,

[069-030]there was but one thing that by any possibility I could do. I asked him:

[069-031]“Do you publish any fiction--short stories?” “Yes, we’re always glad

[069-032]of a short story, if it’s good.” This was a big daily paper; they have

[069-033]weekly supplements of all conceivable kinds of matter. “Well,” I said,

[069-034]“if I write a story of English life, will you consider it?” “With

[069-035]pleasure.” I left him, and went out as if my existence were henceforth

[069-036]provided for.’

[069-037]He laughed heartily, and was joined by his hearers.

[069-038]‘It was a great thing to be permitted to write a story, but then--what

[069-039]story? I went down to the shore of Lake Michigan; walked there for half

[069-040]an hour in an icy wind. Then I looked for a stationer’s shop, and

[069-041]laid out a few of my remaining cents in the purchase of pen, ink, and

[069-042]paper--my stock of all these things was at an end when I left New York.

[069-043]Then back to the boarding-house. Impossible to write in my bedroom, the

[069-044]temperature was below zero; there was no choice but to sit down in the

[069-045]common room, a place like the smoke-room of a poor commercial hotel in

[069-046]England. A dozen men were gathered about the fire, smoking, talking,

[069-047]quarrelling. Favourable conditions, you see, for literary effort. But

[069-048]the story had to be written, and write it I did, sitting there at the

[069-049]end of a deal table; I finished it in less than a couple of days, a

[069-050]good long story, enough to fill three columns of the huge paper. I stand

[069-051]amazed at my power of concentration as often as I think of it!’

[069-052]‘And was it accepted?’ asked Dora.

[069-053]‘You shall hear. I took my manuscript to the editor, and he told me to

[069-054]come and see him again next morning. I didn’t forget the appointment.

[069-055]As I entered he smiled in a very promising way, and said, “I think

[069-056]your story will do. I’ll put it into the Saturday supplement. Call on

[069-057]Saturday morning and I’ll remunerate you.” How well I remember that

[069-058]word “remunerate”! I have had an affection for the word ever since. And

[069-059]remunerate me he did; scribbled something on a scrap of paper, which

[069-060]I presented to the cashier. The sum was eighteen dollars. Behold me

[069-061]saved!’

[069-062]He sipped his coffee again.

[069-063]‘I have never come across an English editor who treated me with anything

[069-064]like that consideration and general kindliness. How the man had time, in

[069-065]his position, to see me so often, and do things in such a human way,

[069-066]I can’t understand. Imagine anyone trying the same at the office of a

[069-067][[london|London]] newspaper! To begin with, one couldn’t see the editor at all. I

[069-068]shall always think with profound gratitude of that man with the peaked

[069-069]brown beard and pleasant smile.’

[069-070]‘But did the pea-nuts come after that!’ inquired Dora.

[069-071]‘Alas! they did. For some months I supported myself in Chicago, writing

[069-072]for that same paper, and for others. But at length the flow of my

[069-073]inspiration was checked; I had written myself out. And I began to grow

[069-074]home-sick, wanted to get back to England. The result was that I found

[069-075]myself one day in New York again, but without money enough to pay for a

[069-076]passage home. I tried to write one more story. But it happened, as I was

[069-077]looking over newspapers in a reading-room, that I saw one of my Chicago

[069-078]tales copied into a paper published at Troy. Now Troy was not very far

[069-079]off; and it occurred to me that, if I went there, the editor of this

[069-080]paper might be disposed to employ me, seeing he had a taste for my

[069-081]fiction. And I went, up the Hudson by steamboat. On landing at Troy I

[069-082]was as badly off as when I reached Chicago; I had less than a dollar.

[069-083]And the worst of it was I had come on a vain errand; the editor treated

[069-084]me with scant courtesy, and no work was to be got. I took a little room,

[069-085]paying for it day by day, and in the meantime I fed on those loathsome

[069-086]pea-nuts, buying a handful in the street now and then. And I assure you

[069-087]I looked starvation in the face.’

[069-088]‘What sort of a town is Troy?’ asked [[marian-yule|Marian]], speaking for the first

[069-089]time.

[069-090]‘Don’t ask me. They make straw hats there principally, and they sell

[069-091]pea-nuts. More I remember not.’

[069-092]‘But you didn’t starve to death,’ said Maud.

[069-093]‘No, I just didn’t. I went one afternoon into a lawyer’s office,

[069-094]thinking I might get some copying work, and there I found an odd-looking

[069-095]old man, sitting with an open Bible on his knees. He explained to me

[069-096]that he wasn’t the lawyer; that the lawyer was away on business,

[069-097]and that he was just guarding the office. Well, could he help me?

[069-098]He meditated, and a thought occurred to him. “Go,” he said, “to

[069-099]such-and-such a boarding-house, and ask for Mr Freeman Sterling. He is

[069-100]just starting on a business tour, and wants a young man to accompany

[069-101]him.” I didn’t dream of asking what the business was, but sped, as fast

[069-102]as my trembling limbs would carry me, to the address he had mentioned. I

[069-103]asked for Mr Freeman Sterling, and found him. He was a photographer,

[069-104]and his business at present was to go about getting orders for the

[069-105]reproducing of old portraits. A good-natured young fellow. He said he

[069-106]liked the look of me, and on the spot engaged me to assist him in a

[069-107]house-to-house visitation. He would pay for my board and lodging, and

[069-108]give me a commission on all the orders I obtained. Forthwith I sat down

[069-109]to a “square meal,” and ate--my conscience, how I ate!’

[069-110]‘You were not eminently successful in that pursuit, I think?’ said

[069-111][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]].

[069-112]‘I don’t think I got half-a-dozen orders. Yet that good Samaritan

[069-113]supported me for five or six weeks, whilst we travelled from Troy to

[069-114]Boston. It couldn’t go on; I was ashamed of myself; at last I told

[069-115]him that we must part. Upon my word, I believe he would have paid my

[069-116]expenses for another month; why, I can’t understand. But he had a vast

[069-117]respect for me because I had written in newspapers, and I do seriously

[069-118]think that he didn’t like to tell me I was a useless fellow. We parted

[069-119]on the very best of terms in Boston.’

[069-120]‘And you again had recourse to pea-nuts?’ asked Dora.

[069-121]‘Well, no. In the meantime I had written to someone in England, begging

[069-122]the loan of just enough money to enable me to get home. The money came a

[069-123]day after I had seen Sterling off by train.’

[069-124]An hour and a half quickly passed, and Jasper, who wished to have a few

[069-125]minutes of Marian’s company before it was time for her to go, cast a

[069-126]significant glance at his sisters. Dora said innocently:

[069-127]‘You wished me to tell you when it was half-past nine, Marian.’

[069-128]And Marian rose. This was a signal Whelpdale could not disregard.

[069-129]Immediately he made ready for his own departure, and in less than five

[069-130]minutes was gone, his face at the last moment expressing blended delight

[069-131]and pain.

[069-132]‘Too good of you to have asked me to come,’ he said with gratitude to

[069-133]Jasper, who went to the door with him. ‘You are a happy man, by Jove! A

[069-134]happy man!’

[069-135]When Jasper returned to the room his sisters had vanished. Marian

[069-136]stood by the fire. He drew near to her, took her hands, and repeated

[069-137]laughingly Whelpdale’s last words.

[069-138]‘Is it true?’ she asked.

[069-139]‘Tolerably true, I think.’

[069-140]‘Then I am as happy as you are.’

[069-141]He released her hands, and moved a little apart.

[069-142]‘Marian, I have been thinking about that letter to your father. I had

[069-143]better get it written, don’t you think?’

[069-144]She gazed at him with troubled eyes.

[069-145]‘Perhaps you had. Though we said it might be delayed until--’

[069-146]‘Yes, I know. But I suspect you had rather I didn’t wait any longer.

[069-147]Isn’t that the truth?’

[069-148]‘Partly. Do just as you wish, Jasper.’

[069-149]‘I’ll go and see him, if you like.’

[069-150]‘I am so afraid--No, writing will be better.’

[069-151]‘Very well. Then he shall have the letter to-morrow afternoon.’

[069-152]‘Don’t let it come before the last post. I had so much rather not.

[069-153]Manage it, if you can.’

[069-154]‘Very well. Now go and say good-night to the girls. It’s a vile night,

[069-155]and you must get home as soon as possible.’

[069-156]She turned away, but again came towards him, murmuring:

[069-157]‘Just a word or two more.’

[069-158]‘[[About]] the letter?’

[069-159]‘No. You haven’t said--’

[069-160]He laughed.

[069-161]‘And you couldn’t go away contentedly unless I repeated for the

[069-162]hundredth time that I love you?’

[069-163]Marian searched his countenance.

[069-164]‘Do you think it foolish? I live only on those words.’

[069-165]‘Well, they are better than pea-nuts.’

[069-166]‘Oh don’t! I can’t bear to--’

[069-167]Jasper was unable to understand that such a jest sounded to her like

[069-168]profanity. She hid her face against him, and whispered the words that

[069-169]would have enraptured her had they but come from his lips. The young man

[069-170]found it pleasant enough to be worshipped, but he could not reply as

[069-171]she desired. A few phrases of tenderness, and his love-vocabulary

[069-172]was exhausted; he even grew weary when something more--the indefinite

[069-173]something--was vaguely required of him.

[069-174]‘You are a dear, good, tender-hearted girl,’ he said, stroking her

[069-175]short, soft hair, which was exquisite to the hand. ‘Now go and get

[069-176]ready.’

[069-177]She left him, but stood for a few moments on the landing before going to

[069-178]the girls’ room.

[069-179]CHAPTER XXIX. CATASTROPHE

[069-180]Marian had finished the rough draft of a paper on James Harrington,

[069-181]author of ‘Oceana.’ Her father went through it by the midnight lamp,

[069-182]and the next morning made his comments. A black sky and sooty rain

[069-183]strengthened his inclination to sit by the study fire and talk at large

[069-184]in a tone of flattering benignity.
