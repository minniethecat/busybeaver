# Section 88

[087-001]His direction was westward; walking at a steady, purposeful pace, with

[087-002]cheery countenance and eyes that gave sign of pleasure as often as they

[087-003]turned to the sun-smitten clouds, he struck across Kensington Gardens,

[087-004]and then on towards Fulham, where he crossed the Thames to Putney. The

[087-005]sun was just setting; he paused a few moments on the bridge, watching

[087-006]the river with a quiet smile, and enjoying the splendour of the sky.

[087-007]Up Putney Hill he walked slowly; when he reached the top it was growing

[087-008]dark, but an unwonted effect in the atmosphere caused him to turn and

[087-009]look to the east. An exclamation escaped his lips, for there before him

[087-010]was the new-risen moon, a perfect globe, vast and red. He gazed at it

[087-011]for a long time.

[087-012]When the daylight had entirely passed, he went forward on to the heath,

[087-013]and rambled, as if idly, to a secluded part, where trees and bushes made

[087-014]a deep shadow under the full moon. It was still quite warm, and scarcely

[087-015]a breath of air moved among the reddening leaves.

[087-016]Sure at length that he was remote from all observation, he pressed into

[087-017]a little copse, and there reclined on the grass, leaning against the

[087-018]stem of a tree. The moon was now hidden from him, but by looking upward

[087-019]he could see its light upon a long, faint cloud, and the blue of the

[087-020]placid sky. His mood was one of ineffable peace. Only thoughts of

[087-021]beautiful things came into his mind; he had reverted to an earlier

[087-022]period of life, when as yet no mission of literary [[literary-realism|realism]] had been

[087-023]imposed upon him, and when his passions were still soothed by natural

[087-024]hope. The memory of his friend [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] was strongly present with him,

[087-025]but of [[amy-reardon|Amy]] he thought only as of that star which had just come into his

[087-026]vision above the edge of dark foliage--beautiful, but infinitely remote.

[087-027]Recalling Reardon’s voice, it brought to him those last words whispered

[087-028]by his dying companion. He remembered them now:

[087-029]We are such stuff

[087-030]As dreams are made on, and our little life

[087-031]Is rounded with a sleep.

[087-032]CHAPTER XXXVI. JASPER’S DELICATE CASE

[087-033]Only when he received Miss Rupert’s amiably-worded refusal to become his

[087-034]wife was [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] aware how firmly he had counted on her accepting him. He

[087-035]told [[dora-milvain|Dora]] with sincerity that his proposal was a piece of foolishness;

[087-036]so far from having any regard for Miss Rupert, he felt towards her with

[087-037]something of antipathy, and at the same time he was conscious of ardent

[087-038]emotions, if not love, for another woman who would be no bad match even

[087-039]from the commercial point of view. Yet so strong was the effect upon him

[087-040]of contemplating a large fortune, that, in despite of reason and desire,

[087-041]he lived in eager expectation of the word which should make him rich.

[087-042]And for several hours after his disappointment he could not overcome the

[087-043]impression of calamity.

[087-044]A part of that impression was due to the engagement which he must now

[087-045]fulfil. He had pledged his word to ask [[marian-yule|Marian]] to marry him without

[087-046]further delay. To shuffle out of this duty would make him too ignoble

[087-047]even in his own eyes. Its discharge meant, as he had expressed it, that

[087-048]he was ‘doomed’; he would deliberately be committing the very error

[087-049]always so flagrant to him in the case of other men who had crippled

[087-050]themselves by early [[literary-marriage|marriage]] with a penniless woman. But events had

[087-051]enmeshed him; circumstances had proved fatal. Because, in his salad

[087-052]days, he dallied with a girl who had indeed many charms, step by step

[087-053]he had come to the necessity of sacrificing his prospects to that raw

[087-054]attachment. And, to make it more irritating, this happened just when the

[087-055]way began to be much clearer before him.

[087-056]Unable to think of work, he left the house and wandered gloomily about

[087-057]Regent’s Park. For the first time in his recollection the confidence

[087-058]which was wont to inspirit him gave way to an attack of sullen

[087-059]discontent. He felt himself ill-used by destiny, and therefore by

[087-060]Marian, who was fate’s instrument. It was not in his nature that this

[087-061]mood should last long, but it revealed to him those darker possibilities

[087-062]which his egoism would develop if it came seriously into conflict with

[087-063]overmastering misfortune. A hope, a craven hope, insinuated itself into

[087-064]the cracks of his infirm resolve. He would not examine it, but conscious

[087-065]of its existence he was able to go home in somewhat better spirits.

[087-066]He wrote to Marian. If possible she was to meet him at half-past

[087-067]nine next morning at Gloucester Gate. He had reasons for wishing this

[087-068]interview to take place on neutral ground.

[087-069]Early in the afternoon, when he was trying to do some work, there

[087-070]arrived a letter which he opened with impatient hand; the writing was

[087-071]Mrs Reardon’s, and he could not guess what she had to communicate.

[087-072]‘DEAR MR MILVAIN,--I am distressed beyond measure to read in this

[087-073]morning’s newspaper that poor [[harold-biffen|Mr Biffen]] has put an end to his life.

[087-074]Doubtless you can obtain more details than are given in this bare report

[087-075]of the discovery of his body. Will you let me hear, or come and see me?’

[087-076]He read and was astonished. Absorbed in his own affairs, he had not

[087-077]opened the newspaper to-day; it lay folded on a chair. Hastily he ran

[087-078]his eye over the columns, and found at length a short paragraph which

[087-079]stated that the body of a man who had evidently committed suicide by

[087-080]taking poison had been found on Putney Heath; that papers in his pockets

[087-081]identified him as one Harold Biffen, lately resident in Goodge Street,

[087-082][[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]]; and that an inquest would be held, &c. He went

[087-083]to Dora’s room, and told her of the event, but without mentioning the

[087-084]letter which had brought it under his notice.

[087-085]‘I suppose there was no alternative between that and starvation. I

[087-086]scarcely thought of Biffen as likely to kill himself. If Reardon had

[087-087]done it, I shouldn’t have felt the least surprise.’

[087-088]‘[[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]] will be bringing us information, no doubt,’ said Dora,

[087-089]who, as she spoke, thought more of that gentleman’s visit than of the

[087-090]event that was to occasion it.

[087-091]‘Really, one can’t grieve. There seemed no possibility of his ever

[087-092]earning enough to live decently upon. But why the deuce did he go all

[087-093]the way out there? Consideration for the people in whose house he lived,

[087-094]I dare say; Biffen had a good deal of native delicacy.’

[087-095]Dora felt a secret wish that someone else possessed more of that

[087-096]desirable quality.

[087-097]Leaving her, Jasper made a rapid, though careful, toilet, and was

[087-098]presently on his way to Westbourne Park. It was his hope that he should

[087-099]reach Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s house before any ordinary afternoon caller could

[087-100]arrive; and so he did. He had not been here since that evening when he

[087-101]encountered Reardon on the road and heard his reproaches. To his great

[087-102]satisfaction, Amy was alone in the drawing-room; he held her hand a

[087-103]trifle longer than was necessary, and returned more earnestly the look

[087-104]of interest with which she regarded him.

[087-105]‘I was ignorant of this affair when your letter came,’ he began, ‘and I

[087-106]set out immediately to see you.’

[087-107]‘I hoped you would bring me some news. What can have driven the poor man

[087-108]to such extremity?’

[087-109]‘Poverty, I can only suppose. But I will see Whelpdale. I hadn’t come

[087-110]across Biffen for a long time.’

[087-111]‘Was he still so very poor?’ asked Amy, compassionately.

[087-112]‘I’m afraid so. His book failed utterly.’

[087-113]‘Oh, if I had imagined him still in such distress, surely I might have

[087-114]done something to help him!’--So often the regretful remark of one’s

[087-115]friends, when one has been permitted to perish.

[087-116]With Amy’s sorrow was mingled a suggestion of tenderness which came of

[087-117]her knowledge that the dead man had worshipped her. Perchance his death

[087-118]was in part attributable to that hopeless love.

[087-119]‘He sent me a copy of his novel,’ she said, ‘and I saw him once or twice

[087-120]after that. But he was much better dressed than in former days, and I

[087-121]thought--’

[087-122]Having this subject to converse upon put the two more quickly at ease

[087-123]than could otherwise have been the case. Jasper was closely observant

[087-124]of the young widow; her finished graces made a strong appeal to his

[087-125]admiration, and even in some degree awed him. He saw that her beauty had

[087-126]matured, and it was more distinctly than ever of the type to which he

[087-127]paid reverence. Amy might take a foremost place among brilliant women.

[087-128]At a dinner-table, in grand toilet, she would be superb; at polite

[087-129]receptions people would whisper: ‘Who is that?’

[087-130]Biffen fell out of the dialogue.

[087-131]‘It grieved me very much,’ said Amy, ‘to hear of the misfortune that

[087-132]befell my cousin.’

[087-133]‘The legacy affair? Why, yes, it was a pity. Especially now that her

[087-134]father is threatened with blindness.’

[087-135]‘Is it so serious? I heard indirectly that he had something the matter

[087-136]with his eyes, but I didn’t know--’

[087-137]‘They may be able to operate before long, and perhaps it will be

[087-138]successful. But in the meantime Marian has to do his work.’

[087-139]‘This explains the--the delay?’ fell from Amy’s lips, as she smiled.

[087-140]Jasper moved uncomfortably. It was a voluntary gesture.

[087-141]‘The whole situation explains it,’ he replied, with some show of

[087-142]impulsiveness. ‘I am very much afraid Marian is tied during her father’s

[087-143]life.’

[087-144]‘Indeed? But there is her mother.’

[087-145]‘No companion for her father, as I think you know. Even if Mr Yule

[087-146]recovers his sight, it is not at all likely that he will be able to work

[087-147]as before. Our difficulties are so grave that--’

[087-148]He paused, and let his hand fail despondently.

[087-149]‘I hope it isn’t affecting your work--your progress?’

[087-150]‘To some extent, necessarily. I have a good deal of will, you remember,

[087-151]and what I have set my mind upon, no doubt, I shall some day achieve.

[087-152]But--one makes mistakes.’

[087-153]There was silence.

[087-154]‘The last three years,’ he continued, ‘have made no slight difference

[087-155]in my position. Recall where I stood when you first knew me. I have done

[087-156]something since then, I think, and by my own steady effort.’

[087-157]‘Indeed, you have.’

[087-158]‘Just now I am in need of a little encouragement. You don’t notice any

[087-159]falling off in my work recently?’

[087-160]‘No, indeed.’

[087-161]‘Do you see my things in [[the-current|The Current]] and so on, generally?’

[087-162]‘I don’t think I miss many of your articles. Sometimes I believe I have

[087-163]detected you when there was no signature.’

[087-164]‘And Dora has been doing well. Her story in that girls’ paper has

[087-165]attracted attention. It’s a great deal to have my mind at rest about

[087-166]both the girls. But I can’t pretend to be in very good spirits.’ He

[087-167]rose. ‘Well, I must try to find out something more about poor Biffen.’

[087-168]‘Oh, you are not going yet, Mr Milvain?’

[087-169]‘Not, assuredly, because I wish to. But I have work to do.’ He stepped

[087-170]aside, but came back as if on an impulse. ‘May I ask you for your advice

[087-171]in a very delicate matter?’

[087-172]Amy was a little disturbed, but she collected herself and smiled in a

[087-173]way that reminded Jasper of his walk with her along [[gorer-street|Gower Street]].

[087-174]‘Let me hear what it is.’

[087-175]He sat down again, and bent forward.

[087-176]‘If Marian insists that it is her duty to remain with her father, am I

[087-177]justified or not in freely consenting to that?’

[087-178]‘I scarcely understand. Has Marian expressed a wish to devote herself in

[087-179]that way?’

[087-180]‘Not distinctly. But I suspect that her conscience points to it. I am in

[087-181]serious doubt. On the one hand,’ he explained in a tone of candour, ‘who

[087-182]will not blame me if our engagement terminates in circumstances such as

[087-183]these? On the other--you are aware, by-the-by, that her father objects

[087-184]in the strongest way to this marriage?’
