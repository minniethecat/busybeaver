# Literary Success

What does it mean to succeed as a writer? *New Grub Street* offers multiple answers — and they are mutually exclusive. Commercial success, critical recognition, artistic integrity: the novel tracks characters who pursue each of these and shows that the literary marketplace, as currently constituted, makes it impossible to achieve more than one.

## The Commercial Model

Jasper Milvain's career defines success in purely commercial terms: income, position, influence. He measures his progress toward "a thousand a year" (P02-204) with the precision of an accountant. His appointment as editor of a fashionable paper is the novel's clearest success story — and it comes at the cost of any literary ambition beyond marketability.

Milvain's success is not merely financial. He achieves social success — membership in the right circles, marriage to the right woman, the ability to patronize his former peers. The novel names him as its "successful man of letters" who "thinks first and foremost of the markets" (P02-185), and the description is not ironic. He is successful by the only metric that the market recognizes.

## The Critical Model

Alfred Yule represents the aspiration to critical recognition — to be respected by one's peers even if one is not wealthy. But Yule's career demonstrates the inadequacy of this model. Years of grinding literary labor have earned him neither wealth nor reputation. His bitterness — "A life of toil! A life of toil!" (055-018) — is the product of pursuing a kind of success that the market does not actually reward.

## The Artistic Model

Reardon and Biffen pursue artistic success — the creation of work that is true, original, and lasting. Both fail commercially. Reardon's *The Optimist* is a critical and commercial disappointment despite its literary merit. Biffen's commitment to "absolute realism in the sphere of the ignobly decent" (024-171) is artistically ambitious but earns him starvation, not recognition.

## Success as Contradiction

Gissing's most radical argument is that these three kinds of success are structurally incompatible under the conditions of the modern literary marketplace. The market rewards what sells, not what is good; critical reputation follows commercial success, not artistic merit; and the serious artist who refuses to commodify his work is punished with poverty and obscurity. There is no synthesis — only a choice, and every choice has a cost.
