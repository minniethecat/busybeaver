# Circulating Library

The circulating library was the economic engine of Victorian fiction publishing. For an annual subscription fee, readers could borrow one volume at a time — the model that made the expensive three-volume novel commercially viable. Without the circulating libraries, above all [[mudies-library|Mudie's]], the entire literary economy depicted in *New Grub Street* would have been impossible. The libraries bought new novels in bulk at a substantial discount, creating an artificial market where novels were written to fill three volumes regardless of what the story required.

## In New Grub Street

The novel treats the circulating library as a familiar feature of literary life — a fact of the trade rather than a subject of extended analysis. A rainy day drives one character "to the circulating library" (024-135), the casual mention indicating how thoroughly the institution was woven into the fabric of middle-class reading habits. Mudie's appears more prominently: characters live "near Mudie's," make "occasion to visit Mudie's," and a novelist is told to go to "Mudie's, and make inquiry if my novel has been asked for" (077-022).

The library's power over literary production was subtle but pervasive. Because libraries demanded three volumes, novels were padded; because libraries preferred "safe" content acceptable to middle-class family subscribers, experimental or morally ambiguous fiction faced an additional barrier. The characters of *New Grub Street* navigate these constraints with varying degrees of awareness — Milvain adapts instinctively, while Reardon resists and suffers.

## Decline

By the time Gissing wrote *New Grub Street* in 1891, the circulating-library system was already under pressure. The single-volume novel at six shillings would soon displace the three-decker, and Mudie's dominance would fade. The novel thus captures a literary marketplace at a moment of transition — the old system decaying but not yet dead, the new system emerging but not yet formed.
