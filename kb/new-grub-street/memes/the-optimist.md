# The Optimist

[[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]]'s second published novel, whose commercial failure marks the turning point in his literary career and precipitates the financial collapse of his household. The novel's poor reception embodies the harsh realities of the late-Victorian [[grub-street|literary marketplace]], where artistic merit offers no guarantee of sales.

## Overview

"The Optimist" followed [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]'s moderately [[literary-ambition|success]]ful debut "[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]]." Unlike its predecessor, the novel was "practically a failure" [P02-134]. [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]], with characteristic pragmatism, reflects that had he written "The Optimist" he "should have gone shrewdly" to work promoting it differently [P02-192]. The failure of this novel leaves [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] unable to secure favourable terms for future work and contributes directly to the deterioration of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]] to [[amy-reardon|Amy]].

## Reception and Aftermath

[[jasper-milvain|Jasper Milvain]]'s assessment is characteristically blunt. When [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] asks whether [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] thinks "The Optimist" weak, the question itself reveals [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]'s damaged confidence [012-024]. The novel's failure creates a lasting stigma: [[publishing-trade|publishers]] become reluctant to commission new work from [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], and [[amy-reardon|Amy]]'s faith in her husband's earning power erodes. After the failure, [[amy-reardon|Amy]] observes that "after the failure of 'The Optimist'; no, they wouldn't" — referring to the impossibility of returning to their former standard of living [013-079].

## Critical Context

Within the novel's literary ecosystem, "The Optimist" represents the precariousness of authorship as a profession. [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]'s artistic sincerity could not save the novel from commercial oblivion — a fate that validates [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]]'s cynical view that [[literary-success|literary success]] depends on marketing and social connections rather than artistic quality. The novel's title is bitterly ironic: [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]'s optimism about his literary future proves entirely unfounded.
