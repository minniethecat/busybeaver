# Section 8

[007-001]At the house door he inquired for Mr [[alfred-yule|Alfred Yule]]. That gentleman had

[007-002]gone in the carriage to [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]], half an hour ago, with his

[007-003]brother.

[007-004]‘[[marian-yule|Miss Yule]]?’

[007-005]Yes, she was within. [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] entered the sitting-room, waited a few

[007-006]moments, and Marian appeared. She wore a dress in which Milvain had

[007-007]not yet seen her, and it had the effect of making him regard her

[007-008]attentively. The smile with which she had come towards him passed from

[007-009]her face, which was perchance a little warmer of hue than commonly.

[007-010]‘I’m sorry your father is away, Miss Yule,’ Jasper began, in an animated

[007-011]voice. ‘I wanted to say good-bye to him. I return to [[london|London]] in a few

[007-012]hours.’

[007-013]‘You are going sooner than you intended?’

[007-014]‘Yes, I feel I mustn’t waste any more time. I think the country air is

[007-015]doing you good; you certainly look better than when I passed you that

[007-016]first day.’

[007-017]‘I feel better, much.’

[007-018]‘My sisters are anxious to see you again. I shouldn’t wonder if they

[007-019]come up this afternoon.’

[007-020]Marian had seated herself on the sofa, and her hands were linked upon

[007-021]her lap in the same way as when Jasper spoke with her here before, the

[007-022]palms downward. The beautiful outline of her bent head was relieved

[007-023]against a broad strip of sunlight on the wall behind her.

[007-024]‘They deplore,’ he continued in a moment, ‘that they should come to know

[007-025]you only to lose you again so soon.

[007-026]‘I have quite as much reason to be sorry,’ she answered, looking at him

[007-027]with the slightest possible smile. ‘But perhaps they will let me write

[007-028]to them, and hear from them now and then.’

[007-029]‘They would think it an honour. Country girls are not often invited to

[007-030]correspond with literary ladies in London.’

[007-031]He said it with as much jocoseness as civility allowed, then at once

[007-032]rose.

[007-033]‘Father will be very sorry,’ Marian began, with one quick glance towards

[007-034]the window and then another towards the door. ‘Perhaps he might possibly

[007-035]be able to see you before you go?’

[007-036]Jasper stood in hesitation. There was a look on the girl’s face which,

[007-037]under other circumstances, would have suggested a ready answer.

[007-038]‘I mean,’ she added, hastily, ‘he might just call, or even see you at

[007-039]the station?’

[007-040]‘Oh, I shouldn’t like to give Mr Yule any trouble. It’s my own fault,

[007-041]for deciding to go to-day. I shall leave by the 2.45.’

[007-042]He offered his hand.

[007-043]‘I shall look for your name in the magazines, Miss Yule.’

[007-044]‘Oh, I don’t think you will ever find it there.’

[007-045]He laughed incredulously, shook hands with her a second time, and strode

[007-046]out of the room, head erect--feeling proud of himself.

[007-047]When [[dora-milvain|Dora]] came home at dinner-time, he informed her of what he had done.

[007-048]‘A very interesting girl,’ he added impartially. ‘I advise you to make

[007-049]a friend of her. Who knows but you may live in London some day, and then

[007-050]she might be valuable--morally, I mean. For myself, I shall do my best

[007-051]not to see her again for a long time; she’s dangerous.’

[007-052]Jasper was unaccompanied when he went to the station. Whilst waiting on

[007-053]the platform, he suffered from apprehension lest Alfred Yule’s seamed

[007-054]visage should present itself; but no acquaintance approached him. Safe

[007-055]in the corner of his third-[[social-class|class]] carriage, he smiled at the last glimpse

[007-056]of the familiar fields, and began to think of something he had decided

[007-057]to write for [[the-west-end|The West End]].

[007-058]CHAPTER IV. AN AUTHOR AND HIS WIFE

[007-059]Eight flights of stairs, consisting alternately of eight and nine steps.

[007-060][[amy-reardon|Amy]] had made the calculation, and wondered what was the cause of this

[007-061]arrangement. The ascent was trying, but then no one could contest the

[007-062]respectability of the abode. In the flat immediately beneath resided a

[007-063]successful musician, whose carriage and pair came at a regular hour each

[007-064]afternoon to take him and his wife for a most respectable drive. In this

[007-065]special building no one else seemed at present to keep a carriage, but

[007-066]all the tenants were gentlefolk.

[007-067]And as to living up at the very top, why, there were distinct

[007-068]advantages--as so many people of moderate income are nowadays hastening

[007-069]to discover. The noise from the street was diminished at this height; no

[007-070]possible tramplers could establish themselves above your head; the air

[007-071]was bound to be purer than that of inferior strata; finally, one had

[007-072]the flat roof whereon to sit or expatiate in sunny weather. True that a

[007-073]gentle rain of soot was wont to interfere with one’s comfort out there

[007-074]in the open, but such minutiae are easily forgotten in the fervour of

[007-075]domestic description. It was undeniable that on a fine day one enjoyed

[007-076]extensive views. The green ridge from Hampstead to Highgate, with

[007-077]Primrose Hill and the foliage of Regent’s Park in the foreground; the

[007-078]suburban spaces of St John’s Wood, Maida Vale, Kilburn; Westminster

[007-079]Abbey and the Houses of Parliament, lying low by the side of the hidden

[007-080]river, and a glassy gleam on far-off hills which meant the Crystal

[007-081]Palace; then the clouded majesty of eastern London, crowned by St Paul’s

[007-082]dome. These things one’s friends were expected to admire. Sunset often

[007-083]afforded rich effects, but they were for solitary musing.

[007-084]A sitting-room, a bedroom, a kitchen. But the kitchen was called

[007-085]dining-room, or even parlour at need; for the cooking-range lent itself

[007-086]to concealment behind an ornamental screen, the walls displayed pictures

[007-087]and bookcases, and a tiny scullery which lay apart sufficed for the

[007-088]coarser domestic operations. This was Amy’s territory during the hours

[007-089]when her husband was working, or endeavouring to work. Of necessity,

[007-090][[edwin-reardon|Edwin Reardon]] used the front room as his study. His writing-table stood

[007-091]against the window; each wall had its shelves of serried literature;

[007-092]vases, busts, engravings (all of the inexpensive kind) served for

[007-093]ornaments.

[007-094]A maid-servant, recently emancipated from the Board school, came at

[007-095]half-past seven each morning, and remained until two o’clock, by which

[007-096]time the Reardons had dined; on special occasions, her services were

[007-097]enlisted for later hours. But it was Reardon’s habit to begin the

[007-098]serious work of the day at about three o’clock, and to continue with

[007-099]brief interruptions until ten or eleven; in many respects an awkward

[007-100]arrangement, but enforced by the man’s temperament and his [[literary-poverty|poverty]].

[007-101]One evening he sat at his desk with a slip of manuscript paper before

[007-102]him. It was the hour of sunset. His outlook was upon the backs of

[007-103]certain large houses skirting Regent’s Park, and lights had begun to

[007-104]show here and there in the windows: in one room a man was discoverable

[007-105]dressing for dinner, he had not thought it worth while to lower the

[007-106]blind; in another, some people were playing billiards. The higher

[007-107]windows reflected a rich glow from the western sky.

[007-108]For two or three hours Reardon had been seated in much the same

[007-109]attitude. Occasionally he dipped his pen into the ink and seemed about

[007-110]to write: but each time the effort was abortive. At the head of the

[007-111]paper was inscribed ‘Chapter III.,’ but that was all.

[007-112]And now the sky was dusking over; darkness would soon fall.

[007-113]He looked something older than his years, which were two-and-thirty; on

[007-114]his face was the pallor of mental suffering. Often he fell into a fit

[007-115]of absence, and gazed at vacancy with wide, miserable eyes. Returning

[007-116]to consciousness, he fidgeted nervously on his chair, dipped his pen

[007-117]for the hundredth time, bent forward in feverish determination to work.

[007-118]Useless; he scarcely knew what he wished to put into words, and his

[007-119]brain refused to construct the simplest sentence.

[007-120]The colours faded from the sky, and night came quickly. Reardon threw

[007-121]his arms upon the desk, let his head fall forward, and remained so, as

[007-122]if asleep.

[007-123]Presently the door opened, and a young, clear voice made inquiry:

[007-124]‘Don’t you want the lamp, Edwin?’

[007-125]The man roused himself, turned his chair a little, and looked towards

[007-126]the open door.

[007-127]‘Come here, Amy.’

[007-128]His wife approached. It was not quite dark in the room, for a glimmer

[007-129]came from the opposite houses.

[007-130]‘What’s the matter? Can’t you do anything?’

[007-131]‘I haven’t written a word to-day. At this rate, one goes crazy. Come and

[007-132]sit by me a minute, dearest.’

[007-133]‘I’ll get the lamp.’

[007-134]‘No; come and talk to me; we can understand each other better.’

[007-135]‘Nonsense; you have such morbid ideas. I can’t bear to sit in the

[007-136]gloom.’

[007-137]At once she went away, and quickly reappeared with a reading-lamp, which

[007-138]she placed on the square table in the middle of the room.

[007-139]‘Draw down the blind, Edwin.’

[007-140]She was a slender girl, but not very tall; her shoulders seemed rather

[007-141]broad in proportion to her waist and the part of her figure below it.

[007-142]The hue of her hair was ruddy gold; loosely arranged tresses made a

[007-143]superb crown to the beauty of her small, refined head. Yet the face

[007-144]was not of distinctly feminine type; with short hair and appropriate

[007-145]clothing, she would have passed unquestioned as a handsome boy of

[007-146]seventeen, a spirited boy too, and one much in the habit of giving

[007-147]orders to inferiors. Her nose would have been perfect but for ever so

[007-148]slight a crook which made it preferable to view her in full face than in

[007-149]profile; her lips curved sharply out, and when she straightened them of

[007-150]a sudden, the effect was not reassuring to anyone who had counted upon

[007-151]her for facile humour. In harmony with the broad shoulders, she had a

[007-152]strong neck; as she bore the lamp into the room a slight turn of

[007-153]her head showed splendid muscles from the ear downward. It was a

[007-154]magnificently clear-cut bust; one thought, in looking at her, of the

[007-155]newly-finished head which some honest sculptor has wrought with his own

[007-156]hand from the marble block; there was a suggestion of ‘planes’ and of

[007-157]the chisel. The atmosphere was cold; ruddiness would have been quite

[007-158]out of place on her cheeks, and a flush must have been the rarest thing

[007-159]there.

[007-160]Her age was not quite two-and-twenty; she had been wedded nearly two

[007-161]years, and had a child ten months old.

[007-162]As for her dress, it was unpretending in fashion and colour, but

[007-163]of admirable fit. Every detail of her appearance denoted scrupulous

[007-164]personal refinement. She walked well; you saw that the foot, however

[007-165]gently, was firmly planted. When she seated herself her posture was

[007-166]instantly graceful, and that of one who is indifferent about support for

[007-167]the back.

[007-168]‘What is the matter?’ she began. ‘Why can’t you get on with the story?’

[007-169]It was the tone of friendly remonstrance, not exactly of affection, not

[007-170]at all of tender solicitude.

[007-171]Reardon had risen and wished to approach her, but could not do so

[007-172]directly. He moved to another part of the room, then came round to the

[007-173]back of her chair, and bent his face upon her shoulder.

[007-174]‘Amy--’

[007-175]‘Well.’

[007-176]‘I think it’s all over with me. I don’t think I shall write any more.’

[007-177]‘Don’t be so foolish, dear. What is to prevent your writing?’

[007-178]‘Perhaps I am only out of sorts. But I begin to be horribly afraid.

[007-179]My will seems to be fatally weakened. I can’t see my way to the end of

[007-180]anything; if I get hold of an idea which seems good, all the sap has

[007-181]gone out of it before I have got it into working shape. In these last

[007-182]few months, I must have begun a dozen different books; I have been

[007-183]ashamed to tell you of each new beginning. I write twenty pages,

[007-184]perhaps, and then my courage fails. I am disgusted with the thing, and

[007-185]can’t go on with it--can’t! My fingers refuse to hold the pen. In mere

[007-186]writing, I have done enough to make much more than three volumes; but

[007-187]it’s all destroyed.’

[007-188]‘Because of your morbid conscientiousness. There was no need to destroy

[007-189]what you had written. It was all good enough for the market.’
