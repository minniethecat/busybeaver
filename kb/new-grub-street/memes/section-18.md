# Section 18

[017-001]He has a kind of cleverness, may do something; but there’s no being sure

[017-002]of that.’

[017-003]These thoughts were not coming into his mind for the first time. On the

[017-004]occasion when he met [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] and [[marian-yule|Marian]] together in the country road he

[017-005]had necessarily reflected upon the possibilities of such intercourse,

[017-006]and with the issue that he did not care to give any particular

[017-007]encouragement to its continuance. He of course heard of Milvain’s

[017-008]leave-taking call, and he purposely refrained from seeing the young man

[017-009]after that. The matter took no very clear shape in his meditations; he

[017-010]saw no likelihood that either of the young people would think much of

[017-011]the other after their parting, and time enough to trouble one’s head

[017-012]with such subjects when they could no longer be postponed. It would

[017-013]not have been pleasant to him to foresee a life of spinsterhood for his

[017-014]daughter; but she was young, and--she was a valuable assistant.

[017-015]How far did that latter consideration weigh with him? He put the

[017-016]question pretty distinctly to himself now that his wife had broached

[017-017]the matter thus unexpectedly. Was he prepared to behave with deliberate

[017-018]selfishness? Never yet had any conflict been manifested between his

[017-019]interests and Marian’s; practically he was in the habit of counting upon

[017-020]her aid for an indefinite period.

[017-021]If indeed he became editor of [[the-study|The Study]], why, in that case her

[017-022]assistance would be less needful. And indeed it seemed probable that

[017-023]young Milvain had a future before him.

[017-024]‘But, in any case,’ he said aloud, partly continuing his thoughts,

[017-025]partly replying to a look of disappointment on his wife’s face, ‘how do

[017-026]you know that he has any wish to come and see Marian?’

[017-027]‘I don’t know anything about it, of course.’

[017-028]‘And you may have made a mistake about her. What made you think she--had

[017-029]him in mind?’

[017-030]‘Well, it was her way of speaking, you know. And then, she asked if you

[017-031]had got a dislike to him.’

[017-032]‘She did? H’m! Well, I don’t think Milvain is any good to Marian. He’s

[017-033]just the kind of man to make himself agreeable to a girl for the fun of

[017-034]the thing.’

[017-035]Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] looked alarmed.

[017-036]‘Oh, if you really think that, don’t let him come. I wouldn’t for

[017-037]anything.’

[017-038]‘I don’t say it for certain.’ He took a sip of his coffee. ‘I have had

[017-039]no opportunity of observing him with much attention. But he’s not the

[017-040]kind of man I care for.’

[017-041]‘Then no doubt it’s better as it is.’

[017-042]‘Yes. I don’t see that anything could be done now. We shall see whether

[017-043]he gets on. I advise you not to mention him to her.’

[017-044]‘Oh no, I won’t.’

[017-045]She moved as if to go away, but her heart had been made uneasy by that

[017-046]short conversation which followed on Marian’s reading the letter, and

[017-047]there were still things she wished to put into words.

[017-048]‘If those young ladies go on writing to her, I dare say they’ll often

[017-049]speak about their brother.’

[017-050]‘Yes, it’s rather unfortunate.’

[017-051]‘And you know, Alfred, he may have asked them to do it.’

[017-052]‘I suppose there’s one subject on which all women can be subtle,’

[017-053]muttered Yule, smiling. The remark was not a kind one, but he did not

[017-054]make it worse by his tone.

[017-055]The listener failed to understand him, and looked with her familiar

[017-056]expression of mental effort.

[017-057]‘We can’t help that,’ he added, with reference to her suggestion. ‘If

[017-058]he has any serious thoughts, well, let him go on and wait for

[017-059]opportunities.’

[017-060]‘It’s a great pity, isn’t it, that she can’t see more people--of the

[017-061]right kind?’

[017-062]‘No use talking about it. Things are as they are. I can’t see that her

[017-063]life is unhappy.’

[017-064]‘It isn’t very happy.’

[017-065]‘You think not?’

[017-066]‘I’m sure it isn’t.’

[017-067]‘If I get The Study things may be different. Though--But it’s no use

[017-068]talking about what can’t be helped. Now don’t you go encouraging her

[017-069]to think herself lonely, and so on. It’s best for her to keep close to

[017-070]work, I’m sure of that.’

[017-071]‘Perhaps it is.’

[017-072]‘I’ll think it over.’

[017-073]Mrs Yule silently left the room, and went back to her sewing.

[017-074]She had understood that ‘Though--’ and the ‘what can’t be helped.’ Such

[017-075]allusions reminded her of a time unhappier than the present, when she

[017-076]had been wont to hear plainer language. She knew too well that, had she

[017-077]been a woman of education, her daughter would not now be suffering from

[017-078]loneliness.

[017-079]It was her own choice that she did not go with her husband and Marian to

[017-080][[john-yule|John Yule]]’s. She made an excuse that the house could not be left to

[017-081]one servant; but in any case she would have remained at home, for her

[017-082]presence must needs be an embarrassment both to father and daughter.

[017-083]Alfred was always ashamed of her before strangers; he could not conceal

[017-084]his feeling, either from her or from other people who had reason for

[017-085]observing him. Marian was not perhaps ashamed, but such companionship

[017-086]put restraint upon her freedom. And would it not always be the same?

[017-087]Supposing Mr Milvain were to come to this house, would it not repel him

[017-088]when he found what sort of person Marian’s mother was?

[017-089]She shed a few tears over her needlework.

[017-090]At midnight the study door opened. Yule came to the dining-room to see

[017-091]that all was right, and it surprised him to find his wife still sitting

[017-092]there.

[017-093]‘Why are you so late?’

[017-094]‘I’ve forgot the time.’

[017-095]‘Forgotten, forgotten. Don’t go back to that kind of language again.

[017-096]Come, put the light out.’

[017-097]PART TWO

[017-098]CHAPTER VIII. TO THE WINNING SIDE

[017-099]Of the acquaintances Yule had retained from his earlier years several

[017-100]were in the well-defined category of men with unpresentable wives. There

[017-101]was [[hinks|Hinks]], for instance, whom, though in anger he spoke of him as a

[017-102]bore, Alfred held in some genuine regard. Hinks made perhaps a hundred a

[017-103]year out of a kind of writing which only certain [[publishing-trade|publishers]] can get rid

[017-104]of and of this income he spent about a third on books. His wife was the

[017-105]daughter of a laundress, in whose house he had lodged thirty years ago,

[017-106]when new to [[london|London]] but already long-acquainted with hunger; they lived

[017-107]in complete harmony, but Mrs Hinks, who was four years the elder, still

[017-108]spoke the laundress tongue, unmitigated and immitigable. Another pair

[017-109]were Mr and Mrs Gorbutt. In this case there were no narrow circumstances

[017-110]to contend with, for the wife, originally a nursemaid, not long after

[017-111]her [[literary-marriage|marriage]] inherited house property from a relative. Mr Gorbutt deemed

[017-112]himself a poet; since his accession to an income he had published, at

[017-113]his own expense, a yearly volume of verses; the only result being to

[017-114]keep alive rancour in his wife, who was both parsimonious and vain.

[017-115]Making no secret of it, Mrs Gorbutt rued the day on which she had wedded

[017-116]a man of letters, when by waiting so short a time she would have been

[017-117]enabled to aim at a prosperous tradesman, who kept his gig and had

[017-118]everything handsome about him. Mrs Yule suspected, not without reason,

[017-119]that this lady had an inclination to strong liquors. Thirdly came Mr

[017-120]and Mrs Christopherson, who were poor as church mice. Even in a friend’s

[017-121]house they wrangled incessantly, and made tragi-comical revelations

[017-122]of their home life. The husband worked casually at irresponsible

[017-123][[journalism-trade|journalism]], but his chosen study was metaphysics; for many years he had

[017-124]had a huge and profound book on hand, which he believed would bring him

[017-125]fame, though he was not so unsettled in mind as to hope for anything

[017-126]else. When an article or two had earned enough [[money-in-literature|money]] for immediate

[017-127]necessities he went off to the [[british-museum|British Museum]], and then the difficulty

[017-128]was to recall him to profitable exertions. Yet husband and wife had an

[017-129]affection for each other. Mrs Christopherson came from Camberwell,

[017-130]where her father, once upon a time, was the smallest of small butchers.

[017-131]Disagreeable stories were whispered concerning her earlier life, and

[017-132]probably the metaphysician did not care to look back in that direction.

[017-133]They had had three children; all were happily buried.

[017-134]These men were capable of better things than they had done or would ever

[017-135]do; in each case their failure to fulfil youthful promise was largely

[017-136]explained by the unpresentable wife. They should have waited; they might

[017-137]have married a social equal at something between fifty and sixty.

[017-138]Another old friend was Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]]. Unwedded he, and perpetually exultant

[017-139]over men who, as he phrased it, had noosed themselves. He made a fair

[017-140]living, but, like Dr Johnson, had no passion for clean linen.

[017-141]Yule was not disdainful of these old companions, and the fact that

[017-142]all had a habit of looking up to him increased his pleasure in their

[017-143]occasional society. If, as happened once or twice in half a year,

[017-144]several of them were gathered together at his house, he tasted a sham

[017-145]kind of social and intellectual authority which he could not help

[017-146]relishing. On such occasions he threw off his habitual gloom and talked

[017-147]vigorously, making natural display of his learning and critical ability.

[017-148]The topic, sooner or later, was that which is inevitable in such a

[017-149]circle--the demerits, the pretentiousness, the personal weaknesses of

[017-150]prominent contemporaries in the world of letters. Then did the room ring

[017-151]with scornful laughter, with boisterous satire, with shouted irony,

[017-152]with fierce invective. After an evening of that kind Yule was unwell and

[017-153]miserable for several days.

[017-154]It was not to be expected that Mr Quarmby, inveterate chatterbox of the

[017-155]Reading-room and other resorts, should keep silence concerning what he

[017-156]had heard of Mr Rackett’s intentions. The rumour soon spread that

[017-157]Alfred Yule was to succeed [[fadge|Fadge]] in the direction of The Study, with the

[017-158]necessary consequence that Yule found himself an object of affectionate

[017-159]interest to a great many people of whom he knew little or nothing. At

[017-160]the same time the genuine old friends pressed warmly about him, with

[017-161]congratulations, with hints of their sincere readiness to assist in

[017-162]filling the columns of the paper. All this was not disagreeable, but in

[017-163]the meantime Yule had heard nothing whatever from Mr Rackett himself and

[017-164]his doubts did not diminish as week after week went by.

[017-165]The event justified him. At the end of October appeared an authoritative

[017-166]announcement that Fadge’s successor would be--not Alfred Yule, but a

[017-167]gentleman who till of late had been quietly working as a sub-editor in

[017-168]the provinces, and who had neither friendships nor enmities among the

[017-169]people of the London literary press. A young man, comparatively fresh

[017-170]from the university, and said to be strong in pure scholarship. The

[017-171]choice, as you are aware, proved a good one, and The Study became an

[017-172]organ of more repute than ever.

[017-173]Yule had been secretly conscious that it was not to men such as he that

[017-174]positions of this kind are nowadays entrusted. He tried to persuade

[017-175]himself that he was not disappointed. But when Mr Quarmby approached him

[017-176]with blank face, he spoke certain wrathful words which long rankled in

[017-177]that worthy’s mind. At home he kept sullen silence.

[017-178]No, not to such men as he--poor, and without social recommendations.

[017-179]Besides, he was growing too old. In literature, as in most other

[017-180]pursuits, the press of energetic young men was making it very hard for

[017-181]a veteran even to hold the little grazing-plot he had won by hard

[017-182]fighting. Still, Quarmby’s story had not been without foundation; it was

[017-183]true that the proprietor of The Study had for a moment thought of Alfred

[017-184]Yule, doubtless as the natural contrast to Clement Fadge, whom he would

[017-185]have liked to mortify if the thing were possible. But counsellors had

[017-186]proved to Mr Rackett the disadvantages of such a choice.

[017-187]Mrs Yule and her daughter foresaw but too well the results of this

[017-188]disappointment, notwithstanding that Alfred announced it to them with

[017-189]dry indifference. The month that followed was a time of misery for all

[017-190]in the house. Day after day Yule sat at his meals in sullen muteness; to

[017-191]his wife he scarcely spoke at all, and his conversation with Marian did

[017-192]not go beyond necessary questions and remarks on topics of business.

[017-193]His face became so strange a colour that one would have thought him

[017-194]suffering from an attack of jaundice; bilious headaches exasperated his

[017-195]savage mood. Mrs Yule knew from long experience how worse than useless

[017-196]it was for her to attempt consolation; in silence was her only safety.

[017-197]Nor did Marian venture to speak directly of what had happened. But

[017-198]one evening, when she had been engaged in the study and was now saying

[017-199]‘Good-night,’ she laid her cheek against her father’s, an unwonted

[017-200]caress which had a strange effect upon him. The expression of sympathy

[017-201]caused his thoughts to reveal themselves as they never yet had done

[017-202]before his daughter.
