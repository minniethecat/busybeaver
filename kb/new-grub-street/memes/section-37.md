# Section 37

[036-001]The would-be sensational story which was now in Mr [[jedwood|Jedwood]]’s hands had

[036-002]perhaps more merit than ‘[[margaret-home|Margaret Home]]’; its brevity, and the fact that

[036-003]nothing more was aimed at than a concatenation of brisk events, made it

[036-004]not unreadable. But [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] thought of it with humiliation. If it

[036-005]were published as his next work it would afford final proof to such

[036-006]sympathetic readers as he might still retain that he had hopelessly

[036-007]written himself out, and was now endeavouring to adapt himself to an

[036-008]inferior public. In spite of his dire necessities he now and then hoped

[036-009]that Jedwood might refuse the thing.

[036-010]At moments he looked with sanguine eagerness to the three or four months

[036-011]he was about to spend in retirement, but such impulses were the mere

[036-012]outcome of his nervous disease. He had no faith in himself under

[036-013]present conditions; the permanence of his sufferings would mean the sure

[036-014]destruction of powers he still possessed, though they were not at

[036-015]his command. Yet he believed that his mind was made up as to the

[036-016]advisability of trying this last resource; he was impatient for the day

[036-017]of departure, and in the interval merely killed time as best he might.

[036-018]He could not read, and did not attempt to gather ideas for his next

[036-019]book; the delusion that his mind was resting made an excuse to him for

[036-020]the barrenness of day after day. His ‘Pliny’ article had been despatched

[036-021]to [[the-wayside|The Wayside]], and would possibly be accepted. But he did not trouble

[036-022]himself about this or other details; it was as though his mind could do

[036-023]nothing more than grasp the bald fact of impending destitution; with the

[036-024]steps towards that final stage he seemed to have little concern.

[036-025]One evening he set forth to make a call upon [[harold-biffen|Harold Biffen]], whom he had

[036-026]not seen since the realist called to acknowledge the receipt of a copy

[036-027]of ‘Margaret Home’ left at his lodgings when he was out. Biffen resided

[036-028]in Clipstone Street, a thoroughfare discoverable in the dim district

[036-029]which lies between Portland Place and [[tottenham-court-road|Tottenham Court Road]]. On knocking

[036-030]at the door of the lodging-house, Reardon learnt that his friend was at

[036-031]home. He ascended to the third storey and tapped at a door which allowed

[036-032]rays of lamplight to issue from great gaps above and below. A sound of

[036-033]voices came from within, and on entering he perceived that Biffen was

[036-034]engaged with a pupil.

[036-035]‘They didn’t tell me you had a visitor,’ he said. ‘I’ll call again

[036-036]later.’

[036-037]‘No need to go away,’ replied Biffen, coming forward to shake hands.

[036-038]‘Take a book for a few minutes. Mr Baker won’t mind.’

[036-039]It was a very small room, with a ceiling so low that the tall lodger

[036-040]could only just stand upright with safety; perhaps three inches

[036-041]intervened between his head and the plaster, which was cracked, grimy,

[036-042]cobwebby. A small scrap of weedy carpet lay in front of the fireplace;

[036-043]elsewhere the chinky boards were unconcealed. The furniture consisted of

[036-044]a round table, which kept such imperfect balance on its central support

[036-045]that the lamp entrusted to it looked in a dangerous position, of three

[036-046]small cane-bottomed chairs, a small wash-hand-stand with sundry rude

[036-047]appurtenances, and a chair-bedstead which the tenant opened at the hour

[036-048]of repose and spread with certain primitive trappings at present kept

[036-049]in a cupboard. There was no bookcase, but a few hundred battered volumes

[036-050]were arranged some on the floor and some on a rough chest. The weather

[036-051]was too characteristic of an English spring to make an empty grate

[036-052]agreeable to the eye, but Biffen held it an axiom that fires were

[036-053]unseasonable after the first of May.

[036-054]The individual referred to as Mr Baker, who sat at the table in the

[036-055]attitude of a student, was a robust, hard-featured, black-haired young

[036-056]man of two-or three-and-twenty; judging from his weather-beaten cheeks

[036-057]and huge hands, as well as from the garb he wore, one would have

[036-058]presumed that study was not his normal occupation. There was something

[036-059]of the riverside about him; he might be a dockman, or even a bargeman.

[036-060]He looked intelligent, however, and bore himself with much modesty.

[036-061]‘Now do endeavour to write in shorter sentences,’ said Biffen, who sat

[036-062]down by him and resumed the lesson, Reardon having taken up a volume.

[036-063]‘This isn’t bad--it isn’t bad at all, I assure you; but you have put all

[036-064]you had to say into three appalling periods, whereas you ought to have

[036-065]made about a dozen.’

[036-066]‘There it is, sir; there it is!’ exclaimed the man, smoothing his wiry

[036-067]hair. ‘I can’t break it up. The thoughts come in a lump, if I may say

[036-068]so. To break it up--there’s the art of compersition.’

[036-069]Reardon could not refrain from a glance at the speaker, and Biffen,

[036-070]whose manner was very grave and kindly, turned to his friend with an

[036-071]explanation of the difficulties with which the student was struggling.

[036-072]‘Mr Baker is preparing for the examination of the outdoor Customs

[036-073]Department. One of the subjects is English composition, and really, you

[036-074]know, that isn’t quite such a simple matter as some people think.’

[036-075]Baker beamed upon the visitor with a homely, good-natured smile.

[036-076]‘I can make headway with the other things, sir,’ he said, striking the

[036-077]table lightly with his clenched fist. ‘There’s handwriting, there’s

[036-078]orthography, there’s arithmetic; I’m not afraid of one of ‘em, as Mr

[036-079]Biffen’ll tell you, sir. But when it comes to compersition, that brings

[036-080]out the sweat on my forehead, I do assure you.

[036-081]‘You’re not the only man in that case, Mr Baker,’ replied Reardon.

[036-082]‘It’s thought a tough job in general, is it, sir?’

[036-083]‘It is indeed.’

[036-084]‘Two hundred marks for compersition,’ continued the man. ‘Now how many

[036-085]would they have given me for this bit of a try, Mr Biffen?’

[036-086]‘Well, well; I can’t exactly say. But you improve; you improve,

[036-087]decidedly. Peg away for another week or two.’

[036-088]‘Oh, don’t fear me, sir! I’m not easily beaten when I’ve set my mind on

[036-089]a thing, and I’ll break up the compersition yet, see if I don’t!’

[036-090]Again his fist descended upon the table in a way that reminded one of

[036-091]the steam-hammer cracking a nut.

[036-092]The lesson proceeded for about ten minutes, Reardon, under pretence of

[036-093]reading, following it with as much amusement as anything could excite

[036-094]in him nowadays. At length Mr Baker stood up, collected his papers and

[036-095]books, and seemed about to depart; but, after certain uneasy movements

[036-096]and glances, he said to Biffen in a subdued voice:

[036-097]‘Perhaps I might speak to you outside the door a minute, sir?’

[036-098]He and the teacher went out, the door closed, and Reardon heard sounds

[036-099]of muffled conversation. In a minute or two a heavy footstep descended

[036-100]the stairs, and Biffen re-entered the room.

[036-101]‘Now that’s a good, honest fellow,’ he said, in an amused tone. ‘It’s

[036-102]my pay-night, but he didn’t like to fork out [[money-in-literature|money]] before you. A very

[036-103]unusual delicacy in a man of that standing. He pays me sixpence for an

[036-104]hour’s lesson; that brings me two shillings a week. I sometimes feel a

[036-105]little ashamed to take his money, but then the fact is he’s a good deal

[036-106]better off than I am.’

[036-107]‘Will he get a place in the Customs, do you think?’

[036-108]‘Oh, I’ve no doubt of it. If it seemed unlikely, I should have told him

[036-109]so before this. To be sure, that’s a point I have often to consider,

[036-110]and once or twice my delicacy has asserted itself at the expense of my

[036-111]pocket. There was a poor consumptive lad came to me not long ago and

[036-112]wanted Latin lessons; talked about going in for the [[london|London]] Matric., on

[036-113]his way to the pulpit. I couldn’t stand it. After a lesson or two I told

[036-114]him his cough was too bad, and he had no right to study until he got

[036-115]into better health; that was better, I think, than saying plainly he had

[036-116]no chance on earth. But the food I bought with his money was choking me.

[036-117]Oh yes, Baker will make his way right enough. A good, modest fellow.

[036-118]You noticed how respectfully he spoke to me? It doesn’t make any

[036-119]difference to him that I live in a garret like this; I’m a man of

[036-120]education, and he can separate this fact from my surroundings.’

[036-121]‘Biffen, why don’t you get some decent position? Surely you might.’

[036-122]‘What position? No school would take me; I have neither credentials

[036-123]nor conventional clothing. For the same reason I couldn’t get a private

[036-124]tutorship in a rich family. No, no; it’s all right. I keep myself alive,

[036-125]and I get on with my work.--By-the-bye, I’ve decided to write a book

[036-126]called “[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer.”’

[036-127]‘What’s the idea?’

[036-128]‘An objectionable word, that. Better say: “What’s the reality?” Well, Mr

[036-129]Bailey is a grocer in a little street by here. I have dealt with him

[036-130]for a long time, and as he’s a talkative fellow I’ve come to know a good

[036-131]deal about him and his history. He’s fond of talking about the struggle

[036-132]he had in his first year of business. He had no money of his own, but

[036-133]he married a woman who had saved forty-five pounds out of a cat’s-meat

[036-134]business. You should see that woman! A big, coarse, squinting creature;

[036-135]at the time of the [[literary-marriage|marriage]] she was a widow and forty-two years old.

[036-136]Now I’m going to tell the true story of Mr Bailey’s marriage and of his

[036-137]progress as a grocer. It’ll be a great book--a great book!’

[036-138]He walked up and down the room, fervid with his conception.

[036-139]‘There’ll be nothing bestial in it, you know. The decently ignoble--as

[036-140]I’ve so often said. The thing’ll take me a year at least. I shall do

[036-141]it slowly, lovingly. One volume, of course; the length of the ordinary

[036-142]French novel. There’s something fine in the title, don’t you think? “Mr

[036-143]Bailey, Grocer”!’

[036-144]‘I envy you, old fellow,’ said Reardon, sighing. ‘You have the right

[036-145]fire in you; you have zeal and energy. Well, what do you think I have

[036-146]decided to do?’

[036-147]‘I should like to hear.’

[036-148]Reardon gave an account of his project. The other listened gravely,

[036-149]seated across a chair with his arms on the back.

[036-150]‘Your wife is in agreement with this?’

[036-151]‘Oh yes.’ He could not bring himself to say that [[amy-reardon|Amy]] had suggested it.

[036-152]‘She has great hopes that the change will be just what I need.’

[036-153]‘I should say so too--if you were going to rest. But if you have to set

[036-154]to work at once it seems to me very doubtful.’

[036-155]‘Never mind. For Heaven’s sake don’t discourage me! If this fails I

[036-156]think--upon my soul, I think I shall kill myself.’

[036-157]‘Pooh!’ exclaimed Biffen, gently. ‘With a wife like yours?’

[036-158]‘Just because of that.’

[036-159]‘No, no; there’ll be some way out of it. By-the-bye, I passed Mrs

[036-160]Reardon this morning, but she didn’t see me. It was in Tottenham Court

[036-161]Road, and [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]] was with her. I felt myself too seedy in appearance to

[036-162]stop and speak.’

[036-163]‘In Tottenham Court Road?’

[036-164]That was not the detail of the story which chiefly held Reardon’s

[036-165]attention, yet he did not purposely make a misleading remark. His mind

[036-166]involuntarily played this trick.

[036-167]‘I only saw them just as they were passing,’ pursued Biffen. ‘Oh, I knew

[036-168]I had something to tell you! Have you heard that [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]] is going to

[036-169]be married?’

[036-170]Reardon shook his head in a preoccupied way.

[036-171]‘I had a note from him this morning, telling me. He asked me to look him

[036-172]up to-night, and he’d let me know all about it. Let’s go together, shall

[036-173]we?’

[036-174]‘I don’t feel much in the humour for Whelpdale. I’ll walk with you, and

[036-175]go on home.’

[036-176]‘No, no; come and see him. It’ll do you good to talk a little.--But I

[036-177]must positively eat a mouthful before we go. I’m afraid you won’t care

[036-178]to join?’
