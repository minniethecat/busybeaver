# Section 47

[046-001]‘Any company with her?’

[046-002]‘A lady--Mrs [[carter|Carter]].’

[046-003]‘Then please to give my name, and ask if Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] can see me.’

[046-004]He was speedily conducted to the drawing-room, where he found the lady

[046-005]of the house, her son, and Mrs Carter. For [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]] his eye sought in

[046-006]vain.

[046-007]‘I’m so glad you have come,’ said Mrs Yule, in a confidential tone. ‘I

[046-008]have been wishing to see you. Of course, you know of our sad trouble?’

[046-009]‘I have heard of it only to-day.’

[046-010]‘From [[edwin-reardon|Mr Reardon]] himself?’

[046-011]‘No; I haven’t seen him.’

[046-012]‘I do wish you had! We should have been so anxious to know how he

[046-013]impressed you.’

[046-014]‘How he impressed me?’

[046-015]‘My mother has got hold of the notion,’ put in [[john-yule|John Yule]], ‘that he’s not

[046-016]exactly compos mentis. I’ll admit that he went on in a queer sort of way

[046-017]the last time I saw him.’

[046-018]‘And my husband thinks he is rather strange,’ remarked Mrs Carter.

[046-019]‘He has gone back to the hospital, I understand--’

[046-020]‘To a new branch that has just been opened in the City Road,’ replied

[046-021]Mrs Yule. ‘And he’s living in a dreadful place--one of the most shocking

[046-022]alleys in the worst part of [[islington|Islington]]. I should have gone to see him,

[046-023]but I really feel afraid; they give me such an account of the place.

[046-024]And everyone agrees that he has such a very wild look, and speaks so

[046-025]strangely.’

[046-026]‘Between ourselves,’ said John, ‘there’s no use in exaggerating. He’s

[046-027]living in a vile hole, that’s true, and Carter says he looks miserably

[046-028]ill, but of course he may be as sane as we are.

[046-029][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] listened to all this with no small astonishment.

[046-030]‘And Mrs Reardon?’ he asked.

[046-031]‘I’m sorry to say she is far from well,’ replied Mrs Yule. ‘To-day she

[046-032]has been obliged to keep her room. You can imagine what a shock it has

[046-033]been to her. It came with such extraordinary suddenness. Without a word

[046-034]of warning, her husband announced that he had taken a clerkship and was

[046-035]going to remove immediately to the East-end. Fancy! And this when he had

[046-036]already arranged, as you know, to go to the South Coast and write his

[046-037]next book under the influences of the sea air. He was anything but well;

[046-038]we all knew that, and we had all joined in advising him to spend the

[046-039]summer at the seaside. It seemed better that he should go alone; Mrs

[046-040]Reardon would, of course, have gone down for a few days now and then.

[046-041]And at a moment’s notice everything is changed, and in such a dreadful

[046-042]way! I cannot believe that this is the behaviour of a sane man!’

[046-043]Jasper understood that an explanation of the matter might have been

[046-044]given in much more homely terms; it was natural that Mrs Yule

[046-045]should leave out of sight the sufficient, but ignoble, cause of her

[046-046]son-in-law’s behaviour.

[046-047]‘You see in what a painful position we are placed,’ continued the

[046-048]euphemistic lady. ‘It is so terrible even to hint that Mr Reardon is not

[046-049]responsible for his actions, yet how are we to explain to our friends

[046-050]this extraordinary state of things?’

[046-051]‘My husband is afraid Mr Reardon may fall seriously ill,’ said Mrs

[046-052]Carter. ‘And how dreadful! In such a place as that!’

[046-053]‘It would be so kind of you to go and see him, Mr Milvain,’ urged Mrs

[046-054]Yule. ‘We should be so glad to hear what you think.’

[046-055]‘Certainly, I will go,’ replied Jasper. ‘Will you give me his address?’

[046-056]He remained for an hour, and before his departure the subject was

[046-057]discussed with rather more frankness than at first; even the word

[046-058]‘[[money-in-literature|money]]’ was once or twice heard.

[046-059]‘Mr Carter has very kindly promised,’ said Mrs Yule, ‘to do his best to

[046-060]hear of some position that would be suitable. It seems a most shocking

[046-061]thing that a successful author should abandon his career in this

[046-062]deliberate way; who could have imagined anything of the kind two

[046-063]years ago? But it is clearly quite impossible for him to go on as

[046-064]at present--if there is really no reason for believing his mind

[046-065]disordered.’

[046-066]A cab was summoned for Mrs Carter, and she took her leave, suppressing

[046-067]her native cheerfulness to the tone of the occasion. A minute or two

[046-068]after, Milvain left the house.

[046-069]He had walked perhaps twenty yards, almost to the end of the silent

[046-070]street in which his friends’ house was situated, when a man came round

[046-071]the corner and approached him. At once he recognised the figure, and in

[046-072]a moment he was face to face with Reardon. Both stopped. Jasper held out

[046-073]his hand, but the other did not seem to notice it.

[046-074]‘You are coming from Mrs Yule’s?’ said Reardon, with a strange smile.

[046-075]By the gaslight his face showed pale and sunken, and he met Jasper’s

[046-076]look with fixedness.

[046-077]‘Yes, I am. The fact is, I went there to hear of your address. Why

[046-078]haven’t you let me know about all this?’

[046-079]‘You went to the flat?’

[046-080]‘No, I was told about you by [[whelpdale|Whelpdale]].’

[046-081]Reardon turned in the direction whence he had come, and began to walk

[046-082]slowly; Jasper kept beside him.

[046-083]‘I’m afraid there’s something amiss between us, Reardon,’ said the

[046-084]latter, just glancing at his companion.

[046-085]‘There’s something amiss between me and everyone,’ was the reply, in an

[046-086]unnatural voice.

[046-087]‘You look at things too gloomily. Am I detaining you, by-the-bye? You

[046-088]were going--’

[046-089]‘Nowhere.’

[046-090]‘Then come to my rooms, and let us see if we can’t talk more in the old

[046-091]way.’

[046-092]‘Your old way of talk isn’t much to my taste, Milvain. It has cost me

[046-093]too much.’

[046-094]Jasper gazed at him. Was there some foundation for Mrs Yule’s seeming

[046-095]extravagance? This reply sounded so meaningless, and so unlike Reardon’s

[046-096]manner of speech, that the younger man experienced a sudden alarm.

[046-097]‘Cost you too much? I don’t understand you.’

[046-098]They had turned into a broader thoroughfare, which, however, was little

[046-099]frequented at this hour. Reardon, his hands thrust into the pockets of

[046-100]a shabby overcoat and his head bent forward, went on at a slow pace,

[046-101]observant of nothing. For a moment or two he delayed reply, then said in

[046-102]an unsteady voice:

[046-103]‘Your way of talking has always been to glorify [[literary-ambition|success]], to insist upon

[046-104]it as the one end a man ought to keep in view. If you had talked so to

[046-105]me alone, it wouldn’t have mattered. But there was generally someone

[046-106]else present. Your words had their effect; I can see that now. It’s very

[046-107]much owing to you that I am deserted, now that there’s no hope of my

[046-108]ever succeeding.’

[046-109]Jasper’s first impulse was to meet this accusation with indignant

[046-110]denial, but a sense of compassion prevailed. It was so painful to see

[046-111]the defeated man wandering at night near the house where his wife

[046-112]and child were comfortably sheltered; and the tone in which he spoke

[046-113]revealed such profound misery.

[046-114]‘That’s a most astonishing thing to say,’ Jasper replied. ‘Of course I

[046-115]know nothing of what has passed between you and your wife, but I feel

[046-116]certain that I have no more to do with what has happened than any other

[046-117]of your acquaintances.’

[046-118]‘You may feel as certain as you will, but your words and your example

[046-119]have influenced my wife against me. You didn’t intend that; I don’t

[046-120]suppose it for a moment. It’s my misfortune, that’s all.’

[046-121]‘That I intended nothing of the kind, you need hardly say, I should

[046-122]think. But you are deceiving yourself in the strangest way. I’m afraid

[046-123]to speak plainly; I’m afraid of offending you. But can you recall

[046-124]something that I said about the time of your [[literary-marriage|marriage]]? You didn’t like

[046-125]it then, and certainly it won’t be pleasant to you to remember it now.

[046-126]If you mean that your wife has grown unkind to you because you are

[046-127]unfortunate, there’s no need to examine into other people’s influence

[046-128]for an explanation of that.’

[046-129]Reardon turned his face towards the speaker.

[046-130]‘Then you have always regarded my wife as a woman likely to fail me in

[046-131]time of need?’

[046-132]‘I don’t care to answer a question put in that way. If we are no longer

[046-133]to talk with the old friendliness, it’s far better we shouldn’t discuss

[046-134]things such as this.’

[046-135]‘Well, practically you have answered. Of course I remember those words

[046-136]of yours that you refer to. Whether you were right or wrong doesn’t

[046-137]affect what I say.’

[046-138]He spoke with a dull doggedness, as though mental fatigue did not allow

[046-139]him to say more.

[046-140]‘It’s impossible to argue against such a charge,’ said Milvain. ‘I am

[046-141]convinced it isn’t true, and that’s all I can answer. But perhaps you

[046-142]think this extraordinary influence of mine is still being used against

[046-143]you?’

[046-144]‘I know nothing about it,’ Reardon replied, in the same unmodulated

[046-145]voice.

[046-146]‘Well, as I have told you, this was my first visit to Mrs Yule’s since

[046-147]your wife has been there, and I didn’t see her; she isn’t very well,

[046-148]and keeps her room. I’m glad it happened so--that I didn’t meet her.

[046-149]Henceforth I shall keep away from the family altogether, so long, at all

[046-150]events, as your wife remains with them. Of course I shan’t tell anyone

[046-151]why; that would be impossible. But you shan’t have to fear that I am

[046-152]decrying you. By Jove! an amiable figure you make of me!’

[046-153]‘I have said what I didn’t wish to say, and what I oughtn’t to have

[046-154]said. You must misunderstand me; I can’t help it.’

[046-155]Reardon had been walking for hours, and was, in truth, exhausted.

[046-156]He became mute. Jasper, whose misrepresentation was wilful, though not

[046-157]maliciously so, also fell into silence; he did not believe that his

[046-158]conversations with Amy had seriously affected the course of events,

[046-159]but he knew that he had often said things to her in private which

[046-160]would scarcely have fallen from his lips if her husband had been

[046-161]present--little depreciatory phrases, wrong rather in tone than in

[046-162]terms, which came of his irresistible desire to assume superiority

[046-163]whenever it was possible. He, too, was weak, but with quite another

[046-164]kind of weakness than Reardon’s. His was the weakness of vanity, which

[046-165]sometimes leads a man to commit treacheries of which he would believe

[046-166]himself incapable. Self-accused, he took refuge in the pretence of

[046-167]misconception, which again was a betrayal of littleness.

[046-168]They drew near to Westbourne Park station.

[046-169]‘You are living a long way from here,’ Jasper said, coldly. ‘Are you

[046-170]going by train?’

[046-171]‘No. You said my wife was ill?’

[046-172]‘Oh, not ill. At least, I didn’t understand that it was anything

[046-173]serious. Why don’t you walk back to the house?’

[046-174]‘I must judge of my own affairs.’

[046-175]‘True; I beg your pardon. I take the train here, so I’ll say

[046-176]good-night.’

[046-177]They nodded to each other, but did not shake hands.

[046-178]A day or two later, Milvain wrote to Mrs Yule, and told her that he

[046-179]had seen Reardon; he did not describe the circumstances under which the

[046-180]interview had taken place, but gave it as his opinion that Reardon

[046-181]was in a state of nervous illness, and made by suffering quite unlike

[046-182]himself. That he might be on the way to positive mental disease seemed

[046-183]likely enough. ‘Unhappily, I myself can be of no use to him; he has

[046-184]not the same friendly feeling for me as he used to have. But it is

[046-185]very certain that those of his friends who have the power should exert

[046-186]themselves to raise him out of this fearful slough of despond. If he

[046-187]isn’t effectually helped, there’s no saying what may happen. One thing

[046-188]is certain, I think: he is past helping himself. Sane literary work

[046-189]cannot be expected from him. It seems a monstrous thing that so good a

[046-190]fellow, and one with such excellent brains too, should perish by the

[046-191]way when influential people would have no difficulty in restoring him to

[046-192]health and usefulness.’
