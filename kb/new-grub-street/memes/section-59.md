# Section 59

[058-001]‘And yet,’ came in a low voice from [[marian-yule|Marian]], ‘you say that you love me.’

[058-002]‘You mean that I speak as if no such thing as love existed. But you

[058-003]asked me what I understood by [[literary-ambition|success]]. I am speaking of worldly things.

[058-004]Now suppose I had said to you:

[058-005]My one aim and desire in life is to win your love. Could you have

[058-006]believed me? Such phrases are always untrue; I don’t know how it

[058-007]can give anyone pleasure to hear them. But if I say to you: All the

[058-008]satisfactions I have described would be immensely heightened if they

[058-009]were shared with a woman who loved me--there is the simple truth.’

[058-010]Marian’s heart sank. She did not want truth such as this; she would have

[058-011]preferred that he should utter the poor, common falsehoods. Hungry for

[058-012]passionate love, she heard with a sense of desolation all this calm

[058-013]reasoning. That [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] was of cold temperament she had often feared; yet

[058-014]there was always the consoling thought that she did not see with perfect

[058-015]clearness into his nature. Now and then had come a flash, a hint of

[058-016]possibilities. She had looked forward with trembling eagerness to some

[058-017]sudden revelation; but it seemed as if he knew no word of the language

[058-018]which would have called such joyous response from her expectant soul.

[058-019]‘We have talked for a long time,’ she said, turning her head as if his

[058-020]last words were of no significance. ‘As [[dora-milvain|Dora]] is not coming, I think I

[058-021]will go now.’

[058-022]She rose, and went towards the chair on which lay her out-of-door

[058-023]things. At once Jasper stepped to her side.

[058-024]‘You will go without giving me any answer?’

[058-025]‘Answer? To what?’

[058-026]‘Will you be my wife?’

[058-027]‘It is too soon to ask me that.’

[058-028]‘Too soon? Haven’t you known for months that I thought of you with far

[058-029]more than friendliness?’

[058-030]‘How was it possible I should know that? You have explained to me why

[058-031]you would not let your real feelings be understood.’

[058-032]The reproach was merited, and not easy to be outfaced. He turned away

[058-033]for an instant, then with a sudden movement caught both her hands.

[058-034]‘Whatever I have done or said or thought in the past, that is of no

[058-035]account now. I love you, Marian. I want you to be my wife. I have never

[058-036]seen any other girl who impressed me as you did from the first. If I had

[058-037]been weak enough to try to win anyone but you, I should have known that

[058-038]I had turned aside from the path of my true happiness. Let us forget for

[058-039]a moment all our circumstances. I hold your hands, and look into your

[058-040]face, and say that I love you. Whatever answer you give, I love you!’

[058-041]Till now her heart had only fluttered a little; it was a great part of

[058-042]her distress that the love she had so long nurtured seemed shrinking

[058-043]together into some far corner of her being whilst she listened to

[058-044]the discourses which prefaced Jasper’s declaration. She was nervous,

[058-045]painfully self-conscious, touched with maidenly shame, but could not

[058-046]abandon herself to that delicious emotion which ought to have been the

[058-047]fulfilment of all her secret imaginings. Now at length there began a

[058-048]throbbing in her bosom. Keeping her face averted, her eyes cast down,

[058-049]she waited for a repetition of the note that was in that last ‘I love

[058-050]you.’ She felt a change in the hands that held hers--a warmth, a moist

[058-051]softness; it caused a shock through her veins.

[058-052]He was trying to draw her nearer, but she kept at full arm’s length and

[058-053]looked irresponsive.

[058-054]‘Marian?’

[058-055]She wished to answer, but a spirit of perversity held her tongue.

[058-056]‘Marian, don’t you love me? Or have I offended you by my way of

[058-057]speaking?’

[058-058]Persisting, she at length withdrew her hands. Jasper’s face expressed

[058-059]something like dismay.

[058-060]‘You have not offended me,’ she said. ‘But I am not sure that you don’t

[058-061]deceive yourself in thinking, for the moment, that I am necessary to

[058-062]your happiness.’

[058-063]The emotional current which had passed from her flesh to his whilst

[058-064]their hands were linked, made him incapable of standing aloof from her.

[058-065]He saw that her face and neck were warmer hued, and her beauty became

[058-066]more desirable to him than ever yet.

[058-067]‘You are more to me than anything else in the compass of life!’ he

[058-068]exclaimed, again pressing forward. ‘I think of nothing but you--you

[058-069]yourself--my beautiful, gentle, thoughtful Marian!’

[058-070]His arm captured her, and she did not resist. A sob, then a strange

[058-071]little laugh, betrayed the passion that was at length unfolded in her.

[058-072]‘You do love me, Marian?’

[058-073]‘I love you.’

[058-074]And there followed the antiphony of ardour that finds its first

[058-075]utterance--a subdued music, often interrupted, ever returning upon the

[058-076]same rich note.

[058-077]Marian closed her eyes and abandoned herself to the luxury of the dream.

[058-078]It was her first complete escape from the world of intellectual routine,

[058-079]her first taste of life. All the pedantry of her daily toil slipped away

[058-080]like a cumbrous garment; she was clad only in her womanhood. Once or

[058-081]twice a shudder of strange self-consciousness went through her, and

[058-082]she felt guilty, immodest; but upon that sensation followed a surge of

[058-083]passionate joy, obliterating memory and forethought.

[058-084]‘How shall I see you?’ Jasper asked at length. ‘Where can we meet?’

[058-085]It was a difficulty. The season no longer allowed lingerings under

[058-086]the open sky, but Marian could not go to his lodgings, and it seemed

[058-087]impossible for him to visit her at her home.

[058-088]‘Will your father persist in unfriendliness to me?’

[058-089]She was only just beginning to reflect on all that was involved in this

[058-090]new relation.

[058-091]‘I have no hope that he will change,’ she said sadly.

[058-092]‘He will refuse to countenance your [[literary-marriage|marriage]]?’

[058-093]‘I shall disappoint him and grieve him bitterly. He has asked me to use

[058-094]my [[money-in-literature|money]] in starting a new review.’

[058-095]‘Which he is to edit?’

[058-096]‘Yes. Do you think there would be any hope of its [[literary-success|success]]?’

[058-097]Jasper shook his head.

[058-098]‘Your father is not the man for that, Marian. I don’t say it

[058-099]disrespectfully; I mean that he doesn’t seem to me to have that kind of

[058-100]aptitude. It would be a disastrous speculation.’

[058-101]‘I felt that. Of course I can’t think of it now.’

[058-102]She smiled, raising her face to his.

[058-103]‘Don’t trouble,’ said Jasper. ‘Wait a little, till I have made myself

[058-104]independent of [[fadge|Fadge]] and a few other men, and your father shall see

[058-105]how heartily I wish to be of use to him. He will miss your help, I’m

[058-106]afraid?’

[058-107]‘Yes. I shall feel it a cruelty when I have to leave him. He has only

[058-108]just told me that his sight is beginning to fail. Oh, why didn’t his

[058-109]brother leave him a little money? It was such unkindness! Surely he had

[058-110]a much better right than [[amy-reardon|Amy]], or than myself either. But literature has

[058-111]been a curse to father all his life. My uncle hated it, and I suppose

[058-112]that was why he left father nothing.’

[058-113]‘But how am I to see you often? That’s the first question. I know what I

[058-114]shall do. I must take new lodgings, for the girls and myself, all in

[058-115]the same house. We must have two sitting-rooms; then you will come to my

[058-116]room without any difficulty. These astonishing proprieties are so easily

[058-117]satisfied after all.’

[058-118]‘You will really do that?’

[058-119]‘Yes. I shall go and look for rooms to-morrow. Then when you come you

[058-120]can always ask for Maud or Dora, you know. They will be very glad of a

[058-121]change to more respectable quarters.’

[058-122]‘I won’t stay to see them now, Jasper,’ said Marian, her thoughts

[058-123]turning to the girls.

[058-124]‘Very well. You are safe for another hour, but to make certain you shall

[058-125]go at a quarter to five. Your mother won’t be against us?’

[058-126]‘Poor mother--no. But she won’t dare to justify me before father.’

[058-127]‘I feel as if I should play a mean part in leaving it to you to tell

[058-128]your father. Marian, I will brave it out and go and see him.’

[058-129]‘Oh, it would be better not to.’

[058-130]‘Then I will write to him--such a letter as he can’t possibly take in

[058-131]ill part.’

[058-132]Marian pondered this proposal.

[058-133]‘You shall do that, Jasper, if you are willing. But not yet; presently.’

[058-134]‘You don’t wish him to know at once?’

[058-135]‘We had better wait a little. You know,’ she added laughing, ‘that my

[058-136]legacy is only in name mine as yet. The will hasn’t been proved. And

[058-137]then the money will have to be realised.’

[058-138]She informed him of the details; Jasper listened with his eyes on the

[058-139]ground.

[058-140]They were now sitting on chairs drawn close to each other. It was with

[058-141]a sense of relief that Jasper had passed from dithyrambs to conversation

[058-142]on practical points; Marian’s excited sensitiveness could not but

[058-143]observe this, and she kept watching the motions of his countenance. At

[058-144]length he even let go her hand.

[058-145]‘You would prefer,’ he said reflectively, ‘that nothing should be said

[058-146]to your father until that business is finished?’

[058-147]‘If you consent to it.’

[058-148]‘Oh, I have no doubt it’s as well.’

[058-149]Her little phrase of self-subjection, and its tremulous tone, called for

[058-150]another answer than this. Jasper fell again into thought, and clearly it

[058-151]was thought of practical things.

[058-152]‘I think I must go now, Jasper,’ she said.

[058-153]‘Must you? Well, if you had rather.’

[058-154]He rose, though she was still seated. Marian moved a few steps away, but

[058-155]turned and approached him again.

[058-156]‘Do you really love me?’ she asked, taking one of his hands and folding

[058-157]it between her own.

[058-158]‘I do indeed love you, Marian. Are you still doubtful?’

[058-159]‘You’re not sorry that I must go?’

[058-160]‘But I am, dearest. I wish we could sit here undisturbed all through the

[058-161]evening.’

[058-162]Her touch had the same effect as before. His blood warmed again, and he

[058-163]pressed her to his side, stroking her hair and kissing her forehead.

[058-164]‘Are you sorry I wear my hair short?’ she asked, longing for more praise

[058-165]than he had bestowed on her.

[058-166]‘Sorry? It is perfect. Everything else seems vulgar compared with this

[058-167]way of yours. How strange you would look with plaits and that kind of

[058-168]thing!’

[058-169]‘I am so glad it pleases you.’

[058-170]‘There is nothing in you that doesn’t please me, my thoughtful girl.’

[058-171]‘You called me that before. Do I seem so very thoughtful?’

[058-172]‘So grave, and sweetly reserved, and with eyes so full of meaning.’

[058-173]She quivered with delight, her face hidden against his breast.

[058-174]‘I seem to be new-born, Jasper. Everything in the world is new to me,

[058-175]and I am strange to myself. I have never known an hour of happiness till

[058-176]now, and I can’t believe yet that it has come to me.’

[058-177]She at length attired herself, and they left the house together, of

[058-178]course not unobserved by the landlady. Jasper walked about half the way

[058-179]to St Paul’s Crescent. It was arranged that he should address a letter

[058-180]for her to the care of his sisters; but in a day or two the change of

[058-181]lodgings would be effected.

[058-182]When they had parted, Marian looked back. But Jasper was walking quickly

[058-183]away, his head bent, in profound meditation.

[058-184]CHAPTER XXV. A FRUITLESS MEETING

[058-185]Refuge from despair is often found in the passion of self-pity and that

[058-186]spirit of obstinate resistance which it engenders. In certain natures

[058-187]the extreme of self-pity is intolerable, and leads to self-destruction;

[058-188]but there are less fortunate beings whom the vehemence of their revolt

[058-189]against fate strengthens to endure in suffering. These latter are rather

[058-190]imaginative than passionate; the stages of their woe impress them as

[058-191]the acts of a drama, which they cannot bring themselves to cut short, so

[058-192]various are the possibilities of its dark motive. The intellectual man

[058-193]who kills himself is most often brought to that decision by conviction

[058-194]of his insignificance; self-pity merges in self-scorn, and the

[058-195]humiliated soul is intolerant of existence. He who survives under like

[058-196]conditions does so because misery magnifies him in his own estimate.
