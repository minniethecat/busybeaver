# Section 62

[061-001]‘I came to ask you what you propose to do in case I go to Croydon.’

[061-002]‘I have no proposal to make whatever.’

[061-003]‘That means, then, that you are content to go on living here?’

[061-004]‘If I have no choice, I must make myself content.’

[061-005]‘But you have a choice.’

[061-006]‘None has yet been offered me.’

[061-007]‘Then I offer it now,’ said [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], speaking less aggressively. ‘I

[061-008]shall have a dwelling rent free, and a hundred and fifty pounds a

[061-009]year--perhaps it would be more in keeping with my station if I say that

[061-010]I shall have something less than three pounds a week. You can either

[061-011]accept from me half this [[money-in-literature|money]], as up to now, or come and take your

[061-012]place again as my wife. Please to decide what you will do.’

[061-013]‘I will let you know by letter in a few days.’

[061-014]It seemed impossible to her to say she would return, yet a refusal to

[061-015]do so involved nothing less than separation for the rest of their lives.

[061-016]Postponement of decision was her only resource.

[061-017]‘I must know at once,’ said Reardon.

[061-018]‘I can’t answer at once.’

[061-019]‘If you don’t, I shall understand you to mean that you refuse to come

[061-020]to me. You know the circumstances; there is no reason why you should

[061-021]consult with anyone else. You can answer me immediately if you will.’

[061-022]‘I don’t wish to answer you immediately,’ [[amy-reardon|Amy]] replied, paling slightly.

[061-023]‘Then that decides it. When I leave you we are strangers to each other.’

[061-024]Amy made a rapid study of his countenance. She had never entertained for

[061-025]a moment the supposition that his wits were unsettled, but none the less

[061-026]the constant recurrence of that idea in her mother’s talk had subtly

[061-027]influenced her against her husband. It had confirmed her in thinking

[061-028]that his behaviour was inexcusable. And now it seemed to her that

[061-029]anyone might be justified in holding him demented, so reckless was his

[061-030]utterance.

[061-031]It was difficult to know him as the man who had loved her so devotedly,

[061-032]who was incapable of an unkind word or look.

[061-033]‘If that is what you prefer,’ she said, ‘there must be a formal

[061-034]separation. I can’t trust my future to your caprice.’

[061-035]‘You mean it must be put into the hands of a lawyer?’

[061-036]‘Yes, I do.’

[061-037]‘That will be the best, no doubt.’

[061-038]‘Very well; I will speak with my friends about it.’

[061-039]‘Your friends!’ he exclaimed bitterly. ‘But for those friends of yours,

[061-040]this would never have happened. I wish you had been alone in the world

[061-041]and penniless.’

[061-042]‘A kind wish, all things considered.’

[061-043]‘Yes, it is a kind wish. Then your [[literary-marriage|marriage]] with me would have been

[061-044]binding; you would have known that my lot was yours, and the knowledge

[061-045]would have helped your weakness. I begin to see how much right there is

[061-046]on the side of those people who would keep women in subjection. You have

[061-047]been allowed to act with independence, and the result is that you have

[061-048]ruined my life and debased your own. If I had been strong enough to

[061-049]treat you as a child, and bid you follow me wherever my own fortunes

[061-050]led, it would have been as much better for you as for me. I was weak,

[061-051]and I suffer as all weak people do.’

[061-052]‘You think it was my duty to share such a home as you have at present?’

[061-053]‘You know it was. And if the choice had lain between that and earning

[061-054]your own livelihood you would have thought that even such a poor home

[061-055]might be made tolerable. There were possibilities in you of better

[061-056]things than will ever come out now.’

[061-057]There followed a silence. Amy sat with her eyes gloomily fixed on the

[061-058]carpet; Reardon looked about the room, but saw nothing. He had thrown

[061-059]his hat into a chair, and his fingers worked nervously together behind

[061-060]his back.

[061-061]‘Will you tell me,’ he said at length, ‘how your position is regarded

[061-062]by these friends of yours? I don’t mean your mother and brother, but the

[061-063]people who come to this house.’

[061-064]‘I have not asked such people for their opinion.’

[061-065]‘Still, I suppose some sort of explanation has been necessary in your

[061-066]intercourse with them. How have you represented your relations with me?’

[061-067]‘I can’t see that that concerns you.’

[061-068]‘In a manner it does. Certainly it matters very little to me how I am

[061-069]thought of by people of this kind, but one doesn’t like to be reviled

[061-070]without cause. Have you allowed it to be supposed that I have made life

[061-071]with me intolerable for you?’

[061-072]‘No, I have not. You insult me by asking the question, but as you

[061-073]don’t seem to understand feelings of that kind I may as well answer you

[061-074]simply.’

[061-075]‘Then have you told them the truth? That I became so poor you couldn’t

[061-076]live with me?’

[061-077]‘I have never said that in so many words, but no doubt it is understood.

[061-078]It must be known also that you refused to take the step which might have

[061-079]helped you out of your difficulties.’

[061-080]‘What step?’

[061-081]She reminded him of his intention to spend half a year in working at the

[061-082]seaside.

[061-083]‘I had utterly forgotten it,’ he returned with a mocking laugh. ‘That

[061-084]shows how ridiculous such a thing would have been.’

[061-085]‘You are doing no literary work at all?’ Amy asked.

[061-086]‘Do you imagine that I have the peace of mind necessary for anything of

[061-087]that sort?’

[061-088]This was in a changed voice. It reminded her so strongly of her husband

[061-089]before his disasters that she could not frame a reply.

[061-090]‘Do you think I am able to occupy myself with the affairs of imaginary

[061-091]people?’

[061-092]‘I didn’t necessarily mean fiction.’

[061-093]‘That I can forget myself, then, in the study of literature?--I wonder

[061-094]whether you really think of me like that. How, in Heaven’s name, do you

[061-095]suppose I spend my leisure time?’

[061-096]She made no answer.

[061-097]‘Do you think I take this calamity as light-heartedly as you do, Amy?’

[061-098]‘I am far from taking it light-heartedly.’

[061-099]‘Yet you are in good health. I see no sign that you have suffered.’

[061-100]She kept silence. Her suffering had been slight enough, and chiefly due

[061-101]to considerations of social propriety; but she would not avow this, and

[061-102]did not like to make admission of it to herself. Before her friends she

[061-103]frequently affected to conceal a profound sorrow; but so long as her

[061-104]child was left to her she was in no danger of falling a victim to

[061-105]sentimental troubles.

[061-106]‘And certainly I can’t believe it,’ he continued, ‘now you declare your

[061-107]wish to be formally separated from me.’

[061-108]‘I have declared no such wish.’

[061-109]‘Indeed you have. If you can hesitate a moment about returning to me

[061-110]when difficulties are at an end, that tells me you would prefer final

[061-111]separation.’

[061-112]‘I hesitate for this reason,’ Amy said after reflecting. ‘You are so

[061-113]very greatly changed from what you used to be, that I think it doubtful

[061-114]if I could live with you.’

[061-115]‘Changed?--Yes, that is true, I am afraid. But how do you think this

[061-116]change will affect my behaviour to you?’

[061-117]‘Remember how you have been speaking to me.’

[061-118]‘And you think I should treat you brutally if you came into my power?’

[061-119]‘Not brutally, in the ordinary sense of the word. But with faults of

[061-120]temper which I couldn’t bear. I have my own faults. I can’t behave as

[061-121]meekly as some women can.’

[061-122]It was a small concession, but Reardon made much of it.

[061-123]‘Did my faults of temper give you any trouble during the first year of

[061-124]our married life?’ he asked gently.

[061-125]‘No,’ she admitted.

[061-126]‘They began to afflict you when I was so hard driven by difficulties

[061-127]that I needed all your sympathy, all your forbearance. Did I receive

[061-128]much of either from you, Amy?’

[061-129]‘I think you did--until you demanded impossible things of me.’

[061-130]‘It was always in your power to rule me. What pained me worst, and

[061-131]hardened me against you, was that I saw you didn’t care to exert your

[061-132]influence. There was never a time when I could have resisted a word of

[061-133]yours spoken out of your love for me. But even then, I am afraid, you no

[061-134]longer loved me, and now--’

[061-135]He broke off, and stood watching her face.

[061-136]‘Have you any love for me left?’ burst from his lips, as if the words

[061-137]all but choked him in the utterance.

[061-138]Amy tried to shape some evasive answer, but could say nothing.

[061-139]‘Is there ever so small a hope that I might win some love from you

[061-140]again?’

[061-141]‘If you wish me to come and live with you when you go to Croydon I will

[061-142]do so.’

[061-143]‘But that is not answering me, Amy.’

[061-144]‘It’s all I can say.’

[061-145]‘Then you mean that you would sacrifice yourself out of--what? Out of

[061-146]pity for me, let us say.’

[061-147]‘Do you wish to see Willie?’ asked Amy, instead of replying.

[061-148]‘No. It is you I have come to see. The child is nothing to me, compared

[061-149]with you. It is you, who loved me, who became my wife--you only I care

[061-150]about. Tell me you will try to be as you used to be. Give me only that

[061-151]hope, Amy; I will ask nothing except that, now.’

[061-152]‘I can’t say anything except that I will come to Croydon if you wish

[061-153]it.’

[061-154]‘And reproach me always because you have to live in such a place, away

[061-155]from your friends, without a hope of the social [[literary-ambition|success]] which was your

[061-156]dearest ambition?’

[061-157]Her practical denial that she loved him wrung this taunt from his

[061-158]anguished heart. He repented the words as soon as they were spoken.

[061-159]‘What is the good?’ exclaimed Amy in irritation, rising and moving away

[061-160]from him. ‘How can I pretend that I look forward to such a life with any

[061-161]hope?’

[061-162]He stood in mute misery, inwardly cursing himself and his fate.

[061-163]‘I have said I will come,’ she continued, her voice shaken with nervous

[061-164]tension. ‘Ask me or not, as you please, when you are ready to go there.

[061-165]I can’t talk about it.’

[061-166]‘I shall not ask you,’ he replied. ‘I will have no woman slave dragging

[061-167]out a weary life with me. Either you are my willing wife, or you are

[061-168]nothing to me.’

[061-169]‘I am married to you, and that can’t be undone. I repeat that I shan’t

[061-170]refuse to obey you. I shall say no more.’

[061-171]She moved to a distance, and there seated herself, half turned from him.

[061-172]‘I shall never ask you to come,’ said Reardon, breaking a short silence.

[061-173]‘If our married life is ever to begin again it must be of your seeking.

[061-174]Come to me of your own will, and I shall never reject you. But I will

[061-175]die in utter loneliness rather than ask you again.’

[061-176]He lingered a few moments, watching her; she did not move. Then he took

[061-177]his hat, went in silence from the room, and left the house.

[061-178]It rained harder than before. As no trains were running at this hour,

[061-179]he walked in the direction where he would be likely to meet with an

[061-180]omnibus. But it was a long time before one passed which was any use to

[061-181]him. When he reached home he was in cheerless plight enough; to make

[061-182]things pleasanter, one of his boots had let in water abundantly.

[061-183]‘The first sore throat of the season, no doubt,’ he muttered to himself.

[061-184]Nor was he disappointed. By Tuesday the cold had firm grip of him. A

[061-185]day or two of influenza or sore throat always made him so weak that with

[061-186]difficulty he supported the least physical exertion; but at present he

[061-187]must go to his work at the hospital. Why stay at home? To what purpose

[061-188]spare himself? It was not as if life had any promise for him. He was

[061-189]a machine for earning so much money a week, and would at least give

[061-190]faithful work for his wages until the day of final breakdown.
