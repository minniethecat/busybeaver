# Section 75

[074-001]‘I know you didn’t, [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]. But you make me think that--’

[074-002]‘Don’t be so literal again, my dear girl. Come here and forgive me.’

[074-003]She did not approach, but only because the painful thought he had

[074-004]excited kept her to that spot.

[074-005]‘Come, [[marian-yule|Marian]]! Then I must come to you.’

[074-006]He did so and held her in his arms.

[074-007]‘Try your hand at a novel, dear, if you can possibly make time. Put me

[074-008]in it, if you like, and make me an insensible masculine. The experiment

[074-009]is worth a try I’m certain. At all events do a few chapters, and let

[074-010]me see them. A chapter needn’t take you more than a couple of hours I

[074-011]should think.’

[074-012]Marian refrained from giving any promise. She seemed irresponsive to

[074-013]his caresses. That thought which at times gives trouble to all women of

[074-014]strong emotions was working in her: had she been too demonstrative, and

[074-015]made her love too cheap? Now that Jasper’s love might be endangered, it

[074-016]behoved her to use any arts which nature prompted. And so, for once, he

[074-017]was not wholly satisfied with her, and at their parting he wondered what

[074-018]subtle change had affected her manner to him.

[074-019]‘Why didn’t Marian come to speak a word?’ said [[dora-milvain|Dora]], when her brother

[074-020]entered the girls’ sitting-room about ten o’clock.

[074-021]‘You knew she was with me, then?’

[074-022]‘We heard her voice as she was going away.’

[074-023]‘She brought me some enspiriting news, and thought it better I should

[074-024]have the reporting of it to you.’

[074-025]With brevity he made known what had befallen.

[074-026]‘Cheerful, isn’t it? The kind of thing that strengthens one’s trust in

[074-027]Providence.’

[074-028]The girls were appalled. Maud, who was reading by the fireside, let her

[074-029]book fall to her lap, and knit her brows darkly.

[074-030]‘Then your [[literary-marriage|marriage]] must be put off, of course?’ said Dora.

[074-031]‘Well, I shouldn’t be surprised if that were found necessary,’ replied

[074-032]her brother caustically. He was able now to give vent to the feeling

[074-033]which in Marian’s presence was suppressed, partly out of consideration

[074-034]for her, and partly owing to her influence.

[074-035]‘And shall we have to go back to our old lodgings again?’ inquired Maud.

[074-036]Jasper gave no answer, but kicked a footstool savagely out of his way

[074-037]and paced the room.

[074-038]‘Oh, do you think we need?’ said Dora, with unusual protest against

[074-039]economy.

[074-040]‘Remember that it’s a matter for your own consideration,’ Jasper replied

[074-041]at length. ‘You are living on your own resources, you know.’

[074-042]Maud glanced at her sister, but Dora was preoccupied.

[074-043]‘Why do you prefer to stay here?’ Jasper asked abruptly of the younger

[074-044]girl.

[074-045]‘It is so very much nicer,’ she replied with some embarrassment.

[074-046]He bit the ends of his moustache, and his eyes glared at the impalpable

[074-047]thwarting force that to imagination seemed to fill the air about him.

[074-048]‘A lesson against being over-hasty,’ he muttered, again kicking the

[074-049]footstool.

[074-050]‘Did you make that considerate remark to Marian?’ asked Maud.

[074-051]‘There would have been no harm if I had done. She knows that I shouldn’t

[074-052]have been such an ass as to talk of marriage without the prospect of

[074-053]something to live upon.’

[074-054]‘I suppose she’s wretched?’ said Dora.

[074-055]‘What else can you expect?’

[074-056]‘And did you propose to release her from the burden of her engagement?’

[074-057]Maud inquired.

[074-058]‘It’s a confounded pity that you’re not rich, Maud,’ replied her brother

[074-059]with an involuntary laugh. ‘You would have a brilliant [[literary-success|reputation]] for

[074-060]wit.’

[074-061]He walked about and ejaculated splenetic phrases on the subject of his

[074-062]ill-luck.

[074-063]‘We are here, and here we must stay,’ was the final expression of his

[074-064]mood. ‘I have only one superstition that I know of and that forbids me

[074-065]to take a step backward. If I went into poorer lodgings again I should

[074-066]feel it was inviting defeat. I shall stay as long as the position is

[074-067]tenable. Let us get on to Christmas, and then see how things look.

[074-068]Heavens! Suppose we had married, and after that lost the [[money-in-literature|money]]!’

[074-069]‘You would have been no worse off than plenty of literary men,’ said

[074-070]Dora.

[074-071]‘Perhaps not. But as I have made up my mind to be considerably better

[074-072]off than most literary men that reflection wouldn’t console me much.

[074-073]Things are in statu quo, that’s all. I have to rely upon my own efforts.

[074-074]What’s the time? Half-past ten; I can get two hours’ work before going

[074-075]to bed.’

[074-076]And nodding a good-night he left them.

[074-077]When Marian entered the house and went upstairs, she was followed by her

[074-078]mother. On Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s countenance there was a new distress, she had been

[074-079]crying recently.

[074-080]‘Have you seen him?’ the mother asked.

[074-081]‘Yes. We have talked about it.’

[074-082]‘What does he wish you to do, dear?’

[074-083]‘There’s nothing to be done except wait.’

[074-084]‘Father has been telling me something, Marian,’ said Mrs Yule after a

[074-085]long silence. ‘He says he is going to be blind. There’s something

[074-086]the matter with his eyes, and he went to see someone about it this

[074-087]afternoon. He’ll get worse and worse, until there has been an operation;

[074-088]and perhaps he’ll never be able to use his eyes properly again.’

[074-089]The girl listened in an attitude of despair.

[074-090]‘He has seen an oculist?--a really good doctor?’

[074-091]‘He says he went to one of the best.’

[074-092]‘And how did he speak to you?’

[074-093]‘He doesn’t seem to care much what happens. He talked of going to the

[074-094]workhouse, and things like that. But it couldn’t ever come to that,

[074-095]could it, Marian? Wouldn’t somebody help him?’

[074-096]‘There’s not much help to be expected in this world,’ answered the girl.

[074-097]Physical weariness brought her a few hours of oblivion as soon as she

[074-098]had lain down, but her sleep came to an end in the early morning, when

[074-099]the pressure of evil dreams forced her back to consciousness of real

[074-100]sorrows and cares. A fog-veiled sky added its weight to crush her

[074-101]spirit; at the hour when she usually rose it was still all but as dark

[074-102]as midnight. Her mother’s voice at the door begged her to lie and

[074-103]rest until it grew lighter, and she willingly complied, feeling indeed

[074-104]scarcely capable of leaving her bed.

[074-105]The thick black fog penetrated every corner of the house. It could be

[074-106]smelt and tasted. Such an atmosphere produces low-spirited languor even

[074-107]in the vigorous and hopeful; to those wasted by suffering it is the very

[074-108]reek of the bottomless pit, poisoning the soul. Her face colourless as

[074-109]the pillow, Marian lay neither sleeping nor awake, in blank extremity of

[074-110]woe; tears now and then ran down her cheeks, and at times her body was

[074-111]shaken with a throe such as might result from anguish of the torture

[074-112]chamber.

[074-113]Midway in the morning, when it was still necessary to use artificial

[074-114]light, she went down to the sitting-room. The course of household life

[074-115]had been thrown into confusion by the disasters of the last day or two;

[074-116]Mrs Yule, who occupied herself almost exclusively with questions of

[074-117]economy, cleanliness, and routine, had not the heart to pursue her round

[074-118]of duties, and this morning, though under normal circumstances she would

[074-119]have been busy in ‘turning out’ the dining-room, she moved aimlessly and

[074-120]despondently about the house, giving the servant contradictory orders

[074-121]and then blaming herself for her absent-mindedness. In the troubles of

[074-122]her husband and her daughter she had scarcely greater share--so far

[074-123]as active participation went--than if she had been only a faithful old

[074-124]housekeeper; she could only grieve and lament that such discord had come

[074-125]between the two whom she loved, and that in herself was no power even to

[074-126]solace their distresses. Marian found her standing in the passage, with

[074-127]a duster in one hand and a hearth-brush in the other.

[074-128]‘Your father has asked to see you when you come down,’ Mrs Yule

[074-129]whispered.

[074-130]‘I’ll go to him.’

[074-131]Marian entered the study. Her father was not in his place at the

[074-132]writing-table, nor yet seated in the chair which he used when he had

[074-133]leisure to draw up to the fireside; he sat in front of one of the

[074-134]bookcases, bent forward as if seeking a volume, but his chin was propped

[074-135]upon his hand, and he had maintained this position for a long time. He

[074-136]did not immediately move. When he raised his head Marian saw that he

[074-137]looked older, and she noticed--or fancied she did--that there was some

[074-138]unfamiliar peculiarity about his eyes.

[074-139]‘I am obliged to you for coming,’ he began with distant formality.

[074-140]‘Since I saw you last I have learnt something which makes a change in my

[074-141]position and prospects, and it is necessary to speak on the subject. I

[074-142]won’t detain you more than a few minutes.’

[074-143]He coughed, and seemed to consider his next words.

[074-144]‘Perhaps I needn’t repeat what I have told your mother. You have learnt

[074-145]it from her, I dare say.’

[074-146]‘Yes, with much grief.’

[074-147]‘Thank you, but we will leave aside that aspect of the matter. For a few

[074-148]more months I may be able to pursue my ordinary work, but before long

[074-149]I shall certainly be disabled from earning my livelihood by literature.

[074-150]Whether this will in any way affect your own position I don’t know. Will

[074-151]you have the goodness to tell me whether you still purpose leaving this

[074-152]house?’

[074-153]‘I have no means of doing so.’

[074-154]‘Is there any likelihood of your marriage taking place, let us say,

[074-155]within four months?’

[074-156]‘Only if the executors recover my money, or a large portion of it.’

[074-157]‘I understand. My reason for asking is this. My lease of this house

[074-158]terminates at the end of next March, and I shall certainly not be

[074-159]justified in renewing it. If you are able to provide for yourself in

[074-160]any way it will be sufficient for me to rent two rooms after that. This

[074-161]disease which affects my eyes may be only temporary; in due time an

[074-162]operation may render it possible for me to work again. In hope of that I

[074-163]shall probably have to borrow a sum of money on the security of my life

[074-164]insurance, though in the first instance I shall make the most of what I

[074-165]can get for the furniture of the house and a large part of my library;

[074-166]your mother and I could live at very slight expense in lodgings. If the

[074-167]disease prove irremediable, I must prepare myself for the worst. What

[074-168]I wish to say is, that it will be better if from to-day you consider

[074-169]yourself as working for your own subsistence. So long as I remain here

[074-170]this house is of course your home; there can be no question between us

[074-171]of trivial expenses. But it is right that you should understand what my

[074-172]prospects are. I shall soon have no home to offer you; you must look to

[074-173]your own efforts for support.’

[074-174]‘I am prepared to do that, father.’

[074-175]‘I think you will have no great difficulty in earning enough for

[074-176]yourself. I have done my best to train you in writing for the

[074-177]periodicals, and your natural abilities are considerable. If you

[074-178]marry, I wish you a happy life. The end of mine, of many long years of

[074-179]unremitting toil, is failure and destitution.’

[074-180]Marian sobbed.

[074-181]‘That’s all I had to say,’ concluded her father, his voice tremulous

[074-182]with self-compassion. ‘I will only beg that there may be no further

[074-183]profitless discussion between us. This room is open to you, as always,

[074-184]and I see no reason why we should not converse on subjects disconnected

[074-185]with our personal differences.’

[074-186]‘Is there no remedy for cataract in its early stages?’ asked Marian.

[074-187]‘None. You can read up the subject for yourself at the [[british-museum|British Museum]]. I

[074-188]prefer not to speak of it.’

[074-189]‘Will you let me be what help to you I can?’

[074-190]‘For the present the best you can do is to establish a connection for

[074-191]yourself with editors. Your name will be an assistance to you. My advice

[074-192]is, that you send your “Harrington” article forthwith to Trenchard,

[074-193]writing him a note. If you desire my help in the suggestion of new

[074-194]subjects, I will do my best to be of use.’
