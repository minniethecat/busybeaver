# Section 50

[049-001]‘What have you to do with them? I s’pose they’re your relations, ain’t

[049-002]they? And I s’pose the girl Annie Rudd is your niece, ain’t she? At

[049-003]least, she’s your wife’s niece, and that comes to the same thing, I’ve

[049-004]always understood, though I dare say a gentleman as has so many books

[049-005]about him can correct me if I’ve made a mistake.’

[049-006]She looked scornfully, though also with some surprise, round the volumed

[049-007]walls.

[049-008]‘And what of this girl? Will you have the goodness to say what your

[049-009]business is?’

[049-010]‘Yes, I will have the goodness! I s’pose you know very well that I took

[049-011]your niece Annie Rudd as a domestic servant’--she repeated this precise

[049-012]definition--‘as a domestic servant, because Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] ‘appened to ‘arst

[049-013]me if I knew of a place for a girl of that kind, as hadn’t been out

[049-014]before, but could be trusted to do her best to give satisfaction to a

[049-015]good mistress? I s’pose you know that?’

[049-016]‘I know nothing of the kind. What have I to do with servants?’

[049-017]‘Well, whether you’ve much to do with them or little, that’s how it

[049-018]was. And nicely she’s paid me out, has your niece, Miss Rudd. Of all the

[049-019]trouble I ever had with a girl! And now when she’s run away back ‘ome,

[049-020]and when I take the trouble to go arfter her, I’m to be insulted and

[049-021]abused as never was! Oh, they’re a nice respectable family, those Rudds!

[049-022]Mrs Rudd--that’s Mrs Yule’s sister--what a nice, polite-spoken lady she

[049-023]is, to be sure? If I was to repeat the language--but there, I wouldn’t

[049-024]lower myself. And I’ve been a brute of a mistress; I ill-use my

[049-025]servants, and I don’t give ‘em enough to eat, and I pay ‘em worse than

[049-026]any woman in [[london|London]]! That’s what I’ve learnt about myself by going to

[049-027]Perker Street, ‘Olloway. And when I come here to ask Mrs Yule what she

[049-028]means by recommending such a creature, from such a ‘ome, I get insulted

[049-029]by her gentleman husband.’

[049-030]Yule was livid with rage, but the extremity of his scorn withheld him

[049-031]from utterance of what he felt.

[049-032]‘As I said, all this has nothing to do with me. I will let Mrs Yule know

[049-033]that you have called. I have no more time to spare.’

[049-034]Mrs Goby repeated at still greater length the details of her grievance,

[049-035]but long before she had finished Yule was sitting again at his desk in

[049-036]ostentatious disregard of her. Finally, the exasperated woman flung open

[049-037]the door, railed in a loud voice along the passage, and left the house

[049-038]with an alarming crash.

[049-039]It was not long before Mrs Yule returned. Before taking off her things,

[049-040]she went down into the kitchen with certain purchases, and there she

[049-041]learnt from the servant what had happened during her absence. Fear and

[049-042]trembling possessed her--the sick, faint dread always excited by her

[049-043]husband’s wrath--but she felt obliged to go at once to the study. The

[049-044]scene that took place there was one of ignoble violence on Yule’s part,

[049-045]and, on that of his wife, of terrified self-accusation, changing at

[049-046]length to dolorous resentment of the harshness with which she was

[049-047]treated. When it was over, Yule took his hat and went out.

[049-048]He did not return for the mid-day meal, and when [[marian-yule|Marian]], late in the

[049-049]afternoon, came back from the Museum, he was still absent.

[049-050]Not finding her mother in the parlour, Marian called at the head of the

[049-051]kitchen stairs. The servant answered, saying that Mrs Yule was up in

[049-052]her bedroom, and that she didn’t seem well. Marian at once went up and

[049-053]knocked at the bedroom door. In a moment or two her mother came out,

[049-054]showing a face of tearful misery.

[049-055]‘What is it, mother? What’s the matter?’

[049-056]They went into Marian’s room, where Mrs Yule gave free utterance to her

[049-057]lamentations.

[049-058]‘I can’t put up with it, Marian! Your father is too hard with me.

[049-059]I was wrong, I dare say, and I might have known what would have come of

[049-060]it, but he couldn’t speak to me worse if I did him all the harm I could

[049-061]on purpose. It’s all about Annie, because I found a place for her at Mrs

[049-062]Goby’s in the ‘Olloway Road; and now Mrs Goby’s been here and seen your

[049-063]father, and told him she’s been insulted by the Rudds, because Annie

[049-064]went off home, and she went after her to make inquiries. And your

[049-065]father’s in such a passion about it as never was. That woman Mrs Goby

[049-066]rushed into the study when he was working; it was this morning, when I

[049-067]happened to be out. And she throws all the blame on me for recommending

[049-068]her such a girl. And I did it for the best, that I did! Annie promised

[049-069]me faithfully she’d behave well, and never give me trouble, and she

[049-070]seemed thankful to me, because she wasn’t happy at home. And now to

[049-071]think of her causing all this disturbance! I oughtn’t to have done

[049-072]such a thing without speaking about it to your father; but you know how

[049-073]afraid I am to say a word to him about those people. And my sister’s

[049-074]told me so often I ought to be ashamed of myself never helping her and

[049-075]her children; she thinks I could do such a lot if I only liked. And now

[049-076]that I did try to do something, see what comes of it!’

[049-077]Marian listened with a confusion of wretched feelings. But her

[049-078]sympathies were strongly with her mother; as well as she could

[049-079]understand the broken story, her father seemed to have no just cause

[049-080]for his pitiless rage, though such an occasion would be likely enough to

[049-081]bring out his worst faults.

[049-082]‘Is he in the study?’ she asked.

[049-083]‘No, he went out at twelve o’clock, and he’s never been back since. I

[049-084]feel as if I must do something; I can’t bear with it, Marian. He tells

[049-085]me I’m the curse of his life--yes, he said that. I oughtn’t to tell you,

[049-086]I know I oughtn’t; but it’s more than I can bear. I’ve always tried to

[049-087]do my best, but it gets harder and harder for me. But for me he’d

[049-088]never be in these bad tempers; it’s because he can’t look at me without

[049-089]getting angry. He says I’ve kept him back all through his life; but for

[049-090]me he might have been far better off than he is. It may be true; I’ve

[049-091]often enough thought it. But I can’t bear to have it told me like that,

[049-092]and to see it in his face every time he looks at me. I shall have to do

[049-093]something. He’d be glad if only I was out of his way.’

[049-094]‘Father has no right to make you so unhappy,’ said Marian. ‘I can’t see

[049-095]that you did anything blameworthy; it seems to me that it was your duty

[049-096]to try and help Annie, and if it turned out unfortunately, that can’t be

[049-097]helped. You oughtn’t to think so much of what father says in his anger;

[049-098]I believe he hardly knows what he does say. Don’t take it so much to

[049-099]heart, mother.’

[049-100]‘I’ve tried my best, Marian,’ sobbed the poor woman, who felt that even

[049-101]her child’s sympathy could not be perfect, owing to the distance put

[049-102]between them by Marian’s education and refined sensibilities. ‘I’ve

[049-103]always thought it wasn’t right to talk to you about such things, but

[049-104]he’s been too hard with me to-day.’

[049-105]‘I think it was better you should tell me. It can’t go on like this; I

[049-106]feel that just as you do. I must tell father that he is making our lives

[049-107]a burden to us.’

[049-108]‘Oh, you mustn’t speak to him like that, Marian! I wouldn’t for anything

[049-109]make unkindness between you and your father; that would be the worst

[049-110]thing I’d done yet. I’d rather go away and work for my own living than

[049-111]make trouble between you and him.’

[049-112]‘It isn’t you who make trouble; it’s father. I ought to have spoken

[049-113]to him before this; I had no right to stand by and see how much you

[049-114]suffered from his ill-temper.’

[049-115]The longer they talked, the firmer grew Marian’s resolve to front her

[049-116]father’s tyrannous ill-humour, and in one way or another to change the

[049-117]intolerable state of things. She had been weak to hold her peace so

[049-118]long; at her age it was a simple duty to interfere when her mother

[049-119]was treated with such flagrant injustice. Her father’s behaviour was

[049-120]unworthy of a thinking man, and he must be made to feel that.

[049-121]Yule did not return. Dinner was delayed for half an hour, then Marian

[049-122]declared that they would wait no longer. They two made a sorry meal, and

[049-123]afterwards went together into the sitting-room. At eight o’clock they

[049-124]heard the front door open, and Yule’s footstep in the passage. Marian

[049-125]rose.

[049-126]‘Don’t speak till to-morrow!’ whispered her mother, catching at the

[049-127]girl’s arm. ‘Let it be till to-morrow, Marian!’

[049-128]‘I must speak! We can’t live in this terror.’

[049-129]She reached the study just as her father was closing the door behind

[049-130]him. Yule, seeing her enter, glared with bloodshot eyes; shame and

[049-131]sullen anger were blended on his countenance.

[049-132]‘Will you tell me what is wrong, father?’ Marian asked, in a voice which

[049-133]betrayed her nervous suffering, yet indicated the resolve with which she

[049-134]had come.

[049-135]‘I am not at all disposed to talk of the matter,’ he replied, with the

[049-136]awkward rotundity of phrase which distinguished him in his worst humour.

[049-137]‘For information you had better go to Mrs Goby--or a person of some such

[049-138]name--in Holloway Road. I have nothing more to do with it.’

[049-139]‘It was very unfortunate that the woman came and troubled you about

[049-140]such things. But I can’t see that mother was to blame; I don’t think you

[049-141]ought to be so angry with her.’

[049-142]It cost Marian a terrible effort to address her father in these terms.

[049-143]When he turned fiercely upon her, she shrank back and felt as if

[049-144]strength must fail her even to stand.

[049-145]‘You can’t see that she was to blame? Isn’t it entirely against my wish

[049-146]that she keeps up any intercourse with those low people? Am I to be

[049-147]exposed to insulting disturbance in my very study, because she chooses

[049-148]to introduce girls of bad character as servants to vulgar women?’

[049-149]‘I don’t think Annie Rudd can be called a girl of bad character, and

[049-150]it was very natural that mother should try to do something for her. You

[049-151]have never actually forbidden her to see her relatives.’

[049-152]‘A thousand times I have given her to understand that I utterly

[049-153]disapproved of such association. She knew perfectly well that this girl

[049-154]was as likely as not to discredit her. If she had consulted me, I should

[049-155]at once have forbidden anything of the kind; she was aware of that. She

[049-156]kept it secret from me, knowing that it would excite my displeasure. I

[049-157]will not be drawn into such squalid affairs; I won’t have my name spoken

[049-158]in such connection. Your mother has only herself to blame if I am angry

[049-159]with her.’

[049-160]‘Your anger goes beyond all bounds. At the very worst, mother behaved

[049-161]imprudently, and with a very good motive. It is cruel that you should

[049-162]make her suffer as she is doing.’

[049-163]Marian was being strengthened to resist. Her blood grew hot; the

[049-164]sensation which once before had brought her to the verge of conflict

[049-165]with her father possessed her heart and brain.

[049-166]‘You are not a suitable judge of my behaviour,’ replied Yule, severely.

[049-167]‘I am driven to speak. We can’t go on living in this way, father. For

[049-168]months our home has been almost ceaselessly wretched, because of the

[049-169]ill-temper you are always in. Mother and I must defend ourselves; we

[049-170]can’t bear it any longer. You must surely feel how ridiculous it is to

[049-171]make such a thing as happened this morning the excuse for violent anger.

[049-172]How can I help judging your behaviour? When mother is brought to the

[049-173]point of saying that she would rather leave home and everything than

[049-174]endure her misery any longer, I should be wrong if I didn’t speak to

[049-175]you. Why are you so unkind? What serious cause has mother ever given

[049-176]you?’
