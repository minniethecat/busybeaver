# Section 16

[015-001]‘It will amuse you to hear,’ wrote [[dora-milvain|Dora]], ‘that the literary project our

[015-002]brother mentioned in a letter whilst you were still here is really to

[015-003]come to something. He has sent us a specimen chapter, written by himself

[015-004]of the “Child’s History of Parliament,” and Maud thinks she could carry

[015-005]it on in that style, if there’s no hurry. She and I have both set to

[015-006]work on English histories, and we shall be authorities before long.

[015-007]Jolly and Monk offer thirty pounds for the little book, if it suits them

[015-008]when finished, with certain possible profits in the future. Trust [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]]

[015-009]for making a bargain! So perhaps our literary career will be something

[015-010]more than a joke, after all. I hope it may; anything rather than a life

[015-011]of teaching. We shall be so glad to hear from you, if you still care to

[015-012]trouble about country girls.’

[015-013]And so on. [[marian-yule|Marian]] read with a pleased smile, then acquainted her mother

[015-014]with the contents.

[015-015]‘I am very glad,’ said Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]; ‘it’s so seldom you get a letter.’

[015-016]‘Yes.’

[015-017]Marian seemed desirous of saying something more, and her mother had a

[015-018]thoughtful look, suggestive of sympathetic curiosity.

[015-019]‘Is their brother likely to call here?’ Mrs Yule asked, with misgiving.

[015-020]‘No one has invited him to,’ was the girl’s quiet reply.

[015-021]‘He wouldn’t come without that?’

[015-022]‘It’s not likely that he even knows the address.’

[015-023]‘Your father won’t be seeing him, I suppose?’

[015-024]‘By chance, perhaps. I don’t know.’

[015-025]It was very rare indeed for these two to touch upon any subject save

[015-026]those of everyday interest. In spite of the affection between them,

[015-027]their exchange of confidence did not go very far; Mrs Yule, who had

[015-028]never exercised maternal authority since Marian’s earliest childhood,

[015-029]claimed no maternal privileges, and Marian’s natural reserve had been

[015-030]strengthened by her mother’s respectful aloofness. The English fault of

[015-031]domestic reticence could scarcely go further than it did in their case;

[015-032]its exaggeration is, of course, one of the characteristics of those

[015-033]unhappy families severed by differences of education between the old and

[015-034]young.

[015-035]‘I think,’ said Marian, in a forced tone, ‘that father hasn’t much

[015-036]liking for Mr Milvain.’

[015-037]She wished to know if her mother had heard any private remarks on this

[015-038]subject, but she could not bring herself to ask directly.

[015-039]‘I’m sure I don’t know,’ replied Mrs Yule, smoothing her dress. ‘He

[015-040]hasn’t said anything to me, Marian.’

[015-041]An awkward silence. The mother had fixed her eyes on the mantelpiece,

[015-042]and was thinking hard.

[015-043]‘Otherwise,’ said Marian, ‘he would have said something, I should think,

[015-044]about meeting in [[london|London]].’

[015-045]‘But is there anything in--this gentleman that he wouldn’t like?’

[015-046]‘I don’t know of anything.’

[015-047]Impossible to pursue the dialogue; Marian moved uneasily, then rose,

[015-048]said something about putting the letter away, and left the room.

[015-049]Shortly after, Alfred Yule entered the house. It was no uncommon thing

[015-050]for him to come home in a mood of silent moroseness, and this evening

[015-051]the first glimpse of his face was sufficient warning. He entered the

[015-052]dining-room and stood on the hearthrug reading an evening paper. His

[015-053]wife made a pretence of straightening things upon the table.

[015-054]‘Well?’ he exclaimed irritably. ‘It’s after five; why isn’t dinner

[015-055]served?’

[015-056]‘It’s just coming, Alfred.’

[015-057]Even the average man of a certain age is an alarming creature when

[015-058]dinner delays itself; the literary man in such a moment goes beyond all

[015-059]parallel. If there be added the fact that he has just returned from a

[015-060]very unsatisfactory interview with a publisher, wife and daughter may

[015-061]indeed regard the situation as appalling. Marian came in, and at once

[015-062]observed her mother’s frightened face.

[015-063]‘Father,’ she said, hoping to make a diversion, ‘Mr [[hinks|Hinks]] has sent you

[015-064]his new book, and wishes--’

[015-065]‘Then take Mr Hinks’s new book back to him, and tell him that I have

[015-066]quite enough to do without reading tedious trash. He needn’t expect

[015-067]that I’m going to write a notice of it. The simpleton pesters me beyond

[015-068]endurance. I wish to know, if you please,’ he added with savage calm,

[015-069]‘when dinner will be ready. If there’s time to write a few letters, just

[015-070]tell me at once, that I mayn’t waste half an hour.’

[015-071]Marian resented this unreasonable anger, but she durst not reply.

[015-072]At that moment the servant appeared with a smoking joint, and Mrs

[015-073]Yule followed carrying dishes of vegetables. The man of letters seated

[015-074]himself and carved angrily. He began his meal by drinking half a glass

[015-075]of ale; then he ate a few mouthfuls in a quick, hungry way, his head

[015-076]bent closely over the plate. It happened commonly enough that dinner

[015-077]passed without a word of conversation, and that seemed likely to be the

[015-078]case this evening.

[015-079]To his wife Yule seldom addressed anything but a curt inquiry or caustic

[015-080]comment; if he spoke humanly at table it was to Marian.

[015-081]Ten minutes passed; then Marian resolved to try any means of clearing

[015-082]the atmosphere.

[015-083]‘Mr [[quarmby|Quarmby]] gave me a message for you,’ she said. ‘A friend of his,

[015-084]Nathaniel Walker, has told him that Mr Rackett will very likely offer

[015-085]you the editorship of [[the-study|The Study]].’

[015-086]Yule stopped in the act of mastication. He fixed his eyes intently on

[015-087]the sirloin for half a minute; then, by way of the beer-jug and the

[015-088]salt-cellar, turned them upon Marian’s face.

[015-089]‘Walker told him that? Pooh!’

[015-090]‘It was a great secret. I wasn’t to breathe a word to any one but you.’

[015-091]‘Walker’s a fool and Quarmby’s an ass,’ remarked her father.

[015-092]But there was a tremulousness in his bushy eyebrows; his forehead half

[015-093]unwreathed itself; he continued to eat more slowly, and as if with

[015-094]appreciation of the viands.

[015-095]‘What did he say? Repeat it to me in his words.’

[015-096]Marian did so, as nearly as possible. He listened with a scoffing

[015-097]expression, but still his features relaxed.

[015-098]‘I don’t credit Rackett with enough good sense for such a proposal,’ he

[015-099]said deliberately. ‘And I’m not very sure that I should accept it if it

[015-100]were made. That fellow [[fadge|Fadge]] has all but ruined the paper. It will

[015-101]amuse me to see how long it takes him to make Culpepper’s new magazine a

[015-102]distinct failure.’

[015-103]A silence of five minutes ensued; then Yule said of a sudden.

[015-104]‘Where is Hinks’s book?’

[015-105]Marian reached it from a side table; under this roof, literature was

[015-106]regarded almost as a necessary part of table garnishing.

[015-107]‘I thought it would be bigger than this,’ Yule muttered, as he opened

[015-108]the volume in a way peculiar to bookish men.

[015-109]A page was turned down, as if to draw attention to some passage. Yule

[015-110]put on his eyeglasses, and soon made a discovery which had the effect of

[015-111]completing the transformation of his visage. His eyes glinted, his chin

[015-112]worked in pleasurable emotion. In a moment he handed the book to Marian,

[015-113]indicating the small type of a foot-note; it embodied an effusive

[015-114]eulogy--introduced a propos of some literary discussion--of ‘Mr Alfred

[015-115]Yule’s critical acumen, scholarly research, lucid style,’ and sundry

[015-116]other distinguished merits.

[015-117]‘That is kind of him,’ said Marian.

[015-118]‘Good old Hinks! I suppose I must try to get him half-a-dozen readers.’

[015-119]‘May I see?’ asked Mrs Yule, under her breath, bending to Marian.

[015-120]Her daughter passed on the volume, and Mrs Yule read the footnote with

[015-121]that look of slow apprehension which is so pathetic when it signifies

[015-122]the heart’s good-will thwarted by the mind’s defect.

[015-123]‘That’ll be good for you, Alfred, won’t it?’ she said, glancing at her

[015-124]husband.

[015-125]‘Certainly,’ he replied, with a smile of contemptuous irony. ‘If Hinks

[015-126]goes on, he’ll establish my [[literary-success|reputation]].’

[015-127]And he took a draught of ale, like one who is reinvigorated for the

[015-128]battle of life. Marian, regarding him askance, mused on what seemed to

[015-129]her a strange anomaly in his character; it had often surprised her that

[015-130]a man of his temperament and powers should be so dependent upon the

[015-131]praise and blame of people whom he justly deemed his inferiors.

[015-132]Yule was glancing over the pages of the work.

[015-133]‘A pity the man can’t write English.’ What a vocabulary!

[015-134]Obstruent--reliable--particularization--fabulosity--different to--averse

[015-135]to--did one ever come across such a mixture of antique pedantry and

[015-136]modern vulgarism! Surely he has his name from the German hinken--eh,

[015-137]Marian?’

[015-138]With a laugh he tossed the book away again. His mood was wholly changed.

[015-139]He gave various evidences of enjoying the meal, and began to talk freely

[015-140]with his daughter.

[015-141]‘Finished the authoresses?’

[015-142]‘Not quite.’

[015-143]‘No hurry. When you have time I want you to read Ditchley’s new book,

[015-144]and jot down a selection of his worst sentences. I’ll use them for an

[015-145]article on contemporary style; it occurred to me this afternoon.’

[015-146]He smiled grimly. Mrs Yule’s face exhibited much contentment, which

[015-147]became radiant joy when her husband remarked casually that the custard

[015-148]was very well made to-day. Dinner over, he rose without ceremony and

[015-149]went off to his study.

[015-150]The man had suffered much and toiled stupendously. It was not

[015-151]inexplicable that dyspepsia, and many another ill that literary flesh is

[015-152]heir to, racked him sore.

[015-153]Go back to the days when he was an assistant at a bookseller’s in

[015-154]Holborn. Already [[literary-ambition|ambition]] devoured him, and the genuine love of

[015-155]knowledge goaded his brain. He allowed himself but three or four hours

[015-156]of sleep; he wrought doggedly at languages, ancient and modern; he tried

[015-157]his hand at metrical translations; he planned tragedies. Practically he

[015-158]was living in a past age; his literary ideals were formed on the study

[015-159]of Boswell.

[015-160]The head assistant in the shop went away to pursue a business which

[015-161]had come into his hands on the death of a relative; it was a small

[015-162][[publishing-trade|publishing]] concern, housed in an alley off the Strand, and Mr Polo (a

[015-163]singular name, to become well known in the course of time) had his

[015-164]ideas about its possible extension. Among other instances of activity he

[015-165]started a penny weekly paper, called All Sorts, and in the pages of

[015-166]this periodical Alfred Yule first appeared as an author. Before long he

[015-167]became sub-editor of All Sorts, then actual director of the paper. He

[015-168]said good-bye to the bookseller, and his literary career fairly began.

[015-169]Mr Polo used to say that he never knew a man who could work so many

[015-170]consecutive hours as Alfred Yule. A faithful account of all that

[015-171]the young man learnt and wrote from 1855 to 1860--that is, from his

[015-172]twenty-fifth to his thirtieth year--would have the look of burlesque

[015-173]exaggeration. He had set it before him to become a celebrated man, and

[015-174]he was not unaware that the attainment of that end would cost him

[015-175]quite exceptional labour, seeing that nature had not favoured him with

[015-176]brilliant parts. No matter; his name should be spoken among men unless

[015-177]he killed himself in the struggle for success.

[015-178]In the meantime he married. Living in a garret, and supplying himself

[015-179]with the materials of his scanty meals, he was in the habit of making

[015-180]purchases at a little chandler’s shop, where he was waited upon by

[015-181]a young girl of no beauty, but, as it seemed to him, of amiable

[015-182]disposition. One holiday he met this girl as she was walking with a

[015-183]younger sister in the streets; he made her nearer acquaintance, and

[015-184]before long she consented to be his wife and share his garret. His

[015-185]brothers, John and Edmund, cried out that he had made an unpardonable

[015-186]fool of himself in marrying so much beneath him; that he might well have

[015-187]waited until his income improved. This was all very well, but they might

[015-188]just as reasonably have bidden him reject plain food because a few years

[015-189]hence he would be able to purchase luxuries; he could not do without

[015-190]nourishment of some sort, and the time had come when he could not do

[015-191]without a wife. Many a man with brains but no [[money-in-literature|money]] has been compelled

[015-192]to the same step. Educated girls have a pronounced distaste for

[015-193]London garrets; not one in fifty thousand would share [[literary-poverty|poverty]] with

[015-194]the brightest [[literary-genius|genius]] ever born. Seeing that [[literary-marriage|marriage]] is so often

[015-195]indispensable to that very success which would enable a man of parts to

[015-196]mate equally, there is nothing for it but to look below one’s own level,

[015-197]and be grateful to the untaught woman who has pity on one’s loneliness.
