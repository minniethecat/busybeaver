# Section 66

[065-001]‘You remember it very clearly.’

[065-002]‘As if I saw it now! But wait. I turned eastward, and there to my

[065-003]astonishment was a magnificent rainbow, a perfect semicircle, stretching

[065-004]from the foot of Parnes to that of Hymettus, framing Athens and its

[065-005]hills, which grew brighter and brighter--the brightness for which

[065-006]there is no name among colours. Hymettus was of a soft misty warmth, a

[065-007]something tending to purple, its ridges marked by exquisitely soft

[065-008]and indefinite shadows, the rainbow coming right down in front. The

[065-009]Acropolis simply glowed and blazed. As the sun descended all these

[065-010]colours grew richer and warmer; for a moment the landscape was nearly

[065-011]crimson. Then suddenly the sun passed into the lower stratum of cloud,

[065-012]and the splendour died almost at once, except that there remained the

[065-013]northern half of the rainbow, which had become double. In the west, the

[065-014]clouds were still glorious for a time; there were two shaped like great

[065-015]expanded wings, edged with refulgence.’

[065-016]‘Stop!’ cried [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], ‘or I shall clutch you by the throat. I warned you

[065-017]before that I can’t stand those reminiscences.’

[065-018]‘Live in hope. Scrape together twenty pounds, and go there, if you die

[065-019]of hunger afterwards.’

[065-020]‘I shall never have twenty shillings,’ was the despondent answer.

[065-021]‘I feel sure you will sell “[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]].”’

[065-022]‘It’s kind of you to encourage me; but if “Mr Bailey” is ever sold I

[065-023]don’t mind undertaking to eat my duplicate of the proofs.’

[065-024]‘But now, you remember what led me to that. What does a man care for any

[065-025]woman on earth when he is absorbed in contemplation of that kind?’

[065-026]‘But it is only one of life’s satisfactions.’

[065-027]‘I am only maintaining that it is the best, and infinitely preferable to

[065-028]sexual emotion. It leaves, no doubt, no bitterness of any kind. Poverty

[065-029]can’t rob me of those memories. I have lived in an ideal world that was

[065-030]not deceitful, a world which seems to me, when I recall it, beyond the

[065-031]human sphere, bathed in diviner light.’

[065-032]It was four or five days after this that [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]], on going to his work

[065-033]in City Road, found a note from [[carter|Carter]]. It requested him to call at

[065-034]the main hospital at half-past eleven the next morning. He supposed the

[065-035]appointment had something to do with his business at Croydon, whither

[065-036]he had been in the mean time. Some unfavourable news, perhaps; any

[065-037]misfortune was likely.

[065-038]He answered the summons punctually, and on entering the general office

[065-039]was requested by the clerk to wait in Mr Carter’s private room; the

[065-040]secretary had not yet arrived. His waiting lasted some ten minutes, then

[065-041]the door opened and admitted, not Carter, but [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]].

[065-042]Reardon stood up in perturbation. He was anything but prepared, or

[065-043]disposed, for an interview with this lady. She came towards him with

[065-044]hand extended and a countenance of suave friendliness.

[065-045]‘I doubted whether you would see me if I let you know,’ she said.

[065-046]‘Forgive me this little bit of scheming, will you? I have something so

[065-047]very important to speak to you about.’

[065-048]He said nothing, but kept a demeanour of courtesy.

[065-049]‘I think you haven’t heard from [[amy-reardon|Amy]]?’ Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]] asked.

[065-050]‘Not since I saw her.’

[065-051]‘And you don’t know what has come to pass?’

[065-052]‘I have heard of nothing.’

[065-053]‘I am come to see you quite on my own responsibility, quite. I took Mr

[065-054]Carter into my confidence, but begged him not to let Mrs Carter know,

[065-055]lest she should tell Amy; I think he will keep his promise. It seemed to

[065-056]me that it was really my duty to do whatever I could in these sad, sad

[065-057]circumstances.’

[065-058]Reardon listened respectfully, but without sign of feeling.

[065-059]‘I had better tell you at once that Amy’s uncle at [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] is

[065-060]dead, and that in his will he has bequeathed her ten thousand pounds.’

[065-061]Mrs Yule watched the effect of this. For a moment none was visible,

[065-062]but she saw at length that Reardon’s lips trembled and his eyebrows

[065-063]twitched.

[065-064]‘I am glad to hear of her good fortune,’ he said distantly and in even

[065-065]tones.

[065-066]‘You will feel, I am sure,’ continued his mother-in-law, ‘that this must

[065-067]put an end to your most unhappy differences.’

[065-068]‘How can it have that result?’

[065-069]‘It puts you both in a very different position, does it not? But for

[065-070]your distressing circumstances, I am sure there would never have been

[065-071]such unpleasantness--never. Neither you nor Amy is the kind of person to

[065-072]take a pleasure in disagreement. Let me beg you to go and see her again.

[065-073]Everything is so different now. Amy has not the faintest idea that I

[065-074]have come to see you, and she mustn’t on any account be told, for her

[065-075]worst fault is that sensitive pride of hers. And I’m sure you won’t

[065-076]be offended, Edwin, if I say that you have very much the same failing.

[065-077]Between two such sensitive people differences might last a lifetime,

[065-078]unless one could be persuaded to take the first step. Do be generous!

[065-079]A woman is privileged to be a little obstinate, it is always said.

[065-080]Overlook the fault, and persuade her to let bygones be bygones.’

[065-081]There was an involuntary affectedness in Mrs Yule’s speech which

[065-082]repelled Reardon. He could not even put faith in her assurance that

[065-083]Amy knew nothing of this intercession. In any case it was extremely

[065-084]distasteful to him to discuss such matters with Mrs Yule.

[065-085]‘Under no circumstances could I do more than I already have done,’ he

[065-086]replied. ‘And after what you have told me, it is impossible for me to go

[065-087]and see her unless she expressly invites me.’

[065-088]‘Oh, if only you would overcome this sensitiveness!’

[065-089]‘It is not in my power to do so. My [[literary-poverty|poverty]], as you justly say, was the

[065-090]cause of our parting; but if Amy is no longer poor, that is very far

[065-091]from a reason why I should go to her as a suppliant for forgiveness.’

[065-092]‘But do consider the facts of the case, independently of feeling.

[065-093]I really think I don’t go too far in saying that at least some--some

[065-094]provocation was given by you first of all. I am so very, very far from

[065-095]wishing to say anything disagreeable--I am sure you feel that--but

[065-096]wasn’t there some little ground for complaint on Amy’s part? Wasn’t

[065-097]there, now?’

[065-098]Reardon was tortured with nervousness. He wished to be alone, to think

[065-099]over what had happened, and Mrs Yule’s urgent voice rasped upon his

[065-100]ears. Its very smoothness made it worse.

[065-101]‘There may have been ground for grief and concern,’ he answered, ‘but

[065-102]for complaint, no, I think not.’

[065-103]‘But I understand’--the voice sounded rather irritable now--‘that you

[065-104]positively reproached and upbraided her because she was reluctant to go

[065-105]and live in some very shocking place.’

[065-106]‘I may have lost my temper after Amy had shown--But I can’t review our

[065-107]troubles in this way.’

[065-108]‘Am I to plead in vain?’

[065-109]‘I regret very much that I can’t possibly do as you wish. It is all

[065-110]between Amy and myself. Interference by other people cannot do any

[065-111]good.’

[065-112]‘I am sorry you should use such a word as “interference,”’ replied Mrs

[065-113]Yule, bridling a little. ‘Very sorry, indeed. I confess it didn’t occur

[065-114]to me that my good-will to you could be seen in that light.’

[065-115]‘Believe me that I didn’t use the word offensively.’

[065-116]‘Then you refuse to take any step towards a restoration of good

[065-117]feeling?’

[065-118]‘I am obliged to, and Amy would understand perfectly why I say so.’

[065-119]His earnestness was so unmistakable that Mrs Yule had no choice but

[065-120]to rise and bring the interview to an end. She commanded herself

[065-121]sufficiently to offer a regretful hand.

[065-122]‘I can only say that my daughter is very, very unfortunate.’

[065-123]Reardon lingered a little after her departure, then left the hospital

[065-124]and walked at a rapid pace in no particular direction.

[065-125]Ah! if this had happened in the first year of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]], what more

[065-126]blessed man than he would have walked the earth! But it came after

[065-127]irreparable harm. No amount of wealth could undo the ruin caused by

[065-128]poverty.

[065-129]It was natural for him, as soon as he could think with deliberation, to

[065-130]turn towards his only friend. But on calling at the house in Clipstone

[065-131]Street he found the garret empty, and no one could tell him when its

[065-132]occupant was likely to be back. He left a note, and made his way back

[065-133]to [[islington|Islington]]. The evening had to be spent at the hospital, but on his

[065-134]return Biffen sat waiting for him.

[065-135]‘You called about twelve, didn’t you?’ the visitor inquired.

[065-136]‘Half-past.’

[065-137]‘I was at the police-court. Odd thing--but it always happens so--that

[065-138]I should have spoken of Sykes the other night. Last night I came upon

[065-139]a crowd in [[oxford|Oxford]] Street, and the nucleus of it was no other than Sykes

[065-140]himself very drunk and disorderly, in the grip of two policemen. Nothing

[065-141]could be done for him; I was useless as bail; he e’en had to sleep in

[065-142]the cell. But I went this morning to see what would become of him. Such

[065-143]a spectacle when they brought him forward! It was only five shillings

[065-144]fine, and to my astonishment he produced the [[money-in-literature|money]]. I joined him

[065-145]outside--it required a little courage--and had a long talk with him.

[065-146]He’s writing a [[london|London]] Letter for some provincial daily, and the first

[065-147]payment had thrown him off his balance.’

[065-148]Reardon laughed gaily, and made inquiries about the eccentric gentleman.

[065-149]Only when the subject was exhausted did he speak of his own concerns,

[065-150]relating quietly what he had learnt from Mrs Yule. Biffen’s eyes

[065-151]widened.

[065-152]‘So,’ Reardon cried with exultation, ‘there is the last burden off my

[065-153]mind! Henceforth I haven’t a care! The only thing that still troubled

[065-154]me was my inability to give Amy enough to live upon. Now she is provided

[065-155]for in secula seculorum. Isn’t this grand news?’

[065-156]‘Decidedly. But if she is provided for, so are you.’

[065-157]‘Biffen, you know me better. Could I accept a farthing of her

[065-158]money? This has made our coming together again for ever impossible,

[065-159]unless--unless dead things can come to life. I know the value of money,

[065-160]but I can’t take it from Amy.’

[065-161]The other kept silence.

[065-162]‘No! But now everything is well. She has her child, and can devote

[065-163]herself to bringing the boy up. And I--but I shall be rich on my own

[065-164]account. A hundred and fifty a year; it would be a farce to offer

[065-165]Amy her share of it. By all the gods of Olympus, we will go to Greece

[065-166]together, you and I!’

[065-167]‘Pooh!’

[065-168]‘I swear it! Let me save for a couple of years, and then get a good

[065-169]month’s holiday, or more if possible, and, as Pallas Athene liveth!

[065-170]we shall find ourselves at Marseilles, going aboard some boat of the

[065-171]Messageries. I can’t believe yet that this is true. Come, we will have a

[065-172]supper to-night. Come out into Upper Street, and let us eat, drink, and

[065-173]be merry!’

[065-174]‘You are beside yourself. But never mind; let us rejoice by all means.

[065-175]There’s every reason.’

[065-176]‘That poor girl! Now, at last, she’ll be at ease.’

[065-177]‘Who?’

[065-178]‘Amy, of course! I’m delighted on her account. Ah! but if it had come

[065-179]a long time ago, in the happy days! Then she, too, would have gone to

[065-180]Greece, wouldn’t she? Everything in life comes too soon or too late.

[065-181]What it would have meant for her and for me! She would never have hated

[065-182]me then, never. Biffen, am I base or contemptible? She thinks so. That’s

[065-183]how poverty has served me. If you had seen her, how she looked at

[065-184]me, when we met the other day, you would understand well enough why I

[065-185]couldn’t live with her now, not if she entreated me to. That would make

[065-186]me base if you like. Gods! how ashamed I should be if I yielded to such

[065-187]a temptation! And once--’
