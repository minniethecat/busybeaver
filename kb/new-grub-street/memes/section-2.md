# Section 2

[001-001]Having finished his breakfast, he leaned back and began to unfold the

[001-002][[london|London]] paper that had come by post.

[001-003]‘Had [[edwin-reardon|Mr Reardon]] any hopes of that kind at the time of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]], do

[001-004]you think?’ inquired Mrs [[jasper-milvain|Milvain]].

[001-005]‘Reardon? Good heavens, no! Would he were capable of such forethought!’

[001-006]In a few minutes Jasper was left alone in the room. When the servant

[001-007]came to clear the table he strolled slowly away, humming a tune.

[001-008]The house was pleasantly situated by the roadside in a little village

[001-009]named Finden. Opposite stood the church, a plain, low, square-towered

[001-010]building. As it was cattle-market to-day in the town of [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]],

[001-011]droves of beasts and sheep occasionally went by, or the rattle of a

[001-012]grazier’s cart sounded for a moment. On ordinary days the road saw few

[001-013]vehicles, and pedestrians were rare.

[001-014]Mrs Milvain and her daughters had lived here for the last seven years,

[001-015]since the death of the father, who was a veterinary surgeon. The widow

[001-016]enjoyed an annuity of two hundred and forty pounds, terminable with her

[001-017]life; the children had nothing of their own. Maud acted irregularly as

[001-018]a teacher of music; [[dora-milvain|Dora]] had an engagement as visiting governess in a

[001-019]Wattleborough family. Twice a year, as a rule, Jasper came down from

[001-020]London to spend a fortnight with them; to-day marked the middle of his

[001-021]autumn visit, and the strained relations between him and his sisters

[001-022]which invariably made the second week rather trying for all in the house

[001-023]had already become noticeable.

[001-024]In the course of the morning Jasper had half an hour’s private talk

[001-025]with his mother, after which he set off to roam in the sunshine. Shortly

[001-026]after he had left the house, Maud, her domestic duties dismissed for the

[001-027]time, came into the parlour where Mrs Milvain was reclining on the sofa.

[001-028]‘Jasper wants more [[money-in-literature|money]],’ said the mother, when Maud had sat in

[001-029]meditation for a few minutes.

[001-030]‘Of course. I knew that. I hope you told him he couldn’t have it.’

[001-031]‘I really didn’t know what to say,’ returned Mrs Milvain, in a feeble

[001-032]tone of worry.

[001-033]‘Then you must leave the matter to me, that’s all. There’s no money for

[001-034]him, and there’s an end of it.’

[001-035]Maud set her features in sullen determination. There was a brief

[001-036]silence.

[001-037]‘What’s he to do, Maud?’

[001-038]‘To do? How do other people do? What do Dora and I do?’

[001-039]‘You don’t earn enough for your support, my dear.’

[001-040]‘Oh, well!’ broke from the girl. ‘Of course, if you grudge us our food

[001-041]and lodging--’

[001-042]‘Don’t be so quick-tempered. You know very well I am far from grudging

[001-043]you anything, dear. But I only meant to say that Jasper does earn

[001-044]something, you know.’

[001-045]‘It’s a disgraceful thing that he doesn’t earn as much as he needs. We

[001-046]are sacrificed to him, as we always have been. Why should we be pinching

[001-047]and stinting to keep him in idleness?’

[001-048]‘But you really can’t call it idleness, Maud. He is studying his

[001-049]profession.’

[001-050]‘Pray call it trade; he prefers it. How do I know that he’s studying

[001-051]anything? What does he mean by “studying”? And to hear him speak

[001-052]scornfully of his friend Mr Reardon, who seems to work hard all through

[001-053]the year! It’s disgusting, mother. At this rate he will never earn his

[001-054]own living. Who hasn’t seen or heard of such men? If we had another

[001-055]hundred a year, I would say nothing. But we can’t live on what he leaves

[001-056]us, and I’m not going to let you try. I shall tell Jasper plainly that

[001-057]he’s got to work for his own support.’

[001-058]Another silence, and a longer one. Mrs Milvain furtively wiped a tear

[001-059]from her cheek.

[001-060]‘It seems very cruel to refuse,’ she said at length, ‘when another year

[001-061]may give him the opportunity he’s waiting for.’

[001-062]‘Opportunity? What does he mean by his opportunity?’

[001-063]‘He says that it always comes, if a man knows how to wait.’

[001-064]‘And the people who support him may starve meanwhile! Now just think

[001-065]a bit, mother. Suppose anything were to happen to you, what becomes of

[001-066]Dora and me? And what becomes of Jasper, too? It’s the truest kindness

[001-067]to him to compel him to earn a living. He gets more and more incapable

[001-068]of it.’

[001-069]‘You can’t say that, Maud. He earns a little more each year. But for

[001-070]that, I should have my doubts. He has made thirty pounds already this

[001-071]year, and he only made about twenty-five the whole of last. We must

[001-072]be fair to him, you know. I can’t help feeling that he knows what he’s

[001-073]about. And if he does succeed, he’ll pay us all back.’

[001-074]Maud began to gnaw her fingers, a disagreeable habit she had in privacy.

[001-075]‘Then why doesn’t he live more economically?’

[001-076]‘I really don’t see how he can live on less than a hundred and fifty a

[001-077]year. London, you know--’

[001-078]‘The cheapest place in the world.’

[001-079]‘Nonsense, Maud!’

[001-080]‘But I know what I’m saying. I’ve read quite enough about such things.

[001-081]He might live very well indeed on thirty shillings a week, even buying

[001-082]his clothes out of it.’

[001-083]‘But he has told us so often that it’s no use to him to live like that.

[001-084]He is obliged to go to places where he must spend a little, or he makes

[001-085]no progress.’

[001-086]‘Well, all I can say is,’ exclaimed the girl impatiently, ‘it’s very

[001-087]lucky for him that he’s got a mother who willingly sacrifices her

[001-088]daughters to him.’

[001-089]‘That’s how you always break out. You don’t care what unkindness you

[001-090]say!’

[001-091]‘It’s a simple truth.’

[001-092]‘Dora never speaks like that.’

[001-093]‘Because she’s afraid to be honest.’

[001-094]‘No, because she has too much love for her mother. I can’t bear to talk

[001-095]to you, Maud. The older I get, and the weaker I get, the more unfeeling

[001-096]you are to me.’

[001-097]Scenes of this kind were no uncommon thing. The clash of tempers lasted

[001-098]for several minutes, then Maud flung out of the room. An hour later, at

[001-099]dinner-time, she was rather more caustic in her remarks than usual, but

[001-100]this was the only sign that remained of the stormy mood.

[001-101]Jasper renewed the breakfast-table conversation.

[001-102]‘Look here,’ he began, ‘why don’t you girls write something? I’m

[001-103]convinced you could make money if you tried. There’s a tremendous sale

[001-104]for religious stories; why not patch one together? I am quite serious.’

[001-105]‘Why don’t you do it yourself,’ retorted Maud.

[001-106]‘I can’t manage stories, as I have told you; but I think you could. In

[001-107]your place, I’d make a speciality of Sunday-school prize-books; you

[001-108]know the kind of thing I mean. They sell like hot cakes. And there’s so

[001-109]deuced little enterprise in the business. If you’d give your mind to it,

[001-110]you might make hundreds a year.’

[001-111]‘Better say “abandon your mind to it.”’

[001-112]‘Why, there you are! You’re a sharp enough girl. You can quote as well

[001-113]as anyone I know.’

[001-114]‘And please, why am I to take up an inferior kind of work?’

[001-115]‘Inferior? Oh, if you can be a George Eliot, begin at the earliest

[001-116]opportunity. I merely suggested what seemed practicable.

[001-117]But I don’t think you have [[literary-genius|genius]], Maud. People have got that ancient

[001-118]prejudice so firmly rooted in their heads--that one mustn’t write save

[001-119]at the dictation of the Holy Spirit. I tell you, writing is a business.

[001-120]Get together half-a-dozen fair specimens of the Sunday-school prize;

[001-121]study them; discover the essential points of such composition; hit upon

[001-122]new attractions; then go to work methodically, so many pages a day.

[001-123]There’s no question of the divine afflatus; that belongs to another

[001-124]sphere of life. We talk of [[literature-as-trade|literature as a trade]], not of Homer, Dante,

[001-125]and Shakespeare. If I could only get that into poor Reardon’s head. He

[001-126]thinks me a gross beast, often enough. What the devil--I mean what on

[001-127]earth is there in typography to make everything it deals with sacred?

[001-128]I don’t advocate the propagation of vicious literature; I speak only of

[001-129]good, coarse, marketable stuff for the world’s vulgar. You just give it

[001-130]a thought, Maud; talk it over with Dora.’

[001-131]He resumed presently:

[001-132]‘I maintain that we people of brains are justified in supplying the mob

[001-133]with the food it likes. We are not geniuses, and if we sit down in a

[001-134]spirit of long-eared gravity we shall produce only commonplace stuff.

[001-135]Let us use our wits to earn money, and make the best we can of our

[001-136]lives. If only I had the skill, I would produce novels out-trashing the

[001-137]trashiest that ever sold fifty thousand copies. But it needs skill, mind

[001-138]you: and to deny it is a gross error of the literary pedants. To

[001-139]please the vulgar you must, one way or another, incarnate the genius

[001-140]of vulgarity. For my own part, I shan’t be able to address the bulkiest

[001-141]multitude; my talent doesn’t lend itself to that form. I shall write for

[001-142]the upper [[social-class|middle-class]] of intellect, the people who like to feel

[001-143]that what they are reading has some special cleverness, but who can’t

[001-144]distinguish between stones and paste. That’s why I’m so slow in warming

[001-145]to the work. Every month I feel surer of myself, however.

[001-146]That last thing of mine in [[the-west-end|The West End]] distinctly hit the mark; it

[001-147]wasn’t too flashy, it wasn’t too solid. I heard fellows speak of it in

[001-148]the train.’

[001-149]Mrs Milvain kept glancing at Maud, with eyes which desired her attention

[001-150]to these utterances. None the less, half an hour after dinner, Jasper

[001-151]found himself encountered by his sister in the garden, on her face a

[001-152]look which warned him of what was coming.

[001-153]‘I want you to tell me something, Jasper. How much longer shall you look

[001-154]to mother for support? I mean it literally; let me have an idea of how

[001-155]much longer it will be.’

[001-156]He looked away and reflected.

[001-157]‘To leave a margin,’ was his reply, ‘let us say twelve months.’

[001-158]‘Better say your favourite “ten years” at once.’

[001-159]‘No. I speak by the card. In twelve months’ time, if not before, I shall

[001-160]begin to pay my debts. My dear girl, I have the honour to be a tolerably

[001-161]long-headed individual. I know what I’m about.’

[001-162]‘And let us suppose mother were to die within half a year?’

[001-163]‘I should make shift to do very well.’

[001-164]‘You? And please--what of Dora and me?’

[001-165]‘You would write Sunday-school prizes.’

[001-166]Maud turned away and left him.

[001-167]He knocked the dust out of the pipe he had been smoking, and again set

[001-168]off for a stroll along the lanes. On his countenance was just a trace

[001-169]of solicitude, but for the most part he wore a thoughtful smile. Now

[001-170]and then he stroked his smoothly-shaven jaws with thumb and fingers.

[001-171]Occasionally he became observant of wayside details--of the colour of a

[001-172]maple leaf, the shape of a tall thistle, the consistency of a fungus. At

[001-173]the few people who passed he looked keenly, surveying them from head to

[001-174]foot.

[001-175]On turning, at the limit of his walk, he found himself almost face to

[001-176]face with two persons, who were coming along in silent companionship;

[001-177]their appearance interested him. The one was a man of fifty, grizzled,

[001-178]hard featured, slightly bowed in the shoulders; he wore a grey felt hat

[001-179]with a broad brim and a decent suit of broadcloth. With him was a girl

[001-180]of perhaps two-and-twenty, in a slate-coloured dress with very little

[001-181]ornament, and a yellow straw hat of the shape originally appropriated to

[001-182]males; her dark hair was cut short, and lay in innumerable crisp curls.

[001-183]Father and daughter, obviously. The girl, to a casual eye, was neither

[001-184]pretty nor beautiful, but she had a grave and impressive face, with a

[001-185]complexion of ivory tone; her walk was gracefully modest, and she seemed

[001-186]to be enjoying the country air.
