# Section 74

[073-001][[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] was at home, and working. He had but to look at [[marian-yule|Marian]] to see

[073-002]that something wretched had been going on at her home; naturally he

[073-003]supposed it the result of his letter to [[alfred-yule|Mr Yule]].

[073-004]‘Your father has been behaving brutally,’ he said, holding her hands and

[073-005]gazing anxiously at her.

[073-006]‘There is something far worse than that, Jasper.’

[073-007]‘Worse?’

[073-008]She threw off her outdoor things, then took the fatal letter from her

[073-009]pocket and handed it to him. Jasper gave a whistle of consternation, and

[073-010]looked vacantly from the paper to Marian’s countenance.

[073-011]‘How the deuce comes this about?’ he exclaimed. ‘Why, wasn’t your uncle

[073-012]aware of the state of things?’

[073-013]‘Perhaps he was. He may have known that the legacy was a mere form.’

[073-014]‘You are the only one affected?’

[073-015]‘So father says. It’s sure to be the case.’

[073-016]‘This has upset you horribly, I can see. Sit down, Marian. When did the

[073-017]letter come?’

[073-018]‘This morning.’

[073-019]‘And you have been fretting over it all day. But come, we must keep up

[073-020]our courage; you may get something substantial out of the scoundrels

[073-021]still.’

[073-022]Even whilst he spoke his eyes wandered absently. On the last word his

[073-023]voice failed, and he fell into abstraction. Marian’s look was fixed upon

[073-024]him, and he became conscious of it. He tried to smile.

[073-025]‘What were you writing?’ she asked, making involuntary diversion from

[073-026]the calamitous theme.

[073-027]‘Rubbish for the Will-o’-the-Wisp. Listen to this paragraph about

[073-028]English concert audiences.’

[073-029]It was as necessary to him as to her to have a respite before the graver

[073-030]discussion began. He seized gladly the opportunity she offered, and read

[073-031]several pages of manuscript, slipping from one topic to another. To hear

[073-032]him one would have supposed that he was in his ordinary mood; he laughed

[073-033]at his own jokes and points.

[073-034]‘They’ll have to pay me more,’ was the remark with which he closed. ‘I

[073-035]only wanted to make myself indispensable to them, and at the end of

[073-036]this year I shall feel pretty sure of that. They’ll have to give me two

[073-037]guineas a column; by Jove! they will.’

[073-038]‘And you may hope for much more than that, mayn’t you, before long?’

[073-039]‘Oh, I shall transfer myself to a better paper presently. It seems to me

[073-040]I must be stirring to some purpose.’

[073-041]He gave her a significant look.

[073-042]‘What shall we do, Jasper?’

[073-043]‘Work and wait, I suppose.’

[073-044]‘There’s something I must tell you. Father said I had better sign that

[073-045]Harrington article myself. If I do that, I shall have a right to the

[073-046][[money-in-literature|money]], I think. It will at least be eight guineas. And why shouldn’t I

[073-047]go on writing for myself--for us? You can help me to think of subjects.’

[073-048]‘First of all, what about my letter to your father? We are forgetting

[073-049]all about it.’

[073-050]‘He refused to answer.’

[073-051]Marian avoided closer description of what had happened. It was partly

[073-052]that she felt ashamed of her father’s unreasoning wrath, and feared

[073-053]lest Jasper’s pride might receive an injury from which she in turn

[073-054]would suffer; partly that she was unwilling to pain her lover by making

[073-055]display of all she had undergone.

[073-056]‘Oh, he refused to reply! Surely that is extreme behaviour.’

[073-057]What she dreaded seemed to be coming to pass. Jasper stood rather

[073-058]stiffly, and threw his head back.

[073-059]‘You know the reason, dear. That prejudice has entered into his very

[073-060]life. It is not you he dislikes; that is impossible. He thinks of you

[073-061]only as he would of anyone connected with Mr [[fadge|Fadge]].’

[073-062]‘Well, well; it isn’t a matter of much moment. But what I have in mind

[073-063]is this. Will it be possible for you, whilst living at home, to take a

[073-064]position of independence, and say that you are going to work for your

[073-065]own profit?’

[073-066]‘At least I might claim half the money I can earn. And I was thinking

[073-067]more of--’

[073-068]‘Of what?’

[073-069]‘When I am your wife, I may be able to help. I could earn thirty or

[073-070]forty pounds a year, I think. That would pay the rent of a small house.’

[073-071]She spoke with shaken voice, her eyes fixed upon his face.

[073-072]‘But, my dear Marian, we surely oughtn’t to think of marrying so long as

[073-073]expenses are so nicely fitted as all that?’

[073-074]‘No. I only meant--’

[073-075]She faltered, and her tongue became silent as her heart sank.

[073-076]‘It simply means,’ pursued Jasper, seating himself and crossing his

[073-077]legs, ‘that I must move heaven and earth to improve my position. You

[073-078]know that my faith in myself is not small; there’s no knowing what I

[073-079]might do if I used every effort. But, upon my word, I don’t see much

[073-080]hope of our being able to marry for a year or two under the most

[073-081]favourable circumstances.’

[073-082]‘No; I quite understand that.’

[073-083]‘Can you promise to keep a little love for me all that time?’ he asked

[073-084]with a constrained smile.

[073-085]‘You know me too well to fear.’

[073-086]‘I thought you seemed a little doubtful.’

[073-087]His tone was not altogether that which makes banter pleasant between

[073-088]lovers. Marian looked at him fearfully. Was it possible for him in truth

[073-089]so to misunderstand her? He had never satisfied her heart’s desire of

[073-090]infinite love; she never spoke with him but she was oppressed with the

[073-091]suspicion that his love was not as great as hers, and, worse still, that

[073-092]he did not wholly comprehend the self-surrender which she strove to make

[073-093]plain in every word.

[073-094]‘You don’t say that seriously, Jasper?’

[073-095]‘But answer seriously.’

[073-096]‘How can you doubt that I would wait faithfully for you for years if it

[073-097]were necessary?’

[073-098]‘It mustn’t be years, that’s very certain. I think it preposterous for a

[073-099]man to hold a woman bound in that hopeless way.’

[073-100]‘But what question is there of holding me bound? Is love dependent on

[073-101]fixed engagements? Do you feel that, if we agreed to part, your love

[073-102]would be at once a thing of the past?’

[073-103]‘Why no, of course not.’

[073-104]‘Oh, but how coldly you speak, Jasper!’

[073-105]She could not breathe a word which might be interpreted as fear lest

[073-106]the change of her circumstances should make a change in his feeling.

[073-107]Yet that was in her mind. The existence of such a fear meant, of

[073-108]course, that she did not entirely trust him, and viewed his character as

[073-109]something less than noble. Very seldom indeed is a woman free from such

[073-110]doubts, however absolute her love; and perhaps it is just as rare for

[073-111]a man to credit in his heart all the praises he speaks of his beloved.

[073-112]Passion is compatible with a great many of these imperfections of

[073-113]intellectual esteem. To see more clearly into Jasper’s personality was,

[073-114]for Marian, to suffer the more intolerable dread lest she should lose

[073-115]him.

[073-116]She went to his side. Her heart ached because, in her great misery, he

[073-117]had not fondled her, and intoxicated her senses with loving words.

[073-118]‘How can I make you feel how much I love you?’ she murmured.

[073-119]‘You mustn’t be so literal, dearest. Women are so desperately

[073-120]matter-of-fact; it comes out even in their love-talk.’

[073-121]Marian was not without perception of the irony of such an opinion on

[073-122]Jasper’s lips.

[073-123]‘I am content for you to think so,’ she said. ‘There is only one fact in

[073-124]my life of any importance, and I can never lose sight of it.’

[073-125]‘Well now, we are quite sure of each other. Tell me plainly, do you

[073-126]think me capable of forsaking you because you have perhaps lost your

[073-127]money?’

[073-128]The question made her wince. If delicacy had held her tongue, it had no

[073-129]control of HIS.

[073-130]‘How can I answer that better,’ she said, ‘than by saying I love you?’

[073-131]It was no answer, and Jasper, though obtuse compared with her,

[073-132]understood that it was none. But the emotion which had prompted his

[073-133]words was genuine enough. Her touch, the perfume of her passion, had

[073-134]their exalting effect upon him. He felt in all sincerity that to forsake

[073-135]her would be a baseness, revenged by the loss of such a wife.

[073-136]‘There’s an uphill fight before me, that’s all,’ he said, ‘instead of

[073-137]the pretty smooth course I have been looking forward to. But I don’t

[073-138]fear it, Marian. I’m not the fellow to be beaten.

[073-139]You shall be my wife, and you shall have as many luxuries as if you had

[073-140]brought me a fortune.’

[073-141]‘Luxuries! Oh, how childish you seem to think me!’

[073-142]‘Not a bit of it. Luxuries are a most important part of life. I had

[073-143]rather not live at all than never possess them. Let me give you a useful

[073-144]hint; if ever I seem to you to flag, just remind me of the difference

[073-145]between these lodgings and a richly furnished house. Just hint to me

[073-146]that So-and-so, the journalist, goes about in his carriage, and can give

[073-147]his wife a box at the theatre. Just ask me, casually, how I should

[073-148]like to run over to the Riviera when [[london|London]] fogs are thickest. You

[073-149]understand? That’s the way to keep me at it like a steam-engine.’

[073-150]‘You are right. All those things enable one to live a better and fuller

[073-151]life. Oh, how cruel that I--that we are robbed in this way! You can have

[073-152]no idea how terrible a blow it was to me when I read that letter this

[073-153]morning.’

[073-154]She was on the point of confessing that she had swooned, but something

[073-155]restrained her.

[073-156]‘Your father can hardly be sorry,’ said Jasper.

[073-157]‘I think he speaks more harshly than he feels. The worst was, that until

[073-158]he got your letter he had kept hoping that I would let him have the

[073-159]money for a new review.’

[073-160]‘Well, for the present I prefer to believe that the money isn’t all

[073-161]lost. If the blackguards pay ten shillings in the pound you will get two

[073-162]thousand five hundred out of them, and that’s something. But how do you

[073-163]stand? Will your position be that of an ordinary creditor?’

[073-164]‘I am so ignorant. I know nothing of such things.’

[073-165]‘But of course your interests will be properly looked after. Put

[073-166]yourself in communication with this Mr Holden. I’ll have a look into the

[073-167]law on the subject. Let us hope as long as we can. By Jove! There’s no

[073-168]other way of facing it.’

[073-169]‘No, indeed.’

[073-170]‘[[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]] and the rest of them are safe enough, I suppose?’

[073-171]‘Oh, no doubt.’

[073-172]‘Confound them!--It grows upon one. One doesn’t take in the whole of

[073-173]such a misfortune at once. We must hold on to the last rag of hope, and

[073-174]in the meantime I’ll half work myself to death. Are you going to see the

[073-175]girls?’

[073-176]‘Not to-night. You must tell them.’

[073-177]‘[[dora-milvain|Dora]] will cry her eyes out. Upon my word, Maud’ll have to draw in her

[073-178]horns. I must frighten her into economy and hard work.’

[073-179]He again lost himself in anxious reverie.

[073-180]‘Marian, couldn’t you try your hand at fiction?’

[073-181]She started, remembering that her father had put the same question so

[073-182]recently.

[073-183]‘I’m afraid I could do nothing worth doing.’

[073-184]‘That isn’t exactly the question. Could you do anything that would sell?

[073-185]With very moderate [[literary-ambition|success]] in fiction you might make three times as much

[073-186]as you ever will by magazine pot-boilers. A girl like you. Oh, you might

[073-187]manage, I should think.’

[073-188]‘A girl like me?’

[073-189]‘Well, I mean that love-scenes, and that kind of thing, would be very

[073-190]much in your line.’

[073-191]Marian was not given to blushing; very few girls are, even on strong

[073-192]provocation. For the first time Jasper saw her cheeks colour deeply,

[073-193]and it was with anything but pleasure. His words were coarsely

[073-194]inconsiderate, and wounded her.

[073-195]‘I think that is not my work,’ she said coldly, looking away.

[073-196]‘But surely there’s no harm in my saying--’ he paused in astonishment.

[073-197]‘I meant nothing that could offend you.’
