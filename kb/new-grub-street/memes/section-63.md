# Section 63

[062-001]But, midway in the week, [[carter|Carter]] discovered how ill his clerk was.

[062-002]‘You ought to be in bed, my dear fellow, with gruel and mustard plasters

[062-003]and all the rest of it. Go home and take care of yourself--I insist upon

[062-004]it.’

[062-005]Before leaving the office, [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]] wrote a few lines to [[harold-biffen|Biffen]], whom he

[062-006]had visited on the Monday. ‘Come and see me if you can. I am down with a

[062-007]bad cold, and have to keep in for the rest of the week. All the same,

[062-008]I feel far more cheerful. Bring a new chapter of your exhilarating

[062-009]romance.’

[062-010]CHAPTER XXVI. MARRIED WOMAN’S PROPERTY

[062-011]On her return from church that Sunday [[mrs-edmund-yule|Mrs Edmund Yule]] was anxious to

[062-012]learn the result of the meeting between [[amy-reardon|Amy]] and her husband. She hoped

[062-013]fervently that Amy’s anomalous position would come to an end now that

[062-014]Reardon had the offer of something better than a mere clerkship. John

[062-015][[alfred-yule|Yule]] never ceased to grumble at his sister’s permanence in the house,

[062-016]especially since he had learnt that the [[money-in-literature|money]] sent by Reardon each month

[062-017]was not made use of; why it should not be applied for household expenses

[062-018]passed his understanding.

[062-019]‘It seems to me,’ he remarked several times, ‘that the fellow only does

[062-020]his bare duty in sending it. What is it to anyone else whether he

[062-021]lives on twelve shillings a week or twelve pence? It is his business

[062-022]to support his wife; if he can’t do that, to contribute as much to her

[062-023]support as possible. Amy’s scruples are all very fine, if she could

[062-024]afford them; it’s very nice to pay for your delicacies of feeling out of

[062-025]other people’s pockets.’

[062-026]‘There’ll have to be a formal separation,’ was the startling

[062-027]announcement with which Amy answered her mother’s inquiry as to what had

[062-028]passed.

[062-029]‘A separation? But, my dear--!’

[062-030]Mrs Yule could not express her disappointment and dismay.

[062-031]‘We couldn’t live together; it’s no use trying.’

[062-032]‘But at your age, Amy! How can you think of anything so shocking? And

[062-033]then, you know it will be impossible for him to make you a sufficient

[062-034]allowance.’

[062-035]‘I shall have to live as well as I can on the seventy-five pounds a

[062-036]year. If you can’t afford to let me stay with you for that, I must go

[062-037]into cheap lodgings in the country, like poor Mrs Butcher did.’

[062-038]This was wild talking for Amy. The interview had upset her, and for the

[062-039]rest of the day she kept apart in her own room. On the morrow Mrs Yule

[062-040]succeeded in eliciting a clear account of the conversation which had

[062-041]ended so hopelessly.

[062-042]‘I would rather spend the rest of my days in the workhouse than beg him

[062-043]to take me back,’ was Amy’s final comment, uttered with the earnestness

[062-044]which her mother understood but too well.

[062-045]‘But you are willing to go back, dear?’

[062-046]‘I told him so.’

[062-047]‘Then you must leave this to me. The Carters will let us know how things

[062-048]go on, and when it seems to be time I must see Edwin myself.’

[062-049]‘I can’t allow that. Anything you could say on your own account would be

[062-050]useless, and there is nothing to say from me.’

[062-051]Mrs Yule kept her own counsel. She had a full month before her during

[062-052]which to consider the situation, but it was clear to her that these

[062-053]young people must be brought together again. Her estimate of Reardon’s

[062-054]mental condition had undergone a sudden change from the moment when she

[062-055]heard that a respectable post was within his reach; she decided that

[062-056]he was ‘strange,’ but then all men of literary talent had marked

[062-057]singularities, and doubtless she had been too hasty in interpreting the

[062-058]peculiar features natural to a character such as his.

[062-059]A few days later arrived the news of their relative’s death at

[062-060][[wattleborough|Wattleborough]].

[062-061]This threw Mrs Yule into a commotion. At first she decided to accompany

[062-062]her son and be present at the funeral; after changing her mind twenty

[062-063]times, she determined not to go. John must send or bring back the news

[062-064]as soon as possible. That it would be of a nature sensibly to affect

[062-065]her own position, if not that of her children, she had little doubt;

[062-066]her husband had been the favourite brother of the deceased, and on that

[062-067]account there was no saying how handsome a legacy she might receive. She

[062-068]dreamt of houses in South Kensington, of social ambitions gratified even

[062-069]thus late.

[062-070]On the morning after the funeral came a postcard announcing John’s

[062-071]return by a certain train, but no scrap of news was added.

[062-072]‘Just like that irritating boy! We must go to the station to meet him.

[062-073]You’ll come, won’t you, Amy?’

[062-074]Amy readily consented, for she too had hopes, though circumstances

[062-075]blurred them. Mother and daughter were walking about the platform

[062-076]half an hour before the train was due; their agitation would have been

[062-077]manifest to anyone observing them. When at length the train rolled in

[062-078]and John was discovered, they pressed eagerly upon him.

[062-079]‘Don’t you excite yourself,’ he said gruffly to his mother. ‘There’s no

[062-080]reason whatever.’

[062-081]Mrs Yule glanced in dismay at Amy. They followed John to a cab, and took

[062-082]places with him.

[062-083]‘Now don’t be provoking, Jack. Just tell us at once.’

[062-084]‘By all means. You haven’t a penny.’

[062-085]‘I haven’t? You are joking, ridiculous boy!’

[062-086]‘Never felt less disposed to, I assure you.’

[062-087]After staring out of the window for a minute or two, he at length

[062-088]informed Amy of the extent to which she profited by her uncle’s decease,

[062-089]then made known what was bequeathed to himself. His temper grew worse

[062-090]every moment, and he replied savagely to each successive question

[062-091]concerning the other items of the will.

[062-092]‘What have you to grumble about?’ asked Amy, whose face was exultant

[062-093]notwithstanding the drawbacks attaching to her good fortune. ‘If Uncle

[062-094]Alfred receives nothing at all, and mother has nothing, you ought to

[062-095]think yourself very lucky.’

[062-096]‘It’s very easy for you to say that, with your ten thousand.’

[062-097]‘But is it her own?’ asked Mrs Yule. ‘Is it for her separate use?’

[062-098]‘Of course it is. She gets the benefit of last year’s Married Woman’s

[062-099]Property Act. The will was executed in January this year, and I dare say

[062-100]the old curmudgeon destroyed a former one.

[062-101]‘What a splendid Act of Parliament that is!’ cried Amy. ‘The only one

[062-102]worth anything that I ever heard of.’

[062-103]‘But my dear--’ began her mother, in a tone of protest. However, she

[062-104]reserved her comment for a more fitting time and place, and merely said:

[062-105]‘I wonder whether he had heard what has been going on?’

[062-106]‘Do you think he would have altered his will if he had?’ asked Amy with

[062-107]a smile of security.

[062-108]‘Why the deuce he should have left you so much in any case is more

[062-109]than I can understand,’ growled her brother. ‘What’s the use to me of

[062-110]a paltry thousand or two? It isn’t enough to invest; isn’t enough to do

[062-111]anything with.’

[062-112]‘You may depend upon it your cousin [[marian-yule|Marian]] thinks her five thousand

[062-113]good for something,’ said Mrs Yule. ‘Who was at the funeral? Don’t be

[062-114]so surly, Jack; tell us all about it. I’m sure if anyone has cause to be

[062-115]ill-tempered it’s poor me.’

[062-116]Thus they talked, amid the rattle of the cab-wheels. By when they

[062-117]reached home silence had fallen upon them, and each one was sufficiently

[062-118]occupied with private thoughts.

[062-119]Mrs Yule’s servants had a terrible time of it for the next few days. Too

[062-120]affectionate to turn her ill-temper against John and Amy, she relieved

[062-121]herself by severity to the domestic slaves, as an English matron is of

[062-122]course justified in doing. Her daughter’s position caused her even more

[062-123]concern than before; she constantly lamented to herself: ‘Oh, why didn’t

[062-124]he die before she was married!’--in which case Amy would never have

[062-125]dreamt of wedding a penniless author. Amy declined to discuss the new

[062-126]aspect of things until twenty-four hours after John’s return; then she

[062-127]said:

[062-128]‘I shall do nothing whatever until the money is paid to me. And what I

[062-129]shall do then I don’t know.’

[062-130]‘You are sure to hear from Edwin,’ opined Mrs Yule.

[062-131]‘I think not. He isn’t the kind of man to behave in that way.’

[062-132]‘Then I suppose you are bound to take the first step?’

[062-133]‘That I shall never do.’

[062-134]She said so, but the sudden happiness of finding herself wealthy was

[062-135]not without its softening effect on Amy’s feelings. Generous impulses

[062-136]alternated with moods of discontent. The thought of her husband in his

[062-137]squalid lodgings tempted her to forget injuries and disillusions, and to

[062-138]play the part of a generous wife. It would be possible now for them to

[062-139]go abroad and spend a year or two in healthful travel; the result in

[062-140]Reardon’s case might be wonderful. He might recover all the energy of

[062-141]his imagination, and resume his literary career from the point he had

[062-142]reached at the time of his [[literary-marriage|marriage]].

[062-143]On the other hand, was it not more likely that he would lapse into a

[062-144]life of scholarly self-indulgence, such as he had often told her was

[062-145]his ideal? In that event, what tedium and regret lay before her! Ten

[062-146]thousand pounds sounded well, but what did it represent in reality? A

[062-147]poor four hundred a year, perhaps; mere decency of obscure existence,

[062-148]unless her husband could glorify it by winning fame. If he did nothing,

[062-149]she would be the wife of a man who had failed in literature. She would

[062-150]not be able to take a place in society. Life would be supported without

[062-151]struggle; nothing more to be hoped.

[062-152]This view of the future possessed her strongly when, on the second day,

[062-153]she went to communicate her news to Mrs Carter. This amiable lady had

[062-154]now become what she always desired to be, Amy’s intimate friend; they

[062-155]saw each other very frequently, and conversed of most things with much

[062-156]frankness. It was between eleven and twelve in the morning when Amy paid

[062-157]her visit, and she found Mrs Carter on the point of going out.

[062-158]‘I was coming to see you,’ cried Edith. ‘Why haven’t you let me know of

[062-159]what has happened?’

[062-160]‘You have heard, I suppose?’

[062-161]‘Albert heard from your brother.’

[062-162]‘I supposed he would. And I haven’t felt in the mood for talking about

[062-163]it, even with you.’

[062-164]They went into Mrs Carter’s boudoir, a tiny room full of such pretty

[062-165]things as can be purchased nowadays by anyone who has a few shillings

[062-166]to spare, and tolerable taste either of their own or at second-hand. Had

[062-167]she been left to her instincts, Edith would have surrounded herself with

[062-168]objects representing a much earlier stage of artistic development; but

[062-169]she was quick to imitate what fashion declared becoming. Her husband

[062-170]regarded her as a remarkable authority in all matters of personal or

[062-171]domestic ornamentation.

[062-172]‘And what are you going to do?’ she inquired, examining Amy from head

[062-173]to foot, as if she thought that the inheritance of so substantial a sum

[062-174]must have produced visible changes in her friend.

[062-175]‘I am going to do nothing.’

[062-176]‘But surely you’re not in low spirits?’

[062-177]‘What have I to rejoice about?’

[062-178]They talked for a while before Amy brought herself to utter what she was

[062-179]thinking.

[062-180]‘Isn’t it a most ridiculous thing that married people who both wish to

[062-181]separate can’t do so and be quite free again?’

[062-182]‘I suppose it would lead to all sorts of troubles--don’t you think?’

[062-183]‘So people say about every new step in civilisation. What would have

[062-184]been thought twenty years ago of a proposal to make all married women

[062-185]independent of their husbands in money matters? All sorts of absurd

[062-186]dangers were foreseen, no doubt. And it’s the same now about divorce.

[062-187]In America people can get divorced if they don’t suit each other--at

[062-188]all events in some of the States--and does any harm come of it? Just the

[062-189]opposite I should think.’
