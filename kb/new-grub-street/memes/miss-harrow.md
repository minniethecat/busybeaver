# Miss Harrow

A gentlewoman who moves between the Milvain and Yule social circles in *New Grub Street*, functioning as a quiet facilitator of the relationships and social rituals that structure the novel's domestic world.

## Character Overview

Miss Harrow is a recurring minor character in the novel's early chapters, appearing primarily in the first volume (chapters P02–6) with later references in chapters 16, 19, and 52. She serves as a social link between the Milvain sisters (Maud and Dora) and the Yule household, particularly Marian Yule and Edmund Yule. Unlike the novel's literary professionals, she belongs to the domestic sphere — a gentlewoman who receives callers, visits gardens, and facilitates social introductions. Her financial connection to Edmund Yule [002-146] hints at the economic dependencies that underpin the novel's social world.

> Miss Harrow's discreet presence in the Milvain household is captured with characteristic Gissing restraint: "Miss Harrow was reticent concerning her" [002-081]. The narrative itself mirrors this reticence, leaving the precise nature of her circumstances — and her connection to Edmund Yule — deliberately ambiguous.

## Family and Background

Miss Harrow's background is never fully elaborated; she is defined by her social function rather than her personal history. She maintains her own establishment, "receiving her visitors in a small room" [002-153], which suggests modest but independent means. Her connection to Edmund Yule — "it was bestowed upon Edmund, and Miss Harrow had" [002-146] — implies some form of financial arrangement or inheritance between them, though the novel leaves the nature of this connection deliberately opaque. She is "reticent concerning her" [002-081], a phrase that captures both her discretion and the narrative's refusal to fully illuminate her circumstances.

## Career and Pursuits

Miss Harrow has no profession in the literary world that dominates *New Grub Street*. Her role is that of a gentlewoman — visiting, receiving visitors, accompanying young women on outings. She joins Marian Yule and the Milvain sisters on garden excursions [003-050][003-179], suggesting a chaperoning function appropriate to a woman of her age and respectability. A passing reference to "what Miss Harrow gets" [052-162] implies some form of regular income or allowance, possibly connected to Edmund Yule. Her idleness mirrors the predicament of the Milvain sisters, who similarly lack productive occupation and depend on social connections and marriage prospects for economic survival.

## Key Relationships

- **与 [[maud-milvain|Maud Milvain]] 和 [[dora-milvain|Dora Milvain]]**：Miss Harrow is a regular visitor to the Milvain household, where Maud and Dora receive her [002-032]. She accompanies them on outings and the sisters "now and then visited Miss Harrow" in return [002-077]. However, they "very rarely saw Mr" — presumably Edmund Yule — during these visits [002-077], suggesting the relationship between Miss Harrow and Edmund is conducted with a degree of discretion.

- **与 [[marian-yule|Marian Yule]]**：Miss Harrow facilitates Marian's integration into the Milvain social circle. She and Marian attend gatherings together [004-001][006-042], and Marian later acknowledges that "Miss Harrow has been very kind indeed" [019-144]. Miss Harrow serves as a social bridge between Marian's isolated life with her father and the wider world of the Milvains.

- **与 Edmund Yule**：The precise nature of her connection to Edmund Yule is one of the novel's deliberate ambiguities. "It was bestowed upon Edmund, and Miss Harrow had" [002-146] suggests a financial arrangement, possibly an inheritance or settlement. Her reticence on the subject [002-081] and the Milvain sisters' discretion imply a relationship that Victorian propriety would not openly discuss — perhaps a long-standing romantic attachment, a financial dependency, or both.

## Role in the Plot

Miss Harrow appears first in the preface, where a brief remark is attributed to her [P02-169]. In chapter 2, she visits the Milvains and her connection to Edmund Yule is established [002-016][002-032][002-146]. She facilitates social excursions in chapter 3, suggesting a garden outing [003-050] and later joining the party [003-179]. In chapter 4, arrangements are made for her and Marian to attend an event together [004-001], and in chapter 6 she and Marian arrive as expected [006-042]. After the first volume, her presence recedes; she is mentioned in passing in chapters 16 and 19, and a brief reference to her income appears in chapter 52 [052-162]. Her diminishing presence mirrors the novel's pivot from domestic social ritual to the harsher world of literary commerce.

## Thematic Significance

Miss Harrow embodies the quiet economic realities that underlie the novel's domestic surface. Her ambiguous financial relationship with Edmund Yule [002-146] and her modest independent establishment [002-153] illustrate the precarious economic position of unmarried gentlewomen in late-Victorian society — a theme the novel explores more dramatically through characters like Marian Yule and the Milvain sisters. Her "reticence" [002-081] reflects the Victorian code of discretion that concealed such economic arrangements from public view. In a novel obsessed with the economics of writing and marriage, Miss Harrow represents the less visible economy of inheritance, allowance, and quiet dependency that sustained the domestic sphere.
