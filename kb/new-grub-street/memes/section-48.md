# Section 48

[047-001]All the months of summer went by. [[jasper-milvain|Jasper]] kept his word, and never

[047-002]visited Mrs [[alfred-yule|Yule]]’s house; but once in July he met that lady at the

[047-003]Carters’, and heard then, what he knew from other sources, that the

[047-004]position of things was unchanged. In August, Mrs Yule spent a fortnight

[047-005]at the seaside, and [[amy-reardon|Amy]] accompanied her. Milvain and his sisters

[047-006]accepted an invitation to visit friends at [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]], and were out

[047-007]of town about three weeks, the last ten days being passed in the Isle of

[047-008]Wight; it was an extravagant holiday, but [[dora-milvain|Dora]] had been ailing, and her

[047-009]brother declared that they would all work better for the change. Alfred

[047-010]Yule, with his wife and daughter, rusticated somewhere in Kent. Dora and

[047-011][[marian-yule|Marian]] exchanged letters, and here is a passage from one written by the

[047-012]former:

[047-013]‘Jasper has shown himself in an unusually amiable light since we left

[047-014]town. I looked forward to this holiday with some misgivings, as I know

[047-015]by experience that it doesn’t do for him and us to be too much together;

[047-016]he gets tired of our company, and then his selfishness--believe me, he

[047-017]has a good deal of it--comes out in a way we don’t appreciate. But I

[047-018]have never known him so forbearing. To me he is particularly kind, on

[047-019]account of my headaches and general shakiness. It isn’t impossible that

[047-020]this young man, if all goes well with him, may turn out far better than

[047-021]Maud and I ever expected. But things will have to go very well, if the

[047-022]improvement is to be permanent. I only hope he may make a lot of [[money-in-literature|money]]

[047-023]before long. If this sounds rather gross to you, I can only say that

[047-024]Jasper’s moral nature will never be safe as long as he is exposed to

[047-025]the risks of [[literary-poverty|poverty]]. There are such people, you know. As a poor man, I

[047-026]wouldn’t trust him out of my sight; with money, he will be a tolerable

[047-027]creature--as men go.’

[047-028]Dora, no doubt, had her reasons for writing in this strain. She would

[047-029]not have made such remarks in conversation with her friend, but took the

[047-030]opportunity of being at a distance to communicate them in writing.

[047-031]On their return, the two girls made good progress with the book they

[047-032]were manufacturing for Messrs Jolly and Monk, and early in October it

[047-033]was finished. Dora was now writing little things for The English Girl,

[047-034]and Maud had begun to review an occasional novel for an illustrated

[047-035]paper. In spite of their poor lodgings, they had been brought into

[047-036]social relations with Mrs Boston Wright and a few of her friends; their

[047-037]position was understood, and in accepting invitations they had no fear

[047-038]lest unwelcome people should pounce down upon them in their shabby

[047-039]little sitting-room. The younger sister cared little for society such

[047-040]as Jasper procured them; with Marian Yule for a companion she would have

[047-041]been quite content to spend her evenings at home. But Maud relished the

[047-042]introduction to strangers. She was admired, and knew it. Prudence

[047-043]could not restrain her from buying a handsomer dress than those she had

[047-044]brought from her country home, and it irked her sorely that she might

[047-045]not reconstruct all her equipment to rival the appearance of well-to-do

[047-046]girls whom she studied and envied. Her disadvantages, for the present,

[047-047]were insuperable. She had no one to chaperon her; she could not form

[047-048]intimacies because of her poverty. A rare invitation to luncheon, a

[047-049]permission to call at the sacred hour of small-talk--this was all she

[047-050]could hope for.

[047-051]‘I advise you to possess your soul in patience,’ Jasper said to her,

[047-052]as they talked one day on the sea-shore. ‘You are not to blame that you

[047-053]live without conventional protection, but it necessitates your being

[047-054]very careful. These people you are getting to know are not rigid about

[047-055]social observances, and they won’t exactly despise you for poverty; all

[047-056]the same, their charity mustn’t be tested too severely. Be very quiet

[047-057]for the present; let it be seen that you understand that your position

[047-058]isn’t quite regular--I mean, of course, do so in a modest and nice

[047-059]way. As soon as ever it’s possible, we’ll arrange for you to live with

[047-060]someone who will preserve appearances. All this is contemptible,

[047-061]of course; but we belong to a contemptible society, and can’t help

[047-062]ourselves. For Heaven’s sake, don’t spoil your chances by rashness; be

[047-063]content to wait a little, till some more money comes in.’

[047-064]Midway in October, about half-past eight one evening, Jasper received

[047-065]an unexpected visit from Dora. He was in his sitting-room, smoking and

[047-066]reading a novel.

[047-067]‘Anything wrong?’ he asked, as his sister entered.

[047-068]‘No; but I’m alone this evening, and I thought I would see if you were

[047-069]in.

[047-070]‘Where’s Maud, then?’

[047-071]‘She went to see the Lanes this afternoon, and Mrs Lane invited her

[047-072]to go to the Gaiety to-night; she said a friend whom she had invited

[047-073]couldn’t come, and the ticket would be wasted. Maud went back to dine

[047-074]with them. She’ll come home in a cab.’

[047-075]‘Why is Mrs Lane so affectionate all at once? Take your things off; I

[047-076]have nothing to do.’

[047-077]‘Miss Radway was going as well.’

[047-078]‘Who’s Miss Radway?’

[047-079]‘Don’t you know her? She’s staying with the Lanes. Maud says she writes

[047-080]for [[the-west-end|The West End]].’

[047-081]‘And will that fellow Lane be with them?’

[047-082]‘I think not.’

[047-083]Jasper mused, contemplating the bowl of his pipe.

[047-084]‘I suppose she was in rare excitement?’

[047-085]‘Pretty well. She has wanted to go to the Gaiety for a long time.

[047-086]There’s no harm, is there?’

[047-087]Dora asked the question with that absent air which girls are wont to

[047-088]assume when they touch on doubtful subjects.

[047-089]‘Harm, no. Idiocy and lively music, that’s all. It’s too late, or I’d

[047-090]have taken you, for the joke of the thing. Confound it! she ought to

[047-091]have better dresses.’

[047-092]‘Oh, she looked very nice, in that best.’

[047-093]‘Pooh! But I don’t care for her to be running about with the Lanes. Lane

[047-094]is too big a blackguard; it reflects upon his wife to a certain extent.’

[047-095]They gossiped for half an hour, then a tap at the door interrupted them;

[047-096]it was the landlady.

[047-097]‘[[whelpdale|Mr Whelpdale]] has called to see you, sir. I mentioned as Miss Milvain

[047-098]was here, so he said he wouldn’t come up unless you sent to ask him.’

[047-099]Jasper smiled at Dora, and said in a low voice.

[047-100]‘What do you say? Shall he come up? He can behave himself.’

[047-101]‘Just as you please, Jasper.’

[047-102]‘Ask him to come up, Mrs Thompson, please.’

[047-103]Mr Whelpdale presented himself. He entered with much more ceremony than

[047-104]when Milvain was alone; on his visage was a grave respectfulness, his

[047-105]step was light, his whole bearing expressed diffidence and pleasurable

[047-106]anticipation.

[047-107]‘My younger sister, Whelpdale,’ said Jasper, with subdued amusement.

[047-108]The dealer in literary advice made a bow which did him no discredit, and

[047-109]began to speak in a low, reverential tone not at all disagreeable to the

[047-110]ear. His breeding, in truth, had been that of a gentleman, and it was

[047-111]only of late years that he had fallen into the hungry region of New Grub

[047-112]Street.

[047-113]‘How’s the “Manual” going off?’ Milvain inquired.

[047-114]‘Excellently! We have sold nearly six hundred.’

[047-115]‘My sister is one of your readers. I believe she has studied the book

[047-116]with much conscientiousness.’

[047-117]‘Really? You have really read it, Miss Milvain?’

[047-118]Dora assured him that she had, and his delight knew no bounds.

[047-119]‘It isn’t all rubbish, by any means,’ said Jasper, graciously. ‘In the

[047-120]chapter on writing for magazines, there are one or two very good hints.

[047-121]What a pity you can’t apply your own advice, Whelpdale!’

[047-122]‘Now that’s horribly unkind of you!’ protested the other. ‘You might

[047-123]have spared me this evening. But unfortunately it’s quite true, Miss

[047-124]Milvain. I point the way, but I haven’t been able to travel it myself.

[047-125]You mustn’t think I have never succeeded in getting things published;

[047-126]but I can’t keep it up as a profession.

[047-127]Your brother is the successful man. A marvellous facility! I envy him.

[047-128]Few men at present writing have such talent.’

[047-129]‘Please don’t make him more conceited than he naturally is,’ interposed

[047-130]Dora.

[047-131]‘What news of [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]?’ asked Jasper, presently.

[047-132]‘He says he shall finish “[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]], Grocer,” in about a month. He read

[047-133]me one of the later chapters the other night. It’s really very fine;

[047-134]most remarkable writing, it seems to me. It will be scandalous if he

[047-135]can’t get it published; it will, indeed.’

[047-136]‘I do hope he may!’ said Dora, laughing. ‘I have heard so much of “Mr

[047-137]Bailey,” that it will be a great disappointment if I am never to read

[047-138]it.’

[047-139]‘I’m afraid it would give you very little pleasure,’ Whelpdale replied,

[047-140]hesitatingly. ‘The matter is so very gross.’

[047-141]‘And the hero grocer!’ shouted Jasper, mirthfully. ‘Oh, but it’s quite

[047-142]decent; only rather depressing. The decently ignoble--or, the ignobly

[047-143]decent? Which is Biffen’s formula? I saw him a week ago, and he looked

[047-144]hungrier than ever.’

[047-145]‘Ah, but poor [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]! I passed him at King’s Cross not long ago.

[047-146]He didn’t see me--walks with his eyes on the ground always--and I hadn’t

[047-147]the courage to stop him. He’s the ghost of his old self. He can’t live

[047-148]long.’

[047-149]Dora and her brother exchanged a glance. It was a long time since Jasper

[047-150]had spoken to his sisters about the Reardons; nowadays he seldom heard

[047-151]either of husband or wife.

[047-152]The conversation that went on was so agreeable to Whelpdale, that he

[047-153]lost consciousness of time. It was past eleven o’clock when Jasper felt

[047-154]obliged to remind him.

[047-155]‘Dora, I think I must be taking you home.’

[047-156]The visitor at once made ready for departure, and his leave-taking was

[047-157]as respectful as his entrance had been. Though he might not say what

[047-158]he thought, there was very legible upon his countenance a hope that he

[047-159]would again be privileged to meet Miss Dora Milvain.

[047-160]‘Not a bad fellow, in his way,’ said Jasper, when Dora and he were alone

[047-161]again.

[047-162]‘Not at all.’

[047-163]She had heard the story of Whelpdale’s hapless wooing half a year ago,

[047-164]and her recollection of it explained the smile with which she spoke.

[047-165]‘Never get on, I’m afraid,’ Jasper pursued. ‘He has his allowance of

[047-166]twenty pounds a year, and makes perhaps fifty or sixty more. If I were

[047-167]in his position, I should go in for some kind of regular business; he

[047-168]has people who could help him. Good-natured fellow; but what’s the use

[047-169]of that if you’ve no money?’

[047-170]They set out together, and walked to the girls’ lodgings. Dora was about

[047-171]to use her latch-key, but Jasper checked her. ‘No. There’s a light in

[047-172]the kitchen still; better knock, as we’re so late.’

[047-173]‘But why?’

[047-174]‘Never mind; do as I tell you.’

[047-175]The landlady admitted them, and Jasper spoke a word or two with her,

[047-176]explaining that he would wait until his elder sister’s return; the

[047-177]darkness of the second-floor windows had shown that Maud was not yet

[047-178]back.

[047-179]‘What strange fancies you have!’ remarked Dora, when they were upstairs.

[047-180]‘So have people in general, unfortunately.’

[047-181]A letter lay on the table. It was addressed to Maud, and Dora recognised

[047-182]the handwriting as that of a Wattleborough friend.

[047-183]‘There must be some news here,’ she said. ‘Mrs Haynes wouldn’t write

[047-184]unless she had something special to say.

[047-185]Just upon midnight, a cab drew up before the house. Dora ran down to

[047-186]open the door to her sister, who came in with very bright eyes and more

[047-187]colour than usual on her cheeks.

[047-188]‘How late for you to be here!’ she exclaimed, on entering the

[047-189]sitting-room and seeing Jasper.

[047-190]‘I shouldn’t have felt comfortable till I knew that you were back all

[047-191]right.’

[047-192]‘What fear was there?’

[047-193]She threw off her wraps, laughing.
