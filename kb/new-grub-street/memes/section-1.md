[P02-047]PART I.

[P02-048]CHAPTER I. A MAN OF HIS DAY

[P02-049]As the Milvains sat down to breakfast the clock of [[wattleborough|Wattleborough]] parish

[P02-050]church struck eight; it was two miles away, but the strokes were borne

[P02-051]very distinctly on the west wind this autumn morning. Jasper, listening

[P02-052]before he cracked an egg, remarked with cheerfulness:

[P02-053]‘There’s a man being hanged in [[london|London]] at this moment.’

[P02-054]‘Surely it isn’t necessary to let us know that,’ said his sister Maud,

[P02-055]coldly.

[P02-056]‘And in such a tone, too!’ protested his sister [[dora-milvain|Dora]].

[P02-057]‘Who is it?’ inquired Mrs Milvain, looking at her son with pained

[P02-058]forehead.

[P02-059]‘I don’t know. It happened to catch my eye in the paper yesterday that

[P02-060]someone was to be hanged at Newgate this morning. There’s a certain

[P02-061]satisfaction in reflecting that it is not oneself.’

[P02-062]‘That’s your selfish way of looking at things,’ said Maud.

[P02-063]‘Well,’ returned Jasper, ‘seeing that the fact came into my head, what

[P02-064]better use could I make of it? I could curse the brutality of an age

[P02-065]that sanctioned such things; or I could grow doleful over the misery of

[P02-066]the poor fellow. But those emotions would be as little profitable to

[P02-067]others as to myself. It just happened that I saw the thing in a light of

[P02-068]consolation. Things are bad with me, but not so bad as THAT. I might be

[P02-069]going out between Jack Ketch and the Chaplain to be hanged; instead of

[P02-070]that, I am eating a really fresh egg, and very excellent buttered toast,

[P02-071]with coffee as good as can be reasonably expected in this part of the

[P02-072]world.--(Do try boiling the milk, mother.)--The tone in which I spoke

[P02-073]was spontaneous; being so, it needs no justification.’

[P02-074]He was a young man of five-and-twenty, well built, though a trifle

[P02-075]meagre, and of pale complexion. He had hair that was very nearly black,

[P02-076]and a clean-shaven face, best described, perhaps, as of bureaucratic

[P02-077]type. The clothes he wore were of expensive material, but had seen a

[P02-078]good deal of service. His stand-up collar curled over at the corners,

[P02-079]and his necktie was lilac-sprigged.

[P02-080]Of the two sisters, Dora, aged twenty, was the more like him in visage,

[P02-081]but she spoke with a gentleness which seemed to indicate a different

[P02-082]character. Maud, who was twenty-two, had bold, handsome features, and

[P02-083]very beautiful hair of russet tinge; hers was not a face that readily

[P02-084]smiled. Their mother had the look and manners of an invalid, though she

[P02-085]sat at table in the ordinary way. All were dressed as ladies, though

[P02-086]very simply. The room, which looked upon a small patch of garden, was

[P02-087]furnished with old-fashioned comfort, only one or two objects suggesting

[P02-088]the decorative spirit of 1882.

[P02-089]‘A man who comes to be hanged,’ pursued Jasper, impartially, ‘has

[P02-090]the satisfaction of knowing that he has brought society to its last

[P02-091]resource. He is a man of such fatal importance that nothing will serve

[P02-092]against him but the supreme effort of law. In a way, you know, that is

[P02-093][[literary-ambition|success]].’

[P02-094]‘In a way,’ repeated Maud, scornfully.

[P02-095]‘Suppose we talk of something else,’ suggested Dora, who seemed to fear

[P02-096]a conflict between her sister and Jasper.

[P02-097]Almost at the same moment a diversion was afforded by the arrival of the

[P02-098]post. There was a letter for Mrs Milvain, a letter and newspaper for

[P02-099]her son. Whilst the girls and their mother talked of unimportant news

[P02-100]communicated by the one correspondent, Jasper read the missive addressed

[P02-101]to himself.

[P02-102]‘This is from Reardon,’ he remarked to the younger girl. ‘Things are

[P02-103]going badly with him. He is just the kind of fellow to end by poisoning

[P02-104]or shooting himself.’

[P02-105]‘But why?’

[P02-106]‘Can’t get anything done; and begins to be sore troubled on his wife’s

[P02-107]account.’

[P02-108]‘Is he ill?’

[P02-109]‘Overworked, I suppose. But it’s just what I foresaw. He isn’t the

[P02-110]kind of man to keep up literary production as a paying business. In

[P02-111]favourable circumstances he might write a fairly good book once every

[P02-112]two or three years. The failure of his last depressed him, and now he

[P02-113]is struggling hopelessly to get another done before the winter season.

[P02-114]Those people will come to grief.’

[P02-115]‘The enjoyment with which he anticipates it!’ murmured Maud, looking at

[P02-116]her mother.

[P02-117]‘Not at all,’ said Jasper. ‘It’s true I envied the fellow, because he

[P02-118]persuaded a handsome girl to believe in him and share his risks, but I

[P02-119]shall be very sorry if he goes to the--to the dogs. He’s my one serious

[P02-120]friend. But it irritates me to see a man making such large demands upon

[P02-121]fortune. One must be more modest--as I am. Because one book had a sort

[P02-122]of [[literary-success|success]] he imagined his struggles were over. He got a hundred

[P02-123]pounds for “[[on-neutral-ground|On Neutral Ground]],” and at once counted on a continuance

[P02-124]of payments in geometrical proportion. I hinted to him that he couldn’t

[P02-125]keep it up, and he smiled with tolerance, no doubt thinking “He judges

[P02-126]me by himself.” But I didn’t do anything of the kind.--(Toast, please,

[P02-127]Dora.)--I’m a stronger man than Reardon; I can keep my eyes open, and

[P02-128]wait.’

[P02-129]‘Is his wife the kind of person to grumble?’ asked Mrs Milvain.

[P02-130]‘Well, yes, I suspect that she is. The girl wasn’t content to go into

[P02-131]modest rooms--they must furnish a flat. I rather wonder he didn’t start

[P02-132]a carriage for her. Well, his next book brought only another hundred,

[P02-133]and now, even if he finishes this one, it’s very doubtful if he’ll get

[P02-134]as much. “The Optimist” was practically a failure.’

[P02-135]‘Mr Yule may leave them some [[money-in-literature|money]],’ said Dora.

[P02-136]‘Yes. But he may live another ten years, and he would see them both in

[P02-137][[marylebone|Marylebone]] Workhouse before he advanced sixpence, or I’m much mistaken

[P02-138]in him. Her mother has only just enough to live upon; can’t possibly

[P02-139]help them. Her brother wouldn’t give or lend twopence halfpenny.’

[P02-140]‘Has Mr Reardon no relatives!’

[P02-141]‘I never heard him make mention of a single one. No, he has done the

[P02-142]fatal thing. A man in his position, if he marry at all, must take

[P02-143]either a work-girl or an heiress, and in many ways the work-girl is

[P02-144]preferable.’

[P02-145]‘How can you say that?’ asked Dora. ‘You never cease talking about the

[P02-146]advantages of money.’

[P02-147]‘Oh, I don’t mean that for ME the work-girl would be preferable; by

[P02-148]no means; but for a man like Reardon. He is absurd enough to be

[P02-149]conscientious, likes to be called an “artist,” and so on. He might

[P02-150]possibly earn a hundred and fifty a year if his mind were at rest, and

[P02-151]that would be enough if he had married a decent little dressmaker. He

[P02-152]wouldn’t desire superfluities, and the quality of his work would be its

[P02-153]own reward. As it is, he’s ruined.’

[P02-154]‘And I repeat,’ said Maud, ‘that you enjoy the prospect.’

[P02-155]‘Nothing of the kind. If I seem to speak exultantly it’s only because

[P02-156]my intellect enjoys the clear perception of a fact.--A little marmalade,

[P02-157]Dora; the home-made, please.’

[P02-158]‘But this is very sad, Jasper,’ said Mrs Milvain, in her half-absent

[P02-159]way. ‘I suppose they can’t even go for a holiday?’

[P02-160]‘Quite out of the question.’

[P02-161]‘Not even if you invited them to come here for a week?’

[P02-162]‘Now, mother,’ urged Maud, ‘THAT’S impossible, you know very well.’

[P02-163]‘I thought we might make an effort, dear. A holiday might mean

[P02-164]everything to him.’

[P02-165]‘No, no,’ fell from Jasper, thoughtfully. ‘I don’t think you’d get

[P02-166]along very well with [[amy-reardon|Mrs Reardon]]; and then, if her uncle is coming to Mr

[P02-167]Yule’s, you know, that would be awkward.’

[P02-168]‘I suppose it would; though those people would only stay a day or two,

[P02-169]Miss Harrow said.’

[P02-170]‘Why can’t Mr Yule make them friends, those two lots of people?’ asked

[P02-171]Dora. ‘You say he’s on good terms with both.’

[P02-172]‘I suppose he thinks it’s no business of his.’

[P02-173]Jasper mused over the letter from his friend.

[P02-174]‘Ten years hence,’ he said, ‘if Reardon is still alive, I shall be

[P02-175]lending him five-pound notes.’

[P02-176]A smile of irony rose to Maud’s lips. Dora laughed.

[P02-177]‘To be sure! To be sure!’ exclaimed their brother. ‘You have no faith.

[P02-178]But just understand the difference between a man like Reardon and a man

[P02-179]like me. He is the old type of unpractical artist; I am the literary man

[P02-180]of 1882. He won’t make concessions, or rather, he can’t make them;

[P02-181]he can’t supply the market. I--well, you may say that at present I

[P02-182]do nothing; but that’s a great mistake, I am learning my business.

[P02-183]Literature nowadays is a trade. Putting aside [[literary-genius|men of genius]], who may

[P02-184]succeed by mere cosmic force, your successful man of letters is your

[P02-185]skilful tradesman. He thinks first and foremost of the markets; when one

[P02-186]kind of goods begins to go off slackly, he is ready with something new

[P02-187]and appetising. He knows perfectly all the possible sources of income.

[P02-188]Whatever he has to sell he’ll get payment for it from all sorts of

[P02-189]various quarters; none of your unpractical selling for a lump sum to a

[P02-190]middleman who will make six distinct profits. Now, look you: if I had

[P02-191]been in Reardon’s place, I’d have made four hundred at least out of

[P02-192]“The Optimist”; I should have gone shrewdly to work with magazines and

[P02-193]newspapers and foreign [[publishing-trade|publishers]], and--all sorts of people. Reardon

[P02-194]can’t do that kind of thing, he’s behind his age; he sells a manuscript

[P02-195]as if he lived in Sam Johnson’s [[grub-street|Grub Street]]. But our Grub Street of

[P02-196]to-day is quite a different place: it is supplied with telegraphic

[P02-197]communication, it knows what literary fare is in demand in every part of

[P02-198]the world, its inhabitants are men of business, however seedy.’

[P02-199]‘It sounds ignoble,’ said Maud.

[P02-200]‘I have nothing to do with that, my dear girl. Now, as I tell you, I am

[P02-201]slowly, but surely, learning the business. My line won’t be novels;

[P02-202]I have failed in that direction, I’m not cut out for the work. It’s a

[P02-203]pity, of course; there’s a great deal of money in it. But I have plenty

[P02-204]of scope. In ten years, I repeat, I shall be making my thousand a year.’

[P02-205]‘I don’t remember that you stated the exact sum before,’ Maud observed.

[P02-206]‘Let it pass. And to those who have shall be given. When I have a decent

[P02-207]income of my own, I shall marry a woman with an income somewhat larger,

[P02-208]so that casualties may be provided for.’

[P02-209]Dora exclaimed, laughing:

[P02-210]‘It would amuse me very much if the Reardons got a lot of money at Mr

[P02-211]Yule’s death--and that can’t be ten years off, I’m sure.’

[P02-212]‘I don’t see that there’s any chance of their getting much,’ replied

[P02-213]Jasper, meditatively. ‘Mrs Reardon is only his niece. The man’s brother

[P02-214]and sister will have the first helping, I suppose. And then, if it comes

[P02-215]to the second generation, the literary Yule has a daughter, and by her

[P02-216]being invited here I should think she’s the favourite niece. No, no;

[P02-217]depend upon it they won’t get anything at all.’
