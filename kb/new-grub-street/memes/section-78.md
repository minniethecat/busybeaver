# Section 78

[077-001]Two days later, early in the evening, the friends were again enjoying

[077-002]each other’s company in [[edwin-reardon|Reardon]]’s room. Both were invalids, for [[harold-biffen|Biffen]]

[077-003]had of course caught a cold from his exposure in shirt-sleeves on the

[077-004]roof, and he was suffering from the shock to his nerves; but the thought

[077-005]that his novel was safe in the hands of [[publishing-trade|publishers]] gave him energy to

[077-006]resist these influences. The absence of the pipe, for neither had any

[077-007]palate for tobacco at present, was the only external peculiarity of

[077-008]this meeting. There seemed no reason why they should not meet frequently

[077-009]before the parting which would come at Christmas; but Reardon was in a

[077-010]mood of profound sadness, and several times spoke as if already he were

[077-011]bidding his friend farewell.

[077-012]‘I find it difficult to think,’ he said, ‘that you will always struggle

[077-013]on in such an existence as this. To every man of mettle there does come

[077-014]an opportunity, and it surely is time for yours to present itself. I

[077-015]have a superstitious faith in “[[mr-bailey|Mr Bailey]].” If he leads you to triumph,

[077-016]don’t altogether forget me.’

[077-017]‘Don’t talk nonsense.’

[077-018]‘What ages it seems since that day when I saw you in the library at

[077-019]Hastings, and heard you ask in vain for my book! And how grateful I was

[077-020]to you! I wonder whether any mortal ever asks for my books nowadays?

[077-021]Some day, when I am well established at Croydon, you shall go to

[077-022]Mudie’s, and make inquiry if my novels ever by any chance leave the

[077-023]shelves, and then you shall give me a true and faithful report of the

[077-024]answer you get. “He is quite forgotten,” the attendant will say; be sure

[077-025]of it.’

[077-026]‘I think not.’

[077-027]‘To have had even a small [[literary-success|reputation]], and to have outlived it, is a

[077-028]sort of anticipation of death. The man Edwin Reardon, whose name was

[077-029]sometimes spoken in a tone of interest, is really and actually dead. And

[077-030]what remains of me is resigned to that. I have an odd fancy that it will

[077-031]make death itself easier; it is as if only half of me had now to die.’

[077-032]Biffen tried to give a lighter turn to the gloomy subject.

[077-033]‘Thinking of my fiery adventure,’ he said, in his tone of dry

[077-034]deliberation, ‘I find it vastly amusing to picture you as a witness at

[077-035]the inquest if I had been choked and consumed. No doubt it would have

[077-036]been made known that I rushed upstairs to save some particular piece of

[077-037]property--several people heard me say so--and you alone would be able to

[077-038]conjecture what this was. Imagine the gaping wonderment of the coroner’s

[077-039]jury! The Daily Telegraph would have made a leader out of me. “This poor

[077-040]man was so strangely deluded as to the value of a novel in manuscript,

[077-041]which it appears he had just completed, that he positively sacrificed

[077-042]his life in the endeavour to rescue it from the flames.” And

[077-043]the Saturday would have had a column of sneering jocosity on the

[077-044]irrepressibly sanguine temperament of authors. At all events, I should

[077-045]have had my day of fame.’

[077-046]‘But what an ignoble death it would have been!’ he pursued. ‘Perishing

[077-047]in the garret of a lodging-house which caught fire by the overturning of

[077-048]a drunkard’s lamp! One would like to end otherwise.’

[077-049]‘Where would you wish to die?’ asked Reardon, musingly.

[077-050]‘At home,’ replied the other, with pathetic emphasis. ‘I have never had

[077-051]a home since I was a boy, and am never likely to have one. But to die at

[077-052]home is an unreasoning hope I still cherish.’

[077-053]‘If you had never come to [[london|London]], what would you have now been?’

[077-054]‘Almost certainly a schoolmaster in some small town. And one might be

[077-055]worse off than that, you know.’

[077-056]‘Yes, one might live peaceably enough in such a position. And I--I

[077-057]should be in an estate-agent’s office, earning a sufficient salary, and

[077-058]most likely married to some unambitious country girl.

[077-059]I should have lived an intelligible life, instead of only trying to

[077-060]live, aiming at modes of life beyond my reach. My mistake was that of

[077-061]numberless men nowadays. Because I was conscious of brains, I thought

[077-062]that the only place for me was London. It’s easy enough to understand

[077-063]this common delusion. We form our ideas of London from old literature;

[077-064]we think of London as if it were still the one centre of intellectual

[077-065]life; we think and talk like Chatterton. But the truth is that

[077-066]intellectual men in our day do their best to keep away from London--when

[077-067]once they know the place. There are libraries everywhere; papers and

[077-068]magazines reach the north of Scotland as soon as they reach Brompton;

[077-069]it’s only on rare occasions, for special kinds of work, that one is

[077-070]bound to live in London. And as for recreation, why, now that no English

[077-071]theatre exists, what is there in London that you can’t enjoy in almost

[077-072]any part of England? At all events, a yearly visit of a week would be

[077-073]quite sufficient for all the special features of the town. London is

[077-074]only a huge shop, with an hotel on the upper storeys. To be sure, if you

[077-075]make it your artistic subject, that’s a different thing. But neither you

[077-076]nor I would do that by deliberate choice.’

[077-077]‘I think not.’

[077-078]‘It’s a huge misfortune, this will-o’-the-wisp attraction exercised

[077-079]by London on young men of brains. They come here to be degraded, or to

[077-080]perish, when their true sphere is a life of peaceful remoteness. The

[077-081]type of man capable of [[literary-ambition|success]] in London is more or less callous and

[077-082]cynical. If I had the training of boys, I would teach them to think of

[077-083]London as the last place where life can be lived worthily.’

[077-084]‘And the place where you are most likely to die in squalid

[077-085]wretchedness.’

[077-086]‘The one happy result of my experiences,’ said Reardon, ‘is that they

[077-087]have cured me of ambition. What a miserable fellow I should be if I were

[077-088]still possessed with the desire to make a name! I can’t even recall

[077-089]very clearly that state of mind. My strongest desire now is for peaceful

[077-090]obscurity. I am tired out; I want to rest for the remainder of my life.’

[077-091]‘You won’t have much rest at Croydon.’

[077-092]‘Oh, it isn’t impossible. My time will be wholly occupied in a round of

[077-093]all but mechanical duties, and I think that will be the best medicine

[077-094]for my mind. I shall read very little, and that only in the classics.

[077-095]I don’t say that I shall always be content in such a position; in a few

[077-096]years perhaps something pleasanter will offer. But in the meantime

[077-097]it will do very well. Then there is our expedition to Greece to look

[077-098]forward to. I am quite in earnest about that. The year after next, if we

[077-099]are both alive, assuredly we go.’

[077-100]‘The year after next.’ Biffen smiled dubiously.

[077-101]‘I have demonstrated to you mathematically that it is possible.’

[077-102]‘You have; but so are a great many other things that one does not dare

[077-103]to hope for.’

[077-104]Someone knocked at the door, opened it, and said:

[077-105]The friends looked at each other, as if some fear had entered the minds

[077-106]of both. Reardon opened the despatch. It was from his wife, and ran

[077-107]thus:

[077-108]‘Willie is ill of diphtheria. Please come to us at once. I am staying

[077-109]with Mrs [[carter|Carter]], at her mother’s, at [[brighton|Brighton]].’

[077-110]The full address was given.

[077-111]‘You hadn’t heard of her going there?’ said Biffen, when he had read the

[077-112]lines.

[077-113]‘No. I haven’t seen Carter for several days, or perhaps he would

[077-114]have told me. Brighton, at this time of year? But I believe there’s

[077-115]a fashionable “season” about now, isn’t there? I suppose that would

[077-116]account for it.’

[077-117]He spoke in a slighting tone, but showed increasing agitation.

[077-118]‘Of course you will go?’

[077-119]‘I must. Though I’m in no condition for making a journey.’

[077-120]His friend examined him anxiously.

[077-121]‘Are you feverish at all this evening?’

[077-122]Reardon held out a hand that the other might feel his pulse. The beat

[077-123]was rapid to begin with, and had been heightened since the arrival of

[077-124]‘But go I must. The poor little fellow has no great place in my heart,

[077-125]but, when [[amy-reardon|Amy]] sends for me, I must go. Perhaps things are at the worst.’

[077-126]‘When is there a train? Have you a time table?’

[077-127]Biffen was despatched to the nearest shop to purchase one, and in the

[077-128]meanwhile Reardon packed a few necessaries in a small travelling-bag,

[077-129]ancient and worn, but the object of his affection because it had

[077-130]accompanied him on his wanderings in the South. When Harold returned,

[077-131]his appearance excited Reardon’s astonishment--he was white from head to

[077-132]foot.

[077-133]‘Snow?’

[077-134]‘It must have been falling heavily for an hour or more.’

[077-135]‘Can’t be helped; I must go.’

[077-136]The nearest station for departure was London Bridge, and the next train

[077-137]left at 7.20. By Reardon’s watch it was now about five minutes to seven.

[077-138]‘I don’t know whether it’s possible,’ he said, in confused hurry, ‘but I

[077-139]must try. There isn’t another train till ten past nine. Come with me to

[077-140]the station, Biffen.’

[077-141]Both were ready. They rushed from the house, and sped through the soft,

[077-142]steady fall of snowflakes into Upper Street. Here they were several

[077-143]minutes before they found a disengaged cab. Questioning the driver,

[077-144]they learnt what they would have known very well already but for their

[077-145]excitement: impossible to get to London Bridge Station in a quarter of

[077-146]an hour.

[077-147]‘Better to go on, all the same,’ was Reardon’s opinion. ‘If the snow

[077-148]gets deep I shall perhaps not be able to have a cab at all. But you had

[077-149]better not come; I forgot that you are as much out of sorts as I am.’

[077-150]‘How can you wait a couple of hours alone? In with you!’

[077-151]‘Diphtheria is pretty sure to be fatal to a child of that age, isn’t

[077-152]it?’ Reardon asked when they were speeding along City Road.

[077-153]‘I’m afraid there’s much danger.’

[077-154]‘Why did she send?’

[077-155]‘What an absurd question! You seem to have got into a thoroughly morbid

[077-156]state of mind about her. Do be human, and put away your obstinate

[077-157]folly.’

[077-158]‘In my position you would have acted precisely as I have done. I have

[077-159]had no choice.’

[077-160]‘I might; but we have both of us too little practicality. The art

[077-161]of living is the art of compromise. We have no right to foster

[077-162]sensibilities, and conduct ourselves as if the world allowed of ideal

[077-163]relations; it leads to misery for others as well as ourselves. Genial

[077-164]coarseness is what it behoves men like you and me to cultivate. Your

[077-165]reply to your wife’s last letter was preposterous. You ought to have

[077-166]gone to her of your own accord as soon as ever you heard she was

[077-167]rich; she would have thanked you for such common-sense disregard of

[077-168]delicacies. Let there be an end of this nonsense, I implore you!’

[077-169]Reardon stared through the glass at the snow that fell thicker and

[077-170]thicker.

[077-171]‘What are we--you and I?’ pursued the other. ‘We have no belief in

[077-172]immortality; we are convinced that this life is all; we know that human

[077-173]happiness is the origin and end of all moral considerations. What

[077-174]right have we to make ourselves and others miserable for the sake of an

[077-175]obstinate idealism? It is our duty to make the best of circumstances.

[077-176]Why will you go cutting your loaf with a razor when you have a

[077-177]serviceable bread-knife?’

[077-178]Still Reardon did not speak. The cab rolled on almost silently.

[077-179]‘You love your wife, and this summons she sends is proof that her

[077-180]thought turns to you as soon as she is in distress.’
