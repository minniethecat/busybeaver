# Section 56

[055-001]He rustled over some slips of manuscript, whilst [[marian-yule|Marian]] regarded him

[055-002]anxiously.

[055-003]‘Now, I appeal to you, Marian,’ he continued: ‘could I possibly save

[055-004][[money-in-literature|money]] out of an income that has never exceeded two hundred and fifty

[055-005]pounds, and often--I mean even in latter years--has been much less?’

[055-006]‘I don’t see how you could.’

[055-007]‘In one way, of course, I have managed it. My life is insured for five

[055-008]hundred pounds. But that is no provision for possible disablement. If I

[055-009]could no longer earn money with my pen, what would become of me?’

[055-010]Marian could have made an encouraging reply, but did not venture to

[055-011]utter her thoughts.

[055-012]‘Sit down,’ said her father. ‘You are not to work for a few days, and I

[055-013]myself shall be none the worse for a morning’s rest. Poor old [[hinks|Hinks]]!

[055-014]I suppose we shall help him among us, somehow. [[quarmby|Quarmby]], of course, is

[055-015]comparatively flourishing. Well, we have been companions for a quarter

[055-016]of a century, we three. When I first met Quarmby I was a [[grub-street|Grub Street]]

[055-017]gazetteer, and I think he was even poorer than I. A life of toil! A life

[055-018]of toil!’

[055-019]‘That it has been, indeed.’

[055-020]‘By-the-bye’--he threw an arm over the back of his chair--‘what did

[055-021]you think of our imaginary review, the thing we were talking about last

[055-022]night?’

[055-023]‘There are so many periodicals,’ replied Marian, doubtfully.

[055-024]‘So many? My dear child, if we live another ten years we shall see the

[055-025]number trebled.’

[055-026]‘Is it desirable?’

[055-027]‘That there should be such growth of periodicals? Well, from one point

[055-028]of view, no. No doubt they take up the time which some people would

[055-029]give to solid literature. But, on the other hand, there’s a far greater

[055-030]number of people who would probably not read at all, but for the

[055-031]temptations of these short and new articles; and they may be induced to

[055-032]pass on to substantial works. Of course it all depends on the quality of

[055-033]the periodical matter you offer. Now, magazines like’--he named two or

[055-034]three of popular stamp--‘might very well be dispensed with, unless one

[055-035]regards them as an alternative to the talking of scandal or any other

[055-036]vicious result of total idleness. But such a monthly as we projected

[055-037]would be of distinct literary value. There can be no doubt that someone

[055-038]or other will shortly establish it.’

[055-039]‘I am afraid,’ said Marian, ‘I haven’t so much sympathy with literary

[055-040]undertakings as you would like me to have.’

[055-041]Money is a great fortifier of self-respect. Since she had become really

[055-042]conscious of her position as the owner of five thousand pounds, Marian

[055-043]spoke with a steadier voice, walked with firmer step; mentally she felt

[055-044]herself altogether a less dependent being. She might have confessed this

[055-045]lukewarmness towards literary enterprise in the anger which her father

[055-046]excited eight or nine days ago, but at that time she could not have

[055-047]uttered her opinion calmly, deliberately, as now. The smile which

[055-048]accompanied the words was also new; it signified deliverance from

[055-049]pupilage.

[055-050]‘I have felt that,’ returned her father, after a slight pause to command

[055-051]his voice, that it might be suave instead of scornful. ‘I greatly fear

[055-052]that I have made your life something of a martyrdom----’

[055-053]‘Don’t think I meant that, father. I am speaking only of the general

[055-054]question. I can’t be quite so zealous as you are, that’s all. I love

[055-055]books, but I could wish people were content for a while with those we

[055-056]already have.’

[055-057]‘My dear Marian, don’t suppose that I am out of sympathy with you here.

[055-058]Alas! how much of my work has been mere drudgery, mere labouring for a

[055-059]livelihood! How gladly I would have spent much more of my time among

[055-060]the great authors, with no thought of making money of them! If I speak

[055-061]approvingly of a scheme for a new periodical, it is greatly because of

[055-062]my necessities.’

[055-063]He paused and looked at her. Marian returned the look.

[055-064]‘You would of course write for it,’ she said.

[055-065]‘Marian, why shouldn’t I edit it? Why shouldn’t it be your property?’

[055-066]‘My property--?’

[055-067]She checked a laugh. There came into her mind a more disagreeable

[055-068]suspicion than she had ever entertained of her father. Was this

[055-069]the meaning of his softened behaviour? Was he capable of calculated

[055-070]hypocrisy? That did not seem consistent with his character, as she knew

[055-071]it.

[055-072]‘Let us talk it over,’ said [[alfred-yule|Yule]]. He was in visible agitation and his

[055-073]voice shook. ‘The idea may well startle you at first. It will seem to

[055-074]you that I propose to make away with your property before you have even

[055-075]come into possession of it.’ He laughed. ‘But, in fact, what I have in

[055-076]mind is merely an investment for your capital, and that an admirable

[055-077]one. Five thousand pounds at three per cent.--one doesn’t care to reckon

[055-078]on more--represents a hundred and fifty a year. Now, there can be very

[055-079]little doubt that, if it were invested in literary property such as I

[055-080]have in mind, it would bring you five times that interest, and before

[055-081]long perhaps much more. Of course I am now speaking in the roughest

[055-082]outline. I should have to get trustworthy advice; complete and detailed

[055-083]estimates would be submitted to you. At present I merely suggest to you

[055-084]this form of investment.’

[055-085]He watched her face eagerly, greedily. When Marian’s eyes rose to his he

[055-086]looked away.

[055-087]‘Then, of course,’ she said, ‘you don’t expect me to give any decided

[055-088]answer.’

[055-089]‘Of course not--of course not. I merely put before you the chief

[055-090]advantages of such an investment. As I am a selfish old fellow, I’ll

[055-091]talk about the benefit to myself first of all. I should be editor of the

[055-092]new review; I should draw a stipend sufficient to all my needs--quite

[055-093]content, at first, to take far less than another man would ask, and to

[055-094]progress with the advance of the periodical. This position would enable

[055-095]me to have done with mere drudgery; I should only write when I felt

[055-096]called to do so--when the spirit moved me.’ Again he laughed, as though

[055-097]desirous of keeping his listener in good humour. ‘My eyes would be

[055-098]greatly spared henceforth.’

[055-099]He dwelt on that point, waiting its effect on Marian. As she said

[055-100]nothing he proceeded:

[055-101]‘And suppose I really were doomed to lose my sight in the course of a

[055-102]few years, am I wrong in thinking that the proprietor of this periodical

[055-103]would willingly grant a small annuity to the man who had firmly

[055-104]established it?’

[055-105]‘I see the force of all that,’ said Marian; ‘but it takes for granted

[055-106]that the periodical will be successful.’

[055-107]‘It does. In the hands of a publisher like [[jedwood|Jedwood]]--a vigorous man of

[055-108]the new school--its [[literary-ambition|success]] could scarcely be doubtful.’

[055-109]‘Do you think five thousand pounds would be enough to start such a

[055-110]review?’

[055-111]‘Well, I can say nothing definite on that point. For one thing, the

[055-112]coat must be made according to the cloth; expenditure can be largely

[055-113]controlled without endangering [[literary-success|success]]. Then again, I think Jedwood

[055-114]would take a share in the venture. These are details. At present I only

[055-115]want to familiarise you with the thought that an investment of this sort

[055-116]will very probably offer itself to you.’

[055-117]‘It would be better if we called it a speculation,’ said Marian, smiling

[055-118]uneasily.

[055-119]Her one object at present was to oblige her father to understand that

[055-120]the suggestion by no means lured her. She could not tell him that what

[055-121]he proposed was out of the question, though as yet that was the light in

[055-122]which she saw it. His subtlety of approach had made her feel justified

[055-123]in dealing with him in a matter-of-fact way. He must see that she was

[055-124]not to be cajoled. Obviously, and in the nature of the case, he was

[055-125]urging a proposal in which he himself had all faith; but Marian knew

[055-126]his judgment was far from infallible. It mitigated her sense of behaving

[055-127]unkindly to reflect that in all likelihood this disposal of her money

[055-128]would be the worst possible for her own interests, and therefore for

[055-129]his. If, indeed, his dark forebodings were warranted, then upon her

[055-130]would fall the care of him, and the steadiness with which she faced that

[055-131]responsibility came from a hope of which she could not speak.

[055-132]‘Name it as you will,’ returned her father, hardly suppressing a note of

[055-133]irritation. ‘True, every commercial enterprise is a speculation. But let

[055-134]me ask you one question, and beg you to reply frankly. Do you distrust

[055-135]my ability to conduct this periodical?’

[055-136]She did. She knew that he was not in touch with the interests of the

[055-137]day, and that all manner of considerations akin to the prime end of

[055-138]selling his review would make him an untrustworthy editor.

[055-139]But how could she tell him this?

[055-140]‘My opinion would be worthless,’ she replied.

[055-141]‘If Jedwood were disposed to put confidence in me, you also would?’

[055-142]‘There’s no need to talk of that now, father. Indeed, I can’t say

[055-143]anything that would sound like a promise.’

[055-144]He flashed a glance at her. Then she was more than doubtful?

[055-145]‘But you have no objection, Marian, to talk in a friendly way of a

[055-146]project that would mean so much to me?’

[055-147]‘But I am afraid to encourage you,’ she replied, frankly. ‘It is

[055-148]impossible for me to say whether I can do as you wish, or not.’

[055-149]‘Yes, yes; I perfectly understand that. Heaven forbid that I should

[055-150]regard you as a child to be led independently of your own views and

[055-151]wishes! With so large a sum of money at stake, it would be monstrous

[055-152]if I acted rashly, and tried to persuade you to do the same. The matter

[055-153]will have to be most gravely considered.’

[055-154]‘Yes.’ She spoke mechanically.

[055-155]‘But if only it should come to something! You don’t know what it would

[055-156]mean to me, Marian.’

[055-157]‘Yes, father; I know very well how you think and feel about it.’

[055-158]‘Do you?’ He leaned forward, his features working under stress of

[055-159]emotion. ‘If I could see myself the editor of an influential review, all

[055-160]my bygone toils and sufferings would be as nothing; I should rejoice in

[055-161]them as the steps to this triumph. Meminisse juvabit! My dear, I am not

[055-162]a man fitted for subordinate places. My nature is framed for authority.

[055-163]The failure of all my undertakings rankles so in my heart that sometimes

[055-164]I feel capable of every brutality, every meanness, every hateful

[055-165]cruelty. To you I have behaved shamefully. Don’t interrupt me, Marian.

[055-166]I have treated you abominably, my child, my dear daughter--and all the

[055-167]time with a full sense of what I was doing. That’s the punishment of

[055-168]faults such as mine. I hate myself for every harsh word and angry look I

[055-169]have given you; at the time, I hated myself!’

[055-170]‘Father--’

[055-171]‘No, no; let me speak, Marian. You have forgiven me; I know it. You were

[055-172]always ready to forgive, dear. Can I ever forget that evening when I

[055-173]spoke like a brute, and you came afterwards and addressed me as if the

[055-174]wrong had been on your side? It burns in my memory. It wasn’t I who

[055-175]spoke; it was the demon of failure, of humiliation. My enemies sit

[055-176]in triumph, and scorn at me; the thought of it is infuriating. Have I

[055-177]deserved this? Am I the inferior of--of those men who have succeeded

[055-178]and now try to trample on me? No! I am not! I have a better brain and a

[055-179]better heart!’

[055-180]Listening to this strange outpouring, Marian more than forgave the

[055-181]hypocrisy of the last day or two. Nay, could it be called hypocrisy? It

[055-182]was only his better self declared at the impulse of a passionate hope.

[055-183]‘Why should you think so much of these troubles, father? Is it such a

[055-184]great matter that narrow-minded people triumph over you?’
