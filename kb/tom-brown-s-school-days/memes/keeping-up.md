# Keeping-Up

The informal initiation ritual practiced by older boys at Rugby, in which newcomers were tossed repeatedly in a blanket — a tradition suspended between rough welcome and outright bullying, whose moral status depended entirely on whether the victim consented.

## Definition

Keeping-up, or tossing, was the practice of seizing a boy — typically a newcomer — and throwing him repeatedly into the air from a stretched blanket. It could be festive, even affectionate, when the boy submitted willingly and his peers were good-natured; it could be violent and terrifying when forced on an unwilling victim. The practice epitomized the ambiguity of public school traditions: the same act could be a rite of passage or an act of cruelty, depending on the spirit in which it was performed.

(006-080)

## How It Worked at Rugby

The ritual typically occurred in the evening, in the hall or a study, when masters were absent. A group of older boys would seize the newcomer, stretch a blanket between them, and toss him — sometimes gently, sometimes violently — while the room looked on. The victim's response determined the outcome: a boy who took it cheerfully earned respect; a boy who struggled or complained risked a rougher handling and social contempt.

(006-080)

### Consent and Coercion

The moral line ran through consent. Old Brooke's famous speech to the Schoolhouse drew it clearly: "I'll be hanged if we'll toss anyone against their will. No more bullying" (006-061). A boy who submitted to keeping-up as part of joining the community was participating in tradition; a boy who was tossed against his will was being bullied. The distinction was everything, and the novel insists that the boys themselves could and should enforce it.

(006-061)

(006-030)

### The Praepostor's Intervention

The cry "the praepostor of the room is coming" was the signal for the tossing to stop (006-080). The praepostor's authority, when exercised justly, protected newcomers from the tradition's darker version. A good praepostor permitted keeping-up as harmless fun but intervened when it crossed the line into cruelty — embodying the novel's argument that character, not rules, was the ultimate safeguard.

(006-080)

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold did not directly address keeping-up, but his philosophy governed its place in the school's moral economy. By cultivating a sixth form that understood the difference between tradition and tyranny, he created the conditions in which keeping-up could be a harmless custom rather than a tool of oppression. When the sixth form failed — as under Flashman's influence — keeping-up became indistinguishable from bullying.

(006-030)

## The Boys' View

For most boys, keeping-up was simply part of school life — an ordeal to be endured, a story to be retold, a shared experience that marked one's entry into the community. Tom's first encounter with the tradition, interrupted by the praepostor's arrival, was a mild version that left him shaken but not traumatized. For boys less fortunate — those tossed against their will by a hostile group — the experience could be genuinely frightening.

(006-080)

## Significance in the Novel

Keeping-up is the novel's most vivid illustration of the principle that institutions are morally neutral — their character depends on the character of those who operate them. The same blanket, in different hands, could welcome or terrorize. The novel's argument is that the answer to ambiguous traditions is not abolition but cultivation: raise the moral standard of the boys who practice them, and the traditions themselves will be transformed.

(006-061)
