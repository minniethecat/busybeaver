# Calling-Over

The twice-daily roll-call that structured the rhythm of school life at Rugby — a ritual of assembly, accountability, and communal order overseen by the praepostor of the week and the master in the great schoolroom.

## Definition

Calling-over was the formal attendance ritual held in the great school before dinner (early afternoon) and again before locking-up (evening). Every boy in the school assembled, the praepostor of the week called each name in order of form seniority — beginning with the sixth form — and each boy answered "here" before filing out. It was at once a practical mechanism of accountability and a daily enactment of the school's hierarchy.

(005-051)

## How It Worked at Rugby

The ritual was rigidly choreographed. The master ascended the high desk by the door; the praepostor of the week stood beside him on the steps; the other three praepostors marched the aisles with their canes, calling "Silence, silence!" The sixth form assembled nearest the door, the upper school behind them, and the lower school at the far end. Names were called in strict order of seniority — a public reaffirmation of each boy's place in the social order.

### Before Dinner Calling-Over

The midday calling-over was preceded by the gathering of boys from across the close. As three o'clock approached, cricket and football gave way to the muster: "Presently more boys and bigger came out, and boys from other houses on their way to calling-over, and more balls were sent for. The crowd thickened as three o'clock approached; and when the hour struck, one hundred and fifty boys were hard at work" (005-047). The transition from play to discipline was itself part of the ritual — East warned Tom, "You won't be so eager to get into calling-over after a month" (005-049).

### Evening Calling-Over

The evening calling-over at quarter-to-ten was the day's final act of order. After prayers, the boys assembled once more in the great school for the last roll-call before locking-up. It marked the boundary between the governed day and the unsupervised night — a ritual of closure that sent the boys to their dormitories under the formal acknowledgment of the school's authority.

(005-032)

## The Doctor's View

For Dr. Arnold, calling-over was more than attendance-taking — it was an instrument of moral formation. The daily assembly of the entire school, proceeding in orderly silence from sixth form to lowest fag, taught every boy his place in a divinely ordained hierarchy. The master's presence on the high desk, the praepostor's voice calling names, the answering "here" — together they formed a liturgy of school life that was as much about character as about accounting.

## The Boys' View

To Tom Brown, on his first day, calling-over was an adventure — "Yes, come along; nobody'll say anything," said East, leading him into the great school (005-049). But the novelty quickly wore thin. The lower-school boys exploited every moment of inattention: when the praepostors were "small and not well up to their work," the ten minutes before names were called became a free-for-all of acorn-pelting (005-053). For the fags, calling-over was the daily moment of being accounted for — literally, as Tom felt when his name, newly added at the bottom of the list, was called for the first time (007-017).

## Significance in the Novel

Calling-over represents the school's answer to chaos — the ritual that transformed a mob of boys into an ordered community. It appears at key narrative moments: Tom's first calling-over marks his formal entry into school society; the evening calling-over after the fight signals the restoration of order; the Doctor's use of the ritual to address the school reinforces its symbolic weight. In the novel's moral economy, the daily act of answering "here" is the boy's acknowledgment that he belongs to something larger than himself.
