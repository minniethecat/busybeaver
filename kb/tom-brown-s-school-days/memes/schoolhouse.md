# Schoolhouse

The great gray building at the heart of [[rugby-chapel|Rugby School]] — residence of the [[the-doctor|headmaster]] and boarding house to [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]], [[harry-east|Harry East]], [[arthur|Arthur]], [[old-brooke|Old Brooke]], and [[flashman|Flashman]], whose corridors and [[the-studies|studies]] contained the novel's entire moral drama.

> Tom's heart beat quick as he passed the great schoolfield or close, with its noble elms, in which several games at football were going on, and tried to take in at once the long line of gray buildings, beginning with the chapel, and ending with the Schoolhouse, the residence of the headmaster, where the great flag was lazily waving from the highest round tower.
>
> (005-003)

## Geography & Description

The Schoolhouse formed one end of the architectural line that defined [[rugby-school|Rugby]]: "the long line of gray buildings, beginning with [[rugby-chapel|the chapel]], and ending with the Schoolhouse, the residence of the [[the-doctor|headmaster]]" (005-003). It contained the [[the-hall|great hall]] where the boys ate and where [[old-brooke|Brooke]] delivered his speech, the [[the-studies|studies]] where [[praepostor|praepostors]] governed and [[fagging|fags]] served, the dormitories where boys slept and talked after lights-out, and the passages where the daily drama of school life unfolded.

## Life in Schoolhouse

Schoolhouse was more than a building — it was a society. Its fifteen lower-school boys took tea presided over by the old verger; the [[praepostor|praepostors]] governed from their [[the-studies|studies]]; the [[fagging|fags]] ran errands through its passages. Here [[old-brooke|Brooke]] delivered his famous speech after the Schoolhouse match, here [[flashman|Flashman]] roasted [[fagging|fags]] before the fire, here [[arthur|Arthur]] knelt to pray in the shared [[the-studies|study]], and here [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]] spent eight years being transformed from a raw country boy into a [[gentleman-ideal|Christian gentleman]].

> The lower-school boys of the Schoolhouse, some fifteen in number, had tea in the lower-fifth school, and were presided over by the old verger or head-porter. Each boy had a quarter of a loaf of bread and pat of butter, and as much tea as he pleased.
>
> (006-015)

## Significance

Schoolhouse is the novel's primary [[the-stage-coach|stage]] — the en[[the-close|close]]d world within which boyhood is lived, tested, and formed. It is simultaneously a home ([[the-doctor|the Doctor]]'s residence, the boys' [[the-dormitory|dormitory]]), a school (the [[the-studies|studies]], the [[the-hall|hall]]), and a battleground (the passages where [[flashman|Flashman]] tyrannized, the [[the-studies|study]] where [[arthur|Arthur]] knelt). Its architecture — from the great flag on the highest tower to the dark passages below — maps the moral geography of the novel itself.
