## Overview

The School Gates at [[rugby-school|Rugby]] are the formal boundary between the en[[the-close|close]]d world of [[the-great-school|the school]] and the town beyond. They are the first architectural feature a new boy encounters on arrival and the last he passes through on departure. Throughout *[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s School Days*, the gates function as a symbolic threshold — a point of passage marking transitions between childhood and maturity, home and school, the protected and the exposed.

## Arrival

[[tom-brown|Tom]] first sees [[rugby-school|the School]] Gates from the top of the [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] [[the-stage-coach|coach]]. The guard `carried them along the side of [[the-great-school|the school]] [[the-close|close]], round Dead-man's corner, past [[the-great-school|the school]]-gates, and down the High Street to the Spread Eagle` (005-002). [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s `heart beat quick as he passed the [[the-great-school|great school]]field or [[the-close|close]], with its noble elms` (005-003). The gates frame his first glimpse of [[the-great-school|the school]] that will shape him.

[[harry-east|East]], as cicerone, `carried [[tom-brown|Tom]] through the great gates, where were only two or three boys` who subject him to the ritual interrogation: `"You fellow, what's your name? Where do you come from? How old are you? Where do you board?" and, 'What form are you in?'` (005-018). Passing through the gates is an immediate induction into [[rugby-school|Rugby]]'s social order.

## Departures and Arrivals

The gates are the staging-point for end-of-term departures. `Outside [[rugby-school|the School]]-gates were drawn up several chaises and the four-horse [[the-stage-coach|coach]] which [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s party had chartered, the postboys in their best jackets and saddles, and a cornopean player, hired for the occasion, marching up and down` (007-090). Boys `wrapped in greatcoats and mufflers were swallowing hasty mouthfuls, rushing about, tumbling over luggage` (007-090). The cornopean plays `"Drops of Brandy," in allusion, probably, to the slight potations in which the musician and postboys had been already indulging` (007-096). Coaches race one another to Dunchurch, the boys on the roofs `exchanging volleys of peas` (007-099).

## As Threshold and Witness

The gates are also a place of surveillance and consequence. When [[tom-brown|Tom]] is caught poaching by the keeper, his companions watch his humiliating return: `As they passed [[rugby-school|the School]]-gates, [[tadpole|the Tadpole]] and several others who were standing there caught the situation` (009-078). [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s disgrace is public, witnessed at the very threshold of his school world.

[[harry-east|East]] stands by the gates to deliver his parting warning to a boy from a rival house who has been poaching [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] [[fagging|fags]]: `he opened the door and sent the young [[gentleman-ideal|gentleman]] flying into the [[the-quad|quad]]rangle with a parting kick` (011-019). The gates mark the defended boundary of house territory.

## Tom's Return

The gates appear most poignantly in the novel's final chapter, when [[tom-brown|Tom]], now a young man at Oxford, returns to [[rugby-school|Rugby]] for the last time:

> `As he walked up to the town, he felt shy and afraid of being seen, and took the back streets — why, he didn't know, but he followed his instinct. At the School-gates he made a dead pause; there was not a soul in the quadrangle — all was lonely, and silent, and sad` (018-024).

The silence at the gates — the absence of boys, the emptiness of the [[the-quad|quad]]rangle beyond — embodies [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s sense of loss. He is no longer a schoolboy, and the gates that once welcomed him now hold him at a distance. He passes through them anyway, `with another effort`, to walk the [[the-close|close]] one final time.
