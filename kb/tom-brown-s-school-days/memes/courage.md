# Courage

The cardinal virtue of the public school boy — encompassing both physical bravery in fights and on the football field, and the deeper moral courage required to stand alone for what is right against the pressure of one's peers.

## Definition

Courage at Rugby was a layered concept. At its most visible, it was physical — the willingness to take and give hard knocks in football, to face a stronger opponent in a fight, to endure pain without complaint. But the novel insistently develops a deeper form: moral courage, the willingness to defy the crowd, to refuse to participate in bullying, to stand by an unpopular friend, to confess a fault. It is this second courage — rarer and harder — that marks the truly good man.

## How It Worked at Rugby

The school's daily life was a continuous test of courage. Football tested physical courage; the fight tested courage under direct threat; the fagging rebellion tested the courage to defy authority; and Arthur's quiet endurance of persecution tested the courage of patience and forgiveness. Each form of courage built on the others, and a boy who failed one test found every subsequent test harder.

### Physical Courage: The Football Field

The football match was courage's proving ground. Old Brooke, "the colour of mother earth from shoulder to ankle," leading the Schoolhouse bulldogs into the mass of the opposing School, embodied the physical courage the game demanded and celebrated (005-061). Tom's first match, watching and then joining, was his initiation into this code — learning that courage meant continuing when exhausted, hitting hard but fairly, and never complaining.

(005-061)

### Moral Courage: The Fagging Rebellion

Tom and East's strike against unlawful fagging required a rarer courage — the courage to defy the established order. "The cause was righteous — the result had been triumphant to a great extent; but the best of causes have to fight for supremacy in a wicked world" (009-030). Moral courage, unlike physical courage, brought no cheers from the crowd; it brought isolation and retaliation. Yet it was this courage that the novel prized most highly.

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold's sermons were, at their core, arguments for the primacy of moral over physical courage. The courage to confess, to forgive, to stand alone — these were the virtues he sought to cultivate in the sixth form. His influence on Tom, culminating in their final conversation, is the triumph of this vision: the boy who began school believing courage meant never backing down from a fight comes to understand that the truest courage is moral.

(007-020)

## The Boys' View

For the boys, physical courage was the entry ticket to respect. But the novel shows that they also recognized moral courage when they saw it. Arthur, the frail and gentle newcomer, possessed no physical courage — yet his quiet refusal to participate in the cruelties of school life won him a respect that brute strength could not command. East's loyalty to Tom through their shared trials was another form of courage — the courage of steadfast friendship.

(006-061)

## Significance in the Novel

Courage is the novel's central virtue because it is the gateway to all others. Without courage, honour is empty; without courage, kindness is cowardice; without courage, faith is sentimentality. Hughes's argument — and Arnold's — was that the public school's great gift was the cultivation of courage, and that a nation governed by men who had learned courage as boys would be a nation fit to lead.
