# The Infirmary

The school sick-room — the setting for the novel's most emotionally intense passages, where [[arthur|Arthur]]'s fever brought [[tom-brown|Tom]] to prayer and [[the-doctor|Dr. Arnold]] to a bedside vigil.

## Overview

- **Category**: School building / medical facility
- **Location**: [[rugby-school|Rugby School]], Warwickshire
- **Part of**: [[rugby-school|Rugby School]]
- **Chapters**: 016–017

(016-092)

## Description

The infirmary was [[the-great-school|the school]]'s medical ward, a quiet space set apart from the noise and energy of ordinary school life. During [[arthur|Arthur]]'s illness, it became a sacred space — the place where [[tom-brown|Tom]] first prayed in earnest, where [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] sat through the night watching over a boy who might die, where [[the-great-school|the school]]'s whole moral purpose was concentrated into the simple act of caring for the sick.

(016-092)

## Role in the Narrative

[[arthur|Arthur]]'s illness and the vigil in the infirmary form the novel's crisis — the moment when [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s transformation from spirited boy to [[gentleman-ideal|Christian gentleman]] becomes irreversible. It is also the novel's most powerful portrait of [[the-doctor|Dr. Arnold]]: the great [[the-doctor|headmaster]], sitting through the night at a boy's bedside, embodying in action the love he preached from the pulpit.
