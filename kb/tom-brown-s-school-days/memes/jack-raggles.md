# Jack Raggles

Jack Raggles is the long-stop on [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] cricket eleven, described by the narrator as the "toughest and burliest" member of the team. He appears exclusively in Chapter 17, "[[toms-last-match|Tom Brown's Last Match]]," where his robust spirit, unorthodox batting style, and post-match antics provide a vivid portrait of the hearty, unreflective schoolboy athleticism that the novel celebrates.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Jack Raggles
- **Age**: Upper-school boy, older and physically developed
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]
- **Role**: Cricketer for [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] Eleven; long-stop
- **Family**: Not mentioned
- **First appearance**: Chapter 17 (017-015)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 17 (017-108)

## Character

### Cricket and School Spirit

Jack Raggles first appears in the [[mary|Mary]]lebone match, cheering [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]]'s prospects: "Huzza for old [[rugby-school|Rugby]]!" sings out Jack Raggles, the long-stop, toughest and burliest of the eleven" (017-015). His enthusiastic cry captures [[the-great-school|the school]] patriotism that animates the cricket scenes and, by extension, the novel's broader celebration of [[rugby-school|Rugby School]].

### Batting at the Crisis

Raggles's batting is memorably individual. When he presents himself at the wicket during the tense final phase of [[schoolhouse-match|the match]], the narrator describes him with vivid particularity: "Jack Raggles, with his sleeves tucked up above his great brown elbows, scorning pads and gloves, has presented himself at the wicket" (017-069). His refusal of protective gear — pads and gloves — signals a rough, almost pre-modern toughness that distinguishes him from more refined players. He scores a run off a forward drive from [[johnson|Johnson]] before facing his own ball with "only twenty-four runs to make, and four wickets to go down" (017-069).

### Comic Relief

After [[schoolhouse-match|the match]], Raggles provides the comic entertainment at the eleven's celebration: "Jack Raggles shouting comic songs and performing feats of strength" (017-108). This brief postscript rounds out his character — not merely a cricketer but a personality, the kind of boy whose physical presence and good humour make him a natural centre of schoolboy revelry.

### Role in the Narrative

Raggles embodies a particular ideal of muscular, good-natured boyhood that the novel admires without elevating to heroic status. Unlike [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]], who undergoes moral development, or [[arthur|Arthur]], who represents spiritual influence, Raggles is presented as Nature's schoolboy — strong, cheerful, and uncomplicated. His presence in the cricket match diversifies the portrait of [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] character and adds texture to the novel's depiction of school life.
