Mrs. Wixie is the matron at [[rugby-school|Rugby School]]house, responsible for the domestic management of the boys' boarding life. Known interchangeably as "Mrs. Wixie" and "[[mary|Mary]]," she is one of the few female figures within the all-male world of [[the-great-school|the school]]. Though her appearance in the novel is brief, she represents the domestic order and maternal care that underpins school life.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Mrs. Wixie (called [[mary|Mary]] by the boys)
- **Age/Form**: Adult (staff, not a pupil)
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] (staff)
- **Role**: Matron — manages boys' linen, pigeonholes, and domestic arrangements
- **Family**: None mentioned
- **First appearance**: Chapter I, Part II: How the Tide Turned (016-024)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter I, Part II: How the Tide Turned (016-024)

## Character & Personality

Mrs. Wixie is described as "methodical, active, little dark-eyed woman" who efficiently stows away the boys' linen into individual pigeonholes at the start of each half (016-024). She is evidently well-liked by the boys, who greet her with boisterous affection on their return to school. The narrator notes she is "called indifferently by either name" — Mrs. Wixie or [[mary|Mary]] — suggesting a familiar, almost familial relationship between the matron and her charges.

Despite the boys' high-spirited clamouring — shouting questions about [[the-studies|studies]], sleeping arrangements, and school gossip — Mrs. Wixie maintains a brisk, affectionate authority: "Bless the boys! ... why, you'll shake me to death. There, now, do go away up to the housekeeper's room and get your suppers" (016-024). Her character combines maternal warmth with the practical competence required to manage a houseful of schoolboys.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Wixie appears in the opening scene of Part II, Chapter I, as [[tom-brown|Tom]], [[harry-east|East]], and another [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] boy return for a new half-year. The scene establishes the rhythm of school life: the arrival by [[the-stage-coach|coach]], the rush to see familiar faces, the clamour for news and privileges. Mrs. Wixie is the fixed point in this returning tide — the adult who keeps the domestic machinery running while the boys swirl around her.

Her role in the broader narrative is functional but significant. She represents the invisible labour of school life — the linen sorted, the pigeonholes assigned, the domestic order that allows the boys' education and adventures to proceed. Her presence also provides a rare feminine touch in the predominantly masculine world of [[rugby-school|Rugby]], a reminder of the maternal care the boys have left behind at home.

## Significance

Mrs. Wixie, though a minor character, embodies the domestic foundation of school life. Her methodical pigeonhole system, her brisk affection, and her dual identity as "Mrs. Wixie" and "[[mary|Mary]]" speak to the peculiar intimacy of boarding-school domesticity — she is at once a figure of institutional order and maternal warmth. In a novel that celebrates masculine camaraderie and athletic heroism, Mrs. Wixie and figures like [[sally-harrowell]] (the tuck-shop keeper) provide the necessary counterpoint of domestic care, the quiet competence that makes the boys' world possible.
