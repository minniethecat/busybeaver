# The War of Independence

The rebellion of the lower-school fags against the tyranny of Flashman and the fifth-form bullies — a schoolboy insurrection that tested the courage of Tom Brown and East, proved the power of collective resistance, and marked the moral low point of the Schoolhouse before its eventual reform.

## Definition

The War of Independence was the name given by Hughes — borrowing the language of the American Revolution — to the organised refusal of the lower-school boys to submit to illegal fagging imposed by the fifth form. It was triggered by a collapse in legitimate authority: after old Brooke and the right-minded sixth-form boys left the Schoolhouse, the praepostorship fell to weak or corrupt boys who either could not or would not govern. Into this vacuum stepped Flashman and his associates, who began to fag the small boys as if they were praepostors, "and to bully and oppress any who showed signs of resistance" (008-016).

The rebellion was formally declared after Tom and East successfully defended their study against a siege by Flashman's party. That night, in the hall, they told their story to the assembled small boys. The narrator states it plainly: "The war of independence had broken out. Who would join the revolutionary forces? Several others present bound themselves not to fag for the fifth form at once" (008-046).

## How It Unfolded

### The Collapse of Order

The crisis had structural roots. Old Brooke's departure at Christmas, followed by other sixth-form leaders at Easter, left the Schoolhouse without its natural governors. The new praepostors were of two bad kinds: "small young boys, whose cleverness had carried them up to the top of the school, while in strength of body and character they were not yet fit for a share in the government; or else big fellows of the wrong sort — boys whose friendships and tastes had a downward tendency" (008-016). The fifth form, "a sporting and drinking set," quickly filled the power vacuum, and the fags found themselves "ridden over roughshod by a set of boys whom they were not bound to obey, and whose only right over them stood in their bodily powers" (008-016).

### The Siege of the Study

The immediate spark was a direct confrontation. Flashman and his party came to Tom and East's study to force them to fag. The two boys barricaded themselves in, drawing the bolts and reinforcing the door with their sofa. The besiegers kicked a panel in, but the broken pieces jammed in the door, and after "one or two more ineffectual efforts, Flashman and Company retired, vowing vengeance in no mild terms" (008-044). Tom and East then made a daring escape, bolting down the passage while Flashman hurled an empty pickle-jar that narrowly missed Tom's head. East's grim assessment — "He wouldn't mind killing one, if he wasn't caught" (008-045) — captured the stakes of their defiance.

### Diggs the Counsellor

The rebellion found an unlikely strategist in Diggs, familiarly called "the Mucker" — a big, eccentric fifth-form boy who lived by his own code. He advised the rebels not to appeal to masters or praepostors but to stand firm: "Don't you go to anybody at all — you just stand out; say you won't fag. They'll soon get tired of licking you. I've tried it on years ago with their forerunners" (008-050). His counsel was based on experience: the bullies relied on fear, and when boys refused to be afraid, their power crumbled. He also provided critical intelligence on Flashman's character, calling him "a dirty, little, snivelling, sneaking fellow" who "never dared join us, and used to toady the bullies" (008-054).

### The War Intensifies

The rebellion quickly escalated into open warfare. "The house was filled with constant chasings, and sieges, and lickings of all sorts; and in return, the bullies' beds were pulled to pieces and drenched with water, and their names written up on the walls with every insulting epithet which the fag invention could furnish" (008-058). But the resistance had its intended effect: "soon, as Diggs had told them, all the better fellows in the fifth gave up trying to fag them, and public feeling began to set against Flashman and his two or three intimates" (008-058).

Yet victory was partial. Flashman, humiliated and enraged, made the conflict personal. "Come what might, he would make those boys' lives miserable. So the strife settled down into a personal affair between Flashman and our youngsters — a war to the knife, to be fought out in the little cockpit at the end of the bottom passage" (008-061). What had begun as a collective rebellion became an intimate vendetta, driven by Flashman's intolerable shame at having been called "coward and sneak" by boys half his size (008-061).

## Tom Brown's Role

Tom was at the centre of the rebellion from its first moment. When East whispered "Barricade the first. Now, Tom, mind, no surrender," Tom answered through his teeth: "Trust me for that" (008-040, 008-041). His defiance was physical as well as moral: when Flashman caught him and twisted his arm, Tom "couldn't make me cry, though... and I kicked his shins well, I know" (008-058). The rebellion tested and proved his courage — the same courage that would later carry him through the fight with Slogger Williams and his moral stand against the bullies of the next generation.

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold is conspicuously absent from the War of Independence — and this absence is the point. The rebellion occurred precisely because the legitimate authorities (both the masters and the sixth form) had failed. The boys had to solve their own problem, and they did so not by appealing to adult power but by discovering their own collective strength. This was Arnold's deeper theory of school governance: that boys must learn to govern themselves, and that self-government sometimes required resistance to illegitimate authority. The war was a school for character, and its lessons — solidarity, courage, and the refusal to be cowed by bullies — were lessons Arnold would have endorsed.

## Significance in the Novel

The War of Independence is the novel's dramatised argument that moral communities are sustained not by rules but by the character of those who enforce them. The Schoolhouse fell into chaos because good men left and bad men filled their places — a political tragedy in miniature. The rebellion was a partial remedy: it checked Flashman's worst excesses, rallied the small boys around a common cause, and prepared the ground for the restoration of just government under the new sixth form. Most importantly, it forged Tom and East into moral actors — boys who had learned, at twelve years old, that tyranny must be resisted, that solidarity is a weapon, and that the only thing a bully cannot endure is to be despised.
