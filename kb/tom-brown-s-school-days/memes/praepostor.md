# Praepostor

The senior boys of the sixth form at Rugby School who functioned as prefects — maintaining order, overseeing calling-over, disciplining junior boys, and serving as the delegated authority of the masters in the daily governance of the school.

## Definition

A praepostor (Latin: *praepositus*, "one placed in charge") was a sixth-form boy entrusted with disciplinary and organizational duties by the masters. At Rugby, praepostors were the linchpin of school discipline: they kept silence in the schoolroom, called the roll, supervised fagging, and were answerable for the conduct of their houses. Dr. Arnold's great innovation was not to abolish the praepostor system but to elevate it — placing moral responsibility on the sixth form and trusting them to govern by example rather than by cane alone.

(005-050)

## How It Worked at Rugby

The sixth form comprised roughly thirty boys who stood at the apex of the school hierarchy. From among them, a rotating duty of "praepostor of the week" was assigned, responsible for calling the names at calling-over and maintaining order in the great school. In their houses, praepostors controlled the studies, regulated fagging, and — in the best cases — set the moral tone.

### The Praepostor of the Week

Each week, one praepostor took on special responsibilities: standing beside the master at calling-over to call names, marshalling the boys into the close, and overseeing the general conduct of the lower school. The effectiveness of the system depended entirely on the character of the boy holding the office — a weak praepostor of the week meant chaos: "the master of the week being shortsighted, and the praepostors of the week small and not well up to their work, the lower-school boys employ the ten minutes which elapse before their names are called in pelting one another vigorously with acorns" (005-053).

(005-053)

### Duties and Privileges

A praepostor's study was the center of house power. From it, he supervised the fags, dispensed justice, and enjoyed comforts the junior boys could only envy. The study at the fire end of the passage was "the prize," as East explained, rigged with "an iron rod and green baize curtain across the passage" for privacy and warmth (005-029). The praepostor's morning routine — hot water fetched by fags, quiet study, freedom from the jostling of the lower school — marked him as a member of the governing elite.

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold's philosophy transformed the praepostorship from a system of privileged tyranny into an instrument of moral education. Rather than micromanaging discipline, he placed his faith in the sixth form's character — believing that boys who were trusted with responsibility would rise to it, and that their example would do more to shape the school than any quantity of rules. When old Brooke governed the Schoolhouse with "rough but strong and just" rule, the system worked; when weaker or corrupt praepostors succeeded him, the house deteriorated rapidly.

(008-016)

## The Boys' View

To the lower-school boy, the praepostor was a figure of awe and occasional terror. Tom's first encounter came at dinner, seated "at the very bottom of the second table, next to the praepostor (who sat at the end to keep order there)" (005-030). Yet the boys also recognized the difference between just and unjust authority — the praepostor who protected his fags from bullying earned loyalty; the one who abused his position earned the hatred that produced the fagging rebellion.

(005-030)

## Significance in the Novel

The praepostor embodies the novel's central argument about governance: institutions are shaped not by rules but by the character of those who lead. The arc from Brooke (just and strong) through the interregnum after his departure (weak and corrupt) to the Doctor's rebuilding of the sixth form traces the novel's moral thesis. The praepostor, properly understood, is not a bully-in-waiting but a Christian gentleman in training — the public school's answer to the question of how to produce men fit to govern an empire.
