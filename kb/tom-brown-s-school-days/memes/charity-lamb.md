# Charity Lamb

[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s childhood nurse, whose battles with young [[tom-brown|Tom]] over washing and dressing formed the comic "war of independence" of his early years.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Charity Lamb
- **Age/Form**: Adult servant (elderly, of [[brown-family-home|the Brown house]]hold)
- **House**: N/A ([[tom-brown|Brown]] family household, [[vale-of-white-horse|Vale of White Horse]])
- **Role**: Childhood nurse and maid to [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]
- **Family**: None mentioned
- **First appearance**: Ch. II, "The 'War of Independence'" (002-005)
- **Last appearance**: Ch. III, "Sundry Wars and Alliances" (003-004)

## Character & Personality

Charity Lamb is a determined and physically vigorous servant of [[brown-family-home|the Brown house]]hold, whose chief occupation in [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s early years is the losing battle to keep him clean and presentable. She represents the old village order — children christened with Bible names or the cardinal virtues — and embodies a simple, physical approach to childcare that young [[tom-brown|Tom]] easily outmanoeuvres with superior "headwork."

> "Well, from early morning till dewy eve, when she had it out of him in the cold tub before putting him to bed, Charity and Tom were pitted against one another. Physical power was as yet on the side of Charity, but she hadn't a chance with him wherever headwork was wanted." (002-006)

Though outmatched by [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s cleverness, Charity is neither villain nor fool; she is simply the last representative of a nursery regime that [[tom-brown|Tom]] is outgrowing. Her surname "Lamb" contrasts with her combative role in [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s life.

## Role in the Narrative

### The War of Independence (Part I, Ch. II)

Charity's primary function in the narrative is as [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s first antagonist — the representative of adult (and specifically female) authority against which [[tom-brown|Tom]] rebels. The "war of independence" in Chapter II is framed as a comic campaign, with Charity's scrubbing and dressing routines as the battlefield:

> "It had been the immemorial habit of the village to christen children either by Bible names, or by those of the cardinal and other virtues; so that one was forever hearing in the village street or on the green, shrill sounds of 'Prudence! Prudence! thee cum' out o' the gutter.'" (002-005)

[[tom-brown|Tom]]'s two allies in this war are Noah Crooke and [[benjy|Benjamin]] ([[benjy|Benjy]]), retired servants who defend him from Charity and supervise his outdoor education. The alliance of the two old men against Charity's domestic regime signals the gendered division of [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s early upbringing — women control the house, men control the fields and woods.

(002-005 × 1, 002-006 × 1, 002-007 × 1)

### Departure from the Nursery (Part I, Ch. III)

Charity appears only in the early chapters; by the time [[tom-brown|Tom]] leaves for [[rugby-school|Rugby]], his "war of independence" is already won. [[benjy|Benjy]] worries that without his guidance, [[tom-brown|Tom]] might "fall back again into the hands of Charity and the women" (003-004), marking Charity as the emblem of a domestic sphere [[tom-brown|Tom]] has definitively outgrown.

> "He feared much, too, lest Master Tom should fall back again into the hands of Charity and the women." (003-004)

## Significance

Charity Lamb serves a modest but structurally important role in *[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s School Days*. She is the first in a series of authority figures [[tom-brown|Tom]] must negotiate — from Charity (nursery) to the village schoolmaster, to [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] at [[rugby-school|Rugby]]. Her defeat in the "war of independence" marks [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s first step toward the self-reliance that the public school will complete. The comic framing of their struggle — a small boy's cleverness against an old woman's physical persistence — establishes the novel's warm, humorous register before the more serious moral education of [[rugby-school|Rugby]] begins.
