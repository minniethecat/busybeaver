# Tossing

The blanket-tossing ritual of Rugby School — a rough initiation that could be either good-natured sport or cruel hazing, depending on the character of the boys who administered it, and the test in which Tom Brown first proved he was no milksop.

## Definition

Tossing was the practice of placing a new or smaller boy in a blanket held by older boys and pitching him into the air — three tosses being the customary number. It was an unofficial tradition, neither sanctioned nor suppressed by the masters, and its character depended entirely on the boys who performed it. In the hands of good-natured sixth-formers, it was rough sport, a test of a boy's nerve; in the hands of bullies like Flashman, it was an instrument of terror and humiliation.

## How It Worked at Rugby

The ritual took place after locking-up, when the bigger boys gathered in the largest study (Number 7) and summoned the new boys (006-073). A blanket was produced, a boy was placed in it, and the older boys stood around the edges, pitching their captive into the air. Three tosses was the standard — to take them without kicking or crying earned a boy the respect of his peers; to struggle, scream, or cling to the blanket's edge was to invite rougher treatment.

The scene was a procession, almost ceremonial: older boys marched through the passages collecting captives, "each with a captive or two, some willing to be tossed, some sullen, and some frightened to death" (006-073). The willing ones treated it as a sport; the frightened ones found it indistinguishable from torture.

### The Bully's Tossing

Hughes draws a sharp distinction between the tossing administered by good-natured boys and that administered by bullies. "What your real bully likes in tossing is when the boys kick and struggle, or hold on to one side of the blanket, and so get pitched bodily on to the floor; it's no fun to him when no one is hurt or frightened" (006-076). Flashman and his sort took pleasure in the terror of their victims — the begging, the struggling, the impact of a small body against the floorboards. The ritual revealed character: the same act that was rough fellowship among friends became cruelty among bullies.

## Tom Brown's Tossing

Tom's tossing was the making of his reputation. Warned by East that "there'll be tossing tonight, most likely" (006-049), Tom had the choice to hide or face it. He chose to face it. When his turn came, he was placed in the blanket and pitched into the air. "Tom was very near shouting to be set down when he found himself back in the blanket, but thought of East, and didn't; and so took his three tosses without a kick or a cry, and was called a young trump for his pains" (006-075).

The key detail is that Tom nearly cried out — the fear was real — but he suppressed it, thinking of his friend East. This was courage, not bravado: the conquest of natural fear by something stronger. The verdict — "a young trump" — was the highest praise the older boys could bestow, and it marked Tom's entry into the respect of the Schoolhouse.

East had already set the example. When Walker called out "Here's Scud East — you'll be tossed, won't you, young un?" East stepped forward willingly, and "the boy was soon in the blanket" (006-063). The two friends, each in their own way, passed the same test.

## The Boys' View

For the older boys, tossing was entertainment and tradition — a rough welcome that every boy had endured and every boy had the right to administer in turn. But the code was not without limits. When Flashman tried to toss a boy against his will, young Brooke intervened: "I'll be hanged if we'll toss anyone against their will. No more tossing" (006-061). The distinction between willing and unwilling participants was crucial: forced tossing was bullying, and the sixth form's authority existed to prevent it. This moment — Brooke drawing a line that even Flashman could not cross — prefigured the larger conflict of the War of Independence, in which the same issue (illegitimate force versus legitimate tradition) would be fought out on a grander scale.

## Significance in the Novel

Tossing is the novel's first test of Tom's physical courage — the virtue that, in Hughes's muscular Christianity, was the foundation of all other virtues. A boy who could not endure three tosses without crying could not be trusted to stand firm in more serious trials. Tom passed, and the passing mattered: it established his standing in the informal hierarchy of the house and proved to himself (and to East) that he was no coward.

But the episode also introduces the novel's persistent concern with the line between legitimate tradition and abuse. The same blanket that tested a boy's nerve could break his spirit. The difference lay not in the act but in the actors — in whether the boys holding the blanket were friends or bullies. This was the central moral problem of the public school, and tossing was its microcosm.
