# Locking-Up

The daily boundary between the freedom of the school day and the confinement of evening — a fixed hour after which every boy had to be inside his own house, every gate was shut, and the authority of the masters gave way to the informal governance of the studies and dormitories.

## Definition

Locking-up was the evening deadline at Rugby by which all boys were required to return to their respective houses. It was signalled by the tolling of the locking-up bell, and any boy found outside after the bell had rung was reported to the Doctor — a serious matter that could result in a flogging. The hour varied by season: in winter it came earlier, in summer it stretched to eight o'clock or beyond (014-006). Locking-up was not merely a curfew; it was the pivot on which the entire daily routine of the school turned.

## How It Worked at Rugby

The school day at Rugby was structured around fixed points — calling-over, lessons, chapel, meals — and locking-up was the final and most consequential of these. The bell rang, the gates were shut, and the school transformed. The close emptied of boys. The masters retreated to their houses. And inside the boarding houses, a different kind of life began.

The architectural features of the school reflected the seriousness of locking-up. Studies on the ground floor had bars and gratings on their windows, "which little precautions were necessary... to prevent the exit of small boys after locking up, and the entrance of contraband articles" (005-022). Once the bell had rung, the school became a closed world.

### The Evening Rhythm

Tea was served "directly after locking-up" (006-005), making the bell a signal not only for confinement but for the evening meal. After tea came the hours of freedom within the house — the time between locking-up and supper (usually nine or ten o'clock) that the boys prized most. It was the only period of the day wholly free from masters' supervision, when boys could talk, read, spar, carve their names into tables, or conduct the small businesses and conspiracies of schoolboy life (011-028).

### Late for Locking-Up

To be late for locking-up was a serious offence. The consequences were immediate and dramatic: "East, Hall, and Brown, late for locking-up. Must go up to the Doctor's study at once" (007-058). Tom, East, and their comrades once found themselves caught out after an expedition to Caldecott's Spinney, jogging into Rugby "some forty minutes after locking-up" in a hired coach, teeth chattering with cold (007-056). The encounter with the Doctor that followed was surprisingly mild — Tom later reflected, "Don't you remember, he never said anything to us my first half-year for being an hour late for locking-up?" (009-047) — but the terror of being caught was real enough.

## The Doctor's View

For Dr. Arnold, locking-up was a necessary instrument of discipline — the fixed point that ensured order in a community of several hundred boys. But its deeper value lay in what it created: a defined space of time, between locking-up and sleep, in which boys governed themselves. Arnold understood that character was formed not under the eye of masters but in the unsupervised hours when boys had to choose for themselves what kind of conduct was honourable. Locking-up did not end the school day; it transferred its governance from the official hierarchy of masters and praepostors to the informal hierarchy of the studies.

## The Boys' View

For the boys, locking-up was both restriction and liberation. It restricted movement — the close, the fields, the town were all off-limits — but it liberated them from masters' surveillance. The hours after locking-up were the hours of real school life: the conversations by the fire, the schemes hatched in corners, the lotteries drawn in the hall (008-064), the bottled beer drunk in studies that East and Tom had dreamed of stocking (010-015).

Arthur, in his fever, remembered a gentler aspect of the ritual. As he lay ill, he watched the rooks from his window and reflected on how East had once laughed at the idea that "the rooks all had calling-over or prayers, or something of the sort, when the locking-up bell rang." Arthur now believed the rooks indeed observed a ritual: "they really do come and perch, all of them, just about locking-up time; and then first there's a regular chorus of caws; and then they stop a bit, and one old fellow, or perhaps two or three in different trees, caw solos; and then off they all go again" (015-025). The locking-up bell that governed the boys seemed, to Arthur's fevered imagination, to govern the natural world as well.

## Significance in the Novel

Locking-up is the structural spine of the novel's daily life — the fixed point that gives rhythm and shape to the boys' experience. Every major episode of the book is oriented around it: tea at Sally Harrowell's is timed to locking-up; the fight with Slogger Williams comes "directly after locking-up" (014-110); the Derby lottery is drawn after locking-up; Tom's expeditions and adventures end with desperate races to beat the bell. It is the metronome of school life, marking the passage of terms and seasons and the slow growth of the characters within its steady rhythm.

More subtly, locking-up represents the boundary between the official and unofficial worlds of the school. Before the bell, Rugby belonged to the masters; after it, Rugby belonged to the boys. The novel's moral argument depends on this partition: character is formed in the hours when no one is watching, and the clock that marks those hours is the locking-up bell.
