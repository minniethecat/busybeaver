## Overview

The Peacock Inn at Islington is the starting-point of [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s journey to [[rugby-school|Rugby School]]. It is a classic early-nineteenth-century [[the-stage-coach|coach]]ing inn — one of the bustling London hostelries from which the great mail and [[the-stage-coach|stage]] [[the-stage-coach|coach]]es departed for all parts of England. [[tom-brown|Tom]] and his father arrive here from [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] the evening before [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s first term, and it is from the Peacock's yard, at half-past two on a November morning, that the [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] [[the-stage-coach|coach]] carries him north into his new life.

## Arrival at the Peacock

`[[tom-brown|Tom]] and his father arrived in town from [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] the day before` (004-003). The [[squire-brown|Squire]] has chosen the Peacock deliberately: the [[the-stage-coach|Birmingham coach]]es from the City do not pass through [[rugby-school|Rugby]] but deposit passengers at Dunchurch, three miles away. The Peacock's [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]], by contrast, travels the Oxford road and passes directly through [[rugby-school|Rugby]] — getting [[tom-brown|Tom]] to [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] gates by dinner-time.

On arrival, [[tom-brown|Tom]] experiences London for the first time. He is put down at the Belle Savage by the Star [[the-stage-coach|coach]] `just at dusk` and is so excited by `those endless, mysterious, gas-lit streets, with their glare and hum and moving crowds` that `he couldn't talk even` (004-004). But the promise of reaching [[rugby-school|Rugby]] by the [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] reconciles him to an early night.

## The Coffee-Room

The Peacock's coffee-room is one of the novel's most warmly rendered interiors:

> `Tom had run out to see about him, had wondered at all the vehicles passing and repassing, and had finished by asking a waiter if there were any houses called peerages and baronetcies' (004-005). Then being summoned to supper, he had regaled himself in one of the bright little boxes of the Peacock coffee-room, on the beefsteak and unlimited oyster-sauce and brown stout (tasted then for the first time — a day to be marked forever by Tom with a white stone)` (004-006).

The [[squire-brown|Squire]], seated `cozily by the bright fire in the coffee-room with the paper in his hand` (004-005), delivers his parting counsel over a glass of `steaming brandy-and-water` (004-006). These last words of paternal advice — the result of much anxious thought during the journey up from [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] — are meant to be something [[tom-brown|Tom]] `could keep in his head ready for use` (004-016).

## The Departure

The departure is swift and efficient — a [[the-hall|hall]]mark of the [[the-stage-coach|coach]]ing era. The boots rouses [[tom-brown|Tom]] at half-past two: `"Now, sir, time to get up, if you please. [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] [[the-stage-coach|coach]] for Leicester'll be round in half an hour, and don't wait for nobody"` (004-002).

[[tom-brown|Tom]] dresses hurriedly, swallowing his last coffee while `winding his comforter round his throat, and tucking the ends into the breast of his coat` (004-020). The horn sounds; boots announces the [[the-stage-coach|coach]]; the [[squire-brown|Squire]] settles the bill. In a scene of memorable brevity:

> `"Goodbye, father — my love at home." A last shake of the hand. Up goes Tom, the guard catching his hatbox and holding on with one hand, while with the other he claps the horn to his mouth. Toot, toot, toot! the hostlers let go their heads, the four bays plunge at the collar, and away goes the Tally-ho into the darkness, forty-five seconds from the time they pulled up` (004-024).

The [[squire-brown|Squire]]'s laconic verdict — `"Sharp work!"` (004-025) — captures the brisk efficiency of the [[the-stage-coach|coach]]ing system.

## The Tally-ho Coach

The [[the-stage-coach|coach]] itself — the [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] bound for Leicester — is a four-horse fast [[the-stage-coach|coach]]. [[tom-brown|Tom]] rides on the outside, `a Petersham coat with velvet collar, made tight after the abominable fashion of those days` (004-020). The guard muffles [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s feet in straw and puts `the end of an oat-sack over his knees` (004-029) against the November cold. The journey includes stops at roadside inns where passengers take `early purl` — a warm beer drink — and breakfasts of `kidney and pigeon-pie` and coffee (004-040).

The [[the-stage-coach|coach]] passes through St. Albans and other staging posts, with [[martin|the guard]] providing a running commentary on the hazards of the road: rowdy schoolboys with pea-shooters who `peppers everyone's faces` (004-055), irate farmers, and fair-day traffic. The [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] delivers [[tom-brown|Tom]] to [[rugby-school|Rugby]] at last, passing `round Dead-man's corner, past [[the-great-school|the school]]-gates, and down the High Street to the Spread Eagle` (005-002).

## Contrast with the Railway Age

The narrator explicitly contrasts the [[the-stage-coach|coach]]ing experience with modern railway travel:

> `I sometimes think that you boys of this generation are a deal tenderer fellows than we used to be. At any rate you're much more comfortable travellers, for I see every one of you with his rug or plaid, and other dodges for preserving the caloric, and most of you going in, those fuzzy, dusty, padded first-class carriages` (004-027).

The [[the-stage-coach|coach]] journey, though physically harsher, is presented as richer in sensory experience: `the break of dawn and the sunrise, where can they be ever seen in perfection but from a [[the-stage-coach|coach]] roof? You want motion and change and music to see them in their glory` (004-028).

## Significance

The Peacock Inn marks the threshold between [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] childhood and his [[rugby-school|Rugby]] life. The warm coffee-room scene — father and son, beefsteak and oyster-sauce, advice and brandy-and-water — is the last moment of paternal protection before [[tom-brown|Tom]] steps onto the [[the-stage-coach|Tally-ho]] and into the world of school. The forty-five-second departure is emblematic of the [[the-stage-coach|coach]]ing age: precise, unsentimental, and exhilarating.
