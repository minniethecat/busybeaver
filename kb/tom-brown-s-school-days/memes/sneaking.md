# Sneaking

The unforgivable transgression of the schoolboy code — the act of reporting a fellow boy's misdeeds to the masters, which the public school world regarded not as virtuous honesty but as the deepest form of treachery.

## Definition

To sneak was to inform on a fellow boy — to carry tales to a master, to betray a confidence, to break the solidarity that bound the boys together against adult authority. It was distinguished from lying (which was also dishonourable but more common) by its element of betrayal: the sneak did not merely deceive, he delivered his fellows into the hands of their common adversary. A boy caught sneaking was socially dead — no one would speak to him, sit with him, or acknowledge his existence. Flashman, the arch-sneak, demonstrated the type in its most contemptible form: a coward who could not face his enemies directly and resorted to underhanded accusation instead (009-003). His status as both coward and sneak marked him as doubly damned — lacking the courage to fight and the honour to stay silent when questioned (009-003).

## How It Worked at Rugby

The prohibition against sneaking was absolute and enforced by the boys themselves, without reference to the masters. The code demanded that boys settle their own disputes, punish their own offenders, and present a united front to the adult world. A master who encouraged sneaking — who offered rewards for information or punished boys for refusing to name offenders — was seen as waging war on the boys' moral autonomy.

### Sneaking vs. Confession

The code made a crucial distinction between sneaking (informing on others) and confession (admitting one's own fault). A boy who owned up to his own misdeed was honourable; a boy who named another to save himself was a sneak. This distinction was central to the school’s moral economy — the same economy that old Brooke invoked when he declared that he would not pry and interfere, for that only made bullying more underhand (006-030). This distinction was central to the school's moral economy and to the novel's larger argument about conscience and responsibility.

### Flashman the Sneak

Flashman embodied sneaking in its most contemptible form: the coward who struck from the shadows, the informer who used the masters as his weapons (009-003). His accusations against Tom and East were not honest reports but malicious betrayals designed to harm others while protecting himself. His status as "coward and sneak" marked him as doubly damned in the boys' eyes — lacking both the courage to fight and the honour to stay silent.

(009-003)

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold occupied an ambiguous position on sneaking. Officially, he encouraged boys to be truthful with masters; practically, he understood that the anti-sneaking code was essential to the self-governing system he had built. His genius was to navigate the tension — to create a school in which boys policed themselves not out of fear of sneaks but out of shared commitment to a higher standard. This was the same strategy he applied to bullying: not suppression from above, but cultivation of a school culture in which dishonourable conduct became shameful (006-030).

## The Boys' View

For the boys, the anti-sneaking code was non-negotiable. It was the one rule that every boy, regardless of house or form, understood and enforced. The threat of being branded a sneak was the ultimate social sanction — more feared than flogging, more damning than failure. When Tom and East faced the choice between taking a flogging and naming their accomplices, the question was not even asked: a gentleman did not sneak. The code was absolute and its enforcement, though informal, was total — the sneak was simply cut off from the community, a fate worse in a boy’s eyes than any flogging (006-030).

## Significance in the Novel

Sneaking is the negative pole of the honour system — the act that reveals what honour means by showing its absolute violation. The boys' hatred of the sneak was not mere tribalism but a recognition that moral communities depend on trust — and that the informer, by destroying trust, destroys the community itself (006-030). The novel uses sneaking (and the boys' hatred of it) to argue that moral communities must be built on trust, not surveillance; on shared values, not enforced rules. A school in which boys sneaked was a school that had failed to cultivate honour — and a school without honour was, in Hughes's view, no school at all.

(009-003)
