# Old Brooke

The [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] praepostor and [[football-at-rugby|football]] captain whose "rough but strong and just" rule embodied the [[the-doctor|Arnold]]ian ideal — a hero to the younger boys whose departure left a void that lesser men filled with corruption.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Brooke (first name not given; "old Brooke" to the boys, "Brooke" to his peers)
- **Age/Form**: Sixth Form (senior)
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]
- **Role**: [[praepostor|Praepostor]], captain of [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] [[football-at-rugby|football]] team, moral leader
- **Family**: Not specified
- **First appearance**: Chapter V — leads [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] in the great [[football-at-rugby|football]] match (005-001)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter VIII — departed at Christmas (008-016)

## Character & Personality

Brooke is the novel's ideal — the praepostor who governs with strength and justice, the athlete whose courage inspires, the leader whose moral authority is earned rather than imposed. His speech to [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] after [[schoolhouse-match|the match]] — naming [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]] as the house's central problem, pledging to end forced [[keeping-up|tossing]], calling the younger boys to a higher standard — is the novel's moral manifesto. "Rough but strong and just" (008-016) is the epitaph Hughes gives him, and it captures the balance: not gentle, not refined, but fundamentally good.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Football Hero

Brooke's greatness is first displayed on the [[football-at-rugby|football]] field, where as [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] captain he leads his fifty or sixty white-trousered boys against the mass of [[rugby-school|the School]]. His courage, endurance, and skill mark him as the natural leader of the house, and [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s admiration for him is immediate and total.

### The Moral Leader

Brooke's speech after [[schoolhouse-match|the match]] is his finest moment. Standing before the assembled [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]], he names the evil — "First, there's a deal of [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]] going on" — and pledges reform: "I'll be hanged if we'll toss anyone against their will. No more [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]]" (006-061). It is the novel's pivotal moral statement, and its author is not [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] but a boy — proof that the [[the-doctor|Arnold]]ian system could produce leaders worthy of the trust placed in them.

## Significance

Brooke is the novel's proof of concept — the demonstration that the [[the-doctor|Arnold]]ian system worked. A boy entrusted with real authority, held to a high standard, and inspired by Christian preaching, could become a leader whose influence was entirely for good. His departure and the deterioration that followed are the novel's warning: institutions depend on character, and character must be renewed in each generation. A Brooke today does not guarantee a Brooke tomorrow.
