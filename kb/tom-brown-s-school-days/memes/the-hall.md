# The Hall

The great long room of [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] — the community's gathering place for meals, speeches, and the rituals that bound the house together.

## Overview

- **Category**: School interior / dining and assembly hall
- **Location**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]], [[rugby-school|Rugby School]]
- **Part of**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]
- **Chapters**: 005–018

(006-022)

## Geography & Description

The Schoolhouse hall was "a great long high room, with two large fires on one side, and two long tables" (006-022). Boys sat by seniority — the [[the-forms|sixth form]] nearest the door, the [[the-forms|lower school]] at the far end — replicating in the house the hierarchy that the [[the-great-school|great school]] enacted. After supper, the tables were cleared for singing; before calling-over, the hall filled with the chaos of a hundred boys.

(006-022)

## Role in the Narrative

The hall was the setting for the novel's most important speech: [[old-brooke|Old Brooke]] standing before the assembled [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]], naming [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]] as the house's problem, and pledging reform. It was also the scene of the keeping-up ritual — the [[keeping-up|blanket-tossing]] of newcomers that [[old-brooke|Brooke]]'s speech sought to regulate. The hall was where the house became visible to itself as a community.
