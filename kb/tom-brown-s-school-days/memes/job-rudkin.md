# Job Rudkin

Son of the widow Rudkin, Job is a stolid, slow-moving village boy from [[vale-of-white-horse|the Vale]] of White Horse. He is one of [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s childhood companions before [[tom-brown|Tom]] leaves for [[rugby-school|Rugby]], serving as a wrestling opponent and a representative of the sturdy, unpolished country character that [[the-forms|forms]] the backdrop to [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s early life.

## Character & Personality

Job Rudkin is defined by his solid, unflappable slowness. He is described as "slow of offence" (003-025), a boy who wins his wrestling bouts not through aggression or speed but through sheer imperturbable stolidity — he simply cannot be shifted. His physical description is affectionate but unflattering: his clothing is homespun and ill-fitting, his movements deliberate to the point of comedy. When his mother asks whether his cap is on his head, he replies with characteristic slowness: "What! bean't 'ee on ma head, mother?" while slowly extracting a hand from his pocket to check (003-015).

(003-014–015, 003-025)

## Role in the Narrative

### Tom's Village Wrestling Partner

Job appears in Chapter 3 as one of the village boys with whom [[tom-brown|Tom]] played before his departure for [[rugby-school|Rugby]]. [[mrs-brown|Tom's mother]] regularly visited the village cottages, and through these visits [[tom-brown|Tom]] "had thereby made acquaintance with many of the village boys of his own age. There was Job Rudkin, son of widow Rudkin" (003-014). Job is the designated wrestling opponent in the boys' trials of strength that took place before teatime.

The defining detail of Job's character comes through his wrestling style: "it was not long indeed before he could manage to keep his legs against Job, for that hero was slow of offence, and gained his victories chiefly by allowing others to exhaust themselves against his immovable bulk" (003-025). Job's wrestling philosophy — stand still and let opponents tire themselves out — is both comic and oddly admirable, a testament to the unflashy effectiveness of sheer solidity.

(003-014, 003-024–025)

## Significance

Job Rudkin represents the village world that [[tom-brown|Tom]] leaves behind when he enters [[rugby-school|Rugby]]. Unlike the quick-witted, combative schoolboys [[tom-brown|Tom]] will later encounter — [[harry-east|East]], [[flashman|Flashman]], [[old-brooke|Brooke]] — Job is slow, stolid, and undemonstrative. He is memorable precisely for his immovability: in a novel full of energetic striving, Job stands still and lets the world exhaust itself against him. His presence in the early chapters establishes the rural [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] character-types — plain-spoken, physically capable, unpretentious — against which the more complex schoolboy personalities of the later chapters are implicitly measured.
