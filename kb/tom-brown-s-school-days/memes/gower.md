# Gower

Gower is a [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] boy and the chum of [[harry-east|Harry East]]. He appears primarily in Chapter 16, "[[harry-east|Harry East]]'s Dilemmas and Deliverances," where he figures in a significant moral conflict over the use of cribs (translation aids) in preparing second lessons. A practical, unreflective boy, Gower serves as a foil to [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s growing moral seriousness.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Gower (given name never specified in the narrative)
- **Age**: Roughly [[tom-brown|Tom]] and [[harry-east|East]]'s contemporary (13–15 during the main events)
- **Form**: Fifth Form
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]
- **Role**: Schoolboy, [[harry-east|East]]'s chum
- **Family**: Not mentioned
- **First appearance**: Chapter 5 (005-027) — [[harry-east|East]] mentions Gower as his chum to [[tom-brown|Tom]]
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 16 (016-034) — [[harry-east|East]] reconciles with [[tom-brown|Tom]] after the crib disagreement

## Character

### East's Chum

Gower first appears by reference in Chapter 5, when [[harry-east|East]] describes the conditions of their shared [[the-studies|study]] to the newly arrived [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]: "Gower — that's my chum — and I make a fire with paper on the floor after supper generally, only that makes it so smoky" (005-027). This early mention establishes Gower as [[harry-east|East]]'s intimate companion within [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] hierarchy.

### The Crib Controversy

Gower's most substantial appearance comes in Chapter 16, where he becomes entangled in [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s moral awakening. After [[arthur|Arthur]]'s influence has begun to work upon [[tom-brown|Tom]], [[tom-brown|Tom]] resolves to give up using cribs — the illicit translation aids that boys use to prepare their classical lessons. When [[tom-brown|Tom]] announces this resolve to [[harry-east|East]] and Gower, Gower's reaction is immediate and dismissive:

> "Stuff and nonsense!" cried Gower. "Here, East, get down the crib and find the place." (016-004)

When [[tom-brown|Tom]] appeals to Gower directly — "I say, Gower, be a good fellow, and let's try if we can't get on without the crib" (016-010) — Gower deflects, calling [[arthur|Arthur]] "the little prig" and insisting that they will "only use the crib when we can't construe without it" (016-016). This compromise proves hollow, as the crib continues to be used in practice.

### Role in the Narrative

Gower functions as a representative of the ordinary, good-natured but spiritually unawakened boy. He is not a villain — unlike [[flashman|Flashman]] — but neither is he capable of understanding [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s new moral seriousness. His practical "get on with it" attitude illustrates the prevailing ethos of [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] life before [[the-doctor|the Doctor]]'s influence and [[arthur|Arthur]]'s [[friendship]] begin to transform the house culture. [[harry-east|East]]'s eventual break with Gower over the crib question marks one of the novel's turning points in the moral ascendancy of earnestness over pragmatism.

## Key Relationships

- **[[harry-east|Harry East]]** — Gower's chum and [[the-close|close]]st associate. [[harry-east|East]]'s gradual movement away from Gower's practical cynicism toward [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s principled stand [[the-forms|forms]] a key arc in Chapter 16.
- **[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]** — Initially on friendly terms, but [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s resolve to abandon cribs creates tension between them.
- **[[arthur|Arthur]]** — Gower calls [[arthur|Arthur]] a "sucking wiseacre" and "little prig," resenting the younger boy's moral influence on [[tom-brown|Tom]] and [[harry-east|East]].
