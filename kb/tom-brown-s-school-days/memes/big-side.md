# Big-Side

The senior division of Rugby athletic life — the arena where the oldest and strongest boys competed in football and hare-and-hounds, and where younger boys watched from the margins, dreaming of the day they would earn their place on the big-side ground.

## Definition

Big-side was the term for the senior level of organised sport at Rugby, encompassing both big-side football (the full-pitch house matches played by sixth-form and upper boys) and big-side hare-and-hounds (the long-distance cross-country paper chase). It was distinguished from "little-side" — the junior games played by younger and smaller boys on a separate part of the close. The big-side ground occupied the far side of the trees, "where the great matches are played" (005-032), and access to it marked a boy's athletic coming of age.

## How It Worked at Rugby

The geography of the close encoded the hierarchy of the school. East explained it to Tom on his first day: "And all this part where we are is the little-side ground, right up to the trees; and on the other side of the trees is the big-side ground, where the great matches are played" (005-032). The trees were a physical boundary between the world of the small boys and the world of the athletes — a boundary every ambitious boy burned to cross.

### Big-Side Football

The great football matches on big-side were the school's premier sporting events. The entire schoolhouse turned out to watch, and the heroes of big-side — boys like Pater Brooke, "the head of the eleven, the head of big-side football, their leader on this glorious day" (006-027) — commanded a reverence that no classroom achievement could rival. The matches were played at a ferocious pace: Hughes describes a charge "across the level big-side ground, the ball well down amongst them, straight for our goal, like the column of the Old Guard up the slope at Waterloo" (005-069).

### Big-Side Hare-and-Hounds

The paper chase, or hare-and-hounds, was the other great big-side institution. On designated afternoons, two boys (the hares) laid a trail of paper shreds across miles of Warwickshire countryside, and the pack (the hounds) pursued them across hedges, streams, and fields. The last run of the half was the hardest and most prestigious: "if a fellow gets in at the end big-side stands ale and bread and cheese and a bowl of punch" (007-034). For the younger boys, competing in big-side hare-and-hounds was a rite of passage — Tom and East, despite East's prediction that "they could never get in, as it was the hardest run of the year" (007-037), threw themselves into it with reckless enthusiasm.

The hare-and-hounds tradition created its own material culture. Old boys' names were "rudely cut on the old hall tables, or painted upon the big-side cupboard" (007-018) — permanent records of athletic achievement that linked generations of Rugby boys across decades.

## The Boys' View

For the boys, big-side represented everything that made school life glorious. It was physical, competitive, and governed by its own code of honour — a code that rewarded courage and effort far more than skill. When Tom and East failed to finish the hare-and-hounds, they were nevertheless welcomed at the Cock with ale and sympathy, and by evening "all their grievances were forgotten, and they were resolving to go out the first big-side next half, and thinking hare-and-hounds the most delightful of games" (007-086).

The big-side ground was sacred space. When Tom returned to Rugby years later and found "a few of the town boys playing cricket, their wicket pitched on the best piece in the middle of the big-side ground," the narrator calls it "a sin about equal to sacrilege in the eyes of a captain of the eleven" (018-037).

## Significance in the Novel

Big-side is the setting for the novel's argument about the moral value of athletic competition. The football matches and hare-and-hounds chases were not mere recreation; they were the proving ground where boys learned courage, endurance, and fair play — the virtues that Arnold's Rugby sought to cultivate. The "muscular Christianity" that Hughes championed found its purest expression on the big-side ground, where physical and moral strength were tested together.

The big-side ground is also where the novel's deepest themes intersect. Thompson died "in the bright afternoon, while the cricket-match was going on as usual on the big-side ground" (015-009) — life and death, sport and mortality, coexisting on the same patch of grass. The ground that witnessed the school's greatest triumphs also witnessed its quietest grief.
