# Willum (Willum Smith)

A stout young blacksmith's apprentice from [[vale-of-white-horse|the Vale]] of White Horse who appears first at the village "veast" (festival), where he competes in the backswording contest. He later reappears in the novel as a shepherd in Farmer [[thompson|Thompson]]'s employ, involved in the bird-fostering misadventure.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Willum Smith
- **Age**: Nineteen or twenty at first appearance (002-023)
- **House**: N/A — a Vale villager, not a [[rugby-school|Rugby]] boy
- **Role**: Blacksmith's apprentice (early); shepherd (later)
- **Family**: Unknown; has a sweetheart who watches him at the "veast"
- **First appearance**: Chapter 2 (002-023) — at the village festival
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 13 (013-080) — confronted by [[holmes|Holmes]] and [[diggs-the-mucker|Diggs]] during the bird-fostering episode

## Character & Personality

Willum is a robust country youth — physically strong and game for a fight, as demonstrated in his celebrated bout with the shepherd at the backswording competition. He possesses the characteristic Vale stubbornness and simplicity. As a shepherd in later chapters, he is loyal to his employer Farmer [[thompson|Thompson]] but capable of being reasoned with when confronted by stronger-willed opponents like [[holmes|Holmes]].

(002-023–035, 013-072–080)

## Role in the Narrative

### The "Veast" Backswording

Willum's first and most vivid appearance is at the village festival in Chapter 2. He is introduced as the blacksmith's apprentice, a "stout young fellow of nineteen or twenty" (002-023), whose sweetheart is somewhere in the crowd. His bout with the shepherd is "the crack set-to of the day" (002-029) — both men in famous wind and full combat. Willum is defeated when his sweetheart, catching sight of him on [[the-stage-coach|stage]], cries out in alarm; the distraction costs him [[schoolhouse-match|the match]] (002-030). Young [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]], watching from the crowd, resolves to give Willum part of his remaining two shillings in sympathy (002-033).

(002-023, 002-029–035)

### The Bird-Fostering Episode

Willum reappears in Chapter 13 as a shepherd working for Farmer [[thompson|Thompson]]. When [[tom-brown|Tom]], [[harry-east|East]], and their companions are accused of raiding the farmer's fowl-house, Willum is ordered by the farmer to seize the boys. His dialogue reveals his rural dialect and simple loyalty: "Ees, I seen 'em sure enough" (013-073), though he is forced to admit uncertainty — "if it worn't they 'twas chaps as like 'em as two peas'n" (013-074). When [[holmes|Holmes]] and [[diggs-the-mucker|Diggs]] confront the farmer and his men, Willum is warned off by [[holmes|Holmes]]'s stick while [[diggs-the-mucker|Diggs]] faces the other shepherd "cracking his fingers like pistol-shots" (013-080).

(013-072–080)

## Significance

Willum represents the sturdy, plain-spoken rural labouring class of [[vale-of-white-horse|the Vale]] — physically capable, loyal to his employer, but lacking the quick wit and social authority of the [[rugby-school|Rugby]] schoolboys. His two appearances, separated by eleven chapters, show the continuity of village life that runs parallel to [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s school career. His dialect speech ("thee canst plaay," "I doan't care," "leastways") is one of the novel's richest examples of [[vale-of-white-horse|Berkshire]] vernacular, grounding the narrative in a specific rural English world.
