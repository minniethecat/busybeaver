# Rugby School

The great public school whose transformation under [[the-doctor|Dr. Arnold]] made it the Victorian model of [[muscular-christianity|character education]] — the institution that took raw country boys like [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]] and formed them into Christian gentlemen.

## Overview

- **Type**: Public school (boarding school for boys aged ~11–18)
- **Founded**: 1567; reformed under Dr. [[the-doctor|Thomas Arnold]] (1828–1841)
- **Part of**: Independent institution in Warwickshire, England
- **Membership**: Boys entered as "new boys," typically at age ~11, paying fees
- **Key figures**: [[the-doctor|Dr. Arnold]] ([[the-doctor|headmaster]]), the [[praepostor|praepostors]] (sixth-form governors), masters

## Structure & Hierarchy

Rugby was organized as a hierarchy of [[the-forms|forms]] (classes) and houses. The [[the-forms|forms]] determined academic standing — from the lowest to the [[praepostor|Sixth Form]], whose members governed [[the-great-school|the school]] as [[praepostor|praepostors]]. The houses — [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] among them — were the boys' social units, each with its own character, traditions, and loyalties. The [[the-doctor|headmaster]] stood at the apex, delegating daily discipline to the [[praepostor|praepostors]] while reserving ultimate authority.

### Leadership

The Sixth Form [[praepostor|praepostors]] were [[the-great-school|the school]]'s governing body, responsible for maintaining order among the younger boys. Chosen by [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] for character as much as ability, they embodied [[the-doctor|Arnold]]'s principle that a strong, moral [[the-forms|sixth form]] would do more to shape [[the-great-school|the school]] than any quantity of rules. [[old-brooke|Old Brooke]], [[schoolhouse|the Schoolhouse]] praepostor and [[football-at-rugby|football]] captain, was the ideal: physically courageous, morally firm, and devoted to [[the-great-school|the school]]'s traditions.

### Daily Life

Boys rose early, attended lessons in the [[the-great-school|great school]]room, ate in [[the-hall|hall]], played [[football-at-rugby|football]] on [[the-close|the Close]], and worshipped in [[rugby-chapel|the chapel]]. [[fagging|Fagging]] — the system by which younger boys served the [[praepostor|praepostors]] — was the central social institution, teaching obedience and service while also providing opportunities for abuse that the novel's heroes would eventually reform.

## Culture & Traditions

Rugby's culture was defined by its rituals: [[calling-over|calling-over]] each evening, [[sunday-at-rugby|Sunday chapel]], the great [[football-at-rugby|football]] matches, and the [[the-fight|fights]] behind [[rugby-chapel|the chapel]] that settled matters of [[honour-at-rugby|honour]]. The school's unwritten code valued [[courage|physical courage]], loyalty to house, and resistance to tyranny — values that the novel presents as the raw material from which [[gentleman-ideal|Christian gentleman]]liness was formed.

## Significance

Rugby School under [[the-doctor|Arnold]] became the template for the Victorian public school — an institution whose purpose was not merely academic instruction but the formation of character. The novel *[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s School Days* was its testament, spreading the Rugby ideal across the English-speaking world and establishing the public school as a central institution of British culture.
