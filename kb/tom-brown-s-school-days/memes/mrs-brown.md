# Mrs. Brown

[[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s mother — a quiet but constant presence in the novel's emotional background, whose love for her son and anxiety at his departure represent the domestic affections that the public school system both disrupted and deepened.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Mrs. Brown (first name not given)
- **Role**: Mother, wife of [[squire-brown|Squire Brown]]
- **Family**: [[squire-brown|Squire Brown]] (husband), [[tom-brown|Tom]] (eldest son), several younger sons
- **First appearance**: Chapter I (001-001)

## Character & Personality

Mrs. Brown appears only fleetingly, but her influence pervades the novel's early chapters. She is the source of the letters [[tom-brown|Tom]] writes home (007-020), the figure whose love he carries to [[rugby-school|Rugby]], and the representative of the domestic affections that [[the-great-school|the school]] system (007-020), for all its virtues, could not replace. [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s letter to his mother on his first [[sunday-at-rugby|Sunday]] (007-020) is the novel's acknowledgment that the boy's heart, however fully claimed by [[rugby-school|Rugby]], still belonged in part to home.

(007-020)

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Brown is a background figure — the novel's world is overwhelmingly masculine — but her presence reminds the reader that the public school was an interruption of family life, not its replacement. [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s development at [[rugby-school|Rugby]] is built on the foundation of love she and the [[squire-brown|Squire]] provided (007-020).

(007-020)
