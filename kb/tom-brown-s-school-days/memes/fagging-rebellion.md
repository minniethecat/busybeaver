# The Fagging Rebellion

[[tom-brown|Tom]] and [[harry-east|East]]'s organized resistance against the corrupt [[fagging|fagging system]] imposed by [[flashman|Flashman]] — the event that marked [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s emergence as a moral actor and the novel's central argument that righteous resistance to unjust authority is a schoolboy's duty.

## Overview

When [[flashman|Flashman]] and his allies corrupted the [[fagging|fagging system]] into systematic [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]], [[tom-brown|Tom]] and [[harry-east|East]] organized a strike — refusing to perform [[fagging|fag]]ging duties for unjust masters. The rebellion was the novel's pivotal moral event: it transformed [[tom-brown|Tom]] from a spirited boy into a leader, demonstrated the power of collective action, and tested the limits of the praepostor system's self-correction.

## Significance

The rebellion was the novel's proof that character could be exercised through resistance as well as obedience. [[tom-brown|Tom]] and [[harry-east|East]]'s refusal to accept unjust [[fagging|fag]]ging was not disobedience to legitimate authority but resistance to its corruption — a distinction the novel carefully maintains. The rebellion's partial success ([[flashman|Flashman]] was eventually expelled, though "the evil that men and boys too do lives after them" (009-030)) reflects the novel's realism: moral victories are real but rarely complete.
