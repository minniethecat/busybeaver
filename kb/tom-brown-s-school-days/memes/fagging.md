# Fagging

The system of personal service by which junior boys ("fags") performed domestic tasks for senior boys ("praepostors" or sixth-form boys) at Rugby School — a defining institution of Victorian public school hierarchy central to the novel's social world.

## Definition

Fagging was the informal but universal practice at Rugby (and other Victorian public schools) whereby younger boys in the lower forms served older boys in the sixth form and upper fifth. The duties included cleaning studies, running errands, fetching hot water, toasting bread, and generally making oneself useful. Though unregulated by the masters, it was a rigidly enforced social contract whose violation — by either party — could trigger open rebellion.

(007-026)

> Tom, as a new boy, was of right excused fagging for the first month, but in his enthusiasm for his new life this privilege hardly pleased him; and East and others of his young friends, discovering this, kindly allowed him to indulge his fancy, and take their turns at night fagging and cleaning studies.

## How It Worked at Rugby

Each praepostor's study had a complement of fags assigned from the lower school. The system operated largely outside the official school rules: masters rarely intervened, leaving enforcement to the boys themselves. Fags ran personal errands, cleaned rooms, fetched meals from the kitchen, and were expected to answer to any sixth-form boy's summons. In return, a just praepostor offered protection from bullying and a model of conduct — an unjust one could make life miserable.

### Daily Routine

The fag's day began early: fetching hot water for the praepostor's morning ablutions, sometimes requiring a trip down to the kitchen "to beg or steal hot water for him" (007-009). After dinner, fags were expected to be available for errands between meals and calling-over. In the evening they cleaned studies — sweeping floors, tidying desks, preparing fires. On match-days, Schoolhouse fags enjoyed unusual liberty: "none of the Schoolhouse praepostors stay by the door to watch for truants of their side; there is carte blanche to the Schoolhouse fags to go where they like" (005-052).

### Island-Fagging

A special form of the practice, "island-fagging," involved fags being sent on errands across the school grounds — a term whose very mention in Dr. Arnold's final conversation with Tom Brown signals how deeply the system shaped school memory (017-062).

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold and the masters maintained an official distance from fagging. The Doctor's philosophy was not to suppress the system by direct intervention — which "only makes it more underhand, and encourages the wrong boys" — but to elevate the moral character of the sixth form so that power was exercised justly. When praepostors governed well, the whole house benefited; when they abused their position, the younger boys had little recourse.

(006-030)

## The Boys' View

For many junior boys, fagging was simply the cost of admission to school society. Tom Brown, far from resenting his duties, "in his enthusiasm for his new life this privilege hardly pleased him" — he wanted to belong (007-026). But the system had a dark underbelly: when Flashman and his ilk controlled it, fagging became indistinguishable from bullying. The "strike against unlawful fagging" that Tom and East led was not a rejection of the system itself but a demand that it be governed fairly — "The cause was righteous" (009-030).

## Significance in the Novel

Fagging serves as the novel's central metaphor for governance and character formation. Thomas Hughes presents the system not as a relic to be abolished but as a proving ground: the fag learns obedience, the praepostor learns responsibility, and the moral health of the school rises or falls on whether that compact is honoured. The breakdown of fagging under Flashman's influence mirrors the broader collapse of school order after Brooke's departure, and its restoration signals the novel's deepest conviction — that institutions are only as good as the men who run them.
