# Mary

A [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] servant who appears as a warm, bustling figure in the boys' daily life — greeting them on their return from holidays, relaying messages to [[the-doctor|the Doctor]], and nursing [[arthur|Arthur]] through the fever outbreak.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Mary (surname unknown; called indifferently by her Christian name)
- **Age/Form**: Adult servant
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] (servant)
- **Role**: Housemaid, occasional sick-nurse, go-between for boys and [[the-doctor|the Doctor]]
- **Family**: None mentioned
- **First appearance**: Ch. X, "The [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] Return" (010-004)
- **Last appearance**: Ch. XV, "The Fever" (015-031)

## Character & Personality

Mary is one of the few female figures within the all-male world of [[rugby-school|Rugby School]], and her presence softens the institutional harshness. She is addressed by her Christian name alone — "she was called indifferently by either name" (010-004) — marking her as a familiar domestic presence rather than a figure of authority. The boys treat her with a mixture of teasing affection and rough familiarity.

> "Bless the boys!" cries Mary, at last getting in a word; "why, you'll shake me to death. There, now, do go away up to the housekeeper's room and get your suppers; you know I haven't time to talk." (010-008)

Her cheerful tolerance of the boys' energy — even when they nearly shake her to death — establishes her as a benign, motherly figure in the background of [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] life.

## Role in the Narrative

### Daily Life at Schoolhouse (Part II, Ch. X)

Mary first appears at the beginning of Part II, when the boys return from the Christmas holidays. Her exchange with them — answering questions about new boys, directing them to supper, tolerating their boisterousness — paints a quick, vivid picture of the domestic side of public-school life. She is an intermediary between the official world of masters and rules and the unofficial world of the boys.

(010-004 × 1, 010-008 × 1, 010-030 × 1)

### The Fever Outbreak (Part II, Ch. XV)

Mary's most significant role comes during the fever outbreak that kills [[thompson|Thompson]]. When [[arthur|Arthur]] falls ill, Mary becomes the crucial link between the sickroom and the outside world. She keeps [[tom-brown|Tom]] informed of [[arthur|Arthur]]'s condition daily, and when [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] forbids [[tom-brown|Tom]] from visiting (to prevent the fever's spread), Mary is the one who enforces — and occasionally bends — the quarantine.

[[arthur|Arthur]] himself recounts her devotion:

> "Mary has told me every day about you, and how she was obliged to make the Doctor speak to you to keep you away. I'm very glad you didn't get up, for you might have caught it." (015-017)

The detail that Mary had to ask [[the-doctor|the Doctor]] to intervene — implying she had tried and failed to keep [[tom-brown|Tom]] away herself — shows both her care for the boys and the limits of her authority.

When [[arthur|Arthur]] finally recovers enough to speak with [[tom-brown|Tom]], Mary facilitates the visit:

> "I've asked Mary to tell the Doctor that you are with me, so you needn't go down to calling-over." (015-031)

(015-017 × 1, 015-031 × 1)

### Go-Between to Authority (Part II, Ch. XII)

Mary also serves as an informal conduit to [[the-doctor|the Doctor]]. When a boy's [[the-studies|study]] habits become intolerable to his neighbours, "some fellow told Mary, and she told [[the-doctor|the Doctor]]" (012-007) — a path of least resistance that bypasses the official hierarchy of [[praepostor|praepostors]] and masters.

(012-007 × 1)

## Significance

Mary represents the invisible domestic infrastructure of a public school — the servants who cook, clean, nurse, and maintain the daily rhythms that the boys take for granted. Her presence, though brief in the text, humanises [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]. She is also an important structural device during the fever chapters: as the only character who moves freely between the sickroom, the boys, and [[the-doctor|the Doctor]], she enables the narrative to track multiple locations simultaneously without breaking point of view.

The fact that she is known simply as "Mary" — without surname, without formal title — reflects the class realities of Victorian service, but also the genuine affection in which she is held. She is not a comic figure like [[sally-harrowell|Sally Harrowell]], nor an antagonist like [[charity-lamb|Charity Lamb]]; she is simply a good servant, faithful in crisis and cheerful in routine.
