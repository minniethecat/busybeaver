# The Schoolhouse Match

The great [[football-at-rugby|football]] contest that marked [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]]'s first day at [[rugby-school|Rugby]] — fifty or sixty [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] boys in white trousers against the "huge mass" of the rest of [[rugby-school|the School]], a ritual that introduced the novel's central values of courage, endurance, and house loyalty.

(005-035)

## Overview

The match occupied nearly the entirety of Chapter V, the novel's first chapter set entirely at [[rugby-school|Rugby]]. It was played across the full length of the [[the-close|close]], with goals at either end, and involved the entire school — a sprawling, semi-organized contest that was as much social ritual as athletic competition.

(005-055)

## Significance

The match was the novel's opening argument for muscular Christianity. It demonstrated that [[courage|physical courage]], teamwork, and endurance were school virtues as important as any taught in the classroom. [[old-brooke|Old Brooke]]'s leadership on the field — courageous, tireless, and selfless — was the novel's first portrait of the ideal [[rugby-school|Rugby]] boy in action. And [[tom-brown|Tom]]'s eager participation — "I love [[football-at-rugby|football]] so, and have played all my life. Won't [[old-brooke|Brooke]] let me play?" (005-038) — marked his entry into [[the-great-school|the school]]'s emotional life.
