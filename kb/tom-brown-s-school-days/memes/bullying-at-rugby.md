# Bullying at Rugby

The systematic harassment of younger or weaker boys by older ones — the dark underside of the public school system that the novel treats as Rugby's central moral challenge, and whose defeat represents the ultimate vindication of Dr. Arnold's educational philosophy.

## Definition

Bullying at Rugby took forms ranging from casual cruelty to organized persecution. Younger boys could be tossed in blankets against their will, denied access to the fire, beaten for minor infractions, or subjected to sustained campaigns of humiliation. The system was tacitly tolerated so long as it remained "in nooks and corners" — invisible to the masters. It was only when bullying became open and lawless, as under Flashman's influence, that the moral order of the school was threatened enough to provoke reform.

## How It Worked at Rugby

The architecture of school life made bullying easy and detection difficult. Masters lived on the other side of the close, rarely venturing into the boys' quarters after hours. The praepostor system, intended to maintain order, could become an instrument of oppression when the wrong boys held power. Fagging — legitimate service — shaded imperceptibly into bullying when the social contract between senior and junior was broken.

### The Flashman Era

Flashman's tenure marked bullying at its most virulent. He combined physical cruelty with psychological manipulation, targeting Tom and East with "sly blows and taunts" that persisted even after his open power was checked (009-003). His departure did not end the problem — "the evil that men and boys too do lives after them" — but it removed the figure who had made bullying a system rather than a failing (009-030).

### The Rebellion Against Bullying

The fight against bullying is the novel's central moral drama. Old Brooke's speech to the Schoolhouse — "First, there's a deal of bullying going on. I know it well. I don't pry about and interfere; that only makes it more underhand" (006-030) — articulates the paradoxical strategy: not suppression from above, but the cultivation of a school culture in which bullying becomes shameful. The younger boys' declaration — "No more bullying" (006-061) — marks the turning point, and Tom and East's strike against unlawful fagging is the practical application of Brooke's principle.

## The Doctor's View

Dr. Arnold chose indirect influence over direct intervention. He believed that bullying could not be eradicated by rule — it had to be displaced by a higher standard of conduct modeled by the sixth form. When the sixth form governed justly, bullying retreated to the margins; when it failed, as after Brooke's departure, bullying flourished. The Doctor's patience was strategic: he was building not a police state but a moral community.

(006-030)

## The Boys' View

For the junior boys, bullying was the central fact of school life. Tom and East, as "young Ishmaelites, their hands against everyone, and everyone's hand against them" (009-032), experienced it as isolation and persecution. Yet the novel insists that bullying is not inevitable — the boys' own agency, exercised through collective resistance and the support of older allies like Diggs, proves essential to reform. The Doctor's victory over Tom "took eight long years to do it" (017-107); the victory over bullying was similarly slow, and similarly earned.

(009-032)

(008-057)

## Significance in the Novel

Bullying is the negative image of the novel's moral ideal. If Rugby at its best produces Christian gentlemen, Rugby at its worst — the Rugby of Flashman and the lawless fagging — produces tyrants and victims. The novel's argument is that the same institutions that enable bullying (praepostorship, fagging, house autonomy) can, when governed by men of character, become instruments of moral education. The story of Tom Brown's school days is, in large part, the story of bullying's defeat.
