# Walker

Walker is a bigger boy at [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] who appears in Chapter 6, "After the Match," as one of [[the-fight|the ring]]leaders in the ritual [[keeping-up|tossing]] of new boys. Alongside [[flashman|Flashman]], he directs the rough-house [[keeping-up|initiation]] that [[tom-brown|Tom Brown]] and [[harry-east|Harry East]] endure on their first night after the [[football-at-rugby|football]] match.

## Profile

- **Full name**: Walker (given name never specified)
- **Age**: Upper-school boy, old enough to exercise authority over younger boys
- **House**: [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]
- **Role**: Senior boy, participant in new-boy [[keeping-up|initiation]] rituals
- **Family**: Not mentioned
- **First appearance**: Chapter 6 (006-059)
- **Last appearance**: Chapter 6 (006-077)

## Character

### The Tossing Ritual

Walker's sole appearance occurs during the [[keeping-up|tossing]] scene in Number 7, the largest room in [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]]. When the bigger boys gather to toss the new boys in a blanket — a rough but semi-ritualised [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] tradition — Walker serves as a kind of master of ceremonies alongside [[flashman|Flashman]].

A frightened younger boy pleads: "Oh, please, [[flashman|Flashman]], please, Walker, don't toss me! I'll [[fagging|fag]] for you — I'll do anything — only don't toss me" (006-059). Walker's response reveals a more genial aspect than [[flashman|Flashman]]'s outright cruelty: "There's plenty of youngsters don't care about it. Here, here's Scud [[harry-east|East]] — you'll be tossed, won't you, young un?" (006-063). He appeals to the sporting spirit of the ritual rather than its punitive potential.

When the [[keeping-up|tossing]] begins in earnest, Walker leads the proceedings: "Come along then, boys" (006-069), and during the toss: "Now, boys, with a will. Once, twice, thrice, and away!" (006-075). [[flashman|Flashman]] suggests escalating the roughness — "Let's toss two of them together, Walker" (006-077) — positioning Walker as the slightly more restrained counterweight to [[flashman|Flashman]]'s malice.

### Role in the Narrative

Walker illustrates a subtler form of schoolboy [[bullying-at-rugby|bullying]] than [[flashman|Flashman]]'s outright tyranny. He participates in the [[keeping-up|tossing]] not from personal cruelty but from the momentum of tradition — it is "what bigger boys do" to new boys. His presence in the scene establishes a spectrum of [[schoolhouse|Schoolhouse]] power: from [[flashman|Flashman]]'s active malice, through Walker's thoughtless conformity to custom, to the silent discomfort of boys who "crept out from under the beds and behind them" (006-069). This moral grading is central to the novel's exploration of how institutional cultures shape individual behaviour.
