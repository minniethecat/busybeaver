# [[norah-vanstone|Norah]]'s Life as a [[governess|Governess]]

After being forced from [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]], [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]] — the elder, more reserved Vanstone sister — becomes a governess to support herself. Her life in service becomes a poignant symbol of the family's fall and, for Magdalen, a powerful motive to pursue the recovery of their stolen inheritance.

## Overview

Following the family's financial ruin, Norah takes a position as a governess in London, arranged through the school run by Miss Garth's sister. Her letters to Magdalen describe in detail "her hours for teaching, her hours of leisure, her hours for walking out with her pupils." This modest, industrious life contrasts sharply with Magdalen's secretive campaign of deception and disguise. The most dramatic glimpse of Norah's governess life comes when Magdalen, herself in disguise, secretly observes her sister being publicly humiliated by the children in her charge.

## Context

When [[michael-vanstone|Michael Vanstone]] inherited the entire family estate and refused to provide for his brother's orphaned daughters, Norah and Magdalen were left with nothing. Miss Garth took them to her sister's school in London, offering them refuge and the means to earn their living. Unlike Magdalen, who could not endure the quiet life and soon fled to pursue a stage career and a scheme of revenge, Norah accepted her reduced circumstances with quiet dignity and took up the governess's work that Miss Garth had helped her to secure.

## The Event

**The Governess's Routine**: Norah's later letters, as Magdalen recalled, "had described, in the fullest details, her life as a governess — her hours for teaching, her hours of leisure, her hours for walking out with her pupils." She lived in the household of her employer in New Street, Spring Gardens, and took her two young charges for daily walks through St. James's Park and the Green Park.

**Magdalen's Secret Observation**: On a pivotal day — the very morning Magdalen intended to present herself at Noel Vanstone's house to begin her scheme — she was seized by a sudden impulse to see her sister. Using the detailed schedule Norah had provided in her letters, Magdalen drove to New Street and watched her sister emerge from the house:

"She had become thinner since the autumn — her head drooped a little; she walked wearily. Her mourning dress, worn with the modest grace and neatness which no misfortune could take from her, was suited to her altered station; her black gown was made of stuff; her black shawl and bonnet were of the plainest and cheapest kind. The two little girls, walking on either side of her, were dressed in silk. Magdalen instinctively hated them."

**The Public Humiliation**: Following at a distance through the parks, Magdalen witnessed a scene that seared itself into her memory. When the children began to quarrel about which way to walk, "the youngest — a girl of eight or nine years old — flew into a child's vehement passion, cried, screamed, and even kicked at the governess." The people in the street stopped and laughed. One woman asked if Norah was the child's mother; another pitied her audibly for being the child's governess.

An old lady in an open carriage — the children's grandmother — intervened, calling the children into the carriage and beckoning contemptuously to Norah: "It's lucky I happened to pass this way. You never could manage my daughter's children, and you never will." The carriage drove away with Norah seated on the front seat like a servant.

Magdalen, watching in her disguise, was devastated. "Norah made an object of public curiosity and amusement; Norah reprimanded in the open street; Norah, the hired victim of an old woman's insolence and a child's ill-temper, and the same man to thank for it who had sent Frank to China!"

**The Passed Encounter**: Earlier, the sisters had passed each other face to face on the grass. Norah looked at the disguised Magdalen and saw only a stranger: "That glance of an instant struck Magdalen to the heart." She stood rooted to the ground, consumed by "a horror of the vile disguise that concealed her; a yearning to burst its trammels and hide her shameful painted face on Norah's bosom."

## Consequences

The sight of Norah's suffering hardened Magdalen's resolve. The thought that had turned her momentarily away from her scheme — love for her sister — now became the thought that sanctioned any means to achieve her end. Norah's life as a governess continued until her eventual marriage to George Bartram, which restored her to the gentility she had lost. The contrast between the sisters' responses to adversity — Norah's patient endurance and Magdalen's defiant scheming — became one of the novel's central moral dynamics.
