# [[marriage|Coverture]]

Coverture was the English common law doctrine under which a married woman's legal
rights and obligations were subsumed into those of her husband. In Wilkie Collins's
*No Name*, the doctrine functions as the invisible legal architecture that makes the
Vanstone sisters' [[disinheritance|disinheritance]] possible — and against which Magdalen wages her
unconventional campaign.

## The Doctrine

Under coverture, a married woman (*feme covert*) could not own property in her own
name, enter into contracts, or sue or be sued independently. Her legal existence was
suspended during marriage and merged with that of her husband. As William Blackstone
memorably formulated it: "By marriage, the husband and wife are one person in law."

The doctrine originated in medieval common law and persisted into the Victorian era.
It was gradually eroded by legislation — most notably the Married Women's Property
Act of 1870, passed eight years after *No Name* was published — but at the time of
the novel's setting, coverture remained the governing legal framework for married
women's rights.

## Role in *No Name*

The doctrine operates at two critical junctures in the novel:

1. **The Invalid Marriage**: Because [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]]'s American wife was still
   living at the time of his English ceremony with Magdalen and Norah's mother, the
   marriage was legally void. Under coverture principles, Mrs. Vanstone's
   independent means — the fortune she brought to the relationship — was absorbed
   into Andrew's estate, leaving her and her daughters with no separate legal claim.

2. **The Daughters' Illegitimacy**: Because the parents were never legally married,
   Magdalen and Norah were illegitimate at law — "No Name" in the novel's titular
   phrase. They had no right to inherit from their father's estate, which passed
   instead to the nearest legitimate male relative, [[michael-vanstone|Michael Vanstone]].

## Collins's Legal Critique

Collins, who trained as a barrister at Lincoln's Inn, uses coverture to mount a
sustained critique of the legal system's treatment of women. The novel demonstrates
that the law — far from being a neutral arbiter of justice — is a mechanism that
systematically strips women of property, identity, and agency. Magdalen's
extra-legal campaign of disguise and deception can be read as a response to a
system that offers women no lawful remedy.

The novel's timing is significant: published in 1862, it appeared during the
parliamentary debates that would eventually lead to the Married Women's Property
Acts. Collins's detailed dramatisation of the consequences of coverture can be read
as an intervention in this public debate — a fictional case study in the human cost
of legal doctrines that the reform movement sought to abolish.
