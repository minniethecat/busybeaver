# Serle Street, [[london|Lincoln's Inn]]

Serle Street, in [[lincolns-inn|Lincoln's Inn, London]], is the chambers address of [[mr-pendril|William Pendril]],
the Vanstone family solicitor in Wilkie Collins's *No Name*. It serves as the London
hub of the novel's legal correspondence and forms a geographical counterpoint to the
York offices at [[skeldergate|Skeldergate]].

## Role in the Narrative

Serle Street is introduced early in the novel when Mr. Vanstone writes his urgent
letter to "William Pendril, Esq., Serle Street, Lincoln's Inn, London," following
the discovery of his parents' marriage irregularity [[ch014-pendrils-letter]]. The
letter to Serle Street sets in motion the legal process that will culminate in the
catastrophe at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]].

Throughout the novel, Serle Street functions as the fixed point of legal authority.
Letters from Serle Street carry the pronouncements that reshape the Vanstone sisters'
lives: notices of the inheritance loss, legal opinions on their status, and ultimately
the communications that contribute to the resolution. The address appears at key
junctions — "Serle Street, October 27th, 1847," "Serle Street, January 4th, 1848" —
each date marking a significant legal development [[ch045-legal-correspondence]].

The firm of "Wyatt, Pendril, and Gwilt" at Serle Street represents the institutional,
impersonal face of Victorian law. The physical distance between Serle Street and the
emotional world of Combe-Raven — and later between Serle Street and the York
conspiracy — mirrors the gap between legal abstraction and human suffering that the
novel critiques.
