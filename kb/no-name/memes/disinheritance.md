## Overview

Dis[[the-will|inheritance]] is the central thematic device of Wilkie Collins' *No Name*. When [[andrew-vanstone|Mr. and Mrs. Vanstone]] die suddenly without a valid marriage, their daughters [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] are declared illegitimate and lose their inheritance to a distant relative they have never met. The novel uses this legal catastrophe to expose the vulnerability of women under Victorian inheritance law and to explore questions of identity, justice, and moral compromise.

## In the Novel

The disinheritance is triggered by the revelation that the Vanstone parents were not legally married at the time of their daughters' births. Mr. Vanstone had married a woman in America in his youth; that marriage was never dissolved, rendering his subsequent union with Mrs. Vanstone legally void. The couple themselves only learned of this legal obstacle shortly before their deaths, and their attempt to remedy it through a journey to London comes too late.

The legal mechanism of the disinheritance reflects actual Victorian law. Under the inheritance laws of the period, illegitimate children had no automatic right to inherit from their parents. The Vanstone estate passes instead to Andrew Vanstone's brother — a stranger to the daughters — leaving Magdalen and Norah with nothing.

Collins uses the disinheritance as more than a plot device. Through Magdalen's refusal to accept her fate, the novel interrogates the justice of laws that punished children for their parents' actions. As the [[ch003-PREFACE|Preface]] states, Magdalen's struggle embodies "those opposing influences of Good and Evil, which we have all felt, which we have all known."

## Related Concepts
