# The Rivals

Richard Brinsley Sheridan's 1775 comedy of manners, *The Rivals*, serves as the
amateur theatrical production at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] in Wilkie Collins's *No Name* — a
narrative device that introduces the theme of performance and foreshadows
[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s subsequent career of disguise and
deception.

## In the Novel

The amateur production of *The Rivals* is organized by [[mrs-marrable|Mrs. Marrable]],
who visits Combe-Raven with her husband and daughter [[miss-marrable|Miss Marrable]]
to recruit the Vanstone sisters for the cast [[vanstone-parents-death]]. The
production brings Frank Clare into closer contact with Magdalen and marks a
pivotal moment in the novel's early domestic phase — occurring just before the
Vanstone parents' sudden deaths transform the story from comedy of manners into
legal catastrophe.

Magdalen's performance as Lucy, the waiting-maid, astonishes the professional
stage-manager: "My heart alive! she speaks out without telling!" [[ch015-CHAPTER-XV]].
Her unexpected brilliance in the role — and the evident pleasure she takes in
impersonation — is the first explicit foreshadowing of the disguises she will
later adopt as Miss Bygrave, a parlour-maid, and other false identities.

## Dramatic Parallels

Collins's choice of *The Rivals* is thematically resonant. Sheridan's play —
with its plots of mistaken identity, secret correspondence, and romantic schemes
— mirrors the novel's own architecture of deception. Characters like Captain
Absolute (who assumes a false identity to court his beloved) and Mrs. Malaprop
(whose linguistic errors create comic confusion) prefigure Captain Wragge's
performances and Mrs. Wragge's unwitting comedy.

Captain Wragge himself repeatedly invokes theatrical metaphors and Sheridan's
style, framing his conspiracies as performances and himself as "manager" of a
touring company. The [[scientific-dialogues|Scientific Dialogues]] scheme —
Wragge's pose as a learned man — is another instance of the same theatrical
impulse first introduced through *The Rivals*.

## Thematic Significance

The early theatrical episode establishes a governing metaphor for the novel:
that identity in a society without legal protection may depend on performance.
Magdalen's legal nullification — she is "no name" — makes her a natural actress,
and the novel traces the psychological cost of maintaining false identities over
sustained periods. The comedy of *The Rivals* thus darkens into the drama of
*No Name*.
