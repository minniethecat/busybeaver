# Mrs. Ruddock

A lodging-house keeper in [[st-johns-wood|St. John's Wood]] whose conscientious letter to [[mr-loscombe|Mr. Loscombe]] provides the crucial external witness to [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s deteriorating condition in the late stages of the novel.

## Overview

Mrs. Ruddock — Catherine Ruddock — keeps a respectable lodging-house at Park Terrace, St. John's Wood, where Magdalen Vanstone (now Mrs. Noel Vanstone) takes rooms in November 1847. Though she appears only in a single letter and its reply, Mrs. Ruddock's intervention is morally significant: as "a mother of a family" writing "under a sense of responsibility," she alerts Mr. Loscombe to Magdalen's desperate circumstances at a moment when no one else knows her whereabouts. Her letter, dated June 2nd, reveals a woman who has observed her tenant's decline with genuine concern and acts from conscience rather than self-interest.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Ruddock's contribution is confined to two brief chapters (126–127) but carries weight disproportionate to its length.

**The lodging.** Magdalen first came to Mrs. Ruddock's apartments in November 1847, accompanied by her maid. Mrs. Ruddock notes that on both that occasion and the present one, "she has given me no cause to complain of her. She has behaved like a lady, and paid me my due" ([[ch126-CHAPTER-CXXVI]]).

**The warning.** By June 1848, Magdalen is alone, her maid gone and her funds exhausted. She has given notice and is preparing to leave. Mrs. Ruddock observes her burning letters, weeping, and declining rapidly — "In looks and conduct she has altered most shockingly in the last week. I believe there is some dreadful trouble on her mind; and I am afraid, from what I see of her, that she is on the eve of a serious illness" ([[ch126-CHAPTER-CXXVI]]). The letter conveys a quiet urgency: "If they lose to-morrow, they may lose the last chance of finding her."

**The response.** Mr. Loscombe's reply ([[ch127-CHAPTER-CXXVII]]) acknowledges Mrs. Ruddock's letter but, bound by professional constraints, he can offer no direct assistance — he does not know where Magdalen's relations can be found. The exchange dramatizes the isolation into which Magdalen has fallen: even a conscientious stranger cannot help her without the network of family and friends she has systematically severed.

## Relationships

- **[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]** — Mrs. Ruddock's tenant, known to her as "Mrs. Noel Vanstone." She observes Magdalen's physical and mental decline with mounting alarm.
- **[[mr-loscombe|Mr. Loscombe]]** — The lawyer to whom Mrs. Ruddock addresses her warning, having seen his name on letters Magdalen posted. He is the only link she can find to Magdalen's former connections.
