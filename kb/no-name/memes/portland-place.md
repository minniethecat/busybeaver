# Portland Place

The [[london|London]] home of the Tyrrel family, serving as [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]'s residence during her employment as governess and a key location in the novel's later romantic plotline.

## Overview

Portland Place is the London address of the Tyrrel family — friends of [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]] who employ Norah Vanstone as governess after the Combe-Raven catastrophe. It functions as Norah's sanctuary during the novel's middle and later volumes, and as the setting for the developing relationship between Norah and George Bartram.

## Role in the Narrative

Portland Place first enters the narrative when Mrs. Lecount investigates Norah's whereabouts, learning that she is "occupying her second situation as a governess" with "the Tyrrels of Portland Place." The address becomes a recurring location in the later chapters, referenced in the correspondence between characters. George Bartram's visits to Portland Place grow "more and more frequent," marking the quiet romantic subplot that contrasts with Magdalen's dramatic schemes.

Admiral Bartram himself visits Portland Place to satisfy his curiosity about Norah, an intervention that causes friction with George but ultimately confirms Norah's worthiness. Later, when the Tyrrels depart for Paris, the shuttered house at Portland Place signals a narrative transition toward the novel's final act at St. Crux.

- [[mrs-tyrrel]] — mistress of the household, friend of Admiral Bartram
