## Overview

Miss Brock is a minor character in Wilkie Collins's novel *No Name*. She is a young lady residing at the Grange, with whom [[george-bartram]] had a romantic involvement — described by [[admiral-bartram]] as a "sneaking kindness" — in the year preceding the main events of the novel.

## Role in the Plot

Miss Brock appears only by reference, never directly in the narrative. [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]] invokes her as part of his strategy to discourage George's attachment to [[norah-vanstone]]. The admiral proposes that George spend a week at the Grange in Miss Brock's society, giving her "a fair chance of lighting up the old flame again."

> "No longer ago than this time last year there was a sneaking kindness between you and that young lady, to say the least of it."

The admiral frames this as a test of George's commitment: if George can spend a week with Miss Brock and still return steadfast in his attachment to [[norah-vanstone|Norah]], the admiral promises to "knock under to necessity" and cease his objections to the marriage.

## Character

George dismisses the relationship with Miss Brock as "an idle flirtation" rather than a serious attachment, contrasting it with his feelings for [[norah-vanstone|Norah]]. The admiral, however, remembers the affair as significant enough to serve as a potential counterweight to George's new romantic interest.

> "You are confusing an idle flirtation, sir, with a serious attachment. You are altogether mistaken — you are, indeed."

Miss Brock is one of what the admiral calls his "round dozen of darlings" — a series of young women with whom George has been romantically linked over the years. Her narrative function is to illustrate both George's romantic history and the admiral's manipulative efforts to control his nephew's marital choices.
