# Life at [[vauxhall-walk|Vauxhall Walk]]

[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s sojourn at Vauxhall Walk in Lambeth represents the transitional period between her flight from home and her active campaign against the Vanstones. In these shabby London lodgings, she recovers from illness, develops her skills in theatrical disguise, and forms her alliance with Captain Wragge.

## Overview

After fleeing Miss Garth's sister's school and her brief attempt at a stage career in York, Magdalen Vanstone takes lodgings in Vauxhall Walk, Lambeth — a poor district on the southern bank of the Thames. Here, in a neighborhood of "miserable women" and "hideous London vagabonds," she lives under an assumed identity, practicing the art of disguise that will become her primary weapon. Captain Wragge, the charming swindler who claims a distant family connection, visits her here, and it is within these walls that Magdalen first articulates her determination to recover her father's fortune by any means necessary.

## Context

After the failure of the acting company in York — and Captain Wragge's failed theatrical speculations — Magdalen found herself in London with limited funds. Vauxhall Walk, situated in the "street labyrinth of Lambeth" near the old Archiepiscopal Palace, offered cheap lodgings suited to her diminished circumstances. The district was characterized by poverty and desperation: a place where "the sordid struggle with poverty shows itself unreservedly on the filthy pavement."

Magdalen took a room here under an assumed name. Her only possessions were a black box of clothes and the white silk bag containing extracts from her father's will and a lock of Frank Clare's hair. From her window, she could see nothing of the comfortable world she had lost — only the "dingy double row of buildings" that gave Vauxhall Walk its name.

## The Event

**The Illness at Vauxhall Walk**: Soon after taking lodgings, Magdalen suffered a severe fainting fit. The doctor who attended her reported that he had "recovered her with great difficulty from one of the most obstinate fainting-fits I ever remember to have met with." He noted "some heavy distress weighing on her mind" — she sat "perfectly silent, and perfectly unconscious of what goes on about her, for hours together, with a letter in her hand which she will allow nobody to take from her." The letter was from Norah, and its contents — news that Frank Clare had indeed sailed for China, ending all hope of his return — had precipitated the collapse.

**Captain Wragge's Visit**: Captain Wragge, having traced Magdalen to Vauxhall Walk, found her convalescent but altered. He observed her practicing disguises in her room: "She fitted and arranged the gray wig with the dexterity which constant practice had given her; fixed the false eyebrows carefully in their position with the gum she had with her for the purpose, and stained her face with the customary stage materials, so as to change the transparent fairness of her complexion to the dull, faintly opaque color of a woman in ill health."

Magdalen's skill astonished him: "She possesses extraordinary talent as a mimic. She has the flexible face, the manageable voice, and the dramatic knack which fit a woman for character-parts and disguises on the stage." It was here that Wragge conceived the plan of mounting a one-woman theatrical entertainment — an idea that would later evolve into their joint scheme to defraud Noel Vanstone.

**The View from the Window**: Vauxhall Walk itself was described as a place where the national prosperity mocked its inhabitants. "While the national prosperity feasts, like another Belshazzar, on the spectacle of its own magnificence, is the Writing on the Wall, which warns the monarch, Money, that his glory is weighed in the balance, and his power found wanting." This environment — the visible inequality, the daily humiliations of poverty — reinforced Magdalen's sense of injustice and hardened her resolve.

**The Partnership Forged**: In her Vauxhall Walk room, Magdalen and Captain Wragge struck their bargain. She would use her beauty and her talent for disguise; he would contribute his experience in deception and his gift for plausible imposture. Their target: Michael Vanstone — and, after his death, his son Noel. The captain, always alert to financial opportunity, saw in Magdalen a uniquely valuable partner. Magdalen, drained of hope and conventional scruple, saw in Wragge a necessary instrument.

## Consequences

The Vauxhall Walk period was the incubation of Magdalen's transformation. Here she shed the last vestiges of the girl she had been at Combe-Raven and became the woman capable of the deceptions she would carry out at Aldborough. Her mastery of disguise, honed in hours of solitary practice before the looking-glass in her shabby room, would prove decisive in her encounters with Noel Vanstone and Mrs. Lecount. The alliance with Captain Wragge, forged in these Lambeth lodgings, would carry her through the York, Aldborough, and Dumfries phases of her campaign. Yet Vauxhall Walk also marked the last time Magdalen was truly alone with her conscience — before action swept away the possibility of turning back.
