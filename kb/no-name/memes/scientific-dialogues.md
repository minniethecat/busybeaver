# Scientific Dialogues

*Joyce's Scientific Dialogues*, a popular nineteenth-century manual of science
education, becomes an unlikely prop in [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]]'s
campaign of deception during the Aldborough chapters of Wilkie Collins's
*No Name*.

## Overview

Wragge acquires the book as part of a calculated strategy to win the confidence
of [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]], whose late husband was a professor of science.
Having learned from a grocer-shop conversation that Mrs. Lecount's "one weak
point ... is a taste for science, implanted by her deceased husband, the
professor," Wragge purchases "that far-famed pocket-manual of knowledge" during
a trip to Ipswich [[ch058-XII-From-Captain-Wragge-to-Magdalen]].

His plan is characteristically audacious: to "privately inflate" himself "with
as much ready-made science as it will hold," and present himself to Mrs. Lecount
as "the most highly informed man she has met with since the professor's death."
He dubs this operation "Wragge, inflated by Joyce."

## In the Novel

The *Scientific Dialogues* appears at key moments throughout the Aldborough
chapters. Wragge studies it on the beach at dawn, "having been at Joyce's
Scientific Dialogues all the morning" [[ch064-CHAPTER-IV]]. He deploys a
signalling system involving a camp-stool — shifting it from left hand to right
when "talking Joyce" (leading up to a scientific point) and from right to left
when "talking Wragge" (irrelevant remarks) — so that Magdalen can follow his
tactical shifts [[ch064-CHAPTER-IV]].

The scheme succeeds: Mrs. Lecount, initially suspicious of "Mr. Bygrave," is
partially diverted by Wragge's scientific conversation. The book thus functions
as both a literal prop and a metafictional device — Wragge is performing
knowledgeability, just as Magdalen performs Miss Bygrave, just as actors
perform Sheridan.

After the Aldborough campaign ends, Wragge dismisses his prop with characteristic
insouciance: "You're done with already, my friend! ... Yesterday Joyce was my
all-in-all. To-day I don't care that for him!" [[ch065-CHAPTER-V]].

## Thematic Significance

The *Scientific Dialogues* encapsulates Wragge's method: surface knowledge
acquired quickly, deployed with "boundless confidence," and discarded once its
purpose is served. It also contrasts with the novel's treatment of genuine
expertise — Mrs. Lecount's inherited scientific knowledge through her late husband
is real enough to make her a formidable adversary, but Wragge's borrowed learning
proves equal to the task of diverting it.
