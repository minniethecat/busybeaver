## Overview

Marriage in Wilkie Collins's *No Name* is never merely a romantic union—it is a legal event with property consequences, a social institution that defines identity, and a strategic instrument that characters deploy against one another. The novel's entire catastrophe originates in a marriage that came too late: [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] married his daughters' mother only after [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] were born, making them illegitimate at law and extinguishing their inheritance rights. This single fact transforms marriage from a private matter of the heart into the pivot on which the entire plot turns.

## In the Novel

### The Vanstone Parents' Marriage

Andrew and Mrs. Vanstone's marriage, though loving, is legally catastrophic. Because they married after their children's birth, English law regarded Magdalen and Norah as *filius nullius*—children of nobody. The novel underscores the absurdity of this rule through the evident devotion of both parents and the complete normalcy of their family life at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]].

### The Secret Pregnancy

The Vanstones' journey to [[london|London]] conceals a medical consultation confirming Mrs. Vanstone's pregnancy. Her [[letters-and-correspondence|letter]] to Miss Garth, written after the fact, explains:

> Her object in accompanying her husband to London was to see a certain celebrated physician, and to consult him privately on a very delicate and anxious matter connected with the state of her health. In plainer terms still, this anxious matter meant nothing less than the possibility that she might again become a mother.
>
> — Mrs. Vanstone's letter explaining the London journey

### Marriage and [[the-will|The Will]]

Under English law, marriage automatically revoked a prior will—a rule designed to ensure that a testator's spouse and future children would not be disinherited by an outdated document. In the Vanstones' case, this well-intentioned rule produced catastrophe. Andrew's will, which left everything to his wife and daughters, was voided by the very marriage it should have protected. He died before writing a new one—and the absence of [[the-will|a valid will]] meant the absence of any legal recognition of his daughters.

### Marriage as Strategy

Magdalen's scheme to marry [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]] represents the novel's darkest inversion of the marriage plot. Under coverture, a wife's legal identity merged with her husband's—but a wife could also influence testamentary dispositions. Magdalen's plan to reclaim her inheritance through marriage exploits the very legal doctrines that destroyed her family, turning the institution against itself.

### Critique of Victorian Marriage Law

Collins, writing in 1862, uses the novel to expose the harshness of English marriage and inheritance law. The Vanstone sisters are punished not for any wrongdoing but for circumstances entirely beyond their control. The novel implicitly argues for legal reform, a cause Collins pursued more explicitly in *The Woman in White* (1859) and *Man and Wife* (1870), both of which critique specific legal inequities affecting married women and illegitimate children. In *No Name*, [[law-and-legality|the law]] emerges not as a guarantor of justice but as an impersonal machine that transforms private happiness into public ruin.
