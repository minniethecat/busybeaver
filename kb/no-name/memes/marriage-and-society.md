# [[marriage|Marriage]] and Society

Marriage in Wilkie Collins' *No Name* (1862) is never simply a romantic resolution. It is a legal contract, a financial transaction, and — in the novel's most devastating irony — the institution whose absence destroys the Vanstone family. Because [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] and his wife were not legally married at the time of their daughters' birth, Magdalen and Norah are condemned as illegitimate. The novel then traces the consequences through a series of marriages — real, proposed, and fraudulent — each revealing the gap between marriage as social ideal and marriage as legal machinery. ## Marriage as Plot Mechanism

The novel's marriage plot runs on two parallel tracks. On one side, Magdalen's near-marriage to Noel Vanstone — a fraud designed to secure the inheritance she believes is rightfully hers — collapses when Mrs. Lecount exposes her scheme. On the other, Norah's genuine attachment to George Bartram (see [[norahs-marriage-to-george]]) eventually resolves the plot through legitimate union and the recovery of the fortune. Between these poles, Collins examines the institution from every angle: the legal doctrine of [[coverture]] that extinguishes a wife's legal personhood, the financial calculus behind the marriage market, and the social pressure that makes marriage the only respectable destiny for a Victorian woman.
