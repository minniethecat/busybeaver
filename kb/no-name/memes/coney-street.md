# Coney Street, [[york|York]]

Coney Street is a central thoroughfare in York that appears in Wilkie Collins's
*No Name* as the address of Mr. Harkness, the solicitor who posts the reward
notice for [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s capture — representing the reach of the legal
establishment into the northern city.

## Role in the Narrative

Coney Street enters the novel in the reward notice circulated after Magdalen's
disappearance from London. The notice directs informants to "apply at the office
of Mr. Harkness, solicitor, Coney Street, York" or alternatively to Messrs. Wyatt,
Pendril, and Gwilt of [[serle-street|Serle Street, Lincoln's Inn]], London [[ch028-CHAPTER-I]].

When [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] arrives in York on Magdalen's trail, he
asks a local for directions to Coney Street — a detail that Collins uses to
establish Wragge's methodical intelligence-gathering. Wragge tracks the same
legal avenues that are tracking Magdalen: by visiting Coney Street, he confirms
that the lawyer's clerk has taken up position to monitor the railway station and
other exit points.

The pairing of Coney Street (York) with [[serle-street|Serle Street]] (London)
maps the novel's legal geography: the Vanstone case has two nodal points — the
London solicitors who manage the inheritance and the York agent who handles the
local search. [[kingsland|Kingsland]] and [[lincolns-inn|Lincoln's Inn]] form
other points in this network of legal surveillance that constrains the novel's
female protagonist.

## Significance

Coney Street serves a brief but precise function in the novel's legal plot. It
represents the institutional machinery — solicitors, clerks, reward notices —
that pursues Magdalen as a legal object rather than recognizing her as a moral
claimant. The street's mention alongside Serle Street and Lincoln's Inn
underscores the breadth of the legal network that a woman without legal standing
must navigate.
