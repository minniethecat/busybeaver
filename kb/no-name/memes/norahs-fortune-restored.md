# [[norah-vanstone|Norah]]'s Fortune Restored

## Overview

The legal restoration of [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]'s claim to the Vanstone
fortune — the resolution of the inheritance plot that has driven the entire narrative.
After [[george-bartrams-inheritance|George Bartram inherits]] under
[[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]'s Secret Trust, the Trust's marriage condition remains
unfulfilled, and the fortune passes through legal channels to the Vanstone sisters.

## Context

Since [[the-legal-discovery|the legal discovery]] that rendered the Vanstone parents'
marriage invalid, Norah and [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] have been legally
disinherited — declared "Nobody's Children" with no claim to their father's fortune.
Magdalen's entire campaign — the [[the-disguise-plot|disguises]], the
[[the-alborough-courtship|courtship]], the marriage to Noel Vanstone, and the
[[the-secret-trust-destruction|search for the Secret Trust]] — has been directed
toward recovering what was taken from them.

Norah's role throughout the novel has been defined by her resignation to fate. Unlike
Magdalen's active, often transgressive campaign of recovery, Norah accepts the loss
of the fortune and seeks to build an independent life as a governess. Her quiet dignity
and refusal to participate in [[the-inheritance-scheme|the inheritance scheme]] set her
in moral counterpoint to Magdalen, and it is this very quality — her willingness to
accept dispossession — that the novel ultimately vindicates when fortune returns to her
not through conspiracy but through the ordinary operation of law.

## The Event

The Secret Trust established by Noel Vanstone directed his fortune to
[[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]], and after the Admiral's death to
[[george-bartram|George Bartram]] — but only on condition that George marry within a
specified period. The Trust further stipulated that if George failed to comply with
this condition, the fortune would pass to alternate heirs
([[ch123-VI-From-Mr-Loscombe-to-Mrs-Noel-Vanstone]]).

Mr. Loscombe's legal strategy capitalizes on the imperfect drafting of the Trust:
like the Will, it was "not drawn by a lawyer," making its provisions vulnerable to
challenge when the marriage condition goes unmet. The fortune, originally denied to
the Vanstone sisters through a legal technicality, is restored to them through
another — the failure of an informally-drafted Trust to withstand legal scrutiny.

The marriage condition itself is significant: George Bartram is deeply attached to
Norah Vanstone, and the Trust's requirement that he marry within a fixed period forces
a collision between his affection and the coercive mechanism of the Trust. George
refuses to be "bribed into marrying" even the woman he loves, and his principled
resistance to the Trust's terms becomes the legal instrument through which fortune
passes to its rightful heirs. Collins constructs a resolution in which justice
prevails not through the perfection of law but through its imperfections — the
vulnerability of an amateurishly-drafted Trust to legal challenge, combined with the
moral integrity of the man who refuses its terms.

## Consequences

The restoration of Norah's fortune brings the novel's central conflict to its
resolution. It coincides with the reunion of the sisters at
[[the-final-recovery|Aaron's Buildings]], where Magdalen — ill and destitute — is
found by [[mr-kirke|Captain Kirke]]. The legal victory arrives not through Magdalen's
scheming but through the patient persistence of legal representatives and the
structural weaknesses in Noel Vanstone's testamentary arrangements.

The restoration of the fortune affirms the novel's implicit argument: that law,
however harsh in its letter, may yet deliver justice through its mechanisms — and that
the bonds of family persist beyond legal technicalities
([[ch131-CHAPTER-III]], [[ch132-CHAPTER-IV]]).

The resolution is structurally symmetrical. The novel's opening catastrophe — the
legal discovery that invalidates the Vanstone parents' marriage and disinherits their
daughters — is answered by a closing catastrophe in reverse: the legal failure of
Noel Vanstone's Secret Trust, which restores the fortune to those daughters. Both
events turn on the technical vulnerability of informally-drafted legal instruments,
and both illustrate Collins's persistent theme: that law is a machinery as likely to
produce injustice as justice, depending on who operates it and toward what end.
