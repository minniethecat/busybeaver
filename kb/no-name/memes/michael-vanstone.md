## Overview

Michael Vanstone is the elder brother of [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]], from whom he has been estranged for thirty years when the novel opens. When Andrew dies intestate — his [[the-will|will]] having been invalidated by the irregularity of his marriage — the entire [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] estate of eighty thousand pounds passes to Michael under the default rules of English [[law-and-legality|inheritance law]]. Despite being urged by the family [[law-and-legality|law]]yer [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]] to make some provision for his brother's orphaned daughters, Michael refuses absolutely. His own superstitious refusal to confront his mortality leads him to die without a [[the-will|will]] in turn, perpetuating the legal chain of injustice into the next generation.

## Background

The estrangement between Michael and [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] dated back some thirty years before the events of the novel. Michael was the elder son and, by the custom of primogeniture, the natural heir to the family's position. The precise cause of their falling-out is never detailed, but it was deep enough that the two brothers had no [[letters-and-correspondence|communication]] for decades. Michael lived abroad, eventually settling in [[zurich|Zurich]], where he cultivated the habits of a miser. He was a widower; his wife had died some years earlier, leaving him with one son, [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]], with whom he lived "on excellent terms."

## Role in the Narrative

Michael Vanstone never appears directly in the novel; his role is entirely mediated through the reports of [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]], the Vanstone family [[law-and-legality|law]]yer, and through the investigations of [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]]. Despite his physical absence, his decisions drive the entire plot.

When [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]] writes to inform Michael of his brother's death and the [[the-will|inheritance]] he has received, he makes a full and compassionate case for the orphaned daughters — explaining that they are "Nobody's Children" under the [[law-and-legality|law]] and entirely dependent on their uncle's mercy. Michael's response is delivered through a [[london|London]] solicitor, in "insolently curt terms," declaring his instructions "final" and refusing all further [[letters-and-correspondence|correspondence]].

[[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]]'s verdict is damning:

> "In all my unhappy experience of the worst side of human nature, I have never met with a man so utterly dead to every consideration of mercy as Michael Vanstone." ([^1])

Michael's refusal is not merely passive. As [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]] methodically establishes through her questioning of [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]], Michael knew every detail of the tragedy: he knew of the first [[the-will|will]], he knew what prevented the making of a second [[the-will|will]], he had read his brother's letter expressing the "great anxiety" to provide for his daughters — and he rejected them nonetheless.

## The Irony of His Death

The novel's most pointed irony attaches to Michael Vanstone's end. Having refused to honor his brother's wishes because no legal obligation compelled him, Michael himself dies intestate — the victim of his own "insurmountable horror of contemplating the event of his own death." As [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] reports:

> "Two doctors attended him in his last illness; warned him that he was too old a man to hope to get over it; and warned him in vain. He announced his own positive determination not to die. His last words in this world... were: 'I'm getting better every minute; send for the fly directly and take me out for a drive.'" ([^2])

The same night, "Death proved to be the more obstinate of the two," and Michael's entire fortune passed to Noel — perpetuating the same legal accident that had disinherited [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] and [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]].

## Relationships

Michael's only significant relationship within the novel is with his son **[[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]**, with whom he reportedly lived "on the most friendly terms." He had also maintained a connection with [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]] and his nephew [[george-bartram|George Bartram]] (the son of [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]]'s late sister), though the nature of these relationships is not explored in detail. His housekeeper, **[[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]**, had served him since his wife's death and had acquired "a strong influence" over both father and son.
