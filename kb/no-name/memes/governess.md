# The Governess in No Name

The governess occupies an ambiguous position in Victorian society — neither servant
nor gentlewoman, educated but economically dependent — and Wilkie Collins uses this
institution in *No Name* to dramatise the precarious position of unmarried women
without family or fortune.

## The Victorian Governess: Historical Context

In mid-nineteenth-century England, governessing was one of the only respectable
occupations available to educated women who needed to support themselves. The 1851
census recorded approximately 25,000 governesses in England and Wales — a number
that reflected both the expansion of middle-class demand for private education and
the desperate narrowness of women's economic options. The founding of the Governesses'
Benevolent Institution in 1841 testified to the widespread recognition of the
governess's vulnerability: underpaid, socially isolated, and with no pension or
security, the governess was in many ways the emblematic figure of the "distressed
gentlewoman" in Victorian social discourse.

Contemporary commentators described the governess's plight in stark terms. Harriet
Martineau, writing in the *Edinburgh Review* in 1859, called the governess "the
most hardly-worked, the worst paid, and the least considered of all the toiling
sisterhood." The problem was structural: the supply of educated women needing
employment far exceeded the demand from middle-class households, driving wages
down to subsistence levels and leaving a large population of women perpetually on
the edge of destitution.

Collins was keenly attentive to this social reality, and his portrayal of [[norah-vanstone|Norah]]'s
governessing draws on contemporary debates about women's employment, education, and
economic independence that were central to the "Woman Question" of the 1860s. His
sustained attention to the material conditions of women's lives — the wages, the
accommodation, the daily routines — distinguishes *No Name* from the more
romanticized governess narratives that preceded it.

## [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]'s Employment

After the [[disinheritance|disinheritance]], Norah Vanstone takes a position as governess in a
household in [[new-street-spring-gardens|New Street, Spring Gardens, London]], arranged through Miss Garth's
sister. The position is obtained not through Norah's own efforts but through the
patronage network of family connections — a circumstance that underscores how
entirely dependent she is on others' goodwill after the loss of her fortune.

> Her life, as she described it to Miss Garth, consisted of "her hours for
> teaching, her hours of leisure, her hours for walking out with her pupils" — a
> modest, industrious routine that contrasted, in its quiet regularity, with the
> stormy and uncertain career on which Magdalen had embarked. (050-003)

Collins deliberately renders Norah's governess life in flat, repetitive terms: the
diurnal round of lessons, walks, and constrained leisure conveys the narrowness of
her existence without sentimentalizing it. The prose mirrors the life it describes
— orderly, contained, and suffocating in its very predictability.

The position at Evergreen Lodge, Clifton, with the Marrable family represents a
step up from her London situation — better pay, more pleasant surroundings, a
family that treats her with some kindness — but the fundamental dynamic remains
unchanged: Norah is a dependent in a household not her own, her status permanently
ambiguous and her future permanently uncertain.

### The Hierarchy of Victorian Domestic Service

To understand Norah's position at Evergreen Lodge, it is essential to recognize the
governess's unique location in the Victorian domestic hierarchy. A household like
the Marrables' would include:

- The family themselves (Mr. and Mrs. Marrable, their daughters)
- The governess (Norah) — educated, genteel, but salaried
- Upper servants: butler, housekeeper, lady's maid
- Lower servants: housemaids, kitchen maids, footmen

The governess was positioned above the servants in education and manners but below
the family in status and authority. She ate with the family at meals but retired
when guests arrived; she was addressed as "Miss" but paid a wage; she was expected
to model gentility for her pupils while being herself dependent on her employer's
goodwill for her survival.

This structural ambiguity had practical consequences. A governess could not complain
about her treatment without risking her position and her reference — and without a
reference, she could not secure another position. She could not socialize with the
servants without losing the gentility that was her only professional asset, and she
could not socialize with the family without transgressing class boundaries. She was,
in effect, a woman suspended between two worlds, belonging fully to neither.

## Social Position and Moral Significance

The governess's position in the Victorian household was notoriously uncertain. She
was expected to be educated and refined — qualities associated with gentility — yet
she worked for wages and occupied a space between the family and the servants. She
ate with the family but was not of the family; she taught the children manners but
was herself regarded as a kind of superior servant. This structural ambiguity made
the governess peculiarly vulnerable: too genteel to complain, too dependent to
leave, too refined to associate with the servants, and insufficiently born to be
accepted by her employers as an equal.

Collins dramatises this ambiguity most powerfully in the pivotal scene at New Street,
Spring Gardens — a scene that Magdalen, herself disguised, secretly observes:

> She saw Norah enter the room — saw her take her place at the table — saw the
> children look at her with a sly expression of triumph, and the grandmother speak
> to her with a quiet assumption of authority which it was evidently impossible to
> resist. (052-007)

The scene is doubly ironic: Magdalen watches her sister — the woman she loves most
— being reduced to the status of a failed servant by her employers, and she can do
nothing without compromising her own disguise and the entire inheritance scheme. The
governess's helplessness becomes a mirror for Magdalen's own, and the scene
crystallizes the novel's argument that women without money are, in Victorian society,
women without agency.

What makes this scene particularly painful is the presence of the children. The
governess's authority over her pupils is her one remaining mark of status — the one
arena in which she is permitted to exercise judgment and command. When the children
defy her and their grandmother supports them, the last vestige of the governess's
dignity is stripped away. Collins renders this moment with an almost anthropological
precision: the grandmother's quiet assumption of authority, the children's sly
triumph, and Norah's silent endurance all register as symptoms of a larger social
pathology.

### The Governess as Moral Contrast

Norah's acceptance of governessing represents one pole of the novel's moral
spectrum: patient endurance of reduced circumstances through honest work. She
accepts her fate without bitterness, finds what dignity she can in useful
employment, and refuses to compromise her principles even when doing so might
improve her circumstances. This stoic acceptance is presented not as weakness but as
a form of moral clarity — a clarity that Magdalen, for all her brilliance and
determination, has sacrificed in pursuing her campaign.

Magdalen's refusal of the same fate — her flight to the stage and her campaign of
deception — represents the other pole. The novel does not simply endorse one
response over the other; rather, it places them in productive tension. Norah's path
is morally admirable but practically precarious; Magdalen's is morally compromised
but practically effective. The governess figure thus operates as both a social
institution and a narrative device, measuring the distance between the sisters'
responses to adversity and asking the reader to consider what a woman in their
position could reasonably be expected to do.

Collins sharpens this contrast by making the governess's moral position as ambiguous
as her social one. If Norah's patient endurance is noble, it is also, in a sense,
complicit: her refusal to fight back against the system that has wronged her ensures
that the system will continue to wrong others. If Magdalen's defiance is morally
compromised, it is also, in a sense, liberatory: her willingness to break the rules
exposes the rules themselves as arbitrary and unjust. The governess, by embodying
both the dignity and the helplessness of the virtuous woman, becomes the novel's
most searching interrogation of Victorian gender ideology.

## Literary Context

Collins's treatment of the governess draws on a rich tradition of Victorian
governess narratives, most famously Charlotte Brontë's *Jane Eyre* (1847) and Anne
Brontë's *Agnes Grey* (1847). Unlike these novels, however, *No Name* does not make
the governess its central consciousness; Norah's experiences are seen largely from
the outside — through Magdalen's eyes, through Miss Garth's reports, through the
narrative's own summary. This external perspective reinforces the governess's
predicament: she is seen but not heard, her interior life inaccessible to those who
observe her, her story always told by someone else.

The choice to render the governess from the outside is itself a formal argument
about the governess's social position. In *Jane Eyre*, the governess speaks — the
entire novel is her first-person account — and the act of narration is an act of
self-assertion. In *No Name*, the governess is silent, and her silence is the
condition of her social existence. Collins's formal choice thus echoes his thematic
argument: the governess, however admirable, cannot speak for herself in the public
sphere, and her story — like her fate — must be determined by others.
