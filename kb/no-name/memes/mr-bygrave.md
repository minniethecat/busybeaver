# Mr. Bygrave

The alias adopted by [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] during his scheme to
secure the Vanstone inheritance, posing as a retired military man of independent
means residing at North Shingles Villa in Aldborough. The Bygrave identity is the
centrepiece of Wragge's "Skins To Jump Into" method — assuming the name and
circumstances of a deceased person to create a plausible cover story
([[ch058-XII-From-Captain-Wragge-to-Magdalen]], 058-020). Under this name, Wragge
orchestrates the approach to [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]] and the scheme to
marry him to [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] (posing as Miss Bygrave).

## Role in the Narrative

Captain Wragge selects the name "Bygrave" from a meticulously maintained list of
deceased individuals whose identities he has researched — his "Skins To Jump
Into" catalogue. This grotesque filing system documents the names, circumstances,
and personal histories of the recently dead, allowing Wragge to assume a
pre-fabricated identity at a moment's notice ([[ch058-XII-From-Captain-Wragge-to-Magdalen]],
058-018). The fictional Mr. Bygrave is presented as a man of scientific
interests and financial independence, deliberately crafted to appeal to Noel
Vanstone's tastes and to deflect suspicion from [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]
([[ch058-XII-From-Captain-Wragge-to-Magdalen]], 058-022).

The Bygrave party arrives at Aldborough and takes up residence at North Shingles
Villa, where they are listed in the Visitors' List as "Mrs. Bygrave" and "Miss
Bygrave" — a detail Wragge exploits as social proof of their respectability
([[ch059-XIII]], 059-012). Mr. Bygrave's first encounter with Noel Vanstone and
Mrs. Lecount occurs during a walk along the Aldborough sea-front, where Wragge
deploys his characteristic verbosity — a calculated performance of harmless
loquacity — to disarm the housekeeper's analytical instincts while Magdalen
engages Noel Vanstone in separate conversation ([[ch064-CHAPTER-IV]], 064-030).

The Bygrave identity functions on two levels simultaneously. On the surface, it
is a simple imposture — a name borrowed from a dead man to hide Wragge's true
identity. But Wragge's genius is to invest the Bygrave persona with genuine
enthusiasm: his scientific conversation, his investment schemes, his
gentlemanly bearing — all are performed with an actor's commitment that makes
them convincing precisely because they draw on Wragge's own tastes and talents
([[ch058-XII-From-Captain-Wragge-to-Magdalen]], 058-024).

### Mrs. Lecount's Counter-Investigation

Mrs. Lecount, though initially charmed by Mr. Bygrave's scientific conversation —
the two share an interest in natural history that Wragge exploits masterfully —
becomes increasingly suspicious of the Bygrave household. She recognizes
something "vaguely familiar" in Miss Bygrave's voice and manner
([[ch065-CHAPTER-V]], 065-015). Her investigations — including a journey to
London to verify the Bygraves' credentials — eventually threaten to expose the
entire imposture and force Wragge into the desperate counter-measure of
discrediting Lecount herself ([[ch065-CHAPTER-V]], 065-040).

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-wragge|Mrs. Wragge]]** — poses as "Mrs. Bygrave," Mr. Bygrave's
  domestically-inclined wife. Wragge's real wife, with her enormous stature and
  childlike simplicity, is a liability to the scheme that Wragge manages through
  strict behavioral instructions
- **[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]** — poses as "Miss Bygrave,"
  Mr. Bygrave's niece and ward. The Bygrave identity provides the protective
  familial frame within which Magdalen can pursue Noel Vanstone without
  arousing suspicion
- **[[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]** — the target of the Bygrave scheme;
  introduced to Miss Bygrave by Mr. Bygrave himself. Noel's hypochondria and
  vanity make him susceptible to the Bygraves' calculated flattery
- **[[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]** — the primary antagonist who sees through
  the Bygrave imposture. Her intelligence and determination make her Wragge's
  most formidable adversary in the entire novel

## Thematic Significance

The Mr. Bygrave alias represents the most fully realized example of Collins's
recurring motif of identity-as-performance. Unlike Magdalen's disguises, which
are born of desperation and carry immense personal risk, Wragge's Bygrave is
a professional creation — coolly calculated, expertly maintained, and backed
by a systematic method. The contrast between Wragge's professional imposture
and Magdalen's amateur — but more emotionally committed — performances is one
of the novel's richest ironies.

The Bygrave identity also embodies the novel's critique of social identity as
inherently performative. Wragge succeeds as Bygrave not despite being a
fraud, but because the qualities that define a respectable gentleman —
scientific interests, financial independence, polite conversation — are
themselves performable. Collins suggests, through Wragge's effortless
inhabitation of the Bygrave role, that the boundary between authentic and
assumed identity is far more porous than Victorian society wished to
acknowledge.
