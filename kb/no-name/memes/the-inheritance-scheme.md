# The [[the-will|Inheritance]] Scheme

The organized conspiracy devised by [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] at [[aldborough|Aldborough]] to secure the Combe-Raven inheritance for [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] through the calculated entrapment of [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]].

## Overview

Having agreed to assist Magdalen in her pursuit of the inheritance, Captain Wragge brings to the enterprise what Magdalen alone lacks: method. His scheme at Aldborough is a masterpiece of practical conspiracy — a three-act structure of approach, courtship, and marriage that anticipates and neutralizes every obstacle, principally the formidable [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]. Wragge himself describes his method with characteristic self-regard: "I never do things by halves" ([[ch080-CHAPTER-LXXX]]). The scheme is the novel's most sustained comic set-piece and its most disturbing moral transaction — a conspiracy conducted with the precision of a military campaign and the ethics of a fraud.

## The Event

**Phase 1: Approach.** Wragge and Magdalen (disguised as "Miss Bygrave," Wragge's niece) establish themselves at North Shingles, Aldborough, strategically positioned to encounter Noel Vanstone and Mrs. Lecount. Wragge exploits his distant family connection to Noel Vanstone — they are related through the Vanstone line — to engineer a plausible introduction.

**Phase 2: Neutralize Lecount.** Recognizing Mrs. Lecount as the principal obstacle, Wragge orchestrates her removal through an elaborate deception. He plants in Noel Vanstone's mind a step-by-step script — counted off on thumb and fingers — designed to make Lecount believe Noel is acting independently. "To-day you tell Mrs. Lecount I have tried to take you in... To-morrow you cut me on the Parade. The day after you refuse to go out... The next day you accept the suggestion. And the next day to that you go to St. Crux" ([[ch080-CHAPTER-LXXX]]). Lecount is dispatched to [[zurich|Zurich]] on a false errand, leaving the field clear for the marriage.

**Phase 3: The marriage.** With Lecount absent, Wragge arranges a secret wedding. The scheme's success is measured by its outcome: Magdalen becomes Mrs. Noel Vanstone, and the Combe-Raven inheritance is redirected to her control — at least temporarily.

### Key Moments

**The pocket-book scene.** When Noel Vanstone casually mentions [[st-crux-in-the-marsh|St. Crux-in-the-Marsh]] as his destination, Wragge immediately recognizes the strategic opportunity and writes down the address "as follows: 'Admiral Bartram, St. Crux-in-the-Marsh, near Ossory, Essex'" ([[ch080-CHAPTER-LXXX]]). The moment illustrates Wragge's gift for turning chance information into tactical advantage.

**The Zurich letter.** Wragge arranges for a letter from Zurich to arrive at Aldborough on the morning after Noel Vanstone's departure, creating the appearance of urgent business requiring Lecount's presence on the Continent. The ruse succeeds perfectly: Lecount departs, the marriage proceeds, and the scheme reaches its conclusion before she can return.

## Consequences

The inheritance scheme achieves its immediate goal — Magdalen obtains the money — but proves unstable. Lecount's counter-scheme, involving the Secret Trust, ultimately strips Magdalen of the inheritance. Wragge himself vanishes with his share of the proceeds, leaving Magdalen to face the consequences alone. The scheme thus exemplifies the novel's pattern: tactical brilliance married to moral blindness, producing victories that collapse into defeat.

## Associated Characters

- **[[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]]** — The scheme's architect and ringmaster
- **[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]** — Wragge's partner, executing the plan as "Miss Bygrave"
- **[[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]** — The unwitting target
- **[[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]** — The antagonist the scheme is designed to neutralize

## Moral Architecture of the Scheme

Wragge's scheme is structured around a central moral paradox: it pursues a just end — the restoration of stolen inheritance — through unjust means. Collins never lets the reader forget this tension. Wragge himself is magnificently indifferent to moral categories, treating the conspiracy as a purely technical problem. His genius lies in situational manipulation: he understands that Noel Vanstone is governed by two impulses — the desire to assert independence from Mrs. Lecount, and hypochondriacal self-absorption — and he plays both with virtuoso precision.

The five-day countdown he scripts for Noel — "To-day you tell Mrs. Lecount I have tried to take you in... To-morrow you cut me on the Parade..." — is the novel's most ingenious piece of stage-management ([[ch080-CHAPTER-LXXX]]). Wragge doesn't merely instruct Noel what to do; he provides a temporal architecture that makes each step feel like Noel's own idea. The thumb-and-fingers mnemonic device reduces complex social deception to a mechanical sequence a man of Noel's limited intelligence can follow.

## The Zurich Gambit

The Zurich letter represents Wragge's tactical masterpiece. Understanding that Mrs. Lecount's devotion to her employer extends to protecting his financial interests, Wragge fabricates a letter suggesting that a Zurich banking house holds information about a potential inheritance claim against Noel's estate. The letter is timed to arrive the morning after Noel's departure for St. Crux — ensuring that Lecount, unable to consult her master, must act on her own judgment. Her departure for the Continent is the scheme's hinge point; without it, the marriage cannot proceed.

The gambit's brilliance lies in its exploitation of Lecount's own strengths. Her vigilance, her financial acumen, her determination to protect Noel's interests — all the qualities that make her a formidable antagonist — become the vectors of her defeat. Wragge doesn't outsmart Lecount so much as he weaponizes her competence against her.

## The Scheme's Collapse and Legacy

The scheme's collapse traces the same architecture as its success: the conspirators' cleverness contains the seeds of its own undoing. Wragge's decision to vanish with his share of the proceeds — exactly the behavior his history of fraud would predict — leaves Magdalen without her most capable ally. Lecount, returning from Zurich to discover the marriage accomplished, deploys a counter-conspiracy of equal ingenuity: the Secret Trust. The inheritance that Magdalen believed secure proves to have been only borrowed.

Yet the scheme is not a simple failure. It establishes the template for the novel's subsequent action — disguise, deception, the weaponization of legal machinery — and it transforms Magdalen from a wronged daughter into an active agent, however morally compromised. The inheritance scheme is, in effect, her education in the arts of survival that Victorian England offers to women who have been stripped of legal and economic protection.
