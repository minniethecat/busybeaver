# [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]'s Departure

The defeat and departure of [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]] from England — the resolution
of the novel's principal antagonist arc. After her [[mrs-lecounts-counterplot|counter-plot]]
against [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]], Mrs. Lecount's schemes are finally
thwarted, and she leaves for the Continent.

## Context

Mrs. Lecount, [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]'s housekeeper, has been the most
formidable adversary in Magdalen's campaign. Her intelligence, patience, and capacity
for investigation nearly destroyed Magdalen's plans at
[[the-alborough-courtship|Aldborough]] and [[mrs-lecounts-counterplot|Baliol Cottage]].
After Noel's death, Lecount continues to act in what she perceives as her late
master's interest — protecting the Secret Trust from Magdalen's reach
([[ch092-CHAPTER-II]], [[ch093-CHAPTER-III]]).

## The Event

Lecount's defeat is not dramatic — it unfolds through letters and legal maneuvering
rather than direct confrontation. After [[admiral-bartrams-death|Admiral Bartram's
death]], the legal machinery set in motion by [[mr-pendril|Mr. Loscombe]] leaves
Lecount without leverage. The Trust she helped create to exclude Magdalen has, through
the failure of its marriage condition, produced the opposite result: the fortune is
returning to the Vanstone sisters.

Mrs. Lecount, recognizing the finality of the legal resolution, departs England for
the Continent — a quiet exit that mirrors the novel's broader pattern of resolutions
achieved through legal processes rather than dramatic confrontations
([[ch105-CHAPTER-I]]). Her departure, like her schemes, is methodical rather than
melodramatic.

## Consequences

Lecount's departure removes the last active obstacle to
[[norahs-fortune-restored|the restoration of the Vanstone inheritance]]. It also marks
the completion of the novel's antagonist arc: unlike many Victorian villains, Lecount
is neither punished nor redeemed — she simply leaves, her schemes undone not by
Magdalen's cleverness but by the impersonal machinery of law. Her departure confirms
the novel's implicit thesis that law, not individual will, is the true governing force
of human affairs.
