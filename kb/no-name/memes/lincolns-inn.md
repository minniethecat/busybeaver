# [[london|Lincoln's Inn]], London

Lincoln's Inn is one of the four Inns of Court in London — the professional
associations to which every English barrister must belong. In Wilkie Collins's
*No Name*, it appears as the location of Mr. Pendril's legal chambers and
represents the institutional legal world that determines the Vanstone sisters'
fate.

## Collins's Connection

Collins himself was admitted to Lincoln's Inn in 1846 and called to the bar in
1851, though he never practised. His legal training at Lincoln's Inn gave him
the precise knowledge of [[law-and-legality|inheritance law]], testamentary instruments, and the
workings of solicitors' chambers that he draws upon throughout *No Name*.

## Role in the Novel

Mr. Pendril, the Vanstone family solicitor, dispatches his letters from
Lincoln's Inn — the letters that transform private legal facts into the public
knowledge that destroys the sisters' prospects. The Inn functions as the
physical locus of the impersonal legal machinery that Collins critiques: a
distant, masculine institution whose decisions reverberate through the domestic
world of Combe-Raven with devastating consequences.

The choice of Lincoln's Inn — rather than a generic London legal address — is
characteristic of Collins's commitment to legal accuracy. Pendril's chambers at
Lincoln's Inn anchor the novel's legal plot in a specific, verifiable geography.
