# Dunwich

Dunwich is a decayed coastal town on the [[aldborough|Suffolk]] coast, near [[aldborough|Aldborough]], that serves as the destination for a pivotal carriage excursion in Chapters 65 and 66 of Wilkie Collins's *No Name*. Once a "populous and thriving port" much of which has been swallowed by the sea, Dunwich provides the atmospheric backdrop for [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]'s first concerted attempt to expose [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s disguise as "Miss Bygrave."

## The Excursion

The excursion is proposed by Mrs. Lecount herself, writing to [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] at North Shingles with an invitation for a drive to Dunwich. The apparent friendliness alarms the captain, who immediately recognizes it as a tactical shift: "It means that Mrs. Lecount is even sharper than I thought her. She has found you out" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V]. He accepts nonetheless, determined to meet the challenge.

The party consists of [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]], Mrs. Lecount, Captain Wragge, and Magdalen. On the journey out, Mrs. Lecount acts her part flawlessly — "motherly with Magdalen and tenderly attentive to Noel Vanstone" — maintaining "her customary elegance and propriety" as "the only one of the party on that sultry summer's day who was perfectly cool in the hottest part of the journey" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V].

## Ruins and Sand-Hills

Upon arrival, the party visits the ruins of a convent — "the last relic of the once populous city of Dunwich which has survived the destruction of the place, centuries since, by the all-devouring sea" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V]. From there they proceed to a little wood between the village and the low sand-hills overlooking the German Ocean. It is here that Captain Wragge executes a strategic diversion: he maneuvers to separate Magdalen and Noel from Mrs. Lecount, taking the wrong path and becoming lost "with the most consummate dexterity." While the captain and Mrs. Lecount wait on the sand-hills — two determined enemies feigning friendly conversation — Magdalen advances her hold on Noel Vanstone's affections in private.

## Mrs. Lecount's Attack

The return journey is the true theater of the excursion. Mrs. Lecount, as Captain Wragge predicted, "shows her claws." She leads the conversation toward London and Vauxhall Walk, then directly asks Magdalen whether she knows "a lady named Miss Garth" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V]. She proceeds to describe, in Magdalen's own presence and with "a refinement of contempt which was little less than devilish," the disguised visit paid to Vauxhall Walk — effectively recounting Magdalen's own actions while studying her reaction.

Captain Wragge rescues the situation by feigning a sudden "neuralgic attack" in Magdalen, and Noel Vanstone's anxious sympathy completes the diversion. Mrs. Lecount "accepted her defeat without uttering a word," though she flashes a look at the captain as they part that "scorched up the captain's politeness on the spot" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V].

## The Aftermath

The Dunwich excursion proves to be a turning point. Magdalen, though shaken, tells the captain: "She has given me the courage to go on" [^ch065-CHAPTER-V]. Mrs. Lecount, undeterred, immediately begins drafting her letter to [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]] to gather evidence for a second assault. The captain, for his part, institutes the policy of keeping Magdalen confined to her room as "Miss Bygrave" — a strategy of mystification that accelerates Noel Vanstone's infatuation through the frustration of absence.

## Significance

The Dunwich episode exemplifies Collins's technique of transforming a placid seaside excursion into a battle of wits. The ruined convent and sand-hills provide a landscape of decay and erosion that mirrors the moral terrain of the novel — ancient structures undermined by forces beyond their control, much as Magdalen's identity and the Vanstone inheritance have been undermined by the legal catastrophe at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]]. The episode also marks the moment when the conflict between Mrs. Lecount and Captain Wragge becomes explicitly personal, setting the stage for the escalating tactics that culminate in the marriage plot.
