# The Ship Counting

The ship counting is one of the most dramatic and psychologically intense scenes in Wilkie Collins's *No Name*, occurring in Chapter 73 on the night before [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]'s forced marriage to [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]. Alone at [[sea-view|Sea-view Cottage]] (in fact, at [[north-shingles|North Shingles]], [[aldborough|Aldborough]]), she buys laudanum and stages a desperate gamble with fate that brings her to the very edge of suicide.

## Context

Magdalen has spent weeks cultivating Noel Vanstone's affections under the alias "Miss Bygrave," aided by [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] and opposed by [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]]. With the marriage set for Monday and Captain Wragge away, she is overwhelmed by horror at the prospect of the wedding. The chapter traces her psychological deterioration across Thursday, Friday, and Saturday — days in which she twice visits a chemist, purchases laudanum, writes a farewell letter to her sister [[norah-vanstone|Norah]], and finally sets her survival on the passage of ships.

## The Laudanum

On Saturday morning, Magdalen enters the Aldborough chemist's shop and purchases a half-ounce bottle of laudanum, claiming it is for toothache. When she asks whether danger lurks in "such a little drop," the chemist replies: "There is Death in it, miss. [...] Death to the strongest man in England, let him be who he may" [^ch073-CHAPTER-XIII]. The bottle is labeled POISON in large letters.

## The Ship Counting

In the early hours of Sunday morning, after Captain Wragge returns and confirms that "the marriage is a certainty," Magdalen prepares to take the laudanum. But at the moment her lips touch the glass, her body rebels:

> At the first cold touch of the glass on her lips, her strong young life leaped up in her leaping blood, and fought with the whole frenzy of its loathing against the close terror of Death. [^ch073-CHAPTER-XIII]

She opens her window to the dawn and sees a fleet of coasting vessels approaching on the tide. In a moment of desperate superstition — inspired by a newspaper account of a murderer who decided his victim's fate by throwing a plow-spud into the air — she devises her own ordeal:

> She resolved to end the struggle by setting her life or death on the hazard of a chance. [...] For one half-hour to come she determined to wait there and count the vessels as they went by. If in that time an even number passed her, the sign given should be a sign to live. If the uneven number prevailed, the end should be Death. [^ch073-CHAPTER-XIII]

## The Count

The ships pass at irregular intervals through the morning mist. By the twenty-eighth minute, seven ships have gone by — the fatal uneven number. With two minutes remaining, Magdalen takes the poison in one hand and her watch in the other. At the final second, an eighth ship appears:

> She never moved, she never spoke. The death of thought, the death of feeling, seemed to have come to her already. She put back the poison mechanically on the ledge of the window and watched, as in a dream, the ship gliding smoothly on its silent way — gliding till it melted dimly into shadow — gliding till it was lost in the mist. [^ch073-CHAPTER-XIII]

The even number spares her. She whispers, "Providence? [...] Or chance?" and collapses at the window, later found by the maid "sleeping as quietly as a new-born child" [^ch073-CHAPTER-XIII].

## Significance

The ship counting scene is arguably the novel's emotional climax before the resolution. It crystallizes the central tension of Magdalen's character — the conflict between her fierce will to live and the despair produced by the moral degradation her campaign has required. The scene's power derives from its fusion of the sensational (laudanum, suicide, fateful counting) with the psychologically acute (Magdalen's divided consciousness, the involuntary rebellion of her body against her will). It also demonstrates Collins's mastery of suspense through temporal compression: the entire action unfolds in exactly thirty minutes of narrated time.
