# Dumfries ([[baliol-cottage|Baliol Cottage]])

A cottage on the banks of the River Nith, a few miles above the town of Dumfries in southern Scotland, where [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]] and his wife retreat after their hasty marriage — the setting for the novel's Fifth Scene.

## Overview

Baliol Cottage, near Dumfries, represents the last domestic arrangement of Noel Vanstone's short married life. Situated on a bend of the Nith — "a few miles above Dumfries," as the address specifies ([[ch090-CHAPTER-XC]]) — the cottage is remote enough to afford seclusion but close enough to the market town for practical needs. The journey there is traced in the novel's geography: Noel Vanstone and his wife "have been traced across the Solway Firth to Dumfries, and thence to a cottage a few miles from the town, on the banks of the Nith" ([[ch090-CHAPTER-XC]]). It is here that Magdalen, having tracked her quarry north, begins the final phase of her campaign to recover the inheritance.

## Description

Collins's description of the prospect from Baliol Cottage balances beauty against the wintry November light. From the window, the eye follows "the course of the Nith at a bend of the river," with "broad tracts of the level cultivated valley" visible through "wintry gaps in the wooded bank." Boats pass on the river; carts plod along the high-road to Dumfries. The sky is clear, and "the November sun shone as pleasantly as if the year had been younger by two good months." The view is "noted in Scotland for its bright and peaceful charm" — a peace that stands in ironic counterpoint to the predatory scheme unfolding within the cottage walls ([[ch090-CHAPTER-XC]]).

Noel Vanstone, tellingly, is indifferent to the landscape: "If it had been hidden in mist or drenched with rain, Mr. Noel Vanstone would, to all appearance, have found it as attractive as he found it now." He waits sullenly at the window until his servant Louisa knocks, then turns back to the breakfast-table — a man whose wealth has bought him a view he cannot see.

## Role in the Narrative

**The Fifth Scene.** Baliol Cottage is the stage for the novel's Fifth Scene, the penultimate movement in which the conflict between Magdalen and Noel Vanstone is brought to its final domestic setting. The cottage's isolation — surrounded by Scottish countryside, far from London and from the legal apparatus of Lincoln's Inn — makes it both a refuge and a vulnerability.

**Magdalen's pursuit.** Learning of the Vanstones' location through her intelligence network, Magdalen travels north. The journey to Dumfries represents her determination to pursue her objective to whatever remote corner of Britain it may lead. The cottage on the Nith becomes the arena for the last direct confrontation between the disinherited daughter and the man who holds what she believes to be hers.
