## Overview

Mrs. Lecount is the housekeeper and chief antagonist opposing [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] in Wilkie Collins's *No Name*. A Swiss widow of middle age who has served the [[andrew-vanstone|Vanstone]] family since Mrs. Michael Vanstone's death, she wields considerable influence over both [[michael-vanstone|Michael Vanstone]] and his feeble son [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]. Described as a woman of remarkable physical preservation—she could pass for thirty-eight despite being some fifteen years older—Mrs. Lecount combines ingratiating manners with a calculating intelligence that makes her a formidable adversary. Her loyalty, however, is governed entirely by self-interest: she protects the Vanstone fortune because her own livelihood depends upon it.

## Appearance and Manner

Mrs. Lecount's physical appearance is one of the novel's most striking descriptive passages:

> She found herself in the presence of a lady of mild, ingratiating manners, whose dress was the perfection of neatness, taste, and matronly simplicity, whose personal appearance was little less than a triumph of physical resistance to the deteriorating influence of time. If Mrs. Lecount had struck some fifteen or sixteen years off her real age, and had asserted herself to be eight-and-thirty, there would not have been one man in a thousand, or one woman in a hundred, who would have hesitated to believe her.
>
> — *No Name*, Chapter relating Magdalen's first meeting with Mrs. Lecount

Her dark hair, only just turning gray; her smooth white forehead; her plump, dimpled cheeks; her regular white teeth; and her large, melting black eyes—every detail is calculated to disarm suspicion. Yet her thin lips, when they forget to smile, reveal the hardness beneath the surface.

## Role in the Narrative

### Guardian of the Vanstone Fortune

Mrs. Lecount enters the narrative as the housekeeper of Michael Vanstone, the estranged brother who inherits the Combe-Raven estate after Andrew Vanstone's sudden death. As Norah's inquiries reveal:

> She is a native of Switzerland, elderly, and a widow. Her name is Mrs. Lecount.
>
> — Report on the Vanstone household

### The Vauxhall Walk Confrontation

When Magdalen, disguised as the elderly Miss Garth, visits Noel Vanstone's residence in Vauxhall Walk, London, she encounters Mrs. Lecount for the first time. The housekeeper immediately senses something amiss, studying her visitor with those "mild and melting" black eyes while positioning her chair to force Magdalen into the light.

> "Accept my excuses, ma'am, for the place in which I am compelled to receive you," proceeded Mrs. Lecount in fluent English, spoken with a foreign accent. "Mr. Vanstone is only here for a temporary purpose. We leave for the sea-side to-morrow afternoon."
>
> — *No Name*, the Vauxhall Walk interview

Mrs. Lecount's letter refusing further communication with the Vanstone sisters demonstrates her skill at weaponizing politeness: she "takes the highest moral ground, in a tone of spiteful politeness."

### The Aldborough Campaign

At [[aldborough|Aldborough, Suffolk]], where Noel Vanstone takes up summer residence at Sea-view Cottage, the contest between Mrs. Lecount and Magdalen (now disguised as Miss Bygrave) intensifies. Captain Wragge, observing the housekeeper, identifies her one vulnerability:

> Mrs. Lecount's one weak point, if she has such a thing at all, is a taste for science, implanted by her deceased husband, the professor.
>
> — Captain Wragge's letter to Magdalen

Wragge exploits this by purchasing *Joyce's Scientific Dialogues* and inflating himself with "ready-made science" to gain her trust—a strategy that proves temporarily effective.

## Methods and Tactics

Mrs. Lecount operates through a distinctive repertoire of manipulative techniques:

| Tactic | Description | Example |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Politeness as Weapon | Uses elaborate courtesy to deliver refusals and threats | The letter forbidding further contact with the Vanstone sisters |
| Strategic Positioning | Physically arranges scenes to her advantage | Placing Magdalen's chair opposite the window |
| Information Gathering | Systematically collects intelligence about adversaries | Investigating "Miss Bygrave" and Captain Wragge |
| Proxy Communication | Speaks and writes on Noel Vanstone's behalf | Dictating terms while citing her master's "delicate health" |
| Scientific Pretension | Deploys her late husband's scientific learning as social currency | Discussions of hydrostatics with Captain Wragge |

## Analysis

Mrs. Lecount represents a recurring Collins type: the intelligent servant whose competence becomes threatening when aligned against the protagonist. Unlike the novel's other antagonists, she is motivated not by malice but by a clear-eyed calculation of her own interests. She understands that her position depends on Noel Vanstone retaining his fortune—and that the Vanstone sisters' claims threaten that arrangement.

Her tragedy, within the novel's moral framework, is that her intelligence serves no purpose beyond self-preservation. She deploys considerable strategic gifts—surveillance, impersonation-detection, social manipulation—in the service of maintaining a comfortable housekeeping position. Collins implicitly contrasts her cunning with Magdalen's: both women are clever, both use disguise and misdirection, but Magdalen's goals (however questionably pursued) derive from a legitimate moral claim, while Mrs. Lecount's derive from pure self-interest.

## Appearances

Mrs. Lecount appears throughout the middle sections of the novel, from the investigation into the Vanstone household through the Aldborough episodes. Her appearances are concentrated in:

- The Brighton investigation (via reports to Norah and Miss Garth)
- The Vauxhall Walk residence in [[london|London]]
- [[aldborough|Aldborough, Suffolk]], at Sea-view Cottage
- The correspondence plotting against Magdalen and Captain Wragge
