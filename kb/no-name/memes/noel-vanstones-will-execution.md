# [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]'s Will Execution

The moment at [[dumfries|Baliol Cottage, Dumfries]] when [[noel-vanstone|Noel
Vanstone]], under the watchful supervision of [[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]], signs
his last will and testament — creating the Secret Trust that will govern the
disposition of his fortune and drive the novel's final act.

## Context

After his marriage to [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] (who has concealed her
true identity as "Miss Bygrave"), Noel Vanstone becomes increasingly suspicious and
dependent on his housekeeper Mrs. Lecount. It is Lecount who guides him toward a
testamentary arrangement designed to ensure that Magdalen — whom Lecount has correctly
identified as an impostor — never receives a shilling of the Vanstone fortune
([[ch092-CHAPTER-II]]).

## The Event

At Baliol Cottage, Noel Vanstone executes his will under Mrs. Lecount's supervision.
The will bequeaths the entire residue of his estate to [[admiral-bartram|Admiral
Bartram]] as sole executor — but attaches a Secret Trust, a separate letter of
instructions, that the Admiral is to hold privately. The Trust directs that the
fortune pass to [[george-bartram|George Bartram]] only if George marries within a
specified period. If George fails to comply, the fortune is to pass to alternate heirs
([[ch093-CHAPTER-III]], [[ch070-CHAPTER-X]]).

The Trust is "not drawn by a lawyer" — a circumstance that Mr. Loscombe later
identifies as the critical weakness in Noel's testamentary scheme
([[ch123-VI-From-Mr-Loscombe-to-Mrs-Noel-Vanstone]]). The informal drafting means
the Trust is more vulnerable to legal challenge than a properly executed legal
instrument would be.

The Secret Trust letter, kept in Admiral Bartram's possession, is the very document
that Magdalen will later risk everything to find at St. Crux — the object of
[[the-secret-trust-search|the search]] and [[the-secret-trust-destruction|the
discovery]] that form the novel's climactic sequence.

## Consequences

Noel's will achieves the opposite of what he and Lecount intended. By creating a
complex, informally-drafted Trust with a marriage condition, they inadvertently
create the legal pathway through which the Vanstone fortune ultimately returns to
[[norah-vanstone|Norah]] and [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]]. The will is a study
in the irony of testamentary intentions: the more elaborate the exclusion, the more
vulnerable it becomes to the very law it attempts to manipulate.
