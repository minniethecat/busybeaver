[[mrs-wragge|Mrs. Wragge]]'s Fortune is the comic backstory event in Wilkie Collins's *No Name* that explains how [[captain-wragge|Captain Horatio Wragge]] — the novel's roguish confidence-man — acquired, and systematically squandered, the inheritance of his simple-minded giantess wife. Revealed in chapters 35–38 as part of Wragge's autobiographical narrative to [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] at [[aldborough|Aldborough]], the story provides essential background to the Wragge-Magdalen partnership while simultaneously exposing the captain's thoroughgoing moral bankruptcy.

The fortune originated with Matilda Wragge, a "poor ignorant woman, who had served as a waitress at some low eating-house, who had unexpectedly come into a little money, and whose small inheritance he had mercilessly squandered to the last farthing" (Mrs. Vanstone's letter, ch3). The sum was £2,000 — a modest but not trivial inheritance in Victorian England, sufficient for a comfortable middle-class existence if prudently managed. Wragge, however, regarded it as capital for his perpetual schemes, and his marriage to Matilda was, from the beginning, a financial transaction disguised as a romantic one.

The captain's account of meeting his future wife is characteristically self-serving. He presents himself as having rescued a woman of limited mental capacity from exploitation by others, when in fact he was the exploiter. The descriptions of [[mrs-wragge|Mrs. Wragge]] throughout the novel emphasize her gentle, bovine simplicity — "Giants of both sexes are, by a wise dispensation of Providence, created, for the most part, gentle" (ch37) — and her complete intellectual dependence on her husband. Her signature affliction, "the Buzzing in my head," recurs whenever she attempts to think independently, a detail that is simultaneously comic and pathetic.

The squandering of the fortune is never narrated directly but is implied through Wragge's perpetual financial embarrassment and his frank admission of past failures. He had been expelled from his militia regiment, "had tried one employment after another, and had discreditably failed in all. He had lived on his wits, in the lowest and basest meaning of the phrase" (ch3). By the time of the novel's main action, the £2,000 is long gone, and Wragge survives on whatever he can extract from his various schemes — including his partnership with Magdalen, from which he expects substantial profit.

The story of Mrs. Wragge's fortune serves multiple narrative functions. It establishes Wragge's untrustworthiness even as Magdalen is about to entrust him with her fate. It provides the emotional foundation for the Wragge marriage, a union of comic incongruity that Collins uses as counterpoint to the novel's serious marriages — [[magdalen-marriage|Magdalen's]] fraudulent union with [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]] and [[norahs-marriage-to-george|Norah's]] honorable marriage to [[george-bartram|George Bartram]]. And it contributes to the novel's sustained critique of marriage as an economic institution, a theme that connects the Wragges' sordid transaction with the Vanstone parents' [[the-vanstone-parents-secret|illegal union]] and Magdalen's calculated self-sale.
