## Overview

Mr. Clare is one of Wilkie Collins's most entertaining minor characters — a crook-backed, slovenly-dressing philosopher who lives in a small cottage at the edge of the [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] grounds, surrounded by a magnificent library that represents "the one important interest of Mr. Clare's life." A widower of many years, he professes to have transcended all human sentiment, including parental affection, yet his actions repeatedly contradict his misanthropic pronouncements. His long friendship with [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] — conducted through a ritual of evening arguments and morning reconciliations — provides some of the novel's warmest comic passages.

## Background

Mr. Clare belonged to "the younger branch of a family of great antiquity," and the "one inheritance of importance that he had derived from his ancestors was the possession of a magnificent library, which not only filled all the rooms in his modest little dwelling, but lined the staircases and passages as well." He had been a widower for many years and "made no secret of his philosophical resignation to the loss of his wife."

Collins's catalogue of his character is a masterpiece of comic portraiture:

> "He called himself a pauper with a pedigree. He abandoned the entire direction of his household to the slatternly old woman who was his only servant, on the condition that she was never to venture near his books, with a duster in her hand, from one year's end to the other. His favorite poets were Horace and Pope; his chosen philosophers, Hobbes and Voltaire. He took his exercise and his fresh air under protest; and always walked the same distance to a yard, on the ugliest high-road in the neighborhood. He was crooked of back, and quick of temper. He could digest radishes, and sleep after green tea. His views of human nature were the views of Diogenes, tempered by Rochefoucauld; his personal habits were slovenly in the last degree; and his favorite boast was that he had outlived all human prejudices." ([^1])

## Role in the Narrative

Mr. Clare serves as a comic foil to the sentimentality of the Vanstone household and, later, as an unexpected source of steadying wisdom in the aftermath of catastrophe. His relationship with Andrew Vanstone — "almost close enough to be called a friendship" — is conducted through a ritual of evening disputations: "Mr. Vanstone flourishing the stout cudgels of assertion, and Mr. Clare meeting him with the keen edged-tools of sophistry. They generally quarreled at night, and met on the neutral ground of the shrubbery to be reconciled together the next morning."

His most memorably perverse performance is his attitude toward his three sons — Frank, Cecil, and Arthur — whom he regards with philosophical disdain:

> "I look at those boys with a perfectly impartial eye; I dismiss the unimportant accident of their birth from all consideration; and I find them below the average in every respect. The only excuse which a poor gentleman has for presuming to exist in the nineteenth century, is the excuse of extraordinary ability. My boys have been addle-headed from infancy." ([^1])

When Frank returns from his failed engineering apprenticeship, Mr. Clare's greeting is characteristically acid: "Exactly what I predicted. Don't interrupt me by making explanations; and don't frighten the cat."

Yet beneath this performance of indifference, Mr. Clare proves himself a man of substance. After the Vanstone catastrophe, it is he who offers his cottage as accommodation for [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]], and it is he who — in his oblique, unsentimental way — provides comfort and practical support to the bereaved household. [[miss-garth|Miss Garth]], who had initially distrusted him, comes to recognize the genuine kindness beneath the misanthropic exterior.
