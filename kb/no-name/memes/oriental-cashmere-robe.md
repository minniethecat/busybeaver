# The Oriental Cashmere Robe

[[mrs-wragge|Mrs. Wragge]]'s perpetual dressmaking project in Wilkie Collins's *No Name*: a
garment that functions as a recurring comic motif while also serving as cover for
the novel's espionage and counter-espionage in the Aldborough chapters.

## Overview

The Oriental Cashmere Robe is introduced when [[mrs-wragge|Mrs. Wragge]] — who
accompanies her husband [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] to [[aldborough|Aldborough]]
— purchases the expensive fabric in London and resolves to make the dress herself
to save money. Her repeated failures to complete the garment, and her obsessive
rereading of the dressmaking directions, become one of the novel's most sustained
comic running gags. Yet the robe also serves a structural purpose: it provides
[[mrs-lecount|Mrs. Lecount]] with a pretext for entering the Wragge household and
subjecting the women to her surveillance.

## In the Novel

Mrs. Wragge first mentions the robe as an economy measure: "I went shopping in
London, and bought an Oriental Cashmere Robe. It cost a deal of money; and I'm
going to try and save, by making it myself" [[ch063-CHAPTER-III]]. The project
consumes her attention throughout the Aldborough chapters — she repeatedly reads
her directions "for the hundredth time at least" before she can "screw up her
courage to put the scissors into the stuff" [[ch065-CHAPTER-V]].

The robe's narrative function crystallizes during Mrs. Lecount's visit to the
Wragge household. Spotting the garment "lying half made, and half unpicked again,
on the table," Mrs. Lecount seizes it as a conversational opening, claiming a
shared expertise in dressmaking to establish an intimate tone [[ch070-CHAPTER-X]].
She artfully uses the robe as "a means ready at hand of reaching the end she had
in view" — namely, probing Mrs. Wragge and Magdalen for information about
"Mr. Bygrave."

The encounter climaxes when Mrs. Lecount, having extracted what she needs,
"coolly tossed the Oriental Cashmere Robe into Mrs. Wragge's expansive lap and
left the room in an instant" [[ch070-CHAPTER-X]]. The insult transforms
Mrs. Wragge from gentle giantess to outraged defender of her project:
"You nasty foreigner, you ought to be ashamed of yourself!" The robe thus
becomes a measure of character — Mrs. Wragge's simple dignity versus
Mrs. Lecount's calculating disdain.

## Thematic Significance

The robe serves as a quiet counterpoint to the novel's theme of [[identity-and-disguise|disguise
and performance]]. While Magdalen assumes elaborate theatrical identities,
Mrs. Wragge's costume-making is guileless — a domestic activity that inadvertently
provides cover for the conspiracy. The never-finished garment mirrors the novel's
larger concern with incompletion and unresolved identity.
