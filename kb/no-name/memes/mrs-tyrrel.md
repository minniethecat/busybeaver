## Overview

Mrs. Tyrrel is a minor but warmly drawn character in Wilkie Collins's novel *No Name*. She and her husband, Mr. Tyrrel, reside in [[portland-place|Portland Place]], London, and are old friends of [[admiral-bartram]]. Mrs. Tyrrel employs [[norah-vanstone]] as a governess to her children and becomes one of Norah's most loyal supporters.

## Background

The Tyrrels of Portland Place are described as friends whom Admiral Bartram had "lost sight of latterly." They are a family of means — Mr. Tyrrel owns a yacht — and they travel to the Continent. When Norah Vanstone comes into their household as governess, they treat her "with as much kindness and consideration as if she was a member of the family."

> "They are friends of my uncle's, whom he has lost sight of latterly — the Tyrrels of Portland Place — and they treat Miss Vanstone with as much kindness and consideration as if she was a member of the family."

The Tyrrels took Norah with them on their travels to the Continent, and upon their return, Norah immediately set out for Aldborough to search for her missing sister [[magdalen-vanstone]].

## Character and Relationships

Mrs. Tyrrel is described by [[mrs-lecount]] as "an excellent, warm-hearted woman, with a quick temper and very little judgment." She is strongly attached to Norah and heartily interested in her welfare. This loyalty leads her into conflict with Admiral Bartram when he visits Portland Place.

> "Mrs. Tyrrel is one of those people. She is an excellent, warm-hearted woman, with a quick temper and very little judgment; strongly attached to Norah, and heartily interested in Norah's welfare."

During the admiral's visit, he expresses his opinion that Norah's prospects are damaged by her sister Magdalen's scandalous conduct. Mrs. Tyrrel first resents this in his presence as "worldly and selfish," then later interprets it behind his back as an insult to a lady in her own house — a deliberate hint to discourage [[george-bartram]]'s visits.

## Role in the Plot

Mrs. Tyrrel serves several narrative functions. She provides Norah with a secure home and employment after the Vanstone family's reversal of fortune. Her Portland Place residence becomes a meeting point where [[george-bartram]] calls on her, allowing his relationship with Norah to develop. The Tyrrels' departure for Paris also creates a plot complication, as [[mrs-lecount]] must follow them across the Channel to continue her schemes.

When Norah's character is questioned, Mrs. Tyrrel is among those who defend her, "blessing the day when she came into the house." Her warm advocacy counterbalances the novel's more calculating characters.
