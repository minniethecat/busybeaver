# Mrs. Attwood

Mrs. Attwood is a minor character in Wilkie Collins's novel *No Name* who
nonetheless plays a pivotal role in the plot's climactic phase. She serves as
[[mr-loscombe|Mr. Loscombe]]'s housekeeper at his offices in [[lincolns-inn|Lincoln's Inn, London]]. A
respectable elderly widow with a large family of daughters, she becomes an
unwitting source of intelligence for [[magdalen-vanstone]] in her scheme
against [[admiral-bartram]] and [[noel-vanstone]].

Mrs. Attwood exemplifies a recurring character type in Collins's fiction: the
innocent informant whose guilelessness makes them the perfect vehicle for
information the plot needs to convey. Like the servants in *The Woman in White*
whose gossip provides crucial clues, or the landladies in *The Moonstone* whose
observations advance the investigation, Mrs. Attwood speaks without calculation
and is heard with calculation — her openness is the very quality that makes
her useful to Magdalen's schemes.

## Background

Mrs. Attwood is a widow who has seen better days and is described as an
educated person — a detail that distinguishes her from the comic servant
types that populate much Victorian fiction ([[ch088-CHAPTER-IV]], 088-015).
She has a large family of daughters, all of whom are in domestic service. Her
reduced circumstances have led her to the position of housekeeper at
Mr. Loscombe's professional offices — distinct from his private residence,
a separation that Collins notes with the precision of a legal novelist
attentive to the architecture of professional life.

> "She is a widow, with a large family of daughters. Her daughters are all in service." ([[ch088-CHAPTER-IV]], 088-018)

The economic trajectory of Mrs. Attwood's family — from respectability to
reduced circumstances, with daughters dispersed into service — mirrors in
miniature the novel's larger preoccupation with the fragility of middle-class
status. The Vanstone sisters' own fall from comfort to precarity is echoed
in the Attwood family's quieter decline, creating a structural parallel
that Collins leaves for the attentive reader to discover.

Mrs. Attwood takes pride in her daughters, particularly one who serves as an
under-housemaid at [[st-crux-in-the-marsh]], Admiral Bartram's estate. The
daughter regularly writes to her mother, and Mrs. Attwood readily shares
these letters and talks about her daughter's situation — a maternal openness
that Magdalen recognizes as an intelligence asset ([[ch088-CHAPTER-IV]],
088-022).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Attwood's role in the plot is indirect but strategically decisive.
[[magdalen-vanstone]] deliberately cultivates her acquaintance after learning
from [[mr-loscombe]] that Mrs. Attwood's daughter works at St. Crux.

> "I had a motive for making friends with Mrs. Attwood. She is a widow, with a large family of daughters. Her daughters are all in service. One of them is an under-housemaid in the service of Admiral Bartram, at St. Crux-in-the-Marsh." ([[ch090-CHAPTER-I]], 090-022)

Through Mrs. Attwood's willingness to discuss her daughter's letters,
Magdalen gains crucial intelligence about the household arrangements at
Admiral Bartram's estate. Specifically, she learns through Mrs. Attwood
that the admiral is searching for a new parlor-maid — one who must be
young and good-looking ([[ch090-CHAPTER-I]], 090-025). This intelligence
directly enables Magdalen's plan to infiltrate St. Crux disguised as a
servant named Louisa ([[magdalens-parlor-maid-disguise]]).

The mechanism of this intelligence transfer — a mother's pride in her
daughter, exploited without the mother's knowledge — is characteristic
of Collins's unsentimental view of human relationships under the pressure
of necessity. Magdalen does not dislike Mrs. Attwood; she simply perceives
her as an information channel and acts accordingly.

## Character

Mrs. Attwood is portrayed as a respectable, good-natured woman who takes
satisfaction in her daughters' achievements. Her openness about family
matters — natural in a proud mother — is exploited by Magdalen without
Mrs. Attwood's knowledge. There is no indication in the novel that she
ever becomes aware of the role she played in Magdalen's scheme.

This innocence is both Mrs. Attwood's defining characteristic and her
narrative function. Collins needs a character who can transmit information
from St. Crux to London without being part of the conspiracy — someone
whose testimony would withstand scrutiny precisely because she has nothing
to hide. Mrs. Attwood's guilelessness is not a character flaw but a plot
requirement: the intelligence she provides must be legitimate enough to
survive the retrospective examination that the reader, and perhaps
Mrs. Lecount, might bring to bear on the chain of events.
