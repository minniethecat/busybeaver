# Life at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]]

The opening movement of the novel, set at Combe-Raven in Somersetshire, depicting
the peaceful domestic existence of the Vanstone family before the catastrophe of
[[disinheritance|disinheritance]]. This idyllic period establishes the characters, relationships, and
values whose destruction drives the entire plot.

## The Event

Combe-Raven is introduced as the country home of [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]],
a gentleman of comfortable means living with his wife, their two daughters
[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah]], and their governess
[[miss-garth|Miss Garth]] ([[ch006-CHAPTER-I]]). The household is characterised by
warmth and informality — Andrew Vanstone's easy-going nature, Mrs. Vanstone's quiet
devotion, and the contrasting temperaments of the two sisters.

The domestic arrangements are held together by Miss Garth, who has long since
transitioned from governess to household manager:

> Miss Garth had lived too long and too intimately under Mr. Vanstone's roof to
> be parted with for any purely formal considerations; and the first hint at going
> away which she had thought it her duty to drop was dismissed with such
> affectionate warmth of protest that she never repeated it again, except in jest.
> The entire management of the household was, from that time forth, left in her
> hands. ([[ch006-CHAPTER-I]])

The First Scene (chapters 6–20) unfolds through a series of domestic vignettes: the
family gathered at breakfast, Magdalen's theatrical performances, walks in the
grounds, and the arrival of visitors. A key tension is introduced through the visit
of [[frank-clare|Frank Clare]], Magdalen's love interest, and the philosophical
observations of his father [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]], the Vanstones' eccentric
neighbor.

The idyll is broken when Andrew Vanstone receives news from London requiring his
immediate departure. He leaves Combe-Raven with his wife, and within days both are
dead — killed in a railway accident. The family's world collapses in an instant.

## Consequences

The destruction of the Combe-Raven idyll is the novel's inciting catastrophe. The
daughters' discovery that their parents were not legally married at the time of their
birth transforms their grief into legal ruin — they are pronounced illegitimate and
disinherited under the will of [[michael-vanstone|Michael Vanstone]], who
immediately sells the Combe-Raven property upon his arrival from Switzerland.
The Combe-Raven life becomes the lost golden age against which all subsequent
hardship is measured.
