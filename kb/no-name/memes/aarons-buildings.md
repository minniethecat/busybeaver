# Aaron's Buildings

A short, narrow street of poorer-order houses in Camden Town, [[london|London]], where [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] is discovered at her lowest point — "too weak and helpless to support herself," apparently "in the last stage of illness" — by [[mr-kirke|Captain Kirke]] in the novel's closing movement.

## Overview

Aaron's Buildings is the final physical setting of Magdalen's descent. After the relative respectability of St. John's Wood and the shabby-genteel poverty of Vauxhall Walk, she has fallen to a street of "the poorer order" in Camden Town. Collins names it with pointed irony: "Buildings" suggests construction, permanence, community — but the reality is a place of transience, where the destitute are removed to the hospital "if they will have her," or "to the work-house, if they won't" ([[ch129-CHAPTER-CXXIX]]). It is here, in the Last Scene, that the novel's final rescue takes place.

## Description

The street is "short and narrow," its houses "on either side were of the poorer order" ([[ch129-CHAPTER-CXXIX]]). Captain Kirke arrives there by accident: having lost his way in an unfamiliar district, he follows a stranger's hurried directions and turns into a street whose name he reads as he passes the corner: "Aaron's Buildings." A "little crowd of idlers" is assembled around two cabs before one of the houses; a woman disputes with the drivers. The house door stands open, and through it Kirke sees — over the heads of the onlookers — a slatternly girl holding a woman on an old chair placed in the middle of the passage. The woman is too weak to support herself. An old shawl covers her face. When the girl raises her head and restores the shawl, the face is revealed: it is the woman whose "beauty was the haunting remembrance of his life."

## Role in the Narrative

**The Last Scene.** The final scene of the novel (chapters 129–132) begins and ends at Aaron's Buildings. Kirke's chance discovery of Magdalen — gravely ill, abandoned by everyone, about to be taken to the hospital or the workhouse — is the novel's turning point from tragedy toward the possibility of restoration. The recognition is both physical and moral: "The shock of the double recognition — the recognition, at the same moment, of the face, and of the dreadful change in it — struck him speechless and helpless" ([[ch129-CHAPTER-CXXIX]]).

**Kirke's intervention.** Pushing through the crowd, Kirke enters the house and confronts those about to remove her: "What are you doing with that lady?" ([[ch129-CHAPTER-CXXIX]]). His authority — the "suppressed agitation" visible in his face, combined with his tall seafaring presence — halts the removal. The woman holding the chair drops it instantly, asking: "Do you know her, sir? Are you one of her friends?" He does know her, and though he is not yet her friend in the conventional sense, his hereditary connection to her father makes him, at this moment, the only person in the world who can claim the right to save her.

**The turning point.** Aaron's Buildings is both the novel's nadir and the site of its redemptive turn. Magdalen is carried not to the hospital but to comfortable lodgings arranged by Kirke. The Last Scene closes the geography of the novel — from Combe-Raven to Aldborough to St. John's Wood to Vauxhall Walk to Aaron's Buildings — with the suggestion that the downward trajectory has at last been arrested.
