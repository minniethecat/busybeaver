# York ([[skeldergate|Skeldergate]])

The ancient city of York — specifically the narrow street of Skeldergate on the western bank of the River Ouse, and the obscure [[rosemary-lane|Rosemary Lane]] that leads up to the city walls — provides the setting for the novel's Second Scene, in which [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] and [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] form their unlikely alliance.

## Overview

Collins's description of York in Chapter 27 is one of the novel's most atmospheric passages, establishing a physical landscape that mirrors the moral terrain of the conspiracy about to be hatched. Skeldergate is "a narrow street, running nearly north and south, parallel with the course of the river," its few old houses "disguised in melancholy modern costume of whitewash and cement." Shops "of the smaller and poorer order," dingy warehouses, and "joyless private residences of red brick" compose its present aspect ([[ch027-CHAPTER-XXVII]]). At its southern extremity, the street ceases abruptly, opening onto the broad flow of the Ouse with its trees, meadows, and towing-path — a sudden release from urban confinement into natural space.

## Rosemary Lane

The lane itself — where Wragge has his temporary lodgings — is described as "a narrow little lane" leading up to the paved footway surmounting the ancient Walls of York. Its one small row of buildings consists of "cheap lodging-houses, with an opposite view, at the distance of a few feet, of a portion of the massive city wall." Collins emphasizes its obscurity: "Very little light enters it; very few people live in it; the floating population of Skeldergate passes it by; and visitors to the Walk on the Walls, who use it as the way up or the way down, get out of the dreary little passage as fast as they can" ([[ch027-CHAPTER-XXVII]]). It is exactly this obscurity that makes it suitable for Wragge's purposes — a place where a meeting between a disgraced naval officer and a disguised young woman can pass unobserved.

## Role in the Narrative

**The Second Scene.** The door of one of the houses in Rosemary Lane "opened softly on the evening of the twenty-third of September, eighteen hundred and forty-six; and a solitary individual of the male sex sauntered into Skeldergate" ([[ch027-CHAPTER-XXVII]]). This is Captain Wragge, preparing for his encounter with Magdalen. The meeting that follows — in which Magdalen, having tracked Wragge to York through his newspaper advertisement, proposes their conspiracy — transforms both their fortunes.

**A city of consequences.** York also features in the novel's legal geography: Mr. Pendril's investigations touch on events in York, and Skeldergate represents one of several provincial settings through which Collins maps the reach of English law and its failures.
