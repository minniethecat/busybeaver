## Overview

Frank Clare is the only son of the cynical philosopher [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]], and a neighbor of the Vanstone family at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]]. Sent away at seventeen to train as a civil engineer, he returns three years later having failed in the profession. His brief romance with [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] during the private theatricals at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] ends when his weakness of character is exposed by the family's sudden ruin.

## Background

Frank is the youngest of [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]'s sons. When an opportunity arose for him to train under a civil engineer in the north of England — secured through [[andrew-vanstone|Mr. Vanstone]]'s influence — his father cynically wagered a guinea that Frank would "come back on our hands like a bad shilling." [[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]'s prediction proved accurate: after three years of indifferent progress, including postings to Belgium, Frank's supervisor concluded that he lacked the aptitude for engineering and was better suited to "a calling in which superficial qualities would be more likely to succeed." He returned to [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] at the age of twenty, his professional prospects extinguished.

## Role in the Narrative

Frank Clare reappears at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] shortly before the Vanstone family's catastrophe. His return coincides with preparations for a private theatrical performance, in which [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] — his childhood playfellow — casts him opposite her as Falkland in scenes from *The Rivals*. During the rehearsals, an attachment forms between them, though [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] observes the growing familiarity with silent distrust.

When Andrew and [[mrs-vanstone|Mrs. Vanstone]] die suddenly and the daughters discover they are disinherited, Frank's response reveals his fundamental weakness. Rather than standing by [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] in her ruin, he withdraws. His father's philosophy — that Frank is "a compound of negatives" — is borne out in his conduct; he proves incapable of the constancy and moral courage the situation demands. [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] severs their engagement and embarks on the path of deception that drives the novel's central plot.

## Relationships

- **[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]** — Frank's father, a cynical philosopher who views his son with amused contempt and correctly predicts his failure.
- **[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]]** — Frank's childhood playfellow and briefly his fiancée; she casts him as Falkland in the theatricals and later breaks their engagement.
- **[[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]** — Distrusts Frank from the outset and maintains her formal distance, addressing him as "[[mr-clare|Mr. Clare]]" when others call him Frank.
- **[[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]]** — Frank's patron and champion, who refuses to believe the worst of him and secures his engineering opportunity.

## Character Analysis

Frank Clare is a study in inherited weakness. Physically handsome — "his gentle, wandering brown eyes would have looked to better advantage in a woman's face" — he lacks the resolve and substance of his ancestors. His hands, like his eyes, have a "wandering habit," constantly fidgeting with small objects, a physical manifestation of his inner irresolution.

In the novel's moral economy, Frank represents the failure of genteel masculinity. Unlike [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]], who channels his limitations into an honest (if morally flexible) career, Frank simply drifts. His failure as an engineer foreshadows his failure as a suitor: in both roles, he proves unable to meet the demands placed upon him. His departure from the narrative clears the way for [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]]'s transformation from romantic heroine to active agent of her own fate.

## Appearances

Frank Clare appears primarily in the early chapters of the novel, during the [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] sequence. He participates in the private theatricals and the events leading up to the Vanstone parents' deaths, after which his role in the narrative effectively ends.
