# St. John's Wood

A suburban district of [[london|London]] where [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] — now Mrs. Noel Vanstone — takes lodgings after her marriage and the death of her husband, forming the setting for the novel's Sixth Scene.

## Overview

St. John's Wood in the 1840s was a semi-rural suburb north-west of London, a place of respectable lodging-houses, gardens, and a degree of anonymity that suited Magdalen's purposes. It is here, "toward the close of the December afternoon," that Collins places the Sixth Scene: Magdalen alone in her lodgings, the fire burning "sluggishly in the narrow little grate," the view of "wet houses and soaking gardens opposite" darkening fast, and "the bell of the suburban muffin-boy" tinkling drearily in the distance ([[ch105-CHAPTER-CV]]). The domestic comfort implied by "St. John's Wood" is belied by the reality of Magdalen's circumstances — her dress loose "through the wasting of her figure," her face maintaining "its haggard composure, its changeless unnatural calm."

## Role in the Narrative

**The Sixth Scene.** The St. John's Wood chapters (105–106) represent the lowest psychological point of Magdalen's journey. Four months after the Aldborough wedding, "the penalty for that day was paid already — paid in unavailing remorse, in hopeless isolation, in irremediable defeat" ([[ch105-CHAPTER-CV]]). The money from the Combe-Raven inheritance sits untouched while she exhausts herself trying to discover the Secret Trust that would unlock it.

**The lodgings.** Magdalen occupies rooms in a respectable house, paying her landlady weekly. The domestic details are deliberately flattened: the narrow grate, the darkening afternoon, the scattered coins on the table. She gives notice that she "may be obliged to leave these lodgings rather sooner than I anticipated" ([[ch105-CHAPTER-CV]]) — a hint of the further descent that will carry her from St. John's Wood to the far poorer quarters of Vauxhall Walk and eventually to Aaron's Buildings.

**Louisa's role.** It is at St. John's Wood that Magdalen sounds out her servant Louisa, testing whether the girl can be made an ally in the search for the Secret Trust. The conversation that follows is a small but significant piece of character work: Magdalen's isolation is such that a servant — the only person who shares her roof — becomes a strategic asset to be assessed.
