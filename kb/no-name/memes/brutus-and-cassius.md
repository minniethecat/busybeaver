# Brutus and Cassius

Brutus and Cassius are [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]]'s two Labrador dogs, introduced in the St. Crux chapters (Chapters 113-116) of Wilkie Collins's *No Name*. Named after the principal conspirators in Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar*, they are the admiral's constant companions and, more importantly, his secret accomplices at the dinner table.

## Role in the Household

Magdalen first encounters the dogs on her initial evening as parlor-maid at [[st-crux-in-the-marsh|St. Crux]]. As the admiral enters the dining-room, they follow and place themselves "with the utmost gravity and composure, one on each side of his chair" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I]. The admiral introduces them to the new servant: "The dog with the black nose is Brutus, and the dog with the white nose is Cassius. Did you ever hear who Brutus and Cassius were? Ancient Romans? That's right — good girl" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I].

## The Dinner Accomplices

The dogs' primary function is revealed during the meal. The admiral, convinced that many foods upset his digestion but unwilling to offend his cook, has trained Brutus and Cassius as his secret disposal system. When made-dishes are served, Magdalen observes:

> The admiral helped himself liberally from the dish; sent Magdalen to the side-table to get him some bread; and, when he thought her eye was off him, furtively tumbled the whole contents of his plate into Brutus's mouth. Cassius whined faintly as his fortunate comrade swallowed the savory mess at a gulp. "Hush! you fool," whispered the admiral. "Your turn next!" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I]

The admiral maintains scrupulous fairness between the two, alternating dishes between them. When the "unwholesome cream" follows the plain pudding, "the master took the simple pudding, the dogs swallowed the elaborate cream" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I]. With transparent duplicity, the admiral then instructs Magdalen: "Tell the cook, my dear, a capital cream!" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I].

## Comic Function

The dogs serve as a recurring source of comedy throughout the St. Crux chapters. Their behavior mirrors that of old [[mazey|Mazey]], the admiral's coxswain — all three are prone to periodic lapses into their "original wildness." When the dogs abscond from the house for days at a time, returning "lean, dirty, and heartily ashamed of themselves," the admiral treats them with paternal gravity, tying them up in disgrace for a day before formally readmitting them to the dining-room [^ch114-CHAPTER-II].

The after-dinner ritual reinforces the comic pattern: Mazey enters for his glass of port and the toast "God bless the Queen," while Brutus and Cassius "stretched themselves on the rug to digest mushrooms and made gravies in the lubricating heat of the fire" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I].

## Relationship with Magdalen

During [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]]'s undercover service at St. Crux, the dogs provide her with one of her few moments of comfort. On her first full day, as she waits for Mazey to guide her through the house, "Brutus pushed his mighty muzzle companionably into her hand; and Cassius laid his friendly fore-paw on her lap." Their unquestioning acceptance contrasts with the instinctive suspicion of the female servants and stirs Magdalen's memories of [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]]: "It seemed only yesterday since she and the dogs at Combe-Raven had roamed the garden together, and had idled away the summer mornings luxuriously on the shady lawn" [^ch113-CHAPTER-I].

## Significance

Beyond their comic function, Brutus and Cassius contribute to the character portrait of Admiral Bartram. His elaborate subterfuge with the dogs reveals not deceitfulness but a benign weakness — an old man's unwillingness to disappoint his cook combined with a hypochondriacal concern for his health. The dogs humanize him at precisely the point in the novel where he might otherwise appear merely as an obstacle to Magdalen's quest. Their Shakespearean names, deliberately incongruous with their genial Labrador natures, also contribute to the vein of theatrical self-consciousness that runs through the novel.
