## Overview

Mazey is [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]]'s ancient coxswain at [[st-crux-in-the-marsh|St. Crux-in-the-Marsh]] — a "weather-beaten old man" whose "sixty years had written their story of hard work at sea, and hard drinking on shore, on the veteran's grim and wrinkled face." Deaf, devoted, and cheerfully eccentric, he has transformed the corridors of the admiral's decaying mansion into a private navigational system, teaching newcomers their "Pints of the Compass" with the solemnity of a sailing master. When [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] arrives at St. Crux disguised as a parlor-maid, old Mazey becomes her unexpected ally — treating her with a rough kindness that stands in welcome contrast to the sullen hostility of the female servants.

## Role in the Narrative

Mazey appears in the novel's final section, when [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] — now disguised as the parlor-maid "Lucy" — infiltrates [[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]]'s household at St. Crux in search of the Secret Trust that controls the Vanstone [[the-will|inheritance]]. The female servants, sensing something suspicious about the new maid, treat her with "sullen unanimity of distrust." Mazey, by contrast, is immediately taken with her:

> "She's as straight as a poplar," said old Mazey, considering Magdalen's figure in drowsy soliloquy. "I say she's as straight as a poplar, and his honor the admiral says so too!" ([^1])

When the other servants refuse to show [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] around the house, Mazey eagerly volunteers, giving her a tour organized around his personal navigational system. He teaches her to put her back against the long corridor wall and "look Noathe," then repeats the lesson for the east passage, chuckling and winking at her as if imparting profound nautical secrets:

> "When you've got your back ag'in this wall, and when you look straight afore you, you look Noathe. If you ever get lost hereaway, put your back ag'in the wall, look out straight afore you, and say to yourself: 'I look Noathe!' You do that like a good girl, and you won't lose your bearings." ([^1])

His nightly ritual with the admiral — appearing after dinner to drink a glass of port and report "No report, your honor" — is one of the novel's most affectionate set-pieces, capturing the deep bond between the old sailor and his master through the barest economy of words and gesture.

Mazey's character provides warmth and comic relief in the novel's darker final chapters. His kindness to [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] — asking nothing, suspecting nothing — makes him one of the few characters in the book who offers her pure, uncomplicated good[[the-will|will]].

## Character Analysis

Mazey belongs to a tradition of loyal old retainers in Victorian fiction, but Collins invests him with enough eccentric particularity to lift him well above the stereotype. His navigational obsession, his deafness, his model ship, his evening port with the admiral — these details combine to create a character who feels lived-in and real rather than merely functional. The contrast between his rough kindness and the female servants' instinctive hostility also serves a thematic purpose, underscoring the novel's interest in the different forms that loyalty and suspicion can take.
