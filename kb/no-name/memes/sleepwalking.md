# Somnambulism

[[admiral-bartram|Admiral Bartram]]'s constitutional tendency to walk in his sleep is one of the most significant plot devices in the later chapters of Wilkie Collins's *No Name*. His somnambulism directly accounts for the hiding place of the [[the-secret-trust|Secret Trust]], sets the nocturnal atmosphere of the St. Crux sequence, and provides the opportunity for [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]] to discover the document she seeks.

## The Admiral's Condition

The admiral's sleepwalking is referenced throughout the [[st-crux-in-the-marsh|St. Crux]] chapters (Chapters 113-116, 131). Old [[mazey|Mazey]], the admiral's coxswain, sleeps on a truckle-bed placed across his master's bedroom door specifically to guard against the nocturnal wandering. The veteran's fidelity in this duty is absolute: "No entreaties had ever prevailed on him to submit to the usual precaution taken in such cases. He peremptorily declined to be locked into his room; he even ignored his own liability, whenever a dream disturbed him, to walk in his sleep" [^ch116-CHAPTER-IV]. The peculiar arrangement of the truckle-bed, which Magdalen observes on her first day at St. Crux, is explained by this long-standing habit.

## The Ashes of the Tripod

The admiral's sleepwalking is retrospectively revealed as the explanation for why the Secret Trust document was once found in the ashes of the iron tripod in the Banqueting-Hall — known as "Freeze-your-Bones." During a somnambulistic episode, the admiral apparently carried the document there and placed it among the cold ashes. This peculiar behavior precisely matches the disordered logic of his sleepwalking: during the episode Magdalen witnesses, he mutters "Noel! my good fellow, Noel, take it back again! It worries me day and night. I don't know where it's safe; I don't know where to put it" [^ch116-CHAPTER-IV].

## Magdalen's Witness

The most dramatic sleepwalking episode occurs in Chapter 116, when Magdalen, during her midnight search of the east wing, encounters the admiral crossing the Banqueting-Hall in his sleep. Collins renders the scene with characteristic suspense: Magdalen watches as the figure advances through alternating strips of moonlight and shadow, "now mysteriously lost in the shadow, now suddenly visible again in the light, until it reached the fifth and nearest strip of moonlight" [^ch116-CHAPTER-IV].

> A long, gray dressing-gown was wrapped round him. His head was uncovered; his feet were bare. In his left hand he carried his little basket of keys. He passed Magdalen slowly, his lips whispering without intermission, his open eyes staring straight before him with the glassy stare of death.

The admiral proceeds, in his sleeping state, to unlock a buhl cabinet, remove the Trust letter, and then transfer it to a drawer in an oak bureau — changing its hiding place while entirely unconscious. Magdalen, following him, is able to read the opening words of the document confirming its nature as the Secret Trust. She also has the presence of mind to take the admiral's basket of keys from his bedside table after he returns to bed.

## Narrative Function

The sleepwalking device serves multiple purposes in Collins's plot architecture. It provides a psychologically plausible mechanism for the Trust's concealment — the admiral, tormented by the responsibility imposed on him by [[noel-vanstone|Noel Vanstone]]'s deathbed instructions, unconsciously seeks to rid himself of the burden. It creates the atmospheric terror characteristic of sensation fiction, transforming the decaying St. Crux manor into a Gothic space where the boundary between waking and sleeping dissolves. And it enables Magdalen's near-success: without the admiral's somnambulistic revelation, she would never have located the document in the east-wing bureau. Her ultimate failure — interrupted by the drunken Mazey before she can finish reading the letter — only heightens the dramatic irony of the scene.
