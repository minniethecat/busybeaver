## Overview

The sudden deaths of [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] and his wife are the inciting incident of *No Name*. Within weeks of learning that their marriage is legally invalid, both parents die — Mr. Vanstone in a railway accident, Mrs. Vanstone in childbirth. Their deaths transform the novel from a domestic comedy of manners into a sensation narrative of dispossession, disguise, and moral brinkmanship.

## Context

The sequence begins in [[ch006-CHAPTER-I|Chapter I]] with the arrival of a letter from New Orleans. The letter — whose contents are initially withheld from the reader — informs the Vanstones that Andrew's first marriage (to an American woman in his youth) was never legally dissolved, meaning his marriage to his current wife and the legitimacy of their daughters are in question.

The parents depart suddenly for London, hoping to remedy the legal situation. They return apparently reassured, and normal life resumes at [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]] — including the amateur theatrical production of Sheridan's *The Rivals* that involves [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and Frank Clare. But the legal threat has not been resolved, and the parents' sudden deaths transform a theoretical legal problem into an irreversible catastrophe.

## The Event

Mr. Vanstone dies first, killed in a railway accident while travelling. His wife, pregnant and already in fragile health, collapses upon hearing the news. She dies in premature childbirth soon after, leaving [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] orphaned.

The double death is followed by a third blow: the arrival of the family lawyer, Mr. Pendril, who reveals the truth about the parents' invalid marriage. The Vanstone sisters learn they are illegitimate in the eyes of the law and that the family estate will pass to their father's brother — a stranger — rather than to them.

## Consequences

The deaths and subsequent disinheritance split the sisters' paths permanently. [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] accepts her fate, seeking employment as a governess. [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] refuses to submit, embarking on a campaign of disguise and deception to reclaim what she believes is rightfully hers. The novel's central action — Magdalen's moral descent and eventual redemption — follows directly from this single catastrophic event.
