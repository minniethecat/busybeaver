## Overview

Mrs. Wragge is the wife of [[captain-wragge|Captain Horatio Wragge]] and one of Wilkie Collins's most affectionately drawn comic creations. A woman of extraordinary physical stature — "two or three inches over six feet" — combined with a gentle, childlike disposition, she serves as her husband's unwitting accomplice in his various schemes. Formerly a waitress at Darch's Dining-rooms in London, she came into a small inheritance which the captain "mercilessly squandered to the last farthing." Her defining affliction is a mental fog she calls "the Buzzing in my head," which leaves her perpetually confused and dependent on her husband's shouted instructions.

## Background

Mrs. Wragge's origins are humble. Before her marriage, she worked as a waitress at Darch's Dining-rooms in London, where — as she explains to Magdalen — "the Buzzing in my head first began":

> "The gentlemen all came together; the gentlemen were all hungry together; the gentlemen all gave their orders together—" ([^1])

Her small inheritance made her a target for Captain Wragge, who married her for her money and proceeded to spend it all. She now lives in a state of placid submission, responding to her husband's commands like "a well-trained child." The captain shouts at her constantly — not from cruelty, he explains, but because loud speech is "a necessary stimulant to her ideas. Shout at her — and her mind comes up to time. Speak to her — and she drifts miles away from you directly."

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Wragge first appears when Captain Wragge brings [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] to his lodgings in Rosemary Lane, York. The introduction establishes her character with brilliant comic economy:

> "Mrs. Wragge!" cried the captain, shouting at her as if she was fast asleep. "Mrs. Wragge!"

She rises to "an apparently interminable height," and Collins observes that "if Mrs. Wragge and a lamb had been placed side by side, comparison, under those circumstances, would have exposed the lamb as a rank impostor."

Despite her mental limitations, Mrs. Wragge forms an immediate and genuine attachment to Magdalen. When Magdalen kindly straightens her crooked cap — which the captain has been barking at her to adjust — "the moon-face of the giantess brightened for the first time." Her whispered question to Magdalen — "Do you like dress, miss? I do" — reveals a touching, guileless femininity that survives despite her husband's exploitation and her own mental fog.

Throughout the Aldborough chapters, Mrs. Wragge functions as an unwitting prop in the captain's theatrical deceptions, her very guilelessness serving as a form of protective camouflage for the schemes unfolding around her.

## Relationships

Mrs. Wragge's relationship with her husband is one of total, almost canine submission — she looks "submissively down at her husband, whose head, when he stood on tiptoe, barely reached her shoulder." Yet the captain's treatment of her, while condescending, is not cruel; he regards her as a domestic inconvenience to be managed rather than a person to be valued.

Her relationship with **[[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]]** is characterized by a touching, simple kindness. Unlike the other women servants Magdalen encounters — who treat her with "sullen unanimity of distrust" — Mrs. Wragge offers uncomplicated warmth, and Magdalen responds with genuine gentleness.
