# The Sisters' Reunion

The reunion of [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]
at [[aarons-buildings|Aaron's Buildings]] — the emotional resolution of the novel's
central relationship. After months of separation, during which Magdalen pursued her
desperate campaign of disguise and Norah lived in quiet dignity as a governess, the
two sisters are finally brought together.

## Context

Since [[leaving-combe-raven|leaving Combe-Raven]], the sisters have followed radically
different paths. Norah accepted their reduced circumstances with resignation, taking
a position as a governess; Magdalen launched an all-consuming campaign of disguise,
deception, and legal maneuvering to recover the lost inheritance. Their separation
has been both geographic and moral — each sister representing a different response
to catastrophe ([[norahs-governess-life]], [[the-inheritance-scheme]]).

## The Event

Following [[captain-kirkes-return|Captain Kirke's rescue]] of Magdalen and her
gradual physical recovery, Norah receives word of her sister's condition. She travels
to Aaron's Buildings, where Magdalen is convalescing under Kirke's care. The meeting,
described in the novel's final chapters, is suffused with the weight of everything
that has passed:

> *The first agitation of the meeting between the sisters was over; the first vivid
> impressions, half pleasurable, half painful, had softened a little, and Norah and
> Magdalen sat together hand in hand, each rapt in the silent fullness of her own joy.*
> ([[ch132-CHAPTER-IV]])

Norah brings news of [[norahs-fortune-restored|the legal recovery of the inheritance]]
and of her engagement to [[george-bartram|George Bartram]]. Magdalen, in turn, must
reckon with the moral cost of her campaign and the revelation of her true identity to
[[mr-kirke|Captain Kirke]].

## Consequences

The reunion is the novel's true emotional resolution — more significant than the legal
restoration of the fortune or the romantic pairing of Norah and George. It restores
the central relationship that the novel's catastrophe severed, and it allows both
sisters to see themselves through the other's eyes. Norah's steadfast love offers
Magdalen the possibility of moral recovery; Magdalen's fierce devotion, however
misguided in its methods, is finally understood by the sister it was meant to serve.
