## Overview

The will—or more precisely, its absence—is the hidden engine of *No Name*. [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]] made a will leaving everything to his daughters, but that will was written before his [[marriage|marriage]]. Under English law, marriage automatically revoked a prior will. Andrew, preoccupied with his wife's health and the happiness of his family, never updated it. When he died suddenly in the railway accident that also killed his wife, he died intestate—and his entire estate passed to his estranged brother Michael, leaving [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]] and [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] with nothing.

## The Event

### The Two Wills

| Testator | Will Status | Consequence |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| **Andrew Vanstone** | Had a will leaving estate to daughters, but invalidated by subsequent marriage | Dies intestate; estate passes to brother Michael |
| **Michael Vanstone** | Refused to make a will despite repeated urging | Dies intestate; estate passes to son Noel |

The novel presents a double intestacy: two brothers, two refusals to engage with the legal machinery of inheritance, and two cascading consequences.

### Michael Vanstone's Refusal

The elder Vanstone brother's refusal to make a will is presented as a kind of magical thinking:

> The hardest men have their weaknesses; and Mr. Michael Vanstone's weakness seems to have been an insurmountable horror of contemplating the event of his own death. His son, his housekeeper, and his lawyer, had all three tried over and over again to get him to make a will; and had never shaken his obstinate resolution.
>
> — *No Name*, on Michael Vanstone's intestacy

### The Will as Written Document

[[the-will|The will]] is, at its most literal, a piece of writing—a [[letters-and-correspondence|letter]] addressed to posterity. Andrew Vanstone's failure to write this particular document condemns his daughters to legal non-existence. The novel thus establishes an intimate connection between writing and legal identity: to be named in a will is to exist at law; to be unnamed is to become "No Name." [[law-and-legality|The legal system]] recognizes only what is written; oral intentions, however clear, count for nothing.

### Connection to [[marriage|Marriage]]

The legal doctrine of "revocation by marriage" is the mechanism by which Andrew Vanstone's will was voided. His [[marriage|marriage]] to the girls' mother—the act that should have secured their family—became, through the operation of this obscure legal rule, the instrument of their destruction. Collins uses this cruel irony to critique a legal system in which the most intimate personal relationships are governed by rules that ordinary people do not know and cannot be expected to anticipate.
