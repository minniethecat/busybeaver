# Leaving [[combe-raven|Combe-Raven]]

The departure from Combe-Raven is the wrenching event that marks the end of the
Vanstone family's life of comfort and the beginning of [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen]]
and [[norah-vanstone|Norah Vanstone]]'s struggle for survival. After their parents'
sudden deaths in a railway accident and the legal revelation that the entire estate
passes to the estranged Michael Vanstone, the sisters and their governess
[[miss-garth|Miss Garth]] are compelled to leave the only home they have ever known.

## Context

Mr. and Mrs. Vanstone were killed in a railway accident while returning from London,
where they had gone to legalize their marriage. Until their deaths, the family had
lived a comfortable, happy life at Combe-Raven in Somersetshire. Immediately after
the tragedy, the family lawyer Mr. Pendril revealed that Michael Vanstone was the
legal heir to the entire estate — and that he had refused to provide any assistance
to the disinherited daughters. Mr. Pendril delivered the practical arrangements
without sentiment:

> "The servants must not be forgotten," he said. "They must be settled with and
> discharged: I will give them the necessary explanation before I leave. As for the
> house, no questions connected with it need trouble you. The carriages and horses,
> the furniture and plate, and so on, must simply be left on the premises to await
> Mr. Michael Vanstone's further orders." ([[ch029-CHAPTER-III]])

The house, its contents, and all its wealth passed to a man the family had never met.

## The Event

The departure unfolded over several painful days, preserved in Norah's poignant letter
to Mr. Pendril.

**The Last Evening**: Norah described their final night at Combe-Raven with unsparing
clarity:

> "The long, quiet, rainy evening out-of-doors — our last evening at Combe-Raven —
> was a sad trial to us. I think winter-time would have weighed less on our spirits;
> the drawn curtains and the bright lamps, and the companionable fires would have
> helped us. We were only five in the house altogether — after having once been so
> many! I can't tell you how dreary the gray daylight looked, toward seven o'clock,
> in the lonely rooms, and on the noiseless staircase."
> ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]])

The family gathered their possessions in the dining room: "We carried all our
possessions downstairs, and heaped them on the large dining-table, and so made our
preparations together in the same room" ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]]).

**Magdalen's Vigil**: Magdalen spent the entire night awake, sitting in her chair and
thinking. When Norah woke at daylight, she found her sister still there. Magdalen had
"never been to bed; she had not slept all through the night." She had copied extracts
from her father's will and letter, placing them in a tiny white silk bag to keep
always about her person:

> "This tells me in his own words what his last wishes were for both of us," she
> said; "and this is all I want for the future."
> ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]])

This white silk bag — containing the copied extracts, a lock of Frank's hair, and
later her store of bank-notes — becomes the novel's most persistent physical symbol
of Magdalen's dual motivations: love for Frank and the promise made to her dead father.

**The Graveside Promise**: Before leaving, the sisters visited the churchyard where
their parents were buried. Magdalen's behavior at the grave revealed the ferocity of
the resolve forming within her:

> She dropped on her knees at the grave; tore up from it passionately a handful of
> grass; and said something to herself, at the same moment, which, though I followed
> her instantly, I did not get near enough to hear. She turned on me in such a
> frenzied manner, when I tried to raise her from the ground — she looked at me with
> such a fearful wildness in her eyes — that I felt absolutely terrified at the sight
> of her. ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]])

When Norah asked what words she had spoken, Magdalen answered: "A promise to our dead
father." She thrust the tuft of grass into her bosom and hurried away, the paroxysm
leaving her as suddenly as it had come ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]]).

**The Final Sight**: "We only returned to the house in time to hasten away from it to
the train. Perhaps it was better for us so — better that we had only a moment left to
look back before the turn in the road hid the last of Combe-Raven from our view." At
the station, "there was not a soul we knew at the station; nobody to stare at us,
nobody to wish us good-by" ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]]).

The railway itself became a source of horror: "What we felt at the sight of the
railway — what horrible remembrances it forced on our minds of the calamity which has
made us fatherless — I cannot, and dare not, tell you" ([[ch032-CHAPTER-V]]).

## Consequences

The departure from Combe-Raven was the definitive break between the Vanstone sisters'
past and their uncertain future. They took refuge at Miss Garth's sister's school in
London. The event crystallized Magdalen's determination — the promise she made at her
father's grave became the seed of her campaign to recover the inheritance from Michael
Vanstone. The loss of home, combined with [[frank-clares-departure|Frank Clare's
forced departure for China]], transformed Magdalen from a spirited girl into a woman
possessed by a single, desperate purpose.

Miss Garth later reflected on the change: "There has been something weighing on her
mind ever since we left Combe-Raven — weighing far more heavily for the last six weeks
than at first... She has formed some desperate project of contesting the possession
of her father's fortune with Michael Vanstone" ([[ch036-CHAPTER-II]]). The departure
from home thus becomes not merely an event of loss but the originating moment of the
novel's entire conspiracy plot.
