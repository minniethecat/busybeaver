## Overview

Mrs. Vanstone is the mother of [[norah-vanstone|Norah]] and [[magdalen-vanstone|Magdalen Vanstone]], and the wife — in emotional truth if not in law — of [[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]]. Her story is the novel's central tragedy in miniature: a woman who sacrificed her social position for love, lived for over twenty years in happiness at Combe-Raven, and died without being able to protect her daughters from the legal consequences of the irregularity of her marriage. Though she appears directly only in the novel's opening chapters, her choices — and their consequences — drive the entire plot.

## Background

Mrs. Vanstone's history, as recounted by [[mr-pendril|Mr. Pendril]], is one of the novel's most carefully guarded revelations. She met Andrew Vanstone when she was very young — "little more than a girl" — at a time when he was already legally married to a woman in Canada who had abandoned him. Believing (on the basis of reports that later proved premature) that his first wife was dead, Andrew and she began living as husband and wife. She "faced her position and its necessities without flinching," resolving to "sacrifice her life to the man she loved" and to conduct herself so as "never to raise the suspicion that she was not his lawful wife."

Mr. Pendril's account emphasizes her strength of character:

> "Having once resolved to sacrifice her life to the man she loved; having quieted her conscience by persuading herself that his marriage was a legal mockery, and that she was 'his wife in the sight of Heaven,' she set herself from the first to accomplish the one foremost purpose of so living with him, in the world's eye, as never to raise the suspicion that she was not his lawful wife." ([^1])

For over twenty years, she succeeded. She and Andrew raised two daughters at Combe-Raven in apparent respectability and genuine happiness. She bore the premature loss of several other children — "more than one" — and endured "long attacks of illness which had followed those bereavements." When Andrew finally received confirmation that his first wife was indeed dead, they married legally — but the marriage came too late to legitimize their daughters under English law.

## Role in the Narrative

Mrs. Vanstone appears directly only in [[ch006-CHAPTER-I|Chapter I]] and [[ch007-CHAPTER-II|Chapter II]], yet her presence haunts the entire novel. Collins's physical description of her is one of the book's most carefully composed portraits:

> "Although she was now in her forty-fourth year; although she had been tried, in bygone times, by the premature loss of more than one of her children, and by long attacks of illness which had followed those bereavements of former years — she still preserved the fair proportion and subtle delicacy of feature, once associated with the all-adorning brightness and freshness of beauty, which had left her never to return." ([^2])

In the breakfast scene that opens the novel, Mrs. Vanstone is the first to react with visible anxiety to the letter from New Orleans — the letter that confirms the legal catastrophe. While her husband's face "changed color," Mrs. Vanstone "looked excited rather than alarmed. A faint flush rose on her cheeks — her eyes brightened — she stirred the tea round and round in her cup in a restless, impatient manner which was not natural to her." Only Miss Garth notices this reaction, which hints at Mrs. Vanstone's long-suppressed knowledge of her vulnerable position.

Her other significant action is her long-standing arrangement with [[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]] — revealed posthumously through her own letter — to pay him from her private purse to stay away from Combe-Raven. Miss Garth, reading this confession, recognizes "a weakness in Mrs. Vanstone's character which... she had never detected." The payments were hush money, designed to prevent Wragge from harassing Andrew — and in making them, Mrs. Vanstone revealed the anxiety that had shadowed her otherwise serene domestic life.

She and Andrew departed for London to attempt to rectify the legal situation before it became public. They died together in a railway accident before they could execute a new will securing their daughters' future.

## Relationships

Mrs. Vanstone's central relationship is with **[[andrew-vanstone|Andrew Vanstone]]**, to whom she devoted her life with an intensity that — as events prove — required the suppression of considerable fear and the maintenance of a twenty-year secret. Her relationship with her daughters is characterized by maternal warmth, particularly in the opening scenes, though the secret she carried necessarily created a barrier between herself and her children that they could not understand until it was too late.

Her relationship with **[[captain-wragge|Captain Wragge]]** is revealed only after her death: for years she had paid him to protect her family from his predatory schemes, a burden she bore alone.
