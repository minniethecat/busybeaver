# Patagonian Civilization

The culminating civilization of the [[First Men]], arising in the Southern Hemisphere after the [[First Dark Age]] and centered on the vast new plains of [[South America]]. A sober, intellectually-inclined society that worshipped youth yet aged prematurely, the Patagonian Civilization bridged the long darkness between the [[First World State]] and the birth of the [[Second Men]].

## Overview

The Patagonian Civilization was the last great culture of the [[First Men]], emerging from the racial and cultural intermingling that ended the [[First Dark Age]]. Complex climatic changes had rendered southern [[South America]] a fit nursery for civilization, while an immense warping of the [[earth-place|Earth]]'s crust raised a vast new continent connecting [[South America]] with [[Antarctica]]. Here, in the virgin plains of [[Patagonia]] east of the ancient [[Bahia Blanca]], a people arose in whom the ancient dignity of humanity was somewhat restored.

Unlike the feverish, sexually-driven cultures that had preceded it, [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n society was marked by a diminished animal nature and a pronounced capacity for dispassionate cognition. The [[First Men]] of this late period had become a people whose "wild oats had been sown" -- steadier and more single-minded than their ancestors, though less vigorous. Their progress was never swift, but it was remarkably steady; where earlier civilizations had leapt from barbarism to civil life and collapsed within a thousand years, the slow march of the [[Patagonia]]ns required ten times as long.

> Because their animal nature was somewhat subdued, the Patagonians were more capable of dispassionate cognition, and more inclined toward intellectualism. They were a people in whom rational behaviour was less often subverted by passion, though more liable to fail through mere indolence or faintheartedness. (PN:[005-013]

The civilization's defining tragedy was biological senescence. The [[Patagonia]]ns aged with terrible speed. Youth lasted scarcely a decade and a half; by age fifteen an individual was already settling into middle-age, bones becoming brittle, hair grizzled, mind losing its suppleness. This premature aging haunted the entire culture and shaped its deepest spiritual obsession -- an aching, ever-thwarted craving for lost youth and the dream of [[Longevity]] that the [[First Men]] could never recover.

## Dominant Species

The Patagonian Civilization was the final flowering of the [[First Men]], constituted by the original human species in the last phase of its terrestrial career. These late [[first-men|First Men]] had diverged markedly from their ancestors: their sexual impulse was greatly reduced, their parental instinct unusually powerful, and their cognitive powers oriented toward sober empiricism rather than fervent invention. They were a people capable of sustained rational inquiry yet hampered by a constitutional lassitude that the earlier [[First Men]] had never known.

The civilization's ultimate significance lies in its role as the bridge between the first and second human species. The thirty-five survivors of the [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n catastrophe became the sole trustees of the human spirit, preserving as much of [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n culture as they could remember in the great stone library of [[Siberia]]. After ten million years of the [[Second Dark Age]], their remote descendants evolved into the [[Second Men]] -- a nobler species that inherited the [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n legacy of intellectual sobriety and cosmopolitan loyalty without the biological enfeeblement that had doomed its predecessor.

## Political Structure

The Patagonian Civilization evolved from tribal confederations into a vast, highly organized community of autonomous provinces. Its political and cultural centre lay upon the new coast northeast of the ancient [[Falkland Islands]], while its barbarian outskirts included much of [[Brazil]] and [[Peru]]. The absence of serious internal strife -- a rarity in human affairs -- was due to an innately pacific disposition, a genius for organization, and a powerful inherited tradition of cosmopolitanism from the [[First World State]].

> Even when the sailing ships of Patagonia had founded colonies in remote Africa and Australia, these new communities remained at heart one with the mother country. Even when the almost Nordic culture of the new and temperate Antarctic coasts had outshone the ancient centre, the political harmony of the race was never in danger. (PN:[005-016]

In its later industrial phase, society stratified into two sharply divided classes. A small, highly intellectual governing caste ruled as paternal custodians over a docile proletariat. Children were tested early, and the brightest -- regardless of birth -- were selected for training as organizers, scientists, priests, and logicians. The less gifted were educated for obedience, physical health, and cheerful simplicity. The governors came to regard the populace as perpetual children; the people, though materially well-cared-for, grew spiritually starved and quietly resentful. This class fracture, so unlike the brutal exploitation of the [[First World State]], would prove fatal when the atomic secret was rediscovered.

## Culture

### The Cult of Youth

The dominant spiritual movement of [[Patagonia]] was the Cult of the Divine Boy, founded upon the life of a biologically unique prophet -- a "sport" or throwback to earlier vitality -- who remained physically and mentally young decades after his coevals had aged. Born among the Southern [[Andes]], this "Boy who Refused to Grow Up" preached comradeship, loyalty, and the religious duty of keeping the spirit young. His early gospel of rejuvenation through delight in one another's beauty spread across the civilized world, and his extraordinary fecundity -- producing thousands of offspring -- injected a new biological strain into the [[First Men]], bringing about the golden age of [[Patagonia]].

> It was believed that the prophet had actually lived a century as a fresh adolescent. He was designated the Boy who Refused to Grow up. And this vigour of will was possible to him, it was said, because in him the feeble vitality of the race was concentrated many millionfold. (PN:[005-017]

The prophet's mature teaching -- delivered at forty, when most [[Patagonia]]ns were deeply settled into a mental groove -- was a profound vision of life as a game played for its own sake, not for victory. He urged his followers to outgrow the adulation of life itself and to see existence through the calm, exultant eyes of the cosmic playwright. This message proved almost unintelligible to his contemporaries. After his execution for smashing the temple idol, his followers distorted his vision: the violent young hero was sentimentalized into a docile, naive child, and the cult became "an incubus upon the actual young men and women of the race."

### The Rediscovery of the Past

In its latter period, [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n archaeologists unearthed the buried records of the [[First World State]], discovering that all the ground they had painfully won from the wild had been conquered long before and catastrophically lost. This confrontation with a technically and scientifically superior past was profoundly destabilizing. The [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n system of natural knowledge, which had scarcely advanced beyond pre-[[newton|Newton]]ian levels, suddenly came into a vast inheritance of thought.

> The pioneers discovered that all the ground which they had so painfully won from the wild had been conquered long ago, and lost; that on the material side their glory was nothing beside the glory of the past; and that in the sphere of mind they had established only a few scattered settlements where formerly was an empire. (PN:[005-033]

The revelation divided [[Patagonia]]n society into traditionalists who rejected the new learning as a "satanic lie" and progressives who saw in it a compelling majesty and hope. For the first time in [[Patagonia]]n history, physical [[war|conflict]] erupted. The scientifically-advanced [[Antarctica]]n coast triumphed over conservative [[Patagonia]] proper, and within two centuries the world was unified under the new culture.

### Science and Technology

The [[Patagonia]]ns faced a depleted planet. Coal, oil, and accessible metals had been exhausted by the [[First World State]] millennia before. They relied on wind, water, and volcanic power to generate electricity. Their aeronautics failed -- not for want of ingenuity, but for want of the reckless daring that [[Flying]] demanded. Nevertheless, their most brilliant minds became fixated on the one secret that promised limitless power: the annihilation of matter, which their predecessors had known and wisely abandoned.

## Legacy

The Patagonian Civilization's most enduring legacy was the preservation of human knowledge through the Siberian stone library. The thirty-five [[Arctic]] survivors, determined that the human spirit should not perish, devoted their remaining years to engraving thousands of granite tablets recording the outline of all human knowledge -- physics, chemistry, biology, psychology, geometry, history, and personal testaments. Though the tablets were never used as textbooks by the intended distant future, they survived ten million years of the [[Second Dark Age]] to be discovered by the [[Second Men]] as an invaluable historical document illuminating the mind of their [[First Men]] predecessors.

The civilization also stands as the most dramatic cautionary tale in human history about the perils of atomic power. The [[patagonia|Patagonia]]n disaster -- a self-sustaining chain reaction triggered inadvertently during a miners' rebellion -- transformed the planet into an inferno. Two hundred million [[First Men]] were reduced to thirty-five in three months. The geological scars of the catastrophe persisted for millions of years, reshaping continents, boiling oceans, and delaying the emergence of the [[Second Men]] by ten million years.

> Patagonia's death agony was brief. In Africa and Europe a few remote settlements escaped the actual track of the eruptions, but succumbed in a few weeks to hurricanes of steam. Of the two hundred million members of the human race, all were burnt or roasted or suffocated within three months -- all but thirty-five, who happened to be in the neighbourhood of the North Pole. (PN:[005-048]
