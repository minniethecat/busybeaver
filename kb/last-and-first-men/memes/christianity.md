Christianity was the religion centered on the figure of [[jesus|Jesus of Nazareth]], whose ethical ideal of selfless love became one of the enduring spiritual legacies of the [[first-men|First Men]]. Its influence persisted across species, with later human forms — particularly the [[second-men|Second Men]] — realizing aspects of the Christian ideal that the First Men could only aspire to.

## Core Ideal

The central ethical teaching of Christianity was the ideal of loving one's neighbor as oneself. This ideal, alongside the Socratic ideal of dispassionate cognition, formed the moral axis of Western civilization. Together they constituted a vision "of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable" [001-004] — a yearning that exceeded the biological capacities of the species.

## Historical Manifestations

### The Regenerate Christian Brotherhood

In the late [[first-world-state|First World State]], the [[regenerate-christian-brotherhood|Regenerate Christian Brotherhood]] emerged as "overwhelmingly the most powerful of the Western Churches" [004-012]. It regarded the Discoverer of atomic energy as the Son of God in his Second Coming, with the [[daughter-of-man|Daughter of Man]] as his holy counterpart, their union sanctifying the new political order.

### The Perverted Love-Cult

A darker manifestation appeared as a secret cult in the late First World State — a perverted "ancient Christian faith in a God of Love" [004-045] that demonstrated how even the highest ideals could be degraded.

### The Symbol of Flight

The ancient Christian cross lent an additional mystery to the practice of flight. The [[gordelpus|Gordelpus]] cult successfully appropriated this symbolism, with professional fliers regarded as the private servants of Gordelpus — both sacred and outside normal social constraints [004-032].

## Legacy Across Species

### The Second Men as "Natural Christians"

The [[second-men|Second Men]], with their naturally generous dispositions, realized what the First Men only aspired to: they "loved one another in the manner of Jesus, and infused their whole social policy with loving-kindness" [007-010]. Stapledon described them as "Natural Christians," so readily and constantly did they practice the ideal that had been impossible for their predecessors.

### The Conflict with Fate Worship

The Second Men were also "by nature rigorous thinkers" and were "peculiarly liable to be disturbed by the conflict between their religion of love and their loyalty to fate" [007-010]. This tension — between the Christian ideal of love and the worship of cosmic destiny — persisted as one of the deepest spiritual dilemmas across human evolution.
