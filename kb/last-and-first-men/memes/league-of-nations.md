# League of Nations

An international association of national governments that became the primary instrument of diplomacy in the late era of the [[First Men]], and the most important precursor to the [[First World State]].

## Overview

Founded after the [[European War]] as a mechanism for collective security, the League of Nations was never a truly cosmopolitan government -- each member state remained concerned primarily with its own sovereignty. Yet it gradually accumulated genuine prestige and moral authority, becoming "the great concrete focusing point of the growing loyalty toward humanity" [003-002].

## History

### Key Actions

Following the devastating [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]], in which both England and France were shattered, the management of each country was taken over temporarily by the League of Nations [001-025]. This established the precedent for direct international administration over great-power territories.

The League's supreme achievement was cosmopolitan control of the world's fuel reserves. After a severe oil famine "sobered the race," the League imposed strict international regulation over all oilfields and coal fields -- a policy so sound that it "remained a fixed policy of the race long after the League had been superseded" [003-020].

### Decline

The League was fatally compromised by great-power rivalries. When the [[European Confederacy]] formed, [[america|America]] withdrew, interpreting it as a hostile act. China remained, hoping to use the League against its rival.

After the [[russo-german-war|Russo-German War]], the world divided between a League bloc dominated by China and an [[america|America]]n bloc. The organization "no longer stood for cosmopolitanism" [003-002]. Those loyal to humanity worked to reunite the powers -- and succeeded, but the League had been hollowed out.

## Details

### Founded

The League was established by the victorious powers of the [[European War]], seeking a permanent framework for peace. Its founding charter reflected a central tension: it was simultaneously an instrument for preventing [[war|conflict]] and a mechanism for codifying great-power dominance.

### Purpose

The League's stated purpose was the peaceful settlement of international disputes. In practice, it oscillated between genuine arbitration and serving the strongest members. France, for instance, persuaded the League to restrict military aircraft sizes in a manner that placed London within easy striking distance of French airfields while shielding Paris [001-015].

### Structure

The League was an association of sovereign governments, not a supranational body. A High Court adjudicated disputes, and its findings gradually acquired such moral weight that no single power dared openly defy them [003-002].

## Legacy

The League demonstrated both the promise and the fatal limitation of voluntary international cooperation. Its fuel-control regime was its greatest practical contribution, surviving the League's demise by centuries. More importantly, it established in the human imagination the necessity of planetary governance, a lesson that carried forward into the [[First World State]].
