# Disease

**Disease** was a persistent biological affliction that shaped human history across multiple species and eras, from the microbial epidemics of the [[first-men|First Men]] to the species-specific vulnerabilities that emerged as humanity evolved through its eighteen successive forms.

## First Men Era

In the early First Men era, disease was a catastrophic force that could unravel civilizations. During periods of social breakdown, it proved more devastating than warfare itself:

> "Famine, riot, looting, and above all the rapidly accelerating and quite uncontrollable spread of disease, disintegrated both States, and brought war to a standstill." [001-024]

In the war's aftermath, only the most fertile regions could sustain the "diseased remnant of a population" as jungle reclaimed vast territories [004-059].

## Conquest of Microbial Disease

One of the greatest achievements of the [[first-men|First Men]] was the medical conquest of infectious disease at its microbial root:

> "No longer did men and women have to dread for themselves or those dear to them such afflictions as cancer, tuberculosis, angina pectoris, the rheumatic diseases, and the terrible disorders of the nervous system." [004-014]

This victory, attributed to the [[science-concept|scientific knowledge that conquered microbial disease]], was a watershed in human history, freeing civilization from one of its oldest constraints.

## Vulnerability of Later Species

Despite the First Men's triumph, vulnerability to disease re-emerged in later human species when exposed to unfamiliar pathogens. The [[second-men|Second Men]] proved "terribly liable to the diseases of their wretched subhuman kinsfolk" — the baboon-like descendants of the First Men. Only "very rigorous quarantine" stamped out the resulting epidemic. This biological vulnerability was later exploited deliberately: the subhuman tribes weaponized disease against the Second Men, "partly for revenge, but partly also through malicious delight."

## Legacy

Disease serves as a recurring theme across the two-billion-year span of human history. Each human species achieved medical mastery within its own biosphere, yet remained vulnerable when contacting alien biology — whether from subhuman cousins on Earth or from the [[martians|Martian]] cloud-beings. The pattern of conquest followed by renewed vulnerability reflected the endless biological arms race that defined the human condition.
