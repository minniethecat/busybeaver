# Australia

A southern continent on Earth that received Patagonian colonies and was later transformed through geological upheaval into a continuous landmass with the East Indies.

## Location

### Type

Continent

### Context

Australia is located in the Southern Hemisphere, south of the East Indies. It was distant from the main centres of First Men civilization but accessible to the sailing ships of the Patagonian era.

## Physical Description

Australia was an isolated continent — the smallest on Earth. During the moonfall catastrophe, the East Indies and Australia became a continuous continent through geological upheaval [006-031]. A later warping of the planet's crust would produce "an island almost as large as Australia" elsewhere [013-011].

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

Australia was significant during the Patagonian era as a destination for colonization, and during the moonfall era when it was geographically merged with the East Indies.

### Key Events

During the pre-World State period of global rivalry, Australia was one of the "poor relations" of the wealthy American Continental Federation, alongside South Africa and New Zealand [003-004]. Australia, "not through cowardice, but through conflict of loyalties," was soon reduced to neutrality in the global conflicts [002-033]. The sailing ships of Patagonia founded colonies in remote Africa and Australia — though so powerful was Patagonian tradition that these new communities maintained their cultural identity [005-016]. During the moonfall catastrophe, Australia and the East Indies were merged into a continuous continent [006-031].

## Species & Civilizations

The First Men navigated to and settled Australia during the Patagonian era. The continent was drawn into the American sphere of influence during the global rivalry. Patagonian colonists established communities that maintained their cultural traditions.

## Changes Over Time

Australia evolved from an isolated continent to a Patagonian colonial outpost, then merged with the East Indies through catastrophic geological transformation. It later served as a reference point for the scale of new islands produced by crustal warping.
