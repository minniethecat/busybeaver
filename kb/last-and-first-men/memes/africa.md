# Africa

Earth's second-largest continent, which aligned with Asia to form a geopolitical counter-bloc to the American-dominated Western powers during the First Men era.

## Location

### Type

Continent

### Context

Africa occupies the southern portion of the Eastern Hemisphere's landmass. French territories in Africa were a source of colonial rivalry — Italy was "hungry for French territory in Africa" [001-012]. The continent spanned from the Mediterranean coast in the north to South Africa in the south.

## Physical Description

Africa encompasses diverse geography from the Sahara in the north (later transformed into a lake district) to the temperate southern regions. North Africa experienced the same climatic transformation that reshaped the Sahara, turning it into holiday resort country [004-024].

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

Africa played a significant role in the post-European global order. Along with Asia, it formed one of the two great geopolitical blocs that emerged after the eclipse of European power [003-004]. This kind of cosmopolitanism was regarded by Asia and Africa "without sympathy" — the continent rejected the American-European model of international order [003-006].

### Key Events

South Africa and India declared their neutrality at the outbreak of the Anglo-French war [002-033]. In the major global conflict between the American bloc and the Asia-Africa bloc, "Asia, with North Africa, formed geographically the more compact system" of combatants, though America and her dependents were "economically more organized" [003-023]. A West African president of the International Society of Scientific Workers was a famous biologist, known for his interbreeding of man and ape [002-013]. Italy attacked France partly out of hunger for French territory in Africa [001-012].

## Species & Civilizations

The First Men inhabited Africa. South Africa was one of the "poor relations" of the wealthy American Continental Federation [003-004]. The continent was home to diverse cultures and nations, both aligned with and opposed to the American-led global order.

## Changes Over Time

Africa's political alignment shifted from a patchwork of European colonies and independent nations to a key part of the Asia-Africa bloc. The continent's geography was reshaped by climate change and, over geological timescales, by the tectonic events that transformed all continents.
