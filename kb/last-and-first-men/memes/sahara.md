# Sahara

A dramatically transformed region of North Africa that, due to climatic changes, became a lake district and popular resort area during the World State era of the First Men.

## Location

### Type

Region

### Context

Located in North Africa. The Sahara was part of the larger Asia-Africa geopolitical bloc that opposed the American Continental Federation during the global rivalry [003-023].

## Physical Description

Under the changed climatic conditions of the World State era, the Sahara was no longer a desert but "a lake district, crowded with sun-proud holiday resorts" [004-024]. This transformation was part of a broader pattern of climate engineering and natural change — the coasts of Antarctica were also thawed and permanently inhabited during the same period [004-024].

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

The Sahara's transformation represents the technological and climatic mastery achieved by the World State civilization. Its use as a holiday destination illustrates the leisure and affluence that the World State afforded its citizens.

### Key Events

The Sahara's conversion from desert to lake district likely resulted from the same large-scale climatic and geological changes that reshaped other parts of Earth — including the thawing of Antarctica's coasts and the warping of the crust near Patagonia (004-024, [005-010].

## Species & Civilizations

The First Men inhabited the Sahara region during the World State era. The holiday resorts suggest a cosmopolitan, affluent civilization that had mastered climate control and turned previously uninhabitable regions into leisure destinations.

## Changes Over Time

The Sahara transitioned from one of Earth's most inhospitable deserts to a thriving lake district. Over geological time, the region was further reshaped by the tectonic and climatic upheavals that affected all of Earth's geography.
