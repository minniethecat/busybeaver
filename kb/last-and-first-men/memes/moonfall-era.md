# Moonfall Era

The Moonfall Era was the period of geological catastrophe when Earth's moon, under tidal deceleration, spiraled inward and disintegrated — shattering the planet's surface, reducing humanity to a Siberian remnant, and compelling the evolutionary transition from First to Second Men.

## Prelude

The approaching moon had been anticipated for ages. "Even the First Men had learned that the moon must first withdraw from and subsequently once more approach the earth" [012-022]. Astronomers across multiple civilizations had calculated the inevitable: the moon's orbit would decay until it reached "critical proximity and begin to break up into a swarm of fragments like the rings of Saturn" [012-022].

But knowledge of a catastrophe is not preparation for it. When the event arrived, prediction gave way to devastation. The fragments did not form a stable ring — they "would soon crash upon the earth" [012-025], and the ensuing bombardment reshaped the planet beyond recognition.

## The Catastrophe

### Lunar Disintegration

The moon crossed the critical orbital threshold — the Roche limit — and tidal forces tore it apart. What had been Earth's companion for four billion years became a swarm of orbital debris. The largest fragments, still massive enough to devastate continents, began their descent.

### Global Devastation

Falling fragments triggered continent-scale lava flows, permanent volcanic winter, and atmospheric poisoning. Oceans boiled along impact corridors. Continental landmasses fractured and recombined — [[south-america|South America]] mostly "collapsed into hollows," while a new land rose joining [[brazil|Brazil]] to West Africa [006-031].

The [[east-indies|East Indies]] and [[australia|Australia]] became a single continuous continent [006-031]. [[europe|Europe]], once the seat of human civilization, became uninhabitable. The planet's geography was rewritten in a geological afternoon.

## The Siberian Exile

For millions of years afterward, "the planet was uninhabitable save for a fringe of Siberian coast" [006-020]. This narrow strip — "a tropical land, bounded on the south by a forest of volcanoes" [006-028] — became humanity's last refuge. Here the First Men survived, "established as a weakened species" [007-001], for ten million years.

The volcanic wall that protected Siberia also imprisoned it. The First Men's remnant could not expand, could not rebuild civilization, could only endure. They kept a stone library — knowledge carved into rock when all other media had perished — and waited for evolution or extinction.

## Birth of the Second Men

From this Siberian crucible, a new species emerged. The [[second-men|Second Men]] arose from the First Men's remnant: "a new and more human species, which would long outlive the disaster" [007-001]. Natural selection, operating under extreme conditions for ten million years, had produced a hardier, more integrated human type.

The Second Men inherited the stone library and built upon it. Where the First Men had merely survived, the Second Men would rebuild civilization — and eventually lead humanity's [[migration-to-venus|migration to Venus]] when Earth's long-term habitability could no longer be guaranteed.

## Geological Legacy

The moonfall permanently altered Earth. The planet's crust, once stable, remained volcanically active for millions of years. The Siberian refuge eventually expanded as conditions moderated, but Earth would never again be the planet the First Men had known. The catastrophe marked the end of Earth's geological adolescence and the beginning of its scarred maturity.

## End

The Moonfall Era concluded when the planet stabilized sufficiently for the Second Men to expand beyond Siberia and establish a mature global civilization. The trauma of the moonfall seeded the interplanetary ambition that would define the next great chapter of human history.
