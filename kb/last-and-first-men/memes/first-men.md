# First Men

The First Men are *Homo sapiens* — the original human species and ancestral root of all eighteen species that would populate the [[Solar System]] across two billion years.

Viewed from the remote perspective of the [[Last Men]] on [[Neptune]], the First Men represent humanity's tumultuous infancy. They conceived the highest ideals articulated by [[Socrates]] and [[Jesus]], yet were repeatedly undone by primitive tribal passions.

In the narrator's judgment, they were "after all, but superficially human" [001-008]. Their entire struggle "from savagery towards civilization" is but a single moment of precocious awakening in the long sleep of the human spirit [001-002].

Emerging from prehistory on [[Earth]], the First Men achieved a brief zenith around the 20th century. They then constructed a planet-spanning [[First World State]] that endured four thousand years, only to collapse into the [[First Dark Age]].

A partial recovery in [[Patagonia]] produced a sober but diminished civilization, which destroyed itself through catastrophic misuse of atomic power. After ten million years, the remnant in [[Siberia|Siberian exile]] gave way to the [[Second Men]], chronicled alongside the later emergence of the [[Martians]].

## Biology

### Physical Form and Adaptations

The First Men were the biological baseline for all subsequent [[species|human species]]. Their erect bipedal form, dexterous hands, and large brain-case enabled tool-making and abstract thought.

Their nervous system remained precariously balanced between higher cortical functions and ancient subcortical drives — a tension the [[Second Men]] would later resolve. During the Siberian exile, the First Men acquired a thick hairy covering, adapting to extreme Arctic climates over millions of years [007-002].

Physiologically, the First Men were burdened by vestigial structures — the appendix, wisdom teeth, and tonsils — which later species including the [[Second Men]] would shed [007-003].

Their glandular system drove an adolescence of roughly two decades, but the individual's chemical equilibrium was inherently unstable. This contributed to a "general senescence of the species" during the latter phases of their history [005-004].

### Lifespan and Reproduction

Before medical rejuvenation under the [[First World State]], the First Men lived approximately seventy years, with youth lasting some twenty-five. Under the World State, artificial regeneration doubled the active lifespan.

Among the First Men, sexuality was both creative and destructive — "the sexual impulse, which the First Men had ever tended to regard as both divine and obscene" [004-041]. It generated their richest cultural achievements and their most intractable psychological [[war|conflict]]s.

Unlike the later [[Second Men]], who sublimated sexuality into a universal appreciation of living forms, the First Men never achieved a stable integration of their sexual nature with their higher aspirations.

## Psychology

### Mental Architecture

The defining psychological feature of the First Men was a tragic duality. They could conceive ideals of dispassionate intelligence (personified by [[Socrates]]) and selfless love (personified by [[Jesus]]), yet their nervous systems were incapable of sustaining these ideals in collective life.

As the [[Last Men]] observed:

> Unfortunately both these ideals demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable. For many centuries these twin stars enticed the more precociously human of human animals, in vain. And the failure to put these ideals in practice helped to engender in the race a cynical lassitude which was one cause of its decay. [001-004]

This internal contradiction — between aspiration and capacity — defined the First Men across all their historical phases. As the [[last-men|Last Men]] noted, "Animals that were fashioned for hunting and fighting in the wild were suddenly called upon to be citizens, and moreover citizens of a world-community" [001-007].

The species never resolved the tension between its biological inheritance and its cultural demands. This was a problem the [[Second Men]] would later address through a more unified nervous organization.

### Tribal Instinct and Cosmopolitan Aspiration

The deepest fault-line in the First Men's psychology lay between tribal loyalty and cosmopolitan vision. Even at moments of greatest enlightenment, they remained vulnerable to atavistic regression. The peace following the [[European War]]:

> epitomizes both the dawning vision and the incurable blindness, both the impulse toward a higher loyalty and the compulsive tribalism of a race which was, after all, but superficially human. [001-008]

This duality made the First Men peculiarly susceptible to catastrophic miscalculation. The [[Last Men]], reflecting on the [[First World State]], noted that "the unstable nature of the First Men made them peculiarly liable to suffer from such accidents, and especially so in their decline" [003-032].

This psychological instability would echo through the ages, influencing even the remote descendants among the [[Fifth Men]].

## History

### From Savagery to the First World State

The First Men's history on [[Earth]] unfolded across five major phases. The earliest, spanning prehistory to the 20th century, saw the emergence of language, agriculture, cities, and industrial civilization.

This period culminated in the [[European War]] of 1914-1918 and the catastrophic [[Anglo-French War]] that followed within a century. The latter [[war|conflict]] was sparked by a false accusation of sexual assault.

It turned on a stray French aircraft crashing in [[London]] after a British peace overture. This single accident "may be said to have sealed the fate of the First Men" [001-009]. Had it not occurred, "the first Dark Age might never have occurred" [001-015].

There followed the eclipse of [[Europe]], the rise of [[America]] and [[China]] as rival superpowers, and the [[Russo-German War]]. Europe was destroyed by [[america|America]]n poison gas [002-031].

After centuries of [[war|conflict]], the [[First World State]] was established — a unified planetary civilization under a [[world-financial-directorate|World Financial Directorate]]. It endured some four thousand years, dominated by the cult of [[Gordelpus]], the divine principle of motion and energy [004-010].

### The First Dark Age and Patagonian Recovery

The [[First World State]] collapsed when its coal supplies — squandered on ritual [[flying|aviation]] in worship of [[Gordelpus]] — ran out. Economic collapse and the "[[america|America]]n Madness" plague plunged humanity into the [[First Dark Age]]: one hundred thousand years of savagery [005-006].

A slow rebirth occurred in [[South America]], where the [[Patagonia|Patagonian civilization]] arose — sober, less sexual, more intellectually inclined than earlier cultures. They were afflicted by premature aging that curtailed youth to little more than a decade [005-013].

The [[patagonia|Patagonia]]ns achieved a stable world order, rediscovered the sciences of their predecessors, and ultimately unlocked atomic energy. Their civilization destroyed [[Earth]] in a chain reaction that left thirty-five survivors at the [[North Pole]] [005-023].

> The unstable nature of the First Men made them peculiarly liable to suffer from such accidents, and especially so in their decline. [003-032]

### Final Phases and Succession

The Siberian remnant persevered for ten million years through the [[Second Dark Age]], preserving a stone library of human knowledge. From this seed emerged the [[Second Men]] — a species that shed "primitive relics which had hampered the First Men more than was realized" [007-003].

Unlike their predecessors, the [[Second Men]] possessed innate aptitudes for cosmopolitanism, dispassionate cognition, and altruistic love [007-004]. The remaining tribes of the First Men "faded out" [007-013]. Their genetic and cultural legacy persisted through all later species, up to the [[Great Brains]] and the [[Last Men]].

## Cultural Achievements

Despite their fatal flaws, the First Men achieved genuine cultural heights. Their scientific tradition — founded by figures in [[France]] and [[England]] in the 17th and 18th centuries — established dispassionate inquiry into the physical world.

Their philosophical tradition, from [[Socrates]] through the last European intellectuals, articulated ideals of intellectual integrity that would inspire the [[Second Men]]. Their artistic legacy spanned Aeschylus, Michelangelo, and Beethoven.

In [[architecture]], they built the towering residential pylons that would survive as ruins for a hundred millennia [005-006].

## Decline and Legacy

The decline of the First Men was a progressive exhaustion spanning hundreds of millennia. Contributing factors included the inherent instability of the nervous system [001-004] and the species' inability to subordinate tribal instincts to cosmopolitan needs [001-015].

Biological "senescence" shortened youth and curbed vitality [005-004]. A recurring pattern of technological ambition outstripping moral capacity was visible across the [[European War]], the [[Anglo-French War]], and the [[First World State]]'s collapse.

The legacy of the First Men is paradoxical. To the [[Last Men]] on [[Neptune]], they were barely more than clever animals — yet they were the indispensable first spark. They invented [[science]], [[philosophy]], and the concept of a universal human community.

They recorded their knowledge in stone for the [[Second Men]]. They provided the biological and cultural seed from which the [[Second Men]], [[Fifth Men]], and all eighteen species of the [[Solar System]] would develop.

Without the First Men's flawed but heroic struggle toward self-awareness, the two-billion-year epic of humanity could never have begun.
