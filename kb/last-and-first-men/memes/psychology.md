# Psychology

Psychology — the understanding of mind, mentality, and consciousness — is a central preoccupation of *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]*. The book traces the transformation of human psychology from the First Men's fragile individual minds, through species-level variations in temperament and cognition, to the [[last-men|Last Men]]'s [[group-mind|racial group mind]] in which individuality is transcended.

## Definition

In the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian narrative, psychology is not merely a clinical discipline but the study of consciousness itself — how minds are shaped by biology, society, and cosmic circumstance. Each of the eighteen human species represents a distinct psychological experiment, and the differences between species are as much psychological as biological.

## Origin

### First Men's Mentality

The psychology of the [[first-men|First Men]] was characterized by profound limitation. "Unfortunately both these ideals demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable" [001-004]. Their mental powers were perpetually "whipped and enticed by precocious experiences" while the "actual social constitution of his world kept changing so rapidly... that his primitive nature could no longer cope with the complexity of his environment" [001-006].

The First Men's mental decline was a central tragedy. "Scarcely at all beyond your own day is this early culture to be seen progressing, and already in your time the mentality of the race shows signs of decline" [001-002]. Had the world state been achieved "before the general level of mentality had seriously begun to decline, the attainment of the world state might have been regarded, not as an end, but as the first step towards true civilization" [001-009].

### National Psychologies

The First Men were acutely aware of psychological differences between peoples. The French and English possessed "that subtle difference of mentality which had ever made it impossible for these two nations to understand one another" [001-016]. These national psychological differences persisted even as "increased communication, increased cultural intercourse, and a prolonged vigorous campaign for cosmopolitanism, had changed the mentality of Europe" [001-018].

## Details

### Species-Level Psychological Variation

Each human species possessed a distinct psychological character. The [[second-men|Second Men]] had "a natural propensity" for intellectual activities "no less vigorous than the primitive instincts" [007-006]. The [[third-men|Third Men]] witnessed the decline of disciplined thought. The [[eighth-men|Eighth Men]], "deeming themselves to be near the crest of life's achievement, blessed also with longevity and philosophic detachment" [012-006], cultivated a serene psychological distance. The [[last-men|Last Men]] achieved the ultimate psychological transformation — the subsumption of individual minds into the [[group-mind|racial mind]].

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

The evolution of psychology across eighteen species is the book's deepest theme. Each species represents a different answer to the question: what forms can human consciousness take? From the First Men's cramped and declining minds to the Last Men's cosmic group consciousness, the history of humanity is, fundamentally, the history of mind becoming aware of itself.
