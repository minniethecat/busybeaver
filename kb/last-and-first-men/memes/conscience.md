## Overview

Conscience — the inner moral sense — evolves across the eighteen human species from a culturally-bound emotion to a cosmic ethical faculty. In "Last and First Men," conscience serves as both a restraint on human cruelty and, paradoxically, as a justification for it.

## Conscience Among the First Men

The First Men's conscience was deeply shaped by nationalism and ideology. When conflict erupted, one side unleashed "slanders against the Eastern conscience" [003-022], framing their aggression as moral righteousness. Conscience was weaponized — used to justify war rather than prevent it.

The President of the World State embodied the tortured conscience of power. He would "suffer forever the pangs of conscience" [004-004] for his actions, yet continued to wield power. This paradox — knowing the wrong while doing it — characterized the First Men's moral immaturity.

## The Martian War and Collective Guilt

The war against the Martians "left a scar upon the conscience of the victors" [008-037]. Even in victory, humanity recognized it had committed acts that violated its moral self-image. This collective guilt marked a maturation of conscience — from a tool of self-justification to an acknowledgment of shared responsibility.
