# Great Brains

The Great Brains, or Fourth Men, represented one of the most radical evolutionary experiments in human history. They were immense disembodied brains maintained in life-support towers, capable of intellectual achievements far beyond any previous species — yet they were emotionally crippled and ultimately tragic figures.

> Those who sought to produce a super-brain embarked upon a great enterprise of research and experiment in a remote corner of the planet. Working first in secret, they later strove to persuade the world that their ambition was important and practicable.

## Biology

The Great Brains were not bodies with brains but brains without bodies — enormous cerebral organs housed in specialized towers, freed from the constraints of physical form. This allowed intellectual development impossible for embodied minds, but cut them off from the emotional and sensory richness that had defined earlier humanity.

> The great brain was wholly lacking in all normal instinctive responses, save curiosity and constructiveness. Instinctive fear he knew not, though of course he was capable of cold caution in any circumstances which threatened to damage him and hinder his work.

## Psychology

Their supreme intelligence was paradoxically their limitation. Devoid of embodied experience, they could manipulate abstract systems with godlike facility but could not feel or create as the [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]] would. They represented pure cognition unmoored from the rest of human experience.

> Little by little the great brain probed the material universe and the universe of mentality. He mastered the principles of biological evolution, and constructed for his own delight a detailed history of life on earth.

Their curiosity was infinite, but their capacity for empathy, love, or aesthetic experience was nil — a profound price for intellectual transcendence.

## Creation and History

The first successful Great Brain was produced through a combination of selective breeding and laboratory manipulation, building upon the biological expertise of the [[third-men|Third Men]].

> When some three thousand years had passed since his beginning, the unique individual determined to create others of his kind. Not that he suffered from loneliness. Not that he yearned for love, or even for intellectual companionship. But solely for the furtherance of the great work of understanding the cosmos.

Eventually, multiple Great Brains populated the Earth, forming a loose community of intellects engaged in the pure pursuit of knowledge, with no interest in governance or material civilization.

> The normal mind, when it experiences intellectual frustration, can seek recreation in companionship, or physical exercise, or art. But for the Fourth Men there was no such escape. These activities were impossible and meaningless to them. The Great Brains were wholeheartedly interested in the objective universe, and in the life of reason. But they were incapable of any deep personal relationships, even with one another.

## Legacy

Though the Great Brains themselves had no emotional connection to humanity, they inadvertently laid the intellectual groundwork for their successors. The [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]], who would eventually supplant them, inherited the Great Brains' vast stores of knowledge while restoring the emotional and social dimensions the Fourth Men had sacrificed. In this sense, the Great Brains were a necessary phase in human evolution — a detour into pure intellect that enriched the human heritage even as it demonstrated the insufficiency of reason alone.
