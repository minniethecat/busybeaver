## Overview

Individuality in "Last and First Men" is a central philosophical theme that runs through the entire two-billion-year history. Across eighteen human species, the nature and value of individual identity fluctuates dramatically — from the nationalistic pride of the First Men to the collective "super-individuality" of the Last Men. Stapledon uses this spectrum to explore what it means to be a person.

## Individuality Among the First Men

The First Men were characterized by a fierce sense of individuality tied to national identity. In the early chapters, European peoples were described as "proud of its individuality, of its success, of its ideals" [002-005]. This nationalistic individualism contributed directly to the wars that eventually destroyed their civilization.

## The Martian Model: Fluctuating Identity

Among the Martians, individuality operated on a fundamentally different principle. The Martians existed as "cloudlets" of subvital units that could merge and separate. "The same fluctuation between individuality and multiplicity which characterized the units occurred also in the cloudlets" [008-022]. Individual Martians could dissolve into group-minds — a form of existence that was both terrifying and fascinating to human observers.

A key difference from human individuality was that a Martian's "individuality was both far more liable to disruption and far more capable of expansion" [008-026]. They could achieve what humans called the "group-mind," merging multiple individual perspectives into a single conscious entity.

## The Third Men and Musical Transcendence

The Third Men discovered that music could dissolve the barriers of individuality. Through musical experience, "the barriers of their individuality were broken down, so that they themselves became the music" [010-018]. This represented a controlled, aesthetic transcendence of the self — distinct from the biological merging of the Martians.

## The Last Men: Super-Individuality

The Eighteenth Men achieved the highest synthesis of individual and collective existence. They "love and hate. They sin and repent" [015-019], retaining the "simple beauties of human individuality" while simultaneously participating in a communal consciousness. The narrator warns that one "should never cut the string of individuality and scatter the hard-won pearls of personal experience" [015-020].

In their final cosmic moment, the Last Men aspired toward "the community and super-individuality of that great host" [015-060] — a merging not of biological units but of fully realized persons. "Wherever there is individuality of form, there is also an individuality of spirit" [015-073], suggesting that form and consciousness are inseparable.
