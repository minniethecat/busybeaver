# Patagonia

The cradle of the final [[first-men|First Men]] civilization, a region transformed by geological catastrophe into humanity's last terrestrial nursery before the species' near-extinction.

## Location

Patagonia occupies the southern extremity of [[South America]] on [[Earth]], east of the ancient city of [[Bahia Blanca]]. After the fall of the [[First World State]], an immense warping of the [[earth-place|Earth]]'s crust thrust the shallow ocean east and south of Patagonia upward into a vast new continent (PN:[005-010].

This new landmass connected South [[america|America]] with [[Antarctica]] by way of the former Falkland Islands and South Georgia, extending far into the Atlantic. The geological transformation turned what had been a marginal zone into fertile and extensive terrain uniquely suited to nurture a new civilization.

## Physical Description

The virgin plains offered rich, unspoiled terrain while the northern continents lay exhausted. Climatic changes rendered southern South [[america|America]] temperate, creating conditions that favoured settled life (PN:[005-010]. The cultural centre lay upon the new coast northeast of the Falkland Islands, while the outskirts extended into Brazil and Peru (PN:[005-016].

## Historical Significance

### The End of the First Dark Age

Patagonia was the stage on which the [[First Dark Age]] came to its close. After a hundred thousand years of savagery, the rebirth of civil life occurred not in Europe or Asia but in the Southern Hemisphere. A convergence of racial stocks -- descendants of ancient Indian and Peruvian peoples mingled with European cosmopolitan remnants -- produced a population of renewed vigour (PN:[005-011].

The Patagonians were a sober, intellectually inclined people whose "animal nature was somewhat subdued," making them "more capable of dispassionate cognition" than their febrile predecessors (PN:[005-013].

### The Patagonian Civilization

Upon these plains arose the [[Patagonian Civilization]], the last and most measured culture of the [[First Men]]. Unlike earlier civilizations that blazed and collapsed within a thousand years, the Patagonians advanced with steady deliberation across fifteen millennia (PN:[005-015].

Their sailing ships founded colonies in Africa and Australia; the political harmony persisted even when the temperate Antarctic coasts developed a culture that outshone the ancient centre (PN:[005-016].

A biological tragedy shadowed this achievement: the [[First Men]] had entered species-wide senescence. Youth endured scarcely fifteen years; by that age individuals were settling into middle-age, their bones brittle and minds hardening (PN:[005-012]. This premature aging became the civilization's central wound, driving its obsession with [[Longevity]] that the species could no longer sustain.

### Destruction and Legacy

Patagonia's story ends in catastrophe. The rediscovery of atomic power during the civilization's final phase triggered an uncontrollable chain reaction.

Of two hundred million human beings, all were "burnt or roasted or suffocated within three months -- all but thirty-five" who happened to be near the North Pole (PN:[005-048]. The geological scars persisted for millions of years, delaying the emergence of the [[Second Men]].

Yet the legacy endured. The thirty-five survivors preserved human knowledge on granite tablets in [[Siberia]]. After ten million years of the [[Second Dark Age]], their descendants evolved into the [[Second Men]] -- inheriting Patagonian intellectual sobriety without the biological enfeeblement that doomed their ancestors.
