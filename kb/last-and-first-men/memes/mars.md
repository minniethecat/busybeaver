# Mars

The fourth planet of the Solar System, and the homeworld of the [[martians|Martians]], a species of cloud-like composite organisms who invaded [[earth|Earth]] during the age of the [[first-men|First Men]]. Mars represents one of the most extraordinary forms of life in the Solar System's history.

## Physical Description

Mars was a smaller world than Earth, with lower mass and thinner atmosphere. Unlike the blue-and-green Earth, Mars was a reddish, arid planet whose resources were dwindling even before the rise of its dominant life form.

> "Mars was a world whose mass was about one-tenth that of the Earth." [008-012]

In earlier epochs, Mars possessed more atmosphere, more water, and a more temperate climate. "Earlier in the history of Mars, when there were more air, more water, and a more genial temperature" [008-013], conditions were favorable for life. But as the planet aged, its resources diminished, creating existential pressure on its inhabitants.

## The Martian Life Form

The dominant life on Mars was radically different from terrestrial organisms. Rather than individual bodies composed of discrete cells, Martian life was a collective, cloud-like entity.

> "The life which dominated Mars was thus something between an organism and a world-population." [008-021]

The Martians were unified fields of sub-vital units — microscopic particles that together formed a coherent consciousness. Individual "cells" were semi-autonomous but integrated into a planetary-scale intelligence. This unique biology made the Martians both powerful and vulnerable: powerful in their coordinated action, vulnerable in their dependence on an increasingly depleted planet.

### The Invasion of Earth

Driven by resource scarcity, the Martians launched an invasion of Earth. They descended upon the terrestrial world seeking water, atmosphere, and biological resources to sustain their dying planet.

> "Thus ended the first invasion of the Earth from Mars." [008-009]

The invasion was devastating to human civilization. The Martians extracted water from Earth's oceans and returned it to their homeworld: "considerable bulk of water was carried to Mars" [009-015]. Successive raids stripped Earth of resources while the First Men struggled to mount an effective defense.

### The Martian Tragedy

Despite their power, the Martians were trapped in a spiritual crisis. "In Mars, all known diamonds were exposed to some kind of radiation" [008-038], and their collective consciousness, while intellectually brilliant, was spiritually barren.

> "The Martians were in many ways more developed than man, but their development was one-sided." [008-041]

Their civilization eventually collapsed from internal contradictions. The "super-individual of Mars extirpated its own offspring" [009-020] in a self-destructive act that ended Martian civilization. This tragedy became a cautionary tale in later human history, illustrating the dangers of intelligence without wisdom.

## Historical Significance

The Martian invasion was a pivotal event in First Men history, accelerating the decline of terrestrial civilization. The Martians themselves represented an alternative evolutionary path — collective rather than individual, unified rather than diverse — that fascinated later human philosophers.

When the [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]] considered colonizing Mars, "the former was by now without water and without atmosphere" [012-026], confirming that the Martians' resource depletion had been terminal.
