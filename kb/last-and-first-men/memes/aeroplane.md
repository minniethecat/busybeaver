# Aeroplane

The **aeroplane** was the mechanically powered fixed-wing aircraft that served as humanity's primary form of aerial transportation from the era of the [[first-men|First Men]] through the era of the [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]]. It played crucial roles in warfare, commerce, and cultural exchange before eventually being displaced by biological flight and more advanced technologies.

## Early Military Role

In the era of the First Men, the aeroplane was primarily an instrument of war. International rivalries shaped its development:

> "France had persuaded the League of Nations (in one of its less intelligent moments) to restrict the size of military aeroplanes in such a manner that, while London lay within easy striking distance of the French coast, Paris was beyond the range of the British Air Force." [001-015]

This asymmetry contributed directly to the outbreak of the [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]], demonstrating how aeroplane technology shaped geopolitical power dynamics.

## Industrial and Civilian Use

By the era of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], aeroplanes had become tools of industry and personal transport. The [[world-financial-directorate|World Financial Directorate]] building featured aerial landings on every floor for "the dwarf private aeroplanes of the Director" [004-022]. The Chinese industrial working class, however, could not afford such luxuries:

> "The fact that many Chinese industrial workers could not afford to keep a motorcar, let alone an aeroplane, was a source of much self-righteous indignation amongst American employers." [003-011]

## Patagonian Revival

During the [[patagonian-civilization|Patagonian civilization]], electrically driven aeroplanes were constructed as part of a broader technological renaissance [005-037]. Wind and water power generated electricity for industrial operations, enabling a new generation of aerial vehicles.

## Competition with Biological Flight

The [[third-men|Third Men]]'s evolutionary specialization in flight through their own winged bodies created tension between natural and mechanical flight. By the era of the [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]], aerial technology on [[venus|Venus]] had progressed beyond the aeroplane entirely:

> "Not an aeroplane, but either a wingless aerial boat, or a mere suit of overalls." [011-039]

This represented the transition from mechanical flight to integrated personal propulsion that would characterize later human species.
