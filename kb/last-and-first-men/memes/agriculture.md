# Agriculture

Agriculture — the cultivation of plants and animals for human sustenance — is the material foundation upon which every human civilization in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]* was built. Its transformation from scattered subsistence farming to the integrated industrial-agricultural systems of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], and its collapse back to primitive tillage, traces the arc of civilization itself.

## Definition

In the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian narrative, agriculture encompasses all forms of food and resource cultivation — from the First Men's mechanized farming to the [[patagonia|Patagonian]] civilization's return to wind and waterpower for agriculture. The story of agriculture is the story of humanity's relationship with the land that sustains it.

## Origin

### Early Agriculture

The First Men's earliest civilizations were built on agricultural surplus. The tension between settled agriculture and nomadic pastoralism shaped early cultural differences. By the era of advanced First Men civilization, agriculture had been transformed by mechanization and industrial organization.

### The First World State's Agricultural Integration

Under the [[first-world-state|First World State]], the distinction between agricultural and industrial land dissolved entirely. "Nor was there any longer a distinction between agricultural and industrial areas. Industry and agriculture interpenetrated everywhere" [004-022]. The landscape was organized into "columns of habitation and industry," while "the land was everywhere green or brown with the seasonal variations of agriculture, park, and wild reserve" [004-023].

## Details

### Crisis and De-mechanization

The collapse of the First World State disrupted food systems catastrophically. "Industry, agriculture, transport, were at a standstill" [004-008] during the worst periods. In the aftermath, a deliberate de-mechanization policy emerged: "Workers thus thrown out of employment were turned over to agricultural labour; for it was felt that the use of mechanical power in mere tillage must be as soon as possible abolished" [004-049]. This return to manual agriculture represented a civilizational retreat from technological complexity.

The desperation of the collapse period was extreme: "Huge mobs, demented and starving, staggered about the country devouring every kind of vegetable matter and wrangling over the flesh of their own dead" [004-008].

### Renewable Energy and Agriculture

During the [[patagonia|Patagonian]] renaissance, agriculture was powered by renewable sources: "Wind- and waterpower began to be used for the generation of electricity" [005-037]. On the [[patagonia|Patagonian]] volcanic soil, "timber, fruit, wild grains, and other nourishing plants throve on the well-watered volcanic soil" [006-034], demonstrating how agricultural flourishing depended on geographical happenstance.

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

Agriculture serves as a barometer of civilizational health. Its mechanization signals technological advance; its deliberate de-mechanization signals retreat. The book's treatment of agriculture reflects its broader concern with the sustainability of complex societies and humanity's dependence on the biosphere that sustains it.
