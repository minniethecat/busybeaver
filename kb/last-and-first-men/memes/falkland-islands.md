# Falkland Islands

A former island group in the South Atlantic Ocean that, through immense crustal warping, became part of a vast land bridge connecting South America with Antarctica.

## Location

### Type

Former islands / Geological feature

### Context

Located east of Patagonia in the South Atlantic Ocean. An immense warping of the Earth's crust to the east and south of Patagonia transformed what was once a relatively shallow region of the ocean into a vast new land connecting America with Antarctica by way of the former Falkland Islands and South Georgia [005-010].

## Physical Description

Originally a group of remote islands, the Falklands were consumed by the geological upheaval that reshaped the South Atlantic. The landmass that emerged stretched from South America east and northeast into the heart of the Atlantic [005-010].

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

The Falkland Islands are significant primarily for their role in the geological transformation that created the Patagonian-Antarctic land bridge, which was crucial for the development and spread of civilization during the recovery from the First Dark Age.

### Key Events

The warping of the Earth's crust connected Patagonia to Antarctica through the Falkland Islands and South Georgia [005-010]. The Patagonian civilization expanded from its origin east of Bahía Blanca, eventually reaching and encircling "the heights which were once the island of South Georgia" [005-015]. The political and cultural centre of the vastly organized community of autonomous provinces lay upon the new coast north of the former South Georgia island [005-016].

## Species & Civilizations

The Patagonian civilization expanded across the new land that incorporated the former Falkland Islands region. This civilization comprised a vast and highly organized community of autonomous provinces [005-016].

## Changes Over Time

The Falkland Islands were transformed from isolated South Atlantic islands into a continental land bridge through geological upheaval. Over millions of years, the region was further reshaped by the same forces that transformed all of Earth's geography.
