# Andes

The major mountain range of western South America, which served as the birthplace of an influential Patagonian religious leader and was later devastated by atomic catastrophe.

## Location

### Type

Mountain range

### Context

The Andes run along the western edge of South America. The Southern Andes, in particular, were home to shepherd communities during the Patagonian era [005-020]. The mountain range extended northward to connect with the Rockies, forming a continuous spine through both American continents [005-047].

## Physical Description

The Andes are a towering mountain chain that forms the western backbone of South America. Their high-altitude terrain supported pastoral communities. The range continued northward into the Rockies of North America.

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

The Andes were significant during the Patagonian era as a cultural and spiritual centre. They were also the path of catastrophic destruction during the atomic disaster that ended Patagonian civilization.

### Key Events

A notable Patagonian religious figure was born of shepherd parents among the Southern Andes. He first became famous as the leader of a romantic "youth movement" before developing his deeper spiritual philosophy [005-020]. His early teaching, captured in the statement "Grow more alive," evolved into a recognition of an aliveness "far intenser than youth's flicker" [005-026]. The Patagonian civilization met its end when an incandescent hurricane of atomic fury raged along the line of the Andes and the Rockies, scorching both continents with its heat [005-047].

## Species & Civilizations

The Patagonian civilization — the First Men's recovery culture — inhabited the Andean region. Shepherd communities in the Southern Andes produced a religious leader whose movement shaped Patagonian spirituality. The Patagonian sailing ships founded colonies in remote Africa and Australia, spreading Andean-influenced culture globally [005-016].

## Changes Over Time

The Andes witnessed the rise of Patagonian civilization from humble shepherd communities and its eventual destruction in atomic fire. Over geological time, the mountains were reshaped by the same tectonic forces that transformed all continents.
