# Life

The central mystery in "Last and First Men" — not merely biological existence but the evolving capacity of conscious beings to understand and direct their own nature across cosmic timescales.

## Overview

Life in Stapledon's future history is a developmental process spanning two billion years and eighteen human species. What begins as the "life of the senses and of impulse" [001-036] among the First Men becomes, through successive evolutionary transformations, a cosmic force capable of deliberate self-direction. The question "what is life for?" haunts every species, and each answers differently.

## Life as Biological Process

For the First Men, life was fundamentally constrained by planetary resources. "The economic life of the human race had for some time been based on coal, but latterly oil had been found a far more convenient source of power" [001-042]. Material conditions shaped what forms of life were possible. The First Men recognized that their vital processes — "the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable" [001-004] of sustaining their highest ideals — limited their spiritual aspirations.

## The Transformation of Daily Life

Across the eras, the character of daily existence transformed. "And from the townsfolk something of the new way of life was spreading even to the peasants" [001-035], marking the slow diffusion of new patterns of living. Cultural life waxed and waned: "As for France, her cultural life was now grievously reduced. What was once a vital and promising culture became insincere" [001-039], showing how the life of a civilization could wither while biological life continued.

Later human species achieved forms of vitality undreamed of by the First Men. The Second Men cultivated "a degree of vitality" far beyond their predecessors. The Great Brains reduced their biological life to a minimum in pursuit of pure intellectual existence. The Last Men achieved a serene integration of body, mind, and spirit that made the First Men's way of life seem, in retrospect, barely conscious.

## Life as Cosmic Narrative

Stapledon's overarching theme traces life from its humble origins to its eventual cosmic role. Life is not static — it grows in complexity, consciousness, and self-awareness across deep time. Each human species represents an "experiment in living," and the succession of species constitutes life's long education in what it means to be.
