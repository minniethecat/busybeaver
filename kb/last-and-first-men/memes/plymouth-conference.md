# Plymouth Conference

An international scientific meeting held at Plymouth, England, during the First Men era, notable as a site of transnational scientific exchange that transcended the nationalist rivalries of the period.

## Overview

The Plymouth Conference represents one instance of the international scientific cooperation that persisted even during the First Men's era of intense national competition. "It so happened that an international society of scientific workers was meeting in England at Plymouth, and a young Chinese physicist had expressed his desire to make a report to a select committee" [002-010]. This crossing of national and civilizational boundaries — a Chinese scientist presenting to an international audience in England — exemplified science's potential to transcend politics.

## Context

The conference occurred during a period when "the long rivalry between France and England" [001-005] and broader East-West tensions characterized global politics. That an international scientific gathering could convene in England, with a Chinese participant, demonstrated that the republic of science maintained channels of communication even when diplomatic relations were strained.

## Later Scientific-Religious Conferences

The Plymouth Conference prefigured later, more ambitious attempts to unite knowledge communities. "About two centuries after the formation of the first World State, the President of the World declared that the time was ripe for a formal union of science and religion, and called a conference of the leaders of these two great disciplines" [004-017]. This later conference attempted what Plymouth had achieved in miniature — the bridging of different ways of knowing across cultural boundaries.

## Significance

While a minor event in the vast sweep of "Last and First Men," the Plymouth Conference illustrates an enduring theme: science as a transnational enterprise capable of connecting minds across the barriers of nation, race, and ideology. The unnamed "young Chinese physicist" embodies the promise of knowledge unbounded by political geography.
