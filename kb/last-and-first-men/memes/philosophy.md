# Philosophy

Philosophy in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]* is the evolving pursuit of wisdom that transforms across two billion years — from the First Men's tentative inquiries to the [[last-men|Last Men]]'s comprehensive cosmic understanding. Each human species brought a distinct philosophical temperament, and the history of human philosophy is, in [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]'s vision, the history of humanity's deepening relationship with the mystery of existence.

## Definition

Philosophy, as it appears across the eighteen human species, encompasses the systematic inquiry into the nature of reality, mind, value, and cosmic purpose. The [[sixteenth-men|Sixteenth Men]], living in what was "essentially a philosophical age, in fact the supreme age of philosophy" [016-001], represent the apex of philosophical development. Yet even they acknowledged that at their deepest they "found only the shifting sands of mystery" [014-025].

## Origin

### Developed By

The [[first-men|First Men]] approached philosophy with "austere" Germanic rigor but remained at heart romantic [001-032]. The [[second-men|Second Men]], by contrast, possessed "a natural propensity" for science, mathematics, and philosophy "no less vigorous than the primitive instincts" [007-006]. They surpassed the First Men's achievements before discovering the Siberian stone library, which they valued not as truth but as "a vivid historical document" [007-036].

The [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]] practiced philosophy through [[telepathy|telepathic]] communion: "it was by 'telepathic' intercourse in respect of art, science, philosophy, and the appreciation of personalities, that the public mind... of the Fifth Men had being" [011-044]. The [[eighth-men|Eighth Men]], blessed with longevity, achieved "philosophic detachment" [012-006].

### Decline and Recovery

The [[third-men|Third Men]] witnessed philosophy's decline — "philosophy, as a rigorous technical discipline, no longer existed" [004-013]. The [[ninth-men|Ninth Men]], though highly intelligent, were "wholly lacking in philosophical interest" [008-034]. The [[tenth-men|Tenth Men]], capable of mystical intuition, "never seriously disciplined themselves under philosophy" [010-007].

## Details

### Variants Across Species

Philosophy took radically different forms across species. The [[second-men|Second Men]]'s natural philosophy was "unintelligible to readers of this book" [007-035]. The [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]]'s philosophy acknowledged that "the universe was one in which there could be not only private but also great public tragedies" [009-010]. The [[last-men|Last Men]] achieved group-mode philosophical insight where "most... of the perennial philosophical puzzles, especially those connected with the nature of personality, can be so lucidly restated that they cease to be puzzles" [015-033].

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

The arc of philosophy mirrors the book's central theme — the tension between intellectual ascent and ultimate limitation. The [[sixteenth-men|Sixteenth Men]], supreme philosophers, recognized that "the racial purpose, which had been so much confused by philosophical ignorance, might at last become clear" [014-027]. The [[last-men|Last Men]] attained "philosophical insight into the true nature of space and time, mind and its objects, cosmical striving and cosmical perfection" [015-036], yet even this vision was extinguished with the solar nova.
