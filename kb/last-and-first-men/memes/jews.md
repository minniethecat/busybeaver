# Jews

The Jews are one of the few distinct racial groups singled out for sustained attention in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]* — a people who, uniquely among the First Men, maintained a "furtive respect for pure intelligence" through centuries of anti-intellectual decline, and who became indispensable to the [[first-world-state|First World State]].

## Definition

In the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian narrative, the Jews represent the paradoxical survival of intellectual values in an age that had turned against intelligence. Treated with "a similar combination of honour and contempt" as other distinct peoples [004-037], they were simultaneously indispensable and marginalized — respected for their abilities and despised for their difference.

## Origin

### Preservation of Intelligence

While the rest of First Men civilization experienced a progressive deterioration of intelligence after the era of [[war|wars]] and resource crises, "the Jews had made themselves invaluable in the financial organization of the world state, having far outstripped the other races because they alone had preserved a furtive respect for pure intelligence" [004-037]. Long after "intelligence had come to be regarded as disreputable in ordinary men and women, it was expected of the Jews" [004-037]. In time they "had made something like 'a corner' in intelligence" [004-037].

### Role in Crisis

During the collapse of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], Jews played crucial organizational roles. "Confusion was widespread, save here and there where serious organization was attempted by certain Jews" [004-049]. When the truth about the failure of physics to deliver divine power could no longer be concealed, "the hideous truth, which few hitherto had dared to admit even to themselves, was blurted out upon the ether by a prominent Jew: the whole hoary legend of the divine secret was a lie, else why were the physicists temporizing?" [004-057].

## Details

### Cultural Predecessors

The Jewish tradition traced its deepest roots to "Hebrew worship of divine law" [001-005], which represented, alongside Greek tragic art and Indian resignation, one of humanity's earliest intimations of "an alien and supernal beauty, which was to exalt and perplex him again and again throughout his whole career" [001-005]. The ancient Hebrew conception of God even influenced later species — the [[mars-place|Martian]] super-individual's disposition was "something between that of a field-marshal and the God of the ancient Hebrews" [008-042].

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

The Jews serve as a case study in how civilization treats its intellectual classes. Their preservation of intelligence as a value, their indispensability to the world system, and their marginalization all reflect the book's broader themes about the tension between intellectual capacity and social acceptance — a tension that plays out across all eighteen human species.
