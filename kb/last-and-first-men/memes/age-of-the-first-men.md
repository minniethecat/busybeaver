# Age of the First Men

The dawn era of humanity, encompassing the entire arc of [[First Men]] civilization from primitive origins through world unification to catastrophic collapse. Spanning from the earliest tool-making hominids on [[Earth]] through the rise of the [[First World State]] and its eventual downfall, this era represents the first chapter in the two-billion-year history of the human spirit chronicled in [[olaf-stapledon|Olaf Stapledon]]'s "Last and [[first-men|First Men]]." From the remote perspective of the [[Last Men]] on [[Neptune]], the entire struggle of this first species "from savagery towards civilization" appears as but a single fleeting moment of precocious awakening [001-002].

> Long before the human spirit awoke to clear cognizance of the world and itself, it sometimes stirred in its sleep, opened bewildered eyes, and slept again. One of these moments of precocious experience embraces the whole struggle of the First Men from savagery towards civilization. [001-002]

## Time Span

The Age of the First Men stretches across an immense duration. The biological species [[First Men]] ([[first-men|Homo sapiens]]) emerged approximately three hundred thousand years before the present day, but recognizably human civilization arose only in the last five millennia. The era reached its apex in the centuries leading to the founding of the [[First World State]] — achieved "some three hundred and eighty terrestrial years after the [[European War]]" — then persisted through some four thousand years of material achievement before its terminal collapse [004-001].

In the broader [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian timeline, this era directly precedes the [[First Dark Age]] — a hundred-thousand-year period of savagery and barbarism that separated the [[first-men|First Men]]'s civilization from the [[Patagonian Civilization]] and any subsequent flowering [005-001]. The species itself survived in scattered remnants long after the fall of the World State, enduring in Siberian exile across the ten-million-year [[second-dark-age|Second Dark Age]] before finally giving way to the [[Second Men]], a physically nobler and more integrated successor species [006-019].

As the foundation era of the entire human epic, the Age of the First Men established the patterns — tribal [[war|conflict]], spiritual aspiration, intellectual precocity matched by moral immaturity — that would echo through all eighteen subsequent human species.

## Environmental Conditions

Throughout the Age of the First Men, [[Earth]] provided a relatively stable planetary stage. The continents assumed their familiar modern configuration, with temperate zones supporting dense populations across [[Europe]], [[Asia]], and the [[Americas]]. The climate was broadly benign, though punctuated by ice ages during the deep prehistoric phase.

The gradual depletion of natural resources shaped the era's trajectory. Coal accumulated from prehistoric vegetation fueled the initial industrial revolution, but extraction eventually exhausted all accessible deposits. The exhaustion of oil occurred within three centuries of global unification, forcing a shift to electricity generated from coal. Wind power, water power, and volcanic energy were extensively developed but never sufficed to replace fossil fuels at the scale the civilization demanded.

This planetary context created the material conditions within which the drama of the [[First Men]] unfolded — abundant enough to enable civilization's birth, finite enough to enforce its eventual reckoning. The physical geography of the era included the fertile plains of [[Patagonia]], which would later serve as the cradle of humanity's first partial recovery during the [[First Dark Age]] [005-012].

## Rise of Civilization

The ascent of the [[First Men]] was a precocious, unsteady climb from animal existence toward self-conscious community. Two foundational ideals emerged early in the historical record: the commitment of [[Socrates]] to dispassionate intelligence, and the dedication of [[Jesus]] to selfless love of neighbour. These twin stars illuminated the species' highest possibilities but also revealed its inherent limitations as a creature still only "superficially human" [001-008].

> Unfortunately both these ideals demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable. For many centuries these twin stars enticed the more precociously human of human animals, in vain. And the failure to put these ideals in practice helped to engender in the race a cynical lassitude which was one cause of its decay. [001-004]

The rise was punctuated by devastating wars that displayed what the narrator calls "the incompetence of the [[first-men|First Men]] to control their own nature" [001-007]. The [[European War]] — called at the time the [[european-war|War to End War]] — was the first and least destructive of these world [[war|conflict]]s. It was followed by the [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]], triggered by a misunderstanding over a distressed aircraft and a sacrificial princess, which wrecked both England and [[France]] and "may be said to have sealed the fate of the [[first-men|First Men]]" [001-009]. The [[russo-german-war|Russo-German War]] completed the European cycle, scarring the continent with poison gas that rendered vast territories uninhabitable for years.

Yet from this crucible of [[war|conflict]] emerged an unprecedented will for unity. The formation of the European Confederacy, driven by "a devastating distrust of human nature" and the dread of recurring patriotic madness, eventually gave way to the [[First World State]] — a planetary civilization administered by a [[World Financial Directorate]] and animated by the worship of [[Gordelpus]], the Prime Mover. [[science-concept|Science]] held "a position of unique honour among the [[first-men|First Men]]" during this period, not for its contribution to understanding, but because "the application of scientific principles had revolutionized their material circumstances" [004-010]. The establishment of the [[Sacred Order of Scientists]] as a priestly caste fused religious authority with technological power, a union that would dominate human affairs until the civilization's end.

The founding of the World State was sealed by a remarkable symbolic act: the [[america|America]]n [[president-of-the-world|President of the World]] publicly revealed his union with the "Daughter of Man," a woman whose blended racial heritage — "her face, blended of Asia and the West" — embodied the hoped-for unity of all peoples. This fusion of celebrity spectacle with genuine cosmopolitan aspiration would prove characteristic of the era's peculiar genius and its fatal shallowness [004-001].

## Peak and Decline

At its height, the [[First World State]] achieved a material civilization of unprecedented scale, lasting some four thousand years. All continents on [[Earth]] were minutely artificialized, with residential pylons rising three miles into the sky in a forest of cruciform towers. Clouds often encircled the middle heights, and dwellers in the summits required artificial air pressure and oxygen supply. Preventive medicine eliminated cancer, tuberculosis, and nervous disorders. The working day shrank to four or six hours. Every adult possessed a private aeroplane and the freedom to wander the entire planet — a privilege that was simultaneously practical transport and religious obligation.

Yet beneath this material prosperity, a profound spiritual and intellectual decline had set in. The fanatical worship of movement and activity — embodied in the ritual practices of [[Flying]], dancing, and athletics — consumed the civilization's resources while the capacity for genuine thought withered. Intelligence came to be regarded as disreputable; reasoning was permitted only within the narrow sphere of official work. The [[Sacred Order of Scientists]], custodians of the mythical secret of divine power (the "mystery of [[gordelpus|Gordelpus]]"), maintained their authority through a mixture of genuine technical knowledge, institutionalized fraud, and the suppression of independent inquiry.

The treatment of minorities reflected the civilization's deepening contradictions. The [[Jews]], having preserved a furtive respect for intelligence when it had become disreputable in others, controlled much of the financial apparatus. They were regarded with universal suspicion as "embodiments of the powers of evil, harnessed in the service of [[gordelpus|Gordelpus]]," yet they had made themselves indispensable to the financial organization of the world state [004-037]. Meanwhile, a profound species-wide senescence — "a subtle physiological change, which it is tempting to call 'general senescence of the species'" — meant that the unique human gift of prolonged youth, a form of biological [[Longevity]], was gradually lost [005-004]. The [[First Men]] were, in fact, "now a race who had outlived their youth" [005-013].

The collapse arrived when Antarctic coal supplies failed, exposing the civilization's absolute dependence on finite resources and the hollowness of its governing myths. The ruling scientists declared the crisis a divine test and demanded that ritual [[Flying]] be increased rather than reduced. An epidemic of insanity, spread by microbes released during the ensuing civil wars, devastated populations across all continents. The [[American Madness]], once used as a biological weapon against [[China]], now ravaged its creators.

> Like those little rodents whose migration became barred by an encroachment of the sea, so that annually they drowned themselves in thousands, the First Men helplessly continued in their ritualistic behaviour; but unlike the lemmings, they were human enough to be at the same time oppressed by unbelief, an unbelief which, moreover, they dared not recognize. [004-054]

Among the causes of the completeness of the collapse was the fact that after four thousand years of routine, "human nature had been deprived of all its suppleness." The generations "slid rapidly through dusk into night," living "in the conviction of universal decay, and under the legend of a mighty past" [005-001]. Only in [[Patagonia]], where a new civilization would eventually arise, did the human spirit begin to stir once more [005-012].

## Legacy

The Age of the First Men occupies a unique position in the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian timeline as the only era that includes the reader's own present moment, making the entire subsequent narrative a direct commentary on early twentieth-century civilization. The era established enduring patterns that recurred throughout human evolution: the tension between tribal loyalty and cosmopolitan vision, the struggle to reconcile intelligence with instinct, and the perennial risk that material achievement outpaces spiritual growth. These themes would be replayed across [[Earth]], [[Venus]], and [[Neptune]] by all eighteen successor species.

In the judgment of the [[Last Men]], who survey this era from the remote vantage of [[Neptune]] two billion years hence, the [[first-men|First Men]] were "precocious" — creatures who glimpsed truths they could not sustain, built structures they could not preserve, and conceived ideals they could not embody. The entire subsequent history of the eighteen human species — from the [[Second Men]] through the [[Fifth Men]], the [[Great Brains]], and ultimately the [[last-men|Last Men]] themselves — is the story of successive attempts to realize what the [[first-men|First Men]] could only dimly imagine.

Yet the [[last-men|Last Men]]'s judgment is not wholly dismissive. The [[First Men]] invented science. They invented philosophy. They conceived the very idea of a universal human community that transcended tribe and nation — an ideal that the [[Group Mind]] of later species would finally realize. They recorded their knowledge in stone for successor species to discover. They provided the biological and cultural seed from which everything that followed would grow.

What distinguishes the Age of the First Men from all subsequent eras is its foundational character: it was the epoch in which mind first awoke to itself, however imperfectly, and recognized that the cosmos might be understood. The "dispassionate cognizance" that [[France]] and England had pioneered — the willingness to see the universe as it is rather than as the heart desired — was the single most precious thread of [[first-men|First Men]] culture, and its loss in the wars of the twentieth century was, from the [[Last Men]]'s perspective, the era's deepest tragedy [001-032]. Without the [[First Men]]'s flawed but heroic struggle toward self-awareness, the two-billion-year epic of humanity in the Solar System could never have begun.
