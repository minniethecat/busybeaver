# Arctic

Earth's northern polar region, which was transformed by climate engineering into habitable territory during the World State era but later became nearly deserted.

## Location

### Type

Polar region

### Context

The Arctic encompasses the northernmost part of Earth. The arctic islands of Canada, specifically mentioned in the records, were among the habitable polar territories during the World State era [004-024].

## Physical Description

The Arctic is characterized by extreme seasonal light variation and originally cold climate. During the World State era, the arctic islands of Canada were "ingeniously warmed by directed tropical currents" and became the homes of "vigorous northerners" [004-024]. The region, like Antarctica in the south, benefited from the same climate engineering technologies that thawed the polar regions.

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

The Arctic was significant during the World State era as a demonstration of climate engineering capability, and during later eras when its population dwindled.

### Key Events

The arctic islands were made habitable through directed tropical currents, becoming home to a vigorous northern population [004-024]. Save in the arctic, the people of this era were very rarely permitted to approach one another within a distance less than their height — the arctic people maintained unusual customs of physical proximity [004-022]. Far later, within a thousand years of some later catastrophe, "the once-populous arctic and antarctic islands were almost deserted" [013-006].

## Species & Civilizations

The First Men inhabited the Arctic during the World State era. The arctic population was noted for distinctive social customs — they were the exception to the general rule against close physical proximity [004-022]. Later human species experienced the depopulation of the arctic islands.

## Changes Over Time

The Arctic was transformed from uninhabitable ice to warm, populated islands through World State climate engineering, then declined to near-desertion in later eras. The cycle of habitation and abandonment reflects the broader pattern of human civilization's rise and fall across Earth's regions.
