London was the capital of England and one of the great metropolitan centers of [[first-men|First Men]] civilization in the early twentieth century. Its destruction during the [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]] marked one of the most dramatic episodes of the First Men's self-destructive tendencies.

## Location

London was situated in southeastern England, within easy striking distance of the French coast — a vulnerability that [[france|France]] exploited when persuading the [[league-of-nations|League of Nations]] to restrict military aircraft sizes, ensuring "London lay within easy striking distance of the French coast" [001-015].

## Historical Significance

### Key Events

London was the capital struck during the [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]] crisis. The war was sparked when a stray French aircraft, on a peace overture, crashed in London and "in an hour the mood of London was changed, and the whole population of England succumbed to a paroxysm of primitive hate far more extravagant than any that had occurred even in the war against Germany" [001-023].

A fabricated sexual scandal — an Englishwoman who "craving the embrace of a 'cave man,' had seduced a Senegalese corporal" [001-015] — escalated tensions. The war destroyed London and obliterated Paris within days.

### Final Devastation

London was destroyed a second time during the [[war|War]] between America and China. An American air-base established in Ireland led to England being "repeatedly devastated." Airmen passing over the ruins "could still make out the lines of Oxford Street and the Strand among the green and grey tangle of ruins" [003-024].

## Species & Civilizations

London was a city of the [[first-men|First Men]], representing the commercial and imperial power of England at the height of the industrial age. It was also the original publishing city of Olaf Stapledon's narrative — "First published: 1930 (Methuen, London)."
