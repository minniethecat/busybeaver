# Earth

Earth is the birthplace of humanity and the setting for the earliest and most formative chapters of human history. From the rise of the [[first-men|First Men]] to the Martian invasions, Earth was the stage upon which the first acts of the human drama unfolded.

## Geography & Environment

Earth's diverse geography — its continents, oceans, and climatic zones — shaped the development of early human civilizations. The planet's axial tilt and orbital characteristics produced the seasonal rhythms that governed agricultural societies. Its fossil fuel deposits, particularly the vast coal reserves of Antarctica, powered the [[first-world-state|First World State]]'s global civilization [004-025].

> The First Men discovered in the Antarctic continent a vast coalfield, which served them for many thousands of years. [004-025]

The planet's environment proved both nurturing and challenging. It provided the resources for industrial civilization but also subjected humanity to ice ages, sea-level changes, and resource depletion.

## Historical Significance

Earth was the home of the first four human species — First through [[great-brains|Fourth Men]] — and witnessed the rise and fall of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], the [[patagonian-civilization|Patagonian Civilization]], and the [[great-brains|Great Brains]]' scientific civilization.

> For more than ten million years the human race maintained itself on the Earth, in spite of all the hazards of nature and of human folly. [005-001]

The planet experienced two catastrophic Dark Ages. The First Dark Age followed the exhaustion of global resources. The Second Dark Age was far more devastating, triggered by the failure of the World State's energy system and lasting ten million years. Earth was also the target of the Martian invasion — a desperate migration from a dying world that brought interplanetary war to Earth's surface [009-004].

> The Martians, inhabitants of a dying planet, cast envious eyes upon the Earth. [009-004]

## Inhabitants

Earth was home to the [[first-men|First Men]] throughout their entire evolutionary trajectory, from primitive savagery to global civilization [001-002]. The [[second-men|Second Men]] arose in [[patagonia|Patagonia]] and spread across the recovering planet.

> In the far south, in Patagonia, a new and hardier type of humanity was gradually taking shape. [007-001]

The [[third-men|Third Men]] and the [[great-brains|Great Brains]] ([[great-brains|Fourth Men]]) continued the terrestrial human lineage before the center of human civilization shifted to [[venus-place|Venus]] and the outer solar system.

## Later History

After the Martian invasions and the eventual decline of terrestrial civilization, Earth faded from centrality in human affairs. The planet that gave birth to humanity became a backwater — remembered with a mixture of reverence and detachment by later species looking back across two billion years.

> The Earth, once the whole theatre of human existence, became a remote and almost legendary place, the cradle from which the spirit of man had spread across the solar system. [015-001]

The [[last-men|Last Men]], looking back from [[neptune-place|Neptune]], regarded Earth as the sacred origin point from which consciousness had spread to the stars.
