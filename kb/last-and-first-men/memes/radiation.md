# Radiation

Radiation in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]* appears in two transformative forms: as subatomic energy that reshaped human civilization, and as the biological radiant communication that enabled [[mars-place|Martian]] collective consciousness. The concept spans the technological and the biological, the destructive and the unifying.

## Definition

Radiation encompasses both the physical phenomenon of energy emission — harnessed by the [[first-men|First Men]] as subatomic power and later weaponized — and the biological capacity of Martian organisms to emit and receive radiant signals, forming the basis of their unique group consciousness. In both contexts, radiation represents a force that transcends individual boundaries.

## Origin

### Subatomic Energy

The utilization of subatomic energy was a revolutionary breakthrough of the First Men's era. A scientist "had been experimenting to find means for the utilization of subatomic energy by the annihilation of matter" [002-010], leading to a dramatic demonstration on the Devon coast. "The utilization of subatomic energy had indeed been achieved" [002-014], but the destructive potential was immediately apparent — the demonstration obliterated Lundy Island, leaving only "open sea extended where had been Lundy" [002-020].

The knowledge was judged too dangerous for humanity. The discoverer was urged to "destroy your handiwork, and, if it were possible, your memory of your marvellous research" [002-021]. Subatomic physics later grew into a vast but fragmented field where "students of one branch of science knew practically nothing of the work of others" [004-013].

### Destructive Application

The weaponization of atomic energy reached its apogee in the Patagonian catastrophe, where "an incandescent hurricane spread over the whole of Patagonia, reinforcing itself with fresh atomic fury wherever it went" [005-047]. This event demonstrated that radiation, once unleashed, could destroy on a continental scale.

## Details

### Martian Radiant Biology

On [[mars-place|Mars]], radiation served an entirely different function. The Martian cloud-units evolved "the hitherto useless and rudimentary power of emitting radiation" into a specialized faculty, so that "free individuals came to maintain radiant contact with one another, and to behave with ever-increasing coordination" [008-020]. Individual subvital units were specialized for specific functions: "some for emitting radiation, some for absorbing and storing water, some for special sensitivities" [008-022].

The Martians achieved "almost perfect unity of consciousness" within "a huge field of radiation" [008-018] during public enterprises. This radiant coordination was "a coordination impossible even to the most gregarious of animals" [008-036]. However, their dependence on radiation also created vulnerability — a Martian cancer-plague arose in which "the subvital units lost their sensitivity to radiation, so that they proceeded to live as primitive organisms and reproduce without restraint" [008-030].

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

Radiation embodies the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian duality of knowledge: the same physical principle that could power civilization could also annihilate it, and the same radiant communication that unified Martian consciousness could fail catastrophically. This tension between creation and destruction, unity and dissolution, runs throughout the book's treatment of advanced knowledge.
