# Third Men Civilization

The **Third Men Civilization** emerged after a thirty-million-year racial trance of the [[second-men|Second Men]], triggered by geological isolation on an island continent in what was once the North Atlantic [010-003]. The [[third-men|Third Men]] were a smaller, shorter-lived species, seldom living past sixty, with golden eyes, six-fingered hands, and expressive bat-like ears [010-004].

## Overview

Unlike their predecessors, the Third Men were practical rather than theoretical, interested more in living things than abstract reason [010-008]. Their civilization was not a unified political order but a scattered network of tribes and pastoral communities, each taking pride in the unique qualities of their local animals and plants.

## Economy and Society

The Third Men excelled as hunters, herdsmen, and domesticators. They tamed the great leonine wolf and trained it for hunting games, developing relationships with animals that combined economic cooperation with religious symbolism and even sexual intimacy [010-008]. Selective breeding was practiced obsessively — every chieftain boasted not only of his tribe's superiority but of his territory's bears being the noblest of all bears [010-009].

## Religion

The civilization was dominated by worship of Life as an all-pervading spirit. The deity was envisioned as a composite animal, with human hands, eyes, and sexual organs holding the world between divine fingers [010-012]. This religion was complicated by sadism: pain was regarded as a superior mode of experience, and the worship of the life-force was complemented by worship of the environment as that which thwarts and tortures life into nobility [010-014].

## Decline

The Third Men were ultimately supplanted when the [[great-brains|Great Brains]] — the Fourth Men — engineered a successor species and built a new civilization on the labor of domesticated Third Men populations [011-037].

## Legacy

The Third Men's deep intuitive sympathy with living things remained unique among all human species. Their biocentric worldview anticipated later evolutionary philosophy, though they lacked the intellectual rigor to systematize it.
