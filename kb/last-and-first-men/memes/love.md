# Love

The emotional and spiritual bond between beings, which takes radically different forms across the eighteen human species in "Last and First Men," evolving from the turbulent passions of the First Men to the serene group-consciousness of later species.

## Overview

Love in Stapledon's future history is not static but evolutionary. Each human species experiences and expresses love differently — shaped by their biology, their social organization, and their level of spiritual development. The First Men loved with intensity and contradiction; later species achieved forms of love incomprehensible to their predecessors.

## Love Among the First Men

The First Men's love was passionate, contradictory, and frequently destructive. "The delight of soul in soul, he said, was the great rejuvenator; it recreated both lover and beloved" [005-010]. Yet love could also blind: "For love of France was the undoing of the French" [001-004], illustrating how patriotic love could become pathological. Jesus, in the First Men's religious tradition, "stood for unselfish love of neighbours and of God" [001-003] — an ideal rarely achieved in practice.

The First Men's capacity for love was limited by their biology: "both these ideals demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable" [001-026]. Their love was always in tension with their impulses toward hatred and self-destruction.

## Love as Political Instrument

In the First Men's global politics, love was deliberately cultivated as a tool of social control. The cult of the Daughter of Man engineered mass adoration: "Every man on the planet became in imagination her lover" [004-005]. The World President's "love" for humanity was "conceived as a stupendous efficiency, his love as munificence towards his employees" [003-010] — love reduced to administration.

## Love in Later Species

For the Second Men and beyond, love became less individually possessive and more communal. The tension between individual erotic love and the broader love of the community — already visible in the First Men — was gradually resolved in favour of the collective. "He checked them with a young laugh that made them love while they hated" [005-010] captures the paradoxical quality that love retained even as it evolved.
