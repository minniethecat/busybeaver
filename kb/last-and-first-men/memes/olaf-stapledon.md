# Olaf Stapledon

Olaf Stapledon is the historical author of *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]*, acknowledged within the narrative as the "primitive being" whose brain serves as the conduit for the far-future narrator's account.

> This book has two authors, one contemporary with its readers, the other an inhabitant of an age which they would call the distant future. The brain that conceives and writes these sentences lives in the time of Einstein. Yet I, the true inspirer of this book, I who have begotten it upon that brain, I who influence that primitive being's conception, am a being of an age that would seem to you the very far future.

## Identity

### Species

Stapledon was a member of the [[first-men|First Men]], the original human species — referred to by the far-future narrator as a "primitive being" whose mind could only imperfectly grasp the visions being transmitted to it.

### Era

He lived in early twentieth-century Britain, in what the far-future narrator calls "the time of [[einstein|Einstein]]" — a period that, from the perspective of two billion years of future history, appears as the earliest dawn of human self-consciousness.

## Role in the Narrative

### The Two Authors

The book presents itself as having two authors: one "contemporary with its readers" — Stapledon himself — and another, the "true inspirer," who inhabits an age in the very remote future. The far-future author describes influencing Stapledon's "primitive being's conception" to produce the book [P03-001].

> The actual writer thinks he is merely contriving a work of fiction. Though he seeks to tell a plausible story, he neither believes it himself, nor expects others to believe it. Yet the story is true. A being whom you would call a future man has seized the docile but scarcely adequate brain of your contemporary, and is trying to direct its familiar processes for an alien purpose.

### Narrative Scope

The far-future narrator uses Stapledon's brain to convey an immense historical sweep that no single human lifetime could encompass.

> Historians living in your day need grapple only with one moment of the flux of time. But I have to present in one book the essence not of centuries but of aeons. Clearly we cannot walk at leisure through such a tract, in which a million terrestrial years are but as a year is to your historians. We must fly.

### In-Text Reference

Stapledon is directly referenced within the text as an external authority on certain historical details [017-004].

## Philosophical Framework

### Against Utopia

The narrative explicitly rejects the utopian conventions of early science fiction.

> When your writers romance of the future, they too easily imagine a progress toward some kind of Utopia, in which beings like themselves live in unmitigated bliss among circumstances perfectly suited to a fixed human nature. I shall not describe any such paradise. Instead, I shall record huge fluctuations of joy and woe.

This anti-utopian stance — the insistence that the future would bring not paradise but endless struggle and transformation — is one of the book's most distinctive and influential philosophical commitments.

### Beyond Human Understanding

The far-future narrator repeatedly emphasizes the limitations of the First Men brain serving as the conduit.

> You underestimate even the foothills that stand in front of you, and never suspect that far above them, hidden by cloud, rise precipices and snowfields. The mental and spiritual advances which, in your day, mind in the solar system has still to attempt, are overwhelmingly more complex, more precarious and dangerous, than those which I must record.

## Legacy

Stapledon, through this extraordinary narrative device, created a work that transcends its historical moment. As the "primitive" co-author of a history spanning two billion years and eighteen human species, he occupies a unique position in science fiction — simultaneously the earliest and the latest voice in human storytelling, the Alpha and Omega of the human narrative.
