# War

War is the most persistent force shaping the two-billion-year saga of humanity in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]*.

From the tribal skirmishes of early [[First Men]] through the interplanetary [[Martians|Martian]] invasions of the [[Second Men]] era to the final existential struggles of the [[Last Men]], organized conflict recurs as both a symptom of human limitation and a catalyst for evolutionary transformation.

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] presents war as an intrinsic expression of a creature "fashioned for hunting and fighting in the wild" yet "suddenly called upon to be citizens of a world-community" [001-006].

Across eighteen [[First Men|human species]], war drives the narrative arc from [[First Dark Age|darkness]] to [[Last Men Commonwealth|civilization]] and back again, functioning as the engine of both tragedy and transcendence.

## Definition

In the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian cosmos, war encompasses all forms of organized collective violence: national wars between [[First Men]] states, racial conflicts across continents, inter-species [[Martians|warfare]] spanning planets, and the spiritual-ideological struggles that divide [[First World State|civilizations]].

It functions on three levels simultaneously: as a biological inheritance from humanity's predatory past, as a failure of political imagination within the [[First World State|world order]], and as an instrument of cosmic tragedy.

"Fate, directing events toward an allotted end," uses human violence to shape evolutionary outcomes [005-003].

War is distinguished from mere [[Group Mind|group]] aggression by its systematic character — it is the dark twin of [[First World State|civilization]], emerging whenever political organization outstrips moral development.

The [[Last Men]], looking back across two billion years, understood war as the definitive expression of the gap between human power and human wisdom.

## The Wars of the First Men

The [[First Men]] — the earliest human species, contemporary with our own present — were defined by their inability to control their own violent nature.

The opening chapters trace an accelerating cascade of conflicts, each more devastating than the last, culminating in the [[First Dark Age]] that destroyed [[First World State|global civilization]].

### The European War

The [[european-war|European War]], "called at the time the [[european-war|War to End War]], was the first and least destructive of those world conflicts which display so tragically the incompetence of the [[first-men|First Men]] to control their own nature" [001-007].

Despite a genuine and passionate will for peace that emerged during the struggle, the settlement failed outright.

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] captures the tragedy with precision: "The peace which followed the [[european-war|European War]] is one of the most significant moments of ancient history; for it epitomizes both the dawning vision and the incurable blindness, both the impulse toward a higher loyalty and the compulsive tribalism of a race which was, after all, but superficially human" [001-008].

The war introduced [[Flying|aerial warfare]] as a decisive military dimension that would shape all subsequent conflicts among the [[First Men]].

### The Anglo-French War

The [[anglo-french-war|Anglo-French War]] demonstrated how trivial causes could produce catastrophic effects at civilizational scale.

Sparked by a fabricated sexual scandal — an Englishwoman who "craving the embrace of a 'cave man,' had seduced a Senegalese corporal" [001-015] — the war destroyed London and obliterated Paris within days.

The deeper cause was a dispute over [[Flying|air power]], but the immediate trigger was "the public's sexuality, tribalism, and self-righteousness" exploited by the press [001-015].

In what [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] calls "perhaps the most dramatic example of petty cause and mighty effect," a navigational error by a French airman — crashing in London after peace had been declared — reignited the conflagration and sealed the fate of the [[First Men]] species [001-027].

### The Russo-German War and Euro-American War

The [[russo-german-war|Russo-German War]] was ignited by a "typically German book" claiming the cosmos was a dualism in which "a heroic and obviously Nordic spirit ruled by divine right over an un-self-disciplined, yet servile and obviously Slavonic spirit" [001-048].

This ideological pretext masked economic tensions over [[america|America]]n financial domination. The war deployed biological weapons that left a generation sickly and permanently lowered European intelligence — a critical step toward the [[First Dark Age]] [001-052].

It was followed by the Euro-[[america|America]]n War, sparked by "a murder in South Italy" and escalating through lynchings, massacres, and aerial bombardment into full-scale conflict before either side had formally declared war [002-009].

## The Cost

> The goal of world unity was at last achieved — not, however, before the mind of the race had been seriously crippled. [004-001]

By the time the [[First World State]] consolidated global peace under the [[First Men]], the mental vitality of the species had already begun its irreversible decline.

The subsequent [[First Dark Age]] saw humanity reduced to "endless brawls and feuds" between valleys, "small military tyrants," and a population of "yokels" for whom [[First World State|civilization]] was merely a "legend" [005-005].

The cycle of [[First Men]] warfare had consumed the very intelligence needed to sustain peace.

## Inter-Species Conflict

War transcends the [[First Men]]. Each successive human species — eighteen in total — developed its own relationship to organized violence, reflecting their distinct biological and psychological natures.

The [[Second Men]], more spiritually evolved and innately cosmopolitan, still knew war. But "wars were so hampered by impulses of kindliness towards the enemy that they were apt to degenerate into rather violent athletic contests, leading to an orgy of fraternization" [007-004].

Their primary loyalty was to the species as a whole, making sustained conflict psychologically difficult. The [[Second Men]] represented humanity's first genuine departure from the [[First Men]]'s compulsive tribalism.

The [[Third Men]] experienced wars "of a truly religious bitterness" driven by a "perennial conflict" between tenderhearted preservationists and passionate biological manipulators who "willed to create at whatever cost" [010-010].

This era saw the emergence of [[Great Brains]] — vast engineered intellects whose cold rationality introduced an entirely new dimension to strategic calculation.

The [[Martians]] brought warfare to an interplanetary scale. Their invasion of [[Second Men|Earth]] was motivated by both economic need — "The [[martians|Martians]] sought water and vegetable matter" — and religious crusading zeal, to "liberate the terrestrial diamonds" [009-001].

Operating as formless cloud-entities and gigantic mobile "cloud-jellies," they proved nearly invincible: "Cities were knocked down and flattened, human beings masticated into pulp. Man tried weapon after weapon in vain" [009-006].

This [[Martians|Martian]] war nearly extinguished the [[Second Men]] entirely and stands as the most existentially threatening conflict in human history.

On [[Venus]], after the [[Fifth Men]] migrated from [[earth-place|Earth]], human history repeated itself across two hundred million years.

The pattern cycled through "theocratic empires; free and intellectualistic island cities; insecure overlordships of feudal archipelagos; rivalries of high priest and emperor; religious feuds" — all the forms of organized human conflict recurring with "characteristic differences" [013-012].

The [[Fifth Men]], despite their [[Telepathy|telepathic]] gifts and [[Longevity|extended lifespans]], could not escape the gravitational pull of war.

## The Philosophy of War

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]'s treatment of war is deliberately ambivalent, oscillating between moral condemnation and aesthetic appreciation.

In the Preface, the [[Last Men]] narrator acknowledges this tension directly:

> I have imagined for aesthetic purposes that our race will destroy itself. There is today a very earnest movement for peace and international unity; and surely with good fortune and intelligent management it may triumph. Most earnestly we must hope that it will. But I have figured things out in this book in such a manner that this great movement fails. I suppose it incapable of preventing a succession of national wars; and I permit it only to achieve the goal of unity and peace after the mentality of the race has been undermined. (P02-008)

This passage reveals the book's deepest argument about war: it is not merely destructive but tragic. War shapes humanity's evolutionary trajectory precisely through its capacity to destroy.

The "tragedy of a race" (P02-007) is that spiritual maturity always arrives too late, after the vitality that might have used it has been spent in conflict.

The [[First Men]] achieved world peace only after their minds were crippled; the [[Second Men]] developed [[Group Mind|cosmopolitan consciousness]] only to face [[Martians|annihilation]].

The [[Last Men]], facing not war but cosmic extinction, achieve what none of their predecessor species could. They gained the capacity to "contemplate the whole great saga of man as a completed work of art, and to admire it no less for its sudden and tragic end than for the promise in it which was not to be fulfilled" [016-007].

In this final vision, even the wars that crippled earlier humanities are transfigured into elements of a vast aesthetic whole — conflict as counterpoint, destruction as the shadow that gives form to light.

The [[Last Men]]'s [[Last Men Commonwealth|commonwealth]] represents the first human civilization to face annihilation without resorting to war.

War, in the [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]ian cosmos, is thus the definitive expression of the gap between human power and human wisdom — a gap that takes two billion years and eighteen [[First Men|human species]] to close, and closes only when there is nothing left to fight for.
