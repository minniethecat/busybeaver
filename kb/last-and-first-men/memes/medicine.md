Medicine, particularly preventive medicine, was one of the greatest practical achievements of the [[first-men|First Men]]. While the biological sciences eventually stagnated in fundamental theory, they continued to produce remarkable practical benefits that transformed human existence.

## Definition

In the context of the [[first-men|First Men]] civilization, medicine encompassed the biological sciences applied to human health — eliminating diseases, alleviating suffering, and extending vitality. Its crowning achievement was preventive medicine, which systematically eradicated the ancient scourges of humanity.

## Achievements

### The Elimination of Disease

At the height of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], preventive medicine had eliminated an extraordinary range of afflictions. "No longer did men and women have to dread for themselves or those dear to them such afflictions as cancer, tuberculosis, angina pectoris, the rheumatic diseases, and the terrible disorders of the nervous system. No longer were there sudden microbic devastations" [004-026].

### Transformation of Human Life

The impact was revolutionary: "No longer was childbirth an ordeal, and womanhood itself a source of suffering. There were no more chronic invalids, no more lifelong cripples" [004-026]. Only senility remained, and "even this could be repeatedly alleviated by physiological rejuvenation." The removal of these ancient sources of weakness brought about "a pervading buoyancy and optimism impossible to earlier peoples" [004-026].

## Limitations

Despite these practical triumphs, the biological sciences had "long ago become stereotyped in respect of fundamental theories" [004-026]. The First Men achieved mastery over disease without fully understanding the deeper principles of life — a pattern that characterized much of their science.

## Darker Applications

Medical knowledge was also weaponized. During the [[war|War]] between America and China, "American biologists had devised a new malignant germ" targeting the nervous system, causing "all who were even slightly affected incapable of intelligent action" [003-024]. This "American madness" bacillus demonstrated that the same knowledge that healed could also destroy.
