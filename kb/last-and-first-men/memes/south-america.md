# South America

A continent on Earth that played a foundational role in the recovery of human civilization following the First Dark Age, serving as the birthplace of the Patagonian civilization.

## Location

### Type

Continent

### Context

South America is located in the Western Hemisphere of Earth. Following the geological upheavals that reshaped the globe, the southern part of the continent became connected to Antarctica via a land bridge east of Patagonia, stretching through the former Falkland Islands and South Georgia into the Atlantic [005-010].

## Physical Description

Complex climatic changes rendered the southern part of South America a fit nursery for civilization after the First Dark Age [005-010]. The continent was dramatically transformed by geological events — during the later catastrophic moonfall era, South America mostly collapsed into the hollows blasted beneath it, though a new land rose to join Brazil with West Africa [006-031].

## Historical Significance

### Era(s) of Importance

South America was particularly significant during the First Dark Age and the subsequent civilizational recovery. The continent had favourable racial conditions for post-apocalyptic regeneration [005-011]. Various causes combined in South America, and especially in the new and virgin plains of Patagonia, to bring the First Dark Age to an end [005-012].

### Key Events

A major war between North and South America occurred during the period of continental rivalry. During this conflict, an attempt was made to recreate the League of Nations as a Cosmopolitan Sovereignty controlling pooled armaments [003-003]. North America ultimately incorporated much of the hemisphere into its sphere [003-001].

## Species & Civilizations

The Patagonian civilization — a sober, intellectually oriented culture — arose in South America. The Patagonians, described as definitely of higher type than any of their neighbours and more vigorous and acute, spread from their origin east of the ancient Bahia Blanca [005-015]. Patagonian civilization at its height afforded considerable ease and security of life, enabling humans to live to seventy or even eighty years [005-012].

## Changes Over Time

South America's geography transformed radically across the two-billion-year span. The continent served as a cradle for the Patagonian civilization during the First Men era, then suffered catastrophic collapse during the moonfall, and later became connected to Africa via new land masses [006-031]. When Antarctica became more temperate, its coasts developed an "almost Nordic culture" that eventually outshone the ancient Patagonian centre [005-016].
