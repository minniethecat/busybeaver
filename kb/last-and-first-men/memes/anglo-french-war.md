# Anglo-French War

A catastrophic war between England and France, occurring a century after the [[European War]], that destroyed both nations and foreclosed the peaceful unification of the [[First Men]].

## Overview

The Anglo-French War "may be said to have sealed the fate of the [[first-men|First Men]]" [001-009]. It is the most dramatic example in the book of petty cause and mighty effect: a single distressed aircraft reversed a historic peace overture, condemning humanity toward the [[First Dark Age]].

> Through some miscalculation, or a mere defect in his instruments, a French airman went astray, and came to grief in London after the sending of the peace message. Had this not happened, England and France would not have been wrecked. [001-027]

## Context

The war occurred within the [[age-of-the-first-men|Age of the First Men]], roughly a century after the [[European War]]. The deeper cause was a dispute over air power: France had restricted military aeroplane sizes so that London lay within easy striking distance while Paris was scarcely reachable by England. French militarists, seeing a disarmament movement gathering strength, were "eager to strike while yet there was opportunity" [001-015].

The immediate trigger was a fabricated scandal. An Englishwoman seduced a Senegalese corporal in the south of France, then claimed assault when he lost interest. The English press "could not resist this opportunity of trading upon the public's sexuality, tribalism, and self-righteousness" [001-015]. Violence against French subjects followed, and France declared war.

## The Event

The war began with a surprise aerial attack. In two hours, a third of London was destroyed. During the bombardment, the British Cabinet broadcast an unprecedented message of unconditional peace: "From the people of England to the people of France. Catastrophe has fallen on us at your hands. In this hour of agony, all hate and anger have left us" [001-017]. Britain renounced all further hostilities, declaring it better that "a great people should be destroyed than that the whole race should be thrown into turmoil."

The peace message nearly succeeded. Cosmopolitan sentiment in Paris was so strong that "the upshot would almost certainly have been a triumph of sanity" — had not a fatal accident intervened [001-020]. That evening, a French plane descended over western London. Onlookers took it as a messenger of peace. Then a bomb fell near a school and a royal palace, killing a popular young princess.

"A moment's cool thinking would have convinced all onlookers that this disaster was an accident, that the plane was a belated straggler in distress, and no messenger of hate" [001-022]. But reason had no purchase. The British Air Force was ordered to Paris. By Monday, Paris was obliterated. Within days, both nations were shattered by famine, riot, and disease, and the [[league-of-nations|League of Nations]] assumed management [001-025].

## Consequences

The aftermath differed sharply from that of the [[European War]]. "Patriotism simply faded out, for the time, under the influence of extreme disaster. The two enemy peoples cooperated with the League in the work of reconstructing not only each one itself, but each one the other" [001-026].

Yet the long-term cost was catastrophic. The eclipse of France and England removed the critical intelligence that had tempered European romanticism. [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]'s verdict is unambiguous: "but for this trivial accident, the movement which was started by England's peace message might have proceeded steadily and rapidly toward the unification of the race ... And so the first Dark Age might never have occurred" [001-027]. The war ensured the [[First World State]] would be built only after the mind of the race was already declining.
