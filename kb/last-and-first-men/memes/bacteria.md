# Bacteria

Microscopic life-forms that played roles in human disease, biological warfare, and ultimately the recognition that life itself could take forms radically different from Earth's familiar biology. In Stapledon's future history, bacteria serve as both a practical reality and a conceptual bridge — the smallest unit of familiar life pointing toward the alien.

## Overview

Bacteria appear in "Last and First Men" primarily through their effects on human affairs — as agents of disease, as weapons, and as a conceptual bridge to understanding the radically alien biology of Mars. The First Men's vulnerability to microbial life underscored the fragility of their dominion over nature. Their eventual conquest of bacterial disease represented one of their genuine achievements.

## Biological Warfare

The First Men weaponized bacteria. American forces deployed bacilli that specifically targeted the nervous system: "wandering bacilli had got into the brains of several high officials throughout the province, rendering their behaviour incoherent" [003-018]. This biological agent was designed to "disintegrate the highest levels of the nervous system" [003-017], leaving victims incapable of intelligent action or causing "paralysis and finally death" [003-017]. The weapon reduced a Chinese city "into a bedlam" [003-018].

This use of bacteria as weapons revealed a dark truth about the First Men: their scientific ingenuity consistently outpaced their moral development. They could engineer microbes to destroy minds before they could engineer social systems to prevent war.

## Medicine and Mastery

The First Men eventually overcame microbial disease. "No longer did men and women have to dread for themselves or those dear to them such afflictions as cancer, tuberculosis, angina pectoris, the rheumatic diseases, and the terrible disorders of the nervous system" [004-026]. This conquest of bacteria and other pathogens represented one of the First Men's genuine and unambiguous achievements — a gift they passed to all successor species.

## Beyond Earth: The Martian Subvital

The most profound role of microbial-scale life came with the discovery of the [[martians|Martians]]. Their biology was composed of "subvital units" — entities analogous to bacteria but capable of collective organization into conscious cloudlets. "To describe the biology, psychology, and history of a race so alien to humanity" [008-011] required rethinking what "life" meant at its most fundamental level.

Earth's bacteria were merely the smallest familiar units of biology; the Martians revealed that even simpler components could constitute intelligent being. A Martian cloudlet was not a colony of bacteria but a single consciousness distributed across millions of subvital units — a mode of existence that challenged every Earth-born assumption about the relationship between matter and mind.

## Bacteria and the Definition of Life

The Martian discovery forced a philosophical reconsideration. If subvital units could compose conscious beings, then the boundary between "mere chemistry" and "life" was arbitrary. The hierarchy — molecule, bacterium, cell, organism, mind — was revealed as a contingent Earthly arrangement, not a cosmic necessity. "It was from biology that the first hint had come" [012-010] of possibilities that previous science had excluded as impossible.

## From Microbe to Cosmos

Bacteria thus served an unexpected philosophical function in humanity's intellectual evolution. The study of the very small — microbes, subvital units, molecular processes — repeatedly opened doors to the very large: the nature of consciousness, the diversity of possible life, humanity's place in a cosmos indifferent to its categories. The bacteriologist's microscope and the astronomer's telescope were, in the end, instruments of the same inquiry.
