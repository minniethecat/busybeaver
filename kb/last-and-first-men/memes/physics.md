# Physics

Physics in *Last and [[first-men|First Men]]* is the double-edged sword of knowledge — the science that unlocked subatomic energy, promised unlimited power, and ultimately became entangled in religious mysticism and political control during the era of the [[first-world-state|First World State]].

## Definition

Physics, as practiced by the First Men, encompassed the study of matter, energy, and the fundamental forces of nature. The discipline reached its apogee with the discovery of subatomic energy through matter annihilation, but its subsequent fragmentation and politicization mirrored the broader intellectual decline of the species.

## Origin

### The Subatomic Breakthrough

The pivotal moment in First Men physics occurred when "an international society of scientific workers was meeting in England at Plymouth, and a young Chinese physicist had expressed his desire to make a report to a select committee" [002-010]. The Chinese physicist had "been experimenting to find means for the utilization of subatomic energy by the annihilation of matter" [002-010]. The demonstration at Hartland Point confirmed that "the utilization of subatomic energy had indeed been achieved" [002-014].

The demonstration's destructive power — the annihilation of Lundy Island — led the physicists themselves to recoil. The discoverer was urged to "destroy your handiwork, and, if it were possible, your memory of your marvellous research" [002-021]. An English physicist, "one of the few remaining intellectuals of his race, compensated for the severe study of physics by a scarcely less devoted research into the history of English expletives and slang" [002-013].

### Fragmentation and Decline

By the era of the [[first-world-state|First World State]], science had become so vast that "only a tiny fraction of it could be mastered by one brain" [004-013]. "Students of one branch of science knew practically nothing of the work of others in kindred branches. Especially was this the case with the huge science called Subatomic Physics" [004-013]. When rumors circulated that "the mystery of Gordelpus was known to the physicists, each department of subatomic physics was both reluctant to deny the charge explicitly in its own case, and ready to believe that some other department really did possess the secret" [004-014].

## Details

### The Gordelpus Cult

Physics became entangled with religious expectation. The promise of unlimited energy was transformed into the legend of "Gordelpus" — a divine secret supposedly held by the physicists. This fusion of science and religion proved catastrophic: when the truth emerged that no such secret existed, "the whole hoary legend of the divine secret was a lie" [004-057], the revelation contributed to civilizational collapse. "Under strict supervision, he used his own knowledge of physics to experiment in search of the Chinese trick" [002-030] — a policy of directing research "along a blind alley" [002-030] to prevent the dangerous rediscovery of subatomic energy.

## Role in the Narrative

### Significance

Physics embodies the book's central epistemological concern: knowledge can outrun wisdom. The First Men achieved the power to annihilate matter before they achieved the moral capacity to use it responsibly. The subsequent fragmentation, mystification, and politicization of physics illustrates how civilizations can lose knowledge as readily as they gain it.
