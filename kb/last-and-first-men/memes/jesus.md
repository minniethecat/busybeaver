# Jesus

First-century religious leader of the [[first-men|First Men]] era, exemplar of integrity of will and unselfish love, identified by the [[last-men|Last Men]] as a singular mind whose psychological uniqueness defies ordinary explanation.

## Identity

### Species

Jesus belonged to the [[First Men]], the original human species. [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] presents him alongside [[Socrates]] as one of the two supreme ethical exemplars produced by this primitive but brilliant species [001-003].

### Era

Jesus lived during the [[Age of the First Men]], in first-century Roman-occupied Judea -- within that "moment of precocious experience" that, from the remote perspective of the [[Last Men]], encompassed the entire arc of [[first-men|First Men]] civilization "from savagery towards civilization" [001-002].

## Biography

### Early Life

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] provides no biographical details. Jesus is invoked as a moral archetype rather than a historical figure, springing from the Hebrew people who "were also among the first to conceive admiration for Fate" through their "worship of divine law" [001-005].

### Key Actions

Jesus "delighted in the actual human persons around him, and in that flavour of divinity which, for him, pervaded the world" and "stood for unselfish love of neighbours and of God" [001-003]. [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] credits him with awakening humanity to "the ideal of passionate yet self-oblivious worship" and "integrity of will" [001-003].

His teaching of loving one's neighbour as oneself became a defining ethical standard against which all subsequent human species would be measured.

### Death / Legacy

Though his death is not described, his influence persisted across evolutionary epochs. His ideal proved more attainable than the Socratic: the [[Second Men]], with their naturally generous dispositions, "loved one another in the manner of Jesus, and infused their whole social policy with loving-kindness," earning [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]'s description of them as "Natural Christians" [007-010].

## Thought & Influence

### Ideas / Philosophy

Jesus's core teaching was love without reservation -- of neighbour, of God, and of the world as pervaded by divinity [001-003].

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] presents this love-ideal as the necessary complement to the Socratic: "[[socrates|Socrates]] urged intellectual integrity, Jesus integrity of will. Each, of course, though starting with a different emphasis, involved the other" [001-003]. Together they formed "the first, and some would say the greatest, achievement" of Western culture [001-003].

### Impact

Jesus's demand for unselfish love "demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the [[first-men|First Men]] was never really capable" [001-004]. The failure to achieve it "helped to engender in the race a cynical lassitude which was one cause of its decay" [001-004].

Yet the ideal endured: in the late [[First World State]], a perverted "ancient Christian faith in a God of Love" emerged as a secret cult, demonstrating the persistent force of Jesus's vision even in degraded form [004-045].

## In the Narrative

### Role in the Book

Jesus serves as the supreme benchmark of moral integrity. Most significantly, the [[Last Men]] identify him as a "singular mind" -- one of "a few remarkable minds, scattered up and down past ages" whose psychological uniqueness resists ordinary explanation [016-018].

"Your Jesus, your [[socrates|Socrates]], your Gautama, show traces of this uniqueness," the [[last-men|Last Men]] observe, speculating that such singularities may originate from "the cosmic mind, whose future occurrence and eternal existence we earnestly desire" [016-018].
