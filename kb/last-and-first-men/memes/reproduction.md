## Overview

Reproduction takes radically different forms across the species in "Last and First Men." From the familiar sexual reproduction of the First Men to the alien reproductive biology of the Martians and the controlled breeding programs of later eras, reproduction is a lens through which Stapledon explores the relationship between biology, identity, and society.

## Martian Reproduction

Martian reproduction was fundamentally unlike anything on Earth. The "Martian subvital units" reproduced through a process where individuals would merge and then separate into new configurations. There was a key "difference, indeed, in respect of reproduction" [008-019] — the process was not binary but multiplicative. After merging, the units would "part in reproduction" [008-020], creating new individuals that were neither parent but something entirely new.

The Martians had no concept of gender as humans understood it. "There were not partners in reproduction" [008-030] in the human sense. Instead, reproduction was tied to the "impulse to merge physically" — a drive that was both creative and destructive, capable of producing new consciousness or annihilating existing identities.

## Human Reproduction and Control

The later human species increasingly took control of their own reproduction. The World State "encouraged this policy" of selective breeding [010-043], treating human reproduction as a matter of biological policy rather than private choice. The Eighth Men achieved such mastery over biology that reproduction became entirely engineered.

## Reproduction Without Sex

In the Fifth Men and later species, reproduction and sexuality became decoupled. The text notes that some later humans engaged in "sexual intercourse; but no reproduction was ever traced to this source" [012-040], indicating that reproduction had become a separate, controlled biological process distinct from human intimacy.
