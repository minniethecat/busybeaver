# European War

The first of the great world [[war|conflict]]s that demonstrated the [[First Men]]'s fatal incapacity to subordinate tribal instinct to cosmopolitan reason. Fought between 1914 and 1918, it inaugurated a century of escalating violence that culminated in the [[First Dark Age]].

## Overview

The European War pitted Germany against an alliance of France and England in the first global-scale [[war|conflict]] among the [[First Men]]. Though modest by later standards, it introduced aerial [[war|warfare]] and industrial slaughter on a scale that shocked a civilization convinced of its own advancement.

From the remote vantage of the [[Last Men]], the war marked a critical inflection. A genuine will for peace emerged from the carnage, yet the species' psychology proved incapable of sustaining it.

> The peace which followed the European War is one of the most significant moments of ancient history; for it epitomizes both the dawning vision and the incurable blindness, both the impulse toward a higher loyalty and the compulsive tribalism of a race which was, after all, but superficially human. [001-008]

## Background

The war arose from the collision of nationalist rivalries, imperial ambitions, and a rigid alliance system. The [[First Men]], "animals that were fashioned for hunting and fighting in the wild," had been "suddenly called upon to be citizens, and moreover citizens of a world-community" [001-007].

Their nervous systems balanced precariously between cortical reason and subcortical drive. [[Science]] and industry had armed them with unprecedented weapons while moral development lagged far behind. The proximate trigger was an assassination in the Balkans; the deeper cause was the species-wide tension between tribal loyalty and cosmopolitan aspiration.

## Key Events

The war opened in 1914 and rapidly stalemated into trench [[war|warfare]] across Europe. It introduced aerial combat as a decisive military dimension that would escalate catastrophically in the later [[Anglo-French War]].

The scale of death produced a genuine revulsion against war. A peace movement gathered force in every belligerent nation, and for a moment it seemed the [[First Men]] might transcend their violent inheritance. The settlement, however, was punitive. "After the European War the defeated nation, formerly one of the most militaristic of peoples, became for the time the most docile" [001-010] -- a docility that masked rather than resolved the underlying [[war|conflict]].

## Aftermath

The peace proved hollow. Within a century, the [[Anglo-French War]] erupted, sparked by a trivial cause that "may be said to have sealed the fate of the [[first-men|First Men]]" [001-009]. The European War had weakened the nation-state system without replacing it.

After further wars, the exhausted species finally achieved unity through the [[First World State]], roughly three hundred and eighty terrestrial years after the European War [004-001]. Yet "the goal of world unity was at last achieved -- not, however, before the mind of the race had been seriously crippled" [004-001]. The [[First World State]] endured four millennia before collapsing into the [[First Dark Age]].
