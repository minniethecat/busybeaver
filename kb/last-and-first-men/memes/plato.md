# Plato

Classical Greek philosopher of the [[first-men|First Men]] era whose ideal of philosopher-kings became the enduring benchmark for governance across human evolutionary history. His conception of rule by wisdom — not popularity, wealth, or force — persisted as an aspiration through all eighteen human species.

## Identity

### Species

Plato belonged to the [[first-men|First Men]], sharing the species' characteristic duality of aspiration and limitation. His thought emerged during the brief cultural zenith of classical Athens, part of that "moment of precocious experience" that defined the First Men's civilizational arc [001-002].

### Era

Plato lived during the [[age-of-the-first-men]], in the fourth century BCE — the classical Greek period that produced [[socrates|Socrates]], Plato, and the foundational texts of Western philosophy.

## Biography

### Key Actions

Plato founded the Academy in Athens and authored the dialogues that preserved [[socrates|Socrates]]' thought. His *Republic* articulated the ideal of the philosopher-king — a ruler who embodies wisdom and governs in the interest of the common good, subordinating personal ambition to the welfare of the whole.

### Historical Context

Plato wrote in the aftermath of Athens' defeat in the Peloponnesian War and the execution of Socrates. His political philosophy was forged in disillusionment with democracy — a system that, in his view, had killed the wisest man in Athens. This biographical wound gave his ideal of philosopher-kingship its particular urgency: only those trained to recognize the Good could be trusted with power.

## Thought and Influence

### Core Philosophy

Plato conceived the ideal of governance by wisdom rather than popularity — rulers trained in philosophy who would subordinate personal interest to the common good. This stood as the intellectual counterpart to the ethical ideal of [[jesus|Jesus]]'s selfless love [001-003]. Together, the Socratic, Platonic, and Christian ideals formed the moral triad of Western civilization.

### Enduring Tension

The Platonic ideal, like the Socratic and Christian ideals, "demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the First Men was never really capable" [001-004]. This gap between aspiration and capacity was not a failure of the ideal but a limitation of the species. Later human species — with more developed nervous systems — would come closer to realizing Plato's vision.

### Historical Realization

The [[nationalist-party-of-china|Nationalist Party of China]] — "by a route of its own, the Party had approached the ideal of the Platonic governors" [003-012] — represented the closest the First Men ever came to philosopher-kingship. This half civil service, half religious order synthesized Plato's vision with the practical demands of modern governance, drawing also "from the native and literary civil service of old China" [003-010].

## In the Narrative

### Role in the Book

Plato appears in [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]]'s narrative as a historical reference point — the originator of a standard of governance that humanity pursued across two billion years. He is invoked not as a character but as a benchmark, a measure of how far each species advanced toward or fell short of the ideal of rule by wisdom.

## Perspectives on Plato

### The First Men's View

To the First Men, Plato was a cultural ancestor — revered but remote. His ideals were taught in universities and invoked in political rhetoric, but rarely practiced. The tension between Platonic aspiration and democratic reality defined much of Western political thought.

### Later Species' Assessment

Later human species, with their enhanced cognitive capacities, regarded Plato differently. The [[second-men|Second Men]], with their superior vitality and coherence, recognized in Plato an early intuition of truths they had more fully realized. The [[fifth-men|Fifth Men]], with their perfected dispassionate cognition, saw Plato's theory of Forms as a primitive but genuine anticipation of their own direct apprehension of universal structures.

The [[great-brains-civilization|Great Brains]] — the Eighth Men — achieved what Plato could only imagine: governance by pure intelligence, untainted by personal ambition or emotional distortion. Yet even they acknowledged their debt to the Athenian who first articulated the principle that wisdom should rule.

### The Last Men's Retrospective

The [[last-men|Last Men]], surveying two billion years of human striving, placed Plato among the small handful of First Men whose thought transcended their biological limitations. He was remembered not for being right about everything but for asking the right questions — questions that every subsequent species had to answer anew.
