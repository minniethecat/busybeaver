# Socrates

Athenian philosopher of the [[first-men|First Men]] era, exemplar of intellectual integrity and dispassionate intelligence, identified by the [[last-men|Last Men]] as a singular mind whose psychological uniqueness defies ordinary explanation.

## Identity

### Species

Socrates belonged to the [[First Men]], the original human species that seeded all eighteen subsequent forms of humanity. [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] presents him as one of the species' highest spiritual and intellectual achievements [001-003].

### Era

Socrates lived during the [[Age of the First Men]], in fifth-century BCE Athens -- near the zenith of [[first-men|First Men]] cultural development, before the long decline into the [[First World State]] and eventual collapse [001-002].

## Biography

### Early Life

[[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] provides no biographical details. Socrates is invoked as a spiritual archetype, "springing" from peoples who "were also among the first to conceive admiration for Fate" -- the Greeks, whose tragic art first glimpsed "that vision of an alien and supernal beauty" that would "exalt and perplex" humanity across two billion years [001-005].

### Key Actions

Socrates "delighted in the truth for its own sake and not merely for practical ends" and "glorified unbiased thinking, honesty of mind and speech" [001-003]. [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] credits him with awakening humanity to "the ideal of dispassionate intelligence" and the demand for "intellectual integrity" [001-003].

His foundational insistence was "that clear thought is impossible without clear definition of terms, and that without clear thinking man misses fullness of being" [004-046].

### Death / Legacy

Though his death is not described in the text, his legacy persisted for millennia.

In the late [[First World State]] era, "pathetic devotees of intellect" formed secret cults inspired by Socrates, yet "they missed his spirit completely" [004-046]. These "ultimate Socratics" obsessively defined everything from noses to mountains, believing definition alone secured "comradeship with Socrates in the hereafter" [004-046].

## Thought & Influence

### Ideas / Philosophy

Socrates's core teaching was the inseparability of clear thinking from a fully realized life: "without clear thinking man misses fullness of being" [004-046]. His ideal of intellectual integrity demanded "unbiased thinking, honesty of mind and speech" [001-003].

Though his emphasis was on intellect, [[olaf-stapledon|Stapledon]] notes that Socratic intelligence and [[Jesus]]'s integrity of will each "involved the other" -- neither ideal could be complete in isolation [001-003].

### Impact

The Socratic ideal "demanded of the human brain a degree of vitality and coherence of which the nervous system of the [[first-men|First Men]] was never really capable" [001-004]. The failure to achieve it "helped to engender in the race a cynical lassitude which was one cause of its decay" [001-004].

Only the [[Second Men]], with their "superb clarity of mind," later realized "wholly dispassionate cognition" as a natural faculty [007-010].

## In the Narrative

### Role in the Book

Socrates serves as a dual benchmark. He represents the highest intellectual aspiration of the [[First Men]], and the [[Last Men]] present him as a "singular mind" -- one of "a few remarkable minds, scattered up and down past ages" whose psychological uniqueness resists ordinary explanation [016-018].

The [[last-men|Last Men]] speculate that such singularities may originate from "the cosmic mind, whose future occurrence and eternal existence we earnestly desire" [016-018].
