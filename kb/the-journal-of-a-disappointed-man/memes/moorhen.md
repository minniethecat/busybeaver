## Overview

The **Moorhen** (*Gallinula chloropus*) is a common but often overlooked waterbird of British ponds, slow-moving rivers, and marshy ditches. With its slate-black plumage, white undertail, and distinctive red frontal shield, the moorhen is a familiar sight to any country walker — and Barbellion, the tireless field naturalist, was no exception.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's encounters with moorhens belong to the fabric of his Devon rambles — the kind of observation that a less attentive diarist might overlook as too ordinary to record, but which for Barbellion formed the essential texture of a naturalist's life:

> Along the pond's edge, a moorhen picked its way through the reeds — that curious, jerky walk, as though it were perpetually surprised by its own feet. (PN=002-015)

The moorhen features less as a focal subject than as a recurrent presence in Barbellion's landscape — one of the "common objects of the countryside" whose observation marked him as a genuine field naturalist rather than a mere collector of rarities.

## Natural History

The moorhen is widespread and resident across Britain, breeding on freshwater ponds, lakes, canals, and slow-flowing rivers. It builds a bulky nest of reeds and twigs, often on a low branch overhanging water or among marginal vegetation. Though it appears ungainly on land with its exaggerated bobbing gait, the moorhen is a strong swimmer and diver. Its presence is often betrayed by its sharp, explosive *"krrruk"* call.

## Taxonomic Context

The moorhen belongs to the rail family Rallidae, a group that includes coots, crakes, and gallinules. Barbellion, with characteristic taxonomic awareness, would have known it by the contemporary scientific name *Gallinula chloropus*. The species is sometimes called the "common gallinule" in North America, though the European moorhen is a distinct subspecies.
