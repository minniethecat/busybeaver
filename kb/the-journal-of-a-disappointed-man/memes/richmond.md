[[richmond]], a suburban district in southwest London on the River Thames, was the destination of one of Barbellion's more memorable Sunday excursions. The bus journey there, taken in the afternoon from central London, became a study in social discomfort, fleeting attraction, and moments of self-conscious observation in the Park.

## The Bus Journey

The trip to Richmond began inauspiciously. Forced to sit inside the bus, Barbellion found himself facing a row of "fat, ugly, elderly women, all off to visit their married daughters, the usual Sunday jaunt." At [[hammersmith|Hammersmith]] he managed to move outside, only to be caught in a hailstorm at Turnham Green. Sheltering in a closed shop doorway, watching the wet, almost empty street, his mind circled a single thought:

> …thinking, rethinking and thinking over again the same thought, viz., that the bus ride along this route was exceptionally cheap—probably because of competition with the trams. (003-395)

## In the Park

The second bus finally delivered him to Richmond. Two young girls sat in front, repeatedly glancing back to gauge his interest. Barbellion, characteristically, looked *through* them:

> Walked in the Park just conscious of the singing of Larks and the chatter of Jays, but harassed mentally by the question, "To whom shall I send my essay, when finished?" (003-396)

Sheltering from the rain under an oak tree, he was joined by four young men who "gossipped and giggled like girls, put their arms around each other's necks," and discussed the previous night's dinner—Duck and Tomato Soup—and a companion's fancy waistcoat. When Barbellion rose to leave, the youths became "convulsed with laughter." He scowled. (003-396)

## The Pagoda Tearooms

After his walk, Barbellion took tea in the Pagoda tearooms—"dry toast and brown bread and butter"—where he observed two young men quietly playing the fool at a nearby table, and a pair of lovers kissing "in between mouthfuls of bread and butter." (003-396)
