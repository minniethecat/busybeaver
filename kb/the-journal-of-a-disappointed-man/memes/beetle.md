## Overview

Beetles (order Coleoptera) appear throughout Barbellion's journal in four distinct contexts: competitive seasonal collecting, a chance encounter mediated by a pretty governess, the eerie limestone quarry pool at nightfall, and a sunset walk with his beloved. These scattered references reveal the breadth of the young naturalist's entomological interests — from serious specimen collecting to the aesthetic appreciation of a beetle's colour against the grass.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's earliest beetle reference is a competitive note, tallying his egg-collecting season before looking ahead:

> "On reviewing the past egg-season, I find in all I have discovered 232 nests belonging to forty-four species. I only hope I shall be as successful with the beetle-season." (002-028)

A more personal encounter follows — the governess at the beach, who asks if he is a naturalist:

> "The governess is an awfully pretty girl. We have been talking together today and she asked me if I were a naturalist. I said 'Yes.' She said, 'Well, I found a funny little beetle yesterday and Mr. S—— said I ought to have given it to you.' Later, I felt she was looking at me, so I looked at her, across the beach." (002-106)

Barbellion never receives the beetle — nor sees the governess again — but the moment, with its shy mutual glance, becomes one of the diary's most poignant vignettes.

In the sinister limestone quarry pool, the beetle surfaces amid an almost gothic stillness:

> "A large Dytiscus beetle rises to the surface to breathe and every now and then large bubbles of marsh gas come sailing majestically up from the depth and explode quietly into the fetid air." (003-905)

The great diving beetle (*Dytiscus marginalis*) — a common pond predator — becomes part of Barbellion's vivid, almost hallucinatory portrait of a landscape he admits haunts him in retrospect.

Years later, in happier circumstances, a Carabus beetle appears during an evening walk with E——:

> "A large blue Carabus beetle was stumbling about, Culvers cooed in the woods near by. It was delightful to be up 600 feet on a grassy field under the shadow of a large wood at sunset with my darling." (003-957)

## Natural History

The great diving beetle (*Dytiscus marginalis*) is a large freshwater beetle (up to 35 mm) common in ponds and stagnant water across Britain. It breathes by surfacing to trap air under its wing cases. Carabus beetles are ground beetles (Carabidae), many species displaying brilliant metallic colours — Barbellion describes one as "large blue." They are predatory, feeding on slugs, worms, and other invertebrates, and are most active at dusk.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's beetle references span his entire journal (1903–1913), from boyhood competitive collecting to mature aesthetic observation. His dissection work included *Dytiscus* anatomy (recorded alongside *Lumbricus* and *Petromyzon* specimens in 002-260). Beetles — as the most species-rich insect order — offered the young naturalist an inexhaustible field of study, combining the collector's zeal with genuine anatomical curiosity. The Carabus sunset entry, written near the end of the journal, suggests that even as his health declined, Barbellion retained his naturalist's eye for the small and beautiful.
