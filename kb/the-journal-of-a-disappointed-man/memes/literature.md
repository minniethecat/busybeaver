## Overview

Literature is Barbellion's second vocation, running parallel to his [[ambition|scientific ambition]]s and ultimately surpassing them. As his health declines and field work becomes impossible, literary production — the journal itself, essays, articles — becomes his primary mode of self-assertion. His relationship with literature is characteristically double: he is both an enthusiastic consumer and an anxious producer, reading voraciously while measuring his own writing against the masters and finding it wanting.

## In the Journal

### Reading

Barbellion's reading is wide-ranging and undisciplined — the reading of an autodidact following his curiosity wherever it leads. He reads [[science|natural history]], philosophy, poetry, novels, essays, and medical texts. The journal records his responses with the same descriptive attention he brings to natural phenomena: a book is something to be observed, reacted to, and incorporated into his understanding. His library visits were daily rituals: "This evening went to the Library and read about her in Mathilde Blind's introductory essay to her Journal" (003-564). On a typical evening he would "read the *Dublin Review* (article on Samuel Butler), *North American Review* (one on Henry James)" before dinner, and after dinner "Evening Standard, Saturday Westminster" (003-596).

### Literary Ambition

Barbellion's [[ambition|literary ambition]] is one of the journal's driving forces. Despite "uniform [[disappointment|failure]]" — rejected manuscripts from "every conceivable kind of periodical, from *[[punch-magazine|Punch]]* to the *Hibbert Journal*" (004-517) — he never stops writing and submitting. He signed his "articles apprenticing me to journalism for five years" (002-043), committing himself to the writer's life. The journal itself is his great literary project, the work he hopes will justify his existence: "I have always had one ambition — to be a great [[natural-history|naturalist]]" (002-033), but the ambition gradually shifts from science to literature, from observing nature to recording consciousness. He set explicit writing goals: "To write two essays for Cornhill which shall surely induce the Editor to publish" (003-693).

### The Journal as Literature

Barbellion was acutely self-conscious about the journal as a literary artifact. He spent his final years "busy rewriting, editing and bowdlerising my journals for publication against the time when I shall have gone the way of all flesh" (004-433), understanding that "no one else would prepare it for publication if I don't" (004-433). He described his method with striking precision: "In this Journal, my pen is a delicate needle point, tracing out a graph of temperament so as to show its daily fluctuations: grave and gay, up and down, lamentation and revelry, self-love and self-disgust" (004-475).

### Literary Influences

Barbellion's prose bears the imprint of his reading. The influence of [[sir-thomas-browne|Sir Thomas Browne]] is evident in his baroque periods and metaphysical wit. The Romantic confessional tradition — Rousseau, De Quincey — shapes his willingness to expose unflattering truths about himself. And the Victorian [[natural-history|naturalist]]s — Darwin, Hudson — provide the model of precise, attentive description that he applies to both nature and self. He discovered the Goncourt brothers' *Journal* with particular excitement: "I am sure that the match which fired the gunpowder was the discovery of the de Goncourts' Journal! It's extraordinary how I have..." (004-088), seeing in their collaborative diary a precedent for his own project.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion occupies a transitional moment in English literary history: the Edwardian era, poised between Victorian moral seriousness and modernist experimentation. His journal anticipates the confessional literature of the twentieth century — the diaries of Kafka, the notebooks of Camus — while remaining rooted in the Victorian tradition of the man of letters. His prose style, with its mixture of scientific precision and Romantic intensity, is a distinctive achievement of this transitional moment.

He read contemporary periodicals with professional interest: the *[[english-review|English Review]]*, the *[[dublin-review|Dublin Review]]*, the *[[north-american-review|North American Review]]*, and newspapers including the *[[daily-news|Daily News]]* and *[[evening-standard|Evening Standard]]*. His own work appeared in the *American Forum*, which "published my article, but did not pay" (004-517) — a familiar frustration for the aspiring literary journalist.

## Barbellion's Perspective

Literature, for Barbellion, is not an escape from life but a way of living more intensely. The journal is an act of literary creation that is also an act of self-preservation: by writing himself onto the page, he hopes to survive his own death. This hope — that literature can defeat [[death|mortality]] — is the journal's founding illusion and its sustaining power. Whether it is an illusion or not, the journal exists, and that existence is a kind of victory.

## Related Rejections

The [[english-review|English Review]] rejection (003-359) was a defining moment: "To be faintly encouraged and delicately rejected" captures the peculiarly painful position of the talented aspirant who is good enough to receive a personal note but not good enough to be published. This pattern of near-success — accepted but unpaid by the *American Forum*, encouraged but rejected by the *English Review*, manuscript after manuscript returned from "every conceivable kind of periodical" (004-517) — is the rhythm of Barbellion's literary life.
