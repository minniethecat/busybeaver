## Overview

Ambition is one of the most pervasive and fiercely examined themes in Barbellion's journal. It is not a simple desire for success — it is a consuming, almost pathological force that the diarist dissects with unflinching honesty across all three Parts of his journal. From his earliest adolescent declaration of intent to become "a great [[natural-history|naturalist]]" (002-033) through the tortured reflections of his [[london|London]] years, to the mature reckoning of his later entries, ambition functions as both engine and tormentor: the thing that drives him forward and the thing that will not let him rest.

## In the Journal

### Early Aspirations

Barbellion's ambition appears early and intensely. At thirteen, he already records his determination: "I have always had one ambition — to be a great [[natural-history|naturalist]]" (002-033). Even as a child, he confesses, he secretly measured himself against Macaulay and [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]]: "I can remember [[curiosity|wonder]]ing as a child if I were a young Macaulay or [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]] and secretly deciding that I was" (002-149). This precocious self-conception set the stage for a lifetime of measuring the distance between aspiration and achievement.

### Ambition as Internal Struggle

The journal repeatedly frames ambition as a battle fought within the self. Barbellion describes "the struggle with a great ambition and a weak will — unequal to the task of coping with it" (002-128). He is candid about the paralysis that grand ambition can produce: "I have planned on too big a scale, perhaps. I have put too great a strain on my [[genius|talent]]s, I have whipped a flagging will, I have [[honey-bee|bee]]n forever cogitating, worrying, devising means of escape" (002-128). Ambition here is not empowering — it is exhausting.

### The Intersection with Illness

What makes Barbellion's treatment of ambition distinctive is its entanglement with his deteriorating health. He explicitly links the two as the twin poles of his existence: "My life has always [[honey-bee|bee]]n a continuous struggle with ill-health and ambition, and I have mastered neither" (003-544). His illness renders time precious and achievement urgent, and "as fulfilment recedes ambition obsesses me the more" (003-545). In a moment of striking self-[[illness|diagnosis]], he anatomises his predicament: "Given a weak constitution, a great ambition, an amorous nature, and at the same time a very fastidious one, I might have known I was in for trouble" (003-561).

### Ambition as Barometer

In the third Part, Barbellion arrives at a remarkable formulation: ambition is not a constant but a vital sign. "The strength of my ambition at any given moment is the measure of my state of health" (004-207). When his health sinks low, ambition releases its grip; when health returns, it "pinches devilishly hard" (004-207). This intimate coupling of physical condition and psychological drive is one of the journal's most original insights.

### Literary Ambition

Despite an unbroken record of rejection, Barbellion's literary ambition proves astonishingly durable. "In spite of uniform [[disappointment|failure]], my literary ambition has never flagged" (004-517). He catalogs his rejected manuscripts — "from *[[punch-magazine|Punch]]* to the *Hibbert Journal*" (004-517) — with a mixture of [[disappointment|frustration]] and grim perseverance. The ambition to be read, to be known, to justify his existence through publication, persists even as his body fails.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion's conception of ambition draws on several Edwardian intellectual currents. The Carlylean ideal of the "great man" — the exceptional individual who bends history through sheer force of will — haunts his early self-comparisons to Macaulay and [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]]. Simultaneously, the Darwinian and Huxleyan tradition of the self-made [[natural-history|naturalist]] offered a model of meritocratic ascent through patient observation and intellectual labour. Barbellion's journal can be read as the painful document of a man caught between these two models: the romantic genius and the industrious scientist, unable to fulfil either and unable to abandon either.

## Barbellion's Perspective

What distinguishes Barbellion's treatment of ambition is its quality of relentless self-examination. He does not simply *have* ambition — he watches himself have it, analyses its biology, tracks its fluctuations, and names it a pathology: "a morbid diathesis of the mind" (004-226). He calls it a "canker" (002-149) and a "crab" that pinches (004-207), yet he also recognises it as inseparable from his identity. The ambition that tortures him is the same force that produces the journal itself. His honesty about this contradiction — that the thing he resents is the thing that makes him *him* — gives the journal its peculiar power.
