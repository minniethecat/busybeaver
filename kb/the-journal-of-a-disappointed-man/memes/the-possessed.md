**The Possessed** (Бесы, also translated as *Demons* or *The Devils*), [[fyodor-dostoevsky]]'s novel of political nihilism and spiritual catastrophe, was one of the works that nourished Barbellion's restless intellectual life during his [[london|London]] years. He read it alongside [[Friedrich Nietzsche]]'s *Beyond Good and Evil* and the *Journal* of [[marie-bashkirtseff|Marie Bashkirtseff]]—a trinity of texts that spoke to his preoccupation with passion, self-consciousness, and [[death|mortality]].

## A Balaclava Charge

Barbellion invoked *The Possessed* as the literary equivalent of a cavalry charge—a work of overwhelming narrative force. In conversation with his friend R——, he declared:

> "And the last chapter of Dostoevsky's *The Possessed* for a Balaclava Charge." (003-519)

The comparison to the Charge of the Light Brigade—the disastrous but heroic cavalry action of the Crimean War—suggests the novel's effect on Barbellion: a headlong, reckless, exhilarating plunge into darkness.

## Among Kindred Works

The novel appears again in a list of recent [[literature|reading]] that maps Barbellion's intellectual trajectory away from [[zoology]] and toward literature and philosophy:

> *Beyond Good and Evil*. Dostoevsky's *The Possessed*. Marie Bashkirtseff's *Journal*. (003-718)

This constellation of texts—Nietzsche's philosophical critique of morality, [[fyodor-dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]]'s novel of revolutionary conspiracy, and [[marie-bashkirtseff|Bashkirtseff]]'s confessional diary—reflects the convergence of Barbellion's own concerns: the interrogation of self, the hunger for intensity, and the shadow of early death.
