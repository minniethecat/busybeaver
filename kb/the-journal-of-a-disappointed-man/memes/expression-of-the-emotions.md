## About

*The Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* is an 1872 book by [[charles-darwin]] that examines the biological origins of emotional expression in humans and animals. It was Darwin's third major work on evolutionary theory.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion listed Darwin's *Expression of the Emotions* as one of the three most fascinating [[literature|books]] in science he had read, alongside Gaskell's *Origin of Vertebrates* and [[henri-bergson|Bergson]]'s *[[le-rire-bergson|Le Rire]]* (002-274). His admiration aligns with his broader engagement with evolutionary biology and [[science|natural history]].

> The three most fascinating books in Science that I have so far read are (easily):—1. Darwin's *Expression of the Emotions*. 2. Gaskell's *Origin of Vertebrates*. 3. Bergson's *Le Rire*.
>
> (002-274)

## Context

Barbellion's reference to the work appears in the context of his voracious scientific [[literature|reading]] during his early years as a self-taught [[natural-history|naturalist]]. The book's focus on the biological basis of emotion resonates with Barbellion's own intensely introspective journal-keeping, where he meticulously documented his emotional states.
