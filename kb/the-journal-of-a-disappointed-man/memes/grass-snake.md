## Overview

The grass snake (*Natrix helvetica*, formerly *Natrix natrix*) appears in Barbellion's journal in two revealing passages: a dramatic field encounter at a deep limestone quarry pool where he catches one and dissects its stomach contents, and a laboratory entry describing the preparation of a grass snake skull. Together these records show the young naturalist moving between field observation and anatomical investigation — the twin poles of his early scientific practice.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's most sustained description of the grass snake comes within his atmospheric portrait of the quarry pool — a place whose horror fascinates him:

> "Occasionally a Grass-snake would swim across the pool and once I caught one and on opening his stomach found a large fire-bellied Eft inside." (003-905)

The image is stark: the snake has swallowed a fire-bellied eft (newt), and Barbellion's dissection reveals it. The passage sits within a longer description that renders the pool almost mythological — stagnant water, fetid air, marsh gas — the grass snake gliding through it as a native inhabitant of this "horrific" place.

Earlier, he had recorded a different kind of serpent encounter: a Red Viper (*Vipera rubra*, as he tentatively identifies it) captured during assize court reporting, carried home by train in his overcoat pocket, only to revive dramatically on the drawing-room floor (002-072). This adder episode — comic and alarming — demonstrates Barbellion's characteristic blend of scientific risk-taking and youthful recklessness.

The anatomical work is recorded with dark humour:

> "Prepared the skull of grass snake. I fancy I scooped out the eyes with patent delight — I suppose symbolically, as though, on behalf of the rest of suffering humanity, I were wiping off the old score against the beast for its behaviour in the Garden of Eden." (002-250)

## Natural History

The grass snake is Britain's largest native snake, reaching up to 150 cm. It is non-venomous, olive-green with a characteristic yellow-and-black collar behind the head. Typically found near water, it preys primarily on amphibians — Barbellion's discovery of the eft in the snake's stomach is a textbook example of its diet. Grass snakes are strong swimmers and may hunt underwater. Unlike the adder (*Vipera berus*), the grass snake does not constrict or envenomate its prey; it swallows it alive.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's interest in British reptiles connects to his broader anatomical investigations. He dissected multiple species, including frogs, leeches, and the lamprey *Petromyzon fluviatilis* — all collected personally. The grass snake, as a vertebrate, offered a more complex anatomical challenge than the invertebrates that dominated his early dissections. His "patent delight" in scooping out the eyes, however mock-biblical the framing, reveals the visceral satisfaction the young naturalist found in hands-on anatomical work.
