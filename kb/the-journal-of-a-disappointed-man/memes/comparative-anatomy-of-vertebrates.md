**Comparative Anatomy of Vertebrates** (Grundriss der vergleichenden Anatomie der Wirbeltiere), [[Robert Wiedersheim]]'s standard textbook, was a formative text in Barbellion's zoological education—a work he once knew intimately enough to recite. In the emotional economy of the journal, it became a symbol of the scientific self he was leaving behind as music and literature claimed his devotion.

## The Former Devotion

Barbellion looked back on his younger self—the obsessive [[science|zoology]] student who had poured over Wiedersheim's pages—with a mixture of nostalgia and detachment. The textbook represented an enthusiasm that had [[honey-bee|bee]]n genuine but was now definitively superseded:

> I am haunted by the memory of how I have sloughed off one enthusiasm after another. I used to dissect snails in a pie-dish in the kitchen while Mother baked the cakes—the unravelling of the internal economy of a Helix caused as great an emotional storm as today the Unfinished Symphony does! … Wiedersheim (good old Wiedersheim) is now deposed by a text book on Harmony. (003-697)

## A Shabby Figure

In a passage of self-criticism, Barbellion measured his younger self against Wiedersheim's textbook and found the comparison wanting. The student who could recite anatomical details had cut, he felt, a "pitiful" figure—narrow, monomaniacal, lacking the breadth of a fully realised life:

> I preferred Wiedersheim's *Comparative Anatomy of Vertebrates*. I used to take it to bed with me and read it as another might take a French novel. A shabby, pitiful figure I must have cut in those days—a pitiful myopia. (003-599)

## Caressing the Past

Later, during the war years, Barbellion encountered the textbook again and found that it had become a relic—an object to be handled with the tender irony reserved for the enthusiasms of youth:

> "I am caressing my past," I answered. "Wiedersheim's *Comparative Anatomy of Vertebrates*, Smith Woodward's *Vertebrate Paleontology*—why it's like visiting old prospects and seeing how the moss has grown over the stones." I hummed a comic song and then said: "As I can't burn the house down, I shall go to bed." (004-284)
