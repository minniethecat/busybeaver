## Overview

George Bernard Shaw (1856–1950) was an Irish playwright, critic, and polemicist whose iconoclastic wit and social commentary made him one of the most influential literary figures of the early twentieth century. Barbellion encountered Shaw's ideas through his [[literature|writing]], his public lectures at the Fabian Society, and Shaw's broader [[fame|reputation]] as a provocateur. Barbellion's response was characteristically ambivalent — he admired Shaw's intellectual courage while finding him, in person, "mid-Victorian" and tedious (003-629).

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's engagement with Shaw operated on two levels. Intellectually, he absorbed Shaw's maxims — notably "Get what you like or you will be forced to like what you get" (003-002) — and recognised Shaw as a representative of the modern, unsentimental worldview he himself aspired to. At the Fabian Society, however, the reality of Shaw as a public speaker disappointed him: "Shaw bored me. He is mid-Victorian" (003-629). This tension between intellectual admiration and personal disappointment mirrors Barbellion's broader pattern of ambivalent hero-worship throughout the Journal.

## References in the Journal

Barbellion's earliest reference to Shaw appears in Part II, where he recalls Shaw's aphorism about desire and resignation while musing at the [[natural-history-museum-london|Natural History Museum]] (003-002). He later made plans to attend a Fabian Society lecture specifically to hear Shaw speak, procrastinating before finally going (003-627–628). The encounter was anticlimactic — Shaw's Victorian manner disappointed Barbellion's expectation of a modern firebrand (003-629).

Later passages reveal Shaw's lasting influence: Barbellion describes a fellow acquaintance as "a follower of Shaw and a devotee of Bunyan" (003-762), a pairing that captures the odd intellectual alliances of the period. In a moment of self-analysis, Barbellion declares that "Shaw is our great exemplar" of intellectual honesty (003-1004). By Part III, he dismisses "Mr. Shaw and his Scintillations" alongside comfortable essayists as insufficient for the profundity of real life (004-229).

> "I muse on Bernard Shaw's saying, 'Get what you like or you will be forced to like what you get.'" (003-002)

> "Shaw bored me. He is mid-Victorian." (003-629)

## Biographical Note

Shaw was a leading figure of the Fabian Society, a socialist organisation that advocated gradual reform rather than revolution. By the time Barbellion encountered him (c. 1912–1914), Shaw had already written major works including *Man and Superman* (1903) and *Major Barbara* (1905). He would go on to win the Nobel Prize in [[literature|Literature]] in 1925. Shaw's combination of socialist politics, creative evolution philosophy, and theatrical innovation made him one of the most distinctive voices of the Edwardian era.
