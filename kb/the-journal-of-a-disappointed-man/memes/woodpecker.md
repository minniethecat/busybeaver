## Overview

The **Woodpecker** (most likely the Great Spotted Woodpecker, *Dendrocopos major*) is a striking woodland bird known for its drumming display and tree-clinging behaviour. Barbellion noted the woodpecker during his early natural history rambles through the Devon countryside, where the species is a resident breeder in mature broadleaf woods.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's encounters with the woodpecker belong to his early years of active field observation, when every bird sighting was an event to be recorded and savoured. Though his journal entries on the woodpecker are brief, they are embedded in a naturalist's consciousness that extends across the entire work:

> The woodpecker's drumming echoed through the copse — a sound that, once learned, is never mistaken for anything else. (PN=002-012)

The woodpecker appears in the same landscape as his squirrel observations, the two species sharing the patchwork of hedgerow, copse, and mature timber that characterised the Devon farmland of Barbellion's youth.

## Natural History

The Great Spotted Woodpecker is the commonest and most widespread of Britain's three woodpecker species. Its drumming — a rapid, resonant tattoo delivered against a dead branch — serves both as a territorial signal and a means of excavating nest holes and foraging for wood-boring insect larvae. The species is resident year-round in British woodlands, and its striking black, white, and red plumage makes it one of the most readily identified woodland birds.

## Taxonomic Context

Three woodpecker species breed in Britain: the Great Spotted (*Dendrocopos major*), the Lesser Spotted (*Dryobates minor*), and the Green Woodpecker (*Picus viridis*). Barbellion does not specify which species he observed, but the Great Spotted is by far the most likely — it is the most common, the most vocal, and the most frequently encountered in the mixed farmland-woodland mosaic of lowland Devon.
