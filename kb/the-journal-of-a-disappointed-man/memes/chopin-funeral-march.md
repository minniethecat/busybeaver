**Chopin's Funeral March** (Marche funèbre, the third movement of the Piano Sonata No. 2 in B-flat minor, Op. 35), was among the works Barbellion heard during his musical awakening in [[london|London]]. The piece—one of the most recognizable in the Western canon—appears twice in his journal: first at a monumental concert at the [[royal-albert-hall|Royal Albert Hall]], and later in a solo violin arrangement that stirred a desire to arrest time.

## At the Albert Hall

The Funeral March was performed as part of the *Empress of Ireland* Memorial Concert at the [[royal-albert-hall|Royal Albert Hall]], programmed alongside [[symphonie-pathetique|Tchaikovsky's Pathétique]], Wagner's "Trauermarsch" from *Götterdämmerung*, the "Ride of the Valkyries," and a solemn Bach melody—a programme of mourning and transcendence that Barbellion described as "a mountain peak in my existence." (003-491)

## A Later Hearing

Barbellion encountered the Funeral March again, this time in an arrangement that isolated its melodic grief:

> Heard Chopin's Funeral March and other things. It would be fascinating to trace the development of one's emotional reactions to a great work of art. (003-682)

## The Desire to Preserve

A third reference captures a moment of acute sensitivity—the Funeral March played on solo violin, producing an effect so piercing that Barbellion wished to suspend time itself:

> I wanted the moment preserved—as if one could bottle the essence of a moment like a specimen in a jar—for solo violin in Chopin's Funeral March. (003-687)

The image of bottling a moment like a zoological specimen—the [[natural-history|naturalist]]'s instinct applied to aesthetic experience—is characteristic of Barbellion's sensibility.
