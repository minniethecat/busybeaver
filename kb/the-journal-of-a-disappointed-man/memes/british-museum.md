The **British Museum** was the parent institution of the [[natural-history-museum-org|Natural History Museum]] at [[south-kensington|South Kensington]] where Barbellion secured employment in 1911. Although the natural history collections had been physically separated from the main museum in Bloomsbury since 1881, they remained administratively part of the British Museum until 1963, and Barbellion's appointment was formally to the British Museum (Natural History).

## Applying for a Vacancy

Barbellion's path to the British Museum began with an examination and a letter from the Keeper of Zoology:

> I received a letter from the Keeper of Zoology at the British Museum, advising me of three vacancies. (002-126)

The news of vacancies came at a crucial moment in Barbellion's life, offering an escape from his unhappy work as a newspaper reporter. He sat the examination with high hopes:

> Exam, for two vacancies in the British Museum. Good luck this. (002-243)

## Beginning Duties

The day before starting work, Barbellion recorded the event with a mixture of excitement and characteristic self-awareness:

> Tomorrow I begin duties at the British Museum of Natural History. (002-290)

Years later, looking back, he reflected on the institution with the ironic distance of experience:

> On my first arrival at that great institution so grandly styled the British Museum (N.H.), I was enormously impressed. (004-513)

## The Reading Room

Beyond his employment, Barbellion also used the British Museum's famous Reading Room in Bloomsbury—the great circular library where [[karl-marx|Karl Marx]], [[lenin|Lenin]], and countless other scholars had worked. He recorded a visit simply:

> The British Museum Reading Room. (003-325)
