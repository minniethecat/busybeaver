## Overview

The *Fortnightly Review* was one of the most influential periodicals of late-Victorian and Edwardian Britain. Founded in 1865 by Anthony Trollope and Frederic Harrison, it published serious essays on politics, literature, science, and philosophy by leading intellectuals of the day. Unlike its name implied, it actually appeared monthly from 1866 onward. By Barbellion's time in the early 1910s, it remained a respected venue for long-form intellectual journalism, having published figures such as John Morley, T.H. [[thomas-huxley|Huxley]], Walter Bagehot, and [[george-eliot|George Eliot]]. A successful submission to the *Fortnightly* would have marked a significant step up from Barbellion's newspaper journalism.

## Barbellion's Engagement

Barbellion's interaction with the *Fortnightly Review* was marked by rejection. A young and ambitious writer seeking to establish himself beyond newspaper journalism, he submitted an article to the periodical. Its return dealt a sharp blow to his morale:

> It caused me anguish to see my article returned from the *Fortnightly* and lying in a big envelope on the table when I returned home this evening. I can't do any work because of it, and in desperation rushed off to the stately pleasure domes of the White City, and systematically went through all the thrills — from the Mountain Railway to the Wiggle Woggle and the 'Witching Waves. [PN 003-025]

### The Rejection and Its Context

The episode captures a recurring pattern in Barbellion's life: his literary and intellectual ambitions repeatedly frustrated by the realities of the publishing world. The *Fortnightly* rejection must be read against the broader arc of his submissions:

- The **[[the-english-review|English Review]]** returned his essay with a polite, wounding note — "I wish I could use this, but I am really too full" (003-359)
- The **[[american-forum|American Forum]]** published two of his articles but failed to pay him (004-517)
- His anonymous paper with R—— in the **[[american-naturalist|American Naturalist]]** was, by contrast, a "rare jewel" (004-514) — scientific [[fame|recognition]], but without public credit

The *Fortnightly* thus belongs to a pattern of near-misses and partial validations. The rejection is not a single event but an accumulating weight.

### The Escape to White City

Barbellion's response to the rejection is characteristic. Unable to work — paralysed by disappointment — he flees to the amusements of White City, methodically riding every thrill from "the Mountain Railway to the Wiggle Woggle." This oscillation between intense ambition and self-consolatory distraction is a signature rhythm of the journal. The White City episode functions as a release valve: physical sensation crowds out mental anguish. Yet the escape is itself a confession — a man who cannot bear to sit with his own [[disappointment|failure]].

### The Stakes

What was at stake in this rejection? Barbellion was a young man without a university degree, working a low-grade position at the [[natural-history-museum-london|Natural History Museum]], living in rented rooms. Publication in a serious intellectual quarterly would have [[honey-bee|bee]]n not merely a professional credential but an existential validation — proof that his relentless self-education and [[ambition|literary ambition]] were not delusional. The *Fortnightly*'s rejection was therefore more than a career setback; it was a challenge to his entire project of self-making.
