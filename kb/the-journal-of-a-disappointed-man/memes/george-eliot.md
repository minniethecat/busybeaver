## Overview

George Eliot (1819–1880), the pen name of Mary Ann Evans, was one of the most important Victorian novelists. Her works — *Middlemarch*, *Daniel Deronda*, *The Mill on the Floss* — combined psychological depth with broad social vision. She enters Barbellion's Journal obliquely, through a personal connection: Barbellion knew someone "who was once the friend of George Eliot, George Henry Lewes, and Herbert Spencer" (004-517), linking the diarist's modest intellectual circle to the giants of the preceding generation.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion never met Eliot (she died when he was a child), but her presence in the Journal signifies the intellectual lineage he aspired to join. The intermediary figure — a person who had known Eliot, Lewes, and Spencer personally — bridged Barbellion's world of working-class self-education and the great Victorian intellectual tradition. For Barbellion, a young man constructing his identity through voracious [[literature|reading]], Eliot represented the summit of literary achievement: the novelist as philosopher, the writer who understood both emotional depth and social structure.

## References in the Journal

The single but significant reference to George Eliot appears in Part III, where Barbellion describes a connection through an acquaintance:

> "A soul who was once the friend of George Eliot, George Henry Lewes, and Herbert Spencer" (004-517)

This passage places Eliot in a constellation with Lewes (her long-term partner and a prominent philosopher-critic) and Herbert Spencer (the philosopher of evolution). For Barbellion, the chain of personal acquaintance — however attenuated — validated his own intellectual ambitions.

Though Eliot is mentioned only once by name, her influence permeates the Journal's sensibility: the marriage plot, the tension between individual desire and social duty, and the psychological realism that Barbellion brings to his self-portraiture all reflect a post-Eliot literary consciousness.

## Biographical Note

Born Mary Ann Evans in Warwickshire, Eliot adopted her male pen name to ensure her fiction would be taken seriously in a literary culture that dismissed women novelists. Her partnership with George Henry Lewes, a married man unable to obtain a divorce, scandalised Victorian society. Her novels are distinguished by their moral seriousness, psychological penetration, and narrative innovation. She died in 1880, nine years before Barbellion was born.
