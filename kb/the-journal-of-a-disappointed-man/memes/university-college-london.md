**University College London** (UCL) was one of Britain's leading universities during the Edwardian era, known for its strength in the natural sciences and its secular founding principles.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion, largely self-taught in zoology, encountered formal university-level scientific instruction through lectures at UCL:

> …embryological lectures at University College with the more detailed account… (003-105)

His reference to these lectures is tinged with his characteristic ambivalence toward institutional learning—he admired the rigour yet resented the privilege of access that formal education represented, a privilege largely denied to him by his own modest circumstances and deteriorating health.

## In the Journal

Barbellion's relationship with academic institutions was complex. Having secured his position at the [[natural-history-museum-org|Natural History Museum]] through self-study and examination rather than university credential, he simultaneously envied and distrusted the university-educated. University College represented a world of systematic scientific training that he could only observe from the outside.

## Historical Context

Founded in 1826 as "London University," UCL was the first university in England to admit students regardless of religion, and among the first to admit women on equal terms. Its scientific faculty included many of the leading biologists and zoologists of the Victorian and Edwardian periods.
