## Overview

The **Seagull** — more properly the Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus*) or one of its close relatives — appears twice in Barbellion's Journal: once in a domestic scene of boyhood collecting, and once in a moment of piercing adult self-awareness in a London park. Between the two episodes, the gull serves as an unwitting mirror for Barbellion's evolving sensibility.

## Observations in the Journal

The first mention belongs to Barbellion's boyhood, a scene of domestic natural history in progress:

> Frankie is blowing Seagulls' eggs in the scullery. (PN=002-101)

The note is characteristically terse — a snapshot of a household where egg-blowing (the preservation of empty shells for a collection) was a routine activity, and natural history was woven into the fabric of family life.

Years later, in London, the gull re-emerges in a strikingly different register:

> Pleased with myself and everybody till a seagull soared overhead in Kensington Gardens and aroused my vast capacities for envy — I wish I could fly. (PN=003-449)

The entry distills Barbellion's distinctive tone: self-mockery, yearning, and the reflex of a naturalist who cannot see a bird without measuring himself against it. The seagull's effortless flight triggers not gentle admiration but an access of envy — a response he knows to be absurd and yet cannot suppress.

## Natural History

Herring Gulls are large, intelligent, opportunistic feeders found along Britain's entire coastline and increasingly inland, where they exploit landfill sites and urban food sources. Their presence in Kensington Gardens would have been unremarkable — London's parks have long attracted gulls, especially in winter. The species is noted for its complex vocalisations and social behaviour, and for a flight — powerful, buoyant, seemingly effortless — that Barbellion found both beautiful and provoking.
