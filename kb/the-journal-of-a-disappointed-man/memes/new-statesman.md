**The New Statesman**, a British political and cultural weekly founded in 1913 by [[Sidney Webb|Sidney]] and [[Beatrice Webb]], was among the periodicals Barbellion consumed during his evenings of concentrated [[literature|reading]]. Still in its earliest years when it appears in the journal, the magazine had already established itself as a voice of [[Fabian socialism]] and progressive opinion.

## An Evening's Reading

Barbellion mentioned the *New Statesman* as the last item in a long day of [[literature|reading]]—a footnote to an evening that began with the *[[dublin-review|Dublin Review]]* and the *[[north-american-review|North American Review]]*:

> After dinner, read: *Evening Standard*, *Saturday Westminster*, and the *New Statesman*. Smoked six cigarettes and went to bed. Tomorrow Fifth Symphony of Beethoven. (003-596)

The flatness of the entry—a simple list, followed by cigarettes and sleep—captures the rhythm of Barbellion's evenings: solitary, bookish, suspended between the workday and the promise of music to come.

## A Type and a Caricature

On another occasion, Barbellion deployed the *New Statesman* as a social marker—a prop that identified a certain kind of person:

> The sort of person whose Sunday morning will see you with *The New Statesman* under your arm. (003-753)

The remark carries a trace of mockery—Barbellion's characteristic ambivalence toward the intellectual circles he both belonged to and felt excluded from by class, health, and circumstance.
