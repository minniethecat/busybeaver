The **Science Museum**, located on Exhibition Road in South Kensington, originated from the scientific and technological collections of the South Kensington Museum (founded 1857). By the Edwardian period, alongside its neighbour the [[natural-history-museum-org|Natural History Museum]], it was emerging as an independent institution. Its library served as a resource for scientific researchers and self-taught students alike.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion's visit to the Science Museum Library came at a moment of acute psychological crisis—and was itself a therapeutic act:

> After four days of the most profound depression of spirits, bitterness, self-distrust, despair, I emerged from the cloud today quite suddenly (probably the arsenic and strychnine begins to take effect) and walked up Exhibition Road with the intention of visiting the Science Museum Library so as to refer to Schafer's *Essentials of Histology* (I have to watch myself carefully so that I may act *at once* as soon as the balance of mind is restored). (003-chapter)

The passage is remarkable for its clinical self-observation. Barbellion notes his emergence from depression with the detachment of a physician charting symptoms: the "arsenic and strychnine" (standard treatments of the era), the "cloud," the "balance of mind." And his chosen act upon this restoration is equally telling: not socialising, not resting, but walking to a scientific library to consult a histology textbook.

## In the Journal

The Science Museum Library visit illuminates a central theme of the journal: the relationship between scientific work and psychological survival. For Barbellion, science was not merely a profession but a means of maintaining mental equilibrium. The deliberate movement toward the library—"I have to watch myself carefully so that I may act *at once*"—is a self-administered treatment, as calculated as the arsenic and strychnine.

But the library visit is also shadowed by the fragility of his condition. The narrative immediately preceding this passage describes a woman "screaming as if in pain" in the museum lobby, with a passerby saying sternly, "What is the matter with you?"—as if suffering in public were a form of misconduct. This vignette of public distress and its policing mirrors Barbellion's own situation: a man in pain, managing his suffering in public, aware that its visible expression would be treated as a social transgression.

The South Kensington scientific complex—the Natural History Museum, the Science Museum, the [[victoria-and-albert-museum|Victoria and Albert Museum]]—formed the institutional geography within which Barbellion's career and inner life took shape. He worked at one museum, used the library of another, and walked the same streets as the professors whose approval he sought and resented.

## Historical Context

The Science Museum achieved full independence from the Victoria and Albert Museum in 1909. Its collections spanned the history of science, technology, and medicine, and its library held journals and textbooks essential to the working scientist. For Barbellion, whose formal scientific education was limited, libraries like this were a substitute for the university education he never received.
