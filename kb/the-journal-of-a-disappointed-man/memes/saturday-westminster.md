## About

The *Saturday Westminster* (also known as the *Saturday Westminster Gazette*) was a [[london|London]] newspaper published in the early twentieth century. It was one of several evening and weekend papers competing for the [[london|London]] readership in the years before the First World War.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion mentions the *Saturday Westminster* in a single passage that vividly captures his voracious [[literature|reading]] habits. On an October evening in 1913, after a day at the Library, he catalogues his evening's [[illness|consumption]]:

> Returned to the Library and read the Dublin Review (article on Samuel Butler), North American Review (one on Henry James) and dined at seven. After dinner, read: Evening Standard, Saturday Westminster, and the New Statesman. Smoked six cigarettes and went to bed. Tomorrow Fifth Symphony of Beethoven.

(003-596)

The passage is remarkable for its density of cultural intake compressed into a single evening: two heavyweight quarterly reviews, three newspapers, six cigarettes, and the prospect of [[beethoven|Beethoven]] the following day. The *Saturday Westminster* appears here as one element in Barbellion's systematic programme of self-education through print — a newspaper consumed not casually but as part of a deliberate intellectual regimen.

The conjunction of the *Saturday Westminster* with the [[evening-standard]] and [[new-statesman]] places it in the ecology of progressive [[london|London]] journalism that Barbellion inhabited. These were the papers of the politically engaged, intellectually curious lower-middle class — the milieu from which Barbellion was striving to rise.
