**Schubert's Unfinished Symphony** (Symphony No. 8 in B minor, D. 759) was a work Barbellion cited as the emblem of his transformation from zoologist to music-lover. Its emotional impact, he declared, equalled the dissections and nature studies that had once absorbed his youth—a measure of the symphony's power to reorient a life.

## The Measure of Passion

In one of the journal's most revealing passages about the displacement of science by art, Barbellion compared the emotional storm produced by Schubert's symphony to the fervour of his boyhood dissections:

> I am haunted by the memory of how I have sloughed off one enthusiasm after another. I used to dissect snails in a pie-dish in the kitchen while Mother baked the cakes—the unravelling of the internal economy of a Helix caused as great an emotional storm as today the Unfinished Symphony does! (003-697)

## A Longing to Hear

Barbellion's engagement with the symphony was not merely passive. He expressed a desire to have it performed for him—a yearning for the direct experience of the music rather than acquaintance through [[fame|reputation]]:

> I would give anything to be alone with the man to have Schubert's Unfinished Symphony played to us, so as to watch his face. (003-809)

The wish to watch another's face during the performance—to see the symphony's effect registered in a human countenance—reveals Barbellion's fascination with the psychology of aesthetic response.
