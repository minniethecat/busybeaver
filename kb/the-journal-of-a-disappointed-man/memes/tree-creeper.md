## Overview

The Eurasian tree creeper (*Certhia familiaris*) appears in a single journal entry from Barbellion's earliest recorded natural history rambles. The observation — a close-range encounter during a catapult-shooting outing with his friend L—— — is characteristically precise: the bird is in a hedge, about a foot away, and the young naturalist's language is already that of the sporting field ("wonderful shots"). The entry captures the boyish, pre-professional phase of Barbellion's naturalist development.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's tree creeper sighting occurs during the same January 1903 walk that yields a tentative goldfinch identification and an episode of duck-poaching:

> "Went with L—— out catapult shooting. While walking down the main road saw a Goldfinch, but very indistinctly — it might not have been one. Had some wonderful shots at a tree creeper in the hedge about a foot away from me." (002-004)

The "wonderful shots" are, of course, with a catapult — not a camera. The fourteen-year-old Barbellion and his friend L—— are out to shoot birds, not to photograph them. This is an important reminder of the transformation Barbellion would undergo: the boy with the catapult becomes the man who dissects, measures, and publishes scientific observations. The tree creeper, at a foot's distance, is the kind of intimate encounter that would later fuel Barbellion's naturalist vocation even after he had put the catapult aside.

## Natural History

The Eurasian tree creeper is a small passerine (12.5 cm), brown above with intricate streaking that provides excellent camouflage against bark. It feeds by spiralling up tree trunks, probing crevices with its fine, decurved bill for insects and spiders. Unlike the nuthatch, it cannot descend head-first; instead, it flies to the base of a new tree and spirals up again. Its song is a thin, high-pitched trill, easily overlooked. Tree creepers are resident in Britain, inhabiting deciduous and mixed woodland, parks, and mature hedgerows — precisely the habitat where Barbellion encountered his.

## Taxonomic Context

The tree creeper belongs to the family Certhiidae, a small group of bark-foraging passerines. As an unobtrusive woodland specialist, it represents a different category of bird from the showy finches and game birds that dominate Barbellion's early egg-collecting lists. Its inclusion in the diary — even in a single entry — signals the young naturalist's broadening attention beyond the obvious and the collectible, toward the full diversity of the avian life around him.
