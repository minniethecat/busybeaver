[[fleet-street]], the historic centre of British newspaper publishing, was crossed by Barbellion on an ambitious walking route through London that took him from Mark Lane to [[Tower Bridge]], through [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]] and St. Paul's, and as far as [[Charing Cross]]. His journal captures the shifting character of the street—a transitional zone between the working-class East End and the more polished West.

## A London Ramble

Barbellion recorded his walk in a single breathless sentence that maps the geography of his London:

> Went to Mark Lane by train, then walked over the Tower Bridge, and back along Lower Thames Street to London Bridge, up to Whitechapel, St. Paul's, Fleet Street, and Charing Cross, and so home. (003-587)

## The Dirty Little Bookshop

On Fleet Street, Barbellion paused at a bookshop whose display captured a certain literary seediness:

> Loitered at a dirty little Fleet Street bookshop where Paul de Koch's *The Lady with the Three Pairs of Stays* was displayed prominently beside a picture of Oscar Wilde. (003-592)

## The Changing Street

Barbellion was alert to the way London's streets changed character along their length, and Fleet Street marked a threshold between two worlds:

> In Fleet Street, you exchange the Whitechapel sausage restaurants for Taverns with "snacks at the bar," and the chestnut roasters, with their buckets of red-hot coals, for Grub Street camp followers, selling *L'Indépendance Belge* or pamphlets entitled, "Why We Went to War." (003-593)

The juxtaposition of the chestnut roasters of the East End with the Grub Street pamphleteers—peddlers of wartime propaganda rather than literature—captured both the social geography of London and the atmosphere of a city at war.
