**Ancient Hunters and Their Modern Representatives**, [[William Johnson Sollas]]'s popular account of prehistoric human life, was a book that Barbellion read with the kind of excitement he usually reserved for music and literature. Published in 1911, it brought the emerging science of paleoanthropology to a general audience, and for Barbellion it offered a thrilling imaginative escape from the confines of his desk at the [[natural-history-museum-london|Natural History Museum]].

## A Thrilling Read

Barbellion recorded his encounter with Sollas's book in a brief but charged entry:

> Sollas's *Ancient Hunters*. Read Sollas's book *Ancient Hunters*—very thrilling—mind focused on prehistoric man. (003-112, 003-113)

The double mention—first the title alone, then the reaction—suggests a work that seized his attention immediately. The phrase "mind focused on prehistoric man" captures the book's effect: a complete absorption in a world remote from the "Economic Entomology" and statistical drudgery of his daily work.

## The Naturalist's Imagination

For Barbellion, *Ancient Hunters* belonged to the tradition of scientific literature that fired his boyhood ambitions—[[literature|books]] like [[Waterton]]'s *Wanderings* and the works of [[Gilbert White]] that had once made him dream of a life in field biology. Now, in his mid-twenties, confined to museum routine and conscious of declining health, such [[literature|books]] offered a temporary reprieve—a "Balaclava Charge" of the mind into the deep past.
