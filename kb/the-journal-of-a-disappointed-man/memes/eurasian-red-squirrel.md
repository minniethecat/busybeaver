## Overview

The Eurasian Red Squirrel (*Sciurus vulgaris*) is Britain's native tree squirrel, a reddish-brown arboreal rodent with distinctive ear tufts. Barbellion's squirrel encounter, recorded in January 1903, is one of the longest continuous naturalist narratives in the early Journal and doubles as a comedy of manners — the chase through the woods, the unhinged gate, and the confrontation with an angry farmer.

## Observations in the Journal

The squirrel episode unfolds over a single afternoon ramble with L——. Having failed to find any squirrels initially, the pair were about to give up:

> "We could not find one and were just wondering if we should draw blank when L—— noticed one clinging to the bark of a tree with a nut in its mouth." (002-012)

What follows is a kinetic chase scene: "We gave it a good chase, but it escaped into the thickest part of the fir tree, still carrying the nut, and we gave up firing at it." L——, frustrated by the lack of sport, "unhinged a gate which he carried two yards into a copse, and threw it on the ground" (002-012).

The squirrel reappears, and the pursuit intensifies:

> "He saw the Squirrel again and jumped over the hedge into the copse, chasing it from tree to tree with his catty. Having lost it, he climbed a fir tree into a Squirrel's drey at the top and sat there on the tree top." (002-012)

The chaos is completed by the arrival of an angry farmer who catches Barbellion — in the act of attempting to replace the gate — "menacing and silent" (002-012). L——, from his perch in the drey, engages in a surreal negotiation:

> "L—— would not say and would not come down... L—— answered that if he did he would 'gob' [i.e. spit] on him. Eventually L—— climbed down and asked the farmer for a glass of cider. The latter gave him his boot and L—— ran away." (002-012)

Barbellion himself fled. The squirrel, the gate, the drey, the farmer — the entire episode is a miniature picaresque, and the squirrel itself is almost incidental to the human drama it precipitates.

## Natural History

Red squirrels are smaller and lighter than the introduced grey squirrel (*Sciurus carolinensis*), which has largely displaced them across England since its introduction in the late nineteenth century. In Barbellion's time (1903), red squirrels were still common in British woodlands. They build dreys — spherical nests of twigs and leaves — high in tree canopies and feed primarily on tree seeds, particularly pine cones.

## Taxonomic Context

As an arboreal rodent in the family Sciuridae, the red squirrel represents the most visible woodland mammal in the British countryside. Barbellion's engagement with mammals was less systematic than his bird and insect observations — squirrels appear in the Journal as characters in human dramas rather than objects of sustained scientific study.
