## Overview

The European starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*) occupies a small but symbolically potent place in Barbellion's journal. H. G. Wells, in his preface, singles out the image of Barbellion watching starlings in the evening sky as the moment when the young diarist transcends self-consciousness. A second sighting — two starlings settling on a cow's back during a May ramble — is recorded with the same precise, appreciative attention that marks Barbellion's best field notes.

## Observations in the Journal

H. G. Wells, introducing the diary to its first readers, describes Barbellion's naturalist's gift as a progression from self-absorption to self-forgetfulness:

> "But he also says 'I am observing nature!' And at moments comes the clear light. He forgets himself in the twilight cave with the bats or watching the starlings in the evening sky, he becomes just you and I and the mind of mankind gathering knowledge." (P02-002)

Wells's choice of the starlings — flocking, murmuring, dispersing — as the emblem of Barbellion's escape from selfhood is deliberate: the starling's collective, sky-filling behaviour mirrors the naturalist's ideal of dissolving the ego in observation.

Barbellion himself records the starlings in a sun-drenched field on C—— Hill:

> "Afar off down in the valley I had come through, in a convenient break in a holly bush, I could just see a Cow sitting on her matronly haunches in a field. She flicked her ears and two starlings settled on her back." (002-097)

The moment is part of a lengthy, almost painterly inventory of a single May afternoon: the red-tipped whortleberry shoots, the caught lizard, the rabbit darting from a sweetbrier bush, a magpie lifting from the hedge. The starlings, settling on a cow's broad back, are one detail among many — yet the vignette captures the unhurried, receptive quality of Barbellion's field observation at its best.

## Natural History

The European starling is a medium-sized passerine with iridescent black plumage, marked in winter with pale speckling. Highly gregarious, it forms large flocks — sometimes numbering hundreds of thousands — that perform spectacular aerial displays known as murmurations at dusk. Starlings are opportunistic feeders, taking insects, seeds, and fruit, and often forage among livestock, as Barbellion observed. They are cavity nesters, competing aggressively with native species for nest sites. Introduced to North America in the 1890s, they have become one of the world's most successful avian species.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion would have known the starling as a common bird of the British countryside — familiar to the point of being overlooked. That he records it at all, and that Wells elevates the starling-watching to symbolic status, reflects the diarist's capacity to find significance in the ordinary. In his egg-collecting season, Barbellion catalogued 232 nests across forty-four species; it is characteristic of his approach that the starling, not some rarity, becomes the emblem of pure observation.
