## Overview

Hammersmith appears in Barbellion's London journal in three distinct registers: as a waypoint on Sunday bus journeys to Richmond and Kew, as the setting for a friend's riverside sketching encounter, and — most remarkably — as the improbable address of the *Westminster Review*'s editor, whose identity Barbellion pursued through a series of increasingly desperate letters. Together these appearances sketch a West London of transit and happenstance, of lives glimpsed from bus tops and bridges.

## In the Journal

Hammersmith first appears as a bus-change stop on a cold Sunday afternoon:

> "In the afternoon, took a bus to Richmond. No room outside, so had to go inside — curse — and sit opposite a row — curse again — of fat, ugly, elderly women, all off to visit their married daughters, the usual Sunday jaunt. At Hammersmith got on the outside, and at Turnham Green was caught in a hail storm." (003-395)

The comic petulance is characteristic — Barbellion at his most unguarded, irritated by fellow passengers and weather alike. On the return journey, three young women (whom two Italian men have been pursuing) disembark at Hammersmith, and Barbellion watches them go with the same detached curiosity he brings to everything (003-403).

A more romantic Hammersmith emerges in a story told by Barbellion's friend R——:

> "While sketching under Hammersmith Bridge yesterday, R—— heard a whistle, and, looking up, saw a charming 'young thing' leaning over the Bridge parapet smiling like the blessed Damozel out of Heaven." (003-509)

The girl comes down, they discuss pictures, he walks her to the Broadway, and — to Barbellion's exasperation — R—— lets her board a bus without exchanging names. "Young man," Barbellion scolds, "you preferred to let go of authentic life at Hammersmith Broadway, so as to return at once to your precious watercolour painting" (003-512).

The strangest Hammersmith story concerns the *Westminster Review*. After submitting a manuscript and receiving no reply for months, Barbellion tracks the editor to an address in Richmond Row, Shepherd's Bush. He writes repeatedly, receives no answer, and eventually learns from a newspaper that the editor — a woman who had once known George Eliot and G. H. Lewes — had been removed from the Hammersmith Workhouse by a charitable visitor shortly before. The anecdote, related at length in the journal's final section (004-517), turns Hammersmith from a bus stop into the setting for a minor literary mystery.

## Significance

Hammersmith functions as a threshold — neither the rural Devon of Barbellion's origins nor the institutional London of his Museum work, but the in-between London of buses, bridges, and brief encounters. It is the London of ordinary movement, where lives intersect without quite touching. The *Westminster Review* anecdote, with its workhouse, its forgotten literary lady, and its unreturned manuscript, gives Hammersmith a peculiar density — as though the neighbourhood were a repository for lost things.
