## Overview

Samuel Butler (1835–1902) was an English novelist, satirist, and critic whose iconoclastic attacks on Victorian pieties made him a hero to the generation that followed. Barbellion encountered Butler as a reader — he read an article on Butler in the *Dublin Review* and absorbed Butler's aphoristic wit — and ultimately named him among the constitutive influences of his intellectual identity: "a ragbag of Smollett, [[h-g-wells|H. G. Wells]], Samuel Butler, the [[daily-news|Daily News]], and The Devil" (004-496).

## Relationship to Barbellion

Butler was a writer Barbellion discovered rather than inherited. Unlike Shakespeare or [[alfred-tennyson|Tennyson]], Butler was not part of the default cultural furniture — [[literature|reading]] him required [[curiosity|intellectual curiosity]] and a taste for the heterodox. Barbellion recorded [[literature|reading]] "the Dublin Review (article on Samuel Butler)" (003-596), suggesting an active effort to understand Butler's significance.

What drew Barbellion to Butler was likely a shared sensibility: Butler's *The Way of All Flesh* (published posthumously in 1903, the year Barbellion's Journal begins) is a merciless dissection of Victorian family life and religious hypocrisy, narrated with a candour that anticipates Barbellion's own tone. Butler's aphorism — "Why should not chicken be egg?" (003-883) — captures the logical playfulness that appealed to Barbellion's scientific mind.

Barbellion also noted a practical piece of Butler's advice: "Samuel Butler (of Erewhon) advised writers to keep their manuscripts..." (003-789), a reflection of Barbellion's own preoccupation with the fate of his [[literature|writing]] after his death.

## Biographical Note

Butler was the grandson of a bishop and was intended for the Church, but religious doubts led him to emigrate to New Zealand, where he became a successful sheep farmer. He returned to England in 1864 and published *Erewhon* (1872), a satirical utopia that attacked Victorian religion, morality, and Darwinism. His semi-autobiographical novel *The Way of All Flesh*, written between 1873 and 1884, was published posthumously in 1903 — the same year Barbellion's Journal begins. Butler also wrote on evolution, arguing for a neo-Lamarckian position, and on the Homeric question (*The Authoress of the Odyssey*, 1897).
