The **Foreign Office** was the British government department responsible for conducting the United Kingdom's diplomatic relations. During the Edwardian period it occupied a grand Italianate building on Whitehall, and its officials represented the apex of the British civil service elite.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion's single reference to the Foreign Office comes in a moment of acute social comparison:

> …were a high official of the Foreign Office. I, on the contrary, am shy… (003-568)

The juxtaposition—the Foreign Office official, confident and assured, against himself, "shy"—is characteristic of Barbellion's social self-positioning. The Foreign Office represented the established institutional world from which he felt excluded: confident, well-connected, and healthy.

## In the Journal

Barbellion's encounters with institutional power—whether the Foreign Office, the [[devonshire-club|Devonshire Club]], or the [[royal-society|Royal Society]]—repeatedly underscored his sense of being an outsider. His genius was to transform this exclusion into observation, turning his own social awkwardness into a diagnostic instrument with which to dissect Edwardian institutional life.
