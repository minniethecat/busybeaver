## Overview

*Le Rire* (Laughter: An Essay on the Meaning of the Comic), published by [[henri-bergson|Henri Bergson]] in 1900, is one of the philosophical works that Barbellion read during his period of intellectual ferment in [[london|London]]. Listed alongside scientific works in his [[literature|reading]] — "[[henri-bergson|Bergson]]'s *Le Rire*" (002-274) — the book represents Barbellion's characteristic intellectual range, moving easily between science and philosophy, observation and theory.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion's engagement with *Le Rire* is recorded in a brief reference that places it among his intellectual resources. He later applies [[henri-bergson|Bergson]]'s theory of laughter to his own observations (003-943), suggesting that the book was not merely read but absorbed and put to use. This practical relationship to philosophy — ideas as tools rather than objects of contemplation — is characteristic of Barbellion's intellectual style.

## Significance

*Le Rire* represents the philosophical dimension of Barbellion's [[literature|reading]] — the attempt to understand not only nature (through science) but human experience (through philosophy). [[henri-bergson|Bergson]]'s theory that laughter serves a social corrective function would have appealed to Barbellion, whose own journal is, among other things, an extended exercise in self-correction through self-exposure.
