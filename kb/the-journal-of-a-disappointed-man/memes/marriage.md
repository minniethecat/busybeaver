## Overview

Marriage is one of the central thematic arcs of Barbellion's journal, particularly in the second half. It begins as an abstract topic of late-night conversation, becomes an object of intense psychological conflict, and finally arrives as a lived reality — one profoundly altered by his deteriorating health. Barbellion's treatment of marriage is characteristically self-lacerating: he examines his own motives with suspicion, chronicles his fears with clinical precision, and finally confronts the question of whether he has done his wife an injustice by marrying her at all.

## In the Journal

### Courtship and Hesitation

The journal's early references to marriage are filtered through intellectual, almost anthropological observation. Visiting two young women, Barbellion notes with amused detachment that they were "simply sodden with the idea of it" (003-126). But this ironic distance soon collapses into personal turmoil. He questions whether he loves deeply enough: "I don't love her enough for marriage. Mine must be a grand passion, a bouleversement — for I am capable of it" (003-411). The real conflict, however, is not about love but about self: "I am such an egotist, I want, I believe, a Princess of the Blood Royal" (003-466).

### The Wedding

The marriage itself is recorded with the journal's characteristic blend of the clinical and the personal. The entry for [[barbellion-marriage|the wedding]] day notes with deadpan brevity: "Successful marriage with abatement of cold" (004-058). This deliberate understatement — reducing one of life's most significant events to a weather-and-health report — is entirely in keeping with Barbellion's voice: self-aware, self-deprecating, unable to resist the deflation of his own drama.

### Married Life and Illness

The reality of married life is increasingly dominated by his illness. On the anniversary of their engagement, he reflects: "How mad the idea of marriage seemed to me — and my instinct was right: if only I had known!" (004-300). The most devastating passage comes when Barbellion discovers that his doctor had warned his wife about his condition before [[barbellion-marriage|the wedding]]: "So it was she who was the victim in marriage! That harassing question: Did she know?" (004-457). The discovery transforms his understanding of the marriage from a personal tragedy into a moral crisis.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion's approach to marriage reflects the Edwardian transition between Victorian ideals of matrimony and emerging modern attitudes. His demand for a "grand passion" echoes Romantic and fin-de-siècle notions of love as transformative. At the same time, his brutal self-analysis — his [[fame|recognition]] of his own egotism, his fear of commitment, his romantic idealism — anticipates the psychological realism of early twentieth-century literature. The journal's treatment of marriage owes something to the confessional tradition of Rousseau and the self-dissecting prose of Dostoevsky.

## Barbellion's Perspective

What distinguishes Barbellion's treatment of marriage is its painful honesty about the asymmetry of his marital situation. The journal does not simply record a man's feelings about getting married; it records a man realising, too late, that his illness has made the marriage fundamentally unequal. His wife knew what she was entering; he did not know that she knew. The discovery shatters his sense of agency and recasts him as the object of a compassion he never sought. Yet in a characteristic move, he does not exempt himself from blame — he calls his own conduct "superlative [[vanity|egoism]] and superlative [[vanity|conceit]]" (004-457).
