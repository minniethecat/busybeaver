## Overview

The diagnosis of multiple sclerosis is the event that reorganises Barbellion's entire existence. Though the journal does not record the diagnosis as a single dramatic scene — it emerges gradually, through medical consultations, symptoms, and dawning recognition — its effects are everywhere in Part III. After the diagnosis, Barbellion describes his life as "entirely posthumous" (004-155): he is, in his own conception, already dead, and the journal becomes a document written from beyond the grave.

## In the Journal

The diagnosis is approached indirectly. Barbellion records symptoms, consultations with doctors, and his own anxious interpretations. The doctor "asked me suspiciously" (003-199) about symptoms — the suspicion is mutual, patient and doctor circling the same dreaded conclusion. When the diagnosis is finally confirmed, Barbellion's response is characteristic: he does not narrate the moment of revelation but instead records its aftermath — the transformation of his relationship to ambition, to marriage, to the journal itself.

## Significance

The diagnosis is the pivot on which the entire journal turns. Before it, Barbellion is a young man with ambitions and disappointments; after it, he is a dying man taking notes. The journal's title — *The Journal of a Disappointed Man* — acquires its full weight only in light of the diagnosis: the ultimate disappointment is not literary rejection but the foreclosure of a future.
