## Overview

Henri Bergson (1859–1941) was the most prominent philosopher of Barbellion's era, and his ideas surface in the journal at moments of particular intellectual excitement. Bergson's philosophy of creative evolution, his concept of the *élan vital*, and his theory of laughter as a social corrective all appealed to Barbellion's [[natural-history|naturalist]] imagination. For a young man confronting his own [[death|mortality]], Bergson's vitalism — with its emphasis on life as a creative, surging force — offered a philosophical counterweight to medical pessimism.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's relationship to Bergson is that of an enthusiastic autodidact. He reads Bergson in bed ("In bed till 12:30 [[literature|reading]] Bergson and the O.T.," 003-162), groups him with other intellectual passions ("Pragmatism and Bishop Berkeley and Bergson have [[honey-bee|bee]]n my favourite bagatelles," 003-865), and applies Bergsonian ideas to his own observations. His interest is practical rather than academic: he wants ideas he can use, and Bergson's vitalism — the insistence that life cannot be reduced to mechanism — offers a philosophical basis for hope.

## References in the Journal

Bergson appears at intervals across Parts I and II. Barbellion expresses a desire to "know Bergson" (002-237), reads *[[le-rire-bergson|Le Rire]]* (002-274) alongside scientific works, and later applies Bergson's theory of laughter: "Bergson's theory is that laughter is..." (003-943). The references are characteristic of Barbellion's intellectual style: enthusiastic, wide-ranging, and personal. He does not critique Bergson; he absorbs him, testing the ideas against his own experience.

## Biographical Note

Henri Bergson (1859–1941) was a French philosopher who became an international [[fame|celebrity]] in the early twentieth century. His major works — *Time and Free Will* (1889), *Matter and Memory* (1896), *Creative Evolution* (1907), and *[[le-rire-bergson|Laughter]]* (1900) — challenged mechanistic and materialist accounts of life and consciousness. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in [[literature|Literature]] in 1927. During Barbellion's [[london|London]] years (1912–1914), Bergson was at the height of his fame.
