## Overview

The Common Adder (*Vipera berus*) is Britain's only venomous snake, a relatively small viper distinguished by a dark zigzag pattern along its back. Barbellion's encounter with an adder — which he identified as the "fourth species of British Serpent" — is one of the most vivid and darkly comic episodes in the Journal, combining field naturalism, physical danger, and the absurd logistics of transporting a venomous snake by public railway.

## Observations in the Journal

The adder episode is among the longest sustained naturalist passages in Part I. Barbellion recounts the entire sequence: the sighting in the Valley of Rocks, the careful immobilisation under his boot, and the enlistment of a passing baker:

> "At the bottom of one of the hills saw a snake, a Red Viper. Put my boot on him quickly so that he couldn't get away and then recognised him as a specimen of what I consider to be the fourth species of British Serpent — *Vipera rubra*." (002-072)

Barbellion's taxonomic claim — *Vipera rubra* as a distinct species — is zoologically dubious (it is almost certainly a colour variant of *V. berus*), but the confidence with which he asserts it reveals the young naturalist's ambition to contribute to scientific knowledge.

The capture required civilian assistance:

> "I stood for some time with my foot so firmly pressed down on its back that my leg ached... I held on and presently hailed a baker's cart coming along the road." (002-072)

The baker provided string, which Barbellion tied to the snake's tail. What follows is a blend of scientific caution and dark farce:

> "I remember that Adders are tenacious of life and so I continue to carry him at string's length and occasionally wallop him against a stone." (002-072)

The journey home is the passage's comic climax:

> "So home by a two hours' railway journey with the adder in the pocket of my overcoat and the overcoat on the rack over my head. Settled down to the reading of a book on Spinoza's *Ethics*." (002-072)

The juxtaposition of Spinoza's rationalist philosophy with the live viper — the string still dangling from his pocket — is quintessential Barbellion: the life of the mind and the life of the body in unruly coexistence.

The final revelation that the apparently dead snake was very much alive:

> "At home it proved to be quite alive, and, on being pulled out by the string, coiled up on the drawing-room floor and hissed in a fury, to my infinite surprise. Finished him off with the poker and so spoilt the skin." (002-072)

The spoiled skin — the naturalist's specimen ruined — is the perfect deflation of a heroic collecting narrative.

## Natural History

Adders are relatively small snakes, typically 50–65 cm, and are the most northerly distributed snake species, found as far north as the Arctic Circle in Scandinavia. They are viviparous (giving birth to live young) and hibernate through the British winter. Their venom is rarely fatal to healthy adult humans but can cause significant pain and swelling. Adders are generally shy and bite only when stepped on or handled — which makes Barbellion's capture method all the more remarkable.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's "fourth species of British Serpent" claim refers to the recognised British species: the Grass Snake (*Natrix natrix*), the Smooth Snake (*Coronella austriaca*), and the Adder (*Vipera berus*). His *Vipera rubra* — the "Red Viper" — likely represents a regional colour morph rather than a distinct species, but the claim reflects the taxonomic enthusiasm of an era when field identification could still yield genuine discoveries.
