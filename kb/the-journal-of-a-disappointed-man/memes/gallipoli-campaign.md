The **Gallipoli Campaign** (1915–1916), the Allied attempt to force the Dardanelles and knock the [[Ottoman Empire]] out of the [[world-war-i|First World War]], reached Barbellion through the filter of news reports and literature. His response to the campaign—and to [[John Masefield]]'s book about it—reveals his characteristic resistance to the sanitisation of war.

## News from the Dardanelles

While Barbellion lay ill and confined, others of his generation were serving abroad. He noted the campaign with a mixture of envy and guilt:

> Others are seeing great adventures in Gallipoli and elsewhere. "The Triumph is gone down"—news of the sinking reaches us. (004-064)

The quotation "The Triumph is gone down" refers to HMS *Triumph*, a battleship torpedoed off Gallipoli in May 1915—one of the naval disasters that marked the campaign.

## Masefield's Gallipoli

In 1916, John Masefield published *Gallipoli*, a prose account that attempted to find meaning and beauty in the failed campaign. Barbellion read it and reacted with characteristic bluntness:

> Masefield's *Gallipoli*. Still, to call Gallipoli "bloody Hell" is, after all, only a nervous tic. (004-376, 004-382)

The comment suggests Barbellion's impatience with literary attempts to dignify the slaughter. His own experience of suffering—personal, intimate, unheroic—left him suspicious of the rhetoric that surrounded the war dead.
