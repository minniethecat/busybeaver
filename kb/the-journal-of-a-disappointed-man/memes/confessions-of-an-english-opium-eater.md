## Overview

*Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* (1821) is [[thomas-de-quincey|Thomas De Quincey]]'s most famous work, an autobiographical account of his addiction to laudanum (opium dissolved in alcohol). The book is divided into two parts: "The Pleasures of Opium" and "The Pains of Opium." It established the confessional addiction narrative as a literary genre and is celebrated for its ornate prose style and vivid description of drug-induced visions. [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] (1785–1859) was a prolific English essayist and a contemporary of Wordsworth and Coleridge.

## Barbellion's Engagement

[[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] appears at multiple moments in Barbellion's journal as a literary and psychological reference point. Five passages trace an arc from literary allusion to existential identification.

### Last Things

Barbellion quotes [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]]'s observation about the distinctive melancholy of final experiences:

> [De Quincey's observation about the sadness of doing things for the last time]

(002-042)

This theme — the poignancy of endings — resonates throughout Barbellion's journal. His acute consciousness of his own [[death|mortality]], sharpened by his progressive illness, transforms [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]]'s philosophical observation into lived experience. Every ramble, every concert, every evening of [[literature|reading]] carries the shadow of being potentially the last.

### Revenge on the Body

In a characteristically dark and combative passage, Barbellion invokes [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]]'s precedent for posthumous anatomical examination:

> I should like though to have the last kick and, copying De Quincey, arrange to hand it over for dissection to the medical men — out of revenge.

(002-204)

The phrase "out of revenge" is striking. Revenge against what? Against a body that has betrayed him, against the medical establishment that cannot cure him, against the cosmic unfairness of his illness. [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] provides the model for turning the passive fact of death into an active, even aggressive, gesture.

### London as Opium Dream

Barbellion's most evocative use of [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] comes in his descriptions of [[london|London]]. The city's sensory intensity exceeds ordinary perception and demands a new descriptive register:

> London bewilders me. At times it is a phantasmagoria, an opium dream out of De Quincey.

(003-474)

The word "phantasmagoria" is carefully chosen — it denotes a sequence of real or imaginary images, like those seen in a magic lantern show, and it belongs recognisably to [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]]'s vocabulary. [[london|London]] is not merely busy or crowded for Barbellion; it is hallucinatory, a waking dream that dissolves the boundary between perception and imagination.

### Music as De Quincey Set to Sound

When Barbellion experiences [[symphony-no-5-beethoven|Beethoven's Fifth]] Symphony, his synaesthetic response again channels [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]]:

> An epileptic vision or an opium dream — Dostoevsky or De Quincey set to music.

(003-889)

The pairing is revealing. [[fyodor-dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] and [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] represent two modes of extremity — the epileptic seizure and the drug vision, the Russian novel and the English confession. [[beethoven|Beethoven]]'s music, for Barbellion, contains both: the violent neurological disruption and the hallucinatory interior journey. [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] becomes, in this passage, not a writer to be read but a mode of experience to be inhabited.

### Death in Summer

In the journal's late passages, as Barbellion's health declines, he returns to [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] for a final, quieter meditation:

> De Quincey who said Death to him seemed most awful in the summer.

(004-470)

The observation is characteristic of [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] — the counter-intuitive inversion that locates horror not in winter's desolation but in summer's abundance. For Barbellion, [[literature|writing]] in his own final chapters, the remark carries the weight of [[fame|recognition]]. [[death|Death]] is most awful precisely when the world is most alive.

### The Arc of a Literary Relationship

Taken together, these five passages trace a deepening relationship. [[thomas-de-quincey|De Quincey]] begins as a quoted authority, becomes a model for defiant action, then a perceptual lens through which to experience [[london|London]] and music, and finally a companion in the contemplation of death. Few other writers in Barbellion's journal — perhaps only [[thus-spake-zarathustra|Nietzsche]] and [[marie-bashkirtseffs-journal|Marie Bashkirtseff]] — are invoked with this degree of intimacy across so many registers of experience.
