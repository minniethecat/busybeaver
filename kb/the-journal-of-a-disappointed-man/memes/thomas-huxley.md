## Overview

Thomas Henry Huxley (1825–1895) was an English biologist known as "Darwin's Bulldog" for his fierce advocacy of evolutionary theory. His accessible scientific [[literature|writing]]s — particularly *The Cray[[atlantic-cod|fish]]: An Introduction to the Study of Zoology* (1880) — formed part of Barbellion's autodidactic curriculum. Huxley was also a hero to the young Barbellion, who kept a photograph of him on the mantelpiece.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Huxley occupied a dual role in Barbellion's intellectual life: as a scientific authority whose text[[literature|books]] guided his zoological education, and as a symbolic figure of the self-made man of science. "Am [[literature|reading]] Huxley's Cray[[atlantic-cod|fish]]," Barbellion notes matter-of-factly (002-050), treating Huxley as a routine part of his daily study. A more emotional connection surfaces later: "On my mantelpiece stands a photograph of Huxley — the hero of my youth" (003-721). Huxley's trajectory — from naval surgeon's assistant to President of [[royal-society|the Royal Society]] — embodied the meritocratic ascent Barbellion aspired to in his own museum career.

## Biographical Note

Huxley trained as a surgeon and served as assistant surgeon on HMS *Rattlesnake* (1846–1850), during which he studied marine invertebrates and sent papers back to [[royal-society|the Royal Society]]. His debate with Bishop Samuel Wilberforce at Oxford in 1860 became legendary — Huxley's defence of Darwinian evolution against ecclesiastical authority. He coined the term "agnosticism" and served as President of [[royal-society|the Royal Society]] from 1883 to 1885. His grandsons included the novelist Aldous Huxley and the biologist Julian Huxley.
