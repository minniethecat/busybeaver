## Overview

The **Chaffinch** (*Fringilla coelebs*) is one of Britain's commonest and most widespread finches, with the male's bright pink breast and blue-grey cap making it a familiar sight in woodland and gardens. Barbellion encountered the chaffinch during his field ornithology, both as a nest-finder and as a teacher of birdsong identification.

## Observations in the Journal

Finding a chaffinch's nest moved Barbellion deeply, reconnecting him with the boyhood passion that had shaped his life:

> It would take too long and be too sentimental for me to record my feelings on looking into the first nest I found — a Chaffinch's, the first wild bird's eggs I have seen for many years. As I stood with an egg between thumb and forefinger, all the old feelings came rushing back. (PN=002-089)

His ear for birdsong extended to teaching others. He tested a farm boy's identification skills by whistling the chaffinch's call:

> I asked young George, the farm-boy, what bird went like this: I whistled it. He looked abashed and said a Chaffinch. I told Miss Lucy who said George was a silly boy, and Miss Lucy told Farmer Whaley who said George ought to know better — it was a Mistle-Thrush. (PN=001-087)

The anecdote is doubly revealing: Barbellion's own ear was being calibrated against local knowledge, and the chaffinch's song was familiar enough to a farm boy that it was the first bird he guessed.

## Natural History

The chaffinch is resident year-round across the British Isles. The male's song is a cheerful descending trill, often transcribed as *chip-chip-chip-chooee-chooee*. The species nests in the fork of a tree or shrub, building a neat cup of moss, grass, and spider silk lined with feathers — the kind of nest Barbellion would have recognized instantly from his boyhood.

## Taxonomic Context

The chaffinch belongs to the finch family (Fringillidae), alongside the [[european-goldfinch]], another species Barbellion observed. His ornithological interests spanned a wide range of British birds including the [[thrush]], [[starling]], and [[tree-creeper]].
