## Overview

Bats (order Chiroptera) are the only mammals capable of sustained flight. Among all the creatures Barbellion observed, bats hold a special distinction: he studied them intensively, kept them alive in captivity, and contributed his observations to *Barrett-Hamilton's Mammals of Great Britain and Ireland*, a standard reference work. His bat work exemplifies the transition from boyhood collecting to serious amateur science.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's most revealing bat reference is a brief but loaded recollection embedded in a broader reflection on his childhood natural history pursuits:

> "I kept live Bats in our upstairs little-used drawing-room, and Newts and Frogs in pans in the backyard." (004-395)

The upstairs drawing-room — a space of middle-class respectability — transformed into a vivarium: this detail captures the indulgence (or tolerance) of his family and the intensity of his naturalist passion. The same entry reveals the professional recognition his observations earned:

> "Those on Bats indeed were thought fit to be included in a standard work — Barrett-Hamilton's Mammals of Great Britain and Ireland." (004-395)

This contribution to a recognised scientific publication is a significant biographical detail — evidence that Barbellion's natural history was not merely diaristic but had achieved a measure of external validation.

In a darker passage, Barbellion uses the bat as a figure for his own diminished state:

> "All day I have crouched over the fire, reading newspapers, listening to fog signals and the screaming of the baby. … I have been in a torpor, like a Bat in a cavern — really dead yet automatically hanging on to life by my hind legs." (004-356)

The bat's torpor — a state of suspended animation — becomes an image of his own depressed, semi-living condition.

## Natural History

British bats (17 resident species) are insectivorous, using echolocation to navigate and hunt in darkness. They roost in buildings, trees, and caves, and hibernate through the winter months. Barbellion's ability to keep bats alive in captivity suggests he provided appropriate food (live insects) and roosting conditions — a non-trivial feat of husbandry. Gerald Edwin Hamilton Barrett-Hamilton's *A History of British Mammals* (1910–1921) was the definitive work of its era.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's bat studies place him in the tradition of the field naturalist who combines observation with capture and close study. His contribution to Barrett-Hamilton's work — even if modest — connects him to the professional mammalogical community of Edwardian Britain. Bats were relatively understudied at this period, and detailed observations of live behaviour by amateur naturalists were genuinely valuable to science.
