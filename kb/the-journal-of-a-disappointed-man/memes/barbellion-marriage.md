## Overview

Barbellion's marriage is one of the journal's central events — a culmination of the romantic uncertainty that dominates Part II and a turning point in the emotional arc of Part III. Recorded with deadpan brevity ("Successful marriage with abatement of cold," 004-058), the marriage is both a genuine union and a moral crisis: Barbellion later discovers that his doctor warned his wife about his condition before the wedding, transforming his understanding of the marriage from a mutual commitment into something more troubling — an act of compassion he never sought.

## In the Journal

The marriage is approached through prolonged hesitation in Part II — "I don't love her enough for marriage" (003-411), "I am jibbing at the idea of marriage" (003-466) — and then arrives with startling suddenness in Part III. The wedding entry itself is a masterpiece of Barbellion's anti-sentimental style: the cold, the success, the abatement — as if marriage were a medical procedure. The later discovery about his doctor's letter (004-457) transforms the retrospective meaning of the event, introducing an asymmetry of knowledge that haunts the journal's final pages.

## Significance

The marriage is both the fulfilment of a long romantic arc and the introduction of a new moral complexity into the journal. Barbellion's question — "Did she know?" (004-457) — becomes a question about the entire enterprise of his life: how much of what happened to him was known in advance by others, and how much was he the author of his own fate?
