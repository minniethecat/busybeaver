## Overview

Fame — the desire for it, the fantasy of it, the bitter awareness of its absence — runs through Barbellion's journal like a secret current. He is too honest to pretend he does not want recognition, and too self-aware not to examine that wanting with suspicion. Fame in the journal is never simple: it is entangled with vanity, with the fear of death, with the hunger for love, and with the conviction — never entirely abandoned — that he deserves to be known.

## In the Journal

### The Early Dream

From his earliest entries, Barbellion measures himself against [[genius|greatness]]. He "secretly decided" as a child that he was a young Macaulay or [[john-ruskin|Ruskin]] (002-149). His early hope was that his scientific work would become his "magnum opus, which is to bring me fame, fortune" (003-165). This fantasy of precocious genius is both endearing and diagnostic: it reveals the scale of his ambition and the template — the Great Man of letters — through which he imagines fame.

### Rejection and Persistence

The journal documents an unbroken record of literary rejection, but Barbellion's belief in his eventual recognition never entirely dies. "I have for years past received my rejected MSS. back from every conceivable kind of periodical, from *[[punch-magazine|Punch]]* to the *Hibbert Journal*" (004-517). The catalogue of [[disappointment|failure]] is recited with a mixture of bitterness and pride — bitterness at the rejections, pride at the persistence. He has not given up, and that, in his own accounting, is a kind of victory.

### Posthumous Fame

As his health fails, Barbellion's fantasy of fame shifts from the present to the future. He hopes "to last long enough to convince others of what I might have done — had I lived" (003-544). The journal itself becomes the instrument of this posthumous ambition: a message in a bottle, addressed to readers who will discover him only after he is gone. The irony — that the journal *would* find its readers, that Barbellion *would* be remembered — is one of literary history's more poignant vindications.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion's relationship with fame reflects the Romantic cult of the neglected genius — [[john-keats|Keats]], who died believing himself a [[disappointment|failure]]; Chatterton, the boy poet who killed himself at seventeen. But Barbellion's attitude is more modern, more psychological. He does not romanticise neglect; he suffers it, analyses it, and persists despite it. His treatment of fame anticipates the twentieth century's more ambivalent relationship with celebrity — the recognition that the desire to be known is not quite noble, not quite shameful, but deeply, inescapably human.

## Barbellion's Perspective

Barbellion's most distinctive contribution to the literature of fame is his refusal to pretend he is above it. He wants to be known, and he knows this wanting is partly vanity, partly the legitimate desire of a serious writer to find an audience, and partly the terrified response of a [[death|dying]] man to the prospect of oblivion. He does not resolve these motives into a single, respectable position. He simply records them all and lets the contradictions stand. This is, in the end, what makes the journal itself deserving of the fame its author craved.
