The **Recruiting Office** was a local military enlistment centre established across Britain during the First World War to process volunteers and, after 1916, conscripts. For Barbellion, exempted from service by the illness he was only beginning to understand, the Recruiting Office was a site of complicated evasion.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion's visit to the Recruiting Office appears in the context of a deeper, unspoken anxiety about what his wife knew of his prognosis:

> I wonder if she knows. I believe she does but I am afraid to broach the matter in case she doesn't. I think she must know something otherwise she would show more alarm over my leg, and when I went to the Recruiting Office she seemed to show no fear whatever lest they took me. (004-078)

The Recruiting Office, in this account, is less a military institution than a test of domestic knowledge: his wife's calm as he went to enlist was not reassurance but evidence that she already understood he would be rejected. The recruiting sergeant's medical examination becomes, in Barbellion's rendering, a mirror in which his own condition is reflected back at him through another's eyes.

## Historical Context

By the time of Barbellion's visit, the Military Service Act 1916 had introduced conscription in Britain. Medical examinations at recruiting offices rejected large numbers of men as physically unfit—a category into which Barbellion, with his undiagnosed multiple sclerosis and visible leg weakness, would certainly have fallen.
