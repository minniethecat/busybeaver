## Overview

Vanity is one of Barbellion's most frequently confessed sins, and his treatment of it exemplifies the journal's distinctive ethical method: acknowledge the worst about oneself, examine it thoroughly, and — crucially — do not pretend that acknowledgment constitutes reform. As he admits in a moment of characteristic candour, "I am such an egotist" (003-466) — and the admission, however sincere, changes nothing. Barbellion knows he is vain. He knows vanity distorts his perceptions and poisons his relationships. He records these insights with clarity and then remains vain. The honesty, in this case, is not therapeutic; it is simply honest.

## In the Journal

### Early Self-Awareness

From his earliest entries, Barbellion is conscious of his own self-regard. After declaring his ambition to be a great [[natural-history|naturalist]], he immediately catches himself: "This entry may suggest that I am horribly conceited. But really I am as humble as possible" (002-033). The qualification is unconvincing — and he knows it. The pattern is set: assertion of [[genius|greatness]], followed by self-deprecation, followed by the awareness that the self-deprecation is itself a form of vanity.

### Vanity and Relationships

Vanity complicates Barbellion's relations with others. He recognises that his romantic difficulties stem partly from vanity: "I am such an egotist, I want, I believe, a Princess of the Blood Royal" (003-466). He wants what he cannot have, and he suspects that wanting is itself a form of self-flattery — the fantasy that he deserves someone extraordinary because he is extraordinary.

### The Vanity of Self-Deprecation

Barbellion's most sophisticated insight into vanity is that self-criticism can itself be vain. The performance of humility, the elaborate confession of faults, can be another way of drawing attention to oneself. He catches himself at this, too, and the journal becomes a hall of mirrors: vanity [[observation|watching]] vanity [[observation|watching]] vanity.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion's treatment of vanity belongs to a tradition of Christian self-examination — Augustine's *Confessions*, Rousseau's *Confessions* — in which the acknowledgment of sin is the first step toward redemption. But Barbellion's version is secular and, in a sense, more radical: there is no redemption. He describes his ambition as "a morbid diathesis of the mind" (004-226), a clinical term that treats vanity as pathology rather than sin. Vanity is simply a fact about himself, like the colour of his eyes, and the most he can do is record it accurately.

## Barbellion's Perspective

Vanity, in Barbellion's hands, becomes almost endearing. His willingness to admit his conceit, his egoism, his hunger for [[fame|recognition]] — and his unwillingness to pretend that admission cures these faults — gives his self-portrait a credibility that more flattering autobiographies lack. The reader trusts Barbellion because Barbellion does not trust himself.
