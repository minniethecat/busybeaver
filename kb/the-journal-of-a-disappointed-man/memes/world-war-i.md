## Overview

The First World War (1914–1918) erupts during the journal's final Part and becomes a constant, dark presence in Barbellion's late entries. The war transforms his understanding of his own suffering: measured against the deaths of millions, his personal struggle with illness can seem either trivial or emblematic. Barbellion's treatment of the war is characteristically honest — he does not pretend to heroism or patriotism but records the war as another enormous fact, like his illness, that he must somehow accommodate within the small space of a single consciousness.

## References in the Journal

The war enters the journal obliquely at first, then with increasing weight. Barbellion records the death of Rupert Brooke at Skyros — "What more perfect than the death of Rupert Brooke?" (004-367) — with a mixture of admiration and something like envy: Brooke died young and heroic, while Barbellion is dying slowly and obscurely. The war "plucked out of me that canker of self-obsession" (004-155), and Barbellion's recognition that his own suffering is dwarfed by the war's scale is one of the journal's most mature moments.

## Significance

The war serves as the ultimate context for Barbellion's personal drama. His illness, his ambition, his disappointments — all of these are reframed by the war's enormity. Yet Barbellion does not allow the war to obliterate his individuality; he continues to record his own experience, his own thoughts, his own small life, even as the world burns. This is not callousness but a kind of fidelity: the journal's commitment to the personal, even in the face of the historical.
