The **Dublin Review** was a Catholic periodical founded in 1836 by Nicholas Wiseman (later Cardinal) and Daniel O'Connell. Barbellion encountered it during a day of concentrated [[literature|reading]] at the Library in [[london|London]], consuming it alongside the *[[north-american-review|North American Review]]* and several newspapers in a single evening—testament to his omnivorous intellectual appetite.

## An Evening at the Library

After a long ramble through the streets of [[london|London]]—from [[Mark Lane]] to [[tower-bridge|Tower Bridge]], through [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]] and [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]] to [[charing-cross|Charing Cross]]—Barbellion retreated to the Library, where the *Dublin Review*'s article on [[samuel-butler]] caught his attention:

> Returned to the Library and read the *Dublin Review* (article on Samuel Butler), *North American Review* (one on Henry James) and dined at seven. After dinner, read: *Evening Standard*, *Saturday Westminster*, and the *New Statesman*. Smoked six cigarettes and went to bed. Tomorrow Fifth Symphony of Beethoven. (003-596)

## A Self-Conscious Aside

Barbellion was acutely aware of the incongruity between his circumstances and his intellectual [[illness|consumption]]. In a later entry, he reflected with characteristic self-mockery on his [[literature|reading]] habits:

> Think of it—I, earning my livelihood in an office of the Museum—in the Statistical Section—counting insects—adding up columns, "not worth three hundred a year to anyone but a beneficed clergyman"—wrote my essay on Samuel Butler and Bergson—and here I was in a Library reading the *Dublin Review*! (003-598)
