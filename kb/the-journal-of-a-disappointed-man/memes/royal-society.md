## Overview

The Royal Society of London for Improving Natural Knowledge, founded in 1660, is the oldest and most prestigious scientific society in the United Kingdom. For Barbellion, the Royal Society represented the pinnacle of scientific recognition — the institution whose fellowship certified a scientist's achievement. It appears in the journal as an ideal, a distant goal, a measure of what he was not.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion's relationship to the Royal Society is that of an outsider looking in. He references the Society, is aware of its significance, and measures his own achievements against its standards. But the Society remains distant — an institution he reads about, not one he belongs to. The gap between Barbellion the museum worker and the Royal Society fellow is another version of the gap between his actual self and his aspirational self.

## Significance

The Royal Society represents the institutional recognition that Barbellion's scientific ambition aimed at — and never achieved. Its presence in the journal, even as a distant reference point, reveals the conventionality of his aspirations: he wanted what every scientist wanted, the validation of the scientific establishment.
