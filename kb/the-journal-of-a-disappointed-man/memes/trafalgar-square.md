## Overview

Trafalgar Square furnishes one of Barbellion's most vivid London set-pieces: a lunch-hour spent sitting on the fountain's edge, eating sandwiches, watching a pigeon mate, and listening to the "purposeless" roar of motor horns. The passage — a brief interlude in an afternoon that would take him walking down Whitehall to Westminster Bridge and then to Queen's Hall for a Pachmann recital — captures London as Barbellion experienced it at its best: a city of unceasing, absorbing spectacle.

## In the Journal

The Trafalgar Square entry belongs to a long, leisurely account of a day off in central London:

> "After sitting on the wall around the fountain in the middle of Trafalgar Square, eating my sandwiches and feeding the Pigeons with the crumbs, I listened for a moment to the roar of the traffic around three sides of the Square as I stood in the centre quite alone, what time one fat old pigeon, all unconcerned, was treading another." (004-132)

The passage is built from juxtapositions: the solitary diarist in the centre of a roaring city, the indifferent pigeon coupling amid the traffic, the "purposeless" horn-tooting that suggests to Barbellion "a great British Victory perhaps, or Peace Day." He is at once participant and observer — feeding the pigeons, then standing apart to listen and interpret.

The Trafalgar Square moment sits within a larger itinerary: a walk down Whitehall to Westminster Bridge, a loiter by the telescope-man near Boadicea's statue, a Scots Guards piper idling, a man reading "a dirty fragment of newspaper" on the stone steps. Barbellion records each with the same attentive neutrality. The sequence culminates in the declaration: "Everything was *alive* to the finger tips, vividly shining, pulsating" (004-133).

## Significance

Trafalgar Square represents London at its most democratic — a public space where the diarist, eating sandwiches among pigeons, can simultaneously be alone and immersed in the city's thrum. Unlike the Natural History Museum (work) or Hammersmith (transit), Trafalgar Square is a place of pure *being* in the city. The pigeon-treading detail, recorded without comment, exemplifies Barbellion's naturalist eye applied to urban fauna — the same observational habits that served him in Devon hedgerows, transposed to the heart of the metropolis.
