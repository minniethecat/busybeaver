## Overview

*Joseph Vance: An Ill-Written Autobiography* (1906) was the first novel by William De Morgan (1839–1917), published when the author was 67. De Morgan was already well known as a ceramic tile designer and associate of William Morris's Arts and Crafts movement before turning to fiction. The novel, narrated in the first person, traces the life of Joseph Vance from a working-class childhood in [[london|London]] to a successful career as an engineer, combining Dickensian social observation with a distinctive serio-comic voice. It was an immediate popular success and established De Morgan as a late-blooming major novelist of the Edwardian period.

## Barbellion's Engagement

Barbellion recorded simply "*Joseph Vance*" in a list of [[literature|books]] he had read (PN 003-715), alongside William James's *Pragmatism: The Meaning of Truth* and Nietzsche's *Beyond Good and Evil*. The novel reappears in a later passage where Barbellion, reflecting on how he fills his days during his final illness, describes himself as a mental ragbag of his eclectic [[literature|reading]]:

> What I do (goodness knows what E—— does), is to drug my mind with print. I am just a ragbag of Smollett, H. G. Wells, Samuel Butler, the *Daily News*, the Bible, the *Labour Leader*, *Joseph Vance*, etc., etc. [PN 004-496]

This passage, written in May 1917 during Barbellion's terminal decline, reveals his relationship with print as a form of self-anaesthesia — [[literature|reading]] not for intellectual enrichment but to "drug" his mind against the reality of his situation. *Joseph Vance* appears in this list as one among many disparate texts, its presence testifying to the breadth and eclecticism of Barbellion's [[literature|reading]] habits even as his strength failed.
