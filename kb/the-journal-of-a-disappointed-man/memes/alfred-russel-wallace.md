## Overview

Alfred Russel Wallace (1823–1913) was an English [[natural-history|naturalist]], explorer, and biologist who independently conceived the theory of evolution through natural selection, prompting Darwin to publish *On the Origin of Species*. For Barbellion, Wallace was not merely a historical figure but a living scientist one degree of personal separation away — an acquaintance knew Wallace and Haeckel personally, and reported their conversation to the young diarist.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion encountered Wallace through a chain of personal acquaintance. An unnamed individual — perhaps a museum colleague — visited Barbellion at his hotel and described personal meetings with both Wallace and Ernst Haeckel, the German biologist (002-111). For Barbellion, this was electrifying: the giants of nineteenth-century biology were not remote abstractions but living men who could be met, spoken to, known. He later took pride in counting himself among the intellectual lineage that included "[[thomas-huxley|Huxley]], Wallace, Brooks, Lankester" (002-182).

Wallace also appears in a lighter moment, when Barbellion deploys "Wallace's Line" — the biogeographical boundary separating Asian and Australian fauna — in conversation, a display of the scientific knowledge he had acquired through his self-education (004-163–164).

## References in the Journal

A visitor brings news of the great [[natural-history|naturalist]]s:

> "He knows Wallace and Haeckel personally, and I sat at his feet while he told me about them." (002-111)

Wallace listed among the zoological pantheon:

> "The zoologists themselves — Huxley, Wallace, Brooks, Lankester. I chortle to reflect that in this group I can almost claim a place." (002-182)

A conversational display of learning:

> "'Then there's Wallace's Line,' I said, being purposely abstruse." (004-163)

## Biographical Note

Wallace spent four years in the Amazon basin (1848–1852) and eight years in the Malay Archipelago (1854–1862), collecting over 125,000 specimens. In 1858, while suffering from malaria in the Moluccas, he wrote a paper outlining natural selection and sent it to Darwin — precipitating the joint presentation of their theory to the Linnean Society. Wallace later diverged from Darwin on the question of human consciousness, arguing for a spiritual dimension to human evolution. Unlike [[thomas-huxley|Huxley]], who died in 1895, Wallace was still alive during the period of Barbellion's Journal, [[death|dying]] only in 1913.
