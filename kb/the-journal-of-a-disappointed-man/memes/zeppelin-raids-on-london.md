The **Zeppelin Raids on London** were a series of aerial bombing attacks carried out by German [[Zeppelin]] airships during the [[world-war-i|First World War]], bringing the conflict directly to the civilian population of the capital. Barbellion experienced these raids firsthand and recorded his reactions with characteristic candour—terror, fascination, and a grim determination to carry on.

## The First Raid

Barbellion's first recorded experience of a Zeppelin raid came while he was ill in bed, compounding physical suffering with the new psychological strain of aerial bombardment:

> A Zeppelin raid last night. I am down with a feverish cold. (004-043)

The brevity of the entry—a single sentence combining a major military event with a minor ailment—captures the strange texture of life on the home front, where the unprecedented and the mundane coexisted.

## Terror in the Dark

The psychological impact of the raids was profound. The fear of bombs falling from an invisible enemy in the night sky challenged even Barbellion's cultivated stoicism:

> Relief to be down in the country. Zeppelins terrify me. (004-052)

On another night, a neighbour's alarm sent the household scrambling:

> ——— came up very alarmed to say "Zeppelins about," so we put out the lights. (004-044)

## A Motor-Bus Destroyed

The raids inflicted real damage on civilian London. Barbellion recorded the destruction of a motor-omnibus in [[whitechapel|Whitechapel]]—the same East End district he had walked through in earlier, more carefree days:

> A motor-omnibus in Whitechapel was blown to bits. Great scenes in the city. (003-1057)

## Brought Down in Flames

Later in the war, as British air defences improved, the destruction of a raider provided a moment of grim satisfaction:

> A Zeppelin brought down in flames. (004-184)

The image of the burning airship—a "Zeppelin brought down in flames that lit up the sky"—became one of the war's iconic spectacles, witnessed by thousands of Londoners.
