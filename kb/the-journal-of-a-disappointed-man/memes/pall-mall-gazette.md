## About

The *Pall Mall Gazette* was a [[london|London]] evening newspaper published from 1865 to 1923. Named after the [[london|London]] street Pall Mall, it was founded by George Murray Smith and became known for its innovative investigative journalism under the editorship of W.T. Stead (1883–1889). By Barbellion's era in the 1910s, it had declined somewhat from its Stead-era peak but remained a fixture of [[london|London]] evening [[literature|reading]], alongside the *[[evening-standard|Evening Standard]]* and other competitors.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion records [[literature|reading]] the news of Captain Scott's death in the *Pall Mall Gazette* — a moment of profound emotional impact:

> News of Scott's great adventure! Scott dead a year ago!! The news, when I saw it tonight in the *Pall Mall Gazette* gave me cold thrills. I could have wept. … What splendid people we humans are! If there be no loving God to watch us, it's a pity for His sake as much as for our own.

(003-130)

### The Scott News and Its Significance

The passage is remarkable for several reasons. First, the news of Scott's death in the Antarctic reached Britain in February 1913 — a year after Scott perished in March 1912. Barbellion's shock at learning the news "a year ago" reflects the delay in communication from the Antarctic and the staggered way such news reached the public. The *Pall Mall Gazette* was his conduit to one of the defining tragic-heroic narratives of the Edwardian era.

Second, Barbellion's response moves rapidly from personal emotion ("I could have wept") to [[philosophy|philosophical reflection]] ("What splendid people we humans are!") to theological speculation ("If there be no loving God to watch us, it's a pity for His sake as much as for our own"). This three-stage movement — affect, affirmation, doubt — is characteristic of his journalistic method: a news item becomes a prompt for existential reflection.

### The Newspaper Habit

The *Pall Mall Gazette* was one of several [[london|London]] papers Barbellion consumed regularly. PN 003-596 records a typical evening of [[literature|reading]] at the Library:

> Returned to the Library and read the Dublin Review (article on Samuel Butler), North American Review (one on Henry James) and dined at seven. After dinner, read: Evening Standard, Saturday Westminster, and the New Statesman.

Barbellion's newspaper and periodical [[illness|consumption]] was voracious and omnivorous — a self-imposed programme of intellectual self-education conducted through the print culture of Edwardian [[london|London]]. The *Pall Mall Gazette*, as an evening paper, occupied a particular niche in this ecology: it delivered the day's news in time for the evening commute and after-dinner [[literature|reading]].
