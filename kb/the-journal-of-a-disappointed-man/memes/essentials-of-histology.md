The **Essentials of Histology**, a textbook of microscopic tissue anatomy, appears in Barbellion's journal at a moment of crisis. The fear of losing it—of being "done out of" it—became for him a synecdoche of a larger terror: that ill-health would strip him of his scientific equipment and identity altogether.

## The Fear of Loss

After four days of profound [[melancholy|depression]], Barbellion reached for his histology textbook as a talisman—and found even that threatened:

> After four days of the most profound depression of spirits—I should be done out of my *Essentials of Histology*. Even in the midst of my melancholy, the thought of losing my textbook struck me as the last indignity. (003-385)

The textbook was more than a study aid; it represented his membership in the community of working scientists. To lose it was to lose his place among those who did real work—who cut sections, stained slides, and contributed to knowledge.

## The Scientific Self

The *Essentials of Histology* belonged to a shelf of text[[literature|books]]—[[comparative-anatomy-of-vertebrates|Wiedersheim's Comparative Anatomy]], [[Balfour]]'s embryology, [[Smith Woodward]]'s vertebrate paleontology—that constituted Barbellion's intellectual armament. These [[literature|books]] were not merely read but absorbed, recited, taken to bed "as another might take a French novel." That his relationship to them was eventually displaced by music does not diminish their role in forming the mind that recorded the journal.
