The **Entomological Society of London** (later the Royal Entomological Society) was a British learned society devoted to the study of insects, founded in 1833. During the Edwardian period it served as a gathering place for amateur and professional entomologists to exhibit specimens and hear papers. Its headquarters were at 41 Queen's Gate, South Kensington.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion attended a meeting of the Society on 4 March (year unnamed in the journal) and recorded it with his characteristic ironic eye, producing one of the most vivid institutional portraits in the journal—a piece of natural-history writing in which the naturalists themselves become the specimens:

> The Entomological Society
>
> There were a great many Scarabees present who exhibited to one another poor little pinned insects in collecting-boxes … It was really a one-man show, Prof. Poulton, a man of very considerable scientific attainments, being present, and shouting with a raucous voice in a way that must have scared some of the timid, unassuming collectors of our country's butterflies and moths. (003-386)

His description continues with a devastating zoological metaphor:

> Like a great powerful sheepdog, he got up and barked, "Mendelian characters," or "Germ plasm," what time the obedient flock ran together and bleated a pitiful applause. I suppose, having frequently heard these and similar phrases fall from the lips of the great man at these reunions, they have come to regard them as symbols of a ritual which they think it pious to accept without any question. So every time the Professor says, "Allelomorph," or some such phrase, they cross themselves and never venture to ask him what the hell it is all about. (003-386)

## In the Journal

The Entomological Society passage is a miniature masterpiece of the journal's method. Barbellion arrives as an observer but immediately begins to classify: the collectors are "Scarabees" (dung beetles), the professor a "sheepdog," the audience a "flock." The taxonomic impulse—sorting, naming, hierarchising—is applied not to insects but to insect-collectors. The religious metaphor ("cross themselves," "ritual," "pious") transforms the scientific meeting into a parody of a church service, with Poulton as the high priest intoning sacred syllables ("Allelomorph") to a congregation that venerates without understanding.

This is more than satire. Barbellion, who read his own papers at the [[zoological-society-london|Zoological Society]], knew these rituals from the inside. His mockery of the "timid, unassuming collectors" carries the edge of self-recognition: he too was a provincial naturalist seeking validation in metropolitan scientific society. The difference was that he could see the ritual for what it was—and could not help recording what he saw.

The passage also documents a specific moment in the history of biology. Poulton's barking of "Mendelian characters" and "Germ plasm" places the meeting in the ferment of post-Darwinian evolutionary biology, when Weismann's germ plasm theory and the rediscovery of Mendel's laws were transforming the theoretical landscape. The collectors, with their "poor little pinned insects," belonged to an older natural history tradition that the new experimental biology was beginning to displace.

## Historical Context

Professor Edward Bagnall Poulton (1856–1943), the "sheepdog" of Barbellion's account, was Hope Professor of Zoology at Oxford and a leading figure in the study of animal coloration and mimicry. A fervent Darwinian, Poulton was one of the few British biologists who continued to defend natural selection during the "eclipse of Darwinism" around the turn of the century. His presence at the Entomological Society—shouting about Weismann and Mendel—reflected the intellectual currents that were reshaping biology at the time Barbellion was beginning his own scientific career.
