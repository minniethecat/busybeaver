## Overview

The Mole (*Talpa europaea*) is a solitary, subterranean insectivore with dense velvet fur, powerful shovel-like forelimbs, and reduced eyes. In Barbellion's Journal, the mole is not an object of field observation but a specimen for taxidermy — one of the first animals he learned to skin and stuff, following instructions from a boys' periodical. It marks a pivotal step in his transition from passive nature enthusiast to active, hands-on naturalist.

## Observations in the Journal

The mole appears in a remarkable passage of retrospective self-assessment, where Barbellion reviews his boyhood method of learning natural history:

> "Dr. Gordon Staples in the Boy's Own Paper, taught me how to make a skin, and I got hold of a Mole and then a Squirrel (the latter falling to my prowess with a catapult), stuffed them and set them up in cases which I glazed myself." (004-393)

The phrase "falling to my prowess with a catapult" — applied to the squirrel, not the mole — carries a characteristic blend of self-mockery and pride. The mole was presumably found dead or captured, the more humble of his two early taxidermy subjects. That he "got hold of" a mole rather than shooting or catapulting it suggests trapping or scavenging — the practical resourcefulness of a boy with more ambition than equipment.

The mole also appears in a tender, entirely different context. Describing his intimate observation of a loved one's face:

> "I counted the little filaments on her precious mole and saw the sun through the golden down of her throat …" (003-962)

Here "mole" refers to the skin mark, not the animal — but the forensic closeness of the observation, the counting of filaments, is the same meticulous eye Barbellion brought to his natural history.

## Natural History

The European mole is a fossorial mammal adapted to a life spent almost entirely underground. Its cylindrical body, absence of external ears, and powerful digging claws allow it to construct extensive tunnel systems. Moles feed primarily on earthworms and soil invertebrates, consuming up to their body weight daily. Their tell-tale molehills — excavated soil pushed to the surface — are a familiar feature of British pasture and garden. Despite being common, moles are seldom seen alive above ground.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's mole taxidermy places him in the long British tradition of the amateur naturalist who learns through doing — skinning, stuffing, and mounting specimens. The *Boy's Own Paper* (1879–1967) was a widely read weekly that mixed adventure stories with practical instruction in crafts, sports, and natural history. Dr. Gordon Stables (1840–1910), a Scottish naval surgeon and prolific boys' author, contributed natural history columns that shaped a generation of young collectors.
