The **Terra Nova Expedition** (1910–1913), [[Robert Falcon Scott]]'s second Antarctic venture, ended in tragedy when Scott and his four companions perished on the return from the South Pole. News of their deaths reached [[london|London]] in February 1913 and resonated deeply with Barbellion, who found in Scott's final writings a mirror of his own situation—a man recording his last words in conditions of extreme desolation.

## News of a Great Adventure

Barbellion recorded the moment the news broke in a terse entry that captures the shock of the discovery:

> News of Scott's great adventure! Scott dead. (003-130)

The exclamation mark after "adventure" and the flat declaration "Scott dead" trace the arc from excitement to mourning in two phrases.

## Antarctic Cold and Desolation

The image of Scott writing in his tent as the blizzard closed in became for Barbellion a powerful figure for his own predicament. Confined to his room by illness, he imagined himself in the explorer's place:

> I might be Captain Scott writing his last words amid Antarctic cold and desolation. It is very cold in this room. I have piled up a roaring fire. It's Antarctic cold and desolation. London is a blizzard outside the window. (003-651, 003-652)

The slippage from "Antarctic cold" to the cold of his London room—the blizzard outside the window—is characteristic of Barbellion's habit of finding in external events metaphors for his own inner condition.
