## Overview

The Common Frog (*Rana temporaria*) is among the animals Barbellion studies through dissection during his zoological education. Referenced alongside rabbits, pigeons, and dogfish (004-407), the frog is a classic subject of comparative anatomy — a gateway to understanding vertebrate structure. For Barbellion, the frog represents the hands-on, empirical dimension of natural history that complements his reading and field observation.

## Observations in the Journal

The frog appears explicitly in a passage listing Barbellion's dissection subjects. The list — "Rabbits, Pigeons, Frogs, Dogfish" (004-407) — suggests a systematic programme of anatomical study, progressing through representative vertebrate types. This autodidactic approach is characteristic of Barbellion's scientific education: self-directed, enthusiastic, and thorough.

## Significance

The frog, like the rabbit, embodies the practical, sometimes visceral reality of Victorian-Edwardian natural history. Barbellion does not shrink from this reality; he reports it with the same matter-of-factness he brings to all his observations. His "devouring curiosity" (004-407) transforms dissection from mere technique into an expression of intellectual passion.
