## About

The Bible is a collection of sacred texts in Judaism and Christianity, consisting of the Old Testament (Hebrew Scriptures) and the New Testament. By Barbellion's Edwardian era, the Authorised (King James) Version was deeply embedded in English literary culture, its phrases and cadences woven into public discourse, education, and domestic piety.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion's relationship with the Bible is characteristically ambivalent — intellectually agnostic yet rhetorically steeped in its language. Five passages illuminate his stance.

### Cultural Inheritance

In a moment of self-mockery, Barbellion catches himself using biblical idiom and [[curiosity|wonder]]s at its source:

> I believe I am good for another 12 months without abnormal worries. Just now, of course, the Slug ain't exactly on the thorn — on the cabbage in fact as E—— suggested. The Grasshopper is much of a burden and the voice of the Turtle has gone from my land (where did all these Bible phrases come from?).

(004-303)

The parenthetical aside — "where did all these Bible phrases come from?" — reveals that scriptural language inhabits his [[philosophy|thinking]] even when he is not conscious of reaching for it. The references to the sluggard on the thorn (Proverbs), the grasshopper as a burden (Ecclesiastes), and the voice of the turtle (Song of Solomon) come unbidden to a man who has long ceased to believe.

### Family Piety and Agnostic Distance

His maiden aunt F——, distressed by "the agnostic atmosphere" of one of his published articles, deploys the Bible as a moral counterweight:

> Had an amusing letter from my maiden-aunt F——, who does not like "the agnostic atmosphere" in my Academy article. Poor dear! She is sorry if I really feel like that, and, if I do, what a pity to put it into print. Then a Bible reference to the Epistle to the Romans.

(002-116)

Barbellion's response is wry and fond — he records the letter not with anger but with the detachment of someone for whom biblical authority has become a familial artefact rather than a living constraint. The Epistle to the Romans, with its theology of sin and grace, is for his aunt a final trump card; for Barbellion, it is data in an ethnographic sketch.

### Trivialised Piety

Barbellion is sharply critical of the sentimental religiosity he observes in popular culture:

> I can't make out what has come over folk recently: the wit, wisdom and irony on the old tombstones have given place to maudlin sentiment and pious Bible references.

(004-003)

The complaint is aesthetic as much as theological. The "old tombstones" possessed a plain-spoken dignity; modern graveyard piety, in his view, substitutes decorous Bible-quoting for honest confrontation with death — precisely the confrontation his own journal strives to sustain.

### Scripture as Trivia

Among the most telling moments is Barbellion reducing the Bible to a quiz question:

> "Oh! that's right," I said sarcastic and crestfallen. "Now tell me the shortest verse in the Bible and the date of Rameses II."

(003-768)

The pairing of "the shortest verse in the Bible" with "the date of Rameses II" — sacred text and ancient history as equivalent trivia — captures his intellectual temper exactly. Scripture has [[honey-bee|bee]]n demoted from revelation to erudition, a corpus to be mined for curiosities rather than submitted to as authority.

### The Ragbag of Print

In a sweeping catalogue of his mental furnishings, Barbellion lists the Bible alongside newspapers, novels, and political weeklies:

> I am just a ragbag of Smollett, H. G. Wells, Samuel Butler, the Daily News, the Bible, the Labour Leader, Joseph Vance, etc., etc.

(004-496)

The Bible here is one item among many — a source-text in a democratic, undifferentiated diet of print. It neither towers above nor vanishes into the surrounding titles. This is perhaps Barbellion's most honest positioning of Scripture: part of the cultural atmosphere he breathes, neither believed nor discarded, simply there.
