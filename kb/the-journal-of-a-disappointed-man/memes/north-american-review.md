## About

The *North American Review* was the first literary magazine in the United States, founded in Boston in 1815. By the early twentieth century it had become a venerable institution of American letters, publishing essays, fiction, and criticism by leading writers and intellectuals on both sides of the Atlantic.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion encountered the *North American Review* during a characteristic evening of intensive [[literature|reading]] at the Library in [[london|London]]:

> Returned to the Library and read the Dublin Review (article on Samuel Butler), North American Review (one on Henry James) and dined at seven. After dinner, read: Evening Standard, Saturday Westminster, and the New Statesman.

(003-596)

The article on Henry James caught his attention — a detail that aligns with Barbellion's broader literary interests. James, the American expatriate who had become a towering figure in English letters, represented the kind of serious literary culture to which Barbellion aspired. That the *North American Review* was publishing on James in 1913 places it at the centre of transatlantic literary discourse.

The *North American Review* appears here as part of a remarkable evening of [[literature|reading]]: two quarterly reviews (the [[dublin-review]] and the *North American*), followed after dinner by three newspapers ([[evening-standard]], [[saturday-westminster]], [[new-statesman]]). The passage is one of the densest records of Barbellion's periodical [[illness|consumption]] in the entire journal — a snapshot of the Edwardian autodidact's intellectual life conducted entirely through print.
