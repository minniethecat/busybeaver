## Overview

The Honey Bee (*Apis mellifera*) appears in Barbellion's journal in a moment of characteristic enthusiasm: he considers taking up beekeeping. The inquiry "Do you keep Bees here?" (003-056) suggests a practical interest in apiculture that aligns with his broader naturalist identity. Bees represent the intersection of natural history and husbandry — the study of nature combined with the care of nature.

## Observations in the Journal

The bee reference (003-056) is brief but revealing. It occurs in Part II (the London years), suggesting that even in the city, Barbellion sought connections to the natural world. The question about beekeeping is practical — it implies a desire not merely to observe insects but to live alongside them, to participate in their economy.

## Significance

Bees embody a recurring tension in Barbellion's naturalism: the desire to be both a scientific observer and an engaged participant in the natural world. Beekeeping is not laboratory science but a form of craft knowledge, a relationship with a species that spans centuries of human practice. Barbellion's inquiry suggests a naturalist who wants to do as well as see.
