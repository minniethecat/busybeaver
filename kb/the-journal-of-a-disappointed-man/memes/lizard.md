## Overview

The common lizard (*Zootoca vivipara*) makes a single, vivid appearance in Barbellion's journal — a hand-caught specimen on C—— Hill during one of the young naturalist's richest descriptive passages. The entry exemplifies Barbellion's ability to weave precise natural history observation into a larger tapestry of landscape description, each living thing — lizard, starling, rabbit, magpie — rendered with equal, unhurried attention.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion records the lizard catch as part of a densely detailed May 1903 ramble:

> "Here are some of the things I saw: the bark on several of the trees in the mazzard orchards rubbed into a beautifully smooth, polished surface by the Red Devon Cows when scratching where it itched; I put my hand on the smooth almost cherry-red patch of bark and felt delighted and grateful that cows had fleas: the young shoots of the whortleberry plants on the hill were red tipped with the gold of an almost horizontal sun. I caught a little lizard which slipped across my path." (002-097)

The lizard is caught — briefly held, one imagines — the object of a young naturalist's instinctive response. The sentence structure is remarkable: the lizard appears between the red-tipped whortleberry shoots and the distant cow with starlings on her back, one detail in a panorama. The catching is mentioned without emphasis, as though the action were as natural as looking. This is Barbellion's field naturalist's body at work — the hand that reaches for the lizard belongs to the same person who will later dissect frogs, prepare snake skulls, and publish observations in the *Zoologist*.

## Natural History

The common or viviparous lizard is Britain's most widespread reptile, found from sea level to mountain heath. Unlike most reptiles, it gives birth to live young rather than laying eggs — an adaptation to cooler climates. Adults reach 10–16 cm, with brown or grey-brown colouration and variable darker markings. They feed on small invertebrates — spiders, insects, slugs — and are themselves prey for birds of prey, corvids, and snakes. Barbellion's catch — a lizard crossing a path on a sun-warmed hillside — is typical habitat and behaviour for this heliothermic species.

## Taxonomic Context

The common lizard belongs to the family Lacertidae, the wall and sand lizards. Barbellion's interest in reptiles extended beyond casual observation — his journal records serpent captures, adder encounters, and anatomical preparations including a grass snake skull. The lizard catch on C—— Hill represents the first, instinctive tier of his engagement: the unmediated encounter that precedes classification, dissection, and publication.
