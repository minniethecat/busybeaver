The **Register Office** was a civil marriage registry established under the Marriage Act 1836, which permitted couples to be legally married without a religious ceremony. For Barbellion, this secular, bureaucratic venue was the setting for one of the most consequential events in his life—and one of the most emotionally conflicted passages in the journal.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion's announcement of his marriage at the Register Office is layered with doubt, self-scrutiny, and the shadow of medical knowledge:

> Am getting married at ——— Register Office on September 15th. It is impossible to set down here all the labyrinthine ambages of my will and feelings in regard to this event. Such incredible vacillations, doubts, fears. I have been living at a great rate below surface recently. (003-1008)

The blanked-out location ("——— Register Office") is a rare gesture of discretion in a journal otherwise remarkable for its candour. But what follows is unsparing:

> "If you enjoy only twelve months' happiness," the Doctor said to me, "it is worth while." But he makes a recommendation. … At his suggestion E—— went to see him and from his own mouth learnt all the truth about the state of my health, to prevent possible mutual recriminations in the future. To marry an introspective dyspeptic—what a prospect for her! … I exercise my microscopic analysis on her now as well as on myself. (003-1008)

## In the Journal

The Register Office scene crystallises a central tension of the journal: the impossibility of separating private experience from clinical self-observation. Barbellion approaches his own marriage with the same "microscopic analysis" he applies to zoological specimens—dissecting his feelings, examining his fiancée's motives, calculating the probable duration of happiness. The doctor's verdict ("twelve months' happiness") hangs over the ceremony like a prognosis.

The passage also reveals the ethical dimension of Barbellion's self-consciousness. He is aware—painfully aware—of what he is asking of his wife. "To marry an introspective dyspeptic—what a prospect for her!" The exclamation is both self-mockery and genuine moral reckoning. He knew, as she knew, that the marriage was shadowed by his illness from its very beginning. The Register Office, with its secular impersonality, was perhaps the only appropriate setting for a union undertaken in such awareness of its probable brevity.
