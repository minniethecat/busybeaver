## Overview

The Barn Owl (*Strix flammea*, now classified as *Tyto alba*) is a medium-sized owl with a heart-shaped facial disc, pale underparts, and golden-buff upperparts. On 19 January 1903, the thirteen-year-old Barbellion recorded seeing one "flying in broad daylight" during an expedition to A—— Wood with his friends S—— and L——. This diurnal sighting, unusual for a typically nocturnal species, is one of the earliest dated wildlife observations in the Journal.

## Observations in the Journal

The owl observation appears early in Part I, during the same ramble that featured L——'s dramatic fall from a tree:

> "Went to A—— Wood with S—— and L——. Saw a Barn Owl (*Strix flammea*) flying in broad daylight." (002-005)

Barbellion gives the owl its scientific name, a mark of the taxonomic precision he was cultivating even at thirteen. The Latin binomial — italicised in the manuscript — distinguishes this entry from casual bird-watching and signals the young diarist's ambition to be taken seriously as a naturalist.

Later entries suggest Barbellion retained an interest in owls. A dawn awakening yields:

> "Awoke at sunrise to hear an Owl still hooting, a Lark singing" (003-275)

And in a rare moment of self-mockery, he describes himself as:

> "An extremely self-inflated Brown Owl, who hoots up through the breeze" (004-495)

Though the species of these later owls is unspecified, they confirm the continued presence of these birds in Barbellion's sensory world.

## Natural History

Barn owls are among the most widely distributed land birds, found on every continent except Antarctica. They hunt primarily by sound, using their asymmetrically placed ears to locate small mammals in complete darkness. Their silent flight — enabled by specialised feather edges — makes them formidable nocturnal predators. Diurnal hunting, as Barbellion observed, occurs when prey is scarce or when weather conditions have disrupted nocturnal foraging.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion's use of the scientific name *Strix flammea* reflects the ornithological taxonomy of his era. The species has since been reclassified from *Strix* to *Tyto*. The barn owl's distinctive morphology — particularly the heart-shaped facial disc — makes it one of the most recognisable owl species and an excellent subject for a young naturalist learning to distinguish raptor families.
