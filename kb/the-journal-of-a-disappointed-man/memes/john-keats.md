## Overview

John Keats (1795–1821) appears in Barbellion's journal as both a literary touchstone and a personal emblem. Keats's early death from tuberculosis at twenty-five, his posthumous rise from obscurity to canonical status, and his own experience of illness and creative struggle make him a natural point of identification for Barbellion. The Keats who appears in the journal is not the Keats of scholarly appreciation but the Keats of intimate [[fame|recognition]] — a fellow sufferer whose words Barbellion quotes as if they were his own.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's relationship to Keats is that of a reader who finds in a dead poet a living companion. He quotes Keats's line "There is no fiercer Hell than the [[disappointment|failure]] of a great ambition" (002-044) as a summary of his own predicament. He defends Keats against critics who dismissed him as a "little poet" (003-610), aligning himself with the undervalued artist against the establishment. And in the journal's final Part, he reads "accounts of the last hours of Keats" (004-301), as if stu[[death|dying]] the death of a predecessor might illuminate his own approaching end.

## References in the Journal

Keats is referenced across all three Parts of the journal, suggesting a sustained rather than passing interest. The early reference (002-044) frames Keats as a moral authority on ambition and [[disappointment|failure]]. The middle references (003-599, 003-610) place Keats among Barbellion's pantheon of undervalued geniuses alongside Chekhov. The late reference (004-301) places Keats in the company of other famous deaths — Gibbon, Wilde, Baudelaire — as Barbellion contemplates his own [[death|mortality]].

## Biographical Note

John Keats (1795–1821) was an English Romantic poet. Trained as a surgeon, he abandoned medicine for poetry and produced his major works — including the great odes — in a single astonishing year (1819). He died of tuberculosis in Rome at twenty-five, believing himself a [[disappointment|failure]]. His posthumous [[fame|reputation]] grew steadily through the Victorian period, and by Barbellion's time he was recognised as one of the greatest English poets.
