## Overview

The common **Snail** (*Cornu aspersum*, known to Barbellion by the genus *Helix*) was one of the first creatures the young naturalist ever dissected. In a memorable passage that captures both his boyhood enthusiasm and his mother's tolerant domesticity, Barbellion describes unravelling the internal anatomy of snails in a pie-dish in the family kitchen.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion records his snail dissections with characteristic self-reflection, linking the anatomical curiosity of his boyhood to the aesthetic passions of his adult life:

> I used to dissect snails in a pie-dish in the kitchen while Mother baked the cakes — the unravelling of the internal economy of a Helix caused as great an emotional storm as today the Unfinished Symphony does! (PN=003-697)

He also preserved a moment of pure, wondering inquiry that every naturalist will recognise:

> Why should a snail be twisted round? (PN=002-109)

The question — at once childishly simple and philosophically profound — captures Barbellion's essential cast of mind: a curiosity that could pivot from form to function, from morphology to metaphysics, without ever noticing the join.

## Natural History

The garden snail is a pulmonate gastropod, breathing air through a vascularised mantle cavity. Its spiral shell, coiled according to precise mathematical principles, houses the visceral mass in a torsion that twists the body through 180 degrees during embryonic development — the very "twisted round" that Barbellion found so puzzling. The species is hermaphroditic and lays clusters of pearly, calcareous eggs in damp soil.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion uses the genus name *Helix*, a broad nineteenth-century classification that encompassed many of the familiar land snails. Modern taxonomy places the common garden snail in the genus *Cornu* (sometimes still *Helix*), family Helicidae. The pie-dish dissections were a genuine piece of comparative anatomy, however humble the setting — the boy who unravelled snail viscera would grow into the man who prepared the skull of a grass snake and marvelled at the beating heart of a frog.
