The **Outbreak of the First World War** in August 1914—an event that Barbellion persistently called "[[Armageddon]]"—transformed the frame of the journal. His private struggle against declining health, already freighted with existential significance, now unfolded against the backdrop of continental war. The juxtaposition of personal and global catastrophe became one of the journal's defining tensions.

## Armageddon

Barbellion adopted the biblical term "Armageddon" for the war, infusing the geopolitical crisis with apocalyptic weight:

> The spark which fired almost the whole of Europe into flames was the outbreak of Armageddon in August, 1914. (004-115)

The term appears throughout the later journal entries, suggesting a conflict so total that it eclipsed the ordinary vocabulary of war. Barbellion, who had been reading [[friedrich-nietzsche|Nietzsche]] and [[fyodor-dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]], found in the language of revelation a register adequate to the scale of events.

## A Feverish Existence

Living in wartime London, Barbellion experienced the outbreak as an intensification of the already feverish quality of his existence:

> I am feverish, living as I do in Armageddon! (003-784)

The exclamation captures his characteristic fusion of the personal and the historical—his own physical fevers and the fever of a world at war. The sentence refuses to distinguish between them.

## Watching from the Sidelines

Barbellion's ill-health kept him from military service, a fact that generated complex emotions—relief, guilt, envy. He watched others depart:

> I saw ——— off at Waterloo en route for Armageddon. Darling fellow. He shook hands warmly and got into the train. (003-998)

The domestic tenderness of "Darling fellow" against the backdrop of "Armageddon" encapsulates the journal's unique perspective: a man condemned to watch the greatest historical drama of his age from a spectator's seat, recording not the battles but the ripples they sent through the lives of those left behind.
