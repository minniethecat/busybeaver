## Overview

Exmoor — the high, open moorland straddling the Devon–Somerset border — enters Barbellion's journal as a kind of ecstatic shock. His first crossing of the moor, at about age fourteen, overwhelms him with "a flood of ideas, impressions, and delights" that proves literally unrecordable. Exmoor recurs at three critical points in the diary: the boyhood discovery, the adult's regret at having squandered his country years on zoology textbooks, and the dying man's fantasy of haunting the places he loved.

## In the Journal

Barbellion's first Exmoor entry is among the most rhapsodic in the entire journal:

> "To Challacombe and then walked across Exmoor. This is the first time I have been on Exmoor. My first experience of the Moors came bursting in on me with a flood of ideas, impressions, and delights. I cannot write out the history of today. It would take too long and my mind is a palpitating tangle. I have so many things to record that I cannot record one of them." (002-047)

The paradox — an entry about the impossibility of writing an entry — captures something essential about Barbellion's relationship with nature: at its most intense, it exceeds language. "Too much joy, like too much pain, simply makes me prostrate. It wounds the organism." The passage concludes with an uncharacteristically simple declaration: "I wish I were a shepherd!"

Years later, in a long retrospective passage written from London, Barbellion reproaches his younger self for privileging science over experience — and Exmoor is the emblem of what was squandered:

> "election meetings in remote Exmoor villages (and those wonderful Inns where I had to spend the night!) — all are gone — too remote to bear recital — yet just sufficiently clear to harass the mind in my constant endeavours to raise them all again from the dead in my consciousness." (003-599)

At the very end of the diary, in a passage contemplating death, Exmoor returns as one of the places Barbellion would choose to haunt:

> "Think of me flitting about the orchards and farmyards... walking along the coast among the seabirds, climbing Exmoor, bathing in streams and in the sea, haunting all my old loves and passions." (004-407)

The repetition of "Exmoor" across the journal's full span — boyhood, adulthood, approaching death — makes it one of the diary's most persistent geographical touchstones.

## Significance

Exmoor functions in Barbellion's emotional geography as the place of unmediated encounter — nature experienced not through the lens of taxonomy or dissection but as overwhelming sensory flood. It is the counterpoint to the laboratory and the museum: a landscape that resists analysis. His inability to "write out the history of today" is itself the point — Exmoor defeats the naturalist's descriptive machinery, leaving only the raw fact of joy.
