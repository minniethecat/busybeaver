[[whitechapel]], a densely populated working-class district in London's [[East End]], appears in Barbellion's journal as both a waypoint on his exploratory rambles and, later, as a site of wartime violence. His observations capture the social and sensory contrasts between the East End and the more prosperous quarters of the city.

## A Walking Route Through London

On an October walk that traced a long arc through the city, Barbellion passed through Whitechapel as part of an itinerary that began at Mark Lane, crossed Tower Bridge, and proceeded through St. Paul's and [[fleet-street|Fleet Street]] to [[Charing Cross]]:

> Went to Mark Lane by train, then walked over the Tower Bridge, and back along Lower Thames Street to London Bridge, up to Whitechapel, St. Paul's, Fleet Street, and Charing Cross, and so home. (003-587)

## The Sausage Restaurants

Barbellion noted how the character of London's food and commerce shifted as one moved west. The contrast between Whitechapel and Fleet Street was marked by the change from East End sausage restaurants to the taverns and Grub Street pamphleteers of the newspaper district:

> In Fleet Street, you exchange the Whitechapel sausage restaurants for Taverns with "snacks at the bar," and the chestnut roasters, with their buckets of red-hot coals, for Grub Street camp followers, selling *L'Indépendance Belge* or pamphlets entitled, "Why We Went to War." (003-593)

## The Zeppelin Raid

Whitechapel reappears in the journal under darker circumstances during the First World War. On the night of a Zeppelin air raid that brought the war directly to London's civilian population, Barbellion recorded the destruction:

> A motor-omnibus in Whitechapel was blown to bits. Great scenes in the city. (003-1057)

The raid left Barbellion badly shaken—"scared out of my life"—as bombs fell within a quarter of a mile of his own location. The image of a civilian motor-bus destroyed in an East End street captured the new reality of total war reaching into the everyday life of the city.

## Secondhand Clothes Dealers

Whitechapel's association with the clothing trade surfaces in passing references. Barbellion noted "secondhand clothes dealers from Whitechapel" (004-327) and later described "a secondhand-clothes family from Whitechapel taking the air together on the front" (004-338)—glimpses of the district's Jewish immigrant community enjoying a seaside outing.
