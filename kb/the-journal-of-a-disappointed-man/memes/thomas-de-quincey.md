## Overview

Thomas De Quincey (1785–1859) was an English essayist best known for *Confessions of an English Opium-Eater* (1821), a pioneering work of addiction memoir and psychological self-examination. For Barbellion, De Quincey was a recurring touchstone — a writer whose confessional mode, opium-fuelled visions, and unflinching examination of death offered a literary precedent for his own Journal's project of radical self-disclosure.

## Relationship to Barbellion

De Quincey appears throughout the Journal as both a stylistic model and a psychological reference point. Barbellion consciously modelled aspects of his own confessional project on De Quincey: "I have the last kick and, copying De Quincey, arrange to hand it over for publication after my death" (002-204). This reveals that Barbellion saw De Quincey's *Confessions* as a template for posthumous self-revelation — a tradition he was deliberately joining.

At a more visceral level, De Quincey's opium visions provided Barbellion with a vocabulary for describing his own altered states of consciousness — whether from illness, exhaustion, or emotional extremity. He pairs De Quincey with [[fyodor-dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] as writers who render the boundary between reality and nightmare: "Apocalyptic vision or an opium dream — [[fyodor-dostoevsky|Dostoevsky]] or De Quincey set to music" (003-889).

De Quincey also accompanied Barbellion in his meditations on [[death|mortality]]: "I think it was De Quincey who said [[death|Death]] to him seemed..." (004-470), the sentence trailing off as if death itself resists completion.

## Biographical Note

Born in Manchester, De Quincey ran away from school at seventeen and lived in extreme poverty in [[london|London]] and Wales before reconciling with his family and attending Oxford. He first took opium as a student to relieve neuralgia and became a lifelong addict. His *Confessions*, published in the *[[london|London]] Magazine* in 1821, made him famous. He spent his later years in Edinburgh, producing a vast body of essays on subjects ranging from murder ("On Murder Considered as One of the Fine Arts") to philosophy, economics, and literary criticism. His influence extended to Baudelaire, Poe, and Borges — and, as the Journal shows, to Barbellion.
