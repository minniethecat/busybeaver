## Overview

The European Rabbit (*Oryctolagus cuniculus*) appears in Barbellion's journal primarily as a specimen for dissection — part of his autodidactic zoological education. His reference to "cutting open with devouring curiosity Rabbits, Pigeons, Frogs, Dogfish" (004-407) captures the young naturalist's hands-on approach to learning anatomy. The rabbit is not a pet or a pest but an object of scientific curiosity.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's rabbit references are primarily anatomical. Unlike birds, which he observes in the field, rabbits appear on the dissecting table — part of the practical zoology that complements his reading and field observation. The phrase "devouring curiosity" (004-407) is revealing: dissection is not cold or clinical for Barbellion but driven by the same passionate curiosity that fuels all his natural history.

## Significance

The rabbit represents the sometimes-uncomfortable reality of nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century natural history: the study of life required the taking of life. Barbellion's frankness about dissection reflects his larger commitment to honesty — he does not prettify the naturalist's practice.
