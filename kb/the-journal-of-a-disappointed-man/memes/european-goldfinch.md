## Overview

The European Goldfinch (*Carduelis carduelis*) is a small, brightly coloured passerine bird of the finch family. Barbellion's encounters with the goldfinch span both captive and wild observation: he and his companions kept a pet goldfinch, and he spotted one in the wild during a catapult-shooting ramble with friends. These early journal entries (January 1903) reveal the young naturalist's developing observational habits and his companions' enthusiastic — if unrefined — field techniques.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's first wild sighting of a goldfinch is recorded during a walk with his friend L——:

> "While walking down the main road saw a Goldfinch, but very indistinctly—it might not have been one." (002-004)

This tentative identification — the "might not have been one" caveat — exemplifies the young naturalist's cautious empiricism. The same party would later that day bring down a duck they misidentified, showing that the fourteen-year-old Barbellion was still developing his field identification skills.

A few days later, he records his pet bird's domestic routine:

> "Our Goldfinch roosts at 5:30. Joe's kitten is a very small one. 'Magpie' is its name." (002-007)

The juxtaposition of the goldfinch's bedtime with the kitten's growth — both creatures observed with the same patient attention — is characteristic of Barbellion's early journal entries.

## Natural History

Goldfinches are seed-eaters, particularly fond of thistle and teasel seeds, and were commonly kept as cage birds in Edwardian England for their bright plumage and pleasant song. Their population suffered from trapping for the pet trade in the nineteenth century. In the wild, they inhabit woodland edges, orchards, and gardens, often feeding in small flocks.

## Taxonomic Context

As a passerine (perching bird) in the finch family (Fringillidae), the goldfinch represents one of the most accessible entry points to ornithology for a young naturalist. Barbellion's engagement with birds — from pets to field observation to egg collecting — was central to his early identity as a naturalist and features prominently in Part I of the Journal.
