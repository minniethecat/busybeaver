## Overview

Queen's Hall, located in Langham Place, London, was the city's principal concert venue during Barbellion's years there. It is the setting for many of the journal's most vivid passages — the place where Barbellion hears Beethoven's Fifth and Seventh Symphonies, where he watches Henry Wood conduct, and where music temporarily releases him from the grip of illness and self-consciousness.

## Barbellion at Queen's Hall

The journal records Queen's Hall concerts with the descriptive intensity of field observations. Barbellion notes the conductor's gestures ("While the music is calm and serene his right hand moves gently to and fro," 003-703), the behaviour of fellow audience members, and his own emotional responses. These passages are among the journal's most unguarded — music, at Queen's Hall, allows Barbellion to feel without analysing the feeling.

## Significance

Queen's Hall represents the cultural richness of Edwardian London — the access to great art that made the city, despite its costs, indispensable to Barbellion's inner life. When he writes "I must have some music or I shall hear the paralysis" (004-268), he is remembering, among other things, the consolations of Queen's Hall.
