## Overview

*The English Review*, founded in 1908 and edited by Ford Madox Ford (then Ford Madox Hueffer), was one of the most prestigious literary magazines of the Edwardian period, publishing work by Conrad, James, Lawrence, and Wells. For Barbellion, it represented the literary establishment he hoped to enter — and the establishment that, repeatedly, rejected him.

## Barbellion's Response

Barbellion's response to the *English Review* is recorded in a passage of pure [[disappointment|frustration]]: "The *English Review* returns my Essay. I am getting simply furious with an ambition I am unable to satisfy" (003-586). The rejection is not merely a professional setback but a personal wound, one that feeds into the larger complex of ambition, vanity, and disappointment that structures the journal. The *English Review* rejection becomes emblematic of the literary world's [[disappointment|failure]] to recognise him.

## Significance

The *English Review* represents the gatekeeping function of the literary establishment — the barrier between Barbellion's ambition and its fulfilment. His repeated submissions, despite repeated rejections, testify to a persistence that is either admirable or pathological, and the journal refuses to decide which.
