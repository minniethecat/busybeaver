## Overview

Thomas Hardy (1840–1928) was an English novelist and poet whose dark, fatalistic vision of human life resonated with Barbellion's own experience of suffering and disappointment. Barbellion read Hardy's novels early in the Journal and returned to his poetry in the later entries, finding in Hardy's melancholy a reflection of his own. His assessment of Hardy shifted from the ambivalent ("He is altogether delightful") to the genuinely appreciative, particularly of the poetry.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's engagement with Hardy followed the arc of his own maturation. In Part I (1903–1907), he read Hardy's novels as a young man forming his literary tastes, noting simply "Reading Hardy's novels. He is altogether delightful" (002-120). By Part III, after marriage, illness, and the war, Barbellion returned to Hardy's poetry with deeper appreciation: "I thoroughly en[[happiness|joy]] Hardy's poetry for its masterfulness" (004-355). In his final months, Hardy's poems were among the [[literature|books]] Barbellion kept close — "I return to Zion to my [[literature|books]], to Hardy's poems" (004-439). This trajectory from casual reader to devoted admirer mirrors Barbellion's broader intellectual and emotional development.

## References in the Journal

The earliest reference appears in Part I, where the young Barbellion records his [[literature|reading]]:

> "Reading Hardy's novels. He is altogether delightful." (002-120)

A later note in Part III records simply:

> "Hardy's Poetry" (004-346)

The most significant engagement comes in a detailed appreciation:

> "I thoroughly enjoy Hardy's poetry for its masterfulness" (004-355)

In a passage about returning to intellectual life after illness and upheaval:

> "I return to Zion to my books, to Hardy's poems, to Maurice de Guérin" (004-439)

## Biographical Note

Hardy began his career as a novelist, producing major works including *Tess of the d'Urbervilles* (1891) and *Jude the Obscure* (1895). The hostile reception of *Jude* led him to abandon fiction for poetry, and he published eight volumes of verse between 1898 and 1928. Hardy's poetry — concerned with time, loss, love, and the indifference of nature — was being published and republished during the period of Barbellion's Journal (1903–1917), meaning Barbellion was encountering Hardy as a living, still-creating poet.
