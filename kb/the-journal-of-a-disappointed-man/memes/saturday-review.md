## About

The *Saturday Review* was a British weekly review magazine founded in 1855 by Alexander Beresford Hope, covering politics, literature, science, and the arts. During the mid-Victorian period it gained a [[fame|reputation]] for acerbic, establishment-leaning commentary. By Barbellion's era in the 1910s, it had mellowed somewhat but remained a fixture of middle-class [[literature|reading]] — precisely the audience Barbellion delighted in provoking.

## Barbellion's Response

### The War and the Murder

Barbellion's engagement with the *Saturday Review* is concentrated in a single, coruscating passage. The *Review* had written about a murder case, describing it as committed "simply from motives of curiosity." Barbellion seizes on this phrase and turns it against the publication itself:

> The Saturday Review writes: ... What a lot of repulsive things the nice refined people who read the Saturday Review must find in the world just now. For example the War. "Simply from motives of curiosity." Why certainly, no other than these, concerning one of the most remarkable murders in the annals of crime. And murders anyhow are damned interesting — which the Saturday Review isn't.

(004-023, 004-025)

### The Art of the Put-Down

This passage is a masterclass in Barbellion's satirical method. The structure is worth noting:

1. **Framing**: He quotes the *Review* neutrally — "The Saturday Review writes:"
2. **Ventriloquism**: He adopts the voice of its imagined reader — "the nice refined people"
3. **Escalation**: The War itself is dragged in as another "repulsive thing" these refined readers must endure
4. **Pivot**: The *Review*'s formula "simply from motives of curiosity" is borrowed and reapplied
5. **Coup de grâce**: "murders anyhow are damned interesting — which the Saturday Review isn't"

The final line does double work: it dismisses the *Review* as dull while simultaneously aligning Barbellion with the vitality of real crime over the sterility of respectable opinion. It is, in miniature, the journal's entire ethos: the raw, the honest, the "damned interesting" over the polite, the censored, the boring.

### Class and Taste

The target of Barbellion's satire is not really the *Saturday Review* itself — a perfectly competent weekly — but the social formation it represents: the "nice refined people" whose relationship to the world is mediated by a publication that sanitises everything it touches, including murder and war. Barbellion's journal, by contrast, is a space where disgust, fascination, and irreverence can coexist without the smoothing hand of editorial propriety.
