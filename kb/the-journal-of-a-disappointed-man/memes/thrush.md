## Overview

The **Thrush** (*Turdus philomelos*), specifically the Song Thrush and Mistle Thrush, holds a foundational place in Barbellion's autobiography. It was the discovery of a thrush's nest and eggs in childhood that first ignited his passion for natural history — a moment he described as the uncoiling of a watch-spring that had lain hidden inside him.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's most formative natural history experience came early, when a benefactor showed him a thrush's nest:

> He — my good and beneficent angel — presently showed me a Thrush's nest in the fork of a young Oak tree. Never-to-be-forgotten moment! The sight of those blue eggs in the neat cup of moss and dried grass was the starting-point of my career as a naturalist. (PN=001-045)

He reflected on the moment's significance in shaping his life:

> In my childhood my enthusiasm lay like a watch-spring, coiled up and hidden inside me, until that Thrush's nest and eggs seized hold of it by the end and pulled it out by degrees in a long silver ribbon. (PN=001-046)

Throughout his convalescence, the thrush remained a symbol of solace and the accessible beauty of nature:

> I required no great knowledge of a naturalist to produce such delight, but merely the common objects — Sun, Thrush, Grasshopper, Primrose, and Dew. (PN=003-112)

He observed thrush behaviour closely enough to use it as metaphor for his lover:

> Every now and then she raises her head and listens (like a Thrush on the lawn) thinking she hears someone approach. (PN=003-349)

He also recorded an anecdote of childhood misidentification — a Mistle Thrush mistaken by a farm boy:

> Miss Lucy told Farmer Whaley who said George ought to know better — it was a Mistle-Thrush. (PN=001-087)

## Natural History

The Song Thrush breeds across Britain in woodland, hedgerows, and gardens. It is known for its loud, repetitive song and its habit of breaking snail shells on stone "anvils." The Mistle Thrush, a larger relative, is a bold, early-nesting species that sings even in winter storms. Barbellion encountered both in Devon.

## Taxonomic Context

The thrush sits at the centre of Barbellion's ornithological interests, alongside the [[starling]], [[european-goldfinch]], and [[tree-creeper]]. The discovery of its eggs marked the origin point of a scientific curiosity that would eventually lead him to professional work at the [[natural-history-museum-london]].
