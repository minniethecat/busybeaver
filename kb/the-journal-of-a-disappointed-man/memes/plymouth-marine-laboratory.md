## Overview

The Plymouth Marine Laboratory — then the laboratory of the Marine Biological Association (MBA) — represented, for a brief moment, Barbellion's escape route from provincial journalism into professional science. In November 1905, Dr. A—— offered him an assistantship at £60 a year. Barbellion accepted, only to resign before starting when his father's illness required him to take over the family reporting work. The episode encapsulates the central tension of the diary: scientific ambition versus domestic duty, the life of the mind versus economic necessity.

## In the Journal

Barbellion's first reference to Plymouth is transactional — a specimen purchased for dissection:

> "Dissected a Squilla for which I paid 2s. 6d. to the Plymouth Marine Laboratory." (002-147)

The Squilla (mantis shrimp) arrives by post, a fragment of marine biology sent inland to a teenager dissecting in his attic laboratory. Plymouth is already, at this stage, a name that stands for the real scientific world beyond Devon's hedgerows.

The offer comes weeks later:

> "Returned home and found a letter awaiting me from Dr. A—— offering me £60 a year for a temporary job as assistant at the Plymouth Marine Laboratory." (002-157)

Barbellion's immediate reaction is recorded elsewhere: "Left London horribly depressed. They evidently intend to shuffle me off" (002-158). Even the offer of what he most wants — a scientific post — arrives wrapped in the diarist's characteristic suspicion that he is being managed, diminished, sidelined.

Nevertheless, he accepts:

> "Have accepted the Plymouth appointment." (002-161)

The terse entry — four words — is characteristic of Barbellion's recording of life-changing decisions. No elaboration, no triumph. The brevity itself conveys the weight of the moment.

The resignation follows within weeks, when his father suffers a paralytic stroke:

> "Am taking over all his work and have written at once resigning the Plymouth appointment." (002-172)

The sequence — offer, acceptance, resignation — spans fewer than six weeks. H. G. Wells, in his preface, sums up the consequence: "It was a success, gained only to be renounced. His father was ill and he had to stand by his family; our economical country cannot afford to make biologists out of men who can earn a living as hack reporters" (P02-003).

## Significance

The Plymouth appointment is one of the Journal's pivotal life events — a door that opens and closes almost simultaneously. It establishes the pattern Barbellion would live: scientific talent recognised, then thwarted by circumstance. The laboratory at Plymouth, which he never actually worked in, becomes a symbol of the life not lived. His later post at the Natural History Museum (secured in 1911) would partly redeem this loss, but the Plymouth episode remains the diary's most concentrated expression of vocational disappointment.
