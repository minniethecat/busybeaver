## Overview

The common **Pigeon** (*Columba livia domestica*) appears in the Journal in two sharply contrasting registers: as the object of casual, sandwich-sharing affection in Trafalgar Square, and as the subject of serious anatomical investigation in the dissecting dish. The double life of the pigeon in Barbellion's pages mirrors his own double life as both a sensitive observer and a rigorous man of science.

## Observations in the Journal

The most vivid pigeon passage belongs to Barbellion's London years, a moment of solitary contentment in the heart of the capital:

> After sitting on the wall around the fountain in the middle of Trafalgar Square, eating my sandwiches and feeding the Pigeons with the crumbs, I listened for a moment to the roar of the traffic around three sides of the Square as I stood in the centre quite alone, what time one fat old pigeon, all unconcerned, was treading another. (PN=004-132)

The detail of the pigeons — feeding, treading, indifferent to the human drama — is both comic and tender, the observation of a man who cannot help being a naturalist even in the middle of London.

Later, the pigeon reappears under quite different circumstances — not as a companionable lunchtime presence but as an anatomical specimen:

> Her unfeigned interest in my anatomical researches gave me real pleasure, and I took delight in arousing her wonder by pointing out and explaining the brain of a Pigeon or the nervous system of a Dogfish, or a Frog's heart taken out and still beating in the dissecting dish. (PN=004-400)

Here the pigeon is the object of scientific demonstration, its brain laid bare for the edification of a fascinated companion — a scene that captures the peculiar charisma of Barbellion the anatomist.

## Natural History

The feral pigeon of British cities descends from the wild Rock Dove (*Columba livia*), domesticated thousands of years ago and subsequently escaped to colonise urban environments worldwide. Pigeons are among the most successful urban-adapted birds, breeding year-round on ledges and window sills that substitute for their ancestral cliff-face nesting sites. Their homing ability, flocking behaviour, and prodigious reproductive rate have made them one of the most intensively studied of all bird species.
