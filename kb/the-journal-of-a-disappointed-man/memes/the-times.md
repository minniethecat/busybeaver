**The Times**, the leading British daily newspaper founded in 1785, appears in Barbellion's journal during the [[First World War]] as a symbol of the uncertain flow of information in wartime. Its delayed delivery—"two days late"—provoked wry comment during a period when news of the conflict consumed the nation.

## Wartime Delays

In a domestic scene freighted with the anxiety of the war, the late arrival of *The Times* became a focal point of tension. Barbellion recorded a morning exchange during which a visitor, casually paring his nails, remarked on the paper's tardiness:

> A—— came into the room. He looked about him, sat down, took out a pocket knife, a lancet, a pair of scissors, a nail file, and began paring his nails. My father, glancing up from his everlasting *Daily News*, looked over his spectacles at him with some surprise, and returned to his paper without speaking. I sat on the sofa—uncomfortable. A—— then held up a newspaper cutting in his hand, saying, "*The Times* is behindhand." He handed it to my father who put on his glasses and read it. There was a long pause. Neither of us spoke. Then he slowly said: "I wonder why *The Times* is so behindhand. This is two days late." (004-122, 004-128)

## Barbellion and the Press

Barbellion's relationship with newspapers was intimate and professional. His father was a newspaper reporter, and Barbellion himself worked briefly as a reporter's apprentice before securing his position at the [[natural-history-museum-london|Natural History Museum]]. The *[[daily-news|Daily News]]* was his father's paper of choice. *The Times* represented the establishment press—the paper of record against which provincial and popular journalism defined itself.
