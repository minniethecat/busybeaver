## Overview

Death is the journal's most constant companion — more present even than ambition or illness, because it is the horizon against which both are defined. Barbellion confronts death not as an abstraction but as a daily reality: the deaths of neighbours, the death of his father, the prospect of his own death from multiple sclerosis. His treatment of death is characteristically unsentimental and self-aware: he knows that his obsession is partly vanity, partly curiosity, and partly genuine terror, and he refuses to simplify this mixture into anything more noble.

## In the Journal

### Early Fascination

Even before his [[illness|diagnosis]], death fascinates Barbellion. "When we are very young, Death arouses our curiosity," he notes, and the journal records deaths with the same descriptive attention given to rare birds: "There have [[honey-bee|bee]]n three deaths since I came home" (002-211). The sudden death of a Bank Manager becomes material for reflection on how "the mob loves a tragedy" (002-189). Death is, for the young [[natural-history|naturalist]], simply one more phenomenon to observe.

### The Turning Inward

As his own health deteriorates, death ceases to be an external spectacle and becomes an intimate preoccupation. "As an Egotist I hate death because I should cease to be" (003-035). This brutal honesty is typical: Barbellion refuses to dress his fear of death in philosophical consolation. He notes that "most folk, when sick unto death, gain a little consolation" from religion or philosophy, but he himself finds no such comfort (003-036). His ambition to be somebody continues "even in death" (003-037).

### Living with the Prospect

In the final Part, Barbellion arrives at a kind of accommodation — not acceptance, but a weary familiarity: "I have coquetted with death for so long now" (003-864). Yet even this coquetry does not diminish the terror: "How I loathe the prospect of death" (004-267). The journal's final position on death is not resolution but endurance: the simple, astonishing fact of continuing to live and write while knowing that death is near.

## Intellectual Context

Barbellion's meditation on death belongs to a Victorian and Edwardian tradition of mortality-[[literature|writing]] that includes Tennyson's *In Memoriam*, the elegies of Thomas Hardy, and the war poetry that would soon emerge from the trenches. But his voice is distinctive: less rhetorical than Tennyson, less stoic than Hardy, more nakedly personal than either. He writes about death not as a poet or philosopher but as a [[natural-history|naturalist]] observing his own extinction, taking notes until the very end.

## Barbellion's Perspective

Death, for Barbellion, is the ultimate test of his honesty. He refuses to pretend that death gives life meaning, or that suffering ennobles, or that he faces his end with courage. What he does instead is simply record — the fear, the curiosity, the vanity, the rage — with the same fidelity he brings to describing a squid or a [[honey-bee|bee]]ch tree. This is the journal's deepest achievement: it looks at death without blinking, and what it sees is not transcendence but the irreducible, terrified, curious, ambitious self.
