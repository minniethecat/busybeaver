## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) was the English naturalist whose theory of evolution by natural selection transformed nineteenth-century biology. For Barbellion, Darwin was not merely a historical figure but an active intellectual presence — his *Expression of the Emotions in Man and Animals* ranked as one of the three most fascinating science books Barbellion had ever read.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's relationship to Darwin was that of a young naturalist looking up to the towering figure of Victorian science. He never met Darwin (who died when Barbellion was an infant), but Darwin's works were central to his self-education in natural history. The "old gentleman" naturalist Barbellion encountered in Devon advised him to read "Darwin and J. G. Wood" (002-046), confirming Darwin's status as the default starting point for any aspiring naturalist in Edwardian England.

## References in the Journal

> The three most fascinating books in Science that I have so far read are (easily):—1. Darwin's Expression of the Emotions. 2. Gaskell's Origin of Vertebrates. 3. Bergson's Le Rire.
> — (002-274)

Barbellion's high ranking of Darwin's *Expression of the Emotions* is significant: it reveals his interest in the borderland between biology and psychology, between the physical and the emotional — a theme that runs throughout the journal. The same work influenced his own acute self-observation and his recording of minute emotional states.
