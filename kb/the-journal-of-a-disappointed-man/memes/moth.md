## Overview

Moths (order Lepidoptera, suborder Heterocera) are chiefly nocturnal relatives of butterflies. In Barbellion's Journal, moths appear in two distinct registers: as objects of the collecting culture he encountered among London entomologists, and as a literary image — the moth drawn fatally to flame — that resonates with his own sense of being pulled toward an early death.

## Observations in the Journal

Barbellion's most vivid moth reference comes from an Entomological Society meeting where he observes the culture of amateur collectors with characteristic irony:

> "There were a great many Scarabees present who exhibited to one another poor little pinned insects in collecting-boxes … It was really a one-man show, Prof. Poulton, a man of very considerable scientific attainments, being present, and shouting with a raucous voice in a way that must have scared some of the timid, unassuming collectors of our country's butterflies and moths." (003-387)

The dismissive tone — calling the collectors "Scarabees" and describing pinned insects as "poor little" — captures Barbellion's ambivalence about the entomological establishment. He admired scientific rigour but scorned its more pompous representatives.

In Part IV, the moth becomes a figure for his own predicament:

> "One of these days I should not be surprised if this fascinated earth did not fall into it like a moth into a candle." (004-436)

The image of the moth drawn to the candle — helpless, fascinated, self-destroying — is one Barbellion applies to the earth itself, contemplating a cosmic scale of fatal attraction.

## Natural History

Moths comprise the vast majority of Lepidoptera species, with over 2,500 recorded in the British Isles alone. Unlike butterflies, most moths are nocturnal, relying on moonlight for navigation — a behaviour that famously draws them to artificial lights. They occupy every terrestrial habitat and play crucial roles as pollinators and as prey for birds, bats, and other insectivores. The distinction between moths and butterflies is not taxonomic but practical: butterflies are a specialised day-flying clade nested within the broader moth lineage.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion groups moths with butterflies under the general heading of "Lepidoptera," reflecting the amateur naturalist's perspective of his time. His exposure to moth collecting came through the London scientific societies he frequented as a young man, where specimen display and nomenclature were social currencies. His wariness toward this culture aligns with his broader suspicion of institutional science when it lost touch with living nature.
