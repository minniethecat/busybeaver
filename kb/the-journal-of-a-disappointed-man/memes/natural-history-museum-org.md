## Overview

The Natural History Museum in South Kensington was, during Barbellion's time, still formally part of the British Museum (it gained full independence in 1963). It housed one of the world's great natural history collections and employed a staff of scientists, curators, and technicians. For Barbellion, employment at the Museum was both a professional achievement and a source of frustration — it placed him inside the institution he revered while keeping him on its periphery.

## Barbellion's Role

Barbellion worked at the Museum during the London years documented in Part II. His exact position is not specified in the journal, but his references to colleagues, specimens, and the daily rhythm of Museum life suggest an assistant or technical role. The position provided income, access to scientific resources, and proximity to the world of professional natural history — but it did not provide the recognition or autonomy he craved.

## Significance

The Museum embodies the institutional dimension of Barbellion's scientific identity. It is where he belongs professionally and where he feels most acutely his failure to belong fully. The gap between being a Museum employee and being a "great naturalist" is the gap that the journal exists to bridge.
