## Overview

Alfred, Lord Tennyson (1809–1892) was Poet Laureate of Great Britain during much of Queen Victoria's reign. His poetry — "In Memoriam," "The Charge of the Light Brigade," "Ulysses" — was widely memorised and quoted in Edwardian England. Barbellion encountered Tennyson's work primarily through conversational quotation, a testament to the poet's saturation of the educated English consciousness.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Tennyson appears in the Journal as a cultural touchstone rather than a subject of direct [[literature|reading]]. A friend quotes Tennyson "with great aptness" during conversation (003-658), and Barbellion records someone asking for a volume of Tennyson (004-343). These glancing references reveal Tennyson's position as shared cultural property — the poet everyone knew, whether or not they read him. Barbellion's own poetic preferences ran toward [[thomas-hardy|Hardy]] and Browning, yet Tennyson remained the benchmark of "poetry" as a social commodity.

## Biographical Note

Born in Lincolnshire, Tennyson attended Trinity College, Cambridge, where he joined the Apostles, an intellectual secret society. He succeeded Wordsworth as Poet Laureate in 1850 and held the post for forty-two years — the longest tenure in the laureateship's history. His work ranges from lyric (the Arthurian *Idylls of the King*) to elegy (*In Memoriam A.H.H.*) to stirring public verse. He was raised to the peerage as Baron Tennyson in 1884.
