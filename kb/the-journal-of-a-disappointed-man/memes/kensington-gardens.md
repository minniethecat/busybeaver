Kensington Gardens, one of London's Royal Parks adjoining [[hyde-park]], was a frequent setting for Barbellion's walks, romantic encounters, and natural observations during his time in [[london]]. Situated in [[South Kensington]] near the [[natural-history-museum-london|Natural History Museum]] where he worked, the Gardens appear repeatedly in his journal as a stage for moments of exultation, envy, reflection, and courtship.

## Walks and Social Encounters

Barbellion's walks in Kensington Gardens often involved companions and flirtatious conversation. On one August afternoon, a walk with a female companion and another friend led to a moment of playful banter by the Round Pond:

> After tea, we all three walked in Kensington Gardens and sat on a seat by the Round Pond. My umbrella fell to the ground, and I left it there with its nose poking up in a cynical manner, as She remarked. "It's not cynical," I said, "only a little knowing. Won't you let yours fall down to keep it company? Yours is a lady umbrella and a good-looking one—they might flirt together." "Mine doesn't want to flirt," she answered stiffly. (003-043)

On another occasion, walking with his friend R——, Barbellion experienced moments of youthful exuberance that bordered on the grandiose:

> R—— and I walked up through Kensington Gardens like two young Gods! "I even like that bloody thing," I said, pointing to the Albert Memorial. We pointed out pretty girls to one another, watched the children play ring-a-ring-a-roses on the grass. We laughed exultingly at the thought of our dismal colleagues… (003-496)

## Natural Observations

Despite his self-professed shift away from nature study towards music, Barbellion continued to observe the natural world in the Gardens. A passing seagull provoked an unexpected confession:

> In a very cheerful mood. Pleased with myself and everybody till a seagull soared overhead in Kensington Gardens and aroused my vast capacities for envy—I wish I could fly. (003-449)

His attention to seasonal rhythms persisted too, now reframed through a more aesthetic register:

> I look for the first parasol in Kensington Gardens with the same interest as once I sought out the first snowdrop or listened for the first Cuckoo. (003-697)

## Sunset

A brief, untitled entry captures a moment of stillness. Under the heading "Sunset in Kensington Gardens," Barbellion reflects on the instinct for worship that recurs at dawn and dusk—moments when, however "work-sodden" one may be, the rhythms of nature break through the hypnotism of daily routine. The Gardens at twilight offered a space for this kind of reflection. (003-504)

## A Glimpse of Beauty

On his way to the [[royal-albert-hall|Albert Hall]], Barbellion witnessed a scene of maternal beauty that began in the Gardens and continued onto a bus. A young mother with her little boy—"with his long hair and dressed in velvet like little Lord Fauntleroy"—caught his attention. As the bus travelled, her gaze met his:

> In travelling backwards from Kensington Gardens to the boy again, her gaze rested on me a moment and I, of course, rendered the homage that was due. (003-613)
