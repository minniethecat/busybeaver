## Overview

Marie Bashkirtseff (1858--1884) occupies a singular position in Barbellion's journal: she is the figure with whom he identifies most completely. More than [[john-keats]], more than [[beethoven]], more even than [[sir-thomas-browne]] -- Bashkirtseff is not merely an influence but a double, a mirror in which Barbellion sees his own [[ambition]], his own [[self-consciousness]], and his own confrontation with early [[death]]. Both were young diarists burning with the conviction of their own exceptionality. Both were artists who feared obscurity more than extinction. Both died before their work was recognised. And both produced journals that would be published posthumously to considerable acclaim -- journals whose power lies in their unsparing honesty about the self. In Bashkirtseff, Barbellion found not a predecessor but a twin, and his [[fame|recognition]] of this kinship -- "We are identical!" (003-564) -- is one of the journal's most electrifying moments of self-discovery.

## Relationship to Barbellion

Barbellion's relationship to Bashkirtseff differs in kind from his other literary identifications. With [[sir-thomas-browne|Sir Thomas Browne]], the connection is one of stylistic and intellectual paternity -- [[sir-thomas-browne|Browne]] is a father. With [[john-keats|Keats]], it is one of fellow-feeling across the gulf of a century. But with Bashkirtseff, the identification is total and almost uncanny. He does not admire her; he recognises her. The discovery comes by accident, mediated through a book on Strindberg: "I noticed a quotation from one, Marie Bashkirtseff in a book on Strindberg" (003-563). What follows is not gradual appreciation but immediate, shocked [[fame|recognition]]:

> "...nished. We are identical! Oh, Marie Bashkirtseff! how we should have hated one..." (003-564)

The exclamatory punctuation -- the dashes, the exclamation marks, the ellipsis -- conveys the force of the [[fame|recognition]]. This is not a reader finding a kindred spirit; it is a person discovering a ghost who has lived his life before him. The broken sentence "how we should have hated one..." is particularly revealing: Barbellion understands that the very qualities that make them identical -- their consuming [[vanity|self-regard]], their hunger for [[fame|recognition]], their competitive vanity -- would have made them rivals had they met. Identification does not imply affection; it implies uncomfortable truth.

Later in the journal, Barbellion elevates Bashkirtseff to the highest possible position in his personal mythology. He writes:

> "...r Thomas Browne and my mother Marie Bashkirtseff. See what a curious hybrid I..." (004-152)

This is the journal's most audacious declaration of elective kinship. [[sir-thomas-browne|Browne]] and Bashkirtseff are his spiritual parents -- the father of baroque, metaphysical prose and the mother of confessional self-dramatisation. Between them they produce Barbellion: a "curious hybrid" of seventeenth-century learned wit and nineteenth-century Romantic self-absorption. The metaphor of hybridity is exact: Barbellion's journal is precisely the offspring of [[sir-thomas-browne|Browne]]'s philosophical curiosity and Bashkirtseff's emotional intensity, the one providing the intellectual framework, the other the confessional urgency.

Bashkirtseff also serves Barbellion as a practical model. She proved that a diary could be literature -- that the record of a private self could, after death, become a public work of art. Her *Journal*, published in 1887 (abridged) and in full in 1908, had [[honey-bee|bee]]n a European sensation, and Barbellion, [[literature|reading]] it in translation, would have seen a precedent for his own project. He "read the Journal of Marie Bashkirtseff" alongside eating and bathing (003-821), absorbing it as a daily necessity -- sustenance for the project of becoming himself on the page.

## Biographical Note

Marie Bashkirtseff (1858--1884) was born to a wealthy noble family in what is now Ukraine and spent most of her short life in France and Italy. Ambitious from childhood, she pursued both painting and [[literature|writing]] with fierce determination. She studied at the Academie Julian in Paris, where she was one of the first women admitted to the atelier system, and produced a substantial body of realist paintings, the best known of which is *The Meeting* (1884), now in the Musee d'Orsay. Diagnosed with tuberculosis in her early twenties, she began keeping a journal in French at the age of fourteen and continued until her death at twenty-five. The journal -- candid, vain, brilliant, and self-dramatising -- was published posthumously in an abridged French edition in 1887 and became a European literary sensation, drawing admirers that included Gladstone, Shaw, and the young Anais Nin. Barbellion would have encountered it in the expanded 1908 edition or in Mathilde Blind's English translation, which circulated widely in Edwardian literary circles. The parallels between Bashkirtseff's trajectory and Barbellion's own are striking: both were autodidacts of exceptional [[ambition|drive]], both used the journal form as a vehicle for [[ambition|literary ambition]], both confronted terminal illness in their twenties, and both achieved posthumous [[fame|recognition]] through the very diaries they kept while [[death|dying]].
