## Overview

The **Cuckoo** (*Cuculus canorus*) is a migratory bird that arrives in Britain each spring, famous for its distinctive two-note call and its habit of laying eggs in the nests of other birds. For Barbellion, the cuckoo was a seasonal marker — a sound he eagerly listened for in his youth and later heard from his window during the long months of illness.

## Observations in the Journal

In younger days, Barbellion sought the cuckoo's first call with the same keen anticipation he brought to all natural phenomena:

> I look for the first parasol in Kensington Gardens with the same interest as once I sought out the first snowdrop or listened for the first Cuckoo. (PN=004-438)

During an idyllic period at his writing desk, the cuckoo's call was part of a springtime symphony:

> Sat at my escritoire in the Studio this morning writing an Essay, with a large 4-fold window on my left, looking on to woods and fields, with Linnets, Greenfinches, Cuckoos calling. (PN=004-546)

In the final phase of his illness, the cuckoo was among the birds whose calls reached him through the window — a thread connecting his bedridden present to the active field-naturalist past:

> The Swallows have arrived at last, though they were very late, and there are also Cuckoos, Green Woodpeckers, Moorhens, calling from across the park. (PN=004-783)

## Natural History

The cuckoo is a brood parasite: the female lays her eggs singly in the nests of host species — typically reed warblers, dunnocks, or meadow pipits — and the young cuckoo ejects the host's eggs or chicks. Adult cuckoos arrive in Britain from sub-Saharan Africa in April, the males calling persistently to establish territory. By July, adults begin the return migration south. Barbellion's references span this full seasonal arc, from the "first Cuckoo" of spring to the summer chorus.
