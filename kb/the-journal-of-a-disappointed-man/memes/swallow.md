## Overview

The Swallow (*Hirundo rustica*), also known as the Barn Swallow, is a small migratory passerine with dark blue upperparts, a rufous throat, and a deeply forked tail. For Barbellion, the swallow was more than a field observation — it was a seasonal marker, a creature whose comings and goings measured the passage of time against his own mortality. He records their arrival with relief each spring and, in autumn, feels a kinship with their migratory restlessness.

## Observations in the Journal

Swallows appear at emotionally charged moments throughout the Journal. In Part I, the thirteen-year-old Barbellion already registers them with poetic attention:

> "Bees, Poppies, and Swallows!" (002-134)

By Part III, the swallow becomes a figure for his own condition. Watching them from a train window, he extends his characteristic universal sympathy:

> "I felt sorry for the others as well — for the two brown carters in the road ambling along with a timber wagon, for the two old maids in the same compartment with me knitting bedsocks, for the beautiful Swallows darting over the stream, for the rabbit that lopped into the fern just as we passed — they too were all leaving it." (003-233)

In Part IV, the swallow becomes an explicit metaphor for his own psychological state:

> "I am as full of unrest as an autumn Swallow. 'My soul,' I said to them at breakfast with a sardonic grin, 'is like a greyhound in the slips.'" (004-370)

Later that same year he notes their belated spring return:

> "However, the Swallows have arrived at last, though they were very late, and there are also Cuckoos, Green Woodpeckers, Moorhens, calling from across the park." (004-495)

And in a moment of painful self-awareness:

> "I seem unable to apprehend all the significance of having a nine-months old daughter, but some Bullfinches or Swallows seen through the window rouse me more." (004-504)

## Natural History

Swallows are long-distance migrants, breeding across the Northern Hemisphere and wintering in sub-Saharan Africa. They arrive in Britain from late March and depart by October. Their flight is agile and acrobatic as they hawk for flying insects, often near water. They are among the most familiar and beloved of British summer birds, traditionally nesting in barns, stables, and under the eaves of buildings.

## Taxonomic Context

*Hirundo rustica* belongs to the family Hirundinidae, which includes martins and swallows worldwide. Barbellion's close observation of swallows fits his broader pattern of attending to common, visible species — the kind of wildlife that announces the seasons to anyone paying attention. His use of the swallow as a metaphor for psychological unrest also demonstrates the literary sensibility that sets his natural history apart from purely taxonomic writing.
