## Overview

The **Tawny Owl** (*Strix aluco*) is a medium-sized woodland owl native to Britain and much of Eurasia. Barbellion referred to it as the "Brown Owl" and listened to its calls during the long months of his illness, finding in the bird a kindred spirit.

## Observations in the Journal

During the period when he was largely bedridden with multiple sclerosis, Barbellion took comfort in the natural sounds outside his window. The tawny owl's hooting became a nightly companion:

> At night, when the moon is up, I get a great deal of fun out of an extremely self-inflated Brown Owl, who hoots up through the breadth and length of the valley, and then I am sure, listens with satisfaction to his echo. Still, I have much sympathy with that Brown Owl and his hooting. (PN=004-783)

Earlier in his life, Barbellion had risen before dawn specifically to hear owls:

> Got up at sunrise to hear an Owl still hooting, a Lark singing, and several Jackdaws clattering on our tin roof with their toes. (PN=002-240)

He also recorded a barn owl sighting during a woodland walk:

> Went to A—— Wood with S—— and L——. Saw a Barn Owl (*Strix flammea*) flying in broad daylight. (PN=003-590)

## Natural History

The tawny owl is the most common owl in British woodlands. Strictly nocturnal, it is more often heard than seen. Its characteristic *hoo-hoo-hoo* call carries far through still night air. The species is territorial year-round and nests in tree hollows. Barbellion's observation of it "hooting up through the breadth and length of the valley" captures the acoustic experience of this bird in the Devon landscape.

## Taxonomic Context

Barbellion distinguished the Brown Owl from the Barn Owl, demonstrating his familiarity with British owl species. Both were part of his wider interest in birdsong and field ornithology, which also encompassed the [[starling]], [[european-goldfinch]], [[tree-creeper]], and sundry warblers.
