## Overview

Disappointment is the emotional signature of Barbellion's journal — so central that it gives the book its title. It is not a single event but a recurring rhythm: hope rises, hope is dashed, hope rises again in a different form. Barbellion's disappointment is distinctive because he does not simply suffer it; he anatomises it, traces its sources, and — in his most characteristic move — examines his own role in producing it. The journal is not a complaint about life's unfairness but an investigation into the machinery of disappointment itself.

## In the Journal

### The Shape of Disappointment

Barbellion's disappointments take many forms. There is the disappointment of rejected manuscripts: "The *[[the-english-review|English Review]]* returns my Essay. I am getting simply furious with an ambition I am unable to satisfy" (003-586). There is the disappointment of health: "How restless this disappointment has made me" (003-026). And there is the deeper disappointment of self-knowledge — the growing awareness that his ambition may exceed his abilities. "What a bitter disappointment it is to realise that people are not larger-minded" (004-145).

### Disappointment as Philosophy

Barbellion does not merely record disappointments; he tries to build a philosophy around them. "Am attempting to feel after some [[philosophy|practical philosophy]] of living — something that will enable me to accept disappointment with equanimity" (002-149). The attempt is not entirely successful — the journal is proof of that — but the effort itself is characteristic: disappointment is not to be escaped but understood.

### The Nadir and the Zenith

In one of the journal's most revealing passages, Barbellion describes his emotional oscillation: "I go from the nadir of disappointment to the zenith of hope and back again" (004-226). This rhythm — hope and disappointment in perpetual alternation — is the journal's pulse. Disappointment never becomes final; hope never becomes secure. The two are locked in an exhausting, generative dance.

## Intellectual Context

Disappointment has a distinguished literary history — from Ecclesiastes to Samuel Johnson to the existentialists — but Barbellion's treatment is more psychological than philosophical. He is less interested in what disappointment *means* than in what it *does*: how it shapes behaviour, fuels ambition, poisons [[happiness|contentment]], and paradoxically sustains the journal itself. In this he anticipates the twentieth-century literature of disappointment — Beckett, Larkin, Bernhard — while remaining rooted in the Edwardian tradition of candid self-examination.

## Barbellion's Perspective

Barbellion's honesty about disappointment is radical. He does not claim that disappointment has made him wiser or better; he does not find redemption in suffering. He simply records the experience with unsparing precision, and in doing so transforms private frustration into public art. The journal's title is, in this sense, a promise kept: it really is the journal of a disappointed man, and the disappointment it records is real, specific, and unconsoled.
