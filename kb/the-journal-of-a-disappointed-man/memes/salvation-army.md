The **Salvation Army**, founded in 1865 by William and Catherine Booth, was a Christian evangelical movement organised on quasi-military lines. By the Edwardian period it was a prominent and controversial presence in British urban life, known for its street-corner brass bands, open-air meetings, uniforms, and extensive social welfare work among the urban poor.

## Barbellion's Connection

Barbellion never attended a Salvation Army meeting, but he invoked the organisation in one of his most penetrating analyses of crowd psychology—a passage in which the religious assembly is treated not as a spiritual event but as a zoological phenomenon:

> A crowd makes egotists of us all. Most men find it repugnant to them to submerge themselves in a sea of their fellows. A silent, listening crowd is potentially full of commotion. Some poor devils suffocating and unable any longer to bear the strain will shout, "Bravo," or "Hear, hear," at every opportunity. At the feeblest joke we all laugh loudly, welcoming this means of self-survival. (003-873)

From this general theory of crowd behaviour—the "sea of fellows," the suffocating pressure of silence, the laugh as a survival strategy—he moves to his specific case:

> Hence the success of the Salvation Army. To be preached at and prayed for in the mass for long on end is what human nature can't endure in silence and a good deal of self can be smuggled by an experienced Salvationist into "Alleluia" or "The Lord be praised." (003-873)

## In the Journal

This passage exemplifies Barbellion's method as a naturalist of human behaviour. The Salvation Army meeting is analysed not as religion but as ecosystem: individuals struggle for "self-survival" in a "sea of fellows," each "Alleluia" a strategic move to preserve identity against the mass. His framing—"self-survival," "self can be smuggled," "experienced Salvationist"—treats the congregation as a population of organisms, each employing adaptive strategies to avoid dissolution in the group.

The analysis is characteristically double-edged. On one level, it is reductive: religious experience is explained away as crowd psychology, the sacred reduced to the strategic. But on another level, it is sympathetic: Barbellion understood the terror of losing oneself in a crowd, and he recognised in the Salvationist's "Alleluia" the same impulse that drove him to fill a journal with assertions of his own existence. Both are strategies against disappearance.

The passage also reveals the breadth of Barbellion's intellectual range. The analysis of crowd behaviour draws on the same observational framework he applied to [[ant|ants]], [[beetle|beetles]], and the [[entomological-society|entomologists]] themselves. His genius was to see that the naturalist's methods—close observation, classification, the search for adaptive explanation—could be applied as productively to a religious meeting as to a rock pool.

## Historical Context

By the Edwardian period, the Salvation Army had grown from its origins in the East End of London into an international organisation with both fervent supporters and vocal critics. Its military structure, its use of popular music, and its work among the "submerged tenth" of the urban poor made it one of the most visible and distinctive features of British religious life. For intellectuals of Barbellion's generation, it represented both the vitality of popular religion and its susceptibility to sociological analysis.
