# "I must and will be your wife!"

Cried out by [[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]] in a moment of "misery and madness" [002-076] as [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]] attempts to release her from their engagement, this declaration establishes the relentless determination that will define [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s character throughout the novel—and acquires retrospective irony once the secret [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] is concealing comes to light.

## Full Quotation

> "Go where you may," I said, "I go with you! Friends—reputation—I care nothing who I lose, or what I lose! Oh, Eustace, I am only a woman—don't madden me! I can't live without you. I must and will be your wife!" [002-075]

## Context

The quotation occurs in Chapter II, during the period of [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] and [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s engagement. [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] has received [[major-fitz-david|Major Fitz-David]]'s letter of introduction and, reacting to it with visible distress, attempts to release [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] from their engagement—though he cannot tell her why. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s response, a mixture of romantic passion and fierce determination, overrides his objections. The scene takes place at night, under moonlight, with the heightened emotional pitch characteristic of the sensation novel's treatment of romantic crisis.

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s reaction to the letter—"He almost snatched the letter out of my hand; he turned his back on me" [002-045]—establishes the pattern of concealment that will govern the first half of the novel. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s insistence that she "must and will" be his wife, spoken in ignorance of what he is hiding, will be tested by the revelation that follows their marriage.

## Significance

This quotation functions as both a character-defining moment and a structural pivot. At the character level, it establishes [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s defining trait: a will that refuses to be deflected by obstacles, whether those obstacles are social convention, legal limitation, or her husband's own resistance. The same determination that here insists on marriage will later insist on investigation—the "must and will" of the bride becomes the "must and will" of the detective.

The quotation also operates ironically. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] declares that she cares nothing for "friends—reputation," yet it is precisely her husband's reputation—destroyed by the [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]]—that will become the central problem of their marriage. The "what I lose" that she dismisses so readily will turn out to include the social standing, domestic security, and marital transparency that she assumed were hers by right.

Collins uses the quotation's position early in the novel to establish a dramatic irony that will sustain the narrative through its investigative middle section: the reader knows what [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] has pledged, and watches as each new discovery tests whether the pledge can survive the truth.
