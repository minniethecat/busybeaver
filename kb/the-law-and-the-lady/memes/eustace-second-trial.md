# Eustace's Vindication

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]]'s vindication is the narrative's resolution — the legal acknowledgment, however belated, that the Not Proven verdict was wrong (048-003). Achieved not through institutional processes but through his wife's amateur detective work, the vindication represents the novel's ultimate argument: that truth, when pursued with sufficient determination, can overcome even the failures of legal systems (030-030).

## Summary

With the new evidence gathered by [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] — the key, the dressing-gown, and the cosmetic-arsenic theory — [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s case is reopened. The evidence that the original trial lacked is now available, and the result is a declaration that transforms the Not Proven into something approaching exoneration (048-003).

## Context

[[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s entire investigation has been aimed at this moment. Every witness she questioned, every document she studied, every PN reference she tracked was building toward the moment when the legal verdict could be challenged by material fact (028-044).

## Key Details

The vindication is achieved through the combined force of multiple pieces of evidence: [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s narrative (recorded by [[benjamin|Benjamin]]), the physical evidence from [[gleninch|Gleninch]], and the contextual knowledge about cosmetic arsenic use that [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] uncovered. Together, they form a case that the original trial never had the opportunity to consider (050-038).

## Consequences

The vindication resolves the novel's central conflict but leaves open a deeper question: what would have happened if [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] had not been relentless? The novel's answer is sobering — without her, the Not Proven verdict would have stood forever, and an innocent man would have lived under permanent suspicion (014-017). Justice, Collins suggests, is not a property of legal systems but of human determination.
