# Death of Sara Macallan

The death of [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] — [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s first wife — is the event around which the entire novel revolves. She died at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] on the 21st of the month, and the post-mortem examination determined that she had died of "poisoning by arsenic" (017-005). Whether the arsenic was administered by her husband (murder), taken by herself (suicide), or absorbed accidentally through cosmetic use (misadventure) is the question that the trial fails to answer and that [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] must ultimately resolve.

## Summary

[[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] fell ill in early October at [[gleninch|Gleninch]], where she was staying with her husband [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]. Despite the attentions of a nurse ([[christina-ormsay|Christina Ormsay]]) and a physician, her condition worsened, and she died on the 21st. The post-mortem examination revealed arsenic in her body, leading to [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s arrest and trial.

## Context

The household at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] was tense. [[sara-macallan|Sara]] was "a very plain woman" who was jealous of her husband's attractive cousin, [[mrs-beauly|Mrs. Beauly]], who had come to stay at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] (016-018). Also present was [[miserrimus-dexter|Miserrimus Dexter]], [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s eccentric friend. The social dynamics of the household — husband, wife, attractive female guest, deformed male friend — created a volatile chemistry that the trial would attempt to parse as criminal evidence.

## Key Details

### The Illness

The nurse [[christina-ormsay|Christina Ormsay]] testified that [[sara-macallan|Sara]]'s illness began on the 7th of October (036-045). Initially attributed to natural causes, the symptoms were consistent with arsenic poisoning: nausea, pain, and progressive weakness. The attending physician, "Dr. Jerome and Mr. Gale, positively swore that the symptoms were those of poisoning by arsenic" (016-068).

### The Arsenic

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] had purchased arsenic on two occasions — once for the gardener's conservatories and once for the cook's rat poison (017-027, 017-057). He testified that he handed both packets "to my wife immediately on my return home" (017-058), and that "my wife was the person who gave orders to the gardener and cook — not I" (017-058).

### The Post-Mortem

The post-mortem examination "proved that [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] had died of poisoning by arsenic" (017-005). The quantity found was sufficient to be fatal, and no natural disease could account for the symptoms.

### The Alternative Theory

[[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] later suggests that [[sara-macallan|Sara]] had been using arsenic as a cosmetic — a practice not uncommon in the Victorian period — and that her death was the result of this secret habit rather than anyone's deliberate act. This theory, never tested in court, becomes central to [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation.

## Consequences

[[sara-macallan|Sara]]'s death triggers the entire chain of events: the investigation, the trial, the Not Proven verdict, and [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s subsequent detective work. More subtly, the death haunts [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s second marriage from its inception — [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] marries a man defined by his relationship to a dead woman whose fate remains unresolved. The dead [[sara-macallan|Sara]] exerts more narrative force than many living characters, her absence shaping every subsequent event.

## Critical Analysis

[[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] is a classic sensation-novel figure: the dead first wife whose mysterious death casts a shadow over the entire narrative (compare the first Mrs. Rochester in *Jane Eyre* or Laura Fairlie's apparent death in Collins' own *The Woman in White*). Collins innovates on the convention by making the death's ambiguity — rather than the death itself — the engine of the plot, and by giving the second wife ([[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]) the investigative agency typically reserved for male detectives.
