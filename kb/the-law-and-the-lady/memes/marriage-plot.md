# Marriage Plot

The **marriage plot** in Wilkie Collins's *The Law and the Lady* opens the novel in the most conventional terms possible—"Concluding the marriage service of the Church of England" [001-003]—only to systematically dismantle every assumption that the Victorian reader might bring to a wedding narrative. What begins as a romance between [[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]] and [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]] rapidly transforms into an investigation, as the marriage becomes the site where hidden crimes, suppressed identities, and the failures of the legal system converge.

## Summary

The marriage plot unfolds in three movements. First, the courtship and secret wedding: [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] and [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] marry hastily in [[london|London]], with only [[benjamin|Benjamin]] and [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s uncle and aunt as witnesses [001-009]. Second, the revelation: during the honeymoon at [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]], [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] discovers that her husband has concealed a previous trial for the murder of his first wife [003-018]. Third, the transformation: rather than accept the tainted marriage, [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] reconstitutes it as an investigative partnership, working to clear her husband's name and thereby retroactively legitimize their union.

## Context

The novel's marriage plot must be understood against the backdrop of Victorian matrimonial law and custom. The [[matrimonial-causes-act|Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857]] had recently reformed divorce procedures, but marriage remained an institution in which women surrendered legal identity to their husbands. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s decision to sign the marriage register with her husband's concealed surname—"I signed the wrong name in the marriage register" [006-030]—marks the moment at which legal form and hidden truth begin to diverge.

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s mother's opposition to the marriage [001-005, 003-016] introduces the intergenerational dimension of the marriage plot. The older generation's knowledge of [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s past—knowledge that is deliberately withheld from [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]—makes the marriage a transaction conducted under false pretenses from its inception.

## Key Details

**The wedding itself** is rushed and private. Conducted in [[london|London]] rather than in the bride's home parish, witnessed by the minimum legal number, and immediately followed by a departure for [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]], the ceremony has none of the public celebration that Victorian society expected of a respectable marriage. This privacy, initially explained by [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]]'s opposition, is retrospectively revealed as a necessity imposed by [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s legal history.

**The honeymoon at [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]]** becomes the site of revelation. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s idyllic expectation of married life—"we had determined to make our honeymoon a holiday" [003-006]—collapses when she discovers evidence of her husband's previous marriage and trial. The moment is structurally analogous to the Gothic tradition of the bride discovering the forbidden chamber: the husband's past is the locked room that must be opened.

**The marriage under the shadow of "Not Proven"** constitutes the novel's central dramatic situation. [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] offers [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] the option of annulment: "If it be possible to annul our marriage, let it be done" [013-038]. Her refusal—"I must and will be your wife" [002-075]—transforms the marriage from a legal contract into an ethical commitment that the law alone cannot sustain.

## Consequences

The marriage plot's resolution is inseparable from the discovery plot's resolution. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] can only fully become [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s wife once she has cleared his name, and [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] can only fully become a husband once he is no longer "the prisoner at the bar." The novel's ending thus unites the domestic and legal resolutions: marriage is restored not by legal process but by the truth that legal process failed to discover.

## Critical Analysis

Collins's marriage plot rewrites the conventions of the Victorian novel by making the wife the agent of her husband's redemption rather than its passive recipient. Where a novel like *Jane Eyre* ends with marriage as the reward for virtue, *The Law and the Lady* begins with marriage and then interrogates its foundations. The sensation novel's characteristic disruption of domestic security is here enacted through the marriage plot itself: the home, far from being a refuge from legal and social danger, is the very place where the law's failures are most intimately experienced.
