# Lady Clarinda

Lady Clarinda is a character who never appears on stage — she exists only through gifts, gossip, and the reactions of others. A married woman of society who sends "nosegays three times a week" to [[major-fitz-david|Major Fitz-David]] (010-097), she represents the world of romantic intrigue that surrounds [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation without ever directly intersecting it.

## Role in the Novel

Lady Clarinda functions as a comic off-stage presence and a social mirror. Her nosegays to Fitz-David — "With Lady Clarinda's kind regards. To remind [[major-fitz-david|Major Fitz-David]] of his promise" (009-015) — signal that the Major's romantic attentions are distributed rather than exclusive. She is the unseen rival to the railway-girl who tramples her flowers, and her off-stage existence highlights the network of relationships that surround the central mystery.

## Character Description

Though never physically described in the novel, Lady Clarinda is characterized through objects and others' words. She is "a brazen-faced hussy" according to the jealous railway-girl (010-097), and "my charming Lady Clarinda" according to Fitz-David (009-019), who praises her "gift of sympathy" — the same phrase he uses to flatter [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]], suggesting his compliments are formulaic.

## Key Actions & Plot Involvement

### The Nosegay

The nosegay she sends to Fitz-David arrives during [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s visit, becoming a plot device: it reveals the Major's involvement with other women, provides the jealous railway-girl with a target for her fury, and ultimately helps [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] understand the social world she is navigating (010-107).

### The Dinner That Never Happens

Fitz-David enthusiastically proposes a dinner for "You and I and Lady Clarinda" (009-022), placing [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] and Clarinda as parallel objects of his admiration. The dinner is never held — a fitting non-event for a character who exists only in others' plans and reactions.
