# Verdict of Not Proven

The delivery of the verdict of Not Proven in [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]]'s trial is the legal event that defines the entire novel. Neither guilty nor innocent, neither convicted nor acquitted, [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] is left in a state of legal and social limbo — a condition that the novel presents as more cruel than conviction, because it denies the possibility of closure.

## Summary

After hearing all the evidence, the jury — "in doubt whether to condemn or acquit" — "got out of the difficulty by finding a verdict of Not Proven" (011-062). The verdict means that the charge "has not been brought home to the prisoner" (014-018), but it carries no declaration of innocence. [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] leaves the court neither a convicted murderer nor an innocent man — he leaves as a question that the legal system has declined to answer.

## Context

The Scottish legal system offers three verdicts: Guilty, Not Guilty, and Not Proven. The third option — unavailable in English law — allows a jury to express the view that the prosecution's case was plausible but insufficient for conviction.

## Key Details

The verdict's ambiguity is merciless. Friends abandon [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]: "Not Proven won't do for us. If the jury have not found him innocent, we will find him guilty" (012-036). Society treats the verdict as equivalent to conviction, while the law treats it as equivalent to acquittal. [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] exists in the gap between the two.

## Consequences

The Not Proven verdict is what [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] must overturn — not through legal appeal (which she pursues unsuccessfully) but through her own investigation. Her goal is to transform the verdict's ambiguity into certainty: to prove what the law could not, that [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] is innocent.
