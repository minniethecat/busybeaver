# Marriage Register

The **marriage register** in Wilkie Collins's *The Law and the Lady* appears at first as nothing more than the routine bureaucratic conclusion to a wedding ceremony—"the signing of the marriage register" [001-011]—but rapidly acquires significance as the physical site where [[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]]'s married identity is formally inscribed in a document that already contains the first falsehood of her new life. The register is both a piece of legal [[evidence]] and a symbol of the gap between legal form and lived truth that the novel's investigation must bridge.

## Description

The marriage register is a standard Church of England parish register, signed by the bride, groom, and witnesses immediately following the wedding ceremony. In the normal course of events, the register records the legal fact of marriage: the names of the parties, the date, the officiating clergyman, and the witnesses. In *The Law and the Lady*, however, this ordinary document becomes extraordinary through the act of signing. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] signs with what she later realizes is the wrong surname—not her husband's legal name but the name under which she knew him before discovering his previous trial for murder.

## Key Passages

> In the confusion of the moment, I signed the wrong name in the marriage register. [006-030]

> You remember signing your name in the church register. Was there ever a marriage that began as ours began? [014-035]

## Evidentiary Significance

The marriage register functions as evidence on multiple levels. As a legal document, it records the fact of the marriage—but the name [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] signed is not the name that would have appeared on [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s previous marriage register, nor the name under which he was tried. The register thus simultaneously proves and obscures: it proves that a marriage took place, but it obscures the identity of the groom in relation to his legal past.

For [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]], the moment of realizing what she signed becomes a turning point in her understanding of her marriage. The register—which she initially regarded as a mere formality—retroactively reveals itself as the document in which she unknowingly participated in the concealment of her husband's identity. "Shall I own it? My pride shrinks from the confession" [006-030]: the register becomes not just evidence of marriage but evidence of her own complicity in the secret that threatens to destroy it.

## Legal Context

Under Victorian marriage law, the parish register was the authoritative record of a marriage's existence and validity. Errors in the register could—and did—become grounds for challenging the legality of a union. Collins, with his legal training, understood that the marriage register was not merely a ceremonial record but a document with profound legal consequences. By making the register the site of [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s first (unwitting) falsehood within her marriage, Collins transforms a bureaucratic object into a narrative mechanism that links the domestic plot to the legal plot.
