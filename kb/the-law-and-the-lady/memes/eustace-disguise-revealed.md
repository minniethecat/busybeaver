# Eustace's Disguise Revealed

The exposure of [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]]'s false identity — the alias "[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]]" under which he courted and married [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] — is less a single dramatic event than a gradual, agonizing process of revelation. Each new disclosure strips away another layer of the disguise: the letter from Fitz-David reveals the name "Macallan," the trial transcript reveals the murder charge, and the [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]] landlady reveals that [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] does not recognize her own son's wife.

## Summary

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s disguise is both practical (changing his name to escape the stigma of the Not Proven verdict) and psychological (attempting to become a different person — a man without a past). The novel systematically dismantles both dimensions, showing that identity cannot be escaped by changing a name and that the past cannot be outrun by moving to a different country (011-062) (014-017).

## Context

After the trial, [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] fled Scotland and adopted the name "[[eustace-macallan|Woodville]]" (001-016). He met [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] in England, courted her under this false identity, and married her without disclosing his history (002-046). His mother's opposition to the marriage was rooted in her knowledge that the alias would inevitably collapse: she knew that "Woodville" was a fiction and refused to participate in the deception (005-028) (006-015).

## Key Details

The disguise unravels in stages, each revelation building on the last. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s determination to know the truth — rather than to flee from it — is what distinguishes her from the wife who might have simply accepted the alias and lived in ignorance (001-019).

## Consequences

The exposure of [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s disguise paradoxically strengthens [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s marriage. Once the alias is gone, there is nothing left to hide — and it is at this point that [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s real work (proving her husband's innocence) can begin (028-044). The novel suggests that identity founded on concealment is inherently unstable, and that only truth — however painful — can provide a foundation for genuine relationship.
