# The Post-Mortem Examination

The post-mortem examination of [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]]'s body is the forensic event that transforms the novel's mystery from a private tragedy into a public criminal case. The examination "proved that [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] had died of poisoning by arsenic" (017-005), setting in motion the chain of legal events — arrest, trial, and the Not Proven verdict — that define the narrative.

## Summary

Following [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]]'s death at [[gleninch|Gleninch]], a post-mortem examination was conducted. The surgeons who performed it — "Dr. Jerome and Mr. Gale" — "positively swore that the symptoms were those of poisoning by arsenic" (016-068). The finding of arsenic in the body was the sole piece of physical evidence in the case; all other evidence was circumstantial.

## Context

The examination occurred against a background of suspicion already forming around [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]. His purchase of arsenic, his wife's jealousy of [[mrs-beauly|Mrs. Beauly]], and the ambiguous circumstances of her death had already raised questions. The post-mortem converted those questions into charges.

## Key Details

The surgeons testified that the quantity of arsenic found was sufficient to cause death, and that no natural disease could account for the symptoms. The post-mortem report became the foundation of the prosecution's case — the one fact in a sea of inference.

## Consequences

The post-mortem finding is the evidentiary pivot of the entire case. Without it, [[sara-macallan|Sara]]'s death would have been recorded as illness. With it, every domestic detail — the arsenic purchases, the household tensions, the servants' testimony — was reinterpreted as evidence of murder.
