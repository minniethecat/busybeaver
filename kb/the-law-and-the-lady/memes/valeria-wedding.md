# Valeria's Wedding

The wedding of [[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]] to [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]] opens the novel on a note of quiet unease: "The church was in one of the dreary quarters of [[london|London]], situated between the City and the West End; the day was dull; the atmosphere was heavy and damp" (001-010). Conducted against the opposition of [[mrs-macallan|Eustace's mother]] and attended only by [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s uncle, aunt, and the faithful family clerk [[benjamin|Benjamin]], the wedding is the first in a series of events that reveal [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s concealed identity.

## Summary

[[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]] marries [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]] in a private ceremony officiated by her uncle, [[uncle-starkweather|Dr. Starkweather]]. The wedding is notable for what it lacks: no cheering, no sunshine, no grand breakfast, no society attendance. Almost immediately afterward, the first signs of trouble appear — a letter from [[major-fitz-david|Major Fitz-David]] that reveals "[[eustace-macallan|Woodville]]" is not [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s real name, and a honeymoon encounter with [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] ([[mrs-macallan|Eustace's mother]]) who refuses to acknowledge her new daughter-in-law.

## Context

[[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] and [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s [[marriage-plot|courtship]] has been opposed by his mother from the start: "his mother had mercilessly opposed herself to our marriage" (003-016). The reasons for this opposition — which [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] initially attributes to snobbery — become clear only later, when she discovers the scandal that "[[eustace-macallan|Woodville]]" is concealing.

## Key Details

### The Ceremony

[[uncle-starkweather|Dr. Starkweather]] officiates, concluding the service "in those well-known words" and looking at [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] "with a hearty expression of interest on his broad, red face" (001-003). [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s aunt taps her smartly on the shoulder — a gesture of practical affection in a ceremony otherwise devoid of warmth.

### The Ominous Sign

In the confusion of the moment, [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] commits a mistake that her aunt considers "ominous of evil to come": "I signed my married instead of my maiden name" (001-011). In retrospect, this error prefigures the identity crisis that will consume the novel — [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] cannot be "[[valeria-brinton|Mrs. Woodville]]" because [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]] does not exist.

### The Letter

Shortly after the wedding, a letter from [[major-fitz-david|Major Fitz-David]] to [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s uncle reveals that [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s real name is Macallan — and that he has been tried for murder. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] intercepts this letter, and its content becomes the novel's first revelation of the truth.

## Consequences

The wedding sets the entire plot in motion: [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s discovery of the letter, the confrontation with [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] at [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]], and the gradual revelation of [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s past. More profoundly, the wedding establishes the novel's central question: what does it mean to marry a man whose identity is legally contested? The marriage's validity — like [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s innocence — exists in the ambiguous space that the novel explores.

## Critical Analysis

Collins opens with a wedding rather than ending with one — a structural inversion of the Victorian marriage plot that signals the novel's genre as sensation fiction rather than domestic realism. The wedding is not a resolution but a problem: the legal, social, and emotional complications that marriage is supposed to resolve are here the source of the plot's tension.
