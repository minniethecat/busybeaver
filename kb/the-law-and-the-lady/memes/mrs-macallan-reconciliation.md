# Mrs. Macallan's Reconciliation

The reconciliation between [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] and [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] closes the novel's emotional arc. The woman who refused to acknowledge [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] on the beach at Broadstairs — who could not recognize "[[valeria-brinton|Mrs. Woodville]]" because she knew [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]] did not exist — finally accepts her daughter-in-law (003-006) (003-036). The reconciliation is not merely personal but symbolic: it represents the healing of the family rupture that the trial created.

## Summary

After [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s vindication, [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] — who had opposed the marriage from the beginning, who had refused to recognize [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] at [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]], who had represented the family's resistance to [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s new life — finally accepts [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] as her son's wife (048-003). The reconciliation restores the family unit that the trial had shattered.

## Context

[[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]]'s opposition to the marriage had always been rooted in knowledge: she knew that "[[eustace-macallan|Woodville]]" was a fiction and that the truth would eventually emerge (005-028) (006-015). Her behavior at [[ramsgate|Ramsgate]] — cruel as it appeared — was the reaction of a woman who knew her son's secret and could not participate in the deception.

## Consequences

The reconciliation provides the domestic resolution that the legal vindication alone could not supply. The law can declare a man innocent, but only family acceptance can restore the social fabric that the accusation tore apart (050-038).
