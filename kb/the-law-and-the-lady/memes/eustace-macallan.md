# Eustace Macallan

Eustace Macallan is the central figure around whom the plot of _The Law and the Lady_ revolves, though he is more an object of investigation than an active protagonist. Tried for poisoning his first wife and acquitted under the [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]] of Not Proven, he lives under the alias "Woodville" — a concealment that precipitates the novel's action when his second wife, [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]], discovers the truth on the day after their wedding.

## Role in the Novel

Eustace functions as the novel's absent center. He appears primarily through others' accounts — trial testimony, [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s narration, his mother's recollections — rather than through his own actions. His passivity is the problem the novel must solve: he is "resigned to the [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]]" (023-046), and it falls to [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] to reject this resignation.

## Character Description

Eustace is introduced through [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s admiring eyes on their wedding day. He is described as having a lameness from "an injury received in past years" that affects his walk (001-029). His demeanor shifts dramatically when his secret is exposed — from tender husband to tormented man. His true name is revealed as "Eustace Macallan" (010-083), and with it the truth of his past trial.

## Key Actions & Plot Involvement

### The Trial and Its Aftermath

Eustace was tried at [[edinburgh|Edinburgh]] "for poisoning my wife" (011-043). The jury returned a verdict of Not Proven — he was neither convicted nor exonerated. The trial is described in the indictment: "Eustace Macallan was indicted and accused, at the instance of His Majesty's Advocate, of the murder of his wife by poison, at his residence called [[gleninch|Gleninch]]" (015-018).

### The Concealment

To escape the social consequences of the verdict, Eustace adopts the alias "Woodville" and conceals his past from [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]. When she discovers the truth, he appeals to "my honor as a man" (007-055) in a desperate attempt to stop her investigation — revealing how his honor is entangled with silence rather than vindication.

### Passive Acceptance

Eustace's defining trait is his resignation: "My husband is resigned to the [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]]" (028-034). This passivity is the foil to [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s activism. He represents the position that the novel ultimately rejects — that legal ambiguity must be accepted rather than fought.
