# Motive and Intent

The prosecution's case against [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]] founders on one question: motive. As the defense repeatedly points out, no convincing reason has been established for why [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] would want to kill his wife. The novel builds its suspense on this evidentiary absence — and the eventual revelation of the true motive (which belonged to someone else) is the climax of [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation.

## Key Passages

> "I say there is some motive at the bottom of his visit to Ramsgate." (007-006)

> "I understood him, he had several motives for writing to the major." (002-036)

## The Missing Motive

The trial transcript records the prosecution's inability to establish motive. Why would [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]], a wealthy gentleman, kill his wife? There was no financial gain; there was no other woman (the jealous mistress theory collapsed on examination); there was no history of violence. The defense exploited this gap: the absence of motive was itself evidence of innocence.

Yet the novel complicates this reasoning. Motive may be absent from the legal record without being absent from reality. The true motives — jealousy, resentment, fear of exposure — belong to characters whose involvement is not suspected until [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation uncovers it. The novel suggests that legal process, by requiring motives to be proven rather than discovered, systematically excludes the truth.

## Intent and the Law

Victorian criminal law distinguished between act (_actus reus_) and intent (_mens rea_). The prosecution could establish that arsenic killed [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] (the act) but could not establish that [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] intended to kill her (the intent). The Not Proven verdict is, in part, a finding that the act was proven but the intent was not — precisely the split that the [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]] was designed to address.

The novel explores the implications of requiring intent to be proven. What if — as the eventual resolution suggests — the death was neither murder nor suicide but something more complex? The legal binary of guilty/innocent cannot accommodate the truth that the novel uncovers.

## Significance

Collins uses the motive question to dramatize the limits of criminal law. The law treats motive as an element to be proved or disproved; the novel treats motive as a mystery to be understood. [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation is not aimed at proving legal guilt (which would merely convict someone else) but at understanding what actually happened — a goal the adversarial trial system is not designed to achieve.

This distinction between legal truth and narrative truth is the novel's deepest contribution to Victorian legal discourse. The trial produces a verdict; [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] produces a story. The novel suggests that only the story can satisfy the human need for understanding that the verdict leaves unanswered.
