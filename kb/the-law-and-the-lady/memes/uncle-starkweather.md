# Dr. Starkweather

Dr. Starkweather is [[valeria-brinton|Valeria Brinton]]'s uncle — a clergyman who raised her after her father's death and who performs her wedding ceremony in the novel's opening chapter. His function is foundational: he represents the stable, respectable English world that [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] leaves behind. After Chapter 1, he never appears again, existing only in [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s memories and letters.

## Role in the Novel

Starkweather's role is both narrative and symbolic. He closes his prayer book "with a hearty expression of interest on his broad, red face" (001-003) — the last image of conventional English domesticity before [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] enters the destabilized world of [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]]. His absence from the rest of the novel underscores [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s isolation: she cannot turn to him for help because the scandal is precisely what his respectable world would reject.

## Character Description

Collins draws Starkweather in bold, simple strokes: a "broad, red face," a "hearty voice," a man of "loudest and cheeriest" manner (001-018). He is the Dickensian good uncle — warm, bluff, and entirely incapable of comprehending the legal and psychological complexities his niece will face.

## Key Actions & Plot Involvement

### The Wedding

Starkweather officiates [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s wedding to [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] [[eustace-macallan|Woodville]] — the ceremony whose legitimacy will later be questioned. His presence as officiant represents the official, church-sanctioned version of events that the novel will systematically undermine.

### The Absence

[[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] deliberately withholds her troubles from Starkweather: "Dearly as I loved my uncle Starkweather, I shrank from communicating with him" (006-030). This silence is the novel's acknowledgment that the respectable uncle's world has no tools for dealing with [[scotch-verdict|Scotch Verdict]]s and arsenic trials.
