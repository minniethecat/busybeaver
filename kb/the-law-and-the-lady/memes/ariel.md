# Ariel

Ariel is [[miserrimus-dexter|Miserrimus Dexter]]'s cousin and devoted servant — a figure whose very name is an irony. Named after Shakespeare's airy spirit, she is instead described as a creature of "coarse" body and "dormant intelligence" (025-021), a "Caliban" rather than an "Ariel" (025-017). Her relationship with [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] is one of the novel's strangest and most psychologically charged bonds: part servitude, part worship, part something approaching love.

## Role in the Novel

Ariel is introduced when [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] and [[mrs-macallan|Mrs. Macallan]] visit [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s house. She functions as [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s caretaker, gatekeeper, and the person on whom he exercises his need to dominate: "He doesn't hurt me," she says proudly (040-017). Later, when [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] is revealed to be withholding critical evidence about [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]]'s death, Ariel becomes both an obstacle and an unwitting ally in [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation. It is Ariel's insistent demand to "tell her a story" (040-060) that ultimately drives [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] to the narrative performance that reveals the truth.

## Character Description

Ariel is described with unusual harshness for a Collins character: "the girl's round, fleshy, inexpressive face, her rayless and colorless eyes, her coarse nose and heavy chin. A creature half alive; an imperfectly developed animal" (025-021). She wears a man's jacket and hat, speaks with a "coarse, masculine voice" (025-017), and moves in "brute silence, with a lumpish look and a clumsy gait" (025-023). Yet Collins complicates this portrait: her work is "perfectly well done nevertheless" (025-023), and her intelligence, though "dormant," can be awakened.

## Key Actions & Plot Involvement

### The "Delicate Ariel"

[[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] introduces Ariel with theatrical irony: "Wait a little, and you will have another surprise — you will see the 'delicate Ariel'" (025-014). She immediately serves him — brushing, combing, and perfuming his hair and beard — in a ritual of devotion that establishes the peculiar dynamics of their relationship.

### The Warning

When [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] first visits [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] alone, Ariel drives her in the pony-chaise. Midway through the journey, Ariel "astounded me by suddenly bursting into speech" (027-003) — one of the few times she speaks unprompted — warning [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] to stay away from [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]. This moment of unsolicited concern shows that beneath her stolid exterior, Ariel recognizes the danger her master represents.

### Dexter's Cruelty

During [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s crucial visit with [[benjamin|Benjamin]], Ariel is subjected to a degrading "experiment": strings tied to her wrists, cakes placed before her, [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] teasingly withholding food. When [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] protests, Ariel defends her tormentor: "Ariel has no nerves. He doesn't hurt me" (040-017). This scene encapsulates the tragic nature of their bond — devotion indistinguishable from Stockholm syndrome.

### The Story

When [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] launches into his improvised narrative — his veiled account of the events at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] — it is Ariel who demands he continue: "Go on, Master! I like it! I like it! Go on with the story" (040-158). Her insistence drives the narrative forward when [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] might have stopped, unwittingly aiding [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s investigation.

### The Collapse

When [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s mind finally breaks under the strain of concealment and revelation, Ariel's response is sudden and violent. She "brandished an Indian club, torn from a 'trophy' of Oriental weapons," and her "dull eyes glared like the eyes of a wild animal" (040-173). She transforms from passive servant to fierce protector in an instant — the only person who would fight for [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] in his moment of collapse.

### The Asylum

After [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s breakdown, Ariel refuses to leave his side. It was "found impossible to part the faithful Ariel from her master without using the bodily restraints adopted in cases of raging madness" (041-006). [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] arranges for Ariel to accompany [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] to the asylum — a small mercy that acknowledges what the narrative has suggested all along: that these two broken people belong together.

## Critical Analysis

Ariel is one of Collins' most disturbing creations — a character who embodies the Victorian fascination with the "grotesque" while subverting it. Her name's irony (Shakespeare's airy spirit trapped in a "half alive" body) works on multiple levels: she is indeed [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]]'s servant-spirit, executing his commands with mechanical precision. But the Caliban reference is equally apt — she is the "savage and deformed slave" who ultimately recognizes and resists tyranny, however briefly.

Her relationship with [[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] has been read by modern critics as a study in coercive control. He "plays upon" her intelligence "like a musical instrument" (025-049), and her proud insistence that "He doesn't hurt me" is one of the novel's most chilling lines — the voice of the abused defending the abuser. Yet Collins gives her moments of autonomy: the warning to [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]], the demand for stories, and finally, the eruption of protective violence. Ariel is not merely a victim; she is the novel's most vivid illustration of how devotion can be indistinguishable from enslavement.
