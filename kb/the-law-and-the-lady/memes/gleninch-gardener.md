# Gleninch Gardener

The gardener at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] is one of the trial's most consequential minor witnesses. His need for arsenic — to kill insects in the conservatories — provided the ostensible reason for [[eustace-macallan|Eustace Macallan]]'s purchase of the poison. Whether that reason was genuine or a cover for murder became one of the trial's central questions.

## Role in the Novel

The gardener and the cook together "wound the chain of hostile evidence around the prisoner more mercilessly still" (017-031). The gardener's testimony is straightforward: he is asked whether he requested arsenic for the conservatories, and his answer — whatever it is — will either corroborate or undermine [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]'s defense. His role is that of the ordinary man caught in extraordinary circumstances, whose mundane duties become fatal evidence.

## Character Description

The gardener is barely characterized beyond his profession. He appears only in the trial transcript, speaking "on his oath" (017-032) — a man whose job description (killing insects) has suddenly acquired lethal implications. His very ordinariness is the point: the trial transforms everyday domestic arrangements into evidence of murder.

## Key Actions & Plot Involvement

### The Arsenic Purchase

[[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] told the chemist that the arsenic "was wanted by his gardener, to be used, in solution, for the killing of insects in the conservatories" (017-027). The gardener's testimony either confirms or denies this — and the narrative leaves the ambiguity unresolved.

### The Testimony

Under examination, the gardener speaks "as positively as" the cook (017-034) — both servants corroborating the prosecution's timeline. But the defense's counter-narrative suggests that [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] herself managed the household arsenic: "My wife was the person who gave orders to the gardener and cook — not I" (017-058).
