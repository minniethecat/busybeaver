# Valeria's Arsenic Discovery

[[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s discovery that arsenic was commonly used as a cosmetic by Victorian women is the intellectual breakthrough that transforms the case against [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]]. What the trial interpreted as evidence of murder — the arsenic in [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]]'s possession — [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] reinterprets as evidence of a common (if dangerous) beauty practice.

## Summary

Through her own research — reading, [[discovery-process|inquiry]], and piecing together fragments of testimony — [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]] discovers that women of the period routinely used arsenic preparations to improve their complexions. This insight provides the innocent explanation for the arsenic at [[gleninch|Gleninch]] that the trial, with its institutional narrowness, could not conceive.

## Context

[[miserrimus-dexter|Dexter]] had hinted at this possibility in his allegorical narrative, but [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s independent research confirms it. Her discovery represents the triumph of amateur [[discovery-process|inquiry]] over professional legal procedure — the wife-detective who sees what the [[lord-advocate|Lord Advocate]] could not.

## Key Details

The cosmetic use of arsenic was a documented Victorian practice, and [[sara-macallan|Sara Macallan]] — described as "a very plain woman" (016-019) — would have had motive to use it. The arsenic that the prosecution presented as murder weapon becomes, in [[valeria-brinton|Valeria]]'s reinterpretation, a beauty product that [[sara-macallan|Sara]] administered to herself.

## Consequences

The discovery provides the alternative narrative that the defense at trial lacked — a plausible, non-criminal explanation for the arsenic that does not require [[eustace-macallan|Eustace]] to be a murderer. It is the evidence that transforms "Not Proven" into actual proof of innocence.
