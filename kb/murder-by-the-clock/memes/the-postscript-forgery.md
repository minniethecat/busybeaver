## Definition

The postscript forgery is a doctored letter created by [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] in an attempt to break up her husband's affair with [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]]. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] took an undated letter that [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]] — [[marge-myles|Marge]]'s first husband — had sent her before his marriage, and added a fabricated postscript implying that Harry feared [[marge-myles|Marge]] was planning to murder him. The forged letter was given to [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] and later became the object of the search through his papers on the night of his death.

## Creation

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] confessed to the forgery during her interview with [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]. She acknowledged the act with blunt clarity:

> "It wasn't especially nice, but I wasn't dealing with a nice woman. I employed forgery." (028-031)

She explained the mechanics of the deception. [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]] never dated his letters, which made the forgery untraceable. The original letter was harmless enough, but it contained a reference to the camp Harry owned by a lake in Maine — the same lake where he later drowned. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] exploited this detail:

> "I added a postscript to a letter Harry Myles had sent me before he married Marge. Harry never dated his letters. This one was harmless enough, but there was a reference in it to the camp he owned by that lake up in Maine. The postscript that I added changed the whole character of the letter. It made it apparent that Harry very definitely feared Marge was planning to murder him. I gave that letter to Herbert about a month ago, when it seemed that his interest in Marge was becoming dangerously serious." (028-034)

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] immediately recognized the dangerous ambiguity of the document, pressing [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] on whether Herbert had questioned her failure to produce it earlier:

> "But didn't he also ask you why you hadn't said something about the letter at the time of Harry Myles's death?" (028-039)

## As Blackmail Instrument

The forged letter did not simply end Endicott's interest in [[marge-myles|Marge]] — it became a tool of leverage. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] reconstructed the chain of blackmail in his final analysis. [[marge-myles|Marge]] had been paying hush money to her stepmother, [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]], since [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]'s death. Endicott, armed with the forged postscript, positioned himself in the blackmail chain:

> "People are blackmailed into giving up more things than money, Mrs. Endicott. I'm not suggesting that your husband was after money, but I do suggest that to further some abortive purpose Mr. Endicott held the postscript forgery that you made over Marge Myles's head as a threat. I have just found that letter in his trunk, and it is now in my pocket." (029-010)

The letter, hidden in Endicott's trunk, was the object that [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]] had been searching for when Endicott surprised her in his bedroom. It was the "fatal papers" [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] had suspected from the first moments of the investigation.

## In Valcour's Possession

After discovering the letter, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] folded it and put it in his pocket alongside another document — a postscript forged by [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]:

> "He folded the letter and put it in his pocket to keep company with the postscript forged by Mrs. Endicott. It would serve ably in establishing a motive and help the prosecuting attorney to clinch the case." (030-016)

The two forged documents together established the motive that [[chief-andrews|Chief Andrews]] had said was essential for prosecution. Without them, the case would have remained a medical mystery — a man dead of heart failure with no apparent cause.

## Deeper Significance

In his final reflections, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] recognized that the forgery represented more than a tactical move to end an affair. It was an act of comprehensive destruction:

> "Her forgery of that postscript had had a deeper, a more deliberate intention than the mere breaking up of any affair between her husband and Marge Myles: it was to have been a breaking up of all of his affairs. Of him." (031-016)

The postscript forgery was thus revealed as [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s weapon — not a physical weapon like the [[fear-as-murder-method|stiletto]], but a document designed to destroy her husband's entire extramarital life by giving him a tool he could not resist using. In a case defined by the absence of conventional weapons, the forged postscript stands as the murder weapon made of paper and ink.
