## Overview

Roberts is [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott's]] personal maid, an Englishwoman who has served in the [[endicott-house|Endicott household]] for several years (008-065). Her long tenure places her in a uniquely intimate position: she has witnessed the Endicotts' private quarrels and is the one person who can testify about the names spoken with affection or intimacy in the household. Her composed exterior conceals strong opinions and a fragile emotional state that eventually cracks under the pressure of the investigation's violent climax.

## Role in the Story

Roberts first appears when she opens the door to [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] shortly after Endicott's body is discovered. Her face is described as "butchered" and her eyes have "the quality of glass" (004-003) — she is visibly shattered by the event. As she passes [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] on her way out, he senses "an imperative awareness of an attempt at revelations — an attempt to impart to him some special knowledge" (004-008).

In her formal interview, Roberts proves a controlled and elusive witness. She tells [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] that Hollander's name is "the only one she has ever heard spoken by [[herbert-endicott|Mr. Endicott]] in terms that would imply intimacy" (011-045), directly identifying the dead man's closest confidant. When asked about the household atmosphere, she offers a cryptic judgment: "One is justified in having one's beliefs" (008-087).

Roberts's most significant moment occurs during the shooting of [[hollander|Hollander]]. She was left alone with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] while [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] made coffee (019-028). After the gunfire, she is found leaning over Endicott's bed, speaking words that [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] cannot later recall (019-049). Her subsequent collapse — described as a state of hysterical shock — and her defensive, angry reaction to [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s questioning (020-038) suggest she carries knowledge she cannot or will not articulate.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]** (employer): Roberts's loyalty to her mistress is deep but complex. She has witnessed the Endicott marriage at its worst — "quarrelling that hard" (008-074) — yet remains steadfastly in [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s service.
- **[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]** (employer's husband): Privy to his private conversations, Roberts is the source of the information that Hollander was the only friend Endicott spoke of intimately.
- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]** (interrogator): A tense relationship. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] recognises Roberts as a crucial witness but finds her guarded and, ultimately, breaking under pressure.
- **[[hollander|Thomas Hollander]]** (subject of testimony): Roberts identifies Hollander as Endicott's intimate friend based on overheard conversations.

## Analysis

Roberts embodies the classic detective-fiction archetype of the servant who knows everything but says little. Her Englishness — mentioned explicitly (008-067) — sets her apart from the American household staff and perhaps explains her "coolly detached" manner. Her cryptic declaration that "one is justified in having one's beliefs" (008-087) is a masterpiece of implication: it suggests strongly held convictions about the Endicott marriage without specifying what those convictions are.

The detail of her leaning over the bed after the shooting, speaking words no one can recall (019-049), is one of the novel's most tantalising loose threads. Whether she was praying, confessing, or communicating something to the unconscious Endicott remains unresolved. Her subsequent emotional disintegration — the blazing anger in her eyes when [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] presses her (020-038) — suggests a woman whose composure was a carefully maintained performance that violence finally shattered.
