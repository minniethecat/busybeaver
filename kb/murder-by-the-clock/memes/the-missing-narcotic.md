## Summary

During the night of the murder investigation, [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] administered a narcotic to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] to keep her sedated. However, the drug was never actually consumed — [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] pretended to drink it but poured it onto her bedspread instead. This deception, discovered by [[roberts|Roberts]] and later confirmed by [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]], allowed [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] to remain conscious and mobile while everyone believed she was unconscious, making her a prime suspect in the shooting of her husband.

## Roberts's Discovery

[[roberts|Roberts]], driven by her hatred of [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], went to examine her closely while the nurse was downstairs making coffee. She found the bedspread damp at the edge — something had been poured on it, and there was nothing it could have been except the narcotic [021-002]. [[roberts|Roberts]] realized that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] had recovered consciousness and deliberately disposed of the medication [021-004].

## The Distraction Trick

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s method was simple but effective. When [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] handed her the glass containing the narcotic, she claimed to see blood on the dresser. Everyone turned to look, giving her the opportunity to pour out the contents unseen. When they turned back, she appeared to have finished drinking. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] dismissed her claim about the blood as delirium [021-006].

## Valcour's Theory

[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] later incorporated the missing narcotic into his case against [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]. He theorized that she had arranged an elaborate alibi by pretending to have taken the sedative, when in fact she was fully conscious and able to shoot her husband from the balcony to prevent him from accusing Hollander. The damp spot on the spread, combined with the coldness of her skin and her heavy breathing from recent exertion, formed part of the circumstantial evidence against her [023-024].

## Significance

The missing narcotic was a critical break in the case. It transformed [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] from a grief-stricken, sedated widow into a calculating suspect who had the means and opportunity to commit murder. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] later used this evidence directly in his attic confrontation with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], presenting it as proof of her premeditated alibi [029-020].
