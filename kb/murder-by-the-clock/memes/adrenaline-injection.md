## Definition

The adrenaline injection is the emergency medical procedure attempted by [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]] in an effort to revive [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] after his heart had stopped. The procedure involved injecting adrenaline directly into the cardiac muscles — an experimental technique that had shown success in reviving newborns but was rarely attempted on adults. Its failure confirmed Endicott's death and redirected the investigation toward homicide.

## The Procedure

After the [[discovery-of-endicotts-body|discovery of Endicott's body]], [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] and the medical examiner conferred and decided to attempt cardiac revival via adrenaline injection. [[chief-andrews|Chief Andrews]] of the Homicide Bureau explained the rationale to [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]:

> "Worth's going to inject adrenaline into the cardiac muscles." (006-022)

The procedure was medically plausible for the period. Andrews noted that adrenaline "had been used off and on for years" and that "Worth says they try it quite often when a baby is born 'dead.' Sometimes it starts the heart pumping and the baby lives" (006-026). A recent case in Queens, where a man "had been pronounced dead for six hours and was revived" (006-024), lent credibility to the attempt.

[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] prepared the injection in [[herbert-endicotts-bedroom|Endicott's bedroom]] while [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] watched. The detective, though accustomed to violence, found the medical procedure unsettling:

> "He had witnessed any number of accidents and stabbings, but had never been present at an operation, and it worked on his nerves. Even if Endicott weren't dead, he certainly looked it. Suspended animation and catalepsy were all right as figures of speech, but the human illustration was rather ghastly." (008-005)

## Outcome

The adrenaline injection failed to restart Endicott's heart. This had decisive implications for the investigation. Before the procedure, the medical examiner and [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] had maintained that Endicott might not be truly dead — a possibility that kept the case in medical limbo. After the failure, combined with the autopsy results showing no poison or wound, the case pivoted fully toward homicide investigation.

The failure also exposed the tension between the police and medical perspectives. The homicide chief had been skeptical: "It sounds silly to me" (006-026). But the attempt was procedurally necessary — without exhausting medical possibilities, the police could not definitively declare murder.

## Investigative Significance

The adrenaline injection represents the boundary between medical uncertainty and criminal certainty in the case. Its failure is the procedural trigger that transforms Endicott's death from a possible natural event into a probable homicide. Once medicine had done all it could, the police were free to pursue the radical theory that Endicott had been frightened to death — a theory that depended on [[herbert-endicotts-heart-condition|his pre-existing heart condition]].

The procedure also highlights the period's medical technology. The 1920s saw the emergence of adrenaline as an emergency cardiac stimulant, and the novel's detailed depiction of the injection reflects contemporary medical practice. The Queens case cited by Andrews grounds the fictional procedure in real-world medical precedent.
