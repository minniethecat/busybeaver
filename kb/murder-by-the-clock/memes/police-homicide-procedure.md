## Definition

Police homicide procedure in *Murder by the Clock* refers to the systematic approach taken by [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]], the Homicide Bureau, and the [[medical-examiners-office|medical examiner's office]] when investigating [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]'s death. The novel provides a detailed depiction of early 20th-century police methodology, from the initial call to the securing of the crime scene and the coroner's examination.

## In the Novel

The investigation unfolds with notable procedural precision. After [[discovery-of-endicotts-body|discovering the body]], [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] immediately calls the [[new-york-police-department|Central Office]] and his precinct station (002-017), requesting the chief of the Homicide Bureau, the medical examiner, and additional men. He secures the crime scene by stationing officers at all entrances (003-004). The body must remain untouched until the medical examiner arrives (002-007). A photographer documents the body from multiple angles (003-044). The medical examiner and [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] work together to determine cause of death, while detectives gather physical evidence and take witness statements.

## Analysis

The procedural detail in *Murder by the Clock* reflects the early 20th-century professionalization of police work. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s methodical approach — examining soap, towels, window ledges, and balcony floors — embodies the emerging forensic mindset. The novel contrasts this scientific procedure with the more intuitive, psychological aspects of detection. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s classification of knocks (003-002) and his reflection on "the tragedy of a detective's life" (004-010) suggest that procedure alone cannot solve crimes; human insight remains essential. The tension between [[chief-andrews|Chief Andrews]]' institutional authority and [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s more philosophical approach illustrates the evolving nature of detective work.
