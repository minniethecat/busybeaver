## Definition

The poison suspicion was the first conventional murder theory entertained by the police in the Endicott case. Before the autopsy and the [[adrenaline-injection|adrenaline revival attempt]], both the investigating officers and the medical staff considered poison as the most likely cause of [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott's]] death — a hypothesis that the autopsy ultimately ruled out, forcing the investigation into the unconventional territory of [[fear-as-murder-method|death by fear]].

## Initial Suspicion

The possibility of poison was raised almost immediately by Officer [[cassidy|Cassidy]], who hoped for a straightforward case. Upon learning that there were no visible wounds on the body, he asked:

> "Will it be poison, then?" (003-023)

[[cassidy|Cassidy]] then recounted a magazine serial in which a murderer used chemically treated paper matches that "shot dabs of poison into the skin" when struck (003-027). His reference, though drawn from pulp fiction rather than forensic knowledge, reflected the popular association between poison and undetectable murder.

## The Autopsy's Negative Finding

[[chief-andrews|Chief Andrews]] of the Homicide Bureau made the absence of poison the pivot point of the investigation. He understood that without poison or a wound, the case became almost unprosecutable:

> "If the autopsy doesn't show poison or some wound it'll be a nuisance. If it's a straight heart attack, as Dr. Worth claims, we might just as well drop it." (003-050)

The autopsy confirmed no poison, no wound, and no physical cause of death. This negative finding was the investigative turning point. Without conventional evidence, the case required a radical theory — that Endicott had been literally frightened to death.

Andrews tied the poison question directly to the continuation of [[new-york-police-department|Central Office]] involvement:

> "If poisoning is proved and you haven't pinned it on anyone by then, I'll get on the job again." (004-012)

The logic was clear: poison = standard homicide investigation; no poison = medical anomaly, possibly not a crime at all.

## Gender and Poison

[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] reflected on the gendered dimension of poisoning as a murder method during the investigation:

> "When a woman starts out to kill she invariably chooses some weapon, or a poison. Every case has proved it again and again." (004-005)

This observation placed the poison hypothesis within the context of the novel's gender dynamics. If Endicott had been poisoned, the investigation would have naturally focused on the women in his life — [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] or [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]]. The absence of poison destabilized this conventional investigative logic.

## Investigative Significance

The poison suspicion's failure is what makes the Endicott case distinctive. In a typical murder mystery, poison would have been the weapon. By raising and then eliminating poison, the novel clears the ground for its central conceptual innovation: murder without any physical weapon at all. The absence of poison, paradoxically, makes the crime more interesting rather than less — transforming what appeared to be a weakness in the case into its defining characteristic.
