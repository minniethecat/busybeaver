## Definition

The evidence in the Endicott case comprises the physical clues collected by [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] and the [[new-york-police-department|Central Office]] from the [[endicott-house|Endicott house]] crime scene. The evidence was catalogued using an alphabet-based exhibit system, with the [[the-threatening-note|threatening note]] designated Exhibit A, a soiled towel and cake of soap as Exhibits B and C, and the hair brushes and comb completing the initial set. The evidence pointed toward an intruder who had washed, groomed, and wiped away fingerprints after the crime.

## The Exhibit System

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] organized the evidence using an alphabet-labelling system that he developed while waiting for the homicide chief and medical examiner to arrive. He found dark humor in the prosecuting attorney's well-known flair for alphabetized exhibits:

> "The prosecuting attorney would be pleased. He was a man whose flair for alphabeted exhibits amounted to a passion. Lieutenant Valcour hoped that he could find a crushed rose. The prosecuting attorney was at his best with crushed roses." (002-048)

The initial exhibits were:

| Exhibit | Item | Location | Significance |
|---------|------|----------|-------------|
| A | Threatening note | Endicott's desk | Printed in crude pencil on torn paper bag — the initial trigger for [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s call |
| B | Dirty cake of soap | Bathroom wall container | Used by someone with exceptionally dirty hands, never rinsed |
| C | Soiled crumpled towel | Bathroom hamper | Still faintly damp, corroborating recent use |
| D | Hair brushes (silver-backed) | Bathroom | Wiped clean of fingerprints — deliberate concealment |
| E | Comb (silver-rimmed) | Bathroom | Similarly wiped clean |
| F | (Reserved for future) | — | [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] noted "the alphabet had now been depleted down to F" |

## Key Physical Items

### The Soap and Towel

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s discovery of the dirty soap in the bathroom wall container was among his first significant observations. The cake of soap was "so incredibly dirty" that he could not imagine either Mr. or [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] leaving it in such condition (002-045). He concluded that the dirt had come from the murderer's hands and had dried on the soap, indicating the person had washed but not taken time to rinse.

The soiled towel at the top of the hamper was "very dirty and still faintly damp" (002-048), providing a time marker that placed the washing close to the moment of Endicott's death.

### The Dust on the Shelf

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s observation of dust streaks on the cupboard shelf connected the bathroom evidence to the crime scene. Someone had wiped both hands in the accumulated dust on the top shelf of the cupboard where Endicott's body was found:

> "There was nothing there but an accumulation of dust. He felt a distinct and highly satisfactory thrill when he noted that streaks showed where the dust had very recently been rubbed away, as if somebody had deliberately wiped both his hands in it. It linked with the dirty cake of soap." (003-054)

This led [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] to theorize that the intruder had used the dust as an improvised facial disguise.

### The Brushes and Comb

The silver-backed brushes and comb presented a paradox: they showed no fingerprints, yet Endicott had used them. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] deduced that the murderer had used them after washing, then deliberately wiped away all prints:

> "A pair of silver-backed brushes showed no finger marks upon their shining surfaces, nor were there any on the silver rim that backed a comb. One could infer that someone later than Mr. Endicott had used them." (002-048)

He preserved the brushes and comb in a clean towel, betting that evidence remained within the bristles and between the teeth that the murderer had overlooked. The [[new-york-police-department|Central Office]] later confirmed this: the person who used them "had been a blonde and was either a man or a woman with bobbed hair" (014-???).

## Two-Track Investigation

The investigation operated on two parallel tracks, as [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] described:

> "One department of specialists handled the technical and chemical investigation of material things and clues found on the scene of the crime — just as the brushes and comb were shortly to be examined by the proper men down at Central Office. A second department dealt with the human aspect — examining witnesses, looking up all friends or connections of the victim." (004-003)

This bifurcated approach meant the physical evidence moved through the [[new-york-police-department|Central Office]]'s technical chain while [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] himself pursued the human side — interviewing servants, family, and acquaintances.

## The Missing Hat

One piece of evidence was conspicuously absent: Endicott's hat. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] noted this anomaly:

> "He suddenly wondered where Endicott's hat was. It wasn't on Endicott's head, nor in the cupboard, nor in the bedroom, which struck him as strange. He was a strong believer in the paraphrase that where the coat is, there the hat lies, too." (002-055)

The missing hat, along with the top button torn from Endicott's overcoat and the emptied pockets, indicated that the intruder had taken something — possibly the "fatal papers" [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] suspected — and departed with the hat as camouflage or container.

## The Unresolved Search

Throughout the investigation, the question of what the intruder was searching for remained central:

> "What had been the object of the search through Endicott's pockets and among the papers in the left-hand upper drawer of his desk?" (013-???)

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] eventually concluded that the search had been for the forged letter — the postscript that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] had added to [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]'s letter, which Endicott had been using as leverage over [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]].
