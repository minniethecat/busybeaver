## Overview

Mrs. Siddons is the housekeeper of the Endicott household. She is one of the novel's most vividly drawn minor characters — described as a "pencil dressed in black" with an "incredibly thin body" and eyes like "banked fires beneath whose ash hot coals smouldered deeply" [007-048][007-066]. Mrs. Siddons is intelligent, bitter, and possessed of a sharp tongue that she does not hesitate to use. Her long tenure in the Endicott household has given her deep knowledge of the family's secrets.

## Role in the Story

Mrs. Siddons provides [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] with critical background on the Endicott marriage and [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]'s character. In her first interview with [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]], she speaks with startling frankness about Endicott's failings as a husband, her loyalty lying entirely with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] [007-067][007-069]. She later reappears as a person of interest in the investigation, particularly when [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] discovers she was present in [[roberts|Roberts]]'s room during a key timeframe [028-047]. By the novel's end, she has returned to her "native New England hills" [031-010].

## Key Appearances

**Chapter 7 — First Interview**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] interviews Mrs. Siddons by the fireplace. She speaks with "curiously artificial placidity," her clasped hands betraying tension [007-043]. She is remarkably candid about her low opinion of [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]], whom she describes in harsh terms [007-048][007-067]. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] is struck by her intensity.

**Chapter 26 — Second Interview**: Mrs. Siddons has not gone to bed. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] reminds her of her earlier remarks about Endicott and presses her on what she knows [026-002][026-004]. Her answers deepen the portrait of the household's dysfunction.

**Chapter 28 — The [[roberts|Roberts]] Connection**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] questions Mrs. Siddons about her presence in [[roberts|Roberts]]'s room during the critical period. She admits to being there, discussing "a particularly despicable affair" [028-047][028-051]. This timeline becomes important to [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s reconstruction of events.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]** — Her employer, to whom she is fiercely loyal [007-067].
- **[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]** — Her employer, whom she openly despises [007-067][007-069].
- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]** — The detective whose questions she answers with unsettling directness [007-048][026-004].
- **[[roberts|Roberts]]** — The maid with whom she spends time in a key chapter, providing an alibi [028-047].

## Analysis

Mrs. Siddons is a striking character who embodies the novel's interest in the hidden tensions within the Endicott household. Her physical description — a "pencil dressed in black," impossibly thin, with smouldering eyes — draws on gothic imagery to suggest moral intensity and suppressed passion [007-048][007-066]. Unlike the police officers who serve as functional background, Mrs. Siddons is given a distinctive voice and personality that makes her one of the more memorable minor figures. Her loyalty to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] and her contempt for [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] mirror the broader marital fracture at the story's center. Her return to New England at the novel's end suggests a character who has served her purpose and can now exit the stage [031-010].
