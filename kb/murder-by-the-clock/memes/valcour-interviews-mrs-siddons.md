## Overview

[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s interview with [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]], the Endicott housekeeper, takes place in Chapter 7 by the fireplace of the Endicott living room. The interview reveals [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]'s fierce loyalty to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] and her equally fierce contempt for [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]. She speaks with a "curiously artificial placidity" that barely conceals intense emotion [007-043].

## Chronology

- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] enters the room**: After completing other interviews, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] finds [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]] seated by the fireplace. She identifies herself: "Yes, Lieutenant. I am [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]" [007-041].
- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] observes her demeanor**: He places a chair beside hers and notes the tension in her clasped hands [007-043].
- **[[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]] speaks freely**: She makes a coldly dispassionate statement about Endicott that shocks [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] into staring at her "openly and with complete interest" [007-048]. Her eyes are described as "banked fires beneath whose ash hot coals smouldered deeply" [007-048].
- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] challenges her**: He suggests no man is as bad as she claims, and she responds with a slow gesture of negation [007-053][007-067].
- **The interview concludes**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] leaves with a much darker picture of the Endicott household than he had before [008-002].

## Participants

- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]** — The investigating detective, who finds himself genuinely unsettled by [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]'s intensity [007-048].
- **[[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]** — The housekeeper, whose "incredibly thin body" and smouldering presence make her one of the novel's most striking minor characters [007-066].

## Significance

This interview is a turning point in [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s understanding of the Endicott household. Until now, he has seen the case primarily through [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s worried perspective. [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]] provides an insider's view of the marriage's dysfunction, revealing [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] as a man capable of inspiring profound loathing in those who know him best. This interview plants the seeds for [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s later reconstruction of the motive.

## Evidence

- [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]'s description of Endicott's character [007-048][007-067]
- Her mention of the "fortune of her release" — suggesting [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] would be better off without her husband [007-066]
- [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s subsequent reflection on the "tangents and bypaths" the interview opened up [008-002]
