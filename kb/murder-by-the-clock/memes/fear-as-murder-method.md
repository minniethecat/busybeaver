## Definition

Fear as a murder method is the central homicide mechanism in the Endicott case: the deliberate inducement of a fatal [[herbert-endicotts-heart-condition|heart attack]] by exposing [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] to sudden, extreme terror. Because Endicott had a [[herbert-endicotts-heart-condition|pre-existing heart condition]], his death could be caused without a physical wound, poison, or conventional weapon — making it both a uniquely subtle form of murder and a challenging one to prosecute.

## Medical Basis

[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]], the Endicott family physician, established the medical foundation for this theory. He testified that Endicott had suffered a nervous breakdown two years earlier that had left his heart action impaired (003-041). This vulnerability made Endicott susceptible to death from any sudden shock.

[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] articulated the legal corollary to [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]]'s medical finding:

> "If some person who knew that Mr. Endicott was subject to heart attacks were deliberately to shock or scare him suddenly, or even give him a not especially forceful blow over the heart, and he were to die as a result of any one of those things, that would be murder. It would have to be proved pretty conclusively, of course, that it had been done deliberately." (003-048)

This statement establishes the three elements required for the murder charge: knowledge of the heart condition, deliberate action to induce shock, and a causal link between the shock and death.

## Prosecutorial Challenge

The homicide chief, Andrews, immediately recognized the difficulty of presenting this theory to a jury. After [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] pronounced heart failure as the cause, Andrews voiced his skepticism:

> "If it's a straight heart attack, as Dr. Worth claims, we might just as well drop it. Can you imagine getting up before a jury that's been shown a picture by the defense of a big husky like Endicott and saying, 'This man was scared to his death?' Suppose a woman was the defendant. They'd laugh the case out of court." (003-050)

The challenge was twofold: the victim was physically imposing (close to two hundred pounds, powerfully muscled), and a female defendant would undermine the jury's willingness to believe in death-by-fear. The prosecution would need to prove not just that Endicott died of shock, but that the shock was deliberately and knowingly inflicted — a high evidentiary bar.

## The Mechanism in the Endicott Case

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s reconstruction of the crime detailed the exact mechanism. The intruder — later identified as [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]] — was searching through Endicott's clothes in the cupboard when he returned unexpectedly. Trapped, she smeared dust on her face as a crude disguise. When Endicott opened the cupboard door, the sight was sufficiently horrifying to stop his heart:

> "You must have had the stiletto all ready in your hand and have looked pretty horrible altogether, because the shock of seeing you stopped his heart and he crumpled to the floor." (010-???)

The intruder's appearance — dust-smeared face, weapon in hand, unexpected presence in a closed cupboard — combined with Endicott's cardiac vulnerability to produce a fatal shock response. No stab wound was necessary; the fear itself was the weapon.

## Second Attack: The Shooting

The fear-as-murder method was employed a second time when Endicott, having been revived via [[adrenaline-injection|adrenaline injection]], was shot through his hospital window. Although the bullet was the proximate cause, the context remained one of terror: Endicott was a recovering patient who had already survived one murder attempt. The shooting demonstrated that the perpetrator would not stop until Endicott was dead, as [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] explained to [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]]:

> "Many people who reach the state of mental unbalance where they are impelled to commit murder don't stop with the crime. They've tasted blood. They are in a state of abnormal acuteness, and are driven by a new fear: that of discovery and capture." (011-???)

## Narrative Significance

The fear-as-murder method serves as the novel's central conceptual innovation on the detective genre. Rather than poison, gunshot, or blade, the murder weapon is psychological — a weapon that leaves no trace, confounds the autopsy, and forces the investigation into the territory of motive and human psychology rather than physical forensics. This aligns with the novel's broader [[the-clock-motif|preoccupation with time]]: death arrives not through a conventional weapon's impact but through the instant of terror itself, measured in heartbeats.
