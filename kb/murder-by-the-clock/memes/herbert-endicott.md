## Overview

Herbert Endicott is the murder victim around whom the entire investigation revolves. A wealthy married man having an affair with [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]], he is found dead in the cupboard of his own bedroom at the [[endicott-house|Endicott house]] under suspicious circumstances. The cause of death is initially unclear, but his known heart condition becomes central to understanding how he was killed.

## Role in the Story

Herbert Endicott is the absent centre of the narrative — the victim whose death must be explained. He leaves the house at 7 p.m. on the night of his death, ostensibly to visit his mistress [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]] (001-031). His wife [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] later finds a crude [[the-threatening-note|note]] reading "Thursday 9 p.m." on his desk, written on cheap paper with thick pencil (001-048). His body is [[discovery-of-endicotts-body|discovered]] crumpled in the cupboard of his bedroom by [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] (001-077). His pockets have been emptied, though his platinum wristwatch and black pearl shirt studs remain untouched (002-053). The top button of his overcoat is torn away (002-054), suggesting a struggle.

## Relationships

- **[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]** (wife): A deteriorating marriage. Herbert was openly unfaithful, kissing his wife goodbye while heading to his mistress (005-005). She admits to having wished him dead multiple times (004-011).
- **[[marge-myles|Marge Myles]]** (mistress): Herbert was having an affair with [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]], whom he met for lunch at the St. Regis (001-036). She lives at [[marge-myles-apartment|her apartment]] on Fifty-eighth Street.
- **[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]]** (physician): The family doctor who treated Herbert's heart condition. Worth notes he had been "afraid of something like this for quite a while" (003-033).
- **[[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]** (investigator): Never met Herbert alive but pieces together his last movements and relationships.

## Analysis

Herbert Endicott exemplifies the morally ambiguous murder victim. His infidelity, his "elaborate" lying (004-038), and his wife's description of him as "half animal" (005-039) paint a portrait of a man whose character flaws may have contributed to his death. The method of his killing — exploiting a known heart condition — suggests intimate knowledge, narrowing the suspect field to those who knew of his medical history. The contradiction between his dressed-for-outdoors appearance and his body being found in the cupboard raises the central mystery: was he killed before leaving, or did he return and then face his murderer?
