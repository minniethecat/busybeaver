## Overview

The attic of the [[endicott-house|Endicott House]] is a peaked-roof space at the top of the house, accessed by a flight of stairs (028-002). Over the course of [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s investigation, the attic emerges as a location of concealed evidence — including a trunk containing a gun (021-044, 022-023) — and a site of secret meetings involving [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]].

## Physical Description

The attic occupies the uppermost level of the house, beneath a peaked roof (004-050). It has a single window at the "garden end" (028-003), through which natural light enters. The space is described as "pea-sized" — confined and dim. Access is by a flight of stairs leading up from the main house (028-002). The floor is wooden and uneven; [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] uses an upended trunk as a makeshift step or platform (028-004).

## Mrs. Endicott's Trunk

A trunk belonging to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] is kept in the attic. It becomes a key piece of physical evidence in the investigation:

- **The gun**: [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] keeps a revolver in the bottom of her trunk (020-059, 020-060). She directs [[obrian|O'Brian]] to retrieve it from the trunk in her room — "There is a gun in her trunk. She will give it to you" (021-044). The gun is wrapped in a handkerchief (022-023).

- **The trunk as meeting place**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] presses [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] about whether she and another person "went through the trunk together" during a meeting in the attic (004-059). She denies going through the trunk, saying "I wasn't going through a trunk" (004-060).

- **The upended trunk**: During a later attic scene, the trunk is found upended — [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] has been standing on it (028-004).

## The Attic Meeting

A critical plot point concerns a meeting that took place in the attic. In Chapter 4, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] interrogates [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]:

> "Having met Mr. Endicott in the attic has nothing to do with the case?" (004-051)

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] stops sharply when the attic is mentioned. The reference to "[[herbert-endicott|Mr. Endicott]]" in the attic is ambiguous — [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] may be referring to Herbert, or to another person. The exact identity of who [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] met in the attic, and why, remains a point of tension throughout the investigation (004-050–061).

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] later returns to the subject, asking whether [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] told someone something "in this room here or up in the attic" (005-034), suggesting the attic was used for confidential conversations that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] preferred to keep hidden from the rest of the household.

## Valcour's Attic Investigation

In the final chapters, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] mounts a stealthy exploration of the attic:

- He ascends the stairs "softly now, quietly" (028-002), suggesting he expects to find someone or something there.
- He observes the window, the upended trunk, and the general disorder of the space.
- After descending, he reflects that "in the attic there was, for him, no longer anything of interest" (029-032) — suggesting his search has been completed and his conclusions drawn.

## Plot Significance

The attic serves three narrative functions:

1. **Concealment of evidence**: The gun hidden in [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s trunk is a tangible piece of evidence tying her to potential violence.
2. **Secret communication**: The attic meetings represent [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s private dealings, conducted away from the household's observation.
3. **Symbolic space**: As [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] herself notes, "I suppose an attic is the conventional place for" secrets (005-002) — a self-aware comment on the attic's role in the mystery genre.
