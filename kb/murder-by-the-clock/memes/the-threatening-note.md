## Definition

The threatening note is a key piece of physical evidence in the Endicott murder case. It consists of the words "Thursday 9 p.m." printed in thick pencil on a torn piece of brown paper. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] discovers it on her husband's desk after he leaves the [[endicott-house|house]] and interprets it as a death threat. [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] treats it as a significant clue, though he remains cautious about its exact meaning.

## In the Novel

The note serves as the catalyst for [[mrs-endicott-calls-police|Mrs. Endicott's call to the police]]. She finds it lying on [[herbert-endicott|Herbert]]'s flat-topped desk, apparently having fallen from between the pages of a book he slammed down (001-062). The note's crude, almost "intentionally crude" appearance (001-052) and the cheap brown paper — "more like the sort they use at grocers' or small stationers'" (001-050) — stand in contrast to the refined environment of the Endicott household. [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] notices that the same thick-leaded pencil used to write the note is sitting in a tray on the desk (002-056), prompting him to wonder whether Herbert himself wrote it.

## Analysis

The note presents a puzzle within the larger mystery. Its crude execution contrasts with the sophisticated environment, raising questions about authorship. Did someone else leave it as a genuine threat? Did Herbert write it himself as a memorandum? The matching pencil on the desk suggests the latter, but the unusual paper and crude printing point toward the former. [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s observation that "people who threaten are invariably unbalanced emotionally, if not actually deranged" (001-053) introduces the possibility of an outsider, while the note's presence in a locked, occupied house suggests otherwise. The ambiguity of the note — threat or memorandum — mirrors the larger ambiguity of the case.
