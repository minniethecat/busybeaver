## Overview

Marge Myles is [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott's]] mistress, an offstage presence whose shadow falls across the entire investigation. A former stage performer famous for her swimming (005-057), she lives in an apartment on the Drive (001-031). She never appears in person — by the time the investigation begins, she has already vanished. Her disappearance and eventual death form a parallel mystery that intertwines with the main murder case in unexpected ways.

## Role in the Story

Marge Myles exists in the narrative primarily through other characters' accounts. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] first names her as the woman Herbert left to visit at seven o'clock on the night of his death (001-031), describing her as "foreign-looking — the Latin type" with "eyes like black opals" (001-036). Herbert met her for lunch at the St. Regis (001-036).

Her absence becomes as significant as her presence. When [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] discovers her address in the telephone directory — alongside Hollander's — he visits her apartment and meets her mother, [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]] (012-011). [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]] reveals a darker history: Marge was married to a man named [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]], who is now dead, and her mother hints strongly that Marge may have killed him (012-069). "I always told her as how it would," [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]] says. "There's a limit to the number of suckers you can take a man for" (012-041).

The mystery of Marge's disappearance resolves tragically: she is found dead. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] delivers the news bluntly: "Marge is dead" (025-015). The revelation opens new possibilities — had Endicott killed Marge before his own death? — but the truth proves more complex. Marge's death is ultimately connected to a letter, a [[the-postscript-forgery|forged postscript]], and [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s own machinations (028-034).

## Relationships

- **[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]** (lover): The affair was ongoing at the time of Endicott's death. He met her for lunch at the St. Regis and visited her apartment. His feelings for her, according to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], were an infatuation — he "thinks he is in love" (001-031).
- **[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]** (rival): [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] tracked the affair, keeping Marge's address in her personal directory (028-027). She later admits to manipulating evidence to direct suspicion toward Marge, calling her "a straw" (028-085).
- **[[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]]** (mother): Marge's mother, who claims a Spanish heritage through her late husband Alvarez (012-011). She oscillates between maternal concern and cynical realism about her daughter's character.
- **[[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]** (first husband): Deceased under suspicious circumstances. Rumour held that Marge killed him (026-021).

## Analysis

Marge Myles is the novel's most significant absent character — a woman whose body is never seen yet whose life and death drive key plot developments. She functions as a narrative mirror: her disappearance parallels Endicott's "death," and the question of who killed her echoes the question of who killed him. The revelation that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] forged a postscript on [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]'s letter (028-034) — adding a reference to a camp "near a lake" — ties Marge's fate to the central mystery in a way that transforms her from mere mistress to pivotal plot device.

Her characterisation through others' accounts — [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s jealous description, [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]]'s weary cynicism, the police's professional interest — creates a composite portrait of a woman who used her beauty and talent to navigate a precarious social position. The detail that she was "famous for her swimming" (005-057) is poignant: a physical skill that once defined her now serves only as a footnote in a murder investigation.
