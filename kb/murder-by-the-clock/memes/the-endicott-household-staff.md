## Overview

The Endicott household on East Sixty-third Street employed a small but significant domestic staff whose members became key witnesses in [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s investigation. The staff included [[roberts|Roberts]] ([[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s personal maid), at least one cook, and a succession of other servants. During the crisis, the household was supplemented by private nurses — [[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] and [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] — brought in by [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]] to care for the unconscious [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] and the sedated [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]].

## Roberts: The Personal Maid

[[roberts|Roberts]] served as [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s personal maid for several years before the events of the novel [008-065]. She was present in the house on the night of Endicott's collapse and became one of [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s most important early witnesses.

Roberts provided two pieces of critical information. First, she identified [[hollander|Thomas Hollander]] as the only person [[herbert-endicott|Endicott]] spoke of in terms implying genuine friendship [011-045] — leading Valcour to summon Hollander to the house. Second, she advanced a theory that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] had not actually taken her prescribed narcotic, pointing out that there was a glass in the bathroom that might have been used to pour out the sedative rather than drink it [022-054].

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] herself defended Roberts's value despite their strained relationship: "What earthly difference does it make whether she hates or loves me? I'm hiring her services, not her emotions, and her services are excellent" [028-094]. This pragmatic view of domestic service — valuing competence over personal loyalty — reflected the Endicott household's formal, almost businesslike atmosphere.

## The Nurses

[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] arranged for private nursing care on the night of the crisis. [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] was assigned to stay with the sedated [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] throughout the night [008-028], while [[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] was charged with watching over the unconscious [[herbert-endicott|Endicott]] and summoning [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] if he showed signs of regaining consciousness [008-032].

[[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] found the assignment unsettling. Alone in the room with the unconscious Endicott while detectives moved through the house, she felt "not a little atwitter" — her professional experience had not prepared her for a night watch over a possible crime victim [010-042]. Her discomfort was compounded by the formal, impersonal manner of the police officers, which left her feeling isolated [010-052].

[[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]], by contrast, maintained a professional composure throughout. When summoned to view Endicott's body after the fatal shooting, she entered "without the formality of knocking" and regarded the sheet-covered corpse with a "professional" glance "not coloured by any sign of nervousness" [019-022]. Her clinical detachment highlighted the contrast between the medical and domestic staff's responses to the violence unfolding in the house.

## The Cook and Other Servants

The household also included a cook — part of what [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] referred to as her "successive chain of cooks" [028-094], suggesting high turnover in the kitchen. The cook on duty the night of the shooting was in the kitchen when the fatal shot was fired from the balcony. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] noted that the sound of the shot would have travelled clearly to the kitchen through the still night air, yet the cook claimed to have heard nothing [019-031] — a discrepancy that contributed to the growing uncertainty about witness reliability.

## The Patrolman

Although not strictly part of the household, a patrolman whose beat passed the Endicott house formed part of the domestic security arrangement. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] recalled that [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] always left a ten-dollar gold piece to be given to this officer at Christmas, along with a twenty-dollar check for the police "fund" [001-006]. Despite this arrangement, the patrolman had been seen only "twice or three times at most during the past year" [001-006], suggesting the limitations of routine police protection for wealthy households.

## Significance in the Investigation

The household staff served as both witnesses and potential suspects. Their intimate knowledge of the house's layout, routines, and keys made them persons of interest; their testimony provided crucial leads. The staff's varying degrees of loyalty — Roberts's apparent hostility toward her mistress, the nurses' professional neutrality — complicated [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s task of separating useful information from personal bias.
