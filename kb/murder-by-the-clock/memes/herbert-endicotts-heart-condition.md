## Definition

[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]'s heart condition is the medical vulnerability at the centre of the murder case. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]], the family physician, had treated Endicott for a cardiac condition following a nervous breakdown two years prior (003-041). The condition made Endicott susceptible to fatal cardiac arrest triggered by extreme fright — a method of murder that left no physical marks and nearly succeeded in passing as natural death.

## Medical History

[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] reveals to [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] that Endicott had suffered a nervous breakdown two years earlier that "left its action impaired" (003-041). The impairment was specifically cardiac — Endicott's heart was weakened to the point where a severe shock could stop it. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] had been "afraid of something like this for quite a while" (003-033), suggesting he recognized the vulnerability was life-threatening.

The heart condition was known to only a small circle: [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] as treating physician, [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] as wife, and possibly the household staff. This narrow circle of knowledge becomes crucial to the investigation, as the murder method required intimate awareness of Endicott's medical fragility.

## In the Investigation

When Endicott's body is discovered crumpled in the cupboard at the [[endicott-house|Endicott house]], the initial assumption is natural death by heart failure. The absence of visible wounds supports this theory.

> "If it's a straight heart attack, as Dr. Worth claims, we might just as well drop it. Can you imagine getting up before a jury that's been shown a picture by the defense of a big husky like Endicott and saying, 'This man was scared to his death?'" (003-050)

The medical examiner and [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] attempt an experimental revival procedure: injecting [[adrenaline-injection|adrenaline]] directly into the cardiac muscles (006-022). The procedure reflects the period's emerging emergency medicine, where adrenaline had been used "off and on for years" to restart stopped hearts, particularly in newborns (006-026).

When the adrenaline fails and the autopsy reveals no poison or wound, the case turns on a radical theory: Endicott was literally frightened to death. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] reconstructs the scene in his final summation:

> "The shock of seeing you stopped his heart and he crumpled to the floor." (018-041)

## Murder Method

The exploitation of Endicott's heart condition represents an unusual and psychologically sophisticated method of murder. Rather than using a weapon or poison, the killer weaponized Endicott's own medical vulnerability. The method required:

| Element | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| Knowledge of the condition | Narrowed suspect pool to intimates |
| Creating extreme fright | Required staging a terrifying scene |
| Absence of physical evidence | Nearly succeeded as natural death |
| The cupboard setting | Amplified shock of sudden confrontation |

The stiletto found at the scene was likely brandished not to stab but to terrorize — its point pressed against Endicott's chest through the bedspread was sufficient to trigger the fatal cardiac arrest (016-078).

## Significance

The heart condition transforms what appears to be a natural death into a calculated murder. It also inverts the usual detective-fiction hierarchy of evidence: physical clues (the stiletto, the threatening note) are secondary to the medical knowledge that explains *how* the crime was possible. The condition makes Endicott a uniquely vulnerable victim — a man whose body could be turned against him by anyone who knew his secret.
