## Overview

[[hollander|Thomas Hollander]]'s attempt to murder [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] with a [[the-damascus-knife|Damascus steel knife]] presented a paradox: he was Endicott's closest friend, yet he tried to kill him. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s investigation revealed that Hollander's motive was not hatred of Endicott but devotion to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] — a loyalty so intense that it drove him to murder.

## Valcour's Assessment

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] analyzed Hollander's motive with characteristic clarity after the attack: "One had to believe that Hollander had certainly intended to stab Endicott with that knife; the point was irrefutable. Furthermore, Hollander's motives remained clear enough and beautifully simple: he wanted to protect Mrs. Endicott" [022-005].

This assessment reframes the attack not as an act of malice but as a perverse act of protection — Hollander believed that by killing Endicott, he was somehow shielding [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] from danger. The "beautifully simple" quality Valcour identified was the purity of the motive, even as its expression was monstrous.

## The Nature of Hollander's Loyalty

Hollander's relationship with the Endicotts was intimate and complicated. He was described by [[roberts|Roberts]] as the only person [[herbert-endicott|Endicott]] spoke of in terms implying genuine friendship [011-045]. Yet his loyalty appears to have been directed primarily toward [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] rather than her husband. This triangulated dynamic — Hollander as friend to the husband but devoted to the wife — created the psychological conditions for his eventual violence.

When [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] telephoned Hollander to [[valcour-summons-hollander|summon him to the Endicott house]], Hollander's first response was revealing: "He's dead?" [011-046]. The question suggested that Hollander had been expecting Endicott's death — perhaps even hoping for it — before he knew any details of the evening's events.

## The Psychology of Protective Violence

Hollander's case illustrates a dark psychological pattern: the transformation of protective instinct into homicidal intent. He arrived at the Endicott house carrying the Damascus knife not as a defensive precaution but, as events proved, as a weapon he was prepared to use. His willingness to stab an unconscious man — a friend who lay helpless on a bed — demonstrated how completely his protective devotion had been distorted into something murderous.

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] anticipated this danger. He warned [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] that Hollander "is armed with a knife and that he is probably just waiting for a chance to use it" [016-026]. This foresight saved Endicott's life and revealed Valcour's understanding of the psychological forces at work.

## Connection to the Larger Case

Hollander's motive intersected with the case's other psychological threads. Like [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]], who may have murdered her husband [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]; like [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]], who blackmailed her stepdaughter; like [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] herself, whose actions were driven by a complex blend of fear and self-preservation — Hollander exemplified the novel's theme that the most dangerous motives are those born of intimate relationships.
