## Thesis

The murder of [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] was not a single impulsive act but the culmination of events set in motion hours before the fatal shot was fired. By reconstructing the timeline from [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s interviews and physical evidence, a coherent chain of causation emerges — one that the detective himself only fully grasps in the novel's final chapters.

## The Day Before (Afternoon–Early Evening)

[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] spent the afternoon of his death in a state of visible distress. According to [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], he knocked on her door to say goodbye before leaving the house — a gesture she later interpreted as ominous [028-044]. At approximately the same time, [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]], the housekeeper, claimed to have seen the husband of a maid Endicott had wronged loitering outside the house [028-053][028-057]. This sighting would later prove to be a misdirection.

Meanwhile, [[hollander|Thomas Hollander]] — who had tea with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] that afternoon — became convinced that she intended either suicide or homicide [028-069]. His subsequent actions were driven by this belief.

## 7:00–8:00 p.m.: The Collapse

[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] returned home and collapsed. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]] was summoned and diagnosed a severe cardiac episode [008-010]. At [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]]'s direction, two nurses were engaged: [[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] to watch over Endicott [008-032], and [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] to stay with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] [008-028]. The house began its transformation into a sealed medical ward — and, unbeknownst to its occupants, a crime scene.

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], upon finding a [[the-threatening-note|threatening note]] on her husband's desk, telephoned the police [028-074]. [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] arrived shortly thereafter, establishing the police cordon that would paradoxically fail to prevent the murder [003-004].

## 8:00 p.m.–Midnight: The Investigation Begins

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] conducted his initial interviews: [[roberts|Roberts]] the maid, [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]] the housekeeper, and [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] herself. The [[police-homicide-procedure|standard homicide procedure]] of sealing the house and stationing officers at all entrances was implemented [003-004].

Between interviews, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] visited [[marge-myles-apartment|Marge Myles's apartment]], where he encountered [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]] — a encounter that would later prove crucial to understanding the financial entanglements surrounding the case [012-011][012-015].

## Midnight–2:00 a.m.: The Knife Attack

[[hollander|Thomas Hollander]] arrived at the Endicott house and was admitted to the sickroom by [[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] [013-007]. His presence was tense and, as [[nurse-murrow|Nurse Murrow]] observed, unsettling [013-014]. At some point during this vigil, Hollander produced a knife and attempted to stab the unconscious Endicott.

[[cassidy|Cassidy]] and [[hansen|Hansen]], the officers stationed nearby, responded. [[cassidy|Cassidy]]'s shot shattered Hollander's right wrist; Hansen's shot caught him in the right shoulder [016-081]. Endicott survived the attack, but the incident demonstrated that the police cordon had been breached from within.

## 3:00–3:15 a.m.: The Fatal Shot

Despite surviving Hollander's knife, Endicott was killed hours later by a gunshot fired from the [[endicott-house-balcony|balcony]] outside his room [019-031]. The bullet's trajectory and the timing of the shot became critical evidence. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] questioned [[nurse-vickers|Nurse Vickers]] about what she heard and what she observed of [[roberts|Roberts]]'s behavior afterward [019-029][019-034].

The shooting of a man under police protection was a catastrophic failure. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] was forced to consider every occupant of the house as a suspect — including, eventually, the woman who had summoned him.

## 3:15–6:00 a.m.: The Unraveling

In the hours before dawn, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] pieced together the truth. The [[the-postscript-forgery|forged postscript]] on [[harry-myles|Harry Myles]]'s letter implicated [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]] [028-034]. The blackmail paid by [[marge-myles|Marge]] to [[madame-velasquez|Madame Velasquez]] established a financial motive [029-008]. And [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]'s account of the maid's husband loitering outside — which had initially seemed like a credible lead — dissolved under scrutiny [028-053].

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] searched [[endicott-house-attic|the attic]] and found papers in [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]'s trunk [028-108]. He descended with a new understanding of the case, and his confrontation with [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] in Chapter 28 marks the novel's pivot from investigation to resolution.

## 6:00 a.m.–Morning: The Denouement

[[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s reconstruction was devastating in its simplicity. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] — who had summoned the police under the pretense of fearing for her husband's safety — was herself the murderer [031-017]. The motive was not financial but emotional: a complex entanglement of love, resentment, and the desire to control the terms of her husband's death as she could not control his life.

The murderer did not face trial. Confronted by [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s men, she turned the gun on herself: "the muzzle of the gun was in her mouth" [030-058][030-059].

## Conclusion

The timeline reveals a pattern of misdirection that is the novel's structural genius. Each hour of the investigation produces a plausible suspect — the sailor, Hollander, [[marge-myles|Marge Myles]] — only for [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] to eliminate them in turn. The truth, when it emerges at dawn, is both simpler and more psychologically complex than any of the alternatives. [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] did not kill her husband in a moment of passion but in a calculated act that began with her telephone call to the police — a call designed not to prevent a murder but to control its investigation.
