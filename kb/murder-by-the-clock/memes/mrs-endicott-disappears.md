## Overview

In Chapter 27, [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]] steps across the corridor to check on [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] and finds her room empty. The chapter is titled "[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] Cannot Be Found," signaling this as a major plot development. Her disappearance dramatically intensifies the investigation and raises urgent questions about her whereabouts, her possible involvement in the crimes, and her safety.

## Discovery

- **The knock**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] "rapped on the door of [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s room. There was no response. He rapped again, and still there was no response" (027-002).
- **The empty room**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] "turned the knob and the door swung inward. The room was empty" (027-002)(027-003).
- **No signs of struggle**: The room shows no immediate evidence of violence or forced entry, deepening the mystery.
- **Immediate reaction**: [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] faces the alarming possibility that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] — the primary witness and key figure in the investigation — has either fled or been taken.

## Context

[[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s disappearance occurs at a critical juncture in the investigation. Earlier, [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]] had discovered that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] may not have taken the sedative prepared for her by [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]], suggesting she had been lucid and perhaps acting independently throughout the night (021-002)(021-003). Her disappearance transforms her from a potential victim into an active participant whose motives and actions must be urgently investigated.

## Significance

This event marks the turning point where [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] shifts from being a bereaved widow and protected witness to a person of active interest in the investigation. The disappearance raises the central question of the novel's final act: is [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] a victim, an accomplice, or the perpetrator?
