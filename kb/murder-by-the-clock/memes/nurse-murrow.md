## Overview

Nurse Murrow (referred to as "Miss Murrow") is the trained nurse assigned by [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]] to watch over [[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]] after his collapse. She is a competent, professional woman whose internal monologue reveals a life of "sloppy, disillusioning, grilling work" far removed from the romantic notions of nursing [010-042]. Nurse Murrow's vigil at Endicott's bedside places her at the center of the night's events, and she becomes an important witness to the drama that unfolds.

## Role in the Story

Nurse Murrow functions as the guardian of [[herbert-endicotts-bedroom|Endicott's sickroom]] and a witness to the events of the night. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] instructs her to adjust the lighting in the room and tells her to call him if Endicott shows signs of regaining consciousness [008-030][008-032]. She locks the door as instructed, and her solitary vigil becomes a lens through which the reader experiences the tense atmosphere of the house [010-042]. When [[hollander|Thomas Hollander]] arrives to sit with Endicott, she admits him, and her professional instincts clash with the growing sense of danger [013-007][013-014].

## Key Appearances

**Chapter 8 — Instructions from [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]]**: [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] gives Nurse Murrow her orders: adjust the lamps, turn out the ceiling lights, and call him if Endicott shows signs of waking [008-030][008-032].

**Chapter 10 — The Vigil Begins**: Nurse Murrow locks the sickroom door and reflects on her career — years of "pampering peevish patients" and "snappish doctors" without the romance promised by popular fiction [010-042][010-052].

**Chapter 13 — Hollander's Arrival**: Nurse Murrow admits [[hollander|Thomas Hollander]] to sit with Endicott. Her initial impression of him shifts as she observes him in the room [013-007][013-014]. She remains present but fades into her "professional" demeanor [013-042][013-050].

**Chapter 15 — Growing Unease**: Nurse Murrow begins to feel "fidgety" as the night wears on, training a "speculative eye" on her unconscious patient [015-002][015-004].

**Chapter 17 — The Attack**: When Hollander attempts to stab Endicott, the commotion brings Nurse Murrow into action. [[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Worth]] calls her to assist with restoratives after the shooting [017-005][018-016].

## Relationships

- **[[dr-sanford-worth|Dr. Sanford Worth]]** — Her supervising physician; she follows his instructions precisely [008-030][008-032].
- **[[herbert-endicott|Herbert Endicott]]** — Her unconscious patient, whose condition she monitors throughout the night [010-042].
- **[[hollander|Thomas Hollander]]** — The man who arrives to sit with Endicott; his presence makes her uneasy [013-007][013-014].

## Analysis

Nurse Murrow represents the novel's interest in ordinary people caught up in extraordinary circumstances. Her internal monologue in Chapter 10 — reflecting on the gap between the romantic ideal of nursing and its grim reality — is one of the few passages in the novel that steps away from the investigation to offer a character's interior perspective [010-042]. Her professional competence makes her a reliable witness, while her growing unease during the vigil contributes to the novel's mounting tension. She is a functional figure, but the narrator's brief investment in her inner life gives her dimension beyond her plot utility.
