## Definition

The Endicott fortune refers to the considerable wealth of Herbert and [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]], described by [[lieutenant-valcour|Lieutenant Valcour]]'s observation that the Endicotts "must be multimillionaires" (008-058). The fortune is a background condition of the novel's events — it shapes the household's structure, the social world the characters inhabit, and provides a lens through which the investigation considers motive.

## In the Novel

The Endicott wealth is established early through the physical description of the house and its contents. The house on a quiet New York street, the servants, the Sargent watercolour above the mantel (004-022), and the general atmosphere of established affluence all testify to the family's financial standing. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s explicit assessment — "multimillionaires" (008-058) — confirms what the setting implies.

The fortune complicates the investigation by introducing financial motive as a possibility. The prosecutorial case considers that [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]] stood to inherit the estate upon her husband's death (024-028). The household's structure — live-in servants, a family physician on call, a nurse retained for the night — reflects the scale of the Endicott resources and expands the circle of people with access to the household.

The fortune also shapes the social dynamics between characters. References to "the millionaire marriage" (012-065) suggest that wealth was a factor in the Endicotts' union, adding a layer of social calculation to the domestic tragedy. [[mrs-siddons|Mrs. Siddons]]'s outburst — "She will never know to the full the fortune of her release" (007-066) — uses "fortune" in its dual sense, suggesting both the magnitude of [[mrs-endicott|Mrs. Endicott]]'s liberation and the financial estate she would now control.

## Analysis

The Endicott fortune functions in the novel less as a direct motive than as an enabling condition. It creates the household structure (servants, multiple points of entry, a complex social world) within which the murder becomes possible. It also provides the prosecutorial case with a ready-made theory: the wealthy widow who stood to gain. [[lieutenant-valcour|Valcour]]'s investigation must distinguish between the obvious financial narrative and the more psychologically complex reality.

The novel treats wealth with a characteristic 1920s ambivalence. The Endicott affluence is presented as both genuine (the tasteful home, the art, the loyal staff) and precarious (the nervous breakdown, the extramarital affairs, the blackmail threat). Money solves nothing in this world — it merely enlarges the stage on which human weaknesses play out.
