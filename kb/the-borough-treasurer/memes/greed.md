# Greed

The financial motive that underlies the entire chain of events in *The Borough Treasurer* — the [[fraud|embezzlement]] in [[Wilchester]] that set the conspirators on their fatal path.

## Definition in Context

Greed in the novel is the original sin from which all subsequent crimes flow. [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]]'s [[fraud|embezzlement]] of building society funds in [[Wilchester]] was driven by simple financial avarice — the desire for more than they were entitled to. This greed set in motion the chain of events that would culminate in murder.

The novel portrays greed not as cartoonish villainy but as a common human weakness that, left unchecked, leads to catastrophic consequences. The conspirators are not professional criminals but ordinary men whose greed overcame their honesty.

## Key Passages

The [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] is described as a matter of "full [[power|control]]" (015-019) — the conspirators' positions as treasurer and secretary gave them unchecked access to funds. Their greed exploited this trust. The "profitable contracts" (016-004) that followed their move to [[High-Cross|High Cross]] represent greed institutionalized — the systematic extraction of personal gain from public office.

> Kitely's blackmail scheme is itself an expression of greed — his desire to profit from others' crimes rather than expose them. His "mind was already made up to blackmail" (016-004), revealing that greed is not unique to the conspirators.

[[Cotherstone]] reflects on "cases where a man's paid blackmail for years and years" (002-041), his fear revealing how greed, once indulged, creates a permanent vulnerability that others can exploit.

## Character Perspectives

**[[Mallalieu]]** embodies respectable greed — the desire for wealth and [[class|status]] channeled through [[appointment|municipal office]]. **[[Cotherstone]]** represents desperate greed — the willingness to do anything to preserve ill-gotten gains. **[[James-Kitely|Kitely]]** personifies parasitic greed — profiting from others' crimes rather than committing his own. **[[Brereton]]** stands in contrast — a professional whose [[honour|integrity]] is not for sale.

## Plot Significance

Greed is the novel's foundational motive. Without the [[Wilchester]] [[fraud|embezzlement]], there would be no secret to conceal, no blackmail to fear, and no murder to investigate. The novel suggests that greed is the most dangerous of vices — not because it is the most dramatic, but because it compounds, creating ever-larger [[duty|obligation]]s that eventually become impossible to satisfy.
