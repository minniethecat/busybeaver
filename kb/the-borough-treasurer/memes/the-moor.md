# The Moor

The expanse of wild moorland rising above [[Highmarket]] — a landscape that Fletcher uses as both geographical reality and moral metaphor: a place where secrets are buried, where men flee from justice, and where the truth is finally spoken aloud under an open sky.

## Geography

The moor lies immediately above [[Highmarket]], accessible by walking "five miles across the moors and hills" (015-041) from the town. It is a landscape of ridges and hollows, of "nooks and crannies" (016-038) known only to locals, of sudden dips and isolated homesteads. Between the railway line and the town there is "no definite track" (016-005), emphasising the untamed character of this country.

Key locations on the moor include [[hobwick-quarry|Hobwick Quarry]], where crucial evidence is discovered, and the isolated cottage where [[Harborough]] lives as a recluse. The moor connects to [[Wraye]] on its far side — "another road out yonder on the other side of the moors" (019-050).

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher's prose darkens when it reaches the moor. The landscape is described with a bleak beauty that mirrors the novel's moral terrain:

> A curlew flying across the lonely moor. (016-034)

This single image — the curlew's cry over empty land — recurs as a motif. The moor is "lonely" not merely in population but in character: it is a place stripped of the social pretence that defines [[Highmarket]] town life. Here, stripped of respectability, men show their true nature.

The moor's atmospheric power is never merely decorative. When twilight falls across it, the effect is both literal and symbolic:

> The sun had set behind the far ridges; twilight was creeping over the moor. (016-037)

This is the hour when [[Mallalieu]] and [[Stoner]] meet in their fatal confrontation — the gathering darkness of the landscape merging with the moral darkness of their actions.

## Key Events Here

1. **The murder of Stoner** (016-004–040): The moor is the scene of the novel's second violent death. [[Mallalieu]] and [[Stoner]] meet "on the lonely moor between High Gill and Highmarket" (016-004), and what begins as a confrontation ends with Stoner's body hidden in [[hobwick-quarry|Hobwick Quarry]]. The moor's isolation makes murder possible — and its eventual discovery inevitable, as "a moorland shepherd would not recognize it" (017-002) as human remains.

2. **The walking confession** (019-002–015): [[Brereton]] walks with [[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]] across the moor, and as they walk he tells her everything he has discovered. The open landscape becomes the setting for truth-telling: "They walked slowly across the moor as he told it; now dipping into a hollow, now breasting a ridge" (019-008). The physical journey across the moor mirrors the emotional journey toward revelation.

3. **Harborough's refuge** (019-031): "Up here on the moor!" — [[Harborough]]'s chosen isolation places him literally above the corrupt town below. His homestead, "a typical moorland homestead of the smaller sort" (019-018), represents a life of integrity deliberately removed from municipal corruption.

4. **The flight** (022-029–023-012): When [[Cotherstone]] flees, his instinct drives him toward the moor: "Once on those moors he would be safe" (022-029). The moor represents freedom to the guilty as well as sanctuary to the innocent — a moral ambiguity that deepens its symbolic resonance.

## Associated Characters

- **[[Harborough]]**: Lives in isolation on the moor's edge, a man whose past makes town life impossible
- **[[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]]**: Walks the moor with Brereton, learning the truth about her father
- **[[Brereton]]**: Uses the moor walks to piece together the case, away from the prying eyes of [[Highmarket]]
- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Comes to the moor to confront Stoner — and leaves a murderer
- **[[Cotherstone]]**: Flees toward the moor when his guilt is exposed, seeking the same anonymity that sheltered Harborough

## Narrative Atmosphere

The moor is the novel's counterpoint to [[Highmarket]]'s respectable streets. Where the town is defined by social performance — the Mayor's chain, the Borough Treasurer's office, the "atmosphere of mutual dislike and suspicion" (012-012) — the moor strips away pretence. In Fletcher's rendering, it is a place of elemental truth: cold, beautiful, and indifferent to human reputation.

This duality gives the moor its narrative power. It is both sanctuary and trap, freedom and exposure. Characters come to the moor to hide and to confess, to commit crimes and to solve them. It is the landscape of consequence — the place where the moral debts incurred in town must finally be paid.
