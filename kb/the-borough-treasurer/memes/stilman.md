# Major Stilman

A retired infantry officer [028-016] who employed [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] as his housekeeper at Kandahar Cottage, Woking. His death — under circumstances involving heavy doses of medication — becomes a crucial element in the backstory of the Highmarket case [009-066].

## Role in the Story

Major Stilman appears only in retrospect, his death forming part of the backstory that investigators [[Brereton]] and [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]] uncover as they probe the past of [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]. Stilman was a retired officer living at Kandahar Cottage, Woking [009-069] [009-070], where Miss Pett served as his housekeeper for "a many years" [009-070]. Brereton's investigation into Stilman's death reveals a pattern of suspicious medication use that raises questions about Miss Pett's role in his demise [028-022] [028-035].

## Key Relationships

- **[[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]**: Stilman's housekeeper of many years at Kandahar Cottage, Woking [009-070]. After Stilman's death, Miss Pett relocated to Highmarket, where she entered the service of [[tallington|Mr. Tallington]].

## Key Actions

- **Employed Miss Pett at Woking**: Gave Miss Pett a position as housekeeper at Kandahar Cottage, where she remained for many years [009-070]
- **Died under ambiguous circumstances**: Stilman's death involved "strong doses" of medication, and the question of whether Miss Pett "took advantage of Stilman's use of drugs to finish him off" becomes a line of inquiry [028-022] [028-035]
- **Left only his pension**: Stilman "had nothing but his pension" — there was no inheritance or financial motive in his death [028-018]

## Involvement in the Crime

Stilman is a peripheral figure whose death provides investigators with a lens into Miss Pett's history. The theory that Miss Pett may have hastened Stilman's death through his medication [028-035] parallels the broader theme of hidden crimes in the novel. However, Brereton's investigation ultimately finds "nothing against" Miss Pett [028-026], suggesting the suspicions about Stilman's death may be unfounded or unprovable.

## Significance

Major Stilman serves as a narrative device to deepen Miss Pett's character and to introduce the theme of ambiguous deaths that pervades the novel. His military background and retirement to a cottage named "Kandahar" evoke the British imperial context, situating the story within the world of retired colonial officers. The question of whether his death was natural or assisted mirrors the central mystery of Kitely's murder — both cases involving characters whose pasts carry secrets that the present investigation must excavate [028-013].
