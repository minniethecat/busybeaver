# the police-sergeant

The unnamed police-sergeant is a key figure in the initial response to James Kitely's murder. He is among the first officials on the scene, working alongside Dr. Rockcliffe and the local constables [PN=004-039].

## At the Murder Scene

When Brereton arrives at Kitely's cottage, the sergeant is already there, having bent over the body while the doctor examined it. He immediately offers a preliminary conclusion:

> "I've noted that, sir," said the police-sergeant... "There's one of his pockets turned inside out, and all his clothing's been torn open. Robbery, of course—that's what it's been—murder for the sake of robbery!" [PN=004-039]

A constable hands the sergeant Kitely's pocket-book, which had been found empty — further evidence supporting the robbery theory [PN=004-043].

## Examining the Body

Inside the cottage, the sergeant assists Dr. Rockcliffe in the closer examination of Kitely's body in the bedroom [PN=005-009]. When the doctor removes the length of grey cord used to strangle Kitely and lays it on the table, the sergeant is the one who first acknowledges its significance [PN=005-012], [PN=005-021].

The sergeant is perceptive enough to have his own theory about the crime:

> The police sergeant glanced at the doctor as if asking advice on the subject of putting his thoughts into words. [PN=005-021]

When the doctor encourages him to share, the sergeant's observations contribute to the investigation [PN=005-022].

## Questioning Witnesses

The sergeant conducts the initial questioning of Miss Pett, the housekeeper. Following the doctor's instruction to find out what Kitely had on him, he asks:

> "Can you tell aught about this, ma'am? You see your master's met his death at somebody's hands, and there's no doubt he's been robbed, too. Do you happen to know what he had on him?" [PN=005-028]

His questioning is methodical but respectful, focusing on establishing what was taken from the victim [PN=005-031], [PN=005-026].

## Significance

As an unnamed but recurring official presence, the police-sergeant represents the institutional response to crime in Highmarket. His initial assumption that robbery was the motive — while understandable given the evidence — creates a narrative tension, as the reader comes to suspect the crime may have deeper roots in the town's political corruption [PN=004-039], [PN=005-009].
