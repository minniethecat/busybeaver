# Cotherstone

Town councillor of Highmarket and former secretary of the Wilchester building society — a man whose desperate fear of exposure transforms him from corrupt official to murderer.

## Role in the Story

Cotherstone is the more psychologically vulnerable of the two conspirators. Where Mallalieu maintains a surface of composure, Cotherstone's guilt manifests in visible anxiety. He is "a slightly built, wiry man, nervous and quick of movement" — the physical opposite of the heavy, calm Mallalieu (001-005). He had served as secretary to Mallalieu's treasurer in the Wilchester building society, and the two "had full control" of its funds (015-019). Their embezzlement, years in the past, becomes a ticking mechanism when Kitely arrives in Highmarket.

Cotherstone's immediate recognition of Kitely's intent — Kitely's quiet observation that "business seems to be good with you, sir" (001-031) — reveals a man who has long anticipated this moment. His reflection on "cases where a man's paid blackmail for years and years, and been ruined by it in the end" (002-041) betrays his deepest fear: that blackmail is not a single transaction but a lifetime of extortion.

When Kitely is found murdered, the suspicion falls most heavily on Cotherstone. His behavior after the murder — nervous, evasive, protesting too much — confirms what Brereton's instinct had told him from the start.

## Character & Traits

Cotherstone is defined by his nerves. Where Mallalieu projects calm, Cotherstone betrays anxiety in every gesture. He is "a good deal of a contrast to his partner" (001-005): wiry where Mallalieu is heavy, quick where Mallalieu is deliberate, fearful where Mallalieu is calculating.

His moral psychology is the novel's most sustained portrait of guilt. Cotherstone has not merely committed a crime — he has been living with it for years. The Wilchester embezzlement is a weight he carries, and Kitely's arrival transforms that weight into a crushing pressure. His violence, when it comes, is not premeditated in any meaningful sense but the desperate act of a man who sees no other escape.

His relationship with his daughter Lettie adds a domestic dimension to his tragedy. He is not merely a criminal but a father whose exposure will destroy not only himself but his family.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Senior partner and co-conspirator. Mallalieu is the dominant figure in their partnership — "his junior by several years" (001-005), Cotherstone has always followed Mallalieu's lead. Their relationship fractures under pressure as each man's instinct for self-preservation takes over.

- **[[James-Kitely]]**: The blackmailer whose appearance triggers Cotherstone's descent into murder. Kitely had known both men in Wilchester, and his casual remark — "I haven't forgotten" (001-031) — is the threat that Cotherstone cannot bear.

- **[[Brereton]]**: The investigator who suspects Cotherstone from the very beginning. Brereton "became convinced" of Cotherstone's guilt almost immediately (007-003), and his methodical pursuit drives Cotherstone to increasingly desperate measures.

- **[[Lettie-Cotherstone]]**: Cotherstone's daughter, whose affection for Brereton creates an emotional complication that intensifies the tragedy of her father's exposure.

- **[[Bent]]**: Cotherstone's clerk, whose loyalty becomes a complicating factor in the investigation.

## Key Actions

1. **The Wilchester Embezzlement** (backstory, revealed throughout): As secretary of the Wilchester Building Society, Cotherstone participated in the fraud that would later make him vulnerable to blackmail. His role was subordinate to Mallalieu's but equally culpable (001-050, 015-019).

2. **Confronting Kitely's Threat** (Chapters 1–3): Cotherstone immediately grasps the danger Kitely represents. His suggestion to consider "provisional protection" for their timber (001-009) is laced with irony — it is not timber but their own past that needs protecting. His brooding on the endless nature of blackmail (002-041) reveals a man calculating a darker solution.

3. **The Murder of Kitely** (Chapter 4–5): Cotherstone strangles Kitely with his own necktie. The murder weapon, "insignificant in itself, a mean and paltry thing" (005-013), reflects the sordid nature of the crime — not a grand passion but a desperate, ugly act committed by a cornered man.

4. **The Unraveling** (Chapters 6–30): Cotherstone's attempts to deflect suspicion only deepen it. His protest that "I know no more of his murder than you do — not so much, I should think" (008-014) is too vehement to be believed. His flight from Highmarket — Cotherstone's Flight — is the final admission of guilt.

## Involvement in the Crime

Cotherstone is the novel's murderer. While Mallalieu is guilty of fraud and conspiracy, Cotherstone's hands are literally bloodied. The novel traces his arc from corrupt official to blackmail victim to killer — each step driven not by inherent evil but by the compounding pressure of past guilt and present fear.

The ambiguity Fletcher preserves is not about Cotherstone's guilt (which becomes increasingly certain) but about his moral culpability. He is a murderer, but he is also a man who has been living in a trap of his own making for years, and Kitely's blackmail is the spring that closes it.

> Cotherstone protests his innocence with too much vehemence: "I know no more of his murder than you do — not so much, I should think" (008-014). The very excess of his denial marks him as guilty.

## Significance

Cotherstone embodies the novel's psychological realism. He is not a villain in the melodramatic sense — he is an ordinary man whose ordinary greed led to extraordinary consequences. His deterioration from respected councillor to exposed murderer traces the arc of a man who cannot live with what he has done.

His character also serves as a foil to Mallalieu. Where Mallalieu compartmentalizes — treating the Wilchester affair as a past transaction, not a continuing crime — Cotherstone is consumed by it. The contrast between Mallalieu's cold calculation and Cotherstone's nervous disintegration is one of Fletcher's most effective character studies.
