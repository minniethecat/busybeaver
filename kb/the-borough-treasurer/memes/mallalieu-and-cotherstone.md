## Overview

Mallalieu & Cotherstone, Builders and Contractors, is the prosperous building firm that anchors the partners' position in [[Highmarket]] society. Founded after their release from prison and relocation from [[Wilchester]], the business is the mechanism through which John Mallows and Mark Chidforth transform themselves into [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] — respectable men of substance. The firm's success is both a testament to their industry and a monument to their original crime: the two thousand pounds "never recovered" [017-047] from the building society fraud.

## The Premises

### The Ancient Gateway

The firm's yard occupies a distinctive location on [[Highmarket]]'s main street:

> "Halfway along the north side of the main street of Highmarket an ancient stone gateway, imposing enough to suggest that it was originally the entrance to some castellated mansion or manor house, gave access to a square yard, flanked about by equally ancient buildings." [Ch I]

The ancient, imposing gateway — originally part of some grand estate — provides an ironic frame for a business built on stolen capital. The old buildings have been repurposed to store "piles of timber from Norway... stacks of slate from Wales... marble from Aberdeen... cement from Portland" — the materials of modern construction.

### The Brass Plate

A shining brass plate at the entrance bears the firm's name in deeply-engraved letters:

> "On a shining brass plate, set into the wall, just within the gateway, were deeply engraven the words: Mallalieu and Cotherstone, Builders and Contractors." [Ch I]

The brass plate is a declaration of legitimacy — a permanent, public assertion of identity. It is also, of course, a lie: "[[Mallalieu]]" and "[[Cotherstone]]" are names assumed after prison, the identities of Mallows and Chidforth carefully erased.

## The Partnership

### Division of Labour

The partnership reflects the complementary temperaments of the two men:

- **[[Mallalieu]]**: The practical partner — "more concerned with the actual work" of the business. He designs carts, oversees construction, tests new mechanisms. His pride is in tangible achievement: "We'd really ought to have a patent for that idea" [Ch I].
- **[[Cotherstone]]**: The administrative partner — "more concerned with the bookkeeping and letter-writing side of the business." He manages finances, handles correspondence, collects rents. His domain is paper, calculation, and [[secret|concealment]].

Their physical descriptions mirror their roles: [[Mallalieu]] is "big, fleshy... of a large, solemn, well-satisfied countenance"; [[Cotherstone]] is "slightly built, wiry man, nervous and quick of movement" [Ch I].

### The Physical Contrast

Fletcher's initial description of the two partners in their yard establishes their [[reputation|character]]s economically:

> "Mallalieu suggested solidity and almost bovine sleekness; in Cotherstone, activity of speech and gesture was marked well-nigh to an appearance of habitual anxiety." [Ch I]

[[Cotherstone]]'s "habitual [[fear|anxiety]]" — visible from the first chapter — is the psychological toll of [[secret|concealment]]. The firm that looks so solid is built on a foundation of fear.

## The Stolen Capital

### The Unrecovered Money

The [[Wilchester]] building society fraud involved two thousand pounds that was "never recovered" [017-047]. The inference is unavoidable: the partners used the stolen money as seed capital for their business. The thirty years of honest industry that followed were built on a dishonest foundation.

The firm's prosperity — the Norwegian timber, the Welsh slate, the Scottish marble — is the fruit of a poisoned tree. Every brick laid, every contract fulfilled, is shadowed by the original crime.

### The New Carts

In the novel's opening scene, [[Mallalieu]] proudly shows [[Cotherstone]] a batch of new carts designed with an innovative unloading mechanism:

> "All done in one action, d'ye see, Cotherstone? One pull at that pin releases the entire load." [Ch I]

The proud display of innovation and craftsmanship is a moment of genuine achievement — and a reminder that the partners are capable, industrious men who might have succeeded honestly. The tragedy is not that they were incompetent but that their competence was built on crime.

## The Firm's Collapse

When the [[Wilchester]] secret is exposed, the firm collapses with its founders. [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]], the clerk who independently discovered the partners' past, is killed by [[Mallalieu]] at [[hobwick-quarry|Hobwick Quarry]] — a death that originates in the tensions within the business. The partnership, forged in shared crime and sustained by thirty years of mutual dependence, dissolves in suspicion, murder, and flight.

The firm that looked so solid — "a great business" [Ch I] — proves as fragile as the identities that founded it.

## The Firm as Symbol

Mallalieu & Cotherstone represents the paradox at the heart of the novel: the partners have genuinely reformed — they have worked hard, built a legitimate business, contributed to their community — yet their reformation rests on an unreformed crime. The stolen money was never returned; the false identities were never confessed; the [[class|respectability]] was never earned, only performed.

The firm is Fletcher's case study in the limits of self-reinvention. A man may change his name, his town, his profession, his [[class|social standing]] — but he cannot change his past.
