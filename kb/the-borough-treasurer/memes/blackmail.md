# Blackmail

The hidden motive that drives the central crime of the novel — an attempt by [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]] to extract money from [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] by threatening to expose their past.

## Definition in Context

In *The Borough Treasurer*, blackmail is not merely a crime but the narrative's central mechanism of suspense. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] arrives in [[High-Cross|High Cross]] not to retire peacefully but to exploit his knowledge of a past financial scandal involving [[High-Cross|the borough]] treasurer and town councillor (030-063). His demand for money triggers the events that lead to his murder. [[Mallalieu]] recognizes the threat immediately: "he tried to blackmail me" (020-040).

The blackmail scheme is rooted in events that occurred years earlier in [[Wilchester]], where [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] served as treasurer and secretary respectively (001-050). [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s knowledge of their misdeeds gives him leverage, and his attempt to capitalize on it sets the entire plot in motion.

## Key Passages

> "Wants to blackmail us, of course," replied Cotherstone, recognizing Kitely's intent with chilling clarity (002-035).

The conspirators immediately understand what [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] is after. [[Cotherstone]] reflects on "cases where a man's paid blackmail for years and years, and been ruined by it in the end" (002-041), revealing his fear of a lifetime of extortion. The threat is not abstract — [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] names his price directly, prompting [[Mallalieu]]'s outburst: "I see what you're after — and it's blackmail!" (001-057).

> "His idea was blackmail — Mallalieu had no doubt about that," the narrative confirms, as the conspirators weigh their response (017-003).

The blackmailer's identity is gradually confirmed through investigation. [[Brereton]] and the authorities come to [[suspicion|suspect]] that [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] "had endeavoured to blackmail them" (018-015), connecting the [[greed|financial motive]] to the murder.

## Character Perspectives

The blackmail reveals the moral [[reputation|character]] of each participant. **[[Mallalieu]]** admits [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s demand plainly: "he tried to blackmail me. He was to come next day" (020-040). His admission, however, is strategic — designed to explain [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s presence without revealing his own violent response.

**[[Cotherstone]]** views blackmail as an existential threat, dwelling on its corrosive effect: a man could be "ruined by it in the end" (002-041). His desperation drives him to murder rather than submit. **[[James-Kitely|Kitely]]** himself is portrayed as calculating, his "mind already made up to blackmail" (016-004) when he first encounters the conspirators' vulnerability.

## Plot Significance

Blackmail is the concealed engine of the entire plot. It explains [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s otherwise inexplicable move to [[High-Cross|High Cross]], provides the motive for his murder, and connects the present crime to the conspirators' past in [[Wilchester]]. Without the blackmail thread, the novel would be a simple whodunit; with it, the story becomes an exploration of how past sins compound and how desperate men behave when cornered.
