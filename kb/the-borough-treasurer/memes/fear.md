# Fear

The dominant psychological force in *The Borough Treasurer* — the terror of exposure that drives [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] to increasingly desperate acts.

## Definition in Context

Fear in the novel is not abstract anxiety but a concrete, motivating force. [[Cotherstone]]'s every action after [[murder|Kitely's murder]] is driven by fear of discovery. He and [[Mallalieu]] "must move heaven and earth lest suspicion should fall on him" (007-003). This fear transforms respectable citizens into calculating criminals.

The novel traces fear's progression: from diffuse unease when [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] first arrives, to panic when his intentions become clear, to the desperate violence of murder, and finally to the paralyzing terror of inevitable exposure.

## Key Passages

[[Cotherstone]]'s fear is described as all-consuming. He "foresaw that suspicion might fall on [[Cotherstone]]" and prepared accordingly (021-015). His fear is not merely of legal punishment but of social destruction — the loss of everything he has built.

> The conspirators' fear of each other becomes as powerful as their fear of discovery. The "atmosphere of mutual dislike and suspicion between them" (012-012) reflects their knowledge that each would betray the other to save himself.

[[Mallalieu]]'s admission that [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] "tried to blackmail me" (020-040) reveals how fear of exposure drove him to consider desperate measures. The fear that [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] would reveal the [[past|Wilchester affair]] was sufficient to make murder thinkable.

## Character Perspectives

**[[Cotherstone]]** is fear personified — jumpy, paranoid, incapable of concealing his terror. **[[Mallalieu]]** manages his fear more effectively, maintaining a public face of composure while privately calculating his survival. **The community's** fear of a murderer at large feeds the demand for swift justice. **[[Brereton]]** alone seems immune to fear, his professional detachment insulating him from the emotional chaos around him.

## Plot Significance

Fear is the novel's emotional engine. It motivates the murder, shapes the investigation, and provides the psychological depth that elevates the story beyond a simple whodunit. The novel's suspense derives from watching fear work its corrosive effect on the guilty while the innocent struggle against the fear that suspicion casts upon them.
