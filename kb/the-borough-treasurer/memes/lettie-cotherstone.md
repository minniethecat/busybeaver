# Lettie Cotherstone

Lettie Cotherstone, the daughter of Highmarket builder and councillor Cotherstone, is engaged to be married to Bent and serves as her father's primary emotional anchor—her very existence is the reason Cotherstone has built his prosperous life and the thing he fears most to lose.

---

## Role in the Story

Lettie represents normalcy and domestic happiness in a narrative otherwise dominated by guilt, blackmail, and murder. She is Cotherstone's only child and the centre of his emotional world: "All his life was now centred" on Lettie [003-003]. Her engagement to Bent is a source of pride and satisfaction to Cotherstone, who hopes to see her settled in comfort [002-007]. As the crisis over Kitely's blackmail escalates, Cotherstone's thoughts turn "instinctively to Lettie"—fearing that exposure of his past would "ruin Lettie's chances" of a respectable marriage [012-026–027]. Structurally, Lettie functions as the innocent whose welfare raises the stakes of Cotherstone's concealment: every choice he makes is shadowed by the knowledge that discovery would destroy her future.

## Character & Traits

Lettie possesses a "calm and judicial character" that distinguishes her from the more volatile personalities in the novel [012-034]. She is practical and self-possessed—when her father announces he must leave Highmarket for his health, she questions the decision with clear-eyed reasoning, objecting to disrupted wedding arrangements and noting that winter is an odd time to travel [012-037–042]. Her responses to her father's agitation are measured: when Cotherstone claims to have seen a doctor, she is sceptical and probing rather than passively accepting [012-032].

Lettie's maturity is evident in her handling of social situations. She hosts Bent and Garthwaite at her father's house with ease, serving drinks and keeping conversation flowing while Cotherstone is visibly distressed [003-030–038]. She shows an interest in the wider world, remarking that true crime stories are "Facts—much stranger than fiction" [003-033]. Her engagement to Bent appears built on genuine affection and mutual respect; he "laid his hand on Lettie's arm" in a gesture of reassuring intimacy when discussing her father's health [012-041].

## Key Relationships

- **Cotherstone** (father): The relationship is deeply affectionate on both sides. Cotherstone's ambition and his guilt are both driven by his desire to secure Lettie's future. When Kitely's blackmail threatens exposure, Cotherstone's first fear is for Lettie's reputation [012-026–027, 002-007].
- **Bent** (fiancé): Lettie is engaged to Bent, Cotherstone's business partner Mallalieu's nephew. They appear frequently together and their wedding planning is a significant subplot. Lettie is firm about her preferences for the arrangements, showing she is not a passive bride but an active participant in planning her future [012-044]. Bent serves as an intermediary between Cotherstone and Lettie, tasked with "talking her round" about the proposed travel [012-049].
- **Garthwaite** (acquaintance): A neighbour who visits socially; Lettie handles his presence with the same composed hospitality she shows all guests [004-012].

## Key Actions

1. **Manages the tense dinner** (Chapter III): When Cotherstone returns home agitated after Kitely's visit, Lettie maintains a warm social atmosphere for Bent and Garthwaite, serving drinks and deflecting tension with calm competence [003-030–038].

2. **Challenges her father's departure plan** (Chapter XII): When Cotherstone announces he must travel for his health, Lettie questions the logic—why winter, why now, why disrupt the wedding plans? Her resistance is practical rather than emotional, rooted in a clear-eyed assessment of what makes sense [012-037–044].

3. **Insists on managing her own wedding** (Chapter XII): Lettie pushes back against the suggestion that changing wedding arrangements is trivial, declaring "If I am to be married I should like to arrange things in my own way" [012-044]. This assertion of autonomy reveals a quiet strength beneath her calm exterior.

4. **Provides stability during crisis** (Chapters III–V): Throughout the early stages of Cotherstone's mounting panic, Lettie is the still point—unaware of the true cause of her father's distress but instinctively providing the domestic comfort and normalcy that he clings to [003-026, 005-051].

## Significance

Lettie represents the domestic ideal that Cotherstone has built his reformed life around—and that his past crime threatens to destroy. Her character embodies the central thematic tension of the novel: the relationship between private guilt and public respectability. Cotherstone's entire life in Highmarket—his business, his council seat, his home—has been constructed to provide for Lettie, and it is Lettie's future that would be most devastated by exposure. Her calm self-possession also serves as a contrast to the increasingly frantic behaviour of the men around her: while Cotherstone, Mallalieu, and even Bent grow agitated, Lettie remains steady and rational—the moral centre that the men have failed to protect.
