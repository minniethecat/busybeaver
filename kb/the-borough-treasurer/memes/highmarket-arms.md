# Highmarket Arms

The principal hotel and public house of [[Highmarket]], serving as a meeting-place for legal professionals, a refreshment-stop for travellers, and a barometer of the town's shifting public opinion. Throughout the novel, the Highmarket Arms functions as a civic forum — the place where the men of the town gather to discuss events, and where the investigation's progress is measured in the volume and temper of the talk in its big room.

## Geography

The Highmarket Arms is situated at the commercial centre of Highmarket. It offers both a "private room" [017-041] for confidential meetings and a "big room" [030-032] where the townsmen congregate. Mallalieu's departure for the railway station — "Mallalieu had gone away to Hull" [021-003] — is noted as occurring from the Highmarket Arms, suggesting the hotel serves as a departure-point for travel. The inn's dual function as hotel and public house makes it a crossroads: private business in its rooms, public opinion in its bar.

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher uses the Highmarket Arms as a barometer of community sentiment. After pivotal events, it is to the Highmarket Arms that the townsmen gravitate, and what is said there registers the temperature of public feeling:

> "The men in the big room at the Highmarket Arms — all these things had stung him to desperation." [030-032]

This single line reveals the Highmarket Arms' narrative function: it is the gathering-place where the verdict of the town is delivered, informally but irresistibly. The "big room" is the novel's jury of public opinion — the men who sit there are not the twelve in the jury box, but the community whose judgment matters equally.

Earlier in the narrative, Brereton uses the Highmarket Arms as a way-station — he "retired into a private room of the Highmarket Arms, ate a sandwich and drank a glass of ale" [017-041] — the inn providing the investigator with both sustenance and a place to collect his thoughts. And on the night of the crime, "Mallalieu had gone away to Hull" [021-003] from the Highmarket Arms, the departure noted as part of the timeline of that evening.

The Highmarket Arms accommodates both the solitude of the investigator and the collective voice of the town — a dual function that mirrors the novel's oscillation between private knowledge and public judgment.

## Narrative Atmosphere

The Highmarket Arms is the novel's sounding-board — the place where public opinion registers. Fletcher gives the inn's "big room" a collective voice: it is "the men in the big room at the Highmarket Arms" [030-032] who speak, not any one individual. The Highmarket Arms is where the town's judgment takes shape, not in the formal proceedings of the court (which happen at the Town Hall), but in the informal court of public talk. For Cotherstone, who has survived the legal trial, it is the talk in the big room at the Highmarket Arms that proves unbearable — the law may have acquitted him, but the town has not.
