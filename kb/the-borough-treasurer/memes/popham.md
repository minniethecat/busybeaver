# Mr. Popham

Mr. Popham is the senior partner of the London law firm Popham & Pilboody, whose offices are in Cursitor Street [PN=127-1270]. Though he does not appear in person in the novel, his name recurs through his connection to Christopher Pett, and the firm's reputation plays a role in verifying Pett's professional background.

## Role in the Novel

### Brereton's Inquiry

When Brereton first meets Christopher Pett, he notices that Pett's address is "that of Messrs. Popham & Pilboody in Cursitor Street" and asks whether Pett has "Any connection with that well-known firm?" [PN=127-1270]. The question is pointed — Brereton is already suspicious of Pett and testing his credentials.

### Pett's Patron

Pett responds with "soft" reverence, explaining that Mr. Popham "has always been my very good friend" and that he entered Popham's service "at an early age" [PN=127-1274]. According to Pett, Popham "acted very handsomely" — giving him his articles of clerkship, and when Pett was admitted to the rolls two years prior, Popham & Pilboody "very generously" gave him an office in their suite "so that I could have my name up, and do a bit on my own" [PN=127-1274].

Pett concludes emphatically: "Oh yes! — I'm connected — intimately — with that famous firm, Mr. Brereton!" [PN=127-1274].

### The London Connection

The mention of Popham & Pilboody provides a London connection for Christopher Pett that parallels the later investigation by Carfax's agents. While Stobb investigates Miss Pett in Woking, Leykin's London investigation likely touches upon the professional circles of Cursitor Street solicitors — though this is not detailed in the novel, the existence of Pett's London legal connections establishes him as a figure with a plausible public face [PN=291-2918].

## Significance

Mr. Popham, though never appearing on stage, represents the London legal establishment that provides Christopher Pett with his professional cover. The firm's reputation — "that well-known firm" — lends legitimacy to Pett, even as Brereton's instinctive suspicion suggests that anything connected to Christopher Pett merits careful scrutiny. The Popham & Pilboody connection is part of the novel's broader pattern of London legal and investigative figures intervening in the northern provincial case.
