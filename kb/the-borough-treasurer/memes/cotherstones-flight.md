# Cotherstone's Flight

**Cotherstone's Flight** is the planned escape of Councillor Cotherstone from Highmarket following the death of Herbert Stoner. Unlike Mallalieu's dramatic escape from custody, Cotherstone's flight is contemplated in solitude, reflecting his internal turmoil and the impossibility of truly escaping the past.

## Contemplation

In the aftermath of Stoner's death, Cotherstone reflects "on the ease with which he could get away from Highmarket and from England."[017-017] The thought is not a plan of action so much as a recognition of possibility — he has the means to flee, and the thought circles persistently in his mind.

## Mallalieu's Parallel Planning

While Cotherstone contemplates flight, his partner Mallalieu is actively arranging escape routes. Mallalieu knows "a safe place in the Lower Town" of Norcaster "where he could be hidden for a month, three months, six months, without fear of discovery."[022-029] He envisions reaching Norcaster, then making his way "to the Continent from there."[024-067]

## The Appeal of Norcaster

Norcaster features prominently in the escape planning as both a waypoint and a sanctuary. Mallalieu, "well-acquainted" with Norcaster's "obscure parts,"[021-013] sees the city as the "first important stage of h[is]" escape.[021-013] The flight plan is to "strike by devious way for Norcaster"[022-029] across the moors, avoiding detection.

## Thwarted Plans

Mallalieu's plans are discovered through the testimony of a young courting couple who saw him on the moors. "A young man from Norcaster had come over to Highmarket that Sunday afternoon to visit his sweetheart; that this couple had gone up the moors; that they were on the opposite si[de]"[022-013] — their evidence places Mallalieu at the scene and frustrates his escape before it can begin.

## Cotherstone's Arrest

Cotherstone's own flight never materialises. After Mallalieu's dramatic escape from the Town Hall, the police "conducting him to Norcaster, had told him of the Mayor's escape,"[025-002] and the net closes around Cotherstone before he can act on his own plans. The ease of escape he had contemplated proves illusory — the past cannot be outrun, only confronted.

## Significance

Cotherstone's contemplated flight and Mallalieu's actual escape attempt represent two responses to the same predicament. Cotherstone's passive reflection versus Mallalieu's active (if futile) scheming mirrors their contrasting temperaments throughout the novel. Both flights, whether attempted or merely contemplated, are ultimately denied by the inexorable progress of justice.
