# Tallington

The [[Highmarket]] solicitor whose name appears on a bill of exchange as guarantor — a document that proves to be the first hard evidence linking [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] to the thirty-year-old [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester conspiracy]]. His quiet professionalism and moral clarity make him the instrument through which justice is finally served.

## Role in the Story

Tallington is introduced relatively late in the narrative but becomes pivotal to its resolution. As the senior solicitor in [[Highmarket]], his name carries weight — and when a bill of exchange surfaces bearing "Lawyer Tallington's name at the foot o' that bit o' paper" (014-008), it transforms the investigation from speculation to documented proof.

His role is twofold: first, as the legal professional to whom [[Cotherstone]] chooses to confess; second, as the calm, methodical presence who guides that confession into actionable evidence. Unlike [[Brereton]], who is emotionally invested in proving [[Harborough]]'s innocence, Tallington approaches the case with the detachment of a man who has seen enough of human weakness to be unsurprised by it.

## Character & Traits

Tallington is described as "a middle-aged man" (020-009), a detail that carries weight — he belongs to the same generation as [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]], but has chosen a different path. His professional standing in [[Highmarket]] is unquestioned, which makes him the ideal recipient for a confession that will destroy the town's most prominent citizens.

His manner is quiet but incisive. When [[Cotherstone]] finally unburdens himself, Tallington "listened with absorbed attention" (020-010) — not with shock or moral outrage, but with the focused concentration of a professional assessing a client's position. His first response is practical rather than judgmental:

> "There are two things to be done," said Tallington. "They're the only things that can be done." (020-011)

This instinct for action over moralising makes him the novel's most effective legal figure — more so than the magistrates or the formal court system, which are shown to be fallible.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Cotherstone]]** (client turned confessor): Their relationship transforms from ordinary solicitor-client business to something more profound. Cotherstone chooses Tallington as his confessor — "he would tell everything to Tallington" (020-009) — and Tallington proves worthy of that trust, handling the revelation with professional integrity while ensuring justice is served.
- **[[Brereton]]** (fellow legal professional): Though Brereton is the more active investigator, Tallington is the more authoritative legal figure. Brereton brings Tallington into the case at the crucial moment, recognising that his seniority and standing are needed.
- **[[Bent]]** (client): The Bent family are Tallington's clients, and it is through this connection that he becomes involved in the case. Bent's trust in Tallington is absolute, and well-placed.
- **[[Mallalieu]]** (mayor and criminal): Tallington's professional relationship with the Mayor makes the final confrontation more devastating — two pillars of [[Highmarket]] society, one true and one false.

## Key Actions

1. **The bill of exchange** (014-005–013): Tallington's name appears as guarantor on a bill involving [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]]. The document, discovered by workmen, provides the first material evidence linking the present-day officials to the past conspiracy. "There's Tallington's name to back it" (014-013), one man observes — a detail that establishes the paper trail the investigation will follow.

2. **Receiving Cotherstone's confession** (020-009–043): In his private room, Tallington listens as Cotherstone unburdens thirty years of guilt. His methodical questioning — "Did you have any report of what happened?" (020-037), "Were you present at that trial?" (020-039) — extracts the full truth without cruelty or evasion.

3. **The decisive question** (020-043–046): After a "moment of suspense" (020-043), Tallington asks "the question which all three men were longing to have answered" — who killed Kitely? His willingness to press where others hesitate forces the final revelation.

4. **Managing the legal aftermath** (020-049): Tallington's grim observation that "this can't be kept in a corner" (020-015) acknowledges the public dimensions of the case. He understands that private confession must become public justice, and he manages that transition with professional skill.

## Significance

Tallington represents the rule of law in a novel where legal institutions are frequently shown to be inadequate. The magistrates' bench can be manipulated; the police can be misled; even the [[Assizes|assizes court]] can convict the innocent. But Tallington — the individual solicitor in his private room — embodies law as craft, as profession, as moral calling.

His presence also provides structural balance. Where [[Brereton]] is the young, energetic investigator and [[Cotherstone]] is the tormented criminal, Tallington is the steady centre — the professional whose calm competence makes justice possible.
