## Overview

[[murder|Kitely's murder]] is the catalytic event of *The Borough Treasurer* — the crime that transforms a story of municipal [[class|respectability]] and concealed pasts into a murder investigation. [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]], a retired police detective who has recognised [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] as the ex-convicts Mallows and Chidforth, is strangled in [[pine-wood|the Pine Wood]] on the outskirts of [[Highmarket]]. His murder is at once a desperate act of self-preservation and an inevitable consequence of the thirty-year deception the partners have maintained.

## The Crime

### The Location

[[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s body is discovered in [[pine-wood|the Pine Wood]], a secluded area on the outskirts of [[Highmarket]]. The location is significant: isolated enough for a murder to be committed unseen, yet close enough to the town that the body is quickly found. The wood itself becomes a [[reputation|character]] in the investigation — its paths, coppices, and clearings are repeatedly searched for evidence.

### The Method

[[James-Kitely|Kitely]] is strangled with his own necktie. Fletcher's description emphasises the grim irony of the murder weapon:

> "A mean and paltry thing to be the instrument of a foul murder." (005-013)

The choice of weapon — improvised, personal, intimate — suggests a killer who acted on impulse rather than premeditation. Strangulation requires proximity; the murderer stood close enough to his victim to look him in the face.

### The Immediate Aftermath

A crucial detail: [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s pocketbook is found beside the body, emptied of its contents. The murderer has taken the papers [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] carried — the memoranda containing the cryptic references to "M. & C.", "[[wilchester-assizes|Wilchester Assizes]]", and "81". The killer was not merely silencing a [[blackmail|blackmailer]] but recovering evidence that could incriminate him.

> "The pocketbook, as you know, was picked up close to the body. It was empty — as we all saw. Now what can we infer from that but that the murderer actually stopped by his victim to examine the papers?" [011-006]

[[Brereton]]'s reconstruction of this moment — the killer searching by torchlight, hearing a sound in the coppices, fleeing with the papers and leaving the empty case — becomes a key piece of his theory.

## The Investigation Begins

### Discovery of the Body

The body is discovered by [[Cotherstone]], [[Bent]], and [[Brereton]] during a walk. [[Cotherstone]]'s behaviour immediately strikes [[Brereton]] as suspicious:

> "He was not at all sure that Cotherstone's astonishment on hearing Garthwaite's announcement was not feigned, was not a piece of pure acting... Because when Cotherstone, Garthwaite, Bent, and Brereton set out from Cotherstone's house to look at the dead man's body, Cotherstone led the way straight to it." [010-019]

[[Cotherstone]] knew exactly where the body lay — because he had been there before.

### The Suspect

[[harborough|John Harborough]] is immediately [[suspicion|suspected]]. A piece of cord from his coil is found at the scene; he has no alibi for the crucial hours; he had been seen with [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] earlier that day. The circumstantial case against him appears strong — but it is entirely wrong.

### Mallalieu's Manipulation

[[Mallalieu]], as Mayor, immediately takes charge of the police response, directing [[garthwaite|Garthwaite]] to focus on [[harborough|Harborough]]:

> "No matter how much he believed in Harborough's innocence, Harborough was the man to go for — at present. Attention must be concentrated on him, and on him only." [007-004]

This cynical calculation — sacrificing an innocent man to protect his own secret — reveals the moral abyss beneath [[Mallalieu]]'s civic dignity.

## The Motive

### Blackmail

[[James-Kitely|Kitely]] had recognised [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] from the [[Wilchester]] trial thirty years earlier. He had been dropping hints, extracting money, making his knowledge felt:

> "It's thirty years since I first saw you and Mallalieu. That's all... In the dock — at Wilchester Assizes. Eh?" [001-043]

[[the-blackmail|Kitely's blackmail]] was patient, subtle, and escalating. For [[Mallalieu]] — a man who had built a new life, a new identity, a mayoralty — the threat of exposure was existential.

### Cover-up Upon Cover-up

[[murder|Kitely's murder]] is the second crime in a chain. The first was the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] thirty years earlier. The murder is an attempt to bury that original crime — but murder only deepens the guilt. When [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]] independently pieces together the same secret, [[Mallalieu]] kills him too: the [[secret|cover-up]] begets more violence, each crime making the next more necessary and more desperate.

## The Cord

### The Evidence Trail

The cord used to strangle [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] is traced to a coil belonging to [[harborough|Harborough]]:

> "Whoever did this job, cut that length of cord off my coil, which anybody could get at, and used it to throw suspicion on me!" [008-018]

[[harborough|Harborough]]'s insistence that the cord was planted — that anyone could have accessed his coil — is dismissed by the police but proves correct. The cord is not evidence of [[harborough|Harborough]]'s guilt but of the murderer's cunning.

## Impact and Consequences

[[murder|Kitely's murder]] sets in motion the entire machinery of the novel:

1. **The investigation**: [[Brereton]] takes on [[harborough|Harborough]]'s defence and begins his own inquiry
2. **The exposure**: [[Wilchester]] [[trial|court]] records are unearthed, revealing the fraud
3. **The collapse**: [[Cotherstone]] breaks under pressure; [[Mallalieu]] kills [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]] then flees
4. **The reckoning**: Both partners are brought to justice, their thirty-year deception destroyed

The death of one blackmailing ex-detective — "a mean and paltry thing" [005-013], like the necktie that killed him — brings down the most powerful men in [[Highmarket]].
