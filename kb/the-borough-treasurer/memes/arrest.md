## Overview

The arrests of [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] represent the legal climax of *The Borough Treasurer*. After [[Brereton]] and [[tallington|Tallington]]'s relentless investigation exposes the [[Wilchester]] secret, the two partners — once the most respected men in [[Highmarket]] — are taken into custody and formally charged with murder. The arrests unfold in two stages: [[Cotherstone]]'s surrender to the inevitable, and [[Mallalieu]]'s desperate resistance.

## Cotherstone's Arrest

### The Confrontation

[[Brereton]] and [[tallington|Tallington]] confront [[Cotherstone]] directly with the evidence of his past:

> "Are you and your partner, Mr. Mallalieu, the same persons as the Chidforth and Mallows who were prosecuted for fraud at Wilchester Assizes in 1881 and sentenced to two years' imprisonment?" [020-028]

[[Cotherstone]], unlike [[Mallalieu]], lacks the will to maintain the deception. The weight of thirty years of [[secret|concealment]] collapses in a moment.

### Surrender

[[Cotherstone]]'s arrest is marked by resignation rather than resistance. The psychological toll of his double life — the [[fear|anxiety]], the fear of exposure, the corrosive mutual suspicion with [[Mallalieu]] — has exhausted him. Arrest, in some sense, is a release: the secret no longer needs to be kept.

## Mallalieu's Arrest

### Prelude: Flight Interrupted

[[Mallalieu]] has been preparing for this moment since [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s arrival. His specially-designed waistcoat — "a cleverly arranged garment, in which a man could carry a lot of wealth — in paper" [021-015] — holds his entire fortune in portable securities. But before he can execute his planned flight, the net closes.

### The Town Hall

[[Mallalieu]] is arrested at the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] — the seat of his mayoral [[power|authority]]. The location is heavy with irony: the chief magistrate of [[Highmarket]] is taken into custody in the very building where he dispensed justice. The [[trial|magistrates]] who commit him are "a bench of his fellow-[[trial|magistrates]]" (022-002) — men who have sat beside him, deferred to him, respected him.

### The Dramatic Escape

Crucially, the police neglect to search [[Mallalieu]] on arrest:

> "What a mercy, what blessing, he thought, that they had not searched him on his arrest! — that they had delayed that interesting ceremony until his committal!" [022-028]

This oversight leaves [[Mallalieu]] with a concealed key — and the means to execute his escape (see [[mallalieus-escape]]).

## Harborough's Arrest (Earlier)

The novel's arrest motif is established early with [[harborough|Harborough]]'s wrongful detention:

> "There's enough to warrant his arrest... and he'll be committed on it, and he'll go for trial." [008-043]

[[harborough|Harborough]]'s arrest — based on circumstantial evidence and his own stubborn refusal to provide an alibi — serves as a counterpoint to the later arrests. The innocent man is taken first; the guilty men, protected by their [[honour|reputation]], remain free until [[the-investigation|Brereton's investigation]] proves otherwise.

The contrast is stark: when [[harborough|Harborough]] is arrested, he declares defiantly, "I could prove my innocence down to the ground!" [008-026]. When [[Cotherstone]] is confronted, he collapses. When [[Mallalieu]] is taken, he schemes.

## Symbolic Significance

### The Reward Poster

[[the-investigation|Brereton's investigation]] is aided by the offer of a reward:

> "Five hundred pounds would be paid by Mr. Tallington, solicitor, of Highmarket, to any person or persons who would afford information which would lead to the arrest and conviction of the murderer or murderers of the deceased Kitely." [014-005]

The reward — funded by [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]'s anonymous donation — demonstrates the power of money to drive justice, a recurring theme in the novel.

### The Phone Call

Fletcher uses a small but evocative detail when the telephone rings in [[Cotherstone]]'s office:

> "A sudden ringing at the bell of the telephone in the outer office made Cotherstone jump in his chair as if the arresting hand of justice had suddenly been laid on him." [003-004]

The image of the "arresting hand of justice" foreshadows the literal arrest to come and underscores [[Cotherstone]]'s guilty [[guilt-concept|conscience]].
