# Testimony

**Testimony** is the formal giving of evidence by witnesses in legal proceedings. In the Highmarket affair, testimony serves as the primary mechanism through which the truth about the murder, the conspiracy, and the characters of the accused is constructed and contested.

## In the Police Court

The police court hearings generate a "mass of testimony"[020-010] as witnesses are called to give their accounts. Brereton, observing the proceedings, notes that the authority figures are "not only taking in every fact and noting every point, but [are] also weighing up the mass of testimony"[020-010] — a reminder that testimony must be evaluated, not merely recorded.

### Financial Evidence

A constable "testified that after searching the prisoner after his arrest he found on him over twenty pounds in" currency,[009-003] providing material evidence to support the financial motive suggested by other witnesses.

### Character Testimony

The proceedings also draw on character testimony from the community. "There's abundant testimony in the town — from his daughter, from neighbours, from tradesmen — that Harborough was never short of money,"[010-012] a point that cuts both ways: it speaks to Harborough's character while raising the question of where his money came from if not from crime.

## Range of Witnesses

The trial assembles a diverse collection of testimonies: "Myler, of course, and old Pursey, and the sweethearting couple: there were other witnesses, railway folks, medical experts, and townspeople who could contribute some small quota of testimony."[025-023] This range — from expert witnesses to ordinary townspeople — illustrates how testimony weaves together different perspectives into a composite picture of events.

## Strategic Use

Testimony is not merely reported but strategically deployed. After Cotherstone's evidence concluded, a barrister "insisted on recalling the two sweethearts, and he got out of them, separately (each being excluded from the court while the other gave evidence),"[025-025] a tactical move designed to expose inconsistencies between accounts.

## Significance

Testimony in the novel serves as a narrative device as much as a legal mechanism. Through the accumulation, comparison, and strategic deployment of witness accounts, the truth of the Highmarket affair is gradually assembled — piece by piece, voice by voice. The process mirrors the reader's own experience of gathering evidence across the chapters.
