# Garthwaite

Garthwaite, a neighbour of Mallalieu in Highmarket, is the man who discovers the body of James Kitely in the pine wood on the moor—a discovery that transforms a private blackmail into a public murder investigation.

---

## Role in the Story

Garthwaite serves as the novel's primary witness-discoverer, the ordinary citizen whose accidental finding of Kitely's body brings the crime out of darkness and into the machinery of law. His role is structurally essential: without his discovery on the moor, the murder might have gone undetected, or at least been significantly delayed. He is introduced as "your neighbour—Mr. Garthwaite" when Bent brings him to Cotherstone's house with shocking news [003-040]. Throughout the early chapters, Garthwaite functions as both a factual witness and a dramatic device—his detailed account of finding the body provides the reader with the first full description of the crime scene.

## Character & Traits

Garthwaite is a practical, observant man who keeps his composure under extraordinary circumstances. When he arrives at Cotherstone's house, he is careful to warn Lettie before revealing the nature of his news: "don't be frightened" [003-044]. His account of finding Kitely's body is methodical and precise—he describes the location on the moor, the position of the body, the rope around the neck, and the state of the corpse with a detective's eye for physical detail [004-017–025].

He is also considerate of others' sensibilities: when Cotherstone reacts with visible distress to the news, Garthwaite glances at Lettie before continuing, weighing whether to speak freely in her presence [004-012]. His relationship with Mallalieu is neighbourly—he was passing Mallalieu's property on his return from Spennigarth when he made his discovery [004-017]. Garthwaite's own reaction suggests a man unaccustomed to violence: he "had certainly been shocked" by what he found [005-002], yet he maintained the presence of mind to summon help and guide the authorities to the scene.

## Key Relationships

- **Mallalieu** (neighbour): Garthwaite lives near Mallalieu and is walking past his property when he discovers the body. Bent describes him as "that neighbour of his" [006-047]. Garthwaite's account of seeing Mallalieu locking up his garden gate places Mallalieu near the scene shortly before the discovery [004-008].
- **Cotherstone** (acquaintance): Garthwaite brings the news to Cotherstone's house because Bent has brought him there. He observes Cotherstone's agitation with some surprise [004-006]. Cotherstone later accosts Garthwaite outside, demanding details with an intensity that borders on suspicion—"what makes you think it's murder?" [004-011].
- **Bent** (acquaintance): Bent introduces Garthwaite to the Cotherstone household and accompanies him throughout the night's events [003-040, 005-051–052].
- **Brereton** (investigator): Garthwaite guides Brereton to the body on the moor and provides the initial witness account that shapes the investigation [004-023].

## Key Actions

1. **Discovers Kitely's body** (Chapter IV): Returning from Spennigarth across the moor, Garthwaite comes upon Kitely's corpse in the pine wood—a body with a cord around its neck, lying beneath a rock face with a hole in the thatch above [004-017–029]. He touches the body and confirms it is cold, then immediately goes to summon help.

2. **Reports the finding** (Chapter III–IV): Garthwaite brings the news to Cotherstone's house, where Bent, Lettie, and Cotherstone are gathered. His careful, detailed account—delivered while conscious of Lettie's presence—provides the first public knowledge of the murder [003-044–046, 004-006–031].

3. **Guides the investigation party to the scene** (Chapter IV): Garthwaite leads Cotherstone, Brereton, and Bent back to the pine wood, retracing his steps and pointing out the key forensic details: the rope, the rock face, the hole in the thatch [004-021–031]. He also sends a man into town to fetch the police [004-032].

4. **Advises the police on the crime scene** (Chapter IV): When the police inspector arrives, Garthwaite points out that the path to the rock face would show no footprints on its grassy surface—a practical observation that reveals his attentiveness to physical evidence [004-044].

## Involvement in the Crime

Garthwaite has no connection to the crime itself—he is a wholly innocent party whose involvement is purely circumstantial. His discovery of the body is genuinely accidental; he was walking home by a route that took him past the pine wood. The narrative uses Garthwaite's ordinariness to underscore the extraordinary nature of the crime: a neighbour walking home from an errand stumbles upon murder, and the everyday world of Highmarket is suddenly transformed.

## Significance

Garthwaite embodies the role of the civic-minded citizen whose ordinary actions bring crime to light. His methodical reporting of what he saw—and his restraint in not embellishing or speculating beyond the facts—establishes him as a reliable witness. In a novel concerned with how communities respond to hidden crime, Garthwaite represents the public conscience: the person who, upon finding something terrible, does the right thing by reporting it promptly and testifying accurately. His presence also serves the narrative function of the "disinterested witness"—unlike Cotherstone or Mallalieu, Garthwaite has no secret to protect and can speak freely, making his testimony the baseline of truth against which other accounts are measured.
