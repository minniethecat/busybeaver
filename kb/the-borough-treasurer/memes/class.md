## Definition and Role in the Novel

Class in *The Borough Treasurer* is not the grand Victorian panorama of aristocrats and labourers but the fine-grained social distinctions of provincial life. The [[reputation|character]]s occupy the anxious middle ground — "better-class young workingmen" [002-003], "small tradesman and better-class workingman order" [018-011], the "better class of loungers and townsmen" [030-014] — where respectability is both aspiration and fragile possession.

## The Better-Class Workingman

### A Distinct Social Category

Fletcher repeatedly uses the phrase "better-class" to describe the social stratum of his [[reputation|character]]s:

> "Two better-class young workingmen, charged with embezzling the funds of a building society." [002-003]

This is not the working class of factory hands and labourers but skilled workers, clerks, and small tradesmen — men with ambition, education, and social aspirations. Mallows ([[Mallalieu]]) was a builder's foreman; Chidforth ([[Cotherstone]]) was a clerk. Both "continued their education at night schools and institute classes" [018-011].

### The Building Society

The [[Wilchester]] building society whose funds they embezzled was itself a class institution:

> "The members of which were chiefly of the small tradesman and better-class workingman order." [018-011]

The fraud was a betrayal not of distant institutions but of peers — men of the same class who had trusted them.

## Class Mobility and Its Costs

### The Rise to Respectability

After serving their sentences, [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] transform themselves. They move north, change their names, and build a successful business. [[Mallalieu]] becomes Mayor of [[Highmarket]] (serving two terms); [[Cotherstone]] becomes Borough Treasurer (six years in the post). Their rise is a testament to the possibilities of self-reinvention in Victorian England.

But the rise is built on stolen capital — the two thousand pounds "never recovered" [017-047] from the building society fraud.

### The Fragility of Achieved Status

The novel demonstrates how fragile achieved class status can be. A single exposure — [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s knowledge — threatens to undo thirty years of patient construction. The "better class of loungers and townsmen in [[Highmarket]]" [030-014] who gather at the _Arms_ are the audience before whom this social death would be performed.

## Class and Marriage

### Lettie and Bent

[[Cotherstone]]'s [[fear|terror]] about his daughter's engagement reveals the class calculus of marriage:

> "An ambitious young man like Windle Bent would care to ally himself with the daughter of an ex-convict." [012-027]

[[Bent]] is a solicitor, a professional man — a step above [[Cotherstone]] in the social hierarchy. The marriage represents upward mobility for the [[Cotherstone]] family, and its preservation is worth any price.

### Harborough and His Daughter

[[harborough|Harborough]], by contrast, is less concerned with social climbing. His daughter [[avice-harborough|Avice]] is [[trial|court]]ed by [[Brereton]] — a barrister from [[london|London]], significantly above her station. The novel treats this cross-class romance sympathetically, contrasting it with the [[Bent]]-[[Cotherstone]] alliance of convenience.

## Class in the Courtroom

The novel's climactic scenes unfold in the [[trial|court]]house, where class distinctions are stark:

> "The room was full already. Men had come across from the court, and from the crowd outside." [030-014]

The [[justice|legal system]] — judges, barristers, solicitors — operates at a class remove from the "better-class workingmen" it judges. The barrister known as "the Felons' Friend" straddles both worlds, comfortable in [[trial|court]] and at the _Arms_.
