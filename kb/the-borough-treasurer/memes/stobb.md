# Mr. Stobb

Mr. Stobb is an ex-Scotland Yard detective who now works as a private inquiry agent with his partner Leykin [PN=290-2906]. He is employed by the solicitor Carfax to investigate Miss Pett's past, specifically focusing on her time as housekeeper to Major Stilman at Woking.

## Role in the Novel

### Appearance and Demeanour

Stobb is described as "a big, cheery-faced man, who looked like a highly respectable publican" [PN=292-2922]. His pleasant, unimposing appearance belies a sharp investigative mind, and he approaches his work with professional detachment.

### The Woking Investigation

Carfax dispatches Stobb to Woking to investigate Miss Pett's background as housekeeper to Major Stilman at Kandahar Cottage [PN=291-2918]. Stobb completes the investigation efficiently: "I found out all about the lady and her connection with Woking in a very few hours" [PN=292-2924].

He discovers that Major Stilman was an invalid suffering terribly from neuralgia, obtaining drugs from "every chemist in the place" [PN=292-2924]. Stilman was found dead in bed with drugs by his bedside — the official verdict was "death from misadventure — overdose" [PN=292-2924].

### Uncovering the Financial Motive

Stobb digs deeper and uncovers the financial details: Major Stilman "had nothing but his pension, and a capital sum of about two thousand pounds" — and he left that two thousand pounds and his furniture to Miss Pett in a will executed about a year before his death [PN=292-2928]. The will was proved quickly, and Miss Pett "got her legacy," then sold the furniture and left the neighbourhood [PN=292-2928].

### Professional Restraint

When Brereton directly asks Stobb for his theory about Miss Pett's involvement in Stilman's death, Stobb refuses to speculate, stating: "Not my business to say what my theories are, Mr. Brereton. All I had to do was to find out facts, and report them" [PN=293-2932].

## Significance

Stobb represents the professional, evidence-focused detective. His investigation at Woking — combined with Leykin's parallel London inquiries — establishes the crucial pattern in Miss Pett's career: she becomes housekeeper to elderly, wealthy men who subsequently die under suspicious circumstances after making wills in her favour. This evidence is central to exposing her as the true criminal.
