# The Shawl

A steep, "somewhat rugged slope" [004-021] on the outskirts of Highmarket, covered with trees, coppices, and rocks. The Shawl is the central crime scene of the novel — the location where the body of [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]] is discovered, and the place where [[harborough|John Harborough]] keeps his isolated cottage [005-049].

## Geography

The Shawl is a hillside sloping down from the moor above Highmarket. It is described as having "coverts and coppices and rocks" [005-045], with trees scattered across the slope [005-053]. At one end stands Harborough's cottage — "a good quarter of a mile off" from the nearest other dwellings [005-045] — and at the foot of the hill, a cluster of houses marks the edge of town [005-045]. A shortcut path crosses the Shawl, connecting the moor to the lower parts of Highmarket [004-017].

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher presents the Shawl as a lonely, semi-wild place that becomes ominously significant after dark. Miss Pett describes it as unsafe for nighttime walking, noting that "it's not safe to go walking in lonely places in country districts like this late at night" [005-045], citing the distance from habitation and the abundance of cover where "plenty of bad characters" might lurk [005-045].

> "I pointed out to him that in spots like this, where there's naught nearer than them houses at the foot of the hill one way, and Harborough's cottage another way, and both of 'em a good quarter of a mile off, and where there's all these coverts and coppices and rocks, it was not safe for an elderly man who sported a fine gold watch and chain to go wandering about in the darkness." [005-045]

## Key Events Here

- **Discovery of Kitely's body**: [[garthwaite|Garthwaite]] finds Kitely lying dead on the Shawl and runs to alert [[Cotherstone]] and his guests [006-047]
- **Kitely's nightly walks**: Kitely had a habit of walking on the Shawl every night, a routine that Miss Pett repeatedly warned him against [005-037]
- **The investigation begins**: The doctor, [[Bent]], and other townsmen ascend the Shawl with lanterns to examine the scene [004-046]
- **Harborough's arrest**: Police descend on Harborough's cottage at the far end of the Shawl to take him into custody [005-049] [005-058]
- **Miss Pett's shortcut**: On the night of the murder, Miss Pett crossed the Shawl via a shortcut through the wood [004-017]

## Associated Characters

- **[[James-Kitely]]**: The murder victim, whose body is found on the Shawl
- **[[harborough|John Harborough]]**: Lives at the far end of the Shawl in an isolated cottage; the cord from his shed is linked to the murder weapon
- **[[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]**: Kitely's housekeeper, who crossed the Shawl on the night of the murder
- **[[garthwaite|Garthwaite]]**: First to discover Kitely's body on the Shawl
- **[[Bent]]**: Among the party that first examined the crime scene on the Shawl
- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Visited the Shawl during the investigation

## Narrative Atmosphere

The Shawl functions as the novel's primary locus of menace — a liminal space between civilization and the wild moor where the hidden crimes of Highmarket surface into view. Its physical features — darkness, isolation, rock cover, "coppices and coverts" — are repeatedly invoked to create a sense of lurking danger [005-045]. The Shawl at night becomes a stage where the respectable facades of Highmarket's leading citizens are stripped away, and the novel's central mystery is bodied forth in the form of Kitely's corpse [006-047].
