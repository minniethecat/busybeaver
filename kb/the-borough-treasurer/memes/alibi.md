# Alibi

The legal defense of having been elsewhere at the time of a crime — a critical element in the investigation of [[murder|Kitely's murder]] and the subsequent assessment of [[suspicion|suspect]]s.

## Definition in Context

In the procedural framework of *The Borough Treasurer*, the alibi serves as both a shield for the innocent and a weapon for the guilty. Suspects are evaluated by their ability to account for their whereabouts at the time of [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s death. The absence of a credible alibi becomes grounds for suspicion; the presence of a false alibi becomes evidence of guilt.

The novel exploits the dramatic tension inherent in the alibi — the gap between where a [[suspicion|suspect]] claims to have been and where they actually were. [[Cotherstone]]'s movements on the night of the murder become central to the investigation, and his inability to provide a satisfactory account deepens [[Brereton]]'s [[suspicion|suspicions]].

## Key Passages

The investigation into [[murder|Kitely's murder]] immediately focuses on establishing the whereabouts of potential [[suspicion|suspect]]s. [[harborough|Harborough]], initially [[suspicion|suspected]] by the police, faces scrutiny of his movements (010-015). His inability to provide a convincing alibi contributes to his being charged, despite [[Brereton]]'s belief in his innocence.

> The magistrates' examination tests each suspect's account of their time. Witnesses are called to corroborate or contradict the stories told by those under suspicion.

The novel demonstrates how alibis can be both genuine and fabricated. The conspirators construct alibis to deflect suspicion, while genuinely innocent [[reputation|character]]s find themselves unable to prove their whereabouts. This asymmetry — the guilty being better prepared than the innocent — is one of the novel's realistic touches.

## Character Perspectives

**[[harborough|Harborough]]** suffers from the absence of a verifiable alibi, his honest inability to account for every moment being read as guilt. **[[Cotherstone]]** constructs alibis with care, under[[reputation|standing]] their importance in deflecting suspicion (021-015). **[[Brereton]]** evaluates alibis skeptically, recognizing that a too-perfect account is as suspicious as no account at all.

## Plot Significance

The alibi structure drives much of the novel's suspense. The reader knows — or [[suspicion|suspect]]s — who is guilty, but watches as the legal process grapples with competing accounts of where each [[suspicion|suspect]] was and when. The resolution of the alibi question — who can account for their time and who cannot — is central to the novel's climactic revelations.
