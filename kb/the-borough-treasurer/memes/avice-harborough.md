# Avice Harborough

The daughter of [[Harborough]], whose wrongful arrest for [[Kitelys-murder|Kitely's Murder]] transforms her from a conventional young woman into a determined amateur investigator — working alongside [[Brereton]] to uncover the truth behind the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester Affair]].

## Role in the Story

Avice Harborough is introduced as the spirited daughter of John Harborough, a man who lives as a recluse on the edge of the [[the-moor|moors]]. When her father is arrested on suspicion of murdering [[Kitely]], Avice refuses to accept the passive role society would assign her. Instead, she becomes an active partner in [[Brereton]]'s investigation, questioning witnesses, following leads, and providing the emotional determination that complements Brereton's legal methodology.

Her position is unique: she bridges the world of the accused (her father) and the world of the investigators, able to move between both. This dual perspective proves invaluable as the case unfolds.

## Character & Traits

Avice is characterised by her quick intelligence and emotional courage. When Sergeant [[Garthwaite]] suggests her father might be connected to the murder, her response is immediate and fierce:

> "Find the murderer!" exclaimed Avice with a quick flash of her eyes. "Find the murderer!" (008-054)

She is not merely reactive but strategic. It is Avice who first suggests that [[Brereton]] investigate [[Kitely]]'s mysterious past, a line of inquiry that ultimately unravels the entire conspiracy:

> "But Kitely himself!" exclaimed Avice. "Who knows anything about him?" (008-057)

Unlike the passive female figures common in Edwardian fiction, Avice insists on accompanying Brereton to interviews, walking across the moors to meet witnesses, and pressing for answers where others would defer.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Harborough]]** (father): A complicated relationship built on loyalty. Avice never doubts her father's innocence, but her faith is tested by his stubborn refusal to explain himself. "Your father," Brereton observes to her, "is the most mysterious man I ever met" (013-052) — and Avice can only reply, "Not an idea! Not the ghost of one" (013-053) when asked to explain his behaviour.
- **[[Brereton]]** (ally): A working partnership of mutual respect. Brereton provides legal expertise and investigative method; Avice provides local knowledge, determination, and the personal stake that drives the case forward. Their collaboration across the [[the-moor|moors]] — walking, talking, piecing together evidence — forms the investigative spine of the novel.
- **[[Bent]]** (fiancé's father): Bent's father, a local businessman, engages Brereton as counsel at Avice's urging, formally bringing the solicitor into the case.
- **[[Tallington]]** (legal authority): The solicitor to whom Avice eventually turns, though only after considerable hesitation about fully disclosing what she knows.

## Key Actions

1. **Demanding investigation** (008-046–057): Encountering Brereton on the road, Avice insists on an aggressive pursuit of the real murderer, not merely a defence of her father. This conversation pivots the investigation from reactive legal defence to proactive detection.

2. **The suggestion about Kitely** (008-057): Avice is the first to articulate what will become the investigation's central insight — that [[Kitely]] himself, rather than any local suspect, holds the key to the crime. Her question "Who knows anything about him?" opens the path to the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester Affair]].

3. **The moor walk** (019-002–015): Walking with Brereton across the [[the-moor|moors]] above Highmarket, Avice listens in "absolute silence" (019-008) as he lays out the full case. Her composure under devastating revelations demonstrates the strength of character that makes her more than a mere client's daughter.

4. **The scrap-book meeting** (018-019–034): Avice delivers the crucial envelope from her father to Brereton — the scrap-book evidence that will ultimately convict [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]]. Her willingness to act as courier for sensitive evidence shows her commitment to the truth, whatever the cost to community standing.

## Significance

Avice Harborough is the novel's moral centre. Where the male characters — [[Cotherstone]], [[Mallalieu]], even [[Brereton]] — are driven by professional obligation, ambition, or self-preservation, Avice acts from love and loyalty. Her determination to clear her father's name powers the investigation through its darkest moments.

Structurally, she serves as the reader's surrogate: someone outside the legal and municipal establishment who must piece together the truth from fragments of evidence. Her transformation from sheltered daughter to determined investigator mirrors the novel's broader movement from surface respectability to underlying truth.
