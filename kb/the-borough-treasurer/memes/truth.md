## Definition and Role in the Novel

Truth — and its deliberate suppression — is the central thematic engine of *The Borough Treasurer*. The entire plot turns on a thirty-year-old secret: the real identities and criminal past of [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]], who fled [[Wilchester]] after serving time for fraud and rebuilt their lives under assumed names. The [[murder|murder of Kitely]], the blackmailing ex-detective, is a direct consequence of their determination to keep that truth buried.

## Key Moments

### The Buried Truth

[[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]]'s respectable lives in [[Highmarket]] rest on a foundation of deception. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s arrival threatens to expose the truth of their past:

> "It's thirty years since I first saw you and Mallalieu. That's all." [001-050]

[[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s knowledge forces the partners into a corner: the truth of their criminal past would destroy everything — [[Mallalieu]]'s mayoralty, [[Cotherstone]]'s position as Borough Treasurer, and his daughter [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie]]'s marriage prospects.

### Harborough's Silence

When [[harborough|John Harborough]] is arrested for [[murder|Kitely's murder]], he refuses to provide an alibi, insisting the truth will emerge on its own:

> "I shan't say! The truth'll come out in good time without that." [008-022]

His stubborn faith in the self-evident power of truth is both noble and dangerous — it leaves him vulnerable to the machinery of justice while the real murderers remain free.

### Brereton's Investigation

Gifford [[Brereton]], the young barrister who takes up [[harborough|Harborough]]'s defence, embodies the active pursuit of truth:

> "There's some very strange mystery about that man, and I'll have to get at the truth of it — in spite of his determined reticence!" [010-010]

[[Brereton]]'s investigation methodically peels back layers of [[secret|concealment]], from [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]]'s cryptic notes to the [[Wilchester]] [[trial|court]] records.

### Mutual Suspicion Between Partners

As the investigation progresses, the truth begins to corrode the partnership between [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]]:

> "When Mallalieu faced the truth he knew that he suspected Cotherstone, and when Cotherstone put things squarely to himself he knew that he suspected Mallalieu." [012-012]

The truth, once set in motion, cannot be contained — it poisons the very alliance it was meant to protect.

### The Unpleasant Truth

[[Cotherstone]]'s primary motivation is to shield his daughter from the truth of his past:

> "Bent would have the best of excuses for breaking off all relations with the Cotherstone family if the unpleasant truth came out." [012-027]

This domestic dimension elevates truth from a mere plot device to a deeply personal stake.

## Characters and Truth

| Character | Relationship to Truth |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **[[Mallalieu]]** | Active suppressor — manipulates and schemes to keep the past buried |
| **[[Cotherstone]]** | Anxious concealer — fears exposure above all else |
| **[[James-Kitely|Kitely]]** | Possessor of damaging truth — uses it as leverage |
| **[[harborough|Harborough]]** | Trusts truth to vindicate him — refuses to defend himself |
| **[[Brereton]]** | Active seeker — dedicates himself to uncovering the full story |
| **[[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]** | Sends anonymous 900 pounds to fund the defence, knowing [[harborough|Harborough]]'s innocence |

## Thematic Resonance

Fletcher constructs truth as a force that cannot be permanently suppressed. The novel's resolution — the exposure of [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]]'s past — affirms the inexorable logic of revelation. Yet the cost of truth is high: lives are destroyed, [[honour|reputation]]s shattered, and the "plain truth" [006-042] proves anything but simple.
