## Overview

The investigation conducted by Gifford [[Brereton]] forms the narrative spine of *The Borough Treasurer*'s middle and later chapters. Retained to defend [[harborough|John Harborough]] — who stubbornly refuses to provide an alibi — [[Brereton]] undertakes his own parallel investigation. Where the police focus narrowly on [[harborough|Harborough]], [[Brereton]] pursues the deeper mystery: who really killed [[James-Kitely|Kitely]], and why? His methodical detective work peels back thirty years of [[secret|concealment]] to expose the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] and its murderous consequences.

## Phase One: The Defence Strategy

### Retained for the Defence

[[Brereton]], a young barrister staying with his friend Windle [[Bent]] in [[Highmarket]], takes on [[harborough|Harborough]]'s case after the arrest. His initial assessment of [[harborough|Harborough]] is that "that man gives one all the suggestion of innocence" [008-041] — but he knows appearances can deceive.

His first strategic move is to create doubt at the police [[trial|court]] hearing. He cross-examines [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] to introduce "a new atmosphere into the case" [009-078], successfully diverting attention from [[harborough|Harborough]]:

> "Brereton knew that nothing is so useful in the breaking-down of one prejudice as to set up another, and his great object just then was to divert primary prejudice away from his client." [009-078]

### The Alibi Problem

[[Brereton]] articulates the precise legal challenge:

> "What we want to know, in order to fully establish Harborough's innocence is — where was he, what was he doing, from the moment he left his cottage last night until say a quarter past nine." [010-008]

He enlists [[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]] to scour the countryside on her bicycle, seeking witnesses who might have seen her father that night.

## Phase Two: Following the Trail

### Stoner's Pocketbook

The discovery of the dead clerk [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]]'s pocketbook — containing a cryptic entry about "M. & C. — fraud — bldg. soc. — [[wilchester-assizes|Wilchester Assizes]] — 81" [017-047] — opens a new line of inquiry. [[Brereton]] travels to [[Wilchester]], searches the newspaper archives, and finds the trial report of Mallows and Chidforth:

> "It was a very plain story — there were no complications... both had been fully and firmly trusted, and it appeared from the evidence that they had had what practically amounted to unsupervised control of the building society's funds." [018-011]

### The Anonymous Letter

A letter arrives containing £900 and instructions to spare no expense in proving [[harborough|Harborough]]'s innocence — "which is known to the sender" [013-029]. This mysterious benefactor (later revealed as [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]) provides the financial resources to pursue the investigation vigorously.

### Connecting the Threads

[[Brereton]] synthesises evidence from multiple sources:

| Source | [[evidence|Evidence]] |
|--------|----------|
| [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s memoranda | "M. & C.", "[[wilchester-assizes|Wilchester Assizes]]", "81" |
| [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]]'s pocketbook | "Mallows and Chidforth", "fraud", "£2,000" |
| [[Wilchester]] newspaper | [[trial|Trial]] report confirming identities, sentence |
| [[pursey|Mr. Pursey]]'s [[evidence|testimony]] | Verbal [[alibi|account]] of the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] |

The convergence is undeniable: [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] are Mallows and Chidforth, ex-convicts with a secret worth [[murder|killing]] to protect.

## Phase Three: The Confrontation

### Telling Tallington

[[Brereton]] shares his [[verdict|finding]]s with Superintendent [[tallington|Tallington]], a man of "great [[honour|reputation]] for common sense and for probity" [020-009]. Together they confront [[Cotherstone]]:

> "Are you and your partner, Mr. Mallalieu, the same persons as the Chidforth and Mallows who were prosecuted for fraud at Wilchester Assizes in 1881 and sentenced to two years' imprisonment?" [020-028]

### The Unraveling

The confrontation triggers the collapse of the thirty-year deception. [[Cotherstone]] confesses; [[Mallalieu]], learning of [[Cotherstone]]'s breakdown, plans his flight. The investigation has achieved what the police could not: it has found the real murderers and exonerated the innocent man.

## Method and Significance

### Brereton's Method

Brereton's investigation is methodical, evidence-based, and intellectually honest. He follows facts rather than assumptions. Unlike the police — who settle on [[harborough|Harborough]] as the convenient [[suspicion|suspect]] — [[Brereton]] asks the deeper question: *cui bono*? Who benefits from [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s death?

His investigation exemplifies the novel's theme of [[truth]] — the patient, determined pursuit of fact over appearance.

### The Parallel Investigation

The novel structures two parallel investigations:
- **The police investigation** ([[garthwaite|Garthwaite]]): narrow, procedural, focused on the obvious [[suspicion|suspect]]
- **Brereton's investigation**: broad, imaginative, following the trail of motive

The police see the cord from [[harborough|Harborough]]'s coil; [[Brereton]] sees the thirty-year secret that made murder necessary. The contrast is Fletcher's commentary on the limitations of official justice.
