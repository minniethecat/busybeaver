# John Harborough

A "queer character" [005-054] living at the far end of the Shawl — a professional pig-killer who also "does a bit of rat-catching, and a bit of mole-catching — and a good deal of poaching" [005-054]. He becomes the primary early suspect in the murder of [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]] after a piece of cord from his shed is linked to the crime.

## Role in the Story

John Harborough is introduced as the man whose cottage lies at the far end of the Shawl, a quarter-mile from the murder scene [005-045]. When investigators discover that a distinctive cord used in the murder matches cord from Harborough's shed, he becomes the prime suspect [007-004] [007-005]. Despite the circumstantial evidence against him, several characters — including the doctor and [[Mallalieu]] — express doubt about his guilt, noting that "Harborough's not a man that's ever been in trouble" [007-044] and that the cord could have been stolen [005-024].

## Character & Traits

Harborough is described as "an odd person altogether, not only in character but in appearance" [005-054]. He is resourceful and shrewd — "that man Harborough's no fool!" [006-061] — and maintains a fierce independence, living by his various trades. Despite his rough occupation and poaching activities, he is regarded by those who know him as essentially honest: "This isn't his job" [006-061]. The most striking detail about Harborough is the contrast he makes with his daughter: "he's got an exceedingly good-looking and accomplished daughter, a really superior girl who's been well educated and earns her living as a governess" [005-054].

> "To John Harborough's cottage — at the other end of the hill," answered Bent. "He's the man they spoke of in there. He's a queer character — a professional pig-killer, who has other trades as well. He does a bit of rat-catching, and a bit of mole-catching — and a good deal of poaching." [005-054]

## Key Relationships

- **[[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]]**: His daughter, a well-educated governess whose refinement contrasts sharply with her father's rough trade [005-054]. Their relationship is described as "a queer pair" when seen together.
- **[[wraythwaite|John Wraythwaite]]**: Foster-brothers — Harborough's mother raised Wraythwaite [027-039], creating a bond between the two men that transcends their different social stations.
- **[[James-Kitely]]**: The murder victim. Harborough was present at the bank counter when Kitely withdrew money on the day of the murder [007-030], a piece of circumstantial evidence that "looks very black against Harborough" [007-035].

## Key Actions

- **Seen at the bank counter**: Present when Kitely withdrew money on the day of the murder, establishing opportunity [007-030] [007-033]
- **Arrested as prime suspect**: Brought in by the police after the cord from his shed is linked to the crime [007-004] [007-061]
- **Flees or is believed to have fled**: The police issue a description and expect he "'ll probably have made for" somewhere [007-011]
- **Defended by those who know him**: Mallalieu and the doctor both speak in his defence, arguing the evidence is circumstantial and inconsistent with his character [007-044] [006-031]

## Involvement in the Crime

Harborough's connection to the murder is entirely circumstantial: the cord used to strangle Kitely matches cord from his shed [007-005], and he was seen at the bank when Kitely withdrew money [007-030]. However, the narrative establishes early doubts about his guilt. The doctor notes that "somebody might have stolen one of his cords" [005-024], and the "very surest proof that this man Harborough had nothing to do with the murder" is suggested [006-031]. His cottage's isolation and his unconventional lifestyle make him an easy target for suspicion — a classic red herring in the detective fiction tradition.

## Significance

John Harborough embodies the novel's concern with appearances versus reality. His rough trade and poaching make him an obvious suspect in the eyes of the law, but his essential honesty and the loyalty of those who know him point toward his innocence. The contrast between Harborough and his accomplished daughter Avice [005-054] dramatizes the social mobility and class tensions of the period. His foster-brother relationship with the gentleman Wraythwaite [027-039] further complicates the class picture, suggesting bonds that run deeper than social station. Harborough is ultimately a device through which Fletcher explores the dangers of circumstantial evidence and the prejudice against those who live outside conventional society.
