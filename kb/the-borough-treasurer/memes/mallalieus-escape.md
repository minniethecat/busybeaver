## Overview

[[Mallalieu]]'s escape from custody is one of the most dramatic sequences in *The Borough Treasurer*. Arrested at the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] during the hearing into [[murder|Kitely's murder]], [[Mallalieu]] takes advantage of lax security — the police have not yet searched him — to unlock a private door with a concealed key and slip away into the darkness. His flight takes him across [[the-moor|the moors]] to [[Harborough-cottage|the cottage]] of [[christopher-pett|Christopher Pett]] and [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]], where he encounters an unexpected — and deeply ironic — refuge.

## The Arrest

[[Mallalieu]] is [[arrest|taken into custody]] at the [[town-hall|Town Hall]], where the hearing into [[murder|Kitely's murder]] is proceeding. The charges against him are grave — but crucially, the police have delayed searching him:

> "What a mercy, what blessing, he thought, that they had not searched him on his arrest! — that they had delayed that interesting ceremony until his committal!" [022-028]

This omission leaves [[Mallalieu]] with his specially-designed waistcoat (containing his fortune in securities), his revolver, and most importantly — the key that will unlock his prison door.

## The Escape

### Execution

When Superintendent [[tallington|Tallington]] leaves the room to find a constable, [[Mallalieu]] moves instantly:

> "As soon as the superintendent had left him he drew from his pocket a key, went across the room to a door which stood in a corner behind a curtain, unlocked it, opened it gently, looked out, passed into a lobby without, relocked the door behind him, and in another instant was stealing quietly down a private staircase that led to an entrance into the quaint old garden at the back of the premises." [022-028]

His intimate knowledge of the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] — earned over two decades of municipal service — proves invaluable. He navigates through the garden, into an orchard, and then to the coppices at the foot of [[the-shawl|the Shawl]].

### Into the Moors

Fletcher captures the atmosphere of desperate flight:

> "Dusk had fallen over Highmarket before the hearing came to an end, and it was now dark. Mallalieu knew that he had little time to lose — but he also knew that his pursuers would have hard work to catch him." [022-029]

[[Mallalieu]]'s plan is to cross [[the-moor|the moors]], reach [[Norcaster]] by devious paths, and find a safe house in the Lower Town — "from whence he could get away by ship" [022-029].

## The Refuge

### The Pett Household

Exhausted and desperate, [[Mallalieu]] finds his way to the isolated cottage where [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s furniture has been inherited by [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]. Inside, he encounters [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] and her nephew Christopher — the very people whose [[evidence|testimony]] has helped expose his crimes.

[[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]'s reaction when she discovers the fugitive:

> "They took up Mallalieu and his partner today, and tried 'em, and Mallalieu slipped the police somehow, after the case was adjourned, and escaped. And — he's here!" [024-015]

### Irony of the Refuge

[[Mallalieu]] has stumbled into the house of the woman who sent the anonymous £900 to fund [[harborough|Harborough]]'s defence. [[christopher-pett|Christopher Pett]], the legal-minded nephew, immediately recognizes the danger and the potential leverage. He lectures the fugitive:

> "The mere fact of your making your escape, sir, is what some would consider a proof of guilt — it is indeed!" [024-062]

Yet Christopher also sees opportunity in the situation — a prospect of reward, or advantage, or simple self-importance as the keeper of so significant a prisoner.

### "Comfortable Captivity"

The chapter title "Comfortable Captivity" is deeply ironic. [[Mallalieu]], the fugitive, is effectively a prisoner in the [[christopher-pett|Pett]] cottage — fed, sheltered, but unable to leave. His hosts are not his allies; they are calculating their own advantage. The man who planned to vanish into the anonymity of [[Norcaster]] finds himself trapped in a remote cottage, dependent on the mercy of people who have no reason to help him.

## The Escape's Significance

### Proof of Guilt

[[christopher-pett|Christopher Pett]] articulates the legal reality:

> "The mere fact of your making your escape, sir, is what some would consider a proof of guilt." [024-062]

[[Mallalieu]]'s flight confirms what the accumulating evidence suggests: he is a murderer. His escape is simultaneously an act of self-preservation and self-incrimination.

### The Collapse of Respectability

The Mayor of [[Highmarket]] — the silk-hatted, prosperous civic leader — is reduced to a fugitive hiding in a cottage, negotiating with eccentrics for his survival. The collapse is complete: from [[class|respectability]] to criminality, from public honour to private shame.
