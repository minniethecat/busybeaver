# Suspicion

A defining psychological atmosphere of *The Borough Treasurer* — the corrosive distrust that spreads through the community, threatening [[honour|reputation]]s and relationships as the murder investigation unfolds.

## Definition in Context

Suspicion in the novel operates at multiple levels: personal, communal, and institutional. As [[Brereton]] investigates [[murder|Kitely's murder]], "an unformed, vague suspicion" (010-017) takes root in the town, transforming neighbors into potential accusers. The atmosphere of suspicion is generated by the crime itself but sustained by the conspirators' efforts to deflect blame — [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] "must move heaven and earth lest suspicion should fall on him" (007-003).

The community's response is swift: "country folk, always quick to form suspicions, were beginning to ask themselves questions" (009-078). What begins as speculation hardens into conviction, with two primary suspects emerging — [[harborough|Harborough]] and [[Cotherstone]] (010-015).

## Key Passages

> "There are two people under suspicion: Harborough's suspected by the police, and Cotherstone is suspected by me," Brereton declares, articulating the dual track of the investigation (010-015).

The psychology of suspicion is explored with particular subtlety in the [[reputation|character]] of [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie]]. [[Brereton]] confronts her father [[Bent]] about the "atmosphere of mutual dislike and suspicion between them" (012-012), revealing how suspicion has poisoned even domestic relationships. [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie]] herself feels "the atmosphere of suspicion which it engendered in the community" (009-004).

> Cotherstone uses suspicion as a weapon, feeding Brereton false information "to throw suspicion on me" as Bent later realizes (008-018).

After [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s death, "all his suspicions were renewed" in the mind of those who knew the conspirators' past (014-025), demonstrating how suspicion builds upon itself. [[Mallalieu]] eventually confesses: "I believe he killed [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]. I suspicioned it from the first" (020-050).

## Character Perspectives

**[[Brereton]]** embodies rational suspicion — methodical, evidence-based, directed toward solving the crime. His suspicion of [[Cotherstone]] is immediate and sustained, founded on observation rather than prejudice (007-003).

**[[Cotherstone]]** represents the paranoid dimension of suspicion — the fear of being suspected drives him to increasingly desperate acts. He "foresaw that suspicion might fall on [[Cotherstone]]" and prepared accordingly (021-015). **The townspeople** demonstrate communal suspicion, quick to form [[verdict|judgment]]s and slow to revise them (009-078).

**[[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie]]** experiences suspicion as personal anguish, her loyalty to her father tested by the accumulating evidence against him. The "suspicion about my father" (006-020) becomes the emotional core of her storyline.

## Plot Significance

Suspicion functions as both atmosphere and plot mechanism. It creates the tension that propels the investigation forward, while simultaneously serving as a weapon wielded by the guilty to deflect attention. The novel traces how suspicion transforms from diffuse unease into focused accusation, and how the innocent suffer under its weight alongside the guilty.
