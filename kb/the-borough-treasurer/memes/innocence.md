## Definition and Role in the Novel

Innocence in *The Borough Treasurer* operates on multiple levels: the legal innocence of [[harborough|John Harborough]], wrongly accused of murder; the presumed innocence of the respectable [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]], which is revealed as a facade; and the moral innocence of [[reputation|character]]s like [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie Cotherstone]], who know nothing of their parents' crimes. Fletcher systematically interrogates the gap between appearance and reality — who is truly innocent, and how can innocence be proved?

## Harborough's Claim of Innocence

### The Arrest

When [[harborough|John Harborough]] is arrested for [[murder|Kitely's murder]], he responds with defiant confidence:

> "If I liked to say the word, I could prove my innocence down to the ground! There!" [008-026]

Yet he refuses to provide an alibi, creating a paradox: he claims [[evidence|proof]] of innocence exists, but will not produce it. This stubbornness baffles both the police and his defenders.

### Brereton's Assessment

Gifford [[Brereton]], the barrister who takes on [[harborough|Harborough]]'s defence, is struck by the man's bearing:

> "That man gives one all the suggestion of innocence," remarked Brereton, thoughtfully, "and from a merely superficial observation of him, I, personally, should say he is innocent." [008-041]

But [[Brereton]] is too experienced to trust appearances alone — he knows "the most hardened and crafty criminals assume an air of innocence, and keep it up, to the very end" [008-041]. The legal [[evidence|proof]] of innocence requires evidence, not intuition.

### The Anonymous Benefactor

The most dramatic affirmation of [[harborough|Harborough]]'s innocence comes from an unknown source — a letter enclosing £900, with instructions:

> "You are not to spare it nor any endeavour to prove Harborough's innocence — which is known to the sender." [013-029]

This mysterious intervention — from [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]], as later revealed — transforms the defence. The benefactor's certainty ("the sender" who *knows*) contrasts with the public ambiguity surrounding [[harborough|Harborough]].

### The Alibi Problem

[[Brereton]] articulates the precise nature of [[harborough|Harborough]]'s legal jeopardy:

> "What we want to know, in order to fully establish Harborough's innocence is — where was he, what was he doing, from the moment he left his cottage last night until say a quarter past nine." [010-008]

The narrow window between [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s last sighting and the estimated time of death is the critical gap. Proving [[harborough|Harborough]]'s innocence means [[alibi|account]]ing for every minute.

## Mallalieu's Manipulated Innocence

[[Mallalieu]]'s immediate response to [[murder|Kitely's murder]] is to direct suspicion towards [[harborough|Harborough]]:

> "No matter how much he believed in Harborough's innocence, Harborough was the man to go for — at present. Attention must be concentrated on him, and on him only." [007-004]

This cynical calculation — pursuing an innocent man to protect his own [[secret|secrets]] — reveals the moral void beneath [[Mallalieu]]'s respectable exterior.

## Miss Pett's Injured Innocence

[[miss-pett|Miss Pett]], [[Harborough-cottage|Harborough's house]]keeper, deploys a performance of wounded innocence when challenged:

> "She had brought him his tea in silence, his supper with no more than a word... she set it before him with an expression which seemed to say that however badly she herself was treated, she would do her duty by others." [029-003]

Her "injured innocence" is a strategic performance — she is, in fact, the anonymous benefactor and a guardian of [[harborough|Harborough]]'s true interests.

## Thematic Structure

| Layer of Innocence | Example | Reality |
|-------------------|---------|---------|
| Legal innocence | [[harborough|Harborough]] accused of murder | Genuinely innocent — alibi eventually established |
| Assumed innocence | [[Mallalieu]] & [[Cotherstone]] as respectable citizens | False — built on concealed criminal pasts |
| Ignorant innocence | [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie Cotherstone]] | Genuine — she knows nothing of her father's past |
| Strategic innocence | [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] | Performance — she knows more than she reveals |
