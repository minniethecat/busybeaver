# Wilchester Assizes

The assize court in Wilchester where, in 1881 — thirty years before the novel's present — [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] were tried for building society fraud and sentenced to penal servitude [016-033]. The trial at Wilchester Assizes is the foundational event of the novel's backstory, the buried crime whose resurfacing drives the entire plot.

## Geography

Wilchester Assizes is located in Wilchester, a town some four hundred miles from Highmarket [001-037]. The geographical distance between Wilchester and Highmarket is crucial to the plot — Mallalieu and Cotherstone chose their northern exile precisely because "there was nothing whatever to bring Wilchester people into that northern country, nothing to take Highmarket folk anywhere near Wilchester" [Cotherstone's reflections].

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher presents Wilchester Assizes not through physical description but through the fragments of documentary evidence that survive: the scrap of newspaper recording the trial outcome [017-047], the recollections of witnesses like [[pursey|Mr. Pursey]] who remember "the Wilchester Assizes at which the Mallows & Chidforth people were tried" [015-017], and the dread it inspires in the two men whose lives were transformed in its dock.

> "M. & C. — fraud — bldg. soc. — Wilchester Assizes — 81 — £2,000 — money never recovered — two years." [017-047]

## Key Events Here

- **The 1881 fraud trial**: Mallalieu (then Mallows) and Cotherstone (then Chidforth) were prosecuted for building society fraud, sentenced to two years' penal servitude, and the £2,000 was never recovered [016-033] [017-047]
- **Kitely's role**: [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] was present at Wilchester Assizes as the man whose testimony or knowledge helped convict the two defendants — a fact that "became Cotherstone's undoing" [015-028]
- **The sentencing**: "Two years' penal servitude" [020-028] — a punishment that, once served, allowed Mallalieu and Cotherstone to reinvent themselves in Highmarket, until Kitely's reappearance threatened to expose everything

## Associated Characters

- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Tried and sentenced here in 1881; the threat of exposure at a new assize drives his actions in the present
- **[[Cotherstone]]**: Co-defendant in the 1881 trial; the memory of Wilchester Assizes haunts his every move
- **[[James-Kitely]]**: The man whose connection to the Wilchester trial makes him a mortal threat to Mallalieu and Cotherstone
- **[[pursey|Mr. Pursey]]**: Remembers the Wilchester Assizes trial and testifies about its details

## Narrative Atmosphere

Wilchester Assizes functions as the novel's shadow court — the site of a past judgment that has never truly been discharged. Though physically distant, it exerts a gravitational pull on the present, drawing Mallalieu and Cotherstone back toward the exposure they have spent thirty years avoiding. The fragments of evidence that survive from the trial — the newspaper scrap, the pencilled notes [018-009], the witnesses' memories — function like buried bodies that refuse to stay buried.

> In the dock — at Wilchester Assizes. Eh? [001-037]

The phrase echoes through the novel as both threat and prophecy: Mallalieu, who once stood in the dock at Wilchester, will stand in the dock again — this time at Highmarket's own Town Hall court, facing a murder charge that traces directly back to the Wilchester trial and the secret it spawned.
