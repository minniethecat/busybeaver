# Guilt

The psychological and legal condition that drives every major [[reputation|character]] in *The Borough Treasurer* — from [[Cotherstone]]'s thirty-year burden to [[Mallalieu]]'s hardening into remorselessness, from the community's rush to [[verdict|judgment]] to the slow accumulation of evidence that makes denial impossible.

## Definition in Context

Guilt in Fletcher's novel operates on two levels. There is legal guilt — the factual question of who committed [[Kitelys-murder|Kitely's murder]] and who perpetrated the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]]. And there is moral guilt — the internal knowledge of wrongdoing that shapes behaviour, corrodes relationships, and ultimately demands expression.

The novel's psychological acuity lies in its treatment of how these two forms of guilt interact. Legal guilt can be hidden, for a time, through silence and deception. But moral guilt — the knowledge of what one has done — cannot be indefinitely suppressed. It surfaces in [[Cotherstone]]'s trembling hands when [[Kitely]] confronts him (001-057), in [[Mallalieu]]'s desperate violence on the [[the-moor|moor]] (016-030), in the very atmosphere of "mutual dislike and suspicion" (012-012) that pervades [[Highmarket]].

## Key Passages

1. **[[Cotherstone]]'s confrontation with [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]** (001-057–002-041): When [[Kitely]] reveals that he knows their secret, [[Cotherstone]]'s physical reaction — trembling, unable to speak — is the body's confession before the mind can form words. "His arm shook when [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] laid a hand on it" (001-057). Guilt here is visceral, pre-verbal, beyond the [[power|control]] of will.

2. **The blackmail conversation** (002-035–041): [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] discuss their predicament, and [[Cotherstone]]'s response — "Wants to blackmail us, of course" (002-035) — is telling. He reaches for the criminal category ("blackmail") to avoid confronting the underlying crime. The guilt is acknowledged only obliquely, through its consequences.

3. **[[Brereton]]'s hypothesis** (013-002): The investigator articulates what the reader has begun to [[suspicion|suspect]]: the goal is "to definitely fix the guilt on some other person" (013-002). The legal language — "fix the guilt" — reveals how guilt is not merely a feeling but a determination, something that must be established by evidence and argument.

4. **[[Cotherstone]]'s confession to [[tallington|Tallington]]** (020-009–043): The decisive moment. After thirty years, [[the-newspapers|the press]]ure of guilt becomes unbearable. [[Cotherstone]] "would tell everything to [[tallington|Tallington]]" (020-009) — and the relief is almost physical. [[Tallington]] listens, questions, and through his methodical inquiry transforms moral guilt into legal fact.

## Character Perspectives

- **[[Cotherstone]]**: Guilt as corrosion. His entire life in [[Highmarket]] — the successful business, the Borough Treasurership, the [[class|respectability]] — is built on a foundation of fraud. The guilt does not diminish with time; it accumulates, fed by every honour and promotion that he knows he does not deserve.
- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Guilt as hardening. Where [[Cotherstone]] is eaten away by conscience, [[Mallalieu]] is armoured by it. His guilt drives him not toward confession but toward further crime — the murder of [[Stoner]] on the [[the-moor|moor]]. He is the novel's demonstration that guilt, unconfessed, can turn a man into something worse than a criminal: a man capable of [[murder|killing]] to protect his secret.
- **[[Harborough]]**: False guilt. The recluse on the moor is [[suspicion|suspected]] of murder not because of evidence but because his behaviour — isolation, secrecy, strangeness — fits the community's idea of a guilty man. His [[innocence|vindication]] is a critique of guilt-by-[[honour|reputation]].
- **[[Brereton]]**: Guilt as professional problem. For the solicitor, guilt is something to be proved or disproved, a matter of evidence and [[evidence|testimony]]. His detachment is the novel's structural counterweight to the psychological intensity of [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]].

## Plot Significance

Guilt is the novel's organising principle. The [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] created the guilt; [[Kitely]]'s knowledge made that guilt dangerous; the murder was an attempt to escape the consequences of guilt; the investigation is the process of establishing guilt; and the resolution is the moment when guilt is finally named and accepted.

Fletcher's insight is that guilt, in a small community, is contagious. [[Highmarket]] itself becomes guilty — of blindness, of complicity, of valuing [[class|respectability]] over truth. The "atmosphere of mutual dislike and suspicion" (012-012) is the social expression of a community that senses its own corruption but cannot name it.
