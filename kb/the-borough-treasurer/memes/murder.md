# Murder

The central crime around which the plot of *The Borough Treasurer* revolves — the violent killing of [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]], a retired businessman who had recently moved to [[High-Cross|High Cross]].

## Definition in Context

In the world of the novel, "murder" is not merely a crime but the catalyst that exposes the [[secret|hidden past]] of [[High-Cross|the borough]]'s most respected citizens. The killing of [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] is initially thought to be a simple robbery-homicide (004-039), but subsequent investigation reveals deeper motives tied to long-concealed [[secret|secrets]] of municipal corruption. The crime is classified as "as determined a murder as ever I heard of" by those who examine the scene (006-049).

The murder is committed by strangulation, with the victim's own necktie used as the instrument (005-013). The body is discovered in a pine wood on the outskirts of [[High-Cross|High Cross]], dragged to [[secret|concealment]] through undergrowth (004-030).

## Key Passages

> "Well, this is murder, anyway! Are there no signs of the murderer?" demanded the Mayor, his voice sharp with alarm (004-037).

The initial assessment of the crime scene reveals the violent nature of the death. The police surgeon confirms strangulation as the cause, noting that the act was "as determined a murder as ever I heard of" (006-049). The scene shows signs that the killer dragged the body through undergrowth, leaving a "great slur" in the earth (004-030).

> "I say that if he's been murdered, there's been some reason for it outside the mere robbery theory," the doctor observed, challenging the obvious explanation (005-036).

The murder investigation quickly shifts from a robbery motive to something more complex. [[Brereton]], the young [[justice|law]]yer drawn into the case, becomes convinced that the crime was committed by someone known to the victim (007-003). The murder weapon — a necktie — is described as "insignificant in itself, a mean and paltry thing to be the instrument of a foul murder" (005-013).

## Character Perspectives

The murder draws distinct reactions from each key [[reputation|character]], revealing their moral positions. **[[Brereton]]**, the [[justice|law]]yer, immediately [[suspicion|suspect]]s [[Cotherstone]] upon learning of the crime, reconstructing the entire murder scenario in his mind with "lightning-like rapidity of thought" (007-005). **[[Cotherstone]]**, whose guilt grows increasingly apparent, protests his ignorance of the murder, insisting he knows "no more of his murder than you do—not so much" (008-014).

**[[avice-harborough|Avice]]**, [[Bent]]'s daughter, expresses the community's instinctive response: "Find the murderer!" (008-054). Meanwhile, **Dr. [[garthwaite|Garthwaite]]** serves as the rational observer, noting that "the murderer may have been interrupted" during the crime (006-037).

## Plot Significance

The murder of Kitely functions as the plot's central engine. It transforms what appears to be a story of municipal [[class|respectability]] into a criminal investigation that unravels decades of [[secret|concealment]]. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]]'s arrival in [[High-Cross|High Cross]] — ostensibly to retire — is revealed to have been motivated by his knowledge of the past, and his murder is the direct consequence of that knowledge (009-078).

The crime's significance extends beyond the act itself: it exposes the fragility of the social order in [[High-Cross|High Cross]], where [[High-Cross|the borough]] treasurer and town councillor stand revealed as men capable of murder to protect their positions (007-032). The murder investigation occupies chapters 3 through 7 and its consequences reverberate through the remainder of the novel.
