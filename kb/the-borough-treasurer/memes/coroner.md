# the coroner

The coroner is the legal official responsible for conducting inquests into violent or suspicious deaths in Highmarket. Though unnamed throughout the novel, he presides over the inquests into both Kitely's murder and Stoner's death, hearing crucial testimony that shapes the course of both cases.

## Role in the Novel

### Kitely's Inquest

Following the magistrates' committal of Harborough for trial, the coroner holds "an inquest extending over two sittings" and similarly commits Harborough — meaning there is now both a magistrates' committal and a coroner's committal for the capital charge [PN=144-1440]. Christopher Pett makes himself "conspicuous at both police-court and coroner's court" during these proceedings, asking questions wherever he can [PN=144-1442].

### Stoner's Inquest

The inquest into the death of Herbert Stoner — Cotherstone's clerk — is an adjourned inquest that draws the entire town. The coroner's court is packed, and as a coroner "has a wide field in the calling of witnesses," more evidence emerges than was heard by the magistrates [PN=265-2655]. The coroner calls a range of witnesses: Myler, Pursey, the sweethearts who discovered the body, railway officials, medical experts, and various townspeople who could "contribute some small quota of testimony" [PN=265-2655].

### Cotherstone's Testimony

The coroner formally warns Cotherstone "that he need not give any evidence at all" before Cotherstone enters the witness box [PN=265-2655]. Despite this warning, Cotherstone "determinedly entered the witness-box — to swear on oath that he was witness to his partner's crime" [PN=265-2655]. This dramatic moment — the accused man swearing to his partner's guilt — overshadows all other evidence at the inquest.

### The Defence Strategy

Cotherstone's barrister capitalizes on the coroner's proceedings, "quietly elicited from all the medical men the definite opinion that death had been caused by the blow" [PN=265-2659]. He also recalls the sweetheart witnesses, establishing that Mallalieu and Cotherstone were seen separately near the quarry — Mallalieu agitated and muttering, Cotherstone composed — creating the foundation for Cotherstone's attempted defence [PN=265-2659].

## Arrangements with the Police

Before Stoner's inquest, Mallalieu visits the police station and speaks with the coroner's officer and the police about "some arrangements about the inquest," as he seeks to manage the proceedings and ensure that suspicion continues to fall away from himself [PN=229-2299].

## Significance

The coroner's role, though procedurally distinct from the magistrates' court, mirrors and reinforces the official legal process. The coroner's inquests — particularly at Stoner's death — serve as the venue where Cotherstone attempts to turn the blame onto Mallalieu, making the inquest a pivotal dramatic scene in the novel's final act.
