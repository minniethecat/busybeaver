# London

The capital city referenced throughout the novel as the origin of legal expertise, the destination of the anonymous letter, the site of [[Kitely]]'s former employment, and above all, the city from which [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] fled after serving their prison sentences — a place of past disgrace for some, of professional authority for others, and the distant centre of the legal system that will ultimately judge the events in [[Highmarket]].

## Geography

London is situated in southern England, far removed from the northern landscape of [[Highmarket]] and its moors. It is not described visually in the novel — Fletcher never takes the reader there — but is instead constructed through reference and memory: a city of law courts, hotels, and Brixton house-property (011-028), whose institutions reach into the northern town through personnel from London solicitors' offices and barristers from the capital appearing at [[wilchester-assizes|Wilchester Assizes]].

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher presents London primarily as a contrast to [[Highmarket]]'s insular world. The northern town is suspicious of the capital and its emissaries:

> They avoided London with particular care. (002-006)

This avoidance is strategic — [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] have reason to stay away from the city where they were imprisoned and where their past might be recognised. London represents exposure, while [[Highmarket]] represents the respectable facade they have constructed.

For other characters, London represents professional competence. When [[Brereton]] arrives from the capital, he is immediately marked as different — "a young barrister from London" (003-008) whose sharpness the local police sergeant instinctively distrusts (009-005). The "cold climate after London" (003-012) is as much social as meteorological — the warmth of the metropolis is replaced by the chill of provincial suspicion.

[[Kitely]]'s connection to London is more material. Before coming to [[Highmarket]], he worked as "storekeeper at one London hotel, and linen-keeper at another" (009-070), and owned "house-property, in London — Brixton, to be exact" (011-028). This property becomes a tangible link between the capital and the northern town, proving [[Kitely]] was not the simple retired man he appeared to be.

## Key Events Here

1. **The partners' imprisonment** (implied 002-003–006): Though never directly narrated, [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] served their prison terms in London, and upon release "they had nothing but the clothes they stood up in" (002-005). This event, set in the capital before the novel's action begins, constitutes the original sin from which all subsequent events flow.

2. **The anonymous letter** (013-025): "The postmark was London" — the letter that threatens to expose the partners' past bears a London postmark, linking the capital to the machinery of blackmail and exposure. The anonymous letter does not originate from within [[Highmarket]]'s closed community but from the wider world that the partners have tried to escape.

3. **Brereton's investigation in London** (009-013–076): [[Brereton]] travels to London to investigate [[Kitely]]'s past, interviewing hotel managers and tracing his employment history. This investigation — "In London... Yes — and where, in London?" (009-013–014) — unravels the false identity [[Kitely]] constructed in [[Highmarket]] and reveals his connection to [[Harborough]]'s past.

4. **The London detectives** (016-002): Late in the investigation, London detectives arrive to assist with the search for [[Mallalieu]], bringing metropolitan expertise to the northern moors: "some of the Norcaster and London detectives weren't on the job within an hour" (016-002).

## Associated Characters

- **[[Brereton]]**: A young barrister from London whose legal training and metropolitan perspective make him the novel's most effective investigator
- **[[Kitely]]**: Former London hotel worker and Brixton property-owner, whose past in the capital is key to understanding his presence in [[Highmarket]]
- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Served his prison sentence in London and thereafter avoided the city with "particular care" (002-006)
- **[[Cotherstone]]**: Like his partner, a London ex-convict who has reinvented himself in the north
- **[[Harborough]]**: Another ex-prisoner with a London past, whose connection to [[Kitely]] was forged in the capital's criminal justice system
- **[[Bent]]**: The "sharp-looking London friend" (009-005) who assists [[Brereton]]'s investigation

## Narrative Atmosphere

London functions as the novel's absent centre — a place never directly depicted but exerting constant pressure on events. It is the city of punishment, of exposure, of legal authority. Every character's relationship to London defines their relationship to the past: [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] flee it, [[Kitely]] brought property from it, [[Brereton]] brings its legal standards northward.

This geographical opposition — the honest, open northern landscape versus the distant, morally charged metropolis — is one of Fletcher's structuring devices. The novel never entertains the possibility that justice will come from within [[Highmarket]] itself; it must arrive from London, in the person of [[Brereton]], bringing the capital's institutions to bear on the town's secrets.
