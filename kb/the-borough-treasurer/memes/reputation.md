## Definition and Role in the Novel

Reputation is the social capital that [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] have spent thirty years accumulating in [[Highmarket]]. [[Mallalieu]] has risen to Mayor; [[Cotherstone]] serves as Borough Treasurer. Both are fixtures of the community, respected and trusted. The entire machinery of [[secret|concealment]] and murder is set in motion by their [[fear|terror]] of losing that [[honour|reputation]] — of being exposed as ex-convicts.

## Reputation as Motive

### The Terror of Exposure

When [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] arrives and begins dropping hints, [[Cotherstone]]'s immediate reaction is not fear of legal consequences — it is fear of [[honour|reputation]]al ruin. The "unpleasant truth" [012-027] would destroy:

- [[Mallalieu]]'s mayoralty and [[class|social standing]]
- [[Cotherstone]]'s treasurership and business
- [[lettie-cotherstone|Lettie]]'s engagement to Windle [[Bent]]
- The family's entire position in [[Highmarket]] society

### Bent's Marriage Calculation

[[Cotherstone]]'s driving [[fear|anxiety]] is that [[Bent]], an ambitious young man, would not marry the daughter of an ex-convict:

> "It was beyond him to believe that an ambitious young man like Windle Bent would care to ally himself with the daughter of an ex-convict." [012-027]

[[Bent]] himself has a "great [[honour|reputation]] for common sense and for probity" [020-009] — a [[honour|reputation]] he would not risk.

### Private vs. Public Reputation

Fletcher draws a sharp distinction between the two kinds of [[honour|reputation]]. The barrister nicknamed "the Felons' Friend" is described as:

> "One of those people who have no private reputation to lose, and of whom it was well known that he could never withstand the temptation to a bottle of champagne." [030-013]

His public [[honour|reputation]] as a brilliant advocate coexists with a private [[honour|reputation]] for dissipation — [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]], by contrast, have no such margin for error.

## Reputation in Provincial Society

### Highmarket's Judgment

The novel portrays [[Highmarket]] as a community where [[honour|reputation]] is everything. The "better class of loungers and townsmen" [030-014] who gather at the _Arms_ represent the [[trial|court]] of public opinion. When [[Cotherstone]] walks through the [[market-place|Market Place]] with the Felons' Friend:

> "Behind them the crowd sneered and laughed and indulged in audible remarks." [030-013]

The collapse of [[honour|reputation]] is public, immediate, and merciless.

### The Commercial Traveller's Wit

[[herbert-stoner|Stoner]], the commercial traveller who "cultivated a [[honour|reputation]] for wit" [015-006], represents a different relationship to [[honour|reputation]] — performative, lightweight, acquired rather than earned. His manufactured [[honour|reputation]] contrasts with the deadly seriousness with which [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] guard theirs.

## Reputation and Class

The [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] case reveals the class dimension of [[honour|reputation]]. The perpetrators were "better-class young workingmen" [002-003], "each credited with an ambitious desire to rise in the world" [018-011]. Their crime was a shortcut to the [[class|respectability]] they craved — and the fear of losing it drives them to far worse.
