# Power

The municipal authority that structures life in [[High-Cross|High Cross]] — the offices, the contracts, and the influence that [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] wield, and ultimately abuse.

## Definition in Context

Power in *The Borough Treasurer* is primarily institutional — vested in the offices of mayor, borough treasurer, and town councillor. [[Mallalieu]] wears "the mayoral chain for the second time" (002-006), his repeated election signaling both personal popularity and the community's deference to established authority. As "chief magistrate" (006-064), [[Mallalieu|the Mayor]] combines executive and judicial authority.

The novel exposes how institutional power, when unchecked, enables corruption. The conspirators had "full control" of the building society's funds (015-019) — power without [[alibi|account]]ability that made [[fraud|embezzlement]] possible.

## Key Passages

The Mayor's dual role becomes significant when the investigation implicates him. As "chief magistrate" (006-064), he should lead the inquiry; as a [[suspicion|suspect]], he must obstruct it. This conflict between official power and personal guilt is one of the novel's central ironies.

> The "contracts to go through" (002-011) represent power in its economic dimension — the ability to direct municipal spending and profit from it. These contracts are both the mechanism of governance and the vehicle of corruption.

[[Brereton]]'s investigation challenges the power structure of [[High-Cross|High Cross]]. By pursuing evidence wherever it leads — regardless of the [[class|status]] of those implicated — he demonstrates that truth can overcome institutional authority.

## Character Perspectives

**[[Mallalieu]]** wields power comfortably, treating his mayoralty as a natural extension of his business success. **[[Cotherstone]]** is more anxious in power, using his council position defensively — as a shield against suspicion. **[[Brereton]]** respects institutional power but is not intimidated by it. **The community** defers to power instinctively, their trust in their leaders making the eventual exposure more shocking.

## Plot Significance

Power structures provide the novel's institutional framework. The investigation proceeds not against random criminals but against the most powerful men in [[High-Cross|the borough]]. The exposure of [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] is thus not merely a crime solved but a power structure dismantled — the demonstration that no position, however elevated, places one above the [[justice|law]].
