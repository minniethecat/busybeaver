# Highmarket Bank

The single bank serving the small town of [[Highmarket]], located in an old-fashioned building adjacent to the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] — a minor institution that becomes a critical node in the investigation when [[Kitely]]'s visit there on the morning of his death provides the last confirmed sighting of him, and when the bank-manager reveals the connection between [[Kitely]] and [[Harborough]].

## Geography

The bank occupies "the old-fashioned building in the High Street which flanked the Town Hall" (007-022). Its position next to the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] places it at the physical and institutional centre of [[Highmarket]], part of the same fabric of respectability that [[Mallalieu]]'s mayoralty represents. The bank's architecture is of a piece with the town's self-image: solid, old-fashioned, trustworthy — qualities the investigation will reveal as hollow.

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher uses the bank as a site of institutional regularity against which the anomaly of murder is measured. [[Kitely]]'s visit to the bank was entirely routine — he had come to collect his pension, "about noon" (007-024), as he did regularly. The bank-manager, "hurrying down to his place of business" (007-023) when summoned, embodies the ordered world that the murder has disrupted.

The bank-manager himself emerges as one of the novel's most reliable witnesses. A precise and methodical man, he provides a detailed account of [[Kitely]]'s last transaction: "Kitely was in the bank today, about noon — I attended to him myself" (007-024). His testimony establishes the timeline of the murder with institutional precision.

Crucially, the bank-manager also volunteers the information that connects [[Kitely]] to [[Harborough]]:

> He was an ex-detective. Pensioned off, of course. (007-026)

And further, "Harborough was at the counter when Kitely took his money" (007-030). This chance proximity at the bank counter becomes a vital piece of the case — evidence that [[Kitely]] and [[Harborough]] knew each other, and that their connection was not merely casual.

## Key Events Here

1. **Kitely's last transaction** (007-024): [[Kitely]] calls at the bank at noon to draw his pension money. The bank-manager attends to him personally. This is the last confirmed sighting of [[Kitely]] before his death, establishing the critical timeline window.

2. **Mallalieu summons the bank-manager** (007-019–023): When [[Mallalieu]] learns that [[Kitely]] visited the bank on the day of his death, he immediately walks to the bank manager's residence to rouse him — "I'll step round to the bank manager and rouse him" (007-019). The bank-manager hurries down to open the building.

3. **The Harborough connection revealed** (007-026–030): During questioning, the bank-manager reveals that [[Harborough]] — an ex-detective — was at the counter when [[Kitely]] took his money. He notes further that [[Kitely]] and [[Harborough]] exchanged words, and that the banknotes [[Kitely]] received "may be useful in tracing" (007-036).

4. **The banknotes as evidence** (007-036): The bank-manager supplies the numbers of the banknotes given to [[Kitely]], providing a potential trail of evidence — though the notes are never found with the body.

## Associated Characters

- **the bank-manager**: A precise, methodical witness whose institutional memory provides key evidence — he knows both [[Kitely]]'s routine and [[Harborough]]'s history as an ex-detective
- **[[Kitely]]**: His visit to the bank on the morning of his death is the last routine action of his life
- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Summons the bank-manager and is present for the questioning that reveals the [[Harborough]] connection — a moment of irony given his own secrets
- **[[Harborough]]**: Seen at the bank counter talking to [[Kitely]], a sighting that confirms their prior acquaintance
- **[[Brereton]]**: Receives the bank-manager's testimony and pursues the leads it generates

## Narrative Atmosphere

The bank represents the surface order of [[Highmarket]] — the world of regular transactions, pension collections, and reliable clerks — that the murder has shattered. Its proximity to the [[town-hall|Town Hall]] is not accidental: both institutions embody the civic respectability that [[Mallalieu]] has constructed around himself. The bank-manager's testimony, delivered in the measured language of institutional record-keeping, provides a counterpoint to the violence that follows: the precision of a pension payment set against the chaos of murder.
