# Miss Pett

Miss Pett, the elderly housekeeper of the murdered James Kitely, is a sharp-tongued and independent-minded witness whose testimony at the inquest reveals critical information about Kitely's private life, his regular trips to London, and the existence of her nephew Christopher Pett.

---

## Role in the Story

Miss Pett serves as the primary domestic witness to Kitely's life and death. As Kitely's sole household companion—"He'd naught but a housekeeper—Miss Pett" [004-047]—she is the person who can speak most authoritatively about his habits, visitors, and financial circumstances. Her appearances span two key sections of the novel: the immediate aftermath of the murder discovery (Chapters IV–V), when Brereton and the police first interview her at Kitely's cottage, and the inquest (Chapter IX), where her testimony proves unexpectedly consequential. In both settings, Miss Pett's distinctive combination of candour, defensiveness, and sharp observation makes her a memorable and structurally important character.

## Character & Traits

Miss Pett is "an elderly woman" whose physical appearance draws attention: Brereton "looked narrowly at Miss Pett when she opened the door" and the narrator elsewhere notes her "curious features" [004-053, 009-018]. At the inquest she appears in "black-gloved" mourning attire, having "discarded her red and yellow" everyday clothes [009-005]. Her manner combines the brisk competence of a long-term housekeeper with an independent streak that borders on defiance.

She is notably forthright in her responses to authority. When the police sergeant asks about Kitely's habits, she answers directly and without hesitation [005-032–040]. At the inquest, under questioning by the coroner, she matches his formality with equal gravity: her replies are precise, sometimes grim, and occasionally sharp—"I do not—so there!" she retorts when asked if she knows who might have visited Kitely [009-050]. She "drummed her black-gloved fingers" and "toyed with the fur boa" under pressure, but never loses her composure [009-019, 009-039]. Miss Pett is nobody's fool: she has her own views and is unafraid to express them.

## Key Relationships

- **James Kitely** (employer): Miss Pett served as Kitely's housekeeper and was the only other occupant of his cottage. She knew his routines—his pension days, his walking habits, his private nature—and describes him as a man who "wasn't the sort" to share confidences [009-017]. Her loyalty to Kitely is genuine but not sentimental; she speaks of him with the dry precision of someone who observed her employer clearly without illusion.
- **Christopher Pett** (nephew): Miss Pett reveals at the inquest that her nephew is a solicitor's clerk at 23b Cursitor Street—the same firm that, it emerges, employs the lawyer handling some of Kitely's business [009-056]. This connection between the Pett family and the legal profession proves significant to the unfolding investigation.
- **Brereton** (investigator): Brereton interviews Miss Pett at Kitely's cottage and later observes her inquest testimony. His "narrow" scrutiny of her suggests he recognises her potential as a key source of information [004-053].

## Key Actions

1. **Receives Kitely's body** (Chapter IV): When Brereton and the police bring Kitely's body back to the cottage, Miss Pett opens the door and is told to "make ready" for the dead man [004-057]. She handles this grim task with composure.

2. **Provides initial witness account** (Chapter V): In the immediate aftermath, Miss Pett answers the police sergeant's questions about Kitely's pension, his daily walks, and his habits—establishing the timeline of his last day and noting that his walks typically lasted "an hour or a bit longer" [005-032–040].

3. **Testifies at the inquest** (Chapter IX): Miss Pett's inquest testimony is the most detailed and revealing of any witness. She describes Kitely's regular business trips to London, his secrecy about his affairs, and his claim that he had "money coming in from London" [009-022–030]. Crucially, she identifies her nephew Christopher Pett as a solicitor's clerk, creating a link between Kitely's household and the legal world [009-056].

4. **Deflects questions about Kitely's visitors** (Chapter IX): When asked who might have visited Kitely or known his business, Miss Pett refuses to speculate, saying "No!" with finality [009-050]. Whether this is loyalty, ignorance, or deliberate concealment is left ambiguous.

## Significance

Miss Pett is more than a functional witness—she is a character whose presence enriches the novel's social texture. Her combative inquest testimony, with its mix of precise recall and stubborn reticence, is one of the most vivid courtroom scenes in *The Borough Treasurer*. She represents the ordinary person caught up in extraordinary events: a housekeeper doing her job, answering questions as best she can, and refusing to be intimidated by the machinery of justice. Her revelation about Christopher Pett—innocently offered—provides investigators with a thread that leads deeper into Kitely's hidden life. In a narrative filled with male professionals (builders, solicitors, policemen, councillors), Miss Pett's voice is a reminder that crucial evidence often comes from the domestic sphere—from the person who keeps the house, answers the door, and knows the routines that others overlook.
