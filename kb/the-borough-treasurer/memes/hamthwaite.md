# Susan Hamthwaite

Susan Hamthwaite is an old woman who lives alone in a "very lonely place on the moors up above Hobwick Quarry" [PN=018-023]. She emerges as a pivotal witness whose testimony helps establish John Harborough's alibi on the night of Kitely's murder. Described as shrewd and "not devoid of cunning," she is one of the most vividly drawn minor characters in the novel.

## Role in the Novel

### Summoning Avice Harborough

Mrs. Hamthwaite sends a note to Avice Harborough through a boy, saying that if Avice comes to see her, she "will tell you something that you might like to know" [PN=018-024]. The message reaches Avice at the same time Brereton is present, and Avice immediately recognizes its importance: "I'd like to go at once" [PN=018-031]. Despite Brereton's skepticism about why an old woman who knows something relevant hadn't come forward earlier, they both set out for the cottage on the moor.

### The Cottage Interview

When Brereton and Avice arrive at the isolated cottage, Mrs. Hamthwaite explains her delay: she had been away visiting her daughter "up the coast" and had only just returned home. She is described as a shrewd old woman, and her manner during the interview is both warm toward Avice and sharply challenging toward Brereton.

Mrs. Hamthwaite directly challenges Brereton's professional credentials, remarking with a chuckle: "I've talked to lawyers afore today, many's the time!" [PN=019-021] — establishing her familiarity with legal proceedings and her unwillingness to be overawed by a solicitor from Norcaster. She gives Brereton "a sharp look" when he questions her circumstantially [PN=019-035].

### The Alibi Evidence

Mrs. Hamthwaite's key testimony is her confident declaration: "I can tell you where Harborough was between nine o'clock and ten o'clock that night" [PN=019-043]. She asserts her recognition with absolute certainty: "If I don't know him, why then, his own daughter doesn't!" [PN=019-033].

She testifies that on the night of the murder, she went out at ten minutes to nine and returned nearly an hour later, and during that interval she saw Harborough meet a tall, bearded man near her isolated cottage. She insists on the accuracy of her clock, which she sets "by the almanac and the sunrise and sunset every day" — a vivid detail that establishes her independence and carefulness [PN=019-028].

### Under Cross-Examination

Mrs. Hamthwaite proves a resilient and spirited witness. When Brereton questions whether she might be mistaken, she shrugs off doubt entirely: "I'll tell it wherever you like, master — before Lawyer Tallington, or the magistrates, or the crowner, or anybody!" [PN=019-052]. She claps him "on the shoulder" with an energy that defies her age [PN=019-054].

Her testimony about a tall, bearded man on the moors is later confirmed when that man — John Wraythwaite — is identified as Harborough's companion. Mrs. Hamthwaite's observation, made from her lonely cottage doorstep on a dark night, becomes the critical link that completes the alibi.

## Character & Traits

Mrs. Hamthwaite is one of the novel's most vividly realized minor characters. She is sharp, unafraid, and possessed of a wry self-assurance. Her chuckle punctuates her speech — she is amused by Brereton's professional caution and enjoys the role of knowing something the lawyers do not [PN=019-021]. She is described as "not devoid of cunning," with a "smile that was not devoid of meaning" [PN=019-028] — she is no simple countrywoman but someone who understands the weight of what she knows and how to deliver it.

Her clock-setting ritual — "by the almanac and the sunrise and sunset every day" — reveals a self-reliant and precise character, accustomed to living alone on the moors where official time matters less than natural rhythms [PN=019-028]. Her physical gesture of clapping Brereton on the shoulder at the end of the interview signals warmth, confidence, and a kind of rough equality that transcends class distinctions [PN=019-054].

## Key Relationships

- **[[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]]** — she sends for Avice specifically, addressing her as "love" with genuine affection; her motive for coming forward is personal loyalty to Harborough's daughter [PN=019-023]
- **[[brereton|Brereton]]** — the Norcaster solicitor she challenges and ultimately wins over; their exchange is a battle of wits in which the old woman on the moor proves more than a match for the young lawyer [PN=019-021]
- **[[harborough|John Harborough]]** — the man whose alibi she provides; she knows him well enough to identify him unerringly at night: "if I don't know him, why then, his own daughter doesn't!" [PN=019-033]
- **[[wraythwaite|John Wraythwaite]]** — the "tall, bearded man" she saw meeting Harborough on the moors; her description of him becomes a key identification clue [PN=019-043]

## Key Actions

1. **Sent for Avice with crucial information** — recognizing the importance of what she had seen, she took the initiative to contact Harborough's daughter [PN=018-024]
2. **Withstood Brereton's cross-examination** — parried the solicitor's professional skepticism with wit and confidence, establishing her credibility [PN=019-021]
3. **Provided the definitive alibi timeline** — testified that Harborough was on the moors between approximately 8:50 and 9:50 PM, the critical window for Kitely's murder [PN=019-043]
4. **Identified Wraythwaite by description** — her account of the "tall, bearded man" provided the physical description that later converged with Carfax's investigation [PN=019-043]

## Involvement in the Crime

Mrs. Hamthwaite has no involvement in Kitely's murder and was not even in Highmarket at the time — she was visiting her daughter. Her role is that of an independent eyewitness whose testimony, delivered from a position of complete detachment from the town's politics and feuds, carries unusual weight. Her evidence is all the more credible because she came forward unprompted, after returning from her trip and learning of the accusation against Harborough.

## Significance

Susan Hamthwaite, though a minor character in terms of page count, provides the crucial link between Harborough's movements and Wraythwaite's identity. Her testimony about "the tall, bearded man" plants a clue that later converges with Carfax's investigation, completing Harborough's alibi. Beyond her plot function, she is one of Fletcher's most memorable character sketches — a sharp, self-assured old woman on the moors who proves more than a match for the professionals who come to question her. In a novel dominated by male lawyers, officials, and businessmen, Mrs. Hamthwaite's spirited intelligence offers a brief but vivid counterpoint.
