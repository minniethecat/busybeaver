# The Blackmail

The confrontation in which [[Kitely]] reveals to [[Cotherstone]] that he knows the truth about the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester conspiracy]] — and demands payment for his silence. This single conversation transforms a buried past into an active crisis, driving every subsequent event in the novel.

## Context

Thirty years before the novel opens, [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] were tried for fraud at [[wilchester-assizes|Wilchester Assizes]] — a case involving the collapse of a building society and the disappearance of two thousand pounds. Both served prison sentences. Both then reinvented themselves in [[Highmarket]], building respectable lives: [[Mallalieu]] became Mayor, and [[Cotherstone]] became Borough Treasurer.

Into this carefully constructed [[class|respectability]] walks [[Kitely]], an elderly man who has taken a cottage from [[Cotherstone]]. What seems at first an ordinary tenancy is in fact a reconnaissance. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] has recognised the two pillars of [[Highmarket]] society as the men he saw convicted thirty years before — and he intends to profit from that knowledge.

## Summary

The blackmail begins not with a demand but with a revelation. [[Kitely]] calls on [[Cotherstone]] in his office, ostensibly to pay his rent. But as the conversation unfolds, it becomes clear that this elderly, "clean-shaven, grey-haired man, spare of figure, dressed in rusty black" (001-005) is no ordinary tenant.

[[James-Kitely|Kitely]] methodically establishes his knowledge: he names the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester trial]], identifies both [[Cotherstone]] and [[Mallalieu]] as the defendants, and makes clear that he has followed their subsequent careers with interest:

> I say again — you've both done uncommonly well — uncommonly! (001-027)

The tone is studied, calm, and all the more menacing for its lack of overt threat. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] does not need to say "pay me or I will expose you" — the entire conversation is the threat.

[[Cotherstone]]'s reaction is physical collapse. "His arm shook when [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] laid a hand on it" (001-057). The suggestion of violence — "You can't very well choke the life out of me in your own office, can you?" (001-029) — reveals that the thought has crossed [[Cotherstone]]'s mind, and that [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] has anticipated it.

When [[Cotherstone]] reports the encounter to [[Mallalieu]], [[Mallalieu|the Mayor]]'s response is colder and more calculating. "Wants to blackmail us, of course" (002-035). Where [[Cotherstone]] trembles, [[Mallalieu]] schemes. This difference in response will prove decisive, leading ultimately to [[Mallalieu]]'s willingness to use violence where [[Cotherstone]] would only flee.

## Consequences

The blackmail sets in motion every major plot development:

1. **The [[murder|murder of Kitely]]** (003–004): Rather than pay indefinitely, someone — the novel holds this question in suspense — kills the [[blackmail|blackmailer]]. His body is discovered in a [[the-moor|pine wood]], and the investigation begins.

2. **The exposure of the [[Wilchester]] Affair** (014–020): [[Brereton]]'s investigation, following the trail from the murder backward, uncovers the thirty-year-old fraud that [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] had threatened to reveal. The blackmail thus achieves in death what it could not in life: the truth comes out.

3. **The arrest of [[harborough|Harborough]]** (006–008): In a cruel irony, the blackmail that was meant to expose the guilty instead leads to the arrest of an innocent man — [[Harborough]], whose reclusive behaviour makes him an easy [[suspicion|suspect]].

4. **The second murder** (016): [[Stoner]], who was present when the blackmail was discussed, becomes a threat to [[Mallalieu]] and is killed on the [[the-moor|moor]]. The blackmail's consequences escalate from [[blackmail|extortion]] to double murder.

## Investigative Significance

The blackmail is the investigation's hidden centre. [[Brereton]] understands early that to solve [[murder|Kitely's murder]], he must first understand who [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] was and why anyone would want him dead. [[avice-harborough|Avice Harborough]]'s question — "Who knows anything about him?" (008-057) — is the key that unlocks the case.

The blackmail also reveals the novel's moral structure. [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] is not an innocent victim — he is a parasite who feeds on others' guilt. Yet his death is still murder, and the novel insists that even a [[blackmail|blackmailer]] is entitled to the [[justice|law]]'s protection. This refusal to simplify moral categories — victim and perpetrator, innocent and guilty — is what elevates *The Borough Treasurer* above a simple crime story.
