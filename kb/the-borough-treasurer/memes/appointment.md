# Appointment

The system of municipal office-holding that structures power in [[High-Cross|High Cross]] — the mechanism by which [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] secured their positions and the context in which their corruption flourished.

## Definition in Context

In the world of early twentieth-century English municipal governance depicted in *The Borough Treasurer*, appointments to borough offices carry significant power and prestige. [[Mallalieu]] serves as borough treasurer for the second time, wearing "the mayoral chain for the second time" (002-006). [[Cotherstone]] holds the position of town councillor. These appointments give them [[power|control]] over municipal contracts and funds — the very [[power|control]] they abused in [[Wilchester]].

The appointment system is portrayed as a double-edged institution. It enables capable men to serve their community, but it also creates opportunities for self-dealing when oversight is lax and the appointees are dishonest.

## Key Passages

The conspirators' [[past|history]] in [[Wilchester]] reveals how they exploited their appointed positions. As "treasurer and secretary" of the building society (001-050), they had "full [[power|control]]" of its funds (015-019). Their appointments gave them the access and [[power|authority]] to commit fraud, and their respectable [[honour|reputation]]s shielded them from scrutiny.

> The novel examines how municipal appointments — positions of public trust — can become instruments of private gain when held by men without integrity.

The borough's contractual system, overseen by the appointed officials, becomes a vehicle for corruption. The "contracts to go through" (002-011) that [[Mallalieu]] mentions represent both legitimate municipal business and opportunities for illicit profit.

## Character Perspectives

**[[Mallalieu]]** treats his appointment as an entitlement, his long tenure breeding complacency about oversight. **[[Cotherstone]]** sees his position as a bulwark against suspicion — surely a town councillor would not be [[suspicion|suspected]] of murder. **The community** defers to appointed officials, their respect making scrutiny unlikely. **[[Brereton]]**, as an outsider [[justice|law]]yer, is uniquely positioned to see through the facade of municipal [[class|respectability]].

## Plot Significance

The appointment system provides the institutional context for the novel's crimes. Under[[reputation|standing]] how [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] obtained and used their positions is essential to under[[reputation|standing]] both the [[Wilchester-affair|Wilchester fraud]] and [[Kitelys-murder|the murder of Kitely]]. The novel offers a critical portrait of municipal governance, suggesting that unchecked appointments create the conditions for corruption.
