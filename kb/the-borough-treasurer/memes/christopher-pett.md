# Christopher Pett

Christopher Pett, a solicitor's clerk at 23b Cursitor Street in London, is James Kitely's legal adviser and Miss Pett's nephew—a sharp-faced, calculating figure whose knowledge of Kitely's affairs makes him both a valuable witness and a dangerous adversary.

---

## Role in the Story

Christopher Pett occupies a unique position in the novel's web of relationships: he is the legal professional who handled Kitely's private business, the nephew who benefited from his aunt's connection to the murdered man, and ultimately a man whose own interests diverge from the cause of justice. He first appears as a name—"Mr. Christopher Pett, 23b Cursitor Street"—given by his aunt at the inquest [009-058]. When he arrives in Highmarket with Kitely's will, his sharp intelligence and professional manner immediately mark him as a different kind of character from the provincial figures who dominate the earlier chapters [011-001]. Brereton's assessment is swift and pointed: "Oh, I've met Mr. Christopher Pett's sort before" [012-009].

As Kitely's "legal adviser" [013-003], Christopher Pett holds documents and knowledge that are crucial to understanding both the blackmail scheme and its motivations. His later appearances—in Mallalieu's office, at the assizes, and in the novel's violent climax—reveal him as a man whose professional polish conceals more dangerous capabilities.

## Character & Traits

Christopher Pett is physically distinctive: a "sharp face" with a "glint" that suggests calculation rather than warmth [029-009]. He dresses in the uniform of the London professional class—"silk hat, black kid gloves" at the assizes [025-023]. His manner is carefully controlled; even when producing documents under pressure, there is "a certain unwillingness in Christopher Pett's manner as he slowly produced" what was required [024-036].

Brereton's instant wariness of Pett is telling. A man with experience of London's legal underworld, Brereton recognises in Pett a familiar type: the clerk who knows too much and charges too much for his silence. Mallalieu, by contrast, is initially more inclined to trust Pett's professional credentials—or at least to believe that Pett's "abilities in a certain direction" could be useful [024-061]. The narrative is careful to show both sides of Pett: the competent legal professional who "formally handed over" documents with due process [018-004], and the grasping opportunist whose "very different story" contradicts the official account [024-072].

## Key Relationships

- **Miss Pett** (aunt): Christopher Pett's aunt is Kitely's housekeeper, and it is through her that his connection to the case is first established. She describes him as "a solicitor" at the inquest, with evident pride [009-056].
- **James Kitely** (client): Pett served as Kitely's legal adviser and held his will. The exact nature of their professional relationship—and whether Pett was aware of Kitely's blackmail activities—is a matter of significant interest to investigators [013-003, 011-001].
- **Mallalieu** (acquaintance): Pett meets with Mallalieu regarding Kitely's estate and the will. Mallalieu's assessment of Pett wavers between grudging respect for his legal competence and unease at his methods [024-061].
- **Brereton** (adversary): Brereton identifies Pett as a suspicious figure from their first encounter and maintains this assessment throughout. Their dynamic is one of mutual wariness, each recognising in the other a worthy opponent [012-009].

## Key Actions

1. **Delivers Kitely's will** (Chapter XI): Pett arrives in Highmarket bearing Kitely's last will and testament, which contains provisions that shift the investigation's direction and reveal previously unknown aspects of Kitely's affairs [011-001–014].

2. **Handles Kitely's legal affairs** (Chapters XIII, XVIII): As Kitely's legal adviser, Pett is responsible for managing the dead man's estate and producing documents relevant to the investigation. His "formal" handover of papers is observed with suspicion [018-004, 013-003].

3. **Negotiates with Mallalieu** (Chapter XXIV): Pett meets with Mallalieu to discuss financial matters related to Kitely's estate. The conversation reveals Pett's commercial instincts—and his willingness to exploit his position for advantage [024-036–072].

4. **Meets a violent end** (Chapter XXIX): In the novel's climactic sequence, Pett's "sharp face" appears at a critical moment—and in the ensuing confrontation, he is killed. The "glint" of his face, seen briefly before shots are fired, is the last image of a man whose ambition overreached his safety [029-009–010].

## Involvement in the Crime

Christopher Pett's relationship to the central crime is complex. He is not directly involved in Kitely's murder, but his knowledge of Kitely's legal affairs—and by extension, Kitely's blackmail operation—places him in a position of dangerous knowledge. The novel suggests that Pett understood the value of what he knew and was prepared to profit from it. His death in the final chapters closes off one avenue of the investigation but opens another: the manner of his killing provides the final piece of evidence needed to resolve the case.

## Significance

Christopher Pett represents the London dimension of the novel's crime—the professional, impersonal, money-driven world that lies beyond Highmarket's town-council politics and neighbourly relationships. Where Cotherstone's guilt is personal and emotional, Pett's involvement is transactional: he sees an opportunity and pursues it. His character introduces the theme of professional complicity: the lawyer who enables crime not by committing it but by managing its documentary aftermath. His death is also structurally significant, providing the climactic violence that brings the novel's various threads together and forces the final revelation of truth.
