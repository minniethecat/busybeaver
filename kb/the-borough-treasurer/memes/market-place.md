# Market Place

The central public square of [[Highmarket]], where the town's principal buildings — the Town Hall, the police station, the bank, and the gentlemen's club — stand arrayed around an open space that serves as both marketplace and gathering ground. Throughout the novel, the Market Place is a stage for public scrutiny, where private disgrace becomes visible and where the townsfolk gather to watch the powerful fall.

## Geography

The Market Place sits at the heart of Highmarket's civic geography. The police-station opens onto it [030-075]; the select gentlemen's club — "having its house in the Market Place" — is one of its prominent fixtures [030-035]; Bent's solicitor's office faces across it [030-027]; and the Town Hall, with its "ancient door" and interlocking rooms, stands nearby. The Market Place functions as Highmarket's centre of gravity, pulling all public business into its orbit.

## Description in the Novel

Fletcher uses the Market Place as more than a physical location — it is the novel's arena of public exposure. When Cotherstone walks through it after his acquittal, every step is watched:

> "He went out and crossed the Market Place to Bent's office." [030-027]

The Market Place is where private character becomes public knowledge. Mallalieu is observed in his "peregrinations through the Market Place" [023-012], moving among the townspeople as a man of standing — until his arrest rewrites his place in that public order. Later, after the trial, Cotherstone passes through the same square under the gaze of the crowd:

> "Cotherstone walked steadily away through the Market Place when he left the barrister." [030-031]

And the barrister, after the verdict, walks "down the steps and across the Market Place — and behind them the crowd sneered and laughed" [030-013]. The square is the town's conscience made visible.

## Key Events Here

- **Miss Pett observed**: Mallalieu watches Miss Pett in her "peregrinations through the Market Place" [023-012], noting her skeletal thinness — a moment that foreshadows her later dominance over him on the moor
- **Cotherstone's errand to Bent**: After his acquittal, Cotherstone crosses the Market Place to Bent's solicitor's office, seeking reconciliation, but finds Bent absent [030-027]
- **The barrister's exit**: The barrister, arm in arm with Cotherstone, walks across the Market Place to champagne at the Duke's Head — with the crowd "sneering and laughing" behind them [030-013]
- **Cotherstone's solitary walk home**: Cotherstone walks "steadily away through the Market Place" alone, refusing to meet the eyes of anyone he knows [030-031]
- **The gentlemen's club**: A "small and select club, having its house in the Market Place" [030-035], where Highmarket's principal men gather — and which Cotherstone considers facing after his trial
- **The police-station**: The Market Place leads to the doors of the police-station [030-075], connecting civic space to the machinery of law

## Associated Characters

- **[[Mallalieu]]**: Observed by townsfolk as he passes through the Market Place, his public persona anchored to this civic space
- **[[Cotherstone]]**: Crosses the Market Place at moments of acute exposure — after his acquittal, walking to Bent's office, and again when walking home alone
- **[[Miss Pett]]**: Seen in her "peregrinations" through the Market Place, her presence noted by Mallalieu
- **[[Bent]]**: His solicitor's office faces the Market Place, a location that places him at the centre of the town's legal and commercial life
- **The barrister**: Walks across the Market Place to drink champagne, indifferent to public opinion

## Narrative Atmosphere

The Market Place is Fletcher's stage for the novel's theme of public exposure. Every crossing of the square is a moment of visibility — a gauntlet of observation. Cotherstone's walks through the Market Place after his acquittal are among the novel's most charged passages: he is a free man, yet every eye judges him. The crowd that "sneered and laughed" [030-013] behind the barrister, the man who "cut him dead" [030-027] when Cotherstone passed — the Market Place converts private moral judgment into public spectacle. It is, in Fletcher's handling, the conscience of Highmarket made physical.
