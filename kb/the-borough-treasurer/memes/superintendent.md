# the police superintendent

The police superintendent is the senior uniformed officer in charge of the Highmarket police force and the initial investigation into the murder of James Kitely. Though he remains unnamed throughout the novel, he appears in multiple scenes and is a distinct recurring character.

## Role in the Novel

### The Night of the Murder

When Mallalieu arrives at the police station after the discovery of Kitely's body, he finds the superintendent in consultation with the police sergeant, whom the superintendent had "summoned to hear his report" [PN=072-725]. The superintendent has laid out the evidence — the severed wire — on brown paper, "preparatory to being sealed up" [PN=072-729].

### Initial Investigation

The superintendent takes a measured approach when Mallalieu presses for immediate action. When the Mayor asks about the wire and the evidence, the superintendent replies cautiously: "Not yet, Mr. Mayor. We were just consulting as to what's best to be done" [PN=073-733]. He works methodically, giving orders to his constables and organizing patrols of the Shawl area [PN=079-795].

### Suspicions and Evidence

The superintendent is initially drawn toward the obvious suspect, asking Mallalieu directly: "You think it's Harborough, then?" [PN=073-737]. He follows the chain of evidence as it appears, from the gold found on Harborough to the wire connection.

### Professional Conduct

Throughout the investigation, the superintendent functions as a professional but not particularly imaginative police officer. He follows procedures correctly — summoning additional constables, organizing searches, and deferring appropriately to the Mayor and magistrates — but lacks the investigative creativity that Brereton and Carfax's private inquiry bring to the case [PN=086-0867].

## Significance

The superintendent represents the official, procedure-bound police response to the crime. His competent but conventional approach provides a contrast with the more dynamic unofficial investigation conducted by Brereton and Carfax's agents. The fact that he remains unnamed throughout — referred to only as "the superintendent" — underscores his role as a representative of institutional authority rather than an individual character.
