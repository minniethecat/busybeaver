## Overview

The coroner's [[trial|inquest]] into the death of [[James-Kitely|James Kitely]] is the first formal legal proceeding in *The Borough Treasurer*. Held in [[Highmarket]], the [[trial|inquest]] "extending over two sittings" (013-002) serves as a public examination of the evidence gathered by police and by [[Brereton]]'s parallel investigation. It is here that the tension between private knowledge and public [[evidence|proof]] becomes most acute — those who know the truth cannot speak it, and those who speak do not know the truth.

## The First Sitting

### Evidence Presented

The first sitting of the [[trial|inquest]] hears the initial evidence: the [[discovery-of-the-body|discovery of Kitely]]'s body in [[pine-wood|the Pine Wood]], the cord used to strangle him (traced to [[harborough|Harborough]]'s coil), the emptied pocketbook found beside the body, and [[the-bank|the bank]]-manager's [[evidence|testimony]] about the gold [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] was carrying.

The evidence points overwhelmingly to [[harborough|Harborough]] — a circumstantial case that seems impregnable.

### The Atmosphere of the Court

Fletcher captures the charged atmosphere of the [[trial|inquest]] room, where the entire community gathers:

> "The whole town and neighbourhood were astir with excitement." [009-003]

For [[Highmarket]], the [[trial|inquest]] is not merely a legal proceeding but a social event — the respectable citizens watching to see how their own [[class|respectability]] might be implicated.

### Brereton's Strategy

At the first sitting, [[Brereton]]'s strategy is not to prove [[harborough|Harborough]]'s innocence but to create doubt. He introduces the possibility of [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]]'s involvement, deliberately diverting suspicion:

> "Brereton knew that nothing is so useful in the breaking-down of one prejudice as to set up another." [009-078]

His cross-examination of [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] plants the seed of alternative possibility — enough to unsettle the certainty of the police case without yet revealing what he has discovered.

## The Adjournment

The [[trial|inquest]] is adjourned for a week, during which [[Brereton]] intensifies his investigation. The adjournment is crucial to the plot: it gives [[Brereton]] time to travel to [[Wilchester]], uncover the trial records, and piece together the truth about [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]].

## The Second Sitting: Stoner's Inquest

### A New Death

By the time the [[trial|inquest]] reconvenes, a second death has occurred — that of [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]], the clerk who had independently discovered the [[Wilchester]] secret. The [[trial|inquest]] into [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]]'s death runs parallel to the resumed [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] [[trial|inquest]], creating a layered legal proceeding that reflects the accumulating complexity of the case.

### The Verdict

The [[trial|inquest]] on [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]] concludes "with a verdict" as "the dusk of the afternoon" fell (025-026). The timing is symbolically apt — the verdict arrives as darkness gathers, mirroring the moral darkness at the heart of the case.

## The Gap Between Knowledge and Proof

The [[trial|inquest]] scenes dramatise the novel's central concern with the relationship between truth and legal process. Several [[reputation|character]]s know more than they can say:

| Character | What They Know | Why They Cannot Speak |
|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| [[Mallalieu]] | He killed [[James-Kitely|Kitely]] and [[herbert-stoner|Stoner]] | Self-preservation |
| [[Cotherstone]] | [[Mallalieu]]'s guilt, the [[Wilchester]] past | Self-preservation, fear of [[Mallalieu]] |
| [[Brereton]] | [[harborough|Harborough]] is innocent; the partners are guilty | Needs [[evidence|proof]], not just conviction |
| [[miss-pett|Miss Pett]] | [[harborough|Harborough]] is innocent | Protecting her own [[secret|secrets]] |
| [[harborough|Harborough]] | His own alibi | Refuses to reveal it |

The [[trial|inquest]] exposes the structural limitation of legal inquiry: it can only process what witnesses are willing to say and what evidence can be physically presented. The deeper truth — the thirty-year-old fraud, the assumed identities, the systematic deception — lies beyond the [[trial|inquest]]'s reach.

## The Inquest and Community

For [[Highmarket]], the [[trial|inquest]] is a civic drama. The "crowded [[trial|court]]" [009-078] represents the community's investment in the outcome. The [[trial|inquest]] transforms private crime into public spectacle — and in doing so, begins the process of stripping away the [[class|respectability]] that [[Mallalieu]] and [[Cotherstone]] have so carefully constructed.
