# Navajo Prayer

Navajo prayer is not petitionary in the Western sense — it is an act of
participation in beauty. Through song, chant, and ritual utterance, the Navajo
speaker aligns himself with the order of the universe, invoking the [[deity]]
figures of dawn, mountains, and rain. In *Laughing Boy*, prayer is inseparable
from daily life: it frames the morning, seals a marriage, rides with the
horseman, and culminates in the great ceremonies like the [[mountain-chant-ceremony]].
The novel's recurring phrase — "travelling in beauty" — is itself a prayer,
the essence of [[hozoji]] and the ideal toward which all of Navajo life is directed.

## Definition

In Navajo practice, prayer takes three primary forms. **Personal prayer** is
silent or softly spoken, offered at dawn or in moments of solitude — an
individual act of alignment. **Ritual prayer** is chanted by a Singer at
ceremonies, following fixed formulas passed down through generations. **Song
prayer** merges music and words, performed at dances, races, and social
gatherings. Common to all three is the assumption that correctly spoken words,
offered with pure intention, have genuine power to shape reality.

> He made a very brief prayer, and patted his pony's neck. "Little sister, we
> must win. Do not fail me."
>
> （004-085）

Even the briefest prayer — a horseman asking his pony for speed — participates
in this worldview. The words are not casual; they are a compact between the
speaker and the animate world.

## Cultural Context

Prayer in Navajo religion is the mechanism through which humans maintain
relationship with the Holy People. The great ceremonies — [[mountain-chant-ceremony]],
[[night-chant]], and others — are structured around cycles of prayer and song
that may last nine or ten nights. The Singer who leads these ceremonies carries
an immense body of memorized prayer formulas, each one precise and inviolable.

> She marvelled at the men's endurance; they had periods of rest, but there were
> night vigils, and for Mountain Singer, endless preparatory prayers. He did not
> seem tired; rather one would say that he drew rest and strength from his songs.
>
> （012-044）

The Dawn Prayer holds a special place. Each morning, the Navajo rises to greet
the sun with words of beauty and gratitude — an act that sets the day's
spiritual orientation:

> He woke to a feeling of expectation, and made his Dawn Prayer with all the
> gladness that his religion prescribed.
>
> （003-042）

Prayer also accompanies transitions: the House Prayer blesses a new dwelling
(013-099), the marriage prayer sanctifies a union (007-053), and the
prayer-sticks mentioned in passing (011-005) are physical offerings that carry
words to the Holy People.

## In the Novel

Laughing Boy is a man of instinctive prayer. His inner life is punctuated by
moments of direct address to the divine:

> Laughing Boy breathed his prayer to himself, feeling a moment of loneliness,
> "Dawn Boy, Chief …"
>
> （001-051）

He aspires to deeper knowledge. Watching the singers at a dance, he thinks:

> Just like the prayer, "travelling in beauty." It would be good to be a singer
> as well, to express all these things through the prayers. He would like to know
> many of them, to learn to conduct the Mountain Chant.
>
> （001-021）

At his wedding, prayer formalizes the union. [[yellow-singer]] officiates,
dividing the mush in four directions and praying over the couple. Then Laughing
Boy himself must sing:

> Now it was Laughing Boy's turn to make a prayer. He sang the prayer to House
> God with solemn emphasis.
>
> （007-053）

The prayer closes with the quintessential Navajo formula:

> "In beauty it is finished, In beauty it is finished."
>
> （007-054）

For Slim Girl, prayer is more complicated. Her American education has distanced
her from Navajo belief. Yet at the Mountain Chant, she finds herself moved:

> The long and repetitious prayers were often monotonous, chanted to dull, heavy
> music, but in the worst of them there were flashes of poetic feeling. Her
> American training would dismiss it for superstition, but she was not so sure.
>
> （012-024）

In their happiest moments, prayer and song merge with daily joy:

> "It would be good to sing, I think. Let us sing some very beautiful prayers
> together. Everything is good now."
>
> （007-086）

## Prayer Forms in the Novel

| Form | Description | Example in Novel |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Dawn Prayer | Morning greeting of the sun, affirming hozoji | "He made his Dawn Prayer with all the gladness that his religion prescribed" (003-042) |
| Personal Prayer | Silent or whispered address to a deity | Laughing Boy to Dawn Boy (001-051); brief prayer before race (004-085) |
| Marriage Prayer | Ceremonial prayer over couple with corn mush | Yellow Singer's prayer at the wedding (007-053) |
| House Prayer | Blessing a new dwelling | "We are going to make the House Prayer" (013-099) |
