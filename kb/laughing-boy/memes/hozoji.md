# Hozoji (Walking in Beauty)

The Navajo concept of *hozoji* (also rendered *hojoni* or *bik'é hojoni*) — literally "the trail of beauty" or "walking in beauty" — is the spiritual ideal of living in harmony with the natural and supernatural world, expressed through right action, crafted beauty, song, and prayer.

## Definition

*Hozoji nashad* — "travelling in beauty" — describes a state of perfect integration where body, spirit, and world are aligned. Laughing Boy experiences this as he rides to Tsé Lani composing a song and bracelet design simultaneously: "his hands, his feet, his head, his insides all were hozoji, all were very beautiful" (001-002). The concept encompasses physical beauty (the crafted bracelet, the well-kept pony), spiritual beauty (the song, the prayer), and moral order (right relations with people and gods).

## Cultural Context

*Hozoji* is the organizing principle of Navajo religious practice. It is invoked in the Mountain Chant ceremony and embedded in the prayers Laughing Boy aspires to learn: to "conduct the Mountain Chant, and know all the beautiful stories behind the songs and ceremonies" (001-021). The phrase *bik'é hojoni* — "the trail of beauty" — is one of the "oft-repeated phrases" Slim Girl hopes Laughing Boy will teach her, through which "she would learn the content" of Navajo spiritual life (005-004).

The Night Chant includes the prayer lines: "Now with a god I walk, Now I step across the summits of the mountains, Now with a god I walk, Striding across the foothills. Now on the old age trail, now on the path of beauty wandering. In beauty — Hozoji, hozoji, hozoji" (003-038). This prayer directly links physical movement through the landscape with spiritual union — the walker on the path of beauty walks with gods.

## In the Novel

*Hozoji* is the standard against which characters measure their lives. When Laughing Boy is in spiritual alignment — riding with the song flowing, silverwork in mind, pony beneath him — he is *hozoji*. After his encounter with Slim Girl disrupts his peace, he becomes "no longer on the path of beauty, yet tormented by a new beauty" (003-040). This tension between two kinds of beauty — the traditional spiritual path and the disruptive beauty of romantic love — drives the novel's central conflict.

Slim Girl herself recognizes *hozoji* as the key to Laughing Boy's character and to her own project of reintegration into Navajo life. She sees him as "all Navajo, even to his faults" who "would teach her the meaning" of the traditional phrases and the life they describe (005-004). Through him, she seeks to return to the path of beauty she lost through her American schooling.

## Related Practices

- **Singing and prayer** — Songs are vehicles of *hozoji*; Laughing Boy's traveling songs and ceremonial singing express and sustain the state of beauty (001-002, 001-021)
- **Silversmithing** — Creating beautiful objects in silver and turquoise is a material expression of *hozoji*: "to work in silver and turquoise... and lead the Mountain Chant" (001-021)
- **The Mountain Chant** — The major Navajo ceremony that Laughing Boy aspires to learn, containing the prayers and songs that embody *hozoji* (001-021, 003-038)
