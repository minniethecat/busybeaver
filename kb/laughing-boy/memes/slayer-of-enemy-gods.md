# Slayer of Enemy Gods (Nayeinezgani)

Slayer of Enemy Gods (Navajo: Nayeinezgani, also called Monster Slayer) is one
of the Hero Twins — the warrior sons of Changing Woman who, in Navajo origin
narratives, journeyed across the land to kill the monsters that preyed upon the
people. With his brother Child of the Waters, he passed through the Gate of the
Clashing Rocks, crossed Boiling Sands, and slew Monster Eagle, Monster Elk, and
Big God. In *Laughing Boy*, the myth of Slayer of Enemy Gods becomes a personal
lens through which Slim Girl understands her husband — first as a figure of awe,
then as an intimate name, and finally as a recognition that even a godlike
protector cannot kill every enemy.

## Identity

In Navajo tradition, Slayer of Enemy Gods and his twin brother Child of the
Waters were born to Changing Woman (Estsanatlehi) after her miraculous
impregnation by the Sun. The twins grew with supernatural speed and set out to
rid the world of the monsters — enormous beings that devoured humans. Their
weapons are lightning arrows, and they travel on cloud blankets. After
completing their mission, they took their place among the Holy People (yei).

The Navajo name Nayeinezgani appears once in the novel, in Laughing Boy's cry at
the burial scene — "Nayeinezgani!" (020-007) — a moment when the mythic warrior
is invoked directly. Elsewhere, the English translation "Slayer of Enemy Gods"
is used.

## Domain

Slayer of Enemy Gods governs war and protection. He is the archetype of the
warrior who fights on behalf of the people — not for conquest but for
elimination of threats. His story is part of the "Coming Up" narrative cycle,
which Laughing Boy loves best among all the traditional stories:

> He noticed that Slim Girl listened intently. Some day he would be telling his
> children. It seemed a long time for them not to have had any, but he really
> did not know very much about these things. It was the woman's business; the
> children were hers, after all.
>
> （014-075）

## In Navajo Religion

Slayer of Enemy Gods is invoked in the Night Chant (Nightway) ceremony, where
masked dancers representing the Holy People enter the firelight to heal and
bless. His story is recounted by elders as part of the transmission of Navajo
cosmology to the young. Laughing Boy's host recounts "the second part of the
Coming Up story to his children, the part about the Twin Gods, Slayer of Enemy
Gods, and Child of the Waters" (014-074), and the tale soothes the listeners on
a hot, close evening.

A key episode in the Slayer of Enemy Gods cycle concerns the Hunger People:

> "Slayer of Enemy Gods came to the Hunger People, they say… He said, 'Now I am
> going to kill you, because you are bad for my people.' Hunger Chief said, 'If
> you kill us, nobody will be hungry any more, nobody will care about feasting.
> They will not go out and hunt for good food, venison, and fat prairie-dog.
> Nobody will want to go hunting,' he said," they say.
>
> "Slayer of Enemy Gods said, 'Then I shall let you live,' they say."
>
> （007-097, 007-098）

This story — in which the warrior god spares his enemy after recognizing the
necessity of hunger — becomes crucial to Slim Girl's understanding of her own
situation. Hunger, in the myth's allegorical logic, is what drives people to
hunt, to feast, to value food. Without it, life would lose its savor.

## In the Novel

The name "Slayer of Enemy Gods" enters the novel's intimate vocabulary through a
song. The novel opens with Laughing Boy singing as he rides:

> Now Slayer of Enemy Gods, alone I see him coming;
> Alone I see him coming, now Slayer of Enemy Gods.
>
> （001-001）

Slim Girl hears this song and makes a private association: "That is he, she
thought, Slayer of Enemy Gods. He would be shocked to hear me say it, to hear a
woman sing that song" (007-089). A woman singing a war god's song is a
transgression in Navajo tradition, and her silent appropriation of the name
marks both her intimacy with Laughing Boy and her distance from orthodox
practice.

The Hunger People story resonates with Slim Girl on a personal level. Her
American education has left her, in effect, with the Hunger People — the desires
and ambitions she cannot kill:

> She was becoming drowsy and making pictures. There was a story she remembered
> faintly, how Nayeinezgani did not kill the Hunger People. An allegory; her
> Slayer of Enemy Gods could not kill them, either. She could do away with them.
>
> （012-091）

The allegory is double-edged. Slim Girl recognizes that Laughing Boy — her
Slayer of Enemy Gods — cannot destroy the complex of American influence, desire,
and ambition that she carries within herself. Only she can "do away with them."

The name reaches its fullest expression in the reconciliation scene. After
Laughing Boy has defended her against the Pah-Ute who shot her, Slim Girl
speaks:

> "I shall be happy with you anywhere that you wish to take me. As you have
> said, there is nothing between us now. You have made up to me, and revenged
> me, for everything the Americans have done to me, My Slayer of Enemy Gods."
>
> （019-085）

"*My* Slayer of Enemy Gods" — the possessive pronoun completes the
transformation of myth into personal devotion. He is not the people's warrior
here; he is hers.

But the novel's wisdom is that mortal men are not gods. After Slim Girl's death,
Laughing Boy reflects:

> "Slayer of Enemy Gods" she called me. But Slayer of Enemy Gods spared the Cold
> Woman and Old Age Woman and Poverty People and Hunger People. She tried to
> kill the Hunger People; I thought she could. If we had not tried to do that,
> we should have been living happily within the Navajo country long ago. She was
> too daring, she wanted perfection.
>
> （020-021）

The myth, in the end, teaches limits. Slayer of Enemy Gods spared certain
enemies — Cold, Old Age, Poverty, Hunger — because they are necessary to human
life. Slim Girl, "too daring," could not accept that there are enemies one must
live with. Her Slayer of Enemy Gods could protect her from a gunman but not from
the ambition that drove her.
