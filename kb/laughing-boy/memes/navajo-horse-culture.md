# Navajo Horse Culture

The horse is central to Navajo life in *Laughing Boy* — not merely a means of transport but a measure of wealth, a vehicle for personal expression, and the focus of the competitive racing culture that defines masculine identity and social standing.

## Definition

In the Navajo world of the novel, the horse is an extension of the person. Laughing Boy rides the hundred miles from T'o Tlakai to Tsé Lani "to attend a dance, or rather, for the horse-racing that would come afterwards" (001-001). The pony's quality reflects the owner's status: wealthy men own "over five hundred head of horses" (001-012), while Laughing Boy has traded two ponies for his single mare — "she was tough, as a horse had to be to live at all in that country" (001-020). The relationship between Navajo and horse is intimate and constant: "All the time he was passing his hand along the pony's neck, along her back, feeling the lines of tough muscles" (001-022).

## Cultural Context

Horse racing is the central social event alongside the dance. Young men "bunched together — a line of jingling bridles, dark, excited faces" (001-004), their identity defined by their mounts and their riding skill. Betting on races is both entertainment and serious business: belts, bow-guards, and money change hands on race outcomes. Laughing Boy bets and wins, thinking "when he rode his horse in the races, people would see; he would ride past the people, back to T'o Tlakai, with all his winnings" (001-052).

Horses are also a medium for demonstrating personal character. Laughing Boy's tender care of his mare — "He tended his pony minutely. The little mare had had two days of loping; shortly he wanted to race her; three days of rest would not be too much" (001-020) — shows the virtues of patience and attentiveness. The comparison of Slim Girl to a pony — "she was like an arrow notched to a taut bowstring — a movement and a preparedness" (001-020) — reveals how deeply equine metaphors structure Navajo perception.

The horse is bound up with the song, the ride, and the state of *hozoji*. Riding is the context for Laughing Boy's song-making and bracelet-designing — the horse's lope sets the rhythm for both music and thought.

## In the Novel

Horse racing frames the novel's opening. The Tsé Lani gathering is as much about racing as dancing, and Laughing Boy's victory in a race establishes his competence and standing (001-017). Throughout the novel, horses carry characters between worlds: from T'o Tlakai to Tsé Lani, from the Navajo country to American towns, from the hidden hogan to the final tragic confrontation.

The racing scenes in Chapters II and IV depict the intense competition and communal excitement of the sport. Straight Fingers' victory in the big race — "Ya-hai! E-ya-hai! Ei-yei! Straight Fingers had it. Straight Fingers galloped for the line" (004-075) — captures the visceral drama of the event.

## Related Practices

- **Racing** — Organized and impromptu races with betting; the central competitive sport at Navajo gatherings (001-017, 004-075)
- **Pony trading** — Horses are traded, bet, and valued; Laughing Boy traded two horses for his mare (001-020)
- **Traveling songs** — Composed and sung while riding; the horse's gait provides the rhythm (001-001, 001-002)
- **Horse care** — Meticulous attention to the pony before racing; rest, inspection, and bonding are part of the culture (001-020, 001-022)
