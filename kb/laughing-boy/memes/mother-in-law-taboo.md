# Mother-in-Law Taboo

The Navajo prohibition against a man speaking to, looking directly at, or being in close physical proximity to his mother-in-law — one of the strictest avoidance customs in Navajo kinship, whose violation is believed to cause physical illness.

## Definition

The mother-in-law taboo forbids a Navajo man from direct interaction with his wife's mother. He may not speak to her, look at her face, or share close space with her. The prohibition is absolute and carries supernatural sanction: violation is believed to cause illness. When Laughing Boy's peers discuss a man who transgressed: "He looked at his mother-in-law; he spoke to her, they say" (010-045), the implication is shocking — and the consequence follows: "now he has spoken to his mother-in-law, they say. So he has a bad toothache" (010-049). The toothache is understood as direct supernatural punishment for the breach.

## Cultural Context

Mother-in-law avoidance is one of several Navajo kinship regulations that structure social interaction. It functions alongside the joking relationship between brothers-in-law (who may tease and use the otherwise-rude term "brother-in-law" freely) as part of a system of prescribed and prohibited behaviors that regulate extended family relations.

The taboo reflects the central role of matrilocal residence patterns and the potential tension between a man and his wife's natal family. By preventing any direct interaction, the taboo eliminates the possibility of conflict while preserving the relationship through the mediating figure of the wife.

## In the Novel

The mother-in-law taboo appears in the campfire conversation of Chapter X, when Navajo men discuss local news and moral violations. The story of a man who "looked at his mother-in-law" and consequently "has a bad toothache" (010-045, 010-049) is told as a cautionary tale — both a warning and a piece of community gossip that reinforces social norms through supernatural sanction.

The taboo's presence in the novel, though brief, establishes the depth of Navajo social regulation that Laughing Boy and Slim Girl navigate. It also connects to the broader theme of proper and improper relations: Slim Girl's American education has distanced her from these customs, while Laughing Boy embodies their continued force.

## Related Practices

- **Kinship avoidance** — Beyond the mother-in-law, similar avoidances govern relations with certain categories of relatives
- **Joking relationship** — The complementary custom: brothers-in-law may use the otherwise-rude term of address as a form of permitted intimacy (002-069)
- **Supernatural sanction** — Taboo violations result in physical illness; toothache, in the novel's example, is directly attributed to the breach (010-049)
