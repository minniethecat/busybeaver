# Los Palos

Los Palos is the American town that serves as the economic anchor of the
southern Navajo country in the novel. It is a contact zone — the place where
Navajo silver, blankets, and horses enter the American market, and where
American money, goods, and values penetrate Navajo life. For [[slim-girl]],
Los Palos is a place of employment and strategic opportunity; for
[[laughing-boy]], it is an alien world whose power he only dimly perceives.

## Geography and Character

The town is vividly sketched as a typical Southwestern border settlement:

> The town of Los Palos shimmered in the heat. A lot of adobe houses and frame
> shacks pushed carelessly together were beaten down by the sun. Behind them was
> a strip of irrigated green like a backdrop, alfalfa, corn, beans, cottonwoods,
> a mile long and a few hundred yards wide.
>
> （006-063）

The contrast between the "wretched 'dobes and stick hovels on the outskirts"
(007-002) and the irrigated green strip captures the economic inequality that
structures the town. On its margins live Navajos like Slim Girl; at its center
are the trading post, the tourist company, and the missionary's house — American
institutions that shape Navajo lives.

## In the Novel

Los Palos functions as an economic hub. A tourist company based there buys
horses from Laughing Boy and Slim Girl, though "they often got fearful cat-meat"
(010-004) from less scrupulous sellers. The trading post passes on orders for
Slim Girl's [[navajo-blankets|blankets]]:

> Her weaving was winning Laughing Boy's unstinted praise, and, to her surprise
> and great satisfaction, becoming a source of income. The trading post in Los
> Palos would pass on to her occasional orders from tourists or people in the East.
>
> （015-026）

The town is also where Slim Girl maintains her connection to the American world.
She walks in wearing her American clothes, works for the missionary's wife
(implied to be a cover for less respectable activities), and returns to her
Navajo identity at home. Los Palos makes the double life possible — and
ultimately makes it unsustainable.

Laughing Boy's view of Los Palos darkens as the novel progresses. In a moment
of bitter reflection, he thinks:

> The town of Los Palos in the drenching sunlight, quiet, dead-looking beside its
> irrigated fields. What was it? Something in the air, something that perverted
> the world.
>
> （016-065）

## Significance

Los Palos represents the American economic system that both enables and
corrupts. It provides the silver Laughing Boy works, the orders for Slim Girl's
blankets, and the cash that funds their life. But it is also the source of the
"badness" that clings to Slim Girl's reputation — the town where her American
connections, her whiskey, and her secrets reside. The novel's geography maps
its moral landscape: [[tse-lani]] is tradition and authenticity, [[chiziai]]
is the compromised borderland, and Los Palos is the American world whose
proximity makes the traditional Navajo life increasingly difficult to sustain.
