# Sweat-Bath Purification

The Navajo sweat-bath — a purification ritual employing heated stones, water, and steam inside an enclosed structure — performed for physical and spiritual cleansing before significant undertakings, healing ceremonies, and new beginnings.

## Definition

The sweat-bath (also called sweat-lodge) is a ritual purification practice central to Navajo ceremonial life. It involves building a small enclosed structure, heating stones in a fire, placing them inside, and pouring water over them to create steam. Participants enter the structure to sweat, purifying body and spirit. The practice is accompanied by specific songs and ritual actions. Slim Girl insists on the sweat-bath before beginning their new life together: "before we do anything, we must make a sweat-bath. This is all different, here. We are starting off new. We must make a sweat-bath and sing" (008-012).

## Cultural Context

The sweat-bath is not merely physical cleansing but spiritual preparation. It is required before undertaking significant events — Slim Girl demands it before the consummation of her marriage to Laughing Boy — and is part of the healing arsenal of the singer. When Laughing Boy feels spiritually afflicted, "he took a sweat-bath, sang, and tried a fast" (016-011), placing the sweat-bath alongside song and fasting as core spiritual technologies.

The ritual involves specific construction. Laughing Boy "built his sweat-lodge" from available materials, and "since it was hard to get mud out of the frozen ground, covered it with blankets" (021-050), showing the practice adapts to circumstance while retaining its essential form. Slim Girl had to be taught "the ritual and song of purification" because her American schooling had deprived her of this knowledge, but "with faint childhood memories to aid her, she was quick to learn" (008-024).

After the steam bath, participants wash with water and yucca-suds, then rest: "After the steam bath and the water and the foaming yucca-suds, it was good to lie with hair spread" (008-025), completing the purification cycle.

## In the Novel

The sweat-bath appears at three crucial moments. First, when Slim Girl and Laughing Boy establish their marriage at the hidden hogan, she insists on the ritual as a way of consecrating their new beginning and reclaiming Navajo practice (008-012). Second, when Laughing Boy is troubled by spiritual unease, he takes a sweat-bath as part of his self-diagnosis and attempted cure (016-011). Third, in the novel's tragic final section, he builds a sweat-lodge alone in winter, struggling with frozen ground — the difficulty of the construction and his solitary performance underscore his spiritual isolation (021-050).

These three instances trace an arc: from Slim Girl's hopeful embrace of tradition, through Laughing Boy's troubled attempts at self-healing, to his final solitary ritual.

## Related Practices

- **Sweat-lodge construction** — Building the enclosed structure from available materials; Laughing Boy covers his with blankets when mud is frozen (021-050)
- **Yucca-suds washing** — After the sweat, washing with yucca root foam completes the purification (008-025)
- **Purification songs** — The sweat-bath is accompanied by specific ritual songs that Slim Girl must learn (008-024)
- **Fasting** — Combined with sweat-bath and singing as a spiritual diagnostic and healing practice (016-011)
