# Talking God (Hastyealtqaye)

Talking God (Navajo: Hastyealtqaye or Hashchʼéłtiʼí) is one of the principal
Holy People (yei) who appear in the Night Chant ceremony. His distinctive role
is to call from the four directions — "Wu, wu, wu, wu!" — announcing the
approach of the divine company to the ceremonial ground. In *Laughing Boy*,
Talking God features in a dream-vision that dramatizes the tension between
spiritual devotion and mortal frailty, and in the novel's closing ceremonial
scene where the gods return among dancers and sacred songs.

## Identity

Talking God is the herald of the Holy People. In Night Chant ceremonies, his
masked dancer calls four times — from the east, south, west, and north — each
call growing nearer until, with the fourth cry, the gods enter the circle of
firelight. He stands "on a bent rainbow, in company with many other Divine Ones"
(007-099). The mask is a painted rawhide bag trimmed with spruce and feathers,
and the dancer who wears it becomes, for the duration of the ceremony, the god
himself.

Among the yei, Talking God is associated with sacred speech — the power of the
voice to summon, to announce, and to make present that which is distant. His
fourfold call is both a spatial journey (across the four directions) and a
spiritual one (from the realm of the gods into the human circle).

## Domain

Talking God governs communication between the divine and human worlds. He is not
a warrior like Slayer of Enemy Gods, nor a healer — his domain is the threshold,
the moment of arrival, the announcement that makes ceremony possible. Without
his call, the other Holy People cannot enter; he is the necessary precursor to
all that follows in the Night Chant.

The novel mentions sacred geography connected to Talking God: "Yota Kien. Some
of the Divine Ones live there, they say. The two brothers came here when they
were looking for Talking God, they say" (011-011). This passing reference places
Talking God within the broader landscape of Navajo sacred sites — the brothers
(perhaps the Hero Twins) seeking him out as part of the mythic geography.

## In Navajo Religion

Talking God is most prominently associated with the Night Chant (Nightway)
ceremony, a nine-night healing ritual in which masked dancers portray the Holy
People. His call — from distant darkness to the firelight — structures the
ceremony's climactic moments. The initiates who have been through the Night
Chant know that "the gods were no more than men in masks" (012-046), yet the
experience of hearing the call and seeing the masked figures enter remains
powerful even for those who understand the mechanism.

> When she had been a very little girl, she had trembled with terror and awe at
> the sight of the very gods coming into the circle of people. Out in the
> darkness one heard their distant call, repeated as they came nearer, until
> with the fourth cry they entered the firelight. They danced and sang there,
> majestic and strange; then they vanished again to return to their homes in the
> sacred places.
>
> （012-027）

This passage, narrated from Slim Girl's childhood memory, captures the
phenomenological power of the ceremony — even for someone who, as an adult, has
lost her faith. The fourfold call of Talking God structures the experience: the
approach, the suspense, the arrival.

## In the Novel

Talking God appears most vividly in a dream-vision that Laughing Boy experiences
after the storytelling evening:

> He heard Talking God call in the distance, "Wu, wu, wu, wu!" Then the god
> called again, nearer. He knew he was asleep, and tried to wake, to meet the
> god coming. He was terrified lest he should be asleep when the god called for
> the fourth time. He had an idea that he had put himself asleep deliberately,
> against the god's will. The call sounded for a third time, just outside the
> hogan. Talking God was out there, standing on a bent rainbow, in company with
> many other Divine Ones. He had shut himself up in a pitch-black place with no
> entrance; he had locked himself away from the gods under a blanket of dark
> clouds.
>
> （007-099）

The dream dramatizes the central spiritual tension of the novel: the desire to
meet the divine versus the fear of being found unworthy. Laughing Boy, from whom
we expect unwavering faith, experiences terror and self-doubt in the presence of
the god — he fears he will "be asleep when the god called for the fourth time,"
missing the moment of revelation. The "blanket of dark clouds" under which he
hides is both a refuge and a prison.

For Slim Girl, the adult perspective on Talking God is more skeptical. Watching
the ceremony, she slips into the thought that "these worshippers were pretending
to be taken in by the patently absurd":

> Most of the adult spectators had been through the Night Chant initiation; all
> of them knew that the gods were no more than men in masks; how could they be
> so reverent? What was her devout husband's ecstasy, or his devoutness, when he
> himself put on the painted rawhide bag trimmed with spruce and feathers,
> pretending to be Talking God?
>
> （012-046）

The question is not answered, only posed. Slim Girl cannot access the faith that
makes the mask more than a mask, but the novel does not endorse her skepticism
either — it simply records the distance between two ways of seeing.

In the novel's closing pages, Talking God returns in a scene of restoration:

> Talking God and South God and Young Goddess came before them with dancers, and
> all the place was full of sacred songs. They were leading good dances, with
> good music.
>
> （021-073）

This final appearance — Talking God accompanied by other Holy People, the "place
full of sacred songs" — suggests the restoration of ceremonial order after the
disruption of Slim Girl's death. The god who called from the darkness now leads
dances in the light.
