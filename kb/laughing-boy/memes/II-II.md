[002-036] Many visitors were at the hogans scattered about [[tse-lani|Tsé Lani]]. There was much food and much talk. Where they went, they reclined on sheepskins, while two small naked boys brought ears of corn as they were roasted, and calm women set broiled goats’ ribs and corn bread before them. They ate at leisure, having a pleasant feeling of being at a party, yet at ease, and enjoying their appetites. Gossip was exchanged; they discussed crops, sheep, rain, and horses.

[002-037] “I hear you have a horse to race,” a man said to [[laughing-boy|Laughing Boy]].

[002-038] “Yes, I have a good one.”

[002-039] “A man brought a tall bay over from Tsézhin; it is very fast, they say.”

[002-040] “We shall see. I shall bet on my horse.”

[002-041] “Where did you get your bow-guard?”

[002-042] “I made it.”

[002-043] “I’ll give you six dollars for it.”

[002-044] “I don’t want to sell it.”

[002-045] The man changed the subject. “Did you hear about Red Goat? His wives put his saddle outside the door, they say.”

[002-046] “What had he done?” somebody asked.

[002-047] “He drank whiskey; he spent their money on it, so they say.”

[002-048] “They were right, I think.”

[002-049] “I have never tasted whiskey,” Laughing Boy said; “what is it like?”

[002-050] “It tastes bad, but one feels good. Then later one has a headache.”

[002-051] “It sounds like *t’oghlepai*.”

[002-052] “It is stronger. I’ll give you eight dollars for that bow-guard.”

[002-053] “I don’t want to sell it; it is lucky.”

[002-054] “That turquoise is no good, and the work is not very good.”

[002-055] Laughing Boy looked bored. “Give me a smoke, Grandfather.”

[002-056] “The turquoise is too green. Eight dollars is a lot.”

[002-057] “Eight dollars is nothing,” he answered loftily, with a pleasant remembrance of his winnings.

[002-058] “Here, I have nine-fifty. That is all I have.” The man held out the money.

[002-059] “No, I really do not want to sell. I would not sell it for a horse.”

[002-060] “It is a fine bow-guard. If you make many things like that, you will get rich.”

[002-061] Everything was well, Laughing Boy thought. He had money now, and a belt that was ugly, but could be sold to a trader for fifty dollars. People praised his work. That girl was only an incident; one should not let oneself be ruffled so easily.

[002-062] It was good to lie in the sand talking a little, borrowing smokes now and then. Now that he had money, he would buy tobacco when he came to a trading post. Meantime he thought he would hunt up those two Americans to see if they would give him one of their big, white cigarettes. Perhaps they would buy his belt; they were travelling just for fun, people said; they must be rich. Perhaps, too, they would have sweet food, canned goods, and coffee with much sugar in it. He called his friend.

[002-063] “Let us see if those Americans will buy my belt. Let us see what they will give us.”

[002-064] “Good.”

[002-065] They rode off sitting sideways on Jesting Squaw’s Son’s unsaddled horse, heels drumming softly on opposite sides, humming a song together.
