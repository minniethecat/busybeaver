# Navajo Joking Relationship

The Navajo custom of the joking relationship, particularly between brothers-in-law (*shadani*), in which otherwise-impolite forms of address and teasing are not merely permitted but expected — a structured release valve in a highly regulated kinship system.

## Definition

In Navajo kinship, calling a man "brother-in-law" directly is normally rude: "It is rude to call a man brother-in-law, and like most Navajos, he enjoyed using the term, and teaching it, to innocent foreigners" (002-069). However, between actual brothers-in-law, the term becomes a vehicle for permitted intimacy and humor. The joking relationship is the structural complement to the mother-in-law avoidance taboo: where one relationship demands total prohibition of direct interaction, the other demands playful transgression.

Laughing Boy and his brothers-in-law use the term constantly — "Nashto, shadani — give me a smoke, brother-in-law" (002-068), "Match, brother-in-law" (013-051) — as a casual greeting that simultaneously acknowledges and playfully subverts the kinship bond.

## Cultural Context

The joking relationship functions within a broader system of Navajo kinship regulation. It permits the release of tensions that might otherwise build up between men linked by marriage rather than blood. By requiring humor and forbidding genuine offense, the custom transforms a potentially fraught relationship into one of easy camaraderie.

The custom extends to teaching the term to outsiders. Laughing Boy and his companions find amusement in having Americans call them "brother-in-law," precisely because the Americans do not understand the transgressive nature of the address (002-069). This inversion — making the outsider unknowingly perform an insider transgression — is a form of quiet Navajo humor at the expense of the Americans who fail to understand the culture they interact with.

## In the Novel

The joking relationship appears most prominently in the interactions between Laughing Boy, Jesting Squaw's Son, and their circle of male companions. These men ride together, gamble together, and address each other with the easy familiarity of the *shadani* bond. When Jesting Squaw's Son calls out "E-ya, Grandfather, are you going to dance with the horse? Food is ready" (001-023), the mock-grandfather address and the absurd suggestion of dancing with a horse are forms of the permitted teasing that structures Navajo male friendship.

The custom also appears in darker contexts. When Laughing Boy's friends judge his behavior with Slim Girl, their joking takes on an edge of social control: "Jesting Squaw's Son, his brother, and Bay Horse, his brother-in-law, both evidently thought he had made a fool of himself" (012-001). The joking relationship carries the weight of community judgment.

## Related Practices

- **Mother-in-law avoidance** — The complementary kinship regulation: one relationship requires total avoidance, the other requires licensed intimacy (010-045, 010-049)
- **Mock-grandfather address** — Calling a peer "Grandfather" as a form of affectionate teasing (001-023)
- **Teaching outsiders** — Teaching Americans the brother-in-law term without explaining its rude connotation, as a form of quiet cultural humor (002-069)
