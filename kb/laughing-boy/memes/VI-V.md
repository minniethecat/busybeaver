[006-091] They talked as they ate, lounging, while night filled the valley.

[006-092] “Do you speak American then?” [[laughing-boy|Laughing Boy]] asked. “Is it hard to learn?”

[006-093] “It is not hard; we had to learn it. They put me in a room with a Ute girl and a Moqui and a Comanche; all we could do was learn English. Sometimes some Navajo girls sneaked out and talked together, but not often. They did not want us to be Indians.” She rested on her elbow, staring into the fire. “They wanted us to be ashamed of being Indians. They wanted us to forget our mothers and fathers.”

[006-094] “That is a bad thing. Why did they do that?”

[006-095] “Do not talk about it. I do not want to think about those things.”

[006-096] When she had put away the dishes, as they lit their cigarettes she said, “If ever they come to take a child of ours to school, kill her.”

[006-097] “Is it like that?”

[006-098] “Yes.”

[006-099] “I hear you.”

[006-100] They lay side by side against the wall of the house, watching the fire. Her shoulder moved closer to him. He said,

[006-101] “Tell me your true name.”

[006-102] “My name is Came With War. What is yours?”

[006-103] “My parents named me Sings Before Spears. It is a good name. Yours is good.”

[006-104] “Why do they always give women names about war?”

[006-105] “They have always done it. It brings good fortune to the whole People, I think.”

[006-106] She moved so that she touched him. Sings Before Spears!

[006-107] He asked her, “Have you any relatives here? Someone must get a singer to make the prayers over us. There are the four days after that to wait; that is a long time. Let us have them end soon.”

[006-108] She caught her breath and looked at him despairingly. He felt a wind blow between them while he met her eyes, a hollowness behind his heart. He clenched his left hand against his side, repeating slowly,

[006-109] “Four days is a long time to wait,” and then, almost inaudibly, “Oh, beautiful!”

[006-110] She looked away, wanting to laugh, to cry, to swear, and to kick him. He could not know; how could he know? She examined the line of his chin, the set of his lips, so very Indian in their fine chiselling and faint outthrust. Devices ran through her mind. This was a Navajo. This was something her missionaries and teachers never dreamed of. This was part of what she loved. She set her nails into the palms of her hands. Patience.

[006-111] “I have a friend near here who will speak to a Singer tomorrow. He will be here tomorrow night.”

[006-112] They smoked again. At last he said, “I do not think I shall sleep in your house now. I think it will be well to sleep up there.”

[006-113] “Yes; that will be better.”

[006-114] He got his blanket. “I shall forget the trail.”

[006-115] He loomed above her, in the play of darkness and firelight. She saw all the strength of the Navajo people embodied, against the sky, and she felt ashamed before it.

[006-116] “Four days is not long, Laughing Boy.”
