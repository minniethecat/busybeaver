# Bow

The bow is the primary ranged weapon of the Navajo, used for hunting game and in warfare,
crafted from local hardwoods with sinew backing and often decorated with silverwork.
In *Laughing Boy*, the bow is inseparable from the protagonist's identity as a warrior,
hunter, and man of status.

## Description

The Navajo bow is typically a self-bow carved from a single stave of juniper or oak,
reinforced with sinew laid along the back to increase draw weight and prevent breakage.
The bowstring is made of twisted sinew or rawhide. Bows range from three to four feet
in length, suited to mounted use. The grip is sometimes wrapped in leather, and among
skilled silversmiths like Laughing Boy, the back of the bow may bear ornamental silverwork
（001-020）.

The bow is paired with arrows carried in a quiver, and the bow case itself is often
decorated. The bow-guard (*ketloh*) — a leather or silver wrist guard worn on the left
forearm to protect against the bowstring's snap — is a separate article of personal
adornment and carries its own artistic and trade value.

## Cultural Significance

Among the Navajo, the bow is more than a weapon: it is a symbol of male competence.
A man's ability to use the bow for hunting — providing meat for his family — and for
defense — protecting his people — defines his standing in the community. The bow
appears in Navajo mythology, where the Hero Twins used lightning-arrows given by
the Sun Bearer to slay the monsters that threatened the People.

In the novel, the bow is a constant companion to Laughing Boy. When he travels to
the dance at Tsé Lani, he carries his bow; when he races horses, he bets his bow-guard.
The bow is also woven into simile and metaphor throughout the novel — arrows are
compared to racing horses, and Slim Girl's mare is "like an arrow notched to a taut
bowstring"（001-020）, a figure that links speed, precision, and latent power.

The bow also carries spiritual weight. The constellation "Boy Chasing His Arrow"
（007-095）is a Navajo celestial figure referenced in the novel, embedding the bow
and arrow in the cosmological framework of the People.

## Craftsmanship

Bow-making is a skilled craft requiring knowledge of wood selection, seasoning,
shaping, and sinew application. The bowyer must choose a stave with the right grain
and flexibility, carve it to a symmetrical taper, and apply sinew backing with animal
glue. The bowstring must be twisted to exact tension. Decoration — carved patterns,
silver inlay, painting — is the final step.

Laughing Boy is not explicitly shown making a bow in the novel, but his skill as a
silversmith extends to his bow-guard, which he made himself and which draws admiration
at the dance（001-004, 002-060）. The bow-guard, as the bow's companion piece, reflects
the same aesthetic and craft values.

## In the Novel

The bow appears throughout *Laughing Boy* in scenes of racing, conflict, and daily
life. At the Tsé Lani dance, Laughing Boy bets his bow-guard on a horse race and wins
（001-017）. Later, the bow-guard becomes a point of admiration and negotiation — other
men offer to buy it, recognizing its quality（002-017, 002-052, 002-060）.

In a darker context, the bow is a weapon of war. Laughing Boy recounts killing a Ute
raider with an arrow, describing the trajectory with grim precision（008-057）. Another
character, Wolf Killer, was permanently disabled when a Ute arrow grazed his head
（004-063）, a reminder of the bow's lethal capacity.

The novel's most concentrated metaphor for speed and direction draws on the bow:
"Arrows from the bow — no other simile"（004-087）. This passage links the racing
horses at Tsé Lani to the flight of arrows, compressing the novel's themes of
competition, beauty, and violence into a single image.

## Notable Passages

> "The bow-guard is worth more; it is worth ten dollars."（002-019）

> "Arrows from the bow—no other simile. At the tearing gallop, flat-stretched, backs are
> level, the animals race in a straight, wild, converging line."（004-087）

> "I saw my arrow in the air a long time; I saw it strike."（008-057）
