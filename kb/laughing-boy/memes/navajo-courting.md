# Navajo Courting Customs

The tradition of blanket-courting at Navajo dances — a ritualized courtship practice in which a young woman signals romantic interest by pulling a man's blanket, initiating a structured flirtation governed by custom and community observation.

## Definition

At Navajo dances, courtship follows a formal script. A woman interested in a man approaches him from behind and pulls at his blanket — a gesture that is both invitation and challenge. The man's response is prescribed: he "pulled his end of the blanket over his shoulders, assuming the conventional pose of resistance" (001-040). This initial resistance is part of the ritual, not genuine refusal. The two then tug at the blanket between them, with their eyes communicating what custom prevents them from speaking openly: "her long eyes were talking, much as he had seen eyes talk before as they pulled at the blanket, but these were clear as words" (001-039).

The dance proper follows. The couple joins the line of dancers, but unlike the group dancing, courting couples may hold each other: "Instead of dancing in the usual way, they held each other, hardly moving" (001-046). The entire exchange occurs within full view of the community — courting is public ritual, not private encounter.

## Cultural Context

Blanket-courting sits within the broader framework of Navajo social regulation. The dance is the sanctioned venue for young people to meet and express interest. Outside this frame, interactions between unmarried men and women are more constrained. Laughing Boy's discomfort when isolated with Slim Girl — his desire to "be back among the singers" (001-039) — reflects the importance of community witness to proper courtship.

The practice also involves a forfeit game. "Whenever there is a rest, the man asks what forfeit he must pay; by the length of time taken by the girl to give an answer, one judges" the intensity of her interest (001-042). This embeds bargaining and delay into the courtship ritual, prolonging the flirtation within socially acceptable bounds.

## In the Novel

Laughing Boy's encounter with Slim Girl at the Tsé Lani dance follows the blanket-courting script precisely — but with crucial deviations that signal Slim Girl's ambiguous cultural position. She is bolder than convention permits: she pulls his blanket with "unexpected strength" (001-037), and her direct eye contact and laughter mark her as operating outside the standard modesty expected of Navajo girls. Her behavior "made him feel like a fool" (001-037) while also attracting him — the tension between custom and Slim Girl's American-influenced boldness drives their initial dynamic.

The blanket becomes the novel's recurring symbol of courtship, intimacy, and cultural identity. Slim Girl's project of "going back to the blanket" (005-002) explicitly frames her return to Navajo life through the metaphor of the courting blanket.

## Related Practices

- **Blanket-pulling** — The initial signal; the woman pulls the man's blanket from behind to indicate interest (001-035, 001-037)
- **Forfeit-asking** — During dance rests, the man asks the woman's forfeit; the delay in answering measures her interest (001-042)
- **Courtship dancing** — Couples hold each other in a slow, intimate dance distinct from the group line dancing (001-046)
- **Public witness** — All courting occurs in full view of the community; this is protective, not voyeuristic
