# Navajo Gambling Culture

Gambling in *Laughing Boy* is not vice but social institution — the primary mechanism for circulating wealth, testing honor, and establishing masculine reputation. Betting on horse races, wrestling matches, and the moccasin game structures the gatherings that define Navajo community life.

## Definition

Navajo gambling encompasses several forms: horse-race betting (the dominant form at large gatherings), wrestling matches with side bets, and the stick-and-moccasin guessing game. Currency, goods, and personal ornaments all serve as stakes. Laughing Boy arrives at Tsé Lani with nothing but his bow-guard — which he immediately bets (001-017). His journey from pennilessness to "six dollars" through gambling establishes him as a competent participant in the male economy.

The betting is mediated by designated officials: "Hill Singer rode back and forth between, collecting and announcing the bets" (002-007). This formal role ensures transparency and prevents disputes. The public nature of betting — amounts called out, stakes displayed — integrates gambling into the community's shared knowledge and moral economy.

## Cultural Context

Gambling in the Navajo world serves functions beyond entertainment. It redistributes wealth: Laughing Boy's ugly belt can be "sold to a trader for fifty dollars" but is also a betting stake (002-061, 002-024). It tests courage and composure — the gambler who bets big and loses without visible distress demonstrates the emotional regulation valued in Navajo masculinity.

The competitive frame extends to non-gambling contexts. When Laughing Boy bargains with Americans over his belt — "Seventy-five dollars" (002-083) — he employs the same calibration of value and risk that governs his betting. The line between gambling, trade, and gift exchange is porous: all three circulate value through social networks.

## In the Novel

Laughing Boy's relationship with gambling traces his character arc. Early chapters show him as a competent, restrained gambler who bets his bow-guard, wins, and moves on (001-017). His friends mock him good-naturedly for his caution: "Why don't you bet your belt?" (002-020). By the later chapters, the gambling frame gives way to higher-stakes risks — marriage, cultural identity, and ultimately survival.

The moccasin game appears at the social periphery: "These with little sticks and the man with long hair and the ugly mouth on them are ones" (002-114) — the description of cards reveals the collision of Navajo traditional games with American-introduced playing cards, a small instance of cultural syncretism.

## Related Practices

- **Horse-race betting** — The central gambling event at large gatherings; mediated by an announcer who collects and declares bets (002-007)
- **Bow-guard/ belt betting** — Personal ornaments and crafted goods are wagered, not just money (001-017, 002-024)
- **Moccasin game** — A traditional guessing game played at smaller gatherings
- **Wrestling betting** — Side bets on wrestling matches among young men (002-011)
