# Laughing Boy's Marriage

The marriage of [[laughing-boy]] and [[slim-girl]] is a quiet, almost furtive
ceremony — conducted by [[yellow-singer]] without family present, in Slim Girl's
house near [[los-palos]]. It is the pivot point of the novel: the moment when
individual desire overrides communal judgment, when two people from different
worlds commit to building a life together, and when the seeds of the tragedy to
come are planted in the very soil of their happiness.

## Background

By the time of the wedding, Laughing Boy and Slim Girl have known each other
only through the encounters at the [[tse-lani-dance]] and a brief courtship.
Laughing Boy has gambled away his wealth and won it back; Slim Girl has
carefully orchestrated their meetings. The marriage is hasty by Navajo
standards — there has been no family negotiation, no exchange of gifts between
kin groups. Laughing Boy's uncles, especially [[wounded-face]], have not been
consulted.

## The Ceremony

The wedding follows traditional Navajo form despite its unconventional
circumstances. Slim Girl prepares corn mush; Yellow Singer's wife hands her the
medicine basket. The Singer places the mush in the correct position on the floor
(007-051). Laughing Boy enters, concentrating on the ritual's meaning:

> He was thinking hard about what he was doing; he was putting forth every effort
> to make the supernatural forces realize that this was an act of religion, of
> the utmost importance.
>
> （007-051）

Yellow Singer divides the mush in four directions — the ritual gesture that
invokes the sacred order of the Navajo cosmos. He prays over the couple. Then
Laughing Boy must sing his own prayer:

> Now it was Laughing Boy's turn to make a prayer. He sang the prayer to House
> God with solemn emphasis.
>
> （007-053）

The prayer closes with the formula of completion — "In beauty it is finished,
In beauty it is finished" (007-054) — and the ceremony is done:

> The confident, solemn voice ceased. He looked at Slim Girl. Now they were
> married "in a beautiful way." It might seem a little furtive, that ceremony
> without relatives, almost without guests, but now the gods had married them.
>
> （007-055）

## Consequences

The marriage's unconventional nature — no family present, no negotiation —
immediately creates tensions. Laughing Boy's family, particularly Wounded Face,
regards the union with suspicion. At the [[mountain-chant-ceremony]], the
family council directly challenges the marriage:

> "My nephew, we do not think it is good, this thing you are doing. We have
> talked about it a long time among ourselves. We know about that woman, that she—"
>
> （012-053）

Laughing Boy's defiance — "This is my business" — sets him against his family
and community. The marriage, begun with such solemn intention, becomes the
fulcrum on which the novel's central tensions balance: tradition versus
individual choice, family authority versus personal desire, the Navajo way
versus the compromises of the borderland.

The four-day waiting period after the ceremony, during which the couple must
remain together and observe restrictions, is compressed in the narrative — a
structural choice that emphasizes the haste and precariousness of their union.
What should be a time of consolidation becomes instead the prelude to isolation.
