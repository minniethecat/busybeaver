# Navajo Hospitality Customs

The unwritten code of Navajo hospitality that governs the sharing of food, the welcoming of guests, and the obligations of generosity at gatherings — a social ethic that defines community and measures character in *Laughing Boy*.

## Definition

Navajo hospitality is rooted in the obligation to share what one has. At the Tsé Lani dance, the community provides food for all comers: "two small naked boys brought ears of corn as they were roasted, and calm women set broiled goats' ribs and corn bread" (002-036). The feast is communal, with no accounting of who contributed what or who consumed how much. Even Laughing Boy, who arrives with "no money" and must "beg coffee from a trader at Chinlee" (001-004), is fed without question or judgment.

The etiquette of asking is formalized. The phrase "Give me a smoke, Grandfather" (002-005) is a ritual request, not a demand — the kinship term "Grandfather" placing the exchange within a framework of respect and obligation. When Laughing Boy addresses an American with "Nashto, shadani — give me a smoke, brother-in-law" (002-068), the Navajo etiquette of asking collides humorously with the American's incomprehension of the kinship framework.

## Cultural Context

Hospitality in Navajo culture is not optional generosity but social obligation. To refuse to share is to place oneself outside the community. The wealthy man who "owned over five hundred head of horses, and his wife had thick strings of turquoise and coral around her neck" (001-012) is admired not despite his wealth but because his wealth is understood as the product of and resource for community relationships.

The visiting custom — "visitors dropped in; more coffee was made" (005-016) — is automatic. No appointment is needed, no advance warning expected. The hogan door is metaphorically always open, and the arrival of visitors is met with the immediate production of food and coffee.

## In the Novel

Hospitality scenes frame the novel's social world. The Tsé Lani gathering (Chapters I-III) is a continuous feast — "Chunks of meat and roasted corn were passed. The Indians joked and laughed without reducing the speed of their eating" (005-015). Eating together creates the community that the dance and the racing celebrate.

Gift-giving is the material form of hospitality. Cigarettes are shared freely — "borrowing smokes now and then" (002-062) — and the tobacco itself is a marker of wealth: "Now that he had money, he would buy tobacco when he came to the trading post" (002-062). The American tourists' "magic tobacco" (002-070) becomes a novel gift that circulates through Navajo social networks, transformed from commercial product into social currency.

The contrast with American ways is implicit but sharp. Americans "carrying their own plates dipped in gingerly" at the Navajo feast (001-026) — their self-provisioning and hesitation marking them as outside the hospitality system.

## Related Practices

- **Communal feasting** — Food is prepared and shared communally at gatherings; no individual accounting (002-036, 005-015)
- **Smoke-sharing** — Cigarettes are shared with the ritual request "Give me a smoke, Grandfather/Brother-in-law" (002-005, 002-068)
- **Coffee-making for visitors** — Visitors are greeted with fresh coffee as a matter of course (005-016)
- **Gift circulation** — Material goods (tobacco, food, belts) circulate as gifts and bets, not merely as commodities (002-062, 002-083)
