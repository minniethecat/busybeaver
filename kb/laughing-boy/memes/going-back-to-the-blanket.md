# Going Back to the Blanket

The phrase "going back to the blanket" encapsulates a Navajo person's conscious decision to return to traditional Navajo ways — the blanket serving as the metonym for traditional life, material culture, and the web of kinship and ritual that American assimilation programs sought to replace.

## Definition

"Going back to the blanket" is Slim Girl's declared project. After years of American schooling that severed her from Navajo language and practice, she resolves to reclaim her birth culture: "she was 'going back to the blanket,' because under the blanket were the things worth while, and all the rest had failed her" (005-002). The blanket symbolizes traditional Navajo life in its totality — the material object that wraps the body, the courting custom in which it figures, the weaving tradition that produces it, and the community gathered around fires wrapped in blankets.

The phrase has a specific historical resonance: it was used in the assimilation era to describe Native Americans who rejected boarding schools and returned to reservation life. Slim Girl's use of it is thus both personal and political — an act of defiance against the system that tried to erase her Navajo identity.

## Cultural Context

The blanket functions throughout the novel as a multivalent symbol. It is the medium of courtship (the blanket-pulling at dances), the material of warmth and shelter (characters "rolled up in their blankets" to sleep), the sign of wealth and beauty (the rich blankets displayed at gatherings), and now, in Slim Girl's formulation, the emblem of cultural identity itself.

Slim Girl's return project is explicitly spiritual as well as cultural. She wants Laughing Boy to teach her "the meaning of those oft-repeated phrases, 'bik'é hojoni, the trail of beauty'" — through him, "she would learn the content" of Navajo spiritual life (005-004). The blanket and the beauty-way are inseparable: both represent the wholeness she seeks to recover.

## In the Novel

Slim Girl's declaration of "going back to the blanket" in Chapter V is the novel's most explicit statement of cultural recovery. It frames her entire subsequent project: her marriage to Laughing Boy as a means of reintegration, her insistence on traditional rituals like the sweat-bath, and her ultimate fate as the tragic cost of her incomplete return.

The phrase also illuminates the novel's central tension. Laughing Boy, who has never left the blanket, cannot fully understand what Slim Girl has lost or what her return signifies. His Navajo identity is unselfconscious; hers is a deliberate, willed reconstruction. This asymmetry drives their relationship and, ultimately, its tragedy.

## Related Practices

- **Learning traditional phrases** — Slim Girl seeks to learn *bik'é hojoni* and other ritual language from Laughing Boy as part of her return (005-004)
- **Sweat-bath ritual** — She insists on the sweat-bath as a concrete act of re-embracing Navajo practice (008-012)
- **Hogan life** — Living in a traditional hogan rather than an American-style house is both practical and symbolic (005-016)
