# Navajo Silversmithing

The traditional Navajo craft of working silver and turquoise into jewelry and ornaments, which serves as both artistic expression and economic livelihood in the Navajo world depicted in Oliver La Farge's *Laughing Boy* (1929).

## Definition

Navajo silversmithing is the craft of shaping silver into bracelets, belts, bow-guards, and other ornamental pieces, often set with turquoise, coral, or white shell (001-002). The jeweller's skill is a source of personal pride and social standing: Laughing Boy declares "I am a good jeweller... I make silver run like a song" (001-005). The quality of a person's silverwork is publicly judged — other Navajos stop to examine Laughing Boy's bow-guard "which he himself had made" (001-004), and a trader appraises work by noting "the silver is heavy; Mexican silver. That is good work" (002-083).

## Cultural Context

Silverwork sits within the broader Navajo aesthetic ideal of *hozoji* — "travelling in beauty" (001-021). Laughing Boy aspires not only to work silver and turquoise but to integrate his craft with the spiritual path of singing and ceremony: "to work in silver and turquoise, own soft-moving ponies, and lead the Mountain Chant" (001-021). Adornment with silver and stone is central to Navajo ceremonial dress. At the Tsé Lani dance, the crowd presents "flashing silver, turquoise, velveteen shirts" (001-004), and Slim Girl wears "more silver, coral, turquoise, and white shell than he had ever seen on any one person" (001-032).

Silver pieces also function as portable wealth and betting stakes. Belts, bow-guards, and bracelets are wagered in gambling, sold to traders, or offered to American tourists (002-062, 002-063). A well-made belt can command seventy-five dollars (002-083).

## In the Novel

Silversmithing is inseparable from Laughing Boy's identity. The novel opens with him composing a bracelet design in his mind as he rides: "He was thinking about a bracelet he should make, with four smooth bars running together, and a turquoise in the middle" (001-002). His bow-guard — a piece he made himself — is admired by his peers and becomes a betting stake (001-004, 001-017).

Laughing Boy's craft also expresses his emotional state. After meeting Slim Girl, he imagines making "a bracelet about her, thin silver, with stars surrounded by stone-knife-edge" (001-052), translating romantic feeling into silver form. His professional pride is wounded when a man disparages his turquoise: "That turquoise is no good, and the work is not very good" (002-054) — a judgment that stings precisely because craft defines his worth.

The economic dimension of silverwork runs throughout the novel. Laughing Boy's ugly belt can be "sold to a trader for fifty dollars" (002-061), and he sees American tourists as potential buyers for his work (002-062, 002-063).

## Related Practices

- **Bow-guard making** — A wrist guard worn by archers, crafted from silver and often set with stones; Laughing Boy's bow-guard is his finest personal work (001-004)
- **Belt crafting** — Concho belts with silver plaques; these are both adornment and portable currency, bet and sold throughout the novel (002-024, 002-083)
- **Bracelet design** — Thin silver bands incorporating stars and "stone-knife-edge" motifs, envisioned as gifts for beloveds (001-052)
- **Turquoise appraisal** — The quality of turquoise is publicly judged by color and matrix; poor turquoise diminishes the value of the work (002-054)
