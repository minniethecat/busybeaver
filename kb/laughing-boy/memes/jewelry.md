# Jewelry

Navajo silver and turquoise jewelry is the preeminent art form of the People and a
central element of economic and social life in *Laughing Boy*. More than decoration,
jewelry functions as portable wealth, a marker of status, and a medium of artistic
expression through which the novel's protagonist defines his identity.

## Description

Navajo jewelry encompasses a range of forms: heavy silver belts (*sis łichííʼ*) made
from conchos hammered from coin silver; bracelets of smooth or fluted silver bars,
often set with turquoise; necklaces of silver beads (*yooʼ*) strung with turquoise,
coral, and white shell; earrings of cut turquoise or silver drops; and rings set
with single stones or clusters. Each piece is hand-wrought using techniques of
casting, hammering, stamping, and soldering learned from Mexican silversmiths in
the nineteenth century.

At the Tsé Lani dance, the young men are "heavy with jewelry; Horse Giver's Son
wore over four hundred dollars in silver alone"（001-004）. The weight of silver
on the body is a direct measure of a man's wealth — not hidden in a bank but
worn visibly, clanking with movement, available for appraisal, bet, or sale.

## Cultural Significance

Jewelry is the Navajo form of savings: silver dollars and pesos are melted down
and transformed into wearable art, simultaneously storing and displaying wealth.
A man's belt, bracelets, and necklaces represent his economic standing and his
skill as a craftsman. Laughing Boy's bow-guard — silver set with turquoise, made
by his own hand — is his most prized possession and the first thing other men
notice about him（001-004, 002-060）.

Jewelry also functions in social exchange. Betting a belt or bracelet on a horse
race is both a wager and a display of confidence（002-026）. Giving silver as a
bride-price or gift formalizes relationships. Slim Girl promises Laughing Boy,
"I shall give you silver to make jewelry"（003-077）, framing their partnership
in the shared language of craft and wealth.

For women, jewelry is equally significant. Slim Girl's jewelry "clanked" when she
shook herself（006-019）, an audible signal of her prosperity and independence.
At the Tsé Lani dance, Laughing Boy's eye is caught by a woman wearing "more
silver, coral, turquoise, and white shell than he had ever seen"（001-032）.

## Craftsmanship

Navajo silversmithing involves melting coin silver (U.S. dollars or Mexican pesos),
pouring it into ingot molds, and hammering it into sheets or drawing it into wire.
Turquoise is cut, shaped, and polished, then set into bezels soldered onto the
silver base. Stamping — using hand-made steel dies — creates the geometric patterns
characteristic of Navajo work.

Laughing Boy is a skilled jeweler. His bow-guard, which he made himself, draws
admiration wherever he goes. "You are a good jeweler, they say. You made that
bow-guard"（003-016）, Slim Girl tells him. He takes pride in his craft: "I am a
good jeweller," he says; "I make silver run like a song"（quoted in corpus）.

The forge and the anvil are the jeweler's tools, and Laughing Boy's forge at Slim
Girl's house becomes the center of his productive life, paired symbolically with
her loom（009-031）.

## In the Novel

Jewelry threads through the novel as a constant presence. At the opening dance,
the display of silver and turquoise establishes the social world of the Navajo
communities gathered at Tsé Lani（001-004, 001-032）. Laughing Boy's bow-guard is
admired, bet, and nearly sold（001-017, 002-017, 002-052）.

In his relationship with Slim Girl, jewelry is both promise and bond. She provides
him with silver to work, and his jewelry — along with her weaving — is their
shared economic project: "you shall make jewelry"（008-011）, "you will have your
horses and your jewelry"（008-015）, "when we have made much money with your
jewelry and horses and my weaving"（008-019）.

The absence of jewelry is equally significant. When Laughing Boy returns from a
journey stripped of his possessions, he stands before Slim Girl with "no silver
belt, no jewelry, only the lucky bow-guard on his left wrist"（006-003） — a
moment of vulnerability and truth.
