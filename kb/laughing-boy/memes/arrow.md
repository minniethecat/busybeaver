# Arrow

The arrow (*k'aa'*) is the companion to the Navajo bow, together forming the primary
weapon system for hunting and warfare. In *Laughing Boy*, arrows appear in contexts of
violence, racing, and celestial observation, and the arrow's flight becomes the novel's
dominant metaphor for speed, precision, and irreversible consequence.

## Description

A Navajo arrow consists of a straight shaft of hardwood or reed, fletched with eagle
or hawk feathers, tipped with a point of chipped stone or traded iron. The shaft is
grooved or painted with identifying marks, as each warrior's arrows are recognizable.
The quiver — a case of leather or hide — holds a dozen or more arrows and is slung
across the back or attached to the saddle.

The bowstring snaps across the leather bow-guard when the arrow is released. A
well-made arrow flies true over distances of fifty to a hundred yards. In warfare,
the arrow's wound is often fatal — either immediately from the strike or later from
infection, as with the Pah-Ute raider who "bled to death" at a trading post after
Laughing Boy's arrow found its mark（007-042）.

## Cultural Significance

In Navajo mythology, arrows carry cosmic significance. The Hero Twins — Monster Slayer
and Child of the Waters — were given lightning-arrows by their father, the Sun Bearer,
to destroy the monsters that plagued the early world. The constellation "Boy Chasing
His Arrow"（007-095）appears in the novel's night sky, a celestial figure that
Laughing Boy watches as it "moved far across the sky," linking earthly weapons to
the cosmic order.

Arrows also carry metaphorical weight throughout the novel. Slim Girl's mare is
described as "like an arrow notched to a taut bowstring"（001-020）, a figure that
conveys both her beauty and her potential for sudden, decisive motion. Later, the
racing horses are compared to "Arrows from the bow — no other simile"（004-087）,
elevating the competition into something elemental and thrilling.

## Craftsmanship

Arrow-making requires precision: the shaft must be perfectly straight, the fletching
symmetrical, the point securely hafted. The fletcher selects feathers with the right
stiffness and curvature, splitting and trimming them to identical shape. The three-feather
fletching (two hen feathers and one cock feather) spins the arrow in flight for stability.

The novel does not describe arrow-making in detail, but Laughing Boy's skill as a
craftsman — demonstrated in his silverwork — suggests the same care and aesthetic
sensibility applied to weapons. The bow-guard he makes is paired with the bow and
arrows as a complete set of warrior's equipment.

## In the Novel

Arrows appear in the novel's most violent moments. Laughing Boy recounts shooting a
Pah-Ute raider: "I saw my arrow in the air a long time; I saw it strike. He fell off
his horse"（008-057）. The precision of the description — the long arc, the visible
impact — captures both the skill of the archer and the irreversible finality of the act.

The Ute arrow that grazed Wolf Killer's head left him permanently disabled — "by some
strange Ute magic, shrivelled the left side of his body"（004-063） — a reminder that
arrows carry not just physical force but, in Navajo belief, spiritual power.

In the novel's figurative language, the arrow's flight is the highest praise for speed.
The race at Tsé Lani reaches its climax in the image of horses as arrows, and Slim
Girl herself is first described through this metaphor — poised, precise, and already
in motion before the reader realizes it.

## Notable Passages

> "She was like an arrow notched to a taut bowstring — a movement of the hand would
> release level flight swiftly to a mark."（001-020）

> "Arrows from the bow—no other simile. At the tearing gallop, flat-stretched, backs are
> level, the animals race in a straight, wild, converging line."（004-087）

> trading post; there he bled to death."（007-042）
