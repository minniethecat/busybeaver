# Silver Bridle

The silver bridle is one of the most prestigious objects in Navajo material
culture — a fully functional piece of horse tack whose leather headstall is
mounted with heavy silver ornamentation: conchos, bosses, and shaped plaques.
More than equipment, it is portable wealth, a demonstration of the
silversmith's art, and an object of desire at the trading post. In *Laughing
Boy*, the silver bridle appears in two key scenes: an extended trading-post
negotiation that reveals the economics and aesthetics of Navajo silverwork, and
the burial scene where Laughing Boy gives up his silver-mounted bridle as one of
the final offerings laid upon his wife's grave.

## Description

A Navajo silver bridle consists of a leather headstall — the straps that fit
over a horse's head — ornamented with shaped silver pieces along the cheek
straps, browband, and noseband. The silver is worked using the same techniques
as other Navajo silverwork: hammered into dies, filed, and polished to a high
sheen. The weight of the silver is substantial — the bridle that Laughing Boy
brings to the trader is valued at between ninety and a hundred dollars, more
than the combined value of a saddle, blankets, and other trade goods.

> He clanked the bridle onto the counter.
>
> （013-063）

The sound of that clank — heavy silver on wood — is itself a statement of value.
A silver bridle is not for everyday use; it is brought out for dances,
ceremonies, and occasions when a man wishes to display his wealth and his
community's craftsmanship.

## Craftsmanship

The making of a silver bridle is listed among the range of objects a Navajo
silversmith can produce:

> You make many things, rings, bracelets, bow-guards, necklaces, pendants,
> belts, bridles, buttons, hatbands. No two are alike, but they are all of the
> silver, or of silver and turquoise.
>
> （009-046）

A bridle requires more silver than smaller pieces like rings or bracelets, and
the larger surface area of the ornaments demands confident design work. The
silversmith must shape silver to fit the curved surfaces of the horse's head
while maintaining a unified decorative scheme. Like all Navajo silverwork, each
bridle is unique — "no two are alike."

## In the Novel

The silver bridle's most extended appearance occurs in Chapter 13, where
Laughing Boy brings a bridle to trade at the store of Bow's Son. The negotiation
is a set-piece that reveals the trading-post economy and the social dynamics of
Navajo commerce:

> He explained to Slim Girl, and took her silver bridle.
>
> （013-021）

The bridle is visually striking: "It hung from a rafter, among other saddles,
quirts, bridles, pots, Pendleton blankets, ropes, silk handkerchiefs, and axes"
(013-059). The trader hefts it and estimates "ninety to a hundred dollars"
(013-065), a sum that reflects both the weight of the silver and the quality of
the work.

The negotiation is a social performance. Bow's Son holds up the bridle and
declares, "This is the kind of work they do down there" (013-077), using the
object to praise the craftsmanship of Laughing Boy's region. The trader Narrow
Nose reaches for it, wanting "to feel its possession" (013-078), and the
physical transfer of the bridle becomes a choreography of desire and
reluctance: "He started to push over the bridle; Narrow Nose had his fingers on
the heavy silver" (013-085).

The negotiation concludes with a bundle of trade goods exchanged for the bridle —
coffee, crackers, canned plums — and a final flourish:

> Drawing at the reins, he made the bridle seem to walk off the counter.
>
> （013-124）

The bridle reappears at the novel's climax. When Laughing Boy buries Slim Girl
in the rock niche, he covers her body with silver and turquoise. Then, as a
final act:

> Remembering something, he went to his pony, took off his silver-mounted bridle,
> and added it.
>
> （020-054）

The bridle, stripped from his own horse, becomes part of the grave goods — a
relinquishing not only of wealth but of the means of travel, the equipment of
the mobile Navajo life that Slim Girl can no longer share. Alongside the
bow-guard, the silver belt, the turquoise and coral, the bridle is one of the
last objects Laughing Boy touches before he walks away from the burial site and
back toward his homeland.
