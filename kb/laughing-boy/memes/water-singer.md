# Water Singer

A singer at the Tsé Lani gathering whose decision to allow Slim Girl to dance — and the community's subsequent override of that decision — dramatizes the tension between individual religious authority and communal enforcement of ceremonial norms.

## Identity

Water Singer is one of the ceremonial authorities at the Tsé Lani dance. His name follows the Navajo convention of naming singers after elements or qualities associated with their practice. As a singer, he holds the power to permit or exclude participants from ceremony.

## Role in the Narrative

Water Singer's crucial narrative moment comes when he permits Slim Girl to dance, only to have the community overrule him: "They made her stop. Water Singer let her dance, but we stopped him" (003-057). This episode crystallizes Slim Girl's ambiguous status — even a singer's authority is insufficient to integrate her into the ceremonial community — and reveals that Navajo religious authority operates within, not above, community consensus.

## Cultural Context

The singer's authority in Navajo ceremony is substantial but not absolute. The community's ability to override Water Singer's decision demonstrates that ceremonial participation is governed by collective judgment as well as individual expertise. Slim Girl's exclusion, even against a singer's permission, marks the limits of her reintegration into Navajo life.
