# Night Chant

The Night Chant (Nightway) is one of the most important healing ceremonies in
Navajo religion — a nine-night, ten-day ritual in which masked dancers portray
the [[yei]] (Holy People), singers chant extended prayer cycles, and the
community gathers for healing, socializing, and racing. In *Laughing Boy*, the
Night Chant appears at multiple points in the narrative, serving as both a
spiritual center and a social stage where characters' relationships to Navajo
tradition are tested and revealed.

## Background

The Night Chant is a complex ceremony requiring a trained Singer who has
memorized hundreds of songs, prayers, and ritual actions. The ceremony includes
the construction of a medicine lodge, the preparation of prayer-sticks and sand
paintings, and the climactic appearance of masked dancers representing the Holy
People. The full ceremony lasts ten days, with the most sacred elements
occurring at night — hence the English name. Its purpose is healing, both
physical and spiritual, restoring the patient to [[hozoji]].

In Navajo theology, the ceremony was given to the people by the Holy People
themselves. Only initiated Singers may conduct it, and the masks and songs are
treated as powerful and potentially dangerous sacred objects.

## In the Novel

The Night Chant is first mentioned when Laughing Boy refuses to explain its
sacred elements to Slim Girl:

> "I do not think I should show you that. You are a woman, and you have never
> seen the true gods in the Night Chant."
>
> （009-011）

This moment establishes the ceremony's sacred exclusivity and the gender-specific
nature of certain ritual knowledge.

The ceremony serves as a recurring social anchor. Word of a Night Chant spreads
through the dispersed community, drawing people together:

> "There is to be a Night Chant over at Chilbito, by Tseye Buckho. There may be
> racing."
>
> （010-053）

Laughing Boy himself participates as a dancer — a mark of his deep engagement
with traditional practice:

> "They are going to hold a full Night Chant, ten nights. They want us to come,
> he says. Mountain Singer wants me to dance in it; it is a song that I know
> well. I have been in it before when he led."
>
> （011-010）

For Slim Girl, the Night Chant becomes a site of spiritual ambivalence. Her
American education tells her to dismiss it, but the experience resists easy
categories:

> Slim Girl watched the ceremony with interest, feeling in a pronounced fashion
> the mixed emotion she had towards so much that was going on about her. It might
> be a weapon to destroy her, for the very reason that it was a summation and a
> visible expression of many things in her people's life that mattered to her.
>
> （012-023）

Her childhood memory of the ceremony's power surfaces:

> When she had been a very little girl, she had trembled with terror and awe at
> the sight of the very gods coming into the circle of people. Out in the
> darkness one heard their distant call, repeated as they came nearer, until with
> the fourth cry they entered the firelight.
>
> （012-027）

A Night Chant is also held at [[chiziai]], conducted by Mountain Singer
(014-031). The ceremony's geographical reach — Chilbito, Chiziai, T'o Tlakai —
demonstrates its role as a unifying institution across the dispersed Navajo
landscape.

## Ceremonial Structure

| Phase | Duration | Key Elements |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Preparation | Days 1–4 | Building the medicine lodge, preparing prayer-sticks, gathering materials |
| Public gatherings | Throughout | Racing, gambling, social dancing alongside ritual events |
| Night vigils | Nights 1–9 | Prayer cycles, sand painting rituals, purification rites |
| Masked dancers | Nights 8–9 | Yei dancers enter the firelight, perform sacred dances |
| Closing | Day 10 | Final prayers, patient blessing, dispersal |
