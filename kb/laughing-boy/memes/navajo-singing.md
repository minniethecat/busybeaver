# Navajo Singing and Ceremony

The tradition of ceremonial singing in Navajo culture — a practice that combines prayer, music, and ritual to create spiritual order and community cohesion. In *Laughing Boy*, singing is the central religious act, and the singer holds a position of honor and power.

## Definition

Navajo ceremonial singing encompasses the songs, chants, and prayers performed at dances, healing ceremonies, and religious gatherings. Singers are specialists who learn the complex repertoire of songs associated with major ceremonies such as the Mountain Chant and the Night Chant. Laughing Boy aspires to become a singer: "It would be good to be a singer as well, to express all these things through the prayers. He would like to know many of them, to learn to conduct the Mountain Chant" (001-021). The singer's role combines priest, healer, and artist — leading ceremonies, knowing "all the beautiful stories behind the songs," and guiding the community through ritual.

## Cultural Context

Singing is inseparable from the Navajo aesthetic of *hozoji*. As the novel opens, Laughing Boy rides to Tsé Lani composing his own song — "Nashdui bik'é dinni, eya-a, eyo-o … Wildcat's feet hurt" (001-001) — merging song, motion, and identity into a single stream: "Rope's end, shoulders, song, all moved together, and life flowed in one stream" (001-002). His friends tease him: "You should make a song about yourself, and teach the burros to sing it" (001-006), revealing song-making as a social, living practice.

At the Tsé Lani dance, the singing is communal and transformative. The drum and voice create an immersive experience: song "followed song with a rush; when one ended, the next took up, as though the whole night would never suffice to pour out all that was in them" (001-034). The singers lead the gathering with an authority that contrasts sharply with the dancers' "free release": "this was a religious ceremony and a rustic, simple pleasure, the happiness of a natural people" (001-041).

The ceremonial structure includes a formal etiquette: "whenever there is a rest, the man asks what forfeit he must pay" (001-042), embedding social negotiation within the ritual frame. Singing from inside a hogan — "a chink of light showed in the blanketed door of a big, earth-covered winter hogan. Singing came out of it" (005-016) — marks domestic space as sacred space.

## In the Novel

Laughing Boy's identity as a singer-in-training runs parallel to his identity as a silversmith. The two crafts are complementary paths on the trail of beauty: to work silver and to learn the Mountain Chant are twin aspirations (001-021). His songs mark his emotional state throughout the novel — from the joyful traveling songs of Chapter I to the prayer-like intensity after his crisis with Slim Girl.

Water Singer and Yellow Singer appear as figures of religious authority. Their permission or prohibition determines who may participate in ceremony. Slim Girl's ambiguous status is dramatized through her exclusion from the dance: "They made her stop. Water Singer let her dance, but we stopped him" (003-057), revealing the singer's gatekeeping role and the community's enforcement of ceremonial norms.

## Related Practices

- **The Mountain Chant** — The major ceremony Laughing Boy hopes to learn; a multi-night ritual combining songs, prayers, and sand-paintings (001-021)
- **Traveling songs** — Personal songs composed while riding, expressing the singer's identity and state of being (001-001, 001-002)
- **Drum-led singing** — The drummer is central, "intently serious over drawing forth from a bit of hide stretched across the end of a hollow log" the rhythm that drives the ceremony (001-049)
- **Healing singing** — Songs performed inside the medicine-hogan for curing; linked to the sweat-bath purification ritual (008-012, 016-011)
