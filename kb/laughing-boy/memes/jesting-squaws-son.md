# Jesting Squaw's Son

Laughing Boy's closest friend and frequent traveling companion — a young Navajo man whose teasing humor, competitive energy, and steadfast loyalty embody the codes of Navajo male friendship.

## Identity

Jesting Squaw's Son appears throughout the novel as Laughing Boy's companion in travel, gambling, and ceremony. His name reflects his character: he is quick with a joke and irreverent by disposition. At the Tsé Lani dance, he calls out to Laughing Boy, "E-ya, Grandfather, are you going to dance with the horse? Food is ready" (001-023) — the mock-grandfather address and absurd suggestion epitomize his humor. His arm is "over Laughing Boy's shoulder" (001-033) in easy camaraderie, and "on the other side another friend" completes the picture of Navajo male friendship as physical, unselfconscious, and constant.

## Role in the Narrative

Jesting Squaw's Son functions as the normative Navajo male companion against which Laughing Boy's deviations are measured. When Laughing Boy's infatuation with Slim Girl leads him to act against custom, "Jesting Squaw's Son, his brother, and Bay Horse, his brother-in-law, both evidently thought he had made a fool of himself" (012-001) — the friend's judgment carries the weight of community standards.

## Key Relationships

- [[laughing-boy|Laughing Boy]] — Closest friend; their bond is tested by Laughing Boy's relationship with Slim Girl
- [[bay-horse|Bay Horse]] — His brother-in-law; together they form the core of Laughing Boy's male circle

## Character Arc

Jesting Squaw's Son represents the stable, unchanging world of Navajo male friendship. He does not undergo transformation; rather, his constancy highlights Laughing Boy's movement away from the normative path.
