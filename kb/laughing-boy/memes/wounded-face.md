# Wounded Face

Wounded Face is [[laughing-boy]]'s eldest uncle and the patriarchal voice of
family authority at [[tse-lani]]. Described as "sitting apart in his blanket like
a sleepy eagle" (012-001), he embodies the collective wisdom and suspicion of
the older generation. His opposition to Laughing Boy's marriage to [[slim-girl]]
— based on reports from [[red-man]] and his own judgment — makes him the most
formidable obstacle to the couple's acceptance by their Navajo community.

## Identity

Wounded Face lives at Tsé Lani and holds seniority among Laughing Boy's
maternal uncles — the figures who, in matrilineal Navajo society, carry the
greatest authority over a young man's choices. He is older than his brother
Spotted Horse, and his opinions carry corresponding weight. When Red Man brings
news of Slim Girl's dubious reputation, it is Wounded Face he seeks out as the
person whose judgment matters most (006-025).

## Role in the Narrative

Wounded Face appears in two critical sequences. First, at the gambling gathering
before Laughing Boy's marriage, he receives [[red-man]]'s warning about Slim
Girl. His response is measured but firm:

> "No. That is what that young man wants us to do, I think. You saw him, how
> moved he was. We have heard something of what he says, but still, he had reason
> to lie. Besides, it would be no use. He is like me, he is like his mother, and
> his father. You know them. When it is something serious he makes up his mind;
> you cannot move him unless you can convince him."
>
> （006-044）

This passage reveals Wounded Face's complexity: he distrusts Red Man's motives,
understands Laughing Boy's stubbornness, yet reserves judgment. He is not a
simple obstructionist but a careful elder weighing competing claims.

His second major scene is the family council during the [[mountain-chant-ceremony]]:

> "My nephew, we do not think it is good, this thing you are doing. We have
> talked about it a long time among ourselves. We know about that woman, that she—"
>
> （012-053）

Here he speaks for the collective — "we have talked about it" — wielding
the authority of consensus against Laughing Boy's individual choice. When
Laughing Boy resists, Wounded Face dismisses him with the elder's perennial
complaint: "You are young, you do not like to listen" (012-073).

## Key Relationships

- [[laughing-boy]] — nephew; Wounded Face considers him "the best of them" among
  six nephews (006-044), which is precisely why he opposes a marriage he
  believes will harm him
- [[spotted-horse]] — younger brother and fellow uncle, who sits with him at the
  family council
- [[red-man]] — informant whose warnings Wounded Face receives with cautious skepticism
- [[slim-girl]] — the woman whose reputation he questions

## Character Arc

Wounded Face undergoes no character change — he is a structural figure, the
embodiment of tradition's resistance to the novel's central relationship. His
significance lies in what he represents: the inescapable weight of family
opinion in Navajo society. Laughing Boy can defy him, as he does, but cannot
ignore him. The council scene (012-047–073) is the novel's most concentrated
depiction of how Navajo family authority operates — through talk, consensus, and
the accumulated weight of elder opinion rather than command.
