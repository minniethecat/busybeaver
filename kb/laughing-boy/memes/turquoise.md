# Turquoise

Turquoise is the defining gemstone of Navajo silversmithing, appearing throughout *Laughing Boy* as the material that elevates silverwork from craft to art.

## Description

Turquoise is an opaque blue-green mineral that has been mined and traded across the American Southwest for centuries. In Navajo jewelry, it is cut and polished into cabochons and set into silver to create bracelets, rings, necklace pendants, and belt ornaments. The pairing of turquoise's cool blue-green with silver's warm white gleam defines the classic Navajo jewelry aesthetic.

In the novel, turquoise appears in multiple contexts: set in belts (002-074), combined with coral and white shell in necklaces (003-077), and imagined in bracelets that Laughing Boy plans to make (001-052).

## Cultural Significance

For the Navajo, turquoise carries symbolic weight beyond its material beauty. The stone evokes *hozoji* — the Navajo concept of walking in beauty, of harmony and balance. Its blue-green color suggests the sky after rain, a blessing in the arid landscape of the Navajo homeland.

Turquoise jewelry also functions as a store of value. Slim Girl's collection of turquoise, coral, white shell and silver necklaces represents accumulated wealth that she can wear, display, and rely upon — portable capital in a mobile society (003-077). The tourist's immediate preference for the turquoise-studded belt confirms that even outsiders recognize turquoise as the most desirable element of Navajo silverwork.

## Craftsmanship

Working with turquoise requires the silversmith to shape and polish the stone before setting it in silver. Each stone is unique in color and matrix pattern, making every piece of turquoise jewelry one of a kind. The silversmith must design the silver setting to complement the individual stone's shape and character.

Laughing Boy's turquoise work reflects his training and aesthetic judgment. When he imagines making a bracelet "with stars surrounded by stone-knife-edge" (001-052), the turquoise is the centerpiece around which the entire design revolves.

## In the Novel

- **A craftsman's vision**: Riding to Tsé Lani, Laughing Boy daydreams about a bracelet he plans to make: "four smooth bars running together, and a turquoise in the middle — if he could get the silver" (001-050). The turquoise is the focal point of his artistic imagination.

- **The tourist's desire**: At the trading encounter, the tourist immediately asks about the belt with turquoise, singling it out from Laughing Boy's other work as the most valuable piece (002-074).

- **Slim Girl's treasure**: Slim Girl displays her collection of necklaces — turquoise, coral, white shell and silver — as proof of her wealth and independence (003-077).
