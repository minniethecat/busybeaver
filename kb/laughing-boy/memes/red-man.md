# Red Man

Red Man is a minor antagonist in *Laughing Boy* — a wrestler and gambler from
the country near [[chiziai]] whose history with [[slim-girl]] and simmering
resentment of [[laughing-boy]] make him a catalyst for the jealousy and doubt
that strain the protagonists' marriage. Though he appears in only a handful of
scenes, his "smiling implications" (015-003) and strategic gossip embody the
social dangers that Slim Girl's past poses to her carefully constructed new life.

## Identity

Red Man is introduced at the [[tse-lani-dance]] with a simple geographic
identifier: "He comes from down by the railroad" (002-013). This associates him
with the American-influenced borderlands — the same contested cultural space
that produced Slim Girl's double life. Unlike the northern Navajo of Laughing
Boy's country, Red Man moves easily between the worlds of traditional dances,
gambling, and the railroad economy. No Navajo name or clan affiliation is given
for him, consistent with his role as an outsider and troublemaker rather than a
fully rounded character.

He is physically strong — a wrestler who competes at dances — but his real
weapon is talk. He adopts "an attitude of smiling implications, of 'I could and
I would'" (015-003), using insinuation rather than direct confrontation.

## Role in the Narrative

Red Man serves three narrative functions. First, he is a wrestling opponent whom
Laughing Boy defeats at the Tsé Lani dance (002-028), establishing Laughing
Boy's physical prowess while planting the seed of Red Man's resentment. Second,
he is a source of information — and misinformation — about Slim Girl's past. His
warning to [[wounded-face]] is the novel's most explicit statement of her
ambiguous reputation:

> She lives alone, she does no work, she is rich. The Americans make her rich,
> for badness. She is two faces and two tongues. You see her clothes and her
> skin, and hear her voice, but all the rest inside is American badness. I know.
> Hear me, I know.
>
> （006-039）

Third, and most consequentially, he is an agent of jealousy. At later dances, he
hovers near Slim Girl with knowing looks, stirring Laughing Boy's doubts:

> Watching Red Man, it came upon him that, remarkable though his wife was, she
> was subject to the same general laws as other people, and he was fairly sure
> that he was not the first man she had known.
>
> （015-003）

## Key Relationships

- [[slim-girl]] — Red Man once hoped to be with her; she kept him at a distance
  but used the friendship to "remember the ways of The People" (019-070)
- [[laughing-boy]] — rival, both in wrestling and over Slim Girl; Red Man fears
  Laughing Boy might ambush him (015-008)
- [[wounded-face]] — co-conspirator in spreading doubts about Slim Girl's
  character

## Character Arc

Red Man does not change across the novel; he is a static antagonist. What
changes is the reader's understanding of him. Slim Girl's late confession
provides the fullest account:

> I was not happy. I was provided for, I was revenging myself through him, but I
> was not living. I wanted my own people. I was all alone. That was why I made
> friends with Red Man. He is not good, that man. He did not care if I were bad,
> he hoped I might be bad with him. I never was, but I kept him hoping. With him
> I remembered the ways of The People, I became quick again in their speech.
>
> （019-070）

This passage recontextualizes Red Man: he was never a genuine romantic rival,
only a tool Slim Girl used to reconnect with Navajo life after her years at
school. His malice is real, but his significance is less than Laughing Boy
fears. He appears one final time, riding past Chizbitsé on his way to trade
(020-025), a figure receding from the narrative as the novel moves toward its
tragic conclusion.

## Cultural Context

Red Man embodies the ambiguities of the borderlands Navajo — those who live near
the railroad and trading posts, navigating between traditional and American
economies. His wrestling and gambling are traditional pursuits, but his
willingness to spread damaging gossip and his fear of "police and law"
(015-008) mark him as a product of the disrupted world that the novel
chronicles. He is what Slim Girl might have become had she not chosen, through
Laughing Boy and her [[navajo-blankets|weaving]], to rebuild herself as Navajo.

## Notable Passages

> Red Man saw that Wounded Face very much wanted firsthand information. "I live
> not far from Chiziai. I know, not just talk. She lives alone, she does no
> work, she is rich. The Americans make her rich, for badness. She is two faces
> and two tongues."
>
> （006-039）

> Red Man studied him; he was plainly in deadly earnest. He might just as well
> have acted instead of spoken — those men from up there have not yet realized
> the power of police and law. Among Navajos, the reasonable and acceptable way
> to have done, had he acted, would have been from ambush. Red Man felt he had
> had a narrow escape.
>
> （015-008）
