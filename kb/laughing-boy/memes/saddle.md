# Saddle

The Navajo saddle is a central artifact of equestrian life in *Laughing Boy*, serving as both practical riding equipment and a marker of personal wealth, craftsmanship, and social status.

## Description

The Navajo saddle of the novel's era is a Western-style stock saddle, constructed with a wooden tree covered in rawhide and leather, featuring a high cantle and horn (013-024). The saddle is cinched with a leather girth strap passed through a ring and held fast to the horn by the rider's hand during mounted games. Saddles are often ornamented with silver conchos and decorative tooling, reflecting the owner's wealth and taste.

In the trading post, saddles hang from rafters alongside quirts, bridles, pots, Pendleton blankets, ropes, silk handkerchiefs, and axes — a display of valued trade goods (013-059).

## Cultural Significance

Among the Navajo, a fine saddle represents both practical necessity and social standing. A man's saddle is his most important piece of equipment after his horse. The price of a saddle — twenty-seven dollars in the novel's trading-post scene (013-082) — represents a significant investment, nearly two months' wages for a skilled silversmith.

The saddle also carries domestic and relational symbolism. When Red Goat's wives put his saddle outside the door, it signals the end of their marriage — a traditional gesture of rejection that everyone in the community immediately understands.

## Craftsmanship

Navajo saddles of this period were typically Anglo-made trade goods, purchased at trading posts rather than manufactured by the Navajo themselves. The saddle Laughing Boy considers buying is one among many hanging in the trader's store (013-057). However, Navajo craftsmen would repair, customize, and ornament their saddles, adding silverwork and decorative elements that reflected individual taste and status.

The saddle blanket, by contrast, was frequently of Navajo weaving — a point of pride and a domestic art. Slim Girl weaves a saddle-blanket that Laughing Boy selects as the finest piece among their household goods (020-054).

## In the Novel

The saddle appears throughout *Laughing Boy* as both an object of daily use and a symbolic prop in key scenes:

- **The saddle-changing race** at the Tsé Lani dance is a central spectacle of horsemanship. Riders gallop, dismount, transfer saddles between horses at full speed, and remount — all while managing the cinch-strap with one hand. The saddle literally becomes the object of competition, and a turned saddle costs a rider the race (013-029).

- **Laughing Boy's worn saddle** prompts his visit to the trading post, where he purchases a new one for twenty-seven dollars, along with a blanket, tobacco, and a handkerchief — a significant outlay that marks his entry into adult economic life (013-057, 013-082).

- **The corn bag under the saddle**: Laughing Boy carries his horse's feed corn stowed beneath his saddle, a practical detail that grounds the novel's depiction of Navajo travel and horse care (001-052).

- **Wounded Face's hand on his saddle** in the novel's climactic confrontation, staring at the stirrup while delivering difficult news — the saddle becomes an anchor in a moment of emotional weight.

- **Slim Girl's gift**: Among the household goods she and Laughing Boy set aside, the finest piece is "the saddle-blanket of her weaving," representing her contribution to their shared domestic life (020-054).

## Notable Passages

> He was lax in the saddle, leaning back, arm swinging the rope's end in time to the horse's lope. His new red headband was a bright colour among the embers of the sun-struck desert, undulating like a moving graph of the pony's lope, or the music of his song.

> The ponies were saddled and mounted. The cinch-strap was carried through the ring of the girth, then up to the horn, where the rider held it fast with one hand, a finger of which also hooked in the reins. The other hand held a quirt ready to strike.

> The horsemen flashed to earth, bringing their saddles with them, the ponies were wheeled around. Bare arms and backs rippled as the new saddles were swung on, the cinch-strap caught through, held to the horn by the same hand on which the new rider swung as he leaped to the saddle, the horse already in motion under him.

> A man's foot slipped. Everyone laughed and cheered. It was a close race. Now the last men were mounting. The one on the team Laughing Boy was backing lost his grip on the strap, and the saddle turned under him.
