# Navajo Blankets

Navajo blanket weaving is both a practical craft and a spiritual act — the loom
is a site where beauty, identity, and domestic harmony converge. In *Laughing
Boy*, weaving becomes the center of Slim Girl's creative awakening and her
shared life with [[laughing-boy]], embodying the [[hozoji]] ideal of walking in
beauty through purposeful work. The craft connects the novel's characters to
generations of Navajo women, to the [[navajo-silversmithing]] tradition, and to
the broader economy of trading posts and tourists at places like [[los-palos]].

## Definition

In Navajo culture, blanket weaving is a women's art, passed from mother to
daughter. The upright loom holds warp threads under tension; the weaver works
colored weft strands through them with a batten, building the fabric row by row.
The craft requires patience, design sense, and intimate knowledge of wool
preparation — skills that Slim Girl, educated at an American school, must
painfully re-learn.

> The blue shuttle goes under six warp strands, the black, coming under two,
> meets it. A close weave looks like a true diagonal.
>
> （009-042）

The finished blanket is more than a utilitarian object. A well-woven blanket
carries the weaver's design vision, technical skill, and — in the Navajo
understanding — something of her spirit. Blankets serve as clothing, bedding,
saddle covers, door covers, trade goods, and ceremonial gifts.

## Cultural Context

Among the Navajo, weaving holds cosmological significance. According to
tradition, Spider Woman taught the Navajo to weave on a loom gifted by the Holy
People. The loom itself is a sacred structure: the warp represents the
foundation of the world, and the act of weaving mirrors the creative processes
of the [[deity]] figures who shaped the Navajo universe.

The rhythmic sound of weaving — the thump of the batten pounding down the weft —
is, in Navajo domestic life, a sound of peace and order. It signifies that all
is well in the household, that the woman is productively engaged, and that
beauty is being brought into being.

> Outside was the rhythmic thump-thump-thump of a weaver pounding down the
> threads in her loom. A distant child laughed, someone was chopping wood —
> sounds of domesticity.
>
> （003-050）

Weaving also connects to the principle of [[hozoji]] — the Navajo concept of
walking in beauty, of harmony and balance. A weaver who works with focused
intention, creating ordered beauty from raw wool, participates in the
maintenance of hozoji.

## In the Novel

Weaving functions as an arc of Slim Girl's character development. When she first
attempts to weave, she has been away at an American school for years and has
lost the skill:

> To complete her idyll, she wanted to weave, and she found it harder than she
> had expected. She had been taken to school young, before she had become
> skilled, and now it was almost all forgotten. Laughing Boy even had to teach
> her the names of her tools.
>
> （009-006）

Her first blanket is a disaster:

> When she cut the sorry object from the loom, and looked at it, all crooked,
> irregular, and full of holes, she could have cried. She hid it from him.
>
> （009-007）

But she persists. Over time, weaving becomes not just a skill but a form of
self-discovery. The design comes to her from the landscape itself:

> She looked from the conventionalization to the growing stalks; she divided the
> threatening sky into a design. Her first, elaborate blanket had been a
> built-up, borrowed idea, her later ones were uninteresting accidents. Now she
> saw her work complete, loving it and the task of making it. Now she really had
> something to tell her loom.
>
> （009-017）

When her blanket is finished, the wool nap conceals the imperfections, and she
recognizes her own creative power:

> She could see how good her conception had been, how true and sure. She had
> made a beautiful thing. She looked and looked.
>
> （009-027）

The loom and the forge — weaving and [[navajo-silversmithing]] — become the twin
pillars of their shared life. Working side by side, they create not only
beautiful objects but the fabric of their marriage:

> There were long, flawless days when they were at home together, he at his
> forge, she at her loom, time passing with the thump of the batten, the ring of
> the hammer or rasp of the file. There were chatter and laughter, songs, and
> long, rich silences. Work then was all love and inspiration.
>
> （009-031）

Weaving also provides a philosophical lens. Slim Girl, contemplating her loom,
sees a metaphor for life itself:

> This is like time. Here, this little part showing, where I am weaving, is the
> present; the past is rolled up and gone; there are those empty warp cords
> above me.
>
> （016-087）

Her weaving gains recognition. At [[tse-lani]], the women examine her
saddle-blanket and approve — a crucial moment of social acceptance:

> Their opinion of her changed visibly when they learned that it was she who had
> woven Laughing Boy's saddle-blanket. The red background, with the black and
> white interlocked fret of the heat lightning, was a gay and handsome thing.
>
> （011-031）

Later, the trading post at [[los-palos]] passes on orders from tourists,
providing a modest income. Even when sales decline and their marriage strains,
Slim Girl continues weaving because "she found relief and, one might say, a
confidant in her craft" (016-083).
