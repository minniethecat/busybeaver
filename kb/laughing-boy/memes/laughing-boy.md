# Laughing Boy

The young Navajo protagonist of Oliver La Farge's *Laughing Boy* (1929) — a skilled silversmith and aspiring singer whose traditional upbringing, romantic love for Slim Girl, and ultimate tragedy dramatize the cultural collision between Navajo life and American expansion.

## Identity

Laughing Boy is introduced as "lean, slender, tall, and handsome" (001-003), a young Navajo man from T'o Tlakai traveling to the Tsé Lani dance. His name, translated from Navajo, reflects his characteristic gaiety and good humor. His identity is defined by twin crafts: silversmithing, at which he excels ("I am a good jeweller... I make silver run like a song," 001-005), and singing, which he aspires to master through learning the Mountain Chant (001-021). He is a traditional Navajo in the fullest sense — his values, skills, and worldview are shaped entirely by Navajo culture, with only peripheral contact with American ways.

## Role in the Narrative

Laughing Boy is the novel's point-of-view character and moral center. His journey from the opening ride to Tsé Lani through his marriage to Slim Girl, their life at the hidden hogan, and the tragic climax traces a path from innocence through experience to devastating knowledge. His traditional values — the *hozoji* ideal of walking in beauty, the craftsman's pride, the warrior's restraint — are tested against Slim Girl's ambiguous American-influenced past and the encroaching pressure of American law and commerce.

His narrative function is to embody authentic Navajo culture confronting modernity. Unlike Slim Girl, who has been educated by Americans and consciously chooses to "go back to the blanket" (005-002), Laughing Boy has never left. His tragedy is that his unchosen authenticity cannot protect him from forces he does not fully understand.

## Key Relationships

- [[slim-girl|Slim Girl]] — His wife; their relationship drives the entire plot. She chooses him as the vehicle of her return to Navajo life, and her concealed past ultimately destroys them both.
- [[jesting-squaws-son|Jesting Squaw's Son]] — His closest friend and traveling companion; their joking, gambling, and racing embody Navajo male companionship.
- [[yellow-singer|Yellow Singer]] — A singer and religious authority; represents the ceremonial knowledge Laughing Boy aspires to attain.
- [[half-man|Half Man]] — His father's brother; a connection to family and tradition.

## Character Arc

Laughing Boy begins the novel in a state of perfect Navajo integration — riding, singing, crafting silver, living *hozoji*. His encounter with Slim Girl at the Tsé Lani dance disrupts this harmony and initiates his movement into adult complexity. Through his marriage, he experiences both profound love and the strain of a relationship with a woman who is not fully of his world. The novel's devastating conclusion sees him perform a warrior's final act — a response to betrayal that is simultaneously a reassertion of Navajo values and a recognition that those values can no longer protect him.

## Cultural Context

Laughing Boy embodies the Navajo ideal of the complete man: warrior and artist fused. Slim Girl's perception captures this: "one felt the warrior under the gaiety, and by his songs and his silver, he was an artist" (005-004). His character demonstrates that Navajo masculinity is defined by creativity and spiritual practice as much as physical courage. His silversmithing is not a trade but a form of prayer — his bracelet designs, his bow-guard, his songs are all expressions of *hozoji*.
