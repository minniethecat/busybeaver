# Slim Girl

Slim Girl (also called Slender Hair) is the female protagonist of *Laughing Boy* — a Navajo woman whose American education has alienated her from her birth culture, and whose project of "going back to the blanket" through marriage to Laughing Boy drives the novel's central action and tragic conclusion.

## Identity

Slim Girl's identity is defined by fracture. Taken from her Navajo family as a child and educated in an American school, she has lost fluency in Navajo language and custom while never being accepted by the American world. At the Tsé Lani dance, she is introduced as an ambiguous figure: her "suave movements and the rich display of blankets and jewelry" (001-032) mark her as Navajo by dress, but her boldness — pulling Laughing Boy's blanket with "unexpected strength" (001-037), making direct eye contact, laughing at his discomfort — signals her departure from Navajo feminine modesty.

She is described as "too slender, seeming frail" yet possessing "unexpected strength" (001-037). Her pony, like her, is "compact, all black save for the tiny white spot on her forehead" with "the ugly Roman nose of character" — "like an arrow notched to a taut bowstring — a movement and a preparedness" (001-020). The pony description doubles as character portrait: she is coiled potential, waiting to be released.

## Role in the Narrative

Slim Girl is the novel's engine of change. Her deliberate choice of Laughing Boy as husband — "She could tie this man to her as surely as any prisoner; she would follow her clear plan to its victorious end" (005-003) — sets the plot in motion. Her declared project of "going back to the blanket" (005-002) is both genuine cultural reclamation and calculated strategy.

Her narrative function is to embody the tragedy of the in-between. She is too Americanized to be fully accepted by Navajo society (the community forces her to stop dancing at the ceremony, 003-057), yet too Navajo to find fulfillment in the American world that educated her. Her marriage to Laughing Boy is an attempt to bridge this gap — but the gap proves unbridgeable.

## Key Relationships

- [[laughing-boy|Laughing Boy]] — Her husband; she selects him as the embodiment of authentic Navajo masculinity through whom she will reclaim her culture. Her love for him is genuine, but it is also instrumental — a tension that proves fatal.
- American school community — Her unnamed American teachers and the world they represent; she has rejected this world but cannot escape its effects on her.
- Navajo community — She is simultaneously of and excluded from the community; her wealth and beauty are admired, but her American-influenced behavior is judged.

## Character Arc

Slim Girl moves from calculation to genuine love — but the past she has concealed (her sexual history, her contact with American men, her independently accumulated wealth) cannot be integrated into the traditional Navajo marriage she has constructed. When Laughing Boy discovers her secrets, his response — shaped by Navajo warrior ethics — destroys them both.

Her tragedy is that she succeeds in her project of cultural return only to discover that return is incomplete and that the past cannot be erased. She learns the ritual of the sweat-bath (008-024), she embraces the life of the hogan — but she cannot undo what Americanization has made of her.

## Cultural Context

Slim Girl represents the generation of Navajo children taken to American boarding schools in the late 19th and early 20th centuries — a historical trauma that separated children from language, family, and ceremony. Her character is the novel's most explicit engagement with the cultural violence of assimilation policy. Her death is not punishment for transgression but the logical endpoint of a system that made wholeness impossible.
