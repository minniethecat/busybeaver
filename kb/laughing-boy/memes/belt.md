# Concho Belt

The concho belt is the most iconic form of Navajo silverwork in *Laughing Boy*, serving as both personal adornment and a portable display of wealth and craftsmanship.

## Description

The Navajo concho belt consists of a leather strap ornamented with concho disks — oval or circular silver plaques, often stamped or engraved with decorative patterns, sometimes set with turquoise stones. The belts are worn around the waist, with the silver pieces catching light and drawing attention to the wearer. Laughing Boy's belts attract the immediate interest of a tourist, who singles out the one with turquoise in it as the most desirable piece (002-074).

The belts are versatile and personal: a silversmith may wear his own work, displaying his skill directly on his body.

## Cultural Significance

For the Navajo silversmith, the concho belt represents the pinnacle of wearable art. Unlike a bracelet or ring, a belt is large, prominent, and requires significant silver — making it a clear marker of both wealth and technical skill. When Laughing Boy sells his belts to a tourist, the transaction carries weight beyond simple commerce: it is an exchange between cultures, mediated by a guide who translates both language and value (002-067, 002-073).

The belt with turquoise commands the highest interest and price, reflecting the Navajo aesthetic preference for combining silver with blue-green stone — a pairing that echoes the colors of sky and earth central to Navajo cosmology.

## Craftsmanship

Creating a concho belt requires the silversmith to forge or cast individual silver disks, stamp or engrave each with decorative motifs, shape and polish them, and mount them on a leather strap. The work is labor-intensive and demands consistent quality across all conchos — a single flawed piece diminishes the whole belt.

Laughing Boy is described as a belt-maker of some reputation. His assessment "This is a good belt" (002-083) is not boastfulness but a craftsman's honest evaluation of his own work.

## In the Novel

The concho belt appears at a crucial moment of cultural and economic exchange:

- **The trading encounter**: A tourist examines Laughing Boy's belts, pulling them around to inspect the silverwork. The guide translates the tourist's interest into a sale, and Laughing Boy parts with his handiwork for money that will fund his new life (002-067, 002-073).

- **The turquoise belt**: Among Laughing Boy's work, the belt with turquoise is the most prized — singled out by the tourist and commanding the highest price. It represents the peak of his craft and the value that outsiders place on traditional Navajo silverwork (002-074).

- **Status and identity**: Throughout the novel, silverwork functions as portable wealth and personal identity. The belt Laughing Boy wears and sells is not merely an object but an extension of his skill, taste, and social standing.
