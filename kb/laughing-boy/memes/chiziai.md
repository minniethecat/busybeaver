# Chiziai

Chiziai is a small Navajo settlement near the railroad in the southern part of
the Navajo country. In *Laughing Boy*, it is [[slim-girl]]'s home before and
during her marriage — a remote, isolated place where she lives alone in a house
built with American money, maintaining the double life that will ultimately
prove unsustainable. Unlike [[tse-lani]] and [[to-tlakai]], which represent
traditional Navajo community, Chiziai is a borderland — geographically and
culturally between the Navajo world and the encroaching American one.

## Geography

Chiziai lies "down by the railroad track" (002-009), placing it in the southern
Navajo country where the railroad and trading posts have altered traditional
patterns of life. It is near enough to the American town for Slim Girl to walk
in for work, yet remote enough for her house to feel, in Laughing Boy's words,
"too lonely, too strange a life; no one ever comes" (016-041).

The settlement itself is modest — a scattering of dwellings characteristic of
the dispersed Navajo settlement pattern. Slim Girl's house there is a
substantial structure by Navajo standards, built with the resources she
accumulated through her American connections.

## In the Novel

Chiziai is introduced early as Slim Girl's origin point:

> "She is called Slim Girl, I think. She comes from down by the railroad track,
> from near Chiziai, I think."
>
> （002-009）

The settlement is closely associated with Slim Girl's ambiguous status.
[[red-man]], who lives nearby, uses his proximity to Chiziai as a credential
when spreading rumors about her:

> "I live not far from Chiziai. I know, not just talk. She lives alone, she does
> no work, she is rich. The Americans make her rich, for badness."
>
> （006-039）

Laughing Boy's growing unease about Chiziai surfaces in his thoughts as their
marriage strains:

> I am not happy in the house at Chiziai. It is too lonely, too strange a life;
> no one ever comes. We see people only at dances. That American town, what is
> there there? What is this preacher's wife? The look in her face when she
> returns — I do not know. There is something wrong, always something hidden.
>
> （016-041）

A Night Chant ceremony is held at Chiziai, conducted by Mountain Singer
(014-031). After the novel's tragic climax, Laughing Boy is asked where he comes
from and answers simply: "From Chiziai" (021-064) — the place name now weighted
with loss.

## Significance

Chiziai functions in the novel as a spatial metaphor for Slim Girl's divided
self. [[tse-lani]] is the authentic Navajo world she aspires to rejoin;
[[los-palos]] is the American town she must navigate for economic survival;
Chiziai is the compromised middle ground — too far from community for protection,
too close to the American world for purity. Her decision to abandon the Chiziai
house and travel north with Laughing Boy is the novel's turning point.
