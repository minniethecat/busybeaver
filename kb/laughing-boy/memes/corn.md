# Corn

Corn (*naadą́ą́ʼ*) is the foundational food crop of the Navajo people and the central
element of their agricultural and ceremonial life. In *Laughing Boy*, corn appears in
nearly every meal — boiled, roasted, ground into bread or mush — and marks the rhythm
of domestic existence from the hogans of T'o Tlakai to Slim Girl's household at Los Palos.

## Description

The Navajo cultivate several varieties of corn, including white, blue, and yellow,
adapted to the semi-arid conditions of the Navajo country. Corn is planted in irrigated
fields along streams and washes, often alongside beans and squash — the Three Sisters of
Southwestern agriculture. The plant grows in stalks bearing one to three ears, which are
harvested in late summer and dried for year-round storage.

In the novel, corn is consumed in multiple forms: roasted ears of green corn served to
guests（002-036）, corn boiled with meat as a hearty stew（001-026）, and corn bread
baked as a portable staple（002-036）. Corn mush (*tanaashgiizh*) holds particular
ceremonial importance, used as an offering in the Night Chant ceremony（007-051）.

## Cultural Significance

Corn is sacred in Navajo cosmology. It is one of the gifts of the Holy People,
alongside other staples. Corn pollen (*tádídíín*) is used as a blessing and offering
in ceremonies, placed on the tongue of the one being blessed, sprinkled on prayer
sticks, and offered to the dawn. The agricultural cycle — planting, tending,
harvesting — structures the Navajo year and defines the settled rhythm of family life
that Laughing Boy yearns for.

In the novel, cornfields represent stability and prosperity. The irrigated strip at
Los Palos, described as "alfalfa, corn, beans, cottonwoods"（006-063）, paints a
picture of agricultural abundance. Laughing Boy, seeing the spring near Slim Girl's
home, imagines making "a good cornfield" and planting peaches（006-090） — the vision
of domestic rootedness that drives the novel's central conflict.

Corn also marks social and economic status. The ability to buy corn when needed
（003-002）is a measure of prosperity, while the hard labor of "hoeing corn"（005-007）
represents the grueling traditional life that Slim Girl seeks to escape.

## Cultivation and Preparation

Navajo corn cultivation relies on floodwater irrigation: fields are planted in areas
where seasonal rains and snowmelt naturally water the soil. Ditches are dug to direct
water, a skill Laughing Boy recognizes when he observes that "with a couple of ditches
one could make a good cornfield by that spring"（006-090）.

After harvest, corn is dried on the cob and stored in underground pits or above-ground
cribs. Dried kernels are ground on a metate to produce cornmeal, which is the basis
for corn bread, corn mush, and thickening for stews. Roasting ears of corn in the husk
over open coals is the simplest preparation and appears at the feast scene where
"two small naked boys brought ears of corn as they were roasted"（002-036）.

## In the Novel

Corn appears in almost every chapter of *Laughing Boy*, from the opening feast to the
final tragedy. At the Tsé Lani dance, Laughing Boy eats meat "boiled with corn" and
fills himself with bread（001-026）. When traveling, he carries dried corn under his
saddle for his horse（001-052）.

At Slim Girl's home, corn mush is prepared for the Night Chant ceremony, placed in a
medicine basket and set "in the correct place on the floor of the house"（007-051）.
The ceremonial use of corn marks the novel's most sacred moments — the attempt to
restore harmony through the proper rites.

Slim Girl's rejection of the cornfield life — "hoeing corn, packing firewood, growing
square across the hips and flat in the face"（005-007） — defines her ambition to live
differently, in town, earning wages rather than working the land. This tension between
agricultural tradition and modern aspiration is one of the novel's central themes.
