# Silver Bracelet

The silver bracelet is the most intimately personal form of Navajo jewelry in *Laughing Boy* — worn on the wrist, given as a courtship token, and serving as a canvas for the silversmith's artistic vision.

## Description

Navajo silver bracelets are hand-forged from coin silver or ingot, shaped into bands or cuffs, and ornamented with stamping, engraving, repoussé, or stone settings. Bracelets may be thin and delicate or heavy and substantial. They are worn singly or stacked in multiples, producing a distinctive musical clinking when the wearer moves.

Laughing Boy imagines creating a bracelet of "thin silver, with stars surrounded by stone-knife-edge" (001-052), describing both the weight of the piece and its decorative motif — a combination that reflects the Navajo aesthetic of precision and restraint.

## Cultural Significance

The bracelet carries romantic and social meaning beyond its material value. When Laughing Boy rides toward Tsé Lani and imagines the bracelet he will make, he is thinking of the girl he hopes to impress — the bracelet is both a work of art and a courtship offering (001-052).

For the silversmith, a bracelet demonstrates technical mastery in miniature. Unlike a belt, which spreads ornamentation across many conchos, a bracelet concentrates all the silversmith's skill into a compact form. Every hammer mark, every stamped line, every stone setting is immediately visible on the wrist.

Slim Girl wears bracelets that click and flash as she moves, her "braceletted wrists" (003-077) announcing her presence and her wealth before she speaks.

## Craftsmanship

Making a Navajo silver bracelet begins with melting and forging the silver into a flat band. The silversmith then stamps, engraves, or files decorative patterns into the surface, shapes the band around a mandrel, and — if the design calls for it — sets turquoise or other stones. The final piece is polished to the characteristic soft gleam of hand-worked silver.

Laughing Boy's design — "four smooth bars running together, and a turquoise in the middle" — describes a classic split-band style with a central stone setting (001-050). He has already made up his mind about the design; his only uncertainty is whether he can acquire enough silver to execute it.

## In the Novel

- **A craftsman's daydream**: Riding across the desert, Laughing Boy mentally designs a bracelet for the girl he will meet at the dance. The bracelet exists first in his imagination — "thin silver, with stars surrounded by stone-knife-edge" — a gift that will demonstrate both his skill and his intention (001-052).

- **Slim Girl's adornment**: Slim Girl's braceletted wrists are part of her self-presentation as a woman of means and taste. Her jewelry — rings, bracelets, necklaces — is both armor and advertisement (003-077).

- **The craft decision**: Laughing Boy reflects that "when he had started a bracelet; he worried whether it ought to have been a necklace" (009-045) — a craftsman's self-doubt familiar to any artist who has committed to a design.

## Notable Passages

> He was thinking about a bracelet he should make, with four smooth bars running together, and a turquoise in the middle — if he could get the silver.

> He would make a bracelet about her, thin silver, with stars surrounded by stone-knife-edge.

> All this — she touched her rings, and shook her braceletted wrists — "and much more is mine."

> Laughing Boy, having done his thinking and made up his mind, did not mull over his decision, any more than when he had started a bracelet; he worried whether it ought to have been a necklace.
