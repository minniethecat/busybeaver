# Bow-Guard (Ketoh)

The bow-guard (ketoh) is a Navajo leather and silver wrist guard worn on the left
forearm during archery — a practical piece that protects the wrist from the
bowstring's snap while also serving as a canvas for the silversmith's art. In
*Laughing Boy*, the protagonist's bow-guard is his most personal creation: a
silver and turquoise ornament that he makes with his own hands, bets on races,
gives as a love token, and which ultimately serves as the mark by which his
uncle recognizes him. Through the bow-guard, the novel traces themes of
craftsmanship, identity, and the meaning of objects made with care.

## Description

A bow-guard consists of a leather backing that wraps around the left wrist,
faced with a shaped piece of silver, often set with turquoise. The silverwork is
hammered, filed, and polished — the same techniques used in all Navajo
silversmithing. The silver face protects the inner wrist from the lash of the
bowstring when an arrow is released, while the ornamentation transforms a
utilitarian object into a statement of the wearer's taste and the maker's skill.

Laughing Boy's bow-guard, which he makes himself at T'o Tlakai, is described
throughout the novel through its effects on others. Young men stop on the road
"to look at his bow-guard, which he himself had made" (004-045). The trader
Bow's Son recognizes its quality: "It is a fine bow-guard. If you make many
things like that, you will get rich" (003-020). Slim Girl notices its detailed
workmanship: "towards her he turned the lovingly worked silver on the back of
it" (009-061).

> He felt as if she were his own creation, like the bow-guard; at least he had
> selected her, as he had chosen the soft blue turquoise in the ornament.
>
> （004-067）

## Craftsmanship

The making of a bow-guard follows the same process as other Navajo silverwork.
The smith cuts his dies from iron files, hammers out bosses in depressions cut
into a hardwood board, and works the silver with an anvil — often no more than a
small piece of iron obtained from a trader. The craft is described in the
novel's extended passage on silversmithing:

> You make many things, rings, bracelets, bow-guards, necklaces, pendants,
> belts, bridles, buttons, hatbands. No two are alike, but they are all of the
> silver, or of silver and turquoise.
>
> （009-046）

Laughing Boy makes at least two bow-guards in the course of the novel. The first
is made at T'o Tlakai, his home in the North. The second — the one "with
stars-following" (020-067) — he makes at the hogan he shares with Slim Girl near
Chiziai, during the period of their married life when forge and loom work side
by side. Late in the novel, picking up a piece of marbled stone, he reflects
that "I can cut it up and polish it, and use it in rings and bow-guards"
(017-069), showing how the craft is inseparable from his way of seeing the
world.

## In the Novel

The bow-guard appears at nearly every significant turning point in Laughing
Boy's story. It is introduced as a point of pride at the Tsé Lani dance, where
others admire his work. He wagers it on a horse race: "He raced. His horse was
tired, but it won by a nose, which was just as well, since he had bet his
bow-guard" (004-073). The wager reflects both his confidence and the object's
value — it is worth ten dollars, a substantial sum in the novel's economy.

At the dance where he meets Slim Girl, the bow-guard is the one piece of
adornment he retains — "no silver belt, no jewelry, only the lucky bow-guard on
his left wrist" (008-034) — and he gives it to her as a pledge:

> "Take this bow-guard, now, to keep. By first cold moon you will hear from me
> again. My uncles will look for you, or I shall."
>
> （008-036）

Slim Girl, watching him sing after a moment of crisis, fixes on the object:
"While he sang, she looked at his hands locked across his knees, at the
bow-guard on his left wrist. When he loosed his shaft, the bowstring had snapped
down across the leather on the inside; towards her he turned the lovingly worked
silver on the back of it" (009-061). The bow-guard becomes, for her, a focus of
her growing love.

When Laughing Boy places everything except his bow-guard in bets before his most
important race (004-082), the exception marks the object as beyond price — not
even his gambling impulse can risk it. Even in the burial scene, the bow-guard
is singled out: "He changed his old bow-guard for one he had made at their
house" (020-054), leaving the newer one with Slim Girl's body among the silver
and turquoise offerings.

In the novel's final recognition scene, an old man — Laughing Boy's uncle —
pulls at the bow-guard on his wrist:

> The work was good. He touched the silver with his right hand, to show the
> four-points-with-three-points design. But it was not that design, it was not
> the bow-guard he made at T'o Tlakai, it was that one he made at their hogan,
> the one with stars-following.
>
> （020-067）

The distinction between the two bow-guards — the one from his youth and the one
from his marriage — traces the arc of Laughing Boy's life, from the young
silversmith showing off at dances to the widower returning home, carrying the
weight of love and loss.
