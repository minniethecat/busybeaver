# Loom

The Navajo upright loom (*dah iistłʼǫ́*) is the essential tool of the Navajo weaving
tradition, producing the blankets and rugs for which the People are renowned.
In *Laughing Boy*, the loom is Slim Girl's instrument of craft and independence,
the rhythmic counterpoint to Laughing Boy's forge, and a symbol of the domestic
partnership they strive to build.

## Description

The Navajo loom is a vertical frame loom, traditionally set up outdoors under a
shade arbor or inside the hogan. It consists of two upright poles set in the ground,
with upper and lower crossbeams (*łéʼétsoh*) to which the warp threads are tied.
The warp — of hand-spun cotton or wool string — runs vertically under tension.
The weft — of hand-spun, hand-dyed wool yarn — is passed horizontally through the
warp using a batten (*łéʼé nághaʼí*) to pack each row tight.

The loom produces textiles of remarkable density and complexity. The weaver sits
on the ground before the loom, working from the bottom upward, building the design
row by row. A characteristic sound of Navajo domestic life is "the rhythmic
thump-thump-thump of a weaver pounding down the threads in her loom"（003-050）.

## Cultural Significance

Weaving is historically women's work among the Navajo, taught by Spider Woman
(*Na'ashjé'ii Asdzáá*) in the mythological past. The loom and its products embody
the values of patience, skill, and aesthetic vision. A fine blanket represents
months of labor — shearing sheep, cleaning and carding wool, spinning yarn,
dyeing with vegetal colors, and weaving — each step an exercise in discipline.

In the novel's domestic economy, the loom and the forge are paired as equal
contributions. Slim Girl weaves; Laughing Boy makes jewelry. Their shared vision
of prosperity rests on "your forge and my loom"（009-041）, a phrase that
encapsulates their partnership. The threat of separation is framed in these
terms: "Is anything in the world worth the risk of separating your forge and
my loom?"

## Setup and Craft

Setting up a loom requires skill: the frame must be square and rigid, the warp
threads evenly spaced and under consistent tension. Slim Girl, after years away
from traditional life, must relearn weaving. She asks Laughing Boy to "get me
the wood for a loom"（008-011）, acknowledging that "it is a long time since I
have woven, but I have beautiful blankets in my mind."

Her first attempt after the long hiatus produces a "sorry object" — "all crooked,
irregular, and full of holes"（009-007） — that she hides from Laughing Boy in
shame. But persistence and the rhythms of domestic contentment restore her skill.
With practice, she "really had something to tell her loom"（009-017）, and the
ensuing days are described as "long, flawless days when they were at home together,
he at his forge, she at her loom"（009-031）.

## In the Novel

The loom first appears as a sound — the "thump-thump-thump" of weaving heard in the
background of a Navajo settlement（003-050）, an auditory marker of settled domestic
life. Later, it becomes central to Slim Girl's character arc. Her acquisition of a
loom marks her commitment to traditional craft and her partnership with Laughing
Boy（006-081）.

The loom also represents the tension between tradition and change. Slim Girl's
initial failure at weaving（009-007）reflects her dislocation from Navajo life,
shaped by years at the mission school. Her eventual mastery of the loom — producing
beautiful blankets — signals her reclamation of Navajo identity on her own terms.

The loom's symbolic pairing with the forge reaches its fullest expression in the
novel's vision of domestic harmony: "he at his forge, she at her loom, time passing
with the thump of the batten, the ring of the hammer or rasp of the file"（009-031）.
This image of productive partnership is the novel's ideal of a good life.
