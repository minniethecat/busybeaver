## Biography

Hippolyte Bernheim (1840–1919) was a French physician and a prominent member of the Nancy School of hypnosis. Alongside Ambroise-Auguste Liébeault, he advanced the theory that hypnotic phenomena result from suggestion rather than from physiological abnormality — a position that opposed the Salpêtrière School of Jean-Martin Charcot (006-014).

## Role in Psychoanalysis

In 1889, Freud traveled to Nancy to witness the demonstrations of Liébeault and Bernheim. Freud was deeply impressed by their experiments, particularly one in which Bernheim demonstrated that hypnotic experiences, though seemingly forgotten upon waking, could be recovered through persistent urging (006-014). This demonstration of unconscious mental processes later informed Freud's technique of free association. Freud also described a key experiment: if Bernheim gave a hypnotized subject the post-hypnotic injunction to perform an action after waking, the subject would carry it out without conscious awareness of the motive — a phenomenon Freud cited in his discussion of unconscious determination of behavior (018-011).

Freud translated Bernheim's book on suggestion into German and for years practiced hypnotic treatment based on Bernheim's methods before developing psychoanalysis proper (028-004). Freud credited Bernheim with recognizing the universality of suggestibility, though he noted that Bernheim could never define suggestion or its origin, failing to recognize its dependence on sexuality and the libido (027-040, 028-002).
