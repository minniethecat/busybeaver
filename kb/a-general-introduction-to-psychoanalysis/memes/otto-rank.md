# Otto Rank

## Overview

Otto Rank (1884–1939) was an Austrian psychoanalyst, writer, and one of Sigmund Freud's earliest and most valued collaborators. A member of Freud's inner circle in Vienna, Rank served as secretary of the Vienna Psychoanalytic Society and co-editor of the leading psychoanalytic journals of the period.

Freud cited Rank's work extensively in the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, drawing on his studies of mythology, dream interpretation, and literary analysis to illustrate key psychoanalytic concepts.

## Contributions to Psychoanalysis

Rank's work spanned multiple domains within the early psychoanalytic movement. In the *Introductory Lectures*, Freud referenced Rank's investigations in three principal areas:

### Mythological Studies

In his study of the birth of the hero, Rank submitted myths from diverse cultures to psychoanalytic interpretation. Freud noted that Rank "has recognized that these are representations of the conditions of human birth, as we all understand them."

The exposure of the infant hero in a basket on the water symbolizing the act of birth, with water representing the amniotic fluid. This mythological analysis demonstrated how universal symbols in folklore and myth could be decoded through psychoanalytic methods. (010-040)

### Dream Interpretation

Rank contributed several important observations to the study of dreams. In a careful study, he showed how associative processes may be observed in dreams, the freest play being given to the dreamer's associations. (013-024, 013-035)

Rank published an example of dream interpretation that Freud cited in the lectures as particularly instructive. He observed that the dream's peculiar character makes it a fruitful subject to pursue in the study of symbolism. (008-017)

### Literary and Linguistic Analysis

Rank applied psychoanalytic principles to the study of language and literature. His analysis of a slip of the tongue in Shakespeare's *The Merchant of Venice* was described by Freud as "a still more impressive example" of the mechanisms of parapraxes operating in literature. (002-042)

In a valuable book on literary production, Rank demonstrated that dramatists had made use of the same themes that psychoanalysis later discovered. (021-042)

> In a valuable book, Otto Rank has shown that the dramatists of all times have taken their materials principally from the Oedipus-and incest-complexes, with their variations and disguises.
>
> (021-042)

## Role in the Psychoanalytic Movement

Rank occupied a central position in the institutional life of early psychoanalysis. Along with Hanns Sachs, he edited the psychoanalytic journals through which psychoanalysis was brought into connection with the other sciences of the mind. (010-058)

His editorial work helped establish psychoanalysis as a recognized intellectual discipline and facilitated the dissemination of psychoanalytic ideas beyond the clinical setting into art, literature, and mythology.

> You will understand how a journal can have grown on psychoanalytic soil, the sole purpose of which is the furtherance of these relationships. This is the Imago founded in 1912 and edited by Hanns Sachs and Otto Rank. In all of these relations, psychoanalysis is first and foremost the giving, less often the receiving, part.
>
> (010-059)

## Relationship with Freud

Freud initially regarded Rank as one of his most promising disciples and entrusted him with significant responsibilities within the psychoanalytic movement.

The frequency with which Freud cited Rank in the *Introductory Lectures* attests to the high regard in which his work was held during this period.

However, Rank later developed theoretical differences with Freud, particularly concerning the role of the birth trauma in psychopathology, leading to his eventual departure from the Freudian circle in the 1920s.
