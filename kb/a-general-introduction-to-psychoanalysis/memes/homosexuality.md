# Homosexuality

## Overview

Homosexuality, referred to by Freud as *inversion* in his *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, is a form of sexual object-choice in which desire is directed toward persons of the same sex. Freud approaches inversion not as a sign of degeneracy but as a variation in psychosexual development, one that reveals fundamental truths about the nature of human sexuality. (020-003) He distinguishes between *manifest* homosexuality (the conscious, exclusive invert) and *latent* homosexuality (unconscious homosexual tendencies present in all neurotics and, by implication, in all human beings). (020-014) The theory of narcissism provides the deepest explanation: "Homosexual choice of object is originally more natural to narcism than the heterosexual." (026-039)

## Definition

Freud defines inversion in Lecture 20:

> The inclusion of the following factors in our concept "sexual" amply suffices for all practical purposes in ordinary life: the contrast between the sexes, the attainment of sexual excitement, the function of reproduction, the characteristic of an indecency that must be kept concealed. But this is no longer satisfactory to science. For through careful examinations, rendered possible only by the sacrifices and the unselfishness of the subjects, we have come in contact with groups of human beings whose sexual life deviates strikingly from the average. One group among them, the "perverse," have, as it were, crossed off the difference between the sexes from their program. Only the same sex can...
>
> (020-003)


Freud defines inversion as a condition in which only the same sex can arouse sexual desire, while the opposite sex "no longer serve as objects for their sexual desires, and in extreme cases, become a subject for disgust." (020-003) The invert has "foregone any participation in reproduction" and is described as having a changed sexual *object*, as distinguished from those perversions in which the sexual *aim* has changed. (020-005)

Inversion belongs to the first group of perversions — those involving a change of object — alongside fetishism and paedophilia. Freud rejects the characterization of inverts as a "third sex" or the "elect of humanity," noting that they "comprise just as many worthless second-rate individuals as those who possess a different sexual organization." (020-003) The crucial psychoanalytic finding is that homosexuality is not a rare anomaly but a universal latent disposition.

## Development in the Lectures

### Classification as a Perversion (Lecture 20)

In Lecture 20, "The Sexual Life of Man," Freud discusses homosexuality under the rubric of the perversions. He describes the invert as one who has "crossed off the difference between the sexes from their program." (020-003) Inverts are often persons "of high physical, intellectual and ethical development, who are affected only with this one portentous abnormality." (020-003) They are classified under perversions of the sexual *object* — "those whose sexual object has changed" — rather than perversions of the sexual *aim*. (020-005)

### Latent Inversion and Neurosis (Lecture 20)

Freud makes a decisive claim that transforms the clinical significance of homosexuality: "the claim of rareness made by the homosexualists or the inverted immediately collapses when we learn that in the case of no single neurotic do we fail to obtain evidence of homosexual tendencies, and that in a considerable number of symptoms we find the expression of this latent inversion." (020-014) Those who call themselves homosexualists "are the conscious and manifest inverts, but their number is as nothing before the latent homosexualists." (020-014) Freud is "forced to regard the desire for an object of one's own sex as a universal aberration of erotic life." (020-014)

### Paranoia as Defense against Homosexuality (Lecture 26)

Freud develops the connection between homosexuality and paranoia in Lecture 26. Through clinical observation, he concluded that *paranoia persecutoria* "is the form in which the individual guards himself against a homosexual tendency that has become too powerful." (026-033) The transformation from affection to hate in paranoia expresses "the transformation of libidinous impulse into fear, which is a regularly recurring result of the process of suppression." (026-033)

Freud provides a clinical illustration: a young physician who threatened the life of his former best friend after a single night of "complete sexual intercourse." The patient had never felt attracted to women; his psychosis broke out when he succeeded in giving "complete gratification to a woman" for the first time, triggering a sensation "like a sharp incision" around his skull — interpreted as persecution by the former friend. (026-035)

### Narcissism and Object-Choice (Lecture 26)

Freud traces homosexuality to the narcissistic type of object-choice. "Homosexual choice of object is originally more natural to narcism than the heterosexual." (026-039) When a "strong and undesirable homosexual impulse" must be thwarted, "the way back to narcism is made especially easy." (026-039) The libido, after the narcissistic stage, can proceed according to two types of object-choice: the *narcissistic* type, which selects a person similar to the ego, and the *dependent* type, which selects persons valuable for satisfying non-libidinal needs. "A strong fixation of the libido to the narcistic type of object-choice" is present "when there is a disposition toward manifest homosexuality." (026-039)

### Transformation of Object-Choice (Lecture 27)

In Lecture 27, Freud briefly notes that psychoanalytic therapy can influence homosexual object-choice: analysis reveals "the extent to which manifest homosexuality retreats before the methods by which these instinct-components are guided into other channels." (027-032) This is consistent with the thesis that homosexuality represents a fixation at the narcissistic stage of libidinal development, from which redirection is possible.

## Key Examples

### The Young Physician (Paranoia Case)

A young physician threatened the life of his former best friend, the son of a university professor, attributing "truly devilish intentions" to him. Investigation revealed a history of homosexual intimacy between the two friends, including a night of "complete sexual intercourse." The patient experienced no attraction to women until a fiancée broke their engagement due to his lack of affection. His psychosis erupted when he succeeded in having sex with a woman — a "strange pain which cut around his skull like a sharp incision" — interpreted as persecution. His hand was "paralyzed" when he had the opportunity to shoot his former friend, revealing the persistence of the original affection. (026-035)

### The Jealous Woman (Heterosexual Paranoia)

In an apparent counterexample, a young girl believed she was followed by a man with whom she had been intimate. Closer investigation revealed that "she had, as a matter of fact, first laid these maniacal imputations at the door of a woman" — a mother-substitute — and only later transferred the delusion to the man. The condition that the persecutor must be of the same sex "was also originally maintained in this instance," confirming rather than contradicting the theory. (026-038)

### Manifestation in Hysterical Symptoms

Freud notes that neurotic women commonly produce symptoms "impersonating the actions of men." While this "cannot be ascribed directly to homosexuality, it is certainly concerned with its prerequisites." (020-014) Hysterical symptoms express "all those tendencies termed perverse, which seek to represent the genitals through other organs," and the study of such symptoms reveals the universal latent presence of homosexual tendencies. (020-015)
