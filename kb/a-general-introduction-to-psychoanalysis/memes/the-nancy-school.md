# The Nancy School

## Overview

The Nancy School was a school of hypnotism and suggestion founded in the 1880s by [[Hippolyte Bernheim]] and Ambroise-Auguste Liébault in Nancy, France. It represented one pole of the late nineteenth-century debate on the nature of hypnosis, opposing [[Jean-Martin Charcot]]'s Paris-based Salpêtrière School. Freud visited Nancy in 1889, witnessing Bernheim's demonstrations (028-004), and the Nancy School's emphasis on psychological (rather than neurological) mechanisms influenced his early conception of [[Psychoanalysis]].

## History

The Nancy School emerged from the clinical practice of Liébault, a country doctor who treated patients with hypnotic suggestion free of charge. Bernheim, a professor of medicine at the University of Nancy, was initially skeptical but became a convert after witnessing Liébault's results. Together they developed a theory of hypnosis based on **suggestion** rather than neurological predisposition — the key point of contention with Charcot's school in Paris.

By the late 1880s, the Nancy School had gained international recognition, attracting visitors from across Europe and America. Freud was among them, and the experience left a lasting impression on his thinking.

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Freud's relationship to the Nancy School is one of influence followed by transformation. In the *Introductory Lectures*, he recalls:

> "I witnessed also the astonishing demonstrations of Siebault [Liébault] and Bernheim in Nancy." (006-014)

The Nancy School's key insight — that psychological phenomena can produce and remove physical symptoms — became a cornerstone of psychoanalytic theory. However, Freud eventually moved beyond hypnosis, developing [[Free Association]] as a more reliable method for accessing [[The Unconscious]].

Bernheim's work on post-hypnotic suggestion was particularly influential. In these experiments, a subject given a command under hypnosis would carry it out after waking, confabulating a rational reason for the action. This demonstrated that unconscious mental processes could determine conscious behavior — a principle that became fundamental to psychoanalysis.

## Significance

The Nancy School represents a crucial intermediate step between pre-Freudian hypnotism and psychoanalysis proper. Its demonstration that psychological processes can determine physical symptoms prepared the ground for Freud's more radical claim: that *unconscious* psychological processes, not merely hypnotic suggestion, are the determinants of neurotic symptoms.

Freud's acknowledgment of the Nancy School in the lectures — "the demonstrations of Siebault and Bernheim" (006-014) — places hypnosis in the genealogy of psychoanalysis, even as he insists on the superiority of his own method. The transition from hypnotic suggestion → cathartic method → free association traces the evolution from the Nancy School to mature psychoanalysis.
