# Fixation

## Overview

[[Fixation]] is one of the foundational concepts in Freud's theory of the neuroses, designating the tendency of [[Libido|libido]] to become arrested at particular stages, objects, or component drives during psychosexual development. A fixation point functions as a latent vulnerability: when the adult psyche encounters privation or frustration, the libido regresses to these earlier points of attachment, reactivating infantile modes of satisfaction and generating neurotic symptoms. Freud introduces the concept through the clinical observation that neurotic patients appear "fixated upon some very definite part of their past" (018-002), unable to free themselves from it.

## Definition

Freud defines fixation as a developmental arrest of the libido at a particular stage, object, or component drive:

> "The tenacity with which the libido clings to certain tendencies and objects... we designate as the fixation of the libido." (022-022)

This definition implies three essential properties:

1. **Developmental arrest** — During [[Infantile Sexuality]], the libido passes through a series of stages, each with its own erotogenic zone and mode of satisfaction. At any point, an excessive gratification or frustration may cause the libido to become stuck at that stage (021-010).

2. **Constitutional and experiential determination** — Fixation is the product of both inherited constitutional factors (the strength of particular component drives) and early experiences. Freud warns against taking sides in a "most unnecessary controversy" over which factor predominates (022-020).

3. **Latent vulnerability** — A fixation point is not itself pathological. It becomes pathogenic only when the adult psyche, faced with privation, regresses to it: "the stronger the fixations in the path of development, the more readily will the function give way before external difficulties by regressing to those fixations" (022-005).

## Development in the Lectures

### Lecture XVIII: Traumatic Fixation

Freud first introduces fixation in Lecture XVIII ("Traumatic Fixation—the Unconscious") through clinical observation:

> "In the first place, both patients give us the impression of being fixated upon some very definite part of their past; they are unable to free themselves from it and for that reason they are estranged from both present and future." (018-002)

The model here is traumatic: just as a physical trauma can produce lasting somatic effects, a psychic trauma produces a fixation that the patient cannot overcome. The young female patient, for instance, is fixated upon an early erotic experience that "apparently passed by harmless" yet left a permanent mark on her psychic development (018-007).

### Lecture XXII: Fixation and Regression

In Lecture XXII ("Development and Regression—Etiology"), Freud elaborates the relationship between fixation and [[Regression]]:

> "For your comprehension of the neuroses it is necessary to keep in mind this connection between fixation and regression." (022-005)

He argues that fixation and regression are complementary — fixation creates the points to which regression returns, and regression reveals where the fixations lie. The libido, confronted with external obstacles (privation), retreats to the fixation points established during development:

> "In this conflict the libido is enticed by fixations which it has left behind at earlier stages of development." (023-004)

Freud proposes that each individual possesses "possibly even numerous libido fixations in earlier developmental phases" (022-019), and the specific pattern of these fixations determines the individual's predisposition to particular forms of [[Neurosis]].

## Clinical Significance

The concept of fixation provides the bridge between developmental history and adult psychopathology. The clinical sequence follows a three-factor model:

| Factor | Mechanism | Role |
|--------|-----------|------|
| Privation | External or internal obstacles block satisfaction (022-034) | Precipitating cause |
| Fixation | Libido arrested at infantile stages/objects (022-022) | Predisposing cause |
| Regression | Libido retreats to fixation points (022-004) | Pathogenic mechanism |

Fixations established in the course of development offer "an evasion" when adult satisfaction is blocked (023-006). The specific symptoms that emerge depend on the location of the fixation point: oral fixations produce different symptomatology than anal or phallic fixations.

Freud also distinguishes fixation from other vicissitudes of the libido. Unlike [[Sublimation]], which desexualizes the libido and redirects it toward non-sexual aims, and unlike [[Transference]], which redirects it onto the analyst, fixation represents an arrest — a failure of the libido to progress beyond a developmental milestone.
