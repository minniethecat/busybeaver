# Constitutional Factor

## Overview

The [[Constitutional Factor]] (*konstitutioneller Faktor*) is, in Freud's etiological theory of the neuroses, the hereditary and innate component that contributes to the development of [[Neurosis|neurotic illness]]. It stands in a "complemental series" with accidental (traumatic) experiences: the stronger the constitutional factor, the less trauma is required to produce a neurosis, and vice versa. Freud introduces this concept in Lecture XXIII as part of a systematic framework for understanding why different individuals respond differently to similar pathogenic experiences. "The libido fixation of adults," he writes, "which we have referred to as representative of the constitutional factor in the etiological comparison of the neuroses, can be thought of as divisible into two separate factors, the inherited disposition and the tendency acquired in early childhood" (023-011).

## Definition

The constitutional factor is formally defined through the etiological equation that structures Freud's mature theory of neurosis:

> "Cause of the neurosis = Disposition as determined by libido fixation + accidental experiences (traumatic element)" (023-012)

The constitutional factor contributes to neurosis in two distinct registers:

1. **Quantitative** — "The pathogenic significance of the constitutional factors is guided by how much more of a given component impulse is present in the predisposition over and above that of another; one can even conceive the predispositions of all men to be qualitatively the same and to be differentiated only by these quantitative conditions" (023-041). It is not which impulses one has, but how strong they are relative to one another.

2. **Hereditary** — The constitution is transmitted phylogenetically. "The hereditary sexual constitution provides us with manifold tendencies, varying with the special emphasis given one or the other component of the instinct, either individually or in combination" (023-012).

## Development in the Lectures

### The Complemental Series

Freud's most original contribution to the concept of constitutional predisposition is the idea of the **complemental series** (*Ergänzungsreihe*). Constitution and experience are not additive but complementary — they fill in for each other:

> "With the factor of infantile experience, there is again built up a complementary series within the sexual constitution which is perfectly comparable with our first series" (023-012).

If the constitutional factor is strong, a relatively mild traumatic experience may trigger a neurosis; if the constitutional factor is weak, an extreme trauma is required. This relativizes both purely constitutional and purely environmental theories of neurosis.

### Limits of Pedagogy

The constitutional factor sets a hard limit on the prophylactic ambitions of pedagogy. Even the most careful education cannot eliminate the constitutional contribution:

> "So long as we keep our attention fixed essentially on the infantile sexual experiences, we readily come to believe we have done everything for the prophylaxis of nervous afflictions when we have seen to it that this development is retarded, and that the child is spared this type of experience. Yet we already know that the constitutional factor makes itself felt powerfully." (023-021)

### Constitutional Immaturity

In the developmental context, constitutional factors also determine the timing and vulnerability of the child:

> "One of these goals is the result of the constitutional immaturity of the child, who is not yet capable of procreation" (021-018).

### The Quantitative View of Health

In the concluding lectures, Freud returns to the constitutional factor in framing the distinction between health and neurosis:

> "The difference between nervous health and neurosis is entirely a practical one which is determined by the available capacity for enjoyment and accomplishment retained by the individual. It varies presumably with the relative proportion of the energy totals which have remained free and those which have been bound by suppressions, and is quantitative rather than qualitative." (028-024)

This quantitative conception grounds the therapeutic optimism of psychoanalysis: if neurosis is a matter of quantitative balance rather than qualitative defect, then it can be shifted through treatment.

## Relationship to Other Etiological Factors

| Factor | Nature | Role in Neurosis |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| **Constitutional factor** | Innate, hereditary, quantitative | Predisposing — sets the threshold |
| **Infantile experience** | Accidental, early | Precipitating — actualizes the predisposition |
| **Adult trauma** | Accidental, later | Triggering — overwhelms the defenses |
| **Libido fixation** | Acquired in childhood | Mediating — the developmental point to which regression occurs |

## Clinical Significance

The constitutional factor is not a counsel of therapeutic pessimism. Freud explicitly rejects the conclusion that neurosis is inevitable or untreatable when constitution is strong:

> "I do not have to remind you that this conception is the theoretical basis for the certainty that neuroses can be cured, despite their foundation in constitutional disposition." (028-024)

The constitutional factor constrains but does not determine outcome. The therapeutic task is to strengthen the ego's capacity to manage the libidinal demands that constitution imposes. [[Psychoanalysis]] works not by altering constitution but by bringing repressed material into consciousness where "a real decision can be reached."
