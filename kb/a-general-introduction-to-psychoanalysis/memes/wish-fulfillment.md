# Wish Fulfillment

**Wish fulfillment** is the thesis, central to Freud's theory of dreams, that **every dream represents the fulfillment of an unconscious wish**. This is the essential character of the dream: whatever else a dream may be, it is first and foremost a disguised or undisguised satisfaction of a wish that has been repressed from consciousness. (008-026)

## The Universality of the Thesis

Freud held that the wish-fulfilling character of dreams is universal. Even dreams that appear to contradict the thesis — anxiety dreams, punishment dreams, dreams of frustration — are, upon analysis, revealed to be wish fulfillments at the level of the latent dream thoughts: (008-028) (008-034)

> [008-026] The dream is a wish fulfillment and a hallucinatory experience. These are its two main characteristics.

The hallucinatory character of the dream is inseparable from its wish-fulfilling function. The dream does not merely think the wish — it **hallucinates** its fulfillment, presenting the desired state of affairs as a perceptual reality. This regression from thought to perception is made possible by the suspension of motor activity in sleep and the withdrawal of attention from the external world.

## Infantile Dreams as the Model

The simplest and most transparent wish fulfillments are found in the dreams of **children**. The infantile dream requires no interpretation; the wish and its fulfillment are directly visible: (008-021) (008-023)

- A child who was denied a desired food dreams of eating it
- A child who missed an excursion dreams of making the journey
- A child who was forced to go to bed early dreams of staying up

> [008-023] The dream testifies to it only the beautiful wish fulfillment. There is indeed no proverb that says the dream comes from the stomach, but it is similarly a wish fulfillment.

These infantile dreams establish the paradigm: the dream is a reaction to a **daytime wish** that was not satisfied in waking life. Sleep preserves the wish, and the dream provides its hallucinatory satisfaction. (008-021)

## Day Dreams and Wish Fulfillment

The **day dream** (phantasy) provides a waking analogue to the dream's wish-fulfilling function. Day dreams are conscious or preconscious phantasies in which the subject imagines the fulfillment of ambitious or erotic wishes: (005-042) (008-021)

> [008-021] Now these day dreams are actual wish fulfillments, fulfillments of ambitious or erotic wishes, which we know well, but which we omit to mention in our waking thoughts.

The day dream and the night dream share the same psychological structure: both are wish fulfillments. The difference is that the night dream employs the mechanisms of the **dream-work** — condensation, displacement, considerations of representability, and secondary revision — to disguise the wish and evade the dream censor, while the day dream, being acknowledged as mere phantasy, requires less disguise. (005-044)

The hero of the day dream is always the individual himself — a narcissistic core that is preserved in the dream but often obscured by the dream-work. (005-045)

## Disguised Wish Fulfillment

Not all dreams announce themselves as wish fulfillments. Dreams that are painful, frightening, or apparently meaningless are **disguised wish fulfillments** — the wish has been subjected to the dream censor and appears only after distortion: (008-034) (011-004)

> [011-004] Dreams that are recognized as evident wish fulfillments nevertheless have undergone distortion.

The dream censor operates precisely because the wish in question is objectionable — typically sexual or aggressive in nature. The censor permits the wish to achieve hallucinatory satisfaction only on condition that it be sufficiently disguised. The manifest dream — what the dreamer remembers — is thus a **compromise formation**: it satisfies the unconscious wish while appeasing the censor's demand for disguise. (011-004)

## The Wish in Neurotic Symptoms

The concept of wish fulfillment extends beyond dreams to illuminate the nature of **neurotic symptoms**. Just as the dream is the disguised fulfillment of a repressed wish, so the symptom is a compromise that simultaneously expresses and punishes the unconscious wish: (028-022)

> [028-022] In the form of the wish fulfillment they show us what wish impulses have been subjected to the distortion of the dream censor and are capable of producing symptom formation.

Both dreams and symptoms are products of the same unconscious mechanisms — condensation, displacement, symbolization — and both represent attempts at wish fulfillment under the pressure of repression. The dream is the "normal" psychosis of sleep; the symptom is its waking equivalent.

## Wish, Sleep, and the Function of the Dream

The dream not only fulfills a wish but also **preserves sleep**. The unconscious wish — if it were to press directly toward consciousness and motility — would wake the sleeper. By providing hallucinatory satisfaction, the dream binds the excitation of the wish, permitting sleep to continue: (014-002)

> [014-002] The dream is the reaction of the psychic apparatus to the psychic stimuli acting upon it, a reaction that discharges these stimuli by means of a wish fulfillment.

Freud described the dream as the **guardian of sleep**: it takes the disturbing impulse and weaves it into a wish-fulfilling scenario that satisfies the impulse sufficiently to prevent awakening.

## Later Refinements

Freud later acknowledged that the wish-fulfillment thesis required qualification in certain cases:

- **Anxiety dreams** — where the wish fulfillment fails and the repressed wish breaks through in undisguised form, producing terror rather than satisfaction
- **Traumatic dreams** — where the dream repeats a traumatic experience, apparently contradicting the pleasure principle
- **Punishment dreams** — where the dream fulfills a wish of the **super-ego** (the critical agency) to punish the ego, rather than a wish of the id

These exceptions, however, were understood as refinements rather than refutations of the thesis. The traumatic dream, for instance, represents a failure of the wish-fulfilling function — the traumatic excitation is too great to be bound by the dream-work, and the dream regresses to a mere repetition of the trauma. (008-028)
