# Paranoia

## Overview

Paranoia is classified by Freud in the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis* as a chronic systematic insanity — one of the narcissistic psychoneuroses. Unlike the transference neuroses (hysteria and compulsion neurosis), paranoia is characterized by the withdrawal of libido from external objects and its redirection onto the ego, accompanied by the construction of elaborate delusional systems. Freud's analysis of paranoia represents one of his most ambitious extensions of psychoanalytic theory from the individual neuroses to the psychotic conditions. (026-032)

## Definition

Paranoia is a chronic mental illness characterized by the development of a stable, internally coherent delusional system. Unlike the fluctuating symptoms of hysteria or the ritualistic patterns of compulsion neurosis, paranoia produces a fixed interpretation of reality that the patient defends with systematic logic. Freud describes it as "chronic systematic insanity," emphasizing both its persistence and its internal consistency. (026-032)

The types of paranoia are described according to their content:

1. **Megalomania**: Delusions of grandeur, where the patient believes themselves to possess extraordinary powers, wealth, or importance.
2. **Erotomania**: Delusions of being loved, typically by a person of higher social status.
3. **Persecutory paranoia**: Delusions of being persecuted, watched, or conspired against — the most common form.
4. **Jealousy paranoia**: Delusions of a partner's infidelity, often supported by elaborate "evidence." (026-033)

Freud compares and contrasts paranoia with melancholia, noting that both involve a withdrawal of libido from external objects, but with different outcomes — the paranoid projects the conflict outward, while the melancholic turns it inward against the self. (026-041)

> The disease of paranoia, chronic systematic insanity, is given a very uncertain position by the attempts at classification of present-day psychiatry. There is no doubt of its close relationship to dementia praecox. I once was so bold as to propose that paranoia and dementia praecox could be classed together under the common name of paraphrenia.
>
> (026-032)

> The types of paranoia are described according to their content as: megalomania, the mania of persecution, eroto mania, mania of jealousy, etc.
>
> (026-033)

## Development in the Lectures

### Early Appearance in the Parapraxes

The concept of paranoia first appears early in the Lectures, in the discussion of parapraxes. Freud notes that in paranoia, the utilization of trivial details — what others would dismiss as meaningless accidents — is carried to an extreme. The paranoid patient finds profound significance in every minor error or coincidence, a tendency that Freud compares to the psychoanalytic method itself, which also values the apparently trivial. (004-022)

### Narcissistic Neurosis

In Lectures 24–26, Freud situates paranoia within the category of narcissistic psychoneuroses. Unlike the transference neuroses, where the libido remains attached to objects (albeit in a distorted form), in paranoia the libido is withdrawn from objects entirely and redirected onto the ego. This narcissistic withdrawal makes the paranoid patient inaccessible to psychoanalytic therapy — they cannot form a transference relationship with the analyst. (024-009)

### The Mechanism of Projection

Freud identifies projection as the central mechanism in paranoia. An unacceptable internal impulse — typically a homosexual wish — is disowned and attributed to an external persecutor. The classic formula Freud proposes: an unconscious homosexual wish ("I love him") is transformed through negation ("I do not love him — I hate him") and then projection ("He hates me — He persecutes me"). (026-035)

In Lecture 27, Freud connects this mechanism to the broader dynamics of the unconscious. By projecting the unconscious into the external world, the paranoid achieves a kind of "cure" — the internal conflict is resolved by relocating it outside the self, but at the cost of a delusional world-construction. (027-010)

### Paranoia and Dream-Work

In Lecture 28, Freud draws a parallel between paranoid delusion-formation and dream-work. Both involve the processes of condensation and displacement, and both construct a reality that satisfies unconscious wishes. The paranoid delusion is, in this sense, a "waking dream" — a systematic construction that, like the dream, represents a wish-fulfillment achieved through distortion. (028-015)

## Key Examples

### The Jealous Paranoiac

Freud discusses the case of a man suffering from delusions of jealousy. Analysis reveals that the patient's own repressed homosexual impulses toward his rival are the true source of the delusion. The formula "She loves him" is a projection that conceals the unconscious thought "I love him" — the jealousy serves as a defense against homoerotic desire. (026-035)

### The Persecutory Delusion

A patient believes himself to be persecuted by a former friend. Analysis uncovers that the patient had intense unconscious affection for this friend. The affection, unacceptable to the patient's ego, is transformed via negation ("I do not love him") and projection ("He hates me") into a persecutory belief. The delusion "protects" the patient from the more painful acknowledgment of his own desires. (027-010)

## Critical Reception

Freud's theory of paranoia — particularly the postulated link to repressed homosexuality — has been among the most controversial aspects of his work. While the specific etiological claim about homosexuality has been largely abandoned in contemporary psychiatry, Freud's broader insight about the role of projection as a defense mechanism has proven enduring. Modern cognitive models of paranoia emphasize attributional biases and "theory of mind" deficits rather than repressed drives, but continue to recognize the defensive function of paranoid cognition that Freud first identified.
