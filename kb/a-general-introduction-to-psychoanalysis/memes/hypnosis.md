# Hypnosis

## Overview

[[Hypnosis]] occupies a paradoxical place in the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*: it is simultaneously the technique from which psychoanalysis was born and the technique that psychoanalysis had to overcome. Freud traces his own intellectual development from the use of hypnotic suggestion (learned from [[Josef Breuer]] and the [[The Nancy School|Nancy School]]) to the invention of [[Free Association]] as a superior method. In the *Introductory Lectures*, hypnosis serves as a foil against which the distinctive advantages of psychoanalytic technique are articulated.

## Definition

Freud characterizes hypnosis primarily through its relationship to resistance and suggestion:

> "Hypnosis drives back the resistance and makes a certain psychic territory accessible, but against this resistance on the boundary it is powerless." (019-014)

The mechanism is temporary suspension rather than permanent resolution. Hypnosis does not dissolve [[Resistance]]; it merely pushes it aside, and the resistance returns intact once the hypnotic state is lifted. This limitation is precisely what distinguishes hypnosis from psychoanalysis proper.

## Development in the Lectures

### The Breuer Period

Freud first mentions hypnosis in the context of an anecdote about a patient who could not recall what had occurred during the hypnotic state:

> "He maintained he was unable to remember what had happened to him during the hypnosis." (006-014)

This observation — that material arising under hypnosis is not integrated into waking consciousness — already hints at the limitations of the method.

The definitive statement on hypnosis as therapeutic precursor comes in Lecture XIX:

> "Breuer's first patient was treated by means of hypnosis." (019-014)

Freud acknowledges that Breuer's cathartic method, conducted under hypnosis, achieved genuine therapeutic results. The patient's symptoms were traced to forgotten traumatic experiences, and their abreaction under hypnosis brought relief. But Freud came to see that the results were often temporary and dependent on the patient's suggestibility.

### The Turn Away from Hypnosis

In Lecture XXVII, Freud explains why psychoanalysis abandoned hypnosis:

> "You realize that we have excluded hypnosis from our technique of neurosis treatment." (027-040)

Three reasons are given:

1. **Unreliable results** — "We depend on the condition of suggestibility" (028-009). The therapeutic effect depends on the patient's hypnotizability, which varies enormously between individuals.

2. **Failure to resolve resistance** — Hypnosis bypasses rather than works through resistance. "In hypnosis we depend on the condition of being able to induce the hypnotic state, and this we cannot always do" (028-009).

3. **Concealment of the transference** — Hypnosis conceals the [[Transference]] relationship behind the hypnotist's authority, preventing it from becoming an object of analytic work (028-034).

### Suggestion as Residual Problem

Freud acknowledges that suggestion — the core mechanism of hypnosis — cannot be entirely eliminated from any form of psychotherapy:

> "Results as with suggestion under hypnosis." (028-002)

Even psychoanalysis, which replaces hypnotic suggestion with [[Free Association]], must contend with the suggestibility of the patient. The difference is that psychoanalysis makes suggestion itself an object of investigation, tracing its roots to the transference.

## Clinical Significance

| Aspect | Hypnosis | Psychoanalysis |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Mechanism** | Suggestion and abreaction under altered consciousness | Free association and interpretation in waking consciousness |
| **Resistance** | Bypassed temporarily | Worked through systematically |
| **Transference** | Concealed behind authority | Made explicit and analyzed |
| **Patient suggestibility** | Prerequisite for success | Object of investigation |
| **Permanence of results** | Often temporary | Aims at structural change |

Freud summarizes the evolution:

> "The medical profession toward hypnosis, treatment by suggestion, which is practiced today, is practically nothing more than a residue of the hypnotic method." (028-034)

## Historical Context

Hypnosis entered psychoanalysis through two channels:

- **[[Josef Breuer]]** (1842–1925) — Viennese physician who discovered the "talking cure" while treating [[Anna O.]] (Bertha Pappenheim) with hypnosis. Breuer found that under hypnosis the patient could recall the circumstances in which her symptoms first arose, and that the recollection itself brought relief (019-014).

- **[[The Nancy School]]** — The French school of hypnotherapy led by Hippolyte Bernheim and Ambroise-Auguste Liébeault, which demonstrated the power of suggestion. Freud visited Nancy in 1889 and observed Bernheim's demonstrations that hypnotic suggestions could produce and remove symptoms. "Psychotherapy by hypnosis" (029-001) is the umbrella term under which both traditions operated.
