## Biography

Wilhelm Wundt (1832–1920) was a German philosopher and psychologist who founded the first laboratory for experimental psychology at the University of Leipzig. He held "for decades the prestige of a most advantageous academic chair" and attracted gifted students from around the world (P01-005). His development of the doctrine of experimental psychology established the dominant paradigm that psychoanalysis would later challenge and supplement (P01-006).

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Wundt's school proposed the association-experiment, in which subjects responded as quickly as possible to a given stimulus word. Researchers studied reaction time, the nature of the reaction, and possible mistakes upon repetition. Freud noted that this experimental method played "a noteworthy role in the history of psychoanalysis" — for it was the Zurich School, under Bleuler and Jung, who adapted Wundt's method by asking subjects to explain unusual reactions through further associations, thereby revealing the determining influence of unconscious complexes (006-028).

Wundt also offered an observation on slips of the tongue: they occur when bodily fatigue causes the tendency to association to gain the upper hand over intended speech. Freud cited this view but found it contradicted by cases where fatigue was not a factor (003-026). In discussing dream theories, Freud referred to Wundt's description of dreams as characterized by disintegration of association and suspension of critical faculty — a view Freud considered merely descriptive rather than interpretive (005-011).

The preface to the English translation notes that while Wundt enjoyed academic prestige, Freud's focus on the unconscious, the abnormal, sex, and affectivity represented "a great present and a yet greater future" (P01-006).
