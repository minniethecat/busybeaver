# Phobia

## Overview

Phobia is a form of neurotic fear in which anxiety becomes bound to a specific object or situation rather than remaining free-floating. In his *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, Freud classifies phobias under **anxiety hysteria** (*Angsthysterie*), distinguishing them from both conversion hysteria and general anxiety neurosis. Unlike the diffuse, unattached fear of anxiety neurosis, phobic fear is "psychologically more circumscribed and bound up with certain objects or situations." (025-017) The phobia proper is not merely an exaggerated version of normal fear but a neurotic formation in which suppressed libido is transformed into fear and projected onto an external object — a process Freud analogizes to the construction of a fortification against an inner danger.

## Definition

Freud defines phobia as a neurotic condition characterized by fear attached to specific objects or situations, produced by a two-phase process: first, the suppression of libido and its transformation into fear linked to an external danger; second, the construction of precautions and safety devices to avoid contact with that danger. (025-048) The phobia is "comparable to a fortification against outer danger, which is represented by the much feared libido." (025-048) Its fundamental weakness is that "the fort has been strengthened from without and has remained vulnerable within" — the projection of peril from the libido into the environment is never fully successful. (025-049)

Freud groups phobias into three classes based on their relation to real danger:

1. **Exaggerated normal fears**: Objects and situations that have something gruesome for normal people too, such as snakes, spiders, or thunderstorms — exaggerated in intensity but not incomprehensible. (025-018)
2. **Situation-phobias**: Dangers that are real but habitually belittled by normal people — fear of trains, ships, bridges, solitude, crowds, and enclosed spaces. The intensity, not the content, is what appears strange. (025-019)
3. **Unintelligible phobias**: Fears that bear no recognizable relation to danger — an adult man afraid to cross a street, a woman terrified by a cat or mouse. These "cannot possibly be a question of the heightening of common human antipathies." (025-020)

The key distinction between free-floating anxiety and phobic fear is that they are "independent of one another" — the strongest general anxiety need not manifest itself in phobias, and persons hemmed in by agoraphobia can be entirely free of pessimistic expectant fear. (025-021)

> A second form of fear in contrast to the one we have just described is psychologically more circumscribed and bound up with certain objects or situations. It is the fear of the manifold and frequently very peculiar phobias. Stanley Hall, the distinguished American psychologist, has recently taken the trouble to present a whole series of these phobias in gorgeous Greek terminology.
>
> (025-017)

> The two forms of fear that have been described, free-floating fear and the fear which is bound up with phobias, are independent of one another.
>
> (025-020)

## Development in the Lectures

### Classification as Anxiety Hysteria (Lecture 25)

Freud introduces the phobias during his discussion of neurotic fear in Lecture 25, classifying them as a group under anxiety hysteria and regarding them "as a condition closely related to the well-known conversion hysteria." (025-022) This classification is significant: unlike conversion hysteria where the suppressed impulse is converted into a somatic symptom, in anxiety hysteria the suppressed libido is discharged directly as fear and attached to an external object.

### The Two-Phase Model

In Lecture 25, Freud elaborates the two-phase structure of phobic neurosis. The first phase effects the suppression of libido and its transition to fear, which is joined to an external danger. The second phase consists in "building up all those precautions and safety devices which are to prevent contact with this danger which is dealt with as an external fact." (025-048) The first phase corresponds to the ego's flight from the libido it regards as dangerous; the phobia proper is the fortification built against the now-externalized danger.

### Infantile Origins (Lecture 25)

Freud traces the origin of phobias to infantile fear. The first situation phobias of children are darkness and solitude — both involving the absence of the mother. (025-040) A child afraid of the dark calls for someone to speak, explaining "If someone speaks, it is brighter" — the yearning felt in darkness is converted into the fear of darkness. (025-041) Freud's thesis is that infantile fear "has very little to do with real fear, but is closely related to the neurotic fear of adults. It originates in unemployed libido and replaces the object of love that is lacking by an external object or situation." (025-043)

Infantile phobias are "not only the prototypes but the direct prerequisite and prelude to later phobias." (025-043) Every hysteria phobia can be traced to a childhood fear of which it is a continuation, even if the content and name differ. The difference lies in mechanism: in the adult, mere libido in abeyance is insufficient to produce transformation into fear — suppression is required. "The regression to infantile phobia is the bridge where the transformation of libido into fear is conveniently effected." (025-045)

### Symbolic and Phylogenetic Content

Freud acknowledges that some phobic contents are "especially qualified to be objects of fear through phylogenetic inheritance, as Stanley Hall has emphasized." (025-050) Many objects of fear establish connections with danger only by *symbolic relations* — the manifest content of the phobia has "about the same importance for it as the manifest dream facade has for the dream." (025-050)

### Clinical Illustration: The Agoraphobic Girl (Lecture 28)

In Lecture 28, Freud briefly references a clinical case: a girl suffering from agoraphobia who also developed a compulsion neurosis and a sleep ritual. The case illustrates the connection between phobic anxiety and the broader neurotic disposition, where "the same constitutional factor" underlies multiple neurotic formations. (028-029)

## Key Examples

### Agoraphobia — Fear of Open Spaces

Agoraphobia (also called topophobia, fear of spaces) is the most extensively discussed phobic syndrome in the lectures. Freud notes it is "a condition which we are not inclined to count as a compulsion but which we designate as a situation phobia." (017-031) The agoraphobic person is relieved of fear when accompanied across the feared space — behaviour that reveals the infantile prototype. Some phobias like fear of squares are acquired only in later life and signify serious illness, while others like fear of darkness exist from the very beginning and appear rather as peculiarities. (025-021)

### Snakephobia — A Phylogenetic Inheritance

Freud cites snake phobia as an example of a fear common to all human beings, noting Charles Darwin's famous account of being unable to control his fear of a snake even while knowing he was separated from it by thick glass. (025-018) Such phobias have "something gruesome for normal people too" and are candidates for phylogenetic inheritance.

### Childhood Darkness Phobia

The prototypical childhood phobia — fear of the dark — exemplifies the transformation of libido into fear. The child, missing the mother, converts the yearning of unemployed libido into fear of darkness. A child calling to the next room, "Auntie, talk to me, I am afraid," and explaining "If someone speaks, it is brighter," demonstrates that it is not darkness per se that is feared but separation from the love object. (025-041)
