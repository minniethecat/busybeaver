# Identification

## Overview

Identification (*Identifizierung*) is a fundamental psychological mechanism in Freudian psychoanalysis, central to both normal ego development and the formation of neurotic symptoms. In the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, Freud distinguishes two principal forms: narcissistic identification and hysterical identification. Through identification, the ego takes on characteristics of another person — a process that shapes personality from earliest childhood and, in pathological forms, underlies the structure of neurotic symptoms. (026-042)

## Definition

Identification is the process by which the ego assimilates or incorporates attributes, qualities, or characteristics of another person. It is one of the earliest expressions of an emotional tie to another human being and plays a decisive role in the formation of the ego-ideal.

Freud distinguishes two primary varieties of identification in the Lectures:

1. **Narcissistic identification**: The ego identifies with a person who has been abandoned as a love-object. The ego "introjects" the lost object, becoming like it as a substitute for having it. This is the more regressive form and is closely tied to the oral phase of libidinal development. (026-042)

2. **Hysterical identification**: The ego identifies with another person on the basis of a shared element — typically a shared symptom or a shared fantasy. Unlike narcissistic identification, this does not require the abandonment of the object; rather, the identification supplements the object-cathexis. (026-043)

Freud notes that psychoanalysis still had much to learn about the precise mechanisms and varieties of identification at the time of the Lectures. (026-044)

> Through a process which we designate as "narcistic identification" the object is built up within the ego itself, is, so to say, projected upon the ego. The personal ego is now treated in the same manner as the abandoned object, and suffers all the aggression and expressions of revenge on the part of the superego which spring from the sadistic component of the ego.
>
> (026-042)

## Development in the Lectures

### Role in Ego Development

Identification is first discussed in the Lectures in the context of the ego's development. Freud argues that the ego is not a unitary entity from birth but is built up through a series of identifications with important figures — primarily the parents. The child's earliest emotional attachments take the form of identification before they become true object-choices. (001-012) (001-016)

### Prehistory of the Oedipus Complex

In Lecture 21, Freud situates identification within the prehistory of the Oedipus complex. Before the child develops a proper object-cathexis toward the mother, there is a phase of identification with the father — the child wants to *be* the father before wanting to *have* the mother. This early identification later becomes the nucleus of the ego-ideal and the basis for social feelings. (021-008)

### Identification in Symptom Formation

In Lectures 25–26, Freud elaborates on the role of identification in symptom formation. In hysteria, a symptom may arise when the patient identifies with another person who has the same symptom — not through imitation, but through an unconscious identification based on a shared fantasy or wish. (019-034) (026-043)

In compulsion neurosis, identification operates differently: the patient identifies with the prohibitions of the father, internalizing them as the harsh superego. (017-019)

### Narcissistic Neuroses

In the final Lectures (26–27), Freud discusses identification in the context of the narcissistic neuroses — conditions where the libido is withdrawn from external objects and directed back onto the ego. In these conditions, identification replaces object-love entirely. The melancholic's self-reproaches, Freud argues, are actually reproaches against an identified lost object. (026-042) (027-011)

## Key Examples

### The Compulsive Patient's Identification

Freud describes a compulsive patient whose symptom was a ceremonial bedtime ritual. Through analysis, it was revealed that the ritual condensed an identification with both parents: the compulsion repeated the father's strict discipline while simultaneously enacting the mother's anxious care. The symptom was a compromise formation built on dual identifications. (017-019)

### Hysterical Identification in the Barracks

Freud relates a famous clinical vignette: a group of women in a barracks develop the same hysterical symptom. Rather than simple imitation, each woman identified with the first patient through a shared unconscious fantasy — typically a sexual wish — that the symptom expressed. The symptom is an "identification symptom." (019-034)

### Melancholic Identification

Freud extends the logic of narcissistic identification to melancholia. When a loved object is lost, the melancholic ego identifies with the lost object. The harsh self-criticisms characteristic of melancholia are, in reality, reproaches originally directed at the lost love-object, now turned inward through identification. (026-042) (027-011)
