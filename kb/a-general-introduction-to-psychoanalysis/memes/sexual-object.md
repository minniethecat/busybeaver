# Sexual Object

In Freud's theory of the sexual instinct, the **sexual object** (*Sexualobjekt*) is the person (or thing) toward which the instinct is directed. It is distinguished from the **sexual aim** (*Sexualziel*), which is the act or satisfaction the instinct strives to achieve. This distinction, introduced in the *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality* and elaborated throughout the *Introductory Lectures*, is foundational to the psychoanalytic understanding of [[Sexuality]], [[Perversion]], and [[Homosexuality]].

## The Object–Aim Distinction

Freud separates the sexual instinct into two components: the object (the target of attraction) and the aim (the form of satisfaction). The object can be of the opposite sex — the normal developmental outcome — or of the same sex, as in inversion. The aim can likewise vary from genital union to oral, anal, or scopophilic activities. Freud notes that symbolic thinking frequently substitutes one for the other: "there are symbols which are used almost entirely to express sexual objects and processes" (010-057).

> In the dream the symbols are used almost entirely to express sexual objects and processes.
>
> (010-057)

## Variations in Object Choice

### Inversion (Homosexuality)

The most prominent variation of the sexual object is inversion, in which the object is a person of the same sex. In the lectures, Freud classifies inverts "on the one hand, into those whose sexual object has changed, as is the case with exclusive inverts" (020-005). The object change is not a simple preference but a reorganization of the instinct itself, with roots in the bisexual disposition Freud posits for all human beings.

### The Incestuous Object

Freud argues that the first sexual objects are incestuous — the mother and father. The Oedipus complex describes the child's object-cathexis toward the opposite-sex parent. In later development, this primary object choice must be renounced. However, Freud observes that "the return to primary, incestuous sexual objects is quite regular" (022-012) in cases of regression, where libido withdraws from later attachments and re-invests the earliest object ties.

## Object Choice and Libido Development

The development of object choice follows a trajectory from autoerotism through narcissism to object-love. In the normal course, "the infant's libido is for centering on the sexual object in later life" (020-030) — meaning the child's early experiences of satisfaction establish patterns that profoundly shape adult object choice. The Oedipus complex provides the prototype: the child's first object cathexes become the template for all later attachments.

In maturity, the libido must "transfer his libido to a foreign sexual object" (021-041), detaching from the incestuous objects of childhood. The capacity to make this transfer — to find new objects outside the family — is a measure of psychological health. Failure to do so results in fixation or regression.

## Clinical Significance

Disturbances in object choice are central to the neuroses. Freud traces many symptoms to unresolved conflicts over object cathexis: the hysteric remains fixated on an infantile object, the obsessional neurotic defends against a forbidden object choice, and the paraphrenic withdraws object-cathexis entirely. The concept of the sexual object thus bridges Freud's theory of normal development and his theory of psychopathology.
