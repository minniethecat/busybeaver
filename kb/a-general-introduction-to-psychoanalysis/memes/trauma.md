# Trauma

**Trauma** (psychic trauma) is an experience that exceeds the ego's capacity for mastery, producing a **fixation of the libido** at the moment of the traumatic event. Freud identified traumatic experience as one of the two essential etiological factors in the neuroses, operating alongside hereditary predisposition in what he termed the "complementary series." (018-005) (023-012)

## Traumatic Neuroses

The clearest model of traumatic causation is found in the **traumatic neuroses** — conditions that arise following overwhelming experiences such as war, accidents, or natural disasters: (018-005) (024-009)

> [018-005] The traumatic neuroses are grounded in a fixation upon the moment of the trauma.

The traumatic neurotic is unable to extricate himself from the traumatic experience. He relives it in dreams, in waking reverie, and in behavioral repetitions, as though the event were perpetually present. (018-007) This fixation upon the moment of the trauma is the hallmark of the traumatic neurosis.

In traumatic neuroses, the traumatic event produces a **breach in the stimulus barrier** — the protective shield that ordinarily filters external excitations. The ego is flooded with quantities of excitation it cannot bind, and the subsequent symptoms represent repeated attempts at mastering this excess. (024-010)

## The Complementary Series

Freud placed trauma within a broader etiological framework — the **complementary series**: (023-012)

| Factor | Role |
|--------|------|
| Hereditary sexual constitution | Predisposes the individual to specific forms of neurosis |
| Infantile experiences | Produce fixations at particular developmental points |
| **Accidental (traumatic) experiences** | The precipitating cause in adult life |

The significance of trauma depends on its interaction with the constitutional factor: a strong constitution may weather severe trauma without developing a neurosis, while a vulnerable constitution may succumb to experiences that would not be traumatic for others. (023-018)

## Trauma and Infantile Sexuality

Freud extended the concept of trauma beyond the dramatic single event. The sexual experiences of childhood, even those that appear trivial to the adult observer, may carry a **deferred traumatic effect**: (020-040) (022-020)

> [022-020] The neurotic ailment is the product of certain harmful (traumatic) impressions; in particular, of premature sexual experiences.

An experience in childhood may not be traumatic at the time it occurs, but becomes traumatic retrospectively when the child, after puberty, can understand its sexual significance. This concept of **deferred action** (*Nachträglichkeit*) is essential: the memory of the event, not the event itself, exerts the traumatic force. (023-020)

The "traumatic" element in infantile sexuality works through fixation: (023-010)

> [023-010] The researches on the neuroses show that sexual experiences in childhood are in a position to cause a traumatic effect.

These infantile sexual experiences produce **fixation points** to which the libido later regresses when confronted with a frustration in adult life. (022-003)

## Fixation and the Mechanism of Trauma

The traumatic experience exerts its pathogenic effect through the mechanism of **fixation**. The libido becomes arrested at the moment of the trauma, unable to proceed to normal satisfactions. When a later frustration blocks the path of adult sexual gratification, the libido regresses to these earlier fixation points: (018-005) (022-004)

> [018-005] The traumatic neuroses are grounded in a fixation upon the moment of the trauma. These patients regularly repeat the traumatic situation in their dreams.

This is why Freud could say that the traumatic neuroses provide "the most favorable and transparent approach" to understanding the etiology of all neuroses. The mechanism visible in traumatic neuroses — fixation, repetition, and failed mastery — operates in disguised form in the psychoneuroses as well. (024-009)

## Trauma in Relation to the Oedipus Complex

The most significant infantile "trauma" in Freud's later formulation is not a discrete event but the entire experience of the **Oedipus complex** — the child's desire for the opposite-sex parent and rivalry with the same-sex parent. The repression of these wishes, their ultimate failure, and the child's entry into the latency period constitute a universal developmental trauma that shapes the structure of the mind.

## Clinical Implications

The recognition of trauma's role has direct consequences for psychoanalytic treatment:

1. The analyst must trace symptoms back to their traumatic origins in the patient's history
2. The patient's **repetition** of traumatic patterns in the transference is not resistance to remembering but a form of remembering through action
3. Recovery requires the patient to work through the traumatic fixation, not merely to recall the event intellectually
4. The therapeutic task is to help the ego bind the traumatic excitation that has remained unmastered — to transform traumatic memory into narrative memory
