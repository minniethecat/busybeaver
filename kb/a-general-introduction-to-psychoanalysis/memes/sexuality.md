# Sexuality

## Overview

[[Sexuality]], in the psychoanalytic sense, is one of the most radical — and most misunderstood — of Freud's conceptual innovations. In the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, Freud expands the term far beyond its everyday restriction to genital reproductive activity. Sexuality, for Freud, names the entire domain of pleasurable bodily excitation that begins in infancy, fragments into component drives (oral, anal, phallic, voyeuristic, sadistic), and undergoes a complex developmental history before culminating — ideally — in the genital primacy of adulthood. This expanded conception is the foundation upon which the theories of [[Libido]], [[Neurosis]], and [[Infantile Sexuality]] are built.

## Definition

Freud introduces his expanded conception of sexuality in Lecture XX by contrasting it with the popular view that equates sexuality solely with reproduction:

> "You commit the error of confusing sexuality with reproduction and thereby bar the approach to the understanding of the perversions and of the neuroses." (020-023)

The psychoanalytic definition has three essential dimensions:

1. **Developmental scope** — Sexuality does not commence at puberty but begins in earliest infancy. Freud insists that "it is an error to take it for granted that sexuality commences with the ripening of the genitals" (013-026). The suckling infant's oral pleasure is already sexual in the psychoanalytic sense (020-028).

2. **Component-drive architecture** — Sexuality is not a unitary instinct but a composite of partial drives, each seeking its own organ-pleasure. "Infantile sexuality arises in connection with certain erogenous zones on the body surface" (020-031). These component drives operate independently before being organized under genital primacy.

3. **Relation to perversion** — Normal adult sexuality is not the opposite of perversion but its developmental successor. "Perverse sexuality, in brief, is nothing more than infantile sexuality magnified and separated into its component parts" (020-021).

## Development in the Lectures

### Part I: Early Hints

The concept of sexuality first appears indirectly in the discussion of [[Parapraxes|parapraxes]]. Freud notes that many slips of the tongue betray sexual content, hinting at a reservoir of unconscious sexual wishes pressing for expression. The question "because of its relationship to sexuality?" (010-062) foreshadows the centrality of the sexual factor that will dominate Part III.

### Part II: Dreams and Sexual Wishes

In the dream theory of Part II, sexuality emerges more explicitly. Freud's thesis that every dream is the fulfillment of an unconscious wish reveals that the majority of dream-wishes are of infantile sexual origin. The [[Dream Censor|dream censorship]] that distorts dream content operates with particular severity against sexual wishes, transforming them into the bizarre imagery of the manifest dream. [[Symbolism]] in dreams is predominantly sexual symbolism.

### Part III: The Theory of Sexuality

The full exposition of the psychoanalytic theory of sexuality occupies Lectures XX through XXII:

**Lecture XX ("The Sexual Life of Man")** introduces the concepts of perversion, infantile sexuality, and the component drives. Freud demonstrates that "the differences between manifest homosexuality and the normal attitude are not so great as they appear" (020-014) and that "those apparently not concerned with sexuality are discovered as bound to it" (020-016).

**Lecture XXI ("Development of the Libido and Sexual Organizations")** traces the developmental history of sexuality from infancy through the pre-genital organizations (oral, sadistic-anal, phallic) to genital primacy. Freud emphasizes that "the perversions justify the assertion that sexuality and reproduction are not coincident" (021-003).

**Lecture XXII ("Aspects of Development and Regression")** examines how the sexual instincts come into conflict with the ego, producing the conditions for neurosis. "The conflict between the ego and sexuality" (022-029) is presented as the central pathogenic mechanism.

### The Anthropological Dimension

Freud concludes that the task of restraining sexuality is a perennial human problem:

> "The task of restraining this sexuality is never easy; it succeeds here too well, there too poorly. The original plasticity of man and his capacity for education have bound up this transformation most intimately with the prehistory of civilization." (020-024)

## Clinical Significance

The expanded concept of sexuality has direct clinical consequences:

| Clinical Domain | Implication |
|-----------------|-------------|
| **Etiology of neurosis** | "Neuroses arise from sexuality" — specifically from the conflict between sexual wishes and the ego's resistance (022-031) |
| **Symptom formation** | Symptoms represent compromise formations between repressed sexual impulses and defensive forces (023-009) |
| **Therapy** | The analytic task is not to advocate "giving sexuality full reign" (027-003) but to bring the sexual conflict into consciousness where it can be resolved |
| **Sublimation** | The redirection of sexual energy toward non-sexual aims provides the energetic basis for cultural achievement (020-031) |

Freud explicitly warns against the therapeutic misinterpretation that psychoanalysis recommends sexual license:

> "You must not gather that we recommend giving full sway to sexuality. You must seek another solution for the conflict between the claims of sexuality and those of the ego." (027-009–027-010)
