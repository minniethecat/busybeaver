## Biography

Jean-Martin Charcot (1825–1893) was a French neurologist and professor at the Salpêtrière Hospital in Paris. He was one of the most distinguished medical figures of his time and a major influence on the young Sigmund Freud, who studied under him in 1885–1886.

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Freud held Charcot in high regard and referred to him as "my teacher Charcot" in the lectures. Freud recalled Charcot's admonition when students dismissed unpleasant findings: "That doesn't prevent them from *existing*" — a lesson in scientific objectivity that Freud invoked when defending dream interpretation against critics who found its results embarrassing or distasteful. Freud cited Charcot's maxim to argue that "one must be humble, one must keep personal preferences and antipathies in the background, if one wishes to discover the realities of the world" (009-029).

Charcot's work on hysteria and hypnosis at the Salpêtrière provided an important foundation for Freud's early investigations, though Freud later diverged from Charcot's neurological approach in favor of psychological explanation.
