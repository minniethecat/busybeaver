# Oedipus Complex

## Overview

The [[Oedipus Complex]] (*Ödipuskomplex*) is, in Freud's own assessment, the nuclear complex of every [[Neurosis|neurosis]] — the central drama of human psychosexual development that shapes the child's earliest object-relations and lays the foundation for all subsequent psychic conflict. Named after the mythical Greek king Oedipus who unwittingly killed his father and married his mother, the complex describes the universal childhood situation in which the child harbors erotic wishes toward the parent of the opposite sex and hostile, rivalrous impulses toward the parent of the same sex. Freud introduced the concept in the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis* as the culmination of his theory of dream interpretation and the bridge to the general theory of the neuroses. The Oedipus complex is not merely one complex among many; it is "the nuclear complex," the organizing node around which all other psychic formations crystallize.

## Freud's Introduction of the Concept

### Discovery Through Dreams

The Oedipus complex first appears in the *Introductory Lectures* in the context of dream interpretation, where Freud observes that certain dreams — particularly dreams of the death of a parent — are not anomalous but universal:

> "We designate as the Oedipus-complex, because this myth realizes with only a slight softening the two extreme wishes which result from the situation of the son." (013-022)

The myth of Oedipus provides the name because it presents in purified narrative form the two fundamental wishes: the death of the father and the sexual possession of the mother.

> "Furthermore, the Oedipus-complex is more or less well-developed in every human being." (013-023)

Freud is emphatic that this is not a pathological formation but a universal developmental phase.

### The Resistance to Discovery

Freud acknowledges the resistance that the Oedipus complex provokes:

> "On the contrary, psychoanalysis has been reproached for its discovery of the Oedipus-complex." (013-024)

He notes that the very hostility the concept arouses is itself evidence of its validity — a defense against the recognition of unconscious wishes that every human being has experienced:

> "We may also ascribe this Oedipus-complex to those dreamers who were so fortunate as to escape later conflict with their parents." (013-025)

## Elaboration in the Theory of the Neuroses

In Lecture XXI, Freud returns to the Oedipus complex with greater clinical depth.

### The Nuclear Complex

> "Everything we understand by the Oedipus complex which has come to have this meaning for us — the nuclear complex of the neuroses." (021-027)

Freud designates the Oedipus complex as the "nuclear complex" — the core around which all neurotic formations are organized.

### The Two Component Wishes

The complex comprises two fundamental wishes, corresponding to the two great crimes of Oedipus:

> "The mother is one of the sins of Oedipus, patricide the other." (021-038)

These are, in the child's unconscious, the wish to possess the mother sexually and the wish to eliminate the father as rival. Both wishes are unconscious and undergo varying degrees of [[Repression|repression]] as development proceeds.

### The Positive and Negative Forms

> "In this sense the Oedipus-complex is correctly designated as the nuclear complex." (021-041)

Freud recognizes that the Oedipus complex can take a "positive" form (love for the opposite-sex parent, rivalry with the same-sex parent) and a "negative" or inverted form (love for the same-sex parent, rivalry with the opposite-sex parent). In practice, every individual exhibits some mixture of both forms — what later psychoanalysts would call the "complete Oedipus complex."

### The Complex in Culture

> "The two guilty wishes of Oedipus were recognized long before the time of psychoanalysis." (021-043)

Freud argues that the Oedipus complex finds expression in art, literature, and religion — citing Sophocles' *Oedipus Rex* and Shakespeare's *Hamlet* as artistic elaborations of the same unconscious theme:

> "The mother-wife of Oedipus shall not have reminded us of the dream which the mother appears as the..." (021-044)

## The Fate of the Complex

### Resolution and Repression

The Oedipus complex is not permanently sustained. Under normal developmental conditions, it undergoes [[Repression|repression]] — not abolition but burial in the unconscious, where it becomes the nucleus of later neurotic formations:

> "This, then, is the way in which the libido behaves toward the objects of the Oedipus-complex." (021-046)

The resolution involves the child's abandonment of the Oedipal objects and identification with the parent of the same sex — a process that founds the ego-ideal.

### Return in the Transference

> "Active in the discussion of the Oedipus-complex." (023-017)

In psychoanalytic treatment, the Oedipus complex does not remain buried. It returns in the [[Transference|transference]], where the patient re-lives the Oedipal drama with the analyst as its object. This transference re-animation is the central instrument by which the repressed Oedipal wishes are brought into consciousness and resolved.
