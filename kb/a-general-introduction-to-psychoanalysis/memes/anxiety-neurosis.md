# Anxiety Neurosis

## Overview

Anxiety Neurosis (*Angstneurose*) is one of the "actual neuroses" (*Aktualneurosen*) that Freud distinguishes from the psychoneuroses and the transference neuroses. Unlike hysteria or compulsion neurosis, the anxiety neurosis is understood to arise directly from somatic sexual disturbances — particularly coitus interruptus and sexual abstinence — rather than from unconscious psychical conflicts rooted in infantile experience. Freud introduces this category in Lectures 24 and 25 of the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, using it to illustrate the distinction between "actual" (somatic) and "psychoneurotic" (psychogenic) symptom formation.

## Definition

Freud defines the anxiety neurosis as a pure form of "true neurosis" alongside neurasthenia and hypochondria (024-025). Its essential characteristic is that the symptoms — primarily anxious expectation, general irritability, and acute anxiety attacks — are not symbolic expressions of repressed wishes, but direct physiological consequences of dammed-up libido that has not been adequately discharged. As Freud explains: "the anxiety neurosis is caused by everything which keeps the somatic sexual excitement away from the psyche, everything which interferes with its psychical elaboration" (025-026).

> In the first place we find a general condition of anxiety, a condition of free-floating fear as it were, which is ready to attach itself to any appropriate idea, to influence judgment, to give rise to expectations, in fact to seize any opportunity to make itself felt. We call this condition "expectant fear" or "anxious expectation."
>
> (025-016)

## Development in the Lectures

### First Classification (Lecture 24)

Freud first names the anxiety neurosis when outlining his classification of the neuroses. He divides them into the "true neuroses" — neurasthenia, anxiety neurosis, and hypochondria — and the psychoneuroses, which include hysteria and compulsion neurosis (024-025). He notes that the boundaries between these categories are not absolute, observing transitions "between neurasthenia and the transference neuroses, between anxiety neurosis and anxiety hysteria, but also between hypochondria and paraphrenia" (024-026).

### Etiology (Lecture 25)

In Lecture 25, Freud develops the etiology of the anxiety neurosis in detail. He states: "I have named 'anxiety neurosis' a condition which I group with the actual neuroses" (025-016). The clinical picture is characterized by anxious expectation — a free-floating anxiety ready to attach itself to any suitable occasion. Freud identifies the primary cause as the frustration of sexual excitation:

> The anxiety neurosis is caused by everything which keeps the somatic sexual excitement away from the psyche, everything which interferes with its psychical elaboration. The symptoms of anxiety neurosis are substitutes, you might say, for the omitted specific actions following upon sexual excitation. (025-026)

He emphasizes that coitus interruptus, so frequently practised as a contraceptive measure, is a common cause of the anxiety neurosis in men, and especially in women (025-026).

## Relation to Other Neuroses

The anxiety neurosis occupies a border position in Freud's nosology. Its distinction from anxiety hysteria is particularly important: in anxiety hysteria, the anxiety is bound to a specific object or situation (phobia), whereas in anxiety neurosis, it remains free-floating. Unlike the psychoneuroses, which form through the mechanism of repression and the return of the repressed, the anxiety neurosis involves a direct somatic pathway — the libido, prevented from psychical elaboration, is converted directly into anxiety.
