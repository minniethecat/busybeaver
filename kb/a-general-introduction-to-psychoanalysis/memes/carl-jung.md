# Carl Jung

## Biography

Carl Gustav Jung (1875–1961) was a Swiss psychiatrist who became one of the most significant early adherents of [[Psychoanalysis]] before developing his own school of analytical psychology. At the time of Freud's *Introductory Lectures*, Jung was a prominent member of the psychoanalytic movement, leading the Zurich school at the Burghölzli Hospital under the direction of [[Eugen Bleuler]]. His eventual break with Freud — over the nature of the [[Libido]] and the role of childhood sexuality — shaped the later lectures, in which Freud addresses Jung's criticisms without always naming him directly.

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Jung played a crucial role in extending psychoanalysis beyond the Vienna circle. At the Burghölzli Hospital in Zurich, he and Bleuler applied Freud's methods to the study of the psychoses, particularly [[Dementia Praecox]] (schizophrenia). Freud credits their work:

> "The Zurich school, under the leadership of Bleuler and Jung, gave the explanation of the so-called dementia praecox by applying to it the principles of psychoanalysis." (006-028)

This was a critical validation: the Zurich psychiatrists, as respected academic clinicians, lent credibility to psychoanalysis at a time when the Viennese medical establishment was hostile. Jung also served as the first president of the International Psychoanalytic Association (1910–1914).

## Key Contributions

### Dementia Praecox and the Psychoses

Jung's most important early contribution was extending psychoanalytic understanding from the [[Neurosis|neuroses]] to the psychoses. Freud references Jung's work on dementia praecox as evidence that psychoanalytic principles apply beyond the transference neuroses:

> "Dementia praecox, as it was called by C. G. Jung, dating from the time when this investigator was still numbered among the psychoanalysts." (017-028)

### The Concept of the Complex

Jung developed the word-association test and the concept of the "complex" — an emotionally charged cluster of ideas in the [[Unconscious]]. In the lectures, Freud notes:

> "Jung coined for it the very appropriate term 'complex.'" (023-038)

The concept of the complex became one of the most durable contributions to psychoanalytic vocabulary, adopted by Freud and later schools alike.

### Symbolism and Mythology

Jung's interest in mythology, comparative religion, and symbolism anticipated later developments in psychoanalytic cultural criticism. His emphasis on universal symbols paralleled Freud's own work on [[Dream Interpretation|dream symbolism]] while pushing it in a direction Freud would later criticize as insufficiently grounded in sexual etiology.

## Relationship with Freud

In the *Introductory Lectures*, Freud's discussion of Jung is marked by the tension of their recent break. Delivered in 1915–1917, the lectures reflect the aftermath of Jung's 1913 departure from the psychoanalytic movement:

- **Admiration for early work** — Freud gives full credit to Jung for the Zurich school's application of psychoanalysis to the psychoses (006-028, 017-028)
- **Implicit criticism** — When Freud argues that the sexual nature of the libido must not be diluted into a general "psychic energy," he is addressing Jung's reformulation without naming him directly: "If, following the example of Jung, we were to emphasize the original unity of all psychic impulses..." (026-004)

Freud's strategy in the lectures is to acknowledge Jung's contributions to *clinical* psychoanalysis while rejecting his *theoretical* revisions. This reflects Freud's broader concern: that diluting the sexual etiology would return psychoanalysis to the pre-Freudian psychology it had overcome.
