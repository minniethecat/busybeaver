# Day Dream

## Overview

The [[Day Dream]] occupies an intermediate position in Freud's psychology between conscious waking thought and the nocturnal [[Dream-Work|dream]]. It is a phantasy elaborated in the waking state, typically egoistic in content, and serving the same function as the night dream — the imaginary fulfillment of an ungratified wish. Freud treats day dreams at length in Part II of the *Introductory Lectures* because they illuminate the structure of night dreams, provide the "day residues" that supply dream material, and reveal the continuity between normal imaginative life and the phantasy formations that underlie neurotic symptoms. They are, in addition, the raw material from which the poet and artist create.

## Definition

Freud defines day dreams by contrast to night dreams in Lecture V:

> "Day dreams are phantasies — they are phenomena which we all know, and of which we have but a scanty scientific comprehension. These phantasies have received the name 'day dreams,' for they share neither of the characteristics of night dreams." (005-042–005-043)

The two characteristics of night dreams that day dreams lack are: (1) occurrence during sleep, and (2) the dreamer's belief in the reality of the experience. The day dreamer knows she is phantasying:

> "One knows that it is a phantasy, that one is not seeing but thinking something." (005-043)

## Structure and Content

The day dream is consistently egoistic:

> "The hero of the day dreams, however, is always the individual himself, either directly or through a transparent identification with another person." (005-045)

The content is determined by the dreamer's current situation and ungratified wishes. An ambitious young man dreams of professional triumphs; a person thwarted in love dreams of romantic fulfillment. The day dream weaves external occasions — a newspaper item, a passing remark — into its narrative, much as the night dream incorporates the "day residues":

> "Perhaps day dreams bear this name because of their relation to reality, in order to indicate the similarity between the two — to identify them as belonging to waking life." (005-046)

## Relation to Night Dreams

Day dreams provide the material and structure for many night dreams. In Lecture VIII, Freud notes:

> "Now these day dreams are accessible to us in great numbers. Certain very transparent phantasy formations are called day dreams." (008-021)

The relationship is twofold. First, the day dream may serve as a pre-formed narrative that the dream-work employs as its manifest content. Second, both share the same deep structure:

> "The night dream corresponds in its structure to a day dream. It has few gaps, and many of its elements are connected by an apparent logical continuity." (009-009)

The difference is that in the night dream, the wish is regressively transformed into hallucinatory sensory experience; in the day dream, it remains at the level of thought.

## Role in Symptom Formation

In Part III, Freud returns to day dreams in the context of the neuroses:

> "The most prevalent and best known products of phantasy are the so-called 'day dreams,' which we already know, pictured satisfactions of ambitious, megalomaniacal, and erotic wishes." (023-034)

The phantasies that populate day dreams are the same as those that, when repressed and subjected to the primary process, generate neurotic symptoms. The hysterical symptom, for instance, often reproduces a day-dream scenario in disguised form.

Freud notes a developmental progression — the child's play, the adult's day dream, the poet's creation, and the neurotic's symptom are all transformations of the same fundamental activity of wish-fulfillment:

> "One cannot be satisfied with the sparse day dreams which may become conscious." (023-046)

The implication is that the vast majority of phantasy activity remains unconscious, and it is this unconscious phantasy that determines the form of neurotic illness.

## Day Dreams and Artistic Creation

Freud draws a direct line from day dreaming to poetry:

> "Out of his day dreams the poet, with certain transformations, disguises, and omissions, makes the situations which he puts into his novels, his romances, and his dramas." (005-044)

The artist differs from the ordinary day dreamer in two respects: the capacity to elaborate the phantasy into a formally satisfying work, and the capacity to disguise its egoistic origin so that it gives pleasure to others. The artistic work is a socially sanctioned form of wish-fulfillment — akin to [[Sublimation|sublimation]].
