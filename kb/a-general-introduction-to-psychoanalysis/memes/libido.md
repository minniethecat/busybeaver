# Libido

## Overview

[[Libido]] is the fundamental energetic concept of [[Psychoanalysis]], designating the psychic manifestation of the sexual drive. Freud introduces the term in the *Introductory Lectures* as analogous to hunger: a somatic demand that presses upon the mind and compels psychological work. Unlike the narrow colloquial sense of "sex drive," Freudian libido encompasses all the partial drives of infantile sexuality, the perversions, and the sublimated energies of culture and art. Its vicissitudes — displacement, fixation, regression, transformation into [[Anxiety]] — provide the dynamic explanation for neurotic symptoms.

## Definition

Freud defines libido in Lecture XX by direct analogy to the instinct of nutrition:

> "Libido, analogous to hunger, is the force in which the instinct, in this case the sexual instinct, as in the case of hunger, the nutritional instinct, expresses itself." (020-027)

This definition establishes three essential properties of libido:

1. **Quantitative variability** — Libido is not a fixed quantity but can increase, decrease, and be redistributed across the psychic apparatus. The accumulation of unsatisfied libido generates the pressure that drives symptom formation (020-028).

2. **Displacement** — Libido can be redirected from its original sexual aim to non-sexual objects and activities through [[Sublimation]], providing the energetic basis for cultural and intellectual achievement (020-031).

3. **Fixation** — Libido can become attached to particular objects, stages, or component drives from which it is difficult to detach. Fixation points created during [[Infantile Sexuality]] determine the individual's vulnerability to specific forms of [[Neurosis]] (021-010).

## Development in the Lectures

### Part II: Dreams and Libidinal Wishes

The concept of libido is already implicit in the dream theory of Part II, though the term itself awaits introduction in Part III. Freud's thesis that every dream is the fulfillment of an unconscious wish points toward a reservoir of psychic energy that presses for discharge. The [[Dream Censor|dream censorship]] that distorts these wishes operates because the libidinal impulses behind them are unacceptable to the waking ego (009-018). The theory of [[Dream-Work|dream-work]] — condensation, displacement, representation — describes the transformations that libidinal energy undergoes in the passage from unconscious to conscious.

### Part III: The Neuroses

In Lecture XXI ("Development of the Libido and Sexual Organizations"), Freud traces the developmental history of the libido from infancy through adulthood:

> "The lack as well as the presence of centralization fit in well with the fact that both the perverse and the normal sexuality originated from the infantile." (021-032)

The infantile libido, unlike its adult counterpart, lacks centralization — its component drives operate independently, each seeking its own pleasurable satisfaction. Normal sexual development consists in the progressive organization of these partial drives under the primacy of the genital zone (021-035).

In Lecture XXVI ("The Libido Theory and Narcism"), Freud extends the libido concept to encompass the ego's own investment in itself:

> "We called the accumulation of energy which the ego directed towards the object of its sexual striving libido and all others, which proceeded from the instincts of self-preservation, interest." (026-007)

This introduces the crucial distinction between **object-libido** (cathexis directed outward) and **narcissistic libido** (cathexis directed toward the self). The relationship between these two pools explains the dynamics of [[Transference]], in which the patient's libido, withdrawn from its habitual objects, is redirected onto the analyst (027-018).

### The Economic Viewpoint

> "Non-satisfaction of hunger and thirst, the two most elementary instincts of self-preservation, never result in their reversal into anxiety, while the transformation of unsatisfied libido into fear is, as we have heard, one of the best known and most frequently observed phenomena." (026-003)

This asymmetry — libido can be transformed into [[Anxiety]], but hunger cannot — demonstrates the special plasticity of sexual energy and its unique role in the pathogenesis of the neuroses.

## Clinical Significance

The libido theory is a clinical tool. The formation of neurotic symptoms follows a libidinal logic:

| Stage | Mechanism | Clinical Result |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Privation | External/internal obstacles block satisfaction (022-004) | Dammed-up libido |
| Regression | Libido regresses to infantile fixation points (022-012) | Revival of early modes of satisfaction |
| Symptom formation | Regressed libido finds compromise discharge (023-013) | Neurotic symptom |

The task of psychoanalytic therapy is to liberate fixated libido so it can be brought under mature ego governance:

> "A real decision can be reached only if both meet on the same ground. To accomplish this is, I believe, the sole task of therapy." (027-012)

Freud also notes the biological dimension:

> "Sexuality is the only function of the living organism which extends beyond the individual and sees to his kinship with the species. It is undeniable that its practice does not always benefit the individual as do his other performances." (026-005)
