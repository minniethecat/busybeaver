# Psychoneurosis

## Overview

[[Psychoneurosis]] is Freud's nosological term for the neurotic disorders that arise from unconscious psychical conflict, as distinguished from the "actual neuroses" (*Aktualneurosen*) that result from contemporary disturbances in sexual physiology. This distinction, while not extensively elaborated in the *Introductory Lectures*, is fundamental to Freud's clinical classification and defines the domain of disorders accessible to psychoanalytic treatment. The psychoneuroses include [[Hysteria]], [[Compulsion|compulsion neurosis]] (obsessional neurosis), and the phobias — all conditions in which symptoms represent symbolic compromise formations between repressed wishes and defensive forces.

## Definition and Classification

Freud distinguishes psychoneuroses from actual neuroses on etiological grounds. Actual neuroses (neurasthenia, anxiety neurosis) result from current somatic factors — specifically, disturbances in the discharge of sexual excitation — and their symptoms are direct physiological expressions, not symbolic formations. In contrast, psychoneuroses are determined by unconscious psychical conflicts rooted in infantile history:

> "The sexual act, are used by psychoneurosis as the most convenient and appropriate material for symptom formation" (024-026)

> A noteworthy connection between the symptoms of the true neuroses and the psychoneuroses adds a valuable contribution to our knowledge of symptom formation in the latter. The symptom in the true neuroses is frequently the nucleus and incipient stage of development of the psychoneurotic symptom.
>
> (024-026)

This passage is significant: while actual neuroses produce symptoms directly from physiological disturbance, psychoneuroses *borrow* from the somatic realm to express psychical conflicts symbolically.

## Psychoneurosis and Transference

The psychoneuroses are coextensive with the [[Transference Neurosis|transference neuroses]]. Because the patient's libido remains directed toward objects (even if those objects exist only in phantasy), the psychoneurotic patient can develop a [[Transference]] relationship with the analyst. This capacity for transference is what makes the psychoneuroses amenable to psychoanalytic therapy, in contrast to the [[Narcissism|narcissistic neuroses]].

The therapeutic implication is decisive: psychoanalysis can treat psychoneuroses because it can access the unconscious conflicts that produce them. The "key to understanding" (026-006) these conditions lies in the analysis of the transference that develops during treatment.
