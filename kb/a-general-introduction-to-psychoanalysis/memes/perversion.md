# Perversion

## Overview

In the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, the concept of [[Perversion]] serves as a cornerstone of Freud's revolutionary theory of sexuality. Far from treating perversions as rare anomalies or signs of degeneracy, Freud argues that they are the visible, organized expression of component instincts that exist universally in human beings. The perversions, he contends, "have openly and unmistakably shown" (020-016) what neurosis conceals: that sexuality extends far beyond genital procreation and has its roots in the polymorphously perverse disposition of infancy. This insight — that the perversions are not foreign to normality but continuous with it — is what necessitates "an alteration of our concept of sexuality" (021-002).

## Definition

Freud defines perversion through two fundamental characteristics that depart from the conventional understanding of sexuality:

1. **Anatomical extension beyond the genitals** — The perversions demonstrate that any part of the body can become an erogenous zone. "The essential nature of perversion does not consist in overstepping the aim of procreation" (021-011) but in the persistence of infantile component instincts into adult sexual life.

2. **Lingering at preparatory acts** — In perversion, acts that normally serve as foreplay to genital union become ends in themselves. Freud describes how perversions "make use of our knowledge of perversions to concentrate anew" (021-012) the understanding that all sexual activity involves component drives.

The crucial theoretical move is Freud's rejection of the degeneration paradigm: "I should like to defend you against the point of view which sees in a perversion a 'sign of degeneration'" (020-013). Instead, he proposes that perversions are exaggerations or fixations of universal infantile tendencies.

## Development in the Lectures

### The Evidence of the Perversions (Lectures 20–21)

Freud introduces perversions in Lecture 20 as part of his systematic revision of the concept of sexuality. Having discussed the [[Libido|libido theory]] and the sexual organization of the child, he turns to the perversions as a source of evidence:

> "The same picture which the perversions have openly and unmistakably shown us confronts us once more in the study of the sexual life of the child." (020-016)

This parallelism between perversion and infantile sexuality is the linchpin of his argument: the child is, in Freud's famous phrase, "polymorphously perverse."

### Perversion and the Alteration of the Concept of Sexuality

In Lecture 21, Freud directly addresses the resistance his theory meets. The conventional view that sexuality begins at puberty and serves reproduction is contradicted by two facts: the sexual life of children and the existence of the perversions. "It was not only perversions that necessitated an alteration of our concept of sexuality" (021-002), but the combination of both lines of evidence.

### Perversion, Neurosis, and Repression

The relationship between [[Neurosis|neurosis]] and perversion is one of Freud's most important theoretical innovations. He proposes that both arise from the same infantile component instincts, differentiated only by the fate of those instincts under the pressure of [[Repression|repression]]:

> "What here turns toward the outside world in the form of phantasy, there has taken hold of the real objects of the outer world" (022-016)

Where repression succeeds incompletely, the drives return as neurotic symptoms. Where repression is absent, the drives organize into a manifest perversion: "The development toward perversion branches off sharply from that toward neurosis" (023-005).

## Key Examples

### Homosexuality (Inversion)

Freud discusses homosexuality as the most prominent example of perversion, arguing that "all the potentialities of the perversions we have gone over" (020-012) are present in every individual's developmental history. Homosexuality represents a fixation on an object-choice that is a normal stage in development.

### Fetishism and Component Instincts

Freud describes how perversions like fetishism involve the overvaluation of a single component instinct — often one that played a role in childhood sexual investigation — elevated to the status of the exclusive sexual aim. He notes that "all kinds of perversions which have become set as the exclusive sexual activity" (022-038) can be traced to childhood origins.

### Voyeurism and Exhibitionism (Scopophilia)

These paired perversions, which Freud treats as active and passive expressions of the same component instinct, demonstrate how perversions exist along a continuum with normal sexual curiosity: "By the unavoidable traits of perversion" (021-020), Freud argues, all sexuality is shown to contain perverse elements.

## Perversion and Infantile Sexuality

The most radical claim Freud makes is that perversions have "their origin in childhood" (020-021). The infant, in Freud's account, possesses a polymorphously perverse disposition — meaning that any part of the body can become a source of sexual pleasure, and any object can become a sexual object. Normal development consists in the organization of these scattered component instincts under the primacy of the genitals and in the service of reproduction. Perversion occurs when this organization fails, and one or more component instincts persist as the dominant sexual aim.

## Relation to Other Concepts

The concept of perversion is inseparable from several other key psychoanalytic ideas:

- The [[Oedipus Complex]] is the nuclear complex through which component instincts are normally organized — its failure is central to the etiology of perversion.
- [[Fixation]] explains why particular component instincts become the basis of a perversion: the libido remains attached to an early stage of development.
- [[Sublimation]] represents the non-pathological fate of component instincts, in which they are diverted to non-sexual aims.
- The distinction between perversion and [[Neurosis]] is central to Freud's diagnostic framework and his theory of repression.

## Significance

Freud's theory of perversion had far-reaching consequences beyond psychoanalysis. By arguing that the perversions were not qualitatively different from normal sexuality — that they merely represented the persistence of universal infantile tendencies — Freud effectively dismantled the nineteenth-century concept of the "sexual pervert" as a distinct, degenerate type of human being. This continuity thesis was one of the three "great blows to human narcissism" that psychoanalysis delivered, alongside the discovery of the [[The Unconscious|unconscious]] and the recognition of infantile sexuality.
