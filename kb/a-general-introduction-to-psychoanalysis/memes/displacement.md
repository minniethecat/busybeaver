# Displacement

## Overview

Displacement (*Verschiebung*) is one of the two primary processes of the dream-work identified by Freud in his *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, alongside condensation. It is the mechanism by which the psychic emphasis or intensity of a latent dream-thought is transferred onto a different, often seemingly trivial, element in the manifest dream. This shift of emphasis functions as a crucial form of dream censorship, allowing repressed wishes to achieve representation in consciousness while evading the dream censor. (011-016)

## Definition

Freud introduces displacement as the second dream-work process:

> The second process of the dream-work isdisplacement. Fortunately we are already prepared for this, since we know that it is entirely the work of dream censorship. The two evidences of this are firstly, that a latent element is not replaced by one of its constituent parts but by something further removed from it, that is, by a sort of allusion; secondly, that the psychic accent is transferred from an important element to another that is unimportant, so that the dream centers elsewhere and seems strange.
>
> (011-016)


Displacement is the second process of the dream-work. Whereas condensation compresses multiple latent thoughts into a single manifest element, displacement transfers the psychic accent from an important thought to an indifferent one. Freud describes it as an "unheard of" means of expressing thoughts — one without parallel in normal waking mental life. (011-019)

The mechanism operates along two axes:

1. **Selection displacement**: A latent element is replaced not by one of its own constituents, but by something more remote — an allusion.
2. **Emphasis displacement**: The psychic accent shifts from an important element to another, so that the dream centers on something that was peripheral in the latent thoughts. (009-015)

Through displacement, the manifest dream acquires a different center from the latent dream-thoughts — it appears to be about something else entirely. (009-016)

## Development in the Lectures

### Dream-Work Context

Freud introduces displacement in Lecture 11 as the second of the two principal dream-work processes. He positions it chronologically after condensation in the dream's formation: first the latent thoughts undergo compression, then the resulting elements are subjected to displacement. (011-016)

The concept is first foreshadowed in Lecture 9, where Freud discusses how dream censorship operates through omission, modification, and regrouping of material. Displacement represents the most sophisticated form of this regrouping. (009-015) (009-016)

### Elaboration and Secondary Revision

In later lectures, Freud extends the concept of displacement beyond dream formation. He identifies displacement as operating in the formation of neurotic symptoms as well. The same mechanisms — condensation and displacement — that transform latent dream-thoughts into manifest content also transform unconscious conflicts into symptoms. (019-029) (024-026)

In Lecture 23, Freud elaborates on how displacement collaborates with elaboration (*secondary revision*) to create the final dream narrative. The displacement of temporal and logical relations is particularly significant — in dreams, cause-and-effect sequences are often reversed or reordered. (023-006) (023-018) (023-025)

### Relation to Joke-Work

Freud briefly compares dream displacement to the techniques of jokes, noting that both employ allusion and the shifting of emphasis. However, he emphasizes a crucial difference: jokes retain the condition of intelligibility, whereas dream displacement does not — the dream is opaque precisely because displacement has severed the connection between the manifest element and its latent source. (011-018)

## Key Examples

### The Botanical Monograph Dream

Freud references this dream in the Lectures to illustrate displacement: the dreamer's apparently trivial concern with a botanical monograph displaces the underlying complex of professional ambition, rivalry, and childhood memories. The manifest focus on botany is a substitute for the latent concern with career and reputation. (016-023)

### Displacement in Symptom Formation

Freud extends the displacement concept to clinical phenomena. In anxiety hysteria, the fear originally attached to an unconscious impulse becomes displaced onto an external object or situation — the phobic object is a substitute, and the displacement of affect is what makes the phobia possible. (015-014) (015-017)

### The Throne Metaphor

Freud illustrates displacement with a political metaphor: it is as though a minor official gains all the attention while the sovereign goes unnoticed. The manifest dream element is the "minor official" — the latent thought that truly matters is the "sovereign" hidden behind it. (011-047)
