# Narcissism

## Overview

Narcissism (*Narzissmus*), also rendered as *narcism* in the English translation of Freud's *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, is the concept that describes the investment of libido in the ego itself rather than in external objects. Introduced to explain the psychoses — where libido appears withdrawn from the world and turned inward, producing megalomania — narcissism is extended by Freud to become a universal developmental concept: the original condition of libido distribution from which object-love emerges, a state to which the sleeper returns each night, and the libidinal complement to egoism. (026-010) (026-014) (026-015)

## Definition

Freud defines narcissism as "this placing of the libido" on the ego rather than on external objects. The term "was borrowed from one of the perversions described by P. Naecke. In it the grown individual lavishes upon his own body all the affection usually devoted to some foreign sex object." (026-010) But Freud immediately extends the concept beyond perversion: "It is much more probable that this narcism is the general and original condition, out of which the love for an object later develops, without however necessarily causing narcism to disappear." (026-011)

Freud draws a crucial distinction between narcissism and egoism: "narcism is the libidinous complement of egoism. When we speak of egoism we mean only the benefits to the individual; if we speak of narcism we also take into account his libidinous satisfaction." (026-015) Egoism is "the self-evident, the constant, and narcism the variable element." (026-015)

## Development in the Lectures

### Origin in the Study of Psychosis (Lecture 26)

The concept of narcissism originated in the psychoanalytic investigation of dementia praecox. In 1908, Karl Abraham asserted that "the principal characteristic of dementia praecox is that there is no libidinous occupation of objects." When asked what happens to the libido diverted from objects, Abraham answered: "It is turned back upon the ego, and this reflected turning back is the source of the megalomania in dementia praecox." (026-010) This hallucination of greatness is "exactly comparable to the well-known over-estimation of the objects habitual to lovers." (026-010) For the first time, a psychotic condition was understood by comparing it with the normal psychology of love.

### Narcissism as the Original Condition (Lecture 26)

Freud proposes that narcissism is "the general and original condition, out of which the love for an object later develops." (026-011) This recalls the auto-erotic stage of infantile sexuality: "in the beginning many sex instincts seek auto-erotic gratification, and this capacity for auto-eroticism forms the basis for the retardation of sexuality in its education to conformity with fact." (026-011) Auto-eroticism was "the sexual activity of the narcistic stage in the placing of the libido." (026-011)

### The Protoplasmic Analogy (Lecture 26)

To represent the relation of ego-libido to object-libido, Freud uses an analogy from zoology. Simple organisms "stretch out protrusions, known as pseudopia, into which the protoplasm flows. But they can withdraw these protrusions and assume their original shape." The extension of pseudopodia corresponds to the "radiation of libido to the objects," while the central mass of libido remains in the ego. "Under normal conditions ego-libido can be changed into object-libido, and this can again be taken up into the ego, without any trouble." (026-014)

### Sleep as Complete Narcissism (Lecture 26)

Freud applies the narcissism concept to the psychology of sleep: "Sleep is a condition in which all occupations of objects, the libidinous as well as the egoistic, are given up, and are withdrawn into the ego." Sleep restores "the original condition of libido division... a condition of complete narcism in which libido and ego-interest are still united and live indistinguishably in the self-sufficient ego." (026-014) This casts new light on recovery during sleep, suggesting the sleeper "conjures up night after night" the "blissful isolation in the intra-uterine life." (026-014)

### Narcissistic Neuroses (Lectures 22-26)

Freud distinguishes a separate class of disorders — the narcissistic neuroses — from the transference neuroses (hysteria, anxiety, compulsion). In the narcissistic neuroses (dementia praecox, paranoia, melancholia), the ego is "far more deeply involved" and the condition proves inaccessible to psychoanalytic therapy because these patients "have no capacity for transference." (024-008) (027-042)

In Lecture 22, Freud notes that "the study of narcistic neuroses has promised to throw light on the ego in its construction, in the organization of its various functions" — a promise that would be fulfilled in the later structural theory. (022-009)

### Narcissistic Identification (Lecture 26)

Freud describes a process of "narcistic identification" in which the ego incorporates the lost object: in melancholia, "the object cathexis proves to have little power of resistance and is done away with. But the free libido is not transferred to another object; it is withdrawn into the ego. There, however, it is not used in any haphazard way but serves to establish an identification of the ego with the abandoned object." The shadow of the object falls upon the ego. (026-043) In addition to narcistic identification, there is "an hysterical identification" which follows a different mechanism. (026-043)

### Homosexuality and Narcissistic Object-Choice (Lecture 26)

Freud connects narcissism to homosexuality through the theory of object-choice types. "Homosexual choice of object is originally more natural to narcism than the heterosexual." When a "strong and undesirable homosexual impulse" must be thwarted, "the way back to narcism is made especially easy." (026-039) The libido can proceed according to the *narcissistic* type (selecting a person similar to the ego) or the *dependent* type (selecting persons valuable for satisfying non-libidinal needs). "A strong fixation of the libido to the narcistic type of object-choice" corresponds to "a disposition toward manifest homosexuality." (026-039)

### Goethe's Poetic Representation (Lecture 26)

Freud illustrates the economic contrast between narcissism and being in love through Goethe's *West-östlicher Divan*. Suleika's song celebrates the self-sufficient personality: "Mankind's loftiest elation / Shines in personality." Hatem's reply declares that all his self is invested in the beloved: "When I cherish her, then only / Dearer to myself I grow, / If she turned to leave me lonely / I should lose the self I know." The sex object "has virtually sucked up the ego" — an altruistic transformation in which the beloved becomes all-powerful. (026-016)

## Key Examples

### Dementia Praecox and Megalomania

The clinical phenomenon that gave rise to the concept: in dementia praecox, the patient's libido, withdrawn from external objects, is redirected onto the ego, producing grandiose delusions. This "hallucination of greatness is exactly comparable to the well-known over-estimation of the objects habitual to lovers" — the same psychological mechanism operating in opposite directions. (026-010)

### Sleep as Nocturnal Narcissism

Every night, the sleeper withdraws all object-cathexes into the ego, recreating "the original condition of libido division... a condition of complete narcism in which libido and ego-interest are still united and live indistinguishably in the self-sufficient ego." (026-014) This explains the restorative power of sleep and connects the psychology of everyday life to the theory of narcissism.

### Organic Illness and Narcissistic Withdrawal

Freud notes that during organic sickness, libido is withdrawn from objects and concentrated on the suffering body — a phenomenon analogous to the narcissistic withdrawal in psychosis. The sick person loses interest in the external world and becomes preoccupied with bodily sensations, illustrating the fluidity between ego-libido and object-libido. (026-014)
