# Ego

## Overview

The Ego (*das Ich*) is introduced in Freud's *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis* as the psychic agency that mediates between the instinctual demands of the libido and the constraints of external reality. It is the seat of self-preservation, the agent of suppression (*Verdrängung*), the locus of resistance in therapy, and the part of the psychic apparatus that is accessible to consciousness. (026-001) In the lectures, the ego appears in multiple roles: as the adversary of the sexual instincts in the conflict that produces neurosis; as the reservoir of ego-libido in the theory of narcissism; and as the "fellow-worker" of the physician in analytic therapy. (027-023)

## Definition

Freud describes the discovery of ego-libido through the study of psychoses:

> These first interpretations of Abraham's have been maintained in psychoanalysis, and have become the basis of our attitude towards the psychoses. Slowly we familiarized ourselves with the idea that the libido, which we find attached to certain objects, which expresses a striving to attain gratification from these objects, may also forsake them and put in their place the person's own ego. Gradually these ideas were developed more and more consistently. The name for this placing of the libido—narcism—was borrowed from one of the perversions described by P. Naecke. In it the grown individual lavishes upon his own body all the affection usually devoted to some foreign sex object.
>
> (026-010)


Freud defines the ego as the organized psychic agency responsible for the instincts of self-preservation, distinguished from the sexual instincts that pursue libidinal gratification through objects. In the normal state, the ego accommodates the libido; neurosis arises "when his ego has lost the capacity of accommodating the libido." (024-009) The strength of the ego determines the outcome of the conflict: "The stronger the ego, the easier the solution." (024-010)

The ego operates in multiple dimensions:

1. **Instinctual pole**: The ego is the seat of the *ego instincts* — hunger, thirst, and self-preservation — which are fundamentally different from the sexual instincts. They follow the reality principle, learn from necessity, and do not undergo reversal into anxiety. (026-015) (026-018)
2. **Defensive agency**: The ego executes suppression — the "flight from the libido, which it regards dangerous." (025-048) It maintains the counter-siege (*Gegenbesetzung*) that keeps suppressed material unconscious. (027-023)
3. **Therapeutic ally**: In analysis, the ego is "our fellow-worker" whose intelligence helps identify resistances and whose desire to become well motivates cooperation. (027-023)
4. **Libidinal reservoir**: The ego can be the object of libido (ego-libido), a concept foundational to the theory of narcissism. (026-010)

## Development in the Lectures

### Ego Instincts versus Sexual Instincts (Lecture 26)

Freud opens Lecture 26 with the "repeated" distinction between the ego instincts and the sexual instincts. Suppression first taught that the two may be "flatly opposed to each other" — in the struggle the sexual instincts suffer apparent defeat and are forced into regressive methods of satisfaction. (026-001) The two groups have different relations to the educator Necessity: the ego instincts learn from experience and adapt to the reality principle, while the sexual instincts remain more closely allied to the emotional condition of fear. (026-017)

A decisive empirical difference is that non-satisfaction of hunger and thirst "never result in their reversal into anxiety, while the transformation of unsatisfied libido into fear is one of the best known and most frequently observed phenomena." (026-018)

### The Ego in Neurotic Conflict (Lecture 24)

The ego-libido relationship is the key to understanding neurosis. Freud states that a person suffers from neurosis "when his ego has lost the capacity of accommodating the libido." (024-009) This conflict — between the ego as independent individuality and the sexual instinct as a link in the chain of generations — is what makes neurosis "the prerogative of man, and not of animals." (026-021) The excessive development of human libido, with the varied and complicated psychic life it enables, creates the conditions for conflict — and is also responsible for "the great progress that man has made beyond his kinship with animals." (026-021)

### Ego-Libido and Narcism (Lecture 26)

The concept of ego-libido emerged from the psychoanalytic investigation of psychoses. Abraham's 1908 insight — that in dementia praecox the libido is "turned back upon the ego" and this "reflected turning back is the source of megalomania" — opened the study of narcissism. (026-010) Freud adopted the name *narcism* (from P. Naecke) for "this placing of the libido" on the ego rather than on external objects. (026-010)

Freud represents the relation of ego-libido to object-libido with a zoological analogy: like a protoplasmic organism extending and retracting pseudopodia, "under normal conditions ego-libido can be changed into object-libido, and this can again be taken up into the ego, without any trouble." (026-014) Sleep is the condition in which "all occupations of objects, the libidinous as well as the egoistic, are given up, and are withdrawn into the ego" — a "condition of complete narcism in which libido and ego-interest are still united and live indistinguishably in the self-sufficient ego." (026-014)

Freud carefully distinguishes narcism from egoism: "narcism is the libidinous complement of egoism." When speaking of egoism, one means only the benefits to the individual; when speaking of narcism, one also takes into account libidinous satisfaction. "Egoism is the self-evident, the constant, and narcism the variable element." (026-015)

### The Ego in Therapy (Lecture 27)

In Lecture 27, Freud discusses the ego's role in analytic treatment. The resistance that maintains suppression arises "not from the unconscious but from the ego, which is our fellow-worker." (027-023) The therapeutic task is to enable the ego to recognize the resistance and relinquish the counter-siege. The ego's cooperation is enlisted through two motives: the patient's desire to become well, and the help of his intelligence, supported by the analyst's interpretations. (027-023)

The ego that effected suppression was "weak, infantile and may have had reason to denounce the claims of the libido as if they were dangerous." Today it is "strong, experienced and is supported by the assistance of the physician" — and the revived conflict may therefore be guided "to a better issue than a suppression." (027-024)

### The Ego in Dreams (Lecture 9 and 13)

In the dream lectures, Freud characterizes the dream as an expression of the dreamer's "boundless, reckless egoism." The personal ego appears in every dream and "plays the chief part in it, even if it knows how to disguise itself perfectly for the manifest content." (009-022) The dream's egoism is "not unconnected with the sleep-inducing withdrawal from the outer world" — the dreamer retreats into a condition of complete narcissism where all object-cathexes, including egoistic ones, are withdrawn into the ego. (009-022)

## Key Examples

### The Protoplasmic Pseudopodia

To illustrate the dynamics of ego-libido and object-libido, Freud uses the analogy of a simple organism extending and retracting protoplasmic processes. The radiation of libido toward objects is like the extension of pseudopodia; object-libido can be withdrawn back into the ego, just as pseudopodia retract into the central mass. Under normal conditions, this flow is bidirectional and effortless. (026-014)

### The Workman and the Secondary Gain of Illness

Freud illustrates the ego's investment in illness through the image of a crippled workman who receives accident insurance and learns to exploit his injury by begging. The illness attains "a secondary function, which gives strength to its existence" — and the ego becomes invested in maintaining the neurosis for the advantages it provides. (024-007)

### Goethe's Suleika and Hatem

In Lecture 26, Freud quotes Goethe's *West-östlicher Divan* to poetically represent the economic contrast between narcissism and being in love. Suleika's song celebrates self-sufficient personality; Hatem's reply declares that his entire self is invested in the beloved: "When I cherish her, then only / Dearer to myself I grow, / If she turned to leave me lonely / I should lose the self I know." The sex object "has virtually sucked up the ego." (026-016)
