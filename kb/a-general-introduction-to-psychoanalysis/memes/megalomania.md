# Megalomania

## Overview

Megalomania — the delusion of personal grandeur, power, or importance — appears in Freud's *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis* as one of the defining symptoms of paraphrenia, the clinical group that encompasses dementia praecox (schizophrenia) and paranoia. Freud links megalomania to his libido theory, interpreting it as the symptomatic expression of a narcissistic withdrawal of libido from external objects back onto the ego. This conceptual move — from a descriptive symptom to a dynamic-economic explanation — is a signature of Freud's approach to psychopathology in Lecture 26.

## Definition

In the context of Freud's nosology, megalomania is "the mania of persecution" (026-033) accompanied by an exaggerated sense of the self's importance. It is not a primary disorder but a symptom that reveals the underlying libidinal economics of paraphrenia: when the libido, having been withdrawn from object-cathexes, is redirected onto the ego, the resulting narcissistic inflation produces the clinical picture of megalomania.

## Libido Theory and Narcissism

Freud's explanation of megalomania is rooted in his concept of narcissism. He proposes that in paraphrenia, the libido detaches from its objects and flows back to the ego:

> The turning back of the libido to the ego — the narcissistic regression — is the source of the megalomania in dementia praecox. (026-009)

This narcissistic hypercathexis of the ego produces the subjective experience of immense personal significance and power that characterizes megalomanic states. The mechanism is structurally similar to the megalomania of primitive peoples and children, whom Freud regards as existing in a state of primary narcissism.

## Classification Within Paraphrenia

In his classification of paraphrenic symptoms, Freud groups megalomania alongside other content-based manifestations:

> We may classify the symptoms of paraphrenia according to their content as: megalomania, the mania of persecution, erotomania, and the delusions of jealousy. (026-033)

This classification places megalomania as one of the principal forms that the paraphrenic's delusional reconstruction of reality can take — the ego, flooded with the libido withdrawn from objects, creates a world in which it occupies the central, inflated position.

## Clinical Significance

Megalomania is significant not merely as a descriptive symptom but as clinical evidence for the theory of narcissism. The fact that the paraphrenic patient withdraws interest from the external world while simultaneously developing grandiose ideas about the self provides observational support for Freud's hypothesis of a fixed quantity of libido that must be distributed somewhere — if not in object-cathexes, then in the ego itself.
