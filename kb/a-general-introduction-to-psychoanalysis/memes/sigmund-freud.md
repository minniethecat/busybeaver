# Sigmund Freud

## Biography

Sigmund Freud (6 May 1856 – 23 September 1939) was an Austrian neurologist and the founder of psychoanalysis, a clinical method for treating psychopathology through dialogue between a patient and a psychoanalyst. Born in Freiberg, Moravia (now the Czech Republic), Freud qualified as a doctor of medicine at the University of Vienna in 1881. He established his clinical practice in Vienna in 1886, where he developed the theories and techniques that would define psychoanalysis.

> The impartial student of Sigmund Freud need not agree with all his conclusions, and indeed, like the present writer, may be unable to make sex so all-dominating a factor in the psychic life of the past and present as Freud deems it to be, to recognize the fact that he is the most original and creative mind in psychology of our generation.
>
> (P01-004)

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Freud is the originator of psychoanalysis. In the *Introductory Lectures*, he positions himself as the discoverer of the unconscious, the interpreter of dreams, and the theorist of neurosis. The lectures, delivered between 1915 and 1917 at the University of Vienna, represent his most systematic attempt to present psychoanalysis to a general audience.

Freud organized the 28 lectures into three parts:

1. **Part I: The Parapraxes** (Chapters 1–4) — Demonstrating that everyday errors (slips of the tongue, forgetting) have unconscious meaning
2. **Part II: Dreams** (Chapters 5–15) — Presenting the theory that dreams are wish-fulfillments accessible through interpretation
3. **Part III: General Theory of the Neuroses** (Chapters 16–28) — Extending psychoanalytic principles to understand and treat neurotic illness

## Key Contributions

- **The Unconscious**: Freud demonstrated that mental processes occur outside conscious awareness and influence behavior (001-018)
- **Dream Interpretation**: He established that dreams have meaning and can be analyzed through free association (005-001)
- **The Theory of the Neuroses**: He developed a comprehensive theory linking symptom formation to repressed wishes and unconscious conflict (017-012)
- **Transference**: He discovered that patients transfer feelings from past relationships onto the analyst, making it a therapeutic tool (027-001)
- **The Sexual Etiology**: He argued that sexual factors play a central role in the causation of neurosis (020-015)

## Freud as Lecturer

Throughout the lectures, Freud adopts several distinctive rhetorical postures:

- **The guide**: He repeatedly acknowledges that psychoanalysis is difficult to teach and that students must overcome "instinctive opposition" (001-001)
- **The reluctant teacher**: He warns his audience against pursuing psychoanalysis professionally, noting it would "ruin his every chance of success at a university" (001-016)
- **The self-aware narrator**: He frequently interrupts the argument to address the audience's anticipated objections

## Relationship with Contemporaries

Freud's lectures reference his complex relationships with colleagues:

- **Josef Breuer** (1842–1925): Freud's early collaborator on *Studies on Hysteria*; their relationship is discussed in the context of the discovery of the "talking cure"
- **Carl Jung** (1875–1961): Former disciple who broke with Freud over the sexual theory
- **Alfred Adler** (1870–1937): Another former follower who developed "individual psychology"
- **Pierre Janet** (1859–1947): French psychologist whom Freud acknowledges as independently discovering similar findings (017-002)
- **Jean-Martin Charcot** (1825–1893): Freud's teacher at the Salpêtrière, who influenced his understanding of hysteria
