# Parapraxes

## Overview

[[Parapraxes]] — from the Greek *para* (beside) + *praxis* (action), rendering Freud's German *Fehlleistung* (faulty function) — are the "errors" of everyday life that form the subject matter of Part I of the *Introductory Lectures*. These include slips of the tongue, misreadings, mishearings, the temporary forgetting of names and projects, and the mislaying of objects. Freud's thesis, which he demonstrates through an accumulation of examples across four lectures, is that these apparently insignificant errors are not accidents of inattention but meaningful psychic acts in which an unconscious intention interferes with a conscious one. The analysis of parapraxes serves as the simplest and most accessible demonstration of the psychoanalytic method.

## Definition

Freud introduces the category at the opening of Lecture II:

> "I refer to the errors which an individual commits — as for example, slips of the tongue, misreadings, mishearings, temporary forgettings, the mislaying of objects and the losing of them." (002-001)

What distinguishes the psychoanalytic approach is the refusal to accept the conventional explanation:

> "We will not follow the attention theory of psychological errors, or at any rate we will not accept it unconditionally." (002-009)

The attention theory holds that errors occur when we are tired, distracted, or unwell. Freud acknowledges these as facilitating conditions but insists they do not explain the specific form an error takes — why a particular wrong word is substituted, why a particular name is forgotten:

> "The phenomena accompanying these errors, which are not understood and not explained away, we will take as the subject of our assertion." (002-011)

## Types of Parapraxes

### Slips of the Tongue

The slip of the tongue is the "most conspicuous form" of parapraxes (002-017). Freud argues that every such slip has a meaning:

> "I think that the product of the slip has itself a right to be considered a valid psychic act, pursuing its own purpose, a statement with content and meaning of its own." (002-023)

The interference is not random. One can even provoke slips experimentally:

> "One can provoke slips of the tongue, can call them forth, so to speak, by suggestion." (002-013)

### Forgetting

The forgetting of names, projects, and intentions follows the same pattern. What is forgotten is not arbitrarily selected but determined by an unconscious motive:

> "The forgetting of projects can quite commonly be traced to a counter-current which opposes their execution." (003-049)

Where there is no such counter-current, forgetting is simply a failure of memory. But where there is an unavowed reluctance to carry out the project, forgetting serves the interest of that reluctance while sparing the individual the discomfort of admitting it:

> "The cases of forgetting projects are as a rule so clear that they do not admit of a different interpretation." (003-052)

Freud documents cases of repeated forgetting that make the dynamic interpretation inescapable (003-060–003-061).

## The Mechanism of Interference

The psychoanalytic explanation of parapraxes posits two simultaneous tendencies:

> "We speak of a conflict of two tendencies and the suppression of one which then indemnifies itself by producing the error." (004-034)

One tendency is the conscious intention (to say a certain word, to keep an appointment); the other is an unconscious, suppressed intention that interferes with it. The parapraxis is a compromise: the suppressed tendency achieves partial expression without the individual having to acknowledge it consciously.

## Place in the Psychoanalytic System

Parapraxes occupy a strategic position in Freud's exposition. They are the most accessible demonstration that:

1. **Mental determinism** extends to events traditionally dismissed as meaningless (002-042, 002-047)
2. **Unconscious mental processes** operate even in normal, healthy individuals (P01-008)
3. **The psychoanalytic method** — interpreting surface phenomena to reveal hidden motives — applies universally, not only to neurotics (003-008)

> "We have solved the riddle of errors with relatively little trouble. They are not accidents but significant psychic acts." (003-015)

The study of parapraxes thus prepares the ground for the study of dreams (Part II) and of neurotic symptoms (Part III), which operate by the same mechanism of conflict and compromise formation.
