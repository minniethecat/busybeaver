# Josef Breuer

## Biography

Josef Breuer (1842–1925) was a distinguished Viennese physician and physiologist who, together with [[Sigmund Freud]], stands at the origin of [[Psychoanalysis]]. Trained in internal medicine, Breuer had already made significant contributions to physiology — including the discovery of the Hering-Breuer reflex in respiration — before his clinical work with a patient known as Anna O. led to what Freud calls "a discovery of Breuer's, for which he alone deserves the credit" (018-014) — "not the result of a speculation, but of a felicitous observation made possible by the coöperation of the patient" (018-016). Though Breuer ultimately withdrew from the psychoanalytic movement, his contribution was foundational.

## Role in Psychoanalysis

Breuer occupies a unique position in the history of psychoanalysis: he is the discoverer of the **cathartic method**, the clinical technique from which psychoanalysis developed. Freud repeatedly acknowledges this debt throughout the lectures:

> "The study of symptoms was first discovered by J. Breuer in the study and felicitous cure of a case of hysteria which has since become famous." (017-001)

Between 1880 and 1882, Breuer treated a young woman (Anna O.) suffering from severe hysterical symptoms. He discovered that when the patient, under hypnosis, recalled the circumstances in which a symptom first appeared and expressed the emotion originally associated with it, the symptom disappeared. Breuer called this the "cathartic" or "talking cure" method (018-015).

## Key Contributions

### The Case of Anna O. (1880–1882)

The Anna O. case (later identified as Bertha Pappenheim) is the single most important clinical case in the prehistory of psychoanalysis. The patient presented with paralysis, disturbances of vision and speech, and altered states of consciousness. Breuer's crucial observations:

1. **Symptoms have meaning** — Each hysterical symptom was traceable to a specific traumatic experience: "Breuer's first hysterical patient was entirely oriented in this direction, was entirely dominated by an unpleasant experience" (018-004). Freud demonstrated through Breuer's cases that "neurotic symptoms carry just as much meaning as do errors and the dream" (017-028).

2. **Abreaction cures** — Recalling the trauma with its original emotion (abreaction) led to the symptom's disappearance: "It was by these means that Breuer actually achieved the recovery of his hysterical patient" (018-015).

3. **Hysterics suffer from reminiscences** — The famous formula, developed with Freud: "By this first formula, by which Breuer and I, in 1893–1895, accounted for the symptoms of hysteria" (018-007).

### The Cathartic Method

Breuer's method involved putting the patient into a hypnotic state and guiding her to recall the traumatic origins of each symptom. The emotional discharge (catharsis) that accompanied the recollection removed the symptom:

> "Breuer's discovery still remains the foundation of psychoanalytic therapy." (018-019)

Freud later replaced hypnosis with [[Free Association]], but the principle — that making the unconscious conscious has therapeutic effect — remains the core of psychoanalytic treatment.

## Relationship with Freud

Freud's relationship with Breuer evolved from collaboration to divergence. In the lectures, Freud treats Breuer with consistent respect and gratitude, crediting him as the discoverer of the clinical method while distinguishing his own contributions:

- **1880s**: Breuer shares the Anna O. case with the younger Freud, sparking Freud's interest in hysteria
- **1893–1895**: They collaborate on *Studies on Hysteria* (referenced at 018-007)
- **Later divergence**: Breuer, unlike Freud, did not accept the sexual etiology of neurosis. Freud notes this obliquely: "Breuer and I both originally practiced the banishment of their symptoms" (019-014), implying their later theoretical divergence

Freud is careful to give Breuer credit where due while establishing his own theoretical contribution — particularly the role of [[Sexuality]] and the [[Unconscious]] — as distinct advances beyond Breuer's clinical discovery.
