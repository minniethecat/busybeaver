# Hypochondria

## Overview

Hypochondria occupies a distinctive position in Freud's classification of the neuroses. In Lecture 24, he lists it alongside neurasthenia and anxiety neurosis as one of the "true neuroses" or "actual neuroses" — conditions arising from somatic disturbances rather than psychical conflict (024-025). Yet in Lecture 26, Freud goes further, offering a specifically psychoanalytic mechanism for hypochondria that links it to the theory of narcissism, thereby bridging the gap between the actual neuroses and the narcissistic disorders.

## Definition

Freud defines hypochondria as a condition in which the patient experiences anxious and painful preoccupation with the state of their bodily organs. Unlike hysteria, where a bodily symptom symbolically represents an unconscious conflict, the hypochondriacal symptom is understood as a direct somatic disturbance. Freud classifies it as follows:

> The pure forms of true neuroses: neurasthenia, anxiety neurosis, and hypochondria. (024-025)

He notes, however, that the boundaries are fluid: "transitions may most easily be observed between neurasthenia and the transference neuroses, between anxiety neurosis and anxiety hysteria, but also between hypochondria and paraphrenia" (024-026).

## Narcissistic Mechanism (Lecture 26)

The most distinctive contribution Freud makes to the understanding of hypochondria appears in Lecture 26, where he connects it to the theory of narcissism. He proposes that hypochondria involves a pathological concentration of libido upon a particular organ:

> The assumption that the object libido travels to the ego... opens the way to an understanding of hypochondria, where an organ occupies the ego's attention in a similar manner, although not to our senses, as the genitals do in the excited state. (026-022)

This analogy with genital excitation is revealing: just as sexual arousal involves an increased cathexis of the genitals with libido, hypochondria involves an analogous hypercathexis of another organ. The difference is that the hypochondriacal organ-cathexis is pathological, producing pain and anxiety rather than pleasure. The mechanism is essentially narcissistic — the libido, instead of flowing outward to objects, concentrates on a part of the ego's own body.

## Relation to Paraphrenia

Freud notes a continuum between hypochondria and paraphrenia (the group comprising dementia praecox and paranoia) (024-026). This connection is theoretically significant: both conditions involve a withdrawal of libido from objects, but in hypochondria the libido settles on a specific organ, while in paraphrenia it floods the ego as a whole, producing megalomania and other delusional symptoms. Hypochondria may thus be understood as an intermediate form between the actual neuroses (somatic) and the narcissistic neuroses (paraphrenia).
