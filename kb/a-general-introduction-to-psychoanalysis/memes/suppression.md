# Suppression

## Overview

[[Suppression]] (*Unterdrückung*) is the act of consciously or preconsciously inhibiting a mental impulse — an intention, a wish, or a memory — so that it fails to attain expression. Freud distinguishes suppression from [[Repression]] (*Verdrängung*), which operates entirely unconsciously, but he treats the two as points on a single continuum of defensive activity. In the *Introductory Lectures*, suppression is first introduced in the analysis of [[Parapraxes|parapraxes]], where it provides the interfering counter-force that produces the error. It recurs throughout the lectures on dreams and neuroses as one of the fundamental defensive operations of the ego.

## Definition

Suppression appears first in the analysis of errors. When an intention is suppressed, it does not simply vanish:

> "The intention must have undergone a certain suppression in order to be able to manifest itself in the disturbance of another intention." (004-018)

The suppression is never fully successful. The suppressed intention returns, not as itself, but as a disturbance of the conscious intention — the slip, the forgetting, the mislaying. This is the general formula for the parapraxis:

> "We speak of a conflict of two tendencies and the suppression of one which then indemnifies itself by producing the error." (004-034)

## Suppression and Repression

In Lecture XXII, Freud draws the explicit distinction between the two concepts:

> "I beg you not to confuse regression and suppression, and aid you to see clearly the relation between the two processes. Suppression, as you know, is the process by which a conscious act, one which belongs to the foreconscious system, is made unconscious, is pushed into the unconscious." (022-009)

Suppression acts on conscious material — an idea that has already reached the foreconscious — and pushes it back into the unconscious. Repression, by contrast, acts on material that has never been conscious:

> "Similarly we speak of suppression when the unconscious psychic act is not admitted at all to the adjoining foreconscious system, and is turned back at the threshold by the censor. In this respect suppression coincides with regression." (022-010)

The difference is the starting point: suppression deals with a thought that has become conscious (or preconscious) and is then pushed back; repression blocks a thought from ever reaching consciousness. But the terminus is the same — both consign mental content to the unconscious:

> "In a broader conception of mental development you must include suppression as a form of regression, for suppression too is a return from a higher to a lower stage of psychic development." (022-011)

## Clinical Role in the Neuroses

Suppression plays a central role in symptom formation. The ego, confronted with an impulse it cannot accept, suppresses it:

> "Wherever the ego is threatened by an instinctual demand which it does not feel itself able to satisfy by the method of sublimation, there the ego undergoes suppression." (022-033)

The suppressed impulse does not disappear. It remains active in the unconscious, pressing for discharge:

> "The libido, streaming back, finds the fixation points, and by this means, protected itself by suppressions." (023-006)

Here Freud describes the dynamic interplay: suppression drives the libido back, the libido regresses to infantile fixation points, and from those points it generates symptoms that are compromise formations — satisfying the impulse in disguised form while respecting the suppression.

In Lecture XIX, Freud summarizes the mechanism:

> "Suppression then, for any individual impulse, is the prerequisite of symptom formation." (019-024)

The therapeutic task is to reverse the process:

> "We attempted to release the suppression through analytic treatment." (019-030)

But this release is not a simple permission. The patient must work through the reasons for the suppression — the anxieties, the moral prohibitions, the narcissistic injuries — before the impulse can be consciously integrated.

## Suppression in Everyday Life

Suppression is not pathological in itself. In fact, it is an ordinary and necessary psychic function:

> "So much, then, for suppression. It is, however, only a prerequisite, or, if you will, a preliminary condition for the building of symptoms." (019-030)

Every well-socialized adult practices suppression continuously — holding back aggressive remarks, inhibiting sexual thoughts in professional settings, and pushing aside painful memories. It becomes pathological only when the suppressed impulse, finding no adequate discharge, regresses to infantile modes of satisfaction and produces symptoms.

## The Limits of Suppression

Suppression is an energy-consuming process. Maintaining a thought or impulse in the unconscious requires continuous counter-investment. When this counter-investment fails — through fatigue, illness, or the sheer pressure of the impulse — the suppressed material returns:

> "The ego, withdrawing itself from the disagreeable instinctual impulse, which appeared as a temptation, protected itself by suppressions. The libido, streaming back, finds the fixation points." (023-006)

The return of the suppressed is the moment of symptom formation. What returns is never the original impulse in pure form, but a compromise distorted by the same forces that effected the suppression.
