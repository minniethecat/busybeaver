# Alfred Adler

## Overview

Alfred Adler (1870–1937) was an Austrian physician and psychotherapist who was among the earliest associates of Sigmund Freud and the Vienna Psychoanalytic Society. He later developed his own school of thought — Individual Psychology — which diverged from Freudian psychoanalysis on several fundamental points. In the *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, Freud engaged with Adler's ideas both critically and appreciatively, acknowledging his contributions while contesting his theoretical departures.

## Theoretical Contributions

### The Nervous Character

Adler's most prominent concept in the *Introductory Lectures* is that of the "nervous character." Freud described Adler's position as asserting that "the 'nervous character' is the cause instead of the result of the disease" — a reversal of the psychoanalytic view that character traits result from neurotic conflict. (024-007)

> Whoever does not heed these warnings not to mistake the prevarications of the ego for truth, has clear sailing; he avoids all the resistances which oppose the psychoanalytic emphasis upon the unconscious, on sexuality, and on the passiveness of the ego. He will assert with Alfred Adler that the "nervous character" is the cause instead of the result of the neurosis, but he will not be able to explain a single detail of symptom formation or to interpret a single dream.
>
> (024-007)

This disagreement pointed to a deeper divergence: Adler saw neurosis as rooted in the individual's characterological response to felt inferiority, whereas Freud traced it to unconscious conflict and libidinal development.

### Inferiority and Compensation

Freud acknowledged Adler's contribution to the understanding of helplessness and inferiority in psychical life. He referred to "helplessness — inferiority, in the terminology of A. Adler" when discussing the infant's initial state of dependence and the lifelong consequences of this early experience. (025-024) The concept that feelings of inferiority motivate psychological development — which became the cornerstone of Adler's Individual Psychology — is acknowledged in the lectures even as Freud critiqued its claim to explanatory primacy.

### Male and Female Tendencies

Adler's framework included the idea that psychological tendencies could be designated "as male and female" — a theory of psychical bisexuality that Freud noted in the lectures. (015-016, 015-021) This concept, while later abandoned by mainstream psychoanalysis, reflected Adler's interest in the role of social and cultural factors in shaping individual psychology, a theme that would distinguish his later work from Freud's biologically-grounded theory.

## Break with Freud

Adler's relationship with Freud followed a trajectory common among the first generation of psychoanalysts: initial collaboration, growing theoretical divergence, and eventual rupture. Unlike Freud, who emphasized the primacy of the sexual drives and the unconscious, Adler came to see the striving for power and superiority — rooted in the child's experience of inferiority — as the central motivating force in human psychology. This fundamental disagreement led Adler to resign from the Vienna Psychoanalytic Society in 1911, founding his own Society for Free Psychoanalytic Research (P01-006), later renamed the Society for Individual Psychology.

## Legacy

Adler's Individual Psychology influenced later developments in psychotherapy, particularly in the humanistic and cognitive traditions. His concepts of the inferiority complex, birth order effects, and social interest entered popular psychology and continue to be discussed. While Freud's *Introductory Lectures* treat Adler's ideas primarily as contrasts to psychoanalytic theory, the engagement itself testifies to the significance of Adler's challenge and the seriousness with which it was met.
