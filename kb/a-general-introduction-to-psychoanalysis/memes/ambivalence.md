## Overview

**Ambivalence** (_Ambivalenz_) is the emotional condition in which hostile and affectionate feelings are directed simultaneously toward the same person. The term was coined by the Swiss psychiatrist [[Eugen Bleuler]] and adopted by Freud.

Ambivalence is observable across the spectrum of psychic life, from the normal intimacy of personal relationships to the pathological dynamics of [[Melancholia|melancholia]] and the [[Transference|transference]] in psychoanalytic treatment.

## Definition

Freud defines ambivalence as the coexistence of contrary emotional impulses — love and hate, tenderness and hostility — toward a single object. In Lecture 26, he introduces the concept through the clinical picture of melancholia:

> In melancholia as well as in other narcistic conditions a feature of emotional life is strikingly shown which, since the time of Bleuler, we have been accustomed to designate as ambivalence. By this we mean that hostile and affectionate feelings are directed against one and the same person. (026-042)

## Origins and Conceptual History

The term was coined by Eugen Bleuler (1857–1939), the Swiss psychiatrist and early collaborator of Freud, within the context of his work on schizophrenia (then termed dementia praecox). Bleuler identified ambivalence as one of the fundamental symptoms of schizophrenia, alongside autism and loosening of associations.

Freud adopted the term and expanded its application beyond psychosis, recognizing ambivalence as a general feature of human emotional life — one that becomes particularly visible under the magnifying lens of neurosis and psychoanalytic treatment (026-042).

## Clinical Manifestations

### In Melancholia and Narcissistic Conditions

Freud first discusses ambivalence in the context of melancholia. In the process of narcissistic identification — where the lost object is internalized and built up within the ego — the ego becomes the target of the same mixture of love and aggression that was originally directed at the object:

> The personal ego is now treated in the same manner as the abandoned object, and suffers all the aggression and expressions of revenge which were planned for the object. Even the suicidal tendencies of melancholia are more comprehensible when we consider that this bitterness of the patient falls alike on the ego itself and on the object of its love and hate. (026-042)

This insight — that suicidal impulses may represent aggression originally intended for a loved-and-hated other — is one of the most clinically consequential implications of the ambivalence concept.

### In the Transference

Ambivalence manifests with particular clarity in the [[Transference|transference relationship]]. The patient's feelings toward the analyst oscillate between affectionate inclination (positive transference) and hostile impulses (negative transference):

| Transference Type | Emotional Valence | Clinical Significance |
|------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Positive transference | Affectionate, tender, desiring | Fuels the therapeutic work; can become resistance when eroticized |
| Negative transference | Hostile, defiant, aggressive | Almost always a resistance, but equally a form of emotional attachment |
| Ambivalent transference | Simultaneous positive and negative | Reveals the fundamental ambivalence of all intimate relationships |

Freud notes the diagnostic value of ambivalence in the transference:

> When they occur simultaneously they exemplify the ambivalence of emotions which exists in most of the intimate relations between all persons. The hostile feelings connote an emotional attachment just as do the affectionate impulses, just as defiance signifies dependence as well as does obedience. (027-033)

## Theoretical Significance

The concept of ambivalence bridges several domains of psychoanalytic theory (027-033):

| Domain | Role of Ambivalence |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Affect theory** | Demonstrates that contrary emotions are not mutually exclusive but can coexist |
| **Object relations** | Reveals the complexity of attachment — every intimate bond contains the seed of its opposite |
| **Theory of therapy** | Explains resistance as an expression of attachment, not its absence |
| **Psychopathology** | Links melancholia, obsessional neurosis, and paranoia through a shared emotional structure |
