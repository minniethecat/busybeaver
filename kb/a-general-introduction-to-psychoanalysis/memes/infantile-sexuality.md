# Infantile Sexuality

**Infantile sexuality** is the sexual life of the child, which Freud argued begins not at puberty but in **early infancy**, and serves as the developmental foundation from which both adult sexuality and neurotic symptoms arise. The study of infantile sexuality formed a cornerstone of Freud's general theory of the neuroses. (020-031) (020-032)

## The Discovery and Its Resistance

Freud acknowledged that the assertion of a sexual life in childhood provoked the strongest resistance, even among physicians: (020-031)

> [020-031] The first of the two decisive characteristics of infantile sexuality is that it arises in connection with the satisfaction of the great organic needs.

The second characteristic Freud identified was that infantile sexuality has no centralized organization — it is composed of **component instincts** (partial drives), each seeking its own pleasure independently, before they are later unified under the primacy of the genital zone. (020-021)

## Component Instincts and Erogenous Zones

Infantile sexuality is not organized around the genitals as in adulthood. Instead, it manifests through various **erogenous zones** — areas of the body capable of producing sexual excitation:

- **The oral zone** — The infant's sucking at the mother's breast is not merely nutritional; it is the prototype of every later sexual satisfaction
- **The anal zone** — Retention and expulsion of feces produce pleasurable sensations
- **The genital zone** — Early infantile masturbation, later repressed in the latency period

Each component instinct pursues its own satisfaction independently. Only at puberty do these partial drives become subordinated to the genital zone and organized toward reproduction. (020-021)

## Infantile Amnesia and Its Significance

A crucial observation for Freud was the **amnesia** that covers the earliest years of childhood — a phenomenon so universal it is generally taken for granted: (018-028)

> [018-028] The direct continuation of the infantile amnesias, which hides the beginnings of the sexual life from our conscious memory, is the amnesia of the hysteric, and this amnesia we have come to understand as a product of repression.

The amnesia that hides infantile sexuality from conscious memory is not a passive fading of impressions but an active **repression** — the same mechanism that in adulthood produces neurotic amnesias. (018-028)

## The Latency Period

Between approximately ages five and puberty, infantile sexuality enters a **latency period** during which:

- Sexual impulses are sublimated into cultural and educational activities
- The component instincts are partly repressed and partly diverted toward non-sexual aims
- Reaction-formations such as disgust, shame, and morality are constructed against sexual impulses

This latency is a period of **damming-up**; the forces that oppose sexual impulses (disgust, shame, morality) are erected during this time. (020-025)

## Perversion and Infantile Sexuality

The study of the **perversions** provided Freud with a key to understanding infantile sexuality. The perversions demonstrate component instincts that have escaped the normal developmental path and persisted in isolation: (020-021) (021-002)

> [020-021] The assertion that all inclinations to perversion have their origin in childhood, that children have a predisposition to all perversions and indeed practice them to an extent corresponding to their immaturity — this assertion will certainly turn you against me.

Freud argued that the perversions are not "degenerations" but rather **magnified and isolated components** of infantile sexuality: "the neuroses are, so to speak, the negative of the perversions." (020-021) What appears in the pervert as an overt practice appears in the neurotic as a repressed fantasy that generates symptoms.

The perversions reveal what infantile sexuality contains before repression: (021-003)

> [021-003] The sexual perversions of adults, on the other hand, are something very palpable, unambiguous, and tangible. We have from this side, then, the significant agreement that the sexuality of neurotics from its pre-history leads back mostly to perverse sexual experiences of childhood.

## Role in the Development of Neurosis

Infantile sexuality is central to Freud's theory of neurosis formation. Neurotic symptoms are **substitute formations** for repressed infantile sexual impulses: (023-009) (013-025)

> [013-025] The symptoms of the neuroses are, one might say, the sexual activity of the patient, the substitute for it.

When the component instincts of infantile sexuality are inadequately repressed or abnormally strong, they resurface in adult life as neurotic symptoms — compromises between the forbidden impulse and the forces of repression. The neurotic symptom thus represents "magnified infantile sexuality divided into its separate tendencies." (020-021)

The dream, too, in its nightly regression, leads back to this infantile stage: (013-029)

> [013-029] The dream-work nightly leads us back to this infantile stage. Thus it becomes more comprehensible to us that the unconscious of our conscious mental life is that infantile stage which we find again in the dream.

## Clinical Implications

The recognition of infantile sexuality fundamentally altered the theory of the neuroses:

1. **Etiology** — The roots of neurosis lie in the sexual history of early childhood, not simply in hereditary predisposition
2. **Therapeutic task** — Analysis must recover the infantile sexual history that has been repressed, filling in the "infantile memory gaps" (013-008)
3. **Transference** — The patient's feelings toward the analyst are revivals of infantile sexual attitudes toward the parents

The study of infantile sexuality also illuminated the normal course of human development, showing that the path from infancy to adult genital organization passes through a series of stages, each with its own erogenous primacy and characteristic conflicts.
