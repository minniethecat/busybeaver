# Psychoanalysis

## Overview

[[Psychoanalysis]] is at once a method of investigation, a therapeutic technique, and a body of theory. In the *Introductory Lectures*, Freud uses the term to designate the discipline he founded — a discipline that takes [[The Unconscious|unconscious]] mental processes as its primary object of study and that proceeds by interpreting the disguised manifestations of those processes in dreams, symptoms, and [[Parapraxes|parapraxes]]. Unlike academic psychology, which studies conscious experience through introspection, psychoanalysis posits that the greater part of mental life is unconscious and accessible only through the indirect methods of [[Free Association|free association]] and dream interpretation.

## Definition

Freud offers a compact definition early in the first lecture:

> "A General Introduction to Psychoanalysis — I am obliged to proceed as though you knew nothing of the subject and stood in need of preliminary instruction." (001-001)

This framing establishes psychoanalysis as a teachable discipline with a definite content, not an esoteric art. But Freud quickly acknowledges that learning psychoanalysis requires a distinctive mode of engagement:

> "Psychoanalysis is learned, first of all, from oneself, through the study of one's own personality." (001-010)

The analyst must first be analyzed — a principle that distinguishes psychoanalytic training from every other form of medical or academic instruction.

## The Psychoanalytic Method

### Free Association and Dream Interpretation

The fundamental rule of psychoanalytic technique is that the patient must say whatever comes to mind without censorship:

> "There is a feasible way. Psychoanalysis is learned, first of all, from oneself, through the study of one's own personality." (001-010)

Dream interpretation serves as the "royal road" to the unconscious. In Lecture X, Freud notes that psychoanalysis confirmed the insights of earlier dream researchers:

> "The philosopher K. A. Scherner (1861). Psychoanalysis affirmed Scherner's..." (010-011)

The method extends to all the "little indications" of everyday life. Psychoanalysis "sees no reason for hiding matters" (010-019) — it treats the apparently trivial (slips, jokes, forgotten names) as material of the same order as dreams and symptoms.

### Relationship to Psychiatry

Freud is careful to distinguish psychoanalysis from contemporary psychiatry:

> "Psychoanalysis stands in about the same relation to psychiatry as histology does to anatomy." (016-030)

Psychiatry describes and classifies; psychoanalysis explains the dynamic processes that produce the clinical picture. The two disciplines are complementary, not competitive.

## Development in the Lectures

The *Introductory Lectures* are themselves the most systematic exposition of psychoanalysis Freud ever produced. In Lecture XXIV, he acknowledges the limits of the title:

> "The title, A General Introduction to Psychoanalysis, no longer applies to this part of our discussions." (024-005)

By this point the lectures have moved beyond introduction into specialized territory — the general theory of the neuroses — and Freud concedes that the audience has become a seminar rather than a lecture hall.

## Psychoanalysis as Science

Freud repeatedly defends the scientific status of psychoanalysis against its critics:

> "Psychoanalysis is characterized as a science not by the material which it handles but by the technique with which it works." (024-023)

The method, not the subject matter, defines the science. Because psychoanalysis works with interpretations rather than laboratory measurements, its scientific character has been contested from the beginning. Freud's response is that all sciences deal with interpretation at some level; psychoanalysis is distinctive only in making interpretation its explicit and systematic object.
