# Anxiety

## Overview

[[Anxiety]] is, in Freud's own words, "a nodal point at which the most various and important questions converge, a riddle whose solution would be bound to throw a flood of light upon our whole mental existence" (025-003). Unlike simple fear, which is a reaction to an external danger, neurotic anxiety is the transformed manifestation of dammed-up [[Libido|libido]] — unsatisfied sexual excitation that, finding no path to discharge, is converted into the affect of anxiety. This libido-to-anxiety transformation is, as Freud repeatedly emphasizes, "one of the best known and most frequently observed phenomena" in psychoanalysis (026-003).

## Definition

Freud carefully distinguishes anxiety from ordinary fear:

> "Fear itself needs no introduction; everyone has at some time or other experienced this sensation... I think it was a most meritorious act on the part of the English language to have two separate words for two such distinct aspects: 'fear' and 'anxiety.'" (025-002)

The distinction is crucial. Fear has an object — one fears something specific. Anxiety, in the neurotic sense, is objectless; it is a state of expectant readiness for a danger that never arrives from the external world. It arises endogenously from the accumulation of unsatisfied libido:

> "Non-satisfaction of hunger and thirst, the two most elementary instincts of self-preservation, never result in their reversal into anxiety, while the transformation of unsatisfied libido into fear is, as we have heard, one of the best known and most frequently observed phenomena." (026-003)

This asymmetry between the ego instincts and the sexual instincts provides Freud with a crucial argument for the independence of the libido and its unique plasticity.

## Development in the Lectures

### The Dream Theory: Anxiety Dreams

The problem of anxiety already surfaces in Part II, where anxiety dreams pose a challenge to the wish-fulfillment theory. A dream of fear seems to contradict the thesis that every dream is the fulfillment of a wish:

> "It will not be difficult now to come to a better understanding of the anxiety dream." (014-013)

Freud resolves this by arguing that anxiety in the dream signals the failure of the dream-work: the repressed wish breaks through with insufficient distortion, generating anxiety rather than the pleasurable discharge of a successfully disguised wish. "Anxiety is the mark which shows that the suppressed wish proved stronger than the censor" (014-013).

### The True Neuroses and the Actual Neuroses

In Lecture XXIV, Freud discusses the "true neuroses" — neurasthenia, anxiety neurosis, and hypochondria — which arise not from psychic conflict but from the direct somatic effects of dammed-up libido (024-025). Anxiety neurosis occupies a middle position:

> "A noteworthy connection between the symptoms of the true neuroses and the psychoneuroses... contributes much to our knowledge of symptom formation in the latter, namely, that the symptoms of the true neurosis are frequently the nucleus and forerunner of the psychoneurotic symptom." (024-026)

This observation has clinical importance: an untreated anxiety neurosis may provide the somatic core around which a full psychoneurosis (hysteria or compulsion neurosis) later crystallizes.

### Lecture XXV: Fear and Anxiety

Freud devotes Lecture XXV entirely to the problem of anxiety. He identifies the principal forms of neurotic anxiety:

> "Proceeding now to neurotic fear, what are its manifestations and conditions? Much could be said here. First, there is a general apprehensiveness, a freely floating anxiety ready to attach itself for the time being to any suitable idea." (025-016)

This "expectant fear" or general apprehensiveness is the purest form of neurotic anxiety — free-floating, unattached to any specific object. From it, the various phobias develop as secondary formations: the anxiety becomes bound to specific objects or situations (heights, open spaces, animals), which the patient can then avoid, at the cost of a constricted life.

Freud also notes the etiological role of sexual privation:

> "It is not difficult to determine that expectant fear or general apprehensiveness is intimately dependent upon certain processes in the sexual life... It occurs, for instance, to a particularly high degree in persons who practice coitus interruptus." (025-026)

The accumulated sexual excitation, denied its normal discharge, is directly transformed into anxiety — a somatic rather than a psychical process, characteristic of the true neuroses rather than the psychoneuroses.

## Clinical Significance

The libido-to-anxiety transformation has profound implications for psychoanalytic therapy:

| Process | Mechanism | Clinical Presentation |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Libido accumulation | Sexual excitation denied discharge (025-026) | Free-floating anxiety |
| Conversion to anxiety | Direct somatic transformation (026-003) | Anxiety equivalents (tremor, palpitations) |
| Binding in phobia | Anxiety attached to external object (017-031) | Phobic avoidance |
| Symptom formation | Anxiety crystallized as psychoneurotic symptom (024-026) | Hysteria or compulsion neurosis |

The therapeutic task is to trace anxiety back to its libidinal source and restore the libido's capacity for normal discharge — either through direct satisfaction or through [[Sublimation]].
