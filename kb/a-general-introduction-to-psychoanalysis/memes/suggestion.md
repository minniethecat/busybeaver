# Suggestion

## Overview

Suggestion is the psychological mechanism underlying hypnotic therapy. The physician commands the removal of symptoms through authority rather than through insight. In his *Introductory Lectures on Psychoanalysis*, Freud contrasts suggestion with psychoanalytic treatment. (028-001)

Freud was a pupil of Hippolyte Bernheim and practiced hypnosis for years. He concluded that suggestion, while rapid and painless, is unreliable and impermanent. (028-004)

Psychoanalysis rediscovers suggestion in the form of transference. But it harnesses transference for a different purpose: resolving the underlying conflict rather than suppressing symptoms. (027-040)

## Definition

Freud defines direct suggestion as "suggestion directed against the expression of the symptoms, a struggle between your authority and the motives of the disease." (028-002)

The physician "pays no attention during this process to the motives, but only demands of the patient that he suppress their expression in symptoms." (028-002)

It makes no difference whether the patient is hypnotized. Bernheim demonstrated that "suggestion is the essential phenomenon underlying hypnotism, that hypnotism itself is already a result of suggestion." (028-002)

> Direct suggestion is suggestion directed against the expression of the symptoms, a struggle between your authority and the motives of the disease. In this process you pay no attention to the motives, but only demand of the patient that he suppress their expression in symptoms.

## Development in the Lectures

### Bernheim's Suggestive Therapy

Freud describes his personal history with suggestion. He sought out Bernheim in Nancy in 1889 and translated Bernheim's book on suggestion into German. For years, he practiced hypnotic treatment, first with prohibitory suggestions alone, later combining it with Breuer's investigative method. (028-004)

Evaluated against the physician's standard — rapid, reliable, and not unpleasant — suggestion meets only two of three criteria. It is far more rapid than analysis and painless for the patient. But reliability is lacking. (028-004)

"The procedure was in no way reliable. It might succeed in one case, and fail with the next." Results proved impermanent. "After a short time, the old malady had reappeared, or it had been replaced by a new malady." (028-004)

Repeated hypnosis could "deprive the patient of his self-reliance and accustom him to this therapy as though it were a narcotic." (028-004)

### The Transference Connection

Freud acknowledges that psychoanalysis works with suggestion — but in the form of transference. Bernheim stated that all persons are open to suggestion. Freud translates: "Suggestibility in his sense is nothing more than an inclination to transference." (027-040)

The crucial difference: Bernheim could not define suggestion or its origin. "He did not recognize the dependence of suggestibility upon sexuality and the activity of the libido." (027-040) Psychoanalysis "excluded hypnosis from our technique of neurosis only to rediscover suggestion in the shape of transference." (027-040)

### The Erotic Root of Suggestive Authority

Freud provides a striking anecdote. A patient he had repeatedly helped through hypnosis, during a stubborn attack, "suddenly threw her arms around my neck." (028-004) This forced him to confront the nature of the suggestive authority, exposing its erotic foundation.

### The Dynamic Critique

Freud objects that "it is contrary to our dynamic conceptions that we should be able to move a great weight by an inconsiderable force, by attacking it directly without taking the trouble to remove the cause." (028-005)

Suggestion suppresses symptoms without addressing the conflict. Psychoanalysis resolves the conflict by making the unconscious conscious. "In renouncing direct suggestion we have given up nothing that is not replaceable." (028-005)

### Suggestion and Narcissistic Neuroses

Suggestion fails entirely with narcissistic neuroses. These patients "have no capacity for transference, or only insufficient remains of it." (027-042) They reject the physician with indifference, not hostility. His words "leave them cold, make no impression." (027-042)

## Key Examples

### The Patient Who Embraced Freud

During hypnotic treatment, a female patient suddenly embraced Freud. This revealed the erotic component underlying suggestive authority. The incident contributed to his abandonment of hypnosis in favor of psychoanalysis. (028-004)

### The Revolving-Door Patient

A patient's condition, cleared by short hypnotic treatment, returned unchanged when she became angry with Freud. After reconciliation, he removed it again. When she became hostile a second time, it returned again. (028-004)

This cycle illustrates the impermanence of suggestive therapy. Symptoms return as soon as the transference turns negative, because the underlying conflict remains unresolved.

### Bernheim's Waking Suggestion

Bernheim showed that hypnosis was unnecessary for suggestion. He practiced suggestion on waking persons "and could achieve the same results as with suggestion under hypnosis." (028-002) This proved that suggestion is the essential phenomenon underlying hypnotism.
