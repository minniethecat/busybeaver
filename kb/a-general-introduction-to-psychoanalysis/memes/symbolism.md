# Symbolism

**Symbolism** is a mode of dream representation in which certain dream elements serve as **constant and permanent translations** of latent dream thoughts. Unlike the individual dream-censor, which operates uniquely for each dreamer, symbolism draws upon a shared, culturally transmitted reservoir of comparisons. Freud identified symbolism as "a second and independent mode of representation" in the dream, alongside the dream-work proper. (010-008) (010-061)

## The Nature of the Symbol Relationship

The symbol relationship is fundamentally a **comparison**, though not one that is freely invented by the dreamer on each occasion. (010-014) As Freud explained:

> [010-009] In the first place, since symbols are permanent or constant translations, they realize in high degree the demands of the dream censor. Secondly, the dream censor finds symbols useful because they permit us at the same time to avoid the direct naming of a subject matter considered objectionable.

Symbols achieve what the dream censor demands — they allow the dream to express objectionable content (typically sexual) without naming it directly. (010-009)

The number of things that find symbolic representation in the dream is not large: the human body as a whole, parents, children, brothers and sisters, birth, death, nakedness — and above all, **sexual life**. (010-015)

## Symbolic and Suggestive Representation

Freud drew a distinction between two types of indirect representation in dreams:

1. **Symbolic representation** — the dream element is a constant, fixed translation of the latent thought, shared across individuals and cultures
2. **Suggestive representation** — the dream element is an associative or allusive link, unique to the individual dreamer's experience (010-016)

The lines between these two classes may blur, and cases occur where the symbolism appears to be individual and must be understood from the dreamer's own associations.

## Dream Symbols: The Sexual Sphere

The overwhelming majority of dream symbols represent **sexual content** — genitals, sexual processes, and sexual intercourse. (010-017)

### Male Genital Symbols

The male organ is represented by objects that share the property of **penetration** or elongated shape, as well as objects that defy gravity in a manner analogous to erection: (010-020)

> [010-021] The phenomena of erection leads to symbolic representations by balloons, airplanes, and more recently, by Zeppelins.

Other male symbols include:
- Elongated objects: **sticks, umbrellas, trees, poles**
- Weapons and tools: **knives, daggers, lances, sabers, revolvers, rifles**
- Water fixtures: **fountains, watering cans**
- Objects that can be drawn out: **pocket knives, keys, pencils, penholders, nail files, hammers**
- Reptiles and fish, particularly the snake (010-022)
- The **number three**, a sacred number that is a symbolic genital character common to both sexes (010-022)

### Female Genital Symbols

The female genital is represented by **hollow objects** capable of containing or enclosing something: (010-023)

- **Pits, caves, hollows**
- **Vessels, bottles, boxes, jewel-cases, trunks, chests, pockets**
- **Ships, cupboards, rooms**
- **Churches, chapels, doors, gates**

The **room** is a particularly significant symbol, and its representation can be extended to include windows, entrances, and exits. (010-030)

A noteworthy female symbol is the **jewel-case**, in which jewelry and treasure represent the beloved person — a symbolism that extends into waking life where "sweets" frequently serve the same representational function. (010-025)

### Symbols of Sexual Intercourse

> [010-027] All manner of rhythmic activities, such as dancing, riding, climbing, and also violent experiences such as being run over, are symbolic of sexual intercourse. Likewise, certain manual crafts and, by extension, musical instruments.

Other intercourse symbols include:
- **Climbing ladders or stairs**
- **Walking into rooms or through doors**
- The landscape: **mountains and cliffs** as male symbols; **gardens** as female symbols (010-029)
- **Breaking a branch**, **falling into water**, **being rescued from water**

## The House as Body Symbol

Drawing upon the work of K.A. Scherner, Freud noted that the **house** represents the entire human body, an insight that appears with particular clarity in anxiety dreams: (010-033)

> [010-036] When we use the house symbol in our dreams, we are speaking of our own body. The smooth walls stand for smooth-skinned men, the projections and balconies represent women — the house-front may be the body seen from in front or from behind.

Within house symbolism, **wood** and **paper** may represent the mother (the material), while **tables** (the familial gathering) and **beds** (the marital) carry specific relational meanings. (010-038)

## Cultural Parallels and Origins

Freud demonstrated that dream symbolism is **not peculiar to the dreamer**, nor is it a creation of the individual dream-work. Rather, it draws from a vast cultural inheritance: (010-032) (010-042)

> [010-032] Everywhere we find the same symbolism and in many of these instances the symbolic interpretation is known to the persons in question without any instruction.

Parallels to dream symbolism appear in:
- **Mythology and folklore**
- **Religious texts and rituals**
- **Fairy tales, jokes, and witticisms**
- **Popular sayings and songs**
- **Literary usage and colloquial idiom** (010-056)

The sexual symbolism of fire, for example — where the flame is always the male organ and the hearth the female womb — is deeply embedded in mythology, from the Prometheus legend to the vestal cult of Rome. (010-045)

## The Psychological Function of Symbols

Symbolism arises from a **phylogenetic inheritance** — an archaic mode of expression that survives in the unconscious. When the dream censor makes the normal pathways of dream formation impassable, the dream-work regresses to this older, symbolic mode: (010-058) (010-061)

> [010-061] Symbolism is a second and independent mode of representation in the dream, possibly a fragment of our ancient phylogenetic inheritance, which has survived in the unconscious and which the dream-work seizes upon when the more usual modes of representation are blocked.

## Clinical Implications

Because symbols are constant, the analyst can interpret certain dream elements **without the dreamer's associations** — a significant practical advantage. (010-010) However, Freud cautioned against over-reliance on symbolic interpretation:

> [010-010] Interpretation based on a knowledge of symbols is not a technique that can replace or even compare with the associative technique. It is supplementary to the latter, and yields results that are only usable when introduced into the associative context.

The analyst must always verify symbolic interpretations against the patient's free associations, and never impose a symbolic reading where the patient's own material points elsewhere.
