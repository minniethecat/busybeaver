# Battle of Cannae

The Battle of Cannae (216 BC) was Hannibal's greatest victory and Rome's most catastrophic defeat. A Roman army of some 80,000 men was encircled and annihilated — yet the defeat paradoxically demonstrated the resilience of Roman institutions: the Republic refused to negotiate, raised new legions, and ultimately prevailed [004-020].

Montesquieu uses Cannae as the central illustration of Rome's unique capacity to absorb disaster without collapsing — a capacity rooted in her political constitution and the loyalty of her Italian allies [004-007].

## Background

The battle occurred during the Second Punic War (218–201 BC), after Hannibal had already inflicted defeats on the Romans at the Ticinus and Trebia rivers (218 BC) and at Lake Trasimene (217 BC). These earlier victories, though serious, were mere preludes to the shock of Cannae [004-020].

Rome's strategy of confronting Hannibal directly with overwhelming force — the consuls Lucius Aemilius Paullus and Gaius Terentius Varro commanded a combined army of unprecedented size — reflected the Roman conviction that any problem could be solved by massed military power [004-021].

## Course

### Phase 1: Deployment on the Aufidus

The battle took place on the banks of the Aufidus River in Apulia [004-021]. The Roman commanders massed their infantry in unprecedented depth, hoping to break through the Carthaginian center by sheer weight of numbers.

Hannibal, recognizing the Roman plan, deployed his troops in a convex crescent formation, with his least reliable troops in the center and his veteran African and Spanish infantry on the wings.

### Phase 2: Encirclement and Annihilation

As the Roman infantry advanced, the Carthaginian center deliberately gave ground, drawing the Romans deeper into the trap. The African infantry on the wings then wheeled inward, while the Carthaginian cavalry — having routed the Roman horse — closed the encirclement from behind.

The result was one of the most complete tactical envelopments in military history. Livy records the extraordinary intensity of the combat: "so great was the ardor of the conflict, so intent were their minds upon the battle, that an earthquake which violently shook the neighboring region was not perceived by a single combatant" [004-021].

## Outcome

The immediate consequences were devastating. Tens of thousands of Romans perished; the allies of southern Italy, seeing Rome apparently broken, defected to Hannibal [004-007].

Yet the Senate — in an act of constitutional resilience that astonished the ancient world — refused to negotiate with Hannibal. The Senate went before the defeated consul Varro and "thanked him for not having despaired of the republic" [004-014].

Rome raised new legions, including from slaves and criminals, and adopted the delaying strategy of Fabius Maximus that would eventually turn the tide of the war.

## Impact on Rome

Montesquieu identifies the Roman response to Cannae as the supreme demonstration of her institutional strength. Prior to Cannae, "no ally had abandoned her," protected by the thirty colonies that served as ramparts [004-007]. Even after the disaster, the core of the Latin alliance held.

The key contrast Montesquieu draws is with Carthage. When Carthage faced defeat, her institutions could not correct the abuse. When Rome faced Cannae, she could — because "from its origin its constitution was such... that all abuses could be corrected" [008-010]. The battle thus reveals not Roman military superiority but Roman constitutional superiority.

The historian Polybius judged that Hannibal's decision not to march on Rome immediately after Cannae "is firmly believed to have been the preservation of the city and the empire" [004-024]. Whether Rome could have survived an immediate siege remains one of history's great counterfactuals.
