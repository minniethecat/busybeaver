# Constantine the Great

Constantine (Flavius Valerius Constantinus, 272–337 AD) was Roman emperor from 306 to 337 AD.

In Montesquieu's analysis of Roman decline, Constantine occupies a uniquely consequential position: not as a tyrant or a monster like [[nero|Nero]] or Caligula, but as a ruler whose structural decisions — the founding of a new capital, the division of the empire, and the redistribution of the legions — set in motion the forces that would ultimately destroy the Western Empire.

Montesquieu identifies Constantine's reign as the pivot on which the Roman world turned from a unified Mediterranean power centered on Rome to a divided state whose western half could no longer defend itself. "After having weakened the capital, Constantine struck a blow upon the frontiers" (017-007).

## Life

### Early Life

Constantine was born in 272 AD at Naissus (modern Niš, Serbia), the son of Constantius Chlorus, who became one of the four rulers in Diocletian's tetrarchy as Caesar and later Augustus of the West.

His early life was shaped by the political convulsions of the tetrarchic system. He spent his youth at the court of Diocletian in Nicomedia, effectively as a hostage ensuring his father's loyalty, where he observed both the strengths and the instabilities of divided imperial rule.

Montesquieu's text passes over Constantine's origins, treating him less as an individual personality and more as the agent of structural transformations. He appears in the narrative fully formed as an emperor whose decisions would reshape the geography of Roman power.

### Career

Constantine was proclaimed emperor by his father's troops at York in 306 AD upon the death of Constantius. In the years that followed, he fought a series of civil wars against rival claimants — Maxentius, Maximian, and Licinius — before emerging as sole ruler of the empire.

Montesquieu records the military dimension of this struggle with characteristic brevity: "the Asiatic legions of Niger, Constantine those of Licinius" were overcome in the succession of civil wars that plagued the empire (016-004).

The war between Constantine and Licinius, which ended in Licinius's defeat in 324 AD, receives particular notice: "The events of this war are very apparent in the war between Constantine and Licinius" (019-010), as Priscus recorded in his history.

With his victory over Licinius, Constantine became the undisputed master of the Roman world. The empire, which had been divided among multiple rulers since Diocletian, was "united under a single ruler — as under Constantine, under Julian, and finally under Theodosius the Great" (017-015).

This unification, however, was temporary; the structural forces pulling the empire apart were already in motion.

Montesquieu's most sustained criticism of Constantine concerns the emperor's two great projects: the founding of Constantinople and the reorganization of the frontier defenses.

The new capital was, in Montesquieu's view, a act of imperial vanity with disastrous consequences:

> Galerius and Constantius Chlorus, not being able to agree, actually divided the empire; and, this example being afterwards followed by Constantine — who adopted the plan of Galerius and not that of Diocletian — a custom was introduced which was less a change than a revolution. (017-004)

> Furthermore, Constantine's desire to found a new city, and the vanity which prompted him to endow it with his name, determined him to carry the seat of empire to the East. (017-004)

The economic consequences were immediate and severe:

> In order that the new city might in no respect fall short of the old one, Constantine desired that grain should be distributed therein also; and he ordered that the grain of Egypt should be sent to Constantinople, and that of Africa to Rome — a provision which does not appear to me to have been a very sensible one. (017-005)

Montesquieu explicitly faults Constantine for thoughtlessly transplanting the republican institution of the grain dole — which had originated when "the Roman people — sovereign of all others — should naturally have received some part of the tributes" — to a new city "without good reason" (017-005).

This transfer of population, wealth, and administrative attention from Italy to the Bosporus drained the West of its vitality.

The military dimension was equally damaging. Constantine withdrew the legions from the frontier rivers and dispersed them among the provinces:

> After having weakened the capital, Constantine struck a blow upon the frontiers. He withdrew the legions which had been stationed upon the shores of the great rivers, and distributed them in the provinces — a measure which involved two evils: the barrier was removed which kept back the barbarous nations; and the soldiers, addicted to the theatre and the circus, became effeminate. (017-007)

The consequences were visible within a generation:

> When Julian was sent into the Gauls by Constantine, he found that fifty cities along the Rhine had been captured by the barbarians; that the provinces had been sacked; and that there was nothing more than the shadow of a Roman army, which the very name of an enemy was sufficient to put to flight. (017-008)

### Later Years & Death

Constantine died in 337 AD near Nicomedia, having received baptism on his deathbed. He was succeeded by his three sons — Constantine II, Constantius II, and Constans — who promptly fell into dynastic conflict, confirming the instability of the system he had built.

His name echoed through the later empire. Montesquieu's analytical index singles out "the bad policy of Constantine in providing for the distribution of grain" (023-029) as a lasting marker of his structural errors. A later, unrelated Constantine — "Constantine, the son of Heraclius" (022-004) — would preside over yet another phase of imperial decay, but the trajectory had been set by the first.

## Significance

For Montesquieu, Constantine represents the moment when Roman decline became institutionalized — not through vicious tyranny but through structural decisions whose full consequences were not immediately apparent. His significance can be assessed along several axes:

- **The Eastern Drift**: By founding Constantinople and moving the imperial court eastward, Constantine "determined him to carry the seat of empire to the East" (017-004).

This geographical reorientation, combined with the division of the empire, meant that "when the seat of empire came to be established in the East, almost the entire population of Rome proceeded thither" (017-005), depopulating Italy and leaving the West exposed.

- **The Frontier Collapse**: Constantine's redistribution of the legions from the great rivers to interior provinces removed the defensive barrier that had held the barbarians at bay for centuries. This decision transformed the Rhine and Danube from fortified frontiers into highways for invasion (017-007).

- **The Effeminization of the Soldiery**: A subtler but equally consequential effect was moral: soldiers stationed in provincial cities, "addicted to the theatre and the circus, became effeminate" (017-008). The martial virtue that had been the foundation of Roman greatness was systematically eroded.

- **Economic Disruption**:

The transplantation of the grain dole from Rome to Constantinople was an act of institutional mimicry that ignored the organic relationship between the Roman people, their tributes, and their sovereignty. "When the whole system is changed, only such abuses can be remedied as present themselves in theory, whilst those which experience only can discover, are left unprovided for" (017-007).

- **The Division Becomes Permanent**: Although the empire was repeatedly reunited under single rulers — "as under Constantine, under Julian, and finally under Theodosius the Great" (017-015) — the habit and structure of division had taken root. Each partition made the next more likely, until the final separation of East and West became irreversible.

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's judgment of Constantine is remarkably negative for a figure whom Christian historiography has traditionally celebrated.

While acknowledging that the establishment of [[christianity|Christianity]] was aided by the arrival of foreign emperors who brought "something which was peculiar to his own country" (016-006), he does not credit Constantine personally with the Christianization of the empire.

His focus remains relentlessly on the political, military, and economic consequences of the emperor's decisions.

The ecclesiastical historians, by contrast, draw a sharp distinction. Montesquieu notes that they "declare that they only intend to speak of such actions of this emperor as relate to piety, and not of those which relate to the government of the state" (017-007, footnote citing Eusebius and Socrates).

This methodological disclaimer is itself a subtle critique: the sacred historians have, in Montesquieu's implied judgment, systematically omitted the most consequential aspects of Constantine's reign.

The translator Baker's statistical analysis places Constantine within the third period of imperial history, when the rate of violent death among emperors declined from seventy-three percent to fifty-one percent (016-034).

Constantine himself died naturally — a relatively rare outcome in the bloody lottery of imperial succession — but the structural damage he inflicted on the empire long outlasted his personal longevity.
