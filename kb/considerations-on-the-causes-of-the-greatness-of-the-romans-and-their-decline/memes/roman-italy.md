# Roman Italy

Roman Italy is the peninsula on which [[ancient-rome]] rose from a single city beside the Tiber to mistress of the Mediterranean world. Montesquieu treats it not as scenery but as the engine of Roman greatness: a continuous territory peopled by warlike nations whose manpower, once subdued and then incorporated, became the army of the [[roman-republic]].

Its peoples — the Latins, Sabines, Samnites, Etrurians, and the cities of Campania — first resisted Rome with "inconceivable resistance," then supplied the legions that broke [[carthage]] and [[macedon]]. The conquest of Italy and the slow art of binding allies were, for Montesquieu, the foundation upon which all later conquest stood (006-014).

The peninsula was also Rome's first experiment in governing the conquered. The treaty with the Latins after Lake Regillus contained "not a single word which might excite a suspicion of empire," yet became "one of the principal foundations of their power" (006-014) — a method of rule the [[roman-senate-body]] would later extend across the world.

## Geography

The geographical position of Rome, Montesquieu's translator notes, "was admirably adapted to empire," founded "not very far from the center" of southern Europe, northern Africa, and southwestern Asia (001-015). Italy lay at the crossing-point of the ancient world.

Camillus, dissuading the Romans from abandoning their burnt city for Veii, praised the site at length. The speech ends by naming Italy itself as the source of the city's advantage:

> close enough to the sea for all purposes of commerce, and not exposed by too much proximity to the dangers of foreign fleets ; a situation in the center of the regions of Italy, singularly adapted by nature to the increase of a city. (001-015)

Within the peninsula, Rome's earliest territory was minute. Veii, an Etrurian town across the Tiber, lay "only about twelve miles from Rome — so small was yet the territory of the Romans, after three hundred and fifty years of all but incessant war" (001-011).

The decisive geographic fact, against Carthage, was continuity. "The power of Rome was massed in the peninsula of Italy," resting "upon the solid physical base of a territory which was continuous" — where Carthage held only "scattered outposts" (004-025).

## History

### The peoples before and around Rome

Before the Romans, Italy was old. The immigrating tribes "found the Etrurians already old in Italy — a people of high civilization for ancient times" (001-022), who had themselves been preceded by peoples in the stone-using state "usually called savage."

The peninsula's nations were unequal in spirit. Montesquieu divides them sharply between the soft and the warlike:

> The peoples of Italy were not all equally warlike. The Etrurians were enervated by their wealth and by their luxury ; the Tarentines, the Capuans, and nearly all the cities of Campania and Magna Grecia, languished in indolence and in the pursuit of pleasure. (001-010)

Against these stood Rome's hardy neighbors — the Latins, the Hernicans, the Sabines, the Aequians, and the Volscians — who "loved war passionately," surrounded Rome, and "were her equals in point of obstinacy" (001-010). Rome's own power was first "greatly increased by her union with the Sabines" (001-002).

The [[latin-league|Latin cities]] shared Rome's blood and rites. They "were colonies from Alba," and after defeat near Lake Regillus "were reduced to an alliance and military association with the Romans" (001-010).

### Conquest under the Republic

Italy's subjugation was slow precisely because its peoples fought well. "It is this that explains the resistance of the Italian peoples, and at the same time the obstinacy of the Romans in the work of subjugating them" (001-009).

Montesquieu makes this slowness a cause of Roman virtue. Had Rome "rapidly conquered all the neighboring cities," she "would have passed too quickly from poverty to wealth and from wealth to corruption" (001-009). Hard Italian wars gave "victories which did not corrupt them."

The fiercest of the peninsular foes were the Samnites — "the most martial of all the peoples of Italy" (001-011). They were "only subjugated after twenty-four triumphs," yet afterward became "auxiliaries of their conquerors" (004-006) — the pattern of incorporation in miniature.

The [[social-war|allied communities]] were graded into kinds. "Some were united with them by privileges, and by participation in their grandeur — as the Latins and the Hernicans; others by the very act of establishment — as their colonies" (006-005). [[roman-colonies|Colonies]] ringed the homeland: Rome "was surrounded by thirty colonies, which served her as so many ramparts" (004-007).

### Manpower — the peninsula at war

The yield of this integrated Italy was its decisive output: men. Shortly before the Second Punic War, Rome drew from her allies a force of staggering size against the [[gaul|Gauls]]:

> the Romans drew from them and from their allies — that is to say, from a country not much larger than the states of the Pope and of Naples — seven hundred thousand infantry and seventy thousand cavalry, to oppose the Gauls. (004-006)

This capacity grew directly from how Rome treated the conquered. "As the latter always regarded the conquered as instruments of future triumphs, they made soldiers of all the peoples whom they subdued" (004-005). The harder a people fought, the more it was judged fit "to be incorporated into their republic."

The contrast with [[carthage]] was total. As Saint-Evremond's note puts it: Carthage "employed foreigners for its wars and citizens for its traffic ; the other made citizens of all nations, and soldiers of its citizens" (004-005).

Italian loyalty held under the worst blows. "Prior to the battle of Cannae no ally had abandoned her. The reason was, that the Samnites and the other peoples of Italy were accustomed to her domination" (004-007). After a Latin levy was once refused, "ten legions were immediately raised in the city" (003-004).

### Later period

In the imperial centuries the peninsula remained the reservoir from which Rome recruited, then widened. "The levy was made in Rome ; later, the levy was extended to the Latins ; later still, to Italy" (016-005) — the franchise of the army spreading outward as the empire grew.

For Montesquieu's translator, Italy's history is in the end the history of Rome itself. Restricted "to the career and the fate of the Latin stock and its Italian allies, that history virtually runs out with the fall of the Western empire" (019-017).

## Strategic Significance

Italy mattered to Montesquieu because its incorporation taught Rome the art that won the world. Rome "never subdued a nation which they did not use as an instrument in the subjugation of others" (006-005), and Italian allies were the first such instruments — "they availed themselves of the Latins and the Hernicans in order to subjugate the Volscians and the Etrurians" (006-008).

The method was deliberate gradualism. Rome's rulers thought "it was necessary to wait until all nations should become accustomed to obey as free states and as allies before commanding them as subjects, thus leaving them to lose themselves little by little in the Roman Republic" (006-014). Italy was the proving ground for this slow absorption.

The strategic core can be set out plainly:

- Continuous territory — Roman power "was massed in the peninsula of Italy," a single block, unlike Carthage's scattered holdings (004-025).

- Warlike manpower — Italy's hardy nations, once subdued, yielded armies of hundreds of thousands "to oppose the Gauls" (004-006).

- Incorporation, not extermination — Rome "made soldiers of all the peoples whom they subdued" (004-005), converting enemies into legionaries.

- A model of governance — the unthreatening treaty with the Latins became "one of the principal foundations of their power" (006-014).

The deepest cause Montesquieu draws from Italy is moral. Because conquest of the peninsula was slow and poor, it bred [[civic-virtue]] rather than luxury; within "a very small" circumference Rome "exercised those virtues which were to become so fatal to the world" (001-010). The greatness of Rome began as the long, hard mastery of Italy.
