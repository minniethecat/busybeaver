# Gaius Marius

Gaius Marius (157–86 BC) was a Roman general, statesman, and seven-time consul.
His career spanned from the Jugurthine War through the Cimbrian War to the first full-scale civil war of the Roman Republic.

A *novus homo* (new man) from the equestrian order of Arpinum, Marius rose to the highest offices through military talent and popular support.
Montesquieu treats Marius as a pivotal figure whose army reforms severed the bond between citizen and republic.

This reform set in motion the chain of events that led from professionalized legions loyal to their generals, through the civil wars of Marius and Sylla, to the ultimate collapse of the republic under Caesar and [[augustus|Augustus]].

## Life

### Early Life and Rise

Marius was born near Arpinum, the same Italian town that later produced [[cicero|Cicero]].
He came from a family of the equestrian order, not the senatorial aristocracy, making his eventual seven consulships all the more remarkable.

He first distinguished himself under Scipio Aemilianus at the siege of Numantia.
Later he served as tribune of the plebs and praetor before marrying Julia, a patrician of the Julian clan — a connection that would later link him to [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]].

### Military Career and Reforms

Marius's career-defining opportunity came with the war against Jugurtha, king of Numidia (112–106 BC).
Appointed consul in 107 BC, he took the unprecedented step of transforming the Roman army's recruitment basis.

Montesquieu records this reform, citing Sallust's *De Bello Jugurtha*:

> Whilst the domination of Rome was limited to Italy, the republic could easily maintain itself. Every soldier was equally a citizen... Servius Tullius had placed them in the sixth class, and soldiers were taken from the first five only. But Marius, preparing for the war against Jugurtha, enrolled all sorts of men indifferently. *Militia scribere*, says Sallust, *non more majorum, neque classibus, sed uti cujusque libido erat, capite censos plerosque*. [009-001]

This reform was practical in the short term — it solved the manpower shortage and provided Marius with devoted troops.
But its structural consequences were fatal to the republic.

By arming the landless poor and making military service a career rather than a civic duty, Marius created soldiers whose loyalty belonged to their general rather than to Rome itself:

> But, when the arms of the republic were carried beyond the Alps and the sea, the soldiers, who had to be left during many campaigns in the countries which were subdued, gradually lost the spirit of Roman citizens. It was then that the legionaries began to recognize their general only — to found all their hopes upon him — and to see the city at a greater distance. They ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey, and of Caesar. [009-002]

After defeating Jugurtha (through the diplomacy and capture engineered by his quaestor, Lucius Sulla), Marius returned to Rome a hero.
He was elected consul for five consecutive years (104–100 BC) to face the emergency of the Cimbrian and Teutonic migrations — Germanic tribes threatening Italy from the north.

Demonstrating Roman commitment to rebuilding military effectiveness through discipline, Marius restored his legions' readiness through enormous physical labor:

> In order to beat the Cimbrians and the Teutons, Marius commenced by turning the courses of rivers; and Sylla imposed such grievous labors upon his soldiers — demoralized in the war against Mithridates — that they demanded battle as an end of their toils. [002-005]

Marius annihilated the Teutones at Aquae Sextiae in 102 BC and, together with his colleague Catulus, destroyed the Cimbri at Vercellae in 101 BC.
These victories secured Italy's northern frontier and earned Marius the title "Third Founder of Rome" (after Romulus and Camillus).

Plutarch records that even in old age, Marius maintained the rigorous physical discipline of a Roman commander:

> Marius, notwithstanding his age, repaired every day to the Campus Martius. Pompey, at the age of fifty-eight years, went to the field fully armed, with the young men: he mounted his horse, rode with the bridle down, and hurled his javelin. [002-003]

### Rivalry with Sulla and Civil War

The fatal rivalry between Marius and Sulla was rooted in the Jugurthine War, when Sulla, Marius's quaestor, negotiated the capture of Jugurtha and took credit for ending the war.
The breach widened dramatically when the command against Mithridates of Pontus became available in 88 BC.

Marius, though elderly, coveted this prestigious eastern command.
Sulla, now consul, received it from the senate.
Marius then resorted to political manipulation, as recorded in Montesquieu's footnote:

> As Marius — with a view to obtaining command in the war against Mithridates to the prejudice of Sylla — had, with the assistance of the tribune Sulpitius, distributed eight new tribes of Italian peoples among the ancient ones — a measure which rendered the Italians masters of the ballot — it resulted that the latter were mostly of the party of Marius, whilst the old citizens were of the party of Sylla. [011-001]

The tribune Sulpitius Rufus pushed through a law transferring the Mithridatic command to Marius.
Sulla responded by marching his legions on Rome — the first time a Roman army had entered the city under arms.

Marius fled to Africa, narrowly escaping death.
When Sulla departed for the East, Marius returned to Italy, joined forces with the consul Cinna, and captured Rome in 87 BC.
A bloody purge of Sulla's supporters followed before Marius died of natural causes in 86 BC, aged seventy-one, as Sulla prepared to return from the East.

### Factional Alignment and Historical Judgment

Montesquieu and his translator Baker identify Marius as the leader of the popular faction, opposing Sulla's aristocratic party:

> Sylla was the leader of the former, Marius of the latter; and, whilst Marius was in the ascendant — however extensive may have been his massacres and assassinations — he organized no machinery for systematic destruction, he resorted to no such cold-blooded and awful instrument of tyranny as the proscription. [011-020]

The civil war between Marius and Sulla marked a decisive turning point in Roman history.
What had previously been political competition within constitutional bounds became armed factional warfare, from which the republic never recovered.

Baker's translator's note identifies this as the moment when civic virtue visibly collapsed:

> Within these sixty years fall the Social War, the Civil Wars of Marius and Sylla, and the wholesale Proscriptions of the latter. When these great disorderly phenomena are studied in connection with the previous internal contests which had constantly agitated the republic, they are seen to be of a totally different kind, and clearly to indicate the decline of civic virtue. [009-013]

And further, explaining why compromise became impossible:

> The spirit of compromise, by which their dissensions had been so often reconciled, had wholly disappeared in the blood which had been shed under Marius and Sylla. The contest between these chiefs was the first great armed struggle of the factions. [011-031]

Montesquieu also records a striking observation about Roman popular judgment.
Speaking of the electorate, the text notes:

> when the people elevated some man of little worth, as Varro or Marius, it was a species of victory which they achieved over themselves. [008-005]

This remark reflects the aristocratic bias of the sources Montesquieu draws upon, yet it captures the tension inherent in Marius's career — a man of undeniable ability used by the popular faction, whose methods accelerated the destruction of republican institutions.

## Significance

Marius occupies a pivotal position in Montesquieu's analysis of Roman decline.
His importance lies in three interconnected contributions to the fall of the republic.

**First, the military reform.** By abolishing the property qualification and recruiting from the *capite censi*, Marius severed the link between citizenship and military service.

The citizen-soldier who fought to defend his property was replaced by the professional legionary who fought for booty, land grants, and his commander's favor.
As Montesquieu observes, these men "ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey, and of Caesar" [009-002].

**Second, the precedent of armed factionalism.** The civil war between Marius and Sulla was "the first great armed struggle of the factions" [011-031].

Previous Roman political conflicts, even those of the Gracchi, had been contained within tolerable limits.
The Marian-Sullan conflict introduced organized military violence into domestic politics, shattering the norms of constitutional competition.

**Third, the sequence of military autocracy.** Marius's example established the model that Sulla, Pompey, and Caesar would follow, each more successfully than the last.

Baker notes that "the civic demoralization which first exploded in the wars of Marius and Sylla, and soon afterwards reached a fatal issue for the republic in those of Caesar and Pompey" [009-015].

Paradoxically, Rome's military power abroad continued to grow even as its domestic institutions disintegrated.
Montesquieu observes:

> Under Marius, Sylla, Pompey, Caesar, Anthony, and Augustus, Rome continually grew more terrible, and completed the overthrow of all the kings who yet remained. [011-011]

This paradox — external strength masking internal decay — is central to Montesquieu's theory of decline.

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's analysis draws on Plutarch's *Life of Marius*, which provides both the biographical details (Marius's daily attendance at the Campus Martius [002-003]) and social context.
Plutarch notes the enormous wealth that followed Roman conquests, observing that a house costing seventy-five thousand drachmas for Cornelia soon cost over two million for Lucullus [010-003].

Sallust's *De Bello Jugurtha* provides the definitive account of the army reform [009-001].
Appian's *Civil Wars* narrates the horrors of the Marian-Sullan conflict, from which Montesquieu pointedly averts his gaze:

> I pray that I may be permitted to turn away my eyes from the horrors of the wars of Marius and Sylla. Their frightful history may be found in Appian. [011-001]
