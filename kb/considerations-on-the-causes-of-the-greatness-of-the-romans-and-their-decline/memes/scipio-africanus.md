# Scipio Africanus

Publius Cornelius Scipio Africanus (236–183 BC) was the Roman general who defeated [[hannibal|Hannibal]] at the Battle of Zama in 202 BC, ending the Second Punic War and securing Roman supremacy over [[carthage|Carthage]].

Rising to command at the age of twenty-four after the deaths of his father and uncle in Spain, Scipio demonstrated that the Roman Republic could produce military genius of the highest order within its institutional framework.

His career represents both the apex of Roman military excellence and the threshold of the transformation that his own victories would ultimately bring to the Republic.

## Life

### Early Life

Scipio was born in 236 BC into the patrician Cornelii family, one of the most distinguished gentes of the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]]. His father, Publius Cornelius Scipio, served as consul in 218 BC and commanded Roman forces against Hannibal in the early campaigns of the Second Punic War.

The elder Scipio appears during the events leading to the defeat at the Trebia: Baker notes that this disaster "was preceded by a disagreement between the consuls — Scipio reluctant, and Sempronius pressing the battle" [001-021]. The young Scipio accompanied his father and, according to tradition, saved his father's life at the Battle of Ticinus.

When his father and uncle were killed in Spain in 211 BC, the Republic faced a crisis: no senior commander wished to take up the dangerous Spanish command. At twenty-four, Scipio volunteered and was granted proconsular imperium by an extraordinary vote of the people.

This was an early instance of the practice, later so destructive, by which "the most powerful men induced the people to give them extraordinary commissions" [011-005].

### Career

Scipio's first great achievement was the capture of New Carthage (Cartagena) in 209 BC, the Carthaginian capital in Spain. Montesquieu notes the rigorous military training Scipio imposed on his soldiers after this victory: ancient writers record "the exercises to which Scipio Africanus subjected his soldiers after the capture of New Carthage" [002-003].

This discipline was characteristic of Roman practice, whereby "whenever the Romans regarded themselves as in danger, or when they desired to repair some disaster, it was their constant practice to strengthen their military discipline" [002-005]. The capture of New Carthage gave Rome control of Carthaginian silver mines and logistics in Spain.

After consolidating Roman control over Spain — a campaign so successful that "at the close of one campaign, Scipio received the submission of more than one hundred and twenty 'states'" [005-020] — he returned to Rome and was elected consul for 205 BC.

He then proposed the bold strategy of carrying the war to Africa itself. Montesquieu records that "Hannibal, whose enemies constantly grew stronger, was finally reduced to the necessity of making war on the defensive. This suggested to the Romans the idea of carrying the war into Africa; and Scipio descended upon the southern shore of the Mediterranean" [005-002].

Scipio's landing in Africa in 204 BC had an immediate psychological effect. The vulnerability of Carthage's territorial position meant that "all who disembarked upon the coast of Africa — Agathocles, Regulus, Scipio — immediately reduced Carthage to despair" [004-007].

He formed a crucial alliance with Masinissa, a Numidian prince, thereby depriving Carthage of its superior cavalry. Montesquieu writes: "Having conquered Spain, and formed an alliance with Masinissa, Scipio deprived the Carthaginians of this advantage. It was Numidian cavalry that gained the battle of Zama and ended the war" [004-009].

At Zama in 202 BC, Scipio faced Hannibal himself — recalled from Italy after sixteen years of campaigning. Montesquieu describes the battle: "Not being able to incline Scipio to peace, he gave a battle, in which Fortune seemed to take pleasure in confounding his ability, his experience, and his good sense" [005-002].

Hannibal, "in abandoning to the Romans a country in which he had so often vanquished them, shed tears of bitter regret" [005-002].

The victory was complete. "Carthage received peace, not from an enemy, but from a master" [005-002]. The terms required Carthage to deliver its elephants and fleet, pay ten thousand talents over fifty years, and undertake no war without Roman consent.

### Later Years and Death

After Zama, Scipio returned to Rome in triumph and received the honorific cognomen "Africanus." He served a second consulship in 194 BC and accompanied his brother Lucius in the war against Antiochus III of Syria.

His later years were marked by political attacks from rivals, most notably [[cato-the-elder|Cato the Elder]], who prosecuted the Scipio brothers on charges of financial irregularities. Disgusted by the ingratitude of his fellow citizens, Scipio retired to his estate at Liternum, where he died in 183 BC — the same year as his great adversary Hannibal.

## Significance

Montesquieu's analysis of Scipio Africanus reveals a figure who embodied both the strength and the emerging contradictions of the Roman Republic. On the one hand, Scipio represented the full flowering of Roman military virtue: his rigorous discipline, strategic vision, and diplomatic skill in winning over Masinissa demonstrated the Roman capacity to learn, adapt, and overcome any adversary.

His victory at Zama was not merely a triumph of arms but a triumph of Roman institutional culture — a culture that "devoted their whole mind and soul" to the perfection of war [002-001].

On the other hand, Scipio's career also prefigured the pathologies that would later destroy the Republic. His extraordinary command, granted by popular vote outside the ordinary constitutional process, anticipated the practice by which Pompey and Caesar would later "annihilate the authority of the magistrates and of the people themselves" [011-005].

His immense personal prestige created a model of the individual commander whose glory could eclipse the collective institutions of the state.

Baker's observation is telling: "the illustrious Scipio Africanus himself gave such a spectacle after the death of his father and uncle" [015-020] — referring to gladiatorial combats, an institution that exemplified the dark undertone of Roman civilization, where a "savage element" co-existed alongside its highest achievements.

The contrast Montesquieu draws between Scipio and Hannibal is particularly illuminating. He observes that "the presence of Hannibal caused all divisions to cease among the Romans; but the presence of Scipio embittered those which already existed among the Carthaginians" [004-004].

This asymmetry reveals the deeper structural difference between the two states: Rome, governed by laws and senatorial direction, could unite around a foreign threat; Carthage, "governed by abuses," found its internal divisions exacerbated by the pressure of war. Scipio's victory, in this light, was as much the product of Rome's superior political constitution as of his own military genius.
