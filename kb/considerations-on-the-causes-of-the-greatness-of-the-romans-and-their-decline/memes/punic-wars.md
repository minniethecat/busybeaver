# Punic Wars

The Punic Wars were the long contest between [[ancient-rome|Rome]] and [[carthage|Carthage]] for empire over the Western Mediterranean, fought across three wars from the first struggle in the seas near [[roman-italy|Italy]] down to the final destruction of the African city.

For Montesquieu they are a central drama of the [[roman-republic|Republic]]: a war in which two cities of opposite character — one founded upon arms, the other upon commerce — were locked in "a life and death struggle for the empire of the Mediterranean states" (004-025).

He treats the conflict less as a sequence of battles than as a demonstration of causes, asking why Rome, though brought "to such great peril" (004-025), in the end prevailed and "nearly destroyed this nation" (004-002).

His answer runs through the whole moral architecture of the book. Carthage, "become rich sooner than Rome, was also sooner corrupted" (004-002); she "made war with her opulence against Roman poverty" (004-004) and relied largely upon mercenary troops, while Rome "took the lead in their own fighting" (004-025) with the [[roman-army|legions]] of her own citizens.

The wars therefore become Montesquieu's proof that "virtue, constancy, force, and poverty, are inexhaustible" (004-004) where gold is not — and that the very magnitude of the Carthaginian threat, far from breaking Rome, hardened her into the conqueror of the world.

## Background

Montesquieu frames the rivalry as a clash of constitutions before it was a clash of armies. At Rome "public employments were only obtained by virtue, and yielded no profit except honor and a preference of toil," whereas "all that society could bestow upon the citizen was sold at Carthage, and every service performed for the public was paid for by the state" (004-003).

The Carthaginian government, "founded upon commerce" (004-005), rested upon wealth, in which "individuals possessed the wealth of kings" (004-003); two factions divided the city, "the one constantly desired peace, the other, war," so that at Carthage war "separated them the more," while at Rome "war immediately reunited all interests" (004-004).

This difference in [[civic-virtue|civic character]] set the terms of the struggle. The Romans "were ambitious from pride, the Carthaginians from avarice; those would command, these would acquire" (004-004).

Because Carthage calculated receipts and expenses, "the loss of battles, the diminution of her people, the ruin of her commerce, the exhaustion of her public treasury, and the revolt of the neighboring nations, were sufficient to induce Carthage to accept the hardest conditions of peace" (004-005).

But Rome, "solely determined by the sense of her own glory," would never make a peace which she did not impose (004-005).

Montesquieu also judged the Carthaginian establishment less durable: her power "reposed upon a territory which was detached and straggling" — scattered outposts "in Sicily, Sardinia, Corsica, Malta, the Balearic Islands, and Spain" — while "the power of Rome was massed in the peninsula of Italy" (004-025), surrounded by colonies "which served her as so many ramparts" (004-007).

> Gold and silver may be consumed; but virtue, constancy, force, and poverty, are inexhaustible. (004-004)

## Course

### The Struggle for Sicily

Montesquieu touches the first war chiefly to illustrate how Rome turned weakness into mastery.

The Carthaginians "had more experience on sea than the Romans, and better understood the management of ships" (004-009), and in the first war "Regulus was beaten from the moment that the Carthaginians selected plains for battle, in order to favor the operations of their cavalry" (004-009).

Yet when a stranded Carthaginian galley fell into Roman hands, "they used this as a model for the building of others; in three months time their fleet was constructed and equipped and their seamen ready; they put to sea, found the Carthaginian navy, and beat it" (004-011).

In one great naval battle, he notes, "a hundred and thirty thousand Romans engage with a hundred and fifty thousand Carthaginians" (004-011), an entire land army placed upon a fleet.

### Hannibal's War

The second war is the one Montesquieu calls "so famous that all the world is acquainted with it" (004-012), and in [[hannibal|Hannibal]] he sees "that extraordinary man" who confronted a "crowd of obstacles" to give "the finest spectacle that antiquity presents" (004-012).

Descending into Italy, Hannibal won a succession of victories down to the "appalling disaster" of [[battle-of-cannae|Cannae]], "at which time his ascendency attained its maximum" (004-019). His advantage lay in cavalry: "Hannibal owed his principal victories to his Numidian horsemen" (004-009).

Yet Montesquieu argues the conquests themselves undid him — "it was the very conquests which Hannibal made that commenced to change the fortunes of the war" — for, sent into Italy without the full backing of Carthage, "he received very little support" (004-015).

And "when it became necessary to garrison cities... his forces were found to be too small, and he lost portions of his army in detail" (004-015).

Against him stood the supreme Roman virtue: even after Cannae, when Rome "was abandoned by nearly all the peoples of Italy, she did not ask for peace" (004-012).

> Rome was a prodigy of constancy. (004-012)

### The Destruction of Carthage

After Hannibal was driven onto the defensive, Rome carried the war home. "This suggested to the Romans the idea of carrying the war into Africa; and [[scipio-africanus|Scipio]] descended upon the southern shore of the Mediterranean," compelling the Carthaginians "to recall Hannibal" (005-002).

Stripped of his Numidian cavalry by Scipio's alliance with Masinissa — "it was Numidian cavalry that gained the [[battle-of-zama|battle of Zama]] and ended the war" (004-009) — Hannibal lost the decisive field, and "Carthage received peace, not from an enemy, but from a master" (005-002).

Two generations later, at the close of the third war (146 BC), Rome returned to finish what victory had begun, exacting the [[sack-of-carthage|annihilation of the city]] itself under a quibble of language.

> They destroyed Carthage, saying they had promised to spare the city, and not the town. (006-010)

## Outcome

The terms imposed after Zama show Montesquieu's master at work.

Carthage "was required to give hostages, to deliver over her vessels and her elephants, to pay ten thousand talents in fifty years, to engage in no war whatever without the consent of the Roman people; and, in order to hold her in perpetual humiliation, the power of Masinissa, her implacable enemy, was strengthened" (005-002).

This was the Roman pattern of peace as continued domination: in their treaties they "required the fleets of Carthage and of the kings of Asia to be turned over to them" (006-008), reducing a defeated commercial power by burning its ships and forbidding its wars.

The strategic result was a transformed world. Before the fall of Carthage "there were, so to speak, two separate worlds" — Rome and Carthage contending for empire in the West, while the East "was agitated by the quarrels which continued from the time of the death of Alexander the Great" (005-003).

Once the rival was prostrate, the barrier between these worlds collapsed: "the Romans had scarcely subdued the Carthaginians, when they proceeded to assail new peoples, and to display their standard as invaders of the world" (005-004).

The destruction of Carthage, Montesquieu insists, "must be viewed as a much greater step in the career of Roman conquest than the subjugation of" [[ancient-greece|Greece]] (005-010).

## Impact on Rome

For Montesquieu the wars were the forge of Roman greatness, not merely its proof. The pressure of so dangerous an enemy concentrated the city: "the presence of Hannibal caused all divisions to cease among the Romans" (004-004), and after the disaster the senate sent the remains of the army to continue the war in Sicily (004-013).

Rome's institutions — citizen soldiers, annual discipline, colonies as ramparts, a senate that never asked for peace — were vindicated against Carthage's hired armies and divided councils.

The victory also marked a turning point in Rome's career. "After humbling the Carthaginians" Rome "seldom had other than small wars and great victories; whereas, before that task was accomplished, she had had small victories and great wars" (005-003).

Having mastered her one equal, she turned the same maxims outward — the policy of dividing allies, judging kings, and turning each treaty into an alliance that made the conquered "an instrument in the subjugation of others" (006-005).

Thus the struggle that hardened Roman virtue also opened the long road of conquest along which, in Montesquieu's argument, that virtue would eventually be corrupted by the very wealth and dominion it won.

## Key Figures

- **[[hannibal|Hannibal]]** — the Carthaginian general whose victories brought Rome to the edge of ruin before his conquests and want of support undid him (004-015).

- **[[scipio-africanus|Scipio]]** — carried the war into Africa and broke Carthaginian power (005-002).

- **Masinissa** — the Numidian king whose alliance deprived the Carthaginians of their cavalry advantage (004-009) and whose strengthened power was used "to hold her in perpetual humiliation" (005-002).

- **Regulus** — the Roman commander "beaten from the moment that the Carthaginians selected plains for battle" in the first war (004-009).

- **Philip of Macedon** — "made a treaty with Hannibal," but almost nothing came of it (005-003), leaving the East a spectator to the war in the West.

## The Three Wars

| War | Theatre / character | Outcome in Montesquieu |
|-----|---------------------|------------------------|
| First Punic War | Naval struggle; Romans copy a Carthaginian galley and beat the Carthaginian navy (004-011) | Regulus beaten on the plains, yet Rome wins mastery of the sea (004-009) |
| Second Punic War | Hannibal's invasion of Italy down to Cannae (004-019) | "Rome was a prodigy of constancy" (004-012); Zama ends the war (004-009) |
| Third Punic War | Rome returns to the prostrate city, 146 BC | Carthage destroyed "saying they had promised to spare the city, and not the town" (006-010) |
