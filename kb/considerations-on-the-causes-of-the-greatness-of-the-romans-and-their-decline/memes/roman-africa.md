# Roman Africa

Roman Africa — the former territory of [[carthage|Carthage]] — was the province Rome carved from its greatest enemy's homeland. Montesquieu's treatment of Africa centers on a single idea: Rome transformed Carthage's defeat into a permanent system of control, using the Numidian king Masinissa as an auxiliary and African resources as fuel for further conquest.

## Geography

Roman Africa occupied the northern margin of the continent, stretching "along the northern margin of Africa for a distance of about eighteen hundred miles" of coast. (PN=004-025) The province proper — Africa Proconsularis — covered modern Tunisia and western Libya. The fertile Medjerda valley produced grain; the interior yielded olives, marble, and wild beasts for the arena.

## History

### Before Rome

Before Roman conquest, North Africa was divided between the maritime empire of Carthage and the Numidian kingdoms of the interior. Carthage's African subjects bore a crushing tax burden: "the tribute imposed by the Carthaginians upon their African subjects was fifty per cent." — compared to Rome's later rate of about one-third. (PN=004-028)

Most African cities under Carthage, "being but little fortified," surrendered on the first approach of an invader. (PN=004-007) This fragility exposed the structural weakness of Carthaginian rule compared to Rome's network of loyal colonies in Italy.

### Under Rome

The decisive moment came at the end of the Second Punic War. After Hannibal's long campaign in Italy, "this suggested to the Romans the idea of carrying the war into Africa; and Scipio descended upon the southern shore of the Mediterranean." (PN=005-002)

Scipio's successes compelled Carthage to recall Hannibal from Italy. The battle of Zama (202 BC), fought in Numidia, broke Carthaginian power irrecoverably. (PN=005-002) Carthage received peace "not from an enemy, but from a master." (PN=005-002)

Rome's genius lay in how it secured the peace. "In order to hold her in perpetual humiliation, the power of Masinissa, her implacable enemy, was strengthened." (PN=005-002) Masinissa, Eumenes, and Attalus were among those "attached to them by benefits conferred — as Masinissa, Eumenes, and Attalus, who owed to them their kingdoms or their aggrandizement." (PN=006-005)

This client-king system was not incidental but systematic. The Romans used "Eumenes and Masinissa in order to subjugate Philip and Antiochus" just as they had used the Latins to subdue the Volscians. (PN=006-008)

In 146 BC, after the Third Punic War, Carthage was destroyed and its territory became the Roman province of Africa. Under Augustus, Africa Proconsularis was one of the empire's most important provinces, its grain feeding Rome. The province was governed by a proconsul, uniquely among imperial provinces retaining senatorial administration — a mark of its perceived security and importance.

### Later Period

Roman Africa flourished through the early empire, producing figures like Apuleius and Tertullian. In the 5th century AD, the Vandals crossed from Spain and seized the province, depriving the western empire of its grain supply. The Byzantine general Belisarius reconquered Africa in 533 AD, but Arab conquest in the 7th century ended Roman rule permanently.

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu draws several strategic lessons from Africa. First, Carthage's weakness in Africa itself — where most cities surrendered at the first approach of an invader — contrasted with Rome's solid Italian base. "The establishment of Carthage in her territory was less solid than that of Rome in hers." (PN=004-007)

Second, the client-king mechanism was a Roman innovation in imperial control. Rather than governing Numidia directly, Rome maintained Masinissa as a dependent ally who kept Carthage permanently weakened — and who owed his throne entirely to Roman favor. This was "the Roman method of conquering": slow, indirect, and cumulative.

Third, the destruction of Carthage and conversion of its territory into a province marked the moment when Rome transitioned from republican hegemon to imperial master.

Montesquieu traces the onset of Roman corruption to this period — the war against Antiochus of Syria "marks the true epoch of their corruption." (PN=005-012) The acquisition of Africa and Asia brought wealth that undermined Roman virtue.

Finally, the fate of Carthage's literature is emblematic. After 146 BC, Carthage's libraries were distributed to "the half savage princes of Africa." (PN=004-027)

"A few meagre fragments, in a version of Greek or Latin, is all that survives to represent the entire literature" of a once-great nation. (PN=004-027) For Montesquieu, this was the final measure of conquest — not just territory taken, but memory erased.
