# Carthage

Carthage, the Phoenician maritime power on the North African coast, was Rome's most formidable rival. Montesquieu devotes the bulk of Chapter 4 to comparing the two republics, showing why Carthage's commercial foundation doomed it against Rome's military [[civic-virtue|virtue]].

## Geography

Carthage commanded the western Mediterranean from the coast of modern Tunisia. In its prime the city was said to be twenty-three miles in circumference, housing seven hundred thousand inhabitants. (PN=004-025)

Its territorial base was fundamentally unlike Rome's: Roman power was massed in the continuous Italian peninsula. Carthage's holdings were "detached and straggling," stretching eighteen hundred miles of North African coast with outposts in Sicily, Sardinia, Corsica, Malta, the Balearic Islands, and Spain. (PN=004-025)

## History

### Before Rome

Founded as a Phoenician colony, Carthage grew into the dominant commercial empire of the western Mediterranean. Its speech belonged to the Semitic family, closely allied to Hebrew. "Carthage, become rich sooner than Rome, was also sooner corrupted" — its wealth outpaced its political development. (PN=004-002, 004-026)

### Under Rome

Rome fought three Punic Wars against Carthage. In the first (264–241 BC), Rome built a fleet from scratch and won at sea. In the second (218–201 BC), [[hannibal|Hannibal]] invaded Italy, winning the devastating victories of Ticinus, Trebia, Trasymenus, and Cannae. (PN=004-006, 004-005)

But Carthage's mercenary armies could not sustain a long war against Roman citizen-soldiers. Carthage "employed greater forces for attack, Rome for defense" — and Rome's defensive depth proved decisive. (PN=004-006)

> "The Carthaginians availed themselves of foreign troops; the Romans employed their own. As the latter always regarded the conquered as instruments of future triumphs, they made soldiers of all the peoples whom they subdued." (PN=004-005)

After Hannibal's defeat at Zama (202 BC), Carthage received peace not from an enemy but from "a master." It surrendered its fleet and elephants, paid ten thousand talents over fifty years, and agreed to wage no war without Roman consent. (PN=005-002)

The Romans also strengthened [[masinissa|Masinissa]], Carthage's implacable Numidian enemy, to hold the defeated city in perpetual humiliation. (PN=005-002)

In the third Punic War, Rome destroyed Carthage utterly in 146 BC. The city was burned; its libraries were distributed to African princes rather than preserved, so that "a few meagre fragments, in a version of Greek or Latin, is all that survives to represent the entire literature" of this once-great nation. (PN=004-027)

### Later Period

A Roman colony was refounded on the site by Julius Caesar and Augustus, and Carthage later became a major city of Roman Africa. But as an independent power, it was never revived.

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu's Chapter 4 comparison identifies the structural causes of Carthage's defeat. First, Carthage was a commercial republic where public service was commodified. "All that society could bestow upon the citizen was sold at Carthage, and every service performed for the public was paid for by the state." (PN=004-003)

At Rome, public office yielded only honor and a preference of toil. Carthaginian individuals "possessed the wealth of kings," while Roman manners and a habit of poverty kept fortunes nearly equal. (PN=004-003)

Second, factionalism paralyzed Carthage. Two factions reigned — one for peace, one for war — so "it was impossible to enjoy the one or to prosecute the other with effect." (PN=004-004)

At Rome, war reunited interests; at Carthage, it deepened divisions. The people, "governed by abuses," insisted on managing everything themselves. (PN=004-004)

> "Rome, governed by laws, the people suffered the senate to take the direction of affairs; at Carthage, governed by abuses, the people desired to do everything themselves. ... Rome was ambitious from pride, the Carthaginians from avarice; those would command, these would acquire." (PN=004-004)

Third, Carthage's territorial foundation was weak. "The establishment of Carthage in her territory was less solid than that of Rome in hers." Rome was encircled by thirty loyal colonies; most African cities under Carthage surrendered on the first approach of an invader. (PN=004-007)

Fourth, Carthage relied on commerce rather than arms. Montesquieu observes: "Nations established upon commerce may exist for a long time in a state of mediocrity; but their grandeur is of short duration." Carthage's commercial position had already eroded with the founding of Alexandria. (PN=004-008)

Finally, Carthage could not reform itself. In Chapter 8, Montesquieu concludes: "Carthage perished, for the reason that, when it became necessary to retrench abuses, she could not even tolerate the hand of her own Hannibal." (PN=008-010)

Montesquieu also notes Carthage's religious cruelty. The worship of Baal (Moloch), involving child sacrifice in a brazen image, was "the most revolting of which all antiquity contains any account." Carthage's government was severe and its taxation oppressive — a fifty percent land tax on African subjects, compared to Rome's later rate of one-third. (PN=004-028, 004-008)
