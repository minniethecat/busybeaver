# Cicero

Marcus Tullius Cicero (106 BC -- 43 BC) was the greatest orator and intellectual figure of the late [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]], whose voice echoes throughout Montesquieu's *Considerations* as both primary source and object of moral judgment.

In Montesquieu's narrative, Cicero is uniquely positioned: his letters and philosophical works constitute the richest contemporary record of the republic's final generation, yet his character is subjected to a searching comparison with [[cato-the-elder|Cato]] that defines two competing models of republican virtue.

For Montesquieu, Cicero was a man "endowed with admirable parts for a secondary role" but "incapable of the first" (012-005).

## Life

### Early Life

Cicero was a native of Arpinum, a town in Latium, not of the city of Rome itself (006-015).

This provincial origin is significant: the translator Jehu Baker notes, in a broad survey of Latin literary genius, that "the great body of the Latin literature of the classical period was produced by men who were born in the neighboring states of Italy or in the provinces," and lists Cicero among those prose-writers who came from outside the capital (006-015).

He emerged into public life during the convulsions that followed Sulla's revolution. The Rome of his youth was a republic in which "those who were neutral, and who adhered to the party of liberty, were sure to be proscribed" (011-003), a condition that shaped the political choices of his entire generation.

### Career

Cicero's public career reached its zenith with the suppression of the Catilinarian conspiracy (63 BC) and his consulship, but Montesquieu's interest lies elsewhere: in Cicero's role as reluctant witness to the republic's agony under [[julius-caesar|Caesar]].

During the civil war, Cicero sided with [[pompey|Pompey]], and after Pharsalia he "justly accuses [Caesar] of having been slow" (011-013).

His letters to Cassius record that had they foreseen Caesar would "amuse himself as he did in his war at Alexandria," they "would not have made their peace with him" (011-013).

These letters, together with those of Servius Sulpicius, form what Montesquieu calls "a masterpiece of the naivete of men united by a common calamity" (011-015).

Under Caesar's dictatorship, Cicero experienced firsthand the humiliation of a senate reduced to form without substance. He wrote to Atticus:

> "I learn sometimes that a senatus-consultum, passed upon my recommendation, has already been carried into Syria and Armenia before I have become aware of its existence; and many princes have written me letters of thanks for having advised that the title of king should be given them, whom not only I did not know to be kings, but I did not so much as know that they were in the world." (011-015)

His letters to Atticus also furnish Montesquieu's evidence for the deepening [[political-corruption|corruption]] of Roman public life -- the story of Memmius informing the senate of a bargain struck with the consuls involving fabricated laws and forged senatorial decrees (010-001).

After Caesar's assassination, Cicero "ruled the senate throughout" the affair of Caesar's funeral, and held that "it would have been better to have acted with rigor, and to have exposed themselves to destruction" (012-002).

### Later Years & Death

Cicero's final political act sealed his fate.

As a means of ruining Mark Antony, "who was his particular enemy, Cicero resorted to the bad policy of elevating Octavius; and thus, instead of seeking to induce the people to forget Caesar, he placed him before their eyes" (012-005).

The young [[augustus|Octavius]] "flattered him, he praised him, he consulted him, and employed all those artifices which vanity never distrusts" (012-005).

The alliance shifted; Octavius joined with Antony and Lepidus in the Second Triumvirate, and Cicero was proscribed and executed in 43 BC. Montesquieu's verdict is unsparing: "Cicero, who boasted that his robe had destroyed the armies of Anthony, gave to the republic an enemy yet more dangerous" (012-005).

## Significance

For Montesquieu, Cicero embodies the imperfect virtue of a man whose talents exceeded his character.

The comparison with Cato is the lens through which the whole is judged: "With him, virtue was the accessory; with Cato, glory.

Cicero always saw himself first;

Cato as constantly forgot himself.

The latter would have saved the republic for its own sake; the former in order to boast of his services" (012-005).

This does not diminish Cicero's intellectual authority.

Montesquieu draws repeatedly on Cicero as a source: his *Offices* (De Officiis) provides evidence on agrarian law (003-012) and on the Roman contempt for commerce (010-004); his *Tusculan Disputations* attest to the soldier's burden (002-001); and his letters to Atticus serve as the intimate record of a dying republic (010-001, 011-015, 012-002).

Cicero's judgment of [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] carries particular weight. His statement on Caesar's post-victory confiscations -- "in an impious cause, and in a victory yet more abominable, did not confiscate the property of individual citizens, but involved whole provinces and regions in one common calamity" (011-003) -- is a verdict Montesquieu adopts without reservation.

The translator's note adds a final dimension: Cicero, "who is commonly so just in his ethical principles, and who, in point of intellectual force and accomplishments, was probably the greatest and most luminous genius that adorned republican Rome," nevertheless "gives his full sanction to the doctrine of assassination as applied to tyrants" (011-034).

The passage from *De Officiis* is quoted directly:

> "What can be more deeply criminal than to slay, not only a man, but a man who is furthermore an intimate friend? Does he, therefore, involve himself in crime who slays a tyrant -- however intimate a friend he may be? Certainly the Roman people -- who regard this as the most beautiful of all illustrious acts -- do not so view it." (011-034, 011-035)

## Contemporary Views

Cicero's own contemporaries furnish an important assessment. Montesquieu notes that Cicero "reckons [Mithridates] to have been, after Alexander, the greatest of the kings," and that he "attributes to him the distinction of having been indisputably the foremost of all the kings with whom the Roman people had waged war" (007-006).

The translator Baker ranks Cicero among the nobility that Caesar overthrew: "Caesar mastered a nobility of which Lucullus and Hortensius, Sulpicius and Catulus, Pompey and Cicero, Brutus and Cato were members" (011-029).

Middleton's *Life of Cicero* (London, 1839) is cited twice in the translator's notes -- once for the account of Pompey's camp at Pharsalia (011-025) and once for the observation that had Pompey succeeded he "would all but certainly have imitated Sylla in drenching his country in the blood of his defeated fellow-citizens" (011-032).

Both references testify to Cicero's intimate connection with the events that determined the republic's fate.

The chapter on the corruption of the Romans (Chapter 10) opens with Cicero's letters to Atticus as the primary evidence of moral decay: the story of Memmius and the fabricated laws demonstrates how far Roman public life had fallen since Polybius's time, when a Roman was "enthralled by the oath which he had taken" (010-001).
