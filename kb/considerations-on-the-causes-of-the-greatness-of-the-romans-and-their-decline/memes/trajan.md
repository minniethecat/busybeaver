# Trajan

Trajan (Marcus Ulpius Traianus, 53–117 AD) was Roman emperor from 98 to 117 AD, the second of the so-called "Five Good Emperors." In Montesquieu's narrative, he stands as the single most admired prince in Roman history — the adopted successor of Nerva whose reign combined martial conquest, legal development, and public beneficence in a measure that no other emperor matched.

Under Trajan, the [[roman-law|Roman law]] "did not reach its maximum until the time of Trajan" (009-014), and the empire attained its greatest geographical extent — "considerably more than three thousand miles east and west" (015-016).

## Life

### Early Life

Trajan was born in Italica, a Roman settlement in the province of Hispania Baetica (modern Spain), in 53 AD.

His father, Marcus Ulpius Traianus, had served as a senator and governor under the Flavians, giving the younger Trajan entry into the highest circles of Roman public life.

He followed the traditional [[cursus-honorum|cursus honorum]], serving as military tribune, quaestor, praetor, and legionary commander on the Rhine frontier, where his competence and rapport with the soldiery earned him the respect that would later secure his elevation.

Montesquieu records little of Trajan's origins, treating him as a product of the mature imperial system rather than of the old republican aristocracy. His path to power was made possible by the precedent of adoptive succession — a mechanism that, in Montesquieu's analysis, temporarily arrested the degenerative cycle of hereditary tyranny that had plagued the Julio-Claudian and Flavian dynasties.

### Career

Trajan's accession came through the adoption arranged by the elderly emperor Nerva in 97 AD. Montesquieu declares this act one of the signal events of imperial history:

> Nerva adopted Trajan, the most accomplished prince of whom history had ever spoken. It was a blessing to be born in his reign, which was the happiest and most glorious that the Roman people had experienced. He was a great statesman and a great soldier. He possessed a heart which determined him to good ends, and an intellect which disclosed to him the best means for their attainment. He was endowed with a noble, grand, and beautiful soul. He united all the virtues without being extreme in any. He was, in short, a man most admirably adapted to reflect honor upon human nature, and to represent the divine. (015-011)

As emperor, Trajan prosecuted two major wars of conquest.

The Dacian Wars (101–102 and 105–106 AD) brought the kingdom of Dacia, north of the Danube, under Roman control — a victory commemorated by [[roman-triumph|triumphal]] spectacles in which, the translator notes, "no less than ten thousand fought in the spectacles which were given by Trajan after the conquest of Dacia" (015-020).

The annexed province yielded rich gold mines that funded an ambitious program of public works.

Trajan's great public infrastructure included the bridge over the Danube — one of the engineering marvels of antiquity — and aqueducts that Montesquieu ranks among Rome's greatest monuments, alongside "those of Trajan over the Tagus and the Danube" (001-019). These works exemplified the imperial capacity to project Roman civilization across previously impassable frontiers.

His second great campaign turned eastward against the Parthian Empire.

"Trajan executed the project of Caesar: he conducted a successful war against the Parthians" (015-012).

The Parthian war, however, exposed the structural limits of Roman expansion.

Montesquieu explains that the difficulty "consisted in the situations of the two empires, and in the different methods of warfare which were practiced by the two peoples" (015-012).

The Parthians fought with cavalry and archery at a distance, in a terrain of mountains, deserts, and marshes that consumed Roman infantry and supply lines.

Though Trajan advanced as far as Mesopotamia and captured the Parthian capital at Ctesiphon, his conquests proved unsustainable.

Under Trajan, the Roman legal system attained what Montesquieu regards as its apogee: [[roman-law|Roman law]] "did not reach its maximum until the time of Trajan" (009-014). This judgment reflects the broader view that the emperor's reign represented the equilibrium point of Roman civilization — after which decline became, if not yet visible, at least structurally inevitable.

### Later Years & Death

Trajan died in 117 AD at Selinus in Cilicia while returning from the Parthian campaign, having fallen ill amid the strains of eastern warfare.

His death exposed the fragility of his conquests: his successor Hadrian "abandoned the conquests of Trajan, and limited the empire to the Euphrates" (015-014).

This retreat occasioned "much murmuring" (015-014), for it contradicted the Roman religious conviction — embodied in the god Terminus — that Roman boundaries would never retrace their steps (015-014).

Yet Montesquieu treats the abandonment with philosophical equanimity, observing that "the Romans, after so many wars, did not lose that which they voluntarily abandoned, but that their empire was like that of the sea, which is none the less extended when it retires of itself" (015-014).

Trajan's place in the imperial succession is firmly fixed in Montesquieu's summary of the early emperors: "Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, and Antoninus Marcus" (016-031). His reign, together with those of his adoptive successors, marked an interlude of competence between the monsters of the first century and the crises of the third.

## Significance

For Montesquieu, Trajan represents the zenith of the Roman Empire — the point at which conquest, law, and administration achieved their maximum extension and refinement before the long contraction began. His reign is the empirical benchmark against which imperial decline is measured.

Montesquieu's assessment is unreservedly positive yet historically bounded.

Trajan is "the glory of Trajan" (016-002) — a phrase placed in sequence with "the wisdom of Nerva" and "the courage of Hadrian," suggesting that each adoptive emperor contributed a distinct virtue to the imperial office.

But the very completeness of Trajan's achievement contains an implicit warning: the empire could expand no further, and the qualities that made Trajan exceptional — his combination of military genius, administrative capacity, and moral character — could not be reliably transmitted through the imperial succession.

The key dimensions of Trajan's significance in Montesquieu's analysis are:

- **Territorial Maximum**: Under Trajan the empire "extended considerably more than three thousand miles east and west, and, in its greatest breadth, something more than two thousand north and south. Its total area was about six million square miles" (015-016). This was the geographic high-water mark of Roman power — a benchmark never again equaled.

- **Legal Apogee**: Roman law reached its fullest development "until the time of Trajan" (009-014), making his reign the reference point for the entire subsequent history of Roman jurisprudence.

- **The Adoptive Principle**: Trajan's elevation by Nerva demonstrated that merit, not blood, could select an emperor — a principle that produced the finest sequence of rulers Rome ever knew and that, when abandoned by Marcus Aurelius in favor of his son Commodus, precipitated the return of tyranny.

- **The Parthian Limit**: Trajan's eastern campaigns revealed that Rome had reached the geographical limits of its power. The conquests could not be held, and Hadrian's withdrawal (015-014) was a strategic necessity that nonetheless registered as a symbolic defeat.

- **Public Works as Civilization**: Trajan's aqueducts, bridges, and roads represented the constructive face of empire — the transformation of conquered territory into civilized space (001-019).

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's portrait of Trajan is the most laudatory assessment of any emperor in the *Considerations*. He calls him "the most accomplished prince of whom history had ever spoken" (015-011) — a judgment that places Trajan above even the admired [[augustus|Augustus]] and the philosopher-emperor Marcus Aurelius.

The translator Jehu Baker, however, tempers this enthusiasm in a revealing footnote.

Quoting Gibbon, he observes that "Trajan was ambitious of fame; and, as long as mankind shall continue to bestow more liberal applause on their destroyers than on their benefactors, the thirst of military glory will ever be the vice of the most exalted characters" (015-011, translator's note).

Baker adds his own judgment: "If the higher sort of greatness is characterized by being superior to the ignorant opinion of the multitude, and for rising above the common objects of a vulgar ambition, then the fame of the two Antonines rests upon a better foundation, and is more worthy of enlightened approval, than that of Caesar and Trajan" (015-011, translator's note).

Pliny the Younger, a contemporary of Trajan, supplies the most intimate surviving testimony. Montesquieu cites Pliny's *Panegyric on Trajan*, section 42, which details how the emperor enriched the public treasury not through proscriptions and confiscations — the method of earlier tyrants — but through lawful administration (014-007).

The antiquarian dimension of Montesquieu's judgment is captured in the analytical index: "his idea of the character of Trajan" (023-034) is singled out for reference, suggesting that the author's evaluation of Trajan was considered a distinctive and noteworthy element of the *Considerations*.

> He united all the virtues without being extreme in any. He was, in short, a man most admirably adapted to reflect honor upon human nature, and to represent the divine. (015-011)
