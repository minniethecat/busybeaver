# Sulla's Dictatorship

The dictatorship of Lucius Cornelius Sulla (82–79 BC) marked a decisive turning point in the Roman Republic. After victory in Rome's first full-scale civil war, Sulla assumed the dictatorship without the traditional six-month limit [010-007].

He introduced systematic proscription as an instrument of political terror, and enacted constitutional reforms that attempted to restore senatorial supremacy — yet his methods of attaining power proved a template for the very monarchy they were designed to prevent [011-019].

## Background

The dictatorship of Sulla emerged from decades of mounting tension between the aristocratic and popular factions in Roman politics. The Gracchan reforms had already demonstrated that political violence could override constitutional process [011-004].

The Social War (91–88 BC) further destabilized the Republic. The rivalry between Gaius Marius — leader of the popular faction — and Sulla — leader of the aristocratic faction — escalated into armed confrontation when Sulla, in an unprecedented act, marched his legions on Rome itself in 88 BC [011-020].

> Sylla was the leader of the former, Marius of the latter; and, whilst Marius was in the ascendant — however extensive may have been his massacres and assassinations — he organized no machinery for systematic destruction, he resorted to no such cold-blooded and awful instrument of tyranny as the proscription. [011-020]

## Course

### Phase 1: Civil War and the March on Rome (88–82 BC)

Sulla's first march on Rome in 88 BC set a fatal precedent: a Roman general had turned his legions against the capital itself. While Sulla campaigned in the East against Mithridates [007-001], his Marian enemies regained control of Rome.

Upon his return in 83 BC, Sulla fought a second civil war against the Marian faction, culminating in the Battle of the Colline Gate (82 BC). Sulla's victory was total, and he was appointed dictator without the customary six-month limitation [010-007].

### Phase 2: The Proscriptions and Constitutional Reforms (82–79 BC)

Sulla's most notorious innovation was the systematic proscription: the posting of lists of political enemies who could be killed with impunity and whose property was confiscated [011-019].

The proscriptions claimed thousands of lives and concentrated enormous wealth and land in the hands of Sulla's supporters. Simultaneously, Sulla enacted constitutional reforms to weaken the tribunate and restore senatorial authority [011-004].

> Sylla first introduced the method of proscription. Upon his return into the city, after having conquered the party of Marius, he wrote down the names of those whom he wished to have put to death, and promised a reward to any person who should kill them. [011-019]

## Outcome

Sulla's constitutional settlement did not long survive his voluntary abdication in 79 BC and his death the following year. The land distributions to his veterans — Sulla gave the lands of citizens to his soldiers, rather than public land as had been the custom [011-002] — created a class of armed supporters dependent on their general rather than the state.

The abrogation of Sulla's laws became a rallying cry for popular politicians [011-004]. Within a generation, Pompey and Caesar would dismantle what remained of his constitutional architecture.

## Impact on Rome

Montesquieu identifies Sulla's dictatorship as a critical moment in the corruption of the Roman Republic. Chapter XI of the *Considerations* treats Sulla as the first of three figures who successively concentrated power until the Republic collapsed.

> Sylla was an intensely passionate man; and he carried the Romans to liberty with violence. Augustus was a cunning tyrant; and he conducted them gently to servitude. [013-008]

This contrast captures the paradox of Sulla's dictatorship: he claimed to restore the republic, but his methods irreparably damaged republican norms.

The practice of proscription that he introduced was continued under the emperors [016-004], becoming a standard instrument of imperial terror. Sulla's precedent — that a general could seize power by force and remake the constitution — proved more enduring than any of his specific reforms.
