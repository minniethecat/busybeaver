# Edict of Milan

The Edict of Milan, proclaimed in 313 AD by the co-emperors Constantine and Licinius, granted religious toleration throughout the Roman Empire and ended the persecution of Christians. It marked the beginning of Christianity's transformation from a persecuted minority faith to the established religion of the Roman state.

Montesquieu treats the Christianization of the Empire as one of the great transformations in Roman history. "In another direction, the Christian church contributed its powerful influence" [010-008] to the reshaping of Roman society. The Edict of Milan was the political pivot on which this transformation turned.

## Background

Christianity had spread through the Empire for nearly three centuries before Constantine, despite periodic persecutions under emperors such as Nero, Decius, and Diocletian. By the early fourth century, Christians constituted a significant minority — concentrated in the eastern provinces and the cities — that could no longer be suppressed.

Constantine's own path to power was shaped by civil war. "After Alexander, Maximin was elected. He was the first emperor of barbarian origin" [016-012] — and the succeeding decades saw the army elevate and murder emperors at will. The Gordians "perished in Africa," and "the praetorian prefect of the third Gordian" found himself unable to control the forces that had made him [016-013].

Constantine emerged from this chaos as one of several rival claimants. His decision to embrace Christianity — sealed by his victory at the Milvian Bridge in 312 — was at once a personal conversion and a political calculation.

## Course

The Edict of Milan was agreed between Constantine (who controlled the West) and Licinius (who controlled the East) at their meeting in Milan in February 313. Its key provisions were simple: all religions were to be tolerated, confiscated Christian property was to be returned, and Christians were granted full legal equality.

The edict was not, as is sometimes supposed, the establishment of Christianity as the state religion. That would come later, under Theodosius in 380. Rather, it was a declaration of religious neutrality — the first time in Roman history that the state formally recognized the legitimacy of a faith it had previously persecuted.

Constantine himself, Montesquieu notes, was less a saint than a shrewd politician. He "desired that grain should be sent" to Constantinople just as it had been to Rome [017-005], creating a new capital that would be Christian from its founding.

## Outcome

The immediate effect was the end of persecution. Christians could worship openly, build churches, and participate in public life without fear. Confiscated property was restored, and the Church began to accumulate the wealth that would make it a major political force.

But the longer-term consequences were more profound. "Probity and virtue," Montesquieu observes, "gradually succumbed to the spirit of Christianity" [015-021] — not because Christianity was immoral, but because it redirected Roman attention from civic virtue to personal salvation.

Montesquieu also notes the introduction of "the practice of celibacy" along with Christianity [015-023], and the related decline in marriage and population growth that weakened the Empire's demographic base. The old Roman religion had been tied to the state; the new Christian religion was tied to the individual conscience.

## Impact on Rome

For Montesquieu, the Christianization of the Empire represents an ambiguous legacy. On one hand, Christianity brought moral seriousness and a concern for the poor that pagan Rome had lacked. On the other, it contributed to the decline of the martial and civic virtues that had made Rome great.

"Christianity introduced into the empire" new concerns that were fundamentally apolitical [015-023]. The Roman who once lived for the glory of the Republic now lived for the salvation of his soul. This reorientation, Montesquieu suggests, was one factor — among many — in the slow transformation of Roman character.

Constantine's own successors illustrate the instability that Christianity did not cure. "Licinius, Maximin, Constantine, Constantine II, Constantius II, Constans" [016-034] — the list of emperors who followed the Edict is a litany of civil wars, assassinations, and dynastic murders. The institutional problems of the Empire survived the religious transformation intact.
