# Tiberius Gracchus

Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus (163–133 BC) was a Roman tribune whose agrarian reform campaign and subsequent murder represent, in Montesquieu's analysis of Roman history, a decisive turning point.

His attempt to revive the [[roman-law|Licinian Law]] and redistribute public lands exposed the fatal rift between the wealthy senatorial class and the Roman people — a rift that, once settled by violence rather than constitutional process, inaugurated the long unraveling of the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]].

## Life

### Early Life

Tiberius Gracchus was born into a distinguished plebeian family of the gens Sempronia. His father, also named Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus, had served as consul and censor.

His mother Cornelia was the daughter of Scipio Africanus, the conqueror of [[hannibal|Hannibal]]. This dual heritage — plebeian political tradition and patrician military glory — placed the younger Gracchus at the intersection of Rome's competing social orders.

He received an excellent education in Greek and Latin letters and served with distinction in the Third Punic War and the Numantine War in Spain, where his courage and diplomatic skill earned him the respect of soldiers and foreign peoples alike.

### Career

Gracchus was elected tribune of the plebs in 133 BC and embarked upon a program of agrarian reform. The social problem he confronted was profound: in the early republic, Rome's strength had rested upon an equal division of lands among its citizens.

This system ensured a broad class of independent smallholders capable of bearing arms. As Jehu Baker explains, the founders of the ancient republics "made an equal division of the lands. That alone constituted a strong people — that is to say, a well regulated society" [003-002].

The [[roman-law|Licinian Law]] of 369 BC had limited individual occupation of public land to 500 jugera (about 311 English acres). But over generations this restriction had been systematically ignored.

Wealthy citizens occupied vast tracts of the public domain while the smallholder class dwindled, diminishing both the citizen body and the military recruitment base.

Gracchus's solution was to revive the Licinian Law in a modified form. Baker's note summarizes the reform precisely:

> "In this state of things, and to remedy this enormous abuse, Tiberius Gracchus — b. c. 132 — caused the Licinian law to be revived in a modified form. In its new form, the law, while retaining the old limitation of 500 jugera of public land to each occupant, allowed 250 jugera in addition to be occupied for each son of the family; and, whilst providing that in all cases any excess should be resumed by the state for allotment among the poorer class of citizens, it also provided that payment should be made for the improvements which had been put upon the lands thus resumed." [003-015]

The reform was moderate in design — it compensated landowners for improvements and allowed generous family allowances. But it threatened the material interests of the senatorial order, who had long treated public land as private property.

The political significance of this reform extended beyond economics. By restoring the citizen-farmer class, Gracchus aimed to restore the social basis of Roman military power itself.

Montesquieu quotes Gracchus addressing the nobles directly on this point:

> "Tell me, which is the more worth, a citizen or a perpetual slave? a soldier or a man who is useless in war? In order to hold some acres of land more than the other citizens, will you renounce the hope of the conquest of the rest of the world? or place yourselves in danger of seeing those very lands which you refuse to us, taken from you by the enemies of your country?" [003-005]

This speech distills the essence of Gracchus's argument. The senatorial class, by hoarding public lands, was undermining the very military engine that had built Rome's empire and that alone could defend it. The choice he posed was between the narrow self-interest of a few and the survival of the Roman state.

### Later Years and Death

The senatorial opposition to Gracchus's reform was ferocious. When he sought reelection as tribune — deeply contrary to precedent — a group of senators led by Scipio Nasica stormed the assembly on the Capitoline Hill.

In the ensuing violence, Tiberius Gracchus and some three hundred of his supporters were clubbed to death. His body was denied honorable burial; an aedile named Lucretius, later called Vespillo, threw his corpse into the Tiber [012-001].

This act of posthumous desecration, unprecedented in Roman political life, signaled that the conflict between rich and poor had passed beyond the bounds of constitutional deliberation into a new and darker phase.

## Significance

Montesquieu identifies the tribunate of Tiberius Gracchus as a pivotal moment in Roman history. Throughout the early and middle republic, the conflict between patricians and plebeians had been the engine of Roman liberty, producing laws and reforms that strengthened the state.

But by the time of the Gracchi, the underlying conditions had changed fundamentally:

> "In the course of time, when the plebeians had so far humbled the patricians that the distinction between the two had become a vain thing, and when patricians and plebs were indifferently elevated to the honors of office, new disputes arose among the common people... But, inasmuch as the ancient manners had disappeared, as private individuals possessed immense fortunes, and as it is impossible that wealth should not give power, the nobles resisted with more force than the patricians had done; and this was the cause of the death of the Gracchi, and of many others who acted upon their principles." [008-006]

In Montesquieu's account, the earlier class struggles had been productive because they occurred within a framework of shared civic virtue — what he calls "the ancient manners." The Gracchan crisis was different: wealth had replaced birth as the dominant social fact.

The rich would not yield to constitutional pressure as the old patricians had sometimes done. When Gracchus used the tribunate — an office created to protect the plebeians [008-004] — to challenge the economic power of the senatorial class, the response was not legal contestation but murder.

This introduced political assassination into the Roman political process. The precedent would be followed catastrophically: Gaius Gracchus in 121 BC, the proscriptions of Sulla, and the assassination of Julius Caesar.

The Agrarian Law of Tiberius Gracchus also illuminates a broader theme in Montesquieu's analysis: the relationship between land distribution, military power, and political liberty. In the early republic, equal landholding produced a citizen-soldier class with both the means and the motive to defend the state.

As Baker explains in his notes to Chapter 3, the concentration of public land "caused the lands to accumulate in the hands of a few" and "there were but few citizens or soldiers any longer; for the lands, formerly set apart for the support of the latter, were employed now for the maintenance of slaves and artisans" [003-002].

Gracchus understood that this economic transformation threatened Rome's essential nature as a republic of armed citizens, and he attempted to reverse it through law. His failure and murder demonstrated that the Roman ruling class would sooner kill reformers than surrender their material privileges.
