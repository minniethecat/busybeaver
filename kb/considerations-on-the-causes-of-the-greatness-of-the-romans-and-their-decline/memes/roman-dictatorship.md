# Roman Dictatorship

The Roman dictatorship was the Republic's constitutional mechanism for emergency — a temporary suspension of collegial government in which one man wielded supreme authority for a fixed term and a defined purpose. Montesquieu carefully distinguishes this republican institution from the corrupted dictatorship that later destroyed the Republic.

## Definition

The dictator was appointed by the consuls on the Senate's recommendation, held office for a maximum of six months, and was expected to resign once the emergency had passed. "The dictator is represented as saying" that he acted for the state, not for himself (001-015). The dictatorship was an office of duty, not ambition — in its original form.

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

The early dictatorship functioned as intended. The dictator "Fabius, the army being divided" with his master of horse, demonstrated the institution's military effectiveness (001-021). The "creation of a dictator" was one of the means by which the patricians "sought to strengthen" their position against plebeian demands, though "the consulate could also be chosen as dictators" — limiting its use as a purely aristocratic weapon (008-005, 008-006).

### Imperial Period / Late Republic

The institution was transformed — and corrupted — by Sulla and Caesar. Sulla's dictatorship "appeared to be only a momentary calamity" because he "abdicated the dictatorship" (011-002, 011-003). But the precedent had been set: Caesar "aspired to the dictatorship" not as an emergency measure but as a permanent concentration of power (011-006). The republican dictatorship, designed to save the Republic, became the instrument of its destruction.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu presents the dictatorship as a test case of institutional corruption. The same form — one man with supreme authority — could be either the Republic's salvation (Cincinnatus, Fabius) or its ruin (Sulla, Caesar). The difference lay not in the legal powers but in the virtue of the person wielding them and the political culture that constrained them.

## Examples

- **Cincinnatus (458 BC)**: Appointed dictator, defeated the Aequi in sixteen days, and immediately returned to his farm
- **Fabius Maximus (217 BC)**: Appointed dictator after the disaster at Lake Trasimene, conducted the war of attrition against Hannibal (001-021)
- **Sulla (82–79 BC)**: First dictator without a time limit, whose proscriptions and constitutional reforms demonstrated the office's corruptibility (011-002, 011-003)
