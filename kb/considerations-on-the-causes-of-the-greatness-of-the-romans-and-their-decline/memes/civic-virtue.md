# Civic Virtue

The principle of civic virtue (*vertu* in Montesquieu's terminology) designates the moral disposition by which citizens subordinate their personal interests to the public good — the animating spirit that Montesquieu identifies as the foundational cause of Rome's rise to greatness.

## Definition

In Montesquieu's framework, civic virtue is not a private moral quality but a political principle: the "feeling of one's own strength" that enables collective self-governance (002-007). It is the love of country and its laws — a sentiment that, in a republic, replaces the fear that drives despotism and the honor that animates monarchy. In Rome, this virtue was inseparable from military courage: "the warlike virtues were adjudged to be the highest" (002-010).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

During the early Republic, poverty itself was "public virtue" (008-003). Public offices and honors were "only obtained by virtue, and yielded no profit except honor and preference in war" (004-003). This created a self-reinforcing cycle: the state rewarded virtue, which in turn strengthened the state. Even the faults of the Romans, Montesquieu notes, were bound up with "their virtues" (001-008).

### Imperial Period

As Rome expanded, it "lost the Roman virtue — from causes having little or no connection with the greatness of the republic" (009-011). The very success of the Republic eroded its moral foundation. Wealth replaced poverty, private luxury replaced public simplicity, and the civic virtue that had sustained the Republic dissolved — a process Montesquieu traces as the true mechanism of decline.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …and may be fitly called civic virtue. This, therefore, appears to be the true criterion which marks the decadence of the Roman state (009-012)

Montesquieu adopts "the decay of civic virtue as the true criterion" for measuring Rome's decline (009-012). His central thesis is paradoxical: Rome's greatness was self-undermining. The conquests that proved its virtue also introduced the wealth that corrupted it. For "more than four centuries," the decay of civic virtue proceeded invisibly beneath the surface of apparent strength (009-013).

He contrasts virtue with material measures of power: "gold and silver may be consumed; but virtue, constancy, force, and poverty are never exhausted" (004-004). This is the key to his argument that Rome's decline was not primarily military or economic but moral and political.

## Examples

- **Early Roman poverty as virtue**: The equation of poverty with public virtue in the early Republic — citizens who owned little yet defended the state with their lives (008-003)
- **The corruption of wealth**: As conquest brought riches to Rome, the link between public service and personal sacrifice was severed — "wealth — that source of all corruption" (008-003)
- **The endurance of virtue**: Even in decline, the "virtues of great personages" in "the principal families" preserved fragments of the old republican spirit (008-005)
