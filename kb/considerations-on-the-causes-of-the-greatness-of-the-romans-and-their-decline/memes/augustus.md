# Augustus

Augustus (63 BC - 14 AD), born Gaius Octavius and posthumously adopted by [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]], was the founder of the Roman Empire and its first emperor.

For Montesquieu, he is the figure who perfected what Caesar began: the replacement of republican liberty with monarchical power, accomplished not through open violence but through the patient construction of a system that made servitude appear indistinguishable from order (013-004).

"Augustus -- for this is the name which flattery gave to Octavius -- established order; that is to say, lasting servitude" (013-004). His reign marks the pivotal moment when the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]] ceased to function as a free state and became, in substance, a monarchy clothed in republican forms.

## Life

### Early Life

Augustus was born Gaius Octavius in 63 BC, the year of Cicero's consulship and the Catilinarian conspiracy.

He was the grand-nephew of Julius Caesar, who adopted him posthumously in his will.

At the age of nineteen, upon learning of Caesar's assassination, Octavian returned to Italy and claimed his inheritance -- launching himself into the vortex of the [[roman-civil-war|civil wars]] that consumed the last generation of the Republic (011-011).

Montesquieu does not dwell on Augustus's youth, treating him instead as a public actor whose entire career must be read through the lens of his great-uncle's fate.

This is the interpretive key: Augustus "feared revolts among the soldiers, and not conspiracies among the citizens" while contending for power, and once master, "policy induced him to labor for the re-establishment of order, to the end that the conveniences of a despotic government might be felt" (013-006).

### Career

**The Triumviral Period.** Augustus rose through the chaos of the post-Caesarian civil wars.

Montesquieu groups him with the sequence of ambitious commanders -- "under Marius, Sulla, Pompey, Caesar, Anthony, and Augustus, Rome continually grew more terrible, and completed the overthrow of all the kings who yet remained" (011-011).

The proscriptions of the Second Triumvirate, in which Octavian joined with [[marc-antony|Mark Antony]] and Lepidus to purge their enemies, eliminated the last generation of republican leadership and cleared the path toward autocracy.

**The Founding of the Principate.** After defeating Antony at Actium (31 BC), Octavian faced the same problem that had destroyed Caesar: how to exercise supreme power without provoking the fate of a tyrant. His solution defined the Principate.

> This is the key to the whole life of Augustus. He attended the sittings of the senate with a cuirass under his robe; he refused the name of dictator; and, whereas Caesar had insolently said that the republic was nothing, and that his word was law, Augustus spoke incessantly of the dignity of the senate, and of the respect which he had for the republic. (013-006)

He had himself called *imperator* to preserve authority over the legions, created *tribunicia potestas* to control the people, and styled himself *princeps senatus* to govern the Senate (013-005). Every power was republican in name, monarchical in substance.

The constitution he designed was inherently ambiguous: "while the government which he organized was an aristocracy in its relation to civil affairs, it was a monarchy in its relation to the military power" (013-007).

This hybrid, Montesquieu observes, "not being upheld by its own proper force, could only stand during the pleasure of the monarch; and it was, consequently, completely monarchical at bottom" (013-007).

**Domestic Consolidation.** Once established, Augustus moved to secure his regime.

"Under pretext of some riots which took place during the elections, Augustus placed a governor and a garrison in the city" (013-011).

He made the legions permanent, stationed them on the frontiers, established fixed funds for their pay, and ordered that veterans receive money rather than land -- reversing the destabilizing practice of land distribution that had begun under [[lucius-sulla|Sulla]] (013-011).

His maritime policy was equally deliberate. "The principal object of the fleets of Augustus was to serve as security for convoys, and to establish communication between the different parts of the empire" (013-012). The Mediterranean became a Roman lake, patrolled for commerce rather than conquest.

**The Maxim of Peace.** Under the Republic, Montesquieu notes, "the governing principle had been to carry on incessant war; under the emperors the cultivation of peace was a leading maxim of state" (013-009).

This was not benevolence but calculation: "With armies which might set too high a price upon their services, victories were only regarded as subjects of disquietude." Augustus was "very cautious in extending the right of Roman citizenship" and recommended in his testament that "his successors should not seek to extend the empire by new wars" (013-009).

Peace served the stability of the throne.

### Later Years & Death

Augustus reigned for over forty years, periodically performing the gesture of offering to lay down power -- "he asked every ten years to be relieved from the weight of government, and yet always continued to carry the burden" (013-007).

Montesquieu dismisses this as theater: "it was a piece of small finesse, in order to have given to him anew, that which he did not believe he had sufficiently acquired" (013-007).

The assessment is severe: "Sulla voluntarily laid down the dictatorship; but in the entire life of this man -- even in the midst of his excesses -- we see a republican spirit. All the acts, all the regulations of Augustus, tended visibly to the establishment of a monarchy" (013-007).

Augustus died in 14 AD, having outlived nearly all his contemporaries and having consolidated a system that would endure, in one form or another, for centuries. The "spirit of liberty" that once animated republican Rome had been extinguished so gradually that few noticed its departure (011-017).

## Significance

Augustus represents, in Montesquieu's analysis, the triumph of political cunning over republican virtue. His significance lies not in military genius or administrative innovation, but in his mastery of appearances -- the ability to disguise absolute power as constitutional order.

The transformation he achieved was semantic as much as institutional:

> Under Augustus, when the sovereign power comes to be usurped in a free state, whatever tends to establish the unlimited authority of the usurper takes the name of order, whilst, on the other hand, whatever tends to maintain the honest liberty of his subjects is called disorder, dissension, bad government. (013-005)

The contrast with [[lucius-sulla|Sulla]] is deliberate and illuminating.

Sulla, for all his violence, "voluntarily laid down the dictatorship" and preserved a "republican spirit" (013-007).

When Sulla's tyranny ended, "all the world raised the cry of tyranny." Augustus, by contrast, "was a cunning tyrant; and he conducted them gently to servitude" -- and "whilst under Augustus tyranny fortified itself, men only spoke of liberty" (013-008).

Key features of the Augustan system that Montesquieu identifies:

- **The concealment of monarchy.** Augustus "desired to conceal new power under known names and ordinary dignities" -- emperor, tribune, prince of the senate (013-005).

- **The professionalization of the army.** Permanent legions on the frontiers, paid from fixed funds, removed the military from civic life and made it an instrument of the prince alone (013-011).

- **The abandonment of expansion.** The maxim of peace was a structural necessity: standing armies on distant frontiers posed less threat to the emperor than victorious generals returning to Rome (013-009).

- **The gradual extinction of liberty.** "At the beginning of the reign of Augustus, conspiracies were constantly reviving" -- the old republican spirit of tyrannicide lived on (011-017). But "in consequence of the continuance of the tyranny, this spirit of liberty was gradually lost" (011-017).

The system Augustus built contained the seeds of its own degeneration.

Montesquieu traces the arc from Augustus to [[nero|Nero]]: "The civil power, which had been incessantly beaten down, found itself incapable of counterbalancing the military" (015-008).

When the balance Augustus had crafted finally collapsed, each army simply "desired to create an emperor" (015-008).

The order he established was lasting servitude indeed.

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's judgment of Augustus is among the most sustained character assessments in the *Considerations*. He reads Augustus not as a hero or villain but as a political anatomist would read a specimen -- methodically, dispassionately, and with an eye to the general causes at work.

The central paradox is that Augustus succeeded where Caesar failed precisely by learning from Caesar's fate. The cuirass beneath the senator's robe is the emblem of his reign: armed power always present, republican dignity always displayed.

Montesquieu's most concentrated verdict takes the form of a direct comparison:

> Augustus was a cunning tyrant; and he conducted them gently to servitude. Whilst under Sylla the republic regained its powers, all the world raised the cry of tyranny; whilst under Augustus tyranny fortified itself, men only spoke of liberty. (013-008)

The translator Jehu Baker, in his notes, reinforces this reading. He observes that Augustus's central achievement was to make "the conveniences of a despotic government" felt so keenly that the Romans ceased to miss their liberty (013-006). The tragedy, in Montesquieu's telling, is that a people who had once prized freedom above life itself came to prefer the comfort of servitude.

Among classical authorities, Montesquieu draws heavily on the testimony embedded in the Augustan age itself -- the poets and literary men who "flourished at Rome under the patronage of Augustus and the favor of Maecenas" (013-019). Their celebration of the *Pax Augusta* was itself evidence of the transformation: the replacement of republican virtue with imperial tranquility.
