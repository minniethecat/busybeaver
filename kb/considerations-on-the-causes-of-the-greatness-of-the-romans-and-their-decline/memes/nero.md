# Nero

Roman emperor (r. 54--68 AD), the last of the Julio-Claudian dynasty, whose reign epitomized the corruption of absolute power.

In Montesquieu's historical scheme, Nero represents the terminal point of the Julio-Claudian line -- the moment at which the inherited legitimacy derived from [[augustus|Augustus]] and the family of Caesar was extinguished, and with it the last restraint upon military usurpation (015-008).

## Life

### Early Life

Nero was born Lucius Domitius Ahenobarbus in 37 AD, the son of Gnaeus Domitius Ahenobarbus and [[agrippina-the-younger|Agrippina the Younger]], who was herself the great-granddaughter of Augustus.

After Agrippina married the emperor [[claudius|Claudius]] in 49 AD, Nero was adopted by Claudius and elevated above the emperor's own son Britannicus.

He received the education of a Roman aristocrat, tutored by the philosopher [[seneca|Seneca the Younger]] and the praetorian prefect Burrus.

Upon Claudius's death in 54 AD -- widely suspected to have been orchestrated by Agrippina -- Nero was proclaimed emperor at the age of seventeen.

### Career

Nero's early reign (54--59 AD) was relatively moderate, guided by Seneca and Burrus. But after the murder of his mother Agrippina in 59 AD, which he ordered, his rule descended into unrestrained tyranny. He squandered the imperial treasury on extravagant building projects, most famously the Domus Aurea (Golden House), and on lavish public entertainments.

Montesquieu identifies Nero as part of a broader pattern of imperial decadence.

He lists Nero alongside [[caligula|Caligula]], Commodus, and Caracalla as emperors who "were regretted by the people because of the very follies in which they indulged; for these emperors loved with fervor that which the people loved; they contributed to their pleasures with all their power, and even with their persons; they lavished upon them the wealth of the empire" (015-004).

The Roman populace, having lost its political vocation and grown idle on grain distributions, "were accustomed to plays and spectacles" and rewarded the very excesses that impoverished the state (015-004).

A specific innovation that Montesquieu attributes to Nero was the distribution of military donatives in peacetime.

"Formerly, these distributions had only taken place at the conclusion of a war;

Nero made them in time of peace" (015-010).

This practice, by habituating the soldiers to expect gifts without earning them through victory, corrupted the military's loyalty and set a fatal precedent for later emperors.

The Great Fire of Rome in 64 AD devastated much of the city. Nero's response -- blaming the Christians and initiating the first major imperial persecution -- and his subsequent construction of the Domus Aurea on land cleared by the fire, cemented his reputation for monstrous self-indulgence.

During Nero's reign, incremental legal reforms concerning slaves continued: "under Claudius, Nero, Adrian, Antoninus Pius, Severus... there was an increasing legal interference with the private power of the master" (010-007).

However, Nero is more commonly remembered for his court's moral degradation.

Baker notes in the translator's commentary that the Stoic philosophy "exalted the slave of one of Nero's courtiers to be the moral teacher of after times" (016-019) -- a reference to Epictetus, who had been the slave of Epaphroditus, Nero's freedman secretary.

Nero also debased himself by appearing personally in the arena and on the stage.

Montesquieu observes that "Roman gravity did not permit a great personage to descend into the arena or mount upon the stage," yet "Roman emperors appeared upon the arena and the stage; and this folly, which showed the greatest derangement of heart -- a contempt for the comely, the decent, and the fit -- is always associated by the historians with a character for tyranny" (015-005).

### Later Years and Death

The revolt of the provincial governors -- Galba in Spain, Otho in Lusitania, and Vitellius in Germany -- together with the defection of the Praetorian Guard and the Senate's declaration of Nero as a public enemy, forced the emperor to flee Rome in 68 AD.

He committed suicide on 9 June 68 AD, reportedly uttering the words "Qualis artifex pereo" (What an artist dies in me!).

His death at the age of thirty-one extinguished the Julio-Claudian line.

## Significance

Montesquieu draws a structural lesson from Nero's death that transcends the merely biographical:

> "The soldiers had been attached to the family of Caesar, which was the guarantee of all the advantages which they had received from the revolution. The time came when all the great families of Rome had been exterminated by that of Caesar, and when the family of Caesar itself perished in the person of Nero. The civil power, which had been incessantly beaten down, found itself incapable of counterbalancing the military: each army desired to create an emperor." (015-008)

This analysis places Nero at a pivotal inflection point in Roman constitutional history.

Before Nero, the Julio-Claudian dynasty, however debased, provided a thread of inherited legitimacy that kept the armies in check.

After Nero, no such restraint remained.

The Year of the Four Emperors (69 AD) that followed his death demonstrated that military force alone would determine the succession, inaugurating a pattern of violent usurpation that persisted for centuries.

The translator's statistical analysis confirms this trajectory: in the first group of emperors (from Caesar to Marcus Aurelius), over half met violent deaths, and Nero "resorted to self-destruction in order to escape the vengeance of his enemies" (016-031).

Nero appears repeatedly in Montesquieu's catalogue of imperial pathology.

He is cited alongside Caligula, Vitellius, Commodus, and Caracalla as an emperor whose identification with the circus factions -- "which involved no principle and no interest" -- demonstrated "the puerile character of the masses of the Roman people during the decline of the empire" (020-010).

His name became a byword for tyranny so potent that the fourth-century ecclesiastical historian Philostorgius could dismiss the orthodox emperor Gratian by comparing him to Nero (017-009), and the Byzantine chronicler Nicetas could describe Andronicus Comnenus as "the Nero of the Greeks" (023-004).

The analytical index summarizes the genealogical consequence concisely: Caesar's "family destroys the other great families of Rome, and runs out in Nero" (023-026).

## Contemporary Views

> "Nero, Caligula, Commodus, Caracalla, were regretted by the people because of the very follies in which they indulged; for these emperors loved with fervor that which the people loved; they contributed to their pleasures with all their power, and even with their persons; they lavished upon them the wealth of the empire; and, when this was exhausted, the people looked on without pain whilst all the great families were despoiled. They enjoyed the fruits of the tyranny without any alloy of fear, for they found safety in their baseness." (015-004)

> "It was an ancient custom of the Romans that he who enjoyed the honors of a triumph should distribute some denarii to each soldier: it was a small affair. During the civil wars, these donatives were increased. They had previously consisted of money which had been taken from the enemy; but during these unhappy times the money of citizens was taken for the purpose, the soldiers desiring a share where there was no booty. Formerly, these distributions had only taken place at the conclusion of a war; Nero made them in time of peace. The soldiers became accustomed to such gifts; and they were enraged against Galba, who had the courage to tell them that he knew how to choose, but not to purchase them." (015-010)
