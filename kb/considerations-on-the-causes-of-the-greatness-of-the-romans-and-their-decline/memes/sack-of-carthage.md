# Sack of Carthage

The destruction of Carthage in 146 BC was the culmination of the Third Punic War and one of the most complete acts of annihilation in ancient history. The city was razed, its population enslaved, and its territory annexed as the Roman province of Africa.

Montesquieu treats the destruction of Carthage as a decisive turning point. As long as Carthage existed, Rome had a rival that compelled virtue. "Carthage, become rich sooner than Rome, had likewise become corrupt sooner" [004-002] — and it was the competition with Carthage that had disciplined Roman public life. Once Carthage was gone, that discipline dissolved.

## Background

The rivalry between Rome and Carthage had defined the western Mediterranean for over a century. The First Punic War (264–241 BC) had given Rome Sicily; the Second (218–201 BC) had reduced Carthage to a client state stripped of its overseas possessions.

Yet Carthage recovered economically with surprising speed. Its merchants and farmers prospered, and the city's wealth began to alarm influential Romans — above all, Cato the Elder, who famously ended every speech in the Senate with *Carthago delenda est*: "Carthage must be destroyed."

Montesquieu emphasizes the economic dimension of the rivalry. "At Carthage every labor performed for the public was paid for" [004-004], while at Rome citizens served the state from patriotism. The contrast between the commercial Carthaginian ethos and the civic Roman ethos was fundamental.

## Course

### Phase 1: The Ultimatum (149 BC)

Rome found a pretext for war in Carthage's conflict with Numidia, a Roman ally under King Masinissa. When Carthage defended itself against Numidian aggression, Rome declared this a violation of the treaty that had ended the Second Punic War.

A Roman army landed in Africa, and the Carthaginians — desperate to avoid war — surrendered unconditionally. The consuls then delivered a devastating ultimatum: the Carthaginians must abandon their city and resettle at least ten miles inland. For a maritime trading power, this was a death sentence.

### Phase 2: The Siege (149–146 BC)

The Carthaginians, facing extinction, chose to fight. They closed the city gates, improvised weapons from temple metal, and endured a siege that lasted nearly three years. Women cut their hair to make catapult cords; the city's workshops turned out arms day and night.

The Roman command was initially incompetent, but in 147 BC the Senate sent Scipio Aemilianus — the adopted grandson of the Scipio Africanus who had defeated Hannibal — to take charge. Scipio tightened the siege, building a mole across the harbor to cut off supplies.

### Phase 3: The Destruction (Spring 146 BC)

When the city finally fell, the fighting continued for six days through the narrow streets. Scipio ordered the systematic destruction of the city. The fires burned for seventeen days.

Polybius, the Greek historian who was present with Scipio, records that the Roman general wept as he watched Carthage burn. When asked why, Scipio replied that he was thinking of Rome — knowing that the same fate must one day befall his own city.

## Outcome

Carthage was erased as a political entity. Its territory became the Roman province of Africa, which would become one of the Empire's wealthiest regions. Its population was sold into slavery; its libraries — one of the great repositories of Phoenician learning — were dispersed or destroyed.

But Montesquieu directs our attention to the consequences for Rome, not for Carthage. "In states governed by a monarch," he observes, "power is divided between the prince and his ministers" [004-004]. But in a republic, the removal of external pressure was fatal.

## Impact on Rome

For Montesquieu, the destruction of Carthage marks the pivot from greatness to decline. The argument is structural: rivalry with Carthage had forced Rome to maintain the virtues — frugality, patriotism, military discipline — that were the foundation of its success.

Once Carthage was gone, "there was no longer any power that Rome needed to fear" [006-003]. The Senate could indulge its greed for provincial plunder without consequence. The people, no longer threatened by a foreign enemy, could be bought with bread and circuses.

The sequence Montesquieu traces is precise: Carthage destroyed (146 BC) → the tribunate of Tiberius Gracchus and the beginning of civil strife (133 BC) → the rise of Marius and Sulla → the collapse of the Republic. The thirteen years between the sack of Carthage and the Gracchan crisis are the last years of the healthy Republic.
