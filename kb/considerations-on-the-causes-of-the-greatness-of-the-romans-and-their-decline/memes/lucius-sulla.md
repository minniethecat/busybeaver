# Lucius Cornelius Sulla

Lucius Cornelius Sulla (138–78 BC), known as "Sylla" in Montesquieu's orthography, was a Roman general and dictator pivotal to the collapse of the Republic.
Montesquieu treats him as the opening actor of Chapter 11 ("Sylla -- Pompey and Caesar"), the man whose actions first made the republic's destruction inevitable.

Sulla led the aristocratic faction against the popular party of Gaius Marius [011-020].
His march on Rome in 88 BC was the first time a Roman general entered the city sword in hand, teaching "Roman generals to violate that asylum of liberty" [011-002].

He invented the Proscription, a systematic method of murder and confiscation that shattered political neutrality [011-003].
Yet Montesquieu discerns in Sulla "a republican spirit" -- a man whose reforms, though tyrannically executed, "tended constantly to a certain form of republic" [013-007] [013-008].

## Life

### Early Life

Sulla was born in 138 BC to an impoverished branch of the patrician Cornelii gens.
His early years were spent in obscurity and poverty, a background that distinguished him from the wealthy senatorial elite whose cause he would later champion.

His first public service came as quaestor under Marius in the Jugurthine War (107–105 BC), where he negotiated the betrayal and capture of King Jugurtha.
This early association with Marius -- who broke tradition by enrolling capite censi into the legions [009-001] -- curdled into the deadliest rivalry of the late Republic.

### Career

Sulla's rise accelerated through the Social War (91–88 BC) and the First Mithridatic War (89–85 BC).
When Marius, with the tribune Sulpitius, maneuvered to take command of the Mithridatic campaign, Sulla responded with an unprecedented act: he marched his army into Rome itself.

As Montesquieu records, "he had entered into Rome sword in hand, and had thus taught Roman generals to violate that asylum of liberty" [011-002].
The city, which no foreign enemy had entered since the Gallic sack three centuries earlier, fell to one of its own commanders.

After recapturing Rome and proscribing Marius (who fled to Africa), Sulla departed for the East.
During his Asiatic expedition, he "ruined all military discipline" and "accustomed his army to rapine, thus giving it wants which it had never before felt" [011-002].

Having corrupted the soldiers, it was inevitable "that they in their turn should afterwards corrupt their officers" [011-002].
This moral decay was, for Montesquieu, a root cause of the Republic's destruction -- a corruption that outlasted Sulla and infected the armies of Pompey, Caesar, and every commander who followed.

Upon his return to Italy in 83 BC, Sulla waged a brutal civil war against the Marian faction.
His victory was followed by the Proscription, an innovation described in Baker's translator's note:

> Sylla first introduced the method of proscription. Upon his return into the city, after having conquered the party of Marius, he wrote down the names of those whom he doomed to die, and ordered them to be fixed up on tables in the public places of the city, with the promise of a certain reward for the head of each person so proscribed. [011-019]

The first list contained forty senators and 1,600 equites [011-019].
Unlike the chaotic massacres under Marius, the proscription was a bureaucratic machine of terror: new lists were repeatedly posted as new victims occurred to Sulla's memory.

Baker emphasizes the political significance: "this violent and bloody practice originated with the aristocratic, and not with the popular faction" [011-020].
While Marius committed extensive massacres, he "organized no machinery for systematic destruction" comparable to the proscription [011-020].

Sulla "had given the lands of citizens to soldiers, and thus rendered them covetous for the future" [011-002].
From that moment, every soldier looked for an occasion to seize his fellow citizens' property.

He established forty-seven legions in Italy, and these soldiers, "regarding their fortune as contingent upon his life, watched over his safety, and were constantly ready to succor or to avenge him" [011-004].

As dictator (82–79 BC), Sulla enacted three laws "very well calculated to remove the cause of the disorders" [011-001].
They increased the senate's authority, tempered the people's power, and regulated the tribunes.

These reforms aimed to restore senatorial predominance.
But Montesquieu judged that the damage Sulla did through his methods outweighed any good his laws might accomplish.

The most profound consequence was that the proscription made neutrality impossible.
Placed between two ambitious leaders, "those who were neutral, and who adhered to the party of liberty, were sure to be proscribed by the one of the two who might be conqueror" [011-003].

Prudence therefore dictated joining one faction or the other.
This logic of forced partisanship persisted long after Sulla's death.

### Later Years and Death

In 79 BC, Sulla voluntarily laid down the dictatorship.
The act "appeared to be only desirous of living under the protection of his own laws," but Montesquieu identifies it as "itself a sequence of his violences" [011-004].

His forty-seven legions of veterans, bound to him by land grants, ensured his personal safety regardless of formal title.
He retired to his villa at Puteoli, where he died in 78 BC.

The period between Sulla's death and renewed civil war was "a hollow truce, a delusive calm, alarmed by conspiracies and disturbed by seditions" [011-031].
Sulla "left the state filled with the sons, relations, and friends of men who had fallen by his military executions, or perished by his murderous proscriptions" [011-031].

Soon after, Pompey "procured the abrogation of those laws of Sylla which limited the power of the people" [011-005].
Sulla's constitutional settlement was dismantled, even as his precedent of armed force proved irresistible.

## Significance

Montesquieu locates Sulla at the inflection point between the old Republic and the new reality of military autocracy.
His significance is paradoxical: a conservative revolutionary, a restorer who destroyed what he sought to save.

Sulla broke the most fundamental norm of republican politics -- that no general should bring armed force into the political arena.
His march on Rome demonstrated that the legions were loyal to their commander, not to the senate and people.

Montesquieu had explained in Chapter 9 that when Rome's armies were carried beyond Italy, the legionaries "ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey, and of Caesar" [009-002].
Sulla was the first to exploit this structural transformation to its logical conclusion.

The Proscription introduced organized political terror as an instrument of factional supremacy.
By "setting a price upon the heads of those who were not of his own party" [011-003], Sulla made civil war total -- the defeated faced death and dispossession, not mere political eclipse.

Baker identifies the Civil Wars of Marius and Sylla, and the Proscriptions, as phenomena that "clearly indicate the decline of civic virtue" and mark the transition from grandeur to decadence [009-013].

Sulla's distribution of citizen lands to soldiers created a permanent constituency for further expropriations.
Once soldiers learned that military service could yield farms seized from fellow citizens, "there was no longer a soldier who did not look for an occasion" for such seizure [011-002].

This economic incentive for civil war persisted through the triumviral proscriptions of 43 BC and beyond.

Yet Montesquieu insists Sulla was no Augustus.
Where Augustus "conducted them gently to servitude," Sulla "carried the Romans to liberty with violence" [013-008].

Sulla's dictatorship was traditionally constitutional in form -- the office was a recognized republican magistracy.
His reforms aimed at restoring senatorial supremacy within a republican framework, unlike Augustus's disguised monarchy.

"Sulla voluntarily laid down the dictatorship; but in the entire life of this man -- even in the midst of his excesses -- we see a republican spirit" [013-007].
This does not excuse his crimes; it distinguishes two modes of destroying the republic -- Sulla's violent but republican, Caesar's and Augustus's monarchical.

## Contemporary Views

> Sylla enacted three laws which were very well calculated to remove the cause of the disorders which we have seen. These laws increased the authority of the senate, tempered the power of the people, and regulated that of the tribunes. And the fancy which prompted him to abdicate the dictatorship seemed to restore the life of the republic. But, amid the frenzy which had attended his successes, he had done things which rendered it impossible for Rome to retain her liberty. [011-001] [011-002]

> He had entered into Rome sword in hand, and had thus taught Roman generals to violate that asylum of liberty. He had given the lands of citizens to soldiers, and thus rendered them covetous for the future. He had invented the Proscription, and set a price upon the heads of those who were not of his own party. [011-002] [011-003]

> Sulla voluntarily laid down the dictatorship; but in the entire life of this man -- even in the midst of his excesses -- we see a republican spirit. All his measures, although tyrannically executed, tended constantly to a certain form of republic. Sylla was an intensely passionate man; and he carried the Romans to liberty with violence. Augustus was a cunning tyrant; and he conducted them gently to servitude. [013-007] [013-008]

> Sylla first introduced the method of proscription. Upon his return into the city, after having conquered the party of Marius, he wrote down the names of those whom he doomed to die, and ordered them to be fixed up on tables in the public places of the city, with the promise of a certain reward for the head of each person so proscribed. [011-019] -- Translator's Note (Jehu Baker)
