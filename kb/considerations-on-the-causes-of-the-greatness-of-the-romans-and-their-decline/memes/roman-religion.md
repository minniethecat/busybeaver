# Roman Religion

Roman religion was an instrument of state policy as much as a system of belief. Montesquieu treats it as one of the structural forces — alongside law, military discipline, and civic virtue — that sustained the Roman Republic through its centuries of greatness.

## Definition

"The religion of the Romans was but a great political institution" (016-023). Unlike modern conceptions of religion as a matter of private conscience, Roman religion was integrated into every aspect of public life: magistrates consulted the auspices before battles, the Senate deliberated in consecrated spaces, and the pontifical college regulated the ritual calendar. Religion served "as the best guarantee" of public morals and social order (010-002).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

Religion reinforced the authority of established institutions. "The prestige of ancient institutions" was amplified by "the influence of religion" — the two were inseparable (008-005). During the early Republic, religious sanctions complemented legal penalties: what the law could not reach, the fear of divine displeasure restrained.

### Imperial Period

Under the Empire, religion became a tool of imperial legitimacy. "The establishment of Christianity" marked a decisive break: "this religion was no longer a stranger in the empire" (016-006). The Christianization of the Empire transformed the relationship between religious and political authority — a transformation Montesquieu viewed with characteristic ambivalence, recognizing both its civilizing effect and its challenge to traditional Roman institutions.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …religion is always the best guarantee that one can have of the morals of men (010-002)

Montesquieu argues that religion functioned as a check on power and conduct that mere law could not provide. Yet he also notes its darker uses: "those which had religion for a pretext were long ones" — wars justified by religious claims tended to be the most protracted (013-004). He distinguishes between a "religion which is more restraining" and one that merely serves as a pretext for violence, implying that Rome's traditional religion belonged to the former category until corrupted by Eastern influences (015-003).

## Examples

- **The auspices**: Magistrates consulting divine will before major decisions, integrating religion into governance
- **The pontifical college**: The chief priests who regulated the religious calendar and advised the Senate on ritual matters
- **Christianization**: The transformation of Roman religion under Constantine and his successors, replacing the old state cult with a universal faith (016-006)
