# Cincinnatus

> ⚠️ **Corpus-Unavailable**: This figure is not discussed in Montesquieu's *Considerations on the Causes of the Greatness of the Romans and of their Decline*. No corpus citations are available. The page is retained as a cross-reference entry for related Roman figures and concepts.

Roman patrician and dictator (519–430 BC) who, according to legend, was called from his plow to save the Republic and returned to his farm sixteen days later — the enduring symbol of republican virtue uncorrupted by power.

## Life

Cincinnatus is known primarily from Livy and other classical sources, not from Montesquieu's text. His story — summoned from the plow to serve as dictator, defeating the Aequi in sixteen days, and immediately returning to his farm — was the quintessential Roman exemplum of virtuous conduct.

## Significance

Though absent from Montesquieu's *Considerations*, Cincinnatus represents the ideal of republican citizenship that Montesquieu traces to Romulus's foundational legislation: the farmer-soldier who serves the state in war and returns to the plow in peace (010-004).
