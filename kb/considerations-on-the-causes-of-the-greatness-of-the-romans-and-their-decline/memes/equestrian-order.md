# Equestrian Order

The second rank of the Roman elite, below the senatorial order — originally the cavalry class, it evolved into a commercial and administrative aristocracy whose wealth and influence grew as the Republic decayed.

## Structure

The equestrian order (*ordo equester*) was defined by a property qualification of 400,000 sesterces. This was lower than the senatorial census of one million sesterces, but still placed members among the wealthiest citizens.

Equites were distinguished by the narrow purple stripe (*angustus clavus*) on their tunics and the gold ring (*anulus aureus*). In the centuriate assembly, the knights or horsemen constituted a bloc of centuries — originally eighteen — that voted first and carried disproportionate political weight. (008-013)

## History

### Origin

The equestrian order originated in the regal period as the cavalry arm of the Roman army — citizens wealthy enough to provide and maintain a horse for military service. Under the early Republic, the eighteen centuries of equites served as the elite voting bloc in the *comitia centuriata*, where the wealthier classes held a built-in majority.

### Evolution

Over time, the military function of the equites diminished as cavalry was increasingly supplied by auxiliaries.

But their economic and political role expanded dramatically. Equestrians dominated tax collection (*publicani*), banking, and long-distance trade — activities from which senators were legally barred.

The numbers grew substantially: in one episode of the late Republic, forty senators and 1,600 equites were proscribed. (011-019)

Under the Empire, the equestrian order was transformed into an imperial administrative class, supplying prefects, procurators, and governors.

## Role in Roman History

The equestrian order occupied the crucial middle ground between the senatorial aristocracy and the common people. In the healthy Republic, they provided military leadership and commercial enterprise.

But as the Republic decayed, the equites became a destabilizing force. Their control of tax collection in the provinces gave them immense wealth and influence, while their exclusion from the Senate created political resentment. (016-026)

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> Montesquieu discusses the equestrian order primarily in the context of Rome's constitutional balance. The eighteen equestrian centuries, voting first in the centuriate assembly, were part of the mixed constitution's aristocratic element.

> However, Montesquieu recognizes that the equites' commercial role, especially their dominance of provincial tax farming, made them vectors of the same corruption that infected the senatorial class. (008-013) (011-019)
