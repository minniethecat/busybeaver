# Roman Syria

The Seleucid kingdom of Syria was the most powerful of the successor states to [[alexander-the-great|Alexander]]'s empire. Montesquieu devotes sustained attention to its decline, using the kingdom's fate to illustrate Rome's method of indirect conquest — and to mark the moment when Rome itself began to be corrupted by the wealth of the East.

## Geography

The Seleucid empire at its height stretched from the Aegean to the Indus, holding "both high and low Asia." But this vast extent contained a fatal structural weakness. (PN=005-011)

"When the capital and the principal forces are in the provinces of low Asia, the dependencies of high Asia cannot be preserved; and when the seat of empire is in high Asia, it is weakened on the side of the lower provinces." (PN=005-011)

The empire was thus inherently overextended. Seleucus, its founder, destroyed the kingdom of Lysimachus toward the end of his life, but in the ensuing confusion the kingdoms of Pergamus, Cappadocia, and Bithynia broke away as independent states. (PN=005-010)

## History

### Before Rome

The Seleucid dynasty ruled Syria from 312 BC, when Seleucus I Nicator secured Babylon after Alexander's death. At its peak, the empire "possessed nearly all the states of Darius except Egypt." (PN=005-010) But structural weaknesses accumulated: the kings "always looking with extreme envy upon the felicity of the kingdom of Egypt, they were absorbed with the purpose of conquering it." (PN=005-011)

This Egypt-obsession led the Seleucids to neglect the East, where provinces were lost to Parthian expansion. Meanwhile the western provinces were "very badly obeyed." (PN=005-011) The empire suffered from the same two-class division that plagued Egypt — conquering Greeks and conquered Syrians — but with an additional weakness: a court culture of debilitating luxury.

### Under Rome

Rome's involvement with Syria began through Greek intermediaries. The Aetolians, "seeking now to correct their folly by their folly, they called Antiochus, king of Syria, into Greece, as they had previously called in the Romans." (PN=005-010)

Antiochus III (the Great) proved an inept adversary. "Hannibal advised that the war should be renewed in Italy, and that Philip of Macedon should be gained or rendered neutral. Antiochus did nothing of the kind." (PN=005-013)

Instead, "he presented himself in Greece with a small part of his forces; and, as if he had desired to be a spectator of war, instead of engaging in it, he occupied himself with his pleasures only." (PN=005-013)

Defeated, Antiochus "fled back into Asia — more frightened than conquered." (PN=005-013) The Romans pursued him across the Hellespont, defeated him again, and he "consented to the most infamous treaty that was ever accepted by a great prince." (PN=005-014)

Montesquieu marks this moment as pivotal for Rome itself: "the war which the latter made against Antiochus marks the true epoch of their corruption." (PN=005-012) The luxury, vanity, and effeminacy of the Syrian court "became contagious among the Romans themselves." (PN=005-012)

> "Luxury, vanity, and effeminacy, which at no period have quitted the courts of Asia, were especially supreme in that of Syria. The malady extended to the people and to the soldiers, and became contagious among the Romans themselves." (PN=005-012)

After Antiochus's defeat, the Seleucid kingdom persisted in diminished form. Rome continued to weaken it through dynastic manipulation. The Romans "declared in favor of the infant son of Antiochus, and against Demetrius" — working the ruin of Syria "in the character of tutors." (PN=006-009)

Through forced treaty, Rome prohibited Syrian kings from making war on Rome's allies — "that is to say, ordinarily, that he should make no war with any of his neighbors." (PN=006-006)

Pompey annexed what remained of the Seleucid realm as the province of Syria in 64 BC. Under Roman rule, Syria became a key frontier province, its legions guarding against Parthian incursions and its cities — Antioch, Damascus, Palmyra — flourishing as commercial centers.

### Later Period

Roman Syria remained under imperial control until the 7th century AD. Antioch was one of the empire's greatest cities, and Syrian merchants traded as far as India and China. The Arab conquest (634–638 AD) ended Roman rule, though the province's administrative structures and urban network persisted under the Umayyad Caliphate.

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu's treatment of Syria serves three analytical purposes. First, it demonstrates the Roman method of piecemeal reduction. Syria was never conquered in a single war; it was weakened by treaty, stripped of allies, divided by dynastic intrigue, and only formally annexed when it had become "subject, without any one being able to fix the epoch of its subjugation." (PN=006-015)

Second, Syria illustrates Montesquieu's geographic determinism. "If Seleucus had remained at Babylon, and left the maritime provinces to the successors of Antigonus — the empire of Persia would have been invincible to the Greeks, and that of Seleucus to the Romans." (PN=005-011)

The decision to anchor the empire in low Asia (Syria proper) rather than high Asia (Babylon) was, in Montesquieu's view, a fatal strategic error.

Third, and most important for Montesquieu's larger argument, Syria was the conduit through which Eastern luxury corrupted Roman virtue. The wealth seized from Antiochus, the courtly habits observed by Roman commanders, the taste for Asian refinements — these were the seeds of Rome's eventual decline. Conquest gave Rome an empire; Eastern conquest gave Rome the vices that would undo it.
