# Roman Republic
The Roman Republic (*res publica*) was the system of government that arose in [[ancient-rome]] when Rome "having expelled the kiags, established annual consuls" — a change Montesquieu names as "another of the causes which conducted her to such a height of power" (001-006). Within this framework the [[roman-senate-body]] directed affairs, the people supplied the martial energy of the [[roman-army]], and the [[conflict-of-the-orders]] between patricians and plebeians was channeled into a steadily widening body of [[roman-law]]. For Montesquieu the Republic is less a set of offices than a self-correcting [[mixed-constitution]]: the political form in which the [[civic-virtue]] of a poor and warlike people could flourish and produce greatness, and whose corruption would later open the way to [[julius-caesar]] and the end of [[roman-liberty]].

## Definition
Montesquieu treats the Republic as the regime that succeeded the kings, in which "the kingly power" passed not to a hereditary line but to magistrates "changed every year, and who sought to signalize their magistracy in order to obtain a renewal of it," so that "there was not a moment to be lost to ambition" (001-006). Its working parts were balanced against one another: in the [[conflict-of-the-orders]] "the people were sustained, or rather animated, by their tribunes, whilst the patricians were defended by the senate" (008-004). Montesquieu distinguishes Rome from "the ancient republics" of Greece chiefly by the ratio of soldiers to citizens — "which is at present as one to a hundred, might easily be as one to eight among them" — a density of civic and military participation rooted in "an equal division of the lands" (003-002). The translator cautions, however, that even at its most open the Republic was "much less republican, iu fact, than are the monarchies of our present Europe," since provincials and slaves far outnumbered citizens (008-016).

## Role in Roman History
### Republican Period
In its early life Rome was "yet a small republic when, the Latins having refused the contingent of troops which they were obligated to furnish, ten legions were immediately raised in the city" (003-004) — institutional cohesion compensating for small size. Its strength lay in absorbing the people it conquered: where Carthage "employed foreigners for its wars and citizens for its traffic," Rome "made citizens of all nations, and soldiers of its citizens" (004-005), so that even the Samnites, "subjugated after twenty-four triumphs," became "auxiliaries of their conquerors" (004-006). Internally the regime grew steadily more open: after the kings "the goverament became an aristocracy" of patrician families (008-002), but the secession to the Mons Sacer produced the [[tribunate-of-the-plebs]] — a magistracy "whose duty it should be to prevent injustice from being done to the plebeians" (008-004) — until "the distinction between the two had become a vain thing" (008-006). Montesquieu credits the [[comitia-centuriata]] of Servius Tullius and the censors' five-yearly review for keeping "the masses of the people" from making "an abusive use of their own power" (008-009).

### Imperial Period
The same magnitude that fed the Republic's greatness undid it: "the arms of the republic were carried beyond the Alps" (009-002), and "it was solely the magnitude of the republic which produced the evil" (009-005). Once "the republic had become corrupt," revolutionary movements followed (003-003), and the long internal compromise broke down as "private individuals possessed immense fortunes" and "the nobles resisted with more force than the patricians had done" (008-006). The decline of [[civic-virtue]] (009-013) and the rise of [[political-corruption]] dissolved the Republic's spirit while its forms lingered, passing into the empire of [[augustus]].

## Montesquieu's Analysis
Montesquieu's central claim is that the Republic's greatness flowed from the *design* of its constitution — above all its capacity to correct its own faults:

> The government of Eome was admirable in this — that from its origin its constitution was such — whether from the spirit of the people, the influence of the senate, or the authority of certain magistrates — that all abuses could be corrected. (008-010)

By contrast Carthage "perished, for the reason that, when it became necessary to retrench abuses, she could not even tolerate the hand of her own Hannibal" (008-010).

This self-correcting quality is the republican analogue of [[mos-maiorum]]: the [[roman-senate-body]] supplied continuity because "the senate never departed from the ancient maxims" (004-012), while the popular assemblies supplied energy.

Montesquieu even rehabilitates internal strife, endorsing the view that "from the disagreement" of "two diverse humors — that of the people and that of the great" — "proceed all the laws which are made in favor of liberty" (008-001). What sustained this balance was not machinery but the [[civic-virtue]] of the citizens:

> There is nothing so powerful as a repubKc in which the laws are observed, not from fear, not from reason, but from passion — as was the case with the Romans and the Lacedaemonians. (004-005)

The danger peculiar to a republic is that its remedies attack themselves: in a monarchy "dissensions are easily appeased, for the sovereign holds in his hands a coercive authority," but "in a republic such dissensions are more lasting, for the malady ordinarily attacks the very power which should be able to cure it" (004-004).

The translator adds a warning against over-reading Roman institutional wisdom: the dual consulate, he argues, was "rather a necessity growing out of tbe imperiect state of Eoman polity, than as an appointment wise in itself" (001-020) — a reminder that for Montesquieu it is the whole self-balancing system, and the [[civic-virtue]] animating it, that constituted the Republic's genius.
