# Julius Caesar

Julius Caesar (100 BC – 44 BC) is, in Montesquieu's *Considerations*, the man who gave "the final blow to a republic which, in fact, was already virtually ruined as such" (011-027).

For Montesquieu he is not the lone author of Rome's fall but its instrument: where [[lucius-sulla|Sulla]] had taught Roman generals "to violate that asylum of liberty" (011-002), Caesar perfected the lesson, succeeding [[pompey|Pompey]] in the long contest that ended the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]].

"Pompey appeared first; Caesar followed him closely" (011-004), and the latter, "like Sylla, was willing to obtain sovereign power by armed violence" (011-006).

Caesar embodies Montesquieu's central thesis that the republic perished from general causes — corruption of the army, the populace, and the aristocracy together — for which ambitious men merely supplied the occasion.

"If Caesar and Pompey had thought as Cato, others would have thought as Caesar and Pompey; and the republic, ready to perish, would have been drawn to the precipice by another hand" (011-013).

His career thus illustrates how the love of glory and dominion, once a republican virtue, became the engine of tyranny, ending in his own assassination at the hands of the [[roman-senate-body|Senate's]] defenders (011-016).

## Life

### Early Life

Montesquieu records little of Caesar's beginnings, treating him chiefly as a public actor of Rome's last republican generation. He was the younger of the two great rivals: a translator's note fixes that "Pompey was about six years the senior of Caesar; the one was born B.C. 106, the other B.C. 100" (011-004).

Caesar emerged into a Rome whose institutions Sulla had already shattered — an army accustomed to rapine, lands of citizens handed to soldiers, and the Proscription invented to set "a price upon the heads of those who were not of his own party" (011-002, 011-003).

Into this corrupted order Caesar entered as a leader of the popular faction; the analytical index names "Marius and Caesar leaders of the popular faction" (025-005), and Montesquieu marks the "violent character of his ambition" and "his corrupt practices" early in his account (023-026).

### Career

Caesar's rise turned, in Montesquieu's analysis, on the accident of his provincial command.

"By an unfortunate conformity of names, Gaul beyond the Alps had been joined to his government of cisalpine Gaul" (011-008), placing in his hands both the province that "held the city in check" and [[gaul|transalpine Gaul]], "offering Caesar an opportunity to carry on war during many years" (011-009). The result, Montesquieu insists, was decisive for the [[roman-army|army]]:

> If Caesar had not had the government of transalpine Gaul, he would neither have corrupted his soldiers, nor caused his name to be respected by so many victories. (011-009)

His soldiers, growing old with him through the [[gallic-wars|wars in Gaul]] — whose peoples, "according to the description given by Caesar in his Commentaries," were "larger and taller than" the Romans (004-019) — became veterans whose loyalty was personal rather than civic.

When the Senate turned to Pompey against him, Caesar precipitated the [[roman-civil-war|civil war]] by crossing the Rubicon: "The same consternation which Hannibal created in Rome by his victory at Cannae, Caesar created by crossing the Rubicon" (011-010).

Pompey, dismayed, abandoned Italy, and Caesar pursued him into Greece, where the rivalry was settled at Pharsalia (011-013). Caesar's victories rested less on fortune than on faculty: "that extraordinary man possessed so many great qualities in perfection — although he had many vices also" (011-010).

### Later Years & Death

Victory made Caesar master of Rome, and he ruled, Montesquieu notes, behind republican forms: "At first, Caesar governed under the titles of the Roman magistracies; for men are not much touched except by names" (011-013).

His scorn for the Senate, however, was undisguised — he "himself made senatorial decrees, and subscribed to them the names of the first senators that came into his mind" (011-014), and he "attempted to have a diadem placed upon his head, but, seeing that the acclamations of the people ceased, he rejected it" (011-014). Even his famed mercy became an offense:

> Caesar pardoned everybody; but it appears to me that the moderation which a man shows after he has first usurped all does not merit great praise. (011-013)

His clemency, Montesquieu writes, "was insulting. He was regarded, not as pardoning, but as disdaining to punish" (011-014). Under the ancient law that armed every citizen against a usurper of sovereign power (011-016), conspirators — most of them "men who either belonged to his own party, or who had been loaded with benefits by him" (011-016) — struck him down.

The deed only opened a new round of [[roman-civil-war|civil wars]]: Antony seized "Caesar's book of accounts" so that "the dictator reigned more imperiously than during his life" (012-001), and the wars "which pursued the murderers of Caesar" (013-001) ran on through Philippi to Actium and the rise of [[augustus|Octavius]].

## Significance

For Montesquieu, Caesar is the hinge on which the Roman constitution turned from republic to empire. The whole sweep of imperial decline is dated from him: the series of emperors runs "from Caesar to Augustulus" (016-031), and Caesar's own innovation of governing under old magistracies set the template — later emperors "were dictators under the name of emperors" (016-003).

Yet Montesquieu refuses to let one man bear the weight of the revolution. He insists "there is no necessity that we should accuse the ambition of a few individuals, but there is a necessity that we should accuse man — always the more greedy for the acquisition of power in proportion as the amount which he already possesses is great" (011-012).

The deeper cause lay in a [[political-corruption|corruption]] that had infected every order. The Roman who lived in Caesar's day "placed a money value upon everything connected with the interests of the state" — "offices, public duties, suffrages, had acquired a mercenary price" (011-030). Caesar's role was to be the demagogue whom such a state inevitably produced (011-031).

The key episodes by which Montesquieu charts this are:

- **The union of the dynasts** — Caesar joined his interests with Pompey and Crassus; "Cato said it was not the enmity of these men that ruined the republic, but their union" (011-007).

- **The double Gallic command** — the accidental joining of the two Gauls gave him the army and the victories that made revolution possible (011-008, 011-009).

- **The crossing of the Rubicon** — the breach of the *senatus consultum* that declared anyone passing it with troops "sacrilegious and a parricide" (011-009), beginning the civil war (011-010).

- **The defeat of Pompey** — settled at Pharsalia, after which Caesar "encumbered himself with four wars" by his "foolish amour" at Alexandria (011-013).

- **The dictatorship and the diadem** — governing under magistracies, then reaching for the crown and [[imperium|sovereign power]] (011-013, 011-014).

- **The assassination** — the conspiracy of his own beneficiaries, in fulfilment of the ancient doctrine of tyrannicide (011-016, 011-017).

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's own verdict is studiously balanced and ultimately severe. He grants Caesar's genius without reserve — the man would "have been conqueror, no matter what army he had commanded" (011-010) — but treats his ambition as more dangerous precisely because it was direct and armed, unlike the "slower and more moderate" ambition of Pompey (011-006).

The decisive moral judgment is that Caesar's moderation came too late to be virtue: usurpation first, clemency after (011-013).

Montesquieu reads the assassination through the eyes of an ancient rather than a modern philosopher, observing that "in fact, was not the crime of Caesar — who lived under a free government — of such a nature that it could not be punished otherwise than by assassination?" (011-017).

Among the classical authorities Montesquieu weaves into his account, [[cicero|Cicero]] supplies the most pointed contemporary testimony. Cicero, Montesquieu notes, "justly accuses him of having been slow" after Pharsalia (011-013).

Against Caesar's ambition Montesquieu sets the disinterest of [[cato-the-elder|Cato]], for whom "glory" was the end while with Cicero "virtue was the accessory" (012-005).

The translator Jehu Baker adds that opinion has ever since divided between those who paint "the crime of the usurper" and an "imperialism" that made Caesar "its supreme prototype," while the "rational thinker" sees only "the mere completion of a social change" (011-027).

In Baker's summation of the scene, Caesar "mastered a nobility of which Lucullus and Hortensius, Sulpicius and Catulus, Pompey and Cicero, Brutus and Cato were members" (011-029).
