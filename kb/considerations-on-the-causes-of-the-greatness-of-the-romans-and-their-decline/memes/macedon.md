# Macedon

The Hellenistic kingdom north of Greece, surrounded by inaccessible mountains and inhabited by a people admirably adapted to war. Conquered by Rome after the defeat of Philip V at Cynoscephalae (197 BC) and Perseus at Pydna (168 BC), Macedon became a Roman province in 148 BC.

## Geography

Macedon was almost surrounded by inaccessible mountains, providing formidable natural defenses (PN 005-006). Its people were brave, obedient, industrious, and indefatigable — qualities Montesquieu suggests were derived from the climate. The kingdom sat north of Greece proper, with the Thessalians subject to Macedonian rule (PN 005-005).

## History

### Before Rome

Under Philip II and Alexander the Great, Macedon extended its sway over half the known world (PN 006-002). The kingdom persisted in attempting to subdue the Greek city-states, while Greece maintained itself through a balance of power (PN 005-006). Philip V initially won Greek affection through moderation but later became a cruel tyrant, alienating his natural allies (PN 005-007).

### Under Rome

Rome first defeated Philip V at the battle of Cynoscephalae (197 BC), forcing him to withdraw garrisons from all Greece, surrender his vessels, and pay tribute (PN 005-008). The Romans had allied with the Aetolians against Philip, then later humbled those same allies (PN 006-001).

Perseus, son of Philip V, renewed the war but was destroyed by Paulus Aemilius at the battle of Pydna (PN 006-002). Rome then dissolved Macedon into four separate parts, breaking its civil and political unity (PN 006-008).

### Later Period

Macedon sank to the condition of a Roman province in 148 BC (PN 006-002). The region's renowned soldiers later served the Turkish empire — Montesquieu notes that even in his day, the men drawn from this country were the best soldiers of the Ottoman forces (PN 005-006).

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu uses Macedon's defeat to illustrate the decisive superiority of the Roman legion over the Macedonian phalanx:

> Polybius compares the military method of the Romans with that of the Macedonians — which last had been adopted by all the successors of Alexander the Great. He points out the advantages and the inconveniences of the phalanx and the legion. He gives the preference to the latter; and, if we are to judge by the events of the time, it would appear that he was right. (PN 005-008)

The phalanx was a single massive square battalion that could only move as a whole, while the Roman legion's smaller bodies proved far more agile:

> the Macedonian phalanx, which was but a great square battalion, and very heavy throughout, could only move as a whole; whereas the Roman army, divided into small bodies, was more active, and better adapted to all sorts of movements. (PN 005-009)

Rome's treatment of Macedon exemplified its broader policy of divide and conquer. The kingdom was dissolved into four separate republics, its allies were turned against each other, and the senate ensured no power could rise again in the region (PN 006-008, PN 006-014). This methodical dismantling made Macedon a classic case of Roman imperial strategy.
