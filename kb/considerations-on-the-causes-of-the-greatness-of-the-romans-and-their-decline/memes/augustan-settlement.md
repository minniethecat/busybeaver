# Augustan Settlement

The Augustan Settlement of 27 BC was the constitutional reorganization by which Octavian — now granted the title Augustus — transformed the Roman Republic into the Principate. He preserved the outward forms of republican government while concentrating effective military and civil power in a single individual [013-005].

Montesquieu treats this as the pivotal moment when Roman liberty was extinguished — not by violence, but by the subtle manipulation of republican institutions [013-008].

## Background

The settlement emerged from the exhaustion of decades of civil war. After the assassination of Julius Caesar in 44 BC and the subsequent wars of the Second Triumvirate, Octavian emerged as the sole master of the Roman world by 30 BC [011-011].

The Roman people, weary of proscriptions and internecine conflict, were willing to accept any regime that promised stability. Augustus understood this — Montesquieu observes that "the first men of the republic had sought to disgust the people with their own power," preparing the ground for monarchy [013-006].

## Course

### Phase 1: The First Settlement (27 BC)

In 27 BC, Octavian appeared before the Senate and formally returned all his extraordinary powers to the Senate and people of Rome. The Senate, in a carefully choreographed response, refused his resignation and conferred upon him the title "Augustus" and a vast provincial command [013-004].

Montesquieu notes the calculated ambiguity of this arrangement: Augustus placed the turbulent provinces requiring military forces under his own command, while leaving the peaceful, unarmed provinces to the Senate's nominal authority [013-007].

### Phase 2: Consolidation and the Shaping of the Principate (27 BC – 14 AD)

Over the following decades, Augustus progressively accumulated the powers of the various republican magistracies — tribunician power, proconsular authority, the office of Pontifex Maximus — without holding the offices themselves.

The government he organized was "an aristocracy in its relation to civil affairs" but "a monarchy in its relation to the military power" [013-007]. This ambiguous system, Montesquieu argues, could only stand so long as it pleased the monarch — it had no proper force of its own.

## Outcome

The Augustan Settlement permanently altered the Roman constitution. All subsequent emperors would draw their legitimacy from the same combination of powers that Augustus had assembled. The Senate continued to meet, elections continued to be held, and consuls continued to be named — but real power now flowed from the emperor alone [013-006].

Under Augustus, the standing military and naval establishments became permanent [013-014], a transformation that would shape imperial governance for centuries. The empire reached its maximum extent of intercommunication and internal order during the period from Augustus to Marcus Aurelius [013-016].

## Impact on Rome

Montesquieu's judgment on Augustus is severe and carefully distinguished from his judgment on Sulla:

> Augustus was a cunning tyrant; and he conducted them gently to servitude. Whilst under Sylla the republic regained its powers, all the world raised the cry of oppression; under Augustus, men complained no longer of tyranny, but they carefully concealed an abhorrence of servitude beneath the forms of submissive gratitude. [013-008]

Augustus took from the people the power of making laws, and transferred it to the Senate — "but this was only the people's power in appearance, for the Senate was now the creature of the emperor" [014-005]. All the mechanisms of imperial control — the Praetorian Guard, the imperial procurators, the permanent fleets — were established by Augustus [013-012].

The settlement's most enduring consequence was the fiction it established: that Rome remained a republic while being governed as a monarchy. This fiction survived for centuries, shaping the peculiar character of Roman imperial ideology.
