# Gaul

The vast Celtic territory from the Rhine to the Atlantic, encompassing
modern France, Belgium, and adjacent regions west of the Rhine.

Gaul figures twice at pivotal junctures in Montesquieu's analysis: first
as an existential threat that tested Rome's resilience in its early
centuries; later as the theater of Caesar's conquest that fatally
undermined the [[roman-republic|Republic]].

## Geography

Gaul was bounded by the Rhine to the east, the Alps and Pyrenees to the
south, and the Atlantic to the west. The Romans distinguished Cisalpine
Gaul (northern Italy) from Transalpine Gaul (beyond the Alps).

Its terrain ranged from fertile northern plains to the mountainous Massif
Central. Major rivers — the Seine, Loire, Garonne, and Rhone — provided
natural routes for commerce and military movement. (PN 001-014)

## History

### Before Rome

Before Roman contact, Gaul was inhabited by Celtic tribes organized into
loose confederations centered on hill-forts (oppida).

Caesar's Commentaries describe the Gauls as larger and taller than the
Romans, looking with contempt upon their shorter southern neighbors.

The Senonian Gauls crossed the Alps around 400 BC, settling in the Po
Valley and pressing against Etruscan cities. (PN 001-014, PN 004-019)

### Under Rome

Around 390 BC, Senonian Gauls defeated the Romans at the River Allia and
burned Rome itself. Yet Montesquieu notes:

> "The capture of Rome by the Gauls deprived it of none of its strength.
> The army, rather dispersed than vanquished, withdrew almost entire to
> Veii; and the burning of the city was no more than the burning of the
> cabins of a community of shepherds." (PN 001-012)

The immediate cause was a violation of international law. Three Roman
Fabii, sent as ambassadors to mediate between Gauls and Clusians, joined
the Etrurians in battle and killed a Gallic general. When Rome honored
rather than punished them, the enraged Gauls marched on the city.
(PN 001-014)

Centuries of warfare followed. The Romans and Gauls shared what
Montesquieu calls "the same love of glory, the same contempt of death,
the same obstinate resolve to conquer." (PN 004-001) But their arms
differed — the Gallic buckler was small and the long Gallic sword could
only cut, while the Roman short sword, adopted from Spain, could both
cut and thrust. (PN 002-009)

The Romans adapted; the Gauls did not:

> "The trenchant swords of the Gauls, and the elephants of Pyrrhus, only
> surprised them once." (PN 002-008)

Many Romans fleeing the Gauls at the Allia drowned crossing the Tiber,
either unable to swim or weighed down by armor. (PN 002-004)

Caesar's conquest of Transalpine Gaul (58–50 BC) transformed the region
into a Roman province. The wealth and veteran legions he acquired enabled
his crossing of the Rubicon and the overthrow of the Republic.

### Later Period

Roman Gaul prospered under the early Empire. Lugdunum (Lyon), Narbo
(Narbonne), and Lutetia (Paris) became administrative and commercial
centers, linked by Roman roads and gradually Latinized.

By the 3rd century AD, Germanic incursions destabilized the Rhine
frontier. In the 5th century, Frankish tribes crossed the Rhine and
established themselves permanently, giving Gaul its new name: Francia.

## Strategic Significance

Gaul serves a double role in Montesquieu's narrative. The early Gallic
sack demonstrated the Republic's extraordinary resilience: a lesser state
would have collapsed, but Rome treated disaster as a spur to reform.

Centuries later, Caesar's conquest demonstrated the opposite dynamic —
how external military success could corrupt internal institutions by
concentrating power in a single commander with loyal veteran legions.

Baker's note sharpens the contrast between the two peoples. While
Montesquieu attributes "obstinate resolve" to both, the Gallic will
was far more impetuous. (PN 004-016) Machiavelli, quoted by Baker, put it
memorably — Gauls were "more than men at the beginning of a battle, and
afterwards become less than women." (PN 004-017)

The Gauls also exemplified Rome's policy of learning from every enemy.
When the Romans recognized the superiority of the Spanish short sword,
they adopted it immediately; the Gauls clung to their long cutting swords
and were conquered. (PN 002-009) This adaptability was, for Montesquieu,
a chief cause of Roman greatness — and its erosion a chief cause of
decline.

Before the second Punic war, Rome raised immense forces from Italy to
oppose the Gauls — a testament to their enduring threat. (PN 004-006)
The Gauls also served Carthage as mercenaries, counted among the best
troops of the mercenary sort in ancient history. (PN 004-026)
