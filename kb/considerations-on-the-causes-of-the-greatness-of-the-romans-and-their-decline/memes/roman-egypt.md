# Roman Egypt

Egypt, the ancient kingdom of the Nile, was the wealthiest of the successor states to [[alexander-the-great|Alexander]]'s empire. Montesquieu presents it as the supreme case of a state whose natural gifts — situation, fertility, commerce, population, forces on land and sea — ought to have made it formidable, yet whose rulers' vices reduced it to permanent dependence on [[roman-republic|Rome]].

## Geography

Egypt occupies the Nile Valley and Delta in northeastern Africa, commanding the land bridge between Africa and Asia. Its agricultural wealth rested on the annual Nile flood, which made it the breadbasket of the Mediterranean world. The capital, Alexandria, founded by Alexander in 331 BC, became the commercial and intellectual hub of the Hellenistic East.

## History

### Before Rome

Under the Ptolemaic dynasty (305–30 BC), Egypt carried on "nearly all the commerce of the world," and the founding of Alexandria dealt a lasting blow to Carthaginian trade. (PN=004-008)

The Greek kings ruled over two classes of subjects — the conquering Greeks and the conquered Egyptians — a division that proved a persistent source of instability. (PN=005-012)

### Under Rome

Egypt was the last of the major Hellenistic kingdoms to fall under Roman domination. After Octavian (later [[augustus|Augustus]]) defeated Mark Antony and Cleopatra at Actium in 31 BC, Egypt was annexed in 30 BC as a personal province of the emperor. (PN=013-003)

Montesquieu traces the roots of this annexation far earlier. Egypt was one of "but four powers capable of resisting" Rome after the abasement of Carthage, alongside the republics of Greece and the kingdoms of Macedon and Syria. (PN=005-004)

Yet "the cruelty, the cowardice, the avarice and imbecility of its kings, joined to their frightful sensuality, rendered them so odious to their subjects that, for the most part, they were only able to maintain themselves through the protection of the Romans." (PN=005-015)

The Ptolemaic succession system was itself a structural poison. "It was in some sort a fundamental law of the crown of Egypt that sisters succeeded with brothers; and, in order to maintain the unity of the government, the brother married the sister."

Every domestic quarrel became a public disorder, and the people of Alexandria — "an immense populace, always ready to join itself to the first of its rulers who appealed to it" — were a standing threat to stability. (PN=005-016)

> "The sovereigns of Egypt were seated upon unstable thrones; and, being badly established within their own dominions, they were without power abroad." (PN=005-016)

Through the mechanism of testamentary inheritance, Rome acquired its claim: "Egypt was enthralled by the will of the king of Cyrene." (PN=006-006) Rome had already demonstrated its power over Egypt when, "with a word, they drove Antiochus from Egypt," showing that even the strongest Hellenistic kingdom was no match for Roman authority. (PN=006-007)

Under Roman rule, Egypt became an imperial province governed by a prefect appointed directly by the emperor. Its grain shipments — the annona — fed the Roman populace, and its revenues sustained the imperial administration. Senators were forbidden to enter Egypt without imperial permission, so vital was its control to the emperor's power.

### Later Period

Egypt remained a Roman and then Byzantine province until the Arab conquest in the 7th century AD. The grain fleets that once sailed for Rome were redirected to Constantinople after the empire's division, and the old Ptolemaic capital of Alexandria declined in political importance while remaining a center of commerce and theological controversy.

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu's analysis of Egypt serves two purposes in his larger argument. First, Egypt illustrates his thesis that natural advantages mean nothing without sound government. The kingdom possessed "its situation, its fertility, and its commerce… the number of its inhabitants and the strength of its forces on land and sea" — every element of greatness — yet remained permanently feeble. (PN=005-015)

Second, Egypt exemplifies Rome's method of indirect rule. Egypt was not conquered in a single campaign but gradually reduced through long alliance, becoming a subject without anyone being able to "fix the epoch of its subjugation." (PN=006-015) The Romans' practice of maintaining dependent kings until it suited them to absorb the territory directly was perfected in Egypt.

Third, Montesquieu notes a deeper pattern. The kings of Syria, "always looking with extreme envy upon the felicity of the kingdom of Egypt," exhausted themselves in attempts to conquer it — neglecting their own eastern provinces and losing them to revolt. (PN=005-011)

This mutual destruction among the successor kingdoms cleared the field for Rome. As he observes: "nature has prescribed certain boundaries to states. Whenever the Romans transgressed these, they almost invariably perished at the hands of the Parthians." Egypt, for all its weakness, was within Rome's natural sphere. (PN=005-012)
