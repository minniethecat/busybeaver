# Pompey the Great

Pompey (106–48 BC) was the Roman general whom Montesquieu sets, alongside [[julius-caesar]], at the foreground of the Republic's final age. He "appeared first; Caesar followed him closely," the two men effacing all rivals "by their credit, their exploits, and their virtues" (011-004).

For Montesquieu he is the figure through whom the extraordinary command corrupted the constitution: a general so admired that the [[roman-senate-body]] and people surrendered all affairs into his single hand, until his ruin in the [[roman-civil-war]] completed the fall of [[roman-republic|the Republic]] (011-005).

## Life

### Early Life

Montesquieu gives little of Pompey's origins, treating him as the heir of [[lucius-sulla|Sylla]]'s revolution rather than as a youth. The translator notes only the bare chronology: "Pompey was about six years the senior of Caesar; the one was born B. c. 106, the other b. c. 100" (011-004).

He rose in a state already disfigured by the wars of Marius and Sylla, where proscription had made neutrality fatal. "Those who were neutral, and who adhered to the party of liberty, were sure to be proscribed" — so "it was therefore the part of prudence to join the one or the other" (011-003).

He entered history through Sylla's faction. Montesquieu later ranks him with the aristocratic side: "the aristocratic faction, under the lead of Pompey, had very generally lost the old patrician virtues" (011-030).

### Career

Pompey's first act in Montesquieu's account is corrosive: to win the crowd, he dismantled the constitutional safeguards Sylla had restored. "Pompey procured the abrogation of those laws of Sylla which limited the power of the people; and when he had thus sacrificed to his ambition the most salutary laws of his country, he obtained all that he desired" (011-004).

The people then heaped upon him a series of extraordinary commands that hollowed out every ordinary magistracy. Montesquieu presents this as a refrain of dependence:

> Was it necessary to make war against Sertorius? the commission was given to Pompey. Against Mithridates? all cried out for Pompey. Was Rome needing a supply of grain from abroad? The people regarded themselves as ruined if Pompey had not charge of the business. Was it resolved to destroy the pirates? Pompey only was capable of doing it. (011-005)

These commands made him master of [[asia-minor|Asia]] and the East. He finished the war against Mithridates that Lucullus had begun — "Pompey succeeded Lucullus, and Mithridates was overwhelmed" (007-004) — and the wealth of [[roman-syria|the East]] flowed to Rome through him: the treasury was "indebted to the still greater spoils of Asia, brought in by Pompey" (018-014).

Twice victorious, he displayed a restraint Montesquieu admires:

> On two occasions Pompey returned to Eome master of the destinies of the republic; but he had the moderation to disband his armies before entering the capital, and to present himself therein in the character of a simple citizen. (011-006)

Yet his ambition was distinguished from Caesar's only in method, not in aim. "He aspired to the dictatorship, but he wished to obtain it by the suffrages of the people. He would not consent to the usurpation of power, but he desired that it might be placed in his hands" (011-006). His [[roman-triumph|triumphs]] "had commemorated his achievements in Europe, Asia, and Africa" (011-026).

### Later Years & Death

As popular favor cooled, Pompey turned to ruinous expedients — bribery, hired mobs, and the fatal alliance with [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] and Crassus. "He corrupted the people by the use of money, and put into the elections a price upon the suffrage of each citizen" (011-007). [[cato-the-elder|Cato]] judged "it was not the enmity of these men that ruined the republic, but their union" (011-007).

The union destroyed him. "Properly speaking, Pompey did not lend his credit to Caesar, but, without knowing it, he sacrificed it to him" (011-007). When Caesar crossed the Rubicon, Pompey's nerve broke:

> Pompey was dismayed; and, in the first moments of the war, he saw no course to take except that which remains in connection with desperate affairs. He could only succumb and fly. (011-010)

In Greece his strategy was sound — protract the war, starve Caesar, avoid battle — and "so long as Pompey was faithful to his own theory of the contest, the scale inclined in his favor" (011-023). But, weakened by "the weakness of desiring to be approved," he yielded to the taunts of his camp and gave battle (011-010).

The result was Pharsalia and flight. Montesquieu marks the contrast: "The contrast of so much greatness with his wretched flight from Pharsalia, and his miserable death by the hand of an assassin on the coast of Africa, is one of the sharpest which history presents" (011-026). His remnant under Scipio, ignoring Cato's advice, "risked all and lost all" (011-011).

## Significance

Pompey is Montesquieu's case study in how a free state destroys itself by loving one man too well. The Republic had wisely "divided the public power among a great number of magistracies, which mutually supported, checked, and tempered each other" (011-004); the extraordinary commands "annihilated the authority of the magistrates and of the people themselves" (011-005).

Montesquieu draws a general law from his greatness. He warns that excessive preference for one citizen always tends to "either excite the envy of the people, or increase beyond measure their love for the popular favorite" (011-006), corroding the [[imperium|balance of powers]] that had upheld [[roman-liberty|liberty]].

He refuses to make Pompey the cause of the catastrophe. "If Csesar and Pompey had thought as Cato, others would have thought as Csesar and Pompey; and the republic, ready to perish, would have been drawn to the precipice by another hand" (011-013).

The decline came from general causes — the corruption of [[roman-senate-body|aristocracy]], populace, and [[roman-army|army]] together (011-030) — for which Pompey was instrument, not author.

The translator adds that his fate, like Hannibal's and Napoleon's, "was to touch the extremes of fortune; but, unlike them, his fame has not proved to be wholly superior to the reverses by which his career was broken and ended" (011-027).

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu and his translator weigh how far Pompey's defeat condemns him as a soldier. Several judgments are set side by side:

- **Caesar's verdict** — Before sailing for Spain, Caesar said "he was going against an army without a general, and that he should thence return against a general without an army," indicting Pompey's raw [[roman-spain|levies]] rather than the man (011-026).

- **Cicero's reproach** — Cicero "justly accuses him" of being slow, a charge Montesquieu also turns against Caesar's delays after Pharsalia (011-013).

- **The pull of his camp** — Dr. Middleton notes Pompey "had a very difficult part to act," surrounded by senators "perpetually teasing Pompey to the resolution of a battle" until "driven by a kind of shame, and against his judgment" he fought (011-025).

- **The translator's balance** — He concludes Pompey was likely "influenced by the last three causes combined," so that it is "difficult to say with precision to what extent his military conduct was culpable or excusable" (011-024).

The translator's final estimate rescues his reputation from the verdict of mere outcome:

> This able man had nearly doubled both the domain and the revenues of the republic, and the triumphs with which he had been honored had commemorated his achievements in Europe, Asia, and Africa. (011-026)

He adds that "the easy and vulgar method of judging by the event has too much obscured the just fame of Pompey," and that but for the chances that diverted him from his masterly plan, he, "and not Csesar, would have acquired the repute of being the first military man of Kome" (011-026).
