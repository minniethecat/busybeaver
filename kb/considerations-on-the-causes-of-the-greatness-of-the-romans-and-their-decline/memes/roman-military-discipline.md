# Roman Military Discipline

Roman military discipline (*disciplina militaris*) was the "very nerve" of Rome's martial power — a system so effective that Montesquieu treats it not merely as a military technique but as an expression of the same civic virtue that sustained the Republic's political institutions.

## Definition

Military discipline encompassed far more than obedience to orders. It was a comprehensive system of training, organization, and ethos that transformed citizens into soldiers and soldiers into an instrument of policy. "The accounts which we have of the Roman military discipline fully justify the opinion" that it was the foundation of Roman conquest (002-010).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

"At all times the very nerve of their military discipline" was the citizen-soldier who fought for his own country and laws (001-007). The Republic's military system was distinctive: "they had no standing army, but they raised armies when required" — each citizen knew his place in the legion and his duty in battle.

### Imperial Period

Under the empire, discipline decayed. A single commander "had ruined all military discipline" by "accustoming his army to the pleasures of the East" (011-002). Attempts to restore it — "to think of re-establishing the military discipline" — were "an enterprise which always appeared difficult" because the moral foundation on which it rested had dissolved (016-011).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …the identity of military discipline with the constitution of the state (023-025)

Montesquieu argues that military discipline was not a separate domain from political life but an extension of it. The same virtues — obedience to law, subordination of private interest, courage in adversity — operated in both spheres. When civic virtue decayed, military discipline inevitably followed.

"The Romans finally lost their military discipline, and even abandoned the use of their own arms" (018-005). By the late empire, Roman armies were composed largely of barbarian mercenaries who had no stake in Roman liberty — a military manifestation of the deeper political corruption.

## Examples

- **Early Republican discipline**: The "practice to strengthen their military discipline" through constant drill and rigorous punishment (002-005)
- **The corruption of the Eastern campaigns**: Lucullus and others "accustomed his army to the pleasures of the East," undermining the austerity that discipline required (011-002)
- **The abandonment of Roman arms**: "Even abandoned the use of their own arms" — the ultimate symbol of military decay (018-005)
