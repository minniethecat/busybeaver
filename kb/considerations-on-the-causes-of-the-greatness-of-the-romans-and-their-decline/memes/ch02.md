The truth is, that physical strength being the principal quality of the soldier, the object was to degrade rather than enfeeble him. Men so hardened were generally healthy. Among the Eomans, desertions were more rare. The accounts wMcli we have of the Eoman military discipline fully justify the opinion that it gauges the high-water-marli of all human attainment in this particular direction. The extent to which all the active and passive powers of the Eoman soldier were utilized has never been equalled upon a great scale. This supremacy of the Eomans in the military art conducts us at once to the most accurate idea of their position in universal history. At a time when the spirit of war was dominant among mankind, and when, consequently, the warlike virtues were adjudged to be the highest, the Eomans had the merit of beating the world according to its own standard of excellence. The amount of real manhood involved ia such a performance contains the secret of that peculiar enthusiasm which is yet awakened by the Eoman name. The physical type of the Eoman harmonized well with his intense devotion to war. The typical Eoman was short in stature and heavily built ; his head was low and broad; his jaws horseshoe-shaped, and his

At a time in the world's history when war was the leading interest among men, it is easy to see how admirably he was adapted to empire. He was the best embodiment of the military spirit. As suggested in the foregoing remarks, the extreme military character of the Eomans was but a marked instance, upon a great scale, of that ascendency of the profession of arms over the industrial callings, which was a prevailing characteristic of the ancient societies generally. Upon a much smaller scale, the Spartans illustrate the extreme intensity of the same spirit among the Greeks. Comte has done excellent service to rational history by indicating the necessary causes which rendered the civilizations of antiquity much more military in their tone than those of modem times. Emerging from an extremely rude social condition, during which for many successive ages they had been incessantly occupied with the interests of war, when the communities of the ancient world first come into historical view, their life is seen to be — as we should rationally expect to find it — very distinctly military. "Under this aspect," says Comte, " it is constantly necessary to regard the social state of antiquity as radically the inverse of our modem social state, in which war has finally become purely accessory, while among the ancients it was necessarily and habitually preponderant in a high degree. Thus, in ancient
