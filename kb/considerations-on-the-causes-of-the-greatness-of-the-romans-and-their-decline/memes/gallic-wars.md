# Gallic Wars

The Gallic Wars (58–50 BC) were Julius Caesar's military campaigns that resulted in the conquest of Gaul and the extension of Roman control to the Atlantic. According to Caesar's own account in his Commentaries, the Gauls "were larger and taller than the Romans" and looked with contempt upon the short stature of the latter — yet Roman discipline and political cohesion proved decisive [004-019].

Montesquieu treats the Gallic Wars not primarily as a military achievement but as a political watershed. The campaign was launched "without any deliberation of the senate or decree of the people" [013-006].

The army Caesar forged in Gaul became the instrument with which he overthrew the Republic. The conquest thus illustrates Montesquieu's central thesis — that the expansion of Roman power abroad destroyed the very institutions that had made that expansion possible.

## Background

By the 60s BC, the Roman Republic had entered what Montesquieu describes as "a species of anarchy" [013-005]. Pompey, Crassus, and Caesar — the First Triumvirate — had "established an impunity for every variety of public crimes" and "introduced the custom of corrupting the people with money" [013-005].

It was in this environment that Caesar secured the governorship of Gaul. Montesquieu emphasizes the constitutional irregularity: Pompey "gave" Caesar the Gauls as if provinces were personal property [013-006]. No senatorial deliberation approved Caesar's mandate; the traditional checks had dissolved.

The Gauls themselves were a familiar adversary. The Romans had fought wars against Gallic peoples since the early Republic, including the catastrophic sack of Rome in 390 BC. Caesar's Commentaries note that the Gallic sword was limited to a cutting stroke [022-003], while the Roman short sword — adopted after earlier encounters with the Gauls — could both cut and thrust [002-008].

## Course

### Phase 1: Defeat of the Helvetii and Ariovistus (58 BC)

Caesar's campaign began with the migration of the Helvetii, a Gallic tribe attempting to cross Roman territory. Without senatorial authorization and acting on his own initiative, Caesar intercepted and defeated them.

He then turned against Ariovistus, a Germanic chieftain who had crossed the Rhine into eastern Gaul. By presenting himself as the defender of Gallic peoples, Caesar framed his conquest as a protective measure — the familiar Roman tactic of justifying aggression as defense.

### Phase 2: Conquest of the Gallic Interior (57–53 BC)

Over the next five years, Caesar systematically subdued the tribes of central and northern Gaul. His Commentaries describe the Gauls as formidable warriors who "at the beginning of a battle, are more than men" but "at the end are less than women" [004-017].

Caesar also bridged the Rhine and campaigned in Germany, and crossed the Channel to Britain — expeditions that served less to conquer new territory than to demonstrate that no geographic barrier could contain Roman power.

### Phase 3: The Great Revolt and Alesia (52 BC)

The final phase of the war centered on the revolt led by Vercingetorix, who united many Gallic tribes in a coordinated uprising. Caesar besieged the rebel stronghold at Alesia and simultaneously repelled a massive relief army, forcing Vercingetorix to surrender.

By 50 BC, Gaul was effectively pacified. The conquest had been achieved with an army that was personally loyal to Caesar rather than to the Republic — a distinction that would prove fatal to Roman liberty.

## Outcome

The immediate result was the addition of a vast new province to the Roman sphere. The Gallic peoples, who had once been Rome's most feared adversaries, were now incorporated — however incompletely — into the Roman system.

But Montesquieu's analysis focuses on the political, not territorial, consequences. The Gallic Wars made Caesar fabulously wealthy and gave him a battle-hardened army whose loyalty was personal. When the Senate, recognizing the threat, ordered Caesar to disband his legions, he instead crossed the Rubicon and marched on Rome.

The conquest of Gaul thus produced the conditions for the civil war that ended the Republic. As the historian Florus observed, even before the Gallic invasion of 225 BC, the Romans and their allies could muster some three hundred thousand men [004-006]. But a state's military capacity, Montesquieu insists, is worth nothing if its political institutions cannot control the men who command it.

## Impact on Rome

Montesquieu identifies the Gallic Wars as a critical juncture in the transition from Republic to Empire. The mechanism was not simply Caesar's ambition — it was structural. When a republic sends its generals on distant campaigns, those generals acquire resources and loyalties independent of the institutions that appointed them.

Montesquieu explicitly connects the Gallic campaign to the broader pattern of provincial exploitation. After the conquest, Julian — sent into Gaul by Constantine three centuries later — "found that in the whole country there were but few mines" [017-008], a testament to how thoroughly the Romans had extracted Gallic resources.

The Gallic traits that Caesar described — the physical courage but strategic impetuosity — survived in the French character. Montesquieu himself, writing eighteen centuries later, recognized "the similarity of Gallic and French traits" [023-033] and noted "the large proportion in which the Gallic element survives in the French people today" [023-034].
