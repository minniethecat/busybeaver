# Roman Legion

The basic military unit of the Roman army (~5,000 men) — the tactical and organizational innovation that gave Rome its decisive advantage over the phalanx-based armies of the Hellenistic world, and whose discipline Montesquieu treats as an expression of civic virtue.

## Structure

The legion was the fundamental tactical unit of the Roman army, composed of heavily armed infantry supported by cavalry auxiliaries. Its flexible manipular formation — organized into cohorts and centuries — allowed it to maneuver on broken terrain in ways the rigid Macedonian phalanx could not match.

This structural superiority is a recurring theme in Montesquieu's analysis of Roman military success. (002-001)

## History

### Origin

The legion emerged during the early Republic as Rome adapted and improved upon the Greek and Etruscan military models. Its development was driven by Rome's nearly continuous warfare: first against neighboring Latin cities, then against the Samnites, and eventually against the great powers of the Mediterranean. (001-021)

### Evolution

Under the Republic, the legion was a citizen militia — soldiers served seasonally and returned to their farms.

The custom was to divide the legions between the two consuls, though sometimes each consul commanded the whole army on alternate days. This arrangement was one that Livy criticized and Machiavelli later concluded was inferior to unified command. (001-021)

As Rome's wars grew longer and farther from Italy, the legion evolved into a professional standing force, especially after the Marian reforms of the late Republic.

## Role in Roman History

The legion was the instrument of Rome's conquest of the Mediterranean world. Its discipline and tactical adaptability allowed Rome to defeat enemies with vastly different fighting styles.

The legions were trained to leap into action at the least signal, combining individual initiative with collective coordination. (002-005) (002-008)

However, in the late Republic, legions increasingly owed loyalty to their generals rather than the state — a shift Montesquieu identifies as a key factor in the collapse of the Republic.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> Montesquieu devotes Chapter 2 entirely to the Roman art of war, arguing that military discipline was not merely a technical matter but a moral one.

> "They regarded it as requisite that the soldiers should be better disciplined than the generals." (002-001)

> The legion's decline from a citizen militia into a tool of factional commanders mirrored the Republic's broader decline from civic virtue into corruption.
