# Roman Army

The Roman army was the instrument through which [[ancient-rome|Rome]] subdued the peoples of Italy and then the Mediterranean world, and for Montesquieu it is the central proof of his thesis that greatness springs from discipline and labor rather than from fortune.

Consecrating themselves to war "and regarding it as tlie sole art, the" Romans "devoted their whole mind and soul to its perfection" (002-001).

Under the [[roman-republic|Republic]] every soldier was equally a citizen, drawn from men who owned enough property "to cause them to feel an interest in the preservation of the city" (009-001) — a civic foundation that bound the army to the state.

Montesquieu treats the army's history as inseparable from Rome's: the same hardening that overcame [[hannibal|Hannibal]] in the [[punic-wars|Punic Wars]] and the same legions that crossed the Rubicon under [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] were, by the late Empire, recast on principles "exactly the reverse of those which had rendered them masters of the world" (018-004).

The institution thus frames the whole arc from grandeur to decadence.

## Structure

The army's core was the legion, an idea Montesquieu, citing Vegetius, says "was without doubt a god ... who inspired them with" it (002-001).

The legionary carried arms "heavier and stronger than those of any other people whatever," and because a heavy corps could not do everything, the legion was supplied with a light troop able to "sally forth to engage in combat, and, in case of necessity, fall back under cover of its ranks" (002-001).

It was further equipped with cavalry, archers, and slingers, defended by "all sorts of military machines," and required to entrench itself at every encampment so as to be "a species of stronghold" (002-001, 002-002).

Rome's own cavalry was weak — it "only constituted the eleventh part of the legion, and very often less" (018-005) — so the Romans borrowed freely from others (002-008). Where another nation held any advantage, "they at once availed themselves of the same," providing themselves with "Numidian horses, Cretan archers, Balearic slingmen, and Khodian ships" (002-009).

The standing structure rested on the fortified camp, square and uniform, surrounded by ditch and rampart, raised anew "even in the longest marches" (002-002).

## Discipline and Training

For Montesquieu the army's superiority was manufactured, not innate: to wield arms heavier than other men's, "it was necessary that they should render themselves more than men. This they accomplished by incessant labor — which increased their strength" (002-002).

The regimen was relentless — the military step of twenty miles in five hours under sixty-pound loads, running and leaping fully armed, exercises with weapons "which were double the ordinary weight" (002-003) — and the Campus Martius served as a school where citizens trained and then swam the Tiber (002-004).

> Whenever the Eomans regarded themselves as in danger, or when they desired to repair some disaster, it was their constant practice to strengthen their military discipline. (002-005)

This reflex produced a catalogue of disciplinary acts that Montesquieu treats as causes of recovery:

- **Manlius** directed the execution of his own son, who had vanquished an enemy without orders (002-005)

- **Scipio Æmilianus**, after the check at Numantia, "immediately deprives them of everything which had served to enervate them" (002-005)

- **Metellus** repaired the disgrace of the yoke in Numidia by compelling the troops "to resume the observance of the ancient institutions" (002-005)

- **Marius** turned the courses of rivers, and **Sylla** imposed labors so grievous that his men "demanded battle as an end of their toils" (002-005)

- **Publius Nasica** had soldiers build a fleet he did not need — for "Idleness was more feared than the enemy" (002-006)

Because each Roman was "more robust and more inured to war than his enemy," he "counted always upon himself"; their troops "being always the better disciplined," it was likely that even in unfortunate combats they would rally while the enemy somewhere broke (002-007). War, as Montesquieu quotes Josephus, "was for tbem a study, and peace an exercise" (002-009).

## Historical Evolution

### Origin

The earliest army grew directly out of Rome's poverty.

"Eome being a city witliout commerce, and almost without arts, pillage was the only means which private individuals had of enriching themselves," so plunder itself "was therefore regulated by discipline": booty was "first deposited in common, and then it was divided among the soldiers," each man sworn to appropriate nothing (001-007).

In the beginning "the soldiers received no pay," so victories "left them in all their poverty" without corrupting them (001-009). The introduction of pay marks a turning point: only once "the senate having found the means of giving pay to the soldiers" was the ten-year siege of Veii undertaken, after which "a new art" and "another method of conducting war" appeared (001-011).

### Development

Conquest beyond Italy dissolved the citizen-soldier bond. When "the arms of the republic were carried beyond the Alps and the sea, the soldiers, who had to be left during many campaigns in the countries which were subdued, gradually lost the spirit of Roman citizens" (009-002).

The decisive structural break came when [[gaius-marius|Marius]], preparing for war against Jugurtha, "enrolled all sorts of men indifferently" (009-001); having enrolled "men who were without property," Caesar "was compelled to increase the pay" (016-009).

Soldiers thereafter "ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey, and of" Caesar (009-002) — the seed of the [[roman-civil-war|civil wars]].

Under the Empire the rot reached the institution itself: the troops "finally lost their military discipline, and even abandoned the use of their own proper arms," obtaining leave under Gratian to drop first the cuirass and then the helmet (018-005), while emperors increasingly hired barbarian auxiliaries (018-003).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu makes the army the hinge of his entire causal argument: Rome rose and fell by the maxims governing its soldiers. An empire "which is founded by arms needs to be sustained by arms" (018-007), and the army's virtues were not merely martial — it was also "by their prudence, their wisdom, their constancy, their love of glory, and their patriotism" that Rome won supremacy (018-006).

> Thus, they established usages which were exactly the reverse of those which had rendered them masters of the world; and, as it was formerly their constant policy to reserve the military art to Eomans, and to deprive all their neighbors of the advantages of that art, they now abandoned it among themselves and established it among others. (018-004)

The contrast with [[carthage|Carthage]] sharpens the lesson: Carthage relied on mercenary troops, the Romans on their own citizens, and "gold and silver may be consumed; but virtue, constancy, force, and poverty, are inexhaustible" (004-004).

When discipline gave way to luxury and the legions to hireling barbarians, the army that had embodied [[civic-virtue|civic virtue]] became, in Montesquieu's account, the measure of Rome's whole moral economy — labor and discipline building greatness, idleness and corruption marking decline.
