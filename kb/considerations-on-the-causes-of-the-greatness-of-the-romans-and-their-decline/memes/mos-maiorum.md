# Mos Maiorum

*Mos maiorum* — "the custom of the ancestors" — was the unwritten normative order of Roman public life. It was neither law nor mere habit but a binding standard of conduct enforced by social sanction, and its authority was, in many respects, greater than that of written statutes.

## Definition

The *mos maiorum* encompassed the accumulated precedents, rituals, and expectations that defined proper Roman conduct. It governed everything from the sequence of magistracies to the conduct of Senate debates, from religious observances to family authority. When the Romans spoke of "the customs of their ancestors," they invoked the ultimate standard of legitimacy.

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

The *mos maiorum* was the invisible constitution of the Republic. It constrained magistrates more effectively than law could, because violation carried not merely a legal penalty but social and religious disgrace. "Their manners coarse" was not a defect but a feature — the roughness of early Roman custom enforced a discipline that refinement would later erode (001-023).

### Imperial Period

The decay of the *mos maiorum* preceded and enabled the decay of republican institutions. When ancestral custom lost its binding force, no written constitution could substitute for it. The emperors could enact laws but could not legislate the respect for tradition that had sustained the Republic.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu treats custom as inseparable from law and virtue. The "rigidly observed" character of early Roman life was a product of *mos maiorum* as much as of statute. The decline of Rome was, in significant part, a story of customs dissolving faster than laws could be enacted to replace them — and laws, without the support of custom, were mere words.

## Examples

- **Cursus honorum sequence**: The expected order of magistracies, enforced not by law but by custom until Sulla's legislation
- **Senatorial precedence**: The custom by which former consuls spoke first in Senate debates, shaping deliberation through experience
- **Funeral masks (*imagines*)**: The custom of displaying ancestral portraits at funerals, connecting living politicians to the standards of their forebears
