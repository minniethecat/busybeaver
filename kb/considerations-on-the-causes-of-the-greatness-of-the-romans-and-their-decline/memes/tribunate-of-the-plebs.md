# Tribunate of the Plebs

The office created after the First Secession (494 BC) to protect plebeians from patrician abuse — the tribunes' power of veto and their personal inviolability made them the guardians of popular liberty in Montesquieu's analysis of the mixed constitution.

## Structure

Ten tribunes were elected annually by the plebeian assembly (*concilium plebis*). Their persons were declared sacrosanct (*sacrosanctitas*) — inviolable under religious law.

This gave them the physical security to oppose consuls and senators without fear of retaliation. Their principal power was the *intercessio* (veto), by which a single tribune could block legislation, decrees, or official actions. (001-013)

## History

### Origin

The tribunate was created in 494 BC after the First Secession of the Plebs, when the plebeians withdrew from Rome en masse and refused to serve in the army until their grievances were addressed. The office was designed as a counterweight to patrician dominance of the consulship and Senate. (001-023)

### Evolution

During the middle Republic, the tribunate functioned as intended — a check on aristocratic power.

But by the late Republic, tribunes like the Gracchi brothers used the office to push radical reforms, while others became tools of ambitious generals.

The tribune Philippus, for instance, proposed legislation in the late second century BC that would have redistributed public land — a measure later abandoned. (003-012)

Under the Empire, the tribunician power (*tribunicia potestas*) was absorbed by the emperor, becoming one of the legal foundations of imperial authority.

## Role in Roman History

The tribunate was the institutional embodiment of the Conflict of the Orders — the centuries-long struggle between patricians and plebeians.

In Montesquieu's view, this struggle was the creative tension that produced the Republic's greatest achievements. By giving the common people a formal voice in government, the tribunate prevented the violent class rupture that destabilized other ancient republics.

Yet the same office became a weapon of faction in the Republic's final century.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> Montesquieu treats the tribunate as an essential component of Rome's mixed constitution — alongside the consuls (monarchical element), the Senate (aristocratic), and the popular assemblies (democratic).

> The tribunes' veto power served as the popular check on aristocratic ambition.

> When this balance broke down, the tribunes became, in Montesquieu's telling, instruments of the corruption that destroyed the Republic: the flattery of kings replaced the independence of the tribune. (005-005)
