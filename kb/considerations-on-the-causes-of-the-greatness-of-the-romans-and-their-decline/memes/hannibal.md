# Hannibal

Carthaginian general (247–183/181 BC), son of Hamilcar Barca, who led the Carthaginian forces in the Second Punic War (218–201 BC) and came closer than any other enemy to destroying the Roman Republic.

Montesquieu presents Hannibal as the supreme external threat against whom Rome's institutions proved their worth — "the finest spectacle that antiquity presents" (004-012).

His career illustrates the central thesis of the *Considerations*: that Rome's greatness lay not in its good fortune but in its institutional capacity to endure catastrophe.

## Life

### Early Life

Hannibal was born in 247 BC into the Barca family, one of the leading houses of [[carthage|Carthage]].

His father, Hamilcar Barca, commanded Carthaginian forces in Sicily during the closing years of the First Punic War and later expanded Carthaginian power in Spain.

According to tradition, the young Hannibal swore an oath of eternal enmity toward Rome at his father's altar, a pledge that would shape the course of Mediterranean history.

After Hamilcar's death, Hannibal served under his brother-in-law Hasdrubal, then assumed command of the Carthaginian forces in Spain at the age of twenty-six.

### Career

Hannibal's Italian campaign began in 218 BC with his famous crossing of the Alps — the first of "the crowd of obstacles which confronted Hannibal, and which that extraordinary man surmounted" (004-012).

His operational genius was immediately evident.

Within approximately two years of descending from the Alps, he won four major battles "which conducted Hannibal to the summit of his power" (004-019): Ticinus (218 BC), Trebia (218 BC), Trasymenus (217 BC), and Cannae (216 BC).

These victories, however, "were very unequal" (004-020) — Ticinus was a minor engagement, Trebia and Trasymenus were destructive defeats for Rome, and Cannae was an appalling disaster in which the Roman army was virtually annihilated.

Montesquieu identifies Hannibal's cavalry superiority as the decisive tactical factor in his victories: "in the second [Punic War], Hannibal owed his principal victories to his Numidian horsemen" (004-009).

The Romans, whose cavalry was badly armed and mounted on inferior Italian horses, could not match the speed and striking power of the Numidian and Spanish cavalry.

This advantage was only neutralized when [[scipio-africanus|Scipio Africanus]], having conquered Spain and formed an alliance with the Numidian king Masinissa, "deprived the Carthaginians of this advantage" (004-009).

The presence of Hannibal in Italy produced a remarkable effect on Roman domestic politics.

As Montesquieu observes, "the presence of Hannibal caused all divisions to cease among the Romans" (004-004), whereas in Carthage factional strife only intensified under the pressure of war.

This contrast between a republic united by external danger and one divided by internal suspicion is central to Montesquieu's analysis of Roman institutional strength.

Rome responded to the threat with an extraordinary mobilization.

At the height of the crisis, "Rome had constantly on foot from twenty-two to twenty-four legions" (004-006) — a prodigious force for a city-state whose citizen body numbered only about 137,000.

The senate's response to catastrophe revealed the character of Roman institutions: after Cannae, "not even the women were permitted to shed tears" (004-013).

The senate refused to ransom prisoners taken at Cannae and dispatched the survivors to continue the war in Sicily "without recompense and without military honor — until Hannibal should be driven from Italy" (004-013).

### Later Years and Death

Hannibal remained in Italy for thirteen years after Cannae, but his fortunes steadily declined.

Montesquieu identifies the structural cause: Hannibal "had not been sent into Italy by the magistrates of Carthage; and, whether from the jealousy of the one party, or from the too great confidence of the other, he received very little support from home" (004-015).

His forces, sufficient to win battles when concentrated, proved "too small" when dispersed to garrison cities and defend allies (004-015).

Meanwhile, Rome adapted: "a circumstance which contributed much to put the Romans in peril during the second Punic war was the fact that Hannibal straightway armed his soldiers in the Roman manner" (005-009), but the Romans in turn learned from their adversary.

Recalled to Africa in 203 BC to defend Carthage against Scipio's invasion, Hannibal faced his rival at the [[battle-of-zama|Battle of Zama]] (202 BC).

According to Montesquieu, "all that a great statesman and a great general could do, Hannibal did to save his country" (005-002), but "Fortune seemed to take pleasure in confounding his ability, his experience, and his good sense" (005-002).

Carthage was defeated and forced to accept humiliating peace terms.

After the war, Hannibal turned to political reform in Carthage, but was forced into exile by Roman pressure around 195 BC.

He found refuge at the court of [[antiochus-iii|Antiochus III]] of Syria, where "Hannibal advised that the war should be renewed in Italy, and that Philip of Macedon should be gained or rendered neutral" (005-013) — strategic counsel that Antiochus fatally ignored.

Hannibal died by his own hand around 183/181 BC in Bithynia, rather than fall into Roman hands.

## Significance

In Montesquieu's framework, Hannibal plays a paradoxical role. He was the external adversary who nearly destroyed Rome, yet his very threat proved to be a source of Roman strength:

- **Unity through danger**: Hannibal's presence in Italy unified Rome politically, suspending the factional conflicts that were endemic to the [[roman-republic|republic]] (004-004).

- **Institutional resilience**: Rome's ability to withstand Cannae — a catastrophe that would have caused any other state to surrender — demonstrated that the city "was saved by the force of her institutions" (004-013).

- **The education of Roman arms**: The experience of fighting Hannibal's tactical genius forced the Romans to improve their military methods. "The Romans then had discovered or very early learned the art of dividing armies into many battalions and squadrons" (005-008n), developing the tactical flexibility that would conquer the Mediterranean.

- **Carthaginian weakness exposed**: Hannibal's failure exposed the structural defects of Carthage — its reliance on mercenary troops, its faction-ridden politics, and its inability to sustain a long war. "The establishment of Carthage in her territory was less solid than that of Rome in hers" (004-007).

- **The paradox of conquest**: Montesquieu suggests that if Rome had conquered too easily too early, it would have been corrupted by wealth "at the arrival of Pyrrhus, of the Gauls, and of Hannibal" (001-009). The very difficulty of subduing Italy preserved Roman virtue.

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu engages critically with both Livy's rhetorical presentation of Hannibal and with the long-standing debate over Hannibal's strategic decisions:

> "I imagine that Hannibal indulged in very few fine sayings, and fewer still in favor of Fabius and Marcellus and against himself. I regret to see Livy scatter his flowers of rhetoric over these colossal figures of antiquity, and would prefer that he had followed the example of Homer, who, whilst neglecting the decoration of his heroes, knows so well how to make them act." (005-001)

This methodological remark — Montesquieu preferring Homeric narrative to Livian rhetoric — reflects his broader approach to historical causation, which seeks structural explanations rather than moral anecdotes.

On the debated question of whether Hannibal should have marched on Rome after Cannae, Montesquieu takes a skeptical view of the conventional criticism:

> "It is believed that Hannibal committed a signal error in not having laid siege to Rome after the battle of Cannae. It is true that at first the terror of the city was extreme; but the consternation of a warlike people, which nearly always changes into courage, is not like that of a vile populace, which feels nothing but its own weakness." (004-014)

Baker, the translator, notes that "there was a strong opinion among the Romans themselves that he could have done so" (004-023) — Livy himself recorded that "that day's delay...is firmly believed to have been the preservation of the city and the empire" (004-023).

Yet Baker concedes that "the character of Hannibal, as it comes to us in histories written by his enemies, represents him as combining — like all first-rate generals and men of action — consummate audacity with consummate prudence" (004-024), suggesting that his decision not to march on Rome reflected considered judgment rather than error.

Montesquieu himself refuses to dismiss the episode of the sojourn in [[capua|Capua]] as the cause of Hannibal's decline: "It is further said that Hannibal committed a great mistake in leading his army to Capua, where it became enervated.

But it is overlooked that this view does not ascend to the true cause" (004-015).

The true cause was structural: Hannibal commanded mercenaries, not citizen-soldiers, and his home government failed to reinforce him.
