# Roman Senate

The Roman Senate was the central governing institution of the Republic — a body of former magistrates whose collective wisdom and institutional memory provided the strategic continuity that Montesquieu identifies as essential to Rome's enduring greatness.

## Definition

The Senate was not a legislative body in the modern sense but a deliberative council whose resolutions (*senatus consulta*) carried the weight of accumulated authority. Its members were "elected, either by the senate or by the people" for life, creating an institutional memory that transcended the annual rotation of magistrates (001-003).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

During the Republic's golden age, "the senate never departed from the ancient maxims" (004-012). This consistency of policy — in war, diplomacy, and domestic governance — was the Senate's distinctive contribution. "The people suffered the senate to take the direction of affairs" because its authority rested on demonstrated competence rather than coercion (004-004).

The Senate also embodied the stern virtue of the Republic: "The senate refused to ransom the prisoners" taken by Hannibal, preferring to lose citizens than to negotiate with an enemy — a demonstration of the principle that individual lives mattered less than the state's honor (004-013).

### Imperial Period

Under the emperors, the Senate became an instrument: "the senate having found the means of giving the magistracies to the people" was a maneuver to preserve the appearance of republican forms while concentrating real power (001-011). The Senate continued to meet and deliberate, but its independence had evaporated.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu presents the Senate as the institutional embodiment of republican virtue. Its members, having already held high office, brought experience and prudence to deliberation. "The kingly power being elected" — and later, the consular — meant that executive authority was always answerable to a body of experienced statesmen (001-003).

He notes the Senate's role in managing internal conflict: when "the people in order to humble the senate" asserted their power, the resulting tension produced institutional innovation — the tribunes, the right of appeal, the written law — rather than civil war (001-005). The Senate was wise enough to concede what it could not prevent.

## Examples

- **Consistency of policy**: "The senate never departed from the ancient maxims" — Rome's strategic persistence across centuries (004-012)
- **Refusal to ransom prisoners**: After Cannae, the Senate refused to redeem captured soldiers, valuing state honor above individual lives (004-013)
- **Distribution of conquered lands**: "The senate distributed them among the hardy and industrious" — using conquest to strengthen the citizen body (004-027)
