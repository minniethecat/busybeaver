# Caesar's Civil War

The civil war between [[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]] and [[pompey|Pompey]], waged from his crossing of the Rubicon in 49 BC, was for Montesquieu the catastrophe in which the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]] finally fell. It is the centerpiece of his eleventh chapter, on Sylla, Pompey, and Caesar.

He treats it not as the crime of one ambitious man but as the inevitable outcome of a long decay. "It being inevitable that the republic should perish, it was only a question as to how and by whom it should be overthrown." (011-004)

For Montesquieu the deeper cause lay in the [[roman-army|army]] itself. Once Rome's wars passed beyond Italy, the soldiers ceased to belong to the city and belonged instead to their commanders — and military glory, gathered into one pair of hands, could no longer coexist with [[roman-liberty|liberty]].

## Background

Montesquieu locates the origin of the disaster in two great causes treated in his ninth chapter. The first was the very grandeur of empire: armies left for years in conquered provinces forgot the republic and obeyed only their generals.

The legionaries, he writes, "ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey" (009-002). After that, Rome could no longer tell whether the man at the head of an army was her general or her enemy.

The second cause was the sheer magnitude of the city. Its size, he argues, transformed ordinary disputes into armed slaughter — "It was solely the magnitude of the republic which produced the evil, by changing popular tumults into civil wars." (009-005)

[[lucius-sulla|Sylla]] supplied the fatal precedents. In his eastern campaigns he ruined military discipline, accustomed his army to plunder, led troops sword in hand into Rome, gave citizens' lands to soldiers, and invented the proscription (011-002). Each act taught a lesson Caesar would later use.

The proscription destroyed neutrality itself. Once a price was set on the heads of the losing party, no citizen could safely cling to the [[roman-republic|republic]]; the prudent course was simply to join one ambitious leader or the other (011-003). The ground for civil war was thus prepared.

Into this ruin stepped two men of overwhelming credit who "effaced all the other citizens by their credit, their exploits, and their virtues. Pompey appeared first; Caesar followed him closely." (011-004) The contest between them, Montesquieu insists, would have found another shape even had they thought differently.

## Course

Montesquieu narrates the war as a sequence of decisive moments, each turning on a fault of character or a corruption of republican institutions rather than on mere fortune.

### The road to war

The Republic had already armed its destroyers by handing single men the "extraordinary commissions" that concentrated power. [[pompey|Pompey]] was given every great charge in turn — against Sertorius, against Mithridates, against the pirates, even the grain supply (011-005).

The two rivals differed in method, not in aim. Caesar, "like Sylla, was willing to obtain sovereign power by armed violence," whereas Pompey "aspired to the dictatorship, but he wished to obtain it by the suffrages of the people." (011-006)

Their alliance with Crassus — the so-called triumvirate — sealed Rome's fate. Cato, Montesquieu reports, "said it was not the enmity of these men that ruined the republic, but their union" (011-007). The peace of these leaders was simply tyranny by another name.

### The Rubicon

Geography handed Caesar his opportunity: command of both [[gallic-wars|Gaul]] beyond and within the Alps let his soldiers grow old with him while Pompey could not bar his passage into Italy.

A famous senatorial decree had declared anyone crossing the Rubicon with an army a sacrilegious parricide (011-009). Caesar crossed it nonetheless, and the effect was instantaneous.

> The same consternation which Hannibal created in Eome by his victory at Cannae, Caesar created by crossing the Rubicon. (011-010)

Pompey was dismayed. He abandoned the capital, left the public treasure behind, gave up part of his troops and all of Italy, and passed beyond the sea (011-010). His flight, says Montesquieu, "ruined the reputation of his party; and, in civil wars, reputation is power itself." (011-009)

### Pharsalia

In Greece, Pompey held the better position. Commanding superior numbers and the sea, he had formed a masterly plan: protract the war, avoid a decisive battle, and let Caesar's army starve.

But Pompey could not resist the vanity and importunity of the great men in his camp, who taunted him for prolonging his command. Against his own judgment he abandoned his plan.

> he sacrificed all his advantages in order to proceed, with raw troops, to engage in battle with an army which had so often conquered. (011-011)

The battle of Pharsalia (48 BC) was the result, and Pompey lost everything. Caesar had foreseen the outcome, remarking before he left for Spain that "he was going against an army without a general, and that he should thence return against a general without an army." (011-026)

The war did not end there. Pompey's remnants under Scipio fought on in Africa, ignoring Cato's counsel to draw out the war; and when the cause revived under Brutus and Cassius, "the same precipitation ruined the republic a third time." (011-011)

## Outcome

Pompey's end set the moral key of the whole story: the contrast of his earlier greatness "with his wretched flight from Pharsalia, and his miserable death by the hand of an assassin on the coast of Africa" is among the sharpest reversals history presents (011-026).

Caesar, master at last, governed at first "under the titles of the Roman magistracies" (011-013), but his contempt for the [[roman-senate-body|Senate]] was undisguised. He even drafted senatorial decrees himself and signed them with the names of senators chosen at random (011-014).

Montesquieu refuses to admire the victor's famous mercy. "the moderation which a man shows after he has first usurped all does not merit great praise." (011-013) Caesar's clemency, he adds, was felt as an insult — he seemed not to pardon but to disdain to punish.

That contempt cost him his life. By the law of the Greek and Italian republics the assassin of a usurper was held a virtuous man, and most of the conspirators were Caesar's own beneficiaries (011-016). His assassination flowed directly from the regime he had founded.

The war's true terminus, in Montesquieu's account, was the death of the [[roman-republic|republic]] itself.

> At length the Eoman republic was subverted. (011-012)

## Impact on Rome

Caesar's murder did not restore liberty; it merely prolonged the agony. Antony seized Caesar's papers and treasure and "reigned more imperiously than during his life" (012-001), forging decrees and buying men with the dead dictator's gold.

A second round of bloodshed followed. Octavius, Antony, and Lepidus formed a new union and renewed the proscriptions, then hunted down the assassins. The battles of Pharsalia, Philippi, and Actium decided the empire of the world near Greece (012-006).

Montesquieu draws his constitutional lesson plainly. Rome lost her freedom because military success itself had become fatal to a free state. "She lost her liberty for the reason that she accomplished her work too soon." (009-008)

The civil wars, paradoxically, made Rome more terrible abroad even as they killed the republic at home. Under Marius, Sylla, Pompey, Caesar, Antony, and Augustus, Rome "continually grew more terrible, and completed the overthrow of all the kings who yet remained" (011-011) — glory and liberty parting ways.

At bottom the collapse was a collapse of [[civic-virtue|civic virtue]]. By Caesar's day "the Roman who lived in the time of Pompey and Caesar placed a money value upon everything connected with the interests of the state" (011-030); offices and votes had a price, and the [[political-corruption|corruption]] of every order made one-man rule the natural adjustment.

The road to [[augustus|Augustus]] ran straight from the Rubicon: the assassins were destroyed, the unions of ambitious men renewed, until a single heir emerged from the wreckage of the [[roman-republic|republic]].

## Key Figures

- **[[julius-caesar|Julius Caesar]]** — the conqueror who crossed the Rubicon and won at Pharsalia; possessed of so many great qualities that Montesquieu thought he would have triumphed "no matter what army he had commanded" (011-010), yet a usurper whose contempt for the Senate brought him to assassination.

- **[[pompey|Pompey]]** — Caesar's great rival, raised by the people's extraordinary commissions; a commander of real genius undone at Pharsalia by vanity and the importunities of his camp, then killed by an assassin in Africa (011-026).

- **[[lucius-sulla|Sylla]]** — whose earlier dictatorship furnished every precedent — ruined discipline, soldiers' land grants, the proscription — that made the later war possible (011-002).

- **Cato** — the figure of incorruptible virtue, whose warnings about the triumvirate's "union" (011-007) and whose counsel to protract the war went unheeded.

- **Brutus and Cassius** — Caesar's assassins, who revived the republican cause only to ruin it "a third time" through the same precipitation that had doomed Pompey's party (011-011).
