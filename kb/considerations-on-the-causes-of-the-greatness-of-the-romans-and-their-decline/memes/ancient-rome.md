# Ancient Rome

Ancient Rome is the city on the Tiber whose beginnings Montesquieu places at the head of his *Considerations*, because for him the customs of its founding, not the splendor of its later monuments, explain the greatness that followed.

He opens by warning the reader away from any modern picture of the place: early Rome was a rude, orderless settlement "built to enclose plunder, animals, and the fruits of the country" (001-001), a town that its neighbors regarded "not as a city, but as a menacing camp" (001-015).

From this poverty grew the war-driven habits that made the city master of [[roman-italy]] and, in time, of the world. The page follows Montesquieu's own emphasis: the site and its position, the stages by which the city grew under the [[romulus|kings]] and the early [[roman-republic]], and the strategic advantages of its ground.

This founding-centered account is the spine of his whole argument. Rome's want of streets, commerce, and arts forced its people to enrich themselves by war, and so the very weakness of the city became, in his causal logic, the source of its strength — the same poverty that armed the [[roman-army]] and filled the treasury of the [[roman-senate-body]] with the spoils of conquered peoples.

## Geography

Montesquieu's translator gathers in one note the physical relations of the city, presented as the natural stage on which its fortune was played out.

Rome "stood upon some moderate hills which rise upon the left bank of the Tiber, at a distance of about thirteen miles from the ancient mouth of this river" (001-016); these hills, "as indeed the whole face of the adjacent country," are of volcanic origin (001-016).

The Tiber itself owes its fame "wholly from the human interest with which it is associated, and in no appreciable degree from its physical magnitude" (001-016), being navigable for boats of fifty tons for about a hundred miles and only a hundred yards wide at the city (001-016).

The position, not the river, is what Montesquieu's account prizes. Taken together with the ancient state of Southern Europe, Northern Africa, and Southwestern Asia, "the geographical position of Eome was admirably adapted to empire," the city being founded "not very far from the center of these three vast areas" over which its power was destined to extend (001-015).

The argument for the site is put into the mouth of the dictator Camillus, dissuading his countrymen from abandoning the city after the Gauls:

> Not without good reason did gods and men select this place for founding a city : these most healthful hills ; a commodious river, by means of which the produce of the soil may be conveyed from the inland countries, by which maritime supplies may be obtained ; close enough to the sea for all purposes of commerce, and not exposed by too much proximity to the dangers of foreign fleets ; a situation in the center of the regions of Italy, singularly adapted by nature to the increase of a city. (001-015)

## The Beginnings

Rome began, in Montesquieu's telling, as something nearer a fortified war-camp than a town. "The city was even without streets," its houses "distributed without order, and were very small," because the men "constantly at work or assembled in the public place, passed but little of their time in houses" (001-001).

The names of its chief places "bore relation to such usage" — the storing of plunder (001-001). Yet the city's grandeur "appeared very soon in her public structures," and Montesquieu insists that the works giving the highest idea of Roman power "were constructed under the kings" (001-002).

Its fortune was bound up with continual war: to procure citizens, wives, or lands, [[romulus]] "and his successors were almost incessantly engaged in war with their neighbors," returning with the spoils of vanquished peoples — and such, he writes, was "the origin of those Triumphs, which were in after times the principal cause of the grandeur of the" Romans (001-002).

The decisive union came with the Sabines, "a people as hardy and as warlike as the Lacedaemonians," after which Romulus even adopted their larger buckler in place of his Argive one (001-002).

## Growth of the City

Because Rome was "a city witliout commerce, and almost without arts, pillage was the only means which private individuals had of enriching themselves" (001-007). This poverty, far from crippling the city, became its engine, and Montesquieu treats Rome's slow conquest of its neighbors as the very thing that preserved it:

> It was this that gave to the latter victories which did not corrupt them, and which left them in all their poverty. If they had rapidly conquered all the neighboring cities, they would have been found in a state of decline at the arrival of Pyrrhus, of the Gauls, and of Hannibal. (001-009)

Around the city lay the warlike peoples — the Latins, Hernicans, Sabines, Aequians, and Volscians — who "loved war passionately" and offered "an inconceivable resistance" (001-010), in pointed contrast to the Etrurians "enervated by their wealth and by their luxury" and the Capuans and cities of Campania who "languished in indolence" (001-010).

The city's growth came in marked stages: the long siege of [[veii]], which "lasted ten years" and brought "a new art among the" Romans (001-011), is called "a species of revolution" (001-011).

Throughout, the territory remained tiny — Veii itself stood "only about twelve miles from Rome — so small was yet the territory of the Romans, after three hundred and fifty years of all but incessant war" (001-011).

## Later Period

The walls drawn under the kings long defined the city. Montesquieu's translator records, citing Dr. Arnold, that "what are called the walls of Servius Tullius continued to be the walls of Rome for nearly eight hundred years, down to the Emperor Aurelian" (001-017).

Even the great Gallic catastrophe is judged, in his causal account, to have left the city's strength untouched: "The capture of Kome by the Gauls deprived it of none of its strength," the army withdrawing "almost entire to" Veii, so that "the burning of the city was no more than the burning of the cabins of a community of shepherds" (001-012).

Only later — under the Republic and the empire — did the truly imposing works arise, the translator listing "the Pantheon of Agrippa," "the Colisaeum of Vespasian and Titus," the baths, basilicas, aqueducts, bridges, and "that grand system of massive stone" roads connecting the capital with the cities of Italy and the provinces (001-019).

Chief works attributed to the time of the kings included:

- The Temple of Jupiter on the Tarpeian Hill (001-018)

- The great Circus (001-018)

- The "principal Sewer" of the city, whose construction so astonished Dionysius of Halicarnassus (001-018)

## Strategic Significance

For Montesquieu the city's situation and its founding poverty are themselves strategic facts. The ground was "admirably adapted to empire" by lying near the center of three vast regions (001-015), giving Rome reach over Italy, Africa, and the East. But the deeper advantage was moral and structural.

Rome was "a people who are at once proud, enterprising, courageous, and enclosed within walls," a condition that, he argues, must "necessarily throw off their yoke, or experience a softening of their manners" (001-004).

Its very want of wealth bound valor to self-interest: confined "within a circumference which was very small, she exercised those virtues which were to become so fatal to the world" (001-010). The city's slowness to conquer spared it the fate of states that pass too quickly from poverty to wealth and from wealth to corruption (001-009).

The result was a city organized for permanent war — sustaining the discipline of the [[roman-army]], the appetite for conquest that drove the [[punic-wars]] and the long contest against [[carthage]], and the spoils-fed momentum behind every [[roman-triumph]].

In Montesquieu's reading, the place on the Tiber mattered less for its temples than for the customs its founders pressed upon it, customs that turned a settlement of shepherds into the capital of the world.
