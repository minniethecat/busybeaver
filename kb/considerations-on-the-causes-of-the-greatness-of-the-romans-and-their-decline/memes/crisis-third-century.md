# Crisis of the Third Century

The Crisis of the Third Century (235–284 AD) was a prolonged period of civil war, foreign invasion, and economic collapse during which the Roman Empire nearly disintegrated. In fifty years, some twenty-six emperors were proclaimed by the legions, and most were murdered by their own soldiers.

Montesquieu treats this crisis as the logical culmination of the institutional decay he traces throughout the *Considerations*. The army that had once defended the Republic now made and unmade emperors at will.

"After Alexander, Maximin was elected. He was the first emperor of barbarian origin" [016-012] — and his elevation by the soldiery, without senatorial approval, marked the complete transfer of sovereignty to the military.

## Background

The crisis had deep structural roots. Montesquieu identifies the progressive breakdown of the constitutional balance that had characterized the early Empire. The Senate lost authority; the Praetorian Guard became kingmakers; the frontier legions, seeing the Guard's power, asserted their own right to choose emperors.

The first of the soldier-emperors, Maximin, was "a man of gigantic stature" who had risen from the ranks. He "never set foot in Rome" [016-012], governing entirely from the frontier camps — a symbolic rupture with the traditions of civic authority.

The Gordians, who briefly opposed Maximin, "perished in Africa" [016-012]. Their fate illustrated the dilemma: any general who showed competence was a threat to the sitting emperor, and any senator who showed independence was marked for death.

## Course

### Phase 1: Military Anarchy (235–253)

The pattern was grimly consistent. A general would be proclaimed emperor by his troops, march on Rome, defeat the incumbent, rule briefly, and then be murdered by his own men when the next challenger appeared.

"Maximus, Pupienus, and Balbinus" briefly ruled after the Gordians, only to be killed by the Praetorians [016-032]. The list of emperors who followed — "Heliogabalus, Alexander Severus, Maximin, the two Gordians, Maximus and Balbinus, the two Philips, Decius, Gallus, Aemilianus" — is a catalogue of assassination [016-032].

Each murder weakened the mystique of imperial authority and strengthened the conviction of the legions that the purple was theirs to bestow.

### Phase 2: Foreign Invasions and Separatism (253–268)

As Romans fought each other, the frontiers collapsed. "The Romans held the barbarians in check during so many ages" [005-022], but the army that had maintained that defense was now consuming itself in civil war.

The Persians under Shapur I invaded the eastern provinces and captured the emperor Valerian — the first time a Roman emperor had been taken alive by a foreign enemy. In the west, Germanic tribes crossed the Rhine and Danube, raiding deep into Gaul, Italy, and the Balkans.

Breakaway regimes emerged in Gaul (the "Gallic Empire") and in the east (the Palmyrene Empire under Zenobia). The unity of the Roman world, maintained since Augustus, dissolved.

### Phase 3: Recovery under the Illyrian Emperors (268–284)

A succession of emperors drawn from the Illyrian provinces — Claudius Gothicus, Aurelian, Probus — gradually restored imperial control. Aurelian, "a man of extraordinary energy," reconquered both the Gallic and Palmyrene secessions and built the walls around Rome that still bear his name.

But the recovery came at a cost. The Illyrian emperors ruled as military autocrats; the pretense of senatorial authority was abandoned. The emperor was now openly what he had long been in fact: the commander-in-chief of the army.

## Outcome

The crisis ended with the accession of Diocletian in 284, who stabilized the Empire through radical constitutional reform. But the Empire that emerged was fundamentally different from what had preceded it.

The Augustan settlement — in which the emperor governed through existing republican forms — was replaced by an overt military monarchy. The elaborate court ceremonial that Diocletian introduced, the division of the Empire into eastern and western halves, and the regimentation of economic life were all responses to the chaos of the third century.

## Impact on Rome

For Montesquieu, the Crisis of the Third Century is the proof of his central argument. "The Romans held the barbarians in check" [005-022] not because of military superiority — the barbarians were individually formidable warriors, and "the Romans had advantages over that multitude of barbarians who were taken indiscriminately" [005-017] — but because of institutional strength.

When those institutions decayed — when the Senate lost authority, when the laws were ignored, when citizenship was debased — Rome's military power became an instrument of its own destruction. The legions that had conquered the world now consumed the state.

The philosopher-emperor Alexander Severus and "Maximin engaged as many [Germans] as they could" [022-002] to fight their civil wars — hiring barbarians to kill Romans. The distinction between Roman and barbarian, which had been the foundation of Roman identity, dissolved.
