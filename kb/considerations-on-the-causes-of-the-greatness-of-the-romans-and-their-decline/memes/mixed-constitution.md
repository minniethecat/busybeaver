# Mixed Constitution

The theory of the mixed constitution — that Rome's political genius lay in balancing monarchical, aristocratic, and democratic elements — is central to Montesquieu's analysis of Roman greatness. He inherits the thesis from Polybius but develops it into a more general principle of political liberty.

## Definition

A mixed constitution combines elements of monarchy (the consuls' executive power), aristocracy (the Senate's deliberative authority), and democracy (the popular assemblies' legislative and electoral functions). "It was identified with the constitution of their Republic" — the balance was not an abstract ideal but the lived reality of Roman politics (002-005). "Aristotle speaks highly of her constitution" (004-027), and Montesquieu follows this tradition while giving it his own inflection: the key is not mere mixture but the productive tension between the elements.

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

The mixed constitution functioned effectively as long as each element remained within its proper sphere. "Under whatever constitution the state had assumed" — monarchy, republic, or empire — Rome's adaptability was remarkable (009-007). The electoral system was carefully calibrated: "not more than one sixth of all the citizens" could be assembled in the comitia centuriata, ensuring that the wealthy did not entirely dominate (008-016).

### Imperial Period

The balance collapsed when one element — the executive — absorbed the others. "The electoral weight removed the constitution of Rome — even in respect to liberty" (008-014). The mixed constitution gave way to autocracy not because the design was faulty but because the virtue that sustained it had decayed.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …from its origin its constitution was such — whether from the spirit of the people, the power of the senate, or the authority of certain magistrates — that the abuse of power could always be corrected (008-010)

Montesquieu's distinctive contribution is to connect the mixed constitution to liberty: "a more general view of the Roman constitution in its relation to liberty" reveals that institutional balance is the necessary condition of freedom (008-015, 008-012). The constitution was not self-executing — it required the active virtue of citizens to maintain the balance.

## Examples

- **Consuls vs. Senate**: The annual rotation of consuls checked by the Senate's permanent authority
- **Tribunes of the plebs**: The tribunician power as a democratic counterweight to patrician magistrates
- **The comitia centuriata**: The weighted voting system that balanced wealth and numbers
