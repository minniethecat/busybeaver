# Roman Liberty

Roman liberty (*libertas*) was not the modern liberal conception of individual autonomy, but a civic status: the condition of a free citizen governed by law rather than by the arbitrary will of a master. Montesquieu locates the "greatness of the government of Rome" in its capacity to sustain this liberty through institutional design.

## Definition

Liberty in the Roman sense meant participation in public life under laws one had a share in making. "It is easy to establish regulated liberty," Machiavelli observed, and Montesquieu concurs — the difficulty lay not in founding free institutions but in maintaining them against the internal corruption that success itself generates (001-005).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

"The government of Rome depended upon her liberty" — the state's entire structure was organized around the preservation of free institutions (001-010). The tribunes of the people, the right of appeal, and the public character of trials all served to protect the citizen from arbitrary power. "The people, become at last free," exercised their liberty to "humble the senate" and assert popular sovereignty (001-005).

### Imperial Period

Under the emperors, "public liberty was only the more endangered" as power concentrated in a single individual (007-005). The forms of liberty persisted — consuls were still elected, the Senate still deliberated — but "they enjoy no more liberty than was found at Rome during the time of the decemvirs" when the constitution was suspended (008-011).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …the spirit of liberty, of honor, and of glory, which animated the Roman people (005-016)

Montesquieu treats liberty as inseparable from virtue and honor. The decay of one necessarily entails the decay of the others. "Machiavelli attributes Roman liberty to the incessant struggle between the senate and the people" — a thesis Montesquieu largely endorses, seeing institutional conflict as a productive rather than destructive force (008-001).

He notes that "an immoderate desire for liberty" could itself become destructive, as "the entire authority of the state was sometimes at stake" in the plebeian-patrician conflicts (008-002). Liberty required moderation — the balance that the mixed constitution was designed to provide.

## Examples

- **The tribunician power**: The creation of the tribunes as guardians of plebeian liberty against patrician magistrates
- **The Decemvirate crisis**: The suspension of liberty under the decemvirs demonstrated how fragile free institutions could be (008-011)
- **Liberty and military strength**: Montesquieu argues that free citizens made better soldiers because they fought for their own laws and country
