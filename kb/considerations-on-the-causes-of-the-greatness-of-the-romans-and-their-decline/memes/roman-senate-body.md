# Roman Senate

The Roman Senate was the standing deliberative body at the center of the [[roman-republic]], a council drawn from the patrician families and, later, the principal families of the state. While the consuls changed every year, the Senate endured — and it was this permanence that Montesquieu makes the institutional anchor of Roman policy (001-006, 008-006).

Its members were the city's former magistrates, men who had already passed through office and command. Against the annual rotation of consuls, the Senate supplied the continuity that the [[mixed-constitution]] of Rome otherwise lacked — a body that "acted with constant steadiness of purpose" even as popular feeling swung from one extreme to another (009-002).

For Montesquieu the Senate is the organ of the "ancient maxims": the reservoir of policy that conducted foreign affairs, oversaw the public funds, and held conquered nations to the earth long after the legions had passed on (006-001, 008-005). Its constancy is, in his causal scheme, a direct cause of Roman greatness.

## Structure

After the expulsion of the kings, "the goverament became an aristocracy," and "the patrician famihes alone obtained all the magistracies, all the dignities" (008-002). The Senate was composed almost wholly of these patricians, which is why Montesquieu calls it the body "governed more by the ancient maxims" (008-004).

As the [[conflict-of-the-orders]] wore on and the plebeians forced their way into office, the composition broadened. The body came to be filled by "the principal patrician and plebeian families, who were now called nobles, and who were supported by the senate — which body was composed of them" (008-006).

Membership was not itself a magistracy. Montesquieu notes plainly that "The dignity of Senator was not a magistracy" (008-008) — it was a standing rank, held for life, conferred on those who had already served the state.

That rank was policed by the censors, who took the census but also corrected manners. These officers "could drive from the senate whom they would," and one tribune, M. Duronius, "was driven from the senate by the censors" for repealing a law that limited the expense of banquets (008-007, 008-008).

## Functions & Powers

The Senate's first power, in Montesquieu's account, was the conduct of foreign policy. He observes that "the authority of the senate was less limited in affairs without than within the city" — its reach abroad exceeded its reach at home (001-006).

It directed war and the settlement that followed. "This body erected itself into a tribunal for the judgment of all peoples; and at the end of every war it decided upon the punishments and the recompenses which it conceived each to be entitled to" (006-001). It granted and withheld the title of ally, made and broke treaties, and dictated terms to kings.

It managed the public treasure. Among the means by which it defended itself against the populace, Montesquieu lists "the benefactions which it dispensed, and the wise application which it made of the public funds" (008-005). Financial oversight was, for him, an instrument of governance as much as of solvency.

Above every particular power stood a governing rule:

> by a paternal condescension in according to the people a part of their demands in order to induce them to abandon others ; and, finally, by the constant maxim of preferring the preservation of the Kepublic to the prerogatives of any order or of any magistracy whatever. (008-006)

In foreign affairs the Senate spoke "always as masters." Its embassadors, sent to nations that had not yet felt Roman power, were certain to be insulted — "and this was an infallible pretext for a new war" (006-003).

## Historical Evolution

### Origin

The Senate predates the Republic. Montesquieu records that under the kings the body already existed: "Tarquin took the crown without being elected, either by the senate or by the people" (001-003). In the regal interregnum, "The senate named a magistrate for the interregnum, who elected the king" (001-003).

Servius Tullius, reorganizing the city, "had extended the privileges of the people in order to humble the senate" — evidence that the Senate was already a power to be checked before the kings were driven out (001-005). With the expulsion of the Tarquins, the kings' authority passed to the consuls, and the patrician Senate became the permanent core of the new aristocracy (008-002).

### Development

Through the long [[conflict-of-the-orders]], the Senate defended the patrician position against the [[tribunate-of-the-plebs]]. Montesquieu describes the contest directly:

> In these contests the people were sustained, or rather animated, by their tribunes, whilst the patricians were defended by the senate — which body, being composed almost wholly of patricians, was governed more by the ancient maxims, and influenced by a fear that the populace would elevate some tribune to the tyranny. (008-004)

At the height of the [[punic-wars]], the Senate's constancy was most visible. After the disaster at Cannae "the senate, always advised of the number of good soldiers yet remaining, had only to temporize, and never suffered itself to be cast down" (008-010).

It refused to ransom the prisoners and, "according to the ancient maxims," listened to no proposition of peace while the enemy stood in Italy (004-013).

The body's decline came with the empire of the city itself. As the legions served far away, soldiers "ceased to be soldiers of the republic, and came to be soldiers of Marius, of Sylla, of Pompey, and of Csesar" (009-002). When the people could grant their favorites "a formidable power abroad, all the wisdom of the senate was rendered futile, and the republic was lost" (009-002).

In the final convulsions the Senate prayed for protection from its own citizens. Facing [[julius-caesar]], "the senate in its turn raised its voice for Pompey, and felt no hope except in him" (011-005).

Once power had passed to the usurper, the body "possessed power no longer — was become almost ridiculous," and Caesar himself "made senatorial decrees, and subscribed to them the names of the first senators that came into his mind" (011-013, 011-014).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu treats the Senate as the cause of Roman strategic continuity. The legions spread terror, but it was the Senate that converted victory into durable empire:

> In the course of so mucli prosperity — from which men ordinarily grow negligent — the Koman senate was constantly actuated by the same profound policy; and, whilst the armies spread consternation on all sides, it held to the earth the nations which were once overthrown. (006-001)

The mechanism of that continuity was structural. Annual consuls came and went, but the Senate persisted; and so, against the volatility of the popular assemblies, "this body acted with constant steadiness of purpose, whilst the populace passed incessantly from the extreme of fury to the extreme of weakness" (009-002).

This constancy was for Montesquieu a species of [[mos-maiorum]] made institutional — the "ancient maxims" embodied in a permanent council. He sees in the Senate's refusal to bend after Cannae the very temper that made Rome "a prodigy of constancy" (004-012).

The decline of the Senate is, in his scheme, the decline of Rome. When the magnitude of the conquests gave commanders armies and kingdoms of their own, the Senate could no longer restrain them, and its wisdom became futile (009-002). The constancy that had built greatness could not survive the very power it had won.
