# Romulus

Romulus was the legendary founder and first king of Rome, traditionally dated to 753–716 BC. Montesquieu does not narrate the myth — the twin birth, the she-wolf, the fraternal murder — nor does he engage with the question of Romulus's historicity.

Instead, he treats Romulus as the origin point of a specific institutional tradition: the restriction of Roman citizenship to the pursuits of agriculture and war, a foundational choice that would determine the character of the Roman people for centuries.

## Life

### Early Life

According to Roman tradition, Romulus and his twin brother Remus were the sons of the god Mars and the Vestal Virgin Rhea Silvia, abandoned at birth and nursed by a she-wolf before being discovered and raised by the shepherd Faustulus.

Montesquieu's text does not recount these legends. His concern is not with the biography of the founder but with the institutional DNA that the founding implanted in the Roman state.

The early Roman kingdom, "rude, weak, and poor" under its kings (001-018), was nonetheless laying the foundations of a distinctive civic culture. The union with the [[roman-republic|Sabines]] — "a people as hardy and as warlike" as the Romans themselves (001-002) — strengthened the nascent city and established the pattern of incorporation through conquest that would characterize Roman expansion.

### Career

Romulus's principal act as founder, in Montesquieu's account, was the definition of what it meant to be a Roman citizen:

> Romulus allowed only two sorts of occupations to freemen — agriculture and war. Merchants, artificers, tavern-keepers, and those who lived in rented houses, were not citizens. (010-004, citing Dionysius of Halicarnassus)

This restriction was not a minor legal detail. It encoded into the Roman constitution a specific theory of civic virtue: the citizen was a farmer-soldier, rooted in the land, capable of bearing arms, and uncontaminated by the servile associations of commerce and manufacture. "Roman citizens regarded commerce and the arts as occupations for slaves, and did not engage in them" (010-004).

The consequences of this founding choice radiated through Roman history. It meant that "the Roman citizen knew only the art of war, which was the sole road to offices and to honors; and thus it was that the warlike virtues survived after all the others were lost" (010-004).

The martial character of the Roman people — the quality that enabled them to conquer the Mediterranean world — was not an accident of circumstance but a deliberate institutional product, traceable to the founder's original legislation.

### Later Years & Death

According to tradition, Romulus disappeared in a storm after a reign of thirty-seven years and was deified as the god Quirinus. Montesquieu does not record his death; for his purposes, Romulus exists only as the author of the institutional framework that defined the Roman citizen.

The kings who succeeded Romulus — Numa Pompilius, Tullus Hostilius, Ancus Marcius, Tarquinius Priscus, Servius Tullius, and Tarquinius Superbus — continued to build upon this foundation. The great public works of the regal period, "constructed under the kings" (001-002), gave physical form to the civic identity that Romulus had defined in law.

## Significance

Montesquieu's analysis grants Romulus a significance that is institutional rather than biographical. The founder matters not for his personal qualities — which are shrouded in legend — but for the constitutional choices made at the origin of the city, which set the trajectory of Roman development.

The restriction of citizenship to agriculture and war had three enduring consequences:

- **The Martial Monopoly**: By excluding commerce and manufacture from the sphere of honorable citizenship, Romulus ensured that the governing class of Rome would be a military aristocracy. The "sole road to offices and to honors" was war (010-004), and this channeling of elite ambition into military achievement was the engine of Roman conquest.

- **The Contempt for Commerce**: The Roman prejudice against trade — the view that "commerce and the arts were occupations for slaves" (010-004) — was not a cultural accident but a legislated principle. It meant that Rome would remain an agrarian-military state even as it conquered the commercial civilizations of the eastern Mediterranean, with profound consequences for its economic organization.

- **The Persistence of Martial Virtue**: The most important consequence was that "the warlike virtues survived after all the others were lost" (010-004). When republican liberty was extinguished, the martial character implanted by Romulus's legislation outlasted every other Roman virtue.

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu relies on Dionysius of Halicarnassus for the specific content of Romulus's legislation (010-004), reflecting the broader Enlightenment practice of treating the Greek antiquarian sources as repositories of constitutional data rather than as narrative historians.

The translator Jehu Baker expands on the significance of the Roman contempt for industry, observing that "the tone of opinion among the governing classes strongly associated industry with the notion of social degradation, and this for the obvious reason that all the industries of the time were mostly carried on by slaves" (010-005, Translator's note).

This structural linkage between slavery and the devaluation of labor was, Baker argues, "the labor system of the Romans, and of the ancient states generally" (010-005, Translator's note).

Modern scholarship has complicated the picture considerably, recognizing that the Romulus legend was largely constructed retroactively to explain Roman institutions whose origins were already obscure to the classical historians.

But for Montesquieu's purposes, the historicity of Romulus is beside the point; what matters is that the Romans themselves understood their civic character as the product of a deliberate founding act, and that this self-understanding shaped their subsequent development.

> Romulus allowed only two sorts of occupations to freemen — agriculture and war. (010-004)
