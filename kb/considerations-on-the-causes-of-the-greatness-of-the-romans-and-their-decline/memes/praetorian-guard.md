# Praetorian Guard

The elite military unit that served as the Roman emperor's personal bodyguard — created by Augustus, it evolved into a kingmaking force whose power to elevate and depose emperors made it, in Montesquieu's telling, a symptom and accelerant of imperial corruption.

## Structure

The Praetorian Guard was organized into nine cohorts under Augustus, each commanded by a tribune. The entire force was overseen by the praetorian prefect (*praefectus praetorio*), who became one of the most powerful officials in the empire.

Augustus provided that the praetorian soldiers should receive higher pay than ordinary legionaries — a privilege that recognized their proximity to the emperor and their role as the ultimate guarantor of imperial authority. (013-011)

## History

### Origin

The Praetorian Guard originated from the cohort of praetorians that had protected Roman generals during the Republic. Augustus institutionalized this practice by creating a permanent imperial guard stationed in Rome and nearby towns — a departure from Republican tradition, which had forbidden armed troops within the city.

### Evolution

Under Augustus and his immediate successors, the Guard functioned as intended: a professional bodyguard loyal to the imperial house. But after the death of Augustus, the Guard's political ambitions grew with each regime change.

Their power expanded dramatically as they discovered that the empire was, in practice, theirs to give. Praetorian prefects accumulated powers and functions that rivaled those of the emperor's chief ministers, becoming "the masters of the empire" in all but name. (017-002)

## Role in Roman History

The Praetorian Guard became a central actor in the drama of imperial succession. When Pertinax attempted to restore discipline in 193 AD, he was murdered by the Guard after only three months — and the empire was then auctioned to the highest bidder. Later emperors sought to appease the anger of the praetorian guards, who were enraged at the death of their favorites. (016-010)

By the late Empire, the criminal intrigues of Roman praetorians had become a byword for the corruption of imperial institutions. The Guard that Augustus had created to protect the emperor became the greatest threat to any emperor who failed to satisfy its demands. (021-009)

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> Montesquieu treats the Praetorian Guard as a case study in institutional corruption. The Guard began as a rational security measure — the emperor needed protection in a city without a police force. But its concentration of armed force at the center of power made it an irresistible tool for faction and ambition.

> When the praetorians learned that they could make and unmake emperors, the imperial succession became a matter of bribery and violence rather than legitimacy. For Montesquieu, this was not an accident but a structural consequence of concentrating military power in the capital while disarming the citizenry — the very opposite of the Republic's discipline of civic arms.
