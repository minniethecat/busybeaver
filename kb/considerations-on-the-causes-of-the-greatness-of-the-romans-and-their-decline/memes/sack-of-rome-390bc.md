# Sack of Rome (390 BC)

The sack of Rome by the Gauls under Brennus in 390 BC was the greatest trauma of the early Republic. The city was captured and burned, and "a powerful party, headed by the tribunes, is said to have favored the abandonment of Rome" [001-013]. That the Republic survived — and refused to abandon its sacred soil — demonstrated the deep roots of Roman institutional resilience.

Montesquieu emphasizes a striking fact: "The capture of Rome by the Gauls deprived it of none of its strength" [001-012]. The army dispersed across the countryside was intact; the Latin allies remained loyal. Rome's strength lay not in its walls but in its institutions.

## Background

By the early fourth century BC, Rome had emerged as the dominant power in Latium, but the Gauls remained a fearsome adversary to the north. The Romans had already encountered them: "the Romans had many wars with the Gauls" [004-001], driven by "the same love of glory" that characterized all Roman military enterprise.

The immediate cause of the invasion was a dispute between Rome and the Senones, a Gallic tribe that had crossed the Apennines. Roman ambassadors, sent to negotiate, instead joined battle against the Gauls — a violation of diplomatic norms that provoked the Gallic advance on Rome.

The battle that followed, at the river Allia just north of Rome, was a catastrophic Roman defeat. The Gauls "at the beginning of a battle are more than men" [004-017] — their initial charge, delivered with the long slashing sword that the Romans would later learn to counter [002-008], shattered the Roman line.

## Course

### Phase 1: The Fall of the City

After the rout at the Allia, the Gauls entered Rome virtually unopposed. The city was sacked and burned. Surviving defenders retreated to the Capitol, which alone held out against siege.

Montesquieu notes that the Gauls were undisciplined in victory. They failed to secure the countryside, allowing the dispersed Roman army — led by the exiled hero Camillus — to regroup at Veii, the Etruscan city Rome had recently conquered.

### Phase 2: The Siege of the Capitol and Ransom

The Capitol endured a prolonged siege. Legend records that the sacred geese of Juno alerted the defenders to a night assault, saving the citadel. But eventually starvation forced negotiations.

The Gauls agreed to withdraw for a ransom of a thousand pounds of gold. When the Romans complained of false weights, the Gallic chieftain Brennus threw his sword onto the scales, uttering the immortal words: *Vae victis* — "Woe to the conquered."

### Phase 3: Camillus and Recovery

According to Roman tradition — which Montesquieu treats with measured skepticism — Camillus arrived with his army at the moment of the weighing and declared that Rome was ransomed with iron, not gold. The Gauls were driven off, and Rome set about rebuilding.

The city was rebuilt hastily, without the orderly plan that had characterized earlier Roman construction. "The city was rebuilt without order," and this haphazard reconstruction left Rome with the narrow, winding streets that would persist for centuries.

## Outcome

The immediate consequences were severe: the city was in ruins, and many citizens urged relocation to Veii, whose Etruscan infrastructure remained intact.

Yet "the capture of Rome by the Gauls deprived it of none of its strength" [001-012]. The army was reconstituted. The colonies and allies remained loyal. Within decades, Rome had not only recovered but embarked on the expansion that would bring all Italy under its control.

The sack established a durable pattern: "The Romans had many wars with the Gauls," and they learned that "the same love of glory, the same contempt of death" that animated the Republic could overcome even the most terrible defeats [004-001].

## Impact on Rome

Montesquieu identifies the Gallic sack as the first of the great crises that tested — and proved — Roman institutions. The three crises he enumerates together are "the arrival of Pyrrhus, of the Gauls, and of Hannibal" [001-009], each of which threatened Rome's existence but ultimately reinforced its martial character.

The sack had a second, less obvious consequence. The burning of the city destroyed most of Rome's early records, making the early history that Livy and other historians reconstructed necessarily uncertain. Montesquieu is careful to distinguish what is known from what is legendary in the early Republic.

The Romans learned from the Gallic sword: "The trenchant swords of the Gauls" [002-008] led directly to the adoption of the short sword — a weapon better suited to disciplined infantry tactics — a military adaptation that would serve Rome for centuries [002-009].
