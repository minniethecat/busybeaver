# Political Corruption

In Montesquieu's analysis, political corruption is not merely bribery or abuse of office but the systematic decay of the principles that sustain a political order. For Rome, this meant the dissolution of civic virtue — the willingness to subordinate private interest to public good — and with it, the collapse of the Republic's institutional integrity.

## Definition

Corruption in Montesquieu's sense is the process by which "the republic had become corrupt" — when the laws remain formally intact but the spirit that animated them has died (003-003). It marks "the true epoch of their corruption" not in any single event but in the accumulation of changes that transformed Rome from a republic of citizen-soldiers into an empire of subjects (005-012).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

"Before corruption set in, the primitive revenues of the state were sufficient" (003-002). The early Republic's institutions were designed for a small, virtuous community. The very success of Roman arms — the conquest of the Mediterranean — introduced "wealth, and from wealth to corruption" (001-009).

### Imperial Period

Under the empire, corruption became systemic. "A new sort of corruption came in" — not the crude venality of the late Republic but the refined servility of courtiers who competed for imperial favor rather than public honor (017-002). "The Romans finally lost their military discipline, and even abandoned the use of their own arms" — the dissolution had become total (018-005).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

> …the work of the legislator, but of corruption itself. Rome was adapted to suffer it (009-007)

Montesquieu's central insight is that Rome's institutions were themselves complicit in their own corruption. The Republic was "adapted to" expansion, but expansion brought wealth, and wealth dissolved the virtue on which the Republic depended. This paradox — that greatness contains the seeds of decline — is the theoretical core of the *Considerations*.

"The corruption of manners" was accelerated by the influx of "all that might aid in the corruption" from conquered Eastern territories (013-005). Montesquieu traces a causal chain: conquest → wealth → luxury → corruption → loss of liberty → decline.

## Examples

- **The wealth of Asia**: Conquests in the East introduced unprecedented luxury that "transported to Rome hastened the corruption in which the republic was so soon to perish" (007-008)
- **The Gracchan crisis**: The reform attempts of the Gracchi mark "the true epoch" of visible corruption, when private interests openly prevailed over public good (005-012)
- **Imperial court corruption**: Under the emperors, "a new sort of corruption" emerged — the competition for favor rather than honor (017-002)
