# Roman Law

Roman law was not merely a collection of statutes but the structural framework that bound the Roman state together.

From the equal land allotments of the early [[roman-republic|Republic]] to the great codes of [[ancient-rome|Rome]]'s last centuries, the strength of the city was tied to "the constant observance of certain customs" (008-007), policed by magistrates such as the censors and the [[tribunate-of-the-plebs|tribunes of the plebs]].

Montesquieu treats legal integrity as a barometer of political health. So long as the laws were rigidly kept, the equal division of lands "constituted a strong people" and "secured a good army" (003-002); when observance lapsed, Rome's institutions decayed even as her arms still conquered the world.

## Definition

In the Roman conception, law was the foundation of every other right. The translator records the lawyers' own maxim, drawn from the Twelve Tables, that the code "became 'the fountain of all public and all private rights'" (020-018) — a single source from which the whole civil order flowed.

That order was double-edged. Roman law was "largely made up of 'survivals' from the barbarous state of society in which it took its origin," yet it "gradually drew to itself and appropriated more enlightened principles of justice" (020-020). The harsh archaic code and the refined jurisprudence of the classical jurists were stages of one continuous growth.

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

The early Republic's legal development was driven by the plebeian demand for written, public law. The page's source for the Twelve Tables is the decemvirate: under "pretext of giving written laws to the people, they seized the government" (001-010) — both the demand for legal transparency and the danger of concentrating legislative power. On this institution see [[decemviri|the decemvirs]].

Law was also a battlefield. The agrarian laws regulating the public domain provoked "angry controversies and even violent collisions in the bosom of the state" (003-013); the Licinian law capping holdings at 500 jugera "was resisted with great bitterness by the patricians" (003-014), a struggle that culminated in the [[gracchan-reforms|Gracchan crisis]].

A distinct magistracy guarded the law's spirit rather than its letter. The censors "corrected such abuses as the law had not foreseen" (008-007), enforcing the [[mos-maiorum|ancestral custom]] and [[civic-virtue|civic virtue]] on which the constitution rested — for "there are bad examples which are worse than crimes" (008-007).

### Imperial Period

Montesquieu observes that good republican laws can outlive their usefulness: "good laws, which have caused a small republic to become powerful, are afterwards a burden to the state which they have fostered into greatness" (009-006). The legislation that built the empire was ill-suited to governing it.

Under the Empire, lawmaking and jurisprudence nonetheless reached their height. The translator divides the history of Roman jurisprudence into four periods, the third of which — from Cicero to Alexander Severus — was "the learned and splendid age of jurisprudence" (020-018), producing Papinian, Gaius, and Ulpian before the decline.

## Montesquieu's Analysis

For Montesquieu the test of a free constitution is whether it can correct its own faults through law. He praises Rome and England alike because each contains a body which continually inspects it, and which as continually inspects itself:

> In a word, a free government — that is to say, one which is constantly agitated — can never maintain itself if it is not, by its own laws, capable of correction. (008-011)

He also distinguishes laws that win an empire from those fit to keep it, assigning the first the quality of "good" and the second "suitable":

> There is much difference between good laws and suitable ones — between those which are calculated to enable a nation to render itself master of others, and those which are adapted to maintain its power when once acquired. (009-007)

The relaxation of legal rigor thus both reflected and accelerated [[political-corruption|moral decay]]: laws once kept "not from fear, not from reason, but from a passionate temperament" lost their hold as private wealth grew, and "the nobles resisted with more force than the patricians had done" (008-006).

## Legacy

The translator faults Montesquieu for omitting Rome's greatest legal monument and supplies the estimate himself. Of Justinian's compilation he writes:

> The Code, the Pandects, and the Institutes are immortal monuments of ancient law; they constitute the principal nexus through which the juridical ideas of the Romans are connected in one unbroken whole with the juridical ideas of modern societies. (020-015)

The summit of this development was the doctrine that positive law must answer to a higher law.

Roman jurists "rose to the simple and uniform conception that, by the Law of Nature, Liberty is the status of all men," quoting the Institutes that "by the law of nature all men are born free" (020-021) — a conception of [[roman-liberty|liberty]] the translator judged superior even to that of the Greek philosophers.

## The Four Ages of Roman Law

The translator periodizes Roman jurisprudence, combining Hugo and Gibbon, as four nearly equal epochs (020-017, 020-018):

- **Infancy (c. 303 years)** — From the founding of the city to the Twelve Tables. Law was "in its stage of semi-savage infancy," consisting "mainly of unwritten customs" (020-017).

- **The written code (348 years)** — From the Twelve Tables to the birth of Cicero. The law was now "fixed in a written code" (020-018), though no jurist yet rose to the first rank.

- **The classical age (342 years)** — From Cicero to Alexander Severus — "the learned and splendid age of jurisprudence" (020-018), the era of Quintus Mucius, Gaius, Papinian, Paulus, and Ulpian.

- **Decline and codification (330 years)** — From Alexander Severus to Justinian — "one of decline in the law," redeemed by Tribonian, "the presiding genius of the Code, the Pandects, and the Institutes" (020-019).

## Examples

- **The Twelve Tables** — Rome's first written code, which the Roman lawyers held to be "the fountain of all public and all private rights" (020-018), opening the second age of jurisprudence.

- **The agrarian laws** — Public-land legislation, "in the most general sense... simply their public land laws" (003-013); the Licinian law of c. 369 B.C. became "the model for all subsequent legislation of the kind" (003-013).

- **The censorship** — The magistracy that struck at conduct "the ordinary magistrate could not punish" (008-007); a censor could "drive from the senate whom they would" (008-007). It is called "a very wise institution" (008-008).

- **Justinian's codification** — The Code, Pandects, and Institutes, "immortal monuments of ancient law" (020-015) and the bridge between Roman and modern jurisprudence.
