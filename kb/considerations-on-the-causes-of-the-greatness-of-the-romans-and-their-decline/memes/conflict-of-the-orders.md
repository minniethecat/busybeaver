# Conflict of the Orders

The Conflict of the Orders — the prolonged political struggle between the patrician aristocracy and the plebeian majority — was, in Montesquieu's analysis, not a pathology of the Roman Republic but the generative engine of its institutional creativity and the ultimate source of its liberty.

## Definition

The conflict pitted "the patricians and the richer class of citizens" against the common people in a struggle over political rights, economic burdens, and access to office (003-014). Far from being a "sedition of the patricians," it was a structural feature of the Republic that drove continuous institutional adaptation (003-015).

## Role in Roman History

### Republican Period

The conflict produced Rome's most distinctive institutions. When "dissatisfied with the patricians," the plebeians "withdrew to the Mons Sacer" — the first secession — compelling the creation of the tribunes of the plebs (008-003). The plebeians and patricians made "promises of alliance to each other in case the patricians should not observe their pledges" — the conflict spawned constitutional safeguards (008-004).

The struggle gradually transformed Roman society: "the plebeians had so far humbled the patricians that the distinction between them was no longer observed" in certain offices (008-006). This was not a revolutionary overthrow but an evolutionary integration — the patriciate absorbed the plebeian elite into a new nobility.

### Imperial Period

Under the Empire, the conflict ceased — not because it was resolved but because all parties had been subordinated to the emperor. "The patricians and the richest of the commoners blended together" into a single imperial elite (008-014).

## Montesquieu's Analysis

Montesquieu follows Machiavelli in treating the conflict as productive: "Machiavelli attributes Roman liberty to the incessant struggle between the senate and the people" (008-001). The struggle "sought to strengthen the revolutionary spirit" after the expulsion of the kings (008-002) — by channeling dissatisfaction into institutional reform rather than civil war, the Republic turned potential destruction into creative energy.

## Examples

- **The First Secession (494 BC)**: Plebeians withdrew to the Mons Sacer, winning the creation of the tribunes (008-003)
- **The Twelve Tables (451–450 BC)**: The demand for written law as a check on patrician judicial discretion
- **The Licinian-Sextian Laws (367 BC)**: Opening the consulship to plebeians, gradually erasing the patrician monopoly on high office (008-006)
