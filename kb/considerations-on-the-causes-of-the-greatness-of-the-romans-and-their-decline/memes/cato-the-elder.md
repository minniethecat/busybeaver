# Cato the Elder

Marcus Porcius Cato (234 BC – 149 BC) is the archetype of old Roman austerity in Montesquieu's historical scheme. A *novus homo* from Tusculum, he rose through military distinction and moral rigor to the consulship and the censorship.

He dedicated his public life to defending the ancient [[mos-maiorum|customs of the ancestors]] against foreign influences — Greek philosophy, Eastern luxury, and Carthaginian commerce — that he believed would corrupt the [[roman-republic|Roman Republic]].

Montesquieu's *Considerations* names Cato the Elder explicitly as "Cato the Censor," the representative figure of Rome's second legal epoch (020-018). Yet his indirect presence pervades the whole work.

The "Cato" invoked repeatedly as the standard of republican virtue — against whom [[cicero|Cicero]] and [[julius-caesar|Caesar]] are measured — is the great-grandson, Cato the Younger. But the moral template was set by the Elder, whose life defined what it meant to be a Roman of the old school.

## Life

### Early Life

Cato was born in 234 BC at Tusculum, of plebeian origin and modest means. He grew up on his family's Sabine farm, acquiring habits of thrift, labor, and endurance that grounded his political identity.

His early manhood coincided with the [[punic-wars|Second Punic War]], in which he served with distinction against [[hannibal|Hannibal]]. The experience left him convinced that Rome's survival depended on preserving its agrarian, martial character.

He came to the attention of Lucius Valerius Flaccus, a patrician who sponsored his entry into public life, launching the career of the most celebrated *novus homo* before [[gaius-marius|Marius]].

### Career — Consul, Censor, Moral Crusader

Cato's public career was a continuous campaign against wealth and foreign manners that eroded Roman simplicity. Elected consul in 195 BC, he commanded in [[roman-spain|Spain]] with characteristic severity.

It was as censor in 184 BC that he earned his enduring reputation. His censorship was a moral earthquake: he expelled senators for luxurious living, taxed extravagance, and enforced sumptuary regulations.

His hostility to Carthage was legendary. After an embassy to the reviving African city, he returned convinced that Rome would never be secure while Carthage stood.

Thereafter, he ended every speech in the [[roman-senate-body|Senate]] — whatever the topic — with "Carthago delenda est" (Carthage must be destroyed). He died in 149 BC, the year the [[sack-of-carthage|Third Punic War]] began.

### Jurisconsult and Writer

Cato was the first Roman to write history in Latin prose (the *Origines*) and a treatise on agriculture (*De Agri Cultura*) that survives as the oldest complete Latin prose work.

In Montesquieu's text, his jurisprudential legacy receives a pointed assessment. Jehu Baker, surveying the epochs of [[roman-law|Roman law]], places Cato in the period from the Twelve Tables to Cicero's birth.

> Cato the Censor may be taken as the typical representative of the jurisconsults of the period — proud, ignorant, narrow, and barren. (020-018)

The judgment is severe yet captures an essential truth. Cato belonged to an age when law was still an art of the ruling class rather than a science.

The analytical index reinforces this characterization, listing him as "viewed as a representative of the jurisconsults of his age" (023-027).

### Later Years & Death

Cato remained active into extreme old age. He prosecuted enemies, opposed Greek philosophy, and continued to demand Carthage's destruction.

He died in 149 BC at the age of eighty-five. He left behind a model of austere citizenship that echoed through generations — most directly in Cato the Younger, who adopted his name and principles.

## Significance

Cato the Elder occupies a foundational place in Montesquieu's moral history of Rome. He represents the ancient virtue — *virtus* — that made the Republic great: frugality, discipline, devotion to the common good.

This virtue is the baseline against which Montesquieu measures Rome's progressive [[political-corruption|corruption]]. When the author invokes "Cato" as the standard of selfless patriotism — "with Cato, glory" (012-005); "Cato constantly forgot himself" (012-005) — these lines speak of the Younger.

But the paradigm of a Cato who forgets himself for the republic was first established by the Elder.

The trajectory from Cato's austere Rome to bribery-ridden elections of which "even Cato himself" despaired (011-030) is the arc of republican decline. The Elder stands as the last great representative of the older order.

Baker's assessment of Cato as "proud, ignorant, narrow, and barren" is, in its own way, a backhanded tribute. The same stubborn independence that appears as limitation in the jurist was, in the statesman, the source of his strength.

Cato's insistence on destroying Carthage also illustrates a central Montesquieu thesis: the relentless Roman pursuit of national interest. Cato's *delenda est Carthago* was the voice of a people who "constantly increased" in power abroad even while convulsed by "civil wars" at home (011-011).

## Contemporary Views

Montesquieu's references to the Catos measure the distance Rome traveled from republican virtue to imperial corruption. The Elder as Censor embodies the public morality later ages abandoned.

> If Caesar and Pompey had thought as Cato, others would have thought as Caesar and Pompey. (011-013)

The sentiment applies to both Catos. The Republic fell not because villains overthrew it, but because there were too few men of Cato's temper to sustain it.

In the analytical scheme of the *Considerations*, Cato the Elder belongs to the history of Roman jurisprudence. The note naming him the representative jurisconsult of his period (020-018) places him among the builders of [[roman-law|Roman law]].
