# Roman Spain

The Iberian Peninsula — Hispania — was the first great overseas territory Rome seized from [[carthage|Carthage]] during the Second Punic War. Montesquieu treats it as a double case study: the Carthaginians lost Spain because they governed it with cruelty, while the Romans conquered it through persistence that tested even their legendary [[roman-military-discipline|military discipline]].

## Geography

Spain occupies the southwestern extremity of Europe, separated from North Africa by the Strait of Gibraltar and from Gaul by the Pyrenees. Its mineral wealth — silver, copper, and gold — made it an economic prize. Carthage held scattered coastal territories; Roman power eventually extended across the whole peninsula.

## History

### Before Rome

Before the Roman arrival, Spain was inhabited by Iberian and Celtic peoples, with Phoenician and Greek trading colonies along the coast. Carthage established its Spanish empire under the Barcid family after the First Punic War, drawing mercenaries and silver from the peninsula. (PN=004-008)

"The Carthaginians had oppressed the peoples of Spain so cruelly that when the Romans arrived there they were regarded as liberators." (PN=004-008)

Montesquieu draws a general lesson from this: "if we consider the immense sums which Spain cost them in carrying on a war in which they succumbed at last, we shall plainly see that injustice is bad policy, and that it does not even accomplish its own ends." (PN=004-008)

### Under Rome

Rome entered Spain during the Second Punic War (218–201 BC), landing forces to cut off [[hannibal|Hannibal]]'s supply base. Scipio Africanus drove the Carthaginians from the peninsula: "Having conquered Spain, and formed an alliance with Masinissa, Scipio deprived the Carthaginians" of their advantage in Numidian cavalry — an advantage that won the battle of Zama. (PN=004-009)

After Carthage's defeat, the Romans found that holding Spain required far longer than conquering it. The Lusitanian and Celtiberian wars dragged on for decades. The siege of Numantia became emblematic of the difficulty: "Are they beaten before Numantia? Scipio Aemilianus immediately deprived the soldiers of all that had served to enervate them." (PN=002-005)

The Numantians' stubborn resistance was remarkable — they "had reduced twenty thousand" Romans who had been sent against them. (PN=006-011) The eventual Roman victory in 133 BC came only after Scipio Aemilianus restored discipline to a demoralized army, illustrating the Roman principle that military reversals were corrected by returning to austerity, not by changing strategy.

Roman Spain was organized into two provinces — Hispania Citerior and Hispania Ulterior — later reorganized under Augustus into three: Baetica, Lusitania, and Tarraconensis.

The silver mines near New Carthage, the agricultural wealth of Baetis valley, and the export of olive oil and garum made Spain one of the empire's richest provinces.

### Later Period

Spain remained under Roman rule until the early 5th century AD, when Germanic tribes — Suebi, Vandals, and Visigoths — crossed the Pyrenees. The Visigothic kingdom that succeeded Roman authority preserved much of Roman law and urban structure, and Spain's Roman heritage — its language, law, and Christianity — endured through the Islamic period and into the modern era.

## Strategic Significance

Montesquieu mentions Spain repeatedly to illustrate structural arguments. First, Spain was the theater where Carthage demonstrated that cruel governance is self-defeating — the natives welcomed Rome as a liberator. (PN=004-008) This reinforced his broader thesis that moderate government is more durable than despotic rule.

Second, Spain tested Rome's military system. When the legions grew soft between the great wars, defeat in Spain forced a restoration of discipline. "Scipio Aemilianus immediately deprived the soldiers of all that had served to enervate them" — the same pattern of correction-through-austerity that Montesquieu identifies as central to Roman greatness. (PN=002-005)

Third, Spain exemplified the pattern of Carthaginian overextension. Carthage's Spanish holdings were "detached and straggling," stretching across eighteen hundred miles of coast, while "the power of Rome was massed" in continuous Italy. (PN=004-025) This geographic contrast — concentrated versus scattered power — was, in Montesquieu's view, one of the structural reasons Carthage was doomed.

Finally, the Romans' ability to turn Spanish resources against Carthage itself — cavalry, silver, and manpower — showed their genius for making "soldiers of all the peoples whom they subdued." (PN=004-005) Spain was not merely conquered; it was converted into an instrument of further conquest.
