# Ancient Greece

Greece occupies a central place in Montesquieu's argument: not as a Roman province, but as the civilization Rome had to subdue before it could rule the world. He presents Greece as a paradox — a people of extraordinary military and cultural genius whose political fragmentation made conquest inevitable, and whose culture in turn transformed their conquerors.

## Geography

Greece occupies the southern Balkan Peninsula, with the Aegean Sea to the east and the Ionian to the west. Montesquieu notes its proximity to Italy: "the peninsulas of Greece and Italy are only separated by an arm of the Mediterranean — itself an inland sea." (PN=005-003) This geographic closeness made conflict between the Greek and Italian spheres inevitable once Rome expanded eastward.

## History

### Before Rome

In the centuries before Roman intervention, Greece was not a unified state but a world of competing city-states and leagues. Montesquieu catalogues the principal players. (PN=005-004)

The Aetolians were "warlike, hardy, rash, avaricious of gain, always free with their word and their oath, and making war upon land as pirates do upon sea." The Achaeans were "incessantly harassed by their neighbors or by their inconvenient protectors." The Boeotians — "the dullest of the Greeks" — took "the least part they could in general affairs." (PN=005-005)

Lacedaemon (Sparta) "had preserved her power — that is to say, she had retained that warlike spirit which she had received from the institutions of Lycurgus." (PN=005-005)

Athens, by contrast, "without allies, and without power of their own, had ceased to astonish the world except by the flattery of kings." The tribune where Demosthenes had spoken was "no longer ascended except for the proposal of the most cowardly and scandalous decrees." (PN=005-005)

Montesquieu sums up the tragedy: "Greece was otherwise formidable from her situation, her strength, the multitude of her cities, the number of her soldiers, her police, her manners, and her laws. She loved war and she knew the art of war. She would have been invincible if she had been united." (PN=005-006)

### Under Rome

Rome's entry into Greek affairs came through the very disunity Montesquieu describes. After defeating Carthage, Rome moved east. (PN=005-004)

The critical moment was the proclamation of Greek "freedom" by Flamininus at the Isthmian Games (196 BC). Montesquieu is deeply ironic: "The Greeks abandoned themselves to a stupid joy, and believed themselves to be free in fact, for the reason that the Romans had declared them so." (PN=005-010)

This was not liberty but a new form of dependency. Rome "gradually established it as a maxim among the Greek cities that the latter could form no alliance, supply no succors, nor wage any war whatever, without their consent." (PN=005-017)

The Romans also prohibited the Hellenistic kings from recruiting among "the allies of the Romans" — which is to say, from Greece — "thus reducing them to the employment of their own national troops." (PN=005-017)

The Greek military tradition that had produced the victories of Marathon and Leuctra was neutralized not by defeat in battle but by the slow strangulation of alliance politics. Rome "whenever they left a city free, they at once gave rise to two factions among its citizens" — one for the ancient laws, the other for Roman will — and "the latter faction was always the stronger." (PN=006-005)

Greece was formally annexed as the province of Achaea in 146 BC, the same year Carthage was destroyed. But Montesquieu's point is that Greece had already been a Roman dependency for half a century before the formal annexation.

### Later Period

Under Roman rule, Greece retained a privileged cultural status. Romans sent their sons to study in Athens; Greek became the language of the eastern empire; and Greek art, philosophy, and literature permeated Roman elite culture. But Greece's political independence was never restored. It remained a Roman and then Byzantine province until the Ottoman conquest.

## Strategic Significance

For Montesquieu, Greece is the supreme case study in the politics of disunity. "She would have been invincible if she had been united" — this single sentence encapsulates his diagnosis. (PN=005-006) The city-state model that had produced such creative energy in the classical period proved fatal when facing a unitary power like Rome.

Greece also illustrates Montesquieu's theory of Roman conquest as a gradual, indirect process. The Romans did not destroy Greek armies in a single decisive campaign, as they did with Carthage. (PN=006-015)

Instead, they exploited existing divisions, posed as liberators, and slowly converted allies into subjects. "This was a slow method of conquering," Montesquieu observes. Nations became Roman "without any one being able to fix the epoch of its subjugation." (PN=006-015)

Finally, Greece plays a unique role in Montesquieu's corruption narrative. While Syria "corrupted" Rome with Eastern luxury, Greece civilized Rome through its arts and letters. The synthesis of Greek culture and Roman power was, in some sense, the empire's greatest achievement — and, paradoxically, one of the forces that eroded the austere virtue on which Roman greatness was built.

Montesquieu contrasts Athens and Rome on this point. "Athens fell, for the reason that her errors were so sweet and charming that she could not be brought to correct them." (PN=008-010) Rome, by contrast, long retained the capacity for self-correction — until Greek and Asian influences dissolved it from within.
