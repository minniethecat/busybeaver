## Character Description

Mr. Gander is one of the young gentlemen boarders at [[Todgers's]], Mrs. Todgers's commercial boarding-house in London. He is introduced in Chapter IX during the dinner party given for the Miss Pecksniffs — [[Charity Pecksniff]] and [[Mercy Pecksniff]] — who are visiting London under their father's supervision.

## The Witty Turn

Among the diverse collection of boarders — each distinguished by his particular "turn" (sporting, theatrical, debating, literary, vocal, smoking, convivial) — "Mr. Gander was of a witty turn" (009-110). His claim to fame is originating a joke about the serving-boy [[Bailey Junior]], who appeared at the party in an oversized shirt:

> Mr. Gander was of a witty turn, being indeed the gentleman who had originated the sally about "collars"; which sparkling pleasantry was now retailed from mouth to mouth, under the title of Gander's Last, and was received in all parts of the room with great applause.
>
> (009-110)

## The Todgers's Party

Gander forms part of the welcoming deputation for the Miss Pecksniffs. Along with [[Mr. Jinkins]], he "knocked at the door of Mrs. Todgers's room, and, being formally introduced to the two Miss Pecksniffs by their parent who was in waiting, besought the honour of conducting them upstairs" (009-107).

At the party, Gander escorts Mercy Pecksniff: "There was a general cry of 'Hear, hear!' and 'Bravo Jink!' when Mr. Jinkins appeared with Charity on his arm; which became quite rapturous as Mr. Gander followed, escorting Mercy" (009-109).

As the punch flows and the evening becomes increasingly convivial, Dickens notes that "Gander leaves the Gander of all former feasts whole leagues behind. *He* rises to propose a toast. It is, The Father of Todgers's" (009-121). Later, "Gander, in a convulsing speech, gives them the health of Bailey junior; hiccups are heard; and a glass is broken" (009-123).

## After the Party

When [[Seth Pecksniff]] becomes intoxicated and repeatedly escapes his bedroom to deliver moral lectures from the stair banisters, "Jinkins and Gander took the rest upon themselves, and made him as comfortable as they could, on the outside of his bed" (009-171). They are required to return him to bed multiple times — proof, as the narrator dryly observes, of "an irrepressible desire for the improvement of his fellow creatures that nothing could subdue" (009-177).

## Significance

While a minor character, Gander contributes to Dickens's lively portrait of the Todgers's boarding-house society — a microcosm of commercial London, full of young men each nursing their particular pretensions. His "witty turn" and the party's recycling of his singular joke as "Gander's Last" serves as gentle satire of the small scale on which boarding-house reputations are made.
