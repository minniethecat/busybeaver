# Zephaniah Scadder

Zephaniah Scadder is the land agent for the Eden Land Corporation, the smiling, one-eyed swindler who sells [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit]] his ruinous stake in the American settlement of [[eden|Eden]]. He is one of Dickens's most vivid American grotesques: a figure of almost supernatural dishonesty whose most frightening quality is his absolute, unblinking calm.

## Character Portrait

Dickens's physical description of Scadder is unforgettable. He sits in the Eden Land Office "with his right eye fixed on the prospect, and his left on the visitor" — or rather, with one eye open and the other perpetually shut, creating an effect of permanent, asymmetrical vigilance (021-115). This one-eyed gaze becomes a metaphor for Scadder's moral vision: he sees only what serves him.

The [[eden|Eden]] prospectus he shows Martin is a work of fraud dressed as cartography — "a great architectural plan of the Eden settlement, with banks, churches, and a capitol" (021-116). Scadder's selling technique is to let the map speak for him, punctuating his silences with gnomic utterances: "You may judge for yourself what the settlers promise, when you see what the planners plan" (021-122).

## Role in the Novel

Scadder appears in the American chapters (XXI–XXIII) as the human face of the Eden Land Corporation's fraud. General Choke introduces him as "the honestest fellow in the world," a recommendation whose irony becomes apparent only when Martin stands in the pestilential swamp that bears the name of Paradise (021-126). Scadder sells Martin a location in [[eden|Eden]] for a sum that consumes nearly all of Martin's remaining funds, and when Martin later returns to confront him, Scadder is "at his post, in the old place, with his one eye on the prospect" (023-008) — unchanged, unrepentant, and already selling the same dream to the next victim.

Dickens uses Scadder to anatomise a specific American type: the confidence man whose power lies not in bluster but in his uncanny ability to seem trustworthy while being anything but. The name "Scadder" itself suggests a scoundrel or a scab — someone who picks at the wounds of hope.

## Notable Quotes

General Choke's recommendation of Scadder, laced with dramatic irony:

> The General whispered Martin that Scadder was the honestest fellow in the world, and that he wouldn't have given him offence designedly, for ten thousand dollars.
>
> (021-126)
