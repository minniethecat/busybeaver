# Mr. Fips

Mr. Fips is a lawyer of Austin Friars, London, who appears in the later chapters of *Martin Chuzzlewit* to offer [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] mysterious employment cataloguing a library. Small, spare, and peaceable in appearance, with the old-fashioned costume of black shorts and powder, Fips is one of Dickens's most memorable minor grotesques — a man whose every tic and evasion deepens the mystery of who, exactly, is Tom's benefactor. The answer — old [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Martin Chuzzlewit]], working anonymously to make amends — makes Fips the human instrument of the novel's final movement toward [[redemption]].

## Character Portrait

Dickens introduces Fips through the accumulated detail of his extraordinary office in Austin Friars, reached through "a very dark passage on the first floor, oddly situated at the back of a house, across some leads," where a "little blear-eyed glass door" bears his name "painted on it in characters which were meant to be transparent" (039-106). The office itself is "a mighty yellow-jaundiced little office" with "a great, black, sprawling splash upon the floor in one corner, as if some old clerk had cut his throat there, years ago, and had let out ink instead of blood" (039-111).

Fips himself is "small and spare, and looked peaceable, and wore black shorts and powder" (039-109). His manner is one of perfect, unruffled blankness. When meeting Tom, he is described as looking at him "curiously, but with an entire freedom from any such expression as could be reasonably construed into an unusual display of interest" (039-115). His nervous habit is to take up the office wafer-stamp and begin "stamping capital F's all over his legs" (039-122). From the stuffing of his office stool, he draws forth "a piece of horsehair of immense length, which he put into his mouth with a great appearance of appetite" (039-114). Every detail contributes to the impression of a man so thoroughly absorbed in his own obscure routines that the outside world barely registers.

## Mystery and Revelation

Fips's role in the plot is to serve as the anonymous intermediary between old Martin Chuzzlewit and Tom Pinch. He tells Tom explicitly, "I act upon instructions" (039-119) and refuses to name his principal. The mystery deepens with each encounter: Fips pays Tom weekly but offers no information; he warns Tom not to "mention any particulars about your place of employment, to people generally" (040-016); he affects to have "no idea" when his employer might appear (039-126).

John Westlock offers the most plausible reading of Fips: "Fips is an eccentric man; has some knowledge of Pecksniff; despises him, of course; has heard or seen enough of you to know that you are the man he wants; and engages you in his own whimsical manner" (039-161). The truth, when revealed, is both simpler and more satisfying: Fips is the instrument of old Martin's penitence, the mechanism by which the wronged Tom is quietly restored to prosperity without the humiliation of direct patronage.

## Dickens's Treatment

Mr. Fips belongs to Dickens's extensive gallery of legal grotesques — men whose professional blankness has become indistinguishable from personality. Unlike the novel's villains, however, Fips's opacity conceals benevolence rather than fraud. His eccentricities — the horsehair chewing, the wafer-stamp fidgeting, the impenetrable calm — are harmless rather than sinister. He is, in the end, a good man performing a good office, and his peculiarity is simply the outward form of professional discretion elevated to an art.
