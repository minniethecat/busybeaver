# Montague Tigg

Montague Tigg is the novel's most spectacular shape-shifter: a shabby adventurer who reinvents himself as a company director, a [[trust|confidence]] trick played on an entire society. He appears first as a grubby, mustachioed sponger cadging five shillings from [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], and returns transformed into "Tigg Montague, Esquire," chairman of the [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company]] — a title whose every word is a lie. His trajectory from petty [[deception|fraud]] to grand swindle, and his brutal [[crime|murder]] by [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]], form one of the novel's darkest subplots.

## Overview

Tigg makes his entrance in Chapter 4, introducing himself with characteristic bravado: "My name, sir, is Tigg. The name of Montague Tigg will perhaps be familiar to you, in connection with the most remarkable events of the Peninsular War?" (004-034). The claim is, of course, entirely [[deception|fraud]]ulent — and entirely typical. Tigg's method, in both his impoverished and prosperous phases, is to assert importance with such [[trust|confidence]] that the assertion becomes its own evidence.

He operates at first as the sharper half of a partnership with [[chevy-slyme|Chevy Slyme]], a disreputable Chuzzlewit relative. The two haunt inns and sponge off strangers while trading in family gossip about [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s whereabouts — information they attempt to sell to [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]. The Dragon landlady's servant [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] describes him with disgust: "Nothing's good enough for him; all the women is dying for him he thinks, and is overpaid if he winks at 'em" (007-043).

## Biography

### Background & Family

Tigg has no family in the novel — he is one of Dickens's rootless adventurers, a man without history who exists entirely in the present tense of his own invention. His past, teased at in [[deception|fraud]]ulent references to "the Peninsular War" and other imaginary exploits, is a void he fills with whatever narrative suits the moment. This absence of authentic identity is essential to his character: he is pure performance, a walking credit bubble.

### Role in the Plot

Tigg's transformation is one of Dickens's great satiric coups. He [[appearance|reappears]] entirely remade: "He had a world of jet-black shining hair upon his head, upon his cheeks, upon his chin, upon his upper lip. His clothes, symmetrically made, were of the newest fashion and the costliest kind" (027-006). The cadaverous sponger has become a dandy, and the vehicle of his transformation is the Anglo-Bengalee — a company with "everything to gain and nothing at all to lose" that exists solely to extract premiums from gullible investors (027-044).

As chairman, Tigg deploys the rhetoric of disinterested benevolence to disguise pure predation. His portrait hangs in the boardroom, he presides over meetings with an ivory hammer, and he cultivates a staff of accomplices — [[david-crimple|David Crimple]] as secretary, [[doctor-jobling|Doctor Jobling]] as medical officer — who give the enterprise a veneer of institutional legitimacy. The company's name is the joke: "Disinterested" is the precise quality it lacks. Tigg's genius is to understand that people will trust a [[trust|confidence]] trick if it is performed with sufficient scale and style. As he tells [[david-crimple|Crimp]]le, "I rise with circumstances, David. That's a point of genius in itself" (027-024).

His fatal entanglement is with [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]]. Tigg, having somehow learned of [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s attempt to [[crime|murder]] his own father by poison, uses this knowledge to extract [[commerce|money]] from him — a dangerous game, given [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s violent nature. The blackmail escalates until [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], driven past fear into homicidal resolution, follows Tigg into a wood and bludgeons him to death.

### Fate & Resolution

Tigg is [[crime|murder]]ed in Chapter 47, struck down by [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] as he rests on a stile in a lonely lane. The description of his body, discovered the next day, is one of Dickens's most chilling passages: "In one thick solitary spot, it lay among the last year's leaves of oak and beech, just as it had fallen headlong down" (047-039). The swindler who lived by [[appearance|surface]]s ends as an object of pure physical horror — "a dark, dark stain that dyed the whole summer night from earth to heaven."

The [[crime|murder]] precipitates the novel's final crisis. [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s guilt is exposed through the testimony of [[lewsome|Lewsome]] and the detective work of [[nadgett|Nadgett]], and Tigg's connection to [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] is revealed: [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] had employed Tigg as a spy to track [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]]'s movements, making Tigg's schemes part of a larger pattern of [[secret|surveillance]] and manipulation (052-094).

## Character Analysis

Tigg is a study in the relationship between self-invention and self-destruction. His rise from beggary to corporate [[deception|fraud]] is a parable of Victorian capitalism: the Anglo-Bengalee is a Ponzi scheme dressed in mahogany and Turkey carpet, its boardroom rituals a parody of legitimate commerce. Tigg understands that [[trust|confidence]] is a performance and that scale compounds credibility — insights that make him the novel's darkest satirist of economic life.

But he is also, paradoxically, capable of a kind of honesty. He knows exactly what he is, and he never pretends otherwise to those who share the game. His conversations with [[david-crimple|Crimp]]le and Jobling are [[class-and-gentility|frank]] acknowledgments of [[deception|fraud]] among confederates. His fatal mistake is not [[trust|overconfidence]] but a failure of imagination: he cannot believe [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] will actually kill him. The con man who made a career of reading others is, in the end, unable to read the one man who is more dangerous than himself.
