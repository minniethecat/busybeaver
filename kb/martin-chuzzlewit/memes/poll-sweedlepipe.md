# Poll Sweedlepipe

Poll Sweedlepipe (formally Paul Sweedlepipe) is the barber and bird-fancier of Kingsgate Street, London, in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. He is the landlord of [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] and a devoted friend to young [[bailey]], whose precocious worldliness he regards with wondering admiration. Gentle, inquisitive, and tender-hearted, Poll is one of Dickens's most lovingly rendered comic characters — a man so steeped in his twin trades of shaving and bird-keeping that he has become, in his nature, something between a barber and a sparrow.

## Character Portrait

Dickens introduces Poll in Chapter XXVI with one of his finest comic set-pieces. Poll Sweedlepipe's house is "one great bird's nest" — gamecocks in the kitchen, pheasants in the garret, bantams in the cellar, owls in the bedroom, and rabbits covering the staircase in hutches "made from old packing-cases, boxes, drawers, and tea-chests" (026-004). The effect is a "complicated whiff" that salutes every nose entering his "easy shaving-shop" (026-004).

Poll himself is "a little elderly man, with a clammy cold right hand, from which even rabbits and birds could not remove the smell of shaving-soap" (026-006). Dickens extends the ornithological metaphor into Poll's very character: "Poll had something of the bird in his nature; not of the hawk or eagle, but of the sparrow, that builds in chimney-stacks and inclines to human company. He was not quarrelsome, though, like the sparrow; but peaceful, like the dove" (026-006). His walk struts like a pigeon; his speech is prosy like a coo; his inquisitive head-cocked posture suggests the raven — "yet there was no more wickedness in Poll than in a robin" (026-006). The whole portrait is capped by the observation that Poll's ornithological properties, whenever threatening to go too far, were "quenched, dissolved, melted down, and neutralised in the barber" (026-006).

Poll maintains two distinct costumes: in his "sporting character" he wears a velveteen coat, blue stockings, ankle boots, a bright neckerchief, and a very tall hat; in his "more quiet occupation of barber," he subsides into an apron, a flannel jacket, and corduroy knee-shorts (026-008).

## Role in the Novel

Poll Sweedlepipe serves several functions in the novel's London plot. As Mrs. Gamp's landlord, he is a steady witness to — and occasional participant in — the comedy of her alcoholic self-dramatisation. When Bailey returns from Todgers's in his full livery and boots, Poll is "so perfectly confounded by his precocious self-possession" that he can only take Bailey for granted (026-041). It is Poll who introduces Bailey to Mrs. Gamp, setting in motion the alliance that drives much of the later action.

Poll's tender heart is most evident in his solicitude for the sick Lewsome, whose "bony hands and haggard face" impress him wonderfully (029-084). In the novel's closing chapters, Poll's affection for Bailey reaches its culmination when, sobbing over the injured boy, he declares: "He shall go into the business along with me. I am determined he shall. We'll make it Sweedlepipe and Bailey. He shall have the sporting branch" (052-142). This proposed partnership — the gentle barber and the precocious street-boy — is one of the novel's most optimistic visions of how human bonds might transcend class and age.

In his final exchange with old Martin Chuzzlewit, Poll receives the admonition to "take as much care of your lady-lodger as you can, and give her a word or two of good advice now and then" — advice that Poll, ever meek and obliging, unquestioningly accepts (052-150).

## Dickens's Treatment

Poll Sweedlepipe belongs to the tradition of Dickens's benevolent tradesmen — characters whose modest occupations and gentle natures make them havens of decency in a world of fraud and selfishness. Unlike the novel's architects of deception (Pecksniff, Tigg), Poll's work is tangible and honest: he shaves chins and sells birds. The comic extravagance of his bird-filled house is never mockery; Dickens clearly delights in Poll's harmless eccentricity. The portrait is affectionate rather than satirical, and Poll's final elevation to partnership with Bailey represents one of the novel's few uncomplicated happy endings.

> With the exception of the staircase, and his lodger's private apartment, Poll Sweedlepipe's house was one great bird's nest. Gamecocks resided in the kitchen; pheasants wasted the brightness of their golden plumage on the garret; bantams roosted in the cellar; owls had possession of the bedroom; and specimens of all the smaller fry of birds chirrupped and twittered in the shop.
>
> (026-004)
