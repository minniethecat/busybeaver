## A London Landmark

The Monument to the Great Fire of London — a 202-foot Doric column designed by Sir Christopher Wren near London Bridge — appears repeatedly in Martin Chuzzlewit as a geographical marker, a meeting place, and the site of comic reflection. Pecksniff arrives at "a kind of paved yard near the Monument" (008-062) during his London visit, and the area around the Monument serves as a key London setting throughout the novel.

## Mrs. Todgers and the Monument

Mrs. Todgers's commercial boarding-house is located "beneath the shadow of the Monument" (032-002), placing the establishment in the heart of the City of London. The location is significant: the Monument area was a busy commercial district, and the "shadow" it casts over Mrs. Todgers's establishment suggests both the respectability and the slightly overshadowed nature of her trade. The boarding-house becomes a gathering point for characters including the Pecksniff sisters, Augustus Moddle, and the Jinkins circle.

The rooftop of Todgers's offers one of the novel's most striking urban panoramas, with the Monument at its centre:

> For first and foremost, if the day were bright, you observed upon the housetops, stretching far away, a long dark path; the shadow of the Monument; and turning round, the tall original was close beside you, with every hair erect upon his golden head, as if the doings of the city frightened him.
>
> (009-008)

## The Man in the Monument

Dickens devotes a comic set-piece to "the Man in the Monument" (037-004), the keeper of the landmark who admits visitors to climb its 311 steps. This figure is described as "quite as mysterious a being" (037-004) as the Monument itself, with "simple tastes" and a "stony" quality (037-005). His laconic conversation with two visitors — a gentleman and a lady — becomes one of the novel's droll interludes: asked his trade, "The Man in the Monument replied, 'A Tanner'" (037-007), providing a punning punchline typical of Dickens's love of wordplay.

## The Monument as Recurring Geography

The Monument serves as a recurring orientation point in the novel's London geography. Characters are "driven to the Monument" (010-009) or find themselves "hard by the Monument" (037-003), and the narrator uses the phrase "whenever you go to see the Monument" (017-172) to frame general reflections. The landmark's ubiquity in the novel reflects the actual prominence of the Monument in the nineteenth-century City of London, before taller buildings obscured it from view.
