# Bailey Junior

Bailey Junior is the boy-servant — the "youngest gentleman in company" — at [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]'s Commercial Boarding House in [[london|London]]. He is one of Dickens's great comic creations: a Cockney page-boy of inexhaustible cheek and vitality, whose rapid-fire banter and street wisdom cut through the pretensions of the commercial gentlemen he serves. In a novel dominated by selfishness and scheming, Bailey represents something rarer — sheer anarchic energy, the life force that refuses to be subdued.

## Overview

Dickens introduces Bailey with characteristic delight in names and naming. "Benjamin was supposed to be the real name of this young retainer but he was known by a great variety of names. Benjamin, for instance, had been converted into Uncle Ben, and that again had been corrupted into Uncle; which, by an easy transition, had again passed into Barnwell" (009-105). The comic genealogy of his nicknames establishes his nature immediately: he is a boy around whom language proliferates, a figure of irrepressible verbal energy.

His physical description is equally vivid: he appears "in a complete suit of cast-off clothes several sizes too large for him, and in particular, mounted a clean shirt of such extraordinary magnitude, that one of the gentlemen (remarkable for his ready wit) called him 'collars' on the spot" (009-107). The ill-fitting clothes signify both his poverty and his resilience — he wears what he is given and makes it his own.

## Biography

### Background

Very little is given of Bailey's origins. He is simply there at [[todgers|Todgers's]], part of the furniture of [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]'s establishment. His age is indeterminate — old enough to be entrusted with duties, young enough to be treated as a child. He exists to serve and to observe, and his [[surveillance|observation]]s are delivered with a cheek that is his primary weapon against the indignities of his position.

### Life at Todgers's

Bailey's role at [[todgers|Todgers's]] is that of dogsbody, messenger, and reluctant waiter. He mediates between the commercial gentlemen and the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] sisters, announces visitors, and endures the casual cruelties of his social superiors with a resilience expressed through backtalk and comic performance. When [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] exclaims "my gracious, Bailey!" upon hearing that a gentleman has called, he retorts: "Ah! It is my gracious, an't it? Wouldn't I be gracious neither, not if I wos him!" (011-003) and (011-004).

His greatest moment of physical comedy comes during the commercial gentlemen's party, when his delight at the conversation's turn "impelled and forced him to the instantaneous performance of a dancing step, extremely difficult in its nature, and only to be achieved in a moment of ecstasy, which is commonly called The Frog's Hornpipe" (011-014). The dance is pure Bailey: an eruption of bodily joy that defies decorum.

### Later Career

Bailey undergoes one of the novel's more surprising transformations. In the later chapters, he has left [[todgers|Todgers's]] and become a medical student — an improbable ascent that Dickens presents as entirely natural. When [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] and his sister [[ruth-pinch|Ruth]] encounter him in the streets of [[london|London]], he is no longer the boy in cast-off clothes but a young man with prospects. The transformation is both comic and touching — a reminder that in Dickens's world, vitality and cheek can sometimes carry a person upward.

## Character Analysis

Bailey belongs to a distinct Dickensian type: the Cockney servant whose verbal ingenuity and indomitable spirits make him more alive than his social betters. He is kin to Sam Weller and the Artful Dodger — the street-smart youth whose language is a form of self-defense and self-assertion. His speech patterns, with their dropped consonants and inventive syntax, are rendered with phonetic precision that captures both class and character.

His function in the novel is largely tonal. In the [[todgers|Todgers's]] chapters, he provides comic relief from the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]ian hypocrisy and the commercial gentlemen's posturing. But his transformation into a medical student suggests a deeper Dickensian faith: that energy, properly directed, can overcome circumstances. In a novel where inherited selfishness corrupts generations, Bailey represents the possibility of self-creation.

## Key Relationships

- [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]] — his employer, the proprietress of the boarding house where he serves and learns the world [[ch09]]
- [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] — one of the young ladies he serves and cheekily addresses by first name (011-003)
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] — the younger [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] sister, whose interactions with Bailey highlight her own frivolity (009-096)
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — the lodger Bailey helps lock in his room during the drunken commercial gentlemen's [[class-and-gentility|prank]] (009-178)
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — encounters Bailey in his later guise as a medical student, one of the novel's gestures toward renewal (046-010)
