# Crime

Crime in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is not a departure from the novel's moral world but its logical endpoint. The novel traces a progression from moral selfishness through professional fraud to violent murder, suggesting that crime is not a separate category of human behaviour but the extreme form of impulses present in every character who places self-interest above all else. Jonas Chuzzlewit's criminal career — from financial swindling to attempted patricide to murder — is the novel's demonstration that [[selfishness]] and [[greed]], pursued to their conclusion, become indistinguishable from felony.

## Overview

The novel opens its treatment of crime with the Preface's remarkable claim that Jonas is not a monster but a product: "So born and so bred; admired for that which made him hateful, and justified from his cradle in cunning, treachery, and avarice; I claim him as the legitimate issue of the father upon whom those vices are seen to recoil" (P03-004). This is Dickens's anti-sensationalist theory of crime — criminals are made, not born, and their making is a social process for which the community bears responsibility.

## In the Novel

Jonas Chuzzlewit's criminality escalates in a carefully plotted sequence. He begins with financial manipulation — attempting to defraud his father and Pecksniff in business dealings. His marriage to Mercy Pecksniff is itself a form of predation: he marries not for love but for the pleasure of domination. The novel tracks his progression from emotional cruelty (his treatment of Mercy) to attempted murder (his plan to poison his father) to successful murder (his killing of Tigg Montague, who has been blackmailing him).

The murder of Tigg Montague is the novel's most sustained treatment of crime. Tigg, having reinvented himself as the head of the fraudulent Anglo-Bengalee (027-006), discovers Jonas's attempt on his father's life and uses the knowledge for blackmail. Jonas's response is characteristically direct: he murders Tigg in the darkness of a country road (047-001). The scene is handled with Dickens's characteristic blend of melodrama and psychological acuity — Jonas is terrified not by guilt but by the possibility of detection.

The novel's treatment of crime extends beyond Jonas to the institutional fraud of the Anglo-Bengalee. Tigg Montague's company is crime legalised — a scheme that defrauds (027-039) investors while maintaining the forms of commercial respectability. Dickens's point is that the line between criminal fraud and legitimate business is thinner than Victorian society pretends.

### Key Passages

The novel's most chilling treatment of crime is not the murder itself but its aftermath. Jonas, returning from the killing, is haunted not by conscience but by the practical problem of avoiding detection: "the dead man's face was ever before him" in a "shadowy and uncertain way" — not as moral horror but as forensic threat. Dickens's interest is in the psychology of the criminal after the crime: the deterioration of nerve, the paranoia, the self-betrayal that makes detection inevitable.

In the novel's final judgment, old Martin confronts Jonas with the full catalogue of his crimes (052-083), including the attempted murder of his father: "the crime for which you have now to answer." Jonas's response is defiance: "Any lie you can trump up. Any forgery you can invent. Any perjury. Any nickname. Go ahead" (paraphrased, ch52). The defiance is the last expression of the selfishness that has defined him — he will not repent because he cannot recognise any authority beyond his own will.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens uses crime as a diagnostic tool: the nature of a society's crimes reveals its true values. In a novel organised around the critique of selfishness, the central crime is murder for financial gain — the ultimate expression of the logic that values money over human life. Jonas kills his father (in intention), then kills Tigg, because both men stand between him and money. The Anglo-Bengalee defrauds its investors because they value profit over scrutiny. In both cases, crime is what happens when greed becomes the governing principle of conduct.

The novel is careful to distinguish between crime and sin. Pecksniff is not a criminal in any legal sense — he breaks no law — but his hypocrisy is presented as morally more corrupting than Jonas's overt criminality. Dickens suggests that the law is a poor guide to moral character: the worst offences against human relationship are often perfectly legal.

## Notable Quotes

Dickens frames the novel's moral concerns in the Preface:

> to exhibit in their strongest and most exaggerated forms the causes of many vices and crimes that awaken the general horror
>
> (P03-005)

After committing murder, Jonas's mind turns inexorably back to the scene:

> having committed the crime, his fears were now diverted, strangely too, to the place of the commission of the crime
>
> (047-054)

Jonas's confession reveals the chain of moral causation:

> this crime began with me. It began when I first tampered with the truth; when I first put a mask upon myself
>
> (051-111)
