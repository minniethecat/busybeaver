# New York

The city of New York as experienced by Martin Chuzzlewit upon his arrival in America — a bustling port where "all the New York papers" compete in sensationalism, boarding-houses stage elaborate social performances, and a young Englishman's illusions about the New World begin their rapid unraveling.

## Overview

New York is the threshold of Martin Chuzzlewit's American experience. He disembarks from the packet-ship *The Screw* at the port of New York (015-001) and is immediately assailed by newsboys hawking the *New York Sewer*, the *New York Stabber*, and the *New York Rowdy Journal* — a cacophony that prefigures the moral chaos to come (016-002). It is here that Martin meets Colonel Diver, editor of the *Rowdy Journal*, and Major Pawkins, the boarding-house keeper whose establishment introduces him to the pretensions and contradictions of New York society. The city in Dickens's rendering is a place where surfaces are everything and substance nothing, where "smartness" serves as a catch-all virtue and everyone professes equality while jealously guarding every social distinction.

## Description

Dickens's New York is defined less by its physical geography than by its social texture. The boarding-house of Major and Mrs. Pawkins is a microcosm of the city: a place of hollow titles, competitive display, and the relentless pursuit of advantage (017-015). The streets ring with the cries of newsboys selling papers whose names — the *Peeper*, the *Plunderer*, the *Private Listener* — advertise their own dishonesty (016-002). The fashionable drawing-rooms, such as the Norrises', operate on the principle that "there were other fashionable spheres above them, and other fashionable spheres below, and none of the stars in any one of these spheres had anything to say to the stars in any other" (017-035).

What little Martin sees of New York's physical character is filtered through disappointment. Bill Simmons tells him that his brother Ned "sent word home that it brought Old York to his mind, quite vivid, in consequence of being so exactly unlike it in every respect" (013-012) — a wry comment on the gap between American promise and English expectation.

## Events

- **Disembarkation** — Martin and Mark arrive at the port of New York, greeted by a chorus of newsboys hawking sensational papers (015-001)
- **The Pawkins Boarding-House** — Martin takes lodging and is introduced to American social customs, including titles for everyone and ceaseless political talk (017-010)
- **Dinner at the Norrises'** — The arrival of the English "gentleman of the name of Chuzzlewit" causes "an amazing stir" among the New York fashionable set (017-025)
- **General Fladdock's Embarrassment** — The general's horror at being asked to acknowledge a steerage passenger exposes the city's class anxieties (017-035)
- **Departure for Eden** — Martin, unable to find honest work, leaves New York in search of the promised land of Eden (021-001)

## Narrative Function

New York serves as the first act of Martin's American disillusionment — the city where surface glitter masks moral vacancy. It introduces the thematic vocabulary of the American chapters: "smartness" as a euphemism for sharp practice, titles as a substitute for character, and the press as an instrument of intimidation rather than truth. The city also provides Martin with his most important American contact: [[mr-bevan|Mr. Bevan]], who will later rescue him from Eden.

## Quotations

Dickens's New York announces itself through its newspapers — a cacophony of sensationalism:

> "Here's this morning's New York Sewer!" cried one. "Here's this morning's New York Stabber! Here's the New York Family Spy! Here's the New York Private Listener! Here's the New York Peeper! Here's the New York Plunderer! Here's the New York Keyhole Reporter! Here's the New York Rowdy Journal! Here's all the New York papers!"
>
> (016-003)

American democracy, as Dickens portrays it, begins with electoral violence and ends with newsboys hawking the story:

> Some trifling excitement prevailed upon the very brink and margin of the land of liberty; for an alderman had been elected the day before; and Party Feeling naturally running rather high on such an exciting occasion, the friends of the disappointed candidate had found it necessary to assert the great principles of Purity of Election and Freedom of Opinion by breaking a few legs and arms.
>
> (016-002)
