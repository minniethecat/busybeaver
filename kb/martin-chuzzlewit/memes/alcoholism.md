# Alcoholism

Alcoholism runs through Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* as a recurring motif of moral weakness, social comedy, and self-deception. From Sairey Gamp's famous bottle "on the chimley-piece" (025-001) to the "flowing mugs of beer" (020-001) that celebrate landfall, drinking punctuates the novel at every social level — as private vice, public ritual, and comic spectacle. Dickens treats alcoholism with his characteristic blend of humor and moral seriousness: the drunkard is ridiculous but also pitiable, and the social tolerance of drunkenness is an indictment of the world that produces it.

## Overview

Alcohol in *Martin Chuzzlewit* serves multiple narrative functions. It provides comic material — Mrs. Gamp's drinking is the engine of her most memorable dialogue. It marks moral weakness — characters who drink excessively are generally those whose self-control is deficient in other areas. And it creates moments of social commentary, as when the funeral industry lubricates itself with "generous port" (019-001) or when the American boardinghouse scene depicts national drinking habits.

What distinguishes Dickens's treatment from simpler temperance literature is his refusal to reduce alcoholism to a single moral. Mrs. Gamp is not condemned for her drinking — she is celebrated for it, in the sense that her drinking produces the verbal richness that makes her the novel's most vital character. At the same time, the narrative does not glamorize drunkenness: Mrs. Gamp's incompetence as a nurse is directly linked to her intoxication, and the patients in her care suffer for it (025-032).

## In the Novel

### Mrs. Gamp's Bottle

Mrs. Gamp's relationship with alcohol defines her character. Her invention of "Mrs. Harris" is partly a strategy for testifying to her own sobriety — "Mrs. Harris," she constantly asserts, can vouch for her temperance, precisely because she knows her actual drinking would horrify any real witness. Her instruction that the bottle should be left where she can "put my lips to it when I am so dispoged" (025-001) is a masterpiece of alcoholic logic: the passive construction ("let me put my lips to it") pretends that drinking is something that happens to her rather than something she does.

### The Funeral Trade

Mr. Mould the undertaker conducts business "with a glass of generous port between his eye and the light" (019-001). The detail is telling — the funeral industry, like nursing, is lubricated by drink. Mould's port is a class marker (port rather than gin) but also a moral one: he profits from death while comfortably insulated from its reality by alcohol.

### The Atlantic Crossing

Mark Tapley's determination to remain "jolly" under adverse conditions is aided by drink. When the passengers land in America, they celebrate with "certain flowing mugs of beer, as only men just landed from the sea can revel in the generous dainties of the earth" (020-001). Drinking here is convivial, celebratory, and entirely acceptable — a reminder that Dickens's treatment of alcohol is nuanced rather than prohibitionist.

### Betsy Prig's Accusation

The rupture between Mrs. Gamp and Betsey Prig turns on an "ungenerous allusion" to Mrs. Gamp's drinking (040-001). Prig violates the unspoken code of their partnership by naming what both know to be true — that Mrs. Gamp drinks on duty. The scene is comic but also exposes the fragile social contract that enables alcoholism to persist unchallenged: everyone knows, but no one says.

## Key Passages

> "Leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged." (025-001)

This sentence is the novel's definitive statement on alcoholic self-deception. Every clause performs a contradiction: the request not to be offered drink is itself an admission that drink will be wanted; the passive "let me put my lips to it" disclaims agency while securing access.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens's treatment of alcoholism balances comedy and critique. The comedy is in the verbal inventiveness that drinking enables — Mrs. Gamp's speech is never more vivid than when she is "dispoged." The critique is in what drinking conceals: the suffering of patients, the corruption of institutions, the self-deception of the drinker.

This balance distinguishes *Martin Chuzzlewit* from the temperance literature that would become prominent later in the Victorian period. Dickens is not arguing for prohibition but for honesty — the problem is not alcohol itself but the lies people tell themselves and others about their relationship with it.
