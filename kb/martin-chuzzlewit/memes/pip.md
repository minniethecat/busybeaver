# Pip

Mr. Pip is a theatrical gentleman and raconteur who appears in a single scene of the novel — the dinner party at which [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] introduces [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] to his circle of London acquaintances. Along with [[wolf|Mr. Wolf]], Pip represents the disreputable-but-entertaining company that Tigg cultivates: men whose chief accomplishments are drinking, swearing, and telling stories about aristocrats they claim to know.

## The Dinner Scene

Tigg introduces Pip to Jonas as "theatrical man — capital man to know — oh, capital man!" (028-017). Pip's conversation consists almost entirely of anecdotes in which he figures as the intimate of lords and dukes. His signature story involves being appealed to by a Duke to settle a bet: "'Damme!' said the Duke, 'I appeal to Pip then. Come, Pip. Bandy or not bandy? Speak out!'" (028-040). The story's punchline — that Pip provided the decisive testimony and was thereafter welcomed into fashionable society — is a transparent exercise in self-aggrandisement.

Pip punctuates his speech with "a frightful oath" at every opportunity, a verbal tic that Dickens uses to mark his vulgarity even as Pip aspires to gentility (028-019)(028-021)(028-046). His theatrical profession is never specified beyond Tigg's label; he is, like many of Tigg's associates, a man defined more by manner than by substance.

## Narrative Function

Pip and [[wolf|Wolf]] serve a single narrative purpose: to demonstrate the quality of the company that [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] is entering through his association with [[montague-tigg|Tigg]]. The dinner scene is a miniature portrait of moral squalor disguised as worldly sophistication, and Pip's swaggering anecdotes constitute a form of corruption — not financial but spiritual, the corruption of believing that acquaintance with the great confers greatness.
