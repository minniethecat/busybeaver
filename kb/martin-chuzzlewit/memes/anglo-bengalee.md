# The Anglo-Bengalee

The Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company is [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg's]] fraudulent financial enterprise, established in the City of London as the vehicle for his spectacular (and brief) ascent from petty sponger to company chairman. More than a plot device, the Anglo-Bengalee is Dickens's most concentrated satire of Victorian commercial culture: a world in which appearance *is* capital, and "respectability" can be conjured from plate glass, stucco, and an exceptionally large red waistcoat.

## The Architecture of Deception

### Physical Splendor

The company's offices are described in a bravura catalogue of material excess that is also a catalogue of moral emptiness:

> The main offices in a new street in the City, comprising the upper part of a spacious house resplendent in stucco and plate-glass, with wire-blinds in all the windows, and "Anglo-Bengalee" worked into the pattern of every one of them.
>
> (027-001)

Dickens piles detail upon detail with mounting comic energy: "newly plastered, newly painted, newly papered, newly countered, newly floor-clothed, newly tabled, newly chaired, newly fitted up in every way, with goods that were substantial and expensive, and designed (like the company) to last" (027-001). The parenthetical "(like the company)" is the knife-thrust: everything is designed to *look* durable, and nothing is.

### The Porter's Waistcoat

The company's greatest asset is its porter, a man whose only qualification is a vast red waistcoat that "carried more conviction to the minds of sceptics than the whole establishment without him":

> It was a red waistcoat, and it was a very red waistcoat; and it was the reddest waistcoat, that was ever seen. In all the breadth of England there was not another waistcoat to match it.
>
> (027-019)

### The Name as Weapon

The company's very name is its sharpest instrument. The compound "Anglo-Bengalee," with its hyphenated grandeur, is "conjured from the cloudland of Tigg's invention" (027-021). Dickens multiplies the satiric force of this verbal architecture:

> It was a name of magic; a name that was a spell; a name that possessed a strange and mysterious power, and conjured up visions of Oriental magnificence.
>
> (027-023)

## The Mechanics of Fraud

### The "Board"

The Anglo-Bengalee's directors are the supreme expression of Dickens's theme that in commercial society, appearance is everything:

> As for the directors, they existed in the person of a single individual, who filled up the Board with dummies; having a mere nominal existence in the books, and possessing no other substance than a brass plate on the door and a hat in the passage.
>
> (027-049)

### David Crimple as Instrument

[[david-crimple]] serves as the company's secretary and bookkeeper, "a cadaverous, respectably-dressed, and very silent man, with a habit of shaving himself all over" (027-051).

### The Speculative Mechanism

The company's business model rests on the same paradox that animated all Victorian speculation:

> The Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company started into existence one morning; not an infant, but a giant: and went into business, flourishing its documents as though they were the records of a thousand years.
>
> (027-056)

## Tigg's Transformation

### From Sponger to Chairman

Montague Tigg undergoes a spectacular metamorphosis with the company's founding:

> Mr. Tigg had suddenly risen into a new element. The Honourable Mr. Tigg — for he wrote himself "The Honourable" on all occasions now; and somehow or other the title seemed to fit him, as titles sometimes do — was the Chairman, and the Master Spirit, of the Anglo-Bengalee.
>
> (027-065)

### The Philosophy of Credit

Tigg articulates the era's most cynical economic philosophy:

> "Credit, David," said Tigg, "is the true philosopher's stone. It is credit that turns everything to gold."
>
> (027-073)

## The Inevitable Collapse

Dickens provides his readers with the moral arithmetic of fraud from the outset:

> But the Anglo-Bengalee had been established on a principle that was sure to lead to ruin. It was a bubble; and bubbles always burst.
>
> (027-081)

## The Anglo-Bengalee as Dickensian Symbol

Dickens had been a parliamentary reporter and a court reporter before he became a novelist; he knew the mechanics of speculation, bankruptcy, and credit from the inside. The Anglo-Bengalee is his attempt to anatomize that machinery for a popular audience. It belongs to the tradition of satirical joint-stock companies in eighteenth- and nineteenth-century literature — Swift's projectors in the Academy of Lagado, Pope's South Sea Bubble satires, the fraudulent companies that crowd the margins of Smollett and Fielding — but Dickens gives the convention a distinctively Victorian form.
