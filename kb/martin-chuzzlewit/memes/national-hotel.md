# National Hotel

The National Hotel is the principal lodging-house of the raw American frontier settlement where [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit]] and [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] pause on their journey to Eden. Dickens's description of the hotel is a concentrated dose of the American chapters' satire: democratic naming, primitive conditions, and the gulf between rhetoric and reality.

## Description

The hotel announces itself with characteristic American grandiosity: "a large, whitewashed, irregularly-built house, which had a piece of wood nailed on a post before the hospital, on which was painted 'National Hotel'" (021-088).

> Close to the railway was an immense white edifice, like an ugly hospital, on which was painted "National Hotel."
>
> (021-088)

The signboard — painted wood nailed to a post — is the material equivalent of American "tall talk": a grand name for a crude reality. The hotel's proximity to the hospital is a characteristic Dickensian joke about the quality of its accommodations.

Inside, the National Hotel offers "tea, coffee, dried meats, and other substantials for travellers" (021-090). The dining arrangements, in which boarders "both married and single, resided at the National Hotel" (021-090), create the conditions for the public encounters that structure Martin's American experience. The public room where Martin first meets his fellow boarders is "within rifle-shot of the National Hotel" (021-110) — the frontier violence implicit in the geographical reference quietly underscoring the civility the hotel purports to represent.

## Role in the Plot

The National Hotel is where Martin receives the crucial letter from [[bevan|Mr. Bevan]] warning him against the Eden land scheme, and where [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]]'s characteristic good humour begins to crack under the strain of American life. It is at the hotel that [[captain-kedgick|Captain Kedgick]], the landlord, presents Martin to the local citizens as a celebrated "stranger from the old country" — a scene that exposes the hotel's function as a theatre for American self-display.

The hotel also becomes the site of Martin's dawning disillusionment: "he went down the road towards the National Hotel, but now his blighted prospects, and uncertain future, thronged upon his mind" (021-175). The National Hotel is the last place of relative comfort before Eden — which makes it, in retrospect, a paradise by comparison.
