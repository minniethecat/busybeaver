Mr. Bevan is an [[america|America]]n gentleman whom [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit meets during his travels. Unlike the caricatured [[america|America]]ns satirized in the novel, Bevan represents the genuinely decent, intelligent, and [[honour|honourable]] [[america|America]]n—a counterpoint to figures like [[elijah-pogram]] and [[zedekiah-scadder]] (021-001).

## Overview

Mr. Bevan is described as a cultivated, sensible man who treats [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] with genuine kindness. He is one of the "several gentlemen passengers, of the same stamp" who provide [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] relief from the intellectual entanglements of [[mrs-hominy]]. Dickens uses Bevan to demonstrate that [[america|America]] is capable of producing people of genuine "Intellect and Worth" (021-014).

## Biography

### Friendship with Martin

Bevan befriends [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] on the steamboat and during his time in [[new-york|New York]]. He exhibits "so much good sense and high feeling" that [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] finds himself genuinely liking and respecting him. Bevan's behavior leads [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] to reflect: "If this were a republic of Intellect and Worth, instead of vapouring and jobbing, they would not want the levers to keep it in motion" (021-014). Bevan also acts as Martin's guide to New York, showing him the city's public buildings and institutions (017-064) — a gesture of hospitality that contrasts with the commercial predation Martin encounters from men like Scadder.

### Land and Practical Counsel

When Martin expresses his architectural ambitions, Bevan offers practical questions rather than flattery. "Had he bought land?" asked Mr. Bevan (017-034) — the question of a practical man who understands that American success is built on property, not pretension. Bevan's involuntary reaction to Martin's domestic-architecture scheme — his face growing "infinitely longer" — reveals an honesty that Martin, despite his pride, cannot help but trust.

### The Honourable Exception

Bevan serves an important structural function in the novel: he is the [[honour|honourable]] exception that proves the rule of Dickens's [[america|America]]n [[comedy|satire]]. By including a genuinely decent [[america|America]]n character, Dickens avoids the charge of blanket prejudice while maintaining his critique of [[america|America]]n political and social institutions.

## Character Analysis

Bevan represents the ideal of the [[america|America]]n gentleman—educated, sensible, kind, and free of the bombastic patriotism that characterizes figures like Pogram. He is not a comic figure; he is treated with genuine respect. His presence in the novel adds moral complexity to the [[america|America]]n episodes and suggests that Dickens's [[comedy|satire]] is directed at institutional failures rather than national character.

## Quotations

Bevan's quiet honesty is established in his first substantial interaction with Martin, whose ill-conceived scheme to practise architecture in America meets with the American's involuntary candour:

> It would not have been very difficult for anyone to see; but it was particularly easy for Martin, whose perceptions were sharpened by his circumstances, to discern; that the stranger's face grew infinitely longer as the domestic-architecture project was developed. Nor, although he made a great effort to be as encouraging as possible, could he prevent his head from shaking once involuntarily, as if it said in the vulgar tongue, upon its own account, "No go!"
>
> (017-022)
