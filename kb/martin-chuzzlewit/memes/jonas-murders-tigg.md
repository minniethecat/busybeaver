# The Murder of Montague Tigg

Jonas Chuzzlewit murders the blackmailer Montague Tigg in a solitary wood, a crime that drives the novel's final movement toward justice.

## Overview

The murder of Montague Tigg is the dark climax of the Jonas Chuzzlewit plot, the novel's most sustained treatment of crime and guilt. Tigg, having reinvented himself as the chairman of the fraudulent Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company, has been blackmailing Jonas over his role in his father Anthony's death. When Jonas can no longer pay, Tigg threatens exposure. Jonas resolves on murder, travelling from London under cover of night, fashioning a crude club from a fence stake, and ambushing Tigg as he walks home alone through a wood after dining with Pecksniff (047-019). The killing is rendered with extraordinary atmospheric detail: the twilight wood, the departing sunlight, and the sudden violence that leaves "the body of a murdered man" lying "among the last year's leaves of oak and beech" (047-039).

## Prelude

Jonas Chuzzlewit's descent toward murder is charted across many chapters. Having married Mercy Pecksniff and subjected her to domestic cruelty, Jonas lived in constant fear that his role in hastening his father's death would be discovered. Montague Tigg, who had known Jonas in his earlier shabby incarnation, obtained damning information about the death of Anthony Chuzzlewit and used it to extract money. Jonas, already depleted by the Anglo-Bengalee's collapse and unable to meet Tigg's demands, saw murder as his only exit. On the appointed day, Jonas travelled from London, wrestling with his conscience in a series of troubled dreams (047-008). He shaped a "thick, hard, knotted stake" from a fence, peeling the bark and fashioning "its jagged head into a sort of handle" (047-019).

## The Event

The murder is set against an evening of deceptive beauty. As the sun set, "the glory of the departing sun was on his face. The music of the birds was in his ears. Sweet wild flowers bloomed about him" (047-032). Tigg, having dined with Pecksniff at Salisbury, chose the lonely footpath through the wood despite feeling an unaccountable dread. He "looked round, once, upon the evening prospect, sorrowfully" before descending into the hollow where the wood waited (047-033).

The wood itself is rendered as a place of gathering darkness: "the last rays of the sun were shining in, aslant, making a path of golden light along the stems and branches" that "began to die away, yielding gently to the twilight that came creeping on" (047-035). Tigg entered and disappeared among the trees. Shortly afterwards, Jonas came "leaping out" from the other side, "as if it were a hell" (047-038). The body lay "in one thick solitary spot... among the last year's leaves of oak and beech, just as it had fallen headlong down" (047-039).

Jonas, fleeing, felt no remorse—only terror. "He was not sorry for what he had done. He was frightened when he thought of it... but he was not sorry" (047-041). He caught a coach back to London, plagued throughout the journey by the fear that every stranger knew his crime.

## Aftermath

The murder sets the detective machinery of the novel in motion. Nadgett, the secretive investigator employed by the Anglo-Bengalee, has been watching Jonas and gradually pieces together the evidence. The discovery of Tigg's body leads to an inquest, and suspicion slowly tightens around Jonas. His behaviour becomes increasingly erratic, and the murder becomes the lever by which old Martin Chuzzlewit and his allies orchestrate Jonas's downfall. The crime also provides the moral counterweight to the novel's satire of selfishness: Jonas's murderous selfishness represents the logical endpoint of the Chuzzlewit family curse taken to its darkest extreme.

## Narrative Function

The murder is the narrative's descent into genuine evil—a shift from satirical comedy to gothic crime. It demonstrates that the "love of self" which old Martin diagnoses as the family curse is not merely a comic failing but a potentially lethal force. The murder also introduces the novel's detective plot, with Nadgett's patient investigation providing the mechanism through which justice ultimately catches up with Jonas.
