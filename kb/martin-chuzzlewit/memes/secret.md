Secrets, surveillance, and eavesdropping form a recurring structural motif in *Martin Chuzzlewit*, serving both as a plot mechanism — driving revelations and confrontations — and as a moral index: characters who spy are invariably exposed as hypocrites, while those with genuine secrets (young Martin and Mary's love) practise honourable concealment.

## Pecksniff at the Keyhole

The novel's quintessential surveillance scene occurs when Mr. Pecksniff, visiting the Blue Dragon to attend to the mysterious "invalid gentleman" (old Martin Chuzzlewit in disguise), is caught at the keyhole. He goes upstairs "purposing, in the fervour of his affectionate zeal, to apply his ear once more to the keyhole, and quiet his mind by assuring himself that the hard-hearted patient was going on well" (004-036). The scene turns to farce when Pecksniff, stooping to peer through the blocked keyhole, brings his head "into such violent contact with another head" (004-036) — that of the shabby-genteel stranger Tigg Montague, who is eavesdropping from the other side.

This episode crystallises the novel's equation of surveillance with moral degradation: Pecksniff's spying is dressed in the language of "affectionate zeal" but exposed as base curiosity. The physical collision of two eavesdroppers at the same keyhole renders the act absurd.

## Old Martin's Strategic Secrecy

Old Martin Chuzzlewit's concealment of his true intentions constitutes the novel's master secret. He travels in disguise, observes his relatives' behaviour from concealment, and withholds his approval from young Martin — all as a test of character. His secrecy is distinguished from Pecksniff's spying by its benevolent purpose: "in secret you have already done so" (004-009), Pecksniff insinuates of old Martin's provision for his grandson, and he is correct.

The revelation scene late in the novel, where old Martin unmasks Pecksniff before the assembled family, represents the dramatic endpoint of this strategic secrecy — the moment when hidden truths become public.

## Tom Pinch's Discovery

Tom Pinch's accidental witnessing of Pecksniff's true nature — overhearing him through a church window while playing the organ — is the novel's pivotal scene of revelation. Tom, seated in the organ loft, looks through the curtain and "peeped out" [LII] to see Pecksniff's hypocrisy exposed. This moment, where music and moral insight converge, transforms Tom from passive dupe to active moral agent.

> Before he had listened half a dozen seconds, he became as broad awake as ever he had been in all his life. With eyes, and ears, and mouth, wide open, he moved himself a very little with the utmost caution, and gathering the curtain in his hand, peeped out.
> (052-071)

## Honourable Secrets: Martin and Mary

In contrast to the furtive surveillance practised by Pecksniff and others, the love between young Martin and Mary Graham is a secret kept for honourable reasons — her dependent position in old Martin's household makes open declaration impossible. Their meetings in the garden, "a short walk that was least exposed to observation" (014-007), represent the legitimate private sphere that the novel distinguishes from guilty concealment.

## Jonas Chuzzlewit's Criminal Secrecy

Jonas Chuzzlewit's plot to murder his father (018-007), and later his attempt on Tigg Montague (047-010), represent secrecy in its most criminal form. Whereas old Martin's concealment is benign and Pecksniff's is hypocritical, Jonas's secrecy is murderous — the logical terminus of the novel's anatomy of hidden intentions.
