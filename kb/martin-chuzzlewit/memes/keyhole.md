# The Keyhole

The keyhole is one of the most persistent and revealing comic motifs in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. Across nine separate episodes spanning the length of the novel, characters (most prominently [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]]) apply ear or eye to a keyhole to spy, eavesdrop, or gather intelligence. The motif is the material correlative of Pecksniff's moral condition: a man who presents a façade of benevolence to the world while conducting his real business through apertures, in secret, gathering knowledge to be used as power.

## Pecksniff at the Keyhole

The keyhole is intimately associated with Pecksniff from his first appearance. In Chapter II, when the wind "came banging at the wicket" of Pecksniff's house, it is Pecksniff himself who "inquired through the keyhole in a shrill voice, which might have belonged to a wind-item too" (002-011). The image is established at once: Pecksniff does not open doors; he interrogates through them.

The motif reaches its fullest comic expression in Chapter IV, when Pecksniff, anxious about the health and intentions of his wealthy cousin [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin Chuzzlewit]], makes nightly visits to the Blue Dragon inn:

> It happened on the fourth evening, that Mr. Pecksniff walking, as usual, into the bar of the Dragon and finding no Mrs. Lupin there, went straight upstairs; purposing, in the fervour of his affectionate zeal, to apply his ear once more to the keyhole, and quiet his mind by assuring himself that the hard-hearted patient was going on well.
>
> (004-005)

The irony of "affectionate zeal" applied to an ear at a keyhole is the essence of Pecksniff's character—benevolence performed as surveillance, care expressed as intrusion.

## The Collision

The Chapter IV keyhole scene produces the novel's great comic collision. Pecksniff, "stooping hurriedly down to ascertain by personal inspection whether the jealousy of the old man had caused this keyhole to be stopped on the inside," brings his head into violent contact with another head—that of a stranger who, it emerges, has been commissioned by old Martin to guard his door. The stranger's accusation—"You were eavesdropping at that door, you vagabond!" (004-007)—is the plain truth that Pecksniff's elaborate rhetoric has been designed to conceal. The stranger himself, who "smelt like several damp umbrellas, a barrel of beer, a cask of warm brandy-and-water, and a small parlour-full of stale tobacco smoke, mixed" (004-005), introduces the world of [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] and [[chevy-slyme|Chevy Slyme]]—the novel's alternative mode of predation, less successful than Pecksniff's because it lacks his architectural hypocrisy.

> "I've been looking through that keyhole, with short intervals of rest, ever since nine o'clock this morning," the stranger (Tigg) later boasts to Pecksniff (004-043).

## Beyond Pecksniff

The keyhole motif extends beyond Pecksniff into the novel's wider architecture of secrecy and surveillance. In Chapter IX, Pecksniff himself is the object of the gaze when, during the chaotic supper party, a gentleman "called through the keyhole" (009-093). The watcher becomes the watched.

In Chapter XXVIII, the motif migrates to the relationship between [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] and his wife Mercy. Jonas "put his eye to the keyhole" (028-069) to spy on her, and later "still had his eye where the keyhole had been, when she opened it" (028-071). The keyhole here loses its comic character and becomes menacing—a prelude to Jonas's violence.

In the novel's final movement, the keyhole returns for the exposure of [[chevy-slyme|Slyme]], who "listened at the keyhole" (051-209) before creeping away—the last in the novel's long gallery of listeners-at-doors.

By Chapter XLVI, the motif has become so ingrained that even benign characters adopt it: "he might try to peep in through the keyhole" (046-193).

## Significance

The keyhole is more than a comic device. It embodies the novel's central concern with the gap between public profession and private conduct. Pecksniff's architecture—the house with its imposing façade and its "coach-house... kitchen-paved and whitewashed" (002-024)—reproduces in physical form the keyhole's logic: a respectable surface that conceals, and is penetrated by, illicit observation.

The motif also serves a structural function. Information in *Martin Chuzzlewit* rarely passes through open channels; it is gathered covertly, at keyholes, through doors left ajar. The novel's great revelations—[[old-martins-revelation|old Martin's exposure of Pecksniff]], [[toms-discovery|Tom Pinch's discovery of Pecksniff's true character]]—are the culmination of a narrative built upon observation without consent.
