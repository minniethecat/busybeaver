# Suspicion

Suspicion in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is the companion vice to selfishness — the defensive posture that the selfish adopt to protect themselves, which paradoxically leaves them more exposed. Dickens shows that those who suspect everyone become incapable of recognising genuine goodness, rendering themselves easy prey for the cunning.

## Overview

Suspicion runs through the novel as both a character trait and a narrative mechanism. Old Martin Chuzzlewit embodies it most fully: he "had withered in the shell of his suspicion and distrust for so many years, but to become the good man's tool and plaything" (030-091) (030-091). The irony is devastating — decades of guarded suspicion achieve precisely the opposite of their intended effect, delivering the suspicious man into the hands of the arch-hypocrite Mr. Pecksniff.

## In the Novel

The word "suspicion" appears at critical moments of moral blindness. Pecksniff himself weaponises the language of suspicion, telling Mrs. Lupin that her "suspicion... is very natural, and I have no doubt correct" (003-070) (003-070) — a performance of shared vigilance that masks his own designs. The hostess observes that Mary Graham's "looks and manner almost disarm suspicion" (003-067) (003-067), highlighting how suspicion is the default posture in Pecksniff's world, a posture that must be actively disarmed by visible goodness.

Young Martin's journey also involves suspicion. When he encounters Mr. Tigg in a pawnbroker's shop, he "glanced at him hastily, not feeling quite free from a suspicion that this remark implied a doubt of the successful issue of his trip to America" (013-158) (013-158). His suspicion of Tigg is well-founded, but it also reflects his own insecurity — the proud young man's fear of being seen in reduced circumstances.

The novel's master theme is established in Chapter 52, where Old Martin delivers the moral summation: "Self; grasping, eager, narrow-ranging, overreaching self; with its long train of suspicions, lusts, deceits, and all their growing consequences; was the root of the vile tree" (052-002) (052-002). Suspicion is listed first among selfishness's consequences — it is the immediate fruit of the poisoned root.

### Key Passages

The tragedy of suspicion is most evident in Old Martin's relationship with Mary Graham. Chapter 24 reveals that Mary "had a suspicion that the old man regarded Jonas with anything but favour" (024-125) (024-125), yet her honest perception cannot penetrate the fog of Martin's own suspicion. John Westlock's observations are similarly thwarted: a scene in Chapter 29 "almost confirmed John in his suspicion" (029-083) (029-083) of Pecksniff's true character, but the confirmation comes too late to prevent the damage.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens treats suspicion as inherently self-defeating. The structure of the novel demonstrates that the truly dangerous characters — Pecksniff, Jonas — are those who *do not* provoke suspicion in their victims, while the genuinely good — Tom Pinch, Mary Graham — are often viewed with unwarranted doubt. This reversal is the novel's sharpest satirical comment: suspicion is not wisdom but a moral blindness that inverts the world.
