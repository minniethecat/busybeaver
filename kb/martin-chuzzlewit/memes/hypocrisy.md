# Hypocrisy

Hypocrisy is the moral engine of *Martin Chuzzlewit*, driving its plot and defining its most memorable character. Mr. Pecksniff is one of Dickens's greatest creations precisely because he is the most fully realised hypocrite in the novelist's canon — a man whose entire identity is constructed from the gap between profession and practice.

## Overview

The novel's interest in hypocrisy is announced early when Anthony Chuzzlewit warns Pecksniff: "don't you be a hypocrite" (004-093). The warning is ironic — Anthony himself is a hardened cynic — but it introduces the novel's central question: what does it mean to pretend to virtue while practising vice? Pecksniff's reply is disarming in its frankness: "I may be a Hypocrite... but I am not a Brute" (008-022). The capitalisation is Dickens's own, marking "Hypocrite" as a title Pecksniff almost wears with pride.

## In the Novel

Anthony Chuzzlewit provides the novel's cynical theory of hypocrisy: "Hypocrite! why, we are all hypocrites. We were all hypocrites t'other day... The only difference between you and the rest was — shall I tell you the difference between you and the rest now, Pecksniff?" (008-023). The uncompleted comparison is ominous: Pecksniff's hypocrisy is somehow worse than the universal kind. Later, Anthony tells his granddaughters: "You may overdo anything, my darlings. You may overdo even hypocrisy. Ask Jonas!" (011-098) — linking Pecksniff's overdone performance to Jonas's violent nature.

The novel's most penetrating analysis of hypocrisy comes in Chapter 44: "the more he was found out, the more hypocrisy he practised. Let him be discomfited in one quarter, and he refreshed and recompensed himself by carrying the war into another" (044-002). This is the Pecksniffian paradox: exposure does not produce shame but redoubled effort. Hypocrisy is not a fixed mask but a regenerative strategy.

Mary Graham delivers the single-word diagnosis — "Of hypocrisy" (030-124) — when asked what Pecksniff has done. The brevity is devastating: an entire moral character reduced to one word by the novel's clearest-sighted character.

### Key Passages

The climactic unmasking in Chapter 52 uses the word as a verbal weapon: "Listen, hypocrite! Listen, smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave!" Old Martin cries, exposing Pecksniff before the assembled family (052-083). The old man continues: "I could not tell how far your specious hypocrisy had deceived him, knave; and knew no better way of opening his eyes than by presenting you before him in your own servile character" (052-090). The unmasking is the novel's moral climax — hypocrisy finally stripped of its disguise.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens treats hypocrisy not as a simple lie but as a performance that reshapes reality. Pecksniff does not merely say virtuous things while doing vicious things — he *believes* his own performance. His "cheerfulness of conscience" (004-097) is not (or not only) a lie but a psychological achievement: the successful hypocrite is one who has convinced himself. This is what makes Pecksniff more than a stock villain — he is a study in self-deception as the deepest form of hypocrisy.

Anthony's observation that "we are all hypocrites" (008-023) also suggests a broader social critique. The novel extends the charge of hypocrisy beyond Pecksniff to American boosters, fraudulent businessmen, and the social conventions that reward performative virtue.
