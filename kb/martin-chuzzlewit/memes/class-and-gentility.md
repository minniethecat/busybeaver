## Overview

Class and gentility in *Martin Chuzzlewit* are objects of relentless Dickensian satire. The novel dissects the pretensions of those who claim social superiority — from Pecksniff's architectural posturing to the Norris family's American gentility — and exposes the hollowness beneath the performance. Gentility, in Dickens's treatment, is not a quality of character but a costume: worn to impress, deployed to deceive, and invariably revealed as fraudulent under pressure (P03-003).

The novel's critique of gentility is inseparable from its critique of [[selfishness]]. The genteel characters share a common strategy: they use the language and manners of refinement to mask self-interest. Pecksniff speaks of duty and morality while scheming for inheritance (002-027); the American gentry speak of equality and enlightenment while practising the most rigid social exclusion (017-070); the Chuzzlewit relatives perform grief and concern while calculating their shares. Gentility is selfishness dressed for dinner.

## Pecksniff: The Architecture of Gentility

Seth Pecksniff is the novel's supreme study in the weaponization of gentility. His profession — architecture — is itself a metaphor: he designs facades for buildings while constructing a facade of moral respectability for himself. His speech is a paste jewel of moral platitudes; his manners are a lesson in calculated deference. Every "my dear sir" and "my young friend" is a transaction, calibrated to extract advantage from the listener.

Pecksniff's gentility is most dangerous because it is most convincing. Unlike the crude selfishness of Jonas or the transparent fraud of Tigg, Pecksniff's performance is good enough to deceive those predisposed to trust — most tragically, Tom Pinch. The novel's exposure of Pecksniff is accordingly the most thorough: not just his schemes but his entire moral architecture must be demolished, leaving him denounced as a "smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave" (052-083) before those he has wronged.

## American Gentility

The American chapters extend the novel's critique of gentility to a national scale. The Norris family, the Watertoast Association, and the various American worthies who host Martin all perform versions of gentility that are, if anything, more transparently fraudulent than their English counterparts precisely because they are so strenuously asserted. The claim that America has no arbitrary distinctions of rank is belied by the obsessive social ranking that pervades every interaction (017-070).

Mrs. Hominy, the "mother of the modern Gracchi" (023-015), is Dickens's most concentrated satire of American intellectual pretension — a woman whose literary reputation rests on the thinnest of accomplishments and whose gentility is indistinguishable from self-promotion. The American chapters argue that gentility, far from being a European import, flourishes in the democratic republic precisely because status anxiety is so acute. As General Choke observes, the "limited diffusion of a moral sense" (017-088) is, in the American view, Britain's misfortune — a judgment the novel quietly reverses upon its speakers.

## The Norris Family

The Norris family — Mr. Norris the father, Mrs. Norris the mother, the Misses Norris, and Mr. Norris Junior — are the novel's fullest portrait of American gentility in action. They know "all the great dukes, lords, viscounts, marquesses, duchesses, knights, and baronets, quite affectionately" (017-069), yet in the same breath they "enlarged upon the inestimable advantage of having no such arbitrary distinctions in that enlightened land" (017-070). The contradiction is not hypocrisy in the simple sense — the Norrises appear genuinely unaware of it — but a structural feature of a society that claims equality while practising hierarchy.

The Norrises' treatment of race exposes the limits of their enlightenment. They are "abolitionists" in name (017-071), yet find Negroes "such a funny people, so excessively ludicrous in their manners and appearance, that it was wholly impossible for those who knew them well, to associate any serious ideas with such a very absurd part of the creation." Dickens's point is devastating: gentility without moral substance is not merely empty but actively cruel, providing a vocabulary of refinement that rationalizes brutality.

## Gentility and Morality

The novel's final judgment on gentility is that it is not merely irrelevant to moral character but actively corrosive of it. The genuinely good characters — Tom Pinch, Ruth Pinch, Mark Tapley, John Westlock — are notably ungenteel. Tom is a poor organist with a "scanty coat" (005-004); Ruth is a governess in a brass and copper founder's family (009-036); Mark is a servant; John is a plain-spoken lawyer. None performs gentility because none needs to: their worth is in their conduct, not their manners.

Old Martin Chuzzlewit's final speech makes this point explicit. The novel's resolution does not elevate the good characters into gentility but rather dismantles the pretensions of the genteel, redistributing wealth and affection to those whose moral worth has been demonstrated through action. The Pecksniffs are exposed, the Chuzzlewit relatives are dismissed, and the small circle of genuine friendship that closes the novel is notable for its complete indifference to class.
