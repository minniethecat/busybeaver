## Overview

Courtship in *Martin Chuzzlewit* serves as both a plot mechanism and a satirical lens through which Dickens examines human selfishness, social ambition, and genuine affection. The novel presents several courtship arcs — some mercenary, some sincere — that illuminate the moral character of those involved. What distinguishes the novel's treatment of courtship is its insistence that the manner of choosing a spouse reveals character more decisively than almost any other action: how a person loves is how a person *is*.

## Courtship Arcs

### Martin and Mary

The central romance pairs young Martin Chuzzlewit with Mary Graham, his grandfather's companion. Their attachment forms despite old Martin's opposition — and, more importantly, despite Martin's own immaturity. Mary is introduced as old Martin's paid companion, a position that renders her financially dependent and socially vulnerable. (004-004) Young Martin's interest begins as something close to rebellion against his grandfather's authority, but deepens through suffering:

> "Although I had conducted myself from the first with the utmost circumspection," pursued Martin, "I had not managed matters so well but that my grandfather, who is full of jealousy and distrust, suspected me of loving her."
>
> (006-078)

The American interlude is crucial: Martin's foolishness in the Eden scheme, his illness, and his recovery under Mark Tapley's care transform him from a self-regarding young man into someone capable of genuine devotion. Mary, for her part, remains steadfast through the long separation. Their eventual marriage represents the novel's ideal of love founded on mutual respect and tested through adversity, rather than financial calculation.

### Augustus and Mercy

The courtship between Augustus Moddle and Mercy Pecksniff presents a darker satire of the marriage market. (037-044) Augustus, perpetually melancholic, pursues Mercy in a state of passive despair — as if courtship were something that happened *to* him rather than something he *did*:

> He was engaged to Mercy Pecksniff, having proposed to her — or rather, having been accepted by her — in a state of profound melancholy. He was the last of a large stake of young gentlemen, who had all been got rid of but him. (paraphrased, [[ch27|Chapter 27]])

The metaphor is commercial: suitors are a "stake" (a wager or stock), "got rid of" like surplus inventory. Augustus's passivity is the reductio ad absurdum of the novel's critique — courtship without volition, marriage without desire, a man who drifts into matrimony as if carried by a current.

Mercy's acceptance of Augustus is equally hollow. Having been schooled by her father in the arts of strategic affection, she treats Augustus as she has been taught to treat all men: as a means to an end. Their engagement dramatizes the comic-horrific consequences when courtship proceeds without genuine feeling or clear volition — each party waiting for the other to supply the passion neither possesses.

### Ruth and John

Ruth Pinch and John Westlock's romance offers the novel's most hopeful union. Introduced as Tom Pinch's devoted sister, Ruth embodies the domestic virtues the novel celebrates: modesty, warmth, and uncalculating affection. (009-054) John Westlock, Tom's friend, recognizes these qualities immediately. Their relationship develops through shared kindness and mutual recognition of character, providing a counterpoint to the scheming matches elsewhere in the book.

What distinguishes the Ruth-John courtship from the novel's other pairings is its *absence* of drama. There are no obstacles, no misunderstandings, no grand gestures. Their love is the quietest in the novel — and, Dickens implies, the most durable. It models a courtship conducted not as negotiation or performance but as the natural outgrowth of shared affection and compatible character.

### Jonas and Mercy / Charity

Jonas Chuzzlewit's pursuit of the Pecksniff sisters transforms courtship into open economic transaction. He evaluates Charity and Mercy as commodities, weighing their respective merits with the same calculating eye he brings to business (008-055):

> Jonas had an eye to business, and he thought that the elder sister was the better manager. But then the younger was the more agreeable, and on the whole he thought he would take *her*. (paraphrased, [[ch11|Chapter 11]])

The verb "take" is deliberate: Jonas does not woo or court — he acquires. His choice of Mercy, based on her plumpness and apparent tractability, leads to one of Dickens's most harrowing portrayals of marital misery. Mercy, frivolous and vain, is utterly unprepared for the brutality of Jonas's household; Jonas, having purchased a wife, treats her as property. The marriage becomes a prison for both parties — the logical endpoint of courtship conducted as commerce.

Charity, the rejected sister, provides the novel's most cutting portrait of the consequences of the marriage market. Having positioned herself as the sensible, managing sister — the "good investment" — she is passed over for her prettier sibling. Her bitterness curdles into permanent resentment: she ends the novel unmarried, her strategic virtues having failed to secure a buyer.

## Courtship as Social Commentary

Throughout the novel, Dickens uses courtship to expose:

- **Mercenary motives**: Characters treat marriage as a financial arrangement rather than a personal bond. Pecksniff encourages his daughters to "ensnare" wealthy husbands with the same techniques he uses to "ensnare" pupils' parents.
- **Selfishness**: Even apparently romantic gestures often conceal self-interest. Charity's attentiveness to Old Martin is courtship by proxy — a bid for inheritance disguised as filial devotion.
- **Class anxiety**: Courtship negotiations reveal anxieties about social standing. The Pecksniff sisters' marriage prospects are calibrated against their father's fluctuating fortunes, and Jonas's wealth makes him attractive despite his obvious brutality.

The contrast between sincere attachment (Martin-Mary, Tom-Ruth) and calculated matches (Jonas-Mercy, Pecksniff's designs) structures the moral argument of the novel. Love, Dickens insists, cannot be strategized — it can only be recognized, received, and returned.
