# Captain Kedgick

Captain Kedgick is the landlord of the National Hotel in the raw American settlement where [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit]] and [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] arrive on their journey westward. Like many of Dickens's American characters, Kedgick is a study in the gulf between democratic rhetoric and commercial reality: his elaborate hospitality to Martin is revealed, on closer inspection, to be a carefully managed investment in his hotel's reputation.

## Role in the Novel

Kedgick first appears when Martin, having travelled through the American interior, arrives at the settlement where the [[national-hotel|National Hotel]] stands. The narrator describes "a large, whitewashed, irregularly-built house, which had a piece of wood nailed on a post before the hospital, on which was painted 'National Hotel'" (021-088). Kedgick, as landlord, "kindly" takes charge of Martin's correspondence — "through this correspondence when Captain Kedgick, the landlord, kindly came up to see him" (022-008). The adverb is telling: Kedgick's kindness is the American hospitality industry's version of [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s moralizing, a performance that serves commercial ends.

Kedgick's most revealing moment comes when he presents Martin to the local citizens as a celebrity. He "put a thumb and three fingers to either side of the brim of his hat, and swept it off" (022-026) — a gesture of elaborate deference that precedes his introduction of Martin to assembled worthies eager to meet "a stranger from the old country." The scene is a miniature of the American chapters' satire: democratic manners concealing a transaction, the guest transformed into an attraction.

## Character Analysis

Kedgick is not a villain — he is merely a businessman for whom hospitality is inventory. The Captain's title, like many American honorifics in Dickens's satire, signifies nothing in particular: it is part of the "tall talk" that permeates the American chapters, a linguistic inflation that parallels the speculative bubble of the Eden land scheme. When Martin later returns from Eden, Kedgick is still at his post, still extending the same calculated welcome to new arrivals (034-074). He is a fixed point in the American landscape, as immutable and as hollow as the National Hotel's painted sign.
