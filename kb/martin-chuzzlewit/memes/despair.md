# Despair

Despair in *Martin Chuzzlewit* operates across a wide tonal range, from the comic to the genuinely terrifying. Dickens deploys the word in situations of absurd domestic frustration, in the bleak landscape of the American journey, and — most significantly — in the psychological depths of Jonas Chuzzlewit's criminal mind, where "despair was in his very triumph and remorse" (051-122) (051-122).

## Overview

The novel registers despair at three distinct levels. At the comic level, Mr. Pecksniff is "reckless with despair" when Mrs. Todgers reminds him of his late wife (009-141) (009-141), and the youngest gentleman at Todgers's boarding house blows "his melancholy into a flute" in a "ballet of despair" (019-006) (019-006). At the existential level, the American landscape becomes "the grim domains of Giant Despair" — a Bunyanesque vision of a "flat morass, bestrewn with fallen timber" where "the very trees took the aspect of huge weeds, begotten of the slime from which they sprung" (023-035) (023-035). At the moral level, Jonas's despair is not a prelude to redemption but the final consolidation of his evil.

## In the Novel

Martin Chuzzlewit's return to England is framed through despair transcended: "From a painful journey, from a hard life, from a sickbed, from privation and distress, from gloom and disappointment, from almost hopelessness and despair, I have come back to you," he tells his grandfather (043-107) (043-107). Martin's despair is the honest consequence of suffering and poor choices, and his recovery from it is genuine.

Mark Tapley's account of his own despair is characteristically comic, yet structurally significant. Finding "all my hopeful wisions bein' crushed" and "no credit for me nowhere," he "abandons myself to despair" — and the resolution that follows is marriage to Mrs. Lupin and happiness (048-079) (048-079). In Tapley's hands, despair is a step toward the novel's most cheerful resolution.

The Chuzzlewit family council reaches its impasse "in utter despair and hopelessness," a satirical comment on the family's inability to cooperate even when their inheritance depends on it (004-078) (004-078). John Westlock, baffled by the mystery of the visitor to Tom, speaks "in quiet despair" (039-105) (039-105) — the word serving as a register of honest perplexity rather than moral failure.

### Key Passages

The journey to Eden transforms despair into landscape: the travellers "might have entered, in the body, on the grim domains of Giant Despair" as the scene becomes a "flat morass, bestrewn with fallen timber; a marsh on which the good growth of the earth seemed to have been wrecked and cast away" (023-035) (023-035). The Bunyan allusion elevates the American satire into something more ominous.

The novel's most devastating deployment of despair comes in Chapter 51, as Jonas confronts his exposure: "despair was in his very triumph and remorse" (051-122) (051-122). The phrase collapses opposites — triumph into despair, despair into remorse — capturing the psychological chaos of a man who has lost all ground.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens's comic despair — Pecksniff's theatrical anguish, the bird-cage "ballet of despair" (019-006) (019-006), the gentleman of a vocal turn who is "head mute, or chief mourner" in a comic funeral (011-130) (011-130) — functions as a release valve, deflating the emotion's gravity through absurdity. The shift to genuine despair in the American chapters and, finally, in Jonas's disintegration, demonstrates the novel's tonal range.
