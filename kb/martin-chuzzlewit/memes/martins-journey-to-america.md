# Martin's Journey to America

Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger's journey to America occupies roughly a quarter of the novel (Chapters XIII through XXIII) and constitutes its most sustained departure from the English plot. What begins as a desperate flight from poverty and wounded pride becomes a satirical anatomy of American society and—more importantly—the moral education of [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin himself]], who arrives in the New World as a selfish young man and returns, chastened by experience, capable of the growth that the novel's resolution requires.

## Background

After his quarrel with [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin]] and his expulsion from [[pecksniff-house|Mr. Pecksniff's house]], young Martin finds himself without prospects. His grandfather has disinherited him; [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]], the woman he loves, is bound to Old Martin's household. With [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] as his voluntary companion, Martin resolves to seek his fortune in America—the land, as the promotional literature promises, of boundless opportunity. (013-005)

The decision is itself a moral act: Martin could remain in England, reconcile with his grandfather, and accept the modest position his abilities would earn. Instead, pride drives him across the Atlantic. Mark Tapley's motive—to find a situation "jolly" enough to test his philosophy—is, characteristically, the purer one.

## The Atlantic Crossing

The voyage aboard the steamship *Screw* introduces the novel's satirical method for the American chapters: a gallery of national types, each a caricature of American manners and democratic pretension. (013-010) The passengers include:

- **Colonel Diver**, editor of the *New York Rowdy Journal*, who exemplifies American journalism's fusion of bombast and defamation
- **General Choke**, a militia general and land speculator who will prove central to Martin's undoing
- **Mr. Jefferson Brick**, the *Rowdy Journal's* war correspondent, a diminutive figure whose martial rhetoric is comically disproportionate
- **Zephaniah Scadder**, the Eden land agent whose physical appearance—"one eye stood stock still in his head, and the other winked"—emblematizes his moral character (021-005)

Each American type encounters Martin's English assumptions and exposes their limitations. The satire is mutual: Americans are crude, boastful, and hypocritical about their democratic principles; Martin is proud, naïve, and insufferably English.

## New York

In New York, Martin encounters the *Rowdy Journal* and its culture of journalistic character assassination. Colonel Diver and Jefferson Brick embody a press that has substituted vituperation for truth—"the rowdiest" paper in "the rowdiest city on the face of the 'arth." (016-001) Dickens's satire of American journalism (written after his own unhappy 1842 American tour) is savage and specific.

Martin's attempt to establish himself as an architect fails: without capital, connections, or—crucially—humility, he cannot find work. (017-020) His English assumptions about class and profession have no purchase in a society that claims (however dishonestly) to have abolished such distinctions.

## The Eden Disaster

General Choke persuades Martin to invest his remaining capital in land at a new settlement called [[eden|Eden]], on the Mississippi. (021-030) The name is a bitter joke: Eden is a malarial swamp in which nothing flourishes except disease. The promotional illustrations show a thriving town with broad avenues and handsome buildings; the reality is "a few rotten logs" and a handful of shacks: (023-005)

Martin and Mark arrive to find a desolate fever-swamp where settlers—those who survive—live in conditions of squalor and despair. (023-030) Both men contract malaria and nearly die. Mark Tapley's philosophy of jolliness receives its ultimate test as he nurses Martin through his illness, maintaining cheerfulness in circumstances that would crush any ordinary spirit. (023-045)

The Eden episode strips Martin of everything: his money, his health, his pride, his illusions about America, and—most importantly—his illusions about himself. The journey that began as an assertion of independence ends in utter dependence on Mark Tapley's selfless nursing. This is Martin's turning point.

## Return and Transformation

Martin returns to England a changed man. (033-001) The experience has taught him the insufficiency of pride and the value of the qualities he previously scorned—Mark's good-humour, [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch's]] loyalty, Mary Graham's steadfastness. His reconciliation with Old Martin, when it comes, is credible because the journey to America has done the moral work that makes reconciliation possible.

## Thematic Significance

The American interlude serves three functions:

1. **Satirical**: It allows Dickens to anatomize American society with the same moral lens he applies to England—commercial fraud, inflated rhetoric, the gap between principles and practice
2. **Structural**: It removes Martin from the English plot at the moment when his presence would complicate the developing intrigue around Old Martin's fortune
3. **Psychological**: It is the crucible in which Martin's character is tested and transformed—the necessary suffering that precedes moral growth
