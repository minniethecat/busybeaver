## Overview

Friendship in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is the novel's primary moral counterweight to selfishness. Against the scheming relatives, fraudulent businessmen, and self-serving hypocrites who dominate the plot, Dickens sets a small constellation of genuine friendships: Tom Pinch and John Westlock, Mark Tapley and Martin Chuzzlewit, Ruth Pinch and her brother. These relationships are characterized by loyalty without expectation of return, honesty without calculation, and affection without ulterior motive — precisely the qualities the novel's villains lack.

The novel's treatment of friendship is deliberately unsentimental. Tom Pinch's devotion to Pecksniff, which he mistakes for friendship, is exposed as exploitation. Martin's treatment of Mark, which he mistakes for the natural order of master and servant, must be unlearned through suffering (021-104). True friendship, the novel argues, is not instinctive but achieved — it requires the moral clarity to distinguish genuine affection from self-interested attachment.

## Tom Pinch and John Westlock

The friendship between Tom Pinch and John Westlock is the novel's moral benchmark. Westlock, a former Pecksniff pupil, recognizes Tom's exploitation long before Tom himself does — and his recognition is delivered with a bluntness that is itself an act of friendship. Westlock tells Tom plainly: "I was right and you were wrong. That's small matter; you'll be wiser another time" (002-091). His exasperation is a form of affection: he is angry *for* Tom, not *at* him.

John Westlock's testimony to Tom's character is the novel's most direct praise of friendship: "he had never had even a peevish word with Tom during the whole term of their residence in Mr. Pecksniff's house" (012-065). And Westlock adds: "If one man ever had a profound knowledge of another, and saw him in a true light, and in his own proper colours, Tom has that knowledge of Mr. Pecksniff" (012-104) — an irony, since Tom's knowledge is precisely what blinds him to Pecksniff's true nature.

When Westlock inherits a fortune, his first impulse is not self-indulgence but generosity toward Tom — a pattern he repeats throughout the novel, culminating in the secret arrangement of Tom's professional independence through Mr. Fips (039-128). Westlock's friendship is practical: it does not merely sympathize with Tom's exploitation but works to end it.

Tom's side of the friendship is defined by a loyalty so complete it borders on blindness. He cannot see Pecksniff's fraud because his capacity for devotion — the same capacity that makes him a faithful friend — renders him incapable of imagining duplicity in those he serves. His moral education is the novel's central narrative arc: learning to redirect his loyalty from the undeserving (Pecksniff) to the deserving (Westlock, Martin, Ruth).

The novel makes clear that this friendship is a model, not an exception. Westlock's marriage to Ruth Pinch — arranged with Tom's joyful blessing — transforms friendship into kinship, suggesting that the bonds of affection, not the bonds of blood, are what truly constitute family in Dickens's moral universe. Tom's friendship with Westlock, channeled through Ruth, becomes the foundation of a new household — a household organized around freely chosen love rather than inherited obligation.

## Mark Tapley's Devotion

Mark Tapley's friendship with Martin Chuzzlewit is tested by adversity in a way that Tom's is not. Mark follows Martin to America not from naive admiration — he sees Martin's flaws clearly — but from a conviction that cheerful service in difficult circumstances is its own reward. His philosophy of "coming out strong" under hardship is a distinctly Dickensian heroism: not grand gestures but daily endurance, not noble speeches but uncomplaining labor.

Mark articulates his philosophy as a determination to find credit in being jolly precisely when conditions make cheerfulness most difficult: "Any man may be in good spirits and good temper when he's well dressed. There an't much credit in that" (005-029). His boyhood habit of seeking out "trying situations for a man's jollity" reflects a lifelong commitment to cheerful endurance (007-041).

What makes Mark's friendship radical is its asymmetry: he expects nothing from Martin in return. His service is genuinely disinterested — a word the novel's commercial world has corrupted but which Mark restores to its moral meaning. Martin, for his part, must learn to receive friendship as a gift rather than a transaction. The turning point comes when Martin declares to Mark: "We are no longer master and servant, but friends and partners" (021-104).

In the Eden episode, Mark's friendship proves literally life-saving. When Martin falls ill with fever in the malarial swamp, Mark nurses him at risk to his own health (033-023). The scene inverts their social hierarchy: the servant becomes the protector, the gentleman the dependent (033-115). This inversion is the novel's argument about friendship: it is measured not by station but by conduct, not by profession but by sacrifice. Mark later acknowledges Martin's transformation: "ever since that time when I was floored in Eden, you have showed" a new considerateness (043-042). Under the pressure of genuine friendship, Martin makes a solemn resolution: "selfishness was in his breast, and must be rooted out" (033-125).

## False Friendship

Against these genuine friendships, the novel sets a gallery of counterfeits. Mr. Pecksniff's every pronouncement of affection is strategic — his "friendship" is a tool of manipulation, deployed with the same hollow eloquence as his architectural rhetoric. The Chuzzlewit relatives, gathered around Old Martin's supposed deathbed, perform grief they do not feel, their concern calibrated precisely to their hoped-for share of the inheritance. Montague Tigg's hail-fellow-well-met bonhomie masks predatory intent: his hand on your shoulder is always feeling for your wallet. Even Jonas Chuzzlewit's relationship with his father is a travesty of filial devotion, poisoned by greed from the start.

These counterfeit friendships share a common feature: they treat relationships as transactions. Pecksniff offers affection in exchange for advantage; Tigg offers camaraderie in exchange for investment; the relatives offer concern in exchange for inheritance. The novel's moral arithmetic is clear — friendship that calculates is not friendship at all. It belongs to the same commercial logic that governs Pecksniff's architecture and Tigg's insurance: value created from confidence, sustained by deception, and destined for collapse.

The novel's closing pages return to friendship as its final image of moral order. Tom Pinch, writing at his organ, surrounded by the small circle of those who love him freely, embodies what the novel's villains cannot achieve: a life organized around affection rather than advantage. The image is deliberately modest — no fortune, no fame, no grand reconciliation — because genuine friendship, Dickens insists, needs nothing more than itself.

## Quotations

John Westlock's blunt honesty — the hallmark of true friendship — after Pecksniff's daughters dismiss Tom Pinch:

> "Bah!" cried John Westlock, with the utmost disgust and disdain the monosyllable is capable of expressing. "Ladies, good evening. Come, Pinch, it's not worth thinking of. I was right and you were wrong. That's small matter; you'll be wiser another time."
>
> (002-091)

Pecksniff's counterfeit friendship, draped in the language of piety, stands as the novel's definitive portrait of false affection:

> "And Thomas Pinch and I," said Mr. Pecksniff, in a deeper voice, "will walk it yet, in mutual faithfulness and friendship! And if it comes to pass that either of us be run over in any of those busy crossings which divide the streets of life, the other will convey him to the hospital in Hope, and sit beside his bed in Bounty!"
>
> (005-130)
