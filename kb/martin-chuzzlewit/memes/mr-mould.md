# Mr. Mould

The undertaker in *[[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit*, a "little elderly gentleman, bald, and in a suit of black" whose professional performance of grief is perpetually at odds with his commercial satisfaction at a lucrative funeral.

## Overview

Mr. Mould is introduced when [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]], seeking to insinuate himself into the affairs of a dying man, follows a recommendation from "Mr. Mould the undertaker"(011-015). Mould's appearance is a masterpiece of Dickensian caricature: "a little elderly gentleman, bald, and in a suit of black; with a notebook in his hand, a massive gold watch-chain dangling from his fob, and a face in which a queer attempt at melancholy was at odds with a smirk of satisfaction; so that he looked as a man might, who, in the very act of smacking his lips over choice old wine, tried to make believe it was physic"(011-018). This single sentence captures the novel's critique of professions that convert human suffering into revenue — the undertaker who must perform sorrow while privately reckoning profit.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Mr. Mould is a fixture of the commercial world that Dickens anatomizes. His [[commerce|trade]] — undertaking — places him at the intersection of death and commerce, a position he navigates with professional smoothness. His "massive gold watch-chain"(011-018) signals prosperity; his "queer attempt at melancholy" signals the performance required by his [[commerce|trade]].

### Role in the Plot

Mould's primary function is to facilitate [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s access to the house of the dying [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]]. [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] presents himself as a paragon of disinterested benevolence, claiming that "a gentleman is dead; and some person being wanted in the house, you have been recommended by Mr. Mould the undertaker"(011-015). Mould, ever the professional enabler, provides the introduction.

His most characteristic moment comes when he praises the filial [[love|devotion]] of the Chuzzlewit relations — in terms that cannot fail to please [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], who is busy performing exactly such [[love|devotion]]: "anything so filial as this; anything so [[honour|honourable]] to human nature; so calculated to reconcile all of us to the world we live in; never yet came under my [[surveillance|observation]]"(011-025). The irony is complete: Mould's praise of "filial" virtue is delivered by a man who profits from death, to a man whose "filial" [[love|devotion]] is an elaborate [[deception|fraud]]. "It only proves, sir, what was so forcibly observed by the lamented theatrical poet — buried at Stratford — that there is good in everything"(011-025). The reference to Shakespeare as "the lamented theatrical poet — buried at Stratford" is pure Mould: even the greatest poet in the language is framed through the lens of his burial.

Mould reappears in connection with [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]], the novel's other death-adjacent professional. When [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] is offered a nursing engagement that would compete with her duties to Mould, she protests: "I will go to Mr. Mould... and ast him, if you like"(019-010). The undertaker-[[nursing|nurse]] network — Mould provides the bodies, Gamp provides the bedside attendance — is one of Dickens's most savage depictions of a commercial ecology built around death.

### Fate & Resolution

Mr. Mould continues his [[commerce|trade]], unaltered by the novel's events. His function is thematic rather than narrative: he represents a world in which even death has been commodified.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Mould is a minor character who punches far above his narrative weight. The image of the undertaker who "looked as a man might, who, in the very act of smacking his lips over choice old wine, tried to make believe it was physic"(011-018) is one of Dickens's most economically devastating satirical portraits. In a single sentence, it captures the contradiction at the heart of commercialized mourning: the undertaker must perform grief while enjoying profit, and the tension between the two is visible on his face.

Mould's professional rhetoric — his praise of "filial" [[love|devotion]], his citation of Shakespeare as "the lamented theatrical poet — buried at Stratford" — is a masterclass in the language of false sentiment. He speaks the vocabulary of genuine emotion while using it to lubricate commercial transactions. In this, he is the novel's purest example of [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]ian discourse operating in a specific [[commerce|trade]]: the words are warm, the motive is cold.

His connection to [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] extends the [[comedy|satire]] into a networked critique. Mould, Gamp, and the [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee Company]] form a loose confederation of enterprises that profit from human vulnerability — death, sickness, and financial [[despair|desperation]] respectively.
