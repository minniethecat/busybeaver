## Finding a Home in Islington

Tom Pinch, newly independent after breaking with Pecksniff, asks his sister Ruth: "Should you think Islington a good place?" (036-181). The question marks a turning point in the novel — Tom and Ruth, who have been dependents all their lives, are choosing a home of their own. Ruth's joyful recollection that "it used to be called Merry Islington, once upon a time" (036-183) imbues the suburban district with a quality of cheerful anticipation. "Where is Islington? We can't do better than go there tomorrow" (036-185) becomes their shared resolution — a rare moment of agency for characters who have spent the novel being acted upon.

## Tom Pinch's Islington

Dickens invests Islington with Tom Pinch's characteristic warmth. When Tom and Ruth explore the district, "Tom rubbed his hands as if all Islington were his" (036-190). The gesture is comic but touching — Tom, who has so little, takes such innocent pleasure in his modest surroundings that the district itself seems to belong to him by right of appreciation. Islington becomes, in the novel's closing movement, a geography of earned contentment.

## A Place of Domestic Happiness

The Islington home that Tom and Ruth establish represents the novel's ideal of domesticity. It is modest — "a few wrong turns" are taken coming from Islington (037-032) — but it is theirs. Ruth keeps house with the same cheerful competence she brings to everything, and Tom walks daily from Islington to the Temple (040-026, 039-166), bridging the domestic and professional spheres. The Islington chapters are among the novel's sunniest — a reward for characters who have earned their happiness through patience and goodness.

## Merry Islington

The epithet "Merry Islington" (036-183) captures the district's traditional reputation as a place of rural retreat and entertainment for Londoners. By Dickens's time, Islington was being absorbed into the expanding metropolis, but the novel preserves something of its village character — a quality that suits Tom and Ruth's unworldly natures. Islington in Martin Chuzzlewit is less a geographical fact than an emotional state: the distillation of modest, hard-won domestic happiness.

## Notable Quotes

Tom and Ruth plan their future, and a suburb becomes a promise:

> "It used to be called Merry Islington, once upon a time," said Tom. "Perhaps it's merry now; if so, it's all the better. Eh?"
>
> (036-183)
