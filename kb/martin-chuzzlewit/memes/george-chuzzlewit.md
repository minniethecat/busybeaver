# George Chuzzlewit

A bachelor cousin of the Chuzzlewit clan whose defining characteristics — a tendency to corpulence, a taste for overindulgence, and a talent for acerbic retort — make him one of the most vividly sketched members of the extended Chuzzlewit family that assembles to dispute [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s inheritance.

## Overview

George Chuzzlewit appears primarily in the great family-gathering scene of Chapter IV, where the assembled Chuzzlewit relations descend upon [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]]'s house to contest [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit's favour. Dickens introduces him in a cascade of comic physical description: he "claimed to be young but had been younger, and was inclined to corpulency, and rather overfed himself; to that extent, indeed, that his eyes were strained in their sockets, as if with constant surprise; and he had such an obvious disposition to pimples, that the bright spots on his cravat, the rich pattern on his waistcoat, and even his glittering trinkets, seemed to have broken out upon him, and not to have come into existence comfortably"(004-005). This portrait — the overfed body, the surprised eyes, the eruptive accessories — establishes George as a figure of pure comic excess.

## Biography

### Background & Family

George Chuzzlewit is one of the numerous cousins, nephews, and collateral relations who circle [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit's fortune like moths around a flame. His precise genealogical connection to the main Chuzzlewit line is unspecified, but he enjoys the privileges of family membership — including the right to attend gatherings, make claims, and [[commerce|trade]] insults with the other relations. He is a bachelor of independent, if not lavish, means.

### Role in the Plot

George's most memorable moment comes during the family gathering at [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s house. When the discussion turns to the absent [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s rumoured preference for raising a foundling as his heir, George suggests — with spectacular inappropriateness — that [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] might select "swans"(004-030). This absurd interjection earns him a sharp rebuke from the strong-minded widow: "If Mr. George Chuzzlewit has anything to say to *me*... I beg him to speak out like a man; and not to look at me and my daughters as if he could eat us"(004-035).

George's response is one of the novel's finest comic rejoinders: "At all events, if I was a cannibal... I think it would occur to me that a lady who had outlived three husbands, and suffered so very little from their loss, must be most uncommonly tough"(004-040). The line is cruel, witty, and perfectly judged — it punctures the widow's self-importance while acknowledging, in backhanded fashion, her formidable survival skills.

George reappears briefly toward the novel's end, attending gatherings at the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] household and providing minor comic relief: at one point, he "preferred anything to the aggravation of sitting near the breakfast, without being able to eat it"(052-015), a line that confirms his defining trait has survived the narrative unchanged.

### Fate & Resolution

George Chuzzlewit's fate is deliberately understated. He remains on the periphery of the Chuzzlewit family, neither rewarded nor punished — simply present, corpulent, and occasionally witty. His role is not to develop but to decorate; he is one of Dickens's "characters in a crowd" who give the Chuzzlewit gatherings their texture of competitive [[hospitality|host]]ility.

## Character Analysis

George Chuzzlewit is what Dickens's contemporary George Henry Lewes criticized as a "humour" character — a figure defined by a single exaggerated trait rather than by psychological depth. But within Dickens's comic method, this is not a limitation but a technique. George's corpulence, his strained eyes, his eruptive pimples, and his appetite are all externalizations of the family's larger moral condition: the Chuzzlewits are people who consume — [[commerce|money]], food, attention, each other — without ever being satisfied.

His single great moment — the "uncommonly tough" retort — has a structural function that exceeds mere comedy. In a gathering where everyone is performing virtue they do not possess, George's willingness to say something genuinely cutting, without [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]ian pretense, gives him a perverse kind of honesty. He is selfish, greedy, and gluttonous, but he does not pretend otherwise, and this makes him, in the novel's moral calculus, less objectionable than [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]].

The physical description that opens his introduction is one of Dickens's most concentrated feats of comic portraiture. The "bright spots on his cravat, the rich pattern on his waistcoat, and even his glittering trinkets" that "seemed to have broken out upon him"(004-005) are a small masterpiece of metaphoric economy — they make George's sartorial choices into symptoms of the same excessive, eruptive nature that produces his pimples.
