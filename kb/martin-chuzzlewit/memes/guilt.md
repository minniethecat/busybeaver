# Guilt

Guilt in *Martin Chuzzlewit* straddles the legal and the psychological, as Dickens traces Jonas Chuzzlewit's descent from financial fraud to murder and the guilty consciousness that follows. The novel distinguishes between the *fact* of guilt (the crime itself) and the *feeling* of guilt (the tortured awareness of wrongdoing), showing that Jonas experiences only the latter — and even that, as fear rather than moral recognition.

## Overview

The novel deploys "guilt" and "guilty" to mark both external and internal states. Externally, characters are suspected, accused, and ultimately confronted with the question: "Are you guilty?" (051-203) (051-203). Internally, "it was a part of his guilty state of mind" to remember a fatal detail that might expose the crime (051-003) (051-003). Tom Pinch, by contrast, when accused of impropriety by Pecksniff, shows "certainly no fear or guilt" — his innocence is legible on his face (031-091) (031-091). The juxtaposition defines the novel's moral contrast: Jonas's guilty mind betrays itself in every gesture; Tom's innocence needs no defence.

## In the Novel

The language of guilt gathers around Jonas like a contagion. The rhetorical questions of Chapter 47 animate a world that senses his guilt before it is known: "Did no men passing through the dim streets shrink without knowing why, when he came stealing up behind them?" and "had no child in its sleep an indistinct perception of a guilty shadow falling on its bed?" (047-002) (047-002). The passage is a gothic set-piece that transforms guilt into an almost supernatural emanation.

When Jonas is confronted with his crime in Chapter 51, "an irrepressible exclamation burst from the lips of Jonas, as Lewsome entered at the door" — a sound "wholly unlike any sound that had ever fallen on the ears of those who heard it," marking the "sharp and terrible expression of what was working in his guilty breast" (051-077) (051-077). Guilt here is not a concept but a physical eruption.

John Westlock, piecing together the evidence, speaks of "the great probability of the guilt of Jonas being known to other people, who traded in it for their own benefit" (048-131) (048-131) — guilt as a commodity in the novel's economy of blackmail and corruption. Martin, learning of Jonas's crime, is torn between the desire to expose it and the fear that "he would seem... to grasp at this unnatural charge against his relative, and using it as a stepping-stone to his grandfather's favour" (048-134) (048-134).

### Key Passages

The novel's analysis of guilt reaches its peak in the Chapter 47 passage: "Did no men passing through the dim streets shrink without knowing why... As he glided on, had no child in its sleep an indistinct perception of a guilty shadow falling on its bed... Did no dog howl, and strive to break its rattling chain, that it might tear him?" (047-002) (047-002). The triple rhetorical question creates a world alive to guilt, even when its source is hidden.

Tom Pinch's exoneration in Chapter 31 is the novel's counter-image: "No physiognomist that ever dwelt on earth could have construed Tom's face when he heard these words. Wonder was in it, and a mild look of reproach, but certainly no fear or guilt" (031-091) (031-091).

The novel's retrospective framing in Chapter 46 — "If the name could have passed his lips... he would have been saved from the commission of a Guilty Deed, then drawing on towards its black accomplishment" (046-145) (046-145) — suggests that guilt is preventable if the right word is spoken at the right moment.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The Spottletoe outburst — "how it is that everyone who comes into contact with this family is guilty of some gross insult to it" (054-063) (054-063) — extends guilt into the comic register, satirising the Chuzzlewit family's addiction to grievance. The family that suspects everyone else of guilt is itself guilty of the selfishness that breeds crime.
