## The Setting

The Temple is one of London's ancient Inns of Court, the historic legal district between Fleet Street and the Thames. In Martin Chuzzlewit, it provides the setting for Tom Pinch's mysterious employment and serves as a space of quiet purposefulness that contrasts with Pecksniff's rural fraudulence. Mr. Fips declares simply: "The place is in the Temple" (039-132), initiating Tom into the novel's most significant London location.

## Tom Pinch at the Temple

Tom Pinch's removal to the Temple marks the beginning of his independent life after breaking with Pecksniff. He meets Mr. Fips at "the Temple Gate in Fleet Street" (039-136) and is conducted to "uninhabited chambers in the Temple" (040-002) where his mysterious employer has established a quiet office. The Temple's "quiet courtyards" (040-003) become Tom's daily destination — a contrast to the noisy self-promotion of Pecksniff's establishment. Tom's "going to the Temple" (040-027) each day, while his sister Ruth keeps house, represents the modest but honest life that is Tom's reward.

## A Space of Resolution

The Temple is the setting for the novel's resolution. Old Martin Chuzzlewit's insistence that he "should always come out of the Temple by one way" past Tom's chambers (045-003) places the novel's climactic revelations in this legal quarter. The narrator imagines that "the Temple fountain might have leaped up twenty feet to greet the spring of hopeful maidenhood" (045-004), transforming the sober legal setting into a space of emotional renewal and happy endings.

## Symbolic Significance

Dickens contrasts the Temple — a place of genuine professional work — with Pecksniff's fraudulent architectural practice. The legal chambers of the Temple, for all their dust and quiet, represent authentic labour rather than pretence. The novel draws a distinction between the "glass and stone" of modern buildings and the enduring substance of the ancient Temple (022-067), aligning moral worth with institutional solidity.

## Notable Quotes

The strange, enchanted atmosphere of Tom Pinch's daily labour at the Temple:

> There was a ghostly air about these uninhabited chambers in the Temple, and attending every circumstance of Tom's employment there, which had a strange charm in it. Every morning when he shut his door at Islington, he turned his face towards an atmosphere of unaccountable fascination, as surely as he turned it to the London smoke; and from that moment it thickened round and round him all day long, until the time arrived for going home again, and leaving it, like a motionless cloud, behind.
>
> (040-002)
