# Spottletoe

Mr. and Mrs. Spottletoe are Chuzzlewit relatives who appear among the swarm of family members descending upon the Dragon Inn in Salisbury when old [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Martin Chuzzlewit]] falls ill. Mrs. Spottletoe is "Chuzzlewit's own niece" and "was his favourite once" (004-049) — a fact that gives her husband an inexhaustible fund of righteous indignation. The Spottletoes are comic emblems of the grasping [[selfishness]] that Dickens diagnoses as the Chuzzlewit family disease: they care nothing for the old man's health, only for their place in his will.

## Character Portraits

Mr. Spottletoe is a masterpiece of Dickensian physical comedy: "so bald and had such big whiskers, that he seemed to have stopped his hair, by the sudden application of some powerful remedy, in the very act of falling off his head, and to have fastened it irrevocably on his face" (004-081). His whiskers, like his temper, are always in "a very portentous state" (004-084). He is constitutionally incapable of entering a room without immediate confrontation; his first remark to Pecksniff is an accusation: "you are assuming too much to yourself, sir" (004-084).

Mrs. Spottletoe is "much too slim for her years, and of a poetical constitution" (004-081). Where her husband rages, she simmers. Their marriage appears to be a union of complementary grievances: he provides the volume, she the persistence.

## Role in the Novel

The Spottletoes appear in two set-pieces that frame the novel's family satire. In Chapter IV, they are among the relatives who besiege the Dragon Inn after learning of old Martin's illness, each hoping to secure a favourable position in the will. Mr. Spottletoe leads the charge against Pecksniff, whom he correctly identifies as a schemer — though his own motives are no purer. When old Martin vanishes, Spottletoe bursts in with the news in a state of apocalyptic heat: "Gone while we were sitting here. Gone. Nobody knows where he's gone" (004-132).

In the novel's final chapter, the Spottletoes reappear at the wedding breakfast, where Mr. Spottletoe immediately resumes his career of indignation. Finding that the bridegroom has not yet arrived, he declares: "the man who aspires to join this family, and 'prefers not' to be introduced to its members, is an impertinent Puppy" (054-074). His capacity for taking offence is bottomless; his actual influence on events is nil. The Spottletoes are, in the novel's moral economy, what happens when family feeling is reduced entirely to financial expectation.

## Dickens's Treatment

The Spottletoes belong to the novel's larger portrait of the Chuzzlewit family as a collective organism animated by greed. Dickens assembles them — along with [[chevy-slyme|Chevy Slyme]], [[george-chuzzlewit|George Chuzzlewit]], the strong-minded woman, and the solitary female with the toothache — into a chorus of disappointed expectation. None of them gets what they want; the novel's moral logic demands that [[selfishness]] be its own punishment. Mr. Spottletoe's magnificent whiskers and magnificent rage are, in the end, self-consuming: he can neither influence old Martin nor prevent the redistribution of the inheritance to those who deserve it.

## Quotations

Mr. Spottletoe's physical appearance is rendered in one of Dickens's most memorable caricatures:

> First, there was Mr. Spottletoe, who was so bald and had such big whiskers, that he seemed to have stopped his hair, by the sudden application of some powerful remedy, in the very act of falling off his head, and to have fastened it irrevocably on his face.
>
> (004-081)

His combative nature emerges in his challenge to Pecksniff's self-importance:

> "I am sorry to interrupt you, Pecksniff," remarked Mr. Spottletoe, with his whiskers in a very portentous state; "but you are assuming too much to yourself, sir. Who do you imagine has it in contemplation to confer a distinction upon you, sir?"
>
> (004-084)
