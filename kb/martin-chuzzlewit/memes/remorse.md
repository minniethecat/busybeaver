# Remorse

Remorse in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is defined not by its presence but by its absence. Dickens constructs a moral universe in which the capacity for remorse is the test of a character's humanity. The novel's most disturbing figure, Jonas Chuzzlewit, is distinguished precisely by his total immunity to this feeling — not once does he feel genuine remorse, only fear for his own safety.

## Overview

The word "remorse" appears at critical structural points in the novel, almost always in contexts where it is *not* felt. Jonas, having committed murder, rides home "among those sentinels of God" without waking "to remorse, or to abandonment of his design" (047-007) (047-007). After the deed, "there was no more penitence or remorse within him now than there had been while the deed was brewing" (047-052) (047-052). Later, the narrator drives the point home: "It was no contrition or remorse for what he had done that moved him; it was nothing but alarm for his own security" (051-009) (051-009). Remorse, in Jonas's case, is the missing moral reflex that confirms his monstrosity.

## In the Novel

Dickens places Jonas's remorselessness in direct contrast to characters who can feel it. Martin Chuzzlewit, upon returning to England, speaks in "a tone that sounded like remorse" when reflecting on his mistreatment of Tom Pinch (043-066) (043-066). The difference is crucial: Martin's capacity for remorse is the sign of his redeemability. Ruth Pinch, with characteristic charity, imagines that even Charity Pecksniff "would have been full of remorse" for her treatment of Tom (046-051) (046-051).

Charity's own performance of remorse is one of the novel's satirical highlights. She describes her feelings in "a sort of snug and comfortable penitence" — an admission that "Looking back upon my giddy self, now that I am sobered down and made thoughtful, by treading on the very brink of matrimony... I shudder" (046-052) (046-052). Dickens exposes this as self-congratulation disguised as self-reproach, a counterfeit of genuine remorse that serves only to flatter the penitent.

### Key Passages

Jonas after the murder remains unmoved: "Still he was not sorry. No. He had hated the man too much, and had been bent, too desperately and too long, on setting himself free. If the thing could have come over again, he would have done it again" (047-052) (047-052). The narrator returns to this at the novel's close when Jonas confronts exposure: the man feels "no contrition or remorse... it was nothing but alarm for his own security" (051-009) (051-009). He experiences "a triumph yet, in the reflection" that the victim is dead — a psychological state that places him beyond the reach of moral feeling.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Charity Pecksniff's "snug and comfortable penitence" is Dickens's satire of shallow moral feeling — remorse as a social performance rather than a genuine change of heart (046-052) (046-052). The phrase captures the Pecksniffian tendency to turn every emotion, even contrition, into an occasion for self-approval. This contrasts with the genuine remorse Dickens associates with Martin's moral growth.
