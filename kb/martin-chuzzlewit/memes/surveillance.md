# Surveillance

Surveillance in Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* operates as both a plot mechanism and a thematic concern. The novel's most striking instance is Nadgett, the detective employed by Montague Tigg's Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company — a proto-detective figure whose patient, invisible watching anticipates the detective fiction that would emerge later in the Victorian period. Beyond Nadgett, surveillance pervades the novel at the social level: characters are constantly observed, assessed, and judged by others, and the fear of discovery drives much of the plot.

## Overview

Nadgett is one of Dickens's most intriguing minor creations — a man whose profession is to be unnoticed. He is described as someone whose appearance is impossible to recall: "He was the man in the corner; the man whom nobody ever saw; the man whom nobody ever remembered to have seen" (027-005). This invisibility is his professional asset (038-002) — he shadows Jonas Chuzzlewit, collects evidence of the murder plot, and ultimately provides the testimony that brings Jonas to justice.

Surveillance in the novel has a double nature. It is sinister when practiced by institutions — the Anglo-Bengalee is a surveillance operation disguised as a benevolent society — but it is redemptive when it serves justice. Nadgett's watching of Jonas is both creepy (he is a spy) and satisfying (he catches a murderer). Dickens does not resolve this tension, which gives the surveillance theme its unsettling power.

Social surveillance — the watching of reputation, the scrutiny of neighbors, the gossip of drawing-rooms — is a gentler but equally pervasive form of observation. Characters in *Martin Chuzzlewit* are rarely alone in any meaningful sense; they are always being watched, judged, and positioned within a social hierarchy that depends on constant mutual observation.

## In the Novel

### Nadgett: The Invisible Watcher

Nadgett is introduced as the Anglo-Bengalee's secret weapon — a man who "could find out anything" (027-005). His method is patience: he follows Jonas "through the streets, into places of public resort, to the doors of theatres, to the very threshold of his own home" (038-001), accumulating the evidence that will eventually destroy him.

Nadgett is a transitional figure in literary history — a proto-detective who predates the formal detective story. Unlike Sherlock Holmes (who would appear four decades later), Nadgett has no deductive genius — just "patience, foresight, and a habit of method." He is the detective as bureaucrat, and his effectiveness lies precisely in his lack of the dramatic qualities we associate with later fictional detectives.

### Social Surveillance

The novel's social world is structured by watching. Pecksniff is constantly performing for an audience, real or imagined. The Chuzzlewit family gathering (004-005) is an exercise in mutual surveillance — each relative watches the others for signs of favor or disfavor from Old Martin. Todgers's boarding house (008-001) is a theater of social observation where every gentleman's linen and habits are noted and discussed.

### The Anglo-Bengalee as Surveillance Institution

The Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company is itself a surveillance operation. Its business model depends on knowing more about its clients than they know about it — the information asymmetry that makes fraud possible. Tigg's transformation into Montague is a masterpiece of self-surveillance: he watches himself being watched and adjusts his performance accordingly.

## Key Passages

> "He was the man in the corner; the man whom nobody ever saw; the man whom nobody ever remembered to have seen." (027-005)

This description of Nadgett captures the essence of surveillance in its purest form — watching without being watched in return. The repetition ("nobody ever saw... nobody ever remembered") emphasizes the completeness of Nadgett's invisibility.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens's treatment of surveillance is ambivalent. Nadgett's watching of Jonas is narratively satisfying — the villain is caught — but the watcher himself is unsettling. The novel does not celebrate surveillance as a social good; it presents it as a fact of modern life, neither wholly benign nor wholly malign.

The American chapters extend this theme to the national level. The Americans boast of transparency and freedom, but their society — like the English one they mirror — is built on the anxious watching of appearances. The comedy of the *Rowdy Journal* scenes depends on the reader's awareness that everyone involved is performing for an audience they cannot see.
