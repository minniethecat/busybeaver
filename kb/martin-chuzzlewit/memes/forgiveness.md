# Forgiveness

Forgiveness is the most aggressively deployed virtue in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. The novel's opening chapters establish a pattern that holds throughout the narrative: characters who speak most fluently of forgiveness are those who have most to be forgiven for, and the act of "forgiving" becomes, in their hands, a form of domination rather than release.

## Overview

Dickens introduces the Pecksniffian doctrine of forgiveness in Chapter 2, where Mr. Pecksniff refuses to shake John Westlock's hand with a calmness "quite ethereal": "I have forgiven you. I had already forgiven you, even before you ceased to reproach and taunt me. I have embraced you in the spirit, John, which is better than shaking hands" (002-082). John's reply cuts to the heart of the matter: "As to your forgiveness, Mr. Pecksniff, I'll not have it upon such terms. I won't be forgiven" (002-085). This exchange establishes the novel's critique: forgiveness, when wielded as a weapon of moral superiority, is an insult, not a gift.

## In the Novel

Pecksniff's forgiveness is a power play. "You must. You can't help it. Forgiveness is a high quality; an exalted virtue; far above your control or influence, John. I will forgive you" (002-086). The formulation is brilliant in its aggression: by insisting on forgiveness, Pecksniff positions himself as morally superior while simultaneously denying John any agency in the matter. He forgives *against* the other person's will—a contradiction that exposes the whole performance.

The theatrical dimension is explicit: "Forgiveness, entire and pure forgiveness is not incompatible with a wounded heart; perchance when the heart is wounded, it becomes a greater virtue" (002-090). Pecksniff cannot forgive without also advertising his own injury. The wound and the forgiveness are two halves of the same self-dramatization.

By contrast, genuine forgiveness in the novel is almost wordless. When Martin encounters Tom Pinch in Chapter 52 and says simply "Oh, Tom! Dear Tom! I saw you, accidentally, coming here. Forgive me!" (052-109), Tom's response is instantaneous: "Forgive! I'll never forgive you as long as I live, Martin, if you say another syllable about it. Joy to you both! Joy, my dear fellow, fifty thousand times" (052-110). This is forgiveness without performance—it does not name itself, does not linger over its own virtue, does not extract tribute. It moves immediately to joy.

### Key Passages

Mary Graham's plea to Martin in Chapter 14 provides the novel's positive model of forgiveness. Speaking of Old Martin, she asks: "if you would at such a season resolve to think, but once a month, or even once a year, of him, or anyone who ever wronged you, you would forgive him in your heart, I know!" (014-079). Her formulation is everything Pecksniff's is not: quiet, private, addressed to the heart rather than performed for an audience.

In the novel's closing pages, Old Martin speaks to Mercy: "I judged you hastily. I fear I judged you cruelly. Let me know that I have your forgiveness" (054-020). Here, the asker of forgiveness places himself below the one he asks—the exact inversion of Pecksniffian logic.

Pecksniff gets the novel's last word on forgiveness—and it is a masterpiece of self-parody: "There is hardly any person present, Mr. Chuzzlewit, by whom I have not been deceived. I have forgiven those persons on the spot. That was my duty; and, of course, I have done it" (052-125). Even in defeat, he cannot stop forgiving. It is his addiction, his identity, his mask—and by this point, it fools no one.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The satire of forgiveness operates through sheer repetition. Each time Pecksniff deploys forgiveness-language—and he does so with the regularity of a liturgical response—the gap between the word and its meaning widens. Dickens's genius is to let Pecksniff incriminate himself through his own eloquence. The character's most damning trait is not that he forgives falsely but that he has made forgiveness itself suspect, contaminating the moral vocabulary so thoroughly that the reader learns to distrust any character who reaches for it.
