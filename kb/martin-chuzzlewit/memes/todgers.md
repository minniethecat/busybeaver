# Todgers's

Mrs. Todgers's Commercial Boarding-House, situated in a bewildering warren of lanes and byways near the Monument in the City of London. Dickens devotes an entire chapter to describing this establishment and its surroundings, making it one of the novel's most memorable comic set-pieces.

## Overview

Todgers's is introduced in Chapter 8 when [[seth-pecksniff|Mr. Pecksniff]] and his daughters arrive in London and make their way to this establishment, where Pecksniff had once been a lodger and -- as Mrs. Todgers delicately hints -- a potential suitor. The boarding-house serves as the London base for the Pecksniffs, a place where the daughters observe the commercial gentlemen lodgers from the roof-terrace, and where key plot developments involving [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] and his schemes unfold. Dickens returns to Todgers's in [[ch32|Chapter 32]], treating it as a recurring comic stage.


> Several fruit-brokers had their marts near Todgers's; and one of the first impressions wrought upon the stranger's senses was of oranges—of damaged oranges

## Description

Dickens's description of Todgers's and its neighbourhood is one of the great comic set-pieces of the novel. The boarding-house exists in a part of London so dense and obscure that "you couldn't walk about Todgers's neighbourhood, as you could in any other neighbourhood. You groped your way for an hour through lanes and byways, and courtyards, and passages; and you never once emerged upon anything that might be reasonably called a street" (009-003).

The streets around it are choked with damaged oranges "festering in boxes, or mouldering away in cellars," and ancient carved-oak doorways now used only as storehouses (009-004). The house itself has one notable feature: a staircase window whose view is so entirely blocked by neighbouring buildings that it offers only a prospect of brickwork and chimney-pots. The roof-terrace, with its "posts and fragments of rotten lines" and tea-chests full of forgotten plants, provides the young ladies with a vantage point for observing the commercial gentlemen below (009-008).

Inside, the establishment is run with fading gentility by the bony, hard-featured Mrs. Todgers, who nurses a sentimental attachment to the miniature portrait of her younger self "tacked up over the kettle-holder" ([[ch09|009-012--009-013]]). The commercial gentlemen lodgers contribute to a general atmosphere of dingy respectability and barely suppressed chaos.

## Inhabitants & Visitors

- [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]] -- Proprietress, a "bony and hard-featured lady" with a sentimental history involving Mr. Pecksniff (008-072)
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] -- Former lodger and would-be suitor; stays here with his daughters during London visits
- [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] -- Stays at Todgers's and uses the roof-terrace for observation and flirtation
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] -- Stays at Todgers's with her sister; her marriage to Jonas is partly negotiated from here
- [[bailey|Bailey]] -- The youthful porter, "of a playful temperament," who sketches figures on his corduroys with burnt firewood (009-010)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] -- Visits the Pecksniffs at Todgers's during his courtship of Mercy
- [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] -- Appears at Todgers's in connection with the Anglo-Bengalee scheme

## Events

- Pecksniff's arrival and reunion -- Mr. Pecksniff arrives with his daughters; Mrs. Todgers greets him with ecstatic reminiscence of his former attentions ([[ch08|008-073--008-085]])
- The roof-terrace panorama -- The Miss Pecksniffs survey the commercial gentlemen from the rooftop, a comic set-piece described in lavish Dickensian detail ([[ch09|009-008--009-010]])
- The courtship of Jonas and Mercy -- Jonas Chuzzlewit's pursuit of Mercy Pecksniff is conducted partly through visits to Todgers's
- Commercial gentlemen's dinner -- In Chapter 32 the boarding-house table becomes a stage for comic interplay among the lodgers (032-002)
- Charity's return -- After Mercy's marriage, Charity returns alone to Todgers's, a diminished figure in the same setting
