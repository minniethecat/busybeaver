# Eden

A fraudulent land "settlement" on the American frontier, marketed by the Eden Land Corporation as a thriving city but in reality a pestilential swamp. Martin Chuzzlewit purchases a "location" here during his American adventures, and the near-death experience he endures at Eden becomes the crucible of his moral reformation.

## Overview

Eden represents the darkest chapter of Martin's journey and Dickens's most savage satire of American land speculation. The settlement exists triumphantly on paper -- with maps, plans, and a smooth-talking agent named Scadder -- but in reality is a "hideous swamp" choked with slime and matted growth. When [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] and [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] arrive after an increasingly ominous river journey, they find not the bustling city of the prospectus but a handful of rotting log-huts whose inhabitants have either died of fever or fled. As Dickens writes in the chapter heading, the episode provides "Some account of Eden, as it appeared on paper" (021-001).

The stay at Eden nearly kills both men. Martin contracts the fever and is nursed back to health by Mark's tireless devotion. The experience strips Martin of his pride and selfishness -- "Eden was a hard school to learn so hard a lesson in; but there were teachers in the swamp and thicket, and the pestilential air, who had a searching method of their own" (033-124). It is the novel's darkest and most transformative setting.

## Description

Dickens's description of Eden is a masterpiece of anti-pastoral horror. The journey towards it is marked by increasing desolation: "the monotonous desolation of the scene increased to that degree, that for any redeeming feature it presented to their eyes, they might have entered, in the body, on the grim domains of Giant Despair" (023-035).

The arrival itself is devastating: "At last they stopped. At Eden too. The waters of the Deluge might have left it but a week before; so choked with slime and matted growth was the hideous swamp which bore that name" (023-036). The biblical irony is deliberate -- this Eden is no paradise but a post-diluvian wasteland. There are no wharves, no marketplace, no public buildings -- nothing but a few log-houses, "the best, a cowshed or a rude stable" (023-037).

The settlers they meet are hollow-eyed survivors of the fever. One man tells them his youngest child died the previous week; his eldest son lies wrapped in blankets with the chill. He warns them: the night air "is deadly poison" (023-048). Martin and Mark's own cabin is "a miserable cabin, rudely constructed of the trunks of trees; the door of which had either fallen down or been carried away long ago" (023-050).

## Inhabitants & Visitors

- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit Jr.]] -- Purchaser of the Eden "location"; nearly dies there of fever
- [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] -- Martin's companion and nurse; his "jolliness" is finally tested and proven in Eden's adversity
- The fever-wasted settler -- A nameless survivor who greets them with the hollow warning of what awaits
- [[hannibal-chollop|Mr. Chollop]] -- A violent "advocate of Lynch law" who appears in the later Eden chapters, embodying Dickens's critique of American frontier "freedom"

## Events

- The purchase -- Martin is persuaded by General Fladdock and the agent Mr. Scadder to invest in the Eden Land Corporation, buying on the strength of maps and promises rather than reality ([[ch21|021-076--021-110]])
- The journey downriver -- The steamboat voyage grows progressively more desolate as Martin and Mark approach their destination (023-035)
- The arrival -- The devastating first sight of Eden, "the hideous swamp which bore that name" (023-036)
- The fever -- Martin falls gravely ill; Mark nurses him with unwavering devotion, proving his philosophy of jolliness under adversity
- The recovery and escape -- Martin, transformed by his suffering, resolves to return to England and set right the wrongs he has committed (033-124)
- The later reckoning -- Back in England, Martin denounces the Eden Land Corporation before the American lecture audience: "Is the Eden Land Corporation... an Institution of America?" (034-042)
