## Character Description

Mr. Hannibal Chollop is introduced in the Eden episode of the novel's American section. He self-identifies as "fever-proof, and likewise agur" (033-046), a man of the frontier who claims immunity to the diseases that ravage the swamp settlement. In the parlance of his admirers, he is "a splendid sample of our na-tive raw material, sir" (033-066).

Chollop is heavily armed at all times. Dickens provides a satirical catalogue of his weaponry:

> He also carried, amongst other trinkets, a sword-stick, which he called his "Tickler," and a great knife, which (for he was a man of a pleasant turn of humour) he called "Ripper," in allusion to its usefulness as a means of ventilating the stomach of any adversary in a close contest.
>
> (033-066)

He also carries "a brace of revolving pistols in his coat pocket, with seven barrels apiece" (033-066), all in the name of what he calls "rational Liberty" (033-066).

## A Man of Violence

Chollop's record of violence is extensive and presented with ironic understatement. "He had used these weapons with distinguished effect in several instances, all duly chronicled in the newspapers; and was greatly beloved for the gallant manner in which he had 'jobbed out' the eye of one gentleman, as he was in the act of knocking at his own street-door" (033-066).

He openly boasts of killing a man over a political disagreement:

> "I shot him down, sir," pursued Chollop, "for asserting in the Spartan Portico, a tri-weekly journal, that the ancient Athenians went ahead of the present Locofoco Ticket."
>
> (033-072)

Dickens describes him as "a man of a roving disposition; and, in any less advanced community, might have been mistaken for a violent vagabond" (033-067). He moves from place to place, establishing newspapers which he then sells, "for the most part closing the bargain by challenging, stabbing, pistolling, or gouging the new editor, before he had quite taken possession of the property" (033-067).

## The Eden Encounter

Chollop appears at the crude dwelling in Eden where [[Mark Tapley]] and [[Martin Chuzzlewit Jr.]] are staying. The encounter begins with a dispute over spitting distance:

> "I re-quire, sir," said Hannibal, "two foot clear in a circ'lar di-rection, and can engage my-self toe keep within it. I have gone ten foot, in a circ'lar di-rection, but that was for a wager."
>
> (033-050)

Chollop holds a deep contempt for Europeans, repeatedly dismissing them with the sardonic refrain "Europian! Quite Europian!" (033-064). When Martin and Mark express dissatisfaction with the swamp settlement of [[Eden]], Chollop becomes menacing. He boasts of America's freedoms — "No stakes, no dungeons, no blocks, no racks, no scaffolds, no thumbscrews, no pikes, no pillories" — to which Mark retorts, "Nothing but rewolwers and bowie-knives. And what are they? Not worth mentioning!" (033-082)

## As Political Satire

Chollop embodies Dickens's satire of American hypocrisy — the violent reality beneath the rhetoric of liberty. The narrator notes: "He always introduced himself to strangers as a worshipper of Freedom; was the consistent advocate of Lynch law, and slavery; and invariably recommended, both in print and speech, the 'tarring and feathering' of any unpopular person who differed from himself" (033-068).

He calls this philosophy "planting the standard of civilization in the wilder gardens of My country" (033-068). When Mark speaks plainly to him, the narrator observes that "There is little doubt that Chollop would have planted this standard in Eden at Mark's expense, in return for his plainness of speech" (033-069), but for the desolation of the place and his own imminent departure.

Chollop departs Eden with companions named Ripper, Tickler, and others (033-108). He is later mentioned in Chapter XXXIV when Martin recounts the Eden ordeal, referring to "a gentleman named Chollop" who "happened in upon you during your illness" (034-043).
