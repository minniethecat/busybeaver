# Martin's Expulsion from Pecksniff's House

Young Martin is publicly denounced by Pecksniff and driven from his house, triggering his impulsive journey to America.

## Overview

The expulsion of young Martin Chuzzlewit from Mr. Pecksniff's house is the first major rupture in the novel's central relationships. After Martin's grandfather, old Martin Chuzzlewit, has placed him as a pupil under Pecksniff's roof, Pecksniff turns against his charge with calculated ruthlessness. Returning from London with his daughters, Pecksniff refuses even to acknowledge Martin's presence and, when confronted, denounces him as a deceitful reprobate who has "obtained admission" under false pretences (012-177). The scene marks Dickens's first full exposure of Pecksniff's hypocrisy while simultaneously revealing young Martin's prideful stubbornness. Martin's angry response—comparing Pecksniff to "the very last and worst among the vermin of the world"—shows the headstrong impulsiveness that will be tested through the American chapters (012-183).

## Prelude

Martin had been residing at Pecksniff's as an architectural pupil, growing close to Tom Pinch and developing a condescending but genuine affection for him. The two had just returned from a convivial dinner with John Westlock in Salisbury, where Westlock had frankly warned Martin about Pecksniff's true character, calling him "the most consummate scoundrel on the face of the earth" (012-085). Martin, though claiming he already disliked Pecksniff, was not prepared for what awaited him. Pecksniff, meanwhile, had recently quarrelled with old Martin Chuzzlewit and had been humiliated by having his designs on the family fortune exposed. The old man's withdrawal of favour appears to have prompted Pecksniff to retaliate against young Martin as a proxy target, dressing up his spite in the language of wounded virtue (012-177).

## The Event

On a dark winter's morning, Martin and Tom rose at half-past four to meet Pecksniff's coach. When the coach arrived, Pecksniff refused to acknowledge Martin at all—standing with his back toward him and repulsing every attempt at greeting (012-152). Martin, "lost in astonishment," followed in silence back to the house (012-153).

Inside, Pecksniff attended to the fire and invited only Tom to take tea, pointedly ignoring Martin. When Martin finally demanded an explanation, rapping the table and insisting "Presently will not do. Now!" (012-174), Pecksniff launched into his denunciation. He accused Martin of having deceived his "confiding and unsuspicious" nature and declared, "This lowly roof, sir, must not be contaminated by the presence of one who has deceived, and cruelly deceived" (012-179).

Martin made a violent stride forward, but Tom Pinch caught him in his arms. Shaking Tom off, Martin delivered his contemptuous verdict: as Pecksniff "lies there, disgraced, bought, used; a cloth for dirty hands, a mat for dirty feet, a lying, fawning, servile hound, he is the very last and worst among the vermin of the world" (012-183). Flinging his hat on his head, Martin walked out of the house and village. When Tom caught up with him, breathless, Martin declared his resolution: "Yes, I do. I'll go to America!" (012-191).

## Aftermath

Martin's impulsive decision to go to America sets in motion the entire American section of the novel (Chapters 13–34). His departure without money, winter clothing, or any practical plan demonstrates the combination of pride and recklessness that defines his early character. Tom Pinch, devastated, pressed a book upon him with a turned-down leaf—a parting gift from the truest friend Martin had at Pecksniff's (012-194). The expulsion also solidified Pecksniff's control over the household as the unchallenged patriarch, allowing him to pursue his designs on Mary Graham's inheritance and to begin systematically isolating Tom.

## Narrative Function

This event serves as the inciting incident of the novel's main plot. It separates young Martin from his grandfather's influence, from Mary Graham, and from the security of English society, forcing him into a journey of self-discovery and moral education. The confrontation is also the first time Pecksniff's hypocrisy is openly named and scorned by a character within the narrative, establishing the central theme of the novel: the exposure of false virtue against the slow growth of genuine self-knowledge.
