# Poverty

Poverty haunts the margins of *Martin Chuzzlewit*, a novel ostensibly concerned with wealth, inheritance, and the performance of respectability. While the central characters scheme for fortunes and maintain façades of prosperity, poverty is the condition they flee, the fate they dread, and—in the novel's moral accounting—sometimes the station of those who are richer in virtue than the wealthy.

## The Garb of Poverty

Dickens introduces poverty as a moral test in Chapter III, when [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] lectures his daughters on the virtuous poor:

> Rich men who, in the garb of poverty, have found out virtue and recovered the true metal of the human heart.
>
> (003-111)

Pecksniff's endorsement of poverty is, characteristically, entirely self-serving: he praises the poor only to justify not giving them money, and he "finds" virtue in poverty only when that poverty belongs to someone else. The passage is a masterpiece of Pecksniffian logic: poverty is good for the poor, wealth is good for Pecksniff.

## Martin's Descent

The novel's most sustained treatment of poverty comes through [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin Chuzzlewit]]'s experience. Stripped of his expectations, Martin confronts poverty directly—first in London and then in the American wilderness of [[eden|Eden]]. His experience of "poverty, illness, banishment, sorrow, and long travel" (015-021) is Dickens's anatomy of the condition: not merely the absence of money but the comprehensive degradation of body, spirit, and social standing.

> "If I could have been labelled and ticketed as an utterly poverty-stricken man," Martin reflects, "I could have borne it" (015-051).

The irony is that Martin *is* poor, but poverty in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is never simply a material fact—it is a social performance whose worst cruelty is visibility. The truly poor man is not the one who has nothing but the one who cannot conceal it.

## Tom Pinch and Genteel Poverty

[[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] embodies the novel's most complex treatment of the theme. Tom is not destitute—he has his position with Pecksniff, his meals, his garret—but he is kept in a state of deliberate financial dependency. Pecksniff's exploitation of Tom is the novel's critique of a system in which poverty is not an accident but a deliberate condition, maintained by those who benefit from the poor man's gratitude.

Tom's poverty is also the mechanism of the novel's deepest emotional transaction: the half-sovereign he gives to Martin, wrapped in a page of the *Bachelor of Salamanca*, with the note "I don't want it indeed. I should not know what to do with it if I had it" (013-006). Tom's poverty, unlike Pecksniff's wealth, is generous.

## America and the Spectacle of Want

The American chapters extend the novel's treatment of poverty into social critique. The Eden land scheme—selling worthless swampland to emigrants as "a terrestrial paradise" (021-006)—is poverty manufactured as a business model. [[zephaniah-scadder|Scadder]], [[general-choke|General Choke]], and their associates enrich themselves by creating poverty for others. What [[the-anglo-bengalee|the Anglo-Bengalee]] does through fraudulent insurance, the Eden Land Corporation does through fraudulent real estate: both are engines for transferring wealth from the hopeful to the cynical.

## The Moral Economy

In the novel's moral accounting, poverty and wealth are uncoupled from virtue. The poorest characters—Tom Pinch, [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]], [[ruth-pinch|Ruth Pinch]]—are among the richest in spirit. The wealthiest—Pecksniff, old Martin (before his revelation), [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]]—are spiritually impoverished. This inversion is the novel's deepest thematic argument: that the real poverty is not of purse but of heart.
