## The Blighted Man

Augustus Moddle is introduced in Chapter XXXII as the youngest gentleman at [[Todgers's]] boarding-house, defined from the start by his extravagant melancholy:

> He often informed Mrs. Todgers that the sun had set upon him; that the billows had rolled over him; that the Car of Juggernaut had crushed him, and also that the deadly Upas tree of Java had blighted him. His name was Moddle.
>
> (032-024)

The narrator's deadpan delivery — piling catastrophe upon catastrophe before the laconic reveal "His name was Moddle" — establishes him immediately as a comic creation in the tradition of Dickensian excess.

## Her Who Is Another's

Moddle's melancholy has a specific source: a lost love, a woman who is now "Another's." When [[Charity Pecksniff]] initially treats him with haughtiness, he protests to [[Mrs. Todgers]]:

> "Even she turns from me, Mrs. Todgers," said Moddle.
>
> "But I can't avoid her," replied Moddle, "I haven't strength of mind to do it. Oh, Mrs. Todgers, if you knew what a comfort her nose is to me!"
>
> "Her profile, in general," said the youngest gentleman, "but particularly her nose. It's so like hers who is Another's, Mrs. Todgers!"
>
> (032-026)(032-030)

Charity, learning of his fixation, cleverly presents her side-profile to him "as much as possible" (032-033), exploiting his sentimental weakness. Moddle confesses: "When she talks, and when she smiles, I think I'm looking on *her* brow again, Mrs. Todgers" (032-036).

## The Cribbage Courtship

The ensuing courtship unfolds through cribbage games freighted with desperate symbolism:

> On the seventh night of cribbage, when Mrs. Todgers, sitting by, proposed that instead of gambling they should play for "love," Mr. Moddle was seen to change colour. On the fourteenth night, he kissed Miss Pecksniff's snuffers, in the passage, when she went upstairs to bed; meaning to have kissed her hand, but missing it.
>
> (032-037)

The narrator wryly notes that Moddle had "rising prospects, and 'almost' enough to live on. Really it looked very well" (032-038). When Charity, deploying a strategic coldness, tells Moddle they must part for appearances' sake, he is "goaded on to ask whether she could be contented with a blighted heart; and it appearing on further examination that she could be, plighted his dismal troth, which was accepted and returned" (032-057). Moddle now understands his purpose: "it was his mission to communicate to others the blight which had fallen on himself; and that, being a kind of unintentional Vampire, he had had Miss Pecksniff assigned to him by the Fates, as Victim Number One" (032-057).

## The Reluctant Bridegroom

By Chapter XXXVII, Moddle's engagement is established and his melancholy has deepened. When introduced to [[Tom Pinch]], he delivers his signature line:

> "The Scorner has not set his seal upon you. *You* care what becomes of you?" said Moddle.
>
> "I don't," said Mr. Moddle. "The Elements may have me when they please. I'm ready."
>
> (037-091)(037-093)

In Chapter XLVI, Charity drags him furniture shopping for their future home. Moddle's response to every piece is a grim variation on "Perhaps they are ordered already. Perhaps they are Another's" (046-020). When asked to price rosewood chairs, he sighs and calculates:

> "Thirty-two, twelve, six," said Moddle, with a sigh. "And very dear."
>
> (046-033)

The narrator describes the scene: "Miss Pecksniff had quite the air of having taken the unhappy Moddle captive, and brought him up to the contemplation of the furniture like a lamb to the altar. He offered no resistance, but was perfectly resigned and quiet" (046-016).

## Comic Significance

Augustus Moddle is one of Dickens's finest minor comic inventions — the man whose identity is defined by having been emotionally destroyed before the novel begins, who drifts into an engagement as "Victim Number One," and who faces his approaching marriage with the resignation of a condemned man contemplating the gallows. Each appearance deepens the joke: from the cribbage games to the furniture shopping, Moddle's lament remains consistent — everything beautiful and desirable in the world belongs to "Another," and he is merely its blighted spectator.

Through Moddle, Dickens satirizes a particular strain of Romantic self-dramatization, while the cheerful predatory competence of Charity Pecksniff — who exploits Moddle's "blight" for her own matrimonial ends — provides the perfect comic counterpoint.
