# Selfishness

The master theme of *Martin Chuzzlewit* (1843–1844), which Dickens himself described as a novel designed to show "how Selfishness propagates itself." The full original title — *The Life and Adventures of Martin Chuzzlewit* — carries the subtitle that one of its characters is "a selfish man," and the novel investigates selfishness in its many forms: personal, familial, institutional, and national.

## Overview

Selfishness in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is not a simple vice but a self-perpetuating system. Dickens presents it as the root from which all other evils grow — deceit, hypocrisy, greed, treachery. The novel's pivotal insight is articulated in Chapter 52: "Self; grasping, eager, narrow-ranging, overreaching self; with its long train of suspicions, lusts, deceits, and all their growing consequences; was the root of the vile tree" (052-002). This metaphor structures the entire narrative, as characters who are selfish propagate their selfishness in others, creating chains of moral corruption.

## In the Novel

Selfishness appears in every stratum of the novel's world. Old Martin Chuzzlewit embodies the *suspicious* selfishness — the kind that hoards wealth and withholds trust, watching others for signs of the very selfishness he practices himself (006-066). His grandson, young Martin, inherits this disposition: raised in his grandfather's house, he "unconsciously reasoned as a child, 'My guardian takes so much thought of himself, that unless I do the like by myself, I shall be forgotten.' So he had grown selfish" (033-119).

Mr. Pecksniff represents selfishness masked as virtue — the *hypocritical* selfishness that cloaks greed in moral language. Jonas Chuzzlewit embodies the violent, criminal extreme. The American episodes (Chapters 16–34) extend the critique to *national* selfishness, satirizing a culture of self-promotion and fraud. Even minor characters — Mrs. Gamp with her mercenary nursing, Montague Tigg with his financial scheming — illustrate the theme's reach.

### Key Passages

The narrator pauses at Martin's sickbed in Chapter 33 to deliver the novel's psychological thesis: "Selfishness does this especially; so do suspicion, cunning, stealth, and covetous propensities" — vices that "propagate themselves to be their own antagonists" (033-119). Martin's recovery is also a moral recovery: "He never would have known it, but that being newly risen from a bed of dangerous sickness... he felt how nearly Self had dropped into the grave, and what a poor dependent, miserable thing it was" (033-120).

The resolution in Chapter 52 provides the novel's most explicit statement. Old Martin reveals his long game: he saw Pecksniff "had become the incarnation of all selfishness and treachery" (052-002). Young Martin, reformed, articulates the mirror-image paradox: "There is a kind of selfishness... which is constantly upon the watch for selfishness in others; and holding others at a distance by suspicions and distrusts, wonders why they don't approach" (052-081).

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens treats selfishness not through abstract moralizing but through caricature and structural irony. Pecksniff's speeches — dripping with piety while scheming for inheritance — are classic Dickensian satire: the gap between rhetoric and action exposes the vice. The American chapters deploy a broader satirical mode, with place-names like "Eden" (a malarial swamp marketed as paradise) embodying the selfish manipulation of language itself.

The novel's narrative structure reinforces the theme: selfish plans unravel, and the unselfish characters — Tom Pinch, Mark Tapley, Ruth Pinch, John Westlock — are rewarded. Mark Tapley's philosophy of being "jolly" under adversity is the novel's counter-ethic: cheerful selflessness as the antidote to grasping self.
