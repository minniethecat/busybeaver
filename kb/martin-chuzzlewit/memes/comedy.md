# Comedy

Comedy in Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* is both a literary technique and a moral instrument. Through caricature, verbal wit, situational farce, and ironic narrative commentary, Dickens uses comedy to expose hypocrisy, deflate pretension, and create some of his most memorable characters. Mrs. Gamp, with her imaginary friend "Mrs. Harris" and her attachment to the bottle "on the chimley-piece," stands as one of Dickens's greatest comic creations — grotesque, vital, and morally revealing.

## Overview

Dickens's comic method in *Martin Chuzzlewit* operates at multiple registers. At the highest level, the narrative voice itself is a comic instrument — the ironic narrator who describes Pecksniff as "a moral man" while systematically exposing his immorality. At the character level, figures like Sairey Gamp, Mr. Mould the undertaker, and the American boosters are rendered through exaggerated traits that reveal deeper truths. At the level of incident, scenes like the family gathering at Todgers's or the Eden land-swindle deploy farce to expose greed and gullibility.

The novel's comedy is inseparable from its moral purpose. When Dickens makes us laugh at Pecksniff's oily speeches or Mrs. Gamp's drunken rationalizations, he is training our moral perception — comedy becomes a way of seeing through false appearances. This is consistent with the novel's declared subject: "selfishness" is best exposed not through earnest moralizing but through the sharper instrument of ridicule.

## In the Novel

### Mrs. Gamp: Comic Grotesque

Sairey Gamp is the novel's supreme comic achievement. Her speech — a stream of malapropisms, non-sequiturs, and references to the imaginary "Mrs. Harris" — creates a self-contained comic universe. Her famous instruction to "leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged" (025-001) is comic because it performs self-denial while ensuring access. The comedy is built on the gap between Mrs. Gamp's self-presentation as a respectable professional and the reality of her drunken incompetence.

### Pecksniff: The Comedy of Hypocrisy

Mr. Pecksniff's comedy is of a different order — the comedy of self-righteousness. His speeches are masterpieces of moral inflation, as when he offers Martin "the advantages of the best practical architectural education with the comforts of a home, and the constant association with some who... are not unmindful of their moral responsibilities" (002-001). The comedy lies in the disproportion between the elevated language and the shabby reality — Pecksniff's "architectural education" consists of stealing his pupils' designs.

### The American Satire

The American chapters (016-001 through 022-001) represent Dickens's most sustained comic set-piece. From the "literary gentlemen" of the *Rowdy Journal* to the booster rhetoric of Eden, Dickens deploys caricature on a national scale. Mr. Jefferson Brick, the war correspondent who has never seen a war, embodies the gap between American pretension and reality. The comedy here borders on savagery — Dickens was settling scores with his American reception — but it produces some of the novel's most energetic writing.

### Todgers's: The Comedy of Crowded Life

The boarding-house scenes at Todgers's represent a different comic mode — the affectionate observation of communal absurdity. The "constant jangling of the iron handle of the pail" and the "shrill altercations from time to time" (008-001) create a comic symphony of domestic chaos. The boy "in an apron of coarse green baize" who makes "excursive bolts into the neighbouring alleys" is pure comic observation, unburdened by satirical intent.

## Key Passages

> "Leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged." (025-001)

Mrs. Gamp's signature line encapsulates Dickens's comic method: a character defines herself through speech that contradicts its surface meaning. The sentence performs abstinence while securing access — comedy as psychological revelation.

> "He was a moral man" (002-001)

Perhaps the most devastating comic line in the novel — three words that, in context, mean their exact opposite. The narrator's deadpan delivery makes the irony more lethal than any explicit condemnation could be.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The comic technique in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is itself a form of moral judgment. The characters who are treated comically — Pecksniff, Gamp, the American boosters — are those whose vices are most effectively exposed by laughter. The characters treated sympathetically — Tom Pinch, Ruth, Mary Graham — are rarely subjects of comedy. This distribution is not accidental: Dickens reserves comedy for those who deserve it, making laughter a moral act.

The American chapters push this principle to its limit. The comedy becomes so savage that Dickens felt compelled to add the conciliatory Postscript in later editions, acknowledging "national generosity and magnanimity" (022-Postscript). The comedy had done its work too well.
