# Envy

Envy in Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* is the poison that turns family into battlefield. It is the specific form selfishness takes when it looks sideways — at a sibling's marriage prospects, a relative's inheritance, a rival's success. Throughout the novel, envy drives characters to undermine each other, corrupt natural affections, and transform the domestic sphere into a theater of competitive resentment. From Charity Pecksniff's bitter jealousy of her sister Mercy's marriage to the grasping Chuzzlewit relatives circling Old Martin's fortune, Dickens anatomizes envy as the vice that destroys families from within.

## Overview

Envy occupies a distinctive place in *Martin Chuzzlewit*'s moral taxonomy. Unlike greed, which reaches outward for objects, or cruelty, which strikes outward at victims, envy is inherently comparative — it measures the self against others and finds the self wanting. This makes it the most psychologically tormenting of the novel's vices: the envious character suffers even when no one else does.

Dickens connects envy to the family structure with particular acuity. The Chuzzlewit family gathering (004-005) is a study in collective envy — each relative suspects the others of currying favor with Old Martin, and each suspects they themselves are being outmaneuvered. The result is not action but paralysis: the relatives "one and all concurred in hating Mr. Tigg because he didn't" belong to the family — the only thing they can agree on is resentment of an outsider.

The narrator identifies "Envy and Pride" as "twin demons" (006-022) that destroy friendship — a pairing that reveals Dickens's psychological insight. Pride makes one feel entitled to what others have; envy makes one resent their having it. Together, they make genuine relationship impossible.

## In the Novel

### Charity Pecksniff: Envy Personified

Charity Pecksniff is the novel's most sustained portrait of envy. When Mercy secures Jonas Chuzzlewit's proposal, Charity's reaction is diagnosed directly by her father: "Oh, envy, envy, what a passion you are!" (019-001). But Pecksniff's moralizing is characteristically self-serving — he has himself fueled the sisters' rivalry through a lifetime of competitive praise. Charity's envy is not innate wickedness but the predictable outcome of being raised in a household where love is a zero-sum game.

Charity's later behavior — rushing to publish her own marriage to Augustus Moddle, attempting to upstage Mercy at every turn — shows envy hardening into a way of life. Her tragedy is that she cannot enjoy anything unless it can be wielded against her sister.

### The Chuzzlewit Family: Collective Envy

The family gathering scene (004-005) shows envy operating at the collective level. The relatives are described with grotesque physicality — "so bald and had such big whiskers," "almost supernaturally disagreeable," "very dark and very hairy" — as if envy has deformed their bodies as it has their souls. George Chuzzlewit's eyes are "strained in their sockets, as if with constant surprise" at the undeserved good fortune of others.

Mark Tapley diagnoses the family perfectly: "An envious family... would open a field of action as I might do something in" (006-022). His comic perspective reveals the truth — the Chuzzlewits are defined by their envy, and it makes them both ridiculous and dangerous.

### American Envy: National Narcissism

In the American chapters, envy takes a national form. The American gentleman describes his country as "the Envy of the world, sir, and the leaders of Human Civilization" (016-001). This formulation transforms envy from a personal vice into a national boast — America is not envious of others; others are envious of America. Dickens's satire exposes the narcissism beneath this rhetoric, showing how national pride can be indistinguishable from the envious fantasy that everyone covets what you have.

### Tom Pinch: The Absence of Envy

Tom Pinch provides the counter-example. The narrator explicitly notes that "no drop of envy or bitterness was in his soul" (052-001), even when he cannot speak Mary Graham's name without pain. Tom's freedom from envy is his moral superpower — it allows him to genuinely celebrate the happiness of others, even when that happiness excludes him. In a novel saturated with competitive resentment, Tom's immunity to envy marks him as truly virtuous.

## Key Passages

> "Oh, envy, envy, what a passion you are!" (019-001)

Mr. Pecksniff's exclamation is ironic — spoken by a man who has cultivated envy in his daughters for his own purposes — but the sentence captures envy's destructive power in five words. It is a "passion" in the older sense: something one suffers, something one is passive before, a force that possesses rather than a choice one makes.

> "No drop of envy or bitterness was in his soul; but he could not master the firm utterance of her name." (052-001)

Tom's freedom from envy coexists with real grief — the absence of one vice does not immunize him against suffering. This passage distinguishes between envy (resentment of another's good) and sorrow (grief at one's own loss), a distinction the envious characters consistently fail to make.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens treats envy with a mixture of satire and sympathy. Charity Pecksniff is ridiculous — her competitive wedding announcement is farce — but she is also pitiable. The novel understands that envy is often the product of circumstances rather than character: the Chuzzlewit relatives are envious because Old Martin has made them so, dangling his fortune as a prize to be competed for.

The American treatment of envy is purely satirical: "the Envy of the world" is a phrase that collapses under its own pretension. But the satire has a moral point — national envy, like personal envy, is a form of insecurity disguised as superiority.
