# Martin Chuzzlewit Sr.

Old Martin Chuzzlewit is the novel's enigmatic patriarch — a man of "a will of iron, and a voice of brass" whose obsession with being loved for himself rather than his [[commerce|money]] drives the central plot (003-008). Having quarreled with every relative who sought his fortune, he subjects those around him — most of all his grandson, [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]], and his companion, [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] — to ordeals designed to test whether disinterested love is possible. His great narrative act is a prolonged performance of feebleness and dependence in [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s household, a deception that allows him to observe every character's true nature before delivering the novel's definitive moral judgment.

## Overview

Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] first appears in Chapter 3, dangerously ill at the Blue Dragon inn in [[salisbury|Wiltshire]], where the landlady observes him with fear and fascination. He is "a strong and vigorous old man" whose physical power is matched by his suspicious, imperious temperament. He refuses to send for help: "He would have no person sent for. The worse he grew, the more rigid and inflexible he became" (003-008). This tableau — the rich old man, alone but for a hired companion, [[suspicion|distrust]]ful of everyone — establishes the central problem that the novel must solve.

## Biography

### Background & Family

[[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit is the head of a family defined by selfishness — a trait Dickens presents as hereditary, surfacing in variously toxic forms across the Chuzzlewit cousins. His brother, old Anthony, is miserly and emotionally sterile. His grandson, [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]], is the inheritor of his name and his stubborn pride. His cousin [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] is the arch-[[hypocrisy|hypocrite]] who cloaks greed in moral rhetoric. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s relationship to all of them is governed by suspicion: he believes, with considerable justification, that everyone wants his [[commerce|money]].

His great fear — and the insight that shapes the novel — is that wealth makes genuine love impossible. He has made [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]]'s position deliberately precarious: she is to care for him and expect nothing. As he later explains, "I bred this orphan up, to tend me. None of you can know by what degrees I have come to regard her as a daughter; for she has won upon me, by her self-forgetfulness, her tenderness, her patience" (052-079).

### Role in the Plot

Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s great act is a piece of theater. After his illness at the Blue Dragon, he allows himself to be installed in [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s household, where he plays the role of a broken, dependent old man — all the while watching, judging, and laying plans. [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], believing he has captured the prize, reveals himself more completely with every self-serving gesture.

The revelation in Chapter 52 is a Dickensian set-piece of moral reckoning. Summoning the assembled characters, old Martin turns on [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] with devastating rhetorical force: "Listen, [[hypocrisy|hypocrite]]! Listen, smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave!" (052-083). He explains how he set a trap with himself as bait, offering [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] terms "as mean and base, and as degrading to himself as I could render them" — and [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] accepted them all (052-085).

But old Martin's most important confession is about himself. "There is a kind of selfishness," he admits, "which is constantly upon the watch for selfishness in others; and holding others at a distance by suspicions and [[suspicion|distrust]]s, wonders why they don't approach, and don't confide" (052-081). He has been, he realizes, as selfish in his way as the schemers who surrounded him — his suspicion was its own form of self-absorption.

### Fate & Resolution

Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s resolution is [[forgiveness|reconciliation]]. He blesses the union of [[mary-graham|Mary]] and [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]], which he had secretly hoped for from the beginning. He acknowledges [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]]'s goodness and [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]]'s wisdom. He distributes justice: [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] is expelled and ruined, [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s crimes are exposed, and the decent characters are rewarded. Far from dying, the old man emerges from his feigned illness vigorous and authoritative — a patriarch reborn through the process of discovering that some people, after all, are worthy of trust.

## Character Analysis

Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] is the novel's most complex moral figure. He is, simultaneously, the agent of justice and an object of moral critique. His great flaw — the suspiciousness that becomes a kind of selfishness — is the mirror image of the greed he despises in others. Dickens's stroke of genius is to make the patriarch's redemption depend on the very people he [[suspicion|mistrust]]ed: [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]]'s uncalculating goodness, [[mary-graham|Mary]]'s patient [[love|devotion]], even [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]]'s cheerful resilience. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] learns that goodness exists by nearly destroying his own chance to benefit from it.

His performance as a feeble old man is a darkly comic inversion: the patriarch who pretends to be helpless in order to exercise absolute power. By faking dependence, he discovers who is truly dependable. This makes him, in a sense, the novel's secret novelist — a plotter within the plot, watching his characters act out their natures.

## Key Relationships

- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]] — grandson; their estrangement and [[forgiveness|reconciliation]] is the novel's central emotional arc (052-098)
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] — ward and companion; her unselfish [[love|devotion]] redeems his faith in the possibility of disinterested love (052-079)
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — false caretaker; old Martin's long game of pretending dependence exposes [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s villainy completely (052-085)
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — old Martin comes to love [[tom-pinch|Tom]] as the embodiment of goodness without performance (052-104)
- [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] — employed by old Martin as a spy to track [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]]'s movements, without knowing the purpose (052-094)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — great-nephew whose [[crime|murder]] of [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] and attempted patricide represent the darkest form of Chuzzlewit selfishness
