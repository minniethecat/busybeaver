# Old Martin's Revelation

Old Martin Chuzzlewit assembles his allies, strikes Pecksniff down with his stick, and publicly reveals that he has known Pecksniff's true character all along.

## Overview

The climax of Martin Chuzzlewit is not a death or a marriage but an unmasking. Old Martin Chuzzlewit, having spent the entire novel feigning senility and dependence on Pecksniff, summons the principal characters to a London hotel room. One by one, they arrive: John Westlock, Tom Pinch and his sister Ruth, young Martin, Mary Graham, and Mrs. Lupin. Finally, Pecksniff himself enters with "open arms," only to be met with a blow from old Martin's stick that sends him to the floor (052-066). The old man then reveals that he has been playing a long game: he pretended to be Pecksniff's dupe in order to test the characters of everyone around him and to expose the architect's corruption beyond all possible doubt. The chapter is titled, appropriately, "In which the tables are turned, completely upside down" (052-001).


> He advanced with outstretched arms to take the old man’s hand. But he had not seen how the hand clasped and clutched the stick within its grasp.
> (052-066)

## Prelude

Old Martin had been nursing his indignation throughout his residence at Pecksniff's house, "hoarded up" and frequently "in danger of abrupt disclosure" (052-002). His apparent weakness was a deliberate strategy. He had been watching Pecksniff's schemes unfold, testing Mary Graham's loyalty, observing young Martin's growth through suffering, and noting Tom Pinch's unwavering honesty. He had also been quietly communicating with John Westlock and Mark Tapley. On the morning of the revelation, he sent Mark to summon young Martin—a summons Martin received with astonishment—and arranged the gathering that would become Pecksniff's tribunal (052-012). Mark, acting as doorkeeper, admitted each guest in turn while old Martin sat "leaning his hands upon his stick, and clenching them upon the handle" (052-048).

## The Event

The scene is a masterpiece of theatrical staging. Everyone present was "so much surprised and embarrassed by the sight of everybody else, that nobody ventured to speak" (052-055). Old Martin commanded Mark to set the door open—and the last footstep on the stairs belonged to Pecksniff, who "came bounding up with such uncommon expedition that he stumbled twice or thrice" (052-057).

Pecksniff entered crying "Where is my venerable friend?" and advanced with outstretched arms. Old Martin's response was immediate and physical: "with his burning indignation crowded into one vehement burst, and flashing out of every line and wrinkle in his face," he struck Pecksniff down with his stick—"down he went, as heavily and true as if the charge of a Life-Guardsman had tumbled him out of a saddle" (052-067).

Then came the verbal reckoning. "Hear me, rascal! I have summoned you here to witness your own work" (052-070). Old Martin drew young Martin and Mary to his side and acknowledged his own fault in the estrangement: "The fault was mine no less than yours" (052-075). He named the family curse: "The curse of our house... has been the love of self; has ever been the love of self" (052-077). And he explained that his feigned dotage had been a deliberate trial of everyone present—a point reinforced by the symbolic presence of the good-hearted Mrs. Lupin, a woman of no social standing whose kindness had nevertheless made her worthy of a place in the gathering.

## Aftermath

The exposure of Pecksniff is total and irreversible. Stripped of the old man's patronage, exposed before the very people he had wronged, Pecksniff is reduced to a grovelling figure—though characteristically, even in defeat, he attempts to frame his humiliation as martyrdom. The young Martin and Mary Graham are joyfully reunited and their engagement is blessed by old Martin. Tom Pinch, who had suffered most directly from Pecksniff's treachery, is vindicated, and his sister Ruth's marriage to John Westlock is set in motion. The revelation scene resolves nearly every major plot thread at once, clearing the way for the novel's final chapters, which deal with Jonas's fate and the Chuzzlewit legacy.

## Narrative Function

This is the novel's structural climax and its moral centre. It reverses the power dynamic that has held since Chapter 1: the old man who appeared weak is revealed as the master strategist; the architect who built himself up as a moral paragon is exposed as the novel's supreme fraud. The blow of the stick is both literal and symbolic—it is the one act of violence in the novel that the reader is invited to celebrate, the righteous anger that Pecksniff's hypocrisy has earned across fifty-two chapters.
