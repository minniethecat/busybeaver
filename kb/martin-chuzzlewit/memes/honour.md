# Honour

Honour is the most frequently invoked and most systematically debased moral term in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. Across seventy-nine occurrences spanning thirty-eight chapters, the word is spoken most often by the novel's least honourable characters. Dickens's treatment of honour is a sustained exercise in dramatic irony: the more a character protests his honour, the less he possesses.

## Overview

The novel's satire of honour operates through verbal inflation. Mr. Pecksniff appeals to "the honour of human nature" to dissuade young Martin from what Pecksniff himself knows to be true (010-071) (010-071). Montague Tigg repeatedly pledges his "sacred word of honour" — a phrase that circulates through the Anglo-Bengalee scenes like counterfeit currency (027-181) (027-181), (038-081) (038-081). Jonas Chuzzlewit's reply to Tigg is the novel's bluntest exposure of the pattern: "Oh! confound your honour... You may have all the honour, if you like; I'll never bring you to book for that" (038-076) (038-076). Jonas, the novel's most transparent villain, simply dismisses the word as noise.

## In the Novel

The Pecksniffian deployment of "honour" is always strategic. He describes himself as a man "seeking to do my duty in this carnal universe" while denouncing others who have "deceived an honourable, beloved, venerated, and venerable gentleman" (012-179) (012-179). In Chapter 44, he greets Montague with the announcement that his household "can appreciate the honour of your visit" — the word operating as social lubricant in a scene driven entirely by financial calculation (044-033) (044-033). Old Martin's climactic revelation in Chapter 52 provides the counter-narrative: he "made a sacred compact with myself that I would give him credit on the other side for any latent spark of goodness, honour, forbearance — any virtue — that might glimmer in him. For first to last there has been no such thing" (052-088) (052-088).

The American chapters extend the satire of honour into national self-presentation. The "Honourable Elijah Pogram" carries the title as mock-heroic ornament, his name fusing the language of civic virtue with the reality of demagogic bombast (034-127) (034-127). The Anglo-Bengalee scenes reduce honour to a financial instrument — Tigg swearing his "sacred word of honour" on the same breath that he describes an insurance fraud (027-181) (027-181).

Genuine honour in the novel is quiet and needs no declarative prefix. Martin writes to Tom Pinch placing "perfect reliance on your honour and secrecy" — a trust that Tom vindicates without ever speaking of his own honour (014-053) (014-053). Old Martin, completing his arc, speaks of "the goodness and simplicity, the honour and the manly faith of Tom" as the standard by which all other claims are measured (052-104) (052-104). Mercy Pecksniff, in one of her most sympathetic moments, tells Tom "It was brave of you. I honour you for it" — the verb carrying the weight the noun so often lacks (024-099) (024-099).

### Key Passages

The novel's most explicit honour-deflation is Jonas's dismissal: "Oh! confound your honour... You may have all the honour, if you like; I'll never bring you to book for that" (038-076) (038-076). The statement is brutal but accurate: Tigg's honour is indeed worthless, and Jonas's cynicism, however repellent, sees through it.

Old Martin's trial of Pecksniff is framed explicitly in terms of honour: he sought "any latent spark of goodness, honour, forbearance — any virtue — that might glimmer in him. For first to last there has been no such thing" (052-088) (052-088). The verdict is absolute: Pecksniff has no honour at all.

Martin's letter to Tom establishes the novel's positive standard: "I am going to repose great trust in you, knowing that I may do so with perfect reliance on your honour and secrecy" (014-053) (014-053). Tom's honour is real because it is taken for granted, not declared.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The satire of honour is achieved primarily through frequency — the word's sheer repetition by the novel's worst characters empties it of meaning. Pecksniff, Tigg, the American boosters, even Jonas (when it suits him) — all invoke honour while practising its opposite. The treatment recalls Dickens's handling of hypocrisy but is more specifically linguistic: honour-talk becomes a genre of speech that marks its speaker as dishonest. The only character who never claims honour for himself is Tom Pinch, who has the most of it.
