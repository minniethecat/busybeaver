# The Undertaker

The undertaker in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is embodied in [[mr-mould|Mr. Mould]], one of Dickens's great comic-professional creations. Through Mould and his trade, Dickens anatomises the commerce of death—the transformation of grief into a billable service, of mourning into a performed respectability, and of the corpse into a commodity around which an entire professional apparatus has been constructed.

## Mr. Mould: The Performer of Grief

Mr. Mould is introduced in Chapter XIX, in the aftermath of the death of old [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]]. He is "a little elderly gentleman, bald, and in a suit of black; with a bundle of papers in his hand" (019-025)—the physical type of the professional mourner, whose very person is a costume of grief. His professional manner is a performance of solemnity so complete that it has consumed his identity:

> "A damp, moist, unpleasant corpse, if you please," said the undertaker, shaking his head with a solemn air.
>
> (019-028)

The phrase "if you please" is the undertaker's signature—the gesture of deference that masks the commercial transaction. Mould never forgets that he is being paid; his solemnity is itemised, his grief is an invoice. The comedy lies in the absolute sincerity of his performance: Mould believes in his own gravity as thoroughly as [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] believes in his own virtue.

## Mrs. Gamp and the Undertaker

The undertaker shares the death-bed economy with [[sarah-gamp|Sarah Gamp]], the novel's other great professional of mortality. Their encounter in Chapter XIX establishes the division of labour in Dickens's death-industry: Gamp attends the dying, Mould attends the dead. Between them they bracket the mortal moment, each extracting a fee, their professional intimacy sealed when the undertaker "emptied his glass and smacked his lips" in Gamp's company (019-069):

> "And so, too, Mrs. Gamp," said the undertaker, "and I think so also." Mrs. Gamp and the undertaker were in perfect harmony.
>
> (019-030)

The "perfect harmony" between nurse and undertaker is the novel's darkest joke: the professions that attend death are also those that profit from it. Gamp's bottle and Mould's black suit are the twin instruments of an industry that has learned to monetise the one certainty of human life.

## "An Undertaker's Charges"

Mould's professional candour is the source of much of the comedy. He is frank about the economics of his trade in a way that Pecksniff would never be about architecture:

> "Perhaps it is because an undertaker's charges comes dearer than a doctor's," said Mrs. Gamp.
>
> (019-072)

The line is Gamp's, but it is spoken in Mould's presence and with his tacit agreement. The undertaker's bill is the novel's emblem of death's cost—not spiritual or emotional but strictly financial, a line-item in the ledger of a life's closing. Dickens, who had himself reported on the funeral industry's excesses, knew exactly how much a "respectable" death cost and exactly who profited from it.

## The Commerce of Death

The undertaker in *Martin Chuzzlewit* belongs to the novel's wider anatomy of fraudulent professions. Like [[pecksniffs-architectural-practice|Pecksniff's architectural practice]] (which produces nothing), like [[the-anglo-bengalee|the Anglo-Bengalee]] (which insures nothing), the undertaker's trade transforms an unavoidable fact—death—into a performed service whose value is entirely in the performance. The coffin, the hearse, the black plumes, the "damp, moist" solemnity: these are the props of a theatre in which the corpse is the only authentic element.

Yet Dickens does not condemn Mould as he condemns Pecksniff. Mould is a professional doing a necessary job; his vice is not fraud but the inflation of a natural grief into a social obligation. The people who pay for his plumes are the same people who pay for Pecksniff's paste diamonds—but Mould, at least, delivers what he promises: a properly managed corpse, a respectable funeral, a death performed to the satisfaction of the living.

## Significance

The undertaker is one of the novel's most revealing professional portraits because the trade sits at the intersection of the novel's central concerns: performance, commerce, mortality, and the gap between appearance and reality. Mould's black suit is the uniform of a man whose job is to make death presentable—to dress the unfaceable in the costume of social ritual. In a novel about the performance of virtue, the undertaker performs the one role that no amount of Pecksniffian rhetoric can avoid: the acknowledgment that all performances end.
