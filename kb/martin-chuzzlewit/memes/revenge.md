# Revenge

Revenge is the darkest of the passions that drive the plot of *Martin Chuzzlewit*. It is most powerfully embodied in [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]], whose "malignant and revengeful passions" (047-052) propel him from resentment to blackmail to murder, but the impulse toward retaliation surfaces across the novel's moral landscape, testing every character's capacity for forgiveness against their appetite for payback.

## Jonas Chuzzlewit: The Architecture of Revenge

Jonas is the novel's supreme study in revenge. His hatred of his father [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] is the seed from which his later crimes grow; Dickens traces from the earliest chapters "the elements of its own revenge" (021-004) that Jonas's character contains. Jonas's desire to revenge himself upon a world that has denied him—his father's withheld wealth, [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy]]'s disappointment in him, [[montague-tigg|Tigg]]'s blackmail—follows a precise psychological logic: each frustration intensifies his hatred until murder becomes, for him, not a departure from his nature but its completion.

> It was in his nature to seek to revenge himself on the fine clothes and the fine manner of those about him; and he found a gratification in being able to treat them harshly.
>
> (027-104)

The passage captures the social dimension of Jonas's revenge: it is directed not only at individuals but at the entire apparatus of gentility and respectability that excludes him. Jonas cannot be a gentleman, so he will destroy those who can.

## Hatred, Jealousy, and Revenge

Dickens frames the novel's emotional architecture in Chapter XXIV, describing a household "full of the materials of love, hatred, jealousy, and revenge" (024-001). The four passions are presented as a catalogue of human possibility, and the novel traces how each, unchecked, becomes its neighbour. Jonas's revenge is the endpoint of a chain that begins with hatred of his father, transforms into jealousy of those who possess what he lacks, and terminates in the murder of Tigg—the man who knows his secret and wields power over him.

## Revenge Denied

The novel's moral argument about revenge is articulated most clearly in what characters choose *not* to do. [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]], wronged by Pecksniff in the most intimate terms—deceived about [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]]'s affections, exploited for years, dismissed without cause—refuses revenge. His capacity to forgive, to feel sorrow rather than hatred, is the novel's clearest statement that revenge is not inevitable; that the chain of retaliation can be broken by an act of will.

[[old-martins-revelation|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] stages what appears to be a revenge plot—the elaborate scheme to expose Pecksniff—but his final revelation reframes it as justice rather than vengeance. "I have never for a moment forgotten the inestimable services you rendered me," he tells Pecksniff, "and I have not forgotten the patience, the kindness, and the gentleness with which you bore, at first, the sharp trial I put you to." The performance of revenge dissolves into the delivery of justice; the distinction, Dickens suggests, lies in the motive—whether one acts from hatred or from the demands of truth.

## Charity's Revenge

[[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] provides the novel's comic treatment of revenge. Jilted by [[augustus-moddle|Augustus Moddle]], she declares: "I don't want any more revenge. I hope that every possible thing may happen to prevent his getting any other wife" (046-038). Charity's revenge is thwarted before it begins—Moddle has fled to Tasmania—but her declaration is revealing: even the most Pecksniffian of the Pecksniff daughters recognizes revenge as a recognizable human response to injury, however impotent her own version of it.
