## Identity and Role

Mr. Jinkins is the foremost of the young gentlemen boarders at [[Todgers's|Todgers]], Mrs. Todgers's commercial boarding-house in London. He occupies the most desirable room — known as "Jinkins's Particular" — and serves as the unofficial chairman and spokesman of the boarders' collective activities (008-069). When [[Charity Pecksniff]] and [[Mercy Pecksniff]] visit London under the chaperonage of their father [[Seth Pecksniff]], it is Jinkins who leads the boarders' deputation to welcome them.

His position is explicitly stated by Mrs. Todgers: "That is Mr. Jinkins's" — identifying his room as the best accommodation, the one first offered to distinguished guests (008-088). The other boarders treat him with deference, and the jokes at his expense that circulate among them only confirm his centrality to the establishment's social life (008-089).

## Character and Talents

Jinkins is described as possessing a range of gentlemanly accomplishments. "Jinkins is a man of superior talents," the narrator declares, and his gifts extend across multiple domains (009-033). He is "of a fashionable turn" — a man of style and social grace — and the other boarders look to him for leadership in matters of ceremony and entertainment (009-110).

His oratorical skill is particularly noted. At the dinner given for the Miss Pecksniffs, Jinkins delivers speeches, proposes toasts, and manages the social dynamics of the table with practised ease: "Mr. Jinkins thanks them from his heart" (009-122). The other boarders respond with cries of "Hear, hear!" and "Bravo Jink!" whenever he rises to speak (009-109).

## The Welcoming Deputation

Jinkins forms part of the formal deputation — "consisting of Mr. Jinkins, and another gentleman" — that knocks at Mrs. Todgers's door to be introduced to [[Charity Pecksniff]] and [[Mercy Pecksniff]] (009-107). He escorts them upstairs with ceremony, and throughout the evening he occupies the position of honour at the head of the table, with one Miss Pecksniff on either hand (009-115).

During the dinner, Jinkins is attended by the serving-boy [[Bailey|Bailey Junior]], who "seldom stirred from behind Mr. Jinkins's chair, where, with his hands in his pockets, and his legs planted pretty wide apart, he had an eye to what was going on" (009-116). The other boarders "pathetically implored Mr. Jinkins to suffer them to qualify it [the wine] with hot water" — a running joke about the quality of the establishment's provisions (009-118).

## Relationship with Other Boarders

The dynamics among the Todgers's boarders revolve around Jinkins. [[Gander]], the witty boarder, engages in friendly rivalry with him; in his convulsing speech, Gander "glares disdainfully at Jinkins" in mock challenge (009-123). Jinkins in return is reproached "in confidence" by others — the boarders maintain a delicate social economy of jest and deference (009-111).

After the dinner party's emotional conclusion, "Jinkins consoled them both" — the Miss Pecksniffs — demonstrating the solicitous gallantry expected of a gentleman-boarder (009-169). He and [[Gander]] then "took the rest upon themselves," sharing the remaining social duties of the evening (009-171).

## Narrative Function

Jinkins embodies the comic type of the boarding-house gentleman — a man whose talents and pretensions flourish within the narrow sphere of a commercial lodging. His "superior talents" and "fashionable turn" are never tested beyond the walls of Todgers's, and his oratory is expended on toasts and gallantries rather than any public purpose. In this he is an instance of Dickens's recurring theme: the disproportion between social performance and genuine achievement, enacted in miniature within the microcosm of the boarding-house.

## Notable Quotes

Pecksniff, ever the connoisseur of useful acquaintances, pronounces on Jinkins's merits:

> "Jinkins is a man of superior talents," observed Mr. Pecksniff. "I have conceived a great regard for Jinkins. I take Jinkins's desire to pay polite attention to my daughters, as an additional proof of the friendly feeling of Jinkins, Mrs. Todgers."
>
> (009-033)
