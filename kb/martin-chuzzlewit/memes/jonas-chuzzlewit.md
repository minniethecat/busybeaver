# Jonas Chuzzlewit

Jonas Chuzzlewit represents the darkest form of [[selfishness]] in Dickens's taxonomy: not the hypocritical selfishness of [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] or the redeemable selfishness of [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]], but the violent, criminal selfishness that destroys others and ultimately destroys itself. The son of [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] and nephew of [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]], Jonas is raised on the strictest principles of [[selfishness|self-interest]], and his biography traces the logical endpoint of a life governed entirely by "the main chance."

## Overview

Jonas first appears at the family gathering in Chapter 4, then emerges as a major figure when he, his father Anthony, and the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]s travel together to [[london|London]]. His education, Dickens tells us, "had been conducted from his cradle on the strictest principles of the main chance. The very first word he learnt to spell was 'gain,' and the second (when he got into two syllables), '[[commerce|money]]'" (008-031). This training succeeds too well: having been taught to overreach everyone, Jonas imperceptibly acquires the habit of overreaching his own father. The relationship between Jonas and Anthony — old Chuzzlewit's brother — is a grim lesson in the self-perpetuating nature of greed.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Jonas is the only son of [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]], [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s brother. The elder Chuzzlewit raised his son to see the world as a ledger of profit and loss, and Jonas absorbed the lesson completely. Their household is a study in mutual suspicion: father and son watch each other "with a jaundiced eye," each waiting for the other to die (011-097). Anthony's death in Chapter 19, possibly hastened by Jonas's neglect, frees Jonas to pursue his full career of villainy — though the weight of his father's corpse haunts him throughout the novel: "during the whole long seven days and nights, he was always oppressed and haunted by a dreadful sense of its presence in the house" (019-065).

### Role in the Plot

Jonas's courtship of [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] is a masterpiece of Dickensian ugliness — he pursues her not from [[love|affection]] but from a desire to possess and control. Their marriage, contracted hastily after Anthony's death, quickly degenerates into emotional abuse. Jonas berates, threatens, and terrorizes his wife with a violence that stops just short of the physical — until it does not.

His entanglement with [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]]'s [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company]] deepens his [[crime|criminality]]. [[montague-tigg|Tigg]], who knows Jonas's secret — that he attempted to poison his own father — blackmails him into participating in the [[deception|fraud]]ulent scheme. Jonas's simplicity of cunning, the narrator notes, made him "the most credulous of men" in matters involving knavery (027-107).

When [[montague-tigg|Tigg]]'s demands grow too great, Jonas resolves on [[crime|murder]]. He waylays [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] on a country road and bludgeons him to death, a crime uncovered by the private investigator [[nadgett|Nadgett]]. Confronted with evidence of both the [[crime|murder]] and the attempted poisoning of his father, Jonas takes poison himself rather than face justice.

### Fate & Resolution

Jonas's death by suicide is the novel's bleakest resolution. Unlike [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], who is merely humiliated and impoverished, Jonas is destroyed by the logical operation of his own principles. His death echoes Anthony's: the poison intended for the father becomes the poison that kills the son. [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]], with characteristic coldness, later remarks that "the death of Jonas was a judgment on them for their internal dissensions" (054-004).

## Character Analysis

Jonas embodies what Dickens called the "sordid coarseness and [[cruelty|brutality]]" of a character raised without [[love|affection]], religion, or moral instruction of any kind. The preface to the novel defends him against charges of exaggeration, arguing that such natures do exist and that their existence is a social indictment (P03-004).

His villainy is distinguished from [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s by its transparency. Jonas does not moralize; he sneers. He does not pretend to virtue; he openly despises it. He is what [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] would be if [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] lacked the intelligence to cloak his selfishness in piety. Their alliance — [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] eagerly marrying his daughter to a man he knows to be dangerous — illustrates how the novel's two forms of selfishness support each other, each making the other's crimes possible.

## Key Relationships

- [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] — father; their poisonous relationship is the novel's study in the self-propagating nature of greed (008-031)
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] — wife; Jonas's treatment of her escalates from emotional cruelty to domestic terror
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — father-in-law and uneasy ally, each despising the other while profiting from the connection (020-142)
- [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] — blackmailer and eventual victim; their relationship demonstrates how knavery consumes its own practitioners (027-107)
- [[chuffey|Chuffey]] — Anthony's aged clerk, whose confused memories help expose Jonas's crimes
- [[nadgett|Nadgett]] — the private investigator whose methodical work unravels Jonas's guilt
