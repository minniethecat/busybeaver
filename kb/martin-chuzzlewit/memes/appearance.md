# Appearance

*Martin Chuzzlewit* is a novel of surfaces and what lies beneath them. From its opening pages, Dickens trains the reader to distrust appearances—to see that the most virtuous-seeming characters are the most corrupt, that shabby exteriors may hide integrity, and that the gap between how things look and how things are is the novel's fundamental comic and moral terrain.

## Overview

The word "appearance" occurs more than forty times in the novel, and almost always in a context that invites skepticism. Characters are introduced through their physical appearance with a density of descriptive detail that is characteristically Dickensian—but the detail is always pointed, always diagnostic, always moral. Mrs. Lupin of the Blue Dragon is "in outward appearance just what a landlady should be" (003-012) (003-012), and the qualifier "outward" is the operative word: Dickens always leaves room for the inward reality to diverge.

## In the Novel

The novel's treatment of appearance operates on multiple levels. At the most obvious, there is the physical transformation of Montague Tigg. When he first appears, he is "shabby-genteel": his fingers "a long way out of his gloves, and the soles of his feet at an inconvenient distance from the upper leather of his boots" (004-006) (004-006). When he reappears as Tigg Montague, director of the Anglo-Bengalee, his appearance is radically transformed: "His clothes, symmetrically made, were of the newest fashion and the costliest kind. Flowers of gold and blue, and green and blushing red, were on his waistcoat; precious chains and jewels sparkled on his breast" (027-006) (027-006). But the narrator immediately undercuts the transformation: "though changed his name, and changed his outward surface, it was Tigg. The brass was burnished, lacquered, newly stamped; yet it was the true Tigg metal notwithstanding" (027-006) (027-006).

The Pecksniff household is the novel's laboratory of deceptive appearances. Mr. Pecksniff preens before the parson's mirror, "seeing that his hair was rumpled, he took the liberty of borrowing the canonical brush and arranging it" (031-062) (031-062)—vanity dressed as piety, self-regard wearing the vestments of the church. Tom Pinch, arriving late for an appointment, apologizes: "I know it must have a very rude appearance" (020-131) (020-131), and Pecksniff's echo—"It has a very rude appearance, Mr. Pinch" (020-132) (020-132)—is the novel's most compressed statement of the Pecksniffian method: seize on appearance to condemn, because appearance is all that matters to a man who has nothing beneath.

Appearance is also the language of illness and transformation. Martin, discovered in Eden, is "to all appearance, very ill indeed" (033-023) (033-023). The phrase "to all appearance" recurs throughout the novel as a quiet authorial signal: what appears to be may not be. Mr. Pecksniff observes that Martin "was changed, both in his appearance and manner" (018-087) (018-087)—and the change is real, the beginning of Martin's moral reformation.

### Key Passages

The novel's most vivid treatment of appearance-as-deception is Nadgett, the detective: "he always made his appearance as if he had that moment come up a trap" (038-012) (038-012). Nadgett is the character who sees through everyone else's appearances precisely because he has no discernible surface of his own—he is pure observation, invisible, "of all the precious old dummies in appearance that I ever saw, he's about the worst" (038-057) (038-057). Jonas's contempt for Nadgett's appearance is his fatal error: he cannot recognize the one man who sees through him.

The empty box at John Westlock's chambers—which "made its arrival known... by bumping modestly against the outside of the door" and from which extraordinary dishes emerge, served by a man who "vanished, box and all, like something that had never been" (045-041) (045-041)—is the novel's most charming variation on the appearance theme. Here the gap between appearance and reality is benign, comic, almost magical: a celebration of surfaces that deliver more than they promise, rather than less.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Appearance is Dickens's satirical instrument as well as his satirical target. The lavish descriptions of Tigg Montague's costume, Pecksniff's mirror-gazing, and Jonas's comparisons of the Pecksniff sisters' "personal appearance" (008-055) (008-055) are all comedy built on the gap between surface and substance. But Dickens also uses appearance sincerely: Mrs. Lupin's roses, Tom Pinch's ungainliness, Mark Tapley's honest face—these are appearances that *do* correspond to reality, and their presence in the novel prevents the theme from collapsing into cynicism. Not all surfaces lie; the skill is in knowing which to trust.
