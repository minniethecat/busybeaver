Major Pawkins is an [[america|America]]n military figure encountered by [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit during his travels. He is a minor character who contributes to Dickens's satirical portrait of [[america|America]]n martial pretension (021-001).

## Overview

Pawkins is one of several [[america|America]]n characters with military titles—like [[general-choke]] and [[colonel-diver]]—whose [[class-and-gentility|rank]]s are more [[honour|honor]]ific than earned. His presence adds to the atmosphere of self-important martial display that Dickens satirizes in the [[america|American chapters]] (021-002).

## Biography

### American Military Culture

Pawkins appears in the social gatherings and levees that form the backdrop of [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s [[america|America]]n experience. He moves in the same circles as the politicians, journalists, and speculators who populate Dickens's satirical portrait of [[america|America]]n society (021-003).

## Character Analysis

Major Pawkins, like the other [[america|America]]n military figures in the novel, represents the proliferation of self-aggrandizing titles in Jacksonian [[america|America]]. His [[class-and-gentility|rank]] is not a mark of genuine military accomplishment but of [[class-and-gentility|social pretension]]—a minor but consistent element of Dickens's critique (016-124).
