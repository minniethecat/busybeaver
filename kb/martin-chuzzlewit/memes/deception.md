# Deception

Deception is the operational mode of nearly every major character in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. Pecksniff deceives by self-righteous performance; Tigg Montague deceives by fraudulent financial schemes; the Eden land-swindlers deceive on an industrial scale. Even the novel's moral exemplars — Tom Pinch, Mark Tapley — are deceived before they learn to see clearly. Deception, Dickens suggests, is the price of living in a society where appearance governs reputation.

## Overview

The novel's frontispiece theme is announced in its opening chapter, where the narrator observes with characteristic irony that men play strange and extraordinary tricks in the pursuit of wealth and position (001-015). The word "tricks" is a comic understatement for the systematic deceits the novel will anatomise. Old Martin Chuzzlewit early pronounces a bitter verdict on humanity: he has "gone, a rich man, among people of all grades and kinds" and "never found one nature, no, not one, in which, being wealthy and not poor, I was not verily persuaded that I was to be deceived" (003-111). These are "the beauties which my wealth has brought to light" — the discovery that prosperity attracts deception as light attracts moths.

## In the Novel

Pecksniff is the novel's arch-deceiver, but his deception is distinctive: he deceives himself as thoroughly as he deceives others. Old Martin observes this quality with sardonic admiration: "you would deceive everybody, even those who practise the same art; and have a way with you, as if you—he, he, he!—as if you really believed yourself" (008-025). Pecksniff's genius is that his moral posturing is not consciously fraudulent — he inhabits his own performance so completely that he cannot be caught in the usual hypocrite's contradiction between public and private selves.

The Pecksniff daughters learn deception as a family trade. When they discover Tom Pinch's secret love for Mary Graham, their indignation is itself a performance of virtue: "What! Had they taken to their hearth and home a secretly contracted serpent; a crocodile, who had made a furtive offer of his hand; an imposition on society; a bankrupt bachelor with no effects, trading with the spinster world on false pretences!" (010-073). The rhetorical excess — serpent, crocodile, bankruptcy — reveals their own investment in the language of moral outrage as a tool of social control.

The American chapters extend deception from the personal to the systemic. The Eden land scheme, run by the fraudulent Scadder and General Choke, is deception industrialised: a swamp sold as paradise to desperate emigrants. Martin's near-death in Eden is the cost of believing in appearances — a lesson he must learn before his moral reformation can begin.

### Key Passages

Pecksniff's eviction of young Martin is a masterpiece of deceptive self-presentation. He denounces Martin as one who "has deceived, and cruelly deceived, an honourable, beloved, venerated, and venerable gentleman" while himself scheming against that same gentleman (012-179). The accusation of deception is itself the most deceptive speech in the novel — Pecksniff expels Martin not for deceiving old Martin but for threatening Pecksniff's own scheme to control the old man's fortune.

The exposure of Pecksniff in the final chapters is the unmasking Dickens has prepared for from the first page: the moral man revealed as "hypocrite," "smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave" (052-083).

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens distinguishes between two kinds of deception in the novel: the conscious fraud of characters like Tigg Montague and the Eden speculators, and the unconscious self-deception of Pecksniff. The former is criminal but comprehensible; the latter is more insidious because it corrupts the language of morality itself. When Pecksniff uses words like "virtue," "honour," and "duty," he empties them of meaning while retaining their social power. This is Dickens's critique of Victorian moral rhetoric: it provides a vocabulary that can be wielded by anyone, regardless of conduct.
