# "The Autumn Sunset at Salisbury"

The opening of Chapter II is among the most admired passages of descriptive prose in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. After the satirical genealogy of Chapter I, Dickens shifts register entirely, establishing the novel's physical world in a sustained passage of landscape description that moves from light to darkness, warmth to cold, animation to stillness—a microcosm of the novel's moral architecture.

## The Quote

> "It was pretty late in the autumn of the year, when the declining sun, struggling through the mist which had obscured it all day, looked brightly down upon a little Wiltshire village, within an easy journey of the fair old town of Salisbury."
>
> "Like a sudden flash of memory or spirit kindling up the mind of an old man, it shed a glory upon the scene, in which its departed youth and freshness seemed to live again. The wet grass sparkled in the light; the scanty patches of verdure in the hedges—where a few green twigs yet stood together bravely, resisting to the last the tyranny of nipping winds and early frosts—took heart and brightened up; the stream which had been dull and sullen all day long, broke out into a cheerful smile; the birds began to chirp and twitter on the naked boughs, as though the hopeful creatures half believed that winter had gone by, and spring had come already."
>
> (002-001)

## Context

The passage opens the chapter "Wherein certain persons are presented to the reader, with whom he may, if he please, become better acquainted." Before introducing [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] and his household, Dickens establishes the world they inhabit—a world of transient beauty, of light that "was all withdrawn," of a "shining church" that "turned cold and dark." The landscape description is not ornamental but structural: the passage from light to darkness prefigures the moral trajectory the novel will trace.

## Significance

The passage demonstrates several hallmarks of Dickens's descriptive art:

- **The pathetic fallacy with restraint**: The landscape reflects emotion—the stream's "cheerful smile," the birds that "half believed"—but Dickens does not sentimentalize. The sun sets; the gloom of winter "dwelt on everything." Beauty is real but transient.

- **The movement from particular to universal**: The opening establishes precise geography (a Wiltshire village, Salisbury); the closing paragraph generalizes into a meditation on season, mortality, and "the admonition that it is not to her more sensitive and joyous favourites [nature] grants the longest term of life."

- **Prose rhythm**: The long, accumulating sentences of the descriptive passage give way to the short, declarative "A moment, and its glory was no more." The prose itself enacts the sunset it describes.

- **Anti-Pecksniff**: In the paragraph that follows immediately, we meet Pecksniff—a man who has substituted performance for nature. The contrast between the honest beauty of the landscape and the paste-diamond morality of its inhabitant is the novel's moral argument in miniature.
