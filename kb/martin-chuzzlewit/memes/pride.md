# Pride

Pride in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is not merely an individual vice but an ancestral inheritance — the Chuzzlewit family's defining weakness. From the opening chapter, Dickens establishes pride as a trait woven into the family's identity, one that isolates its members from one another and fuels the selfishness that drives the novel's moral architecture.

## Overview

The narrator introduces pride as the Chuzzlewit family's characteristic flaw with satirical precision: "If it should ever be urged... that a Chuzzlewit, in any period of the family history, displayed an overweening amount of family pride, surely the weakness will be considered not only pardonable but laudable, when the immense superiority of the house to the rest of mankind... is taken into account" (001-002). This ironic framing sets the tone: Dickens treats pride not as legitimate self-respect but as delusional self-regard.

## In the Novel

Pride manifests in multiple characters and relationships. Young Martin Chuzzlewit, after his grandfather's rejection, is described as "deeply wounded in his pride and self-love; full of independent schemes, and perfectly destitute of any means of realizing them" (013-004). His pride, inherited from his upbringing, prevents him from seeking reconciliation and drives him toward the disastrous American venture.

The novel's psychological insight is that pride operates relationally. In Chapter 7, Dickens observes of the friendship between young Martin and Tom Pinch: "it was of all possible events among the least probable, that the twin demons, Envy and Pride, would ever arise between them" — because their relationship is built on the dynamic of patron and patronised, "the very essence of their respective characters" (007-002). Pride and envy dissolve only when hierarchy is fixed.

Mr. Pecksniff personifies pride disguised as humility. His protestation — "But I have no pride, for I can afford to do without it!" (009-044) — is the novel's sharpest satirical inversion, exposing how the proudest characters are those who most loudly disavow pride.

### Key Passages

Dickens's most powerful treatment of pride comes in Chapter 13, where the narrator addresses "Teachers of content and honest pride" directly, challenging moralists who preach self-respect from comfort while ignoring those ground down by poverty: "say can any hopeful plant spring up in air so foul that it extinguishes the soul's bright torch as fast as it is kindled!" (013-098). This passage transforms pride from a personal failing into a social question — distinguishing the arrogance of the comfortable from the honest pride denied to the oppressed.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens satirizes pride through structural irony. The Chuzzlewit family's boast of descent from "Adam and Eve" (001-002) is a mock-genealogical joke that undercuts all claims of inherited superiority. Pecksniff's false humility is the novel's running satirical device, in which pride wears the mask of self-abnegation.
