# Nursing

Nursing in Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* is inseparable from its most famous practitioner: Sairey Gamp, the drunken, incompetent, and gloriously entertaining "monthly nurse" who became one of Dickens's most celebrated comic creations. Through Mrs. Gamp and her colleague Betsey Prig, Dickens offers a satirical but socially pointed portrait of pre-reform nursing — a world of unregulated practitioners whose primary qualifications were availability and low fees — written just years before Florence Nightingale would transform the profession.

## Overview

Mrs. Gamp enters the novel as the nurse hired to attend Anthony Chuzzlewit's deathbed, and she quickly becomes one of Dickens's most distinctive characters. Her speech — a river of malapropisms, garbled quotations, and references to the entirely imaginary "Mrs. Harris" — creates a comic voice that is at once hilariously unreliable and psychologically consistent. She nurses "on principle" (for pay) rather than out of compassion, and her attachment to her bottle is proverbial: "Leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged" (025-001).

Dickens's portrait of nursing is satirical but not merely cruel. Mrs. Gamp is incompetent, mercenary, and perpetually drunk — yet she is also resourceful, professionally proud in her own way, and possessed of a vitality that makes her the most memorable character in the novel. The satire targets the system that produced her — an unregulated market in which the dying were at the mercy of whoever would take the job — rather than the woman herself.

## In the Novel

### Mrs. Gamp: The Nurse as Anti-Heroine

Sairey Gamp is introduced with her "large bundle," "pattens," and "gig umbrella of a faded leaf colour" (019-001). Her physical description establishes her as a force of nature rather than a caregiver — she "played innumerable games at quoits" with her pattens on Mr. Pecksniff's legs during the carriage ride, unconscious of the discomfort she causes.

Her professional philosophy is delivered in characteristic style: nursing is something she does "on principle" — the principle being payment. The "Mrs. Harris" she constantly invokes is a phantom witness to her competence, a tactic that allows Dickens to show Mrs. Gamp constructing her own professional reputation through pure invention.

### Betsey Prig: The Partnership

Betsey Prig, Mrs. Gamp's colleague, appears in the memorably hostile sickroom scene where the two nurses fall out over an "ungenerous allusion" to Mrs. Gamp's drinking (040-001). Their partnership is a study in competitive unprofessionalism — two women who would rather quarrel than tend the patient. The scene is comic but also disturbing, as the reader understands that a vulnerable patient lies in their care. Dickens extends this satire beyond the two principals, noting that "disciples of the Prig school of nursing" are "very numerous among young mothers" (046-111), suggesting that the Gamp-Prig model of negligent care is not an isolated phenomenon but a widespread social ill.

### Nursing and the Medical World

The novel's medical world extends beyond Mrs. Gamp to include doctors like Jobling and the undertaker Mr. Mould — a grim professional ecosystem in which nursing, doctoring, and undertaking are linked by their dependence on death. Dickens's satire of this system is social criticism: the novel was published in 1843–44, just before the sanitary reform movement and Nightingale's reforms would begin transforming British healthcare. The novel also touches on nursing in a gentler register: Ruth Pinch, "remembering her tender nursing" of her brother Tom, represents the domestic ideal of care that Gamp's commercial practice perverts (003-043).

## Key Passages

> "Leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged." (025-001)

Mrs. Gamp's most famous line — a masterpiece of comic characterization. The sentence is an act of self-deception performed in public: she forbids being offered drink while ensuring she has access to it. "Dispoged" (disposed) is classic Gamp — a word reshaped by her unique verbal universe.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens's treatment of nursing is the novel's most sustained example of what might be called "affectionate satire." Mrs. Gamp is ridiculous and culpable, but she is not a villain — she is a product of a system that Dickens wants to reform, not destroy. The comedy is a vehicle for social criticism: if you laugh at Mrs. Gamp, the next step is to ask why such figures are entrusted with human life.

The historical context gives the satire added weight. *Martin Chuzzlewit* was published in the same decade that saw the beginnings of professional nursing reform. Dickens would later support Nightingale's work, and Mrs. Gamp can be read as an argument for exactly the reforms Nightingale would champion.
