## Identity

General Fladdock is an American militia officer — "Fladdock in full militia uniform, Fladdock the General" — who appears as a guest at the [[Norris]] family gathering in New York during [[Martin Chuzzlewit Jr.]]'s visit to America (017-103). His title and uniform are objects of both local deference and Dickens's satirical gaze.

## The Norris Gathering

Fladdock's arrival at the Norrises' creates a stir among the assembled company. He is announced with the exclamation "Jiniral Fladdock!" — the American pronunciation rendering "General" as "Jiniral," a marker of the democratic leveling that Dickens finds alternately amusing and alarming in American society (017-078). His entrance is described as an "unlooked-for appearance" that galvanizes the room (017-082).

The Norris family — particularly [[Norris|Mr. Norris]] and his wife — treat Fladdock with exaggerated reverence. He "shook hands with the Norrises three times all round," and the family presses around him, most concerned to learn "to what extent he had become acquainted with the great dukes, lords, viscounts, marquesses, duchesses, knights, and baronets, in whom the people of those benighted countries had delight" (017-084). The irony is double: the Norrises, while professing American republican principles, are desperate to connect themselves, through Fladdock, to European aristocracy.

## Later Mention

Fladdock is referenced again in Chapter XXXIV, when the question "over with our friend General Fladdock, is in port, of course?" suggests his ongoing place in the American social circle that Martin has encountered (034-158).

## Narrative Function

General Fladdock is one of Dickens's caricatures of American types in the novel's American chapters (Ch. XVI–XVII, XXXIII–XXXIV). He represents the intersection of military vanity and social climbing: a militia officer whose rank carries more weight in the drawing-room than on any battlefield. His presence at the Norris gathering allows Dickens to expose the Norris family's hypocrisy — their professed scorn for European titles coexists with an eager curiosity about which aristocrats Fladdock has met.

Like [[Mr. Jefferson Brick]], the war correspondent of [[The Rowdy Journal]], and [[Hannibal Chollop]], the frontier ruffian, Fladdock belongs to the gallery of American grotesques through whom Dickens satirises the gap between American democratic ideals and American social practice.
