# "'Mr. Pecksniff was a moral man'"

The narrator's introduction of [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] in Chapter II is the novel's most sustained passage of ironic characterization—a description in which every word of praise is a knife-thrust, and the accumulation of virtues becomes an inventory of vices. The passage is a textbook demonstration of Dickens's satirical method.

## The Quote

> "Mr. Pecksniff was a moral man—a grave man, a man of noble sentiments and speech—and he had had her christened Mercy. Mercy! oh, what a charming name for such a pure-souled being as the youngest Miss Pecksniff! Her sister's name was Charity. There was a good thing! Mercy and Charity!"
>
> "It was once said of him by a homely admirer, that he had a Fortunatus's purse of good sentiments in his inside. In this particular he was like the girl in the fairy tale, except that if they were not actual diamonds which fell from his lips, they were the very brightest paste, and shone prodigiously. He was a most exemplary man; fuller of virtuous precept than a copy book. Some people likened him to a direction-post, which is always telling the way to a place, and never goes there; but these were his enemies, the shadows cast by his brightness; that was all. His very throat was moral."
>
> (002-003)

## Context

The passage occurs early in Chapter II, as Dickens introduces the novel's central figure of [[hypocrisy]]. Pecksniff is established through a method of cumulative ironic praise: each assertion of his morality is undermined by the very language used to make it. He is "a moral man" whose morals are paste diamonds; a "direction-post" that never arrives at its destination; a throat that "was moral"—the absurdity of the physical detail betraying the hollowness of the moral claim.

## Significance

The passage demonstrates three essential techniques of Dickensian characterization:

1. **Ironic accumulation**: Each sentence adds another layer of praise that is simultaneously an exposure. "Fuller of virtuous precept than a copy-book"—but a copy-book merely *copies* virtue; it does not practice it.

2. **Physical metaphor for moral condition**: "His very throat was moral. You saw a good deal of it." The physicality of the description—the low fence of white cravat, the valley of throat—makes the moral judgment concrete. We *see* Pecksniff before we hear him.

3. **The hostile witness**: "Some people likened him to a direction-post... but these were his enemies, the shadows cast by his brightness." Dickens ventriloquizes Pecksniff's defenders—and in doing so, plants the accusation while pretending to dismiss it. The reader understands that the "enemies" are right.
