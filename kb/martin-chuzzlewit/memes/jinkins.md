# Mr. Jinkins

A young gentleman boarder at [[todgers|Todgers's]] Commercial Boardinghouse, an architectural pupil whose chief claim to narrative attention is his status as the butt of his fellow boarders' jokes — a figure known to the reader almost entirely through the comic artifacts left in his absence.

## Overview

Mr. Jinkins is one of the permanent boarders at [[todgers|Todgers's]], part of the floating population of young professional men who give the establishment its character (009-033). He is introduced not in person but through his belongings: a pair of gaiters chalked with the legend "Jinkins's Particular" and a "sketch in profile, claiming to be the portrait of Jinkins himself"(009-006). This introduction — through the jokes others make at his expense rather than through his own presence — establishes his narrative function: Jinkins exists to be laughed at, a figure of gentle mockery in the boardinghouse's domestic comedy.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Jinkins is an architectural pupil, one of several young men studying the profession while lodging at [[todgers|Todgers's]]. His background is otherwise unspecified; he is a type — the young boarder, the object of communal jest — rather than a fully individuated character.

### Role in the Plot

Jinkins's most significant narrative moment is his "round-robin" — a petition circulated among the boarders at [[todgers|Todgers's]] proposing some collective action. [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]], with "mysterious intelligence," brings the matter to [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]: "Are we ready... to send a favourable reply to Mr. Jinkins's round-robin? That's the first question, [[seth-pecksniff|Mr. Pecksniff]]"(009-035). [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s response — mistaking [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]] for [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] in his distraction — deflects the matter into comedy without resolution, and Jinkins's round-robin, like Jinkins himself, fades from the narrative without consequence (009-107).

The boarders' mockery of Jinkins continues throughout the [[todgers|Todgers's]] chapters. The sketches on his gaiters, the jokes at breakfast, the casual cruelties of boardinghouse life — these constitute the texture of Jinkins's existence in the novel. He is always present and never central, a background figure whose function is to populate the world of [[todgers|Todgers's]] with the minor key of masculine bonhomie.

He reappears briefly toward the novel's end, attending a wedding-related gathering: "Mr. Jinkins ran off to see, accompanied by Mr. [[george-chuzzlewit|George Chuzzlewit]] the bachelor cousin, who preferred anything to the aggravation of sitting near the breakfast, without being able to eat it"(052-015).

### Fate & Resolution

Jinkins's fate is not resolved. He continues boarding at [[todgers|Todgers's]], presumably, an architectural pupil whose career and personal life remain beyond the novel's horizon. His narrative function — to be a face in the crowd — is complete long before the plot resolves.

## Character Analysis

Jinkins is a minor character in the strictest sense: he contributes almost nothing to the plot and exists primarily as an element of atmosphere. But his very insignificance is instructive. In a novel crowded with vivid grotesques and moral exemplars, Jinkins represents the ordinary — the young man of no particular distinction who populates the boardinghouses and offices of Dickens's [[london|London]], neither villain nor hero, simply *there*.

The comic artifacts associated with him — the chalked gaiters, the profile sketch — suggest a convivial masculine culture at [[todgers|Todgers's]] in which Jinkins serves as the designated target. This culture of gentle mockery is the boardinghouse's social glue, and Jinkins's willingness (or inability) to occupy the role of comic butt makes him, in his small way, a contributor to the establishment's domestic economy.

His round-robin, briefly elevated to narrative significance by [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]'s "mysterious intelligence"(009-035), is characteristic: it promises agency and delivers nothing. Jinkins attempts collective action and the attempt dissolves into [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]ian distraction. In this, he is a miniature version of many characters in the novel who try to assert themselves against larger forces and find their efforts absorbed into the surrounding comedy.
