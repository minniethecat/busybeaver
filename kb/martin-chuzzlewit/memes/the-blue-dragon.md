# The Blue Dragon

A village alehouse near Salisbury, Wiltshire, presided over by the good-natured landlady Mrs. Lupin. The Blue Dragon serves as the primary social hub for the Pecksniff household's village, where chance arrivals and accidental meetings drive the early plot of the novel.

## Overview

The Blue Dragon is introduced in the opening chapters as a weather-beaten inn whose creaking signboard announces its presence to the village. Its location, conveniently close to both the village and the residence of Mr. Pecksniff, makes it a natural gathering place -- and the site of the novel's first major coincidence, when a mysterious sick stranger is carried to its door. This stranger turns out to be Martin Chuzzlewit the elder, travelling with his companion [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]], and his arrival at the Dragon sets the entire Pecksniff plot in motion. As Dickens sardonically notes in the chapter heading for [[ch07|Chapter 7]], the inn even becomes a battlefield of sorts: the chapter is titled "In Which Mr. Chevy Slyme Asserts the Independence of His Spirit, and the Blue Dragon Loses a Limb" (007-001).


> Mention has been already made more than once, of a certain Dragon who swung and creaked complainingly before the village alehouse door.
> (003-002)

## Description

The inn is first seen through Dickens's atmospheric description of the Wiltshire village at dusk. The wind deals the old sign "such a cuff as it went, that the Blue Dragon was more rampant than usual ever afterwards, and indeed, before Christmas, reared clean out of its crazy frame" (002-008). The sign -- a faded, ancient dragon swinging and creaking before the alehouse door -- is a recurring image, its complaints in the wind a familiar sound to all who know the village (003-002).

The inn's interior is comfortable and welcoming, reflecting its landlady's character. The mistress of the Blue Dragon is described as "broad, buxom, comfortable, and good looking, with a face of clear red and white, which, by its jovial aspect, at once bore testimony to her hearty participation in the good things of the larder and cellar" (003-012). The Dragon has a bar where Mr. Pecksniff meditates, a sick chamber where the elder Martin recovers, and a general atmosphere of warmth and good cheer that contrasts sharply with the cold Pecksniff household.

## Inhabitants & Visitors

- [[mrs-lupin|Mrs. Lupin]] -- Landlady; "in that name the Blue Dragon was licensed to furnish entertainment, both to man and beast" (003-044)
- [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] -- Former employee, "formerly of the Blue Dragon," whose job it is to be jolly under adverse circumstances (013-162)
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Martin Chuzzlewit Sr.]] -- Arrives incognito as a sick stranger in Chapter 3, nursed by Mary Graham under Mrs. Lupin's roof
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] -- Nurses the elder Martin at the Dragon, forming an early friendship with Mrs. Lupin
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] -- Frequent visitor; meditates at the bar and receives the Chuzzlewit family siege from the Dragon
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] -- Receives Mary Graham's letters through Mrs. Lupin at the Dragon (014-046)
- [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] -- Returns to the Dragon in the novel's closing chapters

## Events

- The mysterious stranger's arrival -- The elder Martin Chuzzlewit, incognito, is brought to the Dragon as a sick traveller, nursed by Mary Graham as Mrs. Lupin looks on ((003-006), (003-012))
- The Chuzzlewit siege -- The family descends upon the Dragon to pressure the elder Martin, "and formally invested it; and Martin Chuzzlewit was in a state of siege" ([[ch04|004-076--004-077]])
- Chevy Slyme's confrontation -- Chapter 7's comic episode in which the Dragon's sign loses a limb in a fracas
- Mary Graham's correspondence -- Mary writes to Tom Pinch under cover to Mrs. Lupin at the Dragon, making the inn a secret post-office for the novel's romance plot (014-046)
- The happy reunion -- In the final chapters, Tom Pinch remarks that he is as well known in the village "as the Church steeple, or the Blue Dragon," a measure of how central the inn is to the novel's geography (039-085)
