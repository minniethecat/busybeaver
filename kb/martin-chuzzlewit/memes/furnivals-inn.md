# Furnival's Inn

Furnival's Inn, High Holborn — John Westlock's chambers in London, a quiet enclave within the city's bustle that serves as sanctuary, meeting-place, and symbol of the novel's moral centre. Tom Pinch finds his way there after escaping Pecksniff's household, and it becomes the staging-ground for the novel's final redemptive movements.

## Overview

Furnival's Inn is more than a London address in *Martin Chuzzlewit*; it is the geographical counterweight to Pecksniff's Wiltshire hypocrisy and Jonas Chuzzlewit's criminal London. John Westlock, the novel's most unambiguously decent young man, lives there — and it is to Furnival's Inn that Tom Pinch instinctively flees after his devastating break with Pecksniff (036-001). Throughout the latter half of the novel, the Inn functions as a safe harbour where plans are laid, alliances formed, and the machinery of justice set in motion against Jonas Chuzzlewit.

## Description

Dickens describes Furnival's Inn with characteristic economy and atmosphere: "There is little enough to see in Furnival's Inn. It is a shady, quiet place, echoing to the footsteps of the stragglers who have business there; and rather monotonous and gloomy on summer evenings" (045-045). Yet its very quietness is its virtue — a retreat from the noise of the city and the noise of Pecksniff's rhetoric alike.

The Inn's location in High Holborn places it within London's legal quarter, appropriate for Westlock's professional life, but also within easy walking distance of Islington and the City. Tom Pinch's journey to reach it — a comic misadventure involving wrong turnings, the Monument, and an awkward encounter with Charity and Mercy Pecksniff — is one of the novel's most endearing sequences (036-040).

## Events

- **Tom's First Arrival** — After his devastating break with Pecksniff, Tom Pinch finds his way to Furnival's Inn, where John Westlock welcomes him without question (036-001)
- **The Search for Lodgings** — Tom and his sister Ruth discover "two small bedrooms and a triangular parlour" nearby, referenced to John Westlock of "Furnival's Inn, High Holborn" (037-010)
- **Martin's Return** — Martin Chuzzlewit, returned from America, comes to Furnival's Inn to reunite with Tom and plan the exposure of Pecksniff (045-001)
- **The Evening at the Window** — Tom, Ruth, Martin, and Mary spend an enchanted evening at the Inn's window, the city's hum and the church clocks' chime marking their quiet happiness (045-045)
- **Nadgett's Surveillance** — The detective Nadgett's investigation of Jonas Chuzzlewit is coordinated from and reported at the Inn (048-001)
- **The Final Resolution** — Plans for confronting Jonas, exposing the murder, and restoring Martin's fortunes are all laid at Furnival's Inn

## Narrative Function

Furnival's Inn represents the domestic and moral alternative to every corrupt institution in the novel. Against Pecksniff's architectural fraudulence stands Westlock's genuine chambers; against Jonas's secretive criminality stands the Inn's open-door policy for friends. It is no accident that the novel's climactic justice — the exposure of Jonas's crimes and the restoration of rightful relationships — is orchestrated from this "shady, quiet place." The Inn is, in Dickens's moral geography, what home should be: a place of welcome, candour, and action on behalf of others.

## Quotations

Dickens renders Furnival's Inn as a sanctuary of abundance and comfort:

> A famous Inn! the hall a very grove of dead game, and dangling joints of mutton; and in one corner an illustrious larder, with glass doors, developing cold fowls and noble joints, and tarts wherein the raspberry jam coyly withdrew itself, as such a precious creature should, behind a lattice work of pastry.
>
> (012-054)

The Inn serves as John Westlock's London home and a place of refuge for Tom Pinch:

> John Westlock lived in Furnival's Inn, High Holborn, which was within a quarter of an hour's walk of Tom's starting-point, but seemed a long way off, by reason of his going two or three miles out of the straight road to make a shortcut.
>
> (036-049)
