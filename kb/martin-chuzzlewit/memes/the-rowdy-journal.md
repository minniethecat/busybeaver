# The Rowdy Journal

The *New York Rowdy Journal* is the fictional newspaper edited by Colonel Diver that appears in the American chapters of *Martin Chuzzlewit*. It represents Dickens's most concentrated satire of American journalism, which he had observed at first hand during his 1842 tour of the United States—a press culture he regarded as combining the worst excesses of partisanship, sensationalism, and character assassination.

## Overview

The *Rowdy Journal* is introduced to [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]] during his Atlantic crossing aboard the steamship *Screw*. Colonel Diver is traveling with his war correspondent, [[mr-jefferson-brick|mr. jefferson brick]] (016-058), a diminutive figure whose martial rhetoric is comically disproportionate to his physical presence. The newspaper's name is itself the satire: "Rowdy" is offered without irony as a journalistic brand, collapsing the distinction between reporting and the violence it purports to document (016-005).

## Satirical Function

Dickens's target is the "penny press" of Jacksonian America—newspapers that had abandoned any pretense of objectivity in favor of partisan vituperation. The *Rowdy Journal*'s method is to attack individuals rather than arguments, to inflate trivia into scandal, and to treat every subject as an occasion for rhetorical violence. Colonel Diver is the embodiment of this culture: a man for whom "editing" means amplifying whatever will sell, regardless of truth.

The satire is double-edged in a way characteristic of the American chapters: Dickens condemns American journalism while also exposing [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin's]] English condescension. Martin is horrified by the *Rowdy Journal*, but his horror is partly snobbery—the English gentleman's disdain for democratic coarseness.

## Key Scenes

- Introduction aboard the *Screw*: Colonel Diver and Jefferson Brick are introduced as fellow passengers; their conversation establishes the *Rowdy Journal*'s tone (016-005)
- The New York office: Martin visits the *Rowdy Journal*'s offices and witnesses its culture of systematic defamation (017-001)
- The "Rowdy" method: Diver explains his editorial philosophy, which consists of accusing public figures of corruption regardless of evidence (016-082)

## Quotations

Colonel Diver defines his newspaper's social position with magnificent self-satisfaction:

> "The New York Rowdy Journal, sir," resumed the colonel, "is, as I expect you know, the organ of our aristocracy in this city."
>
> (016-035)

The Rowdy Journal is hawked alongside papers whose names advertise their own dishonesty — the Sewer, the Stabber, the Peeper, the Plunderer, the Keyhole Reporter:

> "Here's this morning's New York Sewer!" cried one. "Here's this morning's New York Stabber! Here's the New York Family Spy! Here's the New York Private Listener! Here's the New York Peeper! Here's the New York Plunderer! Here's the New York Keyhole Reporter! Here's the New York Rowdy Journal! Here's all the New York papers!"
>
> (016-003)
