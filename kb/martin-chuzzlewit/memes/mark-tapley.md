# Mark Tapley

Mark Tapley is the novel's counter-force to [[selfishness]] — a man whose entire moral philosophy consists of remaining "jolly" under circumstances that give him no reason to be. Formerly the pot-boy at the [[the-blue-dragon|Blue Dragon Inn]] in [[salisbury|Salisbury]], Mark attaches himself to [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] Chuzzlewit and follows him to [[america|America]], where the catastrophic settlement of [[eden|Eden]] tests his principle to its limit. If [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] is the novel's still center of goodness, Mark Tapley is its active, comic, indomitable assertion that cheerfulness is a moral achievement.

## Overview

Mark first appears greeting [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] at the turnpike, already restless: the Blue Dragon is too comfortable. "What's the credit of being jolly when everything's agreeable?" he asks. His project is to find adversity sufficient to make his jolliness meaningful. When [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] departs for [[america|America]], Mark seizes the opportunity. "If I an't your nat'ral born servant, hired by Fate," he declares, striking the table, "there an't such a thing in natur' as a Blue Dragon" (013-179).

## Biography

### Background & Family

Mark was the general factotum at the Blue Dragon, a [[salisbury|Salisbury]] inn kept by [[mrs-lupin|Mrs. Lupin]], a widow who is clearly fond of him. He refuses her unspoken offer of marriage — not because he does not care for her but because the Blue Dragon is too pleasant a situation to test his philosophy. "What would be the likely consequence of us two being married? If I can't be content and comfortable in this here lively Dragon now, is it to be looked for as I should be then?" (007-133). His departure for [[america|America]] is a comic perversity that disguises a real moral originality.

### Role in the Plot

Mark accompanies [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] on the perilous voyage to [[america|America]] and proves indispensable. During the Atlantic crossing, when illness breaks out among the steerage passengers, Mark's usefulness is "much enlarged; and at all times and seasons there he was exerting it" (015-039). In [[america|America]], he navigates the [[deception|fraud]]ulent world of land speculators with more instinctive wariness than the better-educated [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]].

The [[eden|Eden]] episode is Mark's supreme test. When both men fall ill with fever in the malarial swamp, Mark [[nursing|nurse]]s [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] through his own sickness without complaint. "If ever he had thought [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] selfish or inconsiderate," the narrator notes, "he now forgot it" (033-110). When Mark himself collapses, the reversal is complete: [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]], transformed by Mark's example, [[nursing|nurse]]s him in turn. Mark's jolliness has done what no sermon could — it has converted the convert.

Mark also plays a crucial role in the novel's resolution. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] enlists him (via [[john-westlock|John Westlock]]) as a confidential messenger, and Mark witnesses [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s downfall with immense satisfaction. At the novel's close, he marries [[mrs-lupin|Mrs. Lupin]] and becomes the landlord of the Blue Dragon in truth, having proved to his own satisfaction that "a man as could come through half a dozen illnesses in a swamp, hadn't need to be afeard of a landlady's eye" (052-116).

### Fate & Resolution

Mark returns to the Blue Dragon, marries [[mrs-lupin|Mrs. Lupin]], and presides over the inn as its proprietor — a comic fulfillment of his earlier refusal. His final dinner party, prepared under "the joint direction of himself and his Intended," serves as the celebration of all the novel's reunions (053-064).

## Character Analysis

Mark Tapley belongs to a line of Dickensian comic servants — Sam Weller in *Pickwick*, Mark Clark in *The Chimes* — but he is more than comic relief. His philosophy of jolliness is a genuine ethical position: the belief that cheerfulness under difficulty is the only cheerfulness worth having, and that moral worth is measured not by comfort but by what one can endure without complaint.

He is the novel's practical answer to [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]. Where [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] talks about virtue and does nothing, Mark never moralizes and does everything — [[nursing|nurse]]s the sick, carries the weak, survives the swamp. His language is comic and colloquial, but his actions are the novel's most sustained demonstration of what [[generosity|generous]] selflessness [[appearance|looks]] like in practice. He is, in effect, the novel's working model of the good life.

## Key Relationships

- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]] — master and friend; Mark's example is the catalyst for [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s moral transformation (033-117)
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — fellow spirit of generosity; they recognize each other at once as "two of a [[commerce|trade]]" (007-108)
- [[mrs-lupin|Mrs. Lupin]] — landlady of the Blue Dragon and eventual wife; their relationship is the novel's portrait of [[love|affection]] without sentimentality
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — employer in the final chapters; [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] trusts Mark as a confidential agent
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — the antithesis of everything Mark represents; Mark witnesses his downfall with righteous satisfaction
