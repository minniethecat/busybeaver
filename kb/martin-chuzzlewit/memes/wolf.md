# Wolf

Mr. Wolf is a literary gentleman — the editor of a "remarkably clever weekly paper" — who appears in a single scene of the novel as one of [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]]'s disreputable dinner guests. Along with [[pip|Mr. Pip]], Wolf represents the London demi-monde of journalism and entertainment that Tigg cultivates for his schemes (028-028).

## The Dinner Scene

Tigg introduces Wolf to [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] at the dinner as "literary character — you needn't mention it — remarkably clever weekly paper — oh, remarkably clever!" (028-017). Wolf, folding his arms, "resumed his seat in a kind of soft and sleepy mystery" (028-018). Wolf's contribution to the evening's entertainment consists of reciting "the leading points of one or two vastly humorous articles he was then preparing," which are described as being of "a warm complexion" — Dickens's euphemism for mildly scandalous content (028-046).

Wolf's most notable anecdote concerns the late father of [[montague-tigg|Tigg]], who reportedly said of him: "'What's the editor's name, what's the editor's name?' 'Wolf.' 'Wolf, eh? Sharp biter, Wolf. We must keep the Wolf from the door, as the proverb says'" (028-060). The pun on his name — Wolf at the door — is the kind of laboured witticism that passes for cleverness in Tigg's circle, and Wolf's pride in recounting it reveals a man who mistakes being noticed by his betters for being respected by them.

## Narrative Function

Wolf and [[pip|Pip]] serve as specimens of the company [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] is entering. They are not criminals themselves, but their eager participation in Tigg's hospitality — knowing, as they must, that it is funded by fraud — implicates them in the moral atmosphere of the [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee]]. Wolf's "warm" journalism and Pip's theatrical anecdotes are the cultural equivalent of Tigg's financial schemes: entertainments that dress emptiness in the borrowed clothes of substance.
