# "Mrs. Gamp: 'Leave the bottle on the chimley-piece'"

Spoken by [[sarah-gamp|Sarah Gamp]] to [[seth-pecksniff|Mr. Pecksniff]] in Chapter XXV, defending—through the convenient testimony of her imaginary friend Mrs. Harris—her practice of keeping a bottle close at hand while nursing the dying. The speech is the definitive expression of Mrs. Gamp's comic method: self-interest presented as professional necessity, drunkenness disguised as medicinal "nerve."

## The Quote

> "If it wasn't for the nerve a little sip of liquor gives me (I never was able to do more than taste it), I never could go through with what I sometimes has to do. 'Mrs. Harris,' I says, at the very last case as ever I acted in, which it was but a young person, 'Mrs. Harris,' I says, 'leave the bottle on the chimley-piece, and don't ask me to take none, but let me put my lips to it when I am so dispoged, and then I will do what I'm engaged to do, according to the best of my ability.' 'Mrs. Gamp,' she says, in answer, 'if ever there was a sober creetur to be got at eighteen pence a day for working people, and three and six for gentlefolks—night watching,' said Mrs. Gamp with emphasis, 'being a extra charge—you are that inwallable person.'"
>
> (025-005)

## Context

Mrs. Gamp has been summoned to [[pecksniff-house|Mr. Pecksniff's house]] to attend a patient. In the carriage on the way, she delivers this defence of her professional method. The speech invokes her imaginary friend Mrs. Harris—a woman who exists only as a rhetorical device, always available to confirm whatever Mrs. Gamp needs confirmed. Mrs. Harris testifies to Mrs. Gamp's sobriety, her skill, her "inwallable" character; that Mrs. Harris does not exist only strengthens the comedy, since the testimony is circular: Mrs. Gamp invents a witness to praise Mrs. Gamp.

## Significance

The speech is a masterpiece of comic self-justification. Every element serves the same purpose:

- **"the nerve a little sip of liquor gives me"**: Alcohol is reframed as medicine—not pleasure but professional necessity
- **"I never was able to do more than taste it"**: The denial that invites disbelief; Mrs. Gamp's consumption is legendary
- **"leave the bottle on the chimley-piece"**: The definitive formulation—not drinking, merely having the bottle "so dispoged" that she may "put my lips to it"
- **"eighteen pence a day for working people, and three and six for gentlefolks"**: Gamp's sliding scale of mortality, her professional rate-card slipped into a speech about sobriety
- **Mrs. Harris**: The imaginary friend who authenticates everything, a character so vivid in Gamp's narration that she nearly becomes real to the reader

The bottle on the chimneypiece is the novel's supreme emblem of self-deception made comic: the thing Mrs. Gamp pretends not to be doing while doing it continuously.
