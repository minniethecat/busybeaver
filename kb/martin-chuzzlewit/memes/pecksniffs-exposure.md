# The Exposure of Pecksniff

The climactic scene in which Old Martin Chuzzlewit — having spent the entire novel feigning senility and dependence upon Seth Pecksniff — drops his mask and publicly denounces the architect as "hypocrite," "smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave," stripping him of fortune, standing, and the house he has usurped. It is the emotional and structural pivot of *Martin Chuzzlewit*, the point at which all deceptions collapse and justice is dispensed.

## Overview

Throughout the novel, Old Martin Chuzzlewit has appeared broken, manipulated, and diminished — a man whose wealth made him a target for Pecksniff's "saintly semblances" and a prisoner in the house he paid for (036-008). In Chapter 52, the truth is revealed: Old Martin has never been deceived. He has deliberately placed himself in Pecksniff's power, the better to observe the full depths of the man's hypocrisy and to effect a dramatic, public humiliation that would open the eyes of all who had been taken in. As he declares: "I could not tell how far your specious hypocrisy had deceived him, knave; and knew no better way of opening his eyes than by presenting you before him in your own servile character" (052-015).

## Prelude

The exposure has been prepared across the entire novel. Old Martin's apparent weakness — his tolerance of Pecksniff's flattery, his rejection of his own grandson, his complicity in Pecksniff's scheme to separate Martin from Mary Graham — has been a performance. Key foreshadowing moments include Old Martin calling Pecksniff "a hypocrite" to his face early in the novel (004-025), and the burning of the will that would have sanctioned Martin and Mary's marriage, a document destroyed because Martin "had distrusted him again" after Pecksniff's intervention (052-030).

The immediate prelude involves Old Martin, Tom Pinch, John Westlock, Martin the younger, and Mary Graham gathering at the Temple. Old Martin has orchestrated the assembly: "the grace of his design" — a scheme built on patience, observation, and the conviction that Pecksniff would ultimately condemn himself through his own actions (052-030).

## The Event

The denunciation unfolds in a series of escalating revelations:

**Old Martin reveals his deliberate scheme.** He confesses that he "put myself in that man's hands on terms as mean and base, and as degrading to himself as I could render them in words" — testing Pecksniff's character by offering him the most humiliating terms of service, which Pecksniff eagerly accepted (052-020).

**The accusation of predation.** Old Martin accuses Pecksniff of having "already spread your nets" for young Martin — laying plans to capture him as a son-in-law for one of his daughters — even before the grandfather had reconciled with his grandson: "Counting on the restoration of the love you knew I bore him, you designed him for one of your two daughters" (052-010).

**The verdict delivered.** "Listen, hypocrite! Listen, smooth-tongued, servile, crawling knave!" (052-010). Old Martin strips Pecksniff of every pretence, and the effect is total:

> The whole of these proceedings were so agreeable to Mr. Pecksniff that he stood with his eyes fixed upon the floor and his hands clasping one another alternately, as if a host of penal sentences were being passed upon him. Not only did his figure appear to have shrunk, but his discomfiture seemed to have extended itself even to his dress. His clothes seemed to have grown shabbier, his linen to have turned yellow, his hair to have become lank and frowsy; his very boots looked villainous and dim, as if their gloss had departed with his own.
>
> (052-035)

Dickens's description of Pecksniff's physical diminishment is a masterstroke — the external collapse mirrors the internal vacancy now exposed to all.

## Aftermath

The immediate consequences are absolute. Pecksniff is expelled from the house he has occupied. Tom Pinch, whose earlier discovery of Pecksniff's hypocrisy (036-001) had been a private sorrow, now sees him publicly unmasked. Martin and Mary are reconciled to Old Martin and to each other. The will is restored, the inheritance redirected.

But Dickens deepens the exposure beyond the immediate scene. Pecksniff does not reform; "the more he was found out, the more hypocrisy he practised" (044-001) — a revelation of character that makes him one of Dickens's most memorable villains precisely because he is incapable of change. The novel's final glimpse of Pecksniff shows him, years later, reduced to drunken beggary yet still performing piety — a man whose essence is performance and who, stripped of an audience, is truly nothing.

## Narrative Function

The exposure of Pecksniff is the structural climax of the novel's moral argument. It proves what the narrative has insisted from the first: that selfishness, when cloaked in virtue, is the most dangerous form of evil. Old Martin's patience — his willingness to let Pecksniff dig his own grave — is the counterpoint to the novel's other exposures (Jonas's crimes, the American frauds), but it is also the most satisfying because it is the most complete. Pecksniff is not merely punished; he is *revealed*, and in the revelation, every character who was taken in by him is forced to confront their own complicity in preferring flattery to truth.
