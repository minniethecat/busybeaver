# Class and Gentility

The question "what makes a gentleman?" runs through *Martin Chuzzlewit* at every social level, from the Chuzzlewit family's pretensions to the City clerk's aspirations to the American democrats' repudiation of the very concept. Dickens is not interested in class as a fixed hierarchy but as a moral performance: who can convincingly *act* the gentleman, and what that acting reveals about character.

## The Performance of Gentility

### Pecksniff's Performance

[[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] is the novel's supreme artist of gentility. His architectural practice is a front; his real profession is the performance of moral and social superiority. He speaks in pious platitudes, refers constantly to his "duty" and his "conscience," and wraps every self-interested action in the language of disinterested benevolence. His house, with its "charming prospect" and its drawing-boards perpetually turned to [[salisbury|Salisbury Cathedral]], is the stage set for this performance (002-029).

What makes Pecksniff's gentility hollow is precisely its perfection. Dickens shows us Pecksniff alone, drinking, calculating—and the contrast between the public performance and the private man is the measure of his fraudulence. He is "a Hypocrite," as he himself inadvertently admits when denying he is "a Brute" (008-001).

### Tigg's Transformation

[[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] performs the most dramatic class transformation in the novel. He arrives as a shabby sponger cadging crown-pieces at [[the-blue-dragon|the Blue Dragon]]; he re-emerges as "Tigg Montague, Esquire (of Pall Mall and Bengal)," chairman of the [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee]]. The transformation is purely sartorial and rhetorical—his character is unchanged—yet it is sufficient to extract thousands of pounds from respectable investors. Dickens's point is sharp: in a society that judges by surfaces, the surface *is* the reality.

## True Gentility

Against the performers, Dickens sets characters whose gentility is inward and therefore invisible to the status-obsessed:

**[[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]]** is the novel's clearest case. He is poor, socially awkward, and perpetually undervalued. Pecksniff patronizes him. Yet Tom possesses every quality the novel associates with true gentility: loyalty, honesty, learning, kindness, and a complete absence of self-regard. His "wonderful faith" in Pecksniff's pretensions is, ironically, proof of his own superiority—only a genuinely good man would be so incapable of recognizing bad faith (005-002).

**[[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]]** reverses the class logic entirely. His ambition is not to rise but to maintain his cheerfulness under the worst possible conditions. He measures status not by wealth or title but by "credit" for jolliness—a moral economy that makes the novel's social climbing look absurd by comparison.

**[[john-westlock|John Westlock]]** represents the emerging Victorian ideal of the professional gentleman: independent, competent, generous without ostentation. His dinner for Tom and Martin at Salisbury's finest hotel is an act of friendship, not display.

## America and the Rejection of Class

The American chapters offer a satirical counterpoint. In [[eden|Eden]] and on the *Screw*, Americans loudly repudiate English class distinctions—while practicing their own forms of snobbery and exclusion. The American "gentleman" is defined by the assertion that "all men are created equal" combined with the certainty that some are "more equal" than others. Dickens's satire cuts both ways: English gentility is hollow performance, but American egalitarianism is hollow rhetoric.

## The Language of "Gentleman"

The word "gentleman" appears several hundred times in the novel, almost always ironically. Characters invoke it to claim status they do not possess or to insinuate obligations they do not intend to honor. The frequency of the word is inversely proportional to its truth-value: the more a character says "gentleman," the less gentlemanly he is.

## Quotations

The novel's satirical treatment of class pretension is established from the opening chapter:

> As no lady or gentleman, with any claims to polite breeding, can possibly sympathize with the Chuzzlewit Family without being first assured of the extreme antiquity of the race, it is a great satisfaction to know that it undoubtedly descended in a direct line from Adam and Eve; and was, in the very earliest times, closely connected with the agricultural interest.
>
> (001-002)

Pecksniff's hollow performance of moral and social superiority is captured in his pledge to Tom Pinch:

> "And Thomas Pinch and I," said Mr. Pecksniff, in a deeper voice, "will walk it yet, in mutual faithfulness and friendship! And if it comes to pass that either of us be run over in any of those busy crossings which divide the streets of life, the other will convey him to the hospital in Hope, and sit beside his bed in Bounty!"
>
> (005-130)
