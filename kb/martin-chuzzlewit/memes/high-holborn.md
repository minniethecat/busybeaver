# High Holborn

High Holborn is the London thoroughfare that serves as the geographical spine of several of the novel's key London locations. Off it run [[kingsgate-street|Kingsgate Street]] (Mrs. [[sarah-gamp|Gamp]]'s residence) and Furnival's Inn ([[john-westlock|John Westlock]]'s chambers), making Holborn the district where the novel's virtuous characters — Tom, Ruth, John — establish their modest London lives, in pointed contrast to the grander addresses of the novel's [[selfishness|selfish]] characters.

## Narrative Geography

High Holborn first enters the novel through Mrs. Gamp: "her residence in Kingsgate Street, High Holborn" (019-003). The address places Gamp — and her peculiar form of disreputable professionalism — in the commercial heart of London. The area is neither fashionable nor slum; it is the territory of tradespeople, clerks, and nurses, the London of work rather than display.

[[john-westlock|John Westlock]]'s chambers are at Furnival's Inn, High Holborn, "which was within a quarter of an hour's walk of his office" (036-049). The proximity of Westlock's professional and domestic lives — and the proximity of the whole Holborn circle to [[islington|Islington]], where Tom and Ruth live — creates a geography of connection among the novel's good characters. They are all within walking distance of one another, a spatial manifestation of their moral community.

The neighbourhood accumulates significance as the novel progresses. By the final chapters, when Gamp's Kingsgate Street lodgings have been the scene of so much comic drama, the narrator refers casually to "Kingsgate Street, High Holborn" as though the reader were a fellow Londoner who knows the territory (049-002). The repeated invocations of Holborn give the novel a grounding in real London geography that balances its more fantastical elements.
