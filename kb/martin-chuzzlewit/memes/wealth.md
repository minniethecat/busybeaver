# Wealth

Wealth in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is never neutral. Money is the object of nearly every character's desire and the measure of nearly every character's soul. From the opening chapters' assembly of relations circling Old Martin's presumed fortune to Jonas Chuzzlewit's descent into murder, the pursuit of wealth drives the novel's plot and determines its moral universe. Dickens treats money not as a thing but as a test: what you will do to get it, and what you become once you have it, reveals who you are.

## Wealth as Moral Test

### The Inheritance Hunt

The novel opens with the Chuzzlewit family converging on [[pecksniff-house|Mr. Pecksniff's house]] and [[the-blue-dragon|the Blue Dragon]], all seeking to position themselves as [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin's]] heir. Dickens assembles a grotesque catalogue of relatives—Spottletoe, the strong-minded widow, the spinster daughters, [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony]] and [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]—each "winking their red eyes" at the prize (004-081). The inheritance hunt is the novel's structural frame, and it exposes [[selfishness]] as the family's defining trait.

### Old Martin's Experiment

Old Martin Chuzzlewit does not simply suffer his relatives' greed; he weaponizes it. His apparent feebleness, his dependence on [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], and his rejection of [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] are all revealed, in the [[old-martins-revelation|final revelation]], as components of a deliberate experiment designed to expose greed and test character. Wealth, in Old Martin's scheme, is the reagent that separates the honest from the false:

> "I have been the dupe of my own heart, [...] and have been lured and cheated by my own weakness."
>
> This acknowledgement frames Old Martin's recognition that wealth gave others the motive to deceive him and himself the blindness to be deceived.

## The Miser's Fate

[[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] represents the miser-type in its purest form. His firm, *Anthony Chuzzlewit and Son*, is housed in "a dim, dirty, smoky, tumbledown, rotten old house"—wealth accumulated without enjoyment, without generosity, without purpose beyond accumulation itself (011-054)(018-001). His death at his desk, unmourned except by the ancient clerk [[chuffey|Mr. Chuffey]], is the novel's judgment on the life devoted wholly to money:

> "I taught him. I trained him. This is the heir of my bringing-up. Sly, cunning, and covetous, he'll not squander my money."
>
> (011-042)

Anthony's pride in having made Jonas "sly, cunning, and covetous" is the novel's darkest commentary on the transmission of values through wealth: the father trains the son in avarice, and the son graduates to murder.

## Wealth and Fraud

The [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company]] extends the theme from individual greed to institutional fraud. [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]]'s company manufactures the illusion of wealth through plate glass, stucco, and a porter's red waistcoat, extracting real money from victims seduced by the appearance of prosperity (027-045). The Anglo-Bengalee is the novel's diagnosis of a commercial culture in which wealth has become decoupled from production—a world of paper, promises, and publicity.

## The Moral Alternative

Against the novel's gallery of the covetous, Dickens sets characters who are indifferent to wealth or who use it well:

- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] lives in near-poverty without resentment, his riches being wholly inward
- [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] measures prosperity by the opportunity to be "jolly" under adverse circumstances
- [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] uses his modest independence to help [[ruth-pinch|Ruth Pinch]] and Tom
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]], the novel's moral centre, refuses to use her position as Old Martin's companion for material advantage

The novel's resolution rewards these characters with modest, deserved prosperity while the schemers—Pecksniff exposed, Jonas dead by his own poison—lose everything.
