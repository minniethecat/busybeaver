# Mr. Pecksniff's House

The Wiltshire residence of Mr. [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]], situated in a village near Salisbury. More than a home, the house is an extension of Pecksniff's fraudulent persona -- a stage for the performance of moral rectitude, architectural pretension, and domestic tyranny.

## Overview

Pecksniff's house is introduced in Chapter 2, on a blustery autumn evening when the wind chases fallen leaves through the village and the daughters await their father's return. The house is both a domestic space and a professional front: "The brazen plate upon the door (which being Mr. Pecksniff's could not lie) bore this inscription, 'Pecksniff, Architect,' to which Mr. Pecksniff, on his cards of business, added, 'and Land Surveyor'" (002-028). Dickens's parenthetical aside -- "which being Mr. Pecksniff's could not lie" -- is one of the novel's sustained jokes, for Pecksniff's entire professional identity is a lie. His "architectural" practice consists almost entirely of taking in pupils and collecting rents (002-029).

The house is also the long-time home of [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]], Pecksniff's devoted -- and deceived -- assistant, who lives in a cramped chamber and believes his employer to be the best of men (005-055). The house thus embodies the novel's central theme of hypocrisy: outwardly respectable, inwardly hollow.

## Description

Dickens introduces the house through the windstorm that assaults it, with the frightened leaves "taking advantage of the sudden opening of Mr. Pecksniff's front-door, to dash wildly into his passage" (002-010). The daughters, [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] and [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy]], drag their wind-battered father inside, where the domestic rituals of tea, toast, and moral platitudes unfold.

The house's interior reflects Pecksniff's character. His study contains the paraphernalia of sham profession: rolls of paper that suggest architectural designs but which, as Tom Pinch's later discoveries reveal, amount to nothing. The house has a front parlour for receiving guests, a chamber from which Pecksniff in his nightcap waves farewell to departing pupils (005-004), and Tom Pinch's narrow bedroom with its "heartbreaking shop" of books and the organ loft that is his only escape (005-055).

The domestic arrangements are governed by the two daughters, who manage the household with a show of filial devotion that masks their own designs on fortune and marriage. The house's atmosphere of suffocating moral correctness makes it a prison for those who see through it -- and a sanctuary for those who cannot.

## Inhabitants & Visitors

- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] -- Owner and master; the house is his theatre of moral performance
- [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] -- Elder daughter; manages domestic affairs with vinegar and ambition
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] -- Younger daughter; giggling and sentimental, until marriage transforms her
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] -- Resident assistant and organist; lives in devotion to Pecksniff until his disillusionment
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit Jr.]] -- Arrives as Pecksniff's new pupil and architectural apprentice ([[ch05|005-122--005-148]])
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Martin Chuzzlewit Sr.]] -- Visits to install his grandson, setting the stage for the family conflict
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] -- Accompanies the elder Martin on his visits
- [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] -- Former pupil who escapes the house and sees through Pecksniff's hypocrisy
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] -- Visits to court the Pecksniff daughters, bringing his dark presence into the house

## Events

- The windstorm homecoming -- Pecksniff arrives battered by the wind, is nursed by his daughters in a parody of domestic tenderness ([[ch02|002-010--002-023]])
- Martin's installation -- The young Martin Chuzzlewit arrives as Pecksniff's new pupil, complete with a welcoming dinner and Pecksniff's display of architectural "designs" ([[ch05|005-122--005-166]])
- Tom Pinch's daily life -- Years of quiet service in Pecksniff's house, believing in his master's goodness while living in a "heartbreaking shop" of a room (005-055)
- The elder Martin's visits -- The patriarch visits to observe his grandson's progress and Pecksniff's character, his suspicions carefully hidden
- Tom's expulsion -- After Tom's accidental exposure of Pecksniff's designs on Mary Graham, he is cast out of the house he has served for years
- The final reckoning -- The house witnesses the collapse of Pecksniff's fortune and reputation when his treachery is fully exposed
