# Salisbury

Salisbury is the cathedral town in Wiltshire that serves as the geographical anchor for the novel's opening English scenes and as a recurring transit point throughout the narrative. Located "within an easy journey" of the village where [[pecksniff-house|Mr. Pecksniff's house]] and [[the-blue-dragon|the Blue Dragon]] inn are situated, Salisbury is the gateway between the novel's intimate village world and the wider spheres of London and America.

## Setting and Significance

### The Novel's Opening Geography

Dickens places Salisbury in the very first paragraph of the novel:

> It was pretty late in the autumn of the year, when the declining sun, struggling through the mist which had obscured it all day, looked brightly down upon a little Wiltshire village, within an easy journey of the fair old town of Salisbury.
>
> (002-001)

This opening establishes the novel's initial moral geography: a "fair old" cathedral town anchoring a rural world that is about to be disrupted by the arrival of scheming relations and grasping hypocrisy. Salisbury's cathedral—medieval, permanent, genuinely beautiful—stands in implicit contrast to the architectural frauds practiced by [[seth-pecksniff|Mr. Pecksniff]].

### Pecksniff's Architectural Pupils

Salisbury Cathedral is the perpetual subject of the endless architectural drawings produced by Pecksniff's pupils, who "improved himself, for three or five years... in making elevations of Salisbury Cathedral from every possible point of sight" (002-008). Pecksniff himself proudly displays these drawings to [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]]:

> "Salisbury Cathedral from the north. From the south. From the east. From the west. From the southeast. From the nor'west. A bridge. An almshouse. A jail. A church. A powder-magazine. A wine-cellar. A portico. A summer-house. An ice-house."
>
> (005-036)

The absurd catalogue—in which Salisbury Cathedral elevations are mixed indiscriminately with designs for jails and summer-houses—perfectly captures Pecksniff's architectural imposture. His pupils draw the cathedral endlessly but never actually learn to build.

### Arrivals and Departures

Salisbury functions as a narrative threshold throughout the novel. [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] journeys there to meet the new pupil [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]], experiencing Salisbury market-day as a bewildering carnival "teeming with all kinds of mystery and bedevilment" (005-005). Later, Tom walks to Salisbury to dine with his friend [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] (012-001). The town is repeatedly a site of meetings, partings, and transitions—a liminal space between the novel's different worlds.

### Market Day

Dickens's description of Salisbury on market day is one of the novel's great comic set pieces:

> the thoroughfares about the market-place being filled with carts, horses, donkeys, baskets, wagons, garden-stuff, meat, tripe, pies, poultry and huckster's wares of every opposite description and possible variety of character.
>
> (005-005)

The passage revels in the sheer abundance and variety of provincial English life, from the farmers with their "bulky pocket-books that when they were in their pockets it was apoplexy to get them out, and when they were out it was spasms to get them in again" to the "shaggy horses purged of all earthly passions" (005-005).
