# Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger

Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger is the protagonist whose name the novel bears and whose moral arc defines its structure. Grandson of [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit, the younger Martin begins the novel as a product of his grandfather's suspicious household — selfish, proud, and blind to his own faults. Through exile, suffering, and the unearned [[constancy|loyalty]] of [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]], he undergoes the novel's central transformation from [[selfishness]] to [[conscience|self-knowledge]], making him Dickens's most sustained study of moral growth.

## Overview

Martin is introduced in Chapter 5 when he arrives at [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s house as a new architectural pupil — actually a pawn in the inheritance struggle between his grandfather and [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]. His first defining act is defiance: he has fallen in love with [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]], his grandfather's orphan ward and companion, and refuses to give her up. Old Martin disinherits him in a scene of mutual recrimination, and Martin departs for [[america|America]] to make his own fortune (006-066). The [[america|America]]n journey — with its swindlers, land speculators, and the malarial swamp settlement called [[eden|Eden]] — becomes the crucible in which his character is remade.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Martin was raised in his grandfather's house after the death of his parents. Dickens makes clear that his selfishness was not inborn but learned: bred up in a household governed by suspicion and [[selfishness|self-interest]], "Martin had unconsciously reasoned as a child, 'My guardian takes so much thought of himself, that unless I do the like by myself, I shall be forgotten.' So he had grown selfish" (033-119). He is the grandson of [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit and the cousin (by some remove) of [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]], though the two represent opposite branches of the family's moral inheritance.

### Role in the Plot

Martin's early chapters establish him as proud and entitled. He quarrels with his grandfather, refuses [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s hypocritical tutelage, and sets out for [[america|America]] with the faithful [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] as his servant. The [[america|America]]n episodes (Chapters 13–34) form the novel's satirical centerpiece and Martin's moral education. In the [[deception|fraud]]ulent settlement of [[eden|Eden]], both men fall gravely ill. Watching [[mark-tapley|Mark]] [[nursing|nurse]] him without complaint through his own sickness, Martin confronts his selfishness for the first time: "He began to think, how was it that this man who had had so few advantages, was so much better than he who had had so many?" (033-117).

His recovery is explicitly a moral recovery. Rising from a bed of dangerous sickness, "he felt how nearly Self had dropped into the grave, and what a poor dependent, miserable thing it was" (033-120). The transformation is not instantaneous — there are no melodramatic conversions in Dickens — but cumulative, tested by continued hardship and Martin's growing care for [[mark-tapley|Mark]]. By the time they return to England, he is a changed man.

Back home, Martin must prove his [[redemption|reformation]]. He works quietly, asks nothing of his grandfather, and refuses to defend himself against [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s calumnies. His final act of [[conscience|self-knowledge]] comes in Chapter 52, when he confesses: "There is a kind of selfishness ... which is constantly upon the watch for selfishness in others; and holding others at a distance by suspicions and [[suspicion|distrust]]s, wonders why they don't approach" (052-081).

### Fate & Resolution

Martin is reconciled with his grandfather and married to [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] at the novel's close. The marriage is not merely a romantic reward but a narrative seal on his [[redemption|reformation]] — he is trusted with the love he once demanded as a right. Unlike [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] and [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], who are destroyed by their selfishness, Martin is redeemed by recognizing his own.

## Character Analysis

Martin belongs to a distinct Dickensian type: the redeemable sinner, more sympathetic than [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] or [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] because his faults are presented as inheritance rather than essence. His selfishness is an environmental condition — the air he breathed in his grandfather's house — and the novel's psychological argument is that such selfishness can be cured by suffering honestly borne and by the example of unselfishness in others.

His relationship with [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] is the structural engine of his transformation. [[mark-tapley|Mark]]'s cheerful selflessness is not preachy; it is simply present, and Martin's growth consists in learning to see it and, eventually, to emulate it. By the novel's end, Martin has internalized the novel's thesis: that the selfish person is not merely a moral failure but a practical failure, cut off from the human connections that make life worth living.

## Key Relationships

- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — grandfather; their mutual suspicion mirrors the same selfishness in two generations (006-066)
- [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]] — servant and friend; his cheerful [[patience|endurance]] in adversity teaches Martin the insufficiency of self (033-117)
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] — childhood companion and eventual wife; Martin's love for her is the one selfless instinct that survives his early pride
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — fellow pupil at [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s; their friendship survives Martin's worst period
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — architect and antagonist; [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] schemes against Martin to secure [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s inheritance (052-089)
