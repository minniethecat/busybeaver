# Shame

Shame in *Martin Chuzzlewit* functions as a diagnostic tool: Dickens measures his characters by what they are capable of feeling ashamed about, and by what they are not. The concept appears across the novel's full moral range — from the comic refrain "Oh, fie! For shame!" to the devastating convergence of "cowardice, and shame, and guilt" in Jonas Chuzzlewit's final exposure (051-122) (051-122).

## Overview

The novel's most explicit wrestling with shame occurs when young Martin declares to old Martin: "I cannot profess sorrow for that, nor irresolution in that, nor shame in that" — speaking of his love for Mary Graham (043-118) (043-118). Martin's refusal of shame here is a moral assertion: some bonds are too sacred to be disowned. This stands in contrast to Mr. Pecksniff, who deploys "shame" as a weapon against others — accusing Martin of conduct that "really casts a shadow of disgrace and shame upon this family" (004-102) (004-102) — while remaining himself incapable of feeling it.

## In the Novel

Pecksniff's use of "shame" is always performative. He threatens to "publish your shame before a third party" when confronting young Martin, framing himself as a reluctant agent of exposure forced into action by another's misconduct (012-179) (012-179). His "Oh, fie! For shame!" directed at Mercy is a transparent act of paternal authority, a verbal gesture that substitutes for genuine moral concern (020-119) (020-119).

Old Martin's confession to Mary carries a different weight: "I would resolve at no such time to bear him in my mind; wishing to spare myself the shame of such a weakness" (014-080) (014-080). Here shame is real — the self-reproach of a man who knows he has been manipulated. The distinction between Pecksniff's shamelessness and old Martin's genuine susceptibility to shame defines their moral opposition.

Mary Graham experiences "an overwhelming sense of shame and grief" when disclosing Pecksniff's designs upon her to Tom Pinch — "covering her face with her hands again, as if the passion which had sustained her through these disclosures lost itself" in shame (031-052) (031-052). Mary's shame is the reflex of a virtuous character forced to speak of what should not be spoken.

### Key Passages

The novel's most concentrated analysis of shame occurs in the Jonas denouement: "His base triumph, struggling with his cowardice, and shame, and guilt, was so detestable, that they turned away from him, as if he were some obscene and filthy animal, repugnant to the sight" (051-122) (051-122). Shame here is one strand in a knot of negative emotions, each reinforcing the others.

The comic variant appears in Pecksniff's "Oh fie! oh fie, for shame!" at a moment when "they all laughed again — especially Mr. Pecksniff" (044-045) (044-045). The laughter undercuts the moralising, exposing it as theatre.

Charity Pecksniff's complex expression — "a struggle in her face between a sense of triumph and a sense of shame" (037-038) (037-038) — captures shame as a social emotion, felt not for wrongdoing but for the exposure of vulnerability.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The Pecksniffian "Oh, fie! For shame!" becomes a verbal tic, hollowed out through repetition until it signifies nothing. Dickens's satire targets the weaponisation of shame-talk: Pecksniff's genius is to make others feel ashamed while remaining entirely shameless himself. Mrs. Gamp's cry — "Oh, fie for shame, sir, fie for shame!" — participates in the same satirical register, a woman of no moral standing claiming the high ground (029-063) (029-063).
