## Overview

Death permeates *Martin Chuzzlewit* in forms ranging from the grimly comic to the genuinely tragic. Dickens organizes the novel's mortality around two poles: the theatrical, self-serving funerals of the respectable mercantile class, and the violent, unmourned deaths of its villains. Anthony Chuzzlewit's funeral, orchestrated by the undertaker Mr. Mould, is a masterpiece of satirical excess — horses that "reared and pranced, and showed their highest action, as if they knew a man was dead, and triumphed in it" (019-099). Jonas Chuzzlewit's death, by contrast, is discovered offstage: a murderer found "leaning, in an upright position, in the angle of a doorway" with "one solitary drop of blood" upon his waistcoat.

Old Martin Chuzzlewit broods obsessively on the corruption his wealth will unleash after his death. "Another will made and destroyed," he cries, "nothing determined on, nothing done, and I might have died tonight!" For him, death is not a spiritual reckoning but a legal event — the moment when his carefully hoarded fortune passes into the hands of scheming relatives. This anxiety structures the novel's plot, driving Old Martin's elaborate masquerade of senility and his final, theatrical revelation of justice.

## Death Scenes

### Anthony Chuzzlewit's Passing

Anthony Chuzzlewit's death is the novel's first major mortality and its most elaborately staged. The old man, "a strong and vigorous old man, with a will of iron, and a voice of brass," declines gradually, his final words a warning to Mr. Pecksniff about his son Jonas's character. His last recognizable trait is suspicion: even on his deathbed, he maneuvers to control his money and his heir.

The relationship between Anthony and his ancient clerk Chuffey complicates the picture. Decades of shared commercial existence have reduced Chuffey to a "worn-out cask" (011-111), yet the old clerk's grief, when Anthony dies, is the only genuine mourning in the house:

> Chuffey, the ancient clerk, weeps for his master in the only vocabulary of affection they ever shared: "I loved him. He was good to me. We learnt Tare and Tret together at school."
>
> (019-060)

The commercial language — "Tare and Tret" are mercantile terms for weight deductions — is the only vocabulary of affection these men possess. Their entire relationship has been mediated by commerce, yet something human persists beneath the ledgers.

### The Funeral as Theatre

The funeral that follows is Dickens at his most savagely comic. Mr. Mould, the undertaker, presides over a spectacle of competitive mourning in which the trappings of grief — the hearse, the plumes, the professionally solemn attendants — eclipse any genuine feeling:

> "The four hearse-horses, especially, reared and pranced, and showed their highest action, as if they knew a man was dead, and triumphed in it. 'They break us, drive us, ride us; ill-treat, abuse, and maim us for their pleasure — But they die; Hurrah, they die!'"
>
> (019-099)

Jonas, the dead man's son, "glanc[es] stealthily out of the coach-window now and then, to observe its effect upon the crowd," treating his father's funeral as a public performance. Jonas's motto for the occasion — "Spend, and spare not!" — is itself a piece of theatrical extravagance, designed not to honor the dead but to silence critics:

> Mankind is evil in its thoughts and in its base constructions, and Jonas was resolved it should not have an inch to stretch into an ell against him. It never should be charged upon his father's son that he had grudged the money for his father's funeral.
>
> (019-002)

Only old Chuffey grieves genuinely — and his grief is disparaged by Mr. Mould as "indecent, and quite unworthy of such an occasion" (019-025). The undertaker's professional standards of "decency" have no room for authentic sorrow.

### Jonas Chuzzlewit's Murder

If Anthony's death is public theatre, Jonas's is private violence. Having murdered Montague Tigg to conceal his earlier plot against his father, Jonas is discovered dead — having taken poison to evade arrest. The news reaches Old Martin's circle as a dark counterpoint to the novel's comic resolutions. Jonas, who schemed to hasten his father's death with "warning sulkiness" and veiled threats, meets an end stripped of dignity: no funeral procession, no professional mourners, only the anonymous discovery of a body in a doorway.

The symmetry is Dickens's point: the son who treated his father's life as "a certain amount of personal estate, which had no right whatever to be going at large, but ought to be secured in that particular description of iron safe which is commonly called a coffin" (008-031), becomes a corpse himself, unclaimed and unmourned.

### The Memory of the Dead

Old Anthony's death-haunted bedroom becomes a recurring motif. When Old Martin visits the house, he climbs the stairs to the room "in which the bed stood (it was there old Anthony had died)," seeking evidence of foul play. Chuffey, whose mind wanders between past and present, breaks into the novel's feasting scenes with the phantom question: "Who's lying dead upstairs?" The dead, in *Martin Chuzzlewit*, are never quite gone — they linger as memories, accusations, and unsettled debts.

## Death as Commerce

The commercial dimension of death is personified in **Mr. Mould**, the undertaker, for whom mortality is a trade like any other. His professional grief is a commodity, scaled to the price paid:

> "This is one of the most impressive cases, sir," he continued, addressing Mr. Pecksniff, "that I have seen in the whole course of my professional experience." ... "There is no limitation, there is positively **no** limitation" — opening his eyes wide, and standing on tiptoe — "in point of expense! I have orders, sir, to put on my whole establishment of mutes; and mutes come very dear, Mr. Pecksniff; not to mention their drink. To provide silver-plated handles of the very best description, ornamented with angels' heads from the most expensive dies. To be perfectly profuse in feathers. In short, sir, to turn out something absolutely gorgeous."
>
> (019-030)

The undertaker's face embodies this contradiction: "a queer attempt at melancholy was at odds with a smirk of satisfaction; so that he looked as a man might, who, in the very act of smacking his lips over choice old wine, tried to make believe it was physic" (019-025). Grief and profit, mourning and marketing, collapse into a single expression.

Dickens draws a direct parallel between Mould's undertaking business and the novel's other commercial enterprises — Pecksniff's architectural fraud, Tigg's Anglo-Bengalee insurance swindle, Jonas's speculative ventures. All profit from human vulnerability; death is simply the most reliable market. Mould's "mutes" — professional mourners hired to look sorrowful — are the funeral trade's equivalent of Pecksniff's drawing-boards: props for a performance of competence.

This commodification extends to the living. Old Martin's relatives gather at his supposed deathbed like "a funeral party, except — a blessed exception — that there is no body in the house." Their grief is performative because their interest is financial: each relative hopes to be remembered in the will. The novel's central irony is that Old Martin, fully aware of their motives, stages his own pseudo-death — the appearance of senile decay — to test and expose them.

## Violence and Mortality

The novel's treatment of violent death centers on Jonas Chuzzlewit, whose corruption escalates from financial fraud to attempted patricide to murder. His education, Dickens notes, was "conducted from his cradle on the strictest principles of the main chance. The very first word he learnt to spell was 'gain'" (008-031). Jonas's arc traces a causal chain from mercenary self-interest to lethal violence:

> Having been long taught by his father to overreach everybody, he had imperceptibly acquired a love of overreaching that venerable monitor himself. The other [result was] that from his early habits of considering everything as a question of property, he had gradually come to look, with impatience, on his parent as a certain amount of personal estate, which had no right whatever to be going at large, but ought to be secured in that particular description of iron safe which is commonly called a coffin, and banked in the grave.
>
> (008-031)

The passage is among Dickens's most ruthless analyses of how greed breeds violence: the father who teaches his son to see the world in terms of "gain" has, by that very lesson, taught the son to see the father as an asset to be liquidated.

Yet Dickens balances this darkness with the comic resilience of characters like Mark Tapley, who contemplates becoming a gravedigger precisely because it is "a good damp, wormy sort of business" — the least jolly profession he can imagine, and therefore the greatest test of his philosophy of cheerful endurance. That even grave-digging becomes material for comedy is characteristic of the novel's tonal range: death is absurd, death is tragic, and death, above all, is *true* — the one certainty that no amount of Pecksniffian rhetoric can disguise.

At the novel's close, the natural imagery of a "dark and dreary night" offers a closing vision of mortality that transcends commerce:

> The earth covered with a sable pall as for the burial of yesterday; the clumps of dark trees, its giant plumes of funeral feathers, waving sadly to and fro: all hushed, all noiseless, and in deep repose.
>
> (015-002)

The funeral imagery remains — "sable pall," "funeral feathers" — but stripped of Mr. Mould's commercial glee. Death, in this valedictory note, is not a business opportunity but a natural condition.
