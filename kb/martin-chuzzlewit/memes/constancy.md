# Constancy

Constancy — the quality of unwavering loyalty, steadfast commitment, and faithful endurance — is one of the moral anchors of Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit*. In a novel populated by schemers, hypocrites, and self-seekers who shift allegiance with the wind, the constant heart becomes a rare and precious thing. Tom Pinch's constancy to Pecksniff, Ruth Pinch's constancy to her brother, and Mary Graham's constancy to Martin form the moral bedrock against which the novel measures all other relationships.

## Overview

Constancy in *Martin Chuzzlewit* operates on multiple levels: personal loyalty, romantic fidelity, and moral steadfastness. The novel's moral structure is built around the contrast between those who remain constant and those who do not. Dickens's preface declares selfishness as his subject, and inconstancy is selfishness in motion — the readiness to abandon commitments when they no longer serve one's interests.

Tom Pinch is the novel's supreme exemplar of constancy. He remains devoted to Pecksniff long after the reader understands Pecksniff's true character, and his loyalty is presented not as stupidity but as a form of moral innocence that the novel ultimately vindicates. Mary Graham's constancy to Martin across the Atlantic separation (020-001) represents romantic fidelity under the harshest test of distance and deprivation.

The novel's American interlude demonstrates constancy at the national scale — Mark Tapley's determination to remain "jolly" under all circumstances is a comic version of the virtue, a pledge to maintain one's essential character regardless of external conditions (016-001).

## In the Novel

### Tom Pinch's Loyalty

Tom Pinch's constancy is tested most severely in his relationship with Pecksniff. Despite mounting evidence of Pecksniff's hypocrisy, Tom maintains his devotion: "If it was a constant source of pleasure to him to refer to Mr. Pecksniff, and to speak of him as the best of men" (005-001). Old Martin warns Pecksniff: "You are to find that I can be constant, and am not to be diverted from my end" (009-022), a declaration of constancy that — unlike Pecksniff's professions — proves true.

### Romantic Constancy

Mary Graham embodies romantic constancy. After her trials, the narrator reflects: "she had come out from her young trials constant, self-denying, earnest, and devoted" (013-001). This passage articulates the novel's theory of constancy — it is not a passive quality but one forged through suffering. Mary's steadfastness to Martin "for whose sake he was an outcast from his home and fortune" (013-001) is a moral achievement, not a natural disposition.

### The Sea as Anti-Constancy

In a striking passage from the Atlantic crossing, Dickens uses the ocean as a symbol of anti-constancy: "incessant change of place, and form, and hue; constancy in nothing, but eternal strife" (015-001). The sea's lack of constancy mirrors the moral chaos of a world without loyalty — a Hobbesian state of nature from which the constant characters provide the only refuge.

### Constant Association as Irony

Pecksniff's advertisement repeatedly promises pupils "constant association" with morally responsible persons (002-001). This phrase becomes bitterly ironic: the "constant association" Pecksniff offers is anything but constant, as he discards Martin the moment the young man's prospects dim.

## Key Passages

> "She had come out from her young trials constant, self-denying, earnest, and devoted; had acquired in her maidenhood... something of that nobler quality of gentle hearts which is developed often by the sorrows and struggles of matronly years." (013-001)

This passage elevates constancy above mere stubbornness — it is a quality deepened by trial, not merely preserved through it. Mary's youth makes her constancy more remarkable, not less.

> "Constancy in nothing, but eternal strife; on, on, on, they roll." (015-001)

The ocean passage uses constancy's absence to define it — in a world without loyalty, all is chaos and conflict. The rhythmic prose mimics the relentless motion of an inconstant universe.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens exposes constancy's counterfeits through ironic juxtaposition. Pecksniff's "constant association" rhetoric is exposed as marketing language. The Chuzzlewit family gathering (004-005) shows how blood ties provide no constancy when self-interest governs behavior — every relative present has arrived to jockey for Old Martin's fortune, and each would abandon the others instantly if it served their purpose.

Even the funeral trade parodies constancy: Mr. Mould's assistant, "from constant blowing in the fat atmosphere of funerals, had run to seed" (019-001) — constant exposure to the wrong thing corrupts rather than ennobles.
