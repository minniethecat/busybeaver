# Mary Graham

Mary Graham is the novel's heroine in the most understated sense — not a dramatic center of action but a fixed point of moral constancy around whom the story's selfishness and scheming swirl. Orphaned and dependent, she lives as companion to [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit on the harsh condition that she expect nothing from his estate: a deliberate arrangement designed to test whether anyone can love without [[selfishness|self-interest]]. That she passes this test with quiet grace is the novel's answer to its own great question.

## Overview

Mary is introduced in Chapter 3, sitting beside [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s sickbed at the Blue Dragon inn. The landlady observes her with curiosity: plainly dressed, grave but composed, carrying medicines for the old man's attacks because "we never travel without them" (003-021). She is beautiful — a fact Dickens notes without the ironic commentary he applies to most of his characters — but the beauty is secondary to the quality that defines her: a steady, undemonstrative capacity for [[love|devotion]]. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] has arranged her position so as to make selfishness impossible; she is to receive nothing for her care, so any care she gives must be genuine.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Mary is an orphan, taken in by [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit to be his companion. She is unrelated to the Chuzzlewit family by blood or marriage — a fact that the Blue Dragon landlady probes with excusable curiosity and that Mary acknowledges with some embarrassment. "When I told you we were not related," she explains, "I meant not in any way. Not even by marriage" (003-029). Her isolation is nearly absolute: she has no family, no fortune, and no social standing beyond what [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s protection provides.

The terms of that protection are deliberately severe. She is to be his companion, his [[nursing|nurse]], his attendant — and she is to expect nothing from him in return. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] designed the arrangement to prevent her from being, or [[appearance|seeming]], mercenary. In practice, it makes her utterly dependent on his goodwill while denying her any claim on it.

### Role in the Plot

Mary's love for [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] Chuzzlewit places her at the center of the novel's central conflict. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]], having quarreled with his grandson, forbids any communication between them — and [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], who schemes to marry Mary himself to secure his grip on the Chuzzlewit fortune, works to ensure the separation is permanent. [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] regards her as his "stumbling-block" in his campaign to control the old man, knowing that "he had a strong [[love|affection]] for her" and that she would never cooperate with his designs (030-047).

Through all of this, Mary remains steadfast. She endures [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s unwanted advances — including his grotesque marriage proposal — with dignity and quiet resistance. Her constancy to [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] never wavers, even when he is abroad, unreachable, and possibly lost forever. She is, in a novel of schemers and performers, the rare character who simply is what she appears to be.

### Fate & Resolution

Mary's reward is union with [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] — a resolution that [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] had secretly hoped for from the beginning. At the great unmasking in Chapter 52, [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] publicly explains how he had "pleased his fancy with the picture" of love growing between Mary and [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]], and how her [[constancy|steadfastness]] under trial had vindicated his deepest hopes (052-103). She and [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] marry, and at the novel's final celebratory dinner, she and [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] take [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] aside "before dinner, and spoken to him so heartily of the time to come" (053-066).

## Character Analysis

Mary Graham is a difficult heroine: so good, so patient, so selfless that she risks [[appearance|seeming]] insipid. Dickens solves this problem not by complicating her virtue but by complicating her situation. She is not merely good — she is good under conditions deliberately designed to make goodness impossible to fake. Old [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s scheme is essentially an experiment in character: can love exist without hope of reward? Mary's patience, her refusal to demand recognition, her willingness to serve without prospect of inheritance, are the experimental results.

She also serves a structural function. Because she is the object of desire for both [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin]] (genuine love) and [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] (predatory lust), she becomes the litmus that reveals the nature of each man who approaches her. [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s proposal to her, made while he pretends to be [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s devoted servant, is the clearest exposure of his hypocrisy. Young [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]]'s love for her, tested by absence and hardship, proves itself real.

## Key Relationships

- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — ward and companion; her patient care for him under terms of no reward is the novel's definitive image of unselfish love
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]] — her beloved; their love, tested by separation and opposition, is the novel's central [[courtship|romance]] (052-103)
- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — persecutor and would-be husband; his attempts to coerce her into marriage are his most despicable acts (030-047)
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — devoted admirer; his silent, selfless love for her is one of the novel's tenderest subplots
- [[ruth-pinch|Ruth Pinch]] — friend and eventual sister-in-law through the extended Pinch-Chuzzlewit-[[john-westlock|Westlock]] family
