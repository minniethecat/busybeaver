# Mr. Nadgett

Mr. Nadgett is the mysterious investigator employed by [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] at the [[anglo-bengalee|Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company]]. In a novel teeming with vivid, theatrical personalities, Nadgett is remarkable for his near-invisibility — a man "born to be a secret" whose genius lies in being overlooked. He is Dickens's portrait of the emerging Victorian figure of the private detective, and his patient, remorseless accumulation of evidence is what finally brings [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] to the gallows.

## Overview

Nadgett is introduced in Chapter 27, when [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] summons him to investigate [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]. Dickens's description is one of his most memorable character sketches: "He was a short, dried-up, withered old man, who seemed to have secreted his very blood; for nobody would have given him credit for the possession of six ounces of it in his whole body" (027-192). Nadgett's power lies in his lack of distinguishing features — he is the man nobody remembers seeing, the presence nobody registers. "It was no virtue or merit in Nadgett that he transacted all his Anglo-Bengalee [[commerce|business]] secretly and in the closest [[trust|confidence]]; for he was born to be a secret."

## Biography

### Background & Function

Nadgett is employed by the Anglo-Bengalee at the modest salary of "a pound a week" to make inquiries (027-192). Beyond this, almost nothing is known of his origins or private life. He seems to have no home, no family, no existence outside his work. He "always made his appearance as if he had that moment come up a trap" (038-012). The narrator notes that there was "a popular belief that he never went to bed" and that he was "the man who never appeared" (029-106).

### Surveillance of Jonas

[[montague-tigg|Tigg]] assigns Nadgett to investigate [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] shortly after [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] joins the Anglo-Bengalee as a director. Nadgett's methods are systematic and all-encompassing: "He watched him in and out of the Assurance Office, where he was now formally installed as a Director; he dogged his footsteps in the streets; he stood listening when he talked; he sat in coffee-houses when he ate" (038-005). The [[secret|surveillance]] is so comprehensive that "[[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] had no more idea that Mr. Nadgett's eyes were fixed on him, than he had that he was living under the daily inspection and report of a whole order of Jesuits" (038-006).

Nadgett's genius is what might be called negative visibility. He goes about "so stealthily, and kept himself so wrapped up in himself, that the whole object of his life appeared to be to avoid notice" (038-007). People who look at him see only someone who does not wish to be seen — a man who fears being watched, not a man who is watching. This misdirection is his greatest asset.

### Bringing Jonas to Justice

When [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] is [[crime|murder]]ed by [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], it is Nadgett who pieces together the evidence. He tracks the clothing [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] wore during the crime: a dress "stained with clay, and spotted with blood" (051-173). He compiles the timeline, the sightings, the alibis that do not hold. And in Chapter 51, he steps forward to make the accusation: "Murder," said Nadgett, looking round on the astonished group. "Let no one interfere" (051-136).

His transformation in this moment is striking. The man who has been invisible for the entire novel suddenly becomes its most public voice, crying "Murder, Murder, Murder" into the street until "the voices died away into the distant hum" (051-137). The man born to be a secret becomes the agent of exposure.

## Character Analysis

Nadgett represents an emergent figure in nineteenth-century fiction: the professional investigator. He is not a gentleman detective like later creations; he is a bureaucratic functionary of [[secret|surveillance]], paid by the week, methodical rather than brilliant. His power is patience, not deduction. He is less a character than a principle — the principle that in the modern world, secrets cannot be kept, because somewhere there is someone whose job it is to find them.

His relationship to [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] is instructive. [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] deploys him as an instrument of blackmail and control; Nadgett, in his dogged way, pursues his brief beyond its origin, accumulating evidence that ultimately serves justice rather than extortion. The pound-a-week man outlasts the [[trust|confidence]] trickster who employed him, and his work survives to condemn the [[crime|murder]]er who killed his employer.

## Key Relationships

- [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] — his employer at the Anglo-Bengalee, who sets him to investigate [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] for purposes of blackmail and control (027-193)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — the object of his [[secret|surveillance]]; Nadgett's patient accumulation of evidence ultimately exposes [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] as a [[crime|murder]]er (051-164)
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — the recipient of Nadgett's climactic revelation, which enables the final unmasking of [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] (051-136)
