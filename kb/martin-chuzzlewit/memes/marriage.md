# Marriage

Marriage in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is rarely about love. It is an economic transaction, a weapon of control, and a plot mechanism through which Dickens exposes the selfishness that corrupts every human institution. The novel presents a spectrum of marriages—from Jonas Chuzzlewit's murderous union with Mercy Pecksniff to the deferred but faithful bond between young Martin and Mary Graham—that maps the moral condition of each character involved.

## Overview

Dickens treats marriage as a site of moral testing. The way a character approaches marriage—as a business deal, a social ambition, an act of control, or a commitment of love—reveals their deepest values. Old Martin's manipulation of marriage prospects, Jonas's instrumental cruelty, and Charity Pecksniff's desperate scheming all illustrate the same theme: that when marriage is approached selfishly, it becomes not a bond but a battleground.

## In the Novel

The novel's marriage plots begin with Old Martin's attempt to control young Martin's future. He confronts his grandson with the charge "of designing to corrupt the fidelity to himself... of a young creature whom he had trained and educated to be his only disinterested and faithful companion, when he should have disposed of me in marriage to his heart's content" (006-078) (006-078). The language is revealing: Old Martin treats marriage as "disposal," a transaction in which he is the auctioneer. Young Martin's rebellion—"I would dispose of myself in marriage, and would rather not be knocked down by him or any other auctioneer to any bidder whomsoever" (006-078) (006-078)—is one of his first moral assertions.

Jonas Chuzzlewit's marriage to Mercy Pecksniff is the novel's darkest union, a study in matrimonial cruelty. The courtship is a business negotiation between Jonas and Mr. Pecksniff; the wedding, when it comes, shocks even the worldly Mrs. Todgers: "when the marriage took place... I thought he had taken leave of his senses" (032-016) (032-016). The marriage itself becomes a prison, culminating in Jonas's attempt to murder his wife—Dickens's most extreme illustration of marriage corrupted by selfishness.

Charity Pecksniff's arc offers satirical counterpoint. Her "unfortunate marriage" plans, as she later calls her sister's union, mask her own desperation (046-114) (046-114). When she finally secures a husband in the novel's closing pages, Old Martin's dry observation—"You have chosen the period of your marriage unfortunately" (054-034) (054-034)—exposes the vanity and bad timing of her scheme.

### Key Passages

The novel's most poignant marriage-related passage concerns not the living but the dead. Chuffey, the aged clerk, recalls how Old Martin's brother, dying of grief over his son Jonas's cruelty, spoke his last words: "'Spare him, Chuff!' They were the only words he could speak. 'Spare him, Chuff!' I promised him I would" (051-117) (051-117). Behind the novel's satirical treatment of marriage lies this tragic backstory: a father's love that survived his son's monstrousness, and a promise that ultimately brings Jonas to justice.

Mercy's confession in the final chapter—"I couldn't return to him... I could not return to him" with "an instinctive reference to his having forced her marriage on" (054-028) (054-028)—is the novel's most direct condemnation of marriage coerced rather than chosen.

Mary Graham's quiet remark that she and Martin are not related "not in any way. Not even by marriage" (003-029) (003-029) carries the novel's understated alternative: marriage as a bond freely chosen, not a kinship imposed.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Mrs. Gamp's commentary on "the marriage state" (040-054) (040-054) provides comic relief while reinforcing the novel's critique. Her matter-of-fact observation that "others is married, and in the marriage state" treats matrimony as a condition like weather—something that happens to people, not something they choose. Dickens's satire targets not marriage itself but its reduction to a social mechanism, drained of love and filled with calculation.
