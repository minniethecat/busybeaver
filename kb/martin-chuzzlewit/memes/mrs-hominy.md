Mrs. Hominy is an [[america|America]]n literary lady—an authoress and philosopher—who accompanies [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit on part of his journey through the [[america|United States]]. She is the wife of Major Hominy, "one of our chicest spirits," and belongs to "one of our most aristocratic families" (021-003).

## Overview

Mrs. Hominy is introduced to [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] by a fellow passenger who holds her in great esteem as a writer of reviews and analytical disquisitions. Her letters, signed "The Mother of the Modern Gracchi," are regularly printed in public journals with "all the indignation in capitals, and all the sarcasm in italics" (021-010).

## Biography

### Travels with Martin

Mrs. Hominy takes a keen interest in [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] throughout their journey. She sits beside him during receptions, propounds theories, and answers imaginary objections, causing [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] to feel "he must be dreaming, and speaking for two." She quotes interminable passages from her own essays on government and uses Major Hominy's pocket-handkerchief with great ceremony (021-012).

### Reunion with Pogram

At the [[the-watertoast-association|Watertoast]] levee, Mrs. Hominy has a celebrated public confrontation with [[elijah-pogram]] over a vote he had given, which she had "deprecated in a line by itself, set up expressly for the purpose in German text." The exchange becomes a public spectacle, with Mrs. Hominy literally talking "her own books, word for word" (034-003).

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Hominy represents the type of [[america|America]]n literary pretension that Dickens satirizes throughout the [[america|American chapters]]. She is simultaneously absurd and formidable—a "woman of masculine and towering intellect" who has "looked on foreign countries with the eye of a perfect republican hot from the model oven." Her conversations consist largely of patriotic cant, yet she dominates [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] completely (021-010).
