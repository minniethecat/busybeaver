# Mercy Pecksniff

Mercy "Merry" [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] is the younger daughter of [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]], and one of the novel's most dramatically transformed characters. She begins as a giggling coquette — Dickens's stock figure of foolish girlhood — but through the crucible of her marriage to [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]], she becomes a figure of genuine pathos. Her arc from frivolity to suffering to a hard-won widow's freedom is one of the moral rhythms of the novel.

## Overview

Dickens names her with characteristic irony. "Mercy!" the narrator exclaims, "oh, what a charming name for such a pure-souled being as the youngest [[charity-pecksniff|Miss Pecksniff]]!" (002-026). The irony runs both ways: at first, Mercy shows no [[forgiveness|mercy]] in her heedless flirtations and thoughtless cruelties; later, she is shown none by her husband. "Silly Merry," her sister calls her, "Merry being fond for Mercy" (002-044). The diminutive captures everything about her early self — light, insubstantial, and defined entirely by others' amusement at her.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Raised in the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] household alongside her sister [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]], Mercy is the "youngest [[charity-pecksniff|Miss Pecksniff]]" who "sat upon a stool because of her simplicity and humility" (002-025) — a description that, in the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] household, means the opposite of what it says. She is her father's favorite for her pliability, the daughter who laughs on cue and serves as a decorative audience for his moral performances. When [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|young Martin Chuzzlewit]] arrives as a pupil, "Miss Mercy laughed immensely at everything that was said" (005-154).

### Role in the Plot

Mercy's great turning point is her marriage to [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — a match her father arranges and she enters with characteristic thoughtlessness. [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s motives are transparently mercenary, and the warning signs are everywhere. He is already showing his nature before the wedding: "Ecod, my lady! you'll catch it for this, when you are married" (024-167).

The marriage proves catastrophic. [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], revealed as a domestic tyrant, isolates Mercy, belittles her, and subjects her to a regime of psychological cruelty. The laughing girl of the early chapters vanishes; in her place is a woman who "pined day by day" and "wept in the dead of night" (051-027). Her suffering is witnessed most acutely by [[chuffey|old Chuffey]], who becomes her unlikely protector.

Mercy's situation also illuminates her father's moral bankruptcy. When she flees to [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] for refuge, he turns her away — not wanting to antagonize [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], whose [[commerce|money]] he values. This betrayal completes Mercy's disillusionment with her family and drives her toward moral independence.

### Fate & Resolution

[[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s exposure as a [[crime|murder]]er — and his subsequent suicide by poison (051-227) — frees Mercy from her marriage. She emerges widowed but not broken. Unlike [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]], who hardens into bitterness, Mercy is chastened and deepened by suffering. The novel grants her a quiet redemption: she finds shelter with [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s party and, in the closing chapters, is seen as a figure of earned seriousness rather than thoughtless gaiety.

## Character Analysis

Mercy represents one of Dickens's more complex treatments of female character. She begins as a type — the pretty, empty-headed flirt — but the novel refuses to leave her there. Her suffering under [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] is rendered with real psychological acuity; the reader watches a personality disintegrate under sustained cruelty. Her eventual deliverance is not a reward for virtue (she has little) but a testament to Dickens's belief that suffering can be educative.

Her name becomes, by the novel's end, something other than pure irony. She is the character most in need of [[forgiveness|mercy]], and the one who — having received none from her husband or her father — is finally granted it by the narrative. Where [[seth-pecksniff|her father]]'s hypocrisy is exposed and [[charity-pecksniff|her sister]]'s pride is punished, Mercy's story is one of rescue from the consequences of her own folly.

## Key Relationships

- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — her father, who arranges her disastrous marriage for financial advantage and later refuses her refuge (020-025)
- [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] — her elder sister, whose envy of Mercy's early marriage curdles into permanent estrangement (030-004)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — her husband, a domestic tyrant whose cruelty transforms her from flirt to victim (024-167)
- [[chuffey|Mr. Chuffey]] — the old clerk who becomes her unexpected protector, watching over her and speaking for her when she cannot speak for herself (051-027)
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin Chuzzlewit the Younger]] — the pupil she flirted with in her carefree days, a relationship that reveals her early character
- [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] — the gentle assistant who witnesses her suffering and extends her kindness
