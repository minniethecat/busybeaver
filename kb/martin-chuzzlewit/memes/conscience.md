# Conscience

Conscience in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is presented as a faculty that can be corrupted, silenced, or weaponised — but never fully escaped. Dickens traces the arc of conscience from Pecksniff's cheerful self-deception to Jonas Chuzzlewit's tormented guilt, showing that the inner voice, however abused, eventually demands its reckoning.

## Overview

The word "conscience" appears at key moral turning points in the novel. What distinguishes Dickens's treatment from simple moralism is his recognition that conscience can be trained to serve vice rather than virtue. Pecksniff is the supreme example: a man whose conscience has been so thoroughly co-opted by self-interest that it congratulates rather than accuses him.

## In the Novel

Pecksniff is introduced with the novel's most ironic sentence: "Subject to human weaknesses, he was upheld by conscience" (003-124) (003-124). The statement is technically true — Pecksniff is upheld by *something* he calls conscience — but the content of that conscience has been hollowed out and replaced with self-regard. Moments later, he resumes his scheming "with a cheerfulness of conscience, prompting almost a sprightly demeanour" (004-097) (004-097). The cheerfulness is the tell: a genuine conscience would trouble, not cheer.

The Pecksniffian conscience reaches its fullest expression in his financial metaphor: "my conscience is my bank. I have a trifle invested there, a mere trifle, Mr. Jonas; but I prize it as a store of value, I assure you" (020-028) (020-028). The narrator immediately undercuts this: Pecksniff's enemies "would have asserted without scruple that if Mr. Pecksniff's conscience were his bank, and he kept a running account there, he must have overdrawn it beyond all mortal means of computation" (020-029) (020-029). The extended metaphor is Dickens at his most Swiftian — conscience reduced to a ledger entry.

The novel's arc traces conscience as it moves from Pecksniff's domesticated version toward its authentic, terrifying form in Jonas Chuzzlewit. After Jonas commits murder, his conscience becomes not a guide but a tormentor — the inner voice that will not be silenced. The narrator's description of Pecksniff after his schemes succeed — "spreading the plumage of his own bright conscience, felt himself equal to all kinds of flights" (030-003) (030-003) — is positioned as the calm before the moral storm that will eventually break over both men.

### Key Passages

The novel's moral architecture is clearest in the contrast between Pecksniff's and Jonas's relationships with conscience. Pecksniff's conscience is an instrument of self-justification; Jonas's, after his crimes, becomes an instrument of punishment. The one has too much conscience (of the wrong kind), the other too little — until it is too late.

Martin's own conscience is tested in the American episodes. On the voyage out, "Martin quite settled it between himself and his conscience, that in any new settlement it would be absolutely necessary to get as far from Mr. Jefferson Brick as possible" (022-069) (022-069). The casual negotiation between self and conscience — settling things "between himself and his conscience" — is Dickens's diagnosis of how most people manage moral questions: not through principle but through self-interested compromise.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The satire of conscience is among Dickens's most subtle achievements in the novel. He never denies that conscience exists or matters — on the contrary, the novel's entire moral structure depends on it. But he shows that conscience, like any faculty, can be trained. Pecksniff has trained his to applaud him. Jonas tried to silence his and was destroyed by it. The novel's moral question is not "do you have a conscience?" but "what has your conscience been taught to say?"
