# Sarah Gamp

Sarah "Sairey" Gamp is the alcoholic [[nursing|nurse]]-[[nursing|midwife]] through whom Dickens anatomizes the horrors of pre-reform nursing, and simultaneously creates one of literature's greatest comic grotesques. Introduced in Chapter 19 when [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] seeks her out to lay out the body of old [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]], Mrs. Gamp is a fat, husky-voiced woman with "a remarkable power of turning up" her moist eye and "only showing the white of it" (019-020). Her name has entered the English language — a "Gamp" became slang for an umbrella, and "Gampish" for slovenly, tipsy nursing — yet she remains far more than a satirical target: she is a magnificent linguistic creation, a character whose speech-patterns are among Dickens's most virtuosic performances.

## Overview

Mrs. Gamp lodges at a bird-fancier's in Kingsgate Street, High Holborn, where she advertises herself on a signboard as "Midwife" — though her practice encompasses both bringing people into the world and laying them out once they depart it (019-004). She is "easily assailable at night by pebbles, walking-sticks, and fragments of tobacco-pipe," a detail that suggests the urgency of her nocturnal summonses. Her husband, Mr. Gamp, died some twenty years before the novel's action, and it was whispered that she "had exerted such uncommon fortitude as to dispose of Mr. Gamp's remains for the benefit of science" — though the narrator adds that the couple had "long been separated on the ground of incompatibility of temper in their [[alcoholism|drink]]" (019-022).

## Biography

### Background & Family

Mrs. Gamp's defining domestic relationship is not with the late Mr. Gamp but with an imaginary friend: Mrs. Harris. Mrs. Harris is the ever-present, never-seen witness Mrs. Gamp invokes to authenticate every self-serving statement. "Mrs. Harris," she typically begins, before attributing to this phantom whatever praise or justification the moment requires. The device allows Mrs. Gamp to recommend herself while affecting humility; she can claim that Mrs. Harris said she was the best [[nursing|nurse]] in [[london|London]] without appearing to boast. Dickens uses Mrs. Harris to expose the machinery of self-justification with devastating comic precision.

Her one real friendship — and it is real, in its way — is with [[betsy-prig|Betsey Prig]], her "frequent pardner" in nursing. Their relationship, anchored in shared professional territory and mutual [[alcoholism|drink]]ing, culminates in one of the novel's most famous quarrels when [[betsy-prig|Mrs. Prig]] commits the unforgivable sin of denying the existence of Mrs. Harris (049-049).

### Role in the Plot

Mrs. Gamp is initially engaged by [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] to lay out [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]]'s body, leading to her appearance at the undertaker Mould's establishment, where she flatters the entire family while cadging rum and [[commerce|business]] referrals (025-036). She is subsequently hired to mind old Chuffey in Anthony's counting-house — a [[duty|responsibility]] she fulfills by arriving late, [[alcoholism|drink]]ing whenever opportunity permits, and trading in her peculiar brand of morbid cheerfulness.

Her most important engagement is as night-[[nursing|nurse]] to the mysterious patient at the Bull Inn in Holborn, a job she secures through [[betsy-prig|Betsey Prig]]. This patient is, unknown to Gamp, none other than [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]] Chuzzlewit in disguise — making her, unwittingly, an attendant upon the novel's puppet-master. She also tends [[lewsome|Lewsome]], the apothecary's assistant whose testimony will later expose [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]]'s [[crime|murder]] of [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]].

### Fate & Resolution

Mrs. Gamp's great crisis arrives in Chapter 49 during the legendary falling-out with [[betsy-prig|Betsey Prig]]. When Gamp invokes Mrs. Harris yet again, Prig — after [[alcoholism|drink]]ing — delivers the immortal retort: "I don't believe there's no sich a person!" (049-044). The quarrel that follows is one of Dickens's comic masterpieces, destroying the friendship and leaving Mrs. Gamp to mourn the death of Mrs. Harris as a real bereavement. She exits the novel unreformed, unrepentant, and unmatchable.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Gamp is the supreme achievement of Dickens's method of character-through-voice. Her idiolect — "dispoged" for disposed, "creetur" for creature, "berryins" for burials, "deniges" for denies, and countless other distortions — is simultaneously comic and revelatory. Every verbal tic encodes a moral posture: the piety that masks self-dealing, the sentimentality that cloaks indifference to actual suffering. When she declares that "rich folks may ride on camels, but it an't so easy for 'em to see out of a needle's eye," she is congratulating herself on her poverty even as she angles for more [[commerce|money]] (025-046).

Yet there is something magnificent in her sheer vitality. She is impossible to defeat because she is immune to shame. Her umbrella — that symbol of disreputable nursing — is also a symbol of her indestructibility. In Dickens's preface, he defended her as "a fair representation of the hired attendant on the poor in sickness" twenty-four years earlier (P03-008), a reminder that the comedy is grounded in a genuine social evil: the untrained, alcoholic [[nursing|nurse]]s to whom the sick poor were abandoned.

## Key Relationships

- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — first employer in the novel, who engages her to prepare [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]]'s body (019-003)
- [[betsy-prig|Betsey Prig]] — fellow [[nursing|nurse]], [[alcoholism|drink]]ing companion, and "frequent pardner"; their friendship is destroyed when Prig denies the existence of Mrs. Harris (049-049)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — attends his father's deathbed and funeral under her ostensible care
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — tends him unknowingly when he poses as a patient at the Bull Inn as part of his scheme to expose [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]
- [[mr-mould|Mr. Mould]] — the undertaker with whom she maintains a mutually profitable alliance of referrals and rum
