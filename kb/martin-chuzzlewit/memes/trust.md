# Trust

Trust in *Martin Chuzzlewit* functions as both a psychological condition and a narrative engine. The novel's central conflicts originate in broken trust: Old Martin's suspicion estranges his grandson, Pecksniff's false trustworthiness ensnares the Chuzzlewit family, and young Martin's inability to trust others isolates him in America. Restoration of trust is the novel's resolution—and it comes only after characters have learned, through suffering, to extend trust to those who deserve it.

## Overview

Dickens presents trust as fragile and self-perpetuating in both directions. Distrust breeds distrust: the suspicious person "holding others at a distance by suspicions and distrusts, wonders why they don't approach, and don't confide, and calls that selfishness in them" (052-081) (052-081). But trust too can replicate itself—Tom Pinch's unwavering trust in others, though often misplaced, ultimately creates the conditions for genuine community.

## In the Novel

The novel's trust economy is established early. Young Martin, departing for America, entrusts Tom Pinch with his most precious secret: "I am going to repose great trust in you, knowing that I may do so with perfect reliance on your honour and secrecy, and having nobody else just now to trust in" (014-053) (014-053). This is trust born of desperation—Martin has no one else—but it is also the novel's first movement toward genuine confidence in another human being.

Old Martin articulates the tragic consequence of a life lived without trust in his great confession: "oh you whose wealth, like mine, has been a source of continual unhappiness, leading you to distrust the nearest and dearest, and to dig yourself a living grave of suspicion and reserve; take heed that, having cast off all whom you might have bound to you, and tenderly, you do not become in your decay the instrument of such a man as this" (052-102) (052-102). Trust withheld becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy: by suspecting everyone, Old Martin created the conditions in which only the untrustworthy would persist.

The Anglo-Bengalee insurance scheme represents trust exploited as a commodity. Tigg Montague boasts of clients who "trust us as if we were the Mint; yet know no more about us than you do of that crossing-sweeper at the corner" (027-161) (027-161). This is the dark mirror of genuine trust—confidence extracted by performance rather than earned through character.

### Key Passages

The novel's most powerful treatment of trust occurs in the resolution. Old Martin's reflection that "I know the human mind, although I trust it. That is my weakness" (052-129) (052-129) is spoken by Pecksniff in self-justification, but Dickens's irony makes it true in a deeper sense: Old Martin's real weakness was that he did *not* trust the human mind, that he substituted suspicion for faith. The narrator's exclamation—"The man in all this world most proud of his distrust of other men... shall never find the time come home to him, when all his wisdom is an idiot's folly, weighed against a simple heart!" (039-173) (039-173)—is the novel's definitive judgment.

Mary Graham's defiance of Pecksniff—"he shall know of this, trust me, sir" (030-118) (030-118)—demonstrates trust as an act of moral courage. She has no power beyond her word, but her word, grounded in integrity, is sufficient.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Trust is satirized through its counterfeits. Pecksniff's professions of trustworthiness—"I hope not. I trust not" (010-066) (010-066)—are the novel's running joke, each repetition deepening the irony. The Anglo-Bengalee Company extends the satire to institutional scale, where "trust" is a marketing slogan and the trusting are swindled. Yet Dickens is careful not to mock trust itself—only its abuse. The novel ultimately vindicates trust as the basis of all genuine relationship.
