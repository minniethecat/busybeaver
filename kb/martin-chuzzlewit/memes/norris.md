## The Norris Family

The Norrises are a New York family introduced in Chapter XVII, where they host [[Martin Chuzzlewit Jr.]] at a fashionable evening gathering. The family consists of Mr. Norris the father, Mrs. Norris the mother, Mr. Norris Junior the son, and the Misses Norris the daughters — along with Mrs. Norris Senior the grandmother. They represent Dickens's satire of American social pretension and moral hypocrisy.

## Anglophile Obsession

The family is consumed by a fascination with British aristocracy. They have visited England and claim intimate acquaintance with "all the great dukes, lords, viscounts, marquesses, duchesses, knights, and baronets" (017-069). During Martin's visit, they regale him with "recollections of what Mr. Norris the father had said to the marquess, and of what Mrs. Norris the mother had said to the marchioness" (017-069), while simultaneously and contradictorily praising America's freedom from "arbitrary distinctions":

> During the whole of these narrations, and in the very meridian of their enjoyment thereof, both Mr. Norris the father, and Mr. Norris Junior, the son (who corresponded, every post, with four members of the English Peerage), enlarged upon the inestimable advantage of having no such arbitrary distinctions in that enlightened land, where there were no noblemen but nature's noblemen.
>
> (017-070)

## Abolitionism and Racism

When [[Mr. Bevan]] mentions Mark Tapley's encounter with a Black man, "it appeared that all the Norrises were abolitionists" (017-071). Martin, encouraged, expresses sympathy for the enslaved. But the family's response reveals the hollowness of their principles:

> One of the young ladies — the prettiest and most delicate — was mightily amused at the earnestness with which he spoke; and on his craving leave to ask her why, was quite unable for a time to speak for laughing. As soon however as she could, she told him that the Negroes were such a funny people, so excessively ludicrous in their manners and appearance.
>
> (017-071)

Mr. Norris the father settles the matter with the pronouncement: "there is a natural antipathy between the races" (017-072). Mr. Norris the son "made a wry face, and dusted his fingers as Hamlet might after getting rid of Yorick's skull; just as though he had that moment touched a Negro, and some of the black had come off upon his hands" (017-074).

## Class Snobbery

The Norrises also display a rigid class consciousness. When a neighbor holds different religious opinions, Mr. Norris the father observes that "he hadn't the honour of knowing the gentleman," and Mrs. Norris adds that "the people were well enough in their way, but they were not genteel" (017-070).

This snobbery turns against Martin himself when the family discovers he is poor and unknown. The narrator describes their horror:

> A deathlike stillness fell upon the Norisses. If this story should get wind, their country relation had, by his imprudence, forever disgraced them... through all the spheres it would go forth that the Norrises, deceived by gentlemanly manners and appearances, had, falling from their high estate, "received" a dollarless and unknown man.
>
> (017-104)

## The General Fladdock Scene

The arrival of [[General Fladdock]] at the gathering provides further comic exposure of the Norrises' values. "He shook hands with the Norrises three times all round" and the family pressed around him, inquiring above all "to what extent he had become acquainted with the great dukes, lords, viscounts, marquesses, duchesses, knights, and baronets, in whom the people of those benighted countries had delight" (017-084).

When the general confirms the vast superiority of British aristocracy, Mr. Norris the father "gave his head a melancholy shake" and all the Norrises sighed, "quite overwhelmed with despondency" (017-089).

## Later Reference

Mr. Norris is later mentioned in Chapter XXXIII when Martin, stranded in [[Eden]], addresses a letter to [[Mr. Bevan]] "to the care of the memorable Mr. Norris of New York" (033-140).

## As Satire

The Norris family serves as one of Dickens's sharpest satirical portraits in the American chapters. Through them he exposes the contradiction between American professions of equality and the reality of class pretension and racial prejudice. As Mr. Norris the father blandly concludes after Martin's humiliating departure: "the young man has this night beheld a refinement of social manner, and an easy magnificence of social decoration, to which he is a stranger in his own country. Let us hope it may awake a moral sense within him" (017-106).

[[Mr. Bevan]] later remarks with characteristic understatement: "Norris is a very good fellow in his way" (017-120).
