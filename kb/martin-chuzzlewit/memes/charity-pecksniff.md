# Charity Pecksniff

Charity "Cherry" [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] is the elder daughter of [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]], and in many ways his truest heir. Where her sister [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy]] embodies frivolity and flightiness, Charity inherits her father's calculating nature, his eye for the main chance, and his gift for moralizing self-justification. Yet for all her scheming, she ends the novel as Dickens's portrait of a woman who has overplayed her hand — unmarried, bitter, and nursing grievances in permanent spinsterhood.

## Overview

Dickens introduces the two [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] sisters together in Chapter 2, in a passage laden with irony about their names. Charity, the narrator observes, "with her fine strong sense and her mild, yet not reproachful gravity, was so well named, and did so well set off and illustrate her sister" (002-026). The irony cuts deep — neither sister lives up to her name, but Charity's failure is the more damning, for she knows exactly what she is doing. She shares her father's love of performance and his talent for cloaking [[selfishness|self-interest]] in righteous language. Her story is one of repeated romantic disappointment, culminating in a proposal to the absurd [[augustus-modle|Augustus Moddle]] that ends in humiliating abandonment.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Charity is raised in the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] household near [[salisbury|Salisbury]], a domestic theater presided over by her widowed father. She and Mercy are presented as a matched pair of [[hypocrisy|hypocrite]]s from the start: both daughters open "their eyes uncommonly wide" at any mention of [[commerce|money]] (002-048). Charity's role in the household is that of the sensible elder sister — but her "sense" is always deployed in service of [[selfishness|self-interest]]. She is, in miniature, what her father is in full: someone who understands that the appearance of virtue is a form of currency.

### Role in the Plot

Charity's story arc is defined by romantic ambition and its thwarting. At [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]'s boarding house in [[london|London]], she sets her sights on the commercial gentlemen, particularly [[jinkins|Mr. Jinkins]], hemming pocket-handkerchiefs as tokens of her regard (011-002). But her real object is always security and status rather than love.

The pivotal wound of Charity's life comes when her younger sister Mercy marries [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] before her. Charity had herself been considered as a match for [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]; [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s decision to substitute Mercy — "Years will tame down the wildness of my foolish bird, and then it will be caged. But Cherry, [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Mr. Jonas]], Cherry—" (020-025) — is a rejection that festers into permanent resentment. From this point forward, Charity's envy of her sister colors every interaction, and she grows "stung by a sense of slight and injury" (030-004).

Her bitterness deepens during [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s courtship of [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]], a proposal that offends Charity not on moral grounds but because it sidelines her still further. She becomes openly [[hospitality|host]]ile to her father, snapping "Bother, Pa!" when he tries to reconcile (030-007), and [[nursing|nurse]]s her grievances with relentless dedication.

### Fate & Resolution

Charity's final bid for marriage is the most humiliating. She accepts the proposal of [[augustus-modle|Augustus Moddle]], a man so weak-willed that he eventually flees on the morning of the wedding, leaving her a note. "Miss Pecksniff opened it; glanced at it; uttered a piercing shriek; threw it down upon the ground; and fainted away" (054-082). When she recovers, she retreats into the role of the "strong-minded woman" — a figure of fun in Dickens's world, alone and unloved, having driven away every prospect through her own pride and calculation. The novel's closing image of her is that of a "bridal bonnet" disappearing, "with as much state as the dimity bedgown left in it" (054-040).

## Character Analysis

Charity is a study in the corrosive effects of pride and envy. Unlike Mercy, who is capable of growth through suffering, Charity hardens into caricature. She represents a particular Dickensian type: the woman who mistakes calculation for wisdom and ends up with nothing. Her names — "Charity" given, "Cherry" used — are ironic throughout. She has no charity for anyone, least of all her sister; and far from being sweet, she sours with every chapter.

She also serves a structural function as a mirror to her father. When [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] falls, Charity does not mourn him — she has already transferred her energies to her own schemes. Their parallel arcs demonstrate that selfishness, when practiced without the saving grace of [[comedy|humor]] or self-awareness, leads only to isolation.

## Key Relationships

- [[seth-pecksniff|Seth Pecksniff]] — her father; shares his calculating nature and is the only character whose hypocrisy rivals her own (002-026)
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] — her younger sister; Charity's envy of Mercy's early marriage poisons their relationship permanently (030-004)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — the man she might have married; his preference for Mercy is Charity's defining grievance (020-025)
- [[augustus-modle|Augustus Moddle]] — her final suitor, who jilts her at the altar and flees rather than face the marriage (054-082)
- [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] — [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|old Martin]]'s companion, whom Charity resents as a rival for her father's attention and for romantic attention generally (030-007)
- [[montague-tigg|Montague Tigg]] — lodger at [[todgers|Todgers's]] whose attentions briefly flatter Charity before his true nature emerges
