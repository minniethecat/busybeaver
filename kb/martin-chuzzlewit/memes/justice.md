# Justice

Justice in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is not a settled principle but a rhetorical weapon. Characters invoke it most loudly when they are least just, while those who quietly embody fairness—Tom Pinch, John Westlock, Mark Tapley—rarely speak of justice at all. Dickens treats the word as a moral litmus test: the manner in which a character deploys the language of justice reveals their character more reliably than any self-description.

## Overview

The novel distinguishes between the justice that characters claim for themselves and the justice they extend to others. Mr. Pecksniff illustrates the pattern perfectly: "His sense of justice is so fine," he says of Old Martin, "that he will hear even him, although he knows beforehand that nothing can come of it" (043-106). The irony is characteristically Dickensian—Pecksniff's praise of another's justice is itself an act of injustice, a performance designed to disguise his own scheming. In the world of *Martin Chuzzlewit*, justice-talk is almost always a mask for self-interest.

## In the Novel

Three versions of justice compete across the novel's plot. The first is the retributive justice Pecksniff practices against anyone who threatens his position—his expulsion of young Martin in Chapter 12, framed as a dutiful act, and his dismissal of Tom Pinch, which Tom's innocent friend John Westlock has long decried as "injustice to Pecksniff" (012-103). This is justice as power, wielded by the strong against the weak.

The second is the "poetical justice" of fiction itself—an expectation Tom Pinch's sister Ruth holds, imagining that her brother "as if a character in a book" should "by some impossible means" marry the woman he loves (050-063). Tom rejects this framing gently but firmly: "There is a much higher justice than poetical justice, my dear, and it does not order events upon the same principle" (050-063). Dickens here signals his own narrative strategy—the novel will not deliver fairy-tale justice but something harder-won.

The third is genuine justice, which the novel locates not in grand pronouncements but in small acts. Martin, speaking of Tom Pinch, acknowledges that "It's impossible to like Pinch better than I do, or to do greater justice to his good qualities" (012-066). The narrator, describing Martin's gift of diamonds from Mary, adds the aside: "let us do him justice, it was for her sake; not for theirs" (014-094). These quiet moments of fair-dealing—the narrator's commitment to honest assessment—constitute the novel's true justice.

### Key Passages

The word "justice" appears most forcefully in Chapter 50, where Tom Pinch articulates the novel's central insight: that the demand for poetic justice is a form of selfishness. He urges Martin to "be just even in your injustice" (050-035), a paradox that captures the novel's mature moral vision—justice is not about receiving one's due but about granting others theirs, even when one feels wronged.

Old Martin's revelation in Chapter 52 completes the arc. His long-concealed plan to expose Pecksniff is itself a form of moral accounting: he sees that Pecksniff has "become the incarnation of all selfishness and treachery" (052-002), and his unmasking of the hypocrite enacts a justice that the legal system never could. But Old Martin also subjects himself to judgment, confessing that his own suspicious nature set the entire tragedy in motion.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

Dickens treats the language of justice as material for satire. Pecksniff's instruction to his daughter—"remind me to be more than usually particular in praying for Mr. Anthony Chuzzlewit; who has done me an injustice" (004-096)—is a perfect miniature of Pecksniffian logic: grievance dressed as piety, self-pity disguised as prayer. The Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company, Tigg Montague's fraudulent enterprise, extends the satire to institutional injustice, where financial exploitation wears the language of benevolence.
