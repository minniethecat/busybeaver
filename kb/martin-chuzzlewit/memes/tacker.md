# Tacker

Tacker is the assistant and foreman to [[mr-mould|Mr. Mould]], the undertaker whose establishment features in the novel's funereal comedy. A man of "great experience in the trade" (019-086), Tacker serves as the practical executor of Mould's grandiloquent funeral philosophy — the man who actually arranges the horses, the plumes, and the measured solemnity that Mould only declaims upon.

## Role at Mould's

Tacker's relationship with his master is that of the practical subordinate who knows the business better than his employer. When Mr. Mould surveys a funeral procession and asks "is all right?", it is Tacker who assures him with professional pride: "Beautiful show, sir. The horses are prouder and the feathers nobler than anything I have ever seen" (019-082). His approval carries weight because it comes from knowledge, not flattery.

In the comedy of the undertaking chapters, Tacker functions as the straight man to Mould's rhetorical excesses. While Mould philosophises about the "luxuries of the grave," Tacker handles the gloves, the measurements, and the logistics. When Mould becomes maudlin over a particularly fine funeral, "leaning for support on Tacker, who plainly told him that he wasn't as young as he had been" (019-101), Tacker's bluntness provides the deflating counterpoint to Mould's professional sentimentality.

Tacker's professional pride in his work is captured in his response to Mr. Mould:

> "The horses are prouder and fresher than ever I see 'em; and toss their heads, they do, as if they knowed how much their plumes cost."
>
> (019-082)

## Later Appearances

Tacker reappears in Chapter XXV, where he calls upon Mrs. Mould and reports that her husband's spirits are "precious low" — a professional euphemism that only an undertaker's man could deliver with such perfect, unconscious irony (025-018).
