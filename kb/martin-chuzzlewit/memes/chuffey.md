# Mr. Chuffey

Mr. Chuffey is the aged clerk of [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]], and one of the novel's most surprising instruments of justice. Appearing at first to be little more than a senile dependent — "that sleepy-headed old Chuffey," as [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] contemptuously calls him (011-069) — he proves to hold the key to the novel's darkest secret. In a story dominated by schemers, [[hypocrisy|hypocrite]]s, and [[selfishness|self-interest]]ed actors, Chuffey stands out as a figure of guileless [[constancy|fidelity]], and his climactic testimony redeems his apparently meaningless existence.

## Overview

Chuffey first appears at the dinner [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] [[hospitality|host]]s for the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]s in Chapter 11. [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] introduces him dismissively: "Our clerk. Old Chuffey" (011-072). The old man seems barely present — he boggles over his plate, eats mechanically, and retreats to "a dark corner on one side of the fireplace, where he always spent his evenings" (011-112). Yet Dickens invests this apparently negligible figure with a quality that catches the attention: when [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] speaks to him, Chuffey "lighted up into a sentient human creature at the first sound of the voice, so that it was at once a curious and quite a moving sight to see him" (011-090). His entire existence is oriented toward his master.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Almost nothing is known of Chuffey's life outside his service to [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]]. He is a clerk of immense tenure — old enough that [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], now a grown man, was "[[nursing|nurse]]d upon my knee, a child" (051-027). His identity is entirely subsumed in his role; he has no family, no history, no apparent life beyond the dim corner he occupies by the fire. His "palsied arm" and "watery eyes" (011-111) mark him as physically decrepit, and most characters treat him as furniture.

### Role in the Plot

Chuffey's narrative function is transformation from object of pity to agent of revelation. In the early chapters, he is a figure of pathos — mocked by [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]], tolerated by others. In Chapter 18, however, Dickens gives him a moment of startling eloquence. When [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] speaks of his own age and frailty, Chuffey erupts: "I know better. I say he's wrong. He's a boy. That's what he is. So are you, [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Mr. Chuzzlewit]] — a kind of boy" (018-027). The narrator calls this "a burst of eloquence without a parallel" for Chuffey, and the poor old shadow "drew through his palsied arm his master's hand, and held it there, with his own folded upon it, as if he would defend him."

The full revelation comes in Chapter 51, when Chuffey discloses what he and Anthony discovered together. [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] had prepared poison intended for his father, concealed in a drawer. Anthony found it and, in his dying days, made Chuffey promise silence — the old man loved his son and could not bear to destroy him. Chuffey kept that vow at enormous cost: "I have been silent for his love! He urged me to it. He made me promise it upon his dying bed" (051-089). But he also preserved the truth, holding it in his clouded memory until the moment of reckoning.

His testimony unfolds in one of the novel's most powerful set-pieces. "He bought the stuff," Chuffey declares, his face lighting with "unwonted fire," and "put it in that drawer" (051-109) and (051-111). Step by step, he reconstructs the crime that Anthony discovered and forgave — and that [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] never knew had been found out. The senile old man becomes, for this moment, the novel's clearest voice.

### Fate & Resolution

After [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s suicide, Chuffey remains loyal to his last duty: "I can't leave her, poor thing! She has been very good to me" — speaking of [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy]], whom he has watched over (051-126). In a novel where nearly every character pursues [[selfishness|self-interest]], Chuffey's final gesture is to stay with those who need him.

## Character Analysis

Chuffey is one of Dickens's holy fools — a character whose apparent uselessness masks profound moral function. His senility is both real and strategic; it puts him beneath the notice of the wicked, allowing him to observe and remember without interference. He serves as a living rebuke to the novel's central theme of selfishness: his entire existence is defined by [[love|devotion]] to others.

His relationship to [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] is the most purely loving bond in the novel. Where every other relationship is transactional — fathers exploiting daughters, daughters calculating marriage prospects, husbands terrorizing wives — the bond between Anthony and his clerk is one of mutual recognition and protection. Chuffey's promise to keep Anthony's secret, kept at terrible cost through years of watching [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s escalating villainy, is an act of [[constancy|fidelity]] that the novel [[honour|honor]]s by making it the instrument of final justice.

## Key Relationships

- [[anthony-chuzzlewit|Anthony Chuzzlewit]] — his master, the only person whose voice can "light him up" into full consciousness; their bond is the novel's purest relationship (011-090)
- [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]] — Anthony's son, whom Chuffey [[nursing|nurse]]d as a child and whose crimes he ultimately exposes (051-109)
- [[mercy-pecksniff|Mercy Pecksniff]] — [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]]'s abused wife, for whom Chuffey becomes an unexpected protector and advocate (051-027)
- [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Old Martin Chuzzlewit]] — Anthony's brother, who treats Chuffey with the respect [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas]] denies him and who receives his climactic testimony (051-088)
