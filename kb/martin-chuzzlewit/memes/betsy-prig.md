# Betsey Prig

A hospital [[nursing|nurse]] who is Mrs. [[sarah-gamp|Sarah Gamp]]'s professional partner in the nursing [[commerce|trade]] — and the character whose presence provokes the novel's most memorably petty domestic quarrel, the legendary falling-out over Mrs. Prig's refusal to countenance the existence of "Mrs. Harris."

## Overview

Betsey Prig is introduced as [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]'s day-[[nursing|nurse]] counterpart when a patient at the Bull in Holborn requires round-the-clock attendance. [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] describes her as "the best of creeturs"(019-010), but the reader's first actual sight of Mrs. Prig immediately undercuts this warm description: she is "of the Gamp build, but not so fat; and her voice was deeper and more like a man's. She had also a beard"(019-030). This grotesque physical description — the mannish voice, the beard — places Prig in the great Dickensian tradition of female grotesques, alongside [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] herself.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Mrs. Prig is a hospital [[nursing|nurse]], "a fair specimen of a Hospital Nurse" as Dickens notes in the Preface, where he singles her out as evidence of the need for nursing reform（Preface-010）. She works at St. Bartholomew's Hospital ("Bartholomew's"), one of the "noble Institutions" that Dickens acknowledges while criticizing their nursing standards. Her professional relationship with [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] spans "twenty year" of shared cases, "turn and turn about, one off, one on"(019-015).

### Role in the Plot

Mrs. Prig's primary dramatic function is her catastrophic falling-out with [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]. The two [[nursing|nurse]]s have long maintained a professional partnership based on mutual convenience and shared patients. But their alliance shatters over the most trivial of provocations: Mrs. Prig, exhausted and irritable, dares to express doubt about the existence of "Mrs. Harris" — the entirely fictitious friend whom [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] invokes constantly as an authority for her own opinions.

The confrontation is a masterpiece of comic indignation. When [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]] invokes Mrs. Harris's opinion, Mrs. Prig retorts: "I don't believe there's no sich a person!" The breach is immediate and irreparable. [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]'s outrage at this "spiteful" disbelief in her imaginary friend is both absurd and — in Dickens's hands — oddly moving, a testament to the reality that self-serving fictions acquire in the minds of those who deploy them.

The quarrel between Gamp and Prig is one of the novel's great comic set-pieces (029-055), a domestic apocalypse fought over a phantom. But beneath the comedy lies Dickens's serious critique of the nursing profession: these two women, one a drunken private [[nursing|nurse]] and the other a negligent hospital [[nursing|nurse]], are the people entrusted with the care of the sick.

### Fate & Resolution

After the quarrel, Mrs. Prig largely disappears from the narrative. The broken partnership is not mended; Dickens has no interest in reconciling these two, whose alliance was always one of convenience rather than [[love|affection]]. Mrs. Prig's fate is simply to represent, in perpetuity, the inadequacy of the nursing profession before reform.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Prig is a character whose comic grotesquerie serves a serious satirical purpose. The Preface to *[[martin-chuzzlewit-jr|Martin]] Chuzzlewit* explicitly links her to Dickens's campaign for sanitary and nursing reform: "Mrs. Betsey Prig was a fair specimen of a Hospital Nurse; and that the Hospitals, with their means and funds, should have left it to private humanity and enterprise to enter on an attempt to improve that class of persons"（Preface-010）. The beard and the deep voice are comic, but the indictment is genuine.

The Gamp-Prig relationship is a study in professional parasitism disguised as friendship. "We knows each other's ways, and often gives relief when others fail"(019-015) — [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]'s description of their partnership is the language of medical competence applied to a system of mutual exploitation. They "nussed together, turn and turn about" not out of dedication to patients but out of a practical arrangement that maximizes their income while minimizing their individual labour.

Prig's role in the Mrs. Harris quarrel reveals another layer: she is the one character in the novel willing to state the obvious truth that [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]'s authority figure is a fiction. In this, she functions as an accidental truth-teller — her scepticism, born of exhaustion and irritation rather than principle, nevertheless exposes the [[deception|fraud]] at the heart of [[sarah-gamp|Mrs. Gamp]]'s self-presentation.
