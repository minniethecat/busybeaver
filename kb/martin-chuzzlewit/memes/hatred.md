# Hatred

Hatred in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is the emotional engine of the novel's villains and the dark underside of its moral vision. Where [[selfishness]] is the abstract principle that governs Pecksniff and Jonas, hatred is the lived experience of that principle — the visceral, consuming passion that turns self-interest into destruction. Dickens presents hatred not as the opposite of [[love|love]] but as love's corrupted double: both are forms of intense attention to another person, but one seeks to possess and the other to annihilate.

## Hatred as Self-Destruction

The novel's most concentrated study of hatred is [[jonas-chuzzlewit|Jonas Chuzzlewit]]. His hatred of [[montague-tigg|Tigg]] grows from the humiliation of being blackmailed: "a wild hatred and fear... which might have rendered him a terror to himself" (042-011). The hatred is self-consuming — it "intensified his hatred and revenge, and made him set a greater value on the murder, as the only means of safety" (051-009).

> The vague consciousness he possessed of having wrecked his fortune in the murderous venture, intensified his hatred and revenge, and made him set the greater store by what he had gained. The man was dead; nothing could undo that. He felt a triumph yet, in the reflection.
>
> (051-009) Jonas's hatred destroys Tigg, but it also destroys Jonas himself.

For [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]], hatred is more settled and less violent — the cold, permanent resentment of anyone who challenges his self-image. Old Martin, describing Pecksniff's nature, speaks of "hatred of competitors, real or fancied" (003-111) and of "sowing perjury, hatred, and lies among near kindred" (003-128). Pecksniff's hatred is the defensive reflex of the [[hypocrisy|hypocrite]]: he must hate those who see through him because their clear sight threatens the fiction on which his life depends.

## Hatred in the Novel's Moral Economy

The narrator frames the novel in terms of fundamental passions: "certain homely matters of love, hatred, jealousy, and revenge" (024-001). Hatred appears in this catalogue as one of the elemental forces that drive human action. The American chapters broaden the theme to national scale: the American characters speak repeatedly of "this hatred of our country, and her Institutions" (034-041) and "this morbid hatred of our Institutions" (034-057). The satire suggests that hatred, like [[selfishness]], can be institutionalized — that nations, like individuals, can organize their identity around what they despise.
