# America

The United States of America as depicted in Charles Dickens's *Martin Chuzzlewit* — a satirical portrait of early 1840s American society, manners, and institutions, where Martin Chuzzlewit and Mark Tapley spend several months seeking fortune, only to encounter corruption, hollowness, and ultimately disillusionment.

## Overview

America in *Martin Chuzzlewit* is not a neutral backdrop but a satirical canvas on which Dickens paints the absurdities of a young republic's self-regard. Martin Chuzzlewit, having quarrelled with his grandfather and been rejected by Seth Pecksniff, resolves impulsively: "I'll go to America!" (013-005). What awaits him is a world of bombastic newspaper editors, fraudulent land speculators, and a society that loudly proclaims its love of liberty while countenancing slavery and violence. Through the American episodes (Chapters XIII–XXIII, XXXIII–XXXIV), Dickens contrasts the genuine decency of individuals like Mr. Bevan with the performative patriotism and commercial cynicism that pervade public life.

## Description

America appears in the novel primarily through three locations that form a descending arc of disillusionment. **New York** is the port of arrival — a city of "smart" newspapers like the *New York Rowdy Journal* (016-001), boarding-houses where fashion and pretension mingle (017-015), and a pervasive atmosphere of commercial hustle. **The Watertoast Association** represents organised hypocrisy — a "sympathy" society that professes concern for foreign causes while its members embody every vice they claim to oppose (021-001). Finally, **Eden** — a malarial swamp settlement sold as a "terrestrial Paradise" (023-005) — is the nadir, where Martin nearly dies of fever and confronts the full measure of American fraud. Dickens's description is unsparing: the land-jobbers' promises of a thriving city are revealed as "a log-house" and "a few stumps of trees" in a pestilential bog (023-030).

## Events

- **Arrival in New York** — Martin and Mark disembark from *The Screw* and encounter the aggressive newspaper culture and boarding-house society (015-001)
- **The *Rowdy Journal* Episode** — Martin is horrified by Colonel Diver's cynical journalism and the press's power to destroy reputations (016-020)
- **The Norris Dinner Party** — a scene of social pretension where Martin commits the fatal error of knowing a steerage passenger (017-035)
- **The Eden Land Scheme** — Martin, desperate after failing to find work, invests in the fraudulent Eden settlement and nearly dies of fever (023-030)
- **Return from Eden** — Martin and Mark, rescued by Mr. Bevan, return to England humbled and matured (033-001)

## Narrative Function

The American episode serves a dual purpose in the novel's architecture. Structurally, it removes Martin from England, allowing the Pecksniff–Jonas plot to develop in his absence. Thematically, it functions as a mirror to English hypocrisy — the selfishness Martin encounters in America is the same selfishness he fled at home, stripped of its genteel veneer. As Mr. Bevan observes, "no satirist could breathe this air" in a country where any criticism of national character provokes "the foulest and most brutal slander" (016-048). Martin's journey is thus a moral education: he learns to distinguish genuine worth from sham, preparing him for his eventual reconciliation with his grandfather and his marriage to [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]].

## Quotations

> "I don't know," he said. "Yes, I do. I'll go to America!"
>
> (012-191)

Dickens's satire of American self-regard is distilled in Mr. Bevan's observation to Martin:

> You are right. So very right, that I believe no satirist could breathe this air. If another Juvenal or Swift could rise up among us tomorrow, he would be hunted down. If you have any knowledge of our literature, and can give me the name of any man, American born and bred, who has anatomized our follies as a people, and not as this or that party; and who has escaped the foulest and most brutal slander, the most inveterate hatred and intolerant pursuit; it will be a strange name in my ears, believe me.
>
> (016-193)
