# The Resolution of the Will

The final settlement of Old Martin Chuzzlewit's estate — a moment of revelation, reconciliation, and redistribution that resolves the novel's central conflict over inheritance, rewards the virtuous, and completes the moral education that the story's entire architecture has been designed to effect.

## Overview

Inheritance is the engine of *Martin Chuzzlewit*'s plot: every character's relationship to Old Martin is mediated by the question of his will. Pecksniff's hypocrisy, Jonas's crimes, the younger Martin's exile to America, Mary Graham's precarious position — all flow from the promise or threat of disinheritance. The resolution of the will in the novel's closing chapters is therefore far more than a financial transaction; it is the mechanism by which the novel's moral order is restored and its judgments rendered permanent.

## Prelude

The will has a troubled history within the novel. Old Martin's initial will-making was conducted in secrecy, driven by his need to control those around him — a flaw he later acknowledges. After Martin the younger's departure for America, Old Martin wrote a new will "making him his heir, and sanctioning his marriage with Mary" (052-030). But Pecksniff's subsequent intervention — his manipulation of the old man's suspicions — caused Old Martin to "distrust him again, and burn the paper to ashes" (052-030).

The destruction of this document is the novel's darkest moment for the younger Martin and Mary. It leaves Pecksniff seemingly triumphant, Martin apparently disinherited forever, and the marriage forbidden. Yet Old Martin's later confession reveals that even this destruction was part of a larger design: he needed to see Pecksniff fully exposed before he could act.

## The Event

The resolution unfolds across three stages:

**The Confession (Chapter 52).** After denouncing Pecksniff, Old Martin explains his long game. He tells how he had hoped that love might grow between Martin and Mary, and how "the pleasure of such a scheme for the happiness of others was new and indistinct within him" (052-030). He confesses his own fault — that when Martin came to him already loving Mary, he "tortured himself with the reflection that they, so young, to whom he had been so kind a benefactor, were already like the world, and bent on their own selfish, stealthy ends" (052-030). The old man's admission of his own selfishness — his desire to control, to be the author of others' happiness — is the moral key to the entire novel.

**The Restoration (Chapter 53).** Martin and Mary are formally restored to Old Martin's favour. The will is renewed. The marriage is sanctioned. But the inheritance is no longer a tool of control; Old Martin has learned, through his own long experiment in observing Pecksniff, that "self; grasping, eager, narrow-ranging, overreaching self" is "the root of the vile tree" (052-001). His gift to Martin and Mary is therefore framed not as a reward but as a trust.

**The Feast (Chapter 54).** The novel's final celebration, held at the Temple with Tom Pinch at the table's foot, completes the resolution. As Dickens describes it: "If there were a genial face at that board, it was Tom's. They all took their tone from Tom" (054-015). Tom Pinch — the character who asked for nothing and gave everything — becomes the novel's true moral beneficiary: not of money, but of love and community. "His heart was full, he said, of happiness. And so it was. Tom spoke the honest truth. It was" (054-015).

## Aftermath

The will's resolution sends each character to their proper fate. Pecksniff is reduced to drunken beggary, still performing piety. Jonas is dead by his own hand, his crimes exposed. Martin and Mary marry and prosper. Tom Pinch and his sister Ruth find domestic happiness — Ruth with John Westlock, Tom in the quiet satisfaction of being loved by those he loves. The "Jolly Tapley" becomes a real establishment, Mark Tapley having earned his own prosperity through loyalty and good humour.

Dickens's final pages, unusually for him, extend the resolution decades forward into the Postscript (1868), in which the author revisits his American impressions with a new generosity — a gesture of reconciliation that mirrors the novel's own movement from bitterness to forgiveness.

## Narrative Function

The will resolution accomplishes what the novel has been driving toward: the transformation of inheritance from a mechanism of control into an act of love. Old Martin begins the story as a man who uses his wealth to test and manipulate; he ends it as a man who has learned that the only inheritance worth giving is given freely. The novel thus closes not on the melodramatic satisfactions of punishment (though Pecksniff and Jonas are punished) but on the quieter satisfactions of earned contentment — Tom Pinch's happiness, Mark Tapley's jollity, the marriage of true minds that has nothing to do with money and everything to do with character.

## Quotations

Old Martin's climactic denunciation identifies selfishness as the novel's moral root:

> "Self; grasping, eager, narrow-ranging, overreaching self, with its long train of suspicions, lusts, deceits, and all their growing consequences, was the root of the vile tree."
>
> (052-002)

The novel's final feast crowns Tom Pinch — the character who asked nothing — as its true centre of moral gravity:

> Young Martin sat at the head of the table, and Tom Pinch at the foot; and if there were a genial face at that board, it was Tom's. They all took their tone from Tom. Everybody drank to him, everybody looked to him, everybody thought of him, everybody loved him.
>
> (053-066)
