# Austin Friars

Austin Friars is the London address to which [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]] is directed after his dismissal from [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s service — the site of his mysterious new employment and one of the novel's most carefully guarded secrets. The address, and the shadowy Mr. Fips who presides there, constitute the mechanism by which old [[martin-chuzzlewit-sr|Martin Chuzzlewit]] secretly provides for Tom while maintaining the fiction of his estrangement from the novel's virtuous characters.

## The Mystery of the Address

Tom first learns of the position when [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] brings him a letter from a mysterious employer. Tom reads the address aloud:

> "Mr. Fips," said Tom, reading it aloud. "Austin Friars. Austin Friars sounds ghostly, John."
>
> (039-090)

The observation is characteristically Pinchian The observation is characteristically Pinchian — innocent, slightly whimsical, entirely missing the point. The address is not religious; it is the operational front for old Martin's concealed benevolence. Tom, [[ruth-pinch|Ruth]], and [[john-westlock|John Westlock]] travel there "in a flutter" (039-098), and after several wrong turns, arrive at "a very dark, very quiet, and very little street" (039-106) — the anti-spectacle of genuine kindness.

The office is run by Mr. Fips, a colorless functionary whose very blandness is a disguise. "Mr. Fips, of Austin Friars" (039-155) becomes Tom's professional identity, and the work — copying documents — is deliberately undemanding, designed to support Tom without humiliating him. The arrangement is old Martin's characteristic method: generosity conducted through intermediaries, love expressed as indirection.

## Revelation

The secret of Austin Friars is unveiled at the novel's climax. When old Martin finally reveals himself as Tom's anonymous benefactor, the address takes on retrospective significance: "Mr. Fips, of Austin Friars" was old Martin's agent all along. The revelation transforms every scene set in that dark little street from mystery into evidence of the old man's hidden watchfulness. Fips himself appears at the final dinner, "old Fips of Austin Friars, present at the dinner-table" (053-067) — the anonymous functionary now revealed as an instrument of the novel's moral resolution.
