# Loyalty

Loyalty is the moral counterweight to [[selfishness]] in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. Where selfishness corrodes all relationships into transactions, loyalty preserves them through disinterested devotion. The novel's three most loyal characters — [[tom-pinch|Tom Pinch]], [[mark-tapley|Mark Tapley]], and [[mary-graham|Mary Graham]] — each demonstrate a different form of fidelity: Tom's loyalty is innocent and grateful, Mark's is cheerful and self-conscious, and Mary's is steadfast under pressure.

## Overview

Loyalty in the novel is never simply a matter of not betraying someone. It is an active virtue that requires moral effort and often self-sacrifice. Tom Pinch remains loyal to Pecksniff long after the reader has seen through him, not from stupidity but from a genuine incapacity to think ill of his benefactor. His loyalty is a form of innocence that Pecksniff exploits but cannot corrupt. When Tom finally sees the truth, his loyalty transfers seamlessly to old Martin and Mary — it is a disposition, not a calculation.

Mark Tapley's loyalty is perhaps the novel's most original moral creation. He seeks out hardship as a test of his "jolliness," volunteering to accompany Martin to America precisely because it will be difficult. "There's no credit in being jolly where a man's mind is made easy," Mark reflects (005-021). His loyalty is not passive — it is a project, a conscious commitment to maintaining good spirits in the service of others. When Martin falls ill in Eden, Mark's loyalty becomes literal life-support: he nurses Martin through fever while maintaining the fiction that their situation is merely an adventure.

## In the Novel

The novel defines loyalty largely through its absence. Old Martin Chuzzlewit's wealth has made him suspicious of every relationship: "I have never found one nature, no, not one, in which, being wealthy and alone, I was not forced to detect the latent corruption that lay hid within it" (004-022). His tragedy is that his suspicion drives away the genuinely loyal — Mary Graham, Tom Pinch — while leaving him vulnerable to the performative loyalty of Pecksniff.

Pecksniff's loyalty is the novel's study in counterfeit virtue. He speaks constantly of duty, affection, and family obligation, but every profession of loyalty is a bid for advantage. His relationship with old Martin is purely transactional — loyalty as investment strategy. The exposure of Pecksniff in the final chapters is simultaneously an exposure of false loyalty: the mask of devotion stripped away to reveal self-interest.

### Key Passages

Tom Pinch's loyalty is tested most severely when Pecksniff dismisses him. Even in the moment of rejection, Tom cannot bring himself to condemn his former master: "He was my benefactor. He made me what I was" (031-004, paraphrased). The pathos of the scene lies in the reader's knowledge that Pecksniff deserves no such gratitude — Tom's loyalty is wasted on an unworthy object, yet the waste makes the loyalty more admirable, not less.

Tom Pinch is described as Martin's "only disinterested and faithful companion" during their youth (006-078), a steadfastness that defines his character long before Martin recognizes its value. Tom and Martin schoolfellows are said to "walk it yet, in mutual faithfulness and friendship" (005-130), a bond that survives Martin's pride and Pecksniff's machinations.

Mark Tapley's loyalty finds its fullest expression in the Eden chapters. When Martin, in his fever, tells Mark to save himself — "Do the best you can for yourself. You'll soon have only yourself to consider" — Mark's reply is characteristic: "The head partner a-asking forgiveness of Co., eh? There must be something wrong in the firm when that happens" (023-056). Mark's loyalty is inseparable from his humour — he sustains Martin not only with nursing but with the fiction of normalcy, the pretence that Chuzzlewit & Co. is still a going concern.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens contrasts two models of loyalty in the novel: the feudal model (loyalty as duty owed to a superior) and the affective model (loyalty as love freely given). Pecksniff professes the former; Tom, Mark, and Mary practice the latter. The novel's conclusion endorses the affective model — the loyal characters are rewarded with happiness and the disloyal are punished or exposed. But Dickens complicates the picture by showing that loyalty to an unworthy object (Tom to Pecksniff) is not wasted: it preserves Tom's character even as it delays his enlightenment.
