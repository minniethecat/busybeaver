# Duty

Duty is the most frequently invoked and most frequently abused moral concept in *Martin Chuzzlewit*. With nearly forty occurrences across the novel, the language of duty is deployed by characters who use it to disguise self-interest, while the characters who genuinely discharge their duties do so without fanfare or moral rhetoric.

## Overview

Dickens treats duty as a linguistic battleground: a word that ought to signify moral obligation but is so consistently exploited by the novel's villains that its very utterance becomes a signal of hypocrisy. The Pecksniff family are the primary abusers of the word, but the critique extends to a broader satire of Victorians who used moral language to justify exploitation.

## In the Novel

Mr. Pecksniff introduces duty as his personal refrain early in the novel: "I have a duty to discharge, from which, depend upon it, I shall not shrink. No, sir, I shall not shrink" (003-119) (003-119). The repetition is theatrical — Pecksniff's "duty" invariably coincides with his own advantage, and his professions of reluctance to perform it are part of the performance.

His daughters learn the same language. Charity and Mercy Pecksniff speak of their father "with much duty" when performing filial devotion for an audience (005-155) (005-155). Mrs. Lupin, the honest innkeeper, is told by the Pecksniff household to be satisfied that they "know our duty" (010-007) (010-007) — a phrase that closes down inquiry while implying moral superiority.

Old Martin Chuzzlewit identifies the Pecksniffian abuse of duty in his account of his own manipulated past. He describes how Pecksniff, having isolated him, could "sustain the calumny, with the help of a good conscience, and that gentleman's friendship" (010-094) (010-094) — conscience and duty becoming allied terms in the vocabulary of self-justification.

The novel contrasts this rhetorical duty with the genuine article. Young Martin, speaking of Mary Graham, notes that "she has some girlish notions about duty and gratitude, and all the rest of it, which are rather hard to fathom" (006-076) (006-076). His dismissive tone is Dickens's signal: the "girlish notions" are in fact the novel's moral standard, and Martin's inability to fathom them measures his own moral immaturity.

### Key Passages

Pecksniff's most revealing deployment of duty comes when he publicly denounces young Martin: "This lowly roof, sir, must not be contaminated by the presence of one who has deceived, and cruelly deceived, an honourable, beloved, venerated, and venerable gentleman" (012-179) (012-179). Here "duty" becomes the justification for cruelty — the language of moral obligation providing cover for Pecksniff's strategic expulsion of a rival.

The Pecksniffian conscience-bank metaphor extends the theme: "my conscience is my bank. I have a trifle invested there" (020-028) (020-028). Duty and conscience are Pecksniff's spiritual currency — terms he deposits and withdraws as needed, emptied of all meaning through constant self-serving use.

## Dickens's Satirical Treatment

The satire of duty operates through accumulated verbal irony. Each time Pecksniff invokes his "duty," the reader registers the widening gap between word and deed. Dickens never needs to tell us that Pecksniff is lying — the cumulative weight of his duty-talk does the work. The technique reaches its height when Pecksniff, having been exposed by Old Martin in Chapter 52, can no longer deploy the word at all — his moral vocabulary has been stripped from him.
