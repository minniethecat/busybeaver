# Augustus Moddle

A young gentleman boarder at [[todgers|Todgers's]] Commercial Boardinghouse whose defining characteristic is an extravagant, almost parodic melancholy, and who becomes the hapless target of [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]]'s determination to marry.

## Overview

Augustus Moddle is introduced as a man who "often informed [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]] that the sun had set upon him; that the billows had rolled over him; that the Car of Juggernaut had crushed him, and also that the deadly Upas tree of Java had blighted him"(010-008). This catalogue of romantic despair, delivered in the comic register of Dickensian hyperbole, immediately establishes Moddle as one of the novel's most purely comic characters — a walking anthology of fashionable melancholy whose self-pity is as performative as [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]]'s virtue.

His courtship by [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] is one of the novel's sustained comic set-pieces: a determinedly marriage-minded woman pursuing a man who sees himself as too blighted for earthly happiness, with both parties operating on mutually incompatible premises that somehow, improbably, converge on an engagement.

## Biography

### Background & Family

Moddle is "a young gentleman... with rising prospects, and 'almost' enough to live on"(010-030). The qualifying "almost" is characteristic — everything about Moddle hovers at the threshold of adequacy without quite crossing it. His background is genteel but unspecified; he is one of the novel's many young men of uncertain means orbiting the marriage market.

### Role in the Plot

Moddle's courtship by [[charity-pecksniff|Charity Pecksniff]] unfolds over a series of evenings at [[todgers|Todgers's]], each stage more darkly comic than the last. When [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] first treats him with "distant haughtiness," Moddle is "additionally crushed" and remonstrates with [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]: "Even she turns from me, [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]]"(010-010). His attachment to [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] takes a characteristically peculiar form: "Oh, [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]], if you knew what a comfort her nose is to me!"(010-015).

[[charity-pecksniff|Charity]], advised by [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]] to "give Mr. Moddle a little encouragement... and he'll speak fast enough," deploys her side profile strategically, and the cribbage games begin(010-030). "On the seventh night of cribbage, when [[mrs-todgers|Mrs. Todgers]], sitting by, proposed that instead of gambling they should play for 'love,' Mr. Moddle was seen to change colour. On the fourteenth night, he kissed [[charity-pecksniff|Miss Pecksniff]]'s snuffers, in the passage, when she went upstairs to bed; meaning to have kissed her hand, but missing it"(010-032).

The engagement, when it comes, follows [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]]'s strategic withdrawal of [[love|affection]]. She confesses that "people had begun to talk, the thing had been observed, and it was necessary that they should be nothing more to each other, than any gentleman and lady in society usually are"(010-040). Moddle, "who had by this time become in the last degree maudlin, and wept abundantly, inferred from the foregoing avowal, that it was his mission to communicate to others the blight which had fallen on himself; and that, being a kind of unintentional Vampire, he had had [[charity-pecksniff|Miss Pecksniff]] assigned to him by the Fates, as Victim Number One"(010-042). The engagement is accepted with this magnificent inversion of romantic convention — Moddle sees himself not as the suitor but as the vampire, and [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] as his assigned victim.

### Fate & Resolution

The novel's final mention of Moddle comes when [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]], after Mercy's marriage, performs introductions with "evident pride": "Mr. [[tom-pinch|Thomas Pinch]]! Mr. Moddle. Where's my sister?"(054-050). The implication is that [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]] has secured her prize, though the marriage itself is not dramatized. Moddle's fate is to be claimed — his melancholy absorbed into the [[seth-pecksniff|Pecksniff]] family machinery.

## Character Analysis

Moddle is a comic masterpiece in miniature. His melancholy is a parody of Byronic romanticism reduced to suburban scale — the "Car of Juggernaut" and "deadly Upas tree of Java"(010-008)invoke exotic catastrophes to explain what is, at bottom, mere romantic indecision. Dickens's genius here is to make Moddle simultaneously ridiculous and sympathetic: his self-dramatizing gloom is absurd, but his predicament — pursued by a woman whose interest is entirely strategic — is genuinely pitiable.

The cribbage courtship is one of Dickens's finest sustained comic sequences. Each detail accumulates: the fourteen nights, the missed kiss that lands on the snuffers instead of the hand, the proposal framed as vampiric doom. [[charity-pecksniff|Charity]]'s calculation and Moddle's self-absorption make them a perfectly mismatched pair whose engagement satisfies neither but amuses the reader enormously.

Dickens also uses Moddle as a bridge figure in the novel's satirical geography. "From Mr. Moddle to [[eden|Eden]] is an easy and natural transition," the narrator observes(010-050). "Mr. Moddle, living in the atmosphere of [[charity-pecksniff|Miss Pecksniff]]'s love, dwelt (if he had but known it) in a terrestrial Paradise. The thriving city of [[eden|Eden]] was also a terrestrial Paradise, upon the showing of its proprietors." The parallel between Moddle's deluded contentment and the [[america|America]]n swindle of [[eden|Eden]] links the novel's domestic and international [[comedy|satire]]s.
