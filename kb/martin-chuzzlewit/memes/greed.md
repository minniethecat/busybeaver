# Greed

Greed is the economic face of [[selfishness]] in *Martin Chuzzlewit* — selfishness quantified, monetised, and transmitted across generations. The novel traces a lineage of greed from old Anthony Chuzzlewit through his son Jonas to the fraudulent enterprises of Tigg Montague's Anglo-Bengalee Disinterested Loan and Life Assurance Company. Dickens presents greed not as an isolated vice but as a social contagion that corrupts family bonds, commercial institutions, and ultimately the law itself.

## Overview

In the Preface, Dickens explicitly links Jonas's criminality to his upbringing: "I conceive that the sordid coarseness and brutality of Jonas would be unnatural, if there had been nothing in his early education, and in the precept and example always before him, to engender and develop the vices that make him odious" (P03-004). "So born and so bred; admired for that which made him hateful, and justified from his cradle in cunning, treachery, and avarice" — the judgment is as much on Anthony as on Jonas. Greed, Dickens insists, is not innate but taught.

## In the Novel

Anthony Chuzzlewit and his ancient clerk Chuffey form the novel's most chilling portrait of greed's effects. Anthony has spent a lifetime accumulating wealth at the cost of every human relationship. Chuffey, his loyal clerk, has been reduced to a "worn-out cask" by decades of proximity to Anthony's avarice (011-093). Yet even this relationship contains "the redeeming quality of being felt in sympathy with the only creature to whom he was linked by ties of long association" (011-001). The moment is significant: greed has not entirely extinguished the capacity for attachment, even in Anthony's desiccated world.

Jonas Chuzzlewit is his father's son but worse — greed accelerated by impatience. Where Anthony hoarded, Jonas schemes. His plan to murder his father for the inheritance is the novel's most extreme demonstration of greed's logic: when relationships are reduced to financial transactions, murder is merely a shortcut. The comic horror of the Anglo-Bengalee — a life insurance company that insures nothing — applies greed's logic to an institutional scale. Tigg Montague's company is greed systematised, a machine for converting trust into cash (027-045).

### Key Passages

The deathbed scene between Anthony and Chuffey reveals the human cost of greed. The old clerk weeps for his master: "I loved him. He was good to me. We learnt Tare and Tret together at school" (052-083). The commercial language — "Tare and Tret" are mercantile terms for weight deductions — is the only vocabulary of affection these men possess. Their entire relationship has been mediated by commerce, yet something human persists beneath the ledgers.

The novel's moralists point to greed's self-defeating nature. Old Martin, in his final judgment, traces Jonas's destruction to his father's example: "the recoil upon that old man, in his unhonoured age, is not a mere piece of poetical justice, but is the extreme exposition of a direct truth" (P03-004). Greed devours its own — Jonas kills his father, and greed kills Jonas.

## Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's critique of greed is less theological than social. He is less interested in greed as a sin than in greed as a system — the way it reproduces itself through families (Anthony → Jonas), through institutions (the Anglo-Bengalee), and through national cultures (the American land speculation). The Anglo-Bengalee, with its grand offices and empty coffers, is Dickens's satire of Victorian financial capitalism: an elaborate confidence trick that the law cannot touch because it is technically legal.

The novel offers no simple cure for greed. Money itself is not the problem — old Martin's wealth is ultimately used for benevolent purposes — but the love of money to the exclusion of human relationship. The cure, Dickens implies, is not poverty but a reorientation of the affections: from wealth to people, from transaction to gift, from [[selfishness]] to [[generosity]].
