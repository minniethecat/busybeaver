# Aileen Moffatt

## Role

Aileen Moffatt is [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]]'s unwitting rival — the wealthy young woman whom [[captain-warkworth|Captain Warkworth]] has chosen as the financially prudent object of his matrimonial ambitions. She is described in [[ch08|Chapter 8]] as "a dear, simple-minded child" (008-002) — language that reveals how the novel's world values women for their tractability as much as their fortunes. She is not a villain but a structural position in the novel's economy: she represents what money can buy that love cannot.

## Biography

Aileen is young, wealthy, and well-connected — the perfect match for an ambitious officer like Warkworth. Her guardians are protective of both her fortune and her reputation, and Warkworth's courtship is conducted under constraints that he habitually evades. "Report engaged her to at least ten different people at Simla" (008-002), [[duchess-of-crowborough|the Duchess]] gossips, placing Aileen at the center of the marriage market's speculative economy. She is, in the novel's terms, what [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] can never be: legitimate, endowed, and uncomplicated by scandal. Her fortune will fund Warkworth's political career, and her youth and beauty sweeten the transaction.

## Key Scenes

Aileen appears primarily through others' discussions of her rather than in her own voice. The Duchess's gossip in [[ch08|Chapter 8]] and Warkworth's private calculations in [[ch16|Chapter 16]] define her more than any actions of her own. Her structural significance — as the marriage market's preference for fortune over feeling — is more important than her personal characterization.

## Relationships

Her relationship with **[[captain-warkworth|Captain Warkworth]]** is strategic on his side and presumably genuine on hers. She is the beneficiary of a system that rewards wealth and legitimacy — not through any Machiavellian maneuvering but simply by existing as the kind of woman the system was designed to privilege. Her rivalry with **[[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]]** is not personal but structural: a competition between money and love in which money holds every advantage.

## Character Analysis

Aileen is less a fully realized character than a position in the novel's moral economy — she is what Warkworth chooses when he chooses money over love. Ward's treatment of her is notably gentle: she is not villainized for being rich or young or conventional. She is simply the system working as designed, and the novel's critique is of the system rather than of the girl who benefits from it. This restraint is characteristic of Ward's moral method — she critiques structures, not individuals.
