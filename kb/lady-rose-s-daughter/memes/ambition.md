# Ambition in *Lady Rose's Daughter*

## Definition

Ambition in *Lady Rose's Daughter* is presented as a double-edged force — necessary for survival in a competitive society, yet morally corrosive when pursued without restraint. Ward examines ambition primarily through the character of [[captain-warkworth|Captain Harry Warkworth]], whose career calculations drive the novel's romantic plot, but the theme extends to nearly every major character. In the world of the novel, ambition is the engine of social mobility, but the fuel it burns is often integrity, loyalty, and love.

## Manifestations

**Career Ambition.** [[captain-warkworth|Captain Warkworth]]'s driving motive is professional advancement. "Only a few more years in the army, then retirement with ample means, a charming wife, and a seat in Parliament" (016-139) — this vision of his future determines every significant choice he makes. His ambition is not presented as villainous but as ordinary, even rational, within his social context.

**Social Ambition.** [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]] possesses "the subtlest and most intelligent ambition" (008-027) — an ambition expressed not through career but through social influence. Her craving "for society and conversation" drives her to cultivate powerful connections, yet this very success makes her vulnerable to [[lady-henry|Lady Henry]]'s jealousy.

**Thwarted Ambition.** [[colonel-delaney|Colonel Delaney]] resented his wife's indifference to "his professional interests and ambitions" (002-008), revealing how ambition can become a source of marital friction when partners do not share the same aspirational horizon.

**Ambition Renounced.** [[jacob-delafield|Jacob Delafield]], though "most of the normal objects of ambition were within his grasp" (013-009), represents the possibility of a life oriented toward love and service rather than advancement. His example serves as an implicit critique of Warkworth's calculations.

## Key Passages

> To jeopardize a plan so manifestly desirable, so easy to carry out, so far-reaching in its favorable effects upon his life, for the sake of those hard and doubtful alternatives in which a marriage with Julie would involve him, never seriously entered his mind. (016-139)

This passage, in [[ch16|Chapter 16]], lays bare Warkworth's calculus — ambition is not a passion but a program, a plan from which deviation is literally unthinkable.

> The subtlest and most intelligent ambition she had trained and improved till it had become a second nature. (008-027)

[[julie-le-breton|Julie]]'s ambition, described in [[ch08|Chapter 8]], is intellectual rather than mercenary — the desire to exercise influence, to be significant — and this refinement makes it both more admirable and more dangerous.

## Critical Analysis

Ward's treatment of ambition is notable for its refusal to moralize simplistically. Warkworth is not punished for his ambition; he largely succeeds. [[julie-le-breton|Julie]]'s social ambition is portrayed as understandable — even admirable — given her precarious position. The novel suggests that ambition in itself is neutral; what matters is what one is willing to sacrifice for it and whether the ambition serves a purpose larger than self-advancement.
