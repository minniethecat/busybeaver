## Overview

[[jacob-delafield|Jacob Delafield]]'s farewell encompasses the period during which he prepares for and departs on a military expedition to the African interior. The event unfolds against the backdrop of London's social season, intertwining with the Wednesday receptions at [[bruton-street|Bruton Street]], the [[chatton-house|Chatton House]] party, and ultimately [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]]'s [[paris|Paris]] journey. Jacob's departure is marked by a series of leave-takings — from [[lady-henry|Lady Henry]], from [[duchess-of-crowborough|the Duchess]], and most poignantly from [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] — in which suppressed emotions and unspoken attachments surface with unusual force.

## Background

### The Africa Mission

[[jacob-delafield|Jacob Delafield]], a rising officer and the heir to the Delafield family, receives orders for a mission to the African interior. The expedition promises to be arduous and dangerous — a journey into "illimitable and mysterious Africa" [013-074] that will take him far from London society for an extended period. The mission carries both professional significance for Jacob's career and personal consequence for those who care about him.

### Timing and Urgency

The news of Jacob's departure comes amid a turbulent period in the [[bruton-street|Bruton Street]] circle. [[lady-henry|Lady Henry]]'s quarrel with [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] has divided the social set, Warkworth is also preparing to depart for Africa, and [[julie-le-breton|Julie]]'s position grows increasingly precarious:

> "You have forgotten, my dear Evelyn, all this time, that Warkworth goes away — to mid-Africa — in little more than two weeks." [016-028]

## The Farewell Scenes

### A Tense Leave-Taking

The farewells occur over several days, beginning with Warkworth's departure for the Isle of Wight to see his mother and continuing through the social gatherings that mark the end of the London season:

> On the morning following these events, Warkworth went down to the Isle of Wight to see his mother. On the journey he thought much of Julie. They had parted awkwardly the night before. [014-001]

### The Duchess's Gathering

At [[duchess-of-crowborough|the Duchess]] of Crowborough's gathering, the farewells take on a public dimension. [[julie-le-breton|Julie]], caught between her feelings for Warkworth and her obligations to [[duchess-of-crowborough|the Duchess]], stands apart:

> Julie counted for little in these farewells. She stood apart and rather silent. "They have had their goodbye," thought the Duchess, with a thrill she could not help. [017-181]

The Duchess's observation captures the hidden emotional drama — the private goodbye between [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] and Warkworth that has already occurred, invisible to the assembled company.

### The Final Evening

The farewell party breaks up with a series of leave-takings, each charged with unspoken feeling:

> The party broke up. The Duchess kissed her Julie with peculiar tenderness; Delafield pressed her hand, and his deep, kind eyes gave her a lingering look, of which, however, she was quite unconscious; Meredith renewed his half-irritable, half-affectionate counsels of rest and recreation; Mrs. Montresor was conventionally effusive; Montresor alone bade the mistress of the house a somewhat cold and perfunctory farewell. [017-187]

### The Duchess's Parting with Jacob

The most emotionally charged farewell is between Jacob and [[duchess-of-crowborough|the Duchess]], who has been a maternal figure to him:

> After a pause he made a little gesture of farewell which the Duchess understood. She kissed his hand and turned away weeping. [020-092]

## Aftermath

### The African Desert

After Jacob's departure, [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] follows his progress in her imagination. The African landscape becomes a canvas for her anxieties and affections:

> Far, far in the African desert she followed the march of Warkworth's little troop. [022-071]

> Ah, the blinding light — the African scrub and sand — the long, single line — the native porters with their loads — the handful of English officers with that slender figure at their head — the endless, waterless path with its palms and mangoes and mimosas — the scene rushed upon the inward eye and held it. She felt the heat, the thirst, the weariness of bone and brain — all the spell and mystery of the unmapped continent. [022-072]

### Grace Period

The departure also provides a temporal marker for the novel's romantic resolution:

> "And, anyway, the man's off to Africa for a twelvemonth at least. Miss Le Breton will have time to forget him." [020-179]

## Significance

Jacob's farewell serves as the mechanism that forces suppressed emotions into the open. The imminence of departure — the knowledge that months or years may pass before these people see each other again — strips away the conventions of polite society and reveals the true attachments beneath. For [[julie-le-breton|Julie]], the farewell crystallizes the conflict between her lingering bond with Warkworth and the steadier devotion of [[jacob-delafield|Jacob Delafield]]. For Jacob, it is a test of his ability to act on his feelings before circumstance removes the opportunity.
