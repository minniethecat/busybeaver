# Mrs. Delaray

Mrs. Delaray is a celebrated young beauty in London society, mentioned in connection with [[lord-lackington|Lord Lackington]]'s lifelong project — a gallery of a hundred portraits representing the beauty of the reign.

## Lord Lackington's Gallery

[[lord-lackington|Lord Lackington]], though primarily known as a statesman and peer, had been all his life "a skilful artist, a man of fashion, and a harmless haunter of lovely women" (020). Over the decades he had "all but completed a gallery of a hundred portraits" — an extraordinary artistic and social project documenting female beauty across a long reign. Mrs. Delaray's portrait was to be the hundredth in this series, the final jewel in a collection "of which Mrs. Norton was the first" (020).

The significance of this position — the capstone of a lifetime's artistic labor — marks Mrs. Delaray as the recognized beauty of her moment, the heir to a tradition of celebrated women that stretched back through the Victorian era.

## Lord Lackington's Seizure

Mrs. Delaray's portrait is linked to [[lord-lackington|Lord Lackington]]'s final illness. He was engaged upon the portrait "at the time of his seizure" (020), and even as he lay dying, the unfinished painting weighed on his mind. [[lord-uredale|Lord Uredale]] reports that the old man "has been making arrangements with the husband to get it finished" (020) — a final act of artistic dedication from a man who, for all his social rigidity, understood beauty as one of life's consolations.

The young husband who "ran down the steps hastily, raised his hat, and disappeared" (020) from [[lord-lackington|Lord Lackington]]'s house after discussing the portrait's completion is the only direct glimpse the novel offers of Mrs. Delaray's world — a world of youth, beauty, and fashionable life that exists at the periphery of the novel's deeper concerns.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Delaray never appears directly. She exists as a symbol — of the beauty that [[lord-lackington|Lord Lackington]] pursued in art even as he denied his own daughter, of the brilliant social world that judges [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]] by standards she can never fully meet, and of the fragility of all human projects in the face of mortality.
