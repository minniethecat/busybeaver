# Mrs. Delafield

Mrs. Delafield is the widowed mother of [[jacob-delafield|Jacob Delafield]], living in London with an unmarried daughter. She appears only indirectly in the novel — through the recollections of [[sir-wilfrid-bury|Sir Wilfrid Bury]] and through [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]]'s sense of family obligation — but her presence shapes her son's choices in significant ways.

## Character

[[sir-wilfrid-bury|Sir Wilfrid Bury]], who had been an intimate friend of her dead husband Lord Hubert, describes her as "lethargic" and "good-natured" (002). He notes that [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]] had inherited "his mother's inertia without her good temper" during his sluggish Oxford years (002). Mrs. Delafield appears to be a woman of placid temperament, comfortable in the conventional social world, and not given to the intellectual or moral intensities that drive characters like [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]] or [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]] himself.

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Delafield's importance is structural rather than dramatic. As a "somewhat helpless being" (010), she anchors [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]] in London and in family duty. His sense of obligation to her and his sister prevents him from emigrating — a course he seriously considers in his dissatisfaction with London society. "He could not reconcile it to his conscience to go very far from them" (010). In this way, Mrs. Delafield's very helplessness keeps [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]] in England, making possible his eventual role in [[julie-le-breton|Julie]]'s life.

By [[ch24|Chapter XXIV]], [[jacob-delafield|Jacob]] has married, and his mother's presence in his life has presumably receded. But the filial duty she represents is a quiet moral force in the novel — one of the many ties that bind characters to their social world whether they wish it or not.
