# Duke Street

## Location

Duke Street is located in St. James's, a historic district in central London known for its gentlemen's clubs, government offices, and bachelor chambers. The street is within walking distance of the political and social centers of Westminster and Mayfair. [[sir-wilfrid-bury|Sir Wilfrid Bury]] maintains rooms there.

## Significance

Duke Street appears in the novel exclusively as the location of [[sir-wilfrid-bury|Sir Wilfrid Bury]]'s "old rooms" [005-008]. These rooms serve as his London base — a masculine, independent space that contrasts with the drawing-rooms and society houses where most of the novel's action takes place. The Duke Street rooms represent a particular kind of Edwardian male domesticity: comfortable, unshowy, and private.

## Description

Sir Wilfrid's rooms in Duke Street are described with affection. He walks "home to his rooms in Duke Street, St. James's" [005-001] after an evening engagement, the word "home" conveying the comfort he finds there. The rooms are "old rooms" [005-008] — he has kept them for years, and they bear the accumulated character of a long tenancy. In one scene, Sir Wilfrid and a companion "let themselves into [[duke-of-chudleigh|the Duke]] Street house and were groping their way" [005-045] in the dark, suggesting the informality of a bachelor establishment where one comes and goes at one's own hours.

## Associated Characters

- **[[sir-wilfrid-bury|Sir Wilfrid Bury]]** — The principal resident of Duke Street. His rooms there are a fixture of his London life, representing his independence and his rootedness in the masculine world of politics and diplomacy.
