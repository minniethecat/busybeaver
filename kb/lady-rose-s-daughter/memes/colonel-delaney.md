# Colonel Delaney

## Role
was Lady Rose's lawful husband and, legally though not biologically, [[julie-le-breton|Julie Le Breton]]'s father. A man 'absorbed in his military life, silent, narrowly able, and governed by a strict Anglicanism' (002-008), he found his intelligent, emotionally alive wife 'a tiring and trying companion.' When Lady Rose left him for [[marriott-dalrymple|Marriott Dalrymple]], Delaney — 'like Karennine in Tolstoy's great novel' (002-010) — refused to sue for divorce on religious grounds, ensuring that [[julie-le-breton|Julie]] could never be legitimized. His moral rigidity is presented not as unusual cruelty but as the ordinary operation of a system that prioritized principle over people.

## Biography
is defined by his refusal to grant Lady Rose a divorce, recounted in [[ch02|Chapter 2]]. The explicit comparison to Tolstoy's Karenin (002-010) signals Ward's awareness that she is working within a European tradition of novels about failed marriages and their consequences.
