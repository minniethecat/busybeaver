[002-001] MURDERING THE INNOCENTS

[002-002] THOMAS GRADGRIND, sir. A [[thomas-gradgrind|man of realities]]. A man of [[facts]] and

[002-003] calculations. A man who proceeds upon the principle that two and two are

[002-004] four, and nothing over, and who is not to be talked into allowing for

[002-005] anything over. Thomas [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], sir—peremptorily Thomas—Thomas

[002-006] Gradgrind. With a rule and a pair of scales, and the multiplication

[002-007] table always in his pocket, sir, ready to weigh and measure any parcel of

[002-008] human [[nature]], and tell you exactly what it comes to. It is a mere

[002-009] question of figures, a case of simple arithmetic. You might hope to get

[002-010] some other nonsensical belief into the [[reason|head]] of George Gradgrind, or

[002-011] Augustus Gradgrind, or John Gradgrind, or Joseph Gradgrind (all

[002-012] supposititious, non-existent persons), but into the head of Thomas

[002-013] Gradgrind—no, sir!

[002-014] In such terms Mr. Gradgrind always mentally introduced himself, whether

[002-015] to his private circle of acquaintance, or to the public in general. In

[002-016] such terms, no doubt, substituting the words ‘boys and girls,’ for ‘sir,’

[002-017] Thomas Gradgrind now presented Thomas Gradgrind to the little pitchers

[002-018] before him, who were to be filled so full of facts.

[002-019] Indeed, as he eagerly sparkled at them from the cellarage before

[002-020] mentioned, he seemed a kind of cannon loaded to the muzzle with facts,

[002-021] and prepared to blow them clean out of the regions of [[childhood]] at one

[002-022] discharge. He seemed a galvanizing apparatus, too, charged with a grim

[002-023] mechanical substitute for the tender young imaginations that were to be

[002-024] stormed away.

[002-025] ‘Girl number twenty,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, squarely pointing with his

[002-026] square forefinger, ‘I don’t know that girl. Who is that girl?’

[002-027] ‘[[sissy-jupe|Sissy Jupe]], sir,’ explained number twenty, blushing, standing up, and

[002-028] curtseying.

[002-029] ‘Sissy is not a name,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Don’t call yourself Sissy.

[002-030] Call yourself Cecilia.’

[002-031] ‘It’s father as calls me Sissy, sir,’ returned the young girl in a

[002-032] trembling voice, and with another curtsey.

[002-033] ‘Then he has no business to do it,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Tell him he

[002-034] mustn’t. Cecilia Jupe. Let me see. What is your father?’

[002-035] ‘He belongs to [[slearys-circus|the horse-riding]], if you please, sir.’

[002-036] Mr. Gradgrind frowned, and waved off the objectionable calling with his

[002-037] hand.

[002-038] ‘We don’t want to know anything about that, here. You mustn’t tell us

[002-039] about that, here. Your father breaks horses, don’t he?’

[002-040] ‘If you please, sir, when they can get any to break, they do break horses

[002-041] in the ring, sir.’

[002-042] ‘You mustn’t tell us about the ring, here. Very well, then. Describe

[002-043] your father as a horsebreaker. He doctors sick horses, I dare say?’

[002-044] ‘Oh yes, sir.’

[002-045] ‘Very well, then. He is a veterinary surgeon, a farrier, and

[002-046] horsebreaker. Give me your definition of a horse.’

[002-047] (Sissy Jupe thrown into the greatest alarm by this demand.)

[002-048] ‘Girl number twenty unable to define a horse!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, for

[002-049] the general behoof of all the little pitchers. ‘Girl number twenty

[002-050] possessed of no facts, in reference to one of the commonest of animals!

[002-051] Some boy’s definition of a horse. [[bitzer|Bitzer]], yours.’

[002-052] The square finger, moving here and there, lighted suddenly on Bitzer,

[002-053] perhaps because he chanced to sit in the same ray of sunlight which,

[002-054] darting in at one of the bare windows of the intensely white-washed room,

[002-055] irradiated Sissy. For, the boys and girls sat on the face of the

[002-056] inclined plane in two compact bodies, divided up the centre by a narrow

[002-057] interval; and Sissy, being at the corner of a row on the sunny side, came

[002-058] in for the beginning of a sunbeam, of which Bitzer, being at the corner

[002-059] of a row on the other side, a few rows in advance, caught the end. But,

[002-060] whereas the girl was so dark-eyed and dark-haired, that she seemed to

[002-061] receive a deeper and more lustrous colour from the sun, when it shone

[002-062] upon her, the boy was so light-eyed and light-haired that the self-same

[002-063] rays appeared to draw out of him what little colour he ever possessed.

[002-064] His cold eyes would hardly have been eyes, but for the short ends of

[002-065] lashes which, by bringing them into immediate contrast with something

[002-066] paler than themselves, expressed their form. His short-cropped hair

[002-067] might have been a mere continuation of the sandy freckles on his forehead

[002-068] and face. His skin was so unwholesomely deficient in the natural tinge,

[002-069] that he looked as though, if he were cut, he would bleed white.

[002-070] ‘Bitzer,’ said Thomas Gradgrind. ‘Your definition of a horse.’

[002-071] ‘Quadruped. Graminivorous. Forty teeth, namely twenty-four grinders,

[002-072] four eye-teeth, and twelve incisive. Sheds coat in the spring; in marshy

[002-073] countries, sheds hoofs, too. Hoofs hard, but requiring to be shod with

[002-074] iron. Age known by marks in mouth.’ Thus (and much more) Bitzer.

[002-075] ‘Now girl number twenty,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘You know what a horse

[002-076] is.’

[002-077] She curtseyed again, and would have blushed deeper, if she could have

[002-078] blushed deeper than she had blushed all this time. Bitzer, after rapidly

[002-079] blinking at Thomas Gradgrind with both eyes at once, and so catching the

[002-080] light upon his quivering ends of lashes that they looked like the antennæ

[002-081] of busy insects, put his knuckles to his freckled forehead, and sat down

[002-082] again.

[002-083] The third [[gentleman]] now stepped forth. A mighty man at cutting and

[002-084] drying, he was; a government officer; in his way (and in most other

[002-085] people’s too), a professed pugilist; always in [[education|training]], always with a

[002-086] system to force down the general throat like a bolus, always to be heard

[002-087] of at the bar of his little Public-office, ready to fight all England.

[002-088] To continue in fistic phraseology, he had a genius for coming up to the

[002-089] scratch, wherever and whatever it was, and proving himself an ugly

[002-090] customer. He would go in and damage any subject whatever with his right,

[002-091] follow up with his left, stop, exchange, counter, bore his opponent (he

[002-092] always fought All England) to the ropes, and fall upon him neatly. He

[002-093] was certain to knock the wind out of common sense, and render that

[002-094] unlucky adversary deaf to the call of time. And he had it in charge from

[002-095] high authority to bring about the great public-office Millennium, when

[002-096] Commissioners should reign upon earth.

[002-097] ‘Very well,’ said this gentleman, briskly smiling, and folding his arms.

[002-098] ‘That’s a horse. Now, let me ask you girls and boys, Would you paper a

[002-099] room with representations of horses?’

[002-100] After a pause, one half of the children cried in chorus, ‘Yes, sir!’

[002-101] Upon which the other half, seeing in the gentleman’s face that Yes was

[002-102] wrong, cried out in chorus, ‘No, sir!’—as the custom is, in these

[002-103] examinations.

[002-104] ‘Of course, No. Why wouldn’t you?’

[002-105] A pause. One corpulent slow boy, with a wheezy manner of breathing,

[002-106] ventured the answer, Because he wouldn’t paper a room at all, but would

[002-107] paint it.

[002-108] ‘You _must_ paper it,’ said the gentleman, rather warmly.

[002-109] ‘You must paper it,’ said Thomas Gradgrind, ‘whether you like it or not.

[002-110] Don’t tell _us_ you wouldn’t paper it. What do you mean, boy?’

[002-111] ‘I’ll explain to you, then,’ said the gentleman, after another and a

[002-112] dismal pause, ‘why you wouldn’t paper a room with representations of

[002-113] horses. Do you ever see horses walking up and down the sides of rooms in

[002-114] reality—in fact? Do you?’

[002-115] ‘Yes, sir!’ from one half. ‘No, sir!’ from the other.

[002-116] ‘Of course no,’ said the gentleman, with an indignant look at the wrong

[002-117] half. ‘Why, then, you are not to see anywhere, what you don’t see in

[002-118] fact; you are not to have anywhere, what you don’t have in fact. What is

[002-119] called Taste, is only another name for Fact.’ Thomas Gradgrind nodded

[002-120] his approbation.

[002-121] ‘This is a new principle, a discovery, a great discovery,’ said the

[002-122] gentleman. ‘Now, I’ll try you again. Suppose you were going to carpet a

[002-123] room. Would you use a carpet having a representation of flowers upon

[002-124] it?’

[002-125] There being a general conviction by this time that ‘No, sir!’ was always

[002-126] the right answer to this gentleman, the chorus of NO was very strong.

[002-127] Only a few feeble stragglers said Yes: among them Sissy Jupe.

[002-128] ‘Girl number twenty,’ said the gentleman, smiling in the calm strength of

[002-129] knowledge.

[002-130] Sissy blushed, and stood up.

[002-131] ‘So you would carpet your room—or your husband’s room, if you were a

[002-132] grown woman, and had a husband—with representations of flowers, would

[002-133] you?’ said the gentleman. ‘Why would you?’

[002-134] ‘If you please, sir, I am very fond of flowers,’ returned the girl.

[002-135] ‘And is that why you would put tables and chairs upon them, and have

[002-136] people walking over them with heavy boots?’

[002-137] ‘It wouldn’t hurt them, sir. They wouldn’t crush and wither, if you

[002-138] please, sir. They would be the pictures of what was very pretty and

[002-139] pleasant, and I would [[fancy]]—’

[002-140] ‘Ay, ay, ay! But you mustn’t fancy,’ cried the gentleman, quite elated

[002-141] by coming so happily to his point. ‘That’s it! You are never to fancy.’

[002-142] ‘You are not, Cecilia Jupe,’ Thomas Gradgrind solemnly repeated, ‘to do

[002-143] anything of that kind.’

[002-144] ‘Fact, fact, fact!’ said the gentleman. And ‘Fact, fact, fact!’ repeated

[002-145] Thomas Gradgrind.

[002-146] ‘You are to be in all things regulated and governed,’ said the gentleman,

[002-147] ‘by fact. We hope to have, before long, a board of fact, composed of

[002-148] commissioners of fact, who will force the people to be a people of fact,

[002-149] and of nothing but fact. You must discard the word Fancy altogether.

[002-150] You have nothing to do with it. You are not to have, in any object of

[002-151] use or ornament, what would be a contradiction in fact. You don’t walk

[002-152] upon flowers in fact; you cannot be allowed to walk upon flowers in

[002-153] carpets. You don’t find that foreign birds and butterflies come and

[002-154] perch upon your crockery; you cannot be permitted to paint foreign birds

[002-155] and butterflies upon your crockery. You never meet with quadrupeds going

[002-156] up and down walls; you must not have quadrupeds represented upon walls.

[002-157] You must use,’ said the gentleman, ‘for all these purposes, combinations

[002-158] and modifications (in primary colours) of mathematical figures which are

[002-159] susceptible of proof and demonstration. This is the new discovery. This

[002-160] is fact. This is taste.’

[002-161] The girl curtseyed, and sat down. She was very young, and she looked as

[002-162] if she were frightened by the matter-of-fact prospect the world afforded.

[002-163] ‘Now, if Mr. M’Choakumchild,’ said the gentleman, ‘will proceed to give

[002-164] his first lesson here, Mr. Gradgrind, I shall be happy, at your request,

[002-165] to observe his mode of procedure.’

[002-166] Mr. Gradgrind was much obliged. ‘Mr. M’Choakumchild, we only wait for

[002-167] you.’

[002-168] So, Mr. M’Choakumchild began in his best manner. He and some one hundred

[002-169] and forty other schoolmasters, had been lately turned at the same time,

[002-170] in the same factory, on the same principles, like so many pianoforte

[002-171] legs. He had been put through an immense variety of paces, and had

[002-172] answered volumes of head-breaking questions. Orthography, etymology,

[002-173] syntax, and prosody, biography, astronomy, geography, and general

[002-174] cosmography, the sciences of compound proportion, algebra, land-surveying

[002-175] and levelling, vocal music, and drawing from models, were all at the ends

[002-176] of his ten chilled fingers. He had worked his stony way into Her

[002-177] Majesty’s most Honourable Privy Council’s Schedule B, and had taken the

[002-178] bloom off the higher branches of mathematics and physical science,

[002-179] French, German, Latin, and Greek. He knew all about all the Water Sheds

[002-180] of all the world (whatever they are), and all the histories of all the

[002-181] peoples, and all the names of all the rivers and mountains, and all the

[002-182] productions, manners, and customs of all the countries, and all their

[002-183] boundaries and bearings on the two and thirty points of the compass. Ah,

[002-184] rather overdone, M’Choakumchild. If he had only learnt a little less,

[002-185] how infinitely better he might have taught much more!

[002-186] He went to work in this preparatory lesson, not unlike Morgiana in the

[002-187] Forty Thieves: looking into all the vessels ranged before him, one after

[002-188] another, to see what they contained. Say, good M’Choakumchild. When

[002-189] from thy boiling store, thou shalt fill each jar brim full by-and-by,

[002-190] dost thou think that thou wilt always kill outright the robber Fancy

[002-191] lurking within—or sometimes only maim him and distort him!
