[007-001] MRS. SPARSIT

[007-002] MR. BOUNDERBY being a bachelor, an elderly lady presided over his

[007-003] establishment, in consideration of a certain annual stipend. Mrs.

[007-004] [[mrs-sparsit|Sparsit]] was this lady’s name; and she was a prominent figure in

[007-005] attendance on [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s car, as it rolled along in triumph with the

[007-006] [[humility|Bully of humility]] inside.

[007-007] For, Mrs. Sparsit had not only seen different days, but was highly

[007-008] connected. She had a great aunt living in these very times called Lady

[007-009] Scadgers. Mr. Sparsit, deceased, of whom she was the relict, had been by

[007-010] the mother’s side what Mrs. Sparsit still called ‘a Powler.’ Strangers

[007-011] of limited information and dull apprehension were sometimes observed not

[007-012] to know what a Powler was, and even to appear uncertain whether it might

[007-013] be a business, or a political party, or a profession of faith. The

[007-014] better [[class]] of minds, however, did not need to be informed that the

[007-015] Powlers were an ancient stock, who could trace themselves so exceedingly

[007-016] far back that it was not surprising if they sometimes lost

[007-017] themselves—which they had rather frequently done, as respected

[007-018] horse-flesh, blind-hookey, Hebrew monetary transactions, and the

[007-019] Insolvent Debtors’ Court.

[007-020] The late Mr. Sparsit, being by the mother’s side a Powler, married this

[007-021] lady, being by the father’s side a Scadgers. [[lady-scadgers|Lady Scadgers]] (an immensely

[007-022] fat old woman, with an inordinate appetite for butcher’s meat, and a

[007-023] mysterious leg which had now refused to get out of bed for fourteen

[007-024] years) contrived the [[marriage]], at a period when Sparsit was just of age,

[007-025] and chiefly noticeable for a slender body, weakly supported on two long

[007-026] slim props, and surmounted by no [[reason|head]] worth mentioning. He inherited a

[007-027] fair fortune from his uncle, but owed it all before he came into it, and

[007-028] spent it twice over immediately afterwards. Thus, when he died, at

[007-029] twenty-four (the scene of his decease, Calais, and the cause, brandy), he

[007-030] did not leave his widow, from whom he had been separated soon after the

[007-031] honeymoon, in affluent circumstances. That bereaved lady, fifteen years

[007-032] older than he, fell presently at deadly feud with her only relative, Lady

[007-033] Scadgers; and, partly to spite her ladyship, and partly to maintain

[007-034] herself, went out at a salary. And here she was now, in her elderly

[007-035] days, with the Coriolanian style of nose and the dense black eyebrows

[007-036] which had captivated Sparsit, making Mr. Bounderby’s tea as he took his

[007-037] breakfast.

[007-038] If Bounderby had been a Conqueror, and Mrs. Sparsit a captive Princess

[007-039] whom he took about as a feature in his state-processions, he could not

[007-040] have made a greater flourish with her than he habitually did. Just as it

[007-041] belonged to his boastfulness to depreciate his own extraction, so it

[007-042] belonged to it to exalt Mrs. Sparsit’s. In the measure that he would not

[007-043] allow his own [[childhood|youth]] to have been attended by a single favourable

[007-044] circumstance, he brightened Mrs. Sparsit’s juvenile career with every

[007-045] possible advantage, and showered waggon-loads of early roses all over

[007-046] that lady’s path. ‘And yet, sir,’ he would say, ‘how does it turn out

[007-047] after all? Why here she is at a hundred a year (I give her a hundred,

[007-048] which she is pleased to term handsome), keeping the house of Josiah

[007-049] Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]]!’

[007-050] Nay, he made this foil of his so very widely known, that third parties

[007-051] took it up, and handled it on some occasions with considerable briskness.

[007-052] It was one of the most exasperating attributes of Bounderby, that he not

[007-053] only sang his own praises but stimulated other men to sing them. There

[007-054] was a moral infection of clap-trap in him. Strangers, modest enough

[007-055] elsewhere, started up at dinners in Coketown, and boasted, in quite a

[007-056] rampant way, of Bounderby. They made him out to be the Royal arms, the

[007-057] Union-Jack, Magna Charta, John Bull, Habeas Corpus, the Bill of Rights,

[007-058] An Englishman’s house is his castle, Church and State, and God save the

[007-059] Queen, all put together. And as often (and it was very often) as an

[007-060] orator of this kind brought into his peroration,

[007-061] ‘Princes and lords may flourish or may fade,

[007-062] A breath can make them, as a breath has made,’

[007-063] —it was, for certain, more or less understood among the company that he

[007-064] had heard of Mrs. Sparsit.

[007-065] ‘Mr. Bounderby,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘you are unusually slow, sir, with

[007-066] your breakfast this morning.’

[007-067] ‘Why, ma’am,’ he returned, ‘I am thinking about [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]]’s whim;’

[007-068] Tom [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], for a bluff independent manner of speaking—as if somebody

[007-069] were always endeavouring to bribe him with immense sums to say Thomas,

[007-070] and he wouldn’t; ‘Tom [[thomas-gradgrind|Gradgrind]]’s whim, ma’am, of bringing up the

[007-071] tumbling-girl.’

[007-072] ‘The girl is now waiting to know,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘whether she is to

[007-073] go straight to the school, or up to the Lodge.’

[007-074] ‘She must wait, ma’am,’ answered Bounderby, ‘till I know myself. We

[007-075] shall have Tom Gradgrind down here presently, I suppose. If he should

[007-076] wish her to remain here a day or two longer, of course she can, ma’am.’

[007-077] ‘Of course she can if you wish it, Mr. Bounderby.’

[007-078] ‘I told him I would give her a shake-down here, last night, in order that

[007-079] he might sleep on it before he decided to let her have any association

[007-080] with [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]].’

[007-081] ‘Indeed, Mr. Bounderby? Very thoughtful of you!’ Mrs. Sparsit’s

[007-082] Coriolanian nose underwent a slight expansion of the nostrils, and her

[007-083] black eyebrows contracted as she took a sip of tea.

[007-084] ‘It’s tolerably clear to _me_,’ said Bounderby, ‘that the little puss can

[007-085] get small good out of such companionship.’

[007-086] ‘Are you speaking of young Miss Gradgrind, Mr. Bounderby?’

[007-087] ‘Yes, ma’am, I’m speaking of Louisa.’

[007-088] ‘Your observation being limited to “little puss,”’ said Mrs. Sparsit,

[007-089] ‘and there being two little girls in question, I did not know which might

[007-090] be indicated by that expression.’

[007-091] ‘Louisa,’ repeated Mr. Bounderby. ‘Louisa, Louisa.’

[007-092] ‘You are quite another father to Louisa, sir.’ Mrs. Sparsit took a

[007-093] little more tea; and, as she bent her again contracted eyebrows over her

[007-094] steaming cup, rather looked as if her classical countenance were invoking

[007-095] the infernal gods.

[007-096] ‘If you had said I was another father to Tom—young Tom, I mean, not my

[007-097] friend Tom Gradgrind—you might have been nearer the mark. I am going to

[007-098] take young Tom into my office. Going to have him under my wing, ma’am.’

[007-099] ‘Indeed? Rather young for that, is he not, sir?’ Mrs. Sparsit’s ‘sir,’

[007-100] in addressing Mr. Bounderby, was a word of ceremony, rather exacting

[007-101] consideration for herself in the use, than honouring him.

[007-102] ‘I’m not going to take him at once; he is to finish his [[education|educational]]

[007-103] cramming before then,’ said Bounderby. ‘By the Lord Harry, he’ll have

[007-104] enough of it, first and last! He’d open his eyes, that boy would, if he

[007-105] knew how empty of learning _my_ young maw was, at his time of life.’

[007-106] Which, by the by, he probably did know, for he had heard of it often

[007-107] enough. ‘But it’s extraordinary the difficulty I have on scores of such

[007-108] subjects, in speaking to any one on equal terms. Here, for example, I

[007-109] have been speaking to you this morning about tumblers. Why, what do

[007-110] _you_ know about tumblers? At the time when, to have been a tumbler in

[007-111] the mud of the streets, would have been a godsend to me, a prize in the

[007-112] lottery to me, you were at the Italian Opera. You were coming out of the

[007-113] Italian Opera, ma’am, in white satin and jewels, a blaze of splendour,

[007-114] when I hadn’t a penny to buy a link to light you.’

[007-115] ‘I certainly, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, with a [[pride|dignity]] serenely

[007-116] mournful, ‘was familiar with the Italian Opera at a very early age.’

[007-117] ‘Egad, ma’am, so was I,’ said Bounderby, ‘—with the wrong side of it. A

[007-118] hard bed the pavement of its Arcade used to make, I assure you. People

[007-119] like you, ma’am, accustomed from infancy to lie on Down feathers, have no

[007-120] idea _how_ hard a paving-stone is, without trying it. No, no, it’s of no

[007-121] use my talking to _you_ about tumblers. I should speak of foreign

[007-122] dancers, and the West End of London, and May Fair, and lords and ladies

[007-123] and honourables.’

[007-124] ‘I trust, sir,’ rejoined Mrs. Sparsit, with decent resignation, ‘it is

[007-125] not necessary that you should do anything of that kind. I hope I have

[007-126] learnt how to accommodate myself to the changes of life. If I have

[007-127] acquired an interest in hearing of your instructive experiences, and can

[007-128] scarcely hear enough of them, I claim no merit for that, since I believe

[007-129] it is a general sentiment.’

[007-130] ‘Well, ma’am,’ said her patron, ‘perhaps some people may be pleased to

[007-131] say that they do like to hear, in his own unpolished way, what Josiah

[007-132] Bounderby, of Coketown, has gone through. But you must confess that you

[007-133] were born in the lap of luxury, yourself. Come, ma’am, you know you were

[007-134] born in the lap of luxury.’

[007-135] ‘I do not, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit with a shake of her head, ‘deny

[007-136] it.’

[007-137] Mr. Bounderby was obliged to get up from table, and stand with his back

[007-138] to the fire, looking at her; she was such an enhancement of his position.

[007-139] ‘And you were in crack society. Devilish high society,’ he said, warming

[007-140] his legs.

[007-141] ‘It is true, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, with an affectation of humility

[007-142] the very opposite of his, and therefore in no danger of jostling it.

[007-143] ‘You were in the tiptop fashion, and all the rest of it,’ said Mr.

[007-144] Bounderby.

[007-145] ‘Yes, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, with a kind of social widowhood upon

[007-146] her. ‘It is unquestionably true.’

[007-147] Mr. Bounderby, bending himself at the knees, literally embraced his legs

[007-148] in his great satisfaction and laughed aloud. Mr. and Miss Gradgrind

[007-149] being then announced, he received the former with a shake of the hand,

[007-150] and the latter with a kiss.

[007-151] ‘Can [[sissy-jupe|Jupe]] be sent here, Bounderby?’ asked Mr. Gradgrind.

[007-152] Certainly. So Jupe was sent there. On coming in, she curtseyed to Mr.

[007-153] Bounderby, and to his friend Tom Gradgrind, and also to Louisa; but in

[007-154] her confusion unluckily omitted Mrs. Sparsit. Observing this, the

[007-155] blustrous Bounderby had the following remarks to make:

[007-156] ‘Now, I tell you what, my girl. The name of that lady by the teapot, is

[007-157] Mrs. Sparsit. That lady acts as mistress of this house, and she is a

[007-158] highly connected lady. Consequently, if ever you come again into any

[007-159] room in this house, you will make a short stay in it if you don’t behave

[007-160] towards that lady in your most respectful manner. Now, I don’t care a

[007-161] button what you do to _me_, because I don’t affect to be anybody. So far

[007-162] from having high connections I have no connections at all, and I come of

[007-163] the scum of the earth. But towards that lady, I do care what you do; and

[007-164] you shall do what is deferential and respectful, or you shall not come

[007-165] here.’

[007-166] ‘I hope, Bounderby,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, in a conciliatory voice, ‘that

[007-167] this was merely an oversight.’

[007-168] ‘My friend Tom Gradgrind suggests, Mrs. Sparsit,’ said Bounderby, ‘that

[007-169] this was merely an oversight. Very likely. However, as you are aware,

[007-170] ma’am, I don’t allow of even oversights towards you.’

[007-171] ‘You are very good indeed, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, shaking her head

[007-172] with her State humility. ‘It is not worth speaking of.’

[007-173] Sissy, who all this time had been faintly excusing herself with tears in

[007-174] her eyes, was now waved over by the master of the house to Mr. Gradgrind.

[007-175] She stood looking intently at him, and Louisa stood coldly by, with her

[007-176] eyes upon the ground, while he proceeded thus:

[007-177] ‘Jupe, I have made up my mind to take you into my house; and, when you

[007-178] are not in attendance at the school, to employ you about Mrs. Gradgrind,

[007-179] who is rather an invalid. I have explained to Miss Louisa—this is Miss

[007-180] Louisa—the miserable but [[nature|natural]] end of your late career; and you are to

[007-181] expressly understand that the whole of that subject is past, and is not

[007-182] to be referred to any more. From this time you begin your history. You

[007-183] are, at present, ignorant, I know.’

[007-184] ‘Yes, sir, very,’ she answered, curtseying.

[007-185] ‘I shall have the satisfaction of causing you to be strictly educated;

[007-186] and you will be a living proof to all who come into communication with

[007-187] you, of the advantages of the training you will receive. You will be

[007-188] reclaimed and formed. You have been in the habit now of reading to your

[007-189] father, and those people I found you among, I dare say?’ said Mr.

[007-190] Gradgrind, beckoning her nearer to him before he said so, and dropping

[007-191] his voice.

[007-192] ‘Only to father and Merrylegs, sir. At least I mean to father, when

[007-193] Merrylegs was always there.’

[007-194] ‘Never mind Merrylegs, Jupe,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, with a passing frown.

[007-195] ‘I don’t ask about him. I understand you to have been in the habit of

[007-196] reading to your father?’

[007-197] ‘O, yes, sir, thousands of times. They were the happiest—O, of all the

[007-198] happy times we had together, sir!’

[007-199] It was only now when her sorrow broke out, that Louisa looked at her.

[007-200] ‘And what,’ asked Mr. Gradgrind, in a still lower voice, ‘did you read to

[007-201] your father, Jupe?’

[007-202] ‘[[About]] the Fairies, sir, and the Dwarf, and the Hunchback, and the

[007-203] Genies,’ she sobbed out; ‘and about—’

[007-204] ‘Hush!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘that is enough. Never breathe a word of

[007-205] such destructive nonsense any more. Bounderby, this is a case for rigid

[007-206] training, and I shall observe it with interest.’

[007-207] ‘Well,’ returned Mr. Bounderby, ‘I have given you my opinion already, and

[007-208] I shouldn’t do as you do. But, very well, very well. Since you are bent

[007-209] upon it, _very_ well!’

[007-210] So, Mr. Gradgrind and his daughter took Cecilia Jupe off with them to

[007-211] [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]], and on the way Louisa never spoke one word, good or bad.

[007-212] And Mr. Bounderby went about his daily pursuits. And Mrs. Sparsit got

[007-213] behind her eyebrows and meditated in the gloom of that retreat, all the

[007-214] evening.
