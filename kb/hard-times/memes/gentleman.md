# Gentleman

In *Hard Times*, the word "gentleman" is one of Dickens's sharpest instruments of irony. Applied to Gradgrind in the novel's opening chapters, used to describe Harthouse at his entrance, and conspicuously withheld from Sleary and Stephen Blackpool — the two characters who most fully embody its moral substance — the term becomes a site of sustained satirical pressure. The novel asks what a gentleman is worth when the word serves equally to name a "man of realities" who murders children's imaginations (PN: 002-083) and a bored cynic "putting no more faith in anything than Lucifer" (PN: 019-008). The answer, Dickens implies, is that the word has been hollowed out by the very social order it is supposed to dignify.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of the gentleman trope operates through a double movement. On the one hand, he uses the word straight — Gradgrind is called "this gentleman" (PN: 002-097), "the gentleman" (PN: 002-108), "the gentleman" (PN: 002-122), and "the gentleman" (PN: 002-133) repeatedly across Chapter 2's schoolroom scene, without any visible scare quotes. The narrator does not wink; the irony is structural, not tonal. The reader who has been attending to the man's speech — his "Fact, fact, fact!" (PN: 002-144), his insistence that children "mustn't fancy" (PN: 002-140), his plan for "commissioners of fact" to "force the people to be a people of fact" — understands that Gradgrind's claim to the title "gentleman" is the novel's indictment of a culture that confers social esteem without moral scrutiny.

On the other hand, the novel withholds gentlemanly status from its most genuinely gentle characters. Sleary — coarse, lisping, physically ungainly, financially desperate — is the novel's true gentleman: loyal, kind, capable of sacrifice. Stephen Blackpool — a factory hand with no pretensions to gentility — possesses the integrity, the refusal to harm others for his own advantage, and the stubborn dignity that the word "gentleman" once promised to name. Bounderby, for his part, explicitly rejects the label: "You're a gentleman, and I don't pretend to be one. I am Josiah Bounderby of Coketown" (PN: 017-177). The disclaimer is both truthful and self-serving: Bounderby knows he is no gentleman, but he uses the admission to claim a more authentic — and more dangerous — form of authority.

### Key Passages

> "The third gentleman now stepped forth. A mighty man at cutting and drying, he was; a government officer; in his way (and in most other people's too), a professed pugilist; always in training, always with a system to force down the general throat like a bolus, always to be heard of at the bar of his little Public-office, ready to fight all England." (PN: 002-083)

This is the novel's first presentation of Gradgrind as "gentleman," and the portrait is devastating. He is not introduced by name or by moral quality but by function: he is a "government officer," a professional enforcer. The metaphors are violent — "cutting and drying," "pugilist," "force down the general throat." To call such a man "gentleman" is to ask what the word has come to mean.

> "he was a thorough gentleman, made to the model of the time; weary of everything, and putting no more faith in anything than Lucifer." (PN: 019-008)

James Harthouse is introduced with this sentence, and it distills the gentleman-as-cynic in a single devastating clause. He is "made to the model of the time" — a product, not a character. His weariness is the logical endpoint of a gentility that has no moral content: polish without principle, manners without meaning.

> "You must use," said the gentleman, "for all these purposes, combinations and modifications (in primary colours) of mathematical figures which are susceptible of proof and demonstration. This is the new discovery. This is fact. This is taste." (PN: 002-157)

The gentleman prescribes aesthetic principles as if they were geometrical proofs. The passage marries the two central targets of Dickens's satire: the word "gentleman" and the word "fact." Together they produce "taste" — a category that is supposed to involve feeling, discrimination, and pleasure — reduced to mechanical rule. The gentleman, in this world, is the man who knows what facts to apply.

### The Gentleman and the Fact-System

The Novel's deepest charge against the gentleman ideal — as embodied by Gradgrind — is that it has become an instrument of dehumanisation. When Gradgrind says "In this life, we want nothing but Facts, sir; nothing but Facts!" (PN: 002-146), he speaks from within the social authority of the gentleman. His status licenses his philosophy; his gentility makes his cruelty respectable. The "gentleman" who murders imagination (Chapter 2 is titled "Murdering the Innocents") is not a villain in a melodrama but a pillar of the community — and that, Dickens argues, is precisely the problem.

The contrast with Harthouse sharpens the diagnosis. If Gradgrind is the gentleman as sincere ideologue, Harthouse is the gentleman as exhausted performer — the man for whom the forms of gentility remain while their substance has evaporated. His "weariness of everything" is the novel's verdict on a social ideal that has become a costume.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period was the golden age of the gentleman ideal, and *Hard Times* was written at its peak. The public schools (Rugby under Thomas Arnold, from 1828) had begun to articulate a vision of the gentleman as a moral category — the Christian gentleman, whose authority rested on character rather than birth. John Henry Newman's *The Idea of a University* (1852) would codify the gentleman as the product of liberal education. Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859) would democratise the ideal: any man, through industry and integrity, could be a gentleman. The concept was everywhere in mid-Victorian culture, and it was always freighted with moral aspiration.

Dickens's intervention is to show that the ideal, in practice, has been captured by the very forces it was supposed to resist. Gradgrind's gentlemanliness is not a check on his philosophy of Facts but its vehicle. Harthouse's gentlemanliness is not a discipline of character but a mask for his cynicism. The novel's counter-gentlemen — Sleary and Stephen — are excluded from the category by class, by physical appearance, by the very polish that defines gentility in the Coketown sense. The novel thus performs a radical inversion: it suggests that the reform of the gentleman ideal, so central to Victorian moral discourse, has failed, and that genuine human worth must now be sought among those whom the category excludes.

Thomas Carlyle's critique of "gigmanity" — respectability as the religion of the middle class — is a clear influence. Carlyle had argued in *Past and Present* (1843) that the age had substituted the worship of appearances for the worship of God. Dickens's treatment of the gentleman in *Hard Times* is a fictional enactment of this argument: the novel shows a world in which the word "gentleman" has become a term of social description rather than moral evaluation, and the results are catastrophic.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: The gentleman as enforcer of the Fact-system. Gradgrind is never cruel in his own person; he is gentle, patient, and sincere. But his gentlemanly sincerity is precisely what makes his philosophy dangerous: it confers moral legitimacy on a programme that systematically destroys the imaginative life of children. His arc — from certainty to doubt, from the "facts" of Book I to the "what shall I do?" of Book III — is the gentleman's self-correction, and it implies that the ideal might be redeemable if it can learn to feel.

- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: The gentleman as exhausted performer. Harthouse has all the externals of gentility — good looks, good voice, good breeding — and none of the internals. He is "made to the model of the time" (PN: 019-008), a polished surface with nothing behind it. His pursuit of Louisa is the novel's demonstration of what the gentlemanly ideal becomes when it is emptied of moral content: a technique for seduction, a social skill with no social purpose.

- **[[Sleary]]**: The true gentleman in disguise. Sleary is everything a gentleman is not supposed to be: fat, lisping, alcoholic, associated with the disreputable world of the circus. But he is also kind, loyal, courageous, and capable of genuine self-sacrifice. When he shelters Tom Gradgrind at risk to himself, he performs the act of moral generosity that the word "gentleman" was supposed to name. Dickens's point is not that Sleary is a gentleman despite his appearance but that his appearance is irrelevant to his moral worth — and that a society that cannot recognise this has lost the capacity to recognise moral worth at all.

- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: The working-class gentleman of integrity. Stephen makes no claim to gentility; he would be the last person to call himself a gentleman. But his stubborn refusal to harm others — his refusal to join the union on principle, his refusal to betray Rachael, his refusal to implicate Tom even when Tom has framed him — embodies the moral substance that the gentlemanly ideal was supposed to cultivate. Stephen is the novel's proof that integrity does not require polish, and that the word "gentleman" has become a class marker rather than a moral judgement.

## Contrasted With

- **[[fancy]]**: The gentleman Gradgrind's great enemy. "You mustn't fancy," cries the gentleman (PN: 002-140), and the imperative is revealing: fancy is what the Fact-system cannot control, and the gentleman's role is to control. The opposition between the gentleman and fancy is the opposition between social authority and imaginative freedom — and the novel sides, unmistakably, with the second term.

- **[[facts]]**: The gentleman's intellectual armoury. The gentleman in *Hard Times* is defined by his command of facts — by his capacity to classify, measure, and calculate. But the novel shows that facts without feeling are instruments of oppression, and that a gentleman who knows only facts knows nothing worth knowing about human life.

- **[[humanity]]**: What the true gentleman should embody but what the Fact-system destroys. The novel's word for the quality that Gradgrind lacks and that Sleary possesses is not "gentlemanliness" but something more elemental: kindness, fellow-feeling, the willingness to help a fellow creature in distress. The fact that this quality is associated with a lisping circus-master rather than with the gentlemen of Coketown is the novel's most radical argument.

## Critical Perspectives

> George Orwell, in his 1940 essay on Dickens, observed that Dickens's radicalism was always moral rather than political: "He has no constructive suggestions, not even a clear grasp of the nature of the society he is attacking, only an emotional perception that something is wrong." This reading applies with particular force to the gentleman theme: Dickens exposes the hollowness of the ideal without proposing an alternative — unless that alternative is simply the goodness of ordinary people, which is precisely what Sleary and Stephen represent.

> Robin Gilmour, in *The Idea of the Gentleman in the Victorian Novel* (1981), traced the gentleman ideal through the major novelists of the period and argued that Dickens's treatment in *Hard Times* was part of a broader Victorian anxiety about the moral content of gentility. Gilmour reads Harthouse as a figure of the "dandy-gentleman" — the gentleman reduced to style — and Gradgrind as the "bourgeois-gentleman" — the gentleman reduced to function. Both reductions, Gilmour argues, reflect the pressure that industrial capitalism placed on an ideal originally rooted in landed leisure.
