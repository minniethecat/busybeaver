[010-001] STEPHEN BLACKPOOL

[010-002] I ENTERTAIN a weak idea that the English people are as hard-worked as any

[010-003] people upon whom the sun shines. I acknowledge to this ridiculous

[010-004] idiosyncrasy, as a [[reason]] why I would give them a little more play.

[010-005] In the hardest working part of [[coketown|Coketown]]; in the innermost fortifications

[010-006] of that ugly citadel, where [[nature|Nature]] was as strongly bricked out as killing

[010-007] airs and gases were bricked in; at the [[heart]] of the labyrinth of narrow

[010-008] courts upon courts, and close streets upon streets, which had come into

[010-009] existence piecemeal, every piece in a violent hurry for some one man’s

[010-010] purpose, and the whole an unnatural [[family]], shouldering, and trampling,

[010-011] and pressing one another to death; in the last close nook of this great

[010-012] exhausted receiver, where the chimneys, for want of air to make a

[010-013] draught, were built in an immense variety of stunted and crooked shapes,

[010-014] as though every house put out a sign of the kind of people who might be

[010-015] expected to be born in it; among the multitude of Coketown, generically

[010-016] called ‘the Hands,’—a race who would have found more favour with some

[010-017] people, if Providence had seen fit to make them only hands, or, like the

[010-018] lower creatures of the seashore, only hands and stomachs—lived a certain

[010-019] [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]], forty years of age.

[010-020] Stephen looked older, but he had had a hard life. It is said that every

[010-021] life has its roses and thorns; there seemed, however, to have been a

[010-022] misadventure or mistake in Stephen’s case, whereby somebody else had

[010-023] become possessed of his roses, and he had become possessed of the same

[010-024] somebody else’s thorns in addition to his own. He had known, to use his

[010-025] words, a peck of trouble. He was usually called Old Stephen, in a kind

[010-026] of rough homage to the [[facts|fact]].

[010-027] A rather stooping man, with a knitted brow, a pondering expression of

[010-028] face, and a hard-looking head sufficiently capacious, on which his

[010-029] iron-grey hair lay long and thin, Old Stephen might have passed for a

[010-030] particularly intelligent man in his condition. Yet he was not. He took

[010-031] no place among those remarkable ‘Hands,’ who, piecing together their

[010-032] broken intervals of leisure through many years, had mastered difficult

[010-033] sciences, and acquired a knowledge of most unlikely things. He held no

[010-034] station among the Hands who could make speeches and carry on debates.

[010-035] Thousands of his compeers could talk much better than he, at any time.

[010-036] He was a good power-loom weaver, and a man of perfect integrity. What

[010-037] more he was, or what else he had in him, if anything, let him [[performance|show]] for

[010-038] himself.

[010-039] The lights in the great factories, which looked, when they were

[010-040] illuminated, like Fairy palaces—or the travellers by express-train said

[010-041] so—were all extinguished; and the bells had rung for knocking off for the

[010-042] night, and had ceased again; and the Hands, men and women, boy and girl,

[010-043] were clattering home. Old Stephen was standing in the street, with the

[010-044] old sensation upon him which the stoppage of the machinery always

[010-045] produced—the sensation of its having worked and stopped in his own head.

[010-046] ‘Yet I don’t see [[rachael|Rachael]], still!’ said he.

[010-047] It was a wet night, and many groups of young women passed him, with their

[010-048] shawls drawn over their bare heads and held close under their chins to

[010-049] keep the rain out. He knew Rachael well, for a glance at any one of

[010-050] these groups was sufficient to show him that she was not there. At last,

[010-051] there were no more to come; and then he turned away, saying in a tone of

[010-052] disappointment, ‘Why, then, ha’ missed her!’

[010-053] But, he had not gone the length of three streets, when he saw another of

[010-054] the shawled figures in advance of him, at which he looked so keenly that

[010-055] perhaps its mere shadow indistinctly reflected on the wet pavement—if he

[010-056] could have seen it without the figure itself moving along from lamp to

[010-057] lamp, brightening and fading as it went—would have been enough to tell

[010-058] him who was there. Making his pace at once much quicker and much softer,

[010-059] he darted on until he was very near this figure, then fell into his

[010-060] former walk, and called ‘Rachael!’

[010-061] She turned, being then in the brightness of a lamp; and raising her hood

[010-062] a little, showed a quiet oval face, dark and rather delicate, irradiated

[010-063] by a pair of very gentle eyes, and further set off by the perfect order

[010-064] of her shining black hair. It was not a face in its first bloom; she was

[010-065] a woman five and thirty years of age.

[010-066] ‘Ah, lad! ’Tis thou?’ When she had said this, with a smile which would

[010-067] have been quite expressed, though nothing of her had been seen but her

[010-068] pleasant eyes, she replaced her hood again, and they went on together.

[010-069] ‘I thought thou wast ahind me, Rachael?’

[010-070] ‘No.’

[010-071] ‘Early t’night, lass?’

[010-072] ‘’Times I’m a little early, Stephen! ’times a little late. I’m never to

[010-073] be counted on, going home.’

[010-074] ‘Nor going t’other way, neither, ’t seems to me, Rachael?’

[010-075] ‘No, Stephen.’

[010-076] He looked at her with some disappointment in his face, but with a

[010-077] respectful and patient conviction that she must be right in whatever she

[010-078] did. The expression was not lost upon her; she laid her hand lightly on

[010-079] his arm a moment as if to thank him for it.

[010-080] ‘We are such true friends, lad, and such old friends, and getting to be

[010-081] such old folk, now.’

[010-082] ‘No, Rachael, thou’rt as young as ever thou wast.’

[010-083] ‘One of us would be puzzled how to get old, Stephen, without ’t other

[010-084] getting so too, both being alive,’ she answered, laughing; ‘but, anyways,

[010-085] we’re such old friends, and t’ hide a word of honest [[truth]] fro’ one

[010-086] another would be a sin and a pity. ’Tis better not to walk too much

[010-087] together. ’Times, yes! ’Twould be hard, indeed, if ’twas not to be at

[010-088] all,’ she said, with a cheerfulness she sought to communicate to him.

[010-089] ‘’Tis hard, anyways, Rachael.’

[010-090] ‘Try to think not; and ’twill seem better.’

[010-091] ‘I’ve tried a long time, and ’ta’nt got better. But thou’rt right; ’t

[010-092] might mak fok talk, even of thee. Thou hast been that to me, Rachael,

[010-093] through so many year: thou hast done me so much good, and heartened of me

[010-094] in that cheering way, that thy word is a law to me. Ah, lass, and a

[010-095] bright good law! Better than some real ones.’

[010-096] ‘Never fret about them, Stephen,’ she answered quickly, and not without

[010-097] an anxious glance at his face. ‘Let the laws be.’

[010-098] ‘Yes,’ he said, with a slow nod or two. ‘Let ’em be. Let everything be.

[010-099] Let all sorts alone. ’Tis a muddle, and that’s aw.’

[010-100] ‘Always a muddle?’ said Rachael, with another gentle touch upon his arm,

[010-101] as if to recall him out of the thoughtfulness, in which he was biting the

[010-102] long ends of his loose neckerchief as he walked along. The touch had its

[010-103] instantaneous effect. He let them fall, turned a smiling face upon her,

[010-104] and said, as he broke into a good-humoured laugh, ‘Ay, Rachael, lass,

[010-105] awlus a muddle. That’s where I stick. I come to the muddle many times

[010-106] and agen, and I never get beyond it.’

[010-107] They had walked some distance, and were near their own homes. The

[010-108] woman’s was the first reached. It was in one of the many small streets

[010-109] for which the favourite undertaker (who turned a handsome sum out of the

[010-110] one poor ghastly pomp of the neighbourhood) kept a black ladder, in order

[010-111] that those who had done their daily groping up and down the narrow stairs

[010-112] might slide out of this working world by the windows. She stopped at the

[010-113] corner, and putting her hand in his, wished him good night.

[010-114] ‘Good night, [[love|dear]] lass; good night!’

[010-115] She went, with her neat figure and her sober womanly step, down the dark

[010-116] street, and he stood looking after her until she turned into one of the

[010-117] small houses. There was not a flutter of her coarse shawl, perhaps, but

[010-118] had its interest in this man’s eyes; not a tone of her voice but had its

[010-119] echo in his innermost heart.

[010-120] When she was lost to his view, he pursued his homeward way, glancing up

[010-121] sometimes at the sky, where the clouds were sailing fast and wildly.

[010-122] But, they were broken now, and the rain had ceased, and the moon

[010-123] shone,—looking down the high chimneys of Coketown on the deep furnaces

[010-124] below, and casting Titanic shadows of the steam-engines at rest, upon the

[010-125] walls where they were lodged. The man seemed to have brightened with the

[010-126] night, as he went on.

[010-127] His home, in such another street as the first, saving that it was

[010-128] narrower, was over a little shop. How it came to pass that any people

[010-129] found it worth their while to sell or buy the wretched little toys, mixed

[010-130] up in its window with cheap newspapers and pork (there was a leg to be

[010-131] raffled for to-morrow-night), matters not here. He took his end of

[010-132] candle from a shelf, lighted it at another end of candle on the counter,

[010-133] without disturbing the mistress of the shop who was asleep in her little

[010-134] room, and went upstairs into his lodging.

[010-135] It was a room, not unacquainted with the black ladder under various

[010-136] tenants; but as neat, at present, as such a room could be. A few books

[010-137] and writings were on an old bureau in a corner, the furniture was decent

[010-138] and sufficient, and, though the atmosphere was tainted, the room was

[010-139] clean.

[010-140] Going to the hearth to set the candle down upon a round three-legged

[010-141] table standing there, he stumbled against something. As he recoiled,

[010-142] looking down at it, it raised itself up into the form of a woman in a

[010-143] sitting attitude.

[010-144] ‘Heaven’s mercy, woman!’ he cried, falling farther off from the figure.

[010-145] ‘Hast thou come back again!’

[010-146] Such a woman! A disabled, drunken creature, barely able to preserve her

[010-147] sitting posture by steadying herself with one begrimed hand on the floor,

[010-148] while the other was so purposeless in trying to push away her tangled

[010-149] hair from her face, that it only blinded her the more with the dirt upon

[010-150] it. A creature so foul to look at, in her tatters, stains and splashes,

[010-151] but so much fouler than that in her moral infamy, that it was a [[shame|shameful]]

[010-152] thing even to see her.

[010-153] After an impatient oath or two, and some stupid clawing of herself with

[010-154] the hand not necessary to her support, she got her hair away from her

[010-155] eyes sufficiently to obtain a sight of him. Then she sat swaying her

[010-156] body to and fro, and making gestures with her unnerved arm, which seemed

[010-157] intended as the accompaniment to a fit of laughter, though her face was

[010-158] stolid and drowsy.

[010-159] ‘Eigh, lad? What, yo’r there?’ Some hoarse sounds meant for this, came

[010-160] mockingly out of her at last; and her head dropped forward on her breast.

[010-161] ‘Back agen?’ she screeched, after some minutes, as if he had that moment

[010-162] said it. ‘Yes! And back agen. Back agen ever and ever so often. Back?

[010-163] Yes, back. Why not?’

[010-164] Roused by the unmeaning violence with which she cried it out, she

[010-165] scrambled up, and stood supporting herself with her shoulders against the

[010-166] wall; dangling in one hand by the string, a dunghill-fragment of a

[010-167] bonnet, and trying to look scornfully at him.

[010-168] ‘I’ll sell thee off again, and I’ll sell thee off again, and I’ll sell

[010-169] thee off a score of times!’ she cried, with something between a furious

[010-170] menace and an effort at a defiant dance. ‘Come awa’ from th’ bed!’ He

[010-171] was sitting on the side of it, with his face hidden in his hands. ‘Come

[010-172] awa! from ’t. ’Tis mine, and I’ve a right to t’!’

[010-173] As she staggered to it, he avoided her with a shudder, and passed—his

[010-174] face still hidden—to the opposite end of the room. She threw herself

[010-175] upon the bed heavily, and soon was snoring hard. He sunk into a chair,

[010-176] and moved but once all that night. It was to throw a covering over her;

[010-177] as if his hands were not enough to hide her, even in the darkness.
