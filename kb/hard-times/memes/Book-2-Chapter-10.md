[026-001] MRS. SPARSIT’S STAIRCASE

[026-002] MRS. SPARSIT’S nerves being slow to recover their tone, the worthy woman

[026-003] made a stay of some weeks in duration at [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s retreat, where,

[026-004] notwithstanding her anchorite turn of mind based upon her becoming

[026-005] consciousness of her altered station, she resigned herself with noble

[026-006] fortitude to lodging, as one may say, in clover, and feeding on the fat

[026-007] of the land. During the whole term of this recess from the guardianship

[026-008] of the Bank, [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] was a pattern of consistency; continuing to

[026-009] take such pity on Mr. Bounderby to his face, as is rarely taken on man,

[026-010] and to call his portrait a Noodle to _its_ face, with the greatest

[026-011] acrimony and contempt.

[026-012] Mr. Bounderby, having got it into his explosive composition that Mrs.

[026-013] Sparsit was a highly superior woman to perceive that he had that general

[026-014] cross upon him in his deserts (for he had not yet settled what it was),

[026-015] and further that [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] would have objected to her as a frequent visitor

[026-016] if it had comported with his greatness that she should object to anything

[026-017] he chose to do, resolved not to lose sight of Mrs. Sparsit easily. So

[026-018] when her nerves were strung up to the pitch of again consuming

[026-019] sweetbreads in solitude, he said to her at the dinner-table, on the day

[026-020] before her departure, ‘I tell you what, ma’am; you shall come down here

[026-021] of a Saturday, while the fine weather lasts, and stay till Monday.’ To

[026-022] which Mrs. Sparsit returned, in effect, though not of the Mahomedan

[026-023] persuasion: ‘To hear is to obey.’

[026-024] Now, Mrs. Sparsit was not a poetical woman; but she took an idea in the

[026-025] [[nature]] of an allegorical [[fancy]], into her [[reason|head]]. Much watching of Louisa,

[026-026] and much consequent observation of her impenetrable demeanour, which

[026-027] keenly whetted and sharpened Mrs. Sparsit’s edge, must have given her as

[026-028] it were a lift, in the way of inspiration. She erected in her mind a

[026-029] mighty Staircase, with a dark pit of [[shame]] and ruin at the bottom; and

[026-030] down those stairs, from day to day and hour to hour, she saw Louisa

[026-031] coming.

[026-032] It became the business of Mrs. Sparsit’s life, to look up at her

[026-033] staircase, and to watch Louisa coming down. Sometimes slowly, sometimes

[026-034] quickly, sometimes several steps at one bout, sometimes stopping, never

[026-035] turning back. If she had once turned back, it might have been the death

[026-036] of Mrs. Sparsit in spleen and grief.

[026-037] She had been descending steadily, to the day, and on the day, when Mr.

[026-038] Bounderby issued the weekly invitation recorded above. Mrs. Sparsit was

[026-039] in good spirits, and inclined to be conversational.

[026-040] ‘And pray, sir,’ said she, ‘if I may venture to ask a question

[026-041] appertaining to any subject on which you [[performance|show]] reserve—which is indeed

[026-042] hardy in me, for I well know you have a reason for everything you do—have

[026-043] you received intelligence respecting the robbery?’

[026-044] ‘Why, ma’am, no; not yet. Under the circumstances, I didn’t expect it

[026-045] yet. Rome wasn’t built in a day, ma’am.’

[026-046] ‘Very true, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, shaking her head.

[026-047] ‘Nor yet in a week, ma’am.’

[026-048] ‘No, indeed, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, with a gentle melancholy upon

[026-049] her.

[026-050] ‘In a similar manner, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘I can wait, you know. If

[026-051] Romulus and Remus could wait, Josiah Bounderby can wait. They were

[026-052] better off in their [[childhood|youth]] than I was, however. They had a she-wolf for a

[026-053] nurse; I had only a she-wolf for a grandmother. She didn’t give any

[026-054] milk, ma’am; she gave bruises. She was a regular Alderney at that.’

[026-055] ‘Ah!’ Mrs. Sparsit sighed and shuddered.

[026-056] ‘No, ma’am,’ continued Bounderby, ‘I have not heard anything more about

[026-057] it. It’s in hand, though; and [[tom-gradgrind|young Tom]], who rather sticks to business

[026-058] at present—something new for him; he hadn’t the [[education|schooling]] _I_ had—is

[026-059] helping. My injunction is, Keep it quiet, and let it seem to blow over.

[026-060] Do what you like under the rose, but don’t give a sign of what you’re

[026-061] about; or half a hundred of ’em will combine together and get this fellow

[026-062] who has bolted, out of reach for good. Keep it quiet, and the thieves

[026-063] will grow in confidence by little and little, and we shall have ’em.’

[026-064] ‘Very sagacious indeed, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit. ‘Very interesting. The

[026-065] old woman you mentioned, sir—’

[026-066] ‘The old woman I mentioned, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, cutting the matter

[026-067] short, as it was nothing to boast about, ‘is not laid hold of; but, she

[026-068] may take her oath she will be, if that is any satisfaction to her

[026-069] villainous old mind. In the mean time, ma’am, I am of opinion, if you

[026-070] ask me my opinion, that the less she is talked about, the better.’

[026-071] The same evening, Mrs. Sparsit, in her chamber window, resting from her

[026-072] packing operations, looked towards her great staircase and saw Louisa

[026-073] still descending.

[026-074] She sat by Mr. [[james-harthouse|Harthouse]], in an alcove in the garden, talking very low;

[026-075] he stood leaning over her, as they whispered together, and his face

[026-076] almost touched her hair. ‘If not quite!’ said Mrs. Sparsit, straining

[026-077] her hawk’s eyes to the utmost. Mrs. Sparsit was too distant to hear a

[026-078] word of their discourse, or even to know that they were speaking softly,

[026-079] otherwise than from the expression of their figures; but what they said

[026-080] was this:

[026-081] ‘You recollect the man, Mr. Harthouse?’

[026-082] ‘Oh, perfectly!’

[026-083] ‘His face, and his manner, and what he said?’

[026-084] ‘Perfectly. And an infinitely dreary person he appeared to me to be.

[026-085] Lengthy and prosy in the extreme. It was knowing to hold forth, in the

[026-086] humble-virtue school of eloquence; but, I assure you I thought at the

[026-087] time, “My good fellow, you are over-doing this!”’

[026-088] ‘It has been very difficult to me to think ill of that man.’

[026-089] ‘My [[love|dear]] Louisa—as Tom says.’ Which he never did say. ‘You know no good

[026-090] of the fellow?’

[026-091] ‘No, certainly.’

[026-092] ‘Nor of any other such person?’

[026-093] ‘How can I,’ she returned, with more of her first manner on her than he

[026-094] had lately seen, ‘when I know nothing of them, men or women?’

[026-095] ‘My dear Louisa, then consent to receive the submissive representation of

[026-096] your devoted friend, who knows something of several varieties of his

[026-097] excellent fellow-creatures—for excellent they are, I am quite ready to

[026-098] believe, in spite of such little foibles as always helping themselves to

[026-099] what they can get hold of. This fellow talks. Well; every fellow talks.

[026-100] He professes morality. Well; all sorts of humbugs profess morality.

[026-101] From the House of Commons to the House of Correction, there is a general

[026-102] profession of morality, except among our people; it really is that

[026-103] exception which makes our people quite reviving. You saw and heard the

[026-104] case. Here was one of the fluffy [[class|classes]] pulled up extremely short by my

[026-105] esteemed friend Mr. Bounderby—who, as we know, is not possessed of that

[026-106] delicacy which would soften so tight a hand. The member of the fluffy

[026-107] classes was injured, exasperated, left the house grumbling, met somebody

[026-108] who proposed to him to go in for some share in this Bank business, went

[026-109] in, put something in his pocket which had nothing in it before, and

[026-110] relieved his mind extremely. Really he would have been an uncommon,

[026-111] instead of a common, fellow, if he had not availed himself of such an

[026-112] opportunity. Or he may have originated it altogether, if he had the

[026-113] cleverness.’

[026-114] ‘I almost feel as though it must be bad in me,’ returned Louisa, after

[026-115] sitting thoughtful awhile, ‘to be so ready to agree with you, and to be

[026-116] so lightened in my [[heart]] by what you say.’

[026-117] ‘I only say what is reasonable; nothing worse. I have talked it over

[026-118] with my friend Tom more than once—of course I remain on terms of perfect

[026-119] confidence with Tom—and he is quite of my opinion, and I am quite of his.

[026-120] Will you walk?’

[026-121] They strolled away, among the lanes beginning to be indistinct in the

[026-122] twilight—she leaning on his arm—and she little thought how she was going

[026-123] down, down, down, Mrs. Sparsit’s staircase.

[026-124] Night and day, Mrs. Sparsit kept it standing. When Louisa had arrived at

[026-125] the bottom and disappeared in the gulf, it might fall in upon her if it

[026-126] would; but, until then, there it was to be, a Building, before Mrs.

[026-127] Sparsit’s eyes. And there Louisa always was, upon it.

[026-128] And always gliding down, down, down!

[026-129] Mrs. Sparsit saw James Harthouse come and go; she heard of him here and

[026-130] there; she saw the changes of the face he had studied; she, too, remarked

[026-131] to a nicety how and when it clouded, how and when it cleared; she kept

[026-132] her black eyes wide open, with no touch of pity, with no touch of

[026-133] compunction, all absorbed in interest. In the interest of seeing her,

[026-134] ever drawing, with no hand to stay her, nearer and nearer to the bottom

[026-135] of this new Giant’s Staircase.

[026-136] With all her deference for Mr. Bounderby as contradistinguished from his

[026-137] portrait, Mrs. Sparsit had not the smallest intention of interrupting the

[026-138] descent. Eager to see it accomplished, and yet patient, she waited for

[026-139] the last fall, as for the ripeness and fulness of the harvest of her

[026-140] hopes. Hushed in expectancy, she kept her wary gaze upon the stairs; and

[026-141] seldom so much as darkly shook her right mitten (with her fist in it), at

[026-142] the figure coming down.
