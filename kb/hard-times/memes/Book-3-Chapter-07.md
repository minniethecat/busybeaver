[035-001] WHELP-HUNTING

[035-002] BEFORE the ring formed round the [[old-hell-shaft|Old Hell Shaft]] was broken, one figure

[035-003] had disappeared from within it. [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] and his shadow had not

[035-004] stood near [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]], who held her father’s arm, but in a retired place by

[035-005] themselves. When [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] was summoned to the couch, [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]],

[035-006] attentive to all that happened, slipped behind that wicked shadow—a sight

[035-007] in the horror of his face, if there had been eyes there for any sight but

[035-008] one—and whispered in his ear. Without turning his [[reason|head]], he conferred

[035-009] with her a few moments, and vanished. Thus [[tom-gradgrind|the whelp]] had gone out of the

[035-010] circle before the people moved.

[035-011] When the father reached home, he sent a message to Mr. Bounderby’s,

[035-012] desiring his son to come to him directly. The reply was, that Mr.

[035-013] Bounderby having missed him in the crowd, and seeing nothing of him

[035-014] since, had supposed him to be at [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]].

[035-015] ‘I believe, father,’ said Louisa, ‘he will not come back to town

[035-016] to-night.’ Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] turned away, and said no more.

[035-017] In the morning, he went down to the Bank himself as soon as it was

[035-018] opened, and seeing his son’s place empty (he had not the courage to look

[035-019] in at first) went back along the street to meet Mr. Bounderby on his way

[035-020] there. To whom he said that, for reasons he would soon explain, but

[035-021] entreated not then to be asked for, he had found it necessary to employ

[035-022] his son at a distance for a little while. Also, that he was charged with

[035-023] the [[duty]] of vindicating [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]]’s memory, and declaring the

[035-024] thief. Mr. Bounderby quite confounded, stood stock-still in the street

[035-025] after his father-in-law had left him, swelling like an immense

[035-026] soap-bubble, without its beauty.

[035-027] Mr. Gradgrind went home, locked himself in his room, and kept it all that

[035-028] day. When Sissy and Louisa tapped at his door, he said, without opening

[035-029] it, ‘Not now, my dears; in the evening.’ On their return in the evening,

[035-030] he said, ‘I am not able yet—to-morrow.’ He ate nothing all day, and had

[035-031] no candle after dark; and they heard him walking to and fro late at

[035-032] night.

[035-033] But, in the morning he appeared at breakfast at the usual hour, and took

[035-034] his usual place at the table. Aged and bent he looked, and quite bowed

[035-035] down; and yet he looked a wiser man, and a better man, than in the days

[035-036] when in this life he wanted nothing—but [[facts|Facts]]. Before he left the room,

[035-037] he appointed a time for them to come to him; and so, with his gray head

[035-038] drooping, went away.

[035-039] ‘Dear father,’ said Louisa, when they kept their appointment, ‘you have

[035-040] three young [[childhood|children]] left. They will be different, I will be different

[035-041] yet, with Heaven’s help.’

[035-042] She gave her hand to Sissy, as if she meant with her help too.

[035-043] ‘Your wretched brother,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Do you think he had

[035-044] planned this robbery, when he went with you to the lodging?’

[035-045] ‘I fear so, father. I know he had wanted money very much, and had spent

[035-046] a great deal.’

[035-047] ‘The poor man being about to leave the town, it came into his evil brain

[035-048] to cast suspicion on him?’

[035-049] ‘I think it must have flashed upon him while he sat there, father. For I

[035-050] asked him to go there with me. The visit did not originate with him.’

[035-051] ‘He had some conversation with the poor man. Did he take him aside?’

[035-052] ‘He took him out of the room. I asked him afterwards, why he had done

[035-053] so, and he made a plausible excuse; but since last night, father, and

[035-054] when I remember the circumstances by its light, I am afraid I can imagine

[035-055] too truly what passed between them.’

[035-056] ‘Let me know,’ said her father, ‘if your thoughts present your guilty

[035-057] brother in the same dark view as mine.’

[035-058] ‘I fear, father,’ hesitated Louisa, ‘that he must have made some

[035-059] representation to Stephen Blackpool—perhaps in my name, perhaps in his

[035-060] own—which induced him to do in good faith and honesty, what he had never

[035-061] done before, and to wait about the Bank those two or three nights before

[035-062] he left the town.’

[035-063] ‘Too plain!’ returned the father. ‘Too plain!’

[035-064] He shaded his face, and remained silent for some moments. Recovering

[035-065] himself, he said:

[035-066] ‘And now, how is he to be found? How is he to be saved from justice? In

[035-067] the few hours that I can possibly allow to elapse before I publish the

[035-068] [[truth]], how is he to be found by us, and only by us? Ten thousand pounds

[035-069] could not effect it.’

[035-070] ‘Sissy has effected it, father.’

[035-071] He raised his eyes to where she stood, like a good fairy in his house,

[035-072] and said in a tone of softened gratitude and grateful kindness, ‘It is

[035-073] always you, my child!’

[035-074] ‘We had our fears,’ Sissy explained, glancing at Louisa, ‘before

[035-075] yesterday; and when I saw you brought to the side of the litter last

[035-076] night, and heard what passed (being close to [[rachael|Rachael]] all the time), I

[035-077] went to him when no one saw, and said to him, “Don’t look at me. See

[035-078] where your father is. Escape at once, for his sake and your own!” He

[035-079] was in a tremble before I whispered to him, and he started and trembled

[035-080] more then, and said, “Where can I go? I have very little money, and I

[035-081] don’t know who will hide me!” I thought of father’s old [[performance|circus]]. I have

[035-082] not forgotten where [[sleary|Mr. Sleary]] goes at this time of year, and I read of

[035-083] him in a paper only the other day. I told him to hurry there, and tell

[035-084] his name, and ask Mr. Sleary to hide him till I came. “I’ll get to him

[035-085] before the morning,” he said. And I saw him shrink away among the

[035-086] people.’

[035-087] ‘Thank Heaven!’ exclaimed his father. ‘He may be got abroad yet.’

[035-088] It was the more hopeful as the town to which Sissy had directed him was

[035-089] within three hours’ journey of Liverpool, whence he could be swiftly

[035-090] dispatched to any part of the world. But, caution being necessary in

[035-091] communicating with him—for there was a greater danger every moment of his

[035-092] being suspected now, and nobody could be sure at [[heart]] but that Mr.

[035-093] Bounderby himself, in a bullying vein of public zeal, might play a Roman

[035-094] part—it was consented that Sissy and Louisa should repair to the place in

[035-095] question, by a circuitous course, alone; and that the unhappy father,

[035-096] setting forth in an opposite direction, should get round to the same

[035-097] bourne by another and wider route. It was further agreed that he should

[035-098] not present himself to Mr. Sleary, lest his intentions should be

[035-099] mistrusted, or the intelligence of his arrival should cause his son to

[035-100] take flight anew; but, that the communication should be left to Sissy and

[035-101] Louisa to open; and that they should inform the cause of so much misery

[035-102] and [[shame|disgrace]], of his father’s being at hand and of the purpose for which

[035-103] they had come. When these arrangements had been well considered and were

[035-104] fully understood by all three, it was time to begin to carry them into

[035-105] execution. Early in the afternoon, Mr. Gradgrind walked direct from his

[035-106] own house into the country, to be taken up on the line by which he was to

[035-107] travel; and at night the remaining two set forth upon their different

[035-108] course, encouraged by not seeing any face they knew.

[035-109] The two travelled all night, except when they were left, for odd numbers

[035-110] of minutes, at branch-places, up illimitable flights of steps, or down

[035-111] wells—which was the only variety of those branches—and, early in the

[035-112] morning, were turned out on a swamp, a mile or two from the town they

[035-113] sought. From this dismal spot they were rescued by a savage old

[035-114] postilion, who happened to be up early, kicking a horse in a fly: and so

[035-115] were smuggled into the town by all the back lanes where the pigs lived:

[035-116] which, although not a magnificent or even savoury approach, was, as is

[035-117] usual in such cases, the legitimate highway.

[035-118] The first thing they saw on entering the town was the skeleton of

[035-119] Sleary’s Circus. The company had departed for another town more than

[035-120] twenty miles off, and had opened there last night. The connection

[035-121] between the two places was by a hilly turnpike-road, and the travelling

[035-122] on that road was very slow. Though they took but a hasty breakfast, and

[035-123] no rest (which it would have been in vain to seek under such anxious

[035-124] circumstances), it was noon before they began to find the bills of

[035-125] Sleary’s Horse-riding on barns and walls, and one o’clock when they

[035-126] stopped in the market-place.

[035-127] A Grand Morning Performance by the Riders, commencing at that very hour,

[035-128] was in course of announcement by the bellman as they set their feet upon

[035-129] the stones of the street. Sissy recommended that, to avoid making

[035-130] inquiries and attracting attention in the town, they should present

[035-131] themselves to pay at the door. If Mr. Sleary were taking the money, he

[035-132] would be sure to know her, and would proceed with discretion. If he were

[035-133] not, he would be sure to see them inside; and, knowing what he had done

[035-134] with the fugitive, would proceed with discretion still.

[035-135] Therefore, they repaired, with fluttering hearts, to the well-remembered

[035-136] booth. The flag with the inscription SLEARY’S HORSE-RIDING was there;

[035-137] and the Gothic niche was there; but Mr. Sleary was not there. Master

[035-138] Kidderminster, grown too maturely turfy to be received by the wildest

[035-139] credulity as Cupid any more, had yielded to the invincible force of

[035-140] circumstances (and his beard), and, in the capacity of a man who made

[035-141] himself generally useful, presided on this occasion over the

[035-142] exchequer—having also a drum in reserve, on which to expend his leisure

[035-143] moments and superfluous forces. In the extreme sharpness of his look out

[035-144] for base coin, Mr. Kidderminster, as at present situated, never saw

[035-145] anything but money; so Sissy passed him unrecognised, and they went in.

[035-146] The Emperor of Japan, on a steady old white horse stencilled with black

[035-147] spots, was twirling five wash-hand basins at once, as it is the favourite

[035-148] recreation of that monarch to do. Sissy, though well acquainted with his

[035-149] Royal line, had no personal knowledge of the present Emperor, and his

[035-150] reign was peaceful. [[josephine-sleary|Miss Josephine Sleary]], in her celebrated graceful

[035-152] humorously said Cauliflower Act), and Mr. Sleary appeared, leading her

[035-153] in.

[035-156] you!’ when Sissy was recognised both by father and daughter. But they
