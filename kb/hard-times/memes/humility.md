# Humility

"Humility" in *Hard Times* appears almost exclusively in counterfeit. The novel's great satiric invention is "the Bully of humility" — Josiah Bounderby, who has built his fortune and his public identity on the relentless performance of low origins. His humility is a weapon: every reference to his gutter childhood, his abandoned mother, and his self-made success is a demand for admiration disguised as self-deprecation. Against this counterfeited virtue, Dickens sets the genuine article — the unannounced, untheorised humility of Stephen Blackpool and Rachael, who simply live as though other people matter. The novel's thesis is that genuine humility is never self-regarding; it is indistinguishable from love.

## Definition

Bounderby's "humility" is the novel's case study in the corruption of a virtue. His famous origin story — abandoned by his mother, raised in a ditch, self-educated, self-made — is recited at every opportunity, always in the guise of modesty and always with the effect of magnifying the speaker. "I was born in a ditch," Bounderby announces, and the announcement is simultaneously a disavowal of privilege and a claim to moral superiority: I owe nothing to anyone; everything I have, I made. The fiction that underlies this performance — that Bounderby's mother is not dead in a ditch but alive and comfortably maintained — exposes the logic of counterfeit humility: it serves the self while pretending to efface it. ## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of humility is structured as a chiasmus: counterfeit humility (Bounderby, Mrs. Sparsit) is loud, public, and self-advertising; genuine humility (Stephen, Rachael) is silent, private, and self-forgetful. The novel never uses the word "humility" to describe Stephen or Rachael — they do not need the word because they inhabit the reality.

Bounderby's "Bully of humility" is introduced early and sustained relentlessly. The epithet — repeated at key structural moments: his first appearance (PN: 004-016), his domestic tyranny (PN: 007-006), and the exposure of his lies (PN: 033-243) — frames his entire character as a study in the weaponisation of self-deprecation. His "explosive humility" (PN: 004-168) in the scene with Sissy Jupe is characteristic: he uses his supposed low origins to intimidate a child, demanding gratitude for his condescension.

Mrs. Sparsit provides a complementary portrait of counterfeit humility. Her "State humility" (PN: 007-172) is the aristocratic variant of Bounderby's demotic version: she has come down in the world and makes her descent visible in every gesture, every sigh, every "affectation of humility" (PN: 007-141) that is in fact a reproach. Her surveillance of Louisa — conducted under the banner of "duty" — is humility weaponised as malice.

Stephen Blackpool embodies the novel's alternative. He does not perform humility; he simply declines to assert himself at others' expense. When he refuses to join the union, it is not out of pride but out of a promise made to Rachael. When he is ostracised, he does not protest his virtue. His humility is not a stance but a condition — the natural disposition of a man who has learned, through suffering, that the self is not the centre of the universe.

### Key Passages

> "A man who was the Bully of humility." (PN: 004-016)

The novel's definitive characterisation of Bounderby, delivered in the narrator's voice with an authority that brooks no qualification. The paradox — how can humility bully? — is the engine of the satire: Bounderby's humility is aggression disguised as modesty, domination masked as self-abasement. He does not boast of his wealth; he boasts of his poverty, and the boasting is no less self-aggrandising for its inverted form.

> "explosive humility. 'You have one of those strollers' children in the school.'" (PN: 004-168)

The modifier "explosive" captures the violence latent in Bounderby's performance. His humility detonates — it is sudden, loud, and designed to intimidate. The object of the explosion is Sissy Jupe, a child whose father is a circus performer, and the cruelty of the remark is inseparable from its form: Bounderby's self-deprecation is always directed at someone more vulnerable than himself.

> "the Bully of humility, who had built his windy reputation upon lies, and in his boastfulness had put the honest truth as far away from him as if he had advanced the sun's course by a month" (PN: 033-243)

The novel's final judgement on Bounderby, delivered at the moment his fraud is exposed. The "windy reputation" — air, not substance — is the inevitable terminus of counterfeit virtue. Bounderby's humility was always a construction, and constructions collapse. The elaborate temporal metaphor ("advanced the sun's course by a month") dignifies the exposure with cosmic scale: Bounderby's lies were not merely personal failings but a violence against the order of things.

## Historical Context

The Victorian cult of the self-made man provided the cultural logic that Bounderby exploits. Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859), published five years after *Hard Times*, would codify the gospel of individual advancement through industry, thrift, and perseverance. The ideology was not new in 1854 — it had been the dominant narrative of the industrial middle class for a generation — and Dickens's satire targets its central rhetorical device: the self-made man's origin story is always a form of humility that is also a form of self-promotion.

Evangelical Christianity had its own discourse of humility. The doctrine of human depravity insisted on the sinner's unworthiness before God, and the conversion narrative — from sin to salvation — was the Evangelical genre par excellence. Dickens, who distrusted Evangelicalism's theatricality, saw in its conventions the same pattern he satirised in Bounderby: the performance of unworthiness that is actually a claim to special status. The "Bully of humility" is, among other things, a satire of the Evangelical conversion narrative repurposed for economic self-advancement.

The Carlylean "hero" — the great man who shapes history through the force of his personality — is the implicit target of the Bounderby portrait. Thomas Carlyle's *On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History* (1841) had made the hero a central category of Victorian thought, and Bounderby is the hero inverted: a man who claims greatness on the strength of having been born in a ditch and made himself rich. Dickens's point is that the hero-narrative is always, in the wrong hands, an instrument of domination.

## Embodied By

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]** (counterfeit): The "Bully of humility" who has built a career — and a fortune — on the performance of low origins. His humility is an assault weapon: every reference to his gutter childhood is a demand for admiration. The exposure of his lies does not lead to reform but to a quieter, more desperate version of the same performance.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]** (genuine): Humility as the natural disposition of a man who has been taught by suffering that the self is not the centre of the universe. Stephen does not know he is humble; he simply acts as though other people's claims are as real as his own.
- **[[Rachael]]** (genuine): Humility as self-forgetful love. Rachael's care for Stephen's wife is never announced as virtue; it is simply what she does, and her silence about it is the proof of its genuineness.
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]** (counterfeit): Humility as aristocratic reproach. Her "State humility" — the performance of fallen grandeur — is a constant, silent accusation directed at those who have supplanted her. Like Bounderby's, her humility is aggression in a minor key.

## Contrasted With

- **[[pride]]**: The novel's treatment of humility is complicated by its recognition that pride and humility are not simple opposites. Bounderby's counterfeit humility is a form of pride — the pride of the self-made man who cannot stop talking about his making. Stephen's genuine humility is not the opposite of pride but its transcendence: he has no investment in his own importance, and therefore no need to assert or defend it.
- **[[duty]]**: Counterfeit humility and counterfeit duty share a common structure: both are performances of virtue that serve the performer. Bounderby's humility and Mrs. Sparsit's duty are both weapons, and both are exposed — by the novel's end — as forms of self-interest dressed in the language of obligation. (PN: 017-288)

## Critical Perspectives

> Humphry House, in *The Dickens World* (1941), argued that Dickens's treatment of humility reflects a broader Victorian anxiety about the authenticity of virtue in a commercial society. In a world where everything can be bought and sold, how can one distinguish genuine humility from the performance of humility that serves self-advancement? The novel's answer — that genuine virtue is silent, and counterfeit virtue is loud — is a heuristic rather than a solution, but it is a powerful one.

> J. Hillis Miller, in *Charles Dickens: The World of His Novels* (1958), read Bounderby's counterfeit humility as an instance of the larger Dickensian theme of the "unreal" — the self that has become a performance of itself. Bounderby is not merely a hypocrite; he is a man who has lost access to any self that is not a performance, and his "humility" is the mask that has become the face.
