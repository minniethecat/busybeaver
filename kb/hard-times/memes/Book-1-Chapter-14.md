[014-001] THE GREAT MANUFACTURER

[014-002] TIME went on in [[coketown|Coketown]] like its own machinery: so much material wrought

[014-003] up, so much fuel consumed, so many powers worn out, so much money made.

[014-004] But, less inexorable than iron, steel, and brass, it brought its varying

[014-005] seasons even into that wilderness of smoke and brick, and made the only

[014-006] stand that ever _was_ made in the place against its direful uniformity.

[014-007] ‘[[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] is becoming,’ said [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], ‘almost a young woman.’

[014-008] Time, with his innumerable horse-power, worked away, not minding what

[014-009] anybody said, and presently turned out young Thomas a foot taller than

[014-010] when his father had last taken particular notice of him.

[014-011] ‘Thomas is becoming,’ said Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], ‘almost a young man.’

[014-012] Time passed Thomas on in the mill, while his father was thinking about

[014-013] it, and there he stood in a long-tailed coat and a stiff shirt-collar.

[014-014] ‘Really,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘the period has arrived when Thomas ought

[014-015] to go to [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]].’

[014-016] Time, sticking to him, passed him on into Bounderby’s Bank, made him an

[014-017] inmate of Bounderby’s house, necessitated the purchase of his first

[014-018] razor, and exercised him diligently in his calculations relative to

[014-019] number one.

[014-020] The same great manufacturer, always with an immense variety of work on

[014-021] hand, in every stage of development, passed [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] onward in his mill, and

[014-022] worked her up into a very pretty article indeed.

[014-023] ‘I fear, Jupe,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘that your continuance at the school

[014-024] any longer would be useless.’

[014-025] ‘I am afraid it would, sir,’ Sissy answered with a curtsey.

[014-026] ‘I cannot disguise from you, Jupe,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, knitting his

[014-027] brow, ‘that the result of your probation there has disappointed me; has

[014-028] greatly disappointed me. You have not acquired, under Mr. and Mrs.

[014-029] M’Choakumchild, anything like that amount of exact knowledge which I

[014-030] looked for. You are extremely deficient in your [[facts]]. Your

[014-031] acquaintance with figures is very limited. You are altogether backward,

[014-032] and below the mark.’

[014-033] ‘I am sorry, sir,’ she returned; ‘but I know it is quite true. Yet I

[014-034] have tried hard, sir.’

[014-035] ‘Yes,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘yes, I believe you have tried hard; I have

[014-036] observed you, and I can find no fault in that respect.’

[014-037] ‘Thank you, sir. I have thought sometimes;’ Sissy very timid here; ‘that

[014-038] perhaps I tried to learn too much, and that if I had asked to be allowed

[014-039] to try a little less, I might have—’

[014-040] ‘No, Jupe, no,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, shaking his [[reason|head]] in his profoundest

[014-041] and most eminently practical way. ‘No. The course you pursued, you

[014-042] pursued according to the system—the system—and there is no more to be

[014-043] said about it. I can only suppose that the circumstances of your early

[014-044] life were too unfavourable to the development of your reasoning powers,

[014-045] and that we began too late. Still, as I have said already, I am

[014-046] disappointed.’

[014-047] ‘I wish I could have made a better acknowledgment, sir, of your kindness

[014-048] to a poor forlorn girl who had no claim upon you, and of your protection

[014-049] of her.’

[014-050] ‘Don’t shed tears,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Don’t shed tears. I don’t

[014-051] complain of you. You are an affectionate, earnest, good young

[014-052] woman—and—and we must make that do.’

[014-053] ‘Thank you, sir, very much,’ said Sissy, with a grateful curtsey.

[014-054] ‘You are useful to Mrs. Gradgrind, and (in a generally pervading way) you

[014-055] are serviceable in the [[family]] also; so I understand from Miss Louisa,

[014-056] and, indeed, so I have observed myself. I therefore hope,’ said Mr.

[014-057] Gradgrind, ‘that you can make yourself happy in those relations.’

[014-058] ‘I should have nothing to wish, sir, if—’

[014-059] ‘I understand you,’ said Mr. Gradgrind; ‘you still refer to your father.

[014-060] I have heard from Miss Louisa that you still preserve that bottle. Well!

[014-061] If your [[education|training]] in the science of arriving at exact results had been

[014-062] more successful, you would have been wiser on these points. I will say

[014-063] no more.’

[014-064] He really liked Sissy too well to have a contempt for her; otherwise he

[014-065] held her calculating powers in such very slight estimation that he must

[014-066] have fallen upon that conclusion. Somehow or other, he had become

[014-067] possessed by an idea that there was something in this girl which could

[014-068] hardly be set forth in a tabular form. Her capacity of definition might

[014-069] be easily stated at a very low figure, her mathematical knowledge at

[014-070] nothing; yet he was not sure that if he had been required, for example,

[014-071] to tick her off into columns in a parliamentary return, he would have

[014-072] quite known how to divide her.

[014-073] In some stages of his manufacture of the human fabric, the processes of

[014-074] Time are very rapid. Young Thomas and Sissy being both at such a stage

[014-075] of their working up, these changes were effected in a year or two; while

[014-076] Mr. Gradgrind himself seemed stationary in his course, and underwent no

[014-077] alteration.

[014-078] Except one, which was apart from his necessary progress through the mill.

[014-079] Time hustled him into a little noisy and rather dirty machinery, in a

[014-080] by-comer, and made him Member of Parliament for Coketown: one of the

[014-081] respected members for ounce weights and measures, one of the

[014-082] representatives of the multiplication table, one of the deaf honourable

[014-083] [[gentleman|gentlemen]], dumb honourable gentlemen, blind honourable gentlemen, lame

[014-084] honourable gentlemen, dead honourable gentlemen, to every other

[014-085] consideration. Else wherefore live we in a Christian land, eighteen

[014-086] hundred and odd years after our Master?

[014-087] All this while, Louisa had been passing on, so quiet and reserved, and so

[014-088] much given to watching the bright ashes at twilight as they fell into the

[014-089] grate, and became extinct, that from the period when her father had said

[014-090] she was almost a young woman—which seemed but yesterday—she had scarcely

[014-091] attracted his notice again, when he found her quite a young woman.

[014-092] ‘Quite a young woman,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, musing. ‘Dear me!’

[014-093] Soon after this discovery, he became more thoughtful than usual for

[014-094] several days, and seemed much engrossed by one subject. On a certain

[014-095] night, when he was going out, and Louisa came to bid him good-bye before

[014-096] his departure—as he was not to be home until late and she would not see

[014-097] him again until the morning—he held her in his arms, looking at her in

[014-098] his kindest manner, and said:

[014-099] ‘My [[love|dear]] Louisa, you are a woman!’

[014-100] She answered with the old, quick, searching look of the night when she

[014-101] was found at the Circus; then cast down her eyes. ‘Yes, father.’

[014-102] ‘My dear,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘I must speak with you alone and

[014-103] seriously. Come to me in my room after breakfast to-morrow, will you?’

[014-104] ‘Yes, father.’

[014-105] ‘Your hands are rather cold, Louisa. Are you not well?’

[014-106] ‘Quite well, father.’

[014-107] ‘And cheerful?’

[014-108] She looked at him again, and smiled in her peculiar manner. ‘I am as

[014-109] cheerful, father, as I usually am, or usually have been.’

[014-110] ‘That’s well,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. So, he kissed her and went away; and

[014-111] Louisa returned to the serene apartment of the hair-cutting character,

[014-112] and leaning her elbow on her hand, looked again at the short-lived sparks

[014-113] that so soon subsided into ashes.

[014-114] ‘Are you there, Loo?’ said her brother, looking in at the door. He was

[014-115] quite a young gentleman of pleasure now, and not quite a prepossessing

[014-116] one.

[014-117] ‘Dear [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]],’ she answered, rising and embracing him, ‘how long it is since

[014-118] you have been to see me!’

[014-119] ‘Why, I have been otherwise engaged, Loo, in the evenings; and in the

[014-120] daytime old Bounderby has been keeping me at it rather. But I touch him

[014-121] up with you when he comes it too strong, and so we preserve an

[014-122] understanding. I say! Has father said anything particular to you to-day

[014-123] or yesterday, Loo?’

[014-124] ‘No, Tom. But he told me to-night that he wished to do so in the

[014-125] morning.’

[014-126] ‘Ah! That’s what I mean,’ said Tom. ‘Do you know where he is

[014-127] to-night?’—with a very deep expression.

[014-128] ‘No.’

[014-129] ‘Then I’ll tell you. He’s with old Bounderby. They are having a regular

[014-130] confab together up at the Bank. Why at the Bank, do you think? Well,

[014-131] I’ll tell you again. To keep [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]]’s ears as far off as possible,

[014-132] I expect.’

[014-133] With her hand upon her brother’s shoulder, Louisa still stood looking at

[014-134] the fire. Her brother glanced at her face with greater interest than

[014-135] usual, and, encircling her waist with his arm, drew her coaxingly to him.

[014-136] ‘You are very fond of me, an’t you, Loo?’

[014-137] ‘Indeed I am, Tom, though you do let such long intervals go by without

[014-138] coming to see me.’

[014-139] ‘Well, sister of mine,’ said Tom, ‘when you say that, you are near my

[014-140] thoughts. We might be so much oftener together—mightn’t we? Always

[014-141] together, almost—mightn’t we? It would do me a great deal of good if you

[014-142] were to make up your mind to I know what, Loo. It would be a splendid

[014-143] thing for me. It would be uncommonly jolly!’

[014-144] Her thoughtfulness baffled his cunning scrutiny. He could make nothing

[014-145] of her face. He pressed her in his arm, and kissed her cheek. She

[014-146] returned the kiss, but still looked at the fire.

[014-147] ‘I say, Loo! I thought I’d come, and just hint to you what was going on:

[014-148] though I supposed you’d most likely guess, even if you didn’t know. I

[014-149] can’t stay, because I’m engaged to some fellows to-night. You won’t

[014-150] forget how fond you are of me?’

[014-151] ‘No, dear Tom, I won’t forget.’

[014-152] ‘That’s a capital girl,’ said Tom. ‘Good-bye, Loo.’

[014-153] She gave him an affectionate good-night, and went out with him to the

[014-154] door, whence the fires of Coketown could be seen, making the distance

[014-155] lurid. She stood there, looking steadfastly towards them, and listening

[014-156] to his departing steps. They retreated quickly, as glad to get away from

[014-157] [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]]; and she stood there yet, when he was gone and all was quiet.

[014-158] It seemed as if, first in her own fire within the house, and then in the

[014-159] fiery haze without, she tried to discover what kind of woof Old Time,

[014-160] that greatest and longest-established Spinner of all, would weave from

[014-161] the threads he had already spun into a woman. But his factory is a

[014-162] secret place, his work is noiseless, and his Hands are mutes.
