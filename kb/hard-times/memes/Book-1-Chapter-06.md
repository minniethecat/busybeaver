[006-001] SLEARY’S HORSEMANSHIP

[006-002] THE name of the public-house was [[pegasus-arms|the Pegasus]]’s Arms. The Pegasus’s legs

[006-003] might have been more to the purpose; but, underneath the winged horse

[006-004] upon the sign-board, the Pegasus’s Arms was inscribed in Roman letters.

[006-005] Beneath that inscription again, in a flowing scroll, the painter had

[006-006] touched off the lines:

[006-007] Good malt makes good beer,

[006-008] Walk in, and they’ll draw it here;

[006-009] Good wine makes good brandy,

[006-010] Give us a call, and you’ll find it handy.

[006-011] Framed and glazed upon the wall behind the dingy little bar, was another

[006-012] Pegasus—a theatrical one—with real gauze let in for his wings, golden

[006-013] stars stuck on all over him, and his ethereal harness made of red silk.

[006-014] As it had grown too dusky without, to see the sign, and as it had not

[006-015] grown light enough within to see the picture, [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] and Mr.

[006-016] [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]] received no offence from these idealities. They followed the

[006-017] girl up some steep corner-stairs without meeting any one, and stopped in

[006-018] the dark while she went on for a candle. They expected every moment to

[006-019] hear Merrylegs give tongue, but the highly trained performing dog had not

[006-020] barked when the girl and the candle appeared together.

[006-021] ‘Father is not in our room, sir,’ she said, with a face of great

[006-022] surprise. ‘If you wouldn’t mind walking in, I’ll find him directly.’

[006-023] They walked in; and [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]], having set two chairs for them, sped away with

[006-024] a quick light step. It was a mean, shabbily furnished room, with a bed

[006-025] in it. The white night-cap, embellished with two peacock’s feathers and

[006-026] a pigtail bolt upright, in which Signor Jupe had that very afternoon

[006-027] enlivened the varied performances with his chaste Shaksperean quips and

[006-028] retorts, hung upon a nail; but no other portion of his wardrobe, or other

[006-029] token of himself or his pursuits, was to be seen anywhere. As to

[006-030] Merrylegs, that respectable ancestor of the highly trained animal who

[006-031] went aboard the ark, might have been accidentally shut out of it, for any

[006-032] sign of a dog that was manifest to eye or ear in the Pegasus’s Arms.

[006-033] They heard the doors of rooms above, opening and shutting as Sissy went

[006-034] from one to another in quest of her father; and presently they heard

[006-035] voices expressing surprise. She came bounding down again in a great

[006-036] hurry, opened a battered and mangy old hair trunk, found it empty, and

[006-037] looked round with her hands clasped and her face full of terror.

[006-038] ‘Father must have gone down to the Booth, sir. I don’t know why he

[006-039] should go there, but he must be there; I’ll bring him in a minute!’ She

[006-040] was gone directly, without her bonnet; with her long, dark, childish hair

[006-041] streaming behind her.

[006-042] ‘What does she mean!’ said Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]]. ‘Back in a minute? It’s more

[006-043] than a mile off.’

[006-044] Before Mr. Bounderby could reply, a young man appeared at the door, and

[006-045] introducing himself with the words, ‘By your leaves, [[gentleman|gentlemen]]!’ walked

[006-046] in with his hands in his pockets. His face, close-shaven, thin, and

[006-047] sallow, was shaded by a great quantity of dark hair, brushed into a roll

[006-048] all round his [[reason|head]], and parted up the centre. His legs were very robust,

[006-049] but shorter than legs of good proportions should have been. His chest

[006-050] and back were as much too broad, as his legs were too short. He was

[006-051] dressed in a Newmarket coat and tight-fitting trousers; wore a shawl

[006-052] round his neck; smelt of lamp-oil, straw, orange-peel, horses’ provender,

[006-053] and sawdust; and looked a most remarkable sort of Centaur, compounded of

[006-054] the stable and the play-house. Where the one began, and the other ended,

[006-055] nobody could have told with any precision. This gentleman was mentioned

[006-056] in the bills of the day as Mr. E. W. B. Childers, so justly celebrated

[006-057] for his daring vaulting act as the Wild Huntsman of the North American

[006-058] Prairies; in which popular [[performance]], a diminutive boy with an old

[006-059] face, who now accompanied him, assisted as his infant son: being carried

[006-060] upside down over his father’s shoulder, by one foot, and held by the

[006-061] crown of his head, heels upwards, in the palm of his father’s hand,

[006-062] according to the violent paternal manner in which wild huntsmen may be

[006-063] observed to fondle their offspring. Made up with curls, wreaths, wings,

[006-064] white bismuth, and carmine, this hopeful young person soared into so

[006-065] pleasing a Cupid as to constitute the chief delight of the maternal part

[006-066] of the spectators; but in private, where his characteristics were a

[006-067] precocious cutaway coat and an extremely gruff voice, he became of the

[006-068] Turf, turfy.

[006-069] ‘By your leaves, gentlemen,’ said Mr. E. W. B. Childers, glancing round

[006-070] the room. ‘It was you, I believe, that were wishing to see Jupe!’

[006-071] ‘It was,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘His daughter has gone to fetch him, but I

[006-072] can’t wait; therefore, if you please, I will leave a message for him with

[006-073] you.’

[006-074] ‘You see, my friend,’ Mr. Bounderby put in, ‘we are the kind of people

[006-075] who know the value of time, and you are the kind of people who don’t know

[006-076] the value of time.’

[006-077] ‘I have not,’ retorted Mr. Childers, after surveying him from head to

[006-078] foot, ‘the honour of knowing _you_,—but if you mean that you can make

[006-079] more money of your time than I can of mine, I should judge from your

[006-080] appearance, that you are about right.’

[006-081] ‘And when you have made it, you can keep it too, I should think,’ said

[006-082] Cupid.

[006-083] ‘Kidderminster, stow that!’ said Mr. Childers. (Master Kidderminster was

[006-084] Cupid’s mortal name.)

[006-085] ‘What does he come here cheeking us for, then?’ cried Master

[006-086] Kidderminster, showing a very irascible temperament. ‘If you want to

[006-087] cheek us, pay your ochre at the doors and take it out.’

[006-088] ‘Kidderminster,’ said Mr. Childers, raising his voice, ‘stow that!—Sir,’

[006-089] to Mr. Gradgrind, ‘I was addressing myself to you. You may or you may

[006-090] not be aware (for perhaps you have not been much in the audience), that

[006-091] Jupe has missed his tip very often, lately.’

[006-092] ‘Has—what has he missed?’ asked Mr. Gradgrind, glancing at the potent

[006-093] Bounderby for assistance.

[006-094] ‘Missed his tip.’

[006-095] ‘Offered at the Garters four times last night, and never done ’em once,’

[006-096] said Master Kidderminster. ‘Missed his tip at the banners, too, and was

[006-097] loose in his ponging.’

[006-098] ‘Didn’t do what he ought to do. Was short in his leaps and bad in his

[006-099] tumbling,’ Mr. Childers interpreted.

[006-100] ‘Oh!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘that is tip, is it?’

[006-101] ‘In a general way that’s missing his tip,’ Mr. E. W. B. Childers

[006-102] answered.

[006-103] ‘Nine oils, Merrylegs, missing tips, garters, banners, and Ponging, eh!’

[006-104] ejaculated Bounderby, with his laugh of laughs. ‘Queer sort of company,

[006-105] too, for a man who has raised himself!’

[006-106] ‘Lower yourself, then,’ retorted Cupid. ‘Oh Lord! if you’ve raised

[006-107] yourself so high as all that comes to, let yourself down a bit.’

[006-108] ‘This is a very obtrusive lad!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, turning, and knitting

[006-109] his brows on him.

[006-110] ‘We’d have had a young gentleman to meet you, if we had known you were

[006-111] coming,’ retorted Master Kidderminster, nothing abashed. ‘It’s a pity

[006-112] you don’t have a bespeak, being so particular. You’re on the Tight-Jeff,

[006-113] ain’t you?’

[006-114] ‘What does this unmannerly boy mean,’ asked Mr. Gradgrind, eyeing him in

[006-115] a sort of desperation, ‘by Tight-Jeff?’

[006-116] ‘There! Get out, get out!’ said Mr. Childers, thrusting his young friend

[006-117] from the room, rather in the prairie manner. ‘Tight-Jeff or Slack-Jeff,

[006-118] it don’t much signify: it’s only tight-rope and slack-rope. You were

[006-119] going to give me a message for Jupe?’

[006-120] ‘Yes, I was.’

[006-121] ‘Then,’ continued Mr. Childers, quickly, ‘my opinion is, he will never

[006-122] receive it. Do you know much of him?’

[006-123] ‘I never saw the man in my life.’

[006-124] ‘I doubt if you ever _will_ see him now. It’s pretty plain to me, he’s

[006-125] off.’

[006-126] ‘Do you mean that he has deserted his daughter?’

[006-127] ‘Ay! I mean,’ said Mr. Childers, with a nod, ‘that he has cut. He was

[006-128] goosed last night, he was goosed the night before last, he was goosed

[006-129] to-day. He has lately got in the way of being always goosed, and he

[006-130] can’t stand it.’

[006-131] ‘Why has he been—so very much—Goosed?’ asked Mr. Gradgrind, forcing the

[006-132] word out of himself, with great solemnity and reluctance.

[006-133] ‘His joints are turning stiff, and he is getting used up,’ said Childers.

[006-134] ‘He has his points as a Cackler still, but he can’t get a living out of

[006-135] _them_.’

[006-136] ‘A Cackler!’ Bounderby repeated. ‘Here we go again!’

[006-137] ‘A speaker, if the gentleman likes it better,’ said Mr. E. W. B.

[006-138] Childers, superciliously throwing the interpretation over his shoulder,

[006-139] and accompanying it with a shake of his long hair—which all shook at

[006-140] once. ‘Now, it’s a remarkable [[facts|fact]], sir, that it cut that man deeper, to

[006-141] know that his daughter knew of his being goosed, than to go through with

[006-142] it.’

[006-143] ‘Good!’ interrupted Mr. Bounderby. ‘This is good, Gradgrind! A man so

[006-144] fond of his daughter, that he runs away from her! This is devilish good!

[006-145] Ha! ha! Now, I’ll tell you what, young man. I haven’t always occupied

[006-146] my present station of life. I know what these things are. You may be

[006-147] astonished to hear it, but my mother—ran away from _me_.’

[006-148] E. W. B. Childers replied pointedly, that he was not at all astonished to

[006-149] hear it.

[006-150] ‘Very well,’ said Bounderby. ‘I was born in a ditch, and my mother ran

[006-151] away from me. Do I excuse her for it? No. Have I ever excused her for

[006-152] it? Not I. What do I call her for it? I call her probably the very

[006-153] worst woman that ever lived in the world, except my drunken grandmother.

[006-154] There’s no [[family|family pride]] about me, there’s no [[imagination|imaginative]] sentimental

[006-155] humbug about me. I call a spade a spade; and I call the mother of Josiah

[006-156] Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]], without any fear or any favour, what I should call

[006-157] her if she had been the mother of Dick Jones of Wapping. So, with this

[006-158] man. He is a runaway rogue and a vagabond, that’s what he is, in

[006-159] English.’

[006-160] ‘It’s all the same to me what he is or what he is not, whether in English

[006-161] or whether in French,’ retorted Mr. E. W. B. Childers, facing about. ‘I

[006-162] am telling your friend what’s the fact; if you don’t like to hear it, you

[006-163] can avail yourself of the open air. You give it mouth enough, you do;

[006-164] but give it mouth in your own building at least,’ remonstrated E. W. B.

[006-165] with stern irony. ‘Don’t give it mouth in this building, till you’re

[006-166] called upon. You have got some building of your own I dare say, now?’

[006-167] ‘Perhaps so,’ replied Mr. Bounderby, rattling his money and laughing.

[006-168] ‘Then give it mouth in your own building, will you, if you please?’ said

[006-169] Childers. ‘Because this isn’t a strong building, and too much of you

[006-170] might bring it down!’

[006-171] Eyeing Mr. Bounderby from head to foot again, he turned from him, as from

[006-172] a man finally disposed of, to Mr. Gradgrind.

[006-173] ‘Jupe sent his daughter out on an errand not an hour ago, and then was

[006-174] seen to slip out himself, with his hat over his eyes, and a bundle tied

[006-175] up in a handkerchief under his arm. She will never believe it of him,

[006-176] but he has cut away and left her.’

[006-177] ‘Pray,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘why will she never believe it of him?’

[006-178] ‘Because those two were one. Because they were never asunder. Because,

[006-179] up to this time, he seemed to dote upon her,’ said Childers, taking a

[006-180] step or two to look into the empty trunk. Both Mr. Childers and Master

[006-181] Kidderminster walked in a curious manner; with their legs wider apart

[006-182] than the general run of men, and with a very knowing assumption of being

[006-183] stiff in the knees. This walk was common to all the male members of

[006-184] [[sleary|Sleary]]’s company, and was understood to express, that they were always on

[006-185] horseback.

[006-186] ‘Poor Sissy! He had better have apprenticed her,’ said Childers, giving

[006-187] his hair another shake, as he looked up from the empty box. ‘Now, he

[006-188] leaves her without anything to take to.’

[006-189] ‘It is creditable to you, who have never been apprenticed, to express

[006-190] that opinion,’ returned Mr. Gradgrind, approvingly.

[006-191] ‘_I_ never apprenticed? I was apprenticed when I was seven year old.’

[006-192] ‘Oh! Indeed?’ said Mr. Gradgrind, rather resentfully, as having been

[006-193] defrauded of his good opinion. ‘I was not aware of its being the custom

[006-194] to apprentice young persons to—’

[006-195] ‘Idleness,’ Mr. Bounderby put in with a loud laugh. ‘No, by the Lord

[006-196] Harry! Nor I!’

[006-197] ‘Her father always had it in his head,’ resumed Childers, feigning

[006-198] unconsciousness of Mr. Bounderby’s existence, ‘that she was to be taught

[006-199] the deuce-and-all of [[education]]. How it got into his head, I can’t say; I

[006-200] can only say that it never got out. He has been picking up a bit of

[006-201] reading for her, here—and a bit of writing for her, there—and a bit of

[006-202] ciphering for her, somewhere else—these seven years.’

[006-203] Mr. E. W. B. Childers took one of his hands out of his pockets, stroked

[006-204] his face and chin, and looked, with a good deal of doubt and a little

[006-205] hope, at Mr. Gradgrind. From the first he had sought to conciliate that

[006-206] gentleman, for the sake of the deserted girl.

[006-207] ‘When Sissy got into the school here,’ he pursued, ‘her father was as

[006-208] pleased as Punch. I couldn’t altogether make out why, myself, as we were

[006-209] not stationary here, being but comers and goers anywhere. I suppose,

[006-210] however, he had this move in his mind—he was always half-cracked—and then

[006-211] considered her provided for. If you should happen to have looked in

[006-212] to-night, for the purpose of telling him that you were going to do her

[006-213] any little service,’ said Mr. Childers, stroking his face again, and

[006-214] repeating his look, ‘it would be very fortunate and well-timed; very

[006-215] fortunate and well-timed.’

[006-216] ‘On the contrary,’ returned Mr. Gradgrind. ‘I came to tell him that her

[006-217] connections made her not an object for the school, and that she must not

[006-218] attend any more. Still, if her father really has left her, without any

[006-219] connivance on her part—Bounderby, let me have a word with you.’

[006-220] Upon this, Mr. Childers politely betook himself, with his equestrian

[006-221] walk, to the landing outside the door, and there stood stroking his face,

[006-222] and softly whistling. While thus engaged, he overheard such phrases in

[006-223] Mr. Bounderby’s voice as ‘No. _I_ say no. I advise you not. I say by

[006-224] no means.’ While, from Mr. Gradgrind, he heard in his much lower tone

[006-226] has been the subject of a vulgar curiosity, leads to and ends in. Think

[006-227] of it, Bounderby, in that point of view.’

[006-228] Meanwhile, the various members of Sleary’s company gradually gathered

[006-229] together from the upper regions, where they were quartered, and, from

[006-230] standing about, talking in low voices to one another and to Mr. Childers,

[006-231] gradually insinuated themselves and him into the room. There were two or

[006-232] three handsome young women among them, with their two or three husbands,

[006-233] and their two or three mothers, and their eight or nine little [[childhood|children]],

[006-234] who did the fairy business when required. The father of one of the

[006-235] families was in the habit of balancing the father of another of the

[006-236] families on the top of a great pole; the father of a third family often

[006-237] made a pyramid of both those fathers, with Master Kidderminster for the

[006-238] apex, and himself for the base; all the fathers could dance upon rolling

[006-239] casks, stand upon bottles, catch knives and balls, twirl hand-basins,

[006-240] ride upon anything, jump over everything, and stick at nothing. All the

[006-241] mothers could (and did) dance, upon the slack wire and the tight-rope,

[006-242] and perform rapid acts on bare-backed steeds; none of them were at all

[006-243] particular in respect of showing their legs; and one of them, alone in a

[006-244] Greek chariot, drove six in hand into every town they came to. They all

[006-245] assumed to be mighty rakish and knowing, they were not very tidy in their

[006-246] private dresses, they were not at all orderly in their domestic

[006-247] arrangements, and the combined literature of the whole company would have

[006-248] produced but a poor letter on any subject. Yet there was a remarkable

[006-249] gentleness and childishness about these people, a special inaptitude for

[006-250] any kind of sharp practice, and an untiring readiness to help and pity

[006-251] one another, deserving often of as much respect, and always of as much

[006-252] generous construction, as the every-day virtues of any [[class]] of people in

[006-253] the world.

[006-255] one fixed eye, and one loose eye, a voice (if it can be called so) like

[006-256] the efforts of a broken old pair of bellows, a flabby surface, and a

[006-257] muddled head which was never sober and never drunk.

[006-260] Thith ith a bad piethe of bithnith, thith ith. You’ve heard of my Clown

[006-261] and hith dog being thuppothed to have morrithed?’

[006-263] ‘Well, Thquire,’ he returned, taking off his hat, and rubbing the lining

[006-265] it your intenthion to do anything for the poor girl, Thquire?’

[006-267] Gradgrind.

[006-268] ‘Glad to hear it, Thquire. Not that I want to get rid of the child, any

[006-269] more than I want to thtand in her way. I’m willing to take her prentith,
