[011-001] NO WAY OUT

[011-002] THE Fairy palaces burst into illumination, before pale morning showed the

[011-003] monstrous serpents of smoke trailing themselves over [[coketown|Coketown]]. A

[011-004] clattering of clogs upon the pavement; a rapid ringing of bells; and all

[011-005] the melancholy mad elephants, polished and oiled up for the day’s

[011-006] monotony, were at their heavy exercise again.

[011-007] [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen]] bent over his loom, quiet, watchful, and steady. A special

[011-008] contrast, as every man was in the forest of looms where Stephen worked,

[011-009] to the crashing, smashing, tearing piece of mechanism at which he

[011-010] laboured. Never fear, good people of an anxious turn of mind, that Art

[011-011] will consign [[nature|Nature]] to oblivion. Set anywhere, side by side, the work of

[011-012] GOD and the work of man; and the former, even though it be a troop of

[011-013] Hands of very small account, will gain in [[pride|dignity]] from the comparison.

[011-014] So many hundred Hands in this Mill; so many hundred horse Steam Power.

[011-015] It is known, to the force of a single pound weight, what the engine will

[011-016] do; but, not all the calculators of the National Debt can tell me the

[011-017] capacity for good or evil, for [[love]] or hatred, for patriotism or

[011-018] discontent, for the decomposition of virtue into vice, or the reverse, at

[011-019] any single moment in the soul of one of these its quiet servants, with

[011-020] the composed faces and the regulated actions. There is no mystery in it;

[011-021] there is an unfathomable mystery in the meanest of them, for

[011-022] ever.—Supposing we were to reverse our arithmetic for material objects,

[011-023] and to govern these awful unknown quantities by other means!

[011-024] The day grew strong, and showed itself outside, even against the flaming

[011-025] lights within. The lights were turned out, and the work went on. The

[011-026] rain fell, and the Smoke-serpents, submissive to the curse of all that

[011-027] tribe, trailed themselves upon the earth. In the waste-yard outside, the

[011-028] steam from the escape pipe, the litter of barrels and old iron, the

[011-029] shining heaps of coals, the ashes everywhere, were shrouded in a veil of

[011-030] mist and rain.

[011-031] The work went on, until the noon-bell rang. More clattering upon the

[011-032] pavements. The looms, and wheels, and Hands all out of gear for an hour.

[011-033] Stephen came out of the hot mill into the damp wind and cold wet streets,

[011-034] haggard and worn. He turned from his own [[class]] and his own quarter,

[011-035] taking nothing but a little bread as he walked along, towards the hill on

[011-036] which his principal employer lived, in a red house with black outside

[011-037] shutters, green inside blinds, a black street door, up two white steps,

[011-038] BOUNDERBY (in letters very like himself) upon a brazen plate, and a round

[011-039] brazen door-handle underneath it, like a brazen full-stop.

[011-040] [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] was at his lunch. So Stephen had expected. Would his

[011-041] servant say that one of the Hands begged leave to speak to him? Message

[011-042] in return, requiring name of such Hand. Stephen Blackpool. There was

[011-043] nothing troublesome against Stephen Blackpool; yes, he might come in.

[011-044] Stephen Blackpool in the parlour. Mr. Bounderby (whom he just knew by

[011-045] sight), at lunch on chop and sherry. [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] netting at the

[011-046] fireside, in a side-saddle attitude, with one foot in a cotton stirrup.

[011-047] It was a part, at once of Mrs. Sparsit’s dignity and service, not to

[011-048] lunch. She supervised the meal officially, but implied that in her own

[011-049] stately person she considered lunch a weakness.

[011-050] ‘Now, Stephen,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘what’s the matter with _you_?’

[011-051] Stephen made a bow. Not a servile one—these Hands will never do that!

[011-052] Lord bless you, sir, you’ll never catch them at that, if they have been

[011-053] with you twenty years!—and, as a complimentary toilet for Mrs. Sparsit,

[011-054] tucked his neckerchief ends into his waistcoat.

[011-055] ‘Now, you know,’ said Mr. Bounderby, taking some sherry, ‘we have never

[011-056] had any difficulty with you, and you have never been one of the

[011-057] unreasonable ones. You don’t expect to be set up in a coach and six, and

[011-058] to be fed on turtle soup and venison, with a gold spoon, as a good many

[011-059] of ’em do!’ Mr. Bounderby always represented this to be the sole,

[011-060] immediate, and direct object of any Hand who was not entirely satisfied;

[011-061] ‘and therefore I know already that you have not come here to make a

[011-062] complaint. Now, you know, I am certain of that, beforehand.’

[011-063] ‘No, sir, sure I ha’ not coom for nowt o’ th’ kind.’

[011-064] Mr. Bounderby seemed agreeably surprised, notwithstanding his previous

[011-065] strong conviction. ‘Very well,’ he returned. ‘You’re a steady Hand, and

[011-066] I was not mistaken. Now, let me hear what it’s all about. As it’s not

[011-067] that, let me hear what it is. What have you got to say? Out with it,

[011-068] lad!’

[011-069] Stephen happened to glance towards Mrs. Sparsit. ‘I can go, Mr.

[011-070] Bounderby, if you wish it,’ said that self-sacrificing lady, making a

[011-071] feint of taking her foot out of the stirrup.

[011-072] Mr. Bounderby stayed her, by holding a mouthful of chop in suspension

[011-073] before swallowing it, and putting out his left hand. Then, withdrawing

[011-074] his hand and swallowing his mouthful of chop, he said to Stephen:

[011-075] ‘Now you know, this good lady is a born lady, a high lady. You are not

[011-076] to suppose because she keeps my house for me, that she hasn’t been very

[011-077] high up the tree—ah, up at the top of the tree! Now, if you have got

[011-078] anything to say that can’t be said before a born lady, this lady will

[011-079] leave the room. If what you have got to say _can_ be said before a born

[011-080] lady, this lady will stay where she is.’

[011-081] ‘Sir, I hope I never had nowt to say, not fitten for a born lady to year,

[011-082] sin’ I were born mysen’,’ was the reply, accompanied with a slight flush.

[011-083] ‘Very well,’ said Mr. Bounderby, pushing away his plate, and leaning

[011-084] back. ‘Fire away!’

[011-085] ‘I ha’ coom,’ Stephen began, raising his eyes from the floor, after a

[011-086] moment’s consideration, ‘to ask yo yor advice. I need ’t overmuch. I

[011-087] were married on Eas’r Monday nineteen year sin, long and dree. She were

[011-088] a young lass—pretty enow—wi’ good accounts of herseln. Well! She went

[011-089] bad—soon. Not along of me. Gonnows I were not a unkind husband to her.’

[011-090] ‘I have heard all this before,’ said Mr. Bounderby. ‘She took to

[011-091] drinking, left off working, sold the furniture, pawned the clothes, and

[011-092] played old Gooseberry.’

[011-093] ‘I were patient wi’ her.’

[011-094] (‘The more fool you, I think,’ said Mr. Bounderby, in confidence to his

[011-095] wine-glass.)

[011-096] ‘I were very patient wi’ her. I tried to wean her fra ’t ower and ower

[011-097] agen. I tried this, I tried that, I tried t’other. I ha’ gone home,

[011-098] many’s the time, and found all vanished as I had in the world, and her

[011-099] without a sense left to bless herseln lying on bare ground. I ha’ dun ’t

[011-100] not once, not twice—twenty time!’

[011-101] Every line in his face deepened as he said it, and put in its affecting

[011-102] evidence of the suffering he had undergone.

[011-103] ‘From bad to worse, from worse to worsen. She left me. She disgraced

[011-104] herseln everyways, bitter and bad. She coom back, she coom back, she

[011-105] coom back. What could I do t’ hinder her? I ha’ walked the streets

[011-106] nights long, ere ever I’d go home. I ha’ gone t’ th’ brigg, minded to

[011-107] fling myseln ower, and ha’ no more on’t. I ha’ bore that much, that I

[011-108] were owd when I were young.’

[011-109] Mrs. Sparsit, easily ambling along with her netting-needles, raised the

[011-110] Coriolanian eyebrows and shook her [[reason|head]], as much as to say, ‘The great

[011-111] know trouble as well as the small. Please to turn your humble eye in My

[011-112] direction.’

[011-113] ‘I ha’ paid her to keep awa’ fra’ me. These five year I ha’ paid her. I

[011-114] ha’ gotten decent fewtrils about me agen. I ha’ lived hard and sad, but

[011-115] not [[shame|ashamed]] and fearfo’ a’ the minnits o’ my life. Last night, I went

[011-116] home. There she lay upon my har-stone! There she is!’

[011-117] In the strength of his misfortune, and the energy of his distress, he

[011-118] fired for the moment like a proud man. In another moment, he stood as he

[011-119] had stood all the time—his usual stoop upon him; his pondering face

[011-120] addressed to Mr. Bounderby, with a curious expression on it, half shrewd,

[011-121] half perplexed, as if his mind were set upon unravelling something very

[011-122] difficult; his hat held tight in his left hand, which rested on his hip;

[011-123] his right arm, with a rugged propriety and force of action, very

[011-124] earnestly emphasizing what he said: not least so when it always paused, a

[011-125] little bent, but not withdrawn, as he paused.

[011-126] ‘I was acquainted with all this, you know,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘except

[011-127] the last clause, long ago. It’s a bad job; that’s what it is. You had

[011-128] better have been satisfied as you were, and not have got married.

[011-129] However, it’s too late to say that.’

[011-130] ‘Was it an unequal [[marriage]], sir, in point of years?’ asked Mrs. Sparsit.

[011-131] ‘You hear what this lady asks. Was it an unequal marriage in point of

[011-132] years, this unlucky job of yours?’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[011-133] ‘Not e’en so. I were one-and-twenty myseln; she were twenty nighbut.’

[011-134] ‘Indeed, sir?’ said Mrs. Sparsit to her Chief, with great placidity. ‘I

[011-135] inferred, from its being so miserable a marriage, that it was probably an

[011-136] unequal one in point of years.’

[011-137] Mr. Bounderby looked very hard at the good lady in a side-long way that

[011-138] had an odd sheepishness about it. He fortified himself with a little

[011-139] more sherry.

[011-140] ‘Well? Why don’t you go on?’ he then asked, turning rather irritably on

[011-141] Stephen Blackpool.

[011-142] ‘I ha’ coom to ask yo, sir, how I am to be ridded o’ this woman.’

[011-143] Stephen infused a yet deeper gravity into the mixed expression of his

[011-144] attentive face. Mrs. Sparsit uttered a gentle ejaculation, as having

[011-145] received a moral shock.

[011-146] ‘What do you mean?’ said Bounderby, getting up to lean his back against

[011-147] the chimney-piece. ‘What are you talking about? You took her for better

[011-148] for worse.’

[011-149] ‘I mun’ be ridden o’ her. I cannot bear ’t nommore. I ha’ lived under

[011-150] ’t so long, for that I ha’ had’n the pity and comforting words o’ th’

[011-151] best lass living or dead. Haply, but for her, I should ha’ gone

[011-152] battering mad.’

[011-153] ‘He wishes to be free, to marry the female of whom he speaks, I fear,

[011-154] sir,’ observed Mrs. Sparsit in an undertone, and much dejected by the

[011-155] immorality of the people.

[011-156] ‘I do. The lady says what’s right. I do. I were a coming to ’t. I ha’

[011-157] read i’ th’ papers that great folk (fair faw ’em a’! I wishes ’em no

[011-158] hurt!) are not bonded together for better for worst so fast, but that

[011-159] they can be set free fro’ _their_ misfortnet marriages, an’ marry ower

[011-160] agen. When they dunnot agree, for that their tempers is ill-sorted, they

[011-161] has rooms o’ one kind an’ another in their houses, above a bit, and they

[011-162] can live asunders. We fok ha’ only one room, and we can’t. When that

[011-163] won’t do, they ha’ gowd an’ other cash, an’ they can say “This for yo’

[011-164] an’ that for me,” an’ they can go their separate ways. We can’t. Spite

[011-165] o’ all that, they can be set free for smaller wrongs than mine. So, I

[011-166] mun be ridden o’ this woman, and I want t’ know how?’

[011-167] ‘No how,’ returned Mr. Bounderby.

[011-168] ‘If I do her any hurt, sir, there’s a law to punish me?’

[011-169] ‘Of course there is.’

[011-170] ‘If I flee from her, there’s a law to punish me?’

[011-171] ‘Of course there is.’

[011-172] ‘If I marry t’oother dear lass, there’s a law to punish me?’

[011-173] ‘Of course there is.’

[011-174] ‘If I was to live wi’ her an’ not marry her—saying such a thing could be,

[011-175] which it never could or would, an’ her so good—there’s a law to punish

[011-176] me, in every innocent [[childhood|child]] belonging to me?’

[011-177] ‘Of course there is.’

[011-178] ‘Now, a’ God’s name,’ said Stephen Blackpool, ‘[[performance|show]] me the law to help

[011-179] me!’

[011-180] ‘Hem! There’s a sanctity in this relation of life,’ said Mr. Bounderby,

[011-181] ‘and—and—it must be kept up.’

[011-182] ‘No no, dunnot say that, sir. ’Tan’t kep’ up that way. Not that way.

[011-183] ’Tis kep’ down that way. I’m a weaver, I were in a [[facts|fact]]’ry when a chilt,

[011-184] but I ha’ gotten een to see wi’ and eern to year wi’. I read in th’

[011-185] papers every ’Sizes, every Sessions—and you read too—I know it!—with

[011-186] dismay—how th’ supposed unpossibility o’ ever getting unchained from one

[011-187] another, at any price, on any terms, brings blood upon this land, and

[011-188] brings many common married fok to battle, murder, and sudden death. Let

[011-189] us ha’ this, right understood. Mine’s a grievous case, an’ I want—if yo

[011-190] will be so good—t’ know the law that helps me.’

[011-191] ‘Now, I tell you what!’ said Mr. Bounderby, putting his hands in his

[011-192] pockets. ‘There _is_ such a law.’

[011-193] Stephen, subsiding into his quiet manner, and never wandering in his

[011-194] attention, gave a nod.

[011-195] ‘But it’s not for you at all. It costs money. It costs a mint of

[011-196] money.’

[011-197] ‘How much might that be?’ Stephen calmly asked.

[011-198] ‘Why, you’d have to go to Doctors’ Commons with a suit, and you’d have to

[011-199] go to a court of Common Law with a suit, and you’d have to go to the

[011-200] House of Lords with a suit, and you’d have to get an Act of Parliament to

[011-201] enable you to marry again, and it would cost you (if it was a case of

[011-202] very plain sailing), I suppose from a thousand to fifteen hundred pound,’

[011-203] said Mr. Bounderby. ‘Perhaps twice the money.’

[011-204] ‘There’s no other law?’

[011-205] ‘Certainly not.’

[011-206] ‘Why then, sir,’ said Stephen, turning white, and motioning with that

[011-207] right hand of his, as if he gave everything to the four winds, ‘’_tis_ a

[011-208] muddle. ’Tis just a muddle a’toogether, an’ the sooner I am dead, the

[011-209] better.’

[011-210] (Mrs. Sparsit again dejected by the impiety of the people.)

[011-211] ‘Pooh, pooh! Don’t you talk nonsense, my good fellow,’ said Mr.

[011-212] Bounderby, ‘about things you don’t understand; and don’t you call the

[011-213] Institutions of your country a muddle, or you’ll get yourself into a real

[011-214] muddle one of these fine mornings. The institutions of your country are

[011-215] not your piece-work, and the only thing you have got to do, is, to mind

[011-216] your piece-work. You didn’t take your wife for fast and for loose; but

[011-217] for better for worse. If she has turned out worse—why, all we have got

[011-218] to say is, she might have turned out better.’

[011-219] ‘’Tis a muddle,’ said Stephen, shaking his head as he moved to the door.

[011-220] ‘’Tis a’ a muddle!’

[011-221] ‘Now, I’ll tell you what!’ Mr. Bounderby resumed, as a valedictory

[011-222] address. ‘With what I shall call your unhallowed opinions, you have been

[011-223] quite shocking this lady: who, as I have already told you, is a born

[011-224] lady, and who, as I have not already told you, has had her own marriage

[011-225] misfortunes to the tune of tens of thousands of pounds—tens of Thousands

[011-226] of Pounds!’ (he repeated it with great relish). ‘Now, you have always

[011-227] been a steady Hand hitherto; but my opinion is, and so I tell you

[011-228] plainly, that you are turning into the wrong road. You have been

[011-229] listening to some mischievous stranger or other—they’re always about—and

[011-230] the best thing you can do is, to come out of that. Now you know;’ here

[011-231] his countenance expressed marvellous acuteness; ‘I can see as far into a

[011-232] grindstone as another man; farther than a good many, perhaps, because I

[011-233] had my nose well kept to it when I was young. I see traces of the turtle

[011-234] soup, and venison, and gold spoon in this. Yes, I do!’ cried Mr.

[011-235] Bounderby, shaking his head with obstinate cunning. ‘By the Lord Harry,

[011-236] I do!’

[011-237] With a very different shake of the head and deep sigh, Stephen said,

[011-238] ‘Thank you, sir, I wish you good day.’ So he left Mr. Bounderby swelling

[011-239] at his own portrait on the wall, as if he were going to explode himself

[011-240] into it; and Mrs. Sparsit still ambling on with her foot in her stirrup,

[011-241] looking quite cast down by the popular vices.
