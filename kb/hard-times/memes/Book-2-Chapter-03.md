[019-001] THE WHELP

[019-002] IT was very remarkable that a young [[gentleman]] who had been brought up

[019-003] under one continuous system of [[nature|unnatural]] restraint, should be a

[019-004] hypocrite; but it was certainly the case with [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]]. It was very strange

[019-005] that a young gentleman who had never been left to his own guidance for

[019-006] five consecutive minutes, should be incapable at last of governing

[019-007] himself; but so it was with Tom. It was altogether unaccountable that a

[019-008] young gentleman whose [[imagination]] had been strangled in his cradle,

[019-009] should be still inconvenienced by its ghost in the form of grovelling

[019-010] sensualities; but such a monster, beyond all doubt, was Tom.

[019-011] ‘Do you smoke?’ asked [[james-harthouse|Mr. James Harthouse]], when they came to the hotel.

[019-012] ‘I believe you!’ said Tom.

[019-013] He could do no less than ask Tom up; and Tom could do no less than go up.

[019-014] What with a cooling drink adapted to the weather, but not so weak as

[019-015] cool; and what with a rarer tobacco than was to be bought in those parts;

[019-016] Tom was soon in a highly free and easy state at his end of the sofa, and

[019-017] more than ever disposed to admire his new friend at the other end.

[019-018] Tom blew his smoke aside, after he had been smoking a little while, and

[019-019] took an observation of his friend. ‘He don’t seem to care about his

[019-020] dress,’ thought Tom, ‘and yet how capitally he does it. What an easy

[019-021] swell he is!’

[019-022] Mr. James Harthouse, happening to catch Tom’s eye, remarked that he drank

[019-023] nothing, and filled his glass with his own negligent hand.

[019-024] ‘Thank’ee,’ said Tom. ‘Thank’ee. Well, Mr. Harthouse, I hope you have

[019-025] had about a dose of old [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]] to-night.’ Tom said this with one eye

[019-026] shut up again, and looking over his glass knowingly, at his entertainer.

[019-027] ‘A very good fellow indeed!’ returned Mr. James Harthouse.

[019-028] ‘You think so, don’t you?’ said Tom. And shut up his eye again.

[019-029] Mr. James Harthouse smiled; and rising from his end of the sofa, and

[019-030] lounging with his back against the chimney-piece, so that he stood before

[019-031] the empty fire-grate as he smoked, in front of Tom and looking down at

[019-032] him, observed:

[019-033] ‘What a comical brother-in-law you are!’

[019-034] ‘What a comical brother-in-law old Bounderby is, I think you mean,’ said

[019-035] Tom.

[019-036] ‘You are a piece of caustic, Tom,’ retorted Mr. James Harthouse.

[019-037] There was something so very agreeable in being so intimate with such a

[019-038] waistcoat; in being called Tom, in such an intimate way, by such a voice;

[019-039] in being on such off-hand terms so soon, with such a pair of whiskers;

[019-040] that Tom was uncommonly pleased with himself.

[019-041] ‘Oh! I don’t care for old Bounderby,’ said he, ‘if you mean that. I

[019-042] have always called old Bounderby by the same name when I have talked

[019-043] about him, and I have always thought of him in the same way. I am not

[019-044] going to begin to be polite now, about old Bounderby. It would be rather

[019-045] late in the day.’

[019-046] ‘Don’t mind me,’ returned James; ‘but take care when his wife is by, you

[019-047] know.’

[019-048] ‘His wife?’ said Tom. ‘My sister [[louisa-gradgrind|Loo]]? O yes!’ And he laughed, and took

[019-049] a little more of the cooling drink.

[019-050] James Harthouse continued to lounge in the same place and attitude,

[019-051] smoking his cigar in his own easy way, and looking pleasantly at the

[019-052] whelp, as if he knew himself to be a kind of agreeable demon who had only

[019-053] to hover over him, and he must give up his whole soul if required. It

[019-054] certainly did seem that the whelp yielded to this influence. He looked

[019-055] at his companion sneakingly, he looked at him admiringly, he looked at

[019-056] him boldly, and put up one leg on the sofa.

[019-057] ‘My sister Loo?’ said Tom. ‘_She_ never cared for old Bounderby.’

[019-058] ‘That’s the past tense, Tom,’ returned Mr. James Harthouse, striking the

[019-059] ash from his cigar with his little finger. ‘We are in the present tense,

[019-060] now.’

[019-061] ‘Verb neuter, not to care. Indicative mood, present tense. First person

[019-062] singular, I do not care; second person singular, thou dost not care;

[019-063] third person singular, she does not care,’ returned Tom.

[019-064] ‘Good! Very quaint!’ said his friend. ‘Though you don’t mean it.’

[019-065] ‘But I _do_ mean it,’ cried Tom. ‘Upon my honour! Why, you won’t tell

[019-066] me, Mr. Harthouse, that you really suppose my sister Loo does care for

[019-067] old Bounderby.’

[019-068] ‘My [[love|dear]] fellow,’ returned the other, ‘what am I bound to suppose, when I

[019-069] find two married people living in harmony and happiness?’

[019-070] Tom had by this time got both his legs on the sofa. If his second leg

[019-071] had not been already there when he was called a dear fellow, he would

[019-072] have put it up at that great stage of the conversation. Feeling it

[019-073] necessary to do something then, he stretched himself out at greater

[019-074] length, and, reclining with the back of his [[reason|head]] on the end of the sofa,

[019-075] and smoking with an infinite assumption of negligence, turned his common

[019-076] face, and not too sober eyes, towards the face looking down upon him so

[019-077] carelessly yet so potently.

[019-078] ‘You know our governor, Mr. Harthouse,’ said Tom, ‘and therefore, you

[019-079] needn’t be surprised that Loo married old Bounderby. She never had a

[019-080] lover, and the governor proposed old Bounderby, and she took him.’

[019-081] ‘Very dutiful in your interesting sister,’ said Mr. James Harthouse.

[019-082] ‘Yes, but she wouldn’t have been as dutiful, and it would not have come

[019-083] off as easily,’ returned the whelp, ‘if it hadn’t been for me.’

[019-084] The tempter merely lifted his eyebrows; but the whelp was obliged to go

[019-085] on.

[019-086] ‘_I_ persuaded her,’ he said, with an edifying air of superiority. ‘I

[019-087] was stuck into old Bounderby’s bank (where I never wanted to be), and I

[019-088] knew I should get into scrapes there, if she put old Bounderby’s pipe

[019-089] out; so I told her my wishes, and she came into them. She would do

[019-090] anything for me. It was very game of her, wasn’t it?’

[019-091] ‘It was charming, Tom!’

[019-092] ‘Not that it was altogether so important to her as it was to me,’

[019-093] continued Tom coolly, ‘because my liberty and comfort, and perhaps my

[019-094] getting on, depended on it; and she had no other lover, and staying at

[019-095] home was like staying in jail—especially when I was gone. It wasn’t as

[019-096] if she gave up another lover for old Bounderby; but still it was a good

[019-097] thing in her.’

[019-098] ‘Perfectly delightful. And she gets on so placidly.’

[019-099] ‘Oh,’ returned Tom, with contemptuous patronage, ‘she’s a regular girl.

[019-100] A girl can get on anywhere. She has settled down to the life, and _she_

[019-101] don’t mind. It does just as well as another. Besides, though Loo is a

[019-102] girl, she’s not a common sort of girl. She can shut herself up within

[019-103] herself, and think—as I have often known her sit and watch the fire—for

[019-104] an hour at a stretch.’

[019-105] ‘Ay, ay? Has resources of her own,’ said Harthouse, smoking quietly.

[019-106] ‘Not so much of that as you may suppose,’ returned Tom; ‘for our governor

[019-107] had her crammed with all sorts of dry bones and sawdust. It’s his

[019-108] system.’

[019-109] ‘Formed his daughter on his own model?’ suggested Harthouse.

[019-110] ‘His daughter? Ah! and everybody else. Why, he formed Me that way!’

[019-111] said Tom.

[019-112] ‘Impossible!’

[019-113] ‘He did, though,’ said Tom, shaking his head. ‘I mean to say, Mr.

[019-114] Harthouse, that when I first left home and went to old Bounderby’s, I was

[019-115] as flat as a warming-pan, and knew no more about life, than any oyster

[019-116] does.’

[019-117] ‘Come, Tom! I can hardly believe that. A joke’s a joke.’

[019-118] ‘Upon my soul!’ said the whelp. ‘I am serious; I am indeed!’ He smoked

[019-119] with great gravity and [[pride|dignity]] for a little while, and then added, in a

[019-120] highly complacent tone, ‘Oh! I have picked up a little since. I don’t

[019-121] deny that. But I have done it myself; no thanks to the governor.’

[019-122] ‘And your intelligent sister?’

[019-123] ‘My intelligent sister is about where she was. She used to complain to

[019-124] me that she had nothing to fall back upon, that girls usually fall back

[019-125] upon; and I don’t see how she is to have got over that since. But _she_

[019-126] don’t mind,’ he sagaciously added, puffing at his cigar again. ‘Girls

[019-127] can always get on, somehow.’

[019-128] ‘Calling at the Bank yesterday evening, for Mr. Bounderby’s address, I

[019-129] found an ancient lady there, who seems to entertain great admiration for

[019-130] your sister,’ observed Mr. James Harthouse, throwing away the last small

[019-131] remnant of the cigar he had now smoked out.

[019-132] ‘Mother [[mrs-sparsit|Sparsit]]!’ said Tom. ‘What! you have seen her already, have you?’

[019-133] His friend nodded. Tom took his cigar out of his mouth, to shut up his

[019-134] eye (which had grown rather unmanageable) with the greater expression,

[019-135] and to tap his nose several times with his finger.

[019-136] ‘Mother Sparsit’s [[heart|feeling]] for Loo is more than admiration, I should

[019-137] think,’ said Tom. ‘Say affection and devotion. Mother Sparsit never set

[019-138] her cap at Bounderby when he was a bachelor. Oh no!’

[019-139] These were the last words spoken by the whelp, before a giddy drowsiness

[019-140] came upon him, followed by complete oblivion. He was roused from the

[019-141] latter state by an uneasy dream of being stirred up with a boot, and also

[019-142] of a voice saying: ‘Come, it’s late. Be off!’

[019-143] ‘Well!’ he said, scrambling from the sofa. ‘I must take my leave of you

[019-144] though. I say. Yours is very good tobacco. But it’s too mild.’

[019-145] ‘Yes, it’s too mild,’ returned his entertainer.

[019-146] ‘It’s—it’s ridiculously mild,’ said Tom. ‘Where’s the door! Good

[019-147] night!’

[019-148] He had another odd dream of being taken by a waiter through a mist,

[019-149] which, after giving him some trouble and difficulty, resolved itself into

[019-150] the main street, in which he stood alone. He then walked home pretty

[019-151] easily, though not yet free from an impression of the presence and

[019-152] influence of his new friend—as if he were lounging somewhere in the air,

[019-153] in the same negligent attitude, regarding him with the same look.

[019-154] The whelp went home, and went to bed. If he had had any sense of what he

[019-155] had done that night, and had been less of a whelp and more of a brother,

[019-156] he might have turned short on the road, might have gone down to the

[019-157] ill-smelling river that was dyed black, might have gone to bed in it for

[019-158] good and all, and have curtained his head for ever with its filthy

[019-159] waters.
