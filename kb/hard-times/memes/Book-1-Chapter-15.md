[015-001] FATHER AND DAUGHTER

[015-002] ALTHOUGH [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] did not take after Blue Beard, his room was quite

[015-003] a blue chamber in its abundance of blue books. Whatever they could prove

[015-004] (which is usually anything you like), they proved there, in an army

[015-005] constantly strengthening by the arrival of new recruits. In that charmed

[015-006] apartment, the most complicated social questions were cast up, got into

[015-007] exact totals, and finally settled—if those concerned could only have been

[015-008] brought to know it. As if an astronomical observatory should be made

[015-009] without any windows, and the astronomer within should arrange the starry

[015-010] universe solely by pen, ink, and paper, so Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], in _his_

[015-011] Observatory (and there are many like it), had no need to cast an eye upon

[015-012] the teeming myriads of human beings around him, but could settle all

[015-013] their destinies on a slate, and wipe out all their tears with one dirty

[015-014] little bit of sponge.

[015-015] To this Observatory, then: a stern room, with a deadly statistical clock

[015-016] in it, which measured every second with a beat like a rap upon a

[015-017] coffin-lid; [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] repaired on the appointed morning. A window looked

[015-018] towards [[coketown|Coketown]]; and when she sat down near her father’s table, she saw

[015-019] the high chimneys and the long tracts of smoke looming in the heavy

[015-020] distance gloomily.

[015-021] ‘My [[love|dear]] Louisa,’ said her father, ‘I prepared you last night to give me

[015-022] your serious attention in the conversation we are now going to have

[015-023] together. You have been so well trained, and you do, I am happy to say,

[015-024] so much justice to the [[education]] you have received, that I have perfect

[015-025] confidence in your good sense. You are not impulsive, you are not

[015-026] romantic, you are accustomed to view everything from the strong

[015-027] dispassionate ground of [[reason]] and calculation. From that ground alone,

[015-028] I know you will view and consider what I am going to communicate.’

[015-029] He waited, as if he would have been glad that she said something. But

[015-030] she said never a word.

[015-031] ‘Louisa, my dear, you are the subject of a proposal of [[marriage]] that has

[015-032] been made to me.’

[015-033] Again he waited, and again she answered not one word. This so far

[015-034] surprised him, as to induce him gently to repeat, ‘a proposal of

[015-035] marriage, my dear.’ To which she returned, without any visible [[heart|emotion]]

[015-036] whatever:

[015-037] ‘I hear you, father. I am attending, I assure you.’

[015-038] ‘Well!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, breaking into a smile, after being for the

[015-039] moment at a loss, ‘you are even more dispassionate than I expected,

[015-040] Louisa. Or, perhaps, you are not unprepared for the announcement I have

[015-041] it in charge to make?’

[015-042] ‘I cannot say that, father, until I hear it. Prepared or unprepared, I

[015-043] wish to hear it all from you. I wish to hear you state it to me,

[015-044] father.’

[015-045] Strange to relate, Mr. Gradgrind was not so collected at this moment as

[015-046] his daughter was. He took a paper-knife in his hand, turned it over,

[015-047] laid it down, took it up again, and even then had to look along the blade

[015-048] of it, considering how to go on.

[015-049] ‘What you say, my dear Louisa, is perfectly reasonable. I have

[015-050] undertaken then to let you know that—in short, that [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] has

[015-051] informed me that he has long watched your progress with particular

[015-052] interest and pleasure, and has long hoped that the time might ultimately

[015-053] arrive when he should offer you his hand in marriage. That time, to

[015-054] which he has so long, and certainly with great constancy, looked forward,

[015-055] is now come. Mr. Bounderby has made his proposal of marriage to me, and

[015-056] has entreated me to make it known to you, and to express his hope that

[015-057] you will take it into your favourable consideration.’

[015-058] Silence between them. The deadly statistical clock very hollow. The

[015-059] distant smoke very black and heavy.

[015-060] ‘Father,’ said Louisa, ‘do you think I love Mr. Bounderby?’

[015-061] Mr. Gradgrind was extremely discomfited by this unexpected question.

[015-062] ‘Well, my [[childhood|child]],’ he returned, ‘I—really—cannot take upon myself to say.’

[015-063] ‘Father,’ pursued Louisa in exactly the same voice as before, ‘do you ask

[015-064] me to love Mr. Bounderby?’

[015-065] ‘My dear Louisa, no. No. I ask nothing.’

[015-066] ‘Father,’ she still pursued, ‘does Mr. Bounderby ask me to love him?’

[015-067] ‘Really, my dear,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘it is difficult to answer your

[015-068] question—’

[015-069] ‘Difficult to answer it, Yes or No, father?

[015-070] ‘Certainly, my dear. Because;’ here was something to demonstrate, and it

[015-071] set him up again; ‘because the reply depends so materially, Louisa, on

[015-072] the sense in which we use the expression. Now, Mr. Bounderby does not do

[015-073] you the injustice, and does not do himself the injustice, of pretending

[015-074] to anything fanciful, fantastic, or (I am using synonymous terms)

[015-075] sentimental. Mr. Bounderby would have seen you grow up under his eyes,

[015-076] to very little purpose, if he could so far forget what is due to your

[015-077] good sense, not to say to his, as to address you from any such ground.

[015-078] Therefore, perhaps the expression itself—I merely suggest this to you, my

[015-079] dear—may be a little misplaced.’

[015-080] ‘What would you advise me to use in its stead, father?’

[015-081] ‘Why, my dear Louisa,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, completely recovered by this

[015-082] time, ‘I would advise you (since you ask me) to consider this question,

[015-083] as you have been accustomed to consider every other question, simply as

[015-084] one of tangible Fact. The ignorant and the giddy may embarrass such

[015-085] subjects with irrelevant [[fancy|fancies]], and other absurdities that have no

[015-086] existence, properly viewed—really no existence—but it is no compliment to

[015-087] you to say, that you know better. Now, what are the [[facts|Facts]] of this case?

[015-088] You are, we will say in round numbers, twenty years of age; Mr. Bounderby

[015-089] is, we will say in round numbers, fifty. There is some disparity in your

[015-090] respective years, but in your means and positions there is none; on the

[015-091] contrary, there is a great suitability. Then the question arises, Is

[015-092] this one disparity sufficient to operate as a bar to such a marriage? In

[015-093] considering this question, it is not unimportant to take into account the

[015-094] statistics of marriage, so far as they have yet been obtained, in England

[015-095] and Wales. I find, on reference to the figures, that a large proportion

[015-096] of these marriages are contracted between parties of very unequal ages,

[015-097] and that the elder of these contracting parties is, in rather more than

[015-098] three-fourths of these instances, the bridegroom. It is remarkable as

[015-099] showing the wide prevalence of this law, that among the natives of the

[015-100] British possessions in India, also in a considerable part of China, and

[015-101] among the Calmucks of Tartary, the best means of computation yet

[015-102] furnished us by travellers, yield similar results. The disparity I have

[015-103] mentioned, therefore, almost ceases to be disparity, and (virtually) all

[015-104] but disappears.’

[015-105] ‘What do you recommend, father,’ asked Louisa, her reserved composure not

[015-106] in the least affected by these gratifying results, ‘that I should

[015-107] substitute for the term I used just now? For the misplaced expression?’

[015-108] ‘Louisa,’ returned her father, ‘it appears to me that nothing can be

[015-109] plainer. Confining yourself rigidly to Fact, the question of Fact you

[015-110] state to yourself is: Does Mr. Bounderby ask me to marry him? Yes, he

[015-111] does. The sole remaining question then is: Shall I marry him? I think

[015-112] nothing can be plainer than that?’

[015-113] ‘Shall I marry him?’ repeated Louisa, with great deliberation.

[015-114] ‘Precisely. And it is satisfactory to me, as your father, my dear

[015-115] Louisa, to know that you do not come to the consideration of that

[015-116] question with the previous habits of mind, and habits of life, that

[015-117] belong to many young women.’

[015-118] ‘No, father,’ she returned, ‘I do not.’

[015-119] ‘I now leave you to judge for yourself,’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘I have

[015-120] stated the case, as such cases are usually stated among practical minds;

[015-121] I have stated it, as the case of your mother and myself was stated in its

[015-122] time. The rest, my dear Louisa, is for you to decide.’

[015-123] From the beginning, she had sat looking at him fixedly. As he now leaned

[015-124] back in his chair, and bent his deep-set eyes upon her in his turn,

[015-125] perhaps he might have seen one wavering moment in her, when she was

[015-126] impelled to throw herself upon his breast, and give him the pent-up

[015-127] confidences of her heart. But, to see it, he must have overleaped at a

[015-128] bound the artificial barriers he had for many years been erecting,

[015-129] between himself and all those subtle essences of [[humanity]] which will

[015-130] elude the utmost cunning of algebra until the last trumpet ever to be

[015-131] sounded shall blow even algebra to wreck. The barriers were too many and

[015-132] too high for such a leap. With his unbending, utilitarian,

[015-133] matter-of-fact face, he hardened her again; and the moment shot away into

[015-134] the plumbless depths of the past, to mingle with all the lost

[015-135] opportunities that are drowned there.

[015-136] Removing her eyes from him, she sat so long looking silently towards the

[015-137] town, that he said, at length: ‘Are you consulting the chimneys of the

[015-138] Coketown works, Louisa?’

[015-139] ‘There seems to be nothing there but languid and monotonous smoke. Yet

[015-140] when the night comes, Fire bursts out, father!’ she answered, turning

[015-141] quickly.

[015-142] ‘Of course I know that, Louisa. I do not see the application of the

[015-143] remark.’ To do him justice he did not, at all.

[015-144] She passed it away with a slight motion of her hand, and concentrating

[015-145] her attention upon him again, said, ‘Father, I have often thought that

[015-146] life is very short.’—This was so distinctly one of his subjects that he

[015-147] interposed.

[015-148] ‘It is short, no doubt, my dear. Still, the average duration of human

[015-149] life is proved to have increased of late years. The calculations of

[015-150] various life assurance and annuity offices, among other figures which

[015-151] cannot go wrong, have established the fact.’

[015-152] ‘I speak of my own life, father.’

[015-153] ‘O indeed? Still,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘I need not point out to you,

[015-154] Louisa, that it is governed by the laws which govern lives in the

[015-155] aggregate.’

[015-156] ‘While it lasts, I would wish to do the little I can, and the little I am

[015-157] fit for. What does it matter?’

[015-158] Mr. Gradgrind seemed rather at a loss to understand the last four words;

[015-159] replying, ‘How, matter? What matter, my dear?’

[015-160] ‘Mr. Bounderby,’ she went on in a steady, straight way, without regarding

[015-161] this, ‘asks me to marry him. The question I have to ask myself is, shall

[015-162] I marry him? That is so, father, is it not? You have told me so,

[015-163] father. Have you not?’

[015-164] ‘Certainly, my dear.’

[015-165] ‘Let it be so. Since Mr. Bounderby likes to take me thus, I am satisfied

[015-166] to accept his proposal. Tell him, father, as soon as you please, that

[015-167] this was my answer. Repeat it, word for word, if you can, because I

[015-168] should wish him to know what I said.’

[015-169] ‘It is quite right, my dear,’ retorted her father approvingly, ‘to be

[015-170] exact. I will observe your very proper request. Have you any wish in

[015-171] reference to the period of your marriage, my child?’

[015-172] ‘None, father. What does it matter!’

[015-173] Mr. Gradgrind had drawn his chair a little nearer to her, and taken her

[015-174] hand. But, her repetition of these words seemed to strike with some

[015-175] little discord on his ear. He paused to look at her, and, still holding

[015-176] her hand, said:

[015-177] ‘Louisa, I have not considered it essential to ask you one question,

[015-178] because the possibility implied in it appeared to me to be too remote.

[015-179] But perhaps I ought to do so. You have never entertained in secret any

[015-180] other proposal?’

[015-181] ‘Father,’ she returned, almost scornfully, ‘what other proposal can have

[015-182] been made to _me_? Whom have I seen? Where have I been? What are my

[015-183] heart’s experiences?’

[015-184] ‘My dear Louisa,’ returned Mr. Gradgrind, reassured and satisfied. ‘You

[015-185] correct me justly. I merely wished to discharge my [[duty]].’

[015-186] ‘What do _I_ know, father,’ said Louisa in her quiet manner, ‘of tastes

[015-187] and fancies; of aspirations and affections; of all that part of my [[nature]]

[015-188] in which such light things might have been nourished? What escape have I

[015-189] had from problems that could be demonstrated, and realities that could be

[015-190] grasped?’ As she said it, she unconsciously closed her hand, as if upon

[015-191] a solid object, and slowly opened it as though she were releasing dust or

[015-192] ash.

[015-193] ‘My dear,’ assented her eminently practical parent, ‘quite true, quite

[015-194] true.’

[015-195] ‘Why, father,’ she pursued, ‘what a strange question to ask _me_! The

[015-196] baby-preference that even I have heard of as common among children, has

[015-197] never had its innocent resting-place in my breast. You have been so

[015-198] careful of me, that I never had a child’s heart. You have trained me so

[015-199] well, that I never dreamed a child’s dream. You have dealt so wisely

[015-200] with me, father, from my cradle to this hour, that I never had a child’s

[015-201] belief or a child’s fear.’

[015-202] Mr. Gradgrind was quite moved by his success, and by this testimony to

[015-203] it. ‘My dear Louisa,’ said he, ‘you abundantly repay my care. Kiss me,

[015-204] my dear girl.’

[015-205] So, his daughter kissed him. Detaining her in his embrace, he said, ‘I

[015-206] may assure you now, my favourite child, that I am made happy by the sound

[015-207] decision at which you have arrived. Mr. Bounderby is a very remarkable

[015-208] man; and what little disparity can be said to exist between you—if any—is

[015-209] more than counterbalanced by the tone your mind has acquired. It has

[015-210] always been my object so to educate you, as that you might, while still

[015-211] in your early youth, be (if I may so express myself) almost any age.

[015-212] Kiss me once more, Louisa. Now, let us go and find your mother.’

[015-213] Accordingly, they went down to the drawing-room, where the esteemed lady

[015-214] with no nonsense about her, was recumbent as usual, while [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] worked

[015-215] beside her. She gave some feeble signs of returning animation when they

[015-216] entered, and presently the faint transparency was presented in a sitting

[015-217] attitude.

[015-218] ‘Mrs. Gradgrind,’ said her husband, who had waited for the achievement of

[015-219] this feat with some impatience, ‘allow me to present to you Mrs.

[015-220] Bounderby.’

[015-221] ‘Oh!’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, ‘so you have settled it! Well, I’m sure I

[015-222] hope your health may be good, Louisa; for if your head begins to split as

[015-223] soon as you are married, which was the case with mine, I cannot consider

[015-224] that you are to be envied, though I have no doubt you think you are, as

[015-225] all girls do. However, I give you joy, my dear—and I hope you may now

[015-226] turn all your ological studies to good account, I am sure I do! I must

[015-227] give you a kiss of congratulation, Louisa; but don’t touch my right

[015-228] shoulder, for there’s something running down it all day long. And now

[015-229] you see,’ whimpered Mrs. Gradgrind, adjusting her shawls after the

[015-230] affectionate ceremony, ‘I shall be worrying myself, morning, noon, and

[015-231] night, to know what I am to call him!’

[015-232] ‘Mrs. Gradgrind,’ said her husband, solemnly, ‘what do you mean?’

[015-233] ‘Whatever I am to call him, Mr. Gradgrind, when he is married to Louisa!

[015-234] I must call him something. It’s impossible,’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, with a

[015-235] mingled sense of politeness and injury, ‘to be constantly addressing him

[015-236] and never giving him a name. I cannot call him Josiah, for the name is

[015-237] insupportable to me. You yourself wouldn’t hear of Joe, you very well

[015-238] know. Am I to call my own son-in-law, Mister! Not, I believe, unless

[015-239] the time has arrived when, as an invalid, I am to be trampled upon by my

[015-240] relations. Then, what am I to call him!’

[015-241] Nobody present having any suggestion to offer in the remarkable

[015-242] emergency, Mrs. Gradgrind departed this life for the time being, after

[015-243] delivering the following codicil to her remarks already executed:

[015-244] ‘As to the wedding, all I ask, Louisa, is,—and I ask it with a fluttering

[015-245] in my chest, which actually extends to the soles of my feet,—that it may

[015-246] take place soon. Otherwise, I know it is one of those subjects I shall

[015-247] never hear the last of.’

[015-248] When Mr. Gradgrind had presented Mrs. Bounderby, Sissy had suddenly

[015-249] turned her head, and looked, in wonder, in pity, in sorrow, in doubt, in

[015-250] a multitude of emotions, towards Louisa. Louisa had known it, and seen

[015-251] it, without looking at her. From that moment she was impassive, [[pride|proud]]

[015-252] and cold—held Sissy at a distance—changed to her altogether.
