# Nature

In *Hard Times*, "nature" is the novel's most capacious and contested term — operating simultaneously as a description of what human beings are in their irreducible essence and as a name for the organic, living world that Coketown's industrial machinery has banished from experience. Dickens binds these two meanings into a single argument: the same Utilitarian logic that reduces children to vessels for facts also bricks Nature out of the factory town, and the same imaginative faculty that perceives beauty in the natural world is the faculty that enables sympathy, love, and moral judgment. To lose nature — in either sense — is to lose the human.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's handling of nature is structured around a governing opposition between what is **natural** and what is **unnatural** — and he assigns these terms with deliberate polemical force. The Gradgrind educational system, which promises to quantify human nature into a set of facts — "human nature, and tell you exactly what it comes to" (PN: 002-008) — is presented not as misguided but as *unnatural*. Its product is a "monster" (Tom) and a woman so emotionally deadened by the suppression of her nature that she can speak of "all that part of my nature" as something sealed away from the world (PN: 015-187). Coketown itself is described in terms of the natural world's absence: "where Nature was as strongly bricked out as killing airs and gases were bricked in" (PN: 010-010). The industrial town is an "unnatural family," and Bounderby's self-mythologising "birth-in-a-ditch" narrative, Gradgrind's "ologies" curriculum, and the marriage market that trades Louisa for social position are all variations on the same theme: the deformation of natural human relations by artificial systems.

Yet Dickens is not a simple pastoralist. He does not argue that the pre-industrial world was a paradise or that human nature is invariably virtuous. The novel's account of nature is psychologically precise rather than sentimental. Stephen Blackpool's nature — his stubborn integrity and refusal to join the union on grounds of conscience — is presented as genuinely admirable, but it is also what destroys him: natural goodness is no protection against unnatural social arrangements. The circus folk, who preserve something "natural" in their improvisational, bodily, emotionally direct way of life, are nonetheless described as physically awkward, aesthetically ridiculous, and socially marginal — "a kind of people who would never be missed" (PN: 006-252). Nature, in *Hard Times*, is not triumphant; it is beleaguered, fugitive, and yet — Dickens insists — indispensable.

### Key Passages

> "human nature, and tell you exactly what it comes to. It is a mere question of figures, a case of simple arithmetic." (PN: 002-008)

This passage — from Gradgrind's opening address — is the novel's foundational statement on nature-as-problem. The Gradgrindian promise is to *quantify* human nature, to reduce it to a "mere question of figures." The irony, sustained across the entire novel, is that this quantification process does not merely fail — it actively destroys what it claims to measure. The Gradgrind children, raised on this arithmetic of the soul, emerge not as rational adults but as moral and emotional cripples. Louisa cannot love; Tom becomes a thief. The "simple arithmetic" produces a deficit it cannot account for because the thing it excludes — feeling, imagination, conscience — is precisely what makes the calculation possible in the first place. The measurer is himself measured, and found wanting.

> "Upon a nature long accustomed to self-suppression, it told with a vengeance." (PN: 023-029)

This description of Louisa, at the crisis of her near-elopement with Harthouse, is Dickens's most compressed analysis of what the Gradgrind system does to human nature. "Self-suppression" is the key term: the nature is not absent — it has not been educated out of existence — but has been forced underground, "accustomed" to its own concealment until it becomes a stranger to itself. When suppressed nature finally breaks through, it does so not as liberation but as "vengeance" — a destructive force that threatens to undo Louisa entirely. The passage rewrites the Gradgrindian claim to have mastered nature: nature has been suppressed, not extinguished, and suppression is not the same as control.

> "for in natures, as in seas, depth answers unto depth" (PN: 023-059)

This metaphor — one of the novel's most striking images — captures Dickens's theory of human nature as something layered, mysterious, and responsive rather than transparent and calculable. The image of the sea, with its unplumbable depths and its currents that move beneath the surface, is the antithesis of the Gradgrindian grid of facts. It suggests that human beings are known not through measurement but through resonance — "depth answers unto depth" — a mode of understanding that requires imagination, sympathy, and the willingness to acknowledge that some things cannot be reduced to "simple arithmetic."

> "Not that they knew, by name or nature, anything about an Ogre Fact" (PN: 003-012)

This ironic aside about the Gradgrind children's ignorance is a miniature demonstration of Dickens's rhetorical strategy regarding nature. The children know facts — they have been "put through an enormous variety of paces" — but they know nothing of "nature" in either of the word's senses. They cannot recognise an Ogre (a creature of imaginative life) and they cannot recognise the natural world that would give content to the word "horse" beyond its definition as "Quadruped. Graminivorous." The sentence's pun on "by name or nature" is deliberate: the Gradgrind system teaches names without natures, labels without the felt reality they designate.

## Historical Context

*Hard Times* was published in 1854, at the high-water mark of industrial triumphalism in Victorian Britain. The Great Exhibition of 1851 had celebrated the products of industry as the fruits of human progress; the Crystal Palace enclosed nature (trees, fountains) within a structure of iron and glass, symbolically declaring the natural world to be an exhibit within the industrial order. This was the world against which John Ruskin had already begun to protest, and against which William Wordsworth's Romantic critique of industrial alienation — "Getting and spending, we lay waste our powers; / Little we see in Nature that is ours" (1807) — had long since set the terms of debate.

The intellectual context is **Benthamite Utilitarianism**, which treated human beings as rational agents pursuing pleasure and avoiding pain — a model that, in its most reductive forms, rendered "human nature" as a set of predictable responses to measurable stimuli. The novel's attack on this reduction is not merely literary but philosophical: Dickens insists that there is something in human beings — call it imagination, call it sympathy, call it the heart — that cannot be captured by the Utilitarian calculus and that is destroyed by the attempt to do so.

The environmental dimension of "nature" in the novel reflects a specifically Victorian anxiety. Industrial towns like the Preston on which Coketown was modelled grew with terrifying speed — between 1801 and 1851, Preston's population quadrupled — and the rapid expansion left little room for parks, gardens, or public green spaces. The 1833 Factory Act and its successors regulated hours and conditions but said nothing about the quality of the environment in which industrial workers lived. Coketown's total bricking-out of Nature is not merely a symbol; it is a realistic depiction of what Victorian industrialisation did to the English landscape and to the sensory experience of the people who inhabited it.

## Embodied By

- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: The novel's primary case study in suppressed nature. Raised on facts and denied imaginative nurture, Louisa grows into a woman whose emotional life has been so thoroughly "accustomed to self-suppression" (PN: 023-029) that she cannot name her own desires. Her confession to her father — speaking of "all that part of my nature" that has been systematically starved (PN: 015-187) — is the novel's most explicit statement of what the Gradgrind system costs. Louisa's nature is not absent; it has been deformed, and its eventual eruption nearly destroys her.

- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: The counter-example — nature preserved rather than suppressed. Sissy's upbringing among the circus folk, with their physical expressiveness, emotional directness, and indifference to respectable opinion, has allowed her nature to develop along its own lines. She is the novel's argument that nature flourishes in conditions of imaginative freedom and communal affection, not in conditions of rational instruction. Her presence in the Gradgrind household is a living reproach to its educational philosophy.

- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Natural integrity as moral tragedy. Stephen's "nature" — his honest refusal to join the union on grounds of conscience, his loyalty to Rachael, his refusal to speak ill of his drunken wife — is presented as genuinely admirable. But it is also what makes him helpless. Natural goodness, Dickens shows, provides no armour against unnatural social arrangements: Stephen is destroyed not because his nature is flawed but because the world he inhabits has no place for a man who simply wants to do right.

- **[[Slearys-Circus]]**: The survival of nature in miniature. The circus is the one community in the novel that operates outside the logic of industrial production and class hierarchy. Its people labour, but their labour is performance — physical, collaborative, improvisational — and their community is held together by loyalty and mutual care rather than by economic interest. The circus is physically awkward and socially ridiculous, but it is also, Dickens insists, the repository of "the every-day virtues of any class of people in the world" (PN: 006-252). It is nature — human and communal — surviving on the margins of the artificial city.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The Gradgrindian reduction of nature to quantifiable data is the novel's governing antithesis. Facts are what remain when nature — in both senses — has been evacuated. The Gradgrind school promises to deliver "human nature" as a set of facts, but the novel demonstrates that this promise is not merely false but actively destructive: the attempt to quantify nature destroys the thing it measures. (PN: 002-008)
- **[[utilitarianism]]**: Utilitarianism provides the philosophical framework that makes the reduction of nature to calculation seem rational and even moral. Its model of human beings as pleasure-seeking, pain-avoiding calculators is, Dickens argues, a deformation of what human nature actually is — and its application to education, labour relations, and marriage produces the deformations the novel catalogues. (PN: 015-187)
- **[[education]]**: The Gradgrind educational philosophy is the mechanism by which nature is systematically suppressed. The schoolroom — the "plain, bare, monotonous vault" — is the site where "tender young imaginations" are "murdered" (PN: 002-023), and the novel's title for its second chapter, "Murdering the Innocents," makes the violence of this suppression explicit. Education ought to cultivate nature; Gradgrind education destroys it.

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *The Country and the City* (1973), argued that the opposition between the rural and the urban in Victorian fiction is never simply a contrast between two kinds of place — it is always an argument about what kind of human life is possible under different conditions. In *Hard Times*, Williams suggests, Coketown is Dickens's attempt to imagine a world from which the organic rhythms of country life have been entirely eliminated, and the novel's urgent question is whether anything recognisably human can survive in such a world.

> David Craig, in *The Real Foundation* (1973), read *Hard Times* as a novel about the "dehumanising" effects of industrial capitalism and argued that Dickens's invocation of "nature" — both human and environmental — is not a retreat into pastoral nostalgia but a strategic counter-position. Craig's reading highlights the political dimension of the nature/artifice opposition: to insist on the reality of human nature against Utilitarian reductionism is, in the context of 1854, a radical act.
