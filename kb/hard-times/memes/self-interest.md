# Self-Interest

Self-interest is the dark engine of *Hard Times*. Dickens takes the central axiom of classical political economy — that human beings are rational calculators of their own advantage — and dramatises its consequences in the Gradgrind family. The novel's thesis is blunt: raise children on the principle that self-interest is the only reliable motive, and you will produce adults incapable of any other motive. The Gradgrind philosophy does not merely tolerate selfishness; it makes selfishness into a system, and the system destroys those it was meant to serve.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

The novel's treatment of self-interest reaches its climax in the confrontation between Gradgrind and his son Tom, the bank robber. Tom, cornered at last, turns his father's own teachings against him with devastating precision:

> "So many people are employed in situations of trust; so many people, out of so many, will be dishonest. I have heard you talk, a hundred times, of its being a law. How can I help laws? ... you have been so accustomed to consider every other question, as consisting only of one of these two things — self-interest or no self-interest — that I am not at all surprised that you should consider this solely a question of self-interest." (PN: 036-034)

The son speaks the father's doctrine back to him, and Gradgrind has no answer — because Tom is right. Gradgrind has taught his children that all human action reduces to the calculation of advantage, and Tom has simply followed the logic to its terminal point. He robbed the bank because it was in his interest to do so, or so he believed. The theft is not a betrayal of Gradgrindery; it is its fulfillment.

The irony is structural: Gradgrind, who genuinely believed that rational self-interest would produce a well-ordered life, has instead produced a criminal, a near-adulteress (Louisa), and a family in ruins. The novel argues that self-interest, elevated to a universal principle, is self-defeating — it destroys the very bonds (trust, love, duty) on which a functioning society depends.

### Key Passages

> "What you must always appeal to, is a person's self-interest. It's your only hold. We are not to be told that people have any other interest in their conduct." (PN: 036-038–039)

Tom's summary of his father's philosophy is chilling in its clarity. The word "only" does the work: self-interest is not one motive among many but the sole admissible motive. All other reasons for action — love, honour, pity, justice — are dismissed as sentimental fictions.

> Bounderby "assumed that he really was the self-interested man he had occasion to present himself to be" (PN: 021-016)

Bounderby is the novel's case study in self-interest as performance. He presents himself as a man who has risen by looking after Number One — but the novel hints, and eventually reveals, that this self-presentation is a fiction built on the erasure of his mother's sacrifices. Self-interest, in Bounderby's case, is not a fact but a story he tells.

## Historical Context

*Hard Times* was written in the wake of the great Victorian debate about political economy and its moral foundations. Adam Smith's *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) had argued that the pursuit of self-interest, channelled through the market, produced unintended social benefits — the famous "invisible hand." But by the 1850s, critics including Thomas Carlyle, John Ruskin, and Dickens himself had begun to ask what was lost when self-interest was promoted from an empirical observation about markets to a normative principle for all of life. The "Condition of England" novels of the 1840s and 1850s — *Sybil*, *Mary Barton*, *Alton Locke*, *Hard Times* — are united in their insistence that a society governed solely by self-interest will, in the end, cease to be a society at all.

## Embodied By

- **[[thomas-gradgrind]]**: The philosopher of self-interest who discovers, too late, that his philosophy destroys what he loves. His tragedy is that he is not himself selfish — he genuinely believes in the system — but his children have absorbed the lesson he did not mean to teach.
- **[[tom-gradgrind]]**: The son who becomes the reductio ad absurdum of his father's principles. Tom is not innately evil; he is a creature of his education, and his education has taught him that the only reason to do or not do anything is self-interest.
- **[[josiah-bounderby]]**: The self-made man whose self-making turns out to be a self-serving fiction. Bounderby is the novel's demonstration that the rhetoric of self-interest is, in practice, a mask for cruelty, exploitation, and self-deception.

## Contrasted With

- **[[humanity]]**: Where self-interest divides — each person against every other — humanity unites. The novel traces Stephen Blackpool's refusal to betray his fellow workers, even at the cost of his livelihood, as the ethical counter-example to the Gradgrind-Bounderby doctrine.
- **[[duty]]**: Duty, in the novel's moral vocabulary, is what restrains self-interest. It is the obligation one owes to others regardless of personal advantage — and it is precisely what the Gradgrind system cannot account for.
- **[[love]]**: Love is the ultimate refutation of self-interest. It is, by definition, a regard for another's good that is not reducible to one's own advantage. The novel's redemptive arc — Louisa's recovery, Sissy's care — is a demonstration that love survives even the most systematic attempt to educate it out of existence.
