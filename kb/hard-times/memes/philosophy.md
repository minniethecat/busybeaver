# Philosophy

*Hard Times* presents not one but three distinct philosophies, each embodied in a character or group and each tested against the novel's events. The **Gradgrind philosophy** of facts and calculation is the novel's primary target, but Dickens also anatomises the **Sleary philosophy** of amusement and acceptance, and the **Harthouse philosophy** of worldly disillusionment. The novel's moral argument emerges from the collision of these competing worldviews.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

The Gradgrind philosophy is stated most explicitly near the novel's end: "It was a fundamental principle of the Gradgrind philosophy that everything was to be paid for. Nobody was ever on any account to give anybody anything, or render anybody help without purchase" (PN: 036-059). This is the philosophy of the counting-house applied to human relations — a worldview in which obligation is always calculable and never unconditional.

Against this, Dickens sets the Sleary philosophy — "people must be amused" (PN: 006-433) — which sounds merely hedonistic but contains a deeper wisdom. Sleary's philosophy acknowledges human need in a way that Gradgrind's cannot: the need for pleasure, for imagination, for release from the grinding logic of facts. It is no accident that the circus people, not the parliamentarians or manufacturers, are the novel's most reliable moral agents.

The Harthouse philosophy offers a third possibility: the stance of the gentleman-dilettante who has tried everything and believes in nothing. "Wearied and divided, the Harthouse philosophy came as a relief" (PN: 023-030) to Louisa — precisely because it demands nothing of her. It is the philosophy of exhaustion, and Dickens treats it as morally bankrupt even as he acknowledges its seductive power.

### Key Passages

> "The Sleary philosophy was propounded as they went down the street." (PN: 006-433)

The verb "propounded" is mock-heroic — Sleary's philosophy is not a system but a way of living, articulated in fragments and in motion, not in treatises.

> "All that I know is, your philosophy and your teaching will not save me." (PN: 028-136)

Louisa's verdict on her father's philosophy is the novel's turning-point — the moment when the system's failure is acknowledged not by the narrator but by its primary victim.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas Gradgrind]]**: The philosopher of facts, whose philosophy collapses under the weight of its own consequences.
- **[[Sleary]]**: The philosopher of the circus, whose lisp and brandy-and-water do not diminish the moral clarity of his worldview.
- **[[James Harthouse]]**: The philosopher of *ennui*, whose detachment is a luxury purchased by the labour of others.
- **[[Sissy Jupe]]**: The unlettered philosopher whose practical wisdom — fed by love, stories, and direct experience of suffering — proves more reliable than any formal system.

## Contrasted With

- **[[Facts]]**: The Gradgrind philosophy elevates facts to an epistemology; Dickens shows that facts alone cannot generate a philosophy worth living by.
- **[[Heart]]**: The faculty that the Gradgrind philosophy excludes and that the Sleary philosophy — however imperfectly — accommodates.
