[017-001] EFFECTS IN THE BANK

[017-002] A SUNNY midsummer day. There was such a thing sometimes, even in

[017-003] [[coketown|Coketown]].

[017-004] Seen from a distance in such weather, Coketown lay shrouded in a haze of

[017-005] its own, which appeared impervious to the sun’s rays. You only knew the

[017-006] town was there, because you knew there could have been no such sulky

[017-007] blotch upon the prospect without a town. A blur of soot and smoke, now

[017-008] confusedly tending this way, now that way, now aspiring to the vault of

[017-009] Heaven, now murkily creeping along the earth, as the wind rose and fell,

[017-010] or changed its quarter: a dense formless jumble, with sheets of cross

[017-011] light in it, that showed nothing but masses of darkness:—Coketown in the

[017-012] distance was suggestive of itself, though not a brick of it could be

[017-013] seen.

[017-014] The wonder was, it was there at all. It had been ruined so often, that

[017-015] it was amazing how it had borne so many shocks. Surely there never was

[017-016] such fragile china-ware as that of which the millers of Coketown were

[017-017] made. Handle them never so lightly, and they fell to pieces with such

[017-018] ease that you might suspect them of having been flawed before. They were

[017-019] ruined, when they were required to send labouring [[childhood|children]] to school;

[017-020] they were ruined when inspectors were appointed to look into their works;

[017-021] they were ruined, when such inspectors considered it doubtful whether

[017-022] they were quite justified in chopping people up with their machinery;

[017-023] they were utterly undone, when it was hinted that perhaps they need not

[017-024] always make quite so much smoke. Besides [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s gold spoon

[017-025] which was generally received in Coketown, another prevalent fiction was

[017-026] very popular there. It took the form of a threat. Whenever a Coketowner

[017-027] felt he was ill-used—that is to say, whenever he was not left entirely

[017-028] alone, and it was proposed to hold him accountable for the consequences

[017-029] of any of his acts—he was sure to come out with the awful menace, that he

[017-030] would ‘sooner pitch his property into the Atlantic.’ This had terrified

[017-031] the Home Secretary within an inch of his life, on several occasions.

[017-032] However, the Coketowners were so patriotic after all, that they never had

[017-033] pitched their property into the Atlantic yet, but, on the contrary, had

[017-034] been kind enough to take mighty good care of it. So there it was, in the

[017-035] haze yonder; and it increased and multiplied.

[017-036] The streets were hot and dusty on the summer day, and the sun was so

[017-037] bright that it even shone through the heavy vapour drooping over

[017-038] Coketown, and could not be looked at steadily. Stokers emerged from low

[017-039] underground doorways into factory yards, and sat on steps, and posts, and

[017-040] palings, wiping their swarthy visages, and contemplating coals. The

[017-041] whole town seemed to be frying in oil. There was a stifling smell of hot

[017-042] oil everywhere. The steam-engines shone with it, the dresses of the

[017-043] Hands were soiled with it, the mills throughout their many stories oozed

[017-044] and trickled it. The atmosphere of those Fairy palaces was like the

[017-045] breath of the simoom: and their inhabitants, wasting with heat, toiled

[017-046] languidly in the desert. But no temperature made the melancholy mad

[017-047] elephants more mad or more sane. Their wearisome heads went up and down

[017-048] at the same rate, in hot weather and cold, wet weather and dry, fair

[017-049] weather and foul. The measured motion of their shadows on the walls, was

[017-050] the substitute Coketown had to [[performance|show]] for the shadows of rustling woods;

[017-051] while, for the summer hum of insects, it could offer, all the year round,

[017-052] from the dawn of Monday to the night of Saturday, the whirr of shafts and

[017-053] wheels.

[017-054] Drowsily they whirred all through this sunny day, making the passenger

[017-055] more sleepy and more hot as he passed the humming walls of the mills.

[017-056] Sun-blinds, and sprinklings of water, a little cooled the main streets

[017-057] and the shops; but the mills, and the courts and alleys, baked at a

[017-058] fierce heat. Down upon the river that was black and thick with dye, some

[017-059] Coketown boys who were at large—a rare sight there—rowed a crazy boat,

[017-060] which made a spumous track upon the water as it jogged along, while every

[017-061] dip of an oar stirred up vile smells. But the sun itself, however

[017-062] beneficent, generally, was less kind to Coketown than hard frost, and

[017-063] rarely looked intently into any of its closer regions without engendering

[017-064] more death than life. So does the eye of Heaven itself become an evil

[017-065] eye, when incapable or sordid hands are interposed between it and the

[017-066] things it looks upon to bless.

[017-067] [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] sat in her afternoon apartment at the Bank, on the shadier

[017-068] side of the frying street. Office-hours were over: and at that period of

[017-069] the day, in warm weather, she usually embellished with her genteel

[017-070] presence, a managerial board-room over the public office. Her own

[017-071] private sitting-room was a story higher, at the window of which post of

[017-072] observation she was ready, every morning, to greet Mr. Bounderby, as he

[017-073] came across the road, with the sympathizing recognition appropriate to a

[017-074] Victim. He had been married now a year; and Mrs. Sparsit had never

[017-075] released him from her determined pity a moment.

[017-076] The Bank offered no violence to the wholesome monotony of the town. It

[017-077] was another red brick house, with black outside shutters, green inside

[017-078] blinds, a black street-door up two white steps, a brazen door-plate, and

[017-079] a brazen door-handle full stop. It was a size larger than Mr.

[017-080] Bounderby’s house, as other houses were from a size to half-a-dozen sizes

[017-081] smaller; in all other particulars, it was strictly according to pattern.

[017-082] Mrs. Sparsit was conscious that by coming in the evening-tide among the

[017-083] desks and writing implements, she shed a feminine, not to say also

[017-084] aristocratic, grace upon the office. Seated, with her needlework or

[017-085] netting apparatus, at the window, she had a self-laudatory sense of

[017-086] correcting, by her ladylike deportment, the rude business aspect of the

[017-087] place. With this impression of her interesting character upon her, Mrs.

[017-088] Sparsit considered herself, in some sort, the Bank Fairy. The

[017-089] townspeople who, in their passing and repassing, saw her there, regarded

[017-090] her as the Bank Dragon keeping watch over the treasures of the mine.

[017-091] What those treasures were, Mrs. Sparsit knew as little as they did. Gold

[017-092] and silver coin, precious paper, secrets that if divulged would bring

[017-093] vague destruction upon vague persons (generally, however, people whom she

[017-094] disliked), were the chief items in her ideal catalogue thereof. For the

[017-095] rest, she knew that after office-hours, she reigned supreme over all the

[017-096] office furniture, and over a locked-up iron room with three locks,

[017-097] against the door of which strong chamber [[bitzer|the light porter]] laid his [[reason|head]]

[017-098] every night, on a truckle bed, that disappeared at cockcrow. Further,

[017-099] she was lady paramount over certain vaults in the basement, sharply

[017-100] spiked off from communication with the predatory world; and over the

[017-101] relics of the current day’s work, consisting of blots of ink, worn-out

[017-102] pens, fragments of wafers, and scraps of paper torn so small, that

[017-103] nothing interesting could ever be deciphered on them when Mrs. Sparsit

[017-104] tried. Lastly, she was guardian over a little armoury of cutlasses and

[017-105] carbines, arrayed in vengeful order above one of the official

[017-106] chimney-pieces; and over that respectable tradition never to be separated

[017-107] from a place of business claiming to be wealthy—a row of

[017-108] fire-buckets—vessels calculated to be of no physical utility on any

[017-109] occasion, but observed to exercise a fine moral influence, almost equal

[017-110] to bullion, on most beholders.

[017-111] A deaf serving-woman and the light porter completed Mrs. Sparsit’s

[017-112] empire. The deaf serving-woman was rumoured to be wealthy; and a saying

[017-113] had for years gone about among the lower orders of Coketown, that she

[017-114] would be murdered some night when the Bank was shut, for the sake of her

[017-115] money. It was generally considered, indeed, that she had been due some

[017-116] time, and ought to have fallen long ago; but she had kept her life, and

[017-117] her situation, with an ill-conditioned tenacity that occasioned much

[017-118] offence and disappointment.

[017-119] Mrs. Sparsit’s tea was just set for her on a pert little table, with its

[017-120] tripod of legs in an attitude, which she insinuated after office-hours,

[017-121] into the company of the stern, leathern-topped, long board-table that

[017-122] bestrode the middle of the room. The light porter placed the tea-tray on

[017-123] it, knuckling his forehead as a form of homage.

[017-124] ‘Thank you, Bitzer,’ said Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-125] ‘Thank _you_, ma’am,’ returned the light porter. He was a very light

[017-126] porter indeed; as light as in the days when he blinkingly defined a

[017-127] horse, for [[sissy-jupe|girl number twenty]].

[017-128] ‘All is shut up, Bitzer?’ said Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-129] ‘All is shut up, ma’am.’

[017-130] ‘And what,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, pouring out her tea, ‘is the news of the

[017-131] day? Anything?’

[017-132] ‘Well, ma’am, I can’t say that I have heard anything particular. Our

[017-133] people are a bad lot, ma’am; but that is no news, unfortunately.’

[017-134] ‘What are the restless wretches doing now?’ asked Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-135] ‘Merely going on in the old way, ma’am. Uniting, and leaguing, and

[017-136] engaging to stand by one another.’

[017-137] ‘It is much to be regretted,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, making her nose more

[017-138] Roman and her eyebrows more Coriolanian in the strength of her severity,

[017-139] ‘that the united masters allow of any such [[class|class-combinations]].’

[017-140] ‘Yes, ma’am,’ said Bitzer.

[017-141] ‘Being united themselves, they ought one and all to set their faces

[017-142] against employing any man who is united with any other man,’ said Mrs.

[017-143] Sparsit.

[017-144] ‘They have done that, ma’am,’ returned Bitzer; ‘but it rather fell

[017-145] through, ma’am.’

[017-146] ‘I do not pretend to understand these things,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, with

[017-147] [[pride|dignity]], ‘my lot having been signally cast in a widely different sphere;

[017-148] and Mr. Sparsit, as a Powler, being also quite out of the pale of any

[017-149] such dissensions. I only know that these people must be conquered, and

[017-150] that it’s high time it was done, once for all.’

[017-151] ‘Yes, ma’am,’ returned Bitzer, with a demonstration of great respect for

[017-152] Mrs. Sparsit’s oracular authority. ‘You couldn’t put it clearer, I am

[017-153] sure, ma’am.’

[017-154] As this was his usual hour for having a little confidential chat with

[017-155] Mrs. Sparsit, and as he had already caught her eye and seen that she was

[017-156] going to ask him something, he made a pretence of arranging the rulers,

[017-157] inkstands, and so forth, while that lady went on with her tea, glancing

[017-158] through the open window, down into the street.

[017-159] ‘Has it been a busy day, Bitzer?’ asked Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-160] ‘Not a very busy day, my lady. [[About]] an average day.’ He now and then

[017-161] slided into my lady, instead of ma’am, as an involuntary acknowledgment

[017-162] of Mrs. Sparsit’s personal dignity and claims to reverence.

[017-163] ‘The clerks,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, carefully brushing an imperceptible

[017-164] crumb of bread and butter from her left-hand mitten, ‘are trustworthy,

[017-165] punctual, and industrious, of course?’

[017-166] ‘Yes, ma’am, pretty fair, ma’am. With the usual exception.’

[017-167] He held the respectable office of general spy and informer in the

[017-168] establishment, for which volunteer service he received a present at

[017-169] Christmas, over and above his weekly wage. He had grown into an

[017-170] extremely clear-headed, cautious, prudent young man, who was safe to rise

[017-171] in the world. His mind was so exactly regulated, that he had no

[017-172] affections or passions. All his proceedings were the result of the

[017-173] nicest and coldest calculation; and it was not without cause that Mrs.

[017-174] Sparsit habitually observed of him, that he was a young man of the

[017-175] steadiest principle she had ever known. Having satisfied himself, on his

[017-176] father’s death, that his mother had a right of settlement in Coketown,

[017-177] this excellent young economist had asserted that right for her with such

[017-178] a steadfast adherence to the principle of the case, that she had been

[017-179] shut up in the workhouse ever since. It must be admitted that he allowed

[017-180] her half a pound of tea a year, which was weak in him: first, because all

[017-181] gifts have an inevitable tendency to pauperise the recipient, and

[017-182] secondly, because his only reasonable transaction in that commodity would

[017-183] have been to buy it for as little as he could possibly give, and sell it

[017-184] for as much as he could possibly get; it having been clearly ascertained

[017-185] by philosophers that in this is comprised the whole [[duty]] of man—not a

[017-186] part of man’s duty, but the whole.

[017-187] ‘Pretty fair, ma’am. With the usual exception, ma’am,’ repeated Bitzer.

[017-188] ‘Ah—h!’ said Mrs. Sparsit, shaking her head over her tea-cup, and taking

[017-189] a long gulp.

[017-190] ‘Mr. Thomas, ma’am, I doubt Mr. Thomas very much, ma’am, I don’t like his

[017-191] ways at all.’

[017-192] ‘Bitzer,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, in a very impressive manner, ‘do you

[017-193] recollect my having said anything to you respecting names?’

[017-194] ‘I beg your pardon, ma’am. It’s quite true that you did object to names

[017-195] being used, and they’re always best avoided.’

[017-196] ‘Please to remember that I have a charge here,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, with

[017-197] her air of state. ‘I hold a trust here, Bitzer, under Mr. Bounderby.

[017-198] However improbable both Mr. Bounderby and myself might have deemed it

[017-199] years ago, that he would ever become my patron, making me an annual

[017-200] compliment, I cannot but regard him in that light. From Mr. Bounderby I

[017-201] have received every acknowledgment of my social station, and every

[017-202] recognition of my [[family|family descent]], that I could possibly expect. More,

[017-203] far more. Therefore, to my patron I will be scrupulously true. And I do

[017-204] not consider, I will not consider, I cannot consider,’ said Mrs. Sparsit,

[017-205] with a most extensive stock on hand of honour and morality, ‘that I

[017-206] _should_ be scrupulously true, if I allowed names to be mentioned under

[017-207] this roof, that are unfortunately—most unfortunately—no doubt of

[017-208] that—connected with his.’

[017-209] Bitzer knuckled his forehead again, and again begged pardon.

[017-210] ‘No, Bitzer,’ continued Mrs. Sparsit, ‘say an individual, and I will hear

[017-211] you; say Mr. Thomas, and you must excuse me.’

[017-212] ‘With the usual exception, ma’am,’ said Bitzer, trying back, ‘of an

[017-213] individual.’

[017-214] ‘Ah—h!’ Mrs. Sparsit repeated the ejaculation, the shake of the head

[017-215] over her tea-cup, and the long gulp, as taking up the conversation again

[017-216] at the point where it had been interrupted.

[017-217] ‘An individual, ma’am,’ said Bitzer, ‘has never been what he ought to

[017-218] have been, since he first came into the place. He is a dissipated,

[017-219] extravagant idler. He is not worth his salt, ma’am. He wouldn’t get it

[017-220] either, if he hadn’t a friend and relation at court, ma’am!’

[017-221] ‘Ah—h!’ said Mrs. Sparsit, with another melancholy shake of her head.

[017-222] ‘I only hope, ma’am,’ pursued Bitzer, ‘that his friend and relation may

[017-223] not supply him with the means of carrying on. Otherwise, ma’am, we know

[017-224] out of whose pocket _that_ money comes.’

[017-225] ‘Ah—h!’ sighed Mrs. Sparsit again, with another melancholy shake of her

[017-226] head.

[017-227] ‘He is to be pitied, ma’am. The last party I have alluded to, is to be

[017-228] pitied, ma’am,’ said Bitzer.

[017-229] ‘Yes, Bitzer,’ said Mrs. Sparsit. ‘I have always pitied the delusion,

[017-230] always.’

[017-231] ‘As to an individual, ma’am,’ said Bitzer, dropping his voice and drawing

[017-232] nearer, ‘he is as improvident as any of the people in this town. And you

[017-233] know what _their_ improvidence is, ma’am. No one could wish to know it

[017-234] better than a lady of your eminence does.’

[017-235] ‘They would do well,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, ‘to take example by you,

[017-236] Bitzer.’

[017-237] ‘Thank you, ma’am. But, since you do refer to me, now look at me, ma’am.

[017-238] I have put by a little, ma’am, already. That gratuity which I receive at

[017-239] Christmas, ma’am: I never touch it. I don’t even go the length of my

[017-240] wages, though they’re not high, ma’am. Why can’t they do as I have done,

[017-241] ma’am? What one person can do, another can do.’

[017-242] This, again, was among the fictions of Coketown. Any capitalist there,

[017-243] who had made sixty thousand pounds out of sixpence, always professed to

[017-244] wonder why the sixty thousand nearest Hands didn’t each make sixty

[017-245] thousand pounds out of sixpence, and more or less reproached them every

[017-246] one for not accomplishing the little feat. What I did you can do. Why

[017-247] don’t you go and do it?

[017-248] ‘As to their wanting recreations, ma’am,’ said Bitzer, ‘it’s stuff and

[017-249] nonsense. _I_ don’t want recreations. I never did, and I never shall; I

[017-250] don’t like ’em. As to their combining together; there are many of them,

[017-251] I have no doubt, that by watching and informing upon one another could

[017-252] earn a trifle now and then, whether in money or good will, and improve

[017-253] their livelihood. Then, why don’t they improve it, ma’am! It’s the

[017-254] first consideration of a rational creature, and it’s what they pretend to

[017-255] want.’

[017-256] ‘Pretend indeed!’ said Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-257] ‘I am sure we are constantly hearing, ma’am, till it becomes quite

[017-258] nauseous, concerning their wives and families,’ said Bitzer. ‘Why look

[017-259] at me, ma’am! I don’t want a wife and family. Why should they?’

[017-260] ‘Because they are improvident,’ said Mrs. Sparsit.

[017-261] ‘Yes, ma’am,’ returned Bitzer, ‘that’s where it is. If they were more

[017-262] provident and less perverse, ma’am, what would they do? They would say,

[017-263] “While my hat covers my family,” or “while my bonnet covers my

[017-264] family,”—as the case might be, ma’am—“I have only one to feed, and that’s

[017-265] the person I most like to feed.”’

[017-266] ‘To be sure,’ assented Mrs. Sparsit, eating muffin.

[017-267] ‘Thank you, ma’am,’ said Bitzer, knuckling his forehead again, in return

[017-268] for the favour of Mrs. Sparsit’s improving conversation. ‘Would you wish

[017-269] a little more hot water, ma’am, or is there anything else that I could

[017-270] fetch you?’

[017-271] ‘Nothing just now, Bitzer.’

[017-272] ‘Thank you, ma’am. I shouldn’t wish to disturb you at your meals, ma’am,

[017-273] particularly tea, knowing your partiality for it,’ said Bitzer, craning a

[017-274] little to look over into the street from where he stood; ‘but there’s a

[017-275] [[gentleman]] been looking up here for a minute or so, ma’am, and he has come

[017-276] across as if he was going to knock. That _is_ his knock, ma’am, no

[017-277] doubt.’

[017-279] confirmed himself with, ‘Yes, ma’am. Would you wish the gentleman to be

[017-281] ‘I don’t know who it can be,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, wiping her mouth and

[017-282] arranging her mittens.

[017-283] ‘A stranger, ma’am, evidently.’

[017-286] Mrs. Sparsit, ‘but I hold a charge in this establishment from Mr.

[017-287] Bounderby, and I will never shrink from it. If to see him is any part of

[017-310] me.’

[017-346] working people; who appeared to have been taking a shower-bath of
