[003-001] A LOOPHOLE

[003-002] MR. GRADGRIND walked homeward from the school, in a state of considerable

[003-003] satisfaction. It was his school, and he intended it to be a model. He

[003-004] intended every [[childhood|child]] in it to be a model—just as the young Gradgrinds

[003-005] were all models.

[003-006] There were five young Gradgrinds, and they were models every one. They

[003-007] had been lectured at, from their tenderest years; coursed, like little

[003-008] hares. Almost as soon as they could run alone, they had been made to run

[003-009] to the lecture-room. The first object with which they had an

[003-010] association, or of which they had a remembrance, was a large black board

[003-011] with a dry Ogre chalking ghastly white figures on it.

[003-012] Not that they knew, by name or [[nature]], anything about an Ogre Fact

[003-013] forbid! I only use the word to express a monster in a lecturing castle,

[003-014] with Heaven knows how many heads manipulated into one, taking childhood

[003-015] captive, and dragging it into gloomy statistical dens by the hair.

[003-016] No little [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] had ever seen a face in the moon; it was up in the

[003-017] moon before it could speak distinctly. No little [[thomas-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] had ever

[003-018] learnt the silly jingle, Twinkle, twinkle, little star; how I wonder what

[003-019] you are! No little Gradgrind had ever known wonder on the subject, each

[003-020] little Gradgrind having at five years old dissected the Great Bear like a

[003-021] Professor Owen, and driven Charles’s Wain like a locomotive

[003-022] engine-driver. No little Gradgrind had ever associated a cow in a field

[003-023] with that famous cow with the crumpled horn who tossed the dog who

[003-024] worried the cat who killed the rat who ate the malt, or with that yet

[003-025] more famous cow who swallowed [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]] Thumb: it had never heard of those

[003-026] celebrities, and had only been introduced to a cow as a graminivorous

[003-027] ruminating quadruped with several stomachs.

[003-028] To his matter-of-[[facts|fact]] home, which was called [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]], Mr. Gradgrind

[003-029] directed his steps. He had virtually retired from the wholesale hardware

[003-030] trade before he built Stone Lodge, and was now looking about for a

[003-031] suitable opportunity of making an arithmetical figure in Parliament.

[003-032] Stone Lodge was situated on a moor within a mile or two of a great

[003-033] town—called [[coketown|Coketown]] in the present faithful guide-book.

[003-034] A very regular feature on the face of the country, Stone Lodge was. Not

[003-035] the least disguise toned down or shaded off that uncompromising fact in

[003-036] the landscape. A great square house, with a heavy portico darkening the

[003-037] principal windows, as its master’s heavy brows overshadowed his eyes. A

[003-038] calculated, cast up, balanced, and proved house. Six windows on this

[003-039] side of the door, six on that side; a total of twelve in this wing, a

[003-040] total of twelve in the other wing; four-and-twenty carried over to the

[003-041] back wings. A lawn and garden and an infant avenue, all ruled straight

[003-042] like a botanical account-book. Gas and ventilation, drainage and

[003-043] water-service, all of the primest quality. Iron clamps and girders,

[003-044] fire-proof from top to bottom; mechanical lifts for the housemaids, with

[003-045] all their brushes and brooms; everything that [[heart]] could desire.

[003-046] Everything? Well, I suppose so. The little Gradgrinds had cabinets in

[003-047] various departments of science too. They had a little conchological

[003-048] cabinet, and a little metallurgical cabinet, and a little mineralogical

[003-049] cabinet; and the specimens were all arranged and labelled, and the bits

[003-050] of stone and ore looked as though they might have been broken from the

[003-051] parent substances by those tremendously hard instruments their own names;

[003-052] and, to paraphrase the idle legend of Peter Piper, who had never found

[003-053] his way into their nursery, If the greedy little Gradgrinds grasped at

[003-054] more than this, what was it for good gracious goodness’ sake, that the

[003-055] greedy little Gradgrinds grasped it!

[003-056] Their father walked on in a hopeful and satisfied frame of mind. He was

[003-057] an affectionate father, after his manner; but he would probably have

[003-058] described himself (if he had been put, like [[sissy-jupe|Sissy Jupe]], upon a

[003-059] definition) as ‘an eminently practical’ father. He had a particular

[003-060] [[pride]] in the phrase eminently practical, which was considered to have a

[003-061] special application to him. Whatsoever the public meeting held in

[003-062] Coketown, and whatsoever the subject of such meeting, some Coketowner was

[003-063] sure to seize the occasion of alluding to his eminently practical friend

[003-064] Gradgrind. This always pleased the eminently practical friend. He knew

[003-065] it to be his due, but his due was acceptable.

[003-066] He had reached the neutral ground upon the outskirts of the town, which

[003-067] was neither town nor country, and yet was either spoiled, when his ears

[003-068] were invaded by the sound of music. The clashing and banging band

[003-069] attached to [[slearys-circus|the horse-riding]] establishment, which had there set up its

[003-070] rest in a wooden pavilion, was in full bray. A flag, floating from the

[003-071] summit of the temple, proclaimed to mankind that it was ‘[[sleary|Sleary]]’s

[003-072] Horse-riding’ which claimed their suffrages. Sleary himself, a stout

[003-073] modern statue with a money-box at its elbow, in an ecclesiastical niche

[003-074] of early Gothic architecture, took the money. [[josephine-sleary|Miss Josephine Sleary]], as

[003-075] some very long and very narrow strips of printed bill announced, was then

[003-076] inaugurating the entertainments with her graceful equestrian Tyrolean

[003-077] flower-act. Among the other pleasing but always strictly moral wonders

[003-078] which must be seen to be believed, Signor Jupe was that afternoon to

[003-079] ‘elucidate the diverting accomplishments of his highly trained performing

[003-080] dog Merrylegs.’ He was also to exhibit ‘his astounding feat of throwing

[003-081] seventy-five hundred-weight in rapid succession backhanded over his [[reason|head]],

[003-082] thus forming a fountain of solid iron in mid-air, a feat never before

[003-083] attempted in this or any other country, and which having elicited such

[003-084] rapturous plaudits from enthusiastic throngs it cannot be withdrawn.’

[003-085] The same Signor Jupe was to ‘enliven the varied performances at frequent

[003-086] intervals with his chaste Shaksperean quips and retorts.’ Lastly, he was

[003-087] to wind them up by appearing in his favourite character of Mr. William

[003-088] Button, of Tooley Street, in ‘the highly novel and laughable

[003-089] hippo-comedietta of The Tailor’s Journey to Brentford.’

[003-090] Thomas Gradgrind took no heed of these trivialities of course, but passed

[003-091] on as a practical man ought to pass on, either brushing the noisy insects

[003-092] from his thoughts, or consigning them to the House of Correction. But,

[003-093] the turning of the road took him by the back of the booth, and at the

[003-094] back of the booth a number of children were congregated in a number of

[003-095] stealthy attitudes, striving to peep in at the hidden glories of the

[003-096] place.

[003-097] This brought him to a stop. ‘Now, to think of these vagabonds,’ said he,

[003-098] ‘attracting the young rabble from a model school.’

[003-099] A space of stunted grass and dry rubbish being between him and the young

[003-100] rabble, he took his eyeglass out of his waistcoat to look for any child

[003-101] he knew by name, and might order off. Phenomenon almost incredible

[003-102] though distinctly seen, what did he then behold but his own metallurgical

[003-103] [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]], peeping with all her might through a hole in a deal board, and

[003-104] his own mathematical Thomas abasing himself on the ground to catch but a

[003-105] hoof of the graceful equestrian Tyrolean flower-act!

[003-106] Dumb with amazement, Mr. Gradgrind crossed to the spot where his [[family]]

[003-107] was thus disgraced, laid his hand upon each erring child, and said:

[003-108] ‘Louisa!! Thomas!!’

[003-109] Both rose, red and disconcerted. But, Louisa looked at her father with

[003-110] more boldness than Thomas did. Indeed, Thomas did not look at him, but

[003-111] gave himself up to be taken home like a machine.

[003-112] ‘In the name of wonder, idleness, and folly!’ said Mr. Gradgrind, leading

[003-113] each away by a hand; ‘what do you do here?’

[003-114] ‘Wanted to see what it was like,’ returned Louisa, shortly.

[003-115] ‘What it was like?’

[003-116] ‘Yes, father.’

[003-117] There was an air of jaded sullenness in them both, and particularly in

[003-118] the girl: yet, struggling through the dissatisfaction of her face, there

[003-119] was a light with nothing to rest upon, a fire with nothing to burn, a

[003-120] starved [[imagination]] keeping life in itself somehow, which brightened its

[003-121] expression. Not with the brightness natural to cheerful youth, but with

[003-122] uncertain, eager, doubtful flashes, which had something painful in them,

[003-123] analogous to the changes on a blind face groping its way.

[003-124] She was a child now, of fifteen or sixteen; but at no distant day would

[003-125] seem to become a woman all at once. Her father thought so as he looked

[003-126] at her. She was pretty. Would have been self-willed (he thought in his

[003-127] eminently practical way) but for her bringing-up.

[003-128] ‘Thomas, though I have the fact before me, I find it difficult to believe

[003-129] that you, with your [[education]] and resources, should have brought your

[003-130] sister to a scene like this.’

[003-131] ‘I brought _him_, father,’ said Louisa, quickly. ‘I asked him to come.’

[003-132] ‘I am sorry to hear it. I am very sorry indeed to hear it. It makes

[003-133] Thomas no better, and it makes you worse, Louisa.’

[003-134] She looked at her father again, but no tear fell down her cheek.

[003-135] ‘You! Thomas and you, to whom the circle of the sciences is open; Thomas

[003-136] and you, who may be said to be replete with facts; Thomas and you, who

[003-137] have been trained to mathematical exactness; Thomas and you, here!’ cried

[003-138] Mr. Gradgrind. ‘In this degraded position! I am amazed.’

[003-139] ‘I was tired, father. I have been tired a long time,’ said Louisa.

[003-140] ‘Tired? Of what?’ asked the astonished father.

[003-141] ‘I don’t know of what—of everything, I think.’

[003-142] ‘Say not another word,’ returned Mr. Gradgrind. ‘You are childish. I

[003-143] will hear no more.’ He did not speak again until they had walked some

[003-144] half-a-mile in silence, when he gravely broke out with: ‘What would your

[003-145] best friends say, Louisa? Do you attach no value to their good opinion?

[003-146] What would [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] say?’ At the mention of this name, his daughter

[003-147] stole a look at him, remarkable for its intense and searching character.

[003-148] He saw nothing of it, for before he looked at her, she had again cast

[003-149] down her eyes!

[003-150] ‘What,’ he repeated presently, ‘would Mr. Bounderby say?’ All the way to

[003-151] Stone Lodge, as with grave indignation he led the two delinquents home,

[003-152] he repeated at intervals ‘What would Mr. Bounderby say?’—as if Mr.

[003-153] Bounderby had been Mrs. Grundy.
