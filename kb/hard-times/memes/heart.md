# Heart

The "heart" in *Hard Times* is not a mere metaphor for emotion — it is an entire epistemology, a way of knowing that stands opposed to the Gradgrindian regime of Facts. Where Facts count and measure, the heart feels and judges. Where Facts reduce persons to cases, the heart recognises each person as irreplaceable. The novel's moral arc — from Gradgrind's "Now, what I want is, Facts" to his final confession that "there is a wisdom of the Heart" — is the arc from a philosophy that denies the heart's authority to one that acknowledges its primacy. (PN: 035-036)

## Definition

In Dickens's moral vocabulary, "heart" names the faculty of sympathetic identification — the capacity to feel with and for another person that makes moral judgement possible. It is not opposed to reason (Gradgrind is not irrational; he reasons perfectly within his premises) but to a reason that has severed itself from feeling. The heart knows what calculation cannot: that Louisa's marriage is wrong not because it is statistically suboptimal but because she does not love Bounderby; that Stephen Blackpool deserves justice not because justice is efficient but because he is a human being. The novel's repeated image of the hearth — the domestic fire around which family warmth gathers — makes the heart spatial and tangible: it is the centre that makes a house a home, and its absence turns Stone Lodge into a mausoleum. (PN: 004-023)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens stages the conflict between Head and Heart as a series of dramatic oppositions. The novel's opening catechism — Gradgrind demanding definitions of a horse from Sissy Jupe and Bitzer — is the paradigm case: Bitzer's factual definition ("Quadruped. Graminivorous. Forty teeth...") is scientifically accurate and humanly worthless, while Sissy's inability to define the horse she loves is the proof of her humanity (PN: 002-018). The heart knows what the head, unaided, cannot access.

The novel's treatment of the heart is not merely sentimental. Dickens shows that the heart, unmoored from any principle, can be as vulnerable as the head is blind. Louisa's emotional starvation does not make her wise — it makes her vulnerable to Harthouse's seduction. Stephen's good heart does not save him from the Old Hell Shaft. The heart is necessary but not sufficient; the education Dickens imagines is the cultivation of a whole person in whom reason and feeling are integrated.

### Key Passages

> "everything that heart could desire" (PN: 003-045)

The narrator's description of Sleary's Circus deliberately invokes the idiom of fairy-tale abundance. The circus offers "everything that heart could desire" not because it possesses material wealth but because it satisfies the human need for play, wonder, and community — precisely what Gradgrind's "nothing but Facts" excludes.

> "go away before I break my heart!" (PN: 006-375)

Sissy Jupe's cry as she is separated from her father introduces the heart in its most elemental sense: as the seat of love that can be broken. Against the elaborate apparatus of Gradgrindian education, Sissy offers a single irreducible fact: love hurts when it is severed.

> "a disheartening circumstance" (PN: 008-037)

The narrator's description of the parliamentary debate uses "disheartening" in its double sense: both discouraging and, literally, heart-removing. The political system, like the educational one, has been organised to exclude the heart, and the result is a citizenry that cannot feel its way to justice.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period inherited from Romanticism a high valuation of feeling. Wordsworth's "spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings" (1800) and Shelley's defence of the "moral imagination" had established the heart as a legitimate organ of moral knowledge. By the 1850s, this Romantic legacy was under pressure from the intellectual prestige of political economy, which treated feeling as an obstacle to rational calculation.

Evangelical Christianity offered a parallel discourse of the heart. John Wesley had preached a "religion of the heart," and the Evangelical revival emphasised personal conversion and emotional sincerity against what it saw as the dry formalism of the established Church. Dickens was suspicious of Evangelical enthusiasm but shared the conviction that moral life begins in the affections, not the intellect.

The novel's appeal to the heart also had a political dimension. The "Condition of England" debates of the 1840s — Carlyle, Engels, Disraeli — had made the industrial system's treatment of workers the central question. Dickens's contribution was to insist that the question was not soluble by statistics alone but required the imaginative capacity to feel the reality of another's suffering.

## Embodied By

- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: The novel's principal embodiment of heart-wisdom. Sissy cannot define a horse but can care for one; she fails Gradgrind's catechism but sustains Louisa through her breakdown. Her knowledge is practical, relational, and grounded in love — exactly the kind of knowledge the Gradgrindian system cannot recognise and cannot do without.
- **[[Rachael]]**: The heart as steadfast fidelity. Rachael's tireless care for Stephen's alcoholic wife — a woman who has no claim on her beyond the claim of common humanity — is the novel's most radical image of disinterested love. She never theorises her conduct; she simply acts, and her acts constitute the novel's alternative ethics.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: The heart as integrity under pressure. Stephen's goodness is not naive — he knows the world is unjust — but he cannot be other than he is. His tragedy is that a good heart, in a system organised to exclude the heart, is not enough to save him.
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]** (in conversion): The heart discovered late. Gradgrind's conversion — from Fact-worship to the "wisdom of the Heart" — is the novel's central argument that the heart's claims are not optional. A life without heart is not merely incomplete; it is destructive.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The novel's organising binary. Facts are measurable, demonstrable, and public; the heart's knowledge is qualitative, intuitive, and personal. The novel does not deny the value of facts — it denies their sufficiency. A life of facts alone is a starvation diet. (PN: 001-002)
- **[[utilitarianism]]**: The intellectual system that elevates the head's logic into a comprehensive social philosophy. The heart, in Benthamite terms, is an immeasurable and therefore irrelevant variable — and the novel's entire plot demonstrates that this exclusion is catastrophic. (PN: 035-036)

## Critical Perspectives

> F.R. Leavis, in *The Great Tradition* (1948), argued that the heart's claims in *Hard Times* constitute a genuine moral philosophy — what he called the "reverent openness before life" that Dickens shared with D.H. Lawrence. For Leavis, the novel's emotional vocabulary was not a weakness but the vehicle of its moral seriousness.

> George Orwell, in "Charles Dickens" (1939), noted that Dickens's appeal to the heart was politically ambiguous because it located moral regeneration in individual conversion rather than structural change. The reformed Gradgrind is a better man, but Coketown remains Coketown.
