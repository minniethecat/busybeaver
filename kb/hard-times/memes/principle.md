# Principle

In *Hard Times*, **principle** is a word on every tongue — but it means something different to each speaker. For Thomas Gradgrind, principle is the rational foundation upon which a life should be constructed: "Now, what I want is, Facts. Teach these boys and girls nothing but Facts. Facts alone are wanted in life. Plant nothing else, and root out everything else" (PN: 001-006). For Josiah Bounderby, principle is the self-justifying rhetoric of the self-made man. For Bitzer, it is the cold logic of self-interest elevated to a moral code. Dickens's achievement is to show that the word "principle," when detached from human sympathy, becomes an instrument of cruelty.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

The novel opens with Gradgrind's declaration of principle: "The principle on which I bring up my own children" is the principle of facts, of measurable reality, of "two and two are four, and nothing over" (PN: 002-003). This is principle as foundation — the ground upon which an entire life-philosophy is built. Dickens's irony, sustained across the whole novel, is that a life built on such a narrow foundation cannot stand.

Gradgrind's great "discovery" — that Sissy Jupe's father works in a circus — is presented as "a new principle, a discovery, a great discovery" (PN: 002-121). The language of scientific breakthrough applied to the revelation that a child's father is a horse-rider exposes the system's grotesque inversion of values: what matters is not the child's distress but the principle of classification.

When Gradgrind's principles are tested against reality, they fail catastrophically. The narrator notes of Louisa that "unlike her father's principles, and her early training" (PN: 023-017), she is drawn to the warmth and humanity of the Harthouse philosophy — a philosophy that is itself morally hollow, suggesting that the flight from Gradgrind's principles can lead to equally dangerous ground.

### Key Passages

> "It was a fundamental principle of the Gradgrind philosophy that everything was to be paid for. Nobody was ever on any account to give anybody anything, or render anybody help without purchase." (PN: 036-059)

This is the novel's most devastating definition of the Gradgrind principle: human obligation reduced to a cash transaction. The principle that sounds like moral rigour is, in practice, the abolition of generosity.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas Gradgrind]]**: The man of principle who discovers, too late, that principles without love are instruments of destruction.
- **[[Bitzer]]**: The perfect product of principled education — a young man who can justify any self-serving action by reference to the principles he was taught.
- **[[Stephen Blackpool]]**: The working man who acts on unspoken principles of loyalty and honesty that make Gradgrind's articulated principles look hollow by comparison.

## Contrasted With

- **[[Heart]]**: Principle without heart produces Bitzer; heart without principle (in excess) produces sentimentality. Dickens seeks a balance that the novel's structure — the triumph of Sissy Jupe's compassionate wisdom — affirms.
- **Compassion**: The virtue that Gradgrind's principles systematically exclude — signally absent from the novel's intellectual vocabulary.
