# Bounderby's Bank

Josiah Bounderby's bank is both a literal financial institution in Coketown and a symbol of the commercial values that the novel subjects to moral scrutiny. It is the scene of the novel's principal crime — Tom Gradgrind's theft — and the institutional embodiment of Bounderby's "self-made" mythology.

## Geography

- **Type**: commercial institution / bank
- **Location**: Coketown
- **Real-world Model**: Victorian joint-stock bank
- **First Mention**: Book I Chapter 4 — Bounderby is introduced as "a rich man: banker, merchant, manufacturer, and what not" (PN: 004-007)

## Description

Dickens's treatment of the bank is characteristically double-edged. It is the source of Bounderby's wealth and authority, yet it is also the instrument of foreclosure and dispossession: "The bank had foreclosed a mortgage" (PN: 023-069) on one of the Coketown properties, a detail that quietly registers the human cost of Bounderby's commercial empire. The bank is never described in architectural detail — Dickens treats it as invisible infrastructure, the hidden engine of Coketown's economy, visible only in its effects.

## Symbolic Function

The bank serves two symbolic functions in the novel. First, it is the institutional face of Bounderby's self-made-man ideology — he repeatedly invokes his rise from the gutter to the bank's ownership as proof that anyone can succeed through grit alone, a claim the novel systematically dismantles. Second, the bank robbery becomes the novel's mechanism for exposing the moral failure of Gradgrind's Utilitarian education: Tom Gradgrind, reared on Facts and self-interest without moral instruction, becomes the bank's thief.

## Key Events

- **The Bank Robbery**: Tom Gradgrind, the Utilitarian-educated son, steals from Bounderby's bank and frames Stephen Blackpool. The crime is foreshadowed by Mrs. Sparsit's suspicion: "I had suspected young Mr. Tom of this bank-robbery from the first" (PN: 036-024). The robbery is the novel's principal crime plot, driving the action of Book III. (PN: 036-024–036-083)

## In the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Bounderby's identity as a banker is established early. He is "a rich man: banker, merchant, manufacturer" (PN: 004-007), the triple identity that makes him Coketown's leading citizen. The bank is mentioned as part of his biography rather than as a physical place — it is the source of his wealth, not a setting.

### Book II: Reaping

Tom Gradgrind is placed in the bank by Bounderby as a clerk. Mrs. Pegler, Bounderby's mother, reveals that her son "was stuck into old Bounderby's bank (where I never wanted to be)" (PN: 019-087) — the phrase "stuck into" suggesting entrapment rather than opportunity. The bank becomes the site of Tom's accumulating debts.

### Book III: Garnering

The bank robbery is discovered and becomes the novel's climactic mystery. Tom is revealed as the thief, Stephen Blackpool dies clearing his name, and Bounderby's bank — the symbol of his commercial integrity — is exposed as only as honest as the men who run it.
