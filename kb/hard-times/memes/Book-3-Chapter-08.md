[036-001] PHILOSOPHICAL

[036-002] THEY went back into the booth, [[sleary|Sleary]] shutting the door to keep intruders

[036-003] out. [[bitzer|Bitzer]], still holding the paralysed culprit by the collar, stood in

[036-004] the Ring, blinking at his old patron through the darkness of the

[036-005] twilight.

[036-006] ‘Bitzer,’ said [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], broken down, and miserably submissive to

[036-007] him, ‘have you a [[heart]]?’

[036-008] ‘The circulation, sir,’ returned Bitzer, smiling at the oddity of the

[036-009] question, ‘couldn’t be carried on without one. No man, sir, acquainted

[036-010] with the [[facts]] established by Harvey relating to the circulation of the

[036-011] blood, can doubt that I have a heart.’

[036-012] ‘Is it accessible,’ cried Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], ‘to any compassionate

[036-013] influence?’

[036-014] ‘It is accessible to [[reason|Reason]], sir,’ returned the excellent young man.

[036-015] ‘And to nothing else.’

[036-016] They stood looking at each other; Mr. Gradgrind’s face as white as the

[036-017] pursuer’s.

[036-018] ‘What motive—even what motive in reason—can you have for preventing the

[036-019] escape of this wretched [[childhood|youth]],’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘and crushing his

[036-020] miserable father? See his sister here. Pity us!’

[036-021] ‘Sir,’ returned Bitzer, in a very business-like and logical manner,

[036-022] ‘since you ask me what motive I have in reason, for taking young Mr. [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]]

[036-023] back to [[coketown|Coketown]], it is only reasonable to let you know. I have

[036-024] suspected young Mr. Tom of this bank-robbery from the first. I had had

[036-025] my eye upon him before that time, for I knew his ways. I have kept my

[036-026] observations to myself, but I have made them; and I have got ample proofs

[036-027] against him now, besides his running away, and besides his own

[036-028] confession, which I was just in time to overhear. I had the pleasure of

[036-029] watching your house yesterday morning, and following you here. I am

[036-030] going to take young Mr. Tom back to Coketown, in order to deliver him

[036-031] over to [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]. Sir, I have no doubt whatever that Mr. Bounderby

[036-032] will then promote me to young Mr. Tom’s situation. And I wish to have

[036-033] his situation, sir, for it will be a rise to me, and will do me good.’

[036-034] ‘If this is solely a question of [[self-interest]] with you—’ Mr. Gradgrind

[036-035] began.

[036-036] ‘I beg your pardon for interrupting you, sir,’ returned Bitzer; ‘but I am

[036-037] sure you know that the whole social system is a question of

[036-038] self-interest. What you must always appeal to, is a person’s

[036-039] self-interest. It’s your only hold. We are so constituted. I was

[036-040] brought up in that catechism when I was very young, sir, as you are

[036-041] aware.’

[036-042] ‘What sum of money,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘will you set against your

[036-043] expected promotion?’

[036-044] ‘Thank you, sir,’ returned Bitzer, ‘for hinting at the proposal; but I

[036-045] will not set any sum against it. Knowing that your clear head would

[036-046] propose that alternative, I have gone over the calculations in my mind;

[036-047] and I find that to compound a felony, even on very high terms indeed,

[036-048] would not be as safe and good for me as my improved prospects in the

[036-049] Bank.’

[036-050] ‘Bitzer,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, stretching out his hands as though he would

[036-051] have said, See how miserable I am! ‘Bitzer, I have but one chance left

[036-052] to soften you. You were many years at my school. If, in remembrance of

[036-053] the pains bestowed upon you there, you can persuade yourself in any

[036-054] degree to disregard your present interest and release my son, I entreat

[036-055] and pray you to give him the benefit of that remembrance.’

[036-056] ‘I really wonder, sir,’ rejoined the old pupil in an argumentative

[036-057] manner, ‘to find you taking a position so untenable. My [[education|schooling]] was

[036-058] paid for; it was a bargain; and when I came away, the bargain ended.’

[036-059] It was a fundamental principle of the Gradgrind philosophy that

[036-060] everything was to be paid for. Nobody was ever on any account to give

[036-061] anybody anything, or render anybody help without purchase. Gratitude was

[036-062] to be abolished, and the virtues springing from it were not to be. Every

[036-063] inch of the existence of mankind, from birth to death, was to be a

[036-064] bargain across a counter. And if we didn’t get to Heaven that way, it

[036-065] was not a politico-economical place, and we had no business there.

[036-066] ‘I don’t deny,’ added Bitzer, ‘that my schooling was cheap. But that

[036-067] comes right, sir. I was made in the cheapest market, and have to dispose

[036-068] of myself in the dearest.’

[036-069] He was a little troubled here, by [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] and [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] crying.

[036-070] ‘Pray don’t do that,’ said he, ‘it’s of no use doing that: it only

[036-071] worries. You seem to think that I have some animosity against young Mr.

[036-072] Tom; whereas I have none at all. I am only going, on the reasonable

[036-073] grounds I have mentioned, to take him back to Coketown. If he was to

[036-074] resist, I should set up the cry of Stop thief! But, he won’t resist, you

[036-075] may depend upon it.’

[036-076] Mr. Sleary, who with his mouth open and his rolling eye as immovably

[036-077] jammed in his head as his fixed one, had listened to these doctrines with

[036-078] profound attention, here stepped forward.

[036-079] ‘Thquire, you know perfectly well, and your daughter knowth perfectly

[036-080] well (better than you, becauthe I thed it to her), that I didn’t know

[036-081] what your thon had done, and that I didn’t want to know—I thed it wath

[036-082] better not, though I only thought, then, it wath thome thkylarking.

[036-083] However, thith young man having made it known to be a robbery of a bank,

[036-084] why, that’h a theriouth thing; muth too theriouth a thing for me to

[036-085] compound, ath thith young man hath very properly called it.

[036-086] Conthequently, Thquire, you muthn’t quarrel with me if I take thith young

[036-087] man’th thide, and thay he’th right and there’th no help for it. But I

[036-088] tell you what I’ll do, Thquire; I’ll drive your thon and thith young man

[036-089] over to the rail, and prevent expothure here. I can’t conthent to do

[036-090] more, but I’ll do that.’

[036-091] Fresh lamentations from Louisa, and deeper affliction on Mr. Gradgrind’s

[036-092] part, followed this desertion of them by their last friend. But, Sissy

[036-093] glanced at him with great attention; nor did she in her own breast

[036-094] misunderstand him. As they were all going out again, he favoured her

[036-095] with one slight roll of his movable eye, desiring her to linger behind.

[036-096] As he locked the door, he said excitedly:

[036-097] ‘The Thquire thtood by you, Thethilia, and I’ll thtand by the Thquire.

[036-098] More than that: thith ith a prethiouth rathcal, and belongth to that

[036-099] bluthtering Cove that my people nearly pitht out o’ winder. It’ll be a

[036-100] dark night; I’ve got a horthe that’ll do anything but thpeak; I’ve got a

[036-101] pony that’ll go fifteen mile an hour with Childerth driving of him; I’ve

[036-102] got a dog that’ll keep a man to one plathe four-and-twenty hourth. Get a

[036-103] word with the young Thquire. Tell him, when he theeth our horthe begin

[036-104] to danthe, not to be afraid of being thpilt, but to look out for a

[036-105] pony-gig coming up. Tell him, when he theeth that gig clothe by, to jump

[036-106] down, and it’ll take him off at a rattling pathe. If my dog leth thith

[036-107] young man thtir a peg on foot, I give him leave to go. And if my horthe

[036-108] ever thtirth from that thpot where he beginth a danthing, till the

[036-109] morning—I don’t know him?—Tharp’th the word!’

[036-110] The word was so sharp, that in ten minutes Mr. Childers, sauntering about

[036-111] the market-place in a pair of slippers, had his cue, and Mr. Sleary’s

[036-112] equipage was ready. It was a fine sight, to behold the learned dog

[036-113] barking round it, and Mr. Sleary instructing him, with his one

[036-114] practicable eye, that Bitzer was the object of his particular attentions.

[036-115] Soon after dark they all three got in and started; the learned dog (a

[036-116] formidable creature) already pinning Bitzer with his eye, and sticking

[036-117] close to the wheel on his side, that he might be ready for him in the

[036-118] event of his showing the slightest disposition to alight.

[036-119] The other three sat up at the inn all night in great suspense. At eight

[036-120] o’clock in the morning Mr. Sleary and the dog reappeared: both in high

[036-121] spirits.

[036-122] ‘All right, Thquire!’ said Mr. Sleary, ‘your thon may be aboard-a-thip by

[036-123] thith time. Childerth took him off, an hour and a half after we left

[036-124] there latht night. The horthe danthed the polka till he wath dead beat

[036-125] (he would have walthed if he hadn’t been in harneth), and then I gave him

[036-126] the word and he went to thleep comfortable. When that prethiouth young

[036-127] Rathcal thed he’d go for’ard afoot, the dog hung on to hith

[036-128] neck-hankercher with all four legth in the air and pulled him down and

[036-129] rolled him over. Tho he come back into the drag, and there he that,

[036-130] ’till I turned the horthe’th head, at half-patht thixth thith morning.’

[036-131] Mr. Gradgrind overwhelmed him with thanks, of course; and hinted as

[036-132] delicately as he could, at a handsome remuneration in money.

[036-133] ‘I don’t want money mythelf, Thquire; but Childerth ith a [[family]] man, and

[036-134] if you wath to like to offer him a five-pound note, it mightn’t be

[036-135] unactheptable. Likewithe if you wath to thtand a collar for the dog, or

[036-136] a thet of bellth for the horthe, I thould be very glad to take ’em.

[036-137] Brandy and water I alwayth take.’ He had already called for a glass, and

[036-138] now called for another. ‘If you wouldn’t think it going too far,

[036-139] Thquire, to make a little thpread for the company at about three and

[036-140] thixth ahead, not reckoning Luth, it would make ’em happy.’

[036-141] All these little tokens of his gratitude, Mr. Gradgrind very willingly

[036-142] undertook to render. Though he thought them far too slight, he said, for

[036-143] such a service.

[036-144] ‘Very well, Thquire; then, if you’ll only give a Horthe-riding, a

[036-145] bethpeak, whenever you can, you’ll more than balanthe the account. Now,

[036-146] Thquire, if your daughter will ethcuthe me, I thould like one parting

[036-147] word with you.’

[036-148] Louisa and Sissy withdrew into an adjoining room; Mr. Sleary, stirring

[036-149] and drinking his brandy and water as he stood, went on:

[036-150] ‘Thquire,—you don’t need to be told that dogth ith wonderful animalth.’

[036-151] ‘Their instinct,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘is surprising.’

[036-152] ‘Whatever you call it—and I’m bletht if _I_ know what to call it’—said

[036-153] Sleary, ‘it ith athtonithing. The way in whith a dog’ll find you—the

[036-154] dithtanthe he’ll come!’

[036-155] ‘His scent,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘being so fine.’

[036-156] ‘I’m bletht if I know what to call it,’ repeated Sleary, shaking his

[036-157] head, ‘but I have had dogth find me, Thquire, in a way that made me think

[036-158] whether that dog hadn’t gone to another dog, and thed, “You don’t happen

[036-159] to know a perthon of the name of Thleary, do you? Perthon of the name of

[036-160] Thleary, in the Horthe-Riding way—thtout man—game eye?” And whether that

[036-161] dog mightn’t have thed, “Well, I can’t thay I know him mythelf, but I

[036-162] know a dog that I think would be likely to be acquainted with him.” And

[036-163] whether that dog mightn’t have thought it over, and thed, “Thleary,

[036-164] Thleary! O yeth, to be thure! A friend of mine menthioned him to me at

[036-165] one time. I can get you hith addreth directly.” In conthequenth of my

[036-166] being afore the public, and going about tho muth, you thee, there mutht

[036-167] be a number of dogth acquainted with me, Thquire, that _I_ don’t know!’

[036-168] Mr. Gradgrind seemed to be quite confounded by this speculation.

[036-169] ‘Any way,’ said Sleary, after putting his lips to his brandy and water,

[036-170] ‘ith fourteen month ago, Thquire, thinthe we wath at Chethter. We wath

[036-171] getting up our Children in the Wood one morning, when there cometh into

[036-172] our Ring, by the thtage door, a dog. He had travelled a long way, he

[036-173] wath in a very bad condithon, he wath lame, and pretty well blind. He

[036-174] went round to our children, one after another, as if he wath a theeking

[036-175] for a child he know’d; and then he come to me, and throwd hithelf up

[036-176] behind, and thtood on hith two forelegth, weak ath he wath, and then he

[036-177] wagged hith tail and died. Thquire, that dog wath Merrylegth.’

[036-178] ‘Sissy’s father’s dog!’

[036-179] ‘Thethilia’th father’th old dog. Now, Thquire, I can take my oath, from

[036-180] my knowledge of that dog, that that man wath dead—and buried—afore that

[036-181] dog come back to me. Joth’phine and Childerth and me talked it over a

[036-182] long time, whether I thould write or not. But we agreed, “No. There’th

[036-183] nothing comfortable to tell; why unthettle her mind, and make her

[036-184] unhappy?” Tho, whether her father bathely detherted her; or whether he

[036-185] broke hith own heart alone, rather than pull her down along with him;

[036-186] never will be known, now, Thquire, till—no, not till we know how the

[036-187] dogth findth uth out!’

[036-188] ‘She keeps the bottle that he sent her for, to this hour; and she will

[036-189] believe in his [[love|affection]] to the last moment of her life,’ said Mr.

[036-190] Gradgrind.

[036-191] ‘It theemth to prethent two thingth to a perthon, don’t it, Thquire?’

[036-192] said Mr. Sleary, musing as he looked down into the depths of his brandy

[036-193] and water: ‘one, that there ith a love in the world, not all

[036-194] Thelf-interetht after all, but thomething very different; t’other, that

[036-195] it hath a way of ith own of calculating or not calculating, whith

[036-196] thomehow or another ith at leatht ath hard to give a name to, ath the

[036-197] wayth of the dogth ith!’

[036-198] Mr. Gradgrind looked out of window, and made no reply. Mr. Sleary

[036-199] emptied his glass and recalled the ladies.

[036-200] ‘Thethilia my dear, kith me and good-bye! Mith Thquire, to thee you

[036-201] treating of her like a thithter, and a thithter that you trutht and

[036-202] honour with all your heart and more, ith a very pretty thight to me. I

[036-203] hope your brother may live to be better detherving of you, and a greater

[036-204] comfort to you. Thquire, thake handth, firtht and latht! Don’t be croth

[036-205] with uth poor vagabondth. People mutht be amuthed. They can’t be

[036-206] alwayth a learning, nor yet they can’t be alwayth a working, they an’t

[036-207] made for it. You _mutht_ have uth, Thquire. Do the withe thing and the

[036-208] kind thing too, and make the betht of uth; not the wurtht!’

[036-209] ‘And I never thought before,’ said Mr. Sleary, putting his head in at the

[036-210] door again to say it, ‘that I wath tho muth of a Cackler!’
