[032-001] LOST

[032-002] THE robbery at the Bank had not languished before, and did not cease to

[032-003] occupy a front place in the attention of the principal of that

[032-004] establishment now. In boastful proof of his promptitude and activity, as

[032-005] a remarkable man, and a self-made man, and a commercial wonder more

[032-006] admirable than Venus, who had risen out of the mud instead of the sea, he

[032-007] liked to [[performance|show]] how little his domestic affairs abated his business ardour.

[032-008] Consequently, in the first few weeks of his resumed bachelorhood, he even

[032-009] advanced upon his usual display of bustle, and every day made such a rout

[032-010] in renewing his investigations into the robbery, that the officers who

[032-011] had it in hand almost wished it had never been committed.

[032-012] They were at fault too, and off the scent. Although they had been so

[032-013] quiet since the first outbreak of the matter, that most people really did

[032-014] suppose it to have been abandoned as hopeless, nothing new occurred. No

[032-015] implicated man or woman took untimely courage, or made a self-betraying

[032-016] step. More remarkable yet, [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]] could not be heard of, and

[032-017] [[mrs-pegler|the mysterious old woman]] remained a mystery.

[032-018] Things having come to this pass, and showing no latent signs of stirring

[032-019] beyond it, the upshot of [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s investigations was, that he

[032-020] resolved to hazard a bold burst. He drew up a placard, offering Twenty

[032-021] Pounds reward for the apprehension of Stephen Blackpool, suspected of

[032-022] complicity in the robbery of [[coketown|Coketown]] Bank on such a night; he described

[032-023] the said Stephen Blackpool by dress, complexion, estimated height, and

[032-024] manner, as minutely as he could; he recited how he had left the town, and

[032-025] in what direction he had been last seen going; he had the whole printed

[032-026] in great black letters on a staring broadsheet; and he caused the walls

[032-027] to be posted with it in the dead of night, so that it should strike upon

[032-028] the sight of the whole population at one blow.

[032-029] The factory-bells had need to ring their loudest that morning to disperse

[032-030] the groups of workers who stood in the tardy daybreak, collected round

[032-031] the placards, devouring them with eager eyes. Not the least eager of the

[032-032] eyes assembled, were the eyes of those who could not read. These people,

[032-033] as they listened to the friendly voice that read aloud—there was always

[032-034] some such ready to help them—stared at the characters which meant so much

[032-035] with a vague awe and respect that would have been half ludicrous, if any

[032-036] aspect of public ignorance could ever be otherwise than threatening and

[032-037] full of evil. Many ears and eyes were busy with a vision of the matter

[032-038] of these placards, among turning spindles, rattling looms, and whirling

[032-039] wheels, for hours afterwards; and when the Hands cleared out again into

[032-040] the streets, there were still as many readers as before.

[032-041] [[slackbridge|Slackbridge]], the delegate, had to address his audience too that night;

[032-042] and Slackbridge had obtained a clean bill from the printer, and had

[032-043] brought it in his pocket. Oh, my friends and fellow-countrymen, the

[032-044] down-trodden operatives of Coketown, oh, my fellow-brothers and

[032-045] fellow-workmen and fellow-citizens and fellow-men, what a to-do was

[032-046] there, when Slackbridge unfolded what he called ‘that damning document,’

[032-047] and held it up to the gaze, and for the execration of the working-man

[032-048] community! ‘Oh, my fellow-men, behold of what a traitor in the camp of

[032-049] those great spirits who are enrolled upon the holy scroll of Justice and

[032-050] of Union, is appropriately capable! Oh, my prostrate friends, with the

[032-051] galling yoke of tyrants on your necks and the iron foot of despotism

[032-052] treading down your fallen forms into the dust of the earth, upon which

[032-053] right glad would your oppressors be to see you creeping on your bellies

[032-054] all the days of your lives, like the serpent in the garden—oh, my

[032-055] brothers, and shall I as a man not add, my sisters too, what do you say,

[032-056] _now_, of Stephen Blackpool, with a slight stoop in his shoulders and

[032-057] about five foot seven in height, as set forth in this degrading and

[032-058] disgusting document, this blighting bill, this pernicious placard, this

[032-059] abominable advertisement; and with what majesty of denouncement will you

[032-060] crush the viper, who would bring this stain and [[shame]] upon the God-like

[032-061] race that happily has cast him out for ever! Yes, my compatriots,

[032-062] happily cast him out and sent him forth! For you remember how he stood

[032-063] here before you on this platform; you remember how, face to face and foot

[032-064] to foot, I pursued him through all his intricate windings; you remember

[032-065] how he sneaked and slunk, and sidled, and splitted of straws, until, with

[032-066] not an inch of ground to which to cling, I hurled him out from amongst

[032-067] us: an object for the undying finger of scorn to point at, and for the

[032-068] avenging fire of every free and thinking mind to scorch and scar! And

[032-069] now, my friends—my labouring friends, for I rejoice and triumph in that

[032-070] stigma—my friends whose hard but honest beds are made in toil, and whose

[032-071] scanty but independent pots are boiled in hardship; and now, I say, my

[032-072] friends, what appellation has that dastard craven taken to himself, when,

[032-073] with the mask torn from his features, he stands before us in all his

[032-074] native deformity, a What? A thief! A plunderer! A proscribed fugitive,

[032-075] with a price upon his [[reason|head]]; a fester and a wound upon the noble character

[032-076] of the Coketown operative! Therefore, my band of brothers in a sacred

[032-077] bond, to which your [[childhood|children]] and your children’s children yet unborn have

[032-078] set their infant hands and seals, I propose to you on the part of the

[032-079] United Aggregate Tribunal, ever watchful for your welfare, ever zealous

[032-080] for your benefit, that this meeting does Resolve: That Stephen Blackpool,

[032-081] weaver, referred to in this placard, having been already solemnly

[032-082] disowned by the community of Coketown Hands, the same are free from the

[032-083] shame of his misdeeds, and cannot as a [[class]] be reproached with his

[032-084] dishonest actions!’

[032-085] Thus Slackbridge; gnashing and perspiring after a prodigious sort. A few

[032-086] stern voices called out ‘No!’ and a score or two hailed, with assenting

[032-087] cries of ‘Hear, hear!’ the caution from one man, ‘Slackbridge, y’or over

[032-088] hetter in’t; y’or a goen too fast!’ But these were pigmies against an

[032-089] army; the general assemblage subscribed to the gospel according to

[032-090] Slackbridge, and gave three cheers for him, as he sat demonstratively

[032-091] panting at them.

[032-092] These men and women were yet in the streets, passing quietly to their

[032-093] homes, when [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]], who had been called away from [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] some minutes

[032-094] before, returned.

[032-095] ‘Who is it?’ asked Louisa.

[032-096] ‘It is Mr. Bounderby,’ said Sissy, timid of the name, ‘and your brother

[032-097] Mr. [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]], and a young woman who says her name is [[rachael|Rachael]], and that you

[032-098] know her.’

[032-099] ‘What do they want, Sissy [[love|dear]]?’

[032-100] ‘They want to see you. Rachael has been crying, and seems angry.’

[032-101] ‘Father,’ said Louisa, for he was present, ‘I cannot refuse to see them,

[032-102] for a reason that will explain itself. Shall they come in here?’

[032-103] As he answered in the affirmative, Sissy went away to bring them. She

[032-104] reappeared with them directly. Tom was last; and remained standing in

[032-105] the obscurest part of the room, near the door.

[032-106] ‘Mrs. Bounderby,’ said her husband, entering with a cool nod, ‘I don’t

[032-107] disturb you, I hope. This is an unseasonable hour, but here is a young

[032-108] woman who has been making statements which render my visit necessary.

[032-109] Tom [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], as your son, young Tom, refuses for some obstinate reason

[032-110] or other to say anything at all about those statements, good or bad, I am

[032-111] obliged to confront her with your daughter.’

[032-112] ‘You have seen me once before, young lady,’ said Rachael, standing in

[032-113] front of Louisa.

[032-114] Tom coughed.

[032-115] ‘You have seen me, young lady,’ repeated Rachael, as she did not answer,

[032-116] ‘once before.’

[032-117] Tom coughed again.

[032-118] ‘I have.’

[032-119] Rachael cast her eyes proudly towards Mr. Bounderby, and said, ‘Will you

[032-120] make it known, young lady, where, and who was there?’

[032-121] ‘I went to the house where Stephen Blackpool lodged, on the night of his

[032-122] discharge from his work, and I saw you there. He was there too; and an

[032-123] old woman who did not speak, and whom I could scarcely see, stood in a

[032-124] dark corner. My brother was with me.’

[032-125] ‘Why couldn’t you say so, young Tom?’ demanded Bounderby.

[032-126] ‘I promised my sister I wouldn’t.’ Which Louisa hastily confirmed. ‘And

[032-127] besides,’ said the whelp bitterly, ‘she tells her own story so precious

[032-128] well—and so full—that what business had I to take it out of her mouth!’

[032-129] ‘Say, young lady, if you please,’ pursued Rachael, ‘why, in an evil hour,

[032-130] you ever came to Stephen’s that night.’

[032-131] ‘I felt compassion for him,’ said Louisa, her colour deepening, ‘and I

[032-132] wished to know what he was going to do, and wished to offer him

[032-133] assistance.’

[032-134] ‘Thank you, ma’am,’ said Bounderby. ‘Much flattered and obliged.’

[032-135] ‘Did you offer him,’ asked Rachael, ‘a bank-note?’

[032-136] ‘Yes; but he refused it, and would only take two pounds in gold.’

[032-137] Rachael cast her eyes towards Mr. Bounderby again.

[032-138] ‘Oh, certainly!’ said Bounderby. ‘If you put the question whether your

[032-139] ridiculous and improbable account was true or not, I am bound to say it’s

[032-140] confirmed.’

[032-141] ‘Young lady,’ said Rachael, ‘Stephen Blackpool is now named as a thief in

[032-142] public print all over this town, and where else! There have been a

[032-143] meeting to-night where he have been spoken of in the same shameful way.

[032-144] Stephen! The honestest lad, the truest lad, the best!’ Her indignation

[032-145] failed her, and she broke off sobbing.

[032-146] ‘I am very, very sorry,’ said Louisa.

[032-147] ‘Oh, young lady, young lady,’ returned Rachael, ‘I hope you may be, but I

[032-148] don’t know! I can’t say what you may ha’ done! The like of you don’t

[032-149] know us, don’t care for us, don’t belong to us. I am not sure why you

[032-150] may ha’ come that night. I can’t tell but what you may ha’ come wi’ some

[032-151] aim of your own, not mindin to what trouble you brought such as the poor

[032-152] lad. I said then, Bless you for coming; and I said it of my [[heart]], you

[032-153] seemed to take so pitifully to him; but I don’t know now, I don’t know!’

[032-154] Louisa could not reproach her for her unjust suspicions; she was so

[032-155] faithful to her idea of the man, and so afflicted.

[032-156] ‘And when I think,’ said Rachael through her sobs, ‘that the poor lad was

[032-157] so grateful, thinkin you so good to him—when I mind that he put his hand

[032-158] over his hard-worken face to hide the tears that you brought up there—Oh,

[032-159] I hope you may be sorry, and ha’ no bad cause to be it; but I don’t know,

[032-160] I don’t know!’

[032-161] ‘You’re a pretty article,’ growled the whelp, moving uneasily in his dark

[032-162] corner, ‘to come here with these precious imputations! You ought to be

[032-163] bundled out for not knowing how to behave yourself, and you would be by

[032-164] rights.’

[032-165] She said nothing in reply; and her low weeping was the only sound that

[032-166] was heard, until Mr. Bounderby spoke.

[032-167] ‘Come!’ said he, ‘you know what you have engaged to do. You had better

[032-168] give your mind to that; not this.’

[032-169] ‘’Deed, I am loath,’ returned Rachael, drying her eyes, ‘that any here

[032-170] should see me like this; but I won’t be seen so again. Young lady, when

[032-171] I had read what’s put in print of Stephen—and what has just as much [[truth]]

[032-172] in it as if it had been put in print of you—I went straight to the Bank

[032-173] to say I knew where Stephen was, and to give a sure and certain promise

[032-174] that he should be here in two days. I couldn’t meet wi’ Mr. Bounderby

[032-175] then, and your brother sent me away, and I tried to find you, but you was

[032-176] not to be found, and I went back to work. Soon as I come out of the Mill

[032-177] to-night, I hastened to hear what was said of Stephen—for I know wi’

[032-178] [[pride]] he will come back to shame it!—and then I went again to seek Mr.

[032-179] Bounderby, and I found him, and I told him every word I knew; and he

[032-180] believed no word I said, and brought me here.’

[032-181] ‘So far, that’s true enough,’ assented Mr. Bounderby, with his hands in

[032-182] his pockets and his hat on. ‘But I have known you people before to-day,

[032-183] you’ll observe, and I know you never die for want of talking. Now, I

[032-184] recommend you not so much to mind talking just now, as doing. You have

[032-185] undertaken to do something; all I remark upon that at present is, do it!’

[032-186] ‘I have written to Stephen by the post that went out this afternoon, as I

[032-187] have written to him once before sin’ he went away,’ said Rachael; ‘and he

[032-188] will be here, at furthest, in two days.’

[032-189] ‘Then, I’ll tell you something. You are not aware perhaps,’ retorted Mr.

[032-190] Bounderby, ‘that you yourself have been looked after now and then, not

[032-191] being considered quite free from suspicion in this business, on account

[032-192] of most people being judged according to the company they keep. The

[032-193] post-office hasn’t been forgotten either. What I’ll tell you is, that no

[032-194] letter to Stephen Blackpool has ever got into it. Therefore, what has

[032-195] become of yours, I leave you to guess. Perhaps you’re mistaken, and

[032-196] never wrote any.’

[032-197] ‘He hadn’t been gone from here, young lady,’ said Rachael, turning

[032-198] appealingly to Louisa, ‘as much as a week, when he sent me the only

[032-199] letter I have had from him, saying that he was forced to seek work in

[032-200] another name.’

[032-201] ‘Oh, by George!’ cried Bounderby, shaking his head, with a whistle, ‘he

[032-202] changes his name, does he! That’s rather unlucky, too, for such an

[032-203] immaculate chap. It’s considered a little suspicious in Courts of

[032-204] Justice, I believe, when an Innocent happens to have many names.’

[032-205] ‘What,’ said Rachael, with the tears in her eyes again, ‘what, young

[032-206] lady, in the name of Mercy, was left the poor lad to do! The masters

[032-207] against him on one hand, the men against him on the other, he only wantin

[032-208] to work hard in peace, and do what he felt right. Can a man have no soul

[032-209] of his own, no mind of his own? Must he go wrong all through wi’ this

[032-210] side, or must he go wrong all through wi’ that, or else be hunted like a

[032-211] hare?’

[032-212] ‘Indeed, indeed, I pity him from my heart,’ returned Louisa; ‘and I hope

[032-213] that he will clear himself.’

[032-214] ‘You need have no fear of that, young lady. He is sure!’

[032-215] ‘All the surer, I suppose,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘for your refusing to

[032-216] tell where he is? Eh?’

[032-217] ‘He shall not, through any act of mine, come back wi’ the unmerited

[032-218] reproach of being brought back. He shall come back of his own accord to

[032-219] clear himself, and put all those that have injured his good character,

[032-220] and he not here for its defence, to shame. I have told him what has been

[032-221] done against him,’ said Rachael, throwing off all distrust as a rock

[032-222] throws off the sea, ‘and he will be here, at furthest, in two days.’

[032-223] ‘Notwithstanding which,’ added Mr. Bounderby, ‘if he can be laid hold of

[032-224] any sooner, he shall have an earlier opportunity of clearing himself. As

[032-225] to you, I have nothing against you; what you came and told me turns out

[032-226] to be true, and I have given you the means of proving it to be true, and

[032-227] there’s an end of it. I wish you good night all! I must be off to look

[032-228] a little further into this.’

[032-229] Tom came out of his corner when Mr. Bounderby moved, moved with him, kept

[032-230] close to him, and went away with him. The only parting salutation of

[032-231] which he delivered himself was a sulky ‘Good night, father!’ With a

[032-232] brief speech, and a scowl at his sister, he left the house.

[032-233] Since his sheet-anchor had come home, [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] had been sparing of

[032-234] speech. He still sat silent, when Louisa mildly said:

[032-235] ‘Rachael, you will not distrust me one day, when you know me better.’

[032-236] ‘It goes against me,’ Rachael answered, in a gentler manner, ‘to mistrust

[032-237] any one; but when I am so mistrusted—when we all are—I cannot keep such

[032-238] things quite out of my mind. I ask your pardon for having done you an

[032-239] injury. I don’t think what I said now. Yet I might come to think it

[032-240] again, wi’ the poor lad so wronged.’

[032-241] ‘Did you tell him in your letter,’ inquired Sissy, ‘that suspicion seemed

[032-242] to have fallen upon him, because he had been seen about the Bank at

[032-243] night? He would then know what he would have to explain on coming back,

[032-244] and would be ready.’

[032-245] ‘Yes, dear,’ she returned; ‘but I can’t guess what can have ever taken

[032-246] him there. He never used to go there. It was never in his way. His way

[032-247] was the same as mine, and not near it.’

[032-248] Sissy had already been at her side asking her where she lived, and

[032-249] whether she might come to-morrow night, to inquire if there were news of

[032-250] him.

[032-251] ‘I doubt,’ said Rachael, ‘if he can be here till next day.’

[032-252] ‘Then I will come next night too,’ said Sissy.

[032-253] When Rachael, assenting to this, was gone, Mr. Gradgrind lifted up his

[032-254] head, and said to his daughter:

[032-255] ‘Louisa, my dear, I have never, that I know of, seen this man. Do you

[032-256] believe him to be implicated?’

[032-257] ‘I think I have believed it, father, though with great difficulty. I do

[032-258] not believe it now.’

[032-259] ‘That is to say, you once persuaded yourself to believe it, from knowing

[032-260] him to be suspected. His appearance and manner; are they so honest?’

[032-261] ‘Very honest.’

[032-262] ‘And her confidence not to be shaken! I ask myself,’ said Mr. Gradgrind,

[032-263] musing, ‘does the real culprit know of these accusations? Where is he?

[032-264] Who is he?’

[032-265] His hair had latterly began to change its colour. As he leaned upon his

[032-266] hand again, looking gray and old, Louisa, with a face of fear and pity,

[032-267] hurriedly went over to him, and sat close at his side. Her eyes by

[032-268] accident met Sissy’s at the moment. Sissy flushed and started, and

[032-269] Louisa put her finger on her lip.

[032-270] Next night, when Sissy returned home and told Louisa that Stephen was not

[032-271] come, she told it in a whisper. Next night again, when she came home

[032-272] with the same account, and added that he had not been heard of, she spoke

[032-273] in the same low frightened tone. From the moment of that interchange of

[032-274] looks, they never uttered his name, or any reference to him, aloud; nor

[032-275] ever pursued the subject of the robbery, when Mr. Gradgrind spoke of it.

[032-276] The two appointed days ran out, three days and nights ran out, and

[032-277] Stephen Blackpool was not come, and remained unheard of. On the fourth

[032-278] day, Rachael, with unabated confidence, but considering her despatch to

[032-279] have miscarried, went up to the Bank, and showed her letter from him with

[032-280] his address, at a working colony, one of many, not upon the main road,

[032-281] sixty miles away. Messengers were sent to that place, and the whole town

[032-282] looked for Stephen to be brought in next day.

[032-283] During this whole time the whelp moved about with Mr. Bounderby like his

[032-284] shadow, assisting in all the proceedings. He was greatly excited,

[032-285] horribly fevered, bit his nails down to the quick, spoke in a hard

[032-286] rattling voice, and with lips that were black and burnt up. At the hour

[032-287] when the suspected man was looked for, the whelp was at the station;

[032-288] offering to wager that he had made off before the arrival of those who

[032-289] were sent in quest of him, and that he would not appear.

[032-290] The whelp was right. The messengers returned alone. Rachael’s letter

[032-291] had gone, Rachael’s letter had been delivered. Stephen Blackpool had

[032-292] decamped in that same hour; and no soul knew more of him. The only doubt

[032-293] in Coketown was, whether Rachael had written in good faith, believing

[032-294] that he really would come back, or warning him to fly. On this point

[032-295] opinion was divided.

[032-296] Six days, seven days, far on into another week. The wretched whelp

[032-297] plucked up a ghastly courage, and began to grow defiant. ‘_Was_ the

[032-298] suspected fellow the thief? A pretty question! If not, where was the

[032-299] man, and why did he not come back?’

[032-300] Where was the man, and why did he not come back? In the dead of night

[032-301] the echoes of his own words, which had rolled Heaven knows how far away

[032-302] in the daytime, came back instead, and abided by him until morning.
