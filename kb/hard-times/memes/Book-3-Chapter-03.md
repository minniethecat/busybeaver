[031-001] VERY DECIDED

[031-002] THE indefatigable [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]], with a violent cold upon her, her voice

[031-003] reduced to a whisper, and her stately frame so racked by continual

[031-004] sneezes that it seemed in danger of dismemberment, gave chase to her

[031-005] patron until she found him in the metropolis; and there, majestically

[031-006] sweeping in upon him at his hotel in St. James’s Street, exploded the

[031-007] combustibles with which she was charged, and blew up. Having executed

[031-008] her mission with infinite relish, this high-minded woman then fainted

[031-009] away on [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s coat-collar.

[031-010] Mr. Bounderby’s first procedure was to shake Mrs. Sparsit off, and leave

[031-011] her to progress as she might through various stages of suffering on the

[031-012] floor. He next had recourse to the administration of potent

[031-013] restoratives, such as screwing the patient’s thumbs, smiting her hands,

[031-014] abundantly watering her face, and inserting salt in her mouth. When

[031-015] these attentions had recovered her (which they speedily did), he hustled

[031-016] her into a fast train without offering any other refreshment, and carried

[031-017] her back to [[coketown|Coketown]] more dead than alive.

[031-018] Regarded as a classical ruin, Mrs. Sparsit was an interesting [[performance|spectacle]]

[031-019] on her arrival at her journey’s end; but considered in any other light,

[031-020] the amount of damage she had by that time sustained was excessive, and

[031-021] impaired her claims to admiration. Utterly heedless of the wear and tear

[031-022] of her clothes and constitution, and adamant to her pathetic sneezes, Mr.

[031-023] Bounderby immediately crammed her into a coach, and bore her off to Stone

[031-024] Lodge.

[031-025] ‘Now, [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]],’ said Bounderby, bursting into his father-in-law’s

[031-026] room late at night; ‘here’s a lady here—Mrs. Sparsit—you know Mrs.

[031-027] Sparsit—who has something to say to you that will strike you dumb.’

[031-028] ‘You have missed my letter!’ exclaimed [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], surprised by the

[031-029] apparition.

[031-030] ‘Missed your letter, sir!’ bawled Bounderby. ‘The present time is no

[031-031] time for letters. No man shall talk to Josiah Bounderby of Coketown

[031-032] about letters, with his mind in the state it’s in now.’

[031-033] ‘Bounderby,’ said Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]], in a tone of temperate remonstrance, ‘I

[031-034] speak of a very special letter I have written to you, in reference to

[031-035] [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]].’

[031-036] ‘Tom Gradgrind,’ replied Bounderby, knocking the flat of his hand several

[031-037] times with great vehemence on the table, ‘I speak of a very special

[031-038] messenger that has come to me, in reference to Louisa. Mrs. Sparsit,

[031-039] ma’am, stand forward!’

[031-040] That unfortunate lady hereupon essaying to offer testimony, without any

[031-041] voice and with painful gestures expressive of an inflamed throat, became

[031-042] so aggravating and underwent so many facial contortions, that Mr.

[031-043] Bounderby, unable to bear it, seized her by the arm and shook her.

[031-044] ‘If you can’t get it out, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘leave _me_ to get it

[031-045] out. This is not a time for a lady, however highly connected, to be

[031-046] totally inaudible, and seemingly swallowing marbles. Tom Gradgrind, Mrs.

[031-047] Sparsit latterly found herself, by accident, in a situation to overhear a

[031-048] conversation out of doors between your daughter and your precious

[031-049] [[gentleman]]-friend, [[james-harthouse|Mr. James Harthouse]].’

[031-050] ‘Indeed!’ said Mr. Gradgrind.

[031-051] ‘Ah! Indeed!’ cried Bounderby. ‘And in that conversation—’

[031-052] ‘It is not necessary to repeat its tenor, Bounderby. I know what

[031-053] passed.’

[031-054] ‘You do? Perhaps,’ said Bounderby, staring with all his might at his so

[031-055] quiet and assuasive father-in-law, ‘you know where your daughter is at

[031-056] the present time!’

[031-057] ‘Undoubtedly. She is here.’

[031-058] ‘Here?’

[031-059] ‘My [[love|dear]] Bounderby, let me beg you to restrain these loud out-breaks, on

[031-060] all accounts. Louisa is here. The moment she could detach herself from

[031-061] that interview with the person of whom you speak, and whom I deeply

[031-062] regret to have been the means of introducing to you, Louisa hurried here,

[031-063] for protection. I myself had not been at home many hours, when I

[031-064] received her—here, in this room. She hurried by the train to town, she

[031-065] ran from town to this house, through a raging storm, and presented

[031-066] herself before me in a state of distraction. Of course, she has remained

[031-067] here ever since. Let me entreat you, for your own sake and for hers, to

[031-068] be more quiet.’

[031-069] Mr. Bounderby silently gazed about him for some moments, in every

[031-070] direction except Mrs. Sparsit’s direction; and then, abruptly turning

[031-071] upon the niece of [[lady-scadgers|Lady Scadgers]], said to that wretched woman:

[031-072] ‘Now, ma’am! We shall be happy to hear any little apology you may think

[031-073] proper to offer, for going about the country at express pace, with no

[031-074] other luggage than a Cock-and-a-Bull, ma’am!’

[031-075] ‘Sir,’ whispered Mrs. Sparsit, ‘my nerves are at present too much shaken,

[031-076] and my health is at present too much impaired, in your service, to admit

[031-077] of my doing more than taking refuge in tears.’ (Which she did.)

[031-078] ‘Well, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘without making any observation to you

[031-079] that may not be made with propriety to a woman of good [[family]], what I

[031-080] have got to add to that, is that there is something else in which it

[031-081] appears to me you may take refuge, namely, a coach. And the coach in

[031-082] which we came here being at the door, you’ll allow me to hand you down to

[031-083] it, and pack you home to the Bank: where the best course for you to

[031-084] pursue, will be to put your feet into the hottest water you can bear, and

[031-085] take a glass of scalding rum and butter after you get into bed.’ With

[031-086] these words, Mr. Bounderby extended his right hand to the weeping lady,

[031-087] and escorted her to the conveyance in question, shedding many plaintive

[031-088] sneezes by the way. He soon returned alone.

[031-089] ‘Now, as you showed me in your face, Tom Gradgrind, that you wanted to

[031-090] speak to me,’ he resumed, ‘here I am. But, I am not in a very agreeable

[031-091] state, I tell you plainly: not relishing this business, even as it is,

[031-092] and not considering that I am at any time as dutifully and submissively

[031-093] treated by your daughter, as Josiah Bounderby of Coketown ought to be

[031-094] treated by his wife. You have your opinion, I dare say; and I have mine,

[031-095] I know. If you mean to say anything to me to-night, that goes against

[031-096] this candid remark, you had better let it alone.’

[031-097] Mr. Gradgrind, it will be observed, being much softened, Mr. Bounderby

[031-098] took particular pains to harden himself at all points. It was his

[031-099] amiable [[nature]].

[031-100] ‘My dear Bounderby,’ Mr. Gradgrind began in reply.

[031-101] ‘Now, you’ll excuse me,’ said Bounderby, ‘but I don’t want to be too

[031-102] dear. That, to start with. When I begin to be dear to a man, I

[031-103] generally find that his intention is to come over me. I am not speaking

[031-104] to you politely; but, as you are aware, I am _not_ polite. If you like

[031-105] politeness, you know where to get it. You have your gentleman-friends,

[031-106] you know, and they’ll serve you with as much of the article as you want.

[031-107] I don’t keep it myself.’

[031-108] ‘Bounderby,’ urged Mr. Gradgrind, ‘we are all liable to mistakes—’

[031-109] ‘I thought you couldn’t make ’em,’ interrupted Bounderby.

[031-110] ‘Perhaps I thought so. But, I say we are all liable to mistakes and I

[031-111] should feel sensible of your delicacy, and grateful for it, if you would

[031-112] spare me these references to Harthouse. I shall not associate him in our

[031-113] conversation with your intimacy and encouragement; pray do not persist in

[031-114] connecting him with mine.’

[031-115] ‘I never mentioned his name!’ said Bounderby.

[031-116] ‘Well, well!’ returned Mr. Gradgrind, with a patient, even a submissive,

[031-117] air. And he sat for a little while pondering. ‘Bounderby, I see [[reason]]

[031-118] to doubt whether we have ever quite understood Louisa.’

[031-119] ‘Who do you mean by We?’

[031-120] ‘Let me say I, then,’ he returned, in answer to the coarsely blurted

[031-121] question; ‘I doubt whether I have understood Louisa. I doubt whether I

[031-122] have been quite right in the manner of her [[education]].’

[031-123] ‘There you hit it,’ returned Bounderby. ‘There I agree with you. You

[031-124] have found it out at last, have you? Education! I’ll tell you what

[031-125] education is—To be tumbled out of doors, neck and crop, and put upon the

[031-126] shortest allowance of everything except blows. That’s what _I_ call

[031-127] education.’

[031-128] ‘I think your good sense will perceive,’ Mr. Gradgrind remonstrated in

[031-129] all [[humility]], ‘that whatever the merits of such a system may be, it would

[031-130] be difficult of general application to girls.’

[031-131] ‘I don’t see it at all, sir,’ returned the obstinate Bounderby.

[031-132] ‘Well,’ sighed Mr. Gradgrind, ‘we will not enter into the question. I

[031-133] assure you I have no desire to be controversial. I seek to repair what

[031-134] is amiss, if I possibly can; and I hope you will assist me in a good

[031-135] spirit, Bounderby, for I have been very much distressed.’

[031-136] ‘I don’t understand you, yet,’ said Bounderby, with determined obstinacy,

[031-137] ‘and therefore I won’t make any promises.’

[031-138] ‘In the course of a few hours, my dear Bounderby,’ Mr. Gradgrind

[031-139] proceeded, in the same depressed and propitiatory manner, ‘I appear to

[031-140] myself to have become better informed as to Louisa’s character, than in

[031-141] previous years. The enlightenment has been painfully forced upon me, and

[031-142] the discovery is not mine. I think there are—Bounderby, you will be

[031-143] surprised to hear me say this—I think there are qualities in Louisa,

[031-144] which—which have been harshly neglected, and—and a little perverted.

[031-145] And—and I would suggest to you, that—that if you would kindly meet me in

[031-146] a timely endeavour to leave her to her better nature for a while—and to

[031-147] encourage it to develop itself by tenderness and consideration—it—it

[031-148] would be the better for the happiness of all of us. Louisa,’ said Mr.

[031-149] Gradgrind, shading his face with his hand, ‘has always been my favourite

[031-150] [[childhood|child]].’

[031-151] The blustrous Bounderby crimsoned and swelled to such an extent on

[031-152] hearing these words, that he seemed to be, and probably was, on the brink

[031-153] of a fit. With his very ears a bright purple shot with crimson, he pent

[031-154] up his indignation, however, and said:

[031-155] ‘You’d like to keep her here for a time?’

[031-156] ‘I—I had intended to recommend, my dear Bounderby, that you should allow

[031-157] Louisa to remain here on a visit, and be attended by [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] (I mean of

[031-158] course Cecilia Jupe), who understands her, and in whom she trusts.’

[031-159] ‘I gather from all this, Tom Gradgrind,’ said Bounderby, standing up with

[031-160] his hands in his pockets, ‘that you are of opinion that there’s what

[031-161] people call some incompatibility between Loo Bounderby and myself.’

[031-162] ‘I fear there is at present a general incompatibility between Louisa,

[031-163] and—and—and almost all the relations in which I have placed her,’ was her

[031-164] father’s sorrowful reply.

[031-165] ‘Now, look you here, Tom Gradgrind,’ said Bounderby the flushed,

[031-166] confronting him with his legs wide apart, his hands deeper in his

[031-167] pockets, and his hair like a hayfield wherein his windy anger was

[031-168] boisterous. ‘You have said your say; I am going to say mine. I am a

[031-169] Coketown man. I am Josiah Bounderby of Coketown. I know the bricks of

[031-170] this town, and I know the works of this town, and I know the chimneys of

[031-171] this town, and I know the smoke of this town, and I know the Hands of

[031-172] this town. I know ’em all pretty well. They’re real. When a man tells

[031-173] me anything about [[imagination|imaginative]] qualities, I always tell that man, whoever

[031-174] he is, that I know what he means. He means turtle soup and venison, with

[031-175] a gold spoon, and that he wants to be set up with a coach and six.

[031-176] That’s what your daughter wants. Since you are of opinion that she ought

[031-177] to have what she wants, I recommend you to provide it for her. Because,

[031-178] Tom Gradgrind, she will never have it from me.’

[031-179] ‘Bounderby,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘I hoped, after my entreaty, you would

[031-180] have taken a different tone.’

[031-181] ‘Just wait a bit,’ retorted Bounderby; ‘you have said your say, I

[031-182] believe. I heard you out; hear me out, if you please. Don’t make

[031-183] yourself a spectacle of unfairness as well as inconsistency, because,

[031-184] although I am sorry to see Tom Gradgrind reduced to his present position,

[031-185] I should be doubly sorry to see him brought so low as that. Now, there’s

[031-186] an incompatibility of some sort or another, I am given to understand by

[031-187] you, between your daughter and me. I’ll give _you_ to understand, in

[031-188] reply to that, that there unquestionably is an incompatibility of the

[031-189] first magnitude—to be summed up in this—that your daughter don’t properly

[031-190] know her husband’s merits, and is not impressed with such a sense as

[031-191] would become her, by George! of the honour of his alliance. That’s plain

[031-192] speaking, I hope.’

[031-193] ‘Bounderby,’ urged Mr. Gradgrind, ‘this is unreasonable.’

[031-194] ‘Is it?’ said Bounderby. ‘I am glad to hear you say so. Because when

[031-195] Tom Gradgrind, with his new lights, tells me that what I say is

[031-196] unreasonable, I am convinced at once it must be devilish sensible. With

[031-197] your permission I am going on. You know my origin; and you know that for

[031-198] a good many years of my life I didn’t want a shoeing-horn, in consequence

[031-199] of not having a shoe. Yet you may believe or not, as you think proper,

[031-200] that there are ladies—born ladies—belonging to families—Families!—who

[031-201] next to worship the ground I walk on.’

[031-202] He discharged this like a Rocket, at his father-in-law’s head.

[031-203] ‘Whereas your daughter,’ proceeded Bounderby, ‘is far from being a born

[031-204] lady. That you know, yourself. Not that I care a pinch of candle-snuff

[031-205] about such things, for you are very well aware I don’t; but that such is

[031-206] the [[facts|fact]], and you, Tom Gradgrind, can’t change it. Why do I say this?’

[031-207] ‘Not, I fear,’ observed Mr. Gradgrind, in a low voice, ‘to spare me.’

[031-208] ‘Hear me out,’ said Bounderby, ‘and refrain from cutting in till your

[031-209] turn comes round. I say this, because highly connected females have been

[031-210] astonished to see the way in which your daughter has conducted herself,

[031-211] and to witness her insensibility. They have wondered how I have suffered

[031-212] it. And I wonder myself now, and I won’t suffer it.’

[031-213] ‘Bounderby,’ returned Mr. Gradgrind, rising, ‘the less we say to-night

[031-214] the better, I think.’

[031-215] ‘On the contrary, Tom Gradgrind, the more we say to-night, the better, I

[031-216] think. That is,’ the consideration checked him, ‘till I have said all I

[031-217] mean to say, and then I don’t care how soon we stop. I come to a

[031-218] question that may shorten the business. What do you mean by the proposal

[031-219] you made just now?’

[031-220] ‘What do I mean, Bounderby?’

[031-221] ‘By your visiting proposition,’ said Bounderby, with an inflexible jerk

[031-222] of the hayfield.

[031-223] ‘I mean that I hope you may be induced to arrange in a friendly manner,

[031-224] for allowing Louisa a period of repose and reflection here, which may

[031-225] tend to a gradual alteration for the better in many respects.’

[031-226] ‘To a softening down of your ideas of the incompatibility?’ said

[031-227] Bounderby.

[031-228] ‘If you put it in those terms.’

[031-229] ‘What made you think of this?’ said Bounderby.

[031-230] ‘I have already said, I fear Louisa has not been understood. Is it

[031-231] asking too much, Bounderby, that you, so far her elder, should aid in

[031-232] trying to set her right? You have accepted a great charge of her; for

[031-233] better for worse, for—’

[031-234] Mr. Bounderby may have been annoyed by the repetition of his own words to

[031-235] [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]], but he cut the quotation short with an angry start.

[031-236] ‘Come!’ said he, ‘I don’t want to be told about that. I know what I took

[031-237] her for, as well as you do. Never you mind what I took her for; that’s

[031-238] my look out.’

[031-239] ‘I was merely going on to remark, Bounderby, that we may all be more or

[031-240] less in the wrong, not even excepting you; and that some yielding on your

[031-241] part, remembering the trust you have accepted, may not only be an act of

[031-242] true kindness, but perhaps a debt incurred towards Louisa.’

[031-243] ‘I think differently,’ blustered Bounderby. ‘I am going to finish this

[031-244] business according to my own opinions. Now, I don’t want to make a

[031-245] quarrel of it with you, Tom Gradgrind. To tell you the [[truth]], I don’t

[031-246] think it would be worthy of my reputation to quarrel on such a subject.

[031-247] As to your gentleman-friend, he may take himself off, wherever he likes

[031-248] best. If he falls in my way, I shall tell him my mind; if he don’t fall

[031-249] in my way, I shan’t, for it won’t be worth my while to do it. As to your

[031-250] daughter, whom I made Loo Bounderby, and might have done better by

[031-251] leaving Loo Gradgrind, if she don’t come home to-morrow, by twelve

[031-252] o’clock at noon, I shall understand that she prefers to stay away, and I

[031-253] shall send her wearing apparel and so forth over here, and you’ll take

[031-254] charge of her for the future. What I shall say to people in general, of

[031-255] the incompatibility that led to my so laying down the law, will be this.

[031-256] I am Josiah Bounderby, and I had my bringing-up; she’s the daughter of

[031-257] Tom Gradgrind, and she had her bringing-up; and the two horses wouldn’t

[031-258] pull together. I am pretty well known to be rather an uncommon man, I

[031-259] believe; and most people will understand fast enough that it must be a

[031-260] woman rather out of the common, also, who, in the long run, would come up

[031-261] to my mark.’

[031-262] ‘Let me seriously entreat you to reconsider this, Bounderby,’ urged Mr.

[031-263] Gradgrind, ‘before you commit yourself to such a decision.’

[031-264] ‘I always come to a decision,’ said Bounderby, tossing his hat on: ‘and

[031-265] whatever I do, I do at once. I should be surprised at Tom Gradgrind’s

[031-266] addressing such a remark to Josiah Bounderby of Coketown, knowing what he

[031-267] knows of him, if I could be surprised by anything Tom Gradgrind did,

[031-268] after his making himself a party to sentimental humbug. I have given you

[031-269] my decision, and I have got no more to say. Good night!’

[031-270] So Mr. Bounderby went home to his town house to bed. At five minutes

[031-271] past twelve o’clock next day, he directed Mrs. Bounderby’s property to be

[031-272] carefully packed up and sent to Tom Gradgrind’s; advertised his country

[031-273] retreat for sale by private contract; and resumed a bachelor life.
