# Pride

Pride in *Hard Times* is a concept split in two. Dickens draws a sharp moral distinction between the inflated self-regard of those who wield power — the banker, the schoolmaster, the decayed aristocrat — and the quiet dignity of those who possess nothing but their good name. In Coketown, pride is simultaneously the signature sin of the ruling class and the last possession of the honest poor.

## Definition

In the moral universe of *Hard Times*, pride operates along a fault line that Dickens excavates with relentless clarity. On one side stands what the Victorian moral tradition would recognise as *superbia* — the cardinal sin of excessive self-estimation, the refusal to acknowledge one's dependence on others or one's limitations before God. This is the pride of Bounderby, who fabricates an entire autobiography to justify his authority; of Gradgrind, whose intellectual certainty blinds him to the emotional starvation of his own children; of Mrs. Sparsit, whose aristocratic genealogy becomes a weapon of condescension. On the other side stands something closer to *dignitas* — the warranted self-respect that enables a person to endure hardship without moral compromise. This is Stephen Blackpool's "pride" in keeping his word, Rachael's refusal to be humiliated, the working-class refusal to trade integrity for advantage. (PN: 033-215)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of pride is structured as a diagnostic taxonomy. He identifies the species of false pride one by one — intellectual pride in Gradgrind, social pride in Mrs. Sparsit, the pride of false humility in Bounderby — and exposes each through the narrative consequences it generates. Gradgrind's intellectual pride produces two damaged children and a household devoid of love; Bounderby's self-mythologising is literally exposed as fiction when his mother appears; Mrs. Sparsit's class pride reduces her to a spy on a staircase, a grotesque figure of fallen gentility. (PN: 024-183) (PN: 033-201)

At the same time, Dickens recognises pride as a necessary moral resource for those without power. Stephen Blackpool's refusal to join the union is framed not as cowardice but as a "proud consciousness" — a stubborn allegiance to his own conscience against both masters and men. (PN: 021-082) Rachael's quiet pride in her own rectitude is never mocked; it is the moral foundation that enables her to protect Stephen's name after his death. The novel's deepest insight is that pride is context-dependent: the same quality that is a vice in Bounderby is a virtue in Stephen, because pride's moral character is determined by the power relations in which it is embedded.

### Key Passages

> "A man with a larger appreciation of the meaning of those words... pride in the phrase eminently practical" (PN: 003-060)

This early passage introduces pride through the Gradgrindian lens: pride is the self-satisfaction of the Fact-philosopher, the warm glow of having reduced the world to a ledger. The phrase "eminently practical" is the novel's first ironic commentary on intellectual pride — the conviction that one's own mental habits are the measure of all things.

> "His pride in having at any time of his life achieved such a great social distinction as to be a nuisance, an incumbrance, and a pest, was immense." (PN: 004-072)

Bounderby's pride in his own fabricated deprivation — his insistence that he rose from the gutter through sheer force of will — is the novel's most sustained anatomy of false pride. The irony cuts three ways: Bounderby is proud of his alleged suffering, he exaggerates that suffering to justify his present power, and the suffering he claims is the precise opposite of the actual suffering his mills inflict on workers.

> "There's no family pride about me, there's no imaginary... there never was gratuitous insolence or pride in me." (PN: 006-154)

Bounderby's protestation of humility is itself an act of pride — the false modesty that demands recognition for its own self-abnegation. Dickens's genius here is to make Bounderby's denials of pride function as its most telling expression.

> "pride he will come back to shame it" (PN: 032-178)

This compressed formulation captures the dialectic that structures the novel's moral vision: pride that is not grounded in genuine self-knowledge will inevitably generate its own undoing. The trajectory from false pride to exposure and shame is the arc followed by Bounderby, by the young Gradgrind, and — most painfully — by Gradgrind himself.

> "I am contented, and I can keep my pride" (PN: 033-215)

Stephen Blackpool's quiet assertion of working-class dignity is the counterweight to every instance of false pride in the novel. Pride here is not the inflation of self but the refusal to be diminished — the determination to remain a moral agent even when stripped of every material resource. The verb "keep" is significant: Stephen's pride is something he maintains, not something he performs.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period inherited from Christianity a deeply ambivalent attitude toward pride. Theologians and moralists condemned it as the deadliest of the seven deadly sins — the sin of Lucifer, the root of all other vices — while simultaneously celebrating the virtues of independence, self-reliance, and respectability that pride's secular cousins made possible. Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* (1859), published only five years after *Hard Times*, would canonise the Victorian gospel of self-improvement; Dickens's novel, in its treatment of Bounderby's fraudulent self-help narrative, reads as a pre-emptive critique.

The Chartist movement of the 1830s–1840s had politicised working-class dignity, insisting that labourers deserved not only better wages but recognition as moral equals. The language of "respectability" among working-class radicals — clean homes, sober habits, honest dealing — was itself a form of pride, a refusal to accept the degradation that industrial capitalism imposed. Stephen Blackpool's "contented" pride belongs to this tradition, and Dickens's respect for it distinguishes *Hard Times* from the more paternalistic industrial novels of the period.

## Embodied By

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: False pride personified — the self-made man whose autobiography is a fiction designed to legitimate his power. Bounderby's pride takes the paradoxical form of boasting about deprivation: he is proudest when he is most loudly insisting that he has no pride at all. His exposure by his own mother in Book III is the novel's definitive judgement on pride that has no basis in truth.
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: Intellectual pride — the conviction that one's own mental framework exhausts reality. Gradgrind's pride is quieter than Bounderby's but more consequential: it shapes an entire educational system, produces two deeply damaged children, and requires a catastrophic family crisis to dislodge. His arc from certainty to humility is the novel's most sustained meditation on the cost of intellectual arrogance.
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]**: Class pride — the decayed aristocrat who clings to her lineage as compensation for her fallen circumstances. Her "indignant pride" (PN: 033-201) in her connection to the Powler family is pathetic but also dangerous: it fuels her campaign against Louisa and her fantasy of marrying Bounderby. Mrs. Sparsit demonstrates that class pride is corrosive even — perhaps especially — when it has no material basis.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Working-class dignity — the novel's counter-example to every form of false pride. Stephen's pride consists in his refusal to violate his conscience, even when that refusal costs him his place among his fellow workers and, ultimately, his life. His pride is quiet, contented, and grounded in truth — everything that Bounderby's is not.

## Contrasted With

- **[[humility]]**: If false pride is the cardinal sin of *Hard Times*, humility is its implied virtue — but Dickens is too honest to sentimentalise humility as a simple solution. Gradgrind's eventual humility is purchased at terrible cost, and Stephen's pride-as-dignity refuses the self-abasement that the powerful demand of the powerless. The novel suggests that true humility is not self-hatred but accurate self-knowledge — the ability to see oneself without the distortions of either inflation or debasement. (PN: 033-201)
- **[[shame]]**: Pride and shame are dialectical opposites throughout the novel. The trajectory from false pride to exposure and shame — "pride he will come back to shame it" (PN: 032-178) — structures the moral arcs of Bounderby, Tom Gradgrind, and Mrs. Sparsit. Shame in *Hard Times* is not always negative: it can be the corrective that pride requires, the painful but necessary recognition of one's true condition. Stephen Blackpool, significantly, has nothing to be ashamed of, and therefore his pride needs no shattering.

## Critical Perspectives

> G. K. Chesterton, in *Appreciations and Criticisms of the Works of Charles Dickens* (1911), identified Dickens's moral psychology as fundamentally concerned with the "virtues of the poor" — the qualities of loyalty, endurance, and quiet self-respect that flourish in the absence of wealth and power. Chesterton's reading illuminates the distinction Dickens draws between the pride that puffs up and the pride that sustains: the former is always a performance for an audience, the latter an internal condition that requires no witness.

> George Orwell, in his essay "Charles Dickens" (1939), argued that Dickens's moral vision was rooted in a "native generosity" that instinctively sided with the underdog against every form of bullying authority. Orwell's observation applies with particular force to the novel's treatment of pride: Dickens invariably exposes the pride of the powerful as fraudulent while respecting the pride of the powerless as a form of moral self-defence.

> David Lodge, in *Language of Fiction* (1966), analysed Dickens's use of iterative imagery — the repetition of key words and phrases — as a structural device that builds thematic density. The word "pride" and its cognates recur throughout *Hard Times* with shifting moral valences, and Lodge's analysis helps explain how the novel achieves its effect of making a single word carry the weight of an entire moral argument.
