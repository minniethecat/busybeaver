[027-001] LOWER AND LOWER

[027-002] THE figure descended the great stairs, steadily, steadily; always

[027-003] verging, like a weight in deep water, to the black gulf at the bottom.

[027-004] [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], apprised of his wife’s decease, made an expedition from

[027-005] London, and buried her in a business-like manner. He then returned with

[027-006] promptitude to the national cinder-heap, and resumed his sifting for the

[027-007] odds and ends he wanted, and his throwing of the dust about into the eyes

[027-008] of other people who wanted other odds and ends—in [[facts|fact]] resumed his

[027-009] parliamentary duties.

[027-010] In the meantime, [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] kept unwinking watch and ward. Separated

[027-011] from her staircase, all the week, by the length of iron road dividing

[027-012] [[coketown|Coketown]] from [[bounderby-s-country-estate|the country house]], she yet maintained her cat-like

[027-013] observation of [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]], through her husband, through her brother, through

[027-014] [[james-harthouse|James Harthouse]], through the outsides of letters and packets, through

[027-015] everything animate and inanimate that at any time went near the stairs.

[027-016] ‘Your foot on the last step, my lady,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, apostrophizing

[027-017] the descending figure, with the aid of her threatening mitten, ‘and all

[027-018] your art shall never blind me.’

[027-019] Art or [[nature]] though, the original stock of Louisa’s character or the

[027-020] graft of circumstances upon it,—her curious reserve did baffle, while it

[027-021] stimulated, one as sagacious as Mrs. Sparsit. There were times when Mr.

[027-022] James Harthouse was not sure of her. There were times when he could not

[027-023] read the face he had studied so long; and when this lonely girl was a

[027-024] greater mystery to him, than any woman of the world with a ring of

[027-025] satellites to help her.

[027-026] So the time went on; until it happened that [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] was called away

[027-027] from home by business which required his presence elsewhere, for three or

[027-028] four days. It was on a Friday that he intimated this to Mrs. Sparsit at

[027-029] the Bank, adding: ‘But you’ll go down to-morrow, ma’am, all the same.

[027-030] You’ll go down just as if I was there. It will make no difference to

[027-031] you.’

[027-032] ‘Pray, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, reproachfully, ‘let me beg you not to

[027-033] say that. Your absence will make a vast difference to me, sir, as I

[027-034] think you very well know.’

[027-035] ‘Well, ma’am, then you must get on in my absence as well as you can,’

[027-036] said Mr. Bounderby, not displeased.

[027-037] ‘Mr. Bounderby,’ retorted Mrs. Sparsit, ‘your will is to me a law, sir;

[027-038] otherwise, it might be my inclination to dispute your kind commands, not

[027-039] [[heart|feeling]] sure that it will be quite so agreeable to Miss [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] to

[027-040] receive me, as it ever is to your own munificent hospitality. But you

[027-041] shall say no more, sir. I will go, upon your invitation.’

[027-042] ‘Why, when I invite you to my house, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, opening his

[027-043] eyes, ‘I should hope you want no other invitation.’

[027-044] ‘No, indeed, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, ‘I should hope not. Say no

[027-045] more, sir. I would, sir, I could see you gay again.’

[027-046] ‘What do you mean, ma’am?’ blustered Bounderby.

[027-047] ‘Sir,’ rejoined Mrs. Sparsit, ‘there was wont to be an elasticity in you

[027-048] which I sadly miss. Be buoyant, sir!’

[027-049] Mr. Bounderby, under the influence of this difficult adjuration, backed

[027-050] up by her compassionate eye, could only scratch his [[reason|head]] in a feeble and

[027-051] ridiculous manner, and afterwards assert himself at a distance, by being

[027-052] heard to bully the small fry of business all the morning.

[027-053] ‘[[bitzer|Bitzer]],’ said Mrs. Sparsit that afternoon, when her patron was gone on

[027-054] his journey, and the Bank was closing, ‘present my compliments to young

[027-055] Mr. Thomas, and ask him if he would step up and partake of a lamb chop

[027-056] and walnut ketchup, with a glass of India ale?’ Young Mr. Thomas being

[027-057] usually ready for anything in that way, returned a gracious answer, and

[027-058] followed on its heels. ‘Mr. Thomas,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘these plain

[027-059] viands being on table, I thought you might be tempted.’

[027-060] ‘Thank’ee, Mrs. Sparsit,’ said [[tom-gradgrind|the whelp]]. And gloomily fell to.

[027-061] ‘How is Mr. Harthouse, Mr. Tom?’ asked Mrs. Sparsit.

[027-062] ‘Oh, he’s all right,’ said Tom.

[027-063] ‘Where may he be at present?’ Mrs. Sparsit asked in a light

[027-064] conversational manner, after mentally devoting the whelp to the Furies

[027-065] for being so uncommunicative.

[027-066] ‘He is shooting in Yorkshire,’ said Tom. ‘Sent Loo a basket half as big

[027-067] as a church, yesterday.’

[027-068] ‘The kind of [[gentleman]], now,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, sweetly, ‘whom one might

[027-069] wager to be a good shot!’

[027-070] ‘Crack,’ said Tom.

[027-071] He had long been a down-looking young fellow, but this characteristic had

[027-072] so increased of late, that he never raised his eyes to any face for three

[027-073] seconds together. Mrs. Sparsit consequently had ample means of watching

[027-074] his looks, if she were so inclined.

[027-075] ‘Mr. Harthouse is a great favourite of mine,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘as

[027-076] indeed he is of most people. May we expect to see him again shortly, Mr.

[027-077] Tom?’

[027-078] ‘Why, _I_ expect to see him to-morrow,’ returned the whelp.

[027-079] ‘Good news!’ cried Mrs. Sparsit, blandly.

[027-080] ‘I have got an appointment with him to meet him in the evening at the

[027-081] station here,’ said Tom, ‘and I am going to dine with him afterwards, I

[027-082] believe. He is not coming down to the country house for a week or so,

[027-083] being due somewhere else. At least, he says so; but I shouldn’t wonder

[027-084] if he was to stop here over Sunday, and stray that way.’

[027-085] ‘Which reminds me!’ said Mrs. Sparsit. ‘Would you remember a message to

[027-086] your sister, Mr. Tom, if I was to charge you with one?’

[027-087] ‘Well? I’ll try,’ returned the reluctant whelp, ‘if it isn’t a long un.’

[027-088] ‘It is merely my respectful compliments,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘and I fear

[027-089] I may not trouble her with my society this week; being still a little

[027-090] nervous, and better perhaps by my poor self.’

[027-091] ‘Oh! If that’s all,’ observed Tom, ‘it wouldn’t much matter, even if I

[027-092] was to forget it, for Loo’s not likely to think of you unless she sees

[027-093] you.’

[027-094] Having paid for his entertainment with this agreeable compliment, he

[027-095] relapsed into a hangdog silence until there was no more India ale left,

[027-096] when he said, ‘Well, Mrs. Sparsit, I must be off!’ and went off.

[027-097] Next day, Saturday, Mrs. Sparsit sat at her window all day long looking

[027-098] at the customers coming in and out, watching the postmen, keeping an eye

[027-099] on the general traffic of the street, revolving many things in her mind,

[027-100] but, above all, keeping her attention on her staircase. The evening

[027-101] come, she put on her bonnet and shawl, and went quietly out: having her

[027-102] reasons for hovering in a furtive way about the station by which a

[027-103] passenger would arrive from Yorkshire, and for preferring to peep into it

[027-104] round pillars and corners, and out of ladies’ waiting-room windows, to

[027-105] appearing in its precincts openly.

[027-106] Tom was in attendance, and loitered about until the expected train came

[027-107] in. It brought no Mr. Harthouse. Tom waited until the crowd had

[027-108] dispersed, and the bustle was over; and then referred to a posted list of

[027-109] trains, and took counsel with porters. That done, he strolled away idly,

[027-110] stopping in the street and looking up it and down it, and lifting his hat

[027-111] off and putting it on again, and yawning and stretching himself, and

[027-112] exhibiting all the symptoms of mortal weariness to be expected in one who

[027-113] had still to wait until the next train should come in, an hour and forty

[027-114] minutes hence.

[027-115] ‘This is a device to keep him out of the way,’ said Mrs. Sparsit,

[027-116] starting from the dull office window whence she had watched him last.

[027-117] ‘Harthouse is with his sister now!’

[027-118] It was the conception of an inspired moment, and she shot off with her

[027-119] utmost swiftness to work it out. The station for the country house was

[027-120] at the opposite end of the town, the time was short, the road not easy;

[027-121] but she was so quick in pouncing on a disengaged coach, so quick in

[027-122] darting out of it, producing her money, seizing her ticket, and diving

[027-123] into the train, that she was borne along the arches spanning the land of

[027-124] coal-pits past and present, as if she had been caught up in a cloud and

[027-125] whirled away.

[027-126] All the journey, immovable in the air though never left behind; plain to

[027-127] the dark eyes of her mind, as the electric wires which ruled a colossal

[027-128] strip of music-paper out of the evening sky, were plain to the dark eyes

[027-129] of her body; Mrs. Sparsit saw her staircase, with the figure coming down.

[027-130] Very near the bottom now. Upon the brink of the abyss.

[027-131] An overcast September evening, just at nightfall, saw beneath its

[027-132] drooping eyelids Mrs. Sparsit glide out of her carriage, pass down the

[027-133] wooden steps of the little station into a stony road, cross it into a

[027-134] green lane, and become hidden in a summer-growth of leaves and branches.

[027-135] One or two late birds sleepily chirping in their nests, and a bat heavily

[027-136] crossing and recrossing her, and the reek of her own tread in the thick

[027-137] dust that felt like velvet, were all Mrs. Sparsit heard or saw until she

[027-138] very softly closed a gate.

[027-139] She went up to the house, keeping within the shrubbery, and went round

[027-140] it, peeping between the leaves at the lower windows. Most of them were

[027-141] open, as they usually were in such warm weather, but there were no lights

[027-142] yet, and all was silent. She tried the garden with no better effect.

[027-143] She thought of the wood, and stole towards it, heedless of long grass and

[027-144] briers: of worms, snails, and slugs, and all the creeping things that be.

[027-145] With her dark eyes and her hook nose warily in advance of her, Mrs.

[027-146] Sparsit softly crushed her way through the thick undergrowth, so intent

[027-147] upon her object that she probably would have done no less, if the wood

[027-148] had been a wood of adders.

[027-149] Hark!

[027-150] The smaller birds might have tumbled out of their nests, fascinated by

[027-151] the glittering of Mrs. Sparsit’s eyes in the gloom, as she stopped and

[027-152] listened.

[027-153] Low voices close at hand. His voice and hers. The appointment _was_ a

[027-154] device to keep the brother away! There they were yonder, by the felled

[027-155] tree.

[027-156] Bending low among the dewy grass, Mrs. Sparsit advanced closer to them.

[027-157] She drew herself up, and stood behind a tree, like Robinson Crusoe in his

[027-158] ambuscade against the savages; so near to them that at a spring, and that

[027-159] no great one, she could have touched them both. He was there secretly,

[027-160] and had not shown himself at the house. He had come on horseback, and

[027-161] must have passed through the neighbouring fields; for his horse was tied

[027-162] to the meadow side of the fence, within a few paces.

[027-163] ‘My dearest [[love]],’ said he, ‘what could I do? Knowing you were alone,

[027-164] was it possible that I could stay away?’

[027-165] ‘You may hang your head, to make yourself the more attractive; _I_ don’t

[027-166] know what they see in you when you hold it up,’ thought Mrs. Sparsit;

[027-167] ‘but you little think, my dearest love, whose eyes are on you!’

[027-168] That she hung her head, was certain. She urged him to go away, she

[027-169] commanded him to go away; but she neither turned her face to him, nor

[027-170] raised it. Yet it was remarkable that she sat as still as ever the

[027-171] amiable woman in ambuscade had seen her sit, at any period in her life.

[027-172] Her hands rested in one another, like the hands of a statue; and even her

[027-173] manner of speaking was not hurried.

[027-174] ‘My dear [[childhood|child]],’ said Harthouse; Mrs. Sparsit saw with delight that his

[027-175] arm embraced her; ‘will you not bear with my society for a little while?’

[027-176] ‘Not here.’

[027-177] ‘Where, Louisa?

[027-178] ‘Not here.’

[027-179] ‘But we have so little time to make so much of, and I have come so far,

[027-180] and am altogether so devoted, and distracted. There never was a slave at

[027-181] once so devoted and ill-used by his mistress. To look for your sunny

[027-182] welcome that has warmed me into life, and to be received in your frozen

[027-183] manner, is heart-rending.’

[027-184] ‘Am I to say again, that I must be left to myself here?’

[027-185] ‘But we must meet, my dear Louisa. Where shall we meet?’

[027-186] They both started. The listener started, guiltily, too; for she thought

[027-187] there was another listener among the trees. It was only rain, beginning

[027-188] to fall fast, in heavy drops.

[027-189] ‘Shall I ride up to the house a few minutes hence, innocently supposing

[027-190] that its master is at home and will be charmed to receive me?’

[027-191] ‘No!’

[027-192] ‘Your cruel commands are implicitly to be obeyed; though I am the most

[027-193] unfortunate fellow in the world, I believe, to have been insensible to

[027-194] all other women, and to have fallen prostrate at last under the foot of

[027-195] the most beautiful, and the most engaging, and the most imperious. My

[027-196] dearest Louisa, I cannot go myself, or let you go, in this hard abuse of

[027-197] your power.’

[027-198] Mrs. Sparsit saw him detain her with his encircling arm, and heard him

[027-199] then and there, within her (Mrs. Sparsit’s) greedy hearing, tell her how

[027-200] he loved her, and how she was the stake for which he ardently desired to

[027-201] play away all that he had in life. The objects he had lately pursued,

[027-202] turned worthless beside her; such success as was almost in his grasp, he

[027-203] flung away from him like the dirt it was, compared with her. Its

[027-204] pursuit, nevertheless, if it kept him near her, or its renunciation if it

[027-205] took him from her, or flight if she shared it, or secrecy if she

[027-206] commanded it, or any fate, or every fate, all was alike to him, so that

[027-207] she was true to him,—the man who had seen how cast away she was, whom she

[027-208] had inspired at their first meeting with an admiration, an interest, of

[027-209] which he had thought himself incapable, whom she had received into her

[027-210] confidence, who was devoted to her and adored her. All this, and more,

[027-211] in his hurry, and in hers, in the whirl of her own gratified malice, in

[027-212] the dread of being discovered, in the rapidly increasing noise of heavy

[027-213] rain among the leaves, and a thunderstorm rolling up—Mrs. Sparsit

[027-214] received into her mind, set off with such an unavoidable halo of

[027-215] confusion and indistinctness, that when at length he climbed the fence

[027-216] and led his horse away, she was not sure where they were to meet, or

[027-217] when, except that they had said it was to be that night.

[027-218] But one of them yet remained in the darkness before her; and while she

[027-219] tracked that one she must be right. ‘Oh, my dearest love,’ thought Mrs.

[027-220] Sparsit, ‘you little think how well attended you are!’

[027-221] Mrs. Sparsit saw her out of the wood, and saw her enter the house. What

[027-222] to do next? It rained now, in a sheet of water. Mrs. Sparsit’s white

[027-223] stockings were of many colours, green predominating; prickly things were

[027-224] in her shoes; caterpillars slung themselves, in hammocks of their own

[027-225] making, from various parts of her dress; rills ran from her bonnet, and

[027-226] her Roman nose. In such condition, Mrs. Sparsit stood hidden in the

[027-227] density of the shrubbery, considering what next?

[027-228] Lo, Louisa coming out of the house! Hastily cloaked and muffled, and

[027-229] stealing away. She elopes! She falls from the lowermost stair, and is

[027-230] swallowed up in the gulf.

[027-231] Indifferent to the rain, and moving with a quick determined step, she

[027-232] struck into a side-path parallel with the ride. Mrs. Sparsit followed in

[027-233] the shadow of the trees, at but a short distance; for it was not easy to

[027-234] keep a figure in view going quickly through the umbrageous darkness.

[027-235] When she stopped to close the side-gate without noise, Mrs. Sparsit

[027-236] stopped. When she went on, Mrs. Sparsit went on. She went by the way

[027-237] Mrs. Sparsit had come, emerged from the green lane, crossed the stony

[027-238] road, and ascended the wooden steps to the railroad. A train for

[027-239] Coketown would come through presently, Mrs. Sparsit knew; so she

[027-240] understood Coketown to be her first place of destination.

[027-241] In Mrs. Sparsit’s limp and streaming state, no extensive precautions were

[027-242] necessary to change her usual appearance; but, she stopped under the lee

[027-243] of the station wall, tumbled her shawl into a new shape, and put it on

[027-244] over her bonnet. So disguised she had no fear of being recognized when

[027-245] she followed up the railroad steps, and paid her money in the small

[027-246] office. Louisa sat waiting in a corner. Mrs. Sparsit sat waiting in

[027-247] another corner. Both listened to the thunder, which was loud, and to the

[027-248] rain, as it washed off the roof, and pattered on the parapets of the

[027-249] arches. Two or three lamps were rained out and blown out; so, both saw

[027-250] the lightning to advantage as it quivered and zigzagged on the iron

[027-251] tracks.

[027-252] The seizure of the station with a fit of trembling, gradually deepening

[027-253] to a complaint of the heart, announced the train. Fire and steam, and

[027-254] smoke, and red light; a hiss, a crash, a bell, and a shriek; Louisa put

[027-255] into one carriage, Mrs. Sparsit put into another: the little station a

[027-256] desert speck in the thunderstorm.

[027-257] Though her teeth chattered in her head from wet and cold, Mrs. Sparsit

[027-258] exulted hugely. The figure had plunged down the precipice, and she felt

[027-259] herself, as it were, attending on the body. Could she, who had been so

[027-260] active in the getting up of the funeral triumph, do less than exult?

[027-261] ‘She will be at Coketown long before him,’ thought Mrs. Sparsit, ‘though

[027-262] his horse is never so good. Where will she wait for him? And where will

[027-263] they go together? Patience. We shall see.’

[027-264] The tremendous rain occasioned infinite confusion, when the train stopped

[027-265] at its destination. Gutters and pipes had burst, drains had overflowed,

[027-266] and streets were under water. In the first instant of alighting, Mrs.

[027-267] Sparsit turned her distracted eyes towards the waiting coaches, which

[027-268] were in great request. ‘She will get into one,’ she considered, ‘and

[027-269] will be away before I can follow in another. At all risks of being run

[027-270] over, I must see the number, and hear the order given to the coachman.’

[027-271] But, Mrs. Sparsit was wrong in her calculation. Louisa got into no

[027-272] coach, and was already gone. The black eyes kept upon the

[027-273] railroad-carriage in which she had travelled, settled upon it a moment

[027-274] too late. The door not being opened after several minutes, Mrs. Sparsit

[027-275] passed it and repassed it, saw nothing, looked in, and found it empty.

[027-276] Wet through and through: with her feet squelching and squashing in her

[027-277] shoes whenever she moved; with a rash of rain upon her classical visage;

[027-278] with a bonnet like an over-ripe fig; with all her clothes spoiled; with

[027-279] damp impressions of every button, string, and hook-and-eye she wore,

[027-280] printed off upon her highly connected back; with a stagnant verdure on

[027-281] her general exterior, such as accumulates on an old park fence in a

[027-282] mouldy lane; Mrs. Sparsit had no resource but to burst into tears of

[027-283] bitterness and say, ‘I have lost her!’
