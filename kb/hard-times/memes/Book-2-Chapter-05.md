[021-001] MEN AND MASTERS

[021-002] ‘WELL, [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen]],’ said [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]], in his windy manner, ‘what’s this I

[021-003] hear? What have these pests of the earth been doing to _you_? Come in,

[021-004] and speak up.’

[021-005] It was into the drawing-room that he was thus bidden. A tea-table was

[021-006] set out; and Mr. Bounderby’s young wife, and her brother, and a great

[021-007] [[gentleman]] from London, were present. To whom Stephen made his obeisance,

[021-008] closing the door and standing near it, with his hat in his hand.

[021-009] ‘This is the man I was telling you about, [[james-harthouse|Harthouse]],’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[021-010] The gentleman he addressed, who was talking to [[louisa-gradgrind|Mrs. Bounderby]] on the

[021-011] sofa, got up, saying in an indolent way, ‘Oh really?’ and dawdled to the

[021-012] hearthrug where Mr. Bounderby stood.

[021-013] ‘Now,’ said Bounderby, ‘speak up!’

[021-014] After the four days he had passed, this address fell rudely and

[021-015] discordantly on Stephen’s ear. Besides being a rough handling of his

[021-016] wounded mind, it seemed to assume that he really was the self-interested

[021-017] deserter he had been called.

[021-018] ‘What were it, sir,’ said Stephen, ‘as yo were pleased to want wi’ me?’

[021-019] ‘Why, I have told you,’ returned Bounderby. ‘Speak up like a man, since

[021-020] you are a man, and tell us about yourself and this Combination.’

[021-021] ‘Wi’ yor pardon, sir,’ said Stephen Blackpool, ‘I ha’ nowt to sen about

[021-022] it.’

[021-023] Mr. Bounderby, who was always more or less like a Wind, finding something

[021-024] in his way here, began to blow at it directly.

[021-025] ‘Now, look here, Harthouse,’ said he, ‘here’s a specimen of ’em. When

[021-026] this man was here once before, I warned this man against the mischievous

[021-027] strangers who are always about—and who ought to be hanged wherever they

[021-028] are found—and I told this man that he was going in the wrong direction.

[021-029] Now, would you believe it, that although they have put this mark upon

[021-030] him, he is such a slave to them still, that he’s afraid to open his lips

[021-031] about them?’

[021-032] ‘I sed as I had nowt to sen, sir; not as I was fearfo’ o’ openin’ my

[021-033] lips.’

[021-034] ‘You said! Ah! _I_ know what you said; more than that, I know what you

[021-035] mean, you see. Not always the same thing, by the Lord Harry! Quite

[021-036] different things. You had better tell us at once, that that fellow

[021-037] [[slackbridge|Slackbridge]] is not in the town, stirring up the people to mutiny; and

[021-038] that he is not a regular qualified leader of the people: that is, a most

[021-039] confounded scoundrel. You had better tell us so at once; you can’t

[021-040] deceive me. You want to tell us so. Why don’t you?’

[021-041] ‘I’m as sooary as yo, sir, when the people’s leaders is bad,’ said

[021-042] Stephen, shaking his [[reason|head]]. ‘They taks such as offers. Haply ’tis na’

[021-043] the sma’est o’ their misfortuns when they can get no better.’

[021-044] The wind began to get boisterous.

[021-045] ‘Now, you’ll think this pretty well, Harthouse,’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[021-046] ‘You’ll think this tolerably strong. You’ll say, upon my soul this is a

[021-047] tidy specimen of what my friends have to deal with; but this is nothing,

[021-048] sir! You shall hear me ask this man a question. Pray, Mr.

[021-049] Blackpool’—wind springing up very fast—‘may I take the liberty of asking

[021-050] you how it happens that you refused to be in this Combination?’

[021-051] ‘How ’t happens?’

[021-052] ‘Ah!’ said Mr. Bounderby, with his thumbs in the arms of his coat, and

[021-053] jerking his head and shutting his eyes in confidence with the opposite

[021-054] wall: ‘how it happens.’

[021-055] ‘I’d leefer not coom to ’t, sir; but sin you put th’ question—an’ not

[021-056] want’n t’ be ill-manner’n—I’ll answer. I ha passed a promess.’

[021-057] ‘Not to me, you know,’ said Bounderby. (Gusty weather with deceitful

[021-058] calms. One now prevailing.)

[021-059] ‘O no, sir. Not to yo.’

[021-060] ‘As for me, any consideration for me has had just nothing at all to do

[021-061] with it,’ said Bounderby, still in confidence with the wall. ‘If only

[021-062] Josiah Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]] had been in question, you would have joined

[021-063] and made no bones about it?’

[021-064] ‘Why yes, sir. ’Tis true.’

[021-065] ‘Though he knows,’ said Mr. Bounderby, now blowing a gale, ‘that there

[021-066] are a set of rascals and rebels whom transportation is too good for!

[021-067] Now, Mr. Harthouse, you have been knocking about in the world some time.

[021-068] Did you ever meet with anything like that man out of this blessed

[021-069] country?’ And Mr. Bounderby pointed him out for inspection, with an

[021-070] angry finger.

[021-071] ‘Nay, ma’am,’ said Stephen Blackpool, staunchly protesting against the

[021-072] words that had been used, and instinctively addressing himself to Louisa,

[021-073] after glancing at her face. ‘Not rebels, nor yet rascals. Nowt o’ th’

[021-074] kind, ma’am, nowt o’ th’ kind. They’ve not doon me a kindness, ma’am, as

[021-075] I know and feel. But there’s not a dozen men amoong ’em, ma’am—a dozen?

[021-076] Not six—but what believes as he has doon his [[duty]] by the rest and by

[021-077] himseln. God forbid as I, that ha’ known, and had’n [[experience]] o’ these

[021-078] men aw my life—I, that ha’ ett’n an’ droonken wi’ ’em, an’ seet’n wi’

[021-079] ’em, and toil’n wi’ ’em, and lov’n ’em, should fail fur to stan by ’em

[021-080] wi’ the [[truth]], let ’em ha’ doon to me what they may!’

[021-081] He spoke with the rugged earnestness of his place and character—deepened

[021-082] perhaps by a [[pride|proud]] consciousness that he was faithful to his [[class]] under

[021-083] all their mistrust; but he fully remembered where he was, and did not

[021-084] even raise his voice.

[021-085] ‘No, ma’am, no. They’re true to one another, faithfo’ to one another,

[021-086] ’fectionate to one another, e’en to death. Be poor amoong ’em, be sick

[021-087] amoong ’em, grieve amoong ’em for onny o’ th’ monny causes that carries

[021-088] grief to the poor man’s door, an’ they’ll be tender wi’ yo, gentle wi’

[021-089] yo, comfortable wi’ yo, Chrisen wi’ yo. Be sure o’ that, ma’am. They’d

[021-090] be riven to bits, ere ever they’d be different.’

[021-091] ‘In short,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘it’s because they are so full of virtues

[021-092] that they have turned you adrift. Go through with it while you are about

[021-093] it. Out with it.’

[021-094] ‘How ’tis, ma’am,’ resumed Stephen, appearing still to find his [[nature|natural]]

[021-095] refuge in Louisa’s face, ‘that what is best in us fok, seems to turn us

[021-096] most to trouble an’ misfort’n an’ mistake, I dunno. But ’tis so. I know

[021-097] ’tis, as I know the heavens is over me ahint the smoke. We’re patient

[021-098] too, an’ wants in general to do right. An’ I canna think the fawt is aw

[021-099] wi’ us.’

[021-100] ‘Now, my friend,’ said Mr. Bounderby, whom he could not have exasperated

[021-101] more, quite unconscious of it though he was, than by seeming to appeal to

[021-102] any one else, ‘if you will favour me with your attention for half a

[021-103] minute, I should like to have a word or two with you. You said just now,

[021-104] that you had nothing to tell us about this business. You are quite sure

[021-105] of that before we go any further.’

[021-106] ‘Sir, I am sure on ’t.’

[021-107] ‘Here’s a gentleman from London present,’ Mr. Bounderby made a backhanded

[021-108] point at Mr. James Harthouse with his thumb, ‘a Parliament gentleman. I

[021-109] should like him to hear a short bit of dialogue between you and me,

[021-110] instead of taking the substance of it—for I know precious well,

[021-111] beforehand, what it will be; nobody knows better than I do, take

[021-112] notice!—instead of receiving it on trust from my mouth.’

[021-113] Stephen bent his head to the gentleman from London, and showed a rather

[021-114] more troubled mind than usual. He turned his eyes involuntarily to his

[021-115] former refuge, but at a look from that quarter (expressive though

[021-116] instantaneous) he settled them on Mr. Bounderby’s face.

[021-117] ‘Now, what do you complain of?’ asked Mr. Bounderby.

[021-118] ‘I ha’ not coom here, sir,’ Stephen reminded him, ‘to complain. I coom

[021-119] for that I were sent for.’

[021-120] ‘What,’ repeated Mr. Bounderby, folding his arms, ‘do you people, in a

[021-121] general way, complain of?’

[021-122] Stephen looked at him with some little irresolution for a moment, and

[021-123] then seemed to make up his mind.

[021-124] ‘Sir, I were never good at showin o ’t, though I ha had’n my share in

[021-125] [[heart|feeling]] o ’t. ’Deed we are in a muddle, sir. Look round town—so rich as

[021-126] ’tis—and see the numbers o’ people as has been broughten into bein heer,

[021-127] fur to weave, an’ to card, an’ to piece out a livin’, aw the same one

[021-128] way, somehows, ’twixt their cradles and their graves. Look how we live,

[021-129] an’ wheer we live, an’ in what numbers, an’ by what chances, and wi’ what

[021-130] sameness; and look how the mills is awlus a goin, and how they never

[021-131] works us no nigher to ony dis’ant object—ceptin awlus, Death. Look how

[021-132] you considers of us, and writes of us, and talks of us, and goes up wi’

[021-133] yor deputations to Secretaries o’ State ’bout us, and how yo are awlus

[021-134] right, and how we are awlus wrong, and never had’n no reason in us sin

[021-135] ever we were born. Look how this ha growen an’ growen, sir, bigger an’

[021-136] bigger, broader an’ broader, harder an’ harder, fro year to year, fro

[021-137] generation unto generation. Who can look on ’t, sir, and fairly tell a

[021-138] man ’tis not a muddle?’

[021-139] ‘Of course,’ said Mr. Bounderby. ‘Now perhaps you’ll let the gentleman

[021-140] know, how you would set this muddle (as you’re so fond of calling it) to

[021-141] rights.’

[021-142] ‘I donno, sir. I canna be expecten to ’t. ’Tis not me as should be

[021-143] looken to for that, sir. ’Tis them as is put ower me, and ower aw the

[021-144] rest of us. What do they tak upon themseln, sir, if not to do’t?’

[021-145] ‘I’ll tell you something towards it, at any rate,’ returned Mr.

[021-146] Bounderby. ‘We will make an example of half a dozen Slackbridges. We’ll

[021-147] indict the blackguards for felony, and get ’em shipped off to penal

[021-148] settlements.’

[021-149] Stephen gravely shook his head.

[021-150] ‘Don’t tell me we won’t, man,’ said Mr. Bounderby, by this time blowing a

[021-151] hurricane, ‘because we will, I tell you!’

[021-152] ‘Sir,’ returned Stephen, with the quiet confidence of absolute certainty,

[021-153] ‘if yo was t’ tak a hundred Slackbridges—aw as there is, and aw the

[021-154] number ten times towd—an’ was t’ sew ’em up in separate sacks, an’ sink

[021-155] ’em in the deepest ocean as were made ere ever dry land coom to be, yo’d

[021-156] leave the muddle just wheer ’tis. Mischeevous strangers!’ said Stephen,

[021-157] with an anxious smile; ‘when ha we not heern, I am sure, sin ever we can

[021-158] call to mind, o’ th’ mischeevous strangers! ’Tis not by _them_ the

[021-159] trouble’s made, sir. ’Tis not wi’ _them_ ’t commences. I ha no favour

[021-160] for ’em—I ha no reason to favour ’em—but ’tis hopeless and useless to

[021-161] dream o’ takin them fro their trade, ’stead o’ takin their trade fro

[021-162] them! Aw that’s now about me in this room were heer afore I coom, an’

[021-163] will be heer when I am gone. Put that clock aboard a ship an’ pack it

[021-164] off to Norfolk Island, an’ the time will go on just the same. So ’tis

[021-165] wi’ Slackbridge every bit.’

[021-166] Reverting for a moment to his former refuge, he observed a cautionary

[021-167] movement of her eyes towards the door. Stepping back, he put his hand

[021-168] upon the lock. But he had not spoken out of his own will and desire; and

[021-169] he felt it in his heart a noble return for his late injurious treatment

[021-170] to be faithful to the last to those who had repudiated him. He stayed to

[021-171] finish what was in his mind.

[021-172] ‘Sir, I canna, wi’ my little learning an’ my common way, tell the

[021-173] genelman what will better aw this—though some working men o’ this town

[021-174] could, above my powers—but I can tell him what I know will never do ’t.

[021-175] The strong hand will never do ’t. Vict’ry and triumph will never do ’t.

[021-176] Agreeing fur to mak one side unnat’rally awlus and for ever right, and

[021-177] toother side unnat’rally awlus and for ever wrong, will never, never do

[021-178] ’t. Nor yet lettin alone will never do ’t. Let thousands upon thousands

[021-179] alone, aw leading the like lives and aw faw’en into the like muddle, and

[021-180] they will be as one, and yo will be as anoother, wi’ a black unpassable

[021-181] world betwixt yo, just as long or short a time as sich-like misery can

[021-182] last. Not drawin nigh to fok, wi’ kindness and patience an’ cheery ways,

[021-183] that so draws nigh to one another in their monny troubles, and so

[021-184] cherishes one another in their distresses wi’ what they need

[021-185] themseln—like, I humbly believe, as no people the genelman ha seen in aw

[021-186] his travels can beat—will never do ’t till th’ Sun turns t’ ice. Most o’

[021-187] aw, rating ’em as so much Power, and reg’latin ’em as if they was figures

[021-188] in a soom, or machines: wi’out loves and likens, wi’out memories and

[021-189] inclinations, wi’out souls to weary and souls to hope—when aw goes quiet,

[021-190] draggin on wi’ ’em as if they’d nowt o’ th’ kind, and when aw goes

[021-191] onquiet, reproachin ’em for their want o’ sitch humanly feelins in their

[021-192] dealins wi’ yo—this will never do ’t, sir, till God’s work is onmade.’

[021-193] Stephen stood with the open door in his hand, waiting to know if anything

[021-194] more were expected of him.

[021-195] ‘Just stop a moment,’ said Mr. Bounderby, excessively red in the face.

[021-196] ‘I told you, the last time you were here with a grievance, that you had

[021-197] better turn about and come out of that. And I also told you, if you

[021-198] remember, that I was up to the gold spoon look-out.’

[021-199] ‘I were not up to ’t myseln, sir; I do assure yo.’

[021-200] ‘Now it’s clear to me,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘that you are one of those

[021-201] chaps who have always got a grievance. And you go about, sowing it and

[021-202] raising crops. That’s the business of _your_ life, my friend.’

[021-203] Stephen shook his head, mutely protesting that indeed he had other

[021-204] business to do for his life.

[021-205] ‘You are such a waspish, raspish, ill-conditioned chap, you see,’ said

[021-206] Mr. Bounderby, ‘that even your own Union, the men who know you best, will

[021-207] have nothing to do with you. I never thought those fellows could be

[021-208] right in anything; but I tell you what! I so far go along with them for

[021-209] a novelty, that _I_’ll have nothing to do with you either.’

[021-210] Stephen raised his eyes quickly to his face.

[021-211] ‘You can finish off what you’re at,’ said Mr. Bounderby, with a meaning

[021-212] nod, ‘and then go elsewhere.’

[021-213] ‘Sir, yo know weel,’ said Stephen expressively, ‘that if I canna get work

[021-214] wi’ yo, I canna get it elsewheer.’

[021-215] The reply was, ‘What I know, I know; and what you know, you know. I have

[021-216] no more to say about it.’

[021-217] Stephen glanced at Louisa again, but her eyes were raised to his no more;

[021-218] therefore, with a sigh, and saying, barely above his breath, ‘Heaven help

[021-219] us aw in this world!’ he departed.
