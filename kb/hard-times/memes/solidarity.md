# Solidarity

Solidarity — the mutual obligation of working people to one another — is the ethical centre of *Hard Times*'s industrial plot. Dickens, who visited the Preston cotton-workers' strike during the novel's composition, gives the question of worker combination a complexity unusual in Victorian fiction. The novel neither endorses nor condemns trade unionism outright; instead, it asks what solidarity demands of an individual, and what happens when collective action and individual conscience come into conflict. Stephen Blackpool, caught between his fellow workers (who ostracise him for refusing to join the union) and his employer (who dismisses him for refusing to inform on the union), becomes the novel's tortured case study in the cost of moral independence.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of the union question is structured around a paradox. The novel is deeply sympathetic to the workers' grievances — their wages are miserable, their conditions degrading, their masters indifferent. Gradgrind's opening catechism had already declared that "combinations and modifications" of material reality are impossible (PN: 002-157), a doctrine that leaves workers no legitimate means of improving their lot. Yet when the workers do combine, Dickens presents their collective action as itself a form of coercion — Slackbridge, the union organiser, is one of the novel's most repellent characters, a demagogue who exploits the workers' legitimate anger for his own advancement.

Stephen Blackpool occupies the impossible space between these two positions. He will not join the union (he will not be "bound" by a collective decision he cannot endorse), but neither will he betray it to Bounderby. The consequence is that he is abandoned by both sides — "abandonment by all his fellows from a baseless sense of shame" (PN: 020-218). The novel refuses to resolve this tragedy into a tidy moral; it insists only that the situation is, as Stephen says, "a muddle."

### Key Passages

> "'Tis a muddle, and that's aw... Awlus a muddle. That's where I stick. I come to the muddle, and I stop." (PN: 010-099–105)

Stephen's repeated confession of bewilderment is not intellectual failure but moral precision. He refuses to reduce the complexity of industrial relations to a slogan, and in so doing he preserves his humanity — at the cost of his social existence. The "muddle" is Dickens's critique of every party to the industrial conflict: the masters' political economy, the union's rhetoric, and the philanthropic reformers' nostrums are all attempts to simplify what cannot be simplified.

> Gradgrind declares that "for all these purposes, combinations and modifications" of material reality are impossible (PN: 002-157)

Gradgrind's early philosophical pronouncement has a political afterlife he does not foresee. If "combinations" are impossible, then worker combinations are unnatural — and the workers' attempt to combine is not merely illegal but, in the Gradgrindian worldview, a violation of the laws of nature. The novel traces the violence done when a philosophical axiom is enforced as a social policy.

## Historical Context

*Hard Times* was written in the immediate aftermath of the Preston lock-out (1853–54), a bitter industrial dispute in the Lancashire cotton industry that Dickens visited and reported on for *Household Words*. The lock-out pitted mill-owners, determined to break the power of the newly forming trade unions, against operatives organised under the banner of the "Preston Association of Cotton Spinners." Dickens's sympathies were broadly with the workers, but he was suspicious of union militancy and deeply hostile to demagoguery on both sides. The character of Slackbridge is generally read as a portrait of the kind of union organiser Dickens feared — a man who inflames rather than reconciles.

The legal context is also relevant: the Combination Acts, which had made trade unionism a criminal conspiracy, were repealed in 1824, but unions continued to operate in a legal grey area, vulnerable to prosecution under common law. The "Coketown delegates" who pressure Stephen to join are operating at the edge of legality, and their desperation reflects the near-total absence of legal protections for workers.

## Embodied By

- **[[stephen-blackpool]]**: The novel's tragic hero of solidarity. Stephen's refusal to join the union is not a repudiation of his fellow workers — he refuses, under enormous pressure, to inform on them to Bounderby — but an assertion that solidarity must be freely chosen, not coerced. His punishment is to be shunned by the very people he refuses to betray.
- **[[rachael]]**: The novel's image of solidarity as personal care. Rachael's devotion to Stephen's alcoholic wife — a woman who has wronged her grievously — is a solidarity that expects nothing in return. She embodies the novel's argument that the deepest bonds are not institutional but human.
- **[[slackbridge]]**: The false solidarity of the demagogue. Slackbridge uses the language of brotherhood to advance his own position; his solidarity is performative, a tool of self-advancement. In the novel's moral scheme, he is Bounderby's mirror — two kinds of self-interest dressed in different rhetoric.

## Contrasted With

- **[[self-interest]]**: The principle that dissolves solidarity. If every person is a rational calculator of their own advantage, then the bonds that unite workers against their employers are irrational. The novel demonstrates that self-interest, pursued consistently, produces not a harmonious market but a war of all against all.
- **[[class]]**: Class, in the novel's vocabulary, is the structural condition that makes solidarity both necessary and difficult. The workers need solidarity because they are a class; but the experience of class also makes them susceptible to demagogues like Slackbridge, who offer solidarity as a weapon rather than a bond.
