[030-001] VERY RIDICULOUS

[030-002] MR. JAMES HARTHOUSE passed a whole night and a day in a state of so much

[030-003] hurry, that the World, with its best glass in his eye, would scarcely

[030-004] have recognized him during that insane interval, as the brother [[james-harthouse|Jem]] of

[030-005] the honourable and jocular member. He was positively agitated. He

[030-006] several times spoke with an emphasis, similar to the vulgar manner. He

[030-007] went in and went out in an unaccountable way, like a man without an

[030-008] object. He rode like a highwayman. In a word, he was so horribly bored

[030-009] by existing circumstances, that he forgot to go in for boredom in the

[030-010] manner prescribed by the authorities.

[030-011] After putting his horse at [[coketown|Coketown]] through the storm, as if it were a

[030-012] leap, he waited up all night: from time to time ringing his bell with the

[030-013] greatest fury, charging the porter who kept watch with delinquency in

[030-014] withholding letters or messages that could not fail to have been

[030-015] entrusted to him, and demanding restitution on the spot. The dawn

[030-016] coming, the morning coming, and the day coming, and neither message nor

[030-017] letter coming with either, he went down to [[bounderby-s-country-estate|the country house]]. There, the

[030-018] report was, [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] away, and [[louisa-gradgrind|Mrs. Bounderby]] in town. Left for

[030-019] town suddenly last evening. Not even known to be gone until receipt of

[030-020] message, importing that her return was not to be expected for the

[030-021] present.

[030-022] In these circumstances he had nothing for it but to follow her to town.

[030-023] He went to the house in town. Mrs. Bounderby not there. He looked in at

[030-024] the Bank. Mr. Bounderby away and [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] away. Mrs. Sparsit away?

[030-025] Who could have been reduced to sudden extremity for the company of that

[030-026] griffin!

[030-027] ‘Well! I don’t know,’ said [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]], who had his own reasons for being uneasy

[030-028] about it. ‘She was off somewhere at daybreak this morning. She’s always

[030-029] full of mystery; I hate her. So I do that white chap; he’s always got

[030-030] his blinking eyes upon a fellow.’

[030-031] ‘Where were you last night, Tom?’

[030-032] ‘Where was I last night!’ said Tom. ‘Come! I like that. I was waiting

[030-033] for you, Mr. Harthouse, till it came down as _I_ never saw it come down

[030-034] before. Where was I too! Where were you, you mean.’

[030-035] ‘I was prevented from coming—detained.’

[030-036] ‘Detained!’ murmured Tom. ‘Two of us were detained. I was detained

[030-037] looking for you, till I lost every train but the mail. It would have

[030-038] been a pleasant job to go down by that on such a night, and have to walk

[030-039] home through a pond. I was obliged to sleep in town after all.’

[030-040] ‘Where?’

[030-041] ‘Where? Why, in my own bed at Bounderby’s.’

[030-042] ‘Did you see your sister?’

[030-043] ‘How the deuce,’ returned Tom, staring, ‘could I see my sister when she

[030-044] was fifteen miles off?’

[030-045] Cursing these quick retorts of the young [[gentleman]] to whom he was so true

[030-046] a friend, Mr. Harthouse disembarrassed himself of that interview with the

[030-047] smallest conceivable amount of ceremony, and debated for the hundredth

[030-048] time what all this could mean? He made only one thing clear. It was,

[030-049] that whether she was in town or out of town, whether he had been

[030-050] premature with her who was so hard to comprehend, or she had lost

[030-051] courage, or they were discovered, or some mischance or mistake, at

[030-052] present incomprehensible, had occurred, he must remain to confront his

[030-053] fortune, whatever it was. The hotel where he was known to live when

[030-054] condemned to that region of blackness, was the stake to which he was

[030-055] tied. As to all the rest—What will be, will be.

[030-056] ‘So, whether I am waiting for a hostile message, or an assignation, or a

[030-057] penitent remonstrance, or an impromptu wrestle with my friend Bounderby

[030-058] in the Lancashire manner—which would seem as likely as anything else in

[030-059] the present state of affairs—I’ll dine,’ said Mr. James Harthouse.

[030-060] ‘Bounderby has the advantage in point of weight; and if anything of a

[030-061] British [[nature]] is to come off between us, it may be as well to be in

[030-062] [[education|training]].’

[030-063] Therefore he rang the bell, and tossing himself negligently on a sofa,

[030-064] ordered ‘Some dinner at six—with a beefsteak in it,’ and got through the

[030-065] intervening time as well as he could. That was not particularly well;

[030-066] for he remained in the greatest perplexity, and, as the hours went on,

[030-067] and no kind of explanation offered itself, his perplexity augmented at

[030-068] compound interest.

[030-069] However, he took affairs as coolly as it was in [[humanity|human nature]] to do, and

[030-070] entertained himself with the facetious idea of the training more than

[030-071] once. ‘It wouldn’t be bad,’ he yawned at one time, ‘to give the waiter

[030-072] five shillings, and throw him.’ At another time it occurred to him, ‘Or

[030-073] a fellow of about thirteen or fourteen stone might be hired by the hour.’

[030-074] But these jests did not tell materially on the afternoon, or his

[030-075] suspense; and, sooth to say, they both lagged fearfully.

[030-076] It was impossible, even before dinner, to avoid often walking about in

[030-077] the pattern of the carpet, looking out of the window, listening at the

[030-078] door for footsteps, and occasionally becoming rather hot when any steps

[030-079] approached that room. But, after dinner, when the day turned to

[030-080] twilight, and the twilight turned to night, and still no communication

[030-081] was made to him, it began to be as he expressed it, ‘like the Holy Office

[030-082] and slow torture.’ However, still true to his conviction that

[030-083] indifference was the genuine high-breeding (the only conviction he had),

[030-084] he seized this crisis as the opportunity for ordering candles and a

[030-085] newspaper.

[030-086] He had been trying in vain, for half an hour, to read this newspaper,

[030-087] when the waiter appeared and said, at once mysteriously and

[030-088] apologetically:

[030-089] ‘Beg your pardon, sir. You’re wanted, sir, if you please.’

[030-090] A general recollection that this was the kind of thing the Police said to

[030-091] the swell mob, caused Mr. Harthouse to ask the waiter in return, with

[030-092] bristling indignation, what the Devil he meant by ‘wanted’?

[030-093] ‘Beg your pardon, sir. Young lady outside, sir, wishes to see you.’

[030-094] ‘Outside? Where?’

[030-095] ‘Outside this door, sir.’

[030-096] Giving the waiter to the personage before mentioned, as a block-[[reason|head]] duly

[030-097] qualified for that consignment, Mr. Harthouse hurried into the gallery.

[030-098] A young woman whom he had never seen stood there. Plainly dressed, very

[030-099] quiet, very pretty. As he conducted her into the room and placed a chair

[030-100] for her, he observed, by the light of the candles, that she was even

[030-101] prettier than he had at first believed. Her face was innocent and

[030-102] youthful, and its expression remarkably pleasant. She was not afraid of

[030-103] him, or in any way disconcerted; she seemed to have her mind entirely

[030-104] preoccupied with the occasion of her visit, and to have substituted that

[030-105] consideration for herself.

[030-106] ‘I speak to Mr. Harthouse?’ she said, when they were alone.

[030-107] ‘To Mr. Harthouse.’ He added in his mind, ‘And you speak to him with the

[030-108] most confiding eyes I ever saw, and the most earnest voice (though so

[030-109] quiet) I ever heard.’

[030-110] ‘If I do not understand—and I do not, sir’—said [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]], ‘what your honour

[030-111] as a gentleman binds you to, in other matters:’ the blood really rose in

[030-112] his face as she began in these words: ‘I am sure I may rely upon it to

[030-113] keep my visit secret, and to keep secret what I am going to say. I will

[030-114] rely upon it, if you will tell me I may so far trust—’

[030-115] ‘You may, I assure you.’

[030-116] ‘I am young, as you see; I am alone, as you see. In coming to you, sir,

[030-117] I have no advice or encouragement beyond my own hope.’ He thought, ‘But

[030-118] that is very strong,’ as he followed the momentary upward glance of her

[030-119] eyes. He thought besides, ‘This is a very odd beginning. I don’t see

[030-120] where we are going.’

[030-121] ‘I think,’ said Sissy, ‘you have already guessed whom I left just now!’

[030-122] ‘I have been in the greatest concern and uneasiness during the last

[030-123] four-and-twenty hours (which have appeared as many years),’ he returned,

[030-124] ‘on a lady’s account. The hopes I have been encouraged to form that you

[030-125] come from that lady, do not deceive me, I trust.’

[030-126] ‘I left her within an hour.’

[030-127] ‘At—!’

[030-128] ‘At her father’s.’

[030-129] Mr. Harthouse’s face lengthened in spite of his coolness, and his

[030-130] perplexity increased. ‘Then I certainly,’ he thought, ‘do _not_ see

[030-131] where we are going.’

[030-132] ‘She hurried there last night. She arrived there in great agitation, and

[030-133] was insensible all through the night. I live at her father’s, and was

[030-134] with her. You may be sure, sir, you will never see her again as long as

[030-135] you live.’

[030-136] Mr. Harthouse drew a long breath; and, if ever man found himself in the

[030-137] position of not knowing what to say, made the discovery beyond all

[030-138] question that he was so circumstanced. The [[childhood|child]]-like ingenuousness with

[030-139] which his visitor spoke, her modest fearlessness, her [[truth|truthfulness]] which

[030-140] put all artifice aside, her entire forgetfulness of herself in her

[030-141] earnest quiet holding to the object with which she had come; all this,

[030-142] together with her reliance on his easily given promise—which in itself

[030-143] shamed him—presented something in which he was so inexperienced, and

[030-144] against which he knew any of his usual weapons would fall so powerless;

[030-145] that not a word could he rally to his relief.

[030-146] At last he said:

[030-147] ‘So startling an announcement, so confidently made, and by such lips, is

[030-148] really disconcerting in the last degree. May I be permitted to inquire,

[030-149] if you are charged to convey that information to me in those hopeless

[030-150] words, by the lady of whom we speak?’

[030-151] ‘I have no charge from her.’

[030-152] ‘The drowning man catches at the straw. With no disrespect for your

[030-153] judgment, and with no doubt of your sincerity, excuse my saying that I

[030-154] cling to the belief that there is yet hope that I am not condemned to

[030-155] perpetual exile from that lady’s presence.’

[030-156] ‘There is not the least hope. The first object of my coming here, sir,

[030-157] is to assure you that you must believe that there is no more hope of your

[030-158] ever speaking with her again, than there would be if she had died when

[030-159] she came home last night.’

[030-160] ‘Must believe? But if I can’t—or if I should, by infirmity of nature, be

[030-161] obstinate—and won’t—’

[030-162] ‘It is still true. There is no hope.’

[030-163] James Harthouse looked at her with an incredulous smile upon his lips;

[030-164] but her mind looked over and beyond him, and the smile was quite thrown

[030-165] away.

[030-166] He bit his lip, and took a little time for consideration.

[030-167] ‘Well! If it should unhappily appear,’ he said, ‘after due pains and

[030-168] [[duty]] on my part, that I am brought to a position so desolate as this

[030-169] banishment, I shall not become the lady’s persecutor. But you said you

[030-170] had no commission from her?’

[030-171] ‘I have only the commission of my [[love]] for her, and her love for me. I

[030-172] have no other trust, than that I have been with her since she came home,

[030-173] and that she has given me her confidence. I have no further trust, than

[030-174] that I know something of her character and her [[marriage]]. O Mr.

[030-175] Harthouse, I think you had that trust too!’

[030-176] He was touched in the cavity where his [[heart]] should have been—in that

[030-177] nest of addled eggs, where the birds of heaven would have lived if they

[030-178] had not been whistled away—by the fervour of this reproach.

[030-179] ‘I am not a moral sort of fellow,’ he said, ‘and I never make any

[030-180] pretensions to the character of a moral sort of fellow. I am as immoral

[030-181] as need be. At the same time, in bringing any distress upon the lady who

[030-182] is the subject of the present conversation, or in unfortunately

[030-183] compromising her in any way, or in committing myself by any expression of

[030-184] sentiments towards her, not perfectly reconcilable with—in [[facts|fact]] with—the

[030-185] domestic hearth; or in taking any advantage of her father’s being a

[030-186] machine, or of her brother’s being a whelp, or of her husband’s being a

[030-187] bear; I beg to be allowed to assure you that I have had no particularly

[030-188] evil intentions, but have glided on from one step to another with a

[030-189] smoothness so perfectly diabolical, that I had not the slightest idea the

[030-190] catalogue was half so long until I began to turn it over. Whereas I

[030-191] find,’ said Mr. James Harthouse, in conclusion, ‘that it is really in

[030-192] several volumes.’

[030-193] Though he said all this in his frivolous way, the way seemed, for that

[030-194] once, a conscious polishing of but an ugly surface. He was silent for a

[030-195] moment; and then proceeded with a more self-possessed air, though with

[030-196] traces of vexation and disappointment that would not be polished out.

[030-197] ‘After what has been just now represented to me, in a manner I find it

[030-198] impossible to doubt—I know of hardly any other source from which I could

[030-199] have accepted it so readily—I feel bound to say to you, in whom the

[030-200] confidence you have mentioned has been reposed, that I cannot refuse to

[030-201] contemplate the possibility (however unexpected) of my seeing the lady no

[030-202] more. I am solely to blame for the thing having come to this—and—and, I

[030-203] cannot say,’ he added, rather hard up for a general peroration, ‘that I

[030-204] have any sanguine expectation of ever becoming a moral sort of fellow, or

[030-205] that I have any belief in any moral sort of fellow whatever.’

[030-206] Sissy’s face sufficiently showed that her appeal to him was not finished.

[030-207] ‘You spoke,’ he resumed, as she raised her eyes to him again, ‘of your

[030-208] first object. I may assume that there is a second to be mentioned?’

[030-209] ‘Yes.’

[030-210] ‘Will you oblige me by confiding it?’

[030-211] ‘Mr. Harthouse,’ returned Sissy, with a blending of gentleness and

[030-212] steadiness that quite defeated him, and with a simple confidence in his

[030-213] being bound to do what she required, that held him at a singular

[030-214] disadvantage, ‘the only reparation that remains with you, is to leave

[030-215] here immediately and finally. I am quite sure that you can mitigate in

[030-216] no other way the wrong and harm you have done. I am quite sure that it

[030-217] is the only compensation you have left it in your power to make. I do

[030-218] not say that it is much, or that it is enough; but it is something, and

[030-219] it is necessary. Therefore, though without any other authority than I

[030-220] have given you, and even without the knowledge of any other person than

[030-221] yourself and myself, I ask you to depart from this place to-night, under

[030-222] an obligation never to return to it.’

[030-223] If she had asserted any influence over him beyond her plain faith in the

[030-224] truth and right of what she said; if she had concealed the least doubt or

[030-225] irresolution, or had harboured for the best purpose any reserve or

[030-226] pretence; if she had shown, or felt, the lightest trace of any

[030-227] sensitiveness to his ridicule or his astonishment, or any remonstrance he

[030-228] might offer; he would have carried it against her at this point. But he

[030-229] could as easily have changed a clear sky by looking at it in surprise, as

[030-230] affect her.

[030-231] ‘But do you know,’ he asked, quite at a loss, ‘the extent of what you

[030-232] ask? You probably are not aware that I am here on a public kind of

[030-233] business, preposterous enough in itself, but which I have gone in for,

[030-234] and sworn by, and am supposed to be devoted to in quite a desperate

[030-235] manner? You probably are not aware of that, but I assure you it’s the

[030-236] fact.’

[030-237] It had no effect on Sissy, fact or no fact.

[030-238] ‘Besides which,’ said Mr. Harthouse, taking a turn or two across the

[030-239] room, dubiously, ‘it’s so alarmingly absurd. It would make a man so

[030-240] ridiculous, after going in for these fellows, to back out in such an

[030-241] incomprehensible way.’

[030-242] ‘I am quite sure,’ repeated Sissy, ‘that it is the only reparation in

[030-243] your power, sir. I am quite sure, or I would not have come here.’

[030-244] He glanced at her face, and walked about again. ‘Upon my soul, I don’t

[030-245] know what to say. So immensely absurd!’

[030-246] It fell to his lot, now, to stipulate for secrecy.

[030-247] ‘If I were to do such a very ridiculous thing,’ he said, stopping again

[030-248] presently, and leaning against the chimney-piece, ‘it could only be in

[030-249] the most inviolable confidence.’

[030-250] ‘I will trust to you, sir,’ returned Sissy, ‘and you will trust to me.’

[030-251] His leaning against the chimney-piece reminded him of the night with the

[030-252] whelp. It was the self-same chimney-piece, and somehow he felt as if

[030-253] _he_ were the whelp to-night. He could make no way at all.

[030-254] ‘I suppose a man never was placed in a more ridiculous position,’ he

[030-255] said, after looking down, and looking up, and laughing, and frowning, and

[030-256] walking off, and walking back again. ‘But I see no way out of it. What

[030-257] will be, will be. _This_ will be, I suppose. I must take off myself, I

[030-258] imagine—in short, I engage to do it.’

[030-259] Sissy rose. She was not surprised by the result, but she was happy in

[030-260] it, and her face beamed brightly.

[030-261] ‘You will permit me to say,’ continued Mr. James Harthouse, ‘that I doubt

[030-262] if any other ambassador, or ambassadress, could have addressed me with

[030-263] the same success. I must not only regard myself as being in a very

[030-264] ridiculous position, but as being vanquished at all points. Will you

[030-265] allow me the privilege of remembering my enemy’s name?’

[030-266] ‘_My_ name?’ said the ambassadress.

[030-267] ‘The only name I could possibly care to know, to-night.’

[030-268] ‘Sissy Jupe.’

[030-269] ‘Pardon my curiosity at parting. Related to the [[family]]?’

[030-270] ‘I am only a poor girl,’ returned Sissy. ‘I was separated from my

[030-271] father—he was only a stroller—and taken pity on by [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]]. I have

[030-272] lived in the house ever since.’

[030-273] She was gone.

[030-274] ‘It wanted this to complete the defeat,’ said Mr. James Harthouse,

[030-275] sinking, with a resigned air, on the sofa, after standing transfixed a

[030-276] little while. ‘The defeat may now be considered perfectly accomplished.

[030-277] Only a poor girl—only a stroller—only James Harthouse made nothing

[030-278] of—only James Harthouse a Great Pyramid of failure.’

[030-279] The Great Pyramid put it into his head to go up the Nile. He took a pen

[030-280] upon the instant, and wrote the following note (in appropriate

[030-281] hieroglyphics) to his brother:

[030-282] Dear Jack,—All up at Coketown. Bored out of the place, and going in

[030-283] for camels.

[030-284] Affectionately,

[030-285] JEM.

[030-286] He rang the bell.

[030-287] ‘Send my fellow here.’

[030-288] ‘Gone to bed, sir.’

[030-289] ‘Tell him to get up, and pack up.’

[030-290] He wrote two more notes. One, to Mr. Bounderby, announcing his

[030-291] retirement from that part of the country, and showing where he would be

[030-292] found for the next fortnight. The other, similar in effect, to Mr.

[030-293] [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]]. Almost as soon as the ink was dry upon their superscriptions,

[030-294] he had left the tall chimneys of Coketown behind, and was in a railway

[030-295] carriage, tearing and glaring over the dark landscape.

[030-296] The moral sort of fellows might suppose that Mr. James Harthouse derived

[030-297] some comfortable reflections afterwards, from this prompt retreat, as one

[030-298] of his few actions that made any amends for anything, and as a token to

[030-299] himself that he had escaped the climax of a very bad business. But it

[030-300] was not so, at all. A secret sense of having failed and been

[030-301] ridiculous—a dread of what other fellows who went in for similar sorts of

[030-302] things, would say at his expense if they knew it—so oppressed him, that

[030-303] what was about the very best passage in his life was the one of all

[030-304] others he would not have owned to on any account, and the only one that

[030-305] made him [[shame|ashamed]] of himself.
