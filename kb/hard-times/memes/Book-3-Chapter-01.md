[029-001] ANOTHER THING NEEDFUL

[029-002] LOUISA awoke from a torpor, and her eyes languidly opened on her old bed

[029-003] at home, and her old room. It seemed, at first, as if all that had

[029-004] happened since the days when these objects were familiar to her were the

[029-005] shadows of a dream, but gradually, as the objects became more real to her

[029-006] sight, the events became more real to her mind.

[029-007] She could scarcely move her [[reason|head]] for pain and heaviness, her eyes were

[029-008] strained and sore, and she was very weak. A curious passive inattention

[029-009] had such possession of her, that the presence of her little sister in the

[029-010] room did not attract her notice for some time. Even when their eyes had

[029-011] met, and her sister had approached the bed, [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] lay for minutes

[029-012] looking at her in silence, and suffering her timidly to hold her passive

[029-013] hand, before she asked:

[029-014] ‘When was I brought to this room?’

[029-015] ‘Last night, Louisa.’

[029-016] ‘Who brought me here?’

[029-017] ‘[[sissy-jupe|Sissy]], I believe.’

[029-018] ‘Why do you believe so?’

[029-019] ‘Because I found her here this morning. She didn’t come to my bedside to

[029-020] wake me, as she always does; and I went to look for her. She was not in

[029-021] her own room either; and I went looking for her all over the house, until

[029-022] I found her here taking care of you and cooling your head. Will you see

[029-023] father? Sissy said I was to tell him when you woke.’

[029-024] ‘What a beaming face you have, Jane!’ said Louisa, as her young

[029-025] sister—timidly still—bent down to kiss her.

[029-026] ‘Have I? I am very glad you think so. I am sure it must be Sissy’s

[029-027] doing.’

[029-028] The arm Louisa had begun to twine around her neck, unbent itself. ‘You

[029-029] can tell father if you will.’ Then, staying her for a moment, she said,

[029-030] ‘It was you who made my room so cheerful, and gave it this look of

[029-031] welcome?’

[029-032] ‘Oh no, Louisa, it was done before I came. It was—’

[029-033] Louisa turned upon her pillow, and heard no more. When her sister had

[029-034] withdrawn, she turned her head back again, and lay with her face towards

[029-035] the door, until it opened and her father entered.

[029-036] He had a jaded anxious look upon him, and his hand, usually steady,

[029-037] trembled in hers. He sat down at the side of the bed, tenderly asking

[029-038] how she was, and dwelling on the necessity of her keeping very quiet

[029-039] after her agitation and exposure to the weather last night. He spoke in

[029-040] a subdued and troubled voice, very different from his usual dictatorial

[029-041] manner; and was often at a loss for words.

[029-042] ‘My [[love|dear]] Louisa. My poor daughter.’ He was so much at a loss at that

[029-043] place, that he stopped altogether. He tried again.

[029-044] ‘My unfortunate [[childhood|child]].’ The place was so difficult to get over, that he

[029-045] tried again.

[029-046] ‘It would be hopeless for me, Louisa, to endeavour to tell you how

[029-047] overwhelmed I have been, and still am, by what broke upon me last night.

[029-048] The ground on which I stand has ceased to be solid under my feet. The

[029-049] only support on which I leaned, and the strength of which it seemed, and

[029-050] still does seem, impossible to question, has given way in an instant. I

[029-051] am stunned by these discoveries. I have no selfish meaning in what I

[029-052] say; but I find the shock of what broke upon me last night, to be very

[029-053] heavy indeed.’

[029-054] She could give him no comfort herein. She had suffered the wreck of her

[029-055] whole life upon the rock.

[029-056] ‘I will not say, Louisa, that if you had by any happy chance undeceived

[029-057] me some time ago, it would have been better for us both; better for your

[029-058] peace, and better for mine. For I am sensible that it may not have been

[029-059] a part of my system to invite any confidence of that kind. I had proved

[029-060] my—my system to myself, and I have rigidly administered it; and I must

[029-061] bear the responsibility of its failures. I only entreat you to believe,

[029-062] my favourite child, that I have meant to do right.’

[029-063] He said it earnestly, and to do him justice he had. In gauging

[029-064] fathomless deeps with his little mean excise-rod, and in staggering over

[029-065] the universe with his rusty stiff-legged compasses, he had meant to do

[029-066] great things. Within the limits of his short tether he had tumbled

[029-067] about, annihilating the flowers of existence with greater singleness of

[029-068] purpose than many of the blatant personages whose company he kept.

[029-069] ‘I am well assured of what you say, father. I know I have been your

[029-070] favourite child. I know you have intended to make me happy. I have

[029-071] never blamed you, and I never shall.’

[029-072] He took her outstretched hand, and retained it in his.

[029-073] ‘My dear, I have remained all night at my table, pondering again and

[029-074] again on what has so painfully passed between us. When I consider your

[029-075] character; when I consider that what has been known to me for hours, has

[029-076] been concealed by you for years; when I consider under what immediate

[029-077] pressure it has been forced from you at last; I come to the conclusion

[029-078] that I cannot but mistrust myself.’

[029-079] He might have added more than all, when he saw the face now looking at

[029-080] him. He did add it in effect, perhaps, as he softly moved her scattered

[029-081] hair from her forehead with his hand. Such little actions, slight in

[029-082] another man, were very noticeable in him; and his daughter received them

[029-083] as if they had been words of contrition.

[029-084] ‘But,’ said [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], slowly, and with hesitation, as well as with a

[029-085] wretched sense of happiness, ‘if I see reason to mistrust myself for the

[029-086] past, Louisa, I should also mistrust myself for the present and the

[029-087] future. To speak unreservedly to you, I do. I am far from [[heart|feeling]]

[029-088] convinced now, however differently I might have felt only this time

[029-089] yesterday, that I am fit for the trust you repose in me; that I know how

[029-090] to respond to the appeal you have come home to make to me; that I have

[029-091] the right instinct—supposing it for the moment to be some quality of that

[029-092] [[nature]]—how to help you, and to set you right, my child.’

[029-093] She had turned upon her pillow, and lay with her face upon her arm, so

[029-094] that he could not see it. All her wildness and passion had subsided;

[029-095] but, though softened, she was not in tears. Her father was changed in

[029-096] nothing so much as in the respect that he would have been glad to see her

[029-097] in tears.

[029-098] ‘Some persons hold,’ he pursued, still hesitating, ‘that there is a

[029-099] wisdom of the Head, and that there is a wisdom of the Heart. I have not

[029-100] supposed so; but, as I have said, I mistrust myself now. I have supposed

[029-101] the head to be all-sufficient. It may not be all-sufficient; how can I

[029-102] venture this morning to say it is! If that other kind of wisdom should

[029-103] be what I have neglected, and should be the instinct that is wanted,

[029-104] Louisa—’

[029-105] He suggested it very doubtfully, as if he were half unwilling to admit it

[029-106] even now. She made him no answer, lying before him on her bed, still

[029-107] half-dressed, much as he had seen her lying on the floor of his room last

[029-108] night.

[029-109] ‘Louisa,’ and his hand rested on her hair again, ‘I have been absent from

[029-110] here, my dear, a good deal of late; and though your sister’s [[education|training]] has

[029-111] been pursued according to—the system,’ he appeared to come to that word

[029-112] with great reluctance always, ‘it has necessarily been modified by daily

[029-113] associations begun, in her case, at an early age. I ask you—ignorantly

[029-114] and humbly, my daughter—for the better, do you think?’

[029-115] ‘Father,’ she replied, without stirring, ‘if any harmony has been

[029-116] awakened in her young breast that was mute in mine until it turned to

[029-117] discord, let her thank Heaven for it, and go upon her happier way, taking

[029-118] it as her greatest blessing that she has avoided my way.’

[029-119] ‘O my child, my child!’ he said, in a forlorn manner, ‘I am an unhappy

[029-120] man to see you thus! What avails it to me that you do not reproach me,

[029-121] if I so bitterly reproach myself!’ He bent his head, and spoke low to

[029-122] her. ‘Louisa, I have a misgiving that some change may have been slowly

[029-123] working about me in this house, by mere love and gratitude: that what the

[029-124] Head had left undone and could not do, the Heart may have been doing

[029-125] silently. Can it be so?’

[029-126] She made him no reply.

[029-127] ‘I am not too [[pride|proud]] to believe it, Louisa. How could I be arrogant, and

[029-128] you before me! Can it be so? Is it so, my dear?’ He looked upon her

[029-129] once more, lying cast away there; and without another word went out of

[029-130] the room. He had not been long gone, when she heard a light tread near

[029-131] the door, and knew that some one stood beside her.

[029-132] She did not raise her head. A dull anger that she should be seen in her

[029-133] distress, and that the involuntary look she had so resented should come

[029-134] to this fulfilment, smouldered within her like an unwholesome fire. All

[029-135] closely imprisoned forces rend and destroy. The air that would be

[029-136] healthful to the earth, the water that would enrich it, the heat that

[029-137] would ripen it, tear it when caged up. So in her bosom even now; the

[029-138] strongest qualities she possessed, long turned upon themselves, became a

[029-139] heap of obduracy, that rose against a friend.

[029-140] It was well that soft touch came upon her neck, and that she understood

[029-141] herself to be supposed to have fallen asleep. The sympathetic hand did

[029-142] not claim her resentment. Let it lie there, let it lie.

[029-143] It lay there, warming into life a crowd of gentler thoughts; and she

[029-144] rested. As she softened with the quiet, and the consciousness of being

[029-145] so watched, some tears made their way into her eyes. The face touched

[029-146] hers, and she knew that there were tears upon it too, and she the cause

[029-147] of them.

[029-148] As Louisa feigned to rouse herself, and sat up, Sissy retired, so that

[029-149] she stood placidly near the bedside.

[029-150] ‘I hope I have not disturbed you. I have come to ask if you would let me

[029-151] stay with you?’

[029-152] ‘Why should you stay with me? My sister will miss you. You are

[029-153] everything to her.’

[029-154] ‘Am I?’ returned Sissy, shaking her head. ‘I would be something to you,

[029-155] if I might.’

[029-156] ‘What?’ said Louisa, almost sternly.

[029-157] ‘Whatever you want most, if I could be that. At all events, I would like

[029-158] to try to be as near it as I can. And however far off that may be, I

[029-159] will never tire of trying. Will you let me?’

[029-160] ‘My father sent you to ask me.’

[029-161] ‘No indeed,’ replied Sissy. ‘He told me that I might come in now, but he

[029-162] sent me away from the room this morning—or at least—’

[029-163] She hesitated and stopped.

[029-164] ‘At least, what?’ said Louisa, with her searching eyes upon her.

[029-165] ‘I thought it best myself that I should be sent away, for I felt very

[029-166] uncertain whether you would like to find me here.’

[029-167] ‘Have I always hated you so much?’

[029-168] ‘I hope not, for I have always loved you, and have always wished that you

[029-169] should know it. But you changed to me a little, shortly before you left

[029-170] home. Not that I wondered at it. You knew so much, and I knew so

[029-171] little, and it was so natural in many ways, going as you were among other

[029-172] friends, that I had nothing to complain of, and was not at all hurt.’

[029-173] Her colour rose as she said it modestly and hurriedly. Louisa understood

[029-174] the loving pretence, and her heart smote her.

[029-175] ‘May I try?’ said Sissy, emboldened to raise her hand to the neck that

[029-176] was insensibly drooping towards her.

[029-177] Louisa, taking down the hand that would have embraced her in another

[029-178] moment, held it in one of hers, and answered:

[029-179] ‘First, Sissy, do you know what I am? I am so proud and so hardened, so

[029-180] confused and troubled, so resentful and unjust to every one and to

[029-181] myself, that everything is stormy, dark, and wicked to me. Does not that

[029-182] repel you?’

[029-183] ‘No!’

[029-184] ‘I am so unhappy, and all that should have made me otherwise is so laid

[029-185] waste, that if I had been bereft of sense to this hour, and instead of

[029-186] being as learned as you think me, had to begin to acquire the simplest

[029-187] truths, I could not want a guide to peace, contentment, honour, all the

[029-188] good of which I am quite devoid, more abjectly than I do. Does not that

[029-189] repel you?’

[029-190] ‘No!’

[029-191] In the innocence of her brave affection, and the brimming up of her old

[029-192] devoted spirit, the once deserted girl shone like a beautiful light upon

[029-193] the darkness of the other.

[029-194] Louisa raised the hand that it might clasp her neck and join its fellow

[029-195] there. She fell upon her knees, and clinging to this stroller’s child

[029-196] looked up at her almost with veneration.

[029-197] ‘Forgive me, pity me, help me! Have compassion on my great need, and let

[029-198] me lay this head of mine upon a loving heart!’

[029-199] ‘O lay it here!’ cried Sissy. ‘Lay it here, my dear.’
