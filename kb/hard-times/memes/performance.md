# Performance

Performance, in *Hard Times*, is the novel's emblem of imaginative freedom. Sleary's circus — with its trained dogs, equestrian acts, and Shakespearean clown — is not merely entertainment; it is the embodied alternative to the Gradgrind philosophy. Where Facts are fixed, measurable, and uniform, performance is fluid, unpredictable, and irreducibly human. The circus occupies the literal margin of Coketown ("the neutral ground upon the outskirts of the town") just as the imaginative faculty it represents occupies the margin of the utilitarian worldview.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens presents the circus as a site of genuine community — eccentric, tolerant, affectionate — in deliberate contrast to the atomised and hostile relations of Coketown proper. The circus folk, from Mr. Sleary (whose lisp renders him simultaneously comic and strangely dignified) to the child-performer Josephine Sleary, are "a remarkable and most polite people" — a description that quietly rebukes the rudeness and cruelty of the town's respectable citizens.

The novel's plot hinges on performance. Sissy Jupe, the circus child, is the agent of the novel's redemption. It is she who shelters Louisa after the crisis; she who engineers Tom's escape (via the circus's travelling routes); she who, in the end, embodies the possibility of a life organised around something other than self-interest. Performance is not, in Dickens's rendering, a frivolity; it is a necessity — what makes life bearable, and what makes human beings capable of more than calculation.

### Key Passages

> "People mutht be amuthed, Thquire, thomehow. They can't be alwayth a working, nor yet they can't be alwayth a learning." (PN: 006-036–037)

Sleary's lisping apologia is the novel's most famous defence of popular entertainment — and by extension, of the imagination itself. The imperative "mutht be amuthed" grounds performance not in luxury but in human need. Sleary's argument is that a life without amusement is not a life at all.

> "The father of one of the families was in the habit of ... enlivening the varied performances with his chaste Shaksperean quips and retorts" (PN: 006-027)

The presence of Shakespeare — even in the debased form of a clown's "quips and retorts" — within the travelling circus is significant. Dickens positions the circus not as a degradation of culture but as its popular preservation, a vestige of communal art that predates and implicitly judges the Gradgrindian reduction of all value to utility.

## Historical Context

The Victorian circus was a relatively new popular form, evolving from Philip Astley's equestrian shows of the 1760s into the travelling tent-circuses that criss-crossed industrial England. Dickens knew the circus well — his *Sketches by Boz* and *The Old Curiosity Shop* both feature circus performers — and his treatment of Sleary's company reflects a broader Victorian debate about the moral status of popular entertainment. Evangelical reformers frequently condemned the circus as immoral; Dickens, by contrast, found in it a rough-and-ready decency that he preferred to much respectable philanthropy.

## Embodied By

- **[[sissy-jupe]]**: The circus child who becomes the novel's moral centre. Her upbringing among performers has given her what Gradgrind's school could not: practical wisdom, emotional intelligence, and an unshakeable capacity for loyalty and love.
- **[[sleary]]**: The lisping, asthmatic circus proprietor whose philosophy — "people mutht be amuthed" — is the novel's most compressed statement of its own values. Sleary is a comic figure, but Dickens grants him a dignity and moral authority that none of the novel's "respectable" characters possess.
- **[[thomas-gradgrind]]**: As the anti-performer, Gradgrind embodies the rejection of performance. His world has no room for "fancy," for pretending, for the provisional and the playful. His redemption, when it comes, is precisely a discovery of the need for something beyond Facts — a recognition, however belated, of what Sleary has known all along.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: Facts are the novel's anti-performance — fixed, inert, repeatable. Performance, by contrast, is alive, each instance unique. The novel does not simply prefer performance to Facts; it argues that a life of Facts alone is not a human life.
- **[[utilitarianism]]**: The utilitarian calculus has no category for performance, because performance generates no measurable output. Yet it is performance — the imaginative capacity cultivated by the circus — that proves, in the end, to have the greater practical value: it saves Louisa, rescues Tom, and preserves the human bonds that Coketown's industrial logic dissolves.
