# Josephine Sleary

A minor but consistent presence in Sleary's circus, Miss Josephine Sleary embodies the troupe's dedication to performance as a form of community and moral decency in *Hard Times*.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Josephine Sleary
- **Aliases / Epithets**: Miss Josephine Sleary
- **Role**: Minor character — a circus performer whose art is part of the novel's argument for the value of fancy
- **Occupation**: Equestrian performer
- **Family**: Sleary (father, the proprietor of Sleary's Circus)
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 3 — her name appears on the circus bill that Thomas Gradgrind discovers when he finds Louisa and Tom watching the circus (PN: 003-074)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 7 — she performs the Flower Act during the evening when Tom Gradgrind is hidden at the circus (PN: 035-150–035-152)

## Characterisation

Josephine Sleary is never given direct dialogue or psychological interiority; she exists as a figure of grace within the circus ring — her art is her meaning. Dickens presents her not as a developed individual but as a bearer of the circus's values: skill, dedication, and the capacity to delight.

### Performance Art

Her signature act is the "graceful equestrian Tyrolean Flower Act" (PN: 003-074–003-076), which a clown humorously renames the "Cauliflower Act" (PN: 035-151–035-152). The act's recurrence — it appears in Book I and again in Book III — suggests the continuity and reliability of the circus world in contrast to the instability of Coketown's industrial and domestic arrangements.

### Position Within the Circus Hierarchy

Josephine Sleary occupies a distinctive niche within Sleary's Circus. Unlike the clowns and comic riders who provide the broad humour of the ring, or her father Mr. Sleary who manages the enterprise from without, Josephine is presented as a creature of pure performance. Her act requires no words, no explanation, no utilitarian justification — it is, in microcosm, the defence of fancy that the novel as a whole mounts against the Gradgrind philosophy. The circus bill that first names her in Book I Chapter 3 lists her as an attraction alongside "Mr. Sleary's highly trained performing dog Merrylegs" (PN: 003-076), implicitly placing animal and human artistry on a continuum of non-rational delight.

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

When Thomas Gradgrind discovers Louisa and young Tom peeping through the loophole at the circus, the bill advertising the evening's entertainment lists Miss Josephine Sleary as inaugurating the performance "with her graceful equestrian Tyrolean flower-act" (PN: 003-074–003-076). She is a name on a poster — a promise of the forbidden pleasures that draw the Gradgrind children away from their father's regime of fact (PN: 003-073–003-087). The scene establishes the circus as the novel's counter-world to Stone Lodge, a place where curiosity and wonder are not merely permitted but celebrated.

### Book III: Garnering

Josephine Sleary reappears in the novel's penultimate chapter when Sissy Jupe and Louisa bring the fugitive Tom to the circus for shelter. While Tom waits in disguise, the performance continues: "Miss Josephine Sleary, in her celebrated graceful Equestrian Tyrolean Flower Act, was then announced" (PN: 035-150–035-152). Mr. Sleary leads her into the ring. The act proceeds while the novel's crisis — Bitzer's pursuit of Tom, the confrontation in the ring — unfolds, making the performance itself the backdrop against which the novel's moral resolution is staged (PN: 035-130–035-175).

The structural juxtaposition is deliberate: the highest tension of the plot (Tom's capture, Bitzer's ruthless logic) coexists with the most apparently frivolous of diversions (a girl riding a horse with flowers). Dickens's point is that the frivolity is not, in fact, frivolous — it represents a form of human connectedness and joy that the Bitzerian world of calculative self-interest cannot comprehend. Mr. Sleary's decision to help Tom escape is made while the show goes on, as though the ethic of the circus — mutual loyalty, performative generosity — provides the moral framework within which such a decision becomes possible.

## Thematic Significance

Josephine Sleary's place in the novel illustrates the circus as a moral community. She is one of the "remarkable and most polite people" (PN: 003-069) whose eccentric, affectionate world stands in deliberate contrast to the atomised relations of Coketown. The circus folk, from Mr. Sleary to the child-performer Josephine, represent what the Gradgrind system cannot produce: people who create value through imagination and mutual care rather than through the measurable, utilitarian calculus of the factory and the schoolroom.

### The Flower Act as Symbol

The "Tyrolean Flower Act" functions as a compressed allegory of the novel's central argument. A flower — beautiful, fragile, useless in utilitarian terms — is the object around which Josephine's art is organised. That the act is imported from the Tyrol (a region associated in the Victorian imagination with folk culture, pre-industrial simplicity, and Alpine romance) distances it further from Coketown's blackened brick and mechanical rhythms. When the clown renames it the "Cauliflower Act" (PN: 035-151), the joke works on two levels: it punctures the pretension of high art, but it also domesticates the exotic — a cauliflower, unlike a Tyrolean flower, can be eaten. The circus, Dickens suggests, feeds a hunger that the Gradgrind system cannot even name.

## Key Relationships

- **[[sleary]]**: Her father and the proprietor of the circus; he leads her into the ring for her performance (PN: 035-152). Their relationship is one of professional mutuality — he presents, she performs — and it models the circus's alternative to the patriarchal authority that structures both Gradgrind's and Bounderby's households.
- **[[sissy-jupe]]**: A fellow member of the circus troupe in childhood; Sissy's return to the circus in Book III brings Josephine back into the narrative. Though the two never interact directly on the page, they are parallel figures — both daughters of the circus who carry its values into the novel's moral resolution.
