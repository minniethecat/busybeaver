[022-001] FADING AWAY

[022-002] IT was falling dark when [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen]] came out of [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s house. The

[022-003] shadows of night had gathered so fast, that he did not look about him

[022-004] when he closed the door, but plodded straight along the street. Nothing

[022-005] was further from his thoughts than the curious old woman he had

[022-006] encountered on his previous visit to the same house, when he heard a step

[022-007] behind him that he knew, and turning, saw her in [[rachael|Rachael]]’s company.

[022-008] He saw Rachael first, as he had heard her only.

[022-009] ‘Ah, Rachael, my [[love|dear]]! Missus, thou wi’ her!’

[022-010] ‘Well, and now you are surprised to be sure, and with [[reason]] I must say,’

[022-011] the old woman returned. ‘Here I am again, you see.’

[022-012] ‘But how wi’ Rachael?’ said Stephen, falling into their step, walking

[022-013] between them, and looking from the one to the other.

[022-014] ‘Why, I come to be with this good lass pretty much as I came to be with

[022-015] you,’ said the old woman, cheerfully, taking the reply upon herself. ‘My

[022-016] visiting time is later this year than usual, for I have been rather

[022-017] troubled with shortness of breath, and so put it off till the weather was

[022-018] fine and warm. For the same reason I don’t make all my journey in one

[022-019] day, but divide it into two days, and get a bed to-night at the

[022-020] Travellers’ Coffee House down by the railroad (a nice clean house), and

[022-021] go back Parliamentary, at six in the morning. Well, but what has this to

[022-022] do with this good lass, says you? I’m going to tell you. I have heard

[022-023] of Mr. Bounderby being married. I read it in the paper, where it looked

[022-024] grand—oh, it looked fine!’ the old woman dwelt on it with strange

[022-025] enthusiasm: ‘and I want to see his wife. I have never seen her yet.

[022-026] Now, if you’ll believe me, she hasn’t come out of that house since noon

[022-027] to-day. So not to give her up too easily, I was waiting about, a little

[022-028] last bit more, when I passed close to this good lass two or three times;

[022-029] and her face being so friendly I spoke to her, and she spoke to me.

[022-030] There!’ said the old woman to Stephen, ‘you can make all the rest out for

[022-031] yourself now, a deal shorter than I can, I dare say!’

[022-032] Once again, Stephen had to conquer an instinctive propensity to dislike

[022-033] this old woman, though her manner was as honest and simple as a manner

[022-034] possibly could be. With a gentleness that was as [[nature|natural]] to him as he

[022-035] knew it to be to Rachael, he pursued the subject that interested her in

[022-036] her old age.

[022-037] ‘Well, missus,’ said he, ‘I ha seen the lady, and she were young and

[022-038] hansom. Wi’ fine dark thinkin eyes, and a still way, Rachael, as I ha

[022-039] never seen the like on.’

[022-040] ‘Young and handsome. Yes!’ cried the old woman, quite delighted. ‘As

[022-041] bonny as a rose! And what a happy wife!’

[022-042] ‘Aye, missus, I suppose she be,’ said Stephen. But with a doubtful

[022-043] glance at Rachael.

[022-044] ‘Suppose she be? She must be. She’s your master’s wife,’ returned the

[022-045] old woman.

[022-046] Stephen nodded assent. ‘Though as to master,’ said he, glancing again at

[022-047] Rachael, ‘not master onny more. That’s aw enden ’twixt him and me.’

[022-048] ‘Have you left his work, Stephen?’ asked Rachael, anxiously and quickly.

[022-049] ‘Why, Rachael,’ he replied, ‘whether I ha lef’n his work, or whether his

[022-050] work ha lef’n me, cooms t’ th’ same. His work and me are parted. ’Tis

[022-051] as weel so—better, I were thinkin when yo coom up wi’ me. It would ha

[022-052] brought’n trouble upon trouble if I had stayed theer. Haply ’tis a

[022-053] kindness to monny that I go; haply ’tis a kindness to myseln; anyways it

[022-054] mun be done. I mun turn my face fro [[coketown|Coketown]] fur th’ time, and seek a

[022-055] fort’n, dear, by beginnin fresh.’

[022-056] ‘Where will you go, Stephen?’

[022-057] ‘I donno t’night,’ said he, lifting off his hat, and smoothing his thin

[022-058] hair with the flat of his hand. ‘But I’m not goin t’night, Rachael, nor

[022-059] yet t’morrow. ’Tan’t easy overmuch t’ know wheer t’ turn, but a good

[022-060] [[heart]] will coom to me.’

[022-061] Herein, too, the sense of even thinking unselfishly aided him. Before he

[022-062] had so much as closed Mr. Bounderby’s door, he had reflected that at

[022-063] least his being obliged to go away was good for her, as it would save her

[022-064] from the chance of being brought into question for not withdrawing from

[022-065] him. Though it would cost him a hard pang to leave her, and though he

[022-066] could think of no similar place in which his condemnation would not

[022-067] pursue him, perhaps it was almost a relief to be forced away from the

[022-068] endurance of the last four days, even to unknown difficulties and

[022-069] distresses.

[022-070] So he said, with [[truth]], ‘I’m more leetsome, Rachael, under ’t, than I

[022-071] could’n ha believed.’ It was not her part to make his burden heavier.

[022-072] She answered with her comforting smile, and the three walked on together.

[022-073] Age, especially when it strives to be self-reliant and cheerful, finds

[022-074] much consideration among the poor. The old woman was so decent and

[022-075] contented, and made so light of her infirmities, though they had

[022-076] increased upon her since her former interview with Stephen, that they

[022-077] both took an interest in her. She was too sprightly to allow of their

[022-078] walking at a slow pace on her account, but she was very grateful to be

[022-079] talked to, and very willing to talk to any extent: so, when they came to

[022-080] their part of the town, she was more brisk and vivacious than ever.

[022-081] ‘Come to my poor place, missus,’ said Stephen, ‘and tak a coop o’ tea.

[022-082] Rachael will coom then; and arterwards I’ll see thee safe t’ thy

[022-083] Travellers’ lodgin. ’T may be long, Rachael, ere ever I ha th’ chance o’

[022-084] thy coompany agen.’

[022-085] They complied, and the three went on to the house where he lodged. When

[022-086] they turned into a narrow street, Stephen glanced at his window with a

[022-087] dread that always haunted his desolate home; but it was open, as he had

[022-088] left it, and no one was there. The evil spirit of his life had flitted

[022-089] away again, months ago, and he had heard no more of her since. The only

[022-090] evidence of her last return now, were the scantier moveables in his room,

[022-091] and the grayer hair upon his head.

[022-092] He lighted a candle, set out his little tea-board, got hot water from

[022-093] below, and brought in small portions of tea and sugar, a loaf, and some

[022-094] butter from the nearest shop. The bread was new and crusty, the butter

[022-095] fresh, and the sugar lump, of course—in fulfilment of the standard

[022-096] testimony of the Coketown magnates, that these people lived like princes,

[022-097] sir. Rachael made the tea (so large a party necessitated the borrowing

[022-098] of a cup), and the visitor enjoyed it mightily. It was the first glimpse

[022-099] of sociality the host had had for many days. He too, with the world a

[022-100] wide heath before him, enjoyed the meal—again in corroboration of the

[022-101] magnates, as exemplifying the utter want of calculation on the part of

[022-102] these people, sir.

[022-103] ‘I ha never thowt yet, missus,’ said Stephen, ‘o’ askin thy name.’

[022-104] The old lady announced herself as ‘[[mrs-pegler|Mrs. Pegler]].’

[022-105] ‘A widder, I think?’ said Stephen.

[022-106] ‘Oh, many long years!’ Mrs. Pegler’s husband (one of the best on record)

[022-107] was already dead, by Mrs. Pegler’s calculation, when Stephen was born.

[022-108] ‘’Twere a bad job, too, to lose so good a one,’ said Stephen. ‘Onny

[022-109] [[childhood|children]]?’

[022-110] Mrs. Pegler’s cup, rattling against her saucer as she held it, denoted

[022-111] some nervousness on her part. ‘No,’ she said. ‘Not now, not now.’

[022-112] ‘Dead, Stephen,’ Rachael softly hinted.

[022-113] ‘I’m sooary I ha spok’n on ’t,’ said Stephen, ‘I ought t’ hadn in my mind

[022-114] as I might touch a sore place. I—I blame myseln.’

[022-115] While he excused himself, the old lady’s cup rattled more and more. ‘I

[022-116] had a son,’ she said, curiously distressed, and not by any of the usual

[022-117] appearances of sorrow; ‘and he did well, wonderfully well. But he is not

[022-118] to be spoken of if you please. He is—’ Putting down her cup, she moved

[022-119] her hands as if she would have added, by her action, ‘dead!’ Then she

[022-120] said aloud, ‘I have lost him.’

[022-121] Stephen had not yet got the better of his having given the old lady pain,

[022-122] when his landlady came stumbling up the narrow stairs, and calling him to

[022-123] the door, whispered in his ear. Mrs. Pegler was by no means deaf, for

[022-124] she caught a word as it was uttered.

[022-125] ‘Bounderby!’ she cried, in a suppressed voice, starting up from the

[022-126] table. ‘Oh hide me! Don’t let me be seen for the world. Don’t let him

[022-127] come up till I’ve got away. Pray, pray!’ She trembled, and was

[022-128] excessively agitated; getting behind Rachael, when Rachael tried to

[022-129] reassure her; and not seeming to know what she was about.

[022-130] ‘But hearken, missus, hearken,’ said Stephen, astonished. ‘’Tisn’t Mr.

[022-131] Bounderby; ’tis his wife. Yo’r not fearfo’ o’ her. Yo was hey-go-mad

[022-132] about her, but an hour sin.’

[022-133] ‘But are you sure it’s the lady, and not the [[gentleman]]?’ she asked, still

[022-134] trembling.

[022-135] ‘Certain sure!’

[022-136] ‘Well then, pray don’t speak to me, nor yet take any notice of me,’ said

[022-137] the old woman. ‘Let me be quite to myself in this corner.’

[022-138] Stephen nodded; looking to Rachael for an explanation, which she was

[022-139] quite unable to give him; took the candle, went downstairs, and in a few

[022-140] moments returned, lighting [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] into the room. She was followed by the

[022-141] whelp.

[022-142] Rachael had risen, and stood apart with her shawl and bonnet in her hand,

[022-143] when Stephen, himself profoundly astonished by this visit, put the candle

[022-144] on the table. Then he too stood, with his doubled hand upon the table

[022-145] near it, waiting to be addressed.

[022-146] For the first time in her life Louisa had come into one of the dwellings

[022-147] of the Coketown Hands; for the first time in her life she was face to

[022-148] face with anything like individuality in connection with them. She knew

[022-149] of their existence by hundreds and by thousands. She knew what results

[022-150] in work a given number of them would produce in a given space of time.

[022-151] She knew them in crowds passing to and from their nests, like ants or

[022-152] beetles. But she knew from her reading infinitely more of the ways of

[022-153] toiling insects than of these toiling men and women.

[022-154] Something to be worked so much and paid so much, and there ended;

[022-155] something to be infallibly settled by laws of supply and demand;

[022-156] something that blundered against those laws, and floundered into

[022-157] difficulty; something that was a little pinched when wheat was dear, and

[022-158] over-ate itself when wheat was cheap; something that increased at such a

[022-159] rate of percentage, and yielded such another percentage of crime, and

[022-160] such another percentage of pauperism; something wholesale, of which vast

[022-161] fortunes were made; something that occasionally rose like a sea, and did

[022-162] some harm and waste (chiefly to itself), and fell again; this she knew

[022-163] the Coketown Hands to be. But, she had scarcely thought more of

[022-164] separating them into units, than of separating the sea itself into its

[022-165] component drops.

[022-166] She stood for some moments looking round the room. From the few chairs,

[022-167] the few books, the common prints, and the bed, she glanced to the two

[022-168] women, and to Stephen.

[022-169] ‘I have come to speak to you, in consequence of what passed just now. I

[022-170] should like to be serviceable to you, if you will let me. Is this your

[022-171] wife?’

[022-172] Rachael raised her eyes, and they sufficiently answered no, and dropped

[022-173] again.

[022-174] ‘I remember,’ said Louisa, reddening at her mistake; ‘I recollect, now,

[022-175] to have heard your domestic misfortunes spoken of, though I was not

[022-176] attending to the particulars at the time. It was not my meaning to ask a

[022-177] question that would give pain to any one here. If I should ask any other

[022-178] question that may happen to have that result, give me credit, if you

[022-179] please, for being in ignorance how to speak to you as I ought.’

[022-180] As Stephen had but a little while ago instinctively addressed himself to

[022-181] her, so she now instinctively addressed herself to Rachael. Her manner

[022-182] was short and abrupt, yet faltering and timid.

[022-183] ‘He has told you what has passed between himself and my husband? You

[022-184] would be his first resource, I think.’

[022-185] ‘I have heard the end of it, young lady,’ said Rachael.

[022-186] ‘Did I understand, that, being rejected by one employer, he would

[022-187] probably be rejected by all? I thought he said as much?’

[022-188] ‘The chances are very small, young lady—next to nothing—for a man who

[022-189] gets a bad name among them.’

[022-190] ‘What shall I understand that you mean by a bad name?’

[022-191] ‘The name of being troublesome.’

[022-192] ‘Then, by the prejudices of his own [[class]], and by the prejudices of the

[022-193] other, he is sacrificed alike? Are the two so deeply separated in this

[022-194] town, that there is no place whatever for an honest workman between

[022-195] them?’

[022-196] Rachael shook her head in silence.

[022-197] ‘He fell into suspicion,’ said Louisa, ‘with his fellow-weavers,

[022-198] because—he had made a promise not to be one of them. I think it must

[022-199] have been to you that he made that promise. Might I ask you why he made

[022-200] it?’

[022-201] Rachael burst into tears. ‘I didn’t seek it of him, poor lad. I prayed

[022-202] him to avoid trouble for his own good, little thinking he’d come to it

[022-203] through me. But I know he’d die a hundred deaths, ere ever he’d break

[022-204] his word. I know that of him well.’

[022-205] Stephen had remained quietly attentive, in his usual thoughtful attitude,

[022-206] with his hand at his chin. He now spoke in a voice rather less steady

[022-207] than usual.

[022-208] ‘No one, excepting myseln, can ever know what honour, an’ what love, an’

[022-209] respect, I bear to Rachael, or wi’ what cause. When I passed that

[022-210] promess, I towd her true, she were th’ Angel o’ my life. ’Twere a solemn

[022-211] promess. ’Tis gone fro’ me, for ever.’

[022-212] Louisa turned her head to him, and bent it with a deference that was new

[022-213] in her. She looked from him to Rachael, and her features softened.

[022-214] ‘What will you do?’ she asked him. And her voice had softened too.

[022-215] ‘Weel, ma’am,’ said Stephen, making the best of it, with a smile; ‘when I

[022-216] ha finished off, I mun quit this part, and try another. Fortnet or

[022-217] misfortnet, a man can but try; there’s nowt to be done wi’out tryin’—cept

[022-218] laying down and dying.’

[022-219] ‘How will you travel?’

[022-220] ‘Afoot, my kind ledy, afoot.’

[022-221] Louisa coloured, and a purse appeared in her hand. The rustling of a

[022-222] bank-note was audible, as she unfolded one and laid it on the table.

[022-223] ‘Rachael, will you tell him—for you know how, without offence—that this

[022-224] is freely his, to help him on his way? Will you entreat him to take it?’

[022-225] ‘I canna do that, young lady,’ she answered, turning her head aside.

[022-226] ‘Bless you for thinking o’ the poor lad wi’ such tenderness. But ’tis

[022-227] for him to know his heart, and what is right according to it.’

[022-228] Louisa looked, in part incredulous, in part frightened, in part overcome

[022-229] with quick sympathy, when this man of so much self-command, who had been

[022-230] so plain and steady through the late interview, lost his composure in a

[022-231] moment, and now stood with his hand before his face. She stretched out

[022-232] hers, as if she would have touched him; then checked herself, and

[022-233] remained still.

[022-234] ‘Not e’en Rachael,’ said Stephen, when he stood again with his face

[022-235] uncovered, ‘could mak sitch a kind offerin, by onny words, kinder. T’

[022-236] [[performance|show]] that I’m not a man wi’out reason and gratitude, I’ll tak two pound.

[022-237] I’ll borrow ’t for t’ pay ’t back. ’Twill be the sweetest work as ever I

[022-238] ha done, that puts it in my power t’ acknowledge once more my lastin

[022-239] thankfulness for this present action.’

[022-240] She was fain to take up the note again, and to substitute the much

[022-241] smaller sum he had named. He was neither courtly, nor handsome, nor

[022-242] picturesque, in any respect; and yet his manner of accepting it, and of

[022-243] expressing his thanks without more words, had a grace in it that Lord

[022-244] Chesterfield could not have taught his son in a century.

[022-245] [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]] had sat upon the bed, swinging one leg and sucking his walking-stick

[022-246] with sufficient unconcern, until the visit had attained this stage.

[022-247] Seeing his sister ready to depart, he got up, rather hurriedly, and put

[022-248] in a word.

[022-249] ‘Just wait a moment, Loo! Before we go, I should like to speak to him a

[022-250] moment. Something comes into my head. If you’ll step out on the stairs,

[022-251] Blackpool, I’ll mention it. Never mind a light, man!’ Tom was

[022-252] remarkably impatient of his moving towards the cupboard, to get one. ‘It

[022-253] don’t want a light.’

[022-254] Stephen followed him out, and Tom closed the room door, and held the lock

[022-255] in his hand.

[022-256] ‘I say!’ he whispered. ‘I think I can do you a good turn. Don’t ask me

[022-257] what it is, because it may not come to anything. But there’s no harm in

[022-258] my trying.’

[022-259] His breath fell like a flame of fire on Stephen’s ear, it was so hot.

[022-260] ‘That was our light porter at the Bank,’ said Tom, ‘who brought you the

[022-261] message to-night. I call him our light porter, because I belong to the

[022-262] Bank too.’

[022-263] Stephen thought, ‘What a hurry he is in!’ He spoke so confusedly.

[022-264] ‘Well!’ said Tom. ‘Now look here! When are you off?’

[022-265] ‘T’ day’s Monday,’ replied Stephen, considering. ‘Why, sir, Friday or

[022-266] Saturday, nigh ’bout.’

[022-267] ‘Friday or Saturday,’ said Tom. ‘Now look here! I am not sure that I

[022-268] can do you the good turn I want to do you—that’s my sister, you know, in

[022-269] your room—but I may be able to, and if I should not be able to, there’s

[022-270] no harm done. So I tell you what. You’ll know our light porter again?’

[022-271] ‘Yes, sure,’ said Stephen.

[022-272] ‘Very well,’ returned Tom. ‘When you leave work of a night, between this

[022-273] and your going away, just hang about the Bank an hour or so, will you?

[022-274] Don’t take on, as if you meant anything, if he should see you hanging

[022-275] about there; because I shan’t put him up to speak to you, unless I find I

[022-276] can do you the service I want to do you. In that case he’ll have a note

[022-277] or a message for you, but not else. Now look here! You are sure you

[022-278] understand.’

[022-279] He had wormed a finger, in the darkness, through a button-hole of

[022-280] Stephen’s coat, and was screwing that corner of the garment tight up

[022-281] round and round, in an extraordinary manner.

[022-282] ‘I understand, sir,’ said Stephen.

[022-283] ‘Now look here!’ repeated Tom. ‘Be sure you don’t make any mistake then,

[022-284] and don’t forget. I shall tell my sister as we go home, what I have in

[022-285] view, and she’ll approve, I know. Now look here! You’re all right, are

[022-286] you? You understand all about it? Very well then. Come along, Loo!’

[022-287] He pushed the door open as he called to her, but did not return into the

[022-288] room, or wait to be lighted down the narrow stairs. He was at the bottom

[022-289] when she began to descend, and was in the street before she could take

[022-290] his arm.

[022-291] Mrs. Pegler remained in her corner until the brother and sister were

[022-292] gone, and until Stephen came back with the candle in his hand. She was

[022-293] in a state of inexpressible admiration of Mrs. Bounderby, and, like an

[022-294] unaccountable old woman, wept, ‘because she was such a pretty dear.’ Yet

[022-295] Mrs. Pegler was so flurried lest the object of her admiration should

[022-296] return by chance, or anybody else should come, that her cheerfulness was

[022-297] ended for that night. It was late too, to people who rose early and

[022-298] worked hard; therefore the party broke up; and Stephen and Rachael

[022-299] escorted their mysterious acquaintance to the door of the Travellers’

[022-300] Coffee House, where they parted from her.

[022-301] They walked back together to the corner of the street where Rachael

[022-302] lived, and as they drew nearer and nearer to it, silence crept upon them.

[022-303] When they came to the dark corner where their unfrequent meetings always

[022-304] ended, they stopped, still silent, as if both were afraid to speak.

[022-305] ‘I shall strive t’ see thee agen, Rachael, afore I go, but if not—’

[022-306] ‘Thou wilt not, Stephen, I know. ’Tis better that we make up our minds

[022-307] to be open wi’ one another.’

[022-308] ‘Thou’rt awlus right. ’Tis bolder and better. I ha been thinkin then,

[022-309] Rachael, that as ’tis but a day or two that remains, ’twere better for

[022-310] thee, my dear, not t’ be seen wi’ me. ’T might bring thee into trouble,

[022-311] fur no good.’

[022-312] ‘’Tis not for that, Stephen, that I mind. But thou know’st our old

[022-313] agreement. ’Tis for that.’

[022-314] ‘Well, well,’ said he. ‘’Tis better, onnyways.’

[022-315] ‘Thou’lt write to me, and tell me all that happens, Stephen?’

[022-316] ‘Yes. What can I say now, but Heaven be wi’ thee, Heaven bless thee,

[022-317] Heaven thank thee and reward thee!’

[022-318] ‘May it bless thee, Stephen, too, in all thy wanderings, and send thee

[022-319] peace and rest at last!’

[022-320] ‘I towd thee, my dear,’ said Stephen Blackpool—‘that night—that I would

[022-321] never see or think o’ onnything that angered me, but thou, so much better

[022-322] than me, should’st be beside it. Thou’rt beside it now. Thou mak’st me

[022-323] see it wi’ a better eye. Bless thee. Good night. Good-bye!’

[022-324] It was but a hurried parting in a common street, yet it was a sacred

[022-325] remembrance to these two common people. Utilitarian economists,

[022-326] skeletons of schoolmasters, Commissioners of Fact, genteel and used-up

[022-327] infidels, gabblers of many little dog’s-eared creeds, the poor you will

[022-328] have always with you. Cultivate in them, while there is yet time, the

[022-329] utmost graces of the [[fancy|fancies]] and affections, to adorn their lives so much

[022-330] in need of ornament; or, in the day of your triumph, when romance is

[022-331] utterly driven out of their souls, and they and a bare existence stand

[022-332] face to face, Reality will take a wolfish turn, and make an end of you.

[022-333] Stephen worked the next day, and the next, uncheered by a word from any

[022-334] one, and shunned in all his comings and goings as before. At the end of

[022-335] the second day, he saw land; at the end of the third, his loom stood

[022-336] empty.

[022-337] He had overstayed his hour in the street outside the Bank, on each of the

[022-338] two first evenings; and nothing had happened there, good or bad. That he

[022-339] might not be remiss in his part of the engagement, he resolved to wait

[022-340] full two hours, on this third and last night.

[022-341] There was the lady who had once kept Mr. Bounderby’s house, sitting at

[022-342] the first-floor window as he had seen her before; and there was the light

[022-343] porter, sometimes talking with her there, and sometimes looking over the

[022-344] blind below which had BANK upon it, and sometimes coming to the door and

[022-345] standing on the steps for a breath of air. When he first came out,

[022-346] Stephen thought he might be looking for him, and passed near; but the

[022-347] light porter only cast his winking eyes upon him slightly, and said

[022-348] nothing.

[022-349] Two hours were a long stretch of lounging about, after a long day’s

[022-350] labour. Stephen sat upon the step of a door, leaned against a wall under

[022-351] an archway, strolled up and down, listened for the church clock, stopped

[022-352] and watched children playing in the street. Some purpose or other is so

[022-372] It was barely daybreak, when, with a parting look round his room,

[022-374] The town was as entirely deserted as if the inhabitants had abandoned it,
