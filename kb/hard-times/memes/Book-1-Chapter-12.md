[012-001] THE OLD WOMAN

[012-002] OLD STEPHEN descended the two white steps, shutting the black door with

[012-003] the brazen door-plate, by the aid of the brazen full-stop, to which he

[012-004] gave a parting polish with the sleeve of his coat, observing that his hot

[012-005] hand clouded it. He crossed the street with his eyes bent upon the

[012-006] ground, and thus was walking sorrowfully away, when he felt a touch upon

[012-007] his arm.

[012-008] It was not the touch he needed most at such a moment—the touch that could

[012-009] calm the wild waters of his soul, as the uplifted hand of the sublimest

[012-010] [[love]] and patience could abate the raging of the sea—yet it was a woman’s

[012-011] hand too. It was an old woman, tall and shapely still, though withered

[012-012] by time, on whom his eyes fell when he stopped and turned. She was very

[012-013] cleanly and plainly dressed, had country mud upon her shoes, and was

[012-014] newly come from a journey. The flutter of her manner, in the unwonted

[012-015] noise of the streets; the spare shawl, carried unfolded on her arm; the

[012-016] heavy umbrella, and little basket; the loose long-fingered gloves, to

[012-017] which her hands were unused; all bespoke an old woman from the country,

[012-018] in her plain holiday clothes, come into [[coketown|Coketown]] on an expedition of rare

[012-019] occurrence. Remarking this at a glance, with the quick observation of

[012-020] his [[class]], [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]] bent his attentive face—his face, which,

[012-021] like the faces of many of his order, by dint of long working with eyes

[012-022] and hands in the midst of a prodigious noise, had acquired the

[012-023] concentrated look with which we are familiar in the countenances of the

[012-024] deaf—the better to hear what she asked him.

[012-025] ‘Pray, sir,’ said the old woman, ‘didn’t I see you come out of that

[012-026] [[gentleman]]’s house?’ pointing back to [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s. ‘I believe it was

[012-027] you, unless I have had the bad luck to mistake the person in following?’

[012-028] ‘Yes, missus,’ returned Stephen, ‘it were me.’

[012-029] ‘Have you—you’ll excuse an old woman’s curiosity—have you seen the

[012-030] gentleman?’

[012-031] ‘Yes, missus.’

[012-032] ‘And how did he look, sir? Was he portly, bold, outspoken, and hearty?’

[012-033] As she straightened her own figure, and held up her [[reason|head]] in adapting her

[012-034] action to her words, the idea crossed Stephen that he had seen this old

[012-035] woman before, and had not quite liked her.

[012-036] ‘O yes,’ he returned, observing her more attentively, ‘he were all that.’

[012-037] ‘And healthy,’ said the old woman, ‘as the fresh wind?’

[012-038] ‘Yes,’ returned Stephen. ‘He were ett’n and drinking—as large and as

[012-039] loud as a Hummobee.’

[012-040] ‘Thank you!’ said the old woman, with infinite content. ‘Thank you!’

[012-041] He certainly never had seen this old woman before. Yet there was a vague

[012-042] remembrance in his mind, as if he had more than once dreamed of some old

[012-043] woman like her.

[012-044] She walked along at his side, and, gently accommodating himself to her

[012-045] humour, he said Coketown was a busy place, was it not? To which she

[012-046] answered ‘Eigh sure! Dreadful busy!’ Then he said, she came from the

[012-047] country, he saw? To which she answered in the affirmative.

[012-048] ‘By Parliamentary, this morning. I came forty mile by Parliamentary this

[012-049] morning, and I’m going back the same forty mile this afternoon. I walked

[012-050] nine mile to the station this morning, and if I find nobody on the road

[012-051] to give me a lift, I shall walk the nine mile back to-night. That’s

[012-052] pretty well, sir, at my age!’ said the chatty old woman, her eye

[012-053] brightening with exultation.

[012-054] ‘’Deed ’tis. Don’t do’t too often, missus.’

[012-055] ‘No, no. Once a year,’ she answered, shaking her head. ‘I spend my

[012-056] savings so, once every year. I come regular, to tramp about the streets,

[012-057] and see the gentlemen.’

[012-058] ‘Only to see ’em?’ returned Stephen.

[012-059] ‘That’s enough for me,’ she replied, with great earnestness and interest

[012-060] of manner. ‘I ask no more! I have been standing about, on this side of

[012-061] the way, to see that gentleman,’ turning her head back towards Mr.

[012-062] Bounderby’s again, ‘come out. But, he’s late this year, and I have not

[012-063] seen him. You came out instead. Now, if I am obliged to go back without

[012-064] a glimpse of him—I only want a glimpse—well! I have seen you, and you

[012-065] have seen him, and I must make that do.’ Saying this, she looked at

[012-066] Stephen as if to fix his features in her mind, and her eye was not so

[012-067] bright as it had been.

[012-068] With a large allowance for difference of tastes, and with all submission

[012-069] to the patricians of Coketown, this seemed so extraordinary a source of

[012-070] interest to take so much trouble about, that it perplexed him. But they

[012-071] were passing the church now, and as his eye caught the clock, he

[012-072] quickened his pace.

[012-073] He was going to his work? the old woman said, quickening hers, too, quite

[012-074] easily. Yes, time was nearly out. On his telling her where he worked,

[012-075] the old woman became a more singular old woman than before.

[012-076] ‘An’t you happy?’ she asked him.

[012-077] ‘Why—there’s awmost nobbody but has their troubles, missus.’ He answered

[012-078] evasively, because the old woman appeared to take it for granted that he

[012-079] would be very happy indeed, and he had not the [[heart]] to disappoint her.

[012-080] He knew that there was trouble enough in the world; and if the old woman

[012-081] had lived so long, and could count upon his having so little, why so much

[012-082] the better for her, and none the worse for him.

[012-083] ‘Ay, ay! You have your troubles at home, you mean?’ she said.

[012-084] ‘Times. Just now and then,’ he answered, slightly.

[012-085] ‘But, working under such a gentleman, they don’t follow you to the

[012-086] Factory?’

[012-087] No, no; they didn’t follow him there, said Stephen. All correct there.

[012-088] Everything accordant there. (He did not go so far as to say, for her

[012-089] pleasure, that there was a sort of Divine Right there; but, I have heard

[012-090] claims almost as magnificent of late years.)

[012-091] They were now in the black by-road near the place, and the Hands were

[012-092] crowding in. The bell was ringing, and the Serpent was a Serpent of many

[012-093] coils, and the Elephant was getting ready. The strange old woman was

[012-094] delighted with the very bell. It was the beautifullest bell she had ever

[012-095] heard, she said, and sounded grand!

[012-096] She asked him, when he stopped good-naturedly to shake hands with her

[012-097] before going in, how long he had worked there?

[012-098] ‘A dozen year,’ he told her.

[012-099] ‘I must kiss the hand,’ said she, ‘that has worked in this fine factory

[012-100] for a dozen year!’ And she lifted it, though he would have prevented

[012-101] her, and put it to her lips. What harmony, besides her age and her

[012-102] simplicity, surrounded her, he did not know, but even in this fantastic

[012-103] action there was a something neither out of time nor place: a something

[012-104] which it seemed as if nobody else could have made as serious, or done

[012-105] with such a [[nature|natural]] and touching air.

[012-106] He had been at his loom full half an hour, thinking about this old woman,

[012-107] when, having occasion to move round the loom for its adjustment, he

[012-108] glanced through a window which was in his corner, and saw her still

[012-109] looking up at the pile of building, lost in admiration. Heedless of the

[012-110] smoke and mud and wet, and of her two long journeys, she was gazing at

[012-111] it, as if the heavy thrum that issued from its many stories were [[pride|proud]]

[012-112] music to her.

[012-113] She was gone by and by, and the day went after her, and the lights sprung

[012-114] up again, and the Express whirled in full sight of the Fairy Palace over

[012-115] the arches near: little felt amid the jarring of the machinery, and

[012-116] scarcely heard above its crash and rattle. Long before then his thoughts

[012-117] had gone back to the dreary room above the little shop, and to the

[012-118] [[shame|shameful]] figure heavy on the bed, but heavier on his heart.

[012-119] [[machinery|Machinery]] slackened; throbbing feebly like a fainting pulse; stopped.

[012-120] The bell again; the glare of light and heat dispelled; the factories,

[012-121] looming heavy in the black wet night—their tall chimneys rising up into

[012-122] the air like competing Towers of Babel.

[012-123] He had spoken to [[rachael|Rachael]] only last night, it was true, and had walked

[012-124] with her a little way; but he had his new misfortune on him, in which no

[012-125] one else could give him a moment’s relief, and, for the sake of it, and

[012-126] because he knew himself to want that softening of his anger which no

[012-127] voice but hers could effect, he felt he might so far disregard what she

[012-128] had said as to wait for her again. He waited, but she had eluded him.

[012-129] She was gone. On no other night in the year could he so ill have spared

[012-130] her patient face.

[012-131] O! Better to have no home in which to lay his head, than to have a home

[012-132] and dread to go to it, through such a cause. He ate and drank, for he

[012-133] was exhausted—but he little knew or cared what; and he wandered about in

[012-134] the chill rain, thinking and thinking, and brooding and brooding.

[012-135] No word of a new [[marriage]] had ever passed between them; but Rachael had

[012-136] taken great pity on him years ago, and to her alone he had opened his

[012-137] closed heart all this time, on the subject of his miseries; and he knew

[012-138] very well that if he were free to ask her, she would take him. He

[012-139] thought of the home he might at that moment have been seeking with

[012-140] pleasure and pride; of the different man he might have been that night;

[012-141] of the lightness then in his now heavy-laden breast; of the then restored

[012-142] honour, self-respect, and tranquillity all torn to pieces. He thought of

[012-143] the waste of the best part of his life, of the change it made in his

[012-144] character for the worse every day, of the dreadful nature of his

[012-145] existence, bound hand and foot, to a dead woman, and tormented by a demon

[012-146] in her shape. He thought of Rachael, how young when they were first

[012-147] brought together in these circumstances, how mature now, how soon to grow

[012-148] old. He thought of the number of girls and women she had seen marry, how

[012-149] many homes with [[childhood|children]] in them she had seen grow up around her, how she

[012-150] had contentedly pursued her own lone quiet path—for him—and how he had

[012-151] sometimes seen a shade of melancholy on her blessed face, that smote him

[012-152] with remorse and despair. He set the picture of her up, beside the

[012-153] infamous image of last night; and thought, Could it be, that the whole

[012-154] earthly course of one so gentle, good, and self-denying, was subjugate to

[012-155] such a wretch as that!

[012-156] Filled with these thoughts—so filled that he had an unwholesome sense of

[012-157] growing larger, of being placed in some new and diseased relation towards

[012-158] the objects among which he passed, of seeing the iris round every misty

[012-159] light turn red—he went home for shelter.
