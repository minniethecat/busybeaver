# Coketown

The fictional industrial city that is the novel's central [[Coketown|setting]] and its master metaphor — a place where the philosophy of [[facts|Facts]] has been built into brick, smoke, and pavement, and where [[Thomas-Gradgrind]] and [[Josiah-Bounderby]] rule as its intellectual and industrial masters, and where every street and chimney declares the Utilitarian creed.

## Geography

- **Type**: Town (industrial manufacturing centre)
- **Location**: Northern England — the manufacturing districts of the mid-nineteenth century. Dickens locates Coketown within a landscape of coal, canals, and railways, the infrastructure of the Industrial Revolution.
- **Real-world Model**: Based primarily on **Preston, Lancashire**, which Dickens visited in January 1854 during the bitterly fought cotton-workers' lockout. His article for *Household Words* ("On Strike," 11 February 1854) records impressions that fed directly into the novel. Elements of **Manchester** — the quintessential industrial city of the era — also contributed to the composite portrait.
- **First Mention**: Book I Chapter 5, "The Keynote." Bounderby and Gradgrind "now walked" to Coketown, and the narrator halts the action to deliver the novel's most sustained piece of topographical description (PN: 005-002–005-046). The chapter title is self-referential: to "strike the key-note" is to sound the pitch on which the entire novel will be built, and Dickens names Coketown as that note.

## Description

The description of Coketown in "The Keynote" (PN: 005-006–005-046) is the novel's most concentrated passage of symbolic description — a tour de force that transforms a city into an argument. Every detail is double: it describes a place and it indicts a philosophy.

> "It was a town of red brick, or of brick that would have been red if the smoke and ashes had allowed it; but as matters stood, it was a town of unnatural red and black like the painted face of a savage." (PN: 005-006–005-008)

The conditional "would have been red" performs the town's defining rhetorical move: Coketown is what its industry has made it, and the original — the natural, the red — is visible only as a ghost beneath the black. The simile "like the painted face of a savage" is a deliberate provocation: the industrial city, proudest achievement of civilisation, is rendered in the language of the primitive and the grotesque.

> "It was a town of machinery and tall chimneys, out of which interminable serpents of smoke trailed themselves for ever and ever, and never got uncoiled." (PN: 005-009–005-010)

The "interminable serpents" that "never got uncoiled" are the novel's emblem of industrial permanence. Smoke is not an incidental by-product but the town's signature, its breath, its meaning. The serpents are "interminable" — without end — because the industrial process is cyclical and consuming: the mills never stop, the chimneys never cease, and the workers who serve them are caught in the same uncoiling coil.

> "It had a black canal in it, and a river that ran purple with ill-smelling dye." (PN: 005-011–005-012)

The black canal and purple river are the circulatory system of industrial capital. Water — the element of life — has been converted into a medium of waste. The canal is "black" with coal-dust; the river is "purple" with textile dye, the colour of commerce that is also the colour of a bruise. Dickens's colour palette throughout the passage — red, black, purple — is drawn from the industrial process itself: brick, coal, dye, all muted into the "unnatural" tones of a manufactured world.

> "Fact, fact, fact, everywhere in the material aspect of the town; fact, fact, fact, everywhere in the immaterial." (PN: 005-039–005-041)

This is the passage's thesis sentence. The tripled "fact" — incantatory, liturgical — mimics the Gradgrindian creed while exposing its reach. The chiasmus ("material aspect of the town" / "immaterial") insists that Fact is not merely the architecture but the atmosphere, the ideology, the air the Coketowners breathe. Dickens then enumerates: the school, the school of design, the relations between master and man — "everything was fact between the lying-in hospital and the cemetery." From birth to death, Coketown is governed by the measurable and the exchangeable.

### Key Physical Features

- **The red-brick uniformity**: Every street is like every other street; every building is a "pious warehouse" — even the churches (PN: 005-028–005-034). The eighteen chapels belonging to "eighteen religious persuasions" are indistinguishable in their utilitarian design, a satire of denominational competition rendered absurd by architectural sameness.
- **The tall chimneys and "interminable serpents of smoke"**: The chimneys are Coketown's defining vertical feature, and their smoke is the town's permanent atmosphere (PN: 005-009–005-010). In Book II, the town is "shrouded in a haze of its own" even on a sunny day (PN: 017-004–017-005).
- **The black canal and purple river**: The waterways of Coketown have been poisoned by industrial waste — the canal black with coal, the river purple with dye (PN: 005-011–005-012). They are the novel's image of environmental degradation as moral degradation.
- **The steam-engine piston**: Working "monotonously up and down, like the head of an elephant in a state of melancholy madness" (PN: 005-013–005-015) — machinery has acquired a kind of deranged consciousness, and the elephant image suggests both power and captivity.
- **The public houses**: The workers' only relief from the monotony — "low haunts" (PN: 005-070–005-072) that the Temperance societies deplore and the Gradgrind-and-Bounderby calculus cannot comprehend. The public house is Coketown's admission that Fancy will find an outlet, even if only in the destructive form of drink. The Pegasus's Arms, where Sissy Jupe's father performs, stands as the novel's emblem of the working-class public house — "haggard and as shabby, as if, for want of custom, it had itself taken to drinking" (PN: 005-178–005-181).

## Symbolic Function

Coketown is Utilitarianism made visible — the physical manifestation of a philosophy that reduces everything to calculation. Its architecture, its pollution, its monotony are not accidents of industrialisation but the necessary outcomes of a system that cannot justify ornament, variety, or colour.

The city's symbolic function is tripartite. First, it is the **material embodiment of Fact** — "Fact, fact, fact, everywhere in the material aspect of the town" (PN: 005-039–005-040). The buildings are functional because they cannot be anything else; the streets are uniform because difference is waste; the churches are warehouses because religion, like everything else, has been reduced to a measurable proposition.

Second, it is the **structural cause of its inhabitants' suffering**. The town does not merely house misery; it produces it. The piston that "worked monotonously up and down, like the head of an elephant in a state of melancholy madness" (PN: 005-013–005-015) is a portrait not just of a machine but of the workers who serve it. Stephen Blackpool's question — "Are the two so deeply separated in this town, that there is no place whatever for an honest workman between them?" (PN: 022-193–022-194) — names Coketown as a geography of exclusion, a city that has no space for the individual conscience because it is organised entirely around two warring interests.

Third, Coketown functions as the **negative against which the novel's alternative values are defined**. Sleary's Circus — mobile, coloured, ridiculous, kind — is the anti-Coketown. Where the town is fixed, the circus travels. Where the town is monochrome, the circus is painted. Where the town is work, the circus is play. Where the town is fact, the circus is Fancy. The novel's structural arc is the competition between these two spaces for the souls of its characters — and the circus wins. Gradgrind's conversion, Sissy's survival, Tom's escape, and Sleary's final wisdom all occur outside or in flight from Coketown.

The haze that shrouds Coketown in Book II (PN: 017-004–017-011) extends the symbolic register: the town is not merely ugly but illegible, a "dense formless jumble" that "was suggestive of itself, though not a brick of it could be seen." The industrial city has become its own representation — a "blotch" on the landscape that substitutes for the natural world it has consumed.

## Key Events

- **The Keynote (Book I Chapter 5)**: The narrator's sustained portrait of Coketown — the novel's most famous passage of place description — establishes the city as both setting and argument. (PN: 005-002–005-046)
- **Stephen Blackpool's ostracism (Book II Chapter 4–6)**: The union meeting at which Stephen is shunned (PN: 020-203) and his subsequent dismissal by Bounderby crystallise Coketown's structural logic: the city awards no place between capital and labour to the man of conscience.
- **Louisa Bounderby's flight (Book II Chapter 12)**: Louisa returns to her father's house through the rain, leaving Bounderby's Coketown mansion, and the city's division between the industrial centre and the suburban residences of the wealthy is made visible — the rich can leave Coketown; the poor cannot. (PN: 028-001–028-030)
- **The Old Hell Shaft (Book III Chapter 6)**: Stephen Blackpool's death in the abandoned mine pit is Coketown's darkest revelation — the hole the industrial order has left in the earth, unmarked and unguarded. (PN: 034-253–034-298)
- **Bounderby's death "in the Coketown street" (Book III Chapter 9)**: The novel's closing pages report that Bounderby died "of a fit in the Coketown street" (PN: 037-107) — the man who claimed Coketown as his domain is finally absorbed by it, dying in the public space he ruled but never inhabited.

## Associated Characters

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]** — Coketown's leading manufacturer and banker, who styles himself "Josiah Bounderby of Coketown." He is the city's ruling spirit, and his exposure as a fraud implicates the entire industrial order he represents.
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]** — The retired hardware merchant whose educational system provides Coketown with its intellectual justification. Gradgrind is Coketown's philosopher; Bounderby is its practitioner.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]** — The power-loom weaver whose life and death expose Coketown's structural cruelty. He is the city's moral centre precisely because he refuses its terms — neither master nor union man, he stands for a humanity Coketown cannot accommodate.
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]** — The product of Coketown's Fact-education, whose emotional starvation is the city's intimate harvest. Her question to her father — "your philosophy and your teaching will not save me" — is Coketown's indictment from within.
- **[[Sleary]]** — The circus proprietor whose travelling show is Coketown's antithesis. Where Coketown is fixed, Sleary moves; where Coketown is Fact, Sleary is Fancy; where Coketown is work, Sleary is play. His final wisdom — "people mutht be amuthed" — is the novel's verdict on a city that has forgotten how.
- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]** — The circus-child who enters Coketown as an outsider and becomes the agent of its moral correction. She is the proof that the qualities Coketown suppresses — imagination, sympathy, intuitive wisdom — are the qualities that heal.

## In the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Coketown makes its full debut in Chapter 5, "The Keynote," where the narrator halts the forward motion of the plot to deliver a sustained portrait. The passage is deliberately positioned: we have met Gradgrind (Chapter 1), seen his school (Chapter 2), encountered the circus as counter-world (Chapter 3), and been introduced to Bounderby (Chapter 4). Now Dickens shows us the world that produced them all — and the world they are reproducing. (PN: 005-002–005-046)

The remainder of Book I populates this setting. Stephen Blackpool is introduced as a man shaped by Coketown's labour — "forty years of age" but looking older, "he had had a hard life" (PN: 010-019–010-020). The Pegasus's Arms in Pod's End, the shabby public house that serves as the circus's Coketown base, provides the novel's most vivid image of the working-class public house: "As haggard and as shabby, as if, for want of custom, it had itself taken to drinking" (PN: 005-178–005-181).

### Book II: Reaping

Book II opens with another sustained description of Coketown, this time in summer — but even on a "sunny midsummer day," the town is "shrouded in a haze of its own" (PN: 017-004). The narrator's irony sharpens: the mill-owners are "such fragile china-ware" that any regulation threatens to shatter them (PN: 017-016–017-018). The Coketowners' favourite fiction — that they would "sooner pitch his property into the Atlantic" — is a threat of capital strike that Dickens treats with withering sarcasm (PN: 017-025–017-034).

The novel's labour plot unfolds entirely within Coketown's streets and mills. Slackbridge addresses the union with the rhetoric of class solidarity that Coketown's divisions have made inevitable (PN: 020-050–020-091). Stephen Blackpool is ostracised — "Thus easily did Stephen Blackpool fall into the loneliest of lives, the life of solitude among a familiar crowd" (PN: 020-203). The town's geography becomes a map of exclusion: Stephen cannot stay, and the world beyond Coketown offers no destination.

### Book III: Garnering

Coketown in Book III is a place of reckoning. Bounderby's exposure before the assembled company (Book III Chapter 5) strips Coketown's ruling myth of its legitimacy. Stephen Blackpool's death in the Old Hell Shaft — the abandoned mine pit outside the town — literalises Coketown's hidden costs: the earth itself has been hollowed out, and the hole is unmarked (PN: 034-253–034-298).

The novel's final chapter returns Coketown to its symbolic function. Bounderby dies "in the Coketown street" (PN: 037-107); his will begins "its long career of quibble, plunder, false pretences, vile example, little service and much law" (PN: 037-108–037-109). Rachael is seen among the Coketown Hands, "a woman of pensive beauty" (PN: 037-135–037-137). The town endures, and the novel's final pages are an elegy for the human possibilities Coketown has foreclosed — and a tribute to those (Sissy, Rachael, the circus) who have survived its logic.
