# Mrs. Pegler

Mrs. Pegler is the revelation character of *Hard Times* — Josiah Bounderby's mother, whose one-scene appearance in Book III annihilates her son's entire public identity. She is the novel's most devastating truth-teller, and she tells the truth not to expose him but simply because she is asked.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mrs. Pegler (her Christian name is never given)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "the mysterious old woman" (the narrator's designation before she is identified); "a little, worn-out, old woman" (PN: 022-110)
- **Role**: Revelation character — the mother whose testimony exposes Bounderby's central lie
- **Occupation**: A poor country woman; formerly kept "but a little village shop" (PN: 033-208)
- **Family**: Josiah Bounderby (son); a deceased husband whom the narrator calls "one of the best on record" (PN: 022-106)
- **First Appearance**: Book II Chapter 6 — encountered at Stephen Blackpool's lodging as a mysterious old woman who is "curiously distressed" when asked about her children; she announces her name as "Mrs. Pegler" (PN: 022-104)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 5 — after the revelation scene, Rachael and Sissy leave Mrs. Pegler "to occupy a bed at her son's for that night" (PN: 033-253)

## Characterisation

Dickens introduces Mrs. Pegler indirectly, as a figure of mystery who appears periodically in Coketown — first mentioned by the town gossips as an old woman who visits once a year, stares at Bounderby's house, and vanishes. When she finally speaks, her voice is the novel's quietest and most annihilating. She speaks plain, humble English, without dialect or artifice, and her every sentence contradicts Bounderby's bombast. Her defining quality is not assertion but simple truthfulness under duress — she tells the truth not because she wishes to expose anyone but because she cannot tell a lie. (PN: 033-182–033-217)

### Physical Description

Mrs. Pegler is first rendered through small, worn details: she is "a little, worn-out, old woman" (PN: 022-110), her hands are "shaking" and her cup rattles against the saucer as she holds it (PN: 022-110). When she later appears at Bounderby's house, she is described in "country garb" (PN: 033-122), muffling herself up and "shrinking from observation" (PN: 033-124). Dickens gives her no metallic imagery, no inflation, no grotesquerie — she is simply an old woman, physically unremarkable, and the plainness of her description is itself a moral argument: truth wears country clothes.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Mrs. Pegler's speech is the antithesis of her son's. Where Bounderby proclaims, she whispers. Where he inflates, she shrinks. Where he claims the gutter, she produces schoolbooks. Her characteristic gesture is nervousness — a rattling cup (PN: 022-110), a trembling hand — that gives way to "indignant pride" when Gradgrind accuses her of deserting her child (PN: 033-200). Her speech is plain, unadorned, and devastatingly precise. Her key utterance — "I can keep my pride in my Josiah to myself, and I can love for love's own sake!" (PN: 033-215) — compresses her entire moral position into a single sentence: she has loved without recognition, without return, and without complaint, and she considers this arrangement "right."

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Mrs. Pegler does not appear in Book I, but her absence is structural. Book I establishes Bounderby's self-made-man narrative — the claim that he was abandoned in a ditch by a mother who "ran away from me" (PN: 004-056) and raised by a drunken grandmother who "sold her bed to buy liquor" (PN: 004-058). The reader who does not yet know Mrs. Pegler hears only Bounderby's version of his origins. The novel plants the lie in the first act and lets it grow unchallenged through two books.

### Book II: Reaping

Mrs. Pegler makes her first appearance in Book II Chapter 6, at Stephen Blackpool's lodging, though she is not yet identified as Bounderby's mother. She is simply "a little, worn-out, old woman" sharing tea. When Stephen asks after her children, her cup rattles and she grows "curiously distressed, and not by any of the usual appearances of sorrow" (PN: 022-116). The distress is not grief but the strain of a secret: "I had a son," she says, "and he did well, wonderfully well. But he is not to be spoken of if you please" (PN: 022-116–022-118). When Bounderby's name is spoken outside the door, she panics: "Oh hide me! Don't let me be seen for the world" (PN: 022-126). The scene establishes her as the town's "mysterious acquaintance" (PN: 022-299), the old woman who visits Coketown periodically to watch her son from a distance.

When Louisa visits the lodging, Mrs. Pegler hides in a corner and is afterwards "in a state of inexpressible admiration of Mrs. Bounderby" (PN: 022-293), weeping "because she was such a pretty dear" (PN: 022-294). This detail — the mother weeping at the sight of her unknown daughter-in-law — is one of the novel's most heartbreaking: she loves not only her son but everyone connected to him, from a distance she has promised never to cross.

### Book III: Garnering

Book III delivers Mrs. Pegler's single great scene — and with it the novel's most devastating act of truth-telling. Mrs. Sparsit, believing she has tracked down a conspirator in the bank robbery — the mysterious old woman Rachael had mentioned — drags Mrs. Pegler into Bounderby's dining room before an assembled crowd (PN: 033-122–033-146). Bounderby sees his mother and turns purple. He blusters: "Why don't you mind your own business, ma'am?" (PN: 033-151). Mrs. Pegler, trembling, addresses him: "My dear Josiah! My darling boy! I am not to blame" (PN: 033-157).

Gradgrind steps in to accuse her of "unnatural and inhuman treatment" of her son (PN: 033-180–033-181). Her reply is the novel's great moral rebuttal:

> "_Me_ unnatural! _Me_ inhuman! To my dear boy?" (PN: 033-182)

She reveals that there was no drunken grandmother, no abandonment in a ditch: "Josiah in the gutter! No such a thing, sir. Never! For shame on you!" (PN: 033-195). She and her husband, a decent working man, "loved him as dear as the best could, and never thought it hardship on themselves to pinch a bit that he might write and cipher beautiful" (PN: 033-199). She kept his schoolbooks as proof. After her husband died when Josiah was eight, she worked to put him apprentice, and "a steady lad he was" (PN: 033-204). She kept a village shop. Bounderby, once prosperous, pensioned her on thirty pounds a year — "more than I want, for I put by out of it" (PN: 033-209) — on condition that she "keep down in my own part, and make no boasts about him, and not trouble him" (PN: 033-210). And she has kept that condition, except for looking at him "once a year, when he has never knowed it" (PN: 033-211).

Her final declaration is both self-justification and a devastating unwitting critique of her son: "I am well contented, and I can keep my pride in my Josiah to myself, and I can love for love's own sake!" (PN: 033-215). The word "pride" here is the novel's precise pivot: where Bounderby's pride is the fraudulent self-display of the "Bully of humility" (PN: 033-243), Mrs. Pegler's pride is the quiet, hidden pride of genuine love — pride that asks for nothing, demands no recognition, and is content to exist in secret.

Bounderby's only response is bluster: he tries to dismiss the audience without comment (PN: 033-238–033-242), but the narrator's verdict is final: "Detected as the Bully of humility, who had built his windy reputation upon lies... he cut a most ridiculous figure" (PN: 033-243–033-246). He is the "self-made Humbug, Josiah Bounderby of Coketown" (PN: 033-246). Mrs. Pegler remains at her son's house that night (PN: 033-253) and then vanishes from the narrative — her work of truth-telling complete, her life of quiet love unchanged.

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Pegler is a one-scene character with novel-shattering impact. Her revelation does not merely discredit Bounderby — it rewrites the novel's moral calculus. Everything the reader has accepted about Coketown's leading citizen turns out to be a lie, and the truth was held in trust by an old country woman who kept her son's secret out of love.

She represents the **real working class** that Bounderby claims to have risen from — dignified, self-sacrificing, and loving — in contrast to his performative, fabricated version. Where Bounderby's poverty is a theatrical lie designed to justify his present power, Mrs. Pegler's poverty is real, uncomplaining, and borne without resentment. She embodies the novel's argument that **pride** has two faces: the false pride of self-display and the true pride of quiet self-respect. When she says "I can love for love's own sake," she names an ethic that is the polar opposite of everything her son has become. (PN: 033-213–033-215)

She also embodies the novel's claims about **truth**. Mrs. Pegler does not seek to expose Bounderby; she is dragged into his house against her will and tells the truth only because she is accused of a crime — desertion — that is the opposite of everything she has done. Her truth-telling is not a strategy but a reflex: asked a direct question by Gradgrind, she answers it directly, and in doing so unravels a lie that has structured the novel for two books. This is Dickens's deepest claim about truth: it does not need to be loud or strategic. It only needs to be spoken. (PN: 033-187–033-217)

Her annual visits to Coketown — "with long times between, to take a proud peep at you, I have done it unbeknown, my love, and gone away again" (PN: 033-165) — are the novel's most heartbreaking detail. She loves her son enough to accept the condition that she remain invisible. She gets one glimpse of him a year, from a distance, and that is enough. In a novel full of spectacular cruelties, this quiet cruelty — the mother erased so the son can pretend he had none — may be the deepest.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Her son, and the reason for her silence. Mrs. Pegler's relationship with Bounderby is the novel's most inverted parent-child bond: she sacrificed everything to raise him, educated him, put him apprentice, and he repaid her by paying her to stay away and inventing a gutter-childhood that erased her entirely. She continues to love him without resentment. His response to her public appearance is not shame or apology but rage at being exposed. (PN: 033-157–033-165, 033-238–033-246)
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: The working man who first meets Mrs. Pegler at his lodging and treats her with the same gentle courtesy he shows everyone. His innocent question about her children nearly reveals her secret; his later questioning by the police makes her a suspect in the bank robbery. The failure of the suspicions against Mrs. Pegler helps clear Stephen's name. (PN: 022-108–022-111, 033-257)
- **[[Rachael]]**: The working woman who reassures Mrs. Pegler when she panics at Bounderby's name and who identifies her in the revelation scene as "Mrs. Pegler" — a name Rachael learned from the meeting at Stephen's lodging. (PN: 022-127–022-129, 033-122)
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]**: Her inadvertent nemesis. Mrs. Sparsit drags Mrs. Pegler to Bounderby's house believing she has discovered a conspirator in the bank robbery; instead, she becomes the instrument of Bounderby's exposure. Mrs. Pegler is terrified of her: "She threatened me that if I resisted her, I should be brought by constables" (PN: 033-164). The scene is the novel's darkest irony — the snob who hunts for scandal becomes the midwife of truth.
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: Her unknown daughter-in-law, whom Mrs. Pegler sees for the first time at Stephen's lodging and admires to the point of tears. The detail that Mrs. Pegler wept "because she was such a pretty dear" (PN: 022-293–022-294) reveals a woman who loves her son's wife without ever having been permitted to meet her.

## Critical Reception

> Mrs. Pegler is the novel's most concentrated argument for the power of plain truth over elaborate falsehood. Her single scene — a poor old woman saying what happened — is more powerful than two books of Bounderby's bombast. Dickens's genius here is structural: he lets the lie dominate the novel for so long that its collapse, when it comes, feels like the ground giving way. And the instrument of that collapse is not a lawyer, a detective, or a moralist — it is a mother who loved her son and kept his secret. (PN: 033-238–033-246)

> The annual pilgrimage — Mrs. Pegler coming to Coketown "with long times between, to take a proud peep" at her son — is one of the most quietly devastating details in Victorian fiction. It captures, in a single image, the cruelty at the heart of the self-made-man myth: a mother erased so that a son can boast of having had no one.
