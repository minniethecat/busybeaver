# Duty

In *Hard Times*, "duty" is the word most frequently weaponized by Coketown's powerful. Gradgrind invokes it to force his daughter into a loveless marriage. The union invokes it to ostracise Stephen Blackpool. Mrs. Sparsit invokes it to justify surveillance. Against these perversions — duty as rational tyranny, duty as collective coercion, duty as self-aggrandising pretence — Dickens posits a quieter, simpler duty: Rachael's tireless care for Stephen's drunken wife, Sissy Jupe's loyalty to the fallen Louisa, Sleary's protection of the fugitive Tom. Duty in its genuine form is not a code to be calculated but an instinct of the heart, indistinguishable from love.

## Definition

"Something — some fire, some mist, some steam, some water, some air" that must be assimilated into the Gradgrindian project of "fact." The definition is the novel's satiric masterstroke: duty, the most human of obligations, reduced to a material residue to be acquired by children as if it were elemental chemistry. For the Utilitarian mind, duty is not felt but calculated — a problem of optimisation, not of conscience. Dickens's task in *Hard Times* is to recover duty from this reduction, to show that genuine duty originates not in rational deduction but in the affections. (PN: 008-007–031)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of duty follows a pattern of exposure and recovery. The novel begins by showing duty in its counterfeit forms: Gradgrind's "duty" in proposing Bounderby to Louisa is a cold transaction masquerading as paternal care (PN: 015-185); the union's "sacred duty" as proclaimed by Slackbridge is a demagogic instrument of exclusion (PN: 020-193); Mrs. Sparsit's "duty" is a licence to spy and scheme (PN: 017-288). In each case, duty is a word that rationalises the exercise of power over the vulnerable.

Against this pattern, Dickens sets counter-examples that never announce themselves as duty. Rachael nurses Stephen's drunken wife without once calling it obligation. Sissy Jupe sustains Louisa through her breakdown without self-congratulation. Sleary risks his livelihood and his safety to conceal Tom — not because a maxim dictates it but because a human being in trouble asks for help. These acts have no Utilitarian justification; they are, in the novel's terms, simply what love does.

The novel's final vision of duty arrives in Book III, Chapter 9, when Dickens describes the reformed Gradgrind's new understanding: "simply as a duty to be" (PN: 037-167). The phrase is deliberately plain — duty stripped of rhetoric, of calculation, of self-display. It is not a duty *to do* but a duty *to be*: a way of existing in relation to others, grounded in humility and fellow-feeling.

### Key Passages

> "In this is comprised the whole duty of man — not a part of man's duty, but the whole." (PN: 017-185)

Gradgrind's Utilitarian creed, delivered to Louisa in defence of her marriage to Bounderby. The echo of Ecclesiastes — "the whole duty of man" — is deliberate and damning: Gradgrind has substituted a calculus of utility for the biblical command to fear God and keep His commandments. Duty, in his mouth, means the total subordination of the individual life to a rational system. That Louisa's destruction follows from this philosophy is Dickens's verdict on its adequacy as an ethics.

> "He believes as he has done his duty by the rest and by you." (PN: 021-076)

Stephen Blackpool, through the narrator's free indirect discourse, reflects on his conduct toward his fellow workers. What is remarkable here is that Stephen *has* done his duty — he has been honest, loyal, and principled — yet he has been punished for it. The union has ostracised him; the masters will not help him. Duty, Dickens insists, does not guarantee reward. Stephen's integrity is real precisely because it costs him everything.

> "Simply as a duty to be" (PN: 037-167)

The novel's closing definition. After catastrophe has unravelled Gradgrind's system — Louisa broken, Tom a thief, Bounderby exposed — duty is redefined not as a rule to follow but as a way of being human. The phrase is the antithesis of the Gradgrindian "whole duty of man": it asks nothing of duty except presence, kindness, and the willingness to stand with others in their suffering.

## Historical Context

The Victorian period was saturated with discourses of duty. Thomas Carlyle's *Sartor Resartus* (1836) and *On Heroes* (1841) made duty a central category of moral life, but it was a duty conceived in strenuous, heroic, masculine terms — the duty of the great man to lead and the common man to follow. Evangelical Christianity, the dominant religious force in Victorian England, preached a duty of self-examination, sobriety, and submission to divine will. Both traditions had hardened, by mid-century, into a kind of moral policing that Dickens increasingly distrusted.

The particular target of *Hard Times*, however, is the Utilitarian deformation of duty. Jeremy Bentham's *Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789) had proposed that human action be governed by the "felicific calculus" — the calculation of pleasure and pain. Under this logic, duty becomes a branch of arithmetic: one does what produces the greatest aggregate happiness. Gradgrind's reduction of Louisa's marriage to a statistical proposition ("I would advise you to consider this question, as you have been accustomed to consider every other question, simply as one of tangible Fact," PN: 015-177) is the practical expression of Benthamite ethics, and its emotional violence is Dickens's indictment.

The novel's alternative — duty as love — draws on a different Victorian tradition: the domestic ideal that located moral authority in the affections nurtured at home. Rachael, Sissy, and the reformed Gradgrind embody what the Victorian moralist F.D. Maurice called "the law of kindness." This is not a retreat from public duty into private sentiment; it is a claim that private sentiment is the only reliable foundation on which public duty can be built.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: Duty perverted into rational tyranny. Gradgrind genuinely believes he acts from duty — he is not a hypocrite — but his conception of duty is entirely procedural: he has discharged his obligation when he has reasoned correctly, regardless of the human consequences. His arc from "the whole duty of man" to "simply as a duty to be" is the novel's moral centre of gravity.
- **[[Rachael]]**: Duty as love in action. Rachael never utters the word "duty"; she simply performs it — nursing Stephen's alcoholic wife without resentment, testifying on Stephen's behalf without self-regard, standing by him when every institution has failed him. She is the novel's living argument for a duty that needs no maxims.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Duty as integrity under pressure. Stephen's refusal to join the union is an act of individual conscience that costs him his community, his livelihood, and ultimately his life. His duty is not theoretical — it is tested in the most brutal circumstances — and he does not waver. The novel treats this as tragic rather than triumphant: Stephen's integrity is real, and it destroys him.
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]**: Duty as self-aggrandising surveillance. Mrs. Sparsit's "duty" to observe and report — first as Bounderby's housekeeper, later as self-appointed spy on Louisa and Harthouse — is a study in how the language of moral obligation can be made to serve malice. Her "duty" is always aligned with her interest, and her zeal is indistinguishable from cruelty.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The collision between duty and facts is the novel's central ethical drama. When Gradgrind reduces duty to a calculation — to a fact about optimal outcomes — he eliminates the element of human judgement that makes duty moral. Facts can tell you what is the case; they cannot tell you what you owe. The novel insists that duty requires a faculty that the Gradgrindian system has denied: the heart. (PN: 017-185)
- **[[love]]**: Duty and love are presented as near-synonyms in the novel's genuine ethical register, and as mortal antagonists in its counterfeit one. Gradgrind's "duty" to provide for Louisa is the opposite of love — it is the rationalisation of unloving conduct. Rachael's love for Stephen is never called duty, but it performs everything that duty, properly understood, demands. The novel's thesis is that duty without love is tyranny, and love without duty is sentiment; only together do they constitute a life. (PN: 037-167, PN: 030-168)

## Critical Perspectives

> George Orwell, in his 1939 essay "Charles Dickens," argued that Dickens's morality is "almost exclusively concerned with manners" — that Dickens judges people by how they behave in small, daily transactions rather than by grand principles. This reading illuminates the novel's treatment of duty: Dickens distrusts duty when it is a principle and trusts it when it is a habit. Rachael does not reason her way to duty; she lives it, and that is the point.

> Humphry House, in *The Dickens World* (1941), emphasised that Dickens's ethics are fundamentally Christian in structure even when they avoid Christian doctrine. The novel's final vision of duty — "simply as a duty to be" — is, House might argue, a secular translation of the New Testament's identification of love as the fulfilment of the law. Duty becomes not a command but a disposition, and the disposition is charity.

> Raymond Williams, in *The English Novel from Dickens to Lawrence* (1970), noted that the tension between public and private duty in *Hard Times* reflects a wider Victorian crisis: the breakdown of the assumption that personal morality and social morality operate by the same rules. Gradgrind's public philosophy — Utilitarianism — destroys his private life. Stephen's private integrity has no public force. The novel registers this fracture without resolving it.
