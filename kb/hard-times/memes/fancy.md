# Fancy

Fancy is the novel's counter-principle to Facts. Where Facts are hard, measurable, and imposed, Fancy is fluid, irreverent, and indestructible — it persists in the circus ring, in Sissy Jupe's stubborn loyalty, in the fairy-tale shapes that Dickens's own narrative voice assumes. The word appears at a pivotal moment early in the novel: when Gradgrind catches his children peeping at Sleary's circus, the narrator observes that "by nonsense he meant fancy; and truly it is probable that nonsense was the name he gave to it" (PN: 004-128). Fancy is what the Gradgrind system cannot accommodate, cannot name correctly, and cannot extinguish. It is the novel's term for the human capacity to make, play, embellish, and wonder — capacities that have no place in a Utilitarian calculus but without which, Dickens insists, life is not worth living.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens deploys Fancy as both theme and method. The novel's own narrative voice is fanciful: it personifies the steam-engine as a "melancholy mad elephant," describes Coketown's chimneys as "interminable serpents of smoke," and consistently prefers metaphor to exposition. This is not decoration but argument by example — Dickens demonstrates, in the act of writing, the kind of perception that Gradgrind's education would extinguish.

The circus is Fancy's institutional home — but it is a precarious one. Sleary's troupe is perpetually on the edge of bankruptcy; its performers are physically vulnerable (Sleary's "asthma," the riders' injuries); its aesthetic is ridiculed by respectable Coketown. Yet it is from this marginal, ridiculous institution that the novel's acts of rescue and repair come. Sissy Jupe, raised in the circus, becomes the agent who mends the Gradgrind family. Fancy, Dickens insists, is not escape from reality but access to a deeper realism — the realism that recognises that human beings need more than bread and facts.

### Key Passages

> "'You are never to fancy.'" (PN: 002-141)

The gentleman (Gradgrind) issues this prohibition to Sissy Jupe after she admits that she would "fancy" a flower-patterned carpet. The sentence is the novel's ethical summary of Gradgrindism in five monosyllables. It is an interdiction not against falsehood but against the very faculty of imagining what is not immediately present — which is to say, it is an interdiction against the human.

> "By nonsense he meant fancy; and truly it is probable that nonsense was the name he gave to it." (PN: 004-128)

The narrative voice intervenes here to perform a linguistic correction. Gradgrind's category error — confusing Fancy for nonsense — is the novel's central philosophical claim: that the Utilitarian framework lacks the vocabulary to describe, let alone value, the imaginative life that sustains human beings.

> "the rugged fancy of this man" (PN: 013-250)

This phrase describes Stephen Blackpool, the weaver, whose plainness and integrity carry a "rugged fancy" — a rough, unpolished imaginative capacity that survives despite poverty, despite the union, despite Bounderby. The attribution of "fancy" to Stephen, the novel's most grounded character, is significant: Fancy is not the exclusive property of artists and performers but a universal human resource.

## Historical Context

Dickens inherited the term "Fancy" from Romantic aesthetics, particularly **Coleridge's** distinction in the *Biographia Literaria* (1817) between the "primary Imagination" (the living power of creation, akin to divine creation) and the "Fancy" (the associative recombination of fixed sense-data). Coleridge valued Imagination over Fancy; Dickens's usage is less systematic — closer to a general category of imaginative play — but he shares Coleridge's sense that the faculty is under threat from rationalising modernity.

The circus as a symbol draws on the actual history of **Victorian popular entertainment**. Astley's Amphitheatre (London), Cooke's Circus, and travelling shows like Sleary's were fixtures of 19th-century working-class culture. Their association with physical risk, bodily exposure, and social marginality made them ideal symbols for Dickens's argument that the valuable survives in places that respectability despises.

## Embodied By

- **[[Slearys-Circus]]**: The collective embodiment of Fancy — a travelling company of horse-riders, acrobats, and clowns who represent the persistence of play in a world of work. The circus is ridiculous, unprofitable, and physically precarious; these are precisely the traits that make it, in Dickens's symbolic economy, a repository of value.
- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: Raised in the circus, she carries Fancy into the Gradgrind household as a kind of benign contagion. Her inability to "define a horse" (she can only love and care for one) is the novel's paradigm case of Fancy's superiority to Fact-based knowledge.
- **[[Sleary]]**: The circus-master whose lisp and physical infirmity mark him as a figure of fun — yet whose closing words ("the people mutht be amuthed") distill the novel's moral. Sleary's Fancy is not frivolous; it is the wisdom of a man who knows that human beings require more than utility.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The novel's primary opposition. Facts are discrete, measurable, and teachable; Fancy is holistic, qualitative, and caught rather than taught. Gradgrind's tragedy is that he learns the insufficiency of Facts too late — after his system has damaged his children. (PN: 001-002–007)
- **[[utilitarianism]]**: The philosophical system that cannot value Fancy because Fancy's benefits — joy, wonder, sympathy, resilience — resist quantification. The novel argues that this is not a defect of Fancy but of the calculus.
- **[[political-economy]]**: The discourse that reduces all human relations to transactions. Fancy — gratuitous, excessive, unprofitable — is the living refutation of the claim that human worth can be measured in pounds.

## Critical Perspectives

> G. K. Chesterton, in his 1906 study of Dickens, celebrated the circus as "the last triumph of the human spirit over the crushing machinery of industrial capitalism." Chesterton read Sleary's lisp not as a comic tic but as a theological argument: the stammer of the spirit against the fluent lies of the world.

> David Lodge, in "The Rhetoric of *Hard Times*" (1966), analysed the novel's "fanciful" prose style itself as a form of thematic argument. Lodge argued that Dickens's metaphors, similes, and personifications constitute a "counter-language" to the literal, fact-bound discourse of Coketown.
