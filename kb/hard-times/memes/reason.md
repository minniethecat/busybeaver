# Reason

Reason in *Hard Times* is the name of a problem. Gradgrind's educational programme is described as "the education of the reason" (PN: 004-139), and the phrase is meant to sound virtuous: reason, after all, is what distinguishes humans from animals; to educate reason is to cultivate the distinctively human. But Dickens's novel systematically demonstrates that Reason, elevated to a sole principle and stripped of its companions — imagination, sympathy, love — becomes not the flower of humanity but its deformation. The novel does not oppose Reason; it opposes the reduction of the human to Reason alone. The "reasoning animals" of Gradgrind's opening speech are, the novel argues, something less than fully human precisely because they are only reasoning.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

The critique of Reason operates at two levels. At the level of **character**, Gradgrind, Bounderby, and the younger Louisa embody the varieties of Reason's failure: Gradgrind is Reason as sincere conviction (and therefore corrigible), Bounderby is Reason as self-serving ideology (and therefore incorrigible), and Louisa is Reason's victim — the person whose rational education has left her defenceless against the irrational forces (desire, despair, the need for love) that her philosophy cannot acknowledge.

At the level of **form**, the novel's own narrative method is an argument against pure Reason. Dickens's prose is hyperbolic, metaphorical, personifying, digressive — in a word, unreasonable. The steam-engine is a "melancholy mad elephant"; Coketown's buildings are "the painted face of a savage." These are not neutral descriptions but acts of imaginative transformation that demonstrate, in the very texture of the writing, the insufficiency of a purely rationalist epistemology. Dickens does not argue that Reason is wrong; he shows that Reason is narrow.

### Key Passages

> "You are to be in all things regulated and governed... by fact. We hope to have, before long, a board of fact, composed of commissioners of fact, who will force the people to be a people of fact, and of nothing but fact." (PN: 002-116–118)

This is the novel's dystopian vision of Reason institutionalised — a "board of fact" that would govern not by wisdom, justice, or compassion but by the impersonal authority of verified propositions. It is a bureaucracy of the intellect, and Dickens's satire anticipates 20th-century critiques of technocratic governance.

> "the education of the reason" of his family (PN: 004-139)

Gradgrind's phrase is a statement of method and a confession of limitation. The "education of the reason" presumes that reason is the "only faculty to which education should be addressed" — and the word "only" is the novel's target. An education that addresses only Reason produces beings who can calculate but cannot feel, who can classify but cannot love.

> "All that I know is, your philosophy and your teaching will not save me." (Louisa to Gradgrind — implicit paraphrase)

Louisa's collapse in Book II is the novel's verdict on Reason as a life philosophy. Her father's system has given her every intellectual tool and no emotional resource. When her marriage to Bounderby, the attentions of Harthouse, and her own uncomprehended desires converge, Reason offers no guidance — because Reason was never designed to navigate the terrain of the heart.

## Historical Context

*Hard Times* engages with a specific moment in the history of Reason's cultural ascendancy. The **Enlightenment** had elevated rational inquiry as the engine of progress; by the mid-19th century, this project had taken institutional form in the Benthamite programme of legal and social reform, the statistical societies, the "March of Intellect" movement, and the professionalisation of expertise. Each of these developments promised to replace tradition, prejudice, and superstition with rational administration.

Dickens was not hostile to Enlightenment (his novels are full of rational indignation at irrational injustice) but to the **imperialism of Reason** — the claim that rational calculation exhausts the domain of legitimate knowledge. This was also the argument of **Thomas Carlyle**, whose "Signs of the Times" (1829) had diagnosed the age's "Mechanical" character: "Men are grown mechanical in head and in heart, as well as in hand." Dickens's critique of Reason in *Hard Times* is deeply indebted to Carlyle's diagnosis, though Dickens's tone — comic, hyperbolic, performative — is his own.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: Reason as sincere conviction. Gradgrind genuinely believes that a Fact-based education will produce virtuous, productive citizens. His tragedy is that he is intelligent enough to recognise his error and honest enough to admit it — but only after his system has damaged his children. His arc is Reason's self-correction, and it is the novel's concession that Reason is not inherently evil but inherently incomplete.
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Reason as self-justifying ideology. Bounderby uses the language of rationality to defend his own power and wealth. His "self-made man" narrative is a rationalisation — literally, a story told in the language of Reason to disguise the operations of power. When the narrative is exposed as fiction, Bounderby does not reform; he doubles down. He is what Gradgrind might become if Gradgrind lacked a conscience.
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: Reason's product and its victim. Her carefully cultivated rational intelligence cannot answer the questions her life poses. She is the novel's most eloquent argument that Reason without Heart is not half a person but a damaged one.

## Contrasted With

- **[[fancy]]**: Where Reason classifies, Fancy connects. Where Reason measures, Fancy wonders. The novel's structural opposition between the schoolroom and the circus is the opposition between these two modes of apprehending the world. (PN: 002-141)
- **[[imagination]]**: Coleridge's distinction — Reason as the faculty of the finite, Imagination as the faculty of the infinite — is implicit throughout the novel. Gradgrind's Reason can describe a horse (graminivorous, quadruped, forty teeth) but cannot picture one; Sissy Jupe cannot define a horse but knows one intimately when she sees it.
- **[[heart]]**: The novel's term for the entire domain of human experience that Reason cannot reach: love, loyalty, grief, wonder, moral intuition. The novel's argument is not against Reason but against the claim that Reason is sufficient — that the "wisdom of the Head" can substitute for the "wisdom of the Heart."
- **[[experience]]**: Reason deals in abstractions ("the Horse"); experience deals in particulars ("this horse, which I love"). The novel consistently values experiential knowledge over rational taxonomy.

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *The English Novel from Dickens to Lawrence* (1970), argued that Dickens's treatment of Reason in *Hard Times* was a form of "negative critique" — powerful in its exposure of what was wrong with Utilitarian rationalism but limited in its capacity to offer an alternative. Williams reads the circus as a "sentimental solution" to a structural problem.

> Martha Nussbaum, in *Poetic Justice* (1995), offered a defence of Dickens's method from the perspective of moral philosophy. She argues that the novel's "fanciful" narrative style is itself a form of rational argument — an argument that "fancy" is necessary to moral perception and that a purely "rational" account of human flourishing is constitutively inadequate.
