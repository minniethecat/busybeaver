# Political Economy

Political Economy — the "science" that dominated mid-Victorian public discourse — is in *Hard Times* the intellectual cousin of Gradgrind's Facts and the ideological armour of Bounderby's industrial capitalism. Dickens's satire is concentrated and lethal: Political Economy is introduced into the novel not through a treatise or a Parliamentary debate but through a nursery scene, in which even "a prattler three feet high" has been inducted into its doctrines. The reduction of the science to childish recitation is Dickens's method of exposing its pretensions — Political Economy, the novel insists, is not wisdom but a set of self-serving propositions elevated to the status of natural law by those who profit from them.

## Definition

In the world of *Hard Times*, Political Economy names the cluster of doctrines — free trade, laissez-faire, the wage-fund theory, the iron law of wages — that constituted mid-Victorian economic orthodoxy. Its core premise was that markets were self-regulating systems governed by discoverable laws, and that interference with those laws (through trade protection, labour regulation, or poor relief) was not merely inefficient but unnatural. The human consequences of these laws — poverty, unemployment, starvation — were regrettable but, in the language of the science, "inevitable": they were the price of prosperity, and anyone who thought otherwise simply did not understand the principles of the science. (PN: 008-093)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of Political Economy is sharper and more direct than his treatment of Utilitarianism proper. Where Utilitarianism is embodied in Gradgrind — a sincere believer whose sincerity destroys his family — Political Economy is embodied in Bounderby, a hypocrite whose professed adherence to economic "laws" is transparently self-serving. Bounderby's famous self-description — "I am Josiah Bounderby of Coketown" — is a declaration of economic identity: he is what the system has made, and what the system has made is a bully who mistakes his good fortune for moral desert.

The novel's most concentrated satire of Political Economy arrives in its treatment of the "Hands." The term itself — the reduction of human beings to the body part that performs labour — is the linguistic expression of Political Economy's logic. When Bounderby dismisses the workers' grievances as the fantasies of men who "want to be set up in a coach and six, and to be fed on turtle soup and venison, with a gold spoon" (PN: 011-039), he is applying the logic of the wage-fund theory: workers are paid what the market determines they are worth, and their dissatisfaction is evidence not of injustice but of ingratitude.

Harthouse represents Political Economy in its genteel, disengaged form. A gentleman who has "tried" a series of occupations — dragoons, diplomacy, idleness — and found them all equally meaningless, Harthouse drifts into Coketown as a parliamentary candidate's aide. His detachment is the luxury version of Bounderby's brutality: he does not need to believe in Political Economy because he is insulated from its consequences. His seduction of Louisa — amoral, opportunistic, finally cowardly — is the novel's portrait of what Political Economy does to the character of those it spares.

### Key Passages

> "Political Economy, she had only yesterday been set right by a prattler three feet high, for returning to the question, 'What is the first principle of this science?'" (PN: 008-093)

The novel's single explicit naming of Political Economy arrives in a scene of domestic absurdity. The Gradgrind nursery is a seminar room; even the toddler has been conscripted into the indoctrination programme. By framing Political Economy as a nursery catechism, Dickens strips it of intellectual authority: it is not a science but a set of slogans, and the "prattler three feet high" who recites them exposes the doctrine's intellectual shallowness.

> "So many hundred Hands in this Mill; so many hundred horse Steam Power." (PN: 011-016)

The narrator's report of Bounderby's account of Coketown renders the economic calculus in its starkest form. "Hands" and "Steam Power" are inputs in a production function, distinguished only by their unit of measurement. The reduction of human beings to "Hands" — the metonym that Political Economy requires — is the novel's most persistent indictment of the doctrine.

## Historical Context

The "Manchester School" of political economy — led by Richard Cobden and John Bright — dominated British economic policy in the 1840s and 1850s. Their central achievement, the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846, had abolished tariffs on imported grain and committed Britain to free trade. The Anti-Corn Law League's arguments — that free trade would lower food prices, expand markets, and promote international peace — were persuasive to a public that remembered the "Hungry Forties."

But the same logic that justified free trade in grain also justified resistance to factory regulation, opposition to trade unions, and the administration of poor relief on "less eligibility" principles. Edwin Chadwick's design of the New Poor Law (1834), which made workhouse conditions deliberately worse than the worst independent labourer's lot, was Political Economy applied to poverty: if relief were too comfortable, the poor would not work, and the labour market would not clear.

Dickens had attacked the New Poor Law in *Oliver Twist* (1837), and *Hard Times* extends the critique from poor relief to the entire economic ideology. The novel's Coketown — with its "interminable serpents of smoke," its identical red-brick buildings, and its inhabitants reduced to interchangeable units — is the physical manifestation of Political Economy's logic: a world built to the specifications of a doctrine that has forgotten that the subjects of its "laws" are human beings.

## Embodied By

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Political Economy as self-justification. Bounderby is the novel's most sustained portrait of how economic doctrine serves power. His rise-from-the-gutter narrative — exposed, eventually, as a fiction — is his claim to authority: he has succeeded, therefore the system works, therefore those who have not succeeded are to blame. His "Hands" are children, and he is their bully.
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: Political Economy in its sincere, intellectual form. Gradgrind genuinely believes that his children's welfare can be optimised through rational calculation, and his tragedy is that sincerity is no defence against error. His intellectual commitment to Political Economy leads him to propose Bounderby to Louisa — not out of malice but out of conviction that the match is "rational" — and the catastrophe that follows is the novel's verdict on the sufficiency of rational calculation in human affairs.
- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: Political Economy as ennui. Harthouse has no convictions — he has tried several and found them equally empty — but his indifference is itself a form of Political Economy: the luxury of not needing to care, which is the privilege of those whom the system does not crush.

## Contrasted With

- **[[heart]]**: Political Economy's constitutive exclusion. The "science" can count wages, hours, and mortality rates, but it cannot count love, loyalty, or the suffering of a child separated from her father. The novel's entire emotional register — from Sissy's tears to Stephen's dignity — is evidence that Political Economy's account of human life is radically incomplete. (PN: 006-375)
- **[[fancy]]**: Where Political Economy reduces human activity to production and exchange, Fancy names the realm of play, art, and imagination that escapes economic calculation. Sleary's Circus — with its absurd equestrianism and its ethic of mutual care — is the novel's image of a community organised around something other than the logic of the market. (PN: 003-045)

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), read the novel's treatment of Political Economy as part of a broader nineteenth-century debate about the meaning of "culture" — a word that was, in Williams's account, coined in opposition to the reductive logic of industrial capitalism. *Hard Times* participates in this debate by insisting that the "science" of Political Economy is not value-neutral but actively hostile to the values that make life human.

> Catherine Gallagher, in *The Industrial Reformation of English Fiction* (1985), argued that the novel's formal features — its "fable" structure, its refusal of novelistic realism — are themselves a critique of Political Economy's claim to representational authority. By writing a novel that reads like a parable, Dickens refuses the terms of the debate as Political Economy has defined them.
