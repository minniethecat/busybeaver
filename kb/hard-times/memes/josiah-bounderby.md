# Josiah Bounderby

Josiah Bounderby is the central antagonist of *Hard Times* — a wealthy [[Coketown]] manufacturer and banker whose entire public identity rests on the fraudulent claim that he raised himself from the gutter without any help. His exposure in Book III, when his devoted mother [[Mrs-Pegler]] is brought before him, is the novel's masterstroke of moral accounting, and his collapse before the assembled company of [[Thomas-Gradgrind]], [[Louisa-Gradgrind]], and [[Sissy-Jupe]] unmakes the ideology of the self-made man in a single scene.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Josiah Bounderby (styled "Josiah Bounderby of Coketown")
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "the Bully of humility" (PN: 004-018)
- **Role**: Antagonist — the self-made-man myth personified and exposed
- **Occupation**: Banker, merchant, manufacturer — Coketown's leading industrial capitalist
- **Family**: Mrs. Pegler (mother, secretly pensioned and kept away from Coketown on pain of losing her allowance) (PN: 033-208–033-216)
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 4, "MR. BOUNDERBY" — formally introduced as Gradgrind's "bosom friend" (PN: 004-002–004-006)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 9, "FINAL" — his death by a fit in Coketown street is recounted in the narrative's closing summary (PN: 037-106–037-108)

| Claim | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| Abandoned by his mother in a ditch | Raised lovingly by Mrs. Pegler, who put him apprentice after his father died |
| Grandmother "the wickedest old woman that ever lived" | A fiction; Mrs. Pegler raised him alone and with devotion |
| "Nobody to thank for my being here, but myself" | His mother sacrificed everything, pensioned on £30/year to stay silent |
| Self-made manufacturer | Built on the labour of the "Hands" he despises |

## Characterisation

Dickens builds Bounderby out of industrial imagery — metal, brass, inflation — and defines him through the tension between his colossal self-regard and the lie that sustains it. The novel's formal introduction is a sustained passage of anaphoric negation that damns him in the act of defining him:

> He was a rich man: banker, merchant, manufacturer, and what not. A big, loud man, with a stare, and a metallic laugh. A man made out of a coarse material, which seemed to have been stretched to make so much of him. A man with a great puffed head and forehead, swelled veins in his temples, and such a strained skin to his face that it seemed to hold his eyes open, and lift his eyebrows up. A man with a pervading appearance on him of being inflated like a balloon, and ready to start. A man who could never sufficiently vaunt himself a self-made man. A man who was always proclaiming, through that brassy speaking-trumpet of a voice of his, his old ignorance and his old poverty. A man who was the Bully of humility. (PN: 004-007–018)

The anaphoric "A man... A man... A man" — seven iterations — mimics the relentless self-promotion it describes. Every physical detail (puffed head, strained skin, metallic laugh) serves the metaphor of inflation: Bounderby is a balloon, a swelling of hot air, and the novel will eventually puncture him.

### Physical Description

From his first appearance, Bounderby is rendered in terms of brutal materiality. He is "a big, loud man, with a stare, and a metallic laugh. A man made out of a coarse material, which seemed to have been stretched to make so much of him" (PN: 004-007–004-009). His head is "puffed," his skin "strained," his whole person "inflated like a balloon, and ready to start" (PN: 004-010–004-013). His "brassy speaking-trumpet of a voice" announces everything he says as proclamation (PN: 004-015). He looks older than his claimed seven-and-forty; what hair remains "standing up in disorder" seems "constantly blown about by his windy boastfulness" (PN: 004-017–004-022).

### Speech and Mannerisms

Bounderby's most characteristic verbal gesture is the claim to have risen from nothing, delivered in a bombastic register that mixes self-congratulation with a theatrical working-class roughness:

> 'I hadn't a shoe to my foot. As to a stocking, I didn't know such a thing by name. I passed the day in a ditch, and the night in a pigsty. That's the way I spent my tenth birthday. Not that a ditch was new to me, for I was born in a ditch.' (PN: 004-030–034)

His laugh is "metallic" (PN: 004-007), his manner "windy" (PN: 021-002), his mode of address to subordinates blustering: "Why don't you mind your own business, ma'am?" (PN: 033-151). The catchphrase "Josiah Bounderby of Coketown" functions as his self-bestowed title — a name he repeats as though it were a coat of arms (PN: 037-101–037-105).

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Bounderby is introduced in Chapter 4 as Gradgrind's closest associate, the practical complement to Gradgrind's theoretical Utilitarianism. He delivers his gutter-birth origin story to Mrs. Gradgrind on the occasion of his birthday, establishing the self-made-man narrative that will define him through two books: "nobody to thank for my being here, but myself" (PN: 004-051–004-052). He claims his mother abandoned him and his grandmother was "the wickedest and the worst old woman that ever lived" (PN: 004-056–004-058). He hires Sissy Jupe's father's dog as cover for dismissing the circus girl, and later proposes marriage to the sixteen-year-old Louisa Gradgrind — a proposal her father presents as a problem in "facts and figures" rather than love (PN: 015-055–015-064).

### Book II: Reaping

As the married industrialist, Bounderby becomes the face of Coketown's labour relations. He dismisses his workers as "Hands" whose "ultimate object in life" is "to be fed on turtle soup and venison with a gold spoon" (PN: 018-078–018-082). He ostracises Stephen Blackpool for refusing to join the union — not out of principle but because Stephen will not act as Bounderby's informer. His house at the edge of Coketown, presided over by the class-proud Mrs. Sparsit, becomes the novel's emblem of wealth severed from community.

### Book III: Garnering

Book III delivers Bounderby's moral reckoning. Mrs. Sparsit, believing she has discovered Louisa's lover, instead hauls old Mrs. Pegler into Bounderby's dining-room — and the self-made-man myth collapses before the assembled company. Mrs. Pegler reveals that she raised him lovingly, put him apprentice after his father died, and was pensioned on thirty pounds a year "making the condition that I was to keep down in my own part, and make no boasts about him, and not trouble him" (PN: 033-207–033-211). Bounderby's sole response is blustering rage at being exposed. The final chapter reports that five years later he "was to die of a fit in the Coketown street" (PN: 037-107), his will "to begin its long career of quibble, plunder, false pretences, vile example, little service and much law" (PN: 037-108–037-109).

## Thematic Significance

Bounderby embodies the ideological fraud at the heart of industrial capitalism. His self-made-man mythology is the novel's central lie — the claim that wealth is earned in isolation, owing nothing to family, community, or the labour of others. Dickens systematically demolishes this claim: first by showing that Bounderby's prosperity rests on the backs of the "Hands" he despises, then by revealing that his origin story is itself a fiction designed to erase his mother's sacrifice. He is the "Bully of humility" (PN: 004-016) — a man who weaponises pretended deprivation to justify real cruelty.

He also represents the moral poverty that accompanies material wealth in Dickens's Coketown. His marriage to Louisa is a transaction without love (PN: 015-110–015-112). His "charity" — the bank he claims to maintain for workers — is self-advertising. His relationship with Mrs. Sparsit is a study in mutual exploitation under the forms of genteel patronage. In every domain where the novel measures human worth — love, work, family, community — Bounderby is found empty.

Contrasted with [[Stephen-Blackpool]], who possesses actual working-class dignity but no wealth, and with [[Sleary]], whose circus represents a genuinely self-made community based on mutual care, Bounderby demonstrates that the self-made man is a character in a story — and not a true one.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: His wife, some thirty years his junior. Bounderby treats the marriage as he treats all transactions — as a demonstration of his own success. He never pretends to love her nor inquires whether she loves him. When she leaves him, his response is not grief but injured pride. (PN: 015-060–015-066, 037-057–037-063)
- **[[Mrs-Pegler]]**: His mother, whom he has paid to stay away. Mrs. Pegler secretly visits Coketown once a year to glimpse him from a distance. Her revelation in Book III — that she raised him with love and sacrifice, that he was never abandoned — destroys Bounderby's public identity at a stroke. (PN: 033-187–033-216)
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: His "bosom friend" — two men "perfectly devoid of sentiment" in "spiritual relationship" (PN: 004-003–004-006). Gradgrind supplies the intellectual apparatus for Bounderby's practical cruelty; Bounderby supplies the cash and the brute force that make Gradgrind's theories real. Their alliance is the head-and-fist of industrial Utilitarianism.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Bounderby's antithesis — the honest working man whom Bounderby ostracises for refusing to serve as his informant. Where Bounderby's poverty is a theatrical lie, Stephen's is real and unperformed. (PN: 011-050–011-058)
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]**: His housekeeper, a decayed aristocrat whose class pride mirrors Bounderby's in inverse. She imagines herself Bounderby's future wife and works to expose Louisa, only to become the instrument of Bounderby's own exposure when she drags Mrs. Pegler into the house. (PN: 033-134–033-143)
