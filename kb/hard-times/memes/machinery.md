# Machinery

In *Hard Times*, **machinery** is both a literal fact of Coketown's industrial landscape and a governing metaphor for the philosophical system that reduces human beings to interchangeable parts. Dickens's critique operates on both levels: the physical machinery of the factory and the intellectual machinery of Gradgrind's educational method are presented as manifestations of the same dehumanising logic.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

From the novel's opening chapters, machinery is established as the dominant image of Coketown life. The town itself is described as a place "of machinery and tall chimneys, out of which interminable serpents of smoke trailed themselves for ever and ever" (PN: 005-009). The machinery is not merely the background to human life — it has become the organising principle of existence.

The metaphor extends inward: human beings are described in mechanical terms. Mrs. Gradgrind, in her final illness, is said to have been "turned round monotonously like a piece of machinery" (PN: 009-046). The stoppage of the factory machinery creates a silence that is itself oppressive — the workers have been so conditioned that the absence of mechanical noise feels unnatural (PN: 010-044).

Coketown itself functions as one vast machine: "Time went on in Coketown like its own machinery: so much material wrought up, so much fuel consumed, so many powers worn out" (PN: 014-002). The sentence's structure — three parallel clauses, each beginning with "so much" — performs the mechanical repetition it describes.

### Key Passages

> "Time went on in Coketown like its own machinery: so much material wrought up, so much fuel consumed, so many powers worn out." (PN: 014-002)

The accounting language ("so much... so much... so many") reduces human life to a balance-sheet of inputs and outputs. Dickens's irony is that "powers worn out" includes human beings — the workers whose health, youth, and hope are consumed as fuel for the industrial machine.

> Stephen Blackpool "felt amid the jarring of the machinery" the indifference of a system that had no place for an individual workman's troubles (PN: 012-115).

## Embodied By

- **[[Coketown]]**: The town is the novel's supreme image of mechanised existence — a place where even time moves "like its own machinery."
- **[[Stephen Blackpool]]**: The factory hand who is caught in the machinery of both industrial labour and matrimonial law, unable to escape either.
- **[[Thomas Gradgrind]]**: His educational "system" is intellectual machinery — designed to process children into identical products, "like so many pianoforte legs" (PN: 002-170).

## Contrasted With

- **[[Fancy]]**: The imaginative faculty that machinery cannot accommodate — Sissy Jupe's stories and Sleary's circus represent life outside the mechanical.
- **[[Nature]]**: The green world that Coketown's machinery has obliterated; even the canal runs black with dye.
