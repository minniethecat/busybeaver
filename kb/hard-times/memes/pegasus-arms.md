# Pegasus's Arms

The Pegasus's Arms is the public house where the circus folk of Sleary's Horse-Riding lodge when they are in Coketown, and where Sissy Jupe lived with her father, the dog-trainer, before his desertion. Its signboard — a painting of the winged horse of Greek myth — is the novel's single object of ornament, beauty, and fantasy: the counter-image to Gradgrind's "Fact, fact, fact."

## Geography

- **Type**: public house / inn
- **Location**: Coketown, in the vicinity of Sleary's Circus
- **Real-world Model**: unknown
- **First Mention**: Book I Chapter 5 — Sissy Jupe leads Mr. Gradgrind and Mr. Bounderby through Coketown to "the door of a mean little public-house, with dim red lights in it. As haggard and as shabby, as if, for want of custom, it had itself taken to drinking, and had gone the way all drunkards go, and was very near the end of it" (PN: 005-177–005-181)

## Description

Dickens renders the Pegasus's Arms in two contrasted registers: exterior squalor and interior wonder. The approach is grim — a mean, shabby house that looks as if it has itself "taken to drinking" (PN: 005-177–005-181) — but the interior holds the novel's most sustained description of an image as beauty.

### The Sign-Board

The sign-board is the novel's most important painted image. Dickens's narrative voice — habitually ironic, even about objects — drops its guard: "THE name of the public-house was the Pegasus's Arms. The Pegasus's legs might have been more to the purpose; but, underneath the winged horse upon the sign-board, the Pegasus's Arms was inscribed in Roman letters" (PN: 006-002–006-004). The quip about the legs is affectionate, not dismissive — a painter's joke between Dickens and his reader. Beneath the name, "in a flowing scroll, the painter had touched off the lines":

> Good malt makes good beer,
> Walk in, and they'll draw it here;
> Good wine makes good brandy,
> Give us a call, and you'll find it handy. (PN: 006-005–006-010)

The doggerel verse is part of the sign's democratic aesthetic — not high art but popular art, the art of the public house that welcomes all comers.

### The Theatrical Pegasus

Inside, behind the "dingy little bar," hangs a second Pegasus: "a theatrical one — with real gauze let in for his wings, golden stars stuck on all over him, and his ethereal harness made of red silk" (PN: 006-011–006-013). This is the Pegasus of the circus, not of classical myth — made of theatrical materials, painted on glass, embellished with tinsel and fabric. The detail "real gauze let in for his wings" captures the resourcefulness of the circus aesthetic: it creates the illusion of flight from the humblest materials. The contrast with the sign-board Pegasus — painted wood and Roman letters — establishes the inn as a layered space, where art multiplies rather than repeats.

### The Interior

The inn is "mean" and "shabbily furnished" (PN: 006-023–006-024). Dickens's details are precise: "steep corner-stairs" (PN: 006-017), a "battered and mangy old hair trunk" (PN: 006-036), Sissy's room with "a bed in it" and Signor Jupe's "white night-cap, embellished with two peacock's feathers and a pigtail bolt upright" hanging on a nail (PN: 006-023–006-028). The peacock feathers — theatrical costume hung on a nail — are the inn's characteristic detail: beauty improvised from poverty, the circus's aesthetic of making something from nothing.

### The Missing Dog

Merrylegs, the performing dog, is conspicuously absent. "As to Merrylegs, that respectable ancestor of the highly trained animal who went aboard the ark, might have been accidentally shut out of it, for any sign of a dog that was manifest to eye or ear in the Pegasus's Arms" (PN: 006-030–006-032). The dog's silence is the first sign of Sissy's father's desertion — the trained creature has followed its trainer into disappearance.

## Symbolic Function

The Pegasus's Arms is the novel's symbolic counter-space — the place where Fancy lodges, literally, within Coketown. Its name is the novel's thesis in miniature: even in Coketown, there is (barely) a place where the winged horse of poetry finds an "arm" of shelter. Pegasus — the winged horse of Greek myth, born from the blood of Medusa, tamed by Bellerophon, associated with poetic inspiration — is the one figure from classical mythology that enters the novel, and he enters through a pub sign.

### The Sign as Counter-Argument

The sign-board is the novel's visual counter-argument to Gradgrind's "Fact, fact, fact!" It is a painted image — a work of imagination — in public view, on a Coketown street, and nobody can make it stop being what it is. Dickens's comment that Gradgrind and Bounderby "received no offence from these idealities" because it was too dark to see the sign (PN: 006-014–006-016) is one of the novel's sharpest ironies: the two apostles of Fact are shielded from offence by the mere absence of light. The image remains, waiting for daylight — and for readers who can see it.

### Sanctuary of the Imagination

The inn is the one genuinely classless space in the novel. Workers, performers, travellers, Members of Parliament, and bankers all pass through its doors. Gradgrind and Bounderby climb its "steep corner-stairs" (PN: 006-017) — the language of physical awkwardness marks their intrusion into a space their system cannot account for. The Pegasus's Arms is a house of democratic assembly, where the only credential is the price of a drink.

### Pegasus as Symbol

Pegasus embodies poetry, flight, imagination, myth — everything Coketown is not. That his image presides over a public house rather than a temple is Dickens's point: in the industrial age, the imagination survives not in institutions of culture but in the popular, the marginal, the disreputable. The winged horse belongs to the circus and the pub, not to the school and the bank.

## Key Events

- **Sissy Jupe leads Gradgrind and Bounderby to the inn**: Sissy brings the two apostles of Fact to her father's lodging, initiating the novel's central collision between the world of Facts and the world of Fancy (PN: 005-177–005-181)
- **Gradgrind and Bounderby search for Sissy's father**: The two gentlemen climb the steep stairs, enter Sissy's mean little room, and discover Signor Jupe's abandonment — the white night-cap with peacock feathers hanging on a nail, the empty trunk, the silent dog (PN: 006-017–006-041)
- **Sissy discovers her father has fled**: Sissy searches the rooms above, finds the trunk empty, and realises her father has gone to the circus booth — and from there, has disappeared entirely (PN: 006-033–006-041)

## Associated Characters

- [[Sissy-Jupe]] — lived here with her father; the inn is her childhood home and the space of the father-love that becomes her moral foundation
- [[Sleary]] — the circus proprietor who lodges here with his troupe; the inn is his "headquarters" in Coketown
- **Sissy's Father (Signor Jupe, the dog-trainer)** — the unnamed circus performer who abandons Sissy from this room, leaving his theatrical night-cap on a nail as the only trace of his presence
- [[Thomas-Gradgrind]] — intrudes into this space of Fancy and is shielded from its "idealities" only by darkness
- [[Josiah-Bounderby]] — accompanies Gradgrind and delivers his characteristic dismissal: "Merrylegs and nine oils, eh! Pretty well this, for a self-made man!" (PN: 005-185)

## In the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

The Pegasus's Arms appears in Chapters 5–6 of Book I, during Gradgrind and Bounderby's search for Sissy Jupe after her father's desertion. The extended description of the inn — the sign-board, the theatrical Pegasus, the steep stairs, the mean little room — occupies the opening of Chapter 6 ("Sleary's Horsemanship") and constitutes the novel's first sustained treatment of a space defined by Fancy rather than Fact. The chapter's title is itself a joke: "Sleary's Horsemanship" refers not to the circus-master but to the painted winged horse on the inn's sign-board. (PN: 006-002–006-051)

### Book II: Reaping

The Pegasus's Arms does not appear in Book II. The circus has left Coketown, and the world of Fancy is suppressed — the absence of the inn from the narrative is structural, marking the triumph of the Fact-system during the novel's middle movement.

### Book III: Garnering

The inn does not reappear in Book III; the circus has moved on from Coketown entirely. But the Pegasus figure returns, transmuted: when Sissy orchestrates Tom's escape through Sleary's company, the circus itself has become the winged horse — a vehicle of rescue and flight. The inn's symbolic work is complete: it established, in Book I, the possibility that imagination could survive in Coketown, and Book III proves that possibility by showing the circus community — the extended family of the Pegasus's Arms — saving a Gradgrind from the consequences of the Gradgrind system.
