[005-001] THE KEYNOTE

[005-002] COKETOWN, to which Messrs. [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]] and [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] now walked, was a

[005-003] triumph of [[facts|fact]]; it had no greater taint of [[fancy]] in it than Mrs.

[005-004] [[thomas-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] herself. Let us strike the key-note, [[coketown|Coketown]], before pursuing

[005-005] our tune.

[005-006] It was a town of red brick, or of brick that would have been red if the

[005-007] smoke and ashes had allowed it; but as matters stood, it was a town of

[005-008] [[nature|unnatural]] red and black like the painted face of a savage. It was a town

[005-009] of machinery and tall chimneys, out of which interminable serpents of

[005-010] smoke trailed themselves for ever and ever, and never got uncoiled. It

[005-011] had a black canal in it, and a river that ran purple with ill-smelling

[005-012] dye, and vast piles of building full of windows where there was a

[005-013] rattling and a trembling all day long, and where the piston of the

[005-014] steam-engine worked monotonously up and down, like the [[reason|head]] of an

[005-015] elephant in a state of melancholy madness. It contained several large

[005-016] streets all very like one another, and many small streets still more like

[005-017] one another, inhabited by people equally like one another, who all went

[005-018] in and out at the same hours, with the same sound upon the same

[005-019] pavements, to do the same work, and to whom every day was the same as

[005-020] yesterday and to-morrow, and every year the counterpart of the last and

[005-021] the next.

[005-022] These attributes of Coketown were in the main inseparable from the work

[005-023] by which it was sustained; against them were to be set off, comforts of

[005-024] life which found their way all over the world, and elegancies of life

[005-025] which made, we will not ask how much of the fine lady, who could scarcely

[005-026] bear to hear the place mentioned. The rest of its features were

[005-027] voluntary, and they were these.

[005-028] You saw nothing in Coketown but what was severely workful. If the

[005-029] members of a religious persuasion built a chapel there—as the members of

[005-030] eighteen religious persuasions had done—they made it a pious warehouse of

[005-031] red brick, with sometimes (but this is only in highly ornamental

[005-032] examples) a bell in a birdcage on the top of it. The solitary exception

[005-033] was the New Church; a stuccoed edifice with a square steeple over the

[005-034] door, terminating in four short pinnacles like florid wooden legs. All

[005-035] the public inscriptions in the town were painted alike, in severe

[005-036] characters of black and white. The jail might have been the infirmary,

[005-037] the infirmary might have been the jail, the town-hall might have been

[005-038] either, or both, or anything else, for anything that appeared to the

[005-039] contrary in the graces of their construction. Fact, fact, fact,

[005-040] everywhere in the material aspect of the town; fact, fact, fact,

[005-041] everywhere in the immaterial. The M’Choakumchild school was all fact,

[005-042] and the school of design was all fact, and the relations between master

[005-043] and man were all fact, and everything was fact between the lying-in

[005-044] hospital and the cemetery, and what you couldn’t state in figures, or

[005-045] [[performance|show]] to be purchaseable in the cheapest market and saleable in the

[005-046] dearest, was not, and never should be, world without end, Amen.

[005-047] A town so sacred to fact, and so triumphant in its assertion, of course

[005-048] got on well? Why no, not quite well. No? Dear me!

[005-049] No. Coketown did not come out of its own furnaces, in all respects like

[005-050] gold that had stood the fire. First, the perplexing mystery of the place

[005-051] was, Who belonged to the eighteen denominations? Because, whoever did,

[005-052] the labouring people did not. It was very strange to walk through the

[005-053] streets on a Sunday morning, and note how few of _them_ the barbarous

[005-054] jangling of bells that was driving the sick and nervous mad, called away

[005-055] from their own quarter, from their own close rooms, from the corners of

[005-056] their own streets, where they lounged listlessly, gazing at all the

[005-057] church and chapel going, as at a thing with which they had no manner of

[005-058] concern. Nor was it merely the stranger who noticed this, because there

[005-059] was a native organization in Coketown itself, whose members were to be

[005-060] heard of in the House of Commons every session, indignantly petitioning

[005-061] for acts of parliament that should make these people religious by main

[005-062] force. Then came the Teetotal Society, who complained that these same

[005-063] people _would_ get drunk, and showed in tabular statements that they did

[005-064] get drunk, and proved at tea parties that no inducement, human or Divine

[005-065] (except a medal), would induce them to forego their custom of getting

[005-066] drunk. Then came the chemist and druggist, with other tabular

[005-067] statements, showing that when they didn’t get drunk, they took opium.

[005-068] Then came the experienced chaplain of the jail, with more tabular

[005-069] statements, outdoing all the previous tabular statements, and showing

[005-070] that the same people _would_ resort to low haunts, hidden from the public

[005-071] eye, where they heard low singing and saw low dancing, and mayhap joined

[005-072] in it; and where A. B., aged twenty-four next birthday, and committed for

[005-073] eighteen months’ solitary, had himself said (not that he had ever shown

[005-074] himself particularly worthy of belief) his ruin began, as he was

[005-075] perfectly sure and confident that otherwise he would have been a tip-top

[005-076] moral specimen. Then came Mr. Gradgrind and Mr. Bounderby, the two

[005-077] [[gentleman|gentlemen]] at this present moment walking through Coketown, and both

[005-078] eminently practical, who could, on occasion, furnish more tabular

[005-079] statements derived from their own personal [[experience]], and illustrated by

[005-080] cases they had known and seen, from which it clearly appeared—in short,

[005-081] it was the only clear thing in the case—that these same people were a bad

[005-082] lot altogether, gentlemen; that do what you would for them they were

[005-083] never thankful for it, gentlemen; that they were restless, gentlemen;

[005-084] that they never knew what they wanted; that they lived upon the best, and

[005-085] bought fresh butter; and insisted on Mocha coffee, and rejected all but

[005-086] prime parts of meat, and yet were eternally dissatisfied and

[005-087] unmanageable. In short, it was the moral of the old nursery fable:

[005-088] There was an old woman, and what do you think?

[005-089] She lived upon nothing but victuals and drink;

[005-090] Victuals and drink were the whole of her diet,

[005-091] And yet this old woman would NEVER be quiet.

[005-092] Is it possible, I wonder, that there was any analogy between the case of

[005-093] the Coketown population and the case of the little Gradgrinds? Surely,

[005-094] none of us in our sober senses and acquainted with figures, are to be

[005-095] told at this time of day, that one of the foremost elements in the

[005-096] existence of the Coketown working-people had been for scores of years,

[005-097] deliberately set at nought? That there was any Fancy in them demanding

[005-098] to be brought into healthy existence instead of struggling on in

[005-099] convulsions? That exactly in the ratio as they worked long and

[005-100] monotonously, the craving grew within them for some physical relief—some

[005-101] relaxation, encouraging good humour and good spirits, and giving them a

[005-102] vent—some recognized holiday, though it were but for an honest dance to a

[005-103] stirring band of music—some occasional light pie in which even

[005-104] M’Choakumchild had no finger—which craving must and would be satisfied

[005-105] aright, or must and would inevitably go wrong, until the laws of the

[005-106] Creation were repealed?

[005-107] ‘This man lives at Pod’s End, and I don’t quite know Pod’s End,’ said Mr.

[005-108] Gradgrind. ‘Which is it, Bounderby?’

[005-109] Mr. Bounderby knew it was somewhere down town, but knew no more

[005-110] respecting it. So they stopped for a moment, looking about.

[005-111] Almost as they did so, there came running round the corner of the street

[005-112] at a quick pace and with a frightened look, a girl whom Mr. Gradgrind

[005-113] recognized. ‘Halloa!’ said he. ‘Stop! Where are you going! Stop!’

[005-114] Girl number twenty stopped then, palpitating, and made him a curtsey.

[005-115] ‘Why are you tearing about the streets,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘in this

[005-116] improper manner?’

[005-117] ‘I was—I was run after, sir,’ the girl panted, ‘and I wanted to get

[005-118] away.’

[005-119] ‘Run after?’ repeated Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Who would run after _you_?’

[005-120] The question was unexpectedly and suddenly answered for her, by the

[005-121] colourless boy, [[bitzer|Bitzer]], who came round the corner with such blind speed

[005-122] and so little anticipating a stoppage on the pavement, that he brought

[005-123] himself up against Mr. Gradgrind’s waistcoat and rebounded into the road.

[005-124] ‘What do you mean, boy?’ said Mr. Gradgrind. ‘What are you doing? How

[005-125] dare you dash against—everybody—in this manner?’ Bitzer picked up his

[005-126] cap, which the concussion had knocked off; and backing, and knuckling his

[005-127] forehead, pleaded that it was an accident.

[005-128] ‘Was this boy running after you, [[sissy-jupe|Jupe]]?’ asked Mr. Gradgrind.

[005-129] ‘Yes, sir,’ said the girl reluctantly.

[005-130] ‘No, I wasn’t, sir!’ cried Bitzer. ‘Not till she run away from me. But

[005-131] the horse-riders never mind what they say, sir; they’re famous for it.

[005-132] You know the horse-riders are famous for never minding what they say,’

[005-133] addressing Sissy. ‘It’s as well known in the town as—please, sir, as the

[005-134] multiplication table isn’t known to the horse-riders.’ Bitzer tried Mr.

[005-135] Bounderby with this.

[005-136] ‘He frightened me so,’ said the girl, ‘with his cruel faces!’

[005-137] ‘Oh!’ cried Bitzer. ‘Oh! An’t you one of the rest! An’t you a

[005-138] horse-rider! I never looked at her, sir. I asked her if she would know

[005-139] how to define a horse to-morrow, and offered to tell her again, and she

[005-140] ran away, and I ran after her, sir, that she might know how to answer

[005-141] when she was asked. You wouldn’t have thought of saying such mischief if

[005-142] you hadn’t been a horse-rider?’

[005-143] ‘Her calling seems to be pretty well known among ’em,’ observed Mr.

[005-144] Bounderby. ‘You’d have had the whole school peeping in a row, in a

[005-145] week.’

[005-146] ‘Truly, I think so,’ returned his friend. ‘Bitzer, turn you about and

[005-147] take yourself home. Jupe, stay here a moment. Let me hear of your

[005-148] running in this manner any more, boy, and you will hear of me through the

[005-149] master of the school. You understand what I mean. Go along.’

[005-150] The boy stopped in his rapid blinking, knuckled his forehead again,

[005-151] glanced at Sissy, turned about, and retreated.

[005-152] ‘Now, girl,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, ‘take this gentleman and me to your

[005-153] father’s; we are going there. What have you got in that bottle you are

[005-154] carrying?’

[005-155] ‘Gin,’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[005-156] ‘Dear, no, sir! It’s the nine oils.’

[005-157] ‘The what?’ cried Mr. Bounderby.

[005-158] ‘The nine oils, sir, to rub father with.’

[005-159] ‘Then,’ said Mr. Bounderby, with a loud short laugh, ‘what the devil do

[005-160] you rub your father with nine oils for?’

[005-161] ‘It’s what our people aways use, sir, when they get any hurts in the

[005-162] ring,’ replied the girl, looking over her shoulder, to assure herself

[005-163] that her pursuer was gone. ‘They bruise themselves very bad sometimes.’

[005-164] ‘Serve ’em right,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘for being idle.’ She glanced up

[005-165] at his face, with mingled astonishment and dread.

[005-166] ‘By George!’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘when I was four or five years younger

[005-167] than you, I had worse bruises upon me than ten oils, twenty oils, forty

[005-168] oils, would have rubbed off. I didn’t get ’em by posture-making, but by

[005-169] being banged about. There was no rope-dancing for me; I danced on the

[005-170] bare ground and was larruped with the rope.’

[005-171] Mr. Gradgrind, though hard enough, was by no means so rough a man as Mr.

[005-172] Bounderby. His character was not unkind, all things considered; it might

[005-173] have been a very kind one indeed, if he had only made some round mistake

[005-174] in the arithmetic that balanced it, years ago. He said, in what he meant

[005-175] for a reassuring tone, as they turned down a narrow road, ‘And this is

[005-176] Pod’s End; is it, Jupe?’

[005-177] ‘This is it, sir, and—if you wouldn’t mind, sir—this is the house.’

[005-178] She stopped, at twilight, at the door of a mean little public-house, with

[005-179] dim red lights in it. As haggard and as shabby, as if, for want of

[005-180] custom, it had itself taken to drinking, and had gone the way all

[005-181] drunkards go, and was very near the end of it.

[005-182] ‘It’s only crossing the bar, sir, and up the stairs, if you wouldn’t

[005-183] mind, and waiting there for a moment till I get a candle. If you should

[005-184] hear a dog, sir, it’s only Merrylegs, and he only barks.’

[005-185] ‘Merrylegs and nine oils, eh!’ said Mr. Bounderby, entering last with his

[005-186] metallic laugh. ‘Pretty well this, for a self-made man!’
