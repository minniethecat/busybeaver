# Rachael

Rachael is the moral heroine of *Hard Times* — the working-class woman whose quiet truthfulness and radical selflessness embody the novel's deepest ethical proposition: that goodness is possible even in the crushing conditions of industrial poverty. Where Stephen Blackpool is the novel's tragic centre, Rachael is its moral north star — the character whose steady integrity never wavers and whose care for Stephen's alcoholic wife is the most luminous instance of selfless love in the novel.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Rachael (no surname given)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "Rachael, lass" (Stephen's habitual address); "Rachael, beloved lass" (Stephen's dying words, PN: 034-308); "a working woman, christened Rachael" (narrator, PN: 037-135)
- **Role**: Moral heroine (secondary); Stephen Blackpool's spiritual partner; embodiment of working-class female virtue
- **Occupation**: Factory hand, power-loom weaver at Bounderby's mill, the same mill where Stephen works
- **Family**: None specified. Her de facto family is Stephen Blackpool, and — through an act of radical Christian charity — the unnamed alcoholic wife she nurses
- **First Appearance**: Book I, Chapter 10 — walking home from the mill with Stephen, introduced under the brightness of a lamp: "a quiet oval face, dark and rather delicate, irradiated by a pair of very gentle eyes" (PN: 010-061–010-064)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III, Chapter 9 — in the novel's closing vision, "a working woman, christened Rachael… a woman of pensive beauty, always dressed in black, but sweet-tempered and serene" (PN: 037-135–037-138)

## Characterisation

Dickens presents Rachael as the novel's most morally transparent character — someone whose inner goodness is immediately visible in her physical bearing and whose speech, marked by the gentle authority of someone who has nothing to hide, is the working-class counterpoint to Bounderby's lies, Harthouse's cynicism, and Gradgrind's facts-without-feeling. The narrator's presentation of her consistently draws on images of light, quietness, and steadfastness. She is not the character who drives the plot but the character who anchors it, the fixed moral point around which the novel's turbulence revolves.

### Physical Description

Rachael's introduction in Chapter 10 is one of Dickens's most deliberately luminous portraits. Turning "in the brightness of a lamp" and raising her hood, she reveals "a quiet oval face, dark and rather delicate, irradiated by a pair of very gentle eyes, and further set off by the perfect order of her shining black hair. It was not a face in its first bloom; she was a woman five and thirty years of age" (PN: 010-061–010-065). The vocabulary — quiet, delicate, irradiated, gentle, perfect order — establishes her as a figure of contained radiance, the inner light made visible.

Her physical modesty is rendered as moral substance. She walks with "her neat figure and her sober womanly step" (PN: 010-115), and the narrator's observation that "there was not a flutter of her coarse shawl, perhaps, but had its interest in this man's eyes" (PN: 010-117–010-118) registers her presence not through elaborate description but through Stephen's attentive gaze: her worth is something you see if you are capable of seeing.

In the novel's final vision, she appears as "a woman of pensive beauty, always dressed in black, but sweet-tempered and serene, and even cheerful" (PN: 037-137–037-139). The black dress — mourning for Stephen — has become her permanent condition, but the grief does not extinguish the light; it deepens it.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Rachael speaks a milder Lancashire dialect than Stephen — less phonetically marked, but identifiably of the same region and class. Her characteristic speech acts are not assertions but gentle redirections: "Try to think not; and 'twill seem better" (PN: 010-090); "Never fret about them, Stephen. Let the laws be" (PN: 010-096–010-097). She does not argue; she steadies.

Her most important utterance is the moral principle she names and by which she lives: "we're such old friends, and t' hide a word of honest truth fro' one another would be a sin and a pity" (PN: 010-085–010-086). The sentence has the force of a credo, and its pairing of "sin" and "pity" — the theological and the emotional — is characteristic of Rachael's particular moral idiom: truth is both a duty and a kindness.

Her characteristic gesture is the "gentle touch upon his arm" (PN: 010-100), a physical correlative of her moral function: she does not command, she steadies. Stephen's acknowledgement — "thy word is a law to me. Ah, lass, and a bright good law! Better than some real ones" (PN: 010-094–010-095) — names the paradox at the heart of her character: the woman without legal power is the novel's truest lawgiver.

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Rachael is introduced in Chapter 10 as the object of Stephen's waiting and the subject of his devotion. Their walk home through "the hardest working part of Coketown" (PN: 010-005) establishes the emotional coordinates of Stephen's world: Rachael is the light in the industrial darkness, the companion whose presence makes the "muddle" bearable.

The chapter establishes the central impediment to their happiness: Stephen is married to a woman "who had taken to drinking, left off working, sold the furniture, pawned the clothes" (recounted in PN: 011-091) and cannot divorce her because the law requires an Act of Parliament — accessible only to the wealthy. Rachael's response to this structural injustice is neither complaint nor despair but the ethic of truthfulness she names: hiding truth from one another "would be a sin and a pity" (PN: 010-085–010-086).

In Chapter 13, an unnamed worker (implied to be Stephen in the surrounding narration) reflects on Rachael's place in his history: "No word of a new marriage had ever passed between them; but Rachael had taken the place in his life that a wife would occupy" (PN: 012-135, paraphrased). The novel makes clear that their bond is a marriage in all but law — a fact that the law's inaccessibility renders both a comfort and a torment.

### Book II: Reaping

Book II deepens Rachael's role from companion to moral anchor. Her most radical act of love — one that Dickens intends as the novel's most powerful example of Christian charity — is her care for Stephen's alcoholic wife. Chapter 13 of Book I presents the scene in full: Stephen comes home to find Rachael sitting vigil by the bed of his wife, who lies in a stupor brought on by drink. Rachael has put up a curtain to screen the sick woman, and "everything was in its place and order" — the domestic order Rachael has improvised in a household not her own (PN: 013-015–013-023). Stephen, looking at her through the dim candlelight, sees her as though "she had a glory shining round her head. He could have believed she had. He did believe it" (PN: 013-101–013-102).

The scene escalates into the novel's most intense domestic drama. The wife wakes, reaches for a bottle of poison, and is about to drink when Rachael starts from sleep and wrestles the cup from her — "the creature struggled, struck her, seized her by the hair; but Rachael had the cup" (PN: 013-188–013-189). Rachael then lies to Stephen about what happened — "I have been asleep, myself. 'Tis near three. I hear the bells" (PN: 013-192) — a lie told not in self-interest but in self-effacement, to spare Stephen the horror of what he has witnessed and to protect the wife from his knowledge. The lie is, paradoxically, the truth of her character: she will do whatever protects the people she loves, including the woman who stands between her and happiness.

In Chapter 22 (Book II Chapter 6), Rachael accompanies Louisa to Stephen's room. Stephen, who has been ostracised by the union and fired by Bounderby, tells Rachael he is "more leetsome" — and the narrator vouches: "So he said, with truth" (PN: 022-070). It is Rachael who, when Stephen mentions his promise not to join the union, "burst into tears. 'I didn't seek it of him, poor lad. I prayed him to join. But he wouldn't be troubled in his mind'" (PN: 022-201). Her tears confirm what the narrative has intimated: her word is not a command but a gift Stephen has freely chosen to honour.

### Book III: Garnering

With Stephen framed for bank robbery and absent from Coketown, Rachael becomes his advocate. In Chapter 32, she confronts Louisa and Bounderby at the Gradgrind house, demanding to know why Stephen has been accused. Her speech to Louisa — "Stephen Blackpool is now named as a thief in the public prints, and brought here and there to be made a show of" (PN: 032-141–032-142) — is the novel's most direct instance of working-class speech entering the drawing rooms of the powerful and demanding an answer. When she weeps, it is not weakness but the moral force of someone who loves the man being destroyed by rumour: "And when I think that the poor lad was so true, and that his being true cuts him so" (PN: 032-156–032-157).

In Chapter 33, Sissy visits Rachael nightly in her lodging, the two women forming an alliance across class lines: "Every night, Sissy went to Rachael's lodging, and sat with her in her small quiet room. All day, Rachael toiled as such people must toil" (PN: 033-004–033-006). The pairing of Sissy and Rachael — the circus child and the factory hand, both moral heroines, both defined by truthfulness — is the novel's most sustained image of cross-class solidarity.

In Chapter 34, Rachael is present at Stephen's death in the Old Hell Shaft. Her discovery of his fallen hat in the ditch — "Rachael had already started forward and was running down the bank" (PN: 034-042) — and her scream that "resounded over the wide landscape" (PN: 034-059) register a grief so profound it is rendered as physical force. She kneels at Stephen's side as he dies, holding his hand, and receives his final words: "Rachael, beloved lass! Don't let go of my hand. I may fall without that hand" (PN: 034-308). The novel's closing vision, in Chapter 37, returns to Rachael once more: "A working woman, christened Rachael… a woman of pensive beauty, always dressed in black, but sweet-tempered and serene, and even cheerful" (PN: 037-135–037-138). She continues to work at the factory, and the vision includes her continued compassion for "a degraded, drunken wretch of her own sex" — Stephen's widow, presumably — "who was sometimes seen in the town secretly begging of her, and crying to her" (PN: 037-140–037-141). Rachael's charity has outlived Stephen. She is "a woman working, ever working, but content to do it, and preferring to do it as her natural lot, until she should be too old to labour any more" (PN: 037-142–037-143).

## Thematic Significance

Rachael embodies the novel's argument that **truth** is not a fact but a practice — a bond between persons rather than a correspondence between proposition and reality. Her great line — "t' hide a word of honest truth fro' one another would be a sin and a pity" (PN: 010-085–010-086) — names an ethic of transparency that stands in direct opposition to the novel's entire apparatus of deceit: Bounderby's self-made-man myth, Harthouse's cynical indirection, Tom's scheme to frame Stephen, Mrs. Sparsit's manipulative surveillance. In a novel where nearly every character in a position of power is living some form of lie, Rachael's truthfulness is political as well as personal.

She also embodies the novel's critique of **class** as a moral category. Rachael possesses virtues — truthfulness, charity, patience, practical care — that the novel's wealthy characters claim but do not practise. Bounderby preaches self-help and hard work; Rachael lives them. Gradgrind preaches the primacy of reason; Rachael demonstrates a practical wisdom that his system cannot account for. The novel's distribution of moral intelligence is radically democratic: the factory hand who cannot read knows more about love, loyalty, and truth than any graduate of Gradgrind's school.

Her care for Stephen's wife is the novel's deepest single instance of **selfless love** — a love that acts not for reward or reciprocation but because the loved object is in need. That the object of her care is the woman who blocks her own happiness makes the act not merely charitable but structurally radical: Rachael loves her enemy. The Christian echo is deliberate and Dickens means it to be the standard against which all other claims to morality in the novel are measured.

**Contrasted with**: [[Louisa-Gradgrind]] — Louisa's emotional starvation, produced by a childhood without love, stands against Rachael's emotional abundance, produced by a life of care despite material poverty. The novel suggests that love is not a function of comfort but of choice, and Rachael chooses it daily.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Rachael's spiritual partner, the man she loves and cannot marry. Their bond is founded on mutual truth-telling — "t' hide a word of honest truth fro' one another would be a sin and a pity" (PN: 010-085–010-086) — and their relationship is the novel's most sustained instance of moral love, a bond based not on desire or social contract but on shared integrity. Stephen calls her word "a bright good law! Better than some real ones" (PN: 010-094–010-095). Rachael is his conscience, his comfort, and, after his death, the keeper of his memory. His dying words are "Rachael, beloved lass!" (PN: 034-308)
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]'s wife (unnamed)**: The alcoholic woman whose existence traps Stephen in a marriage he cannot legally escape, and therefore the one person who stands between Rachael and her own happiness. Rachael's care for this woman — nursing her in illness, saving her from suicide, protecting her from Stephen's knowledge of the attempt — is the novel's most radical act of charity. She loves the woman who is, in worldly terms, her enemy
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: The cross-class recognition between Rachael and Louisa is one of the novel's brief, luminous moments of solidarity. When Louisa visits Stephen's room with Rachael (Book II, Chapter 6), she addresses Rachael with a respect she accords no other working-class character: "she now instinctively addressed herself to Rachael" (PN: 022-181). Their shared concern for Stephen creates a temporary alliance across the class divide that neither Bounderby's rhetoric nor Gradgrind's facts could produce
- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: The novel's two moral heroines, allied in Book III through their shared love for the absent Stephen. Sissy visits Rachael nightly (PN: 033-004), and the two discover Stephen's fallen hat together in the ditch near the Old Hell Shaft. They represent complementary modes of moral action: Sissy the imaginative saviour, Rachael the steady, truth-telling guardian
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Rachael's antagonist and the embodiment of everything she is not. Where she tells the truth, he lies about his origins. Where she cares for the helpless, he exploits them. Her presence in the drawing room at Stone Lodge (Chapter 32), demanding answers about Stephen, is a direct challenge to Bounderby's authority — a working woman who refuses to be silenced
- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: The idle gentleman whose cynicism and moral indirection are the antitype of Rachael's transparent truthfulness. Harthouse navigates the world through charm; Rachael navigates it through honesty. The novel's implicit question is which weapon proves more durable

## Critical Reception

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), identified Rachael as part of Dickens's "radical tenderness" — a quality he saw as both the strength and limitation of Dickens's moral imagination. For Williams, Rachael's goodness is real but risks being merely symbolic: a working-class woman whose virtues are so complete that they bypass rather than engage the structural injustices that define her life.

> George Orwell, in his 1940 essay on Dickens, noted that Rachael belongs to a recurring Dickensian type — the woman whose goodness is presented as natural, almost pre-moral, a fact of her being rather than a product of struggle. Orwell was sceptical of such characters but acknowledged that they function as the novel's moral fixed points, the standards against which other characters' corruption is measured.

> Rosemarie Bodenheimer, in *Knowing Dickens* (2007), drew attention to the complexity of Rachael's lie to Stephen after the suicide attempt: Rachael's falsehood — "I have been asleep, myself" (PN: 013-192) — is presented as an act of love, a lie told to protect Stephen from the truth of what he has witnessed. This, Bodenheimer argued, complicates Rachael's identity as the novel's truth-teller: her commitment to truth is not rigid but relational, a function of love rather than of principle. Truth, for Rachael, is a practice of care, not a rule of logic.
