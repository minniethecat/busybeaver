[025-001] HEARING THE LAST OF IT

[025-002] MRS. SPARSIT, lying by to recover the tone of her nerves in Mr.

[025-003] [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]]’s retreat, kept such a sharp look-out, night and day, under her

[025-004] Coriolanian eyebrows, that her eyes, like a couple of lighthouses on an

[025-005] iron-bound coast, might have warned all prudent mariners from that bold

[025-006] rock her Roman nose and the dark and craggy region in its neighbourhood,

[025-007] but for the placidity of her manner. Although it was hard to believe

[025-008] that her retiring for the night could be anything but a form, so severely

[025-009] wide awake were those classical eyes of hers, and so impossible did it

[025-010] seem that her rigid nose could yield to any relaxing influence, yet her

[025-011] manner of sitting, smoothing her uncomfortable, not to say, gritty

[025-012] mittens (they were constructed of a cool fabric like a meat-safe), or of

[025-013] ambling to unknown places of destination with her foot in her cotton

[025-014] stirrup, was so perfectly serene, that most observers would have been

[025-015] constrained to suppose her a dove, embodied by some freak of [[nature]], in

[025-016] the earthly tabernacle of a bird of the hook-beaked order.

[025-017] She was a most wonderful woman for prowling about the house. How she got

[025-018] from story to story was a mystery beyond solution. A lady so decorous in

[025-019] herself, and so highly connected, was not to be suspected of dropping

[025-020] over the banisters or sliding down them, yet her extraordinary facility

[025-021] of locomotion suggested the wild idea. Another noticeable circumstance

[025-022] in [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] was, that she was never hurried. She would shoot with

[025-023] consummate velocity from the roof to the hall, yet would be in full

[025-024] possession of her breath and [[pride|dignity]] on the moment of her arrival there.

[025-025] Neither was she ever seen by human vision to go at a great pace.

[025-026] She took very kindly to Mr. [[james-harthouse|Harthouse]], and had some pleasant conversation

[025-027] with him soon after her arrival. She made him her stately curtsey in the

[025-028] garden, one morning before breakfast.

[025-029] ‘It appears but yesterday, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘that I had the

[025-030] honour of receiving you at the Bank, when you were so good as to wish to

[025-031] be made acquainted with Mr. Bounderby’s address.’

[025-032] ‘An occasion, I am sure, not to be forgotten by myself in the course of

[025-033] Ages,’ said Mr. Harthouse, inclining his [[reason|head]] to Mrs. Sparsit with the

[025-034] most indolent of all possible airs.

[025-035] ‘We live in a singular world, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit.

[025-036] ‘I have had the honour, by a coincidence of which I am proud, to have

[025-037] made a remark, similar in effect, though not so epigrammatically

[025-038] expressed.’

[025-039] ‘A singular world, I would say, sir,’ pursued Mrs. Sparsit; after

[025-040] acknowledging the compliment with a drooping of her dark eyebrows, not

[025-041] altogether so mild in its expression as her voice was in its dulcet

[025-042] tones; ‘as regards the intimacies we form at one time, with individuals

[025-043] we were quite ignorant of, at another. I recall, sir, that on that

[025-044] occasion you went so far as to say you were actually apprehensive of Miss

[025-045] [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]].’

[025-046] ‘Your memory does me more honour than my insignificance deserves. I

[025-047] availed myself of your obliging hints to correct my timidity, and it is

[025-048] unnecessary to add that they were perfectly accurate. Mrs. Sparsit’s

[025-049] talent for—in [[facts|fact]] for anything requiring accuracy—with a [[solidarity|combination]] of

[025-050] strength of mind—and [[family|Family]]—is too habitually developed to admit of any

[025-051] question.’ He was almost falling asleep over this compliment; it took

[025-052] him so long to get through, and his mind wandered so much in the course

[025-053] of its execution.

[025-054] ‘You found [[louisa-gradgrind|Miss Gradgrind]]—I really cannot call her Mrs. Bounderby; it’s

[025-055] very absurd of me—as youthful as I described her?’ asked Mrs. Sparsit,

[025-056] sweetly.

[025-057] ‘You drew her portrait perfectly,’ said Mr. Harthouse. ‘Presented her

[025-058] dead image.’

[025-059] ‘Very engaging, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, causing her mittens slowly to

[025-060] revolve over one another.

[025-061] ‘Highly so.’

[025-062] ‘It used to be considered,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘that Miss [[thomas-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] was

[025-063] wanting in animation, but I confess she appears to me considerably and

[025-064] strikingly improved in that respect. Ay, and indeed here _is_ Mr.

[025-065] Bounderby!’ cried Mrs. Sparsit, nodding her head a great many times, as

[025-066] if she had been talking and thinking of no one else. ‘How do you find

[025-067] yourself this morning, sir? Pray let us see you cheerful, sir.’

[025-068] Now, these persistent assuagements of his misery, and lightenings of his

[025-069] load, had by this time begun to have the effect of making Mr. Bounderby

[025-070] softer than usual towards Mrs. Sparsit, and harder than usual to most

[025-071] other people from his wife downward. So, when Mrs. Sparsit said with

[025-072] forced lightness of [[heart]], ‘You want your breakfast, sir, but I dare say

[025-073] Miss Gradgrind will soon be here to preside at the table,’ Mr. Bounderby

[025-074] replied, ‘If I waited to be taken care of by my wife, ma’am, I believe

[025-075] you know pretty well I should wait till Doomsday, so I’ll trouble _you_

[025-076] to take charge of the teapot.’ Mrs. Sparsit complied, and assumed her

[025-077] old position at table.

[025-078] This again made the excellent woman vastly sentimental. She was so

[025-079] humble withal, that when Louisa appeared, she rose, protesting she never

[025-080] could think of sitting in that place under existing circumstances, often

[025-081] as she had had the honour of making Mr. Bounderby’s breakfast, before

[025-082] Mrs. Gradgrind—she begged pardon, she meant to say Miss Bounderby—she

[025-083] hoped to be excused, but she really could not get it right yet, though

[025-084] she trusted to become familiar with it by and by—had assumed her present

[025-085] position. It was only (she observed) because Miss Gradgrind happened to

[025-086] be a little late, and Mr. Bounderby’s time was so very precious, and she

[025-087] knew it of old to be so essential that he should breakfast to the moment,

[025-088] that she had taken the liberty of complying with his request; long as his

[025-089] will had been a law to her.

[025-090] ‘There! Stop where you are, ma’am,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘stop where you

[025-091] are! Mrs. Bounderby will be very glad to be relieved of the trouble, I

[025-092] believe.’

[025-093] ‘Don’t say that, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, almost with severity,

[025-094] ‘because that is very unkind to Mrs. Bounderby. And to be unkind is not

[025-095] to be you, sir.’

[025-096] ‘You may set your mind at rest, ma’am.—You can take it very quietly,

[025-097] can’t you, Loo?’ said Mr. Bounderby, in a blustering way to his wife.

[025-098] ‘Of course. It is of no moment. Why should it be of any importance to

[025-099] me?’

[025-100] ‘Why should it be of any importance to any one, Mrs. Sparsit, ma’am?’

[025-101] said Mr. Bounderby, swelling with a sense of slight. ‘You attach too

[025-102] much importance to these things, ma’am. By George, you’ll be corrupted

[025-103] in some of your notions here. You are old-fashioned, ma’am. You are

[025-104] behind [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]]’s [[childhood|children]]’s time.’

[025-105] ‘What is the matter with you?’ asked Louisa, coldly surprised. ‘What has

[025-106] given you offence?’

[025-107] ‘Offence!’ repeated Bounderby. ‘Do you suppose if there was any offence

[025-108] given me, I shouldn’t name it, and request to have it corrected? I am a

[025-109] straightforward man, I believe. I don’t go beating about for

[025-110] side-winds.’

[025-111] ‘I suppose no one ever had occasion to think you too diffident, or too

[025-112] delicate,’ Louisa answered him composedly: ‘I have never made that

[025-113] objection to you, either as a child or as a woman. I don’t understand

[025-114] what you would have.’

[025-115] ‘Have?’ returned Mr. Bounderby. ‘Nothing. Otherwise, don’t you, Loo

[025-116] Bounderby, know thoroughly well that I, Josiah Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]],

[025-117] would have it?’

[025-118] She looked at him, as he struck the table and made the teacups ring, with

[025-119] a proud colour in her face that was a new change, Mr. Harthouse thought.

[025-120] ‘You are incomprehensible this morning,’ said Louisa. ‘Pray take no

[025-121] further trouble to explain yourself. I am not curious to know your

[025-122] meaning. What does it matter?’

[025-123] Nothing more was said on this theme, and Mr. Harthouse was soon idly gay

[025-124] on indifferent subjects. But from this day, the Sparsit action upon Mr.

[025-125] Bounderby threw Louisa and James Harthouse more together, and

[025-126] strengthened the dangerous alienation from her husband and confidence

[025-127] against him with another, into which she had fallen by degrees so fine

[025-128] that she could not retrace them if she tried. But whether she ever tried

[025-129] or no, lay hidden in her own closed heart.

[025-130] Mrs. Sparsit was so much affected on this particular occasion, that,

[025-131] assisting Mr. Bounderby to his hat after breakfast, and being then alone

[025-132] with him in the hall, she imprinted a chaste kiss upon his hand, murmured

[025-133] ‘My benefactor!’ and retired, overwhelmed with grief. Yet it is an

[025-134] indubitable fact, within the cognizance of this history, that five

[025-135] minutes after he had left the house in the self-same hat, the same

[025-136] descendant of the Scadgerses and connexion by [[marriage|matrimony]] of the Powlers,

[025-137] shook her right-hand mitten at his portrait, made a contemptuous grimace

[025-138] at that work of art, and said ‘Serve you right, you Noodle, and I am glad

[025-139] of it.’

[025-140] Mr. Bounderby had not been long gone, when [[bitzer|Bitzer]] appeared. Bitzer had

[025-141] come down by train, shrieking and rattling over the long line of arches

[025-142] that bestrode the wild country of past and present coal-pits, with an

[025-143] express from [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]]. It was a hasty note to inform Louisa that Mrs.

[025-144] Gradgrind lay very ill. She had never been well within her daughter’s

[025-145] knowledge; but, she had declined within the last few days, had continued

[025-146] sinking all through the night, and was now as nearly dead, as her limited

[025-147] capacity of being in any state that implied the ghost of an intention to

[025-148] get out of it, allowed.

[025-149] Accompanied by the lightest of porters, fit colourless servitor at

[025-150] Death’s door when Mrs. Gradgrind knocked, Louisa rumbled to Coketown,

[025-151] over the coal-pits past and present, and was whirled into its smoky jaws.

[025-152] She dismissed the messenger to his own devices, and rode away to her old

[025-153] home.

[025-154] She had seldom been there since her marriage. Her father was usually

[025-155] sifting and sifting at his parliamentary cinder-heap in London (without

[025-156] being observed to turn up many precious articles among the rubbish), and

[025-157] was still hard at it in the national dust-yard. Her mother had taken it

[025-158] rather as a disturbance than otherwise, to be visited, as she reclined

[025-159] upon her sofa; young people, Louisa felt herself all unfit for; [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] she

[025-160] had never softened to again, since the night when the stroller’s child

[025-161] had raised her eyes to look at Mr. Bounderby’s intended wife. She had no

[025-162] inducements to go back, and had rarely gone.

[025-163] Neither, as she approached her old home now, did any of the best

[025-164] influences of old home descend upon her. The dreams of childhood—its

[025-165] airy fables; its graceful, beautiful, [[humanity|humane]], impossible adornments of

[025-166] the world beyond: so good to be believed in once, so good to be

[025-167] remembered when outgrown, for then the least among them rises to the

[025-168] stature of a great Charity in the heart, suffering little children to

[025-169] come into the midst of it, and to keep with their pure hands a garden in

[025-170] the stony ways of this world, wherein it were better for all the children

[025-171] of Adam that they should oftener sun themselves, simple and trustful, and

[025-172] not worldly-wise—what had she to do with these? Remembrances of how she

[025-173] had journeyed to the little that she knew, by the enchanted roads of what

[025-174] she and millions of innocent creatures had hoped and imagined; of how,

[025-175] first coming upon Reason through the tender light of [[fancy|Fancy]], she had seen

[025-176] it a beneficent god, deferring to gods as great as itself; not a grim

[025-177] Idol, cruel and cold, with its victims bound hand to foot, and its big

[025-178] dumb shape set up with a sightless stare, never to be moved by anything

[025-179] but so many calculated tons of leverage—what had she to do with these?

[025-180] Her remembrances of home and childhood were remembrances of the drying up

[025-181] of every spring and fountain in her young heart as it gushed out. The

[025-182] golden waters were not there. They were flowing for the fertilization of

[025-183] the land where grapes are gathered from thorns, and figs from thistles.

[025-184] She went, with a heavy, hardened kind of sorrow upon her, into the house

[025-185] and into her mother’s room. Since the time of her leaving home, Sissy

[025-186] had lived with the rest of the family on equal terms. Sissy was at her

[025-187] mother’s side; and Jane, her sister, now ten or twelve years old, was in

[025-188] the room.

[025-189] There was great trouble before it could be made known to Mrs. Gradgrind

[025-190] that her eldest child was there. She reclined, propped up, from mere

[025-191] habit, on a couch: as nearly in her old usual attitude, as anything so

[025-192] helpless could be kept in. She had positively refused to take to her

[025-193] bed; on the ground that if she did, she would never hear the last of it.

[025-194] Her feeble voice sounded so far away in her bundle of shawls, and the

[025-195] sound of another voice addressing her seemed to take such a long time in

[025-196] getting down to her ears, that she might have been lying at the bottom of

[025-197] a well. The poor lady was nearer [[truth|Truth]] than she ever had been: which had

[025-198] much to do with it.

[025-199] On being told that Mrs. Bounderby was there, she replied, at

[025-200] cross-purposes, that she had never called him by that name since he

[025-201] married Louisa; that pending her choice of an objectionable name, she had

[025-202] called him J; and that she could not at present depart from that

[025-203] regulation, not being yet provided with a permanent substitute. Louisa

[025-204] had sat by her for some minutes, and had spoken to her often, before she

[025-205] arrived at a clear understanding who it was. She then seemed to come to

[025-206] it all at once.

[025-207] ‘Well, my [[love|dear]],’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, ‘and I hope you are going on

[025-208] satisfactorily to yourself. It was all your father’s doing. He set his

[025-209] heart upon it. And he ought to know.’

[025-210] ‘I want to hear of you, mother; not of myself.’

[025-211] ‘You want to hear of me, my dear? That’s something new, I am sure, when

[025-212] anybody wants to hear of me. Not at all well, Louisa. Very faint and

[025-213] giddy.’

[025-214] ‘Are you in pain, dear mother?’

[025-215] ‘I think there’s a pain somewhere in the room,’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, ‘but

[025-216] I couldn’t positively say that I have got it.’

[025-217] After this strange speech, she lay silent for some time. Louisa, holding

[025-218] her hand, could feel no pulse; but kissing it, could see a slight thin

[025-219] thread of life in fluttering motion.

[025-220] ‘You very seldom see your sister,’ said Mrs. Gradgrind. ‘She grows like

[025-221] you. I wish you would look at her. Sissy, bring her here.’

[025-222] She was brought, and stood with her hand in her sister’s. Louisa had

[025-223] observed her with her arm round Sissy’s neck, and she felt the difference

[025-224] of this approach.

[025-225] ‘Do you see the likeness, Louisa?’

[025-226] ‘Yes, mother. I should think her like me. But—’

[025-227] ‘Eh! Yes, I always say so,’ Mrs. Gradgrind cried, with unexpected

[025-228] quickness. ‘And that reminds me. I—I want to speak to you, my dear.

[025-229] Sissy, my good girl, leave us alone a minute.’ Louisa had relinquished

[025-230] the hand: had thought that her sister’s was a better and brighter face

[025-231] than hers had ever been: had seen in it, not without a rising feeling of

[025-232] resentment, even in that place and at that time, something of the

[025-233] gentleness of the other face in the room; the sweet face with the

[025-234] trusting eyes, made paler than watching and sympathy made it, by the rich

[025-235] dark hair.

[025-236] Left alone with her mother, Louisa saw her lying with an awful lull upon

[025-237] her face, like one who was floating away upon some great water, all

[025-238] resistance over, content to be carried down the stream. She put the

[025-239] shadow of a hand to her lips again, and recalled her.

[025-240] ‘You were going to speak to me, mother.’

[025-241] ‘Eh? Yes, to be sure, my dear. You know your father is almost always

[025-242] away now, and therefore I must write to him about it.’

[025-243] ‘[[About]] what, mother? Don’t be troubled. About what?’

[025-244] ‘You must remember, my dear, that whenever I have said anything, on any

[025-245] subject, I have never heard the last of it: and consequently, that I have

[025-246] long left off saying anything.’

[025-247] ‘I can hear you, mother.’ But, it was only by dint of bending down to

[025-248] her ear, and at the same time attentively watching the lips as they

[025-249] moved, that she could link such faint and broken sounds into any chain of

[025-250] connexion.

[025-251] ‘You learnt a great deal, Louisa, and so did your brother. Ologies of

[025-252] all kinds from morning to night. If there is any Ology left, of any

[025-253] description, that has not been worn to rags in this house, all I can say

[025-254] is, I hope I shall never hear its name.’

[025-255] ‘I can hear you, mother, when you have strength to go on.’ This, to keep

[025-256] her from floating away.

[025-257] ‘But there is something—not an Ology at all—that your father has missed,

[025-258] or forgotten, Louisa. I don’t know what it is. I have often sat with

[025-259] Sissy near me, and thought about it. I shall never get its name now.

[025-260] But your father may. It makes me restless. I want to write to him, to

[025-261] find out for God’s sake, what it is. Give me a pen, give me a pen.’

[025-262] Even the power of restlessness was gone, except from the poor head, which

[025-263] could just turn from side to side.

[025-264] She fancied, however, that her request had been complied with, and that

[025-265] the pen she could not have held was in her hand. It matters little what

[025-266] figures of wonderful no-meaning she began to trace upon her wrappers.

[025-267] The hand soon stopped in the midst of them; the light that had always

[025-268] been feeble and dim behind the weak transparency, went out; and even Mrs.

[025-269] Gradgrind, emerged from the shadow in which man walketh and disquieteth

[025-270] himself in vain, took upon her the dread solemnity of the sages and

[025-271] patriarchs.
