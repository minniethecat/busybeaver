# Love

In *Hard Times*, love is the great absence — the capacity that the Gradgrind system cannot name, measure, or teach, and which it therefore excludes with the violence of a philosophy that mistakes its own omissions for universal truths. The novel is structured around the consequences of this absence: Louisa has never learned to love, Tom loves only himself, Bounderby cannot love (his denial of his mother is also a denial of the capacity to be loved). Against this desolation, Dickens places Sissy Jupe — the only character who speaks the language of love naturally, whose "Father loved me, first" (PN: 009-128) is the novel's simplest and most devastating sentence, asserting love as foundation, as origin, as what the Gradgrind children never had.

## Definition

Love in *Hard Times* operates less as a defined term than as a felt lacuna — the thing that ought to be present in human relations and is conspicuously, catastrophically missing. The novel does not offer a theory of love so much as it documents the deformations produced by love's absence. When Louisa asks her father "do you think I love Mr. Bounderby?" (PN: 015-060), she is asking a question her education has given her no resources to answer: she does not know what love feels like, whether she feels it, or whether it matters. Dickens presents this epistemological void not as Louisa's personal deficiency but as the logical outcome of a philosophy that recognises only measurable quantities and dismisses affective bonds as irrelevant to rational decision-making.

The novel distinguishes implicitly between several forms of love: parental love (Sissy's father, the Gradgrinds' negation of it); erotic or companionate love (Stephen and Rachael, Louisa's non-experience of it); and the generalised love that the narrator calls "the wisdom of the Heart" (PN: 035-036) — the capacity for sympathy, loyalty, and self-giving that the Gradgrind system cannot generate and the circus community preserves.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens treats love not as a sentimental theme but as a diagnostic instrument. The presence or absence of love is the test that exposes every character and institution in the novel. The Gradgrind household — orderly, prosperous, fact-governed — is revealed as barren because no love flows through it. The Bounderby marriage — a triumph of rational calculation — is exposed as a form of legalised exploitation because love is not among its terms. The union of Stephen and Rachael — chaste, devoted, mutually sustaining — is the novel's normative image of love, and it is precisely what the law forbids them from formalising as marriage. (PN: 013-005–015)

The novel's formal strategy is to make the reader feel the absence of love before it is named. The famous opening scene in the schoolroom — "Now, what I want is, Facts" — contains no mention of love because love does not belong to the vocabulary Gradgrind permits. But the scene's emotional temperature — the bare room, the numbered child, the "square forefinger" that points but never touches — communicates love's absence through sensory deprivation. Dickens then introduces Sissy Jupe, whose every utterance is saturated with the language of love: "My darling," "My love," the father who "shook all over, and said nothing but 'My darling;' and 'My love!'" (PN: 009-191). The contrast between Sissy's affective fluency and the Gradgrind children's emotional muteness is the novel's central argument about what the system destroys.

The narrator's description of Stephen Blackpool makes the point explicit: Stephen possesses "a capacity for good or evil, for love or hatred, for patriotism or discontent" (PN: 011-017) — a full human range that the novel insists belongs to working people no less than to their masters. The phrase is significant: love is listed first among the capacities, and it is presented not as a virtue but as a capacity — something one is capable of, or starved of, or trained out of.

### Key Passages

> "Did your father love her?" Louisa asked these questions with a strong, wild, wandering interest peculiar to her; an interest gone astray like a banished creature, and hiding in solitary places. (PN: 009-125–127)

Louisa's interrogation of Sissy about her father's love for her mother is one of the novel's most painful scenes. Louisa asks about love as if it were a foreign language she is trying to learn by eavesdropping. The narrator's description — "a banished creature, and hiding in solitary places" — figures love itself as an exile in Louisa's psyche: present enough to prompt the question, absent enough that the question can only be asked of someone else.

> "O yes! As dearly as he loves me. Father loved me, first, for her sake. From that time, he has always loved me." (PN: 009-128)

Sissy's answer is the novel's simplest and most radical sentence. "Father loved me, first" — love as the primary datum, the origin from which all else follows. Sissy has never been taught to doubt that she is loved; her certainty is the ontological opposite of Louisa's bewilderment. The phrase "for her sake" layers the loves — father for mother, father for daughter — in a chain of affection that is utterly foreign to the Gradgrind household, where each member seems to exist in a separate emotional cell.

> "Louisa, do you think I love Mr. Bounderby?" ... "does Mr. Bounderby ask me to love him?" (PN: 015-060, 015-066)

Louisa's two questions to her father bracket the novel's critique of Utilitarian marriage. The first question asks Gradgrind to recognise that love might be relevant to a decision about marriage. When he evades, she asks the second: does Bounderby even pretend that love is part of the transaction? The answer is no — Bounderby has proposed in the language of business, and Gradgrind has evaluated the proposal with statistics. Love does not enter the calculation. Louisa's questions are devastating because they expose what the system excludes, and they are tragic because the exposure changes nothing.

> "who was serviceable to, or beloved by, another, while this" (PN: 013-010)

Stephen's meditation on the injustice of marriage law — that he is bound to a wife who has abandoned him while the woman he loves (Rachael) can never be his wife — turns on the word "beloved." The passive construction is exact: Stephen does not speak of loving Rachael so much as of her being *beloved by* someone who is not free to marry her. Love is a fact of his experience; the law's refusal to recognise it is the novel's indictment of an institution that privileges form over substance.

> "love and patience could abate the bitterness of this" (PN: 012-010)

The narrator's description of Stephen's care for his alcoholic wife — "love and patience could abate the bitterness of this" — presents love not as sentiment but as practice. Stephen loves his wife not because she is lovable but because he is capable of loving; the capacity persists even when its object has ceased to deserve it. This is the novel's most demanding image of love — as duty freely assumed, as patience without reward — and it anticipates Gradgrind's late recognition that there is "a wisdom of the Heart."

## Historical Context

Dickens wrote *Hard Times* during a period of intense cultural contestation over the nature and place of love in social life. Several intersecting contexts inform the novel's treatment:

**Victorian Domestic Ideology** had elevated the home to a sacred sphere governed by womanly affection — the "angel in the house" celebrated by Coventry Patmore's 1854 poem of that name (published the same year as *Hard Times*). This ideology treated love as woman's special province and the family as a refuge from the competitive brutality of the marketplace. Dickens's novel both draws on this tradition and complicates it: Louisa's tragedy is that she has been denied access to precisely the affective resources that domestic ideology promised to women, while Sissy Jupe — the novel's most loving character — is the daughter of a circus clown, not a middle-class household.

**Benthamite Utilitarianism** had no vocabulary for love. Jeremy Bentham's felicific calculus reduced all human motivation to the pursuit of pleasure and the avoidance of pain; love, being unmeasurable and irreducible to calculation, simply fell out of the system. Gradgrind's inability to answer Louisa's question about love is not a personal failing but a philosophical one: the Utilitarian framework he has adopted cannot recognise love as a category of concern.

**The Marriage Debates** of the 1850s — culminating in the Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 — turned on the question of whether marriage was a contract (dissoluble by mutual consent) or a sacrament (indissoluble). Dickens was broadly in favour of divorce reform, and Stephen Blackpool's predicament — trapped in a marriage to an alcoholic wife he cannot divorce because he is poor — is the novel's contribution to this debate. But the novel's deeper point is that reform of marriage law is insufficient without reform of the philosophy that makes love invisible.

## Embodied By

- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: Love's only native speaker in the novel. Sissy has been loved by her father and carries that love as a kind of emotional literacy that the Gradgrind children lack. Her language — "My darling," "My love," "Father loved me, first" — belongs to a world of affective fluency that the novel treats as both archaic (the pre-industrial circus) and redemptive (the future she helps build for Louisa and her own children).
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: Love as an unknown country. Louisa's tragedy is not that she loves the wrong person but that she does not know what love is. Her questions to Sissy and to her father are acts of reconnaissance — a cartography of an emotional territory her education never permitted her to enter. Her final state — neither happily remarried nor romantically fulfilled, but having "children about her" — is the novel's chastened image of love found late, in another generation.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Love blocked by law. Stephen's love for Rachael is the novel's most fully realised image of adult devotion, and it cannot be consummated because the law will not permit divorce to a working man. Stephen's capacity for love is presented as a fact of his character (PN: 011-017); the law that prevents its expression is the novel's sharpest critique of an institution that claims to protect marriage while destroying love.
- **[[Rachael]]**: Love as steadfast presence. Rachael never speaks of love directly; she embodies it through care — tending Stephen, defending his name, remaining constant when all other consolations fail. She is the novel's argument that love is not a declaration but a practice: something one does rather than something one says.
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: The incapacity to love. Bounderby's denial of his mother — his insistence that she abandoned him, his refusal to acknowledge her care — is also a denial of the very possibility of being loved. He cannot receive love because his self-mythology requires that he owe nothing to anyone. His marriage to Louisa is a transaction in which love is not even pretended; his exposure as a fraud in Book III is the novel's final judgement on a life built without love.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The Gradgrind philosophy substitutes Facts for Love — measurable quantities for unmeasurable bonds. The novel's opening scene is a catechism of Facts delivered in a "plain, bare, monotonous vault of a school-room" (PN: 001-001), and the absence of warmth in that room is precisely the absence of love. Facts are what you can know without feeling; love is what you can only know by feeling. The novel argues that a life organised around the former is a life impoverished of the latter.
- **[[marriage]]**: What Coketown substitutes for Love. Every marriage in the novel — Louisa and Bounderby's, Stephen and his wife's, even the elder Gradgrinds' — is a transaction that performs the legal form of union without the emotional substance. The novel's one genuine love relationship (Stephen and Rachael) is precisely the one the law forbids from becoming a marriage. (PN: 013-005–015)
- **[[duty]]**: What Bounderby and Gradgrind call Love when they need a moral vocabulary for what is actually obligation. Louisa is expected to "do her duty" by marrying Bounderby; Gradgrind frames his philosophy as a "duty" to the children he educates. Duty is love's impersonator in Coketown — it mimics love's form (obligation, commitment) while evacuating its substance (affection, desire, warmth).

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), argued that *Hard Times*' treatment of personal relations — including love — was both its strength and its limit. Dickens could render the felt absence of love with extraordinary power, but he could only imagine love's restoration in private, domestic, and finally sentimental terms. The circus's "people mutht be amuthed" is, for Williams, an inadequate answer to the structural deformations the novel has so powerfully diagnosed.

> F.R. Leavis, in *The Great Tradition* (1948), read the Sissy Jupe strand of the novel as Dickens's affirmation of "the creativeness of life" against the deathliness of the Utilitarian system. For Leavis, Sissy's capacity to love — and to teach love to the next generation — is not a sentimental evasion but the novel's essential moral argument: that love is not a luxury but a necessity of human flourishing.

> Catherine Waters, in *Dickens and the Politics of the Family* (1997), has argued that *Hard Times* stages a conflict between two models of love: the patriarchal love that Gradgrind claims to offer (love as discipline, love as the inculcation of useful habits) and the maternal love that Sissy embodies (love as presence, love as unconditional acceptance). The novel's resolution — Sissy's children having "a childhood of the mind" — represents the triumph of the maternal model, but Waters notes that this triumph remains strictly domestic and does not extend to the public world of Coketown.
