# Sleary

Mr. Sleary is the proprietor of Sleary's Horse-Riding, a travelling circus, and — improbable as it seems — the novel's wisest character. Stout, asthmatic, lisping, one eye fixed from a circus injury, he appears at first glance to be one of Dickens's grotesques: a figure meant for comedy rather than moral weight. Yet it is Sleary who shelters Sissy Jupe when her father abandons her, who releases her to Gradgrind for her own good, who hides Tom Gradgrind when the law closes in, and who delivers the novel's final, unanswerable moral: "People mutht be amuthed. They can't be alwayth a-learning, nor they can't be alwayth a-working. They an't made for it" (PN: 006-427). In a novel about the war between Fact and Fancy, Sleary is Fancy's general — disreputable in appearance, ludicrous in speech, and absolutely right.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mr. Sleary (given name never stated; known only by surname)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "Signor Jupe-Sleary" (shared credit with Sissy's father, suggesting the circus's theatrical/Italian billing conventions); "Thquire" (his own lisping pronunciation of "Squire," a running joke that doubles as moral commentary)
- **Role**: Hidden sage — the alternative father figure and moral anchor; the novel's anti-Gradgrind
- **Occupation**: Proprietor of Sleary's Horse-Riding, a travelling circus
- **Family**: Josephine Sleary (daughter, an accomplished equestrian who performs the "Tyrolean Flower Act," also known as the "Cauliflower Act"); the entire circus troupe (his adopted family, bound by loyalty rather than contract)
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 3, "A LOOPHOLE" — glimpsed as "a stout modern statue with a money-box at his elbow" in the Gothic niche at the entrance of Sleary's Horse-Riding (PN: 003-072–003-074)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 7 — his final conversation with Gradgrind after arranging Tom's escape: "You don't need to be told that I want to be obliged to you. You know that" — answered by Sleary's refusal of obligation: "you make too much of it, Thquire" (PN: 035-320–035-327)


## Characterisation

Dickens constructs Sleary as a deliberate paradox: a figure whose physical and verbal disqualifications from serious discourse — his lisp, his one eye, his asthma, his stoutness, his trade — are precisely the qualities through which the novel's deepest truths are uttered. The lisp that would preclude a man from the Gradgrind schoolroom or the Bounderby counting-house becomes, in Dickens's hands, the vehicle of moral authority. When Sleary tells Gradgrind to "thtick to the facts, Thir!" (PN: 006-322), the lisp transforms the Utilitarian commandment into a self-subverting joke — the word "facts" cannot be pronounced clearly by the man who understands them better than anyone.

### Physical Description

Sleary is introduced in Book I Chapter 3 in a tableau of grotesquerie: "a stout modern statue with a money-box at his elbow: in an ecclesiastical niche of early Gothic architecture" (PN: 003-072–003-074). The incongruity — a fat circus proprietor lodged in a pseudo-Gothic church niche — establishes him as a figure who is always out of place, always slightly absurd, always more than he appears.

His full first portrait comes in Book I Chapter 6: "a stout man as already mentioned, with one fixed eye, and one loose eye, a voice (if it can be called so) like the efforts of a broken pair of bellows, a flabby surface, and a muddled head which was never sober and never drunk" (PN: 006-254–006-256). The "one fixed eye, and one loose eye" is the signature physical trait — one eye stays steady while the other rolls independently, a comic disability that also reads as a metaphor for double vision: Sleary looks at the everyday and the transcendent simultaneously. His asthma gives his speech "the efforts of a broken pair of bellows" — every word costs him breath, and yet he speaks more truth than any character in the novel. His "flabby surface" conceals a core of iron loyalty; his "muddled head which was never sober and never drunk" suggests a man who lives perpetually between categories, defying the binary logic of the Fact-system.

By Book III, the physical description has softened into something approaching dignity. In the booth at the end of the novel, Sleary is "very short of breath" (PN: 035-201) but entirely in command — of his troupe, of the situation, of the moral calculus. The broken bellows voice that was comic in Book I has become, by Book III, the voice of the novel's moral summation.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Sleary's speech is the most distinctive in the novel. His lisp transforms sibilants into a running commentary on his own social position: "Thquire" for "Squire," "thtick" for "stick," "mutht" for "must," "amuthed" for "amused." The lisp is comic, but Dickens consistently uses it to deliver the novel's most serious propositions. The gap between the form (comic defect) and the content (moral truth) is itself the novel's argument: wisdom does not reside where the world expects to find it.

His characteristic phrases become a kind of philosophy in fragments. "Thtick to the facts, Thir!" (PN: 006-322) — spoken to Gradgrind — carries the full weight of irony: the circus-master who cannot pronounce the word "facts" correctly tells the apostle of Facts to stick to them. "Make the betht of uth; not the wurtht!" (PN: 006-427) is his moral creed, a humanism more generous than anything the Gradgrind system can offer. And "People mutht be amuthed. They can't be alwayth a-learning, nor they can't be alwayth a-working. They an't made for it" (PN: 006-427) is the novel's thesis, spoken by a lisping circus-master to a Member of Parliament.

Sleary's theatrical vocabulary is a running joke and a structural motif. He speaks continually of what is "thtrongly in my memory" and "in print" — casting life in the language of performance because, in the novel's terms, life IS a performance: a better one than Coketown's grim recitation of facts. His beverage is brandy-and-water, which he stirs, stares into, and uses as a prop for his moments of deepest reflection — "musing as he looked down into the depths of his brandy and water" (PN: 036-192). The brandy-and-water, like the lisp, is part of the comic surface that masks the moral core.


## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Sleary first appears in Chapter 3 as a distant figure: the circus proprietor glimpsed through a hole in a deal board by Louisa and Tom Gradgrind, whose forbidden peeping at the circus establishes the novel's central conflict between the world of Facts and the world of Fancy (PN: 003-072–003-074).

His first substantial scene comes in Chapter 6, "SLEARY'S HORSEMANSHIP," when Gradgrind and Bounderby arrive at the circus to find Sissy Jupe after her father has abandoned her. The scene is a set-piece of inverted authority. Gradgrind, the MP, and Bounderby, the banker, stand in the sawdust of a circus booth, while Sleary — lisping, asthmatic, half-drunk — holds the moral high ground. He tells them of Sissy's father: a clown and dog-trainer who, "the dogs didn't take to him," whose career failed when "the public didn't take to the dogs," and who finally abandoned his daughter believing she would fare better without him (PN: 006-277–006-309).

In this scene, Sleary makes two decisions that define the novel's moral architecture. First, he releases Sissy to Gradgrind — not because Gradgrind is right but because "your daughter will be taken care of" (Sleary's reasoning is practical: "Sithy will be better off with you than with uth") (PN: 006-334). Second, he delivers the novel's moral in his closing speech: "People mutht be amuthed. They can't be alwayth a-learning, nor they can't be alwayth a-working. They an't made for it. You mutht have uth, Thquire. Do the wit and the learning what it can, there never was found a requiem that could thpeak to a poor man's heart like the thtuff-thinging of the wind through a harp" (PN: 006-427). Gradgrind, characteristically, "did not take much notice of this oration"; but the novel does. Sleary has spoken the thesis of *Hard Times* at its midpoint, and the remaining 580 pages will prove him right.

### Book II: Reaping

Sleary largely disappears from Book II, but his absence is structural. The circus — the world of Fancy — has been driven from Coketown; what remains is the Fact-system grinding through its consequences. Louisa marries Bounderby without love. Tom descends into gambling and theft. Stephen Blackpool is ostracised and dies. The "reaping" of Book II is precisely the harvest of a world without amusement, without Fancy, without Sleary's lisping wisdom.

Sissy Jupe, however, carries the circus's moral code into the Gradgrind household, and her effectiveness throughout Book II — nursing Mrs. Gradgrind, befriending Louisa, waiting faithfully for a father who will never return — is the continuing proof of Sleary's philosophy in action.

### Book III: Garnering

Sleary's return in Book III is the novel's moral turning-point. When Tom Gradgrind, exposed as the bank robber and hunted by Bitzer, needs to escape England, it is Sissy who thinks of the circus and Sleary who makes it possible. The reunion scene in Chapter 7 brings the full company back: Sleary's Horse-Riding is now in a town some distance from Coketown, and Sissy returns to find Sleary presiding over the same sawdust ring, "saving his wind" but still in command (PN: 035-154–035-157).

Sleary's willingness to help Tom — "a young man whom he had never seen" — is offered without conditions, without moralising, without price. His language is practical: "There'th a property-houthe, you thee, for Jack to hide in" (PN: 035-226). But the subtext is radical. The circus, which Coketown despises, proves more reliable than any institution the town possesses. When Bitzer arrives to claim Tom, it is Sleary who negotiates the escape — dressing Tom in blackface disguise, hiding him in the circus wagon, and arranging his passage out of England (PN: 035-327–035-331).

Sleary's final exchange with Gradgrind completes the novel's arc. Gradgrind offers thanks; Sleary waves them away: "you make too much of it, Thquire" (PN: 035-320). There is no debt between them because Sleary does not operate within the economy of obligation that governs Coketown. He acts not from duty or calculation but from what the novel calls, simply, humanity.


## Key Relationships

- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: Sleary's surrogate daughter — the circus-child he protects, releases to Gradgrind for her own good, and welcomes back when she returns seeking help. Their relationship is the novel's model of functional parenthood: love without possession, loyalty without strings. When Sissy's father abandons her, Sleary steps in; when Sleary releases her, it is because he puts her welfare above his own interests. (PN: 006-277–006-334)
- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: The ideological adversary who becomes, by the novel's end, Sleary's beneficiary and — in a deeper sense — his pupil. Their encounters frame the novel: Book I Chapter 6 (Gradgrind takes Sissy from the circus) and Book III Chapter 7 (the circus saves Gradgrind's son). In both scenes, the distribution of moral authority is inverted: the lisping circus-master teaches the MP. (PN: 006-322–006-427; PN: 035-313–035-350)
- **[[Josephine-Sleary]]**: Sleary's daughter, the accomplished equestrian who performs the "Tyrolean Flower Act." Her skill and grace are Sleary's pride, and her presence in the troupe demonstrates that the circus is a family — an actual family, not the distorted one Gradgrind produces. (PN: 003-074; PN: 035-150)
- **The Circus Troupe**: Sleary's "family" — the collective of riders, acrobats, clowns, and dog-trainers whose loyalty to each other and to Sissy is the novel's demonstration that community can exist outside the logic of contract and capital. They shelter Tom not because they know him but because Sissy asks. (PN: 035-226–035-241)
- **[[Tom-Gradgrind]]**: The Gradgrind son whom Sleary helps escape. Sleary asks no moral questions and imposes no conditions; he simply helps, because a human being in trouble has asked for help. His response to Tom is the novel's refutation of the retributive logic that governs Coketown's institutions. (PN: 035-313–035-331)
- **[[Bitzer]]**: Sleary's antagonist in the novel's final crisis. Bitzer, the perfect product of Gradgrind education, arrives to claim Tom in the name of Facts — "the whole system is a question of self-interest" (PN: 036-094). Sleary's response is not argument but action: he arranges the escape while Bitzer is distracted, proving that the world of Fancy is, in a crisis, more effective than the world of Facts.


## Thematic Significance

Sleary is the anti-Gradgrind — everything the Fact-system despises (circus, entertainment, physicality, lisp, sentiment) and yet the novel's most reliable source of moral truth. His lisp, his one eye, his brandy-and-water, his theatrical billing — all of it constitutes a counter-system to Coketown's grim machinery of fact and calculation.

The circus over which he presides is the novel's alternative society. It is ranked by skill rather than capital, bound by loyalty rather than contract, animated by joy rather than profit. It shelters the abandoned, rescues the guilty, and asks nothing in return. In a novel filled with institutions that fail their people — the school, the factory, the bank, the Parliament, the marriage — the circus succeeds. It is the only institution in *Hard Times* that reliably produces kindness.

Sleary's lisp carries the novel's deepest argument. In a world that values fluent speech — Gradgrind's declarations, Bounderby's bombast, Harthouse's insinuations — the man who cannot pronounce the word "facts" correctly is the one who understands them. His disability is also his qualification: he speaks from outside the system of verbal mastery that Coketown's ruling class deploys to justify its cruelty. The lisp is not an impediment to truth but its vehicle. Dickens's radical gesture is to place the novel's moral summation in the mouth of a lisping, asthmatic, half-drunk circus-master — and to make that summation unanswerable.

Sleary's theatrical/legal language — "thtrongly in my memory," "in print" — is a running joke that doubles as narrative commentary. He thinks in performance terms because, in the novel's symbolic economy, life IS a performance: and a better one than Coketown's grim recitation of facts. The circus is not a diversion from reality but a more honest version of it. Sleary's troupe knows what Coketown's respectable citizens do not: that all social life is performance, that the self-made-man narrative is a script, that the MP's parliamentary manner is a role. The difference is that the circus admits it.


## Contrasted With

Sleary is the novel's structural counter-figure to three other characters:

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: The alternative father figure. Where Gradgrind offers Facts, Sleary offers Fancy. Where Gradgrind calculates, Sleary feels. Where Gradgrind produces broken children, Sleary produces a community that shelters the broken. Gradgrind's journey from certainty to doubt is also a journey toward Sleary's wisdom. (PN: 006-427; PN: 035-313–035-327)
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: The genuine self-made man versus the fraudulent one. Bounderby's poverty story is a lie designed to justify cruelty; Sleary's circus is a genuinely self-made community, built from nothing but skill and mutual obligation, that extends kindness to strangers. The novel's comparison is implicit but devastating: the man who claims to have risen from a ditch is a fraud; the man who lives in a circus wagon is the real gentleman. (PN: 004-007–004-022)
- **[[facts]]**: Sleary's lisp transforms "facts" into a word that cannot be pronounced without irony. His philosophy — "People mutht be amuthed" — is the novel's definitive answer to the Gradgrind creed of "Facts, sir; nothing but Facts!" (PN: 001-002; PN: 006-427)


## Critical Reception

> G. K. Chesterton, in his 1906 study of Dickens, celebrated Sleary and his circus as "the last triumph of the human spirit over the crushing machinery of industrial capitalism." Chesterton read Sleary's lisp not as a comic tic but as a theological argument: the stammer of the spirit against the fluent lies of the world. For Chesterton, Sleary's "people must be amused" was the central doctrine of Dickens's social philosophy — a defence of pleasure, play, and popular entertainment against the Utilitarian demand that every activity justify itself by its measurable output.

> Recent criticism (Martha Nussbaum, *Poetic Justice*, 1995; David Kurnick, *Empty Houses*, 2012) has recovered Sleary as a figure of serious moral intelligence, reading his lisp and his theatrical language not as comic defects but as markers of an alternative epistemology — a way of knowing that the Gradgrind system cannot recognise.
