[013-001] RACHAEL

[013-002] A CANDLE faintly burned in the window, to which the black ladder had

[013-003] often been raised for the sliding away of all that was most precious in

[013-004] this world to a striving wife and a brood of hungry babies; and [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen]]

[013-005] added to his other thoughts the stern reflection, that of all the

[013-006] casualties of this existence upon earth, not one was dealt out with so

[013-007] unequal a hand as Death. The inequality of Birth was nothing to it.

[013-008] For, say that the [[childhood|child]] of a King and the child of a Weaver were born

[013-009] to-night in the same moment, what was that disparity, to the death of any

[013-010] human creature who was serviceable to, or [[love|beloved]] by, another, while this

[013-011] abandoned woman lived on!

[013-012] From the outside of his home he gloomily passed to the inside, with

[013-013] suspended breath and with a slow footstep. He went up to his door,

[013-014] opened it, and so into the room.

[013-015] Quiet and peace were there. [[rachael|Rachael]] was there, sitting by the bed.

[013-016] She turned her [[reason|head]], and the light of her face shone in upon the midnight

[013-017] of his mind. She sat by the bed, watching and tending his wife. That is

[013-018] to say, he saw that some one lay there, and he knew too well it must be

[013-019] she; but Rachael’s hands had put a curtain up, so that she was screened

[013-020] from his eyes. Her disgraceful garments were removed, and some of

[013-021] Rachael’s were in the room. Everything was in its place and order as he

[013-022] had always kept it, the little fire was newly trimmed, and the hearth was

[013-023] freshly swept. It appeared to him that he saw all this in Rachael’s

[013-024] face, and looked at nothing besides. While looking at it, it was shut

[013-025] out from his view by the softened tears that filled his eyes; but not

[013-026] before he had seen how earnestly she looked at him, and how her own eyes

[013-027] were filled too.

[013-028] She turned again towards the bed, and satisfying herself that all was

[013-029] quiet there, spoke in a low, calm, cheerful voice.

[013-030] ‘I am glad you have come at last, Stephen. You are very late.’

[013-031] ‘I ha’ been walking up an’ down.’

[013-032] ‘I thought so. But ’tis too bad a night for that. The rain falls very

[013-033] heavy, and the wind has risen.’

[013-034] The wind? True. It was blowing hard. Hark to the thundering in the

[013-035] chimney, and the surging noise! To have been out in such a wind, and not

[013-036] to have known it was blowing!

[013-037] ‘I have been here once before, to-day, Stephen. Landlady came round for

[013-038] me at dinner-time. There was some one here that needed looking to, she

[013-039] said. And ‘deed she was right. All wandering and lost, Stephen.

[013-040] Wounded too, and bruised.’

[013-041] He slowly moved to a chair and sat down, drooping his head before her.

[013-042] ‘I came to do what little I could, Stephen; first, for that she worked

[013-043] with me when we were girls both, and for that you courted her and married

[013-044] her when I was her friend—’

[013-045] He laid his furrowed forehead on his hand, with a low groan.

[013-046] ‘And next, for that I know your [[heart]], and am right sure and certain that

[013-047] ’tis far too merciful to let her die, or even so much as suffer, for want

[013-048] of aid. Thou knowest who said, “Let him who is without sin among you

[013-049] cast the first stone at her!” There have been plenty to do that. Thou

[013-050] art not the man to cast the last stone, Stephen, when she is brought so

[013-051] low.’

[013-052] ‘O Rachael, Rachael!’

[013-053] ‘Thou hast been a cruel sufferer, Heaven reward thee!’ she said, in

[013-054] compassionate accents. ‘I am thy poor friend, with all my heart and

[013-055] mind.’

[013-056] [Picture: Stephen and Rachael in the sick room]

[013-057] The wounds of which she had spoken, seemed to be about the neck of the

[013-058] self-made outcast. She dressed them now, still without showing her. She

[013-059] steeped a piece of linen in a basin, into which she poured some liquid

[013-060] from a bottle, and laid it with a gentle hand upon the sore. The

[013-061] three-legged table had been drawn close to the bedside, and on it there

[013-062] were two bottles. This was one.

[013-063] It was not so far off, but that Stephen, following her hands with his

[013-064] eyes, could read what was printed on it in large letters. He turned of a

[013-065] deadly hue, and a sudden horror seemed to fall upon him.

[013-066] ‘I will stay here, Stephen,’ said Rachael, quietly resuming her seat,

[013-067] ‘till the bells go Three. ’Tis to be done again at three, and then she

[013-068] may be left till morning.’

[013-069] ‘But thy rest agen to-morrow’s work, my dear.’

[013-070] ‘I slept sound last night. I can wake many nights, when I am put to it.

[013-071] ’Tis thou who art in need of rest—so white and tired. Try to sleep in

[013-072] the chair there, while I watch. Thou hadst no sleep last night, I can

[013-073] well believe. To-morrow’s work is far harder for thee than for me.’

[013-074] He heard the thundering and surging out of doors, and it seemed to him as

[013-075] if his late angry mood were going about trying to get at him. She had

[013-076] cast it out; she would keep it out; he trusted to her to defend him from

[013-077] himself.

[013-078] ‘She don’t know me, Stephen; she just drowsily mutters and stares. I

[013-079] have spoken to her times and again, but she don’t notice! ’Tis as well

[013-080] so. When she comes to her right mind once more, I shall have done what I

[013-081] can, and she never the wiser.’

[013-082] ‘How long, Rachael, is ’t looked for, that she’ll be so?’

[013-083] ‘Doctor said she would haply come to her mind to-morrow.’

[013-084] His eyes fell again on the bottle, and a tremble passed over him, causing

[013-085] him to shiver in every limb. She thought he was chilled with the wet.

[013-086] ‘No,’ he said, ‘it was not that. He had had a fright.’

[013-087] ‘A fright?’

[013-088] ‘Ay, ay! coming in. When I were walking. When I were thinking. When

[013-089] I—’ It seized him again; and he stood up, holding by the mantel-shelf,

[013-090] as he pressed his dank cold hair down with a hand that shook as if it

[013-091] were palsied.

[013-092] ‘Stephen!’

[013-093] She was coming to him, but he stretched out his arm to stop her.

[013-094] ‘No! Don’t, please; don’t. Let me see thee setten by the bed. Let me

[013-095] see thee, a’ so good, and so forgiving. Let me see thee as I see thee

[013-096] when I coom in. I can never see thee better than so. Never, never,

[013-097] never!’

[013-098] He had a violent fit of trembling, and then sunk into his chair. After a

[013-099] time he controlled himself, and, resting with an elbow on one knee, and

[013-100] his head upon that hand, could look towards Rachael. Seen across the dim

[013-101] candle with his moistened eyes, she looked as if she had a glory shining

[013-102] round her head. He could have believed she had. He did believe it, as

[013-103] the noise without shook the window, rattled at the door below, and went

[013-104] about the house clamouring and lamenting.

[013-105] ‘When she gets better, Stephen, ’tis to be hoped she’ll leave thee to

[013-106] thyself again, and do thee no more hurt. Anyways we will hope so now.

[013-107] And now I shall keep silence, for I want thee to sleep.’

[013-108] He closed his eyes, more to please her than to rest his weary head; but,

[013-109] by slow degrees as he listened to the great noise of the wind, he ceased

[013-110] to hear it, or it changed into the working of his loom, or even into the

[013-111] voices of the day (his own included) saying what had been really said.

[013-112] Even this imperfect consciousness faded away at last, and he dreamed a

[013-113] long, troubled dream.

[013-114] He thought that he, and some one on whom his heart had long been set—but

[013-115] she was not Rachael, and that surprised him, even in the midst of his

[013-116] imaginary happiness—stood in the church being married. While the

[013-117] ceremony was performing, and while he recognized among the witnesses some

[013-118] whom he knew to be living, and many whom he knew to be dead, darkness

[013-119] came on, succeeded by the shining of a tremendous light. It broke from

[013-120] one line in the table of commandments at the altar, and illuminated the

[013-121] building with the words. They were sounded through the church, too, as

[013-122] if there were voices in the fiery letters. Upon this, the whole

[013-123] appearance before him and around him changed, and nothing was left as it

[013-124] had been, but himself and the clergyman. They stood in the daylight

[013-125] before a crowd so vast, that if all the people in the world could have

[013-126] been brought together into one space, they could not have looked, he

[013-127] thought, more numerous; and they all abhorred him, and there was not one

[013-128] pitying or friendly eye among the millions that were fastened on his

[013-129] face. He stood on a raised stage, under his own loom; and, looking up at

[013-130] the shape the loom took, and hearing the burial service distinctly read,

[013-131] he knew that he was there to suffer death. In an instant what he stood

[013-132] on fell below him, and he was gone.

[013-133] —Out of what mystery he came back to his usual life, and to places that

[013-134] he knew, he was unable to consider; but he was back in those places by

[013-135] some means, and with this condemnation upon him, that he was never, in

[013-136] this world or the next, through all the unimaginable ages of eternity, to

[013-137] look on Rachael’s face or hear her voice. Wandering to and fro,

[013-138] unceasingly, without hope, and in search of he knew not what (he only

[013-139] knew that he was doomed to seek it), he was the subject of a nameless,

[013-140] horrible dread, a mortal fear of one particular shape which everything

[013-141] took. Whatsoever he looked at, grew into that form sooner or later. The

[013-142] object of his miserable existence was to prevent its recognition by any

[013-143] one among the various people he encountered. Hopeless labour! If he led

[013-144] them out of rooms where it was, if he shut up drawers and closets where

[013-145] it stood, if he drew the curious from places where he knew it to be

[013-146] secreted, and got them out into the streets, the very chimneys of the

[013-147] mills assumed that shape, and round them was the printed word.

[013-148] The wind was blowing again, the rain was beating on the house-tops, and

[013-149] the larger spaces through which he had strayed contracted to the four

[013-150] walls of his room. Saving that the fire had died out, it was as his eyes

[013-151] had closed upon it. Rachael seemed to have fallen into a doze, in the

[013-152] chair by the bed. She sat wrapped in her shawl, perfectly still. The

[013-153] table stood in the same place, close by the bedside, and on it, in its

[013-154] real proportions and appearance, was the shape so often repeated.

[013-155] He thought he saw the curtain move. He looked again, and he was sure it

[013-156] moved. He saw a hand come forth and grope about a little. Then the

[013-157] curtain moved more perceptibly, and the woman in the bed put it back, and

[013-158] sat up.

[013-159] With her woful eyes, so haggard and wild, so heavy and large, she looked

[013-160] all round the room, and passed the corner where he slept in his chair.

[013-161] Her eyes returned to that corner, and she put her hand over them as a

[013-162] shade, while she looked into it. Again they went all round the room,

[013-163] scarcely heeding Rachael if at all, and returned to that corner. He

[013-164] thought, as she once more shaded them—not so much looking at him, as

[013-165] looking for him with a brutish instinct that he was there—that no single

[013-166] trace was left in those debauched features, or in the mind that went

[013-167] along with them, of the woman he had married eighteen years before. But

[013-168] that he had seen her come to this by inches, he never could have believed

[013-169] her to be the same.

[013-170] All this time, as if a spell were on him, he was motionless and

[013-171] powerless, except to watch her.

[013-172] Stupidly dozing, or communing with her incapable self about nothing, she

[013-173] sat for a little while with her hands at her ears, and her head resting

[013-174] on them. Presently, she resumed her staring round the room. And now,

[013-175] for the first time, her eyes stopped at the table with the bottles on it.

[013-176] Straightway she turned her eyes back to his corner, with the defiance of

[013-177] last night, and moving very cautiously and softly, stretched out her

[013-178] greedy hand. She drew a mug into the bed, and sat for a while

[013-179] considering which of the two bottles she should choose. Finally, she

[013-180] laid her insensate grasp upon the bottle that had swift and certain death

[013-181] in it, and, before his eyes, pulled out the cork with her teeth.

[013-182] Dream or reality, he had no voice, nor had he power to stir. If this be

[013-183] real, and her allotted time be not yet come, wake, Rachael, wake!

[013-184] She thought of that, too. She looked at Rachael, and very slowly, very

[013-185] cautiously, poured out the contents. The draught was at her lips. A

[013-186] moment and she would be past all help, let the whole world wake and come

[013-187] about her with its utmost power. But in that moment Rachael started up

[013-188] with a suppressed cry. The creature struggled, struck her, seized her by

[013-189] the hair; but Rachael had the cup.

[013-190] Stephen broke out of his chair. ‘Rachael, am I wakin’ or dreamin’ this

[013-191] dreadfo’ night?’

[013-192] ‘’Tis all well, Stephen. I have been asleep, myself. ’Tis near three.

[013-193] Hush! I hear the bells.’

[013-194] The wind brought the sounds of the church clock to the window. They

[013-195] listened, and it struck three. Stephen looked at her, saw how pale she

[013-196] was, noted the disorder of her hair, and the red marks of fingers on her

[013-197] forehead, and felt assured that his senses of sight and hearing had been

[013-198] awake. She held the cup in her hand even now.

[013-199] ‘I thought it must be near three,’ she said, calmly pouring from the cup

[013-200] into the basin, and steeping the linen as before. ‘I am thankful I

[013-201] stayed! ’Tis done now, when I have put this on. There! And now she’s

[013-202] quiet again. The few drops in the basin I’ll pour away, for ’tis bad

[013-203] stuff to leave about, though ever so little of it.’ As she spoke, she

[013-204] drained the basin into the ashes of the fire, and broke the bottle on the

[013-205] hearth.

[013-206] She had nothing to do, then, but to cover herself with her shawl before

[013-207] going out into the wind and rain.

[013-208] ‘Thou’lt let me walk wi’ thee at this hour, Rachael?’

[013-209] ‘No, Stephen. ’Tis but a minute, and I’m home.’

[013-210] ‘Thou’rt not fearfo’;’ he said it in a low voice, as they went out at the

[013-211] door; ‘to leave me alone wi’ her!’

[013-212] As she looked at him, saying, ‘Stephen?’ he went down on his knee before

[013-213] her, on the poor mean stairs, and put an end of her shawl to his lips.

[013-214] ‘Thou art an Angel. Bless thee, bless thee!’

[013-215] ‘I am, as I have told thee, Stephen, thy poor friend. Angels are not

[013-216] like me. Between them, and a working woman fu’ of faults, there is a

[013-217] deep gulf set. My little sister is among them, but she is changed.’

[013-218] She raised her eyes for a moment as she said the words; and then they

[013-219] fell again, in all their gentleness and mildness, on his face.

[013-220] ‘Thou changest me from bad to good. Thou mak’st me humbly wishfo’ to be

[013-221] more like thee, and fearfo’ to lose thee when this life is ower, and a’

[013-222] the muddle cleared awa’. Thou’rt an Angel; it may be, thou hast saved my

[013-223] soul alive!’

[013-224] She looked at him, on his knee at her feet, with her shawl still in his

[013-225] hand, and the reproof on her lips died away when she saw the working of

[013-226] his face.

[013-227] ‘I coom home desp’rate. I coom home wi’out a hope, and mad wi’ thinking

[013-228] that when I said a word o’ complaint I was reckoned a unreasonable Hand.

[013-229] I told thee I had had a fright. It were the Poison-bottle on table. I

[013-230] never hurt a livin’ creetur; but happenin’ so suddenly upon ’t, I thowt,

[013-231] “How can _I_ say what I might ha’ done to myseln, or her, or both!”’

[013-232] She put her two hands on his mouth, with a face of terror, to stop him

[013-233] from saying more. He caught them in his unoccupied hand, and holding

[013-234] them, and still clasping the border of her shawl, said hurriedly:

[013-235] ‘But I see thee, Rachael, setten by the bed. I ha’ seen thee, aw this

[013-236] night. In my troublous sleep I ha’ known thee still to be there.

[013-237] Evermore I will see thee there. I nevermore will see her or think o’

[013-238] her, but thou shalt be beside her. I nevermore will see or think o’

[013-239] anything that angers me, but thou, so much better than me, shalt be by

[013-240] th’ side on’t. And so I will try t’ look t’ th’ time, and so I will try

[013-241] t’ trust t’ th’ time, when thou and me at last shall walk together far

[013-242] awa’, beyond the deep gulf, in th’ country where thy little sister is.’

[013-243] He kissed the border of her shawl again, and let her go. She bade him

[013-244] good night in a broken voice, and went out into the street.

[013-245] The wind blew from the quarter where the day would soon appear, and still

[013-246] blew strongly. It had cleared the sky before it, and the rain had spent

[013-247] itself or travelled elsewhere, and the stars were bright. He stood

[013-248] bare-headed in the road, watching her quick disappearance. As the

[013-249] shining stars were to the heavy candle in the window, so was Rachael, in

[013-250] the rugged [[fancy]] of this man, to the common experiences of his life.
