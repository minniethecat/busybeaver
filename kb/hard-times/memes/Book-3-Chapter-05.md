[033-001] FOUND

[033-002] DAY and night again, day and night again. No [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]]. Where

[033-003] was the man, and why did he not come back?

[033-004] Every night, [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] went to [[rachael|Rachael]]’s lodging, and sat with her in her

[033-005] small neat room. All day, Rachael toiled as such people must toil,

[033-006] whatever their anxieties. The smoke-serpents were indifferent who was

[033-007] lost or found, who turned out bad or good; the melancholy mad elephants,

[033-008] like the Hard Fact men, abated nothing of their set routine, whatever

[033-009] happened. Day and night again, day and night again. The monotony was

[033-010] unbroken. Even Stephen Blackpool’s disappearance was falling into the

[033-011] general way, and becoming as monotonous a wonder as any piece of

[033-012] machinery in [[coketown|Coketown]].

[033-013] ‘I misdoubt,’ said Rachael, ‘if there is as many as twenty left in all

[033-014] this place, who have any trust in the poor [[love|dear]] lad now.’

[033-015] She said it to Sissy, as they sat in her lodging, lighted only by the

[033-016] lamp at the street corner. Sissy had come there when it was already

[033-017] dark, to await her return from work; and they had since sat at the window

[033-018] where Rachael had found her, wanting no brighter light to shine on their

[033-019] sorrowful talk.

[033-020] ‘If it hadn’t been mercifully brought about, that I was to have you to

[033-021] speak to,’ pursued Rachael, ‘times are, when I think my mind would not

[033-022] have kept right. But I get hope and strength through you; and you

[033-023] believe that though appearances may rise against him, he will be proved

[033-024] clear?’

[033-025] ‘I do believe so,’ returned Sissy, ‘with my whole [[heart]]. I feel so

[033-026] certain, Rachael, that the confidence you hold in yours against all

[033-027] discouragement, is not like to be wrong, that I have no more doubt of him

[033-028] than if I had known him through as many years of trial as you have.’

[033-029] ‘And I, my dear,’ said Rachel, with a tremble in her voice, ‘have known

[033-030] him through them all, to be, according to his quiet ways, so faithful to

[033-031] everything honest and good, that if he was never to be heard of more, and

[033-032] I was to live to be a hundred years old, I could say with my last breath,

[033-033] God knows my heart. I have never once left trusting Stephen Blackpool!’

[033-034] ‘We all believe, up at the Lodge, Rachael, that he will be freed from

[033-035] suspicion, sooner or later.’

[033-036] ‘The better I know it to be so believed there, my dear,’ said Rachael,

[033-037] ‘and the kinder I feel it that you come away from there, purposely to

[033-038] comfort me, and keep me company, and be seen wi’ me when I am not yet

[033-039] free from all suspicion myself, the more grieved I am that I should ever

[033-040] have spoken those mistrusting words to the young lady. And yet I—’

[033-041] ‘You don’t mistrust her now, Rachael?’

[033-042] ‘Now that you have brought us more together, no. But I can’t at all

[033-043] times keep out of my mind—’

[033-044] Her voice so sunk into a low and slow communing with herself, that Sissy,

[033-045] sitting by her side, was obliged to listen with attention.

[033-046] ‘I can’t at all times keep out of my mind, mistrustings of some one. I

[033-047] can’t think who ’tis, I can’t think how or why it may be done, but I

[033-048] mistrust that some one has put Stephen out of the way. I mistrust that

[033-049] by his coming back of his own accord, and showing himself innocent before

[033-050] them all, some one would be confounded, who—to prevent that—has stopped

[033-051] him, and put him out of the way.’

[033-052] ‘That is a dreadful thought,’ said Sissy, turning pale.

[033-053] ‘It _is_ a dreadful thought to think he may be murdered.’

[033-054] Sissy shuddered, and turned paler yet.

[033-055] ‘When it makes its way into my mind, dear,’ said Rachael, ‘and it will

[033-056] come sometimes, though I do all I can to keep it out, wi’ counting on to

[033-057] high numbers as I work, and saying over and over again pieces that I knew

[033-058] when I were a [[childhood|child]]—I fall into such a wild, hot hurry, that, however

[033-059] tired I am, I want to walk fast, miles and miles. I must get the better

[033-060] of this before bed-time. I’ll walk home wi’ you.’

[033-061] ‘He might fall ill upon the journey back,’ said Sissy, faintly offering a

[033-062] worn-out scrap of hope; ‘and in such a case, there are many places on the

[033-063] road where he might stop.’

[033-064] ‘But he is in none of them. He has been sought for in all, and he’s not

[033-065] there.’

[033-066] ‘True,’ was Sissy’s reluctant admission.

[033-067] ‘He’d walk the journey in two days. If he was footsore and couldn’t

[033-068] walk, I sent him, in the letter he got, the money to ride, lest he should

[033-069] have none of his own to spare.’

[033-070] ‘Let us hope that to-morrow will bring something better, Rachael. Come

[033-071] into the air!’

[033-072] Her gentle hand adjusted Rachael’s shawl upon her shining black hair in

[033-073] the usual manner of her wearing it, and they went out. The night being

[033-074] fine, little knots of Hands were here and there lingering at street

[033-075] corners; but it was supper-time with the greater part of them, and there

[033-076] were but few people in the streets.

[033-077] ‘You’re not so hurried now, Rachael, and your hand is cooler.’

[033-078] ‘I get better, dear, if I can only walk, and breathe a little fresh.

[033-079] ‘Times when I can’t, I turn weak and confused.’

[033-080] ‘But you must not begin to fail, Rachael, for you may be wanted at any

[033-081] time to stand by Stephen. To-morrow is Saturday. If no news comes

[033-082] to-morrow, let us walk in the country on Sunday morning, and strengthen

[033-083] you for another week. Will you go?’

[033-084] ‘Yes, dear.’

[033-085] They were by this time in the street where [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s house stood.

[033-086] The way to Sissy’s destination led them past the door, and they were

[033-087] going straight towards it. Some train had newly arrived in Coketown,

[033-088] which had put a number of vehicles in motion, and scattered a

[033-089] considerable bustle about the town. Several coaches were rattling before

[033-090] them and behind them as they approached Mr. Bounderby’s, and one of the

[033-091] latter drew up with such briskness as they were in the act of passing the

[033-092] house, that they looked round involuntarily. The bright gaslight over

[033-093] Mr. Bounderby’s steps showed them [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] in the coach, in an

[033-094] ecstasy of excitement, struggling to open the door; Mrs. Sparsit seeing

[033-095] them at the same moment, called to them to stop.

[033-096] ‘It’s a coincidence,’ exclaimed Mrs. Sparsit, as she was released by the

[033-097] coachman. ‘It’s a Providence! Come out, ma’am!’ then said Mrs. Sparsit,

[033-098] to some one inside, ‘come out, or we’ll have you dragged out!’

[033-099] Hereupon, no other than [[mrs-pegler|the mysterious old woman]] descended. Whom Mrs.

[033-100] Sparsit incontinently collared.

[033-101] ‘Leave her alone, everybody!’ cried Mrs. Sparsit, with great energy.

[033-102] ‘Let nobody touch her. She belongs to me. Come in, ma’am!’ then said

[033-103] Mrs. Sparsit, reversing her former word of command. ‘Come in, ma’am, or

[033-104] we’ll have you dragged in!’

[033-105] The [[performance|spectacle]] of a matron of classical deportment, seizing an ancient

[033-106] woman by the throat, and hauling her into a dwelling-house, would have

[033-107] been under any circumstances, sufficient temptation to all true English

[033-108] stragglers so blest as to witness it, to force a way into that

[033-109] dwelling-house and see the matter out. But when the phenomenon was

[033-110] enhanced by the notoriety and mystery by this time associated all over

[033-111] the town with the Bank robbery, it would have lured the stragglers in,

[033-112] with an irresistible attraction, though the roof had been expected to

[033-113] fall upon their heads. Accordingly, the chance witnesses on the ground,

[033-114] consisting of the busiest of the neighbours to the number of some

[033-115] five-and-twenty, closed in after Sissy and Rachael, as they closed in

[033-116] after Mrs. Sparsit and her prize; and the whole body made a disorderly

[033-117] irruption into Mr. Bounderby’s dining-room, where the people behind lost

[033-118] not a moment’s time in mounting on the chairs, to get the better of the

[033-119] people in front.

[033-120] ‘Fetch Mr. Bounderby down!’ cried Mrs. Sparsit. ‘Rachael, young woman;

[033-121] you know who this is?’

[033-122] ‘It’s Mrs. Pegler,’ said Rachael.

[033-123] ‘I should think it is!’ cried Mrs. Sparsit, exulting. ‘Fetch Mr.

[033-124] Bounderby. Stand away, everybody!’ Here old Mrs. Pegler, muffling

[033-125] herself up, and shrinking from observation, whispered a word of entreaty.

[033-126] ‘Don’t tell me,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, aloud. ‘I have told you twenty

[033-127] times, coming along, that I will _not_ leave you till I have handed you

[033-128] over to him myself.’

[033-129] Mr. Bounderby now appeared, accompanied by [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] and [[tom-gradgrind|the whelp]],

[033-130] with whom he had been holding conference up-stairs. Mr. Bounderby looked

[033-131] more astonished than hospitable, at sight of this uninvited party in his

[033-132] dining-room.

[033-133] ‘Why, what’s the matter now!’ said he. ‘Mrs. Sparsit, ma’am?’

[033-134] ‘Sir,’ explained that worthy woman, ‘I trust it is my good fortune to

[033-135] produce a person you have much desired to find. Stimulated by my wish to

[033-136] relieve your mind, sir, and connecting together such imperfect clues to

[033-137] the part of the country in which that person might be supposed to reside,

[033-138] as have been afforded by the young woman, Rachael, fortunately now

[033-139] present to identify, I have had the happiness to succeed, and to bring

[033-140] that person with me—I need not say most unwillingly on her part. It has

[033-141] not been, sir, without some trouble that I have effected this; but

[033-142] trouble in your service is to me a pleasure, and hunger, thirst, and cold

[033-143] a real gratification.’

[033-144] Here Mrs. Sparsit ceased; for Mr. Bounderby’s visage exhibited an

[033-145] extraordinary [[solidarity|combination]] of all possible colours and expressions of

[033-146] discomfiture, as old Mrs. Pegler was disclosed to his view.

[033-147] ‘Why, what do you mean by this?’ was his highly unexpected demand, in

[033-148] great warmth. ‘I ask you, what do you mean by this, Mrs. Sparsit,

[033-149] ma’am?’

[033-150] ‘Sir!’ exclaimed Mrs. Sparsit, faintly.

[033-151] ‘Why don’t you mind your own business, ma’am?’ roared Bounderby. ‘How

[033-152] dare you go and poke your officious nose into my [[family]] affairs?’

[033-153] This allusion to her favourite feature overpowered Mrs. Sparsit. She sat

[033-154] down stiffly in a chair, as if she were frozen; and with a fixed stare at

[033-155] Mr. Bounderby, slowly grated her mittens against one another, as if they

[033-156] were frozen too.

[033-157] ‘My dear Josiah!’ cried Mrs. Pegler, trembling. ‘My darling boy! I am

[033-158] not to blame. It’s not my fault, Josiah. I told this lady over and over

[033-159] again, that I knew she was doing what would not be agreeable to you, but

[033-160] she would do it.’

[033-161] ‘What did you let her bring you for? Couldn’t you knock her cap off, or

[033-162] her tooth out, or scratch her, or do something or other to her?’ asked

[033-163] Bounderby.

[033-164] ‘My own boy! She threatened me that if I resisted her, I should be

[033-165] brought by constables, and it was better to come quietly than make that

[033-166] stir in such a’—Mrs. Pegler glanced timidly but proudly round the

[033-167] walls—‘such a fine house as this. Indeed, indeed, it is not my fault!

[033-168] My dear, noble, stately boy! I have always lived quiet, and secret,

[033-169] Josiah, my dear. I have never broken the condition once. I have never

[033-170] said I was your mother. I have admired you at a distance; and if I have

[033-171] come to town sometimes, with long times between, to take a [[pride|proud]] peep at

[033-172] you, I have done it unbeknown, my love, and gone away again.’

[033-173] Mr. Bounderby, with his hands in his pockets, walked in impatient

[033-174] mortification up and down at the side of the long dining-table, while the

[033-175] spectators greedily took in every syllable of Mrs. Pegler’s appeal, and

[033-176] at each succeeding syllable became more and more round-eyed. Mr.

[033-177] Bounderby still walking up and down when Mrs. Pegler had done, Mr.

[033-178] [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] addressed that maligned old lady:

[033-179] ‘I am surprised, madam,’ he observed with severity, ‘that in your old age

[033-180] you have the face to claim Mr. Bounderby for your son, after your

[033-181] [[nature|unnatural]] and inhuman treatment of him.’

[033-182] ‘_Me_ unnatural!’ cried poor old Mrs. Pegler. ‘_Me_ inhuman! To my dear

[033-183] boy?’

[033-184] ‘Dear!’ repeated Mr. Gradgrind. ‘Yes; dear in his self-made prosperity,

[033-185] madam, I dare say. Not very dear, however, when you deserted him in his

[033-186] infancy, and left him to the brutality of a drunken grandmother.’

[033-187] ‘_I_ deserted my Josiah!’ cried Mrs. Pegler, clasping her hands. ‘Now,

[033-188] Lord forgive you, sir, for your wicked imaginations, and for your scandal

[033-189] against the memory of my poor mother, who died in my arms before Josiah

[033-190] was born. May you repent of it, sir, and live to know better!’

[033-191] She was so very earnest and injured, that Mr. Gradgrind, shocked by the

[033-192] possibility which dawned upon him, said in a gentler tone:

[033-193] ‘Do you deny, then, madam, that you left your son to—to be brought up in

[033-194] the gutter?’

[033-195] ‘Josiah in the gutter!’ exclaimed Mrs. Pegler. ‘No such a thing, sir.

[033-196] Never! For [[shame]] on you! My dear boy knows, and will give _you_ to

[033-197] know, that though he come of humble parents, he come of parents that

[033-198] loved him as dear as the best could, and never thought it hardship on

[033-199] themselves to pinch a bit that he might write and cipher beautiful, and

[033-200] I’ve his books at home to show it! Aye, have I!’ said Mrs. Pegler, with

[033-201] indignant pride. ‘And my dear boy knows, and will give _you_ to know,

[033-202] sir, that after his beloved father died, when he was eight years old, his

[033-203] mother, too, could pinch a bit, as it was her [[duty]] and her pleasure and

[033-204] her pride to do it, to help him out in life, and put him ’prentice. And

[033-205] a steady lad he was, and a kind master he had to lend him a hand, and

[033-206] well he worked his own way forward to be rich and thriving. And _I_’ll

[033-207] give you to know, sir—for this my dear boy won’t—that though his mother

[033-208] kept but a little village shop, he never forgot her, but pensioned me on

[033-209] thirty pound a year—more than I want, for I put by out of it—only making

[033-210] the condition that I was to keep down in my own part, and make no boasts

[033-211] about him, and not trouble him. And I never have, except with looking at

[033-212] him once a year, when he has never knowed it. And it’s right,’ said poor

[033-213] old Mrs. Pegler, in affectionate championship, ‘that I _should_ keep down

[033-214] in my own part, and I have no doubts that if I was here I should do a

[033-215] many unbefitting things, and I am well contented, and I can keep my pride

[033-216] in my Josiah to myself, and I can love for love’s own sake! And I am

[033-217] ashamed of you, sir,’ said Mrs. Pegler, lastly, ‘for your slanders and

[033-218] suspicions. And I never stood here before, nor never wanted to stand

[033-219] here when my dear son said no. And I shouldn’t be here now, if it hadn’t

[033-220] been for being brought here. And for shame upon you, Oh, for shame, to

[033-221] accuse me of being a bad mother to my son, with my son standing here to

[033-222] tell you so different!’

[033-223] The bystanders, on and off the dining-room chairs, raised a murmur of

[033-224] sympathy with Mrs. Pegler, and Mr. Gradgrind felt himself innocently

[033-225] placed in a very distressing predicament, when Mr. Bounderby, who had

[033-226] never ceased walking up and down, and had every moment swelled larger and

[033-227] larger, and grown redder and redder, stopped short.

[033-228] ‘I don’t exactly know,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘how I come to be favoured

[033-229] with the attendance of the present company, but I don’t inquire. When

[033-230] they’re quite satisfied, perhaps they’ll be so good as to disperse;

[033-231] whether they’re satisfied or not, perhaps they’ll be so good as to

[033-232] disperse. I’m not bound to deliver a lecture on my family affairs, I

[033-233] have not undertaken to do it, and I’m not a going to do it. Therefore

[033-234] those who expect any explanation whatever upon that branch of the

[033-235] subject, will be disappointed—particularly Tom Gradgrind, and he can’t

[033-236] know it too soon. In reference to the Bank robbery, there has been a

[033-237] mistake made, concerning my mother. If there hadn’t been

[033-238] over-officiousness it wouldn’t have been made, and I hate

[033-239] over-officiousness at all times, whether or no. Good evening!’

[033-240] Although Mr. Bounderby carried it off in these terms, holding the door

[033-241] open for the company to depart, there was a blustering sheepishness upon

[033-242] him, at once extremely crestfallen and superlatively absurd. Detected as

[033-243] the [[humility|Bully of humility]], who had built his windy reputation upon lies, and

[033-244] in his boastfulness had put the honest [[truth]] as far away from him as if

[033-245] he had advanced the mean claim (there is no meaner) to tack himself on to

[033-246] a pedigree, he cut a most ridiculous figure. With the people filing off

[033-247] at the door he held, who he knew would carry what had passed to the whole

[033-248] town, to be given to the four winds, he could not have looked a Bully

[033-249] more shorn and forlorn, if he had had his ears cropped. Even that

[033-250] unlucky female, Mrs. Sparsit, fallen from her pinnacle of exultation into

[033-251] the Slough of Despond, was not in so bad a plight as that remarkable man

[033-252] and self-made Humbug, Josiah Bounderby of Coketown.

[033-253] Rachael and Sissy, leaving Mrs. Pegler to occupy a bed at her son’s for

[033-254] that night, walked together to the gate of [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]] and there parted.

[033-255] Mr. Gradgrind joined them before they had gone very far, and spoke with

[033-256] much interest of Stephen Blackpool; for whom he thought this signal

[033-257] failure of the suspicions against Mrs. Pegler was likely to work well.

[033-258] As to the whelp; throughout this scene as on all other late occasions, he

[033-259] had stuck close to Bounderby. He seemed to feel that as long as

[033-260] Bounderby could make no discovery without his knowledge, he was so far

[033-261] safe. He never visited his sister, and had only seen her once since she

[033-262] went home: that is to say on the night when he still stuck close to

[033-263] Bounderby, as already related.

[033-264] There was one dim unformed fear lingering about his sister’s mind, to

[033-265] which she never gave utterance, which surrounded the graceless and

[033-266] ungrateful boy with a dreadful mystery. The same dark possibility had

[033-267] presented itself in the same shapeless guise, this very day, to Sissy,

[033-268] when Rachael spoke of some one who would be confounded by Stephen’s

[033-269] return, having put him out of the way. [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] had never spoken of

[033-270] harbouring any suspicion of her brother in connexion with the robbery,

[033-271] she and Sissy had held no confidence on the subject, save in that one

[033-272] interchange of looks when the unconscious father rested his gray [[reason|head]] on

[033-273] his hand; but it was understood between them, and they both knew it.

[033-274] This other fear was so awful, that it hovered about each of them like a

[033-275] ghostly shadow; neither daring to think of its being near herself, far

[033-276] less of its being near the other.

[033-277] And still the forced spirit which the whelp had plucked up, throve with

[033-278] him. If Stephen Blackpool was not the thief, let him show himself. Why

[033-279] didn’t he?

[033-280] Another night. Another day and night. No Stephen Blackpool. Where was

[033-281] the man, and why did he not come back?
