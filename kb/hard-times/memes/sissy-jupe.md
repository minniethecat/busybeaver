# Sissy Jupe

Cecilia "Sissy" Jupe is the moral heroine of *Hard Times* — the circus-child whose inability to produce Gradgrindian definitions conceals a deep, intuitive wisdom that the novel's entire Utilitarian apparatus cannot recognise, and upon which every major character ultimately depends for redemption.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Cecilia Jupe
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "Girl number twenty" (Gradgrind's school); "Sissy" (her father's name for her, and the name by which the novel knows her); "Jupe" (Gradgrind's impersonal designation); "the stroller's child" (narrator, at the novel's emotional climax)
- **Role**: Moral heroine, emotional centre, counter-symbol to the Gradgrind system
- **Occupation**: Circus child (Sleary's Horse-Riding) → adopted daughter and pupil in the Gradgrind household → caretaker to Mrs. Gradgrind → moral anchor of the household
- **Family**: Father (circus performer, clown, dog-trainer — abandoned her believing she would fare better without him); mother (dancer, died in childbirth); adoptive father Thomas Gradgrind; later husband and children
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 2, "Murdering the Innocents" — "Girl number twenty" is called upon by Mr. Gradgrind and cannot produce a definition of a horse (PN: 002-025–002-047)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 9, "Final" — in the novel's prophetic close, "happy Sissy's happy children loving her; all children loving her" (PN: 037-158)

## Characterisation

Dickens presents Sissy Jupe as a figure of embodied moral intelligence — one who knows less than anyone in the Gradgrind school by its own measures, yet whose intuitive grasp of human need exceeds that of every other character. She is the novel's argument that love, loyalty, and imagination constitute a form of knowledge that Fact-based education not only fails to teach but actively destroys.

### Physical Description

Sissy is given her most sustained physical description through contrast with her opposite number, Bitzer, as they sit on opposite sides of the Gradgrind schoolroom in the same ray of sunlight. Where Bitzer is pale, colourless, and bloodless — "so light-eyed and light-haired that the self-same rays appeared to draw out of him what little colour he ever possessed" — Sissy is "so dark-eyed and dark-haired, that she seemed to receive a deeper and more lustrous colour from the sun, when it shone upon her" (PN: 002-060–002-063). This initial chiaroscuro — the warm, receptive child of the circus versus the cold, efficient child of the system — establishes the novel's central moral-aesthetic binary. Where Bitzer would "bleed white" if cut (PN: 002-069), Sissy bleeds warm life.

Recurring physical notes include her "dark eyes" (PN: 009-049), her "pretty modest head" (PN: 009-108), and — at the novel's emotional summit — the image of her face shining "like a beautiful light upon the darkness" of Louisa's despair (PN: 029-192–029-193). Dickens's visual language for Sissy consistently draws on light, warmth, and colour — everything Coketown and the Gradgrind system are not.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Sissy's speech is defined by emotional directness and a near-total absence of rhetorical self-consciousness. Where Bounderby blusters and Gradgrind pronounces and Harthouse insinuates, Sissy simply states: "Father loved me, first" (PN: 009-128) — four words that contain the novel's entire counter-argument to the doctrine of Facts. Her characteristic mode is the question asked without guile or calculation: "May I try?" (PN: 029-175); "Will you let me?" (PN: 029-159); "I am quite sure" (PN: 030-242). She blushes, she trembles, she curtseys; her body registers emotion her education never taught her to suppress. Her most devastating power in the novel — the ability to disarm James Harthouse and drive him from Coketown — proceeds entirely from her "child-like ingenuousness," her "modest fearlessness," her "entire forgetfulness of herself" (PN: 030-138–030-141).

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Sissy is introduced in the novel's second chapter as a failure: "Girl number twenty unable to define a horse!" (PN: 002-048). The scene is the novel's most famous and most densely ironic. Sissy has grown up with horses — her father is a horse-rider in Sleary's Circus, she has lived among performing horses her entire life — yet she cannot produce the kind of definition Gradgrind's system demands. Dickens's irony cuts both ways: the system that defines her as ignorant cannot perceive the knowledge she actually possesses, and cannot recognise that her inability to reduce a horse to a taxonomic list ("Quadruped. Graminivorous. Forty teeth…") is in fact the mark of someone who knows horses as living creatures rather than as collections of facts (PN: 002-047–002-075).

Her admission to the Gradgrind household is framed explicitly as an educational experiment. When Gradgrind finds Sissy abandoned by her father, he declares: "I have made up my mind to take you into my house… You will be reclaimed and formed" (PN: 007-177–007-188). She is to be living proof that the system works — a raw specimen of circus ignorance transformed by Fact. The novel immediately subverts this premise: Sissy fails at every subject, cannot calculate percentages, and persists in the "fantastic hope" that her father will return (PN: 009-241).

Her long confession to Louisa in Chapter 9 — the novel's single longest sustained passage of dialogue — reveals the inner world the system cannot reach. She tells of her father's failing career, his despair when the public would not laugh, his tenderness with her, their shared reading of "wrong books" (the *Arabian Nights* — "I am never to speak of them here"), and his final abandonment, sending her on an errand so that she would not see him go (PN: 009-117–009-219). The passage is a complete inversion of Gradgrindian values: her father is a clown, an illiterate man, a failure by every economic measure — and he loved her. "Father loved me, first" (PN: 009-128).

### Book II: Reaping

Sissy's role in the second book is largely subordinate — she nurses Mrs. Gradgrind, looks after young Jane, and waits for letters that never come — but her presence defines by counterpoint. While Louisa marries Bounderby without love, while Tom descends into gambling and debt, while Gradgrind presides over the "reaping" of his educational philosophy, Sissy alone in the household remains whole. She is the control group the experiment forgot to include: the child who was loved, and who therefore knows how to love.

Her most significant action in Book II is her persistent waiting for her father. "Every letter that I see in Mr. Gradgrind's hand takes my breath away and blinds my eyes, for I think it comes from father" (PN: 009-223–009-224). Gradgrind dismisses this as the baselessness of "fantastic hope" unsupported by sound principles — yet the novel's narrator observes that "it did seem (though not to him, for he saw nothing of it) as if fantastic hope could take as strong a hold as Fact" (PN: 009-240–009-241). The line is the novel's thesis in miniature.

### Book III: Garnering

Sissy's role in the final book is transformative — she becomes the agent of every major redemption the novel offers.

**Louisa's recovery**. When Louisa returns to Stone Lodge broken — "so proud and so hardened, so confused and troubled… that everything is stormy, dark, and wicked to me" — it is Sissy who receives her. The scene between them in Chapter 1 of Book III is the novel's emotional summit. Louisa, kneeling, clings "to this stroller's child" and begs: "Forgive me, pity me, help me! Have compassion on my great need, and let me lay this head of mine upon a loving heart!" Sissy's reply — "O lay it here! Lay it here, my dear" (PN: 029-197–029-199) — marks the exact point at which the novel's emotional logic reverses the Gradgrind hierarchy. The stroller's child has what the educated daughter lacks.

**Harthouse's removal**. In Chapter 2, Sissy confronts James Harthouse alone and, without threat or stratagem, compels him to leave Coketown forever. Her weapon is simple truthfulness: "You may be sure, sir, you will never see her again as long as you live" (PN: 030-134–030-135). Harthouse, a man who has navigated the world through charm and indirection, finds himself "vanquished at all points" by "her modest fearlessness, her truthfulness which put all artifice aside" (PN: 030-139–030-140). "It had no effect on Sissy, fact or no fact" (PN: 030-237) — the line is devastating in its simplicity. Against the one person in the novel who does not operate within his system of irony and self-interest, Harthouse has no defence.

**Tom's escape**. In Chapter 7, Sissy orchestrates Tom's escape through the circus — hiding him in Sleary's company in blackface disguise, and arranging his passage out of England. The plan is entirely Sissy's: "Sissy has effected it, father" (PN: 035-070). She returns to the circus community that raised her and asks them to shelter the brother of the woman she loves. The circus people, who "would never be missed by anybody with the least grain of imagination," prove more reliable than any institution in Coketown.

**The final vision**. The novel's closing paragraphs place Sissy at the centre of its prophetic resolution. She marries, has children, and raises them — not on Facts, but on "imaginative graces and delights, without which the heart of infancy will wither up" (PN: 037-162). The vision is deliberately opposed to the Gradgrind experiment: "happy Sissy's happy children loving her; all children loving her; she, grown learned in childish lore; thinking no innocent and pretty fancy ever to be despised" (PN: 037-158–037-160). Sissy becomes the mother Gradgrind's own children never had, raising a new generation with "a childhood of the mind no less than a childhood of the body" (PN: 037-154–037-155).

## Thematic Significance

Sissy Jupe is the novel's most complete counter-argument to the Gradgrind system. Where that system values what can be measured, she embodies what cannot be: love, loyalty, imagination, and intuitive moral judgement. Her "failure" at the Gradgrind school — she cannot define a horse, cannot calculate percentages, cannot state whether a nation with fifty millions of money is "prosperous" without first knowing "who had got the money, and whether any of it was mine" (PN: 009-079) — is Dickens's demonstration that the school's definition of success is itself a moral failure.

The novel structures her as a living repudiation of the Fact/Fancy binary. Sissy is not "fanciful" in the sense of being unrealistic; she is in fact the most practically effective character in the book. She nurses Mrs. Gradgrind, rears young Jane, recovers Louisa, banishes Harthouse, saves Tom, and eventually raises a family. Her effectiveness proceeds precisely from the qualities the Gradgrind system would extinguish: her capacity to love, to imagine another's pain, to act from loyalty rather than calculation. She is the novel's proof that the "wisdom of the Heart" (PN: 035-036) is not a sentimental luxury but a practical necessity.

The horse scene in Chapter 2 functions as the novel's philosophical key. Sissy knows horses — has lived among them, cared for them, grown up with them — but she cannot produce Gradgrind's definition. Bitzer, who has probably never touched a horse in his life, can recite: "Quadruped. Graminivorous. Forty teeth…" Dickens's point is that the kind of knowledge the system values has no necessary relationship to the kind of knowledge that matters. Sissy's "ignorance" is in fact a deeper form of knowing — the knowledge that comes from living with and caring for a thing, rather than classifying it. (PN: 002-047–002-075)

At every turn, the novel insists that what Sissy offers — love, loyalty, the persistence of "fantastic hope" — is more real and more durable than the Facts on which Coketown is built. "It had no effect on Sissy, fact or no fact" (PN: 030-237): the line is Dickens's final verdict on the sufficiency of Facts as a guide to life.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: Adoptive father and ideological antagonist. Gradgrind takes Sissy in as an educational experiment — "You will be reclaimed and formed" (PN: 007-187–007-188) — and she becomes instead the agent of his moral reformation. By the novel's end, the reformed Gradgrind has learned from Sissy what his system could not teach: that "there is a wisdom of the Head, and... there is a wisdom of the Heart" (PN: 035-036).
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: Sissy's foil, confidante, and ultimately the object of her saving love. Where Sissy was loved by her father and carries that love as emotional literacy, Louisa was raised on Facts and is emotionally illiterate. Sissy's love heals Louisa where Gradgrind's philosophy could not. Louisa kneeling at Sissy's feet — "Forgive me, pity me, help me!" (PN: 029-197) — is the novel's most radical image of the inversion of its own social hierarchy.
- **[[Tom-Gradgrind]] (Tom)**: The Gradgrind son whom Sissy helps escape, despite his self-absorption and moral bankruptcy. Her loyalty to Tom is an extension of her love for Louisa.
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: The factory owner and self-made-man who regards Sissy with contempt — she is a "stroller's child," beneath his notice. His dismissal of her mirrors the system's inability to perceive her value.
- **[[Bitzer]]**: Sissy's structural opposite — the successful product of Gradgrind education. Where Sissy cannot define a horse but can feel for one, Bitzer can define a horse but "would bleed white" if cut (PN: 002-069). Bitzer becomes the novel's informer and antagonist; Sissy becomes its saviour. The novel's moral arithmetic is clear: better to be Sissy, who knows nothing and loves everything, than Bitzer, who knows everything and loves nothing.
- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: The idle gentleman whose designs on Louisa Sissy single-handedly defeats. Her absolute truthfulness is the one weapon against which his irony has no defence.
- **[[Slearys-Circus]]**: Sissy's original community — the world of Fancy from which she came and to which she returns to save Tom. The circus's willingness to help, no questions asked, stands in pointed contrast to the institutional morality of Coketown.
- **Her Father (the Clown)**: The unnamed circus performer whose love for Sissy — and his belief that she would be better off without him — is the novel's foundational act of love. "Father loved me, first" (PN: 009-128). He is Sissy's proof that love exists, and the source of her capacity to love others.

## Critical Reception

> F. R. Leavis, in *The Great Tradition* (1948), identified Sissy Jupe as central to the novel's "fable" form. For Leavis, Sissy is not a realistic character but a moral symbol — the embodiment of what the Gradgrind world lacks. Her "simplicity" is not naivety but a concentrated moral intelligence that the novel's Utilitarian characters cannot comprehend.

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), offered a more sceptical reading: Sissy's circus origins and her redemptive function risk sentimentality, substituting individual kindness for the structural critique industrial capitalism required. Williams's critique has been influential, though recent feminist and ethical criticism has tended to recover Sissy as an active moral agent rather than a passive symbol.

> David Lodge, in *Language of Fiction* (1966), analysed the horse-definition scene as a linguistic masterstroke: Sissy's silent knowledge versus Bitzer's verbal catalogue stages a critique of language itself — of what can be said in the Gradgrind idiom, and what must remain unsaid.
