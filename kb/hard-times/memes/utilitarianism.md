# Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism — the radical reform philosophy that proposed to rebuild society on the single principle of "the greatest happiness of the greatest number" — is the intellectual antagonist of *Hard Times*. Dickens does not engage the philosophy through formal argument but through embodiment: Benthamite calculation becomes Gradgrind's square forefinger, Bounderby's self-justifying bluster, and the "plain, bare, monotonous vault" of M'Choakumchild's schoolroom. The novel's verdict is that a philosophy that can only count what can be measured will inevitably mistreat what cannot — love, imagination, loyalty, and the irreducible singularity of a human life.

## Definition

In the context of *Hard Times*, Utilitarianism functions as both a specific historical target (the Benthamite programme of rational reform) and a broader diagnosis of the industrial age's tendency to treat persons as instruments. Bentham's *Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* (1789) proposed the "felicific calculus" — the quantification of pleasure and pain as the basis for moral and political decision. Applied to education, this meant a curriculum of "useful knowledge" purged of poetry, imagination, and classical humanism. Applied to political economy, it meant that human beings were "hands" — factors of production whose welfare could be calculated in wages and mortality statistics. Dickens saw in this programme not scientific progress but a new form of cruelty: cruelty that called itself reason. (PN: 001-002)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's critique of Utilitarianism operates through dramatic irony: the philosophy's own logic is allowed to unfold until it destroys the family of its most fervent advocate. Thomas Gradgrind is not a villain but a true believer — "a man of realities. A man of facts and calculations" (PN: 002-002) — and his children's ruin is the novel's empirical refutation of his creed. Louisa's emotional starvation, Tom's moral collapse, and Gradgrind's own anguished self-reckoning are not sentimental counter-arguments to Utilitarianism; they are, in the novel's terms, the *results* of Utilitarianism — the harvest of a system that sowed nothing but Facts.

The schoolroom scenes of Book I are the novel's most concentrated satire of Utilitarian education. M'Choakumchild, "turned... out of an enormous factory" of teacher training, knows "all about all the Water Sheds of all the world" and can " grind false children to any fineness" — yet he possesses no wisdom, no sympathy, and no capacity to recognise the human being behind the pupil (PN: 002-010). The factory metaphor is precise: Utilitarian education does not form persons; it manufactures units.

The novel also indicts Utilitarian political economy. When Bounderby dismisses the "Hands" as "a set of rascals and rebels" who "want to be set up in a coach and six, and to be fed on turtle soup and venison, with a gold spoon" (PN: 011-039), he is applying the logic of Benthamite calculation to labour relations: workers are costs to be minimised, and their grievances are irrational because they have not been mathematically demonstrated. Stephen Blackpool's tragedy — a man of integrity destroyed by a system that recognises neither integrity nor tragedy — is the human cost of this logic.

The novel reserves its narrative denouement for the disclosure that Utilitarianism's own patriarch cannot live by its principles. Gradgrind's conversion — from "Now, what I want is, Facts" (PN: 001-002) to the chastened recognition that "there is a wisdom of the Head, and... there is a wisdom of the Heart" (PN: 035-036) — is the novel's final word on the sufficiency of calculation as a life philosophy.

### Key Passages

> "With his unbending, utilitarian, matter-of-fact face, he hardened her again; and the moment shot away into the past, leaving her alone with the remembrance of the look he threw upon her." (PN: 015-132)

The novel's single explicit use of the word "utilitarian" — applied as an adjective to Gradgrind's face at the moment he fails his daughter. Louisa has come to her father in desperation, seeking an acknowledgment of her suffering, and his Utilitarian expression "hardens her again." The sentence enacts the philosophy's consequence: not illumination but hardening, not connection but isolation.

> "Utilitarian economists, skeletons of schoolmasters, Commissioners of Fact, genteel and used-up infidels, gabblers of many little dog's-eared creeds, the poor you will have always with you." (PN: 037-014)

The novel's closing catalogue of the forces that have failed the poor. "Utilitarian economists" appear first — the theorists whose calculations justified the industrial order — alongside the educators and commissioners who implemented it. The biblical echo ("the poor you will have always with you," Mark 14:7) is charged: Christ's words become, in Dickens's recontextualisation, an indictment of a philosophy that accepted poverty as a datum rather than a scandal.

> "Political Economy, she had only yesterday been set right by a prattler three feet high, for returning to the question, 'What is the first principle of this science?'" (PN: 008-093)

The satire of Political Economy — the intellectual cousin of Utilitarianism — is sharpened by its domestic scale. Even the Gradgrind nursery is a seminar room, and even a toddler can be indoctrinated into a "science" that treats human relations as market transactions. The absurdity of a "prattler three feet high" lecturing on political economy is Dickens's method of exposing the absurdity of the doctrine itself.

## Historical Context

Jeremy Bentham (1748–1832) published *An Introduction to the Principles of Morals and Legislation* in 1789, proposing that the moral quality of an action could be calculated by measuring its tendency to produce pleasure or pain. The principle — "the greatest happiness of the greatest number" — was radical in its egalitarianism (everyone counted for one, no one for more than one) and reductive in its method (all values must be commensurable on a single scale).

By the 1830s, Bentham's followers — James Mill, John Stuart Mill, Edwin Chadwick — had translated Utilitarian philosophy into administrative practice. Chadwick's design of the New Poor Law (1834), with its workhouse system and "less eligibility" principle (conditions in the workhouse must be worse than the worst independent labourer's lot), was Utilitarian calculation applied to poverty: deterrence was rational because it minimised the "cost" of relief. Dickens's satire of the "Poor Law" in *Oliver Twist* (1837) had already made him one of Utilitarianism's most prominent literary opponents.

The "Manchester School" of political economy — Richard Cobden, John Bright, and the Anti-Corn Law League — extended Utilitarian logic to trade policy, arguing that free markets would naturally produce the greatest aggregate welfare. Dickens's Coketown, with its "interminable serpents of smoke" and its inhabitants reduced to "Hands," is in part a response to their optimism. The novel insists that aggregate welfare can conceal individual misery — that a philosophy that counts only what it can measure will systematically undercount suffering.

## Embodied By

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: The human incarnation of Benthamite principle. A retired wholesale hardware merchant who models his school, his household, and his parenting on the conviction that "everything... was to be paid for" and nothing exists that cannot be weighed, measured, or priced. His tragedy — and the novel's deepest argument — is that he is not a hypocrite but a sincere believer whose sincerity destroys his children.
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Utilitarianism as self-justifying bombast. Bounderby invokes political economy to rationalise his own wealth and his workers' poverty, but his famous rise-from-the-gutter narrative — the "Fact" on which his authority rests — is eventually exposed as a fiction. He demonstrates that Utilitarian "facts" are never neutral: those in power decide what counts as a fact.
- **[[MChoakumchild]]**: Utilitarianism applied to education. A teacher manufactured by a training system that prizes information over wisdom, he "knew all about all the Water Sheds of all the world" yet is incapable of recognising a child's soul. His name — "choke-um-child" — is the novel's judgement on Utilitarian pedagogy.

## Contrasted With

- **[[fancy]]**: If Utilitarianism is the philosophy of measurable value, Fancy is the principle of immeasurable worth — the imaginative faculty that enables play, wonder, and sympathy. Sleary's Circus, with its absurd horseback riders and dancing dogs, is the novel's image of a world that escapes the Utilitarian calculus. (PN: 003-120)
- **[[heart]]**: The novel's central binary — Head versus Heart — maps directly onto the Utilitarian debate. The "wisdom of the Heart" that Gradgrind finally acknowledges is precisely what Benthamite calculation cannot access. Love, loyalty, and fellow-feeling are not quantities to be optimised; they are dispositions to be cultivated. (PN: 035-036)
- **[[imagination]]**: Utilitarianism's constitutive blind spot. A philosophy that can only count may be accurate, but it cannot be adequate — because adequacy to human experience requires the capacity to enter into another's suffering. Imagination is the faculty Utilitarianism denies, and its absence is what makes Gradgrind's rule so devastating.

## Critical Perspectives

> John Stuart Mill, Bentham's most distinguished disciple, published *Utilitarianism* in 1861 — seven years after *Hard Times*. Mill's version of the philosophy was far more nuanced than Bentham's, distinguishing higher from lower pleasures and insisting that "it is better to be a human being dissatisfied than a pig satisfied." Mill's reformulation can be read, in part, as a response to the kind of critique Dickens's novel embodied: the charge that Benthamite Utilitarianism could not account for the qualitative dimension of human experience.

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), argued that Dickens's critique of Utilitarianism was powerful but finally incomplete. By locating the alternative to Utilitarianism in the circus — a world of play, generosity, and spontaneous fellow-feeling — Dickens offered, in Williams's view, a sentimental resolution to a structural problem. The novel could imagine a circus that escapes the logic of industrial capitalism but could not imagine transforming the industrial system itself.

> Martha Nussbaum, in *Poetic Justice* (1995), read *Hard Times* as a defence of the "literary imagination" against the Utilitarian reduction of persons to economic functions. For Nussbaum, the novel makes a philosophical argument through narrative form: the reader's sympathetic engagement with characters like Louisa and Stephen is itself the education in "fancy" that the Gradgrindian system denies. Reading fiction, in this account, is the antidote to Utilitarian calculation.
