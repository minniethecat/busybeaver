# Slackbridge

Slackbridge is the trade-union delegate in *Hard Times* and the novel's most sustained portrait of a working-class demagogue. His function in the narrative is to demonstrate that class solidarity without individual conscience is as dangerous as capital without compassion — a structural parallel to [[Josiah-Bounderby]] that Dickens draws through the symmetry of their rhetoric, their self-interest, and their shared willingness to use Stephen Blackpool as an instrument.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Slackbridge (no first name given)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "the delegate" (PN: 032-041); "the orator" (PN: 020-084); "fugleman" (PN: 020-199); "a regular qualified leader of the people: that is, a most confounded scoundrel" (Bounderby's epithet, PN: 021-038)
- **Role**: Antagonistic foil — the demagogue of labour, structurally paired with Bounderby as demagogue of capital
- **Occupation**: Trade union delegate and orator for the United Aggregate Tribunal
- **Family**: Not stated
- **First Appearance**: Book II, Chapter 4 — "MEN AND BROTHERS": introduced mid-speech, declaiming to the assembled Hands at the union meeting, his physical description delivered by the narrator as a judgment on the gap between his pretensions and his person (PN: 020-002–020-027)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III, Chapter 4 — "LOST": having denounced Stephen Blackpool as a thief and a craven, he sits "demonstratively panting" after receiving three cheers from the assembled crowd (PN: 032-090)

## Characterisation

Dickens introduces Slackbridge through his rhetoric before giving him a body — a structural choice that establishes the primacy of performance over person in this character. The reader first encounters Slackbridge's voice: "Oh, my friends, the down-trodden operatives of Coketown!" (PN: 020-002), a cascade of appositional vocatives that multiply without deepening, naming the workers in ever-longer chains of fellow-ship without specifying what action they are to take. Only after the narrator has shown us the speech does he show us the speaker — and the contrast is the point.

The narrator's judgment is unusually direct, even for Dickens. Slackbridge is "not so honest, he was not so manly, he was not so good-humoured" as the workers he addresses; "he substituted cunning for their simplicity, and passion for their safe solid sense" (PN: 020-026–020-027). This is not a narrator describing a character; it is a narrator prosecuting one. The verb "substituted" is the key: Slackbridge's demagoguery is a replacement of authentic working-class qualities with their theatrical counterfeits.

### Physical Description

Slackbridge is "an ill-made, high-shouldered man, with lowering brows, and his features crushed into an habitually sour expression" (PN: 020-027). The phrase "crushed into" suggests a face shaped not by nature but by permanent resentment — the sourness has become structural, as though his features have been pressed into a mould of grievance. He dresses in "mongrel dress" (PN: 020-028), neither the plain working clothes of the Hands nor the gentleman's attire of the masters, and this sartorial indeterminacy is itself Dickens's signal: Slackbridge belongs to neither class but exploits both.

His characteristic gesture — "holding out his right hand at arm's length (as the manner of all Slackbridges is), to still the thundering sea" (PN: 020-086) — is a studied piece of oratorical choreography, the gesture of command disguised as the gesture of appeal. After Stephen's expulsion, "Slackbridge, who had kept his oratorical arm extended during the going out, as if he were repressing with infinite solicitude and by a wonderful moral power the vehement passions of the multitude" (PN: 020-186–020-187), maintains the pose even when it serves no practical purpose, because the pose is the purpose: Slackbridge exists in the posture of leadership, not in its substance.

Another telling detail: when seated, Slackbridge wipes "his hot forehead — always from left to right, and never the reverse way" (PN: 020-105). The narrator's parenthetical "always" and "never" are a small comic stroke that reveals a larger truth — Slackbridge's gestures, even the involuntary ones, are mechanised, habitual, the bodily equivalent of his rhetorical formulas.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Slackbridge's rhetoric is Dickens's exhibit in the case against demagogic oratory. His speeches are built on three devices: (1) **appositional inflation** — the piling-up of near-synonyms ("my friends and fellow-countrymen, the down-trodden operatives of Coketown, oh, my fellow-brothers and fellow-workmen and fellow-citizens and fellow-men" [PN: 032-043–032-045]); (2) **classical pretension** — invocations of Roman Brutus and Spartan mothers that aggrandise a trade dispute into epic (PN: 020-189–020-192); and (3) **apocalyptic moralising** — casting Stephen's refusal to join the union as a crime against "the holy and eternal privileges of Brotherhood" (PN: 020-010) and a "sacred and a God-like cause" (PN: 020-196).

The language is overheated to the point of self-parody. When Stephen is accused of the bank robbery, Slackbridge calls him "that dastard craven" (PN: 032-072), "a thief! A plunderer! A proscribed fugitive, with a price upon his head" (PN: 032-074–032-075), and demands the workers "crush the viper, who would bring this stain and shame upon the God-like race" (PN: 032-060). The escalation from "dastard craven" to "viper" to metaphysical threat is a textbook demagogue's move: the particular (Stephen) is inflated into the cosmic (the defilement of a God-like race), and the inflated abstraction justifies any penalty.

Dickens's narrator intervenes to label this method: "what other froth and fume he had in him" (PN: 020-014). The phrase "froth and fume" dismisses Slackbridge's oratory as substance-less agitation — soap and smoke, the by-products of a mechanical churning that produces heat but no light. The final description — "gnashing and perspiring after a prodigious sort" (PN: 032-085) — reduces Slackbridge to his body: a sweating, grinding machine whose verbal output is the overflow of physical exertion rather than thought.

## Role in the Narrative

Slackbridge appears in two extended scenes, both structured around Stephen Blackpool, and both turning on the same mechanism: Slackbridge speaks, the crowd responds, Stephen is isolated.

**Book II, Chapter 4 ("MEN AND BROTHERS")**. Slackbridge addresses the union meeting, denouncing Stephen as "a traitor" (PN: 020-165) for refusing to join the combination. When Stephen is permitted to speak — at the insistence of the assembled Hands, not Slackbridge — he explains his position with quiet dignity, and Dickens records the effect: "There was a propriety, not to say a dignity in these words, that made the hearers yet more quiet and attentive" (PN: 020-136). The moment is crucial: Stephen's plain speech moves the crowd more than Slackbridge's oratory, and Slackbridge knows it. He "jumped up and stood beside him, gnashing and tearing" (PN: 020-121), the verb "gnashing" suggesting not anger but a kind of mechanical, frustrated consumption — Slackbridge grinding against the fact that sincerity is more powerful than rhetoric.

After Stephen departs, Slackbridge retakes command by invoking classical parallels — Brutus, Spartan mothers (PN: 020-189–020-192) — and whipping the crowd into cheers for the United Aggregate Tribunal. The narrator's verdict on the crowd's complicity is carefully measured: "The multitude of doubtful faces (a little conscience-stricken) brightened at the sound" (PN: 020-200). The parenthetical "a little conscience-stricken" is the novel's ethical barometer: the workers know they have done wrong, but Slackbridge's rhythmic cheering drowns out the conscience they cannot quite silence.

**Book III, Chapter 4 ("LOST")**. After Stephen has been framed for the bank robbery, Slackbridge seizes the wanted poster as "that damning document" (PN: 032-046) and delivers a speech of pure prosecutorial invention. He claims to have "pursued him through all his intricate windings" (PN: 032-064) and "hurled him out from amongst us" (PN: 032-066) — a rewriting of history in which Slackbridge, not the crowd's own decency, silenced Stephen in Book II. The resolution of disownership (PN: 032-080–032-083) is Slackbridge's ultimate rhetorical product: having created the conditions for Stephen's isolation, he now formalises that isolation as union policy, making the group's cruelty look like principle.

**Book II, Chapter 5**. Slackbridge does not appear in person but is named by Bounderby, who calls him "a most confounded scoundrel" (PN: 021-038) and threatens to "make an example of half a dozen Slackbridges" (PN: 021-146). Stephen's reply — that a hundred Slackbridges could be sewn into sacks and sunk in the deepest ocean, and the "commotion" would continue, because Slackbridge is a symptom, not a cause (PN: 021-153–021-165) — is the novel's most perceptive political analysis. Stephen understands that Slackbridge fills a structural need: the workers' grievances are real, and if Slackbridge did not articulate them, someone else would.

## Thematic Significance

Slackbridge embodies Dickens's critique of **collective coercion** as the mirror image of industrial exploitation. Bounderby uses capital to compel obedience; Slackbridge uses solidarity to compel conformity. Neither man can accommodate Stephen Blackpool, who insists on the primacy of individual conscience over collective demand. The novel's structure — Stephen is crushed between Bounderby on one side and Slackbridge on the other — makes the parallel explicit: "the two so deeply separated" (PN: 022-193) are not labour and capital but two forms of organised power, each demanding that the individual surrender his judgment to the group.

The portrait is, by Dickens's own admission and by the judgment of critics from his time to the present, **problematic as political analysis**. Dickens gives voice to no articulate unionist — no worker who could explain why collective bargaining might be a rational response to the power asymmetry between a factory owner and a single hand. Slackbridge's rhetoric is always presented as "froth and fume" (PN: 020-014), his motives as self-serving, his methods as manipulative. The novel provides no counter-example of a legitimate labour organiser; the honest workers — Stephen, Rachael — are precisely the ones who refuse to organise.

Within the novel's moral framework, however, Slackbridge serves a consistent function: he demonstrates that **rhetoric without truth** is as destructive as calculation without compassion. His speeches are the linguistic equivalent of Gradgrind's Facts — a closed system of propositions that admits no counter-evidence and tolerates no dissent. When a lone voice calls out "Slackbridge, y'or over hetter in't; y'or a goen too fast" (PN: 032-087–032-088), the novel treats this as the remnant of working-class good sense — but also as politically ineffective: "these were pigmies against an army; the general assemblage subscribed to the gospel according to Slackbridge" (PN: 032-088–032-090). The word "gospel" is carefully chosen: Slackbridge's oratory is a secular religion, complete with sacred causes, holy scrolls, and God-like races, and his audience's assent is an act of faith, not reason.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: Slackbridge's antagonist and victim. Slackbridge's rhetorical pursuit of Stephen — from the union meeting (PN: 020-121–020-195) to the placard denunciation (PN: 032-046–032-090) — is the novel's case study in how collective power can be wielded against an individual with as much cruelty as any factory owner's. Stephen's quiet refusal — "I ha' nowt to sen about it" (PN: 021-021) — is the moral opposite of Slackbridge's verbal torrent: sincerity against rhetoric, silence against noise, one man speaking for himself against one man speaking for the crowd
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: The structural parallel to Slackbridge. Bounderby denounces Slackbridge as "a most confounded scoundrel" (PN: 021-038) while performing the same function — the exploitation of workers through a self-serving ideology — from the other side of the class divide. Both men use Stephen as an instrument; both men demand that Stephen sacrifice his integrity to their cause; both men are revealed, in their different registers, to be frauds
- **[[Rachael]]**: The implicit contrast to Slackbridge. Where Slackbridge speaks for the crowd in overheated abstractions, Rachael speaks for Stephen in quiet particularity. Where Slackbridge demands collective conformity, Rachael offers individual care. She is the alternative model of working-class solidarity — solidarity as love, not as coercion — that the novel holds up against Slackbridge's demagoguery

## Critical Reception

> George Bernard Shaw, in his preface to *Hard Times* (1912), noted that Dickens "knew nothing of the Trade Union movement" and that Slackbridge was "a mere caricature, drawn from the outside, of the sort of man Dickens supposed a labour leader to be." Shaw, himself a socialist, nevertheless conceded that Dickens's broader target — the tyranny of organised opinion over individual conscience — was legitimate, even if his example was poorly chosen.

> F. R. Leavis, in *The Great Tradition* (1948), defended Slackbridge as "not a portrait of a trade unionist but a portrait of a type — the demagogue, the manipulator of mass emotion — that happens in this instance to operate in a trade-union context." Leavis argued that the novel's structure — Slackbridge and Bounderby as parallel demagogues — was more important than the empirical accuracy of the Slackbridge portrait.

> Contemporary critics, including David Craig in *The Industrial Novels* (1979), have been less forgiving. Craig observed that Dickens "gives no space to the legitimate grievances that produced unionism in the first place" and that Slackbridge's function is to "discredit collective action by equating it with demagoguery." The absence of any articulate, honest unionist in the novel — Stephen and Rachael are precisely the workers who refuse to join — is, in this reading, a structural flaw that limits the novel's claim to be a comprehensive portrait of industrial society.
