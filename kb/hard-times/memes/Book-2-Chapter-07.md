[023-001] GUNPOWDER

[023-002] MR. JAMES HARTHOUSE, ‘going in’ for his adopted party, soon began to

[023-003] score. With the aid of a little more coaching for the political sages, a

[023-004] little more genteel listlessness for the general society, and a tolerable

[023-005] management of the assumed honesty in dishonesty, most effective and most

[023-006] patronized of the polite deadly sins, he speedily came to be considered

[023-007] of much promise. The not being troubled with earnestness was a grand

[023-008] point in his favour, enabling him to take to the hard Fact fellows with

[023-009] as good a grace as if he had been born one of the tribe, and to throw all

[023-010] other tribes overboard, as conscious hypocrites.

[023-011] ‘Whom none of us believe, my [[love|dear]] [[louisa-gradgrind|Mrs. Bounderby]], and who do not believe

[023-012] themselves. The only difference between us and the professors of virtue

[023-013] or benevolence, or philanthropy—never mind the name—is, that we know it

[023-014] is all meaningless, and say so; while they know it equally and will never

[023-015] say so.’

[023-016] Why should she be shocked or warned by this reiteration? It was not so

[023-017] unlike her father’s principles, and her early [[education|training]], that it need

[023-018] startle her. Where was the great difference between the two schools,

[023-019] when each chained her down to material realities, and inspired her with

[023-020] no faith in anything else? What was there in her soul for James

[023-021] [[james-harthouse|Harthouse]] to destroy, which [[thomas-gradgrind|Thomas Gradgrind]] had nurtured there in its

[023-022] state of innocence!

[023-023] It was even the worse for her at this pass, that in her mind—implanted

[023-024] there before her eminently practical father began to form it—a struggling

[023-025] disposition to believe in a wider and nobler [[humanity]] than she had ever

[023-026] heard of, constantly strove with doubts and resentments. With doubts,

[023-027] because the aspiration had been so laid waste in her [[childhood|youth]]. With

[023-028] resentments, because of the wrong that had been done her, if it were

[023-029] indeed a whisper of the [[truth]]. Upon a [[nature]] long accustomed to

[023-030] self-suppression, thus torn and divided, the Harthouse philosophy came as

[023-031] a relief and justification. Everything being hollow and worthless, she

[023-032] had missed nothing and sacrificed nothing. What did it matter, she had

[023-033] said to her father, when he proposed her husband. What did it matter,

[023-034] she said still. With a scornful self-reliance, she asked herself, What

[023-035] did anything matter—and went on.

[023-036] Towards what? Step by step, onward and downward, towards some end, yet

[023-037] so gradually, that she believed herself to remain motionless. As to Mr.

[023-038] Harthouse, whither _he_ tended, he neither considered nor cared. He had

[023-039] no particular design or plan before him: no energetic wickedness ruffled

[023-040] his lassitude. He was as much amused and interested, at present, as it

[023-041] became so fine a [[gentleman]] to be; perhaps even more than it would have

[023-042] been consistent with his reputation to confess. Soon after his arrival

[023-043] he languidly wrote to his brother, the honourable and jocular member,

[023-044] that the Bounderbys were ‘great fun;’ and further, that the female

[023-045] [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]], instead of being the Gorgon he had expected, was young, and

[023-046] remarkably pretty. After that, he wrote no more about them, and devoted

[023-047] his leisure chiefly to their house. He was very often in their house, in

[023-048] his flittings and visitings about the [[coketown|Coketown]] district; and was much

[023-049] encouraged by Mr. Bounderby. It was quite in Mr. Bounderby’s gusty way

[023-050] to boast to all his world that _he_ didn’t care about your highly

[023-051] connected people, but that if his wife [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]]’s daughter did, she

[023-052] was welcome to their company.

[023-053] Mr. James Harthouse began to think it would be a new sensation, if the

[023-054] face which changed so beautifully for the whelp, would change for him.

[023-055] He was quick enough to observe; he had a good memory, and did not forget

[023-056] a word of the brother’s revelations. He interwove them with everything

[023-057] he saw of the sister, and he began to understand her. To be sure, the

[023-058] better and profounder part of her character was not within his scope of

[023-059] perception; for in natures, as in seas, depth answers unto depth; but he

[023-060] soon began to read the rest with a student’s eye.

[023-061] Mr. Bounderby had taken possession of a house and grounds, about fifteen

[023-062] miles from the town, and accessible within a mile or two, by a railway

[023-063] striding on many arches over a wild country, undermined by deserted

[023-064] coal-shafts, and spotted at night by fires and black shapes of stationary

[023-065] engines at pits’ mouths. This country, gradually softening towards the

[023-066] neighbourhood of Mr. Bounderby’s retreat, there mellowed into a rustic

[023-067] landscape, golden with heath, and snowy with hawthorn in the spring of

[023-068] the year, and tremulous with leaves and their shadows all the summer

[023-069] time. The bank had foreclosed a mortgage effected on the property thus

[023-070] pleasantly situated, by one of the Coketown magnates, who, in his

[023-071] determination to make a shorter cut than usual to an enormous fortune,

[023-072] overspeculated himself by about two hundred thousand pounds. These

[023-073] accidents did sometimes happen in the best regulated [[family|families]] of

[023-074] Coketown, but the bankrupts had no connexion whatever with the

[023-075] improvident [[class|classes]].

[023-076] It afforded Mr. Bounderby supreme satisfaction to instal himself in this

[023-077] snug little estate, and with demonstrative [[humility]] to grow cabbages in

[023-078] the flower-garden. He delighted to live, barrack-fashion, among the

[023-079] elegant furniture, and he bullied the very pictures with his origin.

[023-080] ‘Why, sir,’ he would say to a visitor, ‘I am told that Nickits,’ the late

[023-081] owner, ‘gave seven hundred pound for that Seabeach. Now, to be plain

[023-082] with you, if I ever, in the whole course of my life, take seven looks at

[023-083] it, at a hundred pound a look, it will be as much as I shall do. No, by

[023-084] George! I don’t forget that I am Josiah Bounderby of Coketown. For

[023-085] years upon years, the only pictures in my possession, or that I could

[023-086] have got into my possession, by any means, unless I stole ’em, were the

[023-087] engravings of a man shaving himself in a boot, on the blacking bottles

[023-088] that I was overjoyed to use in cleaning boots with, and that I sold when

[023-089] they were empty for a farthing a-piece, and glad to get it!’

[023-090] Then he would address Mr. Harthouse in the same style.

[023-091] ‘Harthouse, you have a couple of horses down here. Bring half a dozen

[023-092] more if you like, and we’ll find room for ’em. There’s stabling in this

[023-093] place for a dozen horses; and unless Nickits is belied, he kept the full

[023-094] number. A round dozen of ’em, sir. When that man was a boy, he went to

[023-095] Westminster School. Went to Westminster School as a King’s Scholar, when

[023-096] I was principally living on garbage, and sleeping in market baskets.

[023-097] Why, if I wanted to keep a dozen horses—which I don’t, for one’s enough

[023-098] for me—I couldn’t bear to see ’em in their stalls here, and think what my

[023-099] own lodging used to be. I couldn’t look at ’em, sir, and not order ’em

[023-100] out. Yet so things come round. You see this place; you know what sort

[023-101] of a place it is; you are aware that there’s not a completer place of its

[023-102] size in this kingdom or elsewhere—I don’t care where—and here, got into

[023-103] the middle of it, like a maggot into a nut, is Josiah Bounderby. While

[023-104] Nickits (as a man came into my office, and told me yesterday), Nickits,

[023-105] who used to act in Latin, in the Westminster School plays, with the

[023-106] chief-justices and nobility of this country applauding him till they were

[023-107] black in the face, is drivelling at this minute—drivelling, sir!—in a

[023-108] fifth floor, up a narrow dark back street in Antwerp.’

[023-109] It was among the leafy shadows of this retirement, in the long sultry

[023-110] summer days, that Mr. Harthouse began to prove the face which had set him

[023-111] wondering when he first saw it, and to try if it would change for him.

[023-112] ‘Mrs. Bounderby, I esteem it a most fortunate accident that I find you

[023-113] alone here. I have for some time had a particular wish to speak to you.’

[023-114] It was not by any wonderful accident that he found her, the time of day

[023-115] being that at which she was always alone, and the place being her

[023-116] favourite resort. It was an opening in a dark wood, where some felled

[023-117] trees lay, and where she would sit watching the fallen leaves of last

[023-118] year, as she had watched the falling ashes at home.

[023-119] He sat down beside her, with a glance at her face.

[023-120] ‘Your brother. My young friend Tom—’

[023-121] Her colour brightened, and she turned to him with a look of interest. ‘I

[023-122] never in my life,’ he thought, ‘saw anything so remarkable and so

[023-123] captivating as the lighting of those features!’ His face betrayed his

[023-124] thoughts—perhaps without betraying him, for it might have been according

[023-125] to its instructions so to do.

[023-126] ‘Pardon me. The expression of your sisterly interest is so beautiful—Tom

[023-127] should be so [[pride|proud]] of it—I know this is inexcusable, but I am so

[023-128] compelled to admire.’

[023-129] ‘Being so impulsive,’ she said composedly.

[023-130] ‘Mrs. Bounderby, no: you know I make no pretence with you. You know I am

[023-131] a sordid piece of human nature, ready to sell myself at any time for any

[023-132] reasonable sum, and altogether incapable of any Arcadian proceeding

[023-133] whatever.’

[023-134] ‘I am waiting,’ she returned, ‘for your further reference to my brother.’

[023-135] ‘You are rigid with me, and I deserve it. I am as worthless a dog as you

[023-136] will find, except that I am not false—not false. But you surprised and

[023-137] started me from my subject, which was your brother. I have an interest

[023-138] in him.’

[023-139] ‘Have you an interest in anything, Mr. Harthouse?’ she asked, half

[023-140] incredulously and half gratefully.

[023-142] must say now—even at the hazard of appearing to make a pretence, and of

[023-161] ‘Allow me to be frank. Do you think he games at all?’

[023-165] ‘Yes.’
