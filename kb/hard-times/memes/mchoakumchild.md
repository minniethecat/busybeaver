# Mr. M'Choakumchild

The schoolmaster at Gradgrind's model school, whose name provides *Hard Times* with its most brutally humorous pun — "choak 'em, child" — and whose professional training embodies Dickens's critique of the new Utilitarian educational system that produces identically-manufactured teachers who know everything and understand nothing.

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mr. M'Choakumchild (first name never given)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "the schoolmaster," "good M'Choakumchild" (Dickens's ironic address)
- **Role**: Minor character — the schoolmaster, executive arm of Gradgrind's educational philosophy
- **Occupation**: Schoolmaster at Gradgrind's model school in Coketown; government-certified teacher
- **Family**: Mrs. M'Choakumchild (mentioned only once by Sissy, who notes that neither Mr. nor Mrs. M'Choakumchild ever makes mistakes — PN: 001-009-061)
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 2 (PN: 001-002-163) — called upon by Gradgrind to deliver his first lesson before the government inspector
- **Last Appearance**: Book I Chapter 9 (PN: 001-009-097) — last referenced in the narrative through Sissy's recollection of his classroom statistical exercises

## Characterisation

Dickens introduces M'Choakumchild through the novel's most concentrated satire of the professionalised education system. The schoolmaster is presented as an industrial product: "He and some one hundred and forty other schoolmasters, had been lately turned at the same time, in the same factory, on the same principles, like so many pianoforte legs" (PN: 001-002-168–170). The factory metaphor — teachers as identical manufactured components, turned out on a production line — is one of the novel's sharpest critiques of the new teacher-training colleges. M'Choakumchild is not a person so much as a standardised process.

His training is itemised with devastating precision. He has mastered orthography, etymology, syntax, prosody, biography, astronomy, geography, cosmography, the sciences of compound proportion, algebra, land-surveying and levelling, vocal music, drawing from models — the catalogue runs on through "all the Water Sheds of all the world" and "all the histories of all the peoples" (PN: 001-002-172–183) until it becomes indistinguishable from parody. The litany exhausts itself in its own excess, and then Dickens delivers the judgement: "Ah, rather overdone, M'Choakumchild. If he had only learnt a little less, how infinitely better he might have taught much more!" (PN: 001-002-184–185). This sentence is the novel's thesis on education in miniature: the accumulation of isolated facts, however vast, is not merely insufficient but actively counterproductive. The more M'Choakumchild knows, the worse a teacher he becomes, because his knowledge has no organising principle beyond its own display.

### Physical Description

Dickens gives M'Choakumchild almost no physical description — a choice that is itself significant. The schoolmaster is defined entirely by his training, his certification, and his function. He appears as a list of qualifications rather than a body. The one physical detail is telling in its chill: the "ologies" that comprise his learning are "all at the ends of his ten chilled fingers" (PN: 001-002-175–176). The word "chilled" suggests not merely catalogue knowledge but knowledge that has been drained of warmth, of life, of human connection — knowledge that can be pointed to but cannot be felt.

### Speech and Mannerisms

M'Choakumchild is virtually never given direct dialogue. He "began in his best manner" (PN: 001-002-168), and Dickens describes only the content of his teaching, not his voice. This voicelessness is a deliberate authorial strategy: the schoolmaster, like the pianoforte legs he resembles, is an interchangeable part. His speech, when reported indirectly through Sissy, is all statistics and definitions — "he would try me once more" (PN: 001-009-092) — the relentless persistence of a system that has no capacity to recognise when it is failing.

## Role in the Narrative

M'Choakumchild's narrative function is concentrated in Book I. He is introduced in Chapter 2 as the instrument through which the Fact-system will be applied to the "little pitchers" assembled in the schoolroom. The famous catalogue of his accomplishments (PN: 001-002-168–191) establishes the novel's critique of teacher-training as dehumanising standardisation. Dickens explicitly compares him to Morgiana in the Forty Thieves — pouring boiling oil into jars to kill the robbers within — and asks whether his "boiling store" of facts will "kill outright the robber Fancy lurking within — or sometimes only maim him and distort him" (PN: 001-002-186–191). The question is the novel's central educational concern, and it is posed directly to M'Choakumchild.

In Chapter 9, "Sissy's Progress," M'Choakumchild reappears as the relentless antagonist of Sissy Jupe's imagination. His report that Sissy "had a very dense head for figures" (PN: 001-009-018) epitomises a system that can only measure deficiency, never recognise capacity. Sissy's recollections of his classroom — the statistics of drowning, the natural history questions she cannot answer — document the daily operation of an educational machine that confuses memorisation with understanding. M'Choakumchild's last appearance in the text comes through Sissy's voice: "M'Choakumchild said that in a given time a hundred thousand persons went to sea on long voyages, and only five hundred of them were drowned" (PN: 001-009-097). The statistician who reduces human loss to a percentage is the perfected product of the system Gradgrind has built.

## Thematic Significance

M'Choakumchild represents the professionalisation of the Fact-system — a crucial development in Dickens's critique. Where Gradgrind is the self-made apostle of Utilitarian philosophy, M'Choakumchild is its state-certified technician. He has been produced by the newly-minted teacher-training colleges that Dickens viewed with deep suspicion, institutions that turned out "churned and ruffled" schoolmasters (PN: 001-002-168–171) as though education were a branch of industrial manufacture.

The name "M'Choakumchild" is Dickens's most explicit pun in the novel, and it contains a whole argument. It names exactly what the system does: it chokes the child. Fancy, imagination, wonder — the "robber" lurking in every vessel — is to be killed by the boiling oil of Facts. The genitive "M'" (a contraction of "Mac," son of) is an Irish and Scottish form, perhaps suggesting the marginal or provincial origins of the new class of government teachers, but the function takes precedence over any personal identity.

The "pianoforte legs" metaphor is equally devastating. Teachers are not trained but manufactured; they are not individuals but identical standardised parts; they are not even whole pianos but mere legs — components of an instrument they do not themselves possess. The piano is an object of art and beauty, but its legs are purely functional. M'Choakumchild has been turned on a lathe, not educated.

He is not evil. This is essential to Dickens's point. M'Choakumchild is a conscientious professional who has done everything the system asked of him — mastered all the ologies, passed all the examinations, achieved certification under "Her Majesty's most Honourable Privy Council's Schedule B" (PN: 001-002-176–177). The tragedy is that his completeness as a product makes him perfectly destructive as a teacher. "If he had only learnt a little less, how infinitely better he might have taught much more" — the line is not an attack on the man but on the system that created him, a system that confuses the accumulation of information with the cultivation of minds.

## Key Relationships

- **[[Thomas-Gradgrind]]**: M'Choakumchild is Gradgrind's employee and the executive instrument of his educational philosophy. Gradgrind summons him to deliver the first lesson (PN: 001-002-166), and their relationship models the division of labour in the Utilitarian education system: Gradgrind provides the doctrine, M'Choakumchild provides the delivery.
- **[[Sissy-Jupe]]**: Sissy is M'Choakumchild's designated failure — the student who cannot be processed by his statistical machinery. Her "very dense head for figures" (PN: 001-009-018) is the schoolmaster's diagnostic verdict, but the novel reverses the judgement: it is not Sissy who is deficient but the system that cannot recognise her intelligence. Her classroom recollections (PN: 001-009-058–097) preserve M'Choakumchild's pedagogical voice in memory, and that voice — statistics, definitions, retests — indicts itself.
