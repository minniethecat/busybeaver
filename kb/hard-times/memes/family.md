# Family

In *Hard Times*, "family" is the institution that the novel simultaneously mourns and reimagines. The Gradgrind family — the novel's central case study — is a family in name only: a household organised not around affection but around the transmission of Facts, where father and children are bound by doctrine rather than love. Dickens traces the consequences of this hollow domesticity through Louisa's emotional paralysis and Tom's moral collapse. Against this failed model, the novel quietly assembles a counter-tradition of surrogate families — Sleary's circus, the bond between Stephen Blackpool and Rachael, Sissy Jupe's healing presence — that suggest what family might be if it were built on sympathy rather than system.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens structures his critique of family through contrast. The Gradgrind household at Stone Lodge is presented as the domestic extension of the schoolroom: "the education of the reason of my family" (PN: 004-139) is Gradgrind's proud formulation, and the novel treats it as an indictment. The substitution of Facts for affection — the belief that a family can be administered like a school syllabus — is the original error from which all subsequent damage flows. Louisa, raised without imaginative nurture, cannot recognise her own feelings; Tom, raised without moral guidance, becomes a thief and a fugitive. The family built on reason produces unreason.

Dickens sharpens this critique through Bounderby, who boasts that he has no family at all. "Had a family at all. I have a great mind to..." (PN: 004-106) — the incomplete sentence betrays the boast's fragility. Bounderby's claim to be self-made, to have risen without family ties, is the novel's central lie, exposed when his mother Mrs Pegler — "herself in the family-way" (PN: 006-289) — is revealed to have sacrificed everything for his advancement. The man who denies family is sustained by the very family he denies.

The novel also registers the structural damage industrialisation inflicts on family life. Coketown itself is figured as "an unnatural family, shouldering, and trampling" (PN: 010-010) — an image that collapses the distinction between domestic and urban space, suggesting a city organised on anti-family principles. The working-class families of Coketown appear only in fragments — Stephen's blighted marriage, Rachael's solitude — making their bond all the more significant as a counter-image of fidelity without institutional sanction.

### Key Passages

> "crossed to the spot where his family" (PN: 003-106)

This early, apparently neutral description of Gradgrind's movements already encodes the novel's argument: Gradgrind "crosses to" his family as one crosses to a scheduled appointment. Family is a location, not a relationship.

> "There's no family pride about me, there's no im..." (PN: 006-154)

Bounderby's self-exculpation — his insistence that he has no "family pride" — is the novel's signal that his entire persona is constructed on a denial of family. The broken-off "im—" suggests the sentence cannot finish because the claim is unsustainable.

> "presence of his family, and said in a faltering way" (PN: 009-230)

Gradgrind's faltering — his inability to speak firmly in the presence of his family — marks the moment when the Fact-system begins to crack. The man who has ruled his household by maxims now finds himself unable to address it. This is the beginning of Gradgrind's re-education, and it occurs precisely at the site of his deepest failure: the family he built.

> "a'am! I don't want a wife and family. Why should they?'" (PN: 017-259)

Bounderby's rhetorical question is the novel's answer. Why should anyone want a wife and family? Because without them — without the network of attachment and obligation that family represents — the self is impoverished, deluded, and ultimately exposed as fraudulent.

## Historical Context

Victorian domestic ideology elevated the family to a quasi-sacred institution: the home as haven, the wife as "angel in the house" (Coventry Patmore's poem of that title appeared in 1854, the same year as *Hard Times*). This idealisation coexisted with systematic assaults on working-class family life. The **1834 Poor Law Amendment Act** had separated husbands from wives and parents from children in the workhouse system — a bureaucratic fragmentation of the family that Dickens had already attacked in *Oliver Twist* (1838). Industrial urbanisation further eroded traditional family structures by severing the household from the workplace: the father who laboured in the factory no longer laboured at home.

The cult of the **self-made man** — Samuel Smiles's *Self-Help* would appear in 1859, but the ideology was already ascendant in 1854 — provided a complementary assault on family by valorising the individual who supposedly owed nothing to kin or inheritance. Bounderby is Dickens's satire of this figure: a man whose boast of self-making is exposed as a lie sustained by maternal sacrifice.

Dickens's own life informs the novel's treatment of family. His childhood experience of the blacking warehouse — his father in debtors' prison, his family fragmented — made the question of what holds a family together existentially urgent for him. The novel's argument — that family must be held together by love, not by doctrine or economic calculation — is rooted in that experience.

## Embodied By

- **[[Gradgrind family]]**: The novel's central case study in family failure. Built on Facts instead of affection, the household produces Louisa's emotional numbness and Tom's moral vacancy. Gradgrind's late recognition of what he has done — "I have proved my — my system to myself" — is a father's confession that his family was an experiment, not a home.
- **[[Slearys-Circus]]**: The novel's structural counter-family. The circus is a collective that operates by loyalty rather than contract, kindness rather than calculation. It shelters Sissy Jupe; it rescues Tom Gradgrind at the end. It is the only institution in the novel that reliably produces familial care, and it is significant that this care is extended to strangers — the circus redefines family as elective kinship rather than blood obligation.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]] and [[Rachael]]**: The working-class domestic ideal the novel cannot quite allow itself to name as marriage. Trapped in a blighted legal union with a drunken wife, Stephen finds in Rachael the companionship and moral support that the law denies him. Their bond is the novel's argument that family is not a legal form but a human fact — and that industrial England's laws and institutions systematically prevent that fact from realising itself.

## Contrasted With

- **[[facts]]**: The novel's organising binary. Facts are what Gradgrind substitutes for love in his family; facts are the curriculum that replaces affection. The novel argues that a family built on facts is not a family at all — it is a schoolroom in which the children dwindle rather than grow. (PN: 004-139)
- **[[individual]]**: Bounderby's ideal of the self-made man — the individual who owes nothing to family, who has "no family pride" — is exposed as both fraudulent and impoverishing. The novel treats the denial of family as a form of self-mutilation: what Bounderby loses is not just honesty but the capacity for relationship itself. (PN: 004-106, 006-154)

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *The English Novel from Dickens to Lawrence* (1970), argued that *Hard Times* registers the destruction of the "knowable community" — the network of face-to-face relationships that characterised pre-industrial life — and that the novel's surrogate families (the circus, Stephen and Rachael) represent a nostalgic reaching-back toward forms of community that industrialisation had already rendered impossible.

> Catherine Waters, in *Dickens and the Politics of the Family* (1997), reads the novel's family structures as sites of ideological contest: the Gradgrind household embodies the disciplinary family of the mid-Victorian bourgeoisie; the circus offers an alternative model of the family as performance — contingent, mobile, and defined by practice rather than blood. Waters argues that Sissy Jupe, who moves between these two family systems, is the novel's agent of domestic reformation.
