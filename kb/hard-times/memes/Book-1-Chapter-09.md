[009-001] SISSY’S PROGRESS

[009-002] SISSY JUPE had not an easy time of it, between Mr. M’Choakumchild and

[009-003] [[mrs-gradgrind|Mrs. Gradgrind]], and was not without strong impulses, in the first months

[009-004] of her probation, to run away. It hailed [[facts]] all day long so very

[009-005] hard, and life in general was opened to her as such a closely ruled

[009-006] ciphering-book, that assuredly she would have run away, but for only one

[009-007] restraint.

[009-008] It is lamentable to think of; but this restraint was the result of no

[009-009] arithmetical process, was self-imposed in defiance of all calculation,

[009-010] and went dead against any table of probabilities that any Actuary would

[009-011] have drawn up from the premises. The girl believed that her father had

[009-012] not deserted her; she lived in the hope that he would come back, and in

[009-013] the faith that he would be made the happier by her remaining where she

[009-014] was.

[009-015] The wretched ignorance with which [[sissy-jupe|Jupe]] clung to this consolation,

[009-016] rejecting the superior comfort of knowing, on a sound arithmetical basis,

[009-017] that her father was an [[nature|unnatural]] vagabond, filled [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] with

[009-018] pity. Yet, what was to be done? M’Choakumchild reported that she had a

[009-019] very dense [[reason|head]] for figures; that, once possessed with a general idea of

[009-020] the globe, she took the smallest conceivable interest in its exact

[009-021] measurements; that she was extremely slow in the acquisition of dates,

[009-022] unless some pitiful incident happened to be connected therewith; that she

[009-023] would burst into tears on being required (by the mental process)

[009-024] immediately to name the cost of two hundred and forty-seven muslin caps

[009-025] at fourteen-pence halfpenny; that she was as low down, in the school, as

[009-026] low could be; that after eight weeks of induction into the elements of

[009-027] [[political-economy|Political Economy]], she had only yesterday been set right by a prattler

[009-028] three feet high, for returning to the question, ‘What is the first

[009-029] principle of this science?’ the absurd answer, ‘To do unto others as I

[009-030] would that they should do unto me.’

[009-031] Mr. Gradgrind observed, shaking his head, that all this was very bad;

[009-032] that it showed the necessity of infinite grinding at the mill of

[009-033] knowledge, as per system, schedule, blue book, report, and tabular

[009-034] statements A to Z; and that Jupe ‘must be kept to it.’ So Jupe was kept

[009-035] to it, and became low-spirited, but no wiser.

[009-036] ‘It would be a fine thing to be you, Miss [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]]!’ she said, one night,

[009-037] when Louisa had endeavoured to make her perplexities for next day

[009-038] something clearer to her.

[009-039] ‘Do you think so?’

[009-040] ‘I should know so much, Miss Louisa. All that is difficult to me now,

[009-041] would be so easy then.’

[009-042] ‘You might not be the better for it, Sissy.’

[009-043] Sissy submitted, after a little hesitation, ‘I should not be the worse,

[009-044] Miss Louisa.’ To which Miss Louisa answered, ‘I don’t know that.’

[009-045] There had been so little communication between these two—both because

[009-046] life at [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]] went monotonously round like a piece of machinery

[009-047] which discouraged human interference, and because of the prohibition

[009-048] relative to Sissy’s past career—that they were still almost strangers.

[009-049] Sissy, with her dark eyes wonderingly directed to Louisa’s face, was

[009-050] uncertain whether to say more or to remain silent.

[009-051] ‘You are more useful to my mother, and more pleasant with her than I can

[009-052] ever be,’ Louisa resumed. ‘You are pleasanter to yourself, than _I_ am

[009-053] to _my_self.’

[009-054] ‘But, if you please, Miss Louisa,’ Sissy pleaded, ‘I am—O so stupid!’

[009-055] Louisa, with a brighter laugh than usual, told her she would be wiser

[009-056] by-and-by.

[009-057] ‘You don’t know,’ said Sissy, half crying, ‘what a stupid girl I am. All

[009-058] through school hours I make mistakes. Mr. and Mrs. M’Choakumchild call

[009-059] me up, over and over again, regularly to make mistakes. I can’t help

[009-060] them. They seem to come natural to me.’

[009-061] ‘Mr. and Mrs. M’Choakumchild never make any mistakes themselves, I

[009-062] suppose, Sissy?’

[009-063] ‘O no!’ she eagerly returned. ‘They know everything.’

[009-064] ‘Tell me some of your mistakes.’

[009-065] ‘I am almost [[shame|ashamed]],’ said Sissy, with reluctance. ‘But to-day, for

[009-066] instance, Mr. M’Choakumchild was explaining to us about Natural

[009-067] Prosperity.’

[009-068] ‘National, I think it must have been,’ observed Louisa.

[009-069] ‘Yes, it was.—But isn’t it the same?’ she timidly asked.

[009-070] ‘You had better say, National, as he said so,’ returned Louisa, with her

[009-071] dry reserve.

[009-072] ‘National Prosperity. And he said, Now, this schoolroom is a Nation.

[009-073] And in this nation, there are fifty millions of money. Isn’t this a

[009-074] prosperous nation? Girl number twenty, isn’t this a prosperous nation,

[009-075] and a’n’t you in a thriving state?’

[009-076] ‘What did you say?’ asked Louisa.

[009-077] ‘Miss Louisa, I said I didn’t know. I thought I couldn’t know whether it

[009-078] was a prosperous nation or not, and whether I was in a thriving state or

[009-079] not, unless I knew who had got the money, and whether any of it was mine.

[009-080] But that had nothing to do with it. It was not in the figures at all,’

[009-081] said Sissy, wiping her eyes.

[009-082] ‘That was a great mistake of yours,’ observed Louisa.

[009-083] ‘Yes, Miss Louisa, I know it was, now. Then Mr. M’Choakumchild said he

[009-084] would try me again. And he said, This schoolroom is an immense town, and

[009-085] in it there are a million of inhabitants, and only five-and-twenty are

[009-086] starved to death in the streets, in the course of a year. What is your

[009-087] remark on that proportion? And my remark was—for I couldn’t think of a

[009-088] better one—that I thought it must be just as hard upon those who were

[009-089] starved, whether the others were a million, or a million million. And

[009-090] that was wrong, too.’

[009-091] ‘Of course it was.’

[009-092] ‘Then Mr. M’Choakumchild said he would try me once more. And he said,

[009-093] Here are the stutterings—’

[009-094] ‘Statistics,’ said Louisa.

[009-095] ‘Yes, Miss Louisa—they always remind me of stutterings, and that’s

[009-096] another of my mistakes—of accidents upon the sea. And I find (Mr.

[009-097] M’Choakumchild said) that in a given time a hundred thousand persons went

[009-098] to sea on long voyages, and only five hundred of them were drowned or

[009-099] burnt to death. What is the percentage? And I said, Miss;’ here Sissy

[009-100] fairly sobbed as confessing with extreme contrition to her greatest

[009-101] error; ‘I said it was nothing.’

[009-102] ‘Nothing, Sissy?’

[009-103] ‘Nothing, Miss—to the relations and friends of the people who were

[009-104] killed. I shall never learn,’ said Sissy. ‘And the worst of all is,

[009-105] that although my poor father wished me so much to learn, and although I

[009-106] am so anxious to learn, because he wished me to, I am afraid I don’t like

[009-107] it.’

[009-108] Louisa stood looking at the pretty modest head, as it drooped abashed

[009-109] before her, until it was raised again to glance at her face. Then she

[009-110] asked:

[009-111] ‘Did your father know so much himself, that he wished you to be well

[009-112] taught too, Sissy?’

[009-113] Sissy hesitated before replying, and so plainly showed her sense that

[009-114] they were entering on forbidden ground, that Louisa added, ‘No one hears

[009-115] us; and if any one did, I am sure no harm could be found in such an

[009-116] innocent question.’

[009-117] ‘No, Miss Louisa,’ answered Sissy, upon this encouragement, shaking her

[009-118] head; ‘father knows very little indeed. It’s as much as he can do to

[009-119] write; and it’s more than people in general can do to read his writing.

[009-120] Though it’s plain to _me_.’

[009-121] ‘Your mother?’

[009-122] ‘Father says she was quite a scholar. She died when I was born. She

[009-123] was;’ Sissy made the terrible communication nervously; ‘she was a

[009-124] dancer.’

[009-125] ‘Did your father [[love]] her?’ Louisa asked these questions with a strong,

[009-126] wild, wandering interest peculiar to her; an interest gone astray like a

[009-127] banished creature, and hiding in solitary places.

[009-128] ‘O yes! As dearly as he loves me. Father loved me, first, for her sake.

[009-129] He carried me about with him when I was quite a baby. We have never been

[009-130] asunder from that time.’

[009-131] ‘Yet he leaves you now, Sissy?’

[009-132] ‘Only for my good. Nobody understands him as I do; nobody knows him as I

[009-133] do. When he left me for my good—he never would have left me for his

[009-134] own—I know he was almost broken-hearted with the trial. He will not be

[009-135] happy for a single minute, till he comes back.’

[009-136] ‘Tell me more about him,’ said Louisa, ‘I will never ask you again.

[009-137] Where did you live?’

[009-138] ‘We travelled about the country, and had no fixed place to live in.

[009-139] Father’s a;’ Sissy whispered the awful word, ‘a clown.’

[009-140] ‘To make the people laugh?’ said Louisa, with a nod of intelligence.

[009-141] ‘Yes. But they wouldn’t laugh sometimes, and then father cried. Lately,

[009-142] they very often wouldn’t laugh, and he used to come home despairing.

[009-143] Father’s not like most. Those who didn’t know him as well as I do, and

[009-144] didn’t love him as dearly as I do, might believe he was not quite right.

[009-145] Sometimes they played tricks upon him; but they never knew how he felt

[009-146] them, and shrunk up, when he was alone with me. He was far, far timider

[009-147] than they thought!’

[009-148] ‘And you were his comfort through everything?’

[009-149] She nodded, with the tears rolling down her face. ‘I hope so, and father

[009-150] said I was. It was because he grew so scared and trembling, and because

[009-151] he felt himself to be a poor, weak, ignorant, helpless man (those used to

[009-152] be his words), that he wanted me so much to know a great deal, and be

[009-153] different from him. I used to read to him to cheer his courage, and he

[009-154] was very fond of that. They were wrong books—I am never to speak of them

[009-155] here—but we didn’t know there was any harm in them.’

[009-156] ‘And he liked them?’ said Louisa, with a searching gaze on Sissy all this

[009-157] time.

[009-158] ‘O very much! They kept him, many times, from what did him real harm.

[009-159] And often and often of a night, he used to forget all his troubles in

[009-160] wondering whether the Sultan would let the lady go on with the story, or

[009-161] would have her head cut off before it was finished.’

[009-162] ‘And your father was always kind? To the last?’ asked Louisa

[009-163] contravening the great principle, and wondering very much.

[009-164] ‘Always, always!’ returned Sissy, clasping her hands. ‘Kinder and kinder

[009-165] than I can tell. He was angry only one night, and that was not to me,

[009-166] but Merrylegs. Merrylegs;’ she whispered the awful fact; ‘is his

[009-167] performing dog.’

[009-168] ‘Why was he angry with the dog?’ Louisa demanded.

[009-169] ‘Father, soon after they came home from performing, told Merrylegs to

[009-170] jump up on the backs of the two chairs and stand across them—which is one

[009-171] of his tricks. He looked at father, and didn’t do it at once.

[009-172] Everything of father’s had gone wrong that night, and he hadn’t pleased

[009-173] the public at all. He cried out that the very dog knew he was failing,

[009-174] and had no compassion on him. Then he beat the dog, and I was

[009-175] frightened, and said, “Father, father! Pray don’t hurt the creature who

[009-176] is so fond of you! O Heaven forgive you, father, stop!” And he stopped,

[009-177] and the dog was bloody, and father lay down crying on the floor with the

[009-178] dog in his arms, and the dog licked his face.’

[009-179] Louisa saw that she was sobbing; and going to her, kissed her, took her

[009-180] hand, and sat down beside her.

[009-181] ‘Finish by telling me how your father left you, Sissy. Now that I have

[009-182] asked you so much, tell me the end. The blame, if there is any blame, is

[009-183] mine, not yours.’

[009-184] ‘Dear Miss Louisa,’ said Sissy, covering her eyes, and sobbing yet; ‘I

[009-185] came home from the school that afternoon, and found poor father just come

[009-186] home too, from the booth. And he sat rocking himself over the fire, as

[009-187] if he was in pain. And I said, “Have you hurt yourself, father?” (as he

[009-188] did sometimes, like they all did), and he said, “A little, my darling.”

[009-189] And when I came to stoop down and look up at his face, I saw that he was

[009-190] crying. The more I spoke to him, the more he hid his face; and at first

[009-191] he shook all over, and said nothing but “My darling;” and “My love!”’

[009-192] Here [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]] came lounging in, and stared at the two with a coolness not

[009-193] particularly savouring of interest in anything but himself, and not much

[009-194] of that at present.

[009-195] ‘I am asking Sissy a few questions, Tom,’ observed his sister. ‘You have

[009-196] no occasion to go away; but don’t interrupt us for a moment, Tom dear.’

[009-197] ‘Oh! very well!’ returned Tom. ‘Only father has brought old [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]]

[009-198] home, and I want you to come into the drawing-room. Because if you come,

[009-199] there’s a good chance of old Bounderby’s asking me to dinner; and if you

[009-200] don’t, there’s none.’

[009-201] ‘I’ll come directly.’

[009-202] ‘I’ll wait for you,’ said Tom, ‘to make sure.’

[009-203] Sissy resumed in a lower voice. ‘At last poor father said that he had

[009-204] given no satisfaction again, and never did give any satisfaction now, and

[009-205] that he was a shame and disgrace, and I should have done better without

[009-206] him all along. I said all the affectionate things to him that came into

[009-207] my [[heart]], and presently he was quiet and I sat down by him, and told him

[009-208] all about the school and everything that had been said and done there.

[009-209] When I had no more left to tell, he put his arms round my neck, and

[009-210] kissed me a great many times. Then he asked me to fetch some of the

[009-211] stuff he used, for the little hurt he had had, and to get it at the best

[009-212] place, which was at the other end of town from there; and then, after

[009-213] kissing me again, he let me go. When I had gone down-stairs, I turned

[009-214] back that I might be a little bit more company to him yet, and looked in

[009-215] at the door, and said, “Father dear, shall I take Merrylegs?” Father

[009-216] shook his head and said, “No, Sissy, no; take nothing that’s known to be

[009-217] mine, my darling;” and I left him sitting by the fire. Then the thought

[009-218] must have come upon him, poor, poor father! of going away to try

[009-219] something for my sake; for when I came back, he was gone.’

[009-220] ‘I say! Look sharp for old Bounderby, Loo!’ Tom remonstrated.

[009-221] ‘There’s no more to tell, Miss Louisa. I keep the nine oils ready for

[009-222] him, and I know he will come back. Every letter that I see in Mr.

[009-223] Gradgrind’s hand takes my breath away and blinds my eyes, for I think it

[009-224] comes from father, or from [[sleary|Mr. Sleary]] about father. Mr. Sleary promised

[009-225] to write as soon as ever father should be heard of, and I trust to him to

[009-226] keep his word.’

[009-227] ‘Do look sharp for old Bounderby, Loo!’ said Tom, with an impatient

[009-228] whistle. ‘He’ll be off if you don’t look sharp!’

[009-229] After this, whenever Sissy dropped a curtsey to Mr. Gradgrind in the

[009-230] presence of his [[family]], and said in a faltering way, ‘I beg your pardon,

[009-231] sir, for being troublesome—but—have you had any letter yet about me?’

[009-232] Louisa would suspend the occupation of the moment, whatever it was, and

[009-233] look for the reply as earnestly as Sissy did. And when Mr. Gradgrind

[009-234] regularly answered, ‘No, Jupe, nothing of the sort,’ the trembling of

[009-235] Sissy’s lip would be repeated in Louisa’s face, and her eyes would follow

[009-236] Sissy with compassion to the door. Mr. Gradgrind usually improved these

[009-237] occasions by remarking, when she was gone, that if Jupe had been properly

[009-238] trained from an early age she would have remonstrated to herself on sound

[009-239] principles the baselessness of these fantastic hopes. Yet it did seem

[009-240] (though not to him, for he saw nothing of it) as if fantastic hope could

[009-241] take as strong a hold as Fact.

[009-242] This observation must be limited exclusively to his daughter. As to Tom,

[009-243] he was becoming that not unprecedented triumph of calculation which is

[009-244] usually at work on number one. As to Mrs. Gradgrind, if she said

[009-245] anything on the subject, she would come a little way out of her wrappers,

[009-246] like a feminine dormouse, and say:

[009-247] ‘Good gracious bless me, how my poor head is vexed and worried by that

[009-248] girl Jupe’s so perseveringly asking, over and over again, about her

[009-249] tiresome letters! Upon my word and honour I seem to be fated, and

[009-250] destined, and ordained, to live in the midst of things that I am never to

[009-251] hear the last of. It really is a most extraordinary circumstance that it

[009-252] appears as if I never was to hear the last of anything!’

[009-253] At about this point, Mr. Gradgrind’s eye would fall upon her; and under

[009-254] the influence of that wintry piece of fact, she would become torpid

[009-255] again.
