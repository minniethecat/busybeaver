# Class

In *Hard Times*, "class" is not an abstract sociological category but the lived experience of separation — the invisible architecture that governs whom one marries, whom one trusts, and where one belongs. Dickens presents Coketown as a society fractured along class lines so deep that the space between master and worker, rich and poor, becomes a kind of no-man's-land in which an honest man has no place to stand.

## Definition

Class in *Hard Times* operates on two distinct but interlocking planes. On the surface, it is a matter of economic position: the mill-owners and bankers versus the factory "hands." But Dickens's deeper interest is in class as a structure of feeling — the habits of deference, resentment, self-justification, and mutual incomprehension that make economic inequality into a moral and psychological prison. The novel treats class not as a natural hierarchy but as a constructed reality maintained by language (Bounderby's self-mythologising birth-in-a-ditch narrative), by institutions (Gradgrind's school, the bank, the marriage market), and by force of habit on both sides.

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens's treatment of class is unusual for its moment. He refuses both the comfortable paternalism that would sentimentalise the poor and the laissez-faire logic that would treat them as mere economic units. The novel's structural innovation is to place Stephen Blackpool — a factory hand — at the moral centre of the narrative, and to frame his suffering not as individual misfortune but as a structural predicament. Stephen's famous question to Bounderby — "Are the two so deeply separated in this town, that there is no place whatever for an honest workman between them?" (PN: 022-192) — names the novel's central class analysis. Between the organised power of capital and the organised power of labour, there is no space for the individual conscience.

Dickens balances this structural critique with a surprising generosity toward groups that class discourse typically dismisses. Sleary's circus folk are described as possessing "the every-day virtues of any class of people in the world" (PN: 006-252) — a deliberate levelling gesture that insists on common humanity across the social spectrum even as the novel catalogues the forces that deny it.

### Key Passages

> "He turned from his own class and his own quarter" (PN: 011-034)

This description of Stephen Blackpool captures the paradox of his position. Ostracised by his fellow workers for refusing to join the union, Stephen becomes a man without a class — turned from his own people and never accepted by the masters. His isolation is Dickens's demonstration that class is not merely an economic category but a community, and to lose one's class is to lose one's place in the world.

> "'that the united masters allow of any such class-combinations'" (PN: 017-139)

Mrs. Sparsit's dismissive phrase "class-combinations" — used in conversation with Bitzer about the union's petition — distills the upper-class view of working-class solidarity as something illegitimate and faintly ridiculous. The hyphenated compound itself performs the dismissal: as if workers associating together were a kind of unnatural hybrid, a "combination" rather than a legitimate organisation.

> "perhaps by a proud consciousness that he was faithful to his class under all their mistrust" (PN: 021-082)

Even as Stephen is rejected by the union, he maintains a stubborn loyalty to his working-class identity. The phrase "proud consciousness" is significant: Dickens grants Stephen a class consciousness that is neither the mechanical solidarity of Slackbridge's rhetoric nor the self-interested calculation of the masters, but something closer to personal integrity.

## Historical Context

*Hard Times* was published in 1854, at a moment when the "Condition of England" question dominated public discourse. The Chartist movement of the 1830s and 1840s had brought working-class political demands into the open; the Great Exhibition of 1851 had celebrated industrial progress while making visible the gap between those who produced wealth and those who enjoyed it. Dickens's own career had given him an unusually intimate knowledge of class mobility and its discontents — from the blacking factory of his childhood to the literary celebrity of his adulthood.

The novel engages directly with the language of **Political Economy**, the dominant intellectual framework for understanding class relations in Victorian England. Political Economy treated wages, prices, and labour as governed by impersonal laws, and it regarded trade unions as "combinations" that interfered with the natural operation of the market. Bounderby's insistence that workers want "to be set up in a coach and six, and to be fed on turtle soup and venison, with a gold spoon" is a caricature of this view — and Dickens's way of exposing its bad faith.

## Embodied By

- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: The novel's primary vehicle for class analysis. Stephen is a factory hand who refuses to join the union on grounds of conscience and is consequently ostracised by his own class. His tragedy is that he belongs fully to neither the workers (who reject him) nor the masters (who exploit him). He is class consciousness without class solidarity — a position the novel presents as both morally admirable and practically impossible.
- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: The self-made-man myth made flesh — and exposed as fraud. Bounderby's incessant narration of his own rise from the gutter is revealed in Book III as a fiction designed to legitimate his authority. His class performance is the novel's sharpest critique of the ideology that treats wealth as the natural reward of virtue.
- **[[Slearys-Circus]]**: A community that exists outside the Coketown class structure altogether. The circus folk are neither masters nor hands; they labour, but their labour is performance, not production. They are the novel's imaginative counter-argument to class determinism — proof that solidarity, kindness, and loyalty can flourish in a community that the respectable world dismisses as beneath notice.

## Contrasted With

- **[[marriage]]**: Class governs the marriage market absolutely in *Hard Times*. Louisa's marriage to Bounderby is a class transaction disguised as a rational decision — and its disaster is Dickens's argument that class-based marriage is a form of prostitution. The novel's one functional domestic relationship (Stephen and Rachael's) is precisely the one that class and law forbid from becoming a marriage. (PN: 013-001–020)
- **[[facts]]**: The Gradgrind philosophy that reduces children to vessels for facts is a class philosophy in disguise. Facts are what the ruling class defines as knowledge; imagination, fancy, and feeling — the faculties that might enable cross-class sympathy — are systematically excluded from the curriculum.
- **[[utilitarianism]]**: The ethical language that makes class exploitation seem like rational calculation. Bounderby's treatment of his workers as "Hands" — as instruments of production rather than human beings — is Utilitarianism applied to class relations.

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *Culture and Society* (1958), identified *Hard Times* as a crucial text in the development of the "industrial novel" tradition, arguing that Dickens's strength lies in his ability to render the felt experience of class — the noise, the monotony, the physical ugliness of industrial life — rather than in any programmatic political analysis. Williams's reading highlights Stephen Blackpool as a figure of genuine tragic dignity, caught between forces he cannot control.

> F.R. Leavis, in *The Great Tradition* (1948), controversially elevated *Hard Times* to the status of a "moral fable" rather than a realist novel, arguing that Dickens's class critique operates through symbolic compression — Coketown as the image of industrial civilisation — rather than through documentary fidelity. This reading has been influential but also criticised for undervaluing the novel's specifically Victorian engagement with contemporary class politics.
