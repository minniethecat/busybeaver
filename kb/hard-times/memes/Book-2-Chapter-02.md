[018-001] MR. JAMES HARTHOUSE

[018-002] THE [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] party wanted assistance in cutting the throats of the

[018-003] Graces. They went about recruiting; and where could they enlist recruits

[018-004] more hopefully, than among the fine [[gentleman|gentlemen]] who, having found out

[018-005] everything to be worth nothing, were equally ready for anything?

[018-006] Moreover, the healthy spirits who had mounted to this sublime height were

[018-007] attractive to many of the [[thomas-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] school. They liked fine gentlemen;

[018-008] they pretended that they did not, but they did. They became exhausted in

[018-009] imitation of them; and they yaw-yawed in their speech like them; and they

[018-010] served out, with an enervated air, the little mouldy rations of political

[018-011] economy, on which they regaled their disciples. There never before was

[018-012] seen on earth such a wonderful hybrid race as was thus produced.

[018-013] Among the fine gentlemen not regularly belonging to the Gradgrind school,

[018-014] there was one of a good [[family]] and a better appearance, with a happy turn

[018-015] of humour which had told immensely with the House of Commons on the

[018-016] occasion of his entertaining it with his (and the Board of Directors)

[018-017] view of a railway accident, in which the most careful officers ever

[018-018] known, employed by the most liberal managers ever heard of, assisted by

[018-019] the finest mechanical contrivances ever devised, the whole in action on

[018-020] the best line ever constructed, had killed five people and wounded

[018-021] thirty-two, by a casualty without which the excellence of the whole

[018-022] system would have been positively incomplete. Among the slain was a cow,

[018-023] and among the scattered articles unowned, a widow’s cap. And the

[018-024] honourable member had so tickled the House (which has a delicate sense of

[018-025] humour) by putting the cap on the cow, that it became impatient of any

[018-026] serious reference to the Coroner’s Inquest, and brought the railway off

[018-027] with Cheers and Laughter.

[018-028] Now, this gentleman had a younger brother of still better appearance than

[018-029] himself, who had tried life as a Cornet of Dragoons, and found it a bore;

[018-030] and had afterwards tried it in the train of an English minister abroad,

[018-031] and found it a bore; and had then strolled to Jerusalem, and got bored

[018-032] there; and had then gone yachting about the world, and got bored

[018-033] everywhere. To whom this honourable and jocular, member fraternally said

[018-034] one day, ‘[[james-harthouse|Jem]], there’s a good opening among the hard Fact fellows, and

[018-035] they want men. I wonder you don’t go in for statistics.’ Jem, rather

[018-036] taken by the novelty of the idea, and very hard up for a change, was as

[018-037] ready to ‘go in’ for statistics as for anything else. So, he went in.

[018-038] He coached himself up with a blue-book or two; and his brother put it

[018-039] about among the hard Fact fellows, and said, ‘If you want to bring in,

[018-040] for any place, a handsome dog who can make you a devilish good speech,

[018-041] look after my brother Jem, for he’s your man.’ After a few dashes in the

[018-042] public meeting way, Mr. Gradgrind and a council of political sages

[018-043] approved of Jem, and it was resolved to send him down to [[coketown|Coketown]], to

[018-044] become known there and in the neighbourhood. Hence the letter Jem had

[018-045] last night shown to [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]], which [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] now held in his

[018-046] hand; superscribed, ‘Josiah Bounderby, Esquire, Banker, Coketown.

[018-047] Specially to introduce James Harthouse, Esquire. Thomas Gradgrind.’

[018-048] Within an hour of the receipt of this dispatch and Mr. James Harthouse’s

[018-049] card, Mr. Bounderby put on his hat and went down to the Hotel. There he

[018-050] found Mr. James Harthouse looking out of window, in a state of mind so

[018-051] disconsolate, that he was already half-disposed to ‘go in’ for something

[018-052] else.

[018-053] ‘My name, sir,’ said his visitor, ‘is Josiah Bounderby, of Coketown.’

[018-054] Mr. James Harthouse was very happy indeed (though he scarcely looked so)

[018-055] to have a pleasure he had long expected.

[018-056] ‘Coketown, sir,’ said Bounderby, obstinately taking a chair, ‘is not the

[018-057] kind of place you have been accustomed to. Therefore, if you will allow

[018-058] me—or whether you will or not, for I am a plain man—I’ll tell you

[018-059] something about it before we go any further.’

[018-060] Mr. Harthouse would be charmed.

[018-061] ‘Don’t be too sure of that,’ said Bounderby. ‘I don’t promise it. First

[018-062] of all, you see our smoke. That’s meat and drink to us. It’s the

[018-063] healthiest thing in the world in all respects, and particularly for the

[018-064] lungs. If you are one of those who want us to consume it, I differ from

[018-065] you. We are not going to wear the bottoms of our boilers out any faster

[018-066] than we wear ’em out now, for all the humbugging sentiment in Great

[018-067] Britain and Ireland.’

[018-068] By way of ‘going in’ to the fullest extent, Mr. Harthouse rejoined, ‘Mr.

[018-069] Bounderby, I assure you I am entirely and completely of your way of

[018-070] thinking. On conviction.’

[018-071] ‘I am glad to hear it,’ said Bounderby. ‘Now, you have heard a lot of

[018-072] talk about the work in our mills, no doubt. You have? Very good. I’ll

[018-073] state the [[facts|fact]] of it to you. It’s the pleasantest work there is, and

[018-074] it’s the lightest work there is, and it’s the best-paid work there is.

[018-075] More than that, we couldn’t improve the mills themselves, unless we laid

[018-076] down Turkey carpets on the floors. Which we’re not a-going to do.’

[018-077] ‘Mr. Bounderby, perfectly right.’

[018-078] ‘Lastly,’ said Bounderby, ‘as to our Hands. There’s not a Hand in this

[018-079] town, sir, man, woman, or [[childhood|child]], but has one ultimate object in life.

[018-080] That object is, to be fed on turtle soup and venison with a gold spoon.

[018-081] Now, they’re not a-going—none of ’em—ever to be fed on turtle soup and

[018-082] venison with a gold spoon. And now you know the place.’

[018-083] Mr. Harthouse professed himself in the highest degree instructed and

[018-084] refreshed, by this condensed epitome of the whole Coketown question.

[018-085] ‘Why, you see,’ replied Mr. Bounderby, ‘it suits my disposition to have a

[018-086] full understanding with a man, particularly with a public man, when I

[018-087] make his acquaintance. I have only one thing more to say to you, Mr.

[018-088] Harthouse, before assuring you of the pleasure with which I shall

[018-089] respond, to the utmost of my poor ability, to my friend [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]]’s

[018-090] letter of introduction. You are a man of family. Don’t you deceive

[018-091] yourself by supposing for a moment that I am a man of family. I am a bit

[018-092] of dirty riff-raff, and a genuine scrap of tag, rag, and bobtail.’

[018-093] If anything could have exalted Jem’s interest in Mr. Bounderby, it would

[018-094] have been this very circumstance. Or, so he told him.

[018-095] ‘So now,’ said Bounderby, ‘we may shake hands on equal terms. I say,

[018-096] equal terms, because although I know what I am, and the exact depth of

[018-097] the gutter I have lifted myself out of, better than any man does, I am as

[018-098] [[pride|proud]] as you are. I am just as proud as you are. Having now asserted my

[018-099] independence in a proper manner, I may come to how do you find yourself,

[018-100] and I hope you’re pretty well.’

[018-101] The better, Mr. Harthouse gave him to understand as they shook hands, for

[018-102] the salubrious air of Coketown. Mr. Bounderby received the answer with

[018-103] favour.

[018-104] ‘Perhaps you know,’ said he, ‘or perhaps you don’t know, I married Tom

[018-105] Gradgrind’s daughter. If you have nothing better to do than to walk up

[018-106] town with me, I shall be glad to introduce you to Tom Gradgrind’s

[018-107] daughter.’

[018-108] ‘Mr. Bounderby,’ said Jem, ‘you anticipate my dearest wishes.’

[018-109] They went out without further discourse; and Mr. Bounderby piloted the

[018-110] new acquaintance who so strongly contrasted with him, to the private red

[018-111] brick dwelling, with the black outside shutters, the green inside blinds,

[018-112] and the black street door up the two white steps. In the drawing-room of

[018-113] which mansion, there presently entered to them the most remarkable girl

[018-114] Mr. James Harthouse had ever seen. She was so constrained, and yet so

[018-115] careless; so reserved, and yet so watchful; so cold and proud, and yet so

[018-116] sensitively [[shame|ashamed]] of her husband’s braggart [[humility]]—from which she

[018-117] shrunk as if every example of it were a cut or a blow; that it was quite

[018-118] a new sensation to observe her. In face she was no less remarkable than

[018-119] in manner. Her features were handsome; but their [[nature|natural]] play was so

[018-120] locked up, that it seemed impossible to guess at their genuine

[018-121] expression. Utterly indifferent, perfectly self-reliant, never at a

[018-122] loss, and yet never at her ease, with her figure in company with them

[018-123] there, and her mind apparently quite alone—it was of no use ‘going in’

[018-124] yet awhile to comprehend this girl, for she baffled all penetration.

[018-125] From the mistress of the house, the visitor glanced to the house itself.

[018-126] There was no mute sign of a woman in the room. No graceful little

[018-127] adornment, no fanciful little device, however trivial, anywhere expressed

[018-128] her influence. Cheerless and comfortless, boastfully and doggedly rich,

[018-129] there the room stared at its present occupants, unsoftened and unrelieved

[018-130] by the least trace of any womanly occupation. As Mr. Bounderby stood in

[018-131] the midst of his household gods, so those unrelenting divinities occupied

[018-132] their places around Mr. Bounderby, and they were worthy of one another,

[018-133] and well matched.

[018-134] ‘This, sir,’ said Bounderby, ‘is my wife, [[louisa-gradgrind|Mrs. Bounderby]]: Tom Gradgrind’s

[018-135] eldest daughter. Loo, Mr. James Harthouse. Mr. Harthouse has joined

[018-136] your father’s muster-roll. If he is not Tom Gradgrind’s colleague before

[018-137] long, I believe we shall at least hear of him in connexion with one of

[018-138] our neighbouring towns. You observe, Mr. Harthouse, that my wife is my

[018-139] junior. I don’t know what she saw in me to marry me, but she saw

[018-140] something in me, I suppose, or she wouldn’t have married me. She has

[018-141] lots of expensive knowledge, sir, political and otherwise. If you want

[018-142] to cram for anything, I should be troubled to recommend you to a better

[018-143] adviser than Loo Bounderby.’

[018-144] To a more agreeable adviser, or one from whom he would be more likely to

[018-145] learn, Mr. Harthouse could never be recommended.

[018-146] ‘Come!’ said his host. ‘If you’re in the complimentary line, you’ll get

[018-147] on here, for you’ll meet with no competition. I have never been in the

[018-148] way of learning compliments myself, and I don’t profess to understand the

[018-149] art of paying ’em. In fact, despise ’em. But, your bringing-up was

[018-150] different from mine; mine was a real thing, by George! You’re a

[018-151] gentleman, and I don’t pretend to be one. I am Josiah Bounderby of

[018-152] Coketown, and that’s enough for me. However, though I am not influenced

[018-153] by manners and station, Loo Bounderby may be. She hadn’t my

[018-154] advantages—disadvantages you would call ’em, but I call ’em advantages—so

[018-155] you’ll not waste your power, I dare say.’

[018-156] ‘Mr. Bounderby,’ said Jem, turning with a smile to Louisa, ‘is a noble

[018-157] animal in a comparatively natural state, quite free from the harness in

[018-158] which a conventional hack like myself works.’

[018-159] ‘You respect Mr. Bounderby very much,’ she quietly returned. ‘It is

[018-160] natural that you should.’

[018-161] He was disgracefully thrown out, for a gentleman who had seen so much of

[018-162] the world, and thought, ‘Now, how am I to take this?’

[018-163] ‘You are going to devote yourself, as I gather from what Mr. Bounderby

[018-164] has said, to the service of your country. You have made up your mind,’

[018-165] said Louisa, still standing before him where she had first stopped—in all

[018-166] the singular contrariety of her self-possession, and her being obviously

[018-167] very ill at ease—‘to [[performance|show]] the nation the way out of all its

[018-168] difficulties.’

[018-169] ‘Mrs. Bounderby,’ he returned, laughing, ‘upon my honour, no. I will

[018-170] make no such pretence to you. I have seen a little, here and there, up

[018-171] and down; I have found it all to be very worthless, as everybody has, and

[018-172] as some confess they have, and some do not; and I am going in for your

[018-173] respected father’s opinions—really because I have no choice of opinions,

[018-174] and may as well back them as anything else.’

[018-175] ‘Have you none of your own?’ asked Louisa.

[018-176] ‘I have not so much as the slightest predilection left. I assure you I

[018-177] attach not the least importance to any opinions. The result of the

[018-178] varieties of boredom I have undergone, is a conviction (unless conviction

[018-179] is too industrious a word for the lazy sentiment I entertain on the

[018-180] subject), that any set of ideas will do just as much good as any other

[018-181] set, and just as much harm as any other set. There’s an English family

[018-182] with a charming Italian motto. What will be, will be. It’s the only

[018-183] [[truth]] going!’

[018-184] This vicious assumption of honesty in dishonesty—a vice so dangerous, so

[018-185] deadly, and so common—seemed, he observed, a little to impress her in his

[018-186] favour. He followed up the advantage, by saying in his pleasantest

[018-187] manner: a manner to which she might attach as much or as little meaning

[018-188] as she pleased: ‘The side that can prove anything in a line of units,

[018-189] tens, hundreds, and thousands, Mrs. Bounderby, seems to me to afford the

[018-190] most fun, and to give a man the best chance. I am quite as much attached

[018-191] to it as if I believed it. I am quite ready to go in for it, to the same

[018-192] extent as if I believed it. And what more could I possibly do, if I did

[018-193] believe it!’

[018-194] ‘You are a singular politician,’ said Louisa.

[018-195] ‘Pardon me; I have not even that merit. We are the largest party in the

[018-196] state, I assure you, Mrs. Bounderby, if we all fell out of our adopted

[018-197] ranks and were reviewed together.’

[018-198] Mr. Bounderby, who had been in danger of bursting in silence, interposed

[018-199] here with a project for postponing the family dinner till half-past six,

[018-200] and taking Mr. James Harthouse in the meantime on a round of visits to

[018-201] the voting and interesting notabilities of Coketown and its vicinity.

[018-202] The round of visits was made; and Mr. James Harthouse, with a discreet

[018-203] use of his blue coaching, came off triumphantly, though with a

[018-204] considerable accession of boredom.

[018-205] In the evening, he found the dinner-table laid for four, but they sat

[018-206] down only three. It was an appropriate occasion for Mr. Bounderby to

[018-207] discuss the flavour of the hap’orth of stewed eels he had purchased in

[018-208] the streets at eight years old; and also of the inferior water, specially

[018-209] used for laying the dust, with which he had washed down that repast. He

[018-210] likewise entertained his guest over the soup and fish, with the

[018-211] calculation that he (Bounderby) had eaten in his youth at least three

[018-212] horses under the guise of polonies and saveloys. These recitals, Jem, in

[018-213] a languid manner, received with ‘charming!’ every now and then; and they

[018-214] probably would have decided him to ‘go in’ for Jerusalem again to-morrow

[018-215] morning, had he been less curious respecting Louisa.

[018-216] ‘Is there nothing,’ he thought, glancing at her as she sat at the [[reason|head]] of

[018-217] the table, where her youthful figure, small and slight, but very

[018-218] graceful, looked as pretty as it looked misplaced; ‘is there nothing that

[018-219] will move that face?’

[018-220] Yes! By Jupiter, there was something, and here it was, in an unexpected

[018-221] shape. Tom appeared. She changed as the door opened, and broke into a

[018-222] beaming smile.

[018-223] A beautiful smile. Mr. James Harthouse might not have thought so much of

[018-224] it, but that he had wondered so long at her impassive face. She put out

[018-225] her hand—a pretty little soft hand; and her fingers closed upon her

[018-226] brother’s, as if she would have carried them to her lips.

[018-227] ‘Ay, ay?’ thought the visitor. ‘This whelp is the only creature she

[018-228] cares for. So, so!’

[018-229] The whelp was presented, and took his chair. The appellation was not

[018-230] flattering, but not unmerited.

[018-231] ‘When I was your age, young Tom,’ said Bounderby, ‘I was punctual, or I

[018-232] got no dinner!’

[018-233] ‘When you were my age,’ resumed Tom, ‘you hadn’t a wrong balance to get

[018-234] right, and hadn’t to dress afterwards.’

[018-235] ‘Never mind that now,’ said Bounderby.

[018-236] ‘Well, then,’ grumbled Tom. ‘Don’t begin with me.’

[018-237] ‘Mrs. Bounderby,’ said Harthouse, perfectly hearing this under-strain as

[018-238] it went on; ‘your brother’s face is quite familiar to me. Can I have

[018-239] seen him abroad? Or at some public school, perhaps?’

[018-240] ‘No,’ she resumed, quite interested, ‘he has never been abroad yet, and

[018-241] was educated here, at home. Tom, [[love]], I am telling Mr. Harthouse that

[018-242] he never saw you abroad.’

[018-243] ‘No such luck, sir,’ said Tom.

[018-244] There was little enough in him to brighten her face, for he was a sullen

[018-245] young fellow, and ungracious in his manner even to her. So much the

[018-246] greater must have been the solitude of her [[heart]], and her need of some

[018-247] one on whom to bestow it. ‘So much the more is this whelp the only

[018-248] creature she has ever cared for,’ thought Mr. James Harthouse, turning it

[018-249] over and over. ‘So much the more. So much the more.’

[018-250] Both in his sister’s presence, and after she had left the room, the whelp

[018-251] took no pains to hide his contempt for Mr. Bounderby, whenever he could

[018-252] indulge it without the observation of that independent man, by making wry

[018-253] faces, or shutting one eye. Without responding to these telegraphic

[018-254] communications, Mr. Harthouse encouraged him much in the course of the

[018-255] evening, and showed an unusual liking for him. At last, when he rose to

[018-256] return to his hotel, and was a little doubtful whether he knew the way by

[018-257] night, the whelp immediately proffered his services as guide, and turned

[018-258] out with him to escort him thither.
