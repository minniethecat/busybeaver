[008-001] NEVER WONDER

[008-002] LET us strike the key-note again, before pursuing the tune.

[008-003] When she was half a dozen years younger, [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] had been overheard to

[008-004] begin a conversation with her brother one day, by saying ‘[[tom-gradgrind|Tom]], I

[008-005] wonder’—upon which [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]], who was the person overhearing, stepped

[008-006] forth into the light and said, ‘Louisa, never wonder!’

[008-007] Herein lay the spring of the mechanical art and mystery of educating the

[008-008] [[reason]] without stooping to the cultivation of the sentiments and

[008-009] affections. Never wonder. By means of addition, subtraction,

[008-010] multiplication, and division, settle everything somehow, and never

[008-011] wonder. Bring to me, says M’Choakumchild, yonder baby just able to walk,

[008-012] and I will engage that it shall never wonder.

[008-013] Now, besides very many babies just able to walk, there happened to be in

[008-014] [[coketown|Coketown]] a considerable population of babies who had been walking against

[008-015] time towards the infinite world, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty years and

[008-016] more. These portentous infants being alarming creatures to stalk about

[008-017] in any human society, the eighteen denominations incessantly scratched

[008-018] one another’s faces and pulled one another’s hair by way of agreeing on

[008-019] the steps to be taken for their improvement—which they never did; a

[008-020] surprising circumstance, when the happy adaptation of the means to the

[008-021] end is considered. Still, although they differed in every other

[008-022] particular, conceivable and inconceivable (especially inconceivable),

[008-023] they were pretty well united on the point that these unlucky infants were

[008-024] never to wonder. Body number one, said they must take everything on

[008-025] trust. Body number two, said they must take everything on political

[008-026] economy. Body number three, wrote leaden little books for them, showing

[008-027] how the good grown-up baby invariably got to the Savings-bank, and the

[008-028] bad grown-up baby invariably got transported. Body number four, under

[008-029] dreary pretences of being droll (when it was very melancholy indeed),

[008-030] made the shallowest pretences of concealing pitfalls of knowledge, into

[008-031] which it was the [[duty]] of these babies to be smuggled and inveigled. But,

[008-032] all the bodies agreed that they were never to wonder.

[008-033] There was a library in Coketown, to which general access was easy. Mr.

[008-034] [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] greatly tormented his mind about what the people read in this

[008-035] library: a point whereon little rivers of tabular statements periodically

[008-036] flowed into the howling ocean of tabular statements, which no diver ever

[008-037] got to any depth in and came up sane. It was a disheartening

[008-038] circumstance, but a melancholy [[facts|fact]], that even these readers persisted in

[008-039] wondering. They wondered about human [[nature]], human passions, human hopes

[008-040] and fears, the struggles, triumphs and defeats, the cares and joys and

[008-041] sorrows, the lives and deaths of common men and women! They sometimes,

[008-042] after fifteen hours’ work, sat down to read mere fables about men and

[008-043] women, more or less like themselves, and about [[childhood|children]], more or less

[008-044] like their own. They took De Foe to their bosoms, instead of Euclid, and

[008-045] seemed to be on the whole more comforted by Goldsmith than by Cocker.

[008-046] Mr. Gradgrind was for ever working, in print and out of print, at this

[008-047] eccentric sum, and he never could make out how it yielded this

[008-048] unaccountable product.

[008-049] ‘I am sick of my life, Loo. I, hate it altogether, and I hate everybody

[008-050] except you,’ said the unnatural young Thomas Gradgrind in the

[008-051] hair-cutting chamber at twilight.

[008-052] ‘You don’t hate [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]], Tom?’

[008-053] ‘I hate to be obliged to call her Jupe. And she hates me,’ said Tom,

[008-054] moodily.

[008-055] ‘No, she does not, Tom, I am sure!’

[008-056] ‘She must,’ said Tom. ‘She must just hate and detest the whole set-out

[008-057] of us. They’ll bother her head off, I think, before they have done with

[008-058] her. Already she’s getting as pale as wax, and as heavy as—I am.’

[008-059] Young Thomas expressed these sentiments sitting astride of a chair before

[008-060] the fire, with his arms on the back, and his sulky face on his arms. His

[008-061] sister sat in the darker corner by the fireside, now looking at him, now

[008-062] looking at the bright sparks as they dropped upon the hearth.

[008-063] ‘As to me,’ said Tom, tumbling his hair all manner of ways with his sulky

[008-064] hands, ‘I am a Donkey, that’s what _I_ am. I am as obstinate as one, I

[008-065] am more stupid than one, I get as much pleasure as one, and I should like

[008-066] to kick like one.’

[008-067] ‘Not me, I hope, Tom?’

[008-068] ‘No, Loo; I wouldn’t hurt _you_. I made an exception of you at first. I

[008-069] don’t know what this—jolly old—Jaundiced Jail,’ Tom had paused to find a

[008-070] sufficiently complimentary and expressive name for the parental roof, and

[008-071] seemed to relieve his mind for a moment by the strong alliteration of

[008-072] this one, ‘would be without you.’

[008-073] ‘Indeed, Tom? Do you really and truly say so?’

[008-074] ‘Why, of course I do. What’s the use of talking about it!’ returned Tom,

[008-075] chafing his face on his coat-sleeve, as if to mortify his flesh, and have

[008-076] it in unison with his spirit.

[008-077] ‘Because, Tom,’ said his sister, after silently watching the sparks

[008-078] awhile, ‘as I get older, and nearer growing up, I often sit wondering

[008-079] here, and think how unfortunate it is for me that I can’t reconcile you

[008-080] to home better than I am able to do. I don’t know what other girls know.

[008-081] I can’t play to you, or sing to you. I can’t talk to you so as to

[008-082] lighten your mind, for I never see any amusing sights or read any amusing

[008-083] books that it would be a pleasure or a relief to you to talk about, when

[008-084] you are tired.’

[008-085] ‘Well, no more do I. I am as bad as you in that respect; and I am a Mule

[008-086] too, which you’re not. If father was determined to make me either a Prig

[008-087] or a Mule, and I am not a Prig, why, it stands to reason, I must be a

[008-088] Mule. And so I am,’ said Tom, desperately.

[008-089] ‘It’s a great pity,’ said Louisa, after another pause, and speaking

[008-090] thoughtfully out of her dark corner: ‘it’s a great pity, Tom. It’s very

[008-091] unfortunate for both of us.’

[008-092] ‘Oh! You,’ said Tom; ‘you are a girl, Loo, and a girl comes out of it

[008-093] better than a boy does. I don’t miss anything in you. You are the only

[008-094] pleasure I have—you can brighten even this place—and you can always lead

[008-095] me as you like.’

[008-096] ‘You are a [[love|dear]] brother, Tom; and while you think I can do such things, I

[008-097] don’t so much mind knowing better. Though I do know better, Tom, and am

[008-098] very sorry for it.’ She came and kissed him, and went back into her

[008-099] corner again.

[008-100] ‘I wish I could collect all the Facts we hear so much about,’ said Tom,

[008-101] spitefully setting his teeth, ‘and all the Figures, and all the people

[008-102] who found them out: and I wish I could put a thousand barrels of

[008-103] gunpowder under them, and blow them all up together! However, when I go

[008-104] to live with old [[josiah-bounderby|Bounderby]], I’ll have my revenge.’

[008-105] ‘Your revenge, Tom?’

[008-106] ‘I mean, I’ll enjoy myself a little, and go about and see something, and

[008-107] hear something. I’ll recompense myself for the way in which I have been

[008-108] brought up.’

[008-109] ‘But don’t disappoint yourself beforehand, Tom. Mr. Bounderby thinks as

[008-110] father thinks, and is a great deal rougher, and not half so kind.’

[008-111] ‘Oh!’ said Tom, laughing; ‘I don’t mind that. I shall very well know how

[008-112] to manage and smooth old Bounderby!’

[008-113] Their shadows were defined upon the wall, but those of the high presses

[008-114] in the room were all blended together on the wall and on the ceiling, as

[008-115] if the brother and sister were overhung by a dark cavern. Or, a fanciful

[008-116] [[imagination]]—if such treason could have been there—might have made it out

[008-117] to be the shadow of their subject, and of its lowering association with

[008-118] their future.

[008-119] ‘What is your great mode of smoothing and managing, Tom? Is it a

[008-120] secret?’

[008-121] ‘Oh!’ said Tom, ‘if it is a secret, it’s not far off. It’s you. You are

[008-122] his little pet, you are his favourite; he’ll do anything for you. When

[008-123] he says to me what I don’t like, I shall say to him, “My sister Loo will

[008-124] be hurt and disappointed, Mr. Bounderby. She always used to tell me she

[008-125] was sure you would be easier with me than this.” That’ll bring him

[008-126] about, or nothing will.’

[008-127] After waiting for some answering remark, and getting none, Tom wearily

[008-128] relapsed into the present time, and twined himself yawning round and

[008-129] about the rails of his chair, and rumpled his head more and more, until

[008-130] he suddenly looked up, and asked:

[008-131] ‘Have you gone to sleep, Loo?’

[008-132] ‘No, Tom. I am looking at the fire.’

[008-133] ‘You seem to find more to look at in it than ever I could find,’ said

[008-134] Tom. ‘Another of the advantages, I suppose, of being a girl.’

[008-135] ‘Tom,’ enquired his sister, slowly, and in a curious tone, as if she were

[008-136] reading what she asked in the fire, and it was not quite plainly written

[008-137] there, ‘do you look forward with any satisfaction to this change to Mr.

[008-138] Bounderby’s?’

[008-139] ‘Why, there’s one thing to be said of it,’ returned Tom, pushing his

[008-140] chair from him, and standing up; ‘it will be getting away from home.’

[008-141] ‘There is one thing to be said of it,’ Louisa repeated in her former

[008-142] curious tone; ‘it will be getting away from home. Yes.’

[008-143] ‘Not but what I shall be very unwilling, both to leave you, Loo, and to

[008-144] leave you here. But I must go, you know, whether I like it or not; and I

[008-145] had better go where I can take with me some advantage of your influence,

[008-146] than where I should lose it altogether. Don’t you see?’

[008-147] ‘Yes, Tom.’

[008-148] The answer was so long in coming, though there was no indecision in it,

[008-149] that Tom went and leaned on the back of her chair, to contemplate the

[008-150] fire which so engrossed her, from her point of view, and see what he

[008-151] could make of it.

[008-152] ‘Except that it is a fire,’ said Tom, ‘it looks to me as stupid and blank

[008-153] as everything else looks. What do you see in it? Not a [[performance|circus]]?’

[008-154] ‘I don’t see anything in it, Tom, particularly. But since I have been

[008-155] looking at it, I have been wondering about you and me, grown up.’

[008-156] ‘Wondering again!’ said Tom.

[008-157] ‘I have such unmanageable thoughts,’ returned his sister, ‘that they

[008-158] _will_ wonder.’

[008-159] ‘Then I beg of you, Louisa,’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, who had opened the door

[008-160] without being heard, ‘to do nothing of that description, for goodness’

[008-161] sake, you inconsiderate girl, or I shall never hear the last of it from

[008-162] your father. And, Thomas, it is really [[shame|shameful]], with my poor head

[008-163] continually wearing me out, that a boy brought up as you have been, and

[008-164] whose [[education]] has cost what yours has, should be found encouraging his

[008-165] sister to wonder, when he knows his father has expressly said that she is

[008-166] not to do it.’

[008-167] Louisa denied Tom’s participation in the offence; but her mother stopped

[008-168] her with the conclusive answer, ‘Louisa, don’t tell me, in my state of

[008-169] health; for unless you had been encouraged, it is morally and physically

[008-170] impossible that you could have done it.’

[008-171] ‘I was encouraged by nothing, mother, but by looking at the red sparks

[008-172] dropping out of the fire, and whitening and dying. It made me think,

[008-173] after all, how short my life would be, and how little I could hope to do

[008-174] in it.’

[008-175] ‘Nonsense!’ said Mrs. Gradgrind, rendered almost energetic. ‘Nonsense!

[008-176] Don’t stand there and tell me such stuff, Louisa, to my face, when you

[008-177] know very well that if it was ever to reach your father’s ears I should

[008-178] never hear the last of it. After all the trouble that has been taken

[008-179] with you! After the lectures you have attended, and the experiments you

[008-180] have seen! After I have heard you myself, when the whole of my right

[008-181] side has been benumbed, going on with your master about combustion, and

[008-182] calcination, and calorification, and I may say every kind of ation that

[008-183] could drive a poor invalid distracted, to hear you talking in this absurd

[008-184] way about sparks and ashes! I wish,’ whimpered Mrs. Gradgrind, taking a

[008-185] chair, and discharging her strongest point before succumbing under these

[008-186] mere shadows of facts, ‘yes, I really _do_ wish that I had never had a

[008-187] [[family]], and then you would have known what it was to do without me!’
