[037-001] FINAL

[037-002] IT is a dangerous thing to see anything in the sphere of a vain

[037-003] blusterer, before the vain blusterer sees it himself. [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] felt

[037-004] that [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]] had audaciously anticipated him, and presumed to be

[037-005] wiser than he. Inappeasably indignant with her for her triumphant

[037-006] discovery of [[mrs-pegler|Mrs. Pegler]], he turned this presumption, on the part of a

[037-007] woman in her dependent position, over and over in his mind, until it

[037-008] accumulated with turning like a great snowball. At last he made the

[037-009] discovery that to discharge this highly connected female—to have it in

[037-010] his power to say, ‘She was a woman of [[family]], and wanted to stick to me,

[037-011] but I wouldn’t have it, and got rid of her’—would be to get the utmost

[037-012] possible amount of crowning glory out of the connection, and at the same

[037-013] time to punish Mrs. Sparsit according to her deserts.

[037-014] Filled fuller than ever, with this great idea, Mr. Bounderby came in to

[037-015] lunch, and sat himself down in the dining-room of former days, where his

[037-016] portrait was. Mrs. Sparsit sat by the fire, with her foot in her cotton

[037-017] stirrup, little thinking whither she was posting.

[037-018] Since the Pegler affair, this gentlewoman had covered her pity for Mr.

[037-019] Bounderby with a veil of quiet melancholy and contrition. In virtue

[037-020] thereof, it had become her habit to assume a woful look, which woful look

[037-021] she now bestowed upon her patron.

[037-022] ‘What’s the matter now, ma’am?’ said Mr. Bounderby, in a very short,

[037-023] rough way.

[037-024] ‘Pray, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, ‘do not bite my nose off.’

[037-025] ‘Bite your nose off, ma’am?’ repeated Mr. Bounderby. ‘_Your_ nose!’

[037-026] meaning, as Mrs. Sparsit conceived, that it was too developed a nose for

[037-027] the purpose. After which offensive implication, he cut himself a crust

[037-028] of bread, and threw the knife down with a noise.

[037-029] Mrs. Sparsit took her foot out of her stirrup, and said, ‘Mr. Bounderby,

[037-030] sir!’

[037-031] ‘Well, ma’am?’ retorted Mr. Bounderby. ‘What are you staring at?’

[037-032] ‘May I ask, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, ‘have you been ruffled this

[037-033] morning?’

[037-034] ‘Yes, ma’am.’

[037-035] ‘May I inquire, sir,’ pursued the injured woman, ‘whether _I_ am the

[037-036] unfortunate cause of your having lost your temper?’

[037-037] ‘Now, I’ll tell you what, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘I am not come here to

[037-038] be bullied. A female may be highly connected, but she can’t be permitted

[037-039] to bother and badger a man in my position, and I am not going to put up

[037-040] with it.’ (Mr. Bounderby felt it necessary to get on: foreseeing that if

[037-041] he allowed of details, he would be beaten.)

[037-042] Mrs. Sparsit first elevated, then knitted, her Coriolanian eyebrows;

[037-043] gathered up her work into its proper basket; and rose.

[037-044] ‘Sir,’ said she, majestically. ‘It is apparent to me that I am in your

[037-045] way at present. I will retire to my own apartment.’

[037-046] ‘Allow me to open the door, ma’am.’

[037-047] ‘Thank you, sir; I can do it for myself.’

[037-048] ‘You had better allow me, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, passing her, and

[037-049] getting his hand upon the lock; ‘because I can take the opportunity of

[037-050] saying a word to you, before you go. Mrs. Sparsit, ma’am, I rather think

[037-051] you are cramped here, do you know? It appears to me, that, under my

[037-052] humble roof, there’s hardly opening enough for a lady of your genius in

[037-053] other people’s affairs.’

[037-054] Mrs. Sparsit gave him a look of the darkest scorn, and said with great

[037-055] politeness, ‘Really, sir?’

[037-056] ‘I have been thinking it over, you see, since the late affairs have

[037-057] happened, ma’am,’ said Bounderby; ‘and it appears to my poor judgment—’

[037-058] ‘Oh! Pray, sir,’ Mrs. Sparsit interposed, with sprightly cheerfulness,

[037-059] ‘don’t disparage your judgment. Everybody knows how unerring Mr.

[037-060] Bounderby’s judgment is. Everybody has had proofs of it. It must be the

[037-061] theme of general conversation. Disparage anything in yourself but your

[037-062] judgment, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, laughing.

[037-063] Mr. Bounderby, very red and uncomfortable, resumed:

[037-064] ‘It appears to me, ma’am, I say, that a different sort of establishment

[037-065] altogether would bring out a lady of _your_ powers. Such an

[037-066] establishment as your relation, [[lady-scadgers|Lady Scadgers]]’s, now. Don’t you think

[037-067] you might find some affairs there, ma’am, to interfere with?’

[037-068] ‘It never occurred to me before, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit; ‘but now

[037-069] you mention it, should think it highly probable.’

[037-070] ‘Then suppose you try, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, laying an envelope with a

[037-071] cheque in it in her little basket. ‘You can take your own time for

[037-072] going, ma’am; but perhaps in the meanwhile, it will be more agreeable to

[037-073] a lady of your powers of mind, to eat her meals by herself, and not to be

[037-074] intruded upon. I really ought to apologise to you—being only Josiah

[037-075] Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]]—for having stood in your light so long.’

[037-076] ‘Pray don’t name it, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit. ‘If that portrait

[037-077] could speak, sir—but it has the advantage over the original of not

[037-078] possessing the power of committing itself and disgusting others,—it would

[037-079] testify, that a long period has elapsed since I first habitually

[037-080] addressed it as the picture of a Noodle. Nothing that a Noodle does, can

[037-081] awaken surprise or indignation; the proceedings of a Noodle can only

[037-082] inspire contempt.’

[037-083] Thus saying, Mrs. Sparsit, with her Roman features like a medal struck to

[037-084] commemorate her scorn of Mr. Bounderby, surveyed him fixedly from [[reason|head]] to

[037-085] foot, swept disdainfully past him, and ascended the staircase. Mr.

[037-086] Bounderby closed the door, and stood before the fire; projecting himself

[037-087] after his old explosive manner into his portrait—and into futurity.

[037-088] * * * * *

[037-089] Into how much of futurity? He saw Mrs. Sparsit fighting out a daily

[037-090] fight at the points of all the weapons in the female armoury, with the

[037-091] grudging, smarting, peevish, tormenting Lady Scadgers, still laid up in

[037-092] bed with her mysterious leg, and gobbling her insufficient income down by

[037-093] about the middle of every quarter, in a mean little airless lodging, a

[037-094] mere closet for one, a mere crib for two; but did he see more? Did he

[037-095] catch any glimpse of himself making a [[performance|show]] of [[bitzer|Bitzer]] to strangers, as the

[037-096] rising young man, so devoted to his master’s great merits, who had won

[037-097] [[tom-gradgrind|young Tom]]’s place, and had almost captured young Tom himself, in the

[037-098] times when by various rascals he was spirited away? Did he see any faint

[037-099] reflection of his own image making a vain-glorious will, whereby

[037-100] five-and-twenty Humbugs, past five-and-fifty years of age, each taking

[037-101] upon himself the name, Josiah Bounderby of Coketown, should for ever dine

[037-102] in Bounderby Hall, for ever lodge in Bounderby buildings, for ever attend

[037-103] a Bounderby chapel, for ever go to sleep under a Bounderby chaplain, for

[037-104] ever be supported out of a Bounderby estate, and for ever nauseate all

[037-105] healthy stomachs, with a vast amount of Bounderby balderdash and bluster?

[037-106] Had he any prescience of the day, five years to come, when Josiah

[037-107] Bounderby of Coketown was to die of a fit in the Coketown street, and

[037-108] this same precious will was to begin its long career of quibble, plunder,

[037-109] false pretences, vile example, little service and much law? Probably

[037-110] not. Yet the portrait was to see it all out.

[037-111] Here was [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]] on the same day, and in the same hour, sitting

[037-112] thoughtful in his own room. How much of futurity did _he_ see? Did he

[037-113] see himself, a white-haired decrepit man, bending his hitherto inflexible

[037-114] theories to appointed circumstances; making his [[facts]] and figures

[037-115] subservient to Faith, Hope, and Charity; and no longer trying to grind

[037-116] that Heavenly trio in his dusty little mills? Did he catch sight of

[037-117] himself, therefore much despised by his late political associates? Did

[037-118] he see them, in the era of its being quite settled that the national

[037-119] dustmen have only to do with one another, and owe no [[duty]] to an

[037-120] abstraction called a People, ‘taunting the honourable [[gentleman]]’ with

[037-121] this and with that and with what not, five nights a-week, until the small

[037-122] hours of the morning? Probably he had that much foreknowledge, knowing

[037-123] his men.

[037-124] * * * * *

[037-125] Here was [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] on the night of the same day, watching the fire as in

[037-126] days of yore, though with a gentler and a humbler face. How much of the

[037-127] future might arise before _her_ vision? Broadsides in the streets,

[037-128] signed with her father’s name, exonerating the late [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]],

[037-129] weaver, from misplaced suspicion, and publishing the guilt of his own

[037-130] son, with such extenuation as his years and temptation (he could not

[037-131] bring himself to add, his [[education]]) might beseech; were of the Present.

[037-132] So, Stephen Blackpool’s tombstone, with her father’s record of his death,

[037-133] was almost of the Present, for she knew it was to be. These things she

[037-134] could plainly see. But, how much of the Future?

[037-135] A working woman, christened [[rachael|Rachael]], after a long illness once again

[037-136] appearing at the ringing of the Factory bell, and passing to and fro at

[037-137] the set hours, among the Coketown Hands; a woman of pensive beauty,

[037-138] always dressed in black, but sweet-tempered and serene, and even

[037-139] cheerful; who, of all the people in the place, alone appeared to have

[037-140] compassion on a degraded, drunken wretch of her own sex, who was

[037-141] sometimes seen in the town secretly begging of her, and crying to her; a

[037-142] woman working, ever working, but content to do it, and preferring to do

[037-143] it as her [[nature|natural]] lot, until she should be too old to labour any more?

[037-144] Did Louisa see this? Such a thing was to be.

[037-145] A lonely brother, many thousands of miles away, writing, on paper blotted

[037-146] with tears, that her words had too soon come true, and that all the

[037-147] treasures in the world would be cheaply bartered for a sight of her [[love|dear]]

[037-148] face? At length this brother coming nearer home, with hope of seeing

[037-149] her, and being delayed by illness; and then a letter, in a strange hand,

[037-150] saying ‘he died in hospital, of fever, such a day, and died in penitence

[037-151] and love of you: his last word being your name’? Did Louisa see these

[037-152] things? Such things were to be.

[037-153] Herself again a wife—a mother—lovingly watchful of her [[childhood|children]], ever

[037-154] careful that they should have a childhood of the mind no less than a

[037-155] childhood of the body, as knowing it to be even a more beautiful thing,

[037-156] and a possession, any hoarded scrap of which, is a blessing and happiness

[037-157] to the wisest? Did Louisa see this? Such a thing was never to be.

[037-158] But, happy [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]]’s happy children loving her; all children loving her;

[037-159] she, grown learned in childish lore; thinking no innocent and pretty

[037-160] [[fancy]] ever to be despised; trying hard to know her humbler

[037-161] fellow-creatures, and to beautify their lives of machinery and reality

[037-162] with those [[imagination|imaginative]] graces and delights, without which the [[heart]] of

[037-163] infancy will wither up, the sturdiest physical manhood will be morally

[037-164] stark death, and the plainest national prosperity figures can show, will

[037-165] be the Writing on the Wall,—she holding this course as part of no

[037-166] fantastic vow, or bond, or brotherhood, or sisterhood, or pledge, or

[037-167] covenant, or fancy dress, or fancy fair; but simply as a duty to be

[037-168] done,—did Louisa see these things of herself? These things were to be.

[037-169] Dear reader! It rests with you and me, whether, in our two fields of

[037-170] action, similar things shall be or not. Let them be! We shall sit with

[037-171] lighter bosoms on the hearth, to see the ashes of our fires turn gray and

[037-172] cold.
