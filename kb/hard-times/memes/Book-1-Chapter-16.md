[016-001] HUSBAND AND WIFE

[016-002] MR. BOUNDERBY’S first disquietude on hearing of his happiness, was

[016-003] occasioned by the necessity of imparting it to [[mrs-sparsit|Mrs. Sparsit]]. He could

[016-004] not make up his mind how to do that, or what the consequences of the step

[016-005] might be. Whether she would instantly depart, bag and baggage, to Lady

[016-006] Scadgers, or would positively refuse to budge from the premises; whether

[016-007] she would be plaintive or abusive, tearful or tearing; whether she would

[016-008] break her [[heart]], or break the looking-glass; [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]] could not all

[016-009] foresee. However, as it must be done, he had no choice but to do it; so,

[016-010] after attempting several letters, and failing in them all, he resolved to

[016-011] do it by word of mouth.

[016-012] On his way home, on the evening he set aside for this momentous purpose,

[016-013] he took the precaution of stepping into a chemist’s shop and buying a

[016-014] bottle of the very strongest smelling-salts. ‘By George!’ said Mr.

[016-015] Bounderby, ‘if she takes it in the fainting way, I’ll have the skin off

[016-016] her nose, at all events!’ But, in spite of being thus forearmed, he

[016-017] entered his own house with anything but a courageous air; and appeared

[016-018] before the object of his misgivings, like a dog who was conscious of

[016-019] coming direct from the pantry.

[016-020] ‘Good evening, Mr. Bounderby!’

[016-021] ‘Good evening, ma’am, good evening.’ He drew up his chair, and Mrs.

[016-022] Sparsit drew back hers, as who should say, ‘Your fireside, sir. I freely

[016-023] admit it. It is for you to occupy it all, if you think proper.’

[016-024] ‘Don’t go to the North Pole, ma’am!’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[016-025] ‘Thank you, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, and returned, though short of her

[016-026] former position.

[016-027] Mr. Bounderby sat looking at her, as, with the points of a stiff, sharp

[016-028] pair of scissors, she picked out holes for some inscrutable ornamental

[016-029] purpose, in a piece of cambric. An operation which, taken in connexion

[016-030] with the bushy eyebrows and the Roman nose, suggested with some

[016-031] liveliness the idea of a hawk engaged upon the eyes of a tough little

[016-032] bird. She was so steadfastly occupied, that many minutes elapsed before

[016-033] she looked up from her work; when she did so Mr. Bounderby bespoke her

[016-034] attention with a hitch of his [[reason|head]].

[016-035] ‘Mrs. Sparsit, ma’am,’ said Mr. Bounderby, putting his hands in his

[016-036] pockets, and assuring himself with his right hand that the cork of the

[016-037] little bottle was ready for use, ‘I have no occasion to say to you, that

[016-038] you are not only a lady born and bred, but a devilish sensible woman.’

[016-039] ‘Sir,’ returned the lady, ‘this is indeed not the first time that you

[016-040] have honoured me with similar expressions of your good opinion.’

[016-041] ‘Mrs. Sparsit, ma’am,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘I am going to astonish you.’

[016-042] ‘Yes, sir?’ returned Mrs. Sparsit, interrogatively, and in the most

[016-043] tranquil manner possible. She generally wore mittens, and she now laid

[016-044] down her work, and smoothed those mittens.

[016-045] ‘I am going, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘to marry [[tom-gradgrind|Tom Gradgrind]]’s daughter.’

[016-046] ‘Yes, sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit. ‘I hope you may be happy, Mr.

[016-047] Bounderby. Oh, indeed I hope you may be happy, sir!’ And she said it

[016-048] with such great condescension as well as with such great compassion for

[016-049] him, that Bounderby,—far more disconcerted than if she had thrown her

[016-050] workbox at the mirror, or swooned on the hearthrug,—corked up the

[016-051] smelling-salts tight in his pocket, and thought, ‘Now confound this

[016-052] woman, who could have even guessed that she would take it in this way!’

[016-053] ‘I wish with all my heart, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, in a highly superior

[016-054] manner; somehow she seemed, in a moment, to have established a right to

[016-055] pity him ever afterwards; ‘that you may be in all respects very happy.’

[016-056] ‘Well, ma’am,’ returned Bounderby, with some resentment in his tone:

[016-057] which was clearly lowered, though in spite of himself, ‘I am obliged to

[016-058] you. I hope I shall be.’

[016-059] ‘_Do_ you, sir!’ said Mrs. Sparsit, with great affability. ‘But

[016-060] naturally you do; of course you do.’

[016-061] A very awkward pause on Mr. Bounderby’s part, succeeded. Mrs. Sparsit

[016-062] sedately resumed her work and occasionally gave a small cough, which

[016-063] sounded like the cough of conscious strength and forbearance.

[016-064] ‘Well, ma’am,’ resumed Bounderby, ‘under these circumstances, I imagine

[016-065] it would not be agreeable to a character like yours to remain here,

[016-066] though you would be very welcome here.’

[016-067] ‘Oh, [[love|dear]] no, sir, I could on no account think of that!’ Mrs. Sparsit

[016-068] shook her head, still in her highly superior manner, and a little changed

[016-069] the small cough—coughing now, as if the spirit of prophecy rose within

[016-070] her, but had better be coughed down.

[016-071] ‘However, ma’am,’ said Bounderby, ‘there are apartments at the Bank,

[016-072] where a born and bred lady, as keeper of the place, would be rather a

[016-073] catch than otherwise; and if the same terms—’

[016-074] ‘I beg your pardon, sir. You were so good as to promise that you would

[016-075] always substitute the phrase, annual compliment.’

[016-076] ‘Well, ma’am, annual compliment. If the same annual compliment would be

[016-077] acceptable there, why, I see nothing to part us, unless you do.’

[016-078] ‘Sir,’ returned Mrs. Sparsit. ‘The proposal is like yourself, and if the

[016-079] position I shall assume at the Bank is one that I could occupy without

[016-080] descending lower in the social scale—’

[016-081] ‘Why, of course it is,’ said Bounderby. ‘If it was not, ma’am, you don’t

[016-082] suppose that I should offer it to a lady who has moved in the society you

[016-083] have moved in. Not that _I_ care for such society, you know! But _you_

[016-084] do.’

[016-085] ‘Mr. Bounderby, you are very considerate.’

[016-086] ‘You’ll have your own private apartments, and you’ll have your coals and

[016-087] your candles, and all the rest of it, and you’ll have your maid to attend

[016-088] upon you, and you’ll have your light porter to protect you, and you’ll be

[016-089] what I take the liberty of considering precious comfortable,’ said

[016-090] Bounderby.

[016-091] ‘Sir,’ rejoined Mrs. Sparsit, ‘say no more. In yielding up my trust

[016-092] here, I shall not be freed from the necessity of eating the bread of

[016-093] dependence:’ she might have said the sweetbread, for that delicate

[016-094] article in a savoury brown sauce was her favourite supper: ‘and I would

[016-095] rather receive it from your hand, than from any other. Therefore, sir, I

[016-096] accept your offer gratefully, and with many sincere acknowledgments for

[016-097] past favours. And I hope, sir,’ said Mrs. Sparsit, concluding in an

[016-098] impressively compassionate manner, ‘I fondly hope that [[louisa-gradgrind|Miss Gradgrind]] may

[016-099] be all you desire, and deserve!’

[016-100] Nothing moved Mrs. Sparsit from that position any more. It was in vain

[016-101] for Bounderby to bluster or to assert himself in any of his explosive

[016-102] ways; Mrs. Sparsit was resolved to have compassion on him, as a Victim.

[016-103] She was polite, obliging, cheerful, hopeful; but, the more polite, the

[016-104] more obliging, the more cheerful, the more hopeful, the more exemplary

[016-105] altogether, she; the forlorner Sacrifice and Victim, he. She had that

[016-106] tenderness for his melancholy fate, that his great red countenance used

[016-107] to break out into cold perspirations when she looked at him.

[016-108] Meanwhile the [[marriage]] was appointed to be solemnized in eight weeks’

[016-109] time, and Mr. Bounderby went every evening to [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]] as an accepted

[016-110] wooer. Love was made on these occasions in the form of bracelets; and,

[016-111] on all occasions during the period of betrothal, took a manufacturing

[016-112] aspect. Dresses were made, jewellery was made, cakes and gloves were

[016-113] made, settlements were made, and an extensive assortment of [[facts|Facts]] did

[016-114] appropriate honour to the contract. The business was all Fact, from

[016-115] first to last. The Hours did not go through any of those rosy

[016-116] performances, which foolish poets have ascribed to them at such times;

[016-117] neither did the clocks go any faster, or any slower, than at other

[016-118] seasons. The deadly statistical recorder in the [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] observatory

[016-119] knocked every second on the head as it was born, and buried it with his

[016-120] accustomed regularity.

[016-121] So the day came, as all other days come to people who will only stick to

[016-122] reason; and when it came, there were married in the church of the florid

[016-123] wooden legs—that popular order of architecture—Josiah Bounderby Esquire

[016-124] of [[coketown|Coketown]], to Louisa eldest daughter of [[thomas-gradgrind|Thomas Gradgrind]] Esquire of

[016-125] Stone Lodge, M.P. for that borough. And when they were united in holy

[016-126] matrimony, they went home to breakfast at Stone Lodge aforesaid.

[016-127] There was an improving party assembled on the auspicious occasion, who

[016-128] knew what everything they had to eat and drink was made of, and how it

[016-129] was imported or exported, and in what quantities, and in what bottoms,

[016-130] whether native or foreign, and all about it. The bridesmaids, down to

[016-131] little Jane Gradgrind, were, in an intellectual point of view, fit

[016-132] helpmates for the calculating boy; and there was no nonsense about any of

[016-133] the company.

[016-134] After breakfast, the bridegroom addressed them in the following terms:

[016-135] ‘Ladies and [[gentleman|gentlemen]], I am Josiah Bounderby of Coketown. Since you have

[016-136] done my wife and myself the honour of drinking our healths and happiness,

[016-137] I suppose I must acknowledge the same; though, as you all know me, and

[016-138] know what I am, and what my extraction was, you won’t expect a speech

[016-139] from a man who, when he sees a Post, says “that’s a Post,” and when he

[016-140] sees a Pump, says “that’s a Pump,” and is not to be got to call a Post a

[016-141] Pump, or a Pump a Post, or either of them a Toothpick. If you want a

[016-142] speech this morning, my friend and father-in-law, Tom Gradgrind, is a

[016-143] Member of Parliament, and you know where to get it. I am not your man.

[016-144] However, if I feel a little independent when I look around this table

[016-145] to-day, and reflect how little I thought of marrying Tom Gradgrind’s

[016-146] daughter when I was a ragged street-boy, who never washed his face unless

[016-147] it was at a pump, and that not oftener than once a fortnight, I hope I

[016-148] may be excused. So, I hope you like my feeling independent; if you

[016-149] don’t, I can’t help it. I _do_ feel independent. Now I have mentioned,

[016-150] and you have mentioned, that I am this day married to Tom Gradgrind’s

[016-151] daughter. I am very glad to be so. It has long been my wish to be so.

[016-152] I have watched her bringing-up, and I believe she is worthy of me. At

[016-153] the same time—not to deceive you—I believe I am worthy of her. So, I

[016-154] thank you, on both our parts, for the good-will you have shown towards

[016-155] us; and the best wish I can give the unmarried part of the present

[016-156] company, is this: I hope every bachelor may find as good a wife as I have

[016-157] found. And I hope every spinster may find as good a husband as my wife

[016-158] has found.’

[016-159] Shortly after which oration, as they were going on a nuptial trip to

[016-160] Lyons, in order that Mr. Bounderby might take the opportunity of seeing

[016-161] how the Hands got on in those parts, and whether they, too, required to

[016-162] be fed with gold spoons; the happy pair departed for the railroad. The

[016-163] bride, in passing down-stairs, dressed for her journey, found Tom waiting

[016-164] for her—flushed, either with his feelings, or the vinous part of the

[016-165] breakfast.

[016-166] ‘What a game girl you are, to be such a first-rate sister, Loo!’

[016-167] whispered Tom.

[016-168] She clung to him as she should have clung to some far better [[nature]] that

[016-169] day, and was a little shaken in her reserved composure for the first

[016-170] time.

[016-171] ‘Old Bounderby’s quite ready,’ said Tom. ‘Time’s up. Good-bye! I shall

[016-172] be on the look-out for you, when you come back. I say, my dear Loo!

[016-173] AN’T it uncommonly jolly now!’
