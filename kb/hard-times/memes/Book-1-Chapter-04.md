[004-001] MR. BOUNDERBY

[004-002] NOT being Mrs. Grundy, who _was_ [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]?

[004-003] Why, Mr. Bounderby was as near being [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]]’s bosom friend, as a

[004-004] man perfectly devoid of sentiment can approach that spiritual

[004-005] relationship towards another man perfectly devoid of sentiment. So near

[004-006] was Mr. Bounderby—or, if the reader should prefer it, so far off.

[004-007] He was a rich man: banker, merchant, manufacturer, and what not. A big,

[004-008] loud man, with a stare, and a metallic laugh. A man made out of a coarse

[004-009] material, which seemed to have been stretched to make so much of him. A

[004-010] man with a great puffed [[reason|head]] and forehead, swelled veins in his temples,

[004-011] and such a strained skin to his face that it seemed to hold his eyes

[004-012] open, and lift his eyebrows up. A man with a pervading appearance on him

[004-013] of being inflated like a balloon, and ready to start. A man who could

[004-014] never sufficiently vaunt himself a self-made man. A man who was always

[004-015] proclaiming, through that brassy speaking-trumpet of a voice of his, his

[004-016] old ignorance and his old poverty. A man who was the [[humility|Bully of humility]].

[004-017] A year or two younger than his eminently practical friend, Mr. Bounderby

[004-018] looked older; his seven or eight and forty might have had the seven or

[004-019] eight added to it again, without surprising anybody. He had not much

[004-020] hair. One might have fancied he had talked it off; and that what was

[004-021] left, all standing up in disorder, was in that condition from being

[004-022] constantly blown about by his windy boastfulness.

[004-023] In the formal drawing-room of [[stone-lodge|Stone Lodge]], standing on the hearthrug,

[004-024] warming himself before the fire, Mr. Bounderby delivered some

[004-025] observations to [[mrs-gradgrind|Mrs. Gradgrind]] on the circumstance of its being his

[004-026] birthday. He stood before the fire, partly because it was a cool spring

[004-027] afternoon, though the sun shone; partly because the shade of Stone Lodge

[004-028] was always haunted by the ghost of damp mortar; partly because he thus

[004-029] took up a commanding position, from which to subdue Mrs. Gradgrind.

[004-030] ‘I hadn’t a shoe to my foot. As to a stocking, I didn’t know such a

[004-031] thing by name. I passed the day in a ditch, and the night in a pigsty.

[004-032] That’s the way I spent my tenth birthday. Not that a ditch was new to

[004-033] me, for I was born in a ditch.’

[004-034] Mrs. Gradgrind, a little, thin, white, pink-eyed bundle of shawls, of

[004-035] surpassing feebleness, mental and bodily; who was always taking physic

[004-036] without any effect, and who, whenever she showed a symptom of coming to

[004-037] life, was invariably stunned by some weighty piece of [[facts|fact]] tumbling on

[004-038] her; Mrs. Gradgrind hoped it was a dry ditch?

[004-039] ‘No! As wet as a sop. A foot of water in it,’ said Mr. Bounderby.

[004-040] ‘Enough to give a baby cold,’ Mrs. Gradgrind considered.

[004-041] ‘Cold? I was born with inflammation of the lungs, and of everything

[004-042] else, I believe, that was capable of inflammation,’ returned Mr.

[004-043] Bounderby. ‘For years, ma’am, I was one of the most miserable little

[004-044] wretches ever seen. I was so sickly, that I was always moaning and

[004-045] groaning. I was so ragged and dirty, that you wouldn’t have touched me

[004-046] with a pair of tongs.’

[004-047] Mrs. Gradgrind faintly looked at the tongs, as the most appropriate thing

[004-048] her imbecility could think of doing.

[004-049] ‘How I fought through it, _I_ don’t know,’ said Bounderby. ‘I was

[004-050] determined, I suppose. I have been a determined character in later life,

[004-051] and I suppose I was then. Here I am, Mrs. Gradgrind, anyhow, and nobody

[004-052] to thank for my being here, but myself.’

[004-053] Mrs. Gradgrind meekly and weakly hoped that his mother—

[004-054] ‘_My_ mother? Bolted, ma’am!’ said Bounderby.

[004-055] Mrs. Gradgrind, stunned as usual, collapsed and gave it up.

[004-056] ‘My mother left me to my grandmother,’ said Bounderby; ‘and, according to

[004-057] the best of my remembrance, my grandmother was the wickedest and the

[004-058] worst old woman that ever lived. If I got a little pair of shoes by any

[004-059] chance, she would take ’em off and sell ’em for drink. Why, I have known

[004-060] that grandmother of mine lie in her bed and drink her four-teen glasses

[004-061] of liquor before breakfast!’

[004-062] Mrs. Gradgrind, weakly smiling, and giving no other sign of vitality,

[004-063] looked (as she always did) like an indifferently executed transparency of

[004-064] a small female figure, without enough light behind it.

[004-065] ‘She kept a chandler’s shop,’ pursued Bounderby, ‘and kept me in an

[004-066] egg-box. That was the cot of _my_ [[childhood|infancy]]; an old egg-box. As soon as I

[004-067] was big enough to run away, of course I ran away. Then I became a young

[004-068] vagabond; and instead of one old woman knocking me about and starving me,

[004-069] everybody of all ages knocked me about and starved me. They were right;

[004-070] they had no business to do anything else. I was a nuisance, an

[004-071] incumbrance, and a pest. I know that very well.’

[004-072] His [[pride]] in having at any time of his life achieved such a great social

[004-073] distinction as to be a nuisance, an incumbrance, and a pest, was only to

[004-074] be satisfied by three sonorous repetitions of the boast.

[004-075] ‘I was to pull through it, I suppose, Mrs. Gradgrind. Whether I was to

[004-076] do it or not, ma’am, I did it. I pulled through it, though nobody threw

[004-077] me out a rope. Vagabond, errand-boy, vagabond, labourer, porter, clerk,

[004-078] chief manager, small partner, Josiah Bounderby of [[coketown|Coketown]]. Those are

[004-079] the antecedents, and the culmination. Josiah Bounderby of Coketown

[004-080] learnt his letters from the outsides of the shops, Mrs. Gradgrind, and

[004-081] was first able to tell the time upon a dial-plate, from studying the

[004-082] steeple clock of St. Giles’s Church, London, under the direction of a

[004-083] drunken cripple, who was a convicted thief, and an incorrigible vagrant.

[004-084] Tell Josiah Bounderby of Coketown, of your district schools and your

[004-085] model schools, and your [[education|training]] schools, and your whole kettle-of-fish

[004-086] of schools; and Josiah Bounderby of Coketown, tells you plainly, all

[004-087] right, all correct—he hadn’t such advantages—but let us have hard-headed,

[004-088] solid-fisted people—the education that made him won’t do for everybody,

[004-089] he knows well—such and such his education was, however, and you may force

[004-090] him to swallow boiling fat, but you shall never force him to suppress the

[004-091] facts of his life.’

[004-092] Being heated when he arrived at this climax, Josiah Bounderby of Coketown

[004-093] stopped. He stopped just as his eminently practical friend, still

[004-094] accompanied by the two young culprits, entered the room. His eminently

[004-095] practical friend, on seeing him, stopped also, and gave [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]] a

[004-096] reproachful look that plainly said, ‘Behold your Bounderby!’

[004-097] ‘Well!’ blustered Mr. Bounderby, ‘what’s the matter? What is young

[004-098] Thomas in the dumps about?’

[004-099] He spoke of young Thomas, but he looked at Louisa.

[004-100] ‘We were peeping at [[slearys-circus|the circus]],’ muttered Louisa, haughtily, without

[004-101] lifting up her eyes, ‘and father caught us.’

[004-102] ‘And, Mrs. Gradgrind,’ said her husband in a lofty manner, ‘I should as

[004-103] soon have expected to find my children reading poetry.’

[004-104] ‘Dear me,’ whimpered Mrs. Gradgrind. ‘How can you, Louisa and Thomas! I

[004-105] wonder at you. I declare you’re enough to make one regret ever having

[004-106] had a [[family]] at all. I have a great mind to say I wish I hadn’t. _Then_

[004-107] what would you have done, I should like to know?’

[004-108] Mr. Gradgrind did not seem favourably impressed by these cogent remarks.

[004-109] He frowned impatiently.

[004-110] ‘As if, with my head in its present throbbing state, you couldn’t go and

[004-111] look at the shells and minerals and things provided for you, instead of

[004-112] circuses!’ said Mrs. Gradgrind. ‘You know, as well as I do, no young

[004-113] people have [[performance|circus]] masters, or keep circuses in cabinets, or attend

[004-114] lectures about circuses. What can you possibly want to know of circuses

[004-115] then? I am sure you have enough to do, if that’s what you want. With my

[004-116] head in its present state, I couldn’t remember the mere names of half the

[004-117] facts you have got to attend to.’

[004-118] ‘That’s the reason!’ pouted Louisa.

[004-119] ‘Don’t tell me that’s the reason, because it can’t be nothing of the

[004-120] sort,’ said Mrs. Gradgrind. ‘Go and be somethingological directly.’

[004-121] Mrs. Gradgrind was not a scientific character, and usually dismissed her

[004-122] children to their studies with this general injunction to choose their

[004-123] pursuit.

[004-124] In [[truth]], Mrs. Gradgrind’s stock of facts in general was woefully

[004-125] defective; but Mr. Gradgrind in raising her to her high matrimonial

[004-126] position, had been influenced by two reasons. Firstly, she was most

[004-127] satisfactory as a question of figures; and, secondly, she had ‘no

[004-128] nonsense’ about her. By nonsense he meant [[fancy]]; and truly it is

[004-129] probable she was as free from any alloy of that [[nature]], as any human

[004-130] being not arrived at the perfection of an absolute idiot, ever was.

[004-131] The simple circumstance of being left alone with her husband and Mr.

[004-132] Bounderby, was sufficient to stun this admirable lady again without

[004-133] collision between herself and any other fact. So, she once more died

[004-134] away, and nobody minded her.

[004-135] ‘Bounderby,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, drawing a chair to the fireside, ‘you

[004-136] are always so interested in my young people—particularly in Louisa—that I

[004-137] make no apology for saying to you, I am very much vexed by this

[004-138] discovery. I have systematically devoted myself (as you know) to the

[004-139] education of the reason of my family. The reason is (as you know) the

[004-140] only faculty to which education should be addressed. ‘And yet,

[004-141] Bounderby, it would appear from this unexpected circumstance of to-day,

[004-142] though in itself a trifling one, as if something had crept into Thomas’s

[004-143] and Louisa’s minds which is—or rather, which is not—I don’t know that I

[004-144] can express myself better than by saying—which has never been intended to

[004-145] be developed, and in which their reason has no part.’

[004-146] ‘There certainly is no reason in looking with interest at a parcel of

[004-147] vagabonds,’ returned Bounderby. ‘When I was a vagabond myself, nobody

[004-148] looked with any interest at _me_; I know that.’

[004-149] ‘Then comes the question; said the eminently practical father, with his

[004-150] eyes on the fire, ‘in what has this vulgar curiosity its rise?’

[004-151] ‘I’ll tell you in what. In idle [[imagination]].’

[004-152] ‘I hope not,’ said the eminently practical; ‘I confess, however, that the

[004-153] misgiving _has_ crossed me on my way home.’

[004-154] ‘In idle imagination, Gradgrind,’ repeated Bounderby. ‘A very bad thing

[004-155] for anybody, but a cursed bad thing for a girl like Louisa. I should ask

[004-156] Mrs. Gradgrind’s pardon for strong expressions, but that she knows very

[004-157] well I am not a refined character. Whoever expects refinement in _me_

[004-158] will be disappointed. I hadn’t a refined bringing up.’

[004-159] ‘Whether,’ said Gradgrind, pondering with his hands in his pockets, and

[004-160] his cavernous eyes on the fire, ‘whether any instructor or servant can

[004-161] have suggested anything? Whether Louisa or Thomas can have been reading

[004-162] anything? Whether, in spite of all precautions, any idle story-book can

[004-163] have got into the house? Because, in minds that have been practically

[004-164] formed by rule and line, from the cradle upwards, this is so curious, so

[004-165] incomprehensible.’

[004-166] ‘Stop a bit!’ cried Bounderby, who all this time had been standing, as

[004-167] before, on the hearth, bursting at the very furniture of the room with

[004-168] explosive humility. ‘You have one of those strollers’ children in the

[004-169] school.’

[004-170] ‘[[sissy-jupe|Cecilia Jupe]], by name,’ said Mr. Gradgrind, with something of a stricken

[004-171] look at his friend.

[004-172] ‘Now, stop a bit!’ cried Bounderby again. ‘How did she come there?’

[004-173] ‘Why, the fact is, I saw the girl myself, for the first time, only just

[004-174] now. She specially applied here at the house to be admitted, as not

[004-175] regularly belonging to our town, and—yes, you are right, Bounderby, you

[004-176] are right.’

[004-177] ‘Now, stop a bit!’ cried Bounderby, once more. ‘Louisa saw her when she

[004-178] came?’

[004-179] ‘Louisa certainly did see her, for she mentioned the application to me.

[004-180] But Louisa saw her, I have no doubt, in Mrs. Gradgrind’s presence.’

[004-181] ‘Pray, Mrs. Gradgrind,’ said Bounderby, ‘what passed?’

[004-182] ‘Oh, my poor health!’ returned Mrs. Gradgrind. ‘The girl wanted to come

[004-183] to the school, and Mr. Gradgrind wanted girls to come to the school, and

[004-184] Louisa and Thomas both said that the girl wanted to come, and that Mr.

[004-185] Gradgrind wanted girls to come, and how was it possible to contradict

[004-186] them when such was the fact!’

[004-187] ‘Now I tell you what, Gradgrind!’ said Mr. Bounderby. ‘Turn this girl to

[004-188] the right about, and there’s an end of it.’

[004-189] ‘I am much of your opinion.’

[004-190] ‘Do it at once,’ said Bounderby, ‘has always been my motto from a child.

[004-191] When I thought I would run away from my egg-box and my grandmother, I did

[004-192] it at once. Do you the same. Do this at once!’

[004-193] ‘Are you walking?’ asked his friend. ‘I have the father’s address.

[004-194] Perhaps you would not mind walking to town with me?’

[004-195] ‘Not the least in the world,’ said Mr. Bounderby, ‘as long as you do it

[004-196] at once!’

[004-197] So, Mr. Bounderby threw on his hat—he always threw it on, as expressing a

[004-198] man who had been far too busily employed in making himself, to acquire

[004-199] any fashion of wearing his hat—and with his hands in his pockets,

[004-200] sauntered out into the hall. ‘I never wear gloves,’ it was his custom to

[004-201] say. ‘I didn’t climb up the ladder in _them_.—Shouldn’t be so high up,

[004-202] if I had.’

[004-203] Being left to saunter in the hall a minute or two while Mr. Gradgrind

[004-204] went up-stairs for the address, he opened the door of the children’s

[004-205] study and looked into that serene floor-clothed apartment, which,

[004-206] notwithstanding its book-cases and its cabinets and its variety of

[004-207] learned and philosophical appliances, had much of the genial aspect of a

[004-208] room devoted to hair-cutting. Louisa languidly leaned upon the window

[004-209] looking out, without looking at anything, while young Thomas stood

[004-210] sniffing revengefully at the fire. Adam Smith and Malthus, two younger

[004-211] Gradgrinds, were out at lecture in custody; and little Jane, after

[004-212] manufacturing a good deal of moist pipe-clay on her face with

[004-213] slate-pencil and tears, had fallen asleep over vulgar fractions.

[004-214] ‘It’s all right now, Louisa: it’s all right, young Thomas,’ said Mr.

[004-215] Bounderby; ‘you won’t do so any more. I’ll answer for it’s being all

[004-216] over with father. Well, Louisa, that’s worth a kiss, isn’t it?’

[004-217] ‘You can take one, Mr. Bounderby,’ returned Louisa, when she had coldly

[004-218] paused, and slowly walked across the room, and ungraciously raised her

[004-219] cheek towards him, with her face turned away.

[004-220] ‘Always my pet; ain’t you, Louisa?’ said Mr. Bounderby. ‘Good-bye,

[004-221] Louisa!’

[004-222] He went his way, but she stood on the same spot, rubbing the cheek he had

[004-223] kissed, with her handkerchief, until it was burning red. She was still

[004-224] doing this, five minutes afterwards.

[004-225] ‘What are you about, Loo?’ her brother sulkily remonstrated. ‘You’ll rub

[004-226] a hole in your face.’

[004-227] ‘You may cut the piece out with your penknife if you like, [[tom-gradgrind|Tom]]. I

[004-228] wouldn’t cry!’
