# Mrs. Sparsit

Mrs. Sparsit is the comic-grotesque antagonist of *Hard Times* — Josiah Bounderby's housekeeper, a decayed aristocrat who builds the novel's most famous metaphor (the Staircase) in a desperate attempt to see Louisa Bounderby fall into disgrace, only to be humiliated, dismissed, and reduced to squabbling with her horrible relative Lady Scadgers over a "mean little airless lodging."

## Identity

- **Full Name**: Mrs. Sparsit (her Christian name is never given)
- **Aliases / Epithets**: "the niece of Lady Scadgers" (PN: 031-014), "the Bank Fairy" (PN: 022-007), "Mother Sparsit" (used ironically by Tom Gradgrind, PN: 025-127)
- **Role**: Comic-grotesque antagonist — a figure of satiric mockery whose malice is undercut by her absurdity
- **Occupation**: Housekeeper at Bounderby's establishment, on an annual stipend
- **Family**: Lady Scadgers (great aunt, a wealthy invalid with a "mysterious leg"); late Mr. Sparsit (deceased husband, "a Powler" by his mother's side)
- **First Appearance**: Book I Chapter 7, "MRS. SPARSIT" — formally introduced as Bounderby's housekeeper, a "prominent figure in attendance on Mr. Bounderby's car" (PN: 007-001–012)
- **Last Appearance**: Book III Chapter 9, "FINAL" — the narrator's closing vision shows her "fighting out a daily fight at the points of all the weapons in the female armoury, with the grudging, smarting, peevish, tormenting Lady Scadgers" (PN: 037-165–170)

## Characterisation

Dickens constructs Mrs. Sparsit out of classical imagery turned to mock-heroic purpose. She is a "classical ruin" — every reference to Rome, Corinthian columns, and Coriolanus works ironically to suggest a dignity she has long since forfeited to service, malice, and self-delusion. She is, as the narrator notes, an "interesting spectacle" as a ruin — "but considered in any other light, the amount of damage she had by that time sustained was excessive" (PN: 031-018–022).

### Physical Description

Mrs. Sparsit is introduced with "the Coriolanian style of nose and the dense black eyebrows which had captivated Sparsit" (PN: 007-017–019). Her features are consistently described in terms of Roman antiquity — "Roman nose," "classical features," "Coriolanian" eyebrows — but the classical frame is always undercut. She is elderly, a woman who has "seen different days," and her body is the physical metaphor for her social position: a relic of a dying order, imposing from a distance but crumbling on inspection.

Her eyes are "dark" and sharp — in the staircase chapter, "those classical eyes wide awake, with no touch of pity" (PN: 025-009–011). When she darts through the wood to spy on Louisa and Harthouse, "her dark eyes and her hook nose warily in advance of her" (PN: 027-073–075) cast her as a predatory bird — a hawk or crow — scavenging for scandal.

### Speech and Mannerisms

Mrs. Sparsit's speech is defined by her peculiar mode of addressing Bounderby as "sir" — a word she pronounces "with a dignity serenely mournful" (PN: 007-053–055), infusing it with a condescension that reminds him, and everyone present, that she was once above him. Her catchphrase, "I am the servant of circumstances, sir" (PN: 024-041), is her creed in miniature: class pride disguised as humility, self-assertion through self-abasement.

She has a repertoire of physical gestures that Dickens deploys for comic effect: the shake of her head, the compression of her mouth, the knitting of her "Coriolanian eyebrows" — each a miniature performance of aristocratic disapproval. When she learns of Bounderby's marriage to Louisa, her response is a masterpiece of passive aggression: "I hope you may be happy, sir. Oh, indeed I hope you may be happy, sir!" — delivered with "such great condescension as well as with such great compassion for him" that Bounderby is "far more disconcerted than if she had thrown her workbox at the mirror" (PN: 016-021–030).

## Role in the Narrative

### Book I: Sowing

Mrs. Sparsit is introduced in the chapter that bears her name (I.7), where Dickens provides her full backstory: she was a lady of high connexions — a "Powler" by marriage and niece to Lady Scadgers — who fell into her present position through the "imprudence" of marrying a man who "fifteen years older than herself, had died and ruined her" (PN: 007-001–045). Her relationship with Bounderby is mapped through inverted feudal imagery: "If Bounderby had been a Conqueror, and Mrs. Sparsit a captive Princess whom he took about as a feature in his state-processions, he could not have made a greater flourish with her than he habitually did" (PN: 007-049–051). Each uses the other: Bounderby displays her as proof of his conquest over the old order; she condescends to him as a temporary patron, fully convinced of her own superiority.

Her resentment of Louisa crystallises the moment she learns of the marriage (I.16). Her "I hope you may be happy, sir" (PN: 016-019–022) is the novel's definitive example of saying one thing and meaning its opposite — a sound that establishes, in Mrs. Sparsit's mind, "a right to pity him ever afterwards" (PN: 016-031–033).

### Book II: Reaping

Book II is Mrs. Sparsit's book. Installed at the Bank as the "Bank Fairy" (PN: 022-007) — a phrase whose mock-heroic form exactly captures her self-conception — she becomes Bounderby's eyes on Louisa. Her surveillance is the subject of the novel's most elaborate extended metaphor: the Staircase.

In Chapter 10 ("Mrs. Sparsit's Staircase"), Dickens describes how she "erected in her mind a mighty Staircase, with a dark pit of shame and ruin at the bottom; and down those stairs, from day to day and hour to hour, she saw Louisa coming" (PN: 026-029–031). The staircase becomes "the business of Mrs. Sparsit's life" — she watches Louisa descend "sometimes slowly, sometimes quickly, sometimes several steps at one bout, sometimes stopping, never turning back" (PN: 026-033–036). She keeps "her black eyes wide open, with no touch of pity, with no touch of compunction, all absorbed in interest" (PN: 026-058–060).

The staircase is a work of imaginative genius — and it is completely wrong. This is the novel's deepest irony: Mrs. Sparsit, who serves the Gradgrind-Bounderby world of hard Facts, is herself capable of the most elaborate flight of Fancy in the entire novel. She can vividly imagine Louisa's fall — every step, every pause, every acceleration — but what she imagines is not what happens. The Fact-system has not eliminated imagination; it has merely perverted it into malice.

Her triumph comes in Chapter 11 ("Lower and Lower") when she physically tracks Louisa to the country house where Harthouse waits, creeps through the wood "like Robinson Crusoe in his ambuscade against the savages" (PN: 027-095–098), and overhears the rendezvous. "There they were yonder, by the felled tree" (PN: 027-088–089). She has caught them — or so she believes.

### Book III: Garnering

Book III delivers Mrs. Sparsit's humiliation. Having raced back to Coketown in a rainstorm to inform Bounderby of Louisa's "affair," she arrives "more dead than alive" — a "classical ruin" who is "an interesting spectacle" only in that capacity (PN: 031-018–022). Bounderby drags her to Stone Lodge, where she learns what she cannot bear: Louisa has not run away with Harthouse. Louisa has gone to her father. Nothing happened.

Bounderby turns on her instantly. "We shall be happy to hear any little apology you may think proper to offer, for going about the country at express pace, with no other luggage than a Cock-and-a-Bull, ma'am!" (PN: 031-033–035). Mrs. Sparsit can only take "refuge in tears" (PN: 031-040). She is packed off home with instructions to soak her feet in hot water — the grand lady, reduced to a medicated footbath.

Her final dismissal occurs in the last chapter. Bounderby, unable to tolerate her presence now that she has served her purpose, suggests she "try" living with Lady Scadgers (PN: 037-163–165). Mrs. Sparsit, with "her Roman features like a medal struck to commemorate her scorn of Mr. Bounderby" (PN: 037-168–170), delivers her final judgment — "Nothing that a Noodle does, can awaken surprise or indignation; the proceedings of a Noodle can only inspire contempt" (PN: 037-174–176) — and "ascended the staircase." The word is deliberate: she has spent two books watching Louisa on an imaginary staircase; now she mounts a real one, into a future of "grudging, smarting, peevish, tormenting" combat with Lady Scadgers (PN: 037-178–181).

## Thematic Significance

Mrs. Sparsit is the novel's most sustained exploration of the intersection between class and moral squalor. She combines aristocratic pretension — the Powler connection, the Lady Scadgers lineage, the classical nose — with genuine moral degradation: spying, rejoicing in others' downfall, living as a parasite on an industrial fortune she despises. She is, in effect, what happens when class pride encounters economic dependence — a person who must serve those she considers beneath her, and who copes by converting her service into condescension.

Her staircase metaphor is the novel's most important statement about the nature of imagination itself. Dickens has spent the entire novel arguing that the Fact-system destroys the capacity for Fancy — but Mrs. Sparsit, who operates entirely within the Gradgrind-Bounderby world, is the most imaginative figure in the book. She creates an entire narrative architecture, a "mighty Staircase" that exists only in her mind, and populates it with a Louisa who descends inexorably toward ruin. The problem is not that Mrs. Sparsit lacks imagination; it is that her imagination serves cruelty. She wants Louisa to fall so badly that she invents the fall — and in doing so, she reveals that the greatest danger of the Fact-system is not that it destroys Fancy but that it perverts it, channelling the human capacity for story-making into the service of malice.

She also functions as Bounderby's mirror. Both are self-mythologisers: Bounderby invents a gutter-birth to justify his wealth; Mrs. Sparsit clings to a Powler connection to deny her dependence. Both are parasites — he on the labour of the Hands, she on his household budget — and both despise what they feed on. Bounderby's exposure (his mother reveals he was never abandoned) parallels Mrs. Sparsit's (the staircase leads nowhere, Louisa did not fall), and both are dismissed from the novel's moral universe in the final chapter: he dies of apoplexy in the street; she fights with Lady Scadgers over a diminished pension.

Contrasted with Louisa Gradgrind (whose inner life Mrs. Sparsit cannot see, only project upon), Rachael (whose genuine female virtue exposes Mrs. Sparsit's performative respectability), and Sissy Jupe (whose imagination serves love, where Mrs. Sparsit's serves malice).

## Key Relationships

- **[[Josiah-Bounderby]]**: Her employer and patron — and her mirror. Both are self-mythologisers whose public identities are built on lies (his gutter-birth, her Powler dignity). Their relationship is mutual exploitation: Bounderby uses her as a trophy of class conquest; she uses him for a salary while despising him. When she fails him, he dismisses her without a second thought. (PN: 007-049–051, 016-019–033, 037-165–176)
- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: The object of her surveillance and the subject of her staircase fantasy. Mrs. Sparsit resents Louisa for marrying Bounderby (a match Mrs. Sparsit may have hoped for herself) and channels that resentment into an obsessive campaign of moral espionage. She watches Louisa not to protect Bounderby but to enjoy Louisa's destruction — and when that destruction fails to materialise, she is herself destroyed. (PN: 026-029–036, 027-073–098, 031-018–040)
- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: The gentleman whose arrival at the Bank — and whose immediate interest in Louisa — sets Mrs. Sparsit's staircase in motion. She is charmed by his "gallantry at ease" (PN: 024-064) and, after extracting the details of his family and intentions, becomes his unwitting ally in the surveillance of Louisa. Her interview with him (II.7) is a masterpiece of comic social negotiation between a decayed aristocrat and a jaded gentleman. (PN: 023-175–220)
- **[[Lady-Scadgers]]**: Mrs. Sparsit's great aunt, an immensely fat invalid with a "mysterious leg" who never leaves her bed. Lady Scadgers is the anchor of Mrs. Sparsit's class pretensions and, in the novel's closing vision, her nemesis — the "grudging, smarting, peevish, tormenting" relation with whom Mrs. Sparsit must now live, in a "mean little airless lodging, a mere closet for one, a mere crib for two" (PN: 037-178–181). Her fate is to become what she always was: a dependent relative, tolerated for her connections, despised for her poverty.
- **[[Tom-Gradgrind]]**: Tom is the one who coins "Mother Sparsit" and who correctly diagnoses her feeling for Louisa as "more than admiration — affection and devotion" (PN: 025-128–130). He recognises her as a fellow spy — both are watching Louisa for their own reasons — but unlike Tom, Mrs. Sparsit lacks the intelligence to conceal her surveillance or the self-awareness to recognise her own motives.

## Critical Reception

> Mrs. Sparsit is Dickens's most brilliant comic-pathetic creation — a figure who is simultaneously ridiculous (her Powler pretensions, her classical nose, her "Bank Fairy" self-conception) and genuinely sinister (her staircase, her surveillance, her hunger for Louisa's ruin). The staircase metaphor, which takes over the novel for two full chapters, is one of the great imaginative achievements of Victorian fiction — and its collapse, when Louisa does not fall, is the novel's most damning verdict on the moral imagination untethered from charity. Mrs. Sparsit is what Fancy looks like when it serves cruelty: a mighty Staircase that leads nowhere, erected by a woman who cannot see that she herself is at the bottom of the pit she has dug for another. (PN: 026-029–060, 037-163–181)
