[034-001] THE STARLIGHT

[034-002] THE Sunday was a bright Sunday in autumn, clear and cool, when early in

[034-003] the morning [[sissy-jupe|Sissy]] and [[rachael|Rachael]] met, to walk in the country.

[034-004] As [[coketown|Coketown]] cast ashes not only on its own [[reason|head]] but on the

[034-005] neighbourhood’s too—after the manner of those pious persons who do

[034-006] penance for their own sins by putting other people into sackcloth—it was

[034-007] customary for those who now and then thirsted for a draught of pure air,

[034-008] which is not absolutely the most wicked among the vanities of life, to

[034-009] get a few miles away by the railroad, and then begin their walk, or their

[034-010] lounge in the fields. Sissy and Rachael helped themselves out of the

[034-011] smoke by the usual means, and were put down at a station about midway

[034-012] between the town and [[josiah-bounderby|Mr. Bounderby]]’s retreat.

[034-013] Though the green landscape was blotted here and there with heaps of coal,

[034-014] it was green elsewhere, and there were trees to see, and there were larks

[034-015] singing (though it was Sunday), and there were pleasant scents in the

[034-016] air, and all was over-arched by a bright blue sky. In the distance one

[034-017] way, Coketown showed as a black mist; in another distance hills began to

[034-018] rise; in a third, there was a faint change in the light of the horizon

[034-019] where it shone upon the far-off sea. Under their feet, the grass was

[034-020] fresh; beautiful shadows of branches flickered upon it, and speckled it;

[034-021] hedgerows were luxuriant; everything was at peace. Engines at pits’

[034-022] mouths, and lean old horses that had worn the circle of their daily

[034-023] labour into the ground, were alike quiet; wheels had ceased for a short

[034-024] space to turn; and the great wheel of earth seemed to revolve without the

[034-025] shocks and noises of another time.

[034-026] They walked on across the fields and down the shady lanes, sometimes

[034-027] getting over a fragment of a fence so rotten that it dropped at a touch

[034-028] of the foot, sometimes passing near a wreck of bricks and beams overgrown

[034-029] with grass, marking the site of deserted works. They followed paths and

[034-030] tracks, however slight. Mounds where the grass was rank and high, and

[034-031] where brambles, dock-weed, and such-like vegetation, were confusedly

[034-032] heaped together, they always avoided; for dismal stories were told in

[034-033] that country of the old pits hidden beneath such indications.

[034-034] The sun was high when they sat down to rest. They had seen no one, near

[034-035] or distant, for a long time; and the solitude remained unbroken. ‘It is

[034-036] so still here, Rachael, and the way is so untrodden, that I think we must

[034-037] be the first who have been here all the summer.’

[034-038] As Sissy said it, her eyes were attracted by another of those rotten

[034-039] fragments of fence upon the ground. She got up to look at it. ‘And yet

[034-040] I don’t know. This has not been broken very long. The wood is quite

[034-041] fresh where it gave way. Here are footsteps too.—O Rachael!’

[034-042] She ran back, and caught her round the neck. Rachael had already started

[034-043] up.

[034-044] ‘What is the matter?’

[034-045] ‘I don’t know. There is a hat lying in the grass.’ They went forward

[034-046] together. Rachael took it up, shaking from head to foot. She broke into

[034-047] a passion of tears and lamentations: [[stephen-blackpool|Stephen Blackpool]] was written in his

[034-048] own hand on the inside.

[034-049] ‘O the poor lad, the poor lad! He has been made away with. He is lying

[034-050] murdered here!’

[034-051] ‘Is there—has the hat any blood upon it?’ Sissy faltered.

[034-052] They were afraid to look; but they did examine it, and found no mark of

[034-053] violence, inside or out. It had been lying there some days, for rain and

[034-054] dew had stained it, and the mark of its shape was on the grass where it

[034-055] had fallen. They looked fearfully about them, without moving, but could

[034-056] see nothing more. ‘Rachael,’ Sissy whispered, ‘I will go on a little by

[034-057] myself.’

[034-058] She had unclasped her hand, and was in the act of stepping forward, when

[034-059] Rachael caught her in both arms with a scream that resounded over the

[034-060] wide landscape. Before them, at their very feet, was the brink of a

[034-061] black ragged chasm hidden by the thick grass. They sprang back, and fell

[034-062] upon their knees, each hiding her face upon the other’s neck.

[034-063] ‘O, my good Lord! He’s down there! Down there!’ At first this, and her

[034-064] terrific screams, were all that could be got from Rachael, by any tears,

[034-065] by any prayers, by any representations, by any means. It was impossible

[034-066] to hush her; and it was deadly necessary to hold her, or she would have

[034-067] flung herself down the shaft.

[034-068] ‘Rachael, [[love|dear]] Rachael, good Rachael, for the love of Heaven, not these

[034-069] dreadful cries! Think of Stephen, think of Stephen, think of Stephen!’

[034-070] By an earnest repetition of this entreaty, poured out in all the agony of

[034-071] such a moment, Sissy at last brought her to be silent, and to look at her

[034-072] with a tearless face of stone.

[034-073] ‘Rachael, Stephen may be living. You wouldn’t leave him lying maimed at

[034-074] the bottom of this dreadful place, a moment, if you could bring help to

[034-075] him?’

[034-076] ‘No, no, no!’

[034-077] ‘Don’t stir from here, for his sake! Let me go and listen.’

[034-078] She shuddered to approach [[old-hell-shaft|the pit]]; but she crept towards it on her hands

[034-079] and knees, and called to him as loud as she could call. She listened,

[034-080] but no sound replied. She called again and listened; still no answering

[034-081] sound. She did this, twenty, thirty times. She took a little clod of

[034-082] earth from the broken ground where he had stumbled, and threw it in. She

[034-083] could not hear it fall.

[034-084] The wide prospect, so beautiful in its stillness but a few minutes ago,

[034-085] almost carried despair to her brave [[heart]], as she rose and looked all

[034-086] round her, seeing no help. ‘Rachael, we must lose not a moment. We must

[034-087] go in different directions, seeking aid. You shall go by the way we have

[034-088] come, and I will go forward by the path. Tell any one you see, and every

[034-089] one what has happened. Think of Stephen, think of Stephen!’

[034-090] She knew by Rachael’s face that she might trust her now. And after

[034-091] standing for a moment to see her running, wringing her hands as she ran,

[034-092] she turned and went upon her own search; she stopped at the hedge to tie

[034-093] her shawl there as a guide to the place, then threw her bonnet aside, and

[034-094] ran as she had never run before.

[034-095] Run, Sissy, run, in Heaven’s name! Don’t stop for breath. Run, run!

[034-096] Quickening herself by carrying such entreaties in her thoughts, she ran

[034-097] from field to field, and lane to lane, and place to place, as she had

[034-098] never run before; until she came to a shed by an engine-house, where two

[034-099] men lay in the shade, asleep on straw.

[034-100] First to wake them, and next to tell them, all so wild and breathless as

[034-101] she was, what had brought her there, were difficulties; but they no

[034-102] sooner understood her than their spirits were on fire like hers. One of

[034-103] the men was in a drunken slumber, but on his comrade’s shouting to him

[034-104] that a man had fallen down the Old Hell Shaft, he started out to a pool

[034-105] of dirty water, put his head in it, and came back sober.

[034-106] With these two men she ran to another half-a-mile further, and with that

[034-107] one to another, while they ran elsewhere. Then a horse was found; and

[034-108] she got another man to ride for life or death to the railroad, and send a

[034-109] message to [[louisa-gradgrind|Louisa]], which she wrote and gave him. By this time a whole

[034-110] village was up: and windlasses, ropes, poles, candles, lanterns, all

[034-111] things necessary, were fast collecting and being brought into one place,

[034-112] to be carried to the Old Hell Shaft.

[034-113] It seemed now hours and hours since she had left the lost man lying in

[034-114] the grave where he had been buried alive. She could not bear to remain

[034-115] away from it any longer—it was like deserting him—and she hurried swiftly

[034-116] back, accompanied by half-a-dozen labourers, including the drunken man

[034-117] whom the news had sobered, and who was the best man of all. When they

[034-118] came to the Old Hell Shaft, they found it as lonely as she had left it.

[034-119] The men called and listened as she had done, and examined the edge of the

[034-120] chasm, and settled how it had happened, and then sat down to wait until

[034-121] the implements they wanted should come up.

[034-122] Every sound of insects in the air, every stirring of the leaves, every

[034-123] whisper among these men, made Sissy tremble, for she thought it was a cry

[034-124] at the bottom of the pit. But the wind blew idly over it, and no sound

[034-125] arose to the surface, and they sat upon the grass, waiting and waiting.

[034-126] After they had waited some time, straggling people who had heard of the

[034-127] accident began to come up; then the real help of implements began to

[034-128] arrive. In the midst of this, Rachael returned; and with her party there

[034-129] was a surgeon, who brought some wine and medicines. But, the expectation

[034-130] among the people that the man would be found alive was very slight

[034-131] indeed.

[034-132] There being now people enough present to impede the work, the sobered man

[034-133] put himself at the head of the rest, or was put there by the general

[034-134] consent, and made a large ring round the Old Hell Shaft, and appointed

[034-135] men to keep it. Besides such volunteers as were accepted to work, only

[034-136] Sissy and Rachael were at first permitted within this ring; but, later in

[034-137] the day, when the message brought an express from Coketown, [[thomas-gradgrind|Mr. Gradgrind]]

[034-138] and Louisa, and Mr. Bounderby, and [[tom-gradgrind|the whelp]], were also there.

[034-139] The sun was four hours lower than when Sissy and Rachael had first sat

[034-140] down upon the grass, before a means of enabling two men to descend

[034-141] securely was rigged with poles and ropes. Difficulties had arisen in the

[034-142] construction of this machine, simple as it was; requisites had been found

[034-143] wanting, and messages had had to go and return. It was five o’clock in

[034-144] the afternoon of the bright autumnal Sunday, before a candle was sent

[034-145] down to try the air, while three or four rough faces stood crowded close

[034-146] together, attentively watching it: the man at the windlass lowering as

[034-147] they were told. The candle was brought up again, feebly burning, and

[034-148] then some water was cast in. Then the bucket was hooked on; and the

[034-149] sobered man and another got in with lights, giving the word ‘Lower away!’

[034-150] As the rope went out, tight and strained, and the windlass creaked, there

[034-151] was not a breath among the one or two hundred men and women looking on,

[034-152] that came as it was wont to come. The signal was given and the windlass

[034-153] stopped, with abundant rope to spare. Apparently so long an interval

[034-154] ensued with the men at the windlass standing idle, that some women

[034-155] shrieked that another accident had happened! But the surgeon who held

[034-156] the watch, declared five minutes not to have elapsed yet, and sternly

[034-157] admonished them to keep silence. He had not well done speaking, when the

[034-158] windlass was reversed and worked again. Practised eyes knew that it did

[034-159] not go as heavily as it would if both workmen had been coming up, and

[034-160] that only one was returning.

[034-161] The rope came in tight and strained; and ring after ring was coiled upon

[034-162] the barrel of the windlass, and all eyes were fastened on the pit. The

[034-163] sobered man was brought up and leaped out briskly on the grass. There

[034-164] was an universal cry of ‘Alive or dead?’ and then a deep, profound hush.

[034-165] When he said ‘Alive!’ a great shout arose and many eyes had tears in

[034-166] them.

[034-167] ‘But he’s hurt very bad,’ he added, as soon as he could make himself

[034-168] heard again. ‘Where’s doctor? He’s hurt so very bad, sir, that we donno

[034-169] how to get him up.’

[034-170] They all consulted together, and looked anxiously at the surgeon, as he

[034-171] asked some questions, and shook his head on receiving the replies. The

[034-172] sun was setting now; and the red light in the evening sky touched every

[034-173] face there, and caused it to be distinctly seen in all its rapt suspense.

[034-174] The consultation ended in the men returning to the windlass, and the

[034-175] pitman going down again, carrying the wine and some other small matters

[034-176] with him. Then the other man came up. In the meantime, under the

[034-177] surgeon’s directions, some men brought a hurdle, on which others made a

[034-178] thick bed of spare clothes covered with loose straw, while he himself

[034-179] contrived some bandages and slings from shawls and handkerchiefs. As

[034-180] these were made, they were hung upon an arm of the pitman who had last

[034-181] come up, with instructions how to use them: and as he stood, shown by the

[034-182] light he carried, leaning his powerful loose hand upon one of the poles,

[034-183] and sometimes glancing down the pit, and sometimes glancing round upon

[034-184] the people, he was not the least conspicuous figure in the scene. It was

[034-185] dark now, and torches were kindled.

[034-186] It appeared from the little this man said to those about him, which was

[034-187] quickly repeated all over the circle, that the lost man had fallen upon a

[034-188] mass of crumbled rubbish with which the pit was half choked up, and that

[034-189] his fall had been further broken by some jagged earth at the side. He

[034-190] lay upon his back with one arm doubled under him, and according to his

[034-191] own belief had hardly stirred since he fell, except that he had moved his

[034-192] free hand to a side pocket, in which he remembered to have some bread and

[034-193] meat (of which he had swallowed crumbs), and had likewise scooped up a

[034-194] little water in it now and then. He had come straight away from his

[034-195] work, on being written to, and had walked the whole journey; and was on

[034-196] his way to Mr. Bounderby’s country house after dark, when he fell. He

[034-197] was crossing that dangerous country at such a dangerous time, because he

[034-198] was innocent of what was laid to his charge, and couldn’t rest from

[034-199] coming the nearest way to deliver himself up. The Old Hell Shaft, the

[034-200] pitman said, with a curse upon it, was worthy of its bad name to the

[034-201] last; for though Stephen could speak now, he believed it would soon be

[034-202] found to have mangled the life out of him.

[034-203] When all was ready, this man, still taking his last hurried charges from

[034-204] his comrades and the surgeon after the windlass had begun to lower him,

[034-205] disappeared into the pit. The rope went out as before, the signal was

[034-206] made as before, and the windlass stopped. No man removed his hand from

[034-207] it now. Every one waited with his grasp set, and his body bent down to

[034-208] the work, ready to reverse and wind in. At length the signal was given,

[034-209] and all the ring leaned forward.

[034-210] For, now, the rope came in, tightened and strained to its utmost as it

[034-211] appeared, and the men turned heavily, and the windlass complained. It

[034-212] was scarcely endurable to look at the rope, and think of its giving way.

[034-213] But, ring after ring was coiled upon the barrel of the windlass safely,

[034-214] and the connecting chains appeared, and finally the bucket with the two

[034-215] men holding on at the sides—a sight to make the head swim, and oppress

[034-216] the heart—and tenderly supporting between them, slung and tied within,

[034-217] the figure of a poor, crushed, human creature.

[034-218] A low murmur of pity went round the throng, and the women wept aloud, as

[034-219] this form, almost without form, was moved very slowly from its iron

[034-220] deliverance, and laid upon the bed of straw. At first, none but the

[034-221] surgeon went close to it. He did what he could in its adjustment on the

[034-222] couch, but the best that he could do was to cover it. That gently done,

[034-223] he called to him Rachael and Sissy. And at that time the pale, worn,

[034-224] patient face was seen looking up at the sky, with the broken right hand

[034-225] lying bare on the outside of the covering garments, as if waiting to be

[034-226] taken by another hand.

[034-227] They gave him drink, moistened his face with water, and administered some

[034-228] drops of cordial and wine. Though he lay quite motionless looking up at

[034-229] the sky, he smiled and said, ‘Rachael.’ She stooped down on the grass at

[034-230] his side, and bent over him until her eyes were between his and the sky,

[034-231] for he could not so much as turn them to look at her.

[034-232] ‘Rachael, my dear.’

[034-233] She took his hand. He smiled again and said, ‘Don’t let ’t go.’

[034-234] ‘Thou’rt in great pain, my own dear Stephen?’

[034-235] ‘I ha’ been, but not now. I ha’ been—dreadful, and dree, and long, my

[034-236] dear—but ’tis ower now. Ah, Rachael, aw a muddle! Fro’ first to last, a

[034-237] muddle!’

[034-238] The spectre of his old look seemed to pass as he said the word.

[034-239] ‘I ha’ fell into th’ pit, my dear, as have cost wi’in the knowledge o’

[034-240] old fok now livin, hundreds and hundreds o’ men’s lives—fathers, sons,

[034-241] brothers, dear to thousands an’ thousands, an’ keeping ’em fro’ want and

[034-242] hunger. I ha’ fell into a pit that ha’ been wi’ th’ Firedamp crueller

[034-243] than battle. I ha’ read on ’t in the public petition, as onny one may

[034-244] read, fro’ the men that works in pits, in which they ha’ pray’n and

[034-245] pray’n the lawmakers for Christ’s sake not to let their work be murder to

[034-246] ’em, but to spare ’em for th’ wives and [[childhood|children]] that they loves as well

[034-247] as gentlefok loves theirs. When it were in work, it killed wi’out need;

[034-248] when ’tis let alone, it kills wi’out need. See how we die an’ no need,

[034-249] one way an’ another—in a muddle—every day!’

[034-250] He faintly said it, without any anger against any one. Merely as the

[034-251] [[truth]].

[034-252] ‘Thy little sister, Rachael, thou hast not forgot her. Thou’rt not like

[034-253] to forget her now, and me so nigh her. Thou know’st—poor, patient,

[034-254] suff’rin, dear—how thou didst work for her, seet’n all day long in her

[034-255] little chair at thy winder, and how she died, young and misshapen, awlung

[034-256] o’ sickly air as had’n no need to be, an’ awlung o’ working people’s

[034-257] miserable homes. A muddle! Aw a muddle!’

[034-258] Louisa approached him; but he could not see her, lying with his face

[034-259] turned up to the night sky.

[034-260] ‘If aw th’ things that tooches us, my dear, was not so muddled, I

[034-261] should’n ha’ had’n need to coom heer. If we was not in a muddle among

[034-262] ourseln, I should’n ha’ been, by my own fellow weavers and workin’

[034-263] brothers, so mistook. If Mr. Bounderby had ever know’d me right—if he’d

[034-264] ever know’d me at aw—he would’n ha’ took’n offence wi’ me. He would’n

[034-265] ha’ suspect’n me. But look up yonder, Rachael! Look aboove!’

[034-266] Following his eyes, she saw that he was gazing at a star.

[034-267] [Picture: Stephen Blackpool recovered from the Old Hell Shaft]

[034-268] ‘It ha’ shined upon me,’ he said reverently, ‘in my pain and trouble down

[034-269] below. It ha’ shined into my mind. I ha’ look’n at ’t and thowt o’

[034-270] thee, Rachael, till the muddle in my mind have cleared awa, above a bit,

[034-271] I hope. If soom ha’ been wantin’ in unnerstan’in me better, I, too, ha’

[034-272] been wantin’ in unnerstan’in them better. When I got thy letter, I

[034-273] easily believen that what the yoong ledy sen and done to me, and what her

[034-274] brother sen and done to me, was one, and that there were a wicked plot

[034-275] betwixt ’em. When I fell, I were in anger wi’ her, an’ hurryin on t’ be

[034-276] as onjust t’ her as oothers was t’ me. But in our judgments, like as in

[034-277] our doins, we mun bear and forbear. In my pain an’ trouble, lookin up

[034-278] yonder,—wi’ it shinin on me—I ha’ seen more clear, and ha’ made it my

[034-279] dyin prayer that aw th’ world may on’y coom toogether more, an’ get a

[034-280] better unnerstan’in o’ one another, than when I were in ’t my own weak

[034-281] seln.’

[034-282] Louisa hearing what he said, bent over him on the opposite side to

[034-283] Rachael, so that he could see her.

[034-284] ‘You ha’ heard?’ he said, after a few moments’ silence. ‘I ha’ not

[034-285] forgot you, ledy.’

[034-286] ‘Yes, Stephen, I have heard you. And your prayer is mine.’

[034-287] ‘You ha’ a father. Will yo tak’ a message to him?’

[034-288] ‘He is here,’ said Louisa, with dread. ‘Shall I bring him to you?’

[034-289] ‘If yo please.’

[034-290] Louisa returned with her father. Standing hand-in-hand, they both looked

[034-291] down upon the solemn countenance.

[034-292] ‘Sir, yo will clear me an’ mak my name good wi’ aw men. This I leave to

[034-293] yo.’

[034-294] Mr. [[mrs-gradgrind|Gradgrind]] was troubled and asked how?

[034-295] ‘Sir,’ was the reply: ‘yor son will tell yo how. Ask him. I mak no

[034-296] charges: I leave none ahint me: not a single word. I ha’ seen an’ spok’n

[034-297] wi’ yor son, one night. I ask no more o’ yo than that yo clear me—an’ I

[034-298] trust to yo to do ’t.’

[034-299] The bearers being now ready to carry him away, and the surgeon being

[034-300] anxious for his removal, those who had torches or lanterns, prepared to

[034-301] go in front of the litter. Before it was raised, and while they were

[034-302] arranging how to go, he said to Rachael, looking upward at the star:

[034-303] ‘Often as I coom to myseln, and found it shinin’ on me down there in my

[034-304] trouble, I thowt it were the star as guided to Our Saviour’s home. I

[034-305] awmust think it be the very star!’

[034-306] They lifted him up, and he was overjoyed to find that they were about to

[034-307] take him in the direction whither the star seemed to him to lead.

[034-308] ‘Rachael, beloved lass! Don’t let go my hand. We may walk toogether

[034-309] t’night, my dear!’

[034-310] ‘I will hold thy hand, and keep beside thee, Stephen, all the way.’

[034-311] ‘Bless thee! Will soombody be pleased to coover my face!’

[034-312] They carried him very gently along the fields, and down the lanes, and

[034-313] over the wide landscape; Rachael always holding the hand in hers. Very

[034-314] few whispers broke the mournful silence. It was soon a funeral

[034-315] procession. The star had shown him where to find the God of the poor;

[034-316] and through [[humility]], and sorrow, and forgiveness, he had gone to his

[034-317] Redeemer’s rest.
