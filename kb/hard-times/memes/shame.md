# Shame

In *Hard Times*, shame is not merely one emotion among others — it is the novel's principal moral diagnostic. The capacity to feel shame signals moral aliveness; its absence signals moral death. Dickens structures his moral universe around this distinction, placing every character on a spectrum from healthy shame (Sissy's reluctance at the catechism, Stephen's self-respect) to pathological shamelessness (Bounderby's self-mythologising, Slackbridge's demagoguery) to the peculiar tragedy of Louisa, who was educated *out* of shame — taught that feelings are irrelevant — so that when she should feel shame at her near-seduction by Harthouse, she feels only emptiness.

## Definition

Shame in *Hard Times* operates on two interlocking levels. On one level, it is a social sanction: the public exposure of fault, the disgrace that attaches to a name associated with misconduct. This is the shame Stephen Blackpool's drunken wife represents — a "shameful figure heavy on the bed" (PN: 012-118), a visible, embodied disgrace that cannot be hidden. On a deeper level, shame is the internalised moral faculty that registers the gap between one's actions and one's principles — what we might call *conscience*. The novel's argument is that the second, internalised shame is the necessary condition of moral life, and that Coketown's Utilitarian culture systematically destroys it by teaching that only Facts matter and feelings are irrelevant. (PN: 028-135)

## In *Hard Times*

### Dickens's Treatment

Dickens presents shame as a faculty that must be *educated* — and can be mis-educated. The Gradgrind system does not teach children to be shameless; rather, by denying the legitimacy of all feeling, it leaves them without the vocabulary or the capacity to register moral failure. Louisa's confession to her father is the novel's most devastating articulation of this condition: "I do not know that I am ashamed, I do not know that I am degraded" (PN: 028-135). The tragedy is not that Louisa has done something shameful and cannot admit it, but that she has been so thoroughly stripped of emotional education that she cannot identify shame when she experiences it. She has the sensation without the concept — anomie without the framework to name it.

The novel counterpoints this educated shamelessness with two healthier forms of shame. Sissy Jupe's "I am almost ashamed" (PN: 009-065) at her inability to answer the Fact-catechism is *appropriate* shame — the recognition that one has fallen short of a legitimate standard. Stephen Blackpool's refusal to be "ashamed and fearful" (PN: 011-115) of his integrity represents the flip side: the *refusal* of false shame, the insistence that integrity needs no apology. Between them, Sissy and Stephen map the proper boundaries of shame — what one should and should not be ashamed of.

The novel's villains are distinguished by their incapacity for shame. Bounderby's entire identity is a performance of shameless self-celebration; Slackbridge's rhetoric ("shame and disgrace" (PN: 020-219), "this stain and shame upon the God-like" (PN: 032-060)) deploys the language of shame without any corresponding moral substance; James Harthouse treats shame as a social inconvenience he is too sophisticated to feel. Mrs. Sparsit, the most complex case, constructs an entire metaphorical architecture of shame — her "Staircase, with a dark pit of shame and ruin at the bottom" (PN: 026-029) — and then attempts to push Louisa down it, weaponising shame as an instrument of social control.

### Key Passages

> "I do not know that I am ashamed, I do not know that I am degraded" (PN: 028-135)

Louisa's confession to her father is the novel's most concentrated statement on shame. The double negative — "I do not know that I am" — performs the condition it describes: the inability to affirm or deny shame because the very capacity for moral self-assessment has been erased by her education. This is not shamelessness but something worse: the absence of the faculty by which shamelessness could be recognised.

> "Staircase, with a dark pit of shame and ruin at the bottom" (PN: 026-029)

Mrs. Sparsit's staircase is the novel's most elaborate metaphorical construction — an architecture of shame in which Louisa is imagined descending, step by step, from respectability into disgrace. The staircase literalises shame as a spatial and social trajectory: to fall is to be shamed, and to be shamed is to fall. The metaphor is Mrs. Sparsit's, but its power derives from the culture it describes — a Coketown in which shame is deployed as a weapon of class and gender policing.

> "I am almost ashamed" (PN: 009-065)

Sissy Jupe's quiet admission, offered "with reluctance," is the novel's model of healthy shame. She is ashamed not of who she *is* but of failing to meet a demand — the catechism of Facts — that the novel has already shown to be absurd. Her shame is proportionate, self-aware, and, crucially, *felt*: it is a sign that her moral faculties are intact, undamaged by a system designed to extinguish them.

> "made him ashamed of himself" (PN: 030-305)

The moment of Tom Gradgrind's moral reformation — however partial and belated — is marked by shame. Rachael's testimony, which reveals the truth about Stephen's death, "presented something in which he was concerned that shamed him" (PN: 030-143). Shame is the mechanism of moral recognition: to be ashamed is to see oneself as one has been, and to begin the work of becoming otherwise.

## Historical Context

Shame in Victorian England was undergoing a significant transformation. In the earlier 19th century, shame operated primarily as an external, social sanction — the public exposure of fault, the loss of reputation, the "disgrace" that attached to a name. Evangelical Christianity, with its emphasis on conscience and the internalised scrutiny of the self, worked to deepen this model: shame was not merely what others thought of you, but what you knew about yourself in the sight of God.

The Utilitarian movement complicated this picture further. Bentham's "Panopticon" — the model prison in which inmates would be perpetually visible to an unseen inspector — theorised shame as a mechanism of social control: the internalisation of surveillance. But if the Utilitarian model used shame to regulate behaviour, it also — in Dickens's view — undermined the very conditions of moral selfhood. A system that reduces all value to measurable utility cannot distinguish between the shame one *should* feel (remorse for cruelty) and the shame one *should not* (embarrassment at poverty or ignorance). Coketown teaches its children to be ashamed of imagination, of feeling, of Fancy — and then discovers, too late, that it has produced adults incapable of being ashamed of anything that matters.

The novel's treatment of shame also reflects contemporary debates about women's honour and the sexual double standard. Mrs. Sparsit's staircase is a gendered metaphor: the "pit of shame and ruin" is a specifically female destination, and the descent she imagines for Louisa is the narrative of the fallen woman — a narrative that was a staple of Victorian moral discourse and a persistent target of Dickens's critique.

## Embodied By

- **[[Louisa-Gradgrind]]**: The novel's most complex study of shame's absence. Educated to suppress all feeling, Louisa reaches adulthood unable to identify shame when she experiences it. Her confession — "I do not know that I am ashamed" — is the novel's indictment of an education that treats emotional life as irrelevant. She is not shameless; she is shame-illiterate, trained out of the moral vocabulary she needs.
- **[[Stephen-Blackpool]]**: The novel's model of proper shame-resistance. Ostracised by his fellow workers for refusing to join the union, Stephen is "not ashamed and fearful" (PN: 011-115) of his integrity. He refuses to be shamed for what is not shameful, while remaining acutely conscious of what *is* — his blighted marriage to a "shame and disgrace" (PN: 009-205) of a wife. Stephen's tragedy is that he can feel shame but has no power to remedy what causes it.
- **[[James-Harthouse]]**: The cynical gentleman who has perfected the art of shamelessness. Harthouse treats shame as a provincial inconvenience — something Coketown worries about and he does not. His attempted seduction of Louisa operates by neutralising shame as a category: he presents their emotional entanglement as natural, inevitable, and therefore beyond moral judgment. He is the novel's demonstration of what shamelessness looks like when it is backed by class confidence.
- **[[Mrs-Sparsit]]**: The novel's great theorist of shame. Her staircase — a "dark pit of shame and ruin at the bottom" — is the most elaborate metaphor the novel offers for shame as social architecture. Mrs. Sparsit does not merely observe shame; she *constructs* it, imagines it, and attempts to weaponise it against Louisa. She is the guardian of a shame-culture she does not herself feel — policing boundaries she enforces for others.

## Contrasted With

- **[[pride]]**: Shame's dialectical opposite. The novel treats pride — when it is pride in integrity, in honest labour, in moral self-respect — as a legitimate defence against false shame. Stephen Blackpool's "proud consciousness that he was faithful to his class" is the novel's model of a pride that properly refuses shame. Bounderby's pride, by contrast, is shamelessness masquerading as self-made virtue. (PN: 021-082)
- **[[facts]]**: The Gradgrind doctrine that erases shame by erasing feeling. If only Facts matter, then shame — an emotion, not a fact — has no legitimate claim on the self. The novel argues that a Fact-saturated education produces precisely the condition Louisa diagnoses: the inability to know whether one is ashamed. (PN: 028-135)
- **[[dignity]]**: What proper shame protects and what false shame violates. Stephen Blackpool's dignity survives his circumstances because he knows what to be ashamed of and what not to be. Louisa's dignity is what Mrs. Sparsit's staircase threatens — and what Sissy Jupe's intervention preserves. Dignity in *Hard Times* is the capacity to stand upright in a moral sense, and shame is the faculty that tells you when you have lost your footing.

## Critical Perspectives

> Raymond Williams, in *The English Novel from Dickens to Lawrence* (1970), identified shame as a structuring emotion in Dickens's fiction, arguing that Dickens's childhood experience of the blacking factory — the "secret" shame of his own family's descent — gave him an intimate understanding of shame as a social and psychological force. In *Hard Times*, Williams suggests, this private shame is transmuted into a public critique: the novel shows how an industrial society produces shame in the wrong places and erases it from the right ones.

> Rosemarie Bodenheimer, in *Knowing Dickens* (2007), argues that Dickens's treatment of shame is inseparable from his treatment of knowledge. To know something about oneself — to be "ashamed of oneself" — is the beginning of moral education in Dickens's world. The Gradgrind system fails precisely because it substitutes knowing *facts* for knowing *oneself*, producing characters who can recite definitions but cannot recognise their own moral condition.

> George Levine, in *Darwin and the Novelists* (1988), reads Louisa Gradgrind's inability to feel shame in the context of mid-Victorian debates about determinism and free will. If character is determined by environment — by education, by circumstance — then moral responsibility (and the shame that registers its violation) becomes philosophically problematic. Louisa's tragedy, in Levine's reading, is that she has been *determined* to feel nothing, and the novel's pathos lies in her struggle to recover a moral agency she was never given.
