## Overview

Paris appears in _Deadlock_ primarily through the recollections of [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]], for whom the city represents a stage in his peripatetic life — a way station between Russia and England, marked by aesthetic experience and fleeting connections.

## In the Novel

Shatov's visit to Paris is evoked through fragmentary memories, rendered in the characteristic style of Richardson's impressionist narrative:

> "… of the events of his week in Paris. Listening to the strange inf…" [001-077]

The temporal ambiguity of the memory is foregrounded — Miriam wonders whether the visit was recent or distant, the uncertainty itself conveying the peculiar temporality of expatriate life:

> "…o a man of forty. Perhaps the Paris visit had been some time ago.…" [001-078]

Paris is also rendered as a liminal space, a stage in a journey rather than a destination:

> "…fe, England had beckoned him. Paris had been just a stage on his …" [001-079]

A later reference connects Paris to themes of personal transformation and departure:

> "…ll in my life. I was going to Paris the next day." [002-179]

For Shatov, who "knew Paris and where to stay cheaply," the city belongs to the realm of practical knowledge — a resource to be shared:

> "…en thinking of going and knew Paris and where to stay cheaply and…" [002-181]

## Significance

Paris in _Deadlock_ functions as a node in the geography of European cosmopolitanism that Shatov embodies. Unlike [[london]], which is rendered in sensory immediacy, Paris exists as memory and anticipation — a place imagined and recalled rather than inhabited in the narrative present. This temporal displacement is fitting for a city that, in early modernist literature, often figured as a site of artistic possibility hovering just beyond the horizon of English experience.
