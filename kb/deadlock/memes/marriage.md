## Overview

Marriage in "Deadlock" is the horizon against which Miriam's independence is measured. As an unmarried woman navigating a relationship with Mr. Shatov that hovers between friendship, intellectual partnership, and romantic possibility, Miriam confronts the question of whether marriage can accommodate the freedom she values above all else.

## In the Text

The novel's most sustained treatment of marriage comes through the Bergstein interview in Chapter XIII. Mrs. Bergstein's question — "What is your fiancé's attitude towards religion?" (013-022) — introduces the institutional and practical dimensions of interfaith marriage. Miriam's reflection that "the Queen, who is religious, puts love before religion, for woman" (013-024) reveals her awareness of the compromises marriage demands.

Mr. Shatov's willingness to renounce his Judaism for Miriam (013-023) is presented as a profound sacrifice — one that Miriam recognizes she cannot accept. The marriage question thus becomes entangled with questions of identity, belonging, and the ethics of sacrifice.

Earlier in the novel, Miriam reflects on the costs of relationships: "But without it, in the end, there is no shape at all?" (001-022). The question mark is crucial — she does not know whether the shapelessness of solitary life is preferable to the fixed shape that marriage would impose.

## Analysis

Richardson treats marriage not as a romantic destination but as a genuine philosophical problem. Can two individual consciousnesses coexist without one being subordinated to the other? The novel does not answer this question definitively but renders it with the full complexity of lived experience.
