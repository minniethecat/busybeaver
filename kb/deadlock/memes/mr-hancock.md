## Overview

Mr. Hancock is one of the dental practitioners at Wimpole Street where Miriam Henderson works as a secretary. His character is defined by a "clear-cut" reasonableness that Miriam both respects and finds maddeningly limited. Their conflict over the Mudie's library books — a seemingly trivial matter that opens into a larger argument about fairness and the treatment of employees — becomes one of the novel's key examinations of workplace power.

## The Mudie's Books Conflict

The central episode involving Mr. Hancock begins with what appears to be a minor domestic arrangement. Miriam, on her own initiative, has been managing the dental practice's system for borrowing books from Mudie's circulating library, privately copying out the practitioners' book lists and tracking which volumes have arrived. When Mr. Hancock complains that a book went back before he had read it and says "you might keep an eye on my Mudie books," Miriam is "simply furious" (001-002). She sees this as a matter of principle — the casual assumption that the secretary should handle personal errands beyond her professional duties.

Miriam's response is a speech, prepared during a train journey, about "the whole unfairness of the life of employees" (001-002). She tells him "that in future I would have nothing to do with his Mudie books. It was outside my sphere." The argument escalates until the practice offers her a month's salary in lieu of notice — a dismissal that Miriam experiences as "machinery" rather than personal confrontation.

## Character

Mr. Hancock represents a type that the novel treats with ambivalence: the reasonable English employer whose reasonableness conceals unexamined assumptions about class and labour. His "point of view is so clear-cut and so reasonable that it reveals all the things that hold social life together" (001-002) — but it is precisely this smooth reasonableness that Miriam finds intolerable, because it allows no space for the emotional reality of power imbalance.
