## Overview

Walking in _Deadlock_ is not merely a means of transit but a distinctive event-type — a mode of [[consciousness]] in motion. [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]] walks between [[wimpole-street]] and [[tansley-street]], through [[London]] streets and parks, alone and in company. Each walk constitutes an event in the novel's narrative fabric: the rhythm of footsteps provides the ground bass over which thought unfolds, and the changing urban landscape supplies a stream of sensory impressions that Richardson's stream-of-consciousness technique renders with extraordinary fidelity.

## Walking as Consciousness

Walking in the novel is the activity during which Miriam's mind is most freely itself:

> "…daylight remaining to walk home in, then at six, and she…" [003-063]

| Aspect of Walking | Narrative Function |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Solitary walking | Interior monologue; self-reflection |
| Walking home | Transition from professional to private self |
| Walking with another | Conversation in motion; shared perception |
| Walking at night | Heightened sensory awareness; vulnerability |

The phrase "daylight remaining to walk home in" — with the preposition "in" treating daylight as a container for the walk — reveals how walking transforms time into space. The walk home is not measured in minutes but in the quality of light, the texture of the streets, the gradual transition from the discipline of [[work]] to the relative freedom of the evening.

> "…walking sightless along crowded st…" [003-109]

The paradox of "walking sightless" — moving through a "crowded" street without seeing it — captures the peculiar condition of urban walking in which the body navigates while the mind is elsewhere. Miriam's walks are often "sightless" in this sense: the physical act of walking is automatic, while [[consciousness]] pursues its own trajectories.

## Walking Alone

The solitary walk is the novel's privileged mode of introspection:

> "…she would pass…" [003-065]

The conditional and fragmentary syntax — "she would pass" — mirrors the forward momentum of walking itself. Thought in motion is incomplete, provisional, always arriving. Richardson's sentences, in their brokenness and momentum, are walking sentences: they move forward without reaching a final destination.

> "…walked unconscious of fatigue, car…" [003-215]

The state of walking "unconscious of fatigue" is the ideal condition the novel values: a walking so absorbed that the body's demands recede from awareness. This is walking as transport in both senses — physical movement and psychological rapture.

## Walking Together

Walking with another person transforms the event into shared perception:

> "…he swept round to walk at her side, asking him how h…" [003-066]

The physical gesture — "swept round to walk at her side" — is a social act performed through bodily movement. Walking together creates a temporary alignment of pace and direction that is also, implicitly, an alignment of attention. When Miriam walks with [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]], the shared movement becomes a medium for [[conversation]] — talk that would be impossible in the fixed positions of the dining-room.

> "They walked on, to the sound of his res…" [005-022]

"Walked on" — the simple continuity of the phrase — captures the durational quality of shared walking. Conversation does not interrupt the walk; the walk carries the conversation, and the "sound" of the other's voice becomes part of the urban soundscape through which the walkers move.

## The Walk Home

The walk from Wimpole Street to Tansley Street is the novel's most recurrent journey — the daily passage between professional and domestic selves:

> "…daylight remaining to walk home in…" [003-063]

Walking home is a threshold event: it occupies the space between two identities. At Wimpole Street, Miriam is the dental secretary — efficient, subordinate, outwardly focused. At Tansley Street, she is the lodger, the observer, the private consciousness. The walk between them is where one self is shed and the other reassembled.

## Walking and London

The city is not a backdrop for walking but its condition of possibility:

> "…walking in London, she would pass…" [003-065]

"Walking in London" — the preposition is crucial. One walks "in" London, not "through" it. The city surrounds and saturates the walker; it is the medium in which the walk occurs, like water for a swimmer. [[London]]'s streets, its "moving tide" [003-109], its parks and squares — these are not scenery but the substance of the walking event itself.
