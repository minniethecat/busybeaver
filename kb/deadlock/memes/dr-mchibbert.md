## Overview

Dr. McHibbert appears in the novel as a lecturer on philosophical topics, particularly Hegel and the relationship between metaphysics and science. Miriam attends his lecture and is drawn into the intellectual atmosphere he creates — his "unobstructive voice" presenting "his utmost presentation of reasons for the tantalising hope" (001-002). He is described by another audience member as "like a marvellously intelligent bolster but with a heart and a soul. He certainly has a soul" (001-002).

## Philosophy and Impact

McHibbert's influence on Miriam is significant. His statement that "we have no right to believe a theory because we could not be happy unless it were true" (001-002) challenges her instinctive philosophical optimism. She resists this caution, reasoning that "a theory that makes you miserable can't be altogether true" and that "everything depends upon the kind of man who sets up the theory" (001-002). This tension — between rigorous philosophical honesty and the emotional need for affirming ideas — becomes central to Miriam's intellectual development in the novel.

His affirmation that "something exists" and that "metaphysic admits that" (001-002) strikes Miriam with revelatory force: "It's enough. It answers everything. Even to have seen it for a moment is enough." This moment of philosophical clarity through McHibbert's lecture becomes a touchstone for Miriam's ongoing reflections on the nature of reality and consciousness.
