## Overview

Harriett is a vivid presence in Chapter III of *Deadlock*, where Miriam visits the Tansley Street boarding house. She embodies domestic energy — cooking, bantering, caring for children — and serves as a foil to Miriam's more contemplative nature. Her speech is marked by exclamations, mockery, and warmth.

## In the Text

Harriett first appears waiting with Eve as Miriam arrives at their "enclosure" [003-006]. She is described in physical, active terms: "Harriett's active little moving form" [003-022]. In the kitchen, "the voices of Harriett and Mrs. Thimm were railing" [003-025], establishing her as central to the household's verbal culture. Her speech patterns are distinctive — mocking ("Oh of *course*" [003-028]), moaning humorously ("*Oh* dear me" [003-030]), and dramatically declaring "My *word*, it's a funeral this time" [003-040]. She is also maternal: "Lifted on to Harriett's knee she bunched her hands" [003-048], caring for Elspeth. The chapter notes how "Harriett shared her new impressions" [003-050] and how "Across Harriett's face thoughts about Eve" [003-052] played visibly. Her energy is a defining quality — "on the wings of Harriett's rush" [003-056].

## Character Perspectives

Harriett views the world through the lens of practical domesticity and affectionate mockery. She treats Miriam as an interesting outsider and engages in the boarding house's culture of spirited conversation. Her relationship with Gerald and Elspeth suggests a family unit within the larger household.

## Analysis

Harriett represents the vitality of communal domestic life that contrasts with Miriam's solitary boarding-house existence. Richardson renders her through her speech and physical movement rather than interior reflection, making her one of the novel's most vividly externalized characters.
