## Overview

Russia appears in *Deadlock* primarily through the presence of Mr. Shatov, a Russian student living in London. It is a distant but vivid presence — evoked through language, literature, and the political consciousness of its emigrant son.

Miriam never visits Russia, but she encounters it constantly: in conversation, in translation, and in the musical, exotic sound of the Russian language as spoken by Shatov and his compatriot Mr. Rodkin.

## Language as Window

Shatov's most sustained argument for Russia's distinctiveness is linguistic. He claims Russian is "richer than English" (001-130), with six or seven synonyms where English has only one word, each possessing "most delicate individual shades of nuance" (001-130).

> There is also in Russian the most immense *variety* of natural expressions, coming forth from the strong feeling of the Russian nature to all these surrounding influences; each word opens to a whole *aperçu* in this sort. [001-131]

Most significant to Shatov is the "people-language" — "there is no other people-language similar; there is in no one language so immense a variety of tender diminutives and intimate expressions of all natural things" (001-131). This linguistic richness, he argues, flows from Russia's vast geography and the vigor of its people.

## A Landscape of the Mind

Miriam's Russia is a kaleidoscope assembled from Shatov's vivid descriptions. She glimpses:

> A strip of silent sunlit snow, just below Finland, St. Petersburg in the midst of it, rounded squat square white architecture piled solidly beneath a brilliant sky, low sledges smoothly gliding, drawn by three horses, bell-spanned, running wildly abreast. [001-133]

The landscape unfolds in fragments: the white nights of northern winter, spring breaking through "in a single day" (001-133), the Volga winding southward. She imagines villages scattered among vast forests — "the vast forests of Germany small and homely" by comparison — each one "a brilliant miniature of Russia, in every hut a holy image" (001-133).

In the south, wild horses roam and shallow salt lakes stretch to the horizon under "a crystal air so life-giving that one recovered" (001-133). Peasants sing in "strong deep voices" and dance by torchlight.

## The Political Contrast

Russia functions in the novel as a foil to England — it represents a passionate, "recognisable" alternative to English reserve. The political contrast is equally sharp: Shatov marvels at free assembly in Hyde Park, noting that in Russia a man shouting "Chamberlain is a damned liar" would not be met with a smiling policeman (003-105).

He observes that "there is in Russia except in the governing and bourgeois classes almost no hypocrisy" (001-133) — a claim that distinguishes Russia from the English social order Miriam navigates daily.

## The Sound of Russia

What stays with Miriam most is not any specific fact about Russia but the *sound* of it. Hearing Shatov speak in Russian, she experiences something she cannot categorize:

> The strange thing that had touched her was somewhere within the voice; the sound of Russia. So much more strange, so much wider and deeper than the sound of German or French or any of the many tongues she had heard in this house, the inpouring impression was yet not alien. It was not *foreign*. [001-133]

She recognizes the same quality in "kind commercial little Mr. Rodkin" — "the disarming, unforeign thing" (001-133) — and realizes there is something in common between English and Russian, a kinship that stands alone in Europe.
