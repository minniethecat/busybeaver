## Overview

Sissie Bailey is the daughter of [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] and a recurring presence in the Tansley Street boardinghouse that forms the central setting of _Deadlock_. She moves through the narrative in a dual role: at once an indispensable domestic support for her mother and a young woman on the cusp of economic self-sufficiency through the acquisition of [[typewriting]] skills. Her appearances trace a quiet but telling arc from sullen adolescence to the threshold of working life, making her one of the novel's most textured minor characters.

## Life at the Boardinghouse

Sissie operates almost entirely within the domestic frame of [[tansley-street]], where she clears tables, brings tea, and answers the door. Her labour is woven so seamlessly into the rhythm of the house that her presence registers only when it falters:

> "…lain away something, watching Sissie and Mrs. Bailey clear the table, with a smile…" [001-030]

The syntax here — the ellipsis, the watching, the subordinate clause — is characteristic of [[Dorothy M. Richardson]]'s rendering of domestic labour: half-seen, half-acknowledged, yet structurally essential. Sissie's movements define the physical tempo of the house, and when she withdraws into sullenness or disappears to make tea, the narrative texture thins perceptibly.

> "Sissie went away to make tea." [001-091]

This sentence, stripped to subject-verb-infinitive, captures something essential about Sissie's mode of existence in the novel: action without introspection, presence without interiority. She is seen from the outside, by [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]] and by the reader, as a figure performing domestic ritual.

## The Threshold of Independence

Mrs. Bailey's most revealing disclosure about her daughter comes in a moment of maternal anxiety:

> "It's Sissie I worry about," said Mrs. Bailey. Miriam attended curiously. "…" [001-120]

The worry is specific and forward-looking. Sissie stands at a generational pivot point: unlike her mother, whose life is fully absorbed by the boardinghouse, Sissie is acquiring a marketable skill:

> "…Quite soon Sissie would know typewriting; a mar…" [001-125]

| Skill | Status | Implication |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| Typewriting | In training | Economic independence |
| Domestic management | Practised daily | Inherited role |
| Social navigation | Developing | Seen in interactions with lodgers |

The interrupted sentence ("a mar…" — possibly "a marriage" or "a market") leaves Sissie's future deliberately suspended, mirroring the novel's own refusal to resolve the tension between domestic life and [[freedom]]. The typewriting skill functions as what the novel elsewhere calls a "door" — a means of exit from the closed circuit of the boardinghouse into the wider world of [[work]] and wage-earning.

## Relationship with Her Mother

The bond between Sissie and [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] is the emotional core of her appearances. They appear together in many scenes — clearing tables, tending illness, sharing confidences. When Mrs. Bailey suffers from headaches, Sissie shares the burden of care:

> "In the dining-room Sissie responded with unconcealed as…" [001-087]

Miriam, observing the pair, registers both the affection and the constraint. The mother-daughter relation functions as a miniature of the novel's larger concern with [[marriage]] and its alternatives: Mrs. Bailey's life is one possible future for Sissie, typewriting another. The daughter's trajectory is thus charged with [[consciousness]] of choice — or the lack of it.

## Narrative Function

Sissie serves as a structural counterweight in the Baileys' household. Where [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]] brings philosophical intensity and [[mr-gunner|Mr. Gunner]] quiet gentility, Sissie provides the unglamorous continuity of domestic maintenance. Her few words — often "sullen" or dutiful — form a bass note beneath the elaborate conversations that occupy the novel's foreground. She also acts as a relay point: it is Sissie's voice that announces Mr. Shatov's arrival [001-164], and Sissie's movements that punctuate the transitions between scenes. Her presence — or its felt absence — shapes the pacing of domestic episodes, and her gradual emergence into the world of typewriting mirrors the novel's investment in women's access to [[work]] as a form of [[freedom]].
