# Typewriting

Typewriting appears in *Deadlock* as a marker of practical skill and female economic independence. It is associated with Sissie Bailey, for whom it represents a path to self-sufficiency.

## In the Novel

Miriam reflects that "quite soon Sissie would know typewriting; a marketable accomplishment" [001-125]. The phrase captures several social realities of the period: typewriting as a gateway to clerical employment, the value placed on "marketable" skills, and the association of typing with young women entering the workforce.

The single reference is freighted with implication. For Sissie, typewriting is not merely a technical skill but a means of economic agency — a way to participate in the world beyond the domestic sphere. In this, it connects to the novel's broader treatment of women's work and independence.

## Context

In early twentieth-century Britain, typewriting was increasingly taught to young women as a vocational skill, opening opportunities in offices and businesses. The typewriter itself — as a technology — was transforming both the workplace and the nature of written communication. Richardson's brief mention situates her characters within this changing economic landscape.
