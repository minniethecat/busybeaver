## Overview

Solitude pervades *Deadlock* as a defining condition of Miriam's experience. Unlike isolation or loneliness, solitude in Richardson's rendering is both a necessary space for consciousness and a state requiring examination. The novel distinguishes between solitude as chosen withdrawal and solitude as absence of meaningful connection.

## In the Text

Solitude appears as a theme from the opening chapters. Miriam's room is described with its "evening solitude" [001-081], a space where self-awareness crystallizes. Walking through London, she imagines the freedom to "wander here in solitude" [002-051], envying women who can move unobserved through the city. Yet solitude is also subject to critique: "She reflected that solitude was too easy" [002-082], suggesting that withdrawal must be balanced against engagement. In later chapters, solitude becomes a harbour [007-037] and a site of deepened perception, where the years "deepened to a perpetual morning" [011-001]. The silence and solitude of Wimpole Street [002-050] offer an unchanging presence against which thought can unfold.

## Character Perspectives

For Miriam, solitude is not simply being alone but a quality of attention. Her "years of solitude" [011-001] are years of perceptual richness. The novel presents solitude as the precondition for the kind of consciousness Richardson's narrative method requires — a receptive, unhurried awareness.

## Analysis

Solitude in *Deadlock* exemplifies what critics have called Richardson's "feminine sentence" — a narrative mode that values interior space over external action. The concept's ambivalent treatment (solitude as both gift and temptation to withdraw) reflects the modernist tension between subjective experience and social engagement.
