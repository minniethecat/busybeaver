# The Orlys

Mr. and Mrs. Orly are fellow residents at the Tansley Street boardinghouse,
remembered by Miriam primarily in connection with their departure. The
Orlys' exit from the boardinghouse serves as one of the novel's small
markers of social flux — the constant comings and goings that characterize
the transient domestic world Richardson depicts.

## Mrs. Orly's Voice

Mrs. Orly enters Miriam's consciousness through the sound of her voice,
a characteristic Richardson technique for rendering character through
sensory impression rather than physical description:

> "She welcomed the sound of Mrs. Orly's voice, tired and animated at
> once." [003-064]

The voice carries "incidents from Mrs. Orly's conversation pressing forward"
[003-065], suggesting a lively talker whose social presence enlivened the
boardinghouse atmosphere.

## The Orlys' Departure

The Orlys' departure is rendered with the emotional texture of boardinghouse
life — connections formed and dissolved within the constraints of shared
domestic space:

> "The Orlys will be gone away for a month." [008-012]

The goodbyes are cordial but freighted with the awareness that boardinghouse
relationships are inherently temporary:

> "I must say goodbye," said Mrs. Orly. In her sweet little sallow face...
> [008-021]

Mr. Orly's "gusty" manner [008-022] and Mrs. Orly's whispered regret
capture the distinctive registers of a married couple saying farewell.

## Married Life in Miniature

Through the Orlys, Richardson offers a brief but resonant portrait of married
life as observed from the outside. The contrast between Mr. Orly's hearty,
public manner and Mrs. Orly's more intimate affect suggests the different
social performances that marriage requires of men and women.

The Orlys' partnership — their shared departure, their coordinated farewells —
represents one version of coupled life that Miriam observes without directly
commenting upon. In the economy of the novel, such observations accumulate as
part of Miriam's unspoken education in the varieties of human relationship.

## Boardinghouse Transience

The Orlys exemplify the transience of boardinghouse life. Their departure
is a minor event in the novel's narrative economy but a meaningful one in
the texture of Miriam's daily existence. Each departure and arrival
reconfigures the social chemistry of the house, and Miriam's responses to
these shifts are part of the novel's minute attention to the rhythms of
shared domestic space.
