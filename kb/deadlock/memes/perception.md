## Overview

Perception in "Deadlock" is never merely sensory. For Miriam Henderson, to perceive is to interpret, to judge, to position oneself in relation to what is seen. Richardson's prose tracks perception at the level of micro-adjustments — the flicker of a glance, the shift of light, the sudden recognition that transforms a scene. This treatment of perception as active and meaning-saturated is central to the novel's modernist technique.

## In the Text

Miriam's perceptual acuity is both her gift and her burden. She notices what others miss — the quality of light in a room, the exact posture of a body, the barely perceptible tension in a social situation. When she attends a gathering, she is acutely aware of how she is perceived: "nearly always she must appear both imbecile and rude" (002-109). This meta-perception — perceiving others' perception of her — creates the recursive self-consciousness that defines her narrative voice.

The verb "perceive" appears at crucial moments of interpersonal tension. When Mr. Shatov says "I perceive that you are in spite of your denials a most excellent institutrice" (001-020), the formal, slightly stilted word choice marks his foreignness while also signaling his attentiveness to Miriam. Later, in a moment of intense frustration, Miriam thinks: "Why did he not _perceive_ the life there was, the mode of life, in this sitting tranquilly together?" (008-020). Perception here becomes a moral test — to fail to perceive is to fail to understand.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Her entire narrative consciousness is an exercise in perception. She perceives relentlessly, and her perception is always already interpretation.

**Mr. Shatov**: Perceives intellectually rather than sensorily. His "I perceive" (001-020) is a European philosophical formulation, not an English observation.

## Analysis

Richardson's handling of perception distinguishes her from contemporaries like Joyce and Woolf. Where Joyce's stream of consciousness emphasizes linguistic play and Woolf's emphasizes lyrical unity, Richardson's emphasizes the granular texture of perceptual experience — the way consciousness registers the world in a continuous present of noticing.
