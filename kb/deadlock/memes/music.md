## Overview

Music appears throughout "Deadlock" as a symbol of pure aesthetic experience — a form of beauty and emotional truth that exists beyond the reach of language. It represents the possibility of communication and feeling that transcends the social and intellectual barriers Miriam navigates in her daily life.

## In the Text

Music in the novel is often associated with moments of revelation or transcendence. During a social occasion, "nothing but music reached her ears, everything else was silence" (002-051), suggesting music's capacity to absorb attention completely and provide refuge from social complexity.

The novel also uses musical references to characterize social situations. A dismissive observation — "looks rather like a musical comedy" (002-013) — employs musical genre as social shorthand. And the brief appearance of a "musical box" (003-031) introduces a moment of domestic magic: "fairy music, sounding clear and distinct" (003-035).

Mr. Shatov's voice is described as having "musical beauty and expressiveness" (001-145), linking musicality to his foreignness and suggesting that qualities of voice and presence can communicate beyond the content of speech.

## The Piano and Solitude

Music is also linked to Miriam's private creative life. She plays the piano in the boardinghouse drawing-room, and the instrument becomes a locus of solitary expression: "She lit the gas and leaving the door wide sat down to the piano." The piano-playing is not a performance for others but a means of inhabiting consciousness differently — a bodily, rhythmic mode of being that offers respite from the "circling pressing details" of work and social obligation.

The connection between music and solitude is deepened when Miriam's playing is interrupted: "She broke off and turned to see Sissie Bailey, waiting with sullen politeness to speak. Mr. Shatov was out." The sudden cessation of music coincides with the absence of the novel's most musical character — Shatov himself — and Miriam registers the room as "cold and black; horribly empty and still." Music fills a void that conversation and social presence cannot; when it stops, the underlying emptiness of the boardinghouse reasserts itself.

## Shatov's Musicality

Shatov's connection to music runs deeper than the quality of his voice. Miriam speculates that "with that voice he would certainly be musical… the sturdiness and the plaintiveness were like the Russian symphonies." The association of Shatov with Russian symphonic music links him to a national cultural tradition of emotional depth and formal ambition — qualities that distinguish him from the English boarders. His musicality is both a mark of his foreignness and a sign of his attunement to dimensions of experience that the English, in Miriam's view, cannot access.

The Russian language itself is experienced by Miriam as musical. Listening to Shatov speak Russian with Mr. Rodkin, she notes "the charm of the Russian words" — a language whose sounds carry meaning beyond their semantic content. "A Russian word… hung in the air and spread music." For Miriam, who struggles with the inadequacy of English to capture the texture of consciousness, Russian — and music itself — represents an alternative: a form of expression in which sound and sense are not divided.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Responds to music as a sensory and emotional experience. Her appreciation is intuitive rather than analytical, and music often provides moments of respite from the pressure of social and intellectual demands.

## Analysis

Music in "Deadlock" functions as a counterpoint to the verbal and intellectual exchanges that dominate the novel. While Miriam struggles with language — translation, conversation, writing — music offers an alternative mode of experience that is immediate and complete. Richardson's use of musical imagery aligns with the broader modernist interest in music as a model for literary form.
