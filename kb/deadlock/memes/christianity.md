# Christianity

**Christianity** surfaces in *Deadlock* as a recurrent subject of intellectual debate, particularly in the exchanges between [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]] and [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]]. The novel treats Christianity not as a doctrinal matter to be affirmed or denied but as a cultural and historical force whose meanings are actively contested — a lens through which questions of national identity, gender, and intellectual authority are refracted.

## Defending Christianity

Christianity first enters the conversation as something that must be defended — and Miriam's response is one of instinctive resistance:

> They are awful. The others look Christians. They *don't*. Fancy defending Christianity.
> (003-081)

The verb "fancy" carries the weight of Miriam's skepticism: defending Christianity is, to her, an unnatural act, a posture adopted for social reasons rather than intellectual conviction. The passage implies a gap between Christian appearance and reality — between how people look and what they are — that Miriam's consciousness is uniquely attuned to detect.

Mr. Shatov's reply shifts the terms from faith to civilisation. Christianity, he argues, is not a belief system but a cultural inheritance:

> "The thing you are seeing," she said, "is Christianity. I don't mean that there is anything in it; but Christian ideas have made English civilisation; that's what it is."
> (003-082)

The concession "I don't mean that there is anything in it" — attributing the insight to Miriam — is characteristic of the novel's method. Ideas are not presented as propositions to be evaluated but as positions adopted by specific speakers in specific conversational moments. Christianity, in this exchange, is simultaneously defended and hollowed out: it has produced English civilisation, but its truth content is left unexamined.

## Christianity and English Identity

The debate expands to encompass the relationship between Christianity and national identity. Miriam introduces the idea that Christianity in Britain predates the Roman mission — that there was "Christianity in Britain, and British Christian Churches, long before the Romans came" (003-088). For her, this pre-Roman lineage offers "an immense relief, to know we go right back, *ourselves*" — a Christianity unmediated by papal authority, native rather than imported.

Mr. Shatov dismisses this genealogical argument with the sweeping claim that "all these clericalisms are immaterial to *life*" (003-090). His frame is secular and developmental: England's distinctiveness comes not from its Christian inheritance but from "a relatively very rapid and unrestricted *secular* development" facilitated by the Protestant Reformation (003-083). The contrast between Miriam's historical-identity argument and Shatov's secular-progress argument mirrors a wider tension in the novel between the emotional pull of tradition and the rational claims of Enlightenment thought.

The debate touches on Newman, Buckle, the Greek Church, and the Reformation before dissolving into a shared walk through the Inner Circle — the intellectual positions left unresolved, the personal warmth between the speakers undamaged by their disagreement.

## Personal Memory: Newman's Apologia

Amidst the debate, Miriam has a private memory that undercuts the intellectual abstraction of the discussion. She recalls reading Newman's *Apologia Pro Vita Sua* while ill in bed at the Chiswick villa:

> the pages of the Apologia reading themselves without effort into her molten mind, as untroubled beauty and happiness, making what Newman sought seem to be at home in herself, revealing deep inside life a whole new strange place of existence that was yet familiar, so that the gradual awful gathering of his trouble was a personal experience, and the moment of conviction that schism was a deliberate death, a personal conviction.
> (003-090)

This is Christianity received not as argument but as experience — mediated through the physical state of illness, the yellow lamplight, the "molten mind" receptive to beauty rather than proposition. The passage suggests that Miriam's relationship to Christianity is deeper and more personal than her conversational skepticism would indicate. She can argue against Christianity and yet have experienced Newman's spiritual crisis as "a personal conviction."

## Christianity and Women

In a later conversation, Christianity reappears in the context of gender. Miriam argues that Christianity made a difference to the status of women:

> "Christ was the first man to see women as individuals."
> (012-060)

Shatov counters that "it is the Christian countries that have produced the prostitute and the most vile estimations of women in the world" (012-060). The debate is irresolvable on its own terms because the two speakers are using "Christianity" to mean different things: for Miriam, an ideal that has imperfectly altered consciousness; for Shatov, a historical record of actual practice.

Miriam's deeper claim — that "Christianity has made a difference. It has not given things to women that were not there before. Nothing can do that. But it has shed a light on them *which* the best women run away from" (012-060) — is one of the novel's most complex theological statements. Christianity does not confer value on women; it reveals value already present. And this revelation is itself discomforting: "the best women run away from" the light it sheds. The formulation is characteristically Richardsonian — truth as disclosure rather than construction, illumination that we may prefer to avoid.
