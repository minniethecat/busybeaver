## Overview

Mrs. Bailey is the landlady of the Tansley Street boardinghouse, where Miriam Henderson lives. More than a background figure, she represents an alternative model of feminine life — one grounded in domestic management, social competence, and romantic fulfillment rather than intellectual ambition. Her late-life romance with Mr. Gunner provides one of the novel's few examples of unambiguous happiness.

## Biography

Mrs. Bailey presides over the "dreary increasing shabbiness" (001-002) of Tansley Street with a dignity that survives the house's decline. She has known better times, and her shabbiness — "veiled by the dim light" — coexists with a smile that speaks of faraway knowledge.

Her romance with Mr. Gunner transforms her. Miriam observes the change with a mixture of fascination and envy: "Mrs. Bailey had triumphed; justified her everlasting confident smile" (001-019). The middle-aged landlady, with her "half-dyed grey hair and terrible ill-fitting teeth" (001-019), has found what eludes Miriam — mutual love with no agonizing philosophical complications.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Bailey represents domestic competence and social grace as genuine forms of intelligence. She manages her household, her boarders, and her emotions with a skill that Miriam, for all her intellectual acuity, cannot match. Her "quiet withholding dignity" (001-001) is a form of power — the power to maintain composure and connection in circumstances of material decline.

## Relationships

**Miriam Henderson**: A boarder whose aloofness Mrs. Bailey tolerates with patient dignity. Their relationship fluctuates with Miriam's moods and the house's fortunes.

**Mr. Gunner**: Her late-life romance. Their relationship — conducted in the shabby dining room, under the broken gas mantle — redeems the boardinghouse's dreariness.
