## Overview

Mr. Lahitte is a Frenchman staying at the Tansley Street boardinghouse who has prepared a lecture on Spanish literature. He hires Miriam to translate and improve his English manuscript. The encounter becomes a formative experience for Miriam — not because of any merit in Lahitte's work, but because her critical engagement with his empty rhetoric reveals to her the nature of genuine writing versus artificial style.

## The Lecture

Miriam's judgment of Lahitte's lecture is devastating. Reading through his manuscript, she finds "nothing there" — "not a single fact or idea; only Mr. Lahitte; a voice like an empty balloon" (001-002). The lecture, she concludes, "was a fraud. *He* was." His style is characterized by "strings of superlatives, the same ones again and again," leaving his audience restless with "hatred and despair" (001-002).

Yet the encounter proves unexpectedly generative. In correcting his work, Miriam experiences a breakthrough in her own understanding of writing. She identifies his central flaw — "It was *artificial*" — and crystallizes her diagnosis in the phrase "Avoid ornate alias" (001-002). This insight into the difference between ornament and substance becomes a revelation: "These strange unconsciously noticed things, living on in one, coming together at the right moment, part of a *reality*" (001-002).

## Character

Lahitte is seen through a double lens in the novel. To Mr. Shatov, who calls him "a most *pretentious* fellow," he represents a certain French intellectual type. Miriam's own judgment is harsher but more productive — Lahitte becomes for her a negative example, showing her what genuine intellectual work is *not*. His "fair" appearance (unexpectedly so for a Frenchman) and his presence at the boardinghouse breakfast table add physical texture to his role as a foil.
