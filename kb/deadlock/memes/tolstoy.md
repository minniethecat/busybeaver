# Tolstoy

Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910), the Russian novelist, is a recurring reference in
*Deadlock*. His works — particularly *Anna Karenina* — and his reputation as
a thinker are discussed in the Shatov household, and his literary methods and
philosophical vision serve as touchstones for the novel's own exploration of
consciousness and social life.

## Tolstoy in Intellectual Discussion

Tolstoy appears first in a list of Russian literary figures — "at least
Tourgainyeff and Tolstoy" [001-146] — and his name recurs throughout the
novel as a mark of literary seriousness. Mr. Shatov's familiarity with
Tolstoy is part of his self-presentation as a cultured European intellectual.

## Anna Karenina and Autobiographical Reading

The novel engages specifically with *Anna Karenina*, and Mr. Shatov offers
an autobiographical reading of the novel:

> "This book [is] the self-history of Tolstoy. He is Layvin, and Kitty is
> the woman he married." [002-065]

This reading — treating fiction as veiled autobiography — reflects the
interpretive habits of the novel's characters and suggests a parallel with
Richardson's own semi-autobiographical method in *Pilgrimage*.

## Tolstoy and Literary Nationalism

The discussion of Tolstoy intersects with questions of national literary
identity. The assertion that "of Tolstoy it is certainly not true" [001-153]
— presumably regarding some generalization about novelistic writing — reflects
the tendency to exempt great writers from national stereotypes while using
lesser writers to illustrate them. Tolstoy thus functions as a limit case for
thinking about national literary traditions.

## The Tolstoyan Novel and Pilgrimage

Tolstoy's presence in the novel's intellectual conversations serves a
structural purpose beyond mere cultural name-dropping. His model of the novel
as a form capacious enough to contain both intimate psychological drama and
broad social panorama stands behind Richardson's own ambitions in
*Pilgrimage*. The discussion of *Anna Karenina* in the Shatov household
implicitly raises questions that the novel itself addresses: the relationship
between fiction and autobiography, the representation of consciousness, and
the moral dimensions of everyday life.

## Tolstoy and the Novel's Themes

Beyond explicit references, Tolstoy's presence informs the novel's thematic
concerns: the relationship between individual consciousness and social life,
the moral dimensions of everyday existence, and the possibility of
representing inner experience in narrative form. These Tolstoyan themes align
with Richardson's own project in *Pilgrimage*.
