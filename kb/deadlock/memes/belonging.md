## Overview

Belonging is a persistent undercurrent in *Deadlock*, articulated through Miriam's position as a dental secretary moving through London's social worlds. The concept encompasses both literal belonging — the question of where and with whom one fits — and deeper existential belonging: the sense of connectedness to the world and to other people.

## In the Text

The word "belonging" surfaces in several key passages. In Chapter VI, Miriam reflects that the possibility of transforming one's life "meant belonging outside the world of clever women" [006-027], linking belonging to class and intellectual identity. Earlier, the three names "belonging to each character" [002-062] establish a framework for understanding identity labels. In Chapter XII, the ideal is expressed as "a life of their own and yet belonging, everybody would be understood" [012-043] — a vision of simultaneous individuality and connection. The tension between solitude and belonging runs throughout the novel, as Miriam seeks a form of belonging that does not erase the self.

## Character Perspectives

Miriam's longing for belonging is complicated by her resistance to conventional social roles. She observes other women with their "belongings" [003-016] — material possessions that signal social placement — but remains ambivalent about acquiring her own. The novel portrays belonging not as a fixed state but as a negotiation between the desire for connection and the imperative of selfhood.

## Analysis

In the context of *Deadlock*'s modernist sensibility, belonging operates as a problem rather than a given. Richardson refuses the Victorian resolution in which the heroine finds her place; instead, belonging remains suspended, tentative, and perpetually renegotiated through acts of perception.
