## Overview

Politics in "Deadlock" enters the narrative primarily through the figure of Mr. Shatov and his circle of Russian émigré intellectuals. Rather than systematic political argument, the novel presents politics as it is lived — through charged conversations, casual dismissals, and the constant background hum of national and ideological difference. The political discourse of the novel spans anarchism, Zionism, British imperialism, and the politics of assimilation.

## In the Text

Mr. Shatov is introduced as someone whose political formation occurred in "the Russian student circles" (002-151), where radical ideas circulated among young intellectuals. His political identity is filtered through Miriam's perception: "He was an anarchist. There he stood, Russian, come from all that faraway" (002-060). For Miriam, his anarchism is inseparable from his foreignness — a quality as much aesthetic as ideological.

The conversation about British imperialism and the Jews produces some of the novel's most direct political exchange. Mr. Shatov observes critically of English Jews: "Ah it has revolted me to hear them talk of this war, the British Empire, and the subject races" (007-014). Miriam's response — "I know; disgusting; but very British. But the British Empire has done a good deal for the Jews" (007-015) — reveals her own complex negotiation of patriotism and critique.

## Character Perspectives

**Mr. Shatov**: Holds the political positions of the Russian intelligentsia — critical of empire, skeptical of assimilation, and attuned to the power dynamics underlying national identity. His anarchism is more cultural than programmatic.

**Miriam Henderson**: Approaches politics warily, through personal relationships rather than abstract commitment. Her defense of British toleration toward Jews (007-019) reflects both genuine pride in English liberalism and an unexamined investment in national identity.

## Analysis

Politics in "Deadlock" exemplifies Richardson's modernist method: ideology is refracted through consciousness rather than presented as doctrine. Mr. Shatov's warning that "once that is generally recognised, even England will have its antisemitic movement" (007-021) carries prophetic weight precisely because it emerges from intimate conversation rather than political oratory. The novel's treatment of politics anticipates themes that would become urgent in the decades following its 1921 publication.
