## Introduction

Thomas Henry Huxley, the Victorian biologist and public intellectual known as "Darwin's Bulldog," is invoked in Chapter XII of _Deadlock_. Miriam Henderson engages in an extended critique of his views on nature and gender, particularly his concept of "Nature's Salic Law."

## Miriam's Critique

Miriam uses Huxley as an archetype of masculine scientific authority. She challenges his formulation of "Nature's Salic Law" (012-030, 012-034) — the idea that nature itself decrees male primacy — as a projection of social prejudice onto the natural world. Her critique is part of the novel's broader interrogation of scientific claims about gender.

## Significance

Huxley represents the nineteenth-century scientific establishment that Richardson's narrative method implicitly challenges. By having Miriam directly engage with and reject Huxley's ideas, the novel asserts the legitimacy of feminine consciousness as a counter-authority to scientific positivism.
