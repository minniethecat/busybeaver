# Architecture

Architecture appears in *Deadlock* as an element of visual perception and urban experience. Like the novel's treatment of streets, interiors, and public spaces, architectural description serves to ground consciousness in the material world.

## In the Novel

The most notable architectural description occurs in a passage where Miriam observes "rounded squat square white architecture piled solidly beneath a brilliant" sky [001-144]. The juxtaposition of adjectives — rounded yet squat and square, solid yet brilliant — captures the perceptual complexity that characterizes Richardson's descriptive method.

Architecture here is not merely background but an object of active perception, its forms and colors shaping the character's immediate experience.

The reference to "white architecture" suggests the stuccoed terraces and squares of London's Georgian and Regency districts — the built environment through which Miriam moves daily. The "brilliant" light transforms the familiar urban scene, making the ordinary momentarily striking.

## Context

Architectural description in *Deadlock* is consistent with the novel's impressionist method. Buildings, streets, and rooms are rendered not through systematic description but through the flickering attention of consciousness.

What Miriam notices, how she notices it, and what it makes her feel — this is the novel's descriptive logic. The "rounded squat square" passage is typical: a rapid succession of adjectives that prioritizes perceptual impact over objective description.
