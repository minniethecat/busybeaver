## Overview

Science appears in "Deadlock" primarily as a contested epistemological framework — a system of knowledge that both compels and troubles the novel's characters. Through debates between Miriam and Mr. Shatov about the relationship between science, philosophy, and religion, Richardson explores the modernist anxiety about whether scientific method can fully account for human experience.

## In the Text

The most concentrated discussion of science occurs during a conversation in Regent's Park where Mr. Shatov presents a view of intellectual progress: "The progress of philosophy... a series of systems; that of science by the constant addition of small certainties" (007-004). This distinction between philosophy's systemic ambitions and science's incremental accumulation becomes central to the novel's epistemology.

Miriam questions whether one can approach science "from the point of view of _science_ in an unscientific way" (007-007), probing the boundaries of scientific method. The conversation extends to the relationship between different knowledge domains: Mr. Shatov argues that "religion, like metaphysic and science, had a right to its premises" (007-012), articulating a pluralistic view in which different modes of understanding operate under different rules.

The novel also references specific scientific ideas. Darwin appears: "in matter of science the conclusions of Darwin will..." (003-098), suggesting the cultural penetration of evolutionary thought. A brief reference to the comet (001-011) connects to popular scientific interest, while Miriam wonders "whether there was still a nebular theory and whether anything more had been done about the electrical contact of metals" (001-037).


## Analysis

Science in "Deadlock" functions not as a source of definitive answers but as one framework among others for understanding reality. The novel's treatment anticipates later critiques of scientism while acknowledging science's genuine achievements. Richardson's positioning of science alongside philosophy and religion (007-012) reflects the modernist recognition that no single knowledge system can fully account for the complexity of lived experience — a recognition enacted by the novel's own stream-of-consciousness method.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Approaches science with curiosity but without formal training. Her questions about scientific method (007-007) reveal a mind that engages seriously with epistemological problems without accepting scientific authority uncritically.

**Mr. Shatov**: Articulates a view of science as incremental knowledge accumulation (007-004), treating it as one of several valid modes of understanding alongside philosophy and religion.
