## Overview

Mrs. Bergstein appears in only one scene — the climactic interview of Chapter XIII — but her presence crystallizes the novel's central concerns with religion, marriage, and the possibility of crossing cultural boundaries. An Englishwoman who converted from Unitarianism to Judaism through marriage, she represents both a potential model for Miriam and a warning about what such a crossing might cost.

## Biography

Mrs. Bergstein's background is revealed in fragments during the interview. A former Unitarian, she found the step to Judaism possible "without making a break with my earlier convictions" (013-016), suggesting a rational rather than passionate approach to religious conversion.

Her present life is one of wealthy Jewish domesticity — the "great house" (013-005) with its "immense hall" and butler signifies material comfort. Yet the interview reveals the costs of her position. She is an Englishwoman in a Jewish household, navigating the "Jewish religious and social oblivion of women" (013-004), and her "pinched and drawn" face (013-027) suggests that comfort has not brought fulfillment.

## Character Analysis

Mrs. Bergstein represents the rational approach to life's deepest questions — the belief that religious affiliation, like marriage, can be approached as a matter of compatible convictions. Her voice carries "something that was like a cold clean blade thrusting into an intelligible future" (013-011), a clarity that both impresses and chills Miriam.

The interview's key moment comes when Mrs. Bergstein asks about "common religious feeling" (013-026) between Miriam and Mr. Shatov. Her question, born of her own experience, cuts to the heart of Miriam's dilemma: can love survive without shared belief?
