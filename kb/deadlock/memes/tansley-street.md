## Overview

Tansley Street is the gravitational centre of _Deadlock_'s domestic geography. It is the address of [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]]'s boardinghouse, where [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]] lodges alongside [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]], [[mr-gunner|Mr. Gunner]], [[mr-rodkin|Mr. Rodkin]], and the Bailey family. More than a mere address, Tansley Street functions as the novel's primary stage: the dining-room where conversations unfold, the hall where arrivals and departures mark the rhythm of the day, the private rooms where consciousness retreats into itself. It is the counterpoint to [[wimpole-street]] — domestic where the dental practice is professional, intimate where the practice is transactional.

## The Boardinghouse

The boardinghouse at Tansley Street is not a neutral container but an active participant in the novel's social dynamics:

> "…No change at Tansley Street; through the crack in…" [001-030]

The phrase "no change" captures the boardinghouse's defining quality: continuity. In a novel of shifting consciousness and relational flux, Tansley Street is the constant — the place where the same faces gather, the same rituals repeat, the same "crack in" the door admits the same stream of lodgers. This continuity is both comfort and constraint.

> "…nothing but the dining-room at Tansley Street.…" [001-105]

The dining-room metonymically stands for the entire boardinghouse, and the boardinghouse for the entire domestic world. When Miriam's mind fixes on Tansley Street, it is the dining-room she sees — the site of [[conversation]], [[the-evening|the evening]] ritual, and the social performances that constitute the novel's primary action.

## A Russian in London

Tansley Street is also where cultural worlds collide:

> "…an interesting Russian at Tansley Street now.…" [003-062]

The phrase — "an interesting Russian at Tansley Street" — condenses the boardinghouse's role as a site of cultural encounter. [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]]'s Russianness is experienced not in Russia but in a London boardinghouse, where his difference is both attraction and puzzle. Tansley Street becomes, in this framing, a miniature cosmopolis — a place where [[England]] and [[Russia]], [[philosophy]] and domestic routine, meet across the dining table.

> "…He has spent his Christmas at Tansley Street. He would adore Elspet…" [003-060]

The detail that Shatov "spent his Christmas at Tansley Street" is significant: the boardinghouse is not just a temporary lodging but a site of holiday observance, a place where the rituals of English domesticity absorb even its foreign residents.

## The Household Ecosystem

Tansley Street is defined as much by the relationships among its residents as by its physical fabric. Mrs. Bailey presides with a "quiet withholding dignity" that lends the shabby establishment an unexpected gravity. Mr. Rodkin, the commercial traveller and the house's longest-standing boarder, acts as intermediary and sponsor — it is through him that Shatov arrives. Mr. Gunner, "sitting over the empty grate waiting for nothing," embodies the boardinghouse's capacity to absorb aimless lives into its routines. Sissie Bailey moves through the house with a "steady ease of mind" that Miriam finds both enviable and impenetrable.

The household is a precarious economic enterprise. Mrs. Bailey's "excited conspirator's smiles" when communicating news of a new boarder betray the anxiety beneath her dignity — the constant fear of losing a "well-to-do newcomer." Miriam registers "the wretched sense of being caught and linked up again in the shifts and deceptions of the bankrupt house." The boardinghouse is always on the edge of financial failure, and this precarity shapes every social transaction within it.

## The Enduring Centre

> "…her mind set excitedly towards Tansley Street. There was always some…" [003-108]

The mind "set excitedly towards Tansley Street" — the verb of directed motion, the adverb of anticipation — reveals the boardinghouse's emotional charge. However much Miriam chafes against its constraints, Tansley Street is the place her consciousness returns to, "always" containing "some…" — the sentence trailing off into unspecified possibility. The boardinghouse is never fully known, never fully exhausted; each return promises a variation on the familiar.
