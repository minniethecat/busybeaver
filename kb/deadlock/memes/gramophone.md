## Overview

The gramophone appears in _Deadlock_ as a new technology entering domestic space, becoming a site of social negotiation between [[gerald]] and the women at the boardinghouse.

## In the Novel

Gerald introduces the gramophone with an air of technological sophistication, surprised that others have not yet encountered it:

> "…an to say you've never seen a gramophone yet?" murmured Gerald, frowning… [003-032]

The device becomes a point of tension — Gerald's proprietary relationship with the machine contrasts with the curiosity (and occasional indifference) of those around him:

> "…a peremptory hand towards the gramophone. Gerald tried to make a barga… [003-048]

## Context

In the early twentieth century, the gramophone was still a relatively novel domestic technology. Its appearance in _Deadlock_ registers the infiltration of mechanical reproduction into the intimate spaces of everyday life — a theme that connects to the novel's broader interest in [[consciousness]], [[perception]], and the relationship between authentic experience and its mechanical mediation.
