## Overview

Covent Garden appears in *Deadlock* in an early boardinghouse scene, when Miriam breaks an awkward silence by announcing she has come to ask "the best way of getting to Covent Garden early in the morning" (001-003).

The question is partly a pretext — a remembered "kind of enquiry used by boarders to sustain their times of association with Mrs. Bailey" — but it triggers a conversation that reveals the social dynamics of the house.

## The Pretext

Miriam's inquiry is a performance. She adopts the role of the active boarder, someone who goes places and does things, in order to bridge the awkward gap between herself and the group on the sofa. Mrs. Bailey "sat forward as if for conversation" (001-003), and Miriam "moved about the room enlarging on her imaginary enterprise" (001-003).

Yet the performance fails. She becomes aware of "Mr. Gunner's watchfulness," and the group on the sofa presents "a united appearance of kindly, smiling, patient, almost patronising tolerance" (001-003). Behind their amusement lurks the fact that they had been discussing house business — "the eternal difficulties of the house" — and Miriam has interrupted it.

## Engagement and Detachment

Covent Garden functions as more than a failed conversational gambit. Miriam later reflects on the contrast between those who truly engage with London and those who merely drift through the boardinghouse:

> The Canadians and the foreigners were "unconscious of the atmosphere" of the house, "free and active in it," precisely "because they really went to Covent Garden and Petticoat Lane and Saint Paul's." [001-008]

The market thus represents genuine engagement with the city — the difference between those who explore London and those who inhabit its suspended, interior spaces. For Miriam, the imagined Covent Garden outing is a gesture toward the former, an attempt to be the kind of person who "really went" places rather than merely dreamed of going.

## A London of the Mind

The irony is that Covent Garden, for Miriam, remains primarily imaginary. It is a destination she invokes but may never reach — a placeholder for the active life she desires but struggles to claim. In this sense, the market mirrors the novel's larger concern with the gap between the London one imagines and the London one actually inhabits.
