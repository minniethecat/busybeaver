## Overview

Virginia Woolf (1882–1941) stands alongside Dorothy Richardson and James Joyce as one of the defining figures of literary modernism. Though Woolf does not appear as a character in "Deadlock," her contemporaneous experiments with narrative form — particularly in "Mrs Dalloway" (1925) and "To the Lighthouse" (1927) — provide essential context for understanding Richardson's achievement. Woolf's famous essay "Modern Fiction" (1919) articulated the modernist ambition to capture "the luminous halo" of consciousness, an ambition Richardson had been pursuing since "Pointed Roofs" (1915).

## Critical Relationship

Woolf reviewed Richardson's "The Tunnel" (1919) appreciatively, noting Richardson's invention of "the psychological sentence of the feminine gender." This characterization — simultaneously admiring and othering — captures the complex relationship between the two writers. Woolf's recognition of Richardson's formal innovation was genuine, yet her framing of it as specifically "feminine" reflected the gendered terms in which women's experimental writing was discussed.

Richardson, for her part, was critical of Woolf's "A Room of One's Own," finding its feminism too programmatic for her own more experiential approach to women's consciousness. The difference between them is instructive: where Woolf theorized, Richardson rendered; where Woolf argued for women's creative freedom, Richardson simply enacted it through the unapologetic centrality of Miriam Henderson's consciousness.

## Shared Project

Both writers shared the conviction that consciousness — its flows, interruptions, and sudden illuminations — was the proper subject of modern fiction. Both rejected the external, plot-driven realism of their Edwardian predecessors. And both sought to create a prose capable of rendering the texture of lived experience from within. The existence of Woolf's major works, beginning shortly after "Deadlock," confirms that Richardson's experiments were not an isolated phenomenon but part of a broader modernist reimagining of what fiction could do.
