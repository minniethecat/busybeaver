## Overview

Conversation in _Deadlock_ is never merely the exchange of information. It functions as the novel's primary narrative engine — the medium through which characters reveal themselves, test ideas, and negotiate the charged space between private [[consciousness]] and social performance. At the [[tansley-street]] boardinghouse, the dinner table becomes a theatre of conversational styles: [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]]'s philosophical irascibility, [[mr-rodkin|Mr. Rodkin]]'s rapped-out statements, [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]]'s withholding dignity. Dorothy Richardson treats talk not as transparent communication but as a form of action, dense with what is withheld as much as what is spoken.

## Conversation as Narrative Medium

Richardson's rendering of conversation is inseparable from her stream-of-consciousness method. Talk arrives in fragments — broken off, half-heard, reinterpreted through Miriam's perceiving mind:

> "…Bailey sat forward as if for conversation. The spell was partly broken,…" [001-003]

The physical posture — sitting forward — precedes the act of speech, and conversation is registered first as a breaking of "spell," a disturbance of silence. Throughout the novel, the moments before and after talk are as narratively significant as talk itself.

> "…strange abrupt conversation different to any she had read…" [002-062]

| Quality of Conversation | Narrative Function |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| "Strange abrupt" [002-062] | Defamiliarizes the ordinary |
| "Customary" [002-091] | Establishes the boardinghouse as a social world with its own norms |
| "Immovable positive" [002-099] | Reveals ideological rigidity beneath social performance |
| "Long satisfactory" [002-100] | Marks moments of genuine connection |

## The Dinner Table as Arena

The boardinghouse dinner table is the novel's most concentrated site of conversational exchange. Here, Richardson orchestrates group scenes in which talk moves not linearly but in overlapping waves:

> "…they were in the middle of a conversation. Mr. Rodkin answered at once…" [002-103]

The phrase "in the middle" is important: conversation in _Deadlock_ is always already underway, a continuous medium into which characters enter and from which they exit. There is no clean beginning or end to talk, only the ongoing flow of social [[consciousness]].

> "Their conversation was changing. Mr. Shatov had…" [002-104]

Conversation changes — it has its own momentum, its own life independent of any single speaker's intention. This is Richardson's key insight about talk: it is a system, not a sequence of individual utterances. The conversation "was changing" in the passive voice, as if by its own agency.

## Steering and Avoidance

The novel is attentive to the social mechanics of conversational control:

> "…their business to steer the conversation and head it off if it gets ou…" [002-107]

The metaphor of steering — borrowed from navigation — reveals conversation as something that must be managed, directed away from dangerous waters. "Their business" suggests a shared undertaking, a collective labor of maintaining social surfaces. This management of talk is gendered work in the novel: women, particularly [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]], are shown as the primary stewards of conversational equilibrium.

> "…efforts to engage her in direct conversation, that she might hear, thought…" [003-009]

The attempt to "engage" Miriam in "direct conversation" — as opposed to the indirect, ambient talk that surrounds her — marks a social demand that the novel's protagonist frequently resists. Miriam prefers to observe conversation rather than participate in it, a preference that aligns her with the reader.

## Conversation as Revelation

Despite — or because of — its constraints, conversation in _Deadlock_ is also the site of genuine discovery:

> "…suddenly for the first time in conversation are the things you have alw…" [003-019]

The syntax stumbles toward revelation: things that have "always" been known emerge "suddenly" in the act of speaking. Conversation, in this formulation, is not the communication of pre-existing thoughts but the generation of new ones. Talk is thinking aloud, and thinking aloud transforms thought.

> "…had had a long satisfactory conversation. He would have understood…" [002-100]

The adjective "satisfactory" applied to conversation is Richardson's highest conversational compliment. A satisfactory conversation is one in which the gap between what is meant and what is heard narrows — temporarily, provisionally — to the point of genuine contact.

## Silence as Conversational Element

The novel treats silence not as the absence of conversation but as one of its constituent elements:

> "…the relief of their customary conversation held back until they should h…" [002-091]

Conversation "held back" — withheld, deferred — generates a tension that is itself a form of communication. The "relief" comes not from speaking but from the shared knowledge that speech will come, that the customary patterns will reassert themselves. Richardson is one of the few modernist writers to treat conversational silence with the same seriousness as conversational speech.
