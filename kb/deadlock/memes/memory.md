## Overview

Memory functions in *Deadlock* as more than simple recollection — it is the medium through which experience becomes self-knowledge. Richardson's stream-of-consciousness technique treats memory not as a distinct faculty but as the texture of consciousness itself, with past moments continually flowing into present perception. The novel's temporal structure is built not on plot chronology but on the rhythms of remembering, in which the present moment opens onto layers of accumulated experience.

## In the Text

The novel opens with an act of memory: Miriam recovers "a memory of the kind of enquiry" (001-003) that breaks a spell and initiates the narrative. Her inner world is described as "scenes ranged in her memory" (001-008), populated by people who exist as much in recollection as in the present. Memory transforms experience: Mr. Rodkin's life is "caught up whenever his memory touched it" (001-018), and the Bloomsbury Squares are described as "turning into memory" (003-220), their spell already passing into the past.

The novel also attends to memory's failures — the "memory-vista of similar anecdotes" (001-159) that blurs specificity, and the effort of searching "the deepest pit of her memory" (003-066) for a particular recollection. Objects too can be "stiff with lifeless memory" (002-104), suggesting that memory can deaden as well as animate. This duality — memory as both vital and deadening — reflects Richardson's nuanced understanding of how the past inhabits the present.

Crucially, Richardson distinguishes between active recollection and the passive persistence of the past. Miriam does not simply "remember" events; she lives within a consciousness saturated by prior experience. The past is not retrieved but continuously present, shaping perception before it reaches articulation.

## Character Perspectives

Miriam's relationship to memory is fundamentally creative: experience begins "its life as memory" (003-043), implying that the full significance of events emerges only in retrospect. This aligns with Richardson's narrative method, in which present-moment perception and remembered experience are indistinguishable. For Miriam, memory is not a storehouse of fixed facts but a living process through which the self continuously remakes itself.

## Analysis

Memory in *Deadlock* is central to Richardson's modernist technique. Unlike the plotted recollections of traditional fiction, memory here is ambient and ongoing — it is the condition of consciousness rather than a device for exposition. This treatment anticipates later theoretical work on memory as constructive rather than reproductive.

The novel's treatment of memory also has philosophical implications. If identity is constituted through memory — as the novel seems to suggest — then the self is never fixed but always in process. Each act of recollection remakes the rememberer, and the boundary between past and present becomes porous. This aligns *Deadlock* with Henri Bergson's philosophy of duration, in which time is lived as an indivisible flow rather than a sequence of discrete moments.

Richardson's formal innovation — refusing to separate narrated event from narrating consciousness — makes memory not a theme to be discussed but the very substance of the prose. The reader experiences Miriam's world as she does: saturated with the past, oriented toward an emerging future, never simply present.
