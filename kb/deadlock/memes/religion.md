## Overview

Religion in "Deadlock" operates as a framework for examining identity, community, and the limits of individual belief. The novel presents a range of religious positions — from Miriam's lapsed Anglican upbringing to Mr. Shatov's complex relationship with Judaism to Mrs. Bergstein's Unitarian-to-Jewish conversion — without advocating for any single tradition. Instead, religion becomes a lens through which Richardson explores the tension between institutional belonging and individual consciousness.

## In the Text

Miriam's own religious position is uncertain. When asked directly whether she is a Christian, she responds: "I don't know. I was brought up in the Anglican Church" (013-015). This ambivalence characterizes her relationship to organized religion throughout — she cannot fully embrace nor fully reject the tradition of her upbringing.

The novel's most sustained treatment of religion comes through the Bergstein interview in Chapter XIII. Mrs. Bergstein, an Englishwoman who converted to Judaism, explains her path: "I myself was a Unitarian, and therefore able to take the step without making a break with my earlier convictions" (013-016). This rational approach to religious affiliation — treating it as a matter of compatible convictions rather than transformation — contrasts with the emotional stakes the conversation carries for Miriam.

Mr. Shatov's position is more complex. He is described as "not exactly religious" yet unwilling to fully break with Jewish tradition because he sees "the old ritual is the only link between the persecuted Jews and those who are better placed" (013-023). His religious identity is thus bound up with solidarity rather than personal belief.

Earlier in the novel, Miriam reflects that "religion was an 'actual force' in Russia" (001-145), recognizing the cultural embeddedness of religious feeling that differs from English norms. And Mr. Shatov observes that "religion, like metaphysic and science, had a right to its premises" (007-012), articulating a pluralistic view that respects religion's intellectual autonomy.


## Analysis

The novel's treatment of religion reflects its modernist suspicion of institutional authority while recognizing the genuine human needs that religion addresses. The "religion" that Mr. Shatov acknowledges has a "right to its premises" (007-012) is not dogmatic faith but a mode of human understanding that operates alongside science and philosophy. Richardson's focus on the Bergstein interview in the climactic chapter emphasizes that religion, in this novel, is ultimately about how individuals negotiate between inherited tradition and personal authenticity.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Suspended between belief and disbelief, she cannot affirm Christianity but cannot dismiss the cultural and personal significance of religious tradition. Her questioning of Mrs. Bergstein reveals searching rather than settled conviction.

**Mrs. Bergstein**: Represents religion as rational choice — her Unitarian-to-Jewish conversion is presented as a step requiring no break with earlier convictions (013-016). Her approach is intellectual rather than mystical.

**Mr. Shatov**: Holds religion as cultural identity and solidarity. His Judaism is tied to community and history rather than personal faith, and his willingness to renounce it for Miriam (013-023) represents a sacrifice of belonging.
