## Overview

Mr. Gunner is one of the permanent boarders at Tansley Street — those who "stays always" (001-008) in the house's suspended atmosphere. His pedantic manner, his "numb clucking laughter" (001-002), and his persistent presence make him initially an object of Miriam's irritation. But his transformation through his relationship with Mrs. Bailey reveals unexpected depths.

## Biography

Mr. Gunner appears first as a fixture of the Tansley Street dining room — "sitting over the empty grate waiting for nothing" (001-030). He is one of the "human odds and ends" (001-008) that populate the boardinghouse, a man whose life seems to have settled into the house's stagnant rhythms.

His romance with Mrs. Bailey changes everything. The "modest confidence" (001-006) that replaces his earlier manner signals a genuine transformation. When he teases Miriam about the comet — "If you'll give me his name and address we'll take the earliest opportunity of paying a call" (001-012) — there is a new ease in his humor.

## Character Analysis

Mr. Gunner embodies the type that Miriam most instinctively dismisses — the English male pedant, full of "clever phrases that make you see things by a deliberate arrangement" (001-009). Yet his capacity for genuine connection with Mrs. Bailey complicates this dismissal. The novel suggests that even the most unpromising human material contains the possibility of transformation through love.
