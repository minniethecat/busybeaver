## Overview

Literature in "Deadlock" is simultaneously a source of aspiration and anxiety for Miriam Henderson. It represents both the world she wishes to enter — the world of serious writing and intellectual achievement — and the institutional structures that exclude her. Through Miriam's encounters with English literary culture and Russian literature, Richardson explores the gendered, commercial, and national dimensions of literary production in the early twentieth century.

## In the Text

Miriam's relationship to English literature is marked by insecurity. On the occasion of her first lesson with Mr. Shatov, she worries that "reading and discussion would reveal ignorance of English literature" (001-031). This anxiety reflects both her autodidactic background and the gendered barriers to literary authority — she has read widely but lacks the institutional validation of formal education.

The question of what constitutes worthy literature recurs throughout. Miriam considers which texts to read with Mr. Shatov: "Emerson would be perfect for reading; he would see that there was an English writer who knew everything" (001-033). Her choices — Emerson, Stopford Brooke, Matthew Arnold — reveal her desire to present English literature as intellectually substantial while avoiding purely canonical or conventional selections.

The novel also engages with literature as a profession. Mr. Shatov observes critically that "never before I came here did I meet this idea of writing for a living, in this naive widespread form" (006-027), contrasting English commercial literary culture with Continental attitudes. Miriam's ambivalence about "the clever people who had ideas and style and those others... gradually making themselves into writers" (006-027) reflects her simultaneous attraction to and suspicion of the literary marketplace.

## Analysis

Literature in "Deadlock" functions as a site of modernist self-reflection — a novel about a woman who wants to write, whose consciousness is shaped by reading, and who navigates a literary world structured by gender and class. Richardson uses Miriam's literary anxieties to explore broader questions about who gets to create culture and on what terms.

## Emerson as Literary Bridge

Ralph Waldo Emerson plays a distinctive role in the novel as the text that connects Miriam and Mr. Shatov across their cultural divide. Miriam's choice of Emerson is strategic: she hopes to demonstrate that "there was an English writer who knew everything" (001-033). But Emerson is, of course, American — a fact that subtly complicates the English-European binary the novel sets up. Emerson's transcendentalism resonates with both Miriam's intuitive spirituality and Mr. Shatov's philosophical inclinations.

Their first meeting over Emerson, however, does not go as Miriam imagined. "Confronting him she could not imagine him related to Emerson" (001-036). The text becomes a third presence in the room — a shared object that both connects and separates them. Mr. Shatov's response to Emerson is not the one Miriam anticipated: "I do not know this writer," he says, adding "what you say is perhaps not quite good" (001-134). His European critical standards challenge her English (and American) literary assumptions, forcing a more rigorous engagement.

## The Writing Life

Miriam's relationship to writing is defined by frustrated aspiration. She observes with envy "the many young men she had met at the Wilsons' who were mysteriously 'writing' or 'going to write'" (006-027). For these men, writing is a plausible identity, a profession one can inhabit. For Miriam, writing remains a distant possibility — something she _could_ do but cannot claim as her own. The novel's form — Richardson's own modernist prose — enacts what Miriam cannot yet achieve, creating an ironic gap between the consciousness that narrates and the character who aspires.

The gendered dimension of literary production is explored through Mr. Shatov's observation about English writing culture. "That is very English; well; tell me what I shall write" (001-051), he says, positioning English writing as a practical skill rather than an art. His Continental perspective reveals the English literary establishment as unusually commercial — a critique that cuts against the very world Miriam hopes to enter.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Approaches literature as a reader first and aspiring writer second. Her passion for literature is genuine but complicated by class and gender barriers. She envies the "many young men she had met at the Wilsons' who were mysteriously 'writing' or 'going to write'" (006-027).

**Mr. Shatov**: Brings a European perspective that values literature as cultural and intellectual pursuit rather than commercial enterprise. His critique of English literary commercialism (006-027) challenges Miriam's assumptions.
