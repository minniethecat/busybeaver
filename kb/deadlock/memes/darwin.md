## Overview

Charles Darwin (1809–1882) appears in _Deadlock_ as an intellectual touchstone, invoked in debates about [[science]], [[truth]], and the authority of scientific conclusions. His name recurs in conversations between [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]] and the boardinghouse circle, where Darwinism serves as both a monument of English thought and a site of critique.

## In the Novel

Darwin is first invoked alongside Shakespeare as a measure of intellectual depth:

> "…th less depth of truth in it. Darwin or Shakespeare. But Shakespea…" [001-036]

A more extended engagement comes in a discussion of Darwin's scientific stature. Shatov, drawing on Russian intellectual traditions, suggests that a Russian thinker may surpass even Darwin in breadth:

> "…erhaps greater than even your Darwin, certainly with a far wider p…" [003-096]

The conversation turns to the epistemological status of Darwin's conclusions:

> "…e will discover some day that Darwin's conclusions were wrong, tha…" [003-097]

This provokes a defense of science's cumulative character, distinguishing Darwin's specific findings from the enduring methodological revolution he inaugurated:

> "…of science the conclusions of Darwin will _never_ be displaced. It…" [003-098]

The discussion extends to the popularizers of Darwinism, with [[th-huxley|T. H. Huxley]] singled out for particular criticism:

> "…uxley who makes me angry with Darwinism. He didn't find it out, an…" [003-099]

A final fragment reclaims Darwin the man from the controversies surrounding his ideas:

> "…t certainly not the spirit of Darwin, who was a most gentle creatu…" [003-100]

## Significance

The Darwin debate in _Deadlock_ exemplifies the novel's method of embedding intellectual exchange within the texture of everyday social life. The boardinghouse dinner table becomes a site where the authority of science is tested against other ways of knowing — national, philosophical, and experiential.
