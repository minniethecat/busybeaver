## Overview

Jan is a recurring figure in *Deadlock*, appearing across four chapters. Though never fully developed as a character, Jan serves as part of Miriam's social and intellectual milieu — a friend whose opinions about philosophy carry weight and whose practical assistance (lending a cloak) is noted.

## In the Text

Jan's first mention describes how "Jan went about the streets thinking" [002-110], suggesting an introspective nature. A reference to someone who "would scream" [003-135] adds a note of emotional intensity. Jan's philosophical interests emerge in Chapter VII: "It *must* be important to Jan that what Hegel meant was only..." [007-038], indicating engagement with German idealism within Miriam's circle. In Chapter XI, a practical detail — "borrowing a cloak from Jan" [011-008] — anchors Jan in the physical world of the narrative.

## Character Perspectives

Jan appears to share Miriam's intellectual curiosity and participates in the discussions about philosophy and ideas that characterize the novel's social world. The mention of Hegel suggests a shared interest in serious thought.

## Analysis

As with many of Richardson's secondary characters, Jan is rendered through glimpses rather than full portraiture. The character's presence across four chapters (II, III, VII, XI) suggests a sustained if background role in Miriam's London life, contributing to the novel's texture of overlapping social connections.
