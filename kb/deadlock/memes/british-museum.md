## Overview

The British Museum appears in *Deadlock* as both a physical destination and a symbolic one. For Mr. Shatov, newly arrived from the Continent, "Saturday's visit to the British Museum" will be "the first event of his London life" (001-079).

For Miriam, the Museum is already familiar territory — she is a reader there and knows the officials and librarians by sight. It forms part of her London routine, the intellectual world she inhabits alongside her boardinghouse life.

## Shatov's Dream

The Museum functions as a threshold between English and foreign perspectives. From his very first conversation with Miriam, Shatov asks how to gain admission:

> "I shall obtain admission to the British Moozayum." [001-041]

It is, as he later declares with characteristic intensity, "one of my heartmost dreams of England to find myself in midst of all these leeter-aytchoors" (001-047). The Museum represents for him the institutional face of English learning — a temple of knowledge to which he has aspired from afar.

The admission itself proves to have English complications: Shatov needs a householder's recommendation, and Miriam must explain the peculiarly English requirement that a hotel or boardinghouse keeper will not suffice (001-045).

## Miriam's Museum

For Miriam, the Museum is a space of competence and belonging. It is where she can be the guide rather than the outsider. Leading Shatov through its precincts, she occupies the rare position of insider, introducing him to "the librarian" and navigating among "the readers she knew by sight and who knew her" (002-048).

Yet the visit also exposes the gulf between their perspectives. As they approach, Miriam scans Shatov's appearance anxiously:

> She scanned him as he eagerly talked, looking in vain for the presence she had sat with in the drawing-room; the eyes had come back; but that was all. [002-048]

His "frightful hat peaked down," his gloveless hands "grimed with walking in the winter streets," he eats grapes as he walks, "expelling the skins and flinging them from him" (002-048). The disreputable foreigner does not match the intellectual presence she had imagined presenting to the Museum's hallowed halls.

## A Meeting of Worlds

The Museum sequence crystallizes the novel's interest in cultural encounter. Shatov brings to the Museum a lifetime of distant reverence; Miriam brings the ease of proximity.

Each sees the institution differently — he as symbol, she as familiar ground — and the gap between their perspectives is the gap the novel explores: between English and foreign, between ideal and reality, between the dream of a place and the experience of it.
