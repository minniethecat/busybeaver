## Overview

The commercial traveler occupies a distinctive position in the social world of *Deadlock*. As a type, the commercial traveler is neither working-class nor professional, neither fixed in one place nor fully rootless. In [[mr-rodkin|Mr. Rodkin]], the boardinghouse's longest-standing resident, Richardson creates a figure who embodies this social ambiguity.

## In the Text

Mr. Rodkin is introduced through the crack in the dining-room door: "Mr. Rodkin and his newspapers." The detail is characteristic — Rodkin is defined by his habits, his routines, his steady presence. He is the boardinghouse's institutional memory.

Miriam's perception of Rodkin registers the moral quality of his commercial character:

> "…of kind commercial little Mr. Rodkin. Then there was an answer. Th…" [001-144]

The adjective "kind" precedes "commercial" — the moral quality before the social category — in a characteristic Richardson move that privileges personal warmth over occupational label.

Rodkin's commercial identity is registered in his professional manner: "his abstracted stiff-jointed emergence into the hall with his newspaper, his brilliant-eyed, dried-up laugh, his chuckling assertion, like a lawyer" [002-100]. The comparison to a lawyer is telling — Rodkin performs a kind of professional authority.

## Social Significance

The commercial traveler in Edwardian England occupied an ambiguous class position. Neither an employer nor strictly an employee, mobile yet tied to commercial networks, the traveler inhabited a social space that was difficult to classify. Richardson uses Rodkin to register the variety of working lives that the boardinghouse contains.
