## Thesis

Richardson's narrative method in *Deadlock* is not merely a technical innovation but a philosophical argument. By refusing to separate consciousness from its objects — by rendering thought, perception, and sensation in an unbroken flow — the novel demonstrates that reality is not something perceived by a mind but something constituted through the act of perception. The technique is the thesis.

## Context

*Deadlock* (1921), the fifth volume of Richardson's *Pilgrimage* sequence, was written during the same period that James Joyce was composing *Ulysses* and Virginia Woolf was experimenting with the form that would become *Mrs Dalloway*. Unlike Joyce's mythic scaffolding or Woolf's lyrical orchestration, Richardson's method is radically immersive: the reader is placed inside Miriam Henderson's consciousness and never removed. There is no narrator, no frame, no external vantage. The novel's subject is not what Miriam experiences but *that* she experiences — the fact and texture of consciousness itself.

## Evidence

### Consciousness as the Only Medium

From the novel's opening, consciousness is established as the sole medium of narration. Miriam recovers "a memory of the kind of enquiry" (001-003) that breaks a spell, and her inner world is described as "scenes ranged in her memory" (001-008). There is no transition from an external narrator to an internal monologue — the novel begins already inside, and the reader must orient themselves within a mind that does not explain itself.

This radical interiority means that everything in the novel — people, places, events, ideas — exists only as it is perceived. [[london|London]] is not described as a geographical fact but as a texture of impressions; [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]] is not characterized but accumulated through Miriam's successive encounters with him. The novel's ontology is phenomenological: to be is to be perceived.

### The Collapse of Event and Reflection

Traditional fiction distinguishes between what happens and what a character thinks about what happens. Richardson collapses this distinction. When Miriam sits in the boardinghouse dining-room listening to conversation, the "event" is not the words spoken but the movement of her attention across them — noting this inflection, recalling that earlier exchange, anticipating a response, feeling a shift in atmosphere.

This is most visible in the novel's treatment of [[conversation]], which is not reported as dialogue but rendered as a field of awareness. The dinner table at Tansley Street is not a stage for speech but a site of consciousness, where the "meaning" of an exchange is inseparable from Miriam's experience of it. [[silence]] — the gaps between utterances, the unspoken tensions — carries as much narrative weight as speech itself.

### Memory as the Structure of Experience

The novel's temporal structure is built on [[memory]] rather than chronology. The past is not retrieved through flashback but is continuously present in perception. Experience begins "its life as memory" (003-043), meaning that the full significance of events emerges only in retrospect — and that retrospect is not a separate act but the ongoing texture of consciousness.

This aligns Richardson with Henri Bergson's philosophy of duration (*durée*), in which time is lived as an indivisible flow rather than a sequence of discrete moments. Richardson's prose — with its long sentences, its absence of chapter breaks in the conventional sense, its refusal to punctuate one moment's end and another's beginning — embodies this Bergsonian insight at the level of form.

### Perception as Creation

Perhaps the novel's most radical claim is that [[perception]] is not passive reception but active creation. Miriam does not simply "see" London; she makes it through the quality of her attention. The Bloomsbury Squares are described as "turning into memory" (003-220) even as she walks through them — the act of perceiving transforms the perceived. [[beauty]], likewise, is not a property of objects but an event that occurs between a perceiving consciousness and what it attends to.

This has consequences for the novel's treatment of [[meaning]]. Meaning is not inherent in words, actions, or events; it is conferred by the quality of attention brought to bear. When Miriam struggles to understand what someone "really meant," the struggle is not interpretive but perceptual — she is trying to see more clearly, attend more fully.

## Conclusion

Richardson's narrative method in *Deadlock* is a sustained philosophical achievement. By making consciousness the medium rather than the object of narration, she demonstrates that the distinction between "inner" and "outer" — between mind and world — is not a fact but a convention of representation. The novel does not argue for this claim; it realizes it in the reader's experience. Every sentence of *Deadlock* is an experiment in what it means to be a mind encountering a world — and finding, in that encounter, that mind and world are not two things but one continuous process.
