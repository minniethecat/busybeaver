## Introduction

Gounod's opera _Faust_ is the centerpiece of Chapter XI of _Deadlock_. Miriam Henderson and [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]] attend a performance together, and the opera's themes of knowledge, temptation, and damnation resonate with the novel's philosophical concerns.

## The Performance

Miriam and Shatov attend _Faust_ at a large London theatre. Shatov declares it "perhaps the most beautiful" of operas (011-007), and the performance becomes a shared cultural experience that bridges their different backgrounds. Shatov, drawing on his Russian cultural knowledge, calls [[chaliapin|Chaliapin]] "the only true Mephistopheles in Europe" (011-012).

## Philosophical Resonance

Shatov speaks of "a wonderful philosophy in Faust" (011-012), connecting the opera's metaphysical drama to the philosophical conversations that run through the novel. The Faust legend — the pursuit of knowledge at any cost — mirrors the intellectual ambitions and spiritual questions that preoccupy both Miriam and Shatov.

## Significance

The opera outing represents a high point in Miriam and Shatov's relationship — a shared aesthetic experience that temporarily transcends their cultural and linguistic differences. The choice of _Faust_ is thematically apt: a story about a scholar who makes a pact with the devil in exchange for unlimited knowledge and worldly pleasure.
