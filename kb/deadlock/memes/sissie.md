# Sissie Bailey

**Sissie Bailey** is a minor but carefully observed character in [[miriam-henderson|Miriam Henderson]]'s boarding-house circle. The teenage daughter of [[mrs-bailey|Mrs. Bailey]] and stepdaughter of [[mr-gunner|Mr. Gunner]], Sissie appears throughout the early chapters helping with housework and tea service, her sullen manner betraying a young woman restless within the narrow limits of her domestic situation.

## Role in the Household

Sissie is a constant background presence in the routines of the Tansley Street boarding house. She clears tables, mends linen, and serves as messenger between the lodgers and her mother. Miriam's first encounter with her sets the tone:

> She broke off and turned to see Sissie Bailey, waiting with sullen politeness to speak.
> (001-082)

The phrase "sullen politeness" captures Sissie's double position — obliged to be courteous, unwilling to be enthusiastic. She delivers the news that Mr. Shatov is out, and in her "sullenly worried expression" Miriam reads the Baileys' fear of losing their "helpless new boarder" (001-082). Sissie is already, at her young age, alert to the economic precariousness of the household.

When Miriam finds Sissie alone in the dining-room "mending a tablecloth" (001-083), the image is emblematic: the girl, absorbed in endless domestic repair, occupying the room in the absence of the lodgers whose rent sustains it.

## The Night of Mrs. Bailey's Headache

Sissie's most sustained appearance occurs during the episode of Mrs. Bailey's illness. When Miriam goes downstairs to help, Sissie is the intermediary:

> "Mother's lying down," said Sissie sullenly, "she's got one of her headaches." (001-083)

But when Miriam takes action — suggesting compresses and hot fomentations — Sissie's manner transforms:

> In the dining-room Sissie responded with unconcealed astonishment and gratitude to Miriam's suggestions and bustled off for the needed materials, lingering, when she brought them, to make useful suggestions, affectionately controlling Mrs. Bailey's feeble efforts to help in the arrangements, and staying to supply Miriam's needs, a little compact approving presence.
> (001-087)

The shift from sullenness to bustling competence reveals a girl who, given purpose and direction, becomes capable and even affectionate. Her "unconcealed astonishment" suggests that she has rarely been treated as an ally rather than a subordinate. In the warmth of the sickroom, with the tea things distributed in the dim lamplight, Sissie becomes part of a temporary family gathered around Mrs. Bailey's bed — a rare moment of communal solidarity in the otherwise transactional world of the boarding house (001-091).

## Aspirations and Limitations

Mrs. Bailey's ambitions for her daughter form one of the novel's most explicit statements about class and women's work. Sissie is to learn [[typewriting]] — a marketable skill that would elevate her above domestic service:

> "It's Sissie I worry about," said Mrs. Bailey. "She's like her dear father; keeps herself to herself and goes on; she's a splendid little woman in the house; but I feel she ought to be doing something more."
> (001-120)

Mrs. Bailey's plan is precise: typewriting, then French, then perhaps shorthand. The goal is for Sissie to become "a lady typist with a knowledge of French" — a status that would rank her "higher in the world than a dental secretary" on "fifteen shillings a week" (001-120). The specificity of the economic calculation — the wage, the status differential, the incremental acquisition of skills — reveals the narrow margins within which working-class women's lives were planned.

Miriam's private reflection undercuts the optimism of this plan. She foresees Sissie becoming "one of the increasing army of confident illiterate young women in the city," equipped with formulaic phrases ("yours to hand") and reliable but valued only for her utility (001-120). The gulf between Mrs. Bailey's hopes and Miriam's sharp-eyed assessment is the gulf between maternal love and social realism.

## Character

Sissie is capable — "there's nothing she can't turn her hand to," her mother says, from mending locks to fitting windowpanes "neater than a workman and no mess or fuss" (001-120). But capability within the domestic sphere offers no escape from it. Her sullenness is the visible sign of a consciousness that has not yet found a language for its discontent — a younger, less articulate version of the restlessness that defines Miriam herself.
