## Overview

The title *Deadlock* names more than the novel: it names a condition that pervades its world. Miriam Henderson finds herself in deadlocks of various kinds — between work and vocation, between English reserve and Russian expansiveness, between the desire for connection and the fear of being subsumed by others. The concept of deadlock provides a unifying metaphor for the novel's exploration of impasse as a permanent condition of modern consciousness.

## Forms of Deadlock

The novel presents deadlock in several registers. Socially, Miriam is caught between her working life at Wimpole Street and her intellectual aspirations — neither fully professional nor fully leisured. Culturally, she is drawn to Shatov's Russian world while remaining irreducibly English. Philosophically, the debates between individualism and collective identity, science and religion, reach no resolution — each position is stated, interrogated, and left in productive tension.

The boardinghouse itself is a spatial deadlock: a place of neither full privacy nor full community, where residents are "frozen" in a suspended state between arrival and departure. Miriam's consciousness, rendered through Richardson's stream-of-consciousness method, often circles without resolving — thoughts are pursued to their edge and left hanging, mirroring the structural deadlocks of her situation.

## Deadlock as Narrative Principle

Richardson's refusal to resolve the tensions she sets up — between Shatov and Miriam, between England and Russia, between marriage and independence — is not a failure of plotting but a narrative principle. The novel ends not with resolution but with continuation, suggesting that deadlock is not a problem to be solved but a condition to be inhabited. This open-endedness aligns *Deadlock* with modernist aesthetics, which privilege process over closure and experience over conclusion.
