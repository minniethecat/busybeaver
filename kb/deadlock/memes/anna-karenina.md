## Introduction

Leo Tolstoy's novel _Anna Karenina_ occupies approximately eighteen paragraph-numbers of shared reading between Miriam Henderson and [[mr-shatov|Mr. Shatov]] in Chapter II of _Deadlock_. It is the most sustained engagement with a literary work in the novel.

## The Reading Sessions

Miriam and Shatov read _Anna Karenina_ together as part of Shatov's English lessons. The novel becomes a bridge between their two cultures — a Russian masterpiece being read in English by an Englishwoman and a Russian émigré.

The shared reading creates an intimacy that formal conversation cannot.

## Thematic Connections

_Anna Karenina_ raises questions about marriage, passion, social convention, and moral responsibility — themes that resonate directly with Miriam's own situation as an unmarried woman navigating London's social world. Tolstoy's capacity to render interior consciousness aligns with Richardson's own narrative method.

## Significance

The extended discussion of _Anna Karenina_ represents a high point of intellectual exchange in Miriam and Shatov's relationship. Through Tolstoy's novel, they find a common language for discussing the most intimate human questions — love, duty, happiness, and the possibility of authentic life within social constraints.
