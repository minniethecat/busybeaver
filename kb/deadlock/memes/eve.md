## Overview

Eve appears in *Deadlock* as both a memory from Miriam's past and a living presence in the Tansley Street boarding house. In Chapter I, Miriam recalls being "by Eve's bedside at home" [001-083], linking Eve to an earlier phase of life. In Chapter III, Eve emerges as a central figure in the domestic world Miriam visits — a young woman navigating partnership, motherhood, and shared household life with characteristic emotional expressiveness.

## In the Text

The memory of "Eve's bedside at home" [001-083] establishes a long-standing connection between the two women, suggesting shared girlhood or family history. In Chapter III, Eve appears waiting with Harriett as Miriam arrives [003-006]. Her emotional immediacy is striking: when Miriam arrives, "Eve's face appeared in the crowd" [003-011] and she declares possessively, "'Mine,' said Eve blissfully, 'my own'" [003-012]. Eve's "dark little house" [003-058] contrasts with Harriett's "bright one," and her venture at the boarding house is described as something Miriam had "missed the beginning of" [003-001]. She participates in the household's banter — "Oh of course" [003-028] — and her face reflects the currents of domestic emotion alongside Harriett [003-052].

## Character Perspectives

Eve experiences the boarding house community with intense engagement. Her relationship with Miriam carries the weight of shared history; her bond with Harriett suggests chosen family as much as biological connection. The possessiveness of "'mine, my own'" reveals a character who values connection deeply.

## Analysis

Eve and Harriett together form a composite portrait of feminine domestic community in *Deadlock*. Where Miriam observes and reflects, Eve feels and declares. Richardson uses Eve's emotional directness to dramatize the texture of shared living that Miriam both longs for and remains outside of.
