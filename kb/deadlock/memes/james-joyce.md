## Overview

James Joyce (1882–1941) is referenced in the critical apparatus surrounding "Deadlock" as a defining figure of literary modernism whose experiments with stream of consciousness parallel and contrast with Dorothy Richardson's own narrative innovations. While Joyce does not appear as a character in the novel, his presence as a literary contemporary shapes the context in which Richardson's work has been received and understood.

## Literary Context

Richardson's "Pilgrimage" series, of which "Deadlock" (1921) is the fifth volume, pioneered the stream-of-consciousness technique that Joyce would bring to wider attention with "Ulysses" (1922). Richardson began publishing "Pilgrimage" in 1915, predating both Joyce's "Ulysses" and Woolf's major experimental works. The temporal priority of Richardson's technique has been noted by literary historians, though her influence has often been underacknowledged.

## Critical Reception

The relationship between Richardson and Joyce in modernist studies has been one of both comparison and contrast. Where Joyce's stream of consciousness emphasizes linguistic virtuosity, wordplay, and mythic structure, Richardson's method — which she preferred to call "interior monologue" — emphasizes the granular texture of perceptual experience and the continuous present of feminine consciousness. Virginia Woolf noted of Richardson that she had invented "a sentence which we might call the psychological sentence of the feminine gender."
