## Overview

Elspeth is the small child of Gerald and Harriett who appears in the Tansley Street boardinghouse. She is described with attentive physical detail — her "silky rounded golden crown," her "tiny hand on the edge of the table," her "little busy bustling expression" (003-020) — and her presence creates moments of unguarded tenderness in the novel's adult world of intellectual debate and social tension.

## In the Text

Elspeth enters the narrative when Miriam is staying at Tansley Street. She trots into rooms "carefully closing the door with her body, her steady eyes taking in the disposition of affairs" (003-044). Her fascination with the gramophone — standing on tiptoe and waving "a peremptory hand towards the gramophone" (003-048) — provides one of the novel's moments of pure comic observation. Miriam is drawn to her, "delighting in the firm clutch of the tiny hand," finding in Elspeth a presence that exists outside the fraught negotiations of adult relationships.

Richardson's rendering of Elspeth is notable for its resistance to sentimentalizing childhood. Elspeth is observed with the same precision Miriam brings to all her perceptions — "The closest observation revealed no point of arrest. Her undivided impression once caught, could be recovered in each separate feature" (003-048). She is both a real child and, for Miriam, an object of aesthetic contemplation.
