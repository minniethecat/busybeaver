## Overview

Individualism is the philosophical position most closely associated with Miriam Henderson in "Deadlock." Her insistence on the primacy of individual experience, her resistance to social categories and collective identities, and her defense of personal freedom all stem from a deep-seated individualism that the novel both celebrates and interrogates.

## In the Text

The question of individualism is raised directly by Mr. Shatov, who suggests that Miriam is "too individualistic" (006-041). His characterization — offered during a heated conversation in Regent's Park — provokes Miriam's immediate defensive response. She demands "angrily... how it was possible to be too individualistic" (006-042), revealing how central this value is to her self-understanding.

Mr. Shatov elaborates: "A man is first himself. But the peril is of being cut off from his fellow creatures" (006-043). This warning — that individualism risks isolation — articulates the tension that runs throughout the novel. Miriam's fierce independence coexists with genuine loneliness, and her most valued freedom brings with it a "feeling of meanness" (001-026).

The debate extends to political philosophy. Miriam challenges: "Are you a socialist? Do you believe in the opinions of mediocre majorities?" (006-044). Her identification of collective opinion with mediocrity reflects a thoroughgoing individualism that distrusts any subordination of individual judgment to group consensus.

## The Collective Pressure of Life

The intellectual stakes of Miriam's individualism become clearer in a later exchange, where Shatov presses the question from a new angle. "It is the collective pressure of life; an unseen movement. But if you feel this what now becomes of your individualism? Eh?" He chuckles with delight at having found what he takes to be a fatal counter-argument — the claim that social forces shape individual experience so thoroughly that the very idea of the autonomous self is called into question.

Miriam's response is immediate and unrepentant: "But he must be an individual to be affected at all, and no two people are affected in the same way… after this evening I'm more of an individualist than before." The admission — that a conversation explicitly aimed at challenging her individualism has only strengthened it — is telling. For Miriam, the very fact that no two people experience the "collective pressure" identically is proof of the irreducible primacy of individual consciousness.

She goes further, connecting individualism to epistemology: "It is _relief_ to know that science is a smaller kind of truth than philosophy." Her individualist conviction is not merely a temperamental preference but a philosophical position — one that subordinates instrumental, collective knowledge to the personal truths revealed through lived experience and reflection.

## Character Perspectives

**Miriam Henderson**: Holds individualism as a near-absolute value, rooted in her experience of consciousness as irreducibly personal. "A man is first himself" (006-043) — she agrees with the premise but rejects the warning.

**Mr. Shatov**: Believes in "the _race_" (006-045) as a corrective to pure individualism. His perspective, shaped by Jewish collective identity and Russian intellectual traditions, provides the novel's most articulate challenge to Miriam's individualism.

## Individualism and the Novel's Form

Richardson's stream-of-consciousness method is itself an argument for individualism. By restricting the narrative perspective almost entirely to Miriam's interior experience, the novel formally enacts the primacy of individual consciousness that Miriam defends in argument. The reader never receives an omniscient narrator's confirmation or correction — all knowledge is filtered through a single mind. This formal choice makes the novel's treatment of individualism more than thematic: it is structural, a commitment built into the very mode of narration.

## Analysis

Richardson's treatment of individualism in "Deadlock" is characteristically nuanced. The novel endorses Miriam's resistance to social conformity while acknowledging the genuine perils of isolation that Mr. Shatov identifies. The stream-of-consciousness method itself is a formal argument for individualism — it insists on the primacy of individual experience — while the novel's social content explores what is lost when individuals cannot connect.
