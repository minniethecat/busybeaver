**Athenian democracy** serves as Mill's primary classical reference throughout *Considerations on Representative Government*. He invokes Athenian institutions repeatedly — as evidence of sound constitutional design, as a measure of civic virtue, and as a contrast to modern [[democracy|representative government]].

## As a Standard of Civic Excellence

Mill holds up Athens as an exemplar of the educative potential of participatory government.

He observes that the "intellectual standard of an average Athenian citizen" far exceeded that of other ancient peoples — a result, he implies, of the participatory character of Athenian institutions (003-024). This observation supports Mill's broader argument that political participation cultivates intelligence and virtue.

## Constitutional Precautions

Mill finds in the Athenian Constitution evidence for the universality of certain constitutional principles. When arguing that even well-constructed democracies require precautions against the abuse of power, he notes:

> "Such precautions have existed in all well-constructed democracies. The Athenian Constitution had many such provisions, and so has that of the United States." (005-004)

The parallel Mill draws between ancient Athens and the modern [[united-states|United States]] underscores his conviction that sound constitutional design transcends historical periods.

## The Separation of Legislation from Popular Assembly

One of Mill's most important institutional arguments draws directly on Athenian practice. He proposes that law-making be entrusted to a small, expert commission rather than to the popular assembly — and cites Athens as precedent:

> "The necessity of some provision corresponding to this was felt even in the Athenian Democracy, where, in the time of its most complete ascendancy, the popular Ecclesia could pass psephisms (mostly decrees on single matters of policy), but laws, so called, could only be made or altered by a different and less numerous body, renewed annually, called the Nomothetae, whose duty it also was to revise the whole of the laws, and keep them consistent with one another." (005-014)

This distinction — between the popular ecclesia that voiced the people's will and the expert nomothetae that drafted legislation — is Mill's template for the proper division between a [[parliament|representative assembly]] and a legislative commission.

## Administrative Limitations

Mill also notes that the Athenians understood the limits of popular administrative competence.

Unlike modern representative assemblies that meddle in administration, "the Athenians did not confide responsible administrative functions to the popular ecclesia" (007-015), reserving such tasks for accountable officials.

## The Secret Ballot

In discussing the [[ballot|secret ballot]], Mill observes that "even in the least imperfect of the Greek Oligarchies, the ballot had a beneficial operation" (010-006), suggesting that certain institutional mechanisms have enduring value across different constitutional forms.
