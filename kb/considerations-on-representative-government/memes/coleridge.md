**Samuel Taylor Coleridge** (1772–1834) is cited by Mill in Chapter II of *Considerations on Representative Government* as the originator of the distinction between "Permanence" and "Progression" as the two fundamental exigencies of society — a classification Mill regards as "unscientific and incorrect."

## Mill's Critique of the Coleridgean Framework

In Chapter II, Mill engages with the classification of social exigencies into "Order and Progress" (the phraseology of French thinkers) or "Permanence and Progression" (Coleridge's formulation). Mill acknowledges the distinction's appeal but rejects it as inadequate:

> The classification begins and ends with a partition of the exigencies of society between the two heads of Order and Progress (in the phraseology of French thinkers); Permanence and Progression, in the words of Coleridge. This division is plausible and seductive, from the apparently clean-cut opposition between its two members, and the remarkable difference between the sentiments to which they appeal. But I apprehend that (however admissible for purposes of popular discourse) the distinction between Order, or Permanence and Progress, employed to define the qualities necessary in a government, is unscientific and incorrect.
>
> (002-004)

Mill's central objection was that "the agencies which tend to preserve the social good which already exists are the very same which promote the increase of it." Order and Progress, properly understood, are not opposing principles requiring separate institutional provisions but are satisfied by the same conditions — the primary difference being "that a greater degree of those agencies is required for the latter purpose than for the former" (002-008). Progress, in Mill's reformulation, "includes Order, but Order does not include Progress" (002-014).

## Coleridge on Character and Motive

Mill also invokes Coleridge in Chapter VI to support a psychological point about the relationship between character and self-interest. When discussing the danger of class legislation in a democracy, Mill quotes Coleridge's observation about the primacy of character over circumstance in determining human motivation:

> As Coleridge observes, the man makes the motive, not the motive the man. What it is the man's interest to do or refrain from depends less on any outward circumstances than upon what sort of man he is. If you wish to know what is practically a man's interest, you must know the cast of his habitual feelings and thoughts.
>
> (006-016)

This reinforces Mill's argument that simply demonstrating the irrationality of class legislation is insufficient to prevent it — the character of the ruling power matters more than rational calculation of interests.
