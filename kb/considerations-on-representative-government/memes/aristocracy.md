**Aristocracy**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, appears in two distinct but related senses: as a [[government|form of government]] in which a hereditary or wealth-based elite holds political power, and as a **social class** whose interests may conflict with those of the general community. Mill's analysis is comparative — he weighs the claims made for aristocratic government against the evidence of history, and finds both strengths and fatal weaknesses.

Mill treats aristocracy not as an antiquarian curiosity but as a living political force. The British constitution of his day was, in his view, a mixed system in which aristocratic influence remained powerful — and his analysis of its effects is central to his argument for [[representative-government|representative]] reform.

## Aristocracy as a Form of Government

Mill classifies aristocracy alongside monarchy and democracy as one of the three pure forms of government discussed in traditional political theory. But he is skeptical of the classical classification:

> "Governments — pure monarchy, pure aristocracy, pure democracy — such maxims are not merely of no service in establishing good government, but are often an actual hindrance" (012-003)

The theoretical claim for aristocracy is that it concentrates political power in the hands of those with the greatest stake in social stability and the greatest leisure for public affairs. Mill acknowledges a partial truth in this argument but identifies a critical defect:

| Claim for Aristocracy | Mill's Assessment |
|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Stability of interest | The aristocratic class has interests distinct from, and often opposed to, the general welfare (006-013) |
| Superior wisdom | "The Roman aristocracy escaped this characteristic disease" only because of its unique openness to talent (006-009) |
| Public-spiritedness | An open aristocracy "far surpasses democracy" in certain practical capacities, but a closed one degenerates (006-006) |

## The Character of Aristocratic Rule

Mill devotes significant attention to the psychological and cultural effects of aristocratic government. Drawing on historical examples, he argues that aristocracy shapes the character of both rulers and subjects in distinctive ways:

> "The government of the best, in idea, would be an aristocracy of merit; but aristocracy, as a government, means the government of the rich" (006-007)

The practical operation of aristocracy, in Mill's analysis, depends heavily on whether it is "open" or "closed":

1. **Open aristocracy**: A ruling class that continuously admits new members of talent from below — like "the Stewarts and the Peels of the representative aristocracy of Great Britain" — can maintain vigour and adaptability (006-008).
2. **Closed aristocracy**: A hereditary caste that excludes newcomers inevitably decays — "whether the work is done by them or by a bureaucracy, the result is the same" — stagnation and loss of public spirit (006-009).

## Aristocracy versus Bureaucracy

Mill draws a striking parallel between aristocratic government and bureaucratic administration. Both concentrate power in a permanent, specialised governing class, and both suffer from similar defects:

> "The representative democracy and a bureaucracy have nothing in common; all other governments may be classed as either bureaucratic or popular" (006-009)

This passage is significant because Mill identifies the essential distinction in government as being not between monarchy, aristocracy, and democracy, but between systems in which the governing function is monopolised by a distinct class (whether aristocratic or bureaucratic) and systems in which the people participate in their own government.

> "The work of government has been in the hands of governors by profession; which is the essence and meaning of bureaucracy" (006-008)

## Aristocracy and the Narrowing of Sympathies

One of Mill's deepest criticisms of aristocratic government concerns its effect on moral imagination. A governing class insulated from the experience of ordinary life cannot understand the interests of those it governs:

| Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Class interest dominates | "The interest of the monarch, or the interest of the aristocracy, either collective or that of its individual members, is promoted" (006-013) |
| Sympathy contracts | The governing class judges all policy by how it affects their own station |
| Resistance to reform | Aristocratic institutions "are thought to contrast most favourably" with democracy because of their stability — but this stability is purchased at the cost of stagnation (006-005) |

> "In the case of a monarchy or an aristocracy, the governing power is always the interest of a class" — this is the structural defect that no amount of personal virtue can overcome (006-013)

## Aristocracy in Mixed Government

Mill does not dismiss aristocracy entirely. His objection is to aristocracy *as* government, not to the presence of aristocratic elements *within* a [[representation|representative system]]. In fact, he values the "steadiness and prudence" that an aristocratic temper can bring to public deliberation (006-005).

The problem arises only when aristocratic influence becomes dominant rather than contributory. In the British system of his day, Mill saw the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] as an institutionalised aristocratic check on democratic legislation — a role he accepted in principle while criticising its hereditary basis. The solution, he implies, is not to abolish the second chamber but to reconstitute it on a non-hereditary, merit-based foundation.

This distinction — between aristocracy as a component and aristocracy as a governing class — is essential to understanding why Mill is neither a simple democrat nor an aristocratic sympathiser, but an advocate of balanced institutions in which no single [[sinister-interest|class interest]] can dominate.
