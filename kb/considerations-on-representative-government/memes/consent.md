**Consent**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, is the foundational principle of [[freedom|free government]] — the criterion that distinguishes representative institutions from [[despotism]]. Yet Mill's treatment of consent is notably unsentimental. He does not invoke consent as a simple moral axiom; instead, he analyses the conditions under which consent is meaningful, the institutional mechanisms by which it is expressed, and the limits of consent as a guarantee of good government.

Mill's approach to consent is shaped by his rejection of both the social-contract tradition and the [[jeremy-bentham|Benthamite]] reduction of government to habit and coercion. Consent is real and important, but it must be institutionally specified — a people may "consent" to a despotism out of habit or fear, and that consent does not make the despotism legitimate. The question is not *whether* the governed consent but *under what conditions* their consent is free, informed, and effective (004-008).

## The Distinction Between Consent and Habit

Mill opens the *Considerations* with a discussion of the grounds of political obligation, and consent plays a central role in his argument. But he immediately draws a distinction that complicates any simple appeal to consent as the basis of legitimacy:

> "Consenting unto his death" — the reference to the stoning of Stephen establishes that even active consent can be given under conditions that render it normatively meaningless (001-016)

The point is not that consent is irrelevant, but that consent alone is insufficient. The consent that matters — consent that establishes legitimate government — must meet certain conditions:

1. **Knowledge**: The consenting party must understand what is being consented to.
2. **[[freedom|Freedom]]**: The consent must not be coerced or given under conditions of dependency.
3. **Continuity**: Consent must be renewable, not a one-time grant.

> "Government by general consent, or at least, the sanction of the community" is the distinguishing mark of free institutions (004-008)

## Consent and the Abdication of Political Will

One of Mill's most striking arguments concerns the phenomenon of *voluntary* submission to despotism. A people may, through habit, "consent" to their own political nullification:

> "Nothing can hardly be imagined as consenting to undertake it unless as the result of a long habit" — the people accept despotism because they have never known anything else (003-002)

This is a profound challenge to consent-based theories of legitimacy. If a people can consent to despotism — and Mill believes they can and historically have — then consent cannot be the sole criterion of legitimate government. Some additional criterion is needed to distinguish free consent from the mere habituation to power.

Mill's solution is characteristically institutional. Consent is not a single act but an ongoing process, and the institutions that structure consent determine its quality:

| Form of Government | Quality of Consent |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| [[despotism|Despotism]] | Consent is "the result of a long habit" — passive, unreflective (003-002) |
| Limited monarchy | Consent is formal but constrained — "their minds are formed by, and consenting to, this abdication" (003-003) |
| Representative government | Consent is active, informed, and renewable — exercised through [[election|elections]], public deliberation, and institutional checks |

> "Their minds are formed by, and consenting to, this abdication of their own will" (003-003)

The phrase "minds are formed by" is crucial. Mill is describing a feedback loop: institutions shape the minds of those who live under them, and those minds in turn consent to the institutions that shaped them. Breaking this loop — creating the conditions for genuinely free consent — is, in Mill's view, one of the central challenges of political construction.

## Consent in Despotism

Mill's most subtle argument about consent appears in his discussion of despotism. Even despotism, he argues, depends on a form of consent — the despot's willingness *not* to exercise his full power:

> "In so far as the despotism consents not to be despotism; in so far as the ruling power refrains from pressing its authority to the utmost" (003-004)

| Mode of Government | Relationship to Consent |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| Pure despotism | No consent — all power, all the time |
| Mitigated despotism | The despot "consents not to be despotism" — self-limiting (003-004) |
| Tutelary despotism | Consent is suspended but aimed at — the despot governs in the interest of the governed until they are capable of self-government |
| Representative government | Consent is institutionalised — the governed actively participate in their own government |

This passage is remarkable for its inversion of the usual relationship between ruler and ruled. Consent, here, is not something the *governed* give to the *ruler*; it is something the *ruler* gives to the *governed* — a self-limitation, a restraint on the exercise of power. Mill's point is that even despotism, to be tolerable, must incorporate an element of consent — not the consent of the governed (which is absent by definition) but the self-restraint of the governor.

> "The necessity of obtaining the consent of both to the passing of any measure" — the institutionalisation of consent in bicameral government (013-002)

The transition from the despot's voluntary self-restraint to the institutional requirement of consent is, in Mill's framework, the history of [[freedom|free government]]. What begins as an act of grace by the ruler becomes a right of the ruled.

## Consent and the Franchise

Mill's analysis of consent connects directly to his arguments about the [[franchise]]. The vote is the institutional mechanism by which consent is expressed and renewed:

> "He needed nobody's consent to become a public adviser" (007-013)

This observation — made in the context of Mill's argument for proportional representation — cuts against a simplistic equation of consent with majority rule. An [[election|electoral system]] in which a majority can impose its will without the consent of minorities is, in Mill's analysis, a system in which consent is incomplete. The majority has consented to its own rule, but the minority has not consented to be ruled by the majority — and their [[rights|electoral rights]], while formally present, are substantively null.

> "All it is asked for is its consent, and the sole power it possesses is that of withholding it" (005-007)

The withholding of consent — the power to say no — is, for Mill, the irreducible minimum of [[freedom|free government]]. A representative body that cannot refuse its consent to measures it judges harmful is not a check on power but an instrument of it. This is why Mill places such emphasis on the institutional mechanisms by which consent is registered: the [[ballot]], the [[election]], the legislative vote, the popular [[participation|participation]] in public affairs.

> "He should be told what for; to have his consent asked, and his opinion counted for what it is worth" (008-003)

Consent, in this formulation, is inseparable from deliberation. The governed must not only consent but understand what they are consenting to — and their consent must be informed by the knowledge that their "opinion counts."

## The Limits of Consent

Mill is clear-eyed about the limits of consent as a guarantee of good government. Consent can be manufactured, manipulated, or simply irrelevant to the quality of the decisions that follow:

| Limit of Consent | Mill's Response |
|------------------|----------------|
| Consent may be based on ignorance | Require informed deliberation before consent is given (008-003) |
| Consent may be coerced by social pressure | Protect the [[ballot]] to ensure consent is freely given |
| Consent may be habitual rather than active | Design institutions that require active engagement, not passive acquiescence (003-002) |
| Consent cannot guarantee good outcomes | Supplement consent with competence — expert administration insulated from electoral pressure (014-011) |
| A majority may consent to oppress a minority | Institutionalise [[minority|minority representation]] so that consent must be genuinely general (007-008) |

> "They should consent to be governed according to that higher knowledge" — the willing submission of the less informed to the more informed is, for Mill, a form of rational consent (012-006)

This passage reveals the complexity of Mill's position. Consent does not mean that every citizen must independently arrive at every political judgment. It means that the institutional framework within which judgments are made must itself be the object of general consent. The citizen consents to be governed by representative institutions — and that consent authorises the representative to exercise judgment on the citizen's behalf. But this authorisation is always conditional, always revocable, and always dependent on the representative's continued demonstration of superior competence.
