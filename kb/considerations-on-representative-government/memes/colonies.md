**Colonies**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, are treated as a special case of government — distinct both from the self-governing metropolis and from purely despotic dependencies. Mill's analysis of colonial governance, concentrated in Chapter XVIII ("Of the Government of Dependencies by a Free State"), draws on his extensive practical experience as a senior administrator in the [[east-india-company|East India Company]] and reflects his attempt to reconcile liberal principles with the realities of imperial rule.

Mill's position on colonies is among the most controversial aspects of the *Considerations* for modern readers. He defends the legitimacy of colonial governance while insisting that it must be directed toward the eventual [[self-government]] of the colonised — a position that combines liberal imperialism with a developmental theory of political capacity. The colonies are to be governed, but governed *for* their inhabitants, not *for* the benefit of the colonising power (018-004).

## The Justification of Colonial Governance

Mill opens his discussion of dependencies with a distinction between two kinds of imperial relationship: the relationship between a free state and its "dependencies" (colonies of settlement or conquest), and the relationship between member states in a [[federal|federal union]]. Colonies are not provinces — they are distinct political communities whose relationship to the metropolitan power must be justified on grounds that are consistent with the principles of [[freedom|free government]]:

> "Colonies as valuable by affording markets for our commodities" — Mill rejects the commercial justification for empire as insufficient (018-004)

| Justification for Colonial Rule | Mill's Assessment |
|---------------------------------|-------------------|
| Commercial benefit to the metropolis | Rejected — "a mode of thinking which belongs to the infancy of political economy" (018-004) |
| Strategic advantage | Acknowledged but insufficient alone |
| Civilising mission | Accepted with qualifications — the metropolis has a duty to prepare colonies for self-government |
| Protection of the colonised | Genuinely important — but often used as a pretext for exploitation |

> "Which regarded colonies as valuable by affording markets for our commodities, that could be dealt with in no other way" — this is the commercial theory of empire that Mill dismisses as primitive (018-004)

## Colonies and the Principle of Self-Government

Mill's central argument about colonies is that the ultimate goal of colonial governance must be the self-government of the colonised. Colonial rule is legitimate only as a transitional arrangement — a period of tutelage during which the colonised acquire the capacities for [[freedom|self-rule]]:

> "The colonies would be in a much stronger, as well as more dignified, position if they were states completely self-governing" (018-006)

| Stage of Colonial Development | Appropriate Form of Governance |
|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Early settlement / conquest | Direct rule by the metropolitan power — the colonised lack the capacity for self-government |
| Development of local institutions | Increasing participation — the colonised begin to exercise political functions |
| Maturity — capacity for self-government demonstrated | Full self-government — the colonial relationship is dissolved or transformed into a [[federal|federal union]] |

The difficulty, as Mill recognises, is that the transition from the first stage to the third is rarely smooth. The metropolitan power has incentives to delay the transition — colonial possessions confer prestige, commercial advantage, and career opportunities for metropolitan administrators. The colonised, meanwhile, may demand self-government before they have developed the capacities that make it workable — leading to a choice between premature independence (which may fail) and continued subordination (which breeds resentment).

> "She ought to consent to their separation, should the time come when, after full freedom of discussion, they deliberately desire it" (018-007)

## The Dangers of Colonial Misgovernment

Mill is sharply critical of the ways in which colonial governance typically fails. The metropolitan power, even when well-intentioned, governs in ignorance of local conditions and is subject to pressures that systematically distort colonial policy:

> "It seems to them monstrous that any rights of the natives should stand in the way of their acquiring wealth" (018-015)

| Form of Colonial Misgovernment | Mechanism |
|--------------------------------|-----------|
| Exploitation of native populations | Settlers treat native rights as obstacles to their own enrichment (018-015) |
| Governance by amateurs | Colonial administrators lack local knowledge and rotate too frequently |
| Policy determined by metropolitan politics | "The country decides, both for the colonies and for herself, on questions of colonial government" — without colonial input (018-008) |
| "[[parliament|Parliament]]ary jobbing" | Colonial appointments become patronage rather than merit-based |

> "Why does no one ever hear a colonial governor express an opinion?" — because the governor is accountable to the metropolitan government, not to the governed (018-009)

This observation is characteristic of Mill's institutional analysis. The problem with colonial governance is not primarily that colonial administrators are bad people — it is that the institutional structure of colonial rule severs the normal connection between governors and governed. The colonial governor is appointed by and accountable to the metropolitan government, not to the people he governs. His incentives are therefore to satisfy his metropolitan masters, not to serve the interests of the colonial population.

## The East India Company Model

Mill's positive model for colonial governance is drawn from his own experience in the [[east-india-company|East India Company]] — an institution he defends at length in the *Considerations* and elsewhere:

> "If, as in some of our colonies, there are scarcely any fit persons for the representative body except officers of the government" — this is the problem of capacity that the Company model was designed to address (010-018)

The [[east-india-company|East India Company]], in Mill's account, combined several institutional virtues that are absent from ordinary colonial governance:

1. **Professional administration**: Company officials were career administrators with deep local knowledge, not political appointees serving short terms.
2. **Insulation from [[party|party politics]]**: The Company's government was "kept out of the vortex of party and [[parliament|Parliament]]ary jobbing" (018-019).
3. **Accountability to expertise**: The Company's Court of Directors and Board of Control provided oversight by persons with relevant knowledge.
4. **Gradual devolution**: The Company's system allowed for the progressive transfer of functions to [[india-british-governance|India]]n officials as they developed competence.

Mill's defence of the Company model is not a defence of permanent imperial rule — it is a defence of *competent* administration during the period of tutelage that precedes self-government. The Company, in his view, governed [[india-british-governance|India]] better than either the British [[parliament|Parliament]] (which lacked relevant knowledge) or an immediately self-governing [[india-british-governance|India]]n state (which lacked relevant capacity) could have done.

## Colonies and the Future of Empire

Mill's concluding reflections on colonies are forward-looking. He envisions a future in which the colonial relationship is transformed — not into independence in every case, but into a [[federal|federal union]] of self-governing communities:

> "A great improvement if each party understood and acted upon its real interests" — the metropolitan power should recognise that its interest lies in developing, not exploiting, its colonies (018-025)

The ultimate horizon of Mill's colonial thought is not empire but [[federal|federation]] — a union of equal, self-governing communities that share common institutions for common purposes while retaining autonomy in local affairs. This is the same federal model that he analyses in Chapter XVII in the context of the United States, and he sees it as the natural end-state of the colonial relationship — if both parties can rise above short-term interests to pursue their long-term common good.
