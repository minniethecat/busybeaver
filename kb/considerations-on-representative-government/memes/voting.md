**Voting** is the individual act through which citizens participate in [[representation|representative government]]. For Mill, the question of who should vote and how their votes should be weighted is central to the design of a good political system. "Political machinery does not act of itself — it needs, not their simple acquiescence, but their [[participation|active participation]]" (001-006). The [[franchise|suffrage]] is both a right and an instrument of [[education|public education]].

## The Right to Vote

Mill articulates a principled basis for the [[suffrage|franchise]] grounded in individual interest and obligation:

| Principle | Justification | Key PN |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Interest-based claim | Every person "directly interested, and not under positive tutelage, has an admitted claim to a voice" (008-008) |
| Reciprocity of obligation | If a person "is compelled to pay, if he may be compelled to fight, if he is required implicitly to obey, he should be legally entitled to be told what for" (008-003) |
| Educative function | [[participation|Participation]] in public affairs educates "the intelligence and of the sentiments" of even "the very lowest ranks" (008-002) |

> "It is a personal injustice to withhold from any one, unless for the prevention of greater evils, the ordinary privilege of having his voice reckoned in the disposal of affairs in which he has the same interest as other people" (008-003)

## Qualifications and Exclusions

Mill supports [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] in principle but identifies specific exclusions:

| Exclusion | Rationale | Key PN |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Illiteracy | "Wholly inadmissible that any person should participate in the [[franchise|suffrage]] without being able to read, write, and perform the common operations of arithmetic" (008-004) |
| Tax non-payment | Those "who pay no taxes, disposing by their votes of other people's money, have every motive to be lavish" (008-005) |
| Parish relief recipients | One "who can not by his labor suffice for his own support has no claim to the privilege of helping himself to the money of others" (008-006) |

Mill's exclusions are not class-based restrictions but functional thresholds: "in the long run, we might expect that all, except that progressively diminishing class, the recipients of parish relief, would be in possession of votes" (008-007).

## Plural Voting

Mill's most distinctive contribution to voting theory is his advocacy of **[[plurality|plural voting]]** — granting additional votes to citizens whose education or occupation indicates superior judgment:

- **Not property-based**: "I consider it entirely inadmissible, unless as a temporary makeshift, that the superiority of influence should be conferred in consideration of property" (008-010).
- **[[education|Education]] and occupation as criteria**: The test should be "education, which is a much better test" — not of wealth but of "mental superiority" (008-010).
- **Transitional measure**: Plural voting is a bridge between restricted and [[suffrage|universal suffrage]]; "the time will certainly arrive when the only choice will be between this and [[universal-suffrage|equal universal suffrage]]" (008-012).

> "In all human affairs, every person directly interested, and not under positive tutelage, has an admitted claim to a voice, and when any election is to be made to discharge a trust, his opinion should be counted for as much as that of any other person in whose honesty and good sense he has equal confidence" (008-008)

## Voting and Taxation

Mill draws a direct link between the [[suffrage|franchise]] and fiscal responsibility: "the assembly which votes the taxes, either general or local, should be elected exclusively by those who pay something towards the taxes imposed" (008-005). This severs "the power of control from the interest in it" — a violation of the "fundamental principle of [[freedom|free government]]" (008-005).
