**Plurality of votes** — often called **plural voting** — is Mill's proposal for assigning additional electoral weight to citizens who, by reason of occupation, education, or demonstrated capacity, possess superior judgment on public questions. It is one of the most controversial elements of Mill's political theory, and one he defends not as an end in itself but as a **remedy** for the tendency of pure democracy to produce a representative body of "a low grade of intelligence" (007-001).

Mill's argument for plurality is carefully bounded. He does not propose it as a permanent feature of all [[representation|representative system]]s, nor does he advocate it on grounds of wealth or social status. His case is instrumental: if the [[parliament|representative assembly]] is to perform its deliberative function well, it must contain a sufficient number of members with the knowledge and judgment to resist the pressure of [[sinister-interest|class interest]] and popular prejudice. Plurality is a mechanism for securing that result.

## The Principle: Superiority of Intelligence

Mill grounds the case for plurality in a principle he regards as fundamental to [[representative-democracy|representative government]]: that the opinion of the more knowledgeable should carry greater weight than that of the less knowledgeable:

> "If it be asked to what length the principle admits of being carried, or how many votes might be accorded to an individual on the ground of superior qualifications, I answer, that this is not in itself very material, provided the distinctions and gradations are not made arbitrarily, but are such as can be understood and accepted by the general conscience and understanding" (008-011)

The key constraint is that the gradations must be **intelligible** and **acceptable** to the general public. Mill is not proposing a technocratic scheme imposed from above; the criteria for plurality must command the assent of those who will not benefit from them. This is a demanding condition, and Mill is aware that it limits how far the principle can be pushed.

> "The plurality of votes must on no account be carried so far that those who are privileged by it, or the class (if any) to which they mainly belong, shall outweigh by means of it all the rest of the community" (008-011)

This is the essential limiting principle: plurality may give additional weight to the wise, but it must never allow them to **outvote** the rest of the community. The purpose is to inject intelligence into the deliberative process, not to transfer power from the many to the few.

## The Property Question

Mill is emphatic that plurality should not be based on property, despite property serving as a rough proxy for education in most societies. His reasoning is characteristically nuanced:

> "I consider it entirely inadmissible, unless as a temporary makeshift, that the superiority of influence should be conferred in consideration of property. I do not deny that property is a kind of test; education, in most countries, though any thing but proportional to riches, is on the average better in the richer half of society than in the poorer. But the criterion is so imperfect; accident has so much more to do than merit with enabling men to rise in the world" (008-010)

The objection is both moral and practical. Morally, property is an "imperfect" proxy for the qualities that justify extra electoral weight — it measures luck as much as merit. Practically, connecting plurality to wealth would make it "supremely odious" and would undermine the public acceptance on which the system depends (008-010).

Instead, Mill proposes that plurality be attached to **occupational and educational qualifications** — the learned professions, those who have passed certain examinations, and those whose work demonstrates "superiority of means" rather than merely "superiority of means than of intelligence" (008-012). The distinction is subtle but important: the employer who commands capital may qualify, but only if his occupation also demonstrates superior judgment, not merely superior wealth.

## Plurality and Minority Representation

Mill presents plurality as a complement to, not a substitute for, the scheme of [[minority|proportional representation]] he advocates. Even with [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s system ensuring that minorities are represented, there remains the problem that the *quality* of deliberation in the representative body may be low if all members are chosen by voters of average intelligence:

> "I do not propose the plurality as a thing in itself undesirable, but as a means of preventing the complete exclusion of superior minds from the representative body" (008-014)

Plurality and [[minority|minority representation]] address different defects of simple majority rule. [[minority|Minority]] representation ensures that all significant opinion-groups are present in the assembly; plurality ensures that within those groups, the most capable individuals have a better chance of being selected. The two mechanisms together form Mill's comprehensive response to the "danger of a low grade of intelligence in the representative body" (007-001).

| Mechanism | Problem Addressed | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Proportional representation ([[thomas-hare|Hare]]) | Exclusion of minorities | Every substantial opinion-group elects its share of representatives |
| Plurality of votes | Low average quality of representatives | Superior electors carry more weight in selecting representatives |
| Open [[voting|ballot]] (in some contexts) | Voter dependence on patrons | Ensures public accountability for the vote |

## The Limits and Dangers of Plurality

Mill acknowledges that plurality carries its own risks. If the criteria for extra votes are poorly designed, or if they are too extensive, plurality could create a new form of class government — rule by the educated few rather than the many:

1. **Risk of class domination**: If plurality-holders can outvote the rest, "the distinctive evil" of class government would be replicated (008-011).
2. **Risk of social resentment**: If the criteria are perceived as arbitrary or unjust, plurality would "create a privileged class" and provoke reaction (008-010).
3. **Risk of ossification**: If the criteria are fixed and cannot adapt to changing social conditions, plurality would entrench one generation's idea of intelligence against the next.

These risks explain why Mill treats plurality as a device to be used sparingly and with constant attention to its effects. It is a mechanism of **balance**, not of hierarchy — a way to ensure that "superior intellect" has a voice without giving it a veto (012-006).
