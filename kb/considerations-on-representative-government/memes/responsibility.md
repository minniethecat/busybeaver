In Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government*, **responsibility** is a foundational principle of good executive administration. Mill argues that for [[representative-government|representative government]] to function properly, every act must be traceable to a specific individual who bears the full consequences — praise or blame — for its outcome. This principle stands in opposition to rule by boards or committees, where collective decision-making dilutes or annihilates personal accountability.

## Definition and Core Principle

Mill defines responsibility as a personal, undivided obligation attaching to every [[executive]] function, whether superior or subordinate. The governing maxim is that "it should be apparent to all the world who did every thing, and through whose default any thing was left undone" (014-002). Responsibility, in Mill's view, is nullified when nobody knows who is responsible, and even when real, it is weakened by division.

Mill distinguishes between two modes of sharing responsibility: one that merely enfeebles it, and another that destroys it entirely. Responsibility is enfeebled when multiple functionaries must concur on the same act — each still bears some real responsibility, but the penalties of opinion are "always diminished by being shared" (014-002).

## Boards as the Negation of Responsibility

Mill's most forceful critique of collective administration centres on boards. When a board deliberates behind closed doors, with no record of individual votes, responsibility becomes "a mere name" (014-003). He approvingly quotes Bentham's maxim:

> "Boards are screens."
>
> (014-003)

What a board does, Mill argues, is the act of nobody, and nobody can be made to answer for it. The board suffers only in its collective character, and individual members feel no personal stake in the outcome. Mill concludes that "Boards, therefore, are not a fit instrument for executive business" (014-003), admitting them only when giving full discretionary power to a single minister would be even worse.

## Individual vs. Collective Responsibility

Mill contrasts individual responsibility favourably against all forms of collective or shared accountability. He observes that a single minister, bearing "undivided moral responsibility," has the strongest possible motive to appoint fit persons and to oversee their conduct diligently (005-011). A joint-stock company, by comparison, is "better conducted under the responsibility of one" than under a divided command (005-008).

| Form of responsibility | Character | Mill's assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Individual (undivided) | One person receives all praise or blame | The ideal; maximises motive for diligence |
| Shared (concurrence) | Multiple functionaries must assent | Responsibility enfeebled but not destroyed |
| Board (collective) | Anonymous majority vote behind closed doors | Responsibility "a mere name"; unfit for executive business |
| Representative assembly | Collective body, public proceedings | Proper for control and criticism, not for administration |

## Responsibility in Representative Government

Responsibility serves as the linchpin connecting the executive to the nation. Mill identifies "responsibility to those for whose benefit political power is exercised" as one of the great requisites of government (012-004). The representative assembly's proper office is not to [[government|govern]] but to exact responsibility: "to throw the light of publicity on its acts; to compel a full exposition and justification of all of them which any one considers questionable" (005-015).

Mill extends this principle to the appointment power. Subordinate public officers, he argues, should be selected "on the direct responsibility of the minister under whom they serve," and the appointing functionary should be the sole person empowered to remove them — and then only for personal misconduct, not for political convenience (014-008).

> As a general rule, every executive function, whether superior or subordinate, should be the appointed duty of some given individual. It should be apparent to all the world who did every thing, and through whose default any thing was left undone. Responsibility is null when nobody knows who is responsible.
>
> (014-002)

## The Advisory Council Model

Mill reconciles individual responsibility with the need for expert advice through the model of advisory councils. The minister retains "the ultimate decision" resting "undividedly with the minister himself," while councillors are placed under conditions that make it impossible for them not to express an opinion, and impossible for the minister not to listen (014-006).

The exemplar is the Council of the Governor General of India, where members record their opinions in writing, and the governor may override even a unanimous council — but must record his reasons. This ensures that "the chief is individually and effectively responsible for every act of the government," while councillors bear "only the responsibility of advisers" (014-006).

## Responsibility and Secrecy

Mill warns that secrecy destroys responsibility. When an elector casts a secret [[ballot]], he is "released from responsibility altogether," and the vote becomes a matter of personal interest rather than public duty (010-006). The same logic applies to executive functions conducted without public accountability.
