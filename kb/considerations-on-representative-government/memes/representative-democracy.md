**Representative democracy** is, for Mill, the ideal type of [[government]] for a civilized people — the form in which "the whole people, or some numerous portion of them, exercise the governing power through deputies periodically elected by themselves" (007-001). It is distinct from both [[democracy|pure democracy]], in which the people govern directly, and the "false democracy" in which only local majorities are represented while minorities across districts obtain no voice (007-006).

Mill's [[democracy|representative democracy]] is not merely an electoral mechanism but a complete [[political-institution|institutional design]]. "Such a [[democracy|representative democracy]] as has now been sketched," he concludes after laying out its elements in Chapter VII, "secures the ascendancy of the general interest" — provided its institutions are correctly calibrated (008-001). It requires a careful balance between popular control and expert administration, between the [[suffrage|franchise]] and the quality of deliberation.

## Two Classes of Danger

Mill identifies two distinct threats to [[democracy|representative democracy]], which he analyzes systematically:

> "The dangers incident to a representative democracy are of two kinds: danger of a low grade of intelligence in the representative body, and danger of class legislation by the numerical majority" (007-001)

| Danger | Cause | Remedy |
|--------|-------|--------|
| Low intelligence in the representative body | Electoral exclusion of "talents and character"; premium on wealth and local influence | [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme enabling national candidacies; [[voting|plural voting]] |
| Class legislation | Numerical majority of a single class captures all power | [[minority|Minority representation]]; proportional system; educated check on majority |

The first danger is one of competence; the second, of justice. Both must be addressed for [[democracy|representative democracy]] to succeed.

## Representative Democracy versus Bureaucracy

Mill frames the essential political choice in modern states as a dichotomy:

> "The choice has to be made between a representative democracy and a bureaucracy; all other forms are either intermediate or disguised variants" (006-009)

[[bureaucracy|Bureaucracy]] offers administrative efficiency — it can produce "Colberts" of "the aristocratic monarchy of France" who build great systems (006-008). But it does so at the cost of the public's active character. A people governed by bureaucracy becomes passive, declining in "intelligence, activity, and public spirit."

Representative democracy, by contrast:
- Exercises the population's judgment through [[election|electoral]] participation and public discussion.
- Subjects administration to the check of [[public-opinion]] — ensuring that decisions are explained and defended.
- Draws on "the general standard of intelligence and honesty existing in the community" rather than the narrow competence of a mandarin class (006-009).

Mill's conclusion is that [[democracy|representative democracy]], properly constituted, combines the advantages of popular control with the competence that bureaucracy secures through specialization.

## Institutional Requirements

For [[democracy|representative democracy]] to avoid degenerating into the false democracy of local majorities, Mill prescribes several institutional safeguards:

1. **Proportional representation** — "The best friends of [[democracy|representative democracy]] can hardly be without misgiving" when it gives power "only to the local majorities" (007-013). [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s plan of personal representation is the remedy.
2. **A [[parliament|representative assembly]] that does not govern** — The assembly should supervise and deliberate, not administer. Administration belongs to the [[executive]].
3. **Plurality of voices** — The assembly must contain "an élite of every class," not merely the delegates of the majority.
4. **Publicity and discussion** — All proceedings must be open to public scrutiny, enabling the formation of an informed [[public-opinion]].

> "The best government, for any people, is that which is best calculated to give them not merely a good government, but the benefit of improved character through participation" — representative democracy is valuable not only for what it achieves but for what it makes of its citizens

## The English Case

Mill closes Chapter VII with a pragmatic assessment of the prospects for [[democracy|representative democracy]] in England:

Even under the unreformed system, "the English democracy would as yet be content with a much smaller share of power than it at present enjoys" — the immediate need is not more democracy but better structured democracy (007-016). The goal is not to maximize the power of the numerical majority but to "secure the ascendancy of the general interest" through institutions that give voice to every significant element of the nation.
