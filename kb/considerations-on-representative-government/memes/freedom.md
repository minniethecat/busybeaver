**Freedom**, in Mill's political vocabulary, denotes more than the absence of restraint. It is the **positive condition of self-direction** — the capacity of a people to govern itself through institutions of its own choosing and to develop, through the exercise of that capacity, the intelligence and public spirit that make free government sustainable.

Mill's treatment of [[liberty|freedom]] in the *Considerations* is embedded in a developmental theory of political progress. Freedom is not a static possession but a **practice** that must be continuously exercised to be maintained. A people that does not use its [[liberty|freedom]] loses it — not necessarily through conquest or usurpation, but through the atrophy of the capacities on which [[liberty|freedom]] depends.

> "A limited and qualified freedom who will not co-operate actively with the law and the public authorities in the enforcement of just judgment is not fit for liberty" (001-009)

## Freedom as Self-Government

Mill's conception of [[liberty|freedom]] is essentially **republican** in character. Freedom is not the liberty to do as one pleases but the condition of living under laws one has a share in making:

> "The transition to a better freedom than that of fighting and rapine is a slow process" (002-028)

This conception contrasts with the liberal individualism more commonly associated with Mill's *On [[liberty|Liberty]]*. In the *Considerations*, [[liberty|freedom]] is irreducibly **collective** — it is the [[liberty|freedom]] of a people to govern itself, not merely the [[liberty|freedom]] of individuals to be left alone. The two [[liberty|freedom]]s are connected — individual liberty is most secure under [[free-government|free institutions]] — but they are not identical.

| Dimension | Negative Freedom (absence of restraint) | Positive Freedom (capacity for self-rule) |
|-----------|----------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Definition | Freedom from interference | Freedom to participate in government |
| Threat | Coercion by the state or society | Atrophy of public capacities through disuse |
| Safeguard | Legal rights and constitutional limits | Active citizenship and representative institutions |
| Mill's emphasis in *On [[liberty|Liberty]]* | Primary | Secondary |
| Mill's emphasis in *Considerations* | Secondary | Primary |

## The Conditions of Freedom

Mill identifies several conditions that must be satisfied for a people to be capable of [[liberty|freedom]]:

1. **Mental liberty and [[liberty|individuality]]**: Without the capacity for independent judgment, "permanent halt for want of [[liberty|mental liberty]] and [[liberty|individuality]]" is the inevitable result (002-029).

2. **Active cooperation with law**: Freedom is not passive enjoyment but [[participation|active participation]] — those who "will not co-operate actively with the law and the public authorities" cannot sustain [[free-government|free institutions]] (001-009).

3. **Security of person and property**: "The entire duration of British [[liberty|freedom]]" has depended on the protection of individual rights against arbitrary power (003-001).

4. **Public discussion and censure**: "The liberty of discussion and censure through the press" is the mechanism by which government is held accountable (002-021).

> "The community of their individual freedom of action, would fail to enforce the law against powerful individuals" — freedom requires collective capacity, not merely individual right (002-027)

## Freedom versus Despotism

Mill draws the contrast between [[liberty|freedom]] and despotism in developmental terms. [[despotism|Despotism]] may deliver order, security, and even material prosperity. What it cannot deliver is the moral and intellectual development of the governed:

> "That freedom is preferable to despotism: but the last is despotism" — the claim is circular only if we fail to understand freedom as a practice rather than a condition (018-012)

The superiority of [[liberty|freedom]] over despotism is not demonstrated by comparing the immediate outputs of free and [[despotism|despotic government]] — on many measures of administrative efficiency, despotism may score higher. It is demonstrated by comparing the **trajectories** of free and unfree peoples over time. Free peoples improve; unfree peoples stagnate. The reason is that [[liberty|freedom]] is not merely a political arrangement but an **educative process** — it develops, through exercise, the very capacities that make government good.

> "Preparing the Italian people for freedom in unity, but by inciting them to the most heroic exertions of patriotism and self-sacrifice" (001-012)

## The Fragility of Freedom

Mill is acutely aware that [[liberty|freedom]] is fragile. It can be lost not only through conquest or usurpation but through **neglect** — through the gradual disuse of the participatory practices that sustain it:

> "If a people thus prostrated could acquire the dignity of freedom, it would be a miracle" — freedom cannot be conferred from above; it must be won and maintained through continuous exercise (004-007)

This awareness of [[liberty|freedom]]'s fragility informs Mill's institutional recommendations. [[proportional-representation|proportional representation]], [[municipal]] self-government, [[ballot|open voting]], and universal [[education]] are not merely desirable reforms; they are **safeguards of [[liberty|freedom]]** — mechanisms that ensure the continuous exercise of the capacities on which free government depends. A people that ceases to practice [[liberty|freedom]] ceases to be free, and no paper constitution can prevent that decay.
