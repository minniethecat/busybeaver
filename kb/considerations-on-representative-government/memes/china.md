**China** serves in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as a paradigmatic example of a civilization whose institutions were effective for reaching a certain stage of development but then became an obstacle to further progress.

> …the paternal despotism of China, were very fit instruments for carrying those nations up to the point of civilization which they attained. But having reached that point, they were brought to a permanent halt for want of mental liberty and individuality…
>
> (002-029)

## The Paternal Despotism Diagnosis

Mill classified China alongside ancient Egypt as an example of the "paternal despotism" model: a governing system that successfully subdued a people to "industry and order" and gave them "a national life," but which ultimately failed because it did not cultivate the qualities needed for further advancement. The Egyptian hierarchy and "the paternal despotism of China" were both "very fit instruments for carrying those nations up to the point of civilization which they attained." Yet "having reached that point, they were brought to a permanent halt for want of mental liberty and individuality — requisites of improvement which the institutions that had carried them thus far entirely incapacitated them from acquiring" (002-029).

The deeper problem, in Mill's analysis, was that the very institutions responsible for China's early success also prevented its further development. The "institutions did not break down and give place to others," so "further improvement stopped." This was, for Mill, "among the most melancholy facts in history" (002-029). China's stagnation stood in marked contrast to the Jews, whom Mill praised as "the most progressive people of antiquity" precisely because their religious tradition sustained "the antagonism of influences which is the only real security for continued progress" (002-029).

## China in the Context of European Imperialism

Mill also referenced China in his discussion of European settlers abroad. In Chapter XVIII, he noted that the misconduct of European settlers toward native populations was a recurring problem "with the Europeans in China, and already even in Japan." Such settlers, "armed with the prestige and filled with the scornful overbearingness of the conquering nation," tended to regard "the people of the country mere dirt under their feet" (018-015). Mill used this to argue that colonial governments needed strong restraint on their own settlers, not encouragement from public opinion at home.
