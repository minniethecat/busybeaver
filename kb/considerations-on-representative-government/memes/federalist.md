**The Federalist** (commonly known as *The Federalist Papers*) occupies a prominent position in Mill's analysis of [[federal|federal government]]. In Chapter XVII, "Of Federal Representative Governments," Mill references this collection of essays as the authoritative exposition of federal principles and the most instructive treatise on the subject.

## Context in Mill's Analysis

Mill references *The Federalist* in the context of describing the transition from the imperfect confederation under [[confederation|the Articles of Confederation]] to the more perfect union under [[american-constitution|the Constitution]] of the [[united-states|United States]]. The earlier system, in which federal authorities could act only on state governments rather than directly on individual citizens, had broken down within a few years. (017-005)

> The "Federalist," a collection of papers by three of these eminent men, written in explanation and defense of the new federal Constitution while still awaiting the national acceptance, is even now the most instructive treatise we possess on federal government.
>
> (017-005)

Mill notes that the American experiment with a weak confederation broke down early, and it was the men of enlarged knowledge and acquired ascendancy who had founded the Republic — the authors of *The Federalist* among them — who guided the nation through the difficult transition to a more effective federal structure.

## Authorship and Purpose

The papers were written by three of the most eminent figures of the American founding — Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay — during the ratification debates over the proposed Constitution. Their purpose was to explain and defend the new federal Constitution to a skeptical public, addressing concerns about the distribution of power between the federal government and the states.

## The Federalist and the Senate

Mill also references the authors of *The Federalist* in his discussion of the [[senate|United States Senate]]. He notes that in American politics, a statesman of South Carolina who claimed a veto for each state on federal custom laws was considered a thinker "superior to any who has appeared in American politics since the authors of the 'Federalist'" — a judgment that underscores the enduring intellectual stature Mill attributes to the work. (017-008)

## Mill's Assessment

For Mill, *The Federalist* represents not merely a historical document but a living contribution to political science. Its analysis of the conditions necessary for successful federal union — including the need for direct federal authority over citizens, an independent judicial umpire, and a bicameral legislature combining popular and state representation — aligns closely with Mill's own principles of representative government.

Mill's reliance on *The Federalist* also reflects his broader method of drawing on American constitutional experience as a laboratory for testing the principles of representative government, alongside his use of British, French, and classical examples.
