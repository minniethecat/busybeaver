# Legislation

**Legislation** is the function of making, amending, and repealing laws. In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], legislation is a primary duty of the [[parliament|representative assembly]], but it requires specific institutional arrangements to guard against [[class-legislation|class legislation]] and ensure competent deliberation.

## The Representative Body's Primary Function

Mill treats legislation as central to the work of representative institutions. The representative body must be "fitted for the direct business of legislation as for that of administration" (005-012). However, Mill is careful to distinguish between the *controlling* function of the representative body and the *drafting* of legislation, which requires specialized expertise:

> "I do not mean that the assembly should itself frame the laws. A numerous assembly is as little fitted for the direct business of legislation as for that of administration. Its proper province, in regard to legislation, is to decide what laws shall be enacted." (005-012)

## Qualifications for Legislation

Legislative competence requires qualities not uniformly distributed in the population:

> "Better qualifications for legislation exist, and may be found if sought for, than are usually found in the numerical majority." (005-013)

This does not mean excluding the majority, but rather designing institutions that bring expertise to bear on the legislative process while retaining popular control. Mill suggests that a representative body should "leave the business of legislation, except on questions involving the fundamental principles of the constitution or of public policy, to a commission of legislation" (005-014).

## Class Legislation — The Principal Danger

The greatest danger in legislation is [[class-interest|class legislation]] — laws serving the interests of a particular class rather than the whole community. Mill identifies two sources:

| Source | Description | Reference |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Numerical majority | "Danger of [[class-interest|class legislation]] on the part of the numerical majority" | (007-001) |
| Dominant minority | "The danger of [[class-interest|class legislation]], of government intended for the immediate benefit of the dominant class" | (006-019) |

> "It is the danger of class legislation, of government intended for (whether really effecting or not) the immediate benefit of the dominant class, to the lasting detriment of the whole." (006-019)

The [[representation|representative system]] must provide "protection against the [[class-interest|class legislation]] of others, without claiming the power to exercise it in their turn" (007-016).

## Legislation vs. Administration

Mill draws a critical distinction between legislation and administration. The [[parliament|representative assembly]] "should not itself administer, but should control the administration" (005-014). A body "fitted for legislation" requires different qualities than one suited to executive functions:

- **Legislation** — deliberation on general rules, requiring wisdom, foresight, and knowledge of principles
- **Administration** — execution of policies, requiring efficiency and specialized competence

"Such bodies for a council of legislation, qualifies them the more for the office of a council of control over the administration" (005-015).

## Constitutional Legislation

Mill distinguishes "a question of constitutional legislation" from ordinary law-making (012-002). [[constitution|Constitution]]al legislation addresses the fundamental structure of government — including "the constitution of the legislative body itself" (012-002) — and requires special deliberative weight.

## The Escape from Class Legislation

Mill proposes that "the only escape from [[class-interest|class legislation]] in its narrowest, and politically speaking its most dangerous sense, is by such a composition of the representative body as shall prevent any class, even though it be the most numerous, from being able to reduce all others to its own level" (012-010). This requires:

1. **Balanced representation** — no single class dominates
2. **Qualified deliberation** — expertise supplements numbers
3. **Institutional checks** — multiple bodies review legislative output
