Plato (c. 428–348 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher whose political thought is referenced by [[John Stuart Mill]] in *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*. Mill invokes Plato at three junctures in his argument, each time to support the principle that political competence should be tied to the difficulty and responsibility of governance.

## Mill's References to Plato

### The Competent Ruler

Mill cites Plato in support of the idea that governance requires special competence (009-003):

> …general are of opinion, with Plato, that the proper person to be entrusted with power is not the one who seeks it but the one who accepts it as a duty.
>
> (009-003)

This reference anchors Mill's argument that the [[franchise]] and [[participation]] in government should be structured so that the act of voting is understood as a trust rather than a right to be exercised casually.

### The Laborious Duty of Government

Mill returns to Plato to emphasize that proper governance is inherently burdensome (010-017):

> …to perform a laborious duty. Plato had a much juster view of the conditions of good government when he represented the office of a ruler as so onerous that no one would undertake it unless under the pressure of necessity.
>
> (010-017)

This appeals to Mill's broader concern with [[political-institution|political institutions]] that select for [[intelligence]] and public-spiritedness rather than self-interest. A well-designed [[representative-government|representative system]] should make office-holding unattractive to the [[sinister-interest|sinister interests]] that seek personal advantage.

### Political Honors in the *Gorgias*

In discussing the danger of flattery in democratic politics, Mill turns to Plato's *Gorgias* (012-006):

> …political honors will endeavour, as Plato says in the Gorgias, to fashion themselves after the desires of the multitude rather than to improve them.
>
> (012-006)

This supports Mill's analysis of how unchecked democratic pressures can degrade the quality of [[deliberation]] and [[representation]]. The representative who merely mirrors [[public-opinion|popular opinion]], rather than exercising independent judgment, fails the [[trustee]] function that Mill regards as essential to good government.

## Significance in Mill's Argument

Plato appears in Mill's text not as a systematic authority but as a classical touchstone for three related claims.

First, that ruling is a burden, not a prize. Second, that good institutions should attract the reluctant competent rather than the eager self-interested. Third, that the pursuit of popularity distorts political judgment.

These themes run throughout Mill's case for representative institutions that cultivate public virtue rather than merely aggregating private preferences.
## Plato and the Ancient Conception of Politics

Mill's invocation of Plato must be understood against the background of classical political thought. In the second chapter of *Considerations on Representative Government*, Mill contrasts the ancient understanding of politics with the modern:

> Politics, as conceived by the ancients, consisted wholly in this. The natural tendency of men and their works was to degenerate, which tendency, however, by good institutions virtuously administered, it might be possible for an indefinite length of time to counteract. (002-015)

This ancient conception — that the purpose of political institutions is to arrest degeneration rather than to promote progress — informs Plato's demanding standard for rulers. If governance is a permanent struggle against decline, then only those of exceptional virtue and [[intelligence]] can be entrusted with power.

Mill shares Plato's concern with institutional design that selects for these qualities, even as he rejects the classical pessimism about human improvement. The modern view, Mill notes, has shifted toward "the contrary creed, believing that the tendency of things, on the whole, is toward improvement" (002-015).

Yet Mill insists that "we ought not to forget that there is an incessant and ever-flowing current of human affairs toward the worse, consisting of all the follies, all the vices, all the negligences, indolences, and" other human shortcomings (002-015).

This acknowledgment of the constant risk of deterioration brings Mill surprisingly close to Plato's ancient pessimism, even within a fundamentally progressive framework.

Thus Plato serves Mill not merely as a classical ornament but as a genuine antecedent. Both thinkers hold that political institutions must be designed to resist the gravitational pull of mediocrity and self-interest. Both see the quality of rulers — their [[virtue]], [[intelligence]], and public-spiritedness — as the decisive variable in the success or failure of any constitution.
