The **constituency** is the foundational unit of electoral organization in [[representation|representative system]]s — the local district whose voters elect a member to the [[parliament|representative assembly]]. John Stuart Mill subjects the constituency system to sustained critical analysis, arguing that the practice of confining representation to geographical localities produces systematic failures in the quality and inclusiveness of the national [[parliament|legislature]]. His proposed remedy, derived from [[thomas-hare|Thomas Hare]]'s scheme of [[representation|proportional representation]], would fundamentally transform the relationship between representatives and their constituencies (007-007).

## The Problem of Local Constituencies

Mill identifies several structural defects in the system of local constituencies:

| Defect | Description | Key PN |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Exclusion of minorities | A minority "exceeding a third of the local constituency" cannot secure a single representative if the majority votes as a bloc | (007-007) |
| Premium on local influence | Candidates succeed through "lavish expenditure" and local connections rather than intellectual merit | (007-011) |
| Artificial uniformity | Each constituency is treated as if it were "a unanimous constituency" when it is in fact divided | (007-010) |
| Distortion of the national will | The aggregate of local majorities does not represent the diversity of national opinion | (007-004) |

> "A minority exceeding a third of the local constituency, would be able, if it attempted, to return one member" — but under the existing system, such minorities are routinely excluded (007-007)

## The Difficulty of Getting Elected

Under the constituency system, the barrier to entry for candidates of intellectual distinction is high. Mill observes that a person of "talents and character" would face "the difficulty of getting elected by a constituency" — a difficulty compounded by the premium on local connections and expenditure (005-013):

"It would soon be recognized that there is no natural connection between the possession of intellectual distinction and the capacity of getting elected by a constituency" (005-013).

The consequence is that the representative body is filled not with the most capable minds but with those most adept at the local electoral game — a result that Mill considers a betrayal of the purpose of representative institutions.

## Hare's Reform of the Constituency

Mill's solution, following [[thomas-hare|Hare]], is to transform the nature of the constituency itself. Under [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme:

- **Decoupling from locality**: Electors may "give their votes as such in any constituency in which they choose to register" — breaking the monopoly of geographical districts (008-012).
- **Voluntary constituencies**: Each candidate's supporters form a dispersed but voluntary constituency united by shared conviction rather than geographical accident (013-009): "These ten would form his constituency, and the remainder of his votes would be held in reserve."
- **National representation**: The whole nation becomes a single electoral space, with seats allocated proportionally to the support each candidate commands (007-010).

> "The representative of a unanimous constituency. He would represent a thousand electors scattered over the whole country" (007-010)

## Constituency and the Relation to Voters

Mill examines the dynamic between representatives and their constituents, identifying a persistent tension:

"The antagonism between the assembly and those of the constituency, which might subsist indefinitely" if the representative is not held to account between elections (011-002).

The healthy relation between a [[parliament|representative assembly]] and the constituencies is one of ongoing communication and accountability:

1. **The representative must explain** his votes and reasoning to his constituents (011-002).
2. **The constituency must judge** the representative's conduct against the standard of the public good, not local advantage (007-005).
3. **A contested election in every constituency** ensures that "every election is carried on by the entire people" — but this ideal is rarely realized under the geographical system (007-004).

## The Qualities of a Popular Constituency

Mill acknowledges that constituencies possess certain virtues. The "good qualities of a popular constituency" include responsiveness to public opinion and the capacity to hold representatives accountable (014-013). But these virtues are insufficient to overcome the structural defects of the geographical constituency system, which systematically excludes [[minority|minority opinion]] and depresses the intellectual quality of the [[parliament|legislature]].

> "In the present state of the constituency; if it were not, the discordance between the assembly and the constituencies would have reached a point" requiring institutional reform (007-005)
