British governance in India occupies a distinctive place in [[John Stuart Mill]]'s *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*. Drawing on his thirty-five years of service with the [[east-india-company|East India Company]] (1823–1858), Mill uses India as the primary case study for his theory of dependent territories — peoples not yet prepared for [[representative-government|representative government]] and therefore legitimately governed by a more advanced power under strict conditions of accountability.

## Background

Mill served in the Examiner's Office of the [[east-india-company|East India Company]] from 1823 until its dissolution in 1858, rising to the position of Chief Examiner. This experience gave him firsthand knowledge of British administration in India and shaped his views on [[colonies|colonial governance]]. In *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]* (1861), written shortly after the Company's dissolution and the transfer of Indian administration to the Crown, Mill defends the principle of [[despotism|benevolent despotism]] for societies he considered not yet ready for representative institutions.

The [[east-india-company|East India Company]] governed British India through a complex structure of Presidencies (Bengal, Madras, Bombay) with councils composed partly of Company officials and partly of Crown appointees. Mill had defended this system in his 1858 pamphlet *Memorandum of the Improvements in the Administration of India* and in parliamentary testimony.

## Mill's Framework for Dependent Governance

Mill distinguishes between two categories of territories in relation to the governing power:

> (018-003) …a and Australia. Others, like India, are still at a great distance from that state, and the question is how to govern them until they are fit for representative institutions.

The fundamental challenge, as Mill sees it, is that India lacks the political [[civilization|civilisation]] and [[public-opinion|public opinion]] infrastructure necessary for [[representative-democracy|representative government]]:

> (018-015) Among a people like that of India, the utmost efforts of the public authorities are dissipated by the corruption and venality of the agents of government.

This assessment underpins Mill's argument that direct British rule is, for the present, preferable to any premature attempt at [[self-government]].

## The Institutional Structure

Mill analyzes the institutional arrangements for governing India with particular attention to the problem of accountability. The governors-general and council members were appointed by the Crown, but operated at a great distance from effective oversight:

> (013-008) All governors general of India or British America, and all who hold similar positions, should be appointed by the general government, and should be removable by it.

He also examines the council system established under the [[east-india-company|East India Company]]:

> (014-006) …the different Presidencies in India. These councils are composed of members partly appointed by the Crown and partly by the Company.

Mill regards this mixed system as producing practical administrative knowledge, though imperfectly:

> (014-016) …imperfectly carried out, the Indian service being as yet nearly the only branch of the public administration of England conducted on a systematic plan.

## The Problem of Accountability

A central tension in Mill's analysis is how to hold British rulers accountable when the governed population cannot exercise the ordinary democratic check of [[election|elections]]. He identifies this as a structural weakness:

> (018-017) …the responsibility of the British rulers of India to the British nation is chiefly through Parliament, and Parliament has little interest in the details of Indian administration.

Mill argues that the real constraint on British governors in India operates not through formal democratic mechanisms but through the scrutiny of the British public and press — an imperfect but not entirely ineffective substitute for [[representation]]:

> (018-022) …it grew up of itself, not according to any preconceived plan, but by successive adaptations to circumstances.

## Significance in Mill's Argument

India serves Mill's broader thesis in two ways. First, it exemplifies the conditions under which [[representative-democracy|representative government]] is inappropriate — a population lacking the requisite [[intelligence|political intelligence]] and experience. Second, it illustrates the permanent danger of governing distant territories without proper accountability mechanisms. Mill's Indian experience informs his insistence that even [[despotism|despotic government]] must be conducted under the eye of a free press and a vigilant public.
