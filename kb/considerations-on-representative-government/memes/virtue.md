## Definition

In Mill's political theory, virtue encompasses the moral and intellectual qualities of the people that constitute the essential foundation of good government. Mill pairs "virtue and intelligence" as the twin pillars upon which the quality of political institutions depends: "The first element of good government, therefore, being the virtue and intelligence of the human beings composing the community, the most important point of excellence which any form of government can possess is to promote the virtue and intelligence of the people themselves" (002-020). Virtue is thus both the prerequisite for and the product of well-designed political institutions.

## Virtue as the Criterion of Good Government

Mill establishes the promotion of virtue as the primary criterion by which forms of government should be judged. The first question to ask of any political institution is "how far they tend to foster in the members of the community the various desirable qualities, moral and intellectual" (002-020). This is because the well-being of the governed is the sole object of government, and "their good qualities supply the moving force which works the machinery" (002-021). A government's merit consists not only in its institutional efficiency but in "the degree in which it tends to increase the sum of good qualities in the governed, collectively and individually" (002-021).

Mill distinguishes between two criteria: the degree to which government promotes virtue and intelligence in the people, and the quality of the governmental machinery itself. Of these, the former is foundational—without a virtuous and intelligent citizenry, even the best-designed institutions will fail.

## Active Virtue versus Passive Obedience

Mill draws an important distinction between active and passive forms of virtue. While acknowledging that obedience is "the indispensable virtue" for a people in a state of "savage independence" who must first learn to submit to external control before civilization can advance (002-027), he rejects the elevation of passive character as morally superior. Religious traditions, including Christianity, have historically "fostered this sentiment" in favor of the inactive character, but Mill contends that "it is the prerogative of Christianity, as regards this and many other perversions, that it is able to throw them off" (003-019).

The virtues required for self-government are active rather than passive. Mill warns that "it is a complete error to suppose that the virtues of patience and resignation, though always counted among the moral virtues," are sufficient for political life (003-019). Representative government requires citizens to exercise "the virtues of self-help and self-government" (003-023), actively participating in public affairs rather than merely submitting to authority.

## Virtue, Intelligence, and Political Participation

Mill's coupling of virtue with intelligence has direct implications for political participation. He argues that political discussion itself is a means of cultivating these qualities: "It is by political discussion that the manual laborer, whose employment is a routine... learns to feel for and with his fellow citizens, and becomes consciously a member of a great community" (008-002). However, he also acknowledges the tension between equal virtue and unequal knowledge in questions of suffrage, asking: "If with equal virtue, one is superior to the other in knowledge and intelligence... ought they to have equal weight in the election?" (008-008).

The ideally best form of government—representative government—is superior precisely because it "does more than any other polity to raise and improve the virtue and intelligence of the people" (003-002). It promotes both "the individual intellect and virtue of its wisest members" and the broader intellectual and moral advancement of the entire community (002-022).

## The Machinery of Virtuous Government

Mill argues that governments should be designed to "obtain the highest average of virtue and intelligence" from their citizens (002-021). Well-designed institutions create a virtuous cycle: they draw upon existing public virtue to function effectively, while simultaneously cultivating greater virtue through participation. The "salutary forms of procedure and arrangement" (002-021) in representative government—public deliberation, electoral accountability, freedom of discussion—serve both to harness and to develop the moral and intellectual qualities of the people.

This understanding of virtue leads Mill to reject despotism not merely as oppressive but as fundamentally incapable of improving the governed: even a benevolent despotism fails because it does not cultivate the active virtues of self-government in the people themselves (003-023).
