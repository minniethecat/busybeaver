## Definition

Constitutional morality, in Mill's usage, refers to the unwritten ethical norms, traditional understandings, and customary self-restraints that regulate the actual operation of a constitution — as distinct from its formal legal provisions. These norms are not codified in law but are sustained by public opinion and the conscience of office-holders. Mill considered constitutional morality to be of equal practical importance to the written constitution itself (012-003).

## The British Example

Mill illustrated the concept through the British Constitution. By "constitutional law, the crown can refuse its assent to any act of Parliament, and can appoint to office and maintain in it any minister, in opposition to the remonstrances of Parliament. But the constitutional morality of the country nullifies these powers, preventing them from being ever used; and, by requiring that the head of the administration should always be virtually appointed by the House of Commons, makes that body the real sovereign of the state" (005-003).

The unwritten rules that limit lawful powers are effective only so long as they harmonize "with the actual distribution of real political strength." When the balance of social power shifts, constitutional maxims must shift with it or be overturned (005-004).

## Importance Across Forms of Government

Mill argued that constitutional morality is not merely a nicety but essential to the survival of free institutions. Its importance varies with the type of government (012-003):

- In **unbalanced governments** (pure monarchy, pure aristocracy, pure democracy), such maxims are "the only barrier which restrains the government from the utmost excesses in the direction of its characteristic tendency."
- In **imperfectly balanced governments**, where constitutional limits exist but the strongest power can overstep them with "at least temporary impunity," it is "only by doctrines of constitutional morality, recognized and sustained by opinion, that any regard at all is preserved for the checks and limitations of the constitution."
- In **well-balanced governments**, where power is divided and each branch is armed for defense, "the government can only be carried on by forbearance on all sides to exercise those extreme powers." Only by regard for constitutional morality "is the constitution kept in existence."

## Application to the Pledges Question

Mill invoked constitutional morality to frame the debate over whether members of Parliament should be bound by the instructions of their constituents. He distinguished this from questions of "constitutional legislation," placing it instead in the domain of "constitutional morality — the ethics of representative government." It concerned not institutions themselves but "the temper of mind which the electors ought to bring to the discharge of their functions" (012-002).

The distinction was consequential: "Questions of constitutional morality are of no less practical moment than those relating to the constitution itself. The very existence of some governments, and all that renders others endurable, rests on the practical observance of doctrines of constitutional morality" (012-003).
