**Election** is the mechanism through which the people exercise their [[suffrage|right to vote]] and constitute the [[representation|representative]] body that governs them. Mill analyzes election not merely as a procedural necessity but as a critical determinant of the quality of government. "Popular election thus practiced, instead of a security against a low standard of qualifications, operates as a premium upon it" — the design of the electoral system directly shapes the intellectual and moral character of those who govern (001-009).

## Defects of the Existing Electoral System

Mill identifies several structural problems with the first-past-the-post system of his time:

| Defect | Consequence | Key PN |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Local majorities capture all seats | Minorities across districts obtain no representation | (007-005) |
| "Hobson's choice" for voters | Majorities must vote for local party nominees or not vote at all | (007-012) |
| Premium on wealth and local influence | Candidates of "talents and character" are excluded | (007-011) |
| [[universal-suffrage|Unequal suffrage]] in effect | "Every single individual does not count for as much as any other single individual" | (007-004) |

> "Democracy, thus constituted, does not even attain its ostensible object, that of giving the powers of government in all cases to the numerical majority" (007-004)

## Direct versus Two-Stage Election

Mill examines the arguments for indirect (two-stage) election, where primary electors choose secondary electors who then select the representatives:

| Mode | Benefits | Risks |
|------|----------|-------|
| Direct election | Full accountability; educative effect on the population | Susceptible to popular impulse and class domination |
| Two-stage election | Potential filtering for judgment | Reduces the benefits of popular power "much more certainly" than it reduces the dangers (009-002) |

Mill concludes that two-stage election works well only when the intermediate electors have independent functions beyond mere candidate selection — as with the [[constitution|United States Senate]], whose members originally served broader state functions (009-004).

## Electoral Reform and Candidate Quality

Mill's core concern with election is its effect on the caliber of representatives:

> "At present, by universal admission, it is becoming more and more difficult for any one who has only talents and character to gain admission into the House of Commons. The only persons who can get elected are those who possess local influence, or make their way by lavish expenditure" (007-011)

The solution, following [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme, is to allow voters to cast [[voting|ballot]]s beyond their [[constituency|local constituency]]:

- Voters choose from "all the persons of national reputation" rather than only local nominees (007-011).
- Minorities gain representation proportional to their numbers, raising the intellectual standard of the whole body (007-012).
- The electoral contest becomes one of ideas and qualifications rather than money and local connections.

## Plural Voting

As a transitional measure, Mill advocated for [[voting|plural voting]] — granting additional votes to citizens whose education or occupation indicated superior judgment. While practiced in local "vestry elections," Mill acknowledged it was "so unfamiliar in elections to [[parliament|Parliament]] that it is not likely to be soon or willingly adopted" (008-012). He saw it as an intermediate step before the eventual adoption of [[thomas-hare|proportional representation]].
