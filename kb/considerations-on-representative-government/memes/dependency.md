**Dependency**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, refers to a territory or population that is governed by an external sovereign power without full [[representation|representative]] institutions of its own. Mill treats the governance of [[colonies|dependencies]] as a distinct category of political problem — one that cannot be solved by simply extending the institutional forms of the governing country to the dependent territory.

Mill's analysis, concentrated in Chapter XVIII ("Of the Government of Dependencies by a Free State"), is notable for its rejection of the then-prevailing assumption that a dependency could be adequately governed through the ordinary machinery of the metropolitan [[parliament]]. He argues that the "simplest mode of attempting to govern a dependency, is about the worst" (018-012), and that the structural distance between ruler and ruled creates defects that no amount of good intention can overcome.

## The Problem of Governing a Dependency

Mill identifies the fundamental challenge of dependency governance as one of **knowledge and interest**. The metropolitan government lacks both the local knowledge necessary for wise administration and the identity of interest with the governed that constrains power in a self-governing community:

> "It is not by attempting to rule directly a country like India, but by giving it good rulers, that the English people can do their duty to that country; and they can scarcely give it a worse one than an English cabinet minister, who is thinking of English, not Indian politics" (018-018)

The problem is structural rather than personal. Even a capable minister cannot acquire the intimate understanding of a distant society that effective government requires, and the political incentives of the metropolitan system push in directions that are often harmful to the dependency:

| Defect | Cause | Consequence |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| Absence of local knowledge | Geographic and cultural distance | Policies designed for metropolitan conditions are misapplied |
| Divergent interests | The dependency has no electoral weight in the metropolis | Ministers prioritize metropolitan opinion |
| Short time horizons | Cabinet ministers rotate frequently | No sustained engagement with complex local problems |
| Factitious public opinion | "Two or three fluent speakers" in [[parliament|Parliament]] can drive policy (018-018) | Policy swings on metropolitan political winds rather than local realities |

## The Special Commission as an Alternative

Rather than direct parliamentary governance, Mill advocates for a **specialized governing body** — a commission or council with dedicated responsibility for the dependency:

> "Real good government is not compatible with the conditions of the case. There is but a choice of imperfections. The problem is, so to construct the governing body that, under the circumstances of a dependency, it will be as good as the circumstances admit" (018-019)

Mill's argument is characteristically pragmatic: he does not claim that a commission can achieve the standard of government attainable in a self-governing community. The distance between ruler and ruled, and the absence of accountability to the governed, make that impossible. But he insists that a body with **specialized knowledge**, **continuity of membership**, and **direct responsibility** for the dependency will govern better than a parliament whose attention is elsewhere.

This commission, in Mill's conception, should act as "advocate and champion of the dependency before the imperial tribunal" (018-019) — a role that no cabinet minister, caught between competing domestic and imperial pressures, can adequately perform.

## Dependency and Civilization

Mill draws a distinction between two classes of dependency that is central to his analysis. Dependencies populated by people "of her own blood and language" — the settler colonies — are entitled to [[self-government|representative institutions]] "formed in imitation of her own" (018-004). But [[colonies|dependencies]] inhabited by "a dissimilar people" present a different case:

> "The government of a semi-barbarous dependency by a civilized country, and a country of free institutions, is attended with a complication of difficulties" (018-022)

This distinction reflects the broader civilizational framework of Mill's political thought. Peoples at different stages of development require different institutional forms. The error, in Mill's view, is to apply the same template indiscriminately — either to deny self-government to those ready for it, or to impose representative institutions on those not yet prepared to operate them. The [[education|political education]] of the dependent people is the ultimate aim, but it must proceed by stages appropriate to their condition.

## The Federal Case: Dependency between States

Mill extends the concept of dependency to relations between states within [[federal|federal system]]s. A marked inequality of strength among federating states can transform the weaker members into effective [[colonies|dependencies]] of the stronger:

> "A third condition, not less important than the two others, is that there be not a very marked inequality of strength among the several contracting states" (017-004)

If one state dominates the [[federal|federation]] — "would make all Germany a dependency of Prussia" — the federal compact becomes a form of imperial rule under another name (017-004). The institutional forms of equality persist, but the reality of power transforms the weaker members from partners into [[colonies|dependencies]]. This analysis reveals the continuity in Mill's thought between the governance of overseas [[colonies|dependencies]] and the internal structure of composite states: in both cases, the absence of genuine reciprocity in power produces defective government.
