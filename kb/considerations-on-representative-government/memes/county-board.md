The **County Board** is discussed by John Stuart Mill in Chapter XV of *Considerations on Representative Government* as part of his examination of local representative institutions. Mill uses the county board to argue for the superiority of elected over *ex officio* local administration, and for the importance of attracting high-caliber individuals to local government.

## Critique of Existing Institutions

Mill contrasts the proposed county board with two existing English local institutions: Boards of Guardians (which administered poor relief) and the Quarter Sessions (which handled county-level administration through unelected justices of the peace).

Boards of Guardians included both elected members and *ex officio* justices of the peace, the latter limited to one-third of the total. Mill approves of this arrangement as it "secures the presence in these bodies of a more educated class than it would perhaps be practicable to attract thither on any other terms." (015-005)

The *ex officio* members serve as "a virtual representation of another class, having sometimes a different interest from the rest," providing "a check upon the [[class-interest|class interests]] of the farmers or petty shopkeepers who form the bulk of the elected guardians." (015-005)

The Quarter Sessions, by contrast, receive Mill's sharpest criticism. Composed solely of justices of the peace — who hold their positions "virtually by right of their acres" — this body is described as "the most aristocratic in principle which now remains in England." (015-005)

Mill elaborates that it is "far more so than the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]], for it grants public money and disposes of important public interests, not in conjunction with a popular assembly, but alone." (015-005)

Mill concludes that the institution "is obviously at variance with all the principles which are the foundation of [[representative-government|representative government]]." (015-005)


> The institution is the most aristocratic in principle which now remains in England; far more so than the House of Lords, for it grants public money and disposes of important public interests, not in conjunction with a popular assembly, but alone.
>
> (015-005)


## The Case for County Boards

Mill argues that a properly constituted County Board would not require the admixture of *ex officio* members characteristic of Boards of Guardians.

He explains that "the business of a county being on a sufficiently large scale to be an object of interest and attraction to country gentlemen, they would have no more difficulty in getting themselves elected to the Board than they have in being returned to [[parliament|Parliament]] as county members." (015-005)

The scale of county government is itself the guarantee that qualified individuals will seek election. This reflects Mill's broader principle that institutional design must account for the incentives it creates for participation by the most capable members of society. A body whose jurisdiction is too small will attract only those with narrow interests.

## The Quality of Local Representatives

Mill returns to the County Board in his discussion of how different tiers of local government attract different calibers of representative. He observes that "a much more highly qualified class of persons is likely to be obtainable for a County Board than those who compose an average Board of Guardians." (015-008)

This differential in quality provides a rationale for reserving "some higher descriptions of local business" for the county level that "might otherwise have been conveniently managed within itself by each separate Union." (015-008)

This principle — that institutional design should match the scale of jurisdiction to the quality of representation it can attract — reflects Mill's broader argument that local government serves a dual purpose: it is both an instrument of administration and "a school of political capacity and general intelligence." (015-007)

## Local Administration and National Interests

Mill extends his analysis of county government to the distribution of responsibilities between local and central authorities. He begins with the principle that "all business purely local — all which concerns only a single locality — should devolve upon the local authorities." (015-011)

He cites paving, lighting, and cleansing of streets as examples of matters of little consequence beyond the town's inhabitants. However, Mill identifies a class of duties performed locally that are in fact of national importance.

These include "the jails, for instance, most of which in this country are under county management; the local police; the local administration of justice." (015-011) The maladministration of any of these affects not merely the locality but the entire nation.

> Security of person and property, and equal justice between individuals, are the first needs of society and the primary ends of government: if these things can be left to any responsibility below the highest, there is nothing except war and treaties which requires a general government at all.
>
> (015-011)

Rather than strip localities of these responsibilities, Mill advocates central superintendence. Local functionaries may execute duties imposed by the central authority, but "experience is daily forcing upon the public a conviction of the necessity of having at least inspectors appointed by the general [[government]] to see that the local officers do their duty." (015-011)

He draws parallels with inspectors of prisons, factories, and schools — each watching over compliance with parliamentary rules and suggesting improvements where needed. This framework preserves the educative and participatory benefits of local administration while ensuring that functions of national importance meet uniform standards.
