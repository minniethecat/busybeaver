In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], the **public office** is the institutional unit through which the executive and administrative functions of the state are carried out. Mill devotes careful attention to the proper constitution of public offices, the qualifications of those who fill them, and — most critically — the mechanisms of accountability that prevent office-holders from abusing their authority. His analysis draws on the principle of publicity, the distinction between political and administrative functions, and the institutional safeguards of a well-ordered [[constitution]] (002-021).

## The Ideal Constitution of a Public Office

Mill articulates a concise principle for the design of public offices:

"The ideally perfect constitution of a public office is that in which the interest of the functionary is entirely coincident with his duty" (002-021).

This principle requires that the incentives facing an office-holder — salary, tenure, prospect of advancement, accountability to superiors or to the public — be aligned as precisely as possible with the duties of the office. When interest and duty diverge, the office-holder will be tempted to serve his own ends rather than the public good.

> "The ideally perfect constitution of a public office is that in which the interest of the functionary is entirely coincident with his duty." (002-021)

## Publicity and Accountability

The essential safeguard against abuse of public office is publicity. In a [[free-government|free government]], the conduct of public officers is subject to the scrutiny of the public and the press:

"A government of publicity" is one in which official acts are open to inspection and criticism — this being "the most important of all the securities for the good conduct of public officers" (005-009).

| Mechanism of Accountability | Description | Key PN |
|---------------------------|-------------|--------|
| Publicity of official acts | All administrative decisions are open to public inspection | (005-009) |
| [[liberty|Liberty]] of censure | "Full liberty of censure on every public officer, and on every public act or measure" | (006-013) |
| Ministerial responsibility | High officers answer to the [[parliament|representative assembly]] for their conduct | (005-010) |
| Removal for misconduct | Officers who betray their trust may be removed from office | (014-013) |

> "Full liberty of censure on every public officer, and on every public act or measure" is an essential condition of responsible government (006-013)

## Political versus Administrative Offices

Mill draws a crucial distinction between two classes of public offices:

**Policy-making offices** are those held by ministers and high executive officers who "really conduct the public business" (005-015). These officers should be:
- **Politically accountable** to the [[parliament|representative assembly]]
- **Subject to removal** when they lose the confidence of the representatives
- **Open to public criticism** in the press and in parliamentary debate

**Subordinate administrative offices**, by contrast, require a different principle: "All subordinate public officers who are not appointed by some chief executive" should be selected through competitive examination and enjoy security of tenure during good behavior (014-008). Mill argues that:

> "The irremovability of any public officer to whom great interests are entrusted is a provision" essential to the impartial administration of law — but only for those officers who do not set policy (014-013)

## The Problem of Responsibility

Mill identifies a persistent difficulty: "great public officers who can be made responsible" often escape accountability because the complexity of government obscures individual responsibility (005-010). The mechanisms Mill proposes include:

1. **Individual responsibility**: Each officer must answer for his own sphere of action, preventing the diffusion of blame across a department (005-010).
2. **Hierarchical supervision**: Officers are subject to "different supremos" in a chain of command that ensures oversight (017-008).
3. **Merit-based selection**: Public officers "thus selected and trained" through competitive examination are more capable and less susceptible to corruption (018-021).
4. **Responsible presence**: A "responsible public officer" should be present where public business is conducted, providing a point of accountability (010-016).

> "It is in no way unjust that public officers thus selected and trained should receive higher remuneration than those who have not undergone the same preparation" (018-021)

## Public Office and Popular Government

Mill recognizes a tension between the principle of popular control and the professional administration of public offices. The [[parliament|representative assembly]] must not itself administer — that is the function of the executive and its subordinate officers — but must exercise "the popular control essential to [[freedom|free government]]" over those who do (011-001).

The proper relation is:
- **The people**, through their representatives, **control** the public officers.
- **The public officers** **administer** according to law and professional standards.
- **The [[parliament|representative assembly]]** does not "do the business of government" but ensures that those who do are accountable (011-001).
