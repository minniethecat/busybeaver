## Definition and Role in Mill's Theory

Parish relief was the British system of welfare support for the destitute, administered at the parish level. In John Stuart Mill's theory of representative government, the receipt of parish relief served as a key boundary for the electoral franchise: those dependent on public support for subsistence should, he argued, be temporarily disqualified from voting (008-006).

Mill did not regard this disqualification as permanent or punitive. He described it as an exclusion "required by positive reasons, which do not conflict with the principle" of universal access to the suffrage. The exclusions he endorsed were "not in their nature permanent. They exact such conditions only as all are able, or ought to be able, to fulfill if they choose" (008-006).

## The Principle of Reciprocity

Mill's argument rested on a principle of reciprocity in citizenship. A person who cannot support himself by his own labor "has no claim to the privilege of helping himself to the money of others. By becoming dependent on the remaining members of the community for actual subsistence, he abdicates his claim to equal rights with them in other respects." Those who sustain a person's very existence "may justly claim the exclusive management of those common concerns to which he now brings nothing, or less than he takes away" (008-006).

## Practical Provisions

Mill proposed specific practical conditions for the disqualification (008-006):

- A fixed term — "say five years previous to the registry" — during which the applicant's name had not appeared on the parish books as a relief recipient.
- The same principle extended to uncertificated bankrupts and those who had taken the benefit of the Insolvent Act, until debts were paid.
- Non-payment of taxes, when long-persisted, should also disqualify while it lasted.

He expected that under a properly functioning system, the disqualified class would be "progressively diminishing," so that the suffrage would be "with that slight abatement, universal" (008-007).

## Relationship to Universal Suffrage

Mill recognized a tension: even with universal suffrage conditioned on literacy and self-support, "the great majority of voters in most countries, and emphatically in this, would be manual laborers." The "twofold danger, that of too low a standard of political intelligence, and that of class legislation, would still exist in a very perilous degree." This concern led him directly to his proposal for plural voting, which he saw as the necessary corrective to the numerical weight of the least educated class (008-007).
