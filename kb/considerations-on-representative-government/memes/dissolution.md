The **power of dissolution** — the executive's authority to dissolve the representative assembly and call a new parliament — is, in Mill's constitutional theory, a vital mechanism for maintaining the balance between executive and legislature. Mill treats dissolution as the constitutional alternative to both [[united-states|the American system]] of a fixed-term popularly-elected president and the complete dependence of the executive on parliamentary votes.

## Dissolution as Constitutional Safeguard

Mill argues that the executive should possess the power to dissolve parliament and appeal to the people. Without this power, a deadlock between the executive and the legislature could paralyse government for years, with neither branch having "any legal means of ridding itself of the other" (014-010). Surviving such a period without a *coup d'état* requires "such a combination of the love of liberty and the habit of self-restraint as very few nations have yet shown themselves capable of" (014-010).

Mill proposes two alternative constitutional designs for the executive:

| System | Executive tenure | Dissolution power | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| British (prime minister) | Dependent on parliamentary confidence | Possesses dissolution power | Good: balance maintained |
| American (president) | Fixed term, independent of legislature | No dissolution power | Problematic: risk of deadlock |
| Mill's preference | Appointed by parliament for fixed term | Possesses dissolution power | Best: independence without electoral evils |

> The power of dissolving Parliament is one which I think it desirable he should possess, even under the system by which his own tenure of office is secured to him for a fixed period. There ought not to be any possibility of that deadlock in politics which would ensue on a quarrel breaking out between a president and an assembly.
>
> (014-010)

## Dissolution as a Check on the Executive

The dissolution power also serves as a mechanism for resolving political uncertainty. "When there is a real doubt which of two contending parties has the strongest following, it is important that there should exist a constitutional means of immediately testing the point and setting it at rest" (014-011). No other political question can receive proper attention while the balance of forces remains undecided; the interval becomes "an interregnum for purposes of legislative or administrative improvement" (014-011).

## Dissolution and Electoral Discipline

Mill notes that the threat of dissolution disciplines both the executive and the legislature. The "always impending" possibility of "an earlier dissolution keeps the motives for standing well with [[constituent|constituents]]" constantly before members of parliament (011-001). This mechanism ensures that representatives remain attentive to [[public-opinion|public opinion]] between elections.

## Historical Perspective

Mill observes that the absence of institutional mechanisms for peaceful political renewal has been a recurring cause of governmental decay. Historical polities "either fell into dissolution or became stationary (which means deterioration kept up to a point by foreign pressure or domestic strife)" when they lacked means of adapting to changing circumstances (007-014). The dissolution power, by enabling peaceful political renewal, addresses this vulnerability in modern representative systems.

## Limits and Dangers

Mill acknowledges that dissolution is not a universal remedy. Where "the vast power centralized in the chief magistrate, and the insufficient attachment of the mass of the people to free institutions, give him a chance of success in an attempt to subvert the Constitution," no chief magistrate is safe "whom the Parliament can not, by a single vote, reduce to a private station" (014-012). In such circumstances, even complete constitutional dependence of the executive on parliament is "but a weak protection" (014-012).
