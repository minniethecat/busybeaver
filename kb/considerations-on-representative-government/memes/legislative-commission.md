The **Legislative Commission** is John Stuart Mill's institutional proposal for separating the *drafting* of laws from their *enactment*. It forms a central plank of his argument in Chapter V of *Considerations on Representative Government* that representative assemblies are unfit for the direct business of legislation and should instead confine themselves to controlling and sanctioning the work of expert bodies.

## Mill's Proposal

Mill argues that "any government fit for a high state of civilization would have as one of its fundamental elements a small body, not exceeding in number the members of a cabinet, who should act as a Commission of Legislation, having for its appointed office to make the laws." (005-013) He envisions this commission as a permanent institution evolving from the work of codification:

> If the laws of this country were, as surely they will soon be, revised and put into a connected form, the Commission of Codification by which this is effected should remain as a permanent institution, to watch over the work, protect it from deterioration, and make further improvements as often as required. (005-013)

## The Division of Labor: Intelligence and Will

Mill draws a sharp distinction between the function of the commission and that of [[parliament|Parliament]]. The commission "would only embody the element of intelligence in their construction; Parliament would represent that of will." (005-013) No measure would become law until expressly sanctioned by Parliament, and either house could reject a bill or send it back for reconsideration. Parliament could also initiate legislation by referring any subject to the commission with directions to prepare a law.

Crucially, once a bill was framed by the commission, "Parliament should have no power to alter the measure, but solely to pass or reject it; or, if partially disapproved of, remit it to the commission for reconsideration." (005-013) This restriction is designed to prevent the piecemeal tinkering that Mill saw as the besetting vice of parliamentary legislation — "clauses omitted which are essential to the working of the rest; incongruous ones inserted to conciliate some private interest." (005-012)

## Appointment and Tenure

The commissioners would be appointed by the Crown but hold office for a fixed term — Mill suggests five years — and could be removed only on an address from Parliament "grounded either on personal misconduct (as in the case of judges), or on refusal to draw up a bill in obedience to the demands of Parliament." (005-013) At the expiration of the term, a member would cease to hold office unless reappointed, "in order to provide a convenient mode of getting rid of those who had not been found equal to their duties, and of infusing new and younger blood into the body." (005-013)

## Historical Precedents

Mill finds a precedent for this arrangement in the Athenian Democracy, where the popular *Ecclesia* could pass decrees on single matters of policy, but laws "could only be made or altered by a different and less numerous body, renewed annually, called the *Nomothetæ*, whose duty it also was to revise the whole of the laws, and keep them consistent with one another." (005-014)

He proposes that the commission could be integrated into the existing [[british-constitution|British Constitution]] through the machinery of the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]]. If members were made peers for life, Mill suggests, the same convention that leaves judicial functions to the law lords would leave legislation to "professional legislators." (005-014) Government bills would be drawn up by the commission, and private members "would gradually find it convenient... if, instead of bringing in a bill and submitting it directly to the house, they obtained leave to introduce it and have it referred to the Legislative Commission." (005-014)

## Place in Mill's Constitutional Design

The Legislative Commission reappears in Mill's later discussion of the [[second-chamber|Second Chamber]], where he proposes that all who "were or had been members of the Legislative Commission described in a former chapter" should be eligible for the Senate — an arrangement he regards as "an indispensable ingredient in a well constituted popular government." (013-008) This linkage between expert law-drafting and the upper house reflects Mill's broader ambition to combine democratic legitimacy with specialized competence in the architecture of representative institutions.
