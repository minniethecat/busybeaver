## Definition and Background

Oligarchy is a [[government|form of government]] in which political power is effectively held by a small, privileged group — whether distinguished by wealth, family, birth, or control of the machinery of election. Mill treats oligarchy not merely as a classical category of constitution, but as a persistent tendency within ostensibly broader systems of government, including representative democracy.

In the classical typology of constitutions, oligarchy stands alongside monarchy, aristocracy, democracy, and tyranny as one of the basic forms of rule. Mill engages with this tradition but extends the analysis: for him, oligarchy is less a formal constitutional designation than a description of where real power lies, often concealed beneath nominal institutions (006-007).

## Mill's Analysis of Oligarchic Rule

Mill argues that oligarchy, like monarchy, systematically fails to act in the real long-term interest of the governed. The governing few, whether a single monarch or a small oligarchic body, are predisposed to pursue their immediate and apparent interest rather than the common good:

> It is not what their interest is, but what they suppose it to be, that is the important consideration with respect to their conduct; and it is quite conclusive against any theory of government that it assumes the numerical majority to do habitually what is never done, nor expected to be done, save in very exceptional cases, by any other depositaries of power.
>
> (006-015)

This critique applies universally to concentrated power: the problem is structural, not a matter of individual virtue. Mill observes that no oligarchy in known history has taken the "exalted view" of its self-interest that would lead it to govern justly for the benefit of all (006-015).

## Historical Instances

**Rome and Venice**. Mill identifies the Roman Republic and the Venetian Republic as the only aristocracies in history to demonstrate sustained governing capacity and consistent policy across generations. Yet both, upon closer examination, were in reality oligarchies:

> At Venice, though the privileged order was numerous, the actual management of affairs was rigidly concentrated in a small oligarchy within the oligarchy, whose whole lives were devoted to the study and conduct of the affairs of the state.
>
> (006-007)

The Roman Senate, likewise, was composed exclusively of those who had held or would hold high public office, with their lives pledged to public affairs. While this produced capable administration, it did so at the cost of the broader public good, as the dignity and estimation of the governing body were often "wholly incompatible" with the prosperity of the citizens (006-007).

**The Decline of the Roman Republic**. As the Roman republic deteriorated, the case for the secret ballot became irresistible. The oligarchy grew yearly "richer and more tyrannical," the people "poorer and more dependent," and the franchise risked becoming a mere instrument in the hands of unprincipled magnates (010-006).

**Athens**. Even in the comparatively stable Athenian democracy, the ballot served as a protection against intimidation by oligarchic factions. Mill notes that Athenian public figures such as Nicias, Theramenes, and Alcibiades — the men actually entrusted with command — were "known to sympathize more with oligarchy than with democracy" (007-015).

## Oligarchy Within Democracy

One of Mill's most pointed observations is that oligarchic control can emerge within formally democratic institutions. He describes the pre-reform British electoral system in these terms:

> A vote given in opposition to those influences was therefore, in general, an honest, a public-spirited vote; but in any case, and by whatever motive dictated, it was almost sure to be a good vote, for it was a vote against the monster evil, the overruling influence of oligarchy.
>
> (010-009)

The "power of landlords and boroughmongers" constituted an oligarchy that controlled the institutions of the state, even through the forms of popular election (010-009).

Yet Mill also perceived a more subtle danger: as electoral independence grew and old forms of coercion declined, a new kind of oligarchy could emerge. The electors themselves, no longer passive instruments of aristocratic patrons, could become a collective ruling class:

> They are no longer passive instruments of other men's will — mere organs for putting power into the hands of a controlling oligarchy. The electors themselves are becoming the oligarchy.
>
> (010-010)

This remark captures Mill's nuanced understanding: oligarchy is not a fixed constitutional form but a dynamic phenomenon — the concentration of power in a minority segment of the population, which may shift its composition while retaining its exclusionary character.

## Oligarchy and Hare's System

Mill presents [[Thomas Hare]]'s system of personal representation as a structural remedy against oligarchic tendencies within democracy. Under ordinary democratic arrangements, the instructed minority has no distinct political organ and may be entirely excluded from representation. Hare's system, by allowing minorities to aggregate their votes across constituency boundaries, would ensure that the educated and capable — even if they "sympathize more with oligarchy than with democracy" — are represented in their proper numerical proportion rather than being either excluded or dominant (007-015).
