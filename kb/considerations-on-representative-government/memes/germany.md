## Introduction

**Germany** in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* serves as the primary example of an imperfect federation — what he calls "the more imperfect kind of federal government." (017-005) The German Confederation (Deutscher Bund), established in 1815, represented for Mill a model where federal authorities could act only on member governments, not directly on individual citizens. This stood in sharp contrast to the United States Constitution, which Mill regarded as the exemplar of effective federal union.

## The German Confederation as a Weak Federation

Mill distinguishes two modes of organizing a federal union. The inferior mode — "the plan of the German so-called Confederation, and of the Swiss Constitution previous to 1847" — makes federal acts "obligatory only on the governments as such." (017-005) Under this arrangement, the confederation lacks the power to enact laws binding directly on citizens. Mill regarded this as a fundamental structural weakness, noting that it "has never bestowed on Germany a uniform system of customs, nor so much as a uniform coinage." (017-004)

## Historical Context

Mill's references to Germany reflect the pre-unification period, when the German Confederation consisted of numerous sovereign states loosely bound by treaty. He observes that the confederation "has served only to give [[austria|Austria]] and [[prussia|Prussia]] a legal right of intervention" in the internal affairs of smaller states. (017-004) This instrumentalization of federal institutions by the two great powers exemplified, for Mill, the dangers of a confederation lacking direct authority over citizens.

Mill contrasted the free towns of medieval Germany favorably with the feudal monarchies of Europe, noting that "the free towns of Flanders and Germany" along with [[italy|the Italian republics]] had "attained more brilliant prosperity than any others." (003-013)

## The Lesson for Federal Theory

Germany's experience reinforced Mill's argument that effective federal unions require direct authority over citizens, a supreme court to adjudicate jurisdictional disputes, and a rough equality of power among member states. He warned that in any federation where one state is "so much more powerful than the rest as to be capable of vying in strength with many of them combined," that dominant state "will insist on being master of the joint deliberations." (017-004)
