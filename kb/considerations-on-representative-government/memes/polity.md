**Polity**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, is the encompassing term for the political community in its entirety — not merely the [[government]] or the state, but the people, their institutions, their political culture, and the habits of mind that sustain self-government. Mill uses "polity" where a less careful writer might use "state" or "nation" or "government," and the word choice is significant: a polity is a *formed* political community, one that has attained a degree of institutional articulation and civic consciousness.

The concept of polity runs through the *Considerations* from the opening methodological discussion to the closing analysis of [[federal|federal union]]s. For Mill, the central question of political science is not "what is the best form of government in the abstract?" but "what form of government is best *for this particular polity*?" — a question that requires careful attention to the character, capacities, and historical development of the people to be governed (001-003).

## Polity as the Object of Political Construction

Mill opens the *Considerations* with a discussion of two extreme views of [[political-institution|political institutions]] — the mechanical view (government can be designed and imposed) and the organic view (government grows spontaneously from the character of the people). His own position is a synthesis: institutions are constructed, but they must be constructed *for* a particular polity, taking account of its specific characteristics:

> "Constitute a polity suited to the people who possess it" — the form of government must fit the governed (001-003)

| View of Government | Role of Polity |
|--------------------|----------------|
| Mechanical | Government can be designed and imposed on any population — the polity is passive material |
| Organic | Government grows spontaneously from the character of the people — the polity determines the government |
| Mill's synthesis | Government is constructed, but must be "suited to the people" — polity constrains but does not determine [[political-institution|institutional design]] (001-003) |

> "Different forms of polity" produce different outcomes — the choice of institutions matters, but it must be appropriate to the people for whom it is made (001-004)

## Polity and Political Character

The most distinctive feature of Mill's concept of polity is its connection to political character. A polity is not merely a set of institutions but a community of citizens with specific habits, capacities, and dispositions:

> "A very different polity from a nation of savages" — the same institutions would produce different results in different populations, because the character of the people is part of the polity (002-029)

| Element of Polity | Contribution to Government |
|--------------------|----------------------------|
| Institutions | The formal structures of government — parliament, executive, courts |
| Laws | The rules that govern conduct and define rights |
| Political culture | The habits, expectations, and norms that shape how institutions actually operate |
| Civic capacity | The knowledge, judgment, and public-spiritedness of the citizenry |
| Historical development | The accumulated experience that shapes what a people can and cannot do politically |

> "Inferior forms of polity will best carry those communities through the intermediate stages" — the best government for a people depends on where they are in their political development (002-030)

This developmental perspective is central to Mill's political thought. A polity is not static — it develops over time, and the form of government appropriate to it changes as it develops. The "inferior forms of polity" that suit a people at an early stage of development would be oppressive at a later stage; the "most perfect polity" that suits an advanced people would be unworkable for a less developed one (004-001).

## Polity and the Conditions of Good Government

Mill argues that the quality of government depends on the quality of the polity that sustains it. The best institutions will fail if the underlying polity lacks the capacities that those institutions presuppose:

> "Popular government is the only polity which can make out any claim to this character" — only a polity in which the people participate can claim to be self-governing (003-009)

The claim is both normative and empirical. Normatively, only [[democracy|popular government]] treats citizens as agents rather than subjects. Empirically, only [[democracy|popular government]] can sustain the active engagement and public-spiritedness on which good government depends. A polity in which the people are passive will produce passive government, regardless of the formal [[political-institution|institutional design]].

> "The benefits of the institution or polity which they recommend" — Mill insists that the benefits of any political arrangement must be demonstrated, not merely asserted (001-012)

## Polity and Institutional Design

Mill's concept of polity has important implications for [[political-institution|institutional design]]. The designer of institutions must ask not "what is the best system in general?" but "what system is best suited to *this* polity, with its specific characteristics?"

> "In constituting a polity, certain provisions ought to be made with a view to permanence" — institutions must be designed for durability, not merely for immediate effect (002-008)

| Design Principle | Implication |
|------------------|-------------|
| Fit to character | Institutions must match the capacities and habits of the governed (001-003) |
| Capacity for development | Institutions should foster the growth of civic capacity, not merely accommodate its current level |
| Permanence | Institutions should be designed to endure — constant constitutional change is a sign of failure (001-006) |
| Adaptability | But institutions must also be capable of adapting as the polity develops (002-030) |

> "The ideal type of the most perfect polity" — Mill holds out an ideal, but recognises that it may not be immediately attainable for every people (004-001)

This is the balance that defines Mill's political science: the "perfect polity" is the regulative ideal, but the actual institutions of any given society must be fitted to its actual condition. The art of political construction is the art of building institutions that are appropriate to the present while capable of developing toward the ideal.
