The question of electoral **pledges** — whether members of [[parliament|Parliament]] should be bound by promises made to their [[constituent|constituents]] during elections — is a central issue in the theory of [[representative-government|representative government]]. Mill addresses pledges in the context of the proper relationship between representatives and the represented, arguing that pledges undermine the [[deliberation|deliberative function]] of Parliament.

## Mill's Position on Pledges

Mill examines the question of pledges as part of his broader discussion of the relationship between electors and their representatives. He categorises the issue as one that touches on fundamental questions of political morality and constitutional design. (012-003)

> The question of pledges is not one of those which vitally concern the existence of representative government, but it is very important to its beneficial operation.
>
> (012-003)

Mill argues that while a representative should generally act in accordance with the views of his constituents, the practice of extracting specific pledges before an election reduces the representative from a trustee to a mere delegate.

## Pledges and the Trustee Model

Mill contends that the proper function of a representative is not simply to mirror the opinions of his constituents, but to exercise independent judgment informed by deliberation with fellow representatives. When a candidate gives specific pledges, he surrenders this deliberative capacity before the discussion has even begun.

The representative should be chosen for his general principles and character, not for his willingness to commit to predetermined positions on specific questions. Mill distinguishes between a representative who broadly shares the views of his constituents — which is healthy and desirable — and one who has contracted himself to vote in a particular way regardless of subsequent debate. (012-009)

> We may affirm that actual pledges should not be required unless from the sinister interest of the representative, or the general habit of disregarding political engagements, there is great danger that, without a pledge, no attention would be paid to the wishes of the constituents.
>
> (012-009)

## Practical Considerations

Mill acknowledges that in certain political contexts, pledges may serve a protective function. When there is widespread distrust of representatives, or when representatives have shown a pattern of disregarding the interests of their constituents, pledges may be justified as a check on abuse.

However, Mill views this as a remedy for a diseased political condition, not as a desirable feature of a well-functioning representative system. The healthier the political culture, the less need there is for pledges. (018-014)

## Pledges and Colonial Governance

In his discussion of colonial governance, Mill extends the principle to imperial administration. He argues that the government should be open on equal terms to all subjects of the empire, rather than being restricted by pledges to particular constituencies or interest groups. The withholding of equal opportunity based on colonial origin, like the binding of representatives by narrow pledges, degrades the political community. (018-014)
