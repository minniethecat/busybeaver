**Suffrage** is the right by which citizens participate in the constitution of the [[representation|representative]] body. Mill treats [[franchise|suffrage]] not as an abstract natural right — "it is not useful, but hurtful, that the constitution of the country should declare ignorance entitled to as much political power as knowledge" — but as a trust to be exercised for the public good (008-004). The question is not whether all should have a voice, but how the [[voting|franchise]] should be distributed to produce the best government for all.

Mill's position is deliberately intermediate: he rejects the exclusion of whole classes from [[franchise|suffrage]] as oligarchic, and he equally rejects "[[universal-suffrage|equal universal suffrage]]" unaccompanied by institutional checks as dangerous to good government (008-012).

## Universal versus Qualified Suffrage

Mill's analysis of [[franchise|suffrage]] begins with a dilemma. On one hand, "excluding fewest from the [[franchise|suffrage]]" is a necessary condition of [[democracy|popular government]]; on the other, "opening to all" without distinction risks lowering the quality of deliberation (006-003).

> "There is not equal suffrage where every single individual does not count for as much as any other single individual" — yet equal suffrage, in a society of unequal intelligence, can produce unequal justice (007-004)

The table below summarizes the competing principles:

| Principle | Rationale | Limit |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Universal [[franchise|suffrage]] | No one should be governed without consent | Ignores differences in capacity to judge |
| Qualified [[franchise|suffrage]] | Only the competent should decide | Easily becomes oligarchic; who decides who is competent? |
| Graduated [[franchise|suffrage]] | Plural voting by education/occupation | Transitional; aim is universal education |
| Exclusion by illiteracy | Cannot read → cannot form independent judgment | Acceptable only if education is freely available |

## Graduated Suffrage and Plural Voting

Mill's distinctive proposal is graduated suffrage — a system in which every citizen has at least one [[voting|vote]], but those with demonstrated capacity receive more:

> "Between this and equal universal suffrage, whoever does not desire the inequality, desires the degradation of the representative body to the level of the lowest" (008-012)

The scheme operates on several principles:
- **Universal baseline**: Every adult receives at least one vote — "the [[franchise|suffrage]] would be, with that slight abatement, universal" (008-007).
- **Superiority of weight**: Additional votes are assigned to "the [[franchise|suffrage]]s of those whose opinion is entitled to greater weight" by reason of education, occupation, or demonstrated public service (008-009).
- **No class monopoly**: The plural votes are not attached to property or inheritance but to individual capacity, preventing the creation of a permanent electoral aristocracy.
- **Open access**: "They leave the [[franchise|suffrage]] accessible to all who are in the normal condition of a human being" — the door to additional votes is open to anyone who attains the qualifications (008-006).

## The Literacy Condition

Mill supports a minimal literacy requirement for the franchise — not as a permanent barrier but as an incentive to education:

> "I regard it as wholly inadmissible that any person should participate in the suffrage without being able to read, write, and perform the common operations of arithmetic" (008-004)

This condition is legitimate, Mill argues, because:
- The state provides education universally — anyone who lacks literacy has refused a public good freely offered.
- Suffrage is a trust, not a right — the voter acts on behalf of others, not merely for himself.
- The requirement is instrumental: it aims at universal literacy, not permanent exclusion.

## Suffrage and the Ballot

Mill's treatment of [[franchise|suffrage]] is inseparable from his analysis of the [[voting|ballot]] — whether voting should be open or secret. In Chapter X, he argues that "the electors must use the [[franchise|suffrage]] in the manner supposed by the constitution, which is that of a public trust" (009-002).

> "Giving the suffrage, and with it the complete ultimate power, to the numerical majority" — if suffrage is exercised in secret, the voter acts on private interest rather than public duty (009-001)

Mill's conclusion on the [[voting|ballot]] is controversial — he held that public elections should use [[ballot|open voting]], while [[ballot|secret ballot]]s are appropriate only where voters face intimidation. The core principle is that [[franchise|suffrage]] must be exercised "on public grounds" rather than for "private interest" (004-004).

## Women's Suffrage

In one of the most forward-looking passages of the *Considerations*, Mill declares:

> "I consider it as irrelevant to political rights as differences in height; that women should have the suffrage. Were it as right as it is wrong that women should be excluded, the inconsistency would be glaring" (008-017)

Mill treats the exclusion of women from the franchise as a self-evident injustice inconsistent with the principles of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]. If [[franchise|suffrage]] is a trust to be exercised for the public good, there is no ground for excluding half the adult population from that trust.
