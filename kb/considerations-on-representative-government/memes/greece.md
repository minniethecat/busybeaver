## Introduction

**Greece** occupies a distinctive dual position in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government*. Ancient Greece — particularly [[athenian-democracy|Athens]] — serves as the classical exemplar of democratic citizenship and civic virtue. Modern Greece, by contrast, appears as a cautionary example of a nation Mill considered insufficiently developed for representative institutions. (004-004)

## Ancient Greece as Democratic Model

Mill repeatedly invokes the ancient Greek city-states as evidence of what participatory government can achieve. He notes that the Greek cities, "while their freedom lasted," were "both more exempt from social injustice and crime, and have attained more brilliant prosperity than any others, or than they themselves after they lost their freedom." (003-013) The intellectual and civic achievement of Athens in particular demonstrated for Mill the educative power of democratic participation.

Mill argued that free institutions produced an unparalleled elevation of human faculties. In a famous passage, he contrasted the stagnation of societies under [[despotism|despotism]] with the dynamism of free communities: if a society "had realized any thing high in intellect or character, it was not from the natural tendency of those institutions to call it forth, but because, like Greece or Rome, it had on some points broken its fetters." (003-003)

> Kingly government, free from the control (though perhaps strengthened by the support) of representative institutions, is the most suitable form of polity for the earliest stages of any community, not excepting a city community like those of ancient Greece; where, accordingly, the government of kings, under some real, but no ostensible or constitutional control by public opinion, did historically precede by an unknown and probably great duration all free institutions, and gave place at last, during a considerable lapse of time, to [[oligarchy|oligarchies]] of a few families.
>
> (004-009)

## The Conditions of Freedom

Mill identified specific conditions that made Greek democracy possible. The small scale of the city-state, the institution of slavery (which freed citizens for public life), and the intensity of civic participation all contributed. However, he was careful not to romanticize: he acknowledged that "in [[italy|the Greek and Italian republics]], it is true, the slavery of a part of the population was one of the circumstances which enabled the rest to govern." (002-029)

## Modern Greece as a Cautionary Example

Mill's view of modern Greece was far less favorable. In discussing the conditions under which representative government is inapplicable, he cited the modern kingdom of Greece as an example where "it can hardly be said that representative institutions have been successful," attributing this to the population lacking "the will or the capacity to fulfill the part which belongs to them in a representative constitution." (004-004) This judgment reflected Mill's broader thesis that representative government requires a people ready to exercise it — a readiness that ancient Greeks possessed but modern Greeks, in his assessment, had not yet regained.
