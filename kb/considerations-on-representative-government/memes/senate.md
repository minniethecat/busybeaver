In *Considerations on Representative Government*, John Stuart Mill discusses the senate as an institutional form that embodies political experience and expertise.

He draws on two principal historical examples — the Roman Senate and the United States Senate — to show how a body of seasoned public officials can serve as a moderating and impelling force within a representative system (013-007). For Mill, the senate is less an institution than a principle: the association of special training and knowledge with representative government.

## The Roman Senate

Mill holds the Roman Senate in the highest regard, calling it "the most consistently prudent and sagacious body that ever administered public affairs" (013-007).

Its strength, in his view, lay in its composition: the Senate "was exclusively composed of persons who had exercised public functions, and had either already filled, or were looking forward to fill the highest offices of the state, at the peril of a severe responsibility in case of incapacity and failure" (006-007).

Once admitted to the Senate, members' lives were "pledged to the conduct of public affairs." They could not leave Italy except on public business, and unless expelled by the censors for disgraceful conduct, they retained their powers and responsibilities until death (006-007).

This created a body in which "every member felt his personal importance entirely bound up with the dignity and estimation of the commonwealth which he administered" (006-007).

> The really governing body, the Senate, was exclusively composed of persons who had exercised public functions, and had either already filled, or were looking forward to fill the highest offices of the state, at the peril of a severe responsibility in case of incapacity and failure.
>
> (006-007)

## The United States Senate

Mill identifies the United States Senate as another exemplary institution. In the American federal system, the Senate represents the states as such, with each state returning an equal number of members regardless of population (009-004).

Senators are chosen not by direct popular vote but by state legislatures — a system of indirect election that Mill considers, under certain conditions, "the very best system that can be adopted" (009-004).

The result, Mill observes, is that the Senate invariably consists of "the most distinguished men among those who have made themselves sufficiently known in public life" (009-004). He contrasts this with the House of Representatives, noting the Lower House has been as remarkable for the absence of personal merit as the Upper House for its presence (017-009).

> The elections thus made have proved eminently successful, and are conspicuously the best of all the elections in the United States, the Senate invariably consisting of the most distinguished men among those who have made themselves sufficiently known in public life.
>
> (009-004)

## The Senate as a Model for Second Chambers

Mill draws on the senate model to propose the composition of an ideal second chamber. "If one House represents popular feeling," he argues, "the other should represent personal merit, tested and guaranteed by actual public service, and fortified by practical experience. If one is the People's Chamber, the other should be the Chamber of Statesmen" (013-007).

Such a chamber "would be fitted for much more than to be a merely moderating body. It would not be exclusively a check, but also an impelling force" (013-007).

Mill proposes that members should be drawn from "all living public men who have passed through important political office or employment" (013-007), creating a council whose authority would rest on demonstrated competence rather than class interest.

| Attribute | Roman Senate | US Senate | Mill's Ideal |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|-------------|
| Composition | Former magistrates and office-holders | Elected by state legislatures | Public men who have held important office |
| Term | Life (subject to censorial review) | Fixed term (indirect election) | Life or long tenure |
| Function | Governing body | Legislative chamber + state representation | Chamber of Statesmen: check + impelling force |
| Key virtue | Experience and responsibility | Distinguished membership | Personal merit, tested by public service |

## The Legislative Commission Model

Beyond the composition of a second chamber, Mill explores its legislative function. Drawing on the Athenian [[democracy]], he notes that even in its most democratic period, the popular assembly could pass decrees on single matters, but laws proper could only be made or altered by the nomothetae, a smaller body renewed annually whose duty included keeping the laws consistent (005-014).

Mill proposes adapting the English [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] into a Legislative Commission — a body of professional legislators appointed as peers for life. Such a commission, he argues, would be no more an innovation than existing administrative boards (005-014).

> A commission for preparing bills would in itself be no more an innovation on the Constitution than the Board for the administration of the Poor Laws, or the Inclosure Commission.
>
> (005-014)

Under this arrangement, bills originating in the Upper House would be drawn up by the commission, the government would entrust it with framing all its bills, and private members of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] would refer their proposals to it for expert drafting.

Legislation would thereby "assume its proper place as a work of skilled labor and special study and experience," while the liberty of being governed only by laws assented to by elected representatives would be fully preserved (005-014).
