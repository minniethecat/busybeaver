## Introduction

**Australia** appears in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as an example of the class of [[dependencies]] populated by settlers of similar civilization to the ruling country, who are "capable of, and ripe for, [[representative-government|representative government]]." (018-003)

In Chapter XVIII, "Of the Government of Dependencies by a Free State," Mill uses the Australian colonies to illustrate the proper principles for governing outlying territories of European settlement.

## Classification as a Dependency

Mill divides dependencies into two classes: those whose population is "of similar civilization to the ruling country, capable of, and ripe for, representative government," and those "still at a great distance from that state." (018-003) The British possessions in [[united-states|America]] and Australia belong to the first class.

He argues that such dependencies ought to be granted the fullest measure of internal [[self-government]], consistent with their remaining part of the empire.

## Self-Government and Colonial Policy

In discussing the proper governance of settler colonies, Mill notes that Britain had, until his own generation, been "on the same bad level with other countries" in restricting the self-government of colonies, claiming "to be the supreme arbiter even of their purely internal concerns." (018-004)

However, he acknowledges that Britain had at length "realized, in rare completeness, the true principle of government" with respect to these dependencies. The Australian and American colonies had been given up to self-government, becoming "substantive powers among nations."

## The Limits of Distant Governance

Mill was skeptical of proposals to integrate distant colonies into a single representative [[federal|federation]] with the mother country. He argued that "countries separated by half the globe do not present the natural conditions for being under one government, or even members of one federation." (018-006)

They lack "a sufficient habit of taking council together" and "are not part of the same public."

This logic supported his view that colonies like Australia should eventually become self-governing, bound to Britain only by shared interests and voluntary union.

> Countries separated by half the globe do not present the natural conditions for being under one government, or even members of one federation. They are not part of the same public; they do not discuss and deliberate in the same arena, but apart, and have only a most imperfect knowledge of what passes in the minds of one another.
>
> (018-006)
