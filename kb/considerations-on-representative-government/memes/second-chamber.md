In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], the **second chamber** (or upper house) is a constitutional mechanism for tempering the power of the popular assembly. Mill evaluates whether a second chamber provides effective restraint on democracy, and if so, what composition best serves that function. He regards the question of "Two Chambers" as "a sort of touchstone which distinguishes the partisans of limited from those of uncontrolled democracy" (013-001).

## The Purpose of a Second Chamber

Mill identifies several rationales for bicameralism, but finds most of them insufficient or secondary to the deeper constitutional need:

| Rationale | Mill's Assessment |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Preventing precipitancy | "A very ill-constituted representative assembly" already requires multiple deliberations through its own forms of business (013-003) |
| Compelling second deliberation | Redundant in any properly organized single chamber |
| Check on despotic tendency of undivided power | **The strongest argument** — "the evil effect produced upon the mind of any holder of power... by the consciousness of having only themselves to consult" (013-003) |
| School of conciliation | "The mutual give and take... between two houses is a perpetual school" of compromise (013-003) |

Mill draws an analogy to the Roman Republic: "The same reason which induced the Romans to have two consuls makes it desirable there should be two Chambers — that neither of them may be exposed to the corrupting influence of undivided power even for the space of a single year" (013-003).

## Conditions for Effectiveness

A second chamber's restraining power depends entirely on its social basis outside the legislature. Mill is skeptical that an aristocratic chamber can check a democratic one in a democratic society:

> "An assembly which does not rest on the basis of some great power in the country is ineffectual against one which does. An aristocratic House is only powerful in an aristocratic state of society."
>
> (013-004)

In a democratic polity, the second chamber must not "hold itself apart... compelling every one to declare himself either with or against it," but should work "as one of the elements in a mixed mass, infusing its leaven" (013-004).

Mill maintains that "in every polity, a centre of resistance to the predominant power in the Constitution... is a fundamental maxim of government" (013-005). However, he doubts that a hereditary or class-based second chamber can serve this role in a truly democratic state:

> "If there are two houses, one considered to represent the people, the other to represent only a class... I can not think that, where democracy is the ruling power in society, the second House would have any real ability to resist even the aberrations of the first."
>
> (013-005)

## Models of Composition

### The Senate of Statesmen (Mill's Preferred Model)

Mill's ideal second chamber is modeled on the [[senate|Roman Senate]] — "the most consistently prudent and sagacious body that ever administered public affairs" (013-007). Its principle is expertise grounded in public service:

- **"If one House represents popular feeling, the other should represent personal merit"** — tested and guaranteed by actual public service (013-007)
- **"If one is the People's Chamber, the other should be the Chamber of Statesmen"** — composed of all living public figures who have passed through important political offices (013-007)
- This body would be "not exclusively a check, but also an impelling force" — its members are "most inclined to lead [the people] forward in any right course" (013-007)

Mill sketches a concrete composition for England: members of a Legislative Commission, chief justices, cabinet ministers with two years' service, commanders-in-chief, governors-general, and senior civil servants with ten years' tenure (013-008).

### The Elected Senate (Alternative)

As an alternative, Mill considers a second chamber **elected by the First Chamber** (similar to indirect election), subject to the restriction that members may not nominate their own colleagues (013-010). He notes this model:

- "Would not be considered to clash with democratic institutions" (013-010)
- Would likely be "well composed" and include capable individuals who could not win popular election (013-010)
- Has historical precedent in the United States Senate (before direct election)

### Adaptation of the House of Lords

Given England's historical circumstances, Mill acknowledges that any second chamber reform must build on the existing House of Lords. He proposes:

| Reform | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Life peerages | Add qualified statesmen as life peers (013-009) |
| Representative peers | Hereditary peers elect representatives, rather than sitting personally (013-009) |
| Hare's plan adaptation | Peers group into constituencies of ten, each electing one representative (013-009) |
| Commons eligibility | Peers not chosen as representatives become eligible for the House of Commons (013-009) |

## The Best Constitution

Mill's conclusion synthesizes his analysis into a single principle:

> "The best constitution of a Second Chamber is that which embodies the greatest number of elements exempt from the class interests and prejudices of the majority, but having in themselves nothing offensive to democratic feeling."
>
> (013-011)

He adds the crucial caveat: "the main reliance for tempering the ascendancy of the majority can [not] be placed in a Second Chamber of any kind. The character of a representative government is fixed by the constitution of the popular House. Compared with this, all other questions relating to the form of government are insignificant" (013-011).

## Relation to Other Reforms

Mill's treatment of the second chamber is integrated with his broader reform program. The [[proportional-representation|proportional representation]] of minorities in the popular house — through [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme — is in his view a more effective check on majority power than any second chamber could be (013-006). A properly composed popular house, representing all significant minorities, "will afford, in a most effective form, the moral centre of resistance which is needed" (013-006).
