# Federal Government

A **federal government** is a system in which separate political communities are united under a common central authority for certain defined purposes, while retaining independent governments for all other purposes. Mill devotes an entire chapter (Chapter XVII) to the conditions, structure, and stability of federal unions, drawing primarily on the American example.

## Definition and Purpose

Mill defines federal union as a middle ground between complete unification and complete separation. Various communities "may often, with advantage, be federally united as to their relations with foreigners" (017-001) while maintaining independence in internal matters. A federal tie allows communities to "remain united by a federal tie" (016-010) where full political integration is impractical.

The essential condition of stable federation is clear:

> "The condition of the stability of a federal government is that the separate governments shall be so many, and shall have such a balance of power, that no one of them, nor any confederacy of a minority of them, shall be strong enough to resist the federal authorities when supported by the majority." (017-003)

## Conditions for Stability

Mill identifies several prerequisites for durable federal unions:

| Condition | Description | Reference |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Mutual non-aggression | Members must not want to fight each other | (017-002) |
| Balanced power | No single state or minority coalition can resist federal authority | (017-003) |
| Common external interests | Foreign relations provide unifying pressure | (017-001) |
| Compatible institutions | Internal political structures should not conflict | (017-009) |
| Sufficient multiplicity | Enough members to prevent dangerous coalitions | (017-010) |

> "When the separate states fight on opposite sides, the federal tie is neither likely to be of long duration, nor, if it is, to be respected." (017-002)

## Powers of a Federal Government

"The powers of a federal government naturally extend not only to peace and war, and all questions which arise between the country and foreign governments, but to the preservation of internal order, and the protection of person and property against wrong" (017-008). Mill argues that the federal authority must be supreme in its defined sphere.

The federal government must have "the power of enforcing obedience on the part of the separate governments to the federal authority when they are recalcitrant" (017-007).

## The American Federal Model

Mill draws extensively on the United States as the primary example of successful federation:

> "The most conspicuous example of a federal government is that of the United States of America." (017-005)

He notes the American federal [[senate|Senate]] as a distinctive institution: the states elect senators "to represent the state in the federal Senate" (009-004), creating a body that represents states as entities, not just populations. The "American federal [[constitution|Constitution]]" (014-009) provides a model of how sovereignty can be divided between general and state governments.

Mill observed that in "the American Republic, the federal and state assemblies" (007-013) together form the representative structure, with distinct jurisdictions.

## Federal vs. Complete Union

Mill distinguishes between "a complete or a merely federal union" (017-011) and argues the choice depends on circumstances:

- A **complete union** (unitary state) requires sufficient homogeneity among the people
- A **federal union** preserves local autonomy while providing collective strength
- A **confederation** (looser than federal) risks instability from centrifugal forces

> "The multiplication of free states is favourable to the progress of civilization, but a federal union of those states is the most effectual mode of reconciling this advantage with the equally important one of collective strength." (017-010)

## Federal Government Structure

Mill considers the internal structure of a federal government particularly important. "The fitting constitution of a federal government within itself, much more depends on local and temporary circumstances" (017-009). He notes that federal systems require:

1. An umpire or court to resolve federal-state conflicts (017-007)
2. Clear demarcation of federal vs. state powers
3. A representative structure that balances population and state interests
