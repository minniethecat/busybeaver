## Introduction

**Italy** appears in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* in two distinct historical contexts. The medieval Italian republics — [[senate|Venice]], [[civilization|Florence]], Genoa — serve as examples of free communities that achieved prosperity through self-government. The 19th-century movement for Italian unification (the Risorgimento) provides Mill with a contemporary illustration of the legitimate formation of a federal union grounded in shared nationality. (017-011)

## The Italian Republics as Models of Free Government

Mill cited the Italian republics and [[germany|the free towns of Germany]] as evidence that free communities consistently outperformed despotic ones. "Contrast the free states of the world, while their freedom lasted, with the cotemporary subjects of monarchical or oligarchical despotism," he wrote: "the Greek cities with [[despotism|the Persian satrapies]]; the Italian republics and the free towns of Flanders and Germany, with the feudal monarchies of Europe." (003-013) The Italian republics demonstrated that self-governing communities could achieve "more brilliant prosperity than any others."

## Venice and Aristocratic Government

Among the Italian republics, Mill reserved particular attention for Venice as an example of aristocratic governance. The Venetian republic, he observed, manifested "high governing capacities" and acted "on steady maxims of policy through many generations" — but only because "the actual management of affairs was rigidly concentrated in a small oligarchy within the oligarchy, whose whole lives were devoted to public business." (006-007) This illustrated his broader argument that effective aristocracies were essentially bureaucracies of public functionaries rather than hereditary leisure classes.

> When once members of the Senate, their lives were pledged to the conduct of public affairs; they were not permitted even to leave Italy except in the discharge of some public trust; and unless turned out of the Senate by the censors for character or conduct deemed disgraceful, they retained their powers and responsibilities to the end of life.
>
> (006-007)

## Italian Unification and the Federal Principle

Mill viewed the contemporary movement for Italian unification as exemplifying the principle that nations defined by shared language, culture, and sentiment have a right to form their own government. He noted that "a union of Italy" presented itself as a case "when a people united by identity of race and language, but partitioned for ages under separate governments, are at length afforded the opportunity of combining into one." (017-011) For Mill, such a union — whether federal or unitary — was entirely legitimate, grounded as it was in the shared nationality of the Italian people.
