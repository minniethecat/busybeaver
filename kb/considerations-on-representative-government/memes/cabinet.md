# Cabinet

A **cabinet** is the body of senior ministers who collectively direct the executive government. In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], the cabinet's role is to administer policy and prepare legislation, while the [[parliament|representative assembly]] retains ultimate control and deliberation.

## Composition and Function

Mill describes the cabinet as a small body comparable in size to a legislative commission. A representative assembly should not itself administer but "should control the administration" (005-011), with the cabinet serving as the executive instrument. He proposes that the assembly should "nominate even the members of a cabinet" (005-011), emphasizing that the representative body holds authority over who serves in executive roles.

The cabinet functions best when structured as "a Commission of Legislation" (005-013) — a specialized body tasked with preparing laws for consideration by the larger assembly. This division of labour separates the technical work of drafting from the political work of deciding.

## Cabinet Officers and Qualifications

Mill proposes that individuals who have held "for two years any cabinet office" (013-008) should be among those entitled to additional votes or special electoral weight. This reflects his view that executive experience confers practical knowledge of government that merits recognition in the political system.

The relationship between the cabinet and the [[parliament|representative assembly]] is one of responsibility: the cabinet executes policy but remains answerable to the representatives of the people.

## Cabinet Government in Dependencies

In colonial contexts, Mill observes that governance through a cabinet presents particular challenges. He notes that an English cabinet minister governing a dependency may act "thinking of" (018-018) domestic political considerations rather than the interests of the dependency itself. A member of a cabinet "under a representative constitution" (018-019) faces different constraints and incentives than one governing a colony or dependency.

Mill criticizes a colonial committee (comparable to "the present cabinet" (018-028)) for objections to local representative institutions, reflecting his broader argument that dependencies deserve participatory governance.

## Relation to Representative Government

The cabinet is essential to Mill's constitutional architecture: it provides executive competence while remaining subordinate to popular control. This arrangement ensures that administration benefits from specialized knowledge without becoming detached from the public interest.

> "…ominate even the members of a cabinet. It is enough that it virtual…" (005-011)
