## Mill's Argument

John Stuart Mill devoted two substantial paragraphs of *Considerations on Representative Government* to arguing that the exclusion of women from the suffrage was indefensible. His case was radical for its time: he considered the difference of sex to be "as entirely irrelevant to political rights as difference in height or in the color of the hair" (008-016).

Mill grounded his argument in the fundamental premise that "all human beings have the same interest in good government; the welfare of all is alike affected by it, and they have equal need of a voice in it to secure their share of its benefits." If there were any difference, he added, "women require it more than men, since, being physically weaker, they are more dependent on law and society for protection" (008-016).

## Against the Dependency Argument

Mill addressed the common objection that women would simply vote as dependents of their male relatives. His response was pragmatic: if women think for themselves, good will be done; if they merely follow their husbands' bidding, no harm is done. "It is a benefit to human beings to take off their fetters, even if they do not desire to walk" (008-017).

He further argued that giving women the vote would elevate their moral position, raise their dignity in the eyes of vulgar men, and improve the quality of voting itself — since a husband would need to find honest reasons for his vote when discussing it with a politically enfranchised wife (008-017).

> Were it as right as it is wrong that they should be a subordinate class, confined to domestic occupations and subject to domestic authority, they would not the less require the protection of the suffrage to secure them from the abuse of that authority. Men, as well as women, do not need political rights in order that they may govern, but in order that they may not be misgoverned.
>
> (008-017)

## The Class-Interest Argument

In discussing the mode of voting, Mill returned to women's exclusion to illustrate the danger of class legislation. Even under so-called "universal suffrage" — which he dismissed as the "silly title of manhood suffrage" — voters would "still have a class interest as distinguished from women." He asked pointedly: if legislation concerning women's property rights, university graduation, or protections against domestic violence were before Parliament, "are not a man's wife and daughters entitled to know whether he votes for or against a candidate who will support these propositions?" (010-011)

## Property Qualification Inconsistency

Mill observed a "flagrant contradiction" in countries where the suffrage depended on property qualifications: a woman who met all the same property conditions as a male elector was nevertheless excluded solely on account of sex. He noted that this was particularly ironic in a country "where a woman now reigns, and that the most glorious ruler whom that country ever had was a woman" — a reference to Queen Victoria (008-018).

## Intellectual Influences

Mill acknowledged that his position was shared by "Bentham, Mr. Samuel Bailey, Mr. Hare, and many other of the most powerful political thinkers of this age and country." He expressed hope that "before the lapse another generation, the accident of sex, no more than the accident of skin, will be deemed a sufficient justification for depriving its possessor of the equal protection and just privileges of a citizen" (008-018).
