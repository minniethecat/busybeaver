**Switzerland** serves in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as the European case study in [[federal|federalism]] — demonstrating both the failures of weak confederation and the conditions under which diverse communities can sustain shared representative institutions.

## The Failed Confederation Model

Mill distinguishes two modes of federal organization: a union of governments (mere alliance) and a union that acts directly on citizens. Switzerland before 1847 exemplified the former — and its failures:

> "The federal authorities may represent the governments solely, and their acts may be obligatory only on the governments as such... The former is the plan of the German so-called Confederation, and of the Swiss Constitution previous to 1847." (017-005)

Under this arrangement, federal authority depended on compliance by cantonal governments. Mill observes that such a system could only avoid breakdown if "the federal government felt its weakness so strongly that it hardly ever attempted to exercise any real authority" (017-005). Switzerland's experience confirmed the general principle: a federation of governments, without direct authority over citizens, is "more likely to be a cause than a preventive of internal wars" (017-005).

## The Transition to Effective Federalism

Mill notes that Switzerland adopted the American model of federalism "within the last dozen years" — that is, with the Constitution of 1848, which followed the brief Sonderbund civil war of 1847. This new constitution, like that of the [[united-states|United States]], made federal law binding directly on individual citizens rather than mediated through cantonal governments (017-005).

The Swiss case thus demonstrates a broader pattern Mill identifies: "the progress of civilization which [[representative-government|representative government]] would have had a decided tendency to promote" (004-008) — federal institutions evolve toward more effective forms as societies develop.

## National Sentiment and Multinational Federation

In Chapter XVI, Mill addresses the challenge of nationality. Switzerland provides his key example of a successful multinational state:

> "Switzerland has a strong sentiment of nationality, though the cantons are of different races, different languages, and different religions." (016-001)

This observation supports Mill's argument that shared political institutions can forge national sentiment across ethnic and linguistic divides — provided those institutions enjoy legitimacy and demonstrate practical benefits. Switzerland had experienced "no interruption of internal peace" for "several centuries" through its federal arrangements (017-002), though Mill wrote before the 1847 civil war that prompted constitutional reform.

## Economic Prosperity and Free Institutions

Mill groups Switzerland with Holland, England, and America as nations whose "superior prosperity" demonstrates the connection between free institutions and economic flourishing (003-013). This comparative prosperity argument reinforces Mill's case that [[representative-government|representative government]] promotes the general welfare.
