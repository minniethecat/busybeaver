**Benjamin Disraeli** (1804–1881) was a British Conservative statesman and novelist who served twice as Prime Minister. He is referenced by [[John Stuart Mill]] in *Considerations on Representative Government* at two points in the argument.

## References in Mill's Text

### Electoral Reform

Mill invokes Disraeli in his discussion of schemes for securing [[minority]] representation. Reviewing proposals to allow constituencies to return three members while restricting each elector to two votes, Mill notes:

> Lord John Russell, in one of his Reform Bills, introduced a provision that certain constituencies should return three members, and that in these each elector should be allowed to vote only for two; and Mr. Disraeli, in the recent debates, revived the memory of the fact by reproaching him for it, being of opinion, apparently, that it befits a Conservative statesman to regard only means, and to disown scornfully all fellow-feeling with any one who is betrayed, even once, into thinking of ends. (007-007)

Mill's sarcasm is directed at Disraeli's dismissal of electoral reform proposals that would give representation to political minorities. For Mill, Disraeli exemplifies the Conservative tendency to prioritize institutional stability over representative fairness.

### Conservative Principles

In a lengthy footnote to his discussion of the government of dependencies, Mill criticizes Disraeli more directly for what he sees as a failure to understand the principles of his own party:

> This blunder of Mr. Disraeli (from which, greatly to his credit, Sir John Pakington took an opportunity soon after of separating himself) is a speaking instance, among many, how little the Conservative leaders understand Conservative principles. (018-025)

Mill uses this criticism to advance a broader claim: "Well would it be for England if Conservatives voted consistently for every thing conservative, and Liberals for every thing liberal." (018-025) The passage reflects Mill's impatience with party politics that obstruct what he regards as measures — like [[minority]] representation — that are "eminently both the one and the other." (018-025)

## Significance in Mill's Argument

Disraeli appears in Mill's text as a representative figure of the [[opposition|Conservative opposition]] to electoral reform, particularly to measures designed to secure the representation of minorities within a majoritarian system.

Mill's criticism is consistent with his broader argument that the [[sinister-interest|sinister interests]] of party too often prevail over the rational consideration of [[representation|representative]] institutions.

The reference also illustrates the contemporary political context of Mill's work: written during the debates over the [[reform-bill-1832|Second Reform Bill]] and its aftermath, *Considerations on Representative Government* engages directly with the positions of sitting [[parliament|Parliamentarians]], including Disraeli, who was then a leading figure in the Conservative opposition.
## Disraeli and the Minority Representation Debate

Mill references Disraeli in the context of parliamentary debates over the representation of minorities. Discussing the problem that under existing electoral arrangements "the minority must yield to the majority" (007-003), Mill surveys several proposals for securing minority representation in the House of Commons.

Among these is the limited vote — a scheme in which electors in three-member constituencies are permitted to vote for only two candidates. Mill notes that "Mr. Disraeli, in the recent debates, revived the memory of the fact that it was originally a Tory scheme" (007-007), referring to the Reform Bill debates of 1867.

By either the limited vote or the cumulative vote, Mill observes, "a minority equalling or exceeding a third of the local constituency, would be able, if it attempted no more, to return one out of three members" (007-007).

Mill regards these schemes as "makeshifts" that "attain the end in a very imperfect manner" (007-007). Nevertheless, he laments that "none of these plans have been carried into effect, as any of them would have" improved the representativeness of the Commons.

Mill's reference to Disraeli situates the minority representation question within the partisan politics of the 1860s. The fact that Disraeli, a Conservative leader, had supported such schemes demonstrates that minority representation was not exclusively the property of any one party.

This strengthens Mill's case that the "virtual blotting out of the minority is no necessary or natural consequence of the representative principle" (007-006). The Disraeli reference thus serves both as historical evidence and as a rhetorical appeal to cross-party consensus on the need for reform.
