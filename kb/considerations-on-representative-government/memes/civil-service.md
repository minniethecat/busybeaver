The **civil service** in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* designates the permanent, professional class of public servants who conduct the daily business of government. Distinct from political ministers who come and go with changes of administration, the civil service constitutes "the permanent strength of the public service" — those who "remain to aid every minister by their experience and traditions, inform him by their knowledge of business, and conduct official details under his general control" (014-014).

## The Permanent Element of Government

Mill draws a sharp distinction between political [[executive|executives]] — mere politicians who may or may not possess professional knowledge of their departments — and the permanent civil servants who provide continuity and expertise. A minister's "general capacity, and the knowledge he ought to possess of the general interests of the country, will not, unless by occasional accident, be accompanied by adequate, and what may be called professional knowledge of the department over which he is called to preside" (014-005). The permanent civil service fills this gap.

> With regard to that large and important body which constitutes the permanent strength of the public service, those who do not change with changes of politics, but remain to aid every minister by their experience and traditions... it is evidently inadmissible that these should be liable to be turned out, and deprived of the whole benefit of their previous service, except for positive, proved, and serious misconduct.
>
> (014-014)

## Competitive Entry and Impartial Selection

Mill insists that entry to the civil service must be through [[competitive-examination|competitive examination]] conducted by persons "not engaged in politics." A minister, dependent on political supporters and subject to solicitations, cannot be trusted to make impartial appointments. The competitive examination removes patronage from the appointment process and ensures that merit, not connection, determines entry (014-015).

The examination should cover "proficiency in the ordinary branches of liberal [[education]]," since candidates enter young, "not as having learned, but in order that they may learn, their profession" (014-015). Mill argues that competitive examinations, beyond selecting the best candidates, produce a "sensible effect" on educational institutions throughout the country, as schoolmasters strive to produce pupils who distinguish themselves (014-016).

## Promotion: Seniority and Selection

Once admitted through competitive examination, civil servants should advance through a "mixed system of seniority and selection" (014-019). Those in routine positions should rise by seniority, while those entrusted with special responsibilities should be selected at the discretion of the department chief. Mill argues that if original appointments are made by open competition, the chief will generally make honest selections, since his establishment will consist of individuals "to whom, but for the official connection, he would have been a stranger" (014-019).

| Aspect | Mill's recommendation |
|---|---|
| Entry | Competitive examination by non-political examiners |
| Security of tenure | Removal only for "positive, proved, and serious misconduct" |
| Routine promotion | Seniority-based |
| Special appointments | Selection by department chief |
| Political independence | Appointees should not be dismissible for political convenience |

## Representation in the Legislature

Mill goes further, proposing that the permanent civil service should have [[representation]] in the second chamber. He suggests that the "Chamber of Statesmen" should include "all living public servants who have held high political or high civil office," giving the civil service a formal voice in [[legislation]] (013-007, 013-008).

## Criticisms and the "Monopoly" Charge

Mill acknowledges the criticism that competitive examination creates a "monopoly" of the civil service by a class of men who perform well on academic tests (018-020). He dismisses this objection as unfounded, arguing that the alternative — appointment by political patronage — produces far worse results. The competitive system at least ensures that appointees possess demonstrated intellectual capacity.
