[[John Stuart Mill]] opens *Considerations on Representative Government* with an analysis of what government is and whether its form can be chosen. He frames the discussion around two conflicting theories: one that treats government as a practical art akin to mechanical invention, another that views it as an organic product of national character, not subject to deliberate choice (001-001).

Mill's own position mediates between these extremes, arguing that while forms of government are indeed a matter of choice, that choice is constrained by the capacities and dispositions of the people who are to live under them (001-013).

## Two Conflicting Theories

Mill identifies two opposing conceptions of [[political-institution|political institutions]]. The first treats government as strictly a practical art: "Forms of government are assimilated to any other expedients for the attainment of human objects. They are regarded as wholly an affair of invention and contrivance" (001-002).

On this view, the task is simply to determine the best form of government and persuade others to adopt it, much as one would select a steam plow or a threshing machine.

The second theory holds that government "is not a matter of choice" but rather a product of the character and historical development of a people, "a sort of spontaneous product, and the science of government a branch (so to speak) of natural history" (001-003). Forms of government, on this view, "are not made, but grow" (001-004).

> Forms of government are not a matter of choice. We must take them, in the main, as we find them. Governments can not be constructed by premeditated design. They "are not made, but grow."
>
> (001-003)

## Government as a Matter of Choice

Mill rejects both theories as exclusive doctrines. He argues that "within the limits set by the three conditions so often adverted to, institutions and forms of government are a matter of choice" (001-013). He insists that "to inquire into the best form of government in the abstract (as it is called) is not a chimerical, but a highly practical employment of scientific intellect" (001-013).

Mill draws an analogy with mechanics: while we cannot make a river run backwards, we do not therefore conclude that watermills "are not made, but grow."

In politics as in mechanics, "the power which is to keep the engine going must be sought for outside the machinery; and if it is not forthcoming, or is insufficient to surmount the obstacles which may reasonably be expected, the contrivance will fail" (001-013).

> The result of what has been said is, that, within the limits set by the three conditions so often adverted to, institutions and forms of government are a matter of choice. To inquire into the best form of government in the abstract (as it is called) is not a chimerical, but a highly practical employment of scientific intellect.
>
> (001-013)

## The Three Conditions

Mill sets out three fundamental conditions that any form of government must satisfy (001-006). First, "the people for whom the form of government is intended must be willing to accept it, or, at least not so unwilling as to oppose an insurmountable obstacle to its establishment." Second, "they must be willing and able to do what is necessary to keep it standing."

Third, "they must be willing and able to do what it requires of them to enable it to fulfill its purposes" (001-006).

These conditions form the backbone of Mill's argument throughout the work: the best form of government in theory may be inapplicable to a people not yet prepared for it. The doctrine of the three conditions establishes that governmental legitimacy fundamentally depends upon the active [[consent]] and capability of the governed.

> The people for whom the form of government is intended must be willing to accept it, or, at least not so unwilling as to oppose an insurmountable obstacle to its establishment. They must be willing and able to do what is necessary to keep it standing. And they must be willing and able to do what it requires of them to enable it to fulfill its purposes.
>
> (001-006)

## Adaptation to National Character

Mill emphasizes that a form of government must be adapted to the particular people it governs. Even a people not actively opposed to a form of government may lack the habits and capacities needed to work it (001-009). He notes that in the ancient world, the difficulty of forming large political communities was a formidable impediment to certain forms of government (001-010).

The broader principle is that "the adaptation of forms of government to the people who are to be governed" is the central problem of political science (001-011).
