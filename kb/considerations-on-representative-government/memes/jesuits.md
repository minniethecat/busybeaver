The **Jesuits** (Society of Jesus) appear in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* primarily through their missions in Paraguay, which Mill characterizes as a form of "government of leading-strings" — a paternalistic despotism that guides a people toward civilization while gradually training them for self-government.

> …such was that of the Jesuits of Paraguay.
>
> (002-029)

## The Government of Leading-Strings

Mill situates the Jesuit missions in Paraguay within his broader taxonomy of governmental forms suited to different stages of civilization. For populations emerging from slavery — persons who have "learned to obey" but only "a direct command," who are "incapable of conforming their conduct to a rule or law" — Mill prescribes what he calls the "government of leading-strings." This is a paternalistic regime that "possesses force, but seldom uses it," maintaining "a general superintendence over all the operations of society" while necessarily leaving "individuals to do much of themselves" (002-029).

Mill cites two historical precedents for this model: "Such appears to have been the idea of the government of the Incas of Peru, and such was that of the Jesuits of Paraguay." He adds the crucial caveat that "leading-strings are only admissible as a means of gradually training the people to walk alone" — the goal must always be to prepare the population for self-government, not to perpetuate tutelage (002-029).

## Jesuit Education and Its Unintended Consequences

Mill invokes the Jesuits a second time in Chapter III, not as governors but as educators. Observing that "the leaders of French philosophy in the eighteenth century had been educated by the Jesuits," he draws the paradoxical conclusion that "even Jesuit education, it seems, was sufficiently real to call forth the appetite for freedom" (003-005). The example serves Mill's argument that any education "which aims at making human beings other than machines, in the long run makes them claim to have the control of their own actions." A "popular education is a failure if it educates the people for any state but that which it will certainly induce them to desire, and most probably to demand" (003-005).

## The Organizational Spirit of the Society

A third reference appears in Mill's comparison of representative democracy with bureaucracy. Commenting on the tendency of bureaucracies to suppress individual genius, Mill notes that "the spirit of the corps (as with the Jesuits) bears down the individuality of its more distinguished members" (006-009). The Jesuit order thus serves Mill as an example of a broader organizational pathology: when professional *esprit de corps* becomes paramount, routine overpowers innovation, and institutions "perish by the immutability of their maxims" (006-009).
