**Canada** is cited by Mill in Chapter XVIII ("Of the Government of Dependencies by a Free State") as the model case of a dependency whose population is sufficiently advanced for representative institutions. Mill praises Britain's policy of granting Canada "complete internal self-government" as "the only proper rule" for dependencies of this class.

> …the representatives of Canada and Australia, even in matters of an imperial character…
>
> (018-006)

## Mill's Argument Against Imperial Representation

Mill used Canada as evidence in his broader argument that Great Britain's overseas dependencies should not be represented in an imperial Parliament. He argued that "countries separated by half the globe do not present the natural conditions for being under one government" because the colonists "are not part of the same public; they do not discuss and deliberate in the same arena, but apart, and have only a most imperfect knowledge of what passes in the minds of one another" (018-006).

> Countries separated by half the globe do not present the natural conditions for being under one government, or even members of one federation. If they had sufficiently the same interests, they have not, and never can have, a sufficient habit of taking council together.
>
> (018-006)

Rather than imperial representation, Mill endorsed the model already applied to Canada: complete internal self-government under a merely nominal British sovereignty. He cited this arrangement as proof that dependencies with a sufficiently advanced population could govern themselves effectively while retaining a "slight bond of connection" with the mother country (018-007).

## Canada and the Class of Advanced Dependencies

Mill classified dependencies into two types: those whose populations were "in a sufficiently advanced state to be fitted for representative government" and those still requiring paternal rule. Canada, along with Australia and "the English possessions in America," represented the first class (018-009). For such dependencies, Mill held that "Great Britain could do perfectly well without her colonies" and "on every principle of morality and justice, she ought to consent to their separation" should they desire it. That they chose to remain connected was, in Mill's view, a testament to the wisdom of the self-government policy (018-007).
