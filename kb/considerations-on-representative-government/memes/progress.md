# Progress

**Progress** is a central concept in Mill's political philosophy, denoting the onward movement of society toward higher states of civilization. For Mill, the capacity of a form of government to promote progress — alongside order or permanence — is the fundamental criterion by which [[political-institution|political institutions]] must be judged.

## Definition and the Progress–Order Duality

Mill defines the two great attributes of good government as the promotion of "the [[virtue]] and [[intelligence]] of the people" — which constitutes progress — and the preservation of existing goods, which constitutes order or permanence. A society is "stationary or progressive" (002-006), and the form of government must be evaluated by its tendency in both respects.

Mill notes the relationship between the two principles:

> "There is need alike to permanence and to progression, only there is needed more emphasis on the progressive tendency." (002-009)

This passage reveals Mill's characteristic tilt toward improvement over mere stability, reflecting the [[reform-of-parliament|reformist spirit]] of his work.

## Uneven Progress Across Domains

Mill recognizes that progress is not uniform across all aspects of society. He observes:

> "Thus there may be progress in wealth, while there is deterioration in virtue." (002-013)

This insight has important implications for Mill's political theory: a form of government may stimulate economic growth while failing to cultivate — or even degrading — the moral character of citizens. True progress therefore requires balanced advancement across material and moral dimensions.

## Progress and Political Development

Mill connects the progress of [[political-institution|political institutions]] directly to the theory of government itself.

He laments that "this retardation of political progress is no doubt a great disadvantage" (001-005), referring to the widespread view that forms of government are not objects of rational choice. Mill's entire project is to accelerate political progress by demonstrating that [[constitution|constitutions]] can be deliberately improved through rational analysis.

## Civilization and the Stages of Progress

Mill places progress within a historical framework of civilizational development. A people may be "incapable of making any progress in civilization until it has learned the first lesson — that of obedience" (002-027). This reflects his view, elaborated throughout the early chapters, that [[representative-government]] requires certain preconditions of social development before it can function.

The political philosopher's task, according to Mill, is to identify "the next necessary step in social progress" (002-029) and to propose institutions suited to that stage. The [[preface]] to the work acknowledges that "no one side appears to have made any progress in providing itself with a better form of government" (P01-002) — underscoring the urgency of Mill's theoretical intervention.

## The Tendency to Promote Progress

For Mill, the superior value of [[representative-government]] lies precisely in its progressive tendency:

> "Short at any point in their progress by defective adaptation of their government to their growing needs." (002-026)

When government fails to adapt to society's development, it arrests progress and produces stagnation. The representative form, by contrast, continually adjusts through electoral accountability and [[public-opinion|public discussion]].
