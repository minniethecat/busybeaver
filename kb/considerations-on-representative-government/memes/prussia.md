**Prussia** appears in Mill's analysis of federal government as an example of how territorial discontinuity and great military powers within a federation can threaten its stability. Mill notes that Prussia (along with Austria) had a "legal right of pouring in their troops to crush the free institutions of the smaller states" under the Germanic Confederation (017-004).

> …served only to give Austria and Prussia a legal right of pouring in their troops to crush the free institutions of the smaller states…
>
> (017-004)

## Prussia and the Failure of the German Confederation

Mill identified the disproportionate power of Prussia and Austria as the central cause of the German Confederation's failure. When two member states are so powerful that "they will be irresistible when they agree; and whenever they differ, every thing will be decided by a struggle for ascendancy between the rivals," the federation cannot function as intended. This dynamic, Mill argued, was "alone enough to reduce the German Bund to almost a nullity" (017-004).

The Confederation, according to Mill, achieved none of the real purposes of a federal union: "It has never bestowed on Germany a uniform system of customs, nor so much as a uniform coinage." Instead, it merely gave Prussia and Austria legal cover to intervene militarily in smaller German states to suppress liberal movements. The result was that "each petty prince has little choice but to be a partisan of one or the other, or to intrigue with foreign governments against both" (017-004).

## The Problem of Territorial Discontinuity

A secondary Prussian example appears in Mill's discussion of nationality and geography. The German colony of East Prussia was "cut off from Germany by part of the ancient Poland," creating a territorial discontinuity that complicated national boundaries. Mill observed that East Prussia, "being too weak to maintain separate independence, must, if geographical continuity is to be maintained, be either under a non-German government, or the intervening Polish territory must be under a German one" (016-004). This example illustrated the general principle that geographical realities often compel departures from the ideal of aligning political boundaries with nationalities.
