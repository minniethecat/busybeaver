**Free [[government]]**, in John Stuart Mill's political theory, is the form of government in which the people exercise control over their rulers through participation, publicity, and the electoral mechanism. It is distinguished from [[despotism]] and [[monarchy]] not merely by the existence of elections but by the cultivation of [[intelligence|public intelligence]] and the active engagement of citizens in political affairs. For Mill, [[freedom|free government]] is "one of the foremost benefits" of a civilized political order — but it requires specific social, intellectual, and institutional conditions to function properly (005-015).

## The Preconditions of Free Government

Mill is explicit that [[freedom|free government]] cannot be sustained everywhere or at all times. It requires a population prepared for [[participation|political participation]]:

"A people may prefer a [[freedom|free government]]; but if, from indolence, or carelessness, or cowardice, or want of public spirit, they are unequal to the exertions necessary for preserving it" — then the institutions of [[liberty|freedom]] will fail (001-009).

The three essential preconditions Mill identifies are:

| Precondition | Description | Key PN |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Public spirit | Citizens must be willing to exert themselves for the common good | (001-009) |
| Intellectual development | The population must possess sufficient understanding to judge political questions | (003-004) |
| Institutional safeguards | Laws and structures must protect against the abuse of power | (003-014) |

> "In proportion as any, no matter what form of government, is defective in these conditions, it falls short of the ideally perfect conception of free government." (003-014)

## Free Government and Public Discussion

One of the distinctive features of [[freedom|free government]], for Mill, is the role of public discussion in shaping political outcomes. Representative institutions provide a platform for the "talking" that educates public opinion:

"Such 'talking' would never be looked upon with displeasure by any government really based on the principle of public discussion and popular control" — it is the mechanism through which intelligence is brought to bear on legislation (005-015).

This emphasis on discussion distinguishes [[freedom|free government]] from bureaucratic [[despotism]], which may be efficient in administration but does nothing to cultivate the intellectual and moral faculties of the governed (006-010).

## The Educational Function

Among the foremost benefits of [[freedom|free government]] is its educative effect on citizens. [[participation|Participation]] in political life — through voting, [[jury|jury service]], and local administration — develops the capacities necessary for self-government:

"Among the foremost benefits of [[freedom|free government]] is that education of the intelligence and of the sentiments which is carried down to the very lowest ranks of the people" (008-002).

This educational function operates through several mechanisms:
- **[[participation|Participation]] in elections**: Citizens must weigh competing claims and form judgments about public affairs (008-002).
- **Service on juries**: The experience of deliberation with fellow citizens cultivates impartial judgment (008-002).
- **Public discussion**: The circulation of opinions through press and debate broadens understanding (005-015).
- **Local government**: Administration of local affairs provides practical training in governance (015-002).

## The Principle of Popular Control

Popular control is, for Mill, the "fundamental principle of [[freedom|free government]]" — but it must be properly channeled through institutions:

"The popular control essential to [[freedom|free government]]" requires that the [[parliament|representative assembly]] exercise oversight over the executive without itself attempting to govern (011-001).

> "There is a radical distinction between controlling the business of government, and actually doing it." (011-001)

This distinction is crucial: a [[freedom|free government]] does not mean the people or their representatives directly administer public affairs. Rather, the representative body controls the executive by requiring justification, investigating misconduct, and withholding consent from objectionable measures.

## Compatibility with Other Institutions

Mill analyzes whether specific institutional arrangements are compatible with the essentials of [[freedom|free government]]. Examining colonial [[colonies|dependencies]], he argues that even a governor-general with broad authority "never could be unduly dependent upon the officers of the colonial government" if the metropolitan power ensures accountability — the framework is "compatible with the essentials of [[freedom|free government]]" as long as ultimate control resides with the represented people (014-010).

The fundamental institutions of [[freedom|free government]] include:
- **Representative assemblies** with the power to control the executive (015-002)
- **Local self-government** — "They exist in England but very imperfectly" (015-002)
- **Publicity of official acts** subject to free criticism (005-009)
- **An independent judiciary** capable of checking executive overreach
