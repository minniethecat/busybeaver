**Thomas Hare** (1806–1891) was a British barrister and political reformer whose system of [[proportional-representation|proportional representation]] — the single transferable vote — received John Stuart Mill's strongest endorsement as the key to genuine [[representative-government|representative government]]. Mill considered Hare's plan "among the greatest improvements yet made in the theory and practice of government" (007-009).

## Hare's System of Proportional Representation

Hare's plan, first published in his *Treatise on the [[election|Election]] of Representatives, [[parliament|Parliament]]ary and Municipal* (1859), addressed what Mill saw as the fundamental defect of representative institutions: the exclusion of minorities from [[parliament|Parliament]].

| Feature | Description | Mill's Assessment |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Single Transferable Vote | Voters rank candidates; surplus votes transfer | "Provides representation for every minority" (007-015) |
| National [[constituency|Constituency]] | Voters select from a national list, not just local | "Secures the representation of every opinion" (007-011) |
| Voluntary Association | Voters freely group by conviction | "Renders necessary the employment of superior intellect" (010-016) |
| [[minority|Minority]] Protection | Every substantial opinion group elects representatives | "Those who did not like the local candidates would have their votes counted elsewhere" (007-011) |

> "On Mr. Hare's system, those who did not like the local candidates would have their votes counted elswhere — every vote would tell for something" (007-011)

Mill was particularly struck by the system's educative effect: by giving every voter a meaningful choice, it "renders necessary the employment of superior intellect" in the [[election|electoral process]] (010-016).

## Mill's Advocacy

Mill's endorsement of Hare's plan in *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]* is one of the most emphatic passages in his political writings:

- **Practical feasibility**: Mill notes that "Mr. Thomas Hare had proved its possibility by drawing up the practical details" embodied in a draft Act of [[parliament|Parliament]] (007-007).
- **Historical opportunity**: Mill laments that "had a plan like Mr. Hare's by good fortune suggested itself to the framers of the American [[constitution|Constitution]]," the defect of [[tyranny|majority tyranny]] might have been avoided (007-013).
- **Intellectual pedigree**: Mill associates Hare with "the most powerful intellects" of the age, including [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] and Samuel Bailey (008-018).

> "A scheme which has the almost unparalleled merit of carrying out in a commendable degree the principles of representative government" (007-007)

The plan was also introduced as a legislative proposal by Lord John Russell in one of his [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bills]], demonstrating its practical political traction (007-007).

## Criticisms and Responses

Mill addresses several objections to Hare's system:

| Objection | Mill's Response |
|-----------|----------------|
| "Voters cannot judge candidates beyond their locality" | In an age of print and communication, "the voter may be a sufficient judge of qualifications" without personal acquaintance (009-003) |
| "Requires too much intelligence from average voters" | The system itself educates; "the voter would have motives to inform himself" (009-002) |
| "Too complex to operate" | The details are "no more complex than the existing registration system" (007-017) |
| "Local constituencies would be destroyed" | "Local constituencies which Mr. Hare's plan requires" remain — the plan uses them as a base, not a limit (008-013) |

> "The ostensible objectors to Mr. Hare's scheme, some profess to think it too complex; but the real objection is simpler — it would make Parliament too independent of local influence" (007-017)

## Legacy

Although Hare's full plan was not adopted in his lifetime, the single transferable vote has been implemented in numerous democracies worldwide. Mill's advocacy in *Considerations* remains one of the most influential philosophical defenses of [[proportional-representation|proportional representation]] in the English language.
