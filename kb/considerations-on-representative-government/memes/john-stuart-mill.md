## The Author and His Work

John Stuart Mill is the author of *Considerations on Representative Government*, first published in 1861. In the preface, Mill acknowledges that the principles advanced in the work are "those to which I have been working up during the greater part of my life," and notes that "most of the practical suggestions have been anticipated by others or by myself" (P01-001). The work represents the culmination of his long engagement with questions of political institutions and their improvement.

## Method and Purpose

Mill situates his project between the exhausted political creeds of his time. Observing that "both Conservatives and Liberals... have lost confidence in the political creeds which they nominally profess," he seeks "not a mere compromise, by splitting the difference between the two, but something wider than either, which, in virtue of its superior comprehensiveness, might be adopted by either Liberal or Conservative without renouncing any thing which he really feels to be valuable in his own creed" (P01-002).

His method throughout the work is to examine the principles of representative government systematically, from the criteria by which forms of government should be judged, through the proper functions of representative bodies, to the details of electoral systems, the role of second chambers, and the governance of dependencies.

> It seems to me, however, from various indications, and from none more than the recent debates on Reform of Parliament, that both Conservatives and Liberals have lost confidence in the political creeds which they nominally profess, while neither side appears to have made any progress in providing itself with a better. Yet such a better doctrine must be possible; not a mere compromise, by splitting the difference between the two, but something wider than either, which, in virtue of its superior comprehensiveness, might be adopted by either Liberal or Conservative without renouncing any thing which he really feels to be valuable in his own creed.
>
> (P01-002)

## Intellectual Context

Mill's argument draws upon the utilitarian tradition associated with Jeremy Bentham. Throughout the work, he engages with the political thought of his contemporaries as well as classical sources, evaluating propositions by their tendency to promote "the virtue and intelligence of the human beings composing the community" (002-020).
