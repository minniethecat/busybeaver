# Republic

A **republic** is a form of government in which supreme power rests in the people and their elected representatives, with no hereditary monarch. Mill discusses republics throughout *Considerations on Representative Government*, drawing on ancient and modern examples, and analyzing their distinctive institutional challenges.

## Historical Precedents

Mill draws on the experience of classical and medieval republics to illuminate general principles of government. He references "the Italian republics and the free towns of Flanders and [[germany|Germany]]" (003-013) as examples of self-governing communities that, despite their imperfections, demonstrated the capacity of popular institutions to produce civic vitality:

> "The Italian republics and the free towns of Flanders and Germany furnish examples of the high degree of [[civilization|civilization]] and prosperity which communities of comparatively small extent have sometimes attained under a republican form." (003-013)

The ancient world provides a more checkered record. Mill notes that in "the decline of the [[roman-republic|Roman republic]], the reasons for the ballot were strong" (010-006), referring to the corruption and intimidation that accompanied the collapse of republican institutions.

## Opposition and Aversion

Mill acknowledges that attachment to [[monarchy|monarchy]] and "aversion to a republic" (001-008) constitute real obstacles to political reform. He observes:

> "The hindrance often amounts to an impossibility, from the unwillingness of the people to accept the form of government." (001-008)

This psychological barrier — the association of stability with monarchy and disorder with republics — is one of the impediments Mill's rational analysis seeks to overcome. He treats it not as a legitimate objection but as a prejudice to be dispelled.

## The Republic and the Executive

In a republic, the [[executive|executive power]] requires special institutional design. Mill notes:

> "It is the duty of the chief of the executive, in a republican government, to be an exception to the general rule." (014-009)

Unlike a monarch, whose personal interest may align with the state's, the republican executive must be institutionally constrained. The selection, tenure, and accountability of the executive present distinct challenges in a republican framework.

## The Ballot and Republican Decline

Mill discusses the ballot in the context of the Roman republic's decline. The secret ballot was introduced as a safeguard against the corruption and intimidation that characterized late republican politics (010-006). Mill's analysis of this history informs his own cautious assessment of the ballot as an electoral mechanism — useful as a protection against undue influence, but potentially undermining the public character of the vote.

## Republic and Representative Government

For Mill, the ideal republic is not the direct democracy of ancient city-states but representative government. The principle of representation solves the problem of scale that doomed the classical republics: a large population can govern itself through elected deputies without the turbulence that plagued the Athenian assembly or the Roman comitia.
