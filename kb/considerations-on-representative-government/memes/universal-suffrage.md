**Universal [[franchise|suffrage]]** — the extension of the [[voting|franchise]] to every adult citizen without exclusion — is treated by Mill not as an abstract right but as a practical question of [[political-institution|institutional design]]. He accepts the principle that "excluding fewest from the [[franchise|suffrage]]" is a necessary condition of [[freedom|free government]], but he insists that [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] alone, without complementary institutional safeguards, is insufficient to secure good government and may even be dangerous to it (008-012).

Mill's analysis is therefore a deliberate middle path: he rejects both the oligarchic exclusion of whole classes from the [[suffrage|franchise]] and the democratic absolutism that treats equal numerical [[franchise|suffrage]] as the sole criterion of political justice. His aim is to reconcile the principle of universal participation with the imperative of competent governance.

## The Case for Universal Suffrage

Mill's endorsement of [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] rests on two distinct but complementary arguments. The first is **protective**: no person or class can safely be excluded from political power, because the excluded will inevitably be sacrificed to the interests of the included (008-002). The second is **educative**: participation in political life develops the intelligence and public spirit of citizens, and this benefit should be extended as widely as possible (003-005).

> "In a country governed by equal and universal suffrage, there is a contested election in every constituency, and the whole nation is engaged in political discussion" (007-004)

This educative function — what Mill calls the "school of public spirit" — is not a secondary benefit of [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] but its primary justification. The purpose of extending the [[suffrage|franchise]] is not simply to give everyone a say in government but to make everyone capable of saying something worth hearing.

## The Dangers of Unqualified Universal Suffrage

Mill identifies several specific dangers that arise when [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] is instituted without complementary institutional checks:

| Danger | Mechanism | Mitigation |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Majority tyranny | A numerical majority can suppress [[minority|minority opinion]]s entirely | [[thomas-hare|Proportional representation]] |
| Class legislation | The largest class governs in its own economic interest | Plural voting by education |
| Lowering of deliberation | Representatives "descend to the level of the lowest" among their constituents | Open voting and public accountability (008-012) |
| Ignorance empowered | Votes of the uninformed count equally with those of the informed | Literacy requirement as condition of [[franchise|suffrage]] |

> "Between this and equal universal suffrage, whoever does not desire the inequality, desires the degradation of the representative body to the level of the lowest" (008-012)

This passage — among Mill's most quoted and most criticised — reveals the tension at the heart of his democratic theory. He wants universal participation, but he also wants competent governance. The resolution, he believes, lies not in restricting the [[suffrage|franchise]] but in weighting it by demonstrated capacity.

## Plural Voting as Complement to Universal Suffrage

Mill's distinctive proposal is that [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] should be **graduated** rather than strictly equal. Every adult citizen should possess at least one vote, but those with demonstrated intellectual competence — through education, occupation, or public service — should receive more:

> "The benefit of completely universal suffrage cannot be obtained without bringing with it the danger that ignorance, and not knowledge, will have the preponderance" (008-013)

The key features of this scheme:
- **Universal baseline**: No adult is excluded from the [[suffrage|franchise]] entirely — "the [[franchise|suffrage]] would be, with that slight abatement, universal" (008-007).
- **Superior weight for superior judgment**: Additional votes are awarded on the basis of individual capacity, not property or inheritance.
- **Open access**: The qualifications for plural votes are available to all who make the effort to attain them — the system is competitive, not hereditary.
- **Temporary expedient**: Mill regards [[plurality|plural voting]] as a transitional measure; universal education would eventually make it unnecessary.

> "The operation even of equal and universal suffrage, if made real by the proportional representation of all opinions, might be expected to raise the standard of intelligence in the representative body" (008-014)

## The Literacy Requirement

Mill supports a minimal literacy condition for the exercise of the [[suffrage|franchise]], not as a permanent exclusion but as a universal incentive to education:

> "I regard it as wholly inadmissible that any person should participate in the suffrage without being able to read, write, and perform the common operations of arithmetic" (008-004)

This requirement is legitimate, in Mill's view, because:
- The state provides universal education — anyone who remains illiterate has refused a freely offered public good.
- [[suffrage|Suffrage]] is exercised on behalf of others, not merely the self — society has a stake in the competence of the voter.
- The requirement is self-extinguishing: once universal education is achieved, the condition is satisfied by everyone.

## Universal Suffrage and the Ballot

Mill's analysis of [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] is closely connected to his analysis of the [[ballot]]. He argues that the [[ballot|secret ballot]] becomes more justifiable precisely when [[franchise|suffrage]] is universal, because the least powerful voters — those at the bottom of the social hierarchy — are most vulnerable to intimidation:

> "Where a community had obtained universal suffrage, the ballot would be desirable. First, because the voters would then be sufficiently numerous to make intimidation impracticable" (010-015)

This argument reveals the pragmatic character of Mill's political theory. He adjusts his institutional recommendations to circumstances rather than deducing them from fixed principles. The [[voting|ballot]] is undesirable in a restricted [[suffrage|franchise]] because it encourages voters to treat their privilege as a private possession; it becomes more defensible under [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] because the scale of the electorate makes intimidation by a single class more difficult.
