# Confederation

A **confederation** is a union of independent states that delegate limited powers to a common government while retaining sovereignty over domestic affairs. Mill treats confederation as a distinct constitutional form in his chapter "Of Federal Representative Governments," analyzing its strengths, weaknesses, and preconditions for success.

## Definition and Distinction from Other Forms

Mill distinguishes confederation from both complete unification and mere alliance. In a confederation, member states "reserve to themselves all powers not expressly delegated to the central authority" (017-003). This contrasts with a fully consolidated state, where sovereignty is undivided, and with a simple treaty alliance, which lacks permanent common institutions.

## Advantages of Confederation

Mill identifies several benefits of the confederate form. It enables smaller states to combine their strength for common purposes — particularly defense and commerce — while preserving their internal autonomy. The confederation structure allows diverse communities to enjoy the advantages of union without sacrificing their distinct political identities.

## Historical Examples

Mill draws on historical instances to illustrate the confederate form. He discusses the German Confederation, observing that "it has never bestowed on [[germany|Germany]] a real national government" (017-004) — a critique of confederations that fail to provide sufficient central authority. The weakness lay in preventing "single members of the confederation from allying themselves with foreign powers against the rest" (017-005), revealing the structural tension between state sovereignty and collective security.

The American experience offers a contrasting example. Mill references the "American, African, and [[australia|Australian]] confederation" (018-006) as instances where the confederate principle has been applied — though he distinguishes the American system, which evolved toward stronger [[federal]] union, from looser confederations.

> The Federal Congress of the American Union is a substantive part of the government of every individual state. Within the limits of its attributions, it makes laws which are obeyed by every citizen individually, executes them through its own officers, and enforces them by its own tribunals. This is the only principle which has been found, or which is ever likely to produce an effective federal government.
>
> (017-005)

## Conditions for Success

Mill argues that confederation is most suitable when member states share common interests, cultural affinity, and a mutual sense of vulnerability to external threats. Without these, the centrifugal forces of state sovereignty tend to overwhelm the centripetal pull of common purpose.

The "warlike proceedings of the independent members of the confederation" (017-010) represent a recurring danger — when member states pursue their own foreign policies or engage in conflicts with one another, the confederation unravels.

## Confederation and Dependencies

Mill extends the confederate principle to imperial governance in his chapter on dependencies. He envisions confederations of settler colonies — "American, African, and Australian" — as a natural evolution of the colonial relationship toward free association among equals (018-006).
