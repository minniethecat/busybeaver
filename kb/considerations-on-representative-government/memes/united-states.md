The **United States** serves throughout Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as the preeminent modern example of federal [[democracy|representative government]]. Mill draws on American institutions as both positive models and cautionary examples, analyzing the federal Constitution, the electoral system, the Supreme Court, and the practical workings of democracy.

## As a Comparative Model

Mill treats the United States as a crucial test case for democratic theory. In discussing constitutional precautions against the abuse of power, he notes that "such precautions have existed in all well-constructed democracies. The [[athenian-democracy|Athenian Constitution]] had many such provisions, and so has that of the United States" (005-004). The American experience provides empirical evidence for Mill's arguments about the strengths and weaknesses of democratic institutions.

## The Federal Constitution

Mill devotes considerable attention to the American federal system in Chapter XVII. He distinguishes two modes of federation: a mere alliance of governments (which failed in both America's early years and in Switzerland before 1847), and a "more perfect mode" where federal authorities act directly on citizens. The US Constitution exemplifies the latter:

> "The Federal Congress of the American Union is a substantive part of the government of every individual state. Within the limits of its attributions, it makes laws which are obeyed by every citizen individually, executes them through its own officers, and enforces them by its own tribunals." (017-005)

## Judicial Review and the Supreme Court

One of Mill's most sustained analyses concerns the American innovation of judicial review. Under the federal Constitution, a Supreme Court serves as "an umpire independent of both" state and federal governments, with power to declare laws unconstitutional:

> "This involves the remarkable consequence, actually realized in the United States, that a court of justice, the highest federal tribunal, is supreme over the various governments, both state and federal, having the right to declare that any law made, or act done by them, exceeds the powers assigned to them by the federal Constitution, and, in consequence, has no legal validity." (017-006)

Mill reports that the institution has worked "eminently beneficial[ly]," attributing its success to the Court's practice of deciding constitutional questions only in actual cases, not in the abstract, and to the "intellectual pre-eminence" and impartiality of its judges (017-006).

## The Senate

Mill praises the American Senate as a model second chamber. Unlike the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]], the US Senate represents the state governments rather than a hereditary aristocracy. He observes that senators, being chosen by state legislatures rather than by popular election, tend to be drawn from the nation's most eminent political minds:

> "The Senate of the United States, thus chosen, has always contained nearly all the political men of established and high reputation in the Union; while the Lower House of Congress has, in the opinion of competent observers, been generally as remarkable for the absence of conspicuous personal merit, as the Upper House for its presence." (017-009)

This arrangement, Mill argues, secures both the federal principle (equal state representation) and the educative function of a second chamber.

## Flaws in American Democracy

Mill is not uncritical. He identifies several failures in American practice:

### The Electoral College

The indirect election of the President, Mill argues, has become a mere formality. Electors "are always chosen under an express engagement to vote for a particular candidate; nor does a citizen ever vote for an elector because of any preference for the man" (009-003). The system nominally designed for deliberation has collapsed into direct popular election by another name.

### The Degradation of Candidates

Mill observes that the presidential nomination process systematically excludes the most qualified individuals:

> "At the election of President, the strongest party never dares put forward any of its strongest men, because every one of these, from the mere fact that he has been long in the public eye, has made himself objectionable to some portion or other of the party." (007-005)

The result is that "the man who is chosen, even by the strongest party, represents perhaps the real wishes only of the narrow margin by which that party outnumbers the other" (007-005).

### Judicial Elections

Mill warns that the American practice of submitting judges to periodic popular re-election — introduced by "some of the new or revised State Constitutions in America" — is "one of the most dangerous errors ever yet committed by democracy" (014-013). He fears that elected judges will feel pressured to render popular rather than just decisions.

## Colonial Governance

In Chapter XVIII, Mill contrasts British colonial policy favorably with American territorial governance. Under the US Constitution, territories are subject to federal authority, whereas British colonies of European race enjoy "the fullest measure of internal self-government" — "much fuller than would belong to it under the Constitution of the United States, being free even to tax at its pleasure the commodities imported from the mother country" (018-004).
