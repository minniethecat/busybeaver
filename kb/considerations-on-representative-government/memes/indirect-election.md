## Definition

Indirect election, or election by two stages, is an electoral mechanism in which primary electors choose a smaller body of intermediate electors, who then select the actual members of the legislature. The system was historically adopted in some representative constitutions as "a slight impediment to the full sweep of popular feeling" — intended to filter the popular will through a presumably more discerning intermediate body (009-001).

## Mill's Critique

John Stuart Mill devoted the entirety of Chapter IX of *Considerations on Representative Government* to arguing against indirect election. He acknowledged the "very plausible defense" of the system — that "less intellect and instruction are required for judging who among our neighbors can be most safely trusted to choose a member of Parliament than who is himself fittest to be one" — but systematically dismantled it (009-001).

### The Cultivation Defeat

Mill's first objection was that indirect election defeats one of the principal benefits of popular government: the cultivation of public spirit and political intelligence among ordinary citizens. If the primary elector is told his sole business is to choose choosers, not the member himself, "the political function to which they are called fails of developing public spirit and political intelligence, of making public affairs an object of interest to their feelings and of exercise to their faculties" (009-002).

### The Practical Identity with Direct Election

Mill argued that wherever there is political engagement, indirect election collapses into direct election in practice. A voter who cares about the outcome will simply choose as elector "a known partisan of the candidate he prefers, or some one who will [[pledge]] himself to vote for that candidate." He cited the election of the President of the United States as proof: "Nominally, the election is indirect; the population at large does not vote for the President; it votes for electors who choose the President. But the electors are always chosen under an express engagement to vote for a particular candidate" (009-003).

> If there is any political activity in the country, all electors who care to vote at all have made up their minds which of these candidates they would like to have, and will make that the sole consideration in giving their vote. The partisans of each candidate will have their list of electors ready, all pledged to vote for that individual; and the only question practically asked of the primary elector will be, which of these lists he will support.
>
> (009-003)

### Disadvantages

Mill listed several additional drawbacks of indirect election (009-005):

- It is "an additional and superfluous wheel in the machinery"
- It is inferior as a means of cultivating public spirit
- The voter is "prevented from identifying himself with his member of Parliament"
- The member feels "a much less active sense of responsibility to his [[constituent|constituents]]"
- The small number of intermediate electors creates "great additional facilities to intrigue, and to every form of corruption"

## The Exception: United States Senate

Mill acknowledged one case where indirect election succeeded: the United States Senate, whose members were chosen by state legislatures rather than by direct popular vote. He considered the Senate "conspicuously the best of all the elections in the United States," invariably consisting of "the most distinguished men among those who have made themselves sufficiently known in public life." However, he argued that these favorable conditions — where the electing body has other important governmental functions beyond merely choosing representatives — are "hardly to be obtained in practice except in a [[federal|federal government]] like that of the United States" (009-004).

## Conclusion

Mill concluded that "every benefit of indirect election which is attainable at all is attainable under direct" election, while indirect election carries "considerable disadvantages peculiar to itself." The "moment the double stage of election began to have any effect, it would begin to have a bad effect" (009-005).
