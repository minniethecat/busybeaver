## Introduction

**Austria** appears in Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as a prominent example of the old European monarchical order — a bureaucratic, multinational empire that Mill contrasted unfavorably with the free states of Europe and America. Austria served Mill as evidence that governments lacking representative character produce neither prosperity nor political improvement. (003-013)

## Austria as a Feudal Monarchy

Mill placed Austria among the "feudal monarchies of Europe" whose "superior prosperity" compared to despotic states was attributable not to monarchy itself but to the "share of freedom" their subjects enjoyed. He listed "[[switzerland|Switzerland]], [[free-government|Holland]], and [[great-britain|England]], with Austria or [[france|ante-revolutionary France]]" as examples where partial freedom, not monarchical form, explained relative success. (003-013) This distinction was central to Mill's argument that the benefits commonly attributed to monarchy were actually the benefits of limited government.

## Bureaucratic Government

In his analysis of government by trained officials, Mill cited the "Russian and Austrian governments, and even the French government under the elder Bourbons" as examples of bureaucratic rule. (006-006) He acknowledged that bureaucracy "accumulates experience" and "makes provision for appropriate practical knowledge," but warned that it was "not equally favorable to individual energy of mind." The disease of bureaucratic government, he argued, was "routine" — and "they perish by the immutability of their maxims."

## The Multinational Empire

Austria exemplified for Mill the problems of governing diverse nationalities under a single, non-representative authority. He noted how "the Austrian court has now for a whole generation made it the great object of its policy to destroy all feeling of separate nationality among its subjects." (016-003) This attempt to suppress national sentiment through centralized rule was, in Mill's view, both unjust and ultimately futile. He observed that Austria and Prussia together dominated the weak German Confederation, using it as "a legal right of intervention" in smaller states. (017-004)

> The Austrian court has now for a whole generation made these tactics its principal means of government, with what fatal success, at the time of the Vienna insurrection and the [[nationality|Hungarian]] contest the world knows too well. Happily there are now signs that improvement is too far advanced to permit this policy to be any longer successful.
>
> (016-003)
