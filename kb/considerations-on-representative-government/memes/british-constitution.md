The **British Constitution** serves as John Stuart Mill's primary empirical reference point throughout *Considerations on Representative Government*. Unlike the [[american-constitution|American Constitution]] — a single codified document — the British Constitution operates through a combination of statutory law, judicial precedent, and, most importantly for Mill, unwritten constitutional maxims that reflect the nation's "positive political morality." (005-002)

## The Unwritten Constitution

Mill identifies the distinction between formal legal powers and the practical operation of constitutional government as the key to understanding the British system. By constitutional law, the Crown possesses extensive prerogatives — it can refuse assent to any act of Parliament and appoint ministers without parliamentary approval. But these legal powers are "nullified" by constitutional morality:

> The unwritten maxims of the Constitution—in other words, the positive political morality of the country; and this positive political morality is what we must look to if we would know in whom the really supreme power in the Constitution resides. (005-002)

This unwritten morality, Mill argues, ensures that the head of administration is "virtually appointed by the House of Commons," making that body "the real sovereign of the state." (005-003)

## The Balance of Powers

Mill addresses the classical theory that a balanced constitution — where different branches check one another — is impossible because "the scales never hang exactly even." (005-002) In the British Constitution, each of the three coordinate members of the sovereignty (Crown, Lords, Commons) possesses powers that could theoretically "stop all the machinery of government." What prevents their aggressive exercise is not legal constraint but political morality.

This arrangement persists, Mill contends, only so long as the unwritten rules "harmonise with the actual distribution of real political strength." (005-004) In England, the popular element possesses "the preponderance of active power out of doors," and the constitutional maxims reflect this reality. Should the legal provisions and unwritten rules cease to give the popular element "that substantial supremacy over every department of the government which corresponds to its real power in the country, the Constitution would not possess the stability which characterizes it." (005-004)

## The Crown and the Cabinet

Under the existing practice of the British Constitution, Mill finds the mechanism for executive appointments to be "on as good a footing as they can be." (005-011) [[parliament|Parliament]] does not nominate ministers directly; rather, the Crown appoints the head of administration "in conformity to the general wishes and inclinations manifested by Parliament," and other ministers on the recommendation of the chief. Each minister bears "the undivided moral responsibility of appointing fit persons to the other offices of administration which are not permanent." (005-011)

Mill contrasts this arrangement favorably with the American presidential system, noting that in a [[republic]] "the nearer it approached in practice to that which has long existed in England, the more likely it would be to work well." (005-011) However, he also warns that representative bodies have a strong tendency "to interfere more and more in the details of administration," identifying this as "one of the practical dangers to which the futurity of representative governments will be exposed." (005-011)

## Representative Government in Practice

Mill concludes that the British government is "a representative government in the correct sense of the term." (005-004) The powers left in hands not directly accountable to the people — the Crown and the House of Lords — are best understood as "precautions which the ruling power is willing should be taken against its own errors." Such precautions, he notes, "have existed in all well-constructed democracies," citing both the Athenian Constitution and that of the [[united-states|United States]]. (005-004)
