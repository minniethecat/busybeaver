The **Reform Bill of 1832** (formally the Representation of the People Act 1832) was the first major reform of the British [[election|electoral system]] in over a century. It expanded the [[suffrage|franchise]], eliminated "rotten boroughs," and redistributed [[parliament|Parliamentary]] seats to growing industrial cities.

John Stuart Mill, writing three decades later in *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, treats the Reform Bill as a pivotal but incomplete step toward genuine [[representative-government|representative government]].

## Background and Provisions

The Reform Bill emerged from decades of agitation for [[parliament|Parliamentary]] reform. The unreformed system was characterized by:

| Problem | Reform Bill Solution |
|---------|---------------------|
| Rotten Boroughs | Disfranchised 56 boroughs with fewer than 2,000 inhabitants |
| Under-represented Cities | Created new seats for Manchester, Birmingham, Leeds, and other industrial centers |
| Restricted [[franchise|Franchise]] | Extended the vote to male householders occupying property worth £10 per year |
| Aristocratic Domination | Reduced (but did not eliminate) the influence of the peerage over [[house-of-commons|Commons]] seats |

> "Lord John Russell, in one of his Reform Bills, introduced a provision that any candidate who was returned by a certain number of electors scattered over the country should be entitled to a seat" (007-007)

Mill notes that Russell's Reform Bill incorporated an early version of [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s proportional representation principle — allowing votes to aggregate across constituency boundaries — though this provision was not enacted (007-007).

## Mill's Analysis

Mill's assessment of the Reform Bill in *Considerations* is characteristically balanced:

**Achievements**:
- It broke the monopoly of aristocratic and Crown influence over the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]].
- It established the principle that representation should reflect population rather than property.
- It opened the door to further reforms by demonstrating that constitutional change was possible without revolution.

**Limitations**:

- The £10 householder [[suffrage|franchise]] still excluded the working classes and all women.
- The reform did not address the deeper problem of [[tyranny|majority tyranny]] — "the [[parliament|Parliament]] thus brought together represents little more than the majority of the majority" (007-004).
- It left the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] untouched as a hereditary second chamber.

> "In any future Reform Bill which lowers greatly the pecuniary condition of the suffrage, it will be indispensable to provide some safeguard against the return of members by the mere votes of the least educated class" (008-012)

Mill's primary concern with any future Reform Bill was not the expansion of the [[suffrage|franchise]] per se, but ensuring that expansion was accompanied by institutional safeguards — particularly [[minority|minority representation]] and educational qualifications — to prevent the "[[tyranny|tyranny of the majority]]" (008-012).

## Electoral Corruption and the Reform Movement

The electoral system that the Reform Bill sought to remedy was not merely unrepresentative but deeply corrupted by oligarchic control. Mill describes a political landscape in which "the higher and richer classes were in complete possession of the government" and the vote itself was largely an instrument of the ruling class (010-009).

The habit of voting at the bidding of an employer or landlord was so firmly established that "hardly any thing was capable of shaking it but a strong popular enthusiasm" (010-009). In such a system, any vote cast in defiance of oligarchic influence was — whatever its motive — "almost sure to be a good vote, for it was a vote against the monster evil, the overruling influence of oligarchy" (010-009).

> The higher and richer classes were in complete possession of the government. Their power was the master grievance of the country. The habit of voting at the bidding of an employer or of a landlord was so firmly established that hardly any thing was capable of shaking it but a strong popular enthusiasm, seldom known to exist but in a good cause. A vote given in opposition to those influences was therefore, in general, an honest, a public-spirited vote; for it was a vote against the monster evil, the overruling influence of oligarchy. Could the voter at that time have been enabled, with safety to himself, to exercise his privilege freely, even though neither honestly nor intelligently, it would have been a great gain to reform, for it would have broken the yoke of the then ruling power in the country — the power which had created and which maintained all that was bad in the institutions and the administration of the state — the power of landlords and boroughmongers.
>
> (010-009)

By Mill's time, conditions had improved since the pre-Reform era, but not completely. He observes that "in counties and in the smaller boroughs, a large amount of servile dependence still remaining; but the temper of the times is adverse to it, and the force of events is constantly tending to diminish it" (010-010).

The very vices of the electoral system — the growth of bribery and its spread to places formerly free from it — paradoxically demonstrated that "the local influences are no longer paramount; that the electors now vote to please themselves, and not other people" (010-010). The electors themselves, once passive instruments of oligarchic will, were becoming the [[oligarchy]].

Yet Mill worried that a further Reform Bill, by extending the suffrage without restructuring the electoral system, could simply replace one form of class [[legislation]] with another. "The present electors, and the bulk of those whom any probable Reform Bill would add to the number, are the middle class, and have as much a class interest, distinct from the working classes, as landlords or great manufacturers" (010-011).

This structural concern — that mere expansion of the electorate without [[minority|minority representation]] would perpetuate rather than cure the exclusion of significant interests — was central to Mill's reform agenda. Underlying the entire movement was Mill's deeper democratic principle: the distinction between "the government of the whole people by the whole people, equally represented" and "the government of the whole people by a mere majority of the people exclusively represented" (007-002).

The latter, Mill argued, "is a government of privilege in favor of the numerical majority, who alone possess practically any voice in the state. This is the inevitable consequence of the manner in which the votes are now taken, to the complete disfranchisement of minorities" (007-002). The Reform Bill of 1832, by breaking the oligarchy's monopoly without providing for proportional [[representation]], had taken a necessary first step but left this deeper structural problem unsolved.

Mill also insisted that any extension of the franchise must be accompanied by educational qualifications. "Universal teaching must precede universal enfranchisement. No one but those in whom an *a priori* theory has silenced common sense will maintain that power over others, over the whole community, should be imparted to people who have not acquired the commonest and most essential requisites for taking care of themselves" (008-004).

## Subsequent Reform

The 1832 Act was followed by the Reform Act of 1867 (which Mill participated in debating as a Member of [[parliament|Parliament]]) and the Reform Act of 1884. Each expanded the [[suffrage|franchise]] further, progressively realizing the movement toward universal male [[franchise|suffrage]].

Mill's *Considerations*, published in 1861, sits between the first and second Reform Acts and addresses the ongoing question: how should the [[election|electoral system]] be designed to produce not just a representative body but a wise and virtuous one? His answer — combining [[thomas-hare|proportional representation]], open [[ballot|balloting]], and educational qualifications — was far more radical than anything enacted in the nineteenth century. Yet his analysis of the Reform Bill's achievements and limitations remains one of the most penetrating contemporary assessments of democratic reform.
