The House of Commons occupies a central position in [[John Stuart Mill]]'s analysis of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]. As the elected chamber of the British [[parliament|Parliament]], the Commons embodies both the strengths and the characteristic defects of [[representation|representative institutions]] as they existed in mid-nineteenth-century Britain. Mill devotes sustained attention to the Commons' functions, composition, and reform across multiple chapters of *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*.

## Constitutional Position

The House of Commons is the popular chamber of the British [[parliament|legislature]], consisting of Members of [[parliament|Parliament]] elected from [[county-board|county]] and borough constituencies. By Mill's time, the [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Act of 1832]] had partially addressed the most egregious defects in representation (the "[[rotten-boroughs|rotten boroughs]]"), but the [[suffrage|franchise]] remained severely restricted by [[property|property qualification]]s.

Mill identifies the Commons' control over taxation and supply as the foundation of its constitutional power (005-007):

> supplies can only be voted by the House of Commons, and though the sanction of the House of Lords and the Crown are also required, they have no power of compelling the Commons to grant supplies.
>
> (005-007)

This power makes the Commons, in Mill's analysis, the effective seat of [[sovereignty]] in the British constitution — a position he regards with mixed approval, since it concentrates both legislative and [[executive|executive power]] in a single body.

## The Proper Functions of the Commons

Mill's most distinctive contribution to the analysis of the House of Commons is his argument that its primary function should be deliberation and control, not direct legislation or administration:

> be virtually appointed by the House of Commons, makes that body the real sovereign of the country.
>
> (005-003)

He warns against the temptation for the Commons to encroach upon executive functions (005-012):

> can not be passed, because the House of Commons will not forego the precious privilege of altering the details of administration.
>
> (005-012)

This concern reflects Mill's broader theory that representative assemblies are ill-suited to the detailed work of [[legislation]] and administration, which requires professional expertise. The Commons should instead focus on "deliberation" — the open discussion and scrutiny of public affairs — and on holding the [[executive]] accountable.

## The Problem of Composition

Mill is sharply critical of the intellectual quality and representativeness of the Commons as constituted in his day. The [[election|electoral system]], he argues, produces a chamber dominated by local interests and class prejudice rather than national wisdom (007-011):

> er to gain admission into the House of Commons. The only persons who can get elected are those who have local influence, or who are willing to spend money.
>
> (007-011)

The solution, Mill argues, is to raise the intellectual standard through institutional reform — particularly the introduction of [[proportional-representation|proportional representation]] and the retention of [[university-representation|university constituencies]]:

> intellectual standard of the House of Commons. Majorities would be compelled to look out for men of character and intellect to represent them.
>
> (007-012)

He also addresses the exclusion of particular groups. The ineligibility of judges and certain [[public-office|office-holder]]s, he argues, deprives the House of valuable expertise:

> they should be eligible to the House of Commons; a justice now refused to Scotland.
>
> (013-009)

## The Path of Reform

The [[reform-of-parliament|Reform Bill of 1832]] had initiated a process of gradual reform, but Mill considers it incomplete. He envisions a House of Commons that is:

1. **Elected on a broad [[suffrage|franchise]]** — including, eventually, [[universal-suffrage|universal suffrage]] tempered by educational qualifications
2. **Structured to represent minorities** — through [[thomas-hare|Hare's system]] of proportional representation
3. **Focused on deliberation** — leaving administration to professional civil servants
4. **Open to talent** — without property barriers or local monopolies on candidacy

## Conclusion

The House of Commons, for Mill, is at once indispensable and deeply flawed. Its popular character gives it legitimacy and ensures that government remains answerable to the governed; but its composition and procedures trap it between the corruptions of local influence and the temptations of administrative meddling. The cure lies not in diminishing the Commons' constitutional role but in reforming the [[election|electoral system]] that produces it.
