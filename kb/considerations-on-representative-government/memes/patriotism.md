## Definition and Mill's Conception

Patriotism, for Mill, is not mere emotional attachment to one's country or flag but an active civic virtue — the disposition to place the public good above private interest in the exercise of political functions. It is closely associated with what Mill calls "public duty" and "public spirit," and he regards it as both a product and a sustaining condition of free institutions.

Mill's treatment of patriotism is distinctive in two respects: first, he argues that genuine patriotism can only arise under [[freedom]], making it a dependent variable of political institutions rather than a pre-existing cultural given; second, he warns that certain institutional designs — notably the secret ballot — can actively corrode the patriotic disposition by teaching citizens to view their political rights as personal property rather than public trusts.

## Patriotism as the Fruit of Freedom

In his analysis of [[despotism]], Mill argues that even a well-intentioned despotism inevitably produces national stagnation and decline. The mechanism is the atrophy of public spirit:

> If, like Greece or Rome, it had realized any thing higher, through the energy, patriotism, and enlargement of mind, which, as national qualities, are the fruits solely of freedom, it relapses in a few generations into the Oriental state.
>
> (003-003)

The triad of "energy, patriotism, and enlargement of mind" are interdependent national qualities that can only flourish when citizens actively participate in their own governance. A despotic government that "leaves things to the government, like leaving them to Providence" extinguishes these qualities, and with them the capacity for national vitality (003-003).

## Patriotism and the Ballot

Mill's most pointed discussion of patriotism arises in his critique of the secret ballot. He argues that the *spirit* of an institution — the impression it makes on the citizen's mind — is as important as its mechanical operation. The ballot teaches the voter that the suffrage is given "for himself — for his particular use and benefit, and not as a trust for the public" (010-003).

The consequence for patriotism is direct and severe:

> Instead of opening his heart to an exalted patriotism and the obligation of public duty, it awakens and nourishes in him the disposition to use a public function for his own interest, pleasure, or caprice; the same feelings and purposes, on a humbler scale, which actuate a despot and oppressor.
>
> (010-003)

Mill insists that the suffrage is not a right in the sense of personal property, but a **trust** — "strictly a matter of duty" to be exercised according to one's "best and most conscientious opinion of the public good." The citizen who treats it otherwise is "unfit to have the suffrage" (010-003). The interpretation that society places on the act of voting shapes the voter's own understanding of his obligations: "What seems to be expected from him by society forms a standard which he may fall below, but which he will seldom rise above" (010-003).

## The Hollow Patriotism of Multi-National Armies

In his chapter on [[nationality]], Mill identifies a debased form of patriotism that serves despotism rather than liberty. An army composed of various nationalities, lacking any organic connection to the people it rules, "has no other patriotism than devotion to the flag." Such armies, held together solely by officers and government, "have been the executioners of liberty through the whole duration of modern history" (016-002).

This passage reveals the distinction Mill draws between authentic patriotism — rooted in fellow-feeling, shared institutions, and civic participation — and the ersatz patriotism of military obedience, which is merely loyalty to command.

## Patriotism in Practice: Lord Durham's Report

Mill offers a concrete example of patriotism in action through his praise of Lord Durham's Report on the affairs of British North America (1839), which established the principle of responsible self-government for Britain's settler colonies. He describes the Report as "the imperishable memorial of that nobleman's courage, patriotism, and enlightened liberality" (018-004). Here patriotism manifests as the willingness to reform imperial governance according to principle rather than habit — a fusion of moral courage and public-spiritedness that Mill presents as exemplary.
