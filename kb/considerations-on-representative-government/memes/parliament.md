Parliament, as the central [[representative-government|representative]] institution, serves three essential functions in Mill's framework. It acts as the organ of public [[public-opinion|opinion]], the committee of [[legislation|legislative]] oversight, and the congress of national deliberation.

Mill distinguishes sharply between Parliament's proper role of controlling government and the improper delegation to it of actual administration (005-012).

## Functions of Parliament

Mill identifies three primary functions of a representative assembly:

| Function | Description | Key PN |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| Control of Government | Oversee and check the executive; refuse assent to acts; appoint/remove officials | (005-003), (005-009) |
| Expression of [[public-opinion|Public Opinion]] | Serve as the nation's committee of grievances and congress of opinions | (005-012) |
| Deliberation | Public discussion and scrutiny of proposed legislation | (005-013) |

> "Instead of the function of governing, for which it is radically unfit, the proper office of a representative assembly is to watch and control the government" (005-012)

Mill argues that Parliament should not itself administer or even draft legislation in detail, but should determine who shall enact laws and ensure those laws serve the public interest (005-013).

## Parliament and the Executive

In the [[constitution|British Constitution]], Parliament's relationship to the executive is one of checks and balances. The Crown possesses a veto over acts of Parliament and appoints ministers, but Parliament controls supply — money can only be granted on the motion of a responsible minister (005-003), (005-007).

> "In addition to this, the Parliament has an office not inferior even to legislation itself — to be at once the nation's committee of grievances and its congress of opinions" (005-015)

Mill observes that ministers may be checked by Parliament, and the interest of ministers in retaining office ensures a degree of accountability. However, he warns that the pressure of getting through bills renders Parliament "more and more incapable of passing any but the most hastily-conceived and ill-considered measures" (005-012).

## Composition and Electoral Basis

A Parliament's legitimacy derives from its representativeness. Mill devotes extensive analysis to the problem of ensuring that Parliament truly reflects the nation rather than merely its majority [[party|faction]]:

- **Majority domination**: A bare majority can capture nearly all seats in a simple plurality system (007-004).
- **Consequence**: Large minorities are left without any representation, undermining the legitimacy of the assembly.
- **[[minority|Minority]] representation**: Mill advocates for [[thomas-hare|Thomas Hare]]'s system of proportional representation. This ensures that every substantial portion of the electorate receives a voice (007-005).
- **Quality of members**: The elector must not simply choose the best individual candidate. Representatives should collectively produce wise governance through deliberation (002-019).

> "The Parliament thus brought together represents little more than the majority of the majority" (007-004)

## Parliament in the British Constitution

The [[constitution|British Constitution]] places Parliament — specifically the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] — at the center of government, yet Mill is keenly aware of its limitations. He notes that the "predominant power in the constitution" has shifted over time, with the [[house-of-commons|Commons]] originally intended only as a check on the Crown (013-004).

Parliament's historical evolution, Mill argues, demonstrates that representative institutions are not static forms but adapt to changes in the distribution of political power and social conditions (004-010).
