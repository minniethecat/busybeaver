# Representative Government

**Representative government** is the ideal type of [[government]] in which the whole people, or a numerous portion of them, exercise the governing power through deputies periodically elected by themselves (005-002). Mill identifies it as "the ideal type of the most perfect polity" (004-001), combining popular control with competent administration.

## Definition and Core Principles

The meaning of [[representative-democracy|representative government]] is "that the whole people, or some numerous portion of them, exercise through deputies periodically elected by themselves the ultimate controlling power" (005-002). This controlling power — "the complete sovereignty of the general body of the people over every act done in their name" (005-003) — is the essential characteristic.

For Mill, the British constitution exemplifies [[representative-democracy|representative government]] "in the correct sense of the term" because "the practical supremacy in the state does reside in the representatives of the people" (005-004). However, he warns that "it is above all necessary to keep separate the idea of a [[representative-democracy|representative government]], and that of a government which acts through a representative body" (005-003) — the latter may not be genuinely representative.

## Preconditions for Success

Mill distinguishes three fundamental failures that can prevent [[representative-democracy|representative government]] from functioning:

> "The first is, when the people are not willing to accept it. The second is, when the people are not willing or able to do what is necessary for its preservation. The third is, when the people are not willing or able to fulfill the duties and discharge the functions which it imposes on them." (004-004)

A people may be "not yet ripe for [[representative-democracy|representative government]]" (006-002) if they have not yet learned "the first lesson of civilization, that of obedience" (004-006). Representative government requires a population willing to labour for it — to "take the trouble necessary to give effect to the legitimate influence of their collective opinion upon the conduct of public affairs" (004-007).

## Relation to Civilization

Mill connects [[representative-democracy|representative government]] directly to the progress of [[civilization]]:

> "There are indeed but few obstacles to the progress of civilization which representative government would not have a decided tendency to remove." (004-008)

The progress of civilization itself tends toward representative institutions — "the progress of civilization which [[representative-democracy|representative government]] would have had a decided tendency to promote" (004-008). As societies advance, the form of government must adapt: "a form of government fit for a high state of civilization would have as one of its fundamental elements, a numerous and variously composed representative body" (005-013).

## Dangers and Limitations

Mill identifies specific dangers facing [[representative-democracy|representative government]]s. One is "the sinister interest of the holders of power: it is the danger of [[class-interest|class legislation]], of government intended for (whether really effecting or not) the immediate benefit of the dominant class, to the lasting detriment of the whole" (006-019). Another is the tendency of representative bodies toward "collective mediocrity" (007-013).

The futurity of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]s faces challenges from "the natural tendency of [[representative-democracy|representative government]], as of modern civilization, towards collective mediocrity" (007-013). Mill was particularly concerned that representative assemblies might fail to attract the most qualified individuals.

## Distinct from Pure Democracy

Mill distinguishes [[representative-democracy|representative government]] from pure [[democracy]] — in [[representative-democracy|representative government]], the people do not govern directly but through chosen deputies. This mediated form combines the legitimating force of popular will with the competence of selected representatives. The [[executive]] functions and [[legislation]] are carried out by elected or appointed officials, not by the people directly.
