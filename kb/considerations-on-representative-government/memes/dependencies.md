The governance of **dependencies** — territories subject to a free state but not fully integrated into its [[representative-government|representative institutions]] — forms the subject of Mill's Chapter XVIII. Mill distinguishes between different classes of dependencies and prescribes different modes of government for each, applying the principles of representative government to the imperial context.

## Definition and Classification

Mill defines dependencies as territories acquired by a state through [[despotism|conquest]] or colonization, which remain subject to the governing authority without being fully incorporated into the representative system of the parent state. He draws a crucial distinction between two classes of dependencies. (018-001)

> States, like all others, may possess dependencies, acquired either by conquest or by colonization, which are not, and from their geographical position or from peculiarities of race or of social institutions cannot be, on an equal footing with the integral parts of the state.
>
> (018-001)

The first class consists of dependencies whose population is sufficiently advanced in civilization to be capable of representative institutions. For these, Mill advocates a system of complete internal [[self-government|self-government]] while remaining under the imperial authority for external affairs. (018-003)

The second class comprises territories whose population is not yet at a stage of civilization adequate for representative government. For these, Mill argues that direct rule by the parent state, administered by professional civil servants, is both necessary and justifiable. (018-004)

> In the case of dependencies of the former class, this country has at length, in the case of Canada and of the Australian [[colonies|colonies]], adopted the only proper rule — that of leaving the internal government of each dependency entirely in the hands of its own people.
>
> (018-004)

## The Principle of Non-Interference

For dependencies capable of self-government, Mill strongly advocates the principle of complete internal autonomy. The parent state should not interfere in the internal affairs of such dependencies, limiting its role to external relations and defense. This principle, Mill observes, has been successfully applied in the cases of Canada and the Australian colonies. (018-005)

Mill argues that the benefits of this arrangement flow both ways: the dependency gains the advantages of self-government and political education, while the parent state is relieved of the burden and moral responsibility of governing people without their consent.

## Government of Less Advanced Dependencies

For territories whose population is not yet fit for representative institutions — Mill mentions India as the primary example — the responsibility falls on the parent state to provide good government through trained administrators. This is not, in Mill's view, a permanent arrangement but a transitional one, during which the dependency is being prepared for eventual self-government. (018-006)

> Thus far of the dependencies whose population is in a sufficiently advanced state to be fitted for representative government.
>
> (018-006)

## The Equal Opportunity Principle

Mill argues that the most effective means of securing the loyalty of dependencies is to open the service of government on perfectly equal terms to their inhabitants. When the leading men of a dependency can aspire to the highest offices of the empire, the sense of collective degradation is removed, and the dependency becomes genuinely integrated into the larger political community. (018-010)

This principle of equal opportunity, Mill contends, transforms the relationship from one of subordination to one of partnership, serving both justice and policy.
