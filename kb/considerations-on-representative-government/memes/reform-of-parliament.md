The **Reform of Parliament** was the central political question of John Stuart Mill's era.

In 1861, Mill opens *Considerations on Representative Government* observing that "both Conservatives and Liberals have lost confidence in the political creeds which they nominally profess, while neither side appears to have made any progress in providing itself with a better" — an impasse he attributes to "the recent debates on Reform of Parliament" (P01-002).

## The Reform Debate Context

Mill situates the Reform of Parliament within a broader crisis of political faith:

| Faction | Position on Reform | Mill's Critique |
|---------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Conservatives | Resistant to further [[suffrage|franchise]] expansion | No positive doctrine — merely "nominal" profession of creed (P01-002) |
| Liberals | Advocating further democratization | Lacking a comprehensive theory of representation beyond extending the vote |
| Radicals (Mill's position) | Reform must be structural, not merely expansive | Neither mere compromise nor simple democratization suffices (P01-002) |

> "Yet such a better doctrine must be possible; not a mere compromise, by splitting the difference between the two, but something wider than either" (P01-002)

Mill's distinctive contribution to the reform debate was to argue that the question was not *whether* to reform [[parliament|Parliament]] but *how* — specifically, how to design electoral institutions that would produce intelligent legislation rather than merely registering majority opinion (P01-002).

## Key Reform Issues

The parliamentary reform debates of Mill's time encompassed several interconnected questions:

1. **[[franchise|Franchise]] Extension**: How far should the vote be extended? Mill supported a broad [[suffrage|franchise]] but conditioned on educational qualification — "the voter must be able to read, write, and perform the common operations of arithmetic" (008-012).

2. **[[constituency|Constituency]] Organization**: Should representation be territorial or functional? Mill advocated for [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s system, which combined local constituencies with national aggregation of preferences (007-007).

3. **[[ballot|Ballot]] Reform**: Should voting be open or secret? Mill controversially supported the [[ballot|open ballot]], arguing that voting is a public trust, not a private right (010-007).

4. **[[house-of-lords|Second Chamber]] Reform**: What should replace the hereditary [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]]? Mill proposed a revising chamber composed of experienced [[public-office|public servant]]s and experts rather than hereditary peers (013-009).

> "I have, in a pamphlet on Parliamentary Reform, expressed myself in favor of the open ballot" (010-007)

## Duration of Parliaments

A further question in the reform debates was the proper length of a parliamentary term. Mill addresses this in Chapter XI, "Of the Duration of Parliaments," where he frames the issue as a balance between accountability and independence (011-001).

Too long a term, Mill warns, risks the member forgetting his responsibility, conducting his duties "with a view to his own personal advantage," or neglecting consultation with constituents. Too short a term denies the representative the opportunity to be judged "not by a single act, but by his course of action" (011-001).

Mill notes that the appropriate duration depends on the broader constitutional context. Where democratic power is weak and requires stimulation, frequent elections are essential — "even three years, in such circumstances, are almost too long a period." Where democracy is ascendant and needs moderating, longer terms help prevent "timid subserviency" to popular opinion (011-001).

Applied to contemporary England, Mill judged the existing seven-year term "of unnecessary length" but "hardly worth altering," particularly since the ever-present possibility of an earlier dissolution kept members attentive to their constituents (011-001).

He observed that annual Parliaments, once the rallying cry of advanced reformers, had largely faded from the political agenda precisely because democratic influences on members had grown so pervasive (011-001).

## Pledges and the Independence of the Representative

A distinct but related question concerned whether constituents should bind their representatives by pledges. Mill addresses this in Chapter XII, arguing that while electors are entitled to full knowledge of a candidate's political opinions, they should not require actual pledges except under narrow circumstances (012-003).

The broader principle, Mill contends, is that electors "ought to be unremitting in their search for a representative of such calibre as to be intrusted with full power of obeying the dictates of his own judgment."

The benefit of a capable representative's independent judgment is certain, "while the hypothesis of his being wrong and their being right on the points of difference is a very doubtful one" (012-009).

> They ought to be unremitting in their search for a representative of such calibre as to be intrusted with full power of obeying the dictates of his own judgment: that they should consider it a duty which they owe to their fellow-countrymen, to do their utmost toward placing men of this quality in the Legislature, and that it is of much greater importance to themselves to be represented by such a man than by one who professes agreement in a greater number of their opinions.
>
> (012-009)

## Mill's Synthesis

Mill's position in the reform debates was distinctive: he rejected both the conservative defense of the status quo and the liberal faith that extending the [[suffrage|franchise]] alone would cure political ills. His synthesis proposed:

- **Institutional pluralism**: Multiple channels of representation, not a single sovereign assembly.
- **Qualified democracy**: Universal participation with graduated influence based on demonstrated competence.
- **[[minority|Minority]] guarantees**: [[thomas-hare|Proportional representation]] to ensure all significant opinion groups have a voice.

This synthesis, Mill believed, was the "better doctrine" that could transcend the sterile Liberal-Conservative debate — "something wider than either, which, in virtue of its superior comprehensiveness, might be adopted by either Liberal or Conservative without renouncing any thing which he really feels to be valuable in his own creed" (P01-002).
