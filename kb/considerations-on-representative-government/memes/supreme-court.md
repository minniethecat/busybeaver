The **Supreme Court** occupies a central position in Mill's analysis of federal government. In Chapter XVII, "Of Federal Representative Governments," Mill identifies the Supreme Court of the United States as the essential institutional mechanism for resolving disputes between federal and state authorities — an independent judicial umpire without which the [[federal|federal system]] could not function.

## The Need for an Independent Umpire

Mill argues that in a federal system, where every citizen owes obedience to two governments — that of the state and that of the federation — the constitutional limits of each authority must be precisely defined. Critically, the power to adjudicate disputes between them must not reside in either government, but in an umpire independent of both. (017-006)

> There must be a Supreme Court of Justice, and a system of subordinate courts in every state of the Union, before whom such questions shall be carried, and whose judgment on them, in the last stage of appeal, shall be final.
>
> (017-006)

This arrangement, Mill observes, produces the remarkable consequence that a court of justice, the highest federal tribunal, is supreme over the various governments, both state and federal — possessing the power to declare laws or acts void if they exceed constitutional authority.

## Judicial Review and Its Operation

Mill examines with admiration the American innovation of judicial review. The Supreme Court, he notes, has the right to declare that any law made, or act done, by either the federal or state governments exceeds the powers assigned to them by the Constitution, and in consequence has no legal validity. (017-007)

Mill acknowledges that before trial was made, there were strong doubts about whether such a tribunal would have the courage to exercise its constitutional power, whether it would exercise it wisely, and whether governments would submit peaceably to its decisions. He reports, however, that over two generations of experience, these apprehensions have been entirely quieted.

## Tocqueville's Analysis of Judicial Method

Mill draws on [[tocqueville|Alexis de Tocqueville]]'s analysis to explain the success of the Supreme Court. The Court's effectiveness derives from its judicial method: it does not declare the law in the abstract, but waits until a case between parties is brought before it. This means its declarations come only after extensive public discussion, after hearing the point fully argued on both sides by skilled advocates, and it decides only as much of the question as the case before it requires. (017-007)

> The eminently beneficial working of so singular a provision is probably, as [[tocqueville|M. de Tocqueville]] remarks, in a great measure attributable to the peculiarity inherent in a court of justice acting as such — namely, that it does not declare the law eo nomine and in the abstract, but waits until a case between man and man is brought before it judicially.
>
> (017-007)

## Institutional Legitimacy

Mill emphasizes that the Supreme Court's authority ultimately rests on public confidence in the intellectual pre-eminence and impartiality of its judges. He warns that the stability of federal institutions depends on maintaining this confidence, and expresses concern about the Supreme Court's judgment in the Dred Scott case declaring slavery to be lawful in the Territories — describing it as a shock to the main pillar of [[american-constitution|the American Constitution]]. (017-007)

## International Tribunal

Beyond domestic federal disputes, Mill sees the Supreme Court as serving a broader function: it is the first great example of a real international tribunal, dispensing international law between states and providing a judicial remedy where the usual remedies of war and diplomacy are precluded by the federal union. (017-008)

> The Supreme Court of the federation dispenses international law, and is the first great example of what is now one of the most prominent wants of civilized society, a real international tribunal.
>
> (017-008)
