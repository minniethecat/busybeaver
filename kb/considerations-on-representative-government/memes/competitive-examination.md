**Competitive examination** is the mechanism Mill advocates for selecting candidates for first appointments to the public service. Rather than relying on ministerial patronage, recommendation, or a mere pass-level threshold, Mill argues that public appointments should be awarded to those candidates who most distinguish themselves in open competition. The principle, though of "recent introduction" in Mill's time, represents for him the single most effective reform for ensuring honest and capable public administration.

## The Case for Competition

Mill's argument for competitive examination rests on two observations. First, ministers cannot be trusted to make impartial appointments: "however disinterested as to his personal wishes," a minister "never will be proof against the solicitations of persons who have the power of influencing his own [[election]], or whose political adherence is important to the ministry" (014-015). Second, a mere pass examination creates perverse incentives. When the examiner's choice is between "blighting the prospects of an individual and performing a duty to the public," the balance "inclines to the side of good-nature," and the standard sinks to "something almost contemptible" (014-016).

> It is also absolutely necessary that the examinations should be competitive, and the appointments given to those who are most successful. A mere pass examination never, in the long run, does more than exclude absolute dunces.
>
> (014-016)

## How Competition Raises Standards

Mill identifies a powerful cascade effect from competitive examination. When appointments go to those who most distinguish themselves among many candidates, and successful competitors are "classed in order of merit":

1. Each candidate is "stimulated to do his very utmost" (014-016)
2. The influence extends to "every place of liberal [[education]] throughout the country" (014-016)
3. Schoolmasters gain "an object of ambition and an avenue to success" in producing high-performing pupils (014-016)
4. The state does more "to raise the quality of educational institutions throughout the country" than through any other means (014-016)

Mill observes that "where there is no inducement to exceed a certain minimum, the minimum comes to be the maximum" (014-016). Competition transforms this dynamic by rewarding excellence rather than mere adequacy.

| System | Incentive | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Patronage | Cultivate political connections | Appointments to the well-connected, regardless of merit |
| Pass examination | Meet the minimum threshold | Standard sinks; "the minimum comes to be the maximum" |
| Competitive examination | Surpass all other candidates | Candidates strive for excellence; educational standards rise nationally |

## The Indian Civil Service Model

Mill points to the Indian civil service as "nearly the only case in which it exists in its completeness" (014-016). The [[east-india-company|East India Company]]'s competitive examination system had already begun to produce measurable effects on middle-class education in Britain, despite encountering resistance from those who preferred the old patronage system. Mill uses this example to demonstrate that competitive examination is not merely theoretical but practically effective.

## Criticisms and Mill's Response

Mill catalogues and rebuts several objections to competitive examination. Critics argue that great men like Clive or Wellington "could not have passed the test" — to which Mill replies that "because Clive and Wellington did not do what was not required of them, they could not have done it if it had been required" (014-018). Others complain that "book-worms" lack "the habits of gentlemen"; Mill retorts that "wherever these are needed, let them be inquired into and separately provided for, not to the exclusion of mental qualifications, but in addition" (014-018).

> We are triumphantly told that neither Clive nor Wellington could have passed the test which is prescribed for an aspirant to an engineer cadetship; as if, because Clive and Wellington did not do what was not required of them, they could not have done it if it had been required.
>
> (014-018)

## Beyond Entry: Limits of Competition

Mill acknowledges that while first appointments should be decided by competitive examination, subsequent promotion "would in most cases be impossible" to decide by the same method (014-019). For routine duties, seniority should govern advancement; for positions requiring special capacity, the department chief should select from the body of qualified candidates. This mixed system preserves the integrity of entry while allowing for the development of professional judgment over a career.
