**Confidence**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, is the trust that citizens repose in their governors and institutions — a trust that is both a condition of effective government and a product of good [[political-institution|institutional design]]. Mill's treatment of confidence is characteristically functional: he is less interested in confidence as a sentiment than in confidence as a mechanism — the practical trust that enables government to act and that constrains government from abusing its power.

Confidence appears throughout the *Considerations* in several distinct but related contexts: the confidence that the people place in their [[representation|representatives]], the confidence that the executive must maintain in [[parliament|Parliament]], the confidence that member states must have in federal institutions, and the confidence that the governed must have in the impartiality and competence of their governors (005-012).

## Confidence as a Condition of Government

Mill's most fundamental claim about confidence is that it is a practical necessity of government. A government that has lost the confidence of the governed cannot govern effectively, regardless of its formal powers:

> "Having lost confidence in the political creeds which they profess" — Mill opens the *Considerations* with an observation about the collapse of public confidence in received political doctrines (P01-002)

| Object of Confidence | Consequence of Loss |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| Political creeds | When people "lose confidence in the political creeds which they profess," the intellectual foundation of government erodes (P01-002) |
| Representatives | When constituents lose confidence in their representatives, the representative relationship is severed |
| Institutions | When citizens lose confidence in institutions, compliance becomes mechanical rather than voluntary |
| The governing class | When the governed lose confidence in their governors, the government rules by coercion rather than consent |

> "Neither party having sufficient confidence in its strength to attempt the thorough reforms which the public good requires" — the absence of confidence produces political paralysis (014-011)

This passage, from Mill's discussion of [[party|party government]], captures a distinctive pathology of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]. When parties lack confidence in their own durability — when each expects soon to be replaced by the other — the result is a bias toward short-term measures and against comprehensive reform. Confidence, in this context, is not merely a sentiment but a structural condition of effective governance.

## Confidence and Executive Accountability

Mill's most institutionally specific discussion of confidence concerns the relationship between the executive and [[parliament|Parliament]]. In the British constitutional system, the government must maintain the confidence of the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] to remain in office:

> "Persons in its confidence, having a right to speak, though not to vote" — Mill describes the mechanism by which the executive is held accountable through parliamentary confidence (005-012)

| Mechanism of Confidence | Institutional Form |
|------------------------|-------------------|
| Ministerial responsibility | Ministers must answer to [[parliament|Parliament]] for their conduct |
| Confidence votes | The government falls if it loses a vote of confidence |
| Question time | "The right to speak" — ministers are interrogated by the opposition |
| Public justification | The government must explain and defend its actions publicly |

> "A place where every interest and shade of opinion in the country can have its cause even passionately pleaded" — the representative body is the arena in which confidence is tested and renewed (005-015)

## Confidence in Federal Systems

Mill extends his analysis of confidence to [[federal|federal system]]s, where the problem of confidence takes on a distinctive form. In a [[federal|federation]], the member states must have confidence in the federal government's impartiality and competence, and the federal government must have confidence that the member states will comply with its decisions:

> "Even these grounds of confidence would not have sufficed to prevent the attempt" — Mill describes the fragility of federal confidence in the American system (017-006)

| Level of Confidence | Federal Mechanism |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| States' confidence in federal impartiality | A federal supreme court that is independent of both federal and state governments |
| Federal confidence in state compliance | Federal enforcement powers, including military force if necessary |
| States' confidence in each other | [[constitution|Constitution]]al guarantees of equal treatment |
| Citizens' confidence in the [[federal|federal system]] | "The same confidence might not be felt in its preservation" if the system appears unstable (017-011) |

> "The same confidence might not be felt in its preservation" — confidence in institutions is self-reinforcing: institutions that are trusted are preserved, and institutions that are preserved are trusted (017-011)

## Confidence and Imperial Governance

In his discussion of [[colonies|dependencies]], Mill identifies a distinctive failure of confidence: the absence of mutual trust between the metropolitan power and the colonial population:

> "Have confidence in each other's objects, nor have confidence in each other's principles of government" — the colonial relationship is characterised by a systematic deficit of mutual confidence (018-006)

This deficit is structural, not personal. Colonial governors and colonial subjects may be well-intentioned, but the institutional structure of colonial rule makes mutual confidence impossible. The metropolis cannot trust the [[dependency|colony]] to govern itself competently; the [[dependency|colony]] cannot trust the metropolis to govern in the [[dependency|colony]]'s interest. This mutual distrust is, in Mill's analysis, one of the strongest arguments for moving colonies toward [[self-government]] — not because self-government guarantees good government, but because it eliminates the structural distrust that makes good government impossible under colonial rule.

> "Each other's objects" are opaque to the other party — the metropolitan power and the colonial population have different interests and different sources of information, and neither can be confident that the other understands or respects its concerns (018-006)
