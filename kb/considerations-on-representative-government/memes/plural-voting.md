## Definition

Plural voting is an electoral mechanism in which some citizens are entitled to cast more than one vote, based on criteria intended to reflect superior mental cultivation. Mill advocated this as a corrective to the numerical dominance of the least educated class in a democracy, arguing that the opinion of the better-informed should carry greater weight in public affairs (008-008).

Mill distinguished plural voting sharply from property-based qualifications, which he considered "supremely odious." Instead, he proposed that additional votes be awarded on the basis of occupation (employers, foremen, professionals), educational attainment (university degrees, examination certificates), and membership in the liberal professions (008-010).

## Mill's Rationale

Mill grounded plural voting in the principle that "every one ought to have a voice" but that "every one should have an equal voice is a totally different proposition." He argued that when two persons have a joint interest, justice does not require their opinions to carry equal weight if one is demonstrably wiser or more virtuous. The difficulty lies only in ascertaining which person is superior — a task that becomes more feasible when assessing groups rather than individuals (008-008).

The proposal was not merely a practical expedient but a declaration of principle. Mill insisted that "it is not useful, but hurtful, that the constitution of the country should declare ignorance to be entitled to as much political power as knowledge." National institutions, he argued, shape the spirit of a people, and they should embody the conviction that "every one is entitled to some influence, but the better and wiser to more than others" (008-014).

> It is not useful, but hurtful, that the constitution of the country should declare ignorance to be entitled to as much political power as knowledge. The national institutions should place all things that they are concerned with before the mind of the citizen in the light in which it is for his good that he should regard them; and as it is for his good that he should think that every one is entitled to some influence, but the better and wiser to more than others, it is important that this conviction should be professed by the state, and embodied in the national institutions.
>
> (008-014)

## Proposed Criteria

Mill's scheme suggested several grounds for awarding plurality of votes (008-010):

- **Occupation**: An employer of labor is on average more intelligent than a laborer; a foreman more than an ordinary laborer; a banker or manufacturer more than a tradesman. The occupation should have been held for at least three years.
- **Liberal professions**: Lawyers, physicians, and other professionals, where genuine qualification is required for entry.
- **University graduates**: Those holding degrees from recognized institutions.
- **Examination certificates**: Voluntary public examinations open to all, allowing even "the poorest individual" to prove his intelligence and claim the privilege.
- **Oxford Associate degree** and similar middle-class examinations.

### Absolute Condition

Mill imposed one firm limit: the plurality must never allow the privileged minority to "outweigh by means of it all the rest of the community." The superiority given to education must stop short of enabling the educated to practice [[class-legislation|class legislation]] themselves. Plural voting was to be a counterpoise against the numerical weight of the uneducated, not a tool for oligarchic domination (008-011).

## Application to Local Government

Mill extended the principle to local representative bodies, noting that in local elections there was less objection to basing plural voting on a money qualification — since "the honest and frugal dispensation of money forms so much larger a part of the business of the local than of the national body" (015-004).

## Historical Context

At the time of Mill's writing, plural voting already existed in British vestry elections and Poor Law guardian elections, though it was "so unfamiliar in [[election|elections]] to [[parliament|Parliament]] that it is not likely to be soon or willingly adopted." Mill predicted that "the time will certainly arrive when the only choice will be between this and equal [[universal-suffrage|universal suffrage]]" (008-012).
