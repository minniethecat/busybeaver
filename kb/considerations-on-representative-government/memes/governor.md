A **governor** in Mill's political vocabulary denotes those who actually conduct the work of government — the professional executive class distinct from the representative or legislative body. Mill uses the term broadly to encompass bureaucratic administrators, colonial governors general, local executives in federal systems, and deputed rulers of dependencies. The central problem of [[representative-government]], as Mill frames it, is to combine the professional skill of trained governors with the general control exercised by [[representation|representative]] bodies over them. (006-008)(006-010)

Mill's treatment of the governor spans multiple dimensions of his political theory: the tension between bureaucratic expertise and democratic accountability, the proper relationship between rulers and ruled, the institutional design of colonial administration, and the role of local executives within [[federal]] systems.

## Professional Governance and Bureaucracy

Mill identifies governance by professional governors as the defining characteristic of [[bureaucracy]]. He observes that in non-representative governments — whether monarchical or aristocratic — the only systems that have consistently displayed high political skill are those where "the work of government has been in the hands of governors by profession, which is the essence and meaning of bureaucracy." (006-008) The crucial distinction, he argues, is not whether governors are trained for office or acquire training through office, but rather whether the system of rule itself is professionalized.

This recognition of bureaucratic competence does not lead Mill to endorse bureaucracy as an ideal. Instead, he frames the central institutional challenge as reconciling two competing goods:

> The most perfect representative government, among a people in any degree ripe for it, and the most perfect imaginable bureaucracy. But it is, at the same time, one of the most important ends of political institutions, to attain as many of the qualities of the one as are consistent with the other.
>
> (006-010)

## Selection, Watching, and Control

The proper function of a representative assembly, in Mill's framework, is not to govern directly but to exercise oversight over those who do. He draws a sharp line between "the work of government properly so called, which can only be well performed after special cultivation, and that of selecting, watching, and, when needful, controlling the governors." (006-010) The former requires trained expertise; the latter is the province of the people's representatives, who act "not on those who do the work, but on those for whose benefit it ought to be done."

This tripartite function — selection, watching, and control — defines the [[representation|representative]] body's relationship to the executive. Mill insists that no progress can be made toward a skilled [[democracy]] unless the democracy accepts that specialized work must be performed by those who possess the requisite skill, while reserving to itself the work of "superintendence and check." (006-010)

## The Governor–Governed Relationship

Addressing the proper relation between rulers and ruled, Mill argues that a people cannot be well governed in opposition to their "primary notions of right, even though these may be in some points erroneous." (012-008) The bond between governors and governed requires more than technical competence; it demands a shared moral framework:

> A correct estimate of the relation which should subsist between governors and governed does not require the electors to consent to be represented by one who intends to govern them in opposition to their fundamental convictions.
>
> (012-008)

This principle places limits on the discretion even of skilled governors. Technical expertise does not grant a license to override the settled moral convictions of the governed community. When fundamental values are at stake, electors are justified in dismissing a governor whose views conflict irreconcilably with their own, regardless of his administrative competence. (012-008)

## Colonial Governors

Mill devotes considerable attention to the institution of colonial governorship, drawing on British experience in [[india-british-governance|India]]. In his proposed design for a reformed [[second-chamber]], he enumerates governors general of India and British America among those qualified for senatorial dignity, alongside judges, cabinet ministers, and military commanders. (013-008) The permanent civil service was also to be represented in the upper house, reflecting Mill's conviction that administrative experience merited a voice in legislation.

The Governor General's Council in India serves Mill as a model of how executive authority should be structured. The council consisted of persons possessing professional knowledge of Indian affairs "which the governor general and governors usually lack, and which it would not be desirable to require of them." (014-006) In this arrangement, every council member was expected to record an opinion, with the governor general retaining the power to override even a unanimous council — but only by recording his reasons in a publicly accessible document:

> The result is, that the chief is individually and effectively responsible for every act of the government. The members of council have only the responsibility of advisers; but it is always known, from documents capable of being produced, and which, if called for by Parliament or public opinion always are produced, what each has advised, and what reasons he gave for his advice.
>
> (014-006)

This institutional mechanism — combining expert advice, the possibility of dissent on the record, ultimate executive authority, and public accountability — embodies Mill's broader theory of how governors should operate: empowered to act decisively, yet constrained by transparency and answerability.

## Local Governors in Federal Systems

In his discussion of [[federal]] government, Mill identifies local governors as instruments for reconciling provincial diversity with national unity. Where different provinces have different preferences — for example, regarding modes of taxation — the constitution may provide "local governors, and provincial assemblies for local purposes" operating under a single central government. (017-012) These local executives administer functions delegated by the provincial assemblies while remaining within the framework of the national state.

Mill cites the *pays d'états* of the old French monarchy as a historical precedent: each province, having agreed to furnish a fixed sum to the crown, assessed and collected it through its own officers, thereby "escaping the grinding despotism of the royal *intendants* and *subdélégués*." (017-012) The local governor in this model serves as a buffer between central authority and provincial self-administration.

## Deputed Governors of Dependencies

For dependencies not yet prepared for [[representative-government]], Mill endorses governance "by the dominant country, or by persons delegated for that purpose by it." (018-015) Such deputed governors exercise a form of legitimate [[despotism]], justified only insofar as it facilitates the subject people's "transition to a higher stage of improvement." The ruling country, Mill insists, "ought to be able to do for its subjects all that could be done by a succession of absolute monarchs," combining irresistible force with the accumulated wisdom of a more advanced civilization. (018-015)

Mill acknowledges the danger of unaccountable appointment. Criticizing the [[east-india-company|East India Company]], he notes that "the supreme dispensers of office — the governor general and governors — were appointed, in fact though not in name, by the same external authority" rather than through proper institutional channels. (018-021) This critique underscores his broader principle: the legitimacy of governors depends not only on their competence but on the accountability mechanisms that surround their appointment, deliberation, and oversight.
