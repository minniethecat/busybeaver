## Definition

In Mill's analysis of [[representative-government|representative government]], **class [[legislation|legislation]]** denotes laws enacted not for the common good but for the exclusive benefit of the class or sectional interest that holds legislative power. It is, for Mill, the "great danger" inherent in all forms of government: that the ruling power will govern in its own interest rather than in the interest of the governed (006-019). The concept is the organizing principle of his critique across Chapters VI through XII, where he examines how different constitutional arrangements either enable or restrain this tendency.

Class legislation differs from ordinary interest-group politics: it represents a structural capture of the legislative function where the identity of the ruling class determines the content of law. Mill treats this not as an occasional pathology but as the "characteristic disease" of representative institutions, requiring deliberate constitutional safeguards (007-001).

## The Danger of Majority Class Legislation

Mill's most sustained treatment concerns the danger of class legislation by a numerical majority. In a system of equal universal suffrage where the laboring class forms [[majority|the majority]], the risk is that "the legislature would be made up of persons who would represent the interests of the numerical majority alone" and would enact laws favoring that class at the expense of others (007-001). This is the core problem that representative government must solve.

The danger is not hypothetical for Mill but arises from the very structure of representation: any body invested with supreme legislative power will be tempted to use it for its own benefit. As he observes, "one of the greatest dangers of democracy, as of all other forms of government, lies in the sinister interest of the holders of power: it is the danger of class legislation" (006-019).

## Class Legislation by the Ruling Few

Mill is equally attentive to the older form of class legislation — rule by a wealthy or aristocratic minority. Before the extension of suffrage, the danger was "class legislation by the few" — laws designed to protect the privileges of property and rank (008-007). Even after democratic reforms, Mill warns that "preserving the educated from the class legislation of the uneducated" without re-establishing oligarchy remains the central institutional challenge (008-011).

Protection against class legislation is therefore reciprocal: each class must be safeguarded from the predatory legislation of others while being prevented from enacting its own (007-016). This principle of mutual protection is the foundation of Mill's arguments for plural voting, proportional representation, and a second chamber.

> The problem is, to find the means of preventing this abuse, without sacrificing the characteristic advantages of popular government.
>
> (007-001)

## Institutional Safeguards

Mill proposes several institutional mechanisms to counter the tendency toward class legislation:

| Mechanism | Chapter | Principle |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Plural voting | VIII | Weighted suffrage gives greater influence to the educated, counterbalancing mere numbers |
| Proportional representation | VII | Ensures minorities are represented, preventing majority monopoly |
| Second chamber | XIII | Provides a check on hasty or class-interested legislation |
| Open ballot | X | Public voting makes legislators accountable to the whole community, not just their class |
| Pledges prohibition | XII | Frees representatives to exercise independent judgment for the general good |

When the electoral system is properly designed, Mill argues, "there can no longer be danger of class legislation; then the electors, being the nation" — because the representative body will mirror the full diversity of society rather than any single class (010-014).

The ultimate institutional solution is to ensure that no single class can command a legislative majority. As Mill writes: "In every government, the power of any body of persons is subject to the danger of class legislation for their own benefit" (012-002) — the task of the constitution is to disperse that power.

> The only escape from class legislation in its narrowest, and politically most dangerous sense, is that the legislature shall include representatives of all the different sections of society, in such numbers that each shall be heard, and that no single section can carry a measure against the combined voice of the rest.
>
> (012-010)

## Relation to Sinister Interest

Class legislation is closely related to Mill's concept of [[sinister-interest|sinister interest]] — the tendency of those in power to pursue their own advantage rather than the common good. While "sinister interest" describes the motivational mechanism, "class legislation" describes its legislative product. Together they form the twin poles of Mill's diagnosis of what makes governments fail.
