The **council** occupies a distinct place in Mill's architecture of representative government. Mill distinguishes councils sharply from boards: where boards make decisions collectively and thereby destroy responsibility, councils advise while leaving ultimate decision with a single responsible individual. This distinction allows government to benefit from "the multitude of councillors" in whom "there is wisdom," without sacrificing the principle of individual accountability (014-004).

## Advisory Councils for the Executive

Mill's central innovation is the advisory council attached to each major executive department. The head of a department, being "a mere politician" who may lack professional knowledge of his portfolio, requires expert advisers (014-005). For military and naval affairs, this is "emphatically necessary," and Mill recommends permanent councils composed of "able and experienced professional men" who do not resign with changes of ministry (014-005).

Councils should be "consultative merely, in this sense, that the ultimate decision should rest undividedly with the minister himself" (014-006). Yet councillors must not be reduced to "ciphers": they must be placed under conditions that make it impossible "not to express an opinion, and impossible for him not to listen to and consider their recommendations, whether he adopts them or not" (014-006).

## The India Council Model

Mill presents the Council of the Governor General of India as an exemplary design. Its features are:

- Members possess "professional knowledge of Indian affairs, which the governor general and governors usually lack" (014-006)
- Every member records an opinion, including reasons when there is dissent
- In ordinary cases, the majority decides
- The governor may override even a unanimous council, but must record his reasons
- The result: "the chief is individually and effectively responsible for every act," while councillors bear "only the responsibility of advisers" (014-006)

> The relation which ought to exist between a chief and this description of advisers is very accurately hit by the constitution of the Council of the Governor General and those of the different Presidencies in India.
>
> (014-006)

Mill calls this "one of the most successful instances of the adaptation of means to ends which political history... has yet to show" (014-007), lamenting that it was "probably destined to perish in the general holocaust" of Indian governmental reform.

## Council of Legislation

Mill also envisions a **Commission of Legislation** — a small body "not exceeding in number the members of a [[cabinet]]" — to serve as a permanent law-drafting council (005-013). This council would embody "the element of intelligence" in law-making, while [[parliament|Parliament]] would represent "that of will." Parliament would retain the power to accept, reject, or remit bills for reconsideration, but would not alter measures itself. Mill observes that even Athenian [[democracy]] separated law-making (entrusted to the *Nomothetæ*) from decree-making by the popular assembly (005-014).

|---|---|---|---|
| Departmental advisory council | Advise minister on professional matters | Permanent expert professionals | Minister decides; council advises |

| Commission of Legislation | Draft laws | Small body of professional legislators | Parliament accepts or rejects; cannot amend |

## Municipal Councils

Mill extends the council principle to local government. Every locality "ought to have its municipal council" (015-006), addressing the "local public intelligence and the local public spirit" (015-001). These councils differ from executive advisory councils in being representative and decision-making rather than purely consultative, but they share the principle that collective [[deliberation]] informs action without destroying accountability.

## Councils vs. Boards

Mill's distinction between councils and boards is fundamental to his administrative theory. A board makes decisions collectively and anonymously, destroying individual responsibility. A council advises openly and on the record, preserving the chief's undivided responsibility while enriching his judgment. Mill quotes Bentham approvingly: "Boards are screens" — but councils, properly constituted, are not screens; they are sources of wisdom that strengthen rather than dilute accountability (014-003).
