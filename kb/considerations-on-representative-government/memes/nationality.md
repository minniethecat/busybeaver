## Definition and Background

Mill defines nationality as the bond that unites a portion of mankind through "common sympathies which do not exist between them and any others" — sympathies that make them co-operate willingly, desire the same government, and wish that government to be exercised by themselves or a portion of themselves exclusively (016-001).

The causes of national feeling are multiple and cumulative. Identity of race and descent, community of language, and community of religion all contribute. Geographical limits play a role. But Mill identifies the strongest cause as **identity of political antecedents** — the possession of a shared national history and the consequent community of recollections: "collective pride and humiliation, pleasure and regret, connected with the same incidents in the past" (016-001). None of these causes is individually indispensable; Switzerland possesses a strong national sentiment despite its cantons differing in race, language, and religion, while Sicily long felt distinct from Naples despite sharing nearly all the usual markers of common nationality (016-001).

## Nationality and Representative Government

Mill's central argument concerning nationality is that **free institutions are next to impossible in a country made up of different nationalities**. His reasoning is structural rather than cultural:

> Among a people without fellow-feeling, especially if they read and speak different languages, the united public opinion necessary to the working of representative government can not exist.
>
> (016-002)

In a multi-national state, the influences that form opinions differ across sections. Different leaders command the confidence of different groups. The same books, newspapers, and speeches fail to reach the entire population. Each section fears injury from the other nationalities more than from the common government. Mutual antipathies outweigh shared grievances against the ruler, and none can rely on the others for fidelity in joint resistance (016-002).

### The Military Problem

Mill identifies a particularly grave danger: the absence of sympathy between the army and the people in a multi-national state:

> Soldiers to whose feelings half or three fourths of the subjects of the same government are foreigners will have no more scruple in mowing them down, and no more desire to ask the reason why, than they would have in doing the same thing against declared enemies.
>
> (016-002)

An army composed of various nationalities "has no other patriotism than devotion to the flag." Such armies, Mill argues, "have been the executioners of liberty through the whole duration of modern history." The government can sustain despotic rule by stationing Hungarian regiments in Italy and Italian regiments in Hungary, ruling "with the iron rod of foreign conquerors" (016-002).

## The Absorption of Nationalities

Mill takes a nuanced position on the merging of nationalities. He argues that when an "inferior and more backward" people is absorbed into a more advanced nationality, the result is beneficial:

> Nobody can suppose that it is not more beneficial to a Breton, or a Basque of French Navarre, to be brought into the current of the ideas and feelings of a highly civilized and cultivated people ... than to sulk on his own rocks, the half-savage relic of past times, revolving in his own little mental orbit, without participation or interest in the general movement of the world.
>
> (016-005)

He extends this judgment to the Welsh and the Scottish Highlanders as members of the British nation (016-005). Generally, the admixture of nationalities and the blending of their attributes is "a benefit to the human race" — not by extinguishing types, but by softening their extreme forms and combining the special aptitudes of each (016-006).

### Conditions for Amalgamation

When a conquering nationality is both more numerous and more improved, and governs with tolerable justice without granting exclusive privileges to its own members, the smaller nationality is gradually reconciled and amalgamated. Mill offers the example of Alsace and Bas-Bretagne in France, both of which had no remaining desire for separation (016-009). He contrasts this with Ireland, where historically "atrociously governed" conditions generated lasting resentment, though he notes that by his own time, equal treatment was rapidly producing reconciliation between "two races perhaps the most fitted of any two in the world to be the completing counterpart of one another" (016-009).

## Equal Nationalities and Federation

The greatest obstacles to blending arise when nationalities are nearly equal in numbers and power. In such cases, each cultivates its distinctive peculiarities with "party obstinacy"; obsolete customs and declining languages are revived to deepen separation; and whatever is granted to one nationality is perceived as taken from the rest (016-010).

Mill offers two paths for such cases:
1. Under a **despotic government** that is neutral among the nationalities, "identity of situation often produces harmony of feeling" over generations, especially if the populations are intermingled geographically (016-010).
2. If the aspiration to **free government** arrives before fusion is complete, separation becomes necessary — "if either freedom or concord is cared for, a necessity for breaking the connection altogether" (016-010). Post-separation, a federal tie may be useful, though Mill notes that each province may prefer federation with neighbors sharing more sympathies (016-010).

## Nationality in Class Analysis

Beyond the question of state boundaries, Mill treats nationality as a factor in the internal structure of representative government. A modern community not divided by "strong antipathies of race, language, or nationality" can be analyzed primarily along lines of economic class (laborers vs. employers of labor), with the representative system designed to balance these interests (006-020). The implication is that strong national divisions within a state disrupt this equilibrium, introducing cross-cutting cleavages that prevent the formation of a unified public opinion on which representative institutions depend.
