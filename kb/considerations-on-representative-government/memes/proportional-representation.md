## Definition

Proportional representation is the principle that every significant body of opinion in the electorate should be represented in the legislature in proportion to its numerical strength. Mill contrasts this with the winner-take-all system of local majorities, arguing that "equal and universal suffrage" is hollow unless "made real by the proportional representation of all minorities" (008-014). Without proportionality, a majority of electors can monopolize all seats, leaving substantial minorities entirely unrepresented.

## The Case Against Exclusive Majority Representation

Mill identifies the exclusion of minority voices as a fundamental defect of the conventional electoral system. Under single-member districts decided by simple majority, "the voice of the instructed minority may have no organs at all in the representative body" (007-013). This system produces "a collective mediocrity" in the legislature and effectively disenfranchises those whose views differ from the local majority.

The key innovation of proportional representation is that it enables voters scattered across multiple constituencies to combine their strength. In Mill's words, the system allows representatives to be returned "proportionally to their numbers" (013-006), ensuring that the minority does not merely exist in the population but has an audible voice in the assembly.

## Hare's Scheme of Personal Representation

Mill champions the specific proposal of Thomas Hare for a system of the single transferable vote. Under Hare's plan, voters across the country could cast their ballots for any candidate, with surplus votes transferring to ensure that every substantial group of electors could secure representation. Mill considered this "among the greatest improvements yet made in the theory and practice of government" (007-009).

The practical effect is to allow the instructed minority scattered throughout the constituencies to "unite to return a number, proportioned to their own numbers, of the very ablest men the country contains" (007-013). These representatives would then participate directly in the general deliberations, elevating the quality of debate and checking the dominance of mere numbers.

## Proportionality and the Weighted Suffrage

Mill extends the proportionality principle beyond the distribution of seats to the weight of individual votes. He argues that, within limits, society should allow "a greater proportional influence to those who have a greater stake in the country" (015-004) or those of demonstrably superior judgment. This is not a rejection of universality but an attempt to prevent numerical superiority from overwhelming instructed opinion entirely.
