In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], **representation** is the central mechanism through which the sovereignty of the people is exercised. It is not merely a procedural convenience but a test of how well the political system translates public opinion into governing capacity. The "really useful fruit produced by national representation" is the training of [[intelligence|public intelligence]] through participation — yet Mill identifies systemic failures that undermine this purpose (004-004).

## National Representation

The ideal of national representation, for Mill, is not simply the aggregation of local constituencies. It requires that the representative body reflect the full diversity of opinion across the nation:

"A really national representation (which is in substance the highest aim of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]) ought to represent all citizens, and not merely the majority" (004-010).

This principle stands in tension with the practical reality of [[election|electoral system]]s that allow dominant local majorities to capture all seats in a district, leaving substantial minorities wholly unrepresented. Mill sees this as a failure not of the democratic principle but of the specific machinery of representation (007-005).

## Democratic Representation and Equality

Mill's analysis distinguishes between the formal equality of [[suffrage|universal suffrage]] and the substantive equality of proportional influence:

| Principle | Description | Key PN |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Representation in proportion to numbers | Every division of the electoral body should have representation proportional to its size | (007-006) |
| Protection of minorities | A "completely equal democracy" in which a single class dominates produces great evils | (007-002) |
| Substantive equality | "Real [[equality|equality of representation]] is not obtained unless any section of electors can secure a representative" | (007-007) |

> "The minority must yield to the majority, the smaller number to the greater, is a familiar idea; and accordingly men think there is no necessity for giving a voice to the minority — it is the province of the majority to rule. But does it follow that the minority should have no representatives at all?" (007-003)

## Hare's Scheme of Proportional Representation

Mill devotes substantial attention to [[thomas-hare|Thomas Hare]]'s proposal for proportional representation, which he considers the most important reform to the representative system. The plan's key innovation is the transferable vote across constituencies:

- **Unit of representation**: The quota is determined by dividing total voters by seats in the House (007-008).
- **Transferable voting**: Electors may vote for any candidate regardless of locality; surplus votes above the quota transfer to the voter's next preference (007-008).
- **[[minority|Minority]] representation**: Every minority "consisting of a sufficiently large number" gains representation proportional to its size (007-010).
- **Candidate quality**: The system reduces the premium on "local influence" and "lavish expenditure," allowing candidates of "talents and character" to gain admission (007-011).

> "Of all modes in which a national representation can possibly be constituted, this one affords the best security for the intellectual qualifications desirable in the representatives" (007-011)

## Local versus National Character

A persistent objection to [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme was that it would destroy "the local character of the representation." Mill dismisses this concern:

| Objection | Mill's Response |
|-----------|----------------|
| Loss of local ties | A nation "consists of persons, not of artificial units, the creation of geography and statistics" (007-017) |
| Constituency attachment | If local ties are valuable, they will survive under any system; if not, no system can preserve them (007-017) |
| Practical administration | The plan's feasibility has been demonstrated — "there is no difficulty in reducing the principle to practice" (007-016) |

## Representation and Competence

Mill distinguishes between the **representative function** and the **governing function**. Representatives should deliberate and control, not administer:

- The representative body should be "an arena in which... every opinion in the country can produce itself in full light" (005-003).
- Actual administration should be handled by a professionally trained [[bureaucracy|civil service]] accountable to the representative body (005-011).
- This division prevents both legislative micromanagement and bureaucratic unaccountability.

## Defects of the Existing System

Mill catalogues systematic failures in representation as practiced in his time:

1. **Majority monopoly** — local majorities capture all seats, leaving minorities wholly unrepresented (007-005)
2. **Geographic distortion** — arbitrary constituency boundaries produce representation that "does not even aim at corresponding to the numbers" (007-015)
3. **Class exclusion** — the working class, though the majority, was almost entirely excluded from Parliament; "the majority in the House [has] been returned by a minority of the electors" (007-014)
4. **Quality degradation** — the existing system penalizes candidates of "talents and character" who lack local connections or wealth (007-011)

## The Democratic Principle
