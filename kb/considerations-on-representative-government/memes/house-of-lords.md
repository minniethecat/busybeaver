The House of Lords, the upper chamber of the British [[parliament|Parliament]], receives sustained though ambivalent treatment in [[John Stuart Mill]]'s *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*. Mill regards the Lords as a historically significant institution whose aristocratic composition is both its distinctive virtue and its fundamental limitation. As an unelected body of hereditary peers, the Lords represents, in Mill's analysis, a transitional stage in the evolution of representative institutions.

## The Historical Role of the Lords

Mill situates the House of Lords within a narrative of constitutional development from aristocratic toward [[democracy|democratic government]]. In earlier periods, the Lords served as a necessary check on monarchical power (013-004):

> ocratic state of society. The House of Lords was once the strongest power in the state, and it is still a considerable one.
>
> (013-004)

This historical role, Mill observes, has been progressively eroded by the rise of democratic forces. The [[reform-bill-1832|Reform Bill of 1832]] marked a decisive shift in the balance of constitutional power away from the Lords and toward the [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]].

## Mill's Critique of the Hereditary Principle

Mill's deepest objection to the House of Lords concerns its hereditary basis. A chamber constituted by birth rather than merit, he argues, inevitably lacks the intellectual and moral authority that a second chamber should possess:

> stituted in the manner of our House of Lords. So soon as conventional rank and hereditary wealth cease to command deference, a House of Lords has no solid foundation.
>
> (013-006)

Mill is sharply critical of the idea that a chamber of hereditary legislators can serve as an effective check on the popular will. Its authority, he argues, rests not on [[political-institution|institutional design]] but on residual social deference — a foundation that is steadily weakening (013-009):

> uilt on the foundation of the House of Lords. It is out of the question to think of creating a second chamber with the same authority as the House of Lords in a country where hereditary rank has not the same prestige.
>
> (013-009)

## The Lords as a Legislative Refiner

Despite his criticism, Mill acknowledges a useful function for the Lords as a revising and delaying chamber. Because the Lords are insulated from electoral pressure, they can exercise independent judgment on legislative proposals — particularly those rushed through the [[house-of-commons|Commons]] by populist impulse (005-012):

> en adopted principally by the House of Lords, the members of which are less subject to the temptations of popular applause than members of the House of Commons.
>
> (005-012)

This moderating function aligns with Mill's broader argument that [[deliberation]] and second thought are essential to good legislation. A properly constituted second chamber should compel the [[house-of-commons|Commons]] to reconsider ill-considered measures. Mill even suggests this function could be enhanced by reforming the Lords into a legislative commission:

> through the machinery of the House of Lords. A commission for preparing bills would be a far more useful body.
>
> (005-014)

## The Future of the Second Chamber

Mill's analysis points toward a fundamental reconstitution of the second chamber on non-hereditary lines. The House of Lords, in his view, cannot survive as a hereditary body in an increasingly democratic age. The question is not whether to reform it but what should replace it — a question Mill addresses in his broader discussion of [[bicameralism|second chambers]]:

> e form of a Second Chamber or House of Lords than in any other shape, this is only because the House of Lords has the advantage of actual existence.
>
> (013-005)
