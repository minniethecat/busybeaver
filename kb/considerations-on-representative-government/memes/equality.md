**Equality**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, is a foundational but contested principle. Mill endorses political equality as a general aspiration — "equality of all citizens" is synonymous with good government in its democratic form — but he complicates this endorsement with a recognition that formal equality can coexist with substantive inequality, and that the institutional mechanisms for achieving equality are far from straightforward.

Mill's treatment of equality is shaped by his rejection of two extreme positions: the radical egalitarianism that would give every citizen an equal vote regardless of competence, and the conservative defence of inequality as natural and inevitable. His own position — universal but weighted [[franchise|suffrage]] — is an attempt to reconcile the principle of equality with the demand for competence in government (007-002).

## Equality and Representation

Mill's primary concern with equality is in the domain of representation. The question is not whether citizens are equal in some abstract sense but whether the [[election|electoral system]] produces "real equality of representation":

> "The former is synonymous with the equality of all citizens; the latter, a government of inequality and privilege" (007-002)

| System | Relationship to Equality |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Universal [[suffrage|franchise]] with proportional representation | "Equality of all citizens" — every vote contributes to representation (007-002) |
| Universal [[suffrage|franchise]] with majoritarian districts | "A government of inequality and privilege" — majorities monopolise representation (007-002) |
| Restricted [[suffrage|franchise]] | Explicit inequality — "one part of the people rule over the rest" (007-003) |

Mill's distinction between formal and real equality is crucial. A system in which every citizen may vote but only the majority's votes produce representation is, in his analysis, not genuinely equal. The minority's votes are wasted, and the result is "a government of inequality and privilege" in which "one part of the people rule over the rest" (007-003).

> "Real equality of representation is not obtained unless every elector's vote counts for as much as any other" (007-007)

## Equality versus Competence

The most distinctive feature of Mill's treatment of equality is his willingness to qualify it in the name of competence. Not all votes should count equally, because not all voters are equally competent to judge public affairs:

> "Less objectionable than inequality of privilege grounded on irrelevant circumstances" — but some inequality may be justified by relevant differences in competence (008-014)

| Basis of Inequality | Mill's Assessment |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Birth or hereditary rank | "Irrelevant" — no connection to political competence |
| Sex | "Difference in height or in the color of the hair" — equally irrelevant (008-016) |
| [[property|Property]] | "A very imperfect test" — partially relevant but insufficient (008-010) |
| [[education|Education]] or [[education|mental cultivation]] | Relevant — "the opinion of the more educated should count for more" |
| Occupation or profession | Partially relevant — some occupations develop capacities relevant to political judgment |

> "Equality of real merit" — this, and only this, is the form of inequality Mill is willing to defend (014-019)

The phrase "equality of real merit" captures Mill's position precisely. He is not defending inequality as such but arguing that equality must be equality *among equals* — and that the relevant equality is equality of merit, not equality of birth, wealth, or station. Where merit is unequal, political influence should reflect that inequality — not to perpetuate privilege but to ensure that the better-informed have proportionally greater influence on decisions that affect everyone.

## Equality and Class

Mill is acutely aware that political equality, even when formally established, can be undermined by social and economic inequality. The equal vote means little if the voter is economically dependent on a landlord or employer who can dictate how the vote is cast:

> "Equality alone is cared for, but not liberty" — the pursuit of equality without attention to the conditions of freedom can produce new forms of domination (004-011)

| Domain of Equality | Condition for Realisation |
|--------------------|---------------------------|
| Political equality (equal vote) | Requires the [[ballot|secret ballot]] — otherwise the dependent voter's "equal" vote belongs to his master |
| Equality of representation | Requires proportional representation — otherwise majority monopolises all seats |
| Equality of participation | Requires economic independence — the dependent person cannot participate freely |
| Equality of consideration | Requires institutional mechanisms that force decision-makers to consider all affected interests |

> "Inequality of privilege grounded on irrelevant circumstances" — this is what Mill most consistently opposes (008-014)

The key word is "irrelevant." Mill is not an egalitarian in the sense of believing that all inequalities are unjust. He is an egalitarian in the sense of believing that inequalities must be justified by relevant differences — and that most existing inequalities fail this test.

## Equality in Federal and Imperial Contexts

Mill extends his analysis of equality to two institutional contexts that test its limits: [[federal|federal union]]s and [[colonies|imperial governance]]. In both, the question is whether equality between member units (in a [[federal|federation]]) or between metropolitan and colonial populations (in an empire) is possible or desirable.

In [[federal|federal system]]s, Mill argues that equality among the member states is essential to the stability of the union:

> "A very marked inequality of strength among the several members" of a federation is a source of instability and potential dissolution (017-004)

In [[colonies|imperial governance]], Mill's position is more ambivalent. He defends a principle of eventual equality — the colonies should be admitted "to equal rights" when they have reached the requisite level of development — but accepts that this equality may need to be deferred:

> "This inequality is, of course, as far as it goes, a disadvantage" — but it may be a necessary stage in the development of self-governing capacity (018-004)

The colonial case reveals both the strength and the limitation of Mill's theory of equality. The strength is that equality remains the regulative ideal — the standard against which existing arrangements are judged. The limitation is that equality can be deferred indefinitely on the grounds that the subordinate population is not yet "ready" for it — a paternalism that Mill's own principles should lead him to question more sharply than they do.
