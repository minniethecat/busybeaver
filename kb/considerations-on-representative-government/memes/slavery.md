# Slavery

**Slavery** is the institution of personal bondage in which one human being is the legal property of another. Mill discusses slavery in multiple contexts throughout *Considerations on Representative Government*: as a historical stage of social development, as a metaphor for political [[despotism|despotism]], and as a moral evil overcome through the [[civilization|progress of civilization]].

## Slavery and Moral Progress

Mill's most striking reference to slavery concerns its abolition in the modern world:

> "It is to the force of moral convictions that negro slavery has been put an end to in the British Empire and in America." (001-016)

This passage appears in Mill's discussion of how forms of government depend on the "willingness of the people to accept them" — the abolition of slavery demonstrates that moral opinion can reshape fundamental social institutions, including those with deep economic roots.

## Slavery as a Stage of Development

Mill treats slavery as a historically transitional institution that, however repugnant, served a developmental function in early societies:

> "Hence even personal slavery, by giving a commencement to regular government and a leisure class, has been the means of accelerating the progress of civilization in early ages." (002-028)

This characteristically complex Millian judgment — slavery is morally abhorrent yet historically functional — reflects his broader method of combining moral condemnation with historical analysis. He immediately qualifies this by noting that the amelioration of slavery "is not the normal condition of slavery, and is generally a sign that it is approaching its end" (002-029).

## Despotism as Political Slavery

Mill uses slavery as a metaphor for political subjection. He describes populations "conquered, and reduced to domestic slavery either by a stronger despot" (003-003), drawing a parallel between personal bondage and political oppression. In a passage about the resilience of subject peoples, he observes:

> "After two generations of that mild slavery, they would probably have had to be ruled as subjects for many generations more." (003-006)

This metaphor extends Mill's analysis of despotism: just as chattel slavery degrades the moral character of the enslaved, political despotism renders a people unfit for self-government.

## The Slavery of the Majority

Mill employs the metaphor of slavery to describe a distinctive danger of [[representative-government|representative government]]:

> "The slavery of the majority to the least enlightened section of the community." (007-012)

This occurs when a numerical majority, lacking enlightenment, imposes its will on the more educated minority — or, perversely, when an enlightened minority is politically enslaved by majority ignorance. Mill's solution — proportional representation and weighted voting — aims to prevent this form of political bondage.

## The Legal Status of Slavery

Mill references the American legal doctrine that "slavery to be of common right" (017-006) — the principle, established by the U.S. Supreme Court's Dred Scott decision, that slaveholders could carry their human property into any federal territory. Mill cites this as evidence of how confederations can fail to protect fundamental rights when member states retain excessive autonomy.
