**Monarchy** is, in Mill's typology of government, rule by a single person — distinguished from [[democracy]] (rule by the many) and aristocracy (rule by the few). Mill analyzes monarchy not as a relic to be dismissed but as a [[government|form of government]] with specific strengths and fatal weaknesses that must be understood in order to appreciate the superiority of [[representative-democracy|representative government]].

Mill recognizes that some peoples, at early stages of civilization, "may be made to endure a monarchy" when they are not yet capable of self-government; others "are equally averse to it" from their earliest emergence as political communities (001-008). The suitability of monarchy is a function not of abstract principle but of the "state of civilization" of the people to be governed.

## Absolute Monarchy

Mill's analysis of absolute monarchy is characteristically nuanced — he acknowledges its theoretical advantages while demonstrating its practical failure:

> "If the good of a people could be insured, despotic monarchy would be the best form of government" — because it concentrates all wisdom and power in a single directing intelligence (003-001)

The attractions of absolute monarchy include:
- **Unity of purpose**: A single ruler can pursue long-term plans without the friction of conflicting wills.
- **Speed of decision**: No need for deliberation or compromise — the monarch decides.
- **Expert administration**: "A great minister, in an aristocratic monarchy," can achieve what no democratic assembly can match in efficiency (006-008).

| Claimed Advantage | Reality | Key PN |
|-------------------|---------|--------|
| Unity and consistency | The monarch cannot supervise everything; delegation creates de facto fragmentation | (003-004) |
| Expert administration | Depends entirely on the character of one person — "accidental" | (005-002) |
| Protection of the weak | The monarch has no motive to favor any class — but also no motive to restrain his own appetites | (005-002) |

> "In an unbalanced government — pure monarchy, pure aristocracy, pure democracy — each is the government of a class, and a class which is not the whole people" (012-003)

## The Incapacity of Absolute Monarchy

Mill's definitive refutation of absolute monarchy rests not on moral condemnation but on an analysis of its structural incapacity:

1. **Cognitive overload**: Even the ablest monarch cannot personally supervise all the functions of government. "Unlimited monarchy overcomes obstacles to the progress of civilization only by means of an aristocracy or a bureaucracy" — delegation that recreates the problem of class government (004-008).
2. **Succession problem**: Hereditary monarchy "puts the government in the hands of a person whose qualifications are accidental" — the accident of birth determines who rules, with no guarantee of competence (005-002).
3. **Passivity of the people**: Under an absolute monarchy, the population has no motive to develop the habits of active citizenship. "They, too, had an absolute monarchy and a hierarchy, and their organized intelligence perished" (002-029).

> "Compared with simple monarchy, representative government is the superior form because it brings both the general standard of intelligence in the community and the individual intellect of its wisest members to bear upon the government" (006-006)

## Constitutional Monarchy

Mill distinguishes sharply between absolute and constitutional monarchy. The latter, in which the monarch reigns but does not govern, can be compatible with representative institutions:

> "A minister in a constitutional monarchy is virtually, by the representative system, appointed by Parliament" — the monarch's formal power is exercised by ministers who answer to the representative body (014-009)

In Mill's analysis, constitutional monarchy serves several legitimate functions:
- **Symbolic continuity**: The monarch represents the unity and historical continuity of the nation.
- **Moderating influence**: A monarch above party can mediate disputes and counsel moderation.
- **Preservation of experience**: The monarch accumulates knowledge of affairs over decades, providing institutional memory.

However, the constitutional monarch must not exercise independent political power — that belongs to the [[parliament|representative assembly]] and the ministers it sustains.

## Monarchy and the Progress of Civilization

Mill situates monarchy within a developmental framework. In early stages of society, "even a monarchy of any great territorial extent could not exist" without the communications and administrative technologies of civilization (001-010). In mature societies, monarchy cannot produce "the best government" because:

- It extinguishes the active character of the people, substituting obedience for participation.
- It concentrates knowledge in a narrow circle while [[representative-democracy|representative government]] diffuses it.
- It governs for the "real" interest of the people only by accident — "neither monarchy nor [[aristocracy|oligarchy]] would be such bad governments as they are if their selfish interest were not resisted" (006-015).

The historical trajectory is clear: as peoples advance in civilization, monarchy becomes increasingly inadequate as a form of government, giving way to the representative institutions that alone can harness the intelligence and virtue of the whole community.
