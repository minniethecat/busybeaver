# Mandate

A **mandate** is the authority conferred by electors upon their representatives for the duration of a legislative term. In Mill's analysis of [[representative-government|representative government]], the concept of mandate is central to debates about whether representatives should act as independent [[trustee|trustees]] or as instructed delegates.

## Duration of the Mandate

Mill considers the question of parliamentary terms and the duration of representatives' mandates. Since an individual is "eligible for the duration of the mandate" (011-002), the length of parliamentary terms directly affects the relationship between representatives and [[constituent|constituents]].

Longer mandates promote independence and deliberation; shorter mandates strengthen accountability.

The balance is delicate: a mandate too brief prevents representatives from exercising independent judgment, while one too long severs the connection to [[public-opinion|popular will]].

> "…ll obey unconditionally every mandate of persons in authority. We m…" (002-006)

## Mandate vs. Instruction

The mandate concept intersects with Mill's discussion of whether representatives should follow "instructions of his constituents" (012-001). Mill opposes the view that representatives are "mere delegates" bound by mandates from their electors. A representative should exercise judgment, not merely transmit predetermined votes.

This distinguishes the *electoral mandate* (authorization to serve for a term) from the *imperative mandate* (binding instructions on how to vote). Mill supports the former and rejects the latter.

## Mandates in Federal Systems

In [[federal|federal]] contexts, Mill notes that the federal government may have authority that extends across state boundaries without requiring "mandates of the federal government which are not legalized by their own courts of justice" (017-005).

This highlights the territorial dimension of mandates — the authority of a government is limited by jurisdiction and the [[consent|consent of the governed]].

## Delegate vs. Trustee

The concept of mandate is central to the distinction between two rival theories of representation: the delegate model and the trustee model. Mill addresses this distinction at length in his discussion of whether representatives should follow the instructions of their constituents.

### The Delegate Model in History

Mill observes that in some political traditions, representatives have been treated as mere agents of their electors. Describing the French States-General, he notes:

> …the States-General were mere delegates; and to such a length was the doctrine carried, that when any important question arose which had not been provided for in their instructions, they had to refer back to their constituents, exactly as an ambassador does to the government from which he is accredited. (012-001)

This model — the imperative mandate — treats the representative as a conduit for predetermined votes rather than as an independent political actor. Mill warns that the tendency to convert "their representative into a delegate" is a "natural and not improbable" danger of broad electoral participation (012-002).

### The Danger of the Delegate Principle

Mill identifies a specific risk in electoral systems that treat representatives as delegates. If representatives are understood "as mere delegates, to carry, as it were, the votes of their constituents to the hustings" (009-005), the representative function is reduced to a mechanical transmission of preferences. This undermines the deliberative purpose of a representative assembly.

The problem is compounded when the delegate principle combines with indirect election: "The moment the double stage of election began to have any effect, it would begin to have a bad effect" (009-005). Mill argues that the purpose of election is to select individuals of superior judgment, not merely to aggregate existing preferences.

### The Trustee Alternative

Against the delegate model, Mill advances the [[trustee]] conception: the representative should exercise independent judgment on behalf of constituents. The mandate conferred by election is an authorization to deliberate and decide, not a set of binding instructions.

This distinction is fundamental to Mill's defense of representative government as an institution that improves upon direct democracy. The representative is chosen precisely because of qualities — [[intelligence]], judgment, public spirit — that the aggregate of individual constituents may lack. Reducing the representative to a delegate forfeits these advantages.

> …a representative government needs to be so framed that even if they do, they shall not be able to effect what ought not to be in the power of any body of persons—class legislation. (012-002)

Thus the mandate, properly understood, empowers the representative to act for the common good rather than to serve as a mere transmitter of private or sectional interests.
