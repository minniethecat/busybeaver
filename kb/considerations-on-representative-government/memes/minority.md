The **minority** — those whose opinions are outnumbered in any given constituency — occupies a central place in Mill's critique of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]. The protection and representation of minorities is not merely a matter of fairness but, in Mill's argument, a structural necessity: a democracy that excludes minorities from representation is not truly democratic. "That the minority must yield to the majority, the smaller number to the greater, is a familiar idea; and accordingly men think there is no necessity for giving a voice to the minority" — but this, Mill argues, confuses decision-making with representation (007-003).

## The Minority Problem

In a first-past-the-post [[election|electoral system]], a minority dispersed across multiple constituencies may obtain no representation at all, even if it constitutes a substantial fraction of the national population:

| Problem | Consequence | Key PN |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| Majority monopoly | A single class that "composes the numerical majority" can dominate all representation | (007-002) |
| Invisible minorities | Opinions concentrated in particular places gain representation; dispersed minorities do not | (007-005) |
| Class domination | "A completely equal democracy, in a nation in which a single class composes the numerical majority, can not be divested of certain evils" (007-002) |

> "The injustice and violation of principle are not less flagrant because those who suffer by them are a minority, for there is not equal suffrage where every single individual does not count for as much as any other single individual in the community" (007-004)

## Hare's Scheme and Minority Representation

Mill finds the solution in [[thomas-hare|Thomas Hare]]'s system of [[representation|proportional representation]] through transferable voting:

- "It secures a representation, in proportion to numbers, of every division of the electoral body: not two great parties alone, but **every minority in the whole nation**, consisting of a sufficiently large number to be, on principles of equal justice, entitled to a representative" (007-010).
- The quota mechanism ensures that "any section of electors" who can muster a sufficient number of votes — regardless of where they live — gains representation (007-008).
- Minorities would "have a chance of obtaining by weight of character and strength of argument an influence which would not belong to their numerical force" (008-001).

## The Function of Antagonism

Mill goes beyond fairness to argue that minority representation serves a vital systemic function:

> "This may be called the function of Antagonism. In every government there is some power stronger than all the rest; and the readiest mode of obtaining the benefits, without the dangers, of this superiority, is to provide an antagonistic power" (007-014)

An unrepresented minority cannot perform this checking function. By ensuring that minority voices are present in the [[parliament|legislature]], the system gains internal resistance against corruption and complacency.

## Majority Tyranny and Its Remedies

Mill connects the minority question to his broader theory of [[democracy]]. Without minority representation, [[democracy|democratic government]] degenerates into "collective mediocrity" — a system where the average opinion of the majority class suppresses all independent thought (007-013).

The historical attempts to mitigate the minority problem before [[thomas-hare|Hare]] included:
- [[reform-bill-1832|Lord John Russell's]] provision that certain constituencies should return three members, with each elector voting for only two (007-007).
- The cumulative vote and limited vote systems — partial measures Mill considered inadequate compared to [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s comprehensive scheme.
