The **American Constitution** figures prominently in John Stuart Mill's *Considerations on Representative Government* as the principal example of a codified republican constitution. Mill draws on American experience to illuminate questions of executive selection, the role of a federal judiciary, and the design of bicameral legislatures — often contrasting American arrangements with those of the [[british-constitution|British Constitution]].

## The Election of the President

Mill devotes sustained attention to the American method of electing the chief executive. He acknowledges the advantage of making "the chief minister constitutionally independent of the legislative body," which renders the executive and legislative branches "an effective check on one another." (014-009) This arrangement accords with "that sedulous avoidance of the concentration of great masses of power in the same hands, which is a marked characteristic of the American federal Constitution." (014-009)

However, Mill judges that "the advantage, in this instance, is purchased at a price above all reasonable estimates of its value." (014-009) He identifies two principal defects. First, a president appointed by the representative body is "certain... to be a more eminent man" — the party leader is "always one of the foremost, and often the very foremost person in political life" — whereas American presidents since the founders have been "almost always either an obscure man, or one who has gained any reputation he may possess in some other field than politics." (014-009) Second, the fixed electoral cycle produces "the great mischief of unintermitted electioneering," in which "the whole community is kept intent on the mere personalities of politics, and every public question is discussed and decided with less reference to its merits than to its expected bearing on the presidential election." (014-009)

## The Supreme Court as Constitutional Umpire

In his treatment of [[federal]] government, Mill identifies the American Supreme Court as the institutional solution to the problem of divided sovereignty. Under a federation, citizens owe obedience to two governments — state and federal — and "it is evidently necessary not only that the constitutional limits of the authority of each should be precisely and clearly defined, but that the power to decide between them in any case of dispute should not reside in either of the governments." (017-006) The Supreme Court serves as "an umpire independent of both," with the authority to declare that any law "exceeds the powers assigned to them by the federal Constitution, and, in consequence, has no legal validity." (017-006)

Mill notes that this was an unprecedented arrangement: "It was natural to feel strong doubts, before trial had been made, how such a provision would work." (017-006) The discussions on the American Constitution before its adoption "give evidence that these natural apprehensions were strongly felt; but they are now entirely quieted." (017-006) He attributes the success of judicial review partly to the incremental, case-by-case nature of common-law adjudication — the Court "does not declare the law *eo nomine* and in the abstract, but waits until a case between man and man is brought before it judicially." (017-006)

Mill concludes with a warning: the confidence on which federal stability depends "has been for the first time impaired by the judgment declaring slavery to be of common right," observing that "the main pillar of the American Constitution is scarcely strong enough to bear many more such shocks." (017-006) This was written before the American Civil War, and reflects Mill's concern with the *Dred Scott* decision.

## The Structure of Congress

Mill commends the American solution to the problem of representing both population and states in a federal legislature. The provision that Congress should consist of two houses — "while one of them is constituted according to population... the other should represent not the citizens, but the state governments" — is described as "exceedingly judicious." (017-009) This arrangement "precludes any undue power from being exercised by the more powerful states over the rest, and guarantees the reserved rights of the state governments." (017-009)

Mill further notes an "incidental advantage" of the [[senate|Senate]]'s indirect election by state legislatures: it raises "the standard of qualifications in one of the houses." (017-009) Because state legislatures have "a strong motive" to select eminent men — "the influence of their state in the general deliberations must be materially affected by the personal weight and abilities of its representatives" — the Senate "has always contained nearly all the political men of established and high reputation in the Union." (017-009) Meanwhile, the House of Representatives has "been generally as remarkable for the absence of conspicuous personal merit, as the Upper House for its presence." (017-009)

## The Example of Federal Union

The American Constitution serves Mill as evidence for the broader argument that federal unions are "always a benefit to the world." (017-010) By providing a mechanism for resolving disputes between states through law rather than war, and by creating a common government for external relations while preserving local autonomy, the American model demonstrates how "the weak, by uniting, can meet on equal terms with the strong." (017-010)
