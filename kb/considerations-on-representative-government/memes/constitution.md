A [[constitution]], in Mill's analysis, is not merely a written document but the entire framework through which political power is organized, exercised, and restrained. The excellence of a constitution is measured by how well it promotes "the virtue and intelligence of the people themselves" — the fundamental criterion Mill applies throughout *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]* (003-009).

## Theory of Constitution

Mill approaches constitutional design from two complementary perspectives:

| Principle | Application | Key PN |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Machinery vs. Organism | Government is not a machine that acts independently — it requires an active, willing populace | (001-006) |
| Adaptation to Conditions | A constitution must suit the "existing stage of civilization" of the people | (001-009) |
| Protective vs. Educative | Constitutions must both protect interests and cultivate public virtue | (003-009) |

> "The excellence of a political constitution is divided between the goodness of the machinery and the virtue and intelligence of the human material" (003-009)

Mill rejects the notion that a constitution can be arbitrarily imposed. A form of government "will not be practically taken into the minds and habits of the people" unless it aligns with their existing capacities and inclinations (001-006).

## The British Constitution

The [[parliament|British Constitution]] provides Mill's primary case study. He describes it as a system of compromises, where:

- The [[house-of-commons|House of Commons]] holds the predominant democratic power (013-005).
- The [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] serves (inadequately, in Mill's view) as a [[second-chamber|second chamber]] (013-004).
- The Crown retains formal powers including a veto over legislation and the appointment of ministers (005-003).
- Practice and convention (the "uniform practice of the Constitution") constrain formal powers (005-007).

> "In the English Constitution there is great difficulty in making any two powers co-ordinate with one another" (005-014)

Mill is critical of the [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]] as a second chamber, arguing that it represents only a single interest (the landed aristocracy) rather than the diversity of perspectives a true revising chamber requires (013-004).

## Comparative Constitutional Analysis

Mill draws on [[constitution|constitutional]] experiences beyond Britain:

| Country | Constitutional Feature | Mill's Assessment |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------|
| United States | Fixed-term President, Federal structure, Judicial review | Praises the Supreme Court as a model; notes federal division of powers as judicious (017-009) |
| Switzerland (pre-1847) | [[federal|Confederation]] model | Describes it as a failed experiment due to weak central authority (017-005) |
| [[east-india-company|East India Company]] | Bureaucratic governance under parliamentary oversight | Notes its evolution as an example of adapting institutions (018-019) |

The [[constitution|American Constitution]]'s provision for judicial review — a "provision of their revised constitutions" — receives particular attention from Mill as a mechanism for resolving constitutional disputes without legislative supremacy (014-013), (017-009).

## Second Chambers and Constitutional Safeguards

A properly designed [[constitution]] must include safeguards against both popular impulsiveness and elite entrenchment. Mill analyzes the [[second-chamber|second chamber]] problem at length (013-004)–(013-009):

- A second chamber should not duplicate the first but provide complementary expertise and perspective.
- Mill warns against any second chamber that could obstruct "any important improvement" or serve merely as a "screen" for oligarchic interests (014-003).
- As [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]] observed, "boards are screens" — collegial bodies often obscure rather than enable accountability (014-003).

> "An easy adaptation of Mr. Hare's plan would prevent the representation of mere local majorities from becoming preponderant in Parliament" (013-009)
