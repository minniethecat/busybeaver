**Intelligence**, in Mill's political vocabulary, refers to the **capacity for reasoned judgment on public questions** — not merely cognitive ability in the abstract, but the specific combination of knowledge, judgment, and public-spiritedness that makes citizens capable of self-government. Alongside virtue, intelligence is one of the two essential "elements" that determine the quality of any [[polity|political community]]:

> "We are nearer the truth, though still not sufficiently near it, when we say that the goodness of a government depends on the degree in which it tends to increase the sum of good qualities in the governed, collectively and individually; including, among those qualities, intelligence as well as virtue" (001-015)

Mill's treatment of intelligence is distinctive because he sees it not as a fixed attribute of individuals but as a **quality of institutions**. Good institutions call intelligence forth; bad institutions suppress it. The design of government is therefore, in large part, a problem of designing for intelligence — creating structures that select for and cultivate the capacity for reasoned public judgment.

## Intelligence as an Element of Government

Mill opens the *Considerations* by situating intelligence alongside other "elements" that determine governmental quality. His framework is systematic:

> "The influence of defects of intelligence in obstructing all the elements of good government" is a central theme of the work, and the "virtue and intelligence of the human beings composing the community" are the ultimate determinants of political outcomes (002-019) (002-020)

This is not an argument for rule by the intelligent — Mill is too much a democrat for that — but an argument that the **average** level of intelligence in the community sets a ceiling on what government can achieve. The "highest average of virtue and intelligence" is the proper goal of [[political-institution|institutional design]] (002-021).

The relationship between intelligence and institutions is reciprocal:

1. **Institutions select for intelligence**: A well-designed [[representation|representative system]] draws the most capable individuals into public life.
2. **Institutions cultivate intelligence**: [[participation|Participation]] in public affairs educates citizens, "calling forth some amount of intelligence and practical ability" (003-002).
3. **Intelligence sustains institutions**: Without a certain level of public intelligence, representative institutions cannot function — they become vehicles for [[sinister-interest|class interest]] or demagoguery.

## The Danger of Low Intelligence in the Representative Body

One of Mill's central anxieties about democracy is that it may produce a representative body of "a low grade of intelligence" (007-001). This is not an aristocratic prejudice but a structural analysis: if representatives are chosen by voters of average capacity, and if those voters select candidates who share their limitations rather than those who surpass them, the assembly will reflect and amplify the intellectual defects of the electorate:

> "The representative body must be so constituted as to secure an adequate amount of intelligence and knowledge in the representative body" (006-011)

The remedy Mill proposes is not to restrict the [[suffrage|franchise]] — he explicitly rejects [[property|property qualification]]s and educational tests for the [[franchise|right to vote]] — but to design electoral mechanisms that give **additional weight** to intelligence without excluding anyone. This is the function of [[plurality|plural voting]], open [[ballot|public voting]], and other devices that tilt the electoral field toward the capable without disenfranchising the less capable.

## Intelligence, Class, and the Suppression of Capacity

Mill identifies a structural connection between class government and the suppression of intelligence. Governments that serve a narrow [[sinister-interest|class interest]] have a positive incentive to keep the population at "a low level of intelligence and education":

> "Their interest is to keep them at a low level of intelligence and education, foment dissensions among them, and even impoverish them" (006-013)

This is a striking claim: the ruling class in a monarchy or aristocracy has a **material interest** in the ignorance of the governed. An educated population would recognize that its interests diverge from those of the ruling class and would demand political power. Intelligence is therefore subversive of class government — which is precisely why class government suppresses it.

The contrast with [[representative-government|representative government]] is clear. A system in which "the intelligence and sentiments of the whole people" are brought to bear on public questions has no interest in suppressing intelligence — on the contrary, its proper functioning depends on cultivating it (003-003). Democratic institutions and public intelligence are mutually reinforcing in a way that class government and intelligence can never be.

| System | Effect on Intelligence | Mechanism |
|--------|----------------------|-----------|
| [[despotism|Despotism]] | Suppresses | "Deadening the intelligence and activity of the people" (002-024) |
| [[aristocracy|Aristocracy]] | Suppresses | Ruling class has an interest in popular ignorance (006-013) |
| [[bureaucracy|Bureaucracy]] | Channels narrowly | Cultivates "trained and experienced intelligence" in officials but not citizens (005-015) |
| Representative government | Cultivates broadly | "[[education|Education]] of the intelligence and of the sentiments" through participation (008-002) |

## Educating Intelligence through Participation

Mill's most original insight about intelligence is that it is **learned through exercise**. The intelligence required for self-government is not a prerequisite that must be met before democratic institutions can be established; it is a capacity that those institutions themselves cultivate:

> "That education of the intelligence and of the sentiments which is carried down to the very lowest ranks of the people when they are called to take a part in acts which directly affect the great interests of their country" (008-002)

This is the educational function of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]. The citizen who participates in [[election|elections]], serves on [[jury|juries]], and engages in [[municipal|local government]] is exercising and developing the very capacities — judgment, deliberation, consideration of the public interest — that make him a better citizen. Intelligence is not a fixed stock that the community possesses in greater or lesser measure; it is a **practice** that institutions either encourage or discourage.

> "The social and political intelligence of the population" is not merely a datum for the institutional designer but a variable that institutions themselves influence (004-008)

The implication is profound: the argument that a people is "not ready" for [[representative-democracy|representative government]] may be true at a given moment, but it cannot be a permanent objection, because the only way a people becomes ready is through the exercise of representative institutions. The circle is broken not by waiting but by beginning — with institutions appropriate to the present level of capacity, designed to cultivate the capacity for more.
