**Property**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, appears in two distinct political roles: as a **qualification for the [[suffrage|franchise]]** — the traditional basis on which the [[franchise|right to vote]] was granted or withheld — and as an **interest requiring protection** — one of the fundamental objects for which government exists. Mill's treatment of property is characteristically nuanced: he rejects property as a sufficient basis for [[rights|political rights]] while acknowledging that property ownership is relevant to political competence and that the security of property is among the primary purposes of government.

Mill's position on property qualifications places him between the radical reformers who would abolish all property tests and the conservatives who regarded property as the sole legitimate basis for [[participation|political participation]]. Property matters, but it matters as one consideration among several — not as the exclusive or even primary criterion for the [[franchise]] (008-010).

## Property as a Basis of Government

Mill opens the *Considerations* with a discussion of the elements of political power, and property is one of the first he names:

> "Add two other elements, property and intelligence, and we are nearer the truth, but still far from it" (001-015)

| Element of Political Power | Mill's Assessment |
|----------------------------|-------------------|
| Numbers | The most visible but not necessarily the most weighty |
| Property | A real source of power, but not coextensive with political wisdom |
| Intelligence | The most important element, and the one least correlated with the others |

Mill's point is that property, numbers, and intelligence are three distinct bases of political influence, and any sound political system must account for all three. A system based purely on numbers (democracy) neglects property and intelligence; a system based purely on property ([[aristocracy|oligarchy]]) neglects numbers and intelligence. The task of [[political-institution|institutional design]] is to give each element its due weight without allowing any one to dominate.

> "The laws of property... the principles of civil law — these are things which every government must either itself do, or see that they are done" (002-025)

## Property and the Security of Person

Before property can be a qualification for [[rights|political rights]], it must be secured as a basic object of government. Mill follows the classical liberal tradition in treating the security of person and property as the most elementary function of the state:

> "To feel his person and property secure — can any state of things be more primitive?" (002-011)

| Function of Government | Relationship to Property |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Security of person | The most basic function — without it, nothing else is possible |
| Security of property | The second basic function — "can any state of things be more primitive?" (002-011) |
| Administration of justice | Adjudicating disputes over property — "equal justice between individuals" (015-011) |
| [[representation|Representation]] | Ensuring that property interests are heard in the making of laws that affect them |

> "Security of person and property, and equal justice between individuals — these are the primary objects of government" (015-011)

The sequence is important. Security of person comes first, then security of property, then equal justice. Property is not an end in itself but a condition of the independence and security that make citizenship possible. A person whose property is insecure cannot participate freely in public life; a government that cannot secure property has failed in its most basic duty.

## Property as a Qualification for the Franchise

Mill's most sustained discussion of property concerns its role as a qualification for the [[suffrage|franchise]]. The traditional British system restricted the vote to property-holders, and Mill must decide where he stands on this deeply contested question:

> "I do not deny that property is a kind of test; but it is a very imperfect one" (008-010)

| Claim for Property Qualification | Mill's Response |
|----------------------------------|-----------------|
| Property indicates a stake in society | Partially true — but "a man's wife and daughters are not his property" (010-011) |
| Property indicates competence | "A very imperfect test" — many property-holders are ignorant, many propertyless are informed (008-010) |
| Property prevents [[class-interest|class legislation]] by the poor | A real concern — but better addressed by [[minority|minority representation]] than by property tests |
| Taxation without representation is tyranny | This argument cuts *against* restricting the [[suffrage|franchise]] to property-holders — those who pay taxes should have the vote (015-004) |

> "Are not a man's wife and daughters his property?" — No, and therefore the property test fails even on its own terms (010-011)

This is a characteristic Millian move: he takes the property argument on its own terms and shows that it proves too much. If the [[suffrage|franchise]] is based on property, then a man's dependents — who are "property" in the traditional legal sense — would have no claim to the vote. But if we reject that implication (as Mill does), we must also reject the premise that property is the sole or primary basis for [[rights|political rights]].

## The Dangers of Property-Based Government

Mill identifies a specific danger in property-based political systems: the tendency of the propertied class to use government to protect its own interests at the expense of the community:

> "Under the name of realized property, allowing those whose progenitors have been rich to take the lion's share of the public burdens" (006-018)

| Danger | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Class legislation | The propertied use their political power to shift tax burdens onto the unpropertied (006-018) |
| Perpetuation of inequality | Property qualifications lock in existing distributions of wealth and power |
| Weakening of security | Paradoxically, property-based government may "weaken the security of property" by making it a source of political resentment (006-014) |
| Exclusion of talent | Those without property but with intelligence are excluded from [[participation|political participation]] |

> "The apprehension that equality of earnings might be imposed" — this is the fear that motivates property-based restrictions on the franchise (006-014)

Mill takes this fear seriously — it is not irrational to worry that a propertyless majority might use the [[suffrage|franchise]] to redistribute property. But his response is not to restrict the [[suffrage|franchise]] but to design institutions that prevent any single class from dominating. [[minority|Minority]] representation, proportional voting, and educational qualifications are Mill's preferred instruments — not the blunt instrument of a property test.

## Property and Independence

Mill's most sympathetic argument for a property qualification concerns not property itself but what property enables: independence of judgment. The person who depends on others for his livelihood cannot be a free political agent:

> "Either derived from property or from a trade or profession" — the voter must have "independent means" of some kind (010-018)

This is not a property qualification in the traditional sense. Mill is not requiring a specific amount of property but a form of economic independence — whether from property, a trade, or a profession. The point is that the voter must not be in a condition of personal dependency that would make his vote the property of another:

- **Property** provides independence through unearned income.
- **Trade** provides independence through self-employment.
- **Profession** provides independence through specialised skill.

Any of these is sufficient, in Mill's view, to establish the independence that the [[suffrage|franchise]] presupposes. What Mill rejects is not property as a basis for independence but property *alone* as a basis for the [[suffrage|franchise]], to the exclusion of other forms of independence.
