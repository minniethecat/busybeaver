# Delegate

A **delegate** is a representative who acts strictly according to the instructions of [[constituent|constituents]], serving as their agent rather than exercising independent judgment. Mill rejects the delegate theory of representation, arguing that it undermines the deliberative function of [[parliament|representative assemblies]].

## The Delegate Theory

The delegate theory holds that representatives are "mere delegates" (009-005) — instruments for transmitting the predetermined will of their electors. Under this view, the representative's role is to vote as instructed, not to deliberate or exercise independent judgment. Mill traces this conception historically, noting that "the States-General were mere delegates" (012-001) in the French constitutional tradition.

The logical conclusion of the delegate theory is that "the representative would be reduced to a delegate" (012-002), stripped of the capacity to weigh arguments, consider new evidence, or change his mind through parliamentary debate.

## Mill's Critique

Mill argues forcefully against the delegate model. Representatives who are "elected under an express pledge, as mere delegates, to carry, as it were, the votes of their constituents" (009-005) cannot fulfill the deliberative function that justifies representative government. A body of instructed delegates is not a legislature — it is a registration machine for predetermined preferences.

The delegate theory further damages the quality of representation by discouraging able individuals from seeking office. Those capable of independent thought will not subject themselves to a role that forbids them from exercising it.

## Delegate vs. Trustee

Mill's preferred model — the [[trustee|trustee]] or independent representative — stands in direct opposition to the delegate theory. While the delegate is "selected solely as a delegate to give a particular vote" (009-004), the trustee is chosen for judgment and character. The trustee may disagree with constituents on particular measures while serving their true interests.

This distinction is fundamental to Mill's defense of [[representative-government|representative government]] as a system that elevates [[deliberation|deliberation]] above mere aggregation of preferences.

## Colonial Delegation

Mill also uses "delegate" in the administrative sense, discussing governance "through a delegated body" (018-018) in colonial contexts. Here the term refers not to the theory of representation but to the practical mechanism of entrusting governance to subordinate officials who act on behalf of the imperial power.
