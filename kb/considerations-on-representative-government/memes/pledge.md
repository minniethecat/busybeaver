# Pledge

A **pledge** is an electoral commitment made by a candidate to support specific policies or vote in particular ways if elected. Mill devotes sustained attention to the question of pledges in Chapter XII of *Considerations on Representative Government*, where he examines whether electors may rightfully bind their representatives through such commitments.

## The Question of Pledges

Mill frames the issue carefully: "the question of pledges is not one of those which vitally concern the excellence of a representative system" (012-003). While pledges are not the most fundamental issue of representative government, they bear directly on the independence and quality of representatives.

The core problem is that requiring pledges reduces the representative from an independent trustee to a mere agent. If electors demand that candidates "pledge himself to all their opinions" (012-002), the representative body becomes a collection of instructed [[delegate|delegates]] rather than a deliberative assembly.

## Legitimate and Illegitimate Pledges

Mill distinguishes between different types of electoral commitments:

1. **Legitimate**: A voter may seek from a candidate "a pledge to those measures which he considers essential" (012-005) as conditions of support, but these should be limited to fundamental principles.
2. **Illegitimate**: Requiring a representative to vote according to constituent instructions on every question destroys [[deliberation|deliberation]] and independent judgment.

Mill advises that "actual pledges should not be required unless" (012-009) on matters of fundamental constitutional principle. On ordinary [[legislation|legislation]], representatives should retain freedom of judgment.

## Enforcement and Accountability

A practical question concerns what happens when a pledge is broken. If a representative's promises "were proved to be false or the pledge to have been broken, he should be liable to be deprived of his seat" (010-016). Mill supports mechanisms for accountability — the elector must have recourse when a representative violates fundamental commitments, but this should not extend to every vote or decision.

## Pledges and [[senate|the Senate]]

Mill notes historical examples where political figures "pledged to the conduct of public affairs" (006-007) through institutional roles like the Senate, contrasting such broad commitments to the public good with narrow electoral pledges to specific interest groups.

> "… the Senate, their lives were pledged to the conduct of public aff…" (006-007)
