**Jeremy Bentham** (1748–1832) was the founder of modern [[utilitarianism]] and one of the most influential philosophical voices in John Stuart Mill's intellectual formation. In *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, Mill invokes Bentham's conceptual apparatus — particularly the analysis of "[[sinister-interest|sinister interests]]" and the critique of institutional screens — to construct his theory of [[representative-government|representative government]].

## Bentham's Conceptual Legacy

Mill draws on several of Bentham's key analytical tools:

| Bentham Concept | Mill's Application | Key PN |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------|
| [[sinister-interest|Sinister Interest]]s | Interests conflicting with the general good, to which any ruling group is susceptible | (006-012) |
| Boards as Screens | Collegial bodies obscure individual accountability — "boards are screens" | (014-003) |
| Complete Classification | The intellectual, moral, and practical qualities needed in representatives | (002-020) |

> "It is happily said by Bentham, 'boards are screens.' What 'the Board' does is to throw over each individual member the shelter of collective responsibility" (014-003)

The concept of "[[sinister-interest|sinister interests]]" — a Benthamite phrase Mill explicitly acknowledges — is central to Mill's argument that any governing class will tend to legislate in its own interest unless checked by countervailing powers (006-012).

## Bentham's Influence on Considerations

Mill's debt to Bentham in *Considerations* manifests in three domains:

1. **Classification of human qualities**: Mill follows "Bentham's more complete classification" when enumerating the intellectual, moral, and practical attributes required of representatives — though Mill adapts rather than adopts Bentham's schema (002-020).

2. **Interest analysis**: The framework of analyzing [[political-institution|political institutions]] through the lens of interests — who benefits, who is checked, what incentives operate — is fundamentally Benthamite. Mill applies it to [[parliament|Parliament]], the [[constitution|Constitution]], and the [[election|electoral system]].

3. **Institutional skepticism**: Bentham's critical stance toward traditional institutions — that they must justify themselves by their utility, not their antiquity — underlies Mill's willingness to propose radical reforms like [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s proportional representation.

> "The useful phrase introduced by Bentham — sinister interests — that is, interests conflicting with the general interest" (006-012)

## Bentham and Constitutional Design

Mill extends Bentham's insight about "screens" to constitutional analysis. The [[house-of-lords|House of Lords]], the [[second-chamber|second chamber]], and even Cabinet government are evaluated by whether they enable or obscure accountability:

- A second chamber composed of a single class "would be merely a screen behind which the dominant interest could operate without responsibility" (014-003).
- Mill joins Bentham and others in considering whether "submitting judicial appointments to [[election|popular election]]" would improve accountability, though he remains cautious (014-013).

## Relationship to Mill

Mill was raised in the Benthamite tradition — his father [[james-mill|James Mill]] was Bentham's close collaborator — but *Considerations* reflects a mature, critical engagement rather than simple discipleship:

- Mill accepts Bentham's analytical method while rejecting its reductive tendencies.
- He values Bentham's institutional critique but supplements it with [[tocqueville|Tocqueville]]'s concern for [[education|civic education]] and participation.
- Mill's emphasis on the "educative" function of [[political-institution|political institutions]] goes beyond Bentham's more mechanical model of interest-balancing.

> "The opinion of Bentham, of Mr. Samuel Bailey, of Mr. Hare, and many other of the most powerful intellects of the age, has been strongly expressed in favor of securities for minority representation" (008-018)
