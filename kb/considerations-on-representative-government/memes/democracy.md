**Democracy** is for Mill the necessary foundation of good government, but it is not sufficient in itself. "Pure democracy would be the only [[government|form of government]] that could be seriously defended" if all citizens possessed equal capacity — but the real question is what kind of democracy best secures competent rule (001-015). Mill distinguishes sharply between a democracy of mere numbers, which threatens to degenerate into [[class-interest|class legislation]], and a "skilled democracy" that incorporates the judgment of the educated and the representation of minorities (006-010).

Mill's treatment of democracy runs throughout the *Considerations*. "Looking at democracy in the way in which it is commonly conceived, as the rule of the numerical majority," he observes, "it is quite conceivable that the ruling power may be under the dominion of sectional or [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s" (006-014). His project is to reconstruct democracy so that it governs for the general interest rather than the interest of the largest class.

## Varieties of Democracy

Mill recognizes multiple forms of democratic government, each with distinct characteristics:

| Form | Defining Feature | Primary Defect |
|------|-----------------|----------------|
| Pure democracy | Direct rule by all citizens | Impractical at scale; susceptible to ignorance |
| Equal democracy | Every individual counts equally | Majorities crush minorities entirely |
| Representative democracy | Rule through elected deputies | Danger of low-caliber representatives |
| Skilled democracy | Weighted voting by capacity | Difficult to institute; resented as aristocratic |

> "A completely equal democracy, in a nation in which a single class composes the numerical majority, enables that class to monopolize all power" (007-002)

## Dangers of Democracy

Mill identifies two classes of danger in representative democracy, which he elaborates in Chapter VI:

**Danger of a low grade of intelligence in the representative body.** When [[election|elections]] are dominated by local interests and wealth, "the only persons who can get elected are those who possess local influence, or make their way by lavish expenditure" — excluding those of "talents and character" (007-011).

**Danger of [[class-interest|class legislation]] by the numerical majority.** "If democracy means the certain ascendancy of the majority," Mill warns, "there is no security for the minority" (007-004). The "false democracy which, instead of giving representation to all, gives it only to the local majorities" is "diametrically opposed to the first principle of democracy — representation in proportion to numbers" (007-006).

> "In the false democracy which, instead of giving representation to all, gives it only to the local majorities... the minority has no organ" (007-015)

## Democracy versus Bureaucracy

Mill frames the essential choice in government as "between a [[representation|representative]] democracy and a bureaucracy; all other forms are either intermediate or disguised variants of one of these" (006-009).

[[bureaucracy|Bureaucracy]], he concedes, secures a high minimum of administrative competence — but at the cost of extinguishing the active character of the people. "A bureaucracy," he argues, "always tends to become a pedantocracy" — it governs by routine and precedent, suppressing innovation and public spirit.

Democracy's advantage is its educative effect on the population:
- [[participation|Participation]] in [[voting]] and public affairs exercises the moral and intellectual faculties.
- The [[public-opinion|public opinion]] that democracy requires fosters discussion and the formation of independent judgment.
- Democratic institutions, by distributing responsibility widely, cultivate "active" rather than "passive" character types.

Mill's synthesis is that "the superiority of [[representative-democracy|representative government]] is that it brings the general standard of intelligence and honesty existing in the community, and the individual intellect and virtue of its wisest members, more directly to bear upon the government" (006-009).

## Achieving a Skilled Democracy

Mill's remedy for the defects of democracy is not less democracy but better structured democracy:

1. **Proportional representation** through [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme, which gives minorities a voice in proportion to their numbers (007-012).
2. **Plural voting** — granting additional [[voting|votes]] to those whose education or occupation indicates superior judgment — as a transitional measure until universal education makes it unnecessary (008-012).
3. **Open [[voting|ballot]]** in public elections (though Mill later modified this view), ensuring that the [[suffrage|franchise]] is exercised on public grounds (004-004).

> "I believe that the English democracy would as yet be content with a much smaller share of power than it at present enjoys" — the reformer's task is to make democracy competent, not merely popular (007-016)
