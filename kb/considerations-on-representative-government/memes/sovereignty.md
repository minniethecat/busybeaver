## Definition and Core Principle

Sovereignty, in Mill's usage, is "the supreme controlling power in the last resort" — the ultimate authority from which all governmental powers derive and to which they are ultimately accountable. In the ideally best form of government, this sovereignty is vested "in the entire aggregate of the community, every citizen not only having a voice in the exercise of that ultimate sovereignty, but being, at least occasionally, called on to take an actual part in the government by the personal discharge of some public function, local or general" (003-007).

This formulation links sovereignty directly to [[participation]]: sovereignty is not merely a legal abstraction but a practical capacity exercised through citizenship. The ideal of popular sovereignty requires not only the right to vote but the opportunity to serve in public functions.

## Sovereignty in Representative Government

In a representative system, the people exercise sovereignty indirectly — "through deputies periodically elected by themselves" — but the ultimate controlling power must reside somewhere in its completeness. Mill insists that the people "must be masters, whenever they please, of all the operations of government" (005-002). The constitutional law need not itself grant this mastery explicitly; what matters is the practical reality of where final control lies.

Mill applies this analysis to the British Constitution, where the Crown, the House of Lords, and the House of Commons are nominally co-ordinate members of the sovereignty, each invested with powers that, if fully exercised, could stop the machinery of government. Yet he observes that the scales never hang exactly even:

> There is almost always a balance, but the scales never hang exactly even. Which of them preponderates is not always apparent on the face of the political institutions.
>
> (005-002)

The reality of sovereign power is determined not by constitutional text but by "the unwritten maxims of the Constitution — in other words, the positive political morality of the country" (005-002). By this standard, the Crown's formal powers to refuse assent to legislation or to appoint ministers against Parliament's will are "nullified" by constitutional morality, making the House of Commons "the real sovereign of the state" (005-003).

## Sovereignty and Federalism

Mill's discussion of the United States Senate introduces a distinct conception of sovereignty in federal systems. The Senate represents the states as guardians of "that portion of their sovereign rights which they have not alienated" to the federal government. In an equal federation, "the internal sovereignty of each state is, by the nature of an equal federation, equally sacred whatever be the size or importance of the state" — hence each state returns two senators regardless of population (009-004).

This federal model divides sovereignty between levels of government rather than concentrating it in a single locus. Mill regards the resulting indirect election of senators (by state legislatures, in his time) as having "proved eminently successful," producing a body "invariably consisting of the most distinguished men" in American public life (009-004). The success of this arrangement, however, depends on conditions "hardly to be obtained in practice except in a federal government" (009-004).

## The Balanced Constitution Problem

Mill engages with the classical argument, "revived by great authorities in our own time, that a balanced constitution is impossible" (005-002). While a constitution may distribute formal powers among co-ordinate branches, practical sovereignty will always settle somewhere. The question is not whether there is a balance but which element preponderates — and the answer lies less in constitutional design than in the political culture that determines how powers are actually exercised.
