**Public opinion** is, in Mill's theory, the ultimate sovereign in a [[representation|representative]] government — the force that both constrains rulers and shapes the laws they enact. It is not merely the aggregate of individual sentiments but a "virtuous and enlightened" judgment that can only form through free discussion and publicity. As Mill writes, "the worth of the public opinion which influences or controls" public functionaries determines the worth of every institution dependent on it (002-021).

Mill treats public opinion as both the essential safeguard of [[democracy]] and a potential source of mediocrity when it is uninformed. "The formation and propagation of a public opinion" requires a class of independent thinkers — "except among those who could make their opinions known without the organs of the press, the press could not have existed" — but once formed, it becomes the atmosphere in which all political action breathes (001-010).

## Formation through Publicity and Discussion

For Mill, public opinion does not arise spontaneously; it must be deliberately cultivated through institutions that expose government actions to scrutiny.

> "Discussion, criticism, and publicity are necessary to the formation of a public opinion, the electors will seldom make any use of the right of suffrage but to serve their private interest" (004-004)

Without the conditions of open debate, the [[suffrage|electoral franchise]] is wasted — voters cannot form judgments on public grounds and retreat to private or local interests. The [[parliament|representative assembly]] itself serves as the nation's committee of grievance and congress of opinions, where "every person in the country may count upon finding somebody who speaks his mind" and where "rival opinions are measured against each other" in open contest (004-004).

The relationship between publicity and public opinion is reciprocal:
- Publicity of government proceedings enables opinion to form on an informed basis.
- Public opinion, once formed, demands further publicity, creating a virtuous cycle.
- Without this cycle, "constitutional control by public opinion" cannot function, and representative institutions collapse into [[aristocracy|oligarchy]] (004-009).

## The Educative Function of Public Opinion

Mill attributes to public opinion an educative power that extends beyond the political sphere. To suppose that "public opinion does good only in so far as it compels a government to bend to it" is to take a "superficial view" (010-013).

The value of public opinion lies in elevating the character of those who participate in it:

| Function | Mechanism | Key PN |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Intellectual elevation | [[participation|Participation]] in discussion develops faculties of judgment | (010-013) |
| Moral discipline | Considering public interests checks selfish impulses | (002-019) |
| National cohesion | A shared opinion creates "united public opinion necessary to the working of [[representative-democracy|representative government]]" | (016-002) |
| Administrative control | Opinion "under whose eyes they act" gives [[public-office|public servant]]s "the feeling of being accountable" | (015-013) |

> "It is not too much to say that among a population almost uneducated, public opinion is already, as every one now knows, the real sovereign" (010-015)

## Dangers of a False Public Opinion

Mill is acutely aware that public opinion can be manufactured rather than genuine. He warns of the "factitious public opinion got up in [[parliament|Parliament]]" — a manufactured sentiment orchestrated by a "compact majority" to serve oligarchic ends (018-018).

The dangers of false public opinion include:
- **Oligarchic capture**: When the "compact majority" fabricates opinion through [[parliament|Parliament]], it reduces the representative body to an instrument of class domination (018-018).
- **Incompetent judgment**: "Irregular appeal before a public opinion wholly incompetent" leads to arbitrary governance, "for want of having secured the decisions of an competent authority" (014-013).
- **Suppression of minorities**: The "ascendant public opinion views with disfavor" interests and opinions that diverge from the majority's settled convictions (007-014).

Against these dangers, Mill insists that the [[parliament|representative assembly]] must contain "the élite of every class" — those whose independent judgment can resist manufactured opinion and speak for the genuine interests of their constituents (007-014).

## Public Opinion and the Executive

The relationship between public opinion and the [[executive]] is one of indirect control. A minister "in a [[monarchy|constitutional monarchy]] is virtually, by the [[representation|representative system]], appointed by [[parliament|Parliament]]" and thus by the public opinion that [[parliament|Parliament]] reflects (014-009). Yet Mill does not advocate direct plebiscitary control:

> "Only that general reference to public opinion necessary in all acts of the government may be wanting" when the executive is too insulated from public scrutiny (009-004)

Public opinion should set the broad direction and moral temper of administration, while leaving the details of execution to qualified professionals. The balance Mill seeks is one in which "the public under whose eyes they act" provides accountability without micromanagement (015-013).
