In Mill's analysis of [[representative-government]], the concept of **majority** is both foundational and deeply problematic. While democratic theory holds that the numerical majority should govern, Mill systematically exposes how simple majority-rule electoral systems can produce outcomes in which a "majority of the majority" — potentially a [[minority]] of the whole nation — exercises power. (007-004) This critique lies at the heart of his case for [[proportional-representation]] and for constitutional safeguards against the unmediated rule of any single class.

The majority principle, for Mill, must be reconciled with two competing imperatives: the need for skilled governance by the intellectually qualified few, and the protection of minority voices against the [[tyranny]] of numbers. His treatment of majority spans historical analysis, class sociology, and institutional design.

## Numerical Majority and the Problem of Class

Mill observes that in any society divided by economic class, the numerical majority naturally belongs to the manual laboring class. In a system of equal and universal [[suffrage]], this demographic fact would, under simple majority rule, deliver political dominance to a single class interest:

> Suppose, then, that in a country governed by equal and universal suffrage, there is a contested election in every constituency, and every election is carried by a small majority. The Parliament thus brought together represents little more than a bare majority of the people.
>
> (007-004)

The problem compounds through the legislative process. A parliament elected by narrow majorities, itself legislating by narrow majorities, can enact measures opposed by the greater part of the nation. "It is possible, therefore, and even probable, that the opinion which has prevailed was agreeable only to a minority of the nation." (007-004) This is the paradox Mill identifies at the core of democratic theory: simple majority rule can systematically produce [[minority]] rule.

## The Two-Class Model and Balanced Interests

Mill constructs a two-class model of society — manual laborers and employers of labor — to demonstrate how representative institutions should be designed. Rather than allowing either class to dominate through its numerical majority, the system should aim for equilibrium:

> The representative system ought to be so constituted as to maintain this state of things; it ought not to allow any of the various sectional interests to be so powerful as to be capable of prevailing against truth and justice, and the other sectional interests combined.
>
> (006-020)

In this model, the majority of each class would be governed by its [[class-interest]], but "there would be a minority of each in whom that consideration would be subordinate to reason, justice, and the good of the whole." (006-020) These cross-class minorities, joining forces, could "turn the scale against any demands of their own majority which were not such as ought to prevail." The institutional challenge is to preserve this dynamic equilibrium against the gravitational pull of numerical dominance.

## Majority of the Majority

Mill's most devastating critique of simple majority systems is encapsulated in his phrase "a majority of the majority." In a first-past-the-post electoral system, a candidate may win a seat with a bare majority of votes in a [[constituency]]. A [[party]] that wins a bare majority of such seats forms a government. At each stage, the losing near-half is excluded entirely:

> Democracy, thus constituted, does not even attain its ostensible object, that of giving the powers of government in all cases to the numerical majority. It does something very different; it gives them to a majority of the majority, who may be, and often are, but a minority of the whole.
>
> (007-004)

This insight drives Mill's advocacy for [[proportional-representation]] and particularly for [[thomas-hare|Hare's system]] of the single transferable vote. The goal is not to abolish majority decision — Mill accepts that "the minority must yield to the majority, the smaller number to the greater" (007-003) — but to ensure that the majority that governs is genuinely composed of a majority of the nation.

## Competence and the Majority

Mill's concern with majority rule extends beyond arithmetic to competence. He cautions that in a democracy where the numerical majority lacks education and political experience, the quality of governance suffers. Addressing the proper work of the representative body, Mill distinguishes between the specialized work of government and the supervisory function of the people's representatives. A democracy, he argues, "has enough to do in providing itself with an amount of mental competency sufficient for its own proper work, that of superintendence and check." (006-010)

The danger is not merely that the majority may govern in its own class interest, but that it may lack the intellectual resources to govern well. Mill's solution is not to disenfranchise the majority — he firmly supports universal [[suffrage]] — but to design institutions that temper numerical power with expertise, deliberation, and plural representation.

## Minorities Within the Majority

A distinctive feature of Mill's analysis is his attention to internal diversity within the majority itself. Even where a class constitutes the numerical majority, it is not monolithic. Mill notes that wherever "the minority in some places has a majority in others," the overall effect may be to give all significant opinion-groups some voice. (007-005) The problem arises when electoral geography and the winner-take-all system combine to silence geographically dispersed minorities entirely.

Mill's proposed remedy — [[thomas-hare|Hare's proportional scheme]] — would allow "the individuals composing the majority" to choose representatives who genuinely reflect their diverse views, rather than being "reduced to" selecting among candidates representing only the dominant local faction. (007-012) Under such a system, even a numerical majority would fragment into its constituent opinion-groups, enabling more authentic [[representation]].

> All principles are most effectually tested by extreme cases.
>
> (007-004)
