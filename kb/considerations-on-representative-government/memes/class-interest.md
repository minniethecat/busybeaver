In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], **[[sinister-interest|class interest]]** is the primary pathology that [[political-institution|political institutions]] must be designed to counteract. Any governing class — whether an aristocracy of birth, a commercial middle class, or a numerical majority of manual laborers — will tend to legislate for its own benefit rather than the common good. The problem is not peculiar to any one class but is inherent in the concentration of unchecked political power (006-014).

## The Nature of Class Legislation

Mill defines the problem precisely: class legislation occurs when government is directed by "the dominion of sectional or [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s," producing conduct that diverges from what the general welfare requires (006-014). This is not a matter of deliberate corruption but of the natural human tendency to favor one's own:

> "Men are not more corrupt when they are united in a party than when they are separate; but a party feeling, and a feeling of common class interest, make that which was for their class interest appear the dictate of justice" (006-018)

The psychological mechanism is insidious — [[sinister-interest|class interest]] does not operate through conscious selfishness alone but through the distortion of perception, causing those in power to mistake their partial good for the general good.

## The Problem of Majority Rule

The gravest danger in [[democracy]] is that the majority class — particularly if it constitutes a homogeneous numerical majority — will use its electoral power to serve its own [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s at the expense of minorities:

| Mechanism | Problem | Key PN |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Numerical domination | A single class holding a majority of votes can exclude all other classes from representation | (006-020) |
| Exclusive administration | Administration conducted "by its exclusive [[sinister-interest|class interest]]" subordinates the common good to partial ends | (008-001) |
| Taxation without representation | Those who pay no taxes "disposing by their votes of other people's money" sever power from interest | (008-005) |

> "If the majority would be mainly governed by their class interests, there would be a minority or minorities whose interests the majority would have every temptation to sacrifice" (006-020)

## Class Interest in the Electoral Process

Class interest operates within the [[election|electoral process]] itself. Mill observes that voters are often moved not by their judgment of the common good but by "the voter's personal interest, or [[sinister-interest|class interest]], or some mean feeling in his nature" (010-008). The problem is compounded by the fact that the middle class — in Mill's analysis — has "as much a [[sinister-interest|class interest]], distinct from the working class," as any other group (010-011).

Yet Mill does not conclude that [[sinister-interest|class interest]] is inescapable. His [[political-institution|institutional design]] aims to create a representative body in which no single class can dominate:

1. **Proportional representation** via [[thomas-hare|Hare]]'s scheme ensures that minorities are not excluded (007-006).
2. **Plural voting** gives additional weight to those with demonstrated [[intelligence|intellectual capacity]], diluting raw numerical dominance (010-016).
3. **Open [[voting|ballot]]** (which Mill later reconsidered) was initially thought to subject the voter to the scrutiny of the public rather than the domination of his class (010-008).

## Institutional Counterweights

Mill's solution to [[sinister-interest|class interest]] is not to eliminate classes — an impossibility — but to build institutions that prevent any single class from exercising unchecked power:

"Nothing very hostile to the [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s or feelings of the rich need be apprehended" in a well-constituted representative body precisely because multiple classes are present and must negotiate (010-016).

The key institutional counterweights include:
- **A second chamber** that provides a check on the [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s dominating the lower house (013-011).
- **An independent executive** with "elements exempt from the [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s and prejudices of the majority" (013-011).
- **Local government** where diverse [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s — "the farmers or petty shopkeepers" — serve as checks on one another (015-005).
- **Judicial impartiality** free from "bias from the individual or [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s of any one else" (018-019).

## Class Interest and the Constitution

The merit of a [[constitution]] is measured, in Mill's analysis, by its capacity to neutralize [[sinister-interest|class interest]]:

"The problem is to find the means of preventing this abuse, without sacrificing the characteristic advantages of [[democracy|popular government]]" (008-001).

A well-designed constitution does not deny the existence of [[sinister-interest|class interest]]s but institutionalizes them in such a way that no single class can impose its will. The representative body should be "a Congress of Opinions" in which every significant interest is heard and must justify itself before the judgment of the whole.

> "The heiress of a great feudal estate, and a merchant of the city, though both members of the rich class, and having a different class interest, who will affirm that they ought to be confounded?" (012-005)
