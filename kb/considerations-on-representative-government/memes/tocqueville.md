## Mill's Engagement with Tocqueville

Alexis de Tocqueville (1805–1859) is referenced by Mill primarily as the author of *Democracy in America*, a work Mill describes as "M. de Tocqueville's great work" (008-002). Mill draws upon Tocqueville's observations of American democracy to support his argument that political participation itself educates and elevates the citizenry.


> I call to witness the entire contents of M. de Tocqueville's great work, and especially his estimate of the Americans. Almost all travelers are struck by the fact that every American is in some sense both a patriot and a person of cultivated intelligence; and M. de Tocqueville has shown how close the connection is between these qualities and their democratic institutions.
>
> (008-002)


## Tocqueville on Democratic Education

In discussing the benefits of extending the suffrage to the working classes, Mill invokes Tocqueville as his key witness. Against those who doubt that political participation can cultivate mental improvement in manual laborers, Mill writes: "If any one supposes that this road will not bring it, I call to witness the entire contents of M. de Tocqueville's great work, and especially his estimate of the Americans" (008-002).

Tocqueville demonstrated "how close the connection is between these qualities and their democratic institutions," showing that "almost all travelers are struck by the fact that every American is in some sense both a patriot and a person of cultivated intelligence" (008-002). This observation supports Mill's broader argument that representative government, by engaging citizens in public affairs, serves as an instrument of national education.

## Tocqueville on Federal Institutions

Mill also draws on Tocqueville's analysis of the American federal system, particularly regarding the role of the Supreme Court. The "eminently beneficial working of so singular a provision" as judicial review is, Mill notes following Tocqueville, "in a great measure attributable to the peculiarity inherent in a court of justice acting as such" (017-006)—specifically, that courts decide concrete cases rather than issuing abstract declarations, allowing for public discussion to precede judicial resolution.
