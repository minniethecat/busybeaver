The **executive** is, in Mill's analysis, the branch of [[government]] charged with the daily administration of public affairs. While the [[parliament|legislature]] deliberates and decides, the executive acts. "In every country, the executive is the branch of the government which has the most direct and powerful interest in the maintenance of order" (004-003). Mill is concerned with how to design an executive that is both effective and accountable — and with the danger that a weak or poorly constituted [[parliament|legislature]] will encroach upon executive functions (006-011).

## Functions of the Executive

Mill identifies several core executive functions, each with its own institutional logic:

| Function | Responsible Body | Principle |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Preparing estimates (budget) | Executive departments | The [[parliament|legislature]] votes supplies but does not prepare them (005-007) |
| Nominating cabinet ministers | Prime minister | [[parliament|Parliament]] recognizes the majority party's candidate; it does not itself nominate (005-011) |
| Power of dissolution | Executive | Allows appeal to the people when [[parliament|Parliament]] no longer reflects public opinion (011-003) |
| Subordinate administration | Executive departments | Must be "divided among a number of hands" for effective management (014-001) |

> "Though the supplies can only be voted by the House of Commons, and though the sanction of the House is also required for the appropriation of the revenues to the different items of the public service, it is not the business of Parliament to prepare the estimates" (005-007)

## Individual Responsibility

A central principle of Mill's executive design is that every function must be assigned to a specific, identifiable individual:

| Principle | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Single-point accountability | "It should be apparent to all the world who did every thing, and through whose default any thing was left undone" (014-002) |
| Undivided responsibility | "Responsibility is null when nobody knows who is responsible; nor, even when real, can it be divided without being weakened" (014-002) |

This principle stands in direct opposition to [[jeremy-bentham|Bentham]]'s observation that "boards are screens" — collegial bodies often obscure individual accountability (014-003).

## Executive and Legislature

The boundary between the executive and the [[parliament|legislature]] is one of the most delicate questions in constitutional design:

- **Encroachment danger**: When the [[parliament|representative assembly]] lacks sufficient [[intelligence|intellectual capacity]], it "will encroach, by special acts, on the province of the executive; it will expel a good, or elevate and uphold a bad ministry" (006-011).
- **[[parliament|Parliament]]ary oversight**: The [[parliament|legislature]] should supervise and control the executive — not administer directly (009-004).
- **Public opinion**: The "opinion of the country" determines "what bodies of men shall furnish the executive government" — this is the democratic link between representation and administration (005-011).

## The Weak Executive Problem

Mill warns that a weak executive invites disorder and private interest capture:

> "If the executive is weak, the country is disturbed by factions, by private interests, by the ambition of powerful men, who can not find their advantage in a quiet support of the established order, but seek their fortune by overturning it" (004-004)

This observation connects the executive to Mill's broader criterion of good government — its capacity to maintain "the degree of order which is necessary to the security of all" (004-004).

## Constitutional Morality and the Executive

Beyond formal constitutional provisions, Mill insists on the importance of "constitutional morality" — the shared norms that govern how power is exercised:

> "The very existence of some governments, and all that renders others endurable, rests on the practical observance of doctrines of constitutional morality; traditional notions as to what is due from each department to the others" (012-003)
