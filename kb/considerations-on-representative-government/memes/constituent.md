# Constituent

A **constituent** is a member of the electoral body that selects a representative. In Mill's theory of [[representative-government|representative government]], the proper relationship between constituents and their representatives is a central question that determines the character of the entire political system.

## The Constituent-Representative Relationship

Mill devotes Chapter XII to the question: "Should a member of the legislature be bound by the instructions of his constituents?" (012-001). His answer is a qualified no — the representative should listen to constituents but not be bound by their instructions on every question.

The constituent body provides the foundation of legitimacy for representative government, but constituents err when they seek to "[[delegate|reduce their representative into a delegate]]" (012-002). The representative owes constituents his judgment, not merely his vote.

> "…to enumerate and classify the constituents of social well-being, so as …" (002-004)

## The Opinions of Constituents

Mill acknowledges the practical force of constituent opinion: "the opinions of the constituents, when they insist on them, determine the vote of the representative" (007-003) in most cases. This is not a normative endorsement but a description of political reality. The challenge is to structure the system so that constituent influence operates as a healthy check rather than a suffocating constraint.

When a representative disagrees with "the majority of his constituents" (012-004), the proper response is not automatic submission but [[deliberation|deliberation]] and explanation. The representative should be prepared to "justify his conduct to his constituents" (010-018).

## Conferences and Communication

Mill endorses direct communication between representatives and constituents. He notes the practice of "public conferences with his constituents" (011-001) as a mechanism for representatives to explain their positions and hear constituent concerns. These conferences serve not to transmit instructions but to enable the exchange of views that enriches deliberation on both sides.

Constituents may also support representatives through "the contributions of his constituents" (010-018) — financial support for [[election|election expenses]] — which Mill considers "unobjectionable" when transparent, as it avoids dependence on wealthy patrons or party machinery.

## Constituent Judgment

Mill's constituents are not passive recipients of representation but active participants in political life. The "common level of their constituents" (009-001) affects the quality of representatives chosen. A constituency that values judgment and character will select better representatives than one that demands pledges and obedience.

The representative, in turn, benefits from familiarity with "the lot of most of his constituents" (012-007) — understanding their circumstances, interests, and concerns is essential to representing them well, even when disagreeing on particular measures.
