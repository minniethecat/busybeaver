# Civilization

**Civilization** in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]* is not merely material progress but the development of a people's collective capacity for order, obedience, and ultimately self-government. Different "stages of civilization" require different forms of government (001-011), and the suitability of [[representative-government|representative government]] depends directly on a nation's civilizational attainment.

## Civilization as a Precondition

Mill argues that a people must reach a certain stage of civilization before [[representative-government|representative government]] becomes viable:

> "A people, in order to advance in civilization, have some lesson to learn, some habit not yet acquired, to the acquisition of which representative government is a hinderance rather than a help." (004-005)

The first and most fundamental lesson is obedience: "To learn the first lesson of civilization, that of obedience. A race which has not yet learned to obey, is not yet fit for [[representative-democracy|representative government]]" (004-006). Mill holds that "no people is capable of making any progress in civilization until it has learned to obey" (002-027).

## Obedience as Foundation

The capacity for obedience is the threshold test. Yet Mill acknowledges its unexciting character:

> "Yet all real civilization is at this price; without such submission, no advance can be made in any of the higher virtues, the intellect is not developed, nor are even the material interests of the community properly cared for." (002-028)

This cultivation of obedience is what "carried those nations up to the point of civilization which they attained" (002-029). Without it, "[[representative-democracy|representative government]] can not permanently exist" (004-005).

## Civilization and Government Forms

Mill's theory establishes a dynamic relationship between civilizational stage and government form:

| Stage | Governance Need | Representative Viability |
|-------|----------------|------------------------|
| Pre-civilization | Absolute rule to instill obedience | Not viable |
| Advancing civilization | Good despotism or mixed government | Becoming viable |
| High civilization | Representative institutions | Ideal and necessary |

A government "fit for a high state of civilization would have as one of its fundamental elements, a numerous and variously composed representative body" (005-013). In such a state, "better qualifications for legislation exist, and may be found if sought for" (005-013).

## The Two-Fold Requirement

Mill identifies two qualities a people must possess for [[representative-democracy|representative government]]:

1. **Willingness to accept it** — the people must desire representative institutions
2. **Willingness and ability to do what is necessary for its preservation** — active civic virtue

> "These are the cases in which representative government can not permanently exist. The first two causes of failure in a representative government." (004-004)

A people "unfitted for [[representative-democracy|representative government]] by the contrary fault" — "the people are no less unfitted for [[representative-democracy|representative government]] by the contrary fault to that last mentioned; by the want of a sufficient desire to take the trouble necessary" (004-007) — fail in the second requirement.

## Progress Through Representation

Representative government and civilization advance together. "There are indeed but few obstacles to the progress of civilization which [[representative-democracy|representative government]] would not have a decided tendency to remove" (004-008). This reciprocal relationship — civilization enables representation, and representation accelerates civilization — is central to Mill's progressive vision.

The "gradual operation" of good institutions ensures "all the civilization which had been gained insensibly" (006-015) is preserved and built upon.
