**Self-[[government]]**, in Mill's *Considerations on [[representative-government|Representative Government]]*, is the condition in which a [[polity|political community]] exercises control over its own affairs through representative institutions rather than being ruled by an external or unaccountable power. It is at once the **goal** of political development — the condition toward which all peoples should progress — and the **means** by which the capacities necessary for [[liberty|freedom]] are acquired.

Mill's treatment of self-government is notable for its refusal of simple answers. He rejects both the claim that all peoples are immediately fit for self-government and the claim that unfitness is a permanent condition justifying indefinite subjection. Instead, he develops a developmental framework in which self-government is a practice that must be learned through exercise — and a practice for which different peoples are ready at different stages of their history.

## Self-Government as Education

The central insight of Mill's analysis is that self-government is a **school**. The capacities it requires — judgment, public-spiritedness, the ability to subordinate private interest to the general welfare — are not innate; they are acquired through the experience of participating in political life:

> "They have to be taught self-government, and this, in its initial stage, requires a course of preparatory training" (002-029)

This training is not merely formal instruction but the lived experience of [[participation|political participation]]. The citizen who votes, serves on a [[jury]], and engages in [[municipal]] affairs is learning — often without knowing it — the habits of mind that self-government demands. The [[voting|ballot]] is a classroom; the town meeting is a school of citizenship.

The developmental framework Mill proposes recognizes distinct stages:

| Stage | Political Condition | Appropriate Government |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Savage | Incapable of organized [[freedom|self-rule]] | "A despotism, which may tame the savage" — rule by a stronger or more civilized power |
| Dependent | Partial capacity emerging | Limited self-government under supervision, with progressive extension of powers |
| Transitional | "Unfit for liberty" but capable of improvement | Representative institutions with safeguards — [[plurality]], indirect election |
| Mature | Full capacity for [[freedom|self-rule]] | Complete [[representative-democracy|representative government]] with universal participation |

> "At some period, however, of their history, almost every people, now civilized, have consisted, in majority, of slaves" (002-029)

This historical observation is crucial to Mill's argument. No people was born fit for self-government; every people that now exercises it passed through stages of unfitness. The question is not whether a people is currently fit, but whether their institutions are designed to **increase** their fitness over time.

## Self-Government and Character

Mill draws a fundamental distinction between the **active** and **passive** types of character, and argues that the form of government determines which type predominates:

> "Now there can be no kind of doubt that the passive type of character is favored by the government of one or a few, and the active self-helping type by that of the many" (003-023)

This is one of Mill's deepest arguments for self-government. It is not merely that self-government produces better laws or policies — it produces better **people**. The subject of a despotism learns obedience, deference, and passivity; the citizen of a self-governing community learns initiative, responsibility, and the habits of collective action. The form of government shapes the character of the governed, and through that character, every other aspect of social life.

> "Irresponsible rulers need the quiescence of the ruled more than they need any activity but that which they can compel. Submissiveness to the prescriptions of men as necessities of nature is the lesson inculcated by all governments upon those who are wholly without participation in them" (003-023)

The contrast between self-government and [[despotism]] is therefore not merely a contrast between [[liberty|freedom]] and constraint, but between two different kinds of human being: the active citizen who thinks and acts for himself, and the passive subject who waits to be told what to do. The choice of political system is a choice about what kind of character to cultivate.

## The Virtues of Self-Help and Self-Government

Mill identifies a "natural incompatibility" between the passive virtues cultivated by authoritarian government and the active virtues required by self-government:

> "The virtues of self-help and self-government there is a natural incompatibility" with the habits of mind inculcated by despotic rule (003-023)

This incompatibility explains why the transition from subjection to self-government is difficult. A people long accustomed to being governed cannot simply be "given" self-government and expected to exercise it well. The habits of passivity, deference, and dependence on authority must be unlearned, and the habits of initiative, critical judgment, and collective responsibility must be acquired. This is the work of generations, not of constitutions.

The practical implication is important: the design of representative institutions must take account of the **present character** of the people while aiming to **improve** it. Institutions that presuppose a level of [[intelligence|public intelligence]] and virtue that does not exist will fail; institutions that make no provision for the development of those qualities will perpetuate unfitness.

## Self-Government and Empire

Mill's analysis of self-government extends to the question of [[colonies|imperial governance]]. He distinguishes between [[colonies|dependencies]] populated by "her own blood and language" — entitled to "representative institutions formed in imitation of her own" — and those inhabited by "a dissimilar people," where the appropriate policy is more gradual:

> "England has always felt under a certain degree of obligation to bestow on such of her outlying populations as were of her own blood and language, and on some who were not, representative institutions formed in imitation of her own; but, until the present generation, she has been on the same bad level with other countries as to the amount of self-government which she allowed them to exercise" (018-004)

The passage reveals both the strengths and the limitations of Mill's framework. He insists that self-government is the appropriate goal for all peoples, but he acknowledges that the **pace** at which it can be extended varies. The settler colonies, sharing Britain's political culture and experience, can be granted full representative institutions immediately. Other [[colonies|dependencies]] require a longer process of preparation — but the direction of travel is the same. Self-government is the destination; only the length of the journey differs.
