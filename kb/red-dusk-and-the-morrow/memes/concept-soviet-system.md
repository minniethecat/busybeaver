## Overview

The Soviet system was the institutional architecture of Bolshevik rule — a pyramid of councils (Soviets) stretching from village and factory committees up to the All-Russian Central Executive Committee and the Council of People's Commissars (Sovnarkom). In theory, the system embodied direct proletarian democracy; in practice, Dukes shows, it functioned as a transmission belt for Bolshevik Party directives, with real power concentrated in the Party apparatus rather than the Soviets themselves.

Dukes' memoir provides a rare ground-level view of how the Soviet system operated in daily life. As someone who navigated Soviet bureaucracy under false identities, he understood its functioning in ways that few Western observers could match. His account reveals a system characterised by paper credentials, ideological conformity, and an ever-present security apparatus that monitored perceived enemies of the revolution.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes documents the paradoxes of Soviet governance with an intelligence officer's eye for institutional dysfunction. He describes how "official 'documents' with prominent seals or stamps" carried quasi-magical authority, with "a big coloured seal" functioning as "a talisman that levelled all obstacles," regardless of the document's actual content (001-076). This observation encapsulates the Soviet system's reliance on form over substance — an bureaucratic authoritarianism dressed in revolutionary language.

The Soviet system's economic policies had devastating consequences. Dukes records how "the persecution of the intelligentsia was at its height" in the summer of 1918, even as the regime depended on educated specialists to keep basic services functioning (004-014). The Soviet apparatus, staffed by ideologues with little administrative experience, presided over industrial collapse, food shortages, and the disintegration of urban life. Dukes documents the effects not through statistics but through vivid scenes: the queues, the hunger, the pervasive fear.

The system also maintained control through surveillance and terror. Dukes describes how Communists were attached to military units "to eavesdrop and report any remarks detrimental to the Bolsheviks" (011-016). The Cheka, operating outside any legal framework, became the Soviet system's enforcement arm, creating an atmosphere where denunciation and self-censorship became survival strategies.

## Dukes' Analysis

Despite his hostility to Bolshevism, Dukes is capable of recognising the Soviet system's appeal to certain constituencies. He notes that "no people in the world are so capable of heroic and self-sacrificing labour for purely altruistic motives as a certain type of Russian" (004-014). Some educated Russians "heroically stuck to their posts" despite persecution, believing they could save something from the wreck. This nuance distinguishes Dukes' account from pure anti-Bolshevik polemic.

His most fundamental critique is that the Soviet system was unsustainable because it conflicted with Russia's national character. The contrast between "Bolshevism and Russianism" runs throughout the memoir — Dukes believed the Soviet system was an artificial imposition that the deeper currents of Russian life would eventually wash away (011-070).
