## Overview

[[Moscow]], the ancient capital of [[Russia]], reclaimed its status as the seat of national government when the Bolshevik [[Council of People's Commissars]] relocated from [[Petrograd]] in March 1918. For [[Paul Dukes]], Moscow was a city of transit, intrigue, and consequence — it was there that he received the "unexpected telegram" from the [[British Foreign Office]] that launched his intelligence career, and it was through Moscow that the [[Bolshevik]] regime directed its policies across the former [[Russian Empire]] (001-009).

Though Petrograd dominates Dukes' narrative, Moscow appears at critical junctures, representing both the nerve centre of Bolshevik power and a place of danger for a [[British intelligence|British agent]] operating undercover. The city was also the site of executions that reverberated through the British intelligence community, shaping the urgency of Dukes' own mission.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes was in Moscow in the early revolutionary period when he received a telegram marked "Urgent" from the British Foreign Office, summoning him to London (001-009). This moment changed the course of his life, transforming him from a civilian observer into a recruited intelligence agent. He departed Moscow for [[Archangel]], beginning the journey that would bring him back to Russia undercover.

> Then one day, when in Moscow, I was handed an unexpected telegram. "Urgent" — from the British Foreign Office. "You are wanted at once in London," it ran. I set out for Archangel without delay. Moscow, with its turbulences, its political wranglings, its increasing hunger, its counterrevolutionary conspiracies, with Count Mirbach and his German designs, was left behind. (001-009)

Throughout the memoir, Moscow reappears as a destination and reference point. The "Banker," whom Dukes cultivated as an informant in Petrograd, had "just returned from Moscow" where he had been offered a post in a new department created to address the ruin of Soviet industry — an offer he found darkly amusing given the regime's incompetence (003-123). When Dukes considered travelling abroad, it was to Moscow he contemplated going (003-139).

The execution of British agent [[Francis Davison]] in Moscow cast a long shadow over Dukes' account. Davison's death at Bolshevik hands underscored both the mortal risk of intelligence work in Soviet Russia and the regime's willingness to execute foreign nationals. As Dukes records in his foreword, the Soviet Government claimed Davison "was shot as an accomplice of my 'provocative activities'" (P01-005).

As a Red Army soldier during his later intelligence work, Dukes was sent to Moscow to consult with the [[National Centre]], "the most promising of the political organizations whose object was to work out a programme acceptable to the Russian people as a whole" (010-008). From Moscow he "received regularly copies of the summaries on the general situation that were submitted to the Soviet of People's Commissars" (010-008), making the city the hub of his intelligence-gathering operation.

When disturbances threatened Petrograd, it was well known that "no troops indigenous to Petrograd or Moscow could be relied upon to fire on their fellow-townspeople" (010-008), revealing the political unreliability of the capital's population.

## Description

Dukes provides less physical description of Moscow than of Petrograd, but his references to the city convey its dual nature: the ancient "mother of Russian cities" now serving as the revolutionary capital of a new political order. The contrast between Moscow's historic Russian character and Petrograd's European cosmopolitanism is implicit throughout the memoir, with Moscow representing the more authentically Russian seat of power to which the Bolsheviks retreated.

One of Dukes' acquaintances travelled from Petrograd to Moscow "with no other passport than a receipted English tailor's bill" (001-076), an anecdote illustrating both the chaotic early days of Bolshevik administration and the peculiar importance of stamped documents — a theme that runs throughout the memoir. Another acquaintance, Markov, found that "living is much better in Moscow" than in Petrograd, a telling detail about the comparative conditions in the two cities during the Civil War period (005-073).

## Historical Significance

Moscow became the capital of Soviet Russia in 1918 and remained the capital of the Soviet Union until its dissolution in 1991. The Bolshevik move from Petrograd to Moscow was both practical — moving the government away from the advancing German armies — and symbolic, retreating to the inland fortress that had historically been the seat of Russian power. Dukes' era captured the transitional moment when the old imperial geometry, centred on Petrograd's European-facing windows, gave way to a new Soviet one anchored in Moscow's [[Kremlin]].
