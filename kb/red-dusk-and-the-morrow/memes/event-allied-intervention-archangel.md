## Overview

In August 1918, Allied forces under British command occupied the northern Russian port of Archangel, ostensibly to protect war supplies from falling into German hands but also to support anti-Bolshevik forces. Paul Dukes was in Archangel shortly after the occupation and provided a rare independent intelligence assessment of what went wrong. His account of the intervention is among the most politically astute passages in his memoir.

## Background

Following the Brest-Litovsk Treaty, the Allies feared that German forces would seize the vast quantities of war material stockpiled at Russia's northern ports. The Czech Legion's revolt along the Trans-Siberian Railway also created an opportunity for a broader anti-Bolshevik front. Archangel, a White Sea port with access to the interior, was chosen as the landing point. British naval forces were joined by small contingents of French, American, and other Allied troops.

## Events

Dukes arrived in Archangel shortly after the initial occupation and was present when the British general was greeted with "flowers strewn in his path as he stepped ashore" (003-025). But popular enthusiasm soon soured. A democratic government headed by the "venerable revolutionist Tchaikovsky" had been established under Allied sponsorship (003-026). One night, a group of Russian officers "summarily arrested this government" while "the allied military leaders slyly shut one eye so as not to see what was going on" (003-026). The ministers were "whisked away by automobile to a waiting steam launch, and carried off to a remote island in the White Sea" (003-026).

## Aftermath

The kidnap crisis exposed the contradictions in Allied policy. As Dukes observed: "who was to untangle the knot? The allied military had connived at the kidnapping by Russian plotters of a Russian government established by order of the Allies!" (003-028). After two days of "wrangling," diplomacy prevailed: "a cruiser was despatched to pick up the wretched ministers shivering on the remote White Sea island" (003-028). They were restored to office but their authority was "tarnished." Even worse for Allied prestige, "all the factories went on strike" (003-028). Dukes concluded with bitter irony: the returned ministers "went on trying to pretend to be a government" (003-028).

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes recounts this episode to a Russian friend who dreams of Allied rescue, and the story serves as a devastating reality check. He tells it to break down "a radiant castle the poor Journalist had built in the air" (003-030). Dukes' assessment is that the Allies, despite their military power, were incapable of establishing a credible alternative to Bolshevism because they could not bridge the gap between Russian democrats and Russian militarists. The intervention, in his view, united the very forces it was meant to divide.
