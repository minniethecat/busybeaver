# Red Army

## Overview

The Workers' and Peasants' Red Army (RKKA) was the military force established by the Bolshevik government in January 1918 to defend the new Soviet state. Under the leadership of Leon Trotsky as Commissar for War, it grew from improvised workers' militias into a disciplined military organisation that ultimately prevailed in the Russian Civil War against the White forces and various foreign interventions. Paul Dukes, an English intelligence officer, served within its ranks and provided a rare first-hand account of its internal workings.

## Structure and Role

The Red Army was formally subordinate to the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies, as indicated by the official certificate Dukes received identifying him as being "in the service of the Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee" (001-080). By the end of 1919, the higher military posts — divisional, artillery, and brigade commanders — were "occupied almost exclusively by former Tsarist generals and colonels," a fact of which "the Bolsheviks are extremely proud" (011-033). These former Tsarist officers were treated with deference but remained closely watched as known anti-Bolsheviks. The lower ranks featured a predominance of "Red" officers turned out from Red cadet schools where they were instructed by Tsarist officers, though few were men of education (011-034).

## Activities

The Red Army employed various methods to maintain discipline and ideological conformity. Every soldier was required to swear a resolution promising to fight "for workmen and peasants against capitalists, imperialists, bankers, generals, landlords, priests, and other bloodsucking riffraff" (011-015). Appointment to posts of responsibility was made conditional upon having relatives near at hand who could be arrested as hostages, a system designed to guarantee loyalty. Dukes' own commander confessed bitterly that he "should prefer to see my wife and my little daughters shot rather than that they be sent to a Red concentration camp" (011-013).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes served in the Red Army personally, providing an intimate portrayal of its character. On joining his regiment he donned a uniform consisting of "a khaki shirt, yellow breeches, putties, a pair of good boots," and on his cap "the Red army badge — a red star with a mallet and plough imprinted on it" (011-002). The uniforms were irregular: "Except for picked troops, smartly apparelled in the best the army stores could provide, the rank and file of recruits wore just anything, and often had only bast slippers in place of boots" (011-003). He observed a striking chasm between the propaganda directed at soldiers and their actual sentiments: most men in his unit spoke critically of the regime "only quietly, for fear of Bolshevist spies" (011-014).

## Historical Context

The Red Army was established after the disintegration of the Imperial Russian Army in the wake of the Bolshevik seizure of power and the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk. Its creation marked the first time a revolutionary Marxist government had attempted to build a conventional military force rather than relying solely on popular militias. Dukes noted that most Russian emigres ultimately admitted that "warring against the so-called Soviet Republic has served above all else to consolidate the position of the Bolshevist leaders" (011-032), while the failure of anti-Bolshevik forces was due largely to their own deficient administration.
