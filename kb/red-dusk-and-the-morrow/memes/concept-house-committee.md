## Overview

The house committee was the Soviet revolution at its most intimate — the point where Bolshevik ideology entered the stairwell, the kitchen, and the bedroom. In Paul Dukes' memoir, the house committee of a Petrograd apartment building becomes a vivid miniature of the entire Soviet system: its class inversions, its bureaucratic pettiness, its surveillance, and its strange blend of empowerment and tyranny.

Dukes, living undercover in shared quarters, observed the house committee's operations from within. His account is valuable precisely because it captures Soviet governance at a scale Western observers rarely noticed — not the grand political theatres of the Kremlin or the Comintern, but the daily reality of who controlled the coal supply, who decided which flat to requisition, and who reported whom to the Cheka. In this domestic microcosm, Dukes found the essence of Bolshevik rule.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes' housekeeper Stepanovna provided him with a detailed account of the committee's composition: it "was formed of three servant girls, the yard-keeper, and the house-porter" and administered the "entire house with forty flats" (002-102). The class inversion was complete — those who had once served the building's bourgeois residents now governed them. Dukes neither romanticises nor condemns this reversal; he simply records it, allowing the reader to draw conclusions.

The committee's power was comprehensive. It allocated living space, adjudicated disputes, monitored residents' political reliability, and reported suspicious behaviour. In the kitchen, listening to Stepanovna and her companion "unburdened their hearts without restraint," Dukes gained insight into how ordinary Russians navigated this new domestic order — simultaneously empowered and endangered by the revolution that had turned their world upside down (002-110).

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes uses the house committee as a lens through which to examine the Bolsheviks' broader project of social transformation. The committee represented both the revolution's democratic promise — ordinary people governing their own affairs — and its authoritarian reality — neighbour informing on neighbour, personal feuds elevated to political crimes. His account is deliberately understated; he lets the absurdities speak for themselves.

The house committee also served Dukes' operational needs. As a spy living under false papers, understanding the committee's dynamics was essential to survival. His ability to navigate this world — to befriend Stepanovna, to avoid attracting the committee's attention, to move through revolutionary Petrograd as if he belonged — was as much an intelligence skill as any dead drop or cipher.
