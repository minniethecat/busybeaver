## Overview

Leon Trotsky (born Lev Davidovich Bronstein, 1879--1940) was, after Lenin, the most prominent figure of the Bolshevik Revolution. As Commissar for Military and Naval Affairs, he founded and commanded the Red Army, transforming a ragtag collection of revolutionary militias into a disciplined fighting force that prevailed in the Russian Civil War. In Paul Dukes' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*, Trotsky appears as the "genius of the Red army" -- a figure of "superhuman energy and indomitable will" whose ambition, egoism, and cruelty mark him as both formidable and dangerous (011-018).

Dukes devotes sustained attention to Trotsky's character and methods, drawing on first-hand accounts from officers who served under him. The portrait that emerges is of a man who regarded "the Russian peasants and workers as cattle and treating them as such" (011-021), a revolutionary whose psychology contained "no elements corresponding to the ordinary conceptions of brutality or humanity" (011-019). Yet Dukes also acknowledges Trotsky's effectiveness: his ability to restore discipline to the army by reinstating Tsarist officers and abolishing soldiers' committees was, by any measure, a remarkable political feat.

## Role in the Narrative

Trotsky enters the narrative through multiple channels. Dukes hears of him from Zorinsky, who reports that "Trotsky is planning to come up to Petrograd" (003-082). The mere mention of Trotsky's name on the telephone, Dukes learns, "stood at once to mean news of Trotsky, or information regarding the Red army" (003-079). Zorinsky's colonel friend confirms "radical changes in the organization of the army, and said Trotsky was planning to establish a similar new regime in the Baltic Fleet" (003-107).

Dukes' most extended treatment of Trotsky comes in Chapter XI, where he provides a character sketch of unusual psychological penetration. Relying on the memoir of Trotsky's schoolmate and prison-companion Dr. Ziv, Dukes reports that in Trotsky's mind, "Men, for him, are mere units -- hundreds, thousands, and hundreds of thousands of units -- by means of which he may carry out his designs" (011-019). He even traces the origin of Trotsky's pseudonym -- adopted from "the senior jailer of the Tsarist prison-house at Odessa" -- arguing that Trotsky chose it because he could not bear having the same name as "the rest of the Bronsteins" (011-020).

Dukes also records the dramatic meeting at which Trotsky abolished the committee system in the Baltic Fleet. An officer who attended told Dukes: "We found it difficult to believe it was Trotsky talking to us" as he restored officers' authority over the Communist commissars (011-024). This anecdote encapsulates the contradictions of Trotsky's position: a revolutionary who, to win a war, had to resurrect the very hierarchies the revolution had ostensibly destroyed.

## Biography

Born to a prosperous Jewish farming family in Yanovka (present-day Ukraine) in 1879, Bronstein became involved in revolutionary politics as a teenager. He was arrested in 1898 and spent two years in prison before being exiled to Siberia, from which he escaped in 1902 using a forged passport bearing the name Trotsky. After playing a leading role in the 1905 Revolution as chairman of the St. Petersburg Soviet, he was again exiled and again escaped. During the 1917 Revolution, Trotsky joined the Bolsheviks and became Lenin's closest lieutenant. As Commissar for Military Affairs, he built the Red Army from scratch and led it to victory in the Civil War. After Lenin's death in 1924, Trotsky was outmanoeuvred by Stalin, exiled in 1929, and eventually assassinated in Mexico City in 1940 by a Soviet agent.

## In Dukes' Account

> Leo Bronstein, the genius of the Red army, now universally known by his more Russian-sounding pseudonym of Trotsky, is the second of the triumvirate of "Lenin, Trotsky, and Zinoviev," who guide the destinies of the Russian and the world revolution. That the accepted order of precedence is not "Trotsky, Lenin, and Zinoviev" must be gall and wormwood to Trotsky's soul. His first outstanding characteristic is overweening ambition; his second -- egoism; his third -- cruelty. (011-018)

This passage introduces Dukes' Trotsky -- brilliant, driven, and monstrous. The biting observation about the "order of precedence" reveals Dukes' interest in the personal rivalries within the Bolshevik leadership, a theme that runs through the memoir. Dukes notes with some irony that even among the officer class, there was grudging respect: asked who understands Russia, a colonel replies "Except, perhaps, Trotsky, or the Germans" (003-089).

The memoir's assessment of Trotsky's methods is unsparing. Dukes describes how "the same measures as were employed by the Tsarist army were introduced to combat wholesale robbery and pillage -- and with even greater severity" (011-022). The restoration of Tsarist officers, the suppression of soldiers' committees, and the use of hostage-taking against officers' families all form part of Dukes' indictment. Yet the effectiveness of Trotsky's approach is not denied -- the Red Army's victory, Dukes implicitly concedes, was largely Trotsky's doing.
