# Okhrana

## Overview

The Okhrana (officially the Department for Protecting Public Security and Order) was the secret police force of the Russian Empire, established in 1881 after the assassination of Tsar Alexander II. It operated as the Tsar's personal security service, employing a network of agents, informants, and *agents provocateurs* to infiltrate and suppress revolutionary movements. After the February Revolution of 1917 dismantled the Tsarist police apparatus, many former Okhrana agents found new employment under the Bolshevik regime they had once been tasked with destroying.

## Structure and Role

The Okhrana functioned as both an intelligence-gathering and a repressive organisation. Its most distinctive operational technique was "provocation" — the deliberate fomentation of revolutionary plots by *agents provocateurs*, who would attract genuine revolutionaries into their orbit and then deliver them en masse to the authorities. Dukes described this method as being copied later by the Cheka: the fomentation of "revolutionary sedition and plots" that "would attract to themselves ardent revolutionaries, and when they had grown to sufficient proportions all participants would be arrested" (002-271). The organisation answered directly to the Tsar, and its officials often moved in the highest circles of the Imperial court.

## Activities

Under the Tsarist regime, the Okhrana systematically penetrated revolutionary groups both within Russia and among the emigre communities abroad. Its network of informants was pervasive, and its methods anticipated many of the practices later perfected by the Cheka. After the revolution, the Bolsheviks — who had been the Okhrana's primary targets — made the pragmatic decision to re-enlist former Tsarist police agents as "specialists," recognising the value of their operational experience (002-269). The irony of this arrangement was not lost on Dukes, who observed the Bolsheviks "re-engaging Tsarist police agents for the Extraordinary Commission" less than a year after overthrowing the regime those agents had served.

## In Dukes' Account

The Okhrana appears in Dukes' narrative through the figure of "the Policeman," a former Tsarist official of ambiguous background whom Marsh had contacted for help in securing his wife's release from Cheka custody. Marsh explained that this man "was, I believe, an official of the Okhrana (the Tsar's personal secret police) before the revolution, and is doing some sort of underhand work for the Cheka" (002-127). When Dukes later met the Policeman and mistakenly referred to his Okhrana past, the man reacted with indignation, insisting he had "never been connected either with the police or the Okhrana" and claiming instead that he "moved in Court circles" and "had the ear of his late Imperial Majesty" (002-204). The encounter illustrates both the fluidity of identity in revolutionary Russia and the embarrassing continuity between Tsarist and Bolshevik security apparatuses.

## Historical Context

The Tsarist police system, of which the Okhrana was the most infamous component, was swept away in the February Revolution of 1917. Dukes witnessed this moment first-hand: "towards evening, after the Tsarist police had been scattered in the Nevsky Prospect, there rose a mighty murmur" (001-005). Yet the institution of secret police proved more durable than the regime it served. Within months, the Bolsheviks had established the Cheka, explicitly modelled on and staffed in part by veterans of the Okhrana. The cycle of institutional continuity in Russian security services — from Okhrana to Cheka to GPU to NKVD to KGB — represents one of the most significant legacies of the Tsarist period.
