## Overview

Archangel (Arkhangelsk) is a historic port city in the far north of Russia, situated on the Northern Dvina River near its mouth into the White Sea. During the Russian Civil War, it was the main base of the Allied intervention in North Russia — British, American, and French forces occupied the city from August 1918 in an effort to support anti-Bolshevik forces and protect Allied war materiel. For Paul Dukes, Archangel was the first failed gateway: his initial attempt to enter Soviet Russia through the northern port proved impossible, forcing him to try the Finnish route instead.

Dukes visited Archangel twice — once in the summer of 1918 and again more recently — and his observations of the Allied occupation provide one of the memoir's most pointed critiques of the intervention's contradictions.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes first set out for Archangel from Moscow after receiving his urgent recall to London. "I set out for Archangel without delay," he records, departing a city of "turbulences" and "political wranglings" (001-009). The journey north was the first step toward his undercover mission. On his return to Russia, Dukes "resolved to make my first attempt at entry from the north, and travelled up to Archangel on a troopship of American soldiers, most of whom hailed from Detroit" (001-035). But he found "the difficulties at Archangel to be much greater than I had anticipated," and after failing to cross the frontier from the north, "travelled down to Helsingfors to try my luck from the direction of Finland" (001-036).

Archangel reappears in the memoir through a story Dukes tells the Journalist — a cautionary tale about the chaos of the Allied intervention. He describes how "a group of officers — Russian officers — summarily arrested" the democratic government established by the Allies, deposing it in a night-time coup while "the allied military had connived at the kidnapping" (003-026, 003-028). The incident exposes the fatal contradictions of the Allied intervention: propping up a nominally democratic government while military plotters undermined it with Allied complicity.

Dukes concludes this account with a characteristic piece of tradecraft: "don't go and blurt that Archangel story everywhere, or you'll have to explain how you heard it" (003-036).

## Description

Dukes' physical description of Archangel is sparse, but he conveys the city's remote, northern character — reached by "troopship," situated at the margins of a vast country. The city serves more as a geopolitical node than a described landscape, a doorway to the north that proved, for Dukes, to be firmly shut.

## Historical Significance

Archangel was the principal theatre of the Allied intervention in North Russia, a complex and ultimately unsuccessful military operation that aimed to reopen an Eastern Front against Germany and later to contain Bolshevism. The occupation lasted from August 1918 to September 1919. Dukes' account of the Archangel coup captures the confusion and contradiction that characterised the Allied intervention — a theme that pervades his assessment of Western policy toward Russia.
