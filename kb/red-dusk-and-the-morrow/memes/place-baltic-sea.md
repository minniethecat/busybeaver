## Overview

The Baltic Sea and its eastern arm, the Gulf of Finland, were the maritime frontier of Bolshevik Russia — and the lifeline through which Paul Dukes and the British intelligence network maintained contact with the outside world. The Baltic Fleet, based at Kronstadt and Helsingfors, controlled the sea approaches to Petrograd; the Gulf of Finland's northern shore, in independent Finland, was the nearest point of safety for fugitives from Bolshevik territory.

Dukes' memoir is punctuated by Baltic crossings. "The boat which was to fetch me away" (013-004) navigated "nearly up to the mouth of the Neva" — a journey that was at once a naval operation and an escape. The Baltic is the memoir's liminal space: neither Bolshevik Russia nor the West, but the passage between them.

## In Dukes' Account

> The boat which was to fetch me away came nearly up to the mouth of the Neva. These boats, operated by British naval intelligence, were the thread connecting the Petrograd station to the outside world. (013-004, narrative paraphrase)

The Baltic crossing is the memoir's climax — the moment when Dukes, after months of undercover work and narrow escapes, finally leaves Bolshevik territory. The boat that "came nearly up to the mouth of the Neva" is the instrument of deliverance, and its arrival is the reward for months of patience and tradecraft.

> The Baltic Fleet, at which Trotsky abolished the traditional naval ranks and replaced officers with elected committees, was in a state of what Dukes described as "organised chaos" — capable of sporadic violence but incapable of sustained naval operations. (011-024, narrative paraphrase)

Dukes' assessment of the Baltic Fleet is characteristically double-edged: the Bolsheviks' revolutionary reforms have destroyed military effectiveness, which is good for the Allies but also means the fleet is unpredictable — a danger to everyone, including its own sailors.

> The Baltic republic of Lithuania had come into existence, and Latvia was still fighting for its independence. The Baltic littoral, once a unified imperial territory, had fragmented into a patchwork of new states, each with its own relationship to Soviet Russia. (014-017, narrative paraphrase)

The post-war transformation of the Baltic littoral — from imperial provinces to independent states — is part of Dukes' broader argument about the consequences of the revolution. The Bolsheviks claimed to represent national self-determination while fighting to reconquer the territories that had declared independence.

## Historical Context

The Baltic Sea was the primary theatre of Allied naval operations against Bolshevik Russia. The Royal Navy operated in the Baltic from 1918 to 1920, supporting the Baltic states' independence struggles, interdicting Bolshevik shipping, and running intelligence agents into and out of Petrograd. The British campaign in the Baltic was one of the Royal Navy's largest post-war operations, involving dozens of warships and thousands of personnel. Dukes' escape by boat was a small but essential component of this larger naval effort.
