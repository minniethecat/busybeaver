## Overview

[[Petrograd]] (known as [[St. Petersburg]] before 1914 and [[Leningrad]] after 1924) was the imperial capital of [[Russia]] and the birthplace of the [[1917 revolutions]]. For [[Paul Dukes]], the city was the central stage of his memoir — it was there that he "witnessed the great Russian Revolution" in March 1917 and to which he later returned in 1918 as a [[British intelligence]] agent to reside undercover among the [[Soviet bureaucracy]] (001-001). The city provides the physical and political geography for almost every chapter of *Red Dusk and the Morrow*, serving as both the seat of [[Bolshevik]] power and the operational theatre of Dukes' secret intelligence work.

Dukes describes Petrograd as a city of stark contrasts: the wide boulevards of the [[Neva River]] delta juxtaposed with crowded tenements, the resplendent palaces of the former [[aristocracy]] now occupied by revolutionary organs, and the "No. 2 Gorokhovaya" — the [[Cheka]] Prefecture — looming over the population as the nerve centre of [[Bolshevik terror]].

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' first encounter with revolutionary Petrograd comes on 11 March 1917, when he walked the Nevsky Prospect under "frosty sunshine" and witnessed the first revolutionary regiments emerge (001-002). He stood outside the Tauride Palace, which had "become the centre of the revolution," observing the chaos of the moment (001-006). This formative experience of witnessing revolution convinced the British authorities to recruit him as an intelligence agent.

Returning to Petrograd in late 1918, Dukes found a city transformed by [[Bolshevist]] rule. The elegance of imperial St. Petersburg had given way to the grim realities of the Cheka state. He resided at No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street, the notorious headquarters of the Petrograd Cheka, describing the building and its cells in harrowing detail. The city's geography permeates his account — from the remote suburb of Staraya Derevnya overlooking the [[Gulf of Finland]] (007-002), to the Kazan Cathedral where he held secret meetings, to the quays of the Neva where he walked in moments of solitary reflection.

Dukes moved through Petrograd under multiple identities, navigating the Soviet [[passport system]] with forged credentials. He describes how a "magical" document with a "big coloured seal" served as "a talisman that levelled all obstacles" in the new Bolshevik capital (001-076).

> No capital in Europe possessed such natural advantages as the city built on the Neva delta. The magnificent, unbroken vista of the Nevsky Prospect and the mile-long, straggling branch of the Neva flowing through the heart of the city made Petrograd one of the most beautiful capitals in the world. (002-013)

## Historical Significance

Petrograd under the early Bolshevik regime (1917–1919) was the laboratory of the world's first [[Communist]] state. All the institutions of Soviet power — the [[Cheka]], the [[Council of People's Commissars]], the [[Red Army]] high command — were headquartered there before the capital moved to [[Moscow]] in March 1918. Dukes' account captures this brief but transformative period when Petrograd was still the centre of Bolshevik power and the front line of the [[Russian Civil War]].
