## Overview

The Winter Palace, the former principal residence of the Russian emperors, stands on the Palace Embankment of the Neva River in Petrograd. As the architectural embodiment of tsarist autocracy, its storming in October 1917 became the defining image of the Bolshevik Revolution. For Paul Dukes, the palace functioned as a visual and symbolic landmark throughout his memoir — the backdrop for revolutionary gatherings on Palace Square, the silent witness to covert meetings in its adjacent gardens, and a constant reminder of the imperial order that Bolshevism had supplanted.

Dukes records the striking contrast between the palace's former grandeur and its reduced status under the new regime, where emperors' private gardens now served as meeting places for spies and couriers.

## In Dukes' Account

The Winter Palace first appears in Dukes' narrative as the setting for a Bolshevik propaganda event on the anniversary of Bloody Sunday. He walked "to the Palace Square, the great space in front of the Winter Palace, famous for another January Sunday — 'Bloody Sunday' — thirteen years before" (005-033). This juxtaposition of the 1905 massacre with the Bolshevik appropriation of the martyrs' memory is characteristic of Dukes' ironic treatment of the regime's historical revisionism.

The palace's gardens provide the setting for one of the memoir's most dramatic encounters. Walking along the river quay, Dukes spotted Ivan Sergeievitch — Melnikoff's friend from Viborg — disguised as a soldier. He led him into "a little garden on the west side of the Winter Palace," where "former emperors and empresses had promenaded" (009-062). In this secluded corner of the imperial grounds, the two men met covertly, aware that "from the windows of the Winter Palace prying eyes were looking down into the garden" (009-090).

This episode encapsulates the memoir's central tension: the clandestine world of intelligence work operating in the shadow of the old regime's monuments, now occupied by a new apparatus of surveillance. The garden where emperors once walked had become a place of whispered conspiracies.

## Historical Significance

The Winter Palace was the symbolic centre of the Russian Empire and, after its storming in October 1917, the symbolic centre of the revolution that overthrew it. By the time of Dukes' account (1918–1919), the palace had been nationalised and partially converted into a museum, though its grounds and surrounding square continued to serve as the stage for Bolshevik political theatre. Dukes' use of the palace's gardens for covert meetings is a microcosm of how the geography of the old regime was repurposed — often surreptitiously — by those who sought to undermine the new one.
