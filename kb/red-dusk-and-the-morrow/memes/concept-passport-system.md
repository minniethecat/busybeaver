## Overview

The passport and identity document system was the bureaucratic skeleton of Bolshevik surveillance, and Paul Dukes understood it better than almost any foreigner in Russia. Operating under multiple false identities — as a Bolshevik functionary, a discharged soldier, a refugee — Dukes depended on forged documents for his survival. His memoir offers a unique perspective on the passport system: neither the abstract analysis of a political scientist nor the complaint of a victim, but the practical knowledge of someone who had to game the system to stay alive.

Dukes' account reveals the passport system as both a tool of state control and a point of vulnerability. The Bolsheviks' reliance on paper credentials — stamps, seals, certificates — created opportunities for those skilled enough to exploit them. Dukes, with his network of conspirators and forgers, moved through the system's gaps while ordinary Russians remained trapped within it.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes' first encounter with the passport system came at the border. Ivan Sergeievitch, his contact in Viborg, asked directly: "'Have you any passport yet, Pavel Pavlovitch?'" (001-047). The answer was no — but the conspirators had a solution. They "collect the passports of all refugees from Petrograd, for they often come in handy" (001-049). This casual admission — that refugees' identities were a resource to be harvested — captures the moral universe of the underground.

The importance of official documents was almost magical. Dukes observed that "official 'documents' with prominent seals or stamps" carried authority regardless of content — "a big coloured seal was a talisman that levelled all obstacles" (001-076). The "certificates of identification" that served as passports in Soviet Russia could be arranged through contacts: "Melnikoff had told me I might safely leave this matter to the Finns, who kept them in stock" (001-068).

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes treats the passport system with professional respect — as an intelligence officer, he recognised good tradecraft when he saw it. The Bolsheviks had created a comprehensive system of population control that made unauthorised movement nearly impossible. Yet the system's complexity was also its weakness: with so many documents, stamps, and issuing authorities, forgery flourished.

The deeper insight in Dukes' account is that the passport system was not merely a security measure but a social instrument. Documents marked their bearers as proletarian or bourgeois, reliable or suspect, entitled to rations or excluded from them. Identity in revolutionary Russia was, in a literal sense, a paper construction — and Dukes, the forger's client, understood this better than the commissars who designed the system.
