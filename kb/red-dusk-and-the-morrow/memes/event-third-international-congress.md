## Overview

The Third International (Comintern) was founded in Moscow in early March 1919 as the Bolsheviks' instrument for exporting revolution. Paul Dukes witnessed the delegates' arrival in Petrograd and described the event with a blend of derision and concern. For Dukes, the Third International represented the dangerous internationalism that alienated the Bolsheviks from the Russian people — an organisation whose "first principle is worldwide civil war between classes" (012-004).

## Background

The First International (1864) and Second International (1889) had been workers' associations focused on labour interests. The Bolsheviks, by contrast, wanted an organisation dedicated to armed insurrection. Dukes explains that the Second International, "called the 'yellow' International because it is composed of moderates, was declared defunct and superseded by the 'real,' that is, the Communist, International" (012-005). The founding congress was held "amid circumstances of great secrecy" and was attended by "a chance gathering of extreme Socialists from about half-a-dozen of the thirty European States, leavened with a similar number of Asiatics" (012-005).

## Events

The delegates travelled to Petrograd after the Moscow congress, and Dukes went to meet them at the Nicholas Station. His description is devastating: "the day was frosty, and for nearly two hours the members of the Third International, standing bareheaded on a specially constructed tribune, wasted time saying exactly the same things over and over again, their speeches being punctuated by the cacophony of three badly-directed bands" (012-006). Despite "their luxurious fur coats the delegates shivered and their faces turned blue. They did not at all look the desperadoes I had half anticipated. Some of them were even effeminate in appearance" (012-006).

Zinoviev, the president, "with his bushy, dishevelled hair, looked like an unrepentant schoolboy amid a group of delinquents caught red-handed in some unauthorized prank" (012-006). The orators "lauded the exemplary order prevailing in Russia and rejoiced at the happiness, contentment, and devotion to the Soviet Government which they encountered at every step" (012-007). Dukes could scarcely conceal his contempt.

## Aftermath

The Third International became the vehicle for Bolshevik subversion abroad. Dukes identified a fundamental tension: the Comintern's agenda of world revolution conflicted with the Soviet government's need to preserve Bolshevik power in one country. Two factions developed: "that of Lenin, whose interests for the time are centred in Russia," and "that of the Third International, which throws discretion to the winds" (012-008). The conflict between Soviet state interests and international revolutionary commitment would define Comintern history until its dissolution in 1943.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes treats the Third International as both a political menace and a comic spectacle. His account of the freezing, shivering revolutionaries mouthing identical slogans captures the mechanical nature of Bolshevik ritual. But his scorn is also strategic: he believed the Comintern's commitment to world revolution fatally undermined any possibility of the Bolsheviks becoming genuine Russian patriots. "To expect this of the Third International," he writes, "is hopeless" (014-004). The Third International was, in his view, the institutional expression of everything that made Bolshevism alien to Russia.
