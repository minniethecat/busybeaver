## Overview

Ivan Sergeievitch was a Russian officer and refugee who became one of Paul Dukes' first contacts upon his return to Soviet Russia in 1918. A friend of Melnikoff, Sergeievitch facilitated Dukes' initial border crossing from Finland and provided him with a safe house in Petrograd. Though he does not appear directly in the narrative after Chapter I, his flat — staffed by his loyal housekeeper Stepanovna and nurse Varia — became Dukes' first refuge and an enduring base of operations.

## Role in the Narrative

Sergeievitch is introduced in the opening chapter as an officer friend of Melnikoff who visits Dukes on a Sunday to confirm his readiness for the journey into Russia. He had been living as a refugee in Finland, struggling to support his wife and children by smuggling Finnish money and butter into Petrograd, where both commodities fetched high premiums. This illicit trade placed him on favourable terms with the Finnish border patrols, who were themselves involved in the same business (001-046).

On the day of departure, Sergeievitch personally accompanied Dukes to the railway station at Viborg and rode part of the way on the train, though the two sat separately to avoid drawing attention to their association (001-053). Before parting, he gave Dukes explicit instructions to use his Petrograd flat whenever needed and warned him to avoid the house porter — a Bolshevik — and the house committee (001-054). The two men maintained the pretence of being strangers, exchanging only a single glance when Sergeievitch disembarked (001-056).

After entering Petrograd, Dukes relied on Sergeievitch's flat as his first safe shelter. The housekeeper Stepanovna and nurse Varia, devoted to their absent master, received Dukes warmly and believed Sergeievitch would one day return with the British to liberate Russia.

> He was a pleasant fellow, kind and considerate. Like many other refugees from Russia he had no financial resources and was trying to make a living for himself, his wife, and his children by smuggling Finnish money and butter into Petrograd, where both were sold at a high premium.
>
> (001-046)

## Biography

Little biographical detail is given beyond Sergeievitch's status as a Russian officer who fled to Finland after the Bolshevik Revolution. He lived in Viborg with his wife and children while maintaining a flat in Petrograd, staffed by a housekeeper, Stepanovna, and a children's nurse, Varia. His flat was located in a small street at the end of Kazanskaya, on the top floor — a detail that later proved useful when Dukes needed to enter discreetly. Sergeievitch's financial situation was precarious; like many White Russian émigrés, he resorted to smuggling to provide for his family.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' portrayal of Sergeievitch is brief but warmly appreciative. The author describes him as "a pleasant fellow, kind and considerate" and credits him with providing the practical advice and material support — a known address, a trusted housekeeper, an introduction by name — that made the difference between survival and capture in those first dangerous days in the city.

The flat on Kazanskaya became the setting for several tense episodes: Dukes' cautious entry past invisible watchers, his first meals of meat and potatoes prepared by the anxious Stepanovna, and his disclosure that he was an Englishman — a revelation that elicited both fear and excitement from the household. The belief that Sergeievitch would return with the British to liberate Russia coloured the servants' devotion to their absent master and their willingness to shelter a stranger who spoke his name.
