## Overview

Rosa Luxembourg appears in Dukes' memoir not as a subject of analysis but as an instrument of Bolshevik propaganda. The German Marxist revolutionary, murdered by Freikorps paramilitaries in Berlin on 15 January 1919, is recorded by Dukes as a name the Bolsheviks appropriated for their own purposes — despite the fact that Luxembourg herself had been a sharp critic of Leninist methods.

Dukes notes that the Bolsheviks distributed a pamphlet titled "Who Were Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxembourg?" (005-060) to educate Russian workers about the German revolutionaries. His account focuses on the gap between the Bolsheviks' public presentation of these figures and their own suppression of genuine revolutionary socialism. The renaming of buildings and the distribution of hagiographic pamphlets are, for Dukes, symptoms of a regime that substitutes slogans for substance.

## Role in the Narrative

Luxembourg figures in the memoir primarily through the Bolshevik propaganda apparatus. Dukes encounters the pamphlet about Liebknecht and Luxembourg during his work as a clerk in the Department of State Control — one of the many pieces of official literature he was responsible for distributing. His account of reading it with bemusement, aware that he is "a British secret service agent reading Bolshevik propaganda in the office of a Soviet department" (005-064), captures the surreal doubleness of his undercover existence.

Dukes also records the Bolsheviks' act of renaming a building: "changing it to 'House of Rosa Luxembourg and Karl Liebknecht'" (012-016). This act of commemorative appropriation is, for Dukes, typical: the Bolsheviks honour dead revolutionaries abroad while crushing living socialists at home. The physical transformation — a building renamed — mirrors the rhetorical transformation Dukes observes throughout: the regime colonises revolutionary language while practising its opposite.

Dukes' account of Luxembourg's and Liebknecht's deaths at the hands of the Freikorps — "had been killed in Berlin, the victims of Junker reaction" (005-002) — is notably sympathetic. He does not dispute their revolutionary credentials, only the Bolsheviks' right to claim them.

## Biography

Rosa Luxembourg was born in 1871 in Zamość, Russian Poland, to a Jewish family. She became one of the most important Marxist theorists of her generation, co-founding the Spartacist League and the German Communist Party (KPD). A fierce critic of both capitalism and Leninist authoritarianism, her essay "The Russian Revolution" (1918) warned that the Bolshevik suppression of democratic institutions would lead to bureaucratic dictatorship — a prediction Dukes would likely have endorsed. She was arrested during the Spartacist uprising of January 1919 and murdered by Freikorps troops under the orders of the Social Democratic government. Her body was thrown into Berlin's Landwehr Canal.

## Political Context

The Bolshevik appropriation of Luxembourg's name is one of Dukes' more subtle observations about the nature of revolutionary propaganda. Luxembourg had explicitly criticised the Bolshevik dissolution of the Constituent Assembly and warned against the dangers of one-party rule. The Bolshevik response after her death was to canonise her as a martyr while ignoring her critique — a technique of political neutralisation through hagiography.

Dukes does not make this argument explicitly — he may not have known the details of Luxembourg's critique — but his repeated emphasis on the gap between Bolshevik propaganda and Bolshevik practice makes the point through narrative accumulation. The pamphlet on Liebknecht and Luxembourg is of a piece with the renaming of the Tauride Palace to "Palace of Uritsky": the regime builds monuments to dead revolutionaries while creating the conditions that make living revolutionary politics impossible.

## In Dukes' Account

> The German Communist leaders, Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxembourg, had been killed in Berlin, the victims of Junker reaction. Little known as Liebknecht and Luxembourg had been outside Germany until their tragic death made them famous, their names were now on everybody's lips. (005-002, 005-003)

Dukes accurately captures the irony of posthumous celebrity — revolutionaries unknown in life become famous through the manner of their death. His description of their killers as "Junker reaction" reveals his own political sympathies: he is not hostile to the German revolutionaries themselves, only to the Bolsheviks who exploit their names.

> I found it stated in Who Were Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxembourg? that the Bolsheviks were fulfilling the ideals for which the German Communists had given their lives. Reading these lines, I reflected that I was a British secret service agent reading Bolshevik propaganda in the office of a Soviet department. (005-064, with narrative interpolation)

This passage captures the double consciousness of Dukes' undercover life. The propaganda he reads in the course of his official duties is filtered through his awareness of his own impossible position — a British spy sitting in a Soviet office, reading pamphlets that celebrate revolutionaries he might privately have respected.
