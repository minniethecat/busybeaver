## Overview

The Kamenostrovsky Prospect (Kamennoostrovsky Prospekt) is a major avenue in Petrograd that runs from the Petrograd Side across the Neva delta to Kamenny Island and the surrounding district known as "The Islands." In Dukes' memoir, the prospect functions as a geographical thread connecting several key locations in his operational network: the hospital where Melnikoff was treated, the Doctor's flat where political discussions took place, and the broader neighbourhood that formed the backdrop to his undercover life.

The prospect's length and its position at the city's watery edge give it a liminal character in the memoir — it is the route one takes to reach the remote suburbs, the hospitals, and the hideaways on the edge of Petrograd.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' first traverse of the prospect comes in the anxious aftermath of an encounter with Cheka agents. "Agitated by this misfortune, I turned my steps in the direction of the hospital where I hoped to find Melnikoff," he records. "The hospital in question was at the extreme end of the Kamenostrovsky Prospect — a good four-mile walk" (002-025). The distance underscores both the city's scale and the physical demands of Dukes' clandestine life, where resources were scarce and walking was the default mode of transport.

The prospect reappears when Dukes trudged "across the river and up the long Kamenostrovsky Prospect to Melnikoff's hospital" after another failed attempt to locate his friend (002-105). The repetition establishes the prospect as a route of anxiety and hope — each journey up its length a negotiation between the possibility of reunion and the reality of the Cheka state.

Perhaps the prospect's most significant appearance is at the Doctor's flat, "one of the big houses at the end of the Kamenostrovsky Prospect," where in January 1919 Dukes sat and learned of the deaths of the German Communist leaders Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxemburg (005-002). The location, at the far end of a long street on the city's watery edge, provides an appropriately remote setting for the Doctor's subversive political analysis.

## Historical Significance

The Kamenostrovsky Prospect was one of the principal arteries of the Petrograd Side, the district that housed many of the city's middle-class and professional residents. In the revolutionary period, its hospitals, apartment blocks, and the relative anonymity of its length made it a natural geography for clandestine activity — far enough from the centre to offer some measure of safety, yet connected enough to serve operational needs.
