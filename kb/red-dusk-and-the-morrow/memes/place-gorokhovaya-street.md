## Overview

No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street was the Petrograd headquarters of the Extraordinary Commission for Combating Counter-Revolution and Sabotage — the Cheka, the Bolshevik secret police. In Paul Dukes' memoir, it stands as the single most feared address in revolutionary Petrograd, a place whose name alone evoked terror among the population. Dukes describes it as "the most notorious of all Bolshevist institutions," an "instrument of terror and inquisition designed forcibly to uproot all anti-Bolshevist sentiment throughout Lenin's dominions" (002-266).

The building at No. 2 Gorokhovaya was simultaneously a prison, an interrogation centre, and an execution site. Dukes' account of it — drawn from first-hand observation, the testimony of prisoners who survived it, and the work of the "Policeman," his informant within the Cheka — provides one of the memoir's most harrowing portraits of Bolshevik institutional violence.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes first learns of No. 2 Gorokhovaya through his contact Marsh, whose wife was imprisoned there. "Mrs. Marsh was imprisoned at the notorious No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street, the address of the Extraordinary Commission," he records, introducing the reader to the building whose name would recur throughout the narrative as a dark refrain (002-127). The journalist Zorinsky provides a characterisation of the place that captures its mythological power among Petrograd residents: "There is one place I avoid, and that is No. 2 Gorokhovaya! When anyone gets arrested I leave him alone" (002-249).

Dukes describes the governance of the building with clinical precision. At its head sits a council "of some half-dozen revolutionary fanatics of the most vehement type," vested with final authority over the fate of prisoners (002-267). Below them operate "Investigators" whose duty is to examine the accused — a bureaucratic euphemism for the machinery of accusation and condemnation that produced summary execution.

The memoir's most devastating passage describes the "terror-hour" at No. 2 Gorokhovaya, the moment each evening when the heavy folding doors opened and prisoners' names were called: "Faces set, white and still, fixed on the heavy folding-door. When it creaked every figure became a statue, a death-statue, stone-livid, breathless, dead in life" (003-156). Those whose names were called "passed out into eternity," their executions conducted in the cellars of the building (003-155).

Dukes was able to navigate this world because of the "Policeman," a Cheka insider who supplied information about prisoners' cases. Through this informant, Dukes learned the status of Madame Marsh, Melnikoff, and others held at Gorokhovaya, receiving regular updates about their transfers, prospects, and the internal politics of the Commission (003-141).

## Description

Dukes renders the physical atmosphere of No. 2 Gorokhovaya in terms of darkness, silence, and dread. The building's interior is "a dingy" space of "bare stairs and passages," its "sombre, sorrow-laden gloom" most intense on December afternoons "when dusk is sinking into darkness" (003-154). The contrast between the mundane exterior of a Petrograd street address and the horror within reflects Dukes' broader theme of how the Bolshevik regime concealed its violence behind bureaucratic facades.
