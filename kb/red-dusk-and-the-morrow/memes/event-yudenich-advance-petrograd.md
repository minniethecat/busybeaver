## Overview

In the autumn of 1919, General Nikolai Yudenich led a White Army offensive from Estonia toward Petrograd that reached the city's outskirts before being repulsed. Paul Dukes, who was in Petrograd at the time, records the Bolsheviks' panic and the destruction that accompanied their preparations to defend the city. The offensive was one of the closest calls the Bolshevik regime faced during the Civil War.

## Background

By 1919, the Civil War had entered its most critical phase. Admiral Kolchak's forces in Siberia and General Denikin's in South Russia had been stopped, but Yudenich's Northwestern Army, supplied by the British and supported by Estonian national forces, represented a direct threat to Petrograd. The Bolshevik leadership scrambled to organise the city's defence, with Trotsky taking personal command. Dukes, ostensibly a Red Army soldier but really a British intelligence agent, was in an extraordinary position to observe both sides.

## Events

The approach of Yudenich's forces threw Petrograd into crisis. Dukes describes how the Bolsheviks prepared to evacuate: "at the time of the last advance of General Yudenich on Petrograd the Bolshevist Government was preparing to move to Vologda" (P01-005). The defence involved the "destruction of strategic points surrounding Petrograd" as the Bolsheviks prepared for street fighting (010-007). Factories were evacuated and the workforce panicked.

Dukes notes that the Bolsheviks were terrified of internal revolt coinciding with the White advance. Troops were "urgently required to suppress uprisings which were feared in the capital" (010-008), but "only troops indigenous to Petrograd or Moscow could be relied upon to fire on their townspeople" (010-008). This admission — that the Bolsheviks could only trust soldiers from outside the city to shoot their fellow citizens — underscores the regime's lack of popular support at a moment of maximum danger.

## Aftermath

Yudenich's offensive failed, but the reasons illuminate the weakness of the White cause. Dukes observes that the White generals "suffer from the same defect as Wrangel, Denikin, and Kolchak, in that they are not politicians" (011-082). Military success could not compensate for political failure: the Whites failed to offer a credible alternative to either Bolshevik terror or the democratic promises of the March Revolution. Yudenich himself escaped to Estonia and then into exile. The Bolsheviks, having survived, intensified both the terror and the military build-up that would enable them to defeat Wrangel the following year.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' account of the Yudenich offensive is characteristically balanced. As a British intelligence officer, he had reason to hope for the success of the White armies, which the British government supported. But he does not present the offensive as a lost opportunity; rather, he uses it to illustrate structural weaknesses in the anti-Bolshevik cause that doomed all of the White campaigns. The passage about the Bolsheviks trusting only non-Petrograd troops to shoot Petrograd citizens is telling — Dukes understood that the regime's weakness and its ruthlessness were two sides of the same coin.
