## Overview

The [[Communist Party]] of [[Russia]], originally the [[Bolshevik]] faction of the [[Russian Social Democratic Labour Party]], was the revolutionary political party led by [[Vladimir Lenin]] that seized power in the [[October Revolution]] of 1917. It established a single-party dictatorship, suppressed all opposition, and sought to transform Russian society according to [[Marxist-Leninist]] ideology. By the period Dukes describes, it had become a privileged elite whose membership conferred material advantages and political immunity.

## Structure and Role

The Communist Party exercised absolute political control over all branches of administrative activity in [[Soviet Russia]]. As popular discontent grew in 1918, the Party tightened its grip — people's cooperative societies and trade unions were "gradually being deprived of their liberties and independence" and the "boss" system under Communist bosses was being introduced (004-012). Party membership was a prerequisite for holding responsible office: every official of the [[Extraordinary Commission]] was required to be a Party member (002-269). *Pravda* served as "the official Press organ of the Communist Party" (004-087). The Party operated as a hierarchical vanguard organisation, with figures like [[Karl Marx]] and Engels revered as "the Moses and Aaron of the Communist Party" in the Bolshevik hagiography (005-003).

## Activities

The Party systematically privileged its members at the expense of the general population. Membership was a route to material comfort in a society of scarcity: one could "get plenty of everything" even without joining the Party, as [[Zorinsky]] told Dukes, though "that door" remained open (002-260). Communists acting as [[house committee]] members confiscated surplus furniture ostensibly for workers, but it "generally went to decorate the rooms of members of the committee or groups of Communists" (006-030). Through its network of Party organisations, it maintained surveillance and discipline: Dukes' [[Red Army]] commander received commendations "through the Communist Party grapevine" while privately despairing of the system (010-008).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes captured the gulf between the Communist Party and ordinary [[Russians]] with striking clarity:

> The gulf between 'the party' and the people yawns at every step. (012-005)

His companion Zorinsky, though not a Party member, kept "that door open" and cynically described the Communists as a class primed to "smash a jaw now and again" when dealing with the rebellious [[peasantry]] (002-261). Dukes noted the extraordinary ideological devotion of Party members, comparing it to Russians' traditional "veneration of icons" (005-003). Yet he also recorded the regime's reluctant admission that "political 'soundness' ... was a bad substitute for administrative ability" and its gradual replacement of the "premium on ignorance" with "a premium on intelligence and training" (004-013).

## Historical Context

The Communist Party traced its origins to the 1903 split of the [[Russian Social Democratic Labour Party]] into Bolshevik (majority) and [[Menshevik]] (minority) factions. After seizing power in 1917, the Bolsheviks dissolved the [[Constituent Assembly]], banned opposition parties, and established the one-party state that would endure for seven decades. The Party's relationship with the Soviet state was deliberately ambiguous: Dukes noted that "though technically not interchangeable, the terms Soviet Government, Third International, Communist Party and their executives often are in practice" (012-008).
