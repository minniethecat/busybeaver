## Overview

The State Duma was the elected legislative assembly of the Russian Empire, established by Nicholas II in the wake of the 1905 Revolution and dissolved by the Bolsheviks in October 1917. For eleven tumultuous years, the Duma was the arena of Russian parliamentary politics — the forum where Milyukov denounced Rasputin, where Kerensky rose to prominence, and where the February Revolution's Provisional Government claimed its mandate.

Dukes' references to the Duma are suffused with elegy. The Tauride Palace, where "Duma members had flitted silently" (001-006), becomes the seat of the Petrograd Soviet and later the "Palace of Uritsky." The physical appropriation of parliamentary space is, for Dukes, the visible symbol of a deeper transformation: the replacement of representative politics with commissar rule. "I pictured to myself," he writes of visiting the renamed palace, "the vaulted lobby where Duma members had flitted silently, now thronged by soldiers and commissars" (012-063, narrative paraphrase).

## Historical Context

The Duma's history was one of frustrated constitutionalism. The First Duma (1906) was dissolved after 73 days for radicalism. The Second (1907) lasted 103 days before being dissolved on manufactured charges of sedition. Nicholas II then altered the electoral law to produce a more conservative Third Duma (1907–1912) and Fourth Duma (1912–1917). The Fourth Duma's Progressive Bloc became the parliamentary voice of liberal opposition to the Tsarist autocracy, and it was Duma deputies who formed the nucleus of the Provisional Government after the February Revolution. The Bolsheviks, who had boycotted the First Duma and used the Fourth only as a propaganda platform, formally dissolved the body by decree in October 1917.

## In Dukes' Account

> The surging throngs surged round the Duma building at the Tauride Palace, where the revolution had found its voice and where, a few months later, that voice would be silenced. (001-005, condensed paraphrase)

Dukes' treatment of the Duma is spatial and symbolic. The Tauride Palace, rather than the institution itself, carries the emotional weight. The Duma building is the stage on which revolutionary drama unfolds — first as the locus of democratic hope, then as the site of its extinguishment.

> I pictured to myself, as I entered the Palace of Uritsky, the vaulted lobby where Duma members had flitted silently, where Milyukov had made his famous speech against Rasputin — now echoing to the boots of Red Armymen and the commands of commissars. (012-063, narrative paraphrase)

The transformation of the Tauride Palace from Duma chamber to "Palace of Uritsky" — named for a murdered Cheka commissar — is for Dukes the revolution in miniature: parliamentary deliberation replaced by terror, elected representatives by appointed commissars, debate by decree.
