## Overview

The Nevsky Prospect is the most famous thoroughfare in St. Petersburg (Petrograd), planned by Peter the Great as the central artery of his new capital. In Paul Dukes' memoir, it serves as both the geographical spine of revolutionary Petrograd and a stage upon which the drama of the Bolshevik era unfolds — from the tense silence of the revolution's outbreak to the gaudy promenades of the regime's new elite.

Dukes' first description of the Nevsky Prospect comes on 11 March 1917, the eve of the February Revolution: "The snow glittered brilliantly in the frosty sunshine. The Nevsky Prospect was almost deserted. The air was tense with excitement" (001-002). The deserted avenue, suspended between the old imperial order and the approaching chaos, functions as a visual metaphor for the revolution itself.

## In Dukes' Account

The Nevsky Prospect appears in the memoir's opening pages as the setting for the revolution's first violent acts. Dukes describes corpses lying in the street after the morning's fighting — "How still those corpses lay over there! Their teeth grinned ghastlily" (001-003) — and a "cordon of police disguised as soldiers, prostrate, firing at intervals" stretched across the wide roadway (001-004). These images establish the revolutionary violence that would define the city for years to come.

After returning to Petrograd undercover, Dukes observes the Nevsky Prospect transformed. The elegant promenade of the imperial era now hosts "fat-faced commissars in leathern jackets with three or four revolvers in their belts" and "sailors with gold watch-chains and rings, with their prostitutes promenading the Nevsky" (003-091). This juxtaposition of revolutionary pretension with ostentatious corruption captures Dukes' critique of the new regime's hypocrisy.

The Nevsky is also a transit corridor for Dukes' operational movements. Crossing the Liteyny Bridge to enter the city, he would pause "to contemplate the marvellous view of the river Neva," noting that "no capital in Europe possesses so beautiful an expanse of water as this city of Peter the Great" (002-013).

## Description

Dukes renders the Nevsky Prospect not merely as a street but as a stage for the human drama of revolution. The wide roadway, the magnificent vista stretching towards the Admiralty spire, the contrast between architectural splendour and revolutionary squalor — all are captured in his account. The transformation of the avenue from the deserted, snow-covered expanse of March 1917 to the corrupt promenade of 1918–1919 charts the degeneration of the revolution itself.

## Historical Significance

As the main artery of imperial St. Petersburg, the Nevsky Prospect was both a symbol of Russian modernity and a political stage. Throughout the revolutionary period, it was the site of demonstrations, parades, and confrontations between the people and the authorities. Dukes' account captures this liminal moment when one of Europe's great boulevards became a theatre for the collapse of an empire.
