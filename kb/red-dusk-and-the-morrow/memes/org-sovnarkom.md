## Overview

The Soviet of People's Commissars (Sovnarkom) was the executive cabinet of the Russian Soviet Republic, established by the Second All-Russian Congress of Soviets on 8 November 1917, the day after the Bolshevik seizure of power. Chaired by Lenin, with Trotsky as Commissar for Foreign Affairs and Stalin as Commissar for Nationalities, Sovnarkom was the institution through which the Bolsheviks governed — issuing decrees, directing the Cheka, managing the war economy, and conducting foreign policy.

Dukes does not describe Sovnarkom as a formal institution. He encounters it through its effects: the decrees that reshape daily life, the commissars who wield arbitrary authority, and the bureaucratic apparatus that employs him under his Krylenko alias. The memoir's portrait of Bolshevik governance is not a study of cabinet government but of how executive power, concentrated in a few hands and enforced by terror, transformed Russian society.

## Structure and Role

Sovnarkom operated as a council of commissars, each heading a commissariat (ministry) responsible for a specific domain — foreign affairs, war, internal affairs, justice, agriculture, and so on. In theory it was accountable to the All-Russian Congress of Soviets and its Central Executive Committee; in practice, it was Lenin's instrument. The Cheka, though formally under the Commissariat of Justice, reported directly to the Party leadership.

Dukes' narrative captures the fragmentation masked by this formal structure. The Extraordinary Commissar he encounters at the frontier station (001-079) bears credentials from the Central Executive Committee, not Sovnarkom — yet his authority is absolute. The commissar at Bieloostrov (001-075) operates under his own imprimatur. Dukes' experience of Bolshevik administration is of overlapping, competing jurisdictions in which formal hierarchy matters less than proximity to power.

## In Dukes' Account

> Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee of the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies — the title, emblazoned across the document that the frontier commissar brandished, summed up the tangle of jurisdictions through which the Soviet state governed. (001-079, narrative paraphrase)

Dukes' encounter with Soviet officialdom is mediated through documents — identity papers, commissar credentials, travel permits. The bureaucratic apparatus is both his enemy (it can expose him) and his shield (forged papers protect him). The memoir's most vivid portrait of Sovnarkom's government is the passport control booth at Bieloostrov, where a single commissar's whim can mean life or death.

> The Bolshevik Government, through its Extraordinary Commission, was busily engaged in what it described as the "liquidation" of its opponents. (001-063, condensed)

Sovnarkom's responsibility for the Cheka's terror is indirect but real. Dukes does not distinguish between the government and its security apparatus — the two are, in his account, effectively identical.

## Historical Context

Sovnarkom governed Russia through civil war, famine, and the construction of a one-party state. Its decrees nationalised industry, abolished private property in land, created the Red Army, established the Cheka, and withdrew Russia from the First World War through the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk. The body was renamed the Council of Ministers in 1946, but its essential character — the concentration of executive authority in a party-dominated cabinet — persisted throughout the Soviet period.
