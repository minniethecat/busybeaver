## Overview

"The Policeman" was the nickname Dukes and Marsh used for Alexei Fomitch, a former Tsarist official who, under Bolshevik rule, had transformed himself into a fixer and intermediary operating at the margins of the Cheka's prison system. "My first impulse when I saw the individual Marsh nicknamed 'the Policeman' was to laugh," Dukes writes, "for anyone less like a policeman than the little man who rose and bowed I have seldom seen. I will not describe him too precisely, but he was short, red-faced, and insignificant-looking" (002-177). The physical description -- a small, unprepossessing man with an exaggerated sense of his own importance -- captures the peculiar character of the wartime Petrograd underworld.

The Policeman represents an entire class of survivors in Dukes' Petrograd: former Tsarist functionaries who had adapted their administrative skills to the service of the new regime, or, more precisely, to the service of whoever could pay. He operated at the intersection of the Cheka's bureaucracy and the black market in favours and information, a position that made him simultaneously indispensable and deeply suspect. His unctuous manner, his oily self-deprecation, and his transparent avarice make him one of the memoir's most memorable minor characters.

## Role in the Narrative

The Policeman appears in Chapter II as Marsh's contact for information about the prison system. Marsh introduces him as a Tsarist official who now works as a scribe in the Department of Public Works but retains connections throughout the Cheka's detention apparatus. His function is to provide intelligence about prisoners -- their location, conditions, and the possibilities (and costs) of securing their release.

His report on Mrs. Marsh's situation is characteristically precise and characteristically mercenary. "Madame Marsh is incarcerated in chamber No. 4 with thirty-eight other women of various station, including titled personages, servant girls, and prostitutes. The chamber is not a large one and I fear the conditions are far from pleasant" (002-179). The bureaucratic precision -- chamber number, inmate count, social categories -- is undercut by the studied understatement of "far from pleasant."

When Marsh asks whether the interrogators might be bribed, the Policeman's response is a masterpiece of corrupt circumlocution: "Yes, that is often so. The investigator can frequently be induced to turn the evidence in favour of the accused. But in this case it is unfortunately useless to offer the usual bribe, for even if Madame Marsh's innocence is proven to the hilt, she will still be detained as a hostage" (002-181). The door to bribery is kept open even as its immediate utility is denied.

The financial negotiation that follows is pure theatre. "I hesitate to refer to such a matter -- but I am afraid this method may be a little more -- er -- costly. Pardon me for --" (002-183). When Marsh interrupts, the Policeman affects indignation: "Oh, Monsieur Marsh, you are well aware that I take nothing for myself. I do this out of friendship to you -- and our gallant allies. But there is a prison janitor, I must give him 5,000, two warders 10,000, a go-between 2,000, odd expenses --" (002-185). The itemised list of bribes, delivered with the pretence of selfless service, is quintessential Policeman.

The Policeman also serves Dukes directly in the Melnikoff case. When Zorinsky's evasions become intolerable, Dukes turns to the Policeman for independent information. The result is the memoir's most devastating revelation: "Dmitri Dmitrievitch Melnikoff. Real name Nicholas Nicholaievitch N. He was shot between the 15th and 20th of January" (005-104, 005-106). The delivery -- from a strip of paper, in a flat voice, by a man whose entire livelihood depends on the continuation of the system that does the shooting -- encapsulates the moral universe of Dukes' Petrograd.

## Biography

The Policeman's real name was Alexei Fomitch. He had been a Tsarist official before the revolution and had found employment under the Bolsheviks as a scribe in the Department of Public Works. His home was the flat of an ex-journalist, Dmitri Konstantinovitch, where he received visitors through a coded system of rings at the door: "Give three short rings, and I will open the door myself" (002-209). He maintained a network of informants and contacts throughout the prison system, charging substantial fees for information and services while insisting he took "nothing for myself."

## In Dukes' Account

> "Yes, that is often so," continued the Policeman in a tone of feigned consolation. "The investigator can frequently be induced to turn the evidence in favour of the accused. But in this case it is unfortunately useless to offer the usual bribe, for even if Madame Marsh's innocence is proven to the hilt, she will still be detained as a hostage until the discovery of Monsieur Marsh." (002-181)

The Policeman's language is a study in the rhetoric of powerlessness. "Feigned consolation," "unfortunately useless," "proven to the hilt" -- every phrase performs sympathy while conveying impossibility. The effect is to naturalise the Cheka's arbitrary cruelty as an immutable fact of life, against which only the Policeman's special ministrations can offer any hope.

> "He was shot between the 15th and 20th of January," said the Policeman. (005-106)

The laconic delivery of Melnikoff's death notice, stripped of all the Policeman's characteristic unction, is one of the memoir's most chilling moments. Stripped of context, stripped of emotion, the sentence simply states a fact -- and in doing so, reveals everything about the world in which Dukes operated.
