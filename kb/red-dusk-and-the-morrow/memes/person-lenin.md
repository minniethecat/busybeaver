## Overview

Vladimir Ilyich Lenin (born Ulyanov, 1870--1924) was the founder of the Bolshevik Party and the principal architect of the October Revolution of 1917. As Chairman of the Council of People's Commissars, he became the de facto dictator of Soviet Russia, presiding over the establishment of a one-party state, the Red Terror, and the Russian Civil War. In Paul Dukes' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*, Lenin appears less as a personally encountered figure than as the ideological force animating the entire Bolshevik apparatus -- a presence felt in every institution, every commissar, and every policy Dukes observed during his undercover mission in Petrograd from 1917 to 1919.

Dukes' portrait of Lenin is that of a remote but all-pervading authority. The Extraordinary Commission (Cheka) is described as operating "throughout Lenin's dominions" (002-266), while the author notes that it was "Germany, who despatched the proletarian dictator Lenin and his satellites to Russia" that "discovered the Achilles heel of the Russian revolution" (001-007). Dukes never claims to have met Lenin personally, but his narrative makes clear that every aspect of Bolshevik governance -- from the abolition of free speech to the institutionalisation of class war -- flowed from Lenin's ideological direction. The polemical tone is unmistakable: Lenin's system, in Dukes' view, crushed the genuine democratic aspirations of the March 1917 revolution.

## Role in the Narrative

Although Lenin never appears as a direct character in Dukes' memoir, his presence looms over every chapter. The Extraordinary Commission -- the Cheka, which Dukes describes in horrified detail -- existed "to uproot all anti-Bolshevist sentiment throughout Lenin's dominions" (002-266). His name is invoked at public demonstrations, his pamphlets litter the streets of Petrograd alongside those of Zinoviev (002-028), and his model of Soviet government is held up as the template for world revolution.

Dukes' informants speak of Lenin's system with a mixture of fear and contempt. Zorinsky, the double-dealing intermediary, voices the common anti-Semitic canard of the queues: "Where are all the Jews? Does Trotsky stand in a queue?" (002-104), while the Doctor's philosophical friend Shura articulates a deeper critique of the Bolshevik project Lenin initiated -- that its impulse "is not love of the worker, but hatred of the bourgeois" (005-009). Dukes himself locates Lenin's fatal miscalculation in the idea that class identity could supersede national identity, arguing that the gulf between party and people can only be bridged by Communists who "cease to be first Communists and then Russians, and to become Russians and nothing else" (014-004).

## Biography

Born in Simbirsk in 1870, Lenin was radicalised by the execution of his elder brother Alexander for a plot against Tsar Alexander III. He became a Marxist while studying law at Kazan University, and by 1903 had led the split of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party into Bolshevik and Menshevik factions. After years of exile in Western Europe, he was transported through Germany in a sealed train in April 1917 -- the German High Command's calculated gamble to destabilise Russia's war effort, which Dukes references when he writes of "Germany, who despatched the proletarian dictator Lenin and his satellites to Russia" (001-007).

Lenin seized power in the October Revolution of November 1917 and immediately set about dismantling the Provisional Government's democratic institutions, establishing instead the soviets as organs of Bolshevik control. He weathered the Civil War (1918--1921), surviving an assassination attempt in 1918, and introduced the New Economic Policy in 1921 as a tactical retreat from war communism -- a flexibility that Dukes acknowledges grudgingly, noting that despite "subsequent casuistic tortuosities," Lenin demonstrated "the ability to swim against the stream" (014-004). Lenin died in January 1924 after a series of strokes.

## In Dukes' Account

> Truly had Russia's foe, Germany, who despatched the proletarian dictator Lenin and his satellites to Russia, discovered the Achilles heel of the Russian revolution! (001-007)

Dukes' framing of Lenin's return to Russia as a German conspiracy sets the tone for the entire memoir. The Bolshevik leader is portrayed as a foreign imposition on the Russian people rather than an organic product of their revolution. This argument recurs when Dukes discusses the Extraordinary Commission -- an institution "designed forcibly to uproot all anti-Bolshevist sentiment throughout Lenin's dominions" (002-266) -- and when he contrasts Lenin's internationalist ideology with what he calls "Russianism," the deeper national character he believes will ultimately reassert itself.

The concluding chapter offers Dukes' most measured assessment: while the Third International's adherents "possess none of the greatness of their master," Lenin himself demonstrated "the ability to swim against the stream" (014-004). This backhanded compliment reflects the memoir's uneasy recognition that Lenin, whatever his faults, possessed political gifts his successors lacked.
