## Overview

The [[Bolshevist coup d'état]] of November 1917 (October by the old Russian calendar) was the armed overthrow of the [[Provisional Government]] by [[Lenin]]'s [[Bolshevik Party]]. [[Paul Dukes]] consistently refers to this event not as a "revolution" but as a "coup d'état," emphasising its character as a minority seizure of power rather than a popular movement. His memoir covers the period roughly a year after the coup, by which time the Bolsheviks had consolidated their hold on the cities while facing growing resistance across the country.

## Background

Following the [[March Revolution]], the Provisional Government under [[Alexander Kerensky]] struggled to maintain authority while continuing Russia's participation in the Great War. The Bolsheviks, led by Lenin, agitated for "peace, land, and bread." Dukes emphasises that the Bolsheviks did not represent the Russian people:

> History scarcely knows a more flagrant misnomer than that of "government of workers and peasants." In the first place, the Bolshevist Government consists not of workers and peasants, but of typical intellectuals. In the second, its policy is categorically repudiated by practically the entire Russian nation, and it keeps in the saddle only by bullying the workers and peasants by whom it purports to have been elected. (012-003)

The coup itself was planned and executed by a small, disciplined party that seized the instruments of coercion and propaganda.

## Events

The Bolsheviks seized key points in Petrograd on the night of 6–7 November 1917 (24–25 October O.S.). The [[Winter Palace]] was captured, and the Provisional Government ministers were arrested. Dukes does not narrate the coup directly — he was writing about its aftermath — but his memoir is drenched in its consequences. Throughout Petrograd, the traces of it were visible:

> Strung across the street from house to house were the shreds of washed-out red flags, with inscriptions that showed they had been hung out a few weeks earlier to celebrate the anniversary of the Bolshevist coup d'état. (002-006)

Dukes describes the political landscape after the coup: "the Bolshevist Government had already secured a monopoly of the Press by closing down all journals expressing opinions antagonistic to them, so that all that was printed was propaganda" (002-029). The constituent assembly elected in free voting was soon dissolved by the Bolsheviks.

## Aftermath

The immediate aftermath was civil war. Zinoviev, who "shirked office in November, 1917, fearing the instability of the Bolshevist coup," later became "chief advocate of all the insaner aspects of Bolshevism" (005-037). By December 1918, the [[Red Terror]] was fully established, and Dukes, now working for [[British intelligence]], found himself navigating a regime that regarded all its former allies as enemies.

> But just when the people, after the Bolshevist coup d'état, were beginning to demand reason in public utterance and deeds instead of promises, down came a super-Tsarist Bolshevist censorship like a huge candle-snuffer and clapping itself on the flame of public criticism, snuffed it out altogether. (005-050)

Dukes notes that "the most important event since the Bolshevist coup d'état in November, 1917" was the regime's retreat from full communism to a partial restoration of private trade (012-027), suggesting that the coup's revolutionary programme proved unworkable.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes returned to Russia about a year after the coup and his entire memoir is an account of life under the regime it created. His choice of terminology — consistently "coup d'état" rather than "revolution" — signals his view that the Bolshevik takeover was an illegitimate seizure of power. He witnessed the first revolutionary regiments emerge in March 1917 and later observed the regime that the November coup had installed, giving him a unique comparative perspective on the two revolutions of 1917.
