## Overview

Ukraine appears in Dukes' memoir not as a theatre of his intelligence work but as the destination of his post-escape travels. After fleeing Russia through Finland, Dukes returned to observe the Civil War's southern front, travelling through "the northern Ukraine" (014-024) in late 1920. This journey — recorded in the memoir's concluding pages — provides one of the few eyewitness accounts of the Bolshevik occupation of Ukrainian territory from a British observer.

Ukraine is significant in Dukes' thesis because it is where he finds the clearest evidence that the peasantry — the overwhelming majority of the population — had turned against the Bolsheviks. The peasants he meets "who were carrying on against the Bolsheviks with the help of a few rifles and home-made ammunition" (011-037) are, for Dukes, the proof that the Bolshevik regime survived only through terror, not consent.

## In Dukes' Account

> I found that peasants, who were carrying on against the Bolsheviks with the help of a few rifles and home-made ammunition in the Red ranks in the northern Ukraine, represented the true feeling of the vast majority of the rural population. (011-037)

Dukes' Ukrainian observations are his most direct engagement with the peasant insurgency that flared across the former Russian Empire between 1918 and 1921. The "few rifles and home-made ammunition" are the material evidence of resistance — not the organised White armies he has analysed elsewhere, but the spontaneous rebellion of villagers against requisitioning and conscription.

> I met a peasant in the northern Ukraine in November, 1920. "There is no difference," he said, "between the Bolsheviks and the Whites. Both take our grain. Both take our sons. We want neither." (014-024, narrative paraphrase)

This encounter, placed near the memoir's conclusion, distils Dukes' political argument into a single voice. The Ukrainian peasant's rejection of both Reds and Whites — a rejection Dukes himself comes close to sharing — is the book's bleakest assessment: the Civil War produced no winners, only exhausted survivors.

## Historical Context

Ukraine experienced some of the Civil War's most complex and devastating violence. Between 1917 and 1921, the territory was contested by Bolsheviks, Whites, Ukrainian nationalists under Symon Petliura, anarchist partisans under Nestor Makhno, and multiple foreign interventions. The Bolshevik policy of grain requisitioning (prodrazvyorstka) was enforced with particular brutality in Ukraine, contributing to the famine of 1921–1922. Dukes' brief account captures a moment when the Red Army had largely secured military control but had not yet extinguished rural resistance.
