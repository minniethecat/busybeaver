# Mensheviks

## Overview

The Mensheviks (from the Russian *menshinstvo*, meaning "minority") were the moderate faction of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, emerging from the 1903 split that also produced Lenin's Bolshevik ("majority") faction. Unlike the Bolsheviks, the Mensheviks advocated a mass-based party, gradual democratic reform, and a transitional period between the bourgeois-democratic and socialist revolutions. By the period Dukes describes, they had been reduced from a major political force to a persecuted opposition, routinely blamed by Bolshevik propaganda for every failure of the Soviet regime.

## Structure and Role

The Mensheviks operated as a democratic socialist party committed to orthodox Marxist doctrine that Russia must pass through a capitalist stage before socialism could be achieved. They participated in the soviets and trade unions alongside Bolsheviks and Socialist Revolutionaries, and after the October Revolution they initially sought to function as a legal opposition. Their leadership included figures such as Julius Martov, though by the Civil War period most prominent Mensheviks had been imprisoned, exiled, or driven underground. Bolshevik propaganda systematically conflated them with counterrevolutionary forces: Dukes recorded Lenin blaming workers' strikes on "the vicious Mensheviks and Socialist-Revolutionaries" who had "agitated in the factories, and persuaded the workers to down tools" (012-021).

## Activities

Under Bolshevik rule, the Mensheviks were progressively stripped of legal existence. Their newspapers were suppressed, their leaders arrested, and their participation in soviet elections became a formality that served Bolshevik propaganda purposes. Dukes observed that when workers protested the food commissariat's failures, the Bolsheviks' response was to denounce the "pernicious agitation of Mensheviks and Socialist-Revolutionaries" (011-066) rather than address the grievances. The Mensheviks became the regime's preferred scapegoat: every instance of popular discontent was attributed to "the vicious Mensheviks" poisoning the minds of otherwise loyal workers. The Party even invited nominal Menshevik participation in soviet debates as a controlled spectacle, as when an "ex-Menshevik now converted to Communism" was paraded before the Petrograd Soviet to testify to Bolshevik superiority (012-082).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes encountered the Mensheviks primarily through the lens of Bolshevik propaganda rather than direct contact. He documented the systematic demonisation of Mensheviks in official discourse, noting how "the pernicious agitation of Mensheviks and Socialist-Revolutionaries" was the standard explanation offered for every instance of worker unrest (011-066). During the 1920 food crisis, Lenin "spoke mainly of tactics," denouncing "the vicious Mensheviks and Socialist-Revolutionaries" who had "agitated in the factories" — when the workers' real complaint was simply hunger (012-021). Dukes' account reveals how thoroughly the Bolsheviks had succeeded in making "Menshevik" a term of abuse, even as they opportunistically recruited individual ex-Mensheviks when it served Party interests.

## Historical Context

The 1903 split at the Second Congress of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party produced the Bolshevik-Menshevik division that would define Russian Marxism for two decades. While the Bolsheviks seized power in 1917, the Mensheviks won significant support in the elections to the Constituent Assembly, obtaining more votes than the Bolsheviks in several regions. Their opposition to Bolshevik dictatorship led to their systematic suppression: by 1921, the Menshevik Party had been effectively destroyed as an organised force within Russia, though it continued in exile. The Menshevik critique — that Russia lacked the economic preconditions for a socialist revolution and that Bolshevik methods would produce a bureaucratic tyranny — proved prophetic.
