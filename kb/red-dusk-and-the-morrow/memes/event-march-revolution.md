## Overview

The March Revolution (February in the Julian calendar then used in Russia) was the first of the two Russian revolutions of 1917, resulting in the abdication of Tsar Nicholas II. Paul Dukes was present in Petrograd when the revolution broke out and recorded his observations with vivid immediacy. For Dukes, the revolution represented the "Declaration of Independence of Russia" — a moment of genuine popular hope that was soon to be betrayed.

## Background

By March 1917, Russia had endured nearly three years of disastrous war with Germany, massive casualties, food shortages, and industrial collapse. Strikes had been spreading through Petrograd's factories. Dukes describes the city on the afternoon of 11 March: "The air was tense with excitement and it seemed as if from the girdling faubourgs of the beautiful city of Peter the Great rose a low, muffled rumbling as of many voices" (001-002). The Tsarist police had already used gunfire against demonstrators; "there were bloodstains on the white snow" (001-002). A cordon of police disguised as soldiers lay prostrate, firing at intervals, because "it was known that the soldiers sided with the people" (001-004).

## Events

The following day, 12 March, the revolution broke out in full. Dukes records: "I saw the first revolutionary regiments come out and witnessed the sacking of the arsenal by the infuriated mob. Over the river the soldiers were breaking into the Kresty Prison" (001-005). A "mighty murmur" swept the city, "whispered in awe on a million lips: 'Revolution!'" (001-005). The Tauride Palace, home of the Duma, became the headquarters of the insurrection — "crushing throngs surged round the Duma building" (001-005). By nightfall, the Tsarist police had been scattered from the Nevsky Prospect.

That night, Dukes entered the Tauride Palace, which "had become the centre of the revolution" (001-006). Inside, "tired soldiers lay sleeping in heaps in every hall and corridor," and the building was transformed into "a revolutionary arsenal" (001-006). The revolution swept away the monarchy: Nicholas II "signed the deed of abdication" (014-015) and the Provisional Government was formed.

## Aftermath

Dukes looks back on the revolution with a mixture of pride and bitterness. "Everyone now knows how the hopes of freedom were blighted," he writes. Germany had "discovered the Achilles heel of the Russian revolution" by despatching "the proletarian dictator Lenin and his satellites to Russia" (001-007). Dukes' memoir picks up the story a year later, by which time the "flowers of the revolution" had "withered under the blast of the Class War" (001-007).

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes was in Petrograd throughout the March Revolution and his account is that of a participant rather than an outside observer. He not only witnessed the crowds but actually infiltrated the Tauride Palace by scrambling over the railings. In the memoir, the revolution serves as the pivotal rupture that makes everything else possible: it is the moment when the "huge pendulum, weighted with the pent-up miseries and woes of a hundred and eighty millions of people," was set in motion (001-005). The question of "how far would it swing" frames the entire book (001-005).
