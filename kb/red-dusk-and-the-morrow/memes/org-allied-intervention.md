# Allied Intervention

## Overview

The Allied Intervention was the deployment of British, French, American, Japanese, and other Allied military forces to various regions of the former Russian Empire between 1918 and 1920. Ostensibly aimed at protecting Allied war material, securing the exit of the Czechoslovak Legion, and preventing German exploitation of Russian resources, the intervention evolved into de facto support for anti-Bolshevik forces. Paul Dukes, as a British intelligence officer operating within Bolshevik Russia, had a unique perspective on the gap between the intervention's stated aims and its actual effects.

## Structure and Role

The intervention was never a unified command. British forces operated primarily in the north (Arkhangelsk and Murmansk) and the Caucasus, the French in Ukraine and the Black Sea, the Japanese and Americans in the Russian Far East, and smaller contingents from other Allied nations in various theatres. Coordination between these forces was limited, and their mandates were often contradictory. Dukes, operating under Foreign Office authority, was part of the intelligence dimension of this intervention — gathering information that would inform British policy decisions about the level and nature of support to provide anti-Bolshevik forces.

## Activities

The intervention forces engaged in limited combat with Red Army units, provided material support to White armies, and attempted to maintain supply lines. However, the intervention's most significant effect, in Dukes' analysis, was precisely the opposite of what was intended. Soldiers sent to fight the Bolsheviks were subjected to propaganda and not always reliable: Dukes noted with irony that "through the Communist Party grapevine" his Red Army commander received commendations, while Allied intervention served as the Bolsheviks' most effective recruiting tool (010-008). The Allies' presence allowed the Bolsheviks to portray themselves as defenders of Russian national sovereignty against foreign invaders.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes encountered the effects of the Allied intervention obliquely but consistently. Rumours of Allied advances circulated constantly among the population: "Now Kronstadt has been taken, now the Allies are in Finland, and so on," as one acquaintance told him, adding cynically, "Personally, I believe they will bungle everything" (003-089). The Bolsheviks exploited Allied intervention extensively in their propaganda, as Zinoviev demonstrated when he assured Red Army recruits they would fight "against capitalists, imperialists, bankers, generals, landlords, priests" — a list in which the Allied powers were unmistakably the intended referents of the first three terms (011-015). Dukes observed that the intervention's failure was not merely military but political, rooted in the Allies' inability to offer a compelling alternative to Bolshevism.

## Historical Context

The Allied intervention was authorised at the Supreme War Council in 1918 in the context of the ongoing war with Germany. The Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, by which Soviet Russia had made a separate peace with the Central Powers, provided the legal pretext for intervention. After the Armistice of November 1918, the intervention's rationale shifted from containing Germany to containing Bolshevism — a mission for which Allied publics had little appetite after four years of war. By 1920, all Allied forces had been withdrawn, having achieved neither their military objectives nor the political transformation of Russia.
