## Overview

The [[Petrograd Soviet]] of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies was the governing council of [[Petrograd]] during the revolutionary period, established in the wake of the [[March Revolution|February Revolution]] of 1917. It functioned as a parallel authority to the [[Provisional Government]] and later became an instrument of [[Bolshevik]] rule. Under the presidency of [[Grigory Zinoviev]], it was one of the most important political institutions in early [[Soviet Russia]], and [[Paul Dukes]] encountered its authority directly through the identity documents it issued.

## Structure and Role

The Soviet was formally composed of elected deputies representing workers, soldiers, and later Red Armymen. Its executive arm included the Central Executive Committee and a network of Extraordinary Commissars who wielded broad administrative powers. Dukes received an identity certificate bearing the heading "Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee of the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies," which identified him as an office clerk in its service:

> I was provided with a certificate of identification which stated that I was Joseph Afirenko, an office clerk, under the heading of "Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee of the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies." (001-079)

This document — complete with "signatures and seal" (001-080) — served as his crucial cover identity, protecting him from [[Cheka]] scrutiny.

The Soviet held elections, though by 1919 these were tightly controlled. Dukes recorded that of 1,390 members elected, "a thousand were fully qualified members of the Communist Party, the remaining three hundred and ninety being professed sympathizers, the majority of them candidates who had been elected on the strength of a signed undertaking to follow the exact instructions of the Party" (012-080).

## Activities

The Soviet served as both an administrative and a propaganda platform. Its president, [[Grigory Zinoviev|Zinoviev]], would address gatherings of soldiers and workers at venues like the Narodny Dom:

> He spoke to us for an hour and assured us we were to fight for workmen and peasants against capitalists, imperialists, bankers, generals, landlords, priests, and other bloodsucking riffraff. (011-015)

When the [[Communist Party]] faced a crisis of membership after a purge reduced numbers by over half, it was at "the elections to the Petrograd Soviet" that the Bolsheviks sought to demonstrate popular support, deploying "threats, bribes, terror, and persuasion" to secure favourable results (012-032). Non-Party candidates who won seats found their cooperation grudgingly invited but "under strict control ... of the Communists" (012-082).

Zinoviev simultaneously served as president of the Petrograd Soviet and president of the [[Third International]], personifying the deliberate overlap between Party, government, and international revolutionary institutions:

> In the centre of a group of prominent Communists at the base of the tribune stood Zinoviev, who, besides being president of the Petrograd Soviet, held also the position of president of the Third International, the institution that is to embrace the world. (005-036)

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' direct experience of the Petrograd Soviet was through the bureaucratic apparatus it maintained. The certificate issued in the name of its Extraordinary Commissar gave him a crucial cover identity as "Joseph Afirenko," a talisman of protection in a city where identity documents meant the difference between life and death. Dukes observed the Soviet's transformation from a revolutionary body into an instrument of Party control, noting the Bolsheviks' boastful claim that "the working class of Red Petrograd would remain true to itself" after rigged elections (012-080).

## Historical Context

The Petrograd Soviet was modelled on the 1905 St. Petersburg Soviet and became the prototype for the soviet (council) system that would govern the entire [[Soviet Union]]. Petrograd's status as the "metropolis of the world revolution" made its Soviet particularly significant, especially under Zinoviev's leadership. After Lenin's death, the Petrograd Soviet was renamed the Leningrad Soviet, and its leadership became a key battleground in the power struggles of the 1920s.
