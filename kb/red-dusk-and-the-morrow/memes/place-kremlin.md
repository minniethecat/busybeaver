## Overview

The Moscow Kremlin — the ancient fortified citadel at the heart of the Russian capital — became the seat of the Bolshevik government when Lenin moved the capital from Petrograd to Moscow in March 1918. For Dukes, operating in Petrograd, the Kremlin was physically distant but symbolically omnipresent: it was from the Kremlin that decrees issued, that the Cheka took its orders, and that the "lying soviets" (003-158) were directed.

Dukes mentions the Kremlin only twice, both times in symbolic contexts. A Bolshevik poster in Red Square features "the Kremlin in the background" (011-010), and he reflects that the true voice of Russia is not to be heard "there, in the Kremlin" (003-158). The Kremlin functions in the memoir not as a described place but as a metonym for centralised Bolshevik authority — the invisible hand that directs the visible terror.

## In Dukes' Account

> A poster showing a crowd of peasants and workers gathered in the Red Square with the Kremlin in the background. (011-010, narrative paraphrase)

This brief mention occurs in Dukes' discussion of Bolshevik propaganda posters. The Kremlin, in the poster's visual grammar, represents legitimacy: the ancient seat of Russian power now occupied by the revolution. Dukes' dry description suggests his scepticism: the posters show what the Bolsheviks want Russians to believe, not what is actually happening.

> Not there, in the Kremlin, nor there in the lying soviets, was Russia's voice to be heard. It was to be heard in the quiet, whispered conversations of ordinary people — in the kitchens and back rooms of the Petrograd apartments, in the villages where peasants spoke only when they were sure no commissar was listening. (003-158, narrative paraphrase)

The Kremlin as the antithesis of authentic Russian voice. Dukes contrasts the official Russia of the Kremlin with the "whispered conversations of ordinary people" — the distinction that structures his entire memoir. The Kremlin produces decrees; the people produce survival.

## Description

The Moscow Kremlin is a fortified complex of palaces, cathedrals, and government buildings occupying a triangular promontory overlooking the Moskva River. Its walls and towers date from the late fifteenth century, making it one of the oldest and most symbolically charged sites in Russia. When the Bolsheviks moved the capital to Moscow in March 1918, the Kremlin became the seat of Sovnarkom (the Council of People's Commissars) and Lenin's personal residence — a transformation that Dukes notes without describing: the ancient heart of Russian state power was now occupied by a regime that claimed to have abolished the state.

## Historical Significance

The Bolshevik decision to move the capital from Petrograd to Moscow was driven by military necessity — the German advance in early 1918 threatened Petrograd — but it had lasting symbolic consequences. Petrograd, the "window to the West" and cradle of the revolution, was replaced by Moscow, the traditional seat of Russian power. The Kremlin, with its medieval walls and Orthodox cathedrals, lent the Bolshevik regime a continuity with Russian state tradition that its revolutionary rhetoric denied. Dukes' two references to the Kremlin capture this duality: it is both the source of revolutionary terror and the inheritor of centuries of Russian autocracy.
