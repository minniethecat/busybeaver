## Overview

Propaganda was among the Bolsheviks' most effective instruments of power. In Paul Dukes' memoir, Bolshevik agitation appears not as a sideshow but as a central pillar of the regime — a constant, pervasive presence that shaped public consciousness, mobilised supporters, and intimidated opponents. Dukes, operating undercover in Russia, was both a target and an observer of this propaganda machinery.

His account is valuable for documenting propaganda not as a disembodied ideological force but as a set of concrete practices: street-corner orators, mass meetings in factories, wall posters, political theatre, and the cultivation of revolutionary saints and martyrs. Dukes' perspective as a British intelligence officer — trained in his own country's traditions of information warfare — gives his observations of Bolshevik methods a professional depth rare in contemporary accounts.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes was struck by the Bolshevik leadership's oratorical skill. Zinoviev, president of the Petrograd Soviet, is described as "unrivalled in his appeal to the ignorant mob" but whose speeches revealed that "logic is unknown to him" — a "consummate gutter-orator" whose "inexhaustible fund of florid and vulgar invective" nonetheless captivated audiences (005-037). This portrait captures a key feature of Bolshevik propaganda: its emotional power and intellectual vacuity.

The Bolsheviks also mastered the politics of martyrdom. Dukes records how the murdered German Communists Karl Liebknecht and Rosa Luxemburg, "little known outside Germany until the time of their death," were elevated to sainthood: "in the hierarchy of Bolshevist saints they were placed second only to Karl Marx and Engels, the Moses and Aaron of the Communist Party" (005-003). His comparison between Bolshevik icon-veneration and Russian Orthodox traditions is characteristically acute — the revolutionaries had, in his view, simply replaced religious icons with political ones.

Dukes also documents how foreign visitors were instrumentalised for propaganda purposes. International delegates were paraded at rallies where they "lauded the exemplary order prevailing in Russia" — often under duress or through carefully managed tours that concealed the regime's failures (012-007). The contrast between propaganda image and lived reality became one of Dukes' enduring themes.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes devotes particular attention to the Bolsheviks' rhetorical strategies, often channelling his analysis through the character of "the Doctor." The Doctor's observation that Communist speeches "often sound like a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury signifying nothing" is paired with the recognition that "behind the interminable jargon there lie both an impulse and an ideal" (005-009). This dual recognition — that Bolshevik propaganda was simultaneously empty verbiage and a genuine expression of revolutionary idealism — distinguishes Dukes from simpler anti-Communist writers.

Dukes also notes the regime's sensitivity to counter-narrative. He describes how Communists were attached to military units "to eavesdrop and report any remarks detrimental to the Bolsheviks" (011-016), and how the Cheka's network of informants ensured that dissent was reported and suppressed. Propaganda and terror were two faces of the same coin — one persuaded, the other compelled.
