## Overview

On 15 March 1917, Tsar Nicholas II signed the deed of abdication aboard his imperial train at Pskov, ending the Romanov dynasty's three-hundred-year rule. Paul Dukes, who had witnessed the revolution in Petrograd days earlier, records the event as the culmination of the popular insurrection he had seen on the streets. For Dukes, the abdication was the moment when "that historic moment" (014-015) passed, though he notes the irony that ordinary Russians would later yearn for a return to stability that the Tsar represented.

## Background

By early March 1917, the situation in Petrograd had slipped beyond the control of the Tsarist authorities. Troops sent to suppress demonstrations joined the revolutionaries instead. Nicholas, at military headquarters in Mogilev, tried to return to the capital but was blocked by revolutionary forces at Pskov. The army high command, believing the Tsar's abdication was the only way to preserve the war effort against Germany, advised him to step down.

## Events

Dukes does not narrate the abdication ceremony itself — he was in Petrograd, not Pskov — but the event pervades his memoir. The Tsar's portrait had been torn down and replaced with revolutionary imagery; the imperial monograms were "being removed" from buildings (009-062). The walls and railings "with imperial monograms that surrounded" the Winter Palace (009-066) were reminders of a world that had vanished.

Dukes' one explicit reference to the signing captures the finality: "that historic moment when Nicholas II signed the deed of abdication from the Russian throne" (014-015). The "deed of abdication" was not, however, the path to liberty that the revolutionaries had imagined. A former Tsarist officer tells Dukes: "at present I serve nobody. I will not serve Trotsky, but I will work with him if he does not interfere with me" (003-087).

## Aftermath

The abdication left a power vacuum that neither the Provisional Government nor the Bolshevik regime could permanently fill. Dukes notes that workers, disillusioned with the Bolsheviks, would later sing couplets demanding: "give us a tsar and pork!" (012-011). The popular memory of the Tsar, stripped of the autocracy that had accompanied it, became a symbol of lost stability. Dukes reports the sardonic factory-floor rhyme: "Down with Lenin and horseflesh, give us a tsar and pork!" (012-011). The execution of the Tsar and his family at Ekaterinburg, which occurred in July 1918, is not directly narrated by Dukes but lurks as a silent horror beneath the surface of his account.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes was no monarchist. But his account of the revolution's aftermath demonstrates that, for many ordinary Russians, the Tsar's fall had brought not liberation but chaos — "starvation and serfdom" (001-007) instead of the "Declaration of Independence of Russia" (001-005) that the revolutionaries had imagined. The abdication page is therefore not a page of mourning for monarchy but a page about the tragedy of a revolution that destroyed what existed without being able to build what it had promised.
