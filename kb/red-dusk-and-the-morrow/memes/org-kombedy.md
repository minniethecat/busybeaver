## Overview

The Committees of the Village Poor (Kombedy) were local rural bodies established by Bolshevik decree in June 1918, tasked with identifying "kulak" grain hoarders, facilitating state requisitioning, and redistributing confiscated land and property to poor peasants. Ostensibly organs of class empowerment, in practice they became instruments of violent expropriation, pitting neighbour against neighbour in a deliberate strategy of rural class war.

Dukes encounters the Kombedy during his journey through the Russian countryside, disguised as a Soviet official. His account is one of the memoir's most vivid passages on rural life: a local peasant describes how the committee "kept the grain for themselves" (008-034) while "the men from the Poor Committee" (008-030) were conscripted into the Red Army. For Dukes, the Kombedy exemplify the Bolshevik technique of governance through division — creating institutions whose real function is not administration but destruction.

## Structure and Role

Kombedy were elected at the village level, with membership theoretically restricted to poor peasants and landless labourers. Their formal mandate included grain requisitioning for the Red Army and urban populations, redistribution of kulak land and property to the poor, and political mobilisation in support of Soviet power. In practice, they operated with minimal oversight and maximal violence. Dukes' informant reports that "the Committee kept the grain for themselves" after promising it to the village, and that "all the men from the Poor Committee" were then conscripted (008-030, 008-034).

The committees were officially dissolved in December 1918 — merged into the rural soviets — but their brief existence had already achieved their real purpose: the destruction of the kulak class as an economic and social force.

## In Dukes' Account

> "Have you a Committee of the Poor in this house?" I asked Stepanovna. The question, routine in any Soviet official's repertoire, opened a window onto the machinery of rural expropriation. (002-101, narrative paraphrase)

Dukes' impersonation of a Soviet official gives him access to conversations about the Kombedy that a foreigner would never hear. The question itself — "Have you a Committee of the Poor?" — is a test of political loyalty, a bureaucratic shibboleth that unlocks information.

> Committees of the Village Poor were bodies from which were elected the members of the village soviets. In theory, this was worker-peasant democracy; in practice, the committees were armed requisitioning squads whose members "kept the grain for themselves" and then found themselves conscripted into the Red Army. (008-027, with narrative elaboration from 008-030, 008-034)

The trajectory Dukes traces — from empowerment to expropriation to conscription — is, in miniature, his thesis about Bolshevik rule: institutions created to liberate become instruments of coercion.

## Historical Context

The Kombedy decree was a response to the grain crisis of spring 1918. Urban populations were starving, the Red Army needed food, and "kulaks" — wealthier peasants — were refusing to sell grain at state-controlled prices. The committees bypassed existing village assemblies (which often included kulaks) and empowered the rural poor to take what the state demanded. The policy succeeded in extracting grain but at catastrophic cost: it destroyed rural social cohesion, provoked peasant uprisings, and contributed to the famines that followed.
