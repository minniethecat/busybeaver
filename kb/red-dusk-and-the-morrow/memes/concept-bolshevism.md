## Overview

[[Bolshevism]] is the revolutionary socialist ideology that emerged from the [[Bolshevik]] faction of the [[Russian Social Democratic Labour Party]] under [[Vladimir Lenin|Lenin]]'s leadership. In [[Paul Dukes]]' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*, Bolshevism is not treated as an abstract political theory but as a lived reality — the governing ideology of [[Soviet Russia]] between 1917 and 1919. Dukes, a [[British intelligence]] officer operating undercover in Russia, witnessed firsthand how Bolshevist ideology transformed Russian society, reshaped its institutions, and sought to export [[revolution]] westward.

Dukes characterises Bolshevism as a doctrine driven more by hatred of the [[bourgeoisie]] than love of the [[proletariat]]. He portrays it as a system that, in its early revolutionary phase, promised utopian transformation but rapidly degenerated into bureaucratic terror, class persecution, and economic collapse. His account is notable both for its insider observations of Bolshevik governance and for its nuanced recognition that Bolshevism appealed to genuine grievances even as it exploited them.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes describes the early Bolshevik regime as one where ideology and practical governance collided constantly. The Bolsheviks rapidly dismantled existing institutions — the army, the civil service, the judiciary — and replaced them with revolutionary organs staffed by loyalists whose chief qualification was ideological purity rather than competence. Dukes notes with irony how "official 'documents' with prominent seals or stamps" carried magical authority, with "a big coloured seal" serving as "a talisman that levelled all obstacles," regardless of the document's actual content (001-076).

The wave of Bolshevism was not confined to Russia. Dukes records how the deaths of German Communist leaders [[Karl Liebknecht]] and [[Rosa Luxemburg]] in January 1919 caused "consternation in the Communist camp," for the Bolshevik leadership had "hoped thus to accelerate the wave of Bolshevism westward across Europe" (005-002). This internationalist ambition was central to Bolshevik ideology — the conviction that revolution in backward Russia could only survive if it sparked proletarian uprisings in the industrialised West.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes devotes sustained attention to analysing the ideological character of Bolshevism, often through the voice of his informant "[[the Doctor]]":

> Behind the interminable jargon of Communist speeches lies both an impulse and an ideal. The ideal is a proletarian millennium, but the impulse is not love of the worker, but hatred of the bourgeois. (005-009)

This diagnosis — that Bolshevism is driven by class hatred rather than constructive vision — recurs throughout the memoir.

On the question of Bolshevism's appeal to non-Russians, Dukes is careful to distinguish between the ideology and its Western enthusiasts. He criticises both "the extreme opponents of Bolshevism" and "the extreme proponents," noting that their shared "unreasoning immoderation" has produced "that oddest of anomalies, the 'parlour Bolshevik'" — Western sympathisers with "inquiring minds but incomplete information" (P01-003).

Perhaps most significantly, Dukes anticipates Bolshevism's decline:

> It is not to outside pressure or internal militarist conspiracies that we must look for the decay of Bolshevism, but must seek the signs of internal organic decomposition. (011-083)

The contrast he draws between "Bolshevism and Russianism" — between the imposed revolutionary ideology and the deeper national character — represents his central thesis about the regime's ultimate instability (011-070).
