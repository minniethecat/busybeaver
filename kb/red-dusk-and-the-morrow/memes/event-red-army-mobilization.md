## Overview

The mobilization of the Red Army was one of the Bolshevik regime's most consequential achievements and a process Paul Dukes experienced from the inside. Originally conceived as a volunteer force, the Red Army soon resorted to mass conscription, sweeping up both peasants and former Tsarist officers. Dukes, needing a cover identity to continue his intelligence work, served in a Red Army regiment and documented both the mechanics of recruitment and the atmosphere of terror that held the force together.

## Background

When the Bolsheviks seized power, Russia's old army was disintegrating. Lenin and Trotsky faced the challenge of building a new military force capable of defending the regime against White armies, Allied interventionists, and peasant uprisings. "The stimulus to build a mighty Red army for world-revolutionary purposes" (004-015) was provided by the escalating Civil War. Trotsky, "the genius of the Red army" (011-018), introduced conscription and employed former Tsarist officers under the supervision of political commissars.

## Events

Dukes describes the process of enlistment from the soldier's perspective. Officers had no choice: "if an officer doesn't want to be shot he either obeys the mobilization order or flees from the country" (011-008). Commanders were made to sign documents that effectively made their families hostages. Dukes' own commander showed him "a copy of a paper I was made to fill in and sign before being given a Red commission. We all have to sign it, and if you were discovered here I should have signed away my wife's life as well as my own" (011-008).

The conscript army was poorly equipped: "except for picked troops, smartly apparelled in the best the army stores could provide, the rank and file of recruits wore just anything, and often had only bast slippers in place of boots" (011-003). Morale was sustained through threat: "the terror that prevails in the Red army" was enforced by "Special Departments" that could execute soldiers at will (011-040). Desertion was common, and Dukes later observed on the Polish front how "many of the thousands who deserted to the Poles were wearing British uniforms which had been supplied, together with so much war material, to Denikin" (011-003).

## Aftermath

The Red Army mobilization succeeded in creating a force that won the Civil War, but at tremendous cost. Dukes notes that the peasant soldiers had no love for the regime: they served under compulsion and would desert at the first opportunity. The army became a permanent institution that consolidated Bolshevik power and served as a model for the militarization of Soviet society. Dukes' observation that "people may, by terror, be made to fight desperately when actually face to face with the enemy" (011-041) captures the fundamental instability of a force built on fear.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' account of his Red Army service is unique. He wore the "khaki shirt, yellow breeches, putties" and grey overcoat of the Red soldier (011-002), attended political indoctrination meetings where Zinoviev "spoke to us for an hour and a half" (011-015), and used his position to observe everything from desertion patterns to officer morale. His assessment is both professional (military) and personal — he knew men who served alongside him and understood their motivations.
