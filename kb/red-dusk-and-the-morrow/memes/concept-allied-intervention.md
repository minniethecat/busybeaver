## Overview

The Allied intervention in Russia was one of the most controversial episodes of the post-World War I settlement, and Paul Dukes was uniquely positioned to assess it. As a British intelligence officer operating inside Bolshevik Russia, he understood both the strategic rationale for intervention and its practical futility. His memoir offers a rare inside perspective on the intervention's failure — not from the battlefield but from the political and intelligence landscape that determined its fate.

Dukes' analysis is notably unsentimental. As a British patriot and professional intelligence officer, he does not question the legitimacy of intervention itself. But his on-the-ground experience convinced him that military intervention without a compelling political alternative to Bolshevism was worse than useless — it consolidated Bolshevik power by enabling the regime to portray itself as the defender of Russian nationhood against foreign invaders.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes documents the intervention's effects from the Russian side. The Bolsheviks skilfully exploited foreign military presence for propaganda purposes, presenting every White advance as a puppet of British and French imperialism. This narrative was effective precisely because it contained a grain of truth — White generals did receive Allied funding and matériel. Dukes records how the Bolshevik regime used intervention to justify internal repression, with the Cheka intensifying its campaign against "counterrevolutionary" elements suspected of collaborating with foreign powers.

The intervention also had direct consequences for Allied nationals in Russia. Dukes describes the precarious position of British and French citizens who remained in Bolshevik-controlled territory after the landings. Many were arrested as suspected spies; others, like Dukes himself, operated under false identities that grew more dangerous as the Cheka's counterintelligence capabilities improved. The memoir conveys the claustrophobic atmosphere of a capital city where foreignness itself had become a crime.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes' central criticism of the intervention is strategic rather than moral. He argues that the Allies fundamentally misunderstood the nature of the Russian conflict by treating it as a conventional war. "The lamentable failure to understand that in the anti-Bolshevist war politics and not military strategy must play the dominant role were the chief causes of the White defeats" (011-031). This insight — that the Russian Civil War was a political contest above all — explains both the Whites' failure and the Bolsheviks' success.

He also identifies the fatal contradiction in Allied policy: intervening to overthrow the Bolsheviks while failing to offer Russian peasants a credible alternative. The Whites' association with landlord restoration, combined with their dependence on foreign support, made them politically toxic regardless of their military capabilities. Dukes observed that the Allies received funds "for counterrevolutionary purposes from representatives of the Allies" (002-034), and that this financial dependency undermined the Whites' nationalist credentials.

The moral dimension does not escape him. He notes the human cost of intervention: the "grievous" position of Russian officers with families who could not flee — "Flight with their families was difficult" (011-025) — and the ordinary people caught between warring armies. The Bolsheviks, for their part, "suspected Marsh, together with other Englishmen, of complicity" in assisting White forces (002-126), illustrating how the intervention poisoned relations and endangered all foreigners in Russia. Yet Dukes' conclusion is not pacifist but realist — intervention failed because it was badly conceived, not because intervention is always wrong.
