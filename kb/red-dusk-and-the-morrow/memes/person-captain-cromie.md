## Overview

Captain Francis Cromie was the British Naval Attaché in Petrograd and, in the months before the Allied intervention, the principal British intelligence officer operating against the Bolshevik regime. By the time Paul Dukes arrived in Russia, Cromie was already dead — killed on 31 August 1918 during a Cheka raid on the British Embassy — but his legend loomed over the entire British intelligence effort. Every Russian contact who had worked with Cromie compared Dukes to him, and Dukes' own cover story included a fabricated link to the dead attaché.

Cromie's death is one of the memoir's first set-pieces. Dukes recounts how a Bolshevik mob, stirred by the Red Terror following the Uritsky assassination, stormed the British Embassy. Cromie "shot down a commissar who attacked him on the main stairway" before himself being killed (001-037). The story establishes the atmosphere of violence and duplicity in which Dukes would operate.

## Role in the Narrative

Cromie never appears alive in Dukes' narrative, but his shadow is everywhere. When Zorinsky introduces himself at the cafe, he immediately invokes Cromie: "I used to provide your Captain Cromie with quite a lot of information" (002-234). The name functions as a credential, a password among the intelligence networks of revolutionary Petrograd. Dukes is perpetually measured against his predecessor.

Zorinsky's tribute is the memoir's most direct assessment: "'Cromie was a fine fellow,' he said, with a suggestion of real sincerity in his voice" (002-240). The qualifier — "a suggestion of real sincerity" — is characteristic Dukes: even in mourning his colleague, he remains the intelligence officer, weighing the authenticity of every utterance.

Cromie also appears in the practical mechanics of Dukes' cover identity. Dukes' assistant, an English mechanic who had been "a friend of Captain Cromie and until recently was known as the captain's chauffeur" (002-014), provides a vital link to the dead officer's network. The living intelligence operation is built on the ghost of the dead one.

## Biography

Francis Newton Allan Cromie was born in 1882 and served in the Royal Navy, reaching the rank of Captain. Posted to Petrograd as Naval Attaché during the First World War, he became deeply involved in intelligence work after the Bolshevik seizure of power. Cromie built a network of anti-Bolshevik Russian contacts and was believed to have been involved in plans to rescue the imperial family. On 31 August 1918 — the day after Uritsky's assassination and Lenin's shooting — Cheka agents raided the British Embassy. Cromie resisted, killing a commissar on the stairway, and was shot dead. The British government posthumously awarded him the CB.

## Political Context

Cromie's death occurred at the most dangerous moment of the Allied presence in Bolshevik Russia — the day after the dual assassinations that triggered the Red Terror. The Cheka raid on the Embassy was part of a coordinated crackdown on foreign missions, justified by the Bolshevik claim that Allied diplomats were conspiring with counter-revolutionaries. Cromie's armed resistance made him a martyr in British intelligence circles and hardened attitudes in London against the Bolshevik regime, contributing to the climate that produced the Allied intervention at Archangel.

## In Dukes' Account

> At last a band of Bolshevist mobsters, led by Lettish guards and Chinese soldiers, encouraged by the Red terror raging in Petrograd and supported by sailors and soldiers whom the news of the attack on Lenin's life had wrought to fever pitch, broke into the fortress, and proceeded to methodically ransack the building. Captain Cromie, late British Naval Attaché and the sole remaining representative of Great Britain, shot down a commissar who attacked him on the main stairway, and himself fell riddled with bullets. (001-037)

Dukes' account of Cromie's death is rich with characteristic motifs: the multi-ethnic composition of the Bolshevik mob ("Lettish guards and Chinese soldiers"), the fevered psychology of the crowd, and the lone British officer's doomed act of defiance.

> "I used to provide your Captain Cromie with quite a lot of information... It is an amusing game — contre-espionnage." (002-234)

Zorinsky's casual revelation that he supplied intelligence to Cromie — described as an "amusing game" — captures the moral ambiguity of the Petrograd intelligence world that Dukes inherited. The dead attaché had worked with double-dealers like Zorinsky out of necessity, just as Dukes now must.
