## Overview

Joseph Krylenko is not a real person but a fabricated identity — the cover name that Paul Dukes adopted during his second mission to Soviet Russia. This alias, built on forged Soviet identity documents, allowed Dukes to move freely through Petrograd, obtain a position as a clerk in the Department of State Control, and operate as a British intelligence agent for months under the very nose of the Cheka. The Krylenko identity is the memoir's most sustained study of the psychology of living under cover.

The name appears at a moment of reinvention: "I changed my name to Joseph Krylenko" (004-016). Dukes' account of forging the documents — laboriously copying the looping Cyrillic signature, learning to inhabit a new identity down to handwriting and manner — is one of the memoir's most detailed procedural passages. The alias was both Dukes' greatest asset and, when betrayed, his greatest vulnerability: "The betrayal of the name 'Krylenko' was of course easily traceable to the Doctor's house" (009-087).

## Role in the Narrative

The Krylenko identity enables Dukes' entire second mission. Without it, he is Paul Dukes, British subject and enemy alien, liable to arrest and execution. With it, he is Joseph Krylenko, a nondescript clerk working in the Department of State Control, a man "unobtrusive in dress, speech, and manner" (005-079). The identity is the instrument that allows him to observe the Bolshevik state from within.

The forgery process is described in painstaking detail. Dukes obtains blank document forms, learns to replicate the elaborate Soviet bureaucratic handwriting, and crafts supporting papers for a fictitious biography. "When they were done I finished the document by affixing my photograph, and stamped it with the necessary design, which had cost me some trouble to make" (004-075). The photograph is real; everything else is invention.

The identity's fatal flaw is its chain of custody. Dukes creates it through the Doctor's network, and every intermediary who handled the documents becomes a potential point of betrayal. After his narrow escape, Dukes reflects that "the betrayal of the name 'Krylenko' was of course easily traceable" (009-087) — the alias that protected him for months became the thread by which the Cheka could unravel his entire network.

## Biography

Joseph Krylenko has no biography independent of the documents Dukes fabricated. The cover story described a man "aged thirty-three, clerical assistant at the head Postal-Telegraph Office, and the son of an official of comparatively humble birth" (005-072). This was the strategic choice: an identity unremarkable enough to escape scrutiny. Dukes selected a role — low-level civil servant — that would explain his presence in government offices without attracting attention. The real Nikolai Krylenko, a prominent Bolshevik prosecutor and Commissar for Justice whose surname Dukes appropriated, was presumably chosen for the protective ambiguity the shared name would provide.

## In Dukes' Account

> I changed my name to Joseph Krylenko. But the time was coming when I should be compelled to discard my pseudonym, and it was time to create a new one. (004-016)

This sentence, brief to the point of reticence, marks the moment of identity change. Dukes passes over the psychological weight of abandoning one's name with characteristic understatement. What follows — the creation of the new documents, the learning of the new signature — is the memoir's closest approach to spycraft procedural.

> The papers were, I believe, as exact a copy of the original as could be desired for practical purposes... When they were done I finished the document by affixing my photograph, and stamped it with the necessary design. (004-075)

The forger's pride is evident in Dukes' assessment of his own work. The phrase "as exact a copy of the original as could be desired" is the voice of the craftsman assessing his product. The false documents are not merely practical necessities but achievements in their own right.

> The betrayal of the name "Krylenko" was of course easily traceable to the Doctor's house, and with the discovery of my calling in the office, the cheka would be fully equipped for my arrest. (009-087)

The inevitable end. Dukes writes this with the clarity of retrospect — the alias that enabled everything was also what made his exposure certain. The name Krylenko, so carefully constructed, became the signal that would lead the Cheka directly to him.
