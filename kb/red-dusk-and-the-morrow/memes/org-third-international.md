# Third International

## Overview

The Third International, also known as the Comintern or the Communist International, was an international organisation founded in Moscow in March 1919 to advocate for world revolution and the establishment of Communist governments in all countries. It was the most important institution established by the Bolsheviks on the international stage, and Dukes witnessed its early operations in Petrograd, which the Bolsheviks proudly called "the Metropolis of the World Revolution."

## Structure and Role

The Third International was conceived as the successor to the First and Second Internationals, and its stated aim was "to reproduce the Communist experiment in all countries" (012-005). Its position in Russia and its relationship to the Soviet Government were deliberately opaque: Dukes observed that "the executives both of the International and of the Government are drawn from the Communist Party, while every member of the Government must also be a member of the Third International." Consequently, "though technically not interchangeable, the terms Soviet Government, Third International, Communist Party and their executives often are in practice" (012-008). The organisation maintained a propaganda apparatus that Dukes later described as "the entire paraphernalia of the Third International and its department of propaganda" (014-005).

## Activities

The inaugural gathering of the Third International, which Dukes attended at the Nicholas railway station in Petrograd, was a carefully staged propaganda spectacle. Orators "with chattering teeth, sang in divers tongues the praises of the Red regime," lauding "the exemplary order prevailing in Russia" and predicting "the immediate advent of the world revolution and the early establishment of Bolshevism in every country" (012-007). All speeches closed with the ritual exclamations: "Long live the Third International! Down with the bourgeoisie!" (012-007). However, Dukes noted the deep irony of the event: "At the very moment when the Third International was haranguing for its own satisfaction outside the Nicholas Station, very different things were happening in the industrial quarters of the city," where workers were protesting the suppression of free speech, freedom of movement, and workers' cooperatives (012-009).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes attended the Third International's founding meeting "neither to cheer nor to jeer, but as an observer" (012-006). He was struck by the contrast between the internationalist rhetoric and the actual composition of the organisation's leadership: "Intellectuality in the party has always been represented largely, though by no means exclusively, by Jews, who dominate the Third International, edit the Soviet journals, and direct propaganda" (012-046). His later assessment was unambiguous: "To expect ... of the Third International ... is hopeless" (014-004). He observed that the International served primarily as an instrument for justifying Bolshevik rule and suppressing dissent abroad under the banner of world revolution.

## Historical Context

The Third International was founded in the aftermath of the First World War, when revolutionary movements swept across Europe. Its first congress coincided with the Spartacist uprising in Germany and the establishment of the Hungarian Soviet Republic. The organisation was led by Grigory Zinoviev, who simultaneously served as "president of the Petrograd Soviet" and "president of the Third International" (005-036). The Comintern functioned until 1943, when Stalin dissolved it as a wartime concession to his Western allies — though Dukes had already identified its essential character in 1919: an instrument for extending Bolshevik control rather than a genuine international workers' movement.
