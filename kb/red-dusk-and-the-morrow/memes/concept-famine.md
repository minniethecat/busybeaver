## Overview

Famine shadows Paul Dukes' memoir as both ever-present threat and political indictment. Writing his concluding chapter as news arrived of "the affliction of Russia with one of her periodical famine scourges," Dukes argues that hunger was not merely natural disaster but direct consequence of Bolshevik economic policy (014-002). The communistic experiment, he contends, had "eliminated the stimulus to produce" — peasants facing grain confiscation had no incentive to plant.

Dukes treats famine as the ultimate measure of Bolshevik governance. A regime that could not feed its people had failed its most basic obligation. His account links food shortages to every aspect of the system: market destruction, peasant persecution, and the prioritisation of revolutionary agitation over agricultural production.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Hunger was woven into daily life. Dukes' narrative is punctuated by food's absence — the "inevitable subject of famine" intruded even into polite conversation, when Marsh returned "with three glasses of tea, apologizing for not providing sugar" (002-205). Sugar's absence became a marker of how thoroughly revolution had disrupted ordinary existence.

The Bolsheviks presided over this catastrophe while their propaganda denied its severity. Dukes notes the gap between official pronouncements and observable reality with the precision of someone who had learned to distrust all Soviet claims.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes acknowledges Russia's structural vulnerability to crop failure while insisting on Bolshevik culpability. "Soviet organizations are incompetent to cope with such a situation," he writes (014-002). The regime blamed "kulak sabotage" when peasants responded to confiscation by reducing production. Dukes saw this as the logical endpoint of a system treating producers as enemies.

His broader argument is that famine demonstrated the Bolsheviks' misreading of human nature. They assumed revolutionary enthusiasm could replace material incentive; the resulting starvation was not an accident but a predictable consequence of economic theory divorced from reality.
