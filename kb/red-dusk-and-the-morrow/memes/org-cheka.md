# Cheka

## Overview

The Cheka (from the Russian abbreviation "ChK" of *Chrezvychaynaya Komissiya*) was the first Soviet secret police organisation, established in December 1917 under the leadership of Felix Dzerzhinsky. It served as the principal instrument of Bolshevik political repression during the Russian Civil War, operating through a network of investigators, informants, and executioners with virtually unlimited powers of arrest, detention, and execution.

## Structure and Role

The Extraordinary Commission was headquartered at No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street in Petrograd, a building that became synonymous with Bolshevik terror. The commission was governed by a "soviet" or council of revolutionary fanatics who held final authority over the fate of prisoners, while "Investigators" examined the accused, collected evidence, and reported their findings to the council (002-267). Every responsible official was required to be a member of the Communist Party, though the lower staff included re-engaged agents of the Tsarist police who had lost their positions after the revolution and were re-enlisted as specialists (002-269). Its remit extended well beyond counter-revolution: the Cheka also targeted "speculation," a term applied to virtually any private economic activity (002-266).

## Activities

The Cheka cast a shadow over all life in Bolshevik Russia. No individual was free from its surveillance — even Communists stood in awe of it, as one of its duties was to unearth "black sheep" within the Party ranks (002-270). The commission's methods included the deliberate fomentation of seditious plots by *agents provocateurs*, a technique inherited from Tsarist police practice. Such movements would attract genuine revolutionaries, and when they had grown sufficiently, all participants would be arrested en masse (002-271). Prisoners were subjected to "every invention of exquisite villainy" to extract the names of accomplices or sympathisers (002-272).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' narrative provides a vivid personal perspective on the Cheka's operations. His acquaintance Marsh, whose wife was imprisoned at No. 2 Gorokhovaya, was in contact with a former Okhrana official who "was doing some sort of underhand work for the Cheka" (002-127). Dukes himself experienced the Cheka's reach indirectly through the pervasive atmosphere of fear it created: Marsh was haunted by anxieties that his wife, "nerve-racked and doubtless underfed, if fed at all, might be subjected to treatment that would test her self-control to the extreme" (002-272). The Cheka was described as "the most notorious of all Bolshevist institutions" (002-266).

## Historical Context

The Cheka was established in the immediate aftermath of the Bolshevik seizure of power, when the new regime faced widespread opposition from both the political right and left. Its founding marked the institutionalisation of the Red Terror as a tool of state policy. The commission's methods drew heavily on the practices of the Tsarist Okhrana — indeed, the Bolsheviks explicitly re-engaged former Tsarist police agents, an irony Dukes noted (002-269). The Cheka served as a model for subsequent Soviet secret police organisations, including the GPU and OGPU, and its name in abbreviated form persisted in later iterations such as the NKVD and KGB.
