## Overview

The Russian peasantry is, in Paul Dukes' analysis, the key to understanding the revolution's course and the Bolsheviks' prospects. While much contemporary commentary focused on political factions, military campaigns, and international intrigue, Dukes insists that the peasant question was fundamental: whoever held the allegiance of the countryside would ultimately prevail. His memoir devotes sustained attention to peasant perspectives, conditions, and grievances — material gathered not from armchair analysis but from conversations during his extensive travels through rural Russia.

Dukes' argument is that Bolshevism's relationship with the peasantry was inherently unstable. The Bolsheviks' land policy — allowing peasants to seize estates — won temporary support, but their grain requisitions, conscription, and anti-religious campaigns alienated the countryside. The Whites, meanwhile, were associated with landlord restoration, making them equally unacceptable. Dukes saw in this deadlock both the explanation for the Civil War's prolongation and the seed of Bolshevism's eventual decay.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes describes the peasantry's material conditions with a reporter's precision. The discrepancy between urban and rural experiences of the revolution — between the political struggles of Petrograd and the subsistence calculations of the village — is a recurring theme. He notes how peasant soldiers, "cooped in their city barracks," were moved to tears by folk songs that reconnected them with their rural roots (011-070). The contrast between "Bolshevism and Russianism" that Dukes draws is, at its core, a contrast between the urban ideology of the revolution and the peasant culture of the nation (011-070).

The Bolsheviks' approach to the peasantry combined ideological disdain with practical exploitation. Grain was requisitioned at gunpoint; conscripts were dragooned into the Red Army; village priests and elders were persecuted. Yet the regime also understood that the peasantry's numerical weight made outright war against the countryside suicidal. Dukes documents this tension — the Bolsheviks simultaneously despised and feared the peasant masses whose labour sustained the revolution.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes' strategic assessment centres on the peasantry's political potential. He identifies "the complete absence of an acceptable programme alternative to Bolshevism" as a key factor in the Whites' failure, noting that "the audibly whispered threats of landlords that in the event of a White victory the land seized by the peasants would be restored to its former owners" doomed any chance of a peasant-White alliance (011-031). Neither Reds nor Whites offered the peasantry what it most wanted: land without conscription, grain requisition, or landlord restoration.

He speculates about the possibility of a peasant-based alternative to Bolshevism, mentioning "peasant leaders such as the 'little fathers,' Balahovitch in Belorussia and Makhno in Ukrainia, who denounce Bolsheviks, Tsars, and landlords alike" (011-083). Such movements represented, in Dukes' view, the authentic political expression of peasant interests — but they lacked the national leadership and organisation needed to challenge the Bolshevik state.
