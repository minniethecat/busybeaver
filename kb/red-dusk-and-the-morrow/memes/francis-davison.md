## Overview

Charles Davison (referred to in some sources as Francis Davison) was a British subject executed in [[Moscow]] on 16 January 1920. The Soviet government claimed he was shot as an accomplice of [[Paul Dukes]]' "provocative activities," but Dukes himself maintained that the British Foreign Office's letter informing him of the execution was "my first intimation that such a person as Mr. Davison had ever existed" (P01-005). The case illustrates the Soviet regime's willingness to attribute conspiracies to Dukes that had no basis in fact.

## Role in the Narrative

Davison appears only in the Foreword of Dukes' memoir, where he is cited as an example of the Soviet government's fabricated accusations. Dukes recounts receiving "an intimation from the Foreign Office that on January 16, 1920, a certain Mr. Charles Davison had been executed in Moscow" and that the Soviet government had claimed Davison was "shot as an accomplice of my 'provocative activities'" (P01-005). Dukes pairs this with the equally baseless allegation that he had instigated a White government during [[General Yudenich]]'s advance on [[Petrograd]], using both incidents to establish his memoir's central theme: the gulf between Soviet propaganda and the reality he witnessed.

The Davison case remains historically obscure — no independent record of his activities or trial has survived — but in Dukes' account it serves as a sharp illustration of how the Bolshevik security apparatus manufactured connections between unrelated individuals to justify political executions.
