## Overview

The Russo-Polish War of 1920 was the first major military campaign of the Red Army against a foreign power — and it ended in disaster for the Bolsheviks. Paul Dukes, who was serving in the Red Army at the time, observed the campaign from the front and later witnessed its aftermath. The Polish victory, in his analysis, "effectually dispelled" the illusion that the Red Army could conquer Europe for world revolution (011-039).

## Background

After defeating most of the White armies by early 1920, the Bolsheviks turned their attention westward. Poland, newly independent after the Versailles Treaty, was seen both as a barrier to revolution in Germany and as a potential target. The Soviet leadership, particularly Trotsky, believed that the Red Army could march through Poland and link up with revolutionary movements in Central Europe. Dukes describes how "the first campaign of the Red army against a non-Russian foe, Poland, was hailed by Bolsheviks as the beginning of the world-revolutionary military era" (011-038).

## Events

The Red Army advanced rapidly into Polish territory in the summer of 1920, reaching the outskirts of Warsaw. But the offensive overextended Soviet supply lines and exposed deep weaknesses in the army's cohesion. Polish forces under Jozef Pilsudski counter-attacked and routed the Red Army, forcing a chaotic retreat. Dukes observed the aftermath from the Polish side: "I found many of the thousands who deserted to the Poles wearing British uniforms which had been supplied, together with so much war material, to Denikin" (011-003). The irony — Bolshevik soldiers fleeing in British-supplied uniforms — captures the tangled logistics of the Civil War period.

The Bolshevik effort to rally the peasantry for the Polish campaign largely failed. Dukes notes that "an appreciable degree of anti-Polish national feeling was aroused" (011-038), enough for a modicum of patriotic enthusiasm, but the regular army collapsed under pressure. "One Polish division was suddenly attacked by a mass of Red soldiers who unexpectedly turned and gave battle" (011-040) — but the anecdote is about a local engagement, not the strategic catastrophe at Warsaw.

## Aftermath

The Polish victory ended Bolshevik expansionism for the remainder of the Civil War. Dukes observes that "the Polish victory effectually dispelled" the myth of Red Army might (011-039). The war also had domestic consequences: it "served even more than the civil wars to consolidate the position of the Red army as a definite institution in the service of the Government" (011-039). Paradoxically, a war the Bolsheviks lost strengthened the army as an instrument of internal control. The Treaty of Riga (1921) established the Soviet-Polish border and left the Bolsheviks to focus on crushing remaining internal resistance.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes saw the Polish war from a unique vantage point — as a Britisher in Red uniform who later crossed to the other side. His account is unsentimental: he acknowledges both the nationalist feeling the war aroused and the military incompetence it exposed. His strategic judgment, that "it served even more than the civil wars to consolidate the position" of the Red Army (011-039), shows the analytical detachment of an intelligence officer assessing what the war meant for the regime's stability rather than who won or lost.
