# British Foreign Office

## Overview

The British Foreign Office was the department of the British government responsible for conducting foreign relations and directing intelligence operations abroad. During the period of the Russian Revolution and Civil War, it oversaw British diplomatic and intelligence activities in Russia, including the work of officers such as Paul Dukes. The Foreign Office represents the institutional framework within which British policy toward Bolshevik Russia was formulated and executed.

## Structure and Role

The Foreign Office operated through a network of diplomatic missions, consulates, and intelligence stations. During the revolutionary period, its traditional diplomatic channels were disrupted by the Bolshevik seizure of power and the subsequent British decision to withdraw diplomatic recognition. The Anglo-Russian Commission, a semi-official body, provided a conduit for communication, and Dukes was "nominally in the service of the British Foreign Office, but the Anglo-Russian Commission had afforded the means of my entry" into Russia (001-008). This arrangement reflected the improvisational character of British operations in Russia, combining official cover with intelligence work.

## Activities

The Foreign Office's primary activities in Russia during Dukes' service involved intelligence gathering, support for Allied nationals, and monitoring Bolshevik political and military developments. The urgency of its work is conveyed by the telegram Dukes received while in Moscow: "'Urgent' — from the British Foreign Office. 'You are wanted at once in London'" (001-009). The telegram prompted his immediate departure, demonstrating the direct line of communication and command between the London headquarters and its field officers. The letter from the British Foreign Office was Dukes' "first intimation" of the work he was to undertake (P01-005).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' relationship with the Foreign Office was characterised by the distance between official cover and actual activity. He described himself as "an official of the intelligence service" rather than a diplomat, and acknowledged that "the Soviet Government has charged me with conspiracies and plots to overthrow the Soviet power" (P01-005). His nominal Foreign Office affiliation provided a thin veneer of legitimacy while the real work of intelligence gathering proceeded through unofficial channels. The "Urgent" telegram summoning him to London suggests that his reports were reaching the highest levels of the British government and that his presence was required for consultations on the rapidly deteriorating situation in Russia.

## Historical Context

The British Foreign Office's involvement in Russia during this period must be understood within the broader context of Allied intervention in the Russian Civil War. Britain, along with France, the United States, and Japan, dispatched troops to various parts of Russia from 1918 onward, ostensibly to protect Allied material and personnel but in practice to oppose Bolshevik rule. The Foreign Office managed the diplomatic and intelligence dimensions of this intervention, which Dukes later assessed as having paradoxically "served above all else to consolidate the position of the Bolshevist leaders" (011-032). Dukes' own career illustrates the transition from wartime intelligence to Cold War espionage as the Foreign Office's focus shifted from German to Bolshevik threats.
