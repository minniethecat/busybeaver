## Overview

The Union of Cooperative Societies (Tsentrosoyuz) was the national federation of Russian consumer cooperatives, an economic movement that by 1917 encompassed millions of members across the Russian Empire. The cooperatives were one of the few institutions of Russian civil society to survive the Bolshevik seizure of power — and one of the last to be extinguished. Dukes, in his concluding chapter, cites their destruction as the definitive evidence that the Bolsheviks' war was not on capitalism but on any form of independent social organisation.

The cooperatives appear in the memoir's final pages as a case study in Bolshevik technique. "The Cooperative Unions represented an organisation which in any other country would have been regarded as a bulwark against revolution" (014-023). Dukes' phrasing is precise: the cooperatives were not counter-revolutionary — they were merely independent. Their crime was autonomy.

## Structure and Role

Pre-revolutionary Russian cooperatives were a vast network of consumer societies, credit unions, and agricultural marketing associations. By 1917 Tsentrosoyuz had approximately 20 million members, making it one of the largest cooperative movements in the world. The cooperatives ran shops, mills, bakeries, and distribution networks that functioned independently of both state and private capital.

After October 1917, the Bolsheviks initially tolerated the cooperatives — they were too large and too essential to food distribution to destroy outright. But toleration was temporary. By decree, cooperative assets were nationalised, their elected boards replaced with state appointees, and their distribution networks absorbed into the Commissariat of Food Supply. What remained was a hollow shell: the same name, the same buildings, but none of the independence that had made the cooperatives a genuine expression of popular economic organisation.

## In Dukes' Account

> The almost incredible fact is that the Bolsheviks suppressed the Union of Cooperative Societies. The Cooperative Unions represented an organisation which in any other country would have been regarded as a bulwark against revolution, and which in Russia itself had been one of the most potent forces in the peaceful and constructive side of the revolution. (014-021, 014-023)

This passage, from the memoir's concluding chapter, is Dukes' most concentrated expression of his central thesis: the Bolsheviks destroyed not capitalism but civil society. The cooperatives were not enemies of the revolution — they were "one of the most potent forces in the peaceful and constructive side of the revolution" — but they were independent of the Party, and independence was the one thing the Bolsheviks could not tolerate.

> The Russian Cooperative Unions up to the time when they were forcibly suppressed by the Bolsheviks had been one of the most successful examples of cooperative organisation in the world. (014-023)

The past perfect — "had been" — carries the weight of elegy. The cooperatives were not defeated in economic competition; they were "forcibly suppressed." Dukes' verb choice is unambiguous: this was not absorption, not nationalisation, not even expropriation. It was suppression — the destruction of an institution because it was independent.

## Historical Context

Bolshevik hostility to cooperatives was ideological and practical. Lenin's 1918 article "On Cooperation" acknowledged their utility but insisted they must be subordinated to Soviet power. The practical concern was control of food distribution: independent cooperatives could not be compelled to enforce state grain requisitioning. By 1920, the cooperative movement had been effectively nationalised, its remaining functions absorbed into Narkomprod (the Commissariat of Food Supply). The New Economic Policy (NEP) of 1921 partially revived cooperative trade, but the movement never regained its pre-revolutionary independence.
