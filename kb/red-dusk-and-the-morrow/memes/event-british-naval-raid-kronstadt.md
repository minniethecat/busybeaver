## Overview

The [[British naval raid on Kronstadt]] was one of the boldest [[Allied]] naval operations of the [[Russian Civil War]]. The [[Royal Navy]] penetrated the defences of the [[Bolsheviks]]' most important [[Baltic]] fortress and sank several Russian warships. [[Paul Dukes]] had a personal stake in the operation: an escape plan that would have carried him to [[Finland]] aboard a Soviet tug was thwarted precisely because "the famous British naval raid on Kronstadt was made and several Russian ships were sunk" (013-003).

## Background

[[Kronstadt]], the island fortress guarding the approaches to [[Petrograd]], was "one of the most impregnable" naval bases in the Baltic (007-006). It had been a stronghold of revolutionary sailors in [[1917]] but by 1919 was under firm Bolshevik control. The British fleet operated in the [[Gulf of Finland]], supporting the [[Baltic States]] in their wars of independence and seeking to contain Bolshevik naval power. Dukes was in Petrograd, preparing his escape.

## Events

Dukes describes the raid in the context of a failed escape scheme. A friend at the Admiralty had arranged for "a tug to be placed at the disposal of this officer for certain work near Kronstadt" (013-003). The plan: Dukes would be disguised as a British admiral who had secretly visited Petrograd; the tug would sail past Kronstadt to Finland using "Soviet signals" and the Soviet flag (013-003).

> Two days before the event, the famous British naval raid on Kronstadt was made and several Russian ships were sunk. My friend was ordered there at once to assist in reorganization, and I — well, I failed to become an admiral. (013-003)

The wry humour conceals the dangerous reality: Dukes had to find another way out of [[Russia]].

Kronstadt also features in his intelligence work. Dukes' earlier journeys included reconnaissance of the approaches to the fortress. He notes that maps "showing the plan of the minefields around Kronstadt and in the Finnish Gulf" (004-062) were among the materials gathered by the network. The fortress's strategic importance — controlling access to Petrograd by sea — made it a constant subject of British attention.

## Aftermath

The raid demonstrated the Royal Navy's capacity to strike at the heart of Bolshevik naval power, but it could not dislodge the regime. Dukes' escape plan was a casualty of the raid's success — the increased alert at Kronstadt and the disruption of naval organisation made his cover story impossible, forcing him eventually to escape through [[Latvia]] instead.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' account of Kronstadt typifies his method of mixing serious operational detail with understated humour. The image of himself as a "disguised British admiral" on a Soviet tug, "revolver" ready if the captain "smelt a rat" (013-003), is both comic and deadly serious. The episode shows how Dukes' personal fate was entangled with wider military operations, and how British naval power — a source of hope to many Russians — could complicate as well as assist the work of [[intelligence]].
