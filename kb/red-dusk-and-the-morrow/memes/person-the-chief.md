## Overview

"The Chief" is the pseudonym Paul Dukes uses for the head of British intelligence who personally briefed him before his return to Soviet Russia in 1918. Though Dukes never names him -- and indeed devotes an elaborate comic passage to the impossibility of doing so -- the figure is almost certainly Sir Mansfield Cumming, the first chief of the British Secret Intelligence Service (MI6), known within the service simply as "C."

Dukes' portrait of the Chief is a masterpiece of controlled revelation. The entire interview takes place in a "roof-labyrinth" of deceptive stairways and false bookshelves, culminating in a darkened office where the Chief sits in silhouette against the window. The atmosphere is part spy thriller, part absurdist comedy -- Dukes describes being so disoriented by the building's architecture that "at that moment I could not have expressed a sane opinion on any subject under the sun" (001-028). The deliberate mystification serves both security purposes and narrative effect: by refusing to name the Chief, Dukes simultaneously protects operational secrecy and invests the figure with an almost mythological quality.

## Role in the Narrative

The Chief appears only in Chapter I, "One of the Crowd," during the sequence where Dukes is recruited for his mission to Russia. The interview is prefaced by a series of comic misdirections. A colonel leads Dukes through a warren of passages and stairways, past a bookshelf containing a fake edition of Thackeray, until finally they enter "a spacious study -- the sanctum of '--er -- the Chief'" (001-027).

The character sketch that follows is teasing. Dukes describes the room in detail -- the desk, the bottles of coloured liquids, the wireless apparatus, the strange maps -- but when he reaches the figure at the desk, he abruptly breaks off: "Alas, no! Pardon me, reader, I was forgetting! There are still things I may not divulge" (001-030). He will not describe the Chief's appearance, nor "even one of his twenty-odd names." What he does convey is the impression the Chief made: "awe-inspired as I was at this first encounter, I soon learned to regard 'the Chief' with feelings of the deepest admiration and respect" (001-030).

The actual briefing is brisk and professional. The Chief discusses routes -- "The words Archangel, Stockholm, Riga, Helsingfors recurred frequently" -- and leaves the operational details to Dukes: "It was finally decided that I alone should determine how and by what route I should return to Russia" (001-031). His parting words are characteristically British in their understatement: "Don't go and get killed" (001-032).

## Political Context

The Chief represented the institutional architecture of British intelligence during the final year of the Great War and the early stages of Allied intervention in Russia. MI6 under Cumming had been active in Russia since before the Bolshevik seizure of power, maintaining networks of agents and informants. The decision to send Dukes -- a Russian-speaking former concert pianist with pre-existing contacts in Petrograd -- reflected the service's preference for unconventional agents who could blend into the local population. The Chief's personal involvement in the briefing underscores the importance British intelligence attached to the Russian mission.

## In Dukes' Account

> Awe-inspired as I was at this first encounter, I soon learned to regard "the Chief" with feelings of the deepest admiration and respect. (001-030)

This single sentence, the only direct character judgement Dukes allows himself, carries considerable weight. In a memoir notable for its scepticism toward authority -- Dukes is critical of Bolshevik commissars, White generals, and Allied policymakers alike -- his unqualified respect for the Chief stands out.

> "Don't go and get killed," said the Chief in conclusion, smiling. "You will put him through the ciphers," he added to the colonel, "and take him to the laboratory to learn the inks and all that." (001-032)

The parting instruction, with its matter-of-fact reference to ciphers and invisible inks, captures the peculiar atmosphere of the intelligence world Dukes inhabited: mortal danger discussed in the same tone as laboratory procedures.
