## Overview

Terijoki (now Zelenogorsk) was a Finnish border town on the Karelian Isthmus, approximately fifty kilometres northwest of Petrograd. For Dukes and the network of fugitives, couriers, and intelligence agents who moved between Bolshevik Russia and the outside world, Terijoki was the threshold — the first point of Finnish jurisdiction, the place where Soviet documents ceased to have force and where the border commandant's disposition could mean passage or arrest.

Dukes mentions Terijoki twice, both times in connection with the border commandant — the official whose authority determined whether fugitives crossed safely or were turned back. The "office of the Commandant at Terijoki, some miles distant" (007-034) is the administrative node in a clandestine network, and the change of commandant — from a sympathetic one to "a man of German origin" (007-043) — transforms the border from an escape route into a trap.

## In Dukes' Account

> The office of the Commandant at Terijoki, some miles distant, was where all fugitives from Soviet Russia had to present themselves before being permitted to proceed further into Finland. (007-034, narrative paraphrase)

The Terijoki commandant's office is the chokepoint in Dukes' escape network. Every refugee, every courier, every piece of intelligence that moved through Finland passed through this single office. The commandant's disposition — benign or hostile — determined everything that followed.

> The new Commandant at Terijoki, a man of German origin, who acted independently of the Helsingfors government, was not so favourably disposed towards fugitives from Bolshevism as his predecessor. (007-043)

The change of commandant is one of the memoir's quiet turning points. The previous official had facilitated passage; the new one, acting "independently of the Helsingfors government," was actively hostile. For Dukes' network, this personnel change meant that the Terijoki route — once reliable — had become dangerous. The fugitive's fate, Dukes implies, could turn on the personality of a single border official.

## Description

Terijoki was a small town on the railway line connecting Petrograd to Helsinki. Before the Russian Revolution it had been a popular summer resort for wealthy Petersburgers; after Finnish independence in 1917 and the closure of the Russian border, it became a frontier post, its population swollen with refugees, its hotels converted to military offices, and its railway station the scene of anxious encounters between fugitives and Finnish officials.

## Historical Significance

The Finnish-Russian border was one of the most important frontiers of the Russian Civil War for Allied intelligence. Finland had declared independence in December 1917, and though it fought its own brief but bloody civil war in 1918, by the time of Dukes' operations it was a sovereign state whose border with Russia was, in theory, closed. In practice, the border was porous — controlled by local commanders rather than central governments — and it was through this porosity that Dukes and other British agents maintained contact between Petrograd and the outside world.
