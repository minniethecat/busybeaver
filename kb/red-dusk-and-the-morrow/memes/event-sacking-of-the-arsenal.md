## Overview

The sacking of the Petrograd arsenal on 12 March 1917 was one of the decisive episodes of the March Revolution. Paul Dukes witnessed the event personally and records it as part of the day "the morrow" arrived — the day the revolution broke into the open. The capture of the arsenal by the "infuriated mob" (001-005) provided the insurrectionary forces with the weapons needed to seize control of the capital.

## Background

By the second day of the March Revolution, the military situation in Petrograd was fluid. Regiments were defecting to the revolutionaries, but many units remained loyal to the Tsar or were still undecided. The arsenal — a key military storehouse — held the rifles, ammunition, and equipment that could determine which side prevailed. The decision of soldiers to side with the people, combined with the sheer momentum of the crowds, made the arsenal a natural target.

## Events

Dukes describes the morning of 12 March with concise power: "I saw the first revolutionary regiments come out and witnessed the sacking of the arsenal by the infuriated mob. Over the river the soldiers were breaking into the Kresty Prison" (001-005). The sacking was not an isolated act of looting but part of a coordinated wave of revolutionary violence. The same mob that emptied the arsenal also swelled the crowds around the Tauride Palace. The weapons seized from the arsenal were immediately carried to the Duma building, which Dukes and other revolutionaries laboured "all night long and the next" to turn "into a revolutionary arsenal" (001-006).

## Aftermath

The fall of the arsenal effectively ended organised Tsarist resistance in the capital. By evening, the Tsarist police "had been scattered in the Nevsky Prospect" (001-005). The distribution of arms to revolutionary workers and soldiers, however, also created a long-term problem: armed elements outside state control would persist through the Provisional Government period and into the Bolshevik era. Dukes' phrase "revolutionary arsenal" applied to the Tauride Palace captures the transformation of the seat of imperial governance into a military headquarters of the insurrection.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' account of the sacking is noteworthy because he does not condemn it. Writing as a British officer and a man of order, he might have been expected to deplore mob violence, but he presents the event as a natural and even heroic episode of the revolution. The "infuriated mob" is treated with sympathy — Dukes clearly believed the anger was justified. His narrative of that day — from corpses on the snow in the morning to the arsenal and the prison, the surging crowds, and finally the whispered word "Revolution" — is one of the most vivid passages in the memoir.
