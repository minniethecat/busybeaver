## Overview

Moisei Uritsky was the Bolshevik Commissar for Internal Affairs in the Northern Commune (Petrograd) and a senior official in the Petrograd Cheka. A Jewish revolutionary of the old guard, Uritsky was assassinated on 30 August 1918 by Leonid Kannegiser, a young poet and former cadet. Dukes records this event as one of the immediate catalysts for the Red Terror — the systematic mass execution of political opponents and "class enemies" that followed the attempt on Lenin's life the same day.

Uritsky appears in Dukes' memoir briefly but at a pivotal narrative moment. His murder is invoked as the justification for mass reprisals: "When the Jewish Commissar Uritsky was murdered Zinoviev shot more than five hundred Russians in one day by way of reprisal" (001-063). This single sentence encapsulates Dukes' broader argument about revolutionary violence: that Bolshevik terror was disproportionate, indiscriminate, and deliberately racialised in its logic of collective punishment.

## Role in the Narrative

Dukes does not encounter Uritsky directly. Instead, Uritsky functions in the memoir as a symbolic figure — the murdered commissar whose death legitimises atrocity. The killing is paired with the simultaneous attempt on Lenin's life as the twin provocations for the Red Terror. Dukes' account emphasises the ethnic dimension: Uritsky is specified as "Jewish," and the reprisals target "more than five hundred Russians" — a framing that underscores the sectarian logic of revolutionary violence.

The assassination is referenced in two other key passages. Dukes notes that "Jewish commissars Volodarsky and Uritsky" were killed — "the former by unknown workmen, the latter by a young student as an act of political terrorism" — without "any widespread organisation of anti-Semitic or anti-Bolshevik feeling" (012-033). He argues that anti-Bolshevik violence was individualistic rather than organised. Dukes also observes that the Tauride Palace, once seat of the Duma, was renamed "Palace of Uritsky" (012-063) — an irony he highlights as typical of Bolshevik appropriation of pre-revolutionary institutions.

## Biography

Uritsky was born in 1873 in Cherkasy (then part of the Russian Empire, now Ukraine) to a Jewish family. He joined the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party and aligned with the Mensheviks before shifting to the Bolsheviks. After the October Revolution he was appointed head of the Petrograd Cheka and Commissar for Internal Affairs for the Northern Commune. His assassination by Kannegiser on 30 August 1918 — the same day Fanny Kaplan shot Lenin in Moscow — triggered the official Red Terror decree of 5 September 1918.

## Political Context

Uritsky's assassination and the Red Terror it helped unleash are central to Dukes' argument about the nature of Bolshevik rule. Dukes portrays the Cheka as an institution that required no external provocation to commit mass murder — the assassination merely provided a pretext. His account of "more than five hundred Russians" shot in a single day (001-063) is presented not as exceptional but as characteristic of the regime's relationship to violence.

The renaming of the Tauride Palace — cradle of Russian parliamentarism — to "Palace of Uritsky" illustrates Dukes' recurring theme: the Bolsheviks appropriated the symbols of pre-revolutionary society while destroying its substance. A building that once housed the Duma now memorialised a Cheka commissar whose death was used to justify its successor's terror apparatus.

## In Dukes' Account

> When the Jewish Commissar Uritsky was murdered Zinoviev shot more than five hundred Russians in one day by way of reprisal for one man who was killed by an opponent acting entirely on his own initiative. (001-063)

Dukes' syntax is deliberate: the specification of "Jewish" commissar and "more than five hundred Russians" frames the reprisal as ethnic collective punishment — an act of revolutionary justice in which the identity of the victim determined the scale of the response.

> The only murders that the opponents of Bolshevism had committed were those of the Jewish commissars Volodarsky and Uritsky, the former by unknown workmen, the latter by a young student as an act of political terrorism; but in neither case was there any widespread organisation of anti-Semitic or anti-Bolshevik feeling. (012-033)

This passage is part of Dukes' broader rebuttal of the Bolshevik claim that their terror was a defensive response to organised counter-revolution. He insists the assassinations were isolated acts by individuals, not the work of a coordinated White movement.
