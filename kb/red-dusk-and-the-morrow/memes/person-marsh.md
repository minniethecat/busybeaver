## Overview

Mr. Marsh is the pseudonym Paul Dukes assigns to a fellow British intelligence operative operating under business cover in Petrograd. "Marsh was a prominent businessman," Dukes writes, "to whom I shall refer as Mr. Marsh" (002-014). In a memoir that often reads as a catalogue of political violence and institutional corruption, Marsh's story provides one of its most affecting human dramas: a husband desperately trying to secure his wife's release from Bolshevik captivity before making his own escape across the Finnish frontier.

Marsh represents the human cost of intelligence work. His wife's arrest, interrogation, and imprisonment by the Cheka -- and Marsh's anguished decision to flee, believing his absence would improve her chances -- form a narrative thread that runs through the memoir's early chapters. The scenes in which Marsh, his head and face "bound up in a big black shawl" (002-274), stumbles into the safe flat at "No. 5" to report his wife's latest ordeal, are among the most emotionally immediate passages Dukes ever wrote.

## Role in the Narrative

Marsh enters the story before Dukes even arrives in Petrograd, as a name on a list of contacts. Dukes' first act upon reaching the city is to seek him out: "I want Mr. Marsh," he tells a servant at Marsh's house, only to receive the reply: "Marsh is in prison" (002-017). This initial disappearance sets the tone -- in Bolshevik Russia, even prominent British businessmen vanish without explanation.

When Marsh does appear, it is in a safe flat accessed through a filthy courtyard, identified only by "No. 5" chalked on the back door. He enters in disguise, his head wrapped in a black shawl. "It was a strange Marsh that emerged from the folds of the black shawl. The invincible smile struggled to maintain itself, but his eyes were bleared and wandered aimlessly, and he shook with agitation despite his efforts to retain self-control" (002-275). His wife, he reports, had been subjected to "seven hours' cross-examination -- uninterruptedly -- no food -- not even allowed to sit down -- until finally she swooned" (002-276).

Marsh's decision to flee is rational but agonising. The Policeman, their Tsarist informant, advises that "it's worse for her that I should be here than if I go. Once they know I've fled, the Policeman says, they will cease plaguing her" (002-279). Dukes promises to take over: "I had already resolved that I would attempt nothing else until we had safely got your wife out of prison. And the day she gets out I will escort her over the frontier myself" (002-280).

The departure scene is among the memoir's most memorable. Marsh is "dressed in a huge sheepskin coat with a fur collar half engulfing his face," using the passport of his coachman (002-293). "Confident that he was doing the right thing by quitting, Marsh had completely recovered his former good spirits and joked boisterously as he put a finishing touch here and there to his disguise" (002-294). But the parting is wrenching: "I heard him embrace them both, heard Maria's convulsive sobs -- and I hurried down the stone stairway and out into the street" (002-297).

## Biography

Marsh was a British businessman whose commercial activities in Russia provided cover for intelligence work. The Bolsheviks "suspected Marsh, together with other Englishmen, of espionage" (002-126). His wife was imprisoned at a Cheka facility and subjected to prolonged interrogation. Marsh himself escaped across the Finnish frontier with the help of a guide named Fita, leaving Dukes to manage the network and attempt to secure Mrs. Marsh's release. His country house and farm were sacked by Bolsheviks, and one of his stable-boys joined the Party and "was now appointed Commissar in supreme control of the establishment" (002-295).

## In Dukes' Account

> It was a strange Marsh that emerged from the folds of the black shawl. The invincible smile struggled to maintain itself, but his eyes were bleared and wandered aimlessly, and he shook with agitation despite his efforts to retain self-control. (002-275)

The "invincible smile" -- a detail Dukes establishes early as Marsh's defining characteristic -- becomes, in its struggle to maintain itself, the measure of how much Marsh has endured. The physical description is precise and devastating.

> "My wife -- " he stammered, half coherently, dropping into a chair and fumbling feverishly for his handkerchief. "She was subjected yesterday -- seven hours' cross-examination -- uninterruptedly -- no food -- not even allowed to sit down -- until finally she swooned. She has said something -- I don't know what. I am afraid -- " (002-276)

The broken syntax, the dashes, the inability to complete a thought -- Dukes' rendering of Marsh's speech captures a man on the edge of breakdown. This is not the language of espionage reports; it is the language of human suffering.
