## Overview

The bourgeoisie was, in Bolshevik ideology, the class enemy — the obstacle whose destruction was the precondition for proletarian liberation. In Paul Dukes' memoir, "bourgeois" functions less as a precise sociological category than as a political curse. He documents how the label was applied promiscuously — to shopkeepers and professors, to peasants who owned a second cow, to foreign socialists who expressed reservations about Bolshevik methods.

Dukes, moving through Russian society in multiple disguises, observed the bourgeoisie's destruction from within. His account captures the economic mechanisms — confiscation, rationing, "tightening" (the progressive squeezing of the propertied classes) — as well as the psychological terror of being marked as a class enemy. The memoir's subtitle, "Red Dusk and the Morrow," gestures toward this twilight of an entire social order: the bourgeoisie were witnessing not merely their political defeat but their extinction as a class.

## In Bolshevik Russia

The Bolsheviks' class war against the bourgeoisie was both ideological and practical. Ideologically, the bourgeoisie had to be eliminated because Marxist theory required it. Practically, bourgeois property — flats, furniture, clothing, food stores — was the revolution's only immediately available resource. Dukes observes the process with a clinical eye: the "bourgeois stamp" of people's dress and bearing marked them for confiscation, while those who could pass for proletarian survived (002-034).

The Bolshevik orators' rhetoric, which Dukes records in detail, was saturated with anti-bourgeois hatred. As the Doctor explains, "the impulse is not love of the worker, but hatred of the bourgeois" (005-009). This hatred was not incidental to Bolshevism but its emotional core — the Bolshevik "believes that if a perfect proletarian state be forcibly established by destroying the bourgeoisie, the perfect proletarian citizen will automatically result" (005-009). Dukes identifies this syllogism — destroy the bourgeois, and utopia follows — as the central fallacy of Bolshevik thought.

The regime's class-war rhetoric also served to delegitimise dissent. Any criticism of Bolshevik policy could be dismissed as "bourgeois" — and therefore counterrevolutionary — regardless of its source or substance. This linguistic inflation, Dukes argues, impoverished political discourse and made genuine debate impossible.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes' most original contribution to understanding the bourgeoisie's fate is his observation that Bolshevism's class war was, in part, a war of envy. The Bolshevik rank and file, drawn from the least educated strata of society, targeted the bourgeoisie not merely for political reasons but because bourgeois culture — its manners, its possessions, its literacy — was an implicit reproach. The destruction of bourgeois Russia was, in this sense, a cultural as much as a political project.

Dukes also notes the irony that the Bolsheviks, having destroyed the old bourgeoisie, were creating a new one. The Party functionaries, Chekists, and Red commanders who replaced the old elites developed their own privileges — better housing, special rations, access to imported goods. The classless society, as Dukes observed it, was generating new classes with remarkable speed.
