## Overview

As Paul Dukes wrote the concluding chapter of his memoir, news was arriving of "the affliction of Russia with one of her periodical famine scourges" (014-002). The famine of 1920-21 was not merely a natural disaster but a consequence of Bolshevik policies — war communism, forced grain requisitions, and the suppression of private trade had combined with drought to produce mass starvation. Dukes' analysis of the famine is inseparable from his broader critique of the regime's economic mismanagement.

## Background

War communism — the Bolsheviks' policy of forced grain requisition, state monopoly of trade, and suppression of markets — had destroyed agricultural incentives. Dukes describes the system of "forced requisitions" whereby grain was seized from peasants, who then hid what they grew (012-026). The elimination of profit motive had "eliminated the stimulus to produce" (014-002). By 1920, agricultural output had collapsed. When drought struck the Volga region, the cumulative effects were catastrophic.

## Events

The famine unfolded as Dukes was completing his memoir. He describes it as "an event which cannot fail to affect the country politically as well as economically" (014-002). His earlier observations of Petrograd life prefigure the larger disaster: the starvation was already visible on the streets, where people's "faces bore the imprint of starvation, distress, and terror" (008-055). Bread queues stretched for blocks; "for three days I have eaten nothing," an old man told Dukes on his first day back in Russia (002-004). "What is the good of money?" the man asked. "Where shall I get bread?" (002-005).

The Bolsheviks, having "reducing the city to a state of starvation" (012-010), now faced a peasantry that "maddened with hunger, disease, and despair" (011-083) might rise against them. The Soviet government had to make "herself completely dependent for rescue upon the countries against which her governors have declared a ruthless political war" (014-002) — exactly as Dukes predicted.

## Aftermath

The famine forced Lenin to abandon war communism and introduce the New Economic Policy (NEP) in 1921, restoring limited private trade. Dukes notes that "the restoration of liberty of trading came too late to be effective" for the famine itself (014-002). The regime's appeal to Western philanthropy — the American Relief Administration under Herbert Hoover — saved millions of lives but created the extraordinary spectacle of a self-proclaimed revolutionary government begging its capitalist enemies for bread. The famine became a permanent stain on Bolshevik legitimacy.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' account of the famine is remarkable for being written in real time — he was drafting his conclusion as the news arrived, and his analysis of what it meant politically was part of the memoir itself. "The Communists are between the devil and the deep sea," he writes. "To say 'Russia first' is equivalent to abandoning hope of the world revolution, for Russia can only be restored by capitalistic and bourgeois enterprise" (014-003). This prediction — that the Bolsheviks would have to choose between ideology and national survival — proved accurate when the NEP was introduced.
