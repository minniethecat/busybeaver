## Overview

The Tauride Palace was one of the most consequential buildings in revolutionary Petrograd. Built in the late 18th century for Prince Grigory Potemkin, it housed the Imperial State Duma from 1906 and became, in March 1917, the centre of the revolution that toppled the Romanov dynasty. After the Bolshevik seizure of power, the palace was renamed the Palace of Uritsky (in honour of the assassinated Cheka chief Moisei Uritsky) and became the meeting place of the Petrograd Soviet.

For Dukes, the Tauride Palace was both a site of personal witness and a symbol of the revolution's trajectory — from the hopeful crowds outside its gates in March 1917 to the cynicism and drudgery of Soviet meetings he observed as an undercover agent.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' first encounter with the Tauride Palace occurred on the night of 11 March 1917, as the February Revolution gathered momentum: "Late that night I stood outside the Tauride Palace, which had become the centre of the revolution. No one was admitted through the great gates without a pass" (001-006). Earlier that day, he had witnessed "crushing throngs" surging around the Duma building at the palace as soldiers were breaking into the Kresty Prison and the arsenal was being sacked by the mob (001-005). Unable to enter through the gates, Dukes describes how he "scrambled up, dropped over the railings, and ran through the bushes" — a surreptitious entry that foreshadowed his later career as an undercover operative.

Dukes returned to the Tauride Palace after the Bolshevik consolidation of power, now as an undercover agent attending the opening day of the Petrograd Soviet. Armed with an order as a guest from his regiment, he made his way to "the famous Tauride Palace, now called 'Palace of Uritsky,' the seat of the former Duma" (012-063). The scene within the palace had changed: the revolutionary fervour of 1917 had given way to bureaucratic routine. Dukes pictures to himself "the memorable days and nights of March, 1917," contrasting the idealism of the revolution's birth with the grim realities of its mature form.

The chapter "The Party and the People" features a section titled "A Meeting outside the Tauride Palace" (012-052), situating the building as the backdrop for the memoir's analysis of the gap between Soviet rhetoric and popular sentiment.

## Historical Significance

The Tauride Palace witnessed the entire arc of the Russian Revolution — from the formation of the Provisional Government within its walls in March 1917 to the Bolshevik dissolution of the Constituent Assembly in January 1918. As the seat of the Petrograd Soviet, it was one of the dual-power institutions that contested authority during the provisional period. Dukes' observations from inside Soviet meetings at the palace offer a rare first-hand account of the early Soviet political culture.
