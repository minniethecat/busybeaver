## Overview

Viborg (now Vyborg, Russia; historically Viipuri in Finnish) was a small town on the Finnish side of the Russo-Finnish border, situated within striking distance of Petrograd. During the period of Dukes' operations, it was part of the newly independent Republic of Finland. For Dukes, Viborg was the springboard for his first entry into Soviet Russia — the place where he met Dmitri Melnikoff, the Russian monarchist officer who would become his indispensable facilitator at the frontier.

Dukes describes Viborg tersely as "the little town of Viborg," set "between the Russian frontier and Helsingfors" (001-037). Its modest scale belied its strategic significance: situated on the route between the Finnish capital and Petrograd, it was a natural staging point for cross-border operations and a node in the Allied intelligence network.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes travelled to Viborg from Helsingfors carrying a letter of introduction to Melnikoff from an agent of the American Secret Service he had met at the British Consulate (001-037). The three days he spent in Viborg with Melnikoff were decisive for his mission. "We spent three days together in Viborg making plans for Petrograd while he drank up all my whisky except a small medicine bottle full which I hid away" (001-040). During this time, Melnikoff devised the arrangements for Dukes to be secretly put across the Finnish-Russian frontier at night.

The parting scene in Viborg is among the memoir's most affecting passages. Dukes describes Melnikoff's transformation for the journey ahead: "He wore a sort of seaman's cap that came right down over his eyes. He had dirtied his face" (001-041). Before departing, Melnikoff invoked "the good old Russian custom" of sitting down together for a minute in silence — a tradition observed before a long journey (001-042).

Viborg reappears later in the memoir when Dukes discovers that the Finnish border commandant at Terijoki has been replaced by a German-sympathising officer (007-035). The shifting allegiances in these small Finnish border towns made Viborg and its environs an unreliable and ever-dangerous corridor.

## Historical Significance

Viborg occupied a strategic position on the Karelian Isthmus, the narrow strip of land between the Gulf of Finland and Lake Ladoga. For centuries it was contested between Sweden, Russia, and Finland. In 1918–1919, as an independent Finnish town near the Soviet frontier, it was a focal point for intelligence operations, refugee flows, and the clandestine movement of agents between the Bolshevik state and the outside world.
