## Overview

The All-Russian Extraordinary Commission for Combating Counter-Revolution and Sabotage — the Cheka, or "Chrezvychayka" — was established by the Bolsheviks in December 1917 and quickly became "the most notorious of all Bolshevist institutions" (002-266). For Paul Dukes, the Cheka was the omnipresent threat that shadowed his every move in Russia. Its Petrograd headquarters at No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street was a name spoken in whispers, a place everyone feared and no one dared mention lightly.

## Background

The Bolsheviks seized power in November 1917 without a functioning security apparatus. Within weeks, they created the Cheka under Felix Dzerzhinsky's leadership, tasked with suppressing counterrevolution. Dukes describes it as "an instrument of terror and inquisition designed forcibly to uproot all anti-Bolshevist sentiment throughout Lenin's dominions" (002-266). Its brutal methods, he notes, were continuous with the worst practices of Tsarism: the technique of "provocation" — sending agents to foment conspiracies that could then be crushed — was "one of the most diabolic of the methods copied from Tsarist days" (002-271).

## Events

The Cheka's Petrograd headquarters at No. 2 Gorokhovaya Street became the focus of the city's terror. Dukes records how the mere mention of the address silenced conversation. When someone was arrested, the response was: "I leave him alone. I am wiser than that. There is only one place I avoid, and that is No. 2 Gorokhovaya!" (002-249). The systematic nature of Cheka operations extended to legal procedure: "at the head of No. 2 Gorokhovaya there sits a 'soviet,' or council, which has the final word as to the fate of prisoners" (002-267).

Prisoners entered through "the dark aperture of No. 2 Gorokhovaya" and rarely emerged (011-026). The building was so notorious that Dukes records someone saying: "'kindly step this way to No. 2 Gorokhovaya! Ugh!' he shuddered. 'No. 2 Gorokhovaya!'" (003-091). Prisoners could also be sent to the Shpalernaya or Deriabinskaya prisons (003-080). The terror had a routine: "the terror-hour had not yet come — it came only at seven each evening" (003-155), when names were called and prisoners were led to execution.

## Aftermath

The Cheka set the pattern for all subsequent Soviet security services: the GPU, OGPU, NKVD, and KGB. Dukes' testimony is significant because it captures the institution at its formative moment, when its methods were being established and its reputation was ripening into terror. The Cheka's reach extended into every aspect of life — "a delegation from No. 2 Gorokhovaya" could search any house, and "there was no house in the city where such a delegation might not one day appear" (009-056).

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes' relationship with the Cheka was one of permanent evasion. He lived under a false identity with a Cheka-issued passport that was itself a deception. He describes the Cheka's infiltrators with professional respect: some agents were "sincere and upright, though demoniacal visionaries, cold as steel, cruel, unpolluted by thirst for filthy lucre, who see the dawn of proletarian liberty only through mists of non-proletarian blood" (002-268). His survival depended on understanding this enemy, and his assessment — that the Cheka was the honest face of Bolshevik rule, the institution that revealed what the regime really was — shapes the entire memoir.
