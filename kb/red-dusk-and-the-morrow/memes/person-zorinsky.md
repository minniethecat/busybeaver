## Overview

Captain Zorinsky is one of the most vivid and morally ambiguous figures in Paul Dukes' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*. A former Tsarist officer who has adapted with cynical skill to life under Bolshevik rule, Zorinsky serves as Dukes' principal intermediary in the attempt to secure the release of the imprisoned officer Melnikoff. He is a double-dealer par excellence -- charming, knowledgeable, and entirely untrustworthy.

Dukes' portrait of Zorinsky is sharp and ambivalent. On the one hand, Zorinsky is an invaluable source of political intelligence, "displaying a remarkable knowledge of everybody's movements and actions" (002-239). On the other, he is a consummate manipulator who extracts sixty thousand roubles from Dukes for a prisoner who, it transpires, has already been shot. Zorinsky embodies what Dukes saw as the moral degradation produced by Bolshevik rule: a world where survival depended on playing all sides and trusting no one.

## Role in the Narrative

Zorinsky first approaches Dukes at the cafe of Vera Alexandrovna on the day of Dukes' return to Petrograd. "Allow me to introduce myself. Captain Zorinsky. You are waiting for Melnikoff, are you not? I am a friend of his" (002-043). Dukes' immediate suspicion -- "Why had Melnikoff not told me I should meet this 'friend of his'?" -- proves prescient (002-044).

The relationship deepens after Melnikoff's arrest. Zorinsky reappears at the moment of crisis, tapping Dukes on the arm outside the building where Melnikoff was seized (002-215). He takes Dukes to his flat -- "large and luxuriously furnished, and showed no signs of molestation" (002-227) -- and explains that his wife is an actress: "She receives as many provisions as she wants and our flat is immune from requisition" (002-227). The contrast between Zorinsky's comfort and the general destitution could not be sharper.

Zorinsky's conversation is a masterclass in manipulation. He speaks candidly of his intelligence work: "It is an amusing game -- contre-espionnage. I used to provide your Captain Cromie with quite a lot of information" (002-234). He shares opposition intelligence reports, offers political analysis, and casually inquires about other members of Dukes' network -- including Marsh: "By the way, you don't happen to know where Marsh is, do you?" (002-242). The question, delivered with studied casualness, reveals Zorinsky's method: every interaction is an intelligence-gathering operation.

The dinner at Zorinsky's flat is one of the memoir's most revealing set-pieces. "A sumptuous banquet for the Petrograd of the period" (002-263) is served while Zorinsky holds forth on the Russian peasant ("a brute") and muses cynically about his own survival strategy: "You get plenty of everything if you keep your wits about you. Even without joining the Communist Party" (002-260, 002-261). His wife Elena Ivanovna, "a jolly little creature, but very much of a spoilt child" (002-254), complains about the vulgar sailors at the theatre while wearing a pink Japanese kimono. The scene is a miniature of the Bolshevik elite's corruption.

Zorinsky's most damning act is invisible to Dukes until too late. He claims to be working on Melnikoff's release, but repeatedly "shelved the subject, giving evasive answers" (005-022). He invents expenses -- payment for "Melnikoff's sister" whom the Doctor reveals "hasn't got one" (005-029). The Doctor's warning is clear: "Look out, my friend, look out. Your friend is certainly a clever and a useful man. But I'm afraid you will have to go on paying for Melnikoff's nonexistent sister" (005-031). By the time Dukes learns through the Policeman that Melnikoff "was shot between the 15th and 20th of January" (005-106), Zorinsky has already pocketed sixty thousand roubles.

## Biography

Zorinsky's background is reconstructed from his own conversation. He was a captain in the Tsarist army who served during the Great War. He claimed not to be a Communist -- "I am not a Communist," he tells Dukes, "but still I keep that door open" (002-260). His wife Elena Ivanovna was an actress whose troupe was "treated well" by the Bolsheviks, receiving provisions and immunity from housing requisitions (002-257). Zorinsky had previously supplied intelligence to Captain Cromie, the British naval attache in Petrograd whose mysterious death at the British Embassy Dukes earlier records (002-234).

## In Dukes' Account

> He also gave his views of the Russian people and the revolution. "The Russian peasant," he said, "is a brute. What he wants is a good hiding, and unless I'm much mistaken the Communists are going to give it to him. Otherwise the Communists go under. In my regiment we used to smash a jaw now and again. It doesn't hurt the peasants; it's plaster." (002-261)

This passage is a study in the casual brutality of Zorinsky's worldview. His remark about the Communists "giving" the peasant a hiding reveals his fundamental indifference to ideology -- he is interested only in who holds power and how to profit from proximity to it.

> "You get plenty of everything if you keep your wits about you," said Zorinsky. "Even without joining the Communist Party. I am not a Communist," he added (somehow I had not suspected it), "but still I keep that door open." (002-260)

Zorinsky's confession of non-membership, delivered over vodka at his well-appointed dinner table, is the memoir's purest expression of the cynical accommodation that Dukes saw as characteristic of the Bolshevik elite's hangers-on.
