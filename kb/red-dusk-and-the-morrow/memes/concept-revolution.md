## Overview

[[Revolution]], for [[Paul Dukes]], was not an abstract political concept but a lived experience. His memoir opens with one of the most vivid eyewitness accounts of the [[March 1917 revolution]] in [[Petrograd]] — the deserted [[Nevsky Prospect]], the bloodstains on snow, the distant crack of rifles. Dukes was present at the birth of revolutionary [[Russia]], and his memoir is at its most powerful when recounting the millenarian hope of those early days — and its subsequent betrayal.

The concept of "revolution" in Dukes' account functions on multiple levels. It is the historical event he witnessed, the political ideology he analysed, and the rhetorical weapon the [[Bolsheviks]] deployed to justify their rule. By documenting the gap between revolutionary promise and Bolshevik reality, Dukes produced one of the first insider critiques of the revolution's degeneration.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes' description of the March revolution is cinematic in its immediacy: "The snow glittered brilliantly in the frosty sunshine on the afternoon of March 11, 1917. The Nevsky Prospect was almost deserted" (001-002). He records the first revolutionary regiments, the sacking of the arsenal, and the surging crowds around the [[Tauride Palace]] — all from the perspective of someone who scrambled over railings to participate (001-005). That night he stood outside the Tauride Palace, which "had become the centre of the revolution," and found himself labouring alongside revolutionists "to turn the Tauride Palace into a revolutionary arsenal" (001-006).

But the revolution Dukes witnessed was not the one that prevailed. The Bolsheviks, he argues, hijacked a popular uprising and transformed it into a class dictatorship. His memoir chronicles this transformation: the replacement of revolutionary spontaneity with [[Party discipline]], of popular hope with bureaucratic terror, of the Declaration of Independence of Russia with the instruments of state repression.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes' analysis of revolution is coloured by his early enthusiasm and subsequent disillusionment:

> I figured to myself a huge pendulum, weighted with the pent-up miseries and woes of a hundred and eighty millions of people, which had suddenly been set in motion. How far would it swing? How many times? When and where would it come to rest? (001-005)

This image of a pendulum — massive, uncontrollable, destructive in its accumulated energy — captures his mature view of revolution as a force that exceeds human direction.

He draws on Lord Bryce's observation that "even when one thinks a view unsound or a scheme unworkable, one must regard all honest efforts to improve this unsatisfactory world with a sympathy which is not inconsistent with searching criticism" (P01-008). This principle — sympathetic but searching — defines Dukes' own approach to revolution. He does not condemn the revolutionary impulse but the Bolsheviks' corruption of it.
