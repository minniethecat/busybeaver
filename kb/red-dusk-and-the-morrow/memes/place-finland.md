## Overview

Finland was a territory of the Russian Empire until the Bolshevik Revolution, after which it declared independence in December 1917. For Paul Dukes, Finland was the critical transit corridor between the West and Soviet Russia — the route through which he entered Bolshevik territory in late 1918, the base from which he coordinated courier operations, and the place of brief refuge when he needed to escape Petrograd. The entire seventh chapter of *Red Dusk and the Morrow* is devoted to Finland, underscoring its operational importance to Dukes' mission.

Finland at this time was "a sort of dumping-ground for secret service agents of many nations," a neutral space where "all nationalities fraternised" in the common cause of penetrating Bolshevik Russia (001-036). Its proximity to Petrograd — just forty-odd miles across the frozen Gulf of Finland — made it an ideal staging ground for intelligence operations.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' relationship with Finland began in Helsingfors (Helsinki), the capital, where he made his first attempt at entering Soviet Russia after failing at Archangel. He describes Helsingfors as "a busy little city bristling with life and intrigue," where he was introduced to a network of anti-Bolshevik operatives (001-036). It was here that Dukes met Melnikoff, the Russian monarchist officer who would become his key facilitator at the Finnish-Russian border.

The chapter "Finland" (Chapter VII) provides the memoir's most detailed account of Dukes' operational methods. He describes making the perilous journey across the frozen Gulf of Finland by sledge — a forty-odd-mile passage over ice, navigating snow ridges and avoiding Bolshevik patrols (007-004). The crossing is rendered with vivid immediacy: "flying at breakneck speed across the ice, windswept after recent storms," twice capsizing completely into the snow (007-005).

Finland also features as a place of disappointment and danger. On one return journey, Dukes was arrested by Finnish patrols who suspected him of being a Bolshevik spy, and discovered that a new commandant had been appointed at Terijoki, a man hostile to Allied agents (007-034, 007-035). His time in Helsingfors was "occupied mostly with endeavours to obtain official assurances" that his couriers would not be seized or shot by the Finns (007-043).

## Description

Dukes describes the Finnish landscape primarily in winter — the frozen Gulf, the snow-covered forests, the remote coastguard stations. The description of Stáraya Derévnya, a remote suburb of Petrograd overlooking the Gulf of Finland, serves as the departure point for his ice crossings (007-002). The Finnish wilderness is portrayed as both a natural barrier and a passageway, its frozen expanses offering cover for clandestine movement.

## Historical Significance

Finland occupied a unique position in the Russian Civil War. An independent republic with a recent history of its own civil war between Reds and Whites, it harboured deep suspicion of the Bolshevik regime while maintaining an official posture of neutrality. This made it, in Dukes' apt phrase, a "dumping-ground" for intelligence agents, a place where British, American, German, and Russian anti-Bolshevik operatives all converged and competed (001-036). The Finnish frontier was one of the most active clandestine corridors in the Allied intervention against Soviet Russia.
