## Overview

General Nikolai Yudenich was a [[White Army]] commander during the [[Russian Civil War]] who led the Northwestern Army's offensive against [[Petrograd]] in the autumn of 1919. His forces advanced to the outskirts of the city, triggering a crisis for the [[Bolshevik]] government. [[Paul Dukes]], who was in Petrograd at the time, records the Soviet government's accusation that he himself was "the instigator of a 'White' Government which should seize power upon the fall of the city" (P01-005) — a charge Dukes dismisses, noting he had no knowledge of or connection with the alleged government.

## Role in the Narrative

Yudenich appears in Dukes' memoir at two critical moments. In the Foreword, Dukes recounts how the Bolshevik government used Yudenich's advance as a pretext to accuse him of conspiracy: "on the occasion of the last advance of General Yudenich on Petrograd the Bolshevist Government asserted that I was the instigator of a 'White' Government" (P01-005). The charge, which Dukes dismisses as entirely fabricated, included a published list of supposed ministers — nearly all of whom Dukes had never met.

Later, in [[Chapter X]], Dukes recounts the story of a bridge engineer with White sympathies who was instructed to destroy strategic bridges to impede Yudenich's advance. The engineer resolved to "blow up the wrong bridge, and, if possible, cut off the Red retreat and assist the White advance," but "by sheer mistake, however, the company he despatched to perform the task blew up the right bridge, thus covering a precipitate Red retreat and effectually checking the White advance" (010-007). The episode reflects the chaos and divided loyalties that characterised the Civil War period.
