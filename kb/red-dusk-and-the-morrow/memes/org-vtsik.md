## Overview

The All-Russian Central Executive Committee (VTsIK) was the permanent legislative body that governed the Russian Soviet Republic between sessions of the All-Russian Congress of Soviets. Under the 1918 Constitution, VTsIK was the "supreme legislative, administrative, and controlling organ" — in theory the Soviet equivalent of a standing parliament. In practice, it was dominated by the Bolshevik Party and increasingly functioned as a ratifying body for Sovnarkom decrees.

Dukes encounters VTsIK not as an institution but as a credential. The frontier commissar who interrogates him brandishes a document bearing the heading "Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee of the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies" (001-079). The title's extravagant length — spanning three tiers of Soviet government — is, for Dukes, the essence of Bolshevik administration: authority cascading through overlapping bodies, each claiming to represent the revolution, each wielding absolute power in its sphere.

## Structure and Role

VTsIK was elected by the All-Russian Congress of Soviets and was nominally accountable to it. In the early months of Soviet power, when the Congress met infrequently, VTsIK exercised real legislative authority — issuing decrees, appointing commissars, and overseeing the Sovnarkom. But as the Bolsheviks consolidated one-party rule, VTsIK's independence evaporated. By the time Dukes was operating in Russia (1918–1919), the body had already been reduced to ceremonial functions, its formal supremacy a fiction maintained for constitutional propriety.

The VTsIK credential that Dukes encounters — "Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee" — illustrates the regime's technique of governance through extraordinary powers. The word "Extraordinary" (chrezvychayny) was the Soviet state's most potent adjective: it suspended normal procedure and conferred unlimited authority. An Extraordinary Commissar of VTsIK answered to no one but the centre, and the centre was Lenin.

## In Dukes' Account

> The heading on the paper read: "Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee of the Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Red Armymen's Deputies." The title was as long as it was terrifying — a cascade of institutions, each more remote from accountability than the last. (001-079, narrative paraphrase)

Dukes' close reading of the commissar's credentials is the memoir's most concentrated study of Soviet bureaucratic language. The title is a palimpsest of revolutionary authority: the Petrograd Soviet (workers' democracy), the Central Executive Committee (Soviet parliament), the Extraordinary Commissar (unlimited executive power). Each layer claims to represent the people; each layer is further removed from them.

> In the service of the Extraordinary Commission? I gasped, taken aback by the revelation. But the Extraordinary Commissar of the Central Executive Committee was a different creature from the Extraordinary Commission of the Cheka — different in title, identical in effect. (001-081, narrative paraphrase)

Dukes' confusion between the Extraordinary Commissar (VTsIK) and the Extraordinary Commission (Cheka) is deliberate. The two bodies, formally distinct, merge in his experience: both wield arbitrary power, both answer only to the centre, both reduce citizens to supplicants.

## Historical Context

VTsIK was formally replaced by the Supreme Soviet under the 1936 Constitution (operative 1937). Throughout its existence it was chaired by Bolshevik loyalists — Yakov Sverdlov (1917–1919) and Mikhail Kalinin (1919–1937). Its decline from legislative body to rubber stamp paralleled the broader transformation of Soviet institutions: what began as organs of workers' power became instruments of party dictatorship.
