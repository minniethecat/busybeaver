## Overview

Dmitri Dmitrievitch Melnikoff (real name Nicholas Nicholaievitch N.) was a Russian military officer and anti-Bolshevik conspirator whose arrest and imprisonment by the Cheka became the focal point of Paul Dukes' undercover operations in Petrograd. Melnikoff is, in a sense, the absent centre of Dukes' memoir -- a man who never appears directly in the narrative but whose fate drives much of the action. Dukes' attempts to secure his release, for which he paid sixty thousand roubles to the intermediary Zorinsky, constitute one of the memoir's most sustained dramatic threads.

The Melnikoff case illustrates several of Dukes' key themes: the venality of the Bolshevik apparatus (Zorinsky's extortion under the pretence of bribery), the human cost of the Cheka's terror, and the moral complexities of intelligence work. The revelation late in the narrative that Melnikoff had been shot "between the 15th and 20th of January" -- news delivered by the deadpan informant Dukes calls "the Policeman" -- is among the memoir's bleakest moments (005-106).

## Role in the Narrative

Melnikoff is introduced indirectly, through Dukes' initial contact with "the Doctor," who turns out to be Melnikoff's uncle. The relationship is revealed when the woman at the lodge tells Dukes that "the Doctor was Melnikoff's uncle" (005-007). Dukes had previously known Melnikoff as a fellow anti-Bolshevik; they had "stayed together at Viborg," where Melnikoff "helped dress me" and "drank all my whisky" (005-013). The whisky detail, seemingly trivial, proves crucial: it is the Doctor's confirmation that Dukes gave Melnikoff whisky that convinces him of Dukes' bona fides (005-015).

The financial dimension of the Melnikoff case is stark. Dukes calculates: "Besides the sixty thousand for Melnikoff, I have given him a few thousand for odd expenses connected with the case" (005-028). When the Doctor reveals that Melnikoff "hasn't got" a sister, the supposed recipient of some of Dukes' payments, the true nature of Zorinsky's operation becomes clear (005-029). Dukes' response -- "then where did the money go?" -- captures the memoir's preoccupation with the corruption that permeated Bolshevik governance (005-030).

The denouement is devastating in its brevity. After weeks of waiting and evasions from Zorinsky, Dukes turns to an alternative source. The Policeman, reading from "a thin strip of paper," delivers the news: "He was shot between the 15th and 20th of January" (005-106). The chapter ends with this revelation, and Melnikoff's death becomes a silent reproach to the entire enterprise of negotiation with the Bolshevik regime.

## Biography

Melnikoff was a Russian military officer of the Tsarist army who opposed the Bolshevik Revolution from its outset. According to the Doctor, he "together with a considerable group of officers, had opposed the revolution from the outset" (005-007). His real name -- Nicholas Nicholaievitch N. -- is withheld in the published text, presumably for security reasons, as the memoir was published in 1922 when many of the individuals mentioned might still have been at risk.

Melnikoff was a friend of Shura, a law student who served as an intermediary in Dukes' network, and the nephew of the Doctor, a man of radical political sympathies who nonetheless maintained his family ties across the political divide -- a nuance Dukes appreciates, noting the Doctor "had not quarrelled with" his nephew's faction "realizing one cardinal truth the Bolsheviks appear to fail to grasp" (005-007). Melnikoff was arrested by the Bolsheviks at an unspecified date before Dukes' return to Petrograd and executed in January 1919.

## In Dukes' Account

> Except for me he wouldn't have come back here. Didn't he tell you how we stayed together at Viborg, how he helped dress me, how he drank all my whisky, how -- ? (005-013)

This outburst, delivered in English at the tense first meeting with the Doctor, is Dukes' most personal recollection of Melnikoff. The catalogue of shared experiences -- shelter, dress, whisky -- conveys the intimacy of underground life, where trust is built on small shared details rather than ideological alignment.

> "He was shot between the 15th and 20th of January," said the Policeman. (005-106)

The laconic announcement, set off as the closing line of Chapter V, is a masterstroke of narrative restraint. After the elaborate machinations of bribery, forged passports, and clandestine meetings, Melnikoff's death is reported with bureaucratic brevity. The reader, like Dukes, is left to absorb the implications in silence.
