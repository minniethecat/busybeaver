## Overview

"The Doctor" is one of the most sympathetic and fully realised figures in Paul Dukes' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*. Never identified by his real name, he was the uncle of the imprisoned officer Melnikoff and became, following a tense and dramatic first encounter, one of Dukes' most reliable confidants in revolutionary Petrograd. The Doctor represents a type that Dukes clearly admired: the educated Russian radical who had supported the March 1917 revolution on principle but was appalled by the Bolsheviks' descent into terror and authoritarianism.

Dukes' portrait of the Doctor is warm and nuanced. "The Doctor, who I learned from the woman at the lodge was Melnikoff's uncle, was a splendid fellow. As a matter of fact, he had sided wholeheartedly with the revolution in March, 1917, and held very radical views, but he thought more than spoke about them" (005-007). In a memoir notable for its sharp political judgements, this character reference stands out for its personal generosity. The Doctor embodies what Dukes saw as the tragedy of the revolution: genuine radicals sidelined by Bolshevik extremists who could not tolerate honest disagreement.

## Role in the Narrative

The Doctor enters the narrative at the beginning of Chapter V, introduced as Dukes' new contact following his return to Petrograd. Their first meeting is one of the memoir's most dramatic scenes -- an encounter charged with mutual suspicion. Dukes presents himself as "a friend of Melnikoff's," and the Doctor responds with polite but categorical denial: "No, sir, he knew nothing whatsoever of his nephew's movements" (005-010). The impasse is broken only when Dukes, in exasperation, switches to English: "Then, damn it all, man, why the devil can't you see I am an Englishman and not a provocateur?" (005-013).

What follows is a remarkable conversion. The Doctor's urbane, fixed smile "suddenly burst into a half-laugh" (005-014) and he asks, in Russian, the question that changes everything: "Was it you who gave him the whisky?" (005-015). The private detail -- Melnikoff's fondness for whisky, shared with Dukes at Viborg -- proves more persuasive than any political argument. The Doctor's immediate reaction is to check for surveillance -- "He opened the front door, stepped on to the landing, looked carefully up and down the stairs" (005-018) -- before returning to deliver the line that seals their alliance: "Why on earth didn't you come before?" (005-019).

Once trust is established, the Doctor becomes an essential anchor in Dukes' network. After every visit to Zorinsky, Dukes "called on the Doctor to tell him the latest news" (005-022). The Doctor provides practical assistance -- arranging a new passport through his friend Shura (005-073) -- but his greater contribution is as a sounding board and moral compass. It is the Doctor who warns Dukes about Zorinsky's honesty, who exclaims "But he hasn't got one!" when Dukes mentions payments for "Melnikoff's sister" (005-029), and who urges caution: "Look out, my friend, look out" (005-031).

The Doctor also serves as the conduit for one of the memoir's most important political analyses. Through his friend Shura, Dukes receives the penetrating diagnosis that Bolshevism's "ideal is a proletarian millennium, but the impulse is not love of the worker, but hatred of the bourgeois" (005-009). The Doctor's study becomes the memoir's intellectual centre -- a space where politics can be discussed honestly, away from the compulsory orthodoxy of the public square.

## Political Context

The Doctor is representative of a significant but largely voiceless segment of Russian society under Bolshevik rule: educated professionals who had welcomed the fall of the Tsar but were alienated by the Bolsheviks' authoritarian methods. Dukes notes that the Doctor "realizing one cardinal truth the Bolsheviks appear to fail to grasp" -- presumably, that honest disagreement is not treason (005-007). The Doctor's study, with its small gatherings, dry biscuits, tea, and frank conversation, represents a fragile pocket of intellectual freedom in an increasingly repressive state.

## In Dukes' Account

> The Doctor, who I learned from the woman at the lodge was Melnikoff's uncle, was a splendid fellow. As a matter of fact, he had sided wholeheartedly with the revolution in March, 1917, and held very radical views, but he thought more than spoke about them. His nephew, Melnikoff, on the contrary, together with a considerable group of officers, had opposed the revolution from the outset, but the Doctor had not quarrelled with them, realizing one cardinal truth the Bolsheviks appear to fail to grasp. (005-007)

This passage captures the Doctor's essential quality: he is a man capable of maintaining human relationships across political divides, a quality Dukes clearly values and contrasts with Bolshevik intolerance.

> The Doctor all at once half rose from his seat. The urbane, fixed smile that had not left his lips since the beginning of the interview suddenly burst into a half-laugh. "Was it you who gave him the whisky?" he broke in, in Russian. (005-014--005-015)

The whisky detail, so seemingly trivial, becomes the hinge on which the entire relationship turns. It is a reminder that in the clandestine world Dukes inhabited, personal credibility -- established through shared experience and small, verifiable facts -- mattered more than political credentials or official identification.
