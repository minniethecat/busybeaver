## Overview

Under Bolshevik rule, elections to the Petrograd Soviet continued — but they were no longer democratic. Paul Dukes, who attended the Fourth Petrograd Soviet and observed election meetings across the city, documented how the Communist Party maintained the appearance of popular representation while systematically suppressing the will of the voters. His account of the Soviet elections is one of the most detailed and damning passages in the memoir.

## Background

The Petrograd Soviet of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies had been formed during the March Revolution and was a genuinely popular institution. After the Bolshevik coup, the Soviet was retained as a legitimising facade. Dukes notes that the Bolsheviks "still maintain the farce of elections" because they want to claim the mantle of popular representation (012-055). But the reality was different: "elections had to be strictly 'controlled'" (004-012).

## Events

Dukes describes specific election meetings in detail. At one, he attended "a meeting outside the Tauride Palace" (012-063) where the Bolsheviks had organised a "great Communist counter-demonstration" against striking workers (012-015). The streets were militarised: "machine-guns barred the entrance" (012-017). The Bolsheviks rigged the process at every level: "the Bolsheviks allow an election to take its normal course, except at the point where the candidate is being chosen, when they step in and insist on a Communist being chosen" (012-056).

The results were devastating for Bolshevik claims of legitimacy. At the elections to the Petrograd Soviet, the Communists secured "100 out of 1,390 seats" (012-032) — roughly seven per cent. Workers openly mocked the party: "government offices mostly miss the elections if they can, and if they can't, send one clerk with a list and a dozen mandates" (012-057). Dukes attended the Fourth Petrograd Soviet opening where "a tall man in cloak" intoned: "I pronounce the Fourth Petrograd Soviet open" (012-075), and the carefully staged proceedings followed a Bolshevik-determined script.

## Aftermath

The election farce had long-term consequences. By demonstrating that even controlled elections could not produce a Bolshevik majority, the regime was forced to abandon even the pretence of democracy. The elections at the Putilov works were held "without notice" to prevent workers from attending (012-060). At another, "the Bolsheviks forgot the women" — failing to invite them to a meeting where they would have voted Communist (012-062). The system functioned, in Dukes' analysis, not to ascertain the popular will but to manufacture the appearance of it.

## Dukes' Perspective

Dukes was present at multiple elections and clearly considered the sheer extent of Bolshevik unpopularity to be a revelation worth documenting exhaustively. His account is carefully factual: he gives numbers (100 out of 1,390), describes specific meetings, and quotes workers directly. One worker, asked if he voted in a Soviet election, replied simply that his community had "a voice in elections" but "whether it's heard is another matter" (012-053). Dukes' conclusion is unambiguous: the Bolsheviks governed in the name of workers and peasants whose views they actively suppressed.
