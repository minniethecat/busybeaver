## Overview

Grigory Yevseyevich Zinoviev (born Hirsch Apfelbaum, 1883--1936) was one of the most visible Bolshevik leaders during the revolutionary period, serving as president of the Petrograd Soviet and later as president of the Communist International (Comintern). In Paul Dukes' memoir *Red Dusk and the Morrow*, Zinoviev receives the most extended personal portrait of any Bolshevik leader -- a consequence of Dukes having attended Zinoviev's public speeches and observed him at close quarters.

Dukes' characterisation of Zinoviev is withering. He describes him as "the consummate gutter-orator," a man whose "rhetoric is of a peculiar order" -- "unrivalled in his appeal to the ignorant mob" but utterly incapable of impressing "any thinking audience" (005-037). The portrait is not merely political critique; it is a work of sharp literary caricature, contrasting Zinoviev's earlier emaciated appearance as a "typical Jewish student" with his later corpulence after "a year's fattening on the Russian proletariat" (005-040). This physical description serves Dukes' broader argument about the Bolshevik leadership's corruption by power.

## Role in the Narrative

Zinoviev is the first Bolshevik leader Dukes describes in extended detail, encountered at the Liebknecht-Luxemburg memorial demonstration in January 1919. Dukes watches as Zinoviev arrives at the Palace Square "amid faint cheers and to the accompaniment of bugles," noting with his characteristic eye for detail that Zinoviev wore "his rich fur coat" as he addressed a shivering crowd of soldiers bribed with a pound of bread to attend (005-036, 005-038, 005-055).

The speech Dukes transcribes -- a denunciation of the German Social Democrat Scheidemann as a "social-traitor" -- is presented as typical Bolshevik oratory: florid, violent, and intellectually empty. But Dukes' real interest lies in the mechanics of the event. He notes that the small crowd was dwindling before the speakers arrived, that the soldiers had been induced to attend by the promise of bread, and that the burning of Scheidemann's effigy was the only moment that genuinely engaged the audience (005-049). This attention to the gap between Bolshevik theatricality and popular response is characteristic of Dukes' approach throughout the memoir.

Dukes also records how Zinoviev's name appeared on pamphlets sold alongside those of Lenin in the streets of Petrograd (002-028), and notes that when a prominent leader such as "Trotsky, Zinoviev, or Lunacharsky" spoke, the same "banal propagandist phraseology was untiringly reeled off" (003-004). The cumulative effect is of a leadership class whose rhetorical methods were as uniform as they were dishonest.

## Political Context

Zinoviev was a close associate of Lenin from 1903 and returned to Russia with him in the sealed train of April 1917. As president of the Petrograd Soviet, he was one of the most powerful men in revolutionary Russia. He opposed the October coup initially -- a fact Dukes does not record, as it would have been unknown to him -- but after the Bolshevik seizure of power he became one of its most ardent defenders. His presidency of the Third International from 1919 placed him at the centre of efforts to export revolution abroad, the very project that Dukes describes as "to reproduce the Communist experiment in all countries" (012-005). Zinoviev was executed in 1936 during Stalin's Great Purge, having been convicted in the first Moscow Show Trial.

## In Dukes' Account

> Zinoviev, whose real name is Apfelbaum, is a very important person in Bolshevist Russia. He is considered one of the greatest orators of the Communist Party, and now occupies the proud position of president of the Third International, the institution that is to effect the world revolution. (005-036)

Dukes' use of Zinoviev's birth name "Apfelbaum" serves a dual function: it is factual biography, but it also participates in the anti-Semitic undercurrent that surfaces periodically in the memoir -- as when he records the queue-saying "Where are all the Jews? Does Trotsky stand in a queue?" (002-104). Modern readers must weigh these passages carefully, recognising both Dukes' intelligence-gathering value and the prejudices of his milieu.

> I never listened to Zinoviev without recalling a meeting in the summer of 1917 when he was the chief speaker. He had just returned to Russia with a group of other Bolshevist leaders (very few of whom were present during the revolution) and was holding incendiary meetings in out-of-the-way places. He was thin and slim and looked the typical Jewish student of any Russian university. But after a year's fattening on the Russian proletariat he had swelled not only politically but physically. (005-040)

This passage exemplifies Dukes' method: the observation is personal and vivid, the political point (Bolshevik leaders enriched themselves while the people starved) is unmistakable, and the literary quality -- the sardonic parenthesis, the physical detail -- lifts it above mere propaganda.
