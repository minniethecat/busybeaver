## Overview

[[Stepanovna]] is the pseudonym [[Paul Dukes]] assigns to the housekeeper of his [[Petrograd]] contact [[Ivan Sergeievitch]] -- a woman who, with no political training or intelligence background, became one of his most valuable supporters during his undercover mission. She embodies what Dukes most admired in the [[Russian people]]: resilience, generosity, and a stubborn refusal to be cowed by the [[Bolshevik regime]]. "I shall call [her] Stepanovna," Dukes writes, introducing her in [[Chapter II]] with characteristic warmth (002-060).

In a memoir dominated by male revolutionaries, spies, and commissars, Stepanovna represents the domestic world that [[Bolshevism]] threatened to destroy. Her kitchen -- always warm, always welcoming, however meagre the fare -- serves as Dukes' sanctuary in a hostile city. Through her conversations and those of her household, Dukes provides some of his most valuable reportage on how ordinary [[Russians]] experienced and survived the [[Revolution]].

## Role in the Narrative

Dukes first encounters Stepanovna upon his return to Petrograd, when he calls at the flat of his friend Ivan Sergeievitch. She receives him nervously -- "her hands still trembled" (002-064) -- fearing, reasonably, that any stranger might be a Bolshevik informer. But her instinctive hospitality overcomes her fear: "May we offer you something to eat, Ivan Pavlovitch?" (002-064). Within hours, Dukes has become a guest in her kitchen, sharing tea with her and her nephew Dmitri.

The household Dukes finds at Stepanovna's is modest but functional. Her nephew Dmitri has "just become a volunteer so as to get Red army rations, so we are better off now" (002-071). The nursing maid Varia rounds out the household. But their material circumstances are precarious: Stepanovna "nearly got arrested this morning at the market when the Reds came and took people buying and selling food" (002-066). The fear of arrest, the struggle for food, the resentment of [[house committees]] -- all the texture of daily life under Bolshevik rule -- comes through in Dukes' record of conversations at Stepanovna's kitchen table.

Stepanovna's political views, as Dukes records them, are those of common sense rather than ideology. She and Varia "unburdened their hearts without restraint," abusing their house committee "composed of people of their own station" and frankly classing "Commissars" and "Communists" as exploiters (002-110). When Dukes reveals his true identity as an Englishman, the effect is "electric" -- she and her household share the widespread belief that the [[Allies]] would "invade Russia and relieve the stricken country" (002-111).

Stepanovna reappears throughout the memoir, always as a point of stability. After the Liebknecht-Luxemburg demonstration in Chapter V, Dukes "made my way to Stepanovna's in the hope of meeting Dmitri" (005-052). It is in her kitchen that Dukes explains the meaning of the demonstration, and where Stepanovna delivers one of the memoir's most memorable lines: upon hearing that the Germans might set up a Soviet government like Lenin's, she exclaims slowly, in "utter bewilderment, 'Is it really possible that the Germans -- are -- such -- fools?'" (005-068).

## Biography

Little is known of Stepanovna's life beyond what Dukes records. She was the housekeeper for Ivan Sergeievitch, a well-to-do Russian whose flat in Petrograd became a way-station for Dukes' clandestine activities. She cared for her nephew Dmitri and employed the nursing maid Varia. Her husband is never mentioned and may have been absent or deceased. Her world was the kitchen of the flat, the Petrograd markets where she risked arrest to buy food, and the house committee that governed the building's forty flats under Bolshevik administration (002-102). She had no formal political affiliation and her opinions, as Dukes records them, were shaped entirely by experience rather than doctrine.

## In Dukes' Account

> Stepanovna and Varia unburdened their hearts without restraint, and somehow it was strange to hear them abusing their house committee, or committee of the poor, as it was also called, composed of people of their own station. "Commissars" and "Communists" they frankly classed as robbers and idlers. (002-110)

This passage captures the essential Dukes thesis about the gap between Bolshevik ideology and Russian reality. The "committee of the poor" was supposed to empower the working class; in practice, it functioned as another layer of petty tyranny, resented by the very people it was meant to serve.

> "Is it really possible," she exclaimed, slowly, and apparently in utter bewilderment, "that the Germans -- are -- such -- fools?" (005-068)

Stepanovna's incredulity at the idea that Germans would willingly adopt Bolshevism is both comic and profound. The line, perfectly timed after Dukes' explanation of [[world revolution]], encapsulates what Dukes presents as ordinary Russian common sense: an instinctive understanding that no people would freely choose the misery Bolshevik rule had imposed.
