## Overview

The Russian intelligentsia occupies a special place in Paul Dukes' memoir. Unlike the Bolshevik leaders whom he analyses with professional detachment, or the peasants whose fate he considers strategically, Dukes writes about the intelligentsia with evident sympathy. As an educated man himself — a musician and linguist as well as an intelligence officer — he understood the intelligentsia's predicament: caught between a regime that distrusted their bourgeois origins and a revolutionary situation that demanded their expertise.

Dukes documents how the Bolshevik regime systematically persecuted the very people it needed to govern. Doctors, engineers, teachers, and administrators were denounced as "counterrevolutionary," denied rations, and subjected to harassment — yet many continued to work, motivated by an ethic of service that Dukes regards as characteristically Russian. His account preserves the dignity of individuals who, in the chaos of revolution, chose to stay at their posts rather than flee.

## In Bolshevik Russia

Dukes records the summer of 1918 as the nadir of the intelligentsia's suffering: "the persecution of the intelligentsia was at its height" (004-014). Yet even in these conditions, he observed something remarkable — a "large number of educated Russians who had heroically stuck to their posts and were struggling in the face of adversity to save at least something from the general wreck" (004-014). This observation captures the dual reality of the intelligentsia's position: victimised by the regime yet essential to civilisation's survival.

The Bolsheviks' relationship with the intelligentsia was fundamentally contradictory. Ideologically, educated professionals were class enemies — "bourgeois specialists" whose loyalties were suspect. Practically, the Soviet state could not function without them. Dukes, having observed both the Tsarist and Bolshevik bureaucracies, understood that the revolution had destroyed administrative capacity without creating anything to replace it. The intelligentsia became the human bridge between the old order and whatever might follow.

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes' most striking claim about the intelligentsia is almost mystical: "No people in the world are so capable of heroic and self-sacrificing labour for purely altruistic motives as a certain type of Russian" (004-014). This assertion, coming from a hard-headed intelligence officer, carries weight. He contrasts the intelligentsia's quiet heroism with the regime's noisy propaganda — while Bolshevik orators declaimed about proletarian liberation, educated Russians were keeping the hospitals open and the trains running.

The Cheka's methods of "provocation" — the Tsarist tactic of infiltrating opposition groups to entrap them — were directed with particular effectiveness against the intelligentsia (002-271). Educated people, more likely to have foreign contacts and political opinions, were natural targets. Dukes' own survival depended on his ability to navigate this landscape of surveillance and denunciation, moving between identities as the situation required.
