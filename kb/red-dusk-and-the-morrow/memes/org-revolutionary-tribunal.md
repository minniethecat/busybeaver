# Revolutionary Tribunal

## Overview

The Revolutionary Tribunals were the judicial organs established by the Bolshevik government to replace the Tsarist court system, operating from late 1917 throughout the Russian Civil War. Guided not by codified law but by the amorphous principle of "revolutionary conscience," these tribunals dispensed a form of summary justice that served political rather than legal ends. Dukes observed their operations from within the Red Army and provided a critical account of their methods and personnel.

## Structure and Role

The structure of the Revolutionary Tribunals was deliberately primitive. Dukes recorded that "the judges are guided by no rules, instructions, or laws, but solely by what is known as 'revolutionary conscience'" (011-050). The qualification for judicial office was not legal training but political reliability: "since none but ardent Communists are admitted to these posts," the fact that judges were "often illiterate" was considered irrelevant to their function (011-050). This arrangement reflected the Bolshevik conviction that class loyalty, not legal expertise, was the proper foundation of a proletarian judiciary. The tribunals' jurisdiction extended over counterrevolutionary crimes, speculation, sabotage, and other politically defined offences.

## Activities

The tribunals functioned as instruments of political terror rather than impartial courts. By late 1920, their practices had become so notorious that the Bolsheviks, after having "abolished all jurisprudence at the universities," found themselves compelled to recruit former Tsarist legal specialists from the Red Army to bring a semblance of order to the judicial process (011-051). Dukes observed that this mirrored the Bolsheviks' pragmatic re-engagement of Tsarist military and industrial experts, underscoring the regime's recognition that "administrative ability" could not be substituted with "political 'soundness'" (004-013). The tribunals dispensed not only imprisonment but execution, operating alongside the Cheka in the broader apparatus of Red Terror.

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' assessment of the Revolutionary Tribunals was unambiguous. His description of the judges — "often illiterate" Communists operating without any legal guidance, guided solely by "revolutionary conscience" — conveys his contempt for the system (011-050). He noted the irony that just as the regime recruited former Tsarist officers to command its army, it eventually had to "comb out from the ranks of the army all who had technical knowledge of Tsarist law" to staff courts that had been designed to abolish bourgeois jurisprudence (011-051). The tribunals represented for Dukes the broader Bolshevik tendency to destroy functional institutions and then belatedly reconstruct them with the same "bourgeois specialists" they had initially denounced.

## Historical Context

The Revolutionary Tribunals were established by a decree of the Council of People's Commissars in November 1917, just weeks after the Bolshevik seizure of power. They were part of a comprehensive dismantling of the Tsarist judicial system: all existing courts were abolished, the legal profession was suppressed, and the principle of judicial independence was rejected as a bourgeois fiction. The tribunals operated until the judicial reforms of 1922, when a more structured legal system was introduced under the New Economic Policy. Their legacy, however, persisted in the Soviet tradition of political courts serving Party interests.
