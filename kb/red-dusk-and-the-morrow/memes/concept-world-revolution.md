## Overview

World revolution was the ideological cornerstone of early Bolshevik foreign policy and the rationale for the Communist International (Comintern). In Paul Dukes' memoir, the concept appears not merely as abstract theory but as the driving force behind Bolshevik propaganda, diplomatic manoeuvring, and military strategy. Dukes, reporting from inside Soviet Russia, witnessed how the expectation of imminent world revolution shaped every aspect of Bolshevik governance — from the rhetoric of mass rallies to the organisation of the Red Army and the treatment of foreign visitors.

The Bolsheviks believed that the Russian Revolution was merely the first spark of a global conflagration. Dukes records this conviction with the scepticism of a British intelligence officer who understood that the revolution's westward spread was far from inevitable. His account captures the tension between Bolshevik millenarianism and the stubborn realities of post-war Europe, where revolution in Germany and Hungary flared and then failed.

## In Bolshevik Russia

The rhetoric of world revolution saturated Bolshevik public life. At a mass meeting in Petrograd commemorating the murdered German Communist leaders Liebknecht and Luxemburg, Zinoviev, president of the Petrograd Soviet and the Third International, asked rhetorically: "'Wherefore are we gathered here today? Is it to celebrate a triumph of world revolution, to hail another conquest over the vicious ogre of Capitalism? Alas, no!'" (005-039). The speech, as Dukes reports it, reveals the Bolshevik leadership's emotional investment in the international cause — and their vulnerability when revolution abroad failed.

Dukes documents how the Comintern, under Zinoviev's leadership, became the "institution that is to effect the world revolution" (005-036). Foreign communists and sympathisers were paraded at rallies and congresses, where "orators, with chattering teeth, sang in divers tongues the praises of the Red regime" and "predicted the immediate advent of the world revolution and the early establishment of Bolshevism in every country" (012-007). The gap between this triumphalist rhetoric and the hunger and disorder Dukes observed in Russia itself is one of his memoir's most persistent ironies.

The internationalist commitment also had a darker side. Dukes notes how even impartial foreign observers — "foreign labour delegates" — were "embarrassingly proclaimed to be forerunners of the world revolution," their presence co-opted for propaganda regardless of their actual views (003-014).

## Dukes' Analysis

Dukes identifies a fundamental contradiction in the Bolshevik commitment to world revolution. In his analysis, the regime devoted its energies to international agitation while neglecting the practical reconstruction of Russia. He observes wryly of the intelligentsia that they had "struggled in the face of adversity to save at least something from the general wreck" while the regime "struggled for the silly politics of Bolshevism and nothing whatever for the world revolution" (004-014).

The tension between revolutionary internationalism and Russian national interest is a recurring theme. Dukes portrays Trotsky, "the genius of the Red army," as one of the "triumvirate" who "guide the destinies of the Russian and the world revolution" (011-018). Trotsky's role in building the Red Army was inseparable from his belief that the army would carry revolution beyond Russia's borders. Yet Dukes suggests that this internationalist vision was already giving way to a more pragmatic Soviet statism by 1919.
