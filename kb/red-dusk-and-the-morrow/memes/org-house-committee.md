# House Committee

## Overview

The House Committees (also known as Committees of the Poor) were local Bolshevik organisations established in apartment buildings and residential blocks throughout Soviet cities during the Russian Civil War. They functioned as the lowest level of Bolshevik administrative control, combining the roles of neighbourhood watch, rationing authority, and political surveillance unit. For ordinary Petrograders trying to survive under Bolshevik rule, the house committee was often the most immediate and intrusive face of the new regime.

## Structure and Role

House committees were composed of residents of the building, typically drawn from the poorest or most politically reliable tenants. They wielded considerable power over daily life: identity documents required the committee's stamp to be valid, a regulation that Dukes encountered when a document he presented was, fortunately, not yet subject to this requirement (004-082). The committees monitored who lived in each apartment, and unregistered lodgers were a constant risk. Dukes was warned by his contact Ivan Sergeievitch: "be careful the house committee do not know, for they will ask who is visiting the house" (001-054). The committees also administered the confiscation and redistribution of "superfluous" furniture — property that "generally went to decorate the rooms of members of the committee or groups of Communists" (006-030).

## Activities

The committees functioned as neighbourhood-level instruments of Bolshevik surveillance and control. Dukes' hosts Stepanovna and Varia, working-class women themselves, "unburdened their hearts without restraint" when discussing "their house committee, or committee of the poor, as it was also called, composed of people of their own station," whom they classed alongside "Commissars" and "Communists" as oppressors (002-110). Every knock on the door carried the threat of a committee inspection: when Dukes was awakened by a loud ring at the bell, his first panicked thought was "Had the house committee heard of the unregistered lodger?" (002-116). The committees held regular meetings that residents were expected to attend, absorbing time and imposing obligations on an already exhausted population (003-074).

## In Dukes' Account

Dukes' experience of the house committee system was one of constant low-level anxiety. The committees represented the regime's intrusion into the most private spaces of daily life. His landlady Stepanovna, despite her working-class credentials and instinctive sympathy for the revolution's egalitarian promises, spoke of her house committee with the same resentment she reserved for Bolshevik commissars (002-110). The requirement that all residents' documents bear the committee's stamp created a paper-based surveillance infrastructure that made anonymity in the city nearly impossible (004-082). For a foreign intelligence officer living under a false identity, the house committee was among the most dangerous elements of the Bolshevik administrative apparatus — not because of its power, but because of its proximity.

## Historical Context

The house committees were part of the Bolshevik strategy of "war communism" — the mobilisation of the entire society for the dual purposes of civil war and class transformation. Modelled loosely on the French Revolution's committees of surveillance, they embodied the Bolshevik conviction that revolution required the penetration of state power into every corner of private life. After the Civil War, the committees' role diminished, but the principle they established — that the state could and should supervise the domestic sphere — endured throughout the Soviet period in the form of housing offices, neighbourhood committees, and resident informant networks.
