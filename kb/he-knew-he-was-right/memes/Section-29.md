## Section 29

[029-001]She was sobbing, and could not answer him. She was half repentant,

[029-002]partly proud of what she had done,--half repentant in that she had

[029-003]lost what had seemed to her to be so good, and full of remorse in

[029-004]that she had so unnecessarily told her secret.

[029-005]"Perhaps," said he, "I ought to assure you that what you have told me

[029-006]shall never be repeated by my lips."

[029-007]She thanked him for this by a motion of her head and hand, not by

[029-008]words;--and then he was gone. How he managed to bid adieu to Mrs.

[029-009]Stanbury and her sister, or whether he saw them as he left the house,

[029-010]she never knew. In her corner of the sofa, weeping in the dark,

[029-011]partly proud and partly repentant, she remained till her sister came

[029-012]to her. "Emily," she said, jumping up, "say nothing about it; not

[029-013]a word. It is of no use. The thing is done and over, and let it

[029-014]altogether be forgotten."

[029-015]"It is done and over, certainly," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[029-016]"Exactly;--and I suppose a girl may do what she likes with herself in

[029-017]that way. If I choose to decline to take anything that is pleasant,

[029-018]and nice, and comfortable, nobody has a right to scold me. And I

[029-019]won't be scolded."

[029-020]"But, my child, who is scolding you?"

[029-021]"You mean to scold me. But it is of no use. The man has gone, and

[029-022]there is an end of it. Nothing that you can say or I can think will

[029-023]bring him back again. I don't want anybody to tell me that it would

[029-024]be better to be Lady Peterborough, with everything that the world has

[029-025]to give, than to live here without a soul to speak to, and to have to

[029-026]go back to those horrible islands next year. You can't think that I

[029-027]am very comfortable."

[029-028]"But what did you say to him, Nora?"

[029-029]"What did I say to him? What could I say to him? Why didn't he ask me

[029-030]to be his wife without saying anything about love? He asked me if I

[029-031]loved him. Of course I don't love him. I would have said I did, but

[029-032]it stuck in my throat. I am willing enough, I believe, to sell myself

[029-033]to the devil, but I don't know how to do it. Never mind. It's done,

[029-034]and now I'll go to bed."

[029-035]She did go to bed, and Mrs. Trevelyan explained to the two ladies as

[029-036]much as was necessary of what had occurred. When Mrs. Stanbury came

[029-037]to understand that the [[social-class|gentleman]] who had been closeted with her

[029-038]would, probably, in a few months be a lord himself, that he was a

[029-039]very rich man, a member of Parliament, and one of those who are

[029-040]decidedly born with gold spoons in their mouths, and understood also

[029-041]that [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] had refused him, she was lost in amazement. Mr.

[029-042]Glascock was about forty years of age, and appeared to Nora Rowley,

[029-043]who was nearly twenty years his junior, to be almost an old man.

[029-044]But to Mrs. Stanbury, who was over sixty, Mr. Glascock seemed to be

[029-045]quite in the flower of his age. The bald place at the top of his head

[029-046]simply showed that he had passed his boyhood, and the grey hairs at

[029-047]the back of his whiskers were no more than outward signs of manly

[029-048]discretion. She could not understand why any girl should refuse such

[029-049]an offer, unless the man were himself bad in morals, or in temper.

[029-050]But Mrs. Trevelyan had told her while Nora and Mr. Glascock were

[029-051]closeted together, that he was believed by them all to be good and

[029-052]gentle. Nevertheless she felt a considerable increase of respect for

[029-053]a young lady who had refused the eldest son of a lord. Priscilla,

[029-054]when she heard what had occurred, expressed to her mother a moderated

[029-055]approval. According to her views a girl would much more often be

[029-056]right to refuse an offer of marriage than to accept it, let him who

[029-057]made the offer be who he might. And the fact of the man having been

[029-058]sent away with a refusal somewhat softened Priscilla's anger at his

[029-059]coming there at all.

[029-060]"I suppose he is a goose," said she to her mother, "and I hope there

[029-061]won't be any more of this kind running after them while they are with

[029-062]us."

[029-063]Nora, when she was alone, wept till her heart was almost broken. It

[029-064]was done, and the man was gone, and the thing was over. She had quite

[029-065]sufficient [[truth|knowledge]] of the world to realise perfectly the difference

[029-066]between such a position as that which had been offered to her, and

[029-067]the position which in all probability she would now be called upon to

[029-068]fill. She had had her chance, and Fortune had placed great things at

[029-069]her disposal. It must be said of her also that the great things which

[029-070]Fortune had offered to her were treasures very valuable in her eyes.

[029-071]Whether it be right and wise to covet or to despise [[money|wealth]] and rank,

[029-072]there was no doubt but that she coveted them. She had been instructed

[029-073]to believe in them, and she did believe in them. In some mysterious

[029-074]manner of which she herself knew nothing, taught by some preceptor

[029-075]the nobility of whose lessons she had not recognised though she had

[029-076]accepted them, she had learned other things also,--to revere truth

[029-077]and love, and to be ambitious as regarded herself of conferring the

[029-078]gift of her whole heart upon some one whom she could worship as a

[029-079]hero. She had spoken the simple truth when she had told her sister

[029-080]that she had been willing to sell herself to the devil, but that

[029-081]she had failed in her attempt to execute the contract. But now as

[029-082]she lay weeping on her bed, tearing herself with remorse, picturing

[029-083]to herself in the most vivid colours all that she had thrown away,

[029-084]telling herself of all that she might have done and all that she

[029-085]might have been, had she not allowed the insane folly of a moment

[029-086]to get the better of her, she received little or no comfort from

[029-087]the reflection that she had been true to her better instincts. She

[029-088]had told the man that she had refused him because she loved Hugh

[029-089]Stanbury;--at least, as far as she could remember what had passed,

[029-090]she had so told him. And how mean it was of her to allow herself to

[029-091]be actuated by an insane passion for a man who had never spoken to

[029-092]her of love, and how silly of her afterwards to confess it! Of what

[029-093]service could such a passion be to her life? Even were it returned,

[029-094]she could not marry such a one as [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. She knew enough of

[029-095]herself to be quite sure that were he to ask her to do so to-morrow,

[029-096]she would refuse him. Better go and be scorched, and bored to

[029-097]death, and buried at the Mandarins, than attempt to regulate a poor

[029-098]household which, as soon as she made one of its number, would be on

[029-099]the sure road to ruin!

[029-100]For a moment there came upon her, not a thought, hardly an

[029-101]idea,--something of a waking dream that she would write to Mr.

[029-102]Glascock and withdraw all that she had said. Were she to do so he

[029-103]would probably despise her, and tell her that he despised her;--but

[029-104]there might be a chance. It was possible that such a declaration

[029-105]would bring him back to her;--and did it not bring him back to her

[029-106]she would only be where she was, a poor lost, shipwrecked creature,

[029-107]who had flung herself upon the rocks and thrown away her only chance

[029-108]of a prosperous voyage across the ocean of life; her only chance, for

[029-109]she was not like other girls, who at any rate remain on the scene

[029-110]of action, and may refit their spars and still win their way. For

[029-111]there were to be no more seasons in [[london|London]], no more living in Curzon

[029-112]Street, no renewed power of entering the ball-rooms and crowded

[029-113]staircases in which high-born wealthy lovers can be conquered. A

[029-114]great prospect had been given to her, and she had flung it aside!

[029-115]That letter of retractation was, however, quite out of the question.

[029-116]The reader must not suppose that she had ever thought that she could

[029-117]write it. She thought of nothing but of coming misery and remorse. In

[029-118]her wretchedness she fancied that she had absolutely disclosed to the

[029-119]man who loved her the name of him whom she had been mad enough to say

[029-120]that she loved. But what did it matter? Let it be as it might, she

[029-121]was destroyed.

[029-122]The next morning she came down to breakfast pale as a ghost; and they

[029-123]who saw her knew at once that she had done that which had made her a

[029-124]wretched woman.

[029-125]CHAPTER XVIII.

[029-126]THE STANBURY CORRESPONDENCE.

[029-127]Half an hour after the proper time, when the others had finished

[029-128]their tea and bread and butter, Nora Rowley came down among them pale

[029-129]as a ghost. Her sister had gone to her while she was dressing, but

[029-130]she had declared that she would prefer to be alone. She would be down

[029-131]directly, she had said, and had completed her toilet without even the

[029-132]assistance of her maid. She drank her cup of tea and pretended to eat

[029-133]her toast; and then sat herself down, very wretchedly, to think of

[029-134]it all again. It had been all within her grasp,--all of which she

[029-135]had ever dreamed! And now it was gone! Each of her three companions

[029-136]strove from time to time to draw her into conversation, but she

[029-137]seemed to be resolute in her refusal. At first, till her utter

[029-138]prostration had become a fact plainly recognised by them all, she

[029-139]made some little attempt at an answer when a direct question was

[029-140]asked of her; but after a while she only shook her head, and was

[029-141]silent, giving way to absolute despair.

[029-142]Late in the evening she went out into the garden, and Priscilla

[029-143]followed her. It was now the end of July, and the summer was in its

[029-144]glory. The ladies, during the day, would remain in the drawing-room

[029-145]with the windows open and the blinds down, and would sit in the

[029-146]evening reading and working, or perhaps pretending to read and work,

[029-147]under the shade of a cedar which stood upon the lawn. No retirement

[029-148]could possibly be more secluded than was that of the garden of the

[029-149][[clock-house|Clock House]]. No stranger could see into it, or hear sounds from out

[029-150]of it. Though it was not extensive, it was so well furnished with

[029-151]those charming garden shrubs which, in congenial soils, become large

[029-152]trees, that one party of wanderers might seem to be lost from another

[029-153]amidst its walls. On this evening Mrs. Stanbury and Mrs. Trevelyan

[029-154]had gone out as usual, but Priscilla had remained with Nora Rowley.

[029-155]After a while Nora also got up and went through the window all alone.

[029-156]Priscilla, having waited for a few minutes, followed her; and caught

[029-157]her in a long green walk that led round the bottom of the orchard.

[029-158]"What makes you so wretched?" she said.

[029-159]"Why do you say I am wretched?"

[029-160]"Because it's so visible. How is one to go on living with you all day

[029-161]and not notice it?"

[029-162]"I wish you wouldn't notice it. I don't think it kind of you to

[029-163]notice it. If I wanted to talk of it, I would say so."

[029-164]"It is better generally to speak of a trouble than to keep it to

[029-165]oneself," said Priscilla.

[029-166]"All the same, I would prefer not to speak of mine," said Nora.

[029-167]Then they parted, one going one way and one the other, and Priscilla

[029-168]was certainly angry at the reception which had been given to the

[029-169]sympathy which she had proffered. The next day passed almost without

[029-170]a word spoken between the two. Mrs. Stanbury had not ventured as yet

[029-171]to mention to her guest the subject of the rejected lover, and had

[029-172]not even said much on the subject to Mrs. Trevelyan. Between the two

[029-173]sisters there had been, of course, some discussion on the matter. It

[029-174]was impossible that it should be allowed to pass without it; but such

[029-175]discussions always resulted in an assertion on the part of Nora that

[029-176]she would not be scolded. Mrs. Trevelyan was very tender with her,

[029-177]and made no attempt to scold her,--tried, at last, simply to console

[029-178]her; but Nora was so continually at work scolding herself, that every

[029-179]word spoken to her on the subject of Mr. Glascock's visit seemed to

[029-180]her to carry with it a rebuke.
