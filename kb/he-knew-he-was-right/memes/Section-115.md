## Section 115

[115-001]Before he went Mrs. Trevelyan thanked him most cordially for the

[115-002]trouble he had taken in procuring for her the address at Willesden,

[115-003]and gave him some account of the journey which she and her mother

[115-004]had made to River's Cottage. He argued with both of them that the

[115-005]unfortunate man must now be regarded as being altogether out of his

[115-006]mind, and something was said as to the great wisdom and experience of

[115-007]Dr. Trite Turbury. Then [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] took his leave; and even Lady

[115-008]Rowley bade him adieu with kind cordiality. "I don't wonder, mamma,

[115-009]that Nora should like him," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[115-010]"That is all very well, my dear, and no doubt he is pleasant, and

[115-011]manly, and all that;--but really it would be almost like marrying a

[115-012]beggar."

[115-013]"For myself," said Mrs. Trevelyan, "if I could begin life again, I do

[115-014]not think that any temptation would induce me to place myself in a

[115-015]man's power."

[115-016]Sir Marmaduke was told of all this on his return home, and he asked

[115-017]many questions as to the nature of Stanbury's work. When it was

[115-018]explained to him,--Lady Rowley repeating as nearly as she could all

[115-019]that Hugh had himself said about it, he expressed his opinion that

[115-020]writing for a penny newspaper was hardly more safe as a source of

[115-021][[money|income]] than betting on horse races. "I don't see that it is wrong,"

[115-022]said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[115-023]"I say nothing about wrong. I simply assert that it is uncertain. The

[115-024]very existence of such a periodical must in itself be most insecure."

[115-025]Sir Marmaduke, amidst the cares of his government at the Mandarins,

[115-026]had, perhaps, had no better opportunity of watching what was going on

[115-027]in the world of letters than had fallen to the lot of Miss Stanbury

[115-028]at [[exeter|Exeter]].

[115-029]"I think your papa is right," said Lady Rowley.

[115-030]"Of course I am right. It is out of the question; and so Nora must

[115-031]be told." He had as yet heard nothing about Mr. Glascock. Had that

[115-032]misfortune been communicated to him his cup would indeed have been

[115-033]filled with sorrow to overflowing.

[115-034]In the evening Nora was closeted with her father. "Nora, my dear,

[115-035]you must understand, once and for all, that this cannot be," said

[115-036]Sir Marmaduke. The Governor, when he was not disturbed by outward

[115-037]circumstances, could assume a good deal of personal [[pride|dignity]], and

[115-038]could speak, especially to his children, with an air of indisputable

[115-039]authority.

[115-040]"What can't be, papa?" said Nora.

[115-041]Sir Marmaduke perceived at once that there was no indication of

[115-042]obedience in his daughter's voice, and he prepared himself for

[115-043]battle. He conceived himself to be very strong, and thought that his

[115-044]objections were so well founded that no one would deny their truth

[115-045]and that his daughter had not a leg to stand on. "This, that your

[115-046]mamma tells me of about Mr. Stanbury. Do you know, my dear, that he

[115-047]has not a shilling in the world?"

[115-048]"I know that he has no fortune, papa,--if you mean that."

[115-049]"And no profession either;--nothing that can be called a profession.

[115-050]I do not wish to argue it, my dear, because there is no room for

[115-051]argument. The whole thing is preposterous. I cannot but think ill

[115-052]of him for having proposed it to you; for he must have known,--must

[115-053]have known, that a young man without an income cannot be accepted as

[115-054]a fitting suitor for a [[social-class|gentleman]]'s daughter. As for yourself, I can

[115-055]only hope that you will get the little idea out of your head very

[115-056]quickly;--but mamma will speak to you about that. What I want you to

[115-057]understand from me is this,--that there must be an end to it."

[115-058]Nora listened to this speech in perfect silence, [[reputation|standing]] before her

[115-059]father, and waiting patiently till the last word of it should be

[115-060]pronounced. Even when he had finished she still paused before she

[115-061]answered him. "Papa," she said at last, and hesitated again before

[115-062]she went on.

[115-063]"Well, my dear."

[115-064]"I can not give it up."

[115-065]"But you must give it up."

[115-066][Illustration: "But you must give it up," said Sir Marmaduke.]

[115-067]"No, papa. I would do anything I could for you and mamma, but that is

[115-068]impossible."

[115-069]"Why is it impossible?"

[115-070]"Because I love him so dearly."

[115-071]"That is nonsense. That is what all girls say when they choose to

[115-072]run against their parents. I tell you that it shall be given up. I

[115-073]will not have him here. I forbid you to see him. It is quite out of

[115-074]the question that you should marry such a man. I do hope, Nora, that

[115-075]you are not going to add to mamma's difficulties and mine by being

[115-076]obstinate and disobedient." He paused a moment, and then added, "I do

[115-077]not think that there is anything more to be said."

[115-078]"Papa."

[115-079]"My dear, I think you had better say nothing further about it. If you

[115-080]cannot bring yourself at the present moment to promise that there

[115-081]shall be an end of it, you had better hold your tongue. You have

[115-082]heard what I say, and you have heard what mamma says. I do not for

[115-083]a moment suppose that you dream of carrying on a communication with

[115-084]this gentleman in opposition to our wishes."

[115-085]"But I do."

[115-086]"Do what?"

[115-087]"Papa, you had better listen to me." Sir Marmaduke, when he heard

[115-088]this, assumed an air of increased authority, in which he intended

[115-089]that paternal anger should be visible; but he seated himself, and

[115-090]prepared to receive, at any rate, some of the arguments with which

[115-091]Nora intended to bolster up her bad cause. "I have promised Mr.

[115-092]Stanbury that I will be his wife."

[115-093]"That is all nonsense."

[115-094]"Do listen to me, papa. I have listened to you and you ought to

[115-095]listen to me. I have promised him, and I must keep my promise. I

[115-096]shall keep my promise if he wishes it. There is a time when a girl

[115-097]must be supposed to know what is best for herself,--just as there is

[115-098]for a man."

[115-099]"I never heard such stuff in all my life. Do you mean that you'll go

[115-100]out and marry him like a beggar, with nothing but what you stand up

[115-101]in, with no friend to be with you, an outcast, thrown off by your

[115-102]mother,--with your father's--curse?"

[115-103]"Oh, papa, do not say that. You would not curse me. You could not."

[115-104]"If you do it at all, that will be the way."

[115-105]"That will not be the way, papa. You could not treat me like that."

[115-106]"And how are you proposing to treat me?"

[115-107]"But, papa, in whatever way I do it, I must do it. I do not say

[115-108]to-day or to-morrow; but it must be the intention and purpose of

[115-109]my life, and I must declare that it is, everywhere. I have made

[115-110]up my mind about it. I am engaged to him, and I shall always say

[115-111]so,--unless he breaks it. I don't care a bit about fortune. I thought

[115-112]I did once, but I have changed all that."

[115-113]"Because this scoundrel has talked sedition to you."

[115-114]"He is not a scoundrel, papa, and he has not talked sedition. I don't

[115-115]know what sedition is. I thought it meant treason, and I'm sure he is

[115-116]not a traitor. He has made me love him, and I shall be true to him."

[115-117]Hereupon Sir Marmaduke began almost to weep. There came first a

[115-118]half-smothered oath and then a sob, and he walked about the room, and

[115-119]struck the table with his fist, and rubbed his bald head impatiently

[115-120]with his hand. "Nora," he said, "I thought you were so different from

[115-121]this! If I had believed this of you, you never should have come to

[115-122]England with Emily."

[115-123]"It is too late for that now, papa."

[115-124]"Your mamma always told me that you had such excellent ideas about

[115-125]marriage."

[115-126]"So I have,--I think," said she, smiling.

[115-127]"She always believed that you would make a match that would be a

[115-128]credit to the family."

[115-129]"I tried it, papa;--the sort of match that you mean. Indeed I was

[115-130]mercenary enough in what I believed to be my views of life. I meant

[115-131]to marry a rich man,--if I could, and did not think much whether I

[115-132]should love him or not. But when the rich man came--"

[115-133]"What rich man?"

[115-134]"I suppose mamma has told you about Mr. Glascock."

[115-135]"Who is Mr. Glascock? I have not heard a word about Mr. Glascock."

[115-136]Then Nora was forced to tell her story,--was called upon to tell it

[115-137]with all its aggravating details. By degrees Sir Marmaduke learned

[115-138]that this Mr. Glascock, who had desired to be his son-in-law, was in

[115-139]very truth the heir to the Peterborough title and estates,--would

[115-140]have been such a son-in-law as almost to compensate, by the

[115-141]brilliance of the connection, for that other unfortunate alliance. He

[115-142]could hardly control his agony when he was made to understand that

[115-143]this embryo peer had in truth been in earnest. "Do you mean that he

[115-144]went down after you into Devonshire?"

[115-145]"Yes, papa."

[115-146]"And you refused him then,--a second time?"

[115-147]"Yes, papa."

[115-148]"Why;--why;--why? You say yourself that you liked him;--that you

[115-149]thought that you would accept him."

[115-150]"When it came to speaking the word, papa, I found that I could not

[115-151]pretend to love him when I did not love him. I did not care for

[115-152]him,--and I liked somebody else so much better! I just told him the

[115-153]plain truth,--and so he went away."

[115-154]The thought of all that he had lost, of all that might so easily have

[115-155]been his, for a time overwhelmed Sir Marmaduke, and drove the very

[115-156]memory of Hugh Stanbury almost out of his head. He could understand

[115-157]that a girl should not marry a man whom she did not like; but he

[115-158]could not understand how any girl should not love such a suitor

[115-159]as was Mr. Glascock. And had she accepted this pearl of men, with

[115-160]her position, with her manners and beauty and appearance, such a

[115-161]connection would have been as good as an assured marriage for every

[115-162]one of Sir Marmaduke's numerous daughters. Nora was just the woman to

[115-163]look like a great lady, a lady of high rank,--such a lady as could

[115-164]almost command men to come and throw themselves at her unmarried

[115-165]sisters' feet. Sir Marmaduke had believed in his daughter Nora, had

[115-166]looked forward to see her do much for the family; and, when the crash

[115-167]had come upon the Trevelyan household, had thought almost as much

[115-168]of her injured prospects as he had of the misfortune of her sister.

[115-169]But now it seemed that more than all the good things of what he had

[115-170]dreamed had been proposed to this unruly girl, in spite of that great

[115-171]crash,--and had been rejected! And he saw more than this,--as he

[115-172]thought. These good things would have been accepted had it not been

[115-173]for this rascal of a penny-a-liner, this friend of that other rascal

[115-174]Trevelyan, who had come in the way of their family to destroy the

[115-175]happiness of them all! Sir Marmaduke, in speaking of Stanbury after

[115-176]this, would constantly call him a penny-a-liner, thinking that the

[115-177]contamination of the penny communicated itself to all transactions of

[115-178]the Daily Record.

[115-179]"You have made your bed for yourself, Nora, and you must lie upon

[115-180]it."

[115-181]"Just so, papa."

[115-182]"I mean that, as you have refused Mr. Glascock's offer, you can never

[115-183]again hope for such an opening in life."

[115-184]"Of course I cannot. I am not such a child as to suppose that there

[115-185]are many Mr. Glascocks to come and run after me. And if there were

[115-186]ever so many, papa, it would be no good. As you say, I have chosen

[115-187]for myself, and I must put up with it. When I see the carriages going

[115-188]about in the streets, and remember how often I shall have to go home

[115-189]in an omnibus, I do think about it a good deal."
