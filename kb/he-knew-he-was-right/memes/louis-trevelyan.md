# Louis Trevelyan

**Louis Trevelyan** is the central protagonist of [[Anthony Trollope]]'s 1869 novel *He Knew He Was Right*. A wealthy young gentleman of good family and education, Trevelyan begins the novel as an apparently ideal husband to [[Emily Trevelyan]], but his descent into obsessive [[Jealousy|jealousy]] over his wife's friendship with [[Colonel Osborne]] drives the novel's main plot. His pathological suspicion — which proves entirely unfounded — destroys his [[Marriage|marriage]], alienates him from his friends and family, drives him to abduct his infant son, and ultimately leads to his mental breakdown and death in Italy. Trevelyan stands as one of Victorian fiction's most psychologically acute portraits of paranoid obsession.

## Background

When the novel opens, Louis Trevelyan is twenty-four years old and has already achieved a position of considerable comfort and respectability:

> When Louis Trevelyan was twenty-four years old, he had all the world before him, and at twenty-four many a man still thinks that the world is his oyster. But Trevelyan had already done something; he had married a wife, he had entered upon the possession of a fortune, he had taken a house in one of the most fashionable streets in London, and he had become a member of the most aristocratic club in existence. [001-151]

He is a man of good repute — "a pearl indeed. Louis Trevelyan was a man of whom all people said good things" [001-211] — handsome, well-educated at Oxford, and possessed of independent means. His marriage to Emily Rowley, the daughter of the Governor of the Mandarin Islands, is celebrated in London society as an ideal match.

## Character and Personality

Trevelyan's character is defined by a fatal contradiction: he is outwardly reasonable and gentle, yet inwardly governed by an inflexible conviction of his own rightness. Trollope presents him as neither villain nor fool, but as a man whose very virtues — his sense of propriety, his belief in masculine authority, his desire for domestic order — become instruments of destruction when fused with pathological jealousy.

His tragedy lies in the title's bitter irony: Trevelyan is absolutely convinced that *he knows he is right* about his wife's supposed infidelity, and this conviction makes him impervious to all evidence, all counsel, and all compassion. The novel traces his progressive isolation as his obsession drives away everyone who tries to help him.

## Key Relationships

### Emily Trevelyan

Trevelyan's relationship with his wife [[Emily Trevelyan]] is the novel's central dramatic axis. What begins as a love match — she is described as "a very handsome young woman" who was "acknowledged to be a pearl" [002-085] — deteriorates with terrifying speed once Trevelyan's jealousy takes hold. He demands she end her friendship with Colonel Osborne; she refuses on principle, seeing the demand as an insult to her honour. This standoff — each convinced of their own rightness — becomes irreparable.

### Colonel Osborne

[[Colonel Osborne]] is the unwitting catalyst of Trevelyan's destruction. A bachelor in his fifties who had known Emily's father in the Colonial Service, Osborne's visits to the Trevelyan household are entirely innocent — but Trevelyan becomes "fixed in his resolution that Colonel Osborne was a man whom she was entitled to see, but not a man whom it was proper for her to receive in her husband's absence" [002-061]. Trevelyan's antagonism toward Osborne reveals more about his own psychology than about any real threat.

### Hugh Stanbury

[[Hugh Stanbury]], Trevelyan's old Oxford friend, serves as the novel's moral counterweight. Where Trevelyan is rigid and suspicious, Stanbury is flexible and trusting. Stanbury attempts to mediate between the estranged couple but ultimately becomes aligned with Emily's cause, taking her side in the public controversy that envelops the separation.

## Descent into Madness

Trevelyan's mental deterioration is charted with clinical precision across the novel's later chapters. After abducting his son and fleeing to Italy, he becomes increasingly paranoid and isolated. Living in a remote villa near Siena with only servants for company, he oscillates between lucidity and delusion. Trollope's depiction of Trevelyan's madness was considered unusually frank and psychologically sophisticated for Victorian fiction.

> Louis Trevelyan felt that he had disgraced himself, and that he could never again hold up his head. He had been masterful, and had failed; he had made demands, and had been refused; he had threatened, and had not carried out his threats. [011-001]

## Significance

Louis Trevelyan is one of Trollope's most ambitious character studies. Unlike the melodramatic villains of much Victorian fiction, Trevelyan is a sympathetic figure whose downfall comes not from wickedness but from psychological illness. The novel's refusal to provide easy answers — Trevelyan is wrong in his jealousy, yet the novel neither excuses Emily's stubbornness nor dismisses Trevelyan's suffering — makes it a remarkably modern treatment of marital breakdown and mental illness.

The character has been discussed by literary scholars as a case study in Victorian masculinity in crisis, pathological jealousy as a form of self-destruction, and the legal and social constraints of nineteenth-century marriage.
