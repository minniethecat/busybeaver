# Propriety

In Victorian society, propriety governed the boundaries of acceptable behaviour, particularly for women. A woman's reputation depended on the perception that she conducted herself with propriety at all times. In *He Knew He Was Right*, the question of whether Emily Trevelyan has violated the code of propriety — by receiving Colonel Osborne's visits — ignites the central conflict of the novel.

## Overview

Propriety in the Victorian sense was not simply about moral right and wrong. It encompassed a complex code of social performance: what could be done, what could be said, and — crucially — what could be *seen* to be done. The appearance of impropriety could be as damaging as impropriety itself. This distinction lies at the heart of the Trevelyan case: Emily insists she has done nothing wrong, while Louis insists that the mere appearance of improper intimacy is itself a violation (004-111).

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian social life was structured around a rigid distinction between proper and improper conduct. For married women, the rules were especially stringent: any private interaction with a man not her husband invited suspicion. The concept of "strictest propriety" functioned as both a personal standard and a mechanism of social control. A woman who failed to observe propriety risked not only her own reputation but her family's standing (016-004).

## Manifestation in the Novel

Trollope deploys the language of propriety at key moments in the narrative. When Emily's conduct is questioned, the issue is framed explicitly in terms of propriety: she is expected "to behave with propriety" (011-108). The novel does not simply endorse this standard — it examines the destructive potential of propriety enforced without room for trust or individual judgment.

The character of Miss Stanbury embodies a particular relationship to propriety: she is "so blind to all impropriety, or pretended to be" (045-088), suggesting that propriety is as much about performance and perception as about genuine moral standards.

## Key Examples

### Emily's Perceived Impropriety

The crisis originates in Colonel Osborne's visits to Emily at the Trevelyan home. Emily sees no impropriety in receiving an old family friend; Louis, and the society he represents, see the visits as a flagrant violation. The novel explores the gap between Emily's conduct and the perception of that conduct — a gap that proves unbridgeable (004-111).

### Miss Stanbury's Propriety

Miss Stanbury represents an older generation's approach to propriety: rigid, certain, and performative. She conducts herself with the "strictest propriety" in all things (016-004) and judges others by the same standard. Her character illustrates both the power and the limitations of propriety as a moral framework.
