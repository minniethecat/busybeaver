## Section 63

[063-001]"It is a sad affair altogether."

[063-002]"The saddest in the world. Lady Milborough has no doubt spoken to you

[063-003]about it."

[063-004]"Well;--yes; she has."

[063-005]"How could she help it? I am not such a fool as to suppose that

[063-006]people are to hold their tongues about me more than they do about

[063-007]others. Intimate as she is with you, of course she has spoken to

[063-008]you."

[063-009]"I was in hopes that something might have been done by this time."

[063-010]"Nothing has been done. Sometimes I think I shall put an end to

[063-011]myself, it makes me so wretched."

[063-012]"Then why don't you agree to forget and forgive and have done with

[063-013]it?"

[063-014]"That is so easily said;--so easily said." After this they walked on

[063-015]in silence for a considerable distance. Mr. Glascock was not anxious

[063-016]to talk about Trevelyan's wife, but he did wish to ask a question or

[063-017]two about Mrs. Trevelyan's sister, if only this could be done without

[063-018]telling too much of his own secret. "There's nothing I think so

[063-019]grand, as walking up a mountain," he said after a while.

[063-020]"It's all very well," said Trevelyan, in a tone which seemed to

[063-021]imply that to him in his present miserable condition all recreations,

[063-022]exercises, and occupations were mere leather and prunella.

[063-023]"I don't mean, you know, in the Alpine Club way," said Glascock. "I'm

[063-024]too old and too stiff for that. But when the path is good, and the

[063-025]air not too cold, and when it is neither snowing, nor thawing, nor

[063-026]raining, and when the sun isn't hot, and you've got plenty of time,

[063-027]and know that you can stop any moment you like and be pushed up by a

[063-028]carriage, I do think walking up a mountain is very fine,--if you've

[063-029]got proper shoes, and a good stick, and it isn't too soon after

[063-030]dinner. There's nothing like the air of Alps." And Mr. Glascock

[063-031]renewed his pace, and stretched himself against the hill at the rate

[063-032]of three miles an hour.

[063-033]"I used to be very fond of Switzerland," said Trevelyan, "but I don't

[063-034]care about it now. My eye has lost all its taste."

[063-035]"It isn't the eye," said Glascock.

[063-036]"Well; no. The truth is that when one is absolutely unhappy one

[063-037]cannot revel in the imagination. I don't believe in the miseries of

[063-038]poets."

[063-039]"I think myself," said Glascock, "that a poet should have a good

[063-040]digestion. By-the-bye, Mrs. Trevelyan and her sister went down to

[063-041][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], in Devonshire."

[063-042]"They did go there."

[063-043]"Have they moved since? A very pretty place is Nuncombe Putney."

[063-044]"You have been there then?"

[063-045]Mr. Glascock blushed again. He was certainly an awkward man, saying

[063-046]things that he ought not to say, and telling secrets which ought not

[063-047]to have been told. "Well;--yes. I have been there,--as it happens."

[063-048]"Just lately do you mean?"

[063-049]Mr. Glascock paused, hoping to find his way out of the scrape, but

[063-050]soon perceived that there was no way out. He could not lie, even

[063-051]in an affair of love, and was altogether destitute of those honest

[063-052]subterfuges,--subterfuges honest in such position,--of which a dozen

[063-053]would have been at once at the command of any woman, and with one

[063-054]of which, sufficient for the moment, most men would have been able

[063-055]to arm themselves. "Indeed, yes," he said, almost stammering as

[063-056]he spoke. "It was lately;--since your wife went there." Trevelyan,

[063-057]though he had been told of the possibility of Mr. Glascock's

[063-058]courtship, felt himself almost aggrieved by this man's intrusion

[063-059]on his wife's retreat. Had he not sent her there that she might

[063-060]be private; and what right had any one to invade such privacy? "I

[063-061]suppose I had better tell the truth at once," said Mr. Glascock. "I

[063-062]went to see Miss Rowley."

[063-063]"Oh, indeed."

[063-064]"My secret will be safe with you, I know."

[063-065]"I did not know that there was a secret," said Trevelyan. "I should

[063-066]have thought that they would have told me."

[063-067]"I don't see that. However, it doesn't matter much. I got nothing by

[063-068]my journey. Are the ladies still at Nuncombe Putney?"

[063-069]"No, they have moved from there to [[london|London]]."

[063-070]"Not back to Curzon Street?"

[063-071]"Oh dear, no. There is no house in Curzon Street for them now." This

[063-072]was said in a tone so sad that it almost made Mr. Glascock weep.

[063-073]"They are staying with an aunt of theirs,--out to the east of the

[063-074]city."

[063-075]"At St. Diddulph's?"

[063-076]"Yes;--with Mr. Outhouse, the clergyman there. You can't conceive

[063-077]what it is not to be able to see your own child; and yet, how can I

[063-078]take the boy from her?"

[063-079]"Of course not. He's only a baby."

[063-080]"And yet all this is brought on me solely by her obstinacy. God

[063-081]knows, however, I don't want to say a word against her. People choose

[063-082]to say that I am to blame, and they may say so for me. Nothing that

[063-083]any one may say can add anything to the weight that I have to bear."

[063-084]Then they walked to the top of the mountain in silence, and in due

[063-085]time were picked up by their proper shepherd and carried down to Susa

[063-086]at a pace that would give an English coachman a concussion of the

[063-087]brain.

[063-088]Why passengers for Turin, who reach Susa dusty, tired, and sleepy,

[063-089]should be detained at that place for an hour and a half instead of

[063-090]being forwarded to their beds in the great city, is never made very

[063-091]apparent. All travelling officials on the continent of Europe are

[063-092]very slow in their manipulation of luggage; but as they are equally

[063-093]correct we will find the excuse for their tardiness in the latter

[063-094]quality. The hour and a half, however, is a necessity, and it is very

[063-095]grievous. On this occasion the two Miss Spaldings ate their supper,

[063-096]and the two gentlemen waited on them. The ladies had learned to

[063-097]regard at any rate Mr. Glascock as their own [[money|property]], and received

[063-098]his services, graciously indeed, but quite as a matter of course.

[063-099]When he was sent from their peculiar corner of the big, dirty

[063-100]refreshment room to the supper-table to fetch an apple, and then

[063-101]desired to change it because the one which he had brought was

[063-102]spotted, he rather liked it. And when he sat down with his knees

[063-103]near to theirs, actually trying to eat a large Italian apple himself

[063-104]simply because they had eaten one, and discussed with them the

[063-105]passage over the Mont Cenis, he began to think that Susa was, after

[063-106]all, a place in which an hour and a half might be whiled away without

[063-107]much cause for complaint.

[063-108]"We only stay one night at Turin," said Caroline Spalding, the elder.

[063-109]"And we shall have to start at ten,--to get through to Florence

[063-110]to-morrow," said Olivia, the younger. "Isn't it cruel, wasting all

[063-111]this time when we might be in bed?"

[063-112]"It is not for me to complain of the cruelty," said Mr. Glascock.

[063-113]"We should have fared infinitely worse if we hadn't met you," said

[063-114]Caroline Spalding.

[063-115]"But our republican simplicity won't allow us to assert that even

[063-116]your society is better than going to bed, after a journey of thirty

[063-117]hours," said Olivia.

[063-118]In the meantime Trevelyan was roaming about the [[social-class|station]] moodily by

[063-119]himself, and the place is one not apt to restore cheerfulness to a

[063-120]moody man by any resources of its own. When the time for departure

[063-121]came Mr. Glascock sought him and found him; but Trevelyan had chosen

[063-122]a corner for himself in a carriage, and declared that he would rather

[063-123]avoid the ladies for the present. "Don't think me uncivil to leave

[063-124]you," he said, "but the truth is, I don't like American ladies."

[063-125]"I do rather," said Mr. Glascock.

[063-126]"You can say that I've got a headache," said Trevelyan. So Mr.

[063-127]Glascock returned to his friends, and did say that Mr. Trevelyan had

[063-128]a headache. It was the first time that a name had been mentioned

[063-129]between them.

[063-130]"Mr. Trevelyan! What a pretty name. It sounds like a novel," said

[063-131]Olivia.

[063-132]"A very clever man," said Mr. Glascock, "and much liked by his own

[063-133]circle. But he has had trouble, and is unhappy."

[063-134]"He looks unhappy," said Caroline.

[063-135]"The most miserable looking man I ever saw in my life," said Olivia.

[063-136]Then it was agreed between them as they went up to Trompetta's hotel,

[063-137]that they would go on together by the ten o'clock train to Florence.

[063-138]CHAPTER XXXVIII.

[063-139]VERDICT OF THE JURY--"MAD, MY LORD."

[063-140]Trevelyan was left alone at Turin when Mr. Glascock went on to

[063-141]Florence with his fair American friends. It was imperatively

[063-142]necessary that he should remain at Turin, though he had no business

[063-143]there of any kind whatever, and did not know a single person in the

[063-144]city. And of all towns in Italy Turin has perhaps less of attraction

[063-145]to offer to the solitary visitor than any other. It is new and

[063-146]parallelogrammatic as an American town, is very cold in cold weather,

[063-147]very hot in hot weather, and now that it has been robbed of its life

[063-148]as a capital, is as dull and uninteresting as though it were German

[063-149]or English. There is the Armoury, and the river Po, and a good hotel.

[063-150]But what are these things to a man who is forced to live alone in a

[063-151]place for four days, or perhaps a week? Trevelyan was bound to remain

[063-152]at Turin till he should hear from Bozzle. No one but Bozzle knew his

[063-153]address; and he could do nothing till Bozzle should have communicated

[063-154]to him tidings of what was being done at St. Diddulph's.

[063-155]There is perhaps no great social question so imperfectly understood

[063-156]among us at the present day as that which refers to the line which

[063-157]divides sanity from [[madness|insanity]]. That this man is sane and that other

[063-158]unfortunately mad we do know well enough; and we know also that one

[063-159]man may be subject to various hallucinations,--may fancy himself to

[063-160]be a teapot, or what not,--and yet be in such a condition of mind as

[063-161]to call for no intervention either on behalf of his friends, or of

[063-162]the law; while another may be in possession of intellectual faculties

[063-163]capable of lucid exertion for the highest purposes, and yet be so mad

[063-164]that bodily restraint upon him is indispensable. We know that the

[063-165]sane man is responsible for what he does, and that the insane man

[063-166]is irresponsible; but we do not know,--we only guess wildly, at the

[063-167]state of mind of those, who now and again act like madmen, though no

[063-168]court or council of experts has declared them to be mad. The bias of

[063-169]the public mind is to press heavily on such men till the law attempts

[063-170]to touch them, as though they were thoroughly responsible; and

[063-171]then, when the law interferes, to screen them as though they were

[063-172]altogether irresponsible. The same juryman who would find a man mad

[063-173]who has murdered a young woman, would in private life express a

[063-174]desire that the same young man should be hung, crucified, or skinned

[063-175]alive, if he had moodily and without reason broken his faith to the

[063-176]young woman in lieu of killing her. Now Trevelyan was, in truth, mad

[063-177]on the subject of his wife's alleged infidelity. He had abandoned

[063-178]everything that he valued in the world, and had made himself wretched

[063-179]in every affair of life, because he could not submit to acknowledge

[063-180]to himself the possibility of error on his own part. For that, in

[063-181]truth, was the condition of his mind. He had never hitherto believed

[063-182]that she had been false to her vow, and had sinned against him

[063-183]irredeemably; but he had thought that in her regard for another man

[063-184]she had slighted him; and, so thinking, he had subjected her to a

[063-185]severity of rebuke which no high-spirited woman could have borne. His

[063-186]wife had not tried to bear it,--in her indignation had not striven to

[063-187]cure the evil. Then had come his resolution that she should submit,

[063-188]or part from him; and, having so resolved, nothing could shake him.

[063-189]Though every friend he possessed was now against him,--including

[063-190]even Lady Milborough,--he was certain that he was right. Had not his

[063-191]wife sworn to obey him, and was not her whole conduct one tissue of

[063-192]disobedience? Would not the man who submitted to this find himself

[063-193]driven to submit to things worse? Let her own her fault, let her

[063-194]submit, and then she should come back to him.
