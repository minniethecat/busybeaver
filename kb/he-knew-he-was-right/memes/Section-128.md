## Section 128

[128-001]"It is done every day," said Sir Marmaduke.

[128-002]"No doubt it is. I admit, however, that I ought to be more careful

[128-003]than some other persons. There is a title and an estate to be

[128-004]perpetuated, and I cannot, perhaps, be justified in taking quite so

[128-005]much [[personal-freedom|liberty]] as some other men may do; but I think I have chosen a

[128-006]woman born to have a high position, and who will make her own way in

[128-007]any society in which she may be placed."

[128-008]"I have no doubt she will," said Sir Marmaduke, who had still

[128-009]sounding in his ears the alarming description which his wife had

[128-010]given him of this infatuated man's proposed bride. But he would have

[128-011]been bound to say as much had Mr. Glascock intended to marry as lowly

[128-012]as did King Cophetua.

[128-013]"She is highly educated, gentle-mannered, as sweetly soft as any

[128-014]English girl I ever met, and very pretty. You have met her, I think."

[128-015]"I do not remember that I have observed her."

[128-016]"She is too young for me, perhaps," said Mr. Glascock; "but that is a

[128-017]fault on the right side." Sir Marmaduke, as he wiped his beard after

[128-018]his breakfast, remembered what his wife had told him about the lady's

[128-019]age. But it was nothing to him. "She is four-and-twenty, I think,"

[128-020]said Mr. Glascock. If Mr. Glascock chose to believe that his intended

[128-021]wife was four-and-twenty instead of something over forty, that was

[128-022]nothing to Sir Marmaduke.

[128-023]"The very best age in the world," said he.

[128-024]They had sent for an officer of the police, and before they had been

[128-025]three hours in Siena they had been told that Trevelyan lived about

[128-026]seven miles from the town, in a small and very remote country house,

[128-027]which he had hired for twelve months from one of the city hospitals.

[128-028]He had hired it furnished, and had purchased a horse and small

[128-029]carriage from a man in the town. To this man they went, and it soon

[128-030]became evident to them that he of whom they were in search was living

[128-031]at this house, which was called Casalunga, and was not, as the police

[128-032]officer told them, on the way to any place. They must leave Siena by

[128-033]the road for Rome, take a turn to the left about a mile beyond the

[128-034]city gate, and continue on along the country lane till they saw a

[128-035]certain round hill to the right. On the top of that round hill was

[128-036]Casalunga. As the country about Siena all lies in round hills, this

[128-037]was no adequate description;--but it was suggested that the country

[128-038]people would know all about it. They got a small open carriage in

[128-039]the market-place, and were driven out. Their driver knew nothing of

[128-040]Casalunga, and simply went whither he was told. But by the aid of the

[128-041]country people they got along over the unmade lanes, and in little

[128-042]more than an hour were told, at the bottom of the hill, that they

[128-043]must now walk up to Casalunga. Though the hill was round-topped, and

[128-044]no more than a hill, still the ascent at last was very steep, and

[128-045]was paved with stones set edgeway in a manner that could hardly have

[128-046]been intended to accommodate wheels. When Mr. Glascock asserted that

[128-047]the signor who lived there had a carriage of his own, the driver

[128-048]suggested that he must keep it at the bottom of the hill. It was

[128-049]clearly not his intention to attempt to drive up the ascent, and Sir

[128-050]Marmaduke and Mr. Glascock were therefore obliged to walk. It was

[128-051]now in the latter half of May, and there was a blazing Italian sky

[128-052]over their heads. Mr. Glascock was acclimated to Italian skies, and

[128-053]did not much mind the work; but Sir Marmaduke, who never did much

[128-054]in walking, declared that Italy was infinitely hotter than the

[128-055]Mandarins, and could hardly make his way as far as the house door.

[128-056]It seemed to both of them to be a most singular abode for such a

[128-057]man as Trevelyan. At the top of the hill there was a huge entrance

[128-058]through a wooden gateway, which seemed to have been constructed with

[128-059]the intention of defying any intruders not provided with warlike

[128-060]ammunition. The gates were, indeed, open at the period of their

[128-061]visit, but it must be supposed that they were intended to be closed

[128-062]at any rate at night. Immediately on the right, as they entered

[128-063]through the gates, there was a large barn, in which two men were

[128-064]coopering wine vats. From thence a path led slanting to the house,

[128-065]of which the door was shut, and all the front windows blocked with

[128-066]shutters. The house was very long, and only of one story for a

[128-067]portion of its length. Over that end at which the door was placed

[128-068]there were upper rooms, and there must have been space enough for a

[128-069]large family with many domestics. There was nothing round or near

[128-070]the residence which could be called a garden, so that its look of

[128-071]desolation was extreme. There were various large barns and outhouses,

[128-072]as though it had been intended by the builder that corn and hay and

[128-073]cattle should be kept there; but it seemed now that there was nothing

[128-074]there except the empty vats at which the two men were coopering. Had

[128-075]the Englishmen gone farther into the granary, they would have seen

[128-076]that there were wine-presses stored away in the dark corners.

[128-077]They stopped and looked at the men, and the men halted for a moment

[128-078]from their work and looked at them; but the men spoke never a word.

[128-079]Mr. Glascock then asked after Mr. Trevelyan, and one of the coopers

[128-080]pointed to the house. Then they crossed over to the door, and Mr.

[128-081]Glascock finding there neither knocker nor bell, first tapped with

[128-082]his knuckles, and then struck with his stick. But no one came. There

[128-083]was not a sound in the house, and no shutter was removed. "I don't

[128-084]believe that there is a soul here," said Sir Marmaduke.

[128-085]"We'll not give it up till we've seen it all at any rate," said Mr.

[128-086]Glascock. And so they went round to the other front.

[128-087]On this side of the house the tilled ground, either ploughed or dug

[128-088]with the spade, came up to the very windows. There was hardly even

[128-089]a particle of grass to be seen. A short way down the hill there

[128-090]were rows of olive trees, [[reputation|standing]] in prim order and at regular

[128-091]distances, from which hung the vines that made the coopering of

[128-092]the vats necessary. Olives and vines have pretty names, and call

[128-093]up associations of landscape beauty. But here they were in no way

[128-094]beautiful. The ground beneath them was turned up, and brown, and

[128-095]arid, so that there was not a blade of grass to be seen. On some

[128-096]furrows the maize or Indian corn was sprouting, and there were

[128-097]patches of growth of other kinds,--each patch closely marked by its

[128-098]own straight lines; and there were narrow paths, so constructed as to

[128-099]take as little room as possible. But all that had been done had been

[128-100]done for economy, and nothing for beauty. The occupiers of Casalunga

[128-101]had thought more of the produce of their land than of picturesque or

[128-102]attractive appearance.

[128-103]The sun was blazing fiercely hot, hotter on this side, Sir Marmaduke

[128-104]thought, even than on the other; and there was not a wavelet of a

[128-105]cloud in the sky. A balcony ran the whole length of the house, and

[128-106]under this Sir Marmaduke took shelter at once, leaning with his back

[128-107]against the wall. "There is not a soul here at all," said he.

[128-108]"The men in the barn told us that there was," said Mr. Glascock;

[128-109]"and, at any rate, we will try the windows." So saying, he walked

[128-110]along the front of the house, Sir Marmaduke following him slowly,

[128-111]till they came to a door, the upper half of which was glazed, and

[128-112]through which they looked into one of the rooms. Two or three of the

[128-113]other windows in this frontage of the house came down to the ground,

[128-114]and were made for egress and ingress; but they had all been closed

[128-115]with shutters, as though the house was deserted. But they now looked

[128-116]into a room which contained some signs of habitation. There was a

[128-117]small table with a marble top, on which lay two or three books, and

[128-118]there were two arm-chairs in the room, with gilded arms and legs,

[128-119]and a morsel of carpet, and a clock on a shelf over a stove, and--a

[128-120]rocking-horse. "The boy is here, you may be sure," said Mr. Glascock.

[128-121]"The rocking-horse makes that certain. But how are we to get at any

[128-122]one!"

[128-123]"I never saw such a place for an Englishman to come and live in

[128-124]before," said Sir Marmaduke. "What on earth can he do here all day!"

[128-125]As he spoke the door of the room was opened, and there was Trevelyan

[128-126][[reputation|standing]] before them, looking at them through the window. He wore an

[128-127]old red English dressing-gown, which came down to his feet, and a

[128-128]small braided Italian cap on his head. His beard had been allowed

[128-129]to grow, and he had neither collar nor cravat. His trousers were

[128-130]unbraced, and he shuffled in with a pair of slippers, which would

[128-131]hardly cling to his feet. He was paler and still thinner than when he

[128-132]had been visited at Willesden, and his eyes seemed to be larger, and

[128-133]shone almost with a brighter brilliancy.

[128-134]Mr. Glascock tried to open the door, but found that it was closed.

[128-135]"Sir Marmaduke and I have come to visit you," said Mr. Glascock,

[128-136]aloud. "Is there any means by which we can get into the house?"

[128-137]Trevelyan stood still and stared at them. "We knocked at the front

[128-138]door, but nobody came," continued Mr. Glascock. "I suppose this is

[128-139]the way you usually go in and out."

[128-140]"He does not mean to let us in," whispered Sir Marmaduke.

[128-141]"Can you open this door," said Mr. Glascock, "or shall we go round

[128-142]again?" Trevelyan had stood still contemplating them, but at last

[128-143]came forward and put back the bolt. "That is all right," said

[128-144]Mr. Glascock, entering. "I am sure you will be glad to see Sir

[128-145]Marmaduke."

[128-146]"I should be glad to see him,--or you, if I could entertain you,"

[128-147]said Trevelyan. His voice was harsh and hard, and his words were

[128-148]uttered with a certain amount of intended grandeur. "Any of the

[128-149]family would be welcome were it not--"

[128-150]"Were it not what?" asked Mr. Glascock.

[128-151]"It can be nothing to you, sir, what troubles I have here. This is my

[128-152]own abode, in which I had flattered myself that I could be free from

[128-153]intruders. I do not want visitors. I am sorry that you should have

[128-154]had trouble in coming here, but I do not want visitors. I am very

[128-155]sorry that I have nothing that I can offer you, Mr. Glascock."

[128-156]"Emily is in Florence," said Sir Marmaduke.

[128-157]"Who brought her? Did I tell her to come? Let her go back to her

[128-158]home. I have come here to be free from her, and I mean to be free. If

[128-159]she wants my money, let her take it."

[128-160]"She wants her child," said Mr. Glascock.

[128-161]"He is my child," said Trevelyan, "and my right to him is better than

[128-162]hers. Let her try it in a court of law, and she shall see. Why did

[128-163]she deceive me with that man? Why has she driven me to this? Look

[128-164]here, Mr. Glascock;--my whole life is spent in this seclusion, and it

[128-165]is her fault."

[128-166]"Your wife is innocent of all fault, Trevelyan," said Mr. Glascock.

[128-167]"Any woman can say as much as that;--and all women do say it.

[128-168]Yet,--what are they worth?"
