# Money

The economic dimension of *He Knew He Was Right*, operating as both a practical constraint on characters' choices and a thematic counterpoint to the novel's exploration of love, honour, and jealousy. Money — who has it, who needs it, and how it shapes marriage — runs beneath the psychological drama like a bass line.

## Overview

Trollope is among the most economically literate of Victorian novelists, and *He Knew He Was Right* is no exception. The narrator notes early that a man "cannot save money in the islands" on a colonial governor's salary (001-163), establishing the economic constraints that shape characters' choices from the first chapter. Nearly every major plot development has a financial dimension: Hugh Stanbury's choice of journalism over law is a decision about income as much as principle; Miss Stanbury's control over her fortune, which she might "leave a shilling of her money to Hugh" or withhold (007-119), gives her power over her nephew and niece; the question of whether Nora Rowley can afford to marry a journalist structures her entire courtship plot. Money is never the primary subject of a Trollope novel — the emotional and moral drama always takes center stage — but it is the constant substrate on which that drama plays out.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian England was a society in which inherited wealth and earned income carried sharply different social meanings. A gentleman was expected to live on inherited property or professional income from a limited set of "honourable" professions — law, medicine, the church, the military. Journalism, Hugh Stanbury's chosen field, occupied an ambiguous position: it provided income but not gentility. The novel registers this tension precisely. Furthermore, the Victorian marriage market made a woman's financial position inseparable from her marital prospects: without a sufficient dowry or inheritance, a woman's choices were severely constrained.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Miss Stanbury's Fortune

Miss Jemima Stanbury is the novel's most significant economic actor. Her wealth, derived from money her late brother "had left the money which he had made to an only" surviving relative (001-185), gives her power over her relatives: she can offer or withhold financial support from Hugh, Dorothy, and others. The narrator observes her firm belief that "men should never look for money" in marriage (001-196), yet her own economic power contradicts this ideal. Her economic independence as a single woman of means is a significant counterpoint to the dependence of married women like Emily Trevelyan.

### The Marriage Market

The novel's multiple courtship plots are all shaped by financial considerations. Nora Rowley's engagement to Hugh Stanbury is shadowed by the question of whether a journalist's income can support a family. Dorothy Stanbury's position as a dependent relative makes her acutely vulnerable. Even Mr. Gibson's comic proposals are structured by financial calculation — he seeks a wife with means as much as charm.

### Professional Independence

Hugh Stanbury's choice of journalism over more conventional professions represents a claim to economic independence on his own terms. His ability to earn a living by writing gives him a freedom that the aristocratic characters, bound by inherited expectations, do not possess.

## Key Examples

### The Allowance Question

Trevelyan's control over the household finances is an unspoken dimension of his power over Emily. The question of what allowance, if any, she would receive if separated from him hangs over the novel's central conflict, making concrete the economic dependency that Victorian marriage imposed on women.

### Miss Stanbury's Will

The question of who will inherit Miss Stanbury's fortune drives significant plot tension. Her power to dispose of her property as she chooses makes her a figure of considerable narrative agency — an economic power that contrasts with her social conservatism.
