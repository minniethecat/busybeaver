## Section 18

[018-001]And then there was that terrible question about the child. Mrs.

[018-002]Trevelyan had said a dozen times to her sister that her husband could

[018-003]not take the boy away from her. Nora, however, had never assented to

[018-004]this, partly from a conviction of her own ignorance, not knowing what

[018-005]might be the power of a husband in such a matter, and partly thinking

[018-006]that any argument would be good and fair by which she could induce

[018-007]her sister to avoid a catastrophe so terrible as that which was now

[018-008]threatened.

[018-009]"I suppose he could take him, if he chose," she said at last.

[018-010]"I don't believe he is wicked like that," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "He

[018-011]would not wish to kill me."

[018-012]"But he will say that he loves baby as well as you do."

[018-013]"He will never take my child from me. He could never be so bad as

[018-014]that."

[018-015]"And you will never be so bad as to leave him," said Nora after a

[018-016]pause. "I will not believe that it can come to that. You know that he

[018-017]is good at heart,--that nobody on earth loves you as he does."

[018-018]So they went on for two days, and on the evening of the second day

[018-019]there came a letter from Trevelyan to his wife. They had neither of

[018-020]them seen him, although he had been in and out of the house. And

[018-021]on the afternoon of the Sunday a new grievance, a very terrible

[018-022]grievance, was added to those which Mrs. Trevelyan was made to bear.

[018-023]Her husband had told one of the servants in the house that Colonel

[018-024]Osborne was not to be admitted. And the servant to whom he had given

[018-025]this order was the--cook. There is no reason why a cook should be

[018-026]less trustworthy in such a matter than any other servant; and in

[018-027]Mr. Trevelyan's household there was a reason why she should be more

[018-028]so,--as she, and she alone, was what we generally call an old family

[018-029]domestic. She had lived with her master's mother, and had known her

[018-030]master when he was a boy. Looking about him, therefore, for some one

[018-031]in his house to whom he could speak,--feeling that he was bound to

[018-032]convey the order through some medium,--he called to him the ancient

[018-033]cook, and imparted to her so much of his trouble as was necessary

[018-034]to make the order intelligible. This he did with various ill-worded

[018-035]assurances to Mrs. Prodgers that there really was nothing amiss. But

[018-036]when Mrs. Trevelyan heard what had been done,--which she did from

[018-037]Mrs. Prodgers herself, Mrs. Prodgers having been desired by her

[018-038]master to make the communication,--she declared to her sister that

[018-039]everything was now over. She could never again live with a husband

[018-040]who had disgraced his wife by desiring her own cook to keep a guard

[018-041]upon her. Had the footman been instructed not to admit Colonel

[018-042]Osborne, there would have been in such instruction some apparent

[018-043]adherence to the recognised usages of society. If you do not desire

[018-044]either your friend or your enemy to be received into your house, you

[018-045]communicate your desire to the person who has charge of the door. But

[018-046]the cook!

[018-047]"And now, Nora, if it were you, do you mean to say that you would

[018-048]remain with him?" asked Mrs. Trevelyan.

[018-049]Nora simply replied that anything under any circumstances would be

[018-050]better than a separation.

[018-051]On the morning of the third day there came the following letter:--

[018-052]Wednesday, June 1, 12 midnight.

[018-053]DEAREST EMILY,

[018-054]You will readily believe me when I say that I never in my

[018-055]life was so wretched as I have been during the last two

[018-056]days. That you and I should be in the same house together

[018-057]and not able to speak to each other is in itself a misery,

[018-058]but this is terribly enhanced by the dread lest this state

[018-059]of things should be made to continue.

[018-060]I want you to understand that I do not in the least

[018-061]suspect you of having as yet done anything wrong,--or

[018-062]having even said anything injurious either to my position

[018-063]as your husband, or to your position as my wife. But I

[018-064]cannot but perceive that you are allowing yourself to be

[018-065]entrapped into an intimacy with [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] which if

[018-066]it be not checked, will be destructive to my happiness and

[018-067]your own. After what had passed before, you cannot have

[018-068]thought it right to receive letters from him which I was

[018-069]not to see, or to write letters to him of which I was

[018-070]not to know the contents. It must be manifest to you that

[018-071]such conduct on your part is wrong as judged by any of

[018-072]the rules by which a wife's conduct can be measured.

[018-073]And yet you have refused even to say that this shall be

[018-074]discontinued! I need hardly explain to you that if you

[018-075]persist in this refusal you and I cannot continue to live

[018-076]together as man and wife. All my hopes and prospects in

[018-077]life will be blighted by such a separation. I have not as

[018-078]yet been able to think what I should do in such wretched

[018-079]circumstances. And for you, as also for Nora, such a

[018-080]catastrophe would be most lamentable. Do, therefore, think

[018-081]of it well, and write me such a letter as may bring me

[018-082]back to your side.

[018-083]There is only one friend in the world to whom I could

[018-084]endure to talk of this great grief, and I have been to

[018-085]her and told her everything. You will know that I mean

[018-086]Lady Milborough. After much difficult conversation I

[018-087]have persuaded her to see you, and she will call in

[018-088]Curzon Street to-morrow about twelve. There can be no

[018-089]kinder-hearted, or more gentle woman in the world than

[018-090]Lady Milborough; nor did any one ever have a warmer friend

[018-091]than both you and I have in her. Let me implore you then

[018-092]to listen to her, and be guided by her advice.

[018-093]Pray believe, dearest Emily, that I am now, as ever, your

[018-094]most affectionate husband, and that I have no wish so

[018-095]strong as that we should not be compelled to part.

[018-096]LOUIS TREVELYAN.

[018-097]This epistle was, in many respects, a very injudicious composition.

[018-098]Trevelyan should have trusted either to the eloquence of his own

[018-099]written words, or to that of the ambassador whom he was about to

[018-100]despatch; but by sending both he weakened both. And then there were

[018-101]certain words in the letter which were odious to Mrs. Trevelyan, and

[018-102]must have been odious to any young wife. He had said that he did not

[018-103]"as yet" suspect her of having done anything wrong. And then, when

[018-104]he endeavoured to explain to her that a separation would be very

[018-105]injurious to herself, he had coupled her sister with her, thus

[018-106]seeming to imply that the injury to be avoided was of a material

[018-107]kind. She had better do what he told her, as, otherwise, she and her

[018-108]sister would not have a roof over their head! That was the nature of

[018-109]the threat which his words were supposed to convey.

[018-110]The matter had become so serious, that Mrs. Trevelyan, haughty and

[018-111]stiff-necked as she was, did not dare to abstain from showing the

[018-112]letter to her sister. She had no other counsellor, at any rate, till

[018-113]Lady Milborough came, and the weight of the battle was too great for

[018-114]her own unaided spirit. The letter had been written late at night, as

[018-115]was shown by the precision of the date, and had been brought to her

[018-116]early in the morning. At first she had determined to say nothing

[018-117]about it to Nora, but she was not strong enough to maintain such a

[018-118]purpose. She felt that she needed the poor consolation of discussing

[018-119]her wretchedness. She first declared that she would not see Lady

[018-120]Milborough. "I hate her, and she knows that I hate her, and she ought

[018-121]not to have thought of coming," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[018-122]But she was at last beaten out of this purpose by Nora's argument,

[018-123]that all the world would be against her if she refused to see her

[018-124]husband's old friend. And then, though the letter was an odious

[018-125]letter, as she declared a dozen times, she took some little

[018-126]comfort in the fact that not a word was said in it about the baby.

[018-127]She thought that if she could take her child with her into any

[018-128]separation, she could endure it, and her husband would ultimately be

[018-129]conquered.

[018-130]"Yes; I'll see her," she said, as they finished the discussion. "As

[018-131]he chooses to send her, I suppose I had better see her. But I don't

[018-132]think he does much to mend matters when he sends the woman whom he

[018-133]knows I dislike more than any other in all [[london|London]]."

[018-134]Exactly at twelve o'clock Lady Milborough's carriage was at the door.

[018-135]Trevelyan was in the house at the time and heard the knock at the

[018-136]door. During those two or three days of absolute wretchedness,

[018-137]he spent most of his hours under the same roof with his wife and

[018-138]sister-in-law, though he spoke to neither of them. He had had his

[018-139]doubts as to the reception of Lady Milborough, and was, to tell

[018-140]the truth, listening with most anxious ear, when her ladyship was

[018-141]announced. His wife, however, was not so bitterly contumacious as

[018-142]to refuse admittance to his friend, and he heard the rustle of the

[018-143]ponderous silk as the old woman was shown up-stairs. When Lady

[018-144]Milborough reached the drawing-room, Mrs. Trevelyan was alone.

[018-145]"I had better see her by myself," she had said to her sister.

[018-146]Nora had then left her, with one word of prayer that she would be as

[018-147]little defiant as possible.

[018-148]"That must depend," Emily had said, with a little shake of her head.

[018-149]There had been a suggestion that the child should be with her, but

[018-150]the mother herself had rejected this.

[018-151]"It would be stagey," she had said, "and clap-trap. There is nothing

[018-152]I hate so much as that."

[018-153]She was sitting, therefore, quite alone, and as stiff as a man in

[018-154]armour, when Lady Milborough was shown up to her.

[018-155]And Lady Milborough herself was not at all comfortable as she

[018-156]commenced the interview. She had prepared many wise words to be

[018-157]spoken, but was not so little ignorant of the [[honour|character]] of the woman

[018-158]with whom she had to deal, as to suppose that the wise words would

[018-159]get themselves spoken without interruption. She had known from the

[018-160]first that Mrs. Trevelyan would have much to say for herself, and the

[018-161]feeling that it would be so became stronger than ever as she entered

[018-162]the room. The ordinary feelings between the two ladies were cold and

[018-163]constrained, and then there was silence for a few moments when the

[018-164]Countess had taken her seat. Mrs. Trevelyan had quite determined that

[018-165]the enemy should fire the first shot.

[018-166]"This is a very sad state of things," said the Countess.

[018-167]"Yes, indeed, Lady Milborough."

[018-168]"The saddest in the world;--and so unnecessary;--is it not?"

[018-169]"Very unnecessary, indeed, as I think."

[018-170]"Yes, my dear, yes. But, of course, we must remember--"

[018-171]Then Lady Milborough could not clearly bring to her mind what it was

[018-172]that she had to remember.

[018-173]"The fact is, my dear, that all this kind of thing is too monstrous

[018-174]to be thought of. Goodness, gracious, me; two young people like you

[018-175]and Louis, who thoroughly love each other, and who have got a baby,

[018-176]to think of being separated! Of course it is out of the question."

[018-177]"You cannot suppose, Lady Milborough, that I want to be separated

[018-178]from my husband?"

[018-179]"Of course not. How should it be possible? The very idea is too

[018-180]shocking to be thought of. I declare I haven't slept since Louis

[018-181]was talking to me about it. But, my dear, you must remember, you

[018-182]know, that a husband has a right to expect some--some--some--a sort

[018-183]of--submission from his wife."
