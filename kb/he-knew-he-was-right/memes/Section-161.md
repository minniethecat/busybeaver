## Section 161

[161-001]"Emily," he said one day, "it has been a terrible tragedy, has it

[161-002]not?" She did not answer his question, sitting silent as it was her

[161-003]custom to do when he addressed her after such fashion as this. At

[161-004]such times she would not answer him; but she knew that he would

[161-005]press her for an answer. "I blame him more than I do you," continued

[161-006]Trevelyan,--"infinitely more. He was a serpent intending to sting me

[161-007]from the first,--not knowing perhaps how deep the sting would go."

[161-008]There was no question in this, and the assertion was one which had

[161-009]been made so often that she could let it pass. "You are young, Emily,

[161-010]and it may be that you will marry again."

[161-011]"Never," she said, with a shudder. It seemed to her then that

[161-012]marriage was so fearful a thing that certainly she could never

[161-013]venture upon it again.

[161-014]"All I ask of you is, that should you do so, you will be more careful

[161-015]of your husband's honour."

[161-016]"Louis," she said, getting up and [[reputation|standing]] close to him, "tell me

[161-017]what it is that you mean." It was now his turn to remain silent,

[161-018]and hers to demand an answer. "I have borne much," she continued,

[161-019]"because I would not vex you in your illness."

[161-020]"You have borne much?"

[161-021]"Indeed and indeed, yes. What woman has ever borne more!"

[161-022]"And I?" said he.

[161-023]"Dear Louis, let us understand each other at last. Of what do you

[161-024]accuse me? Let us, at any rate, know each other's thoughts on this

[161-025]matter, of which each of us is ever thinking."

[161-026]"I make no new accusation."

[161-027]"I must protest then against your using words which seem to convey

[161-028]accusation. Since marriages were first known upon earth, no woman has

[161-029]ever been truer to her husband than I have been to you."

[161-030]"Were you lying to me then at Casalunga when you acknowledged that

[161-031]you had been false to your duties?"

[161-032]"If I acknowledged that, I did lie. I never said that; but yet I did

[161-033]lie,--believing it to be best for you that I should do so. For your

[161-034]honour's sake, for the child's sake, weak as you are, Louis, I must

[161-035]protest that it was so. I have never injured you by deed or thought."

[161-036]"And yet you have lied to me! Is a lie no injury;--and such a lie!

[161-037]Emily, why did you lie to me? You will tell me to-morrow that you

[161-038]never lied, and never owned that you had lied."

[161-039]Though it should kill him, she must tell him the truth now. "You were

[161-040]very ill at Casalunga," she said, after a pause.

[161-041]"But not so ill as I am now. I could breathe that air. I could live

[161-042]there. Had I remained I should have been well now,--but what of

[161-043]that?"

[161-044]"Louis, you were dying there. Pray, pray listen to me. We thought

[161-045]that you were dying; and we knew also that you would be taken from

[161-046]that house."

[161-047]"That was my affair. Do you mean that I could not keep a house over

[161-048]my head?" At this moment he was half lying, half sitting, in a large

[161-049]easy chair in the little drawing-room of their cottage, to which he

[161-050]had been carried from the adjoining bed-room. When not excited, he

[161-051]would sit for hours without moving, gazing through the open window,

[161-052]sometimes with some pretext of a book lying within the reach of his

[161-053]hand; but almost without strength to lift it, and certainly without

[161-054]power to read it. But now he had worked himself up to so much energy

[161-055]that he almost raised himself up in his chair, as he turned towards

[161-056]his wife. "Had I not the world before me, to choose a house in?"

[161-057]"They would have put you somewhere, and I could not have reached

[161-058]you."

[161-059]"In a madhouse, you mean. Yes;--if you had told them."

[161-060]"Will you listen, dear Louis? We knew that it was our duty to bring

[161-061]you home; and as you would not let me come to you, and serve you, and

[161-062]assist you to come here where you are safe,--unless I owned that you

[161-063]had been right, I said that you had been right."

[161-064]"And it was a lie,--you say now?"

[161-065]"All that is nothing. I cannot go through it; nor should you. There

[161-066]is the only question. You do not think that I have been--? I need not

[161-067]say the thing. You do not think that?" As she asked the question, she

[161-068]knelt beside him, and took his hand in hers, and kissed it. "Say that

[161-069]you do not think that, and I will never trouble you further about the

[161-070]past."

[161-071]"Yes;--that is it. You will never trouble me!" She glanced up into

[161-072]his face and saw there the old look which he used to wear when he was

[161-073]at Willesden and at Casalunga; and there had come again the old tone

[161-074]in which he had spoken to her in the bitterness of his wrath:--the

[161-075]look and the tone, which had made her sure that he was a madman. "The

[161-076]craft and subtlety of women passes everything!" he said. "And so at

[161-077]last I am to tell you that from the beginning it has been my doing. I

[161-078]will never say so, though I should die in refusing to do it."

[161-079]After that there was no possibility of further conversation, for

[161-080]there came upon him a fit of coughing, and then he swooned; and in

[161-081]half-an-hour he was in bed, and Dr. Nevill was by his side. "You must

[161-082]not speak to him at all on this matter," said the doctor. "But if he

[161-083]speaks to me?" she asked. "Let it pass," said the doctor. "Let the

[161-084]subject be got rid of with as much ease as you can. He is very ill

[161-085]now, and even this might have killed him." Nevertheless, though this

[161-086]seemed to be stern, Dr. Nevill was very kind to her, declaring that

[161-087]the hallucination in her husband's mind did not really consist of a

[161-088]belief in her infidelity, but arose from an obstinate determination

[161-089]to yield nothing. "He does not believe it; but he feels that were he

[161-090]to say as much, his hands would be weakened and yours strengthened."

[161-091]"Can he then be in his sane mind?"

[161-092]"In one sense all misconduct is [[truth|proof]] of [[madness|insanity]]," said the doctor.

[161-093]"In his case the weakness of the mind has been consequent upon the

[161-094]weakness of the body."

[161-095]Three days after that Nora visited Twickenham from Monkhams in

[161-096]obedience to a telegram from her sister. "Louis," she said, "had

[161-097]become so much weaker, that she hardly dared to be alone with him.

[161-098]Would Nora come to her?" Nora came of course, and Hugh met her at the

[161-099][[social-class|station]], and brought her with him to the cottage. He asked whether

[161-100]he might see Trevelyan, but was told that it would be better that

[161-101]he should not. He had been almost continually silent since the last

[161-102]dispute which he had with his wife; but he had given little signs

[161-103]that he was always thinking of the manner in which he had been

[161-104]brought home by her from Italy, and of the story she had told him

[161-105]of her mode of inducing him to come. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had been her

[161-106]partner in that struggle, and would probably be received, if not with

[161-107]sullen silence, then with some attempt at rebuke. But Hugh did see

[161-108]Dr. Nevill, and learned from him that it was hardly possible that

[161-109]Trevelyan should live many hours. "He has worn himself out," said the

[161-110]doctor, "and there is nothing left in him by which he can lay hold of

[161-111]life again." Of Nora her brother-in-law took but little notice, and

[161-112]never again referred in her hearing to the great trouble of his life.

[161-113]He said to her a word or two about Monkhams, and asked a question now

[161-114]and again as to Lord Peterborough,--whom, however, he always called

[161-115]Mr. Glascock; but Hugh Stanbury's name was never mentioned by him.

[161-116]There was a feeling in his mind that at the very last he had been

[161-117]duped in being brought to England, and that Stanbury had assisted in

[161-118]the deception. To his wife he would whisper little petulant regrets

[161-119]for the loss of the comforts of Casalunga, and would speak of the air

[161-120]of Italy and of Italian skies and of the Italian sun, as though he

[161-121]had enjoyed at his Sienese villa all the luxuries which climate can

[161-122]give, and would have enjoyed them still had he been allowed to remain

[161-123]there. To all this she would say nothing. She knew now that he was

[161-124]failing quickly, and there was only one subject on which she either

[161-125]feared or hoped to hear him speak. Before he left her for ever and

[161-126]ever would he tell her that he had not doubted her faith?

[161-127]She had long discussions with Nora on the matter, as though all the

[161-128]future of her life depended on it. It was in vain that Nora tried to

[161-129]make her understand that if hereafter the spirit of her husband could

[161-130]know anything of the troubles of his mortal life, could ever look

[161-131]back to the things which he had done in the flesh, then would he

[161-132]certainly know the truth, and all suspicion would be at an end. And

[161-133]if not, if there was to be no such retrospect, what did it matter

[161-134]now, for these few last hours before the coil should be shaken off,

[161-135]and all doubt and all sorrow should be at an end? But the wife, who

[161-136]was soon to be a widow, yearned to be acquitted in this world by him

[161-137]to whom her guilt or her innocence had been matter of such vital

[161-138]importance. "He has never thought it," said Nora.

[161-139]"But if he would say so! If he would only look it! It will be all in

[161-140]all to me as long as I live in this world." And then, though they had

[161-141]determined between themselves in spoken words never to regard him

[161-142]again as one who had been mad, in all their thoughts and actions

[161-143]towards him they treated him as though he were less responsible than

[161-144]an infant. And he was mad;--mad though every doctor in England had

[161-145]called him sane. Had he not been mad he must have been a fiend,--or

[161-146]he could not have tortured, as he had done, the woman to whom he owed

[161-147]the closest protection which one human being can give to another.

[161-148]During these last days and nights she never left him. She had done

[161-149]her duty to him well, at any rate since the time when she had been

[161-150]enabled to come near him in Italy. It may be that in the first days

[161-151]of their quarrel, she had not been regardful, as she should have

[161-152]been, of a husband's will,--that she might have escaped this tragedy

[161-153]by submitting herself to the man's wishes, as she had always been

[161-154]ready to submit herself to his words. Had she been able always to

[161-155]keep her neck in the dust under his foot, their married life might

[161-156]have been passed without outward calamity, and it is possible that

[161-157]he might still have lived. But if she erred, surely she had been

[161-158]scourged for her error with scorpions. As she sat at his bedside

[161-159]watching him, she thought of her wasted youth, of her faded beauty,

[161-160]of her shattered happiness, of her fallen hopes. She had still her

[161-161]child,--but she felt towards him that she herself was so sad a

[161-162]creature, so sombre, so dark, so necessarily wretched from this time

[161-163]forth till the day of her death, that it would be better for the boy

[161-164]that she should never be with him. There could be nothing left for

[161-165]her but garments dark with woe, eyes red with weeping, hours sad from

[161-166]solitude, thoughts weary with memory. And even yet,--if he would only

[161-167]now say that he did not believe her to have been guilty, how great

[161-168]would be the change in her future life!
