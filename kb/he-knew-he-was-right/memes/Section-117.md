## Section 117

[117-001]"I don't know, sir. I'm not in the secrets of the Cabinet. I should

[117-002]think he would."

[117-003]"[[About]] as much as my grandmother;--but if the Editor of the 'Jupiter'

[117-004]were to be taken ill, it would work quite a commotion. For myself I

[117-005]should be glad,--on public grounds,--because I don't like his mode of

[117-006]business. But it would have an effect,--because he is a leading man."

[117-007]"I don't see what all this leads to, Mr. Stanbury."

[117-008]"Only to this,--that we who write for the press think that our

[117-009]calling is recognised, and must be recognised as a profession. Talk

[117-010]of permanence, Sir Marmaduke, are not the newspapers permanent? Do

[117-011]not they come out regularly every day,--and more of them, and still

[117-012]more of them, are always coming out? You do not expect a collapse

[117-013]among them."

[117-014]"There will be plenty of newspapers, I do not doubt;--more than

[117-015]plenty, perhaps."

[117-016]"Somebody must write them,--and the writers will be paid."

[117-017]"Anybody could write the most of them, I should say."

[117-018]"I wish you would try, Sir Marmaduke. Just try your hand at a leading

[117-019]article to-night, and read it yourself to-morrow morning."

[117-020]"I've a great deal too much to do, Mr. Stanbury."

[117-021]"Just so. You have, no doubt, the affairs of your Government to look

[117-022]to. We are all so apt to ignore the work of our neighbours! It seems

[117-023]to me that I could go over and govern the Mandarins without the

[117-024]slightest trouble in the world. But no doubt I am mistaken;--just as

[117-025]you are about writing for the newspapers."

[117-026]"I do not know," said Sir Marmaduke, rising from his chair with

[117-027][[pride|dignity]], "that I called here to discuss such matters as these. As it

[117-028]happens, you, Mr. Stanbury, are not the Governor of the Mandarins,

[117-029]and I have not the honour to write for the columns of the penny

[117-030]newspaper with which you are associated. It is therefore useless

[117-031]to discuss what either of us might do in the position held by the

[117-032]other."

[117-033]"Altogether useless, Sir Marmaduke,--except just for the fun of the

[117-034]thing."

[117-035]"I do not see the fun, Mr. Stanbury. I came here, at your request,

[117-036]to hear what you might have to urge against the decision which I

[117-037]expressed to you in reference to my daughter. As it seems that you

[117-038]have nothing to urge, I will not take up your time further."

[117-039]"But I have a great deal to urge, and have urged a great deal."

[117-040]"Have you, indeed?"

[117-041]"You have complained that my work is not permanent. I have shewn that

[117-042]it is so permanent that there is no possibility of its coming to an

[117-043]end. There must be newspapers, and the people trained to write them

[117-044]must be employed. I have been at it now about two years. You know

[117-045]what I earn. Could I have got so far in so short a time as a lawyer,

[117-046]a doctor, a clergyman, a soldier, a sailor, a Government clerk, or

[117-047]in any of those employments which you choose to call professions? I

[117-048]think that is urging a great deal. I think it is urging everything."

[117-049]"Very well, Mr. Stanbury. I have listened to you, and in a certain

[117-050]degree I admire your,--your,--your zeal and ingenuity, shall I say."

[117-051]"I didn't mean to call for admiration, Sir Marmaduke; but suppose you

[117-052]say,--good sense and discrimination."

[117-053]"Let that pass. You must permit me to remark that your position is

[117-054]not such as to justify me in trusting my daughter to your care. As my

[117-055]mind on that matter is quite made up, as is that also of Lady Rowley,

[117-056]I must ask you to give me your promise that your suit to my daughter

[117-057]shall be discontinued."

[117-058]"What does she say about it, Sir Marmaduke?"

[117-059]"What she has said to me has been for my ears, and not for yours."

[117-060]"What I say is for her ears and for yours, and for her mother's ears,

[117-061]and for the ears of any who may choose to hear it. I will never give

[117-062]up my suit to your daughter till I am forced to do so by a full

[117-063]conviction that she has given me up. It is best to be plain, Sir

[117-064]Marmaduke, of course."

[117-065]"I do not understand this, Mr. Stanbury."

[117-066]"I mean to be quite clear."

[117-067]"I have always thought that when a [[social-class|gentleman]] was told by the head of

[117-068]a family that he could not be made welcome in that family, it was

[117-069]considered to be the duty of that gentleman,--as a gentleman,--to

[117-070]abandon his vain pursuit. I have been brought up with that idea."

[117-071]"And I, Sir Marmaduke, have been brought up in the idea that when

[117-072]a man has won the affections of a woman, it is the duty of that

[117-073]man,--as a man,--to stick to her through thick and thin; and I mean

[117-074]to do my duty, according to my idea."

[117-075]"Then, sir, I have nothing further to say, but to take my leave. I

[117-076]must only caution you not to enter my doors." As the passages were

[117-077]dark and intricate, it was necessary that Stanbury should shew Sir

[117-078]Marmaduke out, and this he did in silence. When they parted each of

[117-079]them lifted his hat, and not a word more was said.

[117-080]That same night there was a note put into Nora's hands, as she was

[117-081]following her mother out of one of the theatres. In the confusion she

[117-082]did not even see the messenger who had handed it to her. Her sister

[117-083]Lucy saw that she had taken the note, and questioned her about it

[117-084]afterwards,--with discretion, however, and in privacy. This was the

[117-085]note:--

[117-086]DEAREST LOVE,

[117-087]I have seen your father, who is stern,--after the manner

[117-088]of fathers. What granite equals a parent's flinty bosom!

[117-089]For myself, I do not prefer clandestine arrangements

[117-090]and rope ladders; and you, dear, have nothing of the

[117-091]Lydia about you. But I do like my own way, and like it

[117-092]especially when you are at the end of the path. It is

[117-093]quite out of the question that you should go back to those

[117-094]islands. I think I am justified in already assuming enough

[117-095]of the husband to declare that such going back must not be

[117-096]held for a moment in question. My proposition is that you

[117-097]should authorise me to make such arrangements as may be

[117-098]needed, in regard to licence, banns, or whatever else, and

[117-099]that you should then simply walk from the house to the

[117-100]church and marry me. You are of age, and can do as you

[117-101]please. Neither your father nor mother can have any right

[117-102]to stop you. I do not doubt but that your mother would

[117-103]accompany you, if she were fully satisfied of your

[117-104]purpose. Write to me to the D. R.

[117-105]Your own, ever and ever, and always,

[117-106]H. S.

[117-107]I shall try and get this given to you as you leave the

[117-108]theatre. If it should fall into other hands, I don't much

[117-109]care. I'm not in the least ashamed of what I am doing; and

[117-110]I hope that you are not.

[117-111]CHAPTER LXXII.

[117-112]THE DELIVERY OF THE LAMB.

[117-113][Illustration]

[117-114]It is hoped that a certain quarter of lamb will not have been

[117-115]forgotten,--a quarter of lamb that was sent as a peace-offering from

[117-116][[exeter|Exeter]] to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] by the hands of Miss Stanbury's Martha, not

[117-117]with purposes of corruption, not intended to buy back the allegiance

[117-118]of Dorothy,--folded delicately and temptingly in one of the best

[117-119]table napkins, with no idea of bribery, but sent as presents used

[117-120]to be sent of old in the trains of great ambassadors as signs of

[117-121]friendship and marks of true respect. Miss Stanbury was, no doubt,

[117-122]most anxious that her niece should return to her, but was not,

[117-123]herself, low spirited enough to conceive that a quarter of lamb

[117-124]could be efficacious in procuring such return. If it might be that

[117-125]Dorothy's heart could be touched by mention of the weariness of her

[117-126]aunt's solitary life; and if, therefore, she would return, it would

[117-127]be very well; but it could not be well so, unless the offer should

[117-128]come from Dorothy herself. All of which Martha had been made to

[117-129]understand by her mistress, considerable ingenuity having been

[117-130]exercised in the matter on each side.

[117-131]On her arrival at Lessboro', Martha had hired a fly, and been driven

[117-132]out to Nuncombe Putney; but she felt, she knew not why, a dislike

[117-133]to be taken in her carriage to the door of the cottage; and was put

[117-134]down in the middle of the village, from whence she walked out to Mrs.

[117-135]Stanbury's abode, with the basket upon her arm. It was a good half

[117-136]mile, and the lamb was heavy, for Miss Stanbury had suggested that a

[117-137]bottle of sherry should be put in under the napkin,--and Martha was

[117-138]becoming tired of her burden, when,--whom should she see on the road

[117-139]before her but Brooke Burgess! As she said herself afterwards, it

[117-140]immediately occurred to her, "that all the fat was in the fire." Here

[117-141]had this young man come down, passing through Exeter without even a

[117-142]visit to Miss Stanbury, and had clandestinely sought out the young

[117-143]woman whom he wasn't to marry; and here was the young woman herself

[117-144]flying in her aunt's face, when one scratch of a pen might ruin them

[117-145]both! Martha entertained a sacred, awful, overcoming feeling about

[117-146]her mistress's will. That she was to have something herself she

[117-147]supposed, and her anxiety was not on that score; but she had heard

[117-148]so much about it, had realised so fully the great power which Miss

[117-149]Stanbury possessed, and had had her own feelings so rudely invaded by

[117-150]alterations in Miss Stanbury's plans, that she had come to entertain

[117-151]an idea that all persons around her should continually bear that will

[117-152]in their memory. Hugh had undoubtedly been her favourite, and, could

[117-153]Martha have dictated the will herself, she would still have made Hugh

[117-154]the heir; but she had realised the resolution of her mistress so

[117-155]far as to confess that the bulk of the [[money|property]] was to go back to a

[117-156]Burgess. But there were very many Burgesses; and here was the one

[117-157]who had been selected flying in the very face of the testatrix! What

[117-158]was to be done? Were she to go back and not tell her mistress that

[117-159]she had seen Brooke Burgess at Nuncombe then,--should the fact be

[117-160]found out,--would the devoted anger of Miss Stanbury fall upon

[117-161]her own head? It would be absolutely necessary that she should

[117-162]tell the story, let the consequences be what they might;--but the

[117-163]consequences, probably, would be very dreadful. "Mr. Brooke, that is

[117-164]not you?" she said, as she came up to him, putting her basket down in

[117-165]the middle of the dusty road.

[117-166]"Then who can it be?" said Brooke, giving her his hand to shake.

[117-167]"But what do bring you here, Mr. Brooke? Goodness me, what will

[117-168]missus say?"

[117-169]"I shall make that all straight. I'm going back to Exeter to-morrow."

[117-170]Then there were many questions and many answers. He was sojourning

[117-171]at Mrs. Crocket's, and had been there for the last two days. "Dear,

[117-172]dear, dear," she said over and over again. "Deary me, deary me!" and

[117-173]then she asked him whether it was "all along of Miss Dorothy" that he

[117-174]had come. Of course, it was all along of Miss Dorothy. Brooke made no

[117-175]secret about it. He had come down to see Dorothy's mother and sister,

[117-176]and to say a bit of his own mind about future affairs;--and to see

[117-177]the beauties of the country. When he talked about the beauties of the

[117-178]country, Martha looked at him as the people of Lessboro' and Nuncombe

[117-179]Putney should have looked at [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], when he talked of

[117-180]the church porch at Cockchaffington. "Beauties of the country, Mr.

[117-181]Brooke;--you ought to be ashamed of yourself!" said Martha.
