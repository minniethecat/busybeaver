## Section 81

[081-001]"Well,--don't they? It's only for the name of it they have them.

[081-002]There's the Bishop of Broomsgrove;--he's always sauntering about the

[081-003]place, looking as though he'd be so much obliged if somebody would

[081-004]give him something to do. He's always smiling, and so gracious,--just

[081-005]as if he didn't feel above half sure that he had any right to be

[081-006]where he is, and he thought that perhaps somebody was going to kick

[081-007]him."

[081-008]"And so old Proudie is coming up again," said Brooke. "It certainly

[081-009]is very much the same to us whom they send. He'll get shoved into a

[081-010]corner, as you call it,--only that he'll go into the corner without

[081-011]any shoving." Then there came in a messenger with a card, and Brooke

[081-012]learned that [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] was waiting for him in the strangers'

[081-013]room. In performing the promise made to Dorothy, he had called upon

[081-014]her brother as soon as he was back in [[london|London]], but had not found him.

[081-015]This now was the return visit.

[081-016]"I thought I was sure to find you here," said Hugh.

[081-017]"Pretty nearly sure from eleven till five," said Brooke. "A hard

[081-018]stepmother like the Civil Service does not allow one much chance of

[081-019]relief. I do get across to the club sometimes for a glass of sherry

[081-020]and a biscuit,--but here I am now, at any rate; and I'm very glad

[081-021]you have come." Then there was some talk between them about affairs

[081-022]at [[exeter|Exeter]]; but as they were interrupted before half an hour was

[081-023]over their heads by a summons brought for Burgess from one of

[081-024]the secretaries, it was agreed that they should dine together at

[081-025]Burgess's club on the following day. "We can manage a pretty good

[081-026]beef-steak," said Brooke, "and have a fair glass of sherry. I don't

[081-027]think you can get much more than that anywhere nowadays,--unless you

[081-028]want a dinner for eight at three guineas a head. The magnificence of

[081-029]men has become so intolerable now that one is driven to be humble in

[081-030]one's self-defence." Stanbury assured his acquaintance that he was

[081-031]anything but magnificent in his own ideas, that cold beef and beer

[081-032]was his usual fare, and at last allowed the clerk to wait upon the

[081-033]secretary.

[081-034]"I wouldn't have any other fellow to meet you," said Brooke as they

[081-035]sat at their dinners, "because in this way we can talk over the dear

[081-036]old woman at Exeter. Yes, our fellow does make good soup, and it's

[081-037]about all that he does do well. As for getting a potato properly

[081-038]boiled, that's quite out of the question. Yes, it is a good glass

[081-039]of sherry. I told you we'd a fairish tap of sherry on. Well, I was

[081-040]there, backwards and forwards, for nearly six weeks."

[081-041]"And how did you get on with the old woman?"

[081-042]"Like a house on fire," said Brooke.

[081-043]"She didn't quarrel with you?"

[081-044]"No,--upon the whole she did not. I always felt that it was touch

[081-045]and go. She might or she might not. Every now and then she looked at

[081-046]me, and said a sharp word, as though it was about to come. But I had

[081-047]determined when I went there altogether to disregard that kind of

[081-048]thing."

[081-049]"It's rather important to you,--is it not?"

[081-050]"You mean about her money?"

[081-051]"Of course, I mean about her money," said Stanbury.

[081-052]"It is important;--and so it was to you."

[081-053]"Not in the same degree, or nearly so. And as for me, it was not on

[081-054]the cards that we shouldn't quarrel. I am so utterly a Bohemian in

[081-055]all my ideas of life, and she is so absolutely the reverse, that not

[081-056]to have quarrelled would have been hypocritical on my part or on

[081-057]hers. She had got it into her head that she had a right to rule

[081-058]my life; and, of course, she quarrelled with me when I made her

[081-059]understand that she should do nothing of the kind. Now, she won't

[081-060]want to rule you."

[081-061]"I hope not."

[081-062]"She has taken you up," continued Stanbury, "on altogether a

[081-063]different understanding. You are to her the representative of a

[081-064]family to whom she thinks she owes the restitution of the [[money|property]]

[081-065]which she enjoys. I was simply a member of her own family, to which

[081-066]she owes nothing. She thought it well to help one of us out of what

[081-067]she regarded as her private purse, and she chose me. But the matter

[081-068]is quite different with you."

[081-069]"She might have given everything to you, as well as to me," said

[081-070]Brooke.

[081-071]"That's not her idea. She conceives herself bound to leave all she

[081-072]has back to a Burgess, except anything she may save,--as she says,

[081-073]off her own back, or out of her own belly. She has told me so a score

[081-074]of times."

[081-075]"And what did you say?"

[081-076]"I always told her that, let her do as she would, I should never ask

[081-077]any question about her will."

[081-078]"But she hates us all like poison,--except me," said Brooke. "I never

[081-079]knew people so absurdly hostile as are your aunt and my uncle Barty.

[081-080]Each thinks the other the most wicked person in the world."

[081-081]"I suppose your uncle was hard upon her once."

[081-082]"Very likely. He is a hard man,--and has, very warmly, all the

[081-083]feelings of an injured man. I suppose my uncle Brooke's will was a

[081-084]cruel blow to him. He professes to believe that Miss Stanbury will

[081-085]never leave me a shilling."

[081-086]"He is wrong, then," said Stanbury.

[081-087]"Oh yes;--he's wrong, because he thinks that that's her present

[081-088]intention. I don't know that he's wrong as to the probable result."

[081-089]"Who will have it, then?"

[081-090]"There are ever so many horses in the race," said Brooke. "I'm one."

[081-091]"You're the favourite," said Stanbury.

[081-092]"For the moment I am. Then there's yourself."

[081-093]"I've been scratched, and am altogether out of the betting."

[081-094]"And your sister," continued Brooke.

[081-095]"She's only entered to run for the second money; and, if she'll trot

[081-096]over the course quietly, and not go the wrong side of the posts,

[081-097]she'll win that."

[081-098]"She may do more than that. Then there's Martha."

[081-099]"My aunt will never leave her money to a servant. What she may give

[081-100]to Martha would come from her own savings."

[081-101]"The next is a dark horse, but one that wins a good many races of

[081-102]this kind. He's apt to come in with a fatal rush at the end."

[081-103]"Who is it?"

[081-104]"The hospitals. When an old lady finds in her latter days that she

[081-105]hates everybody, and fancies that the people around her are all

[081-106]thinking of her money, she's uncommon likely to indulge herself in a

[081-107]little bit of revenge, and solace herself with large-handed charity."

[081-108]"But she's so good a woman at heart," said Hugh.

[081-109]"And what can a good woman do better than promote hospitals?"

[081-110]"She'll never do that. She's too strong. It's a maudlin sort of

[081-111]thing, after all, for a person to leave everything to a hospital."

[081-112]"But people are maudlin when they're dying," said Brooke,--"or even

[081-113]when they think they're dying. How else did the Church get the

[081-114]estates, of which we are now distributing so bountifully some of the

[081-115]last remnants down at our office? Come into the next room, and we'll

[081-116]have a smoke."

[081-117]They had their smoke, and then they went at half-price to the play;

[081-118]and, after the play was over, they eat three or four dozen of oysters

[081-119]between them. Brooke Burgess was a little too old for oysters at

[081-120]midnight in September; but he went through his work like a man. Hugh

[081-121]Stanbury's powers were so great, that he could have got up and done

[081-122]the same thing again, after he had been an hour in bed, without any

[081-123]serious inconvenience.

[081-124]But, in truth, Brooke Burgess had still another word or two to say

[081-125]before he went to his rest. They supped somewhere near the Haymarket,

[081-126]and then he offered to walk home with Stanbury, to his chambers in

[081-127]Lincoln's Inn. "Do you know that Mr. Gibson at Exeter?" he asked, as

[081-128]they passed through Leicester Square.

[081-129]"Yes; I knew him. He was a sort of tame-cat parson at my aunt's

[081-130]house, in my days."

[081-131]"Exactly;--but I fancy that has come to an end now. Have you heard

[081-132]anything about him lately?"

[081-133]"Well;--yes I have," said Stanbury, feeling that dislike to speak

[081-134]of his sister which is common to most brothers when in company with

[081-135]other men.

[081-136]"I suppose you've heard of it, and, as I was in the middle of it all,

[081-137]of course I couldn't but know all about it too. Your aunt wanted him

[081-138]to marry your sister."

[081-139]"So I was told."

[081-140]"But your sister didn't see it," said Brooke.

[081-141]"So I understand," said Stanbury. "I believe my aunt was exceedingly

[081-142]liberal, and meant to do the best she could for poor Dorothy; but, if

[081-143]she didn't like him, I suppose she was right not to have him," said

[081-144]Hugh.

[081-145]"Of course she was right," said Brooke, with a good deal of

[081-146]enthusiasm.

[081-147]"I believe Gibson to be a very decent sort of fellow," said Stanbury.

[081-148]"A mean, paltry dog," said Brooke. There had been a little

[081-149]whisky-toddy after the oysters, and Mr. Burgess was perhaps moved to

[081-150]a warmer expression of feeling than he might have displayed had he

[081-151]discussed this branch of the subject before supper. "I knew from the

[081-152]first that she would have nothing to say to him. He is such a poor

[081-153]creature!"

[081-154]"I always thought well of him," said Stanbury, "and was inclined to

[081-155]think that Dolly might have done worse."

[081-156]"It is hard to say what is the worst a girl might do; but I think she

[081-157]might do, perhaps, a little better."

[081-158]"What do you mean?" said Hugh.

[081-159]"I think I shall go down, and ask her to take myself."

[081-160]"Do you mean it in earnest?"

[081-161]"I do," said Brooke. "Of course, I hadn't a chance when I was there.

[081-162]She told me--"

[081-163]"Who told you;--Dorothy?"

[081-164]"No, your aunt;--she told me that Mr. Gibson was to marry your

[081-165]sister. You know your aunt's way. She spoke of it as though the thing

[081-166]were settled as soon as she had got it into her own head; and she was

[081-167]as hot upon it as though Mr. Gibson had been an archbishop. I had

[081-168]nothing to do then but to wait and see."

[081-169]"I had no idea of Dolly being fought for by rivals."

[081-170]"Brothers never think much of their sisters," said Brooke Burgess.

[081-171]"I can assure you I think a great deal of Dorothy," said Hugh. "I

[081-172]believe her to be as sweet a woman as God ever made. She hardly knows

[081-173]that she has a self belonging to herself."

[081-174]"I am sure she doesn't," said Brooke.

[081-175]"She is a dear, loving, sweet-tempered creature, who is only too

[081-176]ready to yield in all things."

[081-177]"But she wouldn't yield about Gibson," said Brooke.

[081-178]"How did she and my aunt manage?"

[081-179]"Your sister simply said she couldn't,--and then that she wouldn't. I

[081-180]never thought from the first moment that she'd take that fellow. In

[081-181]the first place he can't say boo to a goose."

[081-182]"But Dolly wouldn't want a man to say--boo."

[081-183]"I'm not so sure of that, old fellow. At any rate I mean to try

[081-184]myself. Now,--what'll the old woman say?"

[081-185]"She'll be pleased as Punch, I should think," said Stanbury.

[081-186]"Either that;--or else she'll swear that she'll never speak another

[081-187]word to either of us. However, I shall go on with it."

[081-188]"Does Dorothy know anything of this?" asked Stanbury.

[081-189]"Not a word," said Brooke. "I came away a day or so after Gibson was

[081-190]settled; and as I had been talked to all through the affair by both

[081-191]of them, I couldn't turn round and offer myself the moment he was

[081-192]gone. You won't object;--will you?"

[081-193]"Who; I?" said Stanbury. "I shall have no objection as long as Dolly

[081-194]pleases herself. Of course you know that we haven't as much as a

[081-195]brass farthing among us?"
