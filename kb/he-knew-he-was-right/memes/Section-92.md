## Section 92

[092-001]But Mr. Glascock had not come to Florence for this. There were

[092-002]circumstances in his present position which made him feel that he

[092-003]would be gratified in escaping, even at the cost of some seeming

[092-004]incivility. "I must go in to the ladies at once," he said, "or I

[092-005]shall never get a word with them." There came across the minister's

[092-006]brow a momentary frown of displeasure, as though he felt that he were

[092-007]being robbed of that which was justly his own. For an instant his

[092-008]grasp fixed itself more tightly to the coat. It was quite within

[092-009]the scope of his courage to hold a struggling listener by physical

[092-010]strength;--but he remembered that there was a purpose, and he relaxed

[092-011]his hold.

[092-012]"I will take another opportunity," said the minister. "As you have

[092-013]raised that somewhat trite objection of the bearing of children,

[092-014]which we in our country, sir, have altogether got over, I must put

[092-015]you in possession of my views on that subject; but I will find

[092-016]another occasion." Then Mr. Glascock began to reflect whether an

[092-017]American lady, married in England, would probably want to see much of

[092-018]her uncle in her adopted country.

[092-019]Mrs. Spalding was all smiles when her guest reached her. "We did not

[092-020]mean to have such a crowd of people," she said, whispering; "but you

[092-021]know how one thing leads to another, and people here really like

[092-022]short invitations." Then the minister's wife bowed very low to an

[092-023]Italian lady, and for the moment wished herself in Beacon Street. It

[092-024]was a great trouble to her that she could not pluck up courage to

[092-025]speak a word in Italian. "I know more about it than some that are

[092-026]glib enough," she would say to her niece Livy, "but these Tuscans are

[092-027]so particular with their Bocca Toscana."

[092-028]It was almost spiteful on the part of Miss Petrie,--the manner in

[092-029]which, on this evening, she remained close to her friend Caroline

[092-030]Spalding. It is hardly possible to believe that it came altogether

[092-031]from high principle,--from a determination to save her friend from

[092-032]an impending danger. One's friend has no right to decide for one

[092-033]what is, and what is not dangerous. Mr. Glascock after awhile found

[092-034]himself seated on a fixed couch, that ran along the wall, between

[092-035]Carry Spalding and Miss Petrie; but Miss Petrie was almost as bad

[092-036]to him as had been the minister himself. "I am afraid," she said,

[092-037]looking up into his face with some severity, and rushing upon her

[092-038]subject with audacity, "that the works of your Browning have not

[092-039]been received in your country with that veneration to which they are

[092-040]entitled."

[092-041]"Do you mean Mr. or Mrs. Browning?" asked Mr. Glascock,--perhaps with

[092-042]some mistaken idea that the lady was out of her depth, and did not

[092-043]know the difference.

[092-044]"Either;--both; for they are one, the same, and indivisible. The

[092-045]spirit and germ of each is so reflected in the outcome of the other,

[092-046]that one sees only the result of so perfect a combination, and one

[092-047]is tempted to acknowledge that here and there a marriage may have

[092-048]been arranged in Heaven. I don't think that in your country you have

[092-049]perceived this, Mr. Glascock."

[092-050]"I am not quite sure that we have," said Mr. Glascock.

[092-051]"Yours is not altogether an inglorious mission," continued Miss

[092-052]Petrie.

[092-053]"I've got no mission," said Mr. Glascock,--"either from the Foreign

[092-054]Office, or from my own inner convictions."

[092-055]Miss Petrie laughed with a scornful laugh. "I spoke, sir, of the

[092-056]mission of that small speck on the earth's broad surface, of which

[092-057]you think so much, and which we call Great Britain."

[092-058]"I do think a good deal of it," said Mr. Glascock.

[092-059]"It has been more thought of than any other speck of the same size,"

[092-060]said Carry Spalding.

[092-061]"True," said Miss Petrie, sharply;--"because of its iron and coal.

[092-062]But the mission I spoke of was this." And she put forth her hand with

[092-063]an artistic motion as she spoke. "It utters prophecies, though it

[092-064]cannot read them. It sends forth truth, though it cannot understand

[092-065]it. Though its own ears are deaf as adders', it is the nursery of

[092-066]poets, who sing not for their own countrymen, but for the higher

[092-067]sensibilities and newer intelligences of lands, in which philanthropy

[092-068]has made education as common as the air that is breathed."

[092-069]"Wally," said Olivia, coming up to the poetess, in anger that was

[092-070]almost apparent, "I want to take you, and introduce you to the

[092-071]Marchesa Pulti."

[092-072]But Miss Petrie no doubt knew that the eldest son of an English lord

[092-073]was at least as good as an Italian marchesa. "Let her come here,"

[092-074]said the poetess, with her grandest smile.

[092-075]CHAPTER LVI.

[092-076]WITHERED GRASS.

[092-077][Illustration]

[092-078]When Caroline Spalding perceived how direct an attempt had been made

[092-079]by her sister to take the poetess away, in order that she might

[092-080]thus be left alone with Mr. Glascock, her spirit revolted against

[092-081]the manoeuvre, and she took herself away amidst the crowd. If Mr.

[092-082]Glascock should wish to find her again he could do so. And there came

[092-083]across her mind something of a half-formed idea that, perhaps after

[092-084]all her friend Wallachia was right. Were this man ready to take her

[092-085]and she ready to be taken, would such an arrangement be a happy one

[092-086]for both of them? His high-born, wealthy friends might very probably

[092-087]despise her, and it was quite possible that she also might despise

[092-088]them. To be Lady Peterborough, and have the spending of a large

[092-089][[money|fortune]], would not suffice for her happiness. She was sure of that.

[092-090]It would be a leap in the dark, and all such leaps must needs be

[092-091]dangerous, and therefore should be avoided. But she did like the man.

[092-092]Her friend was untrue to her and cruel in those allusions to tinkling

[092-093]cymbals. It might be well for her to get over her liking, and to

[092-094]think no more of one who was to her a foreigner and a stranger,--of

[092-095]whose ways of living in his own home she knew so little, whose people

[092-096]might be antipathetic to her, enemies instead of friends, among whom

[092-097]her life would be one long misery; but it was not on that ground

[092-098]that Miss Petrie had recommended her to start for Rome as soon as

[092-099]Mr. Glascock had reached Florence. "There is no reason," she said to

[092-100]herself, "why I should not marry a man if I like him, even though

[092-101]he be a lord. And of him I should not be the least afraid. It's the

[092-102]women that I fear." And then she called to mind all that she had ever

[092-103]heard of English countesses and duchesses. She thought that she knew

[092-104]that they were generally cold and proud, and very little given to

[092-105]receive outsiders graciously within their ranks. Mr. Glascock had

[092-106]an aunt who was a Duchess, and a sister who would be a Countess.

[092-107]Caroline Spalding felt how her back would rise against these new

[092-108]relations, if it should come to pass that they should look unkindly

[092-109]upon her when she was taken to her own home;--how she would fight

[092-110]with them, giving them scorn for scorn; how unutterably miserable

[092-111]she would be; how she would long to be back among her own equals, in

[092-112]spite even of her love for her husband. "How grand a thing it is,"

[092-113]she said, "to be equal with those whom you love!" And yet she was to

[092-114]some extent allured by the social position of the man. She could

[092-115]perceive that he had a charm of manner which her countrymen lacked.

[092-116]He had read, perhaps, less than her uncle;--knew, perhaps, less than

[092-117]most of those men with whom she had been wont to associate in her

[092-118]own city life at home;--was not braver, or more virtuous, or more

[092-119]self-denying than they; but there was a softness and an ease in

[092-120]his manner which was palatable to her, and an absence of that too

[092-121]visible effort of the intellect which is so apt to mark and mar

[092-122]the conversation of Americans. She almost wished that she had been

[092-123]English, in order that the man's home and friends might have suited

[092-124]her. She was thinking of all this as she stood pretending to talk to

[092-125]an American lady, who was very eloquent on the delights of Florence.

[092-126]In the meantime Olivia and Mr. Glascock had moved away together, and

[092-127]Miss Petrie was left alone. This was no injury to Miss Petrie, as her

[092-128]mind at once set itself to work on a sonnet touching the frivolity

[092-129]of modern social gatherings; and when she complained afterwards to

[092-130]Caroline that it was the curse of their mode of life that no moment

[092-131]could be allowed for thought,--in which she referred specially to a

[092-132]few words that Mr. Gore had addressed to her at this moment of her

[092-133]meditations,--she was not wilfully a hypocrite. She was painfully

[092-134]turning her second set of rhymes, and really believed that she had

[092-135]been subjected to a hardship. In the meantime Olivia and Mr. Glascock

[092-136]were discussing her at a distance.

[092-137]"You were being put through your facings, Mr. Glascock," Olivia had

[092-138]said.

[092-139]"Well; yes; and your dear friend, Miss Petrie, is rather a stern

[092-140]examiner."

[092-141]"She is Carry's ally, not mine," said Olivia. Then she remembered

[092-142]that by saying this she might be doing her sister an injury. Mr.

[092-143]Glascock might object to such a bosom friend for his wife. "That is

[092-144]to say, of course we are all intimate with her, but just at this

[092-145]moment Carry is most in favour."

[092-146]"She is very clever, I am quite sure," said he.

[092-147]"Oh yes;--she's a genius. You must not doubt that on the peril of

[092-148]making every American in Italy your enemy."

[092-149]"She is a poet,--is she not?"

[092-150]"Mr. Glascock!"

[092-151]"Have I said anything wrong?" he asked.

[092-152]"Do you mean to look me in the face and tell me that you are not

[092-153]acquainted with her works,--that you don't know pages of them by

[092-154]heart, that you don't sleep with them under your pillow, don't travel

[092-155]about with them in your dressing-bag? I'm afraid we have mistaken

[092-156]you, Mr. Glascock."

[092-157]"Is it so great a sin?"

[092-158]"If you'll own up honestly, I'll tell you something,--in a whisper.

[092-159]You have not read a word of her poems?"

[092-160]"Not a word."

[092-161]"Neither have I. Isn't it horrible? But, perhaps, if I heard Tennyson

[092-162]talking every day, I shouldn't read Tennyson. Familiarity does breed

[092-163]contempt;--doesn't it? And then poor dear Wallachia is such a bore. I

[092-164]sometimes wonder, when English people are listening to her, whether

[092-165]they think that American girls generally talk like that."

[092-166]"Not all, perhaps, with that perfected eloquence."

[092-167]"I dare say you do," continued Olivia, craftily. "That is just the

[092-168]way in which people form their opinions about foreigners. Some

[092-169]specially self-asserting American speaks his mind louder than other

[092-170]people, and then you say that all Americans are self-asserting."

[092-171]"But you are a little that way given, Miss Spalding."

[092-172]"Because we are always called upon to answer accusations against

[092-173]us, expressed or unexpressed. We don't think ourselves a bit better

[092-174]than you; or, if the truth were known, half as good. We are always

[092-175]struggling to be as polished and easy as the French, or as sensible

[092-176]and dignified as the English; but when our defects are thrown in our

[092-177]teeth--"

[092-178]"Who throws them in your teeth, Miss Spalding?"

[092-179]"You look it,--all of you,--if you do not speak it out. You do assume

[092-180]a superiority, Mr. Glascock; and that we cannot endure."

[092-181]"I do not feel that I assume anything," said Mr. Glascock, meekly.

[092-182]"If three gentlemen be together, an Englishman, a Frenchman, and an

[092-183]American, is not the American obliged to be on his mettle to prove

[092-184]that he is somebody among the three? I admit that he is always

[092-185]claiming to be the first; but he does so only that he may not be too

[092-186]evidently the last. If you knew us, Mr. Glascock, you would find us

[092-187]to be very mild, and humble and nice, and good, and clever, and kind,

[092-188]and charitable, and beautiful,--in short, the finest people that have

[092-189]as yet been created on the broad face of God's smiling earth." These

[092-190]last words she pronounced with a nasal twang, and in a tone of voice

[092-191]which almost seemed to him to be a direct mimicry of the American

[092-192]Minister. The upshot of the conversation, however, was that the

[092-193]disgust against Americans which, to a certain degree, had been

[092-194]excited in Mr. Glascock's mind by the united efforts of Mr. Spalding

[092-195]and the poetess, had been almost entirely dispelled. From all of

[092-196]which the reader ought to understand that Miss Olivia Spalding was a

[092-197]very clever young woman.
