## Section 10

[010-001]"Indeed, indeed, I never have," said the husband.

[010-002]"That I can assure you I regard as a matter of course," said the

[010-003]wife.

[010-004]"But you know, Emily, the way in which the world talks."

[010-005]"The world! And do you regard the world, Louis?"

[010-006]"Lady Milborough, I believe, spoke to yourself."

[010-007]"Lady Milborough! No, she did not speak to me. She began to do so,

[010-008]but I was careful to silence her at once. From you, Louis, I am bound

[010-009]to hear whatever you may choose to say to me; but I will not hear

[010-010]from any other lips a single word that may be injurious to your

[010-011]honour." This she said very quietly, with much [[pride|dignity]], and he felt

[010-012]that he had better not answer her. She had given him the promise

[010-013]which he had demanded, and he began to fear that if he pushed the

[010-014]matter further she might go back even from that amount of submission.

[010-015]So he kissed her again, and had the boy brought into the room, and by

[010-016]the time that he went to dress for dinner he was able, at any rate,

[010-017]to seem to be well pleased.

[010-018]"Richard," he said to the servant, as soon as he was down-stairs,

[010-019]"when [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] calls again, say that your mistress is--not at

[010-020]home." He gave the order in the most indifferent tone of voice which

[010-021]he could assume; but as he gave it he felt thoroughly ashamed of it.

[010-022]Richard, who, with the other servants, had of course known that there

[010-023]had been a quarrel between his master and mistress for the last two

[010-024]days, no doubt understood all about it.

[010-025]While they were sitting at dinner on the next day, a Saturday, there

[010-026]came another note from Colonel Osborne. The servant brought it to

[010-027]his mistress, and she, when she had looked at it, put it down by her

[010-028]plate. Trevelyan knew immediately from whom the letter had come, and

[010-029]understood how impossible it was for his wife to give it up in the

[010-030]servant's presence. The letter lay there till the man was out of the

[010-031]room, and then she handed it to Nora. "Will you give that to Louis?"

[010-032]she said. "It comes from the man whom he supposes to be my lover."

[010-033]"Emily!" said he, jumping from his seat, "how can you allow words so

[010-034]horrible and so untrue to fall from your mouth?"

[010-035]"If it be not so, why am I to be placed in such a position as this?

[010-036]The servant knows, of course, from whom the letter comes, and sees

[010-037]that I have been forbidden to open it." Then the man returned to the

[010-038]room, and the remainder of the dinner passed off almost in silence.

[010-039]It was their custom when they dined without company to leave the

[010-040]dining-room together, but on this evening Trevelyan remained for a

[010-041]few minutes that he might read Colonel Osborne's letter. He waited,

[010-042][[reputation|standing]] on the rug with his face to the fire-place, till he was

[010-043]quite alone, and then he opened it. It ran as follows:--

[010-044]House of Commons, Saturday.

[010-045]DEAR EMILY,--

[010-046]Trevelyan, as he read this, cursed Colonel Osborne between his teeth.

[010-047]DEAR EMILY,

[010-048]I called this afternoon, but you were out. I am afraid you

[010-049]will be disappointed by what I have to tell you, but you

[010-050]should rather be glad of it. They say at the C. O. that

[010-051]Sir Marmaduke would not receive their letter if sent

[010-052]now till the middle of June, and that he could not be

[010-053]in [[london|London]], let him do what he would, till the end of

[010-054]July. They hope to have the session over by that time,

[010-055]and therefore the committee is to be put off till next

[010-056]session. They mean to have Lord Bowles home from Canada,

[010-057]and they think that Bowles would like to be here in the

[010-058]winter. Sir Marmaduke will be summoned for February next,

[010-059]and will of course stretch his stay over the hot months.

[010-060]All this will, on the whole, be for the best. Lady Rowley

[010-061]could hardly have packed up her things and come away at a

[010-062]day's notice, whatever your father might have done. I'll

[010-063]call to-morrow at luncheon time.

[010-064]Yours always,

[010-065]F. O.

[010-066]There was nothing objectionable in this letter,--excepting always the

[010-067]"Dear Emily,"--nothing which it was not imperative on Colonel Osborne

[010-068]to communicate to the person to whom it was addressed. Trevelyan must

[010-069]now go up-stairs and tell the contents of the letter to his wife.

[010-070]But he felt that he had created for himself a terrible trouble. He

[010-071]must tell his wife what was in the letter, but the very telling of

[010-072]it would be a renewing of the soreness of his wound. And then what

[010-073]was to be done in reference to the threatened visit for the Sunday

[010-074]morning? Trevelyan knew very well that were his wife denied at

[010-075]that hour, Colonel Osborne would understand the whole matter. He

[010-076]had doubtless in his anger intended that Colonel Osborne should

[010-077]understand the whole matter; but he was calmer now than he had been

[010-078]then, and almost wished that the command given by him had not been so

[010-079]definite and imperious. He remained with his arm on the mantel-piece,

[010-080]thinking of it, for some ten minutes, and then went up into the

[010-081]drawing-room. "Emily," he said, walking up to the table at which she

[010-082]was sitting, "you had better read that letter."

[010-083]"I would so much rather not," she replied haughtily.

[010-084]"Then Nora can read it. It concerns you both equally."

[010-085]Nora, with hesitating hand, took the letter and read it. "They are

[010-086]not to come after all," said she, "till next February."

[010-087]"And why not?" asked Mrs. Trevelyan.

[010-088]"Something about the session. I don't quite understand."

[010-089]"Lord Bowles is to come from Canada," said Louis, "and they think he

[010-090]would prefer being here in the winter. I dare say he would."

[010-091]"But what has that to do with papa?"

[010-092]"I suppose they must both be here together," said Nora.

[010-093]"I call that very hard indeed," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[010-094]"I can't agree with you there," said her husband. "His coming at all

[010-095]is so much of a favour that it is almost a job."

[010-096]"I don't see that it is a job at all," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "Somebody

[010-097]is wanted, and nobody can know more of the service than papa does.

[010-098]But as the other man is a lord, I suppose papa must give way. Does he

[010-099]say anything about mamma, Nora?"

[010-100]"You had better read the letter yourself," said Trevelyan, who was

[010-101]desirous that his wife should know of the threatened visit.

[010-102]"No, Louis, I shall not do that. You must not blow hot and cold too.

[010-103]Till the other day I should have thought that Colonel Osborne's

[010-104]letters were as innocent as an old newspaper. As you have supposed

[010-105]them to be poisoned I will have nothing to do with them."

[010-106]This speech made him very angry. It seemed that his wife, who

[010-107]had yielded to him, was determined to take out the value of her

[010-108]submission in the most disagreeable words which she could utter. Nora

[010-109]now closed the letter and handed it back to her brother-in-law. He

[010-110]laid it down on the table beside him, and sat for a while with his

[010-111]eyes fixed upon his book. At last he spoke again. "Colonel Osborne

[010-112]says that he will call to-morrow at luncheon time. You can admit him,

[010-113]if you please, and thank him for the trouble he has taken in this

[010-114]matter."

[010-115]"I shall not remain in the room if he be admitted," said Mrs.

[010-116]Trevelyan.

[010-117]There was silence again for some minutes, and the cloud upon

[010-118]Trevelyan's brow became blacker than before. Then he rose from his

[010-119]chair and walked round to the sofa on which his wife was sitting. "I

[010-120]presume," said he, "that your wishes and mine in this matter must be

[010-121]the same."

[010-122]"I cannot tell what your wishes are," she replied. "I never was more

[010-123]in the dark on any subject in my life. My wishes at present are

[010-124]confined to a desire to save you as far as may be possible from the

[010-125]shame which must be attached to your own suspicions."

[010-126]"I have never had any suspicions."

[010-127]"A husband without suspicions does not intercept his wife's letters.

[010-128]A husband without suspicions does not call in the aid of his servants

[010-129]to guard his wife. A husband without suspicions--"

[010-130]"Emily," exclaimed [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]], "how can you say such things,--on

[010-131]purpose to provoke him?"

[010-132]"Yes; on purpose to provoke me," said Trevelyan.

[010-133]"And have I not been provoked? Have I not been injured? You say now

[010-134]that you have not suspected me, and yet in what condition do I find

[010-135]myself? Because an old woman has chosen to talk scandal about me, I

[010-136]am placed in a position in my own house which is disgraceful to you

[010-137]and insupportable to myself. This man has been in the habit of coming

[010-138]here on Sundays, and will, of course, know that we are at home. You

[010-139]must manage it as you please. If you choose to receive him, I will go

[010-140]up-stairs."

[010-141]"Why can't you let him come in and go away, just as usual?" said

[010-142]Nora.

[010-143]"Because Louis has made me promise that I will never willingly be

[010-144]in his company again," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "I would have given the

[010-145]world to avoid a promise so disgraceful to me; but it was exacted,

[010-146]and it shall be kept." Having so spoken, she swept out of the room,

[010-147]and went up-stairs to the nursery. Trevelyan sat for an hour with his

[010-148]book before him, reading or pretending to read, but his wife did not

[010-149]come down-stairs. Then Nora went up to her, and he descended to his

[010-150]solitude below. So far he had hardly gained much by the enforced

[010-151]obedience of his wife.

[010-152]On the next morning the three went to church together, and as they

[010-153]were walking home Trevelyan's heart was filled with returning

[010-154]gentleness towards his wife. He could not bear to be at wrath with

[010-155]her after the church service which they had just heard together.

[010-156]But he was softer-hearted than was she, and knowing this, was

[010-157]almost afraid to say anything that would again bring forth from her

[010-158]expressions of scorn. As soon as they were alone within the house he

[010-159]took her by the hand and led her apart. "Let all this be," said he,

[010-160]"as though it had never been."

[010-161]"That will hardly be possible, Louis," she answered. "I cannot forget

[010-162]that I have been--cautioned."

[010-163]"But cannot you bring yourself to believe that I have meant it all

[010-164]for your good?"

[010-165]"I have never doubted it, Louis;--never for a moment. But it has hurt

[010-166]me to find that you should think that such caution was needed for my

[010-167]good."

[010-168]It was almost on his tongue to beg her pardon, to acknowledge that

[010-169]he had made a mistake, and to implore her to forget that he had ever

[010-170]made an objection to Colonel Osborne's visit. He remembered at this

[010-171]moment the painful odiousness of that "Dear Emily;" but he had to

[010-172]reconcile himself even to that, telling himself that, after all,

[010-173]Colonel Osborne was an old man,--a man older even than his wife's

[010-174]father. If she would only have met him with gentleness, he would have

[010-175]withdrawn his command, and have acknowledged that he had been wrong.

[010-176]But she was hard, dignified, obedient, and resentful. "It will, I

[010-177]think," he said, "be better for both of us that he should be asked in

[010-178]to lunch to-day."

[010-179]"You must judge of that," said Emily. "Perhaps, upon the whole, it

[010-180]will be best. I can only say that I will not be present. I will lunch

[010-181]up-stairs with baby, and you can make what excuse for me you please."

[010-182]This was all very bad, but it was in this way that things were

[010-183]allowed to arrange themselves. Richard was told that Colonel Osborne

[010-184]was coming to lunch, and when he came something was muttered to him

[010-185]about Mrs. Trevelyan being not quite well. It was Nora who told the

[010-186]innocent fib, and though she did not tell it well, she did her very

[010-187]best. She felt that her brother-in-law was very wretched, and she was

[010-188]most anxious to relieve him. Colonel Osborne did not stay long, and

[010-189]then Nora went up-stairs to her sister.
