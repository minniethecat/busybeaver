## Section 118

[118-001]"But I ain't,--the least in the world," said Brooke.

[118-002]Then Martha took up her basket, and went on to the cottage, which had

[118-003]been close in sight during their conversation in the road. She felt

[118-004]angry with Dorothy. In such matters a woman is always angry with the

[118-005]woman,--who has probably been quite passive, and rarely with the man,

[118-006]who is ever the real transgressor. Having a man down after her at

[118-007][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]! It had never struck Martha as very horrible that

[118-008]Brooke Burgess should fall in love with Dorothy in the city;--but

[118-009]this meeting, in the remoteness of the country, out of sight even of

[118-010]the village, was almost indecent; and all, too, with Miss Stanbury's

[118-011]will just, as one might say, on the balance! Dorothy ought to have

[118-012]buried herself rather than have allowed Brooke to see her at Nuncombe

[118-013]Putney; and Dorothy's mother and Priscilla must be worse. She trudged

[118-014]on, however, with her lamb, and soon found herself in the presence of

[118-015]the three ladies.

[118-016]"What,--Martha!" said Dorothy.

[118-017]"Yes, miss,--here I am. I'd have been here half-an-hour ago amost, if

[118-018]I hadn't been stopped on the road."

[118-019]"And who stopped you?" asked Priscilla.

[118-020]"Why,--Mr. Brooke, of course."

[118-021]"And what did Mr. Brooke say to you?" asked Dorothy.

[118-022]Martha perceived at once that Dorothy was quite radiant. She told her

[118-023]mistress that she had never seen Miss Dorothy look half so comely

[118-024]before. "Laws, ma'am, she brightened up and speckled about, till it

[118-025]did your heart good to see her in spite of all." But this was some

[118-026]time afterwards.

[118-027]"He didn't say very much," replied Martha, gravely.

[118-028]"But I've got very much to tell you," continued Dorothy. "I'm engaged

[118-029]to be married to Mr. Brooke, and you must congratulate me. It is

[118-030]settled now, and mamma and my sister know all about it."

[118-031]Martha, when she was thus asked directly for congratulation, hardly

[118-032]knew at once how to express herself. Being fully aware of Miss

[118-033]Stanbury's objection to the marriage, she could not venture to

[118-034]express her approbation of it. It was very improper, in Martha's

[118-035]mind, that any young woman should have a follower, when the "missus"

[118-036]didn't approve of it. She understood well enough that, in that matter

[118-037]of followers, privileges are allowed to young ladies which are not

[118-038]accorded to maid servants. A young lady may do things,--have young

[118-039]men to walk and talk with them, to dance with them and embrace them,

[118-040]and perhaps even more than this,--when for half so much a young woman

[118-041]would be turned into the streets without a [[honour|character]]. Martha knew

[118-042]all this, and knew also that Miss Dorothy, though her mother lived

[118-043]in a very little cottage, was not altogether debarred, in the matter

[118-044]of followers, from the privileges of a lady. But yet Miss Dorothy's

[118-045]position was so very peculiar! Look at that will,--or, rather, at

[118-046]that embryo will, which might be made any day, which now probably

[118-047]would be made, and which might affect them both so terribly! People

[118-048]who have not got money should not fly in the face of those who have.

[118-049]Such at least was Martha's opinion very strongly. How could she

[118-050]congratulate Miss Dorothy under the existing circumstances? "I do

[118-051]hope you will be happy, miss;--that you knows," said Martha, in her

[118-052]difficulty. "And now, ma'am;--miss, I mean," she added, correcting

[118-053]herself, in obedience to Miss Stanbury's direct orders about the

[118-054]present,--"missus has just sent me over with a bit of lamb, and a

[118-055]letter as is here in the basket, and to ask how you is,--and the

[118-056]other ladies."

[118-057]"We are very much obliged," said Mrs. Stanbury, who had not

[118-058]understood the point of Martha's speech.

[118-059]"My sister is, I'm sure," said Priscilla, who had understood it.

[118-060]Dorothy had taken the letter, and had gone aside with it, and was

[118-061]reading it very carefully. It touched her nearly, and there had come

[118-062]tears into both her eyes, as she dwelt upon it. There was something

[118-063]in her aunt's allusion to the condition of unmarried women which came

[118-064]home to her especially. She knew her aunt's past history, and now she

[118-065]knew, or hoped that she knew, something of her own future destiny.

[118-066]Her aunt was desolate, whereas upon her the world smiled most

[118-067]benignly. Brooke had just informed her that he intended to make

[118-068]her his wife as speedily as possible,--with her aunt's consent if

[118-069]possible, but if not, then without it. He had ridiculed the idea of

[118-070]his being stopped by Miss Stanbury's threats, and had said all this

[118-071]in such fashion that even Priscilla herself had only listened and

[118-072]obeyed. He had spoken not a word of his own [[money|income]], and none of them

[118-073]had dreamed even of asking him a question. He had been as a god in

[118-074]the little cottage, and all of them had been ready to fall down

[118-075]and worship him. Mrs. Stanbury had not known how to treat him

[118-076]with sufficient deference, and, at the same time, with sufficient

[118-077]affection. He had kissed them all round, and Priscilla had felt an

[118-078]elation which was hardly intelligible to herself. Dorothy, who was so

[118-079]much honoured, had come to enjoy a status in her mother's estimation

[118-080]very different from that which she had previously possessed, and had

[118-081]grown to be quite beautiful in her mother's eyes.

[118-082]There was once a family of three ancient maiden ladies, much

[118-083]respected and loved in the town in which they lived. Their manners

[118-084]of life were well known among their friends, and excited no surprise;

[118-085]but a stranger to the locality once asked of the elder why Miss

[118-086]Matilda, the younger, always went first out of the room? "Matilda

[118-087]once had an offer of marriage," said the dear simple old lady, who

[118-088]had never been so graced, and who felt that such an episode in life

[118-089]was quite sufficient to bestow brevet [[social-class|rank]]. It was believed by Mrs.

[118-090]Stanbury that Dorothy's honours would be carried further than those

[118-091]of Miss Matilda, but there was much of the same feeling in the bosom

[118-092]of the mother towards the fortunate daughter, who, in the eyes of a

[118-093]man, had seemed goodly enough to be his wife.

[118-094]With this swelling happiness round her heart, Dorothy read her aunt's

[118-095]letter, and was infinitely softened. "I had gotten somehow to love

[118-096]to see your pretty face." Dorothy had thought little enough of her

[118-097]own beauty, but she liked being told by her aunt that her face had

[118-098]been found to be pretty. "I am very desolate and solitary here," her

[118-099]aunt said; and then had come those words about the state of maiden

[118-100]women;--and then those other words, about women's duties, and her

[118-101]aunt's prayer on her behalf. "Dear Dorothy, be not such an one."

[118-102]She held the letter to her lips and to her bosom, and could hardly

[118-103]continue its perusal because of her tears. Such prayers from the aged

[118-104]addressed to the young are generally held in light esteem, but this

[118-105]adjuration was valued by the girl to whom it was addressed. She put

[118-106]together the invitation,--or rather the permission accorded to her,

[118-107]to make a visit to [[exeter|Exeter]],--and the intimation in the postscript

[118-108]that Martha knew her mistress's mind; and then she returned to the

[118-109]sitting-room, in which Martha was still seated with her mother, and

[118-110]took the old servant apart. "Martha," she said, "is my aunt happy

[118-111]now?"

[118-112]"Well,--miss."

[118-113]"She is strong again; is she not?"

[118-114]"Sir Peter says she is getting well; and Mr. Martin--; but Mr. Martin

[118-115]isn't much account."

[118-116]"She eats and drinks again?"

[118-117]"Pretty well;--not as it used to be, you know, miss. I tell her she

[118-118]ought to go somewheres,--but she don't like moving nohow. She never

[118-119]did. I tell her if she'd go to Dawlish,--just for a week. But she

[118-120]don't think there's a bed fit to sleep on, nowhere, except just her

[118-121]own."

[118-122]"She would go if Sir Peter told her."

[118-123]"She says that these movings are newfangled fashions, and that the

[118-124]air didn't use to want changing for folk when she was young. I heard

[118-125]her tell Sir Peter herself, that if she couldn't live at Exeter,

[118-126]she would die there. She won't go nowheres, Miss Dorothy. She ain't

[118-127]careful to live."

[118-128]"Tell me something, Martha; will you?"

[118-129]"What is it, Miss Dorothy?"

[118-130]"Be a dear good woman now, and tell me true. Would she be better if I

[118-131]were with her?"

[118-132]"She don't like being alone, miss. I don't know nobody as does."

[118-133]"But now, about Mr. Brooke, you know."

[118-134]"Yes, Mr. Brooke! That's it."

[118-135]"Of course, Martha, I love him better than anything in all the world.

[118-136]I can't tell you how it was, but I think I loved him the very first

[118-137]moment I saw him."

[118-138]"Dear, dear, dear!"

[118-139]"I couldn't help it, Martha;--but it's no good talking about it, for

[118-140]of course I shan't try to help it now. Only this,--that I would do

[118-141]anything in the world for my aunt,--except that."

[118-142]"But she don't like it, Miss Dorothy. That is the truth, you know."

[118-143]"It can't be helped now, Martha; and of course she'll be told at

[118-144]once. Shall I go and tell her? I'd go to-day if you think she would

[118-145]like it."

[118-146]"And Mr. Brooke?"

[118-147]"He is to go to-morrow."

[118-148]"And will you leave him here?"

[118-149]"Why not? Nobody will hurt him. I don't mind a bit about having him

[118-150]with me now. But I can tell you this. When he went away from us once

[118-151]it made me very unhappy. Would Aunt Stanbury be glad to see me,

[118-152]Martha?"

[118-153]Martha's reserve was at last broken down, and she expressed herself

[118-154]in strong language. There was nothing on earth her mistress wanted so

[118-155]much as to have her favourite niece back again. Martha acknowledged

[118-156]that there were great difficulties about Brooke Burgess, and she did

[118-157]not see her way clearly through them. Dorothy declared her purpose of

[118-158]telling her aunt boldly,--at once. Martha shook her head, admiring

[118-159]the honesty and courage, but doubting the result. She understood

[118-160]better than did any one else the peculiarity of mind which made her

[118-161]mistress specially anxious that none of the Stanbury family should

[118-162]enjoy any portion of the Burgess money, beyond that which she herself

[118-163]had saved out of the income. There had been moments in which Martha

[118-164]had hoped that this prejudice might be overcome in favour of Hugh;

[118-165]but it had become stronger as the old woman grew to be older and

[118-166]more feeble,--and it was believed now to be settled as Fate. "She'd

[118-167]sooner give it all to old Barty over the way," Martha had once said,

[118-168]"than let it go to her own kith and kin. And if she do hate any human

[118-169]creature, she do hate Barty Burgess." She assented, however, to

[118-170]Dorothy's proposal; and, though Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla were

[118-171]astounded by the precipitancy of the measure they did not attempt to

[118-172]oppose it.

[118-173]"And what am I to do?" said Brooke, when he was told.

[118-174]"You'll come to-morrow, of course," said Dorothy.

[118-175]"But it may be that the two of us together will be too many for the

[118-176]dear old lunatic."

[118-177]"You shan't call her a lunatic, Brooke. She isn't so much a lunatic

[118-178]as you are, to run counter to her, and disobey her, and all that kind

[118-179]of thing."

[118-180]"And how about yourself?"

[118-181]"How can I help it, Brooke? It is you that say it must be so."

[118-182]"Of course it must. Who is to be stayed from doing what is reasonable

[118-183]because an old woman has a bee on her bonnet. I don't believe in

[118-184]people's wills."

[118-185]"She can do what she likes about it, Brooke."

[118-186]"Of course she can, and of course she will. What I mean is that it

[118-187]never pays to do this or that because somebody may alter his will, or

[118-188]may make a will, or may not make a will. You become a slave for life,

[118-189]and then your dead tyrant leaves you a mourning-ring, and grins at

[118-190]you out of his grave. All the same she'll kick up a row, I fancy, and

[118-191]you'll have to bear the worst of it."
