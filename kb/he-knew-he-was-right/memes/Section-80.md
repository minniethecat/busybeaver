## Section 80

[080-001]"My niece doesn't care about it two straws," said Miss Stanbury. "It

[080-002]is I that care. And I care very much. The things that have been said

[080-003]have been altogether false."

[080-004]"How false, Miss Stanbury?" asked Camilla.

[080-005]"Altogether false,--as false as they can be."

[080-006]"Mr. Gibson must know his own mind," said Camilla.

[080-007]"My dear, there's a little disappointment," said Miss French, "and it

[080-008]don't signify."

[080-009]"There's no disappointment at all," said Miss Stanbury, "and it does

[080-010]signify very much. Now that I've begun, I'll go to the bottom of it.

[080-011]If you say that Mr. Gibson told you to make these statements, I'll go

[080-012]to Mr. Gibson. I'll have it out somehow."

[080-013]"You may have what you like out for us, Miss Stanbury," said Camilla.

[080-014]"I don't believe Mr. Gibson said anything of the kind."

[080-015]"That's civil," said Camilla.

[080-016]"But why shouldn't he?" asked Arabella.

[080-017]"There were the reports, you know," said Mrs. French.

[080-018]"And why shouldn't he deny them when there wasn't a word of truth

[080-019]in them?" continued Camilla. "For my part I think the [[social-class|gentleman]] is

[080-020]bound for the lady's sake to declare that there's nothing in it when

[080-021]there is nothing in it." This was more than Miss Stanbury could bear.

[080-022]Hitherto the enemy had seemed to have the best of it. Camilla was

[080-023]firing broadside after broadside, as though she was assured of

[080-024]victory. Even Mrs. French was becoming courageous; and Arabella was

[080-025]forgetting the place where her chignon ought to have been. "I really

[080-026]do not know what else there is for me to say," remarked Camilla, with

[080-027]a toss of her head, and an air of impudence that almost drove poor

[080-028]Miss Stanbury frantic.

[080-029]It was on her tongue to declare the whole truth, but she refrained.

[080-030]She had schooled herself on this subject vigorously. She would not

[080-031]betray Mr. Gibson. Had she known all the truth,--or had she believed

[080-032]Camilla French's version of the story,--there would have been no

[080-033]betrayal. But looking at the matter with such [[truth|knowledge]] as she had

[080-034]at present, she did not even yet feel herself justified in declaring

[080-035]that Mr. Gibson had offered his hand to her niece, and had been

[080-036]refused. She was, however, sorely tempted. "Very well, ladies," she

[080-037]said. "I shall now see Mr. Gibson, and ask him whether he did give

[080-038]you authority to make such statements as you have been spreading

[080-039]abroad everywhere." Then the door of the room was opened, and in a

[080-040]moment Mr. Gibson was among them. He was true to his promise, and had

[080-041]come to see Arabella with her altered head-dress;--but he had come

[080-042]at this hour thinking that escape in the morning would be easier and

[080-043]quicker than it might have been in the evening. His mind had been

[080-044]full of Arabella and her head-dress even up to the moment of his

[080-045]knocking at the door; but all that was driven out of his brain at

[080-046]once when he saw Miss Stanbury.

[080-047]"Here is Mr. Gibson himself," said Mrs. French.

[080-048]"How do you do, Mr. Gibson?" said Miss Stanbury, with a very stately

[080-049]courtesy. They had never met since the day on which he had been, as

[080-050]he stated, turned out of Miss Stanbury's house. He now bowed to her;

[080-051]but there was no friendly greeting, and the Frenches were able to

[080-052]congratulate themselves on the apparent loyalty to themselves of

[080-053]the gentleman who stood among them. "I have come here, Mr. Gibson,"

[080-054]continued Miss Stanbury, "to put a small matter right in which you

[080-055]are concerned."

[080-056]"It seems to me to be the most insignificant thing in the world,"

[080-057]said Camilla.

[080-058]"Very likely," said Miss Stanbury. "But it is not insignificant

[080-059]to me. Miss Camilla French has asserted publicly that you have

[080-060]authorised her to make a statement about my niece Dorothy."

[080-061]Mr. Gibson looked into Camilla's face doubtingly, inquisitively,

[080-062]almost piteously. "You had better let her go on," said Camilla. "She

[080-063]will make a great many mistakes, no doubt, but you had better let her

[080-064]go on to the end."

[080-065]"I have made no mistake as yet, Miss Camilla. She so asserted, Mr.

[080-066]Gibson, in the hearing of a friend of mine, and she repeated the

[080-067]assertion here in this room to me just before you came in. She says

[080-068]that you have authorised her to declare that--that--that,--I had

[080-069]better speak it out plainly at once."

[080-070]"Much better," said Camilla.

[080-071]"That you never entertained an idea of offering your hand to my

[080-072]niece." Miss Stanbury paused, and Mr. Gibson's jaw fell visibly. But

[080-073]he was not expected to speak as yet; and Miss Stanbury continued her

[080-074]accusation. "Beyond that, I don't want to mention my niece's name, if

[080-075]it can be avoided."

[080-076]"But it can't be avoided," said Camilla.

[080-077]"If you please, I will continue. Mr. Gibson will understand me.

[080-078]I will not, if I can help it, mention my niece's name again, Mr.

[080-079]Gibson. But I still have that confidence in you that I do not think

[080-080]that you would have made such a statement in reference to yourself

[080-081]and any young lady,--unless it were some young lady who had

[080-082]absolutely thrown herself at your head." And in saying this she

[080-083]paused, and looked very hard at Camilla.

[080-084]"That's just what Dorothy Stanbury has been doing," said Camilla.

[080-085]"She has been doing nothing of the kind, and you know she hasn't,"

[080-086]said Miss Stanbury, raising her arm as though she were going to

[080-087]strike her opponent. "But I am quite sure, Mr. Gibson, that you

[080-088]never could have authorised these young ladies to make such an

[080-089]assertion publicly on your behalf. Whatever there may have been of

[080-090]misunderstanding between you and me, I can't believe that of you."

[080-091]Then she paused for a reply. "If you will be good enough to set us

[080-092]right on that point, I shall be obliged to you."

[080-093]Mr. Gibson's position was one of great discomfort. He had given no

[080-094]authority to any one to make such a statement. He had said nothing

[080-095]about Dorothy Stanbury to Camilla; but he had told Arabella, when

[080-096]hard pressed by that lady, that he did not mean to propose to

[080-097]Dorothy. He could not satisfy Miss Stanbury because he feared

[080-098]Arabella. He could not satisfy the Frenches because he feared Miss

[080-099]Stanbury. "I really do not think," said he, "that we ought to talk

[080-100]about a young lady in this way."

[080-101]"That's my opinion, too," said Camilla; "but Miss Stanbury will."

[080-102]"Exactly so. Miss Stanbury will," said that lady. "Mr. Gibson,

[080-103]I insist upon it, that you tell me whether you did give any such

[080-104]authority to Miss Camilla French, or to Miss French."

[080-105]"I wouldn't answer her, if I were you," said Camilla.

[080-106]"I really don't think this can do any good," said Mrs. French.

[080-107]"And it is so very harassing to our nerves," said Arabella.

[080-108]"Nerves! Pooh!" exclaimed Miss Stanbury. "Now, Mr. Gibson, I am

[080-109]waiting for an answer."

[080-110]"My dear Miss Stanbury, I really think it better,--the situation

[080-111]is so peculiar, and, upon my word, I hardly know how not to give

[080-112]offence, which I wouldn't do for the world."

[080-113]"Do you mean to tell me that you won't answer my question?" demanded

[080-114]Miss Stanbury.

[080-115]"I really think that I had better hold my tongue," pleaded Mr.

[080-116]Gibson.

[080-117]"You are quite right, Mr. Gibson," said Camilla.

[080-118]"Indeed, it is wisest," said Mrs. French.

[080-119]"I don't see what else he can do," said Arabella.

[080-120]Then was Miss Stanbury driven altogether beyond her powers of

[080-121]endurance. "If that be so," said she, "I must speak out, though I

[080-122]should have preferred to hold my tongue. Mr. Gibson did offer to my

[080-123]niece the week before last,--twice, and was refused by her. My niece,

[080-124]Dorothy, took it into her head that she did not like him; and, upon

[080-125]my word, I think she was right. We should have said nothing about

[080-126]this,--not a word; but when these false assertions are made on Mr.

[080-127]Gibson's alleged authority, and Mr. Gibson won't deny it, I must

[080-128]tell the truth." Then there was silence among them for a few seconds,

[080-129]and Mr. Gibson struggled hard, but vainly, to clothe his face in

[080-130]a pleasant smile. "Mr. Gibson, is that true?" said Miss Stanbury.

[080-131]But Mr. Gibson made no reply. "It is as true as heaven," said Miss

[080-132]Stanbury, striking her hand upon the table. "And now you had better,

[080-133]all of you, hold your tongues about my niece, and she will hold her

[080-134]tongue about you. And as for Mr. Gibson,--anybody who wants him

[080-135]after this is welcome to him for us. Good-morning, Mrs. French;

[080-136]good-morning, young ladies." And so she stalked out of the room, and

[080-137]out of the house, and walked back to her house in the Close.

[080-138]"Mamma," said Arabella, as soon as the enemy was gone, "I have got

[080-139]such a headache that I think I will go up-stairs."

[080-140]"And I will go with you, dear," said Camilla.

[080-141]Mr. Gibson, before he left the house, confided his secret to the

[080-142]maternal ears of Mrs. French. He certainly had been allured into

[080-143]making an offer to Dorothy Stanbury, but was ready to atone for

[080-144]this crime by marrying her daughter,--Camilla,--as soon as might

[080-145]be convenient. He was certainly driven to make this declaration by

[080-146]intense cowardice,--not to excuse himself, for in that there could

[080-147]be no excuse;--but how else should he dare to suggest that he might

[080-148]as well leave the house? "Shall I tell the dear girl?" asked Mrs.

[080-149]French. But Mr. Gibson requested a fortnight, in which to consider

[080-150]how the proposition had best be made.

[080-151]CHAPTER XLIX.

[080-152]MR. BROOKE BURGESS AFTER SUPPER.

[080-153]Brooke Burgess was a clerk in the office of the Ecclesiastical

[080-154]Commissioners in [[london|London]], and as such had to do with things very

[080-155]solemn, grave, and almost melancholy. He had to deal with the rents

[080-156]of episcopal properties, to correspond with clerical claimants,

[080-157]and to be at home with the circumstances of underpaid vicars and

[080-158]perpetual curates with much less than L300 a-year; but yet he was

[080-159]as jolly and pleasant at his desk as though he were busied about

[080-160]the collection of the malt tax, or wrote his letters to admirals

[080-161]and captains instead of to deans and prebendaries. Brooke Burgess

[080-162]had risen to be a senior clerk, and was held in some respect in his

[080-163]office; but it was not perhaps for the amount of work he did, nor yet

[080-164]on account of the gravity of his demeanour, nor for the brilliancy of

[080-165]his intellect. But if not clever, he was sensible; though he was not

[080-166]a dragon of official virtue, he had a conscience;--and he possessed

[080-167]those small but most valuable gifts by which a man becomes popular

[080-168]among men. And thus it had come to pass in all those battles as to

[080-169]competitive merit which had taken place in his as in other public

[080-170]offices, that no one had ever dreamed of putting a junior over

[080-171]the head of Brooke Burgess. He was tractable, easy, pleasant, and

[080-172]therefore deservedly successful. All his brother clerks called him

[080-173]Brooke,--except the young lads who, for the first year or two of

[080-174]their service, still denominated him Mr. Burgess.

[080-175]"Brooke," said one of his juniors, coming into his room and [[reputation|standing]]

[080-176]before the fireplace with a cigar in his mouth, "have you heard who

[080-177]is to be the new Commissioner?"

[080-178]"Colenso, to be sure," said Brooke.

[080-179]"What a lark that would be. And I don't see why he shouldn't. But it

[080-180]isn't Colenso. The name has just come down."

[080-181]"And who is it?"

[080-182]"Old Proudie, from Barchester."

[080-183]"Why, we had him here years ago, and he resigned."

[080-184]"But he's to come on again now for a spell. It always seems to me

[080-185]that the bishops ain't a bit of use here. They only get blown up, and

[080-186]snubbed, and shoved into corners by the others."

[080-187]"You young reprobate,--to talk of shoving an archbishop into a

[080-188]corner."
