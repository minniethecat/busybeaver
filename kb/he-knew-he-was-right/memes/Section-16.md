## Section 16

[016-001]"What is the matter with him?" [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] had asked.

[016-002]"It is impossible to say what is the matter with him," Mrs. Trevelyan

[016-003]had replied. After that she had at once gone up-stairs to her child,

[016-004]telling herself that she was doing all that the strictest [[social-custom|propriety]]

[016-005]could require in leaving the man's society as soon as her husband

[016-006]was gone. Then there was an awkward minute or two between Nora and

[016-007]Colonel Osborne, and he took his leave.

[016-008]Stanbury at last promised that he would see Trevelyan, repeating,

[016-009]however, very frequently that often-used assertion, that no task

[016-010]is so hopeless as that of interfering between a man and his wife.

[016-011]Nevertheless he promised, and undertook to look for Trevelyan at

[016-012]the Acrobats on that afternoon. At last he got a moment in which

[016-013]to produce the letter from his sister, and was able to turn the

[016-014]conversation for a few minutes to his own affairs. Dorothy's letter

[016-015]was read and discussed by both the ladies with much zeal. "It is

[016-016]quite a strange world to me," said Dorothy, "but I am beginning to

[016-017]find myself more at my ease than I was at first. Aunt Stanbury is

[016-018]very good-natured, and when I know what she wants, I think I shall be

[016-019]able to please her. What you said of her disposition is not so bad to

[016-020]me, as of course a girl in my position does not expect to have her

[016-021]own way."

[016-022]"Why shouldn't she have her share of her own way as well as anybody

[016-023]else?" said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[016-024]"Poor Dorothy would never want to have her own way," said Hugh.

[016-025]"She ought to want it," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[016-026]"She has spirit enough to turn if she's trodden on," said Hugh.

[016-027]"That's more than what most women have," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[016-028]Then he went on with the letter. "She is very generous, and has given

[016-029]me L6 5_s._ in advance of my allowance. When I said I would send part

[016-030]of it home to mamma, she seemed to be angry, and said that she wanted

[016-031]me always to look nice about my clothes. She told me afterwards to do

[016-032]as I pleased, and that I might try my own way for the first quarter.

[016-033]So I was frightened, and only sent thirty shillings. We went out

[016-034]the other evening to drink tea with Mrs. MacHugh, an old lady whose

[016-035]husband was once dean. I had to go, and it was all very nice. There

[016-036]were a great many clergymen there, but many of them were young men."

[016-037]"Poor Dorothy," exclaimed Nora. "One of them was the minor canon who

[016-038]chants the service every morning. He is a bachelor--" "Then there is

[016-039]a hope for her," said Nora--"and he always talks a little as though

[016-040]he were singing the Litany." "That's very bad," said Nora; "fancy

[016-041]having a husband to sing the Litany to you always." "Better that,

[016-042]perhaps, than having him always singing something else," said Mrs.

[016-043]Trevelyan.

[016-044]It was decided between them that Dorothy's state might on the whole

[016-045]be considered as flourishing, but that Hugh was bound as a brother

[016-046]to go down to [[exeter|Exeter]] and look after her. He explained, however, that

[016-047]he was expressly debarred from calling on his sister, even between

[016-048]the hours of half-past nine and half-past twelve on Wednesday

[016-049]mornings, and that he could not see her at all unless he did so

[016-050]surreptitiously.

[016-051]"If I were you I would see my sister in spite of all the old viragos

[016-052]in Exeter," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "I have no idea of anybody taking so

[016-053]much upon themselves."

[016-054]"You must remember, Mrs. Trevelyan, that she has taken upon herself

[016-055]much also in the way of kindness, in doing what perhaps I ought to

[016-056]call charity. I wonder what I should have been doing now if it were

[016-057]not for my Aunt Stanbury."

[016-058]He took his leave, and went at once from Curzon Street to Trevelyan's

[016-059]club, and found that Trevelyan had not been there as yet. In another

[016-060]hour he called again, and was about to give it up, when he met the

[016-061]man whom he was seeking on the steps.

[016-062]"I was looking for you," he said.

[016-063]"Well, here I am."

[016-064]It was impossible not to see in the look of Trevelyan's face, and not

[016-065]to hear in the tone of his voice, that he was, at the moment, in an

[016-066]angry and unhappy frame of mind. He did not move as though he were

[016-067]willing to accompany his friend, and seemed almost to know beforehand

[016-068]that the approaching interview was to be an unpleasant one.

[016-069]"I want to speak to you, and perhaps you wouldn't mind taking a turn

[016-070]with me," said Stanbury.

[016-071]But Trevelyan objected to this, and led the way into the club

[016-072]waiting-room. A club waiting-room is always a gloomy, unpromising

[016-073]place for a confidential conversation, and so Stanbury felt it to be

[016-074]on the present occasion. But he had no alternative. There they were

[016-075]together, and he must do as he had promised. Trevelyan kept on his

[016-076]hat and did not sit down, and looked very gloomy. Stanbury having

[016-077]to commence without any assistance from outward auxiliaries, almost

[016-078]forgot what it was that he had promised to do.

[016-079]"I have just come from Curzon Street," he said.

[016-080]"Well!"

[016-081]"At least I was there about two hours ago."

[016-082]"It doesn't matter, I suppose, whether it was two hours or two

[016-083]minutes," said Trevelyan.

[016-084]"Not in the least. The fact is this; I happened to come upon the two

[016-085]girls there, when they were very unhappy, and your wife asked me to

[016-086]come and say a word or two to you."

[016-087]"Was Colonel Osborne there?"

[016-088]"No; I had met him in the street a minute or two before."

[016-089]"Well, now; look here, Stanbury. If you'll take my advice, you'll

[016-090]keep your hands out of this. It is not but that I regard you as being

[016-091]as good a friend as I have in the world; but, to own the truth, I

[016-092]cannot put up with interference between myself and my wife."

[016-093]"Of course you understand that I only come as a messenger."

[016-094][Illustration: "I only come as a messenger."]

[016-095]"You had better not be a messenger in such a cause. If she has

[016-096]anything to say she can say it to myself."

[016-097]"Am I to understand that you will not listen to me?"

[016-098]"I had rather not."

[016-099]"I think you are wrong," said Stanbury.

[016-100]"In that matter you must allow me to judge for myself. I can easily

[016-101]understand that a young woman like her, especially with her sister to

[016-102]back her, should induce such a one as you to take her part."

[016-103]"I am taking nobody's part. You wrong your wife, and you especially

[016-104]wrong Miss Rowley."

[016-105]"If you please, Stanbury, we will say nothing more about it." This

[016-106]Trevelyan said holding the door of the room half open in his hand, so

[016-107]that the other was obliged to pass out through it.

[016-108]"Good evening," said Stanbury, with much anger.

[016-109]"Good evening," said Trevelyan, with an assumption of indifference.

[016-110]Stanbury went away in absolute wrath, though the trouble which he had

[016-111]had in the interview was much less than he had anticipated, and the

[016-112]result quite as favourable. He had known that no good would come of

[016-113]his visit. And yet he was now full of anger against Trevelyan, and

[016-114]had become a partisan in the matter,--which was exactly that which he

[016-115]had resolutely determined that he would not become. "I believe that

[016-116]no woman on earth could live with him," he said to himself as he

[016-117]walked away. "It was always the same with him,--a desire for mastery,

[016-118]which he did not know how to use when he had obtained it. If it were

[016-119]Nora, instead of the other sister, he would break her sweet heart

[016-120]within a month."

[016-121]Trevelyan dined at his club, and hardly spoke a word to any one

[016-122]during the evening. At about eleven he started to walk home, but

[016-123]went by no means straight thither, taking a long turn through St.

[016-124]James's Park, and by Pimlico. It was necessary that he should make

[016-125]up his mind as to what he would do. He had sternly refused the

[016-126]interference of a friend, and he must be prepared to act on his own

[016-127][[duty|responsibility]]. He knew well that he could not begin again with his

[016-128]wife on the next day as though nothing had happened. Stanbury's visit

[016-129]to him, if it had done nothing else, had made this impossible. He

[016-130]determined that he would not go to her room to-night, but would see

[016-131]her as early as possible in the morning;--and would then talk to her

[016-132]with all the wisdom of which he was master.

[016-133]How many husbands have come to the same resolution; and how few of

[016-134]them have found the words of wisdom to be efficacious!

[016-135]CHAPTER X.

[016-136]HARD WORDS.

[016-137][Illustration]

[016-138]It is to be feared that men in general do not regret as they should

[016-139]do any temporary ill-feeling, or irritating jealousy between husbands

[016-140]and wives, of which they themselves have been the cause. The author

[016-141]is not speaking now of actual love-makings, of intrigues and devilish

[016-142]villany, either perpetrated or imagined; but rather of those passing

[016-143]gusts of short-lived and unfounded suspicion to which, as to other

[016-144]accidents, very well-regulated families may occasionally be liable.

[016-145]When such suspicion rises in the bosom of a wife, some woman

[016-146]intervening or being believed to intervene between her and the man

[016-147]who is her own, that woman who has intervened or been supposed to

[016-148]intervene, will either glory in her position or bewail it bitterly,

[016-149]according to the circumstances of the case. We will charitably

[016-150]suppose that, in a great majority of such instances, she will bewail

[016-151]it. But when such painful jealous doubts annoy the husband, the

[016-152]man who is in the way will almost always feel himself justified in

[016-153]extracting a slightly pleasurable sensation from the transaction.

[016-154]He will say to himself probably, unconsciously indeed, and with

[016-155]no formed words, that the husband is an ass, an ass if he be in

[016-156]a twitter either for that which he has kept or for that which he

[016-157]has been unable to keep, that the lady has shewn a good deal of

[016-158]appreciation, and that he himself is--is--is--quite a Captain bold

[016-159]of Halifax. All the while he will not have the slightest intention

[016-160]of wronging the husband's honour, and will have received no greater

[016-161]favour from the intimacy accorded to him than the privilege of

[016-162]running on one day to Marshall and Snellgrove's, the haberdashers,

[016-163]and on another to Handcocks', the jewellers. If he be allowed to buy

[016-164]a present or two, or to pay a few shillings here or there, he has

[016-165]achieved much. Terrible things now and again do occur, even here in

[016-166]England; but women, with us, are slow to burn their household gods.

[016-167]It happens, however, occasionally, as we are all aware, that the

[016-168]outward garments of a domestic deity will be a little scorched; and

[016-169]when this occurs, the man who is the interloper, will generally find

[016-170]a gentle consolation in his position, let its interest be ever so

[016-171]flaccid and unreal, and its troubles in running about, and the like,

[016-172]ever so considerable and time-destructive.

[016-173]It was so certainly with Colonel Osborne when he became aware that

[016-174]his intimacy with Mrs. Trevelyan had caused her husband uneasiness.

[016-175]He was not especially a vicious man, and had now, as we know, reached

[016-176]a time of life when such vice as that in question might be supposed

[016-177]to have lost its charm for him. A [[social-class|gentleman]] over fifty, popular

[016-178]in [[london|London]], with a seat in Parliament, fond of good dinners, and

[016-179]possessed of everything which the world has to give, could hardly

[016-180]have wished to run away with his neighbour's wife, or to have

[016-181]destroyed the happiness of his old friend's daughter. Such wickedness

[016-182]had never come into his head; but he had a certain pleasure in being

[016-183]the confidential friend of a very pretty woman; and when he heard

[016-184]that that pretty woman's husband was jealous, the pleasure was

[016-185]enhanced rather than otherwise. On that Sunday, as he had left the

[016-186]house in Curzon Street, he had told Stanbury that Trevelyan had just

[016-187]gone off in a huff, which was true enough, and he had walked from

[016-188]thence down Clarges Street, and across Piccadilly to St. James's

[016-189]Street, with a jauntier step than usual, because he was aware that he

[016-190]himself had been the occasion of that trouble. This was very wrong;

[016-191]but there is reason to believe that many such men as Colonel Osborne,

[016-192]who are bachelors at fifty, are equally malicious.
