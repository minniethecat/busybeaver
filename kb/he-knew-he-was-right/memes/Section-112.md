## Section 112

[112-001]"Louis," said the wife, going quickly up to her husband, "I will do

[112-002]anything, if you will give me my child."

[112-003]"What will you do?"

[112-004]"Anything;--say what you want. He is all the world to me, and I

[112-005]cannot live if he be taken from me."

[112-006]"Acknowledge that you have been wrong."

[112-007]"But how;--in what words;--how am I to speak it?"

[112-008]"Say that you have sinned;--and that you will sin no more."

[112-009]"Sinned, Louis;--as the woman did,--in the Scripture? Would you have

[112-010]me say that?"

[112-011]"He cannot think that it is so," said Lady Rowley.

[112-012]But Trevelyan had not understood her. "Lady Rowley, I should have

[112-013]fancied that my thoughts at any rate were my own. But this is useless

[112-014]now. The child cannot go with you to-day, nor can you remain here. Go

[112-015]home and think of what I have said. If then you will do as I would

[112-016]have you, you shall return."

[112-017]With many embraces, with promises of motherly love, and with prayers

[112-018]for love in return, the poor woman did at last leave the house, and

[112-019]return to the cab. As she went there was a doubt on her own mind

[112-020]whether she should ask to kiss her husband; but he made no sign, and

[112-021]she at last passed out without any mark of tenderness. He stood by

[112-022]the cab as they entered it, and closed the door upon them, and then

[112-023]went slowly back to his room. "My poor bairn," he said to the boy;

[112-024]"my poor bairn."

[112-025]"Why for mamma go?" sobbed the child.

[112-026]"Mamma goes--; oh, heaven and earth, why should she go? She goes

[112-027]because her spirit is obstinate, and she will not bend. She is

[112-028]stiff-necked, and will not submit herself. But Louey must love mamma

[112-029]always;--and mamma some day will come back to him, and be good to

[112-030]him."

[112-031]"Mamma is good,--always," said the child. Trevelyan had intended on

[112-032]this very afternoon to have gone up to town,--to transact business

[112-033]with Bozzle; for he still believed, though the aspect of the man was

[112-034]bitter to him as wormwood, that Bozzle was necessary to him in all

[112-035]his business. And he still made appointments with the man, sometimes

[112-036]at Stony Walk, in the Borough, and sometimes at the tavern in

[112-037]Poulter's Court, even though Bozzle not unfrequently neglected to

[112-038]attend the summons of his employer. And he would go to his banker's

[112-039]and draw out money, and then walk about the crowded lanes of the

[112-040]City, and afterwards return to his desolate lodgings at Willesden,

[112-041]thinking that he had been transacting business,--and that this

[112-042]business was exacted from him by the unfortunate position of his

[112-043]affairs. But now he gave up his journey. His retreat had been

[112-044]discovered; and there came upon him at once a fear that if he left

[112-045]the house his child would be taken. His landlady told him on this

[112-046]very day that the boy ought to be sent to his mother, and had made

[112-047]him understand that it would not suit her to find a home any longer

[112-048]for one who was so singular in his proceedings. He believed that his

[112-049]child would be given up at once, if he were not there to guard it.

[112-050]He stayed at home, therefore, turning in his mind many schemes. He

[112-051]had told his wife that he should go either to Italy or to America at

[112-052]once; but in doing so he had had no formed plan in his head. He had

[112-053]simply imagined at the moment that such a threat would bring her to

[112-054]submission. But now it became a question whether he would do better

[112-055]than go to America. He suggested to himself that he should go to

[112-056]Canada, and fix himself with his boy on some remote farm,--far away

[112-057]from any city; and would then invite his wife to join him if she

[112-058]would. She was too obstinate, as he told himself, ever to yield,

[112-059]unless she should be absolutely softened and brought down to the

[112-060]ground by the loss of her child. What would do this so effectually

[112-061]as the interposition of the broad ocean between him and her? He sat

[112-062]thinking of this for the rest of the day, and Louey was left to the

[112-063]charge of the mistress of River's Cottage.

[112-064]"Do you think he believes it, mamma?" Mrs. Trevelyan said to her

[112-065]mother when they had already made nearly half their journey home in

[112-066]the cab. There had been nothing spoken hitherto between them, except

[112-067]some half-formed words of affection intended for consolation to the

[112-068]young mother in her great affliction.

[112-069]"He does not know what he believes, dearest."

[112-070]"You heard what he said. I was to own that I had--sinned."

[112-071]"Sinned;--yes; because you will not obey him like a slave. That is

[112-072]sin--to him."

[112-073]"But I asked him, mamma. Did you not hear me? I could not say

[112-074]the word plainer,--but I asked him whether he meant that sin.

[112-075]He must have known, and he would not answer me. And he spoke of

[112-076]my--transgression. Mamma, if he believed that, he would not let me

[112-077]come back at all."

[112-078]"He did not believe it, Emily."

[112-079]"Could he possibly then so accuse me,--the mother of his child! If

[112-080]his heart be utterly hard and false towards me, if it is possible

[112-081]that he should be cruel to me with such cruelty as that,--still he

[112-082]must love his boy. Why did he not answer me, and say that he did not

[112-083]think it?"

[112-084]"Simply because his reason has left him."

[112-085]"But if he be mad, mamma, ought we to leave him like that? And, then,

[112-086]did you see his eyes, and his face, and his hands? Did you observe

[112-087]how thin he is,--and his back, how bent? And his clothes,--how they

[112-088]were torn and soiled. It cannot be right that he should be left like

[112-089]that."

[112-090]"We will tell papa when we get home," said Lady Rowley, who was

[112-091]herself beginning to be somewhat frightened by what she had seen.

[112-092]It is all very well to declare that a friend is mad when one simply

[112-093]desires to justify one's self in opposition to that friend;--but

[112-094]the matter becomes much more serious when [[truth|evidence]] of the friend's

[112-095][[madness|insanity]] becomes true and circumstantial. "I certainly think that a

[112-096]physician should see him," continued Lady Rowley. On their return

[112-097]home Sir Marmaduke was told of what had occurred, and there was a

[112-098]long family discussion in which it was decided that Lady Milborough

[112-099]should be consulted, as being the oldest friend of [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]]

[112-100]himself with whom they were acquainted. Trevelyan had relatives of

[112-101]his own name living in Cornwall; but Mrs. Trevelyan herself had never

[112-102]even met one of that branch of the family.

[112-103]Sir Marmaduke, however, resolved that he himself would go out and

[112-104]see his son-in-law. He too had called Trevelyan mad, but he did not

[112-105]believe that the madness was of such a nature as to interfere with

[112-106]his own duties in punishing the man who had ill used his daughter. He

[112-107]would at any rate see Trevelyan himself;--but of this he said nothing

[112-108]either to his wife or to his child.

[112-109]CHAPTER LXVIII.

[112-110]MAJOR MAGRUDER'S COMMITTEE.

[112-111][Illustration]

[112-112]Sir Marmaduke could not go out to Willesden on the morning after Lady

[112-113]Rowley's return from River's Cottage, because on that day he was

[112-114]summoned to attend at twelve o'clock before a Committee of the House

[112-115]of Commons, to give his evidence and the fruit of his experience as

[112-116]to the government of British colonies generally; and as he went down

[112-117]to the House in a cab from Manchester Street he thoroughly wished

[112-118]that his friend [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] had not been so efficacious in

[112-119]bringing him home. The task before him was one which he thoroughly

[112-120]disliked, and of which he was afraid. He dreaded the inquisitors

[112-121]before whom he was to appear, and felt that though he was called

[112-122]there to speak as a master of his art of governing, he would in truth

[112-123]be examined as a servant,--and probably as a servant who did not

[112-124]know his business. Had his sojourn at home been in other respects

[112-125]happy, he might have been able to balance the advantage against the

[112-126]inquiry;--but there was no such balancing for him now. And, moreover,

[112-127]the expense of his own house in Manchester Street was so large that

[112-128]this journey, in a pecuniary point of view, would be of but little

[112-129]service to him. So he went down to the House in an unhappy mood; and

[112-130]when he shook hands in one of the passages with his friend Osborne

[112-131]who was on the Committee, there was very little cordiality in his

[112-132]manner. "This is the most ungrateful thing I ever knew," said the

[112-133]Colonel to himself; "I have almost disgraced myself by having this

[112-134]fellow brought home; and now he quarrels with me because that idiot,

[112-135]his son-in-law, has quarrelled with his wife." And Colonel Osborne

[112-136]really did feel that he was a martyr to the ingratitude of his

[112-137]friend.

[112-138]The Committee had been convoked by the House in compliance with the

[112-139]eager desires of a certain ancient pundit of the constitution, who

[112-140]had been for many years a member, and who had been known as a stern

[112-141]critic of our colonial modes of government. To him it certainly

[112-142]seemed that everything that was, was bad,--as regarded our national

[112-143]dependencies. But this is so usually the state of mind of all

[112-144]parliamentary critics, it is so much a matter of course that the

[112-145]members who take up the army or the navy, guns, India, our relations

[112-146]with Spain, or workhouse management, should find everything to

[112-147]be bad, rotten, and dishonest, that the wrath of the member for

[112-148]Killicrankie against colonial peculation and idleness, was not

[112-149]thought much of in the open House. He had been at the work for years,

[112-150]and the Colonial Office were so used to it that they rather liked

[112-151]him. He had made himself free of the office, and the clerks were

[112-152]always glad to see him. It was understood that he said bitter things

[112-153]in the House,--that was Major Magruder's line of business; but he

[112-154]could be quite pleasant when he was asking questions of a private

[112-155]secretary, or telling the news of the day to a senior clerk. As he

[112-156]was now between seventy and eighty, and had been at the work for at

[112-157]least twenty years, most of those concerned had allowed themselves

[112-158]to think that he would ride his hobby harmlessly to the day of his

[112-159]parliamentary death. But the drop from a house corner will hollow

[112-160]a stone by its constancy, and Major Magruder at last persuaded the

[112-161]House to grant him a Committee of Inquiry. Then there came to be

[112-162]serious faces at the Colonial Office, and all the little pleasantries

[112-163]of a friendly opposition were at an end. It was felt that the battle

[112-164]must now become a real fight, and Secretary and Under-Secretary

[112-165]girded up their loins.

[112-166]Major Magruder was chairman of his own committee, and being a man

[112-167]of a laborious turn of mind, much given to blue-books, very patient,

[112-168]thoroughly conversant with the House, and imbued with a strong belief

[112-169]in the efficacy of parliamentary questionings to carry a point, if

[112-170]not to elicit a fact, had a happy time of it during this session.

[112-171]He was a man who always attended the House from 4 p.m. to the time

[112-172]of its breaking up, and who never missed a division. The slight

[112-173]additional task of sitting four hours in a committee-room three days

[112-174]a week, was only a delight the more,--especially as during those four

[112-175]hours he could occupy the post of chairman. Those who knew Major

[112-176]Magruder well did not doubt but that the Committee would sit for many

[112-177]weeks, and that the whole theory of colonial government, or rather of

[112-178]imperial control supervising such government, would be tested to the

[112-179]very utmost. Men who had heard the old Major maunder on for years

[112-180]past on his pet subject, hardly knew how much vitality would be found

[112-181]in him when his maundering had succeeded in giving him a committee.
