## Section 152

[152-001]In a few minutes he was conducted through the house, and found

[152-002]Trevelyan seated in a chair under the verandah which looked down

[152-003]upon the olive trees. He did not even get up from his seat, but put

[152-004]out his left hand and welcomed his old friend. "Stanbury," he said,

[152-005]"I am glad to see you,--for auld lang syne's sake. When I found

[152-006]out this retreat, I did not mean to have friends round me here.

[152-007]I wanted to try what solitude was;--and, by heaven, I've tried

[152-008]it!" He was dressed in a bright Italian dressing-gown or woollen

[152-009]paletot,--Italian, as having been bought in Italy, though, doubtless,

[152-010]it had come from France,--and on his feet he had green worked

[152-011]slippers, and on his head a brocaded cap. He had made but little

[152-012]other preparation for his friend in the way of dressing. His long

[152-013]dishevelled hair came down over his neck, and his beard covered his

[152-014]face. Beneath his dressing-gown he had on a night-shirt and drawers,

[152-015]and was as dirty in appearance as he was gaudy in colours. "Sit

[152-016]down and let us two moralise," he said. "I spend my life here doing

[152-017]nothing,--nothing,--nothing; while you cudgel your brain from day

[152-018]to day to mislead the British public. Which of us two is taking the

[152-019]nearest road to the devil?"

[152-020]Stanbury seated himself in a second arm-chair, which there was there

[152-021]in the verandah, and looked as carefully as he dared to do at his

[152-022]friend. There could be no mistake as to the restless gleam of that

[152-023]eye. And then the affected air of ease, and the would-be cynicism,

[152-024]and the pretence of false motives, all told the same story. "They

[152-025]used to tell us," said Stanbury, "that idleness is the root of all

[152-026]evil."

[152-027]"They have been telling us since the world began so many lies, that I

[152-028]for one have determined never to believe anything again. Labour leads

[152-029]to greed, and greed to selfishness, and selfishness to treachery,

[152-030]and treachery straight to the devil,--straight to the devil. Ha, my

[152-031]friend, all your leading articles won't lead you out of that. What's

[152-032]the news? Who's alive? Who dead? Who in? Who out? What think you of

[152-033]a man who has not seen a newspaper for two months; and who holds no

[152-034]conversation with the world further than is needed for the cooking of

[152-035]his polenta and the cooling of his modest wine-flask?"

[152-036]"You see your wife sometimes," said Stanbury.

[152-037]"My wife! Now, my friend, let us drop that subject. Of all topics of

[152-038]talk it is the most distressing to man in general, and I own that

[152-039]I am no exception to the lot. Wives, Stanbury, are an evil, more

[152-040]or less necessary to humanity, and I own to being one who has not

[152-041]escaped. The world must be populated, though for what reason one does

[152-042]not see. I have helped,--to the extent of one male bantling; and if

[152-043]you are one who consider population desirable, I will express my

[152-044]regret that I should have done no more."

[152-045]It was very difficult to force Trevelyan out of this humour, and it

[152-046]was not till Stanbury had risen apparently to take his leave that he

[152-047]found it possible to say a word as to his mission there. "Don't you

[152-048]think you would be happier at home?" he asked.

[152-049]"Where is my home, Sir Knight of the midnight pen?"

[152-050]"England is your home, Trevelyan."

[152-051]"No, sir; England was my home once; but I have taken the [[personal-freedom|liberty]]

[152-052]accorded to me by my Creator of choosing a new country. Italy is now

[152-053]my nation, and Casalunga is my home."

[152-054]"Every tie you have in the world is in England."

[152-055]"I have no tie, sir;--no tie anywhere. It has been my study to untie

[152-056]all the ties; and, by Jove, I have succeeded. Look at me here. I have

[152-057]got rid of the trammels pretty well,--haven't I?--have unshackled

[152-058]myself, and thrown off the paddings, and the wrappings, and the

[152-059]swaddling clothes. I have got rid of the conventionalities, and can

[152-060]look Nature straight in the face. I don't even want the Daily Record,

[152-061]Stanbury;--think of that!"

[152-062]Stanbury paced the length of the terrace, and then stopped for a

[152-063]moment down under the blaze of the sun, in order that he might think

[152-064]how to address this philosopher. "Have you heard," he said at last,

[152-065]"that I am going to marry your sister-in-law, [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]?"

[152-066]"Then there will be two more full-grown fools in the world certainly,

[152-067]and probably an infinity of young fools coming afterwards. Excuse me,

[152-068]Stanbury, but this solitude is apt to make one plain-spoken."

[152-069]"I got Sir Marmaduke's sanction the day before I left."

[152-070]"Then you got the sanction of an illiterate, ignorant,

[152-071]self-sufficient, and most contemptible old man; and much good may it

[152-072]do you."

[152-073]"Let him be what he may, I was glad to have it. Most probably I shall

[152-074]never see him again. He sails from Southampton for the Mandarins on

[152-075]this day week."

[152-076]"He does,--does he? May the devil sail along with him!--that is all I

[152-077]say. And does my much-respected and ever-to-be-beloved mother-in-law

[152-078]sail with him?"

[152-079]"They all return together,--except Nora."

[152-080]"Who remains to comfort you? I hope you may be comforted;--that is

[152-081]all. Don't be too particular. Let her choose her own friends, and go

[152-082]her own gait, and have her own way, and do you be blind and deaf and

[152-083]dumb and properly submissive; and it may be that she'll give you your

[152-084]breakfast and dinner in your own house,--so long as your hours don't

[152-085]interfere with her pleasures. If she should even urge you beside

[152-086]yourself by her vanity, folly, and disobedience,--so that at last you

[152-087]are driven to express your feeling,--no doubt she will come to you

[152-088]after a while and tell you with the sweetest condescension that she

[152-089]forgives you. When she has been out of your house for a twelvemonth

[152-090]or more, she will offer to come back to you, and to forget

[152-091]everything,--on condition that you will do exactly as she bids you

[152-092]for the future."

[152-093]This attempt at satire, so fatuous, so plain, so false, together

[152-094]with the would-be jaunty manner of the speaker, who, however, failed

[152-095]repeatedly in his utterances from sheer physical exhaustion, was

[152-096]excessively painful to Stanbury. What can one do at any time with a

[152-097]madman? "I mentioned my marriage," said he, "to prove my right to

[152-098]have an additional interest in your wife's happiness."

[152-099]"You are quite welcome, whether you marry the other one or

[152-100]not;--welcome to take any interest you please. I have got beyond all

[152-101]that, Stanbury;--yes, by Jove, a long way beyond all that."

[152-102]"You have not got beyond loving your wife, and your child,

[152-103]Trevelyan?"

[152-104]"Upon my word, yes;--I think I have. There may be a grain of weakness

[152-105]left, you know. But what have you to do with my love for my wife?"

[152-106]"I was thinking more just now of her love for you. There she is at

[152-107]Siena. You cannot mean that she should remain there?"

[152-108]"Certainly not. What the deuce is there to keep her there?"

[152-109]"Come with her then to England."

[152-110]"Why should I go to England with her? Because you bid me, or because

[152-111]she wishes it,--or simply because England is the most damnable,

[152-112]puritanical, God-forgotten, and stupid country on the face of the

[152-113]globe? I know no other reason for going to England. Will you take a

[152-114]glass of wine, Stanbury?" Hugh declined the offer. "You will excuse

[152-115]me," continued Trevelyan; "I always take a glass of wine at this

[152-116]hour." Then he rose from his chair, and helped himself from a

[152-117]cupboard that was near at hand. Stanbury, watching him as he filled

[152-118]his glass, could see that his legs were hardly strong enough to carry

[152-119]him. And Stanbury saw, moreover, that the unfortunate man took two

[152-120]glasses out of the bottle. "Go to England indeed. I do not think much

[152-121]of this country; but it is, at any rate, better than England."

[152-122]Hugh perceived that he could do nothing more on the present occasion.

[152-123]Having heard so much of Trevelyan's debility, he had been astonished

[152-124]to hear the man speak with so much volubility and attempts at

[152-125]high-flown spirit. Before he had taken the wine he had almost sunk

[152-126]into his chair, but still he had continued to speak with the same

[152-127]fluent would-be cynicism. "I will come and see you again," said Hugh,

[152-128]getting up to take his departure.

[152-129]"You might as well save your trouble, Stanbury; but you can come if

[152-130]you please, you know. If you should find yourself locked out, you

[152-131]won't be angry. A hermit such as I am must assume privileges."

[152-132]"I won't be angry," said Hugh, good humouredly.

[152-133]"I can smell what you are come about," said Trevelyan. "You and my

[152-134]wife want to take me away from here among you, and I think it best to

[152-135]stay here. I don't want much for myself, and why should I not live

[152-136]here? My wife can remain at Siena if she pleases, or she can go to

[152-137]England if she pleases. She must give me the same liberty;--the same

[152-138]liberty,--the same liberty." After this he fell a-coughing violently,

[152-139]and Stanbury thought it better to leave him. He had been at Casalunga

[152-140]about two hours, and did not seem as yet to have done any good.

[152-141]He had been astonished both by Trevelyan's weakness, and by his

[152-142]strength; by his folly, and by his sharpness. Hitherto he could see

[152-143]no way for his future sister-in-law out of her troubles.

[152-144]When he was with her at Siena, he described what had taken place

[152-145]with all the accuracy in his power. "He has intermittent days,"

[152-146]said Emily. "To-morrow he will be in quite another frame of

[152-147]mind,--melancholy, silent perhaps, and self-reproachful. We will

[152-148]both go to-morrow, and we shall find probably that he has forgotten

[152-149]altogether what has passed to-day between you and him."

[152-150]So their plans for the morrow were formed.

[152-152]"SAY THAT YOU FORGIVE ME."

[152-153][Illustration]

[152-154]On the following day, again early in the morning, Mrs. Trevelyan and

[152-155]Stanbury were driven out to Casalunga. The country people along the

[152-156]road knew the carriage well, and the lady who occupied it, and would

[152-157]say that the English wife was going to see her mad husband. Mrs.

[152-158]Trevelyan knew that these words were common in the people's mouths,

[152-159]and explained to her companion how necessary it would be to use these

[152-160]rumours, to aid her in putting some restraint over her husband even

[152-161]in this country, should they fail in their effort to take him to

[152-162]England. She saw the doctor in Siena constantly, and had learned from

[152-163]him how such steps might be taken. The measure proposed would be

[152-164]slow, difficult, inefficient, and very hard to set aside, if once

[152-165]taken;--but still it might be indispensable that something should be

[152-166]done. "He would be so much worse off here than he would be at home,"

[152-167]she said;--"if we could only make him understand that it would be

[152-168]so." Then Stanbury asked about the wine. It seemed that of late

[152-169]Trevelyan had taken to drink freely, but only of the wine of the

[152-170]country. But the wine of the country in these parts is sufficiently

[152-171]stimulating, and Mrs. Trevelyan acknowledged that hence had arisen a

[152-172]further cause of fear.

[152-173]They walked up the hill together, and Mrs. Trevelyan, now well

[152-174]knowing the ways of the place, went round at once to the front

[152-175]terrace. There he was, seated in his arm-chair, dressed in the same

[152-176]way as yesterday, dirty, dishevelled, and gaudy with various colours;

[152-177]but Stanbury could see at once that his mood had greatly changed. He

[152-178]rose slowly, dragging himself up out of his chair, as they came up to

[152-179]him, but shewing as he did so,--and perhaps somewhat assuming,--the

[152-180]impotency of querulous sickness. His wife went to him, and took him

[152-181]by the hand, and placed him back in his chair. He was weak, he said,

[152-182]and had not slept, and suffered from the heat; and then he begged her

[152-183]to give him wine. This she did, half filling for him a tumbler, of

[152-184]which he swallowed the contents greedily. "You see me very poorly,

[152-185]Stanbury,--very poorly," he said, seeming to ignore all that had

[152-186]taken place on the previous day.
