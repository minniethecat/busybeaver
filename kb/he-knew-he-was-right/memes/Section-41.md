## Section 41

[041-001]They all went to church on the Sunday morning, and up to that time

[041-002]Nora had not been a moment alone with the man. It had been decided

[041-003]that they should dine early, and then ramble out, when the evening

[041-004]would be less hot than the day had been, to a spot called Niddon

[041-005]Park. This was nearly three miles from Nuncombe, and was a beautiful

[041-006]wild slope of ground, full of ancient, blighted, blasted, but still

[041-007]half-living oaks,--oaks that still brought forth leaves,--overlooking

[041-008]a bend of the river Teign. Park, in the usual sense of the word,

[041-009]there was none, nor did they who lived round [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] know

[041-010]whether Niddon Park had ever been enclosed. But of all the spots in

[041-011]that lovely neighbourhood, Priscilla Stanbury swore that it was the

[041-012]loveliest; and, as it had never yet been seen by Mrs. Trevelyan or

[041-013]her sister, it was determined that they would walk there on this

[041-014]August afternoon. There were four of them,--and, as was natural, they

[041-015]fell into parties of two and two. But Priscilla walked with Nora, and

[041-016][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] walked with his friend's wife. Nora was talkative, but

[041-017]demure in her manner, and speaking now and again as though she were

[041-018]giving words and not thoughts. She felt that there was something to

[041-019]hide, and was suffering from disappointment that their party should

[041-020]not have been otherwise divided. Had Hugh spoken to her and asked her

[041-021]to be his wife, she could not have accepted him, because she knew

[041-022]that they were both poor, and that she was not fit to keep a poor

[041-023]man's house. She had declared to herself most plainly that that must

[041-024]be her course;--but yet she was disappointed, and talked on with the

[041-025][[truth|knowledge]] that she had something to conceal.

[041-026][Illustration: Niddon Park.]

[041-027]When they were seated beneath an old riven, withered oak, looking

[041-028]down upon the river, they were still divided in the same way. In

[041-029]seating herself she had been very anxious not to disarrange that

[041-030]arrangement,--almost equally anxious not to seem to adhere to it

[041-031]with any special purpose. She was very careful that there should be

[041-032]nothing seen in her manner that was in any way special,--but in the

[041-033]meantime she was suffering an agony of trouble. He did not care for

[041-034]her in the least. She was becoming sure of that. She had given all

[041-035]her love to a man who had none to give her in return. As she thought

[041-036]of this she almost longed for the offer of that which she knew she

[041-037]could not have accepted had it been offered to her. But she talked

[041-038]on about the scenery, about the weather,--descanting on the pleasure

[041-039]of living where such loveliness was within reach. Then there came a

[041-040]pause for a moment. "Nora," said Priscilla, "I do not know what you

[041-041]are thinking about, but it is not of the beauty of Niddon Park." Then

[041-042]there came a faint sound as of an hysterical sob, and then a gurgle

[041-043]in the throat, and then a pretence at laughter.

[041-044]"I don't believe I am thinking of anything at all," said Nora.

[041-045]After which Hugh insisted on descending to the bank of the river,

[041-046]but, as the necessity of re-climbing the slope was quite manifest,

[041-047]none of the girls would go with him. "Come, Miss Rowley," said he,

[041-048]"will you not show them that a lady can go up and down a hill as well

[041-049]as a man?"

[041-050]"I had rather not go up and down the hill," said she.

[041-051]Then he understood that she was angry with him; and in some sort

[041-052]surmised the cause of her anger. Not that he believed that she loved

[041-053]him; but it seemed possible to him that she resented the absence of

[041-054]his attention. He went down, and scrambled out on the rocks into

[041-055]the bed of the river, while the girls above looked down upon him,

[041-056]watching the leaps that he made. Priscilla and Mrs. Trevelyan called

[041-057]to him, bidding him beware; but Nora called not at all. He was

[041-058]whistling as he made his jumps, but still he heard their voices, and

[041-059]knew that he did not hear Nora's voice. He poised himself on the edge

[041-060]of a rock in the middle of the stream, and looked up the river and

[041-061]down the river, turning himself carefully on his narrow foothold; but

[041-062]he was thinking only of Nora. Could there be anything nobler than to

[041-063]struggle on with her, if she only would be willing? But then she was

[041-064]young; and should she yield to such a request from him, she would not

[041-065]know what she was yielding. He turned again, jumping from rock to

[041-066]rock till he reached the bank, and then made his way again up to the

[041-067]withered oak.

[041-068]"You would not have repented it if you had come down with me," he

[041-069]said to Nora.

[041-070]"I am not so sure of that," she answered.

[041-071]When they started to return she stepped on gallantly with Priscilla;

[041-072]but Priscilla was stopped by some chance, having some word to say to

[041-073]her brother, having some other word to say to Mrs. Trevelyan. Could

[041-074]it be that her austerity had been softened, and that in kindness she

[041-075]contrived that Nora should be left some yards behind them with her

[041-076]brother? Whether it were kindness, or an unkind error, so it was.

[041-077]Nora, when she perceived what destiny was doing for her, would not

[041-078]interfere with destiny. If he chose to speak to her she would hear

[041-079]him and would answer him. She knew very well what answer she would

[041-080]give him. She had her answer quite ready at her fingers' ends. There

[041-081]was no doubt about her answer.

[041-082]They had walked half a mile together and he had spoken of nothing but

[041-083]the scenery. She had endeavoured to appear to be excited. Oh, yes,

[041-084]the scenery of Devonshire was delightful. She hardly wanted anything

[041-085]more to make her happy. If only this misery respecting her sister

[041-086]could be set right!

[041-087]"And you, you yourself," said he, "do you mean that there is nothing

[041-088]you want in leaving [[london|London]]?"

[041-089]"Not much, indeed."

[041-090]"It sometimes seemed to me that that kind of life was,--was very

[041-091]pleasant to you."

[041-092]"What kind of life, Mr. Stanbury?"

[041-093]"The life that you were living,--going out, being admired, and having

[041-094]the rich and dainty all around you."

[041-095]"I don't dislike people because they are rich," she said.

[041-096]"No; nor do I; and I despise those who affect to dislike them. But

[041-097]all cannot be rich."

[041-098]"Nor all dainty, as you choose to call them."

[041-099]"But they who have once been dainty,--as I call them,--never like

[041-100]to divest themselves of their daintiness. You have been one of the

[041-101]dainty, Miss Rowley."

[041-102]"Have I?"

[041-103]"Certainly; I doubt whether you would be happy if you thought that

[041-104]your daintiness had departed from you."

[041-105]"I hope, Mr. Stanbury, that nothing nice and pleasant has departed

[041-106]from me. If I have ever been dainty, dainty I hope I may remain. I

[041-107]will never, at any rate, give it up of my own accord." Why she said

[041-108]this, she could never explain to herself. She had certainly not

[041-109]intended to rebuff him when she had been saying it. But he spoke not

[041-110]a word to her further as they walked home, either of her mode of life

[041-111]or of his own.

[041-112]CHAPTER XXV.

[041-113]HUGH STANBURY SMOKES HIS PIPE.

[041-114][Illustration]

[041-115][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]], when she went to bed, after her walk to Niddon Park in

[041-116]company with Hugh Stanbury, was full of wrath against him. But she

[041-117]could not own her anger to herself, nor could she even confess to

[041-118]herself,--though she was breaking her heart,--that there really

[041-119]existed for her the slightest cause of grief. But why had he been so

[041-120]stern to her? Why had he gone out of his way to be uncivil to her? He

[041-121]had called her "dainty," meaning to imply by the epithet that she was

[041-122]one of the butterflies of the day, caring for nothing but sunshine

[041-123]and an opportunity of fluttering her silly wings. She had understood

[041-124]well what he meant. Of course he was right to be cold to her if

[041-125]his heart was cold, but he need not have insulted her by his

[041-126]ill-concealed rebukes. Had he been kind to her, he might have rebuked

[041-127]her as much as he liked. She quite appreciated the delightful

[041-128]intimacy of a loving word of counsel from the man she loved,--how

[041-129]nice it is, as it were, to play at marriage, and to hear beforehand

[041-130]something of the pleasant weight of gentle marital authority. But

[041-131]there had been nothing of that in his manner to her. He had told her

[041-132]that she was dainty,--and had so told it her, as she thought, that

[041-133]she might learn thereby, that under no circumstances would he have

[041-134]any other tale to tell her. If he had no other tale, why had he not

[041-135]been silent? Did he think that she was subject to his rebuke merely

[041-136]because she lived under his mother's roof? She would soon shew him

[041-137]that her residence at the [[clock-house|Clock House]] gave him no such authority over

[041-138]her. Then, amidst her wrath and despair, she cried herself asleep.

[041-139]While she was sobbing in bed, he was sitting, with a short, black

[041-140]pipe stuck into his mouth, on the corner of the churchyard wall

[041-141]opposite. Before he had left the house he and Priscilla had spoken

[041-142]together for some minutes about Mrs. Trevelyan. "Of course she was

[041-143]wrong to see him," said Priscilla. "I hesitate to wound her by so

[041-144]saying, because she has been ill-used,--though I did tell her so,

[041-145]when she asked me. She could have lost nothing by declining his

[041-146]visit."

[041-147]"The worst of it is that Trevelyan swears that he will never receive

[041-148]her again if she received him."

[041-149]"He must unswear it," said Priscilla, "that is all. It is out of the

[041-150]question that a man should take a girl from her home, and make her

[041-151]his wife, and then throw her off for so little of an offence as this.

[041-152]She might compel him by law to take her back."

[041-153]"What would she get by that?"

[041-154]"Little enough," said Priscilla; "and it was little enough she got by

[041-155]marrying him. She would have had bread, and meat, and raiment without

[041-156]being married, I suppose."

[041-157]"But it was a love-match."

[041-158]"Yes;--and now she is at Nuncombe Putney, and he is roaming about in

[041-159]London. He has to pay ever so much a year for his love-match, and she

[041-160]is crushed into nothing by it. How long will she have to remain here,

[041-161]Hugh?"

[041-162]"How can I say? I suppose there is no reason against her remaining as

[041-163]far as you are concerned?"

[041-164]"For me personally, none. Were she much worse than I think she is, I

[041-165]should not care in the least for myself, if I thought that we were

[041-166]doing her good,--helping to bring her back. She can't hurt me. I am

[041-167]so fixed, and dry, and established, that nothing anybody says will

[041-168]affect me. But mamma doesn't like it."

[041-169]"What is it she dislikes?"

[041-170]"The idea that she is harbouring a married woman, of whom people say,

[041-171]at least, that she has a lover."

[041-172]"Is she to be turned out because people are slanderers?"

[041-173]"Why should mamma suffer because this woman, who is a stranger to

[041-174]her, has been imprudent? If she were your wife, Hugh--"

[041-175]"God forbid!"

[041-176]"If we were in any way bound to her, of course we would do our duty.

[041-177]But if it makes mamma unhappy I am sure you will not press it. I

[041-178]think Mrs. Merton has spoken to her. And then Aunt Stanbury has

[041-179]written such letters!"

[041-180]"Who cares for Aunt Jemima?"

[041-181]"Everybody cares for her,--except you and I. And now this man who has

[041-182]been here asking the servant questions has upset her greatly. Even

[041-183]your coming has done so, knowing, as she does, that you have come,

[041-184]not to see us, but to make inquiries about Mrs. Trevelyan. She is so

[041-185]annoyed by it, that she does not sleep."
