## Section 109

[109-001]In the meantime Mr. Gibson was sitting by Arabella's bedside, while

[109-002]Mrs. French was trying to make herself busy in her own chamber, next

[109-003]door. There had been a reading of some chapter of the Bible,--or of

[109-004]some portion of a chapter. And Mr. Gibson, as he read, and Arabella,

[109-005]as she listened, had endeavoured to take to their hearts and to make

[109-006]use of the word which they heard. The poor young woman, when she

[109-007]begged her mother to send to her the man who was so dear to her, did

[109-008]so with some half-formed condition that it would be good for her to

[109-009]hear a clergyman read to her. But now the chapter had been read, and

[109-010]the book was back in Mr. Gibson's pocket, and he was sitting with

[109-011]his hand on the bed. "She is so very arrogant," said Bella,--"and

[109-012]so domineering." To this Mr. Gibson made no reply. "I'm sure I have

[109-013]endeavoured to bear it well, though you must have known what I have

[109-014]suffered, Thomas. Nobody can understand it so well as you do."

[109-015]"I wish I had never been born," said Mr. Gibson, tragically.

[109-016]"Don't say that, Thomas,--because it's wicked."

[109-017]"But I do. See all the harm I have done;--and yet I did not mean it."

[109-018]"You must try and do the best you can now. I am not saying what that

[109-019]should be. I am not dictating to you. You are a man, and, of course,

[109-020]you must judge for yourself. But I will say this. You shouldn't do

[109-021]anything just because it is the easiest. I don't suppose I should

[109-022]live after it. I don't indeed. But that should not signify to you."

[109-023]"I don't suppose that any man was ever before in such a terrible

[109-024]position since the world began."

[109-025]"It is difficult;--I am sure of that, Thomas."

[109-026]"And I have meant to be so true. I fancy sometimes that some

[109-027]mysterious agency interferes with the affairs of a man, and drives

[109-028]him on,--and on,--and on,--almost,--till he doesn't know where it

[109-029]drives him." As he said this in a voice that was quite sepulchral

[109-030]in its tone, he felt some consolation in the conviction that this

[109-031]mysterious agency could not affect a man without embuing him with

[109-032]a certain amount of grandeur,--very uncomfortable, indeed, in its

[109-033]nature, but still having considerable value as a counterpoise. [[pride|Pride]]

[109-034]must bear pain;--but pain is recompensed by pride.

[109-035]"She is so strong, Thomas, that she can put up with anything," said

[109-036]Arabella, in a whisper.

[109-037]"Strong;--yes," said he, with a shudder;--"she is strong enough."

[109-038]"And as for love--"

[109-039]"Don't talk about it," said he, getting up from his chair. "Don't

[109-040]talk about it. You will drive me frantic."

[109-041]"You know what my feelings are, Thomas; you have always known them.

[109-042]There has been no change since I was the young thing you first knew

[109-043]me." As she spoke, she just touched his hand with hers; but he did

[109-044]not seem to notice this, sitting with his elbow on the arm of his

[109-045]chair and his forehead on his hand. In reply to what she said to him,

[109-046]he merely shook his head,--not intending to imply thereby any doubt

[109-047]of the truth of her assertion. "You have now to make up your mind and

[109-048]to be bold, Thomas," continued Arabella. "She says that you are a

[109-049]coward; but I know that you are no coward. I told her so, and she

[109-050]said that I was interfering. Oh,--that she should be able to tell me

[109-051]that I interfere when I defend you!"

[109-052]"I must go," said Mr. Gibson, jumping up from his chair. "I must go.

[109-053]Bella, I cannot stand this any longer. It is too much for me. I will

[109-054]pray that I may decide aright. God bless you!" Then he kissed her

[109-055]brow as she lay in bed, and hurried out of the room.

[109-056]He had hoped to go from the house without further converse with any

[109-057]of its inmates; for his mind was disturbed, and he longed to be at

[109-058]rest. But he was not allowed to escape so easily. Camilla met him at

[109-059]the dining-room door, and accosted him with a smile. There had been

[109-060]time for much meditation during the last half hour, and Camilla had

[109-061]meditated. "How do you find her, Thomas?" she asked.

[109-062]"She seems weak, but I believe she is better. I have been reading to

[109-063]her."

[109-064]"Come in, Thomas;--will you not? It is bad for us to stand talking on

[109-065]the stairs. Dear Thomas, don't let us be so cold to each other." He

[109-066]had no alternative but to put his arm round her waist and kiss her,

[109-067]thinking, as he did so, of the mysterious agency which afflicted him.

[109-068]"Tell me that you love me, Thomas," she said.

[109-069]"Of course I love you." The question is not a pleasant one when put

[109-070]by a lady to a [[social-class|gentleman]] whose affections towards her are not strong,

[109-071]and it requires a very good actor to produce an efficient answer.

[109-072]"I hope you do, Thomas. It would be sad, indeed, if you did not. You

[109-073]are not weary of your Camilla,--are you?"

[109-074]For a moment there came upon him an idea that he would confess that

[109-075]he was weary of her, but he found at once that such an effort was

[109-076]beyond his powers. "How can you ask such a question?" he said.

[109-077]"Because you do not--come to me." Camilla, as she spoke, laid her

[109-078]head upon his shoulder, and wept. "And now you have been five minutes

[109-079]with me and nearly an hour with Bella."

[109-080]"She wanted me to read to her," said Mr. Gibson;--and he hated

[109-081]himself thoroughly as he said it.

[109-082]"And now you want to get away as fast as you can," continued Camilla.

[109-083]"Because of the morning service," said Mr. Gibson. This was quite

[109-084]true, and yet he hated himself again for saying it. As Camilla knew

[109-085]the truth of the last plea, she was obliged to let him go; but she

[109-086]made him swear before he went that he loved her dearly. "I think it's

[109-087]all right," she said to herself as he went down the stairs. "I don't

[109-088]think he'd dare make it wrong. If he does;--o-oh!"

[109-089]Mr. Gibson, as he walked into [[exeter|Exeter]], endeavoured to justify his

[109-090]own conduct to himself. There was no moment, he declared to himself,

[109-091]in which he had not endeavoured to do right. Seeing the manner in

[109-092]which he had been placed among these two young women, both of whom

[109-093]had fallen in love with him, how could he have saved himself from

[109-094]vacillation? And by what untoward chance had it come to pass that he

[109-095]had now learned to dislike so vigorously, almost to hate, the one

[109-096]with whom he had been for a moment sufficiently infatuated to think

[109-097]that he loved?

[109-098]But with all his arguments he did not succeed in justifying to

[109-099]himself his own conduct, and he hated himself.

[109-100]CHAPTER LXVI.

[109-101]OF A QUARTER OF LAMB.

[109-102]Miss Stanbury, looking out of her parlour window, saw Mr. Gibson

[109-103]hurrying towards the cathedral, down the passage which leads from

[109-104]Southernhay into the Close. "He's just come from Heavitree, I'll be

[109-105]bound," said Miss Stanbury to Martha, who was behind her.

[109-106]"Like enough, ma'am."

[109-107]"Though they do say that the poor fool of a man has become quite sick

[109-108]of his bargain already."

[109-109]"He'll have to be sicker yet, ma'am," said Martha.

[109-110]"They were to have been married last week, and nobody ever knew why

[109-111]it was put off. It's my belief he'll never marry her. And she'll be

[109-112]served right;--quite right."

[109-113]"He must marry her now, ma'am. She's been buying things all over

[109-114]Exeter, as though there was no end of their money."

[109-115]"They haven't more than enough to keep body and soul together," said

[109-116]Miss Stanbury. "I don't see why I mightn't have gone to service this

[109-117]morning, Martha. It's quite warm now out in the Close."

[109-118]"You'd better wait, ma'am, till the east winds is over. She was at

[109-119]Puddock's only the day before yesterday, buying bed-linen,--the

[109-120]finest they had, and that wasn't good enough."

[109-121]"Psha!" said Miss Stanbury.

[109-122]"As though Mr. Gibson hadn't things of that kind good enough for

[109-123]her," said Martha.

[109-124]Then there was silence in the room for awhile. Miss Stanbury was

[109-125][[reputation|standing]] at one window, and Martha at the other, watching the people

[109-126]as they passed backwards and forwards, in and out of the Close.

[109-127]Dorothy had now been away at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] for some weeks, and her

[109-128]aunt felt her loneliness with a heavy sense of weakness. Never had

[109-129]she entertained a companion in the house who had suited her as well

[109-130]as her niece, Dorothy. Dorothy would always listen to her, would

[109-131]always talk to her, would always bear with her. Since Dorothy had

[109-132]gone, various letters had been interchanged between them. Though

[109-133]there had been anger about Brooke Burgess, there had been no absolute

[109-134]rupture; but Miss Stanbury had felt that she could not write and beg

[109-135]her niece to come back to her. She had not sent Dorothy away. Dorothy

[109-136]had chosen to go, because her aunt had had an opinion of her own as

[109-137]to what was fitting for her heir; and as Miss Stanbury would not give

[109-138]up her opinion, she could not ask her niece to return to her. Such

[109-139]had been her resolution, sternly expressed to herself a dozen times

[109-140]during these solitary weeks; but time and solitude had acted upon

[109-141]her, and she longed for the girl's presence in the house. "Martha,"

[109-142]she said at last, "I think I shall get you to go over to Nuncombe

[109-143]Putney."

[109-144]"Again, ma'am?"

[109-145]"Why not again? It's not so far, I suppose, that the journey will

[109-146]hurt you."

[109-147]"I don't think it'd hurt me, ma'am;--only what good will I do?"

[109-148]"If you'll go rightly to work, you may do good. Miss Dorothy was a

[109-149]fool to go the way she did;--a great fool."

[109-150]"She stayed longer than I thought she would, ma'am."

[109-151]"I'm not asking you what you thought. I'll tell you what. Do you send

[109-152]Giles to Winslow's, and tell them to send in early to-morrow a nice

[109-153]fore-quarter of lamb. Or it wouldn't hurt you if you went and chose

[109-154]it yourself."

[109-155]"It wouldn't hurt me at all, ma'am."

[109-156]"You get it nice;--not too small, because meat is meat at the price

[109-157]things are now; and how they ever see butcher's meat at all is more

[109-158]than I can understand."

[109-159]"People as has to be careful, ma'am, makes a little go a long way."

[109-160]"You get it a good size, and take it over in a basket. It won't hurt

[109-161]you, done up clean in a napkin."

[109-162]"It won't hurt me at all, ma'am."

[109-163]"And you give it to Miss Dorothy with my love. Don't you let 'em

[109-164]think I sent it to my sister-in-law."

[109-165]"And is that to be all, ma'am?"

[109-166]"How do you mean all?"

[109-167]"Because, ma'am, the railway and the carrier would take it quite

[109-168]ready, and there would be a matter of ten or twelve shillings saved

[109-169]in the journey."

[109-170]"Whose affair is that?"

[109-171]"Not mine, ma'am, of course."

[109-172]"I believe you are afraid of the trouble, Martha. Or else you don't

[109-173]like going because they're poor."

[109-174]"It ain't fair, ma'am, of you to say so;--that it ain't. All I ask

[109-175]is,--is that to be all? When I've giv'em the lamb, am I just to come

[109-176]away straight, or am I to say anything? It will look so odd if I'm

[109-177]just to put down the basket and come away without e'er a word."

[109-178]"Martha!"

[109-179]"Yes, ma'am."

[109-180]"You're a fool."

[109-181]"That's true, too, ma'am."

[109-182]"It would be like you to go about in that dummy way,--wouldn't

[109-183]it;--and you that was so fond of Miss Dorothy."

[109-184]"I was fond of her, ma'am."

[109-185]"Of course you'll be talking to her;--and why not? And if she should

[109-186]say anything about returning--"
