## Section 66

[066-001]The double journey, from Turin to Bologna and from Bologna to

[066-002]Florence, is very long, and forms ample time for a considerable

[066-003]intimacy. There had, too, been a long day's journeying together

[066-004]before that; and with no women is a speedy intimacy so possible, or

[066-005]indeed so profitable, as with Americans. They fear nothing,--neither

[066-006]you nor themselves; and talk with as much freedom as though they

[066-007]were men. It may, perhaps, be assumed to be true as a rule that

[066-008]women's society is always more agreeable to men than that of other

[066-009]men,--except for the lack of ease. It undoubtedly is so when the

[066-010]women be young and pretty. There is a feeling, however, among pretty

[066-011]women in Europe that such freedom is dangerous, and it is withheld.

[066-012]There is such danger, and more or less of such withholding is

[066-013]expedient: but the American woman does not recognise the danger; and,

[066-014]if she withhold the grace of her countenance and the pearls of her

[066-015]speech, it is because she is not desirous of the society which is

[066-016]proffered to her. These two American sisters had not withholden their

[066-017]pearls from Mr. Glascock. He was much their senior in age; he was

[066-018]gentle in his manners, and they probably recognised him to be a safe

[066-019]companion. They had no idea who he was, and had not heard his name

[066-020]when they parted from him. But it was not probable that they should

[066-021]have been with him so long, and that they should leave him without

[066-022]further thought of him, without curiosity or a desire to know more

[066-023]of him. They had seen "C. G." in large letters on his dressing-bag,

[066-024]and that was all they had learned as to his identity. He had known

[066-025]their names well, and had once called Olivia by hers, in the

[066-026]hurry of speaking to her sister. He had apologised, and there had

[066-027]been a little laugh, and a discussion about the use of Christian

[066-028]names,--such as is very conducive to intimacy between gentlemen and

[066-029]ladies. When you can talk to a young lady about her own Christian

[066-030]name, you are almost entitled for the nonce to use it.

[066-031]Mr. Glascock went to his hotel, and was very moody and desolate. His

[066-032]name was very soon known there, and he received the honours due to

[066-033]his [[social-class|rank]] and station. "I should like to travel in America," he said

[066-034]to himself, "if I could be sure that no one would find out who I

[066-035]was." He had received letters at Turin, stating that his father was

[066-036]better, and, therefore, he intended to remain two days at Florence.

[066-037]The weather was still very hot, and Florence in the middle of

[066-038]September is much preferable to Naples.

[066-039]That night, when the two Miss Spaldings were alone together, they

[066-040]discussed their fellow-traveller thoroughly. Something, of course,

[066-041]had been said about him to their uncle the minister, to their aunt

[066-042]the minister's wife, and to their cousin the secretary of legation.

[066-043]But travellers will always observe that the dear new friends they

[066-044]have made on their journey are not interesting to the dear old

[066-045]friends whom they meet afterwards. There may be some touch of

[066-046]jealousy in this; and then, though you, the traveller, are fully

[066-047]aware that there has been something special in the case which has

[066-048]made this new friendship more peculiar than others that have sprung

[066-049]up in similar circumstances, fathers and brothers and wives and

[066-050]sisters do not see it in that light. They suspect, perhaps, that

[066-051]the new friend was a bagman, or an opera dancer, and think that the

[066-052]affair need not be made of importance. The American Minister had

[066-053]cast his eye on Mr. Glascock during that momentary parting, and had

[066-054]not thought much of Mr. Glascock. "He was certainly a gentleman,"

[066-055]Caroline had said. "There are a great many English gentlemen," the

[066-056]minister had replied.

[066-057]"I thought you would have asked him to call," Olivia said to her

[066-058]sister. "He did offer."

[066-059]"I know he did. I heard it."

[066-060]"Why didn't you tell him he might come?"

[066-061]"Because we are not in Boston, Livy. It might be the most horrible

[066-062]thing in the world to do here in Florence; and it may make a

[066-063]difference, because Uncle Jonas is minister."

[066-064]"Why should that make a difference? Do you mean that one isn't to see

[066-065]one's own friends? That must be nonsense."

[066-066]"But he isn't a friend, Livy."

[066-067]"It seems to me as if I'd known him for ever. That soft, monotonous

[066-068]voice, which never became excited and never disagreeable, is as

[066-069]familiar to me as though I had lived with it all my life."

[066-070]"I thought him very pleasant."

[066-071]"Indeed you did, Carry. And he thought you pleasant too. Doesn't it

[066-072]seem odd? You were mending his glove for him this very afternoon,

[066-073]just as if he were your brother."

[066-074]"Why shouldn't I mend his glove?"

[066-075]"Why not, indeed? He was entitled to have everything mended after

[066-076]getting us such a good dinner at Bologna. By-the-bye, you never paid

[066-077]him."

[066-078]"Yes, I did,--when you were not by."

[066-079]"I wonder who he is! C. G.! That fine man in the brown coat was his

[066-080]servant, you know. I thought at first that C. G. must have been

[066-081]cracked, and that the tall man was his keeper."

[066-082]"I never knew any one less like a madman."

[066-083]"No;--but the man was so queer. He did nothing, you know. We hardly

[066-084]saw him, if you remember, at Turin. All he did was to tie the shawls

[066-085]at Bologna. What can any man want with another man about with him

[066-086]like that, unless he is cracked either in body or mind?"

[066-087]"You'd better ask C. G. yourself."

[066-088]"I shall never see C. G. again, I suppose. I should like to see him

[066-089]again. I guess you would too, Carry. Eh?"

[066-090]"Of course, I should;--why not?"

[066-091]"I never knew a man so imperturbable, and who had yet so much to say

[066-092]for himself. I wonder what he is! Perhaps he's on business, and that

[066-093]man was a kind of a clerk."

[066-094]"He had livery buttons on," said Carry.

[066-095]"And does that make a difference?"

[066-096]"I don't think they put clerks into livery, even in England."

[066-097]"Nor yet mad doctors," said Olivia. "Well, I like him very much; and

[066-098]the only thing against him is that he should have a man, six feet

[066-099]high, going about with him doing nothing."

[066-100]"You'll make me angry, Livy, if you talk in that way. It's

[066-101]uncharitable."

[066-102]"In what way?"

[066-103]"[[About]] a mad doctor."

[066-104]"It's my belief," said Olivia, "that he's an English swell, a lord,

[066-105]or a duke;--and it's my belief, too, that he's in love with you."

[066-106]"It's my belief, Livy, that you're a regular ass;"--and so the

[066-107]conversation was ended on that occasion.

[066-108]On the next day, about noon, the American Minister, as a part of the

[066-109]duty which he owed to his country, read in a publication of that day,

[066-110]issued for the purpose, the names of the new arrivals at Florence.

[066-111]First and foremost was that of the Honourable Charles Glascock, with

[066-112]his suite, at the York Hotel, en route to join his father, Lord

[066-113]Peterborough, at Naples. Having read the news first to himself, the

[066-114]minister read it out loud in the presence of his nieces.

[066-115]"That's our friend C. G.," said Livy.

[066-116]"I should think not," said the minister, who had his own ideas about

[066-117]an English lord.

[066-118]"I'm sure it is, because of the tall man with the buttons," said

[066-119]Olivia.

[066-120]"It's very unlikely," said the secretary of legation. "Lord

[066-121]Peterborough is a man of immense [[money|wealth]], very old, indeed. They say

[066-122]he is dying at Naples. This man is his eldest son."

[066-123]"Is that any reason why he shouldn't have been civil to us?" asked

[066-124]Olivia.

[066-125]"I don't think he is the sort of man likely to sit up in the

[066-126]banquette; and he would have posted over the Alps. Moreover, he had

[066-127]his suite with him."

[066-128]"His suite was Buttons," said Olivia. "Only fancy, Carry, we've been

[066-129]waited on for two days by a lord as is to be, and didn't know it! And

[066-130]you have mended the tips of his lordship's glove!" But Carry said

[066-131]nothing at all.

[066-132]Late on that same evening, they met Mr. Glascock close to the Duomo,

[066-133]under the shade of the Campanile. He had come out as they had done,

[066-134]to see by moonlight that loveliest of all works made by man's hands.

[066-135]They were with the minister, but Mr. Glascock came up and shook hands

[066-136]with them.

[066-137]"I would introduce you to my uncle, Mr. Spalding," said

[066-138]Olivia,--"only,--as it happens,--we have never yet heard your name."

[066-139]"My name is Mr. Glascock," said he, smiling. Then the introduction

[066-140]was made; and the American Minister took off his hat, and was very

[066-141]affable.

[066-142]"Only think, Carry," said Olivia, when they were alone that evening,

[066-143]"if you were to become the wife of an English lord!"

[066-147][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]], when she escaped from the violence of her lover, at once

[066-148]rushed up to her own room, and managed to fasten herself in before

[066-149]she had been seen by any one. Her elder sister had at once gone to

[066-150]her aunt when, at Hugh's request, she had left the room, thinking it

[066-151]right that Mrs. Outhouse should know what was being done in her own

[066-152]house. Mrs. Outhouse had considered the matter patiently for awhile,

[066-153]giving the lovers the benefit of her hesitation, and had then spoken

[066-154]her mind to Stanbury, as we have already heard. He had, upon the

[066-155]whole, been so well pleased with what had occurred, that he was not

[066-156]in the least angry with the parson's wife when he left the parsonage.

[066-157]As soon as he was gone Mrs. Outhouse was at once joined by her elder

[066-158]niece, but Nora remained for a while alone in her room.

[066-159]Had she committed herself; and if so, did she regret it? He had

[066-160]behaved very badly to her, certainly, taking her by the hand and

[066-161]putting his arm round her waist. And then had he not even attempted

[066-162]to kiss her? He had done all this, although she had been resolute in

[066-163]refusing to speak to him one word of kindness,--though she had told

[066-164]him with all the energy and [[truth|certainty]] of which she was mistress, that

[066-165]she would never be his wife. If a girl were to be subjected to such

[066-166]treatment as this when she herself had been so firm, so discreet,

[066-167]so decided, then indeed it would be unfit that a girl should trust

[066-168]herself with a man. She had never thought that he had been such a one

[066-169]as that, to ill-use her, to lay a hand on her in violence, to refuse

[066-170]to take an answer. She threw herself on the bed and sobbed, and

[066-171]then hid her face,--and was conscious that in spite of this acting

[066-172]before herself she was the happiest girl alive. He had behaved very

[066-173]badly;--of course, he had behaved most wickedly, and she would tell

[066-174]him so some day. But was he not the dearest fellow living? Did ever

[066-175]man speak with more absolute conviction of love in every tone of

[066-176]his voice? Was it not the finest, noblest heart that ever throbbed

[066-177]beneath a waistcoat? Had not his very wickedness come from the

[066-178]overpowering truth of his affection for her? She would never quite

[066-179]forgive him because it had been so very wrong; but she would be

[066-180]true to him for ever and ever. Of course they could not marry.

[066-181]What!--would she go to him and be a clog round his neck, and a weight

[066-182]upon him for ever, bringing him down to the gutter by the burden of

[066-183]her own useless and unworthy self? No. She would never so injure

[066-184]him. She would not even hamper him by an engagement. But yet she

[066-185]would be true to him. She had an idea that in spite of all her

[066-186]protestations,--which, as she looked back upon them, appeared to her

[066-187]to have been louder than they had been,--that through the teeth of

[066-188]her denials, something of the truth had escaped from her. Well,--let

[066-189]it be so. It was the truth, and why should he not know it? Then

[066-190]she pictured to herself a long romance, in which the heroine lived

[066-191]happily on the simple knowledge that she had been beloved. And

[066-192]the reader may be sure that in this romance Mr. Glascock with his

[066-193]splendid prospects filled one of the characters.
