# Obedience

The Victorian doctrine of wifely obedience held that a husband possessed the right to command his wife and a wife bore the duty to obey. In *He Knew He Was Right*, Louis Trevelyan's demand for obedience and Emily's refusal to render it form the central axis of the marital breakdown.

## Overview

In mid-Victorian England, the marriage vow required a wife to promise obedience to her husband. This was not merely ceremonial — it encoded a legal and social hierarchy within the household. The husband was understood as the master of the family, entitled to make decisions and have his wishes followed. The wife's obedience was the correlative of his authority (009-033).

Trollope's novel examines this doctrine under pressure. Louis Trevelyan, convinced of his wife's improper intimacy with Colonel Osborne, demands that Emily cease all contact with Osborne. Emily refuses, not on the ground that the demand is unreasonable, but on the ground that her innocence makes compliance unnecessary. The resulting impasse destroys the marriage (009-045).

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian marriage law subsumed a wife's legal identity into her husband's — the doctrine of coverture. Within this framework, obedience was not merely a moral expectation but a legal obligation. The marriage service of the Book of Common Prayer included the bride's promise to "obey" her husband, and this promise carried social weight throughout the period.

Trollope's contemporaries debated the limits of this doctrine. The Matrimonial Causes Act of 1857 had reformed divorce law, but the cultural expectation of wifely submission remained powerful. The novel explores the collision between this expectation and the reality of individual will (009-054).

## Manifestation in the Novel

The language of obedience pervades the Trevelyan conflict. Louis frames his demand in terms of his "right to expect obedience" (019-001). Emily acknowledges the duty of obedience in principle but argues that "obedience cannot be exacted" when the command is unjust (009-057). The distinction she draws — between obedience as a general duty and obedience to a specific unreasonable command — is one the novel refuses to resolve cleanly.

Trevelyan's obsession with obedience escalates throughout the narrative. He characterises Emily's refusal as "indomitable disobedience" (024-149) and "flagrant disobedience of my instructions" (045-138). His language frames the marital dispute not as a disagreement between equals but as a rebellion against rightful authority.

## Key Examples

### The Promise of Obedience

When Louis first demands that Emily cease correspondence with Colonel Osborne, he invokes the marriage vow: "he asked his wife for a promise of obedience" (009-033). Emily's refusal — "she refused to render to him the obedience which was his privilege" (009-045) — becomes the original breach from which all subsequent conflict flows.

### Disobedience as a Weapon

As the separation hardens, Trevelyan comes to see every independent action by Emily as an act of defiance. He speaks of her "disobedience to my expressed wishes" as though it were a criminal charge (045-170). The novel shows how the demand for obedience, pursued beyond reason, transforms from a marital right into a destructive obsession.

## Related Characters

- **Louis Trevelyan** — demands obedience as his marital right; his inability to compromise destroys the marriage
- **Emily Trevelyan** — acknowledges the duty of obedience in principle but refuses to obey an unjust command
- **Lady Milborough** — represents conventional wisdom: "He has a right to expect obedience" (019-001)
