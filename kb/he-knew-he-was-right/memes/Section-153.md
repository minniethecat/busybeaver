## Section 153

[153-001]"You want change of climate, old fellow," said Stanbury.

[153-002]"Change of everything;--I want change of everything," he said. "If

[153-003]I could have a new body and a new mind, and a new soul!"

[153-004]"The mind and soul, dear, will do well enough, if you will let us

[153-005]look after the body," said his wife, seating herself on a stool near

[153-006]his feet. Stanbury, who had settled beforehand how he would conduct

[153-007]himself, took out a cigar and lighted it;--and then they sat together

[153-008]silent, or nearly silent, for half an hour. She had said that if Hugh

[153-009]would do so, Trevelyan would soon become used to the presence of his

[153-010]old friend, and it seemed that he had already done so. More than

[153-011]once, when he coughed, his wife fetched him some drink in a cup,

[153-012]which he took from her without a word. And Stanbury the while went on

[153-013]smoking in silence.

[153-014]"You have heard, Louis," she said at last, "that, after all, Nora and

[153-015]Mr. Stanbury are going to be married?"

[153-016]"Ah;--yes; I think I was told of it. I hope you may be happy,

[153-017]Stanbury;--happier than I have been." This was unfortunate, but

[153-018]neither of the visitors winced, or said a word.

[153-019]"It will be a pity that papa and mamma cannot be present at the

[153-020]wedding," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[153-021]"If I had to do it again, I should not regret your father's absence;

[153-022]I must say that. He has been my enemy. Yes, Stanbury,--my enemy. I

[153-023]don't care who hears me say so. I am obliged to stay here, because

[153-024]that man would swear every shilling I have away from me if I were in

[153-025]England. He would strive to do so, and the struggle in my state of

[153-026]health would be too much for me."

[153-027]"But Sir Marmaduke sails from Southampton this very week," said

[153-028]Stanbury.

[153-029]"I don't know. He is always sailing, and always coming back again. I

[153-030]never asked him for a shilling in my life, and yet he has treated me

[153-031]as though I were his bitterest enemy."

[153-032]"He will trouble you no more now, Louis," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[153-033]"He cannot trouble you again. He will have left England before you

[153-034]can possibly reach it."

[153-035]"He will have left other traitors behind him,--though none as bad as

[153-036]himself," said Trevelyan.

[153-037]Stanbury, when his cigar was finished, rose and left the husband and

[153-038]wife together on the terrace. There was little enough to be seen at

[153-039]Casalunga, but he strolled about looking at the place. He went into

[153-040]the huge granary, and then down among the olive trees, and up into

[153-041]the sheds which had been built for beasts. He stood and teased the

[153-042]lizards, and listened to the hum of the insects, and wiped away the

[153-043]perspiration which rose to his brow even as he was [[reputation|standing]]. And all

[153-044]the while he was thinking what he would do next, or what say next,

[153-045]with the view of getting Trevelyan away from the place. Hitherto he

[153-046]had been very tender with him, contradicting him in nothing, taking

[153-047]from him good humouredly any absurd insult which he chose to offer,

[153-048]pressing upon him none of the evil which he had himself occasioned,

[153-049]saying to him no word that could hurt either his pride or his

[153-050]comfort. But he could not see that this would be efficacious for the

[153-051]purpose desired. He had come thither to help Nora's sister in her

[153-052]terrible distress, and he must take upon himself to make some plan

[153-053]for giving this aid. When he had thought of all this and made his

[153-054]plan, he sauntered back round the house on to the terrace. She was

[153-055]still there, sitting at her husband's feet, and holding one of his

[153-056]hands in hers. It was well that the wife should be tender, but he

[153-057]doubted whether tenderness would suffice.

[153-058]"Trevelyan," he said, "you know why I have come over here?"

[153-059]"I suppose she told you to come," said Trevelyan.

[153-060]"Well; yes; she did tell me. I came to try and get you back to

[153-061]England. If you remain here, the climate and solitude together will

[153-062]kill you."

[153-063]"As for the climate, I like it;--and as for solitude, I have got used

[153-064]even to that."

[153-065]"And then there is another thing," said Stanbury.

[153-066]"What is that?" asked Trevelyan, starting.

[153-067]"You are not safe here."

[153-068]"How not safe?"

[153-069]"She could not tell you, but I must." His wife was still holding his

[153-070]hand, and he did not at once attempt to withdraw it; but he raised

[153-071]himself in his chair, and fixed his eyes fiercely on Stanbury. "They

[153-072]will not let you remain here quietly," said Stanbury.

[153-073]"Who will not?"

[153-074]"The Italians. They are already saying that you are not fit to be

[153-075]alone; and if once they get you into their hands,--under some Italian

[153-076]medical board, perhaps into some Italian asylum, it might be years

[153-077]before you could get out,--if ever. I have come to tell you what the

[153-078]danger is. I do not know whether you will believe me."

[153-079]"Is it so?" he said, turning to his wife.

[153-080]"I believe it is, Louis."

[153-081]"And who has told them? Who has been putting them up to it?" Now his

[153-082]hand had been withdrawn. "My God, am I to be followed here too with

[153-083]such persecution as this?"

[153-084]"Nobody has told them,--but people have eyes."

[153-085]"Liar, traitor, fiend!--it is you!" he said, turning upon his wife.

[153-086]"Louis, as I hope for mercy, I have said not a word to any one that

[153-087]could injure you."

[153-088]"Trevelyan, do not be so unjust, and so foolish," said Stanbury. "It

[153-089]is not her doing. Do you suppose that you can live here like this

[153-090]and give rise to no remarks? Do you think that people's eyes are not

[153-091]open, and that their tongues will not speak? I tell you, you are in

[153-092]danger here."

[153-093]"What am I to do? Where am I to go? Can not they let me stay till I

[153-094]die? Whom am I hurting here? She may have all my money, if she wants

[153-095]it. She has got my child."

[153-096]"I want nothing, Louis, but to take you where you may be safe and

[153-097]well."

[153-098]"Why are you afraid of going to England?" Stanbury asked.

[153-099]"Because they have threatened to put me--in a madhouse."

[153-100]"Nobody ever thought of so treating you," said his wife.

[153-101]"Your father did,--and your mother. They told me so."

[153-102]"Look here, Trevelyan. Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley are gone. They

[153-103]will have sailed, at least, before we can reach England. Whatever may

[153-104]have been either their wishes or their power, they can do nothing

[153-105]now. Here something would be done,--very soon; you may take my word

[153-106]for that. If you will return with me and your wife, you shall choose

[153-107]your own place of abode. Is not that so, Emily?"

[153-108]"He shall choose everything. His boy will be with him, and I will be

[153-109]with him, and he shall be contradicted in nothing. If he only knew my

[153-110]heart towards him!"

[153-111]"You hear what she says, Trevelyan?"

[153-112]"Yes; I hear her."

[153-113]"And you believe her?"

[153-114]"I'm not so sure of that. Stanbury, how should you like to be locked

[153-115]up in a madhouse and grin through the bars till your heart was

[153-116]broken? It would not take long with me, I know."

[153-117]"You shall never be locked up;--never be touched," said his wife.

[153-118]"I am very harmless here," he said, almost crying; "very harmless. I

[153-119]do not think anybody here will touch me," he added, afterwards. "And

[153-120]there are other places. There are other places. My God, that I should

[153-121]be driven about the world like this!" The conference was ended by his

[153-122]saying that he would take two days to think of it, and by his then

[153-123]desiring that they would both leave him. They did so, and descended

[153-124]the hill together, knowing that he was watching them,--that he would

[153-125]watch them till they were out of sight from the gate;--for, as Mrs.

[153-126]Trevelyan said, he never came down the hill now, knowing that the

[153-127]labour of ascending it was too much for him. When they were at the

[153-128]carriage they were met by one of the women of the house, and strict

[153-129]injunctions were given to her by Mrs. Trevelyan to send on word

[153-130]to Siena if the Signore should prepare to move. "He cannot go far

[153-131]without my knowing it," said she, "because he draws his money in

[153-132]Siena, and lately I have taken to him what he wants. He has not

[153-133]enough with him for a long journey." For Stanbury had suggested that

[153-134]he might be off to seek another residence in another country, and

[153-135]that they would find Casalunga vacant when they reached it on the

[153-136]following Tuesday. But he told himself almost immediately,--not

[153-137]caring to express such an opinion to Emily,--that Trevelyan would

[153-138]hardly have strength even to prepare for such a journey by himself.

[153-139]On the intervening day, the Monday, Stanbury had no occupation

[153-140]whatever, and he thought that since he was born no day had ever been

[153-141]so long. Siena contains many monuments of interest, and much that is

[153-142]valuable in art,--having had a school of painting of its own, and

[153-143]still retaining in its public gallery specimens of its school, of

[153-144]which as a city it is justly proud. There are palaces there to be

[153-145]beaten for gloomy majesty by none in Italy. There is a cathedral

[153-146]which was to have been the largest in the world, and than which few

[153-147]are more worthy of prolonged inspection. The town is old, and quaint,

[153-148]and picturesque, and dirty, and attractive,--as it becomes a town in

[153-149]Italy to be. But in July all such charms are thrown away. In July

[153-150]Italy is not a land of charms to an Englishman. Poor Stanbury did

[153-151]wander into the cathedral, and finding it the coolest place in the

[153-152]town, went to sleep on a stone step. He was awoke by the voice of the

[153-153]priests as they began to chant the vespers. The good-natured Italians

[153-154]had let him sleep, and would have let him sleep till the doors were

[153-155]closed for the night. At five he dined with Mrs. Trevelyan, and then

[153-156]endeavoured to while away the evening thinking of Nora with a pipe in

[153-157]his mouth. He was [[reputation|standing]] in this way at the hotel gateway, when, on

[153-158]a sudden, all Siena was made alive by the clatter of an open carriage

[153-159]and four on its way through the town to the railway. On looking up,

[153-160]Stanbury saw Lord Peterborough in the carriage,--with a lady whom

[153-161]he did not doubt to be Lord Peterborough's wife. He himself had not

[153-162]been recognised, but he slowly followed the carriage to the railway

[153-163][[social-class|station]]. After the Italian fashion, the arrival was three-quarters

[153-164]of an hour before the proper time, and Stanbury had full opportunity

[153-165]of learning their news and telling his own. They were coming up from

[153-166]Rome, and thought it preferable to take the route by Siena than to

[153-167]use the railway through the Maremma; and they intended to reach

[153-168]Florence that night.

[153-169]"And do you think he is really mad?" asked Lady Peterborough.

[153-170]"He is undoubtedly so mad as to be unfit to manage anything for

[153-171]himself, but he is not in such a condition that any one would wish to

[153-172]see him put into confinement. If he were raving mad there would be

[153-173]less difficulty, though there might be more distress."

[153-174]A great deal was said about Nora, and both Lord Peterborough and his

[153-175]wife insisted that the marriage should take place at Monkhams. "We

[153-176]shall be home now in less than three weeks," said Caroline, "and she

[153-177]must come to us at once. But I will write to her from Florence, and

[153-178]tell her how we saw you smoking your pipe under the archway. Not that

[153-179]my husband knew you in the least."

[153-180]"Upon my word no," said the husband,--"one didn't expect to find you

[153-181]here. Good-bye. I hope you may succeed in getting him home. I went

[153-182]to him once, but could do very little." Then the train started, and

[153-183]Stanbury went back to Mrs. Trevelyan.
