## Section 61

[061-001]"I've done all that I could."

[061-002]"And you think she won't see me for a minute?"

[061-003]"She says she won't. I can't bid Martha carry her down."

[061-004]"Then, perhaps, I had better leave you for the present," said Mr.

[061-005]Gibson, after another pause. So he went, a melancholy, blighted man.

[061-006]Leaving the Close, he passed through into Southernhay, and walked

[061-007]across by the new streets towards the Heavitree road. He had no

[061-008]design in taking this route, but he went on till he came in sight

[061-009]of the house in which Mrs. French lived. As he walked slowly by it,

[061-010]he looked up at the windows, and something of a feeling of romance

[061-011]came across his heart. Were his young affections buried there, or

[061-012]were they not? And, if so, with which of those fair girls were

[061-013]they buried? For the last two years, up to last night, Camilla had

[061-014]certainly been in the ascendant. But Arabella was a sweet young

[061-015]woman; and there had been a time,--when those tender passages were

[061-016]going on,--in which he had thought that no young woman ever was so

[061-017]sweet. A period of romance, an era of enthusiasm, a short-lived,

[061-018]delicious holiday of hot-tongued [[madness|insanity]] had been permitted to him

[061-019]in his youth;--but all that was now over. And yet here he was, with

[061-020]three strings to his bow,--so he told himself,--and he had not as yet

[061-021]settled for himself the great business of [[marriage|matrimony]]. He was inclined

[061-022]to think, as he walked on, that he would walk his life alone, an

[061-023]active, useful, but a melancholy man. After such experiences as

[061-024]his, how should he ever again speak of his heart to a woman? During

[061-025]this walk, his mind recurred frequently to Dorothy Stanbury; and,

[061-026]doubtless, he thought that he had often spoken of his heart to her.

[061-027]He was back at his lodgings before three, at which hour he ate an

[061-028]early dinner, and then took the afternoon cathedral service at four.

[061-029]The evening he spent at home, thinking of the romance of his early

[061-030]days. What would Miss Stanbury have said, had she seen him in his

[061-031]easy chair behind the "[[exeter|Exeter]] Argus,"--with a pipe in his mouth?

[061-032]In the meantime, there was an uncomfortable scene in progress between

[061-033]Dorothy and her aunt. Brooke Burgess, as desired, had left the house

[061-034]before eleven, having taken upon himself, when consulted, to say in

[061-035]the mildest terms, that he thought that, in general, young women

[061-036]should not be asked to marry if they did not like to;--which opinion

[061-037]had been so galling to Miss Stanbury that she had declared that he

[061-038]had so scolded her, that she did not know whether she was [[reputation|standing]]

[061-039]on her head or her heels. As soon as Mr. Gibson left her, she sat

[061-040]herself down, and fairly cried. She had ardently desired this thing,

[061-041]and had allowed herself to think of her desire as of one that would

[061-042]certainly be accomplished. Dorothy would have been so happy as the

[061-043]wife of a clergyman! Miss Stanbury's standard for men and women was

[061-044]not high. She did not expect others to be as self-sacrificing, as

[061-045]charitable, and as good as herself. It was not that she gave to

[061-046]herself credit for such virtues; but she thought of herself as one

[061-047]who, from the peculiar circumstances of life, was bound to do much

[061-048]for others. There was no end to her doing good for others,--if only

[061-049]the others would allow themselves to be governed by her. She did not

[061-050]think that Mr. Gibson was a great divine; but she perceived that he

[061-051]was a clergyman, living decently,--of that secret pipe Miss Stanbury

[061-052]knew nothing,--doing his duty punctually, and, as she thought, very

[061-053]much in want of a wife. Then there was her niece, Dolly,--soft,

[061-054]pretty, feminine, without a shilling, and much in want of some one

[061-055]to comfort and take care of her. What could be better than such a

[061-056]marriage! And the overthrow to the girls with the big chignons would

[061-057]be so complete! She had set her mind upon it, and now Dorothy said

[061-058]that it couldn't, and it wouldn't, and it shouldn't be accomplished!

[061-059]She was to be thrown over by this chit of a girl, as she had been

[061-060]thrown over by the girl's brother! And, when she complained, the girl

[061-061]simply offered to go away!

[061-062]At about twelve Dorothy came creeping down into the room in which her

[061-063]aunt was sitting, and pretended to occupy herself on some piece of

[061-064]work. For a considerable time,--for three minutes perhaps,--Miss

[061-065]Stanbury did not speak. She had resolved that she would not speak

[061-066]to her niece again,--at least, not for that day. She would let the

[061-067]ungrateful girl know how miserable she had been made. But at the

[061-068]close of the three minutes her patience was exhausted. "What are you

[061-069]doing there?" she said.

[061-070]"I am quilting your cap, Aunt Stanbury."

[061-071]"Put it down. You shan't do anything for me. I won't have you touch

[061-072]my things any more. I don't like pretended service."

[061-073]"It is not pretended, Aunt Stanbury."

[061-074]"I say it is pretended. Why did you pretend to me that you would have

[061-075]him when you had made up your mind against it all the time?"

[061-076]"But I hadn't--made up my mind."

[061-077]"If you had so much doubt about it, you might have done what I wanted

[061-078]you."

[061-079]"I couldn't, Aunt Stanbury."

[061-080]"You mean you wouldn't. I wonder what it is you do expect."

[061-081]"I don't expect anything, Aunt Stanbury."

[061-082]"No; and I don't expect anything. What an old fool I am ever to look

[061-083]for any comfort. Why should I think that anybody would care for me?"

[061-084]"Indeed, I do care for you."

[061-085]"In what sort of way do you show it? You're just like your brother

[061-086]Hugh. I've disgraced myself to that man,--promising what I could not

[061-087]perform. I declare it makes me sick when I think of it. Why did you

[061-088]not tell me at once?" Dorothy said nothing further, but sat with the

[061-089]cap on her lap. She did not dare to resume her needle, and she did

[061-090]not like to put the cap aside, as by doing so it would seem as though

[061-091]she had accepted her aunt's prohibition against her work. For half

[061-092]an hour she sat thus, during which time Miss Stanbury dropped asleep.

[061-093]She woke with a start, and began to scold again. "What's the good of

[061-094]sitting there all the day, with your hands before you, doing

[061-095]nothing?"

[061-096]But Dorothy had been very busy. She had been making up her mind,

[061-097]and had determined to communicate her resolution to her aunt. "Dear

[061-098]aunt," she said, "I have been thinking of something."

[061-099]"It's too late now," said Miss Stanbury.

[061-100]"I see I've made you very unhappy."

[061-101]"Of course you have."

[061-102]"And you think that I'm ungrateful. I'm not ungrateful, and I don't

[061-103]think that Hugh is."

[061-104]"Never mind Hugh."

[061-105]"Only because it seems so hard that you should take so much trouble

[061-106]about us, and that then there should be so much vexation."

[061-107]"I find it very hard."

[061-108]"So I think that I'd better go back to Nuncombe."

[061-109]"That's what you call gratitude."

[061-110]"I don't like to stay here and make you unhappy. I can't think that I

[061-111]ought to have done what you asked me, because I did not feel at all

[061-112]in that way about Mr. Gibson. But as I have only disappointed you,

[061-113]it will be better that I should go home. I have been very happy

[061-114]here,--very."

[061-115]"Bother!" exclaimed Miss Stanbury.

[061-116]"I have,--and I do love you, though you won't believe it. But I am

[061-117]sure I oughtn't to remain to make you unhappy. I shall never forget

[061-118]all that you have done for me; and though you call me ungrateful, I

[061-119]am not. But I know that I ought not to stay, as I cannot do what you

[061-120]wish. So, if you please, I will go back to Nuncombe."

[061-121]"You'll not do anything of the kind," said Miss Stanbury.

[061-122]"But it will be better."

[061-123]"Yes, of course; no doubt. I suppose you're tired of us all."

[061-124]"It is not that I'm tired, Aunt Stanbury. It isn't that at all."

[061-125]Dorothy had now become red up to the roots of her hair, and her eyes

[061-126]were full of tears. "But I cannot stay where people think that I

[061-127]am ungrateful. If you please, Aunt Stanbury, I will go." Then, of

[061-128]course, there was a compromise. Dorothy did at last consent to remain

[061-129]in the Close, but only on condition that she should be forgiven for

[061-130]her sin in reference to Mr. Gibson, and be permitted to go on with

[061-131]her aunt's cap.

[061-132]CHAPTER XXXVII.

[061-133]MONT CENIS.

[061-134][Illustration]

[061-135]The night had been fine and warm, and it was now noon on a fine

[061-136]September day when the train from Paris reached St. Michael, on the

[061-137]route to Italy by Mont Cenis,--as all the world knows St. Michael

[061-138]is, or was a year or two back, the end of railway travelling in

[061-139]that direction. At the time Mr. Fell's grand project of carrying a

[061-140]line of rails over the top of the mountain was only in preparation,

[061-141]and the journey from St. Michael to Susa was still made by the

[061-142]diligences,--those dear old continental coaches which are now nearly

[061-143]as extinct as our own, but which did not deserve death so fully as

[061-144]did our abominable vehicles. The coupe of a diligence, or better

[061-145]still, the banquette, was a luxurious mode of travelling as compared

[061-146]with anything that our coaches offered. There used indeed to be a

[061-147]certain halo of glory round the occupant of the box of a mail-coach.

[061-148]The man who had secured that seat was supposed to know something

[061-149]about the world, and to be such a one that the passengers sitting

[061-150]behind him would be proud to be allowed to talk to him. But the

[061-151]prestige of the position was greater than the comfort. A night on

[061-152]the box of a mail-coach was but a bad time, and a night inside a

[061-153]mail-coach was a night in purgatory. Whereas a seat up above, on the

[061-154]banquette of a diligence passing over the Alps, with room for the

[061-155]feet, and support for the back, with plenty of rugs and plenty of

[061-156]tobacco, used to be on the Mont Cenis, and still is on some other

[061-157]mountain passes, a very comfortable mode of seeing a mountain route.

[061-158]For those desirous of occupying the coupe, or the three front seats

[061-159]of the body of the vehicle, it must be admitted that difficulties

[061-160]frequently arose; and that such difficulties were very common at

[061-161]St. Michael. There would be two or three of those enormous vehicles

[061-162]preparing to start for the mountain, whereas it would appear that

[061-163]twelve or fifteen passengers had come down from Paris armed with

[061-164]tickets assuring them that this preferable mode of travelling should

[061-165]be theirs. And then assertions would be made, somewhat recklessly,

[061-166]by the officials, to the effect that all the diligence was coupe.

[061-167]It would generally be the case that some middle-aged Englishman who

[061-168]could not speak French would go to the wall, together with his wife.

[061-169]Middle-aged Englishmen with their wives, who can't speak French, can

[061-170]nevertheless be very angry, and threaten loudly, when they suppose

[061-171]themselves to be ill-treated. A middle-aged Englishman, though he

[061-172]can't speak a word of French, won't believe a French official who

[061-173]tells him that the diligence is all coupe, when he finds himself

[061-174]with his unfortunate partner in a roundabout place behind with two

[061-175]priests, a dirty man who looks like a brigand, a sick maid-servant,

[061-176]and three agricultural labourers. The attempt, however, was

[061-177]frequently made, and thus there used to be occasionally a little

[061-178]noise round the bureau at St. Michael.

[061-179]On the morning of which we are speaking two Englishmen had just made

[061-180]good their claim, each independently of the other, each without

[061-181]having heard or seen the other, when two American ladies, coming up

[061-182]very tardily, endeavoured to prove their rights. The ladies were

[061-183]without other companions, and were not fluent with their French,

[061-184]but were clearly entitled to their seats. They were told that the

[061-185]conveyance was all coupe, but perversely would not believe the

[061-186]statement. The official shrugged his shoulders and signified that

[061-187]his ultimatum had been pronounced. What can an official do in such

[061-188]circumstances, when more coupe passengers are sent to him than the

[061-189]coupes at his command will hold? "But we have paid for the coupe,"

[061-190]said the elder American lady, with considerable indignation, though

[061-191]her French was imperfect;--for American ladies understand their

[061-192]rights. "Bah; yes; you have paid and you shall go. What would you

[061-193]have?" "We would have what we have paid for," said the American lady.

[061-194]Then the official rose from his stool and shrugged his shoulders

[061-195]again, and made a motion with both his hands, intended to shew that

[061-196]the thing was finished. "It is a robbery," said the elder American

[061-197]lady to the younger. "I should not mind, only you are so unwell."

[061-198]"It will not kill me, I dare say," said the younger. Then one of

[061-199]the English gentlemen declared that his place was very much at the

[061-200]service of the invalid,--and the other Englishman declared that his

[061-201]also was at the service of the invalid's companion. Then, and not

[061-202]till then, the two men recognised each other. One was Mr. Glascock,

[061-203]on his way to Naples, and the other was Mr. Trevelyan, on his

[061-204]way,--he knew not whither.
