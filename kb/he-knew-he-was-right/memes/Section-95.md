## Section 95

[095-001]"He may be foolish in this," Dorothy said; "but I don't think you

[095-002]should call him a fool."

[095-003]"I shall call him what I please. I suppose this was going on at the

[095-004]time when you refused Mr. Gibson."

[095-005]"Nothing was going on. Nothing has gone on at all," said Dorothy,

[095-006]with as much indignation as she was able to assume.

[095-007]"How can you tell me that? That is an untruth."

[095-008]"It is not--an untruth," said Dorothy, almost sobbing, but driven at

[095-009]the same time to much anger.

[095-010]"Do you mean to say that this is the first you ever heard of it?" And

[095-011]she held out the letter, shaking it in her thin hand.

[095-012]"I have never said so, Aunt Stanbury."

[095-013]"Yes, you did."

[095-014]"I said that nothing--was--going on, when Mr. Gibson--was--. If you

[095-015]choose to suspect me, Aunt Stanbury, I'll go away. I won't stay here

[095-016]if you suspect me. When Brooke spoke to me, I told him you wouldn't

[095-017]like it."

[095-018]"Of course I don't like it." But she gave no reason why she did not

[095-019]like it.

[095-020]"And there was nothing more till this letter came. I couldn't help

[095-021]his writing to me. It wasn't my fault."

[095-022]"Psha!"

[095-023]"If you are angry, I am very sorry. But you haven't a right to be

[095-024]angry."

[095-025]"Go on, Dorothy; go on. I'm so weak that I can hardly stir myself;

[095-026]it's the first moment that I've been out of my bed for weeks;--and of

[095-027]course you can say what you please. I know what it will be. I shall

[095-028]have to take to my bed again, and then,--in a very little time,--you

[095-029]can both--make fools of yourselves,--just as you like."

[095-030]This was an argument against which Dorothy of course found it to be

[095-031]quite impossible to make continued combat. She could only shuffle her

[095-032]letter back into her pocket, and be, if possible, more assiduous than

[095-033]ever in her attentions to the invalid. She knew that she had been

[095-034]treated most unjustly, and there would be a question to be answered

[095-035]as soon as her aunt should be well as to the possibility of her

[095-036]remaining in the Close subject to such injustice; but let her aunt

[095-037]say what she might, or do what she might, Dorothy could not leave

[095-038]her for the present. Miss Stanbury sat for a considerable time quite

[095-039]motionless, with her eyes closed, and did not stir or make signs of

[095-040]life till Dorothy touched her arm, asking her whether she would not

[095-041]take some broth which had been prepared for her. "Where's Martha? Why

[095-042]does not Martha come?" said Miss Stanbury. This was a hard blow, and

[095-043]from that moment Dorothy believed that it would be expedient that

[095-044]she should return to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. The broth, however, was taken,

[095-045]while Dorothy sat by in silence. Only one word further was said that

[095-046]evening by Miss Stanbury about Brooke and his love affair. "There

[095-047]must be nothing more about this, Dorothy; remember that; nothing at

[095-048]all. I won't have it." Dorothy made no reply. Brooke's letter was in

[095-049]her pocket, and it should be answered that night. On the following

[095-050]day she would let her aunt know what she had said to Brooke. Her aunt

[095-051]should not see the letter, but should be made acquainted with its

[095-052]purport in reference to Brooke's proposal of marriage.

[095-053]"I won't have it!" That had been her aunt's command. What right had

[095-054]her aunt to give any command upon the matter? Then crossed Dorothy's

[095-055]mind, as she thought of this, a glimmering of an idea that no one can

[095-056]be entitled to issue commands who cannot enforce obedience. If Brooke

[095-057]and she chose to become man and wife by mutual consent, how could her

[095-058]aunt prohibit the marriage? Then there followed another idea, that

[095-059]commands are enforced by the threatening and, if necessary, by the

[095-060]enforcement of penalties. Her aunt had within her hand no penalty of

[095-061]which Dorothy was afraid on her own behalf; but she had the power

[095-062]of inflicting a terrible punishment on Brooke Burgess. Now Dorothy

[095-063]conceived that she herself would be the meanest creature alive if she

[095-064]were actuated by fears as to money in her acceptance or rejection of

[095-065]a man whom she loved as she did Brooke Burgess. Brooke had an [[money|income]]

[095-066]of his own which seemed to her to be ample for all purposes. But that

[095-067]which would have been sordid in her, did not seem to her to have any

[095-068]stain of sordidness for him. He was a man, and was bound to be rich

[095-069]if he could. And, moreover, what had she to offer in herself,--such a

[095-070]poor thing as was she,--to make compensation to him for the loss of

[095-071]fortune? Her aunt could inflict this penalty, and therefore the power

[095-072]was hers, and the power must be obeyed. She would write to Brooke in

[095-073]a manner that should convey to him her firm decision. But not the

[095-074]less on that account would she let her aunt know that she thought

[095-075]herself to have been ill-used. It was an insult to her, a most

[095-076]ill-natured insult,--that telling her that Brooke had been a fool

[095-077]for loving her. And then that accusation against her of having been

[095-078]false, of having given one reason for refusing Mr. Gibson, while

[095-079]there was another reason in her heart,--of having been cunning and

[095-080]then untrue, was not to be endured. What would her aunt think of her

[095-081]if she were to bear such allegations without indignant protest? She

[095-082]would write her letter, and speak her mind to her aunt as soon as her

[095-083]aunt should be well enough to hear it.

[095-084]As she had resolved, she wrote her letter that night before she went

[095-085]to bed. She wrote it with floods of tears, and a bitterness of heart

[095-086]which almost conquered her. She too had heard of love, and had been

[095-087]taught to feel that the success or failure of a woman's life depended

[095-088]upon that,--whether she did, or whether she did not, by such gifts

[095-089]as God might have given to her, attract to herself some man strong

[095-090]enough, and good enough, and loving enough to make straight for her

[095-091]her paths, to bear for her her burdens, to be the father of her

[095-092]children, the staff on which she might lean, and the wall against

[095-093]which she might grow, feeling the sunshine, and sheltered from the

[095-094]wind. She had ever estimated her own value so lowly as to have told

[095-095]herself often that such success could never come in her way. From her

[095-096]earliest years she had regarded herself as outside the pale within

[095-097]which such joys are to be found. She had so strictly taught herself

[095-098]to look forward to a blank existence, that she had learned to do so

[095-099]without active misery. But not the less did she know where happiness

[095-100]lay; and when the good thing came almost within her reach, when it

[095-101]seemed that God had given her gifts which might have sufficed, when

[095-102]a man had sought her hand whose nature was such that she could have

[095-103]leaned on him with a true worship, could have grown against him as

[095-104]against a wall with perfect confidence, could have lain with her head

[095-105]upon his bosom, and have felt that of all spots that in the world was

[095-106]the most fitting for her,--when this was all but grasped, and must

[095-107]yet be abandoned, there came upon her spirit an agony so bitter

[095-108]that she had not before known how great might be the depth of human

[095-109]disappointment. But the letter was at last written, and when finished

[095-110]was as follows:--

[095-111]The Close, [[exeter|Exeter]], March 1, 186--.

[095-112]DEAR BROOKE,

[095-113]There had been many doubts about this; but at last they were

[095-114]conquered, and the name was written.

[095-115]I have shown your letter to my aunt, as I am sure you will

[095-116]think was best. I should have answered it before, only

[095-117]that I thought that she was not quite well enough to talk

[095-118]about it. She says, as I was sure she would, that what you

[095-119]propose is quite out of the question. I am aware that I

[095-120]am bound to obey her; and as I think that you also ought

[095-121]to do so, I shall think no more of what you have said to

[095-122]me and have written. It is quite impossible now, even if

[095-123]it might have been possible under other circumstances. I

[095-124]shall always remember your great kindness to me. Perhaps

[095-125]I ought to say that I am very grateful for the compliment

[095-126]you have paid me. I shall think of you always;--till I

[095-127]die.

[095-128]Believe me to be,

[095-129]Your very sincere friend,

[095-130]DOROTHY STANBURY.

[095-131]The next day Miss Stanbury again came out of her room, and on the

[095-132]third day she was manifestly becoming stronger. Dorothy had as yet

[095-133]not spoken of her letter, but was prepared to do so as soon as she

[095-134]thought that a fitting opportunity had come. She had a word or two to

[095-135]say for herself; but she must not again subject herself to being told

[095-136]that she was taking her will of her aunt because her aunt was too ill

[095-137]to defend herself. But on the third day Miss Stanbury herself asked

[095-138]the question. "Have you written anything to Brooke?" she asked.

[095-139]"I have answered his letter, Aunt Stanbury."

[095-140]"And what have you said to him?"

[095-141]"I have told him that you disapproved of it, and that nothing more

[095-142]must be said about it."

[095-143]"Yes;--of course you made me out to be an ogre."

[095-144]"I don't know what you mean by that, aunt. I am sure that I told him

[095-145]the truth."

[095-146]"May I see the letter?"

[095-147]"It has gone."

[095-148]"But you have kept a copy," said Miss Stanbury.

[095-149]"Yes; I have got a copy," replied Dorothy; "but I would rather not

[095-150]shew it. I told him just what I tell you."

[095-151]"Dorothy, it is not at all becoming that you should have a

[095-152]correspondence with any young man of such a nature that you should be

[095-153]ashamed to shew it to your aunt."

[095-154]"I am not ashamed of anything," said Dorothy sturdily.

[095-155]"I don't know what young women in these days have come to," continued

[095-156]Miss Stanbury. "There is no respect, no subjection, no obedience, and

[095-157]too often--no modesty."

[095-158]"Does that mean me, Aunt Stanbury?" asked Dorothy.

[095-159]"To tell you the truth, Dorothy, I don't think you ought to have

[095-160]been receiving love-letters from Brooke Burgess when I was lying ill

[095-161]in bed. I didn't expect it of you. I tell you fairly that I didn't

[095-162]expect it of you."

[095-163]Then Dorothy spoke out her mind. "As you think that, Aunt Stanbury,

[095-164]I had better go away. And if you please I will,--when you are well

[095-165]enough to spare me."

[095-166]"Pray don't think of me at all," said her aunt.

[095-167]"And as for love-letters,--Mr. Burgess has written to me once. I

[095-168]don't think that there can be anything immodest in opening a letter

[095-169]when it comes by the post. And as soon as I had it I determined to

[095-170]shew it to you. As for what happened before, when Mr. Burgess spoke

[095-171]to me, which was long, long after all that about Mr. Gibson was over,

[095-172]I told him that it couldn't be so; and I thought there would be no

[095-173]more about it. You were so ill that I could not tell you. Now you

[095-174]know it all."

[095-175]"I have not seen your letter to him."

[095-176]"I shall never shew it to anybody. But you have said things, Aunt

[095-177]Stanbury, that are very cruel."

[095-178]"Of course! Everything I say is wrong."

[095-179]"You have told me that I was telling untruths, and you have called

[095-180]me--immodest. That is a terrible word."

[095-181]"You shouldn't deserve it then."

[095-182]"I never have deserved it, and I won't bear it. No; I won't. If Hugh

[095-183]heard me called that word, I believe he'd tear the house down."
