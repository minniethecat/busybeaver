## Section 105

[105-001]"He is son of the lady we were with at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]," she

[105-002]whispered to her father as she got up to move across the room to

[105-003]welcome her lover. Now Sir Marmaduke had expressed great disapproval

[105-004]of that retreat to Dartmoor, and had only understood respecting it

[105-005]that it had been arranged between Trevelyan and the family in whose

[105-006]custody his two daughters had been sent away into banishment. He

[105-007]was not therefore specially disposed to welcome [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] in

[105-008]consequence of this mode of introduction.

[105-009]Hugh, who had asked for Lady Rowley and Mrs. Trevelyan and had

[105-010]learned that they were out before he had mentioned Miss Rowley's

[105-011]name, was almost prepared to take his sweetheart into his arms. In

[105-012]that half-minute he had taught himself to expect that he would meet

[105-013]her alone, and had altogether forgotten Sir Marmaduke. Young men

[105-014]when they call at four o'clock in the day never expect to find papas

[105-015]at home. And of Sophia and Lucy he had either heard nothing or had

[105-016]forgotten what he had heard. He repressed himself however in time,

[105-017]and did not commit either Nora or himself by any very vehement

[105-018]demonstration of affection. But he did hold her hand longer than he

[105-019]should have done, and Sir Marmaduke saw that he did so.

[105-020]"This is papa," said Nora. "Papa, this is our friend, Mr. Hugh

[105-021]Stanbury." The introduction was made in a manner almost absurdly

[105-022]formal, but poor Nora's difficulties lay heavy upon her. Sir

[105-023]Marmaduke muttered something;--but it was little more than a grunt.

[105-024]"Mamma and Emily are out," continued Nora. "I dare say they will be

[105-025]in soon." Sir Marmaduke looked round sharply at the man. Why was he

[105-026]to be encouraged to stay till Lady Rowley should return? Lady Rowley

[105-027]did not want to see him. It seemed to Sir Marmaduke, in the midst of

[105-028]his troubles, that this was no time to be making new acquaintances.

[105-029]"These are my sisters, Mr. Stanbury," continued Nora. "This is

[105-030]Sophia, and this is Lucy." Sophia and Lucy would have been thoroughly

[105-031]willing to receive their sister's lover with genial kindness if they

[105-032]had been properly instructed, and if the time had been opportune;

[105-033]but, as it was, they had nothing to say. They, also, could only

[105-034]mutter some little sound intended to be more courteous than their

[105-035]father's grunt. Poor Nora!

[105-036]"I hope you are comfortable here," said Hugh.

[105-037]"The house is all very well," said Nora, "but we don't like the

[105-038]neighbourhood."

[105-039]Hugh also felt that conversation was difficult. He had soon come to

[105-040]perceive,--before he had been in the room half a minute,--that the

[105-041]atmosphere was not favourable to his mission. There was to be no

[105-042]embracing or permission for embracing on the present occasion. Had he

[105-043]been left alone with Sir Marmaduke he would probably have told his

[105-044]business plainly, let Sir Marmaduke's manner to him have been what

[105-045]it might; but it was impossible for him to do this with three young

[105-046]ladies in the room with him. Seeing that Nora was embarrassed by

[105-047]her difficulties, and that Nora's father was cross and silent, he

[105-048]endeavoured to talk to the other girls, and asked them concerning

[105-049]their journey and the ship in which they had come. But it was very

[105-050]up-hill work. Lucy and Sophy could talk as glibly as any young

[105-051]ladies home from any colony,--and no higher degree of fluency can

[105-052]be expressed;--but now they were cowed. Their elder sister was

[105-053]shamefully and most undeservedly disgraced, and this man had had

[105-054]something,--they knew not what,--to do with it. "Is Priscilla quite

[105-055]well?" Nora asked at last.

[105-056]"Quite well. I heard from her yesterday. You know they have left the

[105-057][[clock-house|Clock House]]."

[105-058]"I had not heard it."

[105-059]"Oh yes;--and they are living in a small cottage just outside the

[105-060]village. And what else do you think has happened?"

[105-061]"Nothing bad, I hope, Mr. Stanbury."

[105-062]"My sister Dorothy has left her aunt, and is living with them again

[105-063]at Nuncombe."

[105-064]"Has there been a quarrel, Mr. Stanbury?"

[105-065]"Well, yes;--after a fashion there has, I suppose. But it is a long

[105-066]story and would not interest Sir Marmaduke. The wonder is that

[105-067]Dorothy should have been able to stay so long with my aunt. I will

[105-068]tell it you all some day." Sir Marmaduke could not understand why

[105-069]a long story about this man's aunt and sister should be told to

[105-070]his daughter. He forgot,--as men always do in such circumstances

[105-071]forget,--that, while he was living in the Mandarins, his daughter,

[105-072]living in England, would of course pick up new interest and become

[105-073]intimate with new histories. But he did not forget that pressure

[105-074]of the hand which he had seen, and he determined that his daughter

[105-075]Nora could not have any worse lover than the friend of his elder

[105-076]daughter's husband.

[105-077]Stanbury had just determined that he must go, that there was no

[105-078]possibility for him either to say or do anything to promote his cause

[105-079]at the present moment, when the circumstances were all changed by the

[105-080]return home of Lady Rowley and Mrs. Trevelyan. Lady Rowley knew, and

[105-081]had for some days known, much more of Stanbury than had come to the

[105-082]ears of Sir Marmaduke. She understood in the first place that the

[105-083]Stanburys had been very good to her daughter, and she was aware that

[105-084]Hugh Stanbury had thoroughly taken her daughter's part against his

[105-085]old friend Trevelyan. She would therefore have been prepared to

[105-086]receive him kindly had he not on this very morning been the subject

[105-087]of special conversation between her and Emily. But, as it had

[105-088]happened, Mrs. Trevelyan had this very day told Lady Rowley the

[105-089]whole story of Nora's love. The elder sister had not intended to be

[105-090]treacherous to the younger; but in the thorough confidence which

[105-091]mutual grief and close conference had created between the mother

[105-092]and daughter, everything had at last come out, and Lady Rowley had

[105-093]learned the story, not only of Hugh Stanbury's courtship, but of

[105-094]those rich offers which had been made by the heir to the barony of

[105-095]Peterborough.

[105-096]It must be acknowledged that Lady Rowley was greatly grieved and

[105-097]thoroughly dismayed. It was not only that Mr. Glascock was the eldest

[105-098]son of a peer, but that he was represented by the poor suffering wife

[105-099]of the ill-tempered man to be a man blessed with a disposition sweet

[105-100]as an angel's. "And she would have liked him," Emily had said, "if

[105-101]it had not been for this unfortunate young man." Lady Rowley was not

[105-102]worse than are other mothers, not more ambitious, or more heartless,

[105-103]or more worldly. She was a good mother, loving her children, and

[105-104]thoroughly anxious for their welfare. But she would have liked to

[105-105]be the mother-in-law of Lord Peterborough, and she would have liked,

[105-106]dearly, to see her second daughter removed from the danger of those

[105-107]rocks against which her eldest child had been shipwrecked. And when

[105-108]she asked after Hugh Stanbury, and his means of maintaining a wife,

[105-109]the statement which Mrs. Trevelyan made was not comforting. "He

[105-110]writes for a penny newspaper,--and, I believe, writes very well,"

[105-111]Mrs. Trevelyan had said.

[105-112]"For a penny newspaper! Is that respectable?"

[105-113]"His aunt, Miss Stanbury, seemed to think not. But I suppose men of

[105-114]education do write for such things now. He says himself that it is

[105-115]very precarious as an employment."

[105-116]"It must be precarious, Emily. And has he got nothing?"

[105-117]"Not a penny of his own," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[105-118]Then Lady Rowley had thought again of Mr. Glascock, and of the family

[105-119]title, and of Monkhams. And she thought of her present troubles, and

[105-120]of the Mandarins, and the state of Sir Marmaduke's balance at the

[105-121]bankers;--and of the other girls, and of all there was before her to

[105-122]do. Here had been a very Apollo among suitors kneeling at her child's

[105-123]feet, and the foolish girl had sent him away for the sake of a young

[105-124]man who wrote for a penny newspaper! Was it worth the while of

[105-125]any woman to bring up daughters with such results? Lady Rowley,

[105-126]therefore, when she was first introduced to Hugh Stanbury, was not

[105-127]prepared to receive him with open arms.

[105-128]On this occasion the task of introducing him fell to Mrs. Trevelyan,

[105-129]and was done with much graciousness. Emily knew that Hugh Stanbury

[105-130]was her friend, and would sympathise with her respecting her child.

[105-131]"You have heard what has happened to me?" she said. Stanbury,

[105-132]however, had heard nothing of that kidnapping of the child. Though

[105-133]to the Rowleys it seemed that such a deed of iniquity, done in the

[105-134]middle of [[london|London]], must have been known to all the world, he had not

[105-135]as yet been told of it;--and now the story was given to him. Mrs.

[105-136]Trevelyan herself told it, with many tears and an agony of fresh

[105-137]grief; but still she told it as to one whom she regarded as a sure

[105-138]friend, and from whom she knew that she would receive sympathy. Sir

[105-139]Marmaduke sat by the while, still gloomy and out of humour. Why was

[105-140]their family sorrow to be laid bare to this stranger?

[105-141]"It is the cruellest thing I ever heard," said Hugh.

[105-142]"A dastardly deed," said Lady Rowley.

[105-143]"But we all feel that for the time he can hardly know what he does,"

[105-144]said Nora.

[105-145]"And where is the child?" Stanbury asked.

[105-146]"We have not the slightest idea," said Lady Rowley. "I have seen him,

[105-147]and he refuses to tell us. He did say that my daughter should see her

[105-148]boy; but he now accompanies his offer with such conditions that it is

[105-149]impossible to listen to him."

[105-150]"And where is he?"

[105-151]"We do not know where he lives. We can reach him only through a

[105-152]certain man--"

[105-153]"Ah, I know the man," said Stanbury; "one who was a policeman once.

[105-154]His name is Bozzle."

[105-155]"That is the man," said Sir Marmaduke. "I have seen him."

[105-156]"And of course he will tell us nothing but what he is told to tell

[105-157]us," continued Lady Rowley. "Can there be anything so horrible as

[105-158]this,--that a wife should be bound to communicate with her own

[105-159]husband respecting her own child through such a man as that?"

[105-160]"One might possibly find out where he keeps the child," said Hugh.

[105-161]"If you could manage that, Mr. Stanbury!" said Lady Rowley.

[105-162]"I hardly see that it would do much good," said Hugh. "Indeed I do

[105-163]not know why he should keep the place a secret. I suppose he has a

[105-164]right to the boy until the mother shall have made good her claim

[105-165]before the court." He promised, however, that he would do his best to

[105-166]ascertain where the child was kept, and where Trevelyan resided, and

[105-167]then,--having been nearly an hour at the house,--he was forced to

[105-168]get up and take his leave. He had said not a word to any one of the

[105-169]business that had brought him there. He had not even whispered an

[105-170]assurance of his affection to Nora. Till the two elder ladies had

[105-171]come in, and the subject of the taking of the boy had been mooted, he

[105-172]had sat there as a perfect stranger. He thought that it was manifest

[105-173]enough that Nora had told her secret to no one. It seemed to him

[105-174]that Mrs. Trevelyan must have forgotten it;--that Nora herself must

[105-175]have forgotten it, if such forgetting could be possible! He got up,

[105-176]however, and took his leave, and was comforted in some slight degree

[105-177]by seeing that there was a tear in Nora's eye.

[105-178]"Who is he?" demanded Sir Marmaduke, as soon as the door was closed.

[105-179]"He is a young man who was an intimate friend of Louis's," answered

[105-180]Mrs. Trevelyan; "but he is so no longer, because he sees how

[105-181]infatuated Louis has been."
