# Colonial Governance

The colonial backdrop of *He Knew He Was Right*, represented by Sir Rowley's governorship of the Mandarin Islands. Though peripheral to the main plot, the colonial context illuminates the novel's treatment of authority, masculinity, and the relationship between public office and private conduct.

## Overview

Sir Rowley, Emily and Nora's father, serves as governor of the fictional Mandarin Islands — a colonial posting that provides the family's social status but not its wealth. The colonial context is mentioned only briefly but carries significant thematic weight: Sir Rowley's position as a colonial administrator makes him a figure of authority abroad, yet at home he is financially constrained and unable to protect his daughter from her husband's tyranny. The novel thus establishes a subtle contrast between public authority and private powerlessness (001-157).

## Context in Victorian Society

The British Empire was at its height when Trollope wrote, and colonial service was a recognized career path for gentlemen of modest means. A colonial governorship conferred status without necessarily conferring wealth — precisely Sir Rowley's situation. Trollope himself traveled extensively in the colonies and wrote about them, bringing an insider's knowledge to this aspect of the novel. The Mandarin Islands, as a fictional colony, allow Trollope to comment on colonial administration without engaging in specific political controversy.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Public Office, Private Life

The novel's colonial dimension operates primarily as background, but it shapes character in significant ways. Sir Rowley's absence in the colonies means Emily lacks paternal protection during her marital crisis. His colonial authority abroad contrasts with his inability to influence events at home.

### Empire and Masculinity

Trevelyan's domestic tyranny and Sir Rowley's colonial governance represent two faces of Victorian masculine authority — one exercised illegitimately in the private sphere, the other exercised legitimately in the public sphere. The novel's implicit critique is that the same culture of masculine entitlement that justified colonial rule also enabled domestic tyranny.

## Key Examples

### The Mandarin Islands

The fictional Mandarin Islands serve as a blank canvas on which Trollope projects questions about governance, authority, and the relationship between metropole and periphery. Sir Rowley cannot "save money in the islands" (001-163), suggesting the economic limitations of colonial service despite its social prestige.
