# Mrs. Stanbury

Mrs. Stanbury is the widowed mother of Hugh, Priscilla, and Dorothy Stanbury. The widow of the late vicar of Nuncombe Putney, she lives in reduced but respectable circumstances, managing her household with quiet competence and providing a stable domestic anchor for her children.

## Overview

"Mrs. Stanbury, the widow of the late vicar" (013-160), lives at Nuncombe Putney with her daughters. Her circumstances are modest — the family has lost the income and status attached to the vicarage — but her household is characterised by order, affection, and a refusal of self-pity. When Miss Stanbury writes to request one of the girls, it is Mrs. Stanbury who must negotiate between her imperious sister-in-law's demands and her children's interests (013-071).

## Role in the Novel

Mrs. Stanbury functions as a moral baseline: she represents the kind of unassuming, dutiful domesticity that Trollope consistently values. Unlike the drama of the Trevelyan household or the tension of Miss Stanbury's Exeter establishment, Mrs. Stanbury's cottage at Nuncombe Putney is a place of quiet stability. Her children — even the sharp-tongued Priscilla — orbit around her with evident loyalty.

## Relationships

### Hugh Stanbury

Mrs. Stanbury's relationship with her son Hugh is affectionate but marked by the distance created by his London career. "He has been very good to Hugh," she says of a benefactor (014-005), revealing the maternal anxiety that underlies her placid exterior.

### Miss Stanbury

As sister-in-law to the formidable Miss Stanbury, Mrs. Stanbury occupies a position of structural subordination. Miss Stanbury's wealth and imperiousness give her the upper hand; Mrs. Stanbury's responses are invariably tactful and measured (013-136).

## Key Scenes

### Miss Stanbury's Letter

When Miss Stanbury writes to request one of the Stanbury daughters, Mrs. Stanbury must balance competing obligations. Her response — sending Dorothy — is both dutiful and pragmatic (013-098).

## Character Development

Mrs. Stanbury is a static character whose consistency is her virtue. She does not develop because she is already fully formed: a woman who has absorbed loss without bitterness and who manages diminished circumstances without complaint.
