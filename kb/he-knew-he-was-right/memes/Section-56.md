## Section 56

[056-001]Our friend Hugh, as he sat smoking on the knife-board of the omnibus,

[056-002]determined that he would risk everything. If it were ordained that

[056-003]prudence should prevail, the prudence should be hers. Why should he

[056-004]take upon himself to have prudence enough for two, seeing that she

[056-005]was so very discreet in all her bearings? Then he remembered the

[056-006]touch of her hand, which he still felt upon his palm as he sat

[056-007]handling his pipe, and he told himself that after that he was bound

[056-008]to say a word more. And moreover he confessed to himself that he was

[056-009]compelled by a feeling that mastered him altogether. He could not get

[056-010]through an hour's work without throwing down his pen and thinking of

[056-011][[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. It was his destiny to love her,--and there was, to his

[056-012]mind, a mean, pettifogging secrecy, amounting almost to daily lying,

[056-013]in his thus loving her and not telling her that he loved her. It

[056-014]might well be that she should rebuke him; but he thought that he

[056-015]could bear that. It might well be that he had altogether mistaken

[056-016]that touch of her hand. After all it had been the slightest possible

[056-017]motion of no more than one finger. But he would at any rate know the

[056-018]truth. If she would tell him at once that she did not care for him,

[056-019]he thought that he could get over it; but life was not worth having

[056-020]while he lived in this shifty, dubious, and uncomfortable state. So

[056-021]he made up his mind that he would go to St. Diddulph's with his heart

[056-022]in his hand.

[056-023]In the mean time, Mr. Bozzle had been twice to St. Diddulph's;--and

[056-024]now he made a third journey there, two days after Stanbury's visit.

[056-025]Trevelyan, who, in truth, hated the sight of the man, and who

[056-026]suffered agonies in his presence, had, nevertheless, taught himself

[056-027]to believe that he could not live without his assistance. That it

[056-028]should be so was a part of the cruelty of his lot. Who else was there

[056-029]that he could trust? His wife had renewed her intimacy with Colonel

[056-030]Osborne the moment that she had left him. Mrs. Stanbury, who had been

[056-031]represented to him as the most correct of matrons, had at once been

[056-032]false to him and to her trust, in allowing [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] to enter

[056-033]her house. Mr. and Mrs. Outhouse, with whom his wife had now located

[056-034]herself, not by his orders, were, of course, his enemies. His old

[056-035]friend, [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], had gone over to the other side, and had

[056-036]quarrelled with him purposely, with malice prepense, because he would

[056-037]not submit himself to the caprices of the wife who had injured him.

[056-038]His own lawyer had refused to act for him; and his fast and oldest

[056-039]ally, the very person who had sounded in his ear the earliest warning

[056-040]note against that odious villain, whose daily work it was to destroy

[056-041]the peace of families,--even Lady Milborough had turned against him!

[056-042]Because he would not follow the stupid prescription which she, with

[056-043]pig-headed obstinacy, persisted in giving,--because he would not

[056-044]carry his wife off to Naples,--she was ill-judging and inconsistent

[056-045]enough to tell him that he was wrong! Who was then left to him but

[056-046]Bozzle? Bozzle was very disagreeable. Bozzle said things, and made

[056-047]suggestions to him which were as bad as pins stuck into his flesh.

[056-048]But Bozzle was true to his employer, and could find out facts. Had

[056-049]it not been for Bozzle, he would have known nothing of the Colonel's

[056-050]journey to Devonshire. Had it not been for Bozzle, he would never

[056-051]have heard of the correspondence; and, therefore, when he left

[056-052][[london|London]], he gave Bozzle a roving commission; and when he went to

[056-053]Paris, and from Paris onwards, over the Alps into Italy, he furnished

[056-054]Bozzle with his address. At this time, in the midst of all his

[056-055]misery, it never occurred to him to inquire of himself whether

[056-056]it might be possible that his old friends were right, and that

[056-057]he himself was wrong. From morning to night he sang to himself

[056-058]melancholy silent songs of inward wailing, as to the cruelty of his

[056-059]own lot in life;--and, in the mean time, he employed Bozzle to find

[056-060]out for him how far that cruelty was carried.

[056-061]Mr. Bozzle was, of course, convinced that the lady whom he was

[056-062]employed to watch was--no better than she ought to be. That is the

[056-063]usual Bozzlian language for broken vows, secrecy, intrigue, dirt, and

[056-064]adultery. It was his business to obtain [[truth|evidence]] of her guilt. There

[056-065]was no question to be solved as to her innocency. The Bozzlian mind

[056-066]would have regarded any such suggestion as the product of a green

[056-067]softness, the possession of which would have made him quite unfit for

[056-068]his profession. He was aware that ladies who are no better than they

[056-069]should be are often very clever,--so clever, as to make it necessary

[056-070]that the Bozzles who shall at last confound them should be first-rate

[056-071]Bozzles, Bozzles quite at the top of their profession,--and,

[056-072]therefore, he went about his work with great industry and much

[056-073]caution. Colonel Osborne was at the present moment in Scotland.

[056-074]Bozzle was sure of that. He was quite in the north of Scotland.

[056-075]Bozzle had examined his map, and had found that Wick, which was the

[056-076]Colonel's post-town, was very far north indeed. He had half a mind to

[056-077]run down to Wick, as he was possessed by a certain honest zeal, which

[056-078]made him long to do something hard and laborious; but his experience

[056-079]told him that it was very easy for the Colonel to come up to the

[056-080]neighbourhood of St. Diddulph's, whereas the lady could not go down

[056-081]to Wick, unless she were to decide upon throwing herself into her

[056-082]lover's arms,--whereby Bozzle's work would be brought to an end. He,

[056-083]therefore, confined his immediate operations to St. Diddulph's.

[056-084]He made acquaintance with one or two important persons in and about

[056-085]Mr. Outhouse's parsonage. He became very familiar with the postman.

[056-086]He arranged terms of intimacy, I am sorry to say, with the housemaid;

[056-087]and, on the third journey, he made an alliance with the potboy at the

[056-088]Full Moon. The potboy remembered well the fact of the child being

[056-089]brought to "our 'ouse," as he called the Full Moon; and he was

[056-090]enabled to say, that the same "gent as had brought the boy backards

[056-091]and forrards," had since that been at the parsonage. But Bozzle

[056-092]was quite quick enough to perceive that all this had nothing to do

[056-093]with the Colonel. He was led, indeed, to fear that his "governor,"

[056-094]as he was in the habit of calling Trevelyan in his half-spoken

[056-095]soliloquies,--that his governor was not as true to him as he was to

[056-096]his governor. What business had that meddling fellow Stanbury at St.

[056-097]Diddulph's?--for Trevelyan had not thought it necessary to tell his

[056-098]satellite that he had quarrelled with his friend. Bozzle was grieved

[056-099]in his mind when he learned that Stanbury's interference was still

[056-100]to be dreaded; and wrote to his governor, rather severely, to

[056-101]that effect; but, when so writing, he was able to give no further

[056-102]information. Facts, in such cases, will not unravel themselves

[056-103]without much patience on the part of the investigators.

[056-104]CHAPTER XXXIV.

[056-105]PRISCILLA'S WISDOM.

[056-106][Illustration]

[056-107]On the night after the dinner party in the Close, Dorothy was not the

[056-108]only person in the house who laid awake thinking of what had taken

[056-109]place. Miss Stanbury also was full of anxiety, and for hour after

[056-110]hour could not sleep as she remembered the fruitlessness of her

[056-111]efforts on behalf of her nephew and niece.

[056-112]It had never occurred to her, when she had first proposed to herself

[056-113]that Dorothy should become Mrs. Gibson that Dorothy herself would

[056-114]have any objection to such a step in life. Her fear had been that

[056-115]Dorothy would have become over-radiant with triumph at the idea of

[056-116]having a husband, and going to that husband with a [[money|fortune]] of her

[056-117]own. That Mr. Gibson might hesitate she had thought very likely.

[056-118]It is thus in general that women regard the feelings, desires, and

[056-119]aspirations of other women. You will hardly ever meet an elderly lady

[056-120]who will not speak of her juniors as living in a state of breathless

[056-121]anxiety to catch husbands. And the elder lady will speak of

[056-122]the younger as though any kind of choice in such catching was

[056-123]quite disregarded. The man must be a [[social-class|gentleman]],--or, at least,

[056-124]gentlemanlike,--and there must be bread. Let these things be given,

[056-125]and what girl won't jump into what man's arms? Female reader, is it

[056-126]not thus that the elders of your sex speak of the younger? When old

[056-127]Mrs. Stanbury heard that Nora Rowley had refused Mr. Glascock, the

[056-128]thing was to her unintelligible; and it was now quite unintelligible

[056-129]to Miss Stanbury that Dorothy should prefer a single life to

[056-130][[marriage|matrimony]] with Mr. Gibson.

[056-131]It must be acknowledged, on Aunt Stanbury's behalf, that Dorothy was

[056-132]one of those yielding, hesitating, submissive young women, trusting

[056-133]others, but doubting ever of themselves, as to whom it is natural

[056-134]that their stronger friends should find it expedient to decide for

[056-135]them. Miss Stanbury was almost justified in thinking that unless she

[056-136]were to find a husband for her niece, her niece would never find

[056-137]one for herself. Dorothy would drift into being an old maid, like

[056-138]Priscilla, simply because she would never assert herself,--never

[056-139]put her best foot foremost. Aunt Stanbury had therefore taken upon

[056-140]herself to put out a foot; and having carefully found that Mr.

[056-141]Gibson was "willing," had conceived that all difficulties were over.

[056-142]She would be enabled to do her duty by her niece, and establish

[056-143]comfortably in life, at any rate, one of her brother's children. And

[056-144]now Dorothy was taking upon herself to say that she did not like

[056-145]the gentleman! Such conduct was almost equal to writing for a penny

[056-146]newspaper!

[056-147]On the following morning, after breakfast, when Brooke Burgess was

[056-148]gone out to call upon his uncle,--which he insisted upon doing

[056-149]openly, and not under the rose, in spite of Miss Stanbury's great

[056-150]gravity on the occasion,--there was a very serious conversation, and

[056-151]poor Dorothy had found herself to be almost silenced. She did argue

[056-152]for a time; but her arguments seemed, even to herself, to amount to

[056-153]so little! Why shouldn't she love Mr. Gibson? That was a question

[056-154]which she found it impossible to answer. And though she did not

[056-155]actually yield, though she did not say that she would accept the man,

[056-156]still, when she was told that three days were to be allowed to her

[056-157]for consideration, and that then the offer would be made to her

[056-158]in form, she felt that, as regarded the anti-Gibson interest, she

[056-159]had not a leg to stand upon. Why should not such an insignificant

[056-160]creature, as was she, love Mr. Gibson,--or any other man who had

[056-161]bread to give her, and was in some degree like a gentleman? On that

[056-162]night, she wrote the following letter to her sister:--

[056-163]The Close, Tuesday.

[056-164]DEAREST PRISCILLA,

[056-165]I do so wish that you could be with me, so that I could

[056-166]talk to you again. Aunt Stanbury is the most affectionate

[056-167]and kindest friend in the world; but she has always been

[056-168]so able to have her own way, because she is both clever

[056-169]and good, that I find myself almost like a baby with her.

[056-170]She has been talking to me again about Mr. Gibson; and it

[056-171]seems that Mr. Gibson really does mean it. It is certainly

[056-172]very strange; but I do think now that it is true. He is to

[056-173]come on Friday. It seems very odd that it should all be

[056-174]settled for him in that way; but then Aunt Stanbury is so

[056-175]clever at settling things!
