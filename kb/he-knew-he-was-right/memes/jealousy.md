# Jealousy

**Jealousy** is the central destructive force in [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869), examined with a psychological sophistication unusual for Victorian fiction. The novel traces [[Louis Trevelyan]]'s jealous obsession with clinical precision — from its first stirrings through its escalation into full-blown [[Madness|madness]] — arguing implicitly that pathological jealousy is not a moral failing but a disease of the mind. The title's bitter irony crystallizes the theme: Trevelyan's tragedy is that *he knows he is right*; his jealousy feels to him not like delusion but like clarity.

## Jealousy as Pathology

Trollope's treatment of jealousy departs significantly from the conventions of Victorian fiction. Rather than presenting it as a sign of villainy or moral weakness, the novel treats Trevelyan's jealousy as an illness — something that happens *to* him as much as something he chooses. The progression is charted with almost medical precision:

1. **Inception** (Chapters 2–3): Minor irritation at [[Colonel Osborne]]'s visits; the jealousy is still corrigible
2. **Fixation** (Chapters 4–6): Trevelyan becomes "fixed in his resolution"; reason no longer reaches him
3. **Public rupture** (Chapters 6–11): The jealousy drives the [[The Trevelyan Separation|separation]]; private obsession becomes public scandal
4. **Escalation** (Chapters 64–85): Abduction of his son; flight to Italy; increasing isolation
5. **Terminal phase** (Chapters 86–112): [[Madness]] and death; the jealousy has consumed its host entirely

## The Mechanism of Suspicion

The novel is unusually astute about the psychology of jealous suspicion — how it selects evidence, discounts counter-evidence, and constructs a self-reinforcing narrative. Trevelyan does not invent his jealousy from nothing; he fastens onto real facts (Osborne's visits are frequent, Emily is friendly) and interprets them through a distorted lens. His tragedy is that his interpretation feels to him like insight, not delusion:

> Louis Trevelyan having taken a strong dislike to Colonel Osborne, and having failed to make his wife share that dislike, became uneasy. [002-113]

The phrase "failed to make his wife share that dislike" is key: Trevelyan experiences Emily's emotional autonomy as a betrayal. His jealousy is, at bottom, a demand for emotional conformity — a refusal to tolerate that his wife's inner life is not identical to his own.

## Victorian Context

Jealousy in the Victorian period was entangled with specific legal and social realities. A husband's jealousy had legal teeth: he controlled his wife's property, movements, and social contacts. Trevelyan's jealousy is not merely an emotion but a claim of *right* — he believes he is entitled to dictate his wife's friendships, and the law largely supports him. The novel's critique is thus both psychological and social: a society that gives husbands near-absolute power over wives makes pathological jealousy lethal.

## Contrast with Healthy Relationships

Trollope uses contrasting couples to show what an absence of jealousy looks like:

- [[Hugh Stanbury]] never doubts [[Nora Rowley]]'s fidelity, even when circumstances separate them
- [[Dorothy Stanbury]] and Brooke Burgess build their relationship on trust, not suspicion

These counterpoints reinforce the novel's argument that jealousy is not a natural or inevitable dimension of love, but a pathology that destroys it.
