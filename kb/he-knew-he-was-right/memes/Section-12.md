## Section 12

[012-001]Miss Stanbury belonged to the county set, but she lived in a large

[012-002]brick house, [[reputation|standing]] in the Close, almost behind the Cathedral.

[012-003]Indeed it was so close to the eastern end of the edifice that a

[012-004]carriage could not be brought quite up to her door. It was a large

[012-005]brick house, very old, with a door in the middle, and five steps

[012-006]ascending to it between high iron rails. On each side of the door

[012-007]there were two windows on the ground floor, and above that there

[012-008]were three tiers of five windows each, and the house was double

[012-009]throughout, having as many windows looking out behind into a gloomy

[012-010]courtyard. But the glory of the house consisted in this, that there

[012-011]was a garden attached to it, a garden with very high walls, over

[012-012]which the boughs of trees might be seen, giving to the otherwise

[012-013]gloomy abode a touch of freshness in the summer, and a look of space

[012-014]in the winter, which no doubt added something to the [[honour|reputation]] even

[012-015]of Miss Stanbury. The fact,--for it was a fact,--that there was

[012-016]no gloomier or less attractive spot in the whole city than Miss

[012-017]Stanbury's garden, when seen inside, did not militate against this

[012-018]advantage. There were but half-a-dozen trees, and a few square yards

[012-019]of grass that was never green, and a damp ungravelled path on which

[012-020]no one ever walked. Seen from the inside the garden was not much;

[012-021]but, from the outside, it gave a distinct [[reputation|character]] to the house, and

[012-022]produced an unexpressed acknowledgment that the owner of it ought to

[012-023]belong to the county set.

[012-024]The house and all that was in it belonged to Miss Stanbury herself,

[012-025]as did also many other houses in the neighbourhood. She was the owner

[012-026]of the "Cock and Bottle," a very decent second [[social-class|class]] inn on the other

[012-027]side of the Close, an inn supposed to have clerical tendencies, which

[012-028]made it quite suitable for a close. The choristers took their beer

[012-029]there, and the landlord was a retired verger. Nearly the whole of

[012-030]one side of a dark passage leading out of the Close towards the High

[012-031]Street belonged to her; and though the passage be narrow and the

[012-032]houses dark, the locality is known to be good for trade. And she

[012-033]owned two large houses in the High Street, and a great warehouse

[012-034]at St. Thomas's, and had been bought out of land by the Railway at

[012-035]St. David's,--much to her own dissatisfaction, as she was wont to

[012-036]express herself, but, undoubtedly, at a very high price. It will be

[012-037]understood therefore, that Miss Stanbury was wealthy, and that she

[012-038]was bound to the city in which she lived by peculiar ties.

[012-039]But Miss Stanbury had not been born to this [[money|wealth]], nor can she

[012-040]be said to have inherited from her forefathers any of these high

[012-041]privileges which had been awarded to her. She had achieved them by

[012-042]the romance of her life and the manner in which she had carried

[012-043]herself amidst its vicissitudes. Her father had been vicar of

[012-044][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], a parish lying twenty miles west of [[exeter|Exeter]], among

[012-045]the moors. And on her father's death, her brother, also now dead, had

[012-046]become vicar of the same parish,--her brother, whose only son, Hugh

[012-047]Stanbury, we already know, working for the "D. R." up in [[london|London]]. When

[012-048]Miss Stanbury was twenty-one she became engaged to a certain Mr.

[012-049]Brooke Burgess, the eldest son of a banker in Exeter,--or, it might,

[012-050]perhaps, be better said, a banker himself; for at the time Mr.

[012-051]Brooke Burgess was in the firm. It need not here be told how various

[012-052]misfortunes arose, how Mr. Burgess quarrelled with the Stanbury

[012-053]family, how Jemima quarrelled with her own family, how, when her

[012-054]father died, she went out from Nuncombe Putney parsonage, and lived

[012-055]on the smallest pittance in a city lodging, how her lover was untrue

[012-056]to her and did not marry her, and how at last he died and left her

[012-057]every shilling that he possessed.

[012-058]The Devonshire people, at the time, had been much divided as to the

[012-059]merits of the Stanbury quarrel. There were many who said that the

[012-060]brother could not have acted otherwise than he did; and that Miss

[012-061]Stanbury, though by force of character and force of circumstances

[012-062]she had weathered the storm, had in truth been very indiscreet. The

[012-063]results, however, were as have been described. At the period of which

[012-064]we treat, Miss Stanbury was a very rich lady, living by herself in

[012-065]Exeter, admitted, without question, to be one of the county set, and

[012-066]still at variance with her brother's family. Except to Hugh, she had

[012-067]never spoken a word to one of them since her brother's death. When

[012-068]the money came into her hands, she at that time being over forty

[012-069]and her nephew being then just ten years old, she had undertaken to

[012-070]educate him, and to start him in the world. We know how she had kept

[012-071]her word, and how and why she had withdrawn herself from any further

[012-072][[duty|responsibility]] in the matter.

[012-073]And in regard to this business of starting the young man she had been

[012-074]careful to let it be known that she would do no more than start him.

[012-075]In the formal document, by means of which she had made the proposal

[012-076]to her brother, she had been careful to let it be understood that

[012-077]simple education was all that she intended to bestow upon him,--"and

[012-078]that only," she had added, "in the event of my surviving till his

[012-079]education be completed." And to Hugh himself she had declared that

[012-080]any allowance which she made him after he was called to the Bar,

[012-081]was only made in order to give him room for his foot, a spot of

[012-082]ground from whence to make his first leap. We know how he made that

[012-083]leap, infinitely to the disgust of his aunt, who, when he refused

[012-084]obedience to her in the matter of withdrawing from the Daily Record,

[012-085]immediately withdrew from him, not only her patronage and assistance,

[012-086]but even her friendship and acquaintance. This was the letter which

[012-087]she wrote to him--

[012-088]I don't think that writing radical stuff for a penny

[012-089]newspaper is a respectable occupation for a gentleman, and

[012-090]I will have nothing to do with it. If you choose to do

[012-091]such work, I cannot help it; but it was not for such that

[012-092]I sent you to Harrow and Oxford, nor yet up to London and

[012-093]paid L100 a year to Mr. Lambert. I think you are treating

[012-094]me badly, but that is nothing to your bad treatment of

[012-095]yourself. You need not trouble yourself to answer this,

[012-096]unless you are prepared to say that you will not write any

[012-097]more stuff for that penny newspaper. Only I wish to be

[012-098]understood. I will have no connection that I can help,

[012-099]and no acquaintance at all, with radical scribblers and

[012-100]incendiaries.

[012-101]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[012-102]The Close, Exeter, April 15, 186--.

[012-103][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had answered this, thanking his aunt for past favours,

[012-104]and explaining to her,--or striving to do so,--that he felt it to be

[012-105]his duty to earn his bread, as a means of earning it had come within

[012-106]his reach. He might as well have spared himself the trouble. She

[012-107]simply wrote a few words across his own letter in red ink:--"The

[012-108]bread of unworthiness should never be earned or eaten;" and then sent

[012-109]the letter back under a blank envelope to her nephew.

[012-110]She was a thorough Tory of the old school. Had Hugh taken to writing

[012-111]for a newspaper that had cost sixpence, or even threepence, for its

[012-112]copies, she might perhaps have forgiven him. At any rate the offence

[012-113]would not have been so flagrant. And had the paper been conservative

[012-114]instead of liberal, she would have had some qualms of conscience

[012-115]before she gave him up. But to live by writing for a newspaper! and

[012-116]for a penny newspaper!! and for a penny radical newspaper!!! It was

[012-117]more than she could endure. Of what nature were the articles which he

[012-118]contributed it was impossible that she should have any idea, for no

[012-119]consideration would have induced her to look at a penny newspaper, or

[012-120]to admit it within her doors. She herself took in the John Bull and

[012-121]the Herald, and daily groaned deeply at the way in which those once

[012-122]great organs of true British public feeling were becoming demoralised

[012-123]and perverted. Had any reduction been made in the price of either of

[012-124]them, she would at once have stopped her subscription. In the matter

[012-125]of politics she had long since come to think that everything good was

[012-126]over. She hated the name of Reform so much that she could not bring

[012-127]herself to believe in Mr. Disraeli and his bill. For many years she

[012-128]had believed in Lord Derby. She would fain believe in him still if

[012-129]she could. It was the great desire of her heart to have some one in

[012-130]whom she believed. In the bishop of her diocese she did believe, and

[012-131]annually sent him some little comforting present from her own hand.

[012-132]And in two or three of the clergymen around her she believed, finding

[012-133]in them a flavour of the unascetic godliness of ancient days which

[012-134]was gratifying to her palate. But in politics there was hardly a name

[012-135]remaining to which she could fix her faith and declare that there

[012-136]should be her guide. For awhile she thought she would cling to Mr.

[012-137]Lowe; but, when she made inquiry, she found that there was no base

[012-138]there of really well-formed conservative granite. The three gentlemen

[012-139]who had dissevered themselves from Mr. Disraeli when Mr. Disraeli was

[012-140]passing his Reform bill, were doubtless very good in their way; but

[012-141]they were not big enough to fill her heart. She tried to make herself

[012-142]happy with General Peel, but General Peel was after all no more than

[012-143]a shade to her. But the untruth of others never made her untrue, and

[012-144]she still talked of the excellence of George III. and the glories of

[012-145]the subsequent reign. She had a bust of Lord Eldon, before which she

[012-146]was accustomed to stand with hands closed and to weep,--or to think

[012-147]that she wept.

[012-148]She was a little woman, now nearly sixty years of age, with bright

[012-149]grey eyes, and a strong Roman nose, and thin lips, and a sharp-cut

[012-150]chin. She wore a head-gear that almost amounted to a mob-cap, and

[012-151]beneath it her grey hair was always frizzled with the greatest care.

[012-152]Her dress was invariably of black silk, and she had five gowns,--one

[012-153]for church, one for evening parties, one for driving out, and one for

[012-154]evenings at home, and one for mornings. The dress, when new, always

[012-155]went to church. Nothing, she was wont to say, was too good for the

[012-156]Lord's house. In the days of crinolines she had protested that she

[012-157]had never worn one,--a protest, however, which was hardly true; and

[012-158]now, in these later days, her hatred was especially developed in

[012-159]reference to the head-dresses of young women. "Chignon" was a word

[012-160]which she had never been heard to pronounce. She would talk of "those

[012-161]bandboxes which the sluts wear behind their noddles;" for Miss

[012-162]Stanbury allowed herself the use of much strong language. She was

[012-163]very punctilious in all her habits, breakfasting ever at half-past

[012-164]eight, and dining always at six. Half-past five had been her time,

[012-165]till the bishop, who, on an occasion, was to be her guest, once

[012-166]signified to her that such an hour cut up the day and interfered with

[012-167]clerical work. Her lunch was always of bread and cheese, and they who

[012-168]lunched with her either eat that,--or the bread without the cheese.

[012-169]An afternoon "tea" was a thing horrible to her imagination. Tea and

[012-170]buttered toast at half-past eight in the evening was the great luxury

[012-171]of her life. She was as strong as a horse, and had never hitherto

[012-172]known a day's illness. As a consequence of this, she did not believe

[012-173]in the illness of other people,--especially not in the illness of

[012-174]women. She did not like a girl who could not drink a glass of beer

[012-175]with her bread and cheese in the middle of the day, and she thought

[012-176]that a glass of port after dinner was good for everybody. Indeed, she

[012-177]had a thorough belief in port wine, thinking that it would go far to

[012-178]cure most miseries. But she could not put up with the idea that a

[012-179]woman, young or old, should want the stimulus of a glass of sherry

[012-180]to support her at any odd time of the day. Hot concoctions of strong

[012-181]drink at Christmas she would allow to everybody, and was very strong

[012-182]in recommending such comforts to ladies blessed, or about to be

[012-183]blessed, with babies. She took the sacrament every month, and gave

[012-184]away exactly a tenth of her income to the poor. She believed that

[012-185]there was a special holiness in a tithe of a thing, and attributed

[012-186]the commencement of the downfall of the Church of England to rent

[012-187]charges, and the commutation of clergymen's incomes. Since Judas,

[012-188]there had never been, to her thinking, a traitor so base, or an

[012-189]apostate so sinful, as Colenso; and yet, of the nature of Colenso's

[012-190]teaching she was as ignorant as the towers of the cathedral opposite

[012-191]to her.
