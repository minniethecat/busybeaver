## Section 48

[048-001]But she now wrote again to [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], and enclosed to her

[048-002]husband, not a copy of what she had written, but the note itself. It

[048-003]was as follows:--

[048-004][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], Wednesday, August 10.

[048-005]MY DEAR COLONEL OSBORNE,

[048-006]My husband has desired me not to see you, or to write to

[048-007]you, or to hear from you again. I must therefore beg you

[048-008]to enable me to obey him,--at any rate till papa comes to

[048-009]England.

[048-010]Yours truly,

[048-011]EMILY TREVELYAN.

[048-012]And then she wrote to her husband, and in the writing of this letter

[048-013]there was much doubt, much labour, and many changes. We will give it

[048-014]as it was written when completed:--

[048-015]I have received your letter, and will obey your commands

[048-016]to the best of my power. In order that you may not be

[048-017]displeased by any further unavoidable correspondence

[048-018]between me and Colonel Osborne, I have written to him a

[048-019]note, which I now send to you. I send it that you may

[048-020]forward it. If you do not choose to do so, I cannot be

[048-021]answerable either for his seeing me, or for his writing to

[048-022]me again.

[048-023]I send also copies of all the correspondence I have had

[048-024]with Colonel Osborne since you turned me out of your

[048-025]house. When he came to call on me, Nora remained with me

[048-026]while he was here. I blush while I write this;--not for

[048-027]myself, but that I should be so suspected as to make such

[048-028]a statement necessary.

[048-029]You say that I have disgraced you and myself. I have

[048-030]done neither. I am disgraced;--but it is you that have

[048-031]disgraced me. I have never spoken a word or done a thing,

[048-032]as regards you, of which I have cause to be ashamed.

[048-033]I have told Mrs. Stanbury that I and Nora will leave her

[048-034]house as soon as we can be made to know where we are to

[048-035]go. I beg that this may be decided instantly, as else we

[048-036]must walk out into the street without a shelter. After

[048-037]what has been said, I cannot remain here.

[048-038]My sister bids me say that she will relieve you of all

[048-039]burden respecting herself as soon as possible. She will

[048-040]probably be able to find a home with my aunt, Mrs.

[048-041]Outhouse, till papa comes to England. As for myself, I can

[048-042]only say that till he comes, I shall do exactly what you

[048-043]order.

[048-044]EMILY TREVELYAN.

[048-045]Nuncombe Putney, August 10.

[048-046]CHAPTER XXIX.

[048-047]MR. AND MRS. OUTHOUSE.

[048-048]Both Mr. Outhouse and his wife were especially timid in taking upon

[048-049]themselves the cares of other people. Not on that account is it to be

[048-050]supposed that they were bad or selfish. They were both given much to

[048-051]charity, and bestowed both in time and money more than is ordinarily

[048-052]considered necessary, even from persons in their position. But what

[048-053]they gave, they gave away from their own quiet hearth. Had money

[048-054]been wanting to the daughters of his wife's brother, Mr. Outhouse

[048-055]would have opened such small coffer as he had with a free hand. But

[048-056]he would have much preferred that his benevolence should be used

[048-057]in a way that would bring upon him no further [[duty|responsibility]] and

[048-058]no questionings from people whom he did not know and could not

[048-059]understand.

[048-060]The Rev. Oliphant Outhouse had been Rector of St.

[048-061]Diddulph's-in-the-East for the last fifteen years, having married

[048-062]the sister of Sir Marmaduke Rowley,--then simply Mr. Rowley, with a

[048-063]colonial appointment in Jamaica of L120 per annum,--twelve years

[048-064]before his promotion, while he was a curate in one of the populous

[048-065]borough parishes. He had thus been a [[london|London]] clergyman all his life;

[048-066]but he knew almost as little of London society as though he had held

[048-067]a cure in a Westmoreland valley. He had worked hard, but his work had

[048-068]been altogether among the poor. He had no gift of preaching, and had

[048-069]acquired neither [[honour|reputation]] nor popularity. But he could work;--and

[048-070]having been transferred because of that capability to the temporary

[048-071]curacy of St. Diddulph's,--out of one diocese into another,--he had

[048-072]received the living from the bishop's hands when it became vacant.

[048-073]A dreary place was the parsonage of St. Diddulph's-in-the-East for

[048-074]the abode of a [[social-class|gentleman]]. Mr. Outhouse had not, in his whole parish,

[048-075]a parishioner with whom he could consort. The greatest men around

[048-076]him were the publicans, and the most numerous were men employed in

[048-077]and around the docks. Dredgers of mud, navvies employed on suburban

[048-078]canals, excavators, loaders and unloaders of cargo, cattle drivers,

[048-079]whose driving, however, was done mostly on board ship,--such and

[048-080]such like were the men who were the fathers of the families of St.

[048-081]Diddulph's-in-the-East. And there was there, not far removed from the

[048-082]muddy estuary of a little stream that makes its black way from the

[048-083]Essex marshes among the houses of the poorest of the poor into the

[048-084]Thames, a large commercial establishment for turning the carcasses of

[048-085]horses into manure. Messrs. Flowsem and Blurt were in truth the great

[048-086]people of St. Diddulph's-in-the-East; but the closeness of their

[048-087]establishment was not an additional attraction to the parsonage.

[048-088]They were liberal, however, with their money, and Mr. Outhouse was

[048-089]disposed to think,--custom perhaps having made the establishment

[048-090]less objectionable to him than it was at first,--that St.

[048-091]Diddulph's-in-the-East would be more of a Pandemonium than it now

[048-092]was, if by any sanitary law Messrs. Flowsem and Blurt were compelled

[048-093]to close their doors. "Non olet," he would say with a grim smile when

[048-094]the charitable cheque of the firm would come punctually to hand on

[048-095]the first Saturday after Christmas.

[048-096]But such a house as his would be, as he knew, but a poor residence

[048-097]for his wife's nieces. Indeed, without positively saying that he

[048-098]was unwilling to receive them, he had, when he first heard of the

[048-099]breaking up of the house in Curzon Street, shewn that he would rather

[048-100]not take upon his shoulders so great a responsibility. He and his

[048-101]wife had discussed the matter between them, and had come to the

[048-102]conclusion that they did not know what kind of things might have been

[048-103]done in Curzon Street. They would think no evil, they said; but the

[048-104]very idea of a married woman with a lover was dreadful to them. It

[048-105]might be that their niece was free from blame. They hoped so. And

[048-106]even though her sin had been of ever so deep a dye, they would take

[048-107]her in,--if it were indeed necessary. But they hoped that such help

[048-108]from them might not be needed. They both knew how to give counsel to

[048-109]a poor woman, how to rebuke a poor man,--how to comfort, encourage,

[048-110]or to upbraid the poor. Practice had told them how far they might go

[048-111]with some hope of doing good;--and at what stage of demoralisation

[048-112]no good from their hands was any longer within the scope of fair

[048-113]expectation. But all this was among the poor. With what words to

[048-114]encourage such a one as their niece Mrs. Trevelyan,--to encourage her

[048-115]or to rebuke her, as her conduct might seem to make necessary,--they

[048-116]both felt that they were altogether ignorant. To them Mrs. Trevelyan

[048-117]was a fine lady. To Mr. Outhouse, Sir Marmaduke had ever been a fine

[048-118]gentleman, given much to worldly things, who cared more for whist and

[048-119]a glass of wine than for anything else, and who thought that he had

[048-120]a good excuse for never going to church in England because he was

[048-121]called upon, as he said, to show himself in the governor's pew always

[048-122]once on Sundays, and frequently twice, when he was at the seat of his

[048-123]government. Sir Marmaduke manifestly looked upon church as a thing

[048-124]in itself notoriously disagreeable. To Mr. Outhouse it afforded the

[048-125]great events of the week. And Mrs. Outhouse would declare that to

[048-126]hear her husband preach was the greatest joy of her life. It may

[048-127]be understood therefore that though the family connection between

[048-128]the Rowleys and the Outhouses had been kept up with a semblance of

[048-129]affection, it had never blossomed forth into cordial friendship.

[048-130]When therefore the clergyman of St. Diddulph's received a letter from

[048-131]his niece, Nora, begging him to take her into his parsonage till Sir

[048-132]Marmaduke should arrive in the course of the spring, and hinting

[048-133]also a wish that her uncle Oliphant should see Mr. Trevelyan and

[048-134]if possible arrange that his other niece should also come to the

[048-135]parsonage, he was very much perturbed in spirit. There was a long

[048-136]consultation between him and his wife before anything could be

[048-137]settled, and it may be doubted whether anything would have been

[048-138]settled, had not Mr. Trevelyan himself made his way to the parsonage,

[048-139]on the second day of the family conference. Mr. and Mrs. Outhouse had

[048-140]both seen the necessity of sleeping upon the matter. They had slept

[048-141]upon it, and the discourse between them on the second day was so

[048-142]doubtful in its tone that more sleeping would probably have been

[048-143]necessary had not Mr. Trevelyan appeared and compelled them to a

[048-144]decision.

[048-145]"You must remember that I make no charge against her," said

[048-146]Trevelyan, after the matter had been discussed for about an hour.

[048-147]"Then why should she not come back to you?" said Mr. Outhouse,

[048-148]timidly.

[048-149]"Some day she may,--if she will be obedient. But it cannot be now.

[048-150]She has set me at defiance; and even yet it is too clear from the

[048-151]tone of her letter to me that she thinks that she has been right to

[048-152]do so. How could we live together in amity when she addresses me as a

[048-153]cruel tyrant?"

[048-154]"Why did she go away at first?" asked Mrs. Outhouse.

[048-155]"Because she would compromise my name by an intimacy which I did not

[048-156]approve. But I do not come here to defend myself, Mrs. Outhouse. You

[048-157]probably think that I have been wrong. You are her friend; and to

[048-158]you, I will not even say that I have been right. What I want you to

[048-159]understand is this. She cannot come back to me now. It would not be

[048-160]for my honour that she should do so."

[048-161]"But, sir,--would it not be for your welfare, as a Christian?" asked

[048-162]Mr. Outhouse.

[048-163]"You must not be angry with me, if I say that I will not discuss that

[048-164]just now. I did not come here to discuss it."

[048-165]"It is very sad for our poor niece," said Mrs. Outhouse.

[048-166]"It is very sad for me," said Trevelyan, gloomily;--"very sad,

[048-167]indeed. My home is destroyed; my life is made solitary; I do not even

[048-168]see my own child. She has her boy with her, and her sister. I have

[048-169]nobody."

[048-170]"I can't understand, for the life of me, why you should not live

[048-171]together just like any other people," said Mrs. Outhouse, whose

[048-172]woman's spirit was arising in her bosom. "When people are married,

[048-173]they must put up with something;--at least, most always." This she

[048-174]added, lest it might be for a moment imagined that she had had any

[048-175]cause for complaint with her Mr. Outhouse.

[048-176]"Pray excuse me, Mrs. Outhouse; but I cannot discuss that. The

[048-177]question between us is this,--can you consent to receive your two

[048-178]nieces till their father's return;--and if so, in what way shall I

[048-179]defray the expense of their living? You will of course understand

[048-180]that I willingly undertake the expense not only of my wife's

[048-181]maintenance and of her sister's also, but that I will cheerfully

[048-182]allow anything that may be required either for their comfort or

[048-183]recreation."

[048-184]"I cannot take my nieces into my house as lodgers," said Mr.

[048-185]Outhouse.

[048-186]"No, not as lodgers; but of course you can understand that it is for

[048-187]me to pay for my own wife. I know I owe you an apology for mentioning

[048-188]it;--but how else could I make my request to you?"
