## Section 88

[088-001]"I will tell him so," said Nora; "I don't care what may come

[088-002]afterwards, but I will tell him the truth."

[088-003]I know [continued Hugh] that an engagement with me now

[088-004]would be hazardous, because what I earn is both scanty and

[088-005]precarious; but it seems to me that nothing could ever

[088-006]be done without some risk. There are risks of different

[088-007]kinds,--

[088-008]She wondered whether he was thinking when he wrote this of the rock

[088-009]on which her sister's barque had been split to pieces;--

[088-010]and we may hardly hope to avoid them all. For myself,

[088-011]I own that life would be tame to me, if there were no

[088-012]dangers to be overcome.

[088-013]If you do love me, and will say so, I will not ask you

[088-014]to be my wife till I can give you a proper home; but the

[088-015][[truth|knowledge]] that I am the master of the treasure which I

[088-016]desire will give me a double energy, and will make me feel

[088-017]that when I have gained so much I cannot fail of adding to

[088-018]it all other smaller things that may be necessary.

[088-019]Pray,--pray, send me an answer. I cannot reach you except

[088-020]by writing, as I was told by your aunt not to come to the

[088-021]house again.

[088-022]Dearest Nora, pray believe

[088-023]That I shall always be truly yours only,

[088-024]HUGH STANBURY.

[088-025]Write to him! Of course she would write to him. Of course she would

[088-026]confess to him the truth. "He tells me that I owe it to him to say

[088-027]so, and I acknowledge the debt," she said aloud to herself. "And as

[088-028]for a proper home, he shall be the judge of that." She resolved that

[088-029]she would not be a fine lady, not fastidious, not coy, not afraid

[088-030]to take her full share of the risk of which he spoke in such manly

[088-031]terms. "It is quite true. As he has been able to make me love him,

[088-032]I have no right to stand aloof,--even if I wished it." As she was

[088-033]walking up and down the room so resolving her sister came to her.

[088-034]"Well, dear!" said Emily. "May I ask what it is he says?"

[088-035]Nora paused a moment, holding the letter tight in her hand, and then

[088-036]she held it out to her sister. "There it is. You may read it." Mrs.

[088-037]Trevelyan took the letter and read it slowly, during which Nora

[088-038]stood looking out of the window. She would not watch her sister's

[088-039]face, as she did not wish to have to reply to any outward signs of

[088-040]disapproval. "Give it me back," she said, when she heard by the

[088-041]refolding of the paper that the perusal was finished.

[088-042]"Of course I shall give it you back, dear."

[088-043]"Yes;--thanks. I did not mean to doubt you."

[088-044]"And what will you do, Nora?"

[088-045]"Answer it of course."

[088-046]"I would think a little before I answered it," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[088-047]"I have thought,--a great deal, already."

[088-048]"And how will you answer it?"

[088-049]Nora paused again before she replied. "As nearly as I know how to do

[088-050]in such words as he would put into my mouth. I shall strive to write

[088-051]just what I think he would wish me to write."

[088-052]"Then you will engage yourself to him, Nora?"

[088-053]"Certainly I shall. I am engaged to him already. I have been ever

[088-054]since he came here."

[088-055]"You told me that there was nothing of the kind."

[088-056]"I told you that I loved him better than anybody in the world, and

[088-057]that ought to have made you know what it must come to. When I am

[088-058]thinking of him every day, and every hour, how can I not be glad to

[088-059]have an engagement settled with him? I couldn't marry anybody else,

[088-060]and I don't want to remain as I am." The tears came into the married

[088-061]sister's eyes, and rolled down her cheeks, as this was said to her.

[088-062]Would it not have been better for her had she remained as she was?

[088-063]"Dear Emily," said Nora, "you have got Louey still."

[088-064]"Yes;--and they mean to take him from me. But I do not wish to speak

[088-065]of myself. Will you postpone your answer till mamma is here?"

[088-066]"I cannot do that, Emily. What; receive such a letter as that, and

[088-067]send no reply to it!"

[088-068]"I would write a line for you, and explain--"

[088-069]"No, indeed, Emily. I choose to answer my own letters. I have shewn

[088-070]you that, because I trust you; but I have fully made up my mind as

[088-071]to what I shall write. It will have been written and sent before

[088-072]dinner."

[088-073]"I think you will be wrong, Nora."

[088-074]"Why wrong! When I came over here to stay with you, would mamma ever

[088-075]have thought of directing me not to accept any offer till her consent

[088-076]had been obtained all the way from the Mandarins? She would never

[088-077]have dreamed of such a thing."

[088-078]"Will you ask Aunt Mary?"

[088-079]"Certainly not. What is Aunt Mary to me? We are here in her house for

[088-080]a time, under the press of circumstances; but I owe her no obedience.

[088-081]She told Mr. Stanbury not to come here; and he has not come; and I

[088-082]shall not ask him to come. I would not willingly bring any one into

[088-083]Uncle Oliphant's house that he and she do not wish to see. But I will

[088-084]not admit that either of them have any authority over me."

[088-085]"Then who has, dearest?"

[088-086]"Nobody;--except papa and mamma; and they have chosen to leave me to

[088-087]myself."

[088-088]Mrs. Trevelyan found it impossible to shake her sister's firmness,

[088-089]and could herself do nothing, except tell Mrs. Outhouse what was the

[088-090]state of affairs. When she said that she should do this, there almost

[088-091]came to be a flow of high words between the two sisters; but at last

[088-092]Nora assented. "As for knowing, I don't care if all the world knows

[088-093]it. I shall do nothing in a corner. I don't suppose Aunt Mary will

[088-094]endeavour to prevent my posting my letter."

[088-095]Emily at last went to seek Mrs. Outhouse, and Nora at once sat

[088-096]down to her desk. Neither of the sisters felt at all sure that Mrs.

[088-097]Outhouse would not attempt to stop the emission of the letter from

[088-098]her house; but, as it happened, she was out, and did not return till

[088-099]Nora had come back from her journey to the neighbouring post-office.

[088-100]She would trust her letter, when written, to no hands but her

[088-101]own; and as she herself dropped it into the safe custody of the

[088-102]Postmaster-General, it also shall be revealed to the public:--

[088-103]Parsonage, St. Diddulph's, January, 186--.

[088-104]DEAR HUGH,

[088-105]For I suppose I may as well write to you in that way now.

[088-106]I have been made so happy by your affectionate letter. Is

[088-107]not that a candid confession for a young lady? But you

[088-108]tell me that I owe you the truth, and so I tell you the

[088-109]truth. Nobody will ever be anything to me, except you; and

[088-110]you are everything. I do love you; and should it ever be

[088-111]possible, I will become your wife.

[088-112]I have said so much, because I feel that I ought to obey

[088-113]the order you have given me; but pray do not try to see me

[088-114]or write to me till mamma has arrived. She and papa will

[088-115]be here in the spring,--quite early in the spring, we

[088-116]hope; and then you may come to us. What they may say, of

[088-117]course, I cannot tell; but I shall be true to you.

[088-118]Your own, with truest affection,

[088-119]NORA.

[088-120]Of course, you knew that I loved you, and I don't think

[088-121]that you are a conjuror at all.

[088-122][Illustration: Nora's letter.]

[088-123]As soon as ever the letter was written, she put on her bonnet, and

[088-124]went forth with it herself to the post-office. Mrs. Trevelyan stopped

[088-125]her on the stairs, and endeavoured to detain her, but Nora would not

[088-126]be detained. "I must judge for myself about this," she said. "If

[088-127]mamma were here, it would be different, but, as she is not here, I

[088-128]must judge for myself."

[088-129]What Mrs. Outhouse might have done had she been at home at the time,

[088-130]it would be useless to surmise. She was told what had happened

[088-131]when it occurred, and questioned Nora on the subject. "I thought I

[088-132]understood from you," she said, with something of severity in her

[088-133]countenance, "that there was to be nothing between you and Mr.

[088-134]Stanbury--at any rate, till my brother came home?"

[088-135]"I never pledged myself to anything of the kind, Aunt Mary," Nora

[088-136]said. "I think he promised that he would not come here, and I don't

[088-137]suppose that he means to come. If he should do so, I shall not see

[088-138]him."

[088-139]With this Mrs. Outhouse was obliged to be content. The letter was

[088-140]gone, and could not be stopped. Nor, indeed, had any authority been

[088-141]delegated to her by which she would have been justified in stopping

[088-142]it. She could only join her husband in wishing that they both

[088-143]might be relieved, as soon as possible, from the terrible burden

[088-144]which had been thrown upon them. "I call it very hard," said Mr.

[088-145]Outhouse;--"very hard, indeed. If we were to desire them to leave

[088-146]the house, everybody would cry out upon us for our cruelty; and yet,

[088-147]while they remain here, they will submit themselves to no authority.

[088-148]As far as I can see, they may, both of them, do just what they

[088-149]please, and we can't stop it."

[088-150]CHAPTER LIV.

[088-151]MR. GIBSON'S THREAT.

[088-152]Miss Stanbury for a long time persisted in being neither better nor

[088-153]worse. Sir Peter would not declare her state to be precarious, nor

[088-154]would he say that she was out of danger; and Mr. Martin had been so

[088-155]utterly prostrated by the nearly-fatal effects of his own mistake

[088-156]that he was quite unable to rally himself and talk on the subject

[088-157]with any spirit or confidence. When interrogated he would simply

[088-158]reply that Sir Peter said this and Sir Peter said that, and thus

[088-159]add to, rather than diminish, the doubt, and excitement, and varied

[088-160]opinion which prevailed through the city. On one morning it was

[088-161]absolutely asserted within the limits of the Close that Miss Stanbury

[088-162]was dying,--and it was believed for half a day at the bank that she

[088-163]was then lying in articulo mortis. There had got about, too, a report

[088-164]that a portion of the [[money|property]] had only been left to Miss Stanbury

[088-165]for her life, that the Burgesses would be able to reclaim the

[088-166]houses in the city, and that a will had been made altogether in

[088-167]favour of Dorothy, cutting out even Brooke from any share in the

[088-168]inheritance;--and thus [[exeter|Exeter]] had a good deal to say respecting

[088-169]the affairs and state of health of our old friend. Miss Stanbury's

[088-170]illness, however, was true enough. She was much too ill to hear

[088-171]anything of what was going on;--too ill to allow Martha to talk to

[088-172]her at all about the outside public. When the invalid herself would

[088-173]ask questions about the affairs of the world, Martha would be very

[088-174]discreet and turn away from the subject. Miss Stanbury, for instance,

[088-175]ill as she was, exhibited a most mundane interest, not exactly in

[088-176]Camilla French's marriage, but in the delay which that marriage

[088-177]seemed destined to encounter. "I dare say he'll slip out of it yet,"

[088-178]said the sick lady to her confidential servant. Then Martha had

[088-179]thought it right to change the subject, feeling it to be wrong

[088-180]that an old lady on her death-bed should be taking joy in the

[088-181]disappointment of her young neighbour. Martha changed the subject,

[088-182]first to jelly, and then to the psalms of the day. Miss Stanbury

[088-183]was too weak to resist; but the last verse of the last psalm of the

[088-184]evening had hardly been finished before she remarked that she would

[088-185]never believe it till she saw it. "It's all in the hands of Him as is

[088-186]on high, mum," said Martha, turning her eyes up to the ceiling, and

[088-187]closing the book at the same time, with a look strongly indicative of

[088-188]displeasure.
