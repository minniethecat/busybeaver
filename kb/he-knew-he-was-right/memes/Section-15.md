[015-015]So they went out, and finding the verger at the Cathedral door, he

[015-017]aisles, and Dorothy was instructed as to what would be expected from

[015-019]Cathedral service on the morning of every week-day, and on Sundays in

[015-020]the afternoon. On Sunday mornings she was to attend the little church

[015-021]of St. Margaret. On Sunday evenings it was the practice of Miss

[015-022]Stanbury to read a sermon in the dining-room to all of whom her

[015-023]household consisted. Did Dorothy like daily services? Dorothy, who

[015-024]was more patient than her brother, and whose life had been much less

[015-025]energetic, said that she had no objection to going to church every

[015-026]day when there was not too much to do.

[015-027]"There never need be too much to do to attend the Lord's house," said

[015-028]Miss Stanbury, somewhat angrily.

[015-029]"Only if you've got to make the beds," said Dorothy.

[015-030]"My dear, I beg your pardon," said Miss Stanbury. "I beg your pardon,

[015-031]heartily. I'm a thoughtless old woman, I know. Never mind. Now, we'll

[015-032]go in."

[015-033]Later in the evening, when she gave her niece a candlestick to go to

[015-034]bed, she repeated what she had said before.

[015-035]"It'll do very well, my dear. I'm sure it'll do. But if you read in

[015-036]bed either night or morning, I'll never forgive you."

[015-037]This last caution was uttered with so much energy, that Dorothy gave

[015-038]a little jump as she promised obedience.

[015-039]CHAPTER IX.

[015-040]SHEWING HOW THE QUARREL PROGRESSED AGAIN.

[015-041]On one Sunday morning, when the month of May was nearly over, Hugh

[015-042]Stanbury met [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] in Curzon Street, not many yards from

[015-043]Trevelyan's door. Colonel Osborne had just come from the house, and

[015-044]Stanbury was going to it. Hugh had not spoken to Osborne since the

[015-045]day, now a fortnight since, on which both of them had witnessed

[015-046]the scene in the park; but on that occasion they had been left

[015-047]together, and it had been impossible for them not to say a few words

[015-048]about their mutual friends. Osborne had expressed his sorrow that

[015-049]there should be any misunderstanding, and had called Trevelyan a

[015-050]"confounded fool." Stanbury had suggested that there was something in

[015-051]it which they two probably did not understand, and that matters would

[015-052]be sure to come all right. "The truth is Trevelyan bullies her," said

[015-053]Osborne; "and if he goes on with that he'll be sure to get the worst

[015-054]of it." Now,--on this present occasion,--Stanbury asked whether he

[015-055]would find the ladies at home. "Yes, they are both there," said

[015-056]Osborne. "Trevelyan has just gone out in a huff. She'll never be able

[015-057]to go on living with him. Anybody can see that with half an eye."

[015-058]Then he had passed on, and [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] knocked at the door.

[015-059]He was shown up into the drawing-room, and found both the sisters

[015-060]there; but he could see that Mrs. Trevelyan had been in tears. The

[015-061]avowed purpose of his visit,--that is, the purpose which he had

[015-062]avowed to himself,--was to talk about his sister Dorothy. He had told

[015-063]Miss Rowley, while walking in the park with her, how Dorothy had been

[015-064]invited over to [[exeter|Exeter]] by her aunt, and how he had counselled his

[015-065]sister to accept the invitation. Nora had expressed herself very

[015-066]interested as to Dorothy's fate, and had said how much she wished

[015-067]that she knew Dorothy. We all understand how sweet it is, when two

[015-068]such persons as Hugh Stanbury and [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] cannot speak of their

[015-069]love for each other, to say these tender things in regard to some

[015-070]one else. Nora had been quite anxious to know how Dorothy had been

[015-071]received by that old conservative warrior, as Hugh Stanbury had

[015-072]called his aunt, and Hugh had now come to Curzon Street with a letter

[015-073]from Dorothy in his pocket. But when he saw that there had been some

[015-074]cause for trouble, he hardly knew how to introduce his subject.

[015-075]"Trevelyan is not at home?" he asked.

[015-076]"No," said Emily, with her face turned away. "He went out and left us

[015-077]a quarter of an hour since. Did you meet Colonel Osborne?"

[015-078]"I was speaking to him in the street not a moment since." As he

[015-079]answered he could see that Nora was making some sign to her sister.

[015-080]Nora was most anxious that Emily should not speak of what had just

[015-081]occurred, but her signs were all thrown away. "Somebody must tell

[015-082]him," said Mrs. Trevelyan, "and I don't know who can do so better

[015-083]than so old a friend as Mr. Stanbury."

[015-084]"Tell what, and to whom?" he asked.

[015-085]"No, no, no," said Nora.

[015-086]"Then I must tell him myself," said she, "that is all. As for

[015-087][[reputation|standing]] this kind of life, it is out of the question. I should

[015-088]either destroy myself or go mad."

[015-089]"If I could do any good I should be so happy," said Stanbury.

[015-090]"Nobody can do any good between a man and his wife," said Nora.

[015-091]Then Mrs. Trevelyan began to tell her story, putting aside, with an

[015-092]impatient motion of her hands, the efforts which her sister made to

[015-093]stop her. She was very angry, and as she told it, [[reputation|standing]] up, all

[015-094]trace of sobbing soon disappeared from her voice. "The fact is," she

[015-095]said, "he does not know his own mind, or what to fear or what not to

[015-096]fear. He told me that I was never to see Colonel Osborne again."

[015-097]"What is the use, Emily, of your repeating that to Mr. Stanbury?"

[015-098]"Why should I not repeat it? Colonel Osborne is papa's oldest friend,

[015-099]and mine too. He is a man I like very much,--who is a real friend to

[015-100]me. As he is old enough to be my father, one would have thought that

[015-101]my husband could have found no objection."

[015-102]"I don't know much about his age," said Stanbury.

[015-103]"It does make a difference. It must make a difference. I should not

[015-104]think of becoming so intimate with a younger man. But, however, when

[015-105]my husband told me that I was to see him no more,--though the insult

[015-106]nearly killed me, I determined to obey him. An order was given that

[015-107]Colonel Osborne should not be admitted. You may imagine how painful

[015-108]it was; but it was given, and I was prepared to bear it."

[015-109]"But he had been lunching with you on that Sunday."

[015-110]"Yes; that is just it. As soon as it was given Louis would rescind

[015-111]it, because he was ashamed of what he had done. He was so jealous

[015-112]that he did not want me to see the man; and yet he was so afraid that

[015-113]it should be known that he ordered me to see him. He ordered him into

[015-114]the house at last, and I,--I went away up-stairs."

[015-115]"That was on the Sunday that we met you in the park?" asked Stanbury.

[015-116]"What is the use of going back to all that?" said Nora.

[015-117]"Then I met him by chance in the park," continued Mrs. Trevelyan,

[015-118]"and because he said a word which I knew would anger my husband, I

[015-119]left him abruptly. Since that my husband has begged that things might

[015-120]go on as they were before. He could not bear that Colonel Osborne

[015-121]himself should think that he was jealous. Well; I gave way, and the

[015-122]man has been here as before. And now there has been a scene which has

[015-123]been disgraceful to us all. I cannot stand it, and I won't. If he

[015-124]does not behave himself with more manliness,--I will leave him."

[015-125]"But what can I do?"

[015-126]"Nothing, Mr. Stanbury," said Nora.

[015-127]"Yes; you can do this. You can go to him from me, and can tell him

[015-128]that I have chosen you as a messenger because you are his friend.

[015-129]You can tell him that I am willing to obey him in anything. If he

[015-130]chooses, I will consent that Colonel Osborne shall be asked never

[015-131]to come into my presence again. It will be very absurd; but if he

[015-132]chooses, I will consent. Or I will let things go on as they are, and

[015-133]continue to receive my father's old friend when he comes. But if I

[015-134]do, I will not put up with an imputation on my conduct because he

[015-135]does not like the way in which the [[social-class|gentleman]] thinks fit to address

[015-136]me. I take upon myself to say that if any man alive spoke to me as

[015-137]he ought not to speak, I should know how to resent it myself. But I

[015-138]cannot fly into a passion with an old gentleman for calling me by my

[015-139]Christian name, when he has done so habitually for years."

[015-140]From all this it will appear that the great godsend of a rich

[015-141]marriage, with all manner of attendant comforts, which had come in

[015-142]the way of the Rowley family as they were living at the Mandarins,

[015-143]had not turned out to be an unmixed blessing. In the matter of the

[015-144]quarrel, as it had hitherto progressed, the husband had perhaps been

[015-145]more in the wrong than his wife; but the wife, in spite of all her

[015-146]promises of perfect obedience, had proved herself to be a woman very

[015-147]hard to manage. Had she been earnest in her desire to please her lord

[015-148]and master in this matter of Colonel Osborne's visits,--to please

[015-149]him even after he had so vacillated in his own behests,--she might

[015-150]probably have so received the man as to have quelled all feeling of

[015-151]jealousy in her husband's bosom. But instead of doing so she had

[015-152]told herself that as she was innocent, and as her innocence had been

[015-153]acknowledged, and as she had been specially instructed to receive

[015-154]this man whom she had before been specially instructed not to

[015-155]receive, she would now fall back exactly into her old manner with

[015-156]him. She had told Colonel Osborne never to allude to that meeting

[015-157]in the park, and to ask no creature as to what had occasioned her

[015-158]conduct on that Sunday; thus having a mystery with him, which of

[015-159]course he understood as well as she did. And then she had again taken

[015-160]to writing notes to him and receiving notes from him,--none of which

[015-161]she showed to her husband. She was more intimate with him than ever,

[015-162]and yet she hardly ever mentioned his name to her husband. Trevelyan,

[015-163]acknowledging to himself that he had done no good by his former

[015-164]interference, feeling that he had put himself in the wrong on that

[015-165]occasion, and that his wife had got the better of him, had borne with

[015-166]all this, with soreness and a moody savageness of general conduct,

[015-167]but still without further words of anger with reference to the man

[015-168]himself. But now, on this Sunday, when his wife had been closeted

[015-169]with Colonel Osborne in the back drawing-room, leaving him with his

[015-170]sister-in-law, his temper had become too hot for him, and he had

[015-171]suddenly left the house, declaring that he would not walk with the

[015-172]two women on that day. "Why not, Louis?" his wife had said, coming up

[015-173]to him. "Never mind why not, but I shall not," he had answered; and

[015-174]then he left the room.
