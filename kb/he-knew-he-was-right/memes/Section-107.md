## Section 107

[107-001]"What sort of a word?"

[107-002]"He says he wishes you would read up some blue-books, or papers, or

[107-003]reports, or something of that kind, which he says that some of his

[107-004]fellows have sent you. It seems that there are some new rules, or

[107-005]orders, or fashions, which he wants you to have at your fingers'

[107-006]ends. Nothing could be more civil than he was,--but he just wished

[107-007]me to mention this, knowing that you and I are likely to see each

[107-008]other."

[107-009]"I wish I had never come over," said Sir Marmaduke.

[107-010]"Why so?"

[107-011]"They didn't bother me with their new rules and fashions over there.

[107-012]When the papers came somebody read them, and that was enough. I could

[107-013]do what they wanted me to do there."

[107-014]"And so you will here,--after a bit."

[107-015]"I'm not so sure of that. Those young fellows seem to forget that

[107-016]an old dog can't learn new tricks. They've got a young brisk fellow

[107-017]there who seems to think that a man should be an encyclopedia of

[107-018][[truth|knowledge]] because he has lived in a colony over twenty years."

[107-019]"That's the new under-secretary."

[107-020]"Never mind who it is. Osborne, just come up to the library, will

[107-021]you? I want to speak to you." Then Sir Marmaduke, with considerable

[107-022]solemnity, led the way up to the most deserted room in the club, and

[107-023][[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] followed him, well knowing that something was to be

[107-024]said about [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]].

[107-025]Sir Marmaduke seated himself on a sofa, and his friend sat close

[107-026]beside him. The room was quite deserted. It was four o'clock in

[107-027]the afternoon, and the club was full of men. There were men in the

[107-028]morning-room, and men in the drawing-room, and men in the card-room,

[107-029]and men in the billiard-room; but no better choice of a chamber for

[107-030]a conference intended to be silent and secret could have been made

[107-031]in all [[london|London]] than that which had induced Sir Marmaduke to take his

[107-032]friend into the library of "The Acrobats." And yet a great deal of

[107-033]money had been spent in providing this library for "The Acrobats."

[107-034]Sir Marmaduke sat for awhile silent, and had he sat silent for an

[107-035]hour, Colonel Osborne would not have interrupted him. Then, at last,

[107-036]he began, with a voice that was intended to be serious, but which

[107-037]struck upon the ear of his companion as being affected and unlike the

[107-038]owner of it. "This is a very sad thing about my poor girl," said Sir

[107-039]Marmaduke.

[107-040]"Indeed it is. There is only one thing to be said about it, Rowley."

[107-041]"And what's that?"

[107-042]"The man must be mad."

[107-043]"He is not so mad as to give us any relief by his madness,--poor as

[107-044]such comfort would be. He has got Emily's child away from her, and

[107-045]I think it will about kill her. And what is to become of her? As to

[107-046]taking her back to the islands without her child, it is out of the

[107-047]question. I never knew anything so cruel in my life."

[107-048]"And so absurd, you know."

[107-049]"Ah,--that's just the question. If anybody had asked me, I should

[107-050]have said that you were the man of all men whom I could have best

[107-051]trusted."

[107-052]"Do you doubt it now?"

[107-053]"I don't know what to think."

[107-054]"Do you mean to say that you suspect me,--and your daughter too?"

[107-055]"No;--by heavens! Poor dear. If I suspected her, there would be an

[107-056]end of all things with me. I could never get over that. No; I don't

[107-057]suspect her!" Sir Marmaduke had now dropped his affected tone, and

[107-058]was speaking with natural energy.

[107-059]"But you do me?"

[107-060]"No;--if I did, I don't suppose I should be sitting with you here;

[107-061]but they tell me--"

[107-062]"They tell you what?"

[107-063]"They tell me that,--that you did not behave wisely about it. Why

[107-064]could you not let her alone when you found out how matters were

[107-065]going?"

[107-066]"Who has been telling you this, Rowley?"

[107-067]Sir Marmaduke considered for awhile, and then remembering that

[107-068]Colonel Osborne could hardly quarrel with a clergyman, told him the

[107-069]truth. "Outhouse says that you have done her an irretrievable injury

[107-070]by going down to Devonshire to her, and by writing to her."

[107-071]"Outhouse is an ass."

[107-072]"That is easily said;--but why did you go?"

[107-073]"And why should I not go? What the deuce! Because a man like that

[107-074]chooses to take vagaries into his head I am not to see my own

[107-075]godchild!" Sir Marmaduke tried to remember whether the Colonel was in

[107-076]fact the godfather of his eldest daughter, but he found that his mind

[107-077]was quite a blank about his children's godfathers and godmothers.

[107-078]"And as for the letters;--I wish you could see them. The only letters

[107-079]which had in them a word of importance were those about your coming

[107-080]home. I was anxious to get that arranged, not only for your sake, but

[107-081]because she was so eager about it."

[107-082]"God bless her, poor child," said Sir Marmaduke, rubbing the tears

[107-083]away from his eyes with his red silk pocket-handkerchief.

[107-084]"I will acknowledge that those letters,--there may have been one or

[107-085]two,--were the beginning of the trouble. It was these that made this

[107-086]man show himself to be a lunatic. I do admit that. I was bound not to

[107-087]talk about your coming, and I told her to keep the secret. He went

[107-088]spying about, and found her letters, I suppose,--and then he took

[107-089]fire because there was to be a secret from him. Dirty, mean dog! And

[107-090]now I'm to be told by such a fellow as Outhouse that it's my fault,

[107-091]that I have caused all the trouble, because, when I happened to be in

[107-092]Devonshire, I went to see your daughter!" We must do the Colonel the

[107-093]justice of supposing that he had by this time quite taught himself to

[107-094]believe that the church porch at Cockchaffington had been the motive

[107-095]cause of his journey into Devonshire. "Upon my word it is too hard,"

[107-096]continued he indignantly. "As for Outhouse,--only for the gown upon

[107-097]his back, I'd pull his nose. And I wish that you would tell him that

[107-098]I say so."

[107-099]"There is trouble enough without that," said Sir Marmaduke.

[107-100]"But it is hard. By G----, it is hard. There is this comfort;--if

[107-101]it hadn't been me, it would have been some one else. Such a man as

[107-102]that couldn't have gone two or three years, without being jealous of

[107-103]some one. And as for poor Emily, she is better off perhaps with an

[107-104]accusation so absurd as this, than she might have been had her name

[107-105]been joined with a younger man, or with one whom you would have less

[107-106]reason for trusting."

[107-107]There was so much that seemed to be sensible in this, and it was

[107-108]spoken with so well assumed a tone of injured innocence, that Sir

[107-109]Marmaduke felt that he had nothing more to say. He muttered something

[107-110]further about the cruelty of the case, and then slunk away out of the

[107-111]club, and made his way home to the dull gloomy house in Manchester

[107-112]Street. There was no comfort for him there;--but neither was there

[107-113]any comfort for him at the club. And why did that vexatious Secretary

[107-114]of State send him messages about blue books? As he went, he expressed

[107-115]sundry wishes that he was back at the Mandarins, and told himself

[107-116]that it would be well that he should remain there till he died.

[107-117]CHAPTER LXV.

[107-118]MYSTERIOUS AGENCIES.

[107-119][Illustration]

[107-120]When the thirty-first of March arrived, [[exeter|Exeter]] had not as yet been

[107-121]made gay with the marriage festivities of Mr. Gibson and Camilla

[107-122]French. And this delay had not been the fault of Camilla. Camilla had

[107-123]been ready, and when, about the middle of the month, it was hinted

[107-124]to her that some postponement was necessary, she spoke her mind out

[107-125]plainly, and declared that she was not going to stand that kind of

[107-126]thing. The communication had not been made to her by Mr. Gibson in

[107-127]person. For some days previously he had not been seen at Heavitree,

[107-128]and Camilla had from day to day become more black, gloomy, and harsh

[107-129]in her manners both to her mother and her sister. Little notes had

[107-130]come and little notes had gone, but no one in the house, except

[107-131]Camilla herself, knew what those notes contained. She would not

[107-132]condescend to complain to Arabella; nor did she say much in

[107-133]condemnation of her lover to Mrs. French, till the blow came. With

[107-134]unremitting attention she pursued the great business of her wedding

[107-135]garments, and exacted from the unfortunate Arabella an amount of work

[107-136]equal to her own,--of thankless work, as is the custom of embryo

[107-137]brides with their unmarried sisters. And she drew with great audacity

[107-138]on the somewhat slender means of the family for the amount of

[107-139]feminine gear necessary to enable her to go into Mr. Gibson's house

[107-140]with something of the eclat of a well-provided bride. When Mrs.

[107-141]French hesitated, and then expostulated, Camilla replied that she did

[107-142]not expect to be married above once, and that in no cheaper or more

[107-143]productive way than this could her mother allow her to consume her

[107-144]share of the family resources. "What matter, mamma, if you do have to

[107-145]borrow a little money? Mr. Burgess will let you have it when he knows

[107-146]why. And as I shan't be eating and drinking at home any more, nor yet

[107-147]getting my things here, I have a right to expect it." And she ended

[107-148]by expressing an opinion, in Arabella's hearing, that any daughter

[107-149]of a house who proves herself to be capable of getting a husband for

[107-150]herself, is entitled to expect that those left at home shall pinch

[107-151]themselves for a time, in order that she may go forth to the world in

[107-152]a respectable way, and be a credit to the family.

[107-153]Then came the blow. Mr. Gibson had not been at the house for some

[107-154]days, but the notes had been going and coming. At last Mr. Gibson

[107-155]came himself; but, as it happened, when he came, Camilla was out

[107-156]shopping. In these days she often did go out shopping between eleven

[107-157]and one, carrying her sister with her. It must have been but a poor

[107-158]pleasure for Arabella, this witnessing the purchases made, seeing

[107-159]the pleasant draperies, and handling the real linens and admiring

[107-160]the fine cambrics spread out before them on the shop counters by

[107-161]obsequious attendants. And the questions asked of her by her sister,

[107-162]whether this was good enough for so august an occasion, or that

[107-163]sufficiently handsome, must have been harassing. She could not have

[107-164]failed to remember that it ought all to have been done for her,--that

[107-165]had she not been treated with monstrous injustice, with most

[107-166]unsisterly cruelty, all these good things would have been spread on

[107-167]her behoof. But she went on and endured it, and worked diligently

[107-168]with her needle, and folded and unfolded as she was desired, and

[107-169]became as it were quite a younger sister in the house,--creeping out

[107-170]by herself now and again into the purlieus of the city, to find such

[107-171]consolation as she might receive from her solitary thoughts.

[107-172]But Arabella and Camilla were both away when Mr. Gibson called to

[107-173]tell Mrs. French of his altered plans. And as he asked, not for

[107-174]his lady-love, but for Mrs. French herself, it is probable that he

[107-175]watched his opportunity and that he knew to what cares his Camilla

[107-176]was then devoting herself. "Perhaps it is quite as well that I should

[107-177]find you alone," he said, after sundry preludes, to his future

[107-178]mother-in-law, "because you can make Camilla understand this better

[107-179]than I can. I must put off the day for about three weeks."

[107-180]"Three weeks, Mr. Gibson?"

[107-181]"Or a month. Perhaps we had better say the 29th of April." Mr. Gibson

[107-182]had by this time thrown off every fear that he might have entertained

[107-183]of the mother, and could speak to her of such an unwarrantable change

[107-184]of plans with tolerable equanimity.
