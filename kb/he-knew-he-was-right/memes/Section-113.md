## Section 113

[113-001]A Governor from one of the greater colonies had already been under

[113-002]question for nearly a week, and was generally thought to have come

[113-003]out of the fire unscathed by the flames of the Major's criticism.

[113-004]This Governor had been a picked man, and he had made it appear that

[113-005]the control of Downing Street was never more harsh and seldom less

[113-006]refreshing and beautifying than a spring shower in April. No other

[113-007]lands under the sun were so blest, in the way of government, as were

[113-008]the colonies with which he had been acquainted; and, as a natural

[113-009]consequence, their devotion and loyalty to the mother country were

[113-010]quite a passion with them. Now the Major had been long of a mind that

[113-011]one or two colonies had better simply be given up to other nations,

[113-012]which were more fully able to look after them than was England, and

[113-013]that three or four more should be allowed to go clear,--costing

[113-014]England nothing, and owing England nothing. But the well-chosen

[113-015]Governor who had now been before the Committee, had rather staggered

[113-016]the Major,--and things altogether were supposed to be looking up for

[113-017]the Colonial Office.

[113-018]And now had come the day of Sir Marmaduke's martyrdom. He was first

[113-019]requested, with most urbane politeness, to explain the exact nature

[113-020]of the government which he exercised in the Mandarins. Now it

[113-021]certainly was the case that the manner in which the legislative and

[113-022]executive authorities were intermingled in the affairs of these

[113-023]islands, did create a complication which it was difficult for any man

[113-024]to understand, and very difficult indeed for any man to explain to

[113-025]others. There was a Court of Chancery, so called, which Sir Marmaduke

[113-026]described as a little parliament. When he was asked whether the court

[113-027]exercised legislative or executive functions, he said at first that

[113-028]it exercised both, and then that it exercised neither. He knew that

[113-029]it consisted of nine men, of whom five were appointed by the colony

[113-030]and four by the Crown. Yet he declared that the Crown had the control

[113-031]of the court;--which, in fact, was true enough no doubt, as the five

[113-032]open members were not perhaps, all of them, immaculate patriots; but

[113-033]on this matter poor Sir Marmaduke was very obscure. When asked who

[113-034]exercised the patronage of the Crown in nominating the four members,

[113-035]he declared that the four members exercised it themselves. Did he

[113-036]appoint them? No; he never appointed anybody himself. He consulted

[113-037]the Court of Chancery for everything. At last it came out that the

[113-038]chief justice of the islands, and three other officers, always sat in

[113-039]the court;--but whether it was required by the constitution of the

[113-040]islands that this should be so, Sir Marmaduke did not know. It had

[113-041]worked well; that was to say, everybody had complained of it, but

[113-042]he, Sir Marmaduke, would not recommend any change. What he thought

[113-043]best was that the Colonial Secretary should send out his orders, and

[113-044]that the people in the colonies should mind their business and grow

[113-045]coffee. When asked what would be the effect upon the islands, under

[113-046]his scheme of government, if an incoming Colonial Secretary should

[113-047]change the policy of his predecessor, he said that he didn't think it

[113-048]would much matter if the people did not know anything about it.

[113-049]In this way the Major had a field day, and poor Sir Marmaduke

[113-050]was much discomfited. There was present on the Committee a young

[113-051]Parliamentary Under-Secretary, who with much attention had studied

[113-052]the subject of the Court of Chancery in the Mandarins, and who had

[113-053]acknowledged to his superiors in the office that it certainly was of

[113-054]all legislative assemblies the most awkward and complicated. He did

[113-055]what he could, by questions judiciously put, to pull Sir Marmaduke

[113-056]through his difficulties; but the unfortunate Governor had more than

[113-057]once lost his temper in answering the chairman; and in his heavy

[113-058]confusion was past the power of any Under-Secretary, let him be ever

[113-059]so clever, to pull him through. [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] sat by the while and

[113-060]asked no questions. He had been put on the Committee as a respectable

[113-061]dummy; but there was not a member sitting there who did not know that

[113-062]Sir Marmaduke had been brought home as his friend;--and some of them,

[113-063]no doubt, had whispered that this bringing home of Sir Marmaduke

[113-064]was part of the payment made by the Colonel for the smiles of the

[113-065]Governor's daughter. But no one alluded openly to the inefficiency of

[113-066]the [[truth|evidence]] given. No one asked why a Governor so incompetent had

[113-067]been sent to them. No one suggested that a job had been done. There

[113-068]are certain things of which opposition members of Parliament complain

[113-069]loudly;--and there are certain other things as to which they are

[113-070]silent. The line between these things is well known; and should an

[113-071]ill-conditioned, a pig-headed, an underbred, or an ignorant member

[113-072]not understand this line and transgress it, by asking questions which

[113-073]should not be asked, he is soon put down from the Treasury bench, to

[113-074]the great delight of the whole House.

[113-075]Sir Marmaduke, after having been questioned for an entire afternoon,

[113-076]left the House with extreme disgust. He was so convinced of his own

[113-077]failure, that he felt that his career as a Colonial Governor must be

[113-078]over. Surely they would never let him go back to his islands after

[113-079]such an exposition as he had made of his own ignorance. He hurried

[113-080]off into a cab, and was ashamed to be seen of men. But the members

[113-081]of the Committee thought little or nothing about it. The Major, and

[113-082]those who sided with him, had been anxious to entrap their witness

[113-083]into contradictions and absurdities, for the furtherance of their own

[113-084]object; and for the furtherance of theirs, the Under-Secretary from

[113-085]the Office and the supporters of Government had endeavoured to defend

[113-086]their man. But, when the affair was over, if no special admiration

[113-087]had been elicited for Sir Marmaduke, neither was there expressed

[113-088]any special reprobation. The Major carried on his Committee over

[113-089]six weeks, and succeeded in having his blue-book printed; but, as

[113-090]a matter of course, nothing further came of it; and the Court of

[113-091]Chancery in the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]] still continues to hold its own,

[113-092]and to do its work, in spite of the absurdities displayed in its

[113-093]construction. Major Magruder has had his day of success, and now

[113-094]feels that Othello's occupation is gone. He goes no more to the

[113-095]Colonial Office, lives among his friends on the memories of his

[113-096]Committee,--not always to their gratification,--and is beginning to

[113-097]think that as his work is done he may as well resign Killicrankie to

[113-098]some younger politician. Poor Sir Marmaduke remembered his defeat

[113-099]with soreness long after it had been forgotten by all others who had

[113-100]been present, and was astonished when he found that the journals of

[113-101]the day, though they did in some curt fashion report the proceedings

[113-102]of the Committee, never uttered a word of censure against him, as

[113-103]they had not before uttered a word of praise for that pearl of a

[113-104]Governor who had been examined before him.

[113-105]On the following morning he went to the Colonial Office by

[113-106]appointment, and then he saw the young Irish Under-Secretary whom he

[113-107]had so much dreaded. Nothing could be more civil than was the young

[113-108]Irish Under-Secretary, who told him that he had better of course stay

[113-109]in town till the Committee was over, though it was not probable that

[113-110]he would be wanted again. When the Committee had done its work he

[113-111]would be allowed to remain six weeks on service to prepare for his

[113-112]journey back. If he wanted more time after that he could ask for

[113-113]leave of absence. So Sir Marmaduke left the Colonial Office with a

[113-114]great weight off his mind, and blessed that young Irish Secretary as

[113-115]he went.

[113-116]CHAPTER LXIX.

[113-117]SIR MARMADUKE AT WILLESDEN.

[113-118]On the next day Sir Marmaduke purposed going to Willesden. He was in

[113-119]great doubt whether or no he would first consult that very eminent

[113-120]man Dr. Trite Turbury, as to the possibility, and,--if possible,--as

[113-121]to the expediency, of placing Mr. Trevelyan under some control. But

[113-122]Sir Marmaduke, though he would repeatedly declare that his son-in-law

[113-123]was mad, did not really believe in this madness. He did not, that

[113-124]is, believe that Trevelyan was so mad as to be fairly exempt from

[113-125]the penalties of [[duty|responsibility]]; and he was therefore desirous of

[113-126]speaking his own mind out fully to the man, and, as it were, of

[113-127]having his own personal revenge, before he might be deterred by the

[113-128]interposition of medical advice. He resolved therefore that he would

[113-129]not see Sir Trite Turbury, at any rate till he had come back from

[113-130]Willesden. He also went down in a cab, but he left the cab at the

[113-131]public-house at the corner of the road, and walked to the cottage.

[113-132]When he asked whether Mr. Trevelyan was at home, the woman of

[113-133]the house hesitated and then said that her lodger was out. "I

[113-134]particularly wish to see him," said Sir Marmaduke, feeling that the

[113-135]woman was lying to him. "But he ain't to be seen, sir," said the

[113-136]woman. "I know he is at home," said Sir Marmaduke. But the argument

[113-137]was soon cut short by the appearance of Trevelyan behind the woman's

[113-138]shoulder.

[113-139]"I am here, Sir Marmaduke Rowley," said Trevelyan. "If you wish to

[113-140]see me you may come in. I will not say that you are welcome, but you

[113-141]can come in." Then the woman retired, and Sir Marmaduke followed

[113-142]Trevelyan into the room in which Lady Rowley and Emily had been

[113-143]received; but the child was not now in the chamber.

[113-144]"What are these charges that I hear against my daughter?" said Sir

[113-145]Marmaduke, rushing at once into the midst of his indignation.

[113-146]"I do not know what charges you have heard."

[113-147]"You have put her away."

[113-148]"In strict accuracy that is not correct, Sir Marmaduke."

[113-149]"But she is put away. She is in my house now because you have no

[113-150]house of your own for her. Is not that so? And when I came home she

[113-151]was staying with her uncle, because you had put her away. And what

[113-152]was the meaning of her being sent down into Devonshire? What has she

[113-153]done? I am her father, and I expect to have an answer."

[113-154]"You shall have an answer, certainly."

[113-155]"And a true one. I will have no hocus-pocus, no humbug, no Jesuitry."

[113-156]"Have you come here to insult me, Sir Marmaduke? Because, if so,

[113-157]there shall be an end to this interview at once."

[113-158]"There shall not be an end;--by G----, no, not till I have heard what

[113-159]is the meaning of all this. Do you know what people are saying of

[113-160]you;--that you are mad, and that you must be locked up, and your

[113-161]child taken away from you, and your [[money|property]]?"

[113-162]"Who are the people that say so? Yourself;--and, perhaps, Lady

[113-163]Rowley? Does my wife say so? Does she think that I am mad? She did

[113-164]not think so on Thursday, when she prayed that she might be allowed

[113-165]to come back and live with me."

[113-166]"And you would not let her come?"

[113-167]"Pardon me," said Trevelyan. "I would wish that she should come,--but

[113-168]it must be on certain conditions."

[113-169]"What I want to know is why she was turned out of your house?"

[113-170]"She was not turned out."

[113-171]"What has she done that she should be punished?" urged Sir Marmaduke,

[113-172]who was unable to arrange his questions with the happiness which had

[113-173]distinguished Major Magruder. "I insist upon knowing what it is that

[113-174]you lay to her charge. I am her father, and I have a right to know.

[113-175]She has been barbarously, shamefully ill-used, and by G---- I will

[113-176]know."

[113-177]"You have come here to bully me, Sir Marmaduke Rowley."
