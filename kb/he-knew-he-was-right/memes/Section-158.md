## Section 158

[158-001]"Say a word to him in kindness before you leave us," said Mrs.

[158-002]Trevelyan. Then Nora went up to her brother-in-law's bed-side, and

[158-003]told him that she was going, and expressed a hope that he might be

[158-004]stronger when she returned. And as she did so she put her hand upon

[158-005]the bed-side, intending to press his in token of affection. But his

[158-006]face was turned from her, and he seemed to take no notice of her.

[158-007]"Louis," said his wife, "Nora is going to Monkhams. You will say

[158-008]good-bye to her before she goes?"

[158-009]"If she be not my enemy, I will," said he.

[158-010]"I have never been your enemy, Louis," said Nora, "and certainly I am

[158-011]not now."

[158-012]"She had better go," he said. "It is very little more that I expect

[158-013]of any one in this world;--but I will recognise no one as my friend

[158-014]who will not acknowledge that I have been sinned against during the

[158-015]last two years;--sinned against cruelly and utterly." Emily, who

[158-016]was [[reputation|standing]] at the bed-head, shuddered as she heard this, but made

[158-017]no reply. Nor did Nora speak again, but crept silently out of the

[158-018]room;--and in half a minute her sister followed her.

[158-019]"I feared how it would be," said Nora.

[158-020]"We can only do our best. God knows that I try to do mine."

[158-021]"I do not think you will ever see him again," said Hugh to her in the

[158-022]train.

[158-023]"Would you have had me act otherwise? It is not that it would have

[158-024]been a lie. I would not have minded that to ease the shattered

[158-025]feelings of one so infirm and suffering as he. In dealing with mad

[158-026]people I suppose one must be false. But I should have been accusing

[158-027]her; and it may be that he will get well, and it might be that he

[158-028]would then remember what I had said."

[158-029]At the [[social-class|station]] near Monkhams she was met by Lady Peterborough in the

[158-030]carriage. A tall footman in livery came on to the platform to shew

[158-031]her the way and to look after her luggage, and she could not fail to

[158-032]remember that the man might have been her own servant, instead of

[158-033]being the servant of her who now sat in Lord Peterborough's carriage.

[158-034]And when she saw the carriage, and her ladyship's great bay horses,

[158-035]and the glittering harness, and the respectably responsible coachman,

[158-036]and the arms on the panel, she smiled to herself at the sight of

[158-037]these first outward manifestations of the rank and [[money|wealth]] of the man

[158-038]who had once been her lover. There are men who look as though they

[158-039]were the owners of bay horses and responsible coachmen and family

[158-040]blazons,--from whose outward personal appearance, demeanour, and tone

[158-041]of voice, one would expect a following of liveries and a magnificence

[158-042]of belongings; but Mr. Glascock had by no means been such a man. It

[158-043]had suited his taste to keep these things in abeyance, and to place

[158-044]his pride in the oaks and elms of his park rather than in any of

[158-045]those appanages of grandeur which a man may carry about with him. He

[158-046]could talk of his breed of sheep on an occasion, but he never talked

[158-047]of his horses; and though he knew his position and all its glories as

[158-048]well as any nobleman in England, he was ever inclined to hang back a

[158-049]little in going out of a room, and to bear himself as though he were

[158-050]a small personage in the world. Some perception of all this came

[158-051]across Nora's mind as she saw the equipage, and tried to reflect, at

[158-052]a moment's notice, whether the case might have been different with

[158-053]her, had Mr. Glascock worn a little of his tinsel outside when she

[158-054]first met him. Of course she told herself that had he worn it all on

[158-055]the outside, and carried it ever so gracefully, it could have made no

[158-056]difference.

[158-057]It was very plain, however, that, though Mr. Glascock did not like

[158-058]bright feathers for himself, he chose that his wife should wear them.

[158-059]Nothing could be prettier than the way in which Caroline Spalding,

[158-060]whom we first saw as she was about to be stuck into the interior

[158-061]of the diligence, at St. Michel, now filled her carriage as Lady

[158-062]Peterborough. The greeting between them was very affectionate, and

[158-063]there was a kiss in the carriage, even though the two pretty hats,

[158-064]perhaps, suffered something. "We are so glad to have you at last,"

[158-065]said Lady Peterborough. "Of course we are very quiet; but you won't

[158-066]mind that." Nora declared that no house could be too quiet for her,

[158-067]and then said something of the melancholy scene which she had just

[158-068]left. "And no time is fixed for your own marriage? But of course it

[158-069]has not been possible. And why should you be in a hurry? We quite

[158-070]understand that this is to be your home till everything has arranged

[158-071]itself." There was a drive of four or five miles before they reached

[158-072]the park gates, and nothing could be kinder or more friendly than was

[158-073]the new peeress; but Nora told herself that there was no forgetting

[158-074]that her friend was a peeress. She would not be so ill-conditioned as

[158-075]to suggest to herself that her friend patronised her;--and, indeed,

[158-076]had she done so, the suggestion would have been false;--but she could

[158-077]not rid herself of a certain sensation of external inferiority, and

[158-078]of a feeling that the superiority ought to be on her side, as all

[158-079]this might have been hers,--only that she had not thought it worth

[158-080]her while to accept it. As these ideas came into her mind, she hated

[158-081]herself for entertaining them; and yet, come they would. While she

[158-082]was talking about her emblematic beef-steak with Hugh, she had no

[158-083]regret, no uneasiness, no conception that any state of life could be

[158-084]better for her than that state in which an emblematic beef-steak was

[158-085]of vital importance; but she could not bring her mind to the same

[158-086]condition of unalloyed purity while sitting with Lady Peterborough in

[158-087]Lord Peterborough's carriage. And for her default in this respect she

[158-088]hated herself.

[158-089]"This is the beginning of the park," said her friend.

[158-090]"And where is the house?"

[158-091]"You can't see the house for ever so far yet; it is two miles off.

[158-092]There is about a mile before you come to the gates, and over a mile

[158-093]afterwards. One has a sort of feeling when one is in that one can't

[158-094]get out,--it is so big." In so speaking, it was Lady Peterborough's

[158-095]special endeavour to state without a boast facts which were

[158-096]indifferent, but which must be stated.

[158-097]"It is very magnificent," said Nora. There was in her voice the

[158-098]slightest touch of sarcasm, which she would have given the world not

[158-099]to have uttered; but it had been irrepressible.

[158-100]Lady Peterborough understood it instantly, and forgave it, not

[158-101]attributing to it more than its true meaning, acknowledging to

[158-102]herself that it was natural. "Dear Nora," she said,--not knowing what

[158-103]to say, blushing as she spoke,--"the magnificence is nothing; but the

[158-104]man's love is everything."

[158-105]Nora shook herself, and determined that she would behave well. The

[158-106]effort should be made, and the required result should be produced by

[158-107]it. "The magnificence, as an adjunct, is a great deal," she said;

[158-108]"and for his sake, I hope that you enjoy it."

[158-109]"Of course I enjoy it."

[158-110]"Wallachia's teachings and preachings have all been thrown to the

[158-111]wind, I hope."

[158-112]"Not quite all. Poor dear Wally! I got a letter from her the

[158-113]other day, which she began by saying that she would attune her

[158-114]correspondence to my changed condition in life. I understood the

[158-115]reproach so thoroughly! And, when she told me little details of

[158-116]individual men and women, and of things she had seen, and said not a

[158-117]word about the rights of women, or even of politics generally, I felt

[158-118]that I was a degraded creature in her sight. But, though you laugh at

[158-119]her, she did me good,--and will do good to others. Here we are inside

[158-120]Monkhams, and now you must look at the avenue."

[158-121]Nora was now rather proud of herself. She had made the effort, and

[158-122]it had been successful; and she felt that she could speak naturally,

[158-123]and express her thoughts honestly. "I remember his telling me about

[158-124]the avenue the first time I ever saw him;--and here it is. I did not

[158-125]think then that I should ever live to see the glories of Monkhams.

[158-126]Does it go all the way like this to the house?"

[158-127]"Not quite;--where you see the light at the end the road turns to the

[158-128]right, and the house is just before you. There are great iron gates,

[158-129]and terraces, and wondrous paraphernalia before you get up to the

[158-130]door. I can tell you Monkhams is quite a wonder. I have to shut

[158-131]myself up every Wednesday morning, and hand the house over to Mrs.

[158-132]Crutch, the housekeeper, who comes out in a miraculous brown silk

[158-133]gown, to shew it to visitors. On other days, you'll find Mrs. Crutch

[158-134]quite civil and useful;--but on Wednesdays, she is majestic. Charles

[158-135]always goes off among his sheep on that day, and I shut myself up

[158-136]with a pile of books in a little room. You will have to be imprisoned

[158-137]with me. I do so long to peep at the visitors."

[158-138]"And I dare say they want to peep at you."

[158-139]"I proposed at first to shew them round myself;--but Charles wouldn't

[158-140]let me."

[158-141]"It would have broken Mrs. Crutch's heart."

[158-142]"That's what Charles said. He thinks that Mrs. Crutch tells them

[158-143]that I'm locked up somewhere, and that that gives a zest to the

[158-144]search. Some people from Nottingham once did break into old Lady

[158-145]Peterborough's room, and the shew was stopped for a year. There was

[158-146]such a row about it! It prevented Charles coming up for the county.

[158-147]But he wouldn't have got in; and therefore it was lucky, and saved

[158-148]money."

[158-149]By this time Nora was quite at her ease; but still there was before

[158-150]her the other difficulty, of meeting Lord Peterborough. They were

[158-151]driven out of the avenue, and round to the right, and through the

[158-152]iron gate, and up to the huge front door. There, upon the top step,

[158-153]was [[reputation|standing]] Lord Peterborough, with a billycock hat and a very old

[158-154]shooting coat, and nankeen trousers, which were considerably too

[158-155]short for him. It was one of the happinesses of his life to dress

[158-156]just as he pleased as he went about his own place; and it certainly

[158-157]was his pleasure to wear older clothes than any one else in his

[158-158]establishment. "Miss Rowley," he said, coming forward to give her

[158-159]a hand out of the carriage, "I am delighted that you should see

[158-160]Monkhams at last."

[158-161]"You see I have kept you to your promise. Caroline has been telling

[158-162]me everything about it; but she is not quite a complete guide as yet.

[158-163]She does not know where the seven oaks are. Do you remember telling

[158-164]me of the seven oaks?"

[158-165]"Of course I do. They are five miles off;--at Clatton farm, Carry.

[158-166]I don't think you have been near Clatton yet. We will ride there

[158-167]to-morrow." And thus [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] was made at home at Monkhams.

[158-168]She was made at home, and after a week or two she was very happy. She

[158-169]soon perceived that her host was a perfect gentleman, and as such,

[158-170]a man to be much loved. She had probably never questioned the fact,

[158-171]whether Mr. Glascock was a gentleman or not, and now she did not

[158-172]analyse it. It probably never occurred to her, even at the present

[158-173]time, to say to herself that he was certainly that thing, so

[158-174]impossible of definition, and so capable of recognition; but she knew

[158-175]that she had to do with one whose presence was always pleasant to

[158-176]her, whose words and acts towards her extorted her approbation, whose

[158-177]thoughts seemed to her to be always good and manly. Of course she

[158-178]had not loved him, because she had previously known [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]].

[158-179]There could be no comparison between the two men. There was a

[158-180]brightness about Hugh which Lord Peterborough could not rival.

[158-181]Otherwise,--except for this reason,--it seemed to her to be

[158-182]impossible that any young woman should fail to love Lord Peterborough

[158-183]when asked to do so.
