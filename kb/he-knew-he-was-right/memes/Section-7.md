## Section 7

[007-001]Immediately on leaving college he had come to town, and had entered

[007-002]himself at Lincoln's Inn. Now, at the time of our story, he was a

[007-003]barrister of four years' [[reputation|standing]], but had never yet made a guinea.

[007-004]He had never made a guinea by his work as a barrister, and was

[007-005]beginning to doubt of himself whether he ever would do so. Not, as he

[007-006]knew well, that guineas are generally made with ease by barristers

[007-007]of four years' [[reputation|standing]], but because, as he said to his friends, he

[007-008]did not see his way to the knack of it. He did not know an attorney

[007-009]in the world, and could not conceive how any attorney should ever

[007-010]be induced to apply to him for legal aid. He had done his work of

[007-011]learning his trade about as well as other young men, but had had no

[007-012]means of distinguishing himself within his reach. He went the Western

[007-013]Circuit because his aunt, old Miss Stanbury, lived at [[exeter|Exeter]], but, as

[007-014]he declared of himself, had he had another aunt living at York, he

[007-015]would have had nothing whatsoever to guide him in his choice. He sat

[007-016]idle in the courts, and hated himself for so sitting. So it had been

[007-017]with him for two years without any consolation or additional burden

[007-018]from other employment than that of his profession. After that, by

[007-019]some chance, he had become acquainted with the editor of the Daily

[007-020]Record, and by degrees had taken to the writing of articles. He had

[007-021]been told by all his friends, and especially by Trevelyan, that if

[007-022]he did this, he might as well sell his gown and wig. He declared,

[007-023]in reply, that he had no objection to sell his gown and wig. He did

[007-024]not see how he should ever make more money out of them than he would

[007-025]do by such sale. But for the articles which he wrote, he received

[007-026]instant payment, a process which he found to be most consolatory,

[007-027]most comfortable, and, as he said to Trevelyan, as warm to him as a

[007-028]blanket in winter.

[007-029]Trevelyan, who was a year younger than Stanbury, had taken upon

[007-030]himself to be very angry. He professed that he did not think much of

[007-031]the trade of a journalist, and told Stanbury that he was sinking from

[007-032]the highest to almost the lowest business by which an educated man

[007-033]and a [[social-class|gentleman]] could earn his bread. Stanbury had simply replied

[007-034]that he saw some bread on the one side, but none on the other; and

[007-035]that bread from some side was indispensable to him. Then there had

[007-036]come to be that famous war between Great Britain and the republic

[007-037]of Patagonia, and [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] had been sent out as a special

[007-038]correspondent by the editor and proprietor of the Daily Record.

[007-039]His letters had been much read, and had called up a great deal of

[007-040]newspaper pugnacity. He had made important statements which had been

[007-041]flatly denied, and found to be utterly false; which again had been

[007-042]warmly reasserted and proved to be most remarkably true to the

[007-043]letter. In this way the correspondence, and he as its author, became

[007-044]so much talked about that, on his return to England, he did actually

[007-045]sell his gown and wig and declare to his friends,--and to Trevelyan

[007-046]among the number,--that he intended to look to journalism for his

[007-047]future career.

[007-048]He had been often at the house in Curzon Street in the earliest

[007-049]happy days of his friend's marriage, and had thus become

[007-050]acquainted,--intimately acquainted,--with [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. And now again,

[007-051]since his return from Patagonia, that acquaintance had been renewed.

[007-052]Quite lately, since the actual sale of that wig and gown had been

[007-053]effected, he had not been there so frequently as before, because

[007-054]Trevelyan had expressed his indignation almost too openly.

[007-055]"That such a man as you should be so faint-hearted," Trevelyan had

[007-056]said, "is a thing that I can not understand."

[007-057]"Is a man faint-hearted when he finds it improbable that he shall be

[007-058]able to leap his horse over a house?"

[007-059]"What you had to do had been done by hundreds before you."

[007-060]"What I had to do has never yet been done by any man," replied

[007-061]Stanbury. "I had to live upon nothing till the lucky hour should

[007-062]strike."

[007-063]"I think you have been cowardly," said Trevelyan.

[007-064]Even this had made no quarrel between the two men; but Stanbury had

[007-065]expressed himself annoyed by his friend's language, and partly on

[007-066]that account, and partly perhaps on another, had stayed away from

[007-067]Curzon Street. As Nora Rowley had made comparisons about him, so had

[007-068]he made comparisons about her. He had owned to himself that had it

[007-069]been possible that he should marry, he would willingly entrust his

[007-070]happiness to Miss Rowley. And he had thought once or twice that

[007-071]Trevelyan had wished that such an arrangement might be made at some

[007-072]future day. Trevelyan had always been much more sanguine in expecting

[007-073]success for his friend at the Bar, than Stanbury had been for

[007-074]himself. It might well be that such a man as Trevelyan might think

[007-075]that a clever rising barrister would be an excellent husband for his

[007-076]sister-in-law, but that a man earning a precarious living as a writer

[007-077]for a penny paper would be by no means so desirable a connection.

[007-078]Stanbury, as he thought of this, declared to himself that he would

[007-079]not care two straws for Trevelyan in the matter, if he could see his

[007-080]way without other impediments. But the other impediments were there

[007-081]in such strength and numbers as to make him feel that it could not

[007-082]have been intended by Fate that he should take to himself a wife.

[007-083]Although those letters of his to the Daily Record had been so

[007-084]pre-eminently successful, he had never yet been able to earn by

[007-085]writing above twenty-five or thirty pounds a month. If that might be

[007-086]continued to him he could live upon it himself; but, even with his

[007-087]moderate views, it would not suffice for himself and family.

[007-088]He had told Trevelyan that while living as an expectant barrister he

[007-089]had no means of subsistence. In this, as Trevelyan knew, he was not

[007-090]strictly correct. There was an allowance of L100 a year coming to him

[007-091]from the aunt whose residence at Exeter had induced him to devote

[007-092]himself to the Western Circuit. His father had been a clergyman with

[007-093]a small living in Devonshire, and had now been dead some fifteen

[007-094]years. His mother and two sisters were still living in a small

[007-095]cottage in his late father's parish, on the interest of the money

[007-096]arising from a life insurance. Some pittance from sixty to seventy

[007-097]pounds a year was all they had among them. But there was a rich aunt,

[007-098]Miss Stanbury, to whom had come considerable [[money|wealth]] in a manner most

[007-099]romantic,--the little tale shall be told before this larger tale

[007-100]is completed,--and this aunt had undertaken to educate and place

[007-101]out in the world her nephew Hugh. So Hugh had been sent to Harrow,

[007-102]and then to Oxford,--where he had much displeased his aunt by not

[007-103]accomplishing great things,--and then had been set down to make his

[007-104]fortune as a barrister in [[london|London]], with an allowance of L100 a year,

[007-105]his aunt having paid, moreover, certain fees for entrance, tuition,

[007-106]and the like. The very hour in which Miss Stanbury learned that her

[007-107]nephew was writing for a penny newspaper she sent off a dispatch to

[007-108]tell him that he must give up her or the penny paper. He replied by

[007-109]saying that he felt himself called upon to earn his bread in the only

[007-110]line from which, as it seemed to him, bread would be forthcoming. By

[007-111]return of post he got another letter to say that he might draw for

[007-112]the quarter then becoming due, but that that would be the last. And

[007-113]it was the last.

[007-114]Stanbury made an ineffectual effort to induce his aunt to make over

[007-115]the allowance,--or at least a part of it,--to his mother and sisters,

[007-116]but the old lady paid no attention whatever to the request. She never

[007-117]had given, and at that moment did not intend to give, a shilling

[007-118]to the widow and daughters of her brother. Nor did she intend, or

[007-119]had she ever intended, to leave a shilling of her money to Hugh

[007-120]Stanbury,--as she had very often told him. The money was, at her

[007-121]death, to go back to the people from whom it had come to her.

[007-122]When Nora Rowley made those comparisons between Mr. Hugh Stanbury and

[007-123]Mr. Charles Glascock, they were always wound up very much in favour

[007-124]of the briefless barrister. It was not that he was the handsomer man,

[007-125]for he was by no means handsome, nor was he the bigger man, for Mr.

[007-126]Glascock was six feet tall; nor was he better dressed, for Stanbury

[007-127]was untidy rather than otherwise in his outward person. Nor had

[007-128]he any air of fashion or special grace to recommend him, for he

[007-129]was undoubtedly an awkward-mannered man. But there was a glance of

[007-130]sunshine in his eye, and a sweetness in the curl of his mouth when he

[007-131]smiled, which made Nora feel that it would have been all up with her

[007-132]had she not made so very strong a law for her own guidance. Stanbury

[007-133]was a man about five feet ten, with shoulders more than broad in

[007-134]proportion, stout limbed, rather awkward of his gait, with large feet

[007-135]and hands, with soft wavy light hair, with light grey eyes, with a

[007-136]broad, but by no means ugly, nose. His mouth and lips were large, and

[007-137]he rarely showed his teeth. He wore no other beard than whiskers,

[007-138]which he was apt to cut away through heaviness of his hand in

[007-139]shaving, till Nora longed to bid him be more careful. "He doesn't

[007-140]care what sort of a guy he makes of himself," she once said to her

[007-141]sister, almost angrily. "He is a plain man, and he knows it," Emily

[007-142]had replied. Mr. Trevelyan was doubtless a handsome man, and it was

[007-143]almost on Nora's tongue to say something ill-natured on the subject.

[007-144]Hugh Stanbury was reputed to be somewhat hot in spirit and manner. He

[007-145]would be very sage in argument, pounding down his ideas on politics,

[007-146]religion, or social life with his fist as well as his voice. He was

[007-147]quick, perhaps, at making antipathies, and quick, too, in making

[007-148]friendships; impressionable, demonstrative, eager, rapid in his

[007-149]movements,--sometimes to the great detriment of his shins and

[007-150]knuckles; and he possessed the sweetest temper that was ever given

[007-151]to a man for the blessing of a woman. This was the man between whom

[007-152]and Mr. Glascock Nora Rowley found it to be impossible not to make

[007-153]comparisons.

[007-154]On the very day after Lady Milborough's dinner party Stanbury

[007-155]overtook Trevelyan in the street, and asked his friend where he was

[007-156]going eastward. Trevelyan was on his way to call upon his lawyer, and

[007-157]said so. But he did not say why he was going to his lawyer. He had

[007-158]sent to his wife by Nora that morning to know whether she would make

[007-159]to him the promise he required. The only answer which Nora could draw

[007-160]from her sister was a counter question, demanding whether he would

[007-161]ask her pardon for the injury he had done her. Nora had been most

[007-162]eager, most anxious, most conciliatory as a messenger; but no good

[007-163]had come of these messages, and Trevelyan had gone forth to tell all

[007-164]his trouble to his family lawyer. Old Mr. Bideawhile had been his

[007-165]father's ancient and esteemed friend, and he could tell things to

[007-166]Mr. Bideawhile which he could not bring himself to tell to any

[007-167]other living man; and he could generally condescend to accept Mr.

[007-168]Bideawhile's advice, knowing that his father before him had been

[007-169]guided by the same.
