[001-148]CHAPTER I.

[001-149]SHEWING HOW WRATH BEGAN.

[001-150][Illustration]

[001-151]When [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] was twenty-four years old, he had all the world

[001-152]before him where to choose; and, among other things, he chose to go

[001-153]to the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]], and there fell in love with Emily Rowley,

[001-154]the daughter of Sir Marmaduke, the governor. Sir Marmaduke Rowley,

[001-155]at this period of his life, was a respectable middle-aged public

[001-156]servant, in good repute, who had, however, as yet achieved for

[001-157]himself neither an exalted position nor a large [[money|fortune]]. He had been

[001-158]governor of many islands, and had never lacked employment; and now,

[001-159]at the age of fifty, found himself at the Mandarins, with a salary

[001-160]of L3,000 a year, living in a temperature at which 80 deg. in the shade

[001-161]is considered to be cool, with eight daughters, and not a shilling

[001-162]saved. A governor at the Mandarins who is social by nature and

[001-163]hospitable on principle, cannot save money in the islands even on

[001-164]L3,000 a year when he has eight daughters. And at the Mandarins,

[001-165]though hospitality is a duty, the gentlemen who ate Sir Rowley's

[001-166]dinners were not exactly the men whom he or Lady Rowley desired to

[001-167]welcome to their bosoms as sons-in-law. Nor when Mr. Trevelyan came

[001-168]that way, desirous of seeing everything in the somewhat indefinite

[001-169]course of his travels, had Emily Rowley, the eldest of the flock,

[001-170]then twenty years of age, seen as yet any Mandariner who exactly came

[001-171]up to her fancy. And, as Louis Trevelyan was a remarkably handsome

[001-172]young man, who was well connected, who had been ninth wrangler at

[001-173]Cambridge, who had already published a volume of poems, and who

[001-174]possessed L3,000 a year of his own, arising from various perfectly

[001-175]secure investments, he was not forced to sigh long in vain. Indeed,

[001-176]the Rowleys, one and all, felt that providence had been very good to

[001-177]them in sending young Trevelyan on his travels in that direction, for

[001-178]he seemed to be a very pearl among men. Both Sir Marmaduke and Lady

[001-179]Rowley felt that there might be objections to such a marriage as that

[001-180]proposed to them, raised by the Trevelyan family. Lady Rowley would

[001-181]not have liked her daughter to go to England, to be received with

[001-182]cold looks by strangers. But it soon appeared that there was no one

[001-183]to make objections. Louis, the lover, had no living relative nearer

[001-184]than cousins. His father, a barrister of repute, had died a widower,

[001-185]and had left the money which he had made to an only child. The head

[001-186]of the family was a first cousin who lived in Cornwall on a moderate

[001-187]property,--a very good sort of stupid fellow, as Louis said, who

[001-188]would be quite indifferent as to any marriage that his cousin might

[001-189]make. No man could be more independent or more clearly justified in

[001-190]pleasing himself than was this lover. And then he himself proposed

[001-191]that the second daughter, Nora, should come and live with them in

[001-192][[london|London]]. What a lover to fall suddenly from the heavens into such a

[001-193]dovecote!

[001-194]"I haven't a penny-piece to give to either of them," said Sir Rowley.

[001-195]"It is my idea that girls should not have fortunes," said Trevelyan.

[001-196]"At any rate, I am quite sure that men should never look for money.

[001-197]A man must be more comfortable, and, I think, is likely to be more

[001-198]affectionate, when the money has belonged to himself."

[001-199]Sir Rowley was a high-minded [[social-class|gentleman]], who would have liked to have

[001-200]handed over a few thousand pounds on giving up his daughters; but,

[001-201]having no thousands of pounds to hand over, he could not but admire

[001-202]the principles of his proposed son-in-law. As it was about time for

[001-203]him to have his leave of absence, he and sundry of the girls went to

[001-204]England with Mr. Trevelyan, and the wedding was celebrated in London

[001-205]by the Rev. Oliphant Outhouse, of Saint Diddulph-in-the-East, who

[001-206]had married Sir Rowley's sister. Then a small house was taken and

[001-207]furnished in Curzon Street, Mayfair, and the Rowleys went back to the

[001-208]seat of their government, leaving Nora, the second girl, in charge of

[001-209]her elder sister.

[001-210]The Rowleys had found, on reaching London, that they had lighted upon

[001-211]a pearl indeed. Louis Trevelyan was a man of whom all people said

[001-212]all good things. He might have been a fellow of his college had he

[001-213]not been a man of fortune. He might already,--so Sir Rowley was

[001-214]told,--have been in Parliament, had he not thought it to be wiser to

[001-215]wait awhile. Indeed, he was very wise in many things. He had gone

[001-216]out on his travels thus young,--not in search of excitement, to kill

[001-217]beasts, or to encounter he knew not what novelty and amusement,--but

[001-218]that he might see men and know the world. He had been on his travels

[001-219]for more than a year when the winds blew him to the Mandarins. Oh,

[001-220]how blessed were the winds! And, moreover, Sir Rowley found that his

[001-221]son-in-law was well spoken of at the clubs by those who had known him

[001-222]during his university career, as a man popular as well as wise, not

[001-223]a book-worm, or a dry philosopher, or a prig. He could talk on all

[001-224]subjects, was very generous, a man sure to be honoured and respected;

[001-225]and then such a handsome, manly fellow, with short brown hair, a nose

[001-226]divinely chiselled, an Apollo's mouth, six feet high, with shoulders

[001-227]and legs and arms in proportion,--a pearl of pearls! Only, as Lady

[001-228]Rowley was the first to find out, he liked to have his own way.

[001-229]"But his way is such a good way," said Sir Marmaduke. "He will be

[001-230]such a good guide for the girls!"

[001-231]"But Emily likes her way too," said Lady Rowley.

[001-232]Sir Marmaduke argued the matter no further, but thought, no doubt,

[001-233]that such a husband as Louis Trevelyan was entitled to have his own

[001-234]way. He probably had not observed his daughter's temper so accurately

[001-235]as his wife had done. With eight of them coming up around him, how

[001-236]should he have observed their tempers? At any rate, if there were

[001-237]anything amiss with Emily's temper, it would be well that she should

[001-238]find her master in such a husband as Louis Trevelyan.

[001-239]For nearly two years the little household in Curzon Street went on

[001-240]well, or if anything was the matter no one outside of the little

[001-241]household was aware of it. And there was a baby, a boy, a young

[001-242]Louis, and a baby in such a household is apt to make things go

[001-243]sweetly.

[001-244]The marriage had taken place in July, and after the wedding tour

[001-245]there had been a winter and a spring in London; and then they passed

[001-246]a month or two at the sea-side, after which the baby had been born.

[001-247]And then there came another winter and another spring. [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]

[001-248]was with them in London, and by this time Mr. Trevelyan had begun to

[001-249]think that he should like to have his own way completely. His baby

[001-250]was very nice, and his wife was clever, pretty, and attractive. Nora

[001-251]was all that an unmarried sister should be. But,--but there had come

[001-252]to be trouble and bitter words. Lady Rowley had been right when she

[001-253]said that her daughter Emily also liked to have her own way.
