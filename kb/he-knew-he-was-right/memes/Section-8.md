## Section 8

[008-001]"But you are out of your way for Lincoln's Inn Fields," said

[008-002]Stanbury.

[008-003]"I have to call at Twining's. And where are you going?"

[008-004]"I have been three times round St. James's Park to collect my

[008-005]thoughts," said Stanbury, "and now I am on my way to the Daily R.,

[008-006]250, Fleet Street. It is my custom of an afternoon. I am prepared

[008-007]to instruct the British public of to-morrow on any subject, as per

[008-008]order, from the downfall of a European compact to the price of a

[008-009][[london|London]] mutton chop."

[008-010]"I suppose there is nothing more to be said about it," said

[008-011]Trevelyan, after a pause.

[008-012]"Not another word. How should there be? Aunt Jemima has already drawn

[008-013]tight the purse strings, and it would soon be the casual ward in

[008-014]earnest if it were not for the Daily R. God bless the Daily R. Only

[008-015]think what a thing it is to have all subjects open to one, from the

[008-016]destinies of France to the profit proper to a butcher."

[008-017]"If you like it!"

[008-018]"I do like it. It may not be altogether honest. I don't know what

[008-019]is. But it's a deal honester than defending thieves and bamboozling

[008-020]juries. How is your wife?"

[008-021]"She's pretty well, thank you."

[008-022]Stanbury knew at once from the tone of his friend's voice that there

[008-023]was something wrong.

[008-024]"And Louis the less?" he said, asking after Trevelyan's child.

[008-025]"He's all right."

[008-026]"And Miss Rowley? When one begins one's inquiries one is bound to go

[008-027]through the whole family."

[008-028]"Miss Rowley is pretty well," said Trevelyan.

[008-029]Previously to this, Trevelyan when speaking of his sister-in-law to

[008-030]Stanbury, had always called her Nora, and had been wont to speak

[008-031]of her as though she were almost as much the friend of one of them

[008-032]as of the other. The change of tone on this occasion was in truth

[008-033]occasioned by the sadness of the man's thoughts in reference to his

[008-034]wife, but Stanbury attributed it to another cause. "He need not be

[008-035]afraid of me," he said to himself, "and at least he should not show

[008-036]me that he is." Then they parted, Trevelyan going into Twining's

[008-037]bank, and Stanbury passing on towards the office of the Daily R.

[008-038]Stanbury had in truth been altogether mistaken as to the state of his

[008-039]friend's mind on that morning. Trevelyan, although he had, according

[008-040]to his custom, put in a word in condemnation of the newspaper line of

[008-041]life, was at the moment thinking whether he would not tell all his

[008-042]trouble to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. He knew that he should not find anywhere,

[008-043]not even in Mr. Bideawhile, a more friendly or more trustworthy

[008-044]listener. When [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]'s name had been mentioned, he had not

[008-045]thought of her. He had simply repeated the name with the usual

[008-046]answer. He was at the moment cautioning himself against a confidence

[008-047]which after all might not be necessary, and which on this occasion

[008-048]was not made. When one is in trouble it is a great ease to tell one's

[008-049]trouble to a friend; but then one should always wash one's dirty

[008-050]linen at home. The latter consideration prevailed, and Trevelyan

[008-051]allowed his friend to go on without burdening him with the story of

[008-052]that domestic quarrel. Nor did he on that occasion tell it to Mr.

[008-053]Bideawhile; for Mr. Bideawhile was not found at his chambers.

[008-054]CHAPTER V.

[008-055]SHEWING HOW THE QUARREL PROGRESSED.

[008-056]Trevelyan got back to his own house at about three, and on going into

[008-057]the library, found on his table a letter to him addressed in his

[008-058]wife's handwriting. He opened it quickly, hoping to find that promise

[008-059]which he had demanded, and resolving that if it were made he would at

[008-060]once become affectionate, yielding, and gentle to his wife. But there

[008-061]was not a word written by his wife within the envelope. It contained

[008-062]simply another letter, already opened, addressed to her. This letter

[008-063]had been brought up to her during her husband's absence from the

[008-064]house, and was as follows:--

[008-065]Acrobats, Thursday.

[008-066]DEAR EMILY,

[008-067]I have just come from the Colonial Office. It is all

[008-068]settled, and Sir M. has been sent for. Of course, you will

[008-069]tell T. now.

[008-070]Yours, F. O.

[008-071]The letter was, of course, from [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], and Mrs. Trevelyan,

[008-072]when she received it, had had great doubts whether she would enclose

[008-073]it to her husband opened or unopened. She had hitherto refused to

[008-074]make the promise which her husband exacted, but nevertheless, she was

[008-075]minded to obey him. Had he included in his demand any requirement

[008-076]that she should receive no letter from Colonel Osborne, she would not

[008-077]have opened this one. But nothing had been said about letters, and

[008-078]she would not shew herself to be afraid. So she read the note, and

[008-079]then sent it down to be put on Mr. Trevelyan's table in an envelope

[008-080]addressed to him.

[008-081]"If he is not altogether blinded, it will show him how cruelly he has

[008-082]wronged me," said she to her sister. She was sitting at the time with

[008-083]her boy in her lap, telling herself that the child's features were in

[008-084]all respects the very same as his father's, and that, come what come

[008-085]might, the child should always be taught by her to love and respect

[008-086]his father. And then there came a horrible thought. What if the child

[008-087]should be taken away from her? If this quarrel, out of which she saw

[008-088]no present mode of escape, were to lead to a separation between her

[008-089]and her husband, would not the law, and the judges, and the courts,

[008-090]and all the Lady Milboroughs of their joint acquaintance into the

[008-091]bargain, say that the child should go with his father? The judges,

[008-092]and the courts, and the Lady Milboroughs would, of course, say that

[008-093]she was the sinner. And what could she do without her boy? Would not

[008-094]any humility, any grovelling in the dust be better for her than that?

[008-095]"It is a very poor thing to be a woman," she said to her sister.

[008-096]"It is perhaps better than being a dog," said Nora; "but, of course,

[008-097]we can't compare ourselves to men."

[008-098]"It would be better to be a dog. One wouldn't be made to suffer so

[008-099]much. When a puppy is taken away from its mother, she is bad enough

[008-100]for a few days, but she gets over it in a week." There was a pause

[008-101]then for a few moments. Nora knew well which way ran the current of

[008-102]her sister's thoughts, and had nothing at the present moment which

[008-103]she could say on that subject. "It is very hard for a woman to know

[008-104]what to do," continued Emily, "but if she is to marry, I think she

[008-105]had better marry a fool. After all, a fool generally knows that he is

[008-106]a fool, and will trust some one, though he may not trust his wife."

[008-107]"I will never wittingly marry a fool," said Nora.

[008-108]"You will marry Mr. Glascock, of course. I don't say that he is a

[008-109]fool; but I do not think he has that kind of strength which shows

[008-110]itself in perversity."

[008-111]"If he asked me, I should not have him;--and he will never ask me."

[008-112]"He will ask you, and, of course, you'll take him. Why not? You can't

[008-113]be otherwise than a woman. And you must marry. And this man is a

[008-114][[social-class|gentleman]], and will be a peer. There is nothing on earth against him,

[008-115]except that he does not set the Thames on fire. Louis intends to set

[008-116]the Thames on fire some day, and see what comes of it."

[008-117]"All the same, I shall not marry Mr. Glascock. A woman can die, at

[008-118]any rate," said Nora.

[008-119]"No, she can't. A woman must be decent; and to die of want is very

[008-120]indecent. She can't die, and she mustn't be in want, and she oughtn't

[008-121]to be a burden. I suppose it was thought necessary that every man

[008-122]should have two to choose from; and therefore there are so many more

[008-123]of us than the world wants. I wonder whether you'd mind taking that

[008-124]down-stairs to his table? I don't like to send it by the servant; and

[008-125]I don't want to go myself."

[008-126]Then Nora had taken the letter down, and left it where Louis

[008-127]Trevelyan would be sure to find it.

[008-128]He did find it, and was sorely disappointed when he perceived that

[008-129]it contained no word from his wife to himself. He opened Colonel

[008-130]Osborne's note, and read it, and became, as he did so, almost more

[008-131]angry than before. Who was this man that he should dare to address

[008-132]another man's wife as "Dear Emily?" At the moment Trevelyan

[008-133]remembered well enough that he had heard the man so call his wife,

[008-134]that it had been done openly in his presence, and had not given him

[008-135]a thought. But Lady Rowley and Sir Marmaduke had then been present

[008-136]also; and that man on that occasion had been the old friend of the

[008-137]old father, and not the would-be young friend of the young daughter.

[008-138]Trevelyan could hardly reason about it, but felt that whereas the one

[008-139]was not improper, the other was grossly impertinent, and even wicked.

[008-140]And then, again, his wife, his Emily, was to show to him, to her

[008-141]husband, or was not to show to him, the letter which she received

[008-142]from this man, the letter in which she was addressed as "Dear Emily,"

[008-143]according to this man's judgment and wish, and not according to his

[008-144]judgment and wish,--not according to the judgment and wish of him who

[008-145]was her husband, her lord, and her master! "Of course you will tell

[008-146]T. now." This was intolerable to him. It made him feel that he was

[008-147]to be regarded as second, and this man to be regarded as first. And

[008-148]then he began to recapitulate all the good things he had done for his

[008-149]wife, and all the causes which he had given her for gratitude. Had

[008-150]he not taken her to his bosom, and bestowed upon her the half of all

[008-151]that he had simply for herself, asking for nothing more than her

[008-152]love? He had possessed money, position, a name,--all that makes life

[008-153]worth having. He had found her in a remote corner of the world, with

[008-154]no [[money|fortune]], with no advantages of family or social [[reputation|standing]],--so

[008-155]circumstanced that any friend would have warned him against such

[008-156]a marriage; but he had given her his heart, and his hand, and his

[008-157]house, and had asked for nothing in return but that he should be all

[008-158]in all to her,--that he should be her one god upon earth. And he had

[008-159]done more even than this. "Bring your sister," he had said. "The

[008-160]house shall be big enough for her also, and she shall be my sister as

[008-161]well as yours." Who had ever done more for a woman, or shown a more

[008-162]absolute confidence? And now what was the return he received? She was

[008-163]not contented with her one god upon earth, but must make to herself

[008-164]other gods,--another god, and that too out of a lump of the basest

[008-165]clay to be found around her. He thought that he could remember to

[008-166]have heard it said in early days, long before he himself had had

[008-167]an idea of marrying, that no man should look for a wife from among

[008-168]the tropics, that women educated amidst the languors of those sunny

[008-169]climes rarely came to possess those high ideas of conjugal duty and

[008-170]feminine truth which a man should regard as the first requisites of a

[008-171]good wife. As he thought of all this, he almost regretted that he had

[008-172]ever visited the Mandarins, or ever heard the name of Sir Marmaduke

[008-173]Rowley.

[008-174]He should have nourished no such thoughts in his heart. He had,

[008-175]indeed, been generous to his wife and to his wife's family; but we

[008-176]may almost say that the man who is really generous in such matters,

[008-177]is unconscious of his own generosity. The giver who gives the most,

[008-178]gives, and does not know that he gives. And had not she given too?

[008-179]In that matter of giving between a man and his wife, if each gives

[008-180]all, the two are equal, let the things given be what they may! King

[008-181]Cophetua did nothing for his beggar maid, unless she were to him,

[008-182]after he had married her, as royal a queen as though he had taken her

[008-183]from the oldest stock of reigning families then extant. Trevelyan

[008-184]knew all this himself,--had said so to himself a score of times,

[008-185]though not probably in spoken words or formed sentences. But, that

[008-186]all was equal between himself and the wife of his bosom, had been

[008-187]a thing ascertained by him as a [[truth|certainty]]. There was no debt of

[008-188]gratitude from her to him which he did not acknowledge to exist also

[008-189]as from him to her. But yet, in his anger, he could not keep himself

[008-190]from thinking of the gifts he had showered upon her. And he had been,

[008-191]was, would ever be, if she would only allow it, so true to her! He

[008-192]had selected no other friend to take her place in his councils! There

[008-193]was no "dear Mary," or "dear Augusta," with whom he had secrets to

[008-194]be kept from his wife. When there arose with him any question of

[008-195]interest,--question of interest such as was this of the return of Sir

[008-196]Marmaduke to her,--he would show it in all its bearings to his wife.

[008-197]He had his secrets too, but his secrets had all been made secrets for

[008-198]her also. There was not a woman in the world in whose company he took

[008-199]special delight in her absence.
