[040-020]CHAPTER XXIV.

[040-021]NIDDON PARK.

[040-022]As [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] went over to Lessboro', and from thence to Nuncombe

[040-023]Putney, he thought more of himself and [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] than he did of Mr.

[040-024]and Mrs. Trevelyan. As to Mrs. Trevelyan and [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], he felt

[040-025]that he knew everything that it was necessary that he should know.

[040-026]The man had been there, and had seen Mrs. Trevelyan. Of that there

[040-027]could be no doubt. That Colonel Osborne had been wickedly indifferent

[040-028]to the evil consequences of such a visit, and that all the women

[040-029]concerned had been most foolish in permitting him to make it, was his

[040-030]present conviction. But he did not for a moment doubt that the visit

[040-031]had in itself been of all things the most innocent. Trevelyan had

[040-032]sworn that if his wife received the man at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], he

[040-033]would never see her again. She had seen him, and this oath would be

[040-034]remembered, and there would be increased difficulties. But these

[040-035]difficulties, whatever they might be, must be overcome. When he had

[040-036]told himself this, then he allowed his mind to settle itself on Nora

[040-037]Rowley.

[040-038]Hitherto he had known Miss Rowley only as a fashionable girl living

[040-039]with the wife of an intimate friend of his own in [[london|London]]. He had

[040-040]never been staying in the same house with her. Circumstances had

[040-041]never given to him the opportunity of assuming the manner of an

[040-042]intimate friend, justifying him in giving advice, and authorising

[040-043]him to assume that semi-paternal tone which is by far the easiest

[040-044]preliminary to love-making. When a man can tell a young lady what

[040-045]she ought to read, what she ought to do, and whom she ought to know,

[040-046]nothing can be easier than to assure her that, of all her duties,

[040-047]her first duty is to prefer himself to all the world. And any young

[040-048]lady who has consented to receive lessons from such a teacher, will

[040-049]generally be willing to receive this special lesson among others.

[040-050]But Stanbury had hitherto had no such opportunities. In London Miss

[040-051]Rowley had been a fashionable young lady, living in Mayfair, and he

[040-052]had been,--well, anything but a fashionable young man. Nevertheless,

[040-053]he had seen her often, had sat by her very frequently, was quite sure

[040-054]that he loved her dearly, and had, perhaps, some self-flattering

[040-055]idea in his mind that had he stuck to his honourable profession as a

[040-056]barrister, and were he possessed of some comfortable little [[money|fortune]]

[040-057]of his own, he might, perhaps, have been able, after due siege

[040-058]operations, to make this charming young woman his own. Things were

[040-059]quite changed now. For the present, Miss Rowley certainly could not

[040-060]be regarded as a fashionable London young lady. The house in which he

[040-061]would see her was, in some sort, his own. He would be sleeping under

[040-062]the same roof with her, and would have all the advantages which such

[040-063]a position could give him. He would have no difficulty now in asking,

[040-064]if he should choose to ask; and he thought that she might be somewhat

[040-065]softer, somewhat more likely to yield at Nuncombe Putney, than she

[040-066]would have been in London. She was at Nuncombe in weak circumstances,

[040-067]to a certain degree friendless; with none of the excitement of

[040-068]society around her, with no elder sons buzzing about her and

[040-069]filling her mind, if not her heart, with the glories of luxurious

[040-070]primogeniture. Hugh Stanbury certainly did not dream that any

[040-071]special elder son had as yet been so attracted as to have made a

[040-072]journey to Nuncombe Putney on Nora's behalf. But should he on this

[040-073]account,--because she would be, as it were, without means of defence

[040-074]from his attack,--should he therefore take advantage of her weakness?

[040-075]She would, of course, go back to her London life after some short

[040-076]absence, and would again, if free, have her chance among the favoured

[040-077]ones of the earth. What had he to offer to her? He had taken the

[040-078][[clock-house|Clock House]] for his mother, and it would be quite as much as he could

[040-079]do, when Mrs. Trevelyan should have left the village, to keep up that

[040-080]establishment and maintain himself in London,--quite as much as he

[040-081]could do, even though the favours of the "D. R." should flow upon

[040-082]him with their fullest tides. In such circumstances, would it be

[040-083]honourable in him to ask a girl to love him because he found her

[040-084]defenceless in his mother's house?

[040-085]"If there bain't another for Nuncombe," said Mrs. Clegg's Ostler to

[040-086]Mrs. Clegg's Boots, as Stanbury was driven off in a gig.

[040-087]"That be young Stanbury, a-going of whome."

[040-088]"They be all a-going for the Clock House. Since the old 'ooman took

[040-089]to thick there house, there be folk a-comin' and a-goin' every day

[040-090]loike."

[040-091]"It's along of the madam that they keeps there, Dick," said the

[040-092]Boots.

[040-093]"I didn't care if there'd be madams allays. They're the best as is

[040-094]going for trade anyhow," said the ostler. What the ostler said was

[040-095]true. When there comes to be a feeling that a woman's [[honour|character]] is in

[040-096]any way tarnished, there comes another feeling that everybody on the

[040-097]one side may charge double, and that everybody on the other side must

[040-098]pay double, for everything. Hugh Stanbury could not understand why he

[040-099]was charged a shilling a mile, instead of ninepence, for the gig to

[040-100]Nuncombe Putney. He got no satisfactory answer, and had to pay the

[040-101]shilling. The truth was, that gigs to Nuncombe Putney had gone up,

[040-102]since a lady, separated from her husband, with a colonel running

[040-103]after her, had been taken in at the Clock House.

[040-104]"Here's Hugh!" said Priscilla, hurrying to the front door. And Mrs.

[040-105]Stanbury hurried after her. Her son Hugh was the apple of her eye,

[040-106]the best son that ever lived, generous, noble, a thorough

[040-107]man,--almost a god!

[040-108]"Dear, dear, oh dear! Who'd have expected it? God bless you, my boy!

[040-109]Why didn't you write? Priscilla, what is there in the house that he

[040-110]can eat?"

[040-111]"Plenty of bread and cheese," said Priscilla, laughing, with her hand

[040-112]inside her brother's arm. For though Priscilla hated all other men,

[040-113]she did not hate her brother Hugh. "If you wanted things nice to eat

[040-114]directly you got here, you ought to have written."

[040-115]"I shall want my dinner, like any other Christian,--in due time,"

[040-116]said Hugh. "And how is Mrs. Trevelyan,--and how is Miss Rowley?"

[040-117]He soon found himself in company with those two ladies, and

[040-118]experienced some immediate difficulty in explaining the cause of his

[040-119]sudden coming. But this was soon put aside by Mrs. Trevelyan.

[040-120]"When did you see my husband?" she asked.

[040-121]"I saw him yesterday. He was quite well."

[040-122]"Colonel Osborne has been here," she said.

[040-123]"I know that he has been here. I met him at the [[social-class|station]] at [[exeter|Exeter]].

[040-124]Perhaps I should not say so, but I wish he had remained away."

[040-125]"We all wish it," said Priscilla.

[040-126]Then Nora spoke. "But what could we do, Mr. Stanbury? It seemed so

[040-127]natural that he should call when he was in the neighbourhood. We have

[040-128]known him so long; and how could we refuse to see him?"

[040-129]"I will not let any one think that I'm afraid to see any man on

[040-130]earth," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "If he had ever in his life said a word

[040-131]that he should not have said, a word that would have been an insult,

[040-132]of course it would have been different. But the notion of it is

[040-133]preposterous. Why should I not have seen him?"

[040-134]"I think he was wrong to come," said Hugh.

[040-135]"Of course he was wrong;--wickedly wrong," said Priscilla.

[040-136]Stanbury, finding that the subject was openly discussed between

[040-137]them, declared plainly the mission that had brought him to Nuncombe.

[040-138]"Trevelyan heard that he was coming, and asked me to let him know the

[040-139]truth."

[040-140]"Now you can tell him the truth," said Mrs. Trevelyan, with something

[040-141]of indignation in her tone, as though she thought that Stanbury had

[040-142]taken upon himself a task of which he ought to be ashamed.

[040-143]"But Colonel Osborne came specially to pay a visit to

[040-144]Cockchaffington," said Nora, "and not to see us. Louis ought to know

[040-145]that."

[040-146]"Nora, how can you demean yourself to care about such trash?" said

[040-147]Mrs. Trevelyan. "Who cares why he came here? His visit to me was a

[040-148]thing of course. If Mr. Trevelyan disapproves of it, let him say so,

[040-149]and not send secret messengers."

[040-150]"Am I a secret messenger?" said Hugh Stanbury.

[040-151]"There has been a man here, inquiring of the servants," said

[040-152]Priscilla. So that odious Bozzle had made his foul mission known

[040-153]to them! Stanbury, however, thought it best to say nothing of

[040-154]Bozzle,--not to acknowledge that he had ever heard of Bozzle. "I am

[040-155]sure Mrs. Trevelyan does not mean you," said Priscilla.

[040-156]"I do not know what I mean," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "I am so harassed

[040-157]and fevered by these suspicions that I am driven nearly mad." Then

[040-158]she left the room for a minute and returned with two letters. "There,

[040-159]Mr. Stanbury; I got that note from Colonel Osborne, and wrote to him

[040-160]that reply. You know all about it now. Can you say that I was wrong

[040-161]to see him?"

[040-162]"I am sure that he was wrong to come," said Hugh.

[040-163]"Wickedly wrong," said Priscilla, again.

[040-164]"You can keep the letters, and show them to my husband," said Mrs.

[040-165]Trevelyan; "then he will know all about it." But Stanbury declined to

[040-166]keep the letters.

[040-167]He was to remain the Sunday at Nuncombe Putney and return to London

[040-168]on the Monday. There was, therefore, but one day on which he could

[040-169]say what he had to say to Nora Rowley. When he came down to breakfast

[040-170]on the Sunday morning he had almost made up his mind that he had

[040-171]nothing to say to her. As for Nora, she was in a state of mind much

[040-172]less near to any fixed purpose. She had told herself that she loved

[040-173]this man,--had indeed done so in the clearest way, by acknowledging

[040-174]the fact of her love to another suitor, by pleading to that other

[040-175]suitor the fact of her love as an insuperable reason why he should

[040-176]be rejected. There was no longer any doubt about it to her. When

[040-177]Priscilla had declared that Hugh Stanbury was at the door, her heart

[040-178]had gone into her mouth. Involuntarily she had pressed her hands to

[040-179]her sides, and had held her breath. Why had he come there? Had he

[040-180]come there for her? Oh! if he had come there for her, and if she

[040-181]might dare to forget all the future, how sweet,--sweetest of all

[040-182]things in heaven or earth,--might be an August evening with him among

[040-183]the lanes! But she, too, had endeavoured to be very prudent. She

[040-184]had told herself that she was quite unfit to be the wife of a poor

[040-185]man,--that she would be only a burden round his neck, and not an aid

[040-186]to him. And in so telling herself, she had told herself also that she

[040-187]had been a fool not to accept Mr. Glascock. She should have dragged

[040-188]out from her heart the image of this man who had never even whispered

[040-189]a word of love in her ears, and should have constrained herself to

[040-190]receive with affection a man in loving whom there ought to be no

[040-191]difficulty. But when she had been repeating those lessons to herself,

[040-192]Hugh Stanbury had not been in the house. Now he was there;--and what

[040-193]must be her answer if he should whisper that word of love? She had an

[040-194]idea that it would be treason in her to disown the love she felt, if

[040-195]questioned concerning her heart by the man to whom it had been given.
