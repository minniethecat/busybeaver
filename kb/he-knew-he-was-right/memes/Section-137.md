## Section 137

[137-001]"You do love me?" Bella said to him. It was natural that she should

[137-002]ask him; but it would have been better perhaps if she had held her

[137-003]tongue. Had she spoken to him about his house, or his [[money|income]], or the

[137-004]servants, or the duties of his parish church, it would have been

[137-005]easier for him to make a comfortable reply.

[137-006]"Yes;--I love you," he replied; "of course I love you. We have always

[137-007]been friends, and I hope things will go straight now. I have had

[137-008]a great deal to go through, Bella, and so have you;--but God will

[137-009]temper the wind to the shorn lambs." How was the wind to be tempered

[137-010]for the poor lamb who had gone forth shorn down to the very skin!

[137-011]Soon after this Mrs. French returned to the room, and then there was

[137-012]no more romance. Mrs. French had by no means forgiven Mr. Gibson

[137-013]all the trouble he had brought into the family, and mixed a certain

[137-014]amount of acrimony with her entertainment of him. She dictated to

[137-015]him, treated him with but scant respect, and did not hesitate to let

[137-016]him understand that he was to be watched very closely till he was

[137-017]actually and absolutely married. The poor man had in truth no further

[137-018]idea of escape. He was aware that he had done that which made it

[137-019]necessary that he should bear a great deal, and that he had no right

[137-020]to resent suspicion. When a day was fixed in June on which he should

[137-021]be married at the church of Heavitree, and it was proposed that he

[137-022]should be married by banns, he had nothing to urge to the contrary.

[137-023]And when it was also suggested to him by one of the prebendaries of

[137-024]the Cathedral that it might be well for him to change his clerical

[137-025]duties for a period with the vicar of a remote parish in the north

[137-026]of Cornwall,--so as to be out of the way of remark from those whom

[137-027]he had scandalised by his conduct,--he had no objection to make to

[137-028]that arrangement. When Mrs. MacHugh met him in the Close, and told

[137-029]him that he was a gay Lothario, he shook his head with a melancholy

[137-030]self-abasement, and passed on without even a feeling of anger. "When

[137-031]they smite me on the right cheek, I turn unto them my left," he said

[137-032]to himself, when one of the cathedral vergers remarked to him that

[137-033]after all he was going to be married, at last. Even Bella became

[137-034]dominant over him, and assumed with him occasionally the air of one

[137-035]who had been injured.

[137-036]Bella wrote a touching letter to her sister;--a letter that ought to

[137-037]have touched Camilla, begging for forgiveness, and for one word of

[137-038]sisterly love. Camilla answered the letter, but did not send a word

[137-039]of sisterly love. "According to my way of thinking, you have been a

[137-040]nasty sly thing, and I don't believe you'll ever be happy. As for

[137-041]him, I'll never speak to him again." That was nearly the whole of her

[137-042]letter. "You must leave it to time," said Mrs. French wisely; "she'll

[137-043]come round some day." And then Mrs. French thought how bad it would

[137-044]be for her if the daughter who was to be her future companion did not

[137-045]"come round" some day.

[137-046]And so it was settled that they should be married in Heavitree

[137-047]Church,--Mr. Gibson and his first love,--and things went on

[137-048]pretty much as though nothing had been done amiss. The [[social-class|gentleman]]

[137-049]from Cornwall came down to take Mr. Gibson's place at St.

[137-050]Peter's-cum-Pumpkin, while his duties in the Cathedral were

[137-051]temporarily divided among the other priest-vicars,--with some amount

[137-052]of grumbling on their part. Bella commenced her modest preparations

[137-053]without any of the eclat which had attended Camilla's operations, but

[137-054]she felt more [[truth|certainty]] of ultimate success than had ever fallen to

[137-055]Camilla's lot. In spite of all that had come and gone, Bella never

[137-056]feared again that Mr. Gibson would be untrue to her. In regard to

[137-057]him, it must be doubted whether Nemesis ever fell upon him with a

[137-058]hand sufficiently heavy to punish him for the great sins which he

[137-059]had manifestly committed. He had encountered a bad week or two, and

[137-060]there had been days in which, as has been said, he thought of Natal,

[137-061]of ecclesiastical censures, and even of annihilation; but no real

[137-062]punishment seemed to fall upon him. It may be doubted whether, when

[137-063]the whole arrangement was settled for him, and when he heard that

[137-064]Camilla had yielded to the decrees of Fate, he did not rather flatter

[137-065]himself on being a successful man of intrigue,--whether he did not

[137-066]take some glory to himself for his good fortune with women, and pride

[137-067]himself amidst his self-reproaches for the devotion which had been

[137-068]displayed for him by the fair sex in general. It is quite possible

[137-069]that he taught himself to believe that at one time Dorothy Stanbury

[137-070]was devotedly in love with him, and that when he reckoned up his

[137-071]sins she was one of those in regard to whom he accounted himself

[137-072]to have been a sinner. The spirit of intrigue with women, as to

[137-073]which men will flatter themselves, is customarily so vile, so mean,

[137-074]so vapid a reflection of a feeling, so aimless, resultless, and

[137-075]utterly unworthy! Passion exists and has its sway. Vice has its

[137-076]votaries,--and there is, too, that worn-out longing for vice,

[137-077]"prurient, yet passionless, cold-studied lewdness," which drags on

[137-078]a feeble continuance with the aid of money. But the commonest folly

[137-079]of man in regard to women is a weak taste for intrigue, with little

[137-080]or nothing on which to feed it;--a worse than feminine aptitude for

[137-081]male coquetry, which never ascends beyond a desire that somebody

[137-082]shall hint that there is something peculiar; and which is shocked and

[137-083]retreats backwards into its boots when anything like a consequence

[137-084]forces itself on the apprehension. Such men have their glory in their

[137-085]own estimation. We remember how Falstaff flouted the pride of his

[137-086]companion whose victory in the fields of love had been but little

[137-087]glorious. But there are victories going now-a-days so infinitely less

[137-088]glorious, that Falstaff's page was a Lothario, a very Don Juan, in

[137-089]comparison with the heroes whose praises are too often sung by their

[137-090]own lips. There is this recompense,--that their defeats are always

[137-091]sung by lips louder than their own. Mr. Gibson, when he found that he

[137-092]was to escape apparently unscathed,--that people [[reputation|standing]] respectably

[137-093]before the world absolutely dared to whisper words to him of

[137-094]congratulation on this third attempt at marriage within little more

[137-095]than a year, took pride to himself, and bethought himself that he

[137-096]was a gay deceiver. He believed that he had selected his wife,--and

[137-097]that he had done so in circumstances of peculiar difficulty! Poor Mr.

[137-098]Gibson,--we hardly know whether most to pity him, or the unfortunate,

[137-099]poor woman who ultimately became Mrs. Gibson.

[137-100]"And so Bella French is to be the fortunate woman after all," said

[137-101]Miss Stanbury to her niece.

[137-102]"It does seem to me to be so odd," said Dorothy. "I wonder how he

[137-103]looked when he proposed it."

[137-104]"Like a fool,--as he always does."

[137-105]Dorothy refrained from remarking that Miss Stanbury had not always

[137-106]thought that Mr. Gibson looked like a fool, but the idea occurred to

[137-107]her mind. "I hope they will be happy at last," she said.

[137-108]"Pshaw! Such people can't be happy, and can't be unhappy. I don't

[137-109]suppose it much matters which he marries, or whether he marries them

[137-110]both, or neither. They are to be married by banns, they say,--at

[137-111]Heavitree."

[137-112]"I don't see anything bad in that."

[137-113]"Only Camilla might step out and forbid them," said Aunt Stanbury. "I

[137-114]almost wish she would."

[137-115]"She has gone away, aunt,--to an uncle who lives at Gloucester."

[137-116]"It was well to get her out of the way, no doubt. They'll be married

[137-117]before you now, Dolly."

[137-118]"That won't break my heart, aunt."

[137-119]"I don't suppose there'll be much of a wedding. They haven't anybody

[137-120]belonging to them, except that uncle at Gloucester." Then there was a

[137-121]pause. "I think it is a nice thing for friends to collect together at

[137-122]a wedding," continued Aunt Stanbury.

[137-123]"I think it is," said Dorothy, in the mildest, softest voice.

[137-124]"I suppose we must make room for that black sheep of a brother of

[137-125]yours, Dolly,--or else you won't be contented."

[137-126]"Dear, dear, dearest aunt!" said Dorothy, falling down on her knees

[137-127]at her aunt's feet.

[137-128]CHAPTER LXXXIV.

[137-129]SELF-SACRIFICE.

[137-130][Illustration]

[137-131]Trevelyan, when his wife had left him, sat for hours in silence

[137-132]pondering over his own position and hers. He had taken his child to

[137-133]an upper room, in which was his own bed and the boy's cot, and before

[137-134]he seated himself, he spread out various toys which he had been at

[137-135]pains to purchase for the unhappy little fellow,--a regiment of

[137-136]Garibaldian soldiers, all with red shirts, and a drum to give the

[137-137]regiment martial spirit, and a soft fluffy Italian ball, and a

[137-138]battledore and a shuttlecock,--instruments enough for juvenile joy,

[137-139]if only there had been a companion with whom the child could use

[137-140]them. But the toys remained where the father had placed them, almost

[137-141]unheeded, and the child sat looking out of the window, melancholy,

[137-142]silent, and repressed. Even the drum did not tempt him to be noisy.

[137-143]Doubtless he did not know why he was wretched, but he was fully

[137-144]conscious of his wretchedness. In the meantime the father sat

[137-145]motionless, in an old worn-out but once handsome leathern arm-chair,

[137-146]with his eyes fixed against the opposite wall, thinking of the wreck

[137-147]of his life.

[137-148]Thought deep, correct, continued, and energetic is quite compatible

[137-149]with madness. At this time Trevelyan's mind was so far unhinged, his

[137-150]ordinary faculties were so greatly impaired, that they who declared

[137-151]him to be mad were justified in their declaration. His condition was

[137-152]such that the happiness and welfare of no human being,--not even his

[137-153]own,--could safely be entrusted to his keeping. He considered himself

[137-154]to have been so injured by the world, to have been the victim of so

[137-155]cruel a conspiracy among those who ought to have been his friends,

[137-156]that there remained nothing for him but to flee away from them and

[137-157]remain in solitude. But yet, through it all, there was something

[137-158]approaching to a conviction that he had brought his misery upon

[137-159]himself by being unlike to other men; and he declared to himself

[137-160]over and over again that it was better that he should suffer than

[137-161]that others should be punished. When he was alone his reflections

[137-162]respecting his wife were much juster than were his words when he

[137-163]spoke either with her, or to others, of her conduct. He would declare

[137-164]to himself not only that he did not believe her to have been false to

[137-165]him, but that he had never accused her of such crime. He had demanded

[137-166]from her obedience, and she had been disobedient. It had been

[137-167]incumbent upon him,--so ran his own ideas, as expressed to himself

[137-168]in these long unspoken soliloquies,--to exact obedience, or at least

[137-169]compliance, let the consequences be what they might. She had refused

[137-170]to obey or even to comply, and the consequences were very grievous.

[137-171]But, though he pitied himself with a pity that was feminine, yet he

[137-172]acknowledged to himself that her conduct had been the result of his

[137-173]own moody temperament. Every friend had parted from him. All those to

[137-174]whose counsels he had listened, had counselled him that he was wrong.

[137-175]The whole world was against him. Had he remained in England, the

[137-176]doctors and lawyers among them would doubtless have declared him to

[137-177]be mad. He knew all this, and yet he could not yield. He could not

[137-178]say that he had been wrong. He could not even think that he had been

[137-179]wrong as to the cause of the great quarrel. He was one so miserable

[137-180]and so unfortunate,--so he thought,--that even in doing right he had

[137-181]fallen into perdition!
