## Section 52

[052-001]"He is sure to come, aunt."

[052-002]"I've no doubt you know all about it better than any one else. You

[052-003]usually do." Then five minutes were passed in silence. "Heaven and

[052-004]earth! what shall I do with these people that are coming? And I told

[052-005]them especially that it was to meet this young man! It's the way I am

[052-006]always treated by everybody that I have about me."

[052-007]"The train might be ten minutes late, Aunt Stanbury."

[052-008]"Yes;--and monkeys might chew tobacco. There;--there's the omnibus at

[052-009]the Cock and Bottle; the omnibus up from the train. Now, of course,

[052-010]he won't come."

[052-011]"Perhaps he's walking, Aunt Stanbury."

[052-012]"Walking,--with his luggage on his shoulders? Is that your idea of

[052-013]the way in which a [[london|London]] [[social-class|gentleman]] goes about? And there are two

[052-014]flies,--coming up from the train, of course." Miss Stanbury was

[052-015]obliged to fix the side of her chair very close to the window in

[052-016]order that she might see that part of the Close in which the vehicles

[052-017]of which she had spoken were able to pass.

[052-018]"Perhaps they are not coming from the train, Aunt Stanbury."

[052-019]"Perhaps a fiddlestick! You have lived here so much longer than

[052-020]I have done that, of course, you must know all about it." Then

[052-021]there was an interval of another ten minutes, and even Dorothy was

[052-022]beginning to think that Mr. Burgess was not coming. "I've given him

[052-023]up now," said Miss Stanbury. "I think I'll send and put them all

[052-024]off." Just at that moment there came a knock at the door. But there

[052-025]was no cab. Dorothy's conjecture had been right. The London gentleman

[052-026]had walked, and his portmanteau had been carried behind him by a boy.

[052-027]"How did he get here?" exclaimed Miss Stanbury, as she heard the

[052-028]strange voice speaking to Martha down-stairs. But Dorothy knew better

[052-029]than to answer the question.

[052-030]"Miss Stanbury, I am very glad to see you," said Mr. Brooke Burgess,

[052-031]as he entered the room. Miss Stanbury courtesied, and then took

[052-032]him by both hands. "You wouldn't have known me, I dare say," he

[052-033]continued. "A black beard and a bald head do make a difference."

[052-034]"You are not bald at all," said Miss Stanbury.

[052-035]"I am beginning to be thin enough at the top. I am so glad to come to

[052-036]you, and so much obliged to you for having me! How well I remember

[052-037]the old room!"

[052-038]"This is my niece, Miss Dorothy Stanbury, from [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]."

[052-039]Dorothy was about to make some formal acknowledgment of the

[052-040]introduction, when Brooke Burgess came up to her, and shook her hand

[052-041]heartily. "She lives with me," continued the aunt.

[052-042]"And what has become of Hugh?" said Brooke.

[052-043]"We never talk of him," said Miss Stanbury gravely.

[052-044]"I hope there's nothing wrong? I hear of him very often in London."

[052-045]"My aunt and he don't agree;--that's all," said Dorothy.

[052-046]"He has given up his profession as a barrister,--in which he might

[052-047]have lived like a gentleman," said Miss Stanbury, "and has taken to

[052-048]writing for a--penny newspaper."

[052-049]"Everybody does that now, Miss Stanbury."

[052-050]"I hope you don't, Mr. Burgess."

[052-051]"I! Nobody would print anything that I wrote. I don't write for

[052-052]anything, certainly."

[052-053]"I'm very glad to hear it," said Miss Stanbury.

[052-054]Brooke Burgess, or Mr. Brooke, as he came to be called very shortly

[052-055]by the servants in the house, was a good-looking man, with black

[052-056]whiskers and black hair, which, as he said, was beginning to be thin

[052-057]on the top of his head, and pleasant small bright eyes. Dorothy

[052-058]thought that next to her brother Hugh he was the most good-natured

[052-059]looking man she had ever seen. He was rather below the middle height,

[052-060]and somewhat inclined to be stout. But he would boast that he could

[052-061]still walk his twelve miles in three hours, and would add that as

[052-062]long as he could do that he would never recognise the necessity of

[052-063]putting himself on short commons. He had a well-cut nose, not quite

[052-064]aquiline, but tending that way, a chin with a dimple on it, and as

[052-065]sweet a mouth as ever declared the excellence of a man's temper.

[052-066]Dorothy immediately began to compare him with her brother Hugh, who

[052-067]was to her, of all men, the most godlike. It never occurred to her to

[052-068]make any comparison between Mr. Gibson and Mr. Burgess. Her brother

[052-069]Hugh was the most godlike of men; but there was something godlike

[052-070]also about the new comer. Mr. Gibson, to Dorothy's eyes, was by no

[052-071]means divine.

[052-072]"I used to call you Aunt Stanbury," said Brooke Burgess to the old

[052-073]lady; "am I to go on doing it now?"

[052-074]"You may call me what you like," said Miss Stanbury. "Only,--dear

[052-075]me;--I never did see anybody so much altered." Before she went up to

[052-076]dress herself for dinner, Miss Stanbury was quite restored to her

[052-077]good humour, as Dorothy could perceive.

[052-078]The dinner passed off well enough. Mr. Gibson, at the head of the

[052-079]table, did, indeed, look very much out of his element, as though he

[052-080]conceived that his position revealed to the outer world those ideas

[052-081]of his in regard to Dorothy, which ought to have been secret for a

[052-082]while longer. There are few men who do not feel ashamed of being

[052-083]paraded before the world as acknowledged suitors, whereas ladies

[052-084]accept the position with something almost of triumph. The lady

[052-085]perhaps regards herself as the successful angler, whereas the

[052-086]gentleman is conscious of some similitude to the unsuccessful fish.

[052-087]Mr. Gibson, though he was not yet gasping in the basket, had some

[052-088]presentiment of this feeling, which made his present seat of honour

[052-089]unpleasant to him. Brooke Burgess, at the other end of the table,

[052-090]was as gay as a lark. Mrs. MacHugh sat on one side of him, and

[052-091]Miss Stanbury on the other, and he laughed at the two old ladies,

[052-092]reminding them of his former doings in [[exeter|Exeter]],--how he had hunted

[052-093]Mrs. MacHugh's cat, and had stolen Aunt Stanbury's best apricot jam,

[052-094]till everybody began to perceive that he was quite a success. Even

[052-095]Sir Peter Mancrudy laughed at his jokes, and Mrs. Powel, from the

[052-096]other side of Sir Peter, stretched her head forward so that she might

[052-097]become one of the gay party.

[052-098]"There isn't a word of it true," said Miss Stanbury. "It's all pure

[052-099]invention, and a great scandal. I never did such a thing in my life."

[052-100]"Didn't you though?" said Brooke Burgess. "I remember it as well

[052-101]as if it was yesterday, and old Dr. Ball, the prebendary, with the

[052-102]carbuncles on his nose, saw it too."

[052-103]"Dr. Ball had no carbuncles on his nose," said Mrs. MacHugh. "You'll

[052-104]say next that I have carbuncles on my nose."

[052-105]"He had three. I remember each of them quite well, and so does Sir

[052-106]Peter."

[052-107]Then everybody laughed; and Martha, who was in the room, knew that

[052-108]Brooke Burgess was a complete success.

[052-109]In the meantime Mr. Gibson was talking to Dorothy; but Dorothy was

[052-110]endeavouring to listen to the conversation at the other end of the

[052-111]table. "I found it very dirty on the roads to-day outside the city,"

[052-112]said Mr. Gibson.

[052-113]"Very dirty," said Dorothy, looking round at Mr. Burgess as she

[052-114]spoke.

[052-115]"But the pavement in the High Street was dry enough."

[052-116]"Quite dry," said Dorothy. Then there came a peal of laughter from

[052-117]Mrs. MacHugh and Sir Peter, and Dorothy wondered whether anybody

[052-118]before had ever made those two steady old people laugh after that

[052-119]fashion.

[052-120]"I should so like to get a drive with you up to the top of Haldon

[052-121]Hill," said Mr. Gibson. "When the weather gets fine, that is. Mrs.

[052-122]Powel was talking about it."

[052-123]"It would be very nice," said Dorothy.

[052-124]"You have never seen the view from Haldon Hill yet?" asked Mr.

[052-125]Gibson. But to this question Dorothy could make no answer. Miss

[052-126]Stanbury had lifted one of the table-spoons, as though she was going

[052-127]to strike Mr. Brooke Burgess with the bowl of it. And this during

[052-128]a dinner party! From that moment Dorothy turned herself round, and

[052-129]became one of the listeners to the fun at the other end of the table.

[052-130]Poor Mr. Gibson soon found himself "nowhere."

[052-131]"I never saw a man so much altered in my life," said Mrs. MacHugh, up

[052-132]in the drawing-room. "I don't remember that he used to be clever."

[052-133]"He was a bright boy," said Miss Stanbury.

[052-134]"But the Burgesses all used to be such serious, strait-laced people,"

[052-135]said Mrs. MacHugh. "Excellent people," she added, remembering the

[052-136]source of her friend's [[money|wealth]]; "but none of them like that."

[052-137]"I call him a very handsome man," said Mrs. Powel. "I suppose he's

[052-138]not married yet?"

[052-139]"Oh, dear, no," said Miss Stanbury. "There's time enough for him

[052-140]yet."

[052-141]"He'll find plenty here to set their caps at him," said Mrs. MacHugh.

[052-142]"He's a little old for my girls," said Mrs. Powel, laughing. Mrs.

[052-143]Powel was the happy mother of four daughters, of whom the eldest was

[052-144]only twelve.

[052-145]"There are others who are more forward," said Mrs. MacHugh. "What a

[052-146]chance it would be for dear Arabella French!"

[052-147]"Heaven forbid!" said Miss Stanbury.

[052-148]"And then poor Mr. Gibson wouldn't be any longer like the donkey

[052-149]between two bundles of hay," said Mrs. Powel. Dorothy was quite

[052-150]determined that she would never marry a man who was like a donkey

[052-151]between two bundles of hay.

[052-152]When the gentlemen came up into the drawing-room, Dorothy was seated

[052-153]behind the urn and tea-things at a large table, in such a position as

[052-154]to be approached only at one side. There was one chair at her left

[052-155]hand, but at her right hand there was no room for a seat,--only room

[052-156]for some civil gentleman to take away full cups and bring them back

[052-157]empty. Dorothy was not sufficiently ready-witted to see the danger of

[052-158]this position till Mr. Gibson had seated himself in the chair. Then

[052-159]it did seem cruel to her that she should be thus besieged for the

[052-160]rest of the evening as she had been also at dinner. While the tea

[052-161]was being consumed Mr. Gibson assisted at the service, asking ladies

[052-162]whether they would have cake or bread and butter; but when all that

[052-163]was over Dorothy was still in her prison, and Mr. Gibson was still

[052-164]the jailer at the gate. She soon perceived that everybody else was

[052-165]chatting and laughing, and that Brooke Burgess was the centre of a

[052-166]little circle which had formed itself quite at a distance from her

[052-167]seat. Once, twice, thrice she meditated an escape, but she had not

[052-168]the courage to make the attempt. She did not know how to manage it.

[052-169]She was conscious that her aunt's eye was upon her, and that her aunt

[052-170]would expect her to listen to Mr. Gibson. At last she gave up all

[052-171]hope of moving, and was anxious simply that Mr. Gibson should confine

[052-172]himself to the dirt of the paths and the noble prospect from Haldon

[052-173]Hill.

[052-174]"I think we shall have more rain before we are done with it," he

[052-175]said. Twice before during the evening he had been very eloquent about

[052-176]the rain.

[052-177]"I dare say we shall," said Dorothy. And then there came the sound

[052-178]of loud laughter from Sir Peter, and Dorothy could see that he was

[052-179]poking Brooke Burgess in the ribs. There had never been anything so

[052-180]gay before since she had been in Exeter, and now she was hemmed up in

[052-181]that corner, away from it all, by Mr. Gibson!

[052-182]"This Mr. Burgess seems to be different from the other Burgesses,"

[052-183]said Mr. Gibson.

[052-184]"I think he must be very clever," said Dorothy.

[052-185]"Well;--yes; in a sort of a way. What people call a Merry Andrew."

[052-186]"I like people who make me laugh and laugh themselves," said Dorothy.

[052-187]"I quite agree with you that laughter is a very good thing,--in

[052-188]its place. I am not at all one of those who would make the world

[052-189]altogether grave. There are serious things, and there must be serious

[052-190]moments."
