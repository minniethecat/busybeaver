## Section 39

[039-001]"Good morning," said [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]], turning on his heels and walking

[039-002]away. [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] shook himself, inflated his cheeks, and blew

[039-003]forth the breath out of his mouth, put his thumbs up to the armholes

[039-004]of his waistcoat, and walked about the platform as though he thought

[039-005]it to be incumbent on him to show that he was somebody,--somebody

[039-006]that ought not to be insulted,--somebody, perhaps, whom a very pretty

[039-007]woman might prefer to her own husband, in spite of a small difference

[039-008]in age. He was angry, but not quite so much angry as proud. And he

[039-009]was safe, too. He thought that he was safe. When he should come to

[039-010]account for himself and his actions to his old friend, Sir Marmaduke,

[039-011]he felt that he would be able to show that he had been, in all

[039-012]respects, true to friendship. Sir Marmaduke had unfortunately given

[039-013]his daughter to a jealous, disagreeable fellow, and the fault all

[039-014]lay in that. As for Hugh Stanbury,--he would simply despise Hugh

[039-015]Stanbury, and have done with it.

[039-016]Mr. Bozzle, though he had worked hard in the cause, had heard but a

[039-017]word or two. Eaves-droppers seldom do hear more than that. A porter

[039-018]had already told him who was Hugh Stanbury,--that he was Mr. Hugh

[039-019]Stanbury, and that his aunt lived at [[exeter|Exeter]]. And Bozzle, knowing that

[039-020]the lady about whom he was concerned was living with a Mrs. Stanbury

[039-021]at the house he had been watching, put two and two together with

[039-022]his natural cleverness. "God bless my soul! what business is it of

[039-023]yours?" Those words were nearly all that Bozzle had been able to

[039-024]hear; but even those sufficiently indicated a quarrel. "The lady" was

[039-025]living with Mrs. Stanbury, having been so placed by her husband; and

[039-026]young Stanbury was taking the lady's part! Bozzle began to fear that

[039-027]the husband had not confided in him with that perfect faith which he

[039-028]felt to be essentially necessary to the adequate performance of the

[039-029]duties of his great profession. A sudden thought, however, struck

[039-030]him. Something might be done on the journey up to [[london|London]]. He at

[039-031]once made his way back to the ticket-window and exchanged his

[039-032]ticket,--second-[[social-class|class]] for first-class. It was a noble deed, the

[039-033]expense falling all upon his own pocket; for, in the natural course

[039-034]of things, he would have charged his employers with the full

[039-035]first-class fare. He had seen Colonel Osborne seat himself in a

[039-036]carriage, and within two minutes he was occupying the opposite place.

[039-037]The Colonel was aware that he had noticed the man's face lately, but

[039-038]did not know where.

[039-039]"Very fine summer weather, sir," said Bozzle.

[039-040]"Very fine," said the Colonel, burying himself behind a newspaper.

[039-041]"They is getting up their wheat nicely in these parts, sir."

[039-042]The answer to this was no more than a grunt. But Bozzle was not

[039-043]offended. Not to be offended is the special duty of all policemen, in

[039-044]and out of office; and the journey from Exeter to London was long,

[039-045]and was all before him.

[039-046]"A very nice little secluded village is [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]," said

[039-047]Bozzle, as the train was leaving the Salisbury Station.

[039-048]At Salisbury two ladies had left the carriage, no one else had got

[039-049]in, and Bozzle was alone with the Colonel.

[039-050]"I dare say," said the Colonel, who by this time had relinquished his

[039-051]shield, and who had begun to compose himself for sleep, or to pretend

[039-052]to compose himself, as soon as he heard Bozzle's voice. He had been

[039-053]looking at Bozzle, and though he had not discovered the man's trade,

[039-054]had told himself that his companion was a thing of dangers,--a thing

[039-055]to be avoided, by one engaged, as had been he himself, on a special

[039-056]and secret mission.

[039-057]"Saw you there,--calling at the [[clock-house|Clock House]]," said Bozzle.

[039-058]"Very likely," said the Colonel, throwing his head well back into the

[039-059]corner, shutting his eyes, and uttering a slight preliminary snore.

[039-060]"Very nice family of ladies at the Clock House," said Bozzle. The

[039-061]Colonel answered him by a more developed snore. "Particularly Mrs.

[039-062]T----" said Bozzle.

[039-063]The Colonel could not stand this. He was so closely implicated with

[039-064]Mrs. Trevelyan at the present moment that he could not omit to notice

[039-065]an address so made to him. "What the devil is that to you, sir?" said

[039-066]he, jumping up and confronting Bozzle in his wrath.

[039-067]But policemen have always this advantage in their difficulties, that

[039-068]they know to a fraction what the wrath of men is worth, and what it

[039-069]can do. Sometimes it can dismiss a policeman, and sometimes break

[039-070]his head. Sometimes it can give him a long and troublesome job, and

[039-071]sometimes it may be wrath to the death. But in nineteen out of twenty

[039-072]cases it is not a fearful thing, and the policeman knows well when

[039-073]he need not fear it. On the present occasion Bozzle was not at all

[039-074]afraid of Colonel Osborne's wrath.

[039-075]"Well, sir, not much, indeed, if you come to that. Only you was

[039-076]there, sir."

[039-077]"Of course I was there," said the Colonel.

[039-078]"And a very nice young gentleman is Mr. Stanbury," said Bozzle.

[039-079]To this Colonel Osborne made no reply, but again had resort to his

[039-080]newspaper in the most formal manner.

[039-081]"He's going down to his family, no doubt," continued Bozzle.

[039-082]"He may be going to the devil for what I know," said the Colonel, who

[039-083]could not restrain himself.

[039-084]"I suppose they're all friends of Mrs. T.'s?" asked Bozzle.

[039-085]"Sir," said the Colonel, "I believe that you're a spy."

[039-086]"No, Colonel, no; no, no; I'm no spy. I wouldn't demean myself to be

[039-087]such. A spy is a man as has no profession, and nothing to justify his

[039-088]looking into things. Things must be looked into, Colonel; or how's a

[039-089]man to know where he is? or how's a lady to know where she is? But

[039-090]as for spies, except in the way of [[truth|evidence]], I don't think nothing

[039-091]of 'em." Soon after this two more passengers entered the train, and

[039-092]nothing more was said between Bozzle and the Colonel.

[039-093]The Colonel, as soon as he reached London, went home to his lodgings,

[039-094]and then to his club, and did his best to enjoy himself. On the

[039-095]following Monday he intended to start for Scotland. But he could not

[039-096]quite enjoy himself,--because of Bozzle. He felt that he was being

[039-097]watched; and there is nothing that any man hates so much as that,

[039-098]especially when a lady is concerned. Colonel Osborne knew that his

[039-099]visit to Nuncombe Putney had been very innocent; but he did not like

[039-100]the feeling that even his innocence had been made the subject of

[039-101]observation.

[039-102]Bozzle went away at once to Trevelyan, whom he found at his chambers.

[039-103]He himself had had no very deep-laid scheme in his addresses to

[039-104]Colonel Osborne. He had begun to think that very little would come of

[039-105]the affair,--especially after Hugh Stanbury had appeared upon the

[039-106]scene,--and had felt that there was nothing to be lost by presenting

[039-107]himself before the eyes of the Colonel. It was necessary that he

[039-108]should make a report to his employer, and the report might be made

[039-109]a little more full after a few words with the man whom he had been

[039-110]"looking into." "Well, Mr. Trewillian," he said, seating himself

[039-111]on a chair close against the wall, and holding his hat between the

[039-112]knees,--"I've seen the parties, and know pretty much all about it."

[039-113]"All I want to know, Mr. Bozzle, is, whether Colonel Osborne has been

[039-114]at the Clock House?"

[039-115]"He has been there, Mr. Trewillian. There is no earthly doubt about

[039-116]that. From hour to hour I can tell you pretty nearly where he's been

[039-117]since he left London." Then Bozzle took out his memorandum-book.

[039-118]"I don't care about all that," said Trevelyan.

[039-119]"I dare say not, sir; but it may be wanted all the same. Any

[039-120]gentleman acting in our way can't be too particular,--can't have

[039-121]too many facts. The smallest little,--tiddly things,"--and Bozzle

[039-122]as he said this seemed to enjoy immensely the flavour of his own

[039-123]epithet,--"the smallest little 'tiddly' things do so often turn up

[039-124]trumps when you get your evidence into court."

[039-125]"I'm not going to get any evidence into court."

[039-126]"Maybe not, sir. A gentleman and lady is always best out of court as

[039-127]long as things can hang on any way;--but sometimes things won't hang

[039-128]on no way."

[039-129]Trevelyan, who was conscious that the employment of Bozzle was

[039-130]discreditable, and whose affairs in Devonshire were now in the hands

[039-131]of, at any rate, a more honourable ally, was at present mainly

[039-132]anxious to get rid of the ex-policeman. "I have no doubt you've been

[039-133]very careful, Mr. Bozzle," said he.

[039-134]"There isn't no one in the business could be more so, Mr.

[039-135]Trewillian."

[039-136]"And you have found out what it was necessary that I should know.

[039-137]Colonel Osborne did go to the Clock House?"

[039-138]"Was let in at the front door on Friday the 5th, by Sarah French, the

[039-139]housemaid, at 10.37 a.m., and was let out again by the same young

[039-140]woman at 11.41 a.m. Perhaps you'd like to have a copy of the entry,

[039-141]Mr. Trewillian?"

[039-142]"No, no, no."

[039-143]"It doesn't matter. Of course it'll be with me when it's wanted. Who

[039-144]was with him, exactly, at that time, I can't say. There is things,

[039-145]Mr. Trewillian, one can't see. But I don't think as he saw neither

[039-146]Mrs. Stanbury, nor Miss Stanbury,--not to speak to. I did just have

[039-147]one word, promiscuous, with Sarah French, after he was gone. Whether

[039-148]the other young lady was with 'em or not, and if so for how long,

[039-149]I--can't--say. There is things, Mr. Trewillian, which one can't see."

[039-150]How Trevelyan hated the man as he went on with his odious

[039-151]details,--details not one of which possessed the slightest

[039-152]importance. "It's all right, I dare say, Mr. Bozzle. And now about

[039-153]the account."

[039-154]"Quite so, Mr. Trewillian. But there was one question;--just one

[039-155]question."

[039-156]"What question?" said Trevelyan, almost angrily.

[039-157]"And there's another thing I must tell you, too, Mr. Trewillian. I

[039-158]come back to town in the same carriage with the Colonel. I thought it

[039-159]better."

[039-160]"You did not tell him who you were?"

[039-161]"No, Mr. Trewillian; I didn't tell him that. I don't think he'd say

[039-162]if you was to ask him that I told him much of anything. No, Mr.

[039-163]Trewillian, I didn't tell him nothing. I don't often tell folks much

[039-164]till the time comes. But I thought it better, and I did have a word

[039-165]or two with the gent,--just a word or two. He's not so very downy,

[039-166]isn't the Colonel;--for one that's been at it so long, Mr.

[039-167]Trewillian."

[039-168]"I dare say not. But if you could just let me have the account, Mr.

[039-169]Bozzle,--"

[039-170]"The account? Oh, yes;--that is necessary; ain't it? These sort of

[039-171]inquiries do come a little expensive, Mr. Trewillian; because time

[039-172]goes for so much; and when one has to be down on a thing, sharp, you

[039-173]know, and sure, so that counsel on the other side can't part you from

[039-174]it, though he shakes you like a dog does a rat,--and one has to get

[039-175]oneself up ready for all that, you know, Mr. Trewillian,--as I was

[039-176]saying, one can't count one's shillings when one has such a job as

[039-177]this in hand. Clench your nail;--that's what I say; be it even so.

[039-178]Clench your nail;--that's what you've got to do."

[039-179]"I dare say we shan't quarrel about the money, Mr. Bozzle."

[039-180]"Oh dear no. I find I never has any words about the money. But

[039-181]there's that one question. There's a young Mr. Stanbury has gone

[039-182]down, as knows all about it. What's he up to?"

[039-183]"He's my particular friend," said Trevelyan.

[039-184]"Oh--h. He do know all about it, then?"

[039-185]"We needn't talk about that, if you please, Mr. Bozzle."
