## Section 3

[003-001][Illustration: Shewing how wrath began.]

[003-002]Luncheon was served, and the two ladies went down into the

[003-003]dining-room. Mr. Trevelyan did not appear. There was nothing in

[003-004]itself singular in that, as he was accustomed to declare that

[003-005]luncheon was a meal too much in the day, and that a man should eat

[003-006]nothing beyond a biscuit between breakfast and dinner. But he would

[003-007]sometimes come in and eat his biscuit [[reputation|standing]] on the hearth-rug,

[003-008]and drink what he would call half a quarter of a glass of sherry. It

[003-009]would probably have been well that he should have done so now; but

[003-010]he remained in his library behind the dining-room, and when his wife

[003-011]and his sister-in-law had gone up-stairs, he became anxious to learn

[003-012]whether [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] would come on that day, and, if so, whether

[003-013]he would be admitted. He had been told that [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] was to be

[003-014]called for by another lady, a Mrs. Fairfax, to go out and look at

[003-015]pictures. His wife had declined to join Mrs. Fairfax's party, having

[003-016]declared that, as she was going to dine out, she would not leave

[003-017]her baby all the afternoon. [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]], though he strove to

[003-018]apply his mind to an article which he was writing for a scientific

[003-019]quarterly review, could not keep himself from anxiety as to this

[003-020]expected visit from Colonel Osborne. He was not in the least jealous.

[003-021]He swore to himself fifty times over that any such feeling on his

[003-022]part would be a monstrous injury to his wife. Nevertheless he knew

[003-023]that he would be gratified if on that special day Colonel Osborne

[003-024]should be informed that his wife was not at home. Whether the man

[003-025]were admitted or not, he would beg his wife's pardon; but he could,

[003-026]he thought, do so with more thorough efficacy and affection if she

[003-027]should have shown a disposition to comply with his wishes on this

[003-028]day.

[003-029]"Do say a word to Richard," said Nora to her sister in a whisper as

[003-030]they were going up-stairs after luncheon.

[003-031]"I will not," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[003-032]"May I do it?"

[003-033]"Certainly not, Nora. I should feel that I were demeaning myself were

[003-034]I to allow what was said to me in such a manner to have any effect

[003-035]upon me."

[003-036]"I think you are so wrong, Emily. I do indeed."

[003-037]"You must allow me to be the best judge what to do in my own house,

[003-038]and with my own husband."

[003-039]"Oh, yes; certainly."

[003-040]"If he gives me any command I will obey it. Or if he had expressed

[003-041]his wish in any other words I would have complied. But to be told

[003-042]that he would rather not have Colonel Osborne here! If you had seen

[003-043]his manner and heard his words, you would not have been surprised

[003-044]that I should feel it as I do. It was a gross insult,--and it was not

[003-045]the first."

[003-046]As she spoke the fire flashed from her eye, and the bright red colour

[003-047]of her cheek told a tale of her anger which her sister well knew how

[003-048]to read. Then there was a knock at the door, and they both knew that

[003-049]Colonel Osborne was there. Louis Trevelyan, sitting in his library,

[003-050]also knew of whose coming that knock gave notice.

[003-051]CHAPTER II.

[003-052]COLONEL OSBORNE.

[003-053]It has been already said that Colonel Osborne was a bachelor, a man

[003-054]of [[money|fortune]], a member of Parliament, and one who carried his half

[003-055]century of years lightly on his shoulders. It will only be necessary

[003-056]to say further of him that he was a man popular with those among

[003-057]whom he lived, as a politician, as a sportsman, and as a member

[003-058]of society. He could speak well in the House, though he spoke but

[003-059]seldom, and it was generally thought of him that he might have been

[003-060]something considerable, had it not suited him better to be nothing at

[003-061]all. He was supposed to be a Conservative, and generally voted with

[003-062]the Conservative party; but he could boast that he was altogether

[003-063]independent, and on an occasion would take the trouble of proving

[003-064]himself to be so. He was in possession of excellent health; had all

[003-065]that the world could give; was fond of books, pictures, architecture,

[003-066]and china; had various tastes, and the means of indulging them, and

[003-067]was one of those few men on whom it seems that every pleasant thing

[003-068]has been lavished. There was that little slur on his [[honour|good name]] to

[003-069]which allusion has been made; but those who knew Colonel Osborne best

[003-070]were generally willing to declare that no harm was intended, and

[003-071]that the evils which arose were always to be attributed to mistaken

[003-072]jealousy. He had, his friends said, a free and pleasant way with

[003-073]women which women like,--a pleasant way of free friendship; that

[003-074]there was no more, and that the harm which had come had always

[003-075]come from false suspicion. But there were certain ladies about the

[003-076]town,--good, motherly, discreet women,--who hated the name of Colonel

[003-077]Osborne, who would not admit him within their doors, who would not

[003-078]bow to him in other people's houses, who would always speak of him as

[003-079]a serpent, a hyena, a kite, or a shark. Old Lady Milborough was one

[003-080]of these, a daughter of a friend of hers having once admitted the

[003-081]serpent to her intimacy.

[003-082]"Augustus Poole was wise enough to take his wife abroad," said old

[003-083]Lady Milborough, discussing about this time with a [[scandal|gossip]] of hers

[003-084]the danger of Mrs. Trevelyan's position, "or there would have been a

[003-085]break-up there; and yet there never was a better girl in the world

[003-086]than Jane Marriott."

[003-087]The reader may be quite certain that Colonel Osborne had no

[003-088]premeditated evil intention when he allowed himself to become the

[003-089]intimate friend of his old friend's daughter. There was nothing

[003-090]fiendish in his nature. He was not a man who boasted of his

[003-091]conquests. He was not a ravening wolf going about seeking whom he

[003-092]might devour, and determined to devour whatever might come in his

[003-093]way; but he liked that which was pleasant; and of all pleasant things

[003-094]the company of a pretty clever woman was to him the pleasantest. At

[003-095]this exact period of his life no woman was so pleasantly pretty to

[003-096]him, and so agreeably clever, as Mrs. Trevelyan.

[003-097]When Louis Trevelyan heard on the stairs the step of the dangerous

[003-098]man, he got up from his chair as though he too would have gone into

[003-099]the drawing-room, and it would perhaps have been well had he done so.

[003-100]Could he have done this, and kept his temper with the man, he would

[003-101]have paved the way for an easy reconciliation with his wife. But when

[003-102]he reached the door of his room, and had placed his hand upon the

[003-103]lock, he withdrew again. He told himself he withdrew because he would

[003-104]not allow himself to be jealous; but in truth he did so because he

[003-105]knew he could not have brought himself to be civil to the man he

[003-106]hated. So he sat down, and took up his pen, and began to cudgel

[003-107]his brain about the scientific article. He was intent on raising a

[003-108]dispute with some learned pundit about the waves of sound,--but he

[003-109]could think of no other sound than that of the light steps of Colonel

[003-110]Osborne as he had gone up-stairs. He put down his pen, and clenched

[003-111]his fist, and allowed a black frown to settle upon his brow. What

[003-112]right had the man to come there, unasked by him, and disturb his

[003-113]happiness? And then this poor wife of his, who knew so little of

[003-114]English life, who had lived in the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]] almost since she

[003-115]had been a child, who had lived in one colony or another almost since

[003-116]she had been born, who had had so few of those advantages for which

[003-117]he should have looked in marrying a wife, how was the poor girl to

[003-118]conduct herself properly when subjected to the arts and practised

[003-119]villanies of this viper? And yet the poor girl was so stiff in her

[003-120]temper, had picked up such a trick of obstinacy in those tropical

[003-121]regions, that Louis Trevelyan felt that he did not know how to manage

[003-122]her. He too had heard how Jane Marriott had been carried off to

[003-123]Naples after she had become Mrs. Poole. Must he too carry off his

[003-124]wife to Naples in order to place her out of the reach of this hyena?

[003-125]It was terrible to him to think that he must pack up everything and

[003-126]run away from such a one as Colonel Osborne. And even were he to

[003-127]consent to do this, how could he explain it all to that very wife for

[003-128]whose sake he would do it? If she got a hint of the reason she would,

[003-129]he did not doubt, refuse to go. As he thought of it, and as that

[003-130]visit up-stairs prolonged itself, he almost thought it would be best

[003-131]for him to be round with her! We all know what a husband means when

[003-132]he resolves to be round with his wife. He began to think that he

[003-133]would not apologise at all for the words he had spoken,--but would

[003-134]speak them again somewhat more sharply than before. She would be

[003-135]very wrathful with him; there would be a silent enduring indignation,

[003-136]which, as he understood well, would be infinitely worse than any

[003-137]torrent of words. But was he, a man, to abstain from doing that which

[003-138]he believed to be his duty because he was afraid of his wife's anger?

[003-139]Should he be deterred from saying that which he conceived it would

[003-140]be right that he should say, because she was stiff-necked? No. He

[003-141]would not apologise, but would tell her again that it was necessary,

[003-142]both for his happiness and for hers, that all intimacy with Colonel

[003-143]Osborne should be discontinued.

[003-144]He was brought to this strongly marital resolution by the length of

[003-145]the man's present visit; by that and by the fact that, during the

[003-146]latter portion of it, his wife was alone with Colonel Osborne. Nora

[003-147]had been there when the man came, but Mrs. Fairfax had called, not

[003-148]getting out of her carriage, and Nora had been constrained to go down

[003-149]to her. She had hesitated a moment, and Colonel Osborne had observed

[003-150]and partly understood the hesitation. When he saw it, had he been

[003-151]perfectly well-minded in the matter, he would have gone too. But he

[003-152]probably told himself that Nora Rowley was a fool, and that in such

[003-153]matters it was quite enough for a man to know that he did not intend

[003-154]any harm.

[003-155]"You had better go down, Nora," said Mrs. Trevelyan; "Mrs. Fairfax

[003-156]will be ever so angry if you keep her waiting."

[003-157]Then Nora had gone and the two were alone together. Nora had gone,

[003-158]and Trevelyan had heard her as she was going and knew that Colonel

[003-159]Osborne was alone with his wife.

[003-160]"If you can manage that it will be so nice," said Mrs. Trevelyan,

[003-161]continuing the conversation.

[003-162]"My dear Emily," he said, "you must not talk of my managing it, or

[003-163]you will spoil it all."

[003-164]He had called them both Emily and Nora when Sir Marmaduke and Lady

[003-165]Rowley were with them before the marriage, and, taking the [[personal-freedom|liberty]] of

[003-166]a very old family friend, had continued the practice. Mrs. Trevelyan

[003-167]was quite aware that she had been so called by him in the presence of

[003-168]her husband,--and that her husband had not objected. But that was now

[003-169]some months ago, before baby was born; and she was aware also that

[003-170]he had not called her so latterly in presence of her husband. She

[003-171]thoroughly wished that she knew how to ask him not to do so again;

[003-172]but the matter was very difficult, as she could not make such a

[003-173]request without betraying some fear on her husband's part. The

[003-174]subject which they were now discussing was too important to her

[003-175]to allow her to dwell upon this trouble at the moment, and so she

[003-176]permitted him to go on with his speech.
