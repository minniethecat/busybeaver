## Section 162

[162-001]Then came an evening in which he seemed to be somewhat stronger than

[162-002]he had been. He had taken some refreshment that had been prepared

[162-003]for him, and, stimulated by its strength, had spoken a word or two

[162-004]both to Nora and to his wife. His words had been of no especial

[162-005]interest,--alluding to some small detail of his own condition, such

[162-006]as are generally the chosen topics of conversation with invalids.

[162-007]But he had been pronounced to be better, and Nora spoke to him

[162-008]cheerfully, when he was taken into the next room by the man who

[162-009]was always at hand to move him. His wife followed him, and soon

[162-010]afterwards returned, and bade Nora good night. She would sit by her

[162-011]husband, and Nora was to go to the room below, that she might receive

[162-012]her lover there. He was expected out that evening, but Mrs. Trevelyan

[162-013]said that she would not see him. Hugh came and went, and Nora took

[162-014]herself to her chamber. The hours of the night went on, and Mrs.

[162-015]Trevelyan was still sitting by her husband's bed. It was still

[162-016]September, and the weather was very warm. But the windows had been

[162-017]all closed since an hour before sunset. She was sitting there

[162-018]thinking, thinking, thinking. Dr. Nevill had told her that the time

[162-019]now was very near. She was not thinking now how very near it might

[162-020]be, but whether there might yet be time for him to say that one word

[162-021]to her.

[162-022]"Emily," he said, in the lowest whisper.

[162-023]"Darling!" she answered, turning round and touching him with her

[162-024]hand.

[162-025]"My feet are cold. There are no clothes on them."

[162-026]She took a thick shawl and spread it double across the bottom of

[162-027]the bed, and put her hand upon his arm. Though it was clammy with

[162-028]perspiration, it was chill, and she brought the warm clothes up close

[162-029]round his shoulders. "I can't sleep," he said. "If I could sleep,

[162-030]I shouldn't mind." Then he was silent again, and her thoughts went

[162-031]harping on, still on the same subject. She told herself that if ever

[162-032]that act of justice were to be done for her, it must be done that

[162-033]night. After a while she turned round over him ever so gently, and

[162-034]saw that his large eyes were open and fixed upon the wall.

[162-035]She was kneeling now on the chair close by the bed head, and her hand

[162-036]was on the rail of the bedstead supporting her. "Louis," she said,

[162-037]ever so softly.

[162-038]"Well."

[162-039]"Can you say one word for your wife, dear, dear, dearest husband?"

[162-040]"What word?"

[162-041]"I have not been a harlot to you;--have I?"

[162-042]"What name is that?"

[162-043]"But what a thing, Louis! Kiss my hand, Louis, if you believe me."

[162-044]And very gently she laid the tips of her fingers on his lips. For a

[162-045]moment or two she waited, and the kiss did not come. Would he spare

[162-046]her in this the last moment left to him either for justice or for

[162-047]mercy? For a moment or two the bitterness of her despair was almost

[162-048]unendurable. She had time to think that were she once to withdraw

[162-049]her hand, she would be condemned for ever;--and that it must be

[162-050]withdrawn. But at length the lips moved, and with struggling ear she

[162-051]could hear the sound of the tongue within, and the verdict of the

[162-052]dying man had been given in her favour. He never spoke a word more

[162-053]either to annul it or to enforce it.

[162-054]Some time after that she crept into Nora's room. "Nora," she said,

[162-055]waking the sleeping girl, "it is all over."

[162-056]"Is he--dead?"

[162-057]"It is all over. Mrs. Richards is there. It is better than an hour

[162-058]since now. Let me come in." She got into her sister's bed, and there

[162-059]she told the tale of her tardy triumph. "He declared to me at last

[162-060]that he trusted me," she said,--almost believing that real words had

[162-061]come from his lips to that effect. Then she fell into a flood of

[162-062]tears, and after a while she also slept.

[162-063]CHAPTER XCIX.

[162-064]CONCLUSION.

[162-065]At last the maniac was dead, and in his last moments he had made such

[162-066]reparation as was in his power for the evil that he had done. With

[162-067]that slight touch of his dry fevered lips he had made the assertion

[162-068]on which was to depend the future peace and comfort of the woman whom

[162-069]he had so cruelly misused. To her mind the acquittal was perfect;

[162-070]but she never explained to human ears,--not even to those of her

[162-071]sister,--the manner in which it had been given. Her life, as far as

[162-072]we are concerned with it, has been told. For the rest, it cannot be

[162-073]but that it should be better than that which was passed. If there be

[162-074]any retribution for such sufferings in money, [[personal-freedom|liberty]], and outward

[162-075]comfort, such retribution she possessed;--for all that had been his,

[162-076]was now hers. He had once suggested what she should do, were she

[162-077]ever to be married again; and she felt that of such a career there

[162-078]could be no possibility. Anything but that! We all know that widows'

[162-079]practices in this matter do not always tally with wives' vows; but,

[162-080]as regards Mrs. Trevelyan, we are disposed to think that the promise

[162-081]will be kept. She has her child, and he will give her sufficient

[162-082]interest to make life worth having.

[162-083]Early in the following spring [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] was married to Nora

[162-084]Rowley in the parish church of Monkhams,--at which place by that time

[162-085]Nora found herself to be almost as much at home as she might have

[162-086]been under other circumstances. They had prayed that the marriage

[162-087]might be very private;--but when the day arrived there was no

[162-088]very close privacy. The parish church was quite full, there were

[162-089]half-a-dozen bridesmaids, there was a great breakfast, Mrs. Crutch

[162-090]had a new brown silk gown given to her, there was a long article

[162-091]in the county gazette, and there were short paragraphs in various

[162-092]metropolitan newspapers. It was generally thought among his compeers

[162-093]that Hugh Stanbury had married into the aristocracy, and that the

[162-094]fact was a triumph for the profession to which he belonged. It shewed

[162-095]what a Bohemian could do, and that men of the press in England might

[162-096]gradually hope to force their way almost anywhere. So great was the

[162-097]name of Monkhams! He and his wife took for themselves a very small

[162-098]house near the Regent's Park, at which they intend to remain until

[162-099]Hugh shall have enabled himself to earn an additional two hundred

[162-100]a-year. Mrs. Trevelyan did not come to live with them, but kept the

[162-101]cottage near the river at Twickenham. Hugh Stanbury was very averse

[162-102]to any protracted connection with comforts to be obtained from poor

[162-103]Trevelyan's [[money|income]], and told Nora that he must hold her to her

[162-104]promise about the beef-steak in the cupboard. It is our opinion that

[162-105]Mr. and Mrs. Hugh Stanbury will never want for a beef-steak and all

[162-106]comfortable additions until the inhabitants of [[london|London]] shall cease to

[162-107]require newspapers on their breakfast tables.

[162-108]Brooke and Mrs. Brooke established themselves in the house in the

[162-109]Close on their return from their wedding tour, and Brooke at once put

[162-110]himself into intimate relations with the Messrs. Croppers, taking

[162-111]his fair share of the bank work. Dorothy was absolutely installed as

[162-112]mistress in her aunt's house with many wonderful ceremonies, with

[162-113]the unlocking of cupboards, the outpouring of stores, the giving

[162-114]up of keys, and with many speeches made to Martha. This was all

[162-115]very painful to Dorothy, who could not bring herself to suppose it

[162-116]possible that she should be the mistress of that house, during her

[162-117]aunt's life. Miss Stanbury, however, of course persevered, speaking

[162-118]of herself as a worn-out old woman, with one foot in the grave, who

[162-119]would soon be carried away and put out of sight. But in a very few

[162-120]days things got back into their places, and Aunt Stanbury had the

[162-121]keys again. "I knew how it would be, miss," said Martha to her young

[162-122]mistress, "and I didn't say nothing, 'cause you understand her so

[162-123]well."

[162-124]Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla still live at the cottage, which,

[162-125]however, to Priscilla's great disgust, has been considerably improved

[162-126]and prettily furnished. This was done under the auspices of Hugh, but

[162-127]with funds chiefly supplied from the house of Brooke, Dorothy, and

[162-128]Co. Priscilla comes into [[exeter|Exeter]] to see her sister, perhaps, every

[162-129]other week; but will never sleep away from home, and very rarely will

[162-130]eat or drink at her sister's table. "I don't know why, I don't," she

[162-131]said to Dorothy, "but somehow it puts me out. It delays me in my

[162-132]efforts to come to the straw a day." Nevertheless, the sisters are

[162-133]dear friends.

[162-134]I fear that in some previous number a half promise was made that a

[162-135]husband should be found for Camilla French. That half-promise cannot

[162-136]be treated in the manner in which any whole promise certainly would

[162-137]have been handled. There is no husband ready for Cammy French. The

[162-138]reader, however, will be delighted to know that she made up her

[162-139]quarrel with her sister and Mr. Gibson, and is now rather fond

[162-140]of being a guest at Mr. Gibson's house. On her first return to

[162-141]Exeter after the Gibsons had come back from their little Cornish

[162-142]rustication, Camilla declared that she could not and would not bring

[162-143]herself to endure a certain dress of which Bella was very fond;--and

[162-144]as this dress had been bought for Camilla with special reference

[162-145]to the glories of her anticipated married life, this objection was

[162-146]almost natural. But Bella treated it as absurd, and Camilla at last

[162-147]gave way.

[162-148]It need only further be said that though Giles Hickbody and Martha

[162-149]are not actually married as yet,--men and women in their [[social-class|class]] of

[162-150]life always moving towards marriage with great precaution,--it is

[162-151]quite understood that the young people are engaged, and are to be

[162-152]made happy together at some future time.
