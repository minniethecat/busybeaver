# Duty

The Victorian ethic of duty that structures the moral universe of *He Knew He Was Right*, providing characters with a vocabulary of obligation that both constrains their choices and gives their actions moral weight.

## Overview

Duty in Trollope's novel functions as the countervailing force to desire, jealousy, and self-interest. Characters repeatedly frame their decisions in the language of duty. Trevelyan declares that Emily "has yet to learn that it is her duty to do as I tell her" (006-075), invoking marital duty as a weapon of control. The novel explores the tension between competing duties — Emily owes a duty of obedience to her husband but also a duty of self-respect to herself. The narrator presents the complexity of these "high ideas of conjugal duty" (008-169), showing how the language of obligation can mask tyranny.

## Context in Victorian Society

Victorian moral discourse was saturated with the language of duty. From the evangelical emphasis on religious duty to the secular ideal of public service, duty provided a shared moral vocabulary across class and religious lines. For women, duty was primarily defined in domestic terms: duty to father, then husband, then children. For men, duty encompassed professional obligation, public service, and the protection of female dependents. The novel both reflects and critiques this framework, showing how the language of duty can be weaponized — Trevelyan's insistence on his "rights" as a husband is framed as duty even as it becomes tyranny.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Competing Duties

The novel's central conflict can be understood as a clash of competing duties. Emily owes a duty of obedience to her husband under Victorian law and custom, but she also owes a duty of loyalty to her father's old friend Colonel Osborne, and a duty of self-respect to herself. Trevelyan claims a duty to protect his family's honour, but this claimed duty becomes indistinguishable from possessive tyranny.

### Sisterly Duty

Nora Rowley's relationship with Emily exemplifies dutiful sisterhood. She supports Emily through the marriage crisis, provides companionship, and refuses to abandon her even when doing so might advance her own romantic prospects. This sisterly duty is presented as an unambiguous moral good, contrasting with the compromised language of marital duty.

### Professional Duty

Hugh Stanbury's commitment to journalism is framed as a form of professional duty — the obligation to earn an honest living and to serve the public through truthful writing. This professional ethic provides an alternative moral framework to the aristocratic honour code that fails Trevelyan.

## Key Examples

### The Wife's Duty

The novel repeatedly interrogates what a wife owes her husband. Emily's refusal to end her friendship with Osborne is framed by her as consistent with her wifely duty — she has done nothing wrong and to concede would be to admit guilt. Trevelyan frames the same refusal as a violation of duty. The narrator notes that Trevelyan "believed that he would best do his duty by" asserting his authority (004-089), revealing how the language of duty can rationalize control. The novel sides with Emily, suggesting that a husband's demands, however couched in the language of duty, lose their moral force when they become unreasonable.

### The Husband's Duty

Trevelyan's failure is not merely that he is jealous but that he neglects the duties that accompany his claimed rights. A husband's duty, the novel implies, includes trust, protection, and respect — all of which Trevelyan abandons in his jealous obsession.
