## Section 86

[086-001]On the next morning she met him at breakfast. She went down stairs

[086-002]later than usual, not till ten, having hung about her aunt's room,

[086-003]thinking that thus she would escape him for the present. She would

[086-004]wait till he was gone out, and then she would go down. She did wait;

[086-005]but she could not hear the front door, and then her aunt murmured

[086-006]something about Brooke's breakfast. She was told to go down, and she

[086-007]went. But when on the stairs she slunk back to her own room, and

[086-008]stood there for awhile, aimless, motionless, not knowing what to do.

[086-009]Then one of the girls came to her, and told her that Mr. Burgess was

[086-010]waiting breakfast for her. She knew not what excuse to make, and at

[086-011]last descended slowly to the parlour. She was very happy, but had it

[086-012]been possible for her to have run away she would have gone.

[086-013]"Dear Dorothy," he said at once. "I may call you so,--may I not?"

[086-014]"Oh, yes."

[086-015]"And you will love me;--and be my own, own wife?"

[086-016]"No, Mr. Burgess."

[086-017]"No?"

[086-018]"I mean;--that is to say--"

[086-019]"Do you love me, Dorothy?"

[086-020]"Only think how ill Aunt Stanbury is, Mr. Burgess;--perhaps dying!

[086-021]How can I have any thought now except about her? It wouldn't be

[086-022]right;--would it?"

[086-023]"You may say that you love me."

[086-024]"Mr. Burgess, pray, pray don't speak of it now. If you do I must go

[086-025]away."

[086-026]"But do you love me?"

[086-027]"Pray, pray don't, Mr. Burgess!"

[086-028]There was nothing more to be got from her during the whole day than

[086-029]that. He told her in the evening that as soon as Miss Stanbury was

[086-030]well, he would come again;--that in any case he would come again. She

[086-031]sat quite still as he said this, with a solemn face,--but smiling at

[086-032]heart, laughing at heart, so happy! When she got up to leave him, and

[086-033]was forced to give him her hand, he seized her in his arms and kissed

[086-034]her. "That is very, very wrong," she said, sobbing, and then ran to

[086-035]her room,--the happiest girl in all [[exeter|Exeter]]. He was to start early on

[086-036]the following morning, and she knew that she would not be forced to

[086-037]see him again. Thinking of him was so much pleasanter than seeing

[086-038]him!

[086-039]CHAPTER LII.

[086-040]MR. OUTHOUSE COMPLAINS THAT IT'S HARD.

[086-041]Life had gone on during the winter at St. Diddulph's Parsonage in a

[086-042]dull, weary, painful manner. There had come a letter in November from

[086-043]Trevelyan to his wife, saying that as he could trust neither her nor

[086-044]her uncle with the custody of his child, he should send a person

[086-045]armed with due legal authority, addressed to Mr. Outhouse, for the

[086-046]recovery of the boy, and desiring that little Louis might be at once

[086-047]surrendered to the messenger. Then of course there had arisen great

[086-048]trouble in the house. Both Mrs. Trevelyan and [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] had learned

[086-049]by this time that, as regarded the master of the house, they were not

[086-050]welcome guests at St. Diddulph's. When the threat was shewn to Mr.

[086-051]Outhouse, he did not say a word to indicate that the child should be

[086-052]given up. He muttered something, indeed, about impotent nonsense,

[086-053]which seemed to imply that the threat could be of no avail; but there

[086-054]was none of that reassurance to be obtained from him which a positive

[086-055]promise on his part to hold the bairn against all comers would have

[086-056]given. Mrs. Outhouse told her niece more than once that the child

[086-057]would be given to no messenger whatever; but even she did not give

[086-058]the assurance with that energy which the mother would have liked.

[086-059]"They shall drag him away from me by force if they do take him!" said

[086-060]the mother, gnashing her teeth. Oh, if her father would but come!

[086-061]For some weeks she did not let the boy out of her sight; but when no

[086-062]messenger had presented himself by Christmas time, they all began to

[086-063]believe that the threat had in truth meant nothing,--that it had been

[086-064]part of the ravings of a madman.

[086-065]But the threat had meant something. Early on one morning in January

[086-066]Mr. Outhouse was told that a person in the hall wanted to see him,

[086-067]and Mrs. Trevelyan, who was sitting at breakfast, the child being at

[086-068]the moment up-stairs, started from her seat. The maid described the

[086-069]man as being "All as one as a [[social-class|gentleman]]," though she would not go so

[086-070]far as to say that he was a gentleman in fact. Mr. Outhouse slowly

[086-071]rose from his breakfast, went out to the man in the passage, and bade

[086-072]him follow into the little closet that was now used as a study. It is

[086-073]needless perhaps to say that the man was Bozzle.

[086-074]"I dare say, Mr. Houthouse, you don't know me," said Bozzle. Mr.

[086-075]Outhouse, disdaining all complimentary language, said that he

[086-076]certainly did not. "My name, Mr. Houthouse, is Samuel Bozzle, and I

[086-077]live at No. 55, Stony Walk, Union Street, Borough. I was in the Force

[086-078]once, but I work on my own 'ook now."

[086-079]"What do you want with me, Mr. Bozzle?"

[086-080]"It isn't so much with you, sir, as it is with a lady as is under

[086-081]your protection; and it isn't so much with the lady as it is with her

[086-082]infant."

[086-083]"Then you may go away, Mr. Bozzle," said Mr. Outhouse, impatiently.

[086-084]"You may as well go away at once."

[086-085]"Will you please read them few lines, sir," said Mr. Bozzle. "They

[086-086]is in Mr. Trewilyan's handwriting, which will no doubt be familiar

[086-087]characters,--leastways to Mrs. T., if you don't know the gent's

[086-088]fist." Mr. Outhouse, after looking at the paper for a minute, and

[086-089]considering deeply what in this emergency he had better do, did take

[086-090]the paper and read it. The words ran as follows: "I hereby give full

[086-091]authority to Mr. Samuel Bozzle, of 55, Stony Walk, Union Street,

[086-092]Borough, to claim and to enforce possession of the body of my child,

[086-093][[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]]; and I require that any person whatsoever who may

[086-094]now have the custody of the said child, whether it be my wife or any

[086-095]of her friends, shall at once deliver him up to Mr. Bozzle, on the

[086-096]production of this authority.--LOUIS TREVELYAN." It may be explained

[086-097]that before this document had been written there had been much

[086-098]correspondence on the subject between Bozzle and his employer. To

[086-099]give the ex-policeman his due, he had not at first wished to meddle

[086-100]in the matter of the child. He had a wife at home who expressed an

[086-101]opinion with much vigour that the boy should be left with its mother,

[086-102]and that he, Bozzle, should he succeed in getting hold of the child,

[086-103]would not know what to do with it. Bozzle was aware, moreover, that

[086-104]it was his business to find out facts, and not to perform actions.

[086-105]But his employer had become very urgent with him. Mr. Bideawhile had

[086-106]positively refused to move in the matter; and Trevelyan, mad as he

[086-107]was, had felt a disinclination to throw his affairs into the hands of

[086-108]a certain Mr. Skint, of Stamford Street, whom Bozzle had recommended

[086-109]to him as a lawyer. Trevelyan had hinted, moreover, that if Bozzle

[086-110]would make the application in person, that application, if not

[086-111]obeyed, would act with usefulness as a preliminary step for further

[086-112]personal measures to be taken by himself. He intended to return

[086-113]to England for the purpose, but he desired that the order for the

[086-114]child's rendition should be made at once. Therefore Bozzle had come.

[086-115]He was an earnest man, and had now worked himself up to a certain

[086-116]degree of energy in the matter. He was a man loving power, and

[086-117]specially anxious to enforce obedience from those with whom he came

[086-118]in contact by the production of the law's mysterious authority. In

[086-119]his heart he was ever tapping people on the shoulder, and telling

[086-120]them that they were wanted. Thus, when he displayed his document to

[086-121]Mr. Outhouse, he had taught himself at least to desire that that

[086-122]document should be obeyed.

[086-123]Mr. Outhouse read the paper and turned up his nose at it. "You had

[086-124]better go away," said he, as he thrust it back into Bozzle's hand.

[086-125]"Of course I shall go away when I have the child."

[086-126]"Psha!" said Mr. Outhouse.

[086-127]"What does that mean, Mr. Houthouse? I presume you'll not dispute the

[086-128]paternal parent's legal authority?"

[086-129]"Go away, sir," said Mr. Outhouse.

[086-130]"Go away!"

[086-131]"Yes;--out of this house. It's my belief that you are a knave."

[086-132]"A knave, Mr. Houthouse?"

[086-133]"Yes;--a knave. No one who was not a knave would lend a hand towards

[086-134]separating a little child from its mother. I think you are a knave,

[086-135]but I don't think you are fool enough to suppose that the child will

[086-136]be given up to you."

[086-137]"It's my belief that knave is hactionable," said Bozzle,--whose

[086-138]respect, however, for the clergyman was rising fast. "Would you mind

[086-139]ringing the bell, Mr. Houthouse, and calling me a knave again before

[086-140]the young woman?"

[086-141]"Go away," said Mr. Outhouse.

[086-142]"If you have no objection, sir, I should be glad to see the lady

[086-143]before I goes."

[086-144]"You won't see any lady here; and if you don't get out of my house

[086-145]when I tell you, I'll send for a real policeman." Then was Bozzle

[086-146]conquered; and, as he went, he admitted to himself that he had sinned

[086-147]against all the rules of his life in attempting to go beyond the

[086-148]legitimate line of his profession. As long as he confined himself

[086-149]to the getting up of facts nobody could threaten him with a "real

[086-150]policeman." But one fact he had learned to-day. The clergyman of St.

[086-151]Diddulph's, who had been represented to him as a weak, foolish man,

[086-152]was anything but that. Bozzle was much impressed in favour of Mr.

[086-153]Outhouse, and would have been glad to have done that gentleman a

[086-154]kindness had an opportunity come in his way.

[086-155]"What does he want, Uncle Oliphant?" said Mrs. Trevelyan at the foot

[086-156]of the stairs, guarding the way up to the nursery. At this moment the

[086-157]front door had just been closed behind the back of Mr. Bozzle.

[086-158]"You had better ask no questions," said Mr. Outhouse.

[086-159]"But is it about Louis?"

[086-160]"Yes, he came about him."

[086-161]"Well? Of course you must tell me, Uncle Oliphant. Think of my

[086-162]condition."

[086-163]"He had some stupid paper in his hand from your husband, but it meant

[086-164]nothing."

[086-165]"He was the messenger, then?"

[086-166]"Yes, he was the messenger. But I don't suppose he expected to get

[086-167]anything. Never mind. Go up and look after the child." Then Mrs.

[086-168]Trevelyan returned to her boy, and Mr. Outhouse went back to his

[086-169]papers.

[086-170]It was very hard upon him, Mr. Outhouse thought,--very hard. He

[086-171]was threatened with an action now, and most probably would become

[086-172]subject to one. Though he had been spirited enough in presence of the

[086-173]enemy, he was very much out of spirits at this moment. Though he had

[086-174]admitted to himself that his duty required him to protect his wife's

[086-175]niece, he had never taken the poor woman to his heart with a loving,

[086-176]generous feeling of true guardianship. Though he would not give up

[086-177]the child to Bozzle, he thoroughly wished that the child was out of

[086-178]his house. Though he called Bozzle a knave and Trevelyan a madman,

[086-179]still he considered that [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] was the chief sinner, and

[086-180]that [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] had behaved badly. He constantly repeated to

[086-181]himself the old adage, that there was no smoke without fire; and

[086-182]lamented the misfortune that had brought him into close relation

[086-183]with things and people that were so little to his taste. He sat for

[086-184]awhile, with a pen in his hand, at the miserable little substitute

[086-185]for a library table which had been provided for him, and strove to

[086-186]collect his thoughts and go on with his work. But the effort was in

[086-187]vain. Bozzle would be there, presenting his document, and begging

[086-188]that the maid might be rung for, in order that she might hear him

[086-189]called a knave. And then he knew that on this very day his niece

[086-190]intended to hand him money, which he could not refuse. Of what use

[086-191]would it be to refuse it now, after it had been once taken? As he

[086-192]could not write a word, he rose and went away to his wife.
