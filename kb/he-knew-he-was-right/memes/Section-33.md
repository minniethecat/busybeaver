## Section 33

[033-001]"There can be no reason why you should take any notice of the man,"

[033-002]said Trevelyan.

[033-003]"How can I help noticing him when I find him prowling about the

[033-004]place? Of course I shall know who he is."

[033-005]"I don't see that you need know anything about him."

[033-006]"My dear Trevelyan, you cannot have two ambassadors engaged in

[033-007]the same service without communication with each other. And any

[033-008]communication with Mr. Bozzle, except that of sending him back to

[033-009][[london|London]], I will not have." The controversy was ended by the writing of

[033-010]a letter from Trevelyan to Bozzle, which was confided to Stanbury, in

[033-011]which the ex-policeman was thanked for his activity and requested to

[033-012]return to London for the present. "As we are now aware that Colonel

[033-013]Osborne is in the neighbourhood," said the letter, "my friend Mr.

[033-014]Stanbury will know what to do."

[033-015]As soon as this was settled, Stanbury went to the office of the D. R.

[033-016]and made arrangement as to his work for three days. Jones could do

[033-017]the article on the Irish Church upon a pinch like this, although he

[033-018]had not given much study to the subject as yet; and Puddlethwaite,

[033-019]who was great in City matters, would try his hand on the present

[033-020]state of society in Rome, a subject on which it was essential that

[033-021]the D. R. should express itself at once. Having settled these little

[033-022]troubles Stanbury returned to his friend, and in the evening they

[033-023]dined together at a tavern.

[033-024]"And now, Trevelyan, let me know fairly what it is that you wish,"

[033-025]said Stanbury.

[033-026]"I wish to have my wife back again."

[033-027]"Simply that. If she will agree to come back, you will make no

[033-028]difficulty."

[033-029]"No; not quite simply that. I shall desire that she shall be guided

[033-030]by my wishes as to any intimacies she may form."

[033-031]"That is all very well; but is she to give any undertaking? Do you

[033-032]intend to exact any promise from her? It is my opinion that she will

[033-033]be willing enough to come back, and that when she is with you there

[033-034]will be no further cause for quarrelling. But I don't think she will

[033-035]bind herself by any exacted promise; and certainly not through a

[033-036]third person."

[033-037]"Then say nothing about it. Let her write a letter to me proposing to

[033-038]come,--and she shall come."

[033-039]"Very well. So far I understand. And now what about [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]?

[033-040]You don't want me to quarrel with him I suppose?"

[033-041]"I should like to keep that for myself," said Trevelyan, grimly.

[033-042]"If you will take my advice you will not trouble yourself about him,"

[033-043]said Stanbury. "But as far as I am concerned, I am not to meddle or

[033-044]make with him? Of course," continued Stanbury, after a pause, "if I

[033-045]find that he is intruding himself in my mother's house, I shall tell

[033-046]him that he must not come there."

[033-047]"But if you find him installed in your mother's house as a

[033-048]visitor,--how then?"

[033-049]"I do not regard that as possible."

[033-050]"I don't mean living there," said Trevelyan, "but coming backwards

[033-051]and forwards;--going on in habits of intimacy with,--with--?" His

[033-052]voice trembled so as he asked these questions, that he could not

[033-053]pronounce the word which was to complete them.

[033-054]"With Mrs. Trevelyan, you mean."

[033-055]"Yes; with my wife. I don't say that it is so; but it may be so. You

[033-056]will be bound to tell me the truth."

[033-057]"I will certainly tell you the truth."

[033-058]"And the whole truth."

[033-059]"Yes; the whole truth."

[033-060]"Should it be so I will never see her again,--never. And as for

[033-061]him;--but never mind." Then there was another short period of

[033-062]silence, during which Stanbury smoked his pipe and sipped his whisky

[033-063]toddy. "You must see," continued Trevelyan, "that it is absolutely

[033-064]necessary that I should do something. It is all very well for you to

[033-065]say that you do not like detectives. Neither do I like them. But what

[033-066]was I to do? When you condemn me you hardly realise the difficulties

[033-067]of my position."

[033-068]"It is the deuce of a nuisance certainly," said Stanbury, through the

[033-069]cloud of smoke,--thinking now not at all of Mrs. Trevelyan, but of

[033-070]Mrs. Trevelyan's sister.

[033-071]"It makes a man almost feel that he had better not marry at all,"

[033-072]said Trevelyan.

[033-073]"I don't see that. Of course there may come troubles. The tiles may

[033-074]fall on your head, you know, as you walk through the streets. As far

[033-075]as I can see, women go straight enough nineteen times out of twenty.

[033-076]But they don't like being,--what I call looked after."

[033-077]"And did I look after my wife more than I ought?"

[033-078]"I don't mean that; but if I were married,--which I never shall be,

[033-079]for I shall never attain to the respectability of a fixed [[money|income]],--I

[033-080]fancy I shouldn't look after my wife at all. It seems to me that

[033-081]women hate to be told about their duties."

[033-082]"But if you saw your wife, quite innocently, falling into an improper

[033-083]intimacy,--taking up with people she ought not to know,--doing that

[033-084]in ignorance, which could not but compromise yourself;--wouldn't you

[033-085]speak a word then?"

[033-086]"Oh! I might just say, in an off-hand way, that Jones was a rascal,

[033-087]or a liar, or a fool, or anything of that sort. But I would never

[033-088]caution her against Jones. By George, I believe a woman can stand

[033-089]anything better than that."

[033-090]"You have never tried it, my friend."

[033-091]"And I don't suppose I ever shall. As for me, I believe Aunt Stanbury

[033-092]was right when she said that I was a radical vagabond. I dare say I

[033-093]shall never try the thing myself, and therefore it's very easy to

[033-094]have a theory. But I must be off. Good night, old fellow. I'll do the

[033-095]best I can; and, at any rate, I'll let you know the truth."

[033-096]There had been a question during the day as to whether Stanbury

[033-097]should let his sister know by letter that he was expected; but it had

[033-098]been decided that he should appear at Nuncombe without any previous

[033-099]notification of his arrival. Trevelyan had thought that this was very

[033-100]necessary, and when Stanbury had urged that such a measure seemed

[033-101]to imply suspicion, he had declared that in no other way could the

[033-102]truth be obtained. He, Trevelyan, simply wanted to know the facts

[033-103]as they were occurring. It was a fact that Colonel Osborne was down

[033-104]in the neighbourhood of [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. That, at least, had been

[033-105]ascertained. It might very possibly be the case that he would be

[033-106]refused admittance to the [[clock-house|Clock House]],--that all the ladies there

[033-107]would combine to keep him out. But,--so Trevelyan urged,--the truth

[033-108]on this point was desired. It was essentially necessary to his

[033-109]happiness that he should know what was being done.

[033-110]"Your mother and sister," said he, "cannot be afraid of your coming

[033-111]suddenly among them."

[033-112]Stanbury, so urged, had found it necessary to yield, but yet he had

[033-113]felt that he himself was almost acting like a detective policeman, in

[033-114]purposely falling down upon them without a word of announcement. Had

[033-115]chance circumstances made it necessary that he should go in such a

[033-116]manner he would have thought nothing of it. It would simply have been

[033-117]a pleasant joke to him.

[033-118]As he went down by the train on the following day, he almost felt

[033-119]ashamed of the part which he had been called upon to perform.

[033-120]CHAPTER XX.

[033-121]SHEWING HOW COLONEL OSBORNE WENT TO COCKCHAFFINGTON.

[033-122]Together with Miss Stanbury's first letter to her sister-in-law a

[033-123]letter had also been delivered to Mrs. Trevelyan. [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]], as her

[033-124]sister had left the room with this in her hand, had expressed her

[033-125]opinion that it had come from Trevelyan; but it had in truth been

[033-126]written by Colonel Osborne. And when that second letter from Miss

[033-127]Stanbury had been received at the Clock House,--that in which she in

[033-128]plain terms begged pardon for the accusation conveyed in her first

[033-129]letter,--Colonel Osborne had started on his deceitful little journey

[033-130]to Cockchaffington, and Mr. Bozzle, the ex-policeman who had him in

[033-131]hand, had already asked his way to Nuncombe Putney.

[033-132]When Colonel Osborne learned that [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]] had broken up his

[033-133]establishment in Curzon Street, and had sent his wife away into a

[033-134]barbarous retirement in Dartmoor,--for such was the nature of the

[033-135]information on the subject which was spread among Trevelyan's friends

[033-136]in London;--and when he was made aware also that all this was done

[033-137]on his account,--because he was so closely intimate with Trevelyan's

[033-138]wife, and because Trevelyan's wife was, and persisted in continuing

[033-139]to be, so closely intimate with him,--his vanity was gratified.

[033-140]Although it might be true,--and no doubt was true,--that he said much

[033-141]to his friends and to himself of the deep sorrow which he felt that

[033-142]such a trouble should befall his old friend and his old friend's

[033-143]daughter; nevertheless, as he curled his grey whiskers before the

[033-144]glass, and made the most of such remnant of hair as was left on

[033-145]the top of his head, as he looked to the padding of his coat, and

[033-146]completed a study of the wrinkles beneath his eyes, so that in

[033-147]conversation they might be as little apparent as possible, he felt

[033-148]more of pleasure than of pain in regard to the whole affair. It was

[033-149]very sad that it should be so, but it was human. Had it been in his

[033-150]power to set the whole matter right by a word, he would probably have

[033-151]spoken that word; but as this was not possible, as Trevelyan had in

[033-152]his opinion made a gross fool of himself, as [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] was

[033-153]very nice, and not the less nice in that she certainly was fond

[033-154]of himself, as great tyranny had been used towards her, and as he

[033-155]himself had still the plea of old family friendship to protect his

[033-156]conscience,--to protect his conscience unless he went so far as to

[033-157]make that plea an additional sting to his conscience,--he thought

[033-158]that, as a man, he must follow up the matter. Here was a young, and

[033-159]fashionable, and very pretty woman banished to the wilds of Dartmoor

[033-160]for his sake. And, as far as he could understand, she would not have

[033-161]been so banished had she consented to say that she would give up

[033-162]her acquaintance with him. In such circumstances as these was it

[033-163]possible that he should do nothing? Various ideas ran through his

[033-164]head. He began to think that if Trevelyan were out of the way, he

[033-165]might,--might perhaps be almost tempted to make this woman his wife.

[033-166]She was so nice that he almost thought that he might be rash enough

[033-167]for that, although he knew well the satisfaction of being a bachelor;

[033-168]but as the thought suggested itself to him, he was well aware that

[033-169]he was thinking of a thing quite distant from him. The reader is not

[033-170]to suppose that Colonel Osborne meditated any making-away with the

[033-171]husband. Our Colonel was certainly not the man for a murder. Nor did

[033-172]he even think of running away with his friend's daughter. Though he

[033-173]told himself that he could dispose of his wrinkles satisfactorily,

[033-174]still he knew himself and his powers sufficiently to be aware that

[033-175]he was no longer fit to be the hero of such a romance as that. He

[033-176]acknowledged to himself that there was much labour to be gone through

[033-177]in running away with another man's wife; and that the results, in

[033-178]respect to personal comfort, are not always happy. But what if Mrs.

[033-179]Trevelyan were to divorce herself from her husband on the score of

[033-180]her husband's cruelty? Various horrors were related as to the man's

[033-181]treatment of his wife. By some it was said that she was in the prison

[033-182]on Dartmoor,--or, if not actually in the prison, an arrangement which

[033-183]the prison discipline might perhaps make difficult,--that she was in

[033-184]the custody of one of the prison warders who possessed a prim cottage

[033-185]and a grim wife, just outside the prison walls. Colonel Osborne did

[033-186]not himself believe even so much as this, but he did believe that

[033-187]Mrs. Trevelyan had been banished to some inhospitable region, to some

[033-188]dreary comfortless abode, of which, as the wife of a man of fortune,

[033-189]she would have great ground to complain. So thinking, he did not

[033-190]probably declare to himself that a divorce should be obtained,

[033-191]and that, in such event, he would marry the lady,--but ideas came

[033-192]across his mind in that direction. Trevelyan was a cruel Bluebeard;

[033-193]Emily,--as he was studious to call Mrs. Trevelyan,--was a dear

[033-194]injured saint. And as for himself, though he acknowledged to himself

[033-195]that the lumbago pinched him now and again, so that he could not rise

[033-196]from his chair with all the alacrity of youth, yet, when he walked

[033-197]along Pall Mall with his coat properly buttoned, he could not but

[033-198]observe that a great many young women looked at him with admiring

[033-199]eyes.
