## Section 102

[102-001]Sir Marmaduke, who was not himself a good lawyer, had been closeted

[102-002]with the policeman for a quarter of an hour, and had learned the

[102-003]policeman's views. Of course, the father of the child was the person

[102-004]who had done the deed. Whether the cabman had been in the plot or

[102-005]not, was not matter of much consequence. There could be no doubt that

[102-006]some one had told the man to go to Parker's Hotel, as the cab was

[102-007]starting; and it would probably be impossible to punish him in the

[102-008]teeth of such instructions. Sir Marmaduke, however, could doubtless

[102-009]have the cabman summoned. And as for the absolute abduction of the

[102-010]child, the policeman was of opinion that a father could not be

[102-011]punished for obtaining possession of his son by such a stratagem,

[102-012]unless the custody of the child had been made over to the mother

[102-013]by some court of law. The policeman, indeed, seemed to think that

[102-014]nothing could be done, and Sir Marmaduke was inclined to agree with

[102-015]him. When this was explained to Mrs. Trevelyan by her mother, she

[102-016]again became hysterical in her agony, and could hardly be restrained

[102-017]from going forth herself to look for her lost treasure.

[102-018]It need hardly be further explained that Trevelyan had planned the

[102-019]stratagem in concert with Mr. Bozzle. Bozzle, though strongly

[102-020]cautioned by his wife to keep himself out of danger in the matter,

[102-021]was sorely tempted by his employer's offer of a hundred pounds.

[102-022]He positively refused to be a party to any attempt at violence at

[102-023]St. Diddulph's; but when he learned, as he did learn, that Mrs.

[102-024]Trevelyan, with her sister and baby, were to be transferred from St.

[102-025]Diddulph's in a cab to Baker Street, and that the journey was luckily

[102-026]to be made during the shades of evening, his active mind went to

[102-027]work, and he arranged the plan. There were many difficulties, and

[102-028]even some pecuniary difficulty. He bargained that he should have his

[102-029]hundred pounds clear of all deduction for expenses, and then the

[102-030]attendant expenses were not insignificant. It was necessary that

[102-031]there should be four men in the service, all good and true; and men

[102-032]require to be well paid for such [[virtue|goodness]] and truth. There was the

[102-033]man, himself an ex-policeman, who gave the instructions to the first

[102-034]cabman, as he was starting. The cabman would not undertake the job at

[102-035]all unless he were so instructed on the spot, asserting that in this

[102-036]way he would be able to prove that the orders he obeyed came from

[102-037]the lady's husband. And there was the crafty pseudo-waiter, with the

[102-038]napkin and no hat, who had carried the boy to the cab in which his

[102-039]father was sitting. And there were the two cabmen. Bozzle planned

[102-040]it all, and with some difficulty arranged the preliminaries. How

[102-041]successful was the scheme, we have seen; and Bozzle, for a month, was

[102-042]able to assume a superiority over his wife, which that honest woman

[102-043]found to be very disagreeable. "There ain't no fraudulent abduction

[102-044]in it at all," Bozzle exclaimed, "because a wife ain't got no rights

[102-045]again her husband,--not in such a matter as that." Mrs. Bozzle

[102-046]implied that if her husband were to take her child away from her

[102-047]without her leave, she'd let him know something about it. But as

[102-048]the husband had in his possession the note for a hundred pounds,

[102-049]realized, Mrs. Bozzle had not much to say in support of her view of

[102-050]the case.

[102-051]On the morning after the occurrence, while Sir Marmaduke was waiting

[102-052]with his solicitor upon a magistrate to find whether anything could

[102-053]be done, the following letter was brought to Mrs. Trevelyan at

[102-054]Gregg's Hotel:--

[102-055]Our child is safe with me, and will remain so. If you care

[102-056]to obtain legal advice you will find that I as his father

[102-057]have a right to keep him under my protection. I shall

[102-058]do so; but will allow you to see him as soon as I shall

[102-059]have received a full guarantee that you have no idea of

[102-060]withdrawing him from my charge.

[102-061]A home for yourself with me is still open to you,--on

[102-062]condition that you will give me the promise that I have

[102-063]demanded from you; and as long as I shall not hear that

[102-064]you again see or communicate with the person to whose

[102-065]acquaintance I object. While you remain away from me I

[102-066]will cause you to be paid L50 a month, as I do not wish

[102-067]that you should be a burden on others. But this payment

[102-068]will depend also on your not seeing or holding any

[102-069]communication with the person to whom I have alluded.

[102-070]Your affectionate and offended husband,

[102-071]LOUIS TREVELYAN.

[102-072]A letter addressed to the Acrobats' Club will reach me.

[102-073]Sir Rowley came home dispirited and unhappy, and could not give much

[102-074]comfort to his daughter. The magistrate had told him that though the

[102-075]cabman might probably be punished for taking the ladies otherwise

[102-076]than as directed,--if the direction to Baker Street could be

[102-077]proved,--nothing could be done to punish the father. The magistrate

[102-078]explained that under a certain Act of Parliament the mother might

[102-079]apply to the Court of Chancery for the custody of any children under

[102-080]seven years of age, and that the court would probably grant such

[102-081]custody,--unless it were shewn that the wife had left her husband

[102-082]without sufficient cause. The magistrate could not undertake to say

[102-083]whether or no sufficient cause had here been given;--or whether the

[102-084]husband was in fault or the wife. It was, however, clear that nothing

[102-085]could be done without application to the Court of Chancery. It

[102-086]appeared,--so said the magistrate,--that the husband had offered a

[102-087]home to his wife, and that in offering it he had attempted to impose

[102-088]no conditions which could be shown to be cruel before a judge. The

[102-089]magistrate thought that Mr. Trevelyan had done nothing illegal in

[102-090]taking the child from the cab. Sir Marmaduke, on hearing this, was

[102-091]of opinion that nothing could be gained by legal interference. His

[102-092]private desire was to get hold of Trevelyan and pull him limb from

[102-093]limb. Lady Rowley thought that her daughter had better go back to her

[102-094]husband, let the future consequences be what they might. And the poor

[102-095]desolate mother herself had almost brought herself to offer to do so,

[102-096]having in her brain some idea that she would after a while be able to

[102-097]escape with her boy. As for love for her husband, certainly there was

[102-098]none now left in her bosom. Nor could she teach herself to think it

[102-099]possible that she should ever live with him again on friendly terms.

[102-100]But she would submit to anything with the object of getting back her

[102-101]boy. Three or four letters were written to Mr. Trevelyan in as many

[102-102]days from his wife, from Lady Rowley, and from Nora; in which various

[102-103]overtures were made. Trevelyan wrote once again to his wife. She

[102-104]knew, he said, already the terms on which she might come back. These

[102-105]terms were still open to her. As for the boy, he certainly should not

[102-106]leave his father. A meeting might be planned on condition that he,

[102-107]Trevelyan, were provided with a written assurance from his wife that

[102-108]she would not endeavour to remove the boy, and that he himself should

[102-109]be present at the meeting.

[102-110]Thus the first week was passed after Sir Marmaduke's return,--and a

[102-111]most wretched time it was for all the party at Gregg's Hotel.

[102-112]CHAPTER LXII.

[102-113]LADY ROWLEY MAKES AN ATTEMPT.

[102-114][Illustration]

[102-115]Nothing could be more uncomfortable than the state of Sir Marmaduke

[102-116]Rowley's family for the first ten days after the arrival in [[london|London]] of

[102-117]the Governor of the [[mandarin-islands|Mandarin Islands]]. Lady Rowley had brought with

[102-118]her two of her girls,--the third and fourth,--and, as we know, had

[102-119]been joined by the two eldest, so that there was a large family

[102-120]of ladies gathered together. A house had been taken in Manchester

[102-121]Street, to which they had intended to transfer themselves after a

[102-122]single night passed at Gregg's Hotel. But the trouble and sorrow

[102-123]inflicted upon them by the abduction of Mrs. Trevelyan's child, and

[102-124]the consequent labours thrust upon Sir Marmaduke's shoulders had been

[102-125]so heavy, that they had slept six nights at the hotel, before they

[102-126]were able to move themselves into the house prepared for them. By

[102-127]that time all idea had been abandoned of recovering the child by any

[102-128]legal means to be taken as a consequence of the illegality of the

[102-129]abduction. The boy was with his father, and the lawyers seemed to

[102-130]think that the father's rights were paramount,--as he had offered a

[102-131]home to his wife without any conditions which a court of law would

[102-132]adjudge to be cruel. If she could shew that he had driven her to live

[102-133]apart from him by his own bad conduct, then probably the custody of

[102-134]her boy might be awarded to her, until the child should be seven

[102-135]years old. But when the circumstances of the case were explained to

[102-136]Sir Marmaduke's lawyer by Lady Rowley, that [[social-class|gentleman]] shook his head.

[102-137]Mrs. Trevelyan had, he said, no case with which she could go into

[102-138]court. Then by degrees there were words whispered as to the husband's

[102-139]madness. The lawyer said that that was a matter for the doctors. If

[102-140]a certain amount of medical [[truth|evidence]] could be obtained to shew that

[102-141]the husband was in truth mad, the wife could, no doubt, obtain the

[102-142]custody of the child. When this was reported to Mrs. Trevelyan, she

[102-143]declared that conduct such as her husband's must suffice to prove any

[102-144]man to be mad; but at this Sir Marmaduke shook his head, and Lady

[102-145]Rowley sat, sadly silent, with her daughter's hand within her own.

[102-146]They would not dare to tell her that she could regain her child by

[102-147]that plea.

[102-148]During those ten days they did not learn whither the boy had been

[102-149]carried, nor did they know even where the father might be found. Sir

[102-150]Marmaduke followed up the address as given in the letter, and learned

[102-151]from the porter at "The Acrobats" that the gentleman's letters

[102-152]were sent to No. 55, Stony Walk, Union Street, Borough. To this

[102-153]uncomfortable locality Sir Marmaduke travelled more than once. Thrice

[102-154]he went thither, intent on finding his son-in-law's residence. On the

[102-155]two first occasions he saw no one but Mrs. Bozzle; and the discretion

[102-156]of that lady in declining to give any information was most admirable.

[102-157]"Trewillian!" Yes, she had heard the name certainly. It might be that

[102-158]her husband had business engagements with a gent of that name. She

[102-159]would not say even that for certain, as it was not her custom ever

[102-160]to make any inquiries as to her husband's business engagements. Her

[102-161]husband's business engagements were, she said, much too important

[102-162]for the "likes of she" to know anything about them. When was

[102-163]Bozzle likely to be at home? Bozzle was never likely to be at home.

[102-164]According to her showing, Bozzle was of all husbands the most

[102-165]erratic. He might perhaps come in for an hour or two in the middle of

[102-166]the day on a Wednesday, or perhaps would take a cup of tea at home on

[102-167]Friday evening. But anything so fitful and uncertain as were Bozzle's

[102-168]appearances in the bosom of his family was not to be conceived in the

[102-169]mind of woman. Sir Marmaduke then called in the middle of the day on

[102-170]Wednesday, but Bozzle was reported to be away in the provinces. His

[102-171]wife had no idea in which of the provinces he was at that moment

[102-172]engaged. The persevering governor from the islands called again on

[102-173]the Friday evening, and then, by chance, Bozzle was found at home.

[102-174]But Sir Marmaduke succeeded in gaining very little information

[102-175]even from Bozzle. The man acknowledged that he was employed by Mr.

[102-176]Trevelyan. Any letter or parcel left with him for Mr. Trevelyan

[102-177]should be duly sent to that gentleman. If Sir Marmaduke wanted Mr.

[102-178]Trevelyan's address, he could write to Mr. Trevelyan and ask for it.

[102-179]If Mr. Trevelyan declined to give it, was it likely that he, Bozzle,

[102-180]should betray it? Sir Marmaduke explained who he was at some length.

[102-181]Bozzle with a smile assured the governor that he knew very well

[102-182]who he was. He let drop a few words to show that he was intimately

[102-183]acquainted with the whole course of Sir Marmaduke's family affairs.

[102-184]He knew all about the Mandarins, and [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], and Gregg's

[102-185]Hotel,--not that he said anything about Parker's Hotel,--and the

[102-186]Colonial Office. He spoke of Miss Nora, and even knew the names of

[102-187]the other two young ladies, Miss Sophia and Miss Lucy. It was a

[102-188]weakness with Bozzle,--that of displaying his information. He would

[102-189]have much liked to be able to startle Sir Marmaduke by describing

[102-190]the Government House in the island, or by telling him something of

[102-191]his old carriage-horses. But of such information as Sir Marmaduke

[102-192]desired, Sir Marmaduke got none.
