## Section 9

[009-001]And if there had been, how much less would have been her ground of

[009-002]complaint? Let a man have any such friendships,--what friendships he

[009-003]may,--he does not disgrace his wife. He felt himself to be so true of

[009-004]heart that he desired no such friendships; but for a man indulging in

[009-005]such friendships there might be excuse. Even though a man be false,

[009-006]a woman is not shamed and brought unto the dust before all the world.

[009-007]But the slightest rumour on a woman's name is a load of infamy on

[009-008]her husband's shoulders. It was not enough for Caesar that his wife

[009-009]should be true; it was necessary to Caesar that she should not even be

[009-010]suspected. Trevelyan told himself that he suspected his wife of no

[009-011]sin. God forbid that it should ever come to that, both for his sake

[009-012]and for hers; and, above all, for the sake of that boy who was so

[009-013]dear to them both! But there would be the vile whispers, and dirty

[009-014]slanders would be dropped from envious tongues into envious ears, and

[009-015]minds prone to evil would think evil of him and of his. Had not Lady

[009-016]Milborough already cautioned him? Oh, that he should have lived to

[009-017]have been cautioned about his wife;--that he should be told that eyes

[009-018]outside had looked into the sacred shrine of his heart and seen that

[009-019]things there were fatally amiss! And yet Lady Milborough was quite

[009-020]right. Had he not in his hand at this moment a document that proved

[009-021]her to be right? "Dear Emily!" He took this note and crushed it in

[009-022]his fist, and then pulled it into fragments.

[009-023]But what should he do? There was, first of all considerations, the

[009-024]duty which he owed to his wife, and the love which he bore her.

[009-025]That she was ignorant and innocent he was sure; but then she was so

[009-026]contumacious that he hardly knew how to take a step in the direction

[009-027]of guarding her from the effects of her ignorance, and maintaining

[009-028]for her the advantages of her innocence. He was her master, and she

[009-029]must know that he was her master. But how was he to proceed when she

[009-030]refused to obey the plainest and most necessary command which he laid

[009-031]upon her? Let a man be ever so much his wife's master, he cannot

[009-032]maintain his masterdom by any power which the law places in his

[009-033]hands. He had asked his wife for a promise of obedience, and she

[009-034]would not give it to him! What was he to do next? He could, no

[009-035]doubt,--at least he thought so,--keep the man from her presence. He

[009-036]could order the servant not to admit the man, and the servant would

[009-037]doubtless obey him. But to what a condition would he then have been

[009-038]brought! Would not the world then be over for him,--over for him as

[009-039]the husband of a wife whom he could not love unless he respected her?

[009-040]Better that there should be no such world, than call in the aid of a

[009-041]servant to guard the conduct of his wife!

[009-042]As he thought of it all it seemed to him that if she would not obey

[009-043]him, and give him this promise, they must be separated. He would not

[009-044]live with her, he would not give her the privileges of his wife, if

[009-045]she refused to render to him the obedience which was his privilege.

[009-046]The more he thought of it, the more convinced he was that he ought

[009-047]not to yield to her. Let her once yield to him, and then his

[009-048]tenderness should begin, and there should be no limit to it. But he

[009-049]would not see her till she had yielded. He would not see her; and if

[009-050]he should find that she did see [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], then he would tell

[009-051]her that she could no longer dwell under the same roof with him.

[009-052]His resolution on these points was very strong, and yet there came

[009-053]over him a feeling that it was his duty to be gentle. There was a

[009-054]feeling also that that privilege of receiving obedience, which was

[009-055]so indubitably his own, could only be maintained by certain wise

[009-056]practices on his part, in which gentleness must predominate.

[009-057]Wives are bound to obey their husbands, but obedience cannot be

[009-058]exacted from wives, as it may from servants, by aid of law and

[009-059]with penalties, or as from a horse, by punishments and manger

[009-060]curtailments. A man should be master in his own house, but he should

[009-061]make his mastery palatable, equitable, smooth, soft to the touch,

[009-062]a thing almost unfelt. How was he to do all this now, when he had

[009-063]already given an order to which obedience had been refused unless

[009-064]under certain stipulations,--an agreement with which would be

[009-065]degradation to him? He had pointed out to his wife her duty, and she

[009-066]had said she would do her duty as pointed out, on condition that he

[009-067]would beg her pardon for having pointed it out! This he could not and

[009-068]would not do. Let the heavens fall,--and the falling of the heavens

[009-069]in this case was a separation between him and his wife,--but he would

[009-070]not consent to such injustice as that!

[009-071]But what was he to do at this moment,--especially with reference to

[009-072]that note which he had destroyed. At last he resolved to write to his

[009-073]wife, and he consequently did write and send to her the following

[009-074]letter:--

[009-075]May 4.

[009-076]DEAREST EMILY,

[009-077]If Colonel Osborne should write to you again, it will

[009-078]be better that you should not open his letter. As you

[009-079]know his handwriting, you will have no difficulty in so

[009-080]arranging. Should any further letter come from Colonel

[009-081]Osborne addressed to you, you had better put it under

[009-082]cover to me, and take no notice of it yourself.

[009-083]I shall dine at the club to-day. We were to have gone to

[009-084]Mrs. Peacock's in the evening. You had better write a line

[009-085]to say that we shall not be there. I am very sorry that

[009-086]Nora should lose her evening. Pray think very carefully

[009-087]over what I have asked of you. My request to you is, that

[009-088]you shall give me a promise that you will not willingly

[009-089]see Colonel Osborne again. Of course you will understand

[009-090]that this is not supposed to extend to accidental

[009-091]meetings, as to which, should they occur,--and they would

[009-092]be sure to occur,--you would find that they would be

[009-093]wholly unnoticed by me.

[009-094]But I must request that you will comply with my wish in

[009-095]this matter. If you will send for me, I will go to you

[009-096]instantly, and after one word from you to the desired

[009-097]effect, you will find that there will be no recurrence by

[009-098]me to a subject so hateful. As I have done, and am doing

[009-099]what I think to be right, I cannot stultify myself by

[009-100]saying that I think I have been wrong.

[009-101]Yours always, dearest Emily,

[009-102]With the most thorough love,

[009-103]LOUIS TREVELYAN.

[009-104]This letter he himself put on his wife's dressing-room table, and

[009-105]then he went out to his club.

[009-106]CHAPTER VI.

[009-107]SHEWING HOW RECONCILIATION WAS MADE.

[009-108]"Look at that," said Mrs. Trevelyan, when her sister came into her

[009-109]room about an hour before dinner-time. Nora read the letter, and

[009-110]then asked her sister what she meant to do. "I have written to Mrs.

[009-111]Peacock. I don't know what else I can do. It is very hard upon

[009-112]you,--that you should have been kept at home. But I don't suppose Mr.

[009-113]Glascock would have been at Mrs. Peacock's."

[009-114]"And what else will you do, Emily?"

[009-115]"Nothing;--simply live deserted and forlorn till he shall choose to

[009-116]find his wits again. There is nothing else that a woman can do. If he

[009-117]chooses to dine at his club every day, I can't help it. We must put

[009-118]off all the engagements, and that will be hard upon you."

[009-119]"Don't talk about me. It is too terrible to think that there should

[009-120]be such a quarrel."

[009-121]"What can I do? Have I been wrong?"

[009-122]"Simply do what he tells you, whether it is wrong or right. If it's

[009-123]right, it ought to be done, and if it's wrong, it will not be your

[009-124]fault."

[009-125]"That's very easily said, and it sounds logical; but you must know

[009-126]it's unreasonable."

[009-127]"I don't care about reason. He is your husband, and if he wishes it

[009-128]you should do it. And what will be the harm? You don't mean to see

[009-129]Colonel Osborne any more. You have already said that he's not to be

[009-130]admitted."

[009-131]"I have said that nobody is to be admitted. Louis has driven me

[009-132]to that. How can I look the servant in the face and tell him that

[009-133]any special [[social-class|gentleman]] is not to be admitted to see me? Oh dear! oh

[009-134]dear! have I done anything to deserve it? Was ever so monstrous an

[009-135]accusation made against any woman! If it were not for my boy, I would

[009-136]defy him to do his worst."

[009-137]On the day following, Nora again became a messenger between the

[009-138]husband and wife, and before dinner-time a reconciliation had been

[009-139]effected. Of course the wife gave way at last; and of course she gave

[009-140]way so cunningly that the husband received none of the gratification

[009-141]which he had expected in her surrender. "Tell him to come," Nora had

[009-142]urged. "Of course he can come if he pleases," Emily had replied.

[009-143]Then Nora had told Louis to come, and Louis had demanded whether,

[009-144]if he did so, the promise which he had exacted would be given. It

[009-145]is to be feared that Nora perverted the truth a little; but if ever

[009-146]such perversion may be forgiven, forgiveness was due to her. If

[009-147]they could only be brought together, she was sure that there would

[009-148]be a reconciliation. They were brought together, and there was a

[009-149]reconciliation.

[009-150]"Dearest Emily, I am so glad to come to you," said the husband,

[009-151]walking up to his wife in their bed-room, and taking her in his arms.

[009-152][Illustration: Shewing how reconciliation was made.]

[009-153]"I have been very unhappy, Louis, for the last two days," said she,

[009-154]very gravely,--returning his kiss, but returning it somewhat coldly.

[009-155]"We have both been unhappy, I am sure," said he. Then he paused that

[009-156]the promise might be made to him. He had certainly understood that

[009-157]it was to be made without reserve,--as an act on her part which she

[009-158]had fully consented to perform. But she stood silent, with one hand

[009-159]on the dressing-table, looking away from him, very beautiful, and

[009-160]dignified too, in her manner; but not, as far as he could judge,

[009-161]either repentant or submissive. "Nora said that you would make me the

[009-162]promise which I ask from you."

[009-163]"I cannot think, Louis, how you can want such a promise from me."

[009-164]"I think it right to ask it; I do indeed."

[009-165]"Can you imagine that I shall ever willingly see this gentleman again

[009-166]after what has occurred? It will be for you to tell the servant. I

[009-167]do not know how I can do that. But, as a matter of course, I will

[009-168]encourage no person to come to your house of whom you disapprove. It

[009-169]would be exactly the same of any man or of any woman."

[009-170]"That is all that I ask."

[009-171]"I am surprised that you should have thought it necessary to make any

[009-172]formal request in the matter. Your word was quite sufficient. That

[009-173]you should find cause of complaint in Colonel Osborne's coming here

[009-174]is of course a different thing."

[009-175]"Quite a different thing," said he.

[009-176]"I cannot pretend to understand either your motives or your fears.

[009-177]I do not understand them. My own [[pride|self-respect]] prevents me from

[009-178]supposing it to be possible that you have attributed an evil thought

[009-179]to me."
