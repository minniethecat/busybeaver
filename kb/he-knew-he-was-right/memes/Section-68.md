## Section 68

[068-001]"Mr. Outhouse," said the Colonel, "I have thought it best to come

[068-002]to you the very moment that I got back to town from Scotland." Mr.

[068-003]Outhouse bowed, and was bethinking himself slowly what manner of

[068-004]speech he would adopt. "I leave town again to-morrow for Dorsetshire.

[068-005]I am going down to my friends, the Brambers, for partridge shooting."

[068-006]Mr. Outhouse knitted his thick brows, in further inward condemnation.

[068-007]Partridge shooting! yes;--this was September, and partridge shooting

[068-008]would be the probable care and occupation of such a man at such a

[068-009]time. A man without a duty in the world! Perhaps, added to this there

[068-010]was a feeling that, whereas [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] could shoot Scotch grouse

[068-011]in August, and Dorsetshire partridges in September, and go about

[068-012]throughout the whole year like a roaring lion, he, Mr. Outhouse,

[068-013]was forced to remain at St. Diddulph's-in-the-East, from January to

[068-014]December, with the exception of one small parson's week spent at

[068-015]Margate, for the benefit of his wife's health. If there was such a

[068-016]thought, or rather, such a feeling, who will say that it was not

[068-017]natural? "But I could not go through [[london|London]] without seeing you,"

[068-018]continued the Colonel. "This is a most frightful infatuation of

[068-019]Trevelyan!"

[068-020]"Very frightful, indeed," said Mr. Outhouse.

[068-021]"And, on my honour as a [[social-class|gentleman]], not the slightest cause in the

[068-022]world."

[068-023]"You are old enough to be the lady's father," said Mr. Outhouse,

[068-024]managing in that to get one blow at the gallant Colonel.

[068-025]"Just so. God bless my soul!" Mr. Outhouse shrunk visibly at this

[068-026]profane allusion to the Colonel's soul. "Why, I've known her father

[068-027]ever so many years. As you say, I might almost be her father myself."

[068-028]As far as age went, such certainly might have been the case, for the

[068-029]Colonel was older than Sir Marmaduke. "Look here, Mr. Outhouse, here

[068-030]is a letter I got from Emily--"

[068-031]"From Mrs. Trevelyan?"

[068-032]"Yes, from Mrs. Trevelyan; and as well as I can understand, it must

[068-033]have been sent to me by Trevelyan himself. Did you ever hear of such

[068-034]a thing? And now I'm told he has gone away, nobody knows where, and

[068-035]has left her here."

[068-036]"He has gone away,--nobody knows where."

[068-037]"Of course, I don't ask to see her."

[068-038]"It would be imprudent, Colonel Osborne; and could not be permitted

[068-039]in this house."

[068-040]"I don't ask it. I have known [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] since she was an

[068-041]infant, and have always loved her. I'm her godfather, for aught I

[068-042]know,--though one forgets things of that sort." Mr. Outhouse again

[068-043]knit his eyebrows and shuddered visibly. "She and I have been fast

[068-044]friends,--and why not? But, of course, I can't interfere."

[068-045]"If you ask me, Colonel Osborne, I should say that you can do nothing

[068-046]in the matter;--except to remain away from her. When Sir Marmaduke is

[068-047]in England, you can see him, if you please."

[068-048]"See him;--of course, I shall see him. And, by George, Louis

[068-049]Trevelyan will have to see him, too! I shouldn't like to have to

[068-050]stand up before Rowley if I had treated a daughter of his in such a

[068-051]fashion. You know Rowley, of course?"

[068-052]"Oh, yes; I know him."

[068-053]"He's not the sort of man to bear this sort of thing. He'll about

[068-054]tear Trevelyan in pieces if he gets hold of him. God bless my soul--"

[068-055]the eyebrows went to work again,--"I never heard of such a thing in

[068-056]all my life! Does he pay anything for them, Mr. Outhouse?"

[068-057]This was dreadful to the poor clergyman. "That is a subject which

[068-058]we surely need not discuss," said he. Then he remembered that

[068-059]such speech on his part was like to a subterfuge, and he found it

[068-060]necessary to put himself right. "I am repaid for the maintenance here

[068-061]of my nieces, and the little boy, and their attendants. I do not know

[068-062]why the question should be asked, but such is the fact."

[068-063]"Then they are here by agreement between you and him?"

[068-064]"No, sir; they are not. There is no such agreement. But I do not like

[068-065]these interrogatives from a stranger as to matters which should be

[068-066]private."

[068-067]"You cannot wonder at my interest, Mr. Outhouse."

[068-068]"You had better restrain it, sir, till Sir Marmaduke arrives. I shall

[068-069]then wash my hands of the affair."

[068-070]"And she is pretty well;--Emily, I mean?"

[068-071]"Mrs. Trevelyan's health is good."

[068-072]"Pray tell her though I could not--might not ask to see her, I came

[068-073]to inquire after her the first moment that I was in London. Pray

[068-074]tell her how much I feel for her;--but she will know that. When Sir

[068-075]Marmaduke is here, of course, we shall meet. When she is once more

[068-076]under her father's wing, she need not be restrained by any absurd

[068-077]commands from a husband who has deserted her. At present, of course,

[068-078]I do not ask to see her."

[068-079]"Of course, you do not, Colonel Osborne."

[068-080]"And give my love to Nora;--dear little Nora! There can be no reason

[068-081]why she and I should not shake hands."

[068-082]"I should prefer that it should not be so in this house," said the

[068-083]clergyman, who was now [[reputation|standing]],--in expectation that his unwelcome

[068-084]guest would go.

[068-085]"Very well;--so be it. But you will understand I could not be in

[068-086]London without coming and asking after them." Then the Colonel at

[068-087]last took his leave, and Mr. Outhouse was left to his solitude and

[068-088]his sermons.

[068-089]Mrs. Outhouse was very angry when she heard of the visit. "Men of

[068-090]that sort," she said, "think it a fine thing, and talk about it. I

[068-091]believe the poor girl is as innocent as I am, but he isn't innocent.

[068-092]He likes it."

[068-093]"'It is easier,'" said Mr. Outhouse solemnly, "'for a camel to go

[068-094]through the eye of a needle, than for a rich man to enter the kingdom

[068-095]of God.'"

[068-096]"I don't know that he is a rich man," said Mrs. Outhouse; "but he

[068-097]wouldn't have come here if he had been honest."

[068-098]Mrs. Trevelyan was told of the visit, and simply said that of course

[068-099]it was out of the question that she should have seen Colonel Osborne.

[068-100]Nevertheless she seemed to think it quite natural that he should have

[068-101]called, and defended him with some energy when her aunt declared that

[068-102]he had been much to blame. "He is not bound to obey Mr. Trevelyan

[068-103]because I am," said Emily.

[068-104]"He is bound to abstain from evil doing," said Mrs. Outhouse; "and he

[068-105]oughtn't to have come. There; let that be enough, my dear. Your uncle

[068-106]doesn't wish to have it talked about." Nevertheless it was talked

[068-107]about between the two sisters. Nora was of opinion that Colonel

[068-108]Osborne had been wrong, whereas Emily defended him. "It seems to me

[068-109]to have been the most natural thing in life," said she.

[068-110]Had Colonel Osborne made the visit as Sir Marmaduke's friend, feeling

[068-111]himself to be an old man, it might have been natural. When a man has

[068-112]come to regard himself as being, on the score of age, about as fit to

[068-113]be a young lady's lover as though he were an old woman instead of an

[068-114]old man,--which some men will do when they are younger even than was

[068-115]Colonel Osborne,--he is justified in throwing behind him as utterly

[068-116]absurd the suspicions of other people. But Colonel Osborne cannot be

[068-117]defended altogether on that plea.

[068-118]CHAPTER XLII.

[068-119]MISS STANBURY AND MR. GIBSON BECOME TWO.

[068-120]There came to be a very gloomy fortnight at Miss Stanbury's house

[068-121]in the Close. For two or three days after Mr. Gibson's dismissal at

[068-122]the hands of Miss Stanbury herself, Brooke Burgess was still in the

[068-123]house, and his presence saved Dorothy from the full weight of her

[068-124]aunt's displeasure. There was the necessity of looking after Brooke,

[068-125]and scolding him, and of praising him to Martha, and of dispraising

[068-126]him, and of seeing that he had enough to eat, and of watching whether

[068-127]he smoked in the house, and of quarrelling with him about everything

[068-128]under the sun, which together so employed Miss Stanbury that she

[068-129]satisfied herself with glances at Dorothy which were felt to be full

[068-130]of charges of ingratitude. Dorothy was thankful that it should be so,

[068-131]and bore the glances with abject submission. And then there was a

[068-132]great comfort to her in Brooke's friendship. On the second day after

[068-133]Mr. Gibson had gone she found herself talking to Brooke quite openly

[068-134]upon the subject. "The fact was, Mr. Burgess, that I didn't really

[068-135]care for him. I know he's very good and all that, and of course Aunt

[068-136]Stanbury meant it all for the best. And I would have done it if I

[068-137]could, but I couldn't." Brooke patted her on the back,--not in the

[068-138]flesh but in the spirit,--and told her that she was quite right. And

[068-139]he expressed an opinion too that it was not expedient to yield too

[068-140]much to Aunt Stanbury. "I would yield to her in anything that was

[068-141]possible to me," said Dorothy. "I won't," said he; "and I don't think

[068-142]I should do any good if I did. I like her, and I like her money. But

[068-143]I don't like either well enough to sell myself for a price."

[068-144]A great part too of the quarrelling which went on from day to day

[068-145]between Brooke and Miss Stanbury was due to the difference of their

[068-146]opinions respecting Dorothy and her suitor. "I believe you put her up

[068-147]to it," said Aunt Stanbury.

[068-148]"I neither put her up nor down, but I think that she was quite

[068-149]right."

[068-150]"You've robbed her of a husband, and she'll never have another

[068-151]chance. After what you've done, you ought to take her yourself."

[068-152]"I shall be ready to-morrow," said Brooke.

[068-153]"How can you tell such a lie?" said Aunt Stanbury.

[068-154]But after two or three days Brooke was gone to make a journey through

[068-155]the distant part of the county, and see the beauties of Devonshire.

[068-156]He was to be away for a fortnight, and then come back for a day or

[068-157]two before he returned to London. During that fortnight things did

[068-158]not go well with poor Dorothy at [[exeter|Exeter]].

[068-159]"I suppose you know your own business best," her aunt said to her

[068-160]one morning. Dorothy uttered no word of reply. She felt it to be

[068-161]equally impossible to suggest either that she did or that she did

[068-162]not know her own business best. "There may be reasons which I don't

[068-163]understand," exclaimed Aunt Stanbury; "but I should like to know what

[068-164]it is you expect."

[068-165]"Why should I expect anything, Aunt Stanbury?"

[068-166]"That's nonsense. Everybody expects something. You expect to have

[068-167]your dinner by-and-by,--don't you?"

[068-168]"I suppose I shall," said Dorothy, to whom it occurred at the moment

[068-169]that such expectation was justified by the fact that on every day of

[068-170]her life hitherto some sort of a dinner had come in her way.

[068-171]"Yes,--and you think it comes from heaven, I suppose."

[068-172]"It comes by God's [[virtue|goodness]] and your bounty, Aunt Stanbury."

[068-173]"And how will it come when I'm dead? Or how will it come if things

[068-174]should go in such a way that I can't stay here any longer? You don't

[068-175]ever think of that."

[068-176]"I should go back to mamma, and Priscilla."

[068-177]"Psha! As if two mouths were not enough to eat all the meal there is

[068-178]in that tub. If there was a word to say against the man, I wouldn't

[068-179]ask you to have him; if he drank, or smoked, or wasn't a gentleman,

[068-180]or was too poor, or anything you like. But there's nothing. It's all

[068-181]very well to tell me you don't love him, but why don't you love him?

[068-182]I don't like a girl to go and throw herself at a man's head, as those

[068-183]Frenches have done; but when everything has been prepared for you

[068-184]and made proper, it seems to me to be like turning away from good

[068-185]victuals." Dorothy could only offer to go home if she had offended

[068-186]her aunt, and then Miss Stanbury scolded her for making the offer. As

[068-187]this kind of thing went on at the house in the Close for a fortnight,

[068-188]during which there was no going out, and no society at home, Dorothy

[068-189]began to be rather tired of it.
