## Section 30

[030-001]But on the second day she herself accosted Priscilla Stanbury. "Come

[030-002]into the garden," she said, when they two were for a moment alone

[030-003]together; "I want to speak to you." Priscilla, without answering,

[030-004]folded up her work and put on her hat. "Come down to the green walk,"

[030-005]said Nora. "I was savage to you last night, and I want to beg your

[030-006]pardon."

[030-007]"You were savage," said Priscilla, smiling, "and you shall have my

[030-008]pardon. Who would not pardon you any offence, if you asked it?"

[030-009]"I am so miserable!" she said.

[030-010]"But why?"

[030-011]"I don't know. I can't tell. And it is of no use talking about it

[030-012]now, for it is all over. But I ought not to have been cross to you,

[030-013]and I am very sorry."

[030-014]"That does not signify a straw; only so far, that when I have been

[030-015]cross, and have begged a person's pardon,--which I don't do as often

[030-016]as I ought,--I always feel that it begets kindness. If I could help

[030-017]you in your trouble I would."

[030-018]"You can't fetch him back again."

[030-019]"You mean Mr. Glascock. Shall I go and try?"

[030-020]Nora smiled and shook her head. "I wonder what he would say if you

[030-021]asked him. But if he came I should do the same thing."

[030-022]"I do not in the least know what you have done, my dear. I only see

[030-023]that you mope about, and are more down in the mouth than any one

[030-024]ought to be, unless some great trouble has come."

[030-025]"A great trouble has come."

[030-026]"I suppose you have had your choice,--either to accept your lover or

[030-027]to reject him."

[030-028]"No; I have not had my choice."

[030-029]"It seems to me that no one has dictated to you; or, at least, that

[030-030]you have obeyed no dictation."

[030-031]"Of course, I can't explain it to you. It is impossible that I

[030-032]should."

[030-033]"If you mean that you regret what you have done because you have been

[030-034]false to the man, I can sympathise with you. No one has ever a right

[030-035]to be false, and if you are repenting a falsehood, I will willingly

[030-036]help you to eat your ashes and to wear your sackcloth. But if you are

[030-037]repenting a truth--"

[030-038]"I am."

[030-039]"Then you must eat your ashes by yourself, for me; and I do not think

[030-040]that you will ever be able to digest them."

[030-041]"I do not want anybody to help me," said Nora proudly.

[030-042]"Nobody can help you, if I understand the matter rightly. You have

[030-043]got to get the better of your own covetousness and evil desires, and

[030-044]you are in the fair way to get the better of them if you have already

[030-045]refused to be this man's wife because you could not bring yourself to

[030-046]commit the sin of marrying him when you did not love him. I suppose

[030-047]that is about the truth of it; and indeed, indeed, I do sympathise

[030-048]with you. If you have done that, though it is no more than the

[030-049]plainest duty, I will love you for it. One finds so few people that

[030-050]will do any duty that taxes their self-indulgence."

[030-051]"But he did not ask me to marry him."

[030-052]"Then I do not understand anything about it."

[030-053]"He asked me to love him."

[030-054]"But he meant you to be his wife?"

[030-055]"Oh yes;--he meant that of course."

[030-056]"And what did you say?" asked Priscilla.

[030-057]"That I didn't love him," replied Nora.

[030-058]"And that was the truth?"

[030-059]"Yes;--it was the truth."

[030-060]"And what do you regret?--that you didn't tell him a lie?"

[030-061]"No;--not that," said Nora slowly.

[030-062]"What then? You cannot regret that you have not basely deceived a man

[030-063]who has treated you with a loving generosity?" They walked on silent

[030-064]for a few yards, and then Priscilla repeated her question. "You

[030-065]cannot mean that you are sorry that you did not persuade yourself to

[030-066]do evil?"

[030-067]"I don't want to go back to the islands, and to lose myself there,

[030-068]and to be nobody;--that is what I mean. And I might have been so

[030-069]much! Could one step from the very highest rung of the ladder to the

[030-070]very lowest and not feel it?"

[030-071]"But you have gone up the ladder,--if you only knew it," said

[030-072]Priscilla. "There was a choice given to you between the foulest mire

[030-073]of the clay of the world, and the sun-light of the very God. You have

[030-074]chosen the sun-light, and you are crying after the clay! I cannot

[030-075]pity you; but I can esteem you, and love you, and believe in you. And

[030-076]I do. You'll get yourself right at last, and there's my hand on it,

[030-077]if you'll take it." Nora took the hand that was offered to her, held

[030-078]it in her own for some seconds, and then walked back to the house and

[030-079]up to her own room in silence.

[030-080]The post used to come into [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] at about eight in the

[030-081]morning, carried thither by a wooden-legged man who rode a donkey.

[030-082]There is a general understanding that the wooden-legged men in

[030-083]country parishes should be employed as postmen, owing to the great

[030-084]steadiness of demeanour which a wooden leg is generally found to

[030-085]produce. It may be that such men are slower in their operations than

[030-086]would be biped postmen; but as all private employers of labour demand

[030-087]labourers with two legs, it is well that the lame and halt should

[030-088]find a refuge in the less exacting service of the government. The

[030-089]one-legged man who rode his donkey into Nuncombe Putney would reach

[030-090]his post-office not above half an hour after his proper time; but he

[030-091]was very slow in stumping round the village, and seldom reached the

[030-092][[clock-house|Clock House]] much before ten. On a certain morning two or three days

[030-093]after the conversation just recorded it was past ten when he brought

[030-094]two letters to the door, one for Mrs. Trevelyan, and one for Mrs.

[030-095]Stanbury. The ladies had finished their breakfast, and were seated

[030-096]together at an open window. As was usual, the letters were given into

[030-097]Priscilla's hands, and the newspaper which accompanied them into

[030-098]those of Mrs. Trevelyan, its undoubted owner. When her letter was

[030-099]handed to her, she looked at the address closely and then walked away

[030-100]with it into her own room.

[030-101]"I think it's from Louis," said Nora, as soon as the door was closed.

[030-102]"If so, he is telling her to come back."

[030-103]"Mamma, this is for you," said Priscilla. "It is from Aunt Stanbury.

[030-104]I know her handwriting."

[030-105]"From your aunt? What can she be writing about? There is something

[030-106]wrong with Dorothy." Mrs. Stanbury held the letter but did not open

[030-107]it. "You had better read it, my dear. If she is ill, pray let her

[030-108]come home."

[030-109]But the letter spoke of nothing amiss as regarded Dorothy, and did

[030-110]not indeed even mention Dorothy's name. Luckily Priscilla read

[030-111]the letter in silence, for it was an angry letter. "What is it,

[030-112]Priscilla? Why don't you tell me? Is anything wrong?" said Mrs.

[030-113]Stanbury.

[030-114]"Nothing is wrong, mamma,--except that my aunt is a silly woman."

[030-115]"Goodness me! what is it?"

[030-116]"It is a family matter," said Nora smiling, "and I will go."

[030-117]"What can it be?" demanded Mrs. Stanbury again as soon as Nora had

[030-118]left the room.

[030-119]"You shall hear what it can be. I will read it you," said Priscilla.

[030-120]"It seems to me that of all the women that ever lived my Aunt

[030-121]Stanbury is the most prejudiced, the most unjust, and the most given

[030-122]to evil thinking of her neighbours. This is what she has thought fit

[030-123]to write to you, mamma." Then Priscilla read her aunt's letter, which

[030-124]was as follows:--

[030-125]The Close, [[exeter|Exeter]], July 31, 186--.

[030-126]DEAR SISTER STANBURY,

[030-127]I am informed that the lady who is living with you because

[030-128]she could not continue to live under the same roof with

[030-129]her lawful husband, has received a visit at your house

[030-130]from a [[social-class|gentleman]] who was named as her lover before she

[030-131]left her own. I am given to understand that it was because

[030-132]of this gentleman's visits to her in [[london|London]], and because

[030-133]she would not give up seeing him, that her husband would

[030-134]not live with her any longer.

[030-135]"But the man has never been here at all," said Mrs. Stanbury, in

[030-136]dismay.

[030-137]"Of course he has not been here. But let me go on."

[030-138]I have got nothing to do with your visitors, [continued

[030-139]the letter] and I should not interfere but for the credit

[030-140]of the family. There ought to be somebody to explain to

[030-141]you that much of the abominable disgrace of the whole

[030-142]proceeding will rest upon you, if you permit such goings

[030-143]on in your house. I suppose it is your house. At any rate

[030-144]you are regarded as the mistress of the establishment, and

[030-145]it is for you to tell the lady that she must go elsewhere.

[030-146]I do hope that you have done so, or at least that you

[030-147]will do so now. It is intolerable that the widow of my

[030-148]brother,--a clergyman,--should harbour a lady who is

[030-149]separated from her husband and who receives visits from

[030-150]a gentleman who is reputed to be her lover. I wonder

[030-151]much that your eldest daughter should countenance such a

[030-152]proceeding.

[030-153]Yours truly,

[030-154]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[030-155]Mrs. Stanbury, when the letter had been read to her, held up both her

[030-156]hands in despair. "Dear, dear," she exclaimed. "Oh, dear!"

[030-157]"She had such pleasure in writing it," said Priscilla, "that one

[030-158]ought hardly to begrudge it her." The blackest spot in the [[honour|character]]

[030-159]of Priscilla Stanbury was her hatred for her aunt in Exeter. She knew

[030-160]that her aunt had high qualities, and yet she hated her aunt. She was

[030-161]well aware that her aunt was regarded as a shining light by very many

[030-162]good people in the county, and yet she hated her aunt. She could not

[030-163]but acknowledge that her aunt had been generous to her brother, and

[030-164]was now very generous to her sister, and yet she hated her aunt. It

[030-165]was now a triumph to her that her aunt had fallen into so terrible

[030-166]a quagmire, and she was by no means disposed to let the sinning old

[030-167]woman easily out of it.

[030-168]"It is as pretty a specimen," she said, "as I ever knew of malice and

[030-169]eaves-dropping combined."

[030-170]"Don't use such hard words, my dear."

[030-171]"Look at her words to us," said Priscilla. "What business has she to

[030-172]talk to you about the credit of the family and abominable disgrace?

[030-173]You have held your head up in poverty, while she has been rolling in

[030-174]money."

[030-175]"She has been very good to Hugh,--and now to Dorothy."

[030-176]"If I were Dorothy I would have none of her [[virtue|goodness]]. She likes some

[030-177]one to trample on,--some one of the name to patronise. She shan't

[030-178]trample on you and me, mamma."

[030-179]Then there was a discussion as to what should be done; or rather

[030-180]a discourse in which Priscilla explained what she thought fit to

[030-181]do. Nothing, she decided, should be said to Mrs. Trevelyan on the

[030-182]subject; but an answer should be sent to Aunt Stanbury. Priscilla

[030-183]herself would write this answer, and herself would sign it. There was

[030-184]some difference of opinion on this point, as Mrs. Stanbury thought

[030-185]that if she might be allowed to put her name to it, even though

[030-186]Priscilla should write it, the wording of it would be made, in some

[030-187]degree, mild,--to suit her own [[reputation|character]]. But her daughter was

[030-188]imperative, and she gave way.

[030-189]"It shall be mild enough in words," said Priscilla, "and very short."

[030-190]Then she wrote her letter as follows:--

[030-191]Nuncombe Putney, August 1, 186--.

[030-192]DEAR AUNT STANBURY,

[030-193]You have found a mare's nest. The gentleman you speak of

[030-194]has never been here at all, and the people who bring you

[030-195]news have probably hoaxed you. I don't think that mamma

[030-196]has ever disgraced the family, and you can have no reason

[030-197]for thinking that she ever will. You should, at any rate,

[030-198]be sure of what you are saying before you make such cruel

[030-199]accusations.
