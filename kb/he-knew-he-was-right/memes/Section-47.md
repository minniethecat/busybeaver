## Section 47

[047-001]"I have a letter from my husband," she said,--and then paused. But

[047-002]Priscilla, seeing from the fire in her eyes that she was much moved,

[047-003]made no reply, but turned to listen to what might further be said. "I

[047-004]do not know why I should trouble you with his suspicions," continued

[047-005]Mrs. Trevelyan, "or read to you what he says about--[[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]."

[047-006]As she spoke she was holding her husband's letter open in her

[047-007]hands. "There is nothing in it that you do not know. He says

[047-008]I have corresponded with him. So I have;--and he shall see the

[047-009]correspondence. He says that Colonel Osborne visited me. He did come

[047-010]to see me and Nora."

[047-011]"As any other old man might have done," said Nora.

[047-012]"It was not likely that I should openly confess myself to be afraid

[047-013]to see my father's old friend. But the truth is, my husband does not

[047-014]know what a woman is."

[047-015]She had begun by declaring that she would not trouble her friend with

[047-016]any statement of her husband's complaints against her; but now she

[047-017]had made her way to the subject, and could hardly refrain herself.

[047-018]Priscilla understood this, and thought that it would be wise to

[047-019]interrupt her by a word that might bring her back to her original

[047-020]purpose. "Is there anything," said she, "which we can do to help

[047-021]you?"

[047-022]"To help me? No;--God only can help me. But Louis informs me that I

[047-023]am to be turned out of this house, because you demand that we should

[047-024]go."

[047-025]"Who says that?" exclaimed Mrs. Stanbury.

[047-026]"My husband. Listen; this is what he says:--'I am greatly grieved to

[047-027]hear from my friend Mr. Stanbury that your conduct in reference to

[047-028]Colonel Osborne has been such as to make it necessary that you should

[047-029]leave Mrs. Stanbury's house.' Is that true? Is that true?" In her

[047-030]general mode of carrying herself, and of enduring the troubles of

[047-031]her life, Mrs. Trevelyan was a strong woman; but now her grief was

[047-032]too much for her, and she burst out into tears. "I am the most

[047-033]unfortunate woman that ever was born!" she sobbed out through her

[047-034]tears.

[047-035]"I never said that you were to go," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[047-036]"But your son has told Mr. Trevelyan that we must go," said Nora,

[047-037]who felt that her sense of injury against [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] was greatly

[047-038]increased by what had taken place. To her mind he was the person most

[047-039]important in the matter. Why had he desired that they should be sent

[047-040]away from the [[clock-house|Clock House]]? She was very angry with him, and declared

[047-041]to herself that she hated him with all her heart. For this man she

[047-042]had sent away that other lover,--a lover who had really loved her!

[047-043]And she had even confessed that it was so!

[047-044]"There is a misunderstanding about this," said Priscilla.

[047-045]"It must be with your brother, then," said Nora.

[047-046]"I think not," said Priscilla. "I think that it has been with Mr.

[047-047]Trevelyan." Then she went on to explain, with much difficulty,

[047-048]but still with a slow distinctness that was peculiar to her, what

[047-049]had really taken place. "We have endeavoured," she said, "to show

[047-050]you,--my mother and I,--that we have not misjudged you; but it

[047-051]is certainly true that I told my brother that I did not think

[047-052]the arrangement a good one,--quite as a permanence." It was very

[047-053]difficult, and her cheeks were red as she spoke, and her lips

[047-054]faltered. It was an exquisite pain to her to have to give the pain

[047-055]which her words would convey; but there was no help for it,--as she

[047-056]said to herself more than once at the time,--there was nothing to be

[047-057]done but to tell the truth.

[047-058]"I never said so," blurted out Mrs. Stanbury, with her usual

[047-059]weakness.

[047-060]"No, mother. It was my saying. In discussing what was best for us

[047-061]all, with Hugh, I told him,--what I have just now explained."

[047-062]"Then of course we must go," said Mrs. Trevelyan, who had gulped down

[047-063]her sobs and was resolved to be firm,--to give way to no more tears,

[047-064]to bear all without sign of womanly weakness.

[047-065]"You will stay with us till your father comes," said Priscilla.

[047-066]"Of course you will," said Mrs. Stanbury,--"you and Nora. We have got

[047-067]to be such friends, now."

[047-068]"No," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "As to friendship for me, it is out of

[047-069]the question. We must pack up, Nora, and go somewhere. Heaven knows

[047-070]where!"

[047-071]Nora was now sobbing. "Why your brother--should want to turn us

[047-072]out,--after he has sent us here--!"

[047-073]"My brother wants nothing of the kind," said Priscilla. "Your sister

[047-074]has no better friend than my brother."

[047-075]"It will be better, Nora, to discuss the matter no further," said

[047-076]Mrs. Trevelyan. "We must go away,--somewhere; and the sooner the

[047-077]better. To be an unwelcome guest is always bad; but to be unwelcome

[047-078]for such a reason as this is terrible."

[047-079]"There is no reason," said Mrs. Stanbury; "indeed there is none."

[047-080]"Mrs. Trevelyan will understand us better when she is less excited,"

[047-081]said Priscilla. "I am not surprised that she should be indignant now.

[047-082]I can only say again that we hope you will stay with us till Sir

[047-083]Marmaduke Rowley shall be in England."

[047-084]"That is not what your brother means," said Nora.

[047-085]"Nor is it what I mean," said Mrs. Trevelyan. "Nora, we had better

[047-086]go to our own room. I suppose I must write to my husband; indeed,

[047-087]of course I must, that I may send him--the correspondence. I fear

[047-088]I cannot walk out into the street, Mrs. Stanbury, and make you quit

[047-089]of me, till I hear from him. And if I were to go to an inn at once,

[047-090]people would speak evil of me;--and I have no money."

[047-091]"My dear, how can you think of such a thing!" said Mrs. Stanbury.

[047-092]"But you may be quite sure that we shall be gone within three

[047-093]days,--or four at the furthest. Indeed, I will pledge myself not to

[047-094]remain longer than that,--even though I should have to go to the

[047-095]poor-house. Neither I nor my sister will stay in any family,--to

[047-096]contaminate it. Come, Nora." And so speaking she sailed out of the

[047-097]room, and her sister followed her.

[047-098]"Why did you say anything about it? Oh dear, oh dear! why did you

[047-099]speak to Hugh? See what you have done!"

[047-100]"I am sorry that I did speak," replied Priscilla slowly.

[047-101]"Sorry! Of course you are sorry; but what good is that?"

[047-102]"But, mother, I do not think that I was wrong. I feel sure that the

[047-103]real fault in all this is with Mr. Trevelyan, as it has been all

[047-104]through. He should not have written to her as he has done."

[047-105]"I suppose Hugh did tell him."

[047-106]"No doubt;--and I told Hugh; but not after the fashion in which he

[047-107]has told her. I blame myself mostly for this,--that we ever consented

[047-108]to come to this house. We had no business here. Who is to pay the

[047-109]rent?"

[047-110]"Hugh insisted upon taking it."

[047-111]"Yes;--and he will pay the rent; and we shall be a drag upon him, as

[047-112]though he had been fool enough to have a wife and a family of his

[047-113]own. And what good have we done? We had not strength enough to say

[047-114]that that wicked man should not see her when he came;--for he is a

[047-115]wicked man."

[047-116]"If we had done that she would have been as bad then as she is now."

[047-117]"Mother, we had no business to meddle either with her badness or

[047-118]her [[virtue|goodness]]. What had we to do with the wife of such a one as Mr.

[047-119]Trevelyan, or with any woman who was separated from her husband?"

[047-120]"It was Hugh who thought we should be of service to them."

[047-121]"Yes;--and I do not blame him. He is in a position to be of service

[047-122]to people. He can do work and earn money, and has a right to think

[047-123]and to speak. We have a right to think only for ourselves, and we

[047-124]should not have yielded to him. How are we to get back again out of

[047-125]this house to our cottage?"

[047-126]"They are pulling the cottage down, Priscilla."

[047-127]"To some other cottage, mother. Do you not feel while we are living

[047-128]here that we are pretending to be what we are not? After all, Aunt

[047-129]Stanbury was right, though it was not her business to meddle with us.

[047-130]We should never have come here. That poor woman now regards us as her

[047-131]bitter enemies."

[047-132]"I meant to do for the best," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[047-133]"The fault was mine, mother."

[047-134]"But you meant it for the best, my dear."

[047-135]"Meaning for the best is trash. I don't know that I did mean it for

[047-136]the best. While we were at the cottage we paid our way and were

[047-137]honest. What is it people say of us now?"

[047-138]"They can't say any harm."

[047-139]"They say that we are paid by the husband to keep his wife, and paid

[047-140]again by the lover to betray the husband."

[047-141]"Priscilla!"

[047-142]"Yes;--it is shocking enough. But that comes of people going out

[047-143]of their proper course. We were too humble and low to have a right

[047-144]to take any part in such a matter. How true it is that while one

[047-145]crouches on the ground, one can never fall."

[047-146]The matter was discussed in the Clock House all day, between Mrs.

[047-147]Stanbury and Priscilla, and between Mrs. Trevelyan and Nora, in their

[047-148]rooms and in the garden; but nothing could come of such discussions.

[047-149]No change could be made till further instructions should have been

[047-150]received from the angry husband; nor could any kind of argument be

[047-151]even invented by Priscilla which might be efficacious in inducing the

[047-152]two ladies to remain at the Clock House, even should Mr. Trevelyan

[047-153]allow them to do so. They all felt the intolerable injustice, as

[047-154]it appeared to them,--of their subjection to the caprice of an

[047-155]unreasonable and ill-conditioned man; but to all of them it seemed

[047-156]plain enough that in this matter the husband must exercise his own

[047-157]will,--at any rate till Sir Marmaduke should be in England. There

[047-158]were many difficulties throughout the day. Mrs. Trevelyan would not

[047-159]go down to dinner, sending word that she was ill, and that she would,

[047-160]if she were allowed, have some tea in her own room. And Nora said

[047-161]that she would remain with her sister. Priscilla went to them more

[047-162]than once; and late in the evening they all met in the parlour. But

[047-163]any conversation seemed to be impossible; and Mrs. Trevelyan, as she

[047-164]went up to her room at night, again declared that she would rid the

[047-165]house of her presence as soon as possible.

[047-166]One thing, however, was done on that melancholy day. Mrs. Trevelyan

[047-167]wrote to her husband, and enclosed Colonel Osborne's letter to

[047-168]herself, and a copy of her reply. The reader will hardly require to

[047-169]be told that no such further letter had been written by her as that

[047-170]of which Bozzle had given information to her husband. Men whose

[047-171]business it is to detect hidden and secret things, are very apt to

[047-172]detect things which have never been done. What excuse can a detective

[047-173]make even to himself for his own existence if he can detect nothing?

[047-174]Mr. Bozzle was an active-minded man, who gloried in detecting, and

[047-175]who, in the special spirit of his trade, had taught himself to

[047-176]believe that all around him were things secret and hidden, which

[047-177]would be within his power of unravelling if only the slightest clue

[047-178]were put in his hand. He lived by the crookednesses of people, and

[047-179]therefore was convinced that straight doings in the world were quite

[047-180]exceptional. Things dark and dishonest, fights fought and races run

[047-181]that they might be lost, plants and crosses, women false to their

[047-182]husbands, sons false to their fathers, daughters to their mothers,

[047-183]servants to their masters, affairs always secret, dark, foul, and

[047-184]fraudulent, were to him the normal condition of life. It was to be

[047-185]presumed that Mrs. Trevelyan should continue to correspond with her

[047-186]lover,--that old Mrs. Stanbury should betray her trust by conniving

[047-187]at the lover's visit,--that everybody concerned should be steeped to

[047-188]the hips in lies and iniquity. When, therefore, he found at Colonel

[047-189]Osborne's rooms that the Colonel had received a letter with the

[047-190]Lessboro' post-mark, addressed in the handwriting of a woman, he

[047-191]did not scruple to declare that Colonel Osborne had received, on

[047-192]that morning, a letter from Mr. Trevelyan's "lady." But in sending

[047-193]to her husband what she called with so much bitterness, "the

[047-194]correspondence," Mrs. Trevelyan had to enclose simply the copy of one

[047-195]sheet note from herself.
