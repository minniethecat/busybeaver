## Section 74

[074-001]Trevelyan, when he had read as far as this, threw down the letter and

[074-002]tore his hair in despair. "My wife," he exclaimed, "Oh, my wife!" But

[074-003]it was essential that he should read Bozzle's letter, and he

[074-004]persevered.

[074-005]Well; I took to the ground myself as soon as ever I heard

[074-006]that the Colonel was among us, and I hung out at the Full

[074-007]Moon. They had been quite on the square with me at the

[074-008]Full Moon, which I mention, because, of course, it has

[074-009]to be remembered, and it do come up as a hitem. And I'm

[074-010]proud, Mr. Trewilyan, as I did take to the ground myself;

[074-011]for what should happen but I see the Colonel as large as

[074-012]life ringing at the parson's bell at 1.47 p.m. He was let

[074-013]in at 1.49, and he was let out at 2.17. He went away in a

[074-014]cab which it was kept, and I followed him till he was put

[074-015]down at the Arcade, and I left him having his 'ed washed

[074-016]and greased at Trufitt's rooms, half-way up. It was a

[074-017]wonder to me when I see this, Mr. Trewilyan, as he didn't

[074-018]have his 'ed done first, as they most of 'em does when

[074-019]they're going to see their ladies; but I couldn't make

[074-020]nothing of that, though I did try to put too and too

[074-021]together, as I always does.

[074-022]What he did at the parson's, Mr. Trewilyan, I won't

[074-023]say I saw, and I won't say I know. It's my opinion the

[074-024]young woman there isn't on the square, though she's

[074-025]been remembered too, and is a hitem of course. And, Mr.

[074-026]Trewilyan, it do go against the grain with me when they're

[074-027]remembered and ain't on the square. I doesn't expect too

[074-028]much of Human Nature, which is poor, as the saying goes;

[074-029]but when they're remembered and ain't on the square after

[074-030]that, it's too bad for Human Nature. It's more than poor.

[074-031]It's what I calls beggarly.

[074-032]He ain't been there since, Mr. Trewilyan, and he goes out

[074-033]of town to-morrow by the 1.15 p.m. express to Bridport. So

[074-034]he lets on; but of course I shall see to that. That he's

[074-035]been at St. Diddulph's, in the house from 1.47 to 2.17,

[074-036]you may take as a fact. There won't be no shaking of that,

[074-037]because I have it in my mem. book, and no Counsel can get

[074-038]the better of it. Of course he went there to see her, and

[074-039]it's my belief he did. The young woman as was remembered

[074-040]says he didn't, but she isn't on the square. They never is

[074-041]when a lady wants to see her [[social-class|gentleman]], though they comes

[074-042]round afterwards, and tells up everything when it comes

[074-043]before his ordinary lordship.

[074-044]If you ask me, Mr. Trewilyan, I don't think it's ripe yet

[074-045]for the court, but we'll have it ripe before long. I'll

[074-046]keep a look-out, because it's just possible she may leave

[074-047]town. If she do, I'll be down upon them together, and no

[074-048]mistake.

[074-049]Yours most respectful,

[074-050]S. BOZZLE.

[074-051]Every word in the letter had been a dagger to Trevelyan, and yet he

[074-052]felt himself to be under an [[duty|obligation]] to the man who had written

[074-053]it. No one else would or could make facts known to him. If she were

[074-054]innocent, let him know that she were innocent, and he would proclaim

[074-055]her innocence, and believe in her innocence,--and sacrifice himself

[074-056]to her innocence, if such sacrifice were necessary. But if she were

[074-057]guilty, let him also know that. He knew how bad it was, all that

[074-058]bribing of postmen and maidservants, who took his money, and her

[074-059]money also, very likely. It was dirt, all of it. But who had put him

[074-060]into the dirt? His wife had, at least, deceived him,--had deceived

[074-061]him and disobeyed him, and it was necessary that he should know the

[074-062]facts. Life without a Bozzle would now have been to him a perfect

[074-063]blank.

[074-064]The Colonel had been to the parsonage at St. Diddulph's, and had

[074-065]been admitted! As to that he had no doubt. Nor did he really doubt

[074-066]that his wife had seen the visitor. He had sent his wife first

[074-067]into a remote village on Dartmoor, and there she had been visited

[074-068]by her--lover! How was he to use any other word? Iago;--oh, Iago!

[074-069]The pity of it, Iago! Then, when she had learned that this was

[074-070]discovered, she had left the retreat in which he had placed

[074-071]her,--without permission from him,--and had taken herself to

[074-072]the house of a relative of hers. Here she was visited again by

[074-073]her--lover! Oh, Iago; the pity of it, Iago! And then there had been

[074-074]between them an almost constant correspondence. So much he had

[074-075]ascertained as fact; but he did not for a moment believe that Bozzle

[074-076]had learned all the facts. There might be correspondence, or even

[074-077]visits, of which Bozzle could learn nothing. How could Bozzle know

[074-078]where Mrs. Trevelyan was during all those hours which [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]

[074-079]passed in [[london|London]]? That which he knew, he knew absolutely, and on

[074-080]that he could act; but there was, of course, much of which he knew

[074-081]nothing. Gradually the truth would unveil itself, and then he would

[074-082]act. He would tear that Colonel into fragments, and throw his wife

[074-083]from him with all the ignominy which the law made possible to him.

[074-084]But in the meantime he wrote a letter to Mr. Outhouse. Colonel

[074-085]Osborne, after all that had been said, had been admitted at the

[074-086]parsonage, and Trevelyan was determined to let the clergyman know

[074-087]what he thought about it. The oftener he turned the matter in his

[074-088]mind, as he walked slowly up and down the piazza of St. Mark, the

[074-089]more absurd it appeared to him to doubt that his wife had seen the

[074-090]man. Of course she had seen him. He walked there nearly the whole

[074-091]night, thinking of it, and as he dragged himself off at last to his

[074-092]inn, had almost come to have but one desire,--namely, that he should

[074-093]find her out, that the [[truth|evidence]] should be conclusive, that it should

[074-094]be proved, and so brought to an end. Then he would destroy her, and

[074-095]destroy that man,--and afterwards destroy himself, so bitter to him

[074-096]would be his ignominy. He almost revelled in the idea of the tragedy

[074-097]he would make. It was three o'clock before he was in his bedroom, and

[074-098]then he wrote his letter to Mr. Outhouse before he took himself to

[074-099]his bed. It was as follows:--

[074-100]Venice, Oct. 4, 186--.

[074-101]SIR,

[074-102]Information of a certain kind, on which I can place a

[074-103]firm reliance, has reached me, to the effect that Colonel

[074-104]Osborne has been allowed to visit at your house during

[074-105]the sojourn of my wife under your roof. I will thank you

[074-106]to inform me whether this be true; as, although I am

[074-107]confident of my facts, it is necessary, in reference to my

[074-108]ulterior conduct, that I should have from you either an

[074-109]admission or a denial of my assertion. It is of course

[074-110]open to you to leave my letter unanswered. Should you

[074-111]think proper to do so, I shall know also how to deal with

[074-112]that fact.

[074-113]As to your conduct in admitting Colonel Osborne into your

[074-114]house while my wife is there,--after all that has passed,

[074-115]and all that you know that has passed,--I am quite unable

[074-116]to speak with anything like moderation of feeling. Had the

[074-117]man succeeded in forcing himself into your residence, you

[074-118]should have been the first to give me notice of it. As it

[074-119]is, I have been driven to ascertain the fact from other

[074-120]sources. I think that you have betrayed the trust that a

[074-121]husband has placed in you, and that you will find from the

[074-122]public voice that you will be regarded as having disgraced

[074-123]yourself as a clergyman.

[074-124]In reference to my wife herself, I would wish her to know,

[074-125]that after what has now taken place, I shall not feel

[074-126]myself justified in leaving our child longer in her hands,

[074-127]even tender as are his years. I shall take steps for

[074-128]having him removed. What further I shall do to vindicate

[074-129]myself, and extricate myself as far as may be possible

[074-130]from the slough of despond in which I have been submerged,

[074-131]she and you will learn in due time.

[074-132]Your obedient servant,

[074-133]L. TREVELYAN.

[074-134]A letter addressed "poste restante, Venice," will reach me

[074-135]here.

[074-136]If Trevelyan was mad when he wrote this letter, Mr. Outhouse was

[074-137]very nearly as mad when he read it. He had most strongly desired

[074-138]to have nothing to do with his wife's niece when she was separated

[074-139]from her husband. He was a man honest, charitable, and sufficiently

[074-140]affectionate; but he was timid, and disposed to think ill of those

[074-141]whose modes of life were strange to him. Actuated by these feelings,

[074-142]he would have declined to offer the hospitality of his roof to Mrs.

[074-143]Trevelyan, had any choice been left to him. But there had been no

[074-144]choice. She had come thither unasked, with her boy and baggage, and

[074-145]he could not send her away. His wife had told him that it was his

[074-146]duty to protect these women till their father came, and he recognised

[074-147]the truth of what his wife said. There they were, and there they must

[074-148]remain throughout the winter. It was hard upon him,--especially as

[074-149]the difficulties and embarrassments as to money were so disagreeable

[074-150]to him;--but there was no help for it. His duty must be done though

[074-151]it were ever so painful. Then that horrid Colonel had come. And now

[074-152]had come this letter, in which he was not only accused of being an

[074-153]accomplice between his married niece and her lover, but was also

[074-154]assured that he should be held up to public ignominy and disgrace.

[074-155]Though he had often declared that Trevelyan was mad, he would not

[074-156]remember that now. Such a letter as he had received should have been

[074-157]treated by him as the production of a madman. But he was not sane

[074-158]enough himself to see the matter in that light. He gnashed his teeth,

[074-159]and clenched his fist, and was almost beside himself as he read the

[074-160]letter a second time.

[074-161]There had been a method in Trevelyan's madness; for though he had

[074-162]declared to himself that without doubt Bozzle had been right in

[074-163]saying that as the Colonel had been at the parsonage, therefore, as a

[074-164]certainty, Mrs. Trevelyan had met the Colonel there, yet he had not

[074-165]so stated in his letter. He had merely asserted that Colonel Osborne

[074-166]had been at the house, and had founded his accusation upon that

[074-167]alleged fact. The alleged fact had been in truth a fact. So far

[074-168]Bozzle had been right. The Colonel had been at the parsonage; and the

[074-169]reader knows how far Mr. Outhouse had been to blame for his share

[074-170]in the matter! He rushed off to his wife with the letter, declaring

[074-171]at first that Mrs. Trevelyan, Nora, and the child, and the servant,

[074-172]should be sent out of the house at once. But at last Mrs. Outhouse

[074-173]succeeded in showing him that he would not be justified in ill-using

[074-174]them because Trevelyan had ill-used him. "But I will write to him,"

[074-175]said Mr. Outhouse. "He shall know what I think about it." And he did

[074-176]write his letter that day, in spite of his wife's entreaties that

[074-177]he would allow the sun to set upon his wrath. And his letter was as

[074-178]follows:--

[074-179]St. Diddulph's, October 8, 186--.

[074-180]SIR,

[074-181]I have received your letter of the 4th, which is more

[074-182]iniquitous, unjust, and ungrateful, than anything I ever

[074-183]before saw written. I have been surprised from the first

[074-184]at your gross cruelty to your unoffending wife; but even

[074-185]that seems to me more intelligible than your conduct in

[074-186]writing such words as those which you have dared to send

[074-187]to me.
