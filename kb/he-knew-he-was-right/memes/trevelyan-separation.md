# The Trevelyan Separation

**The Trevelyan Separation** is the pivotal event of [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869), marking the point at which [[Louis Trevelyan]]'s private [[Jealousy|jealousy]] becomes an irreversible public catastrophe. Over the novel's opening chapters, Trevelyan's suspicion of his wife [[Emily Trevelyan|Emily]]'s friendship with [[Colonel Osborne]] escalates from private unease to a formal demand, and when Emily refuses on principle, Trevelyan expels her from their home. The separation — which in Victorian terms effectively ended a marriage without the legal finality of divorce — sets every subsequent plot development in motion and stands as the novel's central dramatic rupture.

## Background

The separation's roots lie in the opening chapters. Trevelyan, initially presented as an ideal husband, becomes increasingly disturbed by the frequent visits of Colonel Osborne, an old family friend of Emily's father. Despite Emily's assurances and Osborne's manifest innocence, Trevelyan's jealousy hardens into obsession:

> Louis Trevelyan having taken a strong dislike to Colonel Osborne, and having failed to make his wife share that dislike, became uneasy. [002-113]

He demands that Emily end the friendship entirely. Emily refuses, seeing the demand as an implicit accusation of impropriety — an insult to her honour that she cannot accept without conceding guilt. The standoff is absolute: each is convinced of their own rightness, and neither will yield.

## The Rupture

The separation occurs in stages rather than as a single dramatic scene:

| Stage | Event | Chapter |
|-------|-------|---------|
| 1 | Osborne's visits begin; Trevelyan's unease grows | 2 |
| 2 | Trevelyan demands Emily end the friendship; she refuses | 3 |
| 3 | Trevelyan escalates: forbids Osborne from the house | 4–5 |
| 4 | Trevelyan orders Emily to leave; she departs for Nuncombe Putney | 6 |
| 5 | Public scandal erupts; friends and family take sides | 7–11 |

## Consequences

The separation's consequences radiate through the entire novel:

- **For the Trevelyans**: The marriage is effectively destroyed. Trevelyan descends into [[Madness|madness]] and eventually death. Emily endures social ostracism and the loss of her son, who is abducted by Trevelyan.
- **For the Stanburys**: Emily's refuge at [[Nuncombe Putney]] draws the Stanbury family into the conflict, catalyzing the [[Nora Rowley|Nora]]–[[Hugh Stanbury|Hugh]] romance.
- **For public opinion**: The separation becomes a London scandal, debated in newspapers and drawing rooms, exposing the cruelty of Victorian social judgment.

## Significance

The Trevelyan Separation is one of Victorian fiction's most nuanced treatments of marital breakdown. Unlike the melodramatic separations of sensation fiction (driven by discovered bigamy, secret pasts, or criminal acts), this separation arises from ordinary human failings — jealousy, pride, stubbornness — rendered catastrophic by the rigid legal and social structures of Victorian marriage. The novel refuses to assign blame simply: Louis is wrong in his jealousy, but Emily's principled intransigence contributes to the tragedy.
