## Section 135

[135-001]"I did not think he had so much spirit in him," was Miss Stanbury's

[135-002]answer. Throughout [[exeter|Exeter]] the great wonder arising from the crisis

[135-003]was the amount of spirit which had been displayed by Mr. Gibson.

[135-004]When he was left alone he shook himself, and began to think that if

[135-005]there were danger that such interviews might occur frequently he had

[135-006]better leave Exeter for good. As he put his hand over his forehead,

[135-007]he declared to himself that a very little more of that kind of thing

[135-008]would kill him. When a couple of hours had passed over his head he

[135-009]shook himself again, and sat down and wrote a letter to his intended

[135-010]mother-in-law.

[135-011]I do not mean to complain [he said], God knows I have

[135-012]no right; but I cannot stand a repetition of what has

[135-013]occurred just now. If your younger daughter comes to see

[135-014]me again I must refuse to see her, and shall leave the

[135-015]town. I am ready to make what reparation may be possible

[135-016]for the mistake into which I have fallen.

[135-017]T. G.

[135-018]Mrs. French was no doubt much afraid of her younger daughter, but

[135-019]she was less afraid of her than were other people. Familiarity, they

[135-020]say, breeds contempt; and who can be so familiar with a child as its

[135-021]parent? She did not in her heart believe that Camilla would murder

[135-022]anybody, and she fully realised the conviction that, even after all

[135-023]that was come and gone, it would be better that one of her daughters

[135-024]should have a husband than that neither should be so blessed. If only

[135-025]Camilla could be got out of Exeter for a few months,--how good a

[135-026]thing it would be for them all! She had a brother in Gloucester,--if

[135-027]only he could be got to take Camilla for a few months! And then, too,

[135-028]she knew that if the true rights of her two daughters were strictly

[135-029]and impartially examined, Arabella's claim was much stronger than any

[135-030]that Camilla could put forward to the hand of Mr. Gibson.

[135-031]"You must not go there again, Camilla," the mother said.

[135-032]"I shall go whenever I please," replied the fury.

[135-033]"Now, Camilla, we may as well understand each other. I will not have

[135-034]it done. If I am provoked, I will send to your uncle at Gloucester."

[135-035]Now the uncle at Gloucester was a timber merchant, a man with

[135-036]protuberant eyes and a great square chin,--known to be a very stern

[135-037]man indeed, and not at all afraid of young women.

[135-038]"What do I care for my uncle? My uncle would take my part."

[135-039]"No, he would not. The truth is, Camilla, you interfered with Bella

[135-040]first."

[135-041]"Mamma, how dare you say so!"

[135-042]"You did, my dear. And these are the consequences."

[135-043]"And you mean to say that she is to be Mrs. Gibson?"

[135-044]"I say nothing about that. But I do not see why they shouldn't be

[135-045]married if their hearts are inclined to each other."

[135-046]"I will die first!"

[135-047]"Your dying has nothing to do with it, Camilla."

[135-048]"And I will kill her!"

[135-049]"If you speak to me again in that way I will write to your uncle at

[135-050]Gloucester. I have done the best I could for you both, and I will not

[135-051]bear such treatment."

[135-052]"And how am I treated?"

[135-053]"You should not have interfered with your sister."

[135-054]"You are all in a conspiracy together," shouted Camilla, "you are!

[135-055]There never was anybody so badly treated,--never,--never,--never!

[135-056]What will everybody say of me?"

[135-057]"They will pity you, if you will be quiet."

[135-058]"I don't want to be pitied;--I won't be pitied. I wish I could

[135-059]die,--and I will die! Anybody else would, at any rate, have had their

[135-060]mother and sister with them!" Then she burst into a flood of real,

[135-061]true, womanly tears.

[135-062]After this there was a lull at Heavitree for a few days. Camilla

[135-063]did not speak to her sister, but she condescended to hold some

[135-064]intercourse with her mother, and to take her meals at the family

[135-065]table. She did not go out of the house, but she employed herself in

[135-066]her own room, doing no one knew what, with all that new clothing and

[135-067]household gear which was to have been transferred in her train to

[135-068]Mr. Gibson's house. Mrs. French was somewhat uneasy about the new

[135-069]clothing and household gear, feeling that, in the event of Bella's

[135-070]marriage, at least a considerable portion of it must be transferred

[135-071]to the new bride. But it was impossible at the present moment to open

[135-072]such a subject to Camilla;--it would have been as a proposition to a

[135-073]lioness respecting the taking away of her whelps. Nevertheless, the

[135-074]day must soon come in which something must be said about the clothing

[135-075]and household gear. All the [[money|property]] that had been sent into the

[135-076]house at Camilla's orders could not be allowed to remain as Camilla's

[135-077]perquisites, now that Camilla was not to be married. "Do you know

[135-078]what she is doing, my dear?" said Mrs. French to her elder daughter.

[135-079]"Perhaps she is picking out the marks," said Bella.

[135-080]"I don't think she would do that as yet," said Mrs. French.

[135-081]"She might just as well leave it alone," said Bella, feeling that one

[135-082]of the two letters would do for her. But neither of them dared to

[135-083]speak to her of her occupation in these first days of her despair.

[135-084]Mr. Gibson in the meantime remained at home, or only left his house

[135-085]to go to the Cathedral or to visit the narrow confines of his little

[135-086]parish. When he was out he felt that everybody looked at him, and it

[135-087]seemed to him that people whispered about him when they saw him at

[135-088]his usual desk in the choir. His friends passed him merely bowing to

[135-089]him, and he was aware that he had done that which would be regarded

[135-090]by every one around him as unpardonable. And yet,--what ought he to

[135-091]have done? He acknowledged to himself that he had been very foolish,

[135-092]mad,--quite demented at the moment,--when he allowed himself to think

[135-093]it possible that he should marry Camilla French. But having found out

[135-094]how mad he had been at that moment, having satisfied himself that to

[135-095]live with her as his wife would be impossible, was he not right to

[135-096]break the engagement? Could anything be so wicked as marrying a woman

[135-097]whom he--hated? Thus he tried to excuse himself; but yet he knew that

[135-098]all the world would condemn him. Life in Exeter would be impossible,

[135-099]if no way to social pardon could be opened for him. He was willing to

[135-100]do anything within bounds in mitigation of his offence. He would give

[135-101]up fifty pounds a year to Camilla for his life,--or he would marry

[135-102]Bella. Yes; he would marry Bella at once,--if Camilla would only

[135-103]consent, and give up that idea of stabbing some one. Bella French

[135-104]was not very nice in his eyes; but she was quiet, he thought, and it

[135-105]might be possible to live with her. Nevertheless, he told himself

[135-106]over and over again that the manner in which unmarried men with

[135-107]incomes were set upon by ladies in want of husbands was very

[135-108]disgraceful to the country at large. That mission to Natal which had

[135-109]once been offered to him would have had charms for him now, of which

[135-110]he had not recognised the force when he rejected it.

[135-111]"Do you think that he ever was really engaged to her?" Dorothy said

[135-112]to her aunt. Dorothy was now living in a seventh heaven of happiness,

[135-113]writing love-letters to Brooke Burgess every other day, and devoting

[135-114]to this occupation a number of hours of which she ought to have

[135-115]been ashamed; making her purchases for her wedding,--with nothing,

[135-116]however, of the magnificence of a Camilla,--but discussing everything

[135-117]with her aunt, who urged her on to extravagances which seemed beyond

[135-118]the scope of her own economical ideas; settling, or trying to settle,

[135-119]little difficulties which perplexed her somewhat, and wondering

[135-120]at her own career. She could not of course be married without the

[135-121]presence of her mother and sister, and her aunt,--with something of

[135-122]a grim courtesy,--had intimated that they should be made welcome to

[135-123]the house in the Close for the special occasion. But nothing had been

[135-124]said about Hugh. The wedding was to be in the Cathedral, and Dorothy

[135-125]had a little scheme in her head for meeting her brother among the

[135-126]aisles. He would no doubt come down with Brooke, and nothing perhaps

[135-127]need be said about it to Aunt Stanbury. But still it was a trouble.

[135-128]Her aunt had been so good that Dorothy felt that no step should be

[135-129]taken which would vex the old woman. It was evident enough that

[135-130]when permission had been given for the visit of Mrs. Stanbury and

[135-131]Priscilla, Hugh's name had been purposely kept back. There had been

[135-132]no accidental omission. Dorothy, therefore, did not dare to mention

[135-133]it,--and yet it was essential for her happiness that he should be

[135-134]there. At the present moment Miss Stanbury's intense interest in the

[135-135]Stanbury wedding was somewhat mitigated by the excitement occasioned

[135-136]by Mr. Gibson's refusal to be married. Dorothy was so shocked that

[135-137]she could not bring herself to believe the statement that had reached

[135-138]them through Martha.

[135-139]"Of course he was engaged to her. We all knew that," said Miss

[135-140]Stanbury.

[135-141]"I think there must have been some mistake," said Dorothy. "I don't

[135-142]see how he could do it."

[135-143]"There is no knowing what people can do, my dear, when they're hard

[135-144]driven. I suppose we shall have a lawsuit now, and he'll have to pay

[135-145]ever so much money. Well, well, well! see what a deal of trouble you

[135-146]might have saved!"

[135-147]"But he'd have done the same to me, aunt;--only, you know, I never

[135-148]could have taken him. Isn't it better as it is, aunt? Tell me."

[135-149]"I suppose young women always think it best when they can get their

[135-150]own ways. An old woman like me has only got to do what she is bid."

[135-151]"But this was best, aunt;--was it not?"

[135-152]"My dear, you've had your way, and let that be enough. Poor Camilla

[135-153]French is not allowed to have hers at all. Dear, dear, dear! I didn't

[135-154]think the man would ever have been such a fool to begin with;--or

[135-155]that he would ever have had the heart to get out of it afterwards."

[135-156]It astonished Dorothy to find that her aunt was not loud in

[135-157]reprobation of Mr. Gibson's very dreadful conduct.

[135-158]In the meantime Mrs. French had written to her brother at Gloucester.

[135-159]The maid-servant, in making Miss Camilla's bed, and in "putting the

[135-160]room to rights," as she called it,--which description probably was

[135-161]intended to cover the circumstances of an accurate search,--had

[135-162]discovered, hidden among some linen,--a carving knife! such a

[135-163]knife as is used for the cutting up of fowls; and, after two days'

[135-164]interval, had imparted the discovery to Mrs. French. Instant visit

[135-165]was made to the pantry, and it was found that a very aged but

[135-166]unbroken and sharply-pointed weapon was missing. Mrs. French at once

[135-167]accused Camilla, and Camilla, after some hesitation, admitted that

[135-168]it might be there. Molly, she said, was a nasty, sly, wicked thing,

[135-169]to go looking in her drawers, and she would never leave anything

[135-170]unlocked again. The knife, she declared, had been taken up-stairs,

[135-171]because she had wanted something very sharp to cut,--the bones of

[135-172]her stays. The knife was given up, but Mrs. French thought it best

[135-173]to write to her brother, Mr. Crump. She was in great doubt about

[135-174]sundry matters. Had the carving knife really pointed to a domestic

[135-175]tragedy;--and if so, what steps ought a poor widow to take with

[135-176]such a daughter? And what ought to be done about Mr. Gibson? It ran

[135-177]through Mrs. French's mind that unless something were done at once,

[135-178]Mr. Gibson would escape scot free. It was her wish that he should yet

[135-179]become her son-in-law. Poor Bella was entitled to her chance. But

[135-180]if Bella was to be disappointed,--from fear of carving knives, or

[135-181]for other reasons,--then there came the question whether Mr. Gibson

[135-182]should not be made to pay in purse for the mischief he had done. With

[135-183]all these thoughts and doubts running through her head, Mrs. French

[135-184]wrote to her brother at Gloucester.
