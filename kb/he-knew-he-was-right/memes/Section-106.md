## Section 106

[106-001]"We know him very well," continued Mrs. Trevelyan. "It was he

[106-002]that arranged our journey down to Devonshire. He was very kind

[106-003]about it, and so were his mother and sister. We have every reason

[106-004]to be grateful to Mr. Stanbury." This was all very well, but

[106-005]Nora nevertheless felt that the interview had been anything but

[106-006]successful.

[106-007]"Has he any profession?" asked Sir Marmaduke.

[106-008]"He writes for the press," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[106-009]"What do you mean;--books?"

[106-010]"No;--for a newspaper."

[106-011]"For a penny newspaper," said Nora boldly--"for the Daily Record."

[106-012]"Then I hope he won't come here any more," said Sir Marmaduke. Nora

[106-013]paused a moment, striving to find words for some speech which might

[106-014]be true to her love and yet not unseemly,--but finding no such words

[106-015]ready, she got up from her seat and walked out of the room. "What is

[106-016]the meaning of it all?" asked Sir Marmaduke. There was a silence for

[106-017]a while, and then he repeated his question in another form. "Is there

[106-018]any reason for his coming here,--about Nora?"

[106-019]"I think he is attached to Nora," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[106-020]"My dear," said Lady Rowley, "perhaps we had better not speak about

[106-021]it just now."

[106-022]"I suppose he has not a penny in the world," said Sir Marmaduke.

[106-023]"He has what he earns," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[106-024]"If Nora understands her duty she will never let me hear his name

[106-025]again," said Sir Marmaduke. Then there was nothing more said, and as

[106-026]soon as they could escape, both Lady Rowley and Mrs. Trevelyan left

[106-027]the room.

[106-028]"I should have told you everything," said Nora to her mother that

[106-029]night. "I had no intention to keep anything a secret from you. But we

[106-030]have all been so unhappy about Louey, that we have had no heart to

[106-031]talk of anything else."

[106-032]"I understand all that, my darling."

[106-033]"And I had meant that you should tell papa, for I supposed that

[106-034]he would come. And I meant that he should go to papa himself. He

[106-035]intended that himself,--only, to-day,--as things turned out--"

[106-036]"Just so, dearest;--but it does not seem that he has got any [[money|income]].

[106-037]It would be very rash,--wouldn't it?"

[106-038]"People must be rash sometimes. Everybody can't have an income

[106-039]without earning it. I suppose people in professions do marry without

[106-040]having fortunes."

[106-041]"When they have settled professions, Nora."

[106-042]"And why is not his a settled profession? I believe he receives quite

[106-043]as much at seven and twenty as Uncle Oliphant does at sixty."

[106-044]"But your Uncle Oliphant's income is permanent."

[106-045]"Lawyers don't have permanent incomes, or doctors,--or merchants."

[106-046]"But those professions are regular and sure. They don't marry,

[106-047]without fortunes, till they have made their incomes sure."

[106-048]"Mr. Stanbury's income is sure. I don't know why it shouldn't be

[106-049]sure. He goes on writing and writing every day, and it seems to me

[106-050]that of all professions in the world it is the finest. I'd much

[106-051]sooner write for a newspaper than be one of those old musty, fusty

[106-052]lawyers, who'll say anything that they're paid to say."

[106-053]"My dearest Nora, all that is nonsense. You know as well as I do that

[106-054]you should not marry a man when there is a doubt whether he can keep

[106-055]a house over your head;--that is his position."

[106-056]"It is good enough for me, mamma."

[106-057]"And what is his income from writing?"

[106-058]"It is quite enough for me, mamma. The truth is I have promised, and

[106-059]I cannot go back from it. Dear, dear mamma, you won't quarrel with

[106-060]us, and oppose us, and make papa hard against us. You can do what you

[106-061]like with papa. I know that. Look at poor Emily. Plenty of money has

[106-062]not made her happy."

[106-063]"If Mr. Glascock had only asked you a week sooner," said Lady Rowley,

[106-064]with a handkerchief to her eyes.

[106-065]"But you see he didn't, mamma."

[106-066]"When I think of it I cannot but weep"--and the poor mother burst out

[106-067]into a full flood of tears--"such a man, so good, so gentle, and so

[106-068]truly devoted to you."

[106-069]"Mamma, what's the good of that now?"

[106-070]"Going down all the way to Devonshire after you!"

[106-071]"So did Hugh, mamma."

[106-072]"A position that any girl in England would have envied you. I cannot

[106-073]but feel it. And Emily says she is sure he would come back if he got

[106-074]the very slightest encouragement."

[106-075]"That is quite impossible, mamma."

[106-076]"Why should it be impossible? Emily declares that she never saw a man

[106-077]so much in love in her life;--and she says also that she believes he

[106-078]is abroad now simply because he is broken-hearted about it."

[106-079]"Mr. Glascock, mamma, was very nice and good and all that; but indeed

[106-080]he is not the man to suffer from a broken heart. And Emily is quite

[106-081]mistaken. I told him the whole truth."

[106-082]"What truth?"

[106-083]"That there was somebody else that I did love. Then he said that of

[106-084]course that put an end to it all, and he wished me good-bye ever so

[106-085]calmly."

[106-086]"How could you be so infatuated? Why should you have cut the ground

[106-087]away from your feet in that way?"

[106-088]"Because I chose that there should be an end to it. Now there has

[106-089]been an end to it; and it is much better, mamma, that we should

[106-090]not think about Mr. Glascock any more. He will never come again to

[106-091]me,--and if he did, I could only say the same thing."

[106-092]"You mustn't be surprised, Nora, if I'm unhappy; that is all. Of

[106-093]course I must feel it. Such a connection as it would have been for

[106-094]your sisters! Such a home for poor Emily in her trouble! And as for

[106-095]this other man--"

[106-096]"Mamma, don't speak ill of him."

[106-097]"If I say anything of him, I must say the truth," said Lady Rowley.

[106-098]"Don't say anything against him, mamma, because he is to be my

[106-099]husband. Dear, dear mamma, you can't change me by anything you say.

[106-100]Perhaps I have been foolish; but it is settled now. Don't make me

[106-101]wretched by speaking against the man whom I mean to love all my life

[106-102]better than all the world."

[106-103]"Think of [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]]."

[106-104]"I will think of no one but [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. I tried not to love him,

[106-105]mamma. I tried to think that it was better to make believe that I

[106-106]loved Mr. Glascock. But he got the better of me, and conquered me,

[106-107]and I will never rebel against him. You may help me, mamma;--but you

[106-108]can't change me."

[106-109]CHAPTER LXIV.

[106-110]SIR MARMADUKE AT HIS CLUB.

[106-111]Sir Marmaduke had come away from his brother-in-law the parson in

[106-112]much anger, for Mr. Outhouse, with that mixture of obstinacy and

[106-113]honesty which formed his [[honour|character]], had spoken hard words of Colonel

[106-114]Osborne, and words which by implication had been hard also against

[106-115][[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]]. He had been very staunch to his niece when attacked

[106-116]by his niece's husband; but when his sympathies and assistance were

[106-117]invoked by Sir Marmaduke it seemed as though he had transferred his

[106-118]allegiance to the other side. He pointed out to the unhappy father

[106-119]that [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] had behaved with great cruelty in going to

[106-120]Devonshire, that the Stanburys had been untrue to their trust in

[106-121]allowing him to enter the house, and that Emily had been "indiscreet"

[106-122]in receiving him. When a young woman is called indiscreet by her

[106-123]friends it may be assumed that her [[reputation|character]] is very seriously

[106-124]assailed. Sir Marmaduke had understood this, and on hearing the word

[106-125]had become wroth with his brother-in-law. There had been hot words

[106-126]between them, and Mr. Outhouse would not yield an inch or retract

[106-127]a syllable. He conceived it to be his duty to advise the father

[106-128]to caution his daughter with severity, to quarrel absolutely with

[106-129]Colonel Osborne, and to let Trevelyan know that this had been done.

[106-130]As to the child, Mr. Outhouse expressed a strong opinion that the

[106-131]father was legally entitled to the custody of his boy, and that

[106-132]nothing could be done to recover the child, except what might be

[106-133]done with the father's consent. In fact, Mr. Outhouse made himself

[106-134]exceedingly disagreeable, and sent away Sir Marmaduke with a very

[106-135]heavy heart. Could it really be possible that his old friend Fred

[106-136]Osborne, who seven or eight-and-twenty years ago had been potent

[106-137]among young ladies, had really been making love to his old friend's

[106-138]married daughter? Sir Marmaduke looked into himself, and conceived it

[106-139]to be quite out of the question that he should make love to any one.

[106-140]A good dinner, good wine, a good cigar, an easy chair, and a rubber

[106-141]of whist,--all these things, with no work to do, and men of his own

[106-142][[reputation|standing]] around him were the pleasures of life which Sir Marmaduke

[106-143]desired. Now Fred Osborne was an older man than he, and though Fred

[106-144]Osborne did keep up a foolish system of padded clothes and dyed

[106-145]whiskers, still,--at fifty-two or fifty-three,--surely a man might be

[106-146]reckoned safe. And then, too, that ancient friendship! Sir Marmaduke,

[106-147]who had lived all his life in the comparative seclusion of a colony,

[106-148]thought perhaps more of that ancient friendship than did the Colonel,

[106-149]who had lived amidst the blaze of [[london|London]] life, and who had had many

[106-150]opportunities of changing his friends. Some inkling of all this

[106-151]made its way into Sir Marmaduke's bosom, as he thought of it with

[106-152]bitterness; and he determined that he would have it out with his

[106-153]friend.

[106-154]Hitherto he had enjoyed very few of those pleasant hours which he had

[106-155]anticipated on his journey homewards. He had had no heart to go to

[106-156]his club, and he had fancied that Colonel Osborne had been a little

[106-157]backward in looking him up, and providing him with amusement. He had

[106-158]suggested this to his wife, and she had told him that the Colonel had

[106-159]been right not to come to Manchester Street. "I have told Emily,"

[106-160]said Lady Rowley, "that she must not meet him, and she is quite of

[106-161]the same opinion." Nevertheless, there had been remissness. Sir

[106-162]Marmaduke felt that it was so, in spite of his wife's excuses. In

[106-163]this way he was becoming sore with everybody, and very unhappy. It

[106-164]did not at all improve his temper when he was told that his second

[106-165]daughter had refused an offer from Lord Peterborough's eldest son.

[106-166]"Then she may go into the workhouse for me," the angry father had

[106-167]said, declaring at the same time that he would never give his consent

[106-168]to her marriage with the man who "did dirty work" for the Daily

[106-169]Record,--as he, with his paternal wisdom, chose to express it. But

[106-170]this cruel phrase was not spoken in Nora's hearing, nor was it

[106-171]repeated to her. Lady Rowley knew her husband, and was aware that he

[106-172]would on occasions change his opinion.

[106-173]It was not till two or three days after his visit to St. Diddulph's

[106-174]that he met Colonel Osborne. The Easter recess was then over,

[106-175]and Colonel Osborne had just returned to London. They met on the

[106-176]door-steps of "The Acrobats," and the Colonel immediately began

[106-177]with an apology. "I have been so sorry to be away just when you are

[106-178]here;--upon my word I have. But I was obliged to go down to the

[106-179]duchess's. I had promised early in the winter; and those people

[106-180]are so angry if you put them off. By George, it's almost as bad as

[106-181]putting off royalty."

[106-182]"D----n the duchess," said Sir Marmaduke.

[106-183]"With all my heart," said the Colonel;--"only I thought it as well

[106-184]that I should tell you the truth."

[106-185]"What I mean is, that the duchess and her people make no difference

[106-186]to me. I hope you had a pleasant time; that's all."

[106-187]"Well;--yes, we had. One must get away somewhere at Easter. There is

[106-188]no one left at the club, and there's no House, and no one asks one to

[106-189]dinner in town. In fact, if one didn't go away one wouldn't know what

[106-190]to do. There were ever so many people there that I liked to meet.

[106-191]Lady Glencora was there, and uncommon pleasant she made it. That

[106-192]woman has more to say for herself than any half-dozen men that I

[106-193]know. And Lord Cantrip, your chief, was there. He said a word or two

[106-194]to me about you."
