## Overview

Martha is [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]]'s personal maid, a servant whose intimate position in the Trevelyan household gives her a front-row seat to the early stages of the marital crisis. She appears most prominently in Chapter 13, when Emily — on the verge of leaving her husband — confides in Martha her intention to depart. This scene is one of the novel's most psychologically acute renderings of the mistress-servant relationship, and Martha emerges from it as a figure of unexpected moral substance.

## Role in the Novel

Martha's central narrative function is to serve as witness and confidante during Emily's decision to separate from [[louis-trevelyan|Louis Trevelyan]]. In a Victorian household, the lady's maid occupied a uniquely intimate position — present at the most private moments, yet expected to maintain the fiction of invisibility. Trollope subverts this convention by giving Martha a voice and a moral perspective that complicate the reader's understanding of the situation (013-069).

The scene in which Emily summons Martha to confide her plan is remarkable for its dramatic tension. Emily, isolated by her position and the conventions of her class, has no one else to whom she can speak frankly. Her own mother is in the distant Mandarins, her sister [[nora-rowley|Nora]] is in Exeter, and the social proprieties that govern a wife's conduct forbid her from discussing her husband's behavior with anyone who might be considered her social equal. Martha is the only person available — and so it is to Martha that Emily reveals the decision that will determine the course of her life (013-068).

Martha's response is not what Emily expects. Rather than offering the automatic deference of a servant, Martha questions, challenges, and warns. She asks practical questions — "To live, ma'am?" — that cut through Emily's emotional rhetoric to the material reality of what separation will mean: how will Emily support herself? Where will she go? What will become of her child? (013-099). These questions, coming from a servant, are a kind of moral intervention — the voice of practical wisdom speaking across the class divide.

> "To live, ma'am?" Martha's tone as she asked the question conveyed more than the words themselves. It conveyed a lifetime of understanding about what happened to women who left their husbands — women who, unlike her mistress, had no family name, no independent means, no social position to fall back upon. (013-099)

## Character and Disposition

Martha is characterized by loyalty, practicality, and a quiet wisdom that transcends her station. She has "great gifts" — the narrator notes that she was "never impertinent" — and these gifts include an emotional intelligence that makes her, in some respects, a better adviser to Emily than any of the novel's more elevated characters (013-034).

Her relationship with Emily is complex and delicately drawn. There is genuine affection between the two women, but it is an affection constrained by the rigid hierarchies of Victorian domestic life. Martha can warn, can question, can even — in her own way — reproach, but she cannot overrule. When Emily insists on her course of action, Martha must accept it, and her acceptance carries the weight of someone who understands better than her mistress what the consequences will be.

## Key Scenes

Martha's most significant scene occupies the bulk of Chapter 13, in which Emily's decision to leave is rehearsed through dialogue with her maid. The scene is structured as a series of exchanges in which Emily declares her intentions and Martha, with increasing directness, tests their practicality. Emily's insistence — "I won't have it, Martha" — reveals the limits of the maid's influence: she can advise, but she cannot prevent (013-083).

Martha also appears in later chapters as a messenger and go-between. When [[miss-stanbury|Miss Stanbury]] visits the Trevelyan household, Martha is the servant who receives her (014-051), and her reports to Emily convey information and atmosphere in equal measure — the tone of Miss Stanbury's arrival, the quality of her manner, the implications of her presence.

## Significance

Martha belongs to a tradition in Victorian fiction of the observant servant — a figure who sees more than her employers realize and whose perspective offers the reader an alternative angle on the moral action. Unlike the comic servants of Dickens or the melodramatic retainers of sensation fiction, Martha is rendered with a restraint and realism that makes her moral witness all the more effective. She is not a plot device; she is a person, and her presence in the novel is a quiet reminder that the great dramas of the drawing room are also, always, playing out in the kitchen below.
