## Section 79

[079-001]"What promise?" asked Mr. Gibson.

[079-002]"I said that I would do as you bade me, and so I will. I would have

[079-003]done it sooner if I had known that you wished it. I would never have

[079-004]worn it at all if I had thought that you disliked it."

[079-005]"I think that a little of them is very nice," said Mr. Gibson. Mr.

[079-006]Gibson was certainly an awkward man. But there are men so awkward

[079-007]that it seems to be their especial province to say always the very

[079-008]worst thing at the very worst moment.

[079-009]She became redder than ever as she was thus told of the hugeness of

[079-010]her favourite ornament. She was almost angry now. But she restrained

[079-011]herself, thinking perhaps of how she might teach him taste in days to

[079-012]come as he was teaching her now. "I will change it to-morrow," she

[079-013]said with a smile. "You come and see to-morrow."

[079-014]Upon this he got up and took his hat and made his escape, assuring

[079-015]her that he would come and see her on the morrow. She let him go now

[079-016]without any attempt at further tenderness. Certainly she had gained

[079-017]much during the interview. He had as good as told her in what had

[079-018]been her offence, and of course, when she had remedied that offence,

[079-019]he could hardly refuse to return to her. She got up as soon as she

[079-020]was alone, and looked at her head in the glass, and told herself that

[079-021]the pity would be great. It was not that the chignon was in itself

[079-022]a thing of beauty, but that it imparted so unmistakable an air of

[079-023]fashion! It divested her of that dowdiness which she feared above

[079-024]all things, and enabled her to hold her own among other young women,

[079-025]without feeling that she was absolutely destitute of attraction.

[079-026]There had been a certain homage paid to it, which she had recognised

[079-027]and enjoyed. But it was her ambition to hold her own, not among

[079-028]young women, but among clergymen's wives, and she would certainly

[079-029]obey his orders. She could not make the attempt now because of the

[079-030]complications; but she certainly would make it before she laid her

[079-031]head on the pillow,--and would explain to Camilla that it was a

[079-032]little joke between herself and Mr. Gibson.

[079-033]CHAPTER XLVIII.

[079-034]MR. GIBSON IS PUNISHED.

[079-035]Miss Stanbury was divine in her wrath, and became more and more so

[079-036]daily as new testimony reached her of dishonesty on the part of

[079-037]the Frenches and of treachery on the part of Mr. Gibson. And these

[079-038]people, so empty, so vain, so weak, were getting the better of her,

[079-039]were conquering her, were robbing her of her prestige and her ancient

[079-040]glory, simply because she herself was too generous to speak out and

[079-041]tell the truth! There was a martyrdom to her in this which was almost

[079-042]unendurable.

[079-043]Now there came to her one day at luncheon time,--on the day

[079-044]succeeding that on which Miss French had promised to sacrifice her

[079-045]chignon,--a certain Mrs. Clifford from Budleigh Salterton, to whom

[079-046]she was much attached. Perhaps the distance of Budleigh Salterton

[079-047]from [[exeter|Exeter]] added somewhat to this affection, so that Mrs. Clifford

[079-048]was almost closer to our friend's heart even than Mrs. MacHugh, who

[079-049]lived just at the other end of the cathedral. And in truth Mrs.

[079-050]Clifford was a woman more serious in her mode of thought than Mrs.

[079-051]MacHugh, and one who had more in common with Miss Stanbury than that

[079-052]other lady. Mrs. Clifford had been a Miss Noel of Doddiscombe Leigh,

[079-053]and she and Miss Stanbury had been engaged to be married at the same

[079-054]time,--each to a man of [[money|fortune]]. One match had been completed in the

[079-055]ordinary course of matches. What had been the course of the other we

[079-056]already know. But the friendship had been maintained on very close

[079-057]terms. Mrs. MacHugh was a Gallio at heart, anxious chiefly to remove

[079-058]from herself,--and from her friends also,--all the troubles of life,

[079-059]and make things smooth and easy. She was one who disregarded great

[079-060]questions; who cared little or nothing what people said of her; who

[079-061]considered nothing worth the trouble of a fight;--Epicuri de grege

[079-062]porca. But there was nothing swinish about Mrs. Clifford of Budleigh

[079-063]Salterton. She took life thoroughly in earnest. She was a Tory who

[079-064]sorrowed heartily for her country, believing that it was being

[079-065]brought to ruin by the counsels of evil men. She prayed daily to

[079-066]be delivered from dissenters, radicals, and wolves in sheep's

[079-067]clothing,--by which latter bad name she meant especially a certain

[079-068]leading politician of the day who had, with the cunning of the

[079-069]devil, tempted and perverted the virtue of her own political friends.

[079-070]And she was one who thought that the slightest breath of scandal

[079-071]on a young woman's name should be stopped at once. An antique,

[079-072]pure-minded, anxious, self-sacrificing matron was Mrs. Clifford, and

[079-073]very dear to the heart of Miss Stanbury.

[079-074]After lunch was over on the day in question Mrs. Clifford got Miss

[079-075]Stanbury into some closet retirement, and there spoke her mind as

[079-076]to the things which were being said. It had been asserted in her

[079-077]presence by Camilla French that she, Camilla, was authorised by Mr.

[079-078]Gibson to declare that he had never thought of proposing to Dorothy

[079-079]Stanbury, and that Miss Stanbury had been "labouring under some

[079-080]strange misapprehension in the matter." "Now, my dear, I don't care

[079-081]very much for the young lady in question," said Mrs. Clifford,

[079-082]alluding to Camilla French.

[079-083]"Very little, indeed, I should think," said Miss Stanbury, with a

[079-084]shake of her head.

[079-085]"Quite true, my dear,--but that does not make the words out of her

[079-086]mouth the less efficacious for evil. She clearly insinuated that you

[079-087]had endeavoured to make up a match between this [[social-class|gentleman]] and your

[079-088]niece, and that you had failed." So much was at least true. Miss

[079-089]Stanbury felt this, and felt also that she could not explain the

[079-090]truth, even to her dear old friend. In the midst of her divine wrath

[079-091]she had acknowledged to herself that she had brought Mr. Gibson into

[079-092]his difficulty, and that it would not become her to tell any one

[079-093]of his failure. And in this matter she did not herself accuse Mr.

[079-094]Gibson. She believed that the lie originated with Camilla French, and

[079-095]it was against Camilla that her wrath raged the fiercest.

[079-096]"She is a poor, mean, disappointed thing," said Miss Stanbury.

[079-097]"Very probably;--but I think I should ask her to hold her tongue

[079-098]about Miss Dorothy," said Mrs. Clifford.

[079-099]The consultation in the closet was carried on for about half-an-hour,

[079-100]and then Miss Stanbury put on her bonnet and shawl and descended into

[079-101]Mrs. Clifford's carriage. The carriage took the Heavitree road, and

[079-102]deposited Miss Stanbury at the door of Mrs. French's house. The walk

[079-103]home from Heavitree would be nothing, and Mrs. Clifford proceeded on

[079-104]her way, having given this little help in counsel and conveyance to

[079-105]her friend. Mrs. French was at home, and Miss Stanbury was shown up

[079-106]into the room in which the three ladies were sitting.

[079-107][Illustration: Miss Stanbury visits the Frenches.]

[079-108]The reader will doubtless remember the promise which Arabella had

[079-109]made to Mr. Gibson. That promise she had already fulfilled,--to the

[079-110]amazement of her mother and sister;--and when Miss Stanbury entered

[079-111]the room the elder daughter of the family was seen without her

[079-112]accustomed head-gear. If the truth is to be owned, Miss Stanbury gave

[079-113]the poor young woman no credit for her new simplicity, but put down

[079-114]the deficiency to the charge of domestic slatternliness. She was

[079-115]unjust enough to declare afterwards that she had found Arabella

[079-116]French only half dressed at between three and four o'clock in the

[079-117]afternoon! From which this lesson may surely be learned,--that though

[079-118]the way down Avernus may be, and customarily is, made with great

[079-119]celerity, the return journey, if made at all, must be made slowly. A

[079-120]young woman may commence in chignons by attaching any amount of an

[079-121]edifice to her head; but the reduction should be made by degrees.

[079-122]Arabella's edifice had, in Miss Stanbury's eyes, been the ugliest

[079-123]thing in art that she had known; but, now, its absence offended her,

[079-124]and she most untruly declared that she had come upon the young woman

[079-125]in the middle of the day just out of her bed-room and almost in her

[079-126]dressing-gown.

[079-127]And the whole French family suffered a diminution of power from the

[079-128]strange phantasy which had come upon Arabella. They all felt, in

[079-129]sight of the enemy, that they had to a certain degree lowered their

[079-130]flag. One of the ships, at least, had shown signs of striking,

[079-131]and this element of weakness made itself felt through the whole

[079-132]fleet. Arabella, herself, when she saw Miss Stanbury, was painfully

[079-133]conscious of her head, and wished that she had postponed the

[079-134]operation till the evening. She smiled with a faint watery smile, and

[079-135]was aware that something ailed her.

[079-136]The greetings at first were civil, but very formal, as are those

[079-137]between nations which are nominally at peace, but which are waiting

[079-138]for a sign at which each may spring at the other's throat. In this

[079-139]instance the Juno from the Close had come quite prepared to declare

[079-140]her casus belli as complete, and to fling down her gauntlet, unless

[079-141]the enemy should at once yield to her everything demanded with an

[079-142]abject submission. "Mrs. French," she said, "I have called to-day

[079-143]for a particular purpose, and I must address myself chiefly to Miss

[079-144]Camilla."

[079-145]"Oh, certainly," said Mrs. French.

[079-146]"I shall be delighted to hear anything from you, Miss Stanbury," said

[079-147]Camilla,--not without an air of bravado. Arabella said nothing, but

[079-148]she put her hand up almost convulsively to the back of her head.

[079-149]"I have been told to-day by a friend of mine, Miss Camilla," began

[079-150]Miss Stanbury, "that you declared yourself, in her presence,

[079-151]authorised by Mr. Gibson to make a statement about my niece Dorothy."

[079-152]"May I ask who was your friend?" demanded Mrs. French.

[079-153]"It was Mrs. Clifford, of course," said Camilla. "There is nobody

[079-154]else would try to make difficulties."

[079-155]"There need be no difficulty at all, Miss Camilla," said Miss

[079-156]Stanbury, "if you will promise me that you will not repeat the

[079-157]statement. It can't be true."

[079-158]"But it is true," said Camilla.

[079-159]"What is true?" asked Miss Stanbury, surprised by the audacity of the

[079-160]girl.

[079-161]"It is true that Mr. Gibson authorised us to state what I did state

[079-162]when Mrs. Clifford heard me."

[079-163]"And what was that?"

[079-164]"Only this,--that people had been saying all about Exeter that he

[079-165]was going to be married to a young lady, and that as the report was

[079-166]incorrect, and as he had never had the remotest idea in his mind of

[079-167]making the young lady his wife,--" Camilla, as she said this, spoke

[079-168]with a great deal of emphasis, putting forward her chin and shaking

[079-169]her head,--"and as he thought it was uncomfortable both for the young

[079-170]lady and for himself, and as there was nothing in it the least in the

[079-171]world,--nothing at all, no glimmer of a foundation for the report, it

[079-172]would be better to have it denied everywhere. That is what I said;

[079-173]and we had authority from the gentleman himself. Arabella can say

[079-174]the same, and so can mamma;--only mamma did not hear him." Nor had

[079-175]Camilla heard him, but that incident she did not mention.

[079-176]The circumstances were, in Miss Stanbury's judgment, becoming very

[079-177]remarkable. She did not for a moment believe Camilla. She did not

[079-178]believe that Mr. Gibson had given to either of the Frenches any

[079-179]justification for the statement just made. But Camilla had been so

[079-180]much more audacious than Miss Stanbury had expected, that that lady

[079-181]was for a moment struck dumb. "I'm sure, Miss Stanbury," said Mrs.

[079-182]French, "we don't want to give any offence to your niece,--very far

[079-183]from it."
