## Section 83

[083-001]Nevertheless Arabella did sit down to tea with the two lovers that

[083-002]night. There was a terrible scene between her and Camilla; but

[083-003]Camilla held her own; and Arabella, being the weaker of the two, was

[083-004]vanquished by the expenditure of her own small energies. Camilla

[083-005]argued that as her sister's chance was gone, and as the prize had

[083-006]come in her own way, there was no good reason why it should be lost

[083-007]to the family altogether, because Arabella could not win it. When

[083-008]Arabella called her a treacherous vixen and a heartless, profligate

[083-009]hussy, she spoke out freely, and said that she wasn't going to be

[083-010]abused. A [[social-class|gentleman]] to whom she was attached had asked her for her

[083-011]hand, and she had given it. If Arabella chose to make herself a fool

[083-012]she might,--but what would be the effect? Simply that all the world

[083-013]would know that she, Arabella, was disappointed. Poor Bella at last

[083-014]gave way, put on her discarded chignon, and came down to tea. Mr.

[083-015]Gibson was already in the room when she entered it. "Arabella," he

[083-016]said, getting up to greet her, "I hope you will congratulate me."

[083-017]He had planned his little speech and his manner of making it, and

[083-018]had wisely decided that in this way might he best get over the

[083-019]difficulty.

[083-020]"Oh yes;--of course," she said, with a little giggle, and then a sob,

[083-021]and then a flood of tears.

[083-022]"Dear Bella feels these things so strongly," said Mrs. French.

[083-023]"We have never been parted yet," said Camilla. Then Arabella tapped

[083-024]the head of the sofa three or four times sharply with her knuckles.

[083-025]It was the only protest against the reading of the scene which

[083-026]Camilla had given of which she was capable at that moment. After that

[083-027]Mrs. French gave out the tea, Arabella curled herself upon the sofa

[083-028]as though she were asleep, and the two lovers settled down to proper

[083-029]lover-like conversation.

[083-030]The reader may be sure that Camilla was not slow in making the fact

[083-031]of her engagement notorious through the city. It was not probably

[083-032]true that the tidings of her success had anything to do with Miss

[083-033]Stanbury's illness; but it was reported by many that such was the

[083-034]case. It was in November that the arrangement was made, and it

[083-035]certainly was true that Miss Stanbury was rather ill about the same

[083-036]time. "You know, you naughty Lothario, that you did give her some

[083-037]ground to hope that she might dispose of her unfortunate niece," said

[083-038]Camilla playfully to her own one, when this illness was discussed

[083-039]between them. "But you are caught now, and your wings are clipped,

[083-040]and you are never to be a naughty Lothario again." The clerical

[083-041]Don Juan bore it all, awkwardly indeed, but with good humour, and

[083-042]declared that all his troubles of that sort were over, now and for

[083-043]ever. Nevertheless he did not name the day, and Camilla began to feel

[083-044]that there might be occasion for a little more of that imperious

[083-045]roughness which she had at her command.

[083-046]November was nearly over and nothing had been fixed about the day.

[083-047]Arabella never condescended to speak to her sister on the subject;

[083-048]but on more than one occasion made some inquiry of her mother. And

[083-049]she came to perceive, or to think that she perceived, that her mother

[083-050]was still anxious on the subject. "I shouldn't wonder if he wasn't

[083-051]off some day now," she said at last to her mother.

[083-052]"Don't say anything so dreadful, Bella."

[083-053]"It would serve Cammy quite right, and it's just what he's likely to

[083-054]do."

[083-055]"It would kill me," said the mother.

[083-056]"I don't know about killing," said Arabella; "it's nothing to what

[083-057]I've had to go through. I shouldn't pretend to be sorry if he were to

[083-058]go to Hong-Kong to-morrow."

[083-059]But Mr. Gibson had no idea of going to Hong-Kong. He was simply

[083-060]carrying out his little scheme for securing the advantages of a "long

[083-061]day." He was fully resolved to be married, and was contented to think

[083-062]that his engagement was the best thing for him. To one or two male

[083-063]friends he spoke of Camilla as the perfection of female virtue, and

[083-064]entertained no smallest idea of ultimate escape. But a "long day" is

[083-065]often a convenience. A bill at three months sits easier on a man than

[083-066]one at sixty days; and a bill at six months is almost as little of a

[083-067]burden as no bill at all.

[083-068]But Camilla was resolved that some day should be fixed. "Thomas," she

[083-069]said to her lover one morning, as they were walking home together

[083-070]after service at the cathedral, "isn't this rather a fool's Paradise

[083-071]of ours?"

[083-072]"How a fool's Paradise?" asked the happy Thomas.

[083-073]"What I mean is, dearest, that we ought to fix something. Mamma is

[083-074]getting uneasy about her own plans."

[083-075]"In what way, dearest?"

[083-076]"[[About]] a thousand things. She can't arrange anything till our plans

[083-077]are made. Of course there are little troubles about money when people

[083-078]ain't rich." Then it occurred to her that this might seem to be a

[083-079]plea for postponing rather than for hurrying the marriage, and she

[083-080]mended her argument. "The truth is, Thomas, she wants to know when

[083-081]the day is to be fixed, and I've promised to ask. She said she'd ask

[083-082]you herself, but I wouldn't let her do that."

[083-083]"We must think about it, of course," said Thomas.

[083-084]"But, my dear, there has been plenty of time for thinking. What do

[083-085]you say to January?" This was on the last day of November.

[083-086]"January!" exclaimed Thomas, in a tone that betrayed no triumph. "I

[083-087]couldn't get my services arranged for in January."

[083-088]"I thought a clergyman could always manage that for his marriage,"

[083-089]said Camilla.

[083-090]"Not in January. Besides, I was thinking you would like to be away in

[083-091]warmer weather."

[083-092]They were still in November, and he was thinking of postponing it

[083-093]till the summer! Camilla immediately perceived how necessary it was

[083-094]that she should be plain with him. "We shall not have warm weather,

[083-095]as you call it, for a very long time, Thomas;--and I don't think that

[083-096]it would be wise to wait for the weather at all. Indeed, I've begun

[083-097]to get my things for doing it in the winter. Mamma said that she was

[083-098]sure January would be the very latest. And it isn't as though we had

[083-099]to get furniture or anything of that kind. Of course a lady shouldn't

[083-100]be pressing." She smiled sweetly and leaned on his arm as she said

[083-101]this. "But I hate all girlish nonsense and that kind of thing. It is

[083-102]such a bore to be kept waiting. I'm sure there's nothing to prevent

[083-103]it coming off in February."

[083-104]The 31st of March was fixed before they reached Heavitree, and

[083-105]Camilla went into her mother's house a happy woman. But Mr. Gibson,

[083-106]as he went home, thought that he had been hardly used. Here was a

[083-107]girl who hadn't a shilling of money,--not a shilling till her mother

[083-108]died,--and who already talked about his house, and his furniture,

[083-109]and his [[money|income]], as if it were all her own! Circumstanced as she was,

[083-110]what right had she to press for an early day? He was quite sure that

[083-111]Arabella would have been more discreet and less exacting. He was very

[083-112]angry with his dear Cammy as he went across the Close to his house.

[083-113]CHAPTER LI.

[083-114]SHEWING WHAT HAPPENED DURING MISS STANBURY'S ILLNESS.

[083-115]It was on Christmas-day that Sir Peter Mancrudy, the highest

[083-116]authority on such matters in the west of England, was sent for to see

[083-117]Miss Stanbury; and Sir Peter had acknowledged that things were very

[083-118]serious. He took Dorothy on one side, and told her that Mr. Martin,

[083-119]the ordinary practitioner, had treated the case, no doubt, quite

[083-120]wisely throughout; that there was not a word to be said against

[083-121]Mr. Martin, whose experience was great, and whose discretion was

[083-122]undeniable; but, nevertheless,--at least it seemed to Dorothy that

[083-123]this was the only meaning to be attributed to Sir Peter's words,--Mr.

[083-124]Martin had in this case taken one line of treatment, when he ought

[083-125]to have taken another. The plan of action was undoubtedly changed,

[083-126]and Mr. Martin became very fidgety, and ordered nothing without Sir

[083-127]Peter's sanction. Miss Stanbury was suffering from bronchitis, and a

[083-128]complication of diseases about her throat and chest. Barty Burgess

[083-129]declared to more than one acquaintance in the little parlour behind

[083-130]the bank, that she would go on drinking four or five glasses of

[083-131]new port wine every day, in direct opposition to Martin's request.

[083-132]Camilla French heard the report, and repeated it to her lover, and

[083-133]perhaps another person or two, with an expression of her assured

[083-134]conviction that it must be false,--at any rate, as regarded the

[083-135]fifth glass. Mrs. MacHugh, who saw Martha daily, was much frightened.

[083-136]The peril of such a friend disturbed equally the repose and the

[083-137]pleasures of her life. Mrs. Clifford was often at Miss Stanbury's

[083-138]bed-side,--and would have sat there reading for hours together,

[083-139]had she not been made to understand by Martha that Miss Stanbury

[083-140]preferred that Miss Dorothy should read to her. The sick woman

[083-141]received the Sacrament weekly,--not from Mr. Gibson, but from the

[083-142]hands of another minor canon; and, though she never would admit her

[083-143]own danger, or allow others to talk to her of it, it was known to

[083-144]them all that she admitted it to herself because she had, with much

[083-145]personal annoyance, caused a codicil to be added to her will. "As

[083-146]you didn't marry that man," she said to Dorothy, "I must change it

[083-147]again." It was in vain that Dorothy begged her not to trouble herself

[083-148]with such thoughts. "That's trash," said Miss Stanbury, angrily. "A

[083-149]person who has it is bound to trouble himself about it. You don't

[083-150]suppose I'm afraid of dying;--do you?" she added. Dorothy answered

[083-151]her with some commonplace,--declaring how strongly they all expected

[083-152]to see her as well as ever. "I'm not a bit afraid to die," said the

[083-153]old woman, wheezing, struggling with such voice as she possessed;

[083-154]"I'm not afraid of it, and I don't think I shall die this time; but

[083-155]I'm not going to have mistakes when I'm gone." This was on the eve

[083-156]of the new year, and on the same night she asked Dorothy to write to

[083-157]Brooke Burgess, and request him to come to [[exeter|Exeter]]. This was Dorothy's

[083-158]letter:--

[083-159]Exeter, 31st December, 186--.

[083-160]MY DEAR MR. BURGESS,

[083-161]Perhaps I ought to have written before, to say that Aunt

[083-162]Stanbury is not as well as we could wish her; but, as I

[083-163]know that you cannot very well leave your office, I have

[083-164]thought it best not to say anything to frighten you. But

[083-165]to-night Aunt herself has desired me to tell you that she

[083-166]thinks you ought to know that she is ill, and that she

[083-167]wishes you to come to Exeter for a day or two, if it is

[083-168]possible. Sir Peter Mancrudy has been here every day

[083-169]since Christmas-day, and I believe he thinks she may get

[083-170]over it. It is chiefly in the throat;--what they call

[083-171]bronchitis,--and she has got to be very weak with it, and

[083-172]at the same time very liable to inflammation. So I know

[083-173]that you will come if you can.

[083-174]Yours very truly,

[083-175]DOROTHY STANBURY.

[083-176]Perhaps I ought to tell you that she had her lawyer here

[083-177]with her the day before yesterday; but she does not seem

[083-178]to think that she herself is in danger. I read to her a

[083-179]good deal, and I think she is generally asleep; when I

[083-180]stop she wakes, and I don't believe she gets any other

[083-181]rest at all.

[083-182]When it was known in Exeter that Brooke Burgess had been sent for,

[083-183]then the opinion became general that Miss Stanbury's days were

[083-184]numbered. Questions were asked of Sir Peter at every corner of the

[083-185]street; but Sir Peter was a discreet man, who could answer such

[083-186]questions without giving any information. If it so pleased God, his

[083-187]patient would die; but it was quite possible that she might live.

[083-188]That was the tenor of Sir Peter's replies,--and they were read in any

[083-189]light, according to the idiosyncracies of the reader. Mrs. MacHugh

[083-190]was quite sure that the danger was over, and had a little game of

[083-191]cribbage on the sly with old Miss Wright;--for, during the severity

[083-192]of Miss Stanbury's illness, whist was put on one side in the vicinity

[083-193]of the Close. Barty Burgess was still obdurate, and shook his head.

[083-194]He was of opinion that they might soon gratify their curiosity, and

[083-195]see the last crowning iniquity of this wickedest of old women. Mrs.

[083-196]Clifford declared that it was all in the hands of God; but that

[083-197]she saw no reason why Miss Stanbury should not get about again. Mr.

[083-198]Gibson thought that it was all up with his late friend; and Camilla

[083-199]wished that at their last interview there had been more of charity

[083-200]on the part of one whom she had regarded in past days with respect

[083-201]and esteem. Mrs. French, despondent about everything, was quite

[083-202]despondent in this case. Martha almost despaired, and already was

[083-203]burdened with the cares of a whole wardrobe of solemn funereal

[083-204]clothing. She was seen peering in for half-an-hour at the windows and

[083-205]doorway of a large warehouse for the sale of mourning. Giles Hickbody

[083-206]would not speak above his breath, and took his beer [[reputation|standing]]; but

[083-207]Dorothy was hopeful, and really believed that her aunt would recover.

[083-208]Perhaps Sir Peter had spoken to her in terms less oracular than those

[083-209]which he used towards the public.
