## Section 160

[160-001]"Is not that nonsense, Pris?"

[160-002]"Just at present it is, because mamma and I have both got new gowns

[160-003]for the wedding. Hugh sent them, and ever so much money to buy

[160-004]bonnets and gloves."

[160-005]"He is to be married himself soon,--down at a place called Monkhams.

[160-006]Nora is staying there."

[160-007]"Yes;--with a lord," said Priscilla. "We sha'n't have to go there, at

[160-008]any rate."

[160-009]"You liked Nora when she was here?"

[160-010]"Very much;--though I thought her self-willed. But she is not

[160-011]worldly, and she is conscientious. She might have married that lord

[160-012]herself if she would. I do like her. When she comes to you at [[exeter|Exeter]],

[160-013]if the wedding gown isn't quite worn out, I shall come and see her. I

[160-014]knew she liked him when she was here, but she never said so."

[160-015]"She is very pretty, is she not? He sent me her photograph."

[160-016]"She is handsome rather than pretty. I wonder why it is that you two

[160-017]should be married, and so grandly married, and that I shall never,

[160-018]never have any one to love."

[160-019]"Oh, Priscilla, do not say that. If I have a child will you not love

[160-020]it?"

[160-021]"It will be your child;--not mine. Do not suppose that I complain.

[160-022]I know that it is right. I know that you ought to be married and I

[160-023]ought not. I know that there is not a man in Devonshire who would

[160-024]take me, or a man in Devonshire whom I would accept. I know that I am

[160-025]quite unfit for any other kind of life than this. I should make any

[160-026]man wretched, and any man would make me wretched. But why is it so? I

[160-027]believe that you would make any man happy."

[160-028]"I hope to make Brooke happy."

[160-029]"Of course you will, and therefore you deserve it. We'll go home now,

[160-030]dear, and get mamma's things ready for the great day."

[160-031]On the afternoon before the great day all the visitors were to come,

[160-032]and during the forenoon old Miss Stanbury was in a great fidget.

[160-033]Luckily for Dorothy, her own preparations were already made, so that

[160-034]she could give her time to her aunt without injury to herself. Miss

[160-035]Stanbury had come to think of herself as though all the reality of

[160-036]her life had passed away from her. Every resolution that she had

[160-037]formed had been broken. She had had the great enemy of her life,

[160-038]Barty Burgess, in the house with her upon terms that were intended

[160-039]to be amicable, and had arranged with him a plan for the division of

[160-040]the family [[money|property]]. Her sister-in-law, whom in the heyday of her

[160-041]strength she had chosen to regard as her enemy, and with whom even as

[160-042]yet there had been no reconciliation, was about to become her guest,

[160-043]as was also Priscilla,--whom she had ever disliked almost as much as

[160-044]she had respected. She had quarrelled utterly with Hugh,--in such a

[160-045]manner as to leave no possible chance of a reconciliation,--and he

[160-046]also was about to be her guest. And then, as to her chosen heir, she

[160-047]was now assisting him in doing the only thing, as to which she had

[160-048]declared that if he did do it, he should not be her heir. As she went

[160-049]about the house, under an idea that such a multiplicity of persons

[160-050]could not be housed and fed without superhuman exertion, she thought

[160-051]of all this, and could not help confessing to herself that her life

[160-052]had been very vain. It was only when her eyes rested on Dorothy, and

[160-053]she saw how supremely happy was the one person whom she had taken

[160-054]most closely to her heart, that she could feel that she had done

[160-055]anything that should not have been left undone. "I think I'll sit

[160-056]down now, Dorothy," she said, "or I sha'n't be able to be with you

[160-057]to-morrow."

[160-058]"Do, aunt. Everything is all ready, and nobody will be here for an

[160-059]hour yet. Nothing can be nicer than the rooms, and nothing ever was

[160-060]done so well before. I'm only thinking how lonely you'll be when

[160-061]we're gone."

[160-062]"It'll be only for six weeks."

[160-063]"But six weeks is such a long time."

[160-064]"What would it have been if he had taken you up to [[london|London]], my pet?

[160-065]Are you sure your mother wouldn't like a fire in her room, Dorothy?"

[160-066]"A fire in September, aunt?"

[160-067]"People live so differently. One never knows."

[160-068]"They never have but one fire at Nuncombe, aunt, summer or winter."

[160-069]"That's no reason they shouldn't be comfortable here." However, she

[160-070]did not insist on having the fire lighted.

[160-071]Mrs. Stanbury and Priscilla came first, and the meeting was certainly

[160-072]very uncomfortable. Poor Mrs. Stanbury was shy, and could hardly

[160-073]speak a word. Miss Stanbury thought that her visitor was haughty,

[160-074]and, though she endeavoured to be gracious, did it with a struggle.

[160-075]They called each other ma'am, which made Dorothy uneasy. Each of them

[160-076]was so dear to her, that it was a pity that they should glower at

[160-077]each other like enemies. Priscilla was not at all shy; but she was

[160-078]combative, and, as her aunt said of her afterwards, would not keep

[160-079]her prickles in. "I hope, Priscilla, you like weddings," said Miss

[160-080]Stanbury to her, not knowing where to find a subject for

[160-081]conversation.

[160-082]"In the abstract I like them," said Priscilla. Miss Stanbury did not

[160-083]know what her niece meant by liking weddings in the abstract, and was

[160-084]angry.

[160-085]"I suppose you do have weddings at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] sometimes," she

[160-086]said.

[160-087]"I hope they do," said Priscilla, "but I never saw one. To-morrow

[160-088]will be my first experience."

[160-089]"Your own will come next, my dear," said Miss Stanbury.

[160-090]"I think not," said Priscilla. "It is quite as likely to be yours,

[160-091]aunt." This, Miss Stanbury thought, was almost an insult, and she

[160-092]said nothing more on the occasion.

[160-093]Then came Hugh and the bridegroom. The bridegroom, as a matter of

[160-094]course, was not accommodated in the house, but he was allowed to

[160-095]come there for his tea. He and Hugh had come together; and for Hugh

[160-096]a bed-room had been provided. His aunt had not seen him since he

[160-097]had been turned out of the house, because of his bad practices, and

[160-098]Dorothy had anticipated the meeting between them with alarm. It was,

[160-099]however, much more pleasant than had been that between the ladies.

[160-100]"Hugh," she said stiffly, "I am glad to see you on such an occasion

[160-101]as this."

[160-102]"Aunt," he said, "I am glad of any occasion that can get me an

[160-103]entrance once more into the dear old house. I am so pleased to see

[160-104]you." She allowed her hand to remain in his a few moments, and

[160-105]murmured something which was intended to signify her satisfaction.

[160-106]"I must tell you that I am going to be married myself, to one of the

[160-107]dearest, sweetest, and loveliest girls that ever were seen, and you

[160-108]must congratulate me."

[160-109]"I do, I do; and I hope you may be happy."

[160-110]"We mean to try to be; and some day you must let me bring her to you,

[160-111]and shew her. I shall not be satisfied, if you do not know my wife."

[160-112]She told Martha afterwards that she hoped that Mr. Hugh had sown his

[160-113]wild oats, and that [[marriage|matrimony]] would sober him. When, however, Martha

[160-114]remarked that she believed Mr. Hugh to be as hardworking a young man

[160-115]as any in London, Miss Stanbury shook her head sorrowfully. Things

[160-116]were being very much changed with her; but not even yet was she to be

[160-117]brought to approve of work done on behalf of a penny newspaper.

[160-118]On the following morning, at ten o'clock, there was a procession from

[160-119]Miss Stanbury's house into the Cathedral, which was made entirely on

[160-120]foot;--indeed, no assistance could have been given by any carriage,

[160-121]for there is a back entrance to the Cathedral, near to the Lady

[160-122]Chapel, exactly opposite Miss Stanbury's house. There were many

[160-123]of the inhabitants of the Close there, to see the procession, and

[160-124]the cathedral bells rang out their peals very merrily. Brooke, the

[160-125]bridegroom, gave his arm to Miss Stanbury, which was, no doubt, very

[160-126]improper,--as he should have appeared in the church as coming from

[160-127]quite some different part of the world. Then came the bride, hanging

[160-128]on her brother, then two bridesmaids,--friends of Dorothy's, living

[160-129]in the town; and, lastly, Priscilla with her mother, for nothing

[160-130]would induce Priscilla to take the part of a bridesmaid. "You might

[160-131]as well ask an owl to sing to you," she said. "And then all the

[160-132]frippery would be thrown away upon me." But she stood close to

[160-133]Dorothy, and when the ceremony had been performed, was the first,

[160-134]after Brooke, to kiss her.

[160-135]Everybody acknowledged that the bride was a winsome bride. Mrs.

[160-136]MacHugh was at the breakfast, and declared afterwards that Dorothy

[160-137]Burgess,--as she then was pleased to call her,--was a girl very hard

[160-138]to be understood. "She came here," said Mrs. MacHugh, "two years ago,

[160-139]a plain, silent, shy, dowdy young woman, and we all said that Miss

[160-140]Stanbury would be tired of her in a week. There has never come a time

[160-141]in which there was any visible difference in her, and now she is one

[160-142]of our city beauties, with plenty to say to everybody, with a fortune

[160-143]in one pocket and her aunt in the other, and everybody is saying what

[160-144]a fortunate fellow Brooke Burgess is to get her. In a year or two

[160-145]she'll be at the top of everything in the city, and will make her way

[160-146]in the county too."

[160-147]The compiler of this history begs to add his opinion to that of

[160-148]"everybody," as quoted above by Mrs. MacHugh. He thinks that Brooke

[160-149]Burgess was a very fortunate fellow to get his wife.

[160-150]CHAPTER XCVIII.

[160-151]ACQUITTED.

[160-152]During this time, while Hugh was sitting with his love under the oak

[160-153]trees at Monkhams, and Dorothy was being converted into Mrs. Brooke

[160-154]Burgess in Exeter Cathedral, Mrs. Trevelyan was living with her

[160-155]husband in the cottage at Twickenham. Her life was dreary enough,

[160-156]and there was but very little of hope in it to make its dreariness

[160-157]supportable. As often happens in periods of sickness, the single

[160-158]friend who could now be of service to the one or to the other was

[160-159]the doctor. He came daily to them, and with that quick growth of

[160-160]confidence which medical kindness always inspires, Trevelyan told

[160-161]to this [[social-class|gentleman]] all the history of his married life,--and all

[160-162]that Trevelyan told to him he repeated to Trevelyan's wife. It may

[160-163]therefore be understood that Trevelyan, between them, was treated

[160-164]like a child.

[160-165]Dr. Nevill had soon been able to tell Mrs. Trevelyan that her

[160-166]husband's health had been so shattered as to make it improbable that

[160-167]he should ever again be strong either in body or in mind. He would

[160-168]not admit, even when treating his patient like a child, that he had

[160-169]ever been mad, and spoke of Sir Marmaduke's threat as unfortunate.

[160-170]"But what could papa have done?" asked the wife.

[160-171]"It is often, no doubt, difficult to know what to do; but threats are

[160-172]seldom of avail to bring a man back to reason. Your father was angry

[160-173]with him, and yet declared that he was mad. That in itself was hardly

[160-174]rational. One does not become angry with a madman."

[160-175]One does not become angry with a madman; but while a man has power

[160-176]in his hands over others, and when he misuses that power grossly

[160-177]and cruelly, who is there that will not be angry? The misery of the

[160-178]insane more thoroughly excites our pity than any other suffering

[160-179]to which humanity is subject; but it is necessary that the madness

[160-180]should be acknowledged to be madness before the pity can be felt. One

[160-181]can forgive, or, at any rate, make excuses for any injury when it is

[160-182]done; but it is almost beyond human nature to forgive an injury when

[160-183]it is a-doing, let the condition of the doer be what it may. Emily

[160-184]Trevelyan at this time suffered infinitely. She was still willing to

[160-185]yield in all things possible, because her husband was ill,--because

[160-186]perhaps he was dying; but she could no longer satisfy herself with

[160-187]thinking that all that she admitted,--all that she was still ready to

[160-188]admit,--had been conceded in order that her concessions might tend to

[160-189]soften the afflictions of one whose reason was gone. Dr. Nevill said

[160-190]that her husband was not mad;--and indeed Trevelyan seemed now to be

[160-191]so clear in his mind that she could not doubt what the doctor said to

[160-192]her. She could not think that he was mad,--and yet he spoke of the

[160-193]last two years as though he had suffered from her almost all that a

[160-194]husband could suffer from a wife's misconduct. She was in doubt about

[160-195]his health. "He may recover," the doctor said; "but he is so weak

[160-196]that the slightest additional ailment would take him off." At this

[160-197]time Trevelyan could not raise himself from his bed, and was carried,

[160-198]like a child, from one room to another. He could eat nothing solid,

[160-199]and believed himself to be dying. In spite of his weakness,--and of

[160-200]his savage memories in regard to the past,--he treated his wife on

[160-201]all ordinary subjects with consideration. He spoke much of his money,

[160-202]telling her that he had not altered, and would not alter, the will

[160-203]that he had made immediately on his marriage. Under that will all his

[160-204]property would be hers for her life, and would go to their child when

[160-205]she was dead. To her this will was more than just,--it was generous

[160-206]in the confidence which it placed in her; and he told his lawyer, in

[160-207]her presence, that, to the best of his judgment, he need not change

[160-208]it. But still there passed hardly a day in which he did not make some

[160-209]allusion to the great wrong which he had endured, throwing in her

[160-210]teeth the confessions which she had made,--and almost accusing her

[160-211]of that which she certainly never had confessed, even when, in the

[160-212]extremity of her misery at Casalunga, she had thought that it little

[160-213]mattered what she said, so that for the moment he might be appeased.

[160-214]If he died, was he to die in this belief? If he lived, was he to live

[160-215]in this belief? And if he did so believe, was it possible that he

[160-216]should still trust her with his money and with his child?
