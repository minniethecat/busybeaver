# Social Norms

Social norms — the unwritten yet powerfully enforced codes of respectable behaviour — operate in *He Knew He Was Right* as a kind of invisible legislature, constraining character choices as decisively as any Act of Parliament. The novel's central conflict arises not from a breach of law but from a perceived breach of propriety, and the tragedy unfolds through society's enforcement of norms that neither protagonist can escape.

## Overview

Victorian social norms governed every aspect of middle-class life: visiting hours, forms of address, chaperonage, correspondence between unmarried persons, and — most consequentially for this novel — the conduct of married women. A reputation, once lost, could not be recovered. Trollope's novel anatomises how these norms, internalised as conscience, became instruments of both order and cruelty.

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian cult of respectability was sustained by a dense network of social surveillance: neighbours, relatives, servants, and acquaintances all participated in monitoring and enforcing norms. Lady Milborough, who first alerts Trevelyan to Colonel Osborne's visits, performs precisely this function — she is the voice of society, and her warning, however well-meant, sets the tragedy in motion. The distinction between "county society and town society" (011-146 to 011-147) that the novel draws reflects the nuanced geography of Victorian social stratification.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Propriety as Weapon

The accusation against Emily Trevelyan is never one of adultery — it is of *impropriety*. Colonel Osborne's visits are inappropriate not because anything improper occurs during them, but because a married woman receiving a single gentleman alone creates the *appearance* of impropriety. Emily's insistence on her innocence misses the point: in the court of social opinion, the appearance is the offence. Her sister Nora warns her early that she risks "forfeiting your reputation as an honest woman" (006-064).

### The Social Spiral

Once gossip begins, it proves impossible to contain. Each party's insistence on their own rectitude — Emily on her innocence, Trevelyan on his right to command — hardens the other's position. Society, which might have mediated, instead takes sides: Lady Milborough, the Stanburys, and the Exeter set all form judgments that constrain the principals' room for manoeuvre. The "recognised usages of society" (018-043) prove to be a trap: obey them and Emily appears guilty; defy them and she confirms her guilt.

### Respectability and Its Costs

The novel shows respectability's psychological costs with particular clarity in the character of Dorothy Stanbury, whose life is governed by "the strictest propriety" (016-004). Her constant self-monitoring — asking herself whether she "should behave herself with propriety" (026-122) — illustrates how thoroughly social norms had been internalised, becoming a form of self-policing that preceded any external judgment.

## Key Examples

### The Visiting Protocol

The novel's opening crisis turns on a point of visiting etiquette. Colonel Osborne, an old family friend, calls on Emily while Trevelyan is away. By the norms of the day, this is questionable; by the norms as enforced by Lady Milborough, it is scandalous. The entire tragedy proceeds from this collision between innocent intent and social construction.

### The Exeter Network

Miss Stanbury's Exeter represents a closed social world in which reputation is the primary currency. Aunt Stanbury's power derives not from her wealth alone but from her mastery of social norms — she knows exactly what "propriety" requires and enforces it ruthlessly on her niece Dorothy and nephew Hugh.
