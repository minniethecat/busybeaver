## Section 17

[017-001]He thought a good deal about it on that evening, and was still

[017-002]thinking about it on the following morning. He had promised to go up

[017-003]to Curzon Street on the Monday,--really on some most trivial mission,

[017-004]on a matter of business which no man could have taken in hand whose

[017-005]time was of the slightest value to himself or any one else. But now

[017-006]that mission assumed an importance in his eyes, and seemed to require

[017-007]either a special observance or a special excuse. There was no real

[017-008]reason why he should not have stayed away from Curzon Street for the

[017-009]next fortnight; and had he done so he need have made no excuse to

[017-010]Mrs. Trevelyan when he met her. But the opportunity for a little

[017-011]excitement was not to be missed, and instead of going he wrote to her

[017-012]the following note:--

[017-013]Albany, Monday.

[017-014]DEAR EMILY,

[017-015]What was it all about yesterday? I was to have come up

[017-016]with the words of that opera, but perhaps it will be

[017-017]better to send it. If it be not wicked, do tell me whether

[017-018]I am to consider myself as a banished man. I thought that

[017-019]our little meetings were so innocent,--and so pleasant!

[017-020]The green-eyed monster is of all monsters the most

[017-021]monstrous,--and the most unreasonable. Pray let me have a

[017-022]line, if it be not forbidden.

[017-023]Yours always heartily,

[017-024]F. O.

[017-025]Putting aside all joking, I beg you to remember that I

[017-026]consider myself always entitled to be regarded by you as

[017-027]your most sincere friend.

[017-028]When this was brought to Mrs. Trevelyan, about twelve o'clock in

[017-029]the day, she had already undergone the infliction of those words

[017-030]of wisdom which her husband had prepared for her, and which were

[017-031]threatened at the close of the last chapter. Her husband had come

[017-032]up to her while she was yet in her bed-room, and had striven hard

[017-033]to prevail against her. But his success had been very doubtful. In

[017-034]regard to the number of words, Mrs. Trevelyan certainly had had

[017-035]the best of it. As far as any understanding, one of another, was

[017-036]concerned, the conversation had been useless. She believed herself to

[017-037]be injured and aggrieved, and would continue so to assert, let him

[017-038]implore her to listen to him as loudly as he might. "Yes;--I will

[017-039]listen, and I will obey you," she had said, "but I will not endure

[017-040]such insults without telling you that I feel them." Then he had left

[017-041]her, fully conscious that he had failed, and went forth out of his

[017-042]house into the City, to his club, to wander about the streets, not

[017-043]knowing what he had best do to bring back that state of tranquillity

[017-044]at home which he felt to be so desirable.

[017-045]Mrs. Trevelyan was alone when [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s note was brought to

[017-046]her, and was at that moment struggling with herself in anger against

[017-047]her husband. If he laid any command upon her, she would execute it;

[017-048]but she would never cease to tell him that he had ill-used her. She

[017-049]would din it into his ears, let him come to her as often as he might

[017-050]with his wise words. Wise words! What was the use of wise words when

[017-051]a man was such a fool in nature? And as for Colonel Osborne,--she

[017-052]would see him if he came to her three times a day, unless her husband

[017-053]gave some clearly intelligible order to the contrary. She was

[017-054]fortifying her mind with this resolution when Colonel Osborne's

[017-055]letter was brought to her. She asked whether any servant was waiting

[017-056]for an answer. No,--the servant, who had left it, had gone at once.

[017-057]She read the note, and sat working, with it before her, for a quarter

[017-058]of an hour; and then walked over to her desk and answered it.

[017-059]MY DEAR COLONEL OSBORNE,

[017-060]It will be best to say nothing whatever about the

[017-061]occurrence of yesterday; and if possible, not to

[017-062]think of it. As far as I am concerned, I wish for no

[017-063]change;--except that people should be more reasonable.

[017-064]You can call of course whenever you please; and I am very

[017-065]grateful for your expression of friendship.

[017-066]Yours most sincerely,

[017-067]EMILY TREVELYAN.

[017-068]Thanks for the words of the opera.

[017-069]When she had written this, being determined that all should be open

[017-070]and above board, she put a penny stamp on the envelope, and desired

[017-071]that the letter should be posted. But she destroyed that which she

[017-072]had received from Colonel Osborne. In all things she would act as she

[017-073]would have done if her husband had not been so foolish, and there

[017-074]could have been no reason why she should have kept so unimportant a

[017-075]communication.

[017-076]In the course of the day Trevelyan passed through the hall to the

[017-077]room which he himself was accustomed to occupy behind the parlour,

[017-078]and as he did so saw the note lying ready to be posted, took it up,

[017-079]and read the address. He held it for a moment in his hand, then

[017-080]replaced it on the hall table, and passed on. When he reached his own

[017-081]table he sat down hurriedly, and took up in his hand some Review that

[017-082]was lying ready for him to read. But he was quite unable to fix his

[017-083]mind upon the words before him. He had spoken to his wife on that

[017-084]morning in the strongest language he could use as to the unseemliness

[017-085]of her intimacy with Colonel Osborne; and then, the first thing she

[017-086]had done when his back was turned was to write to this very Colonel

[017-087]Osborne, and tell him, no doubt, what had occurred between her and

[017-088]her husband. He sat thinking of it all for many minutes. He would

[017-089]probably have declared himself that he had thought of it for an hour

[017-090]as he sat there. Then he got up, went up-stairs and walked slowly

[017-091]into the drawing-room. There he found his wife sitting with her

[017-092]sister. "Nora," he said, "I want to speak to Emily. Will you forgive

[017-093]me, if I ask you to leave us for a few minutes?" Nora, with an

[017-094]anxious look at Emily, got up and left the room.

[017-095]"Why do you send her away?" said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[017-096]"Because I wish to be alone with you for a few minutes. Since what I

[017-097]said to you this morning, you have written to Colonel Osborne."

[017-098]"Yes;--I have. I do not know how you have found it out; but I suppose

[017-099]you keep a watch on me."

[017-100]"I keep no watch on you. As I came into the house, I saw your letter

[017-101]lying in the hall."

[017-102]"Very well. You could have read it if you pleased."

[017-103]"Emily, this matter is becoming very serious, and I strongly advise

[017-104]you to be on your guard in what you say. I will bear much for you,

[017-105]and much for our boy; but I will not bear to have my name made a

[017-106]reproach."

[017-107]"Sir, if you think your name is shamed by me, we had better part,"

[017-108]said Mrs. Trevelyan, rising from her chair, and confronting him with

[017-109]a look before which his own almost quailed.

[017-110]"It may be that we had better part," he said, slowly. "But in the

[017-111]first place I wish you to tell me what were the contents of that

[017-112]letter."

[017-113]"If it was there when you came in, no doubt it is there still. Go and

[017-114]look at it."

[017-115]"That is no answer to me. I have desired you to tell me what are its

[017-116]contents."

[017-117]"I shall not tell you. I will not demean myself by repeating anything

[017-118]so insignificant in my own justification. If you suspect me of

[017-119]writing what I should not write, you will suspect me also of lying to

[017-120]conceal it."

[017-121]"Have you heard from Colonel Osborne this morning?"

[017-122]"I have."

[017-123]"And where is his letter?"

[017-124]"I have destroyed it."

[017-125]Again he paused, trying to think what he had better do, trying to be

[017-126]calm. And she stood still opposite to him, confronting him with the

[017-127]scorn of her bright angry eyes. Of course, he was not calm. He was

[017-128]the very reverse of calm. "And you refuse to tell me what you wrote,"

[017-129]he said.

[017-130]"The letter is there," she answered, pointing away towards the door.

[017-131]"If you want to play the spy, go and look at it for yourself."

[017-132]"Do you call me a spy?"

[017-133]"And what have you called me? Because you are a husband, is the

[017-134]privilege of vituperation to be all on your side?"

[017-135]"It is impossible that I should put up with this," he said;--"quite

[017-136]impossible. This would kill me. Anything is better than this. My

[017-137]present orders to you are not to see Colonel Osborne, not to write

[017-138]to him or have any communication with him, and to put under cover to

[017-139]me, unopened, any letter that may come from him. I shall expect your

[017-140]implicit obedience to these orders."

[017-141]"Well;--go on."

[017-142]"Have I your promise?"

[017-143]"No;--no. You have no promise. I will make no promise exacted from me

[017-144]in so disgraceful a manner."

[017-145]"You refuse to obey me?"

[017-146]"I will refuse nothing, and will promise nothing."

[017-147]"Then we must part;--that is all. I will take care that you shall

[017-148]hear from me before to-morrow morning."

[017-149]So saying, he left the room, and, passing through the hall, saw that

[017-150]the letter had been taken away.

[017-151]CHAPTER XI.

[017-152]LADY MILBOROUGH AS AMBASSADOR.

[017-153]"Of course, I know you are right," said Nora to her sister;--"right

[017-154]as far as Colonel Osborne is concerned; but nevertheless you ought to

[017-155]give way."

[017-156]"And be trampled upon?" said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[017-157]"Yes; and be trampled upon, if he should trample on you;--which,

[017-158]however, he is the last man in the world to do."

[017-159]"And to endure any insult and any names? You yourself--you would be a

[017-160]Griselda, I suppose."

[017-161]"I don't want to talk about myself," said Nora, "nor about Griselda.

[017-162]But I know that, however unreasonable it may seem, you had better

[017-163]give way to him now and tell him what there was in the note to

[017-164]Colonel Osborne."

[017-165]"Never! He has ordered me not to see him or to write to him, or to

[017-166]open his letters,--having, mind you, ordered just the reverse a day

[017-167]or two before; and I will obey him. Absurd as it is, I will obey him.

[017-168]But as for submitting to him, and letting him suppose that I think

[017-169]he is right;--never! I should be lying to him then, and I will never

[017-170]lie to him. He has said that we must part, and I suppose it will be

[017-171]better so. How can a woman live with a man that suspects her? He

[017-172]cannot take my baby from me."

[017-173]There were many such conversations as the above between the two

[017-174]sisters before Mrs. Trevelyan received from her husband the

[017-175]communication with which she had been threatened. And Nora, acting on

[017-176]her own judgment in the matter, made an attempt to see Mr. Trevelyan,

[017-177]writing to him a pretty little note, and beseeching him to be kind to

[017-178]her. But he declined to see her, and the two women sat at home, with

[017-179]the baby between them, holding such pleasant conversations as that

[017-180]above narrated. When such tempests occur in a family, a woman will

[017-181]generally suffer the least during the thick of the tempest. While

[017-182]the hurricane is at the fiercest, she will be sustained by the most

[017-183]thorough conviction that the right is on her side, that she is

[017-184]aggrieved, that there is nothing for her to acknowledge, and no

[017-185]position that she need surrender. Whereas her husband will desire a

[017-186]compromise, even amidst the violence of the storm. But afterwards,

[017-187]when the wind has lulled, but while the heavens around are still all

[017-188]black and murky, then the woman's sufferings begin. When passion

[017-189]gives way to thought and memory, she feels the loneliness of her

[017-190]position,--the loneliness, and the possible degradation. It is all

[017-191]very well for a man to talk about his name and his honour; but it is

[017-192]the woman's honour and the woman's name that are, in truth, placed in

[017-193]jeopardy. Let the woman do what she will, the man can, in truth, show

[017-194]his face in the world;--and, after awhile, does show his face. But

[017-195]the woman may be compelled to veil hers, either by her own fault, or

[017-196]by his. Mrs. Trevelyan was now told that she was to be separated from

[017-197]her husband, and she did not, at any rate, believe that she had done

[017-198]any harm. But, if such separation did come, where could she live,

[017-199]what could she do, what position in the world would she possess?

[017-200]Would not her face be, in truth, veiled as effectually as though she

[017-201]had disgraced herself and her husband?
