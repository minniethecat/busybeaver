## Section 103

[103-001]And there were other troubles which fell very heavily upon the poor

[103-002]governor, who had come home as it were for a holiday, and who was a

[103-003]man hating work naturally, and who, from the circumstances of his

[103-004]life, had never been called on to do much work. A man may govern the

[103-005]Mandarins and yet live in comparative idleness. To do such governing

[103-006]work well a man should have a good presence, a flow of words which

[103-007]should mean nothing, an excellent temper, and a love of hospitality.

[103-008]With these attributes Sir Rowley was endowed; for, though his

[103-009]disposition was by nature hot, for governing purposes it had been

[103-010]brought by practice under good control. He had now been summoned

[103-011]home through the machinations of his dangerous old friend Colonel

[103-012]Osborne, in order that he might give the results of his experience in

[103-013]governing before a committee of the House of Commons. In coming to

[103-014]England on this business he had thought much more of his holiday, of

[103-015]his wife and children, of his daughters at home, of his allowance per

[103-016]day while he was to be away from his government, and of his salary to

[103-017]be paid to him entire during his absence, instead of being halved as

[103-018]it would be if he were away on leave,--he had thought much more in

[103-019]coming home on these easy and pleasant matters, than he did on the

[103-020]work that was to be required from him when he arrived. And then it

[103-021]came to pass that he felt himself almost injured when the Colonial

[103-022]Office demanded his presence from day to day, and when clerks

[103-023]bothered him with questions as to which they expected ready replies,

[103-024]but in replying to which Sir Marmaduke was by no means ready. The

[103-025]working men at the Colonial Office had not quite thought that Sir

[103-026]Marmaduke was the most fitting man for the job in hand. There was a

[103-027]certain Mr. Thomas Smith at another set of islands in quite another

[103-028]part of the world, who was supposed by these working men at home to

[103-029]be a very paragon of a governor. If he had been had home,--so said

[103-030]the working men,--no Committee of the House would have been able to

[103-031]make anything of him. They might have asked him questions week after

[103-032]week, and he would have answered them all fluently and would have

[103-033]committed nobody. He knew all the ins and outs of governing,--did

[103-034]Mr. Thomas Smith,--and was a match for the sharpest Committee that

[103-035]ever sat at Westminster. Poor Sir Marmaduke was a man of a very

[103-036]different sort; all of which was known by the working men; but

[103-037]the Parliamentary interest had been too strong, and here was Sir

[103-038]Marmaduke at home. But the working men were not disposed to make

[103-039]matters so pleasant for Sir Marmaduke, as Sir Marmaduke had expected.

[103-040]The Committee would not examine Sir Marmaduke till after Easter, in

[103-041]the middle of April; but it was expected of him that he should read

[103-042]blue-books without number, and he was so catechised by the working

[103-043]men that he almost began to wish himself back at the Mandarins. In

[103-044]this way the new establishment in Manchester Street was not at first

[103-045]in a happy or even in a contented condition.

[103-046]At last, after about ten days, Lady Rowley did succeed in obtaining

[103-047]an interview with Trevelyan. A meeting was arranged through Bozzle,

[103-048]and took place in a very dark and gloomy room at an inn in the City.

[103-049]Why Bozzle should have selected the Bremen Coffee House, in Poulter's

[103-050]Alley, for this meeting no fit reason can surely be given, unless

[103-051]it was that he conceived himself bound to select the most dreary

[103-052]locality within his [[truth|knowledge]] on so melancholy an occasion. Poulter's

[103-053]Alley is a narrow dark passage somewhere behind the Mansion House;

[103-054]and the Bremen Coffee House,--why so called no one can now tell,--is

[103-055]one of those strange houses of public resort in the City at which

[103-056]the guests seem never to eat, never to drink, never to sleep, but to

[103-057]come in and out after a mysterious and almost ghostly fashion, seeing

[103-058]their friends,--or perhaps their enemies, in nooks and corners, and

[103-059]carrying on their conferences in low, melancholy whispers. There is

[103-060]an aged waiter at the Bremen Coffee House; and there is certainly

[103-061]one private sitting-room up-stairs. It was a dingy, ill-furnished

[103-062]room, with an old large mahogany table, an old horse-hair sofa, six

[103-063]horse-hair chairs, two old round mirrors, and an old mahogany press

[103-064]in a corner. It was a chamber so sad in its appearance that no

[103-065]wholesome useful work could have been done within it; nor could men

[103-066]have eaten there with any appetite, or have drained the flowing bowl

[103-067]with any touch of joviality. It was generally used for such purposes

[103-068]as that to which it was now appropriated, and no doubt had been

[103-069]taken by Bozzle on more than one previous occasion. Here Lady Rowley

[103-070]arrived precisely at the hour fixed, and was told that the [[social-class|gentleman]]

[103-071]was waiting up-stairs for her.

[103-072]There had, of course, been many family consultations as to the manner

[103-073]in which this meeting should be arranged. Should Sir Marmaduke

[103-074]accompany his wife;--or, perhaps, should Sir Marmaduke go alone? Lady

[103-075]Rowley had been very much in favour of meeting Mr. Trevelyan without

[103-076]any one to assist her in the conference. As for Sir Marmaduke, no

[103-077]meeting could be concluded between him and his son-in-law without a

[103-078]personal, and probably a violent quarrel. Of that Lady Rowley had

[103-079]been quite sure. Sir Marmaduke, since he had been home, had, in the

[103-080]midst of his various troubles, been driven into so vehement a state

[103-081]of indignation against his son-in-law as to be unable to speak of

[103-082]the wretched man without strongest terms of opprobrium. Nothing was

[103-083]too bad to be said by him of one who had ill-treated his dearest

[103-084]daughter. It must be admitted that Sir Marmaduke had heard only

[103-085]one side of the question. He had questioned his daughter, and had

[103-086]constantly seen his old friend Osborne. The Colonel's journey down

[103-087]to Devonshire had been made to appear the most natural proceeding

[103-088]in the world. The correspondence of which Trevelyan thought so much

[103-089]had been shown to consist of such notes as might pass between any

[103-090]old gentleman and any young woman. The promise which Trevelyan had

[103-091]endeavoured to exact, and which Mrs. Trevelyan had declined to give,

[103-092]appeared to the angry father to be a monstrous insult. He knew that

[103-093]the Colonel was an older man than himself, and his Emily was still to

[103-094]him only a young girl. It was incredible to him that anybody should

[103-095]have regarded his old comrade as his daughter's lover. He did not

[103-096]believe that anybody had, in truth, so regarded the man. The tale had

[103-097]been a monstrous invention on the part of the husband, got up because

[103-098]he had become tired of his young wife. According to Sir Marmaduke's

[103-099]way of thinking, Trevelyan should either be thrashed within an inch

[103-100]of his life, or else locked up in a mad-house. [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] shook

[103-101]his head, and expressed a conviction that the poor man was mad.

[103-102]But Lady Rowley was more hopeful. Though she was as confident about

[103-103]her daughter as was the father, she was less confident about the old

[103-104]friend. She, probably, was alive to the fact that a man of fifty

[103-105]might put on the airs and assume the [[honour|character]] of a young lover;

[103-106]and acting on that suspicion, entertaining also some hope that bad

[103-107]as matters now were they might be mended, she had taken care that

[103-108]Colonel Osborne and Mrs. Trevelyan should not be brought together.

[103-109]Sir Marmaduke had fumed, but Lady Rowley had been firm. "If you think

[103-110]so, mamma," Mrs. Trevelyan had said, with something of scorn in her

[103-111]tone,--"of course let it be so." Lady Rowley had said that it would

[103-112]be better so; and the two had not seen each other since the memorable

[103-113]visit to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]. And now Lady Rowley was about to meet her

[103-114]son-in-law with some slight hope that she might arrange affairs.

[103-115]She was quite aware that present indignation, though certainly a

[103-116]gratification, might be indulged in at much too great a cost. It

[103-117]would be better for all reasons that Emily should go back to her

[103-118]husband and her home, and that Trevelyan should be forgiven for his

[103-119]iniquities.

[103-120]Bozzle was at the tavern during the interview, but he was not seen

[103-121]by Lady Rowley. He remained seated down-stairs, in one of the dingy

[103-122]corners, ready to give assistance to his patron should assistance be

[103-123]needed. When Lady Rowley was shown into the gloomy sitting-room by

[103-124]the old waiter, she found Trevelyan alone, [[reputation|standing]] in the middle of

[103-125]the room, and waiting for her. "This is a sad occasion," he said, as

[103-126]he advanced to give her his hand.

[103-127]"A very sad occasion, Louis."

[103-128]"I do not know what you may have heard of what has occurred, Lady

[103-129]Rowley. It is natural, however, to suppose that you must have heard

[103-130]me spoken of with censure."

[103-131]"I think my child has been ill used, Louis," she replied.

[103-132]"Of course you do. I could not expect that it should be otherwise.

[103-133]When it was arranged that I should meet you here, I was quite aware

[103-134]that you would have taken the side against me before you had heard

[103-135]my story. It is I that have been ill used,--cruelly misused; but I

[103-136]do not expect that you should believe me. I do not wish you to do.

[103-137]I would not for worlds separate the mother from her daughter."

[103-138]"But why have you separated your own wife from her child?"

[103-139]"Because it was my duty. What! Is a father not to have the charge

[103-140]of his own son? I have done nothing, Lady Rowley, to justify a

[103-141]separation which is contrary to the laws of nature."

[103-142]"Where is the boy, Louis?"

[103-143]"Ah;--that is just what I am not prepared to tell any one who has

[103-144]taken my wife's side till I know that my wife has consented to pay to

[103-145]me that obedience which I, as her husband, have a right to demand. If

[103-146]Emily will do as I request of her, as I command her,"--as Trevelyan

[103-147]said this, he spoke in a tone which was intended to give the highest

[103-148]possible idea of his own authority and [[pride|dignity]],--"then she may see

[103-149]her child without delay."

[103-150]"What is it you request of my daughter?"

[103-151]"Obedience;--simply that. Submission to my will, which is surely a

[103-152]wife's duty. Let her beg my pardon for what has occurred,--"

[103-153]"She cannot do that, Louis."

[103-154]"And solemnly promise me," continued Trevelyan, not deigning to

[103-155]notice Lady Rowley's interruption, "that she will hold no further

[103-156]intercourse with that snake in the grass who wormed his way into my

[103-157]house,--let her be humble, and penitent, and affectionate, and then

[103-158]she shall be restored to her husband and to her child." He said this

[103-159]walking up and down the room, and waving his hand, as though he were

[103-160]making a speech that was intended to be eloquent,--as though he had

[103-161]conceived that he was to overcome his mother-in-law by the weight

[103-162]of his words and the magnificence of his demeanour. And yet his

[103-163]demeanour was ridiculous, and his words would have had no weight had

[103-164]they not tended to show Lady Rowley how little prospect there was

[103-165]that she should be able to heal this breach. He himself, too, was so

[103-166]altered in appearance since she had last seen him, bright with the

[103-167]hopes of his young married happiness, that she would hardly have

[103-168]recognised him had she met him in the street. He was thin, and pale,

[103-169]and haggard, and mean. And as he stalked up and down the room, it

[103-170]seemed to her that the very [[reputation|character]] of the man was changed. She

[103-171]had not previously known him to be pompous, unreasonable, and absurd.

[103-172]She did not answer him at once, as she perceived that he had not

[103-173]finished his address;--and, after a moment's pause, he continued.

[103-174]"Lady Rowley, there is nothing I would not have done for your

[103-175]daughter,--for my wife. All that I had was hers. I did not dictate to

[103-176]her any mode of life; I required from her no sacrifices; I subjected

[103-177]her to no caprices; but I was determined to be master in my own

[103-178]house."
