## Section 126

[126-001]"How many of us are there who never know whether we sell ourselves

[126-002]or not?" said Wallachia. "The senator who longs for office, and who

[126-003]votes this way instead of that in order that he may get it, thinks

[126-004]that he is voting honestly. The minister who calls himself a teacher

[126-005]of God's word, thinks that it is God's word that he preaches when

[126-006]he strains his lungs to fill his church. The question is this,

[126-007]Caroline;--would you have loved the same man had he come to you with

[126-008]a woodman's axe in his hand or a clerk's quill behind his ear? I

[126-009]guess not."

[126-010]"As to the woodman's axe, Wally, it is very well in theory; but--"

[126-011]"Things good in theory, Caroline, will be good also when practised.

[126-012]You may be sure of that. We dislike theory simply because our

[126-013]intelligences are higher than our wills. But we will let that pass."

[126-014]"Pray let it pass, Wally. Do not preach me sermons to-night. I am so

[126-015]happy, and you ought to wish me joy."

[126-016]"If wishing you joy would get you joy, I would wish it you while I

[126-017]lived. I cannot be happy that you should be taken from us whither I

[126-018]shall never see you again."

[126-019]"But you are to come to us. I have told him so, and it is settled."

[126-020]"No, dear; I shall not do that. What should I be in the glittering

[126-021]halls of an English baron? Could there be any visiting less fitting,

[126-022]any admixture less appropriate? Could I who have held up my voice

[126-023]in the Music Hall of Lacedaemon, amidst the glories of the West, in

[126-024]the great and free State of Illinois, against the corruption of an

[126-025]English aristocracy,--could I, who have been listened to by two

[126-026]thousand of my countrywomen,--and men,--while I spurned the unmanly,

[126-027]inhuman errors of primogeniture,--could I, think you, hold my tongue

[126-028]beneath the roof of a feudal lord!" Caroline Spalding knew that her

[126-029]friend could not hold her tongue, and hesitated to answer. There had

[126-030]been that fatal triumph of a lecture on the joint rights of men and

[126-031]women, and it had rendered poor Wallachia Petrie unfit for ordinary

[126-032]society.

[126-033]"You might come there without talking politics, Wally," said

[126-034]Caroline.

[126-035]"No, Caroline; no. I will go into the house of no man in which the

[126-036]free expression of my opinion is debarred me. I will not sit even

[126-037]at your table with a muzzled tongue. When you are gone, Caroline,

[126-038]I shall devote myself to what, after all, must be the work of my

[126-039]life, and I shall finish the biographical history of our great

[126-040]hero in verse,--which I hope may at least be not ephemeral. From

[126-041]month to month I shall send you what I do, and you will not refuse

[126-042]me your friendly criticism,--and, perhaps, some slight meed of

[126-043]approbation,--because you are dwelling beneath the shade of a throne.

[126-044]Oh, Caroline, let it not be a upas tree!"

[126-045]The Miss Petries of the world have this advantage,--an advantage

[126-046]which rarely if ever falls to the lot of a man,--that they are never

[126-047]convinced of error. Men, let them be ever so much devoted to their

[126-048]closets, let them keep their work ever so closely veiled from public

[126-049]scrutiny, still find themselves subjected to criticism, and under

[126-050]the necessity of either defending themselves or of succumbing. If,

[126-051]indeed, a man neither speaks, nor writes,--if he be dumb as regards

[126-052]opinion,--he passes simply as one of the crowd, and is in the way

[126-053]neither of convincing nor of being convinced; but a woman may speak,

[126-054]and almost write, as she likes, without danger of being wounded by

[126-055]sustained conflict. Who would have the courage to begin with such a

[126-056]one as Miss Petrie, and endeavour to prove to her that she is wrong

[126-057]from the beginning? A little word of half-dissent, a smile, a shrug,

[126-058]and an ambiguous compliment which is misunderstood, are all the forms

[126-059]of argument which can be used against her. Wallachia Petrie, in

[126-060]her heart of hearts, conceived that she had fairly discussed her

[126-061]great projects from year to year with indomitable eloquence and

[126-062]unanswerable truth,--and that none of her opponents had had a leg

[126-063]to stand upon. And this she believed because the chivalry of men

[126-064]had given to her sex that protection against which her life was one

[126-065]continued protest.

[126-066]"Here he is," said Caroline, as Mr. Glascock came up to them. "Try

[126-067]and say a civil word to him, if he speaks about it. Though he is to

[126-068]be a lord, still he is a man and a brother."

[126-069]"Caroline," said the stern monitress, "you are already learning to

[126-070]laugh at principles which have been dear to you since you left your

[126-071]mother's breast. Alas, how true it is, 'You cannot touch pitch and

[126-072]not be defiled.'"

[126-073]The further progress of these friendly and feminine amenities was

[126-074]stopped by the presence of the [[social-class|gentleman]] who had occasioned them.

[126-075]"Miss Petrie," said the hero of the hour, "Caroline was to tell you

[126-076]of my good [[money|fortune]], and no doubt she has done so."

[126-077]"I cannot wait to hear the pretty things he has to say," said

[126-078]Caroline, "and I must look after my aunt's guests. There is poor

[126-079]Signor Buonarosci without a soul to say a syllable to him, and I must

[126-080]go and use my ten Italian words."

[126-081]"You are about to take with you to your old country, Mr. Glascock,"

[126-082]said Miss Petrie, "one of the brightest stars in our young American

[126-083]firmament." There could be no doubt, from the tone of Miss Petrie's

[126-084]voice, that she now regarded this star, however bright, as one of a

[126-085]sort which is subjected to falling.

[126-086]"I am going to take a very nice young woman," said Mr. Glascock.

[126-087]"I hate that word woman, sir, uttered with the half-hidden sneer

[126-088]which always accompanies its expression from the mouth of a man."

[126-089]"Sneer, Miss Petrie!"

[126-090]"I quite allow that it is involuntary, and not analysed or understood

[126-091]by yourselves. If you speak of a dog, you intend to do so with

[126-092]affection, but there is always contempt mixed with it. The so-called

[126-093]chivalry of man to woman is all begotten in the same spirit. I want

[126-094]no favour, but I claim to be your equal."

[126-095]"I thought that American ladies were generally somewhat exacting as

[126-096]to those privileges which chivalry gives them."

[126-097]"It is true, sir, that the only rank we know in our country is in

[126-098]that precedence which man gives to woman. Whether we maintain that,

[126-099]or whether we abandon it, we do not intend to purchase it at the

[126-100]price of an acknowledgment of intellectual inferiority. For myself, I

[126-101]hate chivalry;--what you call chivalry. I can carry my own chair, and

[126-102]I claim the right to carry it whithersoever I may please."

[126-103]Mr. Glascock remained with her for some time, but made no opportunity

[126-104]for giving that invitation to Monkhams of which Caroline had spoken.

[126-105]As he said afterwards, he found it impossible to expect her to attend

[126-106]to any subject so trivial; and when, afterwards, Caroline told him,

[126-107]with some slight mirth,--the capability of which on such a subject

[126-108]was coming to her with her new ideas of life,--that, though he was

[126-109]partly saved as a man and a brother, still he was partly the reverse

[126-110]as a feudal lord, he began to reflect that Wallachia Petrie would be

[126-111]a guest with whom he would find it very difficult to make things go

[126-112]pleasant at Monkhams. "Does she not bully you horribly?" he asked.

[126-113]"Of course she bullies me," Caroline answered; "and I cannot expect

[126-114]you to understand as yet how it is that I love her and like her; but

[126-115]I do. If I were in distress to-morrow, she would give everything she

[126-116]has in the world to put me right."

[126-117]"So would I," said he.

[126-118]"Ah, you;--that is a matter of course. That is your business now.

[126-119]And she would give everything she has in the world to set the world

[126-120]right. Would you do that?"

[126-121]"It would depend on the amount of my faith. If I could believe in the

[126-122]result, I suppose I should do it."

[126-123]"She would do it on the slightest hope that such giving would have

[126-124]any tendency that way. Her philanthropy is all real. Of course she is

[126-125]a bore to you."

[126-126]"I am very patient."

[126-127]"I hope I shall find you so,--always. And, of course, she is

[126-128]ridiculous--in your eyes. I have learned to see it, and to regret it;

[126-129]but I shall never cease to love her."

[126-130]"I have not the slightest objection. Her lessons will come from over

[126-131]the water, and mine will come from--where shall I say?--over the

[126-132]table. If I can't talk her down with so much advantage on my side, I

[126-133]ought to be made a woman's-right man myself."

[126-134]Poor Lady Rowley had watched Miss Petrie and Mr. Glascock during

[126-135]those moments that they had been together, and had half believed the

[126-136]rumour, and had half doubted, thinking in the moments of her belief

[126-137]that Mr. Glascock must be mad, and in the moments of unbelief that

[126-138]the rumours had been set afloat by the English Minister's wife with

[126-139]the express intention of turning Mr. Glascock into ridicule. It had

[126-140]never occurred to her to doubt that Wallachia was the eldest of that

[126-141]family of nieces. Could it be possible that a man who had known

[126-142]her Nora, who had undoubtedly loved her Nora,--who had travelled

[126-143]all the way from [[london|London]] to [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] to ask Nora to be his

[126-144]wife,--should within twelve months of that time have resolved to

[126-145]marry a woman whom he must have selected simply as being the most

[126-146]opposite to Nora of any female human being that he could find? It was

[126-147]not credible to her; and if it were not true, there might still be a

[126-148]hope. Nora had met him, and had spoken to him, and it had seemed that

[126-149]for a moment or two they had spoken as friends. Lady Rowley, when

[126-150]talking to Mrs. Spalding, had watched them closely; and she had seen

[126-151]that Nora's eyes had been bright, and that there had been something

[126-152]between them which was pleasant. Suddenly she found herself close to

[126-153]Wallachia, and thought that she would trust herself to a word.

[126-154]"Have you been long in Florence?" asked Lady Rowley in her softest

[126-155]voice.

[126-156]"A pretty considerable time, ma'am;--that is, since the fall began."

[126-157]What a voice;--what an accent;--and what words! Was there a man

[126-158]living with sufficient courage to take this woman to England, and

[126-159]shew her to the world as Lady Peterborough?

[126-160]"Are you going to remain in Italy for the summer?" continued Lady

[126-161]Rowley.

[126-162]"I guess I shall;--or, perhaps, locate myself in the purer atmosphere

[126-163]of the Swiss mountains."

[126-164]"Switzerland in summer must certainly be much pleasanter."

[126-165]"I was thinking at the moment of the political atmosphere," said Miss

[126-166]Petrie; "for although, certainly, much has been done in this country

[126-167]in the way of striking off shackles and treading sceptres under foot,

[126-168]still, Lady Rowley, there remains here that pernicious thing,--a

[126-169]king. The feeling of the dominion of a single man,--and that of a

[126-170]single woman is, for aught I know, worse,--with me so clouds the air,

[126-171]that the breath I breathe fails to fill my lungs." Wallachia, as she

[126-172]said this, put forth her hand, and raised her chin, and extended

[126-173]her arm. She paused, feeling that justice demanded that Lady Rowley

[126-174]should have a right of reply. But Lady Rowley had not a word to say,

[126-175]and Wallachia Petrie went on. "I cannot adapt my body to the sweet

[126-176]savours and the soft luxuries of the outer world with any comfort to

[126-177]my inner self, while the circumstances of the society around me are

[126-178]oppressive to my spirit. When our war was raging all around me I was

[126-179]light-spirited as the lark that mounts through the morning sky."
