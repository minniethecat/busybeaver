## Section 27

[027-001]Mrs. Crocket's boy, though he was only about three feet high, was a

[027-002]miracle of skill and discretion. He used the machine, as the patent

[027-003]drag is called, in going down the hills with the utmost care. He

[027-004]never forced the beast beyond a walk if there was the slightest rise

[027-005]in the ground; and as there was always a rise, the journey was slow.

[027-006]But the three ladies enjoyed it thoroughly, and Mrs. Trevelyan was in

[027-007]better spirits than she herself had thought to be possible for her

[027-008]in her present condition. Most of us have recognised the fact that

[027-009]a dram of spirits will create,--that a so-called nip of brandy will

[027-010]create hilarity, or, at least, alacrity, and that a glass of sherry

[027-011]will often "pick up" and set in order the prostrate animal and mental

[027-012]faculties of the drinker. But we are not sufficiently alive to

[027-013]the fact that copious draughts of fresh air,--of air fresh and

[027-014]unaccustomed,--will have precisely the same effect. We do know that

[027-015]now and again it is very essential to "change the air;" but we

[027-016]generally consider that to do that with any chance of advantage, it

[027-017]is necessary to go far afield; and we think also that such change of

[027-018]the air is only needful when sickness of the body has come upon us,

[027-019]or when it threatens to come. We are seldom aware that we may imbibe

[027-020]long potations of pleasure and healthy excitement without perhaps

[027-021]going out of our own county; that such potations are within a day's

[027-022]journey of most of us; and that they are to be had for half-a-crown

[027-023]a head, all expenses told. Mrs. Trevelyan probably did not know that

[027-024]the cloud was lifted off her mind, and the load of her sorrow made

[027-025]light to her, by the special vigour of the air of the Moor; but

[027-026]she did know that she was enjoying herself, and that the world was

[027-027]pleasanter to her than it had been for months past.

[027-028]When they had sat upon their hillocks, and eaten their

[027-029]sandwiches,--regretting that the basket of provisions had not been

[027-030]bigger,--and had drunk their sherry and water out of the little horn

[027-031]mug which Mrs. Crocket had lent them, Nora started off across the

[027-032]moorland alone. The horse had been left to be fed in Princetown, and

[027-033]they had walked back to a bush under which they had rashly left their

[027-034]basket of provender concealed. It happened, however, that on that day

[027-035]there was no escaped felon about to watch what they had done, and

[027-036]the food and the drink had been found secure. Nora had gone off, and

[027-037]as her sister and Priscilla sat leaning against their hillocks with

[027-038]their backs to the road, she could be seen [[reputation|standing]] now on one little

[027-039]eminence and now on another, thinking, doubtless, as she stood on the

[027-040]one how good it would be to be Lady Peterborough, and, as she stood

[027-041]on the other, how much better to be Mrs. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]. Only,--before

[027-042]she could be Mrs. Hugh Stanbury it would be necessary that Mr. Hugh

[027-043]Stanbury should share her opinion,--and necessary also that he should

[027-044]be able to maintain a wife. "I should never do to be a very poor

[027-045]man's wife," she said to herself; and remembered as she said it, that

[027-046]in reference to the prospect of her being Lady Peterborough, the man

[027-047]who was to be Lord Peterborough was at any rate ready to make her his

[027-048]wife, and on that side there were none of those difficulties about

[027-049]house, and money, and position which stood in the way of the Hugh

[027-050]Stanbury side of the question. She was not, she thought, fit to be

[027-051]the wife of a very poor man; but she conceived of herself that she

[027-052]would do very well as a future Lady Peterborough in the drawing-rooms

[027-053]of Monkhams. She was so far vain as to fancy that she could look,

[027-054]and speak, and move, and have her being after the fashion which is

[027-055]approved for the Lady Peterboroughs of the world. It was not clear

[027-056]to her that Nature had not expressly intended her to be a Lady

[027-057]Peterborough; whereas, as far as she could see, Nature had not

[027-058]intended her to be a Mrs. Hugh Stanbury, with a precarious [[money|income]] of

[027-059]perhaps ten guineas a week when journalism was doing well. So she

[027-060]moved on to another little eminence to think of it there. It was

[027-061]clear to her that if she should accept Mr. Glascock she would sell

[027-062]herself, and not give herself away; and she had told herself scores

[027-063]of times before this, that a young woman should give herself away,

[027-064]and not sell herself;--should either give herself away, or keep

[027-065]herself to herself as circumstances might go. She had been quite sure

[027-066]that she would never sell herself. But this was a lesson which she

[027-067]had taught herself when she was very young, before she had come to

[027-068]understand the world and its hard necessities. Nothing, she now told

[027-069]herself, could be worse than to hang like a mill-stone round the neck

[027-070]of a poor man. It might be a very good thing to give herself away for

[027-071]love,--but it would not be a good thing to be the means of ruining

[027-072]the man she loved, even if that man were willing to be so ruined.

[027-073]And then she thought that she could also love that other man a

[027-074]little,--could love him sufficiently for comfortable domestic

[027-075]purposes. And it would undoubtedly be very pleasant to have all the

[027-076]troubles of her life settled for her. If she were Mrs. Glascock,

[027-077]known to the world as the future Lady Peterborough, would it not be

[027-078]within her power to bring her sister and her sister's husband again

[027-079]together? The tribute of the Monkhams' authority and influence to her

[027-080]sister's side of the question would be most salutary. She tried to

[027-081]make herself believe that in this way she would be doing a good deed.

[027-082]Upon the whole, she thought that if Mr. Glascock should give her

[027-083]another chance she would accept him. And he had distinctly promised

[027-084]that he would give her another chance. It might be that this

[027-085]unfortunate quarrel in the Trevelyan family would deter him. People

[027-086]do not wish to ally themselves with family quarrels. But if the

[027-087]chance came in her way she would accept it. She had made up her mind

[027-088]to that, when she turned round from off the last knoll on which she

[027-089]had stood, to return to her sister and Priscilla Stanbury.

[027-090][Illustration: Nora tries to make herself believe.]

[027-091]They two had sat still under the shade of a thorn bush, looking at

[027-092]Nora as she was wandering about, and talking together more freely

[027-093]than they had ever done before on the circumstances that had brought

[027-094]them together. "How pretty she looks," Priscilla had said, as Nora

[027-095]was [[reputation|standing]] with her figure clearly marked by the light.

[027-096]"Yes; she is very pretty, and has been much admired. This terrible

[027-097]affair of mine is a cruel blow to her."

[027-098]"You mean that it is bad for her to come and live here--without

[027-099]society."

[027-100]"Not exactly that,--though of course it would be better for her to go

[027-101]out. And I don't know how a girl is ever to get settled in the world

[027-102]unless she goes out. But it is always an injury to be connected in

[027-103]any way with a woman who is separated from her husband. It must be

[027-104]bad for you."

[027-105]"It won't hurt me," said Priscilla. "Nothing of that kind can hurt

[027-106]me."

[027-107]"I mean that people say such ill-natured things."

[027-108]"I stand alone, and can take care of myself," said Priscilla. "I defy

[027-109]the evil tongues of all the world to hurt me. My personal cares are

[027-110]limited to an old gown and bread and cheese. I like a pair of gloves

[027-111]to go to church with, but that is only the remnant of a prejudice.

[027-112]The world has so very little to give me, that I am pretty nearly sure

[027-113]that it will take nothing away."

[027-114]"And you are contented?"

[027-115]"Well, no; I can't say that I am contented. I hardly think that

[027-116]anybody ought to be contented. Should my mother die and Dorothy

[027-117]remain with my aunt, or get married, I should be utterly alone in the

[027-118]world. Providence, or whatever you call it, has made me a lady after

[027-119]a fashion, so that I can't live with the ploughmen's wives, and at

[027-120]the same time has so used me in other respects, that I can't live

[027-121]with anybody else."

[027-122]"Why should not you get married, as well as Dorothy?"

[027-123]"Who would have me? And if I had a husband I should want a good

[027-124]one,--a man with a head on his shoulders, and a heart. Even if I

[027-125]were young and good-looking, or rich, I doubt whether I could please

[027-126]myself. As it is I am as likely to be taken bodily to heaven, as to

[027-127]become any man's wife."

[027-128]"I suppose most women think so of themselves at some time, and yet

[027-129]they are married."

[027-130]"I am not fit to marry. I am often cross, and I like my own way, and

[027-131]I have a distaste for men. I never in my life saw a man whom I wished

[027-132]even to make my intimate friend. I should think any man an idiot who

[027-133]began to make soft speeches to me, and I should tell him so."

[027-134]"Ah; you might find it different when he went on with it."

[027-135]"But I think," said Priscilla, "that when a woman is married there is

[027-136]nothing to which she should not submit on behalf of her husband."

[027-137]"You mean that for me."

[027-138]"Of course I mean it for you. How should I not be thinking of you,

[027-139]living as you are under the same roof with us? And I am thinking of

[027-140]Louey." Louey was the baby. "What are you to do when after a year or

[027-141]two his father shall send for him to have him under his own care?"

[027-142]"Nothing shall separate me from my child," said Mrs. Trevelyan

[027-143]eagerly.

[027-144]"That is easily said; but I suppose the power of doing as he pleased

[027-145]would be with him."

[027-146]"Why should it be with him? I do not at all know that it would be

[027-147]with him. I have not left his house. It is he that has turned me

[027-148]out."

[027-149]"There can, I think, be very little doubt what you should do," said

[027-150]Priscilla, after a pause, during which she had got up from her seat

[027-151]under the thorn bush.

[027-152]"What should I do?" asked Mrs. Trevelyan.

[027-153]"Go back to him."

[027-154]"I will to-morrow if he will write and ask me. Nay; how could I help

[027-155]myself? I am his creature, and must go or come as he bids me. I am

[027-156]here only because he has sent me."

[027-157]"You should write and ask him to take you."

[027-158]"Ask him to forgive me because he has ill-treated me?"

[027-159]"Never mind about that," said Priscilla, standing over her companion,

[027-160]who was still lying under the bush. "All that is twopenny-halfpenny

[027-161]pride, which should be thrown to the winds. The more right you have

[027-162]been hitherto the better you can afford to go on being right. What is

[027-163]it that we all live upon but self-esteem? When we want praise it is

[027-164]only because praise enables us to think well of ourselves. Every one

[027-165]to himself is the centre and pivot of all the world."

[027-166]"It's a very poor world that goes round upon my pivot," said Mrs.

[027-167]Trevelyan.

[027-168]"I don't know how this quarrel came up," exclaimed Priscilla, "and

[027-169]I don't care to know. But it seems a trumpery quarrel,--as to who

[027-170]should beg each other's pardon first, and all that kind of thing.

[027-171]Sheer and simple nonsense! Ask him to let it all be forgotten. I

[027-172]suppose he loves you?"
