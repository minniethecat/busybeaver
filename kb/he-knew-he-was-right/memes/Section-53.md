## Section 53

[053-001]"Of course," said Dorothy.

[053-002]"And I think that serious conversation upon the whole has more

[053-003]allurements than conversation which when you come to examine it is

[053-004]found to mean nothing. Don't you?"

[053-005]"I suppose everybody should mean something when he talks."

[053-006]"Just so. That is exactly my idea," said Mr. Gibson. "On all such

[053-007]subjects as that I should be so sorry if you and I did not agree. I

[053-008]really should." Then he paused, and Dorothy was so confounded by what

[053-009]she conceived to be the dangers of the coming moment that she was

[053-010]unable even to think what she ought to say. She heard Mrs. MacHugh's

[053-011]clear, sharp, merry voice, and she heard her aunt's tone of pretended

[053-012]anger, and she heard Sir Peter's continued laughter, and Brooke

[053-013]Burgess as he continued the telling of some story; but her own

[053-014]trouble was too great to allow of her attending to what was going on

[053-015]at the other end of the room. "There is nothing as to which I am so

[053-016]anxious as that you and I should agree about serious things," said

[053-017]Mr. Gibson.

[053-018]"I suppose we do agree about going to church," said Dorothy. She knew

[053-019]that she could have made no speech more stupid, more senseless, more

[053-020]inefficacious;--but what was she to say in answer to such an

[053-021]assurance?

[053-022]"I hope so," said Mr. Gibson; "and I think so. Your aunt is a most

[053-023]excellent woman, and her opinion has very great weight with me on all

[053-024]subjects,--even as to matters of church discipline and doctrine, in

[053-025]which, as a clergyman, I am of course presumed to be more at home.

[053-026]But your aunt is a woman among a thousand."

[053-027]"Of course I think she is very good."

[053-028]"And she is so right about this young man and her [[money|property]]. Don't you

[053-029]think so?"

[053-030]"Quite right, Mr. Gibson."

[053-031]"Because you know, to you, of course, being her near relative, and

[053-032]the one she has singled out as the recipient of her kindness, it

[053-033]might have been cause for some discontent."

[053-034]"Discontent to me, Mr. Gibson!"

[053-035]"I am quite sure your feelings are what they ought to be. And for

[053-036]myself, if I ever were,--that is to say, supposing I could be in any

[053-037]way interested--. But perhaps it is premature to make any suggestion

[053-038]on that head at present."

[053-039]"I don't at all understand what you mean, Mr. Gibson."

[053-040]"I thought that perhaps I might take this opportunity of

[053-041]expressing--. But, after all, the levity of the moment is hardly in

[053-042]accordance with the sentiments which I should wish to express."

[053-043]"I think that I ought to go to my aunt now, Mr. Gibson, as perhaps

[053-044]she might want something." Then she did push back her chair, and

[053-045]stand upon her legs,--and Mr. Gibson, after pausing for a moment,

[053-046]allowed her to escape. Soon after that the visitors went, and Brooke

[053-047]Burgess was left in the drawing-room with Miss Stanbury and Dorothy.

[053-048]"How well I recollect all the people," said Brooke; "Sir Peter, and

[053-049]old Mrs. MacHugh, and Mrs. Powel, who then used to be called the

[053-050]beautiful Miss Noel. And I remember every bit of furniture in the

[053-051]room."

[053-052]"Nothing changed except the old woman, Brooke," said Miss Stanbury.

[053-053]"Upon my word, you are the least changed of all,--except that you

[053-054]don't seem to be so terrible as you were then."

[053-055]"Was I very terrible, Brooke?"

[053-056]"My mother had told me, I fancy, that I was never to make a noise,

[053-057]and be sure not to break any of the china. You were always very

[053-058]good-natured, and when you gave me a silver watch I could hardly

[053-059]believe the extent of my own bliss."

[053-060]"You wouldn't care about a watch from an old woman now, Brooke?"

[053-061]"You try me. But what rakes you are here! It's past eleven o'clock,

[053-062]and I must go and have a smoke."

[053-063]"Have a what?" said Miss Stanbury, with a startled air.

[053-064]"A smoke. You needn't be frightened; I don't mean in the house."

[053-065]"No;--I hope you don't mean that."

[053-066]"But I may take a turn round the Close with a pipe;--mayn't I?"

[053-067]"I suppose all young men do smoke now," said Miss Stanbury,

[053-068]sorrowfully.

[053-069]"Every one of them; and they tell me that the young women mean to

[053-070]take to it before long."

[053-071]"If I saw a young woman smoking, I should blush for my sex; and

[053-072]though she were the nearest and dearest that I had, I would never

[053-073]speak to her;--never. Dorothy, I don't think Mr. Gibson smokes."

[053-074]"I'm sure I don't know, aunt."

[053-075]"I hope he doesn't. I do hope that he does not. I cannot understand

[053-076]what pleasure it is that men take in making chimneys of themselves,

[053-077]and going about smelling so that no one can bear to come near them."

[053-078]Brooke merely laughed at this, and went his way, and smoked his pipe

[053-079]out in the Close, while Martha sat up to let him in when he had

[053-080]finished it. Then Dorothy escaped at once to her room, fearful of

[053-081]being questioned by her aunt about Mr. Gibson. She had, she thought

[053-082]now, quite made up her mind. There was nothing in Mr. Gibson that

[053-083]she liked. She was by no means so sure as she had been when she was

[053-084]talking to her sister, that she would prefer a clergyman to any one

[053-085]else. She had formed no strong ideas on the subject of love-making,

[053-086]but she did think that any man who really cared for her, would find

[053-087]some other way of expressing his love than that which Mr. Gibson had

[053-088]adopted. And then Mr. Gibson had spoken to her about her aunt's money

[053-089]in a way that was distasteful to her. She thought that she was quite

[053-090]sure that if he should ask her, she would not accept him.

[053-091]She was nearly undressed, nearly safe for the night, when there came

[053-092]a knock at the door, and her aunt entered the room. "He has come in,"

[053-093]said Miss Stanbury.

[053-094]"I suppose he has had his pipe, then."

[053-095]"I wish he didn't smoke. I do wish he didn't smoke. But I suppose an

[053-096]old woman like me is only making herself a fool to care about such

[053-097]things. If they all do it I can't prevent them. He seems to be a very

[053-098]nice young man--in other things; does he not, Dolly?"

[053-099]"Very nice indeed, Aunt Stanbury."

[053-100]"And he has done very well in his office. And as for his saying that

[053-101]he must smoke, I like that a great deal better than doing it on the

[053-102]sly."

[053-103]"I don't think Mr. Burgess would do anything on the sly, aunt."

[053-104]"No, no; I don't think he would. Dear me; he's not at all like what I

[053-105]fancied."

[053-106]"Everybody seemed to like him very much."

[053-107]"Didn't they? I never saw Sir Peter so much taken. And there was

[053-108]quite a flirtation between him and Mrs. MacHugh. And now, my dear,

[053-109]tell me about Mr. Gibson."

[053-110]"There is nothing to tell, Aunt Stanbury."

[053-111]"Isn't there? From what I saw going on, I thought there would be

[053-112]something to tell. He was talking to you the whole evening."

[053-113]"As it happened he was sitting next to me,--of course."

[053-114]"Indeed he was sitting next to you;--so much so that I thought

[053-115]everything would be settled."

[053-116]"If I tell you something, Aunt Stanbury, you mustn't be angry with

[053-117]me."

[053-118]"Tell me what? What is it you have to tell me?"

[053-119]"I don't think I shall ever care for Mr. Gibson;--not in that way."

[053-120]"Why not, Dorothy?"

[053-121]"I'm sure he doesn't care for me. And I don't think he means it."

[053-122]"I tell you he does mean it. Mean it! Why, I tell you it has all been

[053-123]settled between us. Since I first spoke to you I have explained to

[053-124]him exactly what I intend to do. He knows that he can give up his

[053-125]house and come and live here. I am sure he must have said something

[053-126]about it to you to-night."

[053-127]"Not a word, Aunt Stanbury."

[053-128]"Then he will."

[053-129]"Dear aunt, I do so wish you would prevent it. I don't like him. I

[053-130]don't indeed."

[053-131]"Not like him!"

[053-132]"No;--I don't care for him a bit, and I never shall. I can't help

[053-133]it, Aunt Stanbury. I thought I would try, but I find it would be

[053-134]impossible. You can't want me to marry a man if I don't love him."

[053-135]"I never heard of such a thing in my life. Not love him! And why

[053-136]shouldn't you love him? He's a [[social-class|gentleman]]. Everybody respects him.

[053-137]He'll have plenty to make you comfortable all your life! And then why

[053-138]didn't you tell me before?"

[053-139]"I didn't know, Aunt Stanbury. I thought that perhaps--"

[053-140]"Perhaps what?"

[053-141]"I could not say all at once that I didn't care for him, when I had

[053-142]never so much as thought about it for a moment before."

[053-143]"You haven't told him this?"

[053-144]"No, I have not told him. I couldn't begin by telling him, you know."

[053-145]"Then I must pray that you will think about it again. Have you

[053-146]imagined what a great thing for you it would be to be established for

[053-147]life,--so that you should never have any more trouble again about a

[053-148]home, or about money, or anything? Don't answer me now, Dorothy, but

[053-149]think of it. It seemed to me that I was doing such an excellent thing

[053-150]for both of you." So saying Miss Stanbury left the room, and Dorothy

[053-151]was enabled to obey her, at any rate, in one matter. She did think of

[053-152]it. She laid awake thinking of it almost all the night. But the more

[053-153]she thought of it, the less able was she to realise to herself any

[053-154]future comfort or happiness in the idea of becoming Mrs. Gibson.

[053-155]CHAPTER XXXII.

[053-156]THE "FULL MOON" AT ST. DIDDULPH'S.

[053-157]The receipt of Mrs. Trevelyan's letter on that Monday morning was a

[053-158]great surprise both to Mr. and Mrs. Outhouse. There was no time for

[053-159]any consideration, no opportunity for delaying their arrival till

[053-160]they should have again referred the matter to Mr. Trevelyan. Their

[053-161]two nieces were to be with them on that evening, and even the

[053-162]telegraph wires, if employed with such purpose, would not be quick

[053-163]enough to stop their coming. The party, as they knew, would have left

[053-164][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]] before the arrival of the letter at the parsonage of

[053-165]St. Diddulph's. There would have been nothing in this to have caused

[053-166]vexation, had it not been decided between Trevelyan and Mr. Outhouse

[053-167]that Mrs. Trevelyan was not to find a home at the parsonage. Mr.

[053-168]Outhouse was greatly afraid of being so entangled in the matter as to

[053-169]be driven to take the part of the wife against the husband; and Mrs.

[053-170]Outhouse, though she was full of indignation against Trevelyan, was

[053-171]at the same time not free from anger in regard to her own niece.

[053-172]She more than once repeated that most unjust of all proverbs, which

[053-173]declares that there is never smoke without fire, and asserted broadly

[053-174]that she did not like to be with people who could not live at

[053-175]home, husbands with wives, and wives with husbands, in a decent,

[053-176]respectable manner. Nevertheless the preparations went on busily, and

[053-177]when the party arrived at seven o'clock in the evening, two rooms had

[053-178]been prepared close to each other, one for the two sisters, and the

[053-179]other for the child and nurse, although poor Mr. Outhouse himself was

[053-180]turned out of his own little chamber in order that the accommodation

[053-181]might be given. They were all very hot, very tired, and very dusty,

[053-182]when the cab reached the parsonage. There had been the preliminary

[053-183]drive from Nuncombe Putney to Lessboro'. Then the railway journey

[053-184]from thence to the Waterloo Bridge Station had been long. And it had

[053-185]seemed to them that the distance from the station to St. Diddulph's

[053-186]had been endless. When the cabman was told whither he was to go, he

[053-187]looked doubtingly at his poor old horse, and then at the luggage

[053-188]which he was required to pack on the top of his cab, and laid himself

[053-189]out for his work with a full understanding that it would not be

[053-190]accomplished without considerable difficulty. The cabman made it

[053-191]twelve miles from Waterloo Bridge to St. Diddulph's, and suggested

[053-192]that extra passengers and parcels would make the fare up to ten and

[053-193]six. Had he named double as much Mrs. Trevelyan would have assented.

[053-194]So great was the fatigue, and so wretched the occasion, that there

[053-195]was sobbing and crying in the cab, and when at last the parsonage was

[053-196]reached, even the nurse was hardly able to turn her hand to anything.

[053-197]The poor wanderers were made welcome on that evening without a word

[053-198]of discussion as to the cause of their coming. "I hope you are not

[053-199]angry with us, Uncle Oliphant," [[emily-trevelyan|Emily Trevelyan]] had said, with tears

[053-200]in her eyes. "Angry with you, my dear;--for coming to our house!

[053-201]How could I be angry with you?" Then the travellers were hurried

[053-202]up-stairs by Mrs. Outhouse, and the master of the parsonage was left

[053-203]alone for a while. He certainly was not angry, but he was ill at

[053-204]ease, and unhappy. His guests would probably remain with him for

[053-205]six or seven months. He had resolutely refused all payment from Mr.

[053-206]Trevelyan, but, nevertheless, he was a poor man. It is impossible

[053-207]to conceive that a clergyman in such a parish as St. Diddulph's,

[053-208]without a private income, should not be a poor man. It was but a

[053-209]hand-to-mouth existence which he lived, paying his way as his money

[053-210]came to him, and sharing the proceeds of his parish with the poor.

[053-211]He was always more or less in debt. That was quite understood among

[053-212]the tradesmen. And the butcher who trusted him, though he was a

[053-213]bad churchman, did not look upon the parson's account as he did on

[053-214]other debts. He would often hint to Mr. Outhouse that a little money

[053-215]ought to be paid, and then a little money would be paid. But it was

[053-216]never expected that the parsonage bill should be settled. In such a

[053-217]household the arrival of four guests, who were expected to remain for

[053-218]an almost indefinite number of months, could not be regarded without

[053-219]dismay. On that first evening, Emily and Nora did come down to tea,

[053-220]but they went up again to their rooms almost immediately afterwards;

[053-221]and Mr. Outhouse found that many hours of solitary meditation were

[053-222]allowed to him on the occasion. "I suppose your brother has been told

[053-223]all about it," he said to his wife, as soon as they were together on

[053-224]that evening.
