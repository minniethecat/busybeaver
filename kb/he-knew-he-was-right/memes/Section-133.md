## Section 133

[133-001]"I wish I knew more about it really."

[133-002]"When a man has to leap through a window in the dark, Caroline, of

[133-003]course he doubts whether the feather bed said to be below will be

[133-004]soft enough for him."

[133-005]"I shouldn't fear the leap for myself, if it wouldn't hurt him. Do

[133-006]you think it possible that society can be so formed that a man should

[133-007]lose caste because he doesn't marry just one of his own set?"

[133-008]"It has been so all over the world, my dear. If like to like is to be

[133-009]true anywhere, it should be true in marriage."

[133-010]"Yes;--but with a difference. He and I are like to like. We come of

[133-011]the same race, we speak the same language, we worship the same God,

[133-012]we have the same ideas of culture and of pleasures. The difference is

[133-013]one that is not patent to the eye or to the ear. It is a difference

[133-014]of accidental incident, not of nature or of acquirement."

[133-015]"I guess you would find, Caroline, that a jury of English matrons

[133-016]sworn to try you fairly, would not find you to be entitled to come

[133-017]among them as one of themselves."

[133-018]"And how will that affect him?"

[133-019]"Less powerfully than many others, because he is not impassioned. He

[133-020]is, perhaps--lethargic."

[133-021]"No, Wally, he is not lethargic."

[133-022]"If you ask me I must speak. It would harass some men almost to

[133-023]death; it will not do so with him. He would probably find his

[133-024]happiness best in leaving his old country and coming among your

[133-025]people."

[133-026]The idea of Mr. Glascock,--the future Lord Peterborough,--leaving

[133-027]England, abandoning Monkhams, deserting his duty in the House of

[133-028]Lords, and going away to live in an American town, in order that he

[133-029]might escape the miseries which his wife had brought upon him in his

[133-030]own country, was more than Caroline could bear. She knew that, at

[133-031]any rate, it would not come to that. The lord of Monkhams would live

[133-032]at Monkhams, though the heavens should fall--in regard to domestic

[133-033]comforts. It was clear to Caroline that Wallachia Petrie had in truth

[133-034]never brought home to her own imagination the position of an English

[133-035]peer. "I don't think you understand the people at all," she said

[133-036]angrily.

[133-037]"You think that you can understand them better because you are

[133-038]engaged to this man!" said Miss Petrie, with well-pronounced irony.

[133-039]"You have found generally that when the sun shines in your eyes your

[133-040]sight is improved by it! You think that the love-talk of a few weeks

[133-041]gives clearer instruction than the laborious reading of many volumes

[133-042]and thoughtful converse with thinking persons! I hope that you may

[133-043]find it so, Caroline." So saying Wallachia Petrie walked off in great

[133-044]dudgeon.

[133-045]Miss Petrie, not having learned from her many volumes and her much

[133-046]converse with thoughtful persons to read human nature aright, was

[133-047]convinced by this conversation that her friend Caroline was blind

[133-048]to all results, and was determined to go on with this dangerous

[133-049]marriage, having the rays of that sun of Monkhams so full upon her

[133-050]eyes that she could not see at all. She was specially indignant at

[133-051]finding that her own words had no effect. But, unfortunately, her

[133-052]words had had much effect; and Caroline, though she had contested her

[133-053]points, had done so only with the intention of producing her Mentor's

[133-054]admonitions. Of course it was out of the question that Mr. Glascock

[133-055]should go and live in Providence, Rhode Island, from which thriving

[133-056]town Caroline Spalding had come; but, because that was impossible,

[133-057]it was not the less probable that he might be degraded and made

[133-058]miserable in his own home. That suggested jury of British matrons

[133-059]was a frightful conclave to contemplate, and Caroline was disposed

[133-060]to believe that the verdict given in reference to herself would

[133-061]be adverse to her. So she sat and meditated, and spoke not a word

[133-062]further to any one on the subject till she was alone with the man

[133-063]that she loved.

[133-064]Mr. Spalding at this time inhabited the ground floor of a large

[133-065]palace in the city, from which there was access to a garden which at

[133-066]this period of the year was green, bright, and shady, and which as

[133-067]being in the centre of a city was large and luxurious. From one end

[133-068]of the house there projected a covered terrace, or loggia, in which

[133-069]there were chairs and tables, sculptured ornaments, busts, and old

[133-070]monumental relics let into the wall in profusion. It was half chamber

[133-071]and half garden,--such an adjunct to a house as in our climate would

[133-072]give only an idea of cold, rheumatism, and a false romance, but under

[133-073]an Italian sky, is a luxury daily to be enjoyed during most months of

[133-074]the year. Here Mr. Glascock and Caroline had passed many hours,--and

[133-075]here they were now seated, late in the evening, while all others of

[133-076]the family were away. As far as regarded the rooms occupied by the

[133-077]American Minister, they had the house and garden to themselves, and

[133-078]there never could come a time more appropriate for the saying of a

[133-079]thing difficult to be said. Mr. Glascock had heard from his father's

[133-080]physician, and had said that it was nearly certain now that he

[133-081]need not go down to Naples again before his marriage. Caroline was

[133-082]trembling, not knowing how to speak, not knowing how to begin;--but

[133-083]resolved that the thing should be done. "He will never know you,

[133-084]Carry," said Mr. Glascock. "It is, perhaps, hardly a sorrow to me,

[133-085]but it is a regret."

[133-086]"It would have been a sorrow perhaps to him had he been able to know

[133-087]me," said she, taking the opportunity of rushing at her subject.

[133-088]"Why so? Of all human beings he was the softest-hearted."

[133-089]"Not softer-hearted than you, Charles. But soft hearts have to be

[133-090]hardened."

[133-091]"What do you mean? Am I becoming obdurate?"

[133-092]"I am, Charles," she said. "I have got something to say to you. What

[133-093]will your uncles and aunts and your mother's relations say of me when

[133-094]they see me at Monkhams?"

[133-095]"They will swear to me that you are charming; and then,--when my back

[133-096]is turned,--they'll pick you to pieces a little among themselves. I

[133-097]believe that is the way of the world, and I don't suppose that we are

[133-098]to do better than others."

[133-099]"And if you had married an English girl, a Lady Augusta

[133-100]Somebody,--would they pick her to pieces?"

[133-101]"I guess they would, as you say."

[133-102]"Just the same?"

[133-103]"I don't think anybody escapes, as far as I can see. But that won't

[133-104]prevent their becoming your bosom friends in a few weeks time."

[133-105]"No one will say that you have been wrong to marry an American girl?"

[133-106]"Now, Carry, what is the meaning of all this?"

[133-107]"Do you know any man in your position who ever did marry an American

[133-108]girl;--any man of your [[social-class|rank]] in England?" Mr. Glascock began to think

[133-109]of the case, and could not at the moment remember any instance.

[133-110]"Charles, I do not think you ought to be the first."

[133-111]"And yet somebody must be first, if the thing is ever to be

[133-112]done;--and I am too old to wait on the chance of being the second."

[133-113]She felt that at the rate she was now progressing she would only run

[133-114]from one little suggestion to another, and that he, either wilfully

[133-115]or in sheer simplicity, would take such suggestions simply as jokes;

[133-116]and she was aware that she lacked the skill to bring the conversation

[133-117]round gradually to the point which she was bound to reach. She must

[133-118]make another dash, let it be ever so sudden. Her mode of doing so

[133-119]would be crude, ugly,--almost vulgar she feared; but she would attain

[133-120]her object and say what she had to say. When once she had warmed

[133-121]herself with the heat which argument would produce, then, she was

[133-122]pretty sure, she would find herself at least as strong as he. "I

[133-123]don't know that the thing ought to be done at all," she said. During

[133-124]the last moment or two he had put his arm round her waist; and she,

[133-125]not choosing to bid him desist from embracing her, but unwilling in

[133-126]her present mood to be embraced, got up and stood before him. "I have

[133-127]thought, and thought, and thought, and feel that it should not be

[133-128]done. In marriage, like should go to like." She despised herself for

[133-129]using Wallachia's words, but they fitted in so usefully, that she

[133-130]could not refrain from them. "I was wrong not to know it before, but

[133-131]it is better to know it now, than not to have known it till too late.

[133-132]Everything that I hear and see tells me that it would be so. If you

[133-133]were simply an Englishman, I would go anywhere with you; but I am not

[133-134]fit to be the wife of an English lord. The time would come when I

[133-135]should be a disgrace to you, and then I should die."

[133-136]"I think I should go near dying myself," said he, "if you were a

[133-137]disgrace to me." He had not risen from his chair, and sat calmly

[133-138]looking up into her face.

[133-139]"We have made a mistake, and let us unmake it," she continued. "I

[133-140]will always be your friend. I will correspond with you. I will come

[133-141]and see your wife."

[133-142]"That will be very kind!"

[133-143]"Charles, if you laugh at me, I shall be angry with you. It is right

[133-144]that you should look to your future life, as it is right that I

[133-145]should do so also. Do you think that I am joking? Do you suppose that

[133-146]I do not mean it?"

[133-147]"You have taken an extra dose this morning of Wallachia Petrie, and

[133-148]of course you mean it."

[133-149]"If you think that I am speaking her mind and not my own, you do not

[133-150]know me."

[133-151]"And what is it you propose?" he said, still keeping his seat and

[133-152]looking calmly up into her face.

[133-153]"Simply that our engagement should be over."

[133-154]"And why?"

[133-155]"Because it is not a fitting one for you to have made. I did not

[133-156]understand it before, but now I do. It will not be good for you to

[133-157]marry an American girl. It will not add to your happiness, and may

[133-158]destroy it. I have learned, at last, to know how much higher is your

[133-159]position than mine."

[133-160]"And I am to be supposed to know nothing about it?"

[133-161]"Your fault is only this,--that you have been too generous. I can be

[133-162]generous also."

[133-163]"Now, look here, Caroline, you must not be angry with me if on such

[133-164]a subject I speak plainly. You must not even be angry if I laugh a

[133-165]little."

[133-166]"Pray do not laugh at me!--not now."

[133-167]"I must a little, Carry. Why am I to be supposed to be so ignorant of

[133-168]what concerns my own happiness and my own duties? If you will not sit

[133-169]down, I will get up, and we will take a turn together." He rose from

[133-170]his seat, but they did not leave the covered terrace. They moved on

[133-171]to the extremity, and then he stood hemming her in against a marble

[133-172]table in the corner. "In making this rather wild proposition, have

[133-173]you considered me at all?"

[133-174]"I have endeavoured to consider you, and you only."

[133-175]"And how have you done it? By the aid of some misty, far-fetched

[133-176]ideas respecting English society, for which you have no basis except

[133-177]your own dreams,--and by the fantasies of a rabid enthusiast."

[133-178]"She is not rabid," said Caroline earnestly; "other people think just

[133-179]the same."

[133-180]"My dear, there is only one person whose thinking on this subject

[133-181]is of any avail, and I am that person. Of course, I can't drag you

[133-182]into church to be married, but practically you can not help yourself

[133-183]from being taken there now. As there need be no question about our

[133-184]marriage,--which is a thing as good as done--"
