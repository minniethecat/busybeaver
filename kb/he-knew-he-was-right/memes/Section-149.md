## Section 149

[149-001]Stanbury found some difficulty in making himself a "good boy" in

[149-002]Lady Rowley's presence; and Lady Rowley herself, for some time, felt

[149-003]very strongly the awkwardness of the meeting. She had never formally

[149-004]recognised the young man as her daughter's accepted suitor, and was

[149-005]not yet justified in doing so by any permission from Sir Marmaduke;

[149-006]but, as the young people had been for the last hour or two alone

[149-007]together, with her connivance and sanction, it was indispensable

[149-008]that she should in some way signify her parental adherence to the

[149-009]arrangement. Nora began by talking about Emily, and Trevelyan's

[149-010]condition and mode of living were discussed. Then Lady Rowley said

[149-011]something about their coming journey, and Hugh, with a lucky blunder,

[149-012]spoke of Nora's intended return to Italy. "We don't know how that may

[149-013]be," said Lady Rowley. "Her papa still wishes her to go back with

[149-014]us."

[149-015]"Mamma, you know that that is impossible," said Nora.

[149-016]"Not impossible, my love."

[149-017]"But she will not go back," said Hugh. "Lady Rowley, you would not

[149-018]propose to separate us by such a distance as that?"

[149-019]"It is Sir Marmaduke that you must ask."

[149-020]"Mamma, mamma!" exclaimed Nora, rushing to her mother's side, "it is

[149-021]not papa that we must ask,--not now. We want you to be our friend.

[149-022]Don't we, Hugh? And, mamma, if you will really be our friend, of

[149-023]course, papa will come round."

[149-024]"My dear Nora!"

[149-025]"You know he will, mamma; and you know that you mean to be good and

[149-026]kind to us. Of course I can't go back to the Islands with you. How

[149-027]could I go so far and leave him behind? He might have half-a-dozen

[149-028]wives before I could get back to him--"

[149-029]"If you have not more trust in him than that--!"

[149-030]"Long engagements are awful bores," said Hugh, finding it to be

[149-031]necessary that he also should press forward his argument.

[149-032]"I can trust him as far as I can see him," said Nora, "and therefore

[149-033]I do not want to lose sight of him altogether."

[149-034]Lady Rowley of course gave way and embraced her accepted son-in-law.

[149-035]After all it might have been worse. He saw his way clearly, he said,

[149-036]to making six hundred a year, and did not at all doubt that before

[149-037]long he would do better than that. He proposed that they should be

[149-038]married some time in the autumn, but was willing to acknowledge that

[149-039]much must depend on the position of Trevelyan and his wife. He would

[149-040]hold himself ready at any moment, he said, to start to Italy, and

[149-041]would do all that could be done by a brother. Then Lady Rowley gave

[149-042]him her blessing, and kissed him again,--and Nora kissed him too, and

[149-043]hung upon him, and did not push him away at all when his arm crept

[149-044]round her waist. And that feeling came upon him which must surely be

[149-045]acknowledged by all engaged young men when they first find themselves

[149-046]encouraged by mammas in the taking of liberties which they have

[149-047]hitherto regarded as mysteries to be hidden, especially from maternal

[149-048]eyes,--that feeling of being a fine fat calf decked out with ribbons

[149-049]for a sacrifice.

[149-050]CHAPTER XCI.

[149-051]FOUR O'CLOCK IN THE MORNING.

[149-052]Another week went by and Sir Marmaduke had even yet not surrendered.

[149-053]He quite understood that Nora was not to go back to the Islands.

[149-054]And he had visited Mr. and Mrs. Outhouse at St. Diddulph's in order

[149-055]to secure a home for her there, if it might be possible. Mr. Outhouse

[149-056]did not refuse, but gave the permission in such a fashion as to make

[149-057]it almost equal to a refusal. "He was," he said, "much attached to

[149-058]his niece Nora, but he had heard that there was a love affair." Sir

[149-059]Marmaduke, of course, could not deny the love affair. There was

[149-060]certainly a love affair of which he did not personally approve, as

[149-061]the [[social-class|gentleman]] had no fixed [[money|income]] and as far as he could understand

[149-062]no fixed profession. "Such a love affair," thought Mr. Outhouse, "was

[149-063]a sort of thing that he didn't know how to manage at all. If Nora

[149-064]came to him, was the young man to visit at the house, or was he

[149-065]not?" Then Mrs. Outhouse said something as to the necessity of an

[149-066]anti-Stanbury pledge on Nora's part, and Sir Marmaduke found that

[149-067]that scheme must be abandoned. Mrs. Trevelyan had written from

[149-068]Florence more than once or twice, and in her last letter had said

[149-069]that she would prefer not to have Nora with her. She was at that

[149-070]time living in lodgings at Siena and had her boy there also. She saw

[149-071]her husband every other day; but nevertheless,--according to her

[149-072]statements,--her visits to Casalunga were made in opposition to his

[149-073]wishes. He had even expressed a desire that she should leave Siena

[149-074]and return to England. He had once gone so far as to say that if she

[149-075]would do so, he would follow her. But she clearly did not believe

[149-076]him, and in all her letters spoke of him as one whom she could not

[149-077]regard as being under the guidance of reason. She had taken her

[149-078]child with her once or twice to the house, and on the first occasion

[149-079]Trevelyan had made much of his son, had wept over him, and professed

[149-080]that in losing him he had lost his only treasure; but after that he

[149-081]had not noticed the boy, and latterly she had gone alone. She thought

[149-082]that perhaps her visits cheered him, breaking the intensity of his

[149-083]solitude; but he never expressed himself gratified by them, never

[149-084]asked her to remain at the house, never returned with her into Siena,

[149-085]and continually spoke of her return to England as a step which must

[149-086]be taken soon,--and the sooner the better. He intended to follow her,

[149-087]he said; and she explained very fully how manifest was his wish that

[149-088]she should go, by the temptation to do so which he thought that he

[149-089]held out by this promise. He had spoken, on every occasion of her

[149-090]presence with him, of Sir Marmaduke's attempt to prove him to be a

[149-091]madman; but declared that he was afraid of no one in England, and

[149-092]would face all the lawyers in Chancery Lane and all the doctors

[149-093]in Savile Row. Nevertheless, so said Mrs. Trevelyan, he would

[149-094]undoubtedly remain at Casalunga till after Sir Marmaduke should have

[149-095]sailed. He was not so mad but that he knew that no one else would be

[149-096]so keen to take steps against him as would Sir Marmaduke. As for his

[149-097]health, her account of him was very sad. "He seemed," she said, "to

[149-098]be withering away." His hand was mere skin and bone. His hair and

[149-099]beard so covered his thin long cheeks, that there was nothing left

[149-100]of his face but his bright, large, melancholy eyes. His legs had

[149-101]become so frail and weak that they would hardly bear his weight as he

[149-102]walked; and his clothes, though he had taken a fancy to throw aside

[149-103]all that he had brought with him from England, hung so loose about

[149-104]him that they seemed as though they would fall from him. Once she had

[149-105]ventured to send out to him from Siena a doctor to whom she had been

[149-106]recommended in Florence; but he had taken the visit in very bad part,

[149-107]had told the gentleman that he had no need for any medical services,

[149-108]and had been furious with her, because of her offence in having sent

[149-109]such a visitor. He had told her that if ever she ventured to take

[149-110]such a [[personal-freedom|liberty]] again, he would demand the child back, and refuse her

[149-111]permission inside the gates of Casalunga. "Don't come, at any rate,

[149-112]till I send for you," Mrs. Trevelyan said in her last letter to her

[149-113]sister. "Your being here would do no good, and would, I think, make

[149-114]him feel that he was being watched. My hope is, at last, to get him

[149-115]to return with me. If you were here, I think this would be less

[149-116]likely. And then why should you be mixed up with such unutterable

[149-117]sadness and distress more than is essentially necessary? My health

[149-118]stands wonderfully well, though the heat here is very great. It is

[149-119]cooler at Casalunga than in the town,--of which I am glad for his

[149-120]sake. He perspires so profusely that it seems to me he cannot stand

[149-121]the waste much longer. I know he will not go to England as long as

[149-122]papa is there;--but I hope that he may be induced to do so by slow

[149-123]stages as soon as he knows that papa has gone. Mind you send me a

[149-124]newspaper, so that he may see it stated in print that papa has

[149-125]sailed."

[149-126]It followed as one consequence of these letters from Florence that

[149-127]Nora was debarred from the Italian scheme as a mode of passing her

[149-128]time till some house should be open for her reception. She had

[149-129]suggested to Hugh that she might go for a few weeks to Nuncombe

[149-130]Putney, but he had explained to her the nature of his mother's

[149-131]cottage, and had told her that there was no hole there in which she

[149-132]could lay her head. "There never was such a forlorn young woman,"

[149-133]she said. "When papa goes I shall literally be without shelter."

[149-134]There had come a letter from Mrs. Glascock,--at least it was signed

[149-135]Caroline Glascock, though another name might have been used,--dated

[149-136]from Milan, saying that they were hurrying back to Naples even at

[149-137]that season of the year, because Lord Peterborough was dead. "And

[149-138]she is Lady Peterborough!" said Lady Rowley, unable to repress the

[149-139]expression of the old regrets. "Of course she is Lady Peterborough,

[149-140]mamma; what else should she be?--though she does not so sign

[149-141]herself." "We think," said the American peeress, "that we shall be

[149-142]at Monkhams before the end of August, and Charles says that you are

[149-143]to come just the same. There will be nobody else there, of course,

[149-144]because of Lord Peterborough's death." "I saw it in the paper," said

[149-145]Sir Marmaduke, "and quite forgot to mention it."

[149-146]That same evening there was a long family discussion about Nora's

[149-147]prospects. They were all together in the gloomy sitting-room at

[149-148]Gregg's Hotel, and Sir Marmaduke had not yielded. The ladies had

[149-149]begun to feel that it would be well not to press him to yield.

[149-150]Practically he had yielded. There was now no question of cursing

[149-151]and of so-called disinheritance. Nora was to remain in England, of

[149-152]course with the intention of being married to [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]]; and the

[149-153]difficulty consisted in the need of an immediate home for her. It

[149-154]wanted now but twelve days to that on which the family were to sail

[149-155]from Southampton, and nothing had been settled. "If papa will allow

[149-156]me something ever so small, and will trust me, I will live alone in

[149-157]lodgings," said Nora.

[149-158]"It is the maddest thing I ever heard," said Sir Marmaduke.

[149-159]"Who would take care of you, Nora?" asked Lady Rowley.

[149-160]"And who would walk about with you?" said Lucy.

[149-161]"I don't see how it would be possible to live alone like that," said

[149-162]Sophie.

[149-163]"Nobody would take care of me, and nobody would walk about with me,

[149-164]and I could live alone very well," said Nora. "I don't see why a

[149-165]young woman is to be supposed to be so absolutely helpless as all

[149-166]that comes to. Of course it won't be very nice,--but it need not be

[149-167]for long."

[149-168]"Why not for long?" asked Sir Marmaduke.

[149-169]"Not for very long," said Nora.

[149-170]"It does not seem to me," said Sir Marmaduke, after a considerable

[149-171]pause, "that this gentleman himself is so particularly anxious for

[149-172]the match. I have heard no day named, and no rational proposition

[149-173]made."
