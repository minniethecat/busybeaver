## Section 73

[073-001]"Camilla, don't be foolish," said Mrs. French.

[073-002]"Because he wouldn't what?" asked Brooke. "What ought he to have done

[073-003]that he didn't do?"

[073-004]"I don't know anything about ought," said Camilla. "That's a matter

[073-005]of taste altogether."

[073-006]"I'm the worst hand in the world at a riddle," said Brooke.

[073-007]"How sly you are," continued Camilla, laughing; "as if dear Aunt

[073-008]Stanbury hadn't confided all her hopes to you."

[073-009]"Camilla, dear,--don't," said Arabella.

[073-010]"But when a [[social-class|gentleman]] is hunted, and can't be caught, I don't think

[073-011]he ought to be abused to his face."

[073-012]"But who hunted him, and who abused him?" asked Brooke.

[073-013]"Mind, I don't mean to say a word against Miss Stanbury, Mr. Burgess.

[073-014]We've known her and loved her all our lives;--haven't we, mamma?"

[073-015]"And respected her," said Arabella.

[073-016]"Quite so," continued Camilla. "But you know, Mr. Burgess, that she

[073-017]likes her own way."

[073-018]"I don't know anybody that does not," said Brooke.

[073-019]"And when she's disappointed, she shows it. There's no doubt she is

[073-020]disappointed now, Mr. Burgess."

[073-021]"What's the good of going on, Camilla?" said Mrs. French. Arabella

[073-022]sat silent in her corner, with a conscious glow of satisfaction, as

[073-023]she reflected that the joint disappointment of the elder and the

[073-024]younger Miss Stanbury had been caused by a tender remembrance of her

[073-025]own charms. Had not dear Mr. Gibson told her, in the glowing language

[073-026]of truth, that there was nothing further from his thoughts than the

[073-027]idea of taking Dorothy Stanbury for his wife?

[073-028]"Well, you know," continued Camilla, "I think that when a person

[073-029]makes an attempt, and comes by the worst of it, that person should

[073-030]put up with the defeat, and not say all manner of ill-natured things.

[073-031]Everybody knows that a certain gentleman is very intimate in this

[073-032]house."

[073-033]"Don't, dear," said Arabella, in a whisper.

[073-034]"Yes, I shall," said Camilla. "I don't know why people should hold

[073-035]their tongues, when other people talk so loudly. I don't care a bit

[073-036]what anybody says about the gentleman and us. We have known him for

[073-037]ever so many years, and mamma is very fond of him."

[073-038]"Indeed I am, Camilla," said Mrs. French.

[073-039]"And for the matter of that, so am I,--very," said Camilla, laughing

[073-040]bravely. "I don't care who knows it."

[073-041]"Don't be so silly, child," said Arabella. Camilla was certainly

[073-042]doing her best, and Arabella was grateful.

[073-043]"We don't care what people may say," continued Camilla again.

[073-044]"Of course we heard, as everybody else heard too, that a certain

[073-045]gentleman was to be married to a certain lady. It was nothing to us

[073-046]whether he was married or not."

[073-047]"Nothing at all," said Arabella.

[073-048]"We never spoke ill of the young lady. We did not interfere. If the

[073-049]gentleman liked the young lady, he was quite at [[personal-freedom|liberty]] to marry

[073-050]her, as far as we were concerned. We had been in the habit of seeing

[073-051]him here, almost as a brother, and perhaps we might feel that a

[073-052]connection with that particular young lady would take him from us;

[073-053]but we never hinted so much even as that,--to him or to anyone else.

[073-054]Why should we? It was nothing to us. Now it turns out that the

[073-055]gentleman never meant anything of the kind, whereupon he is pretty

[073-056]nearly kicked out of the house, and all manner of ill-natured things

[073-057]are said about us everywhere." By this time Camilla had become quite

[073-058]excited, and was speaking with much animation.

[073-059]"How can you be so foolish, Camilla?" said Arabella.

[073-060]"Perhaps I am foolish," said Camilla, "to care what anybody says."

[073-061]"What can it all be to Mr. Burgess?" said Mrs. French.

[073-062]"Only this, that as we all like Mr. Burgess, and as he is almost one

[073-063]of the family in the Close, I think he ought to know why we are not

[073-064]quite so cordial as we used to be. Now that the matter is over I have

[073-065]no doubt things will get right again. And as for the young lady, I'm

[073-066]sure we feel for her. We think it was the aunt who was indiscreet."

[073-067]"And then she has such a tongue," said Arabella.

[073-068]Our friend Brooke, of course, knew the whole truth;--knew the nature

[073-069]of Mr. Gibson's failure, and knew also how Dorothy had acted in the

[073-070]affair. He was inclined, moreover, to believe that the ladies who

[073-071]were now talking to him were as well instructed on the subject as

[073-072]was he himself. He had heard, too, of the ambition of the two young

[073-073]ladies now before him, and believed that that ambition was not yet

[073-074]dead. But he did not think it incumbent on him to fight a battle even

[073-075]on behalf of Dorothy. He might have declared that Dorothy, at least,

[073-076]had not been disappointed, but he thought it better to be silent

[073-077]about Dorothy. "Yes," he said, "Miss Stanbury has a tongue; but I

[073-078]think it speaks as much good as it does evil, and perhaps that is a

[073-079]great deal to say for any lady's tongue."

[073-080]"We never speak evil of anybody," said Camilla; "never. It is a rule

[073-081]with us." Then Brooke took his leave, and the three ladies were

[073-082]cordial and almost affectionate in their farewell greetings.

[073-083]Brooke was to start on the following morning before anybody would

[073-084]be up except Martha, and Miss Stanbury was very melancholy during

[073-085]the evening. "We shall miss him very much; shall we not?" she said,

[073-086]appealing to Dorothy. "I am sure you will miss him very much," said

[073-087]Dorothy. "We are so stupid here alone," said Miss Stanbury. When they

[073-088]had drank their tea, she sat nearly silent for half an hour, and then

[073-089]summoned him up into her own room. "So you are going, Brooke?" she

[073-090]said.

[073-091]"Yes; I must go now. They would dismiss me if I stayed an hour

[073-092]longer."

[073-093]"It was good of you to come to the old woman; and you must let me

[073-094]hear of you from time to time."

[073-095]"Of course I'll write."

[073-096]"And, Brooke,--"

[073-097]"What is it, Aunt Stanbury?"

[073-098]"Do you want any money, Brooke?"

[073-099]"No;--none, thank you. I've plenty for a bachelor."

[073-100]"When you think of marrying, Brooke, mind you tell me."

[073-101]"I'll be sure to tell you;--but I can't promise yet when that will

[073-102]be." She said nothing more to him, though she paused once more as

[073-103]though she were going to speak. She kissed him and bade him good-bye,

[073-104]saying that she would not go down-stairs again that evening. He was

[073-105]to tell Dorothy to go to bed. And so they parted.

[073-106]But Dorothy did not go to bed for an hour after that. When Brooke

[073-107]came down into the parlour with his message she intended to go at

[073-108]once, and put up her work, and lit her candle, and put out her hand

[073-109]to him, and said good-bye to him. But, for all that, she remained

[073-110]there for an hour with him. At first she said very little, but by

[073-111]degrees her tongue was loosened, and she found herself talking with a

[073-112]freedom which she could hardly herself understand. She told him how

[073-113]thoroughly she believed her aunt to be a good woman,--how sure she

[073-114]was that her aunt was at any rate honest. "As for me," said Dorothy,

[073-115]"I know that I have displeased her about Mr. Gibson;--and I would go

[073-116]away, only that I think she would be so desolate." Then Brooke begged

[073-117]her never to allow the idea of leaving Miss Stanbury to enter her

[073-118]head. Because Miss Stanbury was capricious, he said, not on that

[073-119]account should her caprices either be indulged or permitted. That

[073-120]was his doctrine respecting Miss Stanbury, and he declared that, as

[073-121]regarded himself, he would never be either disrespectful to her or

[073-122]submissive. "It is a great mistake," he said, "to think that anybody

[073-123]is either an angel or a devil." When Dorothy expressed an opinion

[073-124]that with some people angelic tendencies were predominant, and with

[073-125]others diabolic tendencies, he assented; but declared that it was not

[073-126]always easy to tell the one tendency from the other. At last, when

[073-127]Dorothy had made about five attempts to go, Mr. Gibson's name was

[073-128]mentioned. "I am very glad that you are not going to be Mrs. Gibson,"

[073-129]said he.

[073-130][Illustration: Brooke Burgess takes his leave.]

[073-131]"I don't know why you should be glad."

[073-132]"Because I should not have liked your husband,--not as your husband."

[073-133]"He is an excellent man, I'm sure," said Dorothy.

[073-134]"Nevertheless I am very glad. But I did not think you would accept

[073-135]him, and I congratulate you on your escape. You would have been

[073-136]nothing to me as Mrs. Gibson."

[073-137]"Shouldn't I?" said Dorothy, not knowing what else to say.

[073-138]"But now I think we shall always be friends."

[073-139]"I'm sure I hope so, Mr. Burgess. But indeed I must go now. It is

[073-140]ever so late, and you will hardly get any sleep. Good night." Then he

[073-141]took her hand, and pressed it very warmly, and referring to a promise

[073-142]before made to her, he assured her that he would certainly make

[073-143]acquaintance with her brother as soon as he was back in [[london|London]].

[073-144]Dorothy, as she went up to bed, was more than ever satisfied with

[073-145]herself, in that she had not yielded in reference to Mr. Gibson.

[073-146]CHAPTER XLV.

[073-147]TREVELYAN AT VENICE.

[073-148]Trevelyan passed on moodily and alone from Turin to Venice, always

[073-149]expecting letters from Bozzle, and receiving from time to time the

[073-150]dispatches which that functionary forwarded to him, as must be

[073-151]acknowledged, with great punctuality. For Mr. Bozzle did his work,

[073-152]not only with a conscience, but with a will. He was now, as he had

[073-153]declared more than once, altogether devoted to Mr. Trevelyan's

[073-154]interest; and as he was an active, enterprising man, always on the

[073-155]alert to be doing something, and as he loved the work of writing

[073-156]dispatches, Trevelyan received a great many letters from Bozzle. It

[073-157]is not exaggeration to say that every letter made him for the time

[073-158]a very wretched man. This ex-policeman wrote of the wife of his

[073-159]bosom,--of her who had been the wife of his bosom, and who was the

[073-160]mother of his child, who was at this very time the only woman whom he

[073-161]loved,--with an entire absence of delicacy. Bozzle would have thought

[073-162]reticence on his part to be dishonest. We remember Othello's demand

[073-163]of Iago. That was the demand which Bozzle understood that Trevelyan

[073-164]had made of him, and he was minded to obey that order. But Trevelyan,

[073-165]though he had in truth given the order, was like Othello also in

[073-166]this,--that he would have preferred before all the prizes of the

[073-167]world to have had [[truth|proof]] brought home to him exactly opposite to that

[073-168]which he demanded. But there was nothing so terrible to him as the

[073-169]grinding suspicion that he was to be kept in the dark. Bozzle could

[073-170]find out facts. Therefore he gave, in effect, the same order that

[073-171]Othello gave;--and Bozzle went to work determined to obey it. There

[073-172]came many dispatches to Venice, and at last there came one, which

[073-173]created a correspondence which shall be given here at length. The

[073-174]first is a letter from Mr. Bozzle to his employer:--

[073-175]55, Stony Walk, Union Street, Borough,

[073-176]September 29, 186--, 4.30 P.M.

[073-177]HOND. SIR,

[073-178]Since I wrote yesterday morning, something has occurred

[073-179]which, it may be, and I think it will, will help to bring

[073-180]this melancholy affair to a satisfactory termination and

[073-181]conclusion. I had better explain, Mr. Trewilyan, how I

[073-182]have been at work from the beginning about watching the

[073-183]Colonel. I couldn't do nothing with the porter at the

[073-184]Albany, which he is always mostly muzzled with beer, and

[073-185]he wouldn't have taken my money, not on the square. So,

[073-186]when it was tellegrammed to me as the Colonel was on the

[073-187]move in the North, I put on two boys as knows the Colonel,

[073-188]at eighteenpence a day, at each end, one Piccadilly end,

[073-189]and the other Saville Row end, and yesterday morning,

[073-190]as quick as ever could be, after the Limited Express

[073-191]Edinburgh Male Up was in, there comes the Saville Row End

[073-192]Boy here to say as the Colonel was lodged safe in his

[073-193]downey. Then I was off immediate myself to St. Diddulph's,

[073-194]because I knows what it is to trust to Inferiors when

[073-195]matters gets delicate. Now, there hadn't been no letters

[073-196]from the Colonel, nor none to him as I could make out,

[073-197]though that mightn't be so sure. She might have had 'em

[073-198]addressed to A. Z., or the like of that, at any of the

[073-199]Post-offices as was distant, as nobody could give the

[073-200]notice to 'em all. Barring the money, which I know ain't

[073-201]an object when the end is so desirable, it don't do to be

[073-202]too ubiketous, because things will go astray. But I've

[073-203]kept my eye uncommon open, and I don't think there have

[073-204]been no letters since that last which was sent, Mr.

[073-205]Trewilyan, let any of 'em, parsons, or what not, say what

[073-206]they will. And I don't see as parsons are better than

[073-207]other folk when they has to do with a lady as likes her

[073-208]fancy-man.
