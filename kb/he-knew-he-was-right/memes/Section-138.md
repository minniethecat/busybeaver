## Section 138

[138-001]He had had two enemies, and between them they had worked his ruin.

[138-002]These were [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] and Bozzle. It may be doubted whether he

[138-003]did not hate the latter the more strongly of the two. He knew now

[138-004]that Bozzle had been untrue to him, but his disgust did not spring

[138-005]from that so much as from the feeling that he had defiled himself by

[138-006]dealing with the man. Though he was quite assured that he had been

[138-007]right in his first cause of offence, he knew that he had fallen from

[138-008]bad to worse in every step that he had taken since. Colonel Osborne

[138-009]had marred his happiness by vanity, by wicked intrigue, by a devilish

[138-010]delight in doing mischief; but he, he himself, had consummated the

[138-011]evil by his own folly. Why had he not taken Colonel Osborne by the

[138-012]throat, instead of going to a low-born, vile, mercenary spy for

[138-013]assistance? He hated himself for what he had done;--and yet it was

[138-014]impossible that he should yield.

[138-015]It was impossible that he should yield;--but it was yet open to him

[138-016]to sacrifice himself. He could not go back to his wife and say that

[138-017]he was wrong; but he could determine that the destruction should

[138-018]fall upon him and not upon her. If he gave up his child and then

[138-019]died,--died, alone, without any friend near him, with no word of love

[138-020]in his ears, in that solitary and miserable abode which he had found

[138-021]for himself,--then it would at least be acknowledged that he had

[138-022]expiated the injury that he had done. She would have his [[money|wealth]], his

[138-023]name, his child to comfort her,--and would be troubled no longer by

[138-024]demands for that obedience which she had sworn at the altar to give

[138-025]him, and which she had since declined to render to him. Perhaps there

[138-026]was some feeling that the coals of fire would be hot upon her head

[138-027]when she should think how much she had received from him and how

[138-028]little she had done for him. And yet he loved her, with all his

[138-029]heart, and would even yet dream of bliss that might be possible with

[138-030]her,--had not the terrible hand of irresistible Fate come between

[138-031]them and marred it all. It was only a dream now. It could be no more

[138-032]than a dream. He put out his thin wasted hands and looked at them,

[138-033]and touched the hollowness of his own cheeks, and coughed that he

[138-034]might hear the hacking sound of his own infirmity, and almost took

[138-035]glory in his weakness. It could not be long before the coals of fire

[138-036]would be heaped upon her head.

[138-037]"Louey," he said at last, addressing the child who had sat for an

[138-038]hour gazing through the window without stirring a limb or uttering a

[138-039]sound; "Louey, my boy, would you like to go back to mamma?" The child

[138-040]turned round on the floor, and fixed his eyes on his father's face,

[138-041]but made no immediate reply. "Louey, dear, come to papa and tell him.

[138-042]Would it be nice to go back to mamma?" And he stretched out his hand

[138-043]to the boy. Louey got up, and approached slowly and stood between his

[138-044]father's knees. "Tell me, darling;--you understand what papa says?"

[138-045]"Altro!" said the boy, who had been long enough among Italian

[138-046]servants to pick up the common words of the language. Of course he

[138-047]would like to go back. How indeed could it be otherwise?

[138-048]"Then you shall go to her, Louey."

[138-049]"To-day, papa?"

[138-050]"Not to-day, nor to-morrow."

[138-051]"But the day after?"

[138-052]"That is sufficient. You shall go. It is not so bad with you that one

[138-053]day more need be a sorrow to you. You shall go,--and then you will

[138-054]never see your father again!" Trevelyan as he said this drew his

[138-055]hands away so as not to touch the child. The little fellow had put

[138-056]out his arm, but seeing his father's angry gesture had made no

[138-057]further attempt at a caress. He feared his father from the bottom of

[138-058]his little heart, and yet was aware that it was his duty to try to

[138-059]love papa. He did not understand the meaning of that last threat,

[138-060]but slunk back, passing his untouched toys, to the window, and there

[138-061]seated himself again, filling his mind with the thought that when two

[138-062]more long long days should have crept by, he should once more go to

[138-063]his mother.

[138-064]Trevelyan had tried his best to be soft and gentle to his child.

[138-065]All that he had said to his wife of his treatment of the boy had

[138-066]been true to the letter. He had spared no personal trouble, he had

[138-067]done all that he had known how to do, he had exercised all his

[138-068]intelligence to procure amusement for the boy;--but Louey had hardly

[138-069]smiled since he had been taken from his mother. And now that he was

[138-070]told that he was to go and never see his father again, the tidings

[138-071]were to him simply tidings of joy. "There is a curse upon me," said

[138-072]Trevelyan; "it is written down in the book of my destiny that nothing

[138-073]shall ever love me!"

[138-074]He went out from the house, and made his way down by the narrow path

[138-075]through the olives and vines to the bottom of the hill in front of

[138-076]the villa. It was evening now, but the evening was very hot, and

[138-077]though the olive trees stood in long rows, there was no shade. Quite

[138-078]at the bottom of the hill there was a little sluggish muddy brook,

[138-079]along the sides of which the reeds grew thickly and the dragon-flies

[138-080]were playing on the water. There was nothing attractive in the spot,

[138-081]but he was weary, and sat himself down on the dry hard bank which had

[138-082]been made by repeated clearing of mud from the bottom of the little

[138-083]rivulet. He sat watching the dragon-flies as they made their short

[138-084]flights in the warm air, and told himself that of all God's creatures

[138-085]there was not one to whom less power of disporting itself in God's

[138-086]sun was given than to him. Surely it would be better for him that he

[138-087]should die, than live as he was now living without any of the joys of

[138-088]life. The solitude of Casalunga was intolerable to him, and yet there

[138-089]was no whither that he could go and find society. He could travel if

[138-090]he pleased. He had money at command, and, at any rate as yet, there

[138-091]was no embargo on his personal [[personal-freedom|liberty]]. But how could he travel

[138-092]alone,--even if his strength might suffice for the work? There had

[138-093]been moments in which he had thought that he would be happy in the

[138-094]love of his child,--that the companionship of an infant would suffice

[138-095]for him if only the infant would love him. But all such dreams as

[138-096]that were over. To repay him for his tenderness his boy was always

[138-097]dumb before him. Louey would not prattle as he had used to do. He

[138-098]would not even smile, or give back the kisses with which his father

[138-099]had attempted to win him. In mercy to the boy he would send him back

[138-100]to his mother;--in mercy to the boy if not to the mother also. It was

[138-101]in vain that he should look for any joy in any quarter. Were he to

[138-102]return to England, they would say that he was mad!

[138-103][Illustration: Trevelyan at Casalunga.]

[138-104]He lay there by the brook-side till the evening was far advanced,

[138-105]and then he arose and slowly returned to the house. The labour of

[138-106]ascending the hill was so great to him that he was forced to pause

[138-107]and hold by the olive trees as he slowly performed his task. The

[138-108]perspiration came in profusion from his pores, and he found himself

[138-109]to be so weak that he must in future regard the brook as being beyond

[138-110]the tether of his daily exercise. Eighteen months ago he had been a

[138-111]strong walker, and the snow-bound paths of Swiss mountains had been

[138-112]a joy to him. He paused as he was slowly dragging himself on, and

[138-113]looked up at the wretched, desolate, comfortless abode which he

[138-114]called his home. Its dreariness was so odious to him that he was

[138-115]half-minded to lay himself down where he was, and let the night air

[138-116]come upon him and do its worst. In such case, however, some Italian

[138-117]doctor would be sent down who would say that he was mad. Above

[138-118]all the things, and to the last, he must save himself from that

[138-119]degradation.

[138-120]When he had crawled up to the house, he went to his child, and found

[138-121]that the woman had put the boy to bed. Then he was angry with himself

[138-122]in that he himself had not seen to this, and kept up his practice

[138-123]of attending the child to the last. He would, at least, be true to

[138-124]his resolution, and prepare for the boy's return to his mother. Not

[138-125]knowing how otherwise to manage it, he wrote that night the following

[138-126]note to Mr. Glascock;--

[138-127]Casalunga, Thursday night.

[138-128]MY DEAR SIR,

[138-129]Since you last were considerate enough to call upon me I

[138-130]have resolved to take a step in my affairs which, though

[138-131]it will rob me of my only remaining gratification, will

[138-132]tend to lessen the troubles under which Mrs. Trevelyan is

[138-133]labouring. If she desires it, as no doubt she does, I will

[138-134]consent to place our boy again in her custody,--trusting

[138-135]to her sense of honour to restore him to me should I

[138-136]demand it. In my present unfortunate position I cannot

[138-137]suggest that she should come for the boy. I am unable to

[138-138]support the excitement occasioned by her presence. I will,

[138-139]however, deliver up my darling either to you, or to any

[138-140]messenger sent by you whom I can trust. I beg heartily

[138-141]to apologise for the trouble I am giving you, and to

[138-142]subscribe myself yours very faithfully,

[138-143]LOUIS TREVELYAN.

[138-144]The Hon. C. Glascock.

[138-145]P.S.--It is as well, perhaps, that I should explain that

[138-146]I must decline to receive any visit from Sir Marmaduke

[138-147]Rowley. Sir Marmaduke has insulted me grossly on each

[138-148]occasion on which I have seen him since his return home.

[138-149]CHAPTER LXXXV.

[138-150]THE BATHS OF LUCCA.

[138-151]June was now far advanced, and the Rowleys and the Spaldings had

[138-152]removed from Florence to the Baths of Lucca. Mr. Glascock had

[138-153]followed in their wake, and the whole party were living at the Baths

[138-154]in one of those hotels in which so many English and Americans are

[138-155]wont to congregate in the early weeks of the Italian summer. The

[138-156]marriage was to take place in the last week of the month; and all

[138-157]the party were to return to Florence for the occasion,--with the

[138-158]exception of Sir Marmaduke and Mrs. Trevelyan. She was altogether

[138-159]unfitted for wedding joys, and her father had promised to bear her

[138-160]company when the others left her. Mr. Glascock and Caroline Spalding

[138-161]were to be married in Florence, and were to depart immediately from

[138-162]thence for some of the cooler parts of Switzerland. After that

[138-163]Sir Marmaduke and Lady Rowley were to return to [[london|London]] with their

[138-164]daughters, preparatory to that dreary journey back to the Mandarins;

[138-165]and they had not even yet resolved what they had better do respecting

[138-166]that unfortunate man who was living in seclusion on the hill-top near

[138-167]Siena. They had consulted lawyers and doctors in Florence, but it had

[138-168]seemed that everybody there was afraid of putting the law in force

[138-169]against an Englishman. Doubtless there was a law in respect to the

[138-170]custody of the insane; and it was admitted that if Trevelyan were

[138-171]dangerously mad something could be done; but it seemed that nobody

[138-172]was willing to stir in such a case as that which now existed.

[138-173]Something, it was said, might be done at some future time; but the

[138-174]difficulties were so great that nothing could be done now.
