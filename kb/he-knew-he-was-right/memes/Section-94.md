## Section 94

[094-001]"My husband!"

[094-002]"He will, I suppose, be at least as dear to you as a brother?"

[094-003]"I never had a brother."

[094-004]"Your sister, then! It is the same, I suppose?"

[094-005]"If I were to have a husband, I hope he would be the dearest to me

[094-006]of all. Unless he were so, he certainly would not be my husband. But

[094-007]between a man and his wife there does not spring up that playful,

[094-008]violent intimacy admitting of all liberties, which comes from early

[094-009]nursery associations; and then, there is the difference of sex."

[094-010]"I should not like my wife to call me a fool," he said.

[094-011]"I hope she may never have occasion to do so, Mr. Glascock. Marry an

[094-012]English wife in your own [[social-class|class]],--as, of course, you will,--and then

[094-013]you will be safe."

[094-014]"But I have set my heart fast on marrying an American wife," he said.

[094-015]"Then I can't tell what may befall you. It's like enough, if you do

[094-016]that, that you may be called by some name you will think hard to

[094-017]bear. But you'll think better of it. Like should pair with like, Mr.

[094-018]Glascock. If you were to marry one of our young women, you would lose

[094-019]in [[pride|dignity]] as much as she would lose in comfort." Then they parted,

[094-020]and she went off to say farewell to other guests. The manner in which

[094-021]she had answered what he had said to her had certainly been of a

[094-022]nature to stop any further speech of the same kind. Had she been

[094-023]gentle with him, then he would certainly have told her that she was

[094-024]the American woman whom he desired to take with him to his home in

[094-025]England.

[094-026]CHAPTER LVII.

[094-027]DOROTHY'S FATE.

[094-028]Towards the end of February Sir Peter Mancrudy declared Miss Stanbury

[094-029]to be out of danger, and Mr. Martin began to be sprightly on the

[094-030]subject, taking to himself no inconsiderable share of the praise

[094-031]accruing to the medical faculty in [[exeter|Exeter]] generally for the saving of

[094-032]a life so valuable to the city. "Yes, Mr. Burgess," Sir Peter said

[094-033]to old Barty of the bank, "our friend will get over it this time,

[094-034]and without any serious damage to her constitution, if she will only

[094-035]take care of herself." Barty made some inaudible grunt, intended

[094-036]to indicate his own indifference on the subject, and expressed his

[094-037]opinion to the chief clerk that old Jemima Wideawake,--as he was

[094-038]pleased to call her,--was one of those tough customers who would

[094-039]never die. "It would be nothing to us, Mr. Barty, one way or the

[094-040]other," said the clerk; to which Barty Burgess assented with another

[094-041]grunt.

[094-042]Camilla French declared that she was delighted to hear the news. At

[094-043]this time there had been some sort of a reconciliation between her

[094-044]and her lover. Mrs. French had extracted from him a promise that he

[094-045]would not go to Natal; and Camilla had commenced the preparations for

[094-046]her wedding. His visits to Heavitree were as few and far between as

[094-047]he could make them with any regard to decency; but the 31st of March

[094-048]was coming on quickly, and as he was to be made a possession of them

[094-049]for ever, it was considered to be safe and well to allow him some

[094-050][[personal-freedom|liberty]] in his present condition. "My dear, if they are driven, there

[094-051]is no knowing what they won't do," Mrs. French said to her daughter.

[094-052]Camilla had submitted with compressed lips and a slight nod of her

[094-053]head. She had worked very hard, but her day of reward was coming. It

[094-054]was impossible not to perceive,--both for her and her mother,--that

[094-055]the scantiness of Mr. Gibson's attention to his future bride was

[094-056]cause of some weak triumph to Arabella. She said that it was very odd

[094-057]that he did not come,--and once added with a little sigh that he used

[094-058]to come in former days, alluding to those happy days in which another

[094-059]love was paramount. Camilla could not endure this with an equal mind.

[094-060]"Bella, dear," she said, "we know what all that means. He has made

[094-061]his choice, and if I am satisfied with what he does now, surely you

[094-062]need not grumble." Miss Stanbury's illness had undoubtedly been a

[094-063]great source of contentment to the family at Heavitree, as they had

[094-064]all been able to argue that her impending demise was the natural

[094-065]consequence of her great sin in the matter of Dorothy's proposed

[094-066]marriage. When, however, they heard from Mr. Martin that she would

[094-067]certainly recover, that Sir Peter's edict to that effect had gone

[094-068]forth, they were willing to acknowledge that Providence, having so

[094-069]far punished the sinner, was right in staying its hand and abstaining

[094-070]from the final blow. "I'm sure we are delighted," said Mrs. French,

[094-071]"for though she has said cruel things of us,--and so untrue too,--yet

[094-072]of course it is our duty to forgive her. And we do forgive her."

[094-073]Dorothy had written three or four notes to Brooke since his

[094-074]departure, which contained simple bulletins of her aunt's health.

[094-075]She always began her letters with "My dear Mr. Burgess," and ended

[094-076]them with "yours truly." She never made any allusion to Brooke's

[094-077]declaration of love, or gave the slightest sign in her letters to

[094-078]shew that she even remembered it. At last she wrote to say that her

[094-079]aunt was convalescent; and, in making this announcement, she allowed

[094-080]herself some enthusiasm of expression. She was so happy, and was so

[094-081]sure that Mr. Burgess would be equally so! And her aunt had asked

[094-082]after her "dear Brooke," expressing her great satisfaction with

[094-083]him, in that he had come down to see her when she had been almost

[094-084]too ill to see any one. In answer to this there came to her a real

[094-085]love-letter from Brooke Burgess. It was the first occasion on

[094-086]which he had written to her. The little bulletins had demanded no

[094-087]replies, and had received none. Perhaps there had been a shade of

[094-088]disappointment on Dorothy's side, in that she had written thrice, and

[094-089]had been made rich with no word in return. But, although her heart

[094-090]had palpitated on hearing the postman's knock, and had palpitated in

[094-091]vain, she had told herself that it was all as it should be. She wrote

[094-092]to him, because she possessed information which it was necessary that

[094-093]she should communicate. He did not write to her, because there was

[094-094]nothing for him to tell. Then had come the love-letter, and in the

[094-095]love-letter there was an imperative demand for a reply.

[094-096]What was she to do? To have recourse to Priscilla for advice was

[094-097]her first idea; but she herself believed that she owed a debt

[094-098]of gratitude to her aunt, which Priscilla would not take into

[094-099]account,--the existence of which Priscilla would by no means

[094-100]admit. She knew Priscilla's mind in this matter, and was sure that

[094-101]Priscilla's advice, whatever it might be, would be given without any

[094-102]regard to her aunt's views. And then Dorothy was altogether ignorant

[094-103]of her aunt's views. Her aunt had been very anxious that she should

[094-104]marry Mr. Gibson, but had clearly never admitted into her mind the

[094-105]idea that she might possibly marry Brooke Burgess; and it seemed to

[094-106]her that she herself would be dishonest, both to her aunt and to her

[094-107]lover, if she were to bind this man to herself without her aunt's

[094-108][[truth|knowledge]]. He was to be her aunt's heir, and she was maintained by

[094-109]her aunt's liberality! Thinking of all this, she at last resolved

[094-110]that she would take the bull by the horns, and tell her aunt. She

[094-111]felt that the task would be one almost beyond her strength. Thrice

[094-112]she went into her aunt's room, intending to make a clean breast.

[094-113]Thrice her courage failed her, and she left the room with her tale

[094-114]untold, excusing herself on various pretexts. Her aunt had seemed to

[094-115]be not quite so well, or had declared herself to be tired, or had

[094-116]been a little cross;--or else Martha had come in at the nick of time.

[094-117]But there was Brooke Burgess's letter unanswered,--a letter that was

[094-118]read night and morning, and which was never for an instant out of her

[094-119]mind. He had demanded a reply, and he had a right at least to that.

[094-120]The letter had been with her for four entire days before she had

[094-121]ventured to speak to her aunt on the subject.

[094-122]On the first of March Miss Stanbury came out of her bed-room for the

[094-123]first time. Dorothy, on the previous day, had decided on postponing

[094-124]her communication for this occasion; but, when she found herself

[094-125]sitting in the little sitting-room up-stairs close at her aunt's

[094-126]elbow, and perceived the signs of weakness which the new move had

[094-127]made conspicuous, and heard the invalid declare that the little

[094-128]journey had been almost too much for her, her heart misgave her. She

[094-129]ought to have told her tale while her aunt was still in bed. But

[094-130]presently there came a question, which put her into such a flutter

[094-131]that she was for the time devoid of all resolution. "Has Brooke

[094-132]written?" said Miss Stanbury.

[094-133]"Yes,--aunt; he has written."

[094-134]"And what did he say?" Dorothy was struck quite dumb. "Is there

[094-135]anything wrong?" And now, as Miss Stanbury asked the question, she

[094-136]seemed herself to have forgotten that she had two minutes before

[094-137]declared herself to be almost too feeble to speak. "I'm sure there is

[094-138]something wrong. What is it? I will know."

[094-139]"There is nothing wrong, Aunt Stanbury."

[094-140]"Where is the letter? Let me see it."

[094-141]"I mean there is nothing wrong about him."

[094-142]"What is it, then?"

[094-143]"He is quite well, Aunt Stanbury."

[094-144]"Shew me the letter. I will see the letter. I know that there is

[094-145]something the matter. Do you mean to say you won't shew me Brooke's

[094-146]letter?"

[094-147]There was a moment's pause before Dorothy answered. "I will shew you

[094-148]his letter;--though I am sure he didn't mean that I should shew it to

[094-149]anyone."

[094-150]"He hasn't written evil of me?"

[094-151]"No; no; no. He would sooner cut his hand off than say a word bad

[094-152]of you. He never says or writes anything bad of anybody. But--. Oh,

[094-153]aunt; I'll tell you everything. I should have told you before, only

[094-154]that you were ill."

[094-155]Then Miss Stanbury was frightened. "What is it?" she said hoarsely,

[094-156]clasping the arms of the great chair, each with a thin, shrivelled

[094-157]hand.

[094-158]"Aunt Stanbury, Brooke,--Brooke,--wants me to be his--wife!"

[094-159][Illustration: "Brooke wants me to be his wife."]

[094-160]"What!"

[094-161]"You cannot be more surprised than I have been, Aunt Stanbury; and

[094-162]there has been no fault of mine."

[094-163]"I don't believe it," said the old woman.

[094-164]"Now you may read the letter," said Dorothy, [[reputation|standing]] up. She was

[094-165]quite prepared to be obedient, but she felt that her aunt's manner of

[094-166]receiving the information was almost an insult.

[094-167]"He must be a fool," said Miss Stanbury.

[094-168]This was hard to bear, and the colour went and came rapidly across

[094-169]Dorothy's cheeks as she gave herself a few moments to prepare an

[094-170]answer. She already perceived that her aunt would be altogether

[094-171]adverse to the marriage, and that therefore the marriage could never

[094-172]take place. She had never for a moment allowed herself to think

[094-173]otherwise, but, nevertheless, the blow was heavy on her. We all know

[094-174]how constantly hope and expectation will rise high within our own

[094-175]bosoms in opposition to our own judgment,--how we become sanguine

[094-176]in regard to events which we almost know can never come to pass. So

[094-177]it had been with Dorothy. Her heart had been almost in a flutter of

[094-178]happiness since she had had Brooke's letter in her possession, and

[094-179]yet she never ceased to declare to herself her own conviction that

[094-180]that letter could lead to no good result. In regard to her own wishes

[094-181]on the subject she had never asked herself a single question. As it

[094-182]had been quite beyond her power to bring herself to endure the idea

[094-183]of marrying Mr. Gibson, so it had been quite impossible to her not

[094-184]to long to be Brooke's wife from the moment in which a suggestion to

[094-185]that effect had fallen from his lips. This was a state of things so

[094-186]certain, so much a matter of course, that, though she had not spoken

[094-187]a word to him in which she owned her love, she had never for a moment

[094-188]doubted that he knew the truth,--and that everybody else concerned

[094-189]would know it too. But she did not suppose that her wishes would go

[094-190]for anything with her aunt. Brooke Burgess was to become a rich man

[094-191]as her aunt's heir, and her aunt would of course have her own ideas

[094-192]about Brooke's advancement in life. She was quite prepared to submit

[094-193]without quarrelling when her aunt should tell her that the idea must

[094-194]not be entertained. But the order might be given, the prohibition

[094-195]might be pronounced, without an insult to her own feelings as a

[094-196]woman. "He must be a fool," Miss Stanbury had said, and Dorothy took

[094-197]time to collect her thoughts before she would reply. In the meantime

[094-198]her aunt finished the reading of the letter.
