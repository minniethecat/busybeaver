## Section 148

[148-001]"He has changed his mind about that, I think."

[148-002]"Perhaps he has; but he behaved very badly then. Let him shew up his

[148-003][[money|income]];--that, I take it, is the question in such a case as this. His

[148-004]father was a clergyman, and therefore I suppose he must be considered

[148-005]to be a [[social-class|gentleman]]. But has he means to support a wife, and keep up a

[148-006]house in [[london|London]]? If he has not, that is an end to it, I should say."

[148-007]But Sir Marmaduke could not see his way to any such end, and,

[148-008]although he still looked black upon Nora, and talked to his wife

[148-009]of his determination to stand no contumacy, and hinted at cursing,

[148-010]disinheriting, and the like, he began to perceive that Nora would

[148-011]have her own way. In his unhappiness he regretted this visit to

[148-012]England, and almost thought that the Mandarins were a pleasanter

[148-013]residence than London. He could do pretty much as he pleased there,

[148-014]and could live quietly, without the trouble which encountered him now

[148-015]on every side.

[148-016]Nora, immediately on her return to London, had written a note to

[148-017]Hugh, simply telling him of her arrival and begging him to come and

[148-018]see her. "Mamma," she said, "I must see him, and it would be nonsense

[148-019]to say that he must not come here. I have done what I have said

[148-020]I would do, and you ought not to make difficulties." Lady Rowley

[148-021]declared that Sir Marmaduke would be very angry if Hugh were admitted

[148-022]without his express permission. "I don't want to do anything in the

[148-023]dark," continued Nora, "but of course I must see him. I suppose it

[148-024]will be better that he should come to me than that I should go to

[148-025]him?" Lady Rowley quite understood the threat that was conveyed in

[148-026]this. It would be much better that Hugh should come to the hotel, and

[148-027]that he should be treated then as an accepted lover. She had come to

[148-028]that conclusion. But she was obliged to vacillate for awhile between

[148-029]her husband and her daughter. Hugh came of course, and Sir Marmaduke,

[148-030]by his wife's advice, kept out of the way. Lady Rowley, though she

[148-031]was at home, kept herself also out of the way, remaining above with

[148-032]her two other daughters. Nora thus achieved the glory and happiness

[148-033]of receiving her lover alone.

[148-034]"My own true girl!" he said, speaking with his arms still round her

[148-035]waist.

[148-036]"I am true enough; but whether I am your own,--that is another

[148-037]question."

[148-038]"You mean to be?"

[148-039]"But papa doesn't mean it. Papa says that you are nobody, and that

[148-040]you haven't got an income; and thinks that I had better go back and

[148-041]be an old maid at the Mandarins."

[148-042]"And what do you think yourself, Nora?"

[148-043]"What do I think? As far as I can understand, young ladies are not

[148-044]allowed to think at all. They have to do what their papas tell them.

[148-045]That will do, Hugh. You can talk without taking hold of me."

[148-046]"It is such a time since I have had a hold of you,--as you call it."

[148-047]"It will be much longer before you can do so again, if I go back

[148-048]to the Islands with papa. I shall expect you to be true, you know;

[148-049]and it will be ten years at the least before I can hope to be home

[148-050]again."

[148-051]"I don't think you mean to go, Nora."

[148-052]"But what am I to do? That idea of yours of walking out to the

[148-053]next church and getting ourselves married sounds very nice and

[148-054]independent, but you know that it is not practicable."

[148-055]"On the other hand, I know it is."

[148-056]"It is not practicable for me, Hugh. Of all things in the world I

[148-057]don't want to be a Lydia. I won't do anything that anybody shall ever

[148-058]say that your wife ought not to have done. Young women when they are

[148-059]married ought to have their papas' and mammas' consent. I have been

[148-060]thinking about it a great deal for the last month or two, and I have

[148-061]made up my mind to that."

[148-062]"What is it all to come to, then?"

[148-063]"I mean to get papa's consent. That is what it is to come to."

[148-064]"And if he is obstinate?"

[148-065]"I shall coax him round at last. When the time for going comes, he'll

[148-066]yield then."

[148-067]"But you will not go with them?" As he asked this he came to her and

[148-068]tried again to take her by the waist; but she retreated from him, and

[148-069]got herself clear from his arm. "If you are afraid of me, I shall

[148-070]know that you think it possible that we may be parted."

[148-071]"I am not a bit afraid of you, Hugh."

[148-072]"Nora, I think you ought to tell me something definitely."

[148-073]"I think I have been definite enough, sir. You may be sure of this,

[148-074]however;--I will not go back to the Islands."

[148-075]"Give me your hand on that."

[148-076]"There is my hand. But, remember;--I had told you just as much

[148-077]before. I don't mean to go back. I mean to stay here. I mean;--but I

[148-078]do not think I will tell you all the things I mean to do."

[148-079]"You mean to be my wife?"

[148-080]"Certainly;--some day, when the difficulty about the chairs and

[148-081]tables can settle itself. The real question now is,--what am I to do

[148-082]with myself when papa and mamma are gone?"

[148-083]"Become Mrs. H. Stanbury at once. Chairs and tables! You shall have

[148-084]chairs and tables as many as you want. You won't be too proud to live

[148-085]in lodgings for a few months?"

[148-086]"There must be preliminaries, Hugh,--even for lodgings, though they

[148-087]may be very slender. Papa goes in less than three weeks now, and

[148-088]mamma has got something else to think of than my marriage garments.

[148-089]And then there are all manner of difficulties, money difficulties

[148-090]and others, out of which I don't see my way yet." Hugh began to

[148-091]asseverate that it was his business to help her through all money

[148-092]difficulties as well as others; but she soon stopped his eloquence.

[148-093]"It will be by-and-by, Hugh, and I hope you'll support the burden

[148-094]like a man; but just at present there is a hitch. I shouldn't have

[148-095]come over at all;--I should have stayed with Emily in Italy, had I

[148-096]not thought that I was bound to see you."

[148-097]"My own darling!"

[148-098]"When papa goes, I think that I had better go back to her."

[148-099]"I'll take you!" said Hugh, picturing to himself all the pleasures of

[148-100]such a tour together over the Alps.

[148-101]"No you won't, because that would be improper. When we travel

[148-102]together we must go Darby and Joan fashion, as man and wife. I think

[148-103]I had better go back to Emily, because her position there is so

[148-104]terrible. There must come some end to it, I suppose soon. He will be

[148-105]better, or he will become so bad that,--that medical interference

[148-106]will be unavoidable. But I do not like that she should be alone. She

[148-107]gave me a home when she had one;--and I must always remember that

[148-108]I met you there." After this there was of course another attempt

[148-109]with Hugh's right arm, which on this occasion was not altogether

[148-110]unsuccessful. And then she told him of her friendship for Mr.

[148-111]Glascock's wife, and of her intention at some future time to visit

[148-112]them at Monkhams.

[148-113][Illustration: "I must always remember that I met you there."]

[148-114]"And see all the glories that might have been your own," he said.

[148-115]"And think of the young man who has robbed me of them all! And you

[148-116]are to go there too, so that you may see what you have done. There

[148-117]was a time, Hugh, when I was very nearly pleasing all my friends

[148-118]and shewing myself to be a young lady of high taste and noble

[148-119]fortune,--and an obedient, good girl."

[148-120]"And why didn't you?"

[148-121]"I thought I would wait just a little longer.

[148-122]Because,--because,--because--. Oh, Hugh, how cross you were to me

[148-123]afterwards when you came down to Nuncombe and would hardly speak to

[148-124]me!"

[148-125]"And why didn't I speak to you?"

[148-126]"I don't know. Because you were cross, and surly, and thinking of

[148-127]nothing but your tobacco, I believe. Do you remember how we walked to

[148-128]Niddon, and you hadn't a word for anybody?"

[148-129]"I remember I wanted you to go down to the river with me, and you

[148-130]wouldn't go."

[148-131]"You asked me only once, and I did so long to go with you. Do you

[148-132]remember the rocks in the river? I remember the place as though I saw

[148-133]it now; and how I longed to jump from one stone to another. Hugh, if

[148-134]we are ever married, you must take me there, and let me jump on those

[148-135]stones."

[148-136]"You pretended that you could not think of wetting your feet."

[148-137]"Of course I pretended,--because you were so cross, and so cold. Oh,

[148-138]dear! I wonder whether you will ever know it all."

[148-139]"Don't I know it all now?"

[148-140]"I suppose you do, nearly. There is mighty little of a secret in it,

[148-141]and it is the same thing that is going on always. Only it seems so

[148-142]strange to me that I should ever have loved any one so dearly,--and

[148-143]that for next to no reason at all. You never made yourself very

[148-144]charming that I know of;--did you?"

[148-145]"I did my best. It wasn't much, I dare say."

[148-146]"You did nothing, sir,--except just let me fall in love with you. And

[148-147]you were not quite sure that you would let me do that."

[148-148]"Nora, I don't think you do understand."

[148-149]"I do;--perfectly. Why were you cross with me, instead of saying one

[148-150]nice word when you were down at Nuncombe? I do understand."

[148-151]"Why was it?"

[148-152]"Because you did not think well enough of me to believe that I would

[148-153]give myself to a man who had no fortune of his own. I know it now,

[148-154]and I knew it then; and therefore I wouldn't dabble in the river with

[148-155]you. But it's all over now, and we'll go and get wet together like

[148-156]dear little children, and Priscilla shall scold us when we come

[148-157]back."

[148-158]They were alone in the sitting-room for more than an hour, and Lady

[148-159]Rowley was patient up-stairs as mothers will be patient in such

[148-160]emergencies. Sophie and Lucy had gone out and left her; and there

[148-161]she remained telling herself, as the weary minutes went by, that as

[148-162]the thing was to be, it was well that the young people should be

[148-163]together. [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] could never be to her what Mr. Glascock would

[148-164]have been,--a son-in-law to sit and think about, and dream of, and

[148-165]be proud of,--whose existence as her son-in-law would in itself have

[148-166]been a happiness to her out in her banishment at the other side of

[148-167]the world; but nevertheless it was natural to her, as a soft-hearted

[148-168]loving mother with many daughters, that any son-in-law should be dear

[148-169]to her. Now that she had gradually brought herself round to believe

[148-170]in Nora's marriage, she was disposed to make the best of Hugh, to

[148-171]remember that he was certainly a clever man, that he was an honest

[148-172]fellow, and that she had heard of him as a good son and a kind

[148-173]brother, and that he had behaved well in reference to her Emily and

[148-174]Trevelyan. She was quite willing now that Hugh should be happy, and

[148-175]she sat there thinking that the time was very long, but still waiting

[148-176]patiently till she should be summoned. "You must let me go for mamma

[148-177]for a moment," Nora said. "I want you to see her and make yourself a

[148-178]good boy before her. If you are ever to be her son-in-law, you ought

[148-179]to be in her good graces." Hugh declared that he would do his best,

[148-180]and Nora fetched her mother.
