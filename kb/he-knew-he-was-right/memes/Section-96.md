## Section 96

[096-001]"Hugh, indeed! He's to be brought in between us;--is he?"

[096-002]"He's my brother, and of course I'm obliged to think of him. And if

[096-003]you please, I'll go home as soon as you are well enough to spare me."

[096-004]Quickly after this there were very many letters coming and going

[096-005]between the house in the Close and the ladies at [[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]], and

[096-006][[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] and Brooke Burgess. The correspondent of Brooke Burgess

[096-007]was of course Miss Stanbury herself. The letters to Hugh and to

[096-008]Nuncombe Putney were written by Dorothy. Of the former we need be

[096-009]told nothing at the present moment; but the upshot of all poor

[096-010]Dolly's letters was, that on the tenth of March she was to return

[096-011]home to Nuncombe Putney, share once more her sister's bed and

[096-012]mother's poverty, and abandon the comforts of the Close. Before

[096-013]this became a definite arrangement Miss Stanbury had given way in a

[096-014]certain small degree. She had acknowledged that Dorothy had intended

[096-015]no harm. But this was not enough for Dorothy, who was conscious of

[096-016]no harm either done or intended. She did not specify her terms, or

[096-017]require specifically that her aunt should make apology for that word

[096-018]immodest, or at least withdraw it; but she resolved that she would go

[096-019]unless it was most absolutely declared to have been applied to her

[096-020]without the slightest reason. She felt, moreover, that her aunt's

[096-021]house ought to be open to Brooke Burgess, and that it could not be

[096-022]open to them both. And so she went;--having resided under her aunt's

[096-023]roof between nine and ten months.

[096-024]"Good-bye, Aunt Stanbury," said Dorothy, kissing her aunt, with a

[096-025]tear in her eye and a sob in her throat.

[096-026]"Good-bye, my dear, good-bye." And Miss Stanbury, as she pressed her

[096-027]niece's hand, left in it a bank-note.

[096-028]"I'm much obliged, aunt; I am indeed; but I'd rather not." And the

[096-029]bank-note was left on the parlour table.

[096-030]CHAPTER LVIII.

[096-031]DOROTHY AT HOME.

[096-032]Dorothy was received at home with so much affection and such

[096-033]expressions of esteem as to afford her much consolation in her

[096-034]misery. Both her mother and her sister approved of her conduct.

[096-035]Mrs. Stanbury's approval was indeed accompanied by many expressions

[096-036]of regret as to the good things lost. She was fully alive to the

[096-037]fact that life in the Close at [[exeter|Exeter]] was better for her daughter

[096-038]than life in their little cottage at Nuncombe Putney. The outward

[096-039]appearance which Dorothy bore on her return home was [[truth|proof]] of this.

[096-040]Her clothes, the set of her hair, her very gestures and motions had

[096-041]framed themselves on town ideas. The faded, wildered, washed-out

[096-042]look, the uncertain, purposeless bearing which had come from her

[096-043]secluded life and subjection to her sister had vanished from her.

[096-044]She had lived among people, and had learned something of their gait

[096-045]and carriage. [[money|Money]] we know will do almost everything, and no doubt

[096-046]money had had much to do with this. It is very pretty to talk of the

[096-047]alluring simplicity of a clean calico gown; but poverty will shew

[096-048]itself to be meagre, dowdy, and draggled in a woman's dress, let

[096-049]the woman be ever so simple, ever so neat, ever so independent, and

[096-050]ever so high-hearted. Mrs. Stanbury was quite alive to all that her

[096-051]younger daughter was losing. Had she not received two offers of

[096-052]marriage while she was at Exeter? There was no possibility that

[096-053]offers of marriage should be made in the cottage at Nuncombe Putney.

[096-054]A man within the walls of the cottage would have been considered as

[096-055]much out of place as a wild bull. It had been matter of deep regret

[096-056]to Mrs. Stanbury that her daughter should not have found herself able

[096-057]to marry Mr. Gibson. She knew that there was no matter for reproach

[096-058]in this, but it was a misfortune,--a great misfortune. And in the

[096-059]mother's breast there had been a sad, unrepressed feeling of regret

[096-060]that young people should so often lose their chances in the world

[096-061]through over-fancifulness, and ignorance as to their own good. Now

[096-062]when she heard the story of Brooke Burgess, she could not but think

[096-063]that had Dorothy remained at Exeter, enduring patiently such hard

[096-064]words as her aunt might speak, the love affair might have been

[096-065]brought at some future time to a happy conclusion. She did not say

[096-066]all this; but there came on her a silent melancholy, made expressive

[096-067]by constant little shakings of the head and a continued reproachful

[096-068]sadness of demeanour, which was quite as intelligible to Priscilla

[096-069]as would have been any spoken words. But Priscilla's approval of her

[096-070]sister's conduct was clear, outspoken, and satisfactory. She had been

[096-071]quite sure that her sister had been right about Mr. Gibson; and was

[096-072]equally sure that she was now right about Brooke Burgess. Priscilla

[096-073]had in her mind an idea that if B. B., as they called him, was half

[096-074]as good as her sister represented him to be,--for indeed Dorothy

[096-075]endowed him with every virtue consistent with humanity,--he would

[096-076]not be deterred from his pursuit either by Dolly's letter or by Aunt

[096-077]Stanbury's commands. But of this she thought it wise to say nothing.

[096-078]She paid Dolly the warm and hitherto unaccustomed compliment of

[096-079]equality, assuming to regard her sister's judgment and persistent

[096-080][[personal-freedom|independence]] to be equally strong with her own; and, as she knew

[096-081]well, she could not have gone further than this. "I never shall agree

[096-082]with you about Aunt Stanbury," she said. "To me she seems to be so

[096-083]imperious, so exacting, and also so unjust, as to be unbearable."

[096-084]"But she is affectionate," said Dolly.

[096-085]"So is the dog that bites you, and, for aught I know, the horse that

[096-086]kicks you. But it is ill living with biting dogs and kicking horses.

[096-087]But all that matters little as you are still your own mistress. How

[096-088]strange these nine months have been, with you in Exeter, while we

[096-089]have been at the [[clock-house|Clock House]]. And here we are, together again in the

[096-090]old way, just as though nothing had happened." But Dorothy knew well

[096-091]that a great deal had happened, and that her life could never be as

[096-092]it had been heretofore. The very tone in which her sister spoke to

[096-093]her was proof of this. She had an infinitely greater possession in

[096-094]herself than had belonged to her before her residence at Exeter; but

[096-095]that possession was so heavily mortgaged and so burthened as to make

[096-096]her believe that the change was to be regretted.

[096-097]At the end of the first week there came a letter from Aunt Stanbury

[096-098]to Dorothy. It began by saying that Dolly had left behind her certain

[096-099]small properties which had now been made up in a parcel and sent by

[096-100]the railway, carriage paid. "But they weren't mine at all," said

[096-101]Dolly, alluding to certain books in which she had taken delight. "She

[096-102]means to give them to you," said Priscilla, "and I think you must

[096-103]take them." "And the shawl is no more mine than it is yours, though

[096-104]I wore it two or three times in the winter." Priscilla was of opinion

[096-105]that the shawl must be taken also. Then the letter spoke of the

[096-106]writer's health, and at last fell into such a strain of confidential

[096-107][[scandal|gossip]] that Mrs. Stanbury, when she read it, could not understand

[096-108]that there had been a quarrel. "Martha says that she saw Camilla

[096-109]French in the street to-day, such a guy in her new finery as never

[096-110]was seen before except on May-day." Then in the postscript Dorothy

[096-111]was enjoined to answer this letter quickly. "None of your short

[096-112]scraps, my dear," said Aunt Stanbury.

[096-113]"She must mean you to go back to her," said Mrs. Stanbury.

[096-114]"No doubt she does," said Priscilla; "but Dolly need not go because

[096-115]my aunt means it. We are not her creatures."

[096-116]But Dorothy answered her aunt's letter in the spirit in which it had

[096-117]been written. She asked after her aunt's health, thanked her aunt for

[096-118]the gift of the books,--in each of which her name had been clearly

[096-119]written,--protested about the shawl, sent her love to Martha and her

[096-120]kind regards to Jane, and expressed a hope that C. F. enjoyed her new

[096-121]clothes. She described the cottage, and was funny about the cabbage

[096-122]stumps in the garden, and at last succeeded in concocting a long

[096-123]epistle. "I suppose there will be a regular correspondence," said

[096-124]Priscilla.

[096-125]Two days afterwards, however, the correspondence took altogether

[096-126]another form. The cottage in which they now lived was supposed to be

[096-127]beyond the beat of the wooden-legged postman, and therefore it was

[096-128]necessary that they should call at the post-office for their letters.

[096-129]On the morning in question Priscilla obtained a thick letter from

[096-130]Exeter for her mother, and knew that it had come from her aunt.

[096-131]Her aunt could hardly have found it necessary to correspond with

[096-132]Dorothy's mother so soon after that letter to Dorothy had been

[096-133]written had there not arisen some very peculiar cause. Priscilla,

[096-134]after much meditation, thought it better that the letter should be

[096-135]opened in Dorothy's absence, and in Dorothy's absence the following

[096-136]letter was read both by Priscilla and her mother:--

[096-137]The Close, March 19, 186--.

[096-138]DEAR SISTER STANBURY,

[096-139]After much consideration, I think it best to send under

[096-140]cover to you the enclosed letter from Mr. Brooke Burgess,

[096-141]intended for your daughter Dorothy. You will see that I

[096-142]have opened it and read it,--as I was clearly entitled to

[096-143]do, the letter having been addressed to my niece while she

[096-144]was supposed to be under my care. I do not like to destroy

[096-145]the letter, though, perhaps, that would be best; but I

[096-146]would advise you to do so, if it be possible, without

[096-147]shewing it to Dorothy. I have told Mr. Brooke Burgess what

[096-148]I have done.

[096-149]I have also told him that I cannot sanction a marriage

[096-150]between him and your daughter. There are many reasons of

[096-151]old date,--not to speak of present reasons also,--which

[096-152]would make such a marriage highly inexpedient. Mr. Brooke

[096-153]Burgess is, of course, his own master, but your daughter

[096-154]understands completely how the matter stands.

[096-155]Yours truly,

[096-156]JEMIMA STANBURY.

[096-157]"What a wicked old woman!" said Priscilla. Then there arose a

[096-158]question whether they should read Brooke's letter, or whether they

[096-159]should give it unread to Dorothy. Priscilla denounced her aunt in

[096-160]the strongest language she could use for having broken the seal.

[096-161]"'Clearly entitled,'--because Dorothy had been living with her!"

[096-162]exclaimed Priscilla. "She can have no proper conception of honour

[096-163]or of honesty. She had no more right to open Dorothy's letter than

[096-164]she had to take her money." Mrs. Stanbury was very anxious to read

[096-165]Brooke's letter, alleging that they would then be able to judge

[096-166]whether it should be handed over to Dorothy. But Priscilla's sense of

[096-167]right would not admit of this. Dorothy must receive the letter from

[096-168]her lover with no further stain from unauthorised eyes than that to

[096-169]which it had been already subjected. She was called in, therefore,

[096-170]from the kitchen, and the whole packet was given to her. "Your aunt

[096-171]has read the enclosure, Dolly; but we have not opened it."

[096-172]Dorothy took the packet without a word and sat herself down. She

[096-173]first read her aunt's letter very slowly. "I understand perfectly,"

[096-174]she said, folding it up, almost listlessly, while Brooke's letter lay

[096-175]still unopened on her lap. Then she took it up, and held it awhile in

[096-176]both hands, while her mother and Priscilla watched her. "Priscilla,"

[096-177]she said, "do you read it first."

[096-178]Priscilla was immediately at her side, kissing her. "No, my darling;

[096-179]no," she said; "it is for you to read it." Then Dorothy took the

[096-180]precious contents from the envelope, and opened the folds of the

[096-181]paper. When she had read a dozen words, her eyes were so suffused

[096-182]with tears, that she could hardly make herself mistress of the

[096-183]contents of the letter; but she knew that it contained renewed

[096-184]assurances of her lover's love, and assurance on his part that he

[096-185]would take no refusal from her based on any other ground than that of

[096-186]her own indifference to him. He had written to Miss Stanbury to the

[096-187]same effect; but he had not thought it necessary to explain this to

[096-188]Dorothy; nor did Miss Stanbury in her letter tell them that she had

[096-189]received any communication from him. "Shall I read it now?" said

[096-190]Priscilla, as soon as Dorothy again allowed the letter to fall into

[096-191]her lap.
