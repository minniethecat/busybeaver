## Section 22

[022-001]Mrs. Trevelyan, indeed, did a thing which was sure of itself to

[022-002]render any steps taken for a reconciliation ineffectual. In the midst

[022-003]of all this turmoil,--while she and her husband were still living in

[022-004]the same house, but apart because of their absurd quarrel respecting

[022-005][[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]], she wrote another letter to that [[social-class|gentleman]]. The

[022-006]argument by which she justified this to herself, and to her sister

[022-007]after it was done, was the real [[social-custom|propriety]] of her own conduct

[022-008]throughout her whole intimacy with Colonel Osborne. "But that is

[022-009]just what Louis doesn't want you to do," Nora had said, filled with

[022-010]anger and dismay. "Then let Louis give me an order to that effect,

[022-011]and behave to me like a husband, and I will obey him," Emily had

[022-012]answered. And she had gone on to plead that in her present condition

[022-013]she was under no orders from her husband. She was left to judge for

[022-014]herself, and,--judging for herself,--she knew, as she said, that it

[022-015]was best that she should write to Colonel Osborne. Unfortunately

[022-016]there was no ground for hoping that Colonel Osborne was ignorant

[022-017]of this insane jealousy on the part of her husband. It was better,

[022-018]therefore, she said, that she should write to him,--whom on the

[022-019]occasion she took care to name to her sister as "papa's old

[022-020]friend,"--and explain to him what she would wish him to do, and what

[022-021]not to do. Colonel Osborne answered the letter very quickly, throwing

[022-022]much more of demonstrative affection than he should have done into

[022-023]his "Dear Emily," and his "Dearest Friend." Of course Mrs. Trevelyan

[022-024]had burned this answer, and of course Mr. Trevelyan had been told of

[022-025]the correspondence. His wife, indeed, had been especially careful

[022-026]that there should be nothing secret about the matter,--that it should

[022-027]be so known in the house that Mr. Trevelyan should be sure to hear

[022-028]of it. And he had heard of it, and been driven almost mad by it. He

[022-029]had flown off to Lady Milborough, and had reduced his old friend to

[022-030]despair by declaring that, after all, he began to fear that his wife

[022-031]was--was--was--infatuated by that d---- scoundrel. Lady Milborough

[022-032]forgave the language, but protested that he was wrong in his

[022-033]suspicion. "To continue to correspond with him after what I have said

[022-034]to her!" exclaimed Trevelyan. "Take her to Naples at once,"--said

[022-035]Lady Milborough;--"at once!" "And have him after me?" said Trevelyan.

[022-036]Lady Milborough had no answer ready, and not having thought of

[022-037]this looked very blank. "I should find it harder to deal with her

[022-038]there even than here," continued Trevelyan. Then it was that Lady

[022-039]Milborough spoke of the small town in the west of France, urging

[022-040]as her reason that such a man as Colonel Osborne would certainly

[022-041]not follow them there; but Trevelyan had become indignant at this,

[022-042]declaring that if his wife's [[honour|good name]] could be preserved in no other

[022-043]manner than that, it would not be worth preserving at all. Then Lady

[022-044]Milborough had begun to cry, and had continued crying for a very long

[022-045]time. She was very unhappy,--as unhappy as her nature would allow

[022-046]her to be. She would have made almost any sacrifice to bring the two

[022-047]young people together;--would have willingly given her time, her

[022-048]money, her labour in the cause;--would probably herself have gone

[022-049]to the little town in the west of France, had her going been of any

[022-050]service. But, nevertheless, after her own fashion, she extracted no

[022-051]small enjoyment out of the circumstances of this miserable quarrel.

[022-052]The Lady Milboroughs of the day hate the Colonel Osbornes from the

[022-053]very bottoms of their warm hearts and pure souls; but they respect

[022-054]the Colonel Osbornes almost as much as they hate them, and find it

[022-055]to be an inestimable privilege to be brought into some contact with

[022-056]these roaring lions.

[022-057]But there arose to dear Lady Milborough a great trouble out of this

[022-058]quarrel, irrespective of the absolute horror of the separation of a

[022-059]young husband from his young wife. And the excess of her trouble on

[022-060]this head was great [[truth|proof]] of the real [[virtue|goodness]] of her heart. For, in

[022-061]this matter, the welfare of Trevelyan himself was not concerned;--but

[022-062]rather that of the Rowley family. Now the Rowleys had not given Lady

[022-063]Milborough any special reason for loving them. When she had first

[022-064]heard that her dear young friend Louis was going to marry a girl from

[022-065]the Mandarins, she had been almost in despair. It was her opinion

[022-066]that had he properly understood his own position, he would have

[022-067]promoted his welfare by falling in love with the daughter of some

[022-068]English country gentleman,--or some English peer, to which honour,

[022-069]with his advantages, Lady Milborough thought that he might have

[022-070]aspired. Nevertheless, when the girl from the Mandarins had been

[022-071]brought home as Mrs. Trevelyan, Lady Milborough had received her with

[022-072]open arms,--had received even the sister-in-law with arms partly

[022-073]open. Had either of them shown any tendency to regard her as a

[022-074]mother, she would have showered motherly cares upon them. For Lady

[022-075]Milborough was like an old hen, in her capacity for taking many

[022-076]under her wings. The two sisters had hardly done more than bear

[022-077]with her,--Nora, indeed, bearing with her more graciously than Mrs.

[022-078]Trevelyan; and in return, even for this, the old dowager was full of

[022-079]motherly regard. Now she knew well that Mr. Glascock was over head

[022-080]and ears in love with [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. It only wanted the slightest

[022-081]management and the easiest discretion to bring him on his knees, with

[022-082]an offer of his hand. And, then, how much that hand contained!--how

[022-083]much, indeed, as compared with that other hand, which was to be given

[022-084]in return, and which was,--to speak the truth,--completely empty! Mr.

[022-085]Glascock was the heir to a peer, was the heir to a rich peer, was the

[022-086]heir to a very, very old peer. He was in Parliament. The world spoke

[022-087]well of him. He was not, so to say, by any means an old man himself.

[022-088]He was good-tempered, reasonable, easily led, and yet by no means

[022-089]despicable. On all subjects connected with land, he held an opinion

[022-090]that was very much respected, and was supposed to be a thoroughly

[022-091]good specimen of an upper-class Englishman. Here was a suitor! But it

[022-092]was not to be supposed that such a man as Mr. Glascock would be so

[022-093]violently in love as to propose to a girl whose nearest known friend

[022-094]and female relation was misbehaving herself.

[022-095]Only they who have closely watched the natural uneasiness of human

[022-096]hens can understand how great was Lady Milborough's anxiety on

[022-097]this occasion. [[marriage|Marriage]] to her was a thing always delightful to

[022-098]contemplate. Though she had never been sordidly a match-maker, the

[022-099]course of the world around her had taught her to regard men as fish

[022-100]to be caught, and girls as the anglers who ought to catch them. Or,

[022-101]rather, could her mind have been accurately analysed, it would have

[022-102]been found that the girl was regarded as half-angler and half-bait.

[022-103]Any girl that angled visibly with her own hook, with a manifestly

[022-104]expressed desire to catch a fish, was odious to her. And she was very

[022-105]gentle-hearted in regard to the fishes, thinking that every fish

[022-106]in the river should have the hook and bait presented to him in the

[022-107]mildest, pleasantest form. But still, when the trout was well in

[022-108]the basket, her joy was great; and then came across her unlaborious

[022-109]mind some half-formed idea that a great ordinance of nature was

[022-110]being accomplished in the teeth of difficulties. For,--as she well

[022-111]knew,--there is a difficulty in the catching of fish.

[022-112]Lady Milborough, in her kind anxiety on Nora's behalf,--that the fish

[022-113]should be landed before Nora might be swept away in her sister's

[022-114]ruin,--hardly knew what step she might safely take. Mrs. Trevelyan

[022-115]would not see her again,--having already declared that any further

[022-116]interview would be painful and useless. She had spoken to Trevelyan,

[022-117]but Trevelyan had declared that he could do nothing. What was there

[022-118]that he could have done? He could not, as he said, overlook the gross

[022-119]improprieties of his wife's conduct, because his wife's sister had,

[022-120]or might possibly have, a lover. And then as to speaking to Mr.

[022-121]Glascock himself,--nobody knew better than Lady Milborough how very

[022-122]apt fish are to be frightened.

[022-123]But at last Lady Milborough did speak to Mr. Glascock,--making no

[022-124]allusion whatever to the hook prepared for himself, but saying a word

[022-125]or two as to the affairs of that other fish, whose circumstances, as

[022-126]he floundered about in the bucket of matrimony, were not as happy as

[022-127]they might have been. The care, the discretion, nay, the wisdom with

[022-128]which she did this were most excellent. She had become aware that

[022-129]Mr. Glascock had already heard of the unfortunate affair in Curzon

[022-130]Street. Indeed, every one who knew the Trevelyans had heard of it,

[022-131]and a great many who did not know them. No harm, therefore, could

[022-132]be done by mentioning the circumstance. Lady Milborough did mention

[022-133]it, explaining that the only person really in fault was that odious

[022-134]destroyer of the peace of families, Colonel Osborne, of whom

[022-135]Lady Milborough, on that occasion, said some very severe things

[022-136]indeed. Poor dear Mrs. Trevelyan was foolish, obstinate, and

[022-137]self-reliant;--but as innocent as the babe unborn. That things would

[022-138]come right before long no one who knew the affair,--and she knew it

[022-139]from beginning to end,--could for a moment doubt. The real victim

[022-140]would be that sweetest of all girls, Nora Rowley. Mr. Glascock

[022-141]innocently asked why Nora Rowley should be a victim. "Don't you

[022-142]understand, Mr. Glascock, how the most remote connection with a

[022-143]thing of that kind tarnishes a young woman's [[reputation|standing]] in the world?"

[022-144]Mr. Glascock was almost angry with the well-pleased Countess as he

[022-145]declared that he could not see that Miss Rowley's [[reputation|standing]] was at all

[022-146]tarnished; and old Lady Milborough, when he got up and left her, felt

[022-147]that she had done a good morning's work. If Nora could have known it

[022-148]all, Nora ought to have been very grateful, for Mr. Glascock got into

[022-149]a cab in Eccleston Square and had himself driven direct to Curzon

[022-150]Street. He himself believed that he was at that moment only doing the

[022-151]thing which he had for some time past resolved that he would do; but

[022-152]we perhaps may be justified in thinking that the actual resolution

[022-153]was first fixed by the discretion of Lady Milborough's communication.

[022-154]At any rate he arrived in Curzon Street with his mind fully resolved,

[022-155]and had spent the minutes in the cab considering how he had better

[022-156]perform the business in hand.

[022-157]He was at once shown into the drawing-room, where he found the two

[022-158]sisters, and Mrs. Trevelyan, as soon as she saw him, understood the

[022-159]purpose of his coming. There was an air of determination about him, a

[022-160]manifest intention of doing something, an absence of that vagueness

[022-161]which almost always flavours a morning visit. This was so strongly

[022-162]marked that Mrs. Trevelyan felt that she would have been almost

[022-163]justified in getting up and declaring that, as this visit was paid

[022-164]to her sister, she would retire. But any such declaration on her

[022-165]part was unnecessary, as Mr. Glascock had not been in the room three

[022-166]minutes before he asked her to go. By some clever device of his own,

[022-167]he got her into the back room and whispered to her that he wanted to

[022-168]say a few words in private to her sister.

[022-169]"Oh, certainly," said Mrs. Trevelyan, smiling.

[022-170]"I dare say you may guess what they are," said he. "I don't know what

[022-171]chance I may have."

[022-172]"I can tell you nothing about that," she replied, "as I know nothing.

[022-173]But you have my good wishes."

[022-174]And then she went.

[022-175]It may be presumed that gradually some idea of Mr. Glascock's

[022-176]intention had made its way into Nora's mind by the time that she

[022-177]found herself alone with that gentleman. Why else had he brought into

[022-178]the room with him that manifest air of a purpose? Why else had he

[022-179]taken the very strong step of sending the lady of the house out of

[022-180]her own drawing-room? Nora, beginning to understand this, put herself

[022-181]into an attitude of defence. She had never told herself that she

[022-182]would refuse Mr. Glascock. She had never acknowledged to herself

[022-183]that there was another man whom she liked better than she liked Mr.

[022-184]Glascock. But had she ever encouraged any wish for such an interview,

[022-185]her feelings at this moment would have been very different from what

[022-186]they were. As it was, she would have given much to postpone it, so

[022-187]that she might have asked herself questions, and have discovered

[022-188]whether she could reconcile herself to do that which, no doubt, all

[022-189]her friends would commend her for doing. Of course, it was clear

[022-190]enough to the mind of the girl that she had her [[money|fortune]] to make, and

[022-191]that her beauty and youth were the capital on which she had to found

[022-192]it. She had not lived so far from all taint of corruption as to feel

[022-193]any actual horror at the idea of a girl giving herself to a man,--not

[022-194]because the man had already, by his own capacities in that direction,

[022-195]forced her heart from her,--but because he was one likely to be at

[022-196]all points a good husband. Had all this affair concerned any other

[022-197]girl, any friend of her own, and had she known all the circumstances

[022-198]of the case, she would have had no hesitation in recommending that

[022-199]other girl to marry Mr. Glascock. A girl thrown out upon the world

[022-200]without a shilling must make her hay while the sun shines. But,

[022-201]nevertheless, there was something within her bosom which made her

[022-202]long for a better thing than this. She had dreamed, if she had not

[022-203]thought, of being able to worship a man; but she could hardly worship

[022-204]Mr. Glascock. She had dreamed, if she had not thought, of leaning

[022-205]upon a man all through life with her whole weight, as though that

[022-206]man had been specially made to be her staff, her prop, her support,

[022-207]her wall of comfort and protection. She knew that if she were to

[022-208]marry Mr. Glascock and become Lady Peterborough, in due course she

[022-209]must stand a good deal by her own strength, and live without that

[022-210]comfortable leaning. Nevertheless, when she found herself alone with

[022-211]the man, she by no means knew whether she would refuse him or not.

[022-212]But she knew that she must pluck up courage for an important moment,

[022-213]and she collected herself, braced her muscles, as it were, for a

[022-214]fight, and threw her mind into an attitude of contest.
