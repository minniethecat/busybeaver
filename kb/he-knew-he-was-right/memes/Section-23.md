## Section 23

[023-001]Mr. Glascock, as soon as the door was shut behind Mrs. Trevelyan's

[023-002]back, took a chair and placed it close beside the head of the sofa on

[023-003]which Nora was sitting. "Miss Rowley," he said, "you and I have known

[023-004]each other now for some months, and I hope you have learned to regard

[023-005]me as a friend."

[023-006]"Oh, yes, indeed," said Nora, with some spirit.

[023-007]"It has seemed to me that we have met as friends, and I can most

[023-008]truly say for myself, that I have taken the greatest possible

[023-009]pleasure in your acquaintance. It is not only that I admire you very

[023-010]much,"--he looked straight before him as he said this, and moved

[023-011]about the point of the stick which he was holding in both his

[023-012]hands,--"it is not only that,--perhaps not chiefly that, though I do

[023-013]admire you very much; but the truth is, that I like everything about

[023-014]you."

[023-015]Nora smiled, but she said nothing. It was better, she thought, to let

[023-016]him tell his story; but his mode of telling it was not without its

[023-017]efficacy. It was not the simple praise which made its way with her

[023-018]but a certain tone in the words which seemed to convince her that

[023-019]they were true. If he had really found her, or fancied her to be what

[023-020]he said, there was a manliness in his telling her so in the plainest

[023-021]words that pleased her much.

[023-022]"I know," continued he, "that this is a very bald way of telling--of

[023-023]pleading--my cause; but I don't know whether a bald way may not

[023-024]be the best, if it can only make itself understood to be true. Of

[023-025]course, Miss Rowley, you know what I mean. As I said before, you have

[023-026]all those things which not only make me love you, but which make me

[023-027]like you also. If you think that you can love me, say so; and, as

[023-028]long as I live, I will do my best to make you happy as my wife."

[023-029]There was a clearness of expression in this, and a downright

[023-030]surrender of himself, which so flattered her and so fluttered her

[023-031]that she was almost reduced to the giving of herself up because she

[023-032]could not reply to such an appeal in language less courteous than

[023-033]that of agreement. After a moment or two she found herself remaining

[023-034]silent, with a growing feeling that silence would be taken as

[023-035]conveying consent. There floated quickly across her brain an idea of

[023-036]the hardness of a woman's lot, in that she should be called upon to

[023-037]decide her future fate for life in half a minute. He had had weeks to

[023-038]think of this,--weeks in which it would have been almost unmaidenly

[023-039]in her so to think of it as to have made up her mind to accept the

[023-040]man. Had she so made up her mind, and had he not come to her, where

[023-041]would she have been then? But he had come to her. There he was, still

[023-042]poking about with his stick, waiting for her, and she must answer

[023-043]him. And he was the eldest son of a peer,--an enormous match for her,

[023-044]very proper in all respects; such a man, that if she should accept

[023-045]him, everybody around her would regard her [[money|fortune]] in life as

[023-046]miraculously successful. He was not such a man that any one would

[023-047]point at her and say,--"There; see another of them who has sold

[023-048]herself for money and a title!" Mr. Glascock was not an Apollo, not

[023-049]an admirable Crichton; but he was a man whom any girl might have

[023-050]learned to love. Now he had asked her to be his wife, and it was

[023-051]necessary that she should answer him. He sat there waiting for her

[023-052]very patiently, still poking about the point of his stick.

[023-053]Did she really love him? Though she was so pressed by consideration

[023-054]of time, she did find a moment in which to ask herself the question.

[023-055]With a quick turn of an eye she glanced at him, to see what he was

[023-056]like. Up to this moment, though she knew him well, she could have

[023-057]given no details of his personal appearance. He was a better-looking

[023-058]man than [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]],--so she told herself with a passing thought;

[023-059]but he lacked--he lacked; what was it that he lacked? Was it youth,

[023-060]or spirit, or strength; or was it some outward sign of an inward gift

[023-061]of mind? Was it that he was heavy while Hugh was light? Was it that

[023-062]she could find no fire in his eye, while Hugh's eyes were full of

[023-063]flashing? Or was it that for her, especially for her, Hugh was the

[023-064]appointed staff and appropriate wall of protection? Be all that as it

[023-065]might, she knew at the moment that she did love, not this man, but

[023-066]that other who was writing articles for the Daily Record. She must

[023-067]refuse the offer that was so brilliant, and give up the idea of

[023-068]reigning as queen at Monkhams.

[023-069]"Oh, Mr. Glascock," she said, "I ought to answer you more quickly."

[023-070]"No, dearest; not more quickly than suits you. Nothing ever in this

[023-071]world can be more important both to you and to me. If you want more

[023-072]time to think of it, take more time."

[023-073]"No, Mr. Glascock; I do not. I don't know why I should have paused.

[023-074]Is not the truth best?"

[023-075]"Yes,--certainly the truth is best."

[023-076]"I do not--love you. Pray, pray understand me."

[023-077]"I understand it too well, Miss Rowley." The stick was still going,

[023-078]and the eyes more intently fixed than ever on something opposite.

[023-079]"I do like you; I like you very much. And I am so grateful! I cannot

[023-080]understand why such a man as you should want to make me your wife."

[023-081]"Because I love you better than all the others; simply that. That

[023-082]reason, and that only, justifies a man in wanting to marry a girl."

[023-083]What a good fellow he was, and how flattering were his words! Did he

[023-084]not deserve what he wanted, even though it could not be given without

[023-085]a sacrifice? But yet she did not love him. As she looked at him again

[023-086]she could not there recognise her staff. As she looked at him she was

[023-087]more than ever convinced that that other staff ought to be her staff.

[023-088]"May I come again,--after a month, say?" he asked, when there had

[023-089]been another short period of silence.

[023-090]"No, no. Why should you trouble yourself? I am not worth it."

[023-091]"It is for me to judge of that, Miss Rowley."

[023-092]"All the same, I know that I am not worth it. And I could not tell

[023-093]you to do that."

[023-094]"Then I will wait, and come again without your telling me."

[023-095]"Oh, Mr. Glascock, I did not mean that; indeed I did not. Pray do not

[023-096]think that. Take what I say as final. I like you more than I can say;

[023-097]and I feel a gratitude to you that I cannot express,--which I shall

[023-098]never forget. I have never known any one who has seemed to be so good

[023-099]as you. But-- It is just what I said before." And then she fairly

[023-100]burst into tears.

[023-101]"Miss Rowley," he said, very slowly, "pray do not think that I want

[023-102]to ask any question which it might embarrass you to answer. But my

[023-103]happiness is so greatly at stake; and, if you will allow me to say

[023-104]so, your happiness, too, is so greatly concerned, that it is most

[023-105]important that we should not come to a conclusion too quickly. If I

[023-106]thought that your heart were vacant I would wait patiently. I have

[023-107]been thinking of you as my possible wife for weeks past,--for months

[023-108]past. Of course you have not had such thoughts about me." As he said

[023-109]this she almost loved him for his considerate [[virtue|goodness]]. "It has

[023-110]sometimes seemed to me odd that girls should love men in such a

[023-111]hurry. If your heart be free, I will wait. And if you esteem me, you

[023-112]can see, and try whether you cannot learn to love me."

[023-113]"I do esteem you."

[023-114]"It depends on that question, then?" he said, slowly.

[023-115]She sat silent for fully a minute, with her hands clasped; and then

[023-116]she answered him in a whisper. "I do not know," she said.

[023-117]He also was silent for a while before he spoke again. He ceased to

[023-118]poke with his stick, and got up from his chair, and stood a little

[023-119]apart from her, not looking at her even yet.

[023-120]"I see," he said at last. "I understand. Well, Miss Rowley, I quite

[023-121]perceive that I cannot press my suit any further now. But I shall not

[023-122]despair altogether. I know this, that if I might possibly succeed, I

[023-123]should be a very happy man. Good-bye, Miss Rowley."

[023-124]She took his offered hand and pressed it so warmly, that had he not

[023-125]been manly and big-hearted, he would have taken such pressure as a

[023-126]sign that she wished him to ask her again. But such was his nature.

[023-127]"God bless you," he said, "and make you happy, whatever you may

[023-128]choose to do."

[023-129]Then he left her, and she heard him walk down the stairs with heavy

[023-130]slow steps, and she thought that she could perceive from the sound

[023-131]that he was sad at heart, but that he was resolved not to show his

[023-132]sadness outwardly.

[023-133]When she was alone she began to think in earnest of what she had

[023-134]done. If the reader were told that she regretted the decision which

[023-135]she had been forced to make so rapidly, a wrong impression would

[023-136]be given of the condition of her thoughts. But there came upon her

[023-137]suddenly a strange capacity for counting up and making a mental

[023-138]inventory of all that might have been hers. She knew,--and where is

[023-139]the girl so placed that does not know?--that it is a great thing to

[023-140]be an English peeress. Now, as she stood there thinking of it all,

[023-141]she was [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]] without a shilling in the world, and without a

[023-142]prospect of a shilling. She had often heard her mother speak fearful

[023-143]words of future possible days, when colonial governing should no

[023-144]longer be within the capacity of Sir Marmaduke. She had been taught

[023-145]from a very early age that all the material prosperity of her life

[023-146]must depend on [[marriage|matrimony]]. She could never be comfortably disposed of

[023-147]in the world, unless some fitting man who possessed those things of

[023-148]which she was so bare, should wish to make her his wife. Now there

[023-149]had come a man so thoroughly fitting, so marvellously endowed, that

[023-150]no worldly blessing would have been wanting. Mr. Glascock had more

[023-151]than once spoken to her of the glories of Monkhams. She thought of

[023-152]Monkhams now more than she had ever thought of the place before.

[023-153]It would have been a great privilege to be the mistress of an old

[023-154]time-honoured mansion, to call oaks and elms her own, to know that

[023-155]acres of gardens were submitted to her caprices, to look at herds

[023-156]of cows and oxen, and be aware that they lowed on her own pastures.

[023-157]And to have been the mother of a future peer of England, to

[023-158]have the nursing, and sweet custody and very making of a future

[023-159]senator,--would not that have been much? And the man himself who

[023-160]would have been her husband was such a one that any woman might have

[023-161]trusted herself to him with perfect confidence. Now that he was

[023-162]gone she almost fancied that she did love him. Then she thought of

[023-163]Hugh Stanbury, sitting as he had described himself, in a little

[023-164]dark closet at the office of the "D. R.," in a very old inky

[023-165]shooting-coat, with a tarnished square-cut cloth cap upon his head,

[023-166]with a short pipe in his mouth, writing at midnight for the next

[023-167]morning's impression, this or that article according to the order

[023-168]of his master, "the tallow-chandler;"--for the editor of the Daily

[023-169]Record was a [[social-class|gentleman]] whose father happened to be a grocer in the

[023-170]City, and Hugh had been accustomed thus to describe the family

[023-171]trade. And she might certainly have had the peer, and the acres of

[023-172]garden, and the big house, and the senatorial honours; whereas the

[023-173]tallow-chandler's journeyman had never been so out-spoken. She told

[023-174]herself from moment to moment that she had done right; that she would

[023-175]do the same a dozen times, if a dozen times the experiment could

[023-176]be repeated; but still, still, there was the remembrance of all

[023-177]that she had lost. How would her mother look at her, her anxious,

[023-178]heavily-laden mother, when the story should be told of all that had

[023-179]been offered to her and all that had been refused?
