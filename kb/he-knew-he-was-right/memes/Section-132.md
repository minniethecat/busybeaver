## Section 132

[132-001]"But of course she is going," said Lady Rowley.

[132-002]"Of course she will some day, and of course she'll like it," said

[132-003]Miss Spalding. "We both of us have been there already."

[132-004]"But I mean Monkhams," said Lady Rowley, still simpering.

[132-005]"I declare I believe mamma thinks that your sister is to be married

[132-006]to Mr. Glascock!" said Lucy.

[132-007]"And so she is;--isn't she?" said Lady Rowley.

[132-008]"Oh, mamma!" said Nora, jumping up. "It is Caroline;--this one,

[132-009]this one, this one,"--and Nora took her friend by the arm as she

[132-010]spoke,--"it is this one that is to be Mrs. Glascock."

[132-011]"It is a most natural mistake to make," said Caroline.

[132-012]Lady Rowley became very red in the face, and was unhappy. "I

[132-013]declare," she said, "that they told me it was your elder sister."

[132-014]"But I have no elder sister," said Caroline, laughing.

[132-015]"Of course she is oldest," said Nora,--"and looks to be so, ever so

[132-016]much. Don't you, Miss Spalding?"

[132-017]"I have always supposed so."

[132-018]"I don't understand it at all," said Lady Rowley, who had no image

[132-019]before her mind's eye but that of Wallachia Petrie, and who was

[132-020]beginning to feel that she had disgraced her own judgment by the

[132-021]criticisms she had expressed everywhere as to Mr. Glascock's bride.

[132-022]"I don't understand it at all. Do you mean that both your sisters are

[132-023]younger than you, Miss Spalding?"

[132-024]"I have only got one, Lady Rowley."

[132-025]"Mamma, you are thinking of Miss Petrie," said Nora, clapping both

[132-026]her hands together.

[132-027]"I mean the lady that wears the black bugles."

[132-028]"Of course you do;--Miss Petrie. Mamma has all along thought that Mr.

[132-029]Glascock was going to carry away with him the republican Browning!"

[132-030]"Oh, mamma, how can you have made such a blunder!" said Sophie

[132-031]Rowley. "Mamma does make such delicious blunders."

[132-032]"Sophie, my dear, that is not a proper way of speaking."

[132-033]"But, dear mamma, don't you?"

[132-034]"If somebody has told me wrong, that has not been my fault," said

[132-035]Lady Rowley.

[132-036]The poor woman was so evidently disconcerted that Caroline Spalding

[132-037]was quite unhappy. "My dear Lady Rowley, there has been no fault. And

[132-038]why shouldn't it have been so? Wallachia is so clever, that it is the

[132-039]most natural thing in the world to have thought."

[132-040]"I cannot say that I agree with you there," said Lady Rowley,

[132-041]somewhat recovering herself.

[132-042]"You must know the whole truth now," said Nora, turning to her

[132-043]friend, "and you must not be angry with us if we laugh a little at

[132-044]your poetess. Mamma has been frantic with Mr. Glascock because he has

[132-045]been going to marry,--whom shall I say,--her edition of you. She has

[132-046]sworn that he must be insane. When we have sworn how beautiful you

[132-047]were, and how nice, and how jolly, and all the rest of it,--she has

[132-048]sworn that you were at least a hundred, and that you had a red nose.

[132-049]You must admit that Miss Petrie has a red nose."

[132-050]"Is that a sin?"

[132-051]"Not at all in the woman who has it; but in the man who is going to

[132-052]marry it,--yes. Can't you see how we have all been at cross-purposes,

[132-053]and what mamma has been thinking and saying of poor Mr. Glascock?

[132-054]You mustn't repeat it, of course; but we have had such a battle here

[132-055]about it. We thought that mamma had lost her eyes and her ears and

[132-056]her [[truth|knowledge]] of things in general. And now it has all come out! You

[132-057]won't be angry?"

[132-058]"Why should I be angry?"

[132-059]"Miss Spalding," said Lady Rowley, "I am really unhappy at what has

[132-060]occurred, and I hope that there may be nothing more said about it.

[132-061]I am quite sure that somebody told me wrong, or I should not have

[132-062]fallen into such an error. I beg your pardon,--and Mr. Glascock's!"

[132-063]"Beg Mr. Glascock's pardon, certainly," said Lucy.

[132-064]Miss Spalding looked very pretty, smiled very gracefully, and coming

[132-065]up to Lady Rowley to say good-bye, kissed her on her cheeks. This

[132-066]overcame the spirit of the disappointed mother, and Lady Rowley never

[132-067]said another word against Caroline Spalding or her marriage. "Now,

[132-068]mamma, what do you think of her?" said Nora, as soon as Caroline was

[132-069]gone.

[132-070]"Was it odd, my dear, that I should be astonished at his wanting to

[132-071]marry that other woman?"

[132-072]"But, mamma, when we told you that she was young and pretty and

[132-073]bright!"

[132-074]"I thought that you were all demented. I did indeed. I still think it

[132-075]a pity that he should take an American. I think that Miss Spalding is

[132-076]very nice, but there are English girls quite as nice-looking as her."

[132-077]After that there was not another word said by Lady Rowley against

[132-078]Caroline Spalding.

[132-079]Nora, when she thought of it all that night, felt that she had hardly

[132-080]spoken to Miss Spalding as she should have spoken as to the treatment

[132-081]in England which would be accorded to Mr. Glascock's wife. She became

[132-082]aware of the effect which her own hesitation must have had, and

[132-083]thought that it was her duty to endeavour to remove it. Perhaps, too,

[132-084]the conversion of her mother had some effect in making her feel that

[132-085]she had been wrong in supposing that there would be any difficulty

[132-086]in Caroline's position in England. She had heard so much adverse

[132-087]criticism from her mother that she had doubted in spite of

[132-088]her own convictions;--but now it had come to light that Lady

[132-089]Rowley's criticisms had all come from a most absurd blunder. "Only

[132-090]fancy;"--she said to herself;--"Miss Petrie coming out as Lady

[132-091]Peterborough! Poor mamma!" And then she thought of the reception

[132-092]which would be given to Caroline, and of the place the future Lady

[132-093]Peterborough would fill in the world, and of the glories of Monkhams!

[132-094]Resolving that she would do her best to counteract any evil which

[132-095]she might have done, she seated herself at her desk, and wrote the

[132-096]following letter to Miss Spalding:--

[132-097]MY DEAR CAROLINE,

[132-098]I am sure you will let me call you so, as had you not felt

[132-099]towards me like a friend, you would not have come to me

[132-100]to-day and told me of your doubts. I think that I did not

[132-101]answer you as I ought to have done when you spoke to me.

[132-102]I did not like to say anything off-hand, and in that way

[132-103]I misled you. I feel quite sure that you will encounter

[132-104]nothing in England as Mr. Glascock's wife to make you

[132-105]uncomfortable, and that he will have nothing to repent.

[132-106]Of course Englishmen generally marry Englishwomen; and,

[132-107]perhaps, there may be some people who will think that such

[132-108]a prize should not be lost to their countrywomen. But that

[132-109]will be all. Mr. Glascock commands such universal respect

[132-110]that his wife will certainly be respected, and I do not

[132-111]suppose that anything will ever come in your way that can

[132-112]possibly make you feel that he is looked down upon. I hope

[132-113]you will understand what I mean.

[132-114]As for your changing now, that is quite impossible. If I

[132-115]were you, I would not say a word about it to any living

[132-116]being; but just go on,--straight forward,--in your own

[132-117]way, and take the good the gods provide you,--as the poet

[132-118]says to the king in the ode. And I think the gods have

[132-119]provided for you very well,--and for him.

[132-120]I do hope that I may see you sometimes. I cannot explain

[132-121]to you how very much out of your line "we" shall be;--for

[132-122]of course there is a "we." People are more separated with

[132-123]us than they are, I suppose, with you. And my "we" is

[132-124]a very poor man, who works hard at writing in a dingy

[132-125]newspaper office, and we shall live in a garret and have

[132-126]brown sugar in our tea, and eat hashed mutton. And I shall

[132-127]have nothing a year to buy my clothes with. Still I mean

[132-128]to do it; and I don't mean to be long before I do do it.

[132-129]When a girl has made up her mind to be married, she had

[132-130]better go on with it at once, and take it all afterwards

[132-131]as it may come. Nevertheless, perhaps, we may see each

[132-132]other somewhere, and I may be able to introduce you to the

[132-133]dearest, honestest, very best, and most affectionate man

[132-134]in the world. And he is very, very clever.

[132-135]Yours very affectionately,

[132-136]NORA ROWLEY.

[132-137]Thursday morning.

[132-138]CHAPTER LXXXI.

[132-139]MR. GLASCOCK IS MASTER.

[132-140][Illustration]

[132-141]Caroline Spalding, when she received Nora's letter, was not disposed

[132-142]to give much weight to it. She declared to herself that the girl's

[132-143]unpremeditated expression of opinion was worth more than her studied

[132-144]words. But she was not the less grateful or the less loving towards

[132-145]her new friend. She thought how nice it would be to have Nora at that

[132-146]splendid abode in England of which she had heard so much,--but she

[132-147]thought also that in that splendid abode she herself ought never to

[132-148]have part or share. If it were the case that this were an unfitting

[132-149]match, it was clearly her duty to decide that there should be no

[132-150]marriage. Nora had been quite right in bidding her speak to Mr.

[132-151]Glascock himself, and to Mr. Glascock she would go. But it was very

[132-152]difficult for her to determine on the manner in which she would

[132-153]discuss the subject with him. She thought that she could be firm if

[132-154]her mind were once made up. She believed that perhaps she was by

[132-155]nature more firm than he. In all their intercourse together he had

[132-156]ever yielded to her; and though she had been always pleased and

[132-157]grateful, there had grown upon her an idea that he was perhaps too

[132-158]easy,--that he was a man as to whom it was necessary that they who

[132-159]loved him should see that he was not led away by weakness into folly.

[132-160]But she would want to learn something from him before her decision

[132-161]was finally reached, and in this she foresaw a great difficulty.

[132-162]In her trouble she went to her usual counsellor,--the Republican

[132-163]Browning. In such an emergency she could hardly have done worse.

[132-164]"Wally," she said, "we talk about England, and Italy, and France,

[132-165]as though we knew all about them; but how hard it is to realise the

[132-166]difference between one's own country and others."

[132-167]"We can at least learn a great deal that is satisfactory," said

[132-168]Wallachia. "[[About]] one out of every five Italians can read a book,

[132-169]about two out of every five Englishmen can read a book. Out of every

[132-170]five New Englanders four and four-fifths can read a book. I guess

[132-171]that is knowing a good deal."

[132-172]"I don't mean in statistics."

[132-173]"I cannot conceive how you are to learn anything about any country

[132-174]except by statistics. I have just discovered that the number of

[132-175]illegitimate children--"

[132-176]"Oh, Wally, I can't talk about that,--not now at least. What I cannot

[132-177]realise is this,--what sort of a life it is that they will lead at

[132-178]Monkhams."

[132-179]"Plenty to eat and drink, I guess; and you'll always have to go round

[132-180]in fine clothes."

[132-181]"And that will be all?"

[132-182]"No;--not all. There will be carriages and horses, and all manner

[132-183]of people there who won't care much about you. If he is firm,--very

[132-184]firm;--if he have that firmness which one does not often meet, even

[132-185]in an American man, he will be able, after a while, to give you

[132-186]a position as an English woman of [[social-class|rank]]." It is to be feared that

[132-187]Wallachia Petrie had been made aware of Caroline's idea as to Mr.

[132-188]Glascock's want of purpose.

[132-189]"And that will be all?"

[132-190]"If you have a baby, they'll let you go and see it two or three times

[132-191]a day. I don't suppose you will be allowed to nurse it, because they

[132-192]never do in England. You have read what the Saturday Review says. In

[132-193]every other respect the Saturday Review has been the falsest of all

[132-194]false periodicals, but I guess it has been pretty true in what it has

[132-195]said about English women."
