# Trevelyan Jealousy Crisis

The **Trevelyan Jealousy Crisis** is the overarching dramatic arc of [[Anthony Trollope]]'s *He Knew He Was Right* (1869), spanning from [[Louis Trevelyan]]'s first stirrings of suspicion in the novel's opening chapters through his descent into [[Madness|madness]] and eventual death. Unlike [[The Trevelyan Separation]], which is a specific event, the Jealousy Crisis describes the full trajectory of Trevelyan's psychological deterioration — a process the novel charts with remarkable clinical precision across more than one hundred chapters.

## Arc of the Crisis

| Phase | Chapters | Key Developments |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **Inception** | 2–3 | Trevelyan becomes uneasy about [[Colonel Osborne]]'s visits to [[Emily Trevelyan|Emily]] |
| **Fixation** | 4–6 | Reason fails; Trevelyan demands Emily end the friendship; she refuses |
| **Rupture** | 6–11 | [[The Trevelyan Separation]]; Emily leaves for [[Nuncombe Putney]] |
| **Escalation** | 12–63 | Trevelyan hires the detective Bozzle to spy on Emily; public scandal grows |
| **Abduction** | 64 | Trevelyan abducts his infant son and flees to Italy |
| **Descent** | 65–106 | Isolated in Turin and Casalunga, Trevelyan's mental state deteriorates |
| **Death** | 107–112 | Return to England; brief reunion with Emily; [[Trevelyan Death|death]] |

## The Role of Bozzle

A distinctive feature of the crisis is Trevelyan's employment of **Bozzle**, a private detective, to spy on his estranged wife. This decision marks a point of no return: by turning to a professional investigator, Trevelyan transforms a private marital conflict into a matter of surveillance and documentation. Bozzle's reports, which find nothing incriminating, paradoxically deepen Trevelyan's delusion — he interprets the absence of evidence as evidence of concealment.

## Psychological Realism

What distinguishes the Trevelyan Jealousy Crisis from the conventions of Victorian fiction is its commitment to psychological realism. Trevelyan is not a villain; he is an ill man whose illness takes a form that Victorian society was ill-equipped to recognize or treat. His progressive isolation — from his wife, his friends, his child, and finally from reality itself — is rendered with a clinical precision unusual in nineteenth-century literature:

> "Think of Louis Trevelyan." [106-103]

This brief, devastating line — spoken by a character late in the novel — captures the tragedy: Trevelyan has become a cautionary tale, a name synonymous with the destruction that jealousy wreaks.

## Significance

The Jealousy Crisis is the novel's central contribution to Victorian fiction's engagement with mental illness. By treating jealousy not as a moral flaw but as a pathology — and by tracing its progression with clinical care — Trollope produced one of the era's most sophisticated fictional studies of the disordered mind. The crisis also serves as a vehicle for the novel's social critique: a legal and social system that gives husbands near-absolute power over wives makes pathological jealousy not merely tragic but lethal.
