# Trust

Trust is the central thematic concept of Anthony Trollope's *He Knew He Was Right* (1869): the mutual confidence that marriage requires, and the catastrophic personal and social consequences when it is withdrawn without cause.

## Overview

In the Victorian marriage ideal, trust between husband and wife was not merely a personal virtue but a structural necessity — the legal and social order rested upon the assumption that spouses would maintain mutual confidence. Louis Trevelyan's refusal to trust his wife Emily, despite her demonstrable fidelity, sets in motion the entire tragic arc of the novel. Trust is presented not as an optional sentiment but as the binding agent without which the marital contract cannot function (011-130).

## Context in Victorian Society

The Victorian doctrine of "separate spheres" assigned men the public realm of work and politics and women the private realm of home and family. Within this framework, a husband's trust in his wife's domestic conduct was the linchpin of social respectability. A wife suspected of impropriety — even without evidence — faced social ruin. Conversely, a husband who publicly withdrew his trust damaged his own standing as much as his wife's. The novel dramatises this symmetry: Trevelyan's jealous suspicion destroys not only Emily's reputation but his own sanity and social position.

## Manifestation in the Novel

Trust operates as the novel's organising principle from the opening chapters. Trevelyan begins with full confidence in his wife, but Colonel Osborne's visits — and the social gossip they provoke — cause him to withdraw that trust (008-106). The narrative traces the progressive destruction that follows: separation, legal battles over their child's custody, Trevelyan's descent into paranoid isolation, and ultimately his death.

Key moments in the trust-erosion arc:

- **Initial suspicion**: Trevelyan asks Emily to end her acquaintance with Colonel Osborne. She refuses on principle, viewing compliance as an admission of guilt. Neither will yield, and trust fractures irrevocably (018-098).

- **The spiral**: Once broken, trust proves impossible to restore. Trevelyan "loved his wife" and "to a certain extent he still trusted her" (046-032), but the rational part of his mind has lost control of the jealous part.

- **Public dimension**: The private breakdown becomes public when law and society are invoked — lawyers, friends, and family all take sides, hardening the positions that trust alone might have softened.

## Key Examples

### The Osborne Visits

Colonel Osborne, an old family friend of Emily's father, calls upon her while Trevelyan is away. Society — in the person of Lady Milborough — immediately reads impropriety into these visits. Trevelyan demands that Emily cease receiving Osborne. Her refusal, grounded in pride and a principled objection to appearing guilty, is read by Trevelyan as confirmation of guilt. The tragedy is that both are, in their own terms, "right" — Emily about her innocence, Trevelyan about the social peril of appearances.

### The Italian Exile

In the novel's final act, Trevelyan has fled to Italy with his son, living in paranoid seclusion. His refusal to trust anyone — wife, friends, legal advisors — has reduced him to a state of isolated misery. He "declared that he would be ready to trust to that conviction himself" (032-178), but the declaration is hollow; he has lost the capacity for trust entirely.
