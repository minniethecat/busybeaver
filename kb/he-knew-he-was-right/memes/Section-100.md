## Section 100

[100-001]"Emily!" he exclaimed, and ran to her and embraced her. She did not

[100-002]seek to stop him, but she did not return the kiss which he gave her.

[100-003]Then he held her by her hands, and looked into her face, and she

[100-004]could see how strangely he was altered. She thought that she would

[100-005]hardly have known him, had she not been sure that it was he. She

[100-006]herself was also changed. Who can bear sorrow without such change,

[100-007]till age has fixed the lines of the face, or till care has made them

[100-008]hard and unmalleable? But the effect on her was as nothing to that

[100-009]which grief, remorse, and desolation had made on him. He had had

[100-010]no child with him, no sister, no friend. Bozzle had been his only

[100-011]refuge,--a refuge not adapted to make life easier to such a man as

[100-012]Trevelyan; and he,--in spite of the accusations made by himself

[100-013]against his wife, within his own breast hourly since he had

[100-014]left her,--had found it to be very difficult to satisfy his own

[100-015]conscience. He told himself from hour to hour that he knew that he

[100-016]was right; but in very truth he was ever doubting his own conduct.

[100-017]"You have been ill, Louis," she said, looking at him.

[100-018]"Ill at ease, Emily;--very ill at ease! A sore heart will make the

[100-019]face thin, as well as fever or ague. Since we parted I have not had

[100-020]much to comfort me."

[100-021]"Nor have I,--nor any of us," said she. "How was comfort to come from

[100-022]such a parting?"

[100-023]Then they both stood silent together. He was still holding her by

[100-024]the hand, but she was careful not to return his pressure. She would

[100-025]not take her hand away from him; but she would show him no sign

[100-026]of softness till he should have absolutely acquitted her of the

[100-027]accusation he had made against her. "We are man and wife," he said

[100-028]after awhile. "In spite of all that has come and gone I am yours, and

[100-029]you are mine."

[100-030]"You should have remembered that always, Louis."

[100-031]"I have never forgotten it,--never. In no thought have I been untrue

[100-032]to you. My heart has never changed since first I gave it you." There

[100-033]came a bitter frown upon her face, of which she was so conscious

[100-034]herself, that she turned her face away from him. She still remembered

[100-035]her lesson, that she was not to anger him, and, therefore, she

[100-036]refrained from answering him at all. But the answer was there, hot

[100-037]within her bosom. Had he loved her,--and yet suspected that she was

[100-038]false to him and to her vows, simply because she had been on terms

[100-039]of intimacy with an old friend? Had he loved her, and yet turned her

[100-040]from his house? Had he loved her,--and set a policeman to watch her?

[100-041]Had he loved her, and yet spoken evil of her to all their friends?

[100-042]Had he loved her, and yet striven to rob her of her child? "Will you

[100-043]come to me?" he said.

[100-044]"I suppose it will be better so," she answered slowly.

[100-045]"Then you will promise me--" He paused, and attempted to turn her

[100-046]towards him, so that he might look her in the face.

[100-047]"Promise what?" she said, quickly glancing round at him, and drawing

[100-048]her hand away from him as she did so.

[100-049]"That all intercourse with [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]] shall be at an end."

[100-050]"I will make no promise. You come to me to add one insult to another.

[100-051]Had you been a man, you would not have named him to me after what you

[100-052]have done to me."

[100-053]"That is absurd. I have a right to demand from you such a pledge. I

[100-054]am willing to believe that you have not--"

[100-055]"Have not what?"

[100-056]"That you have not utterly disgraced me."

[100-057]"God in heaven, that I should hear this!" she exclaimed. "Louis

[100-058]Trevelyan, I have not disgraced you at all,--in thought, in word, in

[100-059]deed, in look, or in gesture. It is you that have disgraced yourself,

[100-060]and ruined me, and degraded even your own child."

[100-061]"Is this the way in which you welcome me?"

[100-062]"Certainly it is,--in this way and in no other if you speak to me

[100-063]of what is past, without acknowledging your error." Her brow became

[100-064]blacker and blacker as she continued to speak to him. "It would be

[100-065]best that nothing should be said,--not a word. That it all should be

[100-066]regarded as an ugly dream. But, when you come to me and at once go

[100-067]back to it all, and ask me for a promise--"

[100-068]"Am I to understand then that all idea of submission to your husband

[100-069]is to be at an end?"

[100-070]"I will submit to no imputation on my honour,--even from you. One

[100-071]would have thought that it would have been for you to preserve it

[100-072]untarnished."

[100-073]"And you will give me no assurance as to your future life?"

[100-074]"None;--certainly none. If you want promises from me, there can be no

[100-075]hope for the future. What am I to promise? That I will not have--a

[100-076]lover? What respect can I enjoy as your wife if such a promise be

[100-077]needed? If you should choose to fancy that it had been broken you

[100-078]would set your policeman to watch me again! Louis, we can never live

[100-079]together again ever with comfort, unless you acknowledge in your own

[100-080]heart that you have used me shamefully."

[100-081]"Were you right to see him in Devonshire?"

[100-082]"Of course I was right. Why should I not see him,--or any one?"

[100-083]"And you will see him again?"

[100-084]"When papa comes, of course I shall see him."

[100-085]"Then it is hopeless," said he, turning away from her.

[100-086]"If that man is to be a source of disquiet to you, it is hopeless,"

[100-087]she answered. "If you cannot so school yourself that he shall be the

[100-088]same to you as other men, it is quite hopeless. You must still be

[100-089]mad,--as you have been mad hitherto."

[100-090]He walked about the room restlessly for a time, while she stood with

[100-091]assumed composure near the window. "Send me my child," he said at

[100-092]last.

[100-093]"He shall come to you, Louis,--for a little; but he is not to be

[100-094]taken out from hence. Is that a promise?"

[100-095]"You are to exact promises from me, where my own rights are

[100-096]concerned, while you refuse to give me any, though I am entitled to

[100-097]demand them! I order you to send the boy to me. Is he not my own?"

[100-098]"Is he not mine too? And is he not all that you have left to me?"

[100-099]He paused again, and then gave the promise. "Let him be brought to

[100-100]me. He shall not be removed now. I intend to have him. I tell you

[100-101]so fairly. He shall be taken from you unless you come back to me

[100-102]with such assurances as to your future conduct as I have a right to

[100-103]demand. There is much that the law cannot give me. It cannot procure

[100-104]wife-like submission, love, gratitude, or even decent matronly

[100-105]conduct. But that which it can give me, I will have."

[100-106]She walked off to the door, and then as she was quitting the room she

[100-107]spoke to him once again. "Alas, Louis," she said, "neither can the

[100-108]law, nor medicine, nor religion, restore to you that fine intellect

[100-109]which foolish suspicions have destroyed." Then she left him and

[100-110]returned to the room in which her aunt, and Nora, and the child

[100-111]were all clustered together, waiting to learn the effects of the

[100-112]interview. The two women asked their questions with their eyes,

[100-113]rather than with spoken words. "It is all over," said Mrs. Trevelyan.

[100-114]"There is nothing left for me but to go back to papa. I only hear the

[100-115]same accusations, repeated again and again, and make myself subject

[100-116]to the old insults." Then Mrs. Outhouse knew that she could interfere

[100-117]no further, and that in truth nothing could be done till the return

[100-118]of Sir Marmaduke should relieve her and her husband from all further

[100-119]active concern in the matter.

[100-120]But Trevelyan was still down-stairs waiting for the child. At last it

[100-121]was arranged that Nora should take the boy into the drawing-room, and

[100-122]that Mrs. Outhouse should fetch the father up from the parlour to the

[100-123]room above it. Angry as was Mrs. Trevelyan with her husband, not the

[100-124]less was she anxious to make the boy good-looking and seemly in his

[100-125]father's eyes. She washed the child's face, put on him a clean frill

[100-126]and a pretty ribbon; and, as she did so, she bade him kiss his papa,

[100-127]and speak nicely to him, and love him. "Poor papa is unhappy," she

[100-128]said, "and Louey must be very good to him." The boy, child though he

[100-129]was, understood much more of what was passing around him than his

[100-130]mother knew. How was he to love papa when mamma did not do so? In

[100-131]some shape that idea had framed itself in his mind; and, as he was

[100-132]taken down, he knew it was impossible that he should speak nicely

[100-133]to his papa. Nora did as she was bidden, and went down to the

[100-134]first-floor. Mrs. Outhouse, promising that even if she were put out

[100-135]of the room by Mr. Trevelyan she would not stir from the landing

[100-136]outside the door, descended to the parlour and quickly returned with

[100-137]the unfortunate father. Mr. Outhouse, in the meantime, was still

[100-138]sitting in his closet, tormented with curiosity, but yet determined

[100-139]not to be seen till the intruder should have left his house.

[100-140]"I hope you are well, Nora," he said, as he entered the room with

[100-141]Mrs. Outhouse.

[100-142]"Quite well, thank you, Louis."

[100-143]"I am sorry that our troubles should have deprived you of the home

[100-144]you had been taught to expect." To this Nora made no reply, but

[100-145]escaped, and went up to her sister. "My poor little boy," said

[100-146]Trevelyan, taking the child and placing it on his knee. "I suppose

[100-147]you have forgotten your unfortunate father." The child, of course,

[100-148]said nothing, but just allowed himself to be kissed.

[100-149]"He is looking very well," said Mrs. Outhouse.

[100-150]"Is he? I dare say he is well. Louey, my boy, are you happy?" The

[100-151]question was asked in a voice that was dismal beyond compare, and it

[100-152]also remained unanswered. He had been desired to speak nicely to his

[100-153]papa, but how was it possible that a child should speak nicely under

[100-154]such a load of melancholy? "He will not speak to me," said Trevelyan.

[100-155]"I suppose it is what I might have expected." Then the child was put

[100-156]off his knee on to the floor, and began to whimper. "A few months

[100-157]since he would sit there for hours, with his head upon my breast,"

[100-158]said Trevelyan.

[100-159]"A few months is a long time in the life of such an infant," said

[100-160]Mrs. Outhouse.

[100-161]"He may go away," said Trevelyan. Then the child was led out of the

[100-162]room, and sent up to his mother.

[100-163]"Emily has done all she can to make the child love your memory," said

[100-164]Mrs. Outhouse.

[100-165]"To love my memory! What;--as though I were dead. I will teach him to

[100-166]love me as I am, Mrs. Outhouse. I do not think that it is too late.

[100-167]Will you tell your husband from me, with my compliments, that I shall

[100-168]cause him to be served with a legal demand for the restitution of my

[100-169]child?"

[100-170]"But Sir Marmaduke will be here in a few days."

[100-171]"I know nothing of that. Sir Marmaduke is nothing to me now. My child

[100-172]is my own,--and so is my wife. Sir Marmaduke has no authority over

[100-173]either one or the other. I find my child here, and it is here that

[100-174]I must look for him. I am sorry that you should be troubled, but the

[100-175]fault does not rest with me. Mr. Outhouse has refused to give me up

[100-176]my own child, and I am driven to take such steps for his recovery as

[100-177]the law has put within my reach."
