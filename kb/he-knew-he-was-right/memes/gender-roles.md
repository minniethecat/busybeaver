# Gender Roles

The constrained position of women in Victorian society forms the structural backdrop of *He Knew He Was Right*. Through the experiences of Emily Trevelyan, Nora Rowley, Dorothy Stanbury, and other female characters, Trollope examines how legal, social, and economic asymmetries between men and women shape — and often foreclose — personal freedom.

## Overview

Victorian gender ideology assigned women to the domestic sphere: marriage and motherhood were the only socially sanctioned life paths. A wife's legal identity was subsumed into her husband's under the doctrine of coverture; she could not own property independently, initiate divorce on equal terms, or claim custody of her children. Trollope's novel explores these constraints not through polemic but through the lived experience of its female characters, who repeatedly confront the gap between personal desire and social possibility.

## Context in Victorian Society

The 1857 Matrimonial Causes Act had reformed divorce law, but profound asymmetry remained: a husband could divorce his wife for adultery alone, while a wife had to prove adultery *plus* another offence such as cruelty or desertion. Custody of children defaulted to the father. Middle-class women who did not marry faced economic precarity — the "redundant woman" problem — as employment options were limited to governessing, teaching, or dependence on male relatives.

Trollope wrote *He Knew He Was Right* in 1868–69, at a time when the "Woman Question" was actively debated in periodicals and Parliament. The novel engages this debate obliquely but persistently: its female characters repeatedly articulate their awareness of systematic disadvantage.

## Manifestation in the Novel

### Emily Trevelyan: The Wife Without Recourse

Emily's tragedy is not simply her husband's jealousy but her powerlessness once that jealousy takes hold. She reflects on the legal reality that if a separation occurs, "the law, and the judges, and the courts" would award the child to the father — she would be assumed "the sinner" (008-089 to 008-093). Her recognition that "it is a very poor thing to be a woman" (008-095) is not self-pity but an accurate assessment of her legal position.

### Nora Rowley: The Marriage Market

Nora, Emily's younger sister, faces the classic dilemma of the portionless Victorian girl. Her father, Sir Marmaduke, has eight daughters and inadequate means. Trevelyan articulates the governing assumption: "It is my idea that girls should not have fortunes" (001-195) — meaning they should marry well, since they cannot inherit or earn. Nora resists the conventional path, refusing the wealthy and eligible Mr. Glascock, yet her options remain severely constrained: "A woman can die, at any rate" (008-117), she says, half in jest but with real awareness of her limited alternatives.

### Dorothy Stanbury: The Dependent Woman

Dorothy represents another variant of female constraint: the unmarried woman dependent on a relative's goodwill. Her life in Exeter under her aunt Miss Stanbury's roof is comfortable but entirely contingent — she has no independent means and no path to autonomy except marriage.

## Key Examples

### "A Very Poor Thing to Be a Woman"

In a pivotal chapter-8 conversation, Emily spells out the asymmetry of her position to Nora: a husband who suspects his wife faces no real social penalty; a wife suspected — even falsely — faces social death and the loss of her child. Emily's bitter conclusion that "it would be better to be a dog" (008-098) registers the depth of her despair at a system that offers her no agency.

### The Custody Battle

The novel's subplot concerning the custody of young Louis Trevelyan dramatises the gender asymmetry of Victorian family law. The father's legal right to the child is assumed; the mother must prove his unfitness to overcome it. This legal reality shapes every decision Emily makes, as she understands that open defiance of her husband would cost her the child.
