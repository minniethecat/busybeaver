## Section 121

[121-001]"Perhaps he won't get it after all," said Arabella.

[121-002]"That would be cruel," replied Camilla. "I don't think that even Miss

[121-003]Stanbury is so false as that."

[121-004]Things were going very badly at Heavitree. There was war there,

[121-005]almost everlastingly, though such little playful conversations as the

[121-006]above shewed that there might be an occasional lull in the battle.

[121-007]Mr. Gibson was not doing his duty. That was clear enough. Even Mrs.

[121-008]French, when she was appealed to with almost frantic energy by her

[121-009]younger daughter, could not but acknowledge that he was very remiss

[121-010]as a lover. And Camilla, in her fury, was very imprudent. That very

[121-011]frantic energy which induced her to appeal to her mother was, in

[121-012]itself, [[truth|proof]] of her imprudence. She knew that she was foolish, but

[121-013]she could not control her passion. Twice had she detected Arabella in

[121-014]receiving notes from Mr. Gibson, which she did not see, and of which

[121-015]it had been intended that she should know nothing. And once, when

[121-016]she spent a night away at Ottery St. Mary with a friend,--a visit

[121-017]which was specially prefatory to marriage, and made in reference to

[121-018]bridesmaids' dresses,--Arabella had had,--so at least Camilla was

[121-019]made to believe,--a secret meeting with Mr. Gibson in some of the

[121-020]lanes which lead down from Heavitree to the Topsham road.

[121-021]"I happened to meet him, and spoke two words to him," said Arabella.

[121-022]"Would you have me cut him?"

[121-023]"I'll tell you what it is, Bella;--if there is any underhand game

[121-024]going on that I don't understand, all [[exeter|Exeter]] shall be on fire before

[121-025]you shall carry it out."

[121-026]Bella made no answer to this, but shrugged her shoulders. Camilla

[121-027]was almost at a loss to guess what might be the truth. Would not any

[121-028]sister, so accused on such an occasion, rebut the accusation with

[121-029]awful wrath? But Arabella simply shrugged her shoulders, and went her

[121-030]way. It was now the 15th of April, and there wanted but one short

[121-031]fortnight to their marriage. The man had not the courage to jilt

[121-032]her! She felt sure that he had not heart enough to do a deed of such

[121-033]audacity. And her sister, too, was weak and a coward, and would

[121-034]lack the power to stand on her legs and declare herself to be the

[121-035]perpetrator of such villany. Her mother, as she knew well, would

[121-036]always have preferred that her elder daughter should be the bride;

[121-037]but her mother was not the woman to have the hardihood, now, in

[121-038]the eleventh hour, to favour such an intrigue. Let her wish be

[121-039]what it might, she would not be strong enough to carry through the

[121-040]accomplishment of it. They would all know that that threat of hers

[121-041]of setting Exeter on fire would be carried out after some fashion

[121-042]that would not be inadequate to the occasion. A sister, a mother, a

[121-043]promised lover, all false,--all so damnably, cruelly false! It was

[121-044]impossible. No history, no novel of most sensational interest, no

[121-045]wonderful villany that had ever been wrought into prose or poetry,

[121-046]would have been equal to this. It was impossible. She told herself so

[121-047]a score of times a day. And yet the circumstances were so terribly

[121-048]suspicious! Mr. Gibson's conduct as a lover was simply disgraceful

[121-049]to him as a man and a clergyman. He was full of excuses, which she

[121-050]knew to be false. He would never come near her if he could help it.

[121-051]When he was with her, he was as cold as an archbishop both in word

[121-052]and in action. Nothing would tempt him to any outward manifestation

[121-053]of affection. He would talk of nothing but the poor women of St.

[121-054]Peter-cum-Pumpkin in the city, and the fraudulent idleness of a

[121-055]certain colleague in the cathedral services, who was always shirking

[121-056]his work. He made her no presents. He never walked with her. He was

[121-057]always gloomy,--and he had indeed so behaved himself in public that

[121-058]people were beginning to talk of "poor Mr. Gibson." And yet he could

[121-059]meet Arabella on the sly in the lanes, and send notes to her by the

[121-060]green-grocer's boy! Poor Mr. Gibson indeed! Let her once get him

[121-061]well over the 29th of April, and the people of Exeter might talk

[121-062]about poor Mr. Gibson if they pleased. And Bella's conduct was more

[121-063]wonderful almost than that of Mr. Gibson. With all her cowardice, she

[121-064]still held up her head,--held it perhaps a little higher than was

[121-065]usual with her. And when that grievous accusation was made against

[121-066]her,--made and repeated,--an accusation the very thought and sound

[121-067]of which would almost have annihilated her had there been a decent

[121-068]feeling in her bosom, she would simply shrug her shoulders and walk

[121-069]away. "Camilla," she had once said, "you will drive that man mad

[121-070]before you have done." "What is it to you how I drive him?" Camilla

[121-071]had answered in her fury. Then Arabella had again shrugged her

[121-072]shoulders and walked away. Between Camilla and her mother, too, there

[121-073]had come to be an almost internecine quarrel on a collateral point.

[121-074]Camilla was still carrying on a vast arrangement which she called the

[121-075]preparation of her trousseau, but which both Mrs. French and Bella

[121-076]regarded as a spoliation of the domestic nest, for the proud purposes

[121-077]of one of the younger birds. And this had grown so fearfully that

[121-078]in two different places Mrs. French had found herself compelled to

[121-079]request that no further articles might be supplied to Miss Camilla.

[121-080]The bride elect had rebelled, alleging that as no [[money|fortune]] was to be

[121-081]provided for her, she had a right to take with her such things as she

[121-082]could carry away in her trunks and boxes. Money could be had at the

[121-083]bank, she said; and, after all, what were fifty pounds more or less

[121-084]on such an occasion as this? And then she went into a calculation to

[121-085]prove that her mother and sister would be made so much richer by her

[121-086]absence, and that she was doing so much for them by her marriage,

[121-087]that nothing could be more mean in them than that they should

[121-088]hesitate to supply her with such things as she desired to make her

[121-089]entrance into Mr. Gibson's house respectable. But Mrs. French was

[121-090]obdurate, and Mr. Gibson was desired to speak to her. Mr. Gibson, in

[121-091]fear and trembling, told her that she ought to repress her spirit of

[121-092]extravagance, and Camilla at once foresaw that he would avail himself

[121-093]of this plea against her should he find it possible at any time to

[121-094]avail himself of any plea. She became ferocious, and, turning upon

[121-095]him, told him to mind his own business. Was it not all for him that

[121-096]she was doing it? "She was not," she said, "disposed to submit to any

[121-097]control in such matters from him till he had assumed his legal right

[121-098]to it by [[reputation|standing]] with her before the altar." It came, however, to

[121-099]be known all over Exeter that Miss Camilla's expenditure had been

[121-100]checked, and that, in spite of the joys naturally incidental to a

[121-101]wedding, things were not going well with the ladies at Heavitree.

[121-102]At last the blow came. Camilla was aware that on a certain morning

[121-103]her mother had been to Mr. Gibson's house, and had held a long

[121-104]conference with him. She could learn nothing of what took place

[121-105]there, for at that moment she had taken upon herself to place herself

[121-106]on non-speaking terms with her mother in consequence of those

[121-107]disgraceful orders which had been given to the tradesmen. But Bella

[121-108]had not been at Mr. Gibson's house at the time, and Camilla, though

[121-109]she presumed that her own conduct had been discussed in a manner very

[121-110]injurious to herself, did not believe that any step was being then

[121-111]arranged which would be positively antagonistic to her own views. The

[121-112]day fixed was now so very near, that there could, she felt, be no

[121-113]escape for the victim. But she was wrong.

[121-114]Mr. Gibson had been found by Mrs. French in a very excited state on

[121-115]that occasion. He had wept, and pulled his hair, and torn open his

[121-116]waistcoat, had spoken of himself as a wretch,--pleading, however,

[121-117]at the same time, that he was more sinned against than sinning, had

[121-118]paced about the room with his hands dashing against his brows, and

[121-119]at last had flung himself prostrate on the ground. The meaning of it

[121-120]all was, that he had tried very hard, and had found at last that "he

[121-121]couldn't do it." "I am ready to submit," said he, "to any verdict

[121-122]that you may pronounce against me, but I should deceive you and

[121-123]deceive her if I didn't say at once that I can't do it." He went on

[121-124]to explain that since he had unfortunately entered into his present

[121-125]engagement with Camilla,--of whose position he spoke in quite a

[121-126]touching manner,--and since he had found what was the condition of

[121-127]his own heart and feelings he had consulted a friend,--who, if any

[121-128]merely human being was capable of advising, might be implicitly

[121-129]trusted for advice in such a matter,--and that his friend had told

[121-130]him that he was bound to give up the marriage let the consequences

[121-131]to himself or to others be what they might. "Although the skies

[121-132]should fall on me, I cannot stand at the hymeneal altar with a lie

[121-133]in my mouth," said Mr. Gibson immediately upon his rising from his

[121-134]prostrate condition on the floor. In such a position as this a

[121-135]mother's fury would surely be very great! But Mrs. French was hardly

[121-136]furious. She cried, and begged him to think better of it, and assured

[121-137]him that Camilla, when she should be calmed down by [[marriage|matrimony]], would

[121-138]not be so bad as she seemed;--but she was not furious. "The truth

[121-139]is, Mr. Gibson," she said through her tears, "that, after all, you

[121-140]like Bella best." Mr. Gibson owned that he did like Bella best,

[121-141]and although no bargain was made between them then and there,--and

[121-142]such making of a bargain then and there would hardly have been

[121-143]practicable,--it was understood that Mrs. French would not proceed to

[121-144]extremities if Mr. Gibson would still make himself forthcoming as a

[121-145]husband for the advantage of one of the daughters of the family.

[121-146]So far Mr. Gibson had progressed towards a partial liberation from

[121-147]his thraldom with a considerable amount of courage; but he was well

[121-148]aware that the great act of daring still remained to be done. He

[121-149]had suggested to Mrs. French that she should settle the matter with

[121-150]Camilla,--but this Mrs. French had altogether declined to do. It

[121-151]must, she said, come from himself. If she were to do it, she must

[121-152]sympathise with her child; and such sympathy would be obstructive

[121-153]of the future arrangements which were still to be made. "She always

[121-154]knew that I liked Bella best," said Mr. Gibson,--still sobbing, still

[121-155]tearing his hair, still pacing the room with his waistcoat torn open.

[121-156]"I would not advise you to tell her that," said Mrs. French. Then

[121-157]Mrs. French went home, and early on the following morning it was

[121-158]thought good by Arabella that she also should pay a visit at Ottery

[121-159]St. Mary's. "Good-bye, Cammy," said Arabella as she went. "Bella,"

[121-160]said Camilla, "I wonder whether you are a serpent. I do not think

[121-161]you can be so base a serpent as that." "I declare, Cammy, you do say

[121-162]such odd things that no one can understand what you mean." And so she

[121-163]went.

[121-164]On that morning Mr. Gibson was walking at an early hour along the

[121-165]road from Exeter to Cowley, contemplating his position and striving

[121-166]to arrange his plans. What was he to do, and how was he to do it? He

[121-167]was prepared to throw up his living, to abandon the cathedral, to

[121-168]leave the diocese,--to make any sacrifice rather than take Camilla

[121-169]to his bosom. Within the last six weeks he had learned to regard her

[121-170]with almost a holy horror. He could not understand by what miracle

[121-171]of self-neglect he had fallen into so perilous an abyss. He had long

[121-172]known Camilla's temper. But in those days in which he had been beaten

[121-173]like a shuttlecock between the Stanburys and the Frenches, he had

[121-174]lost his head and had done,--he knew not what. "Those whom the God

[121-175]chooses to destroy, he first maddens," said Mr. Gibson to himself

[121-176]of himself, throwing himself back upon early erudition and pagan

[121-177]philosophy. Then he looked across to the river Exe, and thought that

[121-178]there was hardly water enough there to cover the multiplicity of his

[121-179]sorrows.
