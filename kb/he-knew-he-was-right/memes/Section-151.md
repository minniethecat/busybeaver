## Section 151

[151-001]"I will send a message directly I get there," he said, holding Lady

[151-002]Rowley by the hand, "and will write fully,--to you,--immediately."

[151-003]"God bless you, my dear friend!" said Lady Rowley, crying.

[151-004]"Good night, Sir Marmaduke," said Hugh.

[151-005]"Good night, Mr. Stanbury."

[151-006]Then he gave a hand to the two girls, each of whom, as she took it,

[151-007]sobbed, and looked away from Nora. Nora was [[reputation|standing]] away from them,

[151-008]by herself, and away from the door, holding on to her chair, and with

[151-009]her hands clasped together. She had prepared nothing,--not a word, or

[151-010]an attitude, not a thought, for his farewell. But she had felt that

[151-011]it was coming, and had known that she must trust to him for a cue

[151-012]for her own demeanour. If he could say adieu with a quiet voice, and

[151-013]simply with a touch of the hand, then would she do the same,--and

[151-014]endeavour to think no worse of him. Nor had he prepared anything; but

[151-015]when the moment came he could not leave her after that fashion. He

[151-016]stood a moment hesitating, not approaching her, and merely called her

[151-017]by her name,--"Nora!" For a moment she was still; for a moment she

[151-018]held by her chair; and then she rushed into his arms. He did not much

[151-019]care for her father now, but kissed her hair and her forehead, and

[151-020]held her closely to his bosom. "My own, own Nora!"

[151-021]It was necessary that Sir Marmaduke should say something. There

[151-022]was at first a little scene between all the women, during which he

[151-023]arranged his deportment. "Mr. Stanbury," he said, "let it be so.

[151-024]I could wish for my child's sake, and also for your own, that your

[151-025]means of living were less precarious." Hugh accepted this simply as

[151-026]an authority for another embrace, and then he allowed them all to go

[151-027]to bed.

[151-028]CHAPTER XCII.

[151-029]TREVELYAN DISCOURSES ON LIFE.

[151-030]Stanbury made his journey without pause or hindrance till he reached

[151-031]Florence, and as the train for Siena made it necessary that he should

[151-032]remain there for four or five hours, he went to an inn, and dressed

[151-033]and washed himself, and had a meal, and was then driven to Mr.

[151-034]Spalding's house. He found the American Minister at home, and was

[151-035]received with cordiality; but Mr. Spalding could tell him little or

[151-036]nothing about Trevelyan. They went up to Mrs. Spalding's room, and

[151-037]Hugh was told by her that she had seen Mrs. Trevelyan once since her

[151-038]niece's marriage, and that then she had represented her husband as

[151-039]being very feeble. Hugh, in the midst of his troubles, was amused

[151-040]by a second and a third, perhaps by a fourth, reference to "Lady

[151-041]Peterborough." Mrs. Spalding's latest tidings as to the Trevelyans

[151-042]had been received through "Lady Peterborough" from [[nora-rowley|Nora Rowley]]. "Lady

[151-043]Peterborough" was at the present moment at Naples, but was expected

[151-044]to pass north through Florence in a day or two. They, the Spaldings

[151-045]themselves, were kept in Florence in this very hot weather by this

[151-046]circumstance. They were going up to the Tyrolese mountains for a

[151-047]few weeks as soon as "Lady Peterborough" should have left them for

[151-048]England. "Lady Peterborough" would have been so happy to make Mr.

[151-049]Stanbury's acquaintance, and to have heard something direct from

[151-050]her friend Nora. Then Mrs. Spalding smiled archly, showing thereby

[151-051]that she knew all about [[hugh-stanbury|Hugh Stanbury]] and his relation to Nora

[151-052]Rowley. From all which, and in accordance with the teaching which

[151-053]we got,--alas, now many years ago,--from a great master on the

[151-054]subject, we must conclude that poor, dear Mrs. Spalding was a snob.

[151-055]Nevertheless, with all deference to the memory of that great master,

[151-056]we think that Mrs. Spalding's allusions to the success in life

[151-057]achieved by her niece were natural and altogether pardonable; and

[151-058]that reticence on the subject,--a calculated determination to

[151-059]abstain from mentioning a triumph which must have been very dear to

[151-060]her,--would have betrayed on the whole a condition of mind lower than

[151-061]that which she exhibited. While [[social-class|rank]], [[money|wealth]], and money are held to

[151-062]be good things by all around us, let them be acknowledged as such. It

[151-063]is natural that a mother should be as proud when her daughter marries

[151-064]an Earl's heir as when her son becomes Senior Wrangler; and when we

[151-065]meet a lady in Mrs. Spalding's condition who purposely abstains from

[151-066]mentioning the name of her titled daughter, we shall be disposed to

[151-067]judge harshly of the secret workings of that lady's thoughts on the

[151-068]subject. We prefer the exhibition, which we feel to be natural. Mr.

[151-069]Spalding got our friend by the button-hole, and was making him a

[151-070]speech on the perilous condition in which Mrs. Trevelyan was placed;

[151-071]but Stanbury, urged by the circumstances of his position, pulled out

[151-072]his watch, pleaded the hour, and escaped.

[151-073]He found Mrs. Trevelyan waiting for him at the station at Siena.

[151-074]He would hardly have known her,--not from any alteration that was

[151-075]physically personal to herself, not that she had become older in

[151-076]face, or thin, or grey, or sickly,--but that the trouble of her

[151-077]life had robbed her for the time of that brightness of apparel, of

[151-078]that pride of feminine gear, of that sheen of high-bred womanly

[151-079]bearing with which our wives and daughters are so careful to invest

[151-080]themselves. She knew herself to be a wretched woman, whose work in

[151-081]life now was to watch over a poor prostrate wretch, and who had

[151-082]thrown behind her all ideas of grace and beauty. It was not quickly

[151-083]that this condition had come upon her. She had been unhappy at

[151-084][[nuncombe-putney|Nuncombe Putney]]; but unhappiness had not then told upon the outward

[151-085]woman. She had been more wretched still at St. Diddulph's, and all

[151-086]the outward circumstances of life in her uncle's parsonage had been

[151-087]very wearisome to her; but she had striven against it all, and the

[151-088]sheen and outward brightness had still been there. After that her

[151-089]child had been taken from her, and the days which she had passed in

[151-090]Manchester Street had been very grievous;--but even yet she had not

[151-091]given way. It was not till her child had been brought back to her,

[151-092]and she had seen the life which her husband was living, and that her

[151-093]anger,--hot anger,--had been changed to pity, and that with pity love

[151-094]had returned, it was not till this point had come in her sad life

[151-095]that her dress became always black and sombre, that a veil habitually

[151-096]covered her face, that a bonnet took the place of the jaunty hat that

[151-097]she had worn, and that the prettinesses of her life were lain aside.

[151-098]"It is very good of you to come," she said; "very good. I hardly knew

[151-099]what to do, I was so wretched. On the day that I sent he was so bad

[151-100]that I was obliged to do something." Stanbury, of course, inquired

[151-101]after Trevelyan's health, as they were being driven up to Mrs.

[151-102]Trevelyan's lodgings. On the day on which she had sent the telegram

[151-103]her husband had again been furiously angry with her. She had

[151-104]interfered, or had endeavoured to interfere, in some arrangements as

[151-105]to his health and comfort, and he had turned upon her with an order

[151-106]that the child should be at once sent back to him, and that she

[151-107]should immediately quit Siena. "When I said that Louey could not be

[151-108]sent,--and who could send a child into such keeping,--he told me

[151-109]that I was the basest liar that ever broke a promise, and the vilest

[151-110]traitor that had ever returned evil for good. I was never to come to

[151-111]him again,--never; and the gate of the house would be closed against

[151-112]me if I appeared there."

[151-113]On the next day she had gone again, however, and had seen him, and

[151-114]had visited him on every day since. Nothing further had been said

[151-115]about the child, and he had now become almost too weak for violent

[151-116]anger. "I told him you were coming, and though he would not say so,

[151-117]I think he is glad of it. He expects you to-morrow."

[151-118]"I will go this evening, if he will let me."

[151-119]"Not to-night. I think he goes to bed almost as the sun sets. I am

[151-120]never there myself after four or five in the afternoon. I told him

[151-121]that you should be there to-morrow,--alone. I have hired a little

[151-122]carriage, and you can take it. He said specially that I was not

[151-123]to come with you. Papa goes certainly on next Saturday?" It was a

[151-124]Saturday now,--this day on which Stanbury had arrived at Siena.

[151-125]"He leaves town on Friday."

[151-126]"You must make him believe that. Do not tell him suddenly, but bring

[151-127]it in by degrees. He thinks that I am deceiving him. He would go back

[151-128]if he knew that papa were gone."

[151-129]They spent a long evening together, and Stanbury learned all that

[151-130]Mrs. Trevelyan could tell him of her husband's state. There was no

[151-131]doubt, she said, that his reason was affected; but she thought the

[151-132]state of his mind was diseased in a ratio the reverse of that of his

[151-133]body, and that when he was weakest in health, then were his ideas the

[151-134]most clear and rational. He never now mentioned [[colonel-osborne|Colonel Osborne]]'s

[151-135]name, but would refer to the affairs of the last two years as though

[151-136]they had been governed by an inexorable Fate which had utterly

[151-137]destroyed his happiness without any fault on his part. "You may be

[151-138]sure," she said, "that I never accuse him. Even when he says terrible

[151-139]things of me,--which he does,--I never excuse myself. I do not think

[151-140]I should answer a word, if he called me the vilest thing on earth."

[151-141]Before they parted for the night many questions were of course asked

[151-142]about Nora, and Hugh described the condition in which he and she

[151-143]stood to each other. "Papa has consented, then?"

[151-144]"Yes,--at four o'clock in the morning,--just as I was leaving them."

[151-145]"And when is it to be?"

[151-146]"Nothing has been settled, and I do not as yet know where she will go

[151-147]to when they leave [[london|London]]. I think she will visit Monkhams when the

[151-148]Glascock people return to England."

[151-149]"What an episode in life,--to go and see the place, when it might all

[151-150]now have been hers!"

[151-151]"I suppose I ought to feel dreadfully ashamed of myself for having

[151-152]marred such promotion," said Hugh.

[151-153]"Nora is such a singular girl;--so firm, so headstrong, so good, and

[151-154]so self-reliant that she will do as well with a poor man as she would

[151-155]have done with a rich. Shall I confess to you that I did wish that

[151-156]she should accept Mr. Glascock, and that I pressed it on her very

[151-157]strongly? You will not be angry with me?"

[151-158]"I am only the more proud of her;--and of myself."

[151-159]"When she was told of all that he had to give in the way of wealth

[151-160]and rank, she took the bit between her teeth and would not be turned

[151-161]an inch. Of course she was in love."

[151-162]"I hope she may never regret it;--that is all."

[151-163]"She must change her nature first. Everything she sees at Monkhams

[151-164]will make her stronger in her choice. With all her girlish ways, she

[151-165]is like a rock;--nothing can move her."

[151-166]Early on the next morning Hugh started alone for Casalunga, having

[151-167]first, however, seen Mrs. Trevelyan. He took out with him certain

[151-168]little things for the sick man's table;--as to which, however, he

[151-169]was cautioned to say not a word to the sick man himself. And it was

[151-170]arranged that he should endeavour to fix a day for Trevelyan's return

[151-171]to England. That was to be the one object in view. "If we could get

[151-172]him to England," she said, "he and I would, at any rate, be together,

[151-173]and gradually he would be taught to submit himself to advice." Before

[151-174]ten in the morning, Stanbury was walking up the hill to the house,

[151-175]and wondering at the dreary, hot, hopeless desolation of the spot.

[151-176]It seemed to him that no one could live alone in such a place, in

[151-177]such weather, without being driven to madness. The soil was parched

[151-178]and dusty, as though no drop of rain had fallen there for months.

[151-179]The lizards, glancing in and out of the broken walls, added to the

[151-180]appearance of heat. The vegetation itself was of a faded yellowish

[151-181]green, as though the glare of the sun had taken the fresh colour out

[151-182]of it. There was a noise of grasshoppers and a hum of flies in the

[151-183]air, hardly audible, but all giving [[truth|evidence]] of the heat. Not a human

[151-184]voice was to be heard, nor the sound of a human foot, and there was

[151-185]no shelter; but the sun blazed down full upon everything. He took

[151-186]off his hat, and rubbed his head with his handkerchief as he struck

[151-187]the door with his stick. Oh God, to what misery had a little folly

[151-188]brought two human beings who had had every blessing that the world

[151-189]could give within their reach!
